> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A federal rule is built in public on a slow, dated pipeline, and the influence on it — lobbying spend, comment letters, the revolving door — is largely disclosed; a reader who follows the docket can anticipate where the rule lands and who profits, legally, before the final rule prints.
>
> - The pipeline is **NPRM → public comment → OIRA review → final rule → litigation**, and every stage leaves a public, dated paper trail you can read months ahead.
> - A *hard* rule often **entrenches the biggest incumbents**, because a fixed compliance cost is rounding error for a leader and fatal for a small rival — a "moat by regulation."
> - The proposed rule is **not** the final rule; it is the opening bid in a negotiation that carve-outs, phase-ins, and thresholds reshape — and the direction of that reshaping is readable.
> - The one number to remember: the Volcker Rule drew roughly **18,000 comment letters** and took **three years** from statute to final rule — influence at that scale is loud, slow, and on the record.

In December 2013, after more than three years of drafting, five federal agencies jointly finalized the Volcker Rule — the Dodd-Frank provision meant to stop banks from gambling with depositors' money. The final text ran to nearly 1,000 pages. The proposed version, published in October 2011, had attracted on the order of 18,000 comment letters; the largest banks and their trade associations filed the most detailed and the most lawyered of them. By the time the rule was final, it contained an elaborate set of exemptions for "permitted activities" — market-making, hedging, underwriting — that critics argued reopened much of the door the statute had tried to shut. The banks that lobbied hardest got the carve-outs they asked for. None of this was secret. All of it was on the public docket.

That is the uncomfortable, useful truth this post is built on. The way a rule gets written in the United States is one of the most *transparent* high-stakes negotiations in the economy. The agency must publish its proposal. The public — which in practice means mostly the regulated industry, its lobbyists, and a handful of well-resourced advocacy groups — gets to file comments, and those comments are posted. The White House's regulatory-review office logs its meetings. Lobbyists file quarterly disclosures of who they lobbied and roughly how much they spent. The final rule must respond to the comments in writing. And if industry loses, it sues, and the lawsuit is public too.

For an investor, this transparency is an edge that is hiding in plain sight. "Regulatory capture" — the idea that the regulated end up steering the regulator — is usually invoked as a complaint. We are going to treat it as a *signal to be read*. The question is never "is this corrupt?" It is "given who is filing what, and who benefits from a carve-out, where is this rule most likely to land, and what is that worth to the affected stocks?" This is analysis, not advocacy: we are describing how the machinery works and how it shows up in prices, with no view on whether any rule is good policy.

Why does this edge persist if the docket is public to everyone? Because reading it is *work*, and the work is unglamorous. The signal is buried in hundreds of pages of legal and economic argument that most market participants never open; the financial press covers the NPRM headline and the final-rule headline and almost nothing in between; and the payoff is uncertain and slow, which most traders dislike. The combination — public information, high reading cost, slow payoff — is exactly the kind of inefficiency that survives in plain sight. The practitioner's advantage is not access to secret information; it is the patience to read the documents everyone *could* read and the framework to turn them into a probability distribution over prices. That framework is the rest of this post.

## Foundations: how a rule is actually made

Before we can read the docket, we need the vocabulary. Every term below is something you will see, by name, on a real government website. None of it requires a law degree.

### The Administrative Procedure Act (APA)

Congress passes *statutes* — laws. But a modern statute rarely says exactly what businesses must do. It says, in effect, "the Environmental Protection Agency shall set standards for power-plant emissions," or "the Securities and Exchange Commission shall require disclosure of material risks," and then leaves the detail to an *agency*. The agency fills in that detail by writing *rules* (also called *regulations*), which have the force of law.

The **Administrative Procedure Act of 1946 (the APA)** is the master statute that governs *how* an agency is allowed to write a rule. Think of it as the rulebook for making rules. Its central requirement is that the agency cannot simply decree a rule from behind closed doors. It must use **notice-and-comment rule-making**: tell the public what it intends to do, let the public respond, and explain its final decision in light of those responses. This is the spine of everything that follows. The APA is also what makes the process *slow* and *public* — both of which are gifts to an investor who is paying attention.

### Notice-and-comment: the NPRM and the comment docket

The process begins with a **Notice of Proposed Rule-making (NPRM)**, published in the *Federal Register* (the government's daily journal of official actions). The NPRM is the agency's draft: here is the rule we propose, here is our legal authority, here is our reasoning, here are the questions we want answered. It is genuinely a *proposal* — the opening position, not the final word.

The NPRM opens a **comment period**, usually 30 to 60 days but often extended to 90 or 120 days for a big rule. Anyone can file a comment, and they are filed on a single public website, **regulations.gov**, where each rule has its own *docket* — a numbered folder containing the proposal, every comment, and supporting studies. When people say "read the docket," this is the docket they mean. You can sort comments, read the long technical ones from law firms and trade associations, and watch which arguments the agency engages with.

A "comment" is not a vote. Agencies are not required to do what the majority of commenters want; a rule is not a referendum. What the agency *is* required to do is consider the substantive comments and respond to the significant ones. So a comment letter that supplies new data — an economic study showing a rule would cost \$4 billion a year, say — carries far more weight than 100,000 form-letter postcards. This is why a single 80-page comment from a trade group's economists can move a rule more than a mass campaign.

### OIRA: the White House's review gate

Before a major rule from an executive agency becomes final, it usually passes through the **Office of Information and Regulatory Affairs (OIRA)**, a small but powerful office inside the White House's Office of Management and Budget. OIRA reviews significant rules for cost-benefit analysis and consistency with the administration's priorities. It can sit on a rule, send it back for changes, or wave it through. OIRA review can take up to 90 days (and sometimes much longer), and — crucially — OIRA logs its meetings with outside parties, including which company or trade group came in to argue against a rule. Those meeting logs are public. A flurry of industry meetings at OIRA in the weeks before a rule is finalized is a readable sign that the rule is being contested at the last gate.

### The final rule, the effective date, and litigation

When the agency is done, it publishes the **final rule** in the *Federal Register*, accompanied by a preamble that responds to the comments and explains what changed and why. The final rule sets an **effective date** (when it takes legal force) and often a **compliance date** or a *phase-in* schedule (firms get one, two, sometimes five years to comply). The gap between "final" and "must comply" is where a lot of the economic action sits.

Industry's last move, if it loses, is **litigation**: a petition for review filed in a federal court of appeals, arguing the rule is "arbitrary and capricious," exceeds the agency's statutory authority, or violated the APA's procedures. A court can *vacate* (strike down) the rule, *remand* it (send it back to the agency to redo), or uphold it. The lawsuit is the backstop — and because it is public the moment it is filed, it is yet another readable signal.

### Lobbying disclosure: the LDA filings

How do companies try to shape all of this? Some of it is the comment letters above. But the broader effort — meetings with members of Congress and agency staff, hiring former officials, funding studies — is **lobbying**, and a large share of it is *disclosed by law*. The **Lobbying Disclosure Act (LDA)** requires registered lobbyists and the firms that employ them to file quarterly reports: who they lobbied, on what issues, and how much the client spent on lobbying that quarter. Those filings are public and searchable. They are coarse — the dollar figure is a rough total, not a line item — but they tell you which companies are spending heavily to influence which bills and rules, and when that spending spikes.

### The revolving door

The **revolving door** is the movement of people between government agencies and the industries they regulate. A lawyer leaves the SEC's enforcement division to defend banks; an industry executive joins the agency that oversees their old sector; an agency economist becomes a consultant to the firms whose rules they used to write. None of this is illegal in itself (there are cooling-off periods and recusal rules), and it is not necessarily corrupt — the people who understand an industry best often *are* the people who worked in it. But it shapes the *norms* and *sympathies* inside an agency, and it is, again, largely a matter of public record: appointments are announced, and biographies list prior employers.

### Regulatory capture and rent-seeking

Two more terms close the toolkit. **Regulatory capture** describes a situation where an agency, over time, comes to serve the interests of the industry it regulates rather than the broad public — through some mix of lobbying, the revolving door, information asymmetry (the industry knows the technical details the agency depends on), and simple repeated contact. **Rent-seeking** is the economic term for spending resources to gain an advantage through rules and policy — a bigger slice of the pie — rather than by creating new value. A company that lobbies for a rule that raises its rivals' costs is rent-seeking, even if everything it does is legal and disclosed.

The investor's stance toward all this is deliberately cold. We are not asking whether capture is good or bad. We are asking: *if* a rule is shaped by the disclosed influence we can read, then where does it land, and what is that worth?

### Rule versus guidance versus enforcement

One more distinction sharpens the read, because not every agency action is a rule, and the type tells you how binding and how readable it is. A **rule** (regulation) goes through notice-and-comment and has the force of law; it is the slow, public, contestable object this post is about. **Guidance** — interpretive bulletins, no-action letters, staff FAQs, "Dear CEO" letters — is the agency telling you how it reads existing law; it skips full notice-and-comment, can appear with little warning, and is legally weaker (a firm can challenge guidance that functions as a binding rule without the process). **Enforcement** is the agency suing or fining a specific firm; it sets a precedent the whole sector reads even though no rule changed. For an investor, the practical hierarchy is: a final *rule* is the slowest and most readable; *guidance* is faster and surprises more; an *enforcement action* against a bellwether can reprice a sector overnight with no rule-making at all. Much of what looks like "the SEC cracking down on crypto" or "the FTC signaling on mergers" is enforcement and guidance, not rules — louder and faster, but also easier to reverse and harder to forecast from a docket.

### Cost-benefit analysis: the rule's own scorecard

A significant rule must, by executive order and often by statute, come with a **regulatory impact analysis (RIA)** — the agency's own estimate of the rule's costs and benefits, published with the NPRM. The RIA is gold for an investor because it is the agency's *self-reported* number for what the rule costs the industry, broken out by category and sometimes by firm size. When the agency itself says "this rule imposes \$2.3 billion a year in compliance cost, concentrated in firms above \$10 billion in assets," it has handed you the input to your valuation model and told you where the threshold bites. Industry comment letters then attack the RIA — arguing the agency *understated* costs or *overstated* benefits — and the gap between the agency's number and industry's number brackets your scenario range. OIRA's review centers on this scorecard, which is why an analytically weak RIA is a clue the rule is vulnerable to being sent back or, later, vacated in court for failing to justify itself.

## How comments and lobbying reshape a rule's final form

The single biggest mistake a newcomer makes is treating the NPRM as the rule. The proposed rule is the agency's *ambition*; the final rule is what survives the negotiation. Three levers do most of the reshaping, and all three are readable in the docket.

**Carve-outs (exemptions).** The final rule often exempts categories of activity or types of firm. The Volcker Rule's market-making and hedging exemptions are the classic example. A carve-out is where a comment letter's fingerprints are most visible: industry argues "your rule will accidentally ban this legitimate, valuable activity," supplies examples, and the agency writes an exception. If you read a comment letter asking for exemption X, and the final rule contains a version of exemption X, you have watched influence work in real time.

**Phase-ins (delayed compliance).** Even a hard rule is softer if firms get five years to comply instead of one. A long phase-in lets incumbents amortize the cost, gives them time to build the compliance machinery, and pushes the earnings hit far enough into the future that the market discounts it lightly. Comment letters frequently focus less on *whether* a rule applies than on *when* — because a distant cost is a small cost in present-value terms.

**Thresholds (who is covered).** A rule that applies only to firms above \$50 billion in assets, or to facilities emitting more than a certain tonnage, draws its line somewhere — and exactly where that line sits is fiercely contested. Raise the threshold and you exempt the mid-sized players; lower it and you sweep them in. Smaller firms lobby to be above the line (exempt); the largest firms sometimes lobby to *lower* it, because a rule that also binds their mid-sized rivals is a rule that hurts the competition more than it hurts the leader.

Read together, these three levers explain why the same statute can produce a rule that is brutal or toothless. And the direction of travel between proposal and final is usually visible: count the carve-out requests in the docket, see which ones come with serious legal and economic argument, watch the OIRA meeting log, and you can form a view on whether this rule is hardening or softening *before* the final text prints.

### How to actually read a comment letter

A docket on a major rule can hold tens of thousands of comments, and most of them are noise — form letters, one-line opinions, duplicate campaigns. The skill is filtering for the handful that will actually move the agency. Four tells separate a load-bearing comment from filler.

First, **does it bring data the agency lacks?** Agencies suffer from information asymmetry: the industry knows its own cost structure far better than the regulator does. A comment that supplies a credible, sourced cost estimate — "your monitoring requirement will cost a mid-sized facility \$12 million in year one, here is the engineering breakdown" — forces the agency to either accept the number or rebut it in the final preamble. That is influence with a paper trail. A comment that just says "this rule is burdensome" changes nothing.

Second, **does it propose specific alternative text?** The most effective comments do not merely object; they hand the agency a drafted carve-out, threshold, or phase-in it can adopt almost verbatim. When you later see the final rule contain language close to a commenter's proposed text, you have direct evidence of who steered the outcome. Sophisticated trade associations employ former agency lawyers precisely to draft in the agency's own idiom.

Third, **is it legally framed for the lawsuit to come?** A comment that carefully builds the record — "the agency has not considered X, in violation of the APA" or "this exceeds the authority Congress granted in Section Y" — is laying litigation groundwork. Industry knows that to challenge a rule in court, the argument generally has to have been raised during the comment period. So a docket thick with crisp legal objections is a docket where litigation is being pre-loaded, which raises the probability of the *vacated* branch.

Fourth, **who signed it?** A joint letter from every major firm in a sector, on the same ask, signals a unified industry that is more likely to prevail than one filing scattered, conflicting comments. Conversely, a docket where large incumbents quietly support the rule while small firms scream is the fingerprint of a moat play — the incumbents *want* the cost barrier their smaller rivals are protesting.

Run those four filters and a forty-thousand-comment docket collapses to the ten or twenty letters that matter, and their alignment is your single best read on where the final rule lands.

#### Worked example: pricing in a softened final rule

A regional bank, "MidState," trades at \$40 with earnings per share (EPS) of \$4.00, a price-to-earnings (P/E) multiple of 10×. A proposed capital rule, as written in the NPRM, would force MidState to hold extra capital that the market estimates cuts its sustainable EPS by \$0.60, to \$3.40. At the same 10× multiple, that proposed rule justifies a price of \$34 — a \$6, or 15%, hit. The stock has already drifted down to \$36 as the market prices in *some probability* of the hard version.

You read the docket. Mid-sized banks have filed detailed comments arguing the rule should apply only above \$100 billion in assets; MidState has \$70 billion. The OIRA meeting log shows three meetings with regional-bank trade groups. You judge the threshold carve-out is likely. If the final rule exempts MidState, EPS stays at \$4.00 and the fair value returns toward \$40.

Expected value, with a 70% probability of the carve-out:

```
E[EPS]   = 0.70 × $4.00 + 0.30 × $3.40 = $2.80 + $1.02 = $3.82
Fair val = 10 × $3.82                   = $38.20
Edge     = $38.20 − $36.00              = $2.20  (about +6%)
```

You buy at \$36 with a fair value of \$38.20 and an upside to \$40 if the carve-out fully lands. The point: the docket told you which version of the rule was likely, and the gap between the stock's "hard-rule" price and the probability-weighted fair value was the trade.

## Incumbents versus entrants: the moat-by-regulation

Here is the counterintuitive heart of the matter. We instinctively assume a tough new rule *hurts* the regulated industry. Often it does the opposite for the *biggest* players. The mechanism is simple arithmetic: most compliance costs have a large **fixed** component — a legal and reporting apparatus, a monitoring system, a chief compliance officer and her team — that does not scale down for a small firm. A fixed cost is a small percentage of a giant's revenue and a crushing percentage of a minnow's.

When a rule imposes a fixed cost, it does not fall equally in *economic* terms even though it falls equally in *dollar* terms. The incumbent absorbs it and keeps going. The small rival, whose margins were already thin, cannot — and either exits, sells out, or never enters. The rule has, in effect, raised the *barrier to entry* and *barrier to survival*. Economists call the result a **moat by regulation**: the rule protects the leader's market position more durably than any product advantage could. This is precisely why the largest, most sophisticated incumbents sometimes *welcome* a hard rule — and lobby for the version that binds their competitors as tightly as it binds them.

![Before and after a fixed compliance cost: the incumbent share rises from 40 to 55 percent as small rivals exit](/imgs/blogs/lobbying-rulemaking-and-regulatory-capture-3.png)

This dynamic is general. It shows up in banking (capital and stress-test rules favor the megabanks that can spread the cost), in tobacco and alcohol (heavy regulation entrenches the few national brands), in pharmaceuticals (the cost of clinical trials and FDA compliance is a moat against small competitors), in finance more broadly, and increasingly in technology, where privacy and content-moderation rules cost a giant a rounding error and a startup its runway. The connection to a stock investor's mental model of *competitive advantage* is direct: a durable, rising market share protected by a cost barrier is the textbook definition of an economic moat. For the deeper treatment of why durable moats are worth a premium multiple, see the equity-research note on [economic moats and durable competitive advantage](/blog/trading/equity-research/economic-moats-durable-competitive-advantage); regulation is one of the most underrated sources of one.

The pharmaceutical case deserves a closer look, because the moat there is almost entirely regulatory. To bring a drug to market in the United States, a company must run multi-phase clinical trials and clear FDA review — a process that, including failures along the way, is widely estimated to cost well over a billion dollars per approved drug and take roughly a decade. That cost is *fixed* with respect to how the drug eventually sells: a giant pharma company amortizes it across a global salesforce and a portfolio of products, while a small biotech must raise the entire sum on the promise of a single molecule. The result is exactly the moat-by-regulation pattern: high fixed cost, durable concentration among the firms that can fund it, and a structural barrier that has nothing to do with the chemistry of the drug. When the FDA tightens trial requirements (more patients, longer follow-up, stricter endpoints), it raises that fixed cost — and the large incumbents, far from being hurt, see their barrier deepen. An investor who reads an FDA guidance document as "small biotech just got more expensive to compete with" rather than "regulation is bad for pharma" is reading the competitive effect correctly. The binary nature of a single FDA decision — approve or reject — is a separate, powerful catalyst that the sector-regulation literature treats in its own right; here the point is the *standing* cost barrier, not the one-day event.

Technology is the newest theater for the same play. When the European Union's General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) took effect in 2018, the conventional wisdom was that it would hammer Big Tech's data-hungry business model. Some of that happened. But a large fixed cost of GDPR compliance — privacy engineering, consent infrastructure, legal teams, data-protection officers — was again trivial for the largest platforms and punishing for small ad-tech firms and publishers. Several studies after GDPR found that the *share* of the digital-advertising market held by the largest platforms rose, not fell, as smaller intermediaries that could not afford compliance consolidated or exited. The rule aimed at the giants entrenched them. This is the single most important pattern in the post: when you hear "tough new rules for industry X," your first question should be *which firms in X*, because the answer is very often "the rule helps the biggest ones."

#### Worked example: valuing a compliance-cost moat

A sector has one incumbent, "BigCo," at 40% market share, and a long tail of small rivals splitting the other 60%. The total market is \$10 billion in revenue a year. A new rule imposes a \$30 million-per-year fixed compliance cost on every participant.

For BigCo, revenue is 40% × \$10 billion = \$4 billion, so \$30 million is **0.75%** of sales — a shrug. For a typical small rival with \$150 million in revenue, \$30 million is **20%** of sales — more than its entire operating margin. The small rivals cannot bear it.

Suppose half the small rivals exit, and BigCo captures most of their share, rising from 40% to 55%. Its revenue goes from \$4.0 billion to 55% × \$10 billion = \$5.5 billion. At a 25% operating margin, operating profit rises:

```
Before:  $4.0bn × 25%              = $1.000bn
After:   $5.5bn × 25% − $30m cost  = $1.375bn − $0.030bn = $1.345bn
Gain in operating profit                                  = +$0.345bn  (+34.5%)
```

BigCo pays \$30 million in new compliance cost and earns \$345 million in extra profit from the share it gobbles up — a better than 11-to-1 return on "complying." The rule that looked like a tax on BigCo was a moat-builder. *A fixed cost is a weapon the strong wield against the weak.*

This is also why the capital-cycle intuition — that high returns attract competition and erode — can break down under a regulatory moat: the competition is legally barred from entering. The equity-research piece on [the capital cycle and why high returns attract competition](/blog/trading/equity-research/the-capital-cycle-why-high-returns-attract-competition) is the mirror image of this section; regulation is what lets a leader defy the cycle.

## The litigation backstop: when industry sues to vacate

If a final rule lands harder than industry can stomach, the fight does not end — it moves to court. Trade associations and individual firms file *petitions for review* asking a federal appeals court to vacate the rule. The grounds are usually that the agency acted "arbitrarily and capriciously" (failed to consider an important factor, or didn't justify its choice), exceeded the authority Congress gave it, or skipped a step the APA requires (for example, finalizing a rule that differs so much from the proposal that the public never got to comment on what was actually adopted — a "logical outgrowth" problem).

Litigation matters to an investor for three reasons. First, it *delays*: a rule under challenge is often stayed (paused) or simply ignored by firms betting it will be struck down, so the compliance cost doesn't bite on schedule. Second, it can *vacate* the rule entirely, handing back to the sector whatever the rule would have taken away — frequently a sharp, immediate re-rating. Third, the *filing itself* is a signal: when the biggest names in a sector all sue within days of a final rule, it tells you the rule is real, costly, and contested, and it puts a court date on your calendar as a catalyst.

The base rates here are not trivial. In recent years, courts have grown more willing to strike down agency rules — the Supreme Court's narrowing of agency deference and its "major questions" reasoning (an agency needs clear congressional authorization for rules of vast economic significance) have raised the odds that an aggressive rule gets vacated. For the investor, that means the "vacated" branch of the outcome tree carries more probability weight than it did a decade ago, and the value of being positioned for it has risen.

#### Worked example: a binary expected value across hard, soft, and vacated

You are looking at "EnviroCorp," a compliance-services vendor that *sells* the monitoring systems a new emissions rule would require. Its stock is \$50. Map three outcomes:

- **Hard rule survives** (mandated demand surges): fair value \$70.
- **Soft rule** (watered-down thresholds, less mandated demand): fair value \$52.
- **Vacated in court** (no rule, no mandate, no revenue): fair value \$30.

From the docket — heavy industry opposition, strong "major questions" arguments in the comment letters, a circuit known for skepticism of agencies — you assign probabilities of 35% hard, 35% soft, 30% vacated.

```
E[value] = 0.35×$70 + 0.35×$52 + 0.30×$30
         = $24.50  + $18.20  + $9.00
         = $51.70
Edge vs $50 price = +$1.70  (about +3.4%, but with a fat downside tail)
```

The expected value is barely above the price, and the downside scenario is severe (\$30, a 40% loss). A disciplined reader concludes the *risk-reward is poor* despite a positive expected value, because the vacated tail is large and the upside is not paid enough. *Reading the docket is as much about declining a bad bet as taking a good one.* The same expected-value scaffolding underlies how practitioners size any regulatory bet — the [regulatory risk as an asset-pricing factor](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/regulatory-risk-as-an-asset-pricing-factor) note turns this kind of payoff tree into a discount-rate haircut.

## The revolving door and information asymmetry

The most misunderstood part of capture is the revolving door, because it sounds like a smoking gun and is usually something subtler. When a former agency official joins an industry firm — or vice versa — the cartoon version is "they are buying a corrupt insider." The reality is mostly about *information* and *norms*. An agency writing a technical rule depends on the industry for the facts: how the plumbing actually works, what a requirement would really cost, what is feasible. The industry employs the people who used to make those judgments inside the agency, which makes the industry's comment letters more credible, better-targeted, and harder for the agency to dismiss. The advantage is legal, disclosed (appointments are announced and biographies are public), and durable.

For an investor, the revolving door is a *standing* signal about a sector's influence, not a single catalyst. A regulator whose senior staff is heavily drawn from the industry, and whose alumni populate the industry's government-affairs and law functions, will — all else equal — produce rules with more carve-outs and softer thresholds, because the two sides share a vocabulary and a worldview. You can read this from public appointment announcements and from who signs the most effective comment letters. It does not mean the agency is corrupt; it means the *base rate* of softening in that sector is higher, which you fold into your probability of the soft-rule branch.

Information asymmetry is the engine underneath. The agency cannot know an industry's cost structure as well as the industry does, so the industry's framing of the facts heavily shapes the rule. This is why the most effective comment is the one that supplies data: it fills the agency's information gap on the industry's terms. It is also why thinly staffed agencies regulating complex industries tend to defer more — they have no independent way to check the numbers they are given. A sector that out-resources its regulator on technical expertise will, over time, get rules written closer to its preferences, and that imbalance is observable in budgets, headcounts, and the provenance of the data in the docket.

#### Worked example: the revolving-door tilt as a probability shift

You are modeling a complex rule in a sector where the regulator is thinly staffed and the industry's comment letters are written by former agency officials supplying detailed cost data the agency cannot independently check. Your base case, from the comment alignment alone, is 40% hard / 40% soft / 20% vacated. You judge that the information asymmetry and revolving-door dynamic add a *tilt toward softening* — shifting roughly 15 percentage points from "hard" into "soft."

Adjusted distribution: 25% hard / 55% soft / 20% vacated. Apply it to "BigCo," which is worth \$130 under a hard rule, \$108 under a soft rule, and \$95 if vacated, against a \$100 price:

```
E[base]     = 0.40×$130 + 0.40×$108 + 0.20×$95 = $52 + $43.2 + $19   = $114.20
E[adjusted] = 0.25×$130 + 0.55×$108 + 0.20×$95 = $32.5 + $59.4 + $19 = $110.90
```

The revolving-door tilt *lowers* BigCo's expected value (from \$114.20 to \$110.90) because the softer rule means a smaller moat — BigCo benefits *most* from the hard version that crushes its rivals. The lesson runs against intuition: an industry-friendly regulator can be *bad* for the dominant incumbent, because the incumbent's edge comes from a rule harsh enough to kill the competition. *Capture does not help every firm equally; it helps whoever wins the specific carve-out, and sometimes a soft rule helps the small survivors more than the giant.*

## Reading the docket as a signal

We now have the pieces. Reading the docket means turning the public record into a *probability distribution* over where the rule lands, and then mapping that distribution onto stocks. Here is the structured read.

**Step one: locate the rule on the clock.** Find the NPRM on the *Federal Register* or regulations.gov, note the comment-close date, and estimate the path to a final rule. Big rules typically take 12 to 36 months from proposal to final; the Volcker Rule took three years. The *timing* alone tells you when the catalyst lands and how long you have to position. The regulatory calendar deserves the same discipline as an earnings calendar — see [the regulatory calendar and trading the rule-making clock](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-regulatory-calendar-trading-the-rulemaking-clock) for the full catalyst-mapping method.

![One rule on the docket clock: the Volcker Rule from 2010 statute to 2013 final rule and later relief](/imgs/blogs/lobbying-rulemaking-and-regulatory-capture-5.png)

**Step two: read the comment alignment.** Who is asking for what? Sort the docket for the substantive letters — the ones with data and legal argument, not form letters. Are the largest incumbents asking for a *hard* rule with broad coverage (a sign they want a moat that binds rivals), or for carve-outs and high thresholds (a sign they want the rule softened)? Is the opposition unified or split? A sector where every major firm files the same ask is more likely to get it than one where firms fight each other.

**Step three: weigh the lobbying spend and the OIRA log.** Pull the LDA filings for the firms and trade groups in the sector and look for a *spike* in lobbying spend around the comment period — a signal of how hard the fight is. Check the OIRA meeting log for last-mile lobbying as the rule heads to final. Heavy late-stage industry activity at OIRA correlates with softening, because OIRA is the last gate where the White House can trim a rule.

**Step four: estimate the tilt and the value.** Combine the above into a probability distribution — hard / soft / vacated — and assign fair values to each affected name under each outcome, as in the worked examples. The *expected value minus the current price* is your edge; the *spread between best and worst case* is your risk.

**Step five: map winners and losers.** A rule is never one-directional. A hard rule helps the incumbent and the compliance vendor and hurts the small rival; a vacated rule does the reverse. Build the matrix explicitly so you are positioned across the *relationship*, not just betting on one name. Often the cleanest expression is a *pair*: long the incumbent that benefits from the moat, short (or underweight) the marginal rival that the rule squeezes — a position that profits from the *relative* effect even if the whole sector moves with the market.

![How disclosed influence reaches the rule through lobby spend, the revolving door and expert comments](/imgs/blogs/lobbying-rulemaking-and-regulatory-capture-6.png)

#### Worked example: lobbying ROI as a return on capital

This is the calculation that makes capture legible as economics rather than morality. A trade association representing an industry spends \$8 million lobbying over two years against a proposed rule. The rule, as proposed, would have cost the industry \$2 billion a year in compliance. The final rule, after the carve-outs and a five-year phase-in the industry won, costs roughly \$1.2 billion a year.

The lobbying *avoided* \$800 million a year in cost. Even ignoring the phase-in benefit, the one-year return on the \$8 million lobbying spend is:

```
Cost avoided / lobbying spend = $800m / $8m = 100×  (a 10,000% return)
```

A 100-to-1 return on lobbying is not unusual in the academic literature on the topic, and it explains why lobbying is rational, persistent, and — to an investor — *predictable*. When the prize (cost avoided) is three orders of magnitude larger than the price of the fight, the fight will be fought, and fought professionally. *The reason capture is structural, not accidental, is that the return on rent-seeking dwarfs the return on almost any operating investment.* For how this kind of policy spending nets through to the macro picture and corporate cash flows, the macro-trading note on [fiscal and monetary interaction and the policy mix](/blog/trading/macro-trading/fiscal-monetary-interaction-dominance-policy-mix) traces the same dollars from Washington to the broad market.

## Common misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Regulation hurts the regulated industry."** Often it protects the biggest players. Recall the worked example: a \$30 million fixed compliance cost was 0.75% of a \$4 billion incumbent's sales and 20% of a \$150 million rival's. The rule pushed half the small rivals out and lifted the incumbent's share from 40% to 55%, raising its operating profit by 34.5% — while costing it 0.75% of revenue. The empirical signature is everywhere: heavily regulated industries (large banks, branded tobacco, big pharma) tend to be *more* concentrated and *more* profitable for the survivors, not less. The right question is not "does this rule hurt the industry?" but "which firms in the industry does it hurt, and which does it help?"

**Misconception 2: "Lobbying is hidden."** A large share of it is disclosed by law. LDA filings list, every quarter, which firms spent how much lobbying which chambers and agencies on which issues. Comment letters are posted in full on regulations.gov — you can read the exact carve-out a bank's lawyers requested. OIRA logs its meetings with outside parties. The Volcker Rule's roughly 18,000 comments were all public. The hidden part of influence — relationships, dinners, the revolving door's effect on sympathies — is real but is the *minority* of the mechanism; the majority leaves a paper trail. Treating lobbying as an invisible conspiracy means ignoring a free, searchable dataset.

**Misconception 3: "The proposed rule is the final rule."** It is the opening bid in a negotiation. Between proposal and final, carve-outs are added, thresholds move, and phase-ins stretch. The Volcker Rule's final text contained market-making and hedging exemptions that the proposal had treated far more restrictively. Across rule-making generally, final rules routinely differ materially from proposals — that is the *entire point* of notice-and-comment. An investor who shorts a stock the day an NPRM drops, assuming the worst version is law, is mispricing the negotiation that is about to happen.

**Misconception 4: "A final rule is settled law."** Not until the courts are done with it. Industry sues, rules get stayed, and — increasingly — rules get vacated under "arbitrary and capricious" review or the "major questions" doctrine. A rule can be "final" and still be a live, two-sided bet for years. Pricing a final rule as a certainty ignores the litigation branch that, lately, carries meaningful probability.

## How it shows up in real markets

Three patterns recur. Each is a real, dated phenomenon, and each maps to a side of the trade.

### A hard rule that widened the incumbents' moat

After the 2008 crisis, the Dodd-Frank Act and the Basel capital framework imposed heavy fixed costs on banks: stress testing, living wills, vastly expanded compliance and legal departments, and capital surcharges that scaled with size and complexity. The intent was safety. One durable side effect was concentration. The cost of being a bank rose, and it rose *most* in fixed terms — a compliance and reporting apparatus that a JPMorgan or Bank of America could spread over trillions in assets and a community bank could not. The number of US commercial banks, already on a decades-long decline, kept falling through the 2010s — from roughly 7,000 at the start of the decade toward about 4,000 — with very few new bank charters granted in the years right after the rules landed, while the assets of the largest banks grew as a share of the system. A fixed compliance burden that runs to tens of millions of dollars a year is a fraction of a basis point of a multi-trillion-dollar balance sheet and an existential line item for a \$500 million community bank; the arithmetic of the moat-by-regulation example played out across an entire industry. The biggest banks did not love every rule, and they lobbied hard against the costliest pieces, but the *structure* of the post-crisis rulebook entrenched them.

An investor who understood that "tougher bank regulation" and "stronger megabank franchise" were *complements*, not opposites, read the decade correctly: own the scale players that turn compliance into a barrier, be wary of the sub-scale banks that wear the same cost as a far larger share of earnings. The signal was readable in real time — in the comment dockets where large and small banks asked for opposite things, in the threshold fights over which asset size triggered the heaviest rules (the line that was eventually moved up to \$250 billion in the 2018 rollback, exempting dozens of regional banks), and in the OIRA meeting logs. The mechanism by which bank capital rules throttle credit and reshape balance sheets is the macro plumbing other posts in this series cover; here the point is the *competitive* effect, and it favored the giants.

### A watered-down final rule that re-rated a sector up

The 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA) is the cleanest case of a *favorable* rule re-rating a sector — and it was won partly through disclosed, intense lobbying by corporate America. The statutory corporate tax rate fell from 35% to 21%, effective 2018. The market did not wait for the ink to dry; it front-ran the bill as it advanced through Congress, with the most-taxed domestic sectors rallying as passage looked likely. After enactment, S&P 500 gross buybacks jumped to a record — roughly \$806 billion in 2018, up from about \$519 billion in 2017 — as companies returned the tax windfall to shareholders. The "rule" here was tax law rather than an agency regulation, but the dynamic is identical: a disclosed legislative fight, a readable trajectory, and a re-rating that began before the final text was law. The deep treatment lives in [the 2017 TCJA and the repatriation trade](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-2017-tcja-and-the-repatriation-trade); the chart below is the market's response.

![Statutory corporate tax rate falling from 35 to 21 percent in 2018 alongside record S&P 500 buybacks](/imgs/blogs/lobbying-rulemaking-and-regulatory-capture-8.png)

### A hard rule that prints a public price

The European Union's Emissions Trading System (EU ETS) is the purest example of a *hard* rule whose tightening is read straight off a price screen. The rule is a legal cap on carbon emissions that ratchets down over time; firms must buy allowances (EUAs) to emit, and the allowance trades like any other instrument. As the EU tightened the cap and reformed the market, the allowance price climbed from roughly €6 per tonne in 2017 to about €81 in 2022 and €84 in 2023, before easing to about €65 in 2024 — a more than thirteen-fold move driven by a *rule*, not by supply and demand for a physical commodity. That price *is* the market discounting the rule: a rising EUA is a direct cost to emitters (utilities, steel, cement) and a tailwind to low-carbon competitors. Because the cap schedule is published years ahead, the *direction* was readable long before the price arrived — a hard rule whose docket was, in effect, a calendar. An investor positioned long low-carbon and short carbon-heavy names was trading the rule, not the weather.

The carbon case also illustrates the moat dynamic from the other direction. A high carbon price is a fixed cost per tonne emitted, and it falls hardest on the operators with the dirtiest, least efficient plants — typically the marginal, sub-scale producers — while the largest, most modern, best-hedged operators can pass the cost through or have already invested in lower-emission capacity. So even a rule explicitly designed to *raise* an industry's costs tends to entrench the strongest players within it. The same logic that makes a bank-capital rule a megabank moat makes a carbon rule a moat for the most efficient utility. Whenever a rule imposes a cost, the reflex should be to ask which firms wear it lightly and which it breaks — the answer is the trade.

![EU carbon allowance price rising from about 6 euros per tonne in 2017 to about 84 in 2023](/imgs/blogs/lobbying-rulemaking-and-regulatory-capture-7.png)

### A rule vacated in court

The fourth pattern — a final rule struck down — has become more common as courts narrowed agency deference. When a costly final rule is vacated, the affected sector typically re-rates *up* on the news, because a cost the market had partly discounted suddenly disappears. The trade is to be positioned for the litigation branch when the docket and the legal arguments suggest the rule is vulnerable: heavy "major questions" framing in the comments, a rule that stretches a thin statutory hook, and a challenge filed in a skeptical circuit. The vacated outcome is the high-variance leg of the outcome tree, and — as the binary expected-value example showed — it is the leg that most often re-rates a sector sharply in a single session.

The legal climate that governs this branch shifted materially in the 2020s. For four decades, courts gave agencies wide latitude to interpret ambiguous statutes (the "Chevron deference" doctrine); a sequence of Supreme Court decisions narrowed and then ended that deference, and elevated the "major questions" idea that an agency needs *clear* congressional authorization before it can issue rules of vast economic or political significance. The practical effect for an investor is that the *probability* of a major rule being vacated rose, and rose most for the most aggressive rules — exactly the ones that would otherwise impose the largest costs. That means two things. First, the value of being positioned for the vacated branch went up across the board. Second, the *more* a final rule overreaches relative to its statutory hook, the *more* likely it is to be struck down — so the scariest-looking rules are often the least durable, and a market that panics on the final-rule headline can be handing you the other side of a court-driven reversal months later. Reading the comment letters for the strength of the "major questions" argument is, in this climate, one of the highest-value parts of the whole exercise.

## The playbook: trading the rule before it prints

Pull it together into a repeatable process. The thesis: the rule-making pipeline is public and slow, the influence on it is largely disclosed, so a reader can anticipate where a rule lands and who profits *before* the final rule prints. The trade is to price the *probability distribution* over outcomes, not the headline.

**1. Track the NPRM and the docket.** Set alerts on the *Federal Register* and regulations.gov for rules that touch your sectors. The moment an NPRM drops, read it — not the press coverage, the actual proposal — and find the comment-close date and the agency's own cost estimate.

**2. Read the comment alignment.** Sort the docket for substantive letters. Map who wants the rule hard (incumbents seeking a moat) versus soft (firms seeking carve-outs and high thresholds). A unified industry ask is more likely to land than a split one. Note every requested carve-out, threshold, and phase-in — these are the levers that will reshape the final rule.

**3. Weigh lobbying spend and the OIRA log.** Pull LDA filings for the relevant firms and trade groups; flag spikes in spend around the comment period. Watch the OIRA meeting log as the rule nears final — heavy late industry activity correlates with softening.

**4. Estimate the final-rule tilt.** Assign probabilities to hard / soft / vacated. Be honest about the litigation branch — in the current legal climate, the vacated probability is non-trivial for aggressive rules.

**5. Map winners and losers and value them.** For each affected name, compute fair value under each outcome. The cleanest expression is usually a *pair*: long the incumbent the rule entrenches, underweight or short the marginal rival it squeezes, so you profit from the *relative* effect with less market beta. Compliance vendors are a third, often-overlooked leg — they win on a hard rule and lose on a vacated one.

**6. Size and set invalidation.** Size to the *spread* between your best and worst outcome, not your point estimate; a wide spread (a live litigation tail) demands a smaller position. Define what kills the thesis *in advance*: the comment period closing with the opposite alignment to what you expected, an OIRA meeting log showing the rule hardening when you bet on softening, or a final rule whose carve-outs differ from the ones the docket pointed to. When the disclosed record contradicts your read, exit — the docket that gave you the edge is the same docket that tells you when you are wrong.

The biggest practical pitfall in this whole exercise is *timing decay*: a rule-making can stall for years, get withdrawn after an election, or be quietly shelved, and a stock position held against an event that never arrives bleeds opportunity cost the entire time. This is why the rule-making clock matters as much as the rule's content. If you are expressing the view in the underlying stocks, be honest that you may be early by quarters and size accordingly. If you can express it with options, you are forced to put a *date* on the catalyst, which disciplines the read — a rule you think finalizes in nine months is a different instrument from one that has been "imminent" for three years. And always separate the *continuous* repricing (the slow drift as the market handicaps the rule through the comment period) from the *discrete* repricing (the gap on the final-rule print or the court ruling). The drift is where patient docket-readers accumulate; the gap is where the crowd reacts. Knowing which one you are trading determines whether you are paid for patience or for speed.

A final discipline: keep the analysis politically neutral. The temptation is to let a view about whether a rule is *good* leak into a forecast of where it *lands*. They are different questions. A rule you dislike can still be likely to survive; a rule you favor can still be likely to be vacated. The docket-reader's job is to forecast the outcome the public record actually points to and price it — not to handicap the policy on its merits. The moment your politics start moving your probabilities, the edge is gone, because the market does not pay you to be right about what *should* happen, only about what *will*.

#### Worked example: sizing the pair from the outcome spread

You build the pair: long incumbent "BigCo" at \$100, short marginal rival "SmallCo" at \$20. Your outcome map for the rule:

- **Hard rule** (55% probability): BigCo \$130 (+30), SmallCo \$12 (−8). Pair gain per share-pair = +30 + 8 = +\$38.
- **Soft rule** (30%): BigCo \$108 (+8), SmallCo \$22 (+2). Pair gain = +8 − 2 = +\$6.
- **Vacated** (15%): BigCo \$95 (−5), SmallCo \$26 (+6). Pair gain = −5 − 6 = −\$11.

Expected pair gain per share-pair:

```
E[pair] = 0.55×$38 + 0.30×$6 + 0.15×(−$11)
        = $20.90  + $1.80  − $1.65
        = $21.05  per long-BigCo / short-SmallCo pair
```

The expected gain is strongly positive and the worst case (−\$11) is bounded and modest relative to the upside, so the risk-reward supports the pair. You size so that the −\$11 vacated case is a tolerable drawdown on the book — say 2% of capital at risk in that branch — and you set the invalidation: if the docket alignment flips toward a soft or vacated rule before the comment period closes, you cut the position to the soft-case sizing. *Position the relationship, weight it by the disclosed distribution, and let the worst branch — not the best — set the size.* For the broader discipline of sizing event-driven and pair trades around a binary catalyst, the macro-trading and event-trading playbooks carry the risk-management mechanics this builds on.
