> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Vietnam's frontier-to-emerging upgrade is not a vague hope; it is a sequence of specific, datable legal and operational reforms, and each one clears a named blocker that index providers grade.
>
> - The blockers were concrete: foreign investors had to pre-fund trades, settlement was slow, foreign access was capped, and disclosure was thin and Vietnamese-only. Each reform attacks one blocker by name.
> - The reform roadmap — Circular 68 non-prefunding (Sep 2024), the revised Securities Law (Nov 2024), the KRX trading system going live (May 2025), phased English disclosure — converts neatly into a **catalyst calendar** with FTSE review dates attached.
> - On a FTSE Secondary-Emerging inclusion, the passive bid is roughly **\$5–8 billion** of forced buying, concentrated in a handful of large, liquid stocks with foreign room still open. A later MSCI upgrade would be several times larger.
> - The one number to remember: Vietnam's expected weight in FTSE Emerging at inclusion is around **0.5%**, and multiplying that by the tracked pool is what produces the billion-dollar headline.

On the afternoon of 9 September 2024, the State Securities Commission of Vietnam (the SSC, the country's market regulator) signed Circular 68. To a foreign reader the document looks like dry plumbing: it changed the rules on how a foreign institution funds a stock purchase. But to anyone watching the long-running story of Vietnam's market upgrade, it was the single most important regulatory event in a decade. For years the biggest, most boring obstacle to Vietnam joining the club of "emerging markets" had been a requirement that foreigners deposit cash *before* they were allowed to buy. Circular 68 removed it. The VN-Index, the main stock-market gauge of the Ho Chi Minh City exchange (HOSE), barely twitched that day — because the market had already been pricing the reform in, on and off, for two years.

That gap between the rule changing and the price reacting is the whole game in this post. Vietnam has spent a decade stuck on FTSE Russell's "watchlist" for a potential upgrade from **frontier** to **secondary emerging** status, and on MSCI's longer list of unresolved access problems. The upgrade matters because it is one of the rare events in markets where a government can, by passing the right laws and switching on the right computer system, mechanically summon billions of dollars of foreign buying that *has* to happen — passive index funds are legally obliged to track their benchmark, so when Vietnam enters the benchmark, they must buy.

This is, in other words, a pure example of the spine that runs through this whole series: **law and policy change the rules of the game, the rules change who is forced to do what, and that forced behaviour reprices assets.** The reader who understands the reform roadmap as a *calendar of datable catalysts* — rather than a fuzzy "Vietnam might get upgraded someday" — can position ahead of the crowd, size the flows correctly, and know exactly what would invalidate the trade. Let us build that calendar from first principles.

## Foundations: what an upgrade is and what blocks it

Before any trading, we need the vocabulary. Every term below gets defined from zero, because the upgrade story is a chain and a single missing link breaks the whole thing.

**A market classification.** Index providers — the companies that build the benchmarks fund managers track — sort the world's stock markets into tiers. The three that matter are **Developed** (the US, Japan, Western Europe), **Emerging** (China, India, Brazil, Taiwan, and so on), and **Frontier** (smaller, less accessible markets — Vietnam, Kenya, Romania, Kazakhstan). The two providers that count are **FTSE Russell** and **MSCI**. A "market upgrade" means moving up a tier — for Vietnam, from Frontier to Emerging. FTSE has a useful intermediate rung, **Secondary Emerging**, which is the realistic first target; MSCI jumps straight to Emerging and grades harder.

**Why the tier matters: the size of the money that tracks it.** This is the engine of the entire thesis. Trillions of dollars are managed against emerging-market benchmarks, while only a few tens of billions track frontier benchmarks. A country sitting in the Frontier index is fishing in a small pond; the same country in the Emerging index is suddenly in the path of a vastly larger river of capital. The upgrade does not make Vietnamese companies more profitable overnight — it changes *who is benchmarked to own them*.

There is a further indignity to frontier status that the upgrade also fixes. Within the small frontier-index pool, Vietnam is already a *large* fish — it has been one of the heaviest weights in the FTSE Frontier index, sometimes around a third of it. That sounds flattering, but it is a trap: being a giant weight in a tiny, shrinking pond means frontier-dedicated funds are already maxed out on Vietnam and have no room to add, while the giant emerging-market pool owns essentially none of it. Promotion flips this entirely. Vietnam goes from being an oversized weight in a pool that cannot buy more, to a small weight in a pool that *must* establish a position from zero. The asymmetry of "already-full small buyers losing interest" versus "huge new buyers starting from nothing" is the whole reason an upgrade is a net positive for flows even though Vietnam's *percentage* weight falls when it moves up a tier.

**Passive versus active money.** A **passive fund** (an index fund or ETF) is run by a rule, not a view: it holds whatever the index holds, in the index's weights. When the index adds Vietnam, every passive emerging-market fund *must* buy Vietnamese stocks, regardless of whether the manager likes them — that is the "forced" buying that makes upgrades tradeable. An **active fund** has discretion; it may buy ahead, or not at all. The passive bid is the part you can model with arithmetic; the active bid is the part that front-runs it.

It is worth dwelling on *why* passive money is forced, because the whole trade hangs on it. An index fund's job, contractually, is to deliver the index's return minus a tiny fee. If the index adds Vietnam at a 0.5% weight and the fund does not hold Vietnam, the fund's return will drift away from the benchmark whenever Vietnamese stocks move — a deviation called **tracking error** that the fund is paid to minimise. So on the day the new index weights take effect, the fund manager has no choice: the rulebook says hold Vietnam, and the manager holds Vietnam. There is no committee meeting, no view on valuation, no waiting for a better entry. That mechanical, price-insensitive, calendar-driven obligation is what an upgrade trade is built to exploit. The forced buyer does not care what price they pay; they care only about matching the index — which is exactly the kind of counterparty an informed trader wants on the other side.

**The index effect.** The empirical regularity that a stock tends to rise when it is *added* to a widely-tracked index, and fall when it is *removed*, purely because of the flow rather than any change in the business. The effect is strongest for stocks with a thin tradeable float relative to the forced buying, and it tends to build *before* the effective date as active money front-runs the passive money, then partly fade afterwards. A country-level upgrade is the index effect at industrial scale — an entire market being added at once. The same mechanism, at the single-stock level, is what drives the rebalancing trades in any major developed market.

Now the blockers — the specific operational problems that have kept FTSE and MSCI from pulling the trigger. Each one has a precise name, and each reform on the roadmap is a fix for one of them.

**Pre-funding (the prefunding requirement).** Until 2024, a foreign institution buying Vietnamese shares had to have the full cash sitting in its local account *before* the order could be placed. In every developed market the norm is the opposite: you trade today and settle (pay) two business days later, a convention called **T+2**. Pre-funding is operationally hostile to large global funds — it traps cash, creates currency risk, and is simply not how their systems are built. FTSE explicitly flagged it as the headline blocker. This is the problem Circular 68 solved.

To see why pre-funding is such a deal-breaker, walk through the operational reality for a London-based emerging-market fund. To buy a Vietnamese stock, it must first wire dollars to a custodian in Vietnam, convert them to dong, and park the dong in a local account — all *before* it knows whether its buy order will even fill at the price it wants. If the order does not fill, the cash sits idle in a foreign currency, earning nothing and exposed to the dong moving against it. Multiply that across hundreds of orders a day and the drag is enormous. Global funds are built around the assumption that they can trade first and fund the settlement two days later, netting their buys and sells so they only ever move the small net amount. Pre-funding breaks that entire workflow. It is not that foreigners *cannot* trade Vietnam under pre-funding — they did, for years — but that the friction kept the largest, most systematic, most index-driven pools of capital away. Removing it does not merely tidy up a rule; it opens the door to a class of investor that simply would not operate under the old regime.

**Settlement and Delivery-versus-Payment (DvP).** **Settlement** is the back-office process where the buyer's cash and the seller's shares actually change hands after a trade. **DvP** means the two legs happen simultaneously, so neither side can run off with both the cash and the stock — it is the gold standard for safety. Index providers want a robust, DvP-compliant settlement cycle. Pre-funding was partly a crude workaround for a settlement system that could not otherwise guarantee the cash would be there.

**The KRX trading system.** The exchange's order-matching engine — the computer that pairs buyers and sellers — was old and capacity-constrained. Vietnam contracted the Korea Exchange (KRX) to build a modern replacement, known simply as "the KRX system." A modern matching engine is the technical precondition for newer settlement mechanics, more product types, and the throughput that a much larger foreign investor base would generate. After years of delays, it went live on HOSE in 2025.

The KRX system matters for a reason beyond raw speed. During the 2021 liquidity boom, HOSE's old system repeatedly choked when daily turnover surged, forcing the exchange to halt afternoon trading and even raise the minimum lot size to throttle order volume — an embarrassing episode for a market trying to convince global committees it was ready for prime time. A matching engine that falls over under heavy retail volume is not one a global passive fund will trust to absorb a multi-hundred-million-dollar rebalance. The new system also unlocks features that emerging-market investors take for granted: a partial-settlement model, the technical scaffolding for a central counterparty, and the headroom to add products like covered warrants and, eventually, a deeper derivatives market. The matching engine is, in short, the foundation on which the *later* reforms physically sit — which is exactly why its repeated delays held the whole roadmap hostage.

**Foreign access and the foreign ownership limit (FOL), or "room."** Vietnamese law caps how much of many companies foreigners may collectively own — often 49%, and 30% for banks. When foreigners hit that cap, the stock is "out of room," and the only way to buy more is at a premium in a separate off-exchange market. Index providers treat constrained foreign access as a partial blocker; it does not stop an upgrade, but it shrinks the weight a stock can receive. We cover the room mechanics in depth in the companion post on [foreign ownership limits and the upgrade path](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/foreign-ownership-limits-room-and-the-msci-ftse-upgrade-path); here it matters because it decides *which* stocks get the passive bid.

**Disclosure and English reporting.** Global investors need timely financial information in English. Historically much Vietnamese corporate disclosure was Vietnamese-only and patchy. MSCI in particular grades information flow and equal access for foreign investors. This is the softest, slowest blocker — you cannot fix culture and corporate behaviour with one circular — and it is the one most likely to keep Vietnam off the *MSCI* list even after FTSE moves.

**The revised Securities Law (Law on Securities, amended 2024).** The umbrella statute that governs the market. The 2024 amendment, passed by the National Assembly in November 2024, hard-coded several things index providers wanted into primary law rather than leaving them to circulars: clearer rules on the non-prefunding model, on disclosure obligations, on the legal status of a central counterparty (below), and on penalties for manipulation. Primary law is sturdier and more credible to a foreign committee than a regulator's circular, which is why the upgrade case strengthened materially once it passed.

The hierarchy of Vietnamese rule-making is worth understanding because it determines how much weight a foreign committee gives each reform. At the top sits the **Law** (Luat), passed by the National Assembly — the most durable instrument, hard to reverse. Below it are government **Decrees** (Nghi dinh), then ministry and regulator **Circulars** (Thong tu) like Circular 68. A circular can be amended or withdrawn by the issuing body relatively quietly; a law cannot. So when the non-prefunding model and the CCP framework moved *up* from circular into the amended Securities Law, the reform stopped being a regulator's revocable preference and became the settled law of the land. For an index provider deciding whether to bet its credibility on Vietnam's reforms sticking, that promotion from circular to statute is precisely the kind of durability signal it looks for. The deeper map of who writes these rules — the SSC, the State Bank of Vietnam (SBV), the National Assembly, and the exchanges — is the subject of the companion post on [Vietnam's market legal architecture](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/vietnam-market-legal-architecture-ssc-sbv-and-the-exchanges).

**The central counterparty (CCP) and CCP clearing.** A **central counterparty** is an institution that steps into the middle of every trade, becoming the buyer to every seller and the seller to every buyer, and guaranteeing the trade even if one side defaults. CCP clearing is the modern way to make non-prefunding safe at scale: the CCP, backed by a default fund, absorbs the risk that a foreign buyer fails to pay on settlement day. Vietnam's full CCP model is the last big piece, expected after the KRX system beds in, and it is more central to the harder *MSCI* criteria than to the nearer-term FTSE move.

**FTSE watchlist versus MSCI criteria.** FTSE Russell runs a transparent, checklist-style assessment and publishes a "watchlist" of countries under review, with scheduled review dates (an interim review in spring, the main classification announcement in autumn). MSCI's process is more qualitative, slower, and weights the experience of large institutional investors heavily — so MSCI typically follows FTSE by a few years. The practical sequence for Vietnam is: **FTSE Secondary Emerging first, MSCI Emerging later.**

**The passive-flow math.** Finally, the number everyone quotes. The forced inflow on inclusion is, at heart, three multiplications: the global pool of money benchmarked to the relevant emerging index, times Vietnam's weight in that index, times the share of that money that is genuinely passive (rule-bound) rather than active. We will do this arithmetic carefully in its own section, because the headline "\$5–8 billion" is only as good as the assumptions inside it.

With the vocabulary in place, the reform roadmap stops being a list of acronyms and becomes a calendar.

## The reform roadmap as a catalyst calendar

A **catalyst** is a datable event expected to move a price. The art of trading an upgrade is to stop thinking "Vietnam will be upgraded eventually" and start thinking "here are the specific dates on which new information arrives, and here is how the market should reprice at each one." Put the reforms and the review dates on one axis and you get a calendar you can position against.

![Timeline of Vietnam upgrade catalysts from Circular 68 in 2024 to MSCI review after 2027](/imgs/blogs/vietnams-regulatory-reform-and-upgrade-catalysts-2.png)

Read the calendar left to right. The reforms cluster in 2024–2025 and the index decisions follow.

**September 2024 — Circular 68, non-prefunding.** The SSC removed the pre-funding requirement for foreign institutional investors, allowing securities firms to extend short-term credit so a foreign client can place an order without the cash pre-deposited, with payment due on settlement. This directly answered FTSE's headline blocker. It is the reform with the cleanest one-to-one mapping onto a criterion.

**November 2024 — the revised Securities Law.** The National Assembly passed the amendment, lifting the non-prefunding model and disclosure and CCP provisions from circular into statute. The significance is *credibility*: a foreign classification committee discounts a regulator's circular (which can be quietly reversed) far less than a law passed by the legislature.

**Spring 2025 — FTSE interim review.** FTSE's mid-year check. The realistic expectation here was *not* an upgrade but a constructive note keeping Vietnam on the watchlist and acknowledging the non-prefunding progress while waiting for it to be observed working in practice. Interim reviews rarely upgrade; they set the tone for the autumn decision.

**May 2025 — the KRX system goes live.** After repeated slips, the new matching engine launched on HOSE. This was the operational milestone the market had been waiting on since 2021, and the precondition for the deeper settlement and clearing reforms that the harder criteria need.

**Autumn 2025 — FTSE annual classification.** The main event. This is the date on which FTSE can announce that Vietnam is upgraded to Secondary Emerging, typically with an effective implementation date roughly six to twelve months later. The announcement is the catalyst; the implementation date is when the passive money actually moves.

**2026 — effective inclusion and the passive bid.** If the autumn decision is positive, the *implementation* falls here. On and around the effective date, passive funds rebalance into Vietnam, producing the forced buying. This is the cash-flow event, distinct from the announcement.

**2027 and beyond — MSCI review and CCP clearing.** The longer game. Full CCP clearing and a sustained track record of foreign access feed the MSCI process, which, if it moves, brings a second, larger wave of flows.

The most important distinction on the whole calendar is between the **announcement** date and the **effective implementation** date, because they reprice the market in two different ways. When FTSE *announces* an upgrade in autumn, it is delivering new information: the probability of inclusion jumps from "likely" to "confirmed," and the market reprices the *expectation* immediately, often with a sharp move in the days around the announcement. But the passive money does not move that day — index funds rebalance on the *effective* date, typically six to twelve months later, when the new weights actually take effect in the benchmark. So an upgrade produces two distinct price events: an expectations re-rating on the announcement, then a flow-driven grind into the implementation date as active money front-runs the passive money, often followed by a "sell the news" wobble once the forced buying is done. A trader who treats the announcement and the implementation as the same event will be repeatedly surprised by the timing of the moves.

There is also a quieter set of dates worth tracking between the headline events: the publication of FTSE's detailed country-classification report (which spells out exactly which criteria are still amber), any SSC or HOSE announcements on go-live dates for outstanding infrastructure, and the regular foreign-ownership and turnover data that lets you *observe* whether the non-prefunding reform is actually pulling new foreign money in. Those observation points are where a differentiated view is built — long before the consensus has settled on a probability. The general discipline of reading a regulator's published schedule this way, across any market, is the subject of [the regulatory calendar and trading the rulemaking clock](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-regulatory-calendar-trading-the-rulemaking-clock).

#### Worked example: turning the calendar into a position clock

Take a fund with \$10 million earmarked for the Vietnam upgrade trade and the calendar above. The naive approach buys the basket today and waits. The calendar approach sizes exposure to the *odds at each date*. Suppose you assign a 25% probability to the autumn-2025 upgrade after the spring interim review, and the basket is expected to rise 15% on a confirmed upgrade and fall 5% on a "kept on watchlist" disappointment. The expected move into the autumn date is `0.25 × 15% + 0.75 × (−5%) = 3.75% − 3.75% = 0%`. On those numbers the market is fairly priced and there is no edge in pre-positioning hard. But if your own read of the non-prefunding evidence puts the probability at 45%, the expected move becomes `0.45 × 15% + 0.55 × (−5%) = 6.75% − 2.75% = +4.0%`, or about **\$400,000** of edge on the \$10 million — and *now* you size up into the date. The lesson: the calendar tells you *when* information arrives; your differentiated probability tells you *how much* to bet.

## Each reform maps to an upgrade criterion

The reason the roadmap is credible — and the reason a slip on one piece does not necessarily sink the whole upgrade — is that the reforms are not interchangeable goodwill gestures. Each one is the specific answer to a specific graded criterion. Lay the reforms against the FTSE and MSCI checklists and the mapping is almost one-to-one.

![Matrix mapping each Vietnam reform to its FTSE blocker MSCI blocker and 2025 status](/imgs/blogs/vietnams-regulatory-reform-and-upgrade-catalysts-3.png)

The matrix is worth reading as the analytical core of the post. **Non-prefunding** clears the settlement-cycle blocker for FTSE and improves the DvP picture for MSCI; it went live in September 2024. The **KRX system** answers the trade-matching and infrastructure questions for both, live since May 2025. The revised **Securities Law** strengthens investor rights and the legal framework, passed November 2024. **English disclosure** is the partial one — it improves information flow for FTSE and equal access for MSCI, but it is phasing in rather than done. **CCP clearing** is not really a FTSE Secondary-Emerging gating item at all, but it is central to the harder MSCI clearing-safety criterion, and it is pending into 2027.

The shape of the matrix tells you the trade. The FTSE column is mostly green — the near-term upgrade is well-supported. The MSCI column has more amber and red — the harder upgrade is genuinely further out. This is why a disciplined investor separates the two: the FTSE move is a 2025–2026 event with a high probability and a knowable size; the MSCI move is a 2027-plus call with real execution risk. Conflating them is the most common analytical error in the Vietnam upgrade story.

A subtle point the matrix makes visible: **the reforms are sequenced, not simultaneous.** Non-prefunding had to come before the FTSE interim review could note progress; the KRX system had to go live before deeper clearing reforms were even technically possible; CCP clearing builds on the KRX system. A delay early in the chain pushes everything behind it. That dependency structure is exactly why the history of this upgrade is a history of delays — which we come to shortly.

The matrix also clarifies a distinction that confuses many investors: FTSE and MSCI are *not* grading the same things to the same standard. FTSE Russell runs a relatively transparent, criteria-by-criteria assessment with a published "Quality of Markets" matrix; a country either "passes," "restricts," or "fails" each item, and FTSE tells you which is which. That transparency is why the FTSE path is *forecastable* — you can read the report, see which boxes are still red, and map each to a reform. MSCI's process is deliberately more holistic and qualitative: it surveys large international investors about their *lived experience* of accessing the market, and a market can satisfy every written criterion yet still wait because the big institutions report friction in practice. This is why two reforms that look identical on paper score differently across the two providers, and why the MSCI column carries more amber even where the FTSE column is green. The practical implication for sizing is large: the FTSE move can be modelled as a near-binary event with a knowable date window, while the MSCI move is better treated as a slow-burning option with years of theta to pay.

#### Worked example: how one amber criterion caps the achievable weight

Disclosure being only "partial" rather than "passed" is not a cosmetic footnote — it can directly shrink the flow. Take the \$6 billion midpoint passive bid from the section below, but suppose FTSE upgrades Vietnam on a *reduced* initial weight because the disclosure and foreign-access criteria are judged only partly met, trimming the effective weight from 0.5% to 0.4%. The forced bid falls proportionally: `\$6.0bn × (0.4% ÷ 0.5%) = \$4.8 billion`, a **\$1.2 billion** haircut purely from one amber box on the scorecard. Worse, a partial pass can mean a *phased* inclusion, where the weight is built up over two or three rebalancing dates rather than delivered at once — spreading the same dollars over a longer window and softening the price impact further. The takeaway: the colour of every criterion on the matrix is a dollar figure, not a formality, so the amber boxes deserve as much attention as the green ones.

## The passive-inflow math, done carefully

Now the number. The "\$5–8 billion" headline is repeated everywhere, usually with no working shown. Let us show the working, because the assumptions are where the disagreement lives — and because a trader who can rebuild the number from scratch can stress-test someone else's claim in seconds.

![Waterfall from tracked emerging market pool through Vietnam weight and passive share to forced buying](/imgs/blogs/vietnams-regulatory-reform-and-upgrade-catalysts-4.png)

The estimate is a chain of three multiplications, shown in the figure as a left-to-right narrowing from a huge pool to a concrete forced bid.

**Step one — the tracked pool.** Start with the total assets benchmarked to the relevant FTSE Emerging indices. Across index funds, ETFs, and the active managers who measure themselves against the benchmark, this is on the order of several trillion dollars. For the secondary-emerging tilt of the money most relevant to a new entrant, a working figure of roughly **\$3–5 trillion** of total benchmarked assets is reasonable, though estimates vary with exactly which index family you count.

**Step two — Vietnam's weight.** When a country enters an index, it receives a weight proportional to its *investable* market capitalisation — float-adjusted (only the freely tradeable shares count) and foreign-room-adjusted (shares foreigners cannot buy are excluded). For Vietnam, the consensus first-cut weight in FTSE Emerging is around **0.5%**. This is the single most important number in the whole calculation, and it is genuinely uncertain — it depends on which large-caps still have foreign room on the effective date.

**Step three — the passive share.** Not all benchmarked money is forced. Only the genuinely passive, rule-bound portion *must* buy mechanically on the effective date; active managers have discretion. The passive share of emerging-market money is commonly taken at **20–30%**.

Multiply them and you get the forced bid. The active money — which may be a multiple of the passive money — is not *forced*, but a good deal of it front-runs the event, which is why prices often move well before the implementation date.

#### Worked example: rebuilding the \$5–8 billion number

Take the midpoints. Tracked pool of **\$4 trillion**, Vietnam weight of **0.5%**, passive share of **30%**. The forced passive bid is `\$4,000bn × 0.005 × 0.30 = \$6.0 billion`. Run the conservative corner — \$3 trillion pool, 0.4% weight, 20% passive — and you get `\$3,000bn × 0.004 × 0.20 = \$2.4 billion`. Run the aggressive corner — \$5 trillion, 0.6%, 30% — and you get `\$5,000bn × 0.006 × 0.30 = \$9.0 billion`. So the honest range is roughly **\$2.5–9 billion of forced passive buying**, clustering around \$5–6 billion at the midpoint. Anchored in dong at the kit's USD/VND rate of 25,000, a \$6 billion bid is about **150 trillion VND** — for context, several times a heavy day's total turnover on HOSE. The intuition: the headline is not magic, it is three numbers multiplied, and the one that moves the answer most is Vietnam's index weight.

To make the scale tangible relative to the market itself: Vietnam's investable market cap is large enough that a few billion dollars of net foreign buying, arriving in a compressed window, is a material fraction of the *free float that foreigners can actually touch*. That is the source of the re-rating — not the total market cap, but the much smaller pool of buyable shares the forced money has to chase. The mechanics of how index-driven flows hit a thin float are the same ones we cover in the Vietnam-stocks series post on [foreign flows, ETFs, and the index effect](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/foreign-flows-etfs-and-the-index-effect-vietnam).

Three caveats keep the arithmetic honest, and each one is where a careless headline goes wrong. First, the **tracked pool is not all forced**: the trillions benchmarked to emerging-market indices include vast sums of active money that can choose to ignore Vietnam, so only the passive slice is mechanically obliged to buy. Second, the **weight is provisional until the effective date**: it is computed from float-adjusted, room-adjusted market cap measured close to implementation, so a year of price moves, new listings, or stocks running out of foreign room can shift it meaningfully from today's estimate. Third, **MSCI is a different and larger animal**: because far more money tracks MSCI Emerging than FTSE Emerging, a future MSCI upgrade would imply a passive bid several times the FTSE figure — but it is also years further out and less certain, so it should be valued as a separate, longer-dated option rather than bundled into the near-term number. A disciplined estimate states the pool, the weight, and the passive share explicitly, gives a range rather than a point, and never quietly swaps the FTSE pool for the MSCI pool to inflate the headline.

It also helps to separate the *flow* from the *re-rating*, because they are two different sources of return with very different durations. The flow is a one-time event: the passive funds buy their weight once, and after the effective date the recurring buying is only the trickle of new money into emerging-market funds times Vietnam's small weight — a far smaller, ongoing bid. The re-rating, by contrast, is a *level shift* in the multiple investors are willing to pay, and if it sticks it is permanent. The flow is the fireworks; the re-rating is the change in the weather. A trade built only on the flow is a short-dated event trade that should be largely unwound after implementation; a trade built on the re-rating is a longer-hold structural position. Confusing the two leads investors to hold the event trade too long and abandon the structural trade too early.

## Which stocks get the bid: picking the beneficiaries

The passive money does not arrive evenly. It flows into the specific stocks that enter the index, in proportion to their index weight — and a stock only earns a large weight if it clears three filters at once. Get the filters right and you own what the forced buyers must buy; get them wrong and you own a stock the flows skip.

![Decision tree for selecting Vietnam stocks by size liquidity and foreign room availability](/imgs/blogs/vietnams-regulatory-reform-and-upgrade-catalysts-5.png)

The tree splits on three questions, and a stock must answer all three favourably.

**Filter one — is it large enough to be in the basket?** Index inclusion is gated on free-float market capitalisation. Only the largest, most liquid names make the cut; a small-cap, however good the company, gets no passive bid because it is not in the index. This filter alone narrows the universe to a few dozen names.

**Filter two — is it liquid?** Index providers require minimum trading volume so that funds can actually build and unwind positions. A large-cap that trades thinly fails the **Average Daily Value (ADV)** test and is excluded or under-weighted. Liquidity is what lets the forced money in without moving the price to the moon — and, perversely, the most liquid names get the biggest weights *and* absorb the flow with the least slippage.

**Filter three — does it have foreign room left?** This is the filter most people forget, and it is decisive. A stock can be huge and liquid, but if foreigners have already bought up to the foreign ownership limit, the index *cuts its weight* to reflect what foreigners can actually own — and the incremental passive money has nowhere to go. The biggest beneficiaries are therefore the large, liquid stocks that *still have room open*. A blue-chip already jammed against its foreign cap is a poor upgrade vehicle, because the flow that "should" buy it cannot.

The synthesis: **the ideal beneficiary is large, liquid, and has room.** That is a deliberately short list, and it is where the upgrade trade concentrates its risk.

There is a second-order subtlety in filter three that catches even experienced investors. Foreign room is not static — it shrinks as the upgrade story itself draws foreign buyers in. A stock that has comfortable room *today* can be jammed against its limit by the time the effective date arrives, precisely because the upgrade narrative pulled foreigners in early. The index then cuts its weight to what foreigners can still own, and the stock that looked like the perfect beneficiary delivers a smaller passive bid than the model assumed. So the beneficiary screen is not a one-time snapshot; it is a *monitoring* exercise, watching the foreign-room gauge on each candidate as the date approaches and being willing to rotate out of names that fill up. The room mechanics, the off-exchange "room premium" that develops when a stock fills its cap, and how the limits interact with the upgrade path are covered in depth in [foreign ownership limits, room, and the upgrade path](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/foreign-ownership-limits-room-and-the-msci-ftse-upgrade-path).

The sector mix of the beneficiary list is also not random. Because index weight tracks float-adjusted, room-adjusted size, the upgrade flow concentrates in the heaviest, most liquid sectors of the VN-Index — banks, real estate, and the largest consumer and industrial names. Banks are the heart of the index but carry the *tightest* foreign limit at 30%, so many of the biggest banks are chronically out of room and capture less of the flow than their raw size would suggest. That single fact — the index's biggest sector being the one most constrained on foreign access — is why the beneficiary list is often *smaller* and *less bank-heavy* than a naive "buy the biggest stocks" approach would produce. The sector-by-sector structure of the Vietnamese market, and how banks, brokers, property, and steel chain together, is mapped in the Vietnam-stocks series.

#### Worked example: a liquid large-cap's forced buying versus its float

Take a hypothetical index constituent with a free float worth **\$2 billion** that still has foreign room open, and suppose it earns an 8% weight in the new Vietnam index slice. From a \$6 billion total passive bid, this stock's forced buying is `\$6,000m × 0.08 = \$480 million`. Compare that to a normal day's trading: if the stock trades, say, \$20 million a day, the forced bid is **24 days of average volume** that *must* be bought in a compressed rebalancing window. Even spread over the implementation period, that is a powerful, price-insensitive bid against a fixed float — which is precisely why such names tend to grind higher into the effective date. The intuition: it is the ratio of forced buying to *tradeable float*, not the absolute dollar figure, that drives how hard a stock re-rates.

#### Worked example: the frontier-to-emerging re-rating on a market cap

Re-rating is not only about the flow; it is about the multiple investors are willing to pay once a market is "emerging." Frontier markets typically trade at a discount to emerging markets on price-to-earnings (P/E) — partly for the access frictions the reforms remove. Suppose a Vietnamese company earns **\$100 million** a year and trades on a frontier multiple of 10×, a market cap of **\$1.0 billion**. If the upgrade and the structurally larger, stickier investor base lift the fair multiple to 13× — a modest 3-turn re-rating, well within the frontier-to-emerging gap historically observed — the same earnings now support a **\$1.3 billion** market cap, a 30% gain *with no change in the underlying business*. The intuition: the upgrade pays twice — once through the one-off forced flow, and again, more durably, through a higher multiple on unchanged earnings.

## How the upgrade shows up in the real market and the data

It helps to ground the abstraction in Vietnam's actual price and economic record, because the upgrade story does not happen on a blank canvas — it happens in a market that has just lived through a violent regulatory cycle of its own.

![VN-Index from the 2022 crackdown trough to the recovery into the upgrade window](/imgs/blogs/vietnams-regulatory-reform-and-upgrade-catalysts-6.png)

The VN-Index chart tells the recent backdrop. The index peaked near **1,528** in early January 2022, then collapsed to a trough of **874** by mid-November 2022 as an anti-corruption crackdown on property and corporate-bond issuers froze the credit that had fuelled the boom — the episode covered in the Vietnam-stocks case study on the [2022 bond crisis](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/case-study-2022-bond-crisis-property-bank-contagion). The recovery to roughly **1,267** by end-2024 happened *alongside* the reform push, not by coincidence: a credible upgrade path is exactly the kind of structural story that helps a beaten-down frontier market re-rate. The point for the upgrade trade is that Vietnam enters the FTSE decision window having already absorbed a domestic shock, with valuations well off the highs — a more attractive entry than buying into euphoria.

The real economy gives the upgrade its foundation. The flow is a one-off; the *reason* a larger, stickier investor base will stay is that the underlying economy keeps compounding.

![Vietnam realized FDI from 2019 to 2024 rising into the upgrade decision](/imgs/blogs/vietnams-regulatory-reform-and-upgrade-catalysts-7.png)

Realized foreign direct investment (FDI) — money that actually got spent building factories, not just pledged — rose from about **\$20.4 billion** in 2019 to **\$25.4 billion** in 2024, climbing right through the COVID shock and the 2022 bond crisis. This is the structural case in one chart: global manufacturers keep choosing Vietnam, which means the export earnings, the corporate profits, and ultimately the listed companies the upgrade will showcase keep growing. The upgrade is the financial-market recognition of a real-economy trend that was already in motion. The macro channel — how FDI and the trade surplus feed the currency and the liquidity cycle — connects to the broader framework in [the liquidity cycle and asset prices](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-liquidity-cycle-and-asset-prices).

#### Worked example: the carry cost of pre-positioning a year early

The upgrade trade has a hidden cost: time. Suppose you buy the beneficiary basket twelve months before the expected effective date to be sure you are in front of the flow. Your money is now tied up in Vietnamese equities for a year. The opportunity cost is what that capital would have earned elsewhere — say a US Treasury bill at **4.5%** — plus the currency risk that the dong depreciates against the dollar (it has tended to slip 2–3% a year). On a **\$10 million** position, a year of foregone T-bill yield is `\$10,000,000 × 0.045 = \$450,000`, and a 2.5% dong depreciation is another `\$10,000,000 × 0.025 = \$250,000` of FX drag — a combined **\$700,000**, or 7% of the position, that the upgrade's re-rating must beat *just to break even on pre-positioning a year early*. The takeaway: being right too early is a real cost, so the calendar's job is to get you in as late as you safely can while still beating the crowd.

## Common misconceptions

The upgrade story attracts more confident wrong opinions than almost any topic in Vietnamese markets. Three deserve correcting with numbers.

**Misconception one: "an upgrade means tens of billions pour in overnight."** No. The *forced passive* bid on a FTSE Secondary-Emerging inclusion is roughly **\$5–8 billion**, and it arrives over a rebalancing window of weeks to months around the effective date, not in a single session. The much-larger active money is discretionary and largely front-runs the event. People conflate the gross emerging-market pool (trillions) with the slice that is both Vietnam-weighted *and* passive (single-digit billions) — a confusion of two numbers that differ by three orders of magnitude.

**Misconception two: "FTSE and MSCI are basically the same upgrade."** They are years apart and graded differently. FTSE's checklist-style process can clear Vietnam on the near-term reforms; MSCI's slower, more qualitative process weights large-investor experience and clearing safety heavily, and historically follows FTSE by **two to five years**. Treating a likely 2025–2026 FTSE move as if it delivers the much larger MSCI flows immediately overstates the near-term bid by a wide margin.

**Misconception three: "any big Vietnamese stock benefits."** Only stocks that are large, liquid, *and* have foreign room left get the full passive weight. A blue-chip already pinned at its foreign ownership limit has its index weight cut to what foreigners can actually own, so the incremental forced money skips it. Buying an out-of-room champion "for the upgrade" can mean owning exactly the stock the flow cannot reach.

**Misconception four: "the date is fixed."** The single most reliable feature of this upgrade has been *delay*. The KRX system alone slipped repeatedly across several years before going live. An upgrade "expected in autumn 2025" has a real tail of being pushed to 2026 or later if any criterion is judged not yet observed working in practice. The calendar is a schedule of *possible* catalysts, not a guarantee.

**Misconception five: "passing the law is the same as getting upgraded."** This is the error that most reliably costs investors money on timing. Index providers do not upgrade on legislation; they upgrade on *observed practice*. The non-prefunding model can be law, but FTSE wants to see foreign institutions actually trading under it cleanly across several review cycles before it counts the box as fully green. That observation lag routinely adds quarters or years *after* a reform lands. So the correct mental model is not "law passed, therefore upgrade imminent" but "law passed, therefore the clock on the observation period starts now." An investor who buys the moment a reform is announced and expects the flow within months is conflating the input (the rule) with the output (the verified, working market access), and will sit through a long, frustrating wait.

**Misconception six: "the whole market re-rates equally."** Even within the upgrade, the benefit is concentrated, not broad. The forced flow lands on the specific index constituents in proportion to their weight, and the re-rating accrues most to the large, liquid, room-available names that the new investor base can actually own. A small-cap that never enters the index gets neither the flow nor, usually, the multiple expansion — it may even *lag*, as domestic money rotates out of small-caps and into the blue-chips that foreigners are chasing. "Vietnam gets upgraded" is not the same as "every Vietnamese stock goes up"; the dispersion *within* the market around an upgrade is often as large as the move in the index itself.

## The history of delays and the slip risk

A sober trader treats this upgrade as a base case with a fat tail of postponement, because the track record demands it. Vietnam first went on FTSE's watchlist in **September 2018**. The KRX system was contracted years before it launched and slipped through 2021, 2022, 2023, and 2024 before finally going live in 2025. Each FTSE annual review from 2019 onward produced some version of "good progress, kept on watchlist, not yet." The non-prefunding fix that finally landed in 2024 had been discussed for years.

Why so slow? Three structural reasons. First, **sequencing**: as the mapping matrix showed, the reforms depend on each other, so a delay in one (the KRX system) blocks the next (deeper clearing). Second, **the gap between rule and practice**: index providers do not upgrade on a law being *passed*; they upgrade on the new mechanism being *observed working* through several review cycles, which adds quarters or years of lag after each reform. Third, **the soft criteria**: disclosure quality and the lived experience of foreign investors cannot be legislated into existence overnight, and these are exactly the MSCI-heavy items.

The slip risk is therefore not a vague worry; it is the central scenario to size against. The fact that the reforms now sit in *primary law* (the 2024 Securities Law) rather than reversible circulars genuinely reduces the risk of backsliding — but it does nothing to compress the *observation lag*. The honest framing: the *direction* is now high-confidence, the *timing* is not.

It helps to distinguish three kinds of bad news, because they are not equally damaging. The first and most common is a **roll-forward**: FTSE says "good progress, kept on watchlist, decision deferred." This is a delay, fully consistent with the base case, and it tends to produce a modest, temporary disappointment dip rather than a thesis-breaker — often a *better* entry for a patient investor. The second is a **partial pass**: an upgrade granted but on a reduced or phased weight because some criteria are only amber, which trims the flow (the haircut worked example quantified this). The third, and the only true thesis-breaker, is a **downgrade of the assessment** — FTSE moving a previously-green criterion *back* to amber or red, or explicitly removing Vietnam from the watchlist. That last one is rare and would signal a genuine regression in market access, not mere bureaucratic slowness. The discipline is to react to each kind of news in proportion: shrug at a roll-forward, re-size for a partial pass, and exit on a real downgrade.

A useful historical anchor for sizing the delay risk is the experience of other recent upgrades. When other frontier markets have made the FTSE-to-emerging jump, the gap between the *announcement* and the *effective* date has typically run six to twelve months, and the gap between a country first appearing on the watchlist and actually being upgraded has often run several years — sometimes with multiple deferrals along the way. Kuwait, for instance, spent years on watchlists before its upgrades by both FTSE and MSCI, and the moves, when they came, were followed by exactly the announcement-then-implementation flow pattern described above. Vietnam's path has been longer and bumpier than most, which is itself information: it argues for treating the optimistic timelines with skepticism and building a position that survives one more deferral without forcing a sale.

## How to trade it: the playbook

Everything above converges on a concrete plan. The upgrade is a rare structural catalyst with a knowable size and a fuzzy date, so the playbook is built to harvest the size while respecting the date risk.

**1. Build the catalyst calendar and watch the right dates.** Put the FTSE interim review (spring) and annual classification (autumn) on the calendar every year, plus any announced go-live dates for outstanding infrastructure (CCP clearing). The information arrives on those dates; everything in between is noise and positioning. Treat the *announcement* date and the *effective implementation* date as two separate events — the first reprices on expectations, the second delivers the forced flow.

**2. Position into milestones, not into hope.** Size exposure to your differentiated probability of the next catalyst, the way the position-clock worked example did. Add into evidence that a reform is *observed working* (foreign turnover rising after non-prefunding, KRX system running cleanly) and trim into pure date-speculation where you have no edge over consensus. The asymmetry you want is to own the basket when your upgrade probability exceeds the market's implied probability.

The hard part of step two is estimating the *market's* implied probability so you can tell whether you have an edge at all. There is no listed contract that prints it, so you back it out: if the beneficiary basket would rise roughly 15% on a confirmed upgrade and fall 5% on a deferral, and the basket has *already* rallied, say, 6% on upgrade chatter, then the market is implicitly assigning a probability that makes that 6% fair — somewhere around `(6% + 5%) ÷ (15% + 5%) ≈ 55%`. If your own read of the reform evidence is meaningfully above or below that implied 55%, you have an edge; if it is roughly equal, the move is already in the price and there is no trade. The point of the exercise is not precision — the inputs are rough — but discipline: it forces you to separate "I think the upgrade will happen" (probably true and probably already priced) from "I think it is *more* likely than the market does" (the only version that makes money).

**3. Pick the passive-flow beneficiaries deliberately.** Run every candidate through the three-filter tree: large enough to be in the index, liquid enough to clear the volume test, and *room still open* so the forced money can actually reach it. The concentrated list of large-liquid-with-room names is where the index effect is strongest. Avoid out-of-room champions bought "for the upgrade" — the flow cannot touch them.

**4. Respect the delay risk explicitly.** Assume the date can slip. That means: do not pre-position so early that carry and FX drag eat the re-rating (the year-early worked example showed a 7% hurdle), and do not use leverage that a six-to-twelve-month postponement would blow up. Size for a base case of "upgrade happens, but maybe a year later than the optimists think."

**5. Know what invalidates the view.** The thesis breaks if: a FTSE annual review explicitly downgrades the assessment rather than rolling it forward (a genuine reversal, not a delay); a reform already in law is walked back; the non-prefunding or KRX mechanics are seen to *fail* in practice rather than merely lag; or your beneficiary basket quietly runs out of foreign room before the effective date, gutting its index weight. Any of those is a reason to cut, not to average down. A mere "kept on watchlist again" is a delay, not an invalidation — annoying, but consistent with the base case.

**6. Hedge the macro and currency layer you do not have a view on.** The upgrade trade is a bet on a *legal and operational catalyst*, not on the dong or on the global emerging-market beta. But a long Vietnamese-equity basket carries both risks for free: a sharp dong depreciation erodes the dollar return, and a global emerging-market sell-off can swamp a country-specific re-rating. An investor who wants the *pure* upgrade exposure can think about hedging the dollar/dong and the broad emerging-market beta separately, isolating the idiosyncratic upgrade alpha. Most investors will not bother, but the ones running the trade in size should know that perhaps half the volatility of the naive position has nothing to do with the thesis. The interaction between Vietnam's currency, its credit ceiling, and the flow backdrop connects to [Vietnam's monetary policy framework](/blog/trading/finance/vietnam-monetary-policy-state-bank-dong-credit-ceiling) and to the broader [liquidity cycle](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-liquidity-cycle-and-asset-prices).

**7. Plan the exit before the entry.** Because the flow is a one-off and the re-rating is the durable part, the two legs of the trade have different natural holding periods. The flow-capture portion should be largely unwound into and just after the effective date, when the forced buyers are done and the "sell the news" risk is highest. The re-rating portion — the bet on a permanently higher multiple supported by a larger, stickier investor base and a compounding economy — is a multi-year hold that should only be cut if the structural story (FDI, earnings growth, the upgrade itself) deteriorates. Deciding in advance which dollars are "event" and which are "structural" is what stops an investor from holding the whole position too long out of greed or selling it all too early out of fear.

The deeper point, and the reason this fits the series' spine, is that an index upgrade is one of the cleanest cases anywhere of *law and operational rule-making mechanically creating forced buyers*. A circular on settlement, a new matching engine, an amended statute — each is a dry administrative act, and each is also a datable catalyst that, when it clears the last graded blocker, summons billions of dollars that *have* to be spent. The investor who reads the rulebook as a trading calendar, sizes the flows with arithmetic rather than headlines, and buys the stocks the forced money can actually reach, is positioned for a repricing that the rules themselves are bringing into being.
