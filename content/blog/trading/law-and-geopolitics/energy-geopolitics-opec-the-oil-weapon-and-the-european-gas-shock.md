On 24 February 2022, Russian forces crossed into Ukraine. Within hours, a barrel of Brent crude — the global oil benchmark — jumped from about \$97 to over \$99, and by 8 March it had spiked to nearly \$128, a level not seen since 2008. European natural gas, priced at the Dutch TTF hub, went on a wilder ride still: from a pre-crisis normal near €25 per megawatt-hour to a closing peak of €339 on 26 August 2022 — roughly a thirteen-fold increase. Aluminum smelters in Slovakia and fertilizer plants in Germany switched off because the gas to run them cost more than the product they could sell. And across the Atlantic, in a country that was a net energy exporter and barely touched physically by the war, the price of filling a pickup truck hit a record, the inflation rate ran to a 40-year high, and the Federal Reserve embarked on its fastest interest-rate hiking cycle in four decades.

That chain — a geopolitical decision in one place, a commodity price spike, an inflation impulse, a policy response, and a repricing of stocks and bonds everywhere — is the single most important pattern in energy geopolitics. Energy is where geopolitics hits the real economy hardest, because oil and gas are not ordinary goods you can choose to skip when they get expensive. They are inputs to almost everything: the food you eat (diesel for tractors, gas for fertilizer), the goods you buy (energy in the factory and the freight), the commute you can't avoid. When their price rises, it acts like a tax on growth — money leaves consumers' and firms' pockets and flows to whoever controls the barrels. And a small group of producers, organized as a cartel, can turn that tax up or down on purpose.

This post builds the whole picture from zero. We will define oil supply and demand and why a small change in supply moves the price a lot; the cartel (OPEC and OPEC+), its quotas, its spare capacity, and the swing producer; the strategic reserves governments hold; how a higher oil price transmits into inflation and then into policy; and how natural gas — a regional, pipeline-bound market — behaves differently from oil. Then we go deep on the four episodes that define the field: the 1973 embargo (the original oil weapon), OPEC+ production decisions as a tradeable event, the 2022 European gas shock, and the recurring pattern of oil spikes preceding recessions. Throughout, the lens is analytical and politically neutral: the goal is to understand how an energy supply decision reprices markets, not to take any side.

> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Energy is where geopolitics hits the real economy hardest, because oil and gas are a tax on growth that a cartel can turn up or down; a supply decision made for political reasons reprices every asset through inflation, policy, and growth.
>
> - **Oil and gas demand is inelastic in the short run** — you can't quickly stop driving or heating — so a roughly 3% supply cut can lift the price ~30–40%. That is the lever the cartel pulls.
> - **OPEC+ is a cartel** that sets output quotas; its spare capacity makes it the swing producer, but US shale and demand destruction cap how far it can push.
> - **A higher oil price is an inflation impulse and a tax on growth**: a rough rule is that a sustained +\$10/bbl adds roughly 0.2 percentage points to headline US CPI and shaves real income, which is why almost every postwar US recession was preceded by an oil spike.
> - **The 2022 European gas shock** (TTF €25 → €339/MWh, ~13×) and the **1973 oil embargo** (oil ~4×) are the same mechanism — cut a key supply for political ends — applied to a different commodity through a different chokepoint.
> - **The one number to remember: €339/MWh** — the August 2022 European gas peak, the price at which a continent's industry idled and governments spent hundreds of billions to keep the lights on.

## Foundations: oil, gas, the cartel, and the transmission channel

Start with the commodity itself, because the strange behavior of energy prices comes from one fact about how people use them.

### Why small supply changes move the price a lot

Economists describe how much the quantity people buy responds to price with a number called **elasticity**. If a 1% rise in price causes demand to fall by more than 1%, demand is *elastic* (people cut back a lot); if it falls by less than 1%, demand is *inelastic* (people keep buying). Oil and gas demand is highly inelastic in the short run. If gasoline jumps 20% overnight, you still have to get to work, the airline still has flights booked, the trucking company still has freight to move, and the German factory still needs gas in its furnace this winter. People grumble and pay. Over years they buy a more efficient car or insulate the house, but this week and this quarter, demand barely moves.

That inelasticity is the source of the whole drama. When supply and demand are both steep — buyers don't quit and the cut barrels can't be quickly replaced — the price where they cross has to move a long way to clear the market. A small notch out of supply produces a large jump in price. The figure makes the geometry concrete.

![Steep inelastic oil demand means a small supply cut produces a large price jump](/imgs/blogs/energy-geopolitics-opec-the-oil-weapon-and-the-european-gas-shock-9.png)

#### Worked example: a 3% supply cut and a transport company's fuel bill

Take a regional trucking firm that burns 1,000,000 gallons of diesel a year. At a diesel price of \$3.50/gallon, its annual fuel bill is `1,000,000 × $3.50 = $3,500,000`. Now a producer group removes roughly 3% of barrels from the market. Because demand is inelastic, that 3% supply cut does not lower the price by 3% — the market has to bid the price up by something like 35% to ration the now-scarcer supply. Diesel rises to about `$3.50 × 1.35 = $4.73/gallon`. The firm's fuel bill becomes `1,000,000 × $4.73 = $4,730,000`. The fuel bill rose `$1,230,000`, or 35%, in response to a 3% supply cut. If the firm's operating margin was \$2,000,000, more than half of its profit just evaporated — and it cannot quickly drive fewer miles. The core idea to keep: inelastic demand turns a small supply lever into a large price and profit swing, which is exactly why a cartel bothers to cut at all.

#### Worked example: an oil spike's hit to a household's budget

Now take the same supply cut from the household side, because the consumer hit is what ultimately drives the macro chain. A typical commuting household drives 20,000 miles a year in a vehicle that does 25 miles per gallon, so it burns `20,000 / 25 = 800` gallons of gasoline a year. Say gasoline tracks the same move as diesel and rises from \$3.50 to \$4.73/gallon. The household's annual fuel cost goes from `800 × $3.50 = $2,800` to `800 × $4.73 = $3,784` — an extra `$984` a year, or about \$82 a month. For a household earning \$60,000 a year with little slack, that \$984 does not come out of savings; it comes out of *other spending* — fewer restaurant meals, a delayed appliance purchase, one less trip. Multiply that across a hundred million households and the "tax on growth" becomes concrete: roughly `100,000,000 × $984 = $98.4 billion` of spending diverted from the rest of the economy into fuel, in a single year, from one supply cut. The core idea: an oil spike does not just raise one line item — it quietly drains discretionary spending from the entire consumer economy, which is the first link in the recession chain.

### OPEC, OPEC+, quotas, and spare capacity

A single producer who controls a chunk of world supply can exploit that lever. **OPEC** — the Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries, founded in 1960 — is a group of oil-exporting nations (Saudi Arabia, Iraq, the UAE, Kuwait, Iran, Nigeria, Venezuela, and others) that coordinate output. A group of sellers who agree to restrict supply to lift the price is, in economic terms, a **cartel**. Since 2016, OPEC has coordinated with a wider group including Russia and several others under the banner **OPEC+** (about 23 countries in total). Together they control on the order of 40% of world crude production and a much larger share of the oil that is actually traded across borders.

The cartel works through **quotas** — each member is assigned a production ceiling, and the group as a whole agrees to add or remove barrels. Two concepts make the cartel powerful:

- **Spare capacity.** This is oil a producer *could* pump but is deliberately holding back. Saudi Arabia is the key holder — it can bring a few million barrels per day on or off within weeks. Spare capacity is what lets the group act as the **swing producer**: the one who can move the global balance at will, the way a central bank moves the price of money. A producer with no spare capacity is a price-taker; a producer with spare capacity is a price-maker.
- **The free-rider problem.** Every cartel is unstable because each member is tempted to cheat — to quietly pump above its quota and sell at the high price the others' discipline created. Cartels hold together only when a dominant member (here, Saudi Arabia) is willing to cut the most and police the rest. When discipline breaks, the price collapses, as it did in the 2014–2016 and the brief March 2020 price wars.

### The strategic petroleum reserve

Governments that import oil keep an emergency stockpile called a **strategic petroleum reserve** (SPR). The US SPR, stored in salt caverns on the Gulf Coast, was built after the 1973 embargo precisely so the country would have a buffer against a future supply weapon. When prices spike, a government can *release* barrels from the reserve to add supply and lean against the price — as the US did in 2022, releasing about 180 million barrels over six months, the largest drawdown in the reserve's history. The SPR is a finite, one-time tool: it can blunt a spike, but it cannot replace lost production indefinitely, and a depleted reserve must eventually be refilled (which is itself buying that supports the price later).

### How an oil price rise transmits into the economy

Now connect the commodity to the macro economy and to policy. A higher oil price hits in three layers, and the master chain figure above traced all three:

1. **A tax on consumers.** Households spend a fairly fixed dollar amount on fuel and energy each month; when the price rises, the extra dollars come out of everything else. Spending on restaurants, retail, and travel falls. The economist's phrase is that an oil spike is a *transfer* from oil-consuming countries to oil-producing ones, and within a country from drivers to producers — money leaves the broad economy's pocket.
2. **A margin hit to energy-using firms.** Airlines, trucking, chemicals, plastics, fertilizer, and manufacturing all use energy as a direct input. Higher energy raises their costs; whether they can pass it through to customers determines whether their profit margin shrinks. Airlines, where fuel is 20–30% of operating cost, are the textbook casualty.
3. **An inflation impulse that tightens policy.** Energy is a direct line in the consumer price index (CPI), and it also feeds through into the price of everything that uses energy. A jump in oil and gas therefore raises measured inflation. Central banks, whose job is price stability, respond to higher inflation by raising interest rates — which slows the economy further. This is the link that turns an energy shock into a market-wide event, because interest rates are the master price that discounts every asset. (For the full mechanics of how a central bank moves rates, see the cross-link at the end.)

### Natural gas: a different, more regional market

Oil is a global market: a barrel is a barrel, it travels cheaply by tanker, and one world price (give or take quality and freight) prevails. **Natural gas is not.** Gas is far harder to move — historically it traveled only by pipeline, which physically ties a buyer to whichever supplier the pipe connects to. That makes gas a set of *regional* markets with very different prices: US gas (priced at the Henry Hub), European gas (the Dutch TTF hub), and Asian gas can trade at wildly different levels at the same moment, because the gas physically cannot flow between them fast enough to equalize.

The technology that partially bridges these regions is **liquefied natural gas (LNG)** — gas chilled to about −162°C until it becomes a liquid roughly 1/600th of its gaseous volume, loaded onto specialized tankers, shipped across oceans, and re-gasified at an import terminal. LNG is what lets a buyer who lost a pipeline supplier go shopping on the world market. But LNG is expensive, capacity is limited and slow to build, and in a scramble the price gets bid up violently — which is exactly the story of Europe in 2022. The regional, pipeline-bound nature of gas is why a supply cut to one region (Europe losing Russian pipe) can produce a far more extreme local price move than an oil shock, which the whole world shares.

### Chokepoints: where geography becomes leverage

There is one more structural fact that turns energy into a geopolitical weapon: the world's oil and gas do not just come from a few places — they travel through a few narrow places. A **chokepoint** is a strait, canal, or pipeline through which a large share of energy must pass, and where a disruption would cut supply to many buyers at once. The most important are the **Strait of Hormuz** (through which roughly a fifth of the world's oil passes, between the Persian Gulf and the open ocean), the **Strait of Malacca** (the main route from the Middle East to East Asia), the **Suez Canal** and the adjoining Bab-el-Mandeb strait (the short route from the Gulf to Europe), and the major export pipelines. A handful of these chokepoints carry a majority of internationally traded oil.

Chokepoints matter for two reasons. First, they concentrate risk: a single incident — a tanker attack, a blockade, a closed strait — threatens not one buyer's supply but a whole region's, so the price spikes globally. Second, they explain *why* energy and geopolitics are inseparable. The oil and gas are concentrated in politically volatile regions, and the routes out of those regions are narrow and contested. A geopolitical flare-up near a chokepoint is, almost by definition, a threat to a large slice of world energy supply — which is why the market reprices oil on the *risk* of a disruption long before any barrels are actually lost. This forward-pricing of a feared supply loss is the **geopolitical risk premium** in the oil price, and it is the subject of a dedicated post linked at the end.

### Spare capacity and the shape of the futures curve

One last piece of plumbing makes OPEC+ readable in real time. Oil does not trade at a single price; it trades as a *curve* of futures contracts for delivery in each future month. The shape of that curve carries information. When near-term prices are higher than later-dated ones — a shape called **backwardation** — it signals that the market is short of oil *right now* (buyers will pay a premium for immediate delivery), which is the classic fingerprint of a supply squeeze or a successful cartel cut. When later-dated prices are higher — **contango** — it signals ample current supply and an expectation of higher prices later, the fingerprint of a glut. A practitioner reads the curve's shape, alongside the level of OPEC+ spare capacity, to judge how tight the market actually is and how much room the cartel has to push. A market in steep backwardation with little spare capacity is one supply scare away from a violent spike; a market in contango with ample spare capacity can absorb a shock with barely a ripple.

With the foundations in place, we can read the four episodes that define energy geopolitics.

## The 1973 embargo: the original oil weapon

The phrase "the oil weapon" is not a turn of phrase invented by analysts; it is what the relevant ministers called it at the time. In October 1973, during the Yom Kippur War, the Arab members of OPEC (organized as OAPEC) announced an **embargo** — a ban on oil exports — against the United States and several other countries seen as supporting Israel, and simultaneously cut overall production. The point was explicitly political: to use control of supply as leverage over the foreign policy of the importing countries.

The economic result was a textbook demonstration of inelastic demand meeting a supply cut. The posted price of oil went from about \$3 per barrel to about \$12 within months — roughly a fourfold increase. Because the industrial world had built its post-war prosperity on cheap oil and had no quick substitute, the shock rippled into everything: gasoline rationing and lines at US filling stations, a surge in inflation, and a sharp recession across the OECD. It also produced the worst macroeconomic combination of all — **stagflation**, the simultaneous occurrence of stagnant growth (or recession) and high inflation, which the standard policy playbook of the time had no good answer for.

Half a century later, the 2022 European gas shock would run the exact same script with a different commodity. The figure puts the two side by side, because seeing them together is the fastest way to understand that "the oil weapon" is a *mechanism*, not a one-time historical event.

![Before and after comparison of the 1973 oil embargo and the 2022 European gas shock](/imgs/blogs/energy-geopolitics-opec-the-oil-weapon-and-the-european-gas-shock-4.png)

Both episodes share the structure: a critical, inelastic-demand fuel; a supplier with the power to cut it; a political motive for the cut; a violent price spike; demand destruction and shortage; and an inflation-and-recession aftermath that forced central banks to tighten into weakness. What differs is the commodity (oil in 1973, gas in 2022) and the chokepoint (an export embargo then, a pipeline cut now). Three durable lessons came out of 1973, and each still governs how markets price an energy shock:

- **Control of a critical, inelastic-demand commodity is geopolitical power.** A producer who can cut supply can impose real economic pain on an importer. This is the template every later energy confrontation has followed.
- **The importer's defenses were built in response.** The US created the Strategic Petroleum Reserve, founded the International Energy Agency (a coordinating body for consumer countries with shared emergency reserves), and began the long push toward energy efficiency and supply diversification. Defenses against the weapon are themselves a market force — they cap how much the weapon can be used.
- **An energy shock can break the policy regime, not just dent it.** The stagflation of the 1970s eventually forced the Volcker-era decision to raise US interest rates to nearly 20% to kill inflation, a regime change that reset the price of every asset. (See the cross-link on the Volcker rate shock.)

#### Worked example: the inflation impulse — a +\$40/bbl oil rise into CPI and the rate repricing

Take a sustained oil price rise of +\$40 per barrel — roughly the move from \$80 to \$120, on the order of what 1973 and 2022 delivered in real terms. A widely used rule of thumb among macro economists is that a sustained +\$10/bbl in oil adds very roughly 0.2 percentage points to headline US CPI inflation over the following year (through gasoline, heating, and the energy embedded in other goods). So a +\$40 move implies a rough inflation impulse of `4 × 0.2 = +0.8 percentage points` on headline CPI. Now translate that into policy. If a central bank follows a reaction function in which it raises its policy rate by about 1.5 percentage points for every 1 point of excess inflation (a stylized Taylor-rule slope), then +0.8 points of inflation argues for roughly `0.8 × 1.5 = +1.2 percentage points` of additional rate hikes. A bond priced off that policy rate reprices accordingly: a 10-year note with a duration near 8 falls about `8 × 1.2% = 9.6%` in price for that rate move, all else equal. The intuition to carry: an oil spike is not just a gas-pump story — it is an inflation impulse that, through the central bank's reaction function, reprices the entire bond market and everything discounted off it.

## OPEC+ production decisions as a tradeable event

The 1973 embargo was a one-off political act. The everyday version of the oil weapon is the routine **OPEC+ production decision** — the group's regular meetings (historically monthly or every few weeks) at which it decides to cut, hold, or raise output. Because the cartel is the swing producer, this decision directly moves the global supply balance, and because oil is so widely held and traded, the decision is one of the most reliably market-moving scheduled events in commodities. For a trader, an OPEC+ meeting is a binary-ish event with a known date and a small set of outcomes — the structure of a tradeable catalyst.

The decision tree is simple, and its branches price differently.

![Decision tree of an OPEC plus meeting branching into cut, hold, or raise and the market reaction](/imgs/blogs/energy-geopolitics-opec-the-oil-weapon-and-the-european-gas-shock-2.png)

- **Cut** (remove barrels): tightens supply, pushes the price up. Bullish for oil and for energy producers; bearish for energy-users (airlines, consumers) and, if large enough, an inflation impulse that is bearish for rate-sensitive assets.
- **Hold** (no change): the price drifts on demand and inventory data; the reaction depends entirely on what the market had already priced in.
- **Raise** (add barrels): loosens supply, pushes the price down. The mirror image — bearish for producers, a relief for users and for inflation.

The crucial point for trading is that **the market trades the surprise, not the headline.** If the group cuts by 1 million barrels per day but the market had already priced in a 1.5 million cut, oil can *fall* on a cut, because the news was less bullish than expected. The practitioner's job before an OPEC+ meeting is to know what is in the price — the consensus expectation embedded in the futures curve and in analyst notes — and to position for the deviation from it. This is identical in structure to trading a central-bank rate decision or an economic data release; the mechanics of trading a scheduled event are covered in the event-driven cross-links at the end.

The cartel's history is a cycle of discipline and breakdown, and reading where the group sits in that cycle is half the work. Two episodes are the templates:

- **The 2014–2016 price war.** Faced with surging US shale supply, Saudi Arabia made the strategic choice in late 2014 *not* to cut — to instead flood the market and let the low price drive high-cost shale producers out of business. Oil fell from over \$100 in mid-2014 to under \$30 by early 2016. The strategy partly worked (many shale firms went bankrupt) but partly failed: the survivors got leaner and more efficient, and US production recovered fast once prices did. The lesson the cartel drew was that it could not simply out-produce shale, which led directly to the formation of OPEC+ (adding Russia) in 2016 to broaden the coalition that manages supply.
- **The March 2020 price war.** As COVID-19 demolished oil demand, a disagreement between Saudi Arabia and Russia over who should cut briefly blew up the alliance; Saudi Arabia opened the taps, and with demand collapsing, the front-month US oil contract famously traded *negative* (below −\$37/bbl) in April 2020, as holders paid to avoid taking physical delivery into full storage. The alliance was quickly repaired with a record coordinated cut. The lesson: when discipline cracks at the same moment demand collapses, the price can do things that look impossible.

The investable read: a cohesive, disciplined cartel with low spare capacity is a floor under the price and a source of upside spikes; a fracturing cartel, or one with abundant spare capacity, is a ceiling and a source of downside. Knowing which regime you are in is the difference between fading an OPEC headline and chasing it.

#### Worked example: an energy producer's profit leverage to a higher oil price

Take an oil producer whose all-in cost to lift and deliver a barrel is \$45. At an oil price of \$70, its profit per barrel is `$70 − $45 = $25`. Now an OPEC+ cut lifts oil to \$90 — a `($90 − $70) / $70 = 28.6%` rise in the oil price. The producer's profit per barrel jumps to `$90 − $45 = $45` — a `($45 − $25) / $25 = 80%` rise in profit. A 28.6% move in the commodity produced an 80% move in profit. This amplification is **operating leverage**: because the cost per barrel is roughly fixed, every extra dollar of price drops almost straight to the bottom line. This is why energy-producer equities are so violently sensitive to the oil price, and why "long energy producers" is the cleanest expression of a bullish oil view. The intuition: a fixed-cost business turns a moderate commodity move into a large earnings move, in both directions.

## The 2022 European gas shock

The most extreme energy-geopolitics event of the modern era was not about oil at all. It was Europe's natural gas crisis of 2022 — and it is the perfect case study because it shows the oil weapon's mechanics applied to gas, where the regional, pipeline-bound structure makes the price move far more violent than oil ever does.

### The set-up: a continent wired to one supplier

Before 2022, Europe imported roughly 40% of its natural gas from Russia, much of it through fixed pipelines. Gas is the marginal fuel for European electricity (the last plant dispatched to meet peak demand is often a gas plant, so the gas price sets the power price) and the direct input for heating and for industries like fertilizer, glass, steel, and chemicals. A continent had built its industrial base and its heating on a supply that ran through pipes controlled by a single geopolitical counterparty. The dependence was a latent vulnerability — exactly the kind of chokepoint that turns an ordinary commodity into a weapon.

When that supply was throttled and then largely cut through 2022, Europe could not simply buy the missing gas from somewhere else at the old price. The pipes from Russia were the cheap, fixed route; replacing that volume meant bidding on the *global* LNG market against Asian buyers, and LNG capacity is finite and slow to build. The result was a price explosion.

![Line chart of European TTF natural gas rising from about 25 to 339 euros per megawatt hour in 2022](/imgs/blogs/energy-geopolitics-opec-the-oil-weapon-and-the-european-gas-shock-6.png)

The Dutch TTF benchmark — the European gas hub price — ran from a pre-crisis normal near €25/MWh to a closing peak of €339/MWh on 26 August 2022, roughly a thirteen-fold increase. For comparison, the 2022 oil spike — the same event, the same week — was "only" a move from the high \$70s to about \$128.

![Brent crude rising from about 97 to 128 dollars per barrel around the February 2022 invasion](/imgs/blogs/energy-geopolitics-opec-the-oil-weapon-and-the-european-gas-shock-3.png)

Brent went from \$96.84 on 23 February to \$99.08 the day of the invasion and peaked near \$128 on 8 March — a roughly 60% move from the start of the year, severe but ordinary next to gas. The difference is precisely the regional, un-arbitrageable nature of gas: oil is one global market that the whole world shares the pain of, while gas is a regional market where a single continent can be left stranded. The world could not ship Europe out of its hole fast enough, so the entire shortfall landed in the European price.

### The response: demand destruction, shutdowns, and the LNG scramble

A price that high does what no policy can: it forces **demand destruction** — the involuntary cut in consumption when the price gets so high that buyers physically stop. Across Europe in 2022:

- Energy-intensive industry idled. Roughly half of Europe's aluminum and zinc smelting capacity went offline; fertilizer producers cut output (which fed into food prices); chemical and glass plants throttled back. When the gas to run a furnace costs more than the product is worth, the rational move is to switch off — and Europe's industrial output fell accordingly.
- Governments spent on a vast scale to shield households and firms — on the order of several hundred billion euros across the EU in energy support, price caps, and bailouts of utilities caught between fixed customer prices and exploding wholesale costs.
- Europe scrambled for alternatives: it filled storage early, ramped Norwegian pipeline gas, built floating LNG import terminals (FSRUs) in months rather than years, signed long-term LNG contracts with the US and Qatar, and ran a successful campaign to cut gas demand by roughly 15%. By the winter of 2022–2023 — helped by mild weather — the price had fallen back below €100 and then below €40.

The rerouting of an entire continent's gas supply is worth seeing as a map.

![Map of Europe replacing Russian pipeline gas with Norwegian pipe and US and Qatari LNG plus demand cuts](/imgs/blogs/energy-geopolitics-opec-the-oil-weapon-and-the-european-gas-shock-7.png)

### The hidden financial plumbing stress

The 2022 gas shock also produced a less visible but dangerous second-order effect that is worth understanding, because it shows how an energy spike can threaten the financial system itself. European utilities and energy traders routinely **hedge** their future sales by selling gas and power forward on exchanges — locking in a price today for delivery later. To trade on an exchange you must post **margin** (collateral) that scales with the price; when the price moves against your position, you get a *margin call* demanding more cash, immediately. When gas and power prices went up tenfold, the margin demanded against these perfectly ordinary hedges exploded into the hundreds of billions of euros — even though the hedges were doing their job. Utilities that were economically fine faced a liquidity crisis: they owed enormous collateral *now* against gains they would only realize *later*. Several governments had to extend emergency credit lines to keep solvent companies from failing for lack of short-term cash. The lesson for a market practitioner: a large enough energy price move does not just hurt the obvious losers — it can seize up the financial plumbing through the collateral system, the same way a sudden move in any market can trigger a margin spiral.

The lasting lesson of 2022: a chokepoint dependence (one supplier, one fixed route, for an inelastic input) is a tail risk that sits quietly in the price until it detonates. The repricing when it detonates is enormous because the gap cannot be quickly arbitraged away — and the shock can travel into rates, currencies, equities, and even the collateral plumbing all at once.

#### Worked example: a European utility's cost when TTF goes from €25 to €339/MWh

Take a European utility (or a large industrial gas buyer) that consumes 5 TWh of gas a year — 5,000,000 MWh. At the pre-crisis price of €25/MWh, its annual gas cost is `5,000,000 × €25 = €125,000,000`. At the August 2022 peak of €339/MWh, the same volume costs `5,000,000 × €339 = €1,695,000,000`. The cost rose by `€1,695,000,000 − €125,000,000 = €1,570,000,000` — a thirteen-fold increase, or +1,256%, for buying the identical molecules of gas. Anchoring in dollars at roughly \$1.05/€, that is a cost increase of about `€1,570,000,000 × 1.05 = $1,648,500,000` — over \$1.6 billion of extra cost on one mid-sized buyer. A utility selling power to households at regulated, fixed prices simply cannot absorb that and is forced into either demand destruction or a government bailout. The intuition: when an inelastic input reprices 13×, no balance sheet downstream is large enough to absorb it without the state stepping in.

## The oil-price-as-recession-trigger pattern

Step back from any single episode and look at the recurring shape of energy shocks across the whole postwar record. One of the most robust empirical regularities in macroeconomics is that **almost every US recession was preceded by a sharp rise in the oil price.** The economist James Hamilton documented that of the postwar US recessions, the large majority were preceded within a year or so by a significant oil price spike. The logic is exactly the transmission chain we built: an oil spike is a tax on consumers and a margin hit to firms, it forces tighter policy to fight the inflation impulse, and the combination of the income hit and the rate hikes tips a slowing economy into contraction.

![Timeline of oil price spikes preceding US recessions in 1973, 1979, 1990, 2008, and 2022](/imgs/blogs/energy-geopolitics-opec-the-oil-weapon-and-the-european-gas-shock-8.png)

The pattern is not a perfect predictor — not every oil spike is followed by a recession, and other forces matter — but the asymmetry is striking enough to treat a large, sustained energy price rise as a recession warning. Two refinements make it usable:

- **It is the *change*, not the *level*, that bites.** A doubling of oil over a few months is a shock the economy has to absorb; a high but stable price is something it adapts to. The recession signal is in the rate of change.
- **The mechanism runs through policy.** The reason an oil spike is so dangerous is that it hits when central banks are least able to help: with inflation already rising, the bank cannot cut rates to support growth — it must raise them to fight inflation, the worst possible policy posture for a fragile economy. This is why an energy shock that arrives in a high-inflation environment (2022) is more dangerous than one that arrives in a deflationary one.

The geopolitical context that produces these spikes is captured by indices that try to measure it. The Caldara-Iacoviello Geopolitical Risk (GPR) index — which counts mentions of geopolitical tensions in newspapers — spikes at exactly the moments that have moved energy markets.

![Bar chart of the Geopolitical Risk index at events from the 9-11 attacks to the 2022 Ukraine invasion](/imgs/blogs/energy-geopolitics-opec-the-oil-weapon-and-the-european-gas-shock-10.png)

The GPR index read 277 at the February 2022 invasion (versus a long-run mean of 100) and 512 after the September 2001 attacks. The reason geopolitical risk and energy are so tightly linked is that the world's oil and gas are concentrated in a handful of politically volatile regions and travel through a small number of chokepoints (the Strait of Hormuz, the Suez Canal, key pipelines), so a geopolitical flare-up is, more often than not, a threat to energy supply. (The geopolitical-risk-premium channel — why most shocks fade and which don't — is its own post; see the cross-link.)

A practical caveat keeps this from becoming a doomsday reflex: the *base rate* matters. Most geopolitical scares that spike the GPR index do *not* end in a sustained energy shock or a recession — the feared disruption fails to materialize, the chokepoint stays open, the cartel does not cut, and the premium decays within weeks. The 2024 reading of 140, well off the 2022 peak, is the normal pattern: elevated but fading. The skill is to distinguish a passing scare (fade the premium) from a genuine regime change (a sustained supply loss that resets the price level), because the two look identical in the first 48 hours and diverge completely thereafter. A useful filter: a scare that does not actually remove barrels from the market is a fade; a shock that physically and durably cuts supply — an embargo, a pipeline destroyed, a major producer's exports sanctioned — is the real thing.

## The energy-transition tension

A newer force now sits on top of all of this, and it cuts in a direction that surprises many people. The transition away from fossil fuels — driven by climate policy, investor pressure, and the falling cost of renewables — has reduced investment in new oil and gas supply. Capital that once went into exploring and developing new fields has been redirected or withheld, partly because producers fear that a field developed today may become a *stranded asset* (an oil field or a coal plant that cannot be economically used because demand or policy moved against it) before it pays back.

The tension is this: demand for oil and gas has not fallen as fast as supply investment has. The world still runs largely on fossil fuels, and will for years, even as it builds the alternatives. So we get a structural setup in which **supply is constrained by underinvestment while demand is still robust** — which is a recipe for sharper price spikes, not gentler ones, during the transition. An energy system that is being deliberately starved of new fossil supply, but not yet weaned off fossil demand, is more prone to violent price moves than either the old fossil-heavy system or a future fully-renewable one. The 2021–2022 European gas crunch had this underinvestment as part of its backdrop, not just the war.

There is a self-reinforcing dynamic here worth spelling out. Oil and gas fields deplete: an existing field's output falls a few percent a year, so the industry must continually invest just to *stand still*. If that sustaining investment is cut — because capital markets demand it, because regulation discourages it, or because producers fear stranded assets — then supply quietly tightens year after year even with flat drilling, while demand has not yet rolled over. The gap widens until a shock (a war, a cartel cut, a cold winter) exposes how thin the supply cushion has become, and the price spikes hard. The very policies intended to accelerate the transition can, in the messy middle, raise the volatility and the average level of fossil prices by starving supply faster than they curb demand.

For an investor, the energy-transition tension means two things can both be true at once: the long-run direction is away from fossil fuels, *and* the transition period is one of elevated fossil-fuel price volatility and occasional violent spikes. Betting that energy is "structurally dead" because of the transition has been a recurringly painful trade — the energy sector was among the best-performing parts of the equity market in 2021–2022 precisely while the transition narrative was loudest. The practitioner's posture is to hold the long-run direction and the near-term volatility in the same hand: own some exposure to the spikes (energy producers, real assets) as a hedge against the transition being slower and bumpier than the consensus assumes, while recognizing the terminal direction. (The mechanics of carbon pricing and transition subsidies are a separate post; see the cross-link on energy-and-climate regulation.)

## Common misconceptions

Energy geopolitics is fertile ground for confident, wrong beliefs. Three are worth correcting with numbers.

**Misconception 1: "OPEC controls the oil price."** OPEC+ is powerful — it is the swing producer and can move the balance — but it does not *control* the price, for two hard reasons. First, **spare capacity is finite**: the group can cut, but if it cuts too hard, the high price calls forth more supply from outside the cartel. Second, **US shale changed the game**. Between roughly 2008 and 2019, US crude production roughly doubled (from about 5 million barrels per day to over 12 million), turning the US into the world's largest producer and a fast-responding source of supply: when prices rise, shale drillers add rigs within months and bring on barrels that undercut the cartel's cut. This is why OPEC+'s 2014 attempt to crush shale by *flooding* the market failed to kill it permanently, and why the cartel's pricing power has real ceilings. The price is set by global supply and demand; OPEC+ is the largest single influence on it, not its master.

**Misconception 2: "High oil always helps energy stocks."** Mostly true — but with an important limit called **demand destruction**. If the oil price rises because of strong demand (a booming economy), energy stocks do well and so does the broad market. But if the price rises because of a supply shock severe enough to *cause a recession*, the recession then crushes oil demand, and the oil price — and energy stocks — can collapse. The clearest case is 2008: oil ran to about \$147/bbl in July, then the financial crisis and recession demolished demand and oil fell to about \$35 by December, a roughly 75% crash, taking energy equities down with it. So the relationship is "high oil helps energy stocks *until the oil price is high enough to break growth*," at which point both reverse. A bullish-oil trade has a built-in self-destruct if it succeeds too well.

**Misconception 3: "Gas and oil are one market."** They are not, and 2022 proved it definitively. Oil is a global, easily-shipped commodity with one world price; gas is a set of regional, pipeline-bound markets that can diverge enormously. In 2022, European TTF gas rose ~13× to €339/MWh while Brent oil rose ~60%. At the same moment, US Henry Hub gas — insulated by domestic supply and limited LNG export capacity — was a small fraction of the European price. Treating "energy" as one homogeneous bet ignores that a gas shock can devastate Europe while barely touching US gas, and that oil and gas can move by completely different magnitudes off the same geopolitical event. The investable consequence: you must specify *which* energy and *which* region.

## How it shows up in real markets

The transmission chain is not a textbook theory; it is a sequence of observable repricings. Four patterns recur.

**The OPEC-cut spike.** When OPEC+ surprises with a larger-than-expected cut, the front-month oil contract jumps within seconds of the headline, energy-producer equities rally, airline and transport stocks fall, and — if the cut is large enough to threaten the inflation outlook — bond yields rise as the market prices a tougher central bank. The 2022 record release from the US Strategic Petroleum Reserve was a direct policy counter to exactly this dynamic.

**The 2022 gas blowout.** European utility and energy-intensive industrial stocks fell or required state support; the euro weakened (a continent paying far more for imported energy runs a worse trade balance); European inflation surged, forcing the European Central Bank into rate hikes after years near zero; and the price relationship between US and European gas blew apart, making US LNG exporters and any company with spare LNG cargoes hugely profitable. One geopolitical decision repriced currencies, rates, equities, and the relative value of two continents' gas at once.

**The oil-led inflation and rate repricing.** In 2022, the energy spike was a major component of the inflation surge that drove the Federal Reserve's fastest hiking cycle in 40 years — from near zero to over 5%. The whole bond market repriced, growth and technology stocks (which are most sensitive to the discount rate) fell hard, and value and energy sectors outperformed. This is the cleanest real-world example of the master chain: a war → an energy spike → an inflation impulse → a policy response → a market-wide repricing.

**Energy-sector rotation.** When the regime shifts toward higher and more volatile energy prices, capital rotates *into* the energy sector and out of the sectors that energy taxes (consumer discretionary, transport, rate-sensitive growth). The energy sector's weight in the S&P 500, which had shrunk to the low single digits by 2020, expanded sharply in 2021–2022 as the rotation played out — a visible footprint of the chain at the index level.

**The dollar and the petrodollar link.** Oil is priced and overwhelmingly settled in US dollars worldwide, which ties the energy market to the currency market in a specific way. A higher oil price means importing countries must buy more dollars to pay for the same barrels, which tends to support the dollar; it also worsens the trade balance and currency of net energy importers (Europe, Japan, much of Asia) while helping net exporters. So an energy shock is simultaneously a commodity event, an inflation event, *and* a currency event, and the practitioner who maps all three captures more of the move than one who watches only the oil tape. The structural reason oil clears in dollars — and what it would take to change — is its own topic, linked at the end.

**The safe-haven cross-currents.** A geopolitical energy shock pulls two ways on the classic safe-haven assets. It is risk-off, which supports gold and (sometimes) government bonds; but if it is also an inflation impulse, it pushes bond yields *up* (bond prices down), which is the opposite of the usual flight-to-safety bid. In 2022 this cross-current was stark: bonds, normally the hedge for an equity selloff, fell *alongside* equities because the shock was inflationary, breaking the stock-bond diversification that had worked for a generation. Gold, by contrast, held its safe-haven role. Knowing which way a given shock pushes each haven — risk-off versus inflationary — is what separates a hedge that works from one that compounds the loss.

## How to trade it: the energy-geopolitics playbook

Every mechanism above exists to be turned into a position. Here is the practitioner's playbook, organized by the catalyst.

### Trade the OPEC+ meeting (the cut/hold/raise decision)

An OPEC+ meeting is a scheduled, binary-ish catalyst. The discipline is the same as trading any event:

- **Know what is priced in.** Read the futures curve and the consensus expectation *before* the meeting. The market trades the *surprise* relative to that expectation, not the raw headline. A cut that is smaller than expected is bearish; a hold when a cut was expected is bearish.
- **The position.** A larger-than-expected cut: long oil (or long energy producers, which carry operating leverage to the price). A larger-than-expected raise: the reverse. To express the view with defined risk, options on oil or on energy-sector ETFs let you cap the downside if the meeting surprises against you.
- **The second-order trade.** If the cut is large enough to be an inflation impulse, the better risk-reward may be in the rate-sensitive assets it threatens — short long-duration bonds or rate-sensitive growth equities — rather than in oil itself, which may already have moved.
- **Watch the credibility signal, not just the number.** An OPEC+ headline is only as good as the group's ability to enforce it. A "cut" announced by members who are already producing below their quotas (so the cut is paper, not real barrels) moves the price far less than a cut backed by the swing producer actually pulling barrels. Cross-check the announced cut against compliance data and against whether Saudi Arabia is leading with its own reduction. A cut the market judges un-credible can fade within days — exactly the kind of fade an event trader looks for.

A final discipline on the OPEC+ trade: respect that the cartel's reaction function is itself part of the game. If oil falls too far, the group is more likely to cut (a floor); if oil rises to levels that threaten demand or invite political pressure to add supply, the group is more likely to raise (a ceiling). Trading oil is partly trading the cartel's likely response to the current price — a reflexive loop that rewards thinking one step ahead of the meeting rather than reacting to it.

### Position for an energy-as-inflation impulse

When a geopolitical event threatens energy supply, the highest-conviction expression is often not the commodity but the chain it sets off:

- **Rates:** an energy-driven inflation impulse argues for higher yields and a tougher central bank — position short duration, or for a steeper or higher rate path.
- **Equities:** rotate toward energy producers (operating leverage to the price) and away from the energy-users and rate-sensitive growth names the spike taxes. The 2022 rotation — energy and value up, long-duration tech down — is the template.
- **Consumers and transport:** an oil spike is a tax that hits airlines, trucking, and discretionary consumer spending first and hardest. These are the natural shorts in an energy-shock regime.

### The gas-shock playbook

A regional gas shock (a chokepoint dependence detonating) is rarer and more violent than an oil move:

- The local currency of the energy-importing region weakens (a worse trade balance), so an FX expression is available.
- Energy-intensive industrials and regulated utilities in the affected region are the cleanest shorts; LNG exporters and any holder of flexible cargoes are the cleanest longs.
- Because gas is un-arbitrageable in the short run, the move can go far further than seems reasonable — €339/MWh was unthinkable in 2021. Size for a fat tail, and respect that the reversal (mild weather, demand destruction, new LNG) can be just as violent once the scramble eases.
- The catalysts that end a gas shock are knowable in advance: a mild winter that protects storage, the completion of new import terminals, the success of a demand-reduction campaign, and the eventual ramp of new LNG export capacity. A practitioner who tracks the storage level, the weather forecast, and the LNG order book can judge when the squeeze is structurally over — and that is the moment to flip from the long-volatility, long-energy trade to fading the now-overpriced fear. The 2022–2023 collapse from €339 to under €40 in a few months rewarded exactly that pivot. The broader point: a gas shock is asymmetric on the way up (a vertical spike) but mean-reverting on the way down once supply and demand re-equilibrate, so the trade is not one position but two — long the scramble, then short the relief.

### What invalidates the view

A discipline is only as good as its exit. The bullish-energy thesis is invalidated when:

- **Demand destruction sets in.** If the price rises high enough to break growth, the recession then crushes demand and the trade reverses — the 2008 \$147-to-\$35 collapse is the warning. A bullish-oil trade that is "working too well" (price spiking toward levels that historically trigger recession) is approaching its own self-destruct.
- **Supply responds.** US shale adds rigs, OPEC+ discipline cracks and members cheat, the SPR is released, or demand is voluntarily cut (Europe's 15% gas saving). Any of these closes the supply gap and deflates the price.
- **The geopolitical premium fades.** Most geopolitical shocks fade as the feared supply disruption fails to materialize; the base rate is that the premium decays. Position for the fade once the worst-case supply scenario is priced and not realized — the distinction between a passing scare and a regime change is the whole subject of the geopolitical-risk-premium post linked below.

The throughline of the entire post: an energy supply decision made for political reasons is, mechanically, an inflation-and-growth shock that reprices every asset. Read the supply decision early, size the repricing through the inflation-and-policy chain, position in the assets with the cleanest leverage to it (producers long, rate-sensitives and energy-users short), and know that demand destruction and a supply response are what invalidate the view.
