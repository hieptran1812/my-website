> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) does not steer the economy mainly with a policy rate the way the Fed does; it hands each commercial bank an administrative **credit-growth quota**, manages the dong inside a daily reference band, and licenses every gram of gold imported. These legal levers, not the rate, are what move Vietnamese liquidity and the VN-Index.
>
> - The single most important number in Vietnamese finance each year is the **system credit-growth target** — roughly 14-15%, divided into a quota per bank. Loosen it and the market rallies; cut it and liquidity drains.
> - The dong is a **managed crawl**, not a float: it trades inside a +/- 5% band around an SBV reference rate the bank resets daily and nudges weaker over time (about 23,200 to 24,600 VND per USD across 2019-2024).
> - To keep both a managed currency and its own credit policy, Vietnam gives up the third leg of the **impossible trinity** — free capital flows. That is why capital controls and a gated capital account exist.
> - The one number to remember: a system credit target near **14%** on a loan book of roughly **\$600 billion** equivalent means the SBV is metering out about **\$84 billion** of new credit a year by decree.

On a Friday afternoon in late 2022, dealers on Ho Chi Minh City's trading floors were watching two numbers that no textbook on the Federal Reserve would have prepared them for. The first was the dong, pinned to the weak edge of its trading band against the dollar, with the SBV burning reserves to keep it there. The second was a rumor — later confirmed — that the central bank was about to *raise* the year's system-wide credit-growth target by a couple of percentage points so that banks could lend again into a freezing property and bond market. The VN-Index had already fallen from a peak of 1,528 in January to a trough of 874 in November, a 43% drawdown. What turned it was not a rate cut announced at a press conference. It was an administrative number — a quota — adjusted by a regulator with the power to tell each bank, by name, how much it was allowed to grow its loan book.

That is the thing a developed-market investor has to unlearn first about Vietnam. In the United States or the euro area, monetary policy is largely a price: the central bank sets the price of overnight money (the policy rate) and lets the banking system decide how much to lend at that price. In Vietnam, monetary policy is also a *quantity*, set by decree. The SBV tells the banking system not just what money costs but how much of it may be created. It manages the exchange rate inside a legal band rather than letting it float. And it controls, directly, the import of the one asset Vietnamese households trust more than the dong: gold.

Why does this matter for an investor rather than just a central-banking specialist? Because it changes *what you watch*. If you follow Vietnam the way you follow the Fed — parsing rate decisions and the tone of the governor — you will repeatedly be blindsided, because the rate is the least informative of the SBV's levers. The information lives in quantities: the annual credit target and its bank-by-bank allocation, the daily reference rate and the band width, the level and trend of FX reserves, the gold premium. These are all published or observable, all set in law or by directive, and all repriced by the market with a lag that a prepared investor can trade. The thesis of this post, and of this whole series, is that a rule or a legal lever changes the rules of the game *before* it fully bites, and the edge is reading the lever early. Nowhere is that more literally true than in Vietnam, where the levers are written down as numbers in circulars and decrees.

This post builds that whole toolkit from zero. We will define each legal lever, show how the credit quota throttles liquidity and the VN-Index, walk through the managed dong and why it forces capital controls, watch the 2022 reserve burn, learn to read SBV's signals, and unpack the strange persistence of the SJC gold premium. Then we turn it into a playbook: what to track, how to position banks and cyclicals to the quota cycle, where the tail risk sits, and what invalidates the view. Throughout, the worked examples are anchored in dollars (via roughly 25,000 VND per USD) so the magnitudes are legible to anyone, while keeping the dong figures that practitioners actually see.

## Foundations: how the SBV's legal toolkit actually works

Start with the institution. The **State Bank of Vietnam** (Ngan hang Nha nuoc Viet Nam, SBV) is the country's central bank, but with an important difference from the Fed or the European Central Bank: it is a ministry-level body of the government, not an independent agency. Its governor sits in the cabinet, and its core policy decisions are coordinated with — and often directed by — the broader economic plan. So when we talk about "SBV law", we mean the Law on the State Bank of Vietnam and the Law on Credit Institutions, plus the dense layer of decrees (Nghi dinh) and circulars (Thong tu) the SBV issues to implement them. Those circulars are where the real machinery lives. A circular can set the credit quota, the reserve requirement, the loan-to-deposit ratio, the rules on who may import gold — each one a legal instrument with direct market consequences.

The SBV has the standard central-bank tools, and then it has the ones that make Vietnam distinctive. Take them in turn.

**Policy rates.** The SBV does set interest rates — a refinancing rate (the rate it charges banks to borrow against collateral), a discount rate, and rates on its open-market operations (OMO), the repo-style facility through which it lends short-term cash to banks against government bonds. It also caps deposit rates on short tenors, which directly shapes banks' funding costs. These rates matter, and the SBV moves them, but they are not the binding constraint on how much credit the system creates. The reason is structural: when the quota caps the *quantity* of new loans, the rate can only influence the price of that fixed quantity, not its size. A bank at its quota will not lend more just because borrowing from the SBV got cheaper — it legally cannot. So the rate is a secondary lever that fine-tunes funding costs and signals stance, while the quota does the heavy lifting. That ordering is the opposite of a developed market, where the rate is the primary lever and bank-by-bank quantity caps do not exist. The day-to-day plumbing — net OMO injections and withdrawals, and the SBV's issuance of short-term bills to drain cash — is where the SBV manages the *level* of liquidity inside the quota's ceiling, and it is worth watching, but it operates within the box the quota draws.

**The credit-growth target — the quota.** Each year the SBV announces a *system-wide credit-growth target*: the percentage by which total outstanding loans in the banking system are allowed to grow. Recent targets have run around 14-15%. Crucially, the SBV does not stop at a system number. It allocates that growth as a quota to each individual bank — a strong, well-capitalized bank might be granted room to grow its loan book 16-18%, a weaker one capped at single digits, and a bank in trouble frozen near zero. A bank that hits its quota simply *cannot make new loans* until the SBV grants more room, no matter how many creditworthy borrowers are queuing. This is administrative credit allocation, and it is the single most powerful lever the SBV holds.

**The managed dong.** The dong does not float. The SBV publishes a daily **central reference rate** (ty gia trung tam) against the dollar and allows the market rate to trade within a band of +/- 5% around it. The SBV crawls that reference weaker over time — a slow, deliberate depreciation rather than a free float — and it intervenes by buying and selling dollars from reserves to keep the market rate inside the band. Across 2019-2024 the reference moved from about 23,200 to 24,600 VND per USD, a managed slide of roughly 6% over five years.

**FX reserves and intervention.** To defend the band, the SBV needs a war chest of foreign-exchange reserves — dollars, mostly, accumulated when the trade surplus and FDI inflows are strong. When the dong is under pressure (capital wants to leave, or the trade balance weakens), the SBV sells dollars and buys dong, which props up the currency but drains reserves and, importantly, *drains dong liquidity* from the banking system. Defending the currency and loosening domestic credit pull in opposite directions — a tension we will return to.

**Prudential ratios.** Layered on top are the usual bank-safety rules: a reserve requirement ratio (a fraction of deposits banks must park at the SBV), a loan-to-deposit ratio cap (banks may not lend out more than a set share of their deposits), a cap on the share of short-term deposits that may fund long-term loans, and capital adequacy rules broadly tracking the Basel framework (a bank must hold capital against its risk-weighted assets). These shape how aggressively a bank can use its quota. A bank may have quota room left but be unable to use it because it has bumped its loan-to-deposit ceiling or run low on capital — so the *effective* lending capacity of the system is the quota and the prudential ratios together, not the quota alone.

**The legal hierarchy.** It helps to know where these rules live, because the level of a rule tells you how hard it is to change and therefore how durable the policy is. At the top sit the two governing statutes — the Law on the State Bank of Vietnam and the Law on Credit Institutions — passed by the National Assembly and slow to amend. Below them are decrees (Nghi dinh) issued by the government; the gold monopoly, for instance, lives in Decree 24/2012, which is why reforming it is a government-level decision, not something the SBV can do unilaterally overnight. At the working level are circulars (Thong tu) issued by the SBV itself — the reserve ratio, the loan-to-deposit cap, the gold-auction rules, and the operational guidance behind the credit quota are circular-level instruments the SBV can revise quickly. The credit target each year is set by an SBV directive rather than a statute, which is precisely why it can be adjusted mid-year: it is a fast, low-friction lever by design.

**Gold-import licensing.** Finally, the lever no Western central bank has: the SBV licenses gold imports and, under Decree 24 of 2012, made the state-owned SJC brand the only legally produced gold bar. By controlling the supply of imported and branded gold, the SBV controls the domestic gold price — which, because Vietnamese households hold gold as a savings vehicle and inflation hedge, is itself a monetary lever.

Put these together and what emerges is a central bank that manages the economy by *quantities set in law* — how much credit, what the dong may trade at, how much gold may enter — far more than by a single price. The map at the top of this post shows the four levers converging on bank lending, the dong, and gold, and from there onto system liquidity and the VN-Index.

### Why a quota system at all?

It is worth pausing on the *why*, because the quota looks bizarre to anyone raised on price-based monetary policy. The honest answer is that Vietnam's monetary transmission through interest rates is weak and slow. A large share of borrowing is by state-linked firms and property developers whose demand for credit is not very sensitive to the rate; banks are unevenly capitalized; and the policy-rate channel leaks. When the central bank cannot reliably control the *quantity* of credit by setting its *price*, the blunt-but-effective alternative is to set the quantity directly. The quota also lets the SBV steer credit *away* from sectors it considers speculative — property, securities — and *toward* ones it favors, by adjusting individual banks' room and attaching conditions. It is industrial policy and monetary policy fused into one instrument.

The cost is equally real: a quota rations credit administratively, which can starve good borrowers when the system cap binds, reward banks with political access, and create a year-end scramble as banks race to use unspent room. But for the SBV, the control is worth the distortion. Keep that trade-off in mind — it explains a lot of Vietnamese market behavior, especially the year-end liquidity rhythm.

## How the credit quota throttles lending and liquidity

The quota is where law meets the loan book most directly, so let us trace the mechanism carefully. Banks create money when they lend: a new loan credited to a borrower's account *is* new deposit money in the system. So a cap on how fast loans may grow is, quite literally, a cap on how fast the broad money supply may grow. The transmission runs in a chain, and the figure below lays out each link.

![Chain from a raised SBV credit quota through more bank lending to a higher VN-Index](/imgs/blogs/sbv-monetary-and-banking-law-credit-quotas-and-the-dong-2.png)

When the SBV *raises* the quota — grants banks more room — banks have more legal headroom to lend. New loans flow out to property developers, manufacturers, consumers. Those loans become deposits, which recycle as funding for still more lending and as cash sloshing around the financial system. Some of that liquidity finds its way into equities directly, and a lot of it finds its way in through **margin lending**: Vietnamese retail investors are heavy users of broker margin, and brokers fund that margin from the banking system. Looser system credit means cheaper, more abundant margin, which means more buying power chasing stocks. The VN-Index re-rates higher. When the SBV *cuts* or *exhausts* the quota, the chain runs in reverse: lending stalls, liquidity tightens, margin gets called, and the index slides.

This is why the credit target is the number Vietnamese market participants watch most closely each January, and why mid-year quota *adjustments* — the SBV granting additional room, or reallocating it toward healthy banks — are major market events. They are, functionally, the equivalent of a rate decision in a Western market, except they act on the quantity of credit rather than its price.

The chart below shows the actual targets the SBV has set. Note the 2021 cut to 12% during the pandemic credit-control phase and the steady creep back up toward 15% as the economy needed support.

![Bar chart of Vietnam system credit-growth target by year, 12 to 15 percent](/imgs/blogs/sbv-monetary-and-banking-law-credit-quotas-and-the-dong-3.png)

#### Worked example: a 14% quota on a dollar loan book

Take the system. Vietnam's total outstanding bank credit is roughly **15,000 trillion dong**. At about 25,000 VND per USD, that is **\$600 billion** equivalent. A system credit-growth target of **14%** means the SBV is authorizing, by decree, new credit of:

```
new credit = 0.14 × $600bn = $84bn (about 2,100 trillion dong)
```

That \$84 billion of new lending is the fuel for the year's GDP growth, property construction, and — indirectly — the stock market's liquidity. Now suppose mid-year the SBV lifts the target to **15%**. The extra 1 percentage point is:

```
extra credit = 0.01 × $600bn = $6bn (about 150 trillion dong)
```

Six billion dollars of additional credit, conjured by changing one number in a circular. The takeaway: in Vietnam a single administrative digit, not a rate move, decides whether tens of billions of dollars of new money enter the economy this year.

#### Worked example: a bank that hits its quota

Zoom to one bank. Say a mid-size commercial bank starts the year with a loan book of **250 trillion dong** (\$10 billion) and is granted a **14%** quota. Its legal room for new net lending is:

```
room = 0.14 × $10bn = $1.4bn (35 trillion dong)
```

If the bank lends that out by September, it is *done* — it cannot book new loans for the rest of the year even though demand is strong and its capital is fine, unless the SBV grants more room. So the bank does three things that move markets. First, it front-loads lending early in the year to lock in the spread, which is why Vietnamese credit growth is seasonally fast in Q1-Q2. Second, late in the year it hoards its remaining room for its best clients and chokes off marginal borrowers, tightening liquidity into December. Third, it lobbies hard for a quota top-up. When the SBV grants those top-ups — as it did in late 2022 and again in 2023 — the affected banks' shares typically jump, because more room means more loans means more interest income. The lesson: the quota is not a line item on a banking-sector spreadsheet; it sets the calendar and the share price of every individual bank.

This administrative throttle is the Vietnamese equivalent of the credit channel that, in developed markets, runs through bank capital and the policy rate. If you want the price-based version of how a central bank steers the cost of money, the mechanics are laid out in [how the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates); Vietnam bolts a quantity cap on top of that price.

### The year-end liquidity rhythm and the interbank rate

The quota does something subtle that no rate-based system produces: it imposes a *calendar* on liquidity. Because each bank's room resets on January 1 and cannot be carried forward, the year has a built-in rhythm that a Vietnamese trader feels in the bones. In Q1 and Q2, banks rush to deploy fresh room and lock in a full year of interest income, so credit growth is front-loaded and liquidity is generous — historically a supportive window for the VN-Index. By Q3, the stronger banks are approaching their caps. In Q4, banks that are near their limit stop lending at the margin, hoard remaining room for their best clients, and compete fiercely for deposits to shore up ratios before the year-end audit. The result is a recurring December liquidity squeeze: interbank rates spike, deposit rates rise, and equity-market liquidity thins. Then the calendar flips, new room arrives, and the cycle restarts.

The price you watch for this is the **interbank offered rate** — the rate at which banks lend overnight and short-term cash to each other. When the system is flush (early year, or after the SBV injects cash through OMO), the overnight interbank rate can sit near 1-2%. When liquidity tightens (year-end, or during a reserve-draining dong defense), it can spike to 6-8% or higher in days. A foreign analyst with no access to the SBV's internal quota allocations can still read the *state* of liquidity off the interbank rate, because it is the system's real-time pressure gauge. A persistent spike says the quota is binding or the SBV is draining dong to defend the currency; a collapse back toward 1-2% says room and cash are abundant again.

#### Worked example: a year-end quota squeeze and the cost of cash

Take a bank that enters December having used **96%** of its room. It has unused capacity of:

```
remaining room = (1 − 0.96) × $1.4bn quota = $56m (1.4 trillion dong)
```

That \$56 million is reserved for the bank's largest, most profitable clients; everyone else is turned away or pushed to a non-bank lender. Meanwhile, to meet its year-end loan-to-deposit and reserve ratios, the bank must hold more cash, so it bids aggressively in the interbank market. If the overnight rate jumps from **2%** to **7%**, a bank that needs to borrow **\$200 million** overnight for a week to plug a gap pays:

```
extra cost = $200m × (0.07 − 0.02) × (7 ÷ 365) = $191,781
```

Nearly **\$192,000** in extra funding cost for one week, purely because the quota calendar and prudential ratios collide at year-end. That cost ripples into tighter margin lending and a thinner bid under stocks every December. The takeaway: the quota does not just cap how much credit exists — it shapes *when* liquidity is cheap and when it is scarce, and the interbank rate lets you watch that rhythm in real time.

## The managed dong and the impossible trinity

The second great lever is the currency. To understand why the SBV manages the dong rather than floating it, we need one of the most useful ideas in international finance: the **impossible trinity** (also called the trilemma). It says a country can have at most two of these three things at once:

1. a **fixed or managed exchange rate**,
2. an **independent monetary policy** (it sets its own credit and rate conditions), and
3. **free movement of capital** across its borders.

You cannot have all three. The arithmetic is unforgiving: if money can flow freely *and* you try to hold the exchange rate *and* you try to set your own interest rate, then the moment your rate differs from the world's, capital floods in or out to arbitrage the gap, and either your currency peg breaks or your domestic rate gets dragged to the world rate. Something has to give. The figure shows which two legs Vietnam keeps and which it gives up.

![Impossible trinity triangle showing Vietnam keeps the managed dong and policy control and limits capital flows](/imgs/blogs/sbv-monetary-and-banking-law-credit-quotas-and-the-dong-6.png)

Vietnam's choice is clear and deliberate. It keeps a **managed exchange rate** (the dong band) and an **independent credit policy** (the quota). To make those two compatible, it must sacrifice the third leg — **free capital flows**. That is the deep reason capital controls exist in Vietnam: they are not an accident of an unmodernized system but a structural requirement of running a managed currency alongside domestic credit control. The dong is not freely convertible offshore; foreign investors face limits and procedures on moving money in and out; and the capital account is gated. We treat the legal architecture of those controls in detail in [capital controls and the legal limits on money flows](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/capital-controls-and-the-legal-limits-on-money-flows); here the point is *why* Vietnam must impose them — the trilemma leaves no choice.

Now the mechanics of the managed dong itself. Each morning the SBV sets a central reference rate. The market may trade the dong within +/- 5% of it. In practice the dong almost never strengthens to the floor; the pressure is nearly always toward depreciation, so the market rate hugs the *ceiling* (the weak edge) during stress and the SBV defends from there. Over time the SBV crawls the reference weaker — a controlled depreciation that keeps Vietnamese exports competitive without the disorder of a free float. The figure below shows the band structure and the crawl.

![Managed dong band diagram with ceiling, reference rate, and floor levels and the slow weaker crawl](/imgs/blogs/sbv-monetary-and-banking-law-credit-quotas-and-the-dong-4.png)

The crawl is visible in the data. The SBV's central reference rate slid steadily from about 23,200 in 2019 to 24,600 by 2024, stepping weaker in the stress years of 2022-2023 when the dollar was strong globally and capital-outflow pressure rose.

![Step chart of USD/VND reference rate rising from 23,200 to 24,600 from 2019 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/sbv-monetary-and-banking-law-credit-quotas-and-the-dong-5.png)

For an investor, the practical consequence is that the dong is a *slow, one-directional* risk most of the time — a managed depreciation of low single digits a year — punctuated by faster moves when global dollar strength or domestic stress forces the SBV to step the reference weaker. It is the opposite of a freely floating currency that whips both ways. You can plan around a crawl; you have to hedge against a step.

#### Worked example: the dong moves 23,000 to 24,600 — the FX loss on an unhedged position

Suppose a foreign fund holds a **\$10 million** Vietnamese equity position, entered when the dong was at **23,000** VND per USD. The fund's dong exposure at entry is:

```
dong value = $10,000,000 × 23,000 = 230,000,000,000 VND (230 billion dong)
```

Two years later the SBV has crawled the dong to **24,600**. The dong holdings are unchanged in local terms, but to repatriate the fund must convert dong back to dollars at the weaker rate:

```
dollars back = 230,000,000,000 ÷ 24,600 = $9,349,593
FX loss = $10,000,000 − $9,349,593 = $650,407 (about 6.5%)
```

The fund lost **\$650,000 — 6.5%** of its capital — purely to the managed depreciation, before counting whether the stocks themselves went up or down. The lesson: in a managed-crawl currency the FX drag is small per year but relentless and one-directional, so a foreign investor in Vietnam is implicitly short the dong and must decide whether to hedge that built-in bleed.

## The dong-defense reserve burn: 2022 in close-up

The trilemma is not just theory; it bites hard in stress. 2022 is the textbook case. The Fed was hiking aggressively (its target rose from 0.25% to 4.50% across the year), the dollar surged globally, and capital wanted out of emerging markets and into dollar assets. The dong came under heavy depreciation pressure, pinning the weak edge of its band. The SBV faced the trilemma's sharp end: to hold the band, it had to sell dollars from reserves and buy dong — which props up the currency but *withdraws dong liquidity from the banking system at the very moment the domestic economy needs more of it*.

This is the cruel mechanics of a managed currency under a strong-dollar regime. Defending the dong drains reserves and tightens domestic liquidity; loosening domestic liquidity (more credit, lower rates) weakens the dong further. The SBV cannot do both at full strength simultaneously — that is the trilemma made concrete. Through 2022 the bank chose to defend the currency first, selling an estimated \$20-25 billion of reserves (reserves fell from roughly \$110 billion toward \$87 billion over the year), stepping the reference rate weaker, *and* widening the trading band from +/- 3% to +/- 5% in October to give the dong more room to absorb pressure without burning yet more reserves. The simultaneous liquidity drain — compounded by a domestic bond-and-property crackdown unfolding at the same time — is a big part of why the VN-Index collapsed 43% from peak to trough that year.

![VN-Index line chart peaking near 1,528 in early 2022 and troughing near 874 in November](/imgs/blogs/sbv-monetary-and-banking-law-credit-quotas-and-the-dong-7.png)

#### Worked example: reserve burn versus the stock market

Quantify the burn. Vietnam entered 2022 with FX reserves of about **\$110 billion**, comfortably above the IMF's adequacy guidance of roughly three months of imports (Vietnam imports run near \$30 billion a month, so three months is about **\$90 billion**). Defending the band cost an estimated **\$23 billion**:

```
reserves: $110bn − $23bn = $87bn at the 2022 low
months of import cover: $87bn ÷ $30bn ≈ 2.9 months
```

Reserves fell to *below* the three-month adequacy floor — a genuine warning level. The market read it exactly that way: every fresh sign that the SBV was burning reserves and stepping the dong weaker was a sell signal for the VN-Index, because it meant tighter dong liquidity ahead. The index troughed at **874** in November, within days of the period of heaviest reserve loss. The takeaway: in a managed-currency country, the reserve trend is a direct liquidity indicator for stocks — falling reserves mean the central bank is draining dong to defend the dollar peg, and that drain shows up as lower equity prices.

The flip side came in 2023-2024: as the dollar eased and Vietnam's trade surplus and FDI rebuilt reserves, the SBV could relax, liquidity loosened, and the VN-Index recovered toward 1,267 by end-2024. The currency-and-liquidity cycle is, for Vietnam, a master cycle that the equity market tracks closely. For the global backdrop that drives these cycles — the dollar, US rates, and the strong-dollar squeeze on emerging markets — the cross-asset transmission is mapped in [currency wars and competitive devaluation](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/currency-wars-and-competitive-devaluation).

There is a deeper point hiding in the reserve burn that is worth making explicit, because it is the single most counter-intuitive feature of a managed-currency economy. **Defending the currency is a domestic tightening.** When the SBV sells dollars and buys dong to hold the band, the dong it buys is *removed from circulation* — it sits on the central bank's balance sheet rather than in the banking system. So an act that looks purely external (propping up the exchange rate) is in fact a contraction of the domestic money supply, exactly as if the SBV had raised rates or cut the quota. This is why, in 2022, Vietnamese liquidity tightened savagely even though the SBV's headline policy rate barely moved: the binding tightening came through the FX window, not the rate window. A Western investor watching only the policy rate would have concluded Vietnam was on hold; a Vietnamese investor watching reserves and the interbank rate knew the country was in a hard squeeze. The reserve trend, not the rate, was telling the truth.

The SBV does have a partial escape valve called **sterilization** — when it buys dollars in good times (injecting dong), it can soak the excess dong back up by selling SBV bills, so the FX intervention does not overheat domestic liquidity; and in reverse it can inject dong through OMO to offset a defense. But sterilization is imperfect and limited, especially under sustained pressure. In a serious dong defense the SBV cannot fully offset the liquidity drain, which is exactly why the 2022 currency defense and the domestic credit squeeze arrived together. The lesson for an investor is to treat the SBV's reserve position and its dong-defense activity as a *monetary-policy signal of the first order*, on par with the credit quota — not as a sideshow to the "real" policy rate.

## Reading SBV signals

If the SBV's levers are quantities set in law, then reading the SBV means reading those quantities and their legal announcements rather than parsing the tone of a press conference. Here is the practical signal set, in rough order of market impact.

**The annual credit target (each January).** The headline number — and, more revealingly, how the SBV talks about *allocation*. A high target with a promise of generous room for healthy banks is loosening; a low target or language about "controlling credit to risky sectors" is tightening. The first quota allocations to individual banks, which follow the headline, tell you which banks got room to grow.

**Mid-year quota adjustments.** The SBV's decision to grant additional room — or to reallocate room from banks that cannot use it to banks that can — is the closest thing Vietnam has to an intra-year rate decision. Watch for it especially when the economy is slowing and the property or bond market is stressed.

**The central reference rate path.** A reference rate the SBV is stepping weaker, day after day, signals depreciation pressure and likely reserve loss — a liquidity-negative signal. A stable or strengthening reference signals calm. Watch also for *band-width changes*: widening the band (as in October 2022) is a defensive move that signals the SBV is running low on appetite to burn reserves.

**FX reserves.** Published with a lag and never in real time, but the trend is the single best gauge of how much dong liquidity the SBV is adding or draining. Rising reserves (the SBV buying dollars) inject dong and ease liquidity; falling reserves (selling dollars) drain it. Analysts back out the trend from the SBV's balance sheet, trade data, and FX-market behavior.

**Policy rates and OMO.** Secondary but not irrelevant. Cuts to the refinancing and OMO rates, and net OMO injections of cash, confirm an easing stance; the reverse confirms tightening. In Vietnam these usually *follow* the quantity signals rather than lead them.

**Gold-market signals.** A widening SJC gold premium (next section) tells you households are fleeing the dong into gold — a sign of distrust in the currency or fear of inflation — and can prompt the SBV to intervene with gold-import licenses or auctions.

A practical note on *sequencing*, because it is where most analysts go wrong. The SBV's levers do not move in lockstep, and their order of operation tells you the regime. In an easing regime, the typical sequence is: reserves rebuild (the SBV buys dollars), the interbank rate falls, the SBV cuts policy rates and injects via OMO, and finally it raises or generously allocates the credit quota — liquidity, then price, then quantity. In a tightening regime the order reverses and compresses, often violently: the dollar surges, the dong hits its band, the SBV sells reserves to defend, the interbank rate spikes, and the quota effectively binds as banks pull back — all at once. So an investor who sees reserves falling and the interbank rate spiking should not wait for a formal quota cut or a rate hike to conclude that policy has tightened; the tightening is already happening through the FX and liquidity windows. Reading the *sequence* of the levers, not any single one, is the skill. This is the same discipline that governs all rule-driven markets: a change in the *legal lever* repriced *before* it fully bites is the edge. The general framework — how a rule change drifts into expectations and then into prices — is the spine of this series, set out in [how law moves markets: the transmission chain](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/how-law-moves-markets-the-transmission-chain).

## The SJC gold premium

The third lever is gold, and it produces one of the most striking anomalies in any market: a domestic gold price that can trade **15-20% above the world price** for years at a time. Understanding it requires the law.

Under **Decree 24/2012**, the SBV took monopoly control of gold-bar production and made **SJC** (a state-owned company) the sole legal gold-bar brand. It also requires a license for gold imports, which the SBV grants sparingly. The combination is a textbook supply restriction: the world gold market is deep and arbitrage keeps prices aligned across countries, but Vietnam *severs that arbitrage* by law. A trader who sees Vietnamese gold at a 17% premium cannot simply import cheap world gold and sell it domestically to close the gap — importing requires a license the SBV does not readily grant, and minting it into a sellable SJC bar is a state monopoly. With arbitrage blocked, the domestic price is set by domestic supply (frozen) and domestic demand (strong, because households use gold as a savings and inflation hedge in a managed-currency economy). The premium is the price of the legal barrier. The figure contrasts the open world market with the gated Vietnamese one.

![Before-after diagram comparing the open world gold market with Vietnam's licensed SJC gold market and its premium](/imgs/blogs/sbv-monetary-and-banking-law-credit-quotas-and-the-dong-8.png)

The premium is not a curiosity; it is a monetary signal and a policy headache. When confidence in the dong falls — during depreciation scares or inflation spikes — households rush into gold, the premium blows out, and that itself pressures the dong (people are effectively selling dong to hold gold). The SBV manages it by occasionally auctioning gold or signaling import relaxation to cap the premium. In 2024, with the premium near record highs, the SBV ran gold auctions and announced reforms to the SJC monopoly precisely to bring the domestic price back toward the world price.

The premium also distorts behavior in ways that feed back into the dong. Because Vietnamese households trust gold more than the dong as a long-run store of value, a large share of national savings is held in gold rather than in bank deposits — capital that is, in effect, removed from the formal financial system. Every wave of gold-buying during a depreciation scare both widens the premium and *pulls deposits out of banks*, which tightens funding for the very lending the SBV is trying to encourage. This is why the gold lever is genuinely monetary and not a mere consumer-protection issue: the SBV's control of gold supply is also a (partial) control of where household savings sit, and a blowing premium is a sign that savings are fleeing the formal system. The 2024 reform push — auctions to flood supply, and proposals to end the SJC single-brand monopoly so other producers can mint bars — was aimed squarely at closing the premium and coaxing those savings back toward dong deposits.

#### Worked example: the gold-premium dollar gap

Suppose the world gold price is **\$2,650 per ounce** and SJC gold trades at a **17%** premium. The domestic price in dollar terms is:

```
SJC price = $2,650 × 1.17 = $3,100 per ounce
premium per ounce = $3,100 − $2,650 = $450
```

A Vietnamese household buying one ounce of SJC gold pays **\$450 more** than the same ounce costs in Singapore or London — a tax, in effect, that the legal monopoly imposes on the act of saving in gold. Scale it up: a family converting **\$50,000** of savings into SJC gold pays:

```
premium cost = $50,000 × 0.17 ÷ 1.17 = $7,265
```

About **\$7,300** lost to the premium on a \$50,000 conversion. The lesson: the gold-import monopoly is a hidden levy on Vietnamese savers, and the size of the premium is a real-time thermometer of how much they distrust the dong — which is exactly why the SBV watches it as a monetary signal. For how gold behaves as a global safe-haven asset, see [why gold is the ultimate safe-haven asset](/blog/trading/gold/why-gold-is-the-ultimate-safe-haven-asset).

## The political economy of who gets room

The quota is not allocated by a formula alone, and that fact is itself an investable insight. The SBV grades banks on health — capital adequacy, asset quality, governance, compliance — and the strongest banks get the largest growth allocations. A bank that cleaned up its bad debt, raised capital, and runs conservative ratios is rewarded with room to grow 16-18%, while a bank under restructuring may be capped near zero. So the quota allocation is, in effect, an annual public ranking of bank quality by the regulator, and it compounds: the strong banks grow faster, earn more, build more capital, and earn still larger allocations the next year. Over a few cycles this produces real divergence between the leaders and the laggards in the sector — a structural reason that the best-run Vietnamese banks have outgrown the weak ones, visible directly in their loan-book growth and return on equity.

For a stock-picker, this turns the January allocation into a screening tool. The banks granted the most room are the regulator's stamp of approval; they are the ones whose interest income will grow fastest that year, and historically they have led the sector. The banks frozen near zero are flagged as problem cases — sometimes the first public signal that a bank is in trouble before its financials fully reveal it. Reading the allocation is reading the SBV's private view of each bank, made public. It is a richer signal than any rate decision, because it is bank-specific.

There is a state-ownership dimension layered on top. The largest banks are partly state-owned (the "Big Four" state commercial banks dominate the system), and credit is often steered, formally or informally, toward state priorities — infrastructure, favored industries, state-linked enterprises. This is the industrial-policy face of the quota: it is not a neutral monetary instrument but a tool for directing the *composition* of credit, not just its quantity. An investor should hold both ideas at once: the quota is a powerful liquidity lever, and it is a channel through which the state allocates capital toward its own goals — which is a strength (coordination, stability) and a weakness (misallocation, evergreening of bad loans) at the same time.

#### Worked example: two banks, same quota, different outcomes

Take two banks, each starting with a **\$10 billion** loan book. Bank A is well-capitalized and granted **18%** room; Bank B is under restructuring and capped at **6%**. Over one year:

```
Bank A new lending: 0.18 × $10bn = $1.8bn
Bank B new lending: 0.06 × $10bn = $0.6bn
gap in one year = $1.2bn of loan growth
```

If both banks earn a net interest margin of about **3.5%** on their new lending, the first-year interest-income difference from the allocation alone is:

```
extra income to A vs B = $1.2bn × 0.035 = $42m
```

Roughly **\$42 million** of additional annual interest income for Bank A over Bank B, driven purely by who the regulator deemed healthy enough to grow — and the gap compounds because A's faster growth builds the capital that earns it a larger allocation next year. The takeaway: in Vietnam the credit allocation is a regulator-assigned growth rate per bank, so the January quota list is one of the most direct stock-selection signals in the entire market.

## Common misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "The SBV is just a slower version of the Fed — watch the policy rate."** No. The binding constraint in Vietnam is the credit *quota*, not the rate. In 2022 the SBV's refinancing rate moved only modestly, yet credit conditions tightened dramatically because the system quota and reserve drain choked lending; the VN-Index fell 43% with the policy rate barely moving. If you watch only the rate, you will miss the actual policy stance. The number that mattered was the quota near 14% and whether banks had room left to lend.

**Misconception 2: "The dong is roughly stable, so currency risk is negligible for a foreign investor."** It is stable *day to day* — that is the point of the band — but it crawls one-directionally weaker. From 2019 to 2024 the reference rate slid from 23,200 to 24,600, a 6% managed depreciation, and stress years saw faster steps. A foreign equity investor who ignored that bled 6%-plus over the period purely on FX, on top of any equity move. Stability inside the band is not the same as no currency risk; it is *predictable, persistent* currency risk.

**Misconception 3: "The SJC gold premium is a temporary distortion that will arbitrage away."** It has persisted for over a decade and has run as wide as 15-20%, because the arbitrage that would close it is *illegal* — gold imports require an SBV license and bar production is a state monopoly. A premium sustained by law does not converge on its own; it converges only when the SBV chooses to relax the monopoly (as it began to in 2024). Betting on convergence is really a bet on a specific legal reform, not on market forces.

**Misconception 4: "Vietnam has capital controls because its financial system is underdeveloped."** The controls are a *structural choice*, not a developmental lag. Running a managed dong plus an independent credit policy forces Vietnam to give up free capital flows — the impossible trinity guarantees it. The controls are the price of the other two policies, and they will loosen only as Vietnam decides to float the currency more or cede credit control, not merely as the system "modernizes".

**Misconception 5: "More credit growth is always bullish for the VN-Index."** Up to a point. But a quota pushed too high feeds asset bubbles (property and bond manias) that the SBV then has to lance, as it did with the 2022 crackdown. The bullish phase of the credit cycle is real, but the quota that inflates a bubble is the same lever that, when reversed or when a crackdown hits, produces the 43% drawdown. Position to the *cycle*, not to the simple "credit up = stocks up" rule.

## How it shows up in real markets

The clearest case is the **2022 squeeze**, which braided all three levers together. Globally, the Fed's hikes and a surging dollar pulled capital out of Vietnam; the SBV defended the dong by selling an estimated \$23 billion of reserves and widening the band to +/- 5%, draining dong liquidity. Domestically, the credit quota was near exhausted by mid-year and the SBV was *also* cracking down on the corporate-bond and property sectors. The simultaneous liquidity squeeze and confidence shock sent the VN-Index from 1,528 to 874 — a 43% fall — and blew the SJC gold premium wide as households fled the dong. The recovery came when the SBV *raised the credit target* late in the year and into 2023, the dollar eased, reserves rebuilt, and liquidity returned; the index climbed back to 1,267 by end-2024. Every leg of that round trip was driven by an SBV legal lever, not by an earnings cycle.

A second, quieter case is the **annual January ritual**. Each year the SBV's credit-target announcement and its first quota allocations move bank shares within days, because the allocation determines which banks can grow their loan books — and therefore their interest income — that year. Banks granted generous room outperform; banks frozen near zero (usually weak ones under restructuring) lag. This is a recurring, semi-predictable event that disciplined investors trade around the announcement.

A third is the **dong band-widening of October 2022** — a pure legal-lever move. By widening the band from +/- 3% to +/- 5%, the SBV signaled it was running low on appetite to burn reserves and would let the dong absorb more pressure. The market read it as a controlled-devaluation signal, and the dong promptly moved to the new weak edge. It is a clean example of a rule change (the band width, set by circular) repricing an asset (the dong) almost instantly.

A fourth case is the **2024 gold reform**, a clean demonstration that the SJC premium is a legal artifact, not a market one. Through early 2024 the premium blew out toward record levels — at times the equivalent of \$500-600 an ounce over world gold — as households piled into gold amid currency-depreciation worries. Rather than let the premium signal worsening dong distrust, the SBV intervened with the only tools that work on a legally created scarcity: it ran direct gold-sale auctions to flood domestic supply, began selling gold through the state commercial banks at managed prices, and the government signaled it would amend Decree 24 to end the SJC single-brand monopoly. The premium narrowed sharply in response — not because world arbitrage finally broke through, but because the SBV chose to loosen the legal chokehold. For a trader, the episode confirmed the rule: the premium converges when, and only when, the SBV decides to relax the monopoly, so trading the premium is trading a policy forecast, not a market mean-reversion.

## The playbook: how to trade the SBV's legal levers

Pull it together into a tradeable framework. The core idea is simple: in Vietnam, *liquidity is set by decree*, so you track the decrees, position to the credit-and-currency cycle, and respect the devaluation tail.

**Track three numbers, in this order.**

1. **The credit quota.** The January system target and the mid-year adjustments. Rising or topped-up quota = loosening = bullish for banks and rate-sensitive cyclicals. A low target or talk of "controlling risky-sector credit" = tightening = defensive.
2. **The dong band and reference path.** A reference the SBV is stepping weaker, plus a band-width widening, signals depreciation pressure and reserve loss — liquidity-negative. A stable reference signals calm.
3. **FX reserves.** The cleanest liquidity gauge. Rising reserves inject dong (ease); falling reserves drain it (tighten). Cross-check against the dollar and Vietnam's trade balance.

**Position banks and cyclicals to the quota cycle.** When the SBV loosens — raises the target, grants room, rebuilds reserves — overweight the banks granted the most room (their loan books and interest income grow) and the rate-sensitive cyclicals that feed on credit: property developers, construction, consumer discretionary, brokers (margin-lending volumes rise). When the SBV tightens, rotate to defensives and reduce margin exposure. The Vietnamese retail base is heavily margined, so liquidity swings are amplified in equity prices — a loosening rally and a tightening sell-off both overshoot.

#### Worked example: the margin channel amplifies the quota

Why do Vietnamese rallies and sell-offs overshoot? Because broker margin sits on top of bank credit, so a quota change is *levered* before it reaches the index. Suppose looser system credit lets brokers expand their margin books by **\$2 billion** (50 trillion dong). Retail investors typically run margin at roughly **1.5x** — for every dollar of their own equity they borrow about \$1.50 — so that \$2 billion of new margin supports buying power of:

```
extra buying power = $2bn × (1 + 1.5) ÷ 1.5 ... 
simpler: $2bn margin debt funds positions where margin debt is ~60% of the position
position value = $2bn ÷ 0.60 = $3.3bn of stock demand
```

So roughly **\$3.3 billion** of fresh demand hits a market whose daily turnover is often under \$1 billion — a powerful upward force. The danger is symmetric: when the SBV tightens and brokers pull margin, that same \$3.3 billion of positions must be sold, often into a falling market, triggering margin calls that force more selling. This is the mechanism behind the 2022 overshoot to the downside. The takeaway: the credit quota does not move the VN-Index one-for-one; it moves margin, and margin moves the index with leverage in both directions, which is why you size positions to the *cycle* and respect the violence of a tightening unwind.

**Respect the dong-devaluation tail.** A foreign investor in Vietnam is implicitly short the dong via the managed crawl — budget for roughly low-single-digit annual depreciation as a baseline drag, and recognize the tail risk of a faster step-devaluation during a strong-dollar squeeze (2022 saw the band widen and the reference jump). If your time horizon is long and your conviction is in the equities, you may accept the bleed; if you are leveraged or short-horizon, hedge the dong where you can, knowing the gated capital account makes hedging harder and costlier than in a freely convertible currency.

**Read the gold premium as a confidence gauge.** A blowing-out SJC premium signals households fleeing the dong — a warning of stress and possible SBV intervention. A narrowing premium (especially after the SBV signals import relaxation or auctions) signals returning confidence. It is a free, real-time sentiment indicator on the currency.

**What invalidates the view.** Three things would break the standard "track the quota, position to the cycle" playbook:

- **A move toward a freer float.** If the SBV widens the band materially further or moves toward a managed-float regime, the dong becomes two-way and more volatile, and the implicit short-dong trade changes character. Watch for legal signals here.
- **Abandonment or reform of the quota system.** The SBV has periodically discussed replacing the administrative quota with capital- and risk-based limits (more like Basel). If that reform actually lands, the January-quota signal loses its potency and the credit channel becomes price-driven — a regime change for how you read the SBV.
- **A reserve crisis.** If reserves fell decisively below the three-month-import floor and stayed there, the SBV would lose the ability to defend the dong, forcing either a disorderly devaluation or harsher capital controls — a tail that dominates everything else. The 2022 dip toward 2.9 months was a near-miss; a deeper, sustained breach would invalidate the orderly-crawl assumption entirely.

One more practical caution for the foreign investor specifically: the gated capital account changes not just your returns but your *exits*. Because the dong is not freely convertible and capital flows are procedurally controlled, getting money *out* of Vietnam can be slow and, in a stress scenario, slower still — exactly when you most want to leave. The 2022 squeeze did not see Vietnam restrict outflows, but the structural capacity to do so is always there, because the capital controls that make the managed dong possible are also the tools that could be tightened in a crisis. This asymmetry — easy in, potentially sticky out — is the deep tail risk of investing behind a managed-currency wall, and it is why position sizing in Vietnam should account for liquidity-of-exit, not just expected return. The legal architecture of those controls, and how they have behaved in past EM crises, is the subject of [capital controls and the legal limits on money flows](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/capital-controls-and-the-legal-limits-on-money-flows).

The throughline of the whole series applies cleanly here: a legal lever — a quota number, a band width, an import license — changes the rules of the game; liquidity and prices reprice ahead of the full effect; and the edge is reading the lever early and sizing the move. In most markets the central bank works through a price. In Vietnam it works through quantities set in law, and the investor who learns to read those quantities — the credit quota first, then the dong band and reserves, then the gold premium — sees the VN-Index's master cycle before it shows up in the index. The SBV is not a slower Fed. It is a different machine, and the controls that run it are written down where anyone willing to read decrees and circulars can find them.
