> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — An intervention is a central bank trading its own currency to move the price: to strengthen the yen it sells dollar reserves and buys yen in the spot market, and the only questions that matter are how much it spends, whether it offsets the cash at home, and whether the market believes it.
>
> - **Sterilized** intervention sells dollars but reabsorbs the home currency with a bond sale, so the money supply is unchanged — it is a pure FX bet. **Unsterilized** intervention lets the money supply change, so it doubles as easing or tightening and is far more powerful.
> - The cheapest tool is **words**. Jawboning can move a currency several percent for free — but only if the market believes the bank will follow through, so every empty threat spends down a finite stock of credibility.
> - **Size and surprise beat brute force.** Japan's largest single operation, in October 2022, sold about **\$43 billion** of reserves in a day; its 2024 operations hit a quiet holiday session on purpose so stop-losses did the work the reserves could not.
> - The one number to remember: in the autumn of 2022 Japan spent roughly **\$63 billion** defending the yen near **152** per dollar — real money, drawn from a finite war chest, against a market that trades **\$7.5 trillion** a day.

## The night Tokyo went to war with the market

Just before 5 p.m. New York time on **September 22, 2022**, the dollar was changing hands at almost **146 yen** — a level it had not seen in twenty-four years — and it was still climbing. The Bank of Japan had just finished a policy meeting where it did the one thing the market expected and feared: it left its interest rate pinned near zero while every other major central bank was hiking aggressively. The gap between what you earned holding dollars and what you earned holding yen had blown out to more than four percentage points, and that gap was a magnet pulling the yen down by the hour. Then, without warning, the yen lurched violently the other way. In a matter of minutes the dollar dropped more than five yen. Japan's Ministry of Finance had walked into the market and started selling dollars by the billion to buy back its own currency — its first yen-buying intervention since 1998.

It worked, for about three weeks. Then the selling pressure returned, because the thing that was actually pushing the yen down — that yawning interest-rate gap — had not moved an inch. So on **October 21**, with the dollar back above 151 and threatening 152, Tokyo did it again, and this time it spent even harder: roughly **\$43 billion** in a single operation, the largest one-day yen-buying intervention on record. Over those few weeks of autumn 2022 Japan burned through about **\$63 billion** of its foreign-exchange reserves — a sum that would fund a mid-sized country's entire annual budget — to fight a market that turns over more than seven trillion dollars *every single day*. Two years later, in 2024, it would do the same thing again, three more times, spending tens of billions on each.

This is what currency intervention looks like from the outside: a sudden, brutal, expensive lurch in the price, a finance ministry spending its hard-won savings to defend a number. But underneath that drama is a precise piece of machinery, and the whole point of this post is to take that machine apart. How does a central bank *actually* buy its own currency? Why does it sometimes "sterilize" the operation and sometimes not — and why does that single choice change everything about how powerful the intervention is? When is a sentence in a speech worth more than \$43 billion of reserves? And why, again and again, does the bank that wins not spend the most, but instead picks the moment when no one is watching and hits the market where it is thinnest? An exchange rate is the relative price of two monies; intervention is the rare moment a central bank stops *setting the conditions* for that price and starts *trading the price itself*. Let us see exactly how.

## Foundations: The intervention toolkit

Before we can take apart any single operation, we need the vocabulary, because "intervention" is a loose word that covers four genuinely different tools. Let us build them from zero. The cleanest way to organize them is by cost and by force: the cheapest tool moves nothing but expectations, and the most expensive tool moves the whole economy.

**The pair, the base, and the quote — a one-paragraph refresher.** You never own "a currency" in isolation. Every FX position is a *pair*: USD/JPY is the price of one US dollar measured in Japanese yen, so when USD/JPY goes *up* to 152, it takes *more* yen to buy one dollar, which means the yen has gotten *weaker* and the dollar *stronger*. The first currency (USD here) is the **base**; the second (JPY) is the **quote**. A central bank that wants to *strengthen the yen* therefore wants USD/JPY to go *down*. Keep that straight and everything else follows: defending the yen means pushing the pair down, which means selling dollars and buying yen. We lean on the rate-gap logic here rather than re-deriving it; for the full mechanism of how a rate differential drags a pair around, see [how monetary policy moves currencies](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-monetary-policy-moves-currencies-rate-differentials).

**Tool 1 — Verbal intervention (jawboning).** The central bank, or more often the finance ministry, says something. "We are watching FX moves with a high sense of urgency." "Excessive, one-sided moves are undesirable." "We will not rule out any options." No money changes hands. The bank is trying to move the price purely by changing what traders *expect* it to do next. This is free, and when the bank has a reputation for following through, it can be shockingly effective. It is also the only tool with no balance-sheet limit — you can talk forever — but it has a different limit: credibility, which is finite and which you spend a little of every time you threaten and do nothing.

**Tool 2 — Spot intervention (the actual buying and selling).** This is the headline act: the central bank's trading desk goes into the *spot* market — the market for immediate delivery of currency, as opposed to forwards or swaps, which we cover in [spot, forward, and swap](/blog/trading/forex/spot-forward-and-swap-the-three-ways-to-trade-a-currency) — and trades for real. To strengthen the yen it sells dollars from its reserves and buys yen. To weaken its currency (the Swiss did this for years) it does the reverse: it *prints* its own currency and uses it to buy foreign assets. Selling reserves is the operation with a hard ceiling — you can only sell dollars you actually have — while printing to buy has, in principle, no ceiling at all. That asymmetry, which we will return to, is one of the most important facts in this entire post.

**Tool 3 — The sterilization choice.** Every spot intervention leaves a side effect at home: buying yen *drains* yen out of the banking system; selling yen *floods* it in. The bank then decides whether to *offset* that side effect with a domestic operation (a bond sale or purchase). If it does, the intervention is **sterilized** — the money supply is left untouched, and the operation is a pure FX bet. If it does not, the intervention is **unsterilized**, and the change in the money supply means the bank has *also* eased or tightened policy. This is not a footnote; it is the single biggest determinant of how much an intervention actually moves the rate, and we will give it its own section.

**Tool 4 — The policy rate itself, weaponized for FX.** When all else fails, the bank can change the thing that drives the rate gap in the first place: its interest rate. A defending central bank can hike rates to make its currency more attractive to hold. This is the nuclear option, because the policy rate is set for the *domestic* economy — inflation and employment — and cranking it up to defend a currency means deliberately inflicting pain on your own borrowers, your own housing market, your own businesses. The most famous example is Britain in 1992, hiking from 10% to 12% to 15% in a single panicked day to defend the pound — and failing anyway, the story told in [Soros and the Bank of England, 1992](/blog/trading/finance/soros-bank-of-england-1992-black-wednesday).

Those four tools form a ladder, and central banks climb it roughly in order — words first, because they are free; then spot operations, because they are direct; then rate hikes, only when the currency's fall threatens the whole economy. The higher you climb, the more it costs.

![The intervention toolkit from cheapest verbal to costliest rate hikes](/imgs/blogs/how-central-banks-intervene-in-the-currency-market-5.png)

There is one more piece of foundational vocabulary we need before the mechanics: **reserves**. A central bank's foreign-exchange reserves are its stockpile of *other countries'* money and assets — mostly US Treasury bonds, plus some euros, yen, gold, and deposits. These reserves are the ammunition for any yen-buying-style defence, because to buy your own currency you must sell *something*, and that something is your reserves. A country with \$3 trillion of reserves (China) can intervene almost without limit; a country with \$82 billion (Vietnam, as of 2024) has a war chest measured in weeks of firepower, not years. We will see exactly how that constraint bites later, but hold onto the image now: spot intervention to *strengthen* your currency is a finite resource you are burning, and the market can count your bullets.

## The mechanics: what actually happens when a central bank buys yen

Let us follow the money through a single, concrete operation, because the mechanics are simpler than the drama suggests — and the simplicity is exactly what makes the constraints so unforgiving.

Suppose the Bank of Japan's intervention desk (acting on the Ministry of Finance's instructions — in Japan the MoF owns the reserves and decides, the BoJ executes) decides to defend the yen. It holds a large pile of US Treasury bonds in its reserves. The desk does roughly this: it sells some of those Treasuries (or uses dollar cash it already holds) and, in the spot FX market, it *sells those dollars and buys yen*. The counterparties are the big commercial and investment banks that make markets in USD/JPY. Those banks hand over yen and receive dollars. The new, sudden, enormous demand for yen pushes the *price* of yen up — which, remember, means USD/JPY goes *down*, the yen strengthens.

![How a central bank sells reserves and buys its own currency to lift it](/imgs/blogs/how-central-banks-intervene-in-the-currency-market-2.png)

That is the entire mechanism. There is no magic lever, no secret button — it is a very large market order, placed by an actor with very deep pockets, in the same market where everyone else trades. And that framing immediately tells you the central bank's three problems. First, the market is *enormous*: spot USD/JPY is part of a market that turns over more than two trillion dollars a day in spot alone, so even a \$20 billion order, while large, is a fraction of a single day's flow. Second, the effect is *temporary by default*: the central bank's buying pushes the rate, but the moment it stops, the original forces (the rate gap pulling the yen down) reassert themselves. Third, the ammunition is *finite*: every dollar sold is a dollar gone from the reserves, and when the reserves run low the market knows the bank cannot keep going.

#### Worked example: a \$20 billion yen-buying operation

Suppose USD/JPY is trading at **150.00** and the Ministry of Finance authorizes a **\$20 billion** intervention to strengthen the yen. The desk sells \$20,000,000,000 and buys yen. At a rate of 150 yen per dollar, that order purchases:

\$20,000,000,000 × 150 yen/\$ = **¥3,000,000,000,000** — three trillion yen of demand hitting the market in a compressed window.

Now what does that do to the price? In a thin, jumpy market, a flood of buying that size can knock the pair down several yen — say from 150.00 to **145.00**, a roughly **3.3%** strengthening of the yen. But here is the catch that defines intervention economics. The MoF *sold* \$20 billion of reserves to do this. If the yen later weakens *back* to, say, 155 (because nothing fundamental changed), then to *re-buy* those same \$20 billion of reserves it would now need 155 × \$20B = ¥3,100,000,000,000 — and it only received ¥3,000,000,000,000 when it sold. In dollar terms the operation strengthened the yen by 3.3% for a day; in reserve terms, if it fails, the country has simply transferred wealth to the speculators who sold yen to it cheaply and bought it back dearly. **The takeaway: a yen-buying intervention is a finite bet that the rate will not snap back, and if it does, the central bank — not the market — eats the loss.**

This is why intervention is never a *strategy* on its own. It is a *tactic*: it buys time, smooths a disorderly move, and gives the underlying drivers (the rate gap, the policy stance) a chance to catch up. A central bank that intervenes to fight fundamentals it refuses to change is, in the bleak phrase of the trading floor, throwing good reserves after a bad rate.

A few details of execution matter for understanding what you are watching. First, *who* decides and *who* trades are often different people. In Japan the Ministry of Finance owns the reserves and gives the order; the Bank of Japan merely acts as its agent on the trading floor. In the United States the Treasury and the Federal Reserve share authority, each holding half the operation. This split matters because the FX-policy decision is a *political* one — it is a finance ministry choosing to spend the nation's savings — even though the execution is a technical one. Second, the desk rarely shows its full hand at once. A skilled intervention is often *worked* through the day in waves rather than fired as one giant order, sometimes through multiple banks at once so no single counterparty can see the whole flow and front-run the rest. Third, the operation is frequently *coordinated* — or at least signalled — across central banks. The single most powerful intervention of the modern era, the Plaza Accord of 1985, was a deliberate joint action by the United States, Japan, West Germany, Britain, and France to weaken an overvalued dollar; the dollar index fell from about 123 to 85 over the following two years. Coordinated intervention is rare, but when several major central banks act together the market knows it is facing the combined firepower and resolve of the entire official sector — and that is a fight almost no speculator wants to pick.

One more execution subtlety is worth naming because it confuses even experienced observers: intervention can be *announced* or *secret*, and central banks choose deliberately. An *announced* intervention — where the bank confirms it acted — maximizes the signalling effect: it puts the bank's reputation publicly on the line and warns every speculator that the official sector is now engaged. A *secret* intervention — where the bank acts but neither confirms nor denies — keeps the market guessing, which can be more effective at sowing the two-way fear that makes shorts nervous. Japan in 2022 *announced* its September operation loudly (to maximize the warning) but stayed coy about some later 2024 operations, letting traders wonder whether a given lurch was the MoF or just the market. Months later, the monthly reserve and intervention disclosures confirm what happened, but in the heat of the moment the ambiguity is itself a weapon.

## Sterilized vs unsterilized: the choice that changes everything

Here is the distinction that most explanations skip, and it is the heart of why two interventions of identical size can have wildly different effects. Go back to our \$20 billion yen-buying operation. When the central bank bought ¥3 trillion of yen from the commercial banks, where did those yen *come from*? They came *out* of the domestic banking system — the banks handed over yen and received dollars, so there are now three trillion fewer yen sloshing around in domestic money markets. Buying your own currency *drains* it. That is a tightening of domestic liquidity, whether the bank wanted it or not.

Now the bank faces a choice. It can leave that drain in place — in which case yen have become *scarcer*, which tends to push *up* domestic interest rates, which makes yen *more* attractive to hold, which *reinforces* the FX intervention. That is **unsterilized** intervention: the FX operation and a monetary tightening, bundled together. Or the bank can *offset* the drain by doing a separate domestic operation — buying ¥3 trillion of government bonds from the banks, pushing those same yen right back into the system. Now the money supply is exactly where it started, and the only thing that changed is the FX position. That is **sterilized** intervention: a pure currency bet, with the domestic monetary effect surgically removed.

![Sterilized intervention offsets the cash while unsterilized lets the money supply change](/imgs/blogs/how-central-banks-intervene-in-the-currency-market-3.png)

Why would a central bank ever sterilize, given that the unsterilized version is more powerful? Because it usually does *not want* the monetary side effect. Japan in 2022 wanted to support the yen, but it absolutely did *not* want to tighten domestic policy — its whole strategy was to keep rates near zero to fight decades of deflation. An unsterilized yen-buying intervention would have meant accidentally tightening into a fragile economy, exactly what the BoJ was trying to avoid. So Japan sterilized: it offset the yen drain, leaving the money supply alone, and accepted that its intervention would therefore be *weaker*. This is the recurring tension — the more powerful tool (unsterilized) usually conflicts with the bank's domestic goals, and the tool compatible with those goals (sterilized) is the weaker one.

The empirical literature is blunt about this. Sterilized intervention, the kind most developed-market central banks actually do, has at best a *modest and temporary* effect on the exchange rate. It works mainly through two thin channels: a *signalling* channel (the intervention tells the market what the bank wants and might do next) and a *portfolio-balance* channel (by changing the relative supply of the two currencies' bonds, it nudges the price — but only a little, because both bond markets are vast). Unsterilized intervention, by contrast, works because it is *actual monetary policy* — and monetary policy genuinely moves currencies, as the macro series details in [the central-bank toolkit](/blog/trading/macro-trading/central-bank-toolkit-rates-qe-qt-forward-guidance).

#### Worked example: sterilizing the operation with a bond sale

Take the same **\$20 billion** intervention at **150** yen per dollar, which drained **¥3 trillion** from the banks. To sterilize, the central bank's domestic-operations desk now sells ¥3 trillion of Japanese government bonds *back* to those same banks (or, equivalently, buys them — direction depends on whether it is mopping up or injecting; here the bank wants to *re-inject* the drained yen, so it buys bonds, paying ¥3 trillion of fresh yen into the system).

Let us tally the bank's two desks:

- FX desk: −\$20B reserves, +¥3,000,000,000,000 bought (yen drained from banks).
- Domestic desk: +¥3,000,000,000,000 injected back via bond purchases.
- Net effect on the yen money supply: ¥3,000,000,000,000 − ¥3,000,000,000,000 = **¥0**.

The money supply ends unchanged; only the central bank's *balance sheet composition* shifted — fewer dollar reserves, more domestic bonds. The intervention is now "FX-only." **The takeaway: sterilization is just a second, offsetting trade at home that quarantines the FX operation from monetary policy — which is exactly why a sterilized intervention is weaker, because it deliberately throws away the most powerful channel it has.**

There is a beautiful, slightly perverse corollary here. Because sterilized intervention is weak, central banks that *only* sterilize are essentially betting on the *signalling* channel — they are spending reserves not to overwhelm the market mechanically but to *demonstrate resolve*, to put money behind their words. Which means even the spot operation is, at its core, a credibility play. And that brings us to the dimension that turns out to matter more than size: belief.

It is worth pausing on the two channels through which a sterilized operation works at all, because they explain why the effect is real but small. The **portfolio-balance channel** says that when the bank sells dollar bonds and buys yen, it changes the *relative supply* of the two countries' assets in private hands — there are now slightly more dollar bonds and slightly fewer yen for investors to hold, so to persuade them to hold that mix the prices must shift a little, nudging the exchange rate. The trouble is that both the US Treasury market and the JGB market are measured in the *trillions*, so a \$20 billion operation is a pebble in an ocean — the portfolio-balance push is genuine but tiny. The **signalling channel** is usually the bigger of the two: the operation is a costly, credible message about what the bank wants the rate to be and what it is willing to do next. A bank that puts \$20 billion of its own money on the line is making a statement no press release can match, because talk is cheap and reserves are not. This is why the size of an operation matters less for its *mechanical* impact than for its *informational* impact — a surprisingly large operation screams resolve, and resolve, not the pebble in the ocean, is what flips positioning.

## Words as ammunition: verbal intervention and the credibility account

The cheapest, fastest, and most-used intervention tool is the one that costs nothing to deploy: talking. Officials call this "verbal intervention" and traders call it "jawboning," and on any given week it is far more common than an actual spot operation. A finance minister stands up and says the recent FX move has been "rapid and one-sided" and that authorities are "ready to take appropriate action." Nothing is bought. Nothing is sold. And yet the currency can jump one or two percent in seconds, because the market has just updated its estimate of the *probability* that a real, reserve-burning intervention is coming — and traders who are short the currency now face the risk of being run over, so they cover.

The mechanism here is pure expectations, which is why it lives or dies on one thing: credibility. A central bank's threats are believed in exact proportion to its track record of following through. The first time Japan said "we are watching with urgency" and then actually spent \$20 billion, every subsequent warning gained teeth — traders learned that those words were not a bluff. This is why officials develop an escalating vocabulary that the market learns to read like a thermometer. "We are monitoring developments" is mild. "Moves are excessive and one-sided" is a yellow card. "We will not rule out any options" or "we are ready to act decisively" is the language that immediately precedes a real operation. Seasoned FX traders parse these phrases the way a poker player reads a tell.

But credibility is a *finite account*, and this is the deep insight of verbal intervention. Every time a bank threatens and does *nothing*, it withdraws from that account. Threaten three times with no action and the fourth threat moves nothing — the market has learned the words are empty. The European Central Bank's Mario Draghi spent years building a credibility account and then cashed it in 2012 with three words — "whatever it takes" — that *ended* a crisis with no immediate spending at all, precisely because the market believed he *would* spend. The flip side is the central bank that cries wolf: it discovers, at the worst possible moment, that its words are worthless and only reserves will do. The formal version of this — credibility as a commitment device, and why "don't fight the Fed" is a statement about belief, not firepower — is laid out in [the central-bank game](/blog/trading/game-theory/the-central-bank-game-credibility-commitment-and-dont-fight-the-fed).

#### Worked example: the dollar cost of an empty threat

Suppose a central bank with **\$100 billion** of reserves repeatedly threatens to defend a level but never acts. Model its credibility as a probability `p` that the market assigns to a real intervention following any given warning. Say each *honored* threat (words followed by action) keeps `p` near **0.7**, so a warning alone moves the currency about 1.5% as shorts cover. But each *empty* threat halves `p`: 0.7 → 0.35 → 0.18 → 0.09.

By the fourth empty warning, with `p` ≈ 0.09, the expected move from a verbal threat is roughly 1.5% × (0.09 / 0.7) ≈ **0.2%** — essentially nothing. The bank has talked its words down to worthlessness. Now, to move the currency the same 1.5% it once got for *free*, it must spend *real* reserves — and against a determined market, recovering a 1.5% move can cost many billions. **The takeaway: verbal intervention is not free at all; it is paid for in credibility, and a bank that spends that credibility carelessly converts a tool that cost nothing into one that costs tens of billions.**

This is why the most effective central banks are *stingy* with both words and reserves — they intervene rarely, decisively, and only when they mean it, so that every threat and every operation lands with full force. The Swiss National Bank's "we will enforce this floor with the utmost determination" was believed for years *because* the SNB had a reputation for doing exactly what it said. When that credibility finally broke, it broke catastrophically — which is its own lesson, and the subject of the next section.

## The Japan case: a textbook defence, and its limits

Japan's yen defences of 2022 and 2024 are the cleanest modern case study because they show, in real numbers, both the power and the limits of intervention. Let us walk the timeline.

By autumn 2022 the yen was in free fall, and the reason was no mystery: the US Federal Reserve was hiking rates aggressively to fight inflation while the Bank of Japan held its policy rate near zero. The US-Japan two-year rate differential blew out to over **four percentage points**. Money flooded out of yen and into dollars to capture that gap — the carry trade in its purest form, which the series unpacks in [the carry trade](/blog/trading/forex/the-carry-trade-getting-paid-to-hold-a-currency). USD/JPY ran from 115 at the start of 2022 to past 145 by September. On **September 22, 2022**, with the pair near 146, the MoF intervened for the first time since 1998, selling roughly **\$20 billion** of reserves. On **October 21**, with the pair near 152, it spent again — about **\$43 billion** in a day, the largest single yen-buying operation ever recorded. Total for that autumn: about **\$63 billion**.

Did it work? Partly, and instructively. The interventions *did* slow and then cap the move — USD/JPY did not run away to 160 in 2022 — but they did *not* reverse the trend, because the rate gap that caused it was untouched. The yen stabilized, then drifted weaker again into 2023 and 2024 as the gap persisted. The interventions bought *time and order*; they did not buy a new equilibrium. That is intervention working exactly as theory predicts: a sterilized operation against a powerful fundamental can smooth the path but cannot bend it.

Then came 2024, and the more sophisticated chapter. In April and May 2024, with the pair pressing past 157 and then 160 — a 34-year low — Japan intervened again, spending about **\$36 billion** and **\$23 billion**. But the most revealing operation came in July. On **July 11, 2024**, Japan spent roughly **\$37 billion** — and it chose its moment with surgical care: a US holiday-thinned, low-liquidity session, immediately after a soft US inflation print had already nudged the dollar lower. By hitting a thin market when it was already moving its way, Japan got *maximum bang per dollar of reserve spent*. The pair tumbled, stop-losses triggered, and the move fed on itself.

![USD JPY around the 2024 interventions showing the level Tokyo defended](/imgs/blogs/how-central-banks-intervene-in-the-currency-market-4.png)

And then the market did the rest of Japan's work *for free*. A few weeks later, on **August 5, 2024**, the Bank of Japan finally hiked its policy rate — actually narrowing the rate gap — and the entire over-loved yen-carry trade unwound in a violent cascade. USD/JPY collapsed from about 162 in early July toward 142 by August 5, and the VIX spiked to an intraday **65.7**. Japan had spent tens of billions slowing the fall; in the end it was the *combination* of well-timed surprise intervention *and* an actual change in the rate gap that turned the yen. That carry-unwind mechanism — the steamroller that eventually flattens the trade — is the subject of [carry crashes](/blog/trading/forex/carry-crashes-picking-up-pennies-in-front-of-a-steamroller).

The lesson of Japan is a two-parter. First: even a rich, credible, technically excellent central bank cannot reverse a currency trend driven by a rate gap it refuses to close — intervention smooths, it does not steer. Second: the *way* you intervene matters as much as the *amount* — the \$37 billion of July 2024, fired into a thin market at the right moment, did far more per dollar than a clumsy operation into a deep, liquid, two-way market ever could. Which brings us to the principle that quietly governs all of this.

## Why surprise and credibility beat brute force

If you remember one idea from this entire post, make it this: in currency intervention, *how* and *when* you spend matters more than *how much*. A central bank is not trying to out-muscle a \$7.5-trillion-a-day market by sheer volume — it cannot, no one can. It is trying to *change the market's mind*, to flip positioning, to make the speculators leaning against it suddenly want to be on its side. And the tools for that are surprise, timing, and credibility, not brute force.

Consider the difference between a *telegraphed* defence and a *surprise* one. If a central bank announces in advance that it will defend, say, the 152 level, it has handed the market a free option. Speculators know exactly where the floor is. They can sell the currency right up to 151.99, confident the bank will be buying at 152, and then sell *into* the bank's buying — the bank becomes the buyer of last resort, absorbing every speculative sale at a known price, draining its reserves into a wall of supply that knows precisely where it is. A telegraphed level is a target painted on the bank's own back.

Now consider a *surprise* operation into a thin market. No one knows it is coming. The bank hits the market when liquidity is low — a holiday session, an off-hour — so each dollar of selling moves the price further. The sudden lurch triggers *stop-loss orders*: speculators who were short the currency had protective orders to buy it back if it moved against them, and those orders now fire automatically, *adding* to the bank's buying. The bank's \$37 billion is amplified by a cascade of forced covering it did not have to pay for. The shorts are squeezed; the move sticks; and crucially, the *fear* lingers — every speculator who got run over will hesitate to lean against the bank again. That hesitation is worth more than the reserves spent, because it makes the *next* defence cheaper.

![Why a surprise into a thin market squeezes shorts while a telegraphed defence fails](/imgs/blogs/how-central-banks-intervene-in-the-currency-market-8.png)

This is also why a *peg* — a fully telegraphed, permanent commitment to a level — is the most dangerous game of all. A peg tells every speculator exactly where the line is and dares them to break it. As long as the market believes the bank's commitment, the peg holds with little spending. But the moment the market smells weakness — sees the reserves draining, does the arithmetic on the bullets left — the peg becomes a one-way bet: there is no downside to attacking it, because if it holds you lose nothing, and if it breaks you make a fortune. That is the speculative attack, and it is a coordination game where the attackers' beliefs about *each other* matter as much as the fundamentals; the formal model lives in [bank runs as coordination games](/blog/trading/game-theory/bank-runs-as-coordination-games-diamond-dybvig-and-svb), and the spectrum from soft managed floats to brittle hard pegs is the subject of the sibling post on [pegs, bands, and crawling pegs](/blog/trading/forex/pegs-bands-and-crawling-pegs-the-spectrum-of-fx-regimes).

#### Worked example: the cost when a defence fails

Take a central bank defending a peg at **1.20** (its currency at 1.20 per euro, a floor it has promised to hold). It has **\$50 billion** of reserves committed to buying its own currency whenever the market tries to push below 1.20. Speculators sell **\$50 billion** worth at 1.20, and the bank dutifully buys it all — reserves now near zero. The market, watching the reserves disappear, sells *more*. With no ammunition left, the bank capitulates and lets the currency float. It instantly drops to, say, **0.85** — a roughly **29%** collapse, exactly what happened to EUR/CHF when the Swiss floor broke on **January 15, 2015**.

Now tally the damage. The speculators bought the currency from the bank at 1.20 and can now buy it in the open market at 0.85. Every unit the bank sold to defend the floor, it sold *too cheap by* (1.20 − 0.85) / 1.20 ≈ **29%**. On the \$50 billion the bank spent, that is roughly **\$14.6 billion** of value handed straight to the speculators who attacked — the bank bought high and the market bought low, and the difference is the bank's loss. **The takeaway: a failed defence is the most expensive outcome in all of FX — the bank not only loses the fight but transfers a fortune to the very speculators it was fighting, which is why a bank that cannot win should never start.**

The deeper point is that brute force *invites* the attack it is trying to repel. A bank that visibly tries to overpower the market with size alone advertises a known, finite resource and a known target. A bank that wins instead manages *beliefs*: it keeps its intentions ambiguous, it acts when the market least expects it, and it preserves a reputation for follow-through so that most of the time its *words* are enough and the reserves never have to move. The reserves are the ultimate backstop; the credibility is the actual weapon.

## Reserves as ammunition: counting the bullets

We have said repeatedly that reserves are finite and the market can count them. Let us make that concrete, because it is the constraint that separates a central bank that can intervene at will from one that is one bad week from capitulation.

Reserves are a country's stockpile of foreign money and assets — for most countries, dominated by US Treasury bonds, which are liquid, safe, and (crucially) denominated in the dollars you need to sell when you defend your currency. A rich, surplus-running economy accumulates reserves naturally over years; a deficit country has to buy them, often with borrowed money. The size of the war chest sets the size of the fight you can pick. China's reserves of roughly \$3 trillion mean it can defend the yuan almost indefinitely. Japan's reserves of over \$1 trillion let it spend \$63 billion in a season and barely dent the stockpile. But for an emerging market, reserves are measured not in trillions but in *months of imports* — the rule-of-thumb adequacy yardstick is that you want enough reserves to cover at least three months of your country's import bill, so that even if all foreign financing vanished, you could keep the lights on for a quarter.

How does a country *build* that war chest in the first place? The same intervention machinery, run in reverse. When a managed-float economy is running a trade surplus and attracting foreign investment — as Vietnam did through the 2010s — dollars flow *in*, which would tend to push the local currency *up* and make exports less competitive. To prevent that appreciation, the central bank does the mirror image of a defence: it *prints* local currency and *buys* the incoming dollars, parking them in reserves. This is intervention to keep the currency *weak*, and it builds the stockpile that will later be spent keeping the currency *strong* when the tide turns. But it has a side effect — printing local currency to buy dollars *floods* the domestic system with money, which is inflationary, so the bank must then *sterilize* the inflow by selling domestic bonds to mop up the excess. So the very same sterilization mechanism we met earlier runs constantly in the background of a reserve-accumulating economy, not as a one-off crisis tool but as routine plumbing. The reserves that defend the dong in a bad year were accumulated, sterilized dollar by sterilized dollar, in the good years — which is precisely why a sudden, fast drawdown is so alarming: it is spending in weeks what took years of disciplined accumulation to build.

![Vietnam FX reserves showing the drawdown defending the dong in 2022](/imgs/blogs/how-central-banks-intervene-in-the-currency-market-7.png)

Vietnam is the textbook case of a small war chest under pressure. The State Bank of Vietnam built its reserves up to about **\$109 billion** by 2021 — a hard-won buffer accumulated over years of trade surpluses, remittances, and foreign direct investment. Then 2022 hit: the Fed's aggressive hiking strengthened the dollar against everything, including the dong, and the SBV had to sell dollars to slow the dong's slide. By the end of 2022, reserves had fallen to about **\$87 billion** — roughly **\$22 billion spent** defending the currency in a single year, dropping coverage to under three months of imports, below the adequacy line. That is what counting bullets looks like in practice: an emerging-market central bank that started 2022 with a comfortable buffer ended it visibly depleted, and the market knew it. The full playbook for how an EM central bank like the SBV manages a managed float — bands, crawls, room limits, and the arithmetic of reserve adequacy — is the subject of the sibling post on [FX reserves and intervention in an emerging market](/blog/trading/forex/fx-reserves-and-intervention-in-an-emerging-market).

#### Worked example: how many weeks of firepower does a war chest buy?

Suppose an emerging market has **\$80 billion** of reserves and is defending its currency against persistent outflows of about **\$2 billion per week** (foreign investors pulling out, importers front-loading dollar purchases). To keep coverage above the three-month-of-imports adequacy line, with monthly imports of about **\$30 billion**, it must keep at least 3 × \$30B = **\$90 billion**... but it only has \$80 billion, so it is *already below* the line and every dollar spent makes it worse.

Even ignoring the adequacy floor and spending down toward a hard zero, \$80B / \$2B per week = **40 weeks** of firepower at the current burn rate — but the burn rate is not constant. As reserves visibly fall, speculators pile on, *raising* the outflow to \$4 billion, then \$6 billion a week, and the 40 weeks collapse to a handful. **The takeaway: a war chest does not deplete linearly — the closer it gets to empty, the faster the market drains it, because falling reserves are themselves the signal that triggers the attack, so reserve defences tend to fail suddenly rather than gradually.**

The asymmetry we flagged earlier now comes into full focus. A central bank trying to *strengthen* its currency must *sell* reserves, and reserves are finite — it can be overwhelmed. But a central bank trying to *weaken* its currency *prints* its own money to buy foreign assets, and printing is, in principle, unlimited. This is why the Swiss could hold their floor *capping* the franc's strength for years by printing francs — until the cost became politically and financially unbearable, which is the cost we turn to now.

## The cost of one-sided intervention: the Swiss balance sheet

There is a special kind of intervention that has no reserve limit and yet still fails: the one where a central bank fights to keep its currency *weak*. Because weakening means printing your own money to buy foreign assets, you can never "run out" of ammunition — you have a printing press. But you can run out of *willingness*, because every unit you print to hold the line piles up on your balance sheet as a swelling mountain of foreign assets, and that mountain carries enormous risk.

The Swiss National Bank is the defining example. After the 2011 eurozone crisis, terrified investors poured money into the franc as a haven, driving it so strong it threatened to wreck Swiss exporters. So in September 2011 the SNB declared a **floor**: it would not let the euro fall below **1.20** francs, and it would "enforce this with the utmost determination," printing unlimited francs to buy euros whenever the market tried to push EUR/CHF below 1.20. For three and a half years it worked, and the market believed it. But holding that floor meant the SNB had to keep buying euros, and the euros piled up. Its balance sheet — visible in the relentless climb of its *sight deposits*, the franc liabilities it created to buy those euros — ballooned toward the size of the entire Swiss economy.

![SNB sight deposits ballooning while defending the EUR CHF floor](/imgs/blogs/how-central-banks-intervene-in-the-currency-market-6.png)

By January 2015, the math had turned untenable. The euro was weakening for its own reasons (the ECB was about to launch massive quantitative easing), which meant holding the 1.20 floor would require the SNB to print *even more* francs to buy *even more* of a depreciating currency — accumulating ever-larger losses on a balance sheet already swollen to nation-sized proportions. On **January 15, 2015**, with no warning, the SNB abandoned the floor. EUR/CHF collapsed from 1.20 to as low as **0.85** intraday before settling near parity — a move so violent it bankrupted several retail FX brokers and inflicted billions in losses on anyone who had been short the franc on the assumption the floor would hold forever.

The Swiss case completes our picture of intervention's limits. A reserve-selling defence (to strengthen) fails when you run out of *bullets*. A money-printing defence (to weaken) never runs out of bullets — but it fails when the *balance-sheet cost* of holding the line, and the losses you will book when it breaks, finally exceed your willingness to keep paying. Both are limits; both are countable by the market; and both mean the same thing: intervention against fundamentals is a holding action, never a permanent victory. The franc was "too strong" for fundamental haven-demand reasons, and no amount of printing could change those reasons — it could only postpone the reckoning, at a mounting cost, until the SNB decided the bill was too high.

## Common misconceptions

**"Intervention means a central bank can set its currency wherever it wants."** No. A central bank can *push* the price, smooth a disorderly move, and buy time — but against a powerful fundamental driver like a rate gap, intervention is a holding action, not a steering wheel. Japan spent \$63 billion in 2022 and the yen kept weakening into 2024 because the rate gap that caused the weakness was untouched. The thing that finally turned the yen was the BoJ *hiking* — changing the fundamental — combined with a carry unwind, not the spot operations alone. Intervention moves the price by some percent for some weeks; fundamentals move it by tens of percent over years.

**"Bigger intervention is always more effective."** No — and this is the single most important correction. Japan's most *effective* 2024 operation, the \$37 billion of July 11, worked not because it was the largest but because it was fired into a thin, holiday-quiet market that was already moving Japan's way, triggering a cascade of stop-losses that amplified every dollar spent. A clumsy, telegraphed defence into a deep two-way market burns reserves with little effect. *Surprise, timing, and credibility* are the force multipliers; raw size, deployed badly, is just expensive noise.

**"Sterilized and unsterilized intervention are basically the same thing."** Not even close. Sterilized intervention offsets the domestic money-supply effect and is therefore a *weak*, mostly-signalling tool — most developed-market interventions are sterilized and have only modest, temporary effects. Unsterilized intervention *is* monetary policy bolted onto an FX operation, and it is far more powerful precisely because it changes the money supply and hence domestic rates. The choice to sterilize is usually forced by the bank's domestic goals, and it is exactly what caps the intervention's power.

**"Verbal intervention is just talk — it can't really move a currency."** It moves currencies all the time, sometimes by 1–2% in seconds, because it changes the market's estimate of the probability of a real operation. But it works *only* on the fuel of credibility: a bank that follows through builds an account it can later draw on with words alone, while a bank that bluffs repeatedly finds its threats worthless at the moment it needs them most. Words are not free — they are paid for in credibility, the most valuable and least visible reserve a central bank holds.

**"A central bank defending its currency is fighting a fair fight."** Often it is fighting a *rigged* one — rigged against itself. The moment a defended level is known and the bank's reserves are visibly draining, speculators face a one-way bet: attacking the level costs little if the bank holds and pays a fortune if it breaks. That asymmetry is why telegraphed pegs invite the attacks that destroy them, and why the smart defence keeps its intentions ambiguous and its reputation intact so that most of the time the fight never has to happen.

## How it shows up in real markets

For anyone reading a currency, intervention shows up as a set of recognizable signatures and tradeable signals. Here is how to spot it and how to think about it.

**The sudden, sharp, counter-trend lurch.** The clearest tell of a live spot intervention is a price that has been moving steadily one way suddenly snapping violently the other, often in a thin session, with no obvious news. USD/JPY dropping five yen in minutes on September 22, 2022, with the BoJ on hold and nothing else happening, was the unmistakable signature of Tokyo in the market. When you see a major pair lurch against its trend with no headline behind it, "central bank" should be your first hypothesis.

**The escalating verbal ladder.** Long before reserves move, officials talk, and the vocabulary escalates on a schedule the market learns to read. When Japanese officials moved from "monitoring developments" to "moves are excessive and one-sided" to "we will not rule out any options," each rung raised the probability of a real operation and moved the yen a little on its own. Reading FX is partly reading these statements like a thermometer — the heat in the language tells you how close real spending is.

**The reserve data and the ammunition count.** Markets watch reserve figures obsessively for managed-float and pegged currencies, because falling reserves are the countdown clock on a defence. When Vietnam's reserves fell from \$109 billion toward \$87 billion across 2022, anyone watching knew the SBV's room to keep defending the dong was shrinking, which is itself a bearish signal on the currency. For a pegged currency, the reserve trend *is* the trade: a peg with rising reserves is unbreakable; a peg with collapsing reserves is a coiled spring. This is the EM "wrecking ball" dynamic the dollar imposes on the rest of the world, traced in [the dollar as a wrecking ball](/blog/trading/forex/the-dollar-as-a-wrecking-ball-for-emerging-markets).

**The carry trade's relationship to intervention.** Intervention and the carry trade are intimately linked. A wide rate gap pulls a funding currency like the yen relentlessly weaker via the carry trade, and intervention is the authorities pushing back against that flow. The danger for a carry trader is precisely the *combination* event Japan delivered in 2024: a well-timed intervention that caps the move, followed by an actual policy change (the BoJ hike) that reverses the rate gap, triggering a violent unwind. A carry position is short the very volatility that intervention-plus-policy can manufacture, which is the whole thesis of [carry and volatility](/blog/trading/forex/carry-and-volatility-the-relationship-that-runs-the-trade).

**The options market's fingerprints.** Intervention risk shows up in the FX options market as expensive protection against a sharp move in the defended direction. When the market fears a yen-strengthening intervention, the *risk reversal* — the price gap between calls and puts — skews toward yen-strength protection, and implied volatility for short-dated USD/JPY options jumps. A trader can read intervention risk straight off the vol surface; the mechanics of that skew live in the options series' [trading skew and risk reversals](/blog/trading/options-volatility/trading-skew-risk-reversals-collars-and-the-shape-of-fear). The fingerprints of an *anticipated* intervention are often visible in options prices before any reserve ever moves.

The practical upshot for reading a currency is this: when a managed or defended currency is under pressure, build a small mental dashboard — the rate gap (the fundamental driver), the verbal temperature (how close real action is), the reserve trend (how much ammunition is left), and the options skew (what the market is paying to protect against intervention). Those four together tell you not just *whether* the central bank will act, but whether its action can possibly work.

## The takeaway: intervention is a bet on belief, not a lever on price

Strip away the drama of a \$43 billion day and the lesson of currency intervention is almost philosophical. A central bank that intervenes is not pulling a lever that sets the price; it is placing a *bet* — a bet that it can change the market's mind faster than the fundamentals can change the market's mind back. Sometimes that bet is sound: the move it is fighting is genuinely disorderly, a temporary overshoot, and a sharp, surprising, credible operation can smooth it and let the underlying drivers catch up. Sometimes the bet is hopeless: the bank is fighting a rate gap or a haven flow it refuses to change at the source, and every dollar of reserves it spends is a dollar transferred to the speculators who will eventually win.

The four tools form a hierarchy of cost and force, and the wisest central banks climb the ladder reluctantly. They prefer *words*, because words spend credibility rather than reserves, and credibility — uniquely — *grows* with use when the bank follows through. They turn to *spot operations* sparingly and surgically, picking the thin moment and the surprise angle so that each dollar of finite ammunition is amplified by the market's own stop-losses. They *sterilize* when they must protect domestic policy, accepting a weaker intervention as the price. And they reach for the *policy rate* — the nuclear option — only when the currency's fall threatens the whole economy, because that tool inflicts collateral damage on every borrower in the country.

So when you read a currency under pressure and you see the central bank step in, do not ask "how much can it spend?" Ask the questions that actually decide the outcome. Is it fighting a fundamental it refuses to change, or smoothing a genuine overshoot? Is it sterilizing — and therefore weak — or letting the money supply move? Is it spending its words wisely or crying wolf? And above all: does the market *believe* it? Because an exchange rate is the relative price of two monies, and intervention is the moment a central bank tries to bend that price by spending money and credibility against a market that is always, relentlessly, repricing the gap between two countries' rates and the flow of money across their borders. The bank with the most reserves does not win. The bank the market *believes* does.
