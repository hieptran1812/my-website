> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The Vietnamese dong does not float and is not hard-pegged; the State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) runs a *managed float* by publishing a daily central reference rate and letting USD/VND trade only inside a fixed corridor of plus or minus 5% around it.
>
> - Three numbers define the regime every single day: a **central reference rate** the SBV posts each morning, a **ceiling** 5% above it, and a **floor** 5% below it. The market can move freely between the walls; it cannot trade through them.
> - The dong **crawls weaker in a one-directional staircase**, not a two-way float. Over 2014-2025 USD/VND walked from about 21,340 to roughly 26,300 — a slow, managed devaluation of a few percent a year, punctuated by step-changes in stress years like 2022 and 2024.
> - At the weak-side ceiling the SBV **spends reserves**: it sells dollars and drains dong liquidity to push the rate back inside the band. In 2022 it sold roughly \$22 billion — reserves fell from about \$109B to \$87B — defending exactly that wall.
> - The one number to remember: the band is **+/-5%**, about **2,434 VND wide** at 2024 levels — a corridor of roughly \$0.095 to \$0.10 of room around a dollar that costs about 24,340 dong.

On the trading days of late 2022, anyone watching the Vietnamese dong saw something that looked, at a glance, like a broken price feed. USD/VND would tick higher through the morning, press against a number — and then simply stop, dead, as if it had hit a ceiling. It had. That number was the **ceiling** of the State Bank of Vietnam's trading band, and the reason the rate stalled there was not that sellers ran out of dollars. It was that the central bank was standing at the wall with a fire hose of foreign reserves, selling dollars into the market for every buyer who showed up, so that the published price could not legally print any higher. By the time the year closed, Vietnam had burned through roughly \$22 billion of its reserves — about a fifth of its entire war chest — to hold that line.

That is not how a free-floating currency behaves. A free float gaps up and down on news; nobody defends a number. It is also not how a hard peg behaves; a peg promises one fixed rate forever. The dong lives in between, in a regime that most of the world's currencies actually inhabit but almost nobody explains clearly: the **managed float**. The SBV does not let the market set USD/VND, and it does not fix it either. It steers it — daily, deliberately, within rules it publishes itself.

This post is the anchor of the Vietnam track of this series, and its job is narrow and concrete: to teach you *exactly* how the State Bank runs the dong. We will build up the three moving parts — the daily central reference rate, the plus-or-minus 5% trading band, and the ceiling-and-floor walls — then watch the SBV intervene at the edge, and finally understand why the dong crawls weaker in an orderly staircase instead of floating freely. The thread that runs through it is the spine of this whole series: **an exchange rate is the relative price of two monies, and a regime is the rulebook for who gets to set that price.** For the dong, the answer is: mostly the central bank, within a corridor, with reserves as the price of admission.

## Foundations: How the SBV runs the dong

Before we can watch the machine work, we need a handful of terms pinned down from zero. The subject is buried in jargon — "central rate," "band," "fixing," "intervention" — and almost all of it assumes you already know what an exchange rate is. So let us start there and build up.

**An exchange rate is a price.** When we write **USD/VND = 25,450**, that means one US dollar costs 25,450 Vietnamese dong. The first currency, USD, is the **base**; the second, VND, is the **quote**. The number tells you how many units of the quote currency it takes to buy one unit of the base. If that number goes *up* — say from 24,340 to 25,450 — the base currency (the dollar) got *stronger* and the quote currency (the dong) got *weaker*, because it now takes more dong to buy the same dollar. This direction trips up newcomers constantly, so hold it firmly: **USD/VND rising means the dong is falling.** If you want the full grammar of reading a quote, the sibling post on [base, quote and pips](/blog/trading/forex/base-quote-pips-and-how-to-read-an-fx-quote) lays it out; here we only need that one direction.

**A regime is the rulebook** for how that price is allowed to move. At one extreme — a **free float** — the central bank does nothing, and supply and demand set USD/VND every second; the rate can swing several percent in a day. At the other extreme — a **hard peg** — the price is fixed by law and defended with everything the country has. Vietnam sits in the middle, at a point on the spectrum called a **managed float** (the IMF sometimes labels Vietnam's regime "stabilised arrangement" or "crawl-like," which is the same idea). The companion post on the full [spectrum of FX regimes](/blog/trading/forex/pegs-bands-and-crawling-pegs-the-spectrum-of-fx-regimes) walks every rung from float to dollarization; the dong is one concrete inhabitant of the managed-float rung, and this post is its detailed portrait.

Now the three terms that define the dong's regime specifically:

**1. The central reference rate** (also called the central rate, the daily fixing, or *tỷ giá trung tâm*). Every business morning, the SBV publishes a single number: the reference value of one US dollar in dong for that day. This is *not* the market price. It is the anchor — the center of the corridor the market is allowed to trade in. The SBV introduced this daily-central-rate mechanism in **January 2016**, replacing an older system where it announced occasional one-off devaluations. The shift mattered: instead of holding a fixed number until pressure forced a sudden jump, the SBV now nudges the anchor in small daily increments, which lets the dong depreciate smoothly rather than in violent lurches.

**2. The trading band.** The SBV does not let USD/VND print at any value it likes around the central rate. It allows trading only within a fixed corridor of **plus or minus 5%** around that day's central rate. (The band has been widened over the years — it was +/-1% before 2015, then +/-2%, +/-3%, and reached +/-5% in October 2022, where it has stayed.) The band is the SBV's tolerance for daily two-way movement: the market may wander inside it, but the rate is not allowed to settle outside it.

**3. The ceiling and the floor.** These are just the two edges of the band, computed from the central rate. The **ceiling** is the central rate times 1.05 — the highest USD/VND that commercial banks may quote, which corresponds to the *weakest* the dong may legally trade. The **floor** is the central rate times 0.95 — the lowest USD/VND, the *strongest* the dong may go. Vietnamese banks quote within these walls; a quote outside them would be against the rules.

One more term you will need throughout:

**Reserves** (or **FX reserves**) are the SBV's stockpile of *other people's* money — mostly US dollars held as Treasury bills and bank deposits, plus some gold. Reserves are the ammunition a central bank uses to defend a number. To make the dong *stronger* (push USD/VND down), the SBV *sells dollars and buys dong*. To make it *weaker*, it does the reverse. A central bank with empty reserves cannot defend any promise about its exchange rate — which is why the size of Vietnam's reserve pile, and how fast it gets spent, is the single most important gauge of whether the band will hold. The companion post on [how central banks intervene](/blog/trading/forex/how-central-banks-intervene-in-the-currency-market) goes deep on the global mechanics; here we apply them to the dong.

With those five terms in hand, the whole regime collapses to a single sentence: **the SBV posts a central rate each morning, draws a corridor 5% wide around it, and spends reserves at the edges to keep the market inside the walls.** Everything below is detail on that sentence.

It helps to see *why* Vietnam chose this particular arrangement rather than the two simpler extremes. A pure free float would expose a small, trade-dependent economy to violent currency swings that wreck the planning of every importer and exporter — a 5% gap on a single bad day is survivable for a giant like the US, but it is a calamity for a Vietnamese factory whose entire margin is a few percent. A pure hard peg, on the other hand, would hand the dong a single number to defend, and history is unkind to small countries defending single numbers against a determined global market: the moment reserves look thin, speculators pile in, and the peg breaks in a day. The managed float is the engineering compromise between these failure modes. It gives the economy *most* of the predictability of a peg — the dong moves slowly and one way, so a business can budget around it — while keeping *most* of the flexibility of a float, because the SBV can always slide the central rate or widen the band rather than fight to the death. You give up the cleanest version of each extreme to avoid the worst version of both. That is the deal, and it is a sensible one for the country Vietnam is.

There is also a vocabulary point worth settling, because the labels confuse people. Economists and the IMF use several names for what the SBV does — "managed float," "stabilised arrangement," "crawl-like arrangement," "de facto crawling peg" — and they are all pointing at the same machine from slightly different angles. "Managed float" emphasizes that the SBV *steers* a rate that is nominally market-determined. "Crawling peg" emphasizes that the steered rate *moves in small, regular increments* in one direction. "Stabilised arrangement" is the IMF's technical classification when a currency stays within a 2% band of some trend for six months. For our purposes the precise label does not matter; what matters is the mechanism, which is identical under every name: a daily anchor, a tolerance band, and reserves at the edges.

#### Worked example: how wide is the band, in dollars?

Take the 2024 numbers. The central reference rate was about **24,340 VND** per dollar. The ceiling is 24,340 × 1.05 = **25,557 VND**; the floor is 24,340 × 0.95 = **23,123 VND**. So the band is 25,557 − 23,123 = **2,434 VND wide** — that is the total room the market has, top to bottom.

What is 2,434 dong worth in dollars? At the central rate, one dollar is 24,340 dong, so one dong is 1 / 24,340 = about \$0.0000411. The band width of 2,434 dong is therefore 2,434 × \$0.0000411 ≈ **\$0.10**. Put differently: around a dollar that costs 24,340 dong (call it "\$1"), the SBV gives the market about **ten cents** of total wiggle room — roughly five cents weaker and five cents stronger.

*The takeaway:* a "+/-5% band" sounds generous, but in dollar terms it is a corridor only about a dime wide around each dollar — and in practice the market spends almost all its time pressed against one edge, not roaming the middle.

## The band mechanic: a corridor, not a line

The most common mental error about the dong is to picture the central rate as *the* exchange rate — a single line the SBV defends. It is not a line; it is the **center of a corridor**. The price the public and importers actually transact at is the *market rate*, which lives somewhere inside the walls, usually nowhere near the middle.

![Before and after view of the central rate becoming a corridor with ceiling and floor](/imgs/blogs/usd-vnd-and-the-managed-float-how-the-sbv-runs-the-dong-2.png)

The central rate works like a fence post the SBV plants each morning, with the band as a corridor 5% wide on either side of the post. The bank does not care exactly where inside the corridor the market trades — that is the market's business. It cares intensely about the two walls. The walls are the only hard commitments. As long as USD/VND stays between them, the SBV can mostly sit back. The moment the market presses against a wall, the bank's reserves go to work (we will see exactly how in the intervention section).

This corridor design is what makes the dong a *managed float* rather than a peg. A true peg commits to one number; the SBV commits only to a *range*, and it slides the range itself over time. That gives it two distinct levers most people conflate:

- **Lever 1 — move the center.** By raising the daily central rate a few dong at a time, the SBV walks the whole corridor weaker. This is the slow crawl; it is how structural depreciation happens without a single dramatic devaluation.
- **Lever 2 — widen the walls.** By changing the band percentage (as it did from +/-3% to +/-5% in October 2022), the SBV lets the market move further from the center *without* moving the center itself. This is a pressure-release valve: in a crisis, a wider band lets the dong weaken to where the market wants it, while the SBV's published central rate barely budges and saves face.

The 2022 episode used *both* levers at once: the SBV nudged the central rate up *and* widened the band from +/-3% to +/-5%, because the market wanted the dong weaker than a +/-3% corridor could legally allow. That is the regime's flexibility on display — it can give ground in a controlled way rather than break.

#### Worked example: where does the market actually sit inside the band?

In 2024, with the central rate near **24,340** and the ceiling near **25,557**, the market rate sat around **25,450** dong per dollar — about \$1.045 per 1,000 dong, or one dollar ≈ 25,450 dong.

How close to the ceiling is that? The market's distance below the ceiling was 25,557 − 25,450 = **107 dong**, while the full band was 2,434 dong wide. So the market was sitting at (25,450 − 23,123) / 2,434 = **95.6%** of the way up the corridor — essentially glued to the weak-side ceiling, with the floor 2,327 dong (about \$0.096) away and completely irrelevant in practice.

*The takeaway:* the floor is a theoretical wall that almost never binds. The dong's whole life is spent near the *ceiling*, because the structural pressure on it is one-directional — weaker. The band is symmetric on paper but the regime is asymmetric in reality.

## How the SBV sets the daily central rate

If the central rate is not the market price, where does it come from? The SBV does not pull a number out of the air, and it does not let the market set it. It *recalculates* it each morning from a published recipe — which is why this is a managed float and not a float.

![Pipeline of how the State Bank of Vietnam computes the daily central reference rate](/imgs/blogs/usd-vnd-and-the-managed-float-how-the-sbv-runs-the-dong-4.png)

Officially, the daily central rate is derived from three ingredients:

1. **A weighted basket of eight currencies.** The SBV references the dong against a basket of the currencies of Vietnam's main trading and investment partners — the US dollar, the euro, the Chinese yuan, the Japanese yen, the Singapore dollar, the Korean won, the Taiwan dollar, and the Thai baht. Weighting against a basket (not just the dollar) means that when the dollar surges against *everything*, the dong is allowed to weaken against the dollar without weakening against the basket as a whole. This is the SBV's way of saying "we target our trade competitiveness, not the dollar specifically."
2. **Yesterday's interbank market rate.** The SBV looks at where USD/VND actually traded among banks the previous day. This is the feedback term: it lets the central rate drift toward where the market is pulling, so the corridor follows reality rather than fighting it.
3. **Macro and balance-of-payments objectives.** The SBV explicitly reserves the right to factor in its own policy goals — inflation, monetary conditions, the health of the trade balance and capital flows. This is the discretionary term, and it is why the regime is *managed*: the bank can lean against a move it dislikes, or accommodate one it has decided to allow.

Once the central rate is posted, the +/-5% walls are mechanical: ceiling = central × 1.05, floor = central × 0.95. A fresh corridor is hung around the new center, and the day's trading begins.

The crucial property of this recipe is that it is **smooth and one-directional in stress**. Because the central rate moves a few dong a day rather than jumping, the dong's depreciation arrives as a gentle slope, not a cliff. Compare this to the old pre-2016 system, where the SBV held a fixed rate until pressure built up and then announced a one-off devaluation of 1-2% overnight — a shock that traders could front-run and that spooked the public. The daily-fixing mechanism converts the same total depreciation into a quiet staircase that almost nobody notices day to day. That smoothness is the entire point.

#### Worked example: a few dong a day adds up

Suppose the SBV nudges the central rate higher by an average of **6 dong per business day** over a year of roughly 250 trading days. That is 6 × 250 = **1,500 dong** of upward drift in the central rate over the year — from, say, 23,900 to 25,400.

In percentage terms, 1,500 / 23,900 ≈ **6.3%** of dong depreciation across the year. In dollar terms, a dollar that cost 23,900 dong (≈ "\$1") now costs 25,400 dong — the dong lost about 5.9% of its purchasing power against the dollar (1 − 23,900/25,400). Yet on no single day did the rate move more than a rounding error: 6 dong out of 24,000 is **0.025%**, far below the noise of a normal trading day.

*The takeaway:* the crawl hides a meaningful annual devaluation inside daily moves too small to feel. That is by design — invisible depreciation does not trigger a public rush to buy dollars, while a visible one-off devaluation does.

A historical note makes this design choice vivid. Before the 2016 daily-central-rate mechanism, the SBV ran an *adjustable peg*: it announced a fixed reference rate and held it, sometimes for many months, until pressure built to the point where it had to step the rate weaker in a one-off devaluation of 1% or even 2-3% overnight. Those devaluations were public events, telegraphed by weeks of the dong pressing its old ceiling, and they were a gift to anyone positioned for them. A trader who saw the dong glued to the ceiling and reserves bleeding could load up on dollars, wait for the inevitable announcement, and bank the 2% jump risk-free. Worse, the announcements frightened ordinary Vietnamese, who would rush to convert dong savings into dollars and gold at the first whiff of a devaluation — a self-reinforcing panic that drained reserves faster. The daily-fixing mechanism dissolved both problems. There is no longer a fixed line to bet against, because the line moves a few dong every single day; and there is no longer a discrete event to panic about, because the depreciation arrives as a slope so gentle that the morning newspaper never reports it. The same total devaluation, delivered as a staircase instead of a cliff, is far less destabilizing. This is a general truth about managed currencies: *predictable, gradual moves are stabilizing; surprise, discrete moves are destabilizing*, even when the total magnitude is identical. The SBV traded the drama of occasional devaluations for the quiet of a continuous crawl, and the dong has been calmer for it.

## The managed crawl: an orderly staircase weaker

Step back from any single day and look at the dong over a decade, and the regime's signature appears: a **one-directional staircase**, walking weaker year after year, with the steepest steps in stress years.

![USD/VND year-end levels from 2014 to 2025 showing the managed crawl weaker](/imgs/blogs/usd-vnd-and-the-managed-float-how-the-sbv-runs-the-dong-3.png)

From 2014 to 2025, USD/VND climbed from about **21,340** to roughly **26,300** — meaning the dong lost about 19% of its value against the dollar over eleven years, or very roughly 2% a year on average. But notice the *shape* of the line. It is not a smooth glide and it is not a random walk. It is a series of plateaus punctuated by steps: relative stability in 2018-2021 (the rate even strengthened slightly in 2020-2021 as Vietnam ran big trade surpluses and the dollar was weak), then a sharp step weaker in **2022** to 23,650, and another leg in **2024** to about 25,450, continuing toward 26,300 in 2025.

This shape tells you something the headline 2%-a-year number hides: **the crawl is not constant. It is a function of dollar strength and capital flows.** When the dollar is weak globally and money is flowing *into* Vietnam (FDI building factories, remittances coming home, a fat trade surplus), the dong barely depreciates or even firms — 2020-2021 is the clean example. When the dollar surges and money tries to leave (2022, as the Fed hiked aggressively and the dollar index spiked), the pressure overwhelms the inflows and the SBV lets the dong step weaker rather than burn unlimited reserves defending the old level.

This is the spine of the series in action: **USD/VND rides the gap between US and Vietnamese monetary conditions plus the flow of money across Vietnam's borders.** When US rates rocket and the dollar is king, holding dong instead of dollars becomes painful, capital tries to flee, and the dong has to give ground. The SBV's managed crawl does not stop that pressure — it *paces* it, converting what would be a chaotic float into an orderly retreat. For the deeper mechanism of how rate differentials drive a currency, see [how monetary policy moves currencies](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-monetary-policy-moves-currencies-rate-differentials); the dong is one disciplined example of that universal force.

It is worth being precise about *why* the crawl is one-directional rather than two-way. Three structural forces pull the dong gently weaker over time:

- **Inflation differential.** Vietnam typically runs higher inflation than the United States. If Vietnamese prices rise 3-4% a year and US prices rise 2%, then to keep Vietnamese exports competitively priced, the dong must depreciate by roughly the inflation gap — a couple of percent a year. This is the purchasing-power-parity logic; the [PPP and the real exchange rate](/blog/trading/forex/purchasing-power-parity-and-the-real-exchange-rate) post covers the mechanism, and the dong is a textbook crawler tracking its inflation gap with the dollar.
- **Interest-rate differential.** US dollar deposits often pay more than dong deposits *after* accounting for credibility, especially when the Fed is hiking. That makes holding dong less attractive and adds depreciation pressure.
- **The SBV's own competitiveness target.** Because the central rate is set against a *basket*, the SBV deliberately allows the dong to track the basket weaker so Vietnamese exports stay cheap on world markets. A too-strong dong would hurt the export-driven economy the whole regime is built to protect.

Together these forces mean the equilibrium drift is *weaker*, always. The SBV's job is not to reverse that drift — it is to administer it in small, pre-absorbed doses.

#### Worked example: an importer's cost as the dong crawls

Take a Vietnamese electronics importer who buys **\$1,000,000** of components from abroad each quarter. The cost in dong depends entirely on where USD/VND sits.

At the end of 2023, with USD/VND at **24,270**, that \$1,000,000 order cost 1,000,000 × 24,270 = **24.27 billion dong**. One year later, at the end of 2024, USD/VND had crawled to **25,450**. The *same* \$1,000,000 order now cost 1,000,000 × 25,450 = **25.45 billion dong** — an extra **1.18 billion dong**, or about \$46,400 of extra cost at the new rate, purely from the currency move.

In percentage terms the dong weakened (25,450 − 24,270) / 24,270 = **4.86%** over that year, so the importer's dong cost rose by the same 4.86% even though the dollar price of the components never changed. If the importer's margins were thinner than 4.86%, that single year of crawl wiped out the profit on every dollar of imports unless prices were raised.

*The takeaway:* the crawl is invisible day to day but brutally real over a year for anyone with dollar costs and dong revenues. This is precisely why Vietnamese firms hedge, and why the offshore market for betting on the dong's path exists at all — see the [non-deliverable forwards](/blog/trading/forex/non-deliverable-forwards-trading-uninvestable-currencies) post for how that hedge is actually priced and traded.

## Intervention at the edge: defending the ceiling

So far the band has been a passive corridor. The drama happens at the **walls**. When market pressure pushes USD/VND up against the ceiling — when everyone wants to buy dollars and sell dong faster than the corridor allows — the SBV has to choose: let the rate break the wall (it won't), or stand at the wall and absorb the pressure with reserves. It does the latter.

![Graph of how the State Bank defends the ceiling by selling dollars and draining dong](/imgs/blogs/usd-vnd-and-the-managed-float-how-the-sbv-runs-the-dong-6.png)

Here is the mechanism, step by step, when the dong hits the ceiling:

1. **The market presses the ceiling.** Demand for dollars (importers paying bills, foreigners pulling money out, locals fleeing a weakening currency) exceeds supply at the current rate. USD/VND wants to print above the ceiling, but banks cannot legally quote there.
2. **The SBV sells dollars from reserves.** The bank steps in as the seller of last resort, selling US dollars into the market and buying dong. Every dollar it sells adds to dollar supply at the ceiling, satisfying buyers so the price does not have to rise. This directly drains the reserve pile.
3. **The SBV drains dong liquidity and raises short-term rates.** Simultaneously, the bank can tighten dong money-market conditions — selling SBV bills, raising the overnight rate — so that holding dong (to short, you must borrow dong) becomes expensive and holding dollars in the carry trade becomes less attractive. This is the second front of the defense.
4. **Pressure eases and the rate steps back inside the band.** With more dollars supplied and dong made costly to short, the upward pressure on USD/VND relents and the market trades back below the ceiling — until the next wave.

Notice the two prongs. Selling reserves attacks the *quantity* problem (not enough dollars); tightening dong liquidity attacks the *price-of-leverage* problem (cheap dong funding makes shorting the dong profitable). Both are how a central bank defends a level, and both are covered in general in the [central banks intervene](/blog/trading/forex/how-central-banks-intervene-in-the-currency-market) post. The dong's wrinkle is that the SBV is defending the *ceiling of a band*, not a single peg, so it has a third escape valve a pure pegger lacks: it can simply **let the band step weaker** — nudge the central rate up, or widen the band — and surrender ground in a controlled way rather than defend to the last dollar. That optionality is exactly why the dong regime has never suffered a 1997-style collapse: it bends before it breaks.

The connection to the impossible trinity is worth making explicit, because it explains *why* intervention is ever necessary. A country cannot simultaneously have a fixed exchange rate, free movement of capital, and an independent monetary policy — it can pick at most two. Vietnam picks a **managed (not fully fixed) exchange rate** and an **independent monetary policy**, and pays for that combination by keeping **capital controls** — restrictions on how freely money can flow across its borders. Those controls are the unsung hero of the regime: they throttle the speculative capital that broke pegs across Asia in 1997, which is why the SBV's reserves can defend the band against a manageable flow rather than an unlimited tidal wave. For the full logic, see [the impossible trinity](/blog/trading/forex/the-impossible-trinity-pick-two-of-three); the dong is a live case study in choosing the exchange-rate and monetary-autonomy corners and sacrificing free capital mobility.

#### Worked example: the SBV sells \$1 billion to defend the ceiling

Suppose on a stressful day the market wants to buy a net **\$1,000,000,000** of dollars at the ceiling — importers and outflows all hitting at once. The SBV decides to hold the line and supplies that full billion from reserves.

The dollar leg is simple: reserves fall by **\$1B**. The dong leg is where the second effect lives. At a ceiling near 25,557 VND per dollar, selling \$1B means the SBV *buys back* 1,000,000,000 × 25,557 = **25.56 trillion dong** out of the banking system. That dong simply vanishes from circulation — the SBV has effectively run a tightening operation, draining 25.56 trillion dong of liquidity as a side effect of defending the rate. Tighter dong liquidity pushes up domestic interest rates, which is often *not* what the SBV wants for the domestic economy.

*The takeaway:* every dollar of FX intervention is also a dong-tightening operation. Defending the currency and easing the economy pull in opposite directions — this is the central tension of the impossible trinity, made concrete in 25.56 trillion dong of drained liquidity. It is also why the SBV cannot defend forever: each defended billion both shrinks reserves and squeezes the domestic economy.

It is worth pausing on a refinement the SBV uses to make its ammunition go further: the **forward sale**. Rather than only selling spot dollars (which drains reserves immediately), the SBV has at times sold dollars *forward* — committing to deliver dollars to banks at a set rate weeks or months ahead. A forward sale calms the market today (banks know dollars are coming) without the reserves leaving the building until settlement, and if the pressure passes before delivery, the contracts can sometimes be rolled or netted down. This is a way of *signaling* a defense and buying time without spending the full quantity of reserves up front. It is not free — a forward commitment is still a claim on future reserves, and if the dong keeps weakening the SBV delivers dollars at a rate now below market, taking a loss — but it stretches the same war chest across more days of pressure. The general toolkit of spot intervention, forward intervention, and verbal intervention (jawboning) is laid out in the [how central banks intervene](/blog/trading/forex/how-central-banks-intervene-in-the-currency-market) post; the SBV uses all three, escalating from words to forwards to outright spot sales as pressure mounts.

There is one more subtlety that distinguishes defending a *ceiling* from defending a *peg*, and it is psychological as much as mechanical. When a central bank defends a single fixed number, the market knows the exact line and can size the attack precisely: every dollar of reserves above the line is a target. When the SBV defends a *band that it can move*, the market never knows whether the bank will spend reserves to hold the current ceiling or simply slide the ceiling weaker tomorrow. That ambiguity is a defensive asset. A speculator betting on a band move has to be right not just about direction but about *the SBV's willingness to spend* — and because the SBV has repeatedly shown it will crawl rather than collapse, the expected payoff to attacking the band is muted. The band's movability is, paradoxically, part of what makes it hard to attack.

## The ammunition: reserves and the 2022 drawdown

Intervention is only credible if the SBV has dollars to sell. So the size and trajectory of Vietnam's FX reserves is the single best gauge of how much defense the band can absorb before the SBV has to give ground. The 2022 episode is the cleanest illustration in the data.

![Vietnam FX reserves from 2016 to 2024 with the 2022 drawdown highlighted in red](/imgs/blogs/usd-vnd-and-the-managed-float-how-the-sbv-runs-the-dong-5.png)

Vietnam spent the 2010s building its war chest. Reserves climbed from about \$41B in 2016 to a peak near **\$109B in 2021** — funded by years of trade surpluses, FDI inflows, and remittances (more on those flows below). Then 2022 hit. The Fed raised rates at the fastest pace in forty years, the dollar index spiked to multi-decade highs, and capital across the emerging world ran for dollars. The dong came under relentless pressure against its ceiling, and the SBV did exactly what the mechanism above describes: it sold dollars. Reserves fell from **\$109B to about \$87B** — a drawdown of roughly **\$22 billion**, about a fifth of the entire pile, in a single year of defense.

That drawdown is the red bar in the chart, and it is the most important single fact about the regime's limits. Reserves are not infinite. By late 2022, Vietnam's reserves had fallen to around **2.9 months of imports** — below the rough ~3-month adequacy rule of thumb that the IMF uses as a comfort line. The SBV was approaching the point where defending the old level was no longer prudent. So it did the sensible thing: it *let the band step weaker* (widening to +/-5% in October 2022 and nudging the central rate up), conserving the remaining reserves rather than spending them all defending a level the market had rejected. Reserves then partly rebuilt to about \$90B in 2023 before drifting to roughly \$82B in 2024 as the dollar stayed strong.

This is the regime's wisdom and its weakness in one picture. Its *wisdom* is that it never bets the whole pile on one number — it gives ground before reserves run dry, which is why the dong has never collapsed. Its *weakness* is that it cannot indefinitely resist a determined dollar; the crawl weaker is ultimately not a choice but a release valve. The general logic of reserves as a war chest — how big is enough, what they are made of, how fast they bleed — is covered in the [how central banks intervene](/blog/trading/forex/how-central-banks-intervene-in-the-currency-market) post; here the lesson is specific: watch Vietnam's reserve months-of-imports, because that ratio tells you how much defense the band has left.

The months-of-imports yardstick deserves a word, because it is the number professionals actually track. The idea is simple: divide total reserves by the country's monthly import bill, and you get how many months of imports the country could pay for if every other source of dollars dried up tomorrow. The IMF's rough comfort line is about three months; below that, a country is considered to have a thin buffer against a sudden stop in inflows. By that yardstick Vietnam sits uncomfortably low — around 2.9 months at end-2024 — far below the double-digit cushions that China (about 14 months) or India (about 11) carry. This is not a crisis in itself; Vietnam's reserves are thin partly because its economy and import bill have grown so fast that even a \$80B-plus pile does not stretch as far as it once did. But it is a real constraint, and it is precisely why the SBV is *disciplined* about the crawl: a country with three months of cover cannot afford to fight the dollar to the last billion, so it builds the willingness-to-crawl into the regime from the start. Thin reserves are not a flaw to be ashamed of so much as the reason the band has to be flexible. A country with fourteen months of cover can act stubborn; a country with three months must be nimble, and the dong's managed crawl is exactly the nimble response.

#### Worked example: how many days can \$22 billion defend?

Suppose, hypothetically, that on a bad stretch the net outflow pressing the ceiling runs at **\$1.5 billion per week**. With \$22B available to spend (the amount actually deployed in 2022), the SBV could fund 22 / 1.5 ≈ **14.7 weeks**, or about three and a half months, of defense at that pace before exhausting that slice of reserves.

But spending it is not free. Selling \$22B at an average ceiling near 24,500 VND drains 22,000,000,000 × 24,500 ≈ **539 trillion dong** of domestic liquidity — a massive tightening of the dong money market that pushes domestic rates up and squeezes the economy. So the real constraint is not just "do we have the dollars" but "can the domestic economy tolerate the dong tightening that comes with the defense."

*The takeaway:* reserves are a finite, double-edged resource. A war chest of \$22B buys only a few months of determined defense, and spending it tightens the home economy at exactly the moment it least wants tightening. That arithmetic — not pride — is why the SBV always reserves the right to let the band crawl weaker.

## Offshore pressure: the NDF as the market's verdict

There is one more instrument that tells you whether the band is about to be tested: the **offshore non-deliverable forward**, or NDF. Because Vietnam has capital controls, foreigners cannot freely trade the onshore dong. So an *offshore* market grew up — in Singapore, Hong Kong, London — where traders bet on where USD/VND will be in the future using cash-settled contracts that never actually deliver dong. That offshore price is the global market's uncensored opinion of the dong, free of the onshore band's constraints.

![Offshore NDF implied depreciation premium on the dong from 2018 to 2025](/imgs/blogs/usd-vnd-and-the-managed-float-how-the-sbv-runs-the-dong-7.png)

The useful signal is the **gap** between the offshore NDF-implied rate and the onshore spot. When the one-year NDF prices USD/VND well *above* the onshore rate, the offshore market is saying: "we expect the dong to be meaningfully weaker than the band currently allows — the SBV will have to let it go." That premium is a pressure gauge on the band. In the chart, you can read the regime's stress years directly: the NDF-implied depreciation premium ran around 0.8-1.5% in the calm years (2018, 2020), spiked to about **3.5%** in the 2022 crisis as the offshore market bet hard on a weaker dong, and stayed elevated near 2.8% through the 2024 dollar strength. Low premium, calm band; high premium, the band is about to be tested.

This is the same information the reserve drawdown carries, but seen from the *outside*. Reserves tell you how the SBV is responding; the NDF tells you what the market is demanding. When both flash red at once — reserves draining *and* the NDF premium spiking, as in 2022 — you know a band move is coming, and indeed the SBV widened the band and stepped the central rate weaker that very autumn. The full mechanics of how an NDF is constructed and why it can decouple from onshore spot are in the [non-deliverable forwards](/blog/trading/forex/non-deliverable-forwards-trading-uninvestable-currencies) post; here the point is that the NDF gap is a leading indicator you can watch to anticipate the dong's next step.

A subtle but important consequence: because the offshore NDF is where foreigners express a dong view, it is also where *speculative pressure* concentrates. If the offshore market gets convinced the SBV is about to capitulate, the NDF can run far ahead of onshore spot, and the pressure feeds back: domestic firms, seeing the offshore signal, rush to buy dollars onshore to hedge, which presses the onshore ceiling harder. The capital controls keep this loop from becoming a 1997-style runaway, but they do not eliminate it. The NDF is the channel through which global sentiment leaks into a controlled currency.

#### Worked example: pricing the NDF off the rate gap

A one-year NDF is priced from the interest-rate differential, just like any forward (the [FX forwards and swaps](/blog/trading/forex/fx-forwards-and-swaps-the-real-workhorses-of-the-market) post derives this). Suppose dong one-year deposits pay **5.5%** and US dollar one-year deposits pay **4.5%**, with onshore spot at **24,300** VND per dollar. The interest-parity forward is approximately spot × (1 + dong rate) / (1 + dollar rate) = 24,300 × 1.055 / 1.045 ≈ **24,532** VND.

But suppose the offshore one-year NDF is actually trading at **25,000** — about 1.9% above the parity-implied 24,532. That extra premium is *not* explained by interest rates; it is the market pricing in **expected devaluation** beyond what carry alone justifies. In dollar terms, the offshore market is betting that a dollar (≈ "\$1" at 24,300 dong) will cost about 25,000 dong in a year — a roughly 2.9% weaker dong — versus the 0.95% the rate gap alone would produce.

*The takeaway:* the slice of the NDF rate that exceeds the interest-parity forward *is* the market's bet on a band move. When that slice blows out, the offshore market is calling the SBV's bluff — watch for the band to crawl weaker soon after.

## Common misconceptions

The dong regime attracts a particular set of confident wrong beliefs. Here are the five worth correcting, each with a number.

**Misconception 1: "The dong is pegged to the dollar."** It is not. A peg fixes one number; the dong trades freely inside a 5%-wide corridor whose center the SBV slides over time. From 2014 to 2025 USD/VND moved from about 21,340 to 26,300 — a 23% move that no peg would permit. The dong is a *managed float*, which is a fundamentally different animal: it has a moving target and a tolerance band, not a fixed line.

**Misconception 2: "The central rate is the exchange rate."** The central rate is the *center* of the band, not the price anyone trades at. In 2024 the central rate was about 24,340 while the market traded near 25,450 — a full 1,110 dong (about 4.6%) away, pinned near the ceiling. If you quote the central rate as "the USD/VND rate," you are off by nearly the entire half-width of the band.

**Misconception 3: "A +/-5% band means the dong swings 5% both ways."** It almost never does. The band is symmetric on paper but the regime is one-directional in practice: the dong spends its life near the *ceiling* (the weak edge), because the structural pressure — inflation gap, rate gap, basket competitiveness — is always toward depreciation. The floor (the strong edge) has been effectively irrelevant for years. The market sat at 95.6% of the way up the corridor in 2024, not in the middle.

**Misconception 4: "The SBV can defend any level forever because it controls the band."** Reserves are finite and intervention is self-tightening. In 2022 the SBV spent about \$22B — a fifth of its reserves — and still had to let the dong step weaker, because the alternative was draining reserves below the ~3-month-of-imports safety line and choking the domestic economy with 539 trillion dong of liquidity withdrawal. The SBV does not defend levels to the death; it gives ground in a controlled way precisely *because* its ammunition is limited.

**Misconception 5: "Capital controls are a relic; Vietnam's dong is freely traded."** Capital controls are the load-bearing pillar of the entire regime. They are why foreigners trade the dong *offshore* through NDFs rather than onshore, and they are why the speculative flows that broke the Thai baht and Indonesian rupiah in 1997 cannot mount an unlimited attack on the dong. Without those controls, the SBV's \$82-109B of reserves would be a snack for global macro funds; *with* them, the same reserves are enough to manage a throttled flow. The dong's stability is not despite the controls — it is *because* of them.

## How it shows up in real markets

The regime is not a theory; it produces concrete, datable events. Here are the episodes a market participant actually lived through, and what each one teaches.

**The 2022 defense and the band widening (autumn 2022).** This is the canonical stress test. As the Fed hiked from near zero toward 4.5% and the dollar index spiked to a two-decade high near 114.8 in late September 2022, the dong came under sustained pressure against its then-+/-3% ceiling. The SBV did three things in sequence, and the order is instructive. First it *sold reserves* — the \$22B drawdown — to hold the line. Then, when that proved too costly, it *widened the band* from +/-3% to +/-5% in October 2022, giving the market room to push the dong weaker without the SBV's central rate having to capitulate as visibly. Finally it *raised domestic interest rates* (two hikes in quick succession) to make the dong more attractive to hold. The dong ended 2022 around 23,650, weaker but never in freefall. The lesson: the regime defends in *layers* — reserves first, band-widening second, rate hikes third — and only deploys the more disruptive tools when the cheaper ones are exhausted. The general anatomy of when a currency defense holds or breaks is in [currency crises and the anatomy of a speculative attack](/blog/trading/forex/currency-crises-and-the-anatomy-of-a-speculative-attack); the dong in 2022 is the case where the defense *held* because the country had reserves, capital controls, and the willingness to crawl.

**The 2024 dollar-strength leg.** Through 2024 the dollar stayed strong as the Fed held rates high for longer than markets expected. The dong, with no such high rates of its own, drifted to its ceiling repeatedly, and USD/VND walked from about 24,270 at end-2023 to roughly 25,450 at end-2024 — the 4.86% move that cost our hypothetical importer 1.18 billion dong. The SBV intervened intermittently (selling dollars when the ceiling was tested) but mostly let the central rate crawl, because the pressure was steady rather than panicked. This is the regime in its *normal* mode: not a crisis, just the patient administration of a structural depreciation, paced so that no single day alarms anyone.

**What flows are funding the dong underneath all this.** The reason the SBV *can* run this regime — the reason it had \$109B of reserves to spend — is that Vietnam runs persistent dollar inflows that the bank can accumulate in good times. Three streams matter: a large and growing **goods trade surplus** (about \$24.8B in 2024), steady **FDI disbursement** (about \$25.4B in 2024, as global manufacturers build factories), and **remittances** from the diaspora (about \$16B a year). In calm years these inflows let the SBV *buy* dollars (building reserves) while keeping the dong from strengthening too much. In stress years the inflows shrink relative to outflows and the SBV *sells* dollars (spending reserves). The dong is, at bottom, a balance-of-payments story: it crawls weaker when the dollars going out exceed the dollars coming in, and the SBV's whole apparatus is a buffer that smooths the difference. For how these macro flows interact with the rest of Vietnam's markets, see [the four macro dials](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/the-four-macro-dials-rates-credit-fx-commodities), where FX is one of the four levers that move Vietnamese asset prices.

**Why the dong has never had a 1997.** It is worth naming the dog that did not bark. In 1997 the Thai baht, Indonesian rupiah, and Korean won all broke their pegs and fell 38-83% against the dollar in a matter of months, because they had *fixed* rates, *open* capital accounts, and *insufficient* reserves — a guaranteed recipe for a speculative attack. Vietnam's regime is built to fail differently: the rate is a *band* not a peg (so there is no single line to break), capital controls *throttle* the speculative flow (so no unlimited attack can form), and the SBV gives ground via the crawl *before* reserves run out (so there is no cliff-edge capitulation). The dong's worst year, 2022, was a 3-4% step weaker — not a 50% collapse. That is the managed float doing exactly the job it was designed for: trading the *possibility* of stability-then-catastrophe for the *certainty* of a slow, survivable decline.

![Before and after comparison of a free float versus the dong managed crawl](/imgs/blogs/usd-vnd-and-the-managed-float-how-the-sbv-runs-the-dong-8.png)

The figure above captures the trade the SBV makes. A free float would let USD/VND gap violently on every shock — two-way, unpredictable, and free of reserve cost. The managed crawl instead walks the dong weaker in small, pre-absorbed steps — one-directional, orderly, and paid for in reserves. Neither is "better" on its own; they are different bets. A small, export-driven, capital-controlled economy like Vietnam values the *orderliness* — importers can plan, the public does not panic, and the export sector keeps a predictable competitive edge — and is willing to pay for it in reserves and in the surrender of free capital mobility. A large, deep, open economy like the US or the eurozone values the *autonomy* of a free float and can absorb the volatility. The dong's regime is the rational choice for the country it serves.

## The takeaway: how to read the dong

If you take one thing from this post, make it this: **the dong is not a price the market sets — it is a corridor the State Bank manages, walked weaker on purpose, one small step at a time.** To read USD/VND correctly, you stop asking "where is the rate" and start asking three sharper questions.

*First: where is the market inside the band?* If USD/VND is pinned near the ceiling — as it usually is — the dong is under depreciation pressure and the SBV is likely either selling reserves or about to let the central rate crawl. If it drifts toward the middle, pressure has eased. The market's position inside the corridor, not its absolute level, is the live signal.

*Second: which way are the reserves moving?* Reserves are the regime's fuel gauge. Rising reserves mean the SBV is *buying* dollars — inflows exceed outflows, the dong is comfortable, the crawl will be gentle. Falling reserves — especially toward the ~3-month-of-imports line — mean the SBV is *defending*, and a band move is getting closer. Watch the months-of-imports ratio; in 2024 it sat near 2.9, below comfort.

*Third: what is the offshore NDF saying?* The slice of the one-year NDF rate that exceeds the interest-parity forward is the global market's bet on a band move. When that premium blows out — as it did to ~3.5% in 2022 — the offshore market is calling for a weaker dong than the band allows, and history says the SBV will eventually accommodate it.

Put those three together and you can anticipate the dong's next step before it happens, which is more than you can say for most floating currencies. That is the paradox of a managed float: by making the currency *less* free, the SBV makes it *more* readable. The crawl has a logic, the band has a ceiling, and the reserves have a floor — and once you can see all three, the dong stops looking like a broken price feed and starts looking like exactly what it is: a small, open, export-driven economy administering its own gradual depreciation with the discipline of a central bank that learned the lessons of 1997 and has no intention of repeating them.

And it all comes back to the spine of this series. **An exchange rate is the relative price of two monies, driven by the gap between two countries' interest rates and the flow of money across their borders.** The dong is that principle wearing a uniform: its slow weakening is the inflation-and-rate gap with the dollar made visible, its stress steps are the flow of money in and out of Vietnam made visible, and the SBV's band is simply the institution that decides *how fast* those forces are allowed to express themselves. Read the dong, and you are reading the balance of payments of a country in real time — one orderly step at a time.
