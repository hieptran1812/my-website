> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A commodity exporter's currency is a leveraged bet on the price of what it sells: when the export price rises, national income rises, money flows in, and the currency strengthens.
>
> - **Terms of trade** = the price of what you export divided by the price of what you import. When that ratio improves, the country gets richer for the same effort, and its currency catches the updraft.
> - The Australian dollar tracks **iron ore**, the Canadian dollar tracks **oil**, the Norwegian krone tracks **North Sea oil and gas**, and the Brazilian real tracks **soybeans plus a fat interest rate**.
> - The link is real but not mechanical: it breaks when a **rate differential** or a **global risk-off** flight overwhelms the export-price signal — the Aussie can fall on rising iron ore if the Fed is hiking into a panic.
> - The one number to remember: in our dataset, AUD/USD and the iron-ore price carry a positive correlation of about **+0.50** over 2011-2025 — strong enough to trade around, loose enough to get you hurt if you treat it as a law.

On a single day in 2011, you could buy one Australian dollar for one hundred and ten US cents. The Aussie was trading *above parity* with the world's reserve currency — a currency from a country of twenty-five million people on the far side of the planet was worth more than the dollar that prices oil, settles trade and backs most of the world's debt. Thirteen years later, in 2024, that same Australian dollar fetched just sixty-two US cents. It had lost more than forty percent of its value against the greenback.

What happened in between was not a financial crisis, a war, or a political collapse. What happened was iron ore. In 2011 a tonne of iron ore sold for around one hundred and sixty-eight dollars, at the peak of a Chinese construction boom that was pouring concrete and rolling steel at a pace the world had never seen. By 2016 that same tonne sold for fifty-eight dollars. China's investment binge slowed, new mines in Australia and Brazil flooded the market, and the single price that mattered most to Australia's national income collapsed by two-thirds. The Australian dollar followed it down, almost step for step.

This is the story of the **commodity currencies** — the Australian dollar, the Canadian dollar, the Norwegian krone, the Brazilian real, and a handful of others — and the single most important idea for understanding them: the **terms of trade**. Once you see that a commodity exporter's exchange rate is a *leveraged bet on the price of what it digs up or grows*, a whole class of currencies stops looking random and starts looking like a chart of one or two commodity prices with a currency symbol on the axis.

![Australian dollar tracks iron ore price 2011 to 2025 dual axis chart](/imgs/blogs/terms-of-trade-and-the-commodity-currencies-1.png)

That cover chart is the whole post in one picture. The blue line is AUD/USD; the amber line is the iron-ore price. They are not identical — nothing in markets is — but they rise together and fall together with a discipline that should make you sit up. Our job in this post is to explain *why* that happens, where the mechanism comes from, when it breaks, and how a trader actually uses it.

This is post B5 in the series. It sits squarely on the series' spine: an exchange rate is the relative price of two monies, moved by the gap between two countries' interest rates plus the flow of money across borders. Terms of trade is the channel that turns a *commodity price* into both of those things at once — it moves a country's income, which moves its central bank's rate decisions, and it moves the trade flows that physically buy and sell the currency. That is why commodity currencies are such a clean teaching case: the cause sits right out in the open, printed every day on a commodity exchange.

## Foundations: Terms of trade and the commodity currencies

Let us build this from absolute zero, because the phrase "terms of trade" sounds like jargon and is in fact one of the most intuitive ideas in all of economics.

**The terms of trade is the price of what you sell to the world divided by the price of what you buy from the world.** That is the entire definition. Write it as a ratio:

```
terms of trade = (average price of your exports) / (average price of your imports)
```

When that ratio goes *up*, every unit of stuff you export now buys you more units of imported stuff. You are getting richer without working any harder — the world is simply paying you more for the same shipload of iron ore, oil, or soybeans, while charging you the same for the cars, machines and medicines you buy back. When the ratio goes *down*, the opposite: each shipment buys you less, and the country is quietly poorer.

Here is the everyday-money version. Suppose you are a freelance graphic designer. The "price of your exports" is your hourly rate; the "price of your imports" is the cost of your rent, food and software. If your hourly rate doubles while your costs stay flat, your *personal* terms of trade have improved — you can suddenly afford a nicer apartment, eat out more, save more. You feel richer, you spend more, and the local economy around you picks up a little. A country whose export prices double while import prices hold flat feels exactly that, multiplied across a whole nation. The extra income is real, it is large, and it sloshes through wages, tax revenue and bank balances.

Now, why does that lift the *currency*? Three channels run in parallel, and the figure below traces them.

![Terms of trade income channel pipeline export price to currency](/imgs/blogs/terms-of-trade-and-the-commodity-currencies-2.png)

**Channel one — the income and trade-balance channel.** When the export price rises, the same physical volume of exports brings in more foreign currency. Australia ships roughly the same tonnage of iron ore each year, but at a higher price it earns more US dollars for it. Those dollars have to be converted into Australian dollars to pay miners' wages, royalties to the government, and dividends to shareholders. That conversion is a wall of physical buying of the Australian dollar — real demand, not speculation. A bigger trade surplus literally means more foreigners need to buy your currency to pay you, and that bids the price up.

**Channel two — the interest-rate channel.** A commodity boom is an income boom, and an income boom tends to be inflationary: people spend the windfall, wages rise, the economy runs hot. The central bank — the Reserve Bank of Australia, the Bank of Canada — responds by raising interest rates to cool things down. Higher local interest rates make the currency more attractive to hold, because money parked in that currency earns more. This is the rate-differential mechanism that runs through the entire series; here the *commodity price* is the thing that moves the rate gap. We do not re-derive how rate differentials move currencies — that machinery lives in [how monetary policy moves currencies](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-monetary-policy-moves-currencies-rate-differentials) — but it is worth seeing that terms of trade feeds straight into it.

**Channel three — the capital-flow and sentiment channel.** Markets are forward-looking. When traders see the export price climbing, they anticipate all of the above — the trade surplus, the rate hikes, the stronger economy — and they buy the currency *ahead* of the physical flows. They also buy the country's stocks and bonds, and those purchases require buying the currency first. So the currency often moves before the trade data even confirm the story. This is why the link looks tight on a chart: a fast-moving, anticipatory financial market is front-running a slow-moving real economy.

These three channels also explain why a commodity currency can move *faster and further* than the underlying trade flows alone would justify. The physical conversion of export proceeds is a steady trickle that builds over months as cargoes are shipped and invoices settled. But the financial market does not wait for the trickle — it prices the *expected* future trickle today, all at once. A surprise jump in the iron-ore price can move the Australian dollar within minutes, long before a single extra cargo has been loaded, because traders are repricing months of anticipated conversion flow plus the anticipated rate hikes plus the anticipated equity inflows in one go. This anticipatory front-running is also why the relationship is *noisy*: the market is trading its *forecast* of the commodity's effect, and forecasts are fickle, so the currency overshoots and undershoots the path the slow real flows would imply. The overshoot dynamic — how a currency can swing well past its fundamental value when a shock hits and expectations adjust faster than goods markets — is the subject of [capital flows and the Dornbusch overshoot](/blog/trading/forex/capital-flows-and-the-dornbusch-overshoot), and it is the financial-market reason a real-economy story like terms of trade produces such volatile currency moves.

Put the three together and you have the **income channel**: export price up, national income up, rate-hike odds up, capital in, currency up. It is a remarkably robust chain, and it runs in reverse just as forcefully when the export price falls.

One more foundational point, and it is the one that makes commodity currencies special: **leverage**. A commodity exporter's economy is concentrated. Iron ore and coal are roughly a third of Australia's goods exports; oil and gas dominate Norway's. So a swing in one commodity price moves the *whole* national income by a large fraction. A diversified economy like Germany's makes thousands of different things, so no single price dominates — its currency is a blurry average of many forces. A commodity economy is a *concentrated bet*, and its currency inherits that concentration. That is what we mean when we say the Australian dollar is a *leveraged bet* on iron ore: a 10% move in the ore price hits a much larger share of Australia's income than a 10% move in any single price would hit a diversified economy, so the currency reacts with outsized sensitivity.

### Where the buying pressure actually comes from

It helps to get very concrete about the *plumbing* of the trade channel, because "the currency strengthens" can sound like hand-waving until you see the actual flow of money. Australia sells iron ore to a Chinese steel mill. The contract is almost always priced and settled in **US dollars** — that is true of nearly all globally traded commodities, a fact that makes the US dollar the unit of account for the whole commodity complex and is one reason the dollar dominates FX (the dollar's structural role is covered in [the dollar system](/blog/trading/macro-trading/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy)). So the Australian miner receives a wire of US dollars. But the miner's costs are in Australian dollars: it pays Australian wages, Australian diesel, Australian royalties to the state of Western Australia, and Australian dividends to Australian pension funds. To meet those obligations, the miner — or its bank — must *sell US dollars and buy Australian dollars* on the FX market.

That is the mechanism. Every shipload of iron ore eventually generates a flow of *sell-USD, buy-AUD* orders. When the iron-ore price rises, each shipload generates *more* US dollars and therefore *more* AUD buying. The exchange rate is just a price, and like any price it is set where supply meets demand; a steady, growing wall of structural AUD buying from export proceeds pushes that price up. This is not speculation, not sentiment, not a trade anyone is choosing to put on — it is the mechanical consequence of selling more valuable stuff to foreigners who pay in a different currency. Speculators then *anticipate* and *amplify* this flow, but the bedrock underneath is the real, physical conversion of export revenue.

### Nominal versus real: the terms-of-trade move is partly *meant* to happen

There is a subtle and important point that separates people who half-understand commodity currencies from people who really get them. When a country's terms of trade improve, having its currency appreciate is not a bug — it is, in part, the *economy's own adjustment mechanism* working as designed. A higher real exchange rate (a stronger currency relative to local prices) is how a resource-rich economy *spends* its windfall: a stronger currency makes imported goods cheaper for its citizens, which is precisely how the nation converts a higher export price into a higher standard of living. Economists call the extreme version of this **Dutch disease** — when a commodity boom drives the currency so high that the country's *other* export industries (manufacturing, tourism, agriculture) become uncompetitive and wither, leaving the economy dangerously dependent on the one commodity.

For a trader, the practical upshot is this: a commodity-currency appreciation is *self-reinforcing on the way up and self-reinforcing on the way down*. On the way up, the windfall fuels domestic demand, which raises inflation, which raises rates, which pulls in more capital — and the strong currency itself attracts momentum and carry traders. On the way down, the reverse spiral grinds just as hard. This is why commodity currencies *trend*: the underlying terms-of-trade level moves slowly and persistently, and the currency, the rates, the flows and the sentiment all chase it in the same direction for years at a stretch.

## The headline link: the Australian dollar and iron ore

Australia is the textbook commodity currency, and iron ore is the textbook commodity. Australia produces more than half the iron ore that crosses international borders, and the overwhelming majority of it goes to one customer: China, to be turned into the steel that builds China's apartments, bridges and railways. So the Australian dollar is, to a first approximation, a tradable claim on Chinese steel demand.

Look back at the cover chart, then sit with this scatter, which strips out the time axis and just plots one against the other.

![Scatter of AUD USD versus iron ore price with fit line](/imgs/blogs/terms-of-trade-and-the-commodity-currencies-3.png)

Each dot is one year. The upward-sloping cloud is the relationship: high iron-ore years sit up and to the right (strong Aussie), low iron-ore years sit down and to the left (weak Aussie). The dashed fit line summarizes it, and the correlation coefficient on our dataset is about **+0.50**. That is a real, positive, economically meaningful relationship — strong enough that you would be foolish to ignore iron ore when you trade the Aussie. It is also far from a perfect +1.0, which is the honest part of the story: in roughly half the variance, something *other* than iron ore is driving AUD/USD. We will get to what that something is.

Notice the slope. Our least-squares fit implies that for every extra dollar per tonne on iron ore, AUD/USD rises by roughly **0.0017** — about a sixth of a US cent. That sounds tiny until you remember that iron ore swings by *hundreds* of dollars a tonne over a cycle.

#### Worked example: iron ore from \$60 to \$160 and what the Aussie did

In 2016, iron ore averaged about \$58 a tonne and AUD/USD sat near \$0.724. By 2021, iron ore had roared to about \$159 a tonne — China's post-pandemic stimulus plus a supply shock from a dam disaster in Brazil — and AUD/USD was near \$0.726. Wait: the Aussie barely moved even though iron ore nearly tripled?

This is the honest, instructive case. Round the ore move to a clean \$60 → \$160 jump, a +\$100 per-tonne swing. Our fit slope of 0.0017 says that, *all else equal*, AUD/USD should have risen by 100 × 0.0017 ≈ **0.17**, from \$0.72 to about **\$0.89**. Instead it landed at \$0.726 — almost flat. The \$0.16 of "missing" appreciation went somewhere. It went into the rate differential: by 2021 the US Federal Reserve was about to embark on the fastest hiking cycle in forty years while the RBA sat pinned near zero, and that yawning US-Australia rate gap pulled the Aussie *down* exactly as the iron-ore tailwind was pushing it *up*. The two forces roughly cancelled. **The takeaway: the commodity link is one of several forces on the currency, and a powerful rate-differential move can swallow even a tripling of the export price.**

That single example is worth more than any clean textbook chart, because it teaches the right mental model: terms of trade is a *force*, not a *law*. It pushes hard and consistently in one direction, but it operates in a tug-of-war with the rate differential and global risk appetite. When all three forces align — rising ore, easy Fed, risk-on markets — the Aussie flies. When they fight, the result is a muddle, and the loudest force wins.

It is worth dwelling on *why* China specifically sits behind the Aussie, because it changes how you read the news. China consumes well over half of the world's seaborne iron ore, and the bulk of that comes from Australia and Brazil. China uses that ore to make steel, and it uses steel overwhelmingly for *construction* — apartment towers, bridges, rail, industrial plant. So the demand for Australian iron ore is, at the root, a demand for **Chinese fixed-asset investment**. When you see a headline that China is stimulating its property sector, or conversely that Chinese developers are defaulting and housing starts are collapsing, you are reading a headline about the Australian dollar. The chain runs: Chinese construction → Chinese steel output → Chinese iron-ore imports → the iron-ore price → Australian export income → AUD. Most Aussie traders watch Chinese property data and Dalian iron-ore futures more closely than they watch anything coming out of Australia itself, and they are right to.

#### Worked example: the terms-of-trade ratio in plain numbers

Make the ratio concrete. Suppose, in a simplified Australia, a representative export basket sells for \$100 and a representative import basket costs \$100, so the terms of trade index = 100/100 = **1.00**. Now an iron-ore boom doubles the average export price to \$200 while import prices (cars, electronics, machinery from Asia and Europe) hold at \$100. The new terms of trade = 200/100 = **2.00** — the index has doubled. In real terms, Australia can now import *twice* as much stuff for the same volume of exports. That windfall is enormous: it shows up as a bigger trade surplus, fatter mining profits, higher tax revenue, and a central bank leaning toward higher rates. Every one of those is a reason to buy the currency, which is exactly why a doubling of the export price — even diluted by all the other forces — exerts such relentless upward pressure on the Aussie. **The takeaway: terms of trade is a leverage ratio on national income, and a commodity currency is the financial-market instrument that prices that leverage in real time.**

#### Worked example: a \$1,000,000 long-Aussie position riding the ore cycle

Suppose in late 2014 you put on a \$1,000,000-notional long AUD/USD position when the Aussie traded at \$0.825, betting that iron ore — then around \$97 a tonne and falling — had further to drop and you wanted to *fade* a bounce. You were right about the ore: by 2016 it hit \$58. AUD/USD fell to \$0.724. A long position lost about (0.724 − 0.825) / 0.825 ≈ **−12.2%**, or roughly **−\$122,000** on your million. Had you instead *shorted* the Aussie — the correct expression of a bearish-iron-ore view — you would have made that \$122,000, minus the carry cost of being short a higher-yielding currency. **The takeaway: when you have a commodity view, the cleanest way to express it in size and with leverage is often through the exporter's currency, not the commodity future — but you must get the *direction* of the currency right, which means knowing the commodity-to-currency sign.**

## The petro-currencies: the Canadian dollar, the Norwegian krone, and oil

Swap iron ore for oil and Australia for Canada or Norway, and the same machinery runs. A **petro-currency** is the currency of an economy whose exports — and whose government revenue — lean heavily on crude oil and natural gas. Canada is the world's fourth-largest oil producer; its vast oil-sands reserves in Alberta make energy the country's single biggest export. Norway pumps oil and gas from the North Sea, funnels the proceeds into a sovereign wealth fund that is now the largest in the world, and runs an economy where the petroleum sector is a commanding share of exports and state income. When oil moves, both currencies feel it through every channel in the income chain.

![Petro currency loop graph oil price to currency strength](/imgs/blogs/terms-of-trade-and-the-commodity-currencies-4.png)

That figure shows why the petro-currency link is, if anything, *tighter* than a pure trade story would suggest. Oil hits a petro-economy through two reinforcing routes at once. The first is the trade route: higher oil means more export revenue, a wider trade surplus, more foreign currency to convert. The second is the *fiscal* route: oil-producing governments tax the production heavily — royalties, resource taxes, the dividends from a state oil company — so a higher oil price fattens the national budget, lets the government spend more, and stokes domestic demand and inflation. Both routes push toward higher interest rates and a stronger currency. Two roads, one destination.

Now, the Canadian dollar carries a wrinkle that trips up beginners, and you must internalize it: **the Canadian dollar is quoted as USD/CAD, which *inverts* the loonie**. USD/CAD is "how many Canadian dollars per one US dollar." When the loonie *strengthens*, it takes *fewer* Canadian dollars to buy a US dollar, so USD/CAD *falls*. A strong loonie is a *low* USD/CAD; a weak loonie is a *high* USD/CAD. (If quote conventions feel slippery, the mechanics live in [how to read an FX quote](/blog/trading/forex/base-quote-pips-and-how-to-read-an-fx-quote).) So when oil rises and the loonie strengthens, USD/CAD goes *down*. The relationship between oil and USD/CAD is therefore *negative* — and that sign-flip is exactly the kind of thing that costs people money when they trade a correlation they have not thought through.

![USD CAD versus WTI oil dual axis with inverted loonie scale](/imgs/blogs/terms-of-trade-and-the-commodity-currencies-5.png)

To make the figure readable, we plotted USD/CAD on an *inverted* axis so that "up" means a stronger loonie. With that flip, the loonie and oil rise and fall together, just as the income channel predicts. Keep the underlying convention straight in your head: on a raw USD/CAD chart, a weaker oil price shows up as a *higher* USD/CAD line.

#### Worked example: a Canadian oil exporter's revenue when oil halves

A mid-sized Canadian producer pumps 100,000 barrels of crude a day. At a WTI oil price of \$90 a barrel, its daily gross revenue is 100,000 × \$90 = **\$9,000,000** — nine million US dollars a day flowing into the company, much of which gets converted into Canadian dollars to pay wages, taxes and Alberta royalties. Now oil halves to \$45 (roughly the 2020 collapse, when WTI averaged about \$40 for the year and briefly traded *negative* in April). Daily revenue falls to 100,000 × \$45 = **\$4,500,000** — the company's US-dollar inflow is cut in half. Multiply that across the whole Canadian energy sector and you have a dramatic shrinkage in the daily US-dollar-to-Canadian-dollar conversion flow, plus a hole in federal and provincial budgets, plus a Bank of Canada now more likely to *cut* rates than hike. Every channel points the same way. USD/CAD duly climbed from about 1.16 in 2014 to 1.36 in the 2022 stress and 1.44 by 2024. **The takeaway: an oil price that halves does not just dent the loonie at the margin — it cuts the physical conversion flow, the budget, and the rate outlook all at once, which is why petro-currencies move so hard on oil.**

There is a second subtlety that makes the Canadian dollar a particularly rich case: Canada sells most of its oil to a *single* customer, the United States, and it sells it at a *discount*. Canadian heavy crude (Western Canadian Select) trades below the WTI benchmark because it is lower quality and landlocked, dependent on a limited pipeline network to reach refineries. When pipeline capacity is tight, the discount blows out, and Canadian producers earn even less than the headline WTI price suggests. So a loonie trader does not just watch WTI — they watch the *WCS-to-WTI differential* and the politics of pipeline approvals, because those determine how much of the global oil price actually reaches Canadian income. This is a reminder that "the commodity price" is sometimes a simplification: what matters is the *realized* price the exporter actually receives, net of quality and transport discounts.

Norway's krone tells a subtler version of the same story. Norway is even more concentrated in petroleum than Canada, so on the pure terms-of-trade logic the krone "should" track oil tightly. In practice the link is noisier, for an instructive reason: Norway sterilizes much of its oil windfall. The government funnels petroleum revenue into its sovereign wealth fund — the Government Pension Fund Global, now worth well over a trillion dollars — which invests *abroad*, so a chunk of the oil-dollar inflow never gets converted into krone; it stays in foreign assets by design. That deliberate plumbing weakens the day-to-day oil-to-krone transmission even though the underlying economic exposure is enormous. It is a beautiful example of how *policy* sits between the commodity and the currency, and we will see that theme again. The lesson generalizes: the strength of any commodity-currency link depends not only on how exposed the economy is, but on how much of the windfall is *allowed to flow through* into the domestic currency versus being parked offshore.

## The Brazilian real: a commodity currency with a carry kicker

Australia and Canada are developed economies; their commodity link is relatively pure. The **Brazilian real** adds a second layer that you must hold in your head simultaneously, because it changes how the currency trades.

Brazil is a commodity *superpower* — the world's largest exporter of soybeans, a giant in iron ore, beef, sugar, coffee and increasingly crude oil. So the first layer is the familiar terms-of-trade story: when soft-commodity and metals prices rise (often driven, again, by Chinese demand), Brazil's income rises and the real catches a bid.

The second layer is **carry**. Brazil is an emerging market with structurally high inflation and a central bank that runs *very* high interest rates to fight it — the policy rate sat around 12-13% in 2024 while the US paid around 4.5%. That enormous rate gap makes the real a magnet for the *carry trade*: global investors borrow in a low-yielding currency and park the money in high-yielding reais to pocket the difference. (The full mechanics of carry — and the violent unwinds — live in [FX as a factor zoo](/blog/trading/forex/fx-as-a-factor-zoo-carry-value-momentum-and-dollar) and the [macro carry-trade post](/blog/trading/macro-trading/carry-trade-unwinds-1998-2008-2024-when-leverage-breaks); we do not re-derive them here.)

So the real is a *two-factor* currency: a commodity bet *and* a carry bet stacked on top of each other. In good times — strong commodities, calm global markets, risk-on — both factors pull the same way and the real can rally hard. In bad times — a commodity slump *and* a global risk-off scramble for the dollar — both factors reverse together, and the real can fall with terrifying speed as carry traders unwind. This is why emerging-market commodity currencies are so much more violent than their developed-market cousins: they carry two leveraged bets, not one, and the two are correlated precisely when you least want them to be. The deeper EM-specific machinery — country risk, sudden stops, reserve adequacy — is covered in [emerging-market and sovereign debt](/blog/trading/fixed-income/emerging-market-and-sovereign-debt-yield-with-country-risk).

#### Worked example: the real's two engines in a risk-off shock

Suppose you hold 1,000,000 Brazilian reais of exposure, funded by borrowing US dollars, to earn the roughly 12% − 4.5% ≈ **7.5%** annual carry. For a year, all is calm: you collect about 1,000,000 × 7.5% = **75,000 reais** in carry, and the real even drifts up a couple of percent on firm soybean prices. You feel like a genius. Then a global shock hits — a China growth scare that simultaneously craters commodity prices *and* sends every investor fleeing to the safety of the US dollar. The real drops, say, 15% in a few weeks. Your 1,000,000-real position loses about **150,000 reais** of value in dollar terms — double the *entire year's* carry, gone in weeks — while soybeans fall and your carry edge narrows as the central bank may even cut. **The takeaway: a commodity-plus-carry currency offers you a steady drip of income that flatters your judgment, in exchange for a fat, correlated tail risk that arrives exactly when the commodity story and the risk-off story hit at the same time.**

## Mapping the family: which currency rides which commodity

Before we get to the failure modes, let us lay the whole family out on one map, because the *specificity* of each link is the practical heart of the matter. "Commodity currency" is too coarse — you need to know *which* commodity, because that tells you which chart to watch.

![Commodity currency map grid AUD CAD NOK BRL and their exports](/imgs/blogs/terms-of-trade-and-the-commodity-currencies-6.png)

Read that grid as a cheat sheet. The Australian dollar is a bet on **iron ore** (plus coal and LNG), and underneath that, a bet on **Chinese steel demand** — so an Aussie trader watches Chinese property starts and Dalian iron-ore futures. The Canadian dollar is a bet on **crude oil**, and underneath, on **US energy demand and pipeline capacity** — so a loonie trader watches WTI and the discount on heavy Canadian crude. The Norwegian krone is a bet on **North Sea oil and gas**, and lately on **European natural-gas prices**, which spiked spectacularly during the 2022 energy crisis. The Brazilian real is a bet on **soybeans, iron ore and oil** plus a **high-carry kicker** — so a real trader watches Chinese soybean imports, the Selic policy rate, and global risk appetite all at once.

There are more members of the family that we mention for completeness: the **New Zealand dollar** rides dairy prices (Fonterra's whole-milk-powder auctions move the kiwi), the **South African rand** rides gold and platinum, the **Chilean peso** rides copper so faithfully that the peso is sometimes called a copper proxy, the **Russian ruble** and **Mexican peso** ride oil. Each is a different chart with a currency stamped on it. The skill is not memorizing that "commodity currencies go up when commodities go up" — it is knowing the *specific* export that dominates each one, because that is what lets you anticipate the move instead of explaining it after the fact.

## The long arc: a currency is a low-frequency commodity chart

Step back from the year-to-year wiggles and look at the Australian dollar across its whole modern cycle. The shape tells you something the short charts hide.

![Australian dollar long history 2011 to 2025 with parity line](/imgs/blogs/terms-of-trade-and-the-commodity-currencies-7.png)

The Aussie's great arc — above parity in 2011, then a long structural grind down to the low-sixties by the mid-2020s — is the chart of a *commodity supercycle* told in currency terms. The 2000s and early 2010s were the China construction boom: iron ore and coal prices at multiples of their historical norms, Australia's terms of trade at a century high, the Aussie above parity. Then China rebalanced away from heavy investment, the new supply that high prices had called forth finally arrived, terms of trade normalized, and the currency settled into a permanently lower range. The Aussie did not "go down" because of any single news event; it went down because the *level* of its terms of trade fell from an extraordinary peak back toward something ordinary, and the currency is, over the long run, a low-frequency picture of the terms of trade.

This is the multi-year, structural layer of the same income channel. It is also the layer that pure rate-differential traders miss. A G10 rates trader looking only at the US-Australia yield gap would have been baffled by the Aussie's persistent weakness; the answer was never in the rates screen, it was in the iron-ore screen. **The deepest version of the terms-of-trade idea is this: a commodity currency is a slow-moving real-economy variable wearing a fast-moving financial-market costume.** The costume jitters every day on rate noise and risk sentiment; the body underneath walks at the pace of the commodity supercycle.

## Trading the link: beta, cross-rates and hedging

So far we have explained the *why*. Now the *how* — how a desk actually turns "AUD tracks iron ore" into a position, and the practical tricks that make the commodity link tradeable rather than just true.

The first concept is **beta** — the sensitivity of the currency to the commodity. From our scatter, AUD/USD moves roughly 0.0017 for every \$1/tonne of iron ore, but a more useful way to express it is in *percentage* terms: over a typical cycle, a 10% move in the iron-ore price has historically been associated with something on the order of a 2-4% move in AUD/USD. That fractional beta is the leverage ratio in reverse — the currency moves *less* than the commodity in percentage terms (because the currency also embeds rates, flows and sentiment), but it moves with far more *liquidity* and *leverage* available. A macro fund that wants iron-ore exposure but does not want to deal with futures rolls, storage curves and thin back-month liquidity can buy the Aussie instead and get a diluted, smoother, cheaper version of the same bet.

The second trick is the **cross-rate**, and it is where the craft gets interesting. If you are bullish iron ore but worried that the US dollar is also going to be strong (a Fed-on-the-march scenario that would muddy AUD/USD), you can strip out the dollar by trading the Aussie against *another* currency. **AUD/CAD** pits two commodity currencies against each other — iron ore versus oil — so a position expresses a view on the *relative* terms of trade of Australia and Canada, with the common US-dollar factor largely netted out. **AUD/NZD** pits iron ore against dairy, a pure trans-Tasman commodity-mix bet. **AUD/JPY** is the classic *risk-on* expression — a high-beta commodity currency against the ultimate funding and safe-haven currency — and it behaves almost like a leveraged stock-market index, soaring in risk-on and crashing in risk-off. Choosing the right cross is how a trader isolates the *specific* bet they want and hedges out the factors they do not.

![Grid of cross rates AUD CAD AUD NZD AUD JPY and what each isolates](/imgs/blogs/terms-of-trade-and-the-commodity-currencies-9.png)

The grid is the practitioner's decision table. Read down the left column for the pair, across to what view it expresses, then to the common factor it deletes. The whole art of expressing a commodity view in FX is choosing the row whose "nets out" column matches the risk you most want to avoid.

#### Worked example: isolating the iron-ore-versus-oil view with AUD/CAD

Suppose you believe iron ore will outperform oil over the next quarter — China is stimulating construction while global oil demand softens — but you have *no* view on the US dollar and do not want a Fed surprise to wreck your trade. Buying AUD/USD would expose you to the dollar; so would shorting USD/CAD. Instead you buy **AUD/CAD**. If iron ore rallies and oil sags, the Aussie strengthens and the loonie weakens, and AUD/CAD rises — *regardless* of what the US dollar does, because the dollar moves both legs roughly together and cancels out. On a \$1,000,000 notional, a clean 3% move in your favor is **\$30,000**, earned on a pure relative-commodity view with the dollar risk hedged away. **The takeaway: cross-rates let you trade the *difference* between two terms-of-trade stories and quietly delete the US-dollar factor that would otherwise dominate, which is often the single most useful move in commodity-currency trading.**

The third practical use runs the other way: **hedging**. A real-world miner, oil producer or commodity-importing manufacturer faces exactly this currency-commodity link as a *risk*, not an opportunity. A Japanese steelmaker that buys Australian iron ore in US dollars but sells steel in yen has a layered exposure to the iron-ore price, AUD, USD and JPY all at once. A Canadian oil producer with US-dollar revenue and Canadian-dollar costs is structurally long oil and short USD/CAD whether it likes it or not — which, conveniently, is a *natural hedge*, because a falling oil price (bad for revenue) tends to push USD/CAD up (which inflates the Canadian-dollar value of its US-dollar receipts). Understanding the commodity-currency link is therefore not just a speculator's edge; it is the foundation of how commodity-exposed corporates manage the FX risk baked into their business.

## When the link breaks

We have been honest throughout that the commodity link is a force and not a law. Now let us be systematic about *when* it breaks, because knowing the failure modes is what separates someone who trades the relationship from someone who gets blindsided by it.

![Before and after when the commodity link breaks](/imgs/blogs/terms-of-trade-and-the-commodity-currencies-8.png)

The figure contrasts the regime where the link holds with the regime where it snaps. The link holds when the export price is the loudest thing in the room: ore rising, markets calm, no dramatic rate divergence. The income channel runs cleanly and the currency tracks its commodity. The link *breaks* in three identifiable situations.

**Break one — a dominant rate differential.** This is the one from our earlier worked example. When a major central bank — almost always the Federal Reserve — is moving aggressively, the rate gap can drown out the commodity signal. In 2021-2022, iron ore was firm but the Aussie fell anyway, because the Fed was hiking into the steepest tightening cycle in decades and the relative-rate pull toward the US dollar overwhelmed Australia's commodity tailwind. The commodity was shouting "buy AUD" and the rate gap was screaming "buy USD," and the rate gap won. Whenever the Fed is the main story, expect the commodity links to weaken — the dollar's gravity bends everything (see [the dollar as cross-asset gravity](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-dollar-cross-asset-gravity)).

**Break two — a global risk-off panic.** In a true risk-off scramble — 2008, March 2020, an acute crisis day — investors dump everything risky and run to the safety of the US dollar and the Japanese yen. Commodity currencies are *risk assets*: they are high-beta, often higher-yielding, leveraged to global growth. So in a panic they get sold regardless of what their underlying commodity is doing. The correlation that matters in a crash is not "AUD vs iron ore" but "AUD vs risk appetite," and in a crisis all the risk correlations collapse toward one ([when correlations go to one](/blog/trading/cross-asset/when-correlations-go-to-one-in-a-crisis)). The commodity link is a *fair-weather* relationship; the storm has its own rules.

**Break three — a domestic or policy shock unrelated to the commodity.** A political crisis, a banking wobble, a central bank doing something unexpected, or — as with Norway — a deliberate policy that sterilizes the commodity flow. The currency can decouple from its commodity for reasons that have nothing to do with the export price. Brazil is the classic case: a corruption scandal or a fiscal blowup can sink the real even with soybeans rallying, because the carry-and-country-risk layer takes over.

The practical lesson is to always ask: *what is the loudest force in the room right now?* In calm, commodity-led markets, trade the commodity link with confidence. When the Fed is on the move or markets are panicking, set the commodity chart aside and trade the rate gap and the risk regime instead. The mistake is to treat the link as always-on; the skill is knowing which regime you are in.

There is a deeper and slightly unsettling property here worth naming, because it explains why commodity-currency strategies blow up the way they do. The three forces — the export price, the rate differential, and global risk appetite — are *not independent*. They tend to become correlated at exactly the worst moment. A global recession scare simultaneously crushes commodity prices (less industrial demand), triggers risk-off flight to the dollar (away from high-beta currencies), and prompts the commodity exporter's central bank to *cut* rates (collapsing the carry). All three forces, which in normal times partly offset each other, suddenly point the same direction: down, hard, together. This is why the commodity-currency drawdowns in 2008 and 2020 were so vicious — it was not three separate bets going wrong, it was *one* macro shock pulling every channel in the same direction. A position that looked diversified across "commodity exposure, carry, and risk beta" turns out, in a crisis, to be three copies of the same trade. The honest commodity-currency trader sizes for that correlated tail, not for the placid average.

### A quick tour of the wider family

It is worth a few sentences on the lesser-known members, because each adds a wrinkle that sharpens the general principle. The **New Zealand dollar** rides dairy — Fonterra's fortnightly whole-milk-powder auctions are to the kiwi what iron-ore futures are to the Aussie — which makes the kiwi a clean bet on Chinese and Asian protein demand. The **Chilean peso** is so faithfully tied to copper (Chile is the world's largest copper producer) that desks treat it as a tradable copper proxy; when "Dr. Copper" sneezes on a China-growth scare, the peso catches the cold. The **South African rand** rides gold and platinum, with a heavy dose of domestic political risk layered on top, which makes it one of the most volatile liquid currencies in the world. The **Mexican peso** and the **Russian ruble** are oil currencies, though the peso also trades heavily on US-growth and trade-policy news given Mexico's integration with the US economy. The pattern repeats every time: find the one or two prices that dominate the country's export income, and you have found the chart that drives the currency — adjusted, always, for how much rate-differential and risk-appetite noise is competing for the microphone on any given day.

## Common misconceptions

**"Commodity currencies always go up when commodities go up."** No — they go up when *their specific* commodity goes up *and* no louder force is fighting it. As we saw, iron ore nearly tripled from 2016 to 2021 while AUD/USD ended roughly flat, because the US-Australia rate gap blew out at the same time. The link is conditional, not automatic. Over our full 2011-2025 sample the AUD-iron-ore correlation is about +0.50 — real, but it leaves half the variance to other forces.

**"The Canadian dollar goes up when oil goes up, so USD/CAD goes up when oil goes up."** This sign error costs beginners real money. USD/CAD is *Canadian dollars per US dollar*, so a stronger loonie is a *lower* USD/CAD. Oil up → loonie up → USD/CAD *down*. The relationship between oil and USD/CAD is *negative*. Always nail down which way the pair is quoted before you reason about the sign.

**"If I'm bullish iron ore, I should just buy iron ore futures — the Aussie is a roundabout way to do it."** Sometimes, but the currency is often the *better* vehicle, because it gives you leverage, deep liquidity, low transaction costs, twenty-four-hour trading, and a positive carry if the exporter is the higher-yielding currency. A commodity future has roll costs, storage-curve complications and thinner liquidity in the back months. Many macro funds express a commodity view through the matching currency precisely for these reasons — though you take on the basis risk that the rate gap or risk sentiment moves against you.

**"Norway is a huge oil exporter, so the krone must be the tightest petro-currency."** It is the most *exposed*, but not the most *tightly linked* day to day, because Norway deliberately invests its oil windfall abroad through its sovereign wealth fund, so much of the oil-dollar inflow never gets converted into krone. Policy plumbing sits between the commodity and the currency. Exposure and transmission are different things.

**"A commodity currency is a safe way to bet on global growth."** It is a *leveraged* way, which is the opposite of safe. Commodity currencies are high-beta risk assets that crash in panics. The Brazilian real can hand you a steady 7%+ carry for years and then give it all back in a single risk-off month. Treating a high-carry commodity currency as a sleepy income stream is how carry traders get carried out.

## How it shows up in real markets

**The 2011 peak and the long Aussie bear market (2011-2024).** The cleanest real-world demonstration of the whole thesis. Iron ore peaked near \$168/tonne in 2011 as China's investment boom raged; the Australian dollar peaked above parity at the same time. As China rebalanced and new supply arrived over the next decade, iron ore fell to the \$60s by 2016 and settled around \$100 by the mid-2020s, and the Aussie ground down to the low-sixties. Anyone who understood that AUD is a low-frequency iron-ore chart had the entire decade's direction right from a single commodity view — while pure rates traders kept getting puzzled by an Aussie that stayed weak even when Australia's yields looked fine.

**The 2014-2016 oil crash and the loonie.** WTI crude fell from over \$100 in mid-2014 to under \$30 in early 2016 as US shale flooded the market and OPEC declined to cut. The Canadian dollar followed: USD/CAD climbed from about 1.16 in 2014 to about 1.34 in 2016 — a roughly 15% loonie depreciation that mapped almost perfectly onto the oil collapse, through the trade channel (less export revenue), the fiscal channel (Alberta's budget cratered), and the rate channel (the Bank of Canada cut). Every petro-currency channel firing at once.

**April 2020: negative oil and the petro-currencies.** When WTI briefly traded *negative* on April 20, 2020 — storage tanks full, no buyers, sellers paying to offload barrels — the petro-currencies were already reeling. But notice what dominated: this was a *risk-off* episode, so the US dollar was strengthening against almost everything regardless of oil. The loonie and the krone fell, but the *prime mover* in those weeks was the global dash for dollars, with the oil collapse as a compounding blow. A textbook case of "break two" — in a panic, the risk-off flight overrides the commodity link, even for the currency of the very commodity that is collapsing.

**The 2022 European gas crisis and the krone.** When Russia throttled gas to Europe in 2022, European natural-gas prices spiked to extraordinary levels. Norway, a major gas supplier to Europe, saw a colossal terms-of-trade windfall. Yet the krone's rally was more muted than the raw gas move would imply — partly the sovereign-wealth-fund sterilization, partly a strong US dollar that year capping every non-dollar currency. The exposure was historic; the currency transmission was dampened by policy and by the dollar's dominance. A clean illustration that "exposure" and "currency move" are different quantities.

**The Brazilian real's commodity-plus-carry rides.** Across 2022-2024 the real was one of the better-performing emerging-market currencies in several stretches, powered by firm commodity prices *and* one of the world's highest real interest rates drawing in carry flows. But it remained whip-fast: domestic fiscal worries and global risk-off bouts repeatedly knocked it back, sometimes sharply, even when commodities held up. The real never let traders forget that it carries two leveraged bets, not one. For the broader allocator's lens on how FX sits among asset classes, see [FX, the relative-value layer](/blog/trading/cross-asset/fx-currencies-the-relative-value-layer).

**The mid-2020s Aussie: a textbook "link goes quiet" regime.** The years 2022 through 2024 are a clinic in regime-dependence. Iron ore stayed reasonably firm — averaging around \$120 in 2022, \$110 in 2024, never collapsing — yet AUD/USD ground *down* from about \$0.68 in 2022 to \$0.62 in 2024, a fresh multi-year low. On the pure commodity story this makes no sense: ore was fine, so the Aussie should have been fine. The explanation is "break one." The US Federal Reserve had hiked to over 5% while the Reserve Bank of Australia topped out lower and was widely expected to cut sooner; the resulting US-Australia rate gap, combined with a broadly strong dollar, simply overwhelmed the commodity signal. A trader who had ignored the rate gap and traded only the ore chart would have been long the Aussie into a 9% decline. A trader who asked "what is the loudest force in the room?" would have answered "the Fed" and stepped aside — or shorted the Aussie outright. The episode is the single best modern reminder that the commodity link is a *fair-weather* relationship that goes silent whenever the dollar's rate gravity takes over.

## The takeaway: how to read a commodity currency

Here is what all of this means for how you actually read one of these currencies on a screen.

When you pull up the Australian dollar, do not start with the Australian dollar. Start with **iron ore**, and behind it, with Chinese steel demand. When you pull up the Canadian dollar, start with **WTI crude** and the discount on heavy Canadian oil — and remember the USD/CAD sign-flip, so that a falling oil price you expect to weaken the loonie shows up as a *rising* USD/CAD. When you pull up the Norwegian krone, start with **oil and European gas**, but discount the link for the sovereign-wealth-fund plumbing. When you pull up the Brazilian real, hold *two* charts at once — its commodities *and* its carry — and respect that both reverse together in a risk-off storm.

Then, every single time, ask the one question that governs whether the commodity link is even *operative* today: **what is the loudest force in the room?** If it is the export price — calm markets, no big Fed move — trade the commodity link with confidence; the currency will track its commodity. If it is the **rate differential** — the Fed or another major central bank on the march — the commodity link goes quiet and you should be trading the rate gap, because the dollar's gravity will bend the currency regardless of what iron ore or oil is doing. If it is **risk appetite** — a panic, a flight to safety — forget the commodity entirely and trade the risk regime, because in a crash every commodity currency is just a high-beta risk asset that gets sold.

There is also a sizing discipline that falls straight out of everything above. Because the commodity link is *conditional* — it works when the export price is the loudest force and fails when it is not — you should never bet on it as if it were certain. Size a commodity-link trade for the world in which a Fed surprise or a risk-off shock overrides your thesis, because that world arrives more often than the clean charts suggest, and it arrives with all three forces pointing the same way. The trader who survives commodity currencies is the one who treats the +0.50 correlation as what it is: a strong tendency worth trading around, embedded in a relationship that will, a meaningful fraction of the time, do the opposite of what the commodity is doing. Respect the conditionality and the link is a genuine edge; forget it and the link is a trap that springs precisely when you are most leveraged into it.

That hierarchy is the whole craft. The terms-of-trade income channel is a powerful, persistent, and refreshingly *visible* force — you can watch its driver tick on a commodity exchange in real time. But it operates inside the same two-variable world that governs every currency in this series: the gap between two countries' interest rates, and the flow of money across borders. Terms of trade is simply the cleanest case where a *commodity price* moves both of those at once. Master the commodity currencies and you have not learned a special exception to the rules of FX — you have learned the rules themselves, written in a font large enough to read.

The Australian dollar that was worth a dollar-ten in 2011 and sixty-two cents in 2024 did not get unlucky. It got *cheaper iron ore*. Once you can read that sentence as a currency chart, you can read any commodity currency in the world.
