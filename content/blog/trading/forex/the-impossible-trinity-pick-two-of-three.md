> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A country can choose at most two of three goals: a fixed exchange rate, free movement of capital across its border, and an interest-rate policy set for its own economy. Want all three? The arbitrage between two countries' interest rates will pick the third one for you, violently.
>
> - The three goals are a **fixed exchange rate**, **free capital mobility**, and **independent monetary policy**. The trinity says you get two; the third is surrendered.
> - The enforcer is **carry arbitrage**: if you peg, open the border, *and* keep your rate above the anchor's, traders borrow the cheap currency and buy yours until your reserves run out and a leg snaps.
> - Each corner has real tenants: **the US, euro area, and Japan float** (drop the peg); **Hong Kong and the Gulf hard-peg** (drop their own policy); **China and Vietnam run capital controls** (drop free flows).
> - The one number to remember: a peg means you import the anchor's interest rate. With a dollar peg in 2024, your policy rate is **the Fed's 4.50%**, whether your economy needs it or not.

On 15 January 2015, at 9:30 in the morning Zurich time, the Swiss National Bank held a promise it had made and kept for three and a half years: the euro would never buy fewer than 1.20 Swiss francs. The bank had printed hundreds of billions of francs to enforce that floor, selling its own currency by the truckload to keep it from getting too strong. Markets trusted it. Hedge funds, Polish mortgage borrowers, and Japanese retail traders had all built positions on the assumption that the floor was permanent.

At 9:31, it was gone. The SNB announced it would no longer defend the floor. The euro fell from 1.20 francs to below 0.85 in minutes, a 30% move in the world's most boringly stable currency pair, the kind of move that is supposed to take a decade. Brokers blew up. A retail FX firm in New Jersey went insolvent before lunch. The franc had been a coiled spring, and the spring released because the SNB had been trying to do something the laws of international finance do not permit: hold an exchange-rate target, keep its border open to the world's capital, *and* keep its own interest rate where it wanted it. You cannot have all three. The franc taught that lesson in ninety seconds.

This post is about the rule the SNB ran into, the single most important framework in international macroeconomics. It is called the **impossible trinity**, or the **trilemma**, and once you internalize it, every exchange-rate regime on earth, every currency crisis, every central-bank intervention, snaps into focus as a move in the same three-cornered game. We will build it from zero, prove *why* you can only have two, watch an arbitrageur break a country that tries for three, and then tour the corners: who floats, who pegs, who walls off capital, and why China and Vietnam built a clever cheat that bends the rule without quite breaking it.

![The impossible trinity triangle with the three goals on the corners and the three feasible pairs](/imgs/blogs/the-impossible-trinity-pick-two-of-three-1.png)

That triangle is the whole post in one picture. Three goals on the corners. Three sides, each side being a pair you *can* have. Pick a side; you have grabbed two corners and abandoned the third. There is no point in the middle that touches all three. The rest of this article is just walking around that triangle slowly enough to understand why.

## Foundations: The three goals and why two is the limit

Let me define the three goals precisely, because the whole framework lives or dies on getting them exact. An **exchange rate** is the relative price of two monies, how many units of one currency it takes to buy one unit of another, written as a pair like USD/VND (25,450 dong per dollar) or EUR/USD (1.08 dollars per euro). When this post says a country pursues a goal, it means its central bank, the institution that issues the currency and sets the country's benchmark interest rate, is trying to deliver it.

**Goal 1 — a fixed exchange rate.** The central bank promises that its currency will trade at a set price against an anchor, almost always the US dollar (or the euro for European neighbors). "One dollar equals exactly 7.80 Hong Kong dollars," and the Hong Kong Monetary Authority commits to making that true at all times. The appeal is enormous: importers and exporters get price certainty, foreign investors don't fear a currency wipeout, and inflation gets anchored to the anchor country's. A stable currency is a national amenity, like clean water.

**Goal 2 — free capital mobility.** Money can cross the border freely in both directions. A foreigner can wire dollars in, buy local bonds, and wire the proceeds back out the next day with no permission slip, no quota, no tax. Conversely, a local can move savings abroad whenever they like. This is the default for rich, open economies because it is hugely valuable: capital flows to where it earns the most, which funds investment, deepens markets, and lets a country borrow from the rest of the world to grow faster than its own savings would allow.

**Goal 3 — independent monetary policy.** The central bank sets the country's interest rate to suit the *domestic* economy. If unemployment is high, it cuts rates to stimulate; if inflation runs hot, it hikes to cool things down. This is the single most powerful lever a government has over its own business cycle. A central bank that can't set rates for its own economy has handed away its main macroeconomic steering wheel.

Each goal, taken alone, is plainly desirable. A country would love all three: a rock-stable currency, an open and modern financial system, *and* a central bank that fights its own recessions. The trilemma's cruel claim is that this trio is internally contradictory. You can pair any two, but the moment you reach for the third, an economic force, not a policy choice, a *force*, comes and rips one of them out of your hands.

### Why two is the ceiling: the arbitrage that does the enforcing

The enforcer is **arbitrage**, the act of exploiting a price difference for riskless profit. Here is the mechanism in one sentence, then we'll slow it down: *if money can move freely (Goal 2) and the exchange rate is fixed (Goal 1), then your interest rate is forced to equal the anchor's, which destroys Goal 3.*

Why? Because of a relationship called **interest-rate parity**. Suppose your currency is pegged to the dollar at a rate that everyone believes will hold, and your border is open. Now suppose your central bank tries to be independent and sets your interest rate at 8% while the dollar (the anchor) pays 3%. A trader anywhere in the world can now do the following: borrow dollars at 3%, convert them to your currency at the fixed rate, deposit at 8%, and because the exchange rate is fixed, convert back at the *same* rate later with no currency risk. That is a guaranteed 5% spread for taking essentially no risk. (We are linking out here rather than re-deriving: the rate side of this, how the interest rate and the curve are set, belongs to fixed income; see [the forward-rate mechanics](/blog/trading/fixed-income/forward-rates-what-the-market-expects-rates-to-be) and [how monetary policy moves currencies through the rate differential](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-monetary-policy-moves-currencies-rate-differentials). The trilemma is what happens when that differential collides with a peg.)

A 5% riskless return is not a small thing. It is a screaming siren. Every leveraged fund, every corporate treasury, every carry trader on the planet will pile in, borrowing the cheap currency and buying yours. The inflow is, in principle, *unlimited*: as long as the spread exists and the peg is believed, there is no reason to stop. That flood of demand for your currency would normally push its price up, but you've promised to fix the price, so your central bank must absorb the flood, printing your currency to sell to all those buyers. Printing money expands the money supply, which pushes your interest rate back *down* toward 3%. The arbitrage doesn't stop until your rate equals the anchor's, parity, at which point the free lunch disappears. Your "independent" 8% policy was an illusion the whole time. The peg plus the open border dragged your rate down to the dollar's. Goal 3 is gone.

The same logic runs in every direction. If you keep your own rate (Goal 3) *and* leave the border open (Goal 2), then the gap between your rate and the world's will set off capital flows that move your currency, so you cannot also pin its price (Goal 1 falls). And if you insist on both the fixed rate (Goal 1) and your own policy (Goal 3), then you must *close* the border (Goal 2 falls) so the arbitrageurs can't act on the gap. There is no escape. Two corners, always two, and the arbitrage picks the third.

### Where the idea came from: Mundell, Fleming, and a model born from gold

The trilemma isn't folk wisdom; it has a precise intellectual pedigree, and knowing it helps you trust the framework. In the early 1960s, two economists working largely independently, Robert Mundell at the IMF and Marcus Fleming, also at the IMF, built a model of how a small open economy behaves under fixed versus floating exchange rates with capital mobility. The result is now called the **Mundell-Fleming model**, and the impossible trinity is its sharpest single conclusion. Mundell won the Nobel Prize in 1999 partly for this work, and his insight, that a country's choice of exchange-rate regime mechanically determines whether it has monetary independence, reshaped how central banks think about their own job.

What's striking is that Mundell and Fleming were writing under the **Bretton Woods** system, the post-war arrangement in which most major currencies were pegged to the US dollar, which was in turn pegged to gold at \$35 an ounce. Capital mobility back then was *low*, governments restricted cross-border flows heavily, so countries could hold both their pegs and a measure of policy independence by sitting on the capital-controls corner without anyone calling it that. As controls were dismantled through the 1970s, 80s, and 90s and capital became genuinely mobile, the trilemma's bite sharpened: countries that had quietly enjoyed all three found they now had to choose, and the ones that didn't choose got chosen *for*, by the bond market. Bretton Woods itself died in 1971 precisely because the United States could not simultaneously hold the dollar's gold peg, allow capital to move, and run the loose domestic policy it wanted to fund Vietnam-War spending. The world's reserve currency hit the trilemma wall, and the gold peg was the leg that snapped.

So the trinity is not a quirk of small emerging economies. It governed the dollar itself. Every time you read about a currency regime, you are watching a policymaker make Mundell's choice, usually without saying his name, and the markets enforce his theorem whether or not anyone in the room has heard of it.

#### Worked example: the riskless carry on a credible peg

Take a concrete case. A trader runs a fund and notices that a small country, call it Pegland, pegs its currency, the peg-dollar (PGD), at exactly 1.00 per US dollar, the border is open, and Pegland's central bank is paying 8.00% on one-year deposits while US dollars pay 3.00%. The trader borrows **\$100,000,000** at 3.00%. They convert it at the peg into 100,000,000 PGD (since 1 PGD = \$1). They deposit the PGD at 8.00% for a year, which grows to 108,000,000 PGD. Meanwhile the dollar loan accrues to \$103,000,000 owed. At year-end, the peg still holds, so the trader converts 108,000,000 PGD back to \$108,000,000, repays \$103,000,000, and keeps **\$5,000,000** — a clean 5% on borrowed money, for taking (they believe) no currency risk at all. The takeaway: a credible peg with an open border and a rate gap is a printing press for the people on the other side of the central bank, which is exactly why the gap cannot survive.

## The peg paradox: why a fixed rate hands your interest rate to a foreign country

Let's sit with the most counterintuitive consequence, because it's the one that surprises people most. **Pegging your currency means giving up control of your own interest rate.** It feels backwards. Surely a strong central bank that can hold a peg is *more* powerful, not less? But the trilemma says the opposite: the peg is precisely what chains your rate to the anchor's.

Consider a country, Hong Kong is the cleanest real example, that runs a hard peg to the US dollar and keeps its border wide open to global capital. By the arbitrage argument above, Hong Kong's interest rate is forced to track the Federal Reserve's. When the Fed hikes, Hong Kong hikes, even if Hong Kong's own economy is weak and would prefer a cut. When the Fed cuts, Hong Kong cuts, even if Hong Kong's property market is overheating and would prefer a hike. The Hong Kong Monetary Authority is, on interest rates, essentially a branch office of the Fed. It does not get a vote. The chart below makes the cost vivid: those policy rates are what an economy actually surrenders when it pegs.

![End-2024 policy rates by central bank showing the Fed rate a dollar peg forces you to import](/imgs/blogs/the-impossible-trinity-pick-two-of-three-5.png)

Read that chart through the trilemma. The Bank of Japan held its rate at 0.25% at the end of 2024 because Japan *floats* — it kept its own policy and dropped the peg, so it could run an ultra-loose rate even as the Fed sat at 4.50%. The European Central Bank set 3.00% for the euro area's needs, again because the euro floats against the dollar. But a country that hard-pegs to the dollar doesn't get to be on that chart with its own bar; its bar *is* the Fed's, that red dashed line, 4.50%, no matter what its domestic unemployment or inflation looks like. The peg is a decision to outsource the single most important macroeconomic tool to Washington.

This is why economists say a peg "imports" the anchor's monetary policy. And it explains a pattern that confuses newcomers: why some countries with apparently sound finances still suffer booms and busts. A dollar-pegged economy gets the Fed's interest rate, which is calibrated for the US cycle, not theirs. If the US is booming and hiking while the pegged country is in recession, the pegged country gets punishingly high rates exactly when it needs low ones. The peg delivers stability of the *exchange rate* at the cost of stability of the *domestic economy*. You traded one kind of volatility for another.

#### Worked example: the imported-rate squeeze on a pegged borrower

A property developer in a dollar-pegged economy borrows **\$50,000,000** (in local currency, but rates track the dollar) to build apartments, financed at a floating rate of "the anchor rate plus 2%." When they signed, the Fed's rate was 0.25%, so they paid 2.25%, or **\$1,125,000** a year in interest. Then the Fed, fighting US inflation that has nothing to do with this developer's country, hikes to 5.00%. The peg forces the local rate up in lockstep, so the developer now pays 7.00%, or **\$3,500,000** a year — their interest bill more than tripled, with no change in their own economy, purely because the anchor's central bank moved. The takeaway: under a peg, your borrowing costs are written in a foreign capital, and a foreign central bank can squeeze you to defend a fight that isn't yours.

## The arbitrage in motion: how a country that wants all three gets broken

Now the dramatic case: a country that *refuses* to choose. It pegs the currency (Goal 1), leaves the border open (Goal 2), and stubbornly holds its own interest rate above the anchor's (Goal 3). It wants all three. The trilemma says it can't have them, and the way the contradiction resolves is a currency crisis. Let's walk the mechanism step by step, because this exact sequence, in different costumes, is the plot of Black Wednesday 1992, the Asian crisis of 1997, and every speculative attack since.

![The carry arbitrage pipeline that drains a central bank's reserves and forces a leg to break](/imgs/blogs/the-impossible-trinity-pick-two-of-three-3.png)

Start at the left of that pipeline. The setup: the peg is held, the border is open, the local rate is, say, 8% against an anchor rate of 3%. The 5-point gap is a standing invitation. A trader borrows the anchor currency cheaply (the funding leg), converts at the peg, and parks the cash at the higher local rate (the high-yield leg). This is the **carry trade**, the structural engine of FX markets; we lean on the dedicated treatment in [what moves exchange rates — rates, flows, carry](/blog/trading/macro-trading/what-moves-exchange-rates-rates-flows-carry) and [carry-trade unwinds across 1998, 2008, and 2024](/blog/trading/macro-trading/carry-trade-unwinds-1998-2008-2024-when-leverage-breaks) rather than re-deriving carry here. The point for the trilemma is that the carry flow is *self-reinforcing while the peg holds*: every dollar of guaranteed spread pulls in another dollar of capital.

Now watch the central bank's predicament. All those traders converting anchor currency into the local one create relentless buying pressure on the local currency. To hold the peg, the central bank must lean against that pressure: it sells local currency (effectively printing it) and accumulates foreign reserves. That sounds sustainable, the bank can print its own currency without limit, but printing expands the money supply and pushes the local rate down, which is the very thing the bank was trying to prevent. So to keep its rate *up* (Goal 3) while holding the peg, the bank must do the opposite of accommodating, it must drain liquidity, which only widens the gap and pulls in *more* carry. It is fighting itself.

The crisis comes when sentiment flips. Suppose traders begin to doubt the peg, maybe the country's reserves are visibly shrinking, or its trade deficit is widening, or an election looms. Now the carry trade runs in reverse and turns into a **speculative attack**. Traders who were long the local currency rush to sell it back for the anchor before the peg breaks. The central bank, which was buying reserves on the way up, must now *sell* its reserves to buy back its own currency and defend the peg on the way down. Reserves are finite. The bank has a war chest of, say, \$80 billion; the global capital that can be thrown at it is measured in trillions. The arithmetic is brutal and one-sided. (The formal model of this, why the attack is a self-fulfilling coordination game, belongs to game theory; see [the central-bank credibility game](/blog/trading/game-theory/the-central-bank-game-credibility-commitment-and-dont-fight-the-fed). The trilemma tells you *which* countries are exposed to the attack in the first place: only the ones standing in the impossible middle.)

When the reserves run low enough, the bank faces the trilemma's final, unavoidable choice. It can abandon the peg (let the currency float, sacrificing Goal 1), or it can slam its rate down to the anchor's and surrender Goal 3, or it can throw up emergency capital controls and sacrifice Goal 2. One leg *must* go. The market doesn't care which; it just knows the all-three position is untenable, and it gets paid to prove it. This is what George Soros did to the Bank of England in 1992, betting that the UK couldn't hold the pound in the European exchange-rate mechanism while running its own policy with an open border; the famous case is told in [Soros and Black Wednesday](/blog/trading/finance/soros-bank-of-england-1992-black-wednesday).

#### Worked example: \$100M moving against a peg until reserves break

Make the attack concrete. A central bank pegs at 10.00 local per dollar and holds **\$80,000,000,000** in reserves. A hedge fund believes the peg will break and the currency will fall to 14.00 (a 40% devaluation). The fund borrows **\$100,000,000** worth of local currency (1,000,000,000 units at the 10.00 peg) and sells it to the central bank for **\$100,000,000**, draining that much from the war chest. It is one of dozens of funds doing the same; collectively they sell \$60,000,000,000 in a week, and reserves crater from \$80B to \$20B. The bank capitulates and floats; the currency falls to 14.00. The fund now buys back its 1,000,000,000 local units at 14.00, which costs only **\$71,428,571**, repays the loan, and pockets roughly **\$28,571,429** on the \$100M trade, minus interest — about a 28% return for forcing the inevitable. The takeaway: when a country stands in the impossible middle, breaking the peg isn't a tail risk to bet against, it's an arithmetic certainty to bet *on*, and the central bank's reserves are just the size of the prize.

### The arithmetic of defense: why the central bank almost always loses the outflow fight

It's worth dwelling on the asymmetry, because it's the reason pegs in the impossible middle eventually break, and it's not obvious until you put numbers on it. A central bank has two very different fights to wage, and it is strong in one and fatally weak in the other.

On the **inflow** side, when too much money wants in and the currency is pushed *up* against the peg, the bank defends by selling its own currency and buying foreign reserves. It can do this without limit, because it can create its own currency by typing numbers into a computer. There is no hard ceiling. The only cost is that all that money-printing inflates the domestic economy and bloats the central bank's balance sheet, which is why even the inflow defense eventually becomes politically and economically intolerable (the SNB's franc floor died on the inflow side, its balance sheet ballooning to an uncomfortable share of Swiss GDP).

On the **outflow** side, the side that produces actual crises, the bank faces the opposite problem and it is brutal. When money wants *out* and the currency is pushed *down* against the peg, the bank must buy back its own currency, and to do that it must *sell foreign reserves*. It cannot create dollars. Its dollar reserves are a finite stockpile. Once they hit zero, the defense is over, full stop, regardless of how determined the bank is. This is why the relevant number for any peg's survival is the size of its reserves relative to the pool of capital that could flee. A useful yardstick is **months of import cover**: a country is generally considered to have an adequate buffer if its reserves can pay for at least three months of imports. Vietnam's reserves at the end of 2024, around \$82 billion, covered only about 2.9 months, below that rule of thumb, which is exactly why Vietnam leans on capital controls rather than trying to defend a hard line.

#### Worked example: how many days of defense your reserves buy

Put numbers on the asymmetry. A central bank holds **\$60,000,000,000** in reserves and pegs its currency. In a calm month, daily outflows are manageable, maybe \$1 billion, and the bank barely notices. Then confidence cracks and the outflow jumps to **\$8,000,000,000 a day** as funds rush for the exit. At that burn rate, the war chest lasts **60 ÷ 8 = 7.5 days**. The bank can hike rates to slow the bleeding, raising the cost of shorting the currency, but each hike crushes its own domestic economy (there's Goal 3 dying to defend Goal 1), and the market knows the reserve math as well as the bank does. Seeing only seven days of ammunition, traders *accelerate* their selling to be out before the break, which shortens the runway further. The takeaway: a peg under attack faces a countdown clock the whole market can read, and a defense that visibly has days left invites the very stampede that ends it.

## The three corners, with real tenants

So if you can't have all three, what does the world actually do? Every economy lives at one of the three corners, the three feasible pairs. Naming the corner an economy chose tells you instantly what it gave up, and which crises it's exposed to. Here is the full map.

![A matrix of the three trinity corners, the two goals each keeps, the one it surrenders, and example countries](/imgs/blogs/the-impossible-trinity-pick-two-of-three-4.png)

**Corner A — the float.** Keep free capital and your own policy; let the exchange rate go. This is where the United States, the euro area, Japan, the UK, and Australia live. They run their interest rate for their own economy and leave their borders wide open, and the price they pay is a currency that swings, sometimes a lot. USD/JPY went from 103 to 157 between 2020 and 2024 because Japan kept rates near zero while the Fed hiked to 5.5%, and the float absorbed the entire stress of that rate gap in the exchange rate. A floater never has a currency crisis in the trilemma sense, because there is no peg to break; the currency *is* the shock absorber. The cost is uncertainty: an exporter never knows what next quarter's exchange rate will be.

There's a subtlety worth naming here, because it trips up newcomers who assume "floating" means "the central bank does nothing about the currency." It doesn't. A floater can still *intervene* occasionally, buying or selling its own currency to smooth a disorderly move, and many do; Japan sold tens of billions of dollars in 2022 and 2024 to slow the yen's slide. The line between a managed float and a free float is one of *degree and intent*: a floater intervenes to smooth volatility but does not defend a particular level, whereas a peg commits to a level and must defend it to the death. The trilemma cares about commitment, not activity. As long as a country refuses to defend a fixed level, it keeps Goal 3 and its currency stays the shock absorber, no matter how often it nudges the market. This is why the dollar, the freest float of all, still moves in a recognizable pattern, strong in both US-outperformance and in global panics, the so-called dollar smile, without any of that contradicting its floating status; the pattern comes from flows and rate gaps, not from a defended peg.

**Corner B — the hard peg.** Keep the fixed rate and free capital; surrender your own policy. Hong Kong is the textbook case (pegged to the dollar at 7.80 since 1983, with the Hong Kong Monetary Authority owning no independent rate), along with the Gulf oil states (Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and others peg to the dollar), Denmark (pegged to the euro), and, in the most extreme form, the members of the euro itself, who gave up their national currencies entirely and accept the ECB's single rate. The hard-peggers import their anchor's monetary policy completely, which is fine when their cycle matches the anchor's and painful when it doesn't.

**Corner C — capital controls.** Keep the fixed (or managed) rate and your own policy; wall off free capital movement. This is where China, Vietnam, India, and much of emerging Asia sit. By restricting how money crosses the border, through quotas, approvals, taxes, and a non-convertible currency, these countries break the arbitrage chain at its root. With the border partly closed, a trader *can't* freely borrow dollars, convert, and bank the local rate, so the central bank can hold a managed exchange rate *and* set its own interest rate. The price is a less open, less liquid financial system and the administrative cost of policing the border. We'll spend the next section on this corner, because it's the most misunderstood and the most relevant to Vietnam.

Notice what this map does: it converts a baffling diversity of currency arrangements, why is the yuan managed but the yen free, why does Hong Kong import the Fed but Japan doesn't, into a single three-way classification. Every regime is one of three answers to one question: *which goal are you willing to lose?* The chart below puts numbers on how the world's countries distribute themselves across that spectrum.

![Number of countries by exchange-rate regime from hard peg to free float](/imgs/blogs/the-impossible-trinity-pick-two-of-three-2.png)

Read the spectrum left to right as a journey across the trilemma. The 24 hard-peggers and 40 conventional peggers have chosen the fixed rate and (mostly) open capital, surrendering their own policy. The 31 free-floaters chose policy and openness, surrendering the fixed rate. And the big middle, 55 stabilized/crawling and 45 managed-float economies, are the ones using *partial* capital controls and *partial* exchange-rate management to buy a little of all three. That fat middle is the most important part of the picture, and it's where the trinity gets interesting, because the real world is not three clean corners but a continuum, and the cleverest countries live deliberately inside the triangle rather than on a vertex. The full taxonomy of these managed regimes is its own subject; see [pegs, bands, and crawling pegs — the spectrum of FX regimes](/blog/trading/forex/pegs-bands-and-crawling-pegs-the-spectrum-of-fx-regimes).

## China's middle path: bending the corner without breaking it

The textbook trinity is binary: pick a clean corner. But the textbook is a simplification. The real escape hatch, and the most economically significant one on the planet, is China's, which is to sit *inside* the triangle by partially closing the capital account. It's worth understanding precisely, because it's how the world's second-largest economy navigates the trilemma, and because Vietnam runs a smaller version of the same playbook.

![Before and after comparison of the textbook trinity corners versus China's managed middle path](/imgs/blogs/the-impossible-trinity-pick-two-of-three-6.png)

The before/after above shows the move. On the left, the textbook says you must stand on one corner. On the right, China stands in the interior. It runs a **managed exchange rate**: the People's Bank of China sets a daily reference rate (the "fix") for the yuan against the dollar and allows trading only within a band around it, neither fully fixed like Hong Kong nor fully floating like Japan. It keeps **mostly independent monetary policy**: the PBoC cuts and hikes Chinese rates for Chinese conditions, and through 2024 it ran easier policy than the Fed without the yuan collapsing. And it makes all this possible by **gating capital flows**: China's capital account is not open. Moving large sums of money in or out requires going through controlled channels, quotas for foreign investors buying onshore bonds and stocks, limits on residents moving money abroad, a currency that isn't freely convertible outside approved purposes.

That gate is the whole trick. Because the arbitrage chain requires *free* capital movement to operate, partly closing the border partly defangs the arbitrage. A trader can't simply borrow unlimited dollars, convert at the managed rate, and bank the Chinese deposit rate at scale, the controls choke the flow. So the PBoC gets to manage the exchange rate *and* set its own interest rate, the combination the trinity says is forbidden, by sacrificing the third leg only *partially* and only at the border.

But, and this is the honest part, the cheat is not free, and it is not perfect. China pays for it in three ways. First, the yuan is not a fully usable international currency; you cannot move it as freely as the dollar or euro, which caps its role in global finance. Second, controls leak. Money finds its way across the border through trade mis-invoicing, through Hong Kong, through informal channels, so the PBoC must constantly police a porous wall, and in stress episodes (2015–2016, when capital fled and reserves fell by roughly a trillion dollars) the controls strained badly. Third, defending the managed rate still costs reserves; China holds over \$3 trillion of foreign-exchange reserves precisely because managing the yuan requires a vast war chest. China hasn't repealed the trilemma. It has paid, in convertibility and in administrative effort, to occupy the interior of the triangle instead of a corner.

A neat illustration of how leaky and how managed this corner is comes from the fact that the yuan actually trades as *two prices*. There is the **onshore yuan (CNY)**, traded inside China's gated market under the PBoC's daily fix and band, and the **offshore yuan (CNH)**, traded freely in Hong Kong and beyond, outside the controls. They are the same currency, but because the border separates them, their prices can diverge: when capital wants to flee China and can't easily get out through the onshore market, the pressure escapes into the offshore CNH, which weakens against the controlled CNY. That gap, CNH trading cheaper than CNY, is a live readout of devaluation pressure leaking around the wall, and traders watch it like a pressure gauge. It exists *only because* China sits in the interior of the triangle: a clean floater has no such gap (the price is just the price), and a fully closed economy has no offshore market at all. The two-price yuan is the trilemma's interior corner made visible, the sound of capital trying to cross a wall that's only mostly sealed. Vietnam has the same phenomenon in miniature: the onshore dong versus the offshore non-deliverable-forward market, where the implied rate runs above the onshore band whenever devaluation pressure builds.

#### Worked example: why the carry can't be scaled into a gated currency

Suppose Chinese one-year deposits pay 2.00% and US dollars pay 5.00%, a 3-point gap *in the dollar's favor* this time. Normally a trader would short the yuan (borrow yuan, sell for dollars, bank the 5%) to pocket 3%. To do it at scale, our trader wants to move **\$500,000,000** out of yuan. But the capital account is gated: converting that much yuan to dollars and wiring it offshore runs into quotas and approvals, and the channels that exist, say a quota that permits only \$5,000,000 a year, let through just **1%** of the desired size. The trade that would be trivial in an open economy is throttled to a trickle, so the pressure on the managed rate stays small and the PBoC can hold it while keeping its 2.00% domestic rate. The takeaway: capital controls don't eliminate the rate gap, they cap the *volume* of money that can act on it, which is exactly enough to let a managed-rate country keep its own monetary policy.

## Open the border and the flows whipsaw you: the cost of Goal 2

It's tempting to read the trilemma as "controls are the smart cheat, so why doesn't everyone do it?" The answer is that free capital mobility, Goal 2, is genuinely valuable, and giving it up has a real cost. But the flip side is also true: *keeping* the border open exposes you to the wild swings of global capital, which is its own kind of instability. The emerging economies that chose openness learned this the hard way.

![Emerging-market portfolio flows by year showing the swing from large inflows to sudden stops](/imgs/blogs/the-impossible-trinity-pick-two-of-three-8.png)

That chart is the cost of Goal 2 made visible. When an emerging economy opens its capital account and offers higher interest rates than the developed world, global "hot money" floods in to capture the carry, +\$250 billion in 2012, +\$290 billion in 2021. That feels great while it lasts: cheap funding, a strong currency, booming asset prices. But the same openness that lets money rush in lets it rush *out*, and when the Fed hikes or risk sentiment turns, the flows reverse violently, the −\$40 billion of the 2015 taper-tantrum aftermath, the −\$90 billion of the 2020 COVID shock, the −\$80 billion of 2022. These reversals are called **sudden stops**, and they are the open-economy version of a crisis: not a peg breaking, but the financing for an entire country evaporating in weeks.

Here's the trilemma reading. A country with an open border (Goal 2) and its own policy (Goal 3) has, by construction, given up the fixed rate (Goal 1), so when the flows reverse, the adjustment hits the *exchange rate*. The currency plunges, which is painful (imported inflation, dollar-debt blowups) but is at least an automatic shock absorber that prevents reserves from being drained to zero. A country that instead tried to peg (Goal 1) with an open border (Goal 2) and its own rate (Goal 3) gets the worst of it: the sudden stop becomes a full-blown speculative attack on the peg, exactly the 1997 Asian crisis, where Thailand, Indonesia, and Korea had pegged-ish currencies, open-ish borders, and their own rates, and the peg shattered when the hot money reversed (told in [the Asian financial crisis of 1997](/blog/trading/finance/asian-financial-crisis-1997)). The rupiah fell 83%. That is the trilemma collecting its debt.

So the choice between corners is really a choice about *which* instability you can live with. Float, and you accept a volatile exchange rate. Hard-peg, and you accept an interest rate dictated abroad and the risk of a speculative attack if your fundamentals slip. Control capital, and you accept a closed, less efficient financial system. There is no free option, only different bills.

### Is floating really enough? The modern "dilemma, not trilemma" debate

There's a sharper, more modern wrinkle worth knowing, because it complicates the clean story and it's where the cutting-edge research sits. The classic trilemma promises that if you *float*, you get full monetary independence, your rate is your own, end of story. The economist Hélène Rey argued in influential work that this promise is overstated in a world dominated by a **global financial cycle**, a single tide of risk appetite, largely driven by US monetary policy and global investor mood, that sweeps capital into and out of every open economy at once, *regardless* of whether they float or peg. Her provocative claim: for a country with an open capital account, the choice has collapsed from a *trilemma* (pick two of three) to a *dilemma* (pick one of two). Either you accept that the global financial cycle partly dictates your domestic financial conditions even though you float, or you regain genuine independence by managing the capital account, controls again. Floating, in this view, gives you *less* than full independence; only the capital-controls corner truly buys policy autonomy.

You don't have to fully accept Rey's stronger claim to take the useful lesson from it: floating is not a magic shield. When the Fed hikes and global risk appetite collapses, even floating emerging economies feel their financial conditions tighten, their currencies fall, their bond yields rise, because the open border lets the global cycle in. The clean three-corner triangle is the right *first* model, the one to learn first and reason with by default, but the real world is grayer, and the gradient of capital controls, from fully open to fully closed, is the dial that actually sets how much of the global cycle you import. That gradient is why the IMF's spectrum of regimes has a fat middle rather than three sharp spikes, and why so many countries quietly run *partial* controls they don't advertise.

## Common misconceptions

**"A strong central bank can defend any peg if it's determined enough."** No, and the SNB proved it in 2015. Determination is not the constraint; reserves and the willingness to import unlimited policy pain are. A central bank defending a peg against an inflow must print its own currency without limit (which it *can* do, but which floods its economy and inflates it), and against an outflow must sell finite reserves (which it *cannot* do without limit). The market's capital, trillions, dwarfs any single bank's reserves, tens or low hundreds of billions. Determination buys time, not victory. The SNB held the franc floor for 3.5 years and then surrendered in 90 seconds because the cost, ballooning its balance sheet to defend against a wall of money fleeing the euro, became unbearable. Reserves are a war chest, and war chests run out.

**"Capital controls are a backward, third-world thing that modern economies don't need."** This was the conventional wisdom from the 1990s, and it has aged poorly. China, the world's second-largest economy, runs extensive capital controls *by design* and has used them to keep its monetary independence through multiple global crises. Even the IMF, long the champion of open capital accounts, now concedes that controls can be a legitimate tool in a crisis. Controls are not a relic; they are one of the three corners, with real costs and real benefits, chosen by serious modern economies. The mistake is treating one corner as morally superior to the others.

**"Pegging gives you a stable, low-risk currency."** It gives you a stable *exchange rate*, which is not the same as a low-risk currency. A pegged currency is stable, stable, stable, and then it breaks 30% in a morning. Floating currencies are noisy day to day but rarely gap; pegged currencies are calm day to day and occasionally catastrophic. The risk doesn't disappear under a peg; it gets *stored up*, suppressed by the central bank's defense until the defense fails, then released all at once. The Thai baht traded at a rock-steady 25 per dollar for years, then fell to 56 in months. Stability and safety are different things, and a peg trades one for the other.

**"If a country has a trade surplus and growth, its peg is safe."** Fundamentals help, but the trilemma attack is partly a *self-fulfilling coordination problem*, not just a fundamentals problem. If enough traders believe the peg will break and all sell at once, the reserves drain and the peg breaks *regardless* of the underlying economy, because the central bank physically runs out of the foreign currency it needs to buy back its own. Good fundamentals raise the bar for an attack but don't make a country immune; they just mean the attack needs a bigger trigger. This is why the formal speculative-attack literature treats it as a game of beliefs, not just balance sheets.

**"The euro freed its members from the trilemma."** The opposite: the euro is the trilemma taken to its logical extreme. Euro members didn't escape the trinity; they planted both feet firmly on the hard-peg corner by abolishing their national currencies entirely. They keep a permanently fixed rate (one euro is one euro everywhere) and free capital, and they gave up national monetary policy *completely* to the ECB. Greece in 2010–2012 discovered exactly what that means: in a deep depression, it could not cut its own interest rate or devalue its own currency, because it no longer had either. That is the hard-peg corner's cost, paid in full.

## How it shows up in real markets

The trilemma is not a classroom curiosity; it is the load-bearing logic behind the biggest currency events of the last forty years. Run the framework over a few of them and watch it explain each one.

**Black Wednesday, 1992.** The UK had pinned the pound inside the European exchange-rate mechanism (a fixed rate, Goal 1), kept its border open (Goal 2), and wanted its own policy for a recessionary domestic economy (Goal 3). Germany was hiking rates to fight post-reunification inflation; the UK wanted to cut to fight its recession. The all-three position was untenable. Soros and others sold the pound; the Bank of England burned reserves and hiked its rate from 10% to 12% to 15% in a single day to defend the peg, an act of pure trilemma desperation, choosing to torch Goal 3 to save Goal 1. It failed anyway. By evening the UK abandoned the ERM, let the pound float (surrendered Goal 1), and *immediately* cut rates, getting Goal 3 back the moment it stopped fighting. The pound fell about 15% by year-end. The lesson: when you try to hold all three, the market forces the choice, and you lose the leg you fought hardest for.

**The Asian crisis, 1997.** Thailand, Indonesia, Korea, and others had run dollar-linked exchange rates, opened their borders to court foreign capital, and kept their own (often loose) monetary policies through a boom. For years the carry flooded in. When sentiment turned in mid-1997 and the hot money reversed, the pegs became attack targets. The Thai baht broke first (2 July 1997), then the dominoes fell: the rupiah collapsed 83%, the won and ringgit roughly halved. Every one of these was a country standing in the impossible middle, and the trilemma collected. The survivors learned the lesson and rebuilt with either floats (Korea moved toward floating) or much heavier reserves and managed regimes.

**The Swiss franc floor, 2011–2015.** Switzerland is a floater that *temporarily* tried to grab the fixed-rate corner. In 2011, with the euro crisis driving panicked money into the safe-haven franc, the SNB set a floor: it would not let the euro buy fewer than 1.20 francs, printing unlimited francs to enforce it. This is the inflow side of the trilemma: to hold the floor, the SNB had to expand its balance sheet enormously (its sight deposits ballooned from CHF 100 billion to over CHF 400 billion). It was importing the eurozone's monetary stance to hold the exchange rate, and as the ECB moved toward more easing in late 2014, holding the floor meant printing francs at a pace the SNB judged reckless. On 15 January 2015 it surrendered the fixed-rate corner and returned to the float. The franc spiked 30%. A floater can *visit* the peg corner, but the trilemma charges rent, and eventually the rent gets too high.

**The yen, 2022–2024.** Japan is a committed floater, and it shows the float corner working as designed, painfully but stably. As the Fed hiked to 5.5% while the Bank of Japan held near zero, the rate gap blew out, and because Japan floats, the *exchange rate* absorbed the entire stress: USD/JPY went from 115 to 157. Japan did intervene (selling dollars to slow the yen's fall, see [how central banks intervene in the currency market](/blog/trading/forex/how-central-banks-intervene-in-the-currency-market)), but it never tried to *fix* the rate, because it wanted to keep its own ultra-loose policy. It accepted a weak, volatile yen as the price of monetary independence. That is the float corner's bargain, honored. When the BoJ finally hiked in August 2024, the carry trade that had been built on the gap unwound violently and the yen snapped back, the float's volatility on full display.

**Vietnam, continuously.** Vietnam runs the capital-controls corner, a smaller cousin of China's managed middle. The State Bank of Vietnam sets a daily central reference rate for the dong against the dollar and allows trading within a ±5% band, and it keeps its own monetary policy (its own credit and rate decisions for the domestic economy). It can do both because Vietnam's capital account is not fully open, the dong is not freely convertible, and money cannot cross the border at will. The result is the managed crawl in the chart below: the dong depreciates against the dollar in a controlled, one-directional drift, not a free float and not a hard peg, but a deliberately gated middle.

![USD per VND year-end showing Vietnam's managed crawl and the 2024 trading band](/imgs/blogs/the-impossible-trinity-pick-two-of-three-7.png)

That managed crawl is the trilemma's interior corner in action. The SBV lets the dong slide gradually, around 25,450 per dollar at the end of 2024, with a central rate of 24,340 and a band running from a 23,123 floor to a 25,557 ceiling, rather than defending a hard line that speculators could attack, or floating freely and importing all the world's volatility. The closed capital account is what makes the managed crawl defensible: because a hedge fund can't freely borrow dong, sell it for dollars, and wire the proceeds out at scale, the SBV faces a trickle of pressure rather than the trillion-dollar wall that broke the Thai baht. Vietnam pays for this with a non-convertible currency and the administrative machinery of controlling its border, the price of sitting inside the triangle. The full mechanics of how the SBV runs the dong are in [USD/VND and the managed float](/blog/trading/forex/usd-vnd-and-the-managed-float-how-the-sbv-runs-the-dong); the formal flow-and-pressure dynamics are in [Vietnam's foreign-flow and room-limit games](/blog/trading/game-theory/case-study-vietnam-foreign-flows-room-limits-and-manipulation-games).

#### Worked example: pricing the crawl into a Vietnam carry trade

A foreign fund wants to capture Vietnam's higher rates. Suppose dong deposits pay 5.00% and dollars pay 4.50%, a 0.50-point gap *in the dong's favor*. The fund considers moving **\$10,000,000** into dong for a year. The naive carry looks like +0.50%, or **\$50,000**. But the dong is on a managed crawl that depreciated it roughly 4.6% over 2024 (from about 24,270 to 25,450 per dollar). If that pace continues, the fund's dong, converted back to dollars at year-end, is worth about **4.6% less**, wiping out the 0.50% carry and leaving a loss of roughly **−\$410,000** before fees. And the capital controls mean the fund may not even be able to size the trade freely or exit quickly. The takeaway: in a managed-crawl regime, the central bank's controlled depreciation is *itself* the price of admission, the SBV uses the crawl, not a defended peg, precisely so the carry never offers a free lunch worth attacking.

## The takeaway: read a currency by the corner it stands on

Here is what the trilemma gives you as a reader of currencies. When you encounter any exchange-rate arrangement, anywhere, your first question should be: *which corner is this?* The answer tells you almost everything.

If a country **floats** (US, euro area, Japan, UK), its currency is the shock absorber. Expect a volatile exchange rate that moves with the rate gap, and don't expect a "currency crisis" in the peg-breaking sense, because there's no peg to break. The thing to watch is the interest-rate differential, because the float lets the entire rate gap express itself in the exchange rate; this is the heart of [trading the dollar through DXY, carry, and the dollar smile](/blog/trading/macro-trading/trading-the-dollar-dxy-carry-dollar-smile).

If a country **hard-pegs** (Hong Kong, the Gulf, Denmark), its currency is calm, but its *interest rate* is imported, so watch the anchor's central bank, not the local one, and watch its reserves and fundamentals for any sign the peg might one day become a target. A pegged currency's risk lives in its tail: stable until it isn't.

If a country runs **capital controls** (China, Vietnam, India), it has bought the interior of the triangle, a managed rate plus its own policy, at the cost of a closed border. Watch the *gate*: how tight are the controls, how much pressure is leaking through the offshore market, how fast are reserves moving? The managed crawl is safe only as long as the wall holds.

This also tells you, as a trader, *what kind of bet you're even allowed to make* against each regime. Against a floater, there's no peg to attack, so the trade is a directional view on the rate gap and the flows, you're betting the currency moves, not that a level breaks. Against a hard peg, the asymmetric bet is the classic one: you risk a small carry cost to position for a rare, enormous break, a cheap option on a regime change, which is precisely the structure that paid Soros in 1992 and the funds who shorted the baht in 1997. And against a capital-controlled currency, the onshore market is largely closed to you, so the action moves to the *offshore* market, the CNH, the dong NDF, where the leaking pressure shows up as a gap to the onshore rate. Knowing the corner tells you which of these three games is on the table, and a trader who tries to play the wrong game, shorting a credible hard peg expecting it to drift like a float, or expecting an open floater to "defend" a level it never committed to, has misread the regime at the most basic level.

There's a final, humbling point. The trilemma is a constraint on *policymakers*, but it's a gift to *analysts*, because it dramatically narrows what a country *can* be doing. When you see a central bank simultaneously claiming a stable currency, an open economy, and a free hand on rates, you don't have to take its word for it, you know one of those claims is soft, and your job is to find which. Usually the soft one is the capital account: the country has controls it underplays, or a currency that isn't as convertible as advertised, or a "float" that's quietly managed. The framework lets you cross-examine official descriptions and find the real corner underneath the press release.

And the deepest lesson, the one that ties this back to the spine of the whole series: *you never own a currency in isolation; every position is a relative bet between two monies, driven by the gap between two countries' interest rates and the flow of money across borders.* The trilemma is just the constraint that gap and that flow impose on policymakers. A country that ignores the constraint, that reaches for the stable rate, the open border, *and* the free hand on policy, is not being ambitious. It is offering the rest of us a riskless trade, and the market will take it every time. Pick two of three. The third was never yours to keep.
