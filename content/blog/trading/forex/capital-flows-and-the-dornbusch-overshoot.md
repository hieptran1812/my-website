> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — In the short run a currency is moved by money chasing yield, not by goods crossing a border, and because goods prices are slow to adjust the exchange rate is forced to overreact.
>
> - Capital flows are *fast* (settle in seconds, repriced the same day) and *huge*; trade flows are *slow* (months to redirect a supply chain) and small by comparison. The fast flow sets the price.
> - Rudi Dornbusch's 1976 model joins two speeds: goods prices are **sticky** (frozen by menus and contracts) while the exchange rate is **flexible** (reprices every tick). One variable has to absorb the whole shock immediately, so it overshoots.
> - After a rate cut the currency does not glide to its new weaker level — it *leaps past* it on impact, then slowly converges back as prices catch up. The overreaction is not irrationality; it is forced by the speed mismatch.
> - The one number to remember: a single \$10B yield-chasing inflow that hits the market in a day can swamp a \$1B-a-month trade surplus by a factor of ten — and that is why FX overshoots.

On the morning of 16 December 2014, the Russian central bank did something that should have *strengthened* the ruble: at 1 a.m. Moscow time it hiked its key interest rate from 10.5% to 17%, a 6.5-point emergency jump meant to make holding rubles irresistible. Higher rates pull money in. Money pulling in lifts a currency. That is the textbook. By the close of trading that same day the ruble had fallen to a record low — over 100 to the dollar intraday, down from 60 a week earlier. The single largest rate hike in fifteen years was met with the worst single-day collapse in fifteen years. The textbook ran backwards.

What happened was a flow story, not a rate story. Oil — Russia's export — was in free fall. Money that had parked in ruble assets to earn that fat carry was not waiting to be persuaded by an even fatter coupon; it was sprinting for the exit, and a 17% rate looks like a rounding error next to a 20%-in-a-day capital flight. The exchange rate is the price at which that sprinting money converts back into dollars, and when everyone sprints at once, that price gaps. Trade barely twitched. No Russian factory rerouted a single shipment of gas that week. The currency moved 40% anyway, because the thing that moves a currency *in a hurry* is not trade — it is capital.

Hold the Russia episode as a stress test of every claim in this post. The rate hike was the largest in fifteen years, the kind of move that should have anchored a currency — and it did not even slow the fall, because the *stock* of capital fleeing dwarfed the *flow* incentive a higher coupon offered. The oil collapse was a trade-and-terms-of-trade shock, but it transmitted to the currency not through slowly rerouted exports but through *instantly* repriced capital, as foreigners and locals alike dumped ruble assets in a day. And the move massively overshot any sober estimate of the ruble's fundamental value — the currency more than doubled against the dollar before clawing much of it back over the following years, the signature shape of an overshoot. Russia 2014 is Dornbusch in a single violent week: fast capital, frozen real economy, a currency forced to absorb the whole shock and then some. Everything below is the slow-motion replay of that week.

This post is the capstone of "what moves a currency." We have spent the earlier posts establishing that an exchange rate rides the gap between two countries' interest rates, and that a country's balance of payments is the slow tide underneath. Here we put the two clocks together. We will show *why* capital flows dominate trade flows over any horizon a trader cares about, and then build — from the ground up, with an everyday-money analogy before any equation — the single most important model of short-run FX ever written: Rudi Dornbusch's 1976 overshooting model. By the end you will understand the deepest counterintuitive fact in currencies: a well-behaved, rational market, hit by a perfectly ordinary monetary shock, will *overreact* — and it has no choice.

![Stylised Dornbusch overshoot path showing the exchange rate leaping past its new long run level after a rate cut then gliding back](/imgs/blogs/capital-flows-and-the-dornbusch-overshoot-1.png)

Look at that path before we explain it, because the shape is the whole post. A rate cut lands at time zero. The currency does not slide gently to its new, weaker resting level (the dashed green line). It *jumps clean past it* — overshoots — and only then drifts back down to where it belongs over months and years. The gap between the spike and the dashed line is the overshoot, and the question that animates this entire article is: why would a rational market send the price somewhere it knows is not the destination?

## Foundations: Why capital flows dominate trade flows

Start with the two reasons a foreigner ever needs your currency. To buy your *goods* — your cars, your rice, your software licences — they must first buy your money. That is a **trade flow**. To buy your *assets* — your government bonds, your stocks, your bank deposits — they must also first buy your money. That is a **capital flow**. Every demand for a currency is one or the other: somebody wants what your country sells, or somebody wants to park money where your country pays.

The spine of this whole series is that you never own "a currency" in isolation — every position is a pair, a relative bet, and what moves it is the gap between two countries' rates plus the flow of money across borders. We have leaned hard on the rate-gap half. This post is about the *flow* half, and the single most important thing to internalise is that the two kinds of flow run on wildly different clocks and at wildly different sizes.

**A trade flow is a *flow* in the literal sense — a trickle per unit of time.** A country running a trade surplus of \$12B a year is earning, on average, \$1B a month, \$33M a day. That money arrives because containers were loaded, shipped, unloaded, invoiced, and paid — a physical process with weeks of lag baked in at every step. You cannot double a country's exports next Tuesday because the dollar got cheap; you have to build the factory, sign the contract, fill the order, and sail the ship. Trade responds to the exchange rate, but it responds on the timescale of *supply chains*, which is months to years.

**A capital flow is a *stock* that can move all at once.** The world's accumulated savings — pension funds, sovereign wealth funds, hedge funds, bank treasuries, retail money-market accounts — is a vast pool of *existing* money looking for the best risk-adjusted return at every instant. It does not need to be manufactured. It is already sitting in an account, one click from being somewhere else. When a yield gap opens, that stock does not trickle; it can pivot in a single afternoon. The global FX market turns over roughly \$7.5 trillion *per day*, and the overwhelming majority of that is not paying for goods — it is capital and the hedging of capital.

![Two flows two clocks comparing the fast capital flow path against the slow trade flow path](/imgs/blogs/capital-flows-and-the-dornbusch-overshoot-2.png)

The figure makes the speed mismatch concrete. On the capital side, a yield gap appears, a portfolio manager clicks, the trade settles in seconds, and the currency is repriced the same day. On the trade side, the very same exchange-rate move makes exports cheaper — but then someone has to renegotiate contracts, ship over months, and only after *years* does the trade balance meaningfully shift. Same shock, two clocks. The fast clock wins every short-run contest because it has already finished moving before the slow clock has noticed there was a shock.

This is the difference between a **stock** and a **flow**, and it is worth pausing on because it is the hinge of the entire post. A flow is measured *per unit of time* — \$33M of trade surplus per day. A stock is a *level* — the \$13 trillion of dollar credit owed by borrowers outside the United States, or the trillions in global bond portfolios. A flow can only ever add a thin layer per day. A stock can relocate in bulk. When a stock the size of the world's mobile savings decides to move, it overwhelms a flow the size of one country's daily trade balance the way an ocean overwhelms a garden hose.

You can see the dominance of capital right in the structure of the market itself. Of the roughly \$7.5 trillion that changes hands in FX every day, only about \$2.1 trillion is *spot* — the immediate exchange of one currency for another that you would associate with paying for goods or making a portfolio purchase. The single largest chunk, around \$3.8 trillion a day, is **FX swaps** — short-dated contracts whose overwhelming purpose is *funding and hedging*: borrowing dollars against another currency, rolling a hedge on a foreign-bond position, managing the currency exposure of a capital portfolio. Add outright forwards and currency swaps and you have a market whose plumbing is built around financing and hedging *capital*, not settling *trade*. The market's own anatomy tells you what moves it: the machinery exists to move money across borders for yield and to hedge the resulting exposures, and trade settlement is a minority use of the pipes. If trade drove FX, spot would dominate and the swap market would be a sideshow. It is the other way around.

There is a second structural tell. The US dollar is on roughly *88% of all FX trades* — it is the currency on the other side of almost everything, even trades between two non-dollar currencies, which are routinely routed *through* the dollar. That is not a trade fact; trade between, say, Korea and Thailand has no inherent reason to touch the dollar. It is a *capital* fact: the dollar is the world's funding and reserve currency, the unit in which global capital is borrowed, invested, and hedged. The market is wired around the dollar because global *capital* is wired around the dollar, a system we unpack in [the dollar system: why USD rules markets](/blog/trading/macro-trading/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy). The point for us is narrow: the very shape of the FX market — swap-dominated, dollar-centred — is the fingerprint of capital, not trade.

#### Worked example: the \$10B inflow versus the \$1B-a-month surplus

Take a mid-sized open economy running a healthy goods-trade surplus of \$12B a year. That is \$1B a month of net foreign-currency demand from trade — a steady, structural pull on the currency, and a real one. Now suppose its central bank surprises the market by holding rates high while everyone else is cutting, and a single large global bond fund decides to allocate \$10B into its government bonds over the next week.

Compare the magnitudes. The trade surplus delivers \$1B of currency demand per *month*. The capital inflow delivers \$10B of currency demand in one *week* — and in practice most of it lands in a day or two as the fund executes. That is roughly **\$5B a day of buying versus \$33M a day from trade**, a ratio of about 150 to 1. The trade surplus is real and it matters over years, but on the week the fund moves, it is invisible. The exchange rate is set at the margin by whoever is transacting *now*, and right now that is the fund. The currency rips higher on the capital flow while the trade surplus chugs along underneath, unchanged and unnoticed. The takeaway: over any horizon shorter than a business cycle, the price is made by the fast, large flow, not the slow, small one.

### Hot money has a name for a reason

The fast capital that chases yield gets called **hot money**, and the nickname is earned. It is hot because it is impatient: it arrives the instant the return looks good and leaves the instant it looks bad or merely *less good than somewhere else*. It does not care about your country's long-run productivity or your craftsmanship or your demographics. It cares about the spread between what it earns here and what it could earn elsewhere, adjusted for how scared it is. The moment that spread compresses, or the moment fear rises, the same money that flooded in floods out — and because it is a *stock*, it can all leave together.

This is the asymmetry that makes capital flows so violent. Trade is mean-reverting and gradual: a weaker currency slowly improves the trade balance, which slowly supports the currency — a stabilising negative feedback that takes years to operate. Hot money is the opposite: it is *positive* feedback. Inflows lift the currency, which raises the dollar return on the position, which makes the trade look even better, which pulls in *more* money — until the day it reverses, and the same loop runs in reverse, with everyone exiting through a door sized for normal days.

There is a subtle but crucial consequence of these two feedback signs living in the same currency. The stabilising trade channel and the destabilising capital channel are *always both present* — but they operate on different clocks, so which one you see depends entirely on the horizon you look at. Zoom in to a day, a week, a month, and you see only the capital channel: violent, trend-following, prone to overshoot. Zoom out to a decade and the trade channel finally asserts itself, dragging the wildly overshooting currency back toward something like purchasing-power fair value. The same currency is therefore *unstable* in the short run and *stable* in the long run, and the two are not a contradiction — they are two different feedback loops with two different time constants, fighting a tug-of-war the fast loop wins every day and the slow loop wins every decade. Most of what looks like chaos in FX is just the fast loop being visible while the slow loop is too gradual to see.

![Hot money in and out bar chart of net EM portfolio flows showing floods followed by sudden stops](/imgs/blogs/capital-flows-and-the-dornbusch-overshoot-3.png)

The bars show exactly this in emerging-market portfolio flows. Huge green inflows in the easy-money years — \$250B in 2012, \$290B in 2021 — punctuated by violent red reversals: −\$40B in 2015, −\$90B in 2020, −\$80B in 2022. This is not a gentle tide. It is a flood followed by a sudden stop, and each reversal is a currency crisis somewhere. We treat the mechanics of the carry-into-EM trade and the sudden stop in [the carry into emerging markets and the sudden stop](/blog/trading/forex/the-carry-into-emerging-markets-and-the-sudden-stop); here the point is narrower and sharper — the *speed* and *size* of that money is why a currency can move 40% in a week when trade has not budged.

So we have the first half of the answer to "why does FX overreact?" It overreacts because the thing moving it — capital — is fast, large, and prone to positive-feedback stampedes, while the stabilising force — trade — is slow, small, and gradual. But that alone does not explain *overshooting*. A fast market could still move straight to the right answer quickly. To get a genuine overshoot — a move *past* the destination — we need a second ingredient: a part of the economy that *cannot* move fast. That is where Dornbusch comes in.

## The setup: one fast price and one slow price

Here is the mental model that won Dornbusch his place in every macro textbook, and it is simpler than its reputation. An economy has two kinds of prices. There are **goods prices** — the price of a haircut, a sandwich, an hour of labour, a tonne of steel — and there is the **exchange rate** — the price of the currency itself. Dornbusch's insight was to take seriously that these two prices move at completely different speeds.

**Goods prices are sticky.** They do not change minute to minute. A restaurant reprints its menu once or twice a year. Wages are set by annual contracts. A factory has supply agreements locked in for quarters. Catalogues, price lists, rate cards — the whole apparatus of commerce is built on prices that are *announced and then held* for a while. Economists call this price stickiness, and it is not a quirk; it is how the real economy actually works. After a shock, the general price level in an economy adjusts over *months and years*, not seconds.

**The exchange rate is flexible.** It is a financial price, set in a continuous auction by traders who can transact billions in milliseconds. It reprices every tick of every second. There is no menu to reprint, no contract to renegotiate, no ship to load. If new information arrives at 9:31 a.m., the exchange rate reflects it at 9:31 a.m.

![Sticky goods prices versus flexible exchange rate before after comparison of how each adjusts after a shock](/imgs/blogs/capital-flows-and-the-dornbusch-overshoot-5.png)

The contrast in the figure is the entire engine of the model. On the left, goods prices: menus reprinted yearly, wages set by contract, adjusting over months, effectively *frozen* in the moments after a shock. On the right, the exchange rate: quotes changing every tick, set by live trading, adjusting in seconds — and therefore forced to absorb the whole shock by itself. Hold that asymmetry in your head, because everything that follows is just its logical consequence.

Now ask the question that makes the model click. Suppose a monetary shock hits — say the central bank unexpectedly cuts rates and prints more money. In the *long run*, more money chasing the same goods means a higher price level (inflation) and a proportionally weaker currency; this is the neutral, textbook outcome, and it is the dashed green line in our cover figure. But here is the rub: in the *short run*, the price level **cannot** rise, because prices are sticky. The menus have not been reprinted. So the adjustment that is *supposed* to happen through prices over years has to come from somewhere immediately — and the only price free to move is the exchange rate. The exchange rate ends up doing *more* than its share of the work, precisely because its slow partner is sitting frozen.

#### Worked example: which price moves first

Suppose a central bank permanently increases the money supply by 10%. The long-run, fully-adjusted outcome is textbook: the domestic price level rises 10% and the currency weakens 10% — \$1 that used to buy 100 units of foreign currency now buys 110 (a 10% weaker home currency, more foreign per unit). Both prices have moved 10%, and *relative* prices — real wages, real competitiveness — are exactly where they started. Money is neutral in the long run.

Now run the clock forward from the instant of the shock. On day one, the price level has moved **0%** — menus are unchanged, wages are unchanged, everything in the real economy is still posted at the old prices. But the shock is real and the exchange rate is free. If the long-run answer is "currency 10% weaker and prices 10% higher," and prices are stuck at 0%, then the exchange rate cannot simply go to its long-run 10%-weaker level, because — as we are about to see — the bond market won't let it sit there. It has to go *further than 10%* on impact and then ease back as prices catch up. The instant-one move in the flexible price is *larger* than the eventual long-run move. The takeaway: when one price is frozen, the free price overshoots to compensate.

That is the heart of it. But we have asserted the overshoot, not derived it. *Why* must the exchange rate go past 10% rather than just to 10%? The answer is in the bond market, and specifically in a parity condition we have met before.

## Why the overshoot is forced: uncovered interest parity

The mechanism that *forces* the overshoot is the no-arbitrage condition linking interest rates and expected currency moves — **uncovered interest parity (UIP)**. We treat the master rate-gap relationship in depth in [interest rate differentials, the master variable of FX](/blog/trading/forex/interest-rate-differentials-the-master-variable-of-fx); here we need just its one load-bearing idea, so let me state it plainly and then use it.

UIP says: a global investor must be indifferent between holding two currencies. If a US dollar deposit pays 5% and a foreign deposit pays 2%, why would anyone hold the foreign currency at all? Only if they expect the foreign currency to *appreciate* by about 3% over the year, making up the yield shortfall. In equilibrium, **the interest-rate gap equals the expected currency move.** The higher-yielding currency is expected to *depreciate* by roughly the yield advantage; the lower-yielding currency is expected to *appreciate* by roughly its yield disadvantage. Money is indifferent because the carry and the expected spot move cancel.

Now watch what this does when the central bank cuts rates. The cut makes the domestic currency *lower-yielding*. For investors to be willing to hold it at all, UIP now demands that the currency be expected to **appreciate** going forward. But — and here is the trick — we already know where the currency *ends up* in the long run: at its new, *weaker* equilibrium level (the dashed line), because money is neutral and the money supply is permanently larger. So the currency must: (a) be expected to *appreciate* from here, yet (b) end up *weaker* than where it started. The only way both can be true at once is if the currency first drops *below* its long-run level — overshoots to the weak side — so that the subsequent glide back *up* toward the long-run level provides exactly the appreciation UIP requires.

Read that twice, because it is the keystone. The currency overshoots *so that it has room to appreciate back*. The expected appreciation that UIP demands is *manufactured* by jumping too far first. If the currency only went to its long-run weaker level and sat there, there would be no expected appreciation, the carry shortfall would be unrewarded, and no one would hold the currency — money would keep fleeing and the currency would fall further still. The overshoot is the *equilibrium*: it is exactly the point from which the expected glide-back compensates holders for the now-lower yield.

#### Worked example: sizing the overshoot after a rate cut

Let me put numbers on it. Suppose the home currency starts at 100 (our index, where higher means weaker — more foreign currency per home unit). The central bank cuts rates, and the cut opens up a yield disadvantage versus the rest of the world of, say, **2 percentage points** per year for the duration the gap is expected to persist. The new long-run equilibrium — once prices fully adjust — is a 10%-weaker currency, an index level of **110**.

UIP requires that, sitting at the overshoot point, the currency be expected to appreciate at about 2% per year to compensate for the 2-point yield shortfall. Now we need to know how fast it will glide back, which depends on how fast sticky prices adjust — say prices close a quarter of the remaining gap each year. Working through the model's algebra (which we will not grind out here), the on-impact jump lands the index not at 110 but at roughly **118** — an *18%* depreciation on impact for a shock whose long-run effect is only *10%*. The extra 8 points is the overshoot. From 118 the currency then appreciates back toward 110 over the following years — and that glide *is* the appreciation UIP demanded. The takeaway: the slower prices adjust and the larger the yield shift, the bigger the overshoot — the move on the day can be nearly *double* the eventual move.

### Walking through the model's two moving parts

It helps to see the model as two equations pulling on each other, stated in words. The *first* relationship is the money market. When the central bank cuts rates and adds money, the domestic interest rate falls. A lower domestic rate is the *cause*; everything downstream follows from it. The *second* relationship is UIP, which says the domestic rate equals the foreign rate plus the expected rate of depreciation. With the domestic rate now lower than the foreign rate, the only way UIP can hold is for the market to expect the currency to *appreciate* from wherever it sits today. Those two relationships are the whole engine, and the figure below traces what they jointly produce.

Now layer in the long-run anchor. The third fact is that, eventually, the currency must settle at its new, weaker equilibrium — the dashed line — because the money supply is permanently larger and money is neutral in the long run. Combine the second and third facts and the contradiction resolves itself only one way. The currency must be *expected to appreciate* (fact two) yet *end up weaker than it started* (fact three). The sole path that satisfies both is to drop *below* the long-run level on impact — overshoot — and appreciate back up to it. The depth of the overshoot is whatever it takes to make the *speed* of the glide-back equal to the appreciation UIP demands, given how fast prices adjust.

That last clause is the dial that sets the overshoot's size, and it is worth making vivid. If goods prices adjusted *instantly*, the price level would jump to its new level immediately, the exchange rate would jump straight to the dashed line, and there would be *no* overshoot — the glide-back would be zero because there is nowhere to glide from. The overshoot exists *only* in proportion to how *slow* prices are. The stickier the prices, the longer the glide, the deeper the overshoot the market needs on impact to generate enough total appreciation along the way. Stickiness is not a footnote to the model; stickiness *is* the model. Kill the stickiness and you kill the overshoot.

And notice what this says about the *direction* of causation, because it is the opposite of the naive story. The naive story is "rate cut weakens currency a bit." The Dornbusch story is "rate cut weakens currency a *lot* on impact, *because* it must then strengthen back to satisfy parity." The strengthening-to-come is what forces the excess weakening-now. The future appreciation is not a consolation after the overshoot; it is the *reason* for the overshoot. That inversion — the destination dictating the size of the overreaction on the way there — is the single most counterintuitive and most important idea in short-run exchange-rate economics.

![The overshoot path timeline from before the shock through the overshoot peak to the glide back to the new long run level](/imgs/blogs/capital-flows-and-the-dornbusch-overshoot-4.png)

The timeline lays out the full sequence as a story. Before the shock the rate sits at its old level. At *t = 0* the rate cut hits and capital flees the now-lower-yielding currency. At *t = 0+* the exchange rate jumps clean *past* its new long-run level — the overshoot. That overshoot peak, reached in *minutes*, is the currency's weakest point. Then, over *months*, sticky prices finally start adjusting; and over *years* the currency glides back up to its new long-run resting level. The whole drama — the violent gap and the slow recovery — is the consequence of one fast price compensating for one slow price.

## Putting it together: why FX overreacts

We now have both halves, so let me assemble the complete answer to the question in the title. FX overreacts because of a *collaboration* between two facts.

**Fact one (capital is fast and large):** the money that sets the exchange rate at any instant is mobile financial capital — a giant stock that reprices the currency in seconds when the yield landscape shifts. There is no friction, no shipping lag, no contract to honour. The currency can move its full equilibrium distance immediately.

**Fact two (goods are slow):** the prices that *should* share the burden of adjustment — wages, menus, the general price level — are frozen by stickiness. They will move eventually, over months and years, but not today.

Put them together and the logic is inescapable. A monetary shock requires a certain total adjustment, split in the long run between prices and the exchange rate. In the short run, prices contribute *nothing* because they are stuck. So the exchange rate must contribute *everything* — and because UIP requires the currency to then be expected to appreciate (to compensate for the new lower yield), it must overshoot past its long-run level to create that expected appreciation. The currency does too much today precisely because its partner does nothing today. That is the overshoot, and it is not a market failure. It is what a perfectly rational, perfectly informed market *must* do when one of its prices is glued in place.

This is why we say the overshoot is *forced*, not chosen. No trader sits down and decides to overreact. Each one is simply pricing UIP correctly, given sticky goods prices. The aggregate of all that correct local pricing *is* the overshoot. It is one of the most beautiful results in macro precisely because irrational-looking behaviour at the level of the whole market — a 18% move for a 10% shock — falls straight out of rational behaviour at the level of each participant.

There is a deeper lesson here for how you read any currency. **The exchange rate is the shock absorber of the macro system.** It is the one fully flexible price in an economy stuffed with sticky ones, so whenever the system gets hit and the slow prices cannot respond, the burden lands on the currency — and it lands *with interest*, in the form of an overshoot. When you see a currency move violently on a policy surprise, your first instinct should not be "the market is panicking." It should be "the market is doing the adjustment that wages and prices are too slow to do, and it is doing extra to satisfy parity." We develop the policy-transmission side of this in [how monetary policy moves currencies](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-monetary-policy-moves-currencies-rate-differentials); the overshoot is what that transmission looks like in real time.

#### Worked example: the trader's overshoot scorecard

Suppose you trade a G10 pair and the central bank of the base currency surprises with a 50bp cut nobody expected, and signals more to come — a roughly 1-point downward shift in the expected rate path. Your back-of-envelope reasoning runs like this. Long-run effect on the currency: maybe 4-5% weaker, given the persistence of the gap. But that is the *destination*, not the *day-one move*. Because prices are sticky and UIP must hold, the on-impact move should *overshoot* that — call it 7-8% weaker on the day, before a multi-month glide back toward the 4-5% level.

So if the pair is at, say, 1.2000 (foreign per base), you would expect the base currency to gap to roughly 1.1100-1.1150 on impact — an overshoot — rather than easing gently to its 1.1450-ish long-run level. A trader who *only* priced the long-run move would be short too little and would be steamrolled on the day; a trader who faded the spike too early (betting on instant mean-reversion) would be run over by the overshoot before the slow glide-back ever started. The takeaway: the overshoot tells you the *day-one* move is bigger than the *fundamental* move, and the mean-reversion is real but *slow* — measured in months, not minutes.

## Where the clean model meets messy reality

Dornbusch's model is a triumph of clarity, and like every clean model it is a deliberate simplification. Knowing exactly *where* it bends keeps you from over-trusting it — and, more usefully, the places it bends are themselves tradeable insights.

The first wrinkle is that UIP, the parity condition doing all the work, *fails empirically over short horizons* — and fails in a direction that is itself a famous puzzle. UIP predicts that high-yield currencies should *depreciate* (to compensate holders of low-yield currencies). In the data, over months to a couple of years, high-yield currencies have historically tended to *appreciate* or at least hold up — which is the entire reason the carry trade makes money in the first place. So the appreciation that Dornbusch's glide-back relies on does not always show up on schedule; sometimes the overshot currency keeps overshooting, because fresh capital keeps chasing the carry. This is not a refutation of the model so much as a warning about its *timing*: the forces are real, but the mean-reversion can be deferred for a long time by the same hot money that caused the overshoot. The currency that "should" glide back can instead grind *further* from fair value while the carry holds — until it snaps. The model gives you the gravity; it does not give you the date.

The second wrinkle is that not every shock is the clean, permanent, fully-anticipated monetary shock the textbook assumes. Real shocks are noisy, partial, and constantly revised. A rate cut that the market reads as "one and done" produces a small overshoot; the *same* cut read as "the first of many" produces a large one, because the expected rate *path* — not the single move — is what capital prices. This is why forward guidance and central-bank communication move currencies as much as the actual rate decisions do: they reshape the expected path, and the overshoot scales with the path, not the print. A trader watching a central bank is really trading the second derivative — how the *expectation of future rates* is shifting — which is exactly the variable Dornbusch's "permanent money-supply change" stands in for.

The third wrinkle is that the real economy has *more* than two speeds. Dornbusch simplified the world to one frozen price (goods) and one free price (FX). Reality has a spectrum: financial prices that move in milliseconds, commodity prices that move in days, wages that move in years, and administered or regulated prices that barely move at all. The overshoot is largest in the gap between the *fastest* price (FX) and the *slowest* sticky prices (wages, long-term contracts). In economies where more prices are flexible — heavily indexed economies, or ones with frequent repricing — the overshoot is smaller, because the FX rate has more company in absorbing the shock. The two-speed cartoon is the right *first* model; the refinement is that overshoot magnitude tracks the *width* of the speed gap in a given economy.

None of these wrinkles overturns the core result. They sharpen it. The overshoot is real, its *size* scales with price stickiness and the persistence of the rate-path shift, and its *timing* is muddied by the same carry-chasing capital that drives it. A trader who holds the clean model in one hand and these three wrinkles in the other reads currencies better than one holding either alone.

## Common misconceptions

**"A big FX move on a policy surprise means the market overreacted irrationally."** This is the single most common misread, and Dornbusch is the cure. The overshoot is the *rational* response to sticky prices plus UIP, not a panic. A 10% shock producing an 18% on-impact move is not the market losing its mind; it is the market correctly pricing the fact that prices are frozen and the currency must over-adjust to create the expected appreciation parity demands. The move *looks* excessive only if you forget that the slow prices are sitting on their hands.

**"Trade flows drive exchange rates — a country with a big surplus has a strong currency."** Over decades, the current account and competitiveness do anchor a currency, and we cover that tide in [the balance of payments and the current account](/blog/trading/forex/the-balance-of-payments-and-the-current-account). But over any horizon a trader cares about — days, weeks, months — trade is a rounding error against capital. Japan ran enormous trade and current-account surpluses through 2022 while the yen *collapsed* 20%, because capital was fleeing the zero-yield yen for the high-yield dollar. The surplus was real; it was simply irrelevant to the daily price, which capital set. Big surplus, weak currency — and no contradiction, once you sort flows by speed.

**"If capital can move instantly, the currency should jump straight to the right level — there's no reason to overshoot."** This misses the role of *sticky prices*. If goods prices were *also* flexible, the currency would indeed jump straight to its new long-run level and stop — no overshoot. The overshoot exists *only because* one price (goods) is frozen while the other (FX) is free. It is the interaction of fast and slow that produces the overreaction; a world of all-fast prices, or all-slow prices, has no overshoot. The mismatch is the mechanism.

**"The overshoot means you can always fade the spike for a quick mean-reversion profit."** Careful. The model says the currency *will* glide back from the overshoot — but over *months and years*, as sticky prices grind into line, not over the next hour. Fade the spike too early and you are short the appreciation that UIP guarantees is coming, but you are also fighting a position that can stay overshot for a long time if the shock persists or fresh shocks pile on. The mean-reversion is a slow tide, not a day trade. Many a carry-trade blowup is a trader who confused "it will revert eventually" with "it will revert before my margin call."

**"Overshooting is an old 1970s idea that doesn't apply to modern, deep, liquid markets."** If anything, modern markets make it *worse*. Deeper, faster, more leveraged capital markets mean the flexible price (FX) adjusts even more instantly relative to the sticky one (goods), so the speed mismatch — the very thing that produces the overshoot — is *larger* now than in 1976. The 2022 yen and the 2014-15 dollar, which we turn to next, are textbook overshoots in markets vastly deeper than the ones Dornbusch was modelling.

## How it shows up in real markets

The model is forty-some years old, but you can watch it operate in the tape of the last decade. Two episodes are about as clean as macro ever gets.

### The 2014-15 dollar surge

Through 2014 and into 2015, the broad dollar staged one of its great runs. The mechanism was pure rate-gap-plus-flow. The US Federal Reserve was *exiting* its quantitative-easing programme and telegraphing rate hikes; the European Central Bank and the Bank of Japan were doing the opposite, ramping up easing and pushing rates toward and below zero. The expected path of US rates rose relative to the rest of the developed world — and capital did what capital does. It rotated *into* dollar assets to capture the widening yield advantage, and the dollar ripped.

![The 2014-15 dollar surge shown on the DXY year close line chart with the surge window highlighted](/imgs/blogs/capital-flows-and-the-dornbusch-overshoot-6.png)

The DXY dollar index ran from roughly 80 at end-2013 to 90 at end-2014 to nearly 99 at end-2015 — a 20%-plus move in the world's reserve currency in about two years, driven not by a flood of American exports (the US trade deficit was, as ever, large and persistent) but by the *expectation* of a higher US rate path pulling global capital in. Note the shape: a sharp climb as the policy divergence got priced, an overshoot to the upside as the dollar ran ahead of the eventual fundamentals, and then years of choppy consolidation. The fast price (the dollar) moved its full distance and then some, while the slow prices (relative US versus European goods prices, competitiveness) barely budged on that timescale. This is overshooting at the level of the broad dollar, and it is why we treat the dollar as one tradable macro asset in [trading the dollar: DXY, carry, and the dollar smile](/blog/trading/macro-trading/trading-the-dollar-dxy-carry-dollar-smile).

#### Worked example: a \$10B rotation into US bonds in 2014

Take a single large global allocator deciding in mid-2014 to shift \$10B out of German Bunds and Japanese government bonds and into US Treasuries, to capture the widening yield gap as the Fed turned hawkish while the ECB and BoJ eased. To do it, the fund must *sell* euros and yen and *buy* dollars in the FX market — \$10B of dollar demand, concentrated over the days it takes to execute.

Now scale that across the entire global asset-management industry making the same trade at once. If even fifty such allocators each rotate \$10B, that is **\$500B of dollar buying** crammed into a few months — against a US goods-trade picture that did not improve at all (the deficit *widened*). The trade balance said "sell dollars"; the capital account screamed "buy dollars," and capital won by a distance. The dollar rose ~20% while US exporters got *less* competitive, the exact opposite of what a trade-driven model predicts. The takeaway: a rate-path divergence triggers a capital rotation whose size dwarfs trade, and the currency overshoots in the direction of the yield, not the direction of the trade balance.

### The 2022 yen: a textbook overshoot to the weak side

If 2014-15 was the dollar overshooting up, 2022 was the yen overshooting down — and it is the cleanest single illustration of every idea in this post. Through 2022 the Fed hiked aggressively to fight inflation while the Bank of Japan, almost alone among major central banks, *pinned* its policy rate near zero and capped its bond yields. The US-Japan rate gap, which had been near zero in 2020-21, blew out to over four percentage points. Capital did the only thing it could: it fled the zero-yield yen for the high-yield dollar, in size.

![USD JPY level against the US Japan two year rate gap showing the yen overshooting weaker as the gap blew out](/imgs/blogs/capital-flows-and-the-dornbusch-overshoot-7.png)

The dual-axis chart tells the whole story. The amber line is the US-Japan 2-year rate gap; the blue line is USD/JPY (higher means a weaker yen). They move together with almost embarrassing fidelity: as the gap explodes from near zero in 2021 to roughly 4.35 points in 2022, the yen craters from 115 to 131 by year-end and on toward 157 by 2024 — the weakest in over three decades. And crucially, this happened *while Japan ran a current-account surplus*. The trade tide said "yen should be supported"; the capital flood said "sell the yen for yield," and the yen lost a third of its value against the dollar. The flexible price absorbed the entire rate shock — and overshot, which is exactly why Japan's Ministry of Finance felt compelled to intervene, spending tens of billions of dollars selling its reserves to slow a move it judged to have run *past* fundamentals.

#### Worked example: the yen carry math that drove the flood

Here is why the money moved. In 2022 a global investor could borrow yen at roughly 0.1% and buy 2-year US Treasuries yielding around 4.4% — a gross carry of about **4.3 percentage points a year**, before any currency move. On a \$1,000,000-equivalent position funded in yen, that is **\$43,000 a year** in pure rate pickup for holding the position. And as the trade itself pushed the yen weaker, the *dollar value* of the yen borrowing shrank, handing the trader a *currency gain on top of the carry* — the positive-feedback loop that makes a flow self-reinforcing on the way in.

That is irresistible, so it was done at enormous scale — and the scale *is* the overshoot. Each marginal carry trader selling yen to fund a dollar asset pushed USD/JPY higher, which improved the realised return, which drew in the next trader. The currency went *past* where the rate gap alone would justify, because the flow fed on itself. When the Bank of Japan finally hiked in mid-2024 and the gap began to close, the same loop ran violently in reverse — the August 2024 unwind we anatomise in [carry trade unwinds: 1998, 2008, 2024](/blog/trading/macro-trading/carry-trade-unwinds-1998-2008-2024-when-leverage-breaks). The takeaway: a 4.3-point carry, levered across the global investor base, is a capital flood that overshoots the currency far past its rate-justified level — and overshoots just as hard on the way back.

### When the central bank fights the overshoot

The yen episode raises a question the model answers cleanly: *can* a central bank stop an overshoot? In late 2022, with USD/JPY near 152, Japan's Ministry of Finance and the Bank of Japan stepped into the market and *sold dollars to buy yen* — roughly \$63B of intervention across September and October alone, drawn down from the country's reserves. The logic is straightforward: if the currency has overshot past fundamentals, an official buyer leaning against the move can slow it and buy time for the rate gap to eventually narrow.

But notice the bind the model puts them in. The yen was weak *because* the rate gap was wide, and the BoJ was *choosing* to keep its rate near zero. Intervention sells dollars to support the yen, but it does nothing about the cause — the yield disadvantage that is pulling capital out. So intervention against an overshoot driven by a genuine, ongoing rate gap is, at best, a delaying action: it can lean against the overshoot's *excess*, the part of the move that has run ahead of fundamentals, but it cannot reverse the fundamental move while the rate gap persists. Indeed the yen kept weakening into 2024, all the way to 157, despite repeated rounds of intervention, until the BoJ finally *hiked* in mid-2024 and addressed the cause. The lesson sits exactly where Dornbusch would put it: you can intervene against the overshoot, but you cannot intervene against the rate gap. The currency is the shock absorber, and selling reserves does not unstick the slow prices or close the yield gap that is doing the absorbing. We treat the full mechanics and limits of FX intervention in [the central bank toolkit: rates, QE, QT, and forward guidance](/blog/trading/macro-trading/central-bank-toolkit-rates-qe-qt-forward-guidance).

#### Worked example: the cost of leaning against the wind

Suppose a central bank decides to defend its currency against a capital-driven overshoot by selling \$30B of its FX reserves over a month, buying its own currency in the market. If the overshoot is genuinely excess — the currency has run, say, 5% past fundamentals — and the gap narrows as expected, the bank may *make* money: it bought its own currency cheap and the currency recovers, so its remaining reserves regain value and the defence pays for itself. That is the good case, and it is rare.

The bad case is the one the model warns about. If the rate gap *persists* — the bank refuses to hike — then capital keeps leaving, the \$30B is simply absorbed by the outflow, and the currency weakens *anyway*. The bank has spent a third of a \$90B reserve stack to buy a few weeks. Do that three or four times and the reserves are gone, the market knows it, and the currency gaps on the realisation that the defence is spent — the classic speculative-attack endgame. The takeaway: intervention can shave the *overshoot* (the excess) but cannot defeat the *fundamental* move while the rate gap that causes it is left in place; reserves are a buffer against overshooting, not a substitute for fixing the cause.

### The hot-money loop, drawn out

Both episodes — the dollar up, the yen down — share one structure, and it is worth seeing it as a single mechanism because it explains why these moves go *too far* rather than *just far enough*.

![The hot money loop graph from a yield gap through inflows and a rising currency to a crowded position and a sudden exit](/imgs/blogs/capital-flows-and-the-dornbusch-overshoot-8.png)

A yield gap opens. Capital flows in to capture it. The inflow lifts the currency. The stronger currency *raises* the total return on the position (carry plus FX gain), which makes the trade look even better, which pulls *more* money in. The position grows crowded. Then a shock or a rate cut lands, and because everyone is on the same side, *everyone exits at once* — through a door built for a normal day. The loop that amplified the move on the way in amplifies it on the way out. This is why capital-driven currency moves overshoot in *both* directions: the positive feedback overshoots the entry, and the stampede overshoots the exit. It is also why these trades are so dangerous — the very crowding that makes them feel safe (everyone agrees!) is what makes the unwind catastrophic. We formalise the run-for-the-exit dynamic as a coordination game in [bank runs as coordination games](/blog/trading/game-theory/bank-runs-as-coordination-games-diamond-dybvig-and-svb); a crowded carry trade is a bank run waiting for a trigger.

## The takeaway: how to read a currency through this lens

So what does all this change about how you read a currency? Three things, and they compound.

**First: sort every flow by speed before you weigh it.** When news hits a currency, ask immediately — is this a *capital* story or a *trade* story? Capital stories (a central-bank surprise, a yield-gap shift, a risk-off stampede, a sovereign-rating change) move the price *today*, in size, and can overshoot. Trade stories (an export boom, a terms-of-trade shift, a current-account trend) matter over *years* and are nearly invisible day to day. The most common analytical error in FX is weighting a slow trade fact against a fast capital fact as if they operated on the same clock. They do not. On any trading horizon, capital sets the price, full stop.

**Second: expect the overshoot, and respect its timescale.** When a monetary shock lands, the day-one move will be *larger* than the fundamental, long-run move, because the flexible price is compensating for the frozen one and UIP is forcing an expected glide-back. Do not anchor on the long-run level and conclude "the market has overshot, I'll fade it" — the overshoot is *equilibrium*, and the mean-reversion that follows is a multi-month tide, not an intraday snap. The trader who understands Dornbusch is short *more* than the fundamental on the day, and patient about the glide-back; the trader who does not is either under-positioned or run over fading too early.

**Third: read the exchange rate as the macro system's shock absorber.** It is the one fully flexible price in an economy full of sticky ones, so it bears — with interest — every shock the slow prices cannot. That is a feature, not a bug: a flexible currency is *why* a country with a sticky domestic economy can absorb a global shock without mass unemployment, because the currency takes the hit instead of wages and jobs. When you see a currency lurch on a policy surprise, you are watching the macro system route a shock through its one fast valve. The lurch is the system working, not failing.

This last point also explains why countries that *deny* their currency the right to absorb shocks — by pegging it — end up taking the shock somewhere far more painful instead. A peg freezes the one price that was supposed to be flexible, so when a monetary or capital shock hits, the adjustment that the overshoot would have handled in the currency market has nowhere to go but into the *real* economy: into reserves bleeding out as the central bank defends the peg, into interest rates spiked to punishing levels to hold capital, and ultimately into recession and unemployment when those defences fail. The overshoot is the *cheap* way to absorb a shock; the alternative, paid by a country that refuses to let its currency move, is the *expensive* one — and the history of broken pegs, from the ERM in 1992 to Asia in 1997 to the ruble's managed-float surrenders, is the history of countries discovering that a shock you do not let the exchange rate absorb does not disappear. It just comes due in a worse currency. A floating currency that overshoots is, paradoxically, the *stable* arrangement; the rigid one that cannot overshoot is the fragile one.

Tie it back to the spine of this whole series: an exchange rate is the relative price of two monies, and what moves it is the gap between two countries' rates plus the flow of money across borders. This post is the proof that the *flow* half dominates the short run, and that the flow does not move the price gently to fair value — it overshoots, because the one price free to move must do the work of every price that is stuck. Capital chases yield faster than trade ever could, sticky prices freeze the slow side of the adjustment, and the exchange rate — the only thing that *can* move — moves too much. That overreaction is not the market's flaw. It is the market's most rational, and most beautiful, response to a world where some prices are fast and some are slow.
