> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Foreign exchange is the largest, deepest, most continuous market on the planet, and it has no exchange at all: it is a private network of bank dealers quoting prices to each other and to their clients, around the clock, five days a week.
>
> - Average turnover is about **\$7.5 trillion a day** (BIS, 2022) — more in a single day than the entire annual output of most countries.
> - There is no central marketplace. FX is **over-the-counter (OTC)**: a web of bilateral dealer quotes, so two banks can show you slightly different prices at the same instant.
> - The market is a **tier system** — a small core of top dealers makes the prices, and everyone else reaches that core through banks, prime brokers, and electronic venues.
> - Most "FX" is **not** spot. The single biggest instrument is the **FX swap** (\$3.81T/day), a short-term funding tool — spot is only about **\$2.1T/day**, under a third of the total.

On the morning of 15 January 2015, a currency that was supposed to be one of the most boring, predictable instruments in the world moved more in three minutes than it normally moves in three years. The Swiss National Bank had spent over three years holding the euro at a floor of 1.20 Swiss francs. At 9:30 a.m. Zurich time it abruptly let go. EUR/CHF — the number of francs you get for one euro — fell from 1.20 to below 0.86 in the time it takes to read this paragraph. Brokers blew up. Retail traders woke to find their accounts not just empty but in debt. And almost nobody, anywhere, could get a reliable price for several minutes, because the people who normally *make* the prices had all simultaneously pulled their quotes.

That is a useful way to start, because it tells you something most introductions skip. The foreign exchange market is enormous — \$7.5 trillion changes hands on an average day — and yet on the morning it mattered most, it briefly had no price. How can the deepest market in the world simply stop quoting? The answer is hidden in the *structure*. FX is not an exchange where buyers and sellers meet a machine that matches them. It is a private network of dealers who choose, voluntarily and bilaterally, to show you a price — and when they all step back at once, the "market" reveals that it was never a single place to begin with.

This post is a tour of that structure. We are going to walk up the ladder from a tourist changing \$200 at an airport kiosk all the way to the handful of banks that quote prices to each other in milliseconds, and we will keep returning to one organising idea: an exchange rate is the relative price of two monies, and *who* makes that price, *where*, and *with what plumbing* shapes everything about how a currency behaves. By the end you will understand why FX is OTC, why it runs 24 hours a day but only five days a week, what a "dealer" and a "prime broker" and an "ECN" actually are, and the single most counter-intuitive fact about the world's biggest market: most of its volume is not people buying currencies at all.

## Foundations: How the FX market is built

Before we can talk about dealers and swaps, we need a shared vocabulary. None of it is hard, but every term has to mean exactly one thing, because the whole structure is built out of these pieces.

**An exchange rate is a price, and a price is always relative.** When you read "EUR/USD = 1.0800", that is the price of one euro expressed in dollars: it costs \$1.08 to buy €1. The currency on the left (here, the euro) is the **base** currency — the thing being priced — and the currency on the right (the dollar) is the **quote** currency — the units the price is measured in. You never own a currency in a vacuum. To be "long the euro" is to be simultaneously *short* the dollar; every FX position is a bet on one money *against* another. This is the spine of the whole series and it matters here because it explains why the market is structured around *pairs*, quoted by dealers who must always be willing to do both sides.

**A pip is the smallest standard increment of a price.** For most pairs it is the fourth decimal place: EUR/USD moving from 1.0800 to 1.0801 is a one-pip move. For yen pairs, which are quoted to two decimals, a pip is the second decimal — USD/JPY from 150.00 to 150.01. Pips are how dealers and traders talk about spreads and moves without saying "0.0001" out loud all day. A typical interbank EUR/USD spread is around 0.2 of a pip; a thinly traded emerging-market pair might be 8 or 25 pips wide. Hold that idea; we will price a real order in pips shortly.

**A dealer is a firm that quotes a two-way price and stands ready to trade on it.** This is the load-bearing concept of the entire market. A dealer does not match you with another customer the way a stockbroker routes your order to an exchange. A dealer takes the *other side* of your trade onto its own book. If you want to buy €10 million, the dealer sells you €10 million from its own inventory — and now the dealer is short €10 million and has to decide what to do about that risk. Dealers earn the **bid-offer spread**: they quote a price at which they will buy (the bid) slightly below the price at which they will sell (the offer), and they pocket the difference across thousands of trades. The willingness to quote both sides, continuously, in size, is what makes someone a dealer rather than just another customer.

**"Interbank" means the wholesale market dealers run among themselves.** When a corporate buys euros from its bank, the bank now has a risk it does not want, so it goes and trades in the *interbank* market — the network of large banks dealing with each other — to lay that risk off. The interbank market is the wholesale tier where prices are genuinely *made*; everything below it is a markup on those prices. The biggest dealers — names like JPMorgan, Deutsche Bank, UBS, Citi, XTX Markets, Jane Street — are the **price makers**, and a tiny number of them account for a large share of all global volume.

**A prime broker (PB) lends you its credit and its name.** Here is a problem the structure has to solve: a hedge fund with a clever currency strategy cannot just call ten banks and start trading, because those banks have never heard of it and have no reason to extend it credit. FX trades settle a day or two later, so every trade is also a short-term loan of trust. A **prime broker** — usually one of the big banks — solves this by standing behind the fund: the fund trades in the prime broker's name, the banks face the prime broker (whom they trust) rather than the fund (whom they do not), and the prime broker takes a fee for renting out its creditworthiness. This is how a small fund gets to trade like a giant.

**An ECN is an electronic venue where many dealers' prices meet.** ECN stands for *electronic communication network*, and the most famous in FX are EBS and Refinitiv Matching (historically the two interbank "books"), plus multi-dealer platforms like 360T, FXall, and Currenex. An ECN is the closest thing FX has to an order book: dealers and other participants post bids and offers, and the venue matches them. But — and this is crucial — an ECN is one venue among many, not *the* market. There is no law that says a trade must happen there. Two banks can still deal directly over a chat message or a phone line, and a vast amount of volume does exactly that, "off-screen".

Put those pieces together and you get a layered market: a tiny price-making core, surrounded by banks that distribute those prices, surrounded in turn by funds, corporates, and retail clients who reach the core only through intermediaries above them. The figure that opened this section drew exactly that ladder. Now let's see what makes this shape so different from the markets you already know.

## OTC versus exchange: why FX has no marketplace

If you have ever bought a stock, you have used an **exchange**. The New York Stock Exchange and Nasdaq are central venues: every order to buy or sell a given share flows into one matching engine, gets matched against the best opposing order, and produces a single official "last price" that everyone sees on the same tape. A clearing house sits in the middle and guarantees that both sides get paid even if your counterparty vanishes. The hours are fixed — 9:30 a.m. to 4:00 p.m. in New York — and outside them the market is simply closed.

FX is built the opposite way. It is **over-the-counter**, which literally means the trade happens "across the counter" between two parties rather than through a central exchange. There is no single order book for EUR/USD. There are *hundreds* of dealers, each maintaining its own bid and offer, and a price is whatever a specific dealer is willing to show a specific client at a specific moment. There is no one official last price — there is a *cloud* of nearly-identical prices, and they can differ by a fraction of a pip across dealers at the same instant. There is no central clearing house standing behind every trade (though, as we'll see, a settlement utility called CLS handles much of the payment risk). And there are no fixed hours imposed from above; the market runs continuously because dealers in different time zones keep quoting.

![Before and after comparison of an exchange-based market versus the over-the-counter FX market](/imgs/blogs/the-biggest-market-on-earth-inside-the-interbank-fx-market-3.png)

Why did FX end up this way when stocks and futures are exchange-traded? Partly history and partly necessity. Currencies are issued by sovereign states, traded across borders, and used for real commerce — a German exporter paid in dollars, a Japanese pension fund buying US bonds, a Mexican importer paying for machinery. Those flows happen at every hour of every business day somewhere on Earth, in sizes that range from a \$200 holiday exchange to a \$5 billion central-bank intervention, and they could never fit the rhythm of a single national exchange with fixed hours. A decentralised, dealer-run, always-on market grew up around the actual pattern of global trade and capital flows. The OTC structure isn't a flaw; it is the natural shape of a market that has to serve the whole planet at once.

That structure has three consequences worth internalising, because they govern how you should read a currency.

First, **liquidity is a relationship, not a guarantee.** On an exchange, the order book is the order book; anyone can hit it. In OTC FX, a dealer chooses whether and at what spread to quote *you*. A top-tier hedge fund with a prime-brokerage line gets razor-thin spreads in huge size; a small retail account gets a wider, marked-up price. The same EUR/USD "market" is genuinely a different market depending on who you are and whom you can trade with.

Second, **there is no circuit breaker.** Exchanges halt trading when a stock moves too far too fast. FX has no such switch, because there is no central authority to flip it. When dealers got scared on the Swiss franc morning, nothing *stopped* — there was simply no one quoting, which is a far more frightening kind of gap than a formal halt.

Third, **the "price" you see is a quote, not a fact.** A stock's last trade actually happened at that price. A streaming FX quote is an *offer* to trade that the dealer can pull or re-price in milliseconds. In calm markets the distinction barely matters; the quote is firm enough to trade on. In a panic, the gap between the screen and what you can actually get widens dramatically. We will see exactly that gap in the franc and pound case studies later.

#### Worked example: the cost of crossing the spread

Suppose you are a tourist and you exchange \$10,000 into euros at an airport kiosk that quotes EUR/USD with a 3% markup around the "real" interbank rate of 1.0800. The kiosk buys euros from you (sells you dollars) at one price and sells euros to you at a much worse one. If the kiosk sells you euros at roughly 1.0800 × 1.03 ≈ 1.1124 dollars per euro, your \$10,000 buys you about €8,990. Change it straight back at the kiosk's buy rate of about 1.0800 ÷ 1.03 ≈ 1.0485 and your €8,990 returns only about \$9,426 — you've lost roughly **\$574, about 5.7%**, on a round trip where the underlying rate never moved at all. Now compare an interbank dealer quoting EUR/USD at 1.07999 / 1.08001 — a 0.2-pip spread. On \$10,000,000 that spread costs about **\$185**, or 0.002%. The takeaway: in an OTC market the *price* is almost the same everywhere, but the *spread* — your cost of access — depends entirely on which tier of the ladder you trade from.

## The interbank ladder: who makes the price

Now let's climb the ladder in detail, because the tier you sit on determines almost everything about your experience of the market.

At the very top sits the **price-making core**: a small number of global dealers who quote two-way prices to each other and to their best clients, continuously, in enormous size. There are perhaps a dozen banks and a handful of non-bank market-makers (electronic trading firms like XTX, Jane Street, Citadel Securities, HC Tech) that genuinely *make* prices in the major pairs. When you read that "the market" moved on a data release, what physically happened is that these firms re-priced their bids and offers, more or less in unison, in the milliseconds after the number printed. Their willingness to keep quoting *through* events is what gives the major pairs their famous depth — and their occasional, terrifying withdrawal of quotes is what produces the gaps.

Below the core sit the **regional and second-tier banks**. A mid-sized bank in Brazil or Vietnam or Poland is a customer of the top dealers, not a peer. It takes the core's prices, adds a markup, and distributes them to *its* clients — local corporates, smaller funds, retail brokers. It might make a tight price in its home currency (where it has natural flow and local knowledge) but be a pure price-*taker* in EUR/USD. This is why the same bank can be a sophisticated dealer in one pair and an ordinary client in another.

Alongside the banks sit the **electronic venues** — the ECNs and multi-dealer platforms. Historically the interbank core traded with each other on two anonymous central books, EBS (which became the natural home for EUR/USD and USD/JPY) and Refinitiv Matching (stronger in sterling and the commodity currencies). Today a sprawling ecosystem of venues — Currenex, 360T, FXall, Hotspot, plus every big bank's own "single-dealer platform" — carries the electronic flow. The venues matter because they are where price discovery is partly *centralised* even in an OTC market: when a big order hits EBS, every algorithm watching EBS reacts, and the price propagates across the whole market in milliseconds.

And then there is everyone else — the **buy side**. Hedge funds, asset managers, pension funds, corporates, and retail traders are all *clients*. They consume prices; they do not make them. The crucial structural fact is that most of them cannot reach the core directly. A hedge fund reaches it through a **prime broker**. A corporate reaches it through its **relationship bank**. A retail trader reaches it through a *retail broker*, who in turn reaches a bank or a venue. Every rung adds a markup, which is exactly why the spread you pay grows as you move down the ladder.

![Bar chart of FX turnover by instrument in 2022 showing FX swaps far larger than spot](/imgs/blogs/the-biggest-market-on-earth-inside-the-interbank-fx-market-2.png)

So what does this ladder *do* with a real order? Let's follow one all the way up.

#### Worked example: walking a \$50,000,000 corporate order up the ladder

A European manufacturer has just won a US contract and needs to convert \$50,000,000 of incoming dollars into euros next week. At EUR/USD 1.0800 that is about €46,296,296 it needs to buy. It does not call XTX. It calls its **relationship bank**, where it has credit lines and a salesperson it trusts. The bank's FX desk quotes a firm two-way price — say it offers euros at 1.08010 (a 1-pip markup over the 1.08000 mid). The corporate lifts the offer. Done — from the corporate's point of view the trade is finished in seconds.

But now the *bank* is short €46.3 million and long \$50 million, and it does not want that risk sitting on its book. So the bank's own trader works the position into the wholesale market. It might sell \$50 million through an ECN in five \$10 million clips over a few minutes, or hit several top dealers' streaming prices, paying the interbank spread of perhaps 0.2 of a pip each time. As those clips hit the venues, the top dealers absorb them, net them against opposing flow they already have (maybe a US fund that needs to *sell* euros at the same time), and the risk dissolves into the market. The bank's markup over the interbank price — 0.8 of a pip on €46.3 million, roughly **\$3,700** — is its fee for taking the corporate's risk and managing the unwind. The intuition: a large order is never filled at one price by one counterparty; it is *intermediated* — sliced, passed up the chain, and netted against the world's opposing flow until the risk is gone.

![Pipeline diagram showing a fifty million dollar order moving from corporate to bank to ECN to top dealers](/imgs/blogs/the-biggest-market-on-earth-inside-the-interbank-fx-market-5.png)

Notice what the ladder accomplishes. The corporate got a single, firm, instant price and never had to know anything about ECNs or prime brokers. The bank earned a small spread for absorbing and recycling the risk. The top dealers earned a smaller spread for providing the deep wholesale liquidity. And the \$50 million was netted against opposing flows so that, across the whole market, only a fraction of it ever needed to be a genuine *net* change in who owns euros. That netting — many flows cancelling each other out — is why a market with \$7.5 trillion of daily turnover can run on a far smaller amount of actual capital. Most of that turnover is risk being passed around the dealer chain, not new money entering the market.

### How prime brokerage actually rewires the ladder

It is worth slowing down on the prime broker, because it is the rung that quietly reshaped the modern market. Two decades ago, to trade FX in size you essentially had to *be* a bank, or have direct credit relationships with a dozen of them. That is a forbidding barrier: a new hedge fund with a brilliant currency model but no track record cannot persuade ten global banks to face it directly, because each of those banks would have to do its own credit assessment, sign its own legal documents, and post its own settlement limits to a counterparty it does not know. The cost of *access* — not the cost of the spread, but the cost of being allowed to trade at all — was the real wall.

Prime brokerage knocked that wall down. The fund signs *one* agreement with *one* prime broker (typically a large bank). The fund then trades in the open market — on ECNs, with other banks, on single-dealer platforms — but every trade is "given up" to the prime broker, who steps into the middle. To the banks the fund deals with, the counterparty of record is the prime broker, whom they already trust; to the fund, the prime broker is the single entity it settles with and posts collateral to. The fund gets to trade like a giant against the whole market while only ever managing one credit relationship. In return the prime broker charges a fee — often a few dollars per million traded — for renting out its balance sheet and its name.

This is why a great deal of the "buy side" volume in the BIS survey flows through a surprisingly small number of prime brokers. It also explains a structural fragility that surfaces in crises: because the prime broker is *guaranteeing* its clients' trades, a blow-up at one leveraged client can land on the prime broker's books. After the Swiss franc shock in 2015, several prime brokers took losses when clients who had bet on the franc floor could not cover their positions — the prime broker had guaranteed those trades to the market and was left holding the bag. The convenience of renting credit runs in both directions: it lets small players act big, and it lets one player's failure ripple straight into the bank that stood behind it.

#### Worked example: what a prime-brokerage line is worth

A mid-sized macro hedge fund runs \$2,000,000,000 and turns its book over roughly 50 times a year in FX, for \$100,000,000,000 of annual turnover. Without a prime broker it would need direct credit lines with, say, ten banks — ten sets of legal documents, ten credit committees, ten settlement relationships, and spreads marked wider because each bank sees only a slice of its flow and treats it as an unknown. With a prime broker charging, say, \$2 per million, the fund pays 0.000002 × \$100,000,000,000 = **\$200,000 a year** in prime-brokerage fees — and in exchange gets anonymous, top-tier access to dozens of liquidity sources it could never have reached alone, almost certainly saving far more than \$200,000 in tighter spreads. The intuition: in an OTC market, the scarce resource is not a price — prices are everywhere — it is *credit*, and the prime broker's whole business is manufacturing and renting it.

## How big is "biggest"? The scale of FX

It is worth pausing to feel the scale, because the numbers are so large they stop meaning anything if you just say them quickly. The benchmark source is the **BIS Triennial Central Bank Survey**, a once-every-three-years census in which central banks ask the dealers in their jurisdictions to report their FX volumes over a sample month. The April 2022 survey put average daily turnover at about **\$7.5 trillion**.

Try to hold that next to something familiar. The entire annual gross domestic product of the United Kingdom is roughly \$3.3 trillion. The FX market turns over more than two UK economies *every single day*. The total value of all global goods trade for a year — every container ship, every barrel of oil, every smartphone shipped — is around \$25 trillion; FX does that much in about three and a half days. The world's stock markets trade on the order of \$500–600 billion a day combined; FX is more than ten times larger. There is simply no other market in the same weight class.

![Bar chart of global FX turnover by BIS survey year from 2001 to 2022 showing roughly sixfold growth](/imgs/blogs/the-biggest-market-on-earth-inside-the-interbank-fx-market-4.png)

And it has grown relentlessly. In 2001 daily turnover was about \$1.24 trillion; by 2022 it was \$7.5 trillion — roughly a sixfold increase in two decades. The growth came from electronification (algorithms can quote and trade far more, far faster, than humans on phones), from the rise of non-bank market-makers, from the explosion of cross-border investment, and from the sheer growth of the global economy. There was a dip from 2013 to 2016 — partly a stronger dollar deflating the value of non-dollar volumes, partly a retreat of some banks after post-crisis regulation — but the long arc is straight up and to the right.

Here is the first thing to do with that number: **stop thinking of FX volume as "people buying currencies".** Most of it isn't. A \$7.5 trillion daily figure does not mean \$7.5 trillion of net new currency demand. It means \$7.5 trillion of *gross* transactions, the overwhelming majority of which are dealers recycling risk among themselves, and — as we are about to see — short-term funding operations that are not directional currency bets at all.

It also helps to know how the BIS arrives at the headline so you can read it critically. The survey is a *sample-month* census: in April of the survey year, central banks collect from the dealers in their jurisdiction the total FX they traded, then the BIS nets out double-counting (a trade between two reporting dealers would otherwise be counted twice) and scales the month to a daily average. The result is the best available estimate, but it is an estimate — taken once every three years, in one month, and inflated or deflated by how strong the dollar was that month (since non-dollar volumes are converted *into* dollars to report them). That is part of why the 2013-to-2016 figure dipped: a sharply stronger dollar shrank the reported dollar value of the rest of the world's trading even though the underlying activity had not really fallen. The number is real and the trend is unmistakable, but treat the precise figure as a careful approximation rather than a tick-by-tick measurement.

#### Worked example: how much is the dollar "on" of all of this?

The BIS survey reports that the US dollar is on one side of about **88.5%** of all FX trades (the shares sum to 200% because every trade has two currencies). On \$7.5 trillion of daily turnover, that means roughly 0.885 × \$7.5T ≈ **\$6.6 trillion a day** has a dollar leg. The euro, the second-most-traded currency, is on about 30.5% of trades, or about \$2.3 trillion a day. The reason matters: most currency *pairs* are not quoted directly. To trade Mexican pesos against Thai baht, a dealer almost always goes peso→dollar→baht, because the dollar is the **vehicle currency** with deep liquidity against everything. So the dollar isn't just one of many currencies in this market; it is the hub through which most of the other spokes connect. The takeaway: the structure of the FX market is dollar-shaped, and that is a structural reason — separate from US economic power — that the dollar dominates global finance.

### Who actually makes the price now: the rise of the non-bank market-maker

For most of FX's history, "dealer" and "bank" were synonyms. The price-making core was a club of large investment banks, and if you wanted to make markets in EUR/USD you needed a banking licence, a balance sheet, and a relationship with central banks. That has changed profoundly over the last fifteen years, and the change explains a lot about how prices move today.

A new kind of participant — the **non-bank market-maker** — now sits at the very top of the ladder alongside the banks. Firms like XTX Markets, Jane Street, Citadel Securities, Jump Trading, and HC Tech are not banks. They take no deposits and make no loans. They are technology firms whose entire business is quoting two-way prices and managing the resulting inventory with extraordinarily fast software. In the major pairs, these firms now rank among the largest liquidity providers in the world; XTX, for instance, has at times been one of the single biggest spot FX dealers on the planet despite holding no banking licence. They win by being faster and tighter than the banks: their models re-price in microseconds, their spreads are razor-thin, and they recycle inventory so quickly that they can quote enormous size on a comparatively small capital base.

The shift matters for the structure in three ways. First, it has **tightened spreads** in the liquid pairs to historically low levels, because the non-banks compete ferociously for flow and the banks have had to match them. Second, it has made liquidity more **conditional and faster-moving**: non-bank quotes are firm in calm conditions but can be withdrawn in microseconds when volatility spikes, because these firms are not in the business of warehousing risk through a storm. When everyone's algorithm pulls back at once, the depth that looked bottomless a second earlier can thin dramatically — part of what makes modern "flash" moves so violent. Third, it has **separated price-making from credit**: the non-banks make prices but typically rely on a *bank prime broker* to provide the credit and settlement that let them face the rest of the market. So even the firms at the very top of the price-making pyramid often sit, for credit purposes, behind a bank — another reminder that in OTC FX, credit and price are two different ladders that happen to overlap.

#### Worked example: how a market-maker earns the spread without taking a view

A non-bank market-maker streams EUR/USD at 1.07999 bid / 1.08001 offer — a 0.2-pip spread. Over a busy minute it might sell €50,000,000 to buyers at 1.08001 and, almost simultaneously, buy €48,000,000 from sellers at 1.07999, leaving it net short just €2,000,000 to hedge. On the matched €48,000,000, it captured the full 0.2-pip spread: 0.00002 × \$48,000,000 ≈ **\$960** of essentially riskless profit in one minute, repeated all day across thousands of pairs of trades. The firm never took a meaningful view on where EUR/USD was going; it earned the spread by *intermediating* the constant two-way flow and hedging the small residual. The intuition: a market-maker's edge is not prediction, it is being reliably present on both sides — and the firm that is fastest and tightest captures the most flow, which is exactly why technology firms muscled into the top of a market once owned by banks.

## Most of the volume is swaps, not spot

This is the single most surprising fact about the world's biggest market, and it is the one almost every beginner gets wrong. When people picture "FX trading", they picture **spot** — buying euros, selling yen, at today's price for delivery in a couple of days. Spot is the headline product, the thing on the news ticker, the rate at the airport kiosk. And spot is a *minority* of the market.

In the 2022 survey, average daily turnover broke down roughly like this: **FX swaps \$3.81 trillion, spot \$2.10 trillion, outright forwards \$1.16 trillion, FX options \$0.30 trillion, currency swaps \$0.12 trillion.** Spot is barely 28% of the total. The single largest instrument, by a wide margin, is the **FX swap** — and most people have never heard of it.

So what is an FX swap, and why does so much volume run through it? An FX swap is two trades bundled together: you swap currencies *now* and agree to swap them *back* at a fixed future date and a pre-agreed price. Crucially, **it is not a directional bet on the exchange rate at all.** It is a short-term funding tool — a way to borrow one currency while lending another for a set period, with no exposure to where the exchange rate goes in between.

A concrete picture helps. Imagine a European bank that needs US dollars for a week — perhaps a client needs dollar funding, or the bank has a dollar payment to make. It has euros but not dollars. It could borrow dollars in the money market, but a cheaper, cleaner route is an FX swap: today it gives euros to a counterparty and receives dollars (the "near leg"); in one week it gives the dollars back and receives its euros back at a price agreed today (the "far leg"). For one week the bank *has* the dollars it needs, and it has locked in the cost up front. It never took a view on EUR/USD. It used the FX market as a **funding** market, not a speculation market.

That is why swaps dominate. The world is full of institutions that are temporarily short of one currency and long of another and need to bridge the gap for days or weeks — banks funding their books, corporates managing cash across subsidiaries, fund managers hedging the currency exposure of their foreign bonds. All of that is funding, all of it runs through FX swaps and forwards, and all of it dwarfs the volume of people actually trying to profit from currency moves. The takeaway is profound: **the FX market is, first and foremost, the world's plumbing for short-term cross-border funding, and only secondarily a place to bet on exchange rates.** The mechanics of these instruments — how the forward price is set by the interest-rate gap between the two currencies — is the workhorse of the whole market, and we cover it in depth in a sibling post.

There is a second reason swap turnover is so large that has nothing to do with funding *demand* and everything to do with *tenor*. FX swaps are short — overnight, one-week, one-month are by far the most common — so they have to be *rolled* constantly. A bank that needs dollars on an ongoing basis does not do one long swap; it does a one-week swap and then, a week later, does another, and another. Each roll is a fresh transaction that the BIS survey counts again. So a single, persistent funding need shows up as a stream of swaps that recur every few days, inflating turnover relative to the actual stock of funding outstanding. Spot, by contrast, is a one-and-done transaction — you buy the euros and you are finished — so it generates far less repeat volume per dollar of underlying activity. Part of why swaps look so dominant is simply that they churn.

It is also worth being precise about why spot, despite being a minority of *turnover*, is still the conceptual centre of the market. Every swap and every forward is priced *off* the spot rate plus an interest-rate adjustment. The spot rate is the anchor; the forwards and swaps are spot wearing a funding overlay. So when a beginner watches "the EUR/USD rate", they are watching the spot market — the smaller, but foundational, slice. The swaps are built on top of it. Understanding that the headline you see (spot) sits on a far larger, quieter base (funding swaps) is the key to reading turnover figures correctly: the visible tip is spot, the submerged bulk is funding.

#### Worked example: an FX swap as pure funding

A European bank needs \$100,000,000 for one week. Spot EUR/USD is 1.0800. On the **near leg**, it delivers €92,592,593 (= \$100,000,000 ÷ 1.0800) and receives \$100,000,000. Suppose 1-week dollar rates are higher than euro rates, so the forward price is slightly different — say the agreed far-leg rate is 1.07990. On the **far leg** one week later, the bank delivers the \$100,000,000 back and receives €92,601,167 (= \$100,000,000 ÷ 1.07990). The bank ends with about **€8,574 more** than it started — the small euro gain *is* the cost of borrowing dollars for the week, baked into the price difference between the two legs. The bank had \$100 million for a week, paid for it through the swap points, and took **zero** view on where EUR/USD actually went. That, repeated millions of times a day across every currency pair, is what \$3.81 trillion of daily swap turnover really is: the world funding itself.

## Where it happens: London, the sun, and the wires

A market with no central building still has *geography*, because the dealers who run it sit in physical trading rooms in a handful of cities. The BIS measures where trades are *booked*, and the concentration is striking: the **United Kingdom handles about 38% of all global FX turnover**, the **United States about 19%**, **Singapore about 9%**, **Hong Kong about 7%**, and **Japan about 4%**. London alone, a city in a country whose own currency is a fraction of global trade, is the single largest FX centre on Earth.

![Bar chart of FX turnover by trading centre in 2022 with the United Kingdom the largest at thirty-eight percent](/imgs/blogs/the-biggest-market-on-earth-inside-the-interbank-fx-market-6.png)

Why London? Not because of the pound — UK turnover dwarfs the pound's share of trading. London's dominance is a legacy of empire-era trade finance, a convenient time zone that overlaps both Asia's close and America's open, English-language law and contracts, a deep concentration of banking talent, and decades of regulatory and infrastructure investment. It is a network effect: dealers cluster where the other dealers are, because that is where the liquidity is, and liquidity attracts more dealers. The same logic concentrates Asian FX in Singapore and Hong Kong and keeps a chunk of dollar trading in New York. The lesson for reading the market: when a currency "gaps" on news that breaks during a thin session — say, an Asian-hours headline while London and New York sleep — the move can be violent precisely because the deepest pools of liquidity are not awake to absorb it.

One subtlety worth flagging: the BIS measures where trades are *booked*, not where the traders physically sit or where the matching engines run. A bank may have its sales desk in Tokyo and its risk-management book in London, so a trade arranged in Asia can be *booked* in the UK and counted toward London's share. Much of the actual electronic matching, meanwhile, happens in data centres in a few specific locations — the EBS and Refinitiv engines have historically lived in or near London and New York metro areas, and much of the non-bank flow matches in equinix-style data centres in New Jersey and Slough. So "London is 38% of FX" is partly a statement about where the *books and legal entities* are, and the physical reality of microsecond price discovery is even more concentrated than the survey suggests. For a beginner the headline is enough: FX is dealer-centric, and the dealers cluster, so liquidity clusters with them — in a few cities, and within those cities, in a few buildings full of servers.

That geography is what makes FX a **24-hour, five-day** market. The trading day is a relay that follows the sun. The week opens around Monday morning in Sydney and Tokyo, while it is still Sunday evening in New York. As Asia trades, liquidity builds; then London wakes around 7:00–8:00 UTC and volume surges, because London is the deepest pool. For a few hours in the early afternoon UTC, **London and New York are open at the same time** — the daily peak, when spreads are tightest and the biggest orders can be absorbed. Then Europe goes home, New York thins out into its afternoon, and as America winds down, Sydney is already reopening for the next day. Round and round, continuously, from Sydney's Monday open to New York's Friday close.

![Intraday chart of FX activity by hour of day in UTC showing the London and New York overlap as the peak](/imgs/blogs/the-biggest-market-on-earth-inside-the-interbank-fx-market-7.png)

But notice the *five*. FX is 24-hour but not 24/7. It closes over the weekend — roughly from New York's Friday evening close to Sydney's Monday morning open — because the banks that make the prices are closed and, more fundamentally, because the **settlement systems** that move the actual money are closed. (This is one of the structural differences with crypto, which trades genuinely 24/7 because it settles on a blockchain that never sleeps; we'll return to that contrast in a later post.) The weekend gap is why a major event over the weekend — an election, a devaluation, a war — can produce a "gap open" on Monday, where the first Sydney price is far away from Friday's close, with no trading in between to cushion the move.

#### Worked example: liquidity and the cost of trading the wrong hour

Suppose you need to sell \$30,000,000 of AUD/USD. During the **London–New York overlap**, AUD/USD might be quoted with a 0.6-pip spread and enough depth to absorb \$30 million in one or two clips at a cost of roughly 0.6 pips, or about **\$1,800** (0.00006 × \$30,000,000). Now suppose you must trade the same size at **22:00 UTC**, in the thin gap after New York closes and before Sydney fully ramps up, when relative activity is near its daily low. The spread might widen to 2–3 pips and the available depth shrink, so pushing \$30 million through could cost 3 pips or more and *move* the price against you — easily **\$9,000** of spread plus market impact, several times the overlap cost. The intuition: in FX, *when* you trade is part of the price. The same order is cheap at the daily peak and expensive in the overnight lull, because liquidity is a function of which dealers are awake.

## Settlement: the risk that hides under "done"

There is one more piece of plumbing without which the picture is incomplete, and it is the part that nearly took down the whole system once. When you and a dealer agree a EUR/USD trade, you have not actually exchanged any money yet — you have agreed to. The euros and the dollars change hands a day or two later, and they move through *different* national payment systems in *different* time zones. The euros settle through Europe's system during European hours; the dollars settle through the US system during American hours.

That time gap creates **settlement risk** — also called *Herstatt risk*, after the German bank that made it famous. On 26 June 1974, Bankhaus Herstatt had taken in Deutsche marks from counterparties who were expecting dollars back in New York later that day. German regulators shut the bank down at the end of the German business day — *after* it had received the marks but *before* it had paid out the dollars. Its counterparties had paid one leg and never received the other. A boring back-office timing gap turned into real, permanent losses and a scare that rippled through the global banking system.

The industry's answer, decades later, was **CLS** (Continuous Linked Settlement), a specialised utility that settles both legs of an FX trade *simultaneously* — "payment versus payment" — so that neither side can pay without receiving. If one party fails to deliver, the trade simply doesn't settle and nobody is left out of pocket. CLS now settles a large share of global FX value and has dramatically reduced the systemic version of Herstatt risk. But it is worth knowing that a meaningful slice of FX still settles outside CLS — in currencies it doesn't cover, or in trades structured around it — which is why settlement risk remains a live concern that regulators keep pushing the market to address. The deeper point: in an OTC market with no central clearing house, *who you trade with* and *how the money settles* are not back-office trivia. They are part of the risk of every position.

This is also where the "T+2" convention comes from. Standard spot FX settles two business days after the trade — "T plus 2" — precisely because the money has to move through two different national systems and a settlement utility, and that takes time and overlapping business hours to arrange safely. (A handful of pairs, notably USD/CAD, settle T+1.) The settlement calendar is why a "spot" trade isn't actually instant and why a trade done on a Thursday before a Monday holiday in one of the two countries can have a longer-than-usual settlement window. Beginners often assume FX is as immediate as tapping a card; in reality the price is agreed instantly but the *cash* takes days to move, and that lag is the soil in which settlement risk grows. The growing share of FX settling outside CLS in recent surveys is exactly why central banks keep warning that Herstatt risk, fifty years on, has been *reduced* rather than *eliminated*.

#### Worked example: the gross-versus-net efficiency of FX settlement

Consider two banks that, over a single day, do many EUR/USD trades with each other in both directions, summing to \$5,000,000,000 that Bank A owes Bank B and \$4,950,000,000 that Bank B owes Bank A. Settling every trade **gross** would mean moving the full \$9.95 billion across the wires, with Herstatt-style exposure on each leg. Settling **net** through a utility like CLS, the two banks exchange only the \$50,000,000 difference — about **0.5%** of the gross amount. That netting is why a market that *turns over* \$7.5 trillion a day actually *moves* far less real money through the payment systems, and why the plumbing — netting plus payment-versus-payment — is as important to FX's stability as the dealers are to its prices. The intuition: most of the day's turnover cancels out; only the small net residual is genuine new money changing hands.

## Common misconceptions

**"There is one true exchange rate."** No. Because FX is OTC, there is no single official price — only a tight cloud of quotes that differ by fractions of a pip across dealers, and that *widen* into a real spread of prices in stress. The "rate" you see on a finance website is a composite or a single dealer's feed, not a universal truth. On 15 January 2015, different EUR/CHF "prices" during the franc spike ranged across an enormous band, and which one you got depended entirely on who you could reach. Treating the headline rate as a hard fact is the most common beginner error.

**"FX volume measures demand for currencies."** No. The \$7.5 trillion daily figure is *gross turnover*, dominated by dealers recycling risk among themselves and by FX swaps that are funding operations, not directional bets. Net new demand for a currency on a given day is a tiny fraction of the headline. If you read "\$7.5 trillion traded" as "\$7.5 trillion of buying pressure", you will badly misjudge what the number means.

**"Most FX is people speculating on exchange rates."** No. Spot — the closest thing to a directional currency bet — is only about \$2.1 trillion of the \$7.5 trillion, and even much of *that* is hedging and corporate flow rather than speculation. The single biggest instrument, the FX swap at \$3.81 trillion, takes essentially no view on the exchange rate at all. The market is mostly plumbing.

**"You're trading against a market; the broker is neutral."** Not in OTC FX. Your dealer takes the *other side* of your trade onto its own book — when you buy, it sells to you from inventory. A good dealer manages and recycles that risk in the wholesale market rather than betting against you, but the structural fact remains: there is a counterparty with its own book, not an anonymous matching engine. This is why counterparty credit and prime brokerage exist at all.

**"FX trades 24/7 like crypto."** No — it is 24 *hours*, *five* days. It closes over the weekend because the dealers and, more importantly, the national settlement systems that move the actual currency are closed. That weekend gap is real and tradable: events that break while the market is shut produce gap opens on Monday with no liquidity in between to soften the move.

## How it shows up in real markets

The structure stops being abstract the moment it is stress-tested. Three episodes show the OTC, dealer-centric design doing exactly what its mechanics predict — for better and for worse.

**The Swiss franc, 15 January 2015.** When the SNB abandoned its 1.20 floor, EUR/CHF didn't *trade* down from 1.20 to 0.86 through a series of prices. It *gapped*, because every dealer pulled its quotes at once. In an exchange-traded market a circuit breaker would have halted trading; in OTC FX there was nothing to halt and no one quoting, so for minutes there was effectively no price. Retail brokers, whose clients were heavily long the franc-floor "free money" trade, couldn't close those positions at anything near the screen price; some clients ended up owing their brokers money, and at least one large retail broker needed a bailout. The lesson the structure teaches: in a market where liquidity is a voluntary relationship, it can vanish exactly when you need it most.

**The pound, 26 September 2022.** After the UK's "mini-budget" of unfunded tax cuts spooked markets, GBP/USD slid from about 1.125 toward an all-time intraday low near **1.035** in thin Asian-hours trading on the Monday. Note *when* it happened: the worst of the move came in the gap between New York's close and London's open, the thinnest part of the daily relay, when the deepest pools of liquidity were asleep. A given amount of selling moves the price far more when London and New York aren't there to absorb it. The session geography we charted is not trivia — it is the difference between an orderly decline and a disorderly plunge.

**The yen carry unwind, August 2024.** When the Bank of Japan hiked and the popular "borrow cheap yen, buy higher-yielding assets" trade reversed, USD/JPY fell from about 161.9 in early July to roughly 141.7 by 5 August, and the VIX spiked to an intraday 65.7. The plumbing mattered here in a specific way: the carry trade had been built largely through FX swaps and forwards — funding instruments — and when everyone needed to unwind at once, the *funding* side of the market seized up faster than the *spot* side. A market that is mostly swaps behaves like a funding market under stress: the scramble is for the borrowed currency, not just for a directional exit. We unpack that unwind in detail in the macro-trading series.

In all three, the common thread is that FX's structure — OTC, dealer-quoted, swap-heavy, time-zoned — is invisible in calm markets and decisive in violent ones. The depth that makes the major pairs so cheap to trade on a normal Tuesday is *provided*, voluntarily, by dealers; it is not a property of an exchange that is always there. When the dealers step back, the deepest market on Earth can momentarily have no floor.

## The takeaway: read the structure, not just the price

If you came here thinking of FX as a giant casino where people bet on currencies, the most useful thing you can do is replace that picture with a more accurate one. The foreign exchange market is the world's largest market because it is the world's *plumbing* — the system through which every cross-border payment, hedge, investment, and short-term loan is denominated and funded. The \$7.5 trillion a day is mostly that plumbing at work: dealers recycling risk among themselves, and institutions funding one currency with another through swaps that take no view on the rate. The portion that is actual directional currency speculation is the smallest and most visible slice of a much larger, quieter machine.

That reframing changes how you read everything that follows in this series. When you see a currency move, ask *who* made that price and *which tier* of the market it came from — a thin Asian session or the deep London–New York overlap. When you hear a turnover figure, remember it is gross, swap-heavy, and dollar-centred, not net demand. When you size a trade, remember that your spread is a function of where you sit on the ladder and which dealers will quote you. And when you worry about risk, remember that in an OTC market with no central clearing house, the question "who is on the other side, and how does the money actually settle?" is never academic.

The exchange rate is the relative price of two monies, and now you know the machine that prices it: a private, global, always-on network of dealers, organised in tiers, running mostly on funding flows rather than bets, concentrated in a few cities, and held together by settlement plumbing that most people never see until it breaks. Everything else in currency trading — carry, intervention, pegs, crises — is what happens *on top* of this structure. Get the structure right, and the rest of the series will make far more sense.
