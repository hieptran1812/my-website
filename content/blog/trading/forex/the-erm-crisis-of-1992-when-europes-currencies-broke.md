> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — In 1992 a dozen European currencies were pegged to the German Deutsche Mark through the Exchange Rate Mechanism (ERM), so they all imported one interest rate set by one central bank. When German reunification forced the Bundesbank to jack rates up to fight a domestic spending boom, the rest of Europe — already sliding into recession — had to match those rates to defend their pegs, even though their economies screamed for cuts. That contradiction was unsustainable, and markets attacked the weakest links one by one.
>
> - A fixed exchange rate is a promise; a promise that hurts your own economy to keep is a promise the market will test.
> - The lira fell about 22% and the pound about 15% after both were forced out of the ERM in September 1992; the Spanish peseta was devalued repeatedly and Sweden burned reserves at a 500% overnight rate before giving up.
> - The deep cause was not "speculators" — it was a region sharing one anchor while its members sat in opposite halves of the economic cycle.
> - The one number to remember: the Bundesbank's discount rate hit **8.75%** in 1992, the rate that the whole ERM had to live with.

On the morning of Wednesday, 16 September 1992, the Bank of England did something that, on paper, should have saved the pound. Sterling was sitting on the floor of its allowed band inside the ERM, and sellers were leaning on it. So at 11:00 the Bank raised its base rate from 10% to 12%, and a few hours later announced it would go to 15%. Raising rates makes your currency more attractive to hold — that is the textbook defence. The pound did not move up. It barely flinched. By the evening the Chancellor stood outside the Treasury and announced Britain was leaving the ERM, and the 15% rate that had been promised that afternoon was quietly cancelled before it ever took effect.

That is the day everyone remembers, because a single fund — George Soros's Quantum — booked roughly a billion dollars shorting sterling, and "the man who broke the Bank of England" makes a better headline than "a region with a structural design flaw finally cracked." But Black Wednesday was not really about one trader, and it was not really about one currency. The same week, the Italian lira was forced out of the ERM too. Within a year the Spanish peseta had been devalued three separate times, the Portuguese escudo and Irish punt had been pulled down with it, and the Swedish krona — which was not even formally in the ERM but had pegged itself unilaterally — had been defended with an overnight interest rate of 500% before the Riksbank gave up and let it float. This was a *regional* break. The interesting question is not "how did Soros win" but "why did half a continent's pegged currencies all snap inside twelve months." This post is about that whole story.

![Bar chart of the 1992 ERM crisis showing the lira down 22 percent, the pound down 15 percent, the krona down 10 percent and the peseta down 8 percent](/imgs/blogs/the-erm-crisis-of-1992-when-europes-currencies-broke-1.png)

The spine that runs through every post in this series applies here in its purest form. An exchange rate is the relative price of two monies, and what moves it is the gap between two countries' interest rates plus the flow of money across borders. The ERM tried to *freeze* one of those prices — to hold the relative price of the lira and the Mark, the franc and the Mark, the pound and the Mark, all roughly constant. But the interest-rate gaps underneath those prices were being pulled apart by economies in different cycles. You cannot hold a price fixed when the forces underneath it are diverging without someone paying for the gap, and in 1992 the question of *who pays* got answered the hard way.

## Foundations: The ERM and the one-anchor problem

Let me build this from zero, because the mechanics matter and most people who "know about 1992" only know the Soros anecdote.

**What is an exchange rate, again.** The price of the pound in dollars (call it GBP/USD) is just how many dollars one pound buys. If GBP/USD is 1.77, a pound costs \$1.77. A currency "weakening" or "depreciating" means that number falls — your pound buys fewer dollars. The same logic applies to GBP/DEM (pounds priced in German Marks), which was the price the ERM actually cared about. Throughout this post I will quote pairs in the "X units of quote per 1 unit of base" convention; if a term is new, the foundations post [base, quote, pips and how to read an FX quote](/blog/trading/forex/base-quote-pips-and-how-to-read-an-fx-quote) walks through it slowly.

**What a peg is.** A *floating* currency lets the market set that price moment to moment. A *pegged* currency is one the government promises to hold at, or near, a chosen level against some anchor. The promise is enforced by the central bank standing ready to buy or sell its own currency in unlimited size at the edges. If everyone wants to sell your currency and the price is about to fall below your promised floor, you — the central bank — buy your own currency using your foreign-exchange reserves, soaking up the selling so the price holds. If the pressure is the other way, you sell your own currency (which you can print freely) and accumulate reserves.

**What the ERM was.** The Exchange Rate Mechanism was the currency-pegging arrangement at the heart of the European Monetary System, launched in 1979. It was the stepping stone toward the eventual single currency, the euro, which would arrive in 1999. Under the ERM, each member currency was given a *central rate* against every other member currency, and was allowed to trade only within a narrow band — for most currencies **±2.25%** around that central rate (a few, including the lira originally and later the peseta and sterling, used a wider **±6%** band). If a currency reached the edge of its band, the two central banks involved were *obliged* to intervene to push it back inside, and one or both could adjust their interest rates to make holding the currency more or less attractive.

In practice, the ERM was not a web of equal pegs. It was a *hub and spokes*. The Deutsche Mark was the anchor — the most credible, lowest-inflation currency, run by the most independent and inflation-phobic central bank in the world, the Bundesbank. Everyone else effectively pegged to the Mark and, in doing so, *imported German monetary policy*. That was the whole point: a high-inflation country like Italy or the UK could "borrow" the Bundesbank's credibility by tying its currency to the Mark. If the lira was pegged to the Mark, then Italian inflation had to converge toward German inflation, or the peg would become impossible to hold. The discipline was the feature. This is exactly the "borrowed anchor" logic the [pegs, bands and crawling pegs](/blog/trading/forex/pegs-bands-and-crawling-pegs-the-spectrum-of-fx-regimes) post lays out as a general regime choice.

**The convergence promise.** The deal European governments made with their own electorates and markets was roughly this: tie yourself to the Mark, accept German monetary discipline, let your inflation fall toward Germany's, and in exchange you get lower interest rates over time, currency stability, and eventually membership in a single European currency. For most of the late 1980s it worked. Inflation rates converged. The bands held. Capital controls, which several members still had, were being dismantled, opening the currencies to free cross-border flows. It looked like a slow, managed glide toward monetary union.

**The one-anchor problem.** Here is the flaw baked into the design, and it is the single most important idea in this entire post. When a dozen economies all peg to one anchor, they all share *one* monetary policy — the anchor's. But monetary policy is supposed to be set for the conditions of *one* economy. Interest rates that are right for a booming Germany are wrong for a recessionary Britain. As long as the members' economies move roughly together — same point in the cycle, same inflation pressure — one rate fits all and the system is comfortable. The moment the members' cycles diverge, one rate fits *none*, and the peg starts demanding that some countries run monetary policy that is actively harmful to their own economies. A peg is only as strong as a government's willingness to sacrifice its domestic economy to defend it, and that willingness is finite.

![Diagram of the one anchor problem showing German reunification forcing high Bundesbank rates onto every ERM member, straining the ones in recession until pegs break](/imgs/blogs/the-erm-crisis-of-1992-when-europes-currencies-broke-2.png)

That figure is the entire crisis in one chain, and the rest of this post is just filling in each box with what actually happened. The trigger that pulled the cycles apart was a once-in-a-generation political event: the reunification of Germany.

#### Worked example: what "importing one rate" costs a country in the wrong cycle

Suppose you run a mid-sized ERM economy. Your economy is in recession; unemployment is rising; you would, left to your own devices, cut your policy rate to, say, 4% to stimulate borrowing and spending. But your peg to the Mark requires you to keep your rate near Germany's, which reunification has pushed to roughly 9%. So you hold rates at 9% into a recession.

What does that gap cost? Take a country with, say, \$800 billion of floating-rate borrowing across its government, businesses and mortgage holders. Running rates 5 percentage points higher than your economy needs (9% instead of 4%) is, very roughly, an extra 0.05 × \$800B = **\$40 billion a year** in interest drained out of borrowers' pockets — money that is not being spent or invested, deepening the very recession you are trying to escape. That is the price of the peg, paid not by the central bank but by every mortgage holder and indebted business in the country. The intuition: a peg in the wrong cycle is a hidden tax on your own economy, and at some point the political cost of that tax exceeds the prestige of the peg.

### Why "fix the price" was even a tempting idea

It is easy, looking back, to ask why anyone would build a system with such an obvious flaw. The answer is that for most of its life the flaw was invisible, and the benefits were real. Three forces pushed Europe toward fixing its exchange rates in the first place.

The first was the **memory of the 1970s**. After the post-war Bretton Woods system of fixed-to-the-dollar rates collapsed in 1971-73, European currencies floated, and the result was chaos: wild swings in the relative value of the franc, the lira, the Mark and the pound that disrupted trade between neighbours who did most of their business with each other. A French exporter selling to Germany could see a year's profit margin wiped out by a currency move that had nothing to do with the quality of its product. Europe's economies are deeply integrated — a car built in one country uses parts from five others — and that integration is hard to run when the prices between the currencies lurch around. Fixing the rates was an attempt to give European trade the stability of a single currency without yet having one.

The second was **the credibility transfer**. High-inflation countries — Italy above all, but also France and Britain at various points — wanted to import the Bundesbank's hard-won reputation for sound money. If you cannot convince your own public and your own bond markets that you will keep inflation low, you can borrow someone else's promise: peg to the Mark, and now *breaking* your inflation discipline means breaking your peg, which is a visible, humiliating, politically costly event. The peg ties your own hands in a way the market can see, and that visible commitment lowers your borrowing costs and your inflation expectations. For a decade, this worked: Italian inflation fell from the high teens in the early 1980s toward German levels by the late 1980s, and the lira's peg was a big part of how.

The third was **the political project**. The ERM was never purely an economic arrangement; it was a deliberate step toward European political and monetary union. The whole point was to get the currencies stable enough, and the economies converged enough, that a single currency became feasible. The 1992 Maastricht Treaty — signed in February of that very year — set out the path to the euro. So the pegs were defended not only for economic reasons but for the political prize at the end of the road. That political commitment is why France fought so hard to keep the franc in (and succeeded) while Britain, always more ambivalent about European integration, was quicker to let sterling go.

### The realignments before 1992: the system working as designed

Here is the part most people miss: the ERM was *built* to be adjusted. Between 1979 and 1987 there were around a dozen realignments — coordinated, orderly adjustments where currencies were re-pegged at new central rates to reflect changed fundamentals. A country whose inflation had run hotter than Germany's would devalue a few percent within the system, everyone would agree on the new rates over a weekend, and the bands would continue. This was the *safety valve*. As long as the realignments happened regularly and in modest steps, the pressure never built to dangerous levels, and speculators never got a clean one-way bet, because they could not be sure of the timing or size of the next small adjustment.

Then something changed. After 1987, in the run-up to Maastricht and the euro, European governments decided that realignments were a sign of weakness — that a currency union could not be built on a foundation of currencies that kept devaluing against each other. So they stopped. From 1987 to September 1992, there were essentially no realignments of the major currencies. The safety valve was welded shut in the name of demonstrating commitment to the euro. And the very thing that was supposed to prove the system's strength — "look, we never devalue anymore" — is what made it brittle. With no pressure-release adjustments, the gaps between where the pegs were set and where the fundamentals said they should be grew quietly for five years, until reunification lit the fuse and all of that suppressed adjustment came out at once. The lesson is general and worth holding onto: a fixed-rate system that *can* adjust in small steps is far more robust than one that proves its toughness by refusing to adjust at all. Rigidity is not the same as strength.

## The reunification shock: one country's boom, a continent's bill

The Berlin Wall fell in November 1989, and Germany formally reunified in October 1990. Politically it was a triumph. Economically, it was an enormous, inflationary fiscal shock — and the bill landed not just on Germany but, through the ERM, on all of Europe.

Reunification meant West Germany absorbing the much poorer East. The West German government poured money into the East: infrastructure, pensions, unemployment support, and — fatefully — it converted East German Ostmarks into Deutsche Marks at a politically generous one-to-one rate for many balances, far above their economic value. This was a massive injection of purchasing power and government spending into the German economy. Demand surged. The German budget swung into large deficit. And all that spending, on top of an economy already running near capacity, did exactly what a huge demand stimulus does: it pushed up inflation.

For most central banks, a burst of inflation is unwelcome. For the *Bundesbank*, it was close to a constitutional emergency. The Bundesbank's institutional memory was the hyperinflation of the 1920s, when the Mark's predecessor became literally worthless and people carried banknotes in wheelbarrows. Its entire reason for existing — its fierce, legally protected independence — was to never let that happen again. Faced with reunification-driven inflation pushing toward and past 4%, the Bundesbank did the only thing its mandate allowed: it raised interest rates, hard, and kept them high. The discount rate climbed to **8.75%** by mid-1992, and the Bundesbank made it clear it would not cut until German inflation was back under control, regardless of what the rest of Europe wanted.

Now hold both halves of the picture in your head. Germany: overheating, inflating, needing high rates to cool down. The rest of Europe — Britain, Italy, France, Spain, Sweden, Finland — sliding into recession in the early-1990s downturn, with rising unemployment and weak growth, needing *low* rates to recover. One anchor. Two opposite needs. And the ERM said everyone gets Germany's rate.

It is worth pausing on *why* this divergence was so unusually severe, because it explains why 1992 was a crisis and not just another routine realignment. Normally, economies that trade heavily with each other and share an anchor tend to move roughly in sync — a boom in Germany spills over into demand for French and Italian exports, pulling those economies up too. The cycles correlate. What made reunification different is that it was an *asymmetric* shock: it hit Germany specifically and powerfully, and hit it in a way that pushed German policy in the *opposite* direction from the cyclical needs of its neighbours. Most of Europe was already cooling from the late-1980s expansion when reunification gave Germany alone a violent fiscal jolt upward. So instead of the usual case where one anchor rate is a reasonable compromise across members in similar conditions, you had the anchor needing emergency tightening while everyone else needed easing. The correlation between the members' cycles did not just weaken — it briefly went *negative*. That is the rare condition under which a one-anchor system tears itself apart, and recognizing it in advance is most of the edge in trading these events.

There is also a credibility wrinkle that made the squeeze worse. The Bundesbank was not just *unwilling* to cut — it was institutionally *unable* to be seen bending to foreign political pressure without damaging the very credibility that made the Mark worth anchoring to. If the Bundesbank had cut rates in 1992 to relieve sterling and the lira, it would have signalled that German monetary policy could be bent by the needs of other governments — which would have undermined the whole premise that pegging to the Mark meant importing iron discipline. So the anchor was trapped too, in a sense: the thing the periphery needed it to do was the one thing it could not do without destroying its own value as an anchor. A one-anchor system relies on the anchor being rigidly self-interested; that rigidity is exactly what makes it useless to the periphery in a divergent cycle.

![Before and after diagram of an ERM band showing a currency drifting calmly inside the band versus pinned to the floor while the central bank sells reserves and hikes rates](/imgs/blogs/the-erm-crisis-of-1992-when-europes-currencies-broke-4.png)

The figure above is the mechanical heart of the problem. Inside the band, on the left, life is calm: the currency drifts within its ±2.25% window around the Mark, and the central banks only have to nudge it occasionally. On the right is the situation the whole periphery was drifting into through 1992: the currency pinned to the *bottom* of its band, sellers piling in, and the central bank forced to do two painful things at once — sell its precious foreign reserves to buy its own currency, and hike interest rates to defend the peg. The deeper your recession, the more those high defensive rates hurt, and the more obvious it becomes to everyone — including speculators — that you cannot keep this up.

To connect this to the master variable of FX: an exchange rate fundamentally tracks the *interest-rate differential* between two countries and the flows that differential attracts. The mechanism by which rate gaps drive currencies is the subject of [interest rate differentials, the master variable of FX](/blog/trading/forex/interest-rate-differentials-the-master-variable-of-fx) and, on the policy-transmission side, [how monetary policy moves currencies](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-monetary-policy-moves-currencies-rate-differentials). The ERM did not abolish that differential — it just forbade the *exchange rate* from moving to reflect it. When you forbid a price from adjusting, the pressure does not vanish; it accumulates behind the dam until the dam breaks.

#### Worked example: the rate Germany "should" have had vs the rate Europe needed

A crude but useful way to see the misalignment is a Taylor-rule sketch — the idea that a central bank's "right" rate rises with inflation and with how hot the economy is running. Suppose a neutral rate is 2%, and the rule adds 1.5 points for each point of inflation above a 2% target and subtracts for economic slack.

For Germany in 1992: inflation roughly 4% (2 points over target) and an economy running *hot* (positive output gap, say +1%). Right rate ≈ 2% + 1.5 × 2% + 0.5 × 1% ≈ **5.5%**, and arguably higher given the Bundesbank's hawkishness — consistent with the high-8s it actually ran.

For Britain in 1992: inflation falling toward 3.5% but an economy in deep *recession* (output gap maybe −3%). Right rate ≈ 2% + 1.5 × 1.5% − 0.5 × 3% ≈ **2.75%**. Britain was holding 10% instead.

The gap between what the UK needed (under 3%) and what it was running to defend the peg (10%) is the entire story in two numbers. The intuition: the ERM forced Britain to run monetary policy roughly 7 percentage points tighter than its own economy could bear — and no democracy tolerates that indefinitely.

## The strain on every currency: the dam under pressure

By the summer of 1992 the contradiction was visible to anyone who cared to look, and the foreign-exchange market is a machine for looking. Here is how the strain transmits into an actual speculative attack — the general anatomy is covered in [currency crises and the anatomy of a speculative attack](/blog/trading/forex/currency-crises-and-the-anatomy-of-a-speculative-attack), but 1992 is the canonical case.

A speculator's reasoning in 1992 went like this. "Country X is in recession. Its peg forces it to hold rates far too high. That is politically unsustainable — at some point the government will choose its economy over its peg and devalue or float. If I sell X's currency short now, one of two things happens. Either the peg holds, and I lose only the small carry cost of being short a currency whose interest rate is roughly the same as the one I'm funding in — a few percent a year at most. Or the peg breaks, the currency drops 10-20% in days, and I make a fortune. That is a wonderful asymmetry: limited downside, enormous upside." When a one-way bet like that becomes obvious, it does not stay a niche trade. Hedge funds, banks' proprietary desks, multinational corporates hedging their exposure, even ordinary savers moving money out — they all push the same way at once. The selling becomes self-reinforcing.

This is the cruelty of a *credible-looking but fundamentally misaligned* peg: it offers speculators a free option. The government, by promising to defend a fixed level, has effectively written the market a put option on its currency and is giving it away. The formal treatment of this — why a peg is a coordination game and why an attack can become self-fulfilling once enough players expect it — lives in [the central bank game](/blog/trading/game-theory/the-central-bank-game-credibility-commitment-and-dont-fight-the-fed) and the [bank-runs-as-coordination-games](/blog/trading/game-theory/bank-runs-as-coordination-games-diamond-dybvig-and-svb) post. The short version: defending a peg works only if the market *believes* you will defend it forever. The moment the market does the arithmetic and concludes that defence is too costly to sustain, belief collapses, and the collapse of belief is itself the thing that breaks the peg.

#### Worked example: a \$1 billion short across multiple ERM currencies

Put yourself in the seat of a macro fund in late August 1992 with a \$1 billion risk budget to express the view that "the weak ERM currencies will be forced down against the Mark." You spread the bet across the obvious candidates.

- **\$400 million short sterling** (GBP/DEM). The pound is near its ERM floor around DEM 2.778 per pound. You borrow pounds, sell them for Marks, and wait.
- **\$300 million short the lira** (ITL/DEM). Italy's fiscal position is the weakest of the lot; the lira is the most obvious break.
- **\$200 million short the peseta** and **\$100 million short the krona** as supporting positions.

Your carry cost — the interest you pay to be short these currencies versus the interest you earn on your Mark proceeds — is small, because these countries are holding *high* rates to defend their pegs. Say it nets to roughly 3% a year on the \$1B, or about \$30 million annually, \$2.5 million a month. That is your "insurance premium" for holding the trade.

Now the payoffs. When sterling falls ~15% and the lira ~22% after they exit the ERM, your two big legs alone make roughly 0.15 × \$400M + 0.22 × \$300M = \$60M + \$66M = **\$126 million**, and the peseta and krona legs add tens of millions more. Against a monthly cost of \$2.5 million, you have made well over 50 times your monthly carry in a matter of weeks. The intuition: a misaligned peg hands speculators a bet where the cost of being wrong is a trickle and the reward for being right is a flood — which is exactly why the flood arrives.

## The breaks: September 1992 and the months after

The dam broke in stages. It is worth walking through them, because each one teaches something slightly different.

**Finland, first crack (early September).** The Finnish markka was pegged to the European Currency Unit (a basket dominated by the Mark) but was outside the formal ERM. Finland was in a brutal recession, partly because its big trading partner, the Soviet Union, had just collapsed. On 8 September 1992 Finland abandoned its peg and let the markka float; it fell sharply. The market took note: the first domino had fallen, and nothing terrible had happened to the speculators who bet on it. The free option was paying out.

**Italy and the lira (mid-September).** Italy had the weakest fundamentals of the major members — high public debt, a large deficit, persistent inflation, and a political system poorly suited to the austerity the peg demanded. The lira had been under pressure for weeks; the Bundesbank and Banca d'Italia spent heavily defending it. On 13 September the lira was devalued by about 7% within the ERM in a coordinated realignment — a partial surrender meant to relieve pressure. It did not work. The selling continued, and within days Italy suspended its ERM membership entirely. By the end of the episode the lira was down roughly **22%** against the Mark — the largest move of the crisis.

**Britain and Black Wednesday (16 September).** Sterling had joined the ERM late, in 1990, and at a central rate — DEM 2.95 per pound — that many economists thought was too high to begin with, pegging an overvalued pound into a recession. Through 15 and 16 September the pound was hammered. The Bank of England spent billions of pounds of reserves buying sterling. Then came the famous rate ladder.

![Step chart of the UK rate ladder on Black Wednesday showing the base rate going from 10 to 12 percent then an announced 15 percent that was never enforced before the defence was abandoned](/imgs/blogs/the-erm-crisis-of-1992-when-europes-currencies-broke-5.png)

That ladder is the picture of a failed defence. Base rate 10% at the open, 12% by late morning, an announced 15% in the early afternoon — and the pound *still* would not lift off the floor, because the market had concluded that no rate the politically constrained UK government could actually sustain would change the fundamental story. By evening Britain suspended its ERM membership, and the planned 15% rate was scrapped. Sterling fell hard immediately and was about **15%** lower against the dollar by December.

![Line chart of GBP/USD through 1992 showing the pound near 1.77 before Black Wednesday on September 16 then falling roughly 15 percent to about 1.52 by December](/imgs/blogs/the-erm-crisis-of-1992-when-europes-currencies-broke-3.png)

The pound's path tells you that the rate hikes were a sideshow to the real event. The currency did not stabilize when rates were raised to 12% and 15%; it stabilized only when Britain *gave up the peg* and let the price move to where the fundamentals said it should be. The full story of the trade that profited most from this day — and how Soros's positioning worked — is the companion post [Soros and Black Wednesday, breaking the Bank of England](/blog/trading/forex/soros-and-black-wednesday-breaking-the-bank-of-england-1992); here the point is structural, not personal.

**France and the franc (the line that held).** It is worth dwelling on the currency that *didn't* break, because the contrast is the whole lesson. The French franc came under heavy attack too — not just in September 1992 but again, even more ferociously, in the summer of 1993. France's fundamentals were stronger than Britain's or Italy's: its inflation was already low, close to Germany's, and its economy, while weak, was not as far out of sync. But the deciding factor was political will plus the Bundesbank's cooperation. France and Germany were the two engines of the European project, and both governments treated the franc-Mark link as the indispensable core of the future euro. So when the franc was attacked, the Bundesbank did intervene to support it — buying francs, lending Marks — in a way it pointedly had *not* done for sterling or the lira. Even with that support, the pressure in 1993 was so intense that in August the ERM bands were widened dramatically, from ±2.25% to ±15%, which was close to letting the currencies float in all but name. The franc survived, but only by the system bending so far it almost stopped being a fixed-rate system at all. The lesson: a peg holds when the fundamentals are close *and* the anchor is willing to share the burden — and even then it may survive only by gutting the rules that made it a peg.

**Spain, Portugal, Ireland (the slower bleed).** Spain did not exit, but it could not hold its line either. The peseta was devalued within the ERM by about 5% on the same weekend Italy and Britain fell, then again in November 1992 (~6%), and a third time in May 1993 (~8%) — roughly an 8%+ cumulative slide, repeatedly repegging lower rather than floating. Portugal's escudo and Ireland's punt were dragged into the same realignments. These countries chose the middle path: not a clean float, but a series of managed retreats. It bought them political cover, but it also showed that the "credible peg" was now openly negotiable, which only invited more pressure.

**Sweden and the 500% rate (September–November).** Sweden's story is the most vivid illustration of how far a central bank will go — and how it still loses. The krona was pegged unilaterally to the ECU, not in the ERM, but it faced the identical pressure. To defend the krona in mid-September, the Riksbank raised its *marginal overnight lending rate* — the rate at which banks borrow from the central bank for a single night — to an almost unbelievable **500%**. The logic is real: at 500% annualized, the cost of being short the krona overnight is so punishing that, for a day or two, it freezes out the speculators. But it is a tourniquet, not a cure. You cannot run an economy at a 500% overnight rate; it was an emergency measure that bought hours, not a solution. Sweden held through September, but the pressure returned, and on 19 November 1992 the Riksbank gave up and let the krona float. It fell about **10%**.

#### Worked example: the cost of defending a band with reserves and 500% overnight rates

Two costs sit behind a defence like Sweden's, and it is worth pricing both.

*The reserve cost.* Suppose your central bank holds \$30 billion of foreign reserves and, over a few weeks of attack, you spend \$25 billion of them buying your own currency to hold the floor. If you then capitulate and the currency falls 10%, you bought all that domestic currency at the *expensive* (pre-devaluation) price and now your remaining assets are worth less in your own terms — but worse, the speculators who sold to you at the high price buy back after the fall and pocket the difference. On the \$25 billion you defended, a 10% drop is a **\$2.5 billion** transfer from the central bank (the taxpayer) to the people betting against it. Defending and losing is not neutral; it is a direct wealth transfer to the attackers.

*The rate cost.* A 500% *annualized* overnight rate sounds apocalyptic but applies for one night at a time. On a \$1 billion short krona position, one night of borrowing at 500% annualized costs roughly \$1B × 5.00 / 365 ≈ **\$13.7 million for that single night**. Hold the short for three nights at that punitive rate and you have bled ~\$41 million — real money, and exactly the point: the rate is set high enough that *most* speculators close out rather than pay it. But the funds that believe the peg will break anyway keep paying, because a 10% currency move (\$100 million on a \$1B position) dwarfs even a week of 500% overnight cost. The intuition: a punitive overnight rate can win a battle measured in days, but it cannot win a war measured in months — the math only works for the defender if the attack gives up first, and a fundamentally-justified attack does not give up.

## Which currencies broke, which bent, and which held

Not every ERM currency suffered equally, and the pattern is instructive. The currencies that broke or bent the most were the ones whose economic cycle and political situation diverged *furthest* from Germany's, and whose reserves and credibility were weakest. The ones that held were closest to the German core.

![Matrix comparing ERM currencies showing the pound and lira forced out, the peseta devalued and staying, the krona defended then floated, and the franc holding as the core anchor currency](/imgs/blogs/the-erm-crisis-of-1992-when-europes-currencies-broke-6.png)

Read that matrix as a spectrum of distance from the anchor. The pound and the lira — deep recessions, weak or overvalued entry rates, the lira also burdened by high debt — were forced out entirely. The peseta sat in the middle: too weak to hold its line cleanly, but defended through a sequence of managed devaluations rather than a clean float. The Swedish krona (marked with an asterisk because it was pegged unilaterally, not a formal ERM member) fought hardest with the most extreme rate and still floated in November. And the French franc — this is the crucial one — *held*, barely, but at enormous cost and only because France and Germany were politically committed to the eventual euro and the Bundesbank ultimately intervened to support the franc in a way it would not for the pound or lira. France's economy was closer to Germany's, its inflation was already low, and its political will to stay in was absolute. The franc was the core; the pound and lira were the periphery; and the periphery is always where a one-anchor system breaks first.

The deeper lesson hiding in that matrix is about *asymmetry of adjustment*. In a system anchored to the strongest member, all the adjustment burden falls on the weak. Germany was never going to cut rates to ease the strain on Britain — its mandate forbade it. So the only variable left to move was the *exchange rate* of the weak members, and the only way it could move was to break the peg. A fixed-rate system that cannot force the anchor to share the adjustment is a system that quietly loads all the pain onto whoever is furthest from the center. That is not a flaw the ERM had by accident; it is a property of any peg where the anchor sets policy for itself.

#### Worked example: why the strong anchor never blinks

Put yourself in the Bundesbank's seat. Britain begs you to cut rates to take the pressure off sterling. What is your calculation?

If you cut from 8.75% to, say, 6% to help Britain, German inflation — already at 4% in an overheating economy — accelerates further, perhaps toward 5-6%. Your entire institutional credibility rests on never letting that happen. The cost to *you* of cutting is a domestic inflation problem that could take years and a recession to unwind. The benefit to you is... helping a foreign country hold a peg you did not ask it to adopt at a rate you did not set. The cost-benefit is wildly lopsided. So you do not cut. You make small, symbolic gestures — a token 0.25-point trim in mid-September, which the Bundesbank did offer — but nothing that would actually ease the squeeze.

Quantify the asymmetry: a 2.75-point cut to help Britain might add, say, 1.5 points to German inflation. In a \$2 trillion economy, persistently higher inflation is a multi-hundred-billion-dollar problem in real terms over the years it takes to re-anchor expectations. Against that, "Britain keeps its peg" is worth essentially nothing to a German central banker. The intuition: in a one-anchor system, expecting the anchor to sacrifice its own mandate for the periphery is expecting a rational actor to set its own house on fire to keep a neighbour warm — it does not happen, which is precisely why the periphery has to break instead.

## The regional lesson: one anchor, many cycles

Step back from the day-by-day and the deeper structure comes into focus. 1992 was not a story about clever speculators or weak politicians. It was a story about a *design*: a region that fixed its currencies to one anchor while its members lived in different economic seasons.

![Before and after diagram contrasting the ERM where many currencies kept their own central banks and markets picked off the weak ones versus the euro where one shared currency and one central bank moved the strain into bond spreads](/imgs/blogs/the-erm-crisis-of-1992-when-europes-currencies-broke-8.png)

The figure frames the resolution. The ERM's specific flaw was that it was *halfway* to monetary union: it fixed the exchange rates but left every country with its own central bank, its own currency, and therefore its own door the market could attack. A speculator could pick the weakest currency and bet *that specific peg* would break — there was always a single point of failure to aim at. The euro, which arrived in 1999, "solved" the 1992 problem by removing the doors. There is no Italian lira to short against the Mark anymore; there is one euro and one central bank, the ECB. You cannot break a peg that no longer exists.

But — and this is the punchline that connects 1992 to everything that came after — the *underlying* one-anchor problem did not go away. It moved. Inside the euro, Germany and Greece still sit in different economic cycles and still share one interest rate. The strain that used to show up in currency markets (the lira falling against the Mark) now shows up in *bond markets* (Greek government yields blowing out versus German Bund yields in 2010-2012). The euro converted a currency crisis into a sovereign-debt crisis. The pressure-relief valve that used to be the exchange rate was welded shut, so the pressure found the next-weakest joint — government borrowing costs and, ultimately, the question of whether a country might leave the euro entirely. The mechanics of those sovereign spreads are the subject of [emerging market and sovereign debt, yield with country risk](/blog/trading/fixed-income/emerging-market-and-sovereign-debt-yield-with-country-risk); the point here is that the *one-anchor problem is conserved*. You can change which market expresses it, but you cannot make it disappear as long as one monetary policy serves many cycles.

This is the regional lesson, and it generalizes far beyond Europe. Any time a group of economies shares one anchor — a currency board, a dollar peg across a region, a monetary union — they are making a bet that their cycles will stay synchronized. When that bet is right, the system is calm and the shared anchor delivers stability and credibility cheaply. When that bet is wrong — when one member needs tight money and another needs loose, when an external shock hits the members asymmetrically — the anchor stops being a source of stability and becomes a transmission belt for one country's policy onto another country's economy. And the adjustment, when it finally comes, is violent, because all the pressure that the fixed rate prevented from venting gradually comes out at once.

## Common misconceptions

**"Soros broke the pound."** Soros's Quantum fund made a famously large profit — around \$1 billion — and his aggressive shorting added to the pressure on Black Wednesday. But sterling was overvalued, Britain was in recession holding rates far too high, and the lira had *already* been forced out before the worst of the sterling attack. The pound was going to break; Soros bet correctly and sized it enormously, but he did not create the fault line. If Quantum had not existed, some other fund would have taken the trade, because the asymmetry was sitting in plain sight. The honest framing is "Soros recognized and profited from a structural break that the ERM's own design had guaranteed." The math of why it was a near-free bet is in the \$1B-short worked example above.

**"Higher interest rates always defend a currency."** This is the most expensive misconception, because it *usually* works, which makes the exception so brutal. Raising rates does normally support a currency — higher yield attracts holders. But on Black Wednesday the UK announced rates of 12% and then 15% and the pound did not lift, because the market had concluded those rates were not *credible* — no government in a deep recession could actually hold a 15% base rate, so the announcement changed nothing about the expected endgame. Rate defence works only when the market believes you can sustain the rate. When the rate itself is the evidence of desperation, hiking can even *accelerate* the attack by confirming that the authorities are out of better options.

**"The currencies that broke were just badly managed."** There were genuine policy mistakes — sterling joined the ERM at too high a rate, Italy's fiscal position was weak. But the *core* cause was external and structural: German reunification, a once-in-a-generation shock that pushed German policy in the opposite direction from what the rest of Europe needed. Even a perfectly run peripheral economy would have been squeezed by being forced to hold German rates into a domestic recession. The break was a feature of the system under that shock, not just a failure of individual countries — which is exactly why so *many* broke at once.

**"Sweden's 500% rate proves a central bank can always defend if it's willing."** Sweden was *maximally* willing — a 500% overnight rate is about as serious as a defence gets — and it still failed within weeks. The 500% number is not proof that defence works; it is proof of the *opposite*. It shows that even an astronomical rate only buys days, because you cannot run a real economy at that rate, and a fundamentally-justified attack simply waits you out. Willingness is necessary but not sufficient; the fundamentals have to be defensible too.

**"A bigger pile of reserves would have saved them."** More reserves buy more time, but they do not change the underlying contradiction. The Bank of England, the Banca d'Italia and the Bundesbank collectively spent enormous sums defending the pegs and still lost. Reserves let you absorb selling, but against a one-way bet where every fund, corporate and saver is leaning the same direction, the potential selling is effectively unlimited, while your reserves are finite. Reserves can defeat a *temporary* loss of confidence; they cannot defeat a *correct* market judgment that the peg is misaligned. As the [how central banks intervene](/blog/trading/forex/how-central-banks-intervene-in-the-currency-market) post argues, intervention buys time to fix fundamentals — it is not a substitute for fixing them.

## How it shows up in real markets

The 1992 template — a peg under strain from a divergent cycle, a free-option asymmetry for speculators, a defence that works until belief snaps — recurs constantly. Recognizing the pattern is one of the most reliable edges in macro.

**1997, Asia.** The Asian financial crisis followed the same script with different actors. A string of Asian currencies were pegged (formally or de facto) to the US dollar. When the dollar strengthened and capital that had flooded in began to reverse, the pegs came under attack one after another — Thailand first (the baht broke in July 1997), then Indonesia, Korea, Malaysia, the Philippines. Same shape: a regional set of pegs, one external anchor moving the wrong way, the weakest link breaking first and the contagion spreading down the chain. The drawdowns were even worse than 1992 — the Indonesian rupiah lost over 80%. The full case is [the Asian financial crisis of 1997](/blog/trading/finance/asian-financial-crisis-1997).

**2015, Switzerland.** A direct modern echo of the defence-then-capitulation dynamic. The Swiss National Bank had capped the franc at 1.20 per euro for three years, defending it by printing francs and buying euros until its balance sheet ballooned to unsustainable size. On 15 January 2015 it abandoned the cap with no warning, and EUR/CHF collapsed from 1.20 to below 0.85 intraday before settling near parity — a ~30% move in minutes. The lesson is identical to 1992: a central bank can hold a line for a long time, but when holding it becomes more costly than abandoning it, the line goes, and it goes *instantly* and *violently* because everyone tries to get through the same door at once. The mechanics are in [the SNB 2015 peg break](/blog/trading/forex/the-snb-2015-peg-break-when-a-central-bank-blinks).

**2022, Britain again.** Thirty years almost to the month after Black Wednesday, sterling crashed again — this time not because of a peg, but because a poorly received "mini-budget" of unfunded tax cuts in September 2022 triggered a run on UK assets, a gilt-market crisis (the LDI pension blowup), and a plunge in the pound to its lowest-ever level against the dollar, about 1.035 on 26 September.

![Line chart of GBP/USD in 2022 showing the pound falling from about 1.13 after the mini-budget on September 23 to an all-time low near 1.035 on September 26 before recovering](/imgs/blogs/the-erm-crisis-of-1992-when-europes-currencies-broke-7.png)

The 2022 episode is an echo, not a repeat — and the difference is the lesson of this whole post. In 1992, the pound had a *floor to break*: a fixed level it was promising to hold, which made the attack a clean binary bet. In 2022, sterling floated freely, so there was no peg to break and no single point of failure to aim at — the currency simply repriced sharply and then partly recovered once the Bank of England stepped in to stabilize the gilt market and the worst of the fiscal plan was reversed. A floating currency absorbs a shock by *moving*; a pegged currency absorbs it by *breaking*. That is the single most important practical takeaway from comparing the two charts.

**The general signal.** When you see a currency pegged or heavily managed against an anchor, the questions 1992 teaches you to ask are: Are the two economies in the same point of the cycle, or diverging? Does the peg force the weaker economy to run policy that hurts it? How deep are the reserves relative to the potential selling? And how politically sustainable is the defence? When the answers line up the way they did in Britain and Italy in 1992 — diverging cycles, painful imported policy, finite reserves, fragile politics — you are looking at a free option for the market, and free options eventually get exercised. The way a rising dollar specifically squeezes pegged emerging markets is the subject of [the dollar as a wrecking ball for emerging markets](/blog/trading/forex/the-dollar-as-a-wrecking-ball-for-emerging-markets); the same one-anchor logic runs underneath it.

## The takeaway: read a peg as a promise the market will test

The ERM crisis of 1992 is the cleanest lesson in all of currency trading about what a fixed exchange rate actually is. It is a *promise* — a government's promise to hold a price constant — and a promise is only as good as the cost the promiser is willing to bear to keep it. The ERM's members promised to hold their currencies to the Deutsche Mark, but German reunification raised the cost of that promise to a level several of them could not, and would not, pay. When the cost of keeping a promise exceeds its value, the promise breaks, and markets are extraordinarily good at calculating exactly when that crossover point is reached.

So when you read any pegged or managed currency, do not ask "is the peg holding right now?" — that tells you nothing, because pegs hold right up until the instant they don't. Ask instead the three questions 1992 hands you. First: *is the anchor in the same cycle as the pegged economy, or are they diverging?* Divergence is the fuse. Second: *what does defending the peg cost the domestic economy, and is that cost politically survivable?* When a peg forces a country to run policy that visibly wrecks its own economy — high rates into a recession, as in Britain and Italy — the defence has a shelf life. Third: *who pays for the gap, and for how long?* Reserves are finite, punitive overnight rates buy only days, and the strong anchor will never sacrifice its own mandate to help — so the pressure can only build until the weakest member's exchange rate is the thing that finally moves.

There is a fourth, more subtle thing 1992 teaches, and it is about *timing* rather than *direction*. The hardest part of trading a misaligned peg is not seeing that it is misaligned — that is often obvious for years before the break. The hard part is that pegs are designed to look stable right up until they shatter, so the price gives you no warning. A floating currency under stress drifts lower over weeks, giving you a continuous signal; a pegged currency under identical stress sits dead-flat at its floor, telling you nothing, and then gaps 15-20% in a single session. This means the *carry* of being early — the cost of holding a short while the peg refuses to break — can grind you down, which is exactly what punitive defensive rates are engineered to do. The skill is reading the *political* clock, not just the economic one: watching for the moment when the cost of defence crosses from "expensive but bearable" to "politically impossible," because that crossover, not the underlying misalignment, is what actually triggers the break. In 1992 that moment for Britain arrived the afternoon the 15% rate was announced and the market collectively realized no UK government could hold it. The misalignment had existed for two years; the *break* took an afternoon. Knowing the difference between those two clocks is the entire craft of trading currency crises.

The deepest point loops back to this series' spine. An exchange rate is the relative price of two monies, and that price wants to reflect the gap between the two economies' interest rates and the flows that gap attracts. A peg is an attempt to override that price with political will. It can work for years when the underlying forces are aligned — but the moment the forces diverge, holding the price fixed just dams up the pressure, and a dam holds until it doesn't. 1992 is the picture of that dam breaking across an entire continent in a single autumn. The currencies that broke were not weak because they were badly run; they broke because they were tied to an anchor pulling them the wrong way, and no amount of reserves, rate hikes, or political resolve can hold a price against the fundamentals forever. Learn to spot the diverging cycle behind a calm-looking peg, and you are reading currencies the way the people who profited from 1992 read them — not as a question of *whether* the promise breaks, but of *when*, and *who pays*.
