> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Every exchange-rate regime is a point on one line that runs from "let the market set the price" to "give up the price entirely," and each step toward the rigid end buys you stability by handing away your own interest-rate policy.
>
> - There is no binary "floating vs. fixed." There is a **spectrum**: free float → managed float → crawling peg → band → conventional peg → currency board → dollarization. Each is a different answer to the same question — how much of your exchange rate do you want to control, and what are you willing to pay for it?
> - The price of stability is **monetary autonomy**. A country that fixes its currency tightly cannot freely set its own interest rates while capital moves across its borders. That is the impossible trinity, and every regime is a way of picking which two of the three you keep.
> - The harder the commitment, the bigger the **reserve war chest** you need to back it — a currency board legally holds about \$1 of hard reserves for every unit it issues, and a band can cost tens of billions of dollars to defend at the edge.
> - The one number to remember: roughly **64 of ~195 economies** keep some kind of peg or crawl, about **45 manage their float**, and only **~31 truly let the market set the rate** — most of the world does *not* free-float.

On the night of September 16, 1992, the Bank of England raised its base interest rate from 10% to 12%, and then announced — in the same afternoon — that it would raise it again to 15%. Rates do not normally move in 5-percentage-point lurches inside a single working day. This was not monetary policy. It was a currency defense, and it was failing in real time. By the close, Britain had given up: the pound crashed out of Europe's exchange-rate system, and a hedge-fund manager named George Soros had reportedly made about \$1 billion betting that the defense could not hold.

What was Britain defending? Not its banks, not its bond market — its **exchange rate**. The pound was supposed to stay inside a fixed range against the German mark, and the market had decided the range was a lie. To understand why a central bank would torch its own economy with a 15% rate just to hold a number, you have to understand the thing it was holding: an **exchange-rate regime**. A regime is the set of rules a country picks for how its currency's price gets set — whether the market decides freely, whether the central bank steers it, or whether the country has welded its money to someone else's and thrown away the steering wheel.

This post maps the full spectrum of those choices, from the loosest to the tightest, with real countries living at each point. The thread that runs through all of it is the spine of this whole series: **an exchange rate is the relative price of two monies, and what a regime really decides is who gets to set that price — the market, the central bank, or nobody at all.** Every step toward "nobody at all" buys a quieter exchange rate at the cost of your own monetary independence.

## Foundations: How exchange-rate regimes work

Before we walk the spectrum, we need a few words defined from zero, because the whole subject is buried in jargon that assumes you already know it.

**An exchange rate** is just a price: how many units of one currency it takes to buy one unit of another. When we write **USD/VND = 25,450**, that means one US dollar costs 25,450 Vietnamese dong. The first currency (USD here) is the **base**; the second (VND) is the **quote**. The number tells you how much quote-currency you pay for one unit of base-currency. If the number goes *up*, the base currency (the dollar) got *stronger* and the quote currency (the dong) got *weaker* — it now takes more dong to buy the same dollar. If you want the full grammar of reading a quote, the sibling post on [base, quote and pips](/blog/trading/forex/base-quote-pips-and-how-to-read-an-fx-quote) lays it out; here we only need the direction.

**A regime** is the rulebook for how that price is allowed to move. At one extreme, the rulebook says "the central bank does nothing — supply and demand set the price every second." At the other, it says "the price is a constant by law and we will defend it with everything we have." Between those poles sit a half-dozen intermediate arrangements, each loosening or tightening the leash by a notch.

Three terms recur throughout, so pin them down now:

- **Reserves** (or **FX reserves**) are the central bank's stockpile of *other people's* money — mostly US dollars, held as Treasury bills and bank deposits, plus some euros, yen, and gold. Reserves are the ammunition a central bank uses to defend an exchange rate: to make its own currency stronger, it *sells dollars and buys its own currency*; to make it weaker, it *sells its own currency and buys dollars*. A country with no reserves cannot defend any promise about its exchange rate. The companion post on [how central banks intervene](/blog/trading/forex/how-central-banks-intervene-in-the-currency-market) goes deep on the mechanics; here, just hold the image of a war chest that gets spent down.
- **Devaluation / revaluation** is a deliberate, announced change in a *fixed* rate — the central bank moves the target itself. **Depreciation / appreciation** is a *market-driven* change in a *floating* rate. The words matter: a devaluation is a policy decision ("we are moving the peg from 25 to 28"), while a depreciation is something that just happens to a float.
- **Monetary autonomy** (or **monetary policy independence**) is the freedom to set your own interest rates to suit your own economy — to cut rates in a recession, or hike them to fight inflation — without worrying about what it does to your exchange rate. This is the thing you progressively give up as you move toward harder pegs, and it is the hidden cost on every price tag in this post.

With those three in hand, the entire spectrum becomes legible. Figure 1 above lays it out left to right: each box is a regime, and reading rightward, the central bank commits more and the market controls less. Now let's walk it, one stop at a time.

One fact frames the whole tour, and it surprises most people: **most of the world does not free-float.** The International Monetary Fund classifies every member economy's regime each year in a report called the AREAER (the Annual Report on Exchange Arrangements and Exchange Restrictions), and when you count the buckets, the rigid and intermediate regimes dominate. Roughly **24 economies** have a hard peg or no currency of their own (currency boards and dollarized economies), about **40** run a conventional fixed peg, around **55** run a stabilized or crawling arrangement, roughly **45** manage their float, and only about **31** truly let the market set the rate. Add the first four buckets and you get the headline: the *clean float is the exception, not the rule.* The belief most people carry — that currencies mostly float and pegs are a quaint exception — is exactly backwards.

![Where the world sits on the FX regime spectrum by number of economies](/imgs/blogs/pegs-bands-and-crawling-pegs-the-spectrum-of-fx-regimes-2.png)

Figure 2 makes the distribution concrete: the fattest bars are the stabilized/crawling and managed-float middle, not the free-float end. That shape is the whole subject in one chart — the world overwhelmingly chooses *some* control over its exchange rate, and the rest of this post is about what each of those choices costs. Now let's walk the spectrum, one stop at a time.

## The free float: let the market decide

At the loose end of the spectrum, the central bank simply does not target the exchange rate at all. The price of the currency is whatever the market clears it at, minute by minute, and the central bank spends its reserves on essentially nothing — they sit as a rainy-day fund, not a daily tool. The US dollar, the euro, the Japanese yen, the British pound, the Swiss franc, the Australian dollar — the major reserve currencies of the rich world — all free-float. Nobody at the Federal Reserve wakes up with a dollar level to defend.

What does a free-floater *get* for tolerating a wandering exchange rate? It gets **full monetary autonomy**. The Fed can slam rates to zero in 2020 and hike them to 5.5% in 2023 entirely according to what the US economy needs, and the exchange rate is free to absorb the consequences. The currency becomes a **shock absorber**: when a country's exports collapse, its currency weakens, which makes its remaining exports cheaper and helps the economy heal. The exchange rate does the adjusting so the domestic economy doesn't have to.

The price is **volatility**. A free-floating currency can move several percent in a week on nothing but a shift in interest-rate expectations. USD/JPY went from about 103 at the end of 2020 to about 157 by the end of 2024 — a roughly 52% move in the yen's price — driven overwhelmingly by the widening gap between US and Japanese interest rates, not by any government targeting a level. For a big, diversified, financially deep economy, that volatility is survivable. For a small, import-dependent economy whose companies borrow in dollars, that same volatility can be ruinous, which is exactly why most of the world does *not* free-float.

There's a subtler reason the rich, credible economies can afford to float: their currencies have **deep, liquid markets and trusted institutions**, so a falling currency does not turn into a panic. When the yen weakens, nobody fears the Bank of Japan will lose control or that Japanese inflation will spiral; the move is orderly because the institutions behind it are believed. A small emerging economy lacks that luxury. Its currency market is thin, a modest outflow can move the rate violently, and a falling currency can feed on itself — depreciation raises the local-currency cost of dollar debt, which spooks investors, who sell more, which deepens the depreciation. That self-reinforcing doom loop, which the rich floaters never face, is why free-floating is a privilege of credibility, not a default setting. A country earns the right to float by first building the institutions that make floating safe. The reason the dollar can be both the world's reserve currency and a free-floater is the subject of [the dollar's special role](/blog/trading/macro-trading/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy); for now, note that credibility and float go together.

#### Worked example: how a free float absorbs a shock

Suppose a small country exports \$10 billion of goods a year, all priced in dollars, and its currency floats freely at 100 units per dollar. A global recession cuts world demand and its exports fall to \$8 billion. With a floating currency, the drop in dollar inflows means fewer dollars are being sold for local currency, so the local currency weakens — say from 100 to 125 per dollar, a 20% depreciation. Now each \$1 of export revenue converts to 125 units of local currency instead of 100. An exporter earning \$8 billion brings home 8,000,000,000 × 125 = 1,000,000,000,000 local units — exactly the same 1 trillion local units it earned at \$10 billion when the rate was 100. **The float quietly cushioned a 20% revenue shock by letting the exchange rate, rather than wages and jobs, do the adjusting** — the price the country pays is that importers and dollar-borrowers got 20% poorer overnight.

That trade-off — the currency as shock absorber versus the currency as source of pain — is the choice every regime to the right of the free float is trying to soften.

## The managed float: a hand on the tiller

One notch tighter, the central bank still lets the market set the rate most of the time, but it **leans against** moves it dislikes. There is no published target and no promised band — just a willingness to step in, buy or sell reserves, and smooth the ride or nudge the trend. This is a **managed float** (sometimes a "dirty float," to distinguish it from the "clean" free float).

Most of the world's larger emerging economies live here: India, Indonesia, Brazil, much of Asia and Latin America. The central bank's intervention is discretionary and often deliberately opaque — it does not want speculators to know exactly where it will defend, because a known line is a line that gets tested. Japan, though formally a free-floater, behaves like a managed-floater at extremes: its Ministry of Finance sold roughly \$63 billion of reserves in September–October 2022 to slow the yen's slide past 151 to the dollar, and intervened again in 2024. A country can sit nominally at one point on the spectrum and act like its neighbor when pushed.

The managed float is the great pragmatic compromise. You keep most of your monetary autonomy — you can still move rates fairly freely — and you keep most of the float's shock-absorbing benefit, while shaving off the worst of the day-to-day noise and occasionally fighting a one-way move that you think has overshot fundamentals. The cost is that intervention is **expensive and not always credible**: if the market's view is right and yours is wrong, you can burn through reserves defending a level the fundamentals won't support, and end up with a weaker currency *and* a smaller war chest. We'll see that pattern repeat at every rigid regime to the right.

The managed float also reveals a deeper truth about the whole spectrum: the *line on a map is fuzzier than the labels suggest.* A country's official, declared regime (its **de jure** regime — what it tells the IMF it does) often differs from what it actually does day to day (its **de facto** regime — what its behavior reveals). Plenty of economies that declare a float in fact intervene so regularly that they behave like a soft peg, a phenomenon economists call **"fear of floating"**: they are nominally free but terrified of the volatility a true float would bring, so they quietly manage it. The reverse happens too — a declared peg that has been adjusted so often it's really a crawl. When you place a currency on the spectrum, watch what the central bank *does*, not what its official classification claims; the reserves it spends and the levels it defends tell the true story.

## The crawling peg: a fixed rate that moves on a schedule

Now we cross from "mostly market" into "mostly central bank." A **crawling peg** is a fixed rate that the central bank deliberately and predictably *slides* over time, usually in one direction. Instead of holding the rate constant — which a country with higher inflation than its anchor cannot sustain — the central bank lets it depreciate by a small, pre-announced (or at least pre-understood) amount, a steady crawl rather than sudden jumps.

Why crawl instead of hold flat? Because of **inflation differentials**. If your country runs 4% inflation a year and the United States runs 2%, your goods get relatively more expensive at a constant exchange rate, your exporters lose competitiveness, and pressure builds for a big one-off devaluation — the kind that triggers crises. A crawling peg releases that pressure continuously: the currency depreciates roughly in line with the inflation gap, so the *real* exchange rate (the inflation-adjusted one) stays roughly stable even as the *nominal* rate slides. It is the difference between letting steam out of a kettle slowly and waiting for it to explode. The mechanics of why a higher-inflation currency must weaken over time are the subject of the [purchasing power parity](/blog/trading/forex/purchasing-power-parity-and-the-real-exchange-rate) post; the crawl is PPP turned into policy.

Vietnam is a clean modern example, and we'll return to it in the real-markets section. The dong does not jump and it does not free-float — it **crawls**. Look at the year-end USD/VND levels and you see a remarkably orderly downward staircase rather than the jagged sawtooth of a floating pair.

![USD VND year-end levels showing a managed downward crawl](/imgs/blogs/pegs-bands-and-crawling-pegs-the-spectrum-of-fx-regimes-4.png)

That smoothness is the entire selling point. A crawling peg gives importers and exporters a predictable rate to plan around — they can budget next year's costs knowing the currency will weaken about 2–3%, not 20% — while preserving the safety valve that a hard peg lacks. The cost, again, is autonomy: to keep the crawl on its path, the central bank's interest-rate decisions become partly hostage to the exchange rate. If capital starts fleeing and the crawl threatens to accelerate into a rout, the central bank may have to hike rates to defend it, even if the domestic economy would prefer a cut.

#### Worked example: why a crawl beats a frozen rate

Take two countries that both fix at 100 units per dollar in year zero, and both run 5% domestic inflation versus 2% US inflation — a 3-point inflation gap each year. Country A *freezes* the rate at 100. Country B runs a **crawling peg** that depreciates 3% a year to match the inflation gap.

After five years, Country A's prices have risen by about 1.05⁵ ≈ 27.6%, while US prices rose 1.02⁵ ≈ 10.4%. Country A's goods are now roughly (1.276 / 1.104 − 1) ≈ **15.6% more expensive** in real terms than they were — its exporters are bleeding, and the market is betting on a devaluation it cannot avoid forever. Country B, meanwhile, has let its rate crawl to 100 × 1.03⁵ ≈ **115.9** units per dollar. Its nominal rate is weaker, but because the depreciation tracked the inflation gap, its *real* exchange rate is roughly unchanged and its exporters are still competitive. **Country A bought a stable-looking number and paid for it with a slow-motion crisis; Country B accepted a visibly sliding number and stayed sustainable.** The crawl is the regime that takes inflation seriously.

## The band: a fence around the rate

A **band** (or **target zone**, or **crawling band** if the center itself moves) puts explicit walls on the float. The central bank announces a **central rate** and a permitted range around it — say ±5% — and promises that the market rate may wander freely *inside* the range but will be defended at the edges. Hit the ceiling (the weakest permitted level for the home currency) and the central bank sells reserves to push back; hit the floor (the strongest permitted level) and it buys reserves to push back. Figure 3 above contrasts this with a free float: the float wanders anywhere, the band fences it between defended edges around a known center.

The band is a clever hybrid. Inside the range, the market does the work — the central bank can sit on its hands, conserve reserves, and let the rate breathe, which also lets it keep *some* monetary autonomy because it isn't constantly intervening. Only at the edges does the commitment bite. The band advertises a credible commitment ("we will defend these walls") while reserving day-to-day flexibility ("but we won't fight every small move"). Vietnam's regime since 2016 is exactly this: a daily central reference rate set by the State Bank, with the market allowed to trade ±5% around it.

![USD VND inside the plus or minus five percent band in 2024](/imgs/blogs/pegs-bands-and-crawling-pegs-the-spectrum-of-fx-regimes-7.png)

Figure 7 shows what that looks like with real 2024 numbers: a central rate near 24,340 dong per dollar, a ceiling near 25,557 (the weakest the dong is allowed to get), a floor near 23,123 (the strongest), and a market rate that spent the year pinned near the *ceiling* at about 25,450. That last detail is the tell. When the market lives at the weak edge of the band, it is telling you the band is **binding** — the dong "wants" to be weaker than the rules allow, and the central bank is using reserves and policy to hold the line. A band sitting at its ceiling is a band under pressure.

#### Worked example: defending a band edge with reserves

Suppose the band ceiling is 25,557 dong per dollar and the market keeps trying to push through it — importers and speculators all want to buy dollars at the cheapest dong price they can, driving the dong toward and past the wall. To hold the ceiling, the central bank must **supply the dollars** the market wants at exactly 25,557, soaking up the excess demand.

Say there's net demand for an extra \$2 billion of dollars this month that would otherwise breach the ceiling. The central bank sells \$2,000,000,000 from reserves at 25,557, receiving 2,000,000,000 × 25,557 = 51,114,000,000,000 dong, which it pulls out of circulation. Two things happen at once. First, reserves fall by \$2 billion — if the war chest was \$82 billion, it's now \$80 billion, and a few more months like this start to look dangerous. Second, draining 51 trillion dong out of the banking system **tightens domestic liquidity**, which pushes up local interest rates whether the central bank wanted that or not. **Defending the weak edge of a band simultaneously drains your reserves and tightens your monetary policy — which is the impossible trinity arriving on your doorstep in cash.** That second effect is why a band is not a free lunch: the act of defense quietly costs you the autonomy you were trying to keep.

That liquidity side-effect is the bridge to the next, harder regime — and to the law that governs the whole spectrum.

## The conventional peg: a fixed number, defended

Tighten the band to roughly zero width and you have a **conventional fixed peg**: the central bank commits to hold the rate at a single number (in practice within a paper-thin ±1% or ±2% margin) against an anchor currency, usually the US dollar or the euro. The Saudi riyal has been pegged near 3.75 per dollar since 1986. The Hong Kong dollar is pegged near 7.80 per dollar. The Danish krone is pegged tightly to the euro. A peg says: our money is, for practical purposes, just a re-denominated unit of the anchor.

A peg's appeal is **imported credibility**. A country with a poor inflation history can borrow the anchor central bank's discipline: if your currency is reliably worth 1/3.75 of a dollar, your inflation cannot drift far from US inflation for long, because the fixed rate forces your prices into line with the anchor's. Trade and investment get a stable unit of account; a Saudi exporter and a US importer both know the riyal won't lurch. For a small, open, oil-or-trade-driven economy, that stability can be worth more than the autonomy it costs.

And it does cost autonomy — almost all of it. Under a hard peg with open capital markets, your interest rates are effectively **set by the anchor**. If the Fed hikes, you must hike too, or capital will flood out of your currency chasing the higher dollar yield, and your peg will crack. Hong Kong's interest rates track the Fed's not because Hong Kong's economy needs them to, but because the peg demands it. When the anchor's monetary policy fits your economy, this is fine. When it doesn't — when the Fed is hiking into your recession — a peg can force you to run exactly the wrong policy for your own conditions. That mismatch is the recurring tragedy of fixed regimes, and it is no accident. It's a law.

There's also a choice hidden inside a peg: **what to anchor to.** Most pegs choose the US dollar, because the dollar dominates trade invoicing, commodity pricing, and global borrowing — pegging to it stabilizes the prices that matter most for a small open economy. But pegging to a single foreign currency means you import not just that anchor's monetary policy but its *own* swings against everyone else. When the dollar surged in 2022, every dollar-pegged currency was dragged up against the euro, the yen, and their own neighbors' currencies, hurting their competitiveness against non-dollar trade partners through no fault of their own. Some countries hedge this by pegging to a **basket** of currencies (a weighted blend of, say, the dollar, euro, and yen reflecting their trade pattern) rather than to one anchor, which smooths out any single anchor's idiosyncratic moves. The choice of anchor is itself a bet — a peg doesn't escape currency risk, it just *concentrates* it on the relationship between your anchor and the rest of the world.

A peg also changes who bears the risk inside the economy. Because a credible peg makes the exchange rate look like a sure thing, it quietly encourages local firms and banks to **borrow in the anchor currency** without hedging — why pay for protection against a move that "can't happen"? That unhedged foreign-currency debt is the dry tinder of every peg crisis: as long as the peg holds, the borrowers enjoy cheap dollar funding, but the day the peg breaks, their debt explodes in local-currency terms overnight. The peg's stability is, perversely, what builds the fragility that makes its eventual break so violent. We'll see exactly this fuel ignite in Asia in 1997.

### The law underneath everything: the impossible trinity

Every trade-off in this post is one face of a single iron constraint, the **impossible trinity** (or "trilemma"). It says a country can have at most **two** of these three things at once:

1. A **fixed exchange rate**,
2. **Free movement of capital** across its borders, and
3. **Independent monetary policy** (the freedom to set its own interest rates).

You can have any two, never all three. If you fix your rate *and* let capital flow freely, you lose control of your interest rates — they're dictated by the need to defend the peg (Hong Kong's choice). If you fix your rate *and* keep control of your interest rates, you must wall off capital flows so they can't arbitrage the gap (China for much of its history). If you keep both free capital and independent rates, your exchange rate must float (the US, Japan, the Eurozone). The full speculative-attack logic — *why* a fixed rate plus free capital plus a rate the market disbelieves is a one-way bet for speculators — is the subject of [the impossible trinity post](/blog/trading/forex/the-impossible-trinity-pick-two-of-three) and the game-theory treatment of [the central-bank credibility game](/blog/trading/game-theory/the-central-bank-game-credibility-commitment-and-dont-fight-the-fed). For our purposes, the trilemma is the map's legend: every regime on the spectrum is a different way of picking which corner to give up.

This is why the spectrum isn't really about exchange rates at all — it's about *what you're willing to surrender*. A free float keeps free capital and independent rates, sacrificing the fixed rate. A currency board keeps the fixed rate and free capital, sacrificing rates entirely. Read the whole spectrum through that lens and it snaps into focus.

## The currency board: a peg bound by law and backed dollar-for-dollar

Past the conventional peg lies a much harder commitment: the **currency board**. A conventional peg is a *promise* the central bank can break tomorrow if it loses its nerve. A currency board is a *law* that ties the central bank's hands so completely that there's almost nothing left to lose nerve about. Under a currency board, the monetary authority pledges to hold **enough hard-currency reserves to back the entire local money base** — roughly \$1 of dollars for every unit of local currency it has issued — and to exchange local for anchor at the fixed rate **on demand, without limit**.

The genius (and the cruelty) of the design is that it's **automatic**. The board does not "decide" monetary policy at all. When dollars flow into the economy, the board takes them in, adds them to reserves, and issues exactly the matching amount of local currency. When dollars flow out, locals hand back local currency, the board redeems it at the fixed rate, hands over the dollars, and the local money supply *shrinks by exactly that much*. The money base expands and contracts mechanically with the balance of payments. There is no discretion, no judgment call, no rate-setting committee deciding what the economy needs — and that is precisely the point. Hong Kong has run a currency board since 1983; Bulgaria adopted one in 1997 to end hyperinflation; Argentina ran one (a "convertibility" board) from 1991 to 2002.

![How a currency board backs every issued unit with reserves](/imgs/blogs/pegs-bands-and-crawling-pegs-the-spectrum-of-fx-regimes-5.png)

Figure 5 traces the loop: dollars come in, the board parks them in a 100%-reserve account, and issues local notes against them; to redeem, holders present notes, the board pays out dollars at the fixed rate, and the local base shrinks. Every local unit in circulation has a dollar standing behind it in the vault. That full backing is what makes the peg believable in a way a conventional peg never quite is: you cannot run a board out of reserves the way you can run a discretionary central bank dry, because the board never issues a unit it can't back.

#### Worked example: the dollar-for-dollar backing of a currency board

Take a stylized currency board that pegs at 8 local units per US dollar and has issued 80 billion local units of currency into circulation. By the board's rules, it must hold reserves of at least 80,000,000,000 ÷ 8 = **\$10 billion** — exactly enough to redeem every local unit at the fixed rate if every holder showed up at once. So the board sits on \$10 billion in reserves backing 80 billion local units, a clean 100% cover.

Now suppose \$2 billion of foreign investment arrives. The board takes the \$2 billion (reserves rise to \$12 billion) and, mechanically, issues 2,000,000,000 × 8 = **16 billion new local units** (money base rises to 96 billion). Cover stays exactly 100%: \$12 billion of reserves backing 96 billion units at 8 per dollar. Later, capital reverses and \$3 billion leaves: the board redeems 24 billion local units (3,000,000,000 × 8), reserves fall to \$9 billion, the money base falls to 72 billion units, and cover is *still* exactly 100%. **The board never has to choose between defending the peg and managing the economy, because it has surrendered the choice entirely — the money supply is whatever the balance of payments dictates, and the peg holds by construction.** The cost is total: a currency board has zero monetary autonomy, and when capital floods out, the automatic contraction of the money supply can drive the domestic economy into a brutal deflationary recession with no central bank able to cut rates to cushion it.

That last sentence is Argentina's epitaph. Its currency board held the peso at 1-to-1 with the dollar for a decade and tamed hyperinflation — but when the dollar strengthened and capital fled in 2001, the board's automatic money-supply squeeze, with no escape valve, helped drive unemployment over 20% and the country into default. In 2002 Argentina abandoned the board, and the peso collapsed from 1 to nearly 4 per dollar. A currency board is the most credible peg there is, right up until the moment the credibility costs more than the country can bear.

## Dollarization: throwing away the steering wheel

At the far rigid end of the spectrum, a country stops pretending to have its own currency at all and simply **adopts a foreign one as legal tender**. This is **dollarization** (when the adopted currency is the US dollar) or, more generally, **full currency substitution**. Ecuador dollarized in 2000, El Salvador and Panama use the dollar, Montenegro and Kosovo use the euro, Zimbabwe dollarized after its own currency hyperinflated into oblivion.

Dollarization is the ultimate commitment because there is **nothing left to attack**. There is no exchange rate to defend, because there is no local currency to defend it against — a dollar in Quito is the same dollar as in Miami, with no rate between them. Speculators cannot break a peg that doesn't exist. Inflation falls to roughly the anchor's level, because the country is literally using the anchor's money. Cross-border trade and borrowing get radically simpler. For a country whose own monetary institutions have failed catastrophically — Ecuador after a banking collapse, Zimbabwe after hyperinflation — dollarization can be a credible fresh start that no domestically-controlled regime could offer.

The cost is everything the trilemma says it must be, taken to the limit. A dollarized country has **zero monetary autonomy**: its interest rates are the Fed's, full stop, and it cannot cut them in a recession or print money to fight one. It loses **seigniorage** — the tidy profit a government earns from issuing its own currency, which now flows to the Fed instead. It has no exchange rate to absorb shocks, so when a downturn hits, the entire adjustment must fall on wages, prices, and jobs — an "internal devaluation" that is far more painful than a currency depreciation. And it has no **lender of last resort** in its own money: if its banks face a run, no domestic central bank can conjure dollars to save them. Dollarization buys the deepest stability on the spectrum and pays for it by surrendering the last shred of independence. It is the regime you choose when you trust yourself *less* than you trust the Fed.

## The whole spectrum as one trade-off

Step back and the pattern is unmistakable. Every regime is buying exchange-rate stability with monetary autonomy, at an exchange rate of its own. Figure 6 lays the five archetypes against the three things that matter — how stable the FX rate is, how much rate autonomy you keep, and how big a reserve war chest you need.

![The stability versus autonomy trade-off across FX regimes](/imgs/blogs/pegs-bands-and-crawling-pegs-the-spectrum-of-fx-regimes-6.png)

Read it left to right and the story is monotone. A free float gives you full rate autonomy and demands no reserves to defend — but tolerates the wildest FX swings. A crawling peg trades some autonomy for a calmer, predictable slide. A conventional peg buys high stability but hands most of your interest-rate policy to the anchor and demands a large reserve war chest. A currency board pushes stability to "very high" and autonomy to "none," backed by a legally-mandated 100% reserve. Dollarization completes the journey: total FX stability, zero autonomy, no reserves needed because there's no currency to back. **The further right you go, the more shocks the regime absorbs for you — and the fewer tools you keep to absorb them yourself.**

There is no "best" column. The right choice depends on what a country fears most. A large, diversified, credible economy fears losing control of its own interest rates more than it fears a volatile currency, so it floats. A small, open economy with a history of high inflation fears its own undisciplined monetary policy more than it fears importing the Fed's, so it pegs or dollarizes. The spectrum isn't a ranking; it's a menu of fears.

#### Worked example: the seigniorage a dollarizing country gives up

Seigniorage is the profit a government makes from issuing money: it prints a note that costs a few cents to produce and spends it as if it were worth its full face value, and it earns interest on the reserves backing the currency in circulation. A country that dollarizes hands that profit to the Fed.

Suppose a small economy has \$5 billion worth of its own currency in circulation, and the prevailing dollar interest rate is 4%. By holding its own currency, the public is effectively giving the government an interest-free loan of \$5 billion; the government can hold \$5 billion of interest-bearing dollar reserves against it and pocket 0.04 × 5,000,000,000 = **\$200 million a year** in seigniorage. Dollarize, and that \$5 billion of local notes gets swapped for actual dollars the country must *buy* — and the \$200 million annual interest now accrues to whoever issued those dollars, the United States. **Over a decade, this small open economy quietly transfers roughly \$2 billion of seigniorage to the Fed as the price of borrowing the dollar's credibility — a real, recurring cost that never shows up in the exchange rate but is exactly what "giving up your currency" means.** Stability is never free; dollarization just moves the bill off the FX screen and onto the public accounts.

## Common misconceptions

**"Floating means the government does nothing and fixed means it does everything."** The reality is a smooth gradient, not two boxes. A "free-floating" Japan sold \$63 billion of reserves to slow the yen in 2022. A "fixed" Hong Kong lets its rate drift within a narrow 7.75–7.85 convertibility zone and only intervenes at the edges. Almost every real-world regime is a managed float or a band with some give in it — pure clean floats and zero-tolerance pegs are the rare extremes, not the norm. About **45 economies** run an explicit managed float and another **55 or so** run a stabilized or crawling arrangement; the messy middle is where most of the world actually lives.

**"A peg is permanent and safe."** A peg is exactly as safe as the reserves and the political will behind it, and not one day longer. Britain's ERM commitment looked solid until September 16, 1992, when it broke in an afternoon. Thailand's baht peg looked solid until July 2, 1997, when it broke and the baht fell from 25 to 56 per dollar, taking half of Asia down with it. Switzerland's EUR/CHF 1.20 floor looked solid until 9:30 a.m. on January 15, 2015, when the franc rocketed roughly 30% in minutes. A peg is a promise, and promises about prices are made to be tested. The "safe" feeling a peg gives is itself the danger: it lulls borrowers into taking on unhedged foreign-currency debt, which makes the eventual break catastrophic.

**"More reserves always mean a safer peg."** Reserves help, but they're finite and the market is not. The Swiss National Bank had effectively *unlimited* franc-printing power to defend its floor — it could create as many francs as it wanted to sell — and it *still* gave up, because the balance-sheet and inflation cost of defending grew faster than the benefit. The SNB's sight deposits (a proxy for the francs it had to create to hold the floor) ballooned as the defense dragged on.

![SNB sight deposits ballooning while defending the franc floor](/imgs/blogs/pegs-bands-and-crawling-pegs-the-spectrum-of-fx-regimes-8.png)

Figure 8 shows that war chest swelling from roughly CHF 100 billion in 2011 to about CHF 400 billion by the time the floor was abandoned in January 2015 — and the lesson is that a central bank can lose its nerve even when it can't literally run out of ammunition, because the *cost* of firing it keeps rising. Defending the *strong* side of a peg (printing your own currency) is theoretically unlimited but practically painful; defending the *weak* side (selling finite reserves) is hard-limited by the war chest. Either way, "more reserves" buys time, not immortality. The full anatomy of that break is in [the SNB 2015 peg-break post](/blog/trading/forex/the-snb-2015-peg-break-when-a-central-bank-blinks).

**"A crawling peg is just a slow devaluation, so it's a sign of weakness."** A crawl is a sign of *discipline*, not weakness. Freezing a rate while you run higher inflation than your anchor is the actual weakness — it builds a one-way bet that ends in a crisis. A well-run crawl that tracks the inflation gap keeps the real exchange rate stable and avoids the crisis entirely. The orderly dong staircase in Figure 4 is not Vietnam losing control; it's Vietnam *keeping* control by refusing to pretend the dong can hold a constant number against the dollar while running a positive inflation gap.

**"Dollarization permanently kills inflation."** It anchors inflation to the *anchor's* inflation — which is great when the anchor is the disciplined Fed, but it offers no protection if the anchor itself inflates. A dollarized economy in a high-US-inflation decade imports that inflation with no defense. And it gives up the exchange rate as a shock absorber, so the *next* recession is felt entirely in jobs and wages. Dollarization trades one kind of pain (your own bad monetary policy) for another (no monetary policy at all).

**"A pegged currency is risk-free."** A peg doesn't *remove* exchange-rate risk; it *reshapes* it. A float spreads the risk out into many small daily moves you can see and hedge. A peg replaces those with a single, low-probability, high-severity event: a discrete devaluation. Thailand's baht looked risk-free at 25 per dollar right up to July 1997, when the peg snapped and the rate jumped to 56 — a roughly 124% loss against the dollar overnight for anyone holding baht assets. A trader holding \$1,000,000 of baht-denominated bonds at 25 saw their dollar value fall toward \$1,000,000 × (25 / 56) ≈ **\$446,000** as the peg broke — a \$554,000 hit that arrived not gradually but in a single discontinuous lurch. A peg converts continuous, hedge-able risk into a fat-tailed jump, which is *harder* to manage, not easier. "Stable until it isn't" is the actual risk profile.

**"A float means the central bank stays out of the market."** Almost no real-world "float" is clean. Of the roughly 76 economies that the IMF classifies as floating or free-floating, only about **31** are judged genuinely free — the other ~45 are *managed* floats whose central banks intervene regularly. Even card-carrying free-floaters step in at extremes: Japan sold roughly \$63 billion of reserves in late 2022 to slow the yen. The honest mental model is not "float = hands off, peg = hands on" but a dial of intervention frequency that almost never sits at exactly zero. Reading a currency means asking *how often and how hard* its central bank leans, not whether the label says "float."

## How it shows up in real markets

The spectrum stops being abstract the moment you put real countries on it. Five cases, spread across the regimes, show the trade-offs in the wild.

### Hong Kong: the currency board that won't blink

Hong Kong has run a currency board since 1983, pegging the Hong Kong dollar near 7.80 to the US dollar (with a convertibility zone of 7.75–7.85). The Hong Kong Monetary Authority holds hard reserves comfortably exceeding the entire monetary base — full backing and then some — and the peg has survived the 1997 Asian crisis, the 1998 speculative assault, the 2008 global financial crisis, and the 2022 Fed hiking cycle without breaking. When the Fed hiked aggressively in 2022–2023, Hong Kong's rates rose right alongside, *not* because Hong Kong's economy wanted tighter policy, but because the board demands it: capital flowed toward the higher US yield, the HKD drifted to the weak 7.85 edge, the HKMA bought HKD (selling USD) to defend, that drained HKD liquidity, and local rates rose to match the dollar. That is the impossible trinity operating exactly as written — Hong Kong keeps its fixed rate and free capital, and pays with zero monetary autonomy. The peg's survival across four decades of crises is the strongest real-world evidence that a *fully-backed* board is a genuinely different, harder commitment than a discretionary peg.

### Saudi Arabia: a petro-economy's forty-year dollar peg

The Saudi riyal has been welded to the dollar at **3.75 SAR/USD since 1986**, one of the longest-running conventional pegs on earth, and the reason is written in barrels of oil. Saudi Arabia's overwhelming export, crude oil, is invoiced and settled in *dollars* on world markets. When the thing that earns your whole economy its income is priced in dollars, pegging your currency to the dollar stabilizes the part of the economy that matters most: a barrel that sells for \$80 always converts to exactly 80 × 3.75 = 300 riyals of government revenue, with no exchange-rate guesswork between the sale and the budget. The peg turns a dollar oil price directly into a known riyal income, which is precisely what a state that runs on oil revenue wants.

The price, as always, is autonomy. The trilemma forces the Saudi Arabian Monetary Authority (SAMA) to **import US monetary policy**: with a fixed rate and open capital flows, riyal interest rates must track the Fed's, or capital arbitrages the gap and the peg cracks. When the Fed hiked through 2022–2023, SAMA hiked in lockstep — not because the Saudi economy was overheating, but because the peg demanded it. That is the impossible trinity again: Saudi Arabia keeps a fixed rate and free capital, and pays with its own interest-rate policy.

The peg has been *tested* but never broken. The 2014–2016 oil crash sent crude from over \$100 to under \$30, slashing the dollar revenue backing the riyal; the offshore forward market began pricing a real chance of devaluation, with twelve-month forwards implying the riyal might be cut. SAMA crushed the bet the way only a vast war chest can — it sat on hundreds of billions of dollars in reserves (over \$700 billion at the 2014 peak) and warned banks off speculative forward positions. The forwards subsided and the peg held.

#### Worked example: the cost of importing a Fed hike Saudi Arabia doesn't need

Suppose the Fed hikes its policy rate from 2% to 5% to fight US inflation, while Saudi Arabia's own economy is soft and would prefer cheap money. To hold the peg, SAMA must raise riyal rates from roughly 2% to roughly 5% too. Now take a Saudi firm carrying 1,000,000,000 riyals (about \$266,667,000 at 3.75) of floating-rate debt. Its annual interest bill jumps from 0.02 × 1,000,000,000 = **20,000,000 riyals** to 0.05 × 1,000,000,000 = **50,000,000 riyals** — an extra **30,000,000 riyals a year** (about \$8,000,000) in financing cost it never asked for, imposed purely to defend the exchange rate. Multiply that across every borrower in the economy and you can see the bill: the peg's stability is bought by forcing the *whole* domestic economy to run US interest rates even in a year it would rather cut. That is the autonomy a conventional peg surrenders, paid in cash by every Saudi borrower.

### Vietnam: the textbook crawling band

Vietnam runs the messy-middle regime that most of the developing world actually uses: a **crawling band**. Since 2016 the State Bank of Vietnam sets a daily central reference rate for USD/VND and allows the market to trade within ±5% of it. The central rate itself crawls weaker over time, roughly tracking the inflation and rate gap with the United States, which is why Figure 4's USD/VND line is an orderly downward staircase — from about 21,340 at end-2014 to about 25,450 at end-2024 — rather than the sawtooth of a float. In 2024 the dong spent most of the year pinned near the *weak* (ceiling) edge of its band as a strong dollar and a negative rate differential pulled it down; the SBV leaned against the move with reserves and policy, and its reserves fell to around \$82 billion — below the comfortable three-months-of-imports rule of thumb — which is the visible cost of holding the band. Vietnam's regime is the spectrum's compromise made concrete: enough flexibility to crawl with the inflation gap, enough commitment to anchor expectations, and a reserve bill that comes due when the dollar is strong. The full mechanics of how the SBV runs the dong are in [the USD/VND managed-float post](/blog/trading/forex/usd-vnd-and-the-managed-float-how-the-sbv-runs-the-dong), and the policy backdrop in [Vietnam's monetary policy and credit ceiling](/blog/trading/finance/vietnam-monetary-policy-state-bank-dong-credit-ceiling).

### Ecuador: dollarization as a last resort

Ecuador abolished its own currency, the sucre, in 2000 after a banking collapse and a bout of hyperinflation had destroyed all faith in domestic monetary institutions. It adopted the US dollar outright. The results were exactly what the spectrum predicts: inflation, which had run into triple digits, collapsed toward US levels within a couple of years, and the country got a stable, credible unit of account it could never have produced on its own. But Ecuador also surrendered everything the rigid end demands. It cannot set its own interest rates — they are the Fed's. It cannot devalue to cushion a shock, so when oil prices (its main export) crash, the entire adjustment falls on the domestic economy through falling wages and spending rather than a falling currency. It has no domestic lender of last resort in dollars. And it hands its seigniorage to the United States. Ecuador's experience is the clean test case for the dollarization trade: it bought the deepest stability on the menu by giving up the last shred of monetary sovereignty, and more than two decades later it is still living with both sides of that bargain.

### Denmark: the peg that quietly holds

For a peg that just *works*, look at Denmark. The krone has been pegged to the euro (and the Deutsche mark before it) for decades, kept within a tight ±2.25% band that in practice Denmark holds far tighter, near a central rate around 7.46 krone per euro. Denmark is a member of the EU's ERM II mechanism but, crucially, *not* of the eurozone — it kept its own currency and then bolted it to the euro. The peg holds because Denmark made the trilemma choice cleanly and credibly: it gave up monetary autonomy (the Danish central bank essentially imports the ECB's policy, moving its rates to defend the peg) in exchange for rock-solid exchange-rate stability with its dominant trading partner. When the euro weakened or strengthened, Denmark followed; when speculators tested the peg in early 2015 after the SNB break, Denmark cut rates deeply *negative* and intervened heavily, and the peg held. Denmark is the example that pegs *can* be durable — but only for a country whose economy is so integrated with its anchor that importing the anchor's monetary policy is no great sacrifice. The peg is sustainable precisely because giving up autonomy barely costs Denmark anything; its economic cycle already moves with the eurozone's.

Put the five side by side and the spectrum's logic is complete. Hong Kong and Ecuador chose maximum commitment because they trusted a rule (or the Fed) more than their own discretion. Saudi Arabia chose a hard conventional peg because its dollar-priced oil revenue makes a dollar anchor the natural one, and its reserves make it defensible. Vietnam chose the flexible middle because it wanted both some stability and some room to crawl. Denmark chose a hard peg it can sustain because its anchor's policy fits it anyway. Each picked a different point on the same line, for the same underlying reason: a judgment about which they feared more — a volatile currency, or their own loss of monetary control.

## The takeaway: read the regime before you read the rate

Here is what the spectrum changes about how you read a currency. The first question to ask about any currency is never "is it going up or down?" — it's **"what regime is it under, and is that regime under stress?"** Because the regime tells you what the price *can* do, and that bounds everything else.

If a currency free-floats, its price is information: every move is the market repricing the two countries' relative interest rates and capital flows in real time, and you read it like any other freely-set price — through the [interest-rate differential](/blog/trading/forex/interest-rate-differentials-the-master-variable-of-fx) that this series treats as the master variable. If a currency is pegged or banded, the *quoted* price is half the story; the real signal is in the **pressure on the regime** — where the rate sits inside its band (at the ceiling means weak-side pressure), how fast reserves are draining, what the offshore forward market is pricing for a future devaluation. A pegged currency trading calmly at its peg tells you nothing; a pegged currency pinned at its band edge while reserves bleed tells you a break is being priced.

And the deepest read is this: a regime is a **promise about the future price of money**, and like any promise it has a credibility that the market is constantly testing. The whole spectrum, from float to dollarization, is a ranking of how hard those promises are to break — a float promises nothing and can't be broken, a dollarized economy has made the promise unbreakable by destroying the thing that could break it, and everything in between is a promise of intermediate strength that the market will probe for exactly as long as it suspects the central bank's will or war chest might run out. The pound in 1992, the baht in 1997, the franc in 2015: each was a promise the market decided was breakable, and each break made someone rich and someone else poor.

So when you look at an exchange rate, look *through* it to the regime. Ask what was promised, who is defending it, with how much ammunition, and against how strong a tide. The number on the screen is just the relative price of two monies — but the regime is the set of rules deciding whether that number is allowed to tell the truth. Reading the regime is reading whether the price is free, managed, or fighting for its life. That is the difference between seeing a currency and understanding one.

And that brings the whole spectrum back to the single question this series keeps circling: a regime is just a country's standing answer to *"how much of my monetary autonomy will I trade for exchange-rate stability?"* The free-floater answers "none — I keep all my autonomy and live with a wandering rate." The dollarizer answers "all of it — take my currency, I want your stability more than my own steering wheel." Every peg, crawl, and band in between is a partial answer, a country deciding how much rate-setting freedom it will surrender for how quiet an exchange rate. There is no right answer, only a fit between a country's fears and its institutions — and once you can read that trade-off off the regime, you are no longer looking at a price. You are looking at a choice.
