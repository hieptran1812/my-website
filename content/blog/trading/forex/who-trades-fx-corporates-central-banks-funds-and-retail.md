> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The FX market is roughly \$7.5 trillion a day, and almost none of it is someone "betting the dollar goes up." It is dominated by people who are *forced* to be there — exporters, importers, fund managers rebalancing, central banks managing reserves — and the speculators sit on the other side of those flows.
>
> - **Hedgers and real-money managers move the most cash** and trade for reasons that have nothing to do with predicting the rate. They are price-insensitive: they need the dollars by Friday regardless.
> - **Central banks are the only player who can print one side of the trade** — they manage reserves, peg currencies, and intervene; you do not want to be short a currency its own central bank is buying.
> - **Macro funds and CTAs are the discretionary speculators** — they take the rate-differential and trend bets, and they are the ones whose *unwinds* (August 2024) make the front page.
> - **The one number to remember:** retail is roughly **2-3% of daily volume**, trades the same instruments at a structural cost disadvantage, and is statistically the marginal loser on the other side of better-informed flow.

On the morning of August 5, 2024, the most popular trade on earth blew up. For years, professional money had been borrowing Japanese yen at almost zero and parking it in higher-yielding currencies and assets — the "carry trade." Then the Bank of Japan nudged rates up, U.S. data softened, and the yen ripped from about 162 per dollar to under 142 in a few sessions. The Nikkei fell 12% in a single day. The VIX spiked to 65. Headlines said "traders panic." But which traders?

That is the question this entire post is about. Because "the FX market" is not a homogeneous crowd of speculators staring at charts. It is a stack of very different players with very different *reasons* for being there — a Japanese exporter who must convert dollar revenue into yen to pay workers, a sovereign wealth fund reallocating reserves, a pension fund hedging its foreign bonds, a global-macro hedge fund pressing a directional view, a trend-following CTA whose models just flipped short, a dealer bank quoting two-way prices, and — at the very far end — a retail trader with a 100:1 leveraged account. Each one trades for a different motive, at a different size, against a different counterparty.

The single most useful habit you can build as a currency trader is to stop asking "where is EUR/USD going?" and start asking **"who is on the other side of this trade, and why are they doing it?"** When you buy euros, someone is selling them to you. If that someone is a Swiss exporter mechanically converting a receivable, you have learned almost nothing about the future. If that someone is a central bank defending a level, you have learned a great deal. This post is a field guide to every player in the FX market — their motive, their size, and who they trade against — so you can answer that question in real time.

![The FX player motive map placing corporates, real-money managers, central banks, macro funds, CTAs, dealers, and retail on a grid of motive versus size](/imgs/blogs/who-trades-fx-corporates-central-banks-funds-and-retail-1.png)

The map above is the whole post in one picture, and we will spend the rest of it walking the grid box by box. Two axes organize everything: **why** a player trades (left: they are *forced* to, by a business need; right: they *choose* to, to make money) and **how big** they are (bottom: small; top: enormous). The forced players in the top half — corporates, reserve managers, real-money funds — supply the flow. The speculators on the right — macro funds and CTAs — try to anticipate and ride it. The dealers sit in the middle, intermediating everyone. And retail sits alone in the bottom corner: it *chooses* to trade, like a speculator, but it is tiny, like nobody else who matters.

## Foundations: The cast of the FX market

Before we meet the players, we need the same vocabulary the rest of the series uses. An **exchange rate** is the relative price of two monies: EUR/USD = 1.08 means one euro costs 1.08 U.S. dollars. The first currency (EUR) is the **base**; the second (USD) is the **quote**. You never own "a currency" in isolation — every position is a *pair*, a relative bet that one money will buy more of the other. A **pip** is the smallest standard price increment, the fourth decimal for most pairs (0.0001) and the second decimal for yen pairs (0.01). When EUR/USD moves from 1.0800 to 1.0801, that is one pip.

The market itself is gigantic and lopsided. The Bank for International Settlements runs a survey every three years; the 2022 edition put **average daily turnover at about \$7.5 trillion**. To put that in scale: the entire annual GDP of the United States, roughly \$28 trillion, trades through the FX market in about four days. But that headline hides the first surprise of the market's structure — most of it is not the "buy euros, sell dollars" spot trade a beginner pictures.

![FX turnover by instrument in 2022 showing FX swaps dominate over spot, forwards, currency swaps, and options](/imgs/blogs/who-trades-fx-corporates-central-banks-funds-and-retail-2.png)

The chart breaks the \$7.5 trillion into instruments. **FX swaps** — short-term funding trades where two parties exchange currencies now and reverse it later — are the single biggest bucket at about \$3.8 trillion a day. Plain **spot** (immediate exchange at today's rate) is only about \$2.1 trillion. Outright **forwards** (locking a rate for a future date) add \$1.2 trillion, currency swaps and FX options the rest. The reason this matters for "who trades" is that swaps and forwards are overwhelmingly the tools of *funding and hedging*, not speculation. A bank rolling its dollar funding overnight, or a corporate locking next quarter's euro receipts, lives in the swap-and-forward world. The pure directional bet — "I think the dollar rises" — is a slice of the spot column, and a smaller slice than newcomers assume.

One more structural fact frames everyone: the **U.S. dollar is on one side of roughly 88% of all trades**. Because shares are counted on both sides (each trade involves two currencies, so the percentages sum to 200%), the dollar's 88% means it is the near-universal *unit of account* of the market. Most currencies do not trade directly against each other; they trade through the dollar. If a Thai company needs Brazilian reais, the path is usually baht → dollars → reais. This makes the dollar the gravitational center, a theme the cross-asset series develops in [the dollar as cross-asset gravity](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-dollar-cross-asset-gravity). For our purposes, it means almost everyone we meet below is, at some moment, trading *against the dollar* — and the player on the other side of a dollar trade is frequently a dealer warehousing the position until the next flow comes through.

It is worth pausing on one distinction that organizes the entire cast, because it is more useful than the usual "bank versus fund" labels. Divide every participant into two camps by their *reason* for trading. The first camp trades because a flow of money *forces* them to — an exporter who got paid in dollars, a pension fund that bought a foreign bond, a reserve manager who took in another quarter of trade-surplus dollars. They did not choose the currency exposure; it arrived attached to something else, and they are in the market to manage it. Call this **forced flow**. The second camp trades because it *wants* the currency exposure — a macro fund that thinks the euro will fall, a CTA whose trend model just turned long, a retail trader who likes the chart. Call this **chosen flow**, or speculation. The genius of the market is that the two camps need each other exactly: the forced camp is desperate to *shed* exposure it never wanted, and the chosen camp is eager to *take on* exposure it can't otherwise get. Every trade in the book is, at bottom, a transfer of unwanted risk from someone forced into the market to someone who chose to be there — with a dealer collecting a toll in the middle. Hold that frame and the rest of the cast falls into place.

With base/quote, pip, the \$7.5 trillion, the swap-heavy mix, the dollar's dominance, and the forced-versus-chosen split in hand, we can meet the cast. We will take them roughly in order of how much they move the market, not how famous they are — and fame turns out to be almost inversely related to size.

## Corporates and hedgers: the price-insensitive whale

Start with the player nobody romanticizes and almost everybody underestimates: the corporate hedger. A German carmaker sells vehicles in the United States and gets paid in dollars, but pays its workers, suppliers, and taxes in euros. A Japanese electronics firm books revenue in dollars and euros but reports in yen. An American importer owes a Chinese supplier in renminbi. Every one of these companies has a **currency mismatch** between where its money comes in and where it goes out, and that mismatch is a risk it did not ask for and does not want.

The corporate's motive is not profit from FX — it is *removing* FX from the picture so the business can be judged on the business. This makes corporates the most **price-insensitive** players in the market. A speculator who thinks EUR/USD is expensive will wait for a better level. A treasurer who has a €50,000,000 payable due in ninety days will hedge it now, at whatever the forward rate is, because the alternative — gambling the company's quarterly earnings on a currency move — is not the treasurer's job and could be a firing offense. They trade on the *calendar*, not on the *level*.

![The hedger versus speculator handoff pipeline showing a corporate passing currency risk to a dealer who passes it to a speculator](/imgs/blogs/who-trades-fx-corporates-central-banks-funds-and-retail-3.png)

That price-insensitivity is exactly why the speculator wants to be on the other side. The pipeline above shows the handoff: the corporate has unwanted risk, it pays a dealer to take that risk off its hands, and the dealer lays it off — sometimes immediately, sometimes after warehousing it — to someone who *wants* the directional exposure, which is the speculator. The hedger is happy to pay a small, certain cost (the spread, the forward points) to eliminate a large, uncertain risk. The speculator is happy to accept a large, uncertain risk in exchange for the chance at profit. They are not enemies; they are counterparties whose preferences fit together like two puzzle pieces. This is the foundational trade of the whole market, and it is why the carry trade exists at all — see [the carry trade, getting paid to hold a currency](/blog/trading/forex/the-carry-trade-getting-paid-to-hold-a-currency).

#### Worked example: an exporter hedging a €10,000,000 receivable

A German machine-tool maker ships equipment to a U.S. buyer and will receive \$10,800,000 in ninety days. At today's spot of EUR/USD 1.0800, that is exactly €10,000,000 — the number in its budget. But if the euro strengthens to 1.1500 over those ninety days, those same dollars convert to only \$10,800,000 ÷ 1.1500 = €9,391,304 — a **€608,696 shortfall** purely from the currency. To kill that risk, the treasurer sells \$10,800,000 forward for euros at the 90-day forward rate. Suppose U.S. rates are higher than euro rates, so the forward is 1.0700 (the dollar trades at a forward discount). Locking that in, the company is guaranteed \$10,800,000 ÷ 1.0700 = **€10,093,458** in ninety days, no matter where spot goes.

The takeaway: the treasurer did not predict the euro — they removed it from the income statement, paying with certainty for the right to ignore it. That is the whole psychology of the largest, most consistent flow in the market.

Notice what the corporate is *not* doing: it is not watching a chart, not setting a stop-loss, not caring whether 1.0800 is "cheap." That indifference makes corporate flow lumpy and predictable in aggregate but useless as a forecasting signal. When a model exporter nation runs a huge trade surplus — Japan for decades, China today — its corporates are *structural sellers of dollars and buyers of home currency*, a persistent updraft beneath the exchange rate that has nothing to do with anyone's view. Reading that structural flow is far more useful than guessing the next candle.

There is a subtler corporate behavior worth knowing, because it occasionally inverts the picture: hedging is itself a *choice* about how much and how far forward. Some treasuries hedge 100% of a known receivable; others hedge 50% and leave the rest open; others run rolling programs that hedge the next twelve months in layered tranches. When the currency moves sharply, corporates that were *under*-hedged can be forced to catch up — a Japanese exporter that left dollar revenue unhedged as the yen weakened in 2022 eventually had to sell those accumulated dollars, adding to yen-buying flow at exactly the wrong moment for the speculators short yen. So corporate flow is mostly a slow, calendar-driven tide, but it has a reflexive tail: extreme moves trigger catch-up hedging that amplifies the move. The aggregate of all corporate hedging decisions in a surplus country is one of the great invisible forces in FX, and it is invisible precisely because no corporate treasurer ever appears on financial television to explain it. They are not trying to be right about the currency. They are trying to make the currency *not matter*, and in doing so they create the deepest, steadiest flow in the market.

## Central banks and reserve managers: the player who prints one side

If corporates are the whale, central banks are the player who can change the rules of the ocean. A central bank is unique in the entire market for one reason: it can **create its own currency at will**. The Federal Reserve can produce dollars; the Bank of Japan can produce yen. No hedge fund, no matter how large, can manufacture the thing it trades. This asymmetry is the single most important fact about central banks as market participants, and it has a blunt practical lesson: *do not stand in front of a central bank that is buying its own currency with reserves it controls, and think very carefully before standing in front of one that is selling its own currency, because it has an infinite supply.*

Central banks wear three hats in FX, and they are worth separating. First, as **reserve managers**, they hold the nation's foreign-exchange savings — the dollars, euros, yen, and gold a country accumulates from trade surpluses and capital inflows. These reserves are vast and managed conservatively, and how they are allocated is itself a market force. Second, as **interest-rate setters**, they move the rate differential that, per this series' spine, is the deepest driver of every pair — but that mechanism belongs to the macro and fixed-income series ([how monetary policy moves currencies](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-monetary-policy-moves-currencies-rate-differentials)), so we link out rather than re-derive it. Third, as **interventionists**, they directly buy or sell currency to push a rate where they want it.

![Global reserve currency mix in 2024 showing the dollar at 58 percent followed by the euro, yen, pound, and renminbi](/imgs/blogs/who-trades-fx-corporates-central-banks-funds-and-retail-4.png)

The reserve mix above shows where the world's official savings sit: about 58% in dollars, 20% in euros, with the yen, pound, and renminbi splitting most of the rest. When a reserve manager shifts even a few percent of a multi-trillion-dollar portfolio from one currency to another, that is a flow no private fund can match. These managers, like corporates, are largely *not* trying to make a directional profit — they are managing liquidity, safety, and the ability to defend their own currency in a crisis. They are price-insensitive in a different way than corporates: they trade on policy and prudence, on a horizon measured in years, and they will happily buy a currency that is "overvalued" if it is the currency they need to hold reserves in.

The third hat — **intervention** — is where a central bank becomes the most dangerous counterparty you can face. When the Bank of Japan decides the yen is too weak, it sells dollars from its reserves and buys yen, deliberately pushing USD/JPY down. Intervention is the subject of its own post ([how central banks intervene in the currency market](/blog/trading/forex/how-central-banks-intervene-in-the-currency-market)), but the player-level point is this: a central bank intervening is a counterparty with a non-profit objective, near-unlimited firepower on at least one side, and the willingness to lose money to achieve a policy goal. The game-theory of facing that opponent — when its threat is credible, when it is bluffing — is explored in [the central bank game: credibility, commitment, and don't fight the Fed](/blog/trading/game-theory/the-central-bank-game-credibility-commitment-and-dont-fight-the-fed).

#### Worked example: a reserve manager sizing against a macro fund

Suppose USD/JPY has climbed to 160 and the Japanese authorities decide to intervene. Japan's reserves are roughly \$1.2 trillion. In a single coordinated burst over a few days in 2022, the Ministry of Finance sold about \$63 billion of dollars to buy yen — a real number from that episode. Now consider a macro fund on the other side, short yen as part of a carry position, running a \$2 billion notional book. The fund is large by hedge-fund standards. But \$63 billion of state selling against a \$2 billion private position is a 30:1 mismatch on that day's flow, and the reserve manager can repeat it. The fund's rational response is not to fight — it is to cover, fast, which is precisely what intervention is designed to trigger.

The takeaway: against a reserve manager defending a level, the speculator's edge is not firepower — it is *timing and conviction about whether the defense is sustainable*. Pegs and floors break not because the speculator is bigger, but because the central bank eventually runs out of the *foreign* reserves it needs to sell, as the Swiss National Bank discovered in 2015.

The asymmetry between the two kinds of defense deserves to be stated plainly, because it is the most actionable single idea about central banks as counterparties. To *weaken* its own currency, a central bank prints that currency and buys foreign assets — and it can print without limit, so this defense is essentially infinite. The Swiss National Bank held its 1.20 floor for years by manufacturing francs; the only thing that stopped it was its own political tolerance for a ballooning balance sheet, not a shortage of ammunition. To *strengthen* its own currency, a central bank must sell foreign reserves and buy its own — and reserves are finite. This is the defense that breaks: the Bank of Thailand in 1997, the Bank of England in 1992, every classic speculative attack runs against a central bank that is *spending down a finite pile of foreign currency to prop up its own*. So the first question to ask of any central bank standing in the market is: *is it printing or is it spending?* If it is printing its own currency to weaken it, you do not fight it — you have an infinite opponent. If it is spending finite reserves to strengthen it, you ask how big the pile is and how fast it is draining, because that pile is the clock on the trade. The formal version of this standoff — the speculative-attack model where the central bank's credibility is the variable — lives in the game-theory series; we keep the narrative here and link out to the model.

Reserve management has a second-order effect that ripples through every other player, too. The composition of the world's roughly \$12 trillion in official reserves is a slow but enormous force. For decades the dollar's share has drifted down — from over 70% at the turn of the century toward 58% now — as managers diversify into euros, yen, renminbi, and gold. Each percentage point of a \$12 trillion pile that shifts out of dollars is \$120 billion of dollar-selling spread over years, a structural headwind beneath the dollar that no day-trader can see on a chart but that every macro fund studies in the COFER data. The reserve manager is not trying to move the market; it is trying to keep its nation's savings safe and diversified. But because it is so large, even its prudence is a flow.

## Real-money: pensions, insurers, and asset managers

Between the price-insensitive whales and the profit-seeking sharks sits the largest pool of investment capital on the planet: **real money**. The term is dealer slang for institutions that invest actual savings — pension funds, insurance companies, sovereign wealth funds, and the giant asset managers (think BlackRock, Vanguard, the big mutual-fund complexes) — as opposed to "leveraged money" (hedge funds) that trade on borrowed balance sheets. Real money manages tens of trillions of dollars globally, and a large chunk of it is invested across borders: a U.S. pension fund owning European stocks, a Japanese life insurer owning U.S. Treasuries, a Norwegian sovereign fund owning everything.

Real money's FX motive is *derived*, not primary. They do not wake up wanting to trade currencies; they wake up wanting to own foreign *assets* — a German bond, a Brazilian stock — and the currency exposure comes attached whether they like it or not. This generates two distinct FX flows. The first is **asset-driven**: when a U.S. fund buys €100,000,000 of German bonds, it must first buy €100,000,000, a spot purchase of euros. The second is the **hedge overlay**: many real-money managers do not *want* the currency risk that rides along with the foreign asset, so they sell the currency forward to neutralize it — the same hedging mechanic as the corporate, but at investment-portfolio scale.

![Where real-money FX flow comes from, a graph linking the foreign asset purchase and the currency hedge to the spot and forward market](/imgs/blogs/who-trades-fx-corporates-central-banks-funds-and-retail-5.png)

The figure traces both legs. The buy-the-asset leg pushes spot demand for the foreign currency; the hedge-the-currency leg pushes forward and swap demand the other way. The crucial behavioral point is that these flows are driven by **rebalancing and mandate**, not by a currency view. A pension fund with a target of 30% foreign equities will *sell* foreign currency when those equities rally (they have grown above target and must be trimmed) and *buy* it when they fall — a mechanically *stabilizing*, contrarian flow. This is why month-end and quarter-end rebalancing creates recognizable FX patterns: a big equity rally over a month tends to generate dollar-buying into the month-end fix as foreign investors who own those now-larger U.S. positions rebalance back toward target.

#### Worked example: a pension fund's hedged versus unhedged \$50,000,000 bond holding

A U.S. pension fund buys \$50,000,000 worth of German government bonds. To do so it converts \$50,000,000 into euros at EUR/USD 1.0800, getting €46,296,296 of bonds. Now suppose over the year the bonds return a tidy 3% in euro terms (€47,685,185), but the euro *falls* from 1.0800 to 1.0200. Unhedged, the fund converts back: €47,685,185 × 1.0200 = \$48,638,889 — a **loss of \$1,361,111**, about −2.7%, even though the bond itself made money. The currency ate the return and then some. Had the fund hedged by selling euros forward, it would have locked the conversion near 1.0800 and kept its ~3% bond return in dollars.

The takeaway: for real money, the currency is a *side effect* of an asset decision — and whether they hedge it determines whether that side effect is a rounding error or a portfolio-sinking surprise. The aggregate decision of thousands of these managers to hedge or not is itself one of the great slow tides of the FX market.

Real money matters to you, the reader, in two ways. First, it is *large and slow* — its flows are persistent and somewhat forecastable from rebalancing math, which is why dealers and macro funds obsess over month-end fixes. Second, it is *mostly not your direct counterparty* — these flows clear in the institutional market through dealers, so when they push a pair, you experience it as a trend with no obvious news, the fingerprint of a real-money tide rather than a speculative impulse.

The hedge-ratio decision inside real money is its own slow-moving market force, and it is one of the most underappreciated drivers of major-pair trends. A Japanese life insurer holding trillions of yen of U.S. Treasuries must constantly decide whether to hedge the dollar exposure back to yen. When the cost of that hedge is cheap — when the rate gap is small — they hedge generously, which means persistent forward-selling of dollars. When the hedge cost blows out — when U.S. rates tower over Japanese rates, making it expensive to hedge dollars back to yen — they often *reduce* their hedge ratios, leaving the dollar exposure open, which removes a chunk of dollar-selling and lets the dollar run. So the *same* rate differential that draws speculators into the carry trade also changes how the largest real-money holders hedge, and both effects push the same way. This is why the dollar's biggest trends are so self-reinforcing: high U.S. rates pull in carry speculators *and* push real-money hedgers to under-hedge their existing dollar assets, two enormous flows leaning in the same direction. When that reverses — when U.S. rates fall and hedging gets cheap again — the real-money re-hedging flow can be a slow, grinding source of dollar-selling that lasts for quarters. None of this is a "view" on the dollar. It is the mechanical consequence of how trillions of dollars of foreign bonds get hedged, and it moves the market more than any single fund's bet ever could.

## Macro hedge funds: the discretionary speculators

Now we reach the players everyone *thinks* of when they hear "FX trader": the global-macro hedge funds. These are the discretionary speculators — the Soroses and Druckenmillers of legend, plus dozens of large funds you have never heard of — whose entire job is to form a view about currencies (and rates, and commodities) and bet on it with leverage. When the textbook talks about a speculator, this is the player. And when a currency crisis has a villain, it is usually a macro fund: George Soros versus the Bank of England in 1992 ([Soros and Black Wednesday](/blog/trading/finance/soros-bank-of-england-1992-black-wednesday)) is the archetype.

A macro fund's motive is pure: *profit from being right about a currency's direction*. Unlike corporates and real money, they have no underlying business need — they are in the market *only* to make money on the move. This makes them the most price-sensitive players in the market: they buy what they think is cheap, sell what they think is dear, and they set stops and targets. They are also, crucially, **leveraged money** — they trade on borrowed balance sheets, often controlling \$10 or \$20 of currency exposure for every \$1 of capital. That leverage is what makes them powerful relative to their actual size, and also what makes them fragile.

![A macro fund's leveraged carry position structure, a stack showing capital, borrowed funding, and the resulting notional exposure](/imgs/blogs/who-trades-fx-corporates-central-banks-funds-and-retail-6.png)

The two classic macro trades are **the directional view** and **the carry trade**. The directional view is what it sounds like: "the dollar is overvalued, I'm short DXY." The carry trade — borrowing a low-yield currency to hold a high-yield one and pocketing the rate differential — is the bread-and-butter speculative position the stack above illustrates, and it is the trade that detonated in August 2024. A macro fund running carry is not betting the high-yield currency rises; it is betting it *doesn't fall enough to wipe out the interest it collects*. That is a subtly different and more dangerous bet, because it is profitable in calm times and catastrophic in panics — the payoff profile of "picking up nickels in front of a steamroller."

#### Worked example: a macro fund's \$100,000,000 carry position

A fund borrows yen and buys Mexican peso to harvest the rate gap. Mexican policy rates are about 10.25%; Japanese funding costs about 0.25%. The fund puts up \$10,000,000 of capital and runs \$100,000,000 of notional — 10× leverage. The annual carry is the rate differential on the notional: (10.25% − 0.25%) × \$100,000,000 = **\$10,000,000 a year**, a 100% return *on capital* if the exchange rate simply holds still. That is the seduction. But on a 10% adverse move in USD/MXN, the fund loses 10% × \$100,000,000 = \$10,000,000 — its entire capital. A position that earns 100% in a calm year is wiped out by a single 10% currency move, which is exactly the kind of move a carry unwind produces in days.

The takeaway: the macro carry trader is selling insurance against a currency crash and collecting the premium as "carry" — wonderful until the crash arrives, at which point the leverage that magnified the income magnifies the loss and forces a stampede for the exit. The yen-funded version of this trade is what unwound in 2024; the mechanics of these blowups are catalogued in [carry trade unwinds: 1998, 2008, 2024](/blog/trading/macro-trading/carry-trade-unwinds-1998-2008-2024-when-leverage-breaks).

Who is on the other side of a macro fund? In calm times, the corporates and real money happily sell them the high-yield currency exposure they crave — the hedgers are *offloading* the very risk the fund is *accumulating*. But in a crisis, the sides invert violently: as the carry trade unwinds, the funds all try to buy back the funding currency (yen) at once, and there is no natural seller, so the move overshoots. The August 2024 yen surge is what a one-sided exit looks like.

![The August 2024 yen-carry unwind, USD/JPY collapsing from 162 to 142 in days as the carry trade reversed](/imgs/blogs/who-trades-fx-corporates-central-banks-funds-and-retail-7.png)

The chart above is that unwind in real numbers: USD/JPY fell from about 161.9 on July 3 to roughly 141.7 by August 5, 2024 — a ~12% move in a currency pair in five weeks, an earthquake by FX standards. Every step of that fall was a leveraged carry position being liquidated, often not by choice but by a margin call. The lesson for "who trades FX" is that macro funds are *individually* clever and *collectively* a herd: when they all hold the same trade and it turns, they become each other's counterparties in a doom loop, and the player who fares best is the one who unwound first.

## CTAs and systematic funds: the trend-following machines

Cousins to the discretionary macro fund are the **CTAs** — Commodity Trading Advisors, a regulatory label that in practice means systematic, rules-based, mostly trend-following funds. Where a macro fund forms a discretionary view ("I think the Fed will pivot"), a CTA runs a model that says, mechanically, "USD/JPY has been rising for three months and crossed its 100-day moving average, so go long; when it crosses back, go short." They trade dozens of currency pairs (plus bonds, equities, commodities) on the same signal, with no human deciding any individual trade.

The CTA's motive is profit, like the macro fund, but its *behavior* is different and important to understand because it is **mechanical and momentum-driven**. A CTA does not buy a dip because it looks cheap; it sells a dip because the trend turned down. This makes CTAs *trend amplifiers*: they pile into moves that are already happening, adding fuel to a trend and then yanking it away when the trend breaks. They are the opposite of real-money's stabilizing rebalancing flow — CTAs are *destabilizing* by design, which is precisely why their trend-capture works on average and fails spectacularly at turning points.

![FX style factors and their stylised long-run Sharpe ratios, comparing carry, value, momentum, and the dollar factor](/imgs/blogs/who-trades-fx-corporates-central-banks-funds-and-retail-8.png)

The chart shows the menu of systematic FX "factors" — repeatable patterns that systematic funds harvest. **Carry** (buy high-yield, fund with low-yield) has historically had the highest reward-to-risk, followed by **momentum/trend** (the CTA's bread and butter), **value** (bet on the currency that is cheap on purchasing-power grounds reverting), and the **dollar factor** (the dollar's tendency to move as one bloc). A modern systematic fund usually blends several of these. For "who trades FX," the takeaway is that a large, growing slice of speculative flow is *not* a human with a view at all — it is a model executing a factor, and those models cluster around the same signals, so when momentum and carry point the same way, the systematic herd leans hard, and when they flip, the herd reverses together.

#### Worked example: a CTA flipping a \$200,000,000 USD/JPY position

A trend-following CTA is long \$200,000,000 notional of USD/JPY because the pair has trended up for months. Its model uses, say, a 100-day moving average as the trend filter. When USD/JPY breaks decisively below that average — as it did in early August 2024 — the model does not ask why. It flips: it must sell \$200,000,000 to close the long *and* sell another \$200,000,000 to establish the new short, a **\$400,000,000 swing in dollar-selling** from one fund on one signal. Multiply that by the dozens of CTAs running near-identical trend filters and you get a synchronized wall of selling that has nothing to do with anyone's fundamental view — it is pure mechanical momentum, and it is part of why the 2024 unwind was so violent and so fast.

The takeaway: when you see a sharp, news-light acceleration in a pair that had been trending, you are often watching CTAs flip in unison — a flow you can anticipate by knowing roughly where the popular trend filters sit, even if you can't see the models themselves.

The deeper point about CTAs is that they are *non-fundamental flow with real price impact*. A CTA selling \$400,000,000 of dollars does not believe anything about the U.S. economy; it is obeying a rule. But the corporate, the real-money manager, and even the discretionary macro fund on the other side experience that selling as a real move in the rate, and some of them will react to it — the macro fund may cut its long, the corporate may rush its hedge — so the mechanical flow *induces* fundamental-looking behavior. This is what makes turning points so treacherous: at the exact moment a trend exhausts, the systematic herd is most committed to it, the discretionary players are most crowded into it, and there is the least natural counterparty to absorb the reversal. The CTA is the player that turns a gentle trend into an overshoot and a peaceful reversal into a cascade — not out of malice or insight, but out of rules executed by everyone at once.

## Dealers and market-makers: the plumbing in the middle

Every player above — the corporate, the central bank, the pension fund, the macro fund, the CTA, and the retail trader — trades *through* an intermediary. That intermediary is the **dealer**: the large banks (JPMorgan, Deutsche, UBS, Citi, and a handful of others) that quote two-way prices and stand ready to buy or sell currency on demand. Dealers are the connective tissue of the market, and understanding them is the heart of the "who is on the other side" question, because in a huge fraction of trades, *the dealer is literally the other side* — at least for the moment.

A dealer's motive is neither hedging nor directional speculation; it is **the spread and the flow**. The dealer quotes a bid (where it will buy) and an ask (where it will sell), and the gap — the spread — is its compensation for providing liquidity. When a corporate sells dollars and a macro fund buys dollars, the dealer ideally matches them and pockets the spread on both, taking no risk. But flows rarely net perfectly, so the dealer is left holding **inventory** — a position it didn't choose — which it manages, hedges, and lays off into the interbank market. The interbank market, where dealers trade with each other to offload inventory, is the wholesale core of the whole system; it gets its own post in [inside the interbank FX market](/blog/trading/forex/the-biggest-market-on-earth-inside-the-interbank-fx-market).

The dealer's central skill is reading **order flow**. Because the dealer sees who is trading and in what direction, it has an informational edge no other player has — it knows whether the buying is "real money" (informed, persistent) or "tourist" (uninformed, mean-reverting). This is why the *quality* of your counterparty matters: a dealer will quote a tighter spread to a real-money client whose flow it can predict than to a hedge fund whose flow might be the leading edge of a big informed position. And it is why retail, at the very end of the chain, gets the widest effective spread of all — the dealer (or the retail broker behind it) assumes retail flow is uninformed and prices it accordingly.

#### Worked example: a dealer's spread on a \$50,000,000 EUR/USD ticket

A dealer quotes EUR/USD at 1.0800 / 1.0801 — a one-pip spread, typical for a top major. A real-money client sells €50,000,000. The dealer buys at its bid of 1.0800, paying \$54,000,000. If it can immediately sell those euros to another client at the ask of 1.0801, it receives \$54,005,000 — a **\$5,000 profit** on a round-trip it held for seconds, with no directional risk. That razor-thin one pip, repeated across trillions of dollars of daily volume, is the dealer business. But if no buyer appears and the euro then falls to 1.0790, the dealer's €50,000,000 inventory is worth \$54,000,000 − \$54,000,000 × (1 − 1.0790/1.0800) = a \$50,000 loss — the inventory risk it is paid to warehouse.

The takeaway: the dealer makes a tiny, near-certain margin in exchange for accepting the inventory risk that the rest of the market wants to shed — it is the hub that lets a corporate in Stuttgart and a hedge fund in Connecticut trade with each other without ever meeting.

It is worth being precise about *why* the dealer can profit from spread without being a fortune-teller, because it explains how the whole market hangs together. The dealer is not predicting the rate; it is pricing the *probability that flow nets out before the rate moves against its inventory*. When the dealer is confident the flow is two-sided — a corporate selling here, a real-money buyer arriving there — it quotes tight, because it expects to offset quickly. When it suspects the flow is *one-sided and informed* — a macro fund building a large position that is the leading edge of a trend — it quotes wider or skews its price to discourage that side, because it does not want to be left holding inventory that keeps moving against it. This is the mechanism behind a fact retail traders rarely grasp: the spread you are quoted is not a fixed fee, it is a *judgment about how dangerous your flow is*. Predictable, two-sided flow is cheap to serve; informed or one-sided flow is expensive; and uninformed, high-turnover flow is the most profitable of all to take the other side of, which is exactly the flow the retail product generates. The dealer's edge is informational and structural, not directional — it gets paid to be the counterparty of first resort and to manage the inventory nobody else wants. Strip the dealer out and the market does not function: the corporate's hedge has no buyer, the fund's bet has no seller, and the whole forced-to-chosen risk transfer that defines FX has nowhere to clear.

The structure of the dealing market also explains *where* FX trades. The chart-worthy fact is that the market is geographically concentrated: London handles about 38% of global FX turnover, the United States about 19%, Singapore and Hong Kong another 16% between them. The "London 4pm fix" — the benchmark snapshot used to value enormous real-money portfolios — is a daily concentration of flow precisely because so many of these players reference it. Liquidity is deepest when London and New York overlap, and thinnest in the gaps between sessions, which is when a one-sided flow (a central bank, a forced unwind) can move the rate the most.

## The retail tail: small size, structural disadvantage

We end where the headlines often begin: the retail FX trader. After the late-1990s rise of online brokers and then the smartphone era, millions of individuals got access to leveraged FX trading from a phone. The marketing promises freedom and fortune. The structure delivers something closer to a casino, and the honest data is worth confronting directly.

Start with size. Retail is, by the standards of every player above, *vanishingly small* — on the order of **2-3% of daily turnover**. The entire global retail FX universe trades, in a day, a fraction of what a single large reserve manager can move in an afternoon. This is the first thing to internalize: as a retail trader, you are not a participant in the same sense the others are. You are a price-taker in a market whose price is set by flows thousands of times larger than yours, with information you cannot see.

Now the structural disadvantage, which compounds the size problem. Retail trades the *same instruments* as everyone else but at a *worse price and with worse information*. The effective spread a retail trader pays is multiples of the interbank spread — where a dealer quotes a real-money client one pip on EUR/USD, the retail platform's all-in cost (spread plus commission plus, often, a markup) is several pips. On exotic pairs the gap is enormous: an interbank USD/MXN spread of around 8 pips can become 20-30 for retail. Worse, much retail flow trades against a **B-book**: the broker does not pass the trade to the market at all but takes the other side itself, profiting directly when the client loses — and uninformed leveraged clients lose often enough that this is a viable business model.

![The retail trader's structural cost disadvantage, comparing interbank spreads to retail all-in costs across pair tiers](/imgs/blogs/who-trades-fx-corporates-central-banks-funds-and-retail-9.png)

The figure stacks the costs by pair tier. The point is not that retail trading is impossible — it is that the retail trader starts every position several pips behind, must overcome that drag before earning a cent, and is doing so against counterparties (informed dealers, real money, macro funds) who collectively know more about where the flow is going. Regulators in many jurisdictions now require brokers to publish the share of retail accounts that lose money; the disclosed figures cluster around **70-80% of accounts losing**, a number that should be read not as "most people are bad at it" but as "the structure is built so most people lose."

#### Worked example: the leverage trap on a \$5,000 retail account

A retail trader funds an account with \$5,000 and uses 50:1 leverage to control \$250,000 of EUR/USD. The all-in cost to open is, say, 3 pips — on \$250,000 that is 3 × \$25 = **\$75 gone immediately**, 1.5% of the account, before the market moves at all. Now the position needs the euro to rise just 0.03% to break even on costs. But leverage cuts the other way on risk: a mere 2% adverse move in EUR/USD is 2% × \$250,000 = \$5,000 — the **entire account, wiped out by a move smaller than a normal day's range**. The same leverage that makes a 2% favorable move a 100% gain makes a 2% adverse move a 100% loss, and the cost drag tilts the coin against the trader on every flip.

The takeaway: retail's problem is not that small accounts can't trade — it is that high leverage plus a structural spread disadvantage turns ordinary market noise into account-ending events, on the wrong side of better-informed flow. The retail tail is, in aggregate, the marginal loser whose losses help pay the dealers and brokers in the middle.

## Common misconceptions

**"FX is mostly speculators betting on direction."** The opposite is closer to true. The largest, steadiest flows come from players who are *forced* into the market and do not care about direction — corporates hedging receivables, real money buying foreign assets, reserve managers parking savings. Speculators (macro funds, CTAs) are the *minority* of the dollar flow and an even smaller share of total turnover once you count the \$3.8 trillion-a-day swap market, which is almost entirely funding and hedging. The dramatic directional bets that make headlines are the spice, not the meal.

**"Hedgers and speculators are enemies."** They are counterparties whose preferences *fit together*. The hedger has unwanted risk and will pay to shed it; the speculator wants that risk and is paid to take it. Without speculators willing to warehouse currency risk, hedgers could not hedge — there would be no one to sell to. The healthiest FX market has *both*, in balance. The trade only turns ugly when everyone piles onto the same speculative side, leaving no natural counterparty for the unwind.

**"Central banks are just big traders."** No — a central bank is categorically different because it can *create one side of the trade*. The Fed can print dollars; the BoJ can print yen. This means a central bank can never be forced to sell its *own* currency (it has infinite supply) and can only run out of *foreign* reserves when defending its currency. That asymmetry — infinite ammunition defending a weak currency by printing it, finite ammunition defending a strong-peg by selling foreign reserves — is why some defenses (weakening your own currency) are trivially sustainable and others (the Swiss 1.20 floor, the ERM peg) eventually shatter.

**"Retail is the smart-money frontier — small and nimble beats big and slow."** The data says otherwise. Retail is 2-3% of volume, pays multiples of the institutional spread, frequently trades against a broker who profits from its losses, and 70-80% of disclosed accounts lose money. "Small and nimble" is a myth when small means *uninformed and structurally overcharged*. The advantage of being small is real for *patient, low-leverage, low-cost* trading — but the retail product is sold as the opposite: leveraged, high-turnover, high-cost.

**"More volume in a pair means it's safer to trade."** Volume is necessary but not sufficient. What matters is *who* is creating the volume and whether it is one-sided. A pair can have enormous turnover that is overwhelmingly a single forced flow (a central bank intervening, a mass carry unwind), and in those moments the "deep, liquid" pair gaps violently because the volume is all on one side with no natural counterparty. Liquidity is a measure of *two-sided* depth, and it evaporates exactly when one player's flow overwhelms everyone else's.

## How it shows up in real markets

**Black Wednesday, September 1992.** The textbook clash of speculator versus central bank. George Soros's Quantum Fund and other macro funds bet that the British pound's peg within the European Exchange Rate Mechanism was unsustainable. The Bank of England defended by *buying pounds* with its foreign reserves and hiking rates — from 10% to 12% to a promised 15% in a single day. But defending a *strong* peg means selling *finite* foreign reserves, and the funds knew it. The BoE ran low; the pound was forced out of the ERM and fell about 15% by year-end. The lesson in player terms: the macro funds did not have more money than the Bank of England, but they correctly read that the central bank's *defending* ammunition was finite while their *attacking* conviction was not. See [Soros versus the Bank of England](/blog/trading/finance/soros-bank-of-england-1992-black-wednesday).

**The Swiss franc floor break, January 2015.** For years the Swiss National Bank held a floor of EUR/CHF 1.20, promising to print *unlimited* francs to buy euros and keep the franc from strengthening past that line. This is the *sustainable* kind of defense — printing your own currency, infinite ammunition — and it held for over three years. But it ballooned the SNB's balance sheet to politically untenable size, and on January 15, 2015, the SNB abandoned it without warning. EUR/CHF collapsed from 1.20 to as low as 0.85 in minutes. The players caught short francs — including retail traders and several brokers — were annihilated; some accounts went *negative* because the move outran every stop. The lesson: even an infinitely-defensible peg can break by *policy choice*, and when it does, the one-sided positioning that built up under its shelter unwinds with no liquidity to absorb it.

**The yen carry unwind, August 2024.** Our opening scene, now in player terms. For years, macro funds and CTAs (and plenty of retail "set and forget" carry traders) had borrowed yen near zero and bought higher-yielding everything. The corporates and real money were happy sellers of yen — the structural Japanese surplus and decades of yen weakness made it a comfortable trade. Then the BoJ hiked, the trade's logic flipped, and the leveraged players all rushed to buy back yen at once. With every speculator on the same side and no natural seller, USD/JPY fell ~12% in weeks and the VIX hit 65. The player lesson is the purest one in this whole post: *a trade where every speculator sits on the same side has no one to sell to when it turns* — the counterparty you needed has become your competitor in the exit. See [carry trade unwinds](/blog/trading/macro-trading/carry-trade-unwinds-1998-2008-2024-when-leverage-breaks).

**The Japanese intervention episodes, 2022 and 2024.** Repeatedly, as USD/JPY pushed toward and past 150-160, Japan's Ministry of Finance stepped in to *sell dollars and buy yen* — roughly \$63 billion across September-October 2022, and tens of billions more in 2024. This is the *finite-ammunition* side of intervention (selling foreign reserves to strengthen your own currency), so the market constantly tests how much Japan is willing to spend. The macro funds short yen are not betting Japan won't intervene — they are betting Japan can't *sustain* it against the rate differential pulling the yen down. Reading that standoff is reading the balance of player firepower in real time ([how central banks intervene](/blog/trading/forex/how-central-banks-intervene-in-the-currency-market)).

## The takeaway: read the flow, not the chart

Strip away the jargon and the FX market is a small number of player *types*, each defined by a motive and a size, arranged so that the forced flows of the many supply the speculative bets of the few. Once you can name the players, the most important question in trading a currency stops being "where is it going?" and becomes **"who is making this move, and do they have to keep making it?"**

A move driven by a corporate hedger mechanically converting a receivable tells you nothing about tomorrow — it is calendar-driven and self-extinguishing. A move driven by a reserve manager reallocating tells you about a slow structural tide. A move driven by a central bank defending a level tells you to ask whether its ammunition is the infinite kind (printing its own currency) or the finite kind (selling foreign reserves) — the first you respect, the second you can fade if you're sure the defense will exhaust. A move driven by macro funds and CTAs all leaning the same way tells you a violent unwind is *latent*, waiting for the trigger that flips the herd. And if you are the retail trader at the end of the chain, the most valuable thing you can know is that you are the smallest, worst-informed, most-overcharged player in the room — which is not a reason to quit, but a reason to trade *patiently, with low leverage and low cost*, on the rare occasions you genuinely understand which bigger player is on the other side.

That is the spine of this whole series made personal: an exchange rate is the relative price of two monies, set by the gap between two countries' interest rates and the flow of money across borders — and *every one of those flows has a human or an institution behind it, with a reason*. Learn the reasons, and the chart stops being random noise and starts being a record of who was forced to do what, and when. The trader who reads flow is reading the actual market; the trader who reads only the chart is reading its shadow.
