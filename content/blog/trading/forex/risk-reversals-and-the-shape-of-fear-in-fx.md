> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The 25-delta risk reversal is the price gap between an out-of-the-money call and an out-of-the-money put, and it tells you which way the currency market is afraid.
>
> - A **risk reversal** is one number: the implied volatility of a 25-delta call minus the 25-delta put. Negative means the put is dearer — the market is paying up to insure the **downside**.
> - The **butterfly** is the second number: how far both wings sit above the at-the-money. It tells you *how much* the market fears a big move, in either direction.
> - Together they describe the whole smile in two numbers — a risk reversal (which way it leans) and a butterfly (how curved it is) on top of an at-the-money level (the floor).
> - The one fact to remember: a EUR/USD risk reversal moving from **-0.4 to -2.5 vol** is the market quietly buying downside insurance — fear has a price, and that price is skew.

On the morning of 5 August 2024, a trader watching USD/JPY did not need the spot screen to know something had broken. The risk reversal told the story first. Through July, the one-month 25-delta risk reversal on USD/JPY had been drifting in a direction that looks innocent on a chart and screams in a dealing room: the puts — the right to *sell* dollars and *buy* yen — were getting steadily more expensive than the calls. By the time the yen-carry trade unwound and USD/JPY collapsed from above 161 to near 141 in a matter of sessions, the risk reversal had already been flashing for weeks that the market's fear had a direction, and that direction was *yen strength*.

This is the strange and useful thing about the options market. A spot chart tells you where a currency *is*. The shape of the volatility smile — the risk reversal and the butterfly — tells you what the market is *afraid of*, and how much it is willing to pay to be wrong in a specific direction. It is the closest thing the currency market has to a continuously-quoted fear gauge, and unlike a survey or a positioning report, it is a real price that someone paid real money to set.

The spine of this whole series is that an exchange rate is the relative price of two monies, moved by the gap between two countries' interest rates plus the flow of money across borders. The risk reversal sits one layer above that. It does not tell you the rate; it tells you the market's *asymmetric* worry about which way the rate breaks — and because crashes in FX are nearly always one-directional (a carry pair grinds up and then drops, a peg holds and then snaps), that asymmetry is where the information lives. This post teaches you to read it.

We will build the whole thing from zero. If you have never priced an option, that is fine — we lean on the FX-options primer for the mechanics and on the options-volatility desk's posts for the Greeks, and we keep the focus here on the one question that matters for reading a currency: *what is the shape of the smile telling me about fear and positioning?* By the end you will be able to look at a single number — "EUR/USD 1-month risk reversal is -2.5" — and know what it means, what it costs, and what it is quietly telling you about who is crowded and which way the next break is feared.

## Foundations: Reading the shape of the smile

Start with the only piece of options knowledge you need, and we will define each term as it appears. An **option** is the right, but not the obligation, to trade a currency at a fixed price (the **strike**) on a future date. A **call** is the right to *buy* the base currency; a **put** is the right to *sell* it. You pay a premium up front for that right. The deeper out-of-the-money the strike — the further from where the market is now — the cheaper the option, because it is less likely to pay off.

Here is the wrinkle that makes everything interesting. Two options that are equally far out-of-the-money — say a call 3% above spot and a put 3% below spot — are *not* always priced as if they were equally likely to pay off. The market frequently charges more for one side than the other. When it does, it is telling you it thinks one direction is more dangerous than the other. That asymmetry, quoted as a single number, is the **risk reversal**. Quoted as the *overall* lift of both wings above the middle, it is the **butterfly**. Both are derived from the **volatility smile**, so let us define that.

### Implied volatility, in one paragraph

When you price an option, the one input you cannot directly observe is how much the currency will move between now and expiry. That expected amount of movement is **volatility**, quoted as an annualised percentage. **Implied volatility** (IV) is the volatility number that, plugged into the pricing model, reproduces the option's market price. So IV is just price expressed in a more convenient unit: instead of saying "this option costs \$0.0123 per euro", a dealer says "this option trades at 7.0 vol". Higher IV means a richer option. The FX market is unusual in that it quotes and trades *in vol terms directly* — the volatility-smile primer covers why — so for the rest of this post, when we say a wing is "dearer", we mean its implied vol is higher.

### Delta: how the market labels strikes

The market does not label option strikes by their price level ("the 1.0600 put"). It labels them by **delta**. Delta is how much the option's value moves for a one-unit move in spot, and it runs from 0 to 1 (or 0 to 100%). An at-the-money option has a delta near 0.50. A **25-delta** option is meaningfully out-of-the-money: a 25-delta put sits below spot, a 25-delta call sits above it, and both are roughly symmetric in "moneyness". This is why the standard quote is the **25-delta risk reversal** — it compares a call and a put that are the same distance out, so the only thing left to compare is *price*, and any difference in price is pure directional skew. A 10-delta risk reversal compares the further-out wings, where crash insurance lives.

### The smile, the risk reversal, the butterfly

Plot implied vol on the vertical axis and delta on the horizontal — puts on the left, calls on the right, at-the-money in the middle — and you get the **volatility smile**: a curve that usually dips to a minimum near the money and rises on both wings. Two numbers summarise that curve:

- The **risk reversal (RR)** = (25-delta call IV) − (25-delta put IV). It measures the *tilt* of the smile. Zero means the wings are level; negative means the put wing is higher (the market pays more for downside); positive means the call wing is higher (it pays more for upside). It is the market's directional fear gauge.
- The **butterfly (BF)** = the average of the two wing IVs minus the at-the-money IV. It measures the *curvature* — how far both wings sit above the floor. A fat butterfly means the market is pricing a big move in *either* direction; a thin one means it expects to stay near the money.

So the whole smile reduces to three numbers a dealer can quote in one breath: an at-the-money level (the floor of the smile), a risk reversal (which way it leans), and a butterfly (how curved it is). From those three you can rebuild the 25-delta wings exactly with two market identities:

- 25-delta call IV = ATM + ½·RR + BF
- 25-delta put IV = ATM − ½·RR + BF

That is the entire vocabulary. The rest of this post is learning to *read* it: what a negative RR is telling you, what a fat butterfly is telling you, and how the two together let you read positioning and crash risk off a single quote.

### Why the FX market quotes the smile in these three numbers

It is worth pausing on *why* the FX market organises the smile this way, because it is not how equity options are usually quoted, and the difference is instructive. An equity-options trader typically thinks in terms of strikes and a "skew curve" running from low strikes to high. The FX market, by long-standing convention, quotes three *structures* directly: the at-the-money straddle (which fixes the level), the 25-delta risk reversal (which fixes the tilt), and the 25-delta butterfly (which fixes the curvature). A dealer can stream "EUR/USD 1-month: 7.0 / −2.5 / 0.9" and another dealer instantly knows the entire smile. The FX-options primer covers the full quoting machinery; the reason it matters *here* is that the risk reversal and butterfly are not derived afterthoughts — they are *primary tradeable instruments*. You can call a dealer and trade "the risk reversal" or "the butterfly" as a single package, with the legs delta-hedged, so that what you are buying or selling is *purely* the tilt or *purely* the curvature, stripped of direction and level. That is what makes them such clean reads: each isolates one feature of fear.

There is one more reason the convention is delta-based rather than strike-based, and it is subtle but important. Because FX strikes are quoted by delta, the *same* risk reversal number means the *same thing* across pairs and across time, regardless of where spot is or how volatile the pair is. A 25-delta option is always "the same distance out" in probability terms. So a −2.5 risk reversal on EUR/USD today and a −2.5 on EUR/USD a decade ago describe comparably-tilted smiles, even though spot and the absolute strikes are completely different. This is what lets traders build long histories of the risk reversal and read the *current* level against its own past — "the EUR/USD risk reversal is at the steepest it has been in two years" is a meaningful, comparable statement precisely because the delta convention normalises it. Skew, quoted this way, is a stationary fear gauge.

#### Worked example: building the wings from three quotes

Take EUR/USD in a stressed tape. The dealer quotes a 1-month at-the-money IV of **7.0 vol**, a 25-delta risk reversal of **−2.5 vol** (puts over calls), and a 25-delta butterfly of **0.9 vol**. Plug into the identities:

- 25-delta call IV = 7.0 + ½·(−2.5) + 0.9 = 7.0 − 1.25 + 0.9 = **6.65 vol**
- 25-delta put IV = 7.0 − ½·(−2.5) + 0.9 = 7.0 + 1.25 + 0.9 = **9.15 vol**

The put wing (9.15) sits **2.50 vol** above the call wing (6.65) — exactly the magnitude of the risk reversal, by construction. On a \$1,000,000 notional one-month option, that 2.5-vol gap is not abstract: at these levels it is worth several thousand dollars of extra premium on the put versus the call. The takeaway: three quoted numbers — a level, a tilt, and a curve — fully describe what the market charges to bet either way on the euro.

## The risk reversal, defined: which way the market is afraid

Let us nail the central object. The risk reversal is, at heart, a *trade*: buy the 25-delta call, sell the 25-delta put (or the reverse). Because the two legs are roughly equal in delta, the combined position has very little exposure to small moves and very little time decay near the money — what it *is* exposed to is direction and skew. Its quoted price, in vol points, is simply the IV difference between the two wings. A dealer who says "EUR/USD one-month risk reversal is minus 2.5, puts over calls" means: the put is 2.5 vol points more expensive than the call.

The sign convention trips up everyone once, so fix it now. By the standard market convention used across the major pairs, the risk reversal is **call IV minus put IV**. So:

- **Negative RR** → the put is dearer → the market pays more to protect against the base currency *falling* → fear is to the **downside**.
- **Positive RR** → the call is dearer → the market pays more to protect against the base currency *rising* → fear (or demand) is to the **upside**.

For most pairs in normal times the RR is slightly negative, because the base currency is the riskier one and people pay a small premium to insure its fall. The information is not in the sign — it is in the *change*. A EUR/USD risk reversal sitting at −0.4 for months and then sliding to −2.5 over two weeks is the market quietly, persistently, bidding up downside insurance on the euro. Nobody rang a bell; the skew just got steeper.

![Before and after comparison of a symmetric volatility smile versus a skewed smile](/imgs/blogs/risk-reversals-and-the-shape-of-fear-in-fx-2.png)

The figure above shows the two states side by side. On the left, a *symmetric* smile: the 25-delta put and call both sit at 8.0 vol, two points above the 7.0 ATM, and the risk reversal is exactly zero. The market fears a move but has no view on which way — it would pay the same to insure either tail. On the right, a *skewed* smile: the put wing has lifted to 9.2 while the call wing has sagged to 6.7, and the risk reversal is now −2.5. Same currency, same at-the-money vol, but the *shape* has tilted. That tilt is the entire message: the market is now afraid of the downside specifically.

Why does this matter for reading a currency at all, when you could just look at spot? Because spot only moves *after* the feared event. The skew moves *while the fear is building*. It is a leading read on where the pain is, set by people putting money behind their worry. It is also, crucially, a read on *positioning*: skew gets steep when a trade is crowded and the crowd is hedging. We will come back to that.

There is a further property of the risk reversal that makes it more honest than most sentiment gauges: it is *costly to be wrong about*. A survey respondent who says "I'm bearish euro" pays nothing if the euro rallies. A trader who buys the euro put wing pays real premium that decays every day the euro does *not* fall. So the steepness of the risk reversal is a *revealed* preference, weighted by conviction and size — it is the aggregate of everyone who cared enough about the downside to pay for protection, net of everyone who was willing to sell it. That is a far cleaner signal than opinion, because opinion is free and protection is not. When the risk reversal is deeply negative, it is not that "people feel bearish"; it is that, on net, the market is *paying up* to be insured against the downside, and that payment is the fear made tangible. The whole reason skew is a respected positioning read on desks is that it is denominated in money lost if the fear does not materialise.

#### Worked example: what a one-vol move in the risk reversal is worth

Suppose you hold the EUR/USD risk reversal — long the 25-delta call, short the 25-delta put — on a \$10,000,000 notional, one month to expiry, and the risk reversal moves *in your favour* by one vol point (say from −2.5 to −1.5, the put cheapening relative to the call). The sensitivity of an option's price to a one-point change in its own IV is its **vega**. A one-month 25-delta EUR/USD option on \$10,000,000 has a vega of roughly \$1,800 per vol point per leg. Your call leg gains and your put leg (which you are short) also moves; net, a clean one-vol favourable shift in the risk reversal on this size is worth on the order of **\$3,000 to \$4,000**. The number is approximate — vega drifts as spot and time move — but the lesson is exact: the risk reversal is a traded price with real P&L, not a sentiment indicator you read and shrug at.

## Why a negative risk reversal means fear: the mechanics

It is one thing to define the sign; it is another to understand *why* fear shows up as a dearer put rather than, say, a higher at-the-money vol. The answer is about who is in the market and what they are forced to do.

![Graph of how crowded longs and hedgers bid the put wing and push the risk reversal negative](/imgs/blogs/risk-reversals-and-the-shape-of-fear-in-fx-4.png)

Trace the flow in the figure. Two kinds of players dominate the demand for downside protection in a typical risk currency. First, **crowded longs**: traders who are long the high-yielding side of a carry trade, or long a currency because it has been grinding up, and who want insurance against the move reversing. They buy 25-delta puts. Second, **real-money hedgers**: exporters, fund managers, and corporates who have natural long exposure to a currency and who systematically buy puts to floor their downside. Both groups are *buying the put wing*. To cheapen the hedge, many of them simultaneously *sell the call wing* — giving up some upside to fund the downside protection. That structure, long put + short call, is a **collar**, and it is the single most common options trade in real-money FX. It bids up put IV and offers down call IV — which is exactly a more negative risk reversal.

The result is a self-reinforcing read. When a trade is crowded on the long side, the people in it hedge the same way, the put wing lifts, the call wing sags, and the risk reversal goes negative *before* anything has actually happened to spot. The skew is the crowd's collective hedge, made visible as a price. This is why a steepening risk reversal is so often a *positioning* signal, not just a *fear* signal — the two are the same thing seen from different angles. People hedge what they are exposed to; the shape of the smile is a map of that exposure.

There is a deeper, structural reason FX risk reversals skew the way they do, and it connects straight back to the carry-trade material in this series. Carry trades — borrow a low-yield currency, buy a high-yield one — earn a steady premium and then crash, suddenly and one-directionally, when the trade unwinds. The return distribution is negatively skewed: lots of small gains, occasional large losses. The options market *knows* this, so it charges more for the puts that pay off in the crash. The risk reversal on a high-yielder versus its funding currency is therefore *structurally* negative — the skew is the price of the crash that everyone knows is coming eventually but cannot time. The carry-and-volatility post in this series makes this relationship its whole subject; here, the punchline is that the risk reversal is the *market's quoted estimate of carry-crash risk*.

#### Worked example: the skew premium on a \$1,000,000 collar

You are long \$1,000,000 of EUR/USD spot and you want to collar it for one month — buy a 25-delta put for a floor, sell a 25-delta call for a cap, aiming for roughly zero net premium. Using the stressed smile from earlier (put IV 9.15, call IV 6.65), the put you *buy* is the **expensive** wing and the call you *sell* is the **cheap** wing. If both wings were at the symmetric 8.0 vol, a 25-delta-for-25-delta collar would net out near costless *and* near-symmetric. But with the skew, to make the structure zero-cost you must sell a call that is *closer* to the money (giving up more upside) than the put you buy is from the money — because your cheap call brings in less premium than your dear put costs. Concretely, the **skew premium** is the difference: insuring the downside at 9.15 vol while only being able to sell upside at 6.65 vol means you surrender extra upside worth roughly the 2.5-vol gap, which on \$1,000,000 of one-month notional is on the order of a few thousand dollars of foregone premium. The intuition: when the smile is skewed against you, a "free" collar is never symmetric — the skew is the toll you pay for the hedge being in the popular direction.

## The collar: how the skew prices your hedge

The collar deserves its own section, because it is both the most common real-money FX hedge *and* the trade that the risk reversal directly prices. Understanding it makes the abstract "skew" concrete: the risk reversal is not just a number on a screen, it is the *exchange rate* between giving up upside and buying downside protection.

A collar on a long currency position has three pieces. You hold the spot — long the base currency. You **buy** an out-of-the-money put: this is your floor, the level below which you can no longer lose. You **sell** an out-of-the-money call: this is your cap, the level above which you no longer gain, and the premium it brings in pays for the put. The result is a position that can only land in a band: protected on the downside, surrendered on the upside, and ideally costing nothing up front. It is the natural hedge for anyone who is long a currency they cannot afford to see crash but does not want to pay out of pocket to insure.

![Collar payoff on a one million dollar long EUR/USD position, showing a floored loss and a capped gain](/imgs/blogs/risk-reversals-and-the-shape-of-fear-in-fx-7.png)

The figure shows the payoff at expiry on a \$1,000,000 long EUR/USD position, with the spot leg entered at 1.0800, a put floor struck at 1.0600, and a call cap struck at 1.1000. The dashed grey line is the *naked* long EUR position — unlimited gain, unlimited loss, sliding straight through the origin. The solid blue line is the *collared* position: it flattens at the floor (you cannot lose more than the move from 1.0800 down to 1.0600, about **\$20,000** on \$1,000,000) and it flattens at the cap (you cannot gain more than the move from 1.0800 up to 1.1000, about **\$20,000**). Between the two strikes, the collar tracks the spot one-for-one. The green region is where you make money, the red region where you lose it, and the two flat shelves are the floor and the cap the options bolted on.

Now connect this to the skew. In *this* symmetric illustration the floor and cap sit equidistant from spot — 200 pips each side — which is what you get when the smile is flat. But on a *skewed* smile, that symmetry breaks. The put you buy (the floor) is the dear wing; the call you sell (the cap) is the cheap wing. To keep the structure zero-cost, you must move the cap *closer* to spot — give up *more* upside — because the cheap call you are selling does not bring in enough premium to fund the dear put unless you sell a bigger chunk of upside. So the more negative the risk reversal, the *tighter* the cap relative to the floor: the skew literally sets how much upside you must surrender to buy a given floor for free. The risk reversal is the price of that asymmetry.

This is why the collar and the risk reversal are two views of the same object. A real-money manager hedging a long position with a zero-cost collar is *selling the cheap call and buying the dear put* — which is exactly the flow that steepens the skew. The risk reversal you read on the screen is the aggregate of thousands of these hedges. When you see a steep negative risk reversal, you are seeing a market full of long positions being collared, with the cap pulled in tight because everyone is fighting over the same dear puts. The shape of the smile is the collar flow, made into a price.

#### Worked example: how the skew tightens your collar cap

Stay with the \$1,000,000 long EUR/USD. On a *flat* smile (both 25-delta wings at 8.0 vol), a zero-cost collar might floor you at 1.0600 and cap you at 1.1000 — symmetric, 200 pips each side, max loss and max gain both about **\$20,000**. Now skew the smile to the stressed shape: put wing 9.15, call wing 6.65, a −2.5 risk reversal. The dear 9.15-vol put still costs the same to floor you at 1.0600, but the call you sell at 6.65 vol brings in *less* premium per pip of cap. To still net zero, you must pull the cap in — say to 1.0900 instead of 1.1000 — surrendering upside above a 100-pip gain (about **\$10,000**) instead of a 200-pip gain. Same downside floor, *half* the upside kept. The **\$10,000** of extra surrendered upside is the skew premium, paid not in cash but in foregone gains. The intuition: a negative risk reversal does not make your hedge cost cash — it makes your hedge cost *upside*, and the steeper the skew, the tighter the cap.

## Calm versus stress: how the risk reversal behaves across regimes

The headline cover figure of this post is the most important chart in it, so let us read it properly now. It shows representative one-month 25-delta risk reversals for the major pairs, in calm conditions and in stress.

In **calm** conditions, EUR/USD sits around **−0.4 vol** and USD/JPY around **−0.8 vol**. Slightly negative — there is always a faint preference to insure the downside of the risk currency — but close to flat. The smile is nearly symmetric; the market has no strong directional fear. This is the resting state.

In **stress**, the numbers move hard. EUR/USD stretches to **−2.5 vol**: the put wing has lifted dramatically as the market piles into euro downside protection. USD/JPY, in the specific stress of a *yen-strength* scare, reaches **−4.0 vol** — and notice the label, "yen-up fear". On USD/JPY, the base currency is the dollar, so a negative risk reversal means the market fears the dollar *falling* against the yen, i.e. the yen *strengthening*. That is exactly the carry-unwind direction: everyone is long USD/JPY for the carry, and they all want insurance against the yen ripping higher. AUD/USD in risk-off reaches **−3.5 vol** — the Australian dollar is the textbook risk currency, and when global risk sours, the market pays heavily for AUD downside.

![Horizontal bar chart of one-month at-the-money implied volatility by currency pair](/imgs/blogs/risk-reversals-and-the-shape-of-fear-in-fx-5.png)

Crucially, the risk reversal must be read *relative to the at-the-money level it sits on*. The figure above shows that ATM level by pair: EUR/USD trades around 7.0 vol, USD/JPY around 9.5, AUD/USD around 10.5, while USD/MXN runs near 13 and USD/TRY near 22. A −2.5 risk reversal on EUR/USD (ATM 7.0) is a large *fraction* of the at-the-money — the skew is a big part of the whole price. The same −2.5 on USD/TRY (ATM 22) would be a footnote. So always read the risk reversal as a ratio to the ATM, not as an absolute. A risk reversal that is a third of the at-the-money vol is a screaming directional signal; one that is a twentieth is noise.

This regime-dependence is the practical heart of using the risk reversal. The *level* of the risk reversal tells you the current state of fear; the *change* tells you the direction of travel; and the *ratio to ATM* tells you whether the skew is a dominant feature of the price or a minor tilt. A desk watching a currency does not stare at spot — it watches the risk reversal drift, because the drift is the early-warning system.

#### Worked example: reading the USD/JPY risk reversal in a yen scare

USD/JPY one-month ATM vol is **9.5** and the 25-delta risk reversal is **−4.0**, puts (USD-puts / yen-calls) over calls. Rebuild the wings: 25-delta USD-put IV = 9.5 − ½·(−4.0) = 9.5 + 2.0 = **11.5 vol**, and the 25-delta USD-call IV = 9.5 + ½·(−4.0) = 9.5 − 2.0 = **7.5 vol** (ignoring the butterfly for clarity). The put wing is **4.0 vol** — over 40% of the at-the-money — richer than the call. On a \$5,000,000 one-month USD/JPY option, with a 25-delta vega near \$900 per vol point, that 4-vol skew is worth roughly **\$3,600** of extra premium on the put leg versus the call. The market is paying that much, per \$5,000,000, to insure the yen ripping higher. The takeaway: a −4.0 risk reversal against a 9.5 ATM is not a tilt — it is the market shouting that the crash direction is *yen up*, and putting real money behind it.

## The butterfly: how much the market fears a big move either way

The risk reversal tells you *which way*. The butterfly tells you *how much*. They are independent dimensions of the same smile, and reading only one of them is reading half the message.

Recall the definition: the butterfly is the average of the two wing IVs minus the at-the-money IV. It measures how far the *wings* sit above the *floor* of the smile — the curvature. A thin butterfly means the smile is nearly flat: out-of-the-money options are priced almost like at-the-money ones, so the market expects to stay near the money. A fat butterfly means both wings are bid up well above the middle: the market is pricing a *large* move, and it does not much care which direction — it is the **two-sided** fear of a big jump.

![Before and after comparison of a thin butterfly with flat wings versus a fat butterfly with fat wings](/imgs/blogs/risk-reversals-and-the-shape-of-fear-in-fx-6.png)

The figure contrasts the two. On the left, calm: the ATM is 7.0, the average wing is 7.3, the butterfly is a thin **0.3 vol**, and the smile is nearly flat. The market expects EUR/USD to wander but not jump. On the right, stress: the average wing has lifted to 8.5 while the ATM held at 7.0, the butterfly has fattened to **1.5 vol**, and the smile bows up steeply on both sides. The market is now pricing a big move — but the butterfly alone does not say *which* way. That is the risk reversal's job. The two numbers are orthogonal: you can have a fat butterfly with a near-zero risk reversal (a big two-sided event, like a knife-edge election or a central-bank decision that could go either way) or a thin butterfly with a sharply negative risk reversal (a slow, one-directional crowding into downside hedges).

This is why dealers quote and trade *both*. The butterfly is the **tail premium** — the extra you pay for protection against an outsized move in *either* tail. It fattens before binary events: a referendum, a rate decision with two live outcomes, a peg whose break could trigger a violent move. The risk reversal is the **directional premium** — the extra you pay for one tail over the other. It steepens when a trade is crowded and the crowd leans one way. Together they reconstruct the whole smile, and together they describe both the *size* and the *direction* of the market's fear.

A subtle but important point: when both fatten at once — butterfly up *and* risk reversal more negative — you have the most dangerous configuration. The market is pricing a big move (fat butterfly) and is convinced it will be to the downside (negative risk reversal). That is the pre-crash signature. It is exactly what USD/JPY showed before August 2024 and what AUD/USD shows when a global risk-off is brewing.

#### Worked example: pricing a one-month EUR/USD straddle off the butterfly

A **straddle** is a long call plus a long put at the at-the-money strike — a pure bet that the currency *moves*, in either direction. Its cost is driven almost entirely by the ATM vol, which the butterfly lifts. Take EUR/USD, one month, spot 1.0800, on a \$1,000,000 notional. At a calm ATM of 7.0 vol, the rule-of-thumb cost of an at-the-money straddle is roughly 0.8 × ATM × √(T in years) × spot ≈ 0.8 × 0.070 × √(1/12) × 1.0800 ≈ **0.0175 per euro**, or about **\$16,200** on \$1,000,000 (using the convention that one-month at-the-money premium ≈ 0.4 × annual vol × √(months/12) per side). Now suppose a binary event fattens the butterfly and drags the ATM to 9.0 vol with the wings far above. The same straddle now costs roughly 9.0/7.0 ≈ 1.29× as much — about **\$20,800**. That extra **\$4,600** per \$1,000,000 is the tail premium the fat butterfly put on the price. The intuition: the butterfly is the market charging you more to bet on *a* move when it thinks a big jump is coming, no side specified.

## Reading positioning and crash risk from the shape

We now have the two dials — risk reversal (direction) and butterfly (size) — sitting on top of an at-the-money level (the base). The art is combining them into a read on *positioning* and *crash risk*. This is where the skew earns its keep, because it tells you things no spot chart can: who is crowded, which way the break is feared, and whether the market is pricing a tail.

![Matrix of how to read positioning and crash risk from the risk reversal and the butterfly](/imgs/blogs/risk-reversals-and-the-shape-of-fear-in-fx-8.png)

The matrix above maps four common regimes onto what the smile looks like and what it is telling you:

- **Calm trend.** Risk reversal near zero (−0.4), thin butterfly (0.3). Little fear in either direction; the currency is grinding along and nobody is paying up for protection. Read: positioning is comfortable, no crash is priced. This is the carry trader's paradise — and, paradoxically, the most dangerous regime to be complacent in, because it is the calm that precedes the unwind.
- **Crowded carry.** Risk reversal moderately negative (−2.5), modest butterfly (0.8). The market is leaning one way — longs are hedging an unwind — but it has not yet panicked. Read: the trade is crowded, the crowd is buying downside insurance, and the skew is the early warning. This is the regime to *watch closely*, because the next leg is often the stress regime.
- **Risk-off / panic.** Risk reversal very negative (−4.0), fat butterfly (1.5). The market is pricing a crash on one side, hard, and the whole smile has lifted. Read: maximum directional fear; the puts are screaming. Insurance is expensive precisely when you most want it.
- **Two-sided event.** Risk reversal near zero but *moving*, very fat butterfly (2.0+). A binary event — an election, a knife-edge central-bank decision — where the market fears a big move but genuinely does not know which way. Read: pay up for the straddle, not the directional hedge.

The single most actionable read is the **drift** of the risk reversal in the crowded-carry regime. When a carry pair has been calm and the risk reversal starts steepening — say USD/JPY going from −0.8 toward −2.0 over a few weeks while spot keeps grinding higher — that divergence is the tell. Spot says "all is well, the carry is paying". The skew says "the people in this trade are quietly buying yen-call protection, because they know how it ends". When those two disagree, trust the skew. It is the crowd's hedging behaviour, and the crowd hedges what it is most exposed to.

![Stylised stressed EUR/USD volatility smile showing the put wing sitting higher than the call wing](/imgs/blogs/risk-reversals-and-the-shape-of-fear-in-fx-3.png)

The stylised smile above pulls the whole picture together. It is a EUR/USD smile in stress, built so its 25-delta wings reproduce the data: an at-the-money of 7.0, a risk reversal of −2.5, and a butterfly of 0.9, giving a 25-delta put IV of 9.15 and a 25-delta call IV of 6.65. The amber arrow marks the risk reversal — the vertical gap between the two wings, 2.5 vol, put over call. The whole curve is *tilted*: the downside is the expensive side. A trader glancing at this shape knows, in under a second, that EUR/USD downside is the feared and bid direction, and roughly how strongly. That is the entire value proposition of skew: a continuously-priced, money-backed read on the direction and size of the market's fear, condensed into a shape you can read at a glance.

### The wings beyond 25-delta, and the tenor of fear

Two refinements turn a good read into a great one. The first is *which wings* you look at. The 25-delta risk reversal is the standard, balanced read — it compares moderately out-of-the-money options where there is plenty of liquidity. But the market also quotes the **10-delta** risk reversal, comparing the *further* wings, deep out-of-the-money. The 10-delta wings are where genuine *crash* insurance lives — the options that only pay off in a large, fast move. When the 10-delta risk reversal steepens *faster* than the 25-delta, the market is specifically bidding up tail protection, not just garden-variety downside hedging. A desk watching for crash risk watches the *spread* between the 10-delta and 25-delta skew: when the deep wing runs away from the near wing, the fear is specifically of a *jump*, not a drift. That is a more acute warning than the 25-delta alone.

The second refinement is *tenor* — the time to expiry. Skew has a term structure, just as interest rates do (the fixed-income desk owns the yield-curve concept; the analogy is exact). A one-week risk reversal, a one-month, a three-month, and a one-year all carry different numbers, and the *shape* of that curve is informative. When the *short-dated* risk reversal is far steeper than the long-dated, the market is pricing an *imminent, specific* fear — an event in the next few weeks — while remaining relatively calm about the longer horizon. When the *whole curve* of risk reversals shifts more negative together, the fear is structural and persistent, not event-driven. Before a known binary event — a central-bank meeting, an election — you will often see the risk reversal and butterfly bulge in the tenor that *spans* the event date and stay calm on either side of it. The smile is not a single number; it is a surface across strike *and* time, and reading where on that surface the fear concentrates tells you *when* the market thinks the danger is. The vol-surface post on the options desk maps this whole 3D object; for reading a currency, the practical takeaway is to check not just *the* risk reversal but *which tenor's* risk reversal is moving.

#### Worked example: the 10-delta versus 25-delta crash-risk spread

EUR/USD one-month: the 25-delta risk reversal is **−2.5 vol**, and the 10-delta risk reversal is **−5.5 vol**. The 10-delta is steeper, as it always is — deeper puts are dearer still — but the *spread* between them, **−5.5 − (−2.5) = −3.0 vol**, is the market's pricing of *tail* skew specifically. Now a credit scare hits: the 25-delta risk reversal steepens to −3.5 (a 1.0-vol move) but the 10-delta steepens to −8.5 (a 3.0-vol move), widening the spread to **−5.0 vol**. The near wing barely moved; the *deep* wing ran. The market is not pricing more *ordinary* downside — it is pricing a *crash*, a fast jump in the deep tail. On a \$1,000,000 one-month 10-delta put, that extra 3-vol of tail skew is worth roughly **\$1,500–\$2,500** of additional premium. The intuition: when the deep wing pulls away from the near wing, the fear has changed *character* — from "it might drift down" to "it might gap down" — and that is the most important read the smile offers.

## Common misconceptions

**"A negative risk reversal means the currency is going down."** No. It means the market is *paying more to insure* the downside than the upside — it is a statement about fear and demand for protection, not a forecast. A persistently negative risk reversal can sit under a currency that grinds *higher* for years (that is the carry trade's signature: the put skew is the price of the eventual unwind, not a prediction of next week). The risk reversal is a *positioning and fear* gauge, not a directional forecast. If anything, an *extremely* stretched negative risk reversal can be a contrarian *bullish* signal — everyone has already bought the puts, the hedging flow is exhausted, and a relief rally squeezes them.

**"A higher risk reversal means higher volatility."** No — these are different dimensions. The risk reversal is the *tilt* of the smile; the at-the-money vol is the *level*; the butterfly is the *curvature*. You can have a very negative risk reversal sitting on a low at-the-money (a calm market that nonetheless strongly prefers downside hedges) or a near-zero risk reversal on a high at-the-money (a wild but two-sided market). Reading the risk reversal without the at-the-money level it sits on is meaningless — always take the *ratio*: a −2.5 risk reversal on a 7.0 ATM (a third of the level) is enormous; a −2.5 on a 22 ATM is trivial.

**"The risk reversal and the butterfly tell you the same thing."** No — they are orthogonal. The risk reversal answers "which way", the butterfly answers "how much, either way". A fat butterfly with a flat risk reversal is a two-sided event (big move feared, no side); a thin butterfly with a steep risk reversal is one-directional crowding (small overall move expected, but firmly leaning one way). You need both numbers to describe the smile, and reading only one loses half the information.

**"Skew is mispricing — free money for whoever fades it."** Mostly no. The skew is *structurally* negative on risk currencies for a reason: their returns are negatively skewed (carry crashes are real), so the puts genuinely pay off more often in the crash than a symmetric model would suggest. Selling the put skew to "harvest" the premium is exactly the picking-up-pennies-in-front-of-a-steamroller trade — it works until the steamroller, and the steamroller is what the skew was pricing. The skew is a *fair* premium for a real, fat tail, not a free lunch.

**"Risk reversals only matter to options traders."** No. The risk reversal is one of the cleanest *positioning* reads available to *any* currency trader, because it is set by real hedging flows and updates continuously. A spot or macro trader who watches the risk reversal drift gets an early, money-backed read on when a crowded trade is starting to hedge itself — which is the leading edge of an unwind. You do not have to trade options to read the message they are sending.

**"A flat risk reversal means the market expects no move."** No — it means the market has no *directional* view, which is a completely different thing. A flat risk reversal sitting on a fat butterfly and a high at-the-money describes a market that is bracing for a *large* move while genuinely split on which way it goes — exactly what you see going into a knife-edge referendum or a central-bank decision with two live outcomes. The risk reversal being near zero in that setting is not complacency; it is the market saying "big move coming, no idea which way". This is why you must always read the three numbers *together*: a flat risk reversal is reassuring only if the butterfly is also thin and the at-the-money is low. Read in isolation, the risk reversal will mislead you about half the time.

## How it shows up in real markets

The theory is clean; the real markets are where it bites. Two episodes show the risk reversal doing its job — one a carry-unwind seen coming, one a textbook risk-off.

### USD/JPY before the August 2024 unwind

Through the first half of 2024, USD/JPY was the most beloved carry trade on earth. The US-Japan rate gap was enormous — the US two-year sat above 4% while the Japanese two-year was near zero, a differential of roughly 4 percentage points (this series' interest-rate-differentials post owns the mechanism; the gap is the engine). Everyone was long USD/JPY: borrow yen near zero, buy dollars yielding 5%, collect the carry, and ride the pair from 140 up through 161 by early July. Spot said: this is the best trade in the market.

The risk reversal said something else. As the pair climbed, the one-month 25-delta risk reversal on USD/JPY drifted more and more negative — the *yen-call* wing (protection against the yen strengthening, i.e. USD/JPY falling) getting steadily dearer relative to the USD-call wing. In our representative numbers, a calm USD/JPY risk reversal of around −0.8 stretching toward −4.0 as stress builds. The crowd was long, and the crowd was quietly buying the puts that pay off when the carry unwinds. The skew was the early-warning system, flashing for weeks.

Then the trigger came. The Bank of Japan hiked, the US data softened, the rate gap that funded the whole trade narrowed, and the unwind hit. USD/JPY collapsed from 161.9 on 3 July to about 141.7 by 5 August — roughly a 12% move in a major pair in weeks — and the VIX spiked to an intraday 65.7 on 5 August as the deleveraging spilled across assets. The carry-trade-unwinds and carry-crashes material in this series tells the spot story; the point *here* is that the risk reversal had been pricing the *direction* of that crash long before spot broke. The puts were dear because the market knew which way it would fall. The skew was right.

#### Worked example: what the USD/JPY put protection paid off

Suppose in mid-July 2024 you held \$1,000,000 of a one-month 25-delta USD/JPY put (the right to sell dollars / buy yen) struck around 153, bought when USD/JPY was near 158 and the put IV was rich at, say, 11.5 vol. The premium on a 25-delta one-month put at those levels runs roughly 0.7% of notional — about **\$7,000** for the \$1,000,000 of protection. When USD/JPY collapsed to 141.7, that put went deep in the money: a strike of 153 against a spot of 141.7 is worth roughly (153 − 141.7)/153 ≈ **7.4%** of notional intrinsically, or about **\$74,000**, before time value. A **\$7,000** premium turned into roughly **\$74,000** of payoff — better than a tenfold return on the hedge. The skew made that put *expensive* going in, and it was expensive *for a reason*: the crash it was pricing arrived. The intuition: the risk reversal told you the put was dear because the downside was feared — and the feared downside is exactly the one that paid.

### AUD/USD in a global risk-off

The Australian dollar is the cleanest risk-currency in the majors. It is a leveraged bet on global growth and on China's appetite for Australia's exports — this series' terms-of-trade post ties AUD to iron ore explicitly. When global risk sours, capital flees to the dollar (the dollar-smile post explains why the dollar wins in a panic), and AUD/USD falls. The market knows this, so the AUD/USD risk reversal is *structurally* negative and goes *sharply* more negative whenever risk-off threatens.

In a typical risk-off — a growth scare, a credit wobble, a China slowdown headline — the AUD/USD one-month 25-delta risk reversal stretches toward **−3.5 vol**, puts over calls. At an at-the-money around 10.5 vol, that −3.5 risk reversal is a third of the whole level: the smile is dramatically tilted to the downside. The butterfly fattens at the same time, because a risk-off is also a big-move event. So the AUD/USD smile in risk-off shows the dangerous double signature: very negative risk reversal (one-directional fear, to the downside) *and* fat butterfly (big move priced). A trader who watched that configuration build had a money-backed read that AUD/USD downside was both feared and crowded — well before the spot break confirmed it.

What makes the AUD/USD skew so reliably negative is the *correlation structure* underneath it, and this is worth spelling out because it generalises. The Australian dollar tends to fall *at the same time* as global equities fall and as the dollar rallies — its bad days cluster with everyone else's bad days. In a crisis, the cross-asset desk's "correlations go to one" point bites: AUD, equities, commodities, and EM currencies all sell off together while the dollar and the yen rally together. An option that pays off on AUD weakness therefore pays off precisely when the rest of a typical portfolio is bleeding — which makes it *valuable as a hedge for the whole book*, not just for an AUD position. That extra hedging demand, from people who do not even hold AUD but want crisis insurance, bids the AUD put wing up further. The skew on a risk currency is steep partly because its puts are *systemic* insurance: they pay in the exact state of the world where insurance is most prized. That is a deep reason the risk reversal is structurally negative on AUD, and it will not mean-revert away — it is the price of a real, correlated tail.

The general lesson across both episodes: the risk reversal is a *leading* read because it is set by *hedging* flow, and people hedge before the event, not after. The crowd that is long the carry or long the risk currency buys its protection while spot still looks calm. That protection-buying *is* the steepening skew. By the time spot breaks, the risk reversal has already told the story — and the people who were reading it were positioned, or at least warned, in advance.

#### Worked example: the cost of being late to the AUD/USD hedge

You are long \$2,000,000 of AUD/USD and you want a one-month 25-delta put for downside protection. In calm conditions, with AUD/USD ATM at 10.5 and the risk reversal a mild −1.0, that put might cost roughly 0.9% of notional — about **\$18,000**. But you wait until risk-off is already brewing: the ATM has jumped to 14, the risk reversal has stretched to −3.5, and the put wing IV is now around 14 + 1.75 = 15.75 vol. The same 25-delta put now costs on the order of 1.6% of notional — about **\$32,000**. By being late, you pay nearly **\$14,000 more** per \$2,000,000 for the *same* protection — because both the at-the-money and the skew moved against you while you waited. The intuition: skew is most expensive exactly when you most want the hedge, so the risk reversal is best read as a *warning to hedge early*, not a confirmation to hedge late.

## The takeaway: skew is the market's quoted fear

Step back to the spine of this series. An exchange rate is the relative price of two monies, driven by the rate gap and the flow of money across borders. The risk reversal sits one layer up: it is not the price of the currency, it is the price of *insurance against the currency moving a specific way* — and because that insurance is set by real hedging flows from the people most exposed, it is the cleanest continuously-quoted read on the market's directional fear that exists.

Here is how to actually use it. First, read the risk reversal as a *ratio to the at-the-money*, never as an absolute — a −2.5 on a 7.0 ATM is a roar; the same number on a 22 ATM is a whisper. Second, watch the *drift*, not just the level: a risk reversal steepening while spot grinds the other way is a crowded trade hedging itself, and that divergence is the leading edge of an unwind. Third, read the butterfly *alongside* it: the risk reversal tells you which way the market is afraid, the butterfly tells you how much, and the dangerous configuration — the pre-crash signature — is both fattening at once. Fourth, respect the structural skew: on risk currencies the put wing is dear *for a reason* (carry crashes are real), so fading the skew is selling insurance against a tail that genuinely fattens — the steamroller is exactly what the skew was pricing.

The deeper point is that the shape of the smile is a *map of positioning*. People hedge what they are exposed to, and the wings of the smile are made of those hedges. When you learn to read the risk reversal and the butterfly, you are reading the crowd's collective worry, priced in real money, updated tick by tick. A currency that is grinding up on a calm spot chart but quietly steepening its risk reversal is a currency where the smart, exposed money is buying the exit. That is information no spot chart gives you — and it is sitting in two numbers a dealer can quote in one breath. Learn to read the shape of fear, and you are reading the market's hand.
