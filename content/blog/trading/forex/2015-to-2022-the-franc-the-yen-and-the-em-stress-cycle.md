> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The decade after 2014 is a chain of currency blow-ups, and the same engine drives every one: a shift in the gap between two countries' interest rates, or in the broad dollar cycle, snaps a trade that everyone had quietly crowded into.
>
> - In January 2015 the Swiss National Bank dropped its 1.20 franc floor and the franc gapped roughly 30% in three minutes, wiping out leveraged longs of franc-pairs straight into negative equity.
> - In 2022 the US-Japan rate gap blew out to over four percentage points; the yen fell from 115 to 151 per dollar, Japan spent about \$63 billion of reserves to slow it, and it still fell further to 162 by mid-2024.
> - That same September, the UK's unfunded mini-budget collided with leveraged pension-fund hedges and sent the pound to an all-time low of 1.035 before the Bank of England stepped in to buy gilts.
> - **The one number to remember: 30%.** A G10 exchange rate is supposed to move half a percent on a busy day; the franc moved sixty busy days' worth in three minutes, because a crowded trade does not unwind gently — it gaps.

At 09:30 Central European Time on 15 January 2015, the Swiss National Bank pressed send on a short statement and detonated the calmest corner of the currency market. For three and a half years it had promised to stop the Swiss franc from strengthening past 1.20 per euro, and it had kept that promise by printing francs and buying euros until its balance sheet swelled toward the size of the entire Swiss economy. Traders had come to treat the floor as a law of nature. You could sell francs against that floor and collect a small, steady stream of interest, confident the central bank stood underneath you. Then, in the space of one sentence, the floor was gone. EUR/CHF fell from 1.20 to roughly 0.85 in about three minutes — a near-30% move in a currency that is supposed to twitch by half a percent on an exciting day.

That morning is the opening scene of what I will call the modern FX stress cycle: the run of currency shocks from 2015 through 2024 that defines how a generation of traders now thinks about risk in this market. It is not one story but four — the franc in 2015, the yen and the Bank of Japan in 2022, the gilt and sterling crisis a few weeks later, and emerging markets ground down by a relentless dollar — and yet they rhyme so precisely that learning one teaches you the others. Each began with a trade that paid a little every day and felt nearly riskless because so many people were in it. Each ended when a rate differential or the dollar cycle moved against that trade and the exit door turned out to be one person wide.

This post is the modern chapter of a much older book. The mechanics of pegs, carry, and intervention are covered in this series' foundational posts; here I want to walk the chronicle itself — the dates, the levels, the dollar amounts that were lost — and pull out the single throughline that connects January 2015 to August 2024. An exchange rate is the relative price of two monies, and what moves it most is the gap between two countries' interest rates plus the flow of money across borders. When that gap or that flow lurches, the trade built on the old gap breaks. The figures below are the map of where it broke.

## Foundations: The modern FX stress map

Before the chronicle, the lay of the land. The post-2014 era has a distinct character, and three building blocks explain why the same kind of accident keeps happening.

**An exchange rate is a pair, never a single thing.** This is the spine of the whole series, so I will state it once plainly. You cannot own "the franc" or "the yen" in isolation; you can only own one currency *against* another. Every FX position is a relative bet — long this, short that. The quote EUR/CHF 1.20 means one euro buys 1.20 francs; the first currency (the **base**) is what you are buying, the second (the **quote**) is what you are paying with. When EUR/CHF *falls* from 1.20 to 0.85, the euro got cheaper in franc terms, which is the same sentence as "the franc got stronger." A "currency shock" is always two-sided: someone's funding currency ripped higher, which means the currency they were long collapsed.

**The rate gap is the master variable.** The single largest force on a major exchange rate over months and years is the difference between the two countries' interest rates. If dollars pay 5% and yen pay 0.25%, a saver is paid 4.75% a year just to hold dollars instead of yen, and money flows accordingly — pushing the dollar up against the yen until the expected currency move offsets the rate advantage. This is the engine behind the **carry trade**: borrow the low-rate currency (the **funder**), buy the high-rate one, pocket the difference. The series' rate-differential post derives the mechanism in full and the [fixed-income forward-rate post](/blog/trading/fixed-income/forward-rates-what-the-market-expects-rates-to-be) shows where the rate expectations come from. For our purposes one fact matters: when the gap is wide and stable, carry feels like free money, and crowds pile in. When the gap moves, the crowd has to leave through the same door at once.

**The dollar cycle is the tide under everything.** Roughly 88% of all FX trades have the US dollar on one side, and trillions of dollars of debt outside the United States must be repaid in dollars the borrowers do not print. So when the Federal Reserve hikes and the dollar broadly strengthens, it is not just one pair moving — it is a tightening of financial conditions for the entire planet, and most violently for emerging markets. The [cross-asset post on the dollar](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-dollar-cross-asset-gravity) treats the dollar as a single gravitational force; here it is the tide that decides whether the year is calm or a crisis.

There is one more thing that makes the post-2014 era distinct, and it is worth naming because it changes the character of every shock that follows. For roughly the decade before 2022, the major central banks ran interest rates pinned near zero and bought bonds in size — the era of quantitative easing and "lower for longer." Near-zero rates everywhere meant the rate gaps between major currencies were small, so there was little carry to earn from one G10 currency against another, and the big money chasing yield went *down the credit and emerging-market spectrum* instead — into the dong, the peso, the lira, into anything that still paid. When rates everywhere then snapped higher in 2022 at wildly different speeds, the gaps that had been compressed for a decade blew open in months. A market that had spent ten years training itself to ignore rate differentials suddenly had to reprice every pair around the widest gaps in a generation. The modern stress cycle is, in large part, the sound of a decade of suppressed rate differentials being released at once.

Put those three together — plus that decade of compressed rates suddenly uncoiling — and the modern stress map writes itself. A stable rate gap or a defended peg invites a crowd to sell the cheap-to-fund currency. The crowd grows, leverage builds, and the trade earns a thin, steady carry — until a rate move or a dollar surge breaks the regime, the funding currency snaps back, and the crowd's stop-losses fire all at once into a market with no bids. The timeline below is that pattern playing out five times in nine years.

#### Worked example: the carry that looked free

Suppose in late 2014 you put on the most popular trade in Europe: short the Swiss franc against the euro, betting the SNB's 1.20 floor would hold the franc down forever. You post \$1,000,000 of margin and run the position at 20:1 leverage, controlling \$20,000,000 of EUR/CHF. The franc's interest rate is slightly negative and the euro's is near zero, so the actual carry is thin — call it 0.5% a year on the notional, or \$100,000. For roughly three years the trade does exactly that: it drips \$8,300 a month into your account while EUR/CHF sits pinned at 1.2005, never moving. Annualized on your \$1,000,000 of capital, you are earning 10% with what feels like zero volatility. Every risk model you own says this position is boring. **The lesson: a trade that pays a steady carry with no visible volatility is not low-risk — it is a risk that has not shown up yet, and the size of the eventual move has nothing to do with how quiet the wait was.**

## The 2015 franc shock: when the floor became a trapdoor

The Swiss franc is the developed world's favorite safe haven. When investors are frightened, they buy francs, which pushes the franc up — a problem for a small, export-heavy economy whose watchmakers and pharmaceutical giants sell into the eurozone. So in September 2011, with the euro crisis raging and the franc screaming higher toward parity with the euro, the SNB drew a line: it would not let EUR/CHF fall below 1.20. It would defend that floor "with the utmost determination" and was "prepared to buy foreign currency in unlimited quantities."

For three years the policy worked, and the way it worked is the key to why it broke. To stop the franc from strengthening past 1.20, the SNB had to be a buyer of euros and a seller of freshly created francs every time the market pushed at the floor. There is no natural limit to how many francs a central bank can create, so in principle the defence could go on forever. But every euro bought went onto the SNB's balance sheet as a reserve asset, and those reserves ballooned — from around CHF 100 billion in 2011 toward CHF 400 billion by the end of 2014, an enormous fraction of Swiss GDP. The deeper problem was *what* it was buying: euros, right as the European Central Bank was about to launch large-scale quantitative easing that everyone expected to weaken the euro. The SNB was being asked to absorb an unlimited quantity of a currency it expected to fall. Defending the floor meant pre-committing to a guaranteed loss on its own reserves.

That is the moment a peg's logic flips. A floor is only as strong as the market's belief that defending it is cheaper than abandoning it. Once the SNB concluded that holding 1.20 through ECB QE would cost more than the credibility hit of dropping it, the floor was already gone — the market just did not know yet. On 15 January 2015 the announcement came with no warning, and EUR/CHF did not drift down to a new level. It *gapped*. There were no tradeable prices between roughly 1.20 and 0.85; the market simply jumped the gap, printing prints as low as 0.85 before settling near parity. The [dedicated SNB post](/blog/trading/forex/the-snb-2015-peg-break-when-a-central-bank-blinks) walks the minute-by-minute mechanics; here is the price path.

![EUR/CHF intraday on 15 January 2015 gapping through the SNB 1.20 floor](/imgs/blogs/2015-to-2022-the-franc-the-yen-and-the-em-stress-cycle-3.png)

The damage was not in the size of the move alone — 30% is large but not unimaginable for an emerging-market currency over a year. The damage was that the move happened with no liquidity in between, which made stop-loss orders worthless. A stop-loss says "sell me out if the price reaches X." But a stop only protects you if there is a buyer at X. On the morning of 15 January there were no buyers between 1.20 and 0.85, so a stop at, say, 1.19 did not execute at 1.19 — it executed at whatever price the broker could find, which might be 1.00 or 0.95. For leveraged accounts, that gap blew straight through the margin and into negative equity: clients owed their brokers more than they had deposited. Retail broker Alpari (UK) was driven into insolvency; FXCM, a large US retail broker, needed an emergency \$300 million rescue loan within hours. Even Citigroup and Deutsche Bank reported nine-figure losses on the day.

#### Worked example: the long-EUR/CHF wiped out in 2015

Return to our \$1,000,000 account from the foundations, short franc at 20:1 — long \$20,000,000 of EUR/CHF at 1.2005. The floor breaks and the pair falls to 0.9850 (the day's close). The percentage move against you is (1.2005 − 0.9850) / 1.2005 = 17.9%. On \$20,000,000 of notional, that is a loss of about \$3,580,000. Your account held \$1,000,000. You are not down to zero — you are down \$2,580,000 *below* zero, owing your broker more than two and a half million dollars on a position that had paid you a placid \$8,300 a month for three years. And remember the close of 0.9850 was a relative mercy; intraday prints touched 0.85, where the same notional showed a loss near \$5,800,000. **The lesson: leverage does not just multiply your gains and losses symmetrically — when a market gaps past your stop, leverage converts a "risk-free" carry trade into a debt you owe, and the three years of carry you collected do not begin to cover it.**

There is a second-order detail in the 2015 episode that traders still argue about: the SNB had given every signal that it intended to defend the floor right up to the moment it abandoned it. Days before the break, officials publicly called the floor a cornerstone of policy. That is not deception so much as the unavoidable logic of defending a peg — a central bank that admits it is *considering* abandoning a floor triggers the very run it is trying to prevent, so it must profess total commitment until the instant it stops. The consequence for a trader is harsh: you cannot rely on official reassurance to tell you a peg is safe, because the central bank is *required* to reassure you right up to the end. The [game-theory post on central-bank credibility](/blog/trading/game-theory/the-central-bank-game-credibility-commitment-and-dont-fight-the-fed) models exactly this commitment problem — the peg is a promise that is credible only as long as keeping it is cheaper than breaking it, and the market is always trying to work out which side of that line the central bank is on.

The franc shock taught the market a permanent lesson about *gap risk* — the risk that a price jumps a defended level rather than trading through it. It is the reason FX risk managers now stress-test positions against discontinuous moves, not just daily volatility, and the reason the lovely flat line of a carry trade's P&L is treated with suspicion rather than admiration. It also rewired how the options market prices the franc and other peg-adjacent currencies: after 2015, the implied volatility and the *skew* on franc options — the extra premium demanded for crash protection — carry a permanent memory of the gap, because everyone now knows the placid level can detonate. The [risk-reversals post](/blog/trading/forex/risk-reversals-and-the-shape-of-fear-in-fx) shows how that fear is priced into the shape of the options market. As the [carry-crashes post](/blog/trading/forex/carry-crashes-picking-up-pennies-in-front-of-a-steamroller) puts it, you are picking up pennies in front of a steamroller; 2015 is the year the steamroller had no brakes.

## The pattern: how a crowded trade breaks

Step back from the franc and you can see the template that every shock in this chronicle follows. It has two regimes — a calm one where the trade earns, and a shock one where it gives everything back — and the transition between them is not gradual.

In the calm regime, a stable rate gap or a defended peg creates a one-directional bet that pays carry. Because the bet seems anchored by a central bank or by a wide, durable rate differential, position sizes grow and leverage creeps up. The carry that drips in each day is real, and it lulls everyone: the position's measured volatility is low precisely *because* the anchor is holding, so risk models keep greenlighting more size. Crowding is invisible from inside any single account — you cannot see how many others have the same trade — but it is the thing that makes the eventual exit deadly.

In the shock regime, the anchor moves. The peg breaks, or the rate gap that justified the trade suddenly narrows because the funding central bank hikes or the target central bank cuts. The funding currency, which everyone was short, has to be bought back. But everyone needs to buy it at the same instant, and there is no natural seller on the other side — the natural sellers were the carry traders, who are now all buyers. Prices gap. Stops fire into the gap, forcing more buying. Margin calls force still more. One day erases years of accumulated carry, and the more crowded the trade was, the larger the gap, because crowding *is* the absence of anyone to take the other side.

![Before and after diagram of how a crowded carry or peg trade breaks in a shock](/imgs/blogs/2015-to-2022-the-franc-the-yen-and-the-em-stress-cycle-4.png)

This asymmetry — slow, steady gains punctuated by sudden, violent losses — is the defining statistical signature of FX carry, and it is why a carry strategy's return distribution has a long, ugly left tail. A stylised G10 carry index grinds upward for years and then craters in 2008, again in 2011, a notch in 2015, and again in August 2024. The [carry-and-volatility post](/blog/trading/forex/carry-and-volatility-the-relationship-that-runs-the-trade) makes the link explicit: carry is, in effect, a short volatility position. You are selling insurance against currency disruption, collecting a premium every quiet day, and paying out catastrophically when the disruption finally comes. The 2015 franc was one such payout. The yen in 2022 and 2024 was the next.

#### Worked example: the skew of carry

Take a carry strategy that wins on 95% of days, earning a steady 0.04% per day, and loses on 5% of days, but on those days it loses 1.5%. (These are stylised but in the right ballpark for a leveraged G10 carry book.) Over a 250-day year, the 237 winning days earn 237 × 0.04% = 9.48%, and the 13 losing days lose 13 × 1.5% = 19.5%. Net, the year is *down* about 10% — even though you won 95% of the time. Worse, the losing days cluster: they all hit in one bad week when the regime breaks, so on a single Tuesday you can give back a year of carry. **The lesson: a high win rate tells you almost nothing about a carry trade's true risk, because the entire risk lives in the rare losing days, and those days arrive together — the trade is profitable right up until the one week it ends your year.**

## The 2022 yen collapse and the BoJ's defence

If 2015 was a peg breaking, 2022 was a rate gap exploding. For most of the 2010s the world's major central banks ran near-zero interest rates, so there was not much of a gap to trade. Then came the post-pandemic inflation surge, and the Federal Reserve hiked from near zero to 5.25–5.50% in eighteen months. The Bank of Japan did the opposite of everyone else: it kept its policy rate pinned near zero and capped Japanese government bond yields, because Japan had spent decades fighting *deflation* and was not about to abandon stimulus over an inflation scare it judged temporary. The result was the widest US-Japan rate gap in a generation.

Recall the master variable. When dollars pay 4.5% and yen pay 0.1%, holding dollars instead of yen pays you over four percentage points a year, and money pours out of yen into dollars. The yen had no choice but to fall. USD/JPY went from about 115 at the start of 2022 to 131 by year-end, then 144, then to nearly 152 in October 2022 and on to an eye-watering 162 by mid-2024. This is the cleanest "the pair tracks the rate gap" picture in modern markets: lay the US-Japan two-year yield differential next to USD/JPY and the two lines move as one.

![USD/JPY year-end levels plotted against the US-Japan 2-year rate gap from 2019 to 2025](/imgs/blogs/2015-to-2022-the-franc-the-yen-and-the-em-stress-cycle-5.png)

Japan's authorities did not like the speed of the fall — a currency that drops too fast imports inflation and panics households — so in September and October 2022 the Ministry of Finance ordered the Bank of Japan to intervene: to sell dollars from Japan's reserves and buy yen, leaning against the weakness. They sold roughly \$20 billion in September and another \$43 billion in October, about \$63 billion in total, with the first round timed for the moment USD/JPY breached 145 and a later round near 152. The intervention worked, briefly. Each time, USD/JPY snapped back several yen in minutes. And each time, it drifted weak again within weeks, because intervention does not touch the thing actually driving the pair — the rate gap. Japan could spend reserves to slow the yen; it could not spend reserves to close a four-point interest-rate differential.

![Pipeline of the Bank of Japan's yen defence from the wide rate gap to selling dollars and the yen drifting weak again](/imgs/blogs/2015-to-2022-the-franc-the-yen-and-the-em-stress-cycle-6.png)

This is the central truth about FX intervention, and it is worth dwelling on because it is so often misunderstood. Selling reserves to defend a currency is like bailing a boat with a hole in it: you can keep the water down for a while, but unless you patch the hole — the rate gap, or the underlying flow — you are just buying time. The [series' intervention post](/blog/trading/forex/how-central-banks-intervene-in-the-currency-market) draws the distinction between *sterilised* intervention (which leaves the money supply unchanged and so rarely moves the long-run level) and a change in the actual policy rate (which moves the gap and therefore the trend). Japan's 2022 operations were the former: a speed bump, not a U-turn. The yen kept falling because the BoJ refused to hike, and the only thing that ever truly turned the yen was the day the BoJ finally *did* hike, in 2024 — which brings us to the unwind, later.

It is worth understanding *why* Japan was even in this bind, because it explains why the yen became the funder of choice for the entire world. Japan ran near-zero rates for decades because it was fighting deflation, not inflation; an economy with stagnant prices and an aging population genuinely needed the cheapest possible money, and the BoJ kept it there long after every other central bank had moved on. That made the yen the perfect funding currency: you could borrow it at essentially no cost and there was a credible expectation the BoJ would keep it cheap. The flip side is that Japan's reluctance to hike — rational for its own economy — guaranteed the rate gap would stay wide, which guaranteed the yen would keep weakening, which is precisely the pressure that intervention was trying to fight. Japan was, in effect, intervening against the consequences of its own (defensible) domestic policy. There was no clean way out: hiking to defend the yen would have risked choking the recovery it had spent thirty years engineering. So it chose to sell reserves and accept a weaker yen — buying time and hoping US inflation would cool, the Fed would cut, and the gap would close on its own. That, eventually, is roughly what happened in 2024, but only after the yen had fallen to 162.

The intervention also had a tactical dimension that traders learned to read. Japan's authorities did not announce their operations in advance, and they timed the first round to a moment of thin liquidity and a psychologically charged level (145, then 152), so that a given amount of dollar-selling would produce the maximum jolt. That is intervention as ambush: the goal is not to spend the most reserves but to inflict the most pain on the speculators leaning short the yen, making them less willing to press. It works on positioning and psychology more than on fundamentals. For a trader, the signal hierarchy is clear — verbal warnings ("watching with concern") are cheap and fadeable; "checking rates" (the BoJ calling dealers for quotes, a classic warning shot) means an operation may be imminent; and actual reserve-selling is the real thing but still only a speed bump unless the policy rate follows.

#### Worked example: a \$1M yen-funded carry position in 2022

You run the textbook 2022 carry trade: borrow yen, buy dollars, earn the gap. With \$1,000,000 of capital at 10:1 leverage you fund \$10,000,000 of USD/JPY at the start of 2022, when the pair is 115.0. By October it is 151.9. The pair rose 32%, so your \$10,000,000 long-dollar position is worth about \$3,200,000 more, and on top of that you have been collecting roughly the 4% rate differential on \$10,000,000, another \$400,000 a year. On \$1,000,000 of capital, that is a 360% return in ten months — the carry trade in its glory. Now run the same numbers in reverse for the person on the other side, a Japanese exporter or a fund that was *long yen*: that account lost the same \$3,200,000 plus paid the carry, a 360% loss on capital. **The lesson: the yen's 2022 fall was not a market malfunction — it was the rate gap doing exactly what the master variable says it must, and the dollar amounts are enormous because four points of carry, levered ten times and compounded by a 32% spot move, is a fortune in one direction and a wipe-out in the other.**

#### Worked example: the cost of the BoJ's intervention

When the BoJ sold roughly \$63 billion to defend the yen in autumn 2022, what did it actually spend, and did it work in dollar terms? It sold \$63 billion of reserve assets — mostly US Treasuries — and bought yen at around 145–152 per dollar. Suppose for arithmetic it bought yen at an average of 148. That \$63 billion bought about ¥9.3 trillion. The intervention pushed USD/JPY down by perhaps 7 yen at the moment, from 152 toward 145, a roughly 4.6% move — but only for a few weeks. By 2024 USD/JPY was at 162, far weaker than where Japan had intervened. So in pure mark-to-market terms, the yen Japan bought at 148 was worth less than the dollars it sold, had it simply held them: the intervention "lost money" as a trade, which is normal — the point was never profit, it was to slow a disorderly move. **The lesson: intervention is best judged not as a trade but as crowd control; \$63 billion bought Japan a few weeks of calm and a few yen of level, which is all sterilised intervention can ever buy against a rate gap it refuses to close.**

## The gilt and sterling crisis: a developed market has an EM moment

Six weeks before that October yen intervention, the United Kingdom showed the world that a rich, developed economy with its own freely floating currency can still have a genuine currency crisis — if it frightens its own creditors badly enough. On 23 September 2022, a new UK government delivered a "mini-budget": a package of large, unfunded tax cuts announced without the usual independent fiscal forecast. The bond market's reaction was instant and brutal. If a government is going to borrow much more without saying how it will pay for it, lenders demand a higher yield to hold its debt and a cheaper currency to compensate for the risk. UK government bond (gilt) yields spiked, and the pound went into free fall.

GBP/USD fell from about 1.125 before the mini-budget to 1.085 the day of it, and then, in thin Asian trading in the early hours of 26 September, to 1.035 — an all-time low, briefly trading below where the pound had bottomed even on Black Wednesday in 1992 (covered in the [Soros / Bank of England post](/blog/trading/forex/soros-and-black-wednesday-breaking-the-bank-of-england-1992) and the [finance case study](/blog/trading/finance/soros-bank-of-england-1992-black-wednesday)). For a moment the market openly speculated about parity — one pound, one dollar — a level the pound had never seen.

![GBP/USD intraday path to the 1.035 all-time low in September 2022 and the Bank of England's response](/imgs/blogs/2015-to-2022-the-franc-the-yen-and-the-em-stress-cycle-7.png)

But the truly dangerous part was not the headline currency move — it was the hidden leverage the move exposed. UK pension funds had, for years, run a strategy called liability-driven investment (LDI), using derivatives to hedge the long-dated interest-rate risk in their pension promises. When gilt yields spiked, those derivative hedges generated enormous margin calls. To meet the calls, the funds had to sell gilts — which pushed yields *higher*, which triggered *more* margin calls, in exactly the self-reinforcing doom loop the crowded-trade pattern predicts. This was a carry-crash structure hiding inside the supposedly staid world of pension hedging: a position that earned quietly for years and then demanded cash all at once when the regime broke. The Bank of England had to step in on 28 September with emergency, time-limited gilt purchases — not to support the pound, but to stop the LDI spiral from forcing a fire-sale of the entire long-end gilt market. The mini-budget was reversed within weeks and the prime minister resigned shortly after.

The sterling crisis is the chronicle's reminder that "developed market" is not a guarantee of immunity. The pound is a freely floating, fully convertible major currency, and it still had a 9% peak-to-trough collapse and a near-systemic accident in a fortnight — because the trigger was a loss of *fiscal* credibility, and because leverage was hiding where the regulators were not looking. The currency was the messenger, not the disease; the disease was a hidden, crowded, leveraged hedge that broke when its anchoring assumption (that gilt yields could not move that fast) turned out to be false. That is the same disease as the franc and the yen, dressed in a different costume.

It is worth noticing what the currency was *telling* you that the bond market took a day longer to scream. A loss of fiscal credibility shows up in three places at once: the bond yield rises (lenders demand more to hold the debt), the currency falls (foreigners demand a cheaper entry to compensate for the risk), and, in the worst case, the two move *together* in a way that developed markets almost never see. Normally a rising yield supports a currency — higher rates attract capital, the rate-gap logic of this whole series. But in a credibility crisis, the yield rises *and* the currency falls at the same time, because the higher yield is not "more attractive return," it is "more risk premium for a government markets no longer trust." That simultaneous move — bonds and currency both selling off — is the unmistakable fingerprint of a sovereign-credibility event, and it is exactly what distinguishes a true crisis from an ordinary rate-driven currency move. The [emerging-market sovereign-debt post](/blog/trading/fixed-income/emerging-market-and-sovereign-debt-yield-with-country-risk) treats this risk-premium dynamic as the normal state of affairs for EM borrowers; in September 2022 a G7 sovereign briefly traded like one.

The Bank of England's intervention is also a study in how a central bank threads the needle when two of its goals collide. The BoE was *raising* rates and shrinking its balance sheet to fight inflation — yet here it was, forced to *buy* gilts to stop the LDI spiral. Those look contradictory, and the BoE worked hard to frame them as separate: the gilt purchases were explicitly temporary, narrowly targeted at the long end, financial-stability operations, and unwound within weeks, precisely so they would not be read as a return to monetary-easing QE. The distinction matters because a central bank that is seen to print money to bail out a leveraged trade loses inflation-fighting credibility — the very thing whose loss started the crisis. The BoE's careful "this is financial stability, not monetary policy" messaging was an attempt to put out the fire without burning down the house.

#### Worked example: the LDI margin spiral in dollar terms

A UK pension scheme holds \$1,000,000,000 of long-dated liabilities and hedges them with gilt-derivative positions that, for simplicity, behave like a leveraged long-gilt holding with a duration of 20 years — meaning a 1% rise in yields cuts the hedge's value by about 20%. In late September 2022, 30-year gilt yields jumped roughly 1.2 percentage points in days. That is a mark-to-market hit on the hedge of about 20 × 1.2% = 24%, or \$240,000,000 of collateral the fund must post — in cash — within days. It does not have \$240,000,000 in cash; it has gilts. So it sells gilts to raise the cash, which pushes yields up further, which deepens the loss on the remaining hedge, which demands still more collateral. **The lesson: the pound's headline fall was the visible symptom, but the genuine emergency was a leverage spiral where selling to meet margin calls forced the very price move that triggered more margin calls — a crowded trade breaking, with a central bank as the only buyer left.**

## Emerging markets under a strong dollar

While the franc, the yen, and the pound grabbed the headlines, the broadest stress of the modern cycle was quieter and more grinding: emerging-market currencies bled lower through 2022 under the weight of a surging dollar. This is the chronicle's structural backdrop, and it deserves its own mechanism because it is the purest illustration of the dollar cycle as the tide under everything.

Start from the plumbing fact. Trillions of dollars of debt — corporate, sovereign, bank — are owed by borrowers *outside* the United States, denominated in dollars those borrowers do not issue. When the Fed hikes and the dollar broadly strengthens, three things happen at once to an emerging economy. First, its dollar debt gets heavier in local-currency terms: a Brazilian company that borrowed \$100 million now needs more reais to pay the same dollar coupon. Second, the higher US yield pulls global capital *toward* dollars and *away* from emerging markets, so the hot money that had chased EM yield in the good years reverses — a "sudden stop" of inflows that the [carry-into-EM post](/blog/trading/forex/the-carry-into-emerging-markets-and-the-sudden-stop) treats in detail. Third, the local central bank is forced to choose: hike its own rates hard to defend the currency (crushing its domestic economy), or sell foreign-exchange reserves to prop it up (which drains a finite buffer), or let the currency fall (which imports inflation and makes the dollar debt heavier still). Every option is bad. This is the dollar's "wrecking-ball" mechanism, and the [dedicated wrecking-ball post](/blog/trading/forex/the-dollar-as-a-wrecking-ball-for-emerging-markets) is the full treatment.

![Graph of the dollar cycle as a wrecking ball for emerging markets, from Fed hikes to currency falls and draining reserves](/imgs/blogs/2015-to-2022-the-franc-the-yen-and-the-em-stress-cycle-8.png)

The data make the doom loop visible. As the dollar index climbed from about 96 in 2020-21 to over 114 at its September 2022 peak, a broad emerging-market currency index fell roughly 12% from its 2018 level — the two move in clean opposition. Some currencies fell far more. The dollar's strength was not a verdict on any one country's policy; it was a single global force, the tide going out, that left the weakest-financed economies stranded first. Countries with thin reserve buffers — a few months of import cover rather than the comfortable double-digit cushions of China or India — had the least room to defend and the most to fear. Vietnam is a case in point: the State Bank drew its reserves down hard in 2022 defending the dong, from about \$109 billion in 2021 to \$87 billion, even as the dong slid in its managed crawl; the [USD/VND managed-float post](/blog/trading/forex/usd-vnd-and-the-managed-float-how-the-sbv-runs-the-dong) and the [Vietnam four-dials post](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/the-four-macro-dials-rates-credit-fx-commodities) show how a small open economy absorbs the same dollar tide that the headline crises rode.

The cruelty of the EM doom loop is that the defensive options are not just bad individually — they interact to make each other worse. Hiking rates to defend the currency slows the domestic economy, which can deepen the very capital flight the hike was meant to stop, because investors now fear a recession on top of the currency loss. Selling reserves to prop up the currency drains the buffer that signals the country *can* defend, so each dollar spent makes the next attack more likely, exactly as the SNB's ballooning reserves foreshadowed its own surrender. And letting the currency fall imports inflation through dearer imports and makes every dollar of external debt heavier, which can force a hike anyway. There is no dominant strategy, only a choice among which pain to take first — which is why the [impossible-trinity post](/blog/trading/forex/the-impossible-trinity-pick-two-of-three) frames EM policy as choosing two of three goals (a stable currency, free capital flows, an independent monetary policy) and surrendering the third. Under a strong dollar, the country that tried to keep all three is the one that breaks.

There is also a sorting that happens within "emerging markets" during a dollar surge, and it is information you can trade. The market does not punish all EM currencies equally; it discriminates by the balance of payments. A country that runs a current-account *surplus* — that earns more foreign currency than it spends, through exports and remittances — has a natural source of dollars to defend its currency and is far more resilient. A country that runs a current-account *deficit* must import capital to fund itself, and when that capital flees in a sudden stop, it has nothing to fall back on. So the dollar's wrecking ball does not hit randomly; it concentrates on the deficit countries with thin reserves and large dollar debts, while surplus economies with deep buffers ride it out with only a managed slide. Reading EM stress is, in large part, reading the [balance of payments](/blog/trading/forex/the-balance-of-payments-and-the-current-account): who funds themselves from a position of strength, and who depends on the kindness of foreign lenders that vanishes precisely when it is needed.

#### Worked example: the dollar wrecking-ball on an EM borrower

A Turkish company borrowed \$50,000,000 at the start of 2021, when the dollar bought about 7.4 lira, so the loan was worth ₺370,000,000 in local terms. The dollar then surged against most EM currencies, and the lira — under a central bank that *cut* rates into inflation, the opposite of defence — collapsed past 18 lira per dollar by late 2022. The company still owes \$50,000,000. But at 18 lira, that debt is now ₺900,000,000 in local terms — it has more than *doubled* in the currency the company actually earns, without the company borrowing a single extra dollar. Its revenue, in lira, did not double. **The lesson: a strong dollar does not just move an exchange rate on a screen — it silently inflates the real burden of every dollar debt across the emerging world, which is why a Fed hiking cycle tightens financial conditions for companies that have never set foot in the United States.**

## The 2024 yen-carry unwind: the chronicle closes a loop

The modern stress cycle ends, fittingly, where its biggest trade lived: the yen. By mid-2024 the yen-funded carry trade was the most crowded position on earth. The US-Japan rate gap was still wide, USD/JPY had pushed to 161.9 in early July, and a generation of traders had borrowed near-free yen to buy almost anything with a yield — US Treasuries, Mexican peso, tech stocks, the lot. The trade had paid beautifully for two years. It was, in the language of this post, maximally crowded.

Two things then moved at once. The Bank of Japan, finally confronting domestic inflation, hiked its policy rate at the end of July 2024 — the first real narrowing of the rate gap from the Japanese side in the entire episode. And a weak US jobs report a few days later made the market suddenly fear the Fed had over-tightened and would have to cut fast, narrowing the gap from the *other* side too. Both moves pointed the same way: the gap that justified the yen-funded carry trade was closing. Everyone who was short yen had to buy it back, all at once. USD/JPY fell from 161.9 on 3 July to about 141.7 by 5 August — a roughly 13% move in a month, with the sharpest leg in three trading days — and the unwind metastasized across assets: the VIX equity-fear index spiked to an intraday 65.7 on 5 August, a level seen only in true panics. The [carry-trade-unwinds case study](/blog/trading/macro-trading/carry-trade-unwinds-1998-2008-2024-when-leverage-breaks) sets August 2024 alongside 1998 and 2008 as the same machine breaking in different decades.

What made August 2024 so violent, relative to the modest size of the rate moves that triggered it, was leverage and reflexivity. The yen-funded carry trade was not just large; it was *recursive*. People had borrowed yen to buy assets, and many of those assets were themselves bought on leverage, so when the yen started to rise the losses showed up in two places at once — the funding leg (you now owe more expensive yen) and the asset leg (the thing you bought is falling because everyone else funded the same way is selling). Each loss forced deleveraging, which meant buying back yen and selling assets, which pushed the yen higher and assets lower, which forced more deleveraging. That is reflexivity: the act of unwinding the trade makes the trade more painful to unwind. It is the same self-reinforcing spiral as the gilt-LDI margin loop, just running across currencies and asset classes instead of inside one bond market. The VIX print of 65.7 was the equity market feeling a deleveraging that started in a currency pair most equity traders never watch.

The unwind also exposed a measurement trap that catches even sophisticated investors. Through 2023 and into 2024, the yen carry trade had a *negative* correlation with risk-off events for a while — it kept paying even when stocks wobbled — so risk models that lean on recent correlations classified it as a diversifier, something that hedged the rest of the book. That is exactly backwards in a crisis. Carry's correlation with everything else is benign right up until the unwind, at which point it goes to one: the carry trade, the equities, the EM positions, all funded by the same cheap yen, all sell together. A risk model trained on the calm regime systematically *under*estimates the risk of the crowded trade, because the data it learns from is precisely the period before the break. The [when-correlations-go-to-one post](/blog/trading/cross-asset/when-correlations-go-to-one-in-a-crisis) is the formal version of this trap; August 2024 is the live demonstration.

That is the loop closing. The franc in 2015 was a peg breaking; the yen in 2022 was a rate gap exploding; the yen in 2024 was the rate gap finally *narrowing* and snapping the trade that the wide gap had spawned. Different triggers, identical structure: a crowded position built on an assumed-stable rate regime, unwinding violently the instant the regime moved. The throughline of the entire chronicle is that rate-differential and dollar-cycle shocks keep breaking the trades everyone had crowded into — and they always will, because crowding is what makes a stable-looking trade fragile, and stability is exactly what attracts the crowd.

## Common misconceptions

**"A G10 currency can't move 30% in a day — that's emerging-market stuff."** It can, and it did. EUR/CHF fell roughly 30% on 15 January 2015. The franc is one of the most liquid, safest currencies on the planet, and a developed-market central bank's own decision is what gapped it. The lesson is that the size of a currency does not protect it from a discontinuous move when a defended level breaks; if anything, the calmer the prior regime, the larger the eventual gap, because more leverage accumulated underneath it.

**"Stop-losses cap your downside in FX."** A stop only works if there is a counterparty at your stop price. In 2015 there were no bids between 1.20 and 0.85, so stops executed tens of figures below where they were set, and leveraged accounts went into negative equity — owing the broker money. A stop limits your downside in a *liquid* market; in a gapping market it is a suggestion the market is free to ignore. This is why the modern risk manager stress-tests against gap scenarios, not just daily volatility.

**"Central-bank intervention sets the exchange rate."** Sterilised intervention — selling reserves to buy your own currency — moves the level for hours to weeks, not the trend. Japan spent about \$63 billion in 2022 and the yen still fell another ten yen over the following two years, because intervention does not touch the rate gap that drives the pair. Intervention is crowd control against a disorderly move; only a change in the actual policy rate (the 2024 BoJ hike) moved the yen's trend, because only that changed the gap.

**"A floating currency can't have a crisis like a pegged one."** Sterling in September 2022 was a freely floating, fully convertible major currency, and it fell 9% peak-to-trough in a fortnight and triggered a near-systemic LDI margin spiral that forced emergency central-bank intervention. The float did not prevent the crisis; it just meant the crisis showed up as a price collapse plus a leverage doom loop rather than a peg break. A crisis is about hidden, crowded leverage meeting a credibility shock — the exchange-rate regime only changes the costume.

**"A high win rate means a strategy is safe."** A carry trade can win on 95% of days and still lose money over the year, because the rare losing days are huge and they cluster into a single bad week. Win rate measures frequency; risk lives in the magnitude and correlation of the losses. The flat, smiling P&L line of a carry book is the warning sign, not the all-clear — it means you are short volatility and the premium has not been called yet.

**"The yen and the franc are unrelated stories — different countries, different decades."** They are the same trade with the labels swapped. Both the franc (negative rates) and the yen (zero rates) were the world's favorite *funding* currencies — the cheap money you borrow to buy something with a yield. Both were sold short by an enormous, leveraged, well-behaved crowd that collected a steady carry for years. And both blew up the instant their funding cost moved against the crowd: the franc when the SNB stopped capping it, the yen when the BoJ finally hiked. If you understand why one detonated, you already understand the other, because they were never two stories — they were one structure expressed in two currencies, nine years apart.

## How it shows up in real markets

The chronicle is not a museum. The same machine runs today, and you read live markets through it.

**You read the rate gap as the level, not the noise.** When you see USD/JPY at some level, the first question is not "is the yen cheap?" but "what is the US-Japan rate gap, and is it widening or narrowing?" The 2019–2025 picture — the pair tracking the two-year differential almost one-for-one — is the template. If the gap is wide and stable, the carry trade is on and the pair grinds in the funder's disfavor; the day the gap starts to narrow is the day the crowded side gets nervous. The [macro-trading post on rate differentials moving currencies](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-monetary-policy-moves-currencies-rate-differentials) is the mechanism; the chronicle is the mechanism under stress.

**You treat a too-quiet trade as a warning.** A position that pays a steady carry with no visible volatility is not low-risk — it is a short-volatility position whose risk has not been called. The franc carry in 2014, the yen carry in 2024, the LDI hedges in 2022: all three felt placid right up to the day they did not. When you find yourself in a trade that "just keeps paying" and that everyone you know is also in, you are in the calm regime of the before-after diagram, and the question is not whether the shock comes but whether you have sized for the gap when it does.

**You watch the dollar as a single global dial.** A rising broad dollar (DXY) is an EM-stress gauge before it is anything else. When the Fed is hiking and the dollar is climbing, the question for any emerging-market position is reserve adequacy: how many months of import cover does this central bank have, and how much dollar debt is owed into the strengthening tide? The countries with thin buffers — three months of imports rather than fourteen — are the ones the wrecking ball reaches first. The [dollar-as-cross-asset-gravity post](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-dollar-cross-asset-gravity) treats this as one position you can size and hedge.

**You respect that intervention buys time, not trend.** When a finance ministry announces it is "monitoring the currency with great concern," that is a verbal warning; when it actually sells reserves, that is a speed bump that will move the level for a few weeks and fade — unless the policy rate moves too. Knowing the difference tells you whether to fade the intervention bounce (if the gap is unchanged) or respect it (if a real rate move is coming). Japan 2022 was the former; the BoJ hike of 2024 was the latter, and the difference was thirty yen.

**You expect crises to cluster and correlate.** When the yen-carry unwind hit in August 2024, it did not stay in FX: the VIX spiked to 65.7 and equities, peso, and tech sold off together. A carry unwind is a deleveraging event, and in a deleveraging everything that was funded by the same cheap money sells at once — the [when-correlations-go-to-one post](/blog/trading/cross-asset/when-correlations-go-to-one-in-a-crisis) is the cross-asset version of this story. The franc, the yen, the gilt, and EM are nominally separate markets; in a stress, they are one trade unwinding.

## The takeaway

Read nine years of currency blow-ups and the same sentence keeps writing itself: an exchange rate is the relative price of two monies, what moves it most is the gap between two countries' interest rates plus the flow of money across borders, and the trades that break are the ones built on the *old* gap when a *new* one arrives. The franc broke when the SNB decided defending its euro-buying peg through ECB QE cost more than abandoning it. The yen fell when the US-Japan gap exploded, and rose again when the BoJ finally narrowed it. The pound collapsed when its own government spent its fiscal credibility, and the LDI spiral underneath was a crowded leveraged trade breaking on a rate move. Emerging markets bled because the dollar — the tide under all of it — went out. And the 2024 yen unwind closed the loop, the most crowded trade on earth detonating the instant its rate gap began to close.

So when you look at any currency, do not ask whether it is "strong" or "weak" in isolation — there is no such thing, because every currency is a pair. Ask three questions instead. What is the rate gap, and is it stable or moving? How crowded is the trade that the current gap has spawned — is everyone on the same side, levered, collecting the same quiet carry? And where is the broad dollar in its cycle, because that tide decides whether this year is calm or a crisis. If the gap is wide and stable and the dollar is calm, you are in the carry regime, earning pennies and stacking gap risk you cannot see. The skill is not predicting the exact day the steamroller arrives. It is knowing, every quiet day, that you are standing in front of one — and sizing the position so that when the rate gap moves, you are still solvent on the other side. The traders wiped out in 2015, 2022, and 2024 were not wrong about the carry. They were wrong about the gap.
