> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The carry trade pays a thin, dependable premium most of the time, and then — rarely, violently — hands back years of gains in a single week. That is its defining feature, not a bug: carry returns are *negatively skewed*.
>
> - You earn the **rate gap** (the difference between the high-yield currency you hold and the cheap one you borrowed) day after day. It feels like free money.
> - The risk is not in the gap. It is in the **spot move** — and when the funding currency snaps back, leverage turns a 12% move into a wipeout.
> - Crashes are not random. A **funder policy shift**, a **risk-off shock**, or a **narrowing gap** lights the fuse, and a **deleveraging cascade** does the rest: vol up, margin calls, forced unwind, funder rallies, more margin calls.
> - The one number to remember: **USD/JPY fell from 161.9 to 141.7 — about 12.5% — in the five weeks to August 5, 2024**, and the most levered carry books lost far more than that.

On the morning of Monday, August 5, 2024, traders in Tokyo watched a number that was not supposed to move quickly move very quickly indeed. The dollar bought 161.9 yen on July 3. By the time the panic peaked five weeks later it bought 141.7. That is a 12.5% move in the world's most heavily traded currency pair — a pair so deep and so liquid that a 1% day is a notable event. Japan's equity market, the Nikkei, fell 12.4% in a single session, its worst day since 1987. The VIX, Wall Street's fear gauge, briefly touched 65.7 intraday, a level it had only ever reached during the 2008 financial crisis and the 2020 pandemic crash.

Nothing had "happened" in the way crises usually announce themselves. There was no bank failure on August 5, no sovereign default, no war. The Bank of Japan had simply raised its policy rate by a quarter of a point on July 31 — from roughly 0.1% to 0.25% — and signalled it might raise again. That tiny move was enough, because for years the cheapest money on earth had been yen, and the world had borrowed it in size to buy almost everything that yielded more. When the yen started to strengthen, the trade that everyone loved began to unwind, and the unwind fed on itself.

![The August-2024 yen-carry unwind from 161.9 to 141.7](/imgs/blogs/carry-crashes-picking-up-pennies-in-front-of-a-steamroller-1.png)

This post is about that pattern — the one that recurs across 1998, 2008 and 2024, across yen and franc and emerging-market currencies, across decades and instruments. The carry trade is the purest expression of this series' spine: *you never own a currency in isolation; every position is a pair, a relative bet on the gap between two countries' interest rates.* Carry is the bet that the gap will keep paying you and the exchange rate will not move enough to take it away. Most of the time you win that bet. The trouble is what happens the rest of the time. As the old trading-floor proverb has it, carry is *picking up pennies in front of a steamroller* — and this is the post about the steamroller.

## Foundations: The anatomy of a carry crash

Before we tell the crash stories, we need three ideas built from zero: what the carry trade actually *is*, what *negative skew* means, and what makes an unwind *cascade*. If you already know the carry mechanics cold, you can skim the first subsection — but the skew and the cascade are where the real insight lives, and most explanations skip straight past them.

### What you are actually holding

A **carry trade** in currencies is simple to state. You borrow a currency with a low interest rate — the **funding currency** or **funder** — and you use the proceeds to hold a currency with a high interest rate — the **high-yield** or **target** currency. The interest-rate difference between the two is your income. Because you never own a currency by itself, every carry trade is a *pair*: long the high-yielder, short the funder, and the relevant variable is the gap between their two rates. (We build the trade itself from first principles in [the carry trade — getting paid to hold a currency](/blog/trading/forex/the-carry-trade-getting-paid-to-hold-a-currency); here we assume you know the mechanics and focus on what goes wrong.)

The classic funders are the yen and the Swiss franc — currencies whose central banks held rates near or below zero for the better part of two decades. The classic targets range from the higher-yielding G10 currencies (the Australian and New Zealand dollars, when their central banks ran tighter policy) all the way out to emerging-market currencies that pay double-digit rates: the Mexican peso, the Brazilian real, the Turkish lira, the South African rand.

The "rate gap" can be enormous. In 2024 the Bank of Japan's policy rate sat at about 0.25% while the Reserve Bank of Australia was at 4.35% and the Fed at 4.50%. Borrow yen at a quarter of a percent, hold US dollars at four and a half, and you bank roughly **4.25% a year** for doing nothing but holding the position open. Hold the Brazilian real at 12.25% or the Turkish lira at 50% instead, and the gap is staggering. (We will not re-derive *why* those rates differ — that is the job of [interest-rate differentials, the master variable of FX](/blog/trading/forex/interest-rate-differentials-the-master-variable-of-fx) and, for the curve mechanics, [fixed income's forward-rates post](/blog/trading/fixed-income/forward-rates-what-the-market-expects-rates-to-be). Here the gap is an input.)

So far this sounds like a money machine. The catch is that your *total* return is not just the rate gap. It is the rate gap **plus the spot move** — whatever the exchange rate does to you while you hold. If you are long the Aussie dollar against the yen and the Aussie falls 5%, that 5% spot loss swamps a year of 4.25% carry income. Carry income is small and steady; the spot move is large and unpredictable. That asymmetry is the entire story.

Notice what this implies about the textbook theory of exchange rates. Economists have a clean prediction called *uncovered interest parity*: the high-yielding currency should, on average, *depreciate* by exactly enough to cancel its interest-rate advantage, so that carry earns nothing in expectation. If that held, there would be no carry trade. In practice it fails — and fails systematically — which is the so-called "forward premium puzzle": high-yielders tend to hold their value or even appreciate for long stretches, so carry *does* earn a positive return most of the time. But the theory is not entirely wrong; it is just wrong about *when*. The depreciation the theory predicts does eventually arrive — it just arrives all at once, in the crash, rather than smoothly, day by day. The crash is uncovered interest parity paying out its entire debt in a single instalment. (Why the parity relation fails in the calm and reasserts itself in the crash is the subject of [uncovered interest parity and why it fails — the forward puzzle](/blog/trading/forex/uncovered-interest-parity-and-why-it-fails-the-forward-puzzle); for this post, the key is that the smooth depreciation the textbook expects is replaced by an occasional violent one.)

### Why the funder is a coiled spring

One more piece of the foundation deserves attention, because it is what makes the funder snap-back so violent. The funding currencies are not chosen at random. They are low-yielding *because* their economies have low rates — typically slow-growing, current-account-surplus, safe-haven economies like Japan and Switzerland. A current-account surplus means the country, in aggregate, earns more abroad than it spends, so its residents are net savers and net owners of foreign assets. In normal times those residents and the global carry community alike are *short* their home currency — they have sold it to hold higher-yielding foreign assets.

That leaves the funder structurally *oversold*. When risk appetite turns, two flows reinforce each other: foreign carry traders buy the funder back to close shorts, and domestic investors repatriate their foreign holdings into the safety of home currency. Both are buying the same currency at the same time, and there is no offsetting natural seller, because everyone who wanted to be short already is. A currency that the whole world is short, in a country that is a net foreign-asset owner, is a coiled spring: it does nothing for years and then releases violently. The yen's three-day surge in 1998 and its five-week surge in 2024 are the spring releasing.

### Negative skew: the shape of the payoff

Here is the concept that the proverb is really about. The distribution of carry returns is **negatively skewed**. In plain numbers: most months you make a little, and the months you lose, you lose a lot. The histogram of monthly returns is not a symmetric bell curve. It is a bell curve with most of the mass clustered just above zero and a long, fat tail stretching deep into the red.

![The shape of a carry payoff: many small wins, one huge loss](/imgs/blogs/carry-crashes-picking-up-pennies-in-front-of-a-steamroller-2.png)

This is the opposite of how most people picture investment risk. We are taught to think of risk as *volatility* — how much a return wobbles around its average. But two investments can have the same volatility and completely different shapes. A lottery ticket has positive skew: you lose a tiny amount almost always, and occasionally win huge. Carry has *negative* skew: you win a tiny amount almost always, and occasionally lose huge. Selling insurance has the same shape — you collect premiums month after month, then pay out catastrophically when the rare disaster strikes. That comparison is exact, not loose: a carry trader is functionally **short volatility**, selling crash insurance to the rest of the market and collecting the rate gap as the premium. (The deep relationship between carry and volatility gets its own treatment in [carry and volatility — the relationship that runs the trade](/blog/trading/forex/carry-and-volatility-the-relationship-that-runs-the-trade); we touch it here because skew and vol are two views of the same object.)

Why does skew matter so much? Because the headline statistics that attract money to carry — a high Sharpe ratio, a smooth equity curve, a good "win rate" — are exactly the statistics that negative skew makes lie. A strategy can show a beautiful upward-sloping return line for years and have a *worse* true risk profile than something that looks choppier, because all of its risk is hidden in a tail that simply has not arrived yet. The smoothness is not evidence of safety. It is the calm of the steamroller idling.

### The unwind mechanic: why it cascades

The third foundation is the mechanism that turns a bad week into a crash. A normal market move is self-limiting: prices fall, the asset gets cheaper, bargain hunters step in, and the move stops. A carry unwind is the opposite — it is **self-reinforcing**, because the people holding the trade are forced to sell *more* precisely as the price moves *against* them. Three features make this happen, and they compound.

First, **leverage**. Carry's per-trade return is small — a few percent a year — so to make it worth doing, players borrow heavily against it. A hedge fund might run a carry book at five or ten times its capital. That magnifies the income, but it also magnifies the loss, and it ties the position to a margin account that demands more collateral the moment the trade loses money.

Second, **crowding**. Because the trade is mechanical and the logic is obvious, *everybody* does the same carry trades at once. The same handful of funders (yen, franc) and the same handful of targets. When the unwind starts, there is no diversity of opinion to absorb it — every holder is trying to exit the identical position through the same door at the same moment.

Third, **the funder snap-back**. To put on a yen-funded carry you *sold* yen (to buy the high-yielder). To exit, you must *buy* yen back. When thousands of leveraged players unwind at once, that synchronized buy-back drives the funding currency sharply *up* — which is exactly the spot move that inflicts the loss. The act of exiting the trade is the thing that makes the trade lose money. This is the engine of the cascade.

![The deleveraging cascade that makes an unwind feed on itself](/imgs/blogs/carry-crashes-picking-up-pennies-in-front-of-a-steamroller-3.png)

Read the loop in the figure as a circle, because that is what it is. A shock spikes volatility. Higher volatility breaches risk limits — a fund's value-at-risk model says it is now carrying too much risk, so the desk *must* cut the position. Cutting the position means buying back the funder, which pushes the funder up, which inflicts mark-to-market losses on everyone else still holding, which spikes volatility again, which breaches more limits. Each lap of the loop forces more selling. It stops only when enough leverage has been violently washed out that there is nobody left to be margin-called — capitulation. That is why carry crashes are fast and deep rather than slow and shallow: the selling is not a choice, it is a margin clerk's instruction, and the instruction gets louder the more the price falls.

#### Worked example: the rate gap you bank

Let us make the "pennies" concrete with one clean number before we get to the steamroller. Suppose you hold a **\$1,000,000** position long the US dollar funded in yen at the 2024 rate gap. The Fed pays about 4.50% on dollars; you borrow yen at 0.25%. Your annual carry income is the gap on the full notional:

- Rate gap: 4.50% − 0.25% = **4.25%**.
- On \$1,000,000: 4.25% × \$1,000,000 = **\$42,500 per year**, or about **\$3,540 per month**, or roughly **\$163 per business day**.

That \$163 a day arrives quietly and reliably, like rent. There is no drama, no headline, no decision to make — you simply hold the position and the gap accrues. *This is the penny: a small, steady, almost boring stream of income that lulls the trader into treating the position as safe.* Nothing in the daily P&L warns you about what the spot move can do.

## The skew, quantified: why the average return lies

The single most important — and least intuitive — fact about carry is that its *average* return tells you almost nothing about its *risk*. Skew is the reason. Let us look at what a long-run carry index actually does, because the picture is the argument.

A stylised G10 carry index, with all the funding-and-target legs bundled together and total return rebased to 100 in 2007, grinds upward for years at a time and then plunges. It is not a smooth line with occasional dips; it is a staircase up interrupted by elevator shafts down. The drops cluster in specific, nameable weeks — late 2008, mid-2011, January 2015, August 2024 — and in each the index gives back a year or more of patient accumulation in days.

The trader's instinct is to look at the long upward slope and conclude the strategy is good. The skew-aware reading is the reverse: the long upward slope is *how the strategy lures capital in*, and the cliffs are *where the strategy collects what it is owed*. The two are not separate — the calm periods are when the carry trader is being paid the insurance premium, and the cliffs are when the claims come due. You cannot have the premium without the tail. They are the same trade viewed at different times.

This is why measuring carry with tools built for symmetric returns is so dangerous. A Sharpe ratio divides excess return by volatility, and volatility — standard deviation — treats an upside wobble and a downside wobble identically. But carry's upside wobbles are tiny and its downside wobbles are enormous, so the Sharpe ratio systematically *overstates* how attractive carry is. The same blindness afflicts the maximum-drawdown statistic computed over too short a window: a carry book that has not yet met its crash looks pristine, because the worst thing that can happen to it simply has not happened yet. The absence of a crash in the sample is not evidence of safety. It is a sampling accident.

#### Worked example: the Sharpe ratio that flatters a time bomb

Put numbers on it. Suppose a levered carry book earns a steady **+0.7% per month** for 23 months with very little month-to-month wobble — say a monthly standard deviation of just 0.9%. Annualised, that is an excess return near 8.4% against an annualised volatility near 3.1%, for a **Sharpe ratio of roughly 2.7** — a number so good that any allocator would back up the truck.

Now month 24 arrives: the crash. The book loses **−22%** in a single month. Recompute over the full two years:

- Cumulative simple return: about (1.007)²³ × (1 − 0.22) − 1 ≈ 1.174 × 0.78 − 1 ≈ **−8.4%** over 24 months — the book is now *underwater* despite 23 winning months.
- Volatility explodes once the −22% month enters the sample, and the realised Sharpe collapses from 2.7 toward — and below — zero.

*The lesson is brutal and exact: the very smoothness that produced the 2.7 Sharpe was the warning sign, not the reassurance. A negatively-skewed strategy looks best right before it looks worst.*

## Why carry pays at all: the risk premium for holding the tail

If carry crashes are so brutal, a fair question is why anyone holds the trade — and why, over the very long run, it has nonetheless made money. The answer is the cleanest way to understand the whole strategy, and it reframes the crash from a bug into the price of doing business.

Carry pays because somebody has to bear the tail risk, and bearing unpleasant risk is what you get paid for in markets. Think of two parties. A Japanese pension fund, a corporate treasurer, an investor who wants the safety of a strong, low-yielding currency in a crisis — these are natural *buyers* of yen and franc, the funding currencies. They are willing to accept a low yield (or even pay) for an asset that rallies when the world is on fire. On the other side, the carry trader is willing to be *short* that safe-haven currency — to sell the crash protection — in exchange for the rate gap. The rate gap is, in effect, the insurance premium the safe-haven buyer pays the carry trader for taking the tail off their hands.

Seen this way, the long-run positive return on carry is not a market inefficiency that will be arbitraged away. It is a *risk premium*: persistent compensation for holding an exposure that pays steadily and then occasionally inflicts a large, correlated loss exactly when investors can least afford it — in the depths of a global risk-off panic, when everything else they own is also falling. An exposure that hurts you most precisely when you are already hurting is worth less than its average return suggests, so the market prices it to offer a *higher* average return as compensation. That extra return is the carry premium. It is real, and it is earned, and it is dangerous, all at the same time — and those three facts are inseparable.

![Pennies in front of a steamroller: the levered carry P&L](/imgs/blogs/carry-crashes-picking-up-pennies-in-front-of-a-steamroller-8.png)

The stack in the figure lays the economics out in order. You borrow at the cheap funding rate; you bank the rate gap as a steady income — the pennies; you wear the crash when the funder snaps back — the steamroller; and the net outcome over a full cycle depends entirely on whether the premium you collected in the calm years exceeds the loss you take in the crash year, *after* the leverage multiplier is applied to both. A sensibly-levered carry book wins that trade-off over time. An over-levered one loses it in a single week, no matter how many years of premium it had banked, because leverage scales the loss without bound while the premium it collected was always finite.

This is the deepest point in the post, so it is worth stating plainly: **the carry premium and the carry crash are not two things. They are one thing.** You cannot keep the premium and shed the crash, any more than an insurer can keep the premiums and refuse to pay claims. Every scheme that claims to give you "carry without the crash" — a clever stop-loss, an options overlay, a diversification trick — is really just changing the *shape* of the tail or the *price* of the premium, never removing it. If a strategy genuinely had carry's steady income and none of its skew, it would not pay a premium at all, because there would be no risk to compensate. The free lunch does not exist; what exists is a fairly-priced insurance business that punishes anyone who forgets they are the insurer.

#### Worked example: who is paying you the carry premium

Make the insurance framing concrete. You sell crash protection by holding a **\$2,000,000** long-Aussie, short-yen carry into 2024, earning the roughly 4.1% AUD–JPY rate gap. Across a calm year that is 4.1% × \$2,000,000 = **\$82,000** of premium income — paid to you, in small daily increments, by all the natural yen-buyers on the other side who wanted the safety you were willing to sell.

- In a calm year, you keep the full **\$82,000**. The pension funds and treasurers who bought yen for safety got a slightly worse yield; you got the gap. Everyone is content.
- In a crash year — the August-2024 cascade — the yen surges 12% and your \$2,000,000 position loses 0.12 × \$2,000,000 = **\$240,000** of spot, against \$82,000 of premium. Net **−\$158,000**. The safe-haven buyers you sold protection to are now the ones being made whole: their yen rallied exactly when they needed it to.

*That is the carry premium in one cycle — you are paid \$82,000 a year to stand ready to lose \$240,000 in a crash, and the trade is "fair" only if crashes are rare enough that the premiums add up faster than the claims. Run it un-levered and patiently and the maths works; run it at 5× and one claim erases a decade of premiums.*

## The August-2024 yen unwind, step by step

The cleanest modern case study is the one we opened with, because it happened in slow enough motion to watch the mechanism work, and in a market deep enough that there was no ambiguity about what moved.

The setup had been building for two years. From 2021 to mid-2024 the Bank of Japan held its policy rate near zero while the Fed hiked aggressively to fight inflation, pushing the US two-year yield more than four percentage points above its Japanese equivalent. That gap is the rocket fuel of yen carry. The yen weakened relentlessly as money flowed out of low-yielding yen into high-yielding dollars — USD/JPY climbed from about 115 at end-2021 to 131 in 2022, to 143 in 2023, and to an intraday high near 161.9 in early July 2024. (The mechanism by which a rate gap drags a currency is [interest-rate differentials, the master variable of FX](/blog/trading/forex/interest-rate-differentials-the-master-variable-of-fx); the policy side is [how monetary policy moves currencies](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-monetary-policy-moves-currencies-rate-differentials).)

By July 2024 the yen-carry trade was extraordinarily crowded. Hedge funds, Japanese retail "Mrs. Watanabe" accounts, and global macro books were all short yen against everything that yielded more — dollars, Mexican pesos, equities. Estimates of the aggregate position ran into the hundreds of billions of dollars. Everyone was on the same side of the same boat.

Then the funder turned. On July 31 the Bank of Japan raised its policy rate to 0.25% — a microscopic hike in absolute terms — and Governor Ueda signalled more to come. At nearly the same moment, weak US data and a soft jobs report on August 2 made the market suddenly expect *faster* Fed cuts. Both blades of the scissors closed at once: the yen's rate was going up while the dollar's was going down, so the gap that justified the whole trade started to shrink. The yen, which had only ever weakened, began to strengthen — and a strengthening funder is the carry trader's nightmare.

The cascade did the rest. USD/JPY fell from 161.9 (July 3) to 157.8, to 153.9, to 150.0 by July 31, then 146.5 on August 2 and 141.7 on August 5 — a 12.5% strengthening of the yen in five weeks, with most of the violence in the final three days. The selling was forced, not chosen: as the yen rallied, every leveraged short-yen position took a mark-to-market loss, breached its risk limits, and was made to buy yen back, which drove the yen up further. On August 5 the VIX touched 65.7 intraday and the Nikkei fell over 12%. Then, almost as fast, it stabilised — by August 12 USD/JPY had bounced to 147.2 — because the deleveraging had largely burned itself out. The leverage was washed; the trade had been emptied of its weak hands.

![Carry crashes: peak-to-trough of the trade in five unwinds](/imgs/blogs/carry-crashes-picking-up-pennies-in-front-of-a-steamroller-4.png)

Notice in the crash table that the August-2024 unwind, dramatic as it was, was actually one of the *smaller* carry drawdowns — about −12% — compared with the −28% of 1998 and the −30% of 2008. That is a useful calibration: a 12% peak-to-trough that produced a once-in-a-decade VIX spike and a 1987-magnitude equity day was, by carry-crash standards, *moderate*. The tail can be much fatter than 2024, and it has been.

#### Worked example: a \$1,000,000 levered carry that earned 6%, then lost 20% in a week

Now the steamroller. Take the calm carry book from before and add what every real carry desk adds: **leverage**. Suppose you put up **\$1,000,000** of your own capital and run a yen-funded carry at **5× leverage** — a \$5,000,000 notional position. Walk through a year that ends in an unwind.

- **The pennies.** On \$5,000,000 of notional, the 4.25% rate gap earns 4.25% × \$5,000,000 = **\$212,500** of carry income in a year — which on your \$1,000,000 of capital is a **+21.25% return** before any spot move. Add a gentle favourable drift in the pair and your reported return for eleven months looks like a smooth **+6% and rising** on capital, with barely a down week. The allocator is delighted.
- **The steamroller.** Then the unwind hits and the funder snaps back: the pair moves 12% against you in a single week — close to the actual August-2024 spot move. On \$5,000,000 of notional, a 12% adverse spot move is a loss of 0.12 × \$5,000,000 = **\$600,000**.
- **On your capital, that is a −60% hit in a week** (\$600,000 lost against \$1,000,000 of equity), against carry income for the year of only \$212,500. Net, you are down hundreds of thousands of dollars on the year — and if the move had been the −20% of a 1998-style unwind, the \$1,000,000 loss on notional would have **wiped out your entire capital** in days.

*This is the whole post in one example: leverage turns the small, steady rate gap into a real income stream and turns a moderate spot move into a full wipeout, because the same multiplier sits in front of both.* The pennies and the steamroller are the same leverage looked at on a calm day versus a crash day.

## The deleveraging cascade in detail

It is worth slowing down on exactly why the unwind is so much faster and deeper than ordinary selling, because this is the part traders most often underestimate when they put the trade on. The mechanism has four amplifiers, and they multiply rather than add.

**Amplifier one: value-at-risk and forced selling.** Modern risk management runs on value-at-risk (VaR) — a model estimate of how much a book can lose on a bad day, scaled by recent volatility. When volatility spikes, the *same* position suddenly registers as far riskier, so the model orders the desk to cut it to stay within its risk budget. This is mechanical: it does not care that the trade is now cheap, or that the fundamentals are unchanged. Higher vol means smaller permitted position means forced selling — and the forced selling is what spikes vol further. The risk model that is supposed to protect the fund becomes the accelerant.

**Amplifier two: margin calls and the funding squeeze.** A leveraged carry is financed by a prime broker who holds collateral. When the trade loses money, the broker issues a margin call demanding more collateral *now*. A fund that cannot meet it must liquidate positions immediately — and often liquidates its *most liquid* positions first (which may be entirely unrelated assets) to raise cash, transmitting the carry stress into equities, credit and gold. This is how a yen move becomes a global risk-off event: the margin call does not respect asset-class boundaries. (The cross-asset transmission is the subject of [when correlations go to one in a crisis](/blog/trading/cross-asset/when-correlations-go-to-one-in-a-crisis).)

**Amplifier three: the option-replication feedback.** Many carry-adjacent strategies — and many of the structured products that retail and institutions buy for "enhanced yield" — are implicitly short options on the funding currency. When the funder moves sharply, the dealers who sold those products must hedge by buying the funder, in the same direction as everyone else, which adds another wave of mechanical demand on top of the unwind. The vol surface itself feeds the cascade. (The skew in FX options that prices this fear is covered in [trading skew — risk reversals, collars, and the shape of fear](/blog/trading/options-volatility/trading-skew-risk-reversals-collars-and-the-shape-of-fear).)

**Amplifier four: the liquidity hole.** In calm markets, FX is the deepest market on earth and you can move size at razor-thin spreads. In an unwind, the market-makers widen spreads and pull quotes precisely because they can see the one-way flow coming and do not want to be run over. So at the exact moment everyone needs to exit, the door narrows. Realised volatility and the cost of trading both explode together, and the position you thought you could exit in seconds takes an hour and a brutal price.

![Every carry crash is a volatility crash: VIX at the panic high](/imgs/blogs/carry-crashes-picking-up-pennies-in-front-of-a-steamroller-7.png)

The figure makes the cross-episode point: every carry crash is, at its core, a *volatility* crash. The VIX printed 45.7 in the 1998 LTCM/Russia episode, 80.9 in 2008, 82.7 in the 2020 pandemic, and 65.7 in the August-2024 yen unwind — each a multiple of the long-run "calm" level near 19.5. This is not a coincidence; it is definitional. Carry is short volatility, so the carry crash and the volatility spike are the same event seen from two sides. When vol explodes, the carry trader is the one writing the cheque.

There is a timing wrinkle here that traders consistently get wrong, and it is worth dwelling on because it explains why so many experienced people get caught. The volatility spike and the carry crash happen *together*, not in sequence. There is no comfortable window in which volatility rises, you observe it, and you calmly reduce your position before the loss. The vol spike *is* the forced selling, and the forced selling *is* your loss — they are simultaneous, because in a crowded trade your attempt to exit is itself part of the wave that moves the price. By the time the VIX is printing 65, the damage is already on your book; the high VIX is a *coincident* indicator of the crash, not a *leading* one. The only protection that works is the protection you put on *before* vol was elevated — when it was cheap and nobody wanted it — which is precisely when the carry was paying best and the temptation to skip the insurance was strongest.

There is also a feedback path from the funding markets that deepens the hole. A carry unwind is, mechanically, a sudden global scramble for the funding currency — everyone needs to buy yen or francs or, in the broader crisis case, dollars, all at once. That scramble shows up in the *cross-currency basis*, the premium institutions pay to swap into the scarce currency, which blows out to deeply negative levels in every crisis (it hit roughly −220 basis points in USD/JPY in October 2008 and around −145 in the March-2020 dash for cash). A widening basis makes it more expensive to fund the very position you are trying to hold or exit, which is one more turn of the screw. (The plumbing of that funding squeeze is the cross-currency basis, a dollar-funding stress gauge that we cover separately; here it is enough to know that the funder becomes scarce and expensive at the worst possible moment.)

#### Worked example: the leverage that turns a 12% spot move into a wipeout

Let us isolate the leverage arithmetic, because it is the hinge of the whole strategy and most traders carry the wrong intuition about it. Hold the spot move fixed at the August-2024 figure — a **12% adverse move** in the pair — and vary only the leverage on **\$1,000,000** of capital:

- **No leverage (1×):** \$1,000,000 notional. A 12% spot loss = \$120,000, a **−12%** hit to capital. Painful but survivable; the carry income offsets part of it.
- **3× leverage:** \$3,000,000 notional. A 12% spot loss = \$360,000, a **−36%** hit to capital. A bad year, but you are still in business.
- **5× leverage:** \$5,000,000 notional. A 12% spot loss = \$600,000, a **−60%** hit to capital. You have lost more than half your fund in a week.
- **8× leverage:** \$8,000,000 notional. A 12% spot loss = \$960,000, a **−96%** hit to capital. You are, for practical purposes, **wiped out** — and a 1998-style −20% move at 8× ( \$1,600,000 of loss on \$1,000,000 of capital) leaves you *insolvent*, owing your broker more than you had.

*The point is that leverage does not change the rate gap you earn relative to the spot risk you take — it scales both by the same factor, but the spot risk is the one with the fat tail. At high enough leverage, a single ordinary-sized crash week is not a drawdown, it is the end of the fund.* This is why "blow-up" is the natural state of an over-levered carry book: not because the trade is wrong, but because the leverage that makes the pennies worth picking up is exactly the leverage that lets the steamroller finish you.

## Common misconceptions

Carry attracts a particular set of comforting beliefs, each of which has gotten somebody hurt. Here are the five worth correcting with numbers.

**"The carry income is a cushion against losses."** It is a cushion against a *gentle* spot move and useless against a crash. In our 5× example, a full year of carry income was \$212,500 while one crash week cost \$600,000 — the cushion absorbed about a third of one week's loss. Carry income protects you against the weather; it does nothing against the earthquake. Treating it as a buffer is precisely the mistake that lets traders run too much leverage: the steady accrual feels like a safety margin, so the desk sizes up to "use" it, and the larger position is then exactly the thing that turns the next crash from a bruise into a fatality. The cushion does not just fail to help — believing in it actively makes the eventual loss worse, because it is the reason the position was too big in the first place.

**"A high Sharpe ratio means low risk."** The Sharpe ratio measures return per unit of *volatility*, and carry's risk lives in *skew*, which the Sharpe ratio cannot see. A carry book showing a 2.7 Sharpe over two calm years can go to a deeply negative full-period return the moment the crash month lands, as we computed above. A great Sharpe on a negatively-skewed strategy is a description of how long it has been since the last crash, not of how safe the strategy is.

**"You can stop-loss your way out."** Stop-losses assume you can exit at roughly the price you set. In an unwind the liquidity hole means the market gaps *through* your stop — in January 2015 EUR/CHF gapped from 1.20 to below 0.90 in minutes when the Swiss National Bank abandoned its floor, so a stop set at 1.18 filled near 1.00, not 1.18. The discontinuity is the whole problem: a stop protects you against a slide, not against a gap, and carry crashes are gaps.

**"This time the trade is different / safer."** Every crowded carry has a story for why it is safe — the central bank is committed, the peg will hold, the yield gap is structural. The 2015 Swiss case is the cleanest rebuttal: for years the SNB defended a EUR/CHF floor at 1.20 and the market treated short-franc carry as nearly riskless, right up to the morning the SNB walked away and the franc surged 30%. The safety was an illusion priced into the trade until it wasn't. (The franc episode is dissected in [2015 to 2022 — the franc, the yen, and the EM stress cycle](/blog/trading/forex/2015-to-2022-the-franc-the-yen-and-the-em-stress-cycle).)

**"Diversifying across many carry pairs removes the risk."** It removes the *idiosyncratic* risk and leaves the risk that actually matters. All carry trades share one exposure — the global risk appetite that determines whether leverage expands or contracts. In a true unwind, every high-yielder falls against every funder at once; the correlations that looked low in calm times go to one. A book of twenty "diversified" carry pairs in August 2024 was, in the crash, a single position twenty times over. The diversification was real in the calm — and exactly zero in the moment it was needed.

**"Central-bank intervention will save the trade."** Sometimes a central bank does step in — Japan's Ministry of Finance spent an estimated \$36 billion in April 2024 and more in July trying to slow the yen's *fall*, and in a crash the same authorities may try to slow the funder's *rise*. But intervention smooths, it does not reverse, a move driven by a genuine deleveraging cascade. A central bank can lean against a one-way market for a while; it cannot stand in front of the entire world repatriating and deleveraging at once, and it usually does not try, because a stronger funding currency in a panic is often what its own economy needs. Counting on the cavalry is one more way of refusing to size for the tail. The franc traders who assumed the SNB would defend 1.20 forever learned this the hardest possible way: the central bank's job is its own economy, not your carry position, and the day those two diverge, your position loses.

## How it shows up in real markets

The August-2024 unwind was not new. It was the latest instance of a pattern the market has run, with the same mechanics and different protagonists, for at least three decades. Three episodes deserve the full telling, because together they define the genre.

### 1998: Russia, LTCM, and the three-day yen collapse

The summer of 1998 was a carry trader's paradise turning into a graveyard. Through the mid-1990s, with Japanese rates pinned near zero, the yen-funded carry was the trade of choice for hedge funds and the Wall Street proprietary desks. Borrow yen cheaply, buy higher-yielding assets — including, fatefully, Russian government debt (GKOs) paying enormous yields, and a sprawling set of relative-value bets run at colossal leverage by Long-Term Capital Management, the fund staffed with Nobel laureates whose models said their positions were nearly riskless.

On August 17, 1998, Russia defaulted on its domestic debt and devalued the ruble — which collapsed from about 6 to 21 per dollar. The default detonated the carry complex. As losses mounted, leveraged players had to raise cash and cut risk everywhere at once, and the single biggest funding trade to unwind was the yen carry. The buy-back of yen was so violent that USD/JPY collapsed from 136 to 112 over three trading days in early October — roughly a **15% move in 72 hours**, an almost unthinkable shock in a major pair. LTCM, leveraged more than 25-to-1 against positions premised on calm and convergence, lost almost all of its \$4.6 billion of capital and had to be rescued by a Fed-organised consortium of banks to prevent its forced liquidation from taking the financial system down with it.

![October 1998: USD/JPY fell from 136 to 112 in three trading days](/imgs/blogs/carry-crashes-picking-up-pennies-in-front-of-a-steamroller-5.png)

The 1998 lesson is the foundational one: the carry crash, the leverage blow-up, and the funder snap-back are a single phenomenon. LTCM did not fail because its individual trades were wrong — many of them eventually made money for the banks that took them over. It failed because it was leveraged into a crowded, negatively-skewed set of positions and the tail arrived before the convergence did. The carry book was the transmission belt that turned a Russian default into a global deleveraging. (The full LTCM story is told in [LTCM 1998 — when genius failed](/blog/trading/finance/ltcm-1998-when-genius-failed) and, from the carry angle, in [1998, Russia, LTCM and the FX tremor that shook the world](/blog/trading/forex/1998-russia-ltcm-and-the-fx-tremor-that-shook-the-world).)

### 2008: the global financial crisis

The 2008 carry crash was the largest and most globally synchronised of all — the carry index drew down about 30% peak-to-trough, the deepest in the modern record. Through 2003–2007, with developed-market rates low and emerging-market growth booming, the carry trade had been enormous and global: yen and franc funding poured into Australian and New Zealand dollars, into Icelandic króna paying double digits, into a wide spread of emerging-market currencies and the high-yielding structured products built on top of them. Iceland's entire economy had become, in effect, a leveraged carry trade financed in foreign currency.

When the US mortgage crisis metastasised into a global funding panic in 2008, the deleveraging hit every carry trade at once. The franc and especially the yen surged as the funding trades unwound — USD/JPY fell from around 110 in mid-2008 toward the high 80s, and EUR/JPY (a pure carry barometer, long the higher-yielding euro against the funding yen) collapsed by roughly a third. The Australian dollar, the archetypal commodity-and-carry currency, fell from above parity with the US dollar to near 0.60 — a ~40% drawdown — as both its carry appeal and its commodity-export story evaporated together. Iceland's currency and banking system simply failed. The VIX hit 80.9. Every amplifier from the cascade section fired simultaneously, and the carry complex was at the heart of the FX side of the crisis.

The 2008 lesson is about correlation. In normal times the carry currencies look diversified — the Aussie has its own commodity story, the krona its own, the emerging-markets their own. In the crash they revealed themselves to be a single trade: a bet on global risk appetite and the availability of cheap leverage. When that one factor turned, the "diversified" carry portfolio behaved like one enormous concentrated position, which is exactly what it was. The Iceland story is the most vivid illustration: a tiny economy that had effectively become a leveraged carry vehicle — households borrowed in francs and yen to buy local assets, banks funded themselves in foreign currency to lend at home — discovered in 2008 that when the funding currencies surged and foreign credit vanished simultaneously, there was no domestic backstop large enough. The currency, the banks and the carry trade failed as a single connected object, because they had always been one.

### August 2024: the yen unwind, revisited as a pattern

We have already walked the August-2024 mechanics, so here we place it in the lineage. What is striking is how *little* had to happen for the cascade to start. There was no default, no bank failure, no systemic event — just a 0.15-point BoJ hike, a soft US jobs print, and the realisation that the rate gap had peaked. That was enough, because the trade was crowded and levered to the point where it only needed a nudge in the wrong direction to begin the self-reinforcing loop.

That fragility is the modern lesson. As the carry complex grows and concentrates, the size of the trigger needed to set off a crash *shrinks*. A bigger, more crowded, more levered trade is a more sensitive trade: it needs less to tip it. The August-2024 episode was, by historical standards, a moderate crash — −12% versus 1998's −28% and 2008's −30% — yet it produced a once-in-fifteen-years VIX spike from a trigger that, in a less crowded market, would barely have registered. The steamroller did not need to move far to do damage, because the pennies had been stacked so high in front of it. (The full modern arc, with the volmageddon connection, lives in [2015 to 2022 — the franc, the yen, and the EM stress cycle](/blog/trading/forex/2015-to-2022-the-franc-the-yen-and-the-em-stress-cycle); the unwind family across decades is catalogued in [carry-trade unwinds 1998, 2008, 2024 — when leverage breaks](/blog/trading/macro-trading/carry-trade-unwinds-1998-2008-2024-when-leverage-breaks).)

![The crash trigger map: what lights the fuse on a carry unwind](/imgs/blogs/carry-crashes-picking-up-pennies-in-front-of-a-steamroller-6.png)

Step back and the three episodes share one skeleton, shown in the trigger map. Something collapses the rate gap into irrelevance — a funder that turns (the BoJ hike, the SNB de-peg), a risk-off shock that makes everyone deleverage at once (Russia's default, Lehman's failure), or a gap that narrows because the high-yield central bank stops hiking or the funder starts. The trigger differs; the cascade that follows is always the same. Once you can see the skeleton, every "new" carry crash looks like an old one wearing different clothes.

### The emerging-market version: the same crash, with worse liquidity

The G10 episodes above all involve major, deeply-liquid currencies. The emerging-market carry trade follows the identical logic but with the tail made fatter by thin liquidity and weaker institutions. EM currencies pay the biggest rate gaps — the Mexican peso at over 10%, the Brazilian real above 12%, the Turkish lira near 50% in recent years — precisely because they carry the most risk of a sudden, large depreciation. The premium is bigger because the steamroller is bigger.

The EM carry crash has its own canonical triggers. The 2013 "taper tantrum" is the textbook case: when the Fed merely *hinted* it would slow its bond-buying, capital that had chased EM yield reversed violently, and the so-called "Fragile Five" currencies — the rupee, real, rand, lira and rupiah — sold off hard in weeks. Portfolio flows into EM, which had run at roughly \$250 billion in 2012, swung to *outflows* in stress years. The mechanism is the deleveraging cascade again, but with a crucial difference: EM currencies often trade through onshore restrictions and non-deliverable forwards, so when everyone runs for the exit, the liquidity hole is not a temporary widening of spreads — it can be a near-total absence of any bid at all. A G10 carry crash gaps a few percent through your stop; an EM carry crash can gap tens of percent, and sometimes the central bank slams a window shut entirely. The 1997 Asian crisis is the extreme: the Thai baht, Korean won and Indonesian rupiah lost 54%, 54% and 83% against the dollar respectively when their pegs broke and the carry into them reversed. Sizing for the tail matters even more in EM, because the tail is both fatter and harder to exit.

### How to read — and survive — a carry position

The practical payoff of all this is a short checklist for sizing and reading carry, whether you are running the trade or just trying to understand what the market is doing.

First, **size to the crash, not to the carry**. The right question is never "how much income does this earn?" but "what happens to my capital if the funder moves 15% against me next week?" Pick your leverage so that the answer to the second question is *survivable* — a bad year, not the end. From the leverage worked example, that almost always means running far less leverage than the smooth income stream tempts you toward. A carry book sized so that a 1998-style move is a −30% drawdown rather than a wipeout has bought itself the right to be wrong and come back.

Second, **watch the crowd and the gap, not the income**. The danger rises as the trade gets more crowded and the rate gap stops widening. A carry that everyone owns, at a gap that has peaked, is a carry one trigger away from the cascade. Positioning surveys, the size of speculative short-funder positions, and the slope of the relevant central banks' policy paths tell you more about your risk than your accrued P&L does.

Third, **respect what volatility is pricing**. Cheap implied volatility and a flat options skew on a crowded, levered carry are the market offering you crash insurance at a giveaway price — which is the moment to *buy* it, not to sell more carry. The cheapest time to hedge is exactly when it feels least necessary, because that is when the premium has lulled everyone into complacency. Carry's relationship with the vol surface is the single best early-warning system the trade has, and it is the subject of its own post in this series.

## The takeaway

Carry is not a free lunch and it is not a scam. It is an *insurance business*. You collect a steady premium — the rate gap — for bearing a risk that is small most of the time and catastrophic rarely. Run sensibly, with modest leverage and an honest accounting of the tail, that insurance business has a real, defensible edge; it has paid a positive long-run return precisely *because* people demand compensation for holding a negatively-skewed exposure. The premium is the compensation. The crash is what you are being compensated for.

So the way to read a carry position — your own, or the market's — is not to ask "how much am I earning?" That is the penny, and the penny is reliable and boring and tells you nothing about the danger. The right questions are about the steamroller. *How crowded is the trade?* A trade everyone owns has no buyers when it breaks. *How much leverage is stacked on it?* Leverage scales the rate gap and the tail by the same factor, but only one of them has a fat left side. *What would make the funder snap back?* A funder that has only ever weakened is a funder whose reversal nobody is positioned for. And *what is volatility pricing?* Cheap vol on a crowded, levered carry is the market selling you crash insurance at the worst possible moment to be the seller.

This connects straight back to the series' spine. An exchange rate is the relative price of two monies, and the carry trade is the purest bet that the gap between two countries' interest rates will keep paying you faster than the exchange rate can take it away. Most days, it does. The discipline is to hold that trade knowing — in your position sizing, not just in the back of your mind — that the day it doesn't, it will not take it away gently. It will take it away all at once. Pick up the pennies if the premium is real and you have sized for the steamroller. Just never forget which one you are standing in front of.

And there is a broader use for this even if you never put on a carry trade in your life. The carry crash is one of the market's most reliable transmission mechanisms — the reason a small policy move in Tokyo can crater equities in New York and gold in London on the same morning. When you next see a "risk-off" day with no obvious headline, a strengthening yen or franc, a spiking VIX, and a synchronised sell-off across unrelated assets, you are very likely watching a carry unwind in progress. The trade that pays the quietest premium in the calm is the one that most often writes the loudest cheque in the storm. Knowing how it breaks tells you not just how to trade carry, but how to read the days when everything moves at once and nobody can quite say why.
