> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The "dollar smile" says the US dollar is strong at *both* ends of the global growth spectrum and weak only in the calm middle. Plot dollar strength on the vertical axis and the state of the world on the horizontal axis and you get a smile: two raised corners, a sagging centre.
>
> - **Left cheek (panic):** when the world is scared, everyone scrambles for dollars — to repay dollar debt, to hold the safest asset, to settle margin calls. The dollar rallies *because* everything else is falling.
> - **Right cheek (US outperformance):** when America grows faster and the Fed hikes harder than everyone else, the rate gap widens and global capital chases US yield and growth into the dollar.
> - **The weak middle:** when growth is calm and *synchronised* worldwide, money leaves the safe, low-extra-return dollar to chase higher returns abroad, and the dollar sags.
> - The one fact to remember: a strong dollar is **not** a bet on the US economy *or* a bet on disaster — it can be either. The dollar wins in boom and in panic, and loses only when the world is boringly fine. A 5% adverse move on a \$1,000,000 unhedged dollar position is \$50,000 — so knowing which cheek you are on matters more than knowing the level.

On the morning of 23 March 2020, with the COVID crash a week old and equities in free fall, a strange thing was happening in currency markets. The thing the textbooks say is the *cause* of currency strength — a strong economy, rising rates, a confident central bank — was nowhere to be found. The US economy was collapsing. The Federal Reserve had just slashed interest rates to zero. And yet the dollar was screaming higher: the dollar index had jumped from about 95 to nearly 103 in ten trading days, one of the most violent dollar rallies on record. Emerging-market currencies were in free fall, the Brazilian real and the Indonesian rupiah cratering against a greenback that, by every "fundamental" measure, should have been *weakening*.

Rewind two years, to 2022. The story was the opposite — and the dollar did the same thing. Now the US economy was the strongest in the developed world, the Fed was hiking faster than any other major central bank, and the dollar index ripped to 114.8 in September, a twenty-year high. The yen collapsed past 150, the euro broke below parity for the first time in two decades, and the same emerging-market currencies that fell in the 2020 panic fell again in the 2022 boom.

Two episodes, two opposite economic backdrops — a US collapse and a US boom — and the dollar rallied hard in both. To anyone holding the naive model "strong economy equals strong currency", this is a contradiction. It is not. It is the single most useful framework for reading the dollar, and a former IMF economist named Stephen Jen drew it as a picture you already know how to make with your own face. He called it the **dollar smile**.

![The dollar smile curve: USD strong at both ends, weak in the calm middle](/imgs/blogs/the-dollar-smile-why-the-dollar-wins-in-boom-and-in-panic-1.png)

That picture is the whole post in one image. The horizontal axis is the state of the world — global panic on the far left, a serene synchronised global expansion in the middle, roaring US exceptionalism on the far right. The vertical axis is dollar strength. The curve is a smile: high on both cheeks, sagging in the middle. Everything that follows is an explanation of *why* each part of that curve sits where it does, and what it means for how you should read a dollar move when you see one.

Before we go further, the spine of this whole series, which the smile illustrates perfectly: you never own "a currency" in isolation. Every dollar position is a *pair* — a relative bet against something else. When we say "the dollar is strong", we mean it is strong *relative to* a basket of other monies, and a currency's price is driven by the gap between two countries' interest rates plus the flow of money across borders. The smile is a map of when those two forces — the rate gap and the cross-border flow — line up to push money *into* the dollar, and when they push it out.

## Foundations: The dollar smile, the two cheeks, and the dip

Let us define every term from zero, because the framework is simple but the vocabulary trips beginners.

**The dollar.** When traders say "the dollar" as a single thing, they usually mean the dollar's value against a basket of other major currencies. The most common gauge is the **dollar index (DXY)** — a number that rises when the dollar buys more euros, yen, pounds, and so on, and falls when it buys fewer. The DXY is dominated by the euro (about 58% of the basket), so "the dollar is up" mostly means "the dollar is up against the euro", but the idea generalises. We unpack the DXY's quirks in [the DXY and the dollar's special role in the world](/blog/trading/forex/the-dxy-and-the-dollars-special-role-in-the-world); here, just hold "the dollar = the dollar versus everyone else."

**Risk-on and risk-off.** Markets cycle between two moods. In **risk-on**, investors are confident: they buy stocks, lend to risky borrowers, chase high yields in emerging markets, and feel comfortable being un-hedged. In **risk-off**, fear takes over: they sell risky assets, pull money home, and hide in the safest things they can find. The clearest single gauge of this mood is the **VIX** — the "fear index", which measures how much investors are paying to insure stocks against a crash. A low VIX (say 13) means calm; a spike to 65 means panic.

**The rate gap.** A currency pays you to hold it through its interest rate. If US deposits pay 5% and euro deposits pay 3%, the dollar has a 2-percentage-point yield advantage. Money, all else equal, flows toward the higher yield. This *rate differential* is, as the [interest-rate-differentials post](/blog/trading/forex/interest-rate-differentials-the-master-variable-of-fx) argues, the master variable of FX. The rate gap itself comes from the two countries' central banks; the mechanism by which policy moves currencies is owned by the [macro-trading note on rate differentials](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-monetary-policy-moves-currencies-rate-differentials), so we lean on it rather than re-deriving it.

Now the smile itself. Stephen Jen's insight, formulated around 2001 when he was a currency strategist at Morgan Stanley, was that *two completely different forces* both bid the dollar, and they sit at opposite ends of the growth spectrum.

- **The left cheek — risk-off.** When the world is terrified, the dollar is the asset everyone runs to. Not because America is doing well — often it is doing badly — but because the dollar is the world's reserve currency, the unit in which most global debt and trade is denominated, and the deepest, most liquid market to hide in. In a panic, *safety* beats *yield*, and the safest place is the dollar. The smile's left corner is up.

- **The right cheek — US outperformance.** When America grows faster than the rest of the world and the Fed hikes rates higher and sooner, the dollar's yield advantage widens and its growth story attracts capital. Now it is *yield and growth*, not safety, pulling money in. The smile's right corner is up.

- **The dip — the synchronised middle.** When the global economy is expanding *together*, calmly, with no US standout and no panic, the dollar has no special draw. Its yield advantage is thin, there is no fear premium, and investors happily send money abroad to chase the higher returns available in faster-growing or higher-yielding economies. The dollar sags. The smile's middle is low.

![Matrix of the two cheeks: left cheek risk-off and right cheek US outperformance](/imgs/blogs/the-dollar-smile-why-the-dollar-wins-in-boom-and-in-panic-2.png)

The matrix above is the framework's beating heart: two columns, the left cheek and the right cheek, that reach the *same destination* — a bid dollar — by completely opposite roads. Read down each column. The left cheek is triggered by fear, driven by a scramble for safety, indifferent to the rate gap, and feels like a crash. The right cheek is triggered by US strength, driven by capital chasing yield, powered *by* a widening rate gap, and feels like a melt-up. They could not be more different in mood. They produce the same dollar.

This is the single most important thing a beginner gets wrong. A rising dollar is *not* a signal about the US economy. It can mean "America is booming" or it can mean "the world is ending" — and you cannot tell which from the dollar alone. You have to look at *which cheek* you are on, and the way you tell is to look at risk appetite (the VIX) and the rate gap together. We will build that diagnostic step by step.

**Where the framework came from, and why it is a curve.** Stephen Jen formalised the smile around 2001 at Morgan Stanley, but the observation is older than the name — practitioners had long noticed that the dollar seemed to do well in "good times for America" and in "bad times for everyone", and badly in the lukewarm in-between. Jen's contribution was to draw it as a single, continuous function of *global growth* rather than as two unrelated rules of thumb. That continuity is the point: the dollar's strength is not a step-function with a couple of special cases bolted on; it is a smooth U-shape, where as you slide the world from panic on the left toward synchronised calm in the middle and on to US exceptionalism on the right, dollar strength falls, bottoms, and rises again.

Why a *curve* and not a straight line? Because the dollar is a relative price, and the two things that make money flow into it — a yield advantage and a safety premium — are themselves U-shaped in global growth. The safety premium is highest when growth is worst (the left), collapses to nothing when growth is fine (the middle), and stays near nothing when growth is great. The yield advantage is highest when *US* growth is best relative to the world (the right), shrinks toward zero in the synchronised middle, and is also modest on the far left where the Fed is usually cutting in a panic. Add a falling curve (safety) to a rising curve (yield) and the sum is a smile: high on both ends where one of the two forces is strong, low in the middle where both are weak. The smile is not an arbitrary shape someone drew; it is the sum of two opposing forces, each peaking at a different end of the growth spectrum.

**The horizontal axis is "the world", not "America".** This is worth stressing because beginners instinctively read the x-axis as the US economy. It is not. The x-axis is the *global* growth-and-risk backdrop, and America's role on it is *relative*: on the right cheek, what matters is that the US is outperforming, not that it is strong in isolation; on the left cheek, what matters is that the world is frightened, and the US's own condition is almost irrelevant. Read the x-axis as "how is the world doing, and is America the standout?" — never as "is America doing well?" Get that wrong and the entire framework inverts.

#### Worked example: the two cheeks, same P&L

Suppose on 1 January you put on a **\$1,000,000 long-dollar position** — say, long DXY through a basket, or equivalently short a spread of euros, yen, and pounds. You hold it for one quarter. Consider two completely different worlds.

- **World A (left cheek, panic):** a global shock hits in February. The VIX spikes from 14 to 60, equities fall 20%, and the dollar index rallies 5% as the world scrambles for dollars. Your position, which moves with the dollar, gains roughly 5% × \$1,000,000 = **+\$50,000**.
- **World B (right cheek, boom):** no panic at all. Instead, US growth surprises to the upside, the Fed signals more hikes, the US-vs-rest-of-world rate gap widens by a full point, and the dollar index *also* rallies 5%. Your position *also* gains roughly **+\$50,000**.

Same \$50,000 profit, two opposite worlds. If you had been betting on "America is doing badly" you would have made money in World A; if you had been betting on "America is doing great" you would have made money in World B. The dollar paid out either way — and that is exactly why it is so confusing, and so useful. **The lesson: the dollar's strength tells you money is flowing in; it does not tell you why, so always read the VIX and the rate gap alongside the level.**

## The left cheek: why panic bids the dollar

Start with the more counter-intuitive corner. Why on earth would a currency rally when its own economy is in trouble?

The answer is that in a crisis, investors stop asking "which currency will pay me the most?" and start asking "which currency will *still exist and be liquid* tomorrow?" Three mechanisms, all pulling the same way, turn that question into dollar demand.

**Mechanism 1 — the dollar is the world's safe asset.** Roughly 58% of global central-bank reserves are held in dollars (down from 71% in 2001, but still the dominant share — see the [IMF COFER data we use across the series](/blog/trading/macro-trading/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy)). US Treasuries are the deepest, most liquid pool of "safe" debt on the planet. When you are scared and want to hold something that will not vanish, you hold dollars and Treasuries. Demand for the asset means demand for the currency you buy it in.

**Mechanism 2 — the dollar-funding squeeze.** This is the mechanic most people miss, and it is the most powerful. There are trillions of dollars of debt owed by borrowers *outside* the United States — companies in Brazil, banks in Europe, governments across the emerging world borrow in dollars because that is the currency the world lends in. The Bank for International Settlements puts offshore dollar credit to non-bank borrowers at over \$13 trillion. When a crisis hits, all of those borrowers suddenly need dollars *at once* — to roll over debt, to meet margin calls, to cover positions — and there are not enough to go around. A scramble for a scarce thing pushes its price up. The dollar's price *is* its exchange rate, so it rallies. We cover the plumbing of this in [the global dollar shortage and central-bank swap lines](/blog/trading/forex/the-global-dollar-shortage-and-central-bank-swap-lines); for now, hold the image of a worldwide margin call denominated in dollars.

**Mechanism 3 — repatriation and de-risking.** When risk-off hits, leveraged investors unwind positions. A Japanese pension fund that bought US stocks unhedged, a hedge fund running a carry trade into emerging markets, a European insurer holding dollar bonds — they all cut risk, and cutting risk often means buying back dollars they had effectively sold. The [carry trade](/blog/trading/forex/the-carry-trade-getting-paid-to-hold-a-currency) is funded in cheap currencies and parked in high-yielders; when it unwinds, the flow reverses violently and the funding currency (often the dollar or the yen) gets bought back.

![The flight-to-safety chain that bids the dollar in a panic](/imgs/blogs/the-dollar-smile-why-the-dollar-wins-in-boom-and-in-panic-4.png)

The diagram above traces the chain. A global risk shock fans out into two immediate reactions — investors sell risky assets, and a dollar-funding squeeze begins. The selling sends money hunting for safe assets; the squeeze forces offshore borrowers to repay dollar debt. Both channels converge on the same action — *buy dollars and US Treasuries* — and that buying is what lifts the dollar's left cheek. Notice there is no node for "US economy does well." The left cheek does not need it. The dollar rallies in a panic *because of what everyone else is doing*, not because of anything good happening in America.

Now, the empirical fingerprint. If the left cheek is real, the dollar should rally precisely when fear spikes — that is, the dollar and the VIX should move *together* in stress. They do.

![DXY versus the VIX fear gauge, year-end, 2017 to 2025](/imgs/blogs/the-dollar-smile-why-the-dollar-wins-in-boom-and-in-panic-3.png)

The dual-axis chart plots the dollar index against the VIX at each year-end. The relationship is loose at the annual frequency — these are year-end snapshots, and the VIX is a fast, intraday creature while the dollar is sticky — but the broad pattern holds: the years of elevated stress are not years of dollar weakness. The relationship is far cleaner *intraday during the spikes themselves*, which year-end data smooths away. When the VIX punched to 82.7 in March 2020 and to 65.7 in August 2024, the dollar was bid in the moment, even though by each year's end the panic had faded and the dollar gave some back. The takeaway: the left cheek is an *event*, not an annual average. It switches on in days and fades in weeks, which is exactly why holding a long-dollar position is a decent — though imperfect — hedge against an equity crash.

#### Worked example: the dollar as a crash hedge

You manage a \$1,000,000 stock portfolio and you are worried about a crash. You decide to hedge part of it by going long the dollar — say a **\$1,000,000 notional long-DXY position** alongside the stocks. A panic hits: equities fall 25%, so your stock book loses 25% × \$1,000,000 = **−\$250,000**. But the panic also fires the left cheek: the dollar index jumps 6% in the scramble for safety, so your dollar position gains 6% × \$1,000,000 = **+\$60,000**.

Net, the dollar hedge offset \$60,000 of a \$250,000 equity loss — about 24% of the damage — without your having to forecast the crash, only to recognise that crashes bid the dollar. **The lesson: the left cheek makes a long-dollar position a partial, automatic crash hedge — it pays you in exactly the scenario where your risky assets hurt most.** (Partial, not perfect: the dollar's gain rarely matches the equity loss one-for-one, and the hedge bleeds carry in calm times when the dollar sags. Nothing is free.)

It is worth dwelling on *why* the funding squeeze is the dominant mechanism, because it is the part that turns a normal flight-to-safety into a violent dollar spike. In a calm world, the global financial system runs on a steady recycling of dollars: a European bank borrows dollars short-term in the swap market, lends them out longer-term, and rolls the borrowing over and over. This works fine — until everyone tries to do the opposite at once. In a panic, the lenders of dollars (US money-market funds, mostly) pull back, while the borrowers (everyone else) suddenly need *more* dollars, not fewer, to meet margin and cover losses. Supply of dollars drops and demand for dollars spikes at the same instant. Price of dollars goes vertical. The exchange rate *is* the price of dollars, so the dollar goes vertical too. This is not a slow fundamental adjustment; it is a stampede through a narrow door, and it is why left-cheek moves are measured in days, not quarters.

There is a tell that confirms the funding squeeze is driving a particular dollar rally rather than ordinary safe-haven buying: the **cross-currency basis** blows out. The basis is the extra premium, beyond the interest-rate differential, that a borrower pays to get dollars in the swap market. In calm times it sits near zero; in a funding panic it gaps deeply negative — about −85 basis points for euro/dollar in March 2020 and far worse in 2008. When you see the dollar spiking *and* the basis blowing out, you know you are watching the left cheek's funding mechanism, not just nervous investors buying Treasuries. The [funding-stress post](/blog/trading/forex/the-global-dollar-shortage-and-central-bank-swap-lines) tracks that basis as the single cleanest gauge of a dollar squeeze.

A subtle but important point: the left cheek is *self-reinforcing* for a while, then *self-defeating*. As the dollar rallies in a panic, it tightens financial conditions everywhere — it makes that \$13 trillion of offshore dollar debt more expensive to service, which deepens the stress, which bids the dollar more. That doom loop is the "[dollar as a wrecking ball](/blog/trading/forex/the-dollar-as-a-wrecking-ball-for-emerging-markets)" we cover separately. It runs until a circuit-breaker fires — usually the Fed opening dollar swap lines to foreign central banks (it lent out \$583 billion this way in 2008 and \$449 billion in 2020), which floods the world with dollars and finally caps the rally. The left cheek goes up fast and comes down only when the Fed says so.

## The right cheek: why US outperformance bids the dollar

The right cheek is the corner that *does* match the naive model — a strong economy and a strong currency — but it is worth being precise about the mechanism, because the precision is what lets you forecast it.

The right cheek is not "the US economy is good in absolute terms." It is "the US economy is good *relative to everyone else*." Remember the spine: a currency is a relative price. What lifts the dollar's right cheek is **divergence** — the US pulling ahead of the rest of the developed world — not US strength on its own. If America is booming and so is everyone else, you are in the middle of the smile, not on the right cheek.

The transmission runs through the rate gap.

![The right-cheek mechanism: US exceptionalism widens the rate gap and pulls in capital](/imgs/blogs/the-dollar-smile-why-the-dollar-wins-in-boom-and-in-panic-6.png)

Read the pipeline left to right. A hot US economy generates firm inflation, which forces the Fed to keep policy tight while other central banks, facing weaker economies, cut. That divergence in policy widens the US-versus-rest-of-world interest-rate gap. A wider rate gap makes dollar assets pay more, and global capital — chasing both the higher yield and the stronger growth story — flows into dollars to buy them. The flow is the dollar rally. Each box is a real, observable step, which is why the right cheek is more forecastable than the left: you can watch the relative-growth and relative-policy data in real time, whereas a panic arrives without warning.

The cleanest single number to watch is the **rate differential**. When the US two-year yield sits far above the German or Japanese two-year, capital is paid handsomely to sit in dollars. In 2022, the US-versus-Japan two-year gap blew out to about 4.35 percentage points as the Fed hiked toward 5.5% while the Bank of Japan held rates near zero — and the yen collapsed from 115 to past 150. The pair followed the gap almost mechanically, which is exactly the [USD/JPY-versus-rate-gap relationship](/blog/trading/macro-trading/what-moves-exchange-rates-rates-flows-carry) the series leans on. The rate gap is the right cheek's engine.

#### Worked example: riding the right cheek with the rate gap

You believe the US will out-grow Europe this year and the Fed will stay on hold while the ECB cuts — a classic right-cheek setup. You put on a **\$1,000,000 long-dollar position against the euro** (i.e., short EUR/USD). Over six months your thesis plays out: the US-vs-euro-area rate gap widens by 1.5 percentage points, and EUR/USD falls from 1.1000 to 1.0500 — a 4.5% dollar gain.

Your P&L: 4.5% × \$1,000,000 = **+\$45,000** on the spot move. And because the dollar is the *higher-yielding* leg here, you also *earn* the rate gap while you wait — roughly the US-minus-euro deposit spread, say 2% annualised on \$1,000,000 for half a year = **+\$10,000** of carry. Total ≈ **+\$55,000**, with the carry and the spot move both working *for* you. **The lesson: on the right cheek the rate gap pays you twice — it drives the spot rally and it pays you positive carry to hold the position — which is why right-cheek dollar trends can run for quarters.** (Contrast the left cheek, where you often *pay* carry to be long a safe-haven dollar and rely entirely on the panic for your gain.)

There is a second-order effect worth naming, because it catches people out: the right cheek can *become* the left cheek if the dollar rises far enough. A dollar that strengthens on US exceptionalism tightens financial conditions for the whole world — it raises the local-currency cost of that \$13 trillion of offshore dollar debt and drains capital out of weaker economies. Push that far enough and something breaks abroad: an emerging-market currency crisis, a funding accident, a leveraged blow-up. At that point the *right-cheek* dollar strength has *manufactured* the global stress that fires the *left cheek*, and the two reinforce each other into a genuine dollar squeeze. The 2022 run flirted with this — the violence of the yen and sterling moves was starting to look like a stress event, not just a yield trade — and it is part of why central banks intervened. The cheeks are not hermetically sealed; a strong-enough right cheek can light the left cheek by breaking something. This is why "the dollar smile" is sometimes drawn with the two corners curling up into each other: at the extremes they merge.

This double payoff is why right-cheek regimes tend to be long, grinding trends rather than violent spikes. The left cheek is a lightning strike — a few days of panic — while the right cheek is a season. The 2014–2015 dollar bull market (the Fed tightening while Europe and Japan eased) and the 2022 surge were both multi-quarter right-cheek runs. You can position for them deliberately, sizing into the widening rate gap, in a way you simply cannot for the surprise of the left cheek.

There is one crucial asymmetry between the cheeks that traders exploit: **on the right cheek the dollar is positively correlated with risk assets** (both the dollar and US stocks rise on US strength), while **on the left cheek the dollar is negatively correlated with risk assets** (the dollar rises as stocks fall). The dollar's correlation with everything else *flips sign* depending on which cheek you are on. That sign-flip is the single most useful tell for diagnosing the regime, and it is why a portfolio's dollar exposure can be a hedge in one regime and a risk amplifier in another. The [cross-asset gravity of the dollar](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-dollar-cross-asset-gravity) post unpacks how that correlation reshapes a whole portfolio.

## The weak middle: when the dollar has nothing special to offer

Now the bottom of the smile — the part everyone forgets, because nothing dramatic is happening, which is precisely the point.

The dollar sags when *two* conditions hold at once: the world is calm (low fear, so no left-cheek safety bid) **and** growth is synchronised (no US standout, so no right-cheek divergence bid). With neither cheek firing, the dollar reverts to being just another currency — and a low-extra-return one at that, because in a benign world investors will not pay a premium for safety they do not need.

![The weak middle versus the bid cheeks, before and after](/imgs/blogs/the-dollar-smile-why-the-dollar-wins-in-boom-and-in-panic-8.png)

The before-and-after lays out the switch. The left column is the weak middle: calm markets and a low VIX, growth synced across the world, a narrowing rate gap, and capital happily leaving the dollar to chase higher returns abroad. The right column is what happens when *either* condition breaks — fear spikes *or* the US pulls ahead, growth de-synchronises, the rate gap widens or a safety bid appears, and capital floods back into the dollar. The dollar's weakness requires *both* calm and synchronisation to hold simultaneously; break either one and a cheek lights up. That fragility is why the weak middle rarely lasts as long as the cheeks — the world does not stay boring forever.

In the weak middle, the active flow is *outbound*. With the dollar offering no safety premium and only a thin yield edge, money leaves it for the periphery — emerging-market bonds paying double-digit yields, European equities that have lagged, commodity currencies levered to a global upswing. This is the carry-trade-friendly regime: low volatility and a flat-ish rate gap are exactly when [carry trades](/blog/trading/forex/the-carry-trade-getting-paid-to-hold-a-currency) work best, and carry trades are, in aggregate, a bet *against* the dollar and *for* the high-yielders. The weak middle and the global carry trade are two names for the same regime.

#### Worked example: the cost of being long the dollar in the calm middle

You hold a **\$1,000,000 long-dollar position** through a placid year — synchronised global growth, the VIX pinned near 13, no US standout. Neither cheek fires. The dollar index drifts *down* 4% over the year as money leaves for higher returns abroad. Your position loses 4% × \$1,000,000 = **−\$40,000** on spot.

And the rate gap does not save you, because in the middle it is thin — say the dollar's yield edge over the basket is only 0.5%, earning you +\$5,000 of carry. Net: **−\$40,000 + \$5,000 = −\$35,000**. You lost money holding the world's safest currency in a perfectly pleasant year — not because anything went *wrong*, but because nothing did. **The lesson: the dollar's weak middle is a real, recurring cost of being structurally long dollars; in a calm, synchronised expansion the safe currency is the one you do not want to hold.** The dollar pays you in storms and in US booms, and charges you a slow toll in the calm between them.

This is the deepest insight of the smile, and the one that separates people who understand it from people who half-remember it. The dollar's weakness is not a bearish *event* — it is the *absence* of a reason to own it. There is no crisis pushing it down and no boom pulling it up; there is just the quiet drift of money toward better returns elsewhere. To be bearish the dollar, you do not need to forecast disaster. You only need to forecast *boredom* — a world calm enough and even enough that nobody needs the safe, low-yielding dollar. That is a much easier and more common forecast than calling a crash or a US boom, which is why the weak middle, though undramatic, is where a lot of patient dollar-bearish money is made.

## A picture of all three at once: the dollar through 2014–2025

It helps to see the cheeks and the dip in one continuous history rather than as three separate stories.

![The dollar index 2014 to 2025 with both cheeks and the dip annotated](/imgs/blogs/the-dollar-smile-why-the-dollar-wins-in-boom-and-in-panic-5.png)

The line is the dollar index, year-end, from 2014 to 2025, with three moments marked. The **2017 dip** is the weak middle: 2017 was the year of "synchronised global growth", the phrase on every strategist's lips — Europe, Asia, and the US all expanding together, the VIX at record lows — and the dollar fell all year as money left it for the rest of a booming world. The **2020 mark** is the left cheek: the dollar's intraday March spike to about 103 in the COVID dash-for-dollars, which then faded by year-end as the Fed flooded the system. The **2022 mark** is the right cheek: the surge to a twenty-year high as US exceptionalism and a hawkish Fed widened the rate gap to extremes.

One picture, three regimes, the same currency. The dollar is not "trending up" or "trending down" in any simple sense across this decade — it is *oscillating between the cheeks and the dip*, and which one it is in depends entirely on the state of the world. If you try to trade the dollar with a single directional view ("the dollar always rises" or "the dollar is doomed"), this chart is the rebuttal. The dollar's level is a *function of the regime*, and the smile is the function.

Notice, too, what the chart does *not* show: a clean relationship with the US economy. US growth was solid in 2017 (when the dollar fell) and the US was in a recession scare in early 2020 (when the dollar spiked). The dollar's path is decoupled from the US business cycle in level terms and coupled instead to the *relative* state of the world and to global risk appetite. That decoupling is the smile's signature, and once you see it you cannot un-see it.

## How a strong dollar lands on the rest of the world

A natural question: if the dollar rallies on both cheeks, who is on the other side of that trade? The answer, disproportionately, is the emerging world — and tracing that gives us the smile's most important real-world consequence.

![DXY versus an emerging-market currency index, 2018 to 2025](/imgs/blogs/the-dollar-smile-why-the-dollar-wins-in-boom-and-in-panic-7.png)

The dual-axis chart plots the dollar index against a rebased emerging-market currency index from 2018 to 2025. The two lines are near-mirror images: when the dollar rises, EM currencies fall, and vice versa. Look at 2022 — the dollar surged to 108.5 at year-end (and 114.8 intraday) while the EM-FX index collapsed from 100 to 88. The dollar's right cheek in 2022 was an EM currency crisis. And the dollar's left cheek does the same thing: in the 2020 panic, the EM index cratered as the dollar spiked. *Both cheeks of the smile are bad for emerging markets*, which is the cruel symmetry of the system — EM currencies fall whether the dollar rallies on US strength or on global fear.

The reason ties back to the funding squeeze. Emerging-market governments and companies borrow heavily in dollars. When the dollar rallies — for *either* reason — their dollar debt gets more expensive to service in local-currency terms, financial conditions tighten, capital flows out, and their currencies fall, which makes the dollar debt even more expensive: the wrecking-ball doom loop. The [wrecking-ball post](/blog/trading/forex/the-dollar-as-a-wrecking-ball-for-emerging-markets) and the [game-theory case study on Vietnam's flows and room limits](/blog/trading/game-theory/case-study-vietnam-foreign-flows-room-limits-and-manipulation-games) follow that thread; here, the point is that the smile is not just a US story. Its two cheeks are felt most violently 8,000 miles away, in the currencies that have to absorb the dollar's strength whichever way it comes.

#### Worked example: a dollar-debt borrower caught by both cheeks

An emerging-market company has borrowed **\$1,000,000** in dollars (because dollar funding was cheap), and its revenue is all in local currency. Its home currency is trading at 10 units per dollar, so the debt is worth 10,000,000 local units.

A dollar rally hits — it does not matter which cheek. The home currency falls 20% against the dollar, to 12.5 units per dollar. The debt is still \$1,000,000, but now it is worth 12,500,000 local units — the company's debt burden just jumped by 2,500,000 local units, a **25% increase**, without it borrowing a single extra dollar. If the rally came from the left cheek (global panic), the company is hit just as the local economy weakens; if it came from the right cheek (US boom), it is hit just as US-driven tightening drains capital from its market. **The lesson: a dollar borrower is short the dollar smile — it loses on *both* cheeks, which is why a strong dollar is an emerging-market stress event regardless of the reason behind it.**

## Trading the smile: sizing, carry, and the regime trade

The smile is a diagnostic, but it is also a position-sizing tool, and the two cheeks demand opposite trading styles. Getting the style wrong is how people who "know the smile" still lose money on it.

**The right cheek wants trend-following.** Because it is a slow, multi-quarter grind powered by a widening rate gap, the right cheek rewards patience: you scale into a long-dollar position as the divergence builds, you collect positive carry the whole way (the dollar is the high-yielder), and you ride the trend until the rate gap stops widening. Stops are wide, holding periods are long, and the carry pays you to wait. The 2022 and 2014–2015 runs were textbook right-cheek trends, and a trend-follower made far more from sitting still than from being clever.

**The left cheek wants the opposite — mean reversion and humility.** Because it is a violent spike that the Fed eventually crushes, trying to trend-follow the left cheek is a good way to buy the dollar at the top, right before the swap lines open and it collapses. The left cheek is a trade for people who were *already* long the dollar (as a hedge) before the panic, who then *take profit into the spike* rather than chase it. If you are not already positioned when the panic hits, the disciplined move is usually to wait for the Fed's circuit-breaker and then fade the dollar, not to pile in late.

The deepest sizing lesson is about *carry and correlation together*. On the right cheek, a long-dollar position has positive carry and positive correlation with US risk assets — it is a momentum amplifier, and you should size it as risk, not as a hedge. On the left cheek, a long-dollar position often has *negative* carry (you are long a safe, lower-yielding currency in the moment the Fed is cutting) but negative correlation with risk assets — it is a hedge, and you should size it as insurance, accepting the carry bleed as the premium. The *same instrument* — long dollars — is a risk position in one regime and a hedge in the other. Sizing it identically across regimes is the classic error.

#### Worked example: the carry cost of holding the hedge into a panic

You run a \$1,000,000 stock book and decide to carry a permanent **\$1,000,000 long-dollar hedge** against a crash, knowing the left cheek will pay you if one comes. The catch is the weak-middle toll: in calm years the dollar sags and you *pay* to hold the hedge. Say over three calm years the dollar drifts down a total of 9%, costing 9% × \$1,000,000 = **−\$90,000**, partly offset by, say, +\$15,000 of carry across the three years — a net bleed of about **−\$75,000**, or −\$25,000 a year.

Then, in year four, a panic hits: the left cheek fires, the dollar spikes 7%, and your hedge gains 7% × \$1,000,000 = **+\$70,000** in a few weeks — landing exactly when your stock book is down 25%, or −\$250,000. The hedge nearly recoups its three years of bleed in one panic and offsets 28% of the equity loss. **The lesson: the dollar's weak middle is the *insurance premium* you pay for the left cheek's crash protection — the toll in calm years is the cost of being paid in storms, and you cannot have the payout without paying the premium.** This is the same trade-off, viewed from the buyer's side, that the dollar-borrower example showed from the seller's side.

## Common misconceptions

The smile is simple enough that people think they have it after one reading, and then misuse it. Here are the five errors that matter, each corrected with a number.

**Misconception 1: "A strong dollar means the US economy is strong."** Wrong half the time. The dollar surged in March 2020 *while the US economy was collapsing into the sharpest recession since the Depression* — the dollar index jumped from about 95 to 103 in ten days as GDP cratered. The left cheek bids the dollar *despite* US weakness, not because of US strength. A dollar rally is a flow signal, not a growth signal; to read the growth implication you must first identify the cheek.

**Misconception 2: "The dollar is a safe haven, full stop."** Only on the left cheek. On the right cheek the dollar rises *with* risk assets — in 2022 the dollar and US two-year yields rose together as US stocks were rate-shocked, but the dollar's *driver* was yield, not fear. The dollar's safe-haven property is regime-dependent: it is a haven in a panic and a yield magnet in a boom, and treating it as one fixed thing will get the correlation sign wrong exactly when it matters.

**Misconception 3: "The smile is symmetric — both cheeks are the same height and shape."** No. The left cheek is a *spike* — it goes up in days (the dollar moved ~8% in ten days in March 2020) and fades in weeks once the Fed opens swap lines. The right cheek is a *grind* — a multi-quarter trend driven by a slowly widening rate gap (2022's run built over most of the year to 114.8). They have different time signatures, and a strategy that works on one (e.g. trend-following the right cheek) can be murdered by the other (the left cheek's mean-reverting spike).

**Misconception 4: "The dollar always rallies in a crisis."** Mostly, but not always — and the exception is instructive. If the crisis *originates in the US itself* and damages the dollar's safe-asset status, the left cheek can misfire. During the 2023 regional-banking stress and at moments in 2025 when US fiscal and policy credibility was questioned, the dollar wobbled even as risk came off, because the safety bid is a bid for the dollar's *credibility*, not its geography. The left cheek depends on the dollar still being seen as the safe asset; weaken that belief and the cheek can sag.

**Misconception 5: "The weak middle means the dollar is in a bear market."** No — it means the dollar has no catalyst. The 2017 dollar decline (DXY from ~102 to ~92) was not a bear market in any structural sense; it was the *absence* of a reason to own the dollar in a synchronised boom, and it reversed the moment growth de-synchronised in 2018. The weak middle is a *resting state*, not a trend. Confusing "no reason to own it" with "a reason to short it forever" is how people get run over when a cheek lights up.

## How it shows up in real markets

Three episodes, one for each part of the curve. Real dates, real numbers, the smile in the wild.

### 2020: the COVID dash-for-dollars (the left cheek)

In the second week of March 2020, as COVID lockdowns spread and equities fell into the fastest bear market in history, the dollar did something that bewildered anyone watching the US economy: it *soared*. From around 95 on the dollar index in early March, it spiked to nearly 103 by 23 March — roughly an 8% move in about ten trading days — even as the Fed slashed rates to zero and the US economy ground to a halt.

This was the left cheek in its purest form. Every mechanism fired at once. Offshore borrowers scrambled for dollars to meet margin calls and roll debt; the cross-currency basis — the premium for borrowing dollars in the swap market — blew out to levels not seen since 2008 (the [funding-stress plumbing post](/blog/trading/forex/the-global-dollar-shortage-and-central-bank-swap-lines) tracks that basis). Investors dumped everything and hid in Treasuries. The VIX hit 82.7 intraday, its highest ever. Emerging-market currencies cratered. None of it had anything to do with the US doing *well* — the US was doing terribly. The dollar rallied *because* the world was on fire and the dollar was the fire exit.

And then it ended exactly as the framework predicts: the Fed re-opened its dollar swap lines with foreign central banks, lending out \$449 billion at the peak to relieve the offshore squeeze, and launched unlimited QE. With the dollar shortage relieved, the left cheek faded — the dollar index slid all the way back to about 90 by year-end. The spike up was a panic; the fade down was the Fed flooding the system. A trader who understood the smile shorted the dollar *after* the Fed acted, not before, because the left cheek only breaks when the central bank breaks it.

The 2020 episode is the cleanest left-cheek case study precisely because the US "fundamentals" were so unambiguously *terrible* at the exact moment the dollar was strongest. There was no plausible story in which the dollar rallied 8% in ten days because America's economy was attractive — it was shutting down. The only coherent explanation is the smile's left cheek: a global liquidity panic and a worldwide scramble for the one currency in which the world's debt is denominated. If you ever doubt that a dollar rally can be entirely disconnected from US economic strength, March 2020 is the proof. And the symmetry of the fade matters too: the dollar did not retrace because America suddenly looked better — it retraced because the *plumbing* was fixed. The cause of the rally was a shortage; the cause of the fade was the shortage's cure. Watch the cause, not the level.

### 2022: the US-exceptionalism rally (the right cheek)

2022 was the mirror image. No panic — instead, raging US inflation forcing the most aggressive Fed hiking cycle in forty years, from near-zero to 4.5% in a single year, while the Bank of Japan held rates at zero and the ECB lagged far behind. The US-versus-Japan two-year rate gap blew out to about 4.35 points. Capital poured into the dollar to capture both the yield and the relative-growth story.

The dollar index ground higher all year to 114.8 in late September, a twenty-year high. The yen collapsed from 115 to past 150 — a move so violent Japan's Ministry of Finance spent about \$63 billion in September–October *selling dollars* to defend the yen (and still only slowed it). The euro broke below parity for the first time since 2002. Sterling crashed to an all-time low of 1.035 in the September mini-budget crisis. Every major currency fell against a dollar lifted by the widest rate gap in a generation.

This was the right cheek: a season, not a spike. The rally built over nine months as the rate gap widened, and a trend-follower who leaned into the widening differential rode it for quarters — collecting positive carry the whole way, because the dollar was now the high-yielder. It topped only when the rate gap stopped widening: once the market sensed the Fed was near its peak in late 2022, the dollar rolled over and gave back much of the move into 2023. The right cheek lives and dies with the rate gap, and the rate gap peaked when the hiking cycle did.

### 2017: the synchronised-growth dip (the weak middle)

2017 is the episode nobody remembers, because nothing happened — which is the whole lesson. It was the year of "synchronised global growth": for the first time since before the 2008 crisis, every major economy expanded together. Europe accelerated, China stabilised, emerging markets recovered, and the US was solid but no longer a standout. The VIX spent the year at record lows, repeatedly closing under 10.

Neither cheek had anything to fire it. No panic (so no left-cheek safety bid) and no US divergence (so no right-cheek yield bid) — both conditions for the weak middle held at once. So the dollar did exactly what the bottom of the smile predicts: it fell, steadily, all year, from about 103 on the dollar index in January to about 92 by year-end, a roughly 10% decline with no crisis in sight. Money left the safe, only-modestly-higher-yielding dollar to chase the synchronised global upswing — European equities, emerging-market bonds, commodity currencies all outperformed.

The 2017 decline confused a lot of people precisely because there was no dramatic cause. But there did not need to be. The dollar fell because the world was calm and growing together, which is the one combination in which the dollar has nothing special to offer. When that calm broke in 2018 — trade wars, a Fed still hiking, growth de-synchronising — the dollar promptly turned around and rose. The weak middle was a resting state, and it ended the moment a cheek had a reason to light up.

It is worth contrasting 2017 with 2014–2015 to see how thin the line is between the middle and the right cheek. In 2014, growth was *not* synchronised — the Fed was winding down QE and preparing to hike while Europe and Japan were easing aggressively, so the rate gap was *widening* and the dollar was on the right cheek, rising sharply. By 2017, the rest of the world had caught up: Europe and emerging markets were accelerating, the rate gap stopped widening, and the dollar slid into the middle. Nothing about the *US* changed dramatically between those years — US growth was solid throughout. What changed was *the rest of the world*, which caught up and erased the US's relative advantage. That is the whole game: the dollar's position on the smile is set far more by what the rest of the world is doing than by what America is doing. A trader watching only US data would have been baffled by the 2017 decline; a trader watching the *relative* picture saw the rate gap close and the dollar slide into the middle, right on cue.

## The takeaway: how to read a dollar move

Pull the three episodes together and the smile stops being a curve and becomes a *diagnostic procedure*. When you see the dollar move, do not ask "is the US economy good or bad?" — that question has no stable answer. Ask three questions instead, in order:

1. **What is risk appetite doing?** If the VIX is spiking and equities are falling while the dollar rises, you are on the **left cheek** — this is a panic bid, it will be fast and mean-reverting, and it will likely fade when the Fed acts. The dollar here is a safe-haven and a crash hedge, *negatively* correlated with risk assets.

2. **What is the rate gap doing?** If markets are calm-to-firm, equities are fine or rising, and the dollar is grinding higher alongside a widening US-versus-rest-of-world rate gap, you are on the **right cheek** — this is a yield-and-growth bid, it will be a slower multi-quarter trend, and it pays positive carry. The dollar here is *positively* correlated with US risk assets and trends with the rate differential.

3. **Is it neither?** If the world is calm *and* growth is synchronised *and* the rate gap is thin, you are in the **weak middle**, and the dollar's most likely path is a slow drift *down* as money leaves for better returns abroad. To be bearish here you need not forecast disaster — only continued boredom.

The single most valuable habit the smile gives you is watching the dollar's *correlation with risk assets* and treating its sign as the regime tell. When the dollar and stocks move *opposite* (dollar up as stocks down), you are on the left cheek. When they move *together* (dollar up as US stocks up on US strength), you are on the right cheek. When the dollar drifts down while everything rallies, you are in the middle. The correlation flips sign across the smile, and that sign-flip is more informative than any single level.

And here is the insight to end on, the one that ties back to the spine of this whole series. A currency is a relative price, driven by the rate gap and the flow of money across borders. The dollar smile is simply a map of when those two forces — yield and flow — point *into* the dollar. They point in when America out-yields the world (the rate gap, the right cheek) and when the world is too frightened to be anywhere else (the flow to safety, the left cheek). They point *out* only when the world is calm enough and even enough that neither force applies. So the dollar wins in boom and in panic, and loses only when the world is quietly, unremarkably fine. The dollar is not a bet on America. It is a bet on the world needing dollars — and the world needs them most when it is either terrified or when America is the only game in town. Learn to tell which, and you have learned to read the most important price in finance. The next time you see the dollar rip higher and a commentator reaches for a single tidy reason, you will know to do the harder, more useful thing: check the VIX, check the rate gap, and decide for yourself which cheek of the smile is doing the talking. That one habit will keep you on the right side of the world's most consequential currency far more often than any forecast of where it is "supposed" to go.
