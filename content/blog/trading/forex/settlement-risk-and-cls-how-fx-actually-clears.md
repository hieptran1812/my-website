> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — In foreign exchange, the biggest risk is not that the price moves; it is that you pay your side of a trade and the other party fails before paying theirs. Because the two legs of every FX trade settle in two different countries' payment systems hours apart, you can hand over the full principal and receive nothing back.
>
> - Every FX trade is two payments in two currencies. They clear in two separate national systems, in two time zones — so there is a window where you have paid and not yet been paid.
> - This is **settlement risk**, and when it bites you lose the **whole principal**, not the small price move. A \$10,000,000 trade that goes wrong on price costs you maybe \$100,000; the same trade that fails on settlement costs you the full \$10,000,000.
> - It is named **Herstatt risk** after Bankhaus Herstatt, a German bank whose licence was pulled mid-afternoon on **26 June 1974** — after it had received Deutsche Marks all morning and before it paid out the dollars it owed.
> - **CLS Bank** fixed it with **payment-versus-payment (PvP)**: both legs settle on CLS's own books at the same instant, or neither settles. CLS now settles a large share of the eligible FX market and has all but eliminated principal risk for trades inside its perimeter.
> - The one number to remember: roughly **half a trillion dollars a day** still settles *outside* PvP — Herstatt risk is reduced, not gone.

## The afternoon a bank ran out of time

On the morning of **26 June 1974**, dozens of banks around the world did exactly what they did every day: they sent payments to Bankhaus Herstatt, a mid-sized private bank in Cologne, to settle currency trades. The deals were simple. Herstatt had agreed to sell US dollars and buy Deutsche Marks. So the counterparties paid Marks into Herstatt's account at its German correspondent bank during the German business day. In return, Herstatt was supposed to pay the dollars into their accounts in New York later that same day — because New York is six hours behind Frankfurt, "later that same day" in dollars meant the New York afternoon.

The Marks arrived. They were final, irreversible, gone. And then, at **15:30 Central European Time** — which was still mid-morning in New York, before the dollar payments had been made — the German banking regulator withdrew Herstatt's licence and ordered it into liquidation. The bank had been ruined by speculative currency bets that dwarfed its capital. Its correspondent in New York, Chase Manhattan, froze the account and stopped making outgoing payments. Every counterparty that had paid Herstatt its Marks that morning was now an unsecured creditor of a failed bank for the dollars it was owed. They had paid in full and received nothing.

That is the entire story of settlement risk in one afternoon, and it is why FX market practitioners still say "Herstatt risk" fifty years later. The loss was not that the exchange rate moved. The exchange rate barely mattered. The loss was the **full face value of the dollar leg** — the principal — because the trade settled in two halves, in two countries, hours apart, and the bank died in the gap between them. This post is about that gap: why it exists in the plumbing of every currency trade, what it actually costs when it bites, and how an institution called CLS Bank engineered it almost entirely out of the market.

![Herstatt timezone gap timeline showing EUR paid in the morning and the dollar leg failing in the afternoon](/imgs/blogs/settlement-risk-and-cls-how-fx-actually-clears-1.png)

This series keeps returning to one idea: an exchange rate is the relative price of two monies, and every FX position is a *pair* — a bet on one currency against another. Settlement risk is the brutal, physical version of that idea. You never just "buy euros"; you pay dollars *and* receive euros, as two distinct movements of two distinct national monies through two distinct national payment systems. Most of the time that machinery is invisible. The Herstatt afternoon is what it looks like when one half of the machine stops while the other half has already turned.

## Foundations: Settlement risk and Herstatt risk

Start from zero. A **spot FX trade** is an agreement to exchange a fixed amount of one currency for a fixed amount of another, at an agreed rate, with the actual swap of money happening a short time later — by market convention, two business days later (called **T+2**, "trade date plus two"). The day you agree the deal is the **trade date**. The day the money actually moves is the **settlement date** or **value date**.

Here is the part that everyone glosses over: there is no single "the money moves" event. A currency only exists as balances inside *its own country's* payment system. US dollars live in the US banking system, settled through Fedwire (the Federal Reserve's payment rail) and CHIPS. Euros live in the euro-area system, settled through TARGET2. Yen live in Japan's BOJ-NET. So when you trade euros for dollars, **two separate payments happen in two separate systems**: somebody pays euros inside the European system, and somebody pays dollars inside the American system. Each payment is a domestic transfer within one currency's home rails. There is no global pipe down which "an FX trade" flows as a single object.

These two payments are the **two legs** of the trade. The leg you owe — the currency you are paying out — is your **pay leg**. The leg you are owed — the currency coming to you — is your **receive leg**. In a normal world the two legs land on the same value date and you barely notice. But the two national systems open and close at different local times. By the time the New York dollar system is busy settling, the Frankfurt euro system may already be winding down its day. The two legs do not, and cannot, happen at the same instant unless something forces them to.

**Settlement risk** is the risk that you perform your leg — you pay out the currency you owe, finally and irrevocably — and your counterparty fails before performing theirs. The instant your pay leg is final and their receive leg has not arrived, you are exposed to their full collapse for the **entire principal amount** of the leg you are owed. Not the change in price. The whole notional.

**Herstatt risk** is just the name the market gives to this specific flavour of settlement risk in FX — principal risk arising from the time gap between the two currency legs settling in two different time zones. The terms are used interchangeably. "Herstatt risk" is the historical, evocative name; "settlement risk" or "principal risk" is the technical one.

It helps to separate three different things that can go wrong on a trade, because people lump them together and then misjudge how dangerous each is:

- **Market risk** — the price moves against you between trade date and settlement date. If you agreed to buy euros at 1.0800 and by settlement they are worth 1.0700, you have a paper loss on the *difference*. This is bounded by the size of the move, usually a fraction of a percent over two days.
- **Pre-settlement (replacement) risk** — your counterparty fails *before* settlement day. The trade never happens. You lose the trade, not your money: you go back to the market and replace it, paying or receiving only the *change in price* since you dealt. Painful, but again bounded by the move.
- **Settlement risk (Herstatt / principal risk)** — your counterparty fails *during* the settlement window, after you have irrevocably paid your leg and before they have paid theirs. You lose the **full principal** of the leg you were owed. This is the catastrophic one, and it is unique to instruments where value is exchanged in two separate, non-simultaneous payments.

The whole point of the rest of this post is that the third risk is qualitatively different from the first two. Market risk and replacement risk cost you the *move*; settlement risk costs you the *principal*. The ratio between those two can be a hundred to one.

One more building block makes the danger sharp: **finality**. A payment is *final* when it can no longer be reversed — when the receiving bank's claim on the money is unconditional and the sending bank cannot claw it back. In modern central-bank payment systems, finality is essentially instantaneous and absolute: once Fedwire credits a dollar payment, those dollars are gone for good, with no take-backs. That irreversibility is a *feature* — it is what lets a payment system be a reliable settlement rail. But it is also the blade of settlement risk. The moment your pay leg becomes final, you have given up all control over that money; if the receive leg never comes, there is no mechanism to undo what you have already done. Settlement risk is precisely the interval between *your* leg becoming final and *their* leg becoming final. Before your leg is final you can still cancel; after their leg is final you are safe; the danger is the gap in between, and finality is what makes that gap unrecoverable.

There is also a layer of intermediaries that quietly widens the exposure: **correspondent banks**. Most banks do not hold accounts directly at every foreign central bank. Instead, to pay euros, a non-European bank uses a European **correspondent** — a bank that holds the euro account on its behalf and makes the payment into the local system for it. So a single FX leg can involve your bank, your correspondent, their correspondent, and their bank — a chain of four institutions, each a potential point of failure, each with its own cut-off times. Herstatt itself was a correspondent-chain failure: the Marks reached Herstatt's German correspondent and were final there, while Herstatt's New York correspondent, Chase, simply stopped paying out the dollars once the licence was pulled. The correspondent layer is part of why the window is hard to shrink: it is not two banks and two systems, it is a chain of banks across two systems, each link with its own timing and its own solvency.

#### Worked example: the move versus the principal

Suppose you trade **EUR/USD** at 1.0800: you agree to buy €10,000,000 and pay \$10,800,000 for it (10,000,000 × 1.0800). Your pay leg is \$10,800,000; your receive leg is €10,000,000.

- If the price moves 1% against you before settlement — say EUR/USD falls to 1.0692 — your **market loss** is roughly 1% of the euro value: about €10,000,000 × 0.0108 ≈ **\$108,000** of mark-to-market pain. Annoying, recoverable.
- If instead your counterparty *fails after you have paid the \$10,800,000* and before they deliver the €10,000,000, you lose the **entire €10,000,000** — about **\$10,800,000** at the trade rate. You become an unsecured creditor in their bankruptcy and may recover cents on the dollar, years later.

The settlement-risk loss here is roughly **100 times** the 1% market loss on the same trade. That single comparison is the reason an obscure piece of plumbing called CLS exists.

The figure below makes that magnitude contrast concrete: the same trade, two utterly different loss sizes depending on *which* risk shows up.

## Why the gap exists: two legs, two systems, two clocks

The deepest reason settlement risk exists is not negligence or bad luck. It is a structural feature of how money works. Money is a liability of a banking system, and each currency's banking system is a closed national loop. Dollars are claims on the US banking system; you can only move them by debiting one US bank account and crediting another, through US payment infrastructure, during US operating hours, under US central-bank rules. The same is true of every currency in its own country. There is no supranational ledger where a euro and a dollar can change hands in a single atomic stroke.

So when two banks agree an FX trade, settling it requires two coordinated but **independent** domestic payments. Bank A instructs its euro correspondent to pay euros to Bank B's euro account; Bank B instructs its dollar correspondent to pay dollars to Bank A's dollar account. Each instruction travels its own national rail, governed by its own cut-off times and finality rules. Nothing links them. There is no umpire saying "release the euros only if the dollars are also released."

Now layer on time zones. Frankfurt is six hours ahead of New York. TARGET2, the euro system, has its main settlement window in European morning and early afternoon. Fedwire, the dollar system, runs into the US evening, which is the middle of the European night. So the natural sequence for a European bank selling dollars for euros is: receive (or pay) euros in the European morning, then pay (or receive) the dollars in the US afternoon — *hours later*. The euro leg is final and irreversible long before the dollar leg even begins. That overnight-ish span is the **Herstatt window**: the stretch of time during which one party has paid and the other has not yet.

The window is widest for currency pairs whose home time zones are far apart. The classic worst case in the literature is selling Asia-Pacific currencies (which settle in the Asian morning) for North American dollars (which settle in the US afternoon, the next calendar day in Asia). A bank can have **paid out one leg and be waiting more than a full business day** to receive the other. Across that span the entire principal is exposed to the counterparty's solvency.

#### Worked example: how long the principal is exposed

You are a Tokyo bank. You sell **¥1,500,000,000** (1.5 billion yen) for dollars at USD/JPY 150.00, so you are owed \$10,000,000. The yen leg settles through Japan's BOJ-NET in the **Tokyo morning**. The dollar leg settles through Fedwire in the **New York afternoon** of what is, in Tokyo, the small hours of the next day.

- You irrevocably pay ¥1,500,000,000 at, say, **09:00 Tokyo time**.
- You are not credited the \$10,000,000 until Fedwire processes it at roughly **15:00 New York time**, which is **05:00 the next day in Tokyo**.
- Your principal is therefore exposed for about **20 hours** — overnight, while you sleep, with no way to claw the yen back if the counterparty fails in between.

The lesson: settlement risk is not a fleeting instant of overlap. For some pairs the exposure runs the better part of a day per trade, and it stacks across every open trade you have with that name.

This is also why FX settlement risk historically dwarfed the capital cushions meant to absorb it. A bank's daily settlement exposure to a single large counterparty could exceed its **entire capital base** — because the exposure is gross principal, accumulated across many trades, not a netted small price difference. A 1999 BIS study found that a bank's maximum FX settlement exposure to one counterparty could be more than three times its capital. One Herstatt-style failure of a big name could, in principle, take the counterparty down with it. That is systemic risk hiding inside a back-office process.

It is worth being precise about *why* the window is so stubborn, because the obvious "fix" — just settle both legs at the same time of day — does not work. Each national payment system is a **real-time gross settlement (RTGS)** system run by its central bank: payments settle one by one, in central-bank money, with immediate finality, but only during that system's operating hours. Fedwire's hours are set to the US clock; TARGET2's to the European clock; BOJ-NET's to the Tokyo clock. Their operating windows only partially overlap. For a few hours in the European afternoon, both Europe and the US East Coast are open, which is why CLS's settlement window is squeezed into that narrow band of global overlap. But for many currency pairs there is little or no natural overlap at all — the Tokyo system has closed for the day before New York opens — so the legs *cannot* be made simultaneous within the national systems even in principle. Someone has to go first. And whoever goes first carries the risk.

The exposure also **stacks**. It is not one trade's principal; it is the sum of every unsettled leg you have outstanding to a given counterparty across the window. A dealer running thousands of trades a day with the same handful of large counterparties accumulates a tower of gross principal against each name, all of it live during the settlement window simultaneously. This is why pre-CLS settlement exposure could run to multiples of capital: the risk is gross, additive across trades, and concentrated on a few big names — the precise recipe for a single failure cascading into a systemic event.

The macro-policy machinery that decides those interest rates and operates these payment systems is its own deep topic; the [central-bank toolkit post in the macro-trading series](/blog/trading/macro-trading/central-bank-toolkit-rates-qe-qt-forward-guidance) covers how central banks run the rails. Here the relevant fact is only that *each central bank runs its own rail, on its own clock* — and that is the crack settlement risk lives in.

## Most of "FX" is not even spot — and every bit of it must settle

A common misconception is that this is a small problem because "real" FX is a modest spot market. It is not. The global FX market turns over an astonishing amount every single day, and the headline number has grown almost monotonically across the BIS's three-yearly surveys. Each one of those trades — every leg of every one — has to settle.

![Global daily FX turnover by BIS survey year from 2001 to 2022 rising to over seven trillion dollars](/imgs/blogs/settlement-risk-and-cls-how-fx-actually-clears-6.png)

By the 2022 BIS Triennial Survey, average daily FX turnover reached about **\$7.5 trillion a day**. That is the gross flow of value that the world's settlement plumbing has to move and reconcile, every business day, in two-legged pieces. The number's relentless growth is the reason settlement risk could not be left as a "manage it bilaterally and hope" problem: the value at risk in the Herstatt window scales directly with turnover.

And crucially, most of that turnover is **not spot**. When people picture FX they picture spot — buy euros now, settle in two days. But the largest single instrument by volume is the **FX swap**, where two parties exchange currencies now and agree to reverse the exchange at a future date. Spot is only the second-largest slice.

![Daily FX turnover by instrument in 2022 with FX swaps the largest at 3.8 trillion dollars](/imgs/blogs/settlement-risk-and-cls-how-fx-actually-clears-4.png)

This matters for settlement risk in a specific way. An **FX swap** has *two* exchange dates — a near leg and a far leg — and each of those is itself a two-legged currency settlement. So a swap is, in settlement terms, two Herstatt windows, not one. **Outright forwards** push a single two-legged settlement out to a future value date. The point is that the entire \$7.5 trillion, whatever its instrument label, ultimately resolves into pairs of opposite-currency payments that must each clear in their home system — and every one of those pairs is a potential Herstatt event. The mechanics of forwards and swaps themselves are in the [spot, forward and swap post](/blog/trading/forex/spot-forward-and-swap-the-three-ways-to-trade-a-currency); the only thing to carry here is that *more legs means more settlement events means more principal moving through the gap*.

#### Worked example: the daily value passing through the window

Take a single mid-sized dealer that does **\$50 billion a day** of FX across all instruments. Suppose, conservatively, that a quarter of that notional is in flight in the Herstatt window at the peak of the day — paid out on one leg, not yet received on the other.

- Peak intraday principal exposure ≈ \$50,000,000,000 × 0.25 = **\$12,500,000,000**.
- If that exposure is concentrated against, say, ten large counterparties, the *average* single-name exposure is **\$1,250,000,000** — over a billion dollars to one name, for the hours of the window.
- A bank with, say, \$8,000,000,000 of capital is carrying single-name settlement exposures that are a meaningful fraction of its entire loss-absorbing buffer — and that exposure exists purely because of *when* the legs settle, not because of any view on the market.

The intuition: settlement exposure is enormous relative to capital precisely because it is *gross principal*, briefly, against a handful of names — exactly the configuration that turns one failure into a chain reaction.

## How CLS removes the risk: payment-versus-payment

The fix is simple to state and operationally heroic to build. If the danger is that one leg can settle without the other, then **link the two legs so that neither can settle unless both do**. Pay the euros if and only if the dollars are also paid, at the same moment, on the same books. This principle is called **payment-versus-payment**, or **PvP**. It is the FX cousin of *delivery-versus-payment* (DvP) in securities markets, where the stock changes hands if and only if the cash does.

You cannot do PvP across two separate national systems on their own, because nothing links Fedwire to TARGET2. So in 2002 the world's major FX banks created an institution to *be* the link: **CLS Bank** (Continuous Linked Settlement). CLS is a special-purpose bank that holds accounts at the central banks of the currencies it settles. Because it sits *inside* each currency's system as an account holder, it can debit one bank's euro account and credit another's *on its own ledger*, and simultaneously debit and credit the matching dollar accounts, in one atomic operation. The atomicity that no single national system could provide across currencies, CLS provides on its own consolidated books.

![CLS payment-versus-payment pipeline from trade submission through funding to simultaneous settlement and pay-out](/imgs/blogs/settlement-risk-and-cls-how-fx-actually-clears-5.png)

Walk the mechanism in the figure above, left to right. First, both banks submit their matched trade details to CLS — CLS only settles trades where both sides agree on the terms. Second, CLS calculates **net pay-in requirements**: a bank that has done many trades in a currency only needs to fund its *net* short position in that currency, not the gross of every trade. Third, during a tight settlement window each morning (overlapping the open hours of all the relevant central-bank systems), banks **fund** their net short currencies into their CLS accounts. Fourth, CLS checks that **both** sides of each trade are fully covered. Fifth — the magic step — CLS settles each trade by simultaneously debiting and crediting the two currency accounts **on its own books at the same instant**. Sixth, it pays out the net long positions back to the banks. If a bank fails to fund, the trades that depended on its funding simply do not settle — but no counterparty has paid out a leg and lost it, because the legs only move together, on CLS's ledger, after funding is confirmed.

The key insight is that **settlement and funding are decoupled in time**, but settlement of the two legs is **locked together in time**. Banks pay in *separately* across the window; but the moment of settlement — the simultaneous swap — is atomic. No bank can be in the position Herstatt's counterparties were in, having paid one leg and waiting on the other, because in CLS the legs never move one without the other.

There is a second, almost free benefit: **netting**. Because CLS settles thousands of trades together, each bank only has to fund its net position per currency. Gross trade values in the hundreds of billions collapse into pay-ins of a few percent of that. Less money has to move, which means less liquidity tied up, which means the whole thing is cheaper than gross bilateral settlement on top of being far safer.

#### Worked example: gross principal versus net pay-in under CLS

Suppose over one settlement day a bank has, against all its CLS counterparties combined, the following EUR/USD activity:

- It bought €5,000,000,000 (and owes the matching dollars) and sold €4,800,000,000 (and is owed dollars).
- **Gross** principal that would have to move under old bilateral settlement: about €9,800,000,000 of euro legs plus the matching ~\$10,600,000,000 of dollar legs — call it well over **\$21,000,000,000** of payments criss-crossing two systems, every one exposed in the Herstatt window.
- **Net** under CLS: the bank is net long just €200,000,000 of euros and correspondingly net short the dollars. It funds only its **net short** — on the order of a couple of hundred million dollars — into CLS, and the simultaneous settlement does the rest.

The intuition: PvP kills the *principal* risk by linking the legs, and netting shrinks the *liquidity* needed to a small fraction of gross — so CLS is both dramatically safer and dramatically cheaper than settling each trade bilaterally.

CLS now settles a very large share of the world's eligible FX. The headline operational figure the market quotes is on the order of \$6–7 trillion of gross value settled per day through CLS, in around 18 currencies, for some 70-plus settlement members and many thousands of third-party participants behind them. For trades inside that perimeter, Herstatt risk is — in the strict principal sense — gone.

A word on *who* gets to use CLS, because it shapes the perimeter. CLS has two tiers. A small number of large banks are **settlement members**: they hold accounts directly with CLS and submit trades on their own behalf. Everyone else — thousands of smaller banks, funds, and corporates — participates as **third parties**, routing their trades through a settlement member who acts as their agent. This tiering is efficient (you do not need thousands of direct CLS accounts) but it also means access to PvP protection runs through the membership network: if you are a small institution whose bank is not a CLS member and offers no third-party service for your currency pair, your trade settles bilaterally with full Herstatt risk, no matter how "major" the currencies are. The perimeter is defined by currency *and* counterparty access, not by currency alone.

The settlement window itself is a small daily miracle of timing. Each morning, in the roughly five-hour band where the relevant central-bank systems overlap (centred on the European morning and early afternoon), CLS runs its cycle: it issues each member a schedule of net pay-ins, members fund their short currencies in tranches, and CLS settles the matched trades on its books as funding arrives, paying out the net long positions as it goes. The entire global day's eligible FX — trillions of dollars of gross value across eighteen currencies — is funded, settled leg-against-leg, and paid out inside that window every business day. The reason it can compress trillions of gross into a manageable flow of pay-ins is the netting we are about to quantify.

## What is still unsettled: the perimeter of safety

It is tempting to declare victory, but the honest picture is that CLS shrank the problem rather than abolishing it. There is a perimeter, and outside it the old risk persists. Three conditions must *all* hold for a trade to enjoy PvP protection: the **currencies** must be CLS-eligible, both **counterparties** must be CLS participants (directly or through a member), and the **trade type** must be CLS-settleable. Fail any one, and the trade settles the old way — bilaterally, two legs, two clocks, full Herstatt exposure.

![Decision graph showing CLS eligible trades settle PvP while non CLS currencies or non members keep Herstatt risk](/imgs/blogs/settlement-risk-and-cls-how-fx-actually-clears-7.png)

Look at where the trade can fall out of the safe path. If either currency is not on the CLS list — most emerging-market currencies, including, for example, currencies that trade heavily as **non-deliverable forwards (NDFs)** — the trade cannot go through CLS. If your counterparty is a smaller institution that is neither a member nor a third-party client of a member, the trade is bilateral. And some products and same-day trades fall outside the standard CLS sessions. Each of these routes lands on the red node: **still bilateral, still Herstatt risk**.

The currency coverage is the cleanest way to see the perimeter. The dollar is on roughly 88% of all FX trades, and the other CLS currencies — euro, yen, sterling, the Canadian, Australian and Swiss currencies, and a handful more — cover most of the major-pair flow. But "most of the majors" is not "all of FX."

![FX trade share by currency in 2022 with CLS eligible major currencies shaded green and others not](/imgs/blogs/settlement-risk-and-cls-how-fx-actually-clears-8.png)

The chart sorts currencies by how often each appears on one side of a trade. The green bars are the CLS-eligible majors; the red bars are the long tail of currencies — the renminbi in many of its settlement forms, and the broad "other" bucket of emerging-market currencies — that frequently settle outside PvP. A renminbi-against-a-minor trade, a Turkish lira NDF, a frontier-market spot deal: these still carry the principal risk that Herstatt's counterparties discovered.

The BIS and the Financial Stability Board have flagged exactly this. As FX turnover has grown and trading has shifted toward emerging-market currencies and toward trade types that settle outside CLS, the **absolute** value settling without PvP protection has *risen* even as the *share* protected stayed high. Industry estimates put the daily value of FX settling without PvP at roughly **\$2 trillion or more** — including a meaningful chunk in major currencies that simply did not go through CLS because of timing, product, or counterparty. The risk that everyone "solved" in 2002 is still a large, live, systemically relevant number.

#### Worked example: a trade that falls outside the perimeter

You are a fund settling a 1-month **USD/TRY** (US dollar versus Turkish lira) forward for a notional of **\$10,000,000**. The lira is not a CLS-settlement currency, so this cannot go through CLS PvP. You settle it bilaterally with your bank counterparty.

- You pay your leg first — say you owe the dollars, and you wire \$10,000,000 through the US system.
- Your counterparty is due to pay you the lira. If they fail in the window between your dollars going final and their lira arriving, you lose the **full \$10,000,000 principal**, not the small forward-price difference.
- The mitigation here is *not* PvP — it is old-fashioned credit management: dealing only with strong counterparties, setting settlement limits, and for some currencies using **non-deliverable** structures that cash-settle the *difference* (a small number) rather than exchanging gross principal at all.

The intuition: outside the CLS perimeter you are back in 1974, and the only defences left are the ones that existed before CLS — credit limits, netting agreements, and product design that avoids moving gross principal in the first place.

That last point is worth underlining because it is how a lot of EM-FX *actually* sidesteps Herstatt risk: the **NDF**. A non-deliverable forward never exchanges the two principals. Instead, on the value date the two parties compare the agreed forward rate to a published fixing and one pays the other the *net difference* in dollars. Because only the small net difference moves — and in a single currency — there is no two-legged gross principal to lose. NDFs exist for capital-control reasons (you cannot freely deliver the onshore currency), but a side-effect is that they neatly dodge the settlement-risk problem CLS was built to solve. The Vietnamese dong, for instance, trades offshore as an NDF; the [game-theory case study on Vietnam's foreign-flow and manipulation games](/blog/trading/game-theory/case-study-vietnam-foreign-flows-room-limits-and-manipulation-games) sits at the policy end of that same world.

#### Worked example: how an NDF dodges the principal entirely

You buy a 1-month **USD/INR** (US dollar versus Indian rupee) NDF for a notional of **\$10,000,000** at a forward rate of 84.00 rupees per dollar. The rupee is not CLS-eligible, so a *deliverable* forward would carry full Herstatt risk on \$10,000,000.

- An NDF, instead, settles only the *difference* against a published fixing. Suppose at maturity the fixing is 84.84 rupees per dollar.
- The agreed notional was \$10,000,000 × 84.00 = ₹840,000,000; at the fixing it is worth \$840,000,000 ÷ 84.84 ≈ \$9,900,990. The net cash settlement is the gap — about **\$99,010** — paid in dollars by one party to the other.
- The most that can be lost to settlement risk is therefore roughly **\$99,010**, the net difference — *not* the \$10,000,000 notional. No gross principal ever moves.

The intuition: an NDF converts a two-legged gross-principal exchange into a single small net dollar payment, so even outside the CLS perimeter the principal at risk shrinks from the full notional to the size of the price move — which is exactly the market-risk number, not the Herstatt number.

There is also a quieter, structural reason the un-CLS'd tail keeps growing: the *composition* of FX is shifting toward exactly the trades CLS does not cover. Three trends compound. First, **emerging-market currencies** are a rising share of turnover as trade and capital flows globalise, and most are not CLS-eligible. Second, **same-day and short-dated trades** have grown with electronic and algorithmic trading, and many of these settle too fast to make the CLS window. Third, the rise of **non-bank participants** — funds, principal-trading firms, fintechs — means more flow originates from institutions that are not CLS members and may not have third-party access for every pair. None of these is a failure of CLS; each is simply a part of the market expanding *outside* the box CLS draws. The regulators' fear is arithmetic: a roughly constant *share* of protection applied to a rising *total*, weighted toward the unprotected tail, means the *absolute* value exposed to Herstatt risk grows year after year.

## The line back to bank runs

There is a reason this post links out to a *bank-run* article, and it is not decoration. Settlement risk and the bank run are the same coordination problem wearing different clothes. In a classic bank run, depositors who individually want their money back, all at once, can topple a bank that is solvent on paper but cannot turn its assets into cash fast enough — and the *fear* that others will run is itself the trigger to run. In FX settlement, the analogue is: if I fear my counterparty might fail in the Herstatt window, my best response is to *delay or withhold* my own leg until I see theirs — but if everyone does that, settlement seizes up.

CLS is, in this light, a **commitment device** that resolves the coordination problem. By guaranteeing that the legs move together or not at all, it removes any incentive to withhold, because no one can be left having paid without receiving. It converts a fragile "you go first / no, you go first" standoff into a mechanism where going first is safe. The formal version of this coordination logic — why a guarantee that changes everyone's best response can stabilise an otherwise run-prone system — is exactly the Diamond–Dybvig machinery in the [bank-runs-as-coordination-games post](/blog/trading/game-theory/bank-runs-as-coordination-games-diamond-dybvig-and-svb). Herstatt was an FX bank run in slow motion; CLS is the deposit-insurance-shaped fix.

The parallel runs deeper than analogy. In both cases the underlying institutions can be perfectly solvent and the system can still freeze, purely because each participant's *fear of others' behaviour* makes withholding the rational individual choice. In a deposit run, the bank's loans are fine but everyone wants cash *now* in case everyone else does. In FX settlement, the trade is fine but everyone wants to *see the other leg first* in case the other side fails. Both are self-fulfilling: the fear that triggers the bad behaviour is validated by the bad behaviour. And both are fixed the same way — not by making the institutions more solvent, but by changing the *payoff structure* so that the cooperative move is also the safe move. Deposit insurance does this for runs by guaranteeing depositors regardless of what others do; CLS does it for FX by guaranteeing that your leg is only paid if you simultaneously receive the other. The mechanism, not trust, enforces the good outcome. That is why CLS is more accurately described as financial infrastructure than as a service — it is load-bearing in the same way a clearinghouse or a central bank is load-bearing.

#### Worked example: why "I'll wait to see your leg first" does not scale

Two banks, A and B, each owe the other a leg on a \$10,000,000 EUR/USD trade. Each fears the other might fail.

- If both pay on time, the trade settles cleanly. Best outcome for both.
- If A waits to "see B's leg first" and B does the same, **neither pays** and the trade fails — both incur replacement cost and operational chaos, and trust erodes.
- If A pays and B fails or withholds, A loses the full **\$10,000,000** principal. So A's *individually rational* move, under fear, is to wait — and so is B's.

The standoff is a textbook coordination trap: mutual waiting is a stable, bad equilibrium. **CLS breaks it** by making both legs settle simultaneously on its books, so "pay" is safe regardless of what the other side fears — the good equilibrium is enforced by the mechanism, not by trust.

## Common misconceptions

**"Settlement risk is just a slightly bigger version of market risk."** No — it is a different *kind* of risk, and the magnitudes are not comparable. Market risk on a two-day trade costs you the price *move*, typically well under 1% of notional. Settlement risk costs you the *principal* — 100% of the leg you are owed. As the worked example showed, on a \$10,000,000 trade the market-risk number was around \$108,000 and the settlement-risk number was the full ~\$10,800,000. Treating them as points on the same scale is how back offices used to under-price the thing that could actually kill the firm.

**"CLS clears FX, like a clearinghouse for futures."** Not quite — and the distinction matters. A central counterparty (CCP) like a futures clearinghouse *novates* trades, stepping in as buyer to every seller and seller to every buyer, and takes on the counterparty credit risk itself, backed by margin and a default fund. CLS does **not** novate and does **not** become your counterparty. It is a **settlement** system, not a clearing counterparty: it guarantees that the two legs of an already-agreed trade settle simultaneously or not at all, but it does not take the credit risk of the trade onto its own balance sheet. If a member fails to fund, CLS simply does not settle that member's trades; it does not make the other side whole the way a CCP would. The protection CLS gives is precisely PvP — no principal lost in the gap — not credit guarantee.

**"Once a trade is in CLS, nothing can go wrong."** PvP removes *principal* (Herstatt) risk, but not *liquidity* risk or *replacement* risk. If a bank fails to fund its short currency, its trades do not settle — and the counterparties, while they never lose principal, are suddenly *short the currency they expected to receive* and must scramble to source it in the market, possibly at a worse price, late in the day. CLS has well-rehearsed procedures for exactly this, but "no principal loss" is not "no disruption." The risk is transformed from a catastrophic principal loss into a manageable liquidity scramble — a vastly better problem, but still a problem.

**"CLS settles essentially all FX, so Herstatt risk is history."** The share of *eligible* FX settled via PvP is high, but eligible is not everything. A large and *growing* absolute value — on the order of \$2 trillion a day by industry estimates — settles outside PvP, because of non-CLS currencies, non-member counterparties, same-day trades, and product types that fall outside CLS sessions. The regulators' current worry is not that CLS failed; it is that the *un-CLS'd* tail has grown with EM-FX and is itself now systemically large.

**"The two legs settle at literally the same microsecond."** The marketing phrase is "simultaneous," and it is true *on CLS's own books* — the debit and credit of the two currency accounts are a single atomic operation in CLS's ledger. But the *funding* into and *pay-outs* out of CLS happen across a window of a couple of hours through the national systems. The atomicity is at the ledger level, not at the level of the underlying central-bank payments. That is enough: the thing that must be atomic — the moment value actually changes hands between the two parties — is.

**"Settlement risk was a 1970s problem that modern systems made obsolete."** The opposite is closer to the truth: in *absolute dollar terms*, more value sits in the Herstatt window today than ever before, because turnover has grown faster than PvP coverage has expanded. The BIS flagged in 2019 that the daily value of FX settling without PvP protection had grown to a multi-trillion-dollar figure, including a surprising amount of *major-currency* flow that simply did not route through CLS because of timing, product, or counterparty. Settlement risk is not a museum piece; it is a live, growing, systemically relevant exposure that happens to be well-managed in its core but expanding at its edges. Treating it as solved is precisely the complacency that lets the un-CLS'd tail grow unwatched — which is exactly why the official sector keeps it on the agenda half a century after Herstatt died.

## How it shows up in real markets

**The naming event, 26 June 1974.** Bankhaus Herstatt's failure cost its counterparties an estimated \$620 million in 1974 dollars on the dollar legs they never received — an enormous sum at the time, and the event that put "Herstatt risk" permanently into the FX vocabulary. More importantly, it triggered decades of regulatory work: the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision was itself created in the wake of Herstatt and related 1974 failures, and the reduction of FX settlement risk became a standing agenda item that eventually produced CLS.

**The two decades of "manage it bilaterally," 1974–2002.** For a quarter-century after Herstatt, the market lived with the risk and tried to contain it with credit limits and bilateral netting agreements. A 1996 BIS report ("the Allsopp Report") laid out a three-track strategy: individual banks should measure and limit their exposures, industry should build multi-currency settlement, and central banks should push both. The 1990s saw bilateral and multilateral netting schemes (like ECHO and Multinet) that reduced *exposure* but never solved the core atomicity problem. The lesson the market absorbed was that netting helps but only **PvP** removes principal risk — which is what finally motivated the banks to build CLS.

**The launch, 2002.** CLS went live in September 2002 with seven currencies and a few dozen members, founded and owned by a consortium of the world's largest FX banks. It scaled fast: within a few years it was settling the majority of eligible interbank FX. The netting efficiency it delivered was extraordinary — CLS has reported settling gross values of several trillion dollars a day while requiring pay-ins of only a low single-digit percentage of that gross, because members fund only their multilaterally netted positions per currency. In other words, the system moves perhaps \$50 billion of actual central-bank-money funding to settle well over \$5 trillion of gross trades, a netting ratio on the order of a hundred to one. That liquidity saving is enormous in its own right: it frees up tens of billions of dollars of intraday liquidity that would otherwise be tied up settling FX gross.

**The 2008 stress test.** The 2008 crisis became CLS's first real stress test — and CLS settled straight through the **Lehman Brothers** default in September 2008 *without any counterparty losing principal on a CLS-settled trade with Lehman*, precisely because the PvP logic meant Lehman's unfunded legs simply did not settle rather than settling one-sidedly. When Lehman filed for bankruptcy, it had a large book of open FX trades. For the CLS-settled ones, the mechanism did exactly what it was built to do: trades that Lehman could not fund were simply not settled, and the counterparties — while they had to go to the market to replace those trades — never handed Lehman a leg and got nothing back. Contrast that with what would have happened in a pre-CLS world: counterparties that had paid Lehman their leg in the morning would have become unsecured creditors for the principal of every dollar leg Lehman never paid, a multi-billion-dollar Herstatt event in the middle of the worst week in modern financial history. CLS quietly prevented that. It is the single most important real-world proof point: the mechanism worked exactly as designed in the worst moment it could have been tested, and it is a large part of why the 2008 crisis, for all its horrors, did not include an FX-settlement meltdown.

**The shift the regulators now watch, 2013–present.** The BIS's own analysis (notably a 2019 Quarterly Review article, "FX settlement risk: an unsettled issue") flagged that the *share* of trades settling with PvP protection had **stopped rising and even slipped**, while turnover kept growing — so the *absolute* value at settlement risk was climbing again. The drivers: more trading in non-CLS emerging-market currencies, more same-day and short-dated trades that miss the CLS window, and growth in third-party flows that are harder to corral into PvP. This is why the share line in the data is roughly flat in the 40–55% range rather than marching to 100%.

![Share of global FX settled via CLS payment-versus-payment over time staying in the forty to fifty-five percent range](/imgs/blogs/settlement-risk-and-cls-how-fx-actually-clears-2.png)

That flat-to-falling PvP share, against ever-rising turnover, is the whole modern story in one picture. The percentage protected is high but stuck; the value *not* protected grows with the market. The official-sector response has been to push **PvP adoption wider** — extending CLS-style settlement to more currencies, encouraging on-us PvP at large custodians, and folding settlement-risk reduction into the **FX Global Code** of conduct, whose Principle 35 explicitly tells market participants to reduce settlement risk by using PvP wherever practicable and to actively manage the residual.

**The FX Global Code and the official-sector push.** The reduction of settlement risk is no longer left to individual back offices — it is now codified. The **FX Global Code**, the industry's voluntary set of good-practice principles published in 2017 and updated since, devotes **Principle 35** explicitly to settlement risk, instructing market participants to use PvP settlement mechanisms where available, to measure and monitor their non-PvP exposures, and to manage the residual with bilateral netting, careful counterparty limits, and product choices that reduce gross principal flows. The BIS Committee on Payments and Market Infrastructures has run a multi-year programme to push PvP wider, including encouraging large custodian banks to offer *on-us* PvP (where both legs of a trade between two of the custodian's own clients settle on the custodian's internal books, simultaneously) and supporting new currency additions to CLS and CLS-like regional arrangements. The direction of travel is clear: shrink the un-CLS'd tail by extending PvP, not by hoping the tail stays small.

**The emerging-market frontier.** The growth area for settlement risk is precisely the part of FX that this series cares about most on the policy side: emerging-market currencies. As trade and capital flows shift toward EM, more FX settles in currencies CLS does not cover, often as NDFs or onshore deliverable trades with weaker plumbing. The defences there are still the pre-1974 toolkit — credit limits, careful counterparty selection, and net-settling product designs — plus newer regional and bilateral PvP arrangements that extend the CLS idea currency-pair by currency-pair. China's CIPS and various bilateral central-bank arrangements are early steps toward extending atomic settlement to currencies CLS does not reach. The [biggest-market-on-earth post on the interbank market](/blog/trading/forex/the-biggest-market-on-earth-inside-the-interbank-fx-market) describes the dealer network whose plumbing all of this runs through, and [the map of majors, minors and exotics](/blog/trading/forex/majors-minors-and-exotics-the-map-of-currency-pairs) is exactly the green-versus-red split you see in the currency chart above: the majors are inside the CLS perimeter, the exotics largely outside it.

## The takeaway: read the plumbing, not just the price

If you only ever look at an FX screen, settlement risk is invisible. The rate ticks, the spread is two-tenths of a pip, the trade is "done." But underneath that clean surface, every trade is two payments, in two national monies, through two national systems, on two clocks — and for most of history the deadliest thing that could happen to you in FX was not a bad rate but a counterparty that died in the few hours between your leg and theirs going final. That is settlement risk, named Herstatt risk after the afternoon in 1974 when it cost banks the full principal of the dollars they were owed.

The practical reading skills this leaves you with are concrete. First, **separate the three risks** whenever you size a trade: the price move (small, bounded), the pre-settlement replacement cost (small, bounded), and the settlement principal (the whole notional, catastrophic). They are not the same scale and should not be managed with the same care. Second, **ask which side of the CLS perimeter a trade sits on**. A major-pair trade between two big banks is almost certainly PvP-protected and you can treat principal risk as near-zero. An emerging-market trade, an NDF, a same-day deal, a trade with a small counterparty — those are outside, and your only protection is the old credit toolkit. Third, **respect the residual**: even inside CLS, a counterparty's funding failure turns your safe receive-leg into a same-day liquidity scramble, so the risk is transformed, not vaporised.

This also reframes what "counterparty quality" means in FX. For trades inside the CLS perimeter, the *identity* of your counterparty barely affects your principal risk — the mechanism protects you whether you face the strongest bank in the world or a shaky one, because no leg moves without the other. That is a profound shift: PvP makes settlement principal risk almost *counterparty-independent* inside the box. Outside the box, the opposite is true — counterparty identity is everything, because the only thing standing between you and a full-principal loss is the other side's willingness and ability to pay its leg after you have paid yours. So the single most useful question a settlement desk can ask is binary and structural, not a matter of degree: *is this trade settling PvP, or not?* If yes, principal risk is essentially handled and you can focus on the price. If no, you are managing a 1974-shaped exposure and every classical credit discipline applies. The screen price tells you nothing about which world you are in; only the plumbing does.

And the systemic reading is the one to carry furthest. CLS is one of the quietest, most successful pieces of financial engineering ever built — it took the single largest hidden risk in the world's largest market and, for the eligible core, simply removed it by enforcing that two legs move together or not at all. It is a coordination problem solved by a commitment device, the same shape as deposit insurance solving the bank run. But it solved the problem inside a perimeter, and the world has spent twenty years quietly growing the part of the market that sits *outside* that perimeter. The number to keep in your head is that a couple of trillion dollars a day still settles the way it did in 1974. The plumbing under the price is mostly safe now — but "mostly" is doing a lot of work, and the un-plumbed tail is exactly where the next Herstatt would come from.

The spine of this whole series is that every FX position is a pair, a relative bet between two monies. Settlement risk is the most literal possible version of that truth: you do not own "a currency," you are mid-way through *exchanging* two of them, and the danger lives in the seam where the exchange is half-done.
