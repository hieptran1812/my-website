> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — An exchange rate is not the price of a thing; it is the relative price of two monies, so you never own a currency, only a pair.
>
> - Every FX position is long one money and short another at the same time. There is no neutral way to hold "cash" — even your home currency is a bet against everything else.
> - The two master forces that move a pair are the gap between the two countries' interest rates and the flow of money across borders. Almost everything in this series is a refinement of those two ideas.
> - The market is enormous and quiet: about \$7.5 trillion changes hands every day, and roughly 88% of all trades have the US dollar on one side, so the dollar is the gravity well the rest of the world orbits.
> - The one number to remember: a currency can fall 12% in five weeks when a crowded trade unwinds, as the yen did against the dollar in August 2024 — calm pricing and violent repricing are the same market.

On the morning of 3 July 2024, the Japanese yen was the cheapest it had been against the US dollar in 38 years. It took 161.9 yen to buy a single dollar. For three years a simple bet had paid beautifully: borrow yen, where it cost almost nothing, and park the money in dollars, where it earned over 5%. Pocket the difference. Hedge funds did it, Japanese households did it through their brokerage apps, and the trade was so crowded that everyone assumed the yen would only ever drift lower.

Then, in the space of five weeks, the yen rose almost 13%. By 5 August the dollar bought only 141.7 yen, the US stock market had its worst day in two years, and the fear gauge that measures expected stock swings briefly touched 65.7, a level seen only in genuine panics. Nothing had changed about Japan's economy. What changed was that the Bank of Japan finally nudged its interest rate up, the gap that powered the trade narrowed, and a few large players reached for the exit at the same time. When everyone is on one side of a relative bet, there is no one left to sell to, and the price gaps.

That single episode contains the whole subject of this series. A currency is not a thing you own the way you own a share of a company or an ounce of gold. It is a ratio — the price of one money measured in another money — and that ratio is pulled around by two forces above all others: the gap between two countries' interest rates, and the direction money is flowing across their borders. This first post lays down that spine and shows you the map for everything that follows.

## Foundations: what an exchange rate actually is

Start with the cleanest possible definition, because everything else is built on it.

**An exchange rate is the price of one money expressed in another money.** When you read that EUR/USD is 1.0800, that line is telling you: one euro costs 1.08 US dollars. The first currency in the pair (here, the euro) is called the **base** currency — it is the thing being priced, the "one unit" you are quoting. The second currency (the dollar) is the **quote** or **counter** currency — it is the money you are measuring the price in. So "EUR/USD = 1.0800" reads exactly like "one euro = \$1.08", the same grammar as "one apple = \$0.50".

That ordering matters more than it looks. If EUR/USD rises from 1.0800 to 1.1000, the euro got more expensive in dollar terms — it now takes more dollars to buy one euro. We say the euro **strengthened** and the dollar **weakened**. The number went up, but it went up because the *quote* currency (the dollar) got cheaper. A higher EUR/USD is a weaker dollar. This trips up almost everyone at first, and we devote a whole post to reading a quote line by line, but the core trick is simple: the number is always "how many units of the second currency does one unit of the first cost".

There is a second piece of grammar to learn: the **bid** and the **ask**. A dealer never quotes a single price; it quotes two — the price at which it will buy the base currency from you (the bid) and the slightly higher price at which it will sell it to you (the ask). The gap between them is the **spread**, and it is how the dealer gets paid. A EUR/USD quote of "1.0799 / 1.0801" means the dealer buys euros from you at 1.0799 dollars and sells them to you at 1.0801; you cross that spread every time you trade, paying a tiny toll for the convenience of an instant counterparty. In the deepest pairs the toll is almost nothing; in thin pairs it is the single biggest cost of trading. We dissect the bid, the ask, and the pip in the next post, but the idea to plant now is that the "price" of a currency is really a narrow band, and which edge of the band you get depends on whether you are buying or selling.

Notice what we did *not* do. We never said what a euro is "worth" on its own, in some absolute sense, because there is no such thing. A euro is worth 1.08 dollars, or 0.85 pounds, or 168 yen, or 27,000 Vietnamese dong, and each of those is a different ratio that moves on its own schedule. There is no master price tag floating above all currencies that says "a euro is worth X". Money only has a price *relative to other money*. This is the first and deepest way currencies are different from every other asset, and it is worth sitting with before we go further.

#### Worked example: pricing the same euro three ways

Suppose you have €1,000,000 and you want to know what it is "worth" today. With EUR/USD at 1.0800, it is worth \$1,080,000. With EUR/GBP at 0.8500, the same million euros is worth £850,000. With EUR/JPY at 168.00, it is worth ¥168,000,000. Three different "values", all true at the same instant, none more correct than the others. If tomorrow EUR/USD climbs to 1.0900 but EUR/GBP stays at 0.8500, your euros got 0.93% more valuable measured in dollars and exactly 0% more valuable measured in pounds.

The intuition: a currency has no single value, only a stack of relative prices, so "did the euro go up?" is a meaningless question until you say "up against what?".

### You never own a currency — you own a pair

Here is the consequence that gives this series its name. Because money only has a price relative to other money, **you cannot take a position in a single currency in isolation.** Every FX trade is two trades welded together: you are simultaneously buying one currency and selling another.

When you "buy EUR/USD", you are not buying euros the way you buy a stock. You are buying euros *and* selling dollars in one motion — going long the euro and short the dollar at the same time. If you "sell USD/JPY", you are selling dollars and buying yen. There is no button that buys "just the euro", because to buy a euro you have to pay for it with some other money, and that other money is now your short. Every position is a pair, a spread, a relative bet between two monies.

![Before and after comparison showing a stock is a single bet but a currency is always a pair](/imgs/blogs/why-currencies-are-different-fx-trading-introduction-3.png)

This is genuinely different from how a stock works. When you buy a share of Apple, you own a slice of one company, and its dollar price can go up or down against a fixed yardstick — the dollar. The dollar is the ruler, the share is the thing being measured. In FX there is no fixed ruler. Both sides of the pair are moving. When EUR/USD goes up, you can never immediately tell whether the euro got stronger, the dollar got weaker, or both — you only know the ratio between them changed. To find out which side moved, you have to look at *other* pairs: if EUR/USD rose and USD/JPY fell at the same time, the dollar probably weakened against everything; if EUR/USD rose but EUR/JPY also rose, the euro probably strengthened against everything.

There is a subtler point hiding here, and it is one of the most important ideas in the whole subject. **Even doing nothing is a position.** If you are a person whose salary, rent, and groceries are all in US dollars, then holding dollars feels like holding "nothing", a neutral cash balance. But to a Japanese investor, the same dollars are a leveraged bet that the dollar will hold its value against the yen. Your "home currency" only feels neutral because it is the ruler you happen to measure your life in. The moment you compare it to another money, your cash is revealed as a directional bet you never consciously placed. There is no truly neutral place to stand in currency markets — only different choices of which money to be exposed to.

#### Worked example: the hidden short in your bank account

You are an American holding \$1,000,000 in a checking account, feeling perfectly safe. Over a year the dollar falls 10% against a basket of major currencies — say EUR/USD goes from 1.0800 to 1.2000. Your balance is still \$1,000,000, so in your own eyes you "lost nothing". But a euro that used to cost you \$1.08 now costs \$1.20. A €100,000 European car that was \$108,000 is now \$120,000. Measured against the things a strong euro can buy, your "safe" cash quietly lost about 10% of its purchasing power.

The intuition: holding your home currency is not the absence of a bet, it is a standing bet that your money keeps its value against everyone else's — and sometimes you lose it.

### Reading direction: the quote convention that confuses everyone

Because the price is a ratio, you have to be careful about which way "up" means. There is a fixed convention for which currency is the base and which is the quote, and it is worth internalizing now. For most pairs, the stronger, more senior currency is the base. The euro is the base against almost everything (EUR/USD, EUR/GBP, EUR/JPY), the pound is the base against the dollar (GBP/USD), and the dollar is the base against most of the rest of the world (USD/JPY, USD/CHF, USD/CAD, USD/VND). The rule of thumb traders use is a rough seniority order — euro, then pound, then Australian and New Zealand dollars, then the US dollar, then everything else — and the more senior currency takes the base slot.

This is why the same dollar move looks "up" in one pair and "down" in another, and it catches every beginner at least once. If the dollar strengthens broadly, EUR/USD *falls* (it takes fewer dollars to buy a euro, because the dollar is the quote currency here), but USD/JPY *rises* (it takes more yen to buy a dollar, because the dollar is the base currency here). Same dollar, opposite arrows, purely because of which slot the dollar sits in. The skill you are building is to translate any quote instantly into "how many of the second per one of the first", and then to ask which currency in the pair is the dollar so you know whether a strong-dollar day helps or hurts the number. The mechanics of bid, ask, pips, and lots get the full treatment in [base, quote, pips, and how to read an FX quote](/blog/trading/forex/base-quote-pips-and-how-to-read-an-fx-quote), but the conceptual point belongs here: direction in FX is always relative to an arbitrary-but-fixed ordering, so "the number went up" is never enough — you must know which currency the number is measuring.

#### Worked example: the same dollar move, two opposite arrows

Today EUR/USD is 1.0800 and USD/JPY is 150.00. Overnight the dollar strengthens 1% against both the euro and the yen. EUR/USD falls about 1% to roughly 1.0692, because a stronger dollar means each euro costs fewer dollars. USD/JPY rises about 1% to roughly 151.50, because a stronger dollar means each dollar costs more yen. A trader who was long the dollar made money on both — by *selling* EUR/USD and *buying* USD/JPY — even though one number fell and the other rose.

The intuition: in FX the same view (long the dollar) is expressed as "sell" in one pair and "buy" in another, so you must read the pair's structure before you read its direction.

### Nominal versus real: the price you see hides a second price

One more foundation belongs here, because it sharpens the relative-price idea and you will meet it in every later post. The exchange rate you see quoted — 1.0800, 150.00, 25,450 — is the **nominal** rate, the raw ratio of the two monies. But there is a second, hidden exchange rate underneath it: the **real** exchange rate, which adjusts for the fact that prices inside each country are also moving. A currency can have a stable nominal rate while quietly becoming more or less competitive, because what ultimately matters for trade is not how many yen a dollar buys but how many *Japanese goods* a dollar buys versus how many *American goods* it buys.

Here is why this matters for the spine. If a country runs much higher inflation than its trading partner but keeps its nominal exchange rate fixed, its goods get steadily more expensive to foreigners, its exports suffer, and pressure builds for the nominal rate to fall and restore balance. This is the slow, decade-long anchor behind exchange rates — the idea that nominal rates should eventually drift to keep *real* purchasing power roughly aligned, known as purchasing power parity. It is a weak force on any given day (currencies stray far from it for years, as the yen did, sitting far below its purchasing-power "fair value" through 2024), but it is the gravity that keeps wildly misaligned currencies from drifting forever. The full treatment, the Big Mac index, and the law of one price live in Track B; the point to carry now is that even the price you see is relative in two senses at once — relative to another money, and relative to the price levels inside both countries.

## The two master forces: the rate gap and the flow

If a currency pair is a ratio between two monies, what makes the ratio move? Thousands of things nudge it on any given day, but two forces tower over the rest, and almost every chapter in this series is a refinement of one of them.

**Force one is the interest rate differential** — the gap between what the two countries pay to hold their money. Money, like water, tends to flow toward where it earns the most. If the United States pays 5% to hold dollars and Japan pays 0.5% to hold yen, there is a 4.5-percentage-point reward for moving money out of yen and into dollars. That reward pulls capital toward the dollar, and that buying pressure tends to strengthen the dollar against the yen. The rate gap is the gravitational center of an exchange rate. We do not re-derive *why* central banks set rates where they do — that machinery lives in the [macro-trading series on how monetary policy moves currencies](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-monetary-policy-moves-currencies-rate-differentials) — but the FX consequence is the thing we build everything on.

**Force two is cross-border flow** — the actual movement of money across borders for any reason at all. Some flows chase the rate gap (investors moving money to earn more yield). But flows also come from trade (an exporter selling goods abroad must convert foreign earnings back home), from foreign direct investment (a factory built in another country), from central banks managing their reserves, and from sheer fear (in a panic, money rushes toward whatever feels safest, almost always the dollar). A flow is just demand and supply: to buy a country's assets you must first buy its currency, and that buying moves the price.

The two forces are not independent — and seeing how they relate is the difference between a textbook understanding and a working one. The rate gap is, in a sense, the *cause* and the flow is the *mechanism*. A widening rate gap does not strengthen a currency by magic; it strengthens it *because* the higher yield attracts a flow of capital, and it is the flow — the actual buying — that moves the price. So when you watch a rate gap widen and wait for the currency to follow, what you are really watching for is whether the flow shows up. Usually it does, which is why the rate gap is such a reliable guide. But occasionally the flow refuses to come — because investors fear the country, distrust the central bank, or worry about getting their money back out — and then a wide rate gap fails to support the currency at all. This is precisely the case of a country in crisis hiking rates while its currency keeps falling: the gap is there, but the flow has dried up. Holding both forces in view, and remembering that the gap only works through the flow, is what keeps you from being fooled by a high interest rate that no one trusts.

The chart below is the cleanest real-world picture of the first force. It puts the USD/JPY exchange rate next to the gap between US and Japanese two-year interest rates over 2019 to 2025. Watch how the two lines move together: as the rate gap blew out from almost nothing in 2020 to about 4.35 points in 2022, the yen collapsed from 103 per dollar to 131, then to 157. The rate gap and the pair are practically dancing.

![Chart of USD JPY against the US Japan two year rate gap from 2019 to 2025](/imgs/blogs/why-currencies-are-different-fx-trading-introduction-2.png)

#### Worked example: what a 4.5-point rate gap is worth in a year

You hold a \$1,000,000 position that is long dollars and short yen (you sold USD/JPY's counter currency, the yen, and bought the dollar). The US pays 5.0% on dollars; Japan pays 0.5% on yen. Ignoring any move in the exchange rate, over one year you earn 5.0% on your \$1,000,000 of dollars (\$50,000) and pay 0.5% to borrow the yen you are short (\$5,000). Your net carry is \$50,000 − \$5,000 = \$45,000, or 4.5%, which is exactly the rate gap. You got paid the differential just for holding the position.

The intuition: the rate gap is not a theory — it shows up directly as cash in your account every day you hold the higher-yielding side, which is the entire engine of the carry trade.

It helps to see that the two forces operate on very different clocks, because that mismatch is the source of most of the drama in currencies. The rate gap is slow and sticky. Central banks move in small, telegraphed steps, a quarter-point at a time, with months of warning, so the rate-gap channel produces grinding, persistent trends that can run for years — the yen's three-year slide is the perfect example. Flows, by contrast, can turn on a dime. A piece of news, a missed data print, a sudden spike in fear, and capital that took two years to walk into a trade tries to sprint out in two days. The slow force builds the position; the fast force detonates it.

There is also a difference in *which* flows matter, and it is worth pinning down because the financial press blurs it constantly. Trade flows — an exporter converting foreign sales back home, an importer buying foreign currency to pay a supplier — are real, but they are small and slow relative to the size of the market. The flows that actually move a currency day to day are **capital flows**: investors moving money to chase yield, funds rotating between countries, foreigners buying or dumping a country's bonds and stocks. A country can run a trade deficit for decades and have a strong currency, because the capital flowing in to buy its assets dwarfs the trade flowing out — the United States is the standing example. So when you hear "the trade deficit will sink the currency", be skeptical: trade is the slow undercurrent, but capital is the tide, and capital chases the rate gap. The balance of payments that ties these flows together gets its own post in Track B; for now, just hold that the flow that matters most is yield-seeking capital, which is really force one wearing force two's clothes.

Now hold those two forces in mind together, because the most important thing about them is that they usually point the same way and occasionally rip apart. When US rates are high *and* the world is calm, both the rate gap and the flow point toward the dollar, and the dollar grinds higher. But when a shock hits, flows can violently reverse even though the rate gap hasn't moved — frightened money sells the high-yielders and rushes home, and a trade that everyone loved unwinds in days. The yen in August 2024 is the textbook case: the rate gap barely budged, but the *flow* slammed into reverse, and the pair moved 12% in five weeks. The figure below shows how both channels feed one price, and how a single shock can flip both at once.

![Diagram showing the rate gap and cross border flows both feeding one exchange rate that a shock can reverse](/imgs/blogs/why-currencies-are-different-fx-trading-introduction-4.png)

This is the spine of the whole series, so let me state it once, plainly, in the form you should carry through every later post: *an exchange rate is the relative price of two monies; what moves it is the gap between two countries' interest rates plus the flow of money across borders; and the calm accumulation of carry and the violent unwind of crowded flows are not two different markets but the same one in two moods.*

There is a third force that deserves an honorable mention, even though it sits below the two masters: **expectations and positioning**. Markets price the *future*, not just the present, so what matters is not only today's rate gap but where traders think it is heading. If the US is expected to start cutting rates while Japan is expected to start hiking, the dollar can weaken against the yen *before* either central bank moves a single basis point, because the market front-runs the change. This is why a currency can fall on a rate hike if the hike was smaller than expected, or rally on a hold if the central bank sounds hawkish about the future. Expectations are really a refinement of force one — they are the rate gap the market believes is coming — but they explain why currencies sometimes seem to move "against" the current rate gap. The forward rate, which packages the market's expected path of the rate gap into a single tradeable number, is the bridge between today's gap and tomorrow's, and it gets its own treatment in [the fixed-income post on forward rates](/blog/trading/fixed-income/forward-rates-what-the-market-expects-rates-to-be).

## The biggest, quietest market on earth

People picture currency trading as a frenzy of shouting traders, but the FX market is mostly invisible plumbing. It is the largest financial market in the world by a wide margin — about \$7.5 trillion changes hands every single day, according to the Bank for International Settlements' 2022 survey, which is the closest thing the market has to an official census. For scale, the entire US stock market trades a few hundred billion dollars on a busy day. FX moves the equivalent of the annual output of a large economy before lunch.

Yet it has no central exchange, no opening bell, no single price. It is an **over-the-counter** market: a web of banks, brokers, and electronic networks trading directly with each other, twenty-four hours a day, five days a week, as the trading day rolls from Tokyo to London to New York and back. The biggest banks quote prices to each other on the **interbank** market, the wholesale layer where the real liquidity lives, and everyone else — corporations, funds, smaller banks, and the retail tail — trades at prices derived from there. We pull this structure apart in the post on [the interbank market](/blog/trading/forex/the-biggest-market-on-earth-inside-the-interbank-fx-market), but the headline is that FX is decentralized, continuous, and dominated by a handful of enormous dealers.

Here is the fact that surprises almost everyone, and it reframes what "FX trading" even means. **Most of that \$7.5 trillion is not people betting on exchange rates at all.** The single largest slice is **FX swaps** — short-term arrangements where two parties exchange currencies now and agree to swap them back later, essentially a collateralized loan in two currencies. They are the market's plumbing for funding and hedging, not directional bets. Spot trading — the buy-low-sell-high speculation most people associate with FX — is a minority of the volume. The chart below breaks the daily turnover down by instrument, and the story is stark: swaps dwarf spot.

![Chart of FX turnover by instrument in 2022 showing swaps dominate over spot](/imgs/blogs/why-currencies-are-different-fx-trading-introduction-5.png)

The 24-hour, no-central-exchange structure has practical consequences that shape how currencies trade. Because the market follows the sun, liquidity is not constant through the day. It is thin during the Asian morning, deepens enormously when London opens (London alone handles about 38% of global FX turnover), and peaks during the four-hour window when London and New York are both open — that overlap is when the deepest liquidity and the cleanest prices appear, and also when the biggest data releases land. As New York winds down and before Tokyo wakes, liquidity thins again, and that quiet window is where currencies are most prone to sudden, exaggerated moves on light volume. The August 2024 yen low printed in exactly such a thin Asian session, when there were fewer buyers to absorb the selling. Knowing the rhythm of the trading day is part of reading the market: the same order that barely moves the price at the London-New York peak can punch a hole in it during the Tokyo lull.

The decentralization also means there is no single "official" price for a currency at any instant — there are slightly different prices at different dealers, knitted together by arbitrage into a tight band. This is why FX has no closing price the way a stock does, why a "fixing" (an agreed reference price calculated at a set time, like the 4pm London fix) has to be manufactured for valuation purposes, and why the market can gap through levels overnight when your home exchange is closed. A stock trader checks the closing bell; a currency never closes, so its risks live with you around the clock.

Why does this matter to you as a reader trying to understand currencies? Because it tells you what the market is *for*. The dominant use of FX is not gambling on the next move in EUR/USD; it is the machinery that lets a German company pay a Japanese supplier, lets a fund hedge its overseas stocks back to dollars, and lets banks fund their dollar needs overnight. The speculative layer rides on top of this enormous river of practical, boring, necessary flow. When you trade a currency, you are dipping into a market built mostly for plumbing — which is exactly why the spreads are so tight and the depth so vast in the major pairs. The three ways to trade a currency — spot, forward, and swap — each get their own treatment in [the post on spot, forward, and swap](/blog/trading/forex/spot-forward-and-swap-the-three-ways-to-trade-a-currency).

It also reframes what "liquidity" means and why it is wildly uneven across currencies. The major pairs — EUR/USD, USD/JPY, GBP/USD — are the most liquid financial instruments on the planet, with spreads measured in fractions of a pip and depth that can absorb hundreds of millions of dollars without flinching. Drop one tier to the crosses (pairs not involving the dollar, like EUR/GBP) and the spreads widen. Drop another tier to emerging-market currencies (USD/MXN, USD/TRY) and the spreads can be tens of pips, the depth a fraction of the majors, and the price prone to gapping. Drop to the truly exotic and you may not be able to trade the currency directly at all — you trade a *derivative* of it offshore, because the currency cannot be freely delivered. This liquidity ladder is not a footnote; it is the difference between a currency you can move billions through quietly and one where a modest order moves the market against you. The whole taxonomy of majors, crosses, and exotics, and the liquidity tiers that go with them, gets its own post at the end of Track A.

Why is the major-pair market so deep when the exotic market is so thin? Because depth follows the plumbing. EUR/USD is deep because it sits on top of the enormous, continuous flow of trade, hedging, and funding between the world's two largest currency blocs — every European firm with dollar costs, every dollar fund with euro assets, every bank funding euros or dollars overnight passes through that pair. An exotic currency has none of that supporting river: the only people trading it are a handful of locals, a few foreign investors, and the central bank. So when you trade a major you are dipping a cup into a river; when you trade an exotic you are dipping into a puddle, and the puddle sloshes when you touch it.

#### Worked example: the cost of crossing the spread

You want to trade €1,000,000 against the dollar. In EUR/USD, the most liquid pair on earth, a dealer might quote you a price like 1.07999 / 1.08001 — a spread of just 0.2 pips (a **pip** is the standard smallest price increment, here the fourth decimal place). To buy and immediately sell back, you cross that spread once, costing you about 0.00002 dollars per euro, or roughly \$20 on a million-euro round trip. Now try the same in an illiquid emerging-market pair where the spread is 40 pips: the same round trip costs you about \$4,000, two hundred times more.

The intuition: the major pairs are cheap to trade precisely because they sit on top of trillions in daily plumbing flow, while exotic currencies are expensive because almost no one is on the other side.

## The dollar is the sun everyone orbits

If you remember one structural fact about the currency market, make it this one. The US dollar is not just another currency in the list — it is the gravity well at the center of the entire system. Roughly **88% of all FX trades have the dollar on one side.** Not 88% of dollar trades; 88% of *all* trades, including ones between two non-dollar currencies, because the cheapest way to swap, say, Mexican pesos into Thai baht is often to go peso-to-dollar and then dollar-to-baht. The dollar is the universal intermediary, the money on the other side of almost everything.

![Chart showing the US dollar is on one side of 88 percent of all FX trades](/imgs/blogs/why-currencies-are-different-fx-trading-introduction-8.png)

This dominance has deep roots: the dollar is the world's main **invoicing currency** (most global trade, including oil, is priced in dollars), the dominant **reserve currency** (central banks hold most of their rainy-day savings in dollars — about 57.8% of all disclosed reserves as of late 2024, with the euro a distant second around 20%), and the **funding currency** of the global financial system (an enormous amount of borrowing outside the United States is denominated in dollars). The full story of the dollar system — why it rules, and the eurodollar plumbing beneath it — is a whole track in this series and overlaps with the [cross-asset view of the dollar as market gravity](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-dollar-cross-asset-gravity) and the [macro-trading take on why the dollar rules](/blog/trading/macro-trading/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy).

What makes the dollar's position so durable is that these roles reinforce one another in a loop. Because oil and most commodities are priced in dollars, importers everywhere need dollars, so they hold dollar reserves. Because they hold dollars, it is convenient to borrow and lend in dollars, which deepens the dollar funding market. Because the funding market is deep, the dollar is the cheapest and most liquid currency to trade, which is why it sits on the other side of most trades, which is why it is the natural unit to invoice in. Each role makes the next one more natural, and the whole structure has held for the better part of a century despite repeated predictions of its demise. The dollar's reserve share has drifted down only slowly — from over 70% in 2001 to under 58% today — and no other currency has come close to replacing it. For a trader, the practical implication is not whether the dollar will "stay king" but that the dollar's centrality means *every* currency you look at has a dollar leg lurking inside it, even pairs that don't mention the dollar at all.

For the introduction, the practical takeaway is this: because the dollar is on one side of most trades, **the dollar's mood is the single biggest driver of every other currency.** When the dollar is strong, almost everything else is weak against it, often regardless of those countries' own stories. When traders talk about "the dollar" as if it were one thing, they often mean the **dollar index (DXY)**, a basket that measures the dollar against six developed-market currencies — though, as we will see in a later post, that index is more than half just a bet against the euro and is not really a "broad" dollar measure at all. Still, it is the headline number, and it tells you which way the tide is running.

The funding role deserves a closer look, because it is the channel through which the dollar reaches out and squeezes the rest of the world. An enormous quantity of dollar debt is owed by borrowers *outside* the United States — governments, companies, and banks in emerging markets and beyond who borrowed dollars because dollar loans were cheap and plentiful. This offshore dollar credit runs into the tens of trillions. The catch is that these borrowers earn their income in their own local currency but owe their debt in dollars. So when the dollar strengthens, their debt — measured in the currency they actually earn — silently grows, even though they never borrowed another cent. A strong dollar tightens financial conditions for half the planet at once, which is why a rising dollar so often coincides with stress in emerging markets and why traders watch the dollar as a global risk barometer, not just an American one. The full eurodollar plumbing and the doom-loop dynamics get a whole track later; the point here is that the dollar is not merely one currency among many, it is the denominator of the world's debt.

#### Worked example: the dollar as the world's denominator

A government in an emerging market issues a \$5,000,000,000 dollar bond to fund infrastructure, because borrowing in dollars costs it 6% a year while borrowing in its own currency would cost 14%. For years this looks brilliant — it saves 8 percentage points of interest, roughly \$400,000,000 a year. Then a global risk-off wave hits, the dollar strengthens 30% against its currency, and suddenly the \$5,000,000,000 it owes is worth 30% more in the local currency it collects taxes in. The "cheap" dollar loan has quietly become a budget-busting liability, and the country may have to choose between defaulting and slashing spending — all without having borrowed a single extra dollar.

The intuition: borrowing in dollars while earning in something else is a hidden short on your own currency, and the bill comes due exactly when the dollar is strongest and you can least afford it.

The dollar's centrality also explains a strange asymmetry: the dollar tends to win at *both* ends of the spectrum. It strengthens when the US economy is booming and US rates are high (money flows in to chase yield), and it *also* strengthens when the world is in a panic (money flows in to hide, because the dollar is the safest harbor). It is only weak in the calm middle, when global growth is humming and investors feel brave enough to venture out into riskier, higher-yielding currencies. This pattern is called the **dollar smile**, and it gets its own post — but notice that it is just our two master forces again: the rate gap drives the right side of the smile, and a flight-to-safety flow drives the left side.

#### Worked example: a strong dollar squeezing a foreign borrower

A company in an emerging market borrowed \$10,000,000 because dollar loans were cheap, but it earns its revenue in local currency. When it took the loan, the exchange rate was 20 units of local currency per dollar, so the debt was equivalent to 200,000,000 local units. Then the dollar strengthens 25% and the rate moves to 25 units per dollar. The dollar debt is still \$10,000,000 — but now it takes 250,000,000 local units to repay it. The company's debt just grew by 50,000,000 local units without it borrowing a single extra dollar.

The intuition: a rising dollar tightens the screws on everyone in the world who borrowed dollars but earns something else, which is why a strong dollar acts like a global brake.

## When the calm breaks: carry and the unwind

We have met the two forces and the central role of the dollar. Now watch them collide, because the most dramatic and instructive events in currency markets all share the same shape: a trade that pays steadily for years, then craters in days.

The archetype is the **carry trade** — borrowing a low-yielding currency to hold a high-yielding one, pocketing the rate gap. It is the purest expression of force one. When the yen yields 0.5% and the dollar yields 5%, you can borrow yen, buy dollars, and earn the 4.5% difference. As long as the yen doesn't strengthen by more than 4.5% over the year, you come out ahead. For years it didn't, and the trade looked like free money. The whole logic, P&L, and history of carry get a dedicated post — [the carry trade explained from zero](/blog/trading/forex/the-carry-trade-getting-paid-to-hold-a-currency) — and the master variable behind it, the rate gap, gets its own deep dive in [interest rate differentials, the master variable of FX](/blog/trading/forex/interest-rate-differentials-the-master-variable-of-fx).

The numbers behind the carry trade are worth seeing concretely, because they explain why it became so enormous. On the funding side sit the low-yielders: the yen at around 0.25%, the Swiss franc around 0.50%. On the receiving side sit the high-yielders: the Mexican peso around 10%, the Brazilian real above 12%, and at the extreme the Turkish lira at 50%. A trader who borrows yen at 0.25% and holds Mexican pesos at 10% is collecting close to 10 percentage points a year in pure carry, before any move in the exchange rate. Stretch that across a leveraged book and the income is large and, for long stretches, astonishingly steady. That steadiness is the seduction. It lulls everyone — funds, banks, and households alike — into treating carry income as if it were a salary rather than a risk premium, and into adding leverage to make a good thing better.

But here is the catch that makes FX so treacherous. The carry trade pays a small, steady premium and occasionally suffers a huge, sudden loss — traders call it "picking up pennies in front of a steamroller". The reason is force two. When a carry trade is popular, everyone piles onto the same side: long the high-yielder, short the funder. The position becomes crowded. Then some shock — a central bank surprise, a spike in fear, a bad data print — makes a few players want out. But because everyone is on the same side, there is no one to sell to. The price gaps. The loss frightens more players into selling, which moves the price further, which frightens more players still. The slow accumulation of carry reverses into a violent flight in a self-reinforcing cascade.

That is exactly what the yen did in August 2024. The chart below traces it day by day: USD/JPY fell from 161.9 on 3 July to 141.7 on 5 August — about 12% in five weeks — as the Bank of Japan raised rates, the carry shrank, and the crowded trade stampeded for the exit all at once.

![Chart of the August 2024 yen carry unwind showing USD JPY falling from 162 to 142](/imgs/blogs/why-currencies-are-different-fx-trading-introduction-7.png)

#### Worked example: the steamroller catches the carry

You put \$1,000,000 into the classic yen carry: borrow yen at 0.5%, hold dollars at 5%, for a 4.5% annual carry of \$45,000. You hold from January, collecting carry, feeling clever. Then the August unwind hits and USD/JPY falls 12% in five weeks. Your long-dollar, short-yen position loses 12% of \$1,000,000 = \$120,000 on the exchange-rate move. Against that you have earned a little over half a year of carry, roughly \$26,000. Net, you are down about \$94,000 — and you gave back more than two years' worth of carry in five weeks.

The intuition: carry is a bet that nothing dramatic happens, so it earns a little when markets are calm and loses a lot when flows reverse — the steady premium and the sudden crash are two faces of the same trade.

There is a beautiful piece of theory hiding inside why the carry trade exists at all, and it is worth a paragraph because it overturns a natural intuition. A reasonable person would expect that a currency paying a higher interest rate should be *expected to weaken* by exactly that rate gap, so that no one can get a free lunch — if Japan pays 0.5% and the US pays 5%, the dollar "should" fall 4.5% over the year against the yen, cancelling the rate advantage. This idea has a name, **uncovered interest parity**, and it is one of the most elegant predictions in economics. It is also, empirically, mostly wrong. On average, high-yielding currencies do *not* weaken by the full rate gap; they often weaken less, or even strengthen, so the carry trader keeps the differential as profit. The persistent failure of uncovered interest parity is the entire reason the carry trade pays — it is the market handing out a premium for bearing the risk of the occasional violent crash. We devote a full post to this "forward puzzle" in Track B, but the headline is that the carry trade is not an inefficiency someone forgot to arbitrage away; it is compensation for the steamroller, and the compensation is real precisely because the steamroller is real.

This same shape — quiet pricing, violent repricing — recurs across every crisis in this series. When the Swiss National Bank abandoned its currency floor in January 2015, the franc jumped about 30% against the euro in minutes, bankrupting brokers. When the UK was forced out of the European exchange-rate mechanism on Black Wednesday in 1992, the pound fell about 15% as the Bank of England's defense failed and George Soros made a billion dollars betting against it. When Asian currencies' dollar pegs broke in 1997, the Indonesian rupiah lost 83% of its value. Different decades, different currencies, but always the same anatomy: a price held artificially calm by a crowded trade or a defended peg, then a flow reversal that the calm pricing was never built to absorb. The formal coordination-game model of how a peg actually breaks lives in the [game-theory series on central-bank credibility](/blog/trading/game-theory/the-central-bank-game-credibility-commitment-and-dont-fight-the-fed); we keep the narrative and link out for the math.

## Common misconceptions

A handful of beliefs trip up almost every newcomer to currencies. Each one dissolves the moment you hold the relative-price spine firmly.

**"A strong currency is good and a weak currency is bad."** This is the most stubborn myth, and it is wrong in both directions. A weak currency makes a country's exports cheaper and more competitive abroad, which is why countries sometimes *want* a weaker currency and central banks have, at times, deliberately pushed theirs down. A strong currency makes imports cheap but can hollow out an export sector. There is no universally "good" level — there is only a level that suits a particular economy's needs at a particular moment. The yen falling from 103 to 157 per dollar was a disaster for Japanese consumers buying imported energy and a windfall for Toyota's overseas earnings, at the same time.

**"Cash is the safe, neutral asset."** As we saw, holding your home currency is itself a bet — a standing long position in your own money against every other money. When the US dollar fell roughly 10% against major currencies in 2017, an American holding dollars "did nothing" and still lost about 10% of global purchasing power. There is no neutral cash; there is only a choice of which currency to be exposed to. Safety is relative, just like price.

**"FX is just like trading stocks, but with currencies."** The single-versus-pair difference changes everything. A stock has a fixed ruler (its home currency) and a clear long-term anchor (the company's earnings). A currency has no fixed ruler and no earnings — both sides of every pair float, and the long-run anchor (purchasing power parity) is so weak that prices can stray from it for a decade. The forces are different too: stocks are driven by company fundamentals, currencies by the gap between two countries' interest rates and the flow of capital between them.

**"The dollar index measures the dollar against the world."** The DXY is more than half just a short-euro position (the euro carries about a 57.6% weight), with the yen, pound, Canadian dollar, krona, and franc making up the rest. It contains no Chinese renminbi, no Mexican peso, no emerging-market currencies at all — even though those are where much of the world's actual trade and stress lives. It is a useful headline, but it is a narrow developed-market basket, not a true measure of the dollar against the globe.

**"If a country raises interest rates, its currency always goes up."** Usually, yes — a higher rate widens the gap and pulls in yield-seeking flow. But not always, and the exception is revealing. If a central bank is hiking *because* its currency is in a crisis and inflation is spiraling, higher rates can signal panic rather than strength, and the currency can keep falling. Turkey raised rates to 50% while the lira kept sinking, because investors saw the sky-high rate as confirmation that something was deeply wrong rather than as an attractive yield. The rate gap is the master variable, but flows and confidence can override it when fear takes the wheel — a high rate only attracts capital if investors trust they will get their principal back.

**"A bigger or richer economy has a stronger currency."** There is no such law. The Japanese yen is the currency of the world's fourth-largest economy and spent 2024 at multi-decade lows. The Swiss franc, currency of a country smaller than many US states, is one of the strongest and most prized currencies on earth. What drives a currency's price is not the size of the economy behind it but the rate gap, the flows, and the trust — a small, stable, high-saving country with a credible central bank can have a far stronger currency than a large, indebted one. Currency strength measures relative demand for a money, not national wealth.

**"FX is a zero-sum casino with no fundamentals."** It is true that for every long there is a short, so in a narrow accounting sense one trader's gain is another's loss. But that does not make currencies random. The rate gap and the flows are real, persistent forces that produce real, persistent trends — the yen's three-year slide was not noise, it was the rate gap pulling steadily in one direction. The people on the losing side of those trends were often not speculators at all but hedgers and corporations who held the currency for non-speculative reasons and accepted the loss as a cost of doing business. Calling FX a casino mistakes the presence of a counterparty for the absence of a reason.

## How it shows up in real markets

The spine is not academic. Here are real episodes where you can see the two forces and the relative-price logic acting in plain sight. Read each one as a small exam: before you reach the explanation, ask yourself the three questions — against what, what is the rate gap doing, which way is the flow — and check your read against what actually happened.

**EUR/USD hits parity, September 2022.** For the first time in two decades, one euro bought exactly one dollar — EUR/USD touched 1.00, down from 1.23 the year before. What happened? The US Federal Reserve was hiking aggressively to fight inflation while the European Central Bank lagged, so the US-Europe rate gap blew open in the dollar's favor (force one). At the same time, Russia's invasion of Ukraine triggered an energy crisis that hammered Europe and sent frightened money rushing into the dollar as a haven (force two). Both forces pointed the same way, and the dollar surged to a two-decade high, with the dollar index peaking at 114.8 on 28 September 2022. Parity wasn't a magic level; it was just where the two forces happened to drive the ratio.

**The yen's long slide, 2021 to 2024.** This is the cleanest single illustration of the rate gap in modern markets. As the chart earlier in this post showed, the US-Japan two-year rate gap widened from near zero in 2020 to about 4.35 points by 2022, as the Fed hiked hard while the Bank of Japan kept rates pinned near zero. The yen fell almost in lockstep, from 103 per dollar to 157. No crisis, no drama — just the relentless pull of a widening rate gap, year after year, exactly as force one predicts.

**The August 2024 unwind.** Then came the reversal we opened with. The Bank of Japan finally raised rates a notch on 31 July, the rate gap began to shrink, and the enormous crowded carry trade — long dollars, short yen — stampeded for the exit. USD/JPY fell 12% in five weeks, dragging global stocks down with it as leveraged players sold everything to cover their losses. Force two, the flow, slammed into reverse, and the calm pricing of a beloved trade gave way to a violent repricing in days. This is the carry-crash anatomy we will study in detail.

**The Swiss franc shock, January 2015.** For three years the Swiss National Bank had held a floor under EUR/CHF at 1.20, promising to print unlimited francs to stop the franc from strengthening past that line. The cost of holding it ballooned — the SNB's reserves swelled to defend the floor — until, on 15 January 2015, it abandoned the policy without warning. EUR/CHF collapsed from 1.20 to below 0.85 in minutes, a roughly 30% move that bankrupted several retail brokers whose clients couldn't cover their losses. The lesson, which Track E develops, is that a defended level holds a price artificially calm right up until the defense becomes too costly — and then the repricing is brutal precisely because it was suppressed for so long.

**EUR/USD and the dollar smile, 2020 to 2021.** It is worth adding one episode that runs the *other* way, so the dollar's two-sided behavior is concrete. In the depths of the March 2020 pandemic panic, the dollar spiked violently as the entire world scrambled for the one money everyone owes and trusts — the left side of the dollar smile, the flight-to-safety flow. Then, as central banks flooded the system with liquidity and the global recovery took hold through late 2020 and 2021, the dollar *weakened* steadily; EUR/USD climbed back from around 1.07 to over 1.22, and the dollar index sagged from the high 90s toward 90. Investors felt brave, ventured out of the safe-haven dollar into higher-yielding and riskier currencies, and the dollar drifted down through the calm middle of its smile. Same currency, opposite behavior, eighteen months apart — driven entirely by whether the world was panicking or recovering. The dollar smile is not a quirk; it is our two forces seen from the dollar's side of every trade.

Across all four, notice the same grammar. The exchange rate is always a *relative* price (parity is just one euro equaling one dollar, not the euro being "worth" anything absolute). The driver is always some mix of the rate gap and the flow. And the biggest moves come not from the slow grind of the rate gap but from the sudden reversal of crowded flows — the calm and the crash are the same market.

## The takeaway: how to read any currency from here

You now hold the one idea that organizes everything else, so let me hand it back to you in the form you should actually use.

When you look at any currency — EUR/USD on a screen, the headline that "the dollar is strong", the yen "collapsing", the pound "rallying" — train yourself to ask three questions, in this order.

First: **against what?** A currency never moves on its own; it moves relative to another money. "The dollar is strong" is shorthand for "the dollar is strong against this particular basket of other currencies", and it can be strong against the euro and weak against the peso in the same week. Refuse to accept a one-sided statement about a currency's value. Always supply the missing other side of the pair.

Second: **what is the rate gap doing?** Look at the two countries' interest rates. If the gap is widening in favor of one currency, there is gravitational pull toward that currency, and the carry rewards holding it. The rate differential is the master variable, the thing you check first, because it explains more of the slow, persistent trends than anything else. When you read that a central bank hiked or cut, immediately translate it into "the gap just moved this way, so the pull on the currency just shifted that way".

Third: **which way is the flow running, and is the trade crowded?** The rate gap explains the calm trend; the flow explains the violent breaks. Ask whether money is rushing toward this currency (for yield, for safety, for trade) or away from it, and — crucially — ask whether everyone is already on the same side. A trade that everyone loves is a trade with no buyers left when the mood turns. The most dangerous moment in any currency is not when it is falling, but when it has been quietly, profitably rising for so long that the whole market is leaning one way.

#### Worked example: turning a headline into the three questions

You read: "Dollar surges as Fed signals higher-for-longer; yen hits fresh lows." Run the drill. *Against what?* The headline gives you two answers — the dollar is up broadly, and specifically the dollar is up against the yen, so USD/JPY rose. *What is the rate gap doing?* "Higher-for-longer" means US rates will stay elevated while Japan's stay near zero, so the US-Japan gap is staying wide or widening — force one points firmly at the dollar. *Which way is the flow, and is it crowded?* Money keeps flowing toward the higher-yielding dollar, but if USD/JPY is at "fresh lows" for the yen after a long one-way move, the long-dollar trade may be getting crowded, which raises the odds of a sharp snap-back later. In thirty seconds you have turned a vague headline into a structured read: persistent dollar strength from the rate gap, with a building crowding risk in the flow.

The intuition: the three questions convert any currency headline into the same two-force frame, so you are never guessing — you are checking the gap and the flow.

Hold those three questions and the rest of this series becomes a set of tools for answering them better. Track B sharpens the rate-gap question into real parity conditions. Track C turns the carry into a strategy with a known fat tail. Track D explains why the dollar sits on the other side of all of it. Tracks E and H show what happens when central banks try to override the forces and the forces win anyway. And Track G grounds it in a real emerging-market currency you can watch in real time.

It is worth being honest about what this framework does and does not give you, because that honesty is itself part of thinking like a trader. The two forces do not tell you tomorrow's price. They are not a crystal ball; they are a way of organizing what you know so that you are surprised by the right things and unsurprised by the rest. A trader using this frame still loses on plenty of individual trades, because flows can stay irrational longer than any single position can survive, and because the crowding that makes a trade dangerous is exactly the crowding that makes it profitable right up until the break. What the frame buys you is the ability to know *why* you are in a position, what would change your mind, and where the hidden risk is sitting — which is the difference between trading a view and gambling on a number. The capstone of this series, in Track H, assembles the frame into a written, repeatable process: a scorecard that weighs the rate differential, the flows and positioning, valuation, and the vol the market is pricing, so that forming a currency view becomes a discipline rather than a hunch.

There is one more habit worth building now, before any of the machinery. **Watch other pairs to locate the move.** Because every quote is a ratio, a single pair can never tell you which side moved. If you want to know whether a EUR/USD rally is "really" a strong euro or "really" a weak dollar, glance at a third pair: if the dollar is also falling against the yen, the pound, and the peso at the same time, you are watching a dollar story, not a euro story, and you should expect it to show up everywhere. If instead EUR/USD is rising while the euro is *also* rising against the yen and the pound, then the euro is the mover and the story is European. This triangulation — using a third currency to decompose a two-currency move — is the single most useful reflex in reading FX, and it falls straight out of the relative-price spine: a price is two monies, so to isolate one you need a third reference point.

The deepest thing to carry forward is the one we started with. There is no neutral ground in currency markets. Every position is a pair, every pair is a relative bet between two monies, and even the "cash" in your pocket is a quiet wager that your money holds its value against everyone else's. Once you stop looking for the absolute value of a currency and start asking "relative to what, pulled by which rate gap, pushed by whose flow", you are no longer reading a price. You are reading a balance of forces — and that is what it means to think about currencies like a trader.

That shift in perspective is genuinely hard, because everything in everyday life trains you to think of money as a fixed yardstick. Prices are in dollars, salaries are in dollars, your sense of "how much is a lot" is calibrated in dollars, so the dollar feels like the unmoving ground beneath the economy. The single most valuable thing this series can do is dislodge that feeling. The dollar is not the ground; it is one currency on one side of a ratio, moving against every other money all the time, and the only reason it feels fixed is that it happens to be the ruler you measure your life with. A Japanese reader feels the same fixedness about the yen, a Vietnamese reader about the dong, and each is equally an illusion of perspective. The moment you can hold two monies in view at once and ask which is moving against which, currencies stop being mysterious and start being legible — a balance of two well-understood forces, readable by anyone willing to ask the three questions in order.
