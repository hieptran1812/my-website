> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The 1997 Asian crisis was not a story about bad luck or speculators alone; it was a balance-sheet trap. A region that pegged its currencies softly to the dollar, borrowed short-term dollars heavily, and ran current-account deficits had built a machine that worked beautifully right up until the peg moved — and then it ran in reverse, violently.
>
> - The setup was a **double mismatch**: dollar-denominated debt against local-currency revenue, and short-term funding against long-term assets. The peg hid both risks until July 2 1997, when the baht floated.
> - Once the baht broke, a **doom loop** took over: a falling currency inflates the local value of dollar debt, which forces firms to buy dollars and banks to pull credit, which drops the currency further. The fall feeds itself.
> - **Contagion** spread by analogy — markets sold every economy with the same shape. Indonesia was hit worst: the rupiah lost roughly **83%** of its value against the dollar.
> - The one number to remember: **−83%**. That is what an unhedged dollar debt does to you when your currency cracks. The lasting lesson — Asia spent the next two decades building reserve war chests so it would never have to ask the IMF for help again.

On the morning of July 2 1997, the Bank of Thailand did something it had sworn for months it would never do. It let the baht go.

For more than a decade the baht had been pinned near 25 to the US dollar — a number so stable that Thai companies treated it less like a market price and more like a law of physics. You could borrow dollars in Tokyo or New York at low rates, bring them home, and never once think about what would happen if 25 became 30, or 40, or 56. The central bank had promised the peg would hold, and it had spent its reserves and its credibility defending that promise through the spring. By late June the reserves were a fiction — most of what looked like a war chest had already been sold forward to speculators and was no longer available. So the defence ended. The baht was allowed to float, and within hours it was sinking. By the depths of the crisis it would take **56 baht** to buy one dollar — a fall of more than half.

What happened over the following twelve months was not one crisis but five, chained together. Thailand was first. Then the same logic that broke the baht reached out and broke the Indonesian rupiah (the worst hit of all, down roughly **83%**), the Korean won, the Malaysian ringgit, and the Philippine peso. Economies that had been the envy of the developing world — the "Asian tigers," posting growth rates the West could only dream of — were suddenly queuing at the door of the International Monetary Fund. This post is about *why*: not who to blame, but the exact mechanical chain that turned a managed-currency devaluation into a region-wide solvency crisis. That chain — peg plus dollar debt plus deficit — is the canonical EM currency crisis, and every version since has rhymed with it.

## Foundations: how the Asian miracle became fragile

Before we can understand the break, we have to understand what was being broken — and to do that, three plain-English terms.

**A peg.** A currency peg is a promise by a central bank to keep its currency at (or very near) a fixed exchange rate against another currency — here, the US dollar. Thailand's baht "peg" was a *soft* peg: officially the baht tracked a basket of currencies, but in practice it was managed to stay close to 25 per dollar. The central bank kept that promise the only way a central bank can — by standing ready to buy its own currency with its **foreign-exchange reserves** (its stockpile of dollars) whenever the market tried to push the baht weaker. As long as it had enough reserves, it could absorb the selling and hold the line. A peg, in other words, is a promise backed by a finite amount of ammunition.

**Foreign-exchange reserves.** These are the dollars (and other hard currencies, plus gold) a central bank holds. They are the defence fund for the peg. The crucial, under-appreciated point is that what matters is not the headline reserve number but the *usable, unencumbered* reserves — the dollars you can actually sell tomorrow, net of what you have already promised away in forward contracts. Thailand's reported reserves in mid-1997 looked adequate. Its *usable* reserves had been quietly drained to almost nothing.

**A current-account deficit.** A country's current account is, roughly, its trade balance plus the net income it earns from abroad. A *deficit* means the country imports more than it exports — it spends more on the world than the world spends on it. A deficit is not inherently bad: it simply means the country is being financed by the rest of the world, taking in foreign capital to cover the gap. But that financing is a *flow*, and flows can stop. A country running a persistent current-account deficit is structurally dependent on foreign money continuing to arrive. Thailand's deficit in 1996 was around 8% of GDP — enormous, and entirely dependent on capital continuing to pour in.

Now stitch these together with the spine of this whole series: **an exchange rate is the relative price of two monies, and what moves it is the gap between two countries' interest rates plus the flow of money across borders.** In the early 1990s, US interest rates were low and Asian growth was spectacular. Capital flowed downhill, from the low-yielding, slow-growing developed world into the high-yielding, fast-growing tigers. That flow financed the deficit, propped up the peg, and — here is the trap — convinced everyone that the whole arrangement was permanent. The flow validated itself. Every dollar that came in pushed asset prices up and made the *next* dollar feel safer. (We unpack that self-validating inflow phase in detail in [the carry into emerging markets and the sudden stop](/blog/trading/forex/the-carry-into-emerging-markets-and-the-sudden-stop).)

The fragility was not visible in any single number. It was visible only in the *shape* of the regional balance sheet. That shape is the double mismatch, and it is the heart of the whole story.

## The double mismatch: the trap inside the balance sheet

A bank or a property developer in Bangkok or Jakarta in 1996 faced an irresistible deal. At home, you could lend money — to fund a condo tower, a steel mill, a shopping mall — at local interest rates of 12%, 15%, sometimes higher. Abroad, you could *borrow* US dollars at perhaps 6%. The peg said one dollar would always cost 25 baht, today and at repayment. So the trade looked free: borrow dollars cheap, convert to baht, lend at the higher local rate, pocket the spread. With the peg holding, the currency risk seemed to be zero.

It was not zero. It was hidden. And it was stacked two layers deep.

![Two column figure showing dollar debt against local revenue and short-term funding against long-term assets, hidden by the peg](/imgs/blogs/the-1997-asian-crisis-thb-idr-and-krw-in-freefall-2.png)

**Mismatch one — the currency mismatch.** The debt was in dollars. The revenue that would repay it was in baht (rents from that condo tower, sales from that mill). As long as the peg held, this was invisible: 25 baht of revenue covered 1 dollar of debt, forever. But the moment the baht weakened, the equation inverted. Now it took *more* baht to buy each dollar of debt repayment, while the revenue stream was still denominated in the now-cheaper baht. The borrower's liabilities ballooned in local terms while their income did not. A firm that was comfortably solvent at 25 could be deeply insolvent at 50 — not because its business had changed at all, but purely because of the exchange rate.

**Mismatch two — the maturity mismatch.** The borrowing was short-term: dollar loans that had to be *rolled over* (refinanced) every 90 or 180 days. The lending was long-term: ten-year property developments, infrastructure, factories you cannot liquidate on demand. This is exactly the fragility of a bank, where you borrow short (deposits, callable any day) and lend long (mortgages). It works only as long as the short-term funding keeps being renewed. The instant foreign lenders decide *not* to roll the dollar loans — to ask for their money back instead — the borrower has to find dollars *now*, against an asset they cannot sell for years. (This is the same coordination fragility behind a classic bank run; the game-theory of "everyone runs because everyone else might" is laid out in [bank runs as coordination games](/blog/trading/game-theory/bank-runs-as-coordination-games-diamond-dybvig-and-svb).)

Stack the two mismatches and you have a balance sheet that is a loaded trap. It is profitable and stable across the entire range of "the peg holds." It is catastrophic the instant the peg moves *and* the lenders stop rolling — and those two triggers are correlated, because the thing that scares lenders into not rolling is precisely the fear that the peg will move.

Why did no one hedge? You *can* insure against currency moves — you buy a forward contract that locks in your future exchange rate (we cover exactly how in [FX forwards and swaps](/blog/trading/forex/fx-forwards-and-swaps-the-real-workhorses-of-the-market)). The answer is brutally simple: hedging costs money, and the peg made it look like wasted money. Why pay an insurance premium on a rate that, by official guarantee, never moves? Every quarter you paid for the hedge and the baht stayed at 25, you looked like a fool who had thrown away profit. The market punished prudence and rewarded the naked dollar borrow — right up until it didn't.

#### Worked example: an Indonesian firm with \$10M of dollar debt

Take a mid-sized Indonesian manufacturer in early 1997. It has borrowed **\$10,000,000** from foreign banks to build a factory. The rupiah trades at **2,400 per dollar**, so on its books that debt is worth **\$10,000,000 × 2,400 = 24,000,000,000 rupiah** (24 billion rupiah). The factory and the firm's sales are all rupiah-denominated; the firm earns, say, 6 billion rupiah a year. The debt is four years of earnings — heavy, but manageable.

Now the rupiah collapses to **16,800 per dollar** (its crisis trough — a fall of about 83% in the rupiah's value, or equivalently the dollar rising sevenfold against it). The dollar debt has not changed: it is still **\$10,000,000**. But measured in rupiah, that debt is now worth **\$10,000,000 × 16,800 = 168,000,000,000 rupiah** (168 billion rupiah). The liability has grown **seven-fold** — from 24 billion to 168 billion rupiah — while the factory still earns the same 6 billion rupiah a year. The debt has gone from four years of earnings to **twenty-eight years** of earnings. The firm did nothing wrong. It did not over-borrow by the standards of early 1997. It is simply, overnight, insolvent — destroyed by a number printed on a foreign-exchange screen.

*The lesson: unhedged dollar debt is a short position on your own currency, and when your currency cracks, the loss is not linear — it is the full inverse of the move, landing all at once.*

That single mechanism — the local-currency value of dollar debt exploding — is the engine of the entire crisis. Everything else is this, repeated across thousands of firms and banks and five countries at once. And it explains why this was a *solvency* crisis, not merely a currency wobble. A 50% currency fall is painful but survivable for an economy whose debts are in its own currency (it just makes exports cheaper and imports dearer). For an economy whose debts are in dollars, a 50% fall doubles those debts. That is the difference between a recession and a collapse, and it is why this series keeps returning to the same warning about [the dollar as a wrecking ball for emerging markets](/blog/trading/forex/the-dollar-as-a-wrecking-ball-for-emerging-markets).

## How the dollar debt actually piled up

It is worth pausing on *how* the region accumulated so much dollar debt, because the mechanism matters for spotting the next one. Three channels fed it.

The first was the **interest-rate gap itself** — the master variable of FX. Local rates were high (that is what fast-growing economies with inflation pay); dollar rates were low. The gap was the bait. Borrowing in the cheap currency to invest in the expensive one is the [carry trade](/blog/trading/forex/the-carry-trade-getting-paid-to-hold-a-currency), and a credible peg turns the carry trade from a risky bet into what looks like free money, because the peg appears to remove the one risk that normally eats carry profits — the currency moving against you.

The second was **financial liberalization**. Through the early 1990s, Thailand and its neighbours opened their capital accounts — they made it easy for foreign money to flow in and for domestic banks to borrow abroad. Thailand even created the Bangkok International Banking Facility, explicitly designed to channel foreign (mostly dollar) lending into the domestic economy. The intention was to import cheap capital to fuel growth. The effect was to import a mountain of short-term dollar liabilities.

The third was **moral hazard** — the quiet belief that the government stood behind the banks. If everyone assumes that a politically connected bank will be bailed out when it gets into trouble, then lending to it carries less risk than the fundamentals justify, so too much money flows to it on too-easy terms. Capital piled into projects — half-empty office towers, speculative real estate — that would never have been funded under honest pricing of risk.

There was also a fourth, more subtle ingredient that almost nobody outside the FX market was watching: the **yen carry trade and the dollar-yen exchange rate**. A large share of the dollar lending into Asia was funded ultimately by Japanese banks and Japanese savers, who borrowed cheaply at home (Japanese rates were near zero through the 1990s) and lent into the higher-yielding tigers. That made the whole edifice quietly dependent on the yen staying weak and the dollar-yen rate staying calm. And it tied the baht's fate to a pair of currencies on the other side of the region. Because the baht was pegged to the *dollar*, when the dollar rose sharply against the yen in 1995-96 (USD/JPY went from about 80 to over 125), the baht was dragged up with it — making Thai exports uncompetitive against Japanese goods and widening the very current-account deficit that would later sink the peg. The currency you are pegged to is not a neutral anchor; it has its own life, and it can drag you somewhere you did not choose to go. This is the curse of a hard peg: you import the swings of a currency whose central bank is not thinking about you at all.

#### Worked example: the spread that lured a \$1B carry book

Consider a fund running a **\$1,000,000,000** carry position into Thai assets in 1996. It borrows dollars at **6%** (annual cost: **\$60,000,000**) and buys Thai instruments yielding **12%** (annual income: **\$120,000,000**, since the peg lets it treat baht and dollars as interchangeable). The net carry is **\$120,000,000 − \$60,000,000 = \$60,000,000 a year**, a clean **6%** return on the borrowed billion, apparently risk-free because the peg fixes the exchange rate.

Here is the asymmetry that should have screamed warning. The fund collects **\$60,000,000 a year**. But if the baht falls by just **5%**, the fund's billion dollars of baht assets are worth 5% less in dollar terms — a **\$50,000,000** hit, nearly a full year of carry, gone in one small move. A **20%** fall wipes out **\$200,000,000** — more than three years of accumulated carry. And the baht did not fall 20%; it fell more than 50%. The carry trade pays you a thin, steady premium to stand in front of a rare, enormous loss.

*The lesson: a pegged-currency carry trade is selling earthquake insurance — you collect small premiums every quiet quarter and pay out everything the one time the ground actually moves.* (This skew — small steady gains, occasional catastrophic loss — is the defining feature of carry, covered in depth in [carry crashes: picking up pennies in front of a steamroller](/blog/trading/forex/carry-crashes-picking-up-pennies-in-front-of-a-steamroller).)

By the start of 1997, then, the region had built up exactly the configuration that makes a currency crisis not just possible but explosive: large stocks of short-term, unhedged dollar debt, sitting on top of soft pegs, financed by hot foreign capital that could leave as fast as it came, in economies running current-account deficits that *depended* on that capital staying. The fuse was laid. It only needed a spark.

## The baht breaks: July 2 1997

The spark, as so often, was that fundamentals and the peg drifted apart and the market noticed before the central bank would admit it.

Through 1996 and early 1997 the cracks showed. Thai exports stalled (partly because the dollar — and therefore the dollar-pegged baht — had strengthened against the Japanese yen, making Thai goods expensive in their key markets). The property bubble that all the cheap dollar lending had inflated began to deflate; a major Thai finance company, Finance One, collapsed in early 1997. The current-account deficit stayed gaping. Speculators — hedge funds and the trading desks of large banks — did the arithmetic and concluded the baht was overvalued and the peg unsustainable. So they attacked it.

The mechanics of a speculative attack are worth being precise about, because they are the same in every currency crisis (we lay out the full anatomy in [currency crises and the anatomy of a speculative attack](/blog/trading/forex/currency-crises-and-the-anatomy-of-a-speculative-attack)). To bet against a pegged currency, a speculator *borrows* the local currency and *sells it* for dollars at the pegged rate. If the peg breaks and the currency falls, they buy it back cheaply, repay the loan, and keep the difference. To *defend* the peg, the central bank must do the opposite: buy its own currency, selling dollars from its reserves, and it can also raise interest rates to make holding (or borrowing to short) the local currency more painful. It is a direct contest, and the central bank's ammunition is finite while the speculators' ability to borrow and sell is, in practice, very large.

The Bank of Thailand fought hard and fought dirty. Through the spring of 1997 it intervened massively — and crucially, much of its intervention was in the *forward* market. Rather than spend its visible spot reserves, it sold dollars forward, promising to deliver dollars at a future date. This let the headline reserve figure stay reassuringly high while the *real*, usable reserves bled out invisibly. By late June, Thailand had committed something on the order of \$30 billion in forward contracts. The reserve war chest that markets thought was defending the baht was, in truth, already spent.

On July 2 1997, the game was up. The Bank of Thailand abandoned the peg and let the baht float. It did not glide down — it gapped. Within the day it had fallen sharply; over the following months it fell to **56 per dollar**, less than half its old value. The peg that had been a law of physics for a decade was gone in a morning.

![Grouped bar of local currency per US dollar before the crisis and at the crisis trough on a log scale](/imgs/blogs/the-1997-asian-crisis-thb-idr-and-krw-in-freefall-3.png)

The chart above shows what "the peg broke" meant in raw exchange-rate terms across the five currencies — how many units of each it took to buy one dollar before the crisis versus at the trough. Note that the *vertical axis is a log scale*, because the currencies live on wildly different number lines: the baht went from 25 to 56, while the rupiah went from 2,400 to nearly 17,000. On a normal axis the baht's move would be an invisible sliver next to the rupiah; on a log scale you can see that, in *proportional* terms, the rupiah's collapse dwarfs them all.

#### Worked example: the speculator who shorted \$1B against the baht

Take a fund that, in early 1997, decided the peg would break. It borrows **25,000,000,000 baht** (25 billion baht) — at the pegged rate of **25 per dollar**, that is a **\$1,000,000,000** short position — and immediately sells the baht for **\$1,000,000,000**, which it parks in dollar deposits.

It pays interest on the borrowed baht. Suppose Thai rates are **12%** and it holds the position for half a year: the carry *cost* is roughly **25,000,000,000 × 12% × 0.5 = 1,500,000,000 baht**, about **\$60,000,000** at the entry rate. That is the price of being early and wrong — the bleed if the peg holds.

Then the peg breaks and the baht falls to **50 per dollar**. Now the fund buys back the **25,000,000,000 baht** it owes. At 50 per dollar, that costs only **25,000,000,000 ÷ 50 = \$500,000,000**. It borrowed the equivalent of a billion dollars and repays it for half a billion. Gross profit: **\$1,000,000,000 − \$500,000,000 = \$500,000,000**, minus the \$60M carry cost — call it **\$440,000,000** net on a position that required relatively little capital up front.

*The lesson: a speculative attack is a bet with a small, bounded cost (the carry while you wait) and a huge, leveraged payoff (the full devaluation), which is exactly why a wobbling peg attracts a swarm — the risk/reward is asymmetric in the attacker's favour.* It is the mirror image of the central bank's position, which is paid the thin carry to stand in front of an enormous, one-sided loss. (This is the same trade George Soros ran against the pound in 1992 — see [Soros and Black Wednesday](/blog/trading/forex/soros-and-black-wednesday-breaking-the-bank-of-england-1992).)

## The doom loop: why the fall fed itself

A normal currency depreciation is self-correcting. A weaker currency makes a country's exports cheaper and more competitive, and makes imports dearer, so the trade balance improves, demand for the currency recovers, and it finds a floor. This is the textbook stabilizer, and for an economy with debts in its own currency, it mostly works.

The 1997 economies did not get the stabilizer. They got the opposite — a *destabilizer*, because of the dollar debt. Here the falling currency did not heal the balance sheet; it detonated it.

![Graph of the doom loop where a falling currency inflates dollar debt forcing more dollar buying and a further fall](/imgs/blogs/the-1997-asian-crisis-thb-idr-and-krw-in-freefall-4.png)

Trace the loop. The peg breaks and the currency falls. The local-currency value of every firm's and bank's dollar debt instantly explodes (mismatch one). Two things happen at once. First, foreign lenders, watching their borrowers turn insolvent on paper, refuse to roll over the short-term loans (mismatch two) and demand repayment. Second, the firms themselves, terrified of the debt growing further, scramble to buy dollars *now* — either to repay the debt or to hedge what remains. Both of those reactions mean *selling local currency to buy dollars*. That selling pushes the currency down further. Which inflates the dollar debt further. Which triggers more refusals to roll and more panic dollar-buying. The currency's fall is not a one-off repricing; it is a feedback loop that accelerates.

Meanwhile the central bank, to try to stem the exit, hikes interest rates savagely — Thailand, Korea, and Indonesia all pushed rates to punishing levels. But high rates, intended to make the currency attractive to hold, also choke the domestic economy: businesses that depended on credit cannot refinance, banks that are already wounded by the FX losses stop lending entirely, and the real economy seizes up. So the defence of the currency *deepens* the recession, which makes the country look even less creditworthy, which sends *more* capital fleeing. There is no good move. Every lever the authorities can pull either weakens the currency or strangles the economy, and usually both.

This is why these events are described as "twin crises" — a currency crisis and a banking crisis welded together, each feeding the other. The currency fall bankrupts the banks (via their dollar debt); the bankrupt banks stop lending, which deepens the slump and the capital flight, which weakens the currency more. (We trace this exact reversal dynamic — the "sudden stop" — in [the carry into emerging markets and the sudden stop](/blog/trading/forex/the-carry-into-emerging-markets-and-the-sudden-stop).)

The macroeconomic numbers were brutal. Thailand's economy contracted by more than 10% in 1998. Indonesia's shrank by about 13% — a depression. Unemployment soared, poverty rates that had been falling for a generation jumped, and in Indonesia the economic catastrophe brought down a government: President Suharto, in power for 31 years, resigned in May 1998 amid riots. A currency that fell because of a balance-sheet mismatch had, two steps down the chain, toppled a regime.

#### Worked example: the bank that the loop bankrupted

Consider a Korean bank with **\$5,000,000,000** of short-term foreign-currency borrowing, funding **\$5,000,000,000** of long-term local-currency loans. At the start of 1997, with the won near **900 per dollar**, the books balance: assets and liabilities are matched in size, and the bank earns a healthy spread.

The won falls to **1,800 per dollar** (a roughly 50% drop, in the range of the actual 54% collapse). The bank's dollar liabilities, in won terms, *double*: what was 4,500 billion won of debt (5bn × 900) is now 9,000 billion won (5bn × 1,800). Its won-denominated loan assets have not grown at all — and worse, many of *its* borrowers are themselves firms with dollar debt who are now defaulting. So the asset side is shrinking from defaults at the same moment the liability side is doubling from the FX move. The bank's equity — the thin cushion between assets and liabilities — is wiped out many times over. It is insolvent.

Now the second mismatch bites: the bank's foreign lenders, seeing this, refuse to roll the **\$5,000,000,000** of short-term funding. The bank must repay dollars it does not have, against loans it cannot call in. It either gets rescued by the state (Korea nationalized much of its banking system) or it fails. Multiply this by a banking system's worth of institutions and you have a national solvency crisis, not a liquidity hiccup.

*The lesson: when banks themselves carry the currency-and-maturity mismatch, the doom loop does not just hurt individual firms — it takes down the financial plumbing of the entire country, which is why the recessions were depressions.*

## Contagion: one break, five collapses

Here is the question that makes 1997 more than a Thai story. The baht broke because of *Thailand's* fundamentals — its deficit, its property bubble, its spent reserves. So why did Indonesia, Korea, Malaysia, and the Philippines collapse too? Their economies were not identical to Thailand's. Korea in particular was an industrial powerhouse, an OECD member, not an obvious twin of a Thai property bubble.

The answer is **contagion**, and the cleanest way to understand it is as *repricing by analogy*. Before July 2, the market had been pricing each Asian economy on the comforting assumption that its peg was solid and its dollar borrowing was therefore safe. The baht break shattered that assumption — not just for Thailand, but for the entire *category*. Markets looked around and asked: which other economies have this same shape? Soft dollar peg? Check. Large short-term dollar debt? Check. Current-account deficit? Check. And one by one, they sold every economy that fit the template, *before* its fundamentals had visibly deteriorated, because the whole point was that Thailand's fundamentals had looked fine too until they didn't.

![Graph showing contagion spreading from Thailand to Indonesia Korea Malaysia and the Philippines into IMF programs](/imgs/blogs/the-1997-asian-crisis-thb-idr-and-krw-in-freefall-6.png)

Contagion of this kind runs through several concrete channels, not just sentiment:

- **The wake-up call (information contagion).** The baht break was new information — not about Thailand, but about how markets and central banks behave under stress. It revealed that a peg everyone trusted could be abandoned in a morning, and that the reserve numbers defending these pegs might be hollow. Investors who had not looked closely at Indonesian or Korean balance sheets now did, and did not like what they saw.
- **Forced selling.** A fund that lost money on the baht needs to raise cash and cut risk. The easiest things to sell are its *other* emerging-Asia positions — even the healthy ones. So losses in Thailand mechanically generated selling in Korea and Indonesia, regardless of those countries' fundamentals.
- **Real trade and competition links.** Once the baht had devalued, Thai exports got cheaper, which pressured competing exporters in Malaysia and Korea, giving *those* countries a reason to want (or to be expected to allow) a weaker currency too. Devaluation became partly self-fulfilling across the region.
- **Common lenders.** The same Japanese and European banks that had lent dollars to Thailand had also lent to Indonesia and Korea. When they pulled back from one, they pulled back from all — a single common creditor transmits a shock across every borrower it touches.

The result was sequential collapse. The chart at the top of this post ranks the damage: the Indonesian rupiah was the worst hit, falling roughly **83%** against the dollar (from about 2,400 to a trough near 16,800 per dollar). The Thai baht and Korean won each fell about **54%**. The Malaysian ringgit fell about **47%**, the Philippine peso about **38%**. Korea, the world's eleventh-largest economy at the time, came within days of running out of usable reserves and defaulting on its sovereign debt — a scenario that would have dwarfed Thailand — before a record IMF package pulled it back from the edge.

Indonesia's outsized fall deserves a word, because it shows how the doom loop and politics can amplify each other. Indonesia entered the crisis with arguably *better* headline fundamentals than Thailand — a smaller current-account deficit, more reserves. But it had an enormous stock of unhedged private-sector dollar debt, and when the doom loop started, the political response made it worse: an IMF program that closed banks without a deposit guarantee triggered actual bank runs, and uncertainty about Suharto's grip and succession destroyed confidence entirely. The rupiah did not just fall; it went into free fall, overshooting any sensible "fair value" massively, because once a currency loses all credibility the only buyers vanish and there is no floor. An exchange rate, remember, is a relative price set by flows — and when the flow is *everyone trying to get out at once*, the price can go almost anywhere.

#### Worked example: contagion priced into the won before any data moved

Put yourself at a London trading desk on, say, July 10 1997 — eight days after the baht broke. Nothing in Korea's published economic data has changed. But you now know two things you did not fully price before: pegs in this region can be abandoned overnight, and the "reserves" defending them may be illusory.

You estimate Korea has perhaps **\$30,000,000,000** in *usable* reserves against well over **\$100,000,000,000** of short-term foreign debt coming due within a year. If even a third of that debt is not rolled — **\$33,000,000,000** — Korea's reserves are gone and the won must float. You do not wait for the data to confirm it. You sell the won *now*, at the still-defended rate near **900 per dollar**, exactly as you would have sold the baht in June. Thousands of desks make the same calculation. The selling itself drains Korea's reserves and makes the non-rolling more likely — the bet creates the conditions that win the bet.

*The lesson: contagion is not irrational panic; it is rational re-pricing of a hidden risk that was always there, triggered by one example that proved the risk was real — which is why economies with the same balance-sheet shape fall in sequence even when their data still looks fine.*

This is the deep reason contagion is so feared: it means a crisis can spread to a fundamentally *solvent* economy purely because that economy shares a vulnerability template with a fallen neighbour. (When everything in a region sells off together, correlations that were comfortably low in calm times snap to one — a phenomenon we cover in [when correlations go to one in a crisis](/blog/trading/cross-asset/when-correlations-go-to-one-in-a-crisis).)

It is worth dwelling on one detail of the contagion, because it is the part that genuinely shocked policymakers at the time: the crisis jumped to *Korea*, which on paper had almost nothing in common with a Thai property bubble. Korea was an industrial giant — Samsung, Hyundai, the chaebol conglomerates — an OECD member with a respected central bank. What it shared with Thailand was not the property speculation but the *financing structure*: the chaebol had grown by piling on debt, much of it short-term and foreign-currency, channelled through merchant banks that had borrowed dollars offshore to fund domestic lending. When the regional wake-up call came and foreign banks started pulling dollar credit from "emerging Asia" as a bloc, Korea's merchant banks could not roll their short-term dollar funding. The maturity mismatch did the rest. Korea's reserves drained toward zero in late 1997, and the country — the eleventh-largest economy on earth — came within days of a sovereign default. The lesson policymakers took away was sobering: it was not poverty or backwardness that made you vulnerable, it was the *shape of your balance sheet*. A rich, sophisticated economy with the wrong financing structure was just as exposed as a poor one.

There is one apparent exception that proves the rule: **Malaysia**, which in September 1998 broke ranks and imposed capital controls — it fixed the ringgit, banned the offshore trading of the currency, and refused IMF money. At the time this was treated as heresy. In hindsight it is more ambiguous: Malaysia avoided the deepest IMF conditionality and recovered respectably, suggesting that for an economy with relatively *less* foreign-currency debt, slamming the capital-account door shut to stop the outflow was a defensible move. But capital controls are a tool that works best *before* the panic and at a real long-run cost (foreign investors remember being trapped, and demand a higher return forever after). The Malaysian experiment did not refute the doom loop — it interrupted the loop by force, by simply forbidding the capital flight that powered it.

## The IMF aftermath: rescue, conditions, and controversy

As reserves ran out, the crisis countries had nowhere left to turn but the International Monetary Fund — the global lender of last resort for governments. The IMF assembled enormous rescue packages: roughly **\$17 billion** for Thailand, around **\$40 billion** for Indonesia, and a record **\$58 billion** for Korea. The money came with strings — the famous "conditionality."

The IMF's playbook in 1997 was the standard one for a balance-of-payments crisis, and it had three pillars: raise interest rates sharply (to stabilize the currency and stop capital flight), tighten the government budget (to restore fiscal credibility), and impose structural reforms (close insolvent banks, dismantle crony connections, open markets). The logic was that the crisis revealed deep structural rot, and the cure was to force a cleanup in exchange for the cash.

This is where the story becomes genuinely contested, and an honest post has to say so. The IMF's prescription was designed for a crisis of *government* profligacy — a state that had borrowed and spent too much. But the Asian crisis was overwhelmingly a *private-sector* crisis: the governments were mostly running balanced or surplus budgets, and the dollar debt sat on corporate and bank balance sheets. Applying the standard fiscal-austerity medicine to a private-debt crisis arguably made things worse. The deep interest-rate hikes, intended to defend the currency, throttled economies that were already collapsing — exactly the destabilizing arm of the doom loop. The demand to close banks *without* deposit insurance, in Indonesia, sparked the very bank runs that deepened the panic. Critics, including some economists who would later run the World Bank, argued the IMF treated a heart attack with a diet plan: technically a fix for a real long-run problem, catastrophically wrong for the acute emergency.

Defenders counter that the money came with no realistic alternative — the countries were out of reserves, and *someone* had to provide dollars or the collapse would have been even worse — and that the structural problems (crony lending, hollow reserves, fragile banks) were real and did need fixing. Both things can be true: the rescue prevented outright default, and the conditions deepened the recession. The debate reshaped how the Fund approaches crises; later programs (and the Fed's swap lines in 2008 and 2020) leaned far more toward providing liquidity fast and worrying about conditions later.

#### Worked example: the cost of the rescue in plain dollars

Korea's IMF-led package was about **\$58,000,000,000**. To see what that means, set it against the hole it was filling. Korea's short-term external debt due within a year was well over **\$100,000,000,000**, and its usable reserves had dwindled toward **\$30,000,000,000** or less. The gap — the dollars Korea owed soon but could not produce — was on the order of **\$70,000,000,000**.

The package, combined with a coordinated agreement by foreign banks to *roll over* (extend) rather than call their Korean loans, bridged that gap. Crucially, the rollover agreement mattered as much as the cash: it directly attacked the maturity mismatch by converting short-term debt that was being yanked into longer-term debt that would stay. Korea recovered remarkably fast — it was growing again by 1999 and repaid the IMF early, in 2001, ahead of schedule.

*The lesson: the rescue worked not because the dollar amount magically covered every debt, but because it broke the run — once lenders believed the dollars would be there, the panic to grab them first subsided, which is the same dynamic that ends any bank run.*

## The lasting lesson: never again

The deepest consequence of 1997 was not economic but psychological, and it reshaped the entire emerging world's approach to currencies for the next twenty years. The lesson Asia drew, fairly or not, was: *never again depend on the IMF.* The conditions had been humiliating and the medicine had hurt; whatever the rights and wrongs of the debate, no Asian government wanted to be in that position twice. So they self-insured.

![Two column figure contrasting the fragile 1997 setup of thin reserves and soft pegs with the post crisis model of reserve war chests and managed floats](/imgs/blogs/the-1997-asian-crisis-thb-idr-and-krw-in-freefall-8.png)

The self-insurance took three concrete forms, and you can still see all three in EM balance sheets today:

**Reserve war chests.** Above all, the region accumulated enormous foreign-exchange reserves. If the crisis was caused by having too few usable dollars when the run came, the answer was to hold a colossal stockpile — far more than any conventional metric said was "needed" — so that no speculator could ever credibly bet the central bank would run out. This is precautionary hoarding on a national scale: expensive (reserves earn low returns and represent capital not invested at home), but the price of never again being at the IMF's mercy. China's reserves grew into the *trillions*; across emerging Asia, reserves measured in months of imports rose far above the old danger lines.

![Bar chart of EM foreign exchange reserves in months of imports with a three month adequacy line](/imgs/blogs/the-1997-asian-crisis-thb-idr-and-krw-in-freefall-7.png)

The chart above shows the modern result, using the standard yardstick of reserve adequacy: **months of imports** a country's reserves can cover. The old rule of thumb — the dashed red line — is roughly three months. The crisis economies now sit well above it: Thailand around eight months, China fourteen, India eleven. (Vietnam, a later-developing economy still building its buffer, sits right at the three-month line — which is exactly why its central bank manages the dong's depreciation so carefully rather than letting it float; see [USD/VND and the managed float](/blog/trading/forex/usd-vnd-and-the-managed-float-how-the-sbv-runs-the-dong).) The whole point of the war chest is to make the peg-or-managed-float *unbreakable*, by removing the speculator's core bet that reserves will run dry.

**Managed floats instead of hard pegs.** A rigid peg is a binary: it holds, or it snaps catastrophically. A *managed float* — where the central bank guides the currency but lets it move within a range — is more flexible. It can absorb a shock gradually, bending instead of breaking, which removes the one-way bet that makes a hard peg a magnet for attack. Most of emerging Asia moved toward managed floats after 1997, accepting more day-to-day currency volatility in exchange for far less risk of a sudden, total break. (The full spectrum from hard peg to free float is mapped in [pegs, bands, and crawling pegs](/blog/trading/forex/pegs-bands-and-crawling-pegs-the-spectrum-of-fx-regimes).)

**Borrowing at home.** The currency mismatch was the original sin. So the region worked, slowly, to be able to borrow in its *own* currency — developing deeper domestic bond markets so that governments and firms could fund themselves in baht or won or rupiah, debt that does not explode when the currency falls. This was the slowest fix (deep local bond markets take a decade or more to build), but it is the most fundamental: an economy that borrows in its own currency cannot be destroyed by the doom loop, because a weaker currency no longer inflates its debt.

There is a profound macroeconomic irony buried in all this. By collectively hoarding reserves — which mostly means buying US Treasuries — emerging Asia became a giant net *lender* to the United States. The "savings glut" of the 2000s, which helped keep US interest rates low and arguably helped inflate the US housing bubble that burst in 2008, was in significant part the emerging world self-insuring against another 1997. The trauma of the Asian crisis is woven, through this channel, into the origins of the *next* global crisis. Money flowing "uphill" from poor, fast-growing countries to the rich, slow-growing United States is not how the textbook says capital should move — but it is exactly what a region that has decided "never again" will do.

## Common misconceptions

**"It was caused by greedy speculators."** Speculators were the *trigger*, not the *cause*. A speculative attack can only break a currency that is already vulnerable; you cannot, in the long run, force a currency away from a level its fundamentals support. The baht fell because Thailand had a gaping deficit, a deflating property bubble, a pile of short-term dollar debt, and spent reserves. Speculators simply noticed and applied the pressure that exposed the rot. Blaming the speculators is like blaming the person who pointed out that the dam already had cracks. The deeper cause was the policy mix — soft peg plus open capital account plus unhedged dollar borrowing — that built the cracks.

**"A weaker currency should have helped, like it does in the textbooks."** For a normal economy, yes: devaluation boosts exports and is self-correcting. But that stabilizer only works when your debts are in your own currency. With large unhedged dollar debt, the devaluation *destroys* balance sheets faster than it helps exports, because the debt explodes overnight while the export boost takes quarters to arrive. The dollar-debt mismatch flips the textbook sign: the stabilizer becomes a destabilizer. This is the single most important and most counter-intuitive fact of EM currency crises.

**"The Asian economies were fundamentally rotten — it was inevitable."** This overstates it. These were genuinely fast-growing, high-saving economies with strong export sectors; their *governments* were mostly fiscally responsible. The flaw was specific and financial — the maturity-and-currency mismatch in the private sector and banks — not a general economic rottenness. Korea recovered to grow strongly within two years; these were not basket-case economies but well-run ones with a dangerous financing structure. The crisis was a balance-sheet accident, not a verdict on the "Asian model" of development.

**"The IMF fixed it."** The IMF provided the dollars that prevented outright default, which mattered. But its standard fiscal-austerity-and-high-rates prescription was arguably wrong for a private-sector crisis and deepened the recessions, and one of its conditions (closing Indonesian banks without deposit insurance) directly worsened the panic. The honest verdict is mixed: the money helped, several of the conditions hurt, and the episode forced a real rethink of how the Fund handles crises.

**"This was a one-off; modern EM is immune."** The *specific* 1997 setup — soft dollar pegs plus huge short-term dollar debt — is much rarer now, precisely because of the reserve war chests and managed floats described above. But the *underlying* vulnerability — borrowing in a currency you do not print — never went away. Every dollar-strength episode since (2013's taper tantrum, 2015, 2018, 2022) has stressed the EM economies that still carry dollar debt. The form changed; the physics did not.

**"The currencies overshot, so they were obviously cheap — you should have bought the bottom."** This is the most expensive misconception of all, and it kills traders in every crisis. Yes, the rupiah at 16,800 was almost certainly *below* any reasonable fair value — it had overshot massively. But "cheap" and "about to bounce" are not the same thing. In a full-blown crisis, a currency can stay irrationally cheap for as long as the panic lasts, because the buyers who would normally arbitrage it back have themselves been wiped out or have fled. Worse, trying to "catch the falling knife" by buying the currency means standing against the doom loop while it is still running — the very forces (dollar-buying, credit collapse, capital flight) that drove the overshoot are still active. The disciplined move is not to call the bottom but to wait for the *flow* to turn: for reserves to stop draining, for the rollover panic to subside, for the central bank or the IMF to credibly break the run. Value tells you a currency is cheap; only flows and the breaking of the panic tell you it is *done* falling. Confusing the two is how you lose money being right.

## How it shows up in real markets

The 1997 template is not a museum piece. It is a live checklist that FX and EM investors still run, and its fingerprints are all over later episodes.

**The taper tantrum, 2013.** When the US Federal Reserve merely *hinted* it would slow its bond-buying, capital began flowing back toward the dollar, and the "Fragile Five" — Brazil, India, Indonesia, South Africa, Turkey — sold off hard. The common thread: current-account deficits financed by hot foreign money, exactly the 1997 dependency. The difference was that this time the reserve war chests and floating currencies let the economies bend rather than break. The currencies fell, but no peg snapped and no doom loop fully ignited. The defenses built after 1997 worked.

**The 2018 and 2022 dollar surges.** A strong dollar is a tightening of financial conditions for everyone who borrowed in dollars — it is the wrecking ball this series keeps naming. In 2018, Turkey and Argentina (both with the old vulnerabilities — Turkey with large corporate dollar debt, Argentina with dollar sovereign debt and thin reserves) had outright currency crises while better-insured economies merely wobbled. In 2022, the most aggressive Fed hiking cycle in decades drove the dollar to multi-decade highs and pulled capital out of emerging markets en masse — the same gravity, the same sorting of the insured from the exposed.

The chart below shows that hot-money pattern in the modern data — net portfolio flows into emerging markets, which pour in during the calm years and reverse out hard in the stress years (2015, 2020, 2022). It is the 1997 "flow that can stop" made visible across a decade: the sudden stop is not a historical curiosity but a recurring feature of how capital treats the emerging world.

![Bar chart of emerging market portfolio flows showing money pouring in then reversing out in stress years](/imgs/blogs/the-1997-asian-crisis-thb-idr-and-krw-in-freefall-5.png)

**The practitioner's checklist.** A trader sizing up an EM currency today runs essentially the 1997 diagnostic, four questions deep:

1. *What is the currency regime?* A hard peg that looks stretched is the most dangerous configuration — it offers the speculator a one-way bet. A genuine float or a wide-band managed float is far safer because it can absorb shocks continuously.
2. *How much short-term external (especially dollar) debt is there, relative to usable reserves?* This single ratio — short-term external debt to reserves — is the best early-warning gauge there is. Below one, the economy can self-defend; well above one, it is one confidence shock from a run.
3. *Is the current account in deficit, and who is financing it?* A deficit financed by stable long-term FDI is fine; a deficit financed by hot portfolio money is the 1997 trap.
4. *How are usable reserves trending, and is the central bank hiding interventions in the forward market?* A central bank that is quietly selling reserves forward to flatter its headline number is doing exactly what Thailand did in 1997 — and the forward book is where the truth hides.

When several of those line up red, you are looking at a 1997-shaped vulnerability, and the right posture is the one this series teaches throughout: respect the asymmetry. The carry pays you a little to stand in front of a rare, enormous loss, and the time to be paid that carry is *not* the moment the reserves are draining and the peg is stretched. (For the full discipline of weighing carry, flows, valuation, and regime into a single currency call, see [building an FX view: the currency trading playbook](/blog/trading/forex/building-an-fx-view-the-currency-trading-playbook-capstone).)

## The takeaway

Strip the 1997 Asian crisis down to its bones and it is a single sentence: *if you peg your currency, borrow short-term in dollars, and run a deficit financed by money that can leave, you have built a machine that converts a small loss of confidence into a total collapse.* The peg made the dollar borrowing feel safe, which encouraged more of it; the short-term funding meant lenders could pull out instantly; the deficit meant the economy could not survive them pulling out. And the dollar debt meant that the very act of the currency falling — normally a healing mechanism — became the accelerant that burned everything down.

The single most important idea to carry away is the inversion of the textbook. For most of economics, a weaker currency is a shock absorber. For an economy short its own currency through unhedged dollar debt, a weaker currency is a bomb. That is why this series returns again and again to a deceptively simple question: *what currency are the debts in?* Everything about how an emerging-market currency behaves in a crisis — whether it bends or snaps, recovers or free-falls — flows from that one fact. An exchange rate is the relative price of two monies, and when one of those monies is the dollar and you owe it but cannot print it, the price can move further and faster against you than any model of "fair value" would ever predict, because the move and the debt feed each other.

The reason this matters far beyond 1997 is that the world did not abolish the vulnerability — it built defenses against the specific shape of it. The reserve war chests, the managed floats, the local-currency bond markets: these are the scar tissue, and they are why the taper tantrum and the 2022 dollar surge bruised emerging markets instead of shattering them. But the underlying physics — borrowing in a currency you do not control — is permanent. So when you read a currency, do not start with the growth rate or the central bank's reassurances. Start with the balance sheet: the regime, the debt's denomination, the maturity, and the usable reserves. That is where 1997 lives, and that is where the next one will start.
