> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The world is built to be short dollars: foreign banks hold long-dated dollar assets funded by short-dated dollar borrowing they must constantly roll, so in a panic everyone reaches for the same dollars at once and the cross-currency basis — the extra price of borrowing dollars through the FX market — blows out.
>
> - A foreign bank that lends dollars for years but borrows them for months is *structurally short dollars*: it owes dollars it does not have and must refinance forever.
> - In a crisis that short turns into a stampede. Funding markets seize, and the **cross-currency basis** goes deeply negative — it hit roughly **−220 basis points** for yen borrowers in 2008.
> - The Fed's **central-bank swap lines** are the fix: the Fed lends dollars to foreign central banks, who relend them to their banks. Draws spiked to **\$583 billion in 2008** and **\$449 billion in 2020**.
> - The one number to remember: the dollar is on **~88%** of all FX trades, so a dollar shortage is not one market's problem — it is *everyone's* problem at the same time.

## The morning the world ran out of dollars

On the morning of **March 18, 2020**, the most important market in the world stopped working — and it was the one almost nobody outside of bank treasury desks had ever heard of. It was not the stock market, though stocks were in free fall. It was not even the US Treasury market, though that was buckling too. It was the market where a German insurer, a Japanese pension fund, or a Brazilian airline goes to borrow dollars for three months. That market — the plumbing that lets the non-American world fund its dollar bills — simply jammed.

The symptom was eerie. The dollar, the asset everyone was panicking *about*, went *up* — sharply. The US Dollar Index ripped higher even as the Federal Reserve was slashing rates to zero and promising unlimited bond-buying. Every reflex said the dollar should fall when the Fed eases. Instead it spiked, because the world was not selling dollars. The world was *scrambling* for them. A Korean bank that owed dollars next week, a Norwegian fund that had to roll its dollar hedges, a Mexican corporate with a dollar bond coupon due — all of them reached for the same dollars in the same hours, and there were not enough to go around. The price of borrowing a dollar through the FX market — a price called the **cross-currency basis**, which we will spend this post understanding — gapped to levels last seen in the autumn of 2008.

Then, over the following two weeks, the fire went out. Not because risk appetite returned — it had not — but because the Federal Reserve turned on a set of pipes it keeps for exactly this emergency: **central-bank liquidity swap lines**. The Fed lent hundreds of billions of dollars to the European Central Bank, the Bank of Japan, the Bank of England, the Swiss National Bank, and others. They relent those dollars to their own banks. The scramble eased, the basis snapped back toward zero, and the most dangerous phase of the March 2020 panic was over. This post is about why that scramble happens at all, why it is *built into* the structure of the global financial system, and how a few central banks acting as one quietly became the world's lender of last resort — in a currency most of the borrowers do not even print.

![Fed swap-line draws spike in 2008 and 2020 then collapse to near zero](/imgs/blogs/the-global-dollar-shortage-and-central-bank-swap-lines-1.png)

That single chart is the whole story in one picture. In normal times the Fed's swap lines sit at essentially zero — nobody needs them. Then a crisis hits and the draws explode to the size of a mid-sized country's entire economy: **\$583 billion** at the December 2008 peak, **\$449 billion** in the spring of 2020. The spikes are not gentle. They are vertical, because a dollar shortage is not a slow erosion — it is a fire alarm that goes off everywhere at once. Our job is to understand what is burning.

This sits at the heart of this series' spine: an exchange rate is the relative price of two monies, and what moves it is the gap between two countries' interest rates plus the flow of money across borders. A dollar shortage is the most violent version of that "flow of money across borders" — it is the entire world trying to push capital toward dollars through a pipe that is too narrow, and the FX market is where the pressure shows up.

## Foundations: Why the world is structurally short dollars

Before we can understand a *crisis* in dollar funding, we have to understand why the world owes dollars it does not have even on a calm Tuesday. The answer is one of the most important and least appreciated facts in global finance, so let us build it from zero.

Start with what a dollar even is for someone outside the United States. The dollar is not just America's currency — it is the world's invoicing currency, its borrowing currency, and its reserve currency. When a Vietnamese factory buys cotton from India, the contract is very likely priced in dollars. When a Brazilian company wants to borrow long-term money cheaply, it often issues a *dollar* bond, not a real bond, because the global pool of dollar lenders is enormous and deep. When a Japanese pension fund wants the higher yields available in US assets, it buys dollar bonds. And crucially, banks *outside* the United States — European, Japanese, Korean, Canadian banks — hold trillions of dollars of dollar-denominated assets on their balance sheets. We call this whole apparatus the **offshore dollar system** or, by an older name, the **eurodollar** system: dollars that live and circulate on balance sheets outside US borders, beyond the direct reach of US banks and, for the most part, US regulators. (For the full anatomy of how those offshore dollars are created, see the sibling post on [eurodollars and the offshore dollar system](/blog/trading/forex/eurodollars-and-the-offshore-dollar-system).)

Here is the central problem, and it is structural — meaning it is baked into the shape of the system, not a mistake anyone made. A foreign bank that holds dollar *assets* needs dollar *funding* to pay for them. But that bank does not have a natural, stable source of dollars the way a US bank does. A US bank takes dollar deposits from American households and firms; dollars flow into it every day as a matter of course. A German or Japanese bank has *euro* or *yen* deposits as its bedrock. To fund its dollar assets, it has to *manufacture* dollar funding — by borrowing dollars short-term in wholesale markets, or by using the FX market to swap its home currency into dollars for a while. Either way, that funding is short-dated and has to be **rolled over** — refinanced again and again, week after week, year after year.

![Long dollar assets funded by short dollar borrowing that must roll over](/imgs/blogs/the-global-dollar-shortage-and-central-bank-swap-lines-2.png)

Look at the two sides of that figure, because the gap between them *is* the structural short. On the left are the dollar assets: US mortgage bonds, corporate bonds, dollar loans to Asian and emerging-market firms, trade-finance and project loans. These are *long* — they mature in years and cannot be sold quickly without taking a loss. On the right is the dollar funding: overnight and three-month borrowing, FX swaps that last days to months, wholesale deposits that can flee in a heartbeat. The funding is *short*. A bank with long, illiquid dollar assets and short, flighty dollar funding has a **maturity mismatch** in a currency it cannot print. That is the definition of being structurally short: it owes dollars it does not have, on a clock it does not control.

### The currency mismatch on top of the maturity mismatch

Banks have always run a maturity mismatch — borrowing short and lending long is the basic business model of banking, the thing that makes a bank a bank. A domestic bank in its own currency can survive a funding scare because its own central bank stands behind it as the lender of last resort: if depositors flee, the central bank can create the home currency and lend it to the bank against good collateral. That is the entire point of having a central bank.

But a foreign bank short of *dollars* has a problem its home central bank cannot directly solve. The Bank of Japan can create unlimited *yen*. It cannot create *dollars*. If a Japanese bank cannot roll its dollar funding, the Bank of Japan cannot simply print the dollars to save it. So the foreign bank carries not just a maturity mismatch but a **currency mismatch** layered on top: the asset and the funding are both in dollars, but the institution's natural backstop is in a currency that does not help. This is why a dollar funding squeeze is uniquely dangerous. The normal fire department — the home central bank — shows up to the fire holding the wrong kind of hose.

#### Worked example: the size of one bank's roll

Suppose a mid-sized European bank holds **\$10,000,000,000** (ten billion dollars) of US corporate and mortgage bonds — a perfectly ordinary position for a global bank. It funds them not with stable dollar deposits but with three-month dollar borrowing. That means every three months it must find **\$10,000,000,000** of fresh dollar funding to replace the maturing tranche, or sell the bonds.

In a calm quarter, this is trivial: it borrows the dollars in the wholesale market at, say, the three-month dollar rate of **5.00%**, costing roughly \$10,000,000,000 × 5.00% × (90 ÷ 360) = **\$125,000,000** in interest for the quarter, and rolls on. The position earns a spread over funding and life is good.

Now suppose the funding market tightens and the bank cannot borrow dollars directly at all — it can only get them through an FX swap, where the effective cost jumps by the cross-currency basis. We will price that exact scenario later. The point for now: this bank is *permanently* one failed roll away from a crisis, and there are hundreds of banks like it. The structural short is not a tail risk sitting in a drawer — it is the everyday operating condition of the global banking system.

### How big is the short?

This is not a niche exposure. The Bank for International Settlements tracks dollar credit extended to non-bank borrowers *outside* the United States — a clean proxy for the size of the offshore dollar machine — and the number is staggering and growing.

![Offshore dollar credit outstanding rising past thirteen trillion dollars](/imgs/blogs/the-global-dollar-shortage-and-central-bank-swap-lines-3.png)

That stack has climbed from about **\$6 trillion in 2010** to roughly **\$13.4 trillion by 2024**. Every dollar of it is someone's dollar liability — a dollar that has to be serviced, refinanced, and ultimately repaid by a borrower who does not print dollars and earns much of their income in another currency. The bigger this stack grows, the bigger the structural short grows, and the more dollars the world will need to find in a panic. The dollar's dominance — it sits on roughly 88% of all FX trades, as we will see — means this is not a problem confined to one country or one currency. It is the load-bearing wall of the whole edifice, and when it shakes, everything shakes.

### Who, exactly, is short — and why they can't just stop

It helps to name the players, because "the world is short dollars" stays vague until you see whose desks actually carry the exposure. Four groups dominate.

The first is **non-US banks** — the European, Japanese, Korean, and Canadian institutions we have been describing. They run dollar asset books because dollar lending is profitable and the dollar capital markets are the deepest on earth. Their dollar funding gap — dollar assets minus stable dollar liabilities — runs into the trillions in aggregate. The second is **emerging-market corporates and governments** that issue dollar bonds because they can borrow more, longer, and cheaper in dollars than in their own thin local-currency markets. A Brazilian utility or an Indonesian property developer with a dollar bond is short dollars: it owes dollars but earns reais or rupiah. The third is **institutional investors who hedge** — a European pension fund that buys US Treasuries for yield but does not want the currency risk hedges by *selling dollars forward*, which means it must keep *buying* dollars in the spot-and-swap market to roll that hedge. Its hedging program is a standing, mechanical demand for dollar funding. The fourth is anyone running a **carry trade** funded in a cheap currency and invested in a dollar asset, which is a dollar short by another name.

The critical point is that none of these players can simply *stop* being short. The non-US bank cannot dump a years-long loan book overnight without crystallizing huge losses. The EM corporate cannot repay a ten-year dollar bond early. The pension fund cannot abandon its hedge without taking on currency risk its mandate forbids. The short is *sticky* — embedded in long-dated contracts and institutional mandates — which is precisely why it cannot be unwound when the squeeze hits. Everyone needs to keep rolling, all at once, exactly when rolling becomes impossible. That stickiness is the difference between a market that gently reprices and one that seizes.

### A short history of how the short got so big

The offshore dollar system was not designed; it grew. It began in the 1950s and 1960s, when Soviet and European banks started holding dollar deposits *outside* the United States — partly to keep dollar balances beyond the reach of US authorities during the Cold War, partly because London banks found they could lend those dollars profitably without US regulatory constraints. The "eurodollar" market was born: dollars on European balance sheets, lent and re-lent among banks, entirely outside the US banking system.

Once that market existed, it ballooned. The petrodollar recycling of the 1970s — oil exporters earning dollars and depositing them in international banks, which lent them on to developing countries — poured fuel on it (the [petrodollar and dollar dominance](/blog/trading/finance/petrodollar-and-dollar-dominance) story is the macro backdrop to this plumbing). Financial globalization in the 1990s and 2000s multiplied it again, as banks everywhere built dollar businesses and investors everywhere reached for dollar assets. By the eve of the 2008 crisis, European banks alone had built dollar asset books estimated in the trillions, funded heavily in US money markets — the exact fault line that ruptured when those money markets froze. Every decade added to the structural short, and every decade made the eventual squeeze larger. The history is not a digression; it is *why* the swap lines had to be invented when they were.

So the foundation is this: the world is short dollars by construction. Foreign banks and firms hold long dollar assets funded by short dollar liabilities, in a currency their home central bank cannot create, and the short is sticky — locked into long contracts and mandates that cannot be unwound in a hurry. On a calm day, the FX swap market and wholesale funding markets quietly refinance this short, over and over, and nobody notices. The whole drama of this post is what happens on the day that quiet machinery jams.

## The squeeze: how a calm market becomes a stampede

A structural short is not, by itself, a crisis. It is a vulnerability — dry tinder. What turns it into a fire is a shock that makes everyone reach for dollars at the same moment. To see why the dynamic is so violent, we have to understand that a dollar shortage feeds on itself.

In normal times, the dollar funding market clears smoothly because dollars circulate. A bank that is short dollars today borrows from a bank that is long dollars today, and tomorrow the flows reverse. The market is like a busy intersection where traffic flows because everyone keeps moving. A shock is what happens when every driver slams the brakes at once.

![Stress makes banks hoard dollars which widens the basis and deepens the squeeze](/imgs/blogs/the-global-dollar-shortage-and-central-bank-swap-lines-4.png)

Trace the loop in that figure, because the feedback is the entire mechanism. A shock hits — a Lehman, a pandemic, a sudden repricing of risk — and risk appetite collapses. The first reflex of every bank treasurer is the same: **hoard your dollars**. Do not lend them out; you might need them yourself. So the banks that *were* the suppliers of dollars to the funding market suddenly stop supplying. At the same time, demand for dollars *surges*, because everyone with a dollar liability wants to lock in their funding before it disappears, and everyone holding risky assets is selling them for the safety of cash — dollar cash. Supply falls and demand spikes in the same instant.

With direct borrowing markets seizing up, the demand floods into the one channel still open: the **FX swap market**, where you can turn your home currency into dollars for a fixed term. The crush of demand there pushes the price of dollars-via-swaps far above the price implied by interest rates alone. That gap — the extra premium you pay to get dollars through the FX market — is the **cross-currency basis**, and it blows out. A blown-out basis makes rolling funding far more expensive, which squeezes the structurally short institutions harder, which forces some of them to sell dollar assets to raise cash, which drives asset prices down and risk appetite *lower* — feeding the shock that started the whole loop. The squeeze is self-reinforcing. Left alone, it spirals until something breaks or someone with an unlimited supply of dollars steps in.

### Why the FX swap market is where it shows up

It is worth slowing down on *why* the cross-currency basis is the pressure gauge. When a Japanese bank needs dollars for three months and cannot borrow them directly, it does an FX swap: today it gives a counterparty yen and receives dollars at the spot rate; in three months it returns the dollars and takes back its yen at a pre-agreed forward rate. Economically, it has borrowed dollars and posted yen as collateral. The cost of that synthetic dollar loan is baked into the forward rate.

There is a textbook law — **covered interest parity** — that says this synthetic dollar borrowing should cost exactly the same as borrowing dollars directly. If it were cheaper to borrow dollars via the swap, arbitrageurs would pile in until the gap closed. The cross-currency basis measures how far reality departs from that law. A basis of zero means the textbook holds. A *negative* basis means borrowing dollars through the FX swap costs *more* than the textbook says — you are paying a premium to get your hands on dollars. Negative basis is, quite literally, the price of a dollar shortage. (The mechanics of why covered parity breaks at all are the subject of the sibling post on [the cross-currency basis when covered parity breaks](/blog/trading/forex/the-cross-currency-basis-when-covered-parity-breaks); here we care about what it tells us in a crisis.)

### The money-market fund: the trigger nobody watches

There is one player whose behavior turns a worry into a squeeze, and it sits inside the United States: the **US money-market fund**. These funds — pools where corporations and households park cash for safety and a small yield — are a huge supplier of short-term dollars to the world's banks. In normal times a US money-market fund happily lends dollars to a French or Japanese bank for a few weeks at a tiny premium. Those loans are a major source of the short-dated dollar funding that non-US banks rely on.

Now consider what a money-market fund does in a panic. Its own investors — nervous corporates and savers — start pulling their cash out, because in a crisis everyone wants their money in hand. To meet those redemptions, the fund must raise cash *fast*, so it stops rolling its loans to foreign banks and demands repayment. Worse, money-market funds are terrified of "breaking the buck" — letting their share price dip below \$1 — which happened to one famous fund the day after Lehman failed and triggered a stampede out of the whole sector. So at the first sign of stress, money-market funds yank their dollars home and refuse to lend to anyone they perceive as risky, which in a global panic means *every* foreign bank.

That is the trigger. The moment money-market funds pull back, the non-US banks lose a primary source of dollar funding overnight, through no fault of their own. They turn to the FX swap market for dollars instead — and the crush of that diverted demand is precisely what blows the basis out. In both 2008 and 2011 and again in 2020, a retreat by US money-market funds from foreign-bank lending was the proximate spark. The structural short is the tinder; the money-market fund's flight is the match. A reader who wants an early warning of a dollar squeeze should watch money-market fund flows and the spread between unsecured bank funding rates and risk-free rates — they often move before the basis does.

#### Worked example: the cost of the squeeze on a \$10,000,000 roll

Let us price the squeeze concretely. Our European bank needs to roll **\$10,000,000** of three-month dollar funding through the FX swap market, because direct borrowing has dried up. Suppose the "fair" cost of dollar funding — the rate covered interest parity says it should be — is **5.00%** per year.

In calm conditions the basis is roughly **−15 basis points**, a minor friction. The bank's all-in cost of dollars via the swap is about 5.00% + 0.15% = **5.15%**. For one quarter that is \$10,000,000 × 5.15% × (90 ÷ 360) = **\$128,750**.

Now the crisis hits and the basis blows out to **−150 basis points** (−1.50%). The convention is that a more-negative basis means *paying more* for dollars, so the all-in cost becomes 5.00% + 1.50% = **6.50%**. For the quarter that is \$10,000,000 × 6.50% × (90 ÷ 360) = **\$162,500**.

The squeeze just added **\$162,500 − \$128,750 = \$33,750** to the cost of a single \$10,000,000 roll for three months — a jump of about **26%** in funding cost, purely from the basis. Scale that to a bank rolling \$50 billion of dollar funding and the basis blowout costs it on the order of **\$170 million a quarter** in extra funding. The intuition: the cross-currency basis is a tax on being short dollars, and in a crisis the tax rate explodes.

#### Worked example: what −150bps means in forward points

The same squeeze shows up in the forward rate the bank is quoted. Say spot USD/JPY is **150.00** and the three-month interest rates are 5.00% for dollars and 0.50% for yen. With no basis, covered parity gives a three-month forward of roughly 150.00 × (1 + 0.0050 × 0.25) ÷ (1 + 0.0500 × 0.25) ≈ **148.34** — the yen at a forward premium because it carries the lower rate.

When the basis goes to **−150bps**, the dollar borrower is effectively charged an extra 1.50% per year, which shifts the forward against them by roughly 150.00 × 0.0150 × 0.25 ≈ **0.56 yen**, to about **147.78**. The bank receives fewer yen back per dollar at the far leg — that worse forward *is* the premium it pays for dollars, expressed in the price of the pair. The intuition: a dollar shortage does not announce itself in a headline rate; it hides inside the forward points, where only a treasury desk feels it.

## The basis blowout: reading the pressure gauge

Now that we know the cross-currency basis is the price of a dollar shortage, we can use it as a crisis thermometer. When the basis is near zero, dollar funding is plentiful. When it plunges deeply negative, the world is scrambling. The history of the basis is therefore a history of every dollar funding panic of the last two decades.

![Cross-currency basis plunges deeply negative in 2008 and 2020 crises](/imgs/blogs/the-global-dollar-shortage-and-central-bank-swap-lines-5.png)

That chart is the heartbeat monitor of the dollar funding system. In calm years the basis sits in a narrow band near zero — a basis of **−12 to −15 basis points** for EUR/USD in late 2019 and again in late 2024 is just the ordinary cost of regulatory frictions. Then the crises hit and the line craters. In the autumn of 2008, the three-month USD/JPY basis plunged to roughly **−220 basis points** — a savage premium, meaning yen-based borrowers were effectively paying over two extra percentage points a year just to get their hands on dollars. The EUR/USD basis hit about **−160 basis points** in the same panic. In March 2020 the USD/JPY basis blew out again, to about **−145 basis points**, and EUR/USD to about **−85**. The eurozone crisis of 2011–2012 produced its own deep dive to **−150** for EUR/USD as European banks, frozen out of US money-market funding, scrambled for dollars.

Each of those spikes is a moment when the structural short turned into an active stampede. And notice the asymmetry: the basis can sit at −10 for years and then gap to −150 in days. It is not a gently varying price. It is a fault line that holds and holds and then ruptures, because the dynamic that drives it — everyone reaching for the same dollars at once — is a coordination failure, not a gradual repricing. This is the same coordination logic that turns a solvent bank into a failed one in a classic run; the parallel to [bank runs as coordination games](/blog/trading/game-theory/bank-runs-as-coordination-games-diamond-dybvig-and-svb) is exact, only here the "deposit" everyone is fleeing for is the dollar itself.

### Who feels the basis, and who profits from it

A blown-out basis is a cost to the structurally short — the foreign banks and corporates who *need* dollars. But it is, symmetrically, an *opportunity* for whoever has dollars to spare. A US money-market fund, a US bank, or a sovereign wealth fund sitting on idle dollars can lend them through the FX swap market and earn that fat negative basis as extra yield, taking essentially no currency risk (the swap is fully hedged). In principle this arbitrage should close the basis instantly.

Why doesn't it? Because in a crisis the people *with* dollars are hoarding too, and the banks who would normally intermediate the arbitrage are constrained — by balance-sheet limits, by regulatory ratios that make holding the trade expensive, by their own risk managers screaming to reduce exposure. The arbitrage that should snap the basis back to zero is gummed up exactly when it is needed most. That is why an *outside* actor with an unlimited, unconstrained supply of dollars and no balance-sheet limit — a central bank — is the only entity that can reliably break the squeeze. Which brings us to the fix.

#### Worked example: the carry an unconstrained lender earns

Suppose a US-based fund has **\$10,000,000** of idle dollars during the March 2020 squeeze, when the three-month USD/JPY basis is **−145 basis points**. It lends the dollars through a fully-hedged FX swap to a Japanese counterparty desperate for them. Over and above the ordinary dollar interest it would have earned, the basis hands it an extra **1.45%** annualized for taking, in theory, no net currency risk.

On \$10,000,000 for three months that extra return is \$10,000,000 × 1.45% × (90 ÷ 360) = **\$36,250** of nearly-free carry for the quarter. Annualized and scaled, this is why dealers *love* a wide basis — if they have the balance sheet to hold it. The reason the basis stays wide anyway is that, in a panic, almost nobody does. The intuition: the basis is wide *because* the natural arbitrageurs are sidelined, which is exactly the condition that calls for a lender who never runs out of dollars and never worries about its balance sheet.

## Swap lines: the fix, and the world's lender of last resort

Here is the elegant solution central banks worked out, refined over 2008, and perfected in 2020. The Federal Reserve cannot directly lend dollars to a Japanese or German bank — those banks are not its counterparties and not under its regulation. But the Fed *can* lend dollars to the Bank of Japan or the European Central Bank, which *can* lend to their own banks. So the Fed deals with the foreign central bank, and the foreign central bank deals with its own banks. The dollars flow through a chain.

![Fed lends dollars to a foreign central bank which relends to local banks](/imgs/blogs/the-global-dollar-shortage-and-central-bank-swap-lines-6.png)

Follow the pipeline. The Fed creates dollar reserves — which it can do without limit, because it is the issuer of dollars. It **swaps** those dollars for an equivalent amount of the foreign central bank's currency at the current spot rate, with an agreement to reverse the swap at the same rate at a set future date. So the Fed now holds, say, euros, and the ECB now holds dollars. Crucially, because the reversal is at the *same* exchange rate, the Fed takes on **no currency risk** — it gets back exactly the dollars it lent, plus interest. The ECB then auctions those dollars to its own banks against collateral. Those banks finally have the dollars they were scrambling for. The squeeze eases. The basis, which had blown out to −145, snaps back toward zero as the artificial shortage is filled by a supplier who cannot run dry.

This is why central-bank swap lines are correctly described as making the Fed the **world's lender of last resort in dollars**. The traditional lender of last resort lends the home currency to home banks. The swap-line network extends that backstop across borders: the Fed, acting through a handful of major central banks, stands behind the dollar funding of banks in countries it does not regulate, in a currency those countries cannot print. It is one of the most consequential and least-understood pieces of financial architecture on earth, and it works precisely because the Fed faces no balance-sheet constraint and no currency risk on the trade.

### Why the swap line, and not just dollar loans?

A natural question: why the elaborate swap structure? Why not have the Fed simply lend dollars directly to foreign banks, or sell dollars outright? Three reasons, each load-bearing.

First, **counterparty and regulatory reach**. The Fed knows and trusts the major central banks; it does not want to vet the collateral and creditworthiness of thousands of foreign commercial banks it does not supervise. By dealing only with central banks, it delegates the credit risk of the local banks to the institution best placed to bear it — the local central bank, which *does* regulate them and takes the loss if one fails.

Second, **no FX risk and no permanent money-printing**. Because the swap reverses at the same exchange rate, the Fed is not betting on the euro or the yen, and the dollars it creates are *temporary* — they come back when the swap unwinds. This is not quantitative easing in disguise; it is a fully-collateralized, self-liquidating loan. When the crisis passes and banks no longer need the dollars, the swaps mature, the dollars are returned and extinguished, and the swap-line balance falls back to zero — which is exactly the collapse you see on the right-hand side of the very first chart in this post.

Third, **speed and signaling**. A standing network of swap lines can be activated in hours. And the mere *announcement* that the lines are open and the price has been cut often calms the market before a single dollar is drawn, because it removes the fear that drives the hoarding. The swap line is as much a confidence device as a funding device — the same "don't fight the central bank" logic explored in [the central-bank game](/blog/trading/game-theory/the-central-bank-game-credibility-commitment-and-dont-fight-the-fed). Once the market believes the dollars will be there, the rush to grab them first subsides.

### The two tiers — and the edge of the safety net

The swap-line network is not one undifferentiated backstop; it has a deliberate structure, and that structure decides which banks live and which are left to fend for themselves.

At the center are the **standing swap lines**: permanent, uncapped arrangements among the Fed and five core central banks — the European Central Bank, the Bank of Japan, the Bank of England, the Swiss National Bank, and the Bank of Canada. These six made their swap network permanent in 2013, precisely so it could be activated in hours rather than negotiated mid-crisis. For a bank in the eurozone, Japan, the UK, Switzerland, or Canada, the Fed backstop is always there, always uncapped, ready before the alarm rings. This is the inner circle of the dollar safety net.

Outside that core is a **temporary tier**: lines the Fed opens *only* in acute stress, with selected additional central banks, for a limited time. In 2008 and again in 2020 the Fed extended temporary lines to a further group — including the central banks of Australia, Brazil, Mexico, South Korea, Singapore, and a few others — capped in size and time-limited. These countries get a backstop *when the Fed judges the crisis systemic enough*, but it is discretionary, capped, and can be withdrawn. They are the second ring.

And then there is everyone else — most of the emerging world — with **no Fed swap line at all**. A bank in a country outside both tiers that runs short of dollars in a crisis has no Fed backstop. It must rely on its own central bank's reserves, on the International Monetary Fund, or on luck. This is why foreign-exchange reserves matter so much for those countries: reserves are the *self-insurance* a country buys precisely because it sits outside the swap-line net. The structural short is global and democratic; the official cure is hierarchical and exclusive. Which ring a country sits in is one of the most important and least-discussed facts about its financial stability — and it maps almost exactly onto which currencies tend to collapse in a dollar squeeze versus which hold.

### The repo backstop for everyone else

The Fed knows the swap-line network leaves most of the world outside, so in March 2020 it added a second tool aimed at the second and third rings: the **FIMA repo facility** (Foreign and International Monetary Authorities). Through it, a foreign central bank that holds US Treasuries — and most do, as reserves — can temporarily *pledge* those Treasuries to the Fed in exchange for dollars, rather than dumping them on the open market.

This matters because, without it, a central bank short of dollars in a crisis would do the most damaging thing possible: sell its US Treasuries to raise cash. If dozens of central banks sell Treasuries at once, the world's safe asset crashes — which is part of what nearly happened in March 2020. The FIMA repo gives those central banks a pressure valve: borrow dollars against your Treasuries instead of selling them. It does not extend the *credit* backstop of a swap line — a country still needs to *own* the Treasuries — but it converts a country's existing reserves into usable dollars without a fire-sale. Together, the swap lines (for the inner rings) and the FIMA repo (for any reserve-holding central bank) form a layered dollar safety net: a credit line for the core, a collateralized pressure valve for the rest. It is the most complete the architecture has ever been — and it is still incomplete, because a country with few Treasuries to pledge and no swap line has neither.

#### Worked example: a bank rescued by the swap line

Return to our European bank, mid-March 2020, needing to roll **\$10,000,000** of three-month funding when the basis is **−150bps** and direct markets are frozen. Without a backstop, its only option is the blown-out swap market, costing the **\$162,500** we computed — assuming it can find a counterparty at all.

Now the Fed opens its swap line with the ECB and cuts the price, and the ECB auctions dollars to its banks at a rate close to the Fed's overnight index swap rate plus a small spread — call it **5.25%** all-in, far below the panic level. The bank borrows its \$10,000,000 from the ECB's dollar auction. Its quarterly cost is \$10,000,000 × 5.25% × (90 ÷ 360) = **\$131,250** — back near the calm-market cost of \$128,750, and *\$31,250 cheaper* than the panic swap market. More importantly, the bank no longer has to *fire-sell* its dollar bonds to raise cash. The forced selling that fed the doom loop stops. Multiply this across hundreds of banks and you see how a few hundred billion of swap-line dollars puts out a fire that would otherwise have consumed trillions in asset values. The intuition: the swap line does not just lower the cost of the roll — it removes the *need to sell*, which is the channel through which a funding squeeze becomes a market crash.

## 2008 versus 2020: the same fire, a faster hose

The swap-line system was not handed down complete. It was *invented* in the heat of 2008, improvised as Lehman's collapse froze global dollar funding, and then refined into a fast, pre-positioned tool that was redeployed in days in 2020. Comparing the two episodes is the best way to see both how the tool works and how much central banks learned.

![Side by side comparison of the 2008 and 2020 swap-line responses](/imgs/blogs/the-global-dollar-shortage-and-central-bank-swap-lines-8.png)

In **2008**, the lines had to be built mid-panic. The Fed first opened modest swap arrangements in December 2007 as the funding strains began, then scaled them dramatically after Lehman failed in September 2008. By December 2008 the outstanding draws peaked at **\$583 billion** — at the time an almost unimaginable number, larger than the entire balance sheet of many central banks. But because the response was reactive — the architecture was being assembled while the building burned — the squeeze had time to do real damage. The USD/JPY cross-currency basis hit roughly **−220 basis points**, the deepest in the modern record, and the funding stress ran for months. The world learned, the hard way, exactly how short of dollars it was.

In **2020**, the Fed had the playbook ready. Within days of the March dash-for-cash, it reactivated and *enhanced* the standing swap lines with five core central banks, lowered the price, lengthened the maturities, and reopened temporary lines with nine more. The draws peaked lower — about **\$449 billion** — and, more tellingly, the basis was stopped at a less extreme **−145bps** for USD/JPY and normalized within weeks rather than months. The difference is the difference between a fire department that has to invent the fire truck after the alarm rings and one that keeps the engine fueled in the station. Same fire. Much faster hose. The 2020 episode is, in this sense, a quiet triumph of institutional memory.

### The doom loop, and the dollar's perverse strength

One more pattern ties the episodes together and connects back to this series' spine. In *both* crises, the dollar *rose* even as the Fed eased — the perverse strength we saw on March 18, 2020. This is the funding squeeze showing up directly in the exchange rate.

![The dollar index spikes in the 2008 and 2020 funding squeezes](/imgs/blogs/the-global-dollar-shortage-and-central-bank-swap-lines-7.png)

The logic is mechanical, not sentimental. When the world is short dollars and scrambling to fund, that scramble *is* demand for dollars in the FX market. Everyone who needs to roll a dollar liability is, in effect, a forced buyer of dollars. Everyone selling risky assets is rotating into dollar cash. So the dollar — the very asset the crisis is *about* — appreciates, sometimes violently, exactly when US policy is at its most stimulative. You can see it in the index: the DXY jumped in 2008 and again in 2020, both moments of acute funding stress. This is the "panic cheek" of the **dollar smile** — the pattern where the dollar wins both when the US is booming *and* when the world is panicking, sagging only in the calm middle. The dollar shortage is *the* mechanism behind the left side of that smile, which is exactly why the sibling post on [the dollar smile](/blog/trading/forex/the-dollar-smile-why-the-dollar-wins-in-boom-and-in-panic) and this one are two views of the same animal: the funding squeeze is *why* the dollar wins in panic.

#### Worked example: the squeeze's profit and loss in pips

Put a number on the perverse strength. Suppose a Japanese exporter expects to receive **\$10,000,000** in three months and, in calm times, has hedged by agreeing to sell those dollars forward at USD/JPY **150.00**, locking in ¥1,500,000,000.

Now the March 2020 squeeze hits, the dollar spikes, and spot jumps to **111.00** — wait, the yen actually *strengthened* against the dollar in early 2020 as the carry trade unwound, so let us use a cleaner squeeze case: the autumn-2022 dollar surge, when USD/JPY ran from 140 toward **150** on US rate strength. A US importer who had *not* hedged and owed **\$0** but needed to *buy* \$10,000,000 of goods funding watched the cost in yen terms rise as the dollar climbed: at 140 the \$10,000,000 cost ¥1,400,000,000; at 150 it cost ¥1,500,000,000 — an extra **¥100,000,000**, about **\$667,000** at the new rate, purely from the dollar's appreciation. The intuition: in a dollar squeeze, *every* unhedged dollar payable gets more expensive in home-currency terms at the worst possible moment, which is why corporates rush to buy dollars forward, which pushes the dollar higher still — the squeeze and the strong dollar are the same event seen from two desks.

## Common misconceptions

**"A dollar shortage means there literally aren't enough dollars."** No. The total quantity of dollars in the world does not shrink in a crisis — if anything the Fed is creating more. A "shortage" means dollars stop *circulating*: the banks and funds that hold them hoard them, so the dollars that exist are not available to those who need them. It is a distribution and confidence failure, not a quantity one. That is also why the fix is not "print more dollars" in the abstract but "get dollars to the specific institutions that are short" — which is exactly what swap lines do. In March 2020 there were trillions of idle dollars; the problem was that none of them would move.

**"Swap lines are a bailout of foreign banks paid for by US taxpayers."** This is the most common political misreading, and it is wrong on the economics. The swaps are fully collateralized — the Fed holds the foreign central bank's currency for the duration — and reverse at the *same* exchange rate, so the Fed bears no FX risk and earns interest on the dollars it lends. Every swap-line dollar ever extended has been repaid in full, with interest; the Fed has actually *made* money on the program. The "taxpayer" is not on the hook because the loan is to a central bank, against that central bank's currency, with a contractual reversal. It is the safest large-scale lending the Fed does.

**"The cross-currency basis should always be zero because of arbitrage."** In a textbook with frictionless balance sheets, yes. In reality the basis is persistently slightly negative even in calm times — around **−10 to −15 basis points** for EUR/USD — because the banks who would arbitrage it face regulatory capital charges and balance-sheet costs for holding the trade. The post-2008 leverage rules made balance sheet scarce and expensive, so the arbitrage that should flatten the basis is permanently a little impaired, and *severely* impaired in a crisis. The basis is the visible price of those balance-sheet frictions.

**"Only banks care about this — it's plumbing, not markets."** The plumbing *is* the market in a crisis. A dollar funding squeeze forces fire-sales of dollar assets — Treasuries, corporate bonds, EM debt — which is why even the US Treasury market malfunctioned in March 2020. It drives the dollar higher, which crushes emerging-market currencies and triggers the [dollar wrecking-ball](/blog/trading/forex/the-dollar-as-a-wrecking-ball-for-emerging-markets) dynamic. And it transmits the panic globally in hours. The basis and the swap lines are not a sideshow to the crisis — for a few critical days, they *are* the crisis.

**"If the Fed is the world's lender of last resort, the system is safe."** Safer, not safe. The swap-line network covers a *core* group — the major advanced-economy central banks — plus a temporary tier in extreme stress. A bank in a country *outside* that network has no Fed backstop and must rely on its own reserves and the IMF. The dollar safety net has a clearly drawn edge, and which side of it a country sits on is a first-order question for that country's financial stability. The structural short is global; the official backstop is not.

## How it shows up in real markets

**The autumn of 2008.** After Lehman failed on September 15, the dollar funding market froze solid. European banks, which had funded vast dollar asset books in US money markets, were suddenly frozen out as those money-market funds — terrified after one "broke the buck" — pulled their dollars home. The USD/JPY basis cratered to about **−220bps**; the EUR/USD basis to about **−160bps**. The Fed responded by escalating swap lines from a modest facility to, eventually, *uncapped* lines with the core central banks, and draws peaked at **\$583 billion** in December. It was the moment the world discovered, in real time, that the dollar funding of the global banking system had no backstop — and the moment that backstop was built.

**The eurozone crisis, 2011–2012.** As the sovereign-debt panic spread, US money-market funds again fled European bank paper, re-creating a dollar squeeze without a fresh global shock — just fear of European banks. The EUR/USD basis blew out to roughly **−150bps** in late 2011. In a coordinated move on November 30, 2011, six central banks — the Fed, ECB, BoE, BoJ, SNB, and Bank of Canada — cut the price of the dollar swap lines together. The basis began to mend almost immediately. It was a clean demonstration that the *announcement* and *pricing* of the backstop, not just its use, calms the squeeze.

**The March 2020 dash-for-cash.** As the pandemic shutdown hit, every investor on earth tried to raise cash at once, and for the non-US world that meant dollars. The USD/JPY basis blew out to about **−145bps**; the dollar index spiked; even US Treasuries — the world's safe asset — were sold for cash. The Fed reactivated and enhanced the swap lines on March 15, lowered the price and lengthened maturities on March 19, and added nine temporary lines. Draws peaked near **\$449 billion**. The basis normalized within weeks. The 2008 architecture, kept warm, worked faster the second time.

**March 2023 and the regional-bank scare.** When Silicon Valley Bank failed and Credit Suisse was absorbed by UBS, the Fed and core central banks pre-emptively moved the standing swap lines from weekly to *daily* operations to reassure the market that dollars would be available. Draws stayed modest — about **\$60 billion** — precisely because the signal worked. This is the mature form of the tool: used as a confidence backstop that, by existing and being visibly ready, often prevents the squeeze it is designed to cure.

**The persistent quarter-end and year-end basis.** Even with no crisis at all, the basis reliably dips at quarter-ends and especially year-ends, because banks shrink their balance sheets for regulatory reporting dates and pull back from the FX-swap arbitrage. A reader watching the basis tighten predictably into December 31 each year is watching the everyday, non-crisis version of the same balance-sheet friction that explodes in a panic. It is the structural short breathing in and out.

**Japan's structural hedging demand.** A quieter, permanent version of the squeeze runs through Japan. Japanese life insurers and pension funds hold enormous portfolios of US bonds for yield, and many hedge the currency by selling dollars forward — which means they are constant, mechanical buyers of dollar funding through the FX swap market. This standing demand keeps the USD/JPY basis structurally more negative than most other pairs even in calm times (around **−35 to −40bps** in late 2024 versus **−12** for EUR/USD), and it is why the yen basis tends to blow out the *furthest* in a crisis, as we saw with the **−220bps** print in 2008. The yen basis is not just a crisis gauge; it is a live readout of Japan's permanent hedging machine, with the crisis spikes layered on top.

**The dollar's reserve-currency feedback.** There is a self-reinforcing loop worth naming. Because the dollar is the funding currency, foreign institutions hold dollar assets; because they hold dollar assets, they need dollar funding; because they need dollar funding, demand for dollars is deep and constant, which keeps the dollar's borrowing markets the most liquid on earth; and that depth is *why* foreign institutions choose to borrow and invest in dollars in the first place. The structural short is both a *consequence* of dollar dominance and a *cause* of it. This is why the dollar's special role is so durable: every crisis that proves how short the world is of dollars also proves how indispensable the dollar is, which deepens the next cycle's short. The squeeze is not a bug in dollar hegemony — it is one of the load-bearing struts that holds it up.

## The takeaway: read the dollar as a funding instrument, not just a currency

The deepest lesson of the dollar shortage is that the dollar is not merely *a* currency that happens to be popular. It is the **funding currency of the world**, and that role gives it a behavior no other currency has: it gets *stronger* in a global panic, when every other risk asset is getting weaker. If you treat the dollar as merely America's money, this looks irrational — why would the dollar rally when the Fed is cutting to zero? Once you see the structural short, it is obvious. A panic is a margin call on the entire non-US financial system, and that margin call is payable in dollars.

So when you read a currency — the whole point of this series — read the dollar with a second lens. The first lens is the rate gap: the dollar's level versus the gap between US and foreign interest rates, the master variable of FX. The second lens is *funding stress*: when the cross-currency basis blows out, when swap-line draws spike, when the dollar rallies despite dovish US policy, you are watching the structural short turn active. Those two lenses can point in *opposite* directions — the Fed can be cutting rates (rate-gap bearish for the dollar) while the world scrambles for dollar funding (squeeze bullish for the dollar) — and in a crisis the funding lens wins, hard and fast.

For a practitioner, three things follow. First, **watch the basis as a stress gauge**: a basis grinding from −20 toward −80 is the early tremor of a dollar funding squeeze, often before equities have fully noticed. Second, **respect the swap line as the circuit-breaker**: when the Fed opens or re-prices the lines, the squeeze has a ceiling, and fading the dollar's panic spike becomes a far better bet than it was the day before. Third, **know which side of the network you are on**: an institution or a country inside the core swap-line group has a Fed backstop; one outside it is on its own, and its currency and its banks carry a tail risk the insiders do not.

The world is short dollars by construction, and it always will be, because the offshore dollar system is the cheapest, deepest way for the planet to borrow and trade. That structural short is a quiet, useful machine on a calm day and a doom loop on a bad one. The single most important piece of crisis architecture built in our lifetime is the set of pipes that lets a handful of central banks act as one to flood that short with dollars on a few hours' notice. It is invisible until the morning the world runs out of dollars — and then it is the only thing that matters.
