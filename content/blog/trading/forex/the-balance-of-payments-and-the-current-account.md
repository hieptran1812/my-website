> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A country's books always balance: whatever it spends abroad on net (a current-account deficit) is exactly matched by capital it borrows or sells in from abroad (a financial-account surplus). The two sides sum to zero by accounting law, so a "trade deficit" really means *you are importing capital*.
>
> - The **current account** records the flow of goods, services, income, and gifts; the **financial account** records who owns what across borders. By construction, **current account + capital/financial account = 0**.
> - A deficit is not automatically bad. It is **benign** when the imported capital funds productive investment and arrives as stable, long-term money (FDI); it is **dangerous** when it funds consumption and arrives as hot, reversible portfolio flows.
> - A persistent deficit is a *structural pull down* on a currency — the country must keep attracting capital to pay for it — but it can run for decades if the world is happy to keep lending (see the United States, at **−3.3% of GDP** in 2024).
> - **The one number to remember:** Vietnam ran a **+\$24.8B goods trade surplus** in 2024, and that surplus — plus **\$16B** of remittances and **\$25.4B** of disbursed FDI — is what lets the State Bank crawl the dong gently weaker instead of devaluing it.

In the autumn of 1992 the British government discovered, in a single afternoon, that it could not pay its way. The pound was pegged inside Europe's Exchange Rate Mechanism, and to hold the peg the Bank of England had to buy pounds with its foreign-currency reserves faster than speculators were selling them. The reserves drained. The Bank raised interest rates from 10% to 12% to 15% in the space of hours to make holding pounds attractive — and still the money flowed out the door. By the evening of 16 September, Black Wednesday, the UK had given up: it left the ERM and let the pound fall. The story is usually told as a tale of George Soros and a billion-dollar bet, and we cover that drama [in its own case study](/blog/trading/finance/soros-bank-of-england-1992-black-wednesday). But underneath the drama is a piece of plumbing that decides the fate of every currency on earth: a country has to *finance* the gap between what it buys from the world and what it sells. That financing is the balance of payments.

You do not need a currency crisis to care about this. Every exchange rate is, at bottom, a price that clears two opposing flows: the flow of money paid for goods and services, and the flow of money chasing assets and yield. When those two flows do not match at the current price, the price has to move — and "the price" is the currency. Read a country's balance of payments and you are reading the deep current that pulls its currency up or down over years, the tide beneath the day-to-day waves of interest-rate news. This is the second great force in our series' spine. The first is the rate gap — covered in [how monetary policy moves currencies](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-monetary-policy-moves-currencies-rate-differentials). The second is the *flow of money across borders*, and the balance of payments is the ledger that records every dollar of it.

By the end of this post you will be able to take any country's external accounts apart piece by piece, say what a deficit actually means without flinching, judge whether a given deficit is safe or scary, and trace exactly how Vietnam's quiet surpluses keep the dong on its managed leash. Here is the whole machine, taken apart.

## Foundations: The balance of payments identity

Start from zero, with no jargon. The **balance of payments** (BoP) is a complete record of every economic transaction between the residents of one country and the rest of the world over a period — usually a quarter or a year. Every time a Vietnamese exporter sells coffee to Germany, an American family books a hotel in Paris, a Japanese pension fund buys a US Treasury bond, or a worker in Texas wires money home to Manila, that transaction lands somewhere in the balance of payments. It is the country's bank statement with the world.

The single most important fact about this statement is that *it always balances*. Not approximately, not usually — always, by the rules of double-entry bookkeeping. Every transaction has two sides. If you, a resident, buy a \$100 jacket from abroad, two things happen at once: a \$100 import shows up (money owed out for a good), and a \$100 payment shows up (money actually leaving, or a \$100 IOU you handed the seller). The "what you bought" and the "how you paid" are two entries for one event, and they are equal and opposite. Sum every such pair across the whole country and the grand total is, definitionally, zero.

That is why the headline you always hear — "the trade deficit" — is only ever *half* of a sentence. The other half is hiding in plain sight, and finding it is the whole point of this post.

### The two big accounts

The balance of payments is split into two main accounts, and you must keep them straight.

The **current account** records flows that are *gone for good* — they do not create a future claim. It has four parts:

1. **The trade balance in goods** — exports minus imports of physical things (cars, oil, coffee, semiconductors). This is the part that makes the news.
2. **The trade balance in services** — exports minus imports of invisible things (tourism, shipping, software licences, consulting, education). A British university teaching a foreign student is a *service export*; an American family's Paris holiday is a *service import*.
3. **Primary income** — the interest, dividends, and wages that flow across borders. If foreigners own \$1 trillion of your bonds and you pay them 4%, that \$40B of interest is an income *outflow* in your current account. If your firms own factories abroad and repatriate the profits, that is an income *inflow*.
4. **Secondary income (transfers)** — gifts with nothing given in return: foreign aid, and crucially, **remittances** — the money migrant workers send home. For Vietnam, the Philippines, Mexico, and dozens of other countries, this is a giant, steady inflow.

Add those four and you have the **current-account balance**. A surplus means the country, on net, *sold more to the world than it bought and received more income and gifts than it paid* — it is a net lender to the world this year. A deficit means the opposite — it is a net borrower.

The **capital and financial account** records flows that *create or extinguish a claim* — they change who owns what across borders. (In the modern IMF framework the tiny "capital account" covers things like debt forgiveness and patent sales; the big one is the **financial account**. People loosely say "capital account" to mean the whole financing side, and so will we when the distinction does not matter.) Its parts:

1. **Foreign direct investment (FDI)** — a foreigner buying or building a lasting business: Samsung building a phone factory in Bắc Ninh, or buying a controlling stake in a local firm. Long-term, sticky money.
2. **Portfolio investment** — a foreigner buying your stocks and bonds without control: a pension fund buying VND government bonds or shares on the Ho Chi Minh exchange. More liquid, faster to leave.
3. **Other investment** — cross-border bank loans and deposits.
4. **Reserve assets** — the change in the central bank's stash of foreign currency. When the State Bank of Vietnam buys dollars to stop the dong rising, its reserves grow, and that shows up here.

A small but important note on **signs**, because they are the thing people fumble. In the balance of payments, a *credit* (money the country is owed, or an asset of ours that a foreigner buys) is recorded as a plus, and a *debit* (money the country owes, or a foreign asset we buy) as a minus. So an export is a plus in the current account (the world owes us for goods shipped), an import is a minus (we owe the world). On the financing side, when a foreigner buys our bond, *we* receive their money — that is an inflow of capital, a plus in the financial account. The mirror logic is what forces the two accounts to be opposite in sign and equal in size: the same transaction that debits one account credits the other. You do not have to memorise the convention so long as you remember the punchline — **a current-account deficit and a financial-account surplus are the same dollars wearing two hats.**

### The identity that makes it all click

Here is the law that ties the two accounts together, and it is worth carving into stone:

> **Current account + Capital/Financial account = 0**

(In practice there is a small fudge line called *net errors and omissions* that mops up measurement noise, because no country can track every transaction perfectly. Conceptually, treat it as zero.)

This is not an empirical finding that *happens* to hold most years. It is an accounting identity, true by construction, like "assets = liabilities + equity" on a balance sheet. It must hold for the same reason your own household budget must: every dollar you spend that you did not earn this year had to come from *somewhere* — savings drawn down, or money borrowed, or an asset sold. A country is no different. If it buys more from the world than it sells (a current-account deficit), the difference must be financed by selling assets or borrowing from the world (a financial-account surplus). The two are the same dollars, counted from two sides.

#### Worked example: a −\$800B deficit is an +\$800B capital import

Take a large economy running a current-account deficit of **−\$800B** for the year — roughly the scale of the United States. Read the identity literally:

```
Current account            = -800   (net buyer of the world's goods/services/income)
Financial account          = +800   (net seller of assets / net borrower from the world)
-----------------------------------------------------
Sum                        =    0
```

What does that +\$800B financial-account surplus *physically mean*? It means that over the year, foreigners acquired **\$800B more of American assets than Americans acquired of foreign assets**. They bought \$800B (net) of Treasury bonds, corporate bonds, stocks, real estate, and whole companies. The country did not "lose" \$800B — it *swapped* \$800B of future claims (IOUs and ownership stakes handed to foreigners) for \$800B of present goods and services it got to consume and invest now.

So the phrase "the US has an \$800B trade deficit" and the phrase "the world invested \$800B net into the US this year" describe **the exact same event from two sides of the ledger.** You cannot have one without the other. *The intuition is that a trade deficit is not money vanishing — it is the country importing capital, and the figure below shows the two sides locking together dollar for dollar.*

### Flow versus stock — the distinction that trips everyone

One more foundation, because nearly every confused argument about trade deficits is really a flow-versus-stock confusion. The balance of payments measures **flows**: how many dollars crossed the border *this year*. The accumulation of all past flows is a **stock**: the **net international investment position** (NIIP) — the total of what the country owns abroad minus what foreigners own of it.

A country can run current-account deficits for thirty years (a flow, year after year) and build up a large negative NIIP (a stock — it owes the world a lot). The flow is the annual gap; the stock is the running total of all the IOUs and ownership stakes accumulated from financing those gaps. The danger signals live in *both*: a flow that suddenly cannot be financed (a "sudden stop") triggers an acute crisis; a stock that grows too large relative to the economy's ability to service it is a slow-burn solvency problem. Keep them separate. When a politician says "we owe China trillions," that is a stock claim (and a sloppy one). When they say "we run a deficit with China," that is a flow claim. They are related but they are not the same number, and the policy implications differ.

## How goods and capital cross the border in opposite directions

Let us make the financing side tangible, because "the financial account is a surplus" is an abstraction until you watch the dollars move. The mechanism is a loop, and once you see the loop you will never un-see it.

Suppose a US importer buys \$1,000,000 of electronics from a Vietnamese factory. The importer pays in dollars (or the bank converts dollars to dong, but follow the dollars). Now a Vietnamese exporter — or its bank — is sitting on \$1,000,000. The exporter mostly wants dong to pay wages and suppliers at home, so it sells those dollars. *Someone has to buy them.* In aggregate, across the whole country and the whole year, the dollars a country earns from selling to the world, plus the dollars foreigners bring in to invest, must equal the dollars the country spends buying from the world, plus the dollars its residents send abroad to invest. The foreign-exchange market is the clearing house where those flows meet, and the exchange rate is the price that makes them meet.

So trace the loop for a deficit country. It pays dollars *out* to buy imports. Those dollars pile up in foreign hands. The foreigners do not stuff them under a mattress — dollars earn nothing idle — so they recycle them back *in* by buying the deficit country's bonds, stocks, and businesses. The trade outflow becomes the capital inflow. The two legs are the same dollars going around a circle.

![Pipeline diagram showing dollars paid out for imports landing abroad, being held by foreigners, used to buy bonds and stocks and real estate, then flowing back in as capital](/imgs/blogs/the-balance-of-payments-and-the-current-account-3.png)

This loop is why a current-account deficit and a financial-account surplus are mechanically inseparable. The dollars that leave to pay for the trade deficit are the very dollars that come back as the capital inflow. If foreigners ever *refused* to recycle — if they took their export dollars and demanded gold, or simply stopped buying the deficit country's assets — the loop would break, and the exchange rate would have to fall far enough to choke off the imports and balance the books the hard way. That refusal, when it happens suddenly, is the anatomy of a currency crisis. We will get there.

#### Worked example: financing a deficit at a rising cost

Make the cost of financing concrete. Say a country runs a steady current-account deficit and, to fund it, foreigners hold a growing stock of its government bonds — call it **\$5,000,000,000,000** (\$5 trillion) of foreign-held debt, paying an average **3%**. The annual interest the country ships abroad is:

```
Foreign-held debt          = 5,000,000,000,000      ($5T)
Average coupon             = 3.0%
Annual interest outflow    = 5,000,000,000,000 x 0.03
                           = 150,000,000,000         ($150B / year)
```

That \$150B is a **primary-income outflow** — it sits in the *current account*, making the deficit bigger next year, which requires more borrowing, which raises the interest bill further. Now suppose a wave of inflation pushes the average rate the country must pay from 3% to **5%**. Once the stock has rolled over, the interest outflow becomes:

```
Annual interest outflow    = 5,000,000,000,000 x 0.05
                           = 250,000,000,000         ($250B / year)
```

An extra **\$100B a year** flows abroad, purely on the financing cost. *The lesson is that financing a deficit is cheap when the world lends to you at low rates and brutal when those rates rise — the same stock of debt can cost \$150B or \$250B depending on what the market charges, which is exactly why a deficit country lives or dies on the confidence of its foreign creditors.*

## What a deficit actually means — and when it bites

Now we can answer the question that the whole topic exists to answer. A persistent current-account deficit puts *downward pressure* on a currency, because the country has to keep attracting foreign capital to finance it, and to keep attracting that capital it must offer something — higher yields, cheaper assets, or a weaker currency that makes its exports competitive and its assets cheap to foreign buyers. The "weaker currency" lever is the one that operates through the exchange rate. A country that the world is reluctant to lend to must let its currency fall until the imports shrink and the deficit closes.

But — and this is the part that separates people who understand the balance of payments from people who just panic at the word "deficit" — **a deficit is not automatically a problem.** Whether it is benign or dangerous depends on two questions, and only two:

1. **What is the borrowed capital buying?** If the deficit funds *productive investment* — factories, infrastructure, machinery that will raise future output — then the country is doing exactly what a young person taking a student loan does: borrowing today to earn more tomorrow. The future income services the debt. This is *good* debt. If instead the deficit funds *current consumption* — financing a lifestyle the country cannot afford from its own production — then there is no future income to service the debt, and the bill simply grows. This is *bad* debt.

2. **How is the deficit financed?** If foreigners fund it with **stable, long-term FDI** — building businesses they intend to run for decades — that money cannot flee at the first wobble; a factory cannot be wired out of the country overnight. If they fund it with **hot, short-term portfolio money** — bonds and stocks that can be sold and repatriated in a single afternoon — then the financing is fickle, and a change of mood can trigger a stampede for the exits. That stampede is a *sudden stop*, and it is how the 1997 [Asian financial crisis](/blog/trading/finance/asian-financial-crisis-1997) destroyed a string of currencies that had looked fine the week before.

![Decision graph showing a current-account deficit branching by whether it funds investment or consumption and whether it is financed by stable FDI or hot portfolio money, leading to benign or dangerous outcomes](/imgs/blogs/the-balance-of-payments-and-the-current-account-5.png)

The best deficit is one that funds investment and is financed by FDI: that is a country importing both capital and capacity, on terms that cannot run away. The worst is one that funds consumption and is financed by hot money: a country living beyond its means on a credit line that can be yanked at any moment. Most real countries sit somewhere in between, and the analyst's job is to figure out *where* — not to react to the headline sign of the balance.

#### Worked example: the same deficit, two destinies

Take two countries, A and B, each running an identical current-account deficit of **−\$50B a year** in an economy of **\$1,000B** GDP — a deficit of **5% of GDP**, large enough to demand attention.

```
Country A (the good deficit)
  Deficit:                     -$50B  (5% of GDP)
  Financed by:                  $45B FDI + $5B portfolio
  Capital used for:             ports, power plants, factories
  Result:                       output rises ~4%/yr; exports grow;
                                deficit shrinks on its own by year 5
```

```
Country B (the bad deficit)
  Deficit:                     -$50B  (5% of GDP)
  Financed by:                  $10B FDI + $40B hot portfolio
  Capital used for:             imported consumer goods, a property boom
  Result:                       no new export capacity; on a risk-off
                                shock the $40B tries to leave at once
```

Country A's deficit is a feature: it is borrowing the world's savings to build the engine that will repay them. Within a few years its new factories export, the trade balance improves, and the deficit narrows without a crisis. Country B's deficit is a fault line: the \$40B of hot money is one bad headline away from a stampede, and when it goes, the currency has to collapse far enough to slam the import door shut overnight. *Two countries, the same −5% headline, opposite fates — which is why "is the deficit big?" is the wrong question and "what is it buying and who is funding it?" is the right one.*

## The deeper cause: the savings–investment gap

We have been treating the current-account balance as if it were *caused* by trade — by whether a country's exporters out-compete its importers. That is true on the surface, but underneath it there is a more fundamental identity, and grasping it is what separates a real understanding from a journalist's. The current-account balance is, by a second accounting law, **exactly equal to the gap between what a country saves and what it invests**:

> **Current-account balance = National saving − Domestic investment**

This is not a coincidence with the trade story; it is the same fact seen from the income side rather than the goods side. A country that invests more than it saves has to fund the difference from somewhere, and the only somewhere left is the rest of the world — so it imports the savings of foreigners, which *is* a current-account deficit. A country that saves more than it invests has surplus savings it must park abroad, which *is* a current-account surplus. The trade flows are how this savings–investment gap physically expresses itself, but the gap is the driver.

This is why **Myth 4 above is so important**: you cannot fix a trade deficit without changing the savings–investment balance. If a country consumes lavishly, runs big government deficits, and saves little, while still investing heavily, it *must* run a current-account deficit — by identity — no matter what its trade policy looks like. The United States runs a persistent deficit not because its exporters are uncompetitive but because, as a nation, it invests more than it saves (low household saving, large fiscal deficits). Tariffs do not touch that gap; they just reshuffle which goods and which partners the deficit shows up against. Until national saving rises or investment falls, the deficit is structurally locked in.

#### Worked example: the saving gap drives the deficit

Take a country with national income of **\$1,000B** in a year, and trace its uses of that income:

```
National income                 = 1,000
   consumed (households + govt)  =   850
   saved                         =   150    (national saving = 15%)
Domestic investment             =   230    (factories, housing, kit)
----------------------------------------------------------
Saving - investment             =   150 - 230 = -80
Current-account balance         =   -80     (a deficit of 8% of GDP)
```

The country wants to invest \$230B but only saves \$150B of its own income. The missing **\$80B** must be borrowed from abroad — and that borrowing *is* the −\$80B current-account deficit, financed by an +\$80B financial-account surplus. Now suppose the government cuts its deficit and lifts national saving to \$230B with investment unchanged: the gap closes to zero and so does the current-account deficit, with no change in tariffs or trade deals at all. *The lesson is that the current account is decided in the savings–investment accounts, not at the customs house — a deficit is a country choosing to invest more than it saves, and only changing that choice changes the balance.*

This is also why the *quality* test from the decision graph maps onto the saving gap. A deficit that funds **investment** (the \$230B going into productive capital) is a country borrowing to build — the good deficit. A deficit driven by **low saving / high consumption** (the same gap, but because saving collapsed rather than investment rose) is a country borrowing to live beyond its means — the bad deficit. Same identity, opposite stories, and the savings–investment split is how you tell them apart.

## Current-account balances around the world

Look at where the major economies actually sit, because the spread is wide and it tells a story. The cover chart at the top of this post ranks the 2024 current-account balance as a share of GDP, and it sorts the world into two camps. On the surplus side: **Switzerland at +6.2%**, **Vietnam at +4.8%**, **Japan at +3.6%**, the **euro area at +2.9%**, **China at +1.4%**. On the deficit side: **Australia at −1.4%**, the **United Kingdom at −2.7%**, the **United States at −3.3%**.

Read these as *structural personalities*, not as scorecards where surplus is "winning". Switzerland and Japan are aging, high-saving creditor nations: they produce more than they consume and lend the excess to the world, which is exactly why the franc and the yen behave as **safe-haven currencies** that strengthen in a panic — in a crisis their residents repatriate the savings they parked abroad, and that homeward flow bids the currency up. The euro area's surplus is dominated by Germany's export machine. China runs a modest surplus, a shadow of its 10%-of-GDP peak in the 2000s. Vietnam's +4.8% is the high-growth-exporter signature we will dissect in detail.

On the deficit side, the United States is the special case that the whole system bends around. It has run a current-account deficit nearly every year for four decades and shows no sign of crisis, for one reason: the dollar is the world's reserve currency, so foreigners *want* to hold US assets almost without limit. The US gets to import capital on the easiest terms on earth — it is the deficit that never has to stop, because the demand for dollars is structurally bottomless. That privilege is its own subject, covered in [the dollar system](/blog/trading/macro-trading/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy) and the [petrodollar](/blog/trading/finance/petrodollar-and-dollar-dominance). For every *other* country, a −3.3% deficit would be a yellow flag; for the US it is just Tuesday. **The same number means different things depending on whether the world is forced to hold your money.**

The diverging shape of that chart — surpluses on the right, deficits on the left, summing across the whole world to roughly zero — is itself the deepest fact here. Global current-account balances must net to zero, because the world as a whole cannot run a deficit with itself. Every deficit country is borrowing from a surplus country. The surplus nations (Germany, Japan, China, the oil exporters, Switzerland) are the world's lenders; the deficit nations (the US, the UK, much of the developing world) are its borrowers. The balance of payments is, in the end, a map of who is saving and who is spending across the entire planet.

## The financing flip side: who funds whom

We have established that a current-account deficit is financed by a financial-account surplus. Now look at the *quality* of that financing, because it is the single best predictor of whether a deficit is a quiet structural feature or a coming crisis.

Stable financing means **FDI** and long-term debt: money that has committed to staying. Samsung's \$20B-plus of factories in Vietnam are not leaving on a bad week — they are bolted to the ground, and the jobs and exports they create improve the current account itself. Fragile financing means **short-term portfolio flows**: foreign holdings of local stocks and bonds that can be sold in an afternoon and wired home before the central bank wakes up. The 2013 "taper tantrum" — when the mere *hint* that the US Federal Reserve would slow its bond-buying sent capital fleeing emerging markets — is the textbook case. The countries that got hurt worst (the "Fragile Five": Brazil, India, Indonesia, South Africa, Turkey) shared one trait: large current-account deficits financed by hot money. When the tide of cheap dollars went out, their financing vanished and their currencies fell 15–25% in months. Countries with the same-sized deficits but FDI-heavy financing rode it out.

This is where the balance of payments connects to the [carry trade](/blog/trading/macro-trading/carry-trade-unwinds-1998-2008-2024-when-leverage-breaks). Hot money chasing a deficit country's high yields *is* the carry trade in macro form: foreign capital pours in to earn the spread, financing the deficit and propping up the currency on the way in — and then unwinds violently, pulling the currency down twice as fast, on the way out. A deficit financed by carry is a deficit financed by the most fickle money there is.

#### Worked example: a sudden stop in dollars

Picture an emerging economy with a current-account deficit of **−\$30B a year**, financed by **\$25B of foreign portfolio money** (bonds and stocks) and only **\$5B of FDI**. As long as foreigners keep rolling over their holdings, the financing arrives, the central bank holds the line, and the currency is stable. Now a risk-off shock hits — a US rate spike, say — and **\$20B of that portfolio money decides to leave within a quarter.**

```
Financing needed (deficit):       -$30B / yr  ->  -$7.5B this quarter
Portfolio money fleeing:          -$20B this quarter
Net dollar drain this quarter:    -$27.5B  must be found somewhere
Central bank FX reserves:          $40B
```

To stop the currency collapsing, the central bank sells reserves to meet the dollar demand — but at this burn rate, \$40B of reserves lasts barely a quarter and a half. The market can *see* the reserves draining, which only accelerates the flight (why hold an asset whose backstop is visibly running out?). The bank's choices narrow to two: spend the last reserves defending a level it cannot hold (the Black Wednesday path), or let the currency fall far and fast enough to halt the imports and tempt capital back at a cheaper price. *This is the sudden stop in miniature: the same deficit that was perfectly financeable on Monday becomes a full-blown crisis on Friday, not because the deficit changed but because the willingness to finance it did — which is why the composition of the financing, not the size of the deficit, is what keeps a currency analyst up at night.*

## Vietnam's balance of payments: the surplus that funds the dong

Now the worked case the whole post has been building toward, because Vietnam is a near-perfect illustration of how the balance of payments determines a currency's path. The State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) runs the dong as a **managed crawl**, not a free float — it lets the currency depreciate slowly and one-directionally inside a band, rather than letting the market push it around freely (the mechanics of that regime are in [Vietnam's monetary policy](/blog/trading/finance/vietnam-monetary-policy-state-bank-dong-credit-ceiling)). The question is: *what allows the SBV to run such a gentle crawl instead of suffering periodic devaluations?* The answer is its balance of payments, and specifically three steady streams of dollars.

The first and biggest is the **goods trade surplus**. Vietnam has transformed into an export manufacturing hub — phones, electronics, textiles, furniture — and since the late 2010s it has run a persistent surplus in goods. In 2024 that surplus was **+\$24.8B**, near a record. Every one of those dollars is a dollar the country *earned* from the world that does not have to be borrowed.

![Bar chart of Vietnam's goods trade surplus in billions of dollars by year from 2019 to 2024, showing a persistent and widening surplus](/imgs/blogs/the-balance-of-payments-and-the-current-account-4.png)

Look at the trajectory: the surplus was a modest \$10.9B in 2019, dipped during the COVID supply shocks, then surged to \$28.0B in 2023 and \$24.8B in 2024 as Vietnam captured manufacturing diverted from China. A widening goods surplus is a *structural inflow of dollars*, year after year, and it is the bedrock of the dong's stability. Without it, the SBV would be defending the currency with reserves alone — a losing game, as we just saw.

But the goods surplus is not the whole current account. Vietnam runs a *services and primary-income deficit* that partly offsets it: it imports shipping and insurance services, pays tourism outflows, and — critically — the foreign-owned factories that drive the goods surplus repatriate their profits as a primary-income *outflow*. That is the catch in Vietnam's model: much of the export machine is FDI-owned, so a chunk of the earnings flows back out as foreign profits. This is why Vietnam's *overall* current-account surplus (+4.8% of GDP) is smaller than its eye-popping goods surplus would suggest. The goods number is the gross inflow; the current account is the net after services and income drains.

#### Worked example: building Vietnam's current account from the parts

Assemble a stylised Vietnam current account for a recent year, in the four standard buckets, to see how the parts net out:

```
Goods trade balance         = +24.8   (the export-manufacturing surplus)
Services balance            =  -12.0   (imported shipping, insurance, tourism)
Primary income              =  -18.0   (foreign factories repatriate profits)
Secondary income (transfers)= +16.0   (remittances from workers abroad)
----------------------------------------------------------------
Current-account balance     =  +10.8   (a surplus, ~2-3% of GDP)
```

(These are illustrative magnitudes consistent with Vietnam's published balances, not the exact official line items, which the SBV reports with a lag.) Notice what happens: the giant +\$24.8B goods surplus is more than halved by the services and primary-income drains, and then *rescued* by the **+\$16B of remittances**. Strip out remittances and Vietnam's current account would be barely positive or even slip into deficit. *The lesson is that remittances are not a charity footnote — they are a load-bearing pillar of Vietnam's external accounts, the transfer inflow that turns a leaky goods surplus into a comfortable current-account surplus and gives the dong its cushion.* The full mechanics of how remittances and FDI fund the dong get their own deep dive in [remittances, FDI, and the current account that funds the dong](/blog/trading/forex/remittances-fdi-and-the-current-account-that-funds-the-dong).

The second stream is exactly that — **remittances**. Vietnamese workers abroad send home roughly **\$16–19B a year**, and this lands in the current account as secondary income (a transfer with nothing given in return). It is remarkably stable: it does not collapse in a recession the way exports might, because the diaspora keeps supporting families back home regardless of the cycle. The third stream is **FDI disbursement** — the actual dollars foreign firms bring in to build and run their Vietnamese operations — which hit **\$25.4B in 2024**, a record. FDI lands in the *financial account*, not the current account, but for the SBV's purposes it is the same thing: a fat, steady stream of dollars arriving at the door, demanding to be converted into dong to pay for land, wages, and construction.

![Grouped bar chart of Vietnam's annual remittances and disbursed FDI in billions of dollars from 2018 to 2024, showing two large and steady dollar inflows](/imgs/blogs/the-balance-of-payments-and-the-current-account-6.png)

Stack the three streams together and you can see the whole engine. The goods surplus, the remittances, and the FDI are three large, mostly steady dollar inflows. Against them sit the services and income outflows. The *net* is a country that, year after year, takes in more dollars than it sends out — which means the SBV is usually a *buyer* of dollars (to stop the dong rising too fast) and can bank the excess as **foreign-exchange reserves**, the war chest it uses to smooth the crawl when flows wobble.

![Stack diagram showing Vietnam's trade surplus, remittances, and FDI as dollar inflows, offset by a services and income drag, netting to dollars that build reserves](/imgs/blogs/the-balance-of-payments-and-the-current-account-7.png)

#### Worked example: why the SBV can crawl instead of devalue

Put concrete numbers to the cushion. Suppose in a given year Vietnam's external accounts deliver, on net:

```
Goods surplus:               +$24.8B
Remittances:                 +$16.0B
FDI disbursed:               +$25.4B
   gross steady inflows:     +$66.2B
Services + income outflow:   -$30.0B   (shipping, tourism, repatriated profits)
Net dollar inflow:           +$36.2B
```

A net **+\$36B** of dollars arriving each year is enormous relative to the size of Vietnam's FX market. Left alone, that flood of dollars would push the dong *stronger* (more dollars chasing dong bids the dong up). The SBV does not want a strong dong — it would hurt the export machine — so it leans against it: it buys the surplus dollars, adding them to reserves, and lets the dong crawl gently *weaker* in a controlled way to keep exporters competitive and offset the inflation gap with the US. *The takeaway is that a managed crawl is only possible when the balance of payments runs a surplus: the SBV crawls the dong from a position of dollar abundance, choosing the pace, rather than being forced into a devaluation by a dollar shortage — the polar opposite of the Black Wednesday position, where a deficit country burned reserves it did not have.*

You can see the result in the dong's actual path. Despite all those inflows, the dong has *not* strengthened — it has crawled steadily weaker, from about 21,340 per dollar in 2014 to roughly 26,300 by 2025. The surplus does not reverse the depreciation; it *moderates and controls* it. The inflows give the SBV room to manage a gentle, predictable decline (driven mostly by the inflation and interest-rate gap with the US — the rate-gap force from our spine) rather than suffering the abrupt, panicky devaluations that hit deficit-running emerging markets.

![Line chart of USD/VND year-end levels from 2014 to 2025 showing the dong on a steady managed crawl weaker, from about 21,340 to about 26,300 per dollar](/imgs/blogs/the-balance-of-payments-and-the-current-account-8.png)

That chart is the balance of payments made visible. A deficit-running EM with hot-money financing would show a jagged line full of cliffs (a 30% drop here, a peg break there). Vietnam shows a smooth, gentle slope — the signature of a country whose external accounts are in surplus and whose financing is dominated by sticky FDI rather than fickle portfolio flows. The dong is not strong, but it is *managed*, and what makes it manageable is the balance of payments underneath.

It is worth being honest about where the cracks could appear, because no external position is bulletproof. Vietnam's reserve cushion is *thin*: roughly \$82B in 2024, which covers under three months of imports — below the conventional three-month adequacy rule and well under the reserves of regional peers like Thailand (eight months) or India (eleven). That thinness showed in 2022, when a strong dollar and capital pressure forced the SBV to spend reserves defending the dong — they fell from about \$109B in 2021 to \$87B in 2022, a hard, fast drawdown that was a genuine warning shot. The lesson is that even a surplus country can be squeezed if the cushion is thin and the global dollar tide turns: the balance-of-payments surplus is the dong's first line of defence, but the reserves are the second, and Vietnam's second line is shorter than it looks. A trader reading the dong watches both — the surplus flows *and* the months-of-imports cover — because a surplus that arrives steadily is comforting only as long as nothing forces the SBV to bridge a sudden gap from a shallow reserve pool.

A further subtlety: not all of Vietnam's inflows are equally sticky. The FDI is the stickiest — a chip-assembly plant does not pack up and leave on a bad week. Remittances are remarkably stable across the cycle, as we noted, because diaspora support to families does not track the business cycle. The goods surplus is more variable: it swells when global demand for electronics and textiles is strong and shrinks when Western consumers pull back, so it carries the cyclicality of Vietnam's export markets. And the portfolio flows into Vietnamese stocks and bonds — the smallest slice — are the fickle part, the bit that can reverse on a global risk-off move (the dynamics of which the [foreign-flows case study](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/foreign-flows-etfs-and-the-index-effect-vietnam) traces in detail). The reason Vietnam's currency path is smooth rather than jagged is precisely that its inflow *mix* is weighted toward the sticky end — FDI, remittances, and an earned trade surplus — rather than the fickle end. Composition, again, is destiny.

## Common misconceptions

The balance of payments generates more confident nonsense than almost any topic in economics. Here are the five myths worth dismantling, each with a number.

**Myth 1: "A trade deficit means the country is being ripped off / losing money."** No. A deficit is a swap: the country gets goods and services *now* in exchange for handing foreigners claims on its future (IOUs and ownership). The US ran a −3.3%-of-GDP deficit in 2024 and got \$800B-ish of real stuff in exchange for \$800B-ish of paper claims that mostly pay 4%. Whether that is a good deal depends entirely on what it did with the imported capital, not on the sign of the balance. A deficit is a financing fact, not a fleecing.

**Myth 2: "A surplus is always good and a deficit is always bad."** No. A surplus means a country consumes *less* than it produces and lends the rest abroad — it is a virtue only if the country has nothing better to do with the savings at home. Japan's chronic surplus reflects an aging, under-investing society as much as a competitive one. Meanwhile a young, fast-growing country *should* run a deficit, importing the world's capital to build out faster than its own savings allow — that is what Vietnam's neighbors did during their high-growth decades. The sign of the balance tells you nothing about welfare on its own.

**Myth 3: "We have a trade deficit with China, so China owes us / we owe China."** This confuses bilateral flow with total stock and total balance. Bilateral deficits are meaningless in isolation — you run a "deficit" with your supermarket and a "surplus" with your employer, and nobody is being exploited. What matters is the *aggregate* current account against the *whole world*, and the *stock* of net claims (the NIIP) — not the score against any one partner. A −\$300B bilateral goods gap with one country can sit inside a perfectly healthy overall position.

**Myth 4: "The current account and the capital account are independent policy levers."** No — they are two sides of one identity, and you cannot move one without the other moving in lockstep. A government cannot "fix the trade deficit" without changing the savings-investment balance that drives it. If a country invests more than it saves, it *must* run a current-account deficit and import the difference, by accounting law. Tariffs that shrink imports without changing national saving just shift the deficit around (a stronger currency, or different trade partners) rather than closing it. The deficit lives in the gap between what a country saves and what it invests, and only that gap closing will close it.

**Myth 5: "Foreign-exchange reserves are the country's savings / wealth."** Reserves are the central bank's *liquidity buffer* — its ability to meet dollar demand and smooth the currency, measured against the country's import bill and short-term external debt. Vietnam's roughly \$82B of reserves in 2024 covered under three months of imports — below the conventional adequacy threshold — even though the country runs a healthy current-account surplus. Reserves are a flow-management tool, not a measure of national wealth, and a surplus country can still have a thin reserve cushion if it has not banked enough of its inflows.

**Myth 6: "A country that wants its currency stronger should just run a bigger surplus."** This sounds intuitive and is mostly backwards as a policy lever. A current-account surplus does put upward pressure on a currency, but a surplus is not something a government dials up at will — it is the savings–investment gap expressing itself, plus the cumulative competitiveness of the export base, neither of which moves quickly. Worse, many surplus countries actively *resist* the currency strength their surplus would otherwise create: Vietnam's SBV buys the surplus dollars and crawls the dong *weaker* precisely to protect the exporters who generate the surplus; China spent the 2000s recycling its enormous surplus into US Treasuries to keep the yuan from appreciating. A surplus is a source of currency strength only if the central bank *lets* it be — and export-led economies usually do the opposite, banking the inflow as reserves rather than allowing the currency to rise and erode their edge. The surplus is real; whether it shows up in the exchange rate is a policy choice.

## How it shows up in real markets

Theory is only useful if it changes how you read a screen. Here is how the balance of payments shows up in the day-to-day life of a currency, with real episodes.

**The slow gravity on a currency.** Over multi-year horizons, persistent current-account deficits are a steady downward pull and persistent surpluses an upward pull — this is one of the few things long-run FX valuation models (alongside [purchasing power parity](/blog/trading/forex/purchasing-power-parity-and-the-real-exchange-rate)) actually get right. The British pound has drifted structurally weaker across decades partly because the UK has run a near-permanent current-account deficit and de-industrialised its export base. The Swiss franc and Japanese yen have a structural bid because both countries are chronic surplus creditors whose residents hold vast foreign assets. You will not trade a single day on the current account — but you ignore it at your peril when forming a *view* over months and years.

**The safe-haven mechanism.** Why does the yen *strengthen* in a global panic, when you would expect a slowing-export nation's currency to fall? Because Japan is a giant net creditor: in a crisis, Japanese investors and firms repatriate the savings they have parked abroad, selling foreign assets and buying yen to bring the money home. That repatriation flow — a financial-account reversal driven by the country's creditor status — bids the yen up exactly when everything else is falling. The current-account surplus, accumulated over decades into a vast positive NIIP, is *why* the yen behaves as a haven. The same logic makes the franc a haven and is one reason it took a [central-bank shock to break the franc's floor](/blog/trading/finance/asian-financial-crisis-1997) rather than market forces. The creditor/debtor status that the balance of payments tracks is the deep cause of which currencies rally in a panic and which collapse.

**The sudden stop and the crisis.** The acute danger is always the financing side reversing. The 1997 Asian crisis, the 2013 taper tantrum, the 2018 Argentina and Turkey blow-ups — all the same script: a country running a current-account deficit financed by hot foreign capital, a shift in global risk appetite, capital flight, reserves draining, and a currency in free fall once the central bank can no longer plug the gap. A trader watching an EM currency reads the balance of payments as a *fragility gauge*: how big is the deficit, how much of the financing is fickle portfolio money versus sticky FDI, and how many months of import cover do the reserves provide? A deficit of −1% financed by FDI with eight months of reserves is calm; a deficit of −6% financed by carry money with two months of reserves is a powder keg. The number that matters most is rarely the deficit itself — it is the *composition and the cushion*.

**The terms-of-trade swing.** For commodity exporters, the current account swings with export prices, and the currency rides along. When iron ore booms, Australia's trade surplus swells, its current account improves, and the Australian dollar tends to strengthen — the [terms of trade](/blog/trading/forex/terms-of-trade-and-the-commodity-currencies) feeding straight through the balance of payments into the exchange rate. When the commodity rolls over, the flow reverses. The balance of payments is the channel through which a country's export prices become its currency's level.

**The J-curve and why a weaker currency does not fix a deficit overnight.** Here is a subtlety that catches out even professionals. The textbook cure for a current-account deficit is a weaker currency: a cheaper currency makes exports more competitive and imports dearer, so the trade balance should improve. But it does not happen *immediately* — in fact, right after a depreciation the deficit usually gets *worse* before it gets better, tracing a shape economists call the **J-curve**. The reason is timing. In the short run, the *volumes* of exports and imports are locked in by contracts already signed — you still import the same quantity of oil and parts you committed to last quarter — but their *prices* in your own currency just jumped because the currency fell. So your import bill rises in domestic-currency terms while export volumes have not yet had time to grow. The deficit widens. Only over the following six to eighteen months, as buyers respond to the new prices — foreigners buying more of your now-cheaper exports, locals substituting away from now-expensive imports — do the volumes adjust and the balance improve. A trader who shorts a currency expecting its weakness to "fix" the deficit and snap it back must survive the J-curve's initial dip first. The adjustment is real, but it runs on the timescale of trade contracts, not trading screens.

**The flow-versus-stock trap in the news.** When you read "the trade deficit hit a record," ask the second-half question the headline omits: *and who financed it, on what terms?* A record deficit financed by eager foreign buyers of your bonds at low yields is a sign of *strength* (the world wants your assets); a record deficit financed only by a falling currency and draining reserves is a sign of *stress*. The same flow number means opposite things depending on the financing. Train yourself to never read a current-account number without immediately asking for its mirror image in the financial account — because by the identity, the mirror image is always there.

## The takeaway: read the tide, not just the waves

Here is what the balance of payments gives you that nothing else does: the *deep current* under a currency. Interest-rate differentials — the other half of our series' spine — drive the day-to-day and the cycle-to-cycle moves, the waves you can see. The balance of payments is the tide: slower, harder to spot in any single session, but it sets the level the waves break on. A currency with a structural surplus and a creditor's balance sheet has a floor under it that no amount of bad news fully removes; a currency with a chronic deficit financed by hot money has a trapdoor under it that no amount of good news fully closes.

So when you size up a currency, run the balance-of-payments checklist before you touch the charts. *What is the current-account balance, in percent of GDP?* A surplus is a tailwind, a deficit a headwind. *If it is a deficit, what is the capital buying and who is funding it?* Investment funded by FDI is benign; consumption funded by carry money is a fault line. *How thick is the reserve cushion, in months of imports?* Under three months is thin. *Is the country a net creditor or debtor to the world — what is the NIIP?* The creditors strengthen in a panic; the debtors crater. Answer those four questions — the balance, the composition, the cushion, and the creditor status — and you know whether the currency in front of you has a tide pushing it up or pulling it down, before you have looked at a single interest-rate print or drawn a single trendline.

This connects all the way back to the spine. You never own a currency in isolation — you own a *claim on a country's external position*. A long dong position is, underneath, a bet that Vietnam's trade surplus, remittances, and FDI keep arriving faster than its services and income outflows drain away, so the SBV can keep crawling rather than being forced to devalue. A short pound is, underneath, a bet that the UK's chronic deficit and thin export base keep the structural gravity working. The exchange rate is the price that clears a country's flows with the world, and the balance of payments is the ledger of those flows. Learn to read it, and you are reading the one force that decides, over years, which way a currency truly wants to go.
