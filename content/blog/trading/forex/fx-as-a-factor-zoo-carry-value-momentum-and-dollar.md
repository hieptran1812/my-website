> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Currency returns are not one undifferentiated blob of noise; they cluster into a few repeatable *styles* — carry, value, momentum, and the dollar — that a systematic fund harvests as long-short baskets rather than single bets.
>
> - A **factor** is a rule that sorts every currency, goes long the top, shorts the bottom, and earns the spread between them — so it is dollar-neutral and lives or dies on the *ranking*, not the market's direction.
> - The four big FX factors are **carry** (long high-rate, short low-rate), **value** (long cheap-vs-PPP, short expensive), **momentum/trend** (long what is rising, short what is falling), and **dollar** (long or short the whole world against USD when the dollar trends).
> - Each factor's standalone Sharpe ratio is modest — roughly 0.55 for carry, 0.45 for momentum, 0.35 for value, 0.30 for the dollar — but they *crash at different times*, so a blend can lift the combined Sharpe well above any single leg.
> - The one number to remember: **carry crashed about −12% in the August-2024 yen unwind in a matter of days — and trend, already short the yen-funded longs, was the leg that cushioned the blend.**

On the morning of August 5, 2024, every trader running a currency carry book woke up to the same red screen. The trade that had paid like clockwork for two years — borrow yen at almost zero, park the proceeds in higher-yielding currencies, pocket the gap — went into reverse all at once. The Bank of Japan had nudged rates up and signalled more to come; a soft US jobs print landed on top of it; and the yen, which had been the cheap fuel of the whole carry complex, ripped from about 162 per dollar to roughly 142 in a handful of sessions. The VIX, Wall Street's fear gauge, briefly printed in the mid-60s — a level normally reserved for genuine financial crises. A stylised G10 carry index gave back something like 12% in days.

Here is the part that separates a gambler from a systematic shop. The funds that blew up were the ones who thought they had *one* trade: long carry, leveraged, and nothing else. The funds that survived — and some that even made money that week — were running a *portfolio of styles*. Their carry leg bled, yes. But their trend leg had been short the yen-funded longs for weeks because the trend was clearly down. Their value leg had flagged half those high-yielders as expensive months earlier. The losses on one style were partly paid for by gains on another. That is not luck. That is the whole reason professionals stopped thinking of FX as a collection of currency calls and started thinking of it as a *factor zoo*.

This post is the capstone of the carry track, and it widens the lens. Carry, which we have treated as a single trade, turns out to be one species in a small zoo of FX styles — systematic, rule-based bets that have paid a premium across decades and dozens of currencies. We will define each one, show exactly how a desk *builds* it as a long-short basket, look at its historical Sharpe ratio, and — the punchline — see how they diversify each other so that a blend is sturdier than any single factor alone.

![The four FX style factors as a grid of signal and construction](/imgs/blogs/fx-as-a-factor-zoo-carry-value-momentum-and-dollar-1.png)

The spine of this whole series holds here too. You never own "a currency" in isolation; every position is a *pair*, a relative bet between two monies. A factor just makes that explicit and industrial: it ranks every currency against the others, buys the top of the ranking, sells the bottom, and collects the spread. What changes from factor to factor is only the *ranking rule* — the rate gap, the valuation gap, the recent trend, or the dollar's own direction.

## Foundations: FX returns as systematic factors

Before we meet the four factors, we need three building blocks: what a *factor* is, why we build it *long-short*, and how we *measure* whether it actually works. Get these right and the rest of the post is just four worked examples of the same machine.

### What is a factor?

Start with everyday money. Suppose a friend tells you a rule: "every month, rent out your spare room to the tenant willing to pay the most, and evict the one paying the least." You are not betting that rents in general go up. You are betting that *your ranking* — highest-payer versus lowest-payer — keeps earning you a spread, month after month, regardless of where the overall rental market goes. That spread is your *factor return*. The rule is your *factor*.

A **factor** in markets is exactly that: a *rule that ranks every asset in a universe by some characteristic, then earns the return difference between the top and the bottom of the ranking.* The characteristic might be the interest rate a currency pays (that is carry), how cheap it looks versus a fair-value anchor (value), how strongly it has been trending (momentum), or whether the dollar as a whole is rising or falling (the dollar factor). The factor is the *ranking rule plus the long-short trade built on top of it.*

Why does this matter? Because once you express a view as a factor, you stop caring about the *level* of the market and start caring only about the *cross-section* — the spread between the best-ranked and worst-ranked currencies. A factor is, by construction, a relative-value engine. That is the series spine made mechanical.

The word "factor" comes from the academic finance literature, where it has a precise meaning: a factor is a *common source of return* that explains why a whole group of assets move together. In equities, the famous factors are value (cheap stocks beat expensive ones), size (small companies beat large), momentum (winners keep winning), and quality (profitable, stable firms beat junky ones). The discovery that drove a generation of systematic investing was that these same patterns — value, momentum, and a carry-like "high-income" tilt — show up in *every* asset class, including currencies, bonds, and commodities. FX is, if anything, the cleanest laboratory for them, because the universe is small (a few dozen liquid currencies), the instruments are cheap to trade, and the rate-gap signal that drives carry is observable in real time. So when a quant says "the FX factor zoo," they mean the currency cousins of the same value/momentum/carry patterns that pervade markets. The "zoo" label is a wry nod to the fact that academics have catalogued *hundreds* of candidate factors; in FX, only a handful — the four in this post — survive honest, out-of-sample scrutiny with real trading costs.

One more piece of vocabulary that will save confusion later. The two ends of the ranking have names: the **long leg** is the basket of top-ranked currencies you buy, and the **short leg** is the basket of bottom-ranked currencies you sell. The return of the factor is the long leg's return *minus* the short leg's return — the spread. When people say "the carry factor returned 4% this year," they mean the long-high-yield basket beat the short-low-yield basket by 4 percentage points, regardless of what the dollar or the broad market did. That subtraction is the whole trick: it cancels the common move and leaves the pure style.

#### Worked example: a factor return is a spread, not a level

Suppose at the start of a month the highest-yielding currency in your G10 universe pays 5.0% a year and the lowest pays 0.5%. You build a carry factor: go long \$10,000,000 of the high-yielder, short \$10,000,000 of the low-yielder. Your *net* market exposure is zero dollars — you are long as much as you are short. Over the month, the carry you earn is the rate gap: (5.0% − 0.5%) × (1/12) × \$10,000,000 = 4.5% × 0.0833 × \$10,000,000 ≈ \$37,500 before any move in the exchange rates themselves. If *both* currencies happen to weaken 2% against everything else that month, it does not touch your P&L — you are long one and short the other, so the common move cancels. *A factor pays you for the spread it ranks on and is deliberately blind to the level of the market.*

### Why long-short? The dollar-neutral basket

The phrase "go long the top, short the bottom" is doing heavy lifting, so let us be precise. A factor desk does not buy one currency and sell one currency. It typically sorts the whole universe — say the ten liquid G10 currencies, or thirty including emerging markets — into the ranking, then goes **long a basket of the top names** (often the top third) and **short a basket of the bottom names** (the bottom third), sizing the two legs so the *net dollar exposure is zero*. The whole construction is sometimes called a **long-short, dollar-neutral basket**.

![Long-short basket construction from a ranking to a dollar-neutral spread](/imgs/blogs/fx-as-a-factor-zoo-carry-value-momentum-and-dollar-2.png)

Three things fall out of building it this way, and each is the reason factor desks exist:

- **It isolates the *style*, not the *market*.** Because the long and short legs are equal in size, a broad move that lifts or sinks all currencies against the dollar washes out. What is left is purely the spread the factor ranks on. You have surgically extracted "the carry premium" or "the value premium" from everything else going on.
- **It is repeatable across dozens of names.** A single pair trade lives and dies on idiosyncratic news. A basket of five longs and five shorts diversifies the idiosyncratic noise away and leaves the systematic tilt. The law of large numbers is quietly working for you.
- **It is fundable and scalable.** A dollar-neutral book needs far less capital than an outright directional position of the same gross size, because the long leg's financing is partly met by the short leg's proceeds. This is why the carry trade is so often described as "borrow low, lend high" — the funding leg *is* the short leg of the basket.

The cost of this elegance: a factor is not riskless. The longs and shorts are correlated in stress (in a panic, the high-yielders you are long *and* the low-yielders you are short can both move violently in the same direction relative to safe havens), and that is exactly when factor crashes happen. We will return to this — it is the entire reason diversification across factors is the real prize.

There is also a practical question that separates a textbook factor from a tradable one: *how do you build the legs in practice?* You do not literally take out a yen loan and wire the proceeds to a Brazilian bank. In real FX, every leg is expressed through **forwards and FX swaps** — the instruments covered in the [spot, forward, and swap](/blog/trading/forex/spot-forward-and-swap-the-three-ways-to-trade-a-currency) post. A long-carry position is simply *buying the high-yielder forward and selling the funder forward*, and the forward price already bakes in the interest-rate gap through covered interest parity. That is why a factor desk can run a large book with relatively little cash: forwards are leveraged instruments that require only margin, not the full notional. It also means the "carry" you earn shows up mechanically as the *roll* of the forward toward spot, not as an interest coupon hitting your account. The economics are identical; the plumbing is forwards. Keep that in mind whenever this post says "go long" a currency — it means a forward or swap position, sized so the basket nets to zero dollars of market exposure.

### How do we measure a factor? The Sharpe ratio

To compare factors, we need one number that captures "how much return per unit of risk." That number is the **Sharpe ratio** — the average excess return of a strategy divided by the standard deviation (the volatility) of those returns. A Sharpe of 1.0 means you earn one unit of return for each unit of risk you take; 0.5 means half a unit. Higher is better. Crucially, the Sharpe ratio is what improves when you *diversify*, because adding an uncorrelated return stream raises the numerator (return) faster than the denominator (risk).

A reasonable mental anchor: a single long-only equity index has run a Sharpe of roughly 0.4–0.5 over the very long run. Individual FX factors are in a similar, modest range. None of them is a money machine on its own. The magic is in the blend, which we build toward across the rest of the post.

It is worth being honest about what the Sharpe ratio *hides*, because that omission is the central tension of this whole post. The Sharpe ratio treats all volatility as equal — an upside surprise counts as "risk" just as much as a downside one — and it assumes returns are roughly bell-shaped. Carry returns are emphatically *not* bell-shaped: they have a long, fat *left* tail (the crashes) and a thin right tail (no equivalent windfalls). So carry's Sharpe of 0.55 *overstates* how attractive it really is, because the number averages away the fact that the bad days are catastrophically bad and the good days are merely fine. A more skew-aware investor looks past the Sharpe to the *maximum drawdown* (how much the strategy lost peak-to-trough in its worst episode) and the *skewness* of returns. By those measures, carry looks worse and value looks better than their Sharpes suggest. The reason we still lead with the Sharpe is that it is the one number that improves *cleanly* with diversification — and diversification is the lesson. Just keep in the back of your mind that a high-Sharpe, negative-skew factor like carry is a wolf in sheep's clothing: the headline number flatters it, and only the tail tells the truth.

![Stylised Sharpe ratios of the four FX factors over 2000 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/fx-as-a-factor-zoo-carry-value-momentum-and-dollar-3.png)

The chart above is the headline of the whole post. Over a long sample (roughly 2000–2024), the four FX factors have run stylised Sharpe ratios of about **0.55 for carry, 0.45 for momentum/trend, 0.35 for value, and 0.30 for the dollar**. Two takeaways jump out. First, *every one of them is modest* — there is no free lunch, and a Sharpe near 0.5 means plenty of losing years. Second, *they are all in the same ballpark*, which is precisely what makes a blend attractive: when you combine return streams of similar quality that do not move together, the combined Sharpe can exceed any individual leg. Hold that thought; it is the destination.

A note on positioning before we go deep. This series does not re-derive interest rates, the yield curve, or monetary-policy transmission — those live in the [fixed-income](/blog/trading/fixed-income/the-yield-curve-explained-the-most-important-chart-in-finance) and [macro-trading](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-monetary-policy-moves-currencies-rate-differentials) tracks, and we link out rather than repeat them. Here we take the rate gap as given and ask: *how do you turn it, and three other signals, into a portfolio of systematic FX bets?*

## Carry: getting paid the rate gap

Carry is the oldest and most studied FX factor, and we have spent a whole track on it, so here we treat it as one species in the zoo and connect it to the others.

The ranking rule is the simplest imaginable: **sort currencies by their short-term interest rate.** Go long the high-rate currencies, short the low-rate ones. The factor return is, to first order, the *interest-rate differential* you collect — the gap between what your long leg pays and what your short leg costs — plus or minus whatever the exchange rates do. The whole bet is a wager that exchange rates do *not* move enough to erase the rate gap, which is precisely the empirical failure of [uncovered interest parity](/blog/trading/forex/uncovered-interest-parity-and-why-it-fails-the-forward-puzzle) (UIP) — the textbook prediction that high-rate currencies should depreciate by exactly their rate advantage. They don't, on average. Carry harvests that gap.

![A stylised G10 carry index total return from 2007 to 2025](/imgs/blogs/fx-as-a-factor-zoo-carry-value-momentum-and-dollar-4.png)

Look at the shape of that index. For long stretches it is a calm, upward grind — the carry premium dripping in month after month. Then, abruptly, it falls off a cliff: 2008, 2011, 2015, and again in August 2024. This is the defining signature of carry: **it earns a small premium most of the time and gives a chunk of it back violently, rarely.** Statisticians call this *negative skew* — the distribution of returns has a long, fat *left* tail. You are, in effect, being paid an insurance premium for selling crash insurance. Most months you collect; occasionally you pay a large claim.

This shape is *why* carry has a positive long-run Sharpe yet terrifies risk managers. The Sharpe ratio measures return per unit of *average* volatility, but it does not capture the skew. Two strategies with the same Sharpe can have wildly different tail behaviour — and carry's tail is its worst feature. It is also the single most important reason to hold carry *inside a blend* rather than alone: the other factors tend to do well exactly when carry is bleeding.

There is a deeper reason carry's premium survives even though everyone knows about it. The factor is, in economic terms, a *risk premium* — you are being compensated for taking on a specific danger that other investors want to offload. Which danger? The danger that, in a global panic, the high-yielding currencies you are long will collapse precisely when an investor most needs cash and stability. High-yielding currencies are the ones the world is least willing to hold in a crisis; low-yielding "funder" currencies like the yen and the Swiss franc are the ones it flees *into*. So carry pays a premium in calm times as compensation for absorbing crash risk in bad times. That is not a market inefficiency that arbitrage will erase — it is a genuine transfer of risk, and the premium persists because the risk is real. Anyone who pockets the carry premium without respecting the crash is, in effect, selling earthquake insurance and spending the premiums as if earthquakes never happen.

A second structural point: carry's crashes are *correlated with each other and with everything risky*. The 1998 LTCM/Russia unwind cost the carry trade roughly 28% peak-to-trough; the 2008 global financial crisis took about 30%; the 2011 eurozone scare about 10%; the 2015 Swiss-franc shock about 8%; and August 2024 about 12%. Notice that these are the same dates equities crashed, credit spreads blew out, and volatility spiked. Carry is, in the language of portfolio construction, a *high-beta-to-risk* strategy dressed up as a steady income stream. That correlation is why carry alone is so dangerous in a multi-asset portfolio — it tends to lose money at the exact moment your stocks, your credit, and your other risk assets are also losing money. The factor zoo's answer is to pair carry with factors whose crashes land on *different* dates, and the dollar and trend factors do exactly that.

#### Worked example: the carry P&L on a \$10,000,000 high-yield basket

Suppose your carry basket is long \$10,000,000 of a high-yielder paying 7.0% and short \$10,000,000 of a funder paying 0.5%. Over a year, if exchange rates *do not move*, you earn the gross rate gap: (7.0% − 0.5%) × \$10,000,000 = 6.5% × \$10,000,000 = \$650,000. Now suppose a carry crash hits and the high-yielder falls 10% against the funder over a single bad week. The currency loss is 10% × \$10,000,000 = \$1,000,000 — which wipes out a year and a half of carry in five days. Your net for the year is \$650,000 − \$1,000,000 = −\$350,000. *Carry pays you a steady drip and bills you a lump sum; the only defence is to size it small and pair it with factors that profit from the same crash.*

That worked example is the heart of the carry track. For the borrow-low-lend-high mechanics from scratch, see the dedicated post on [the carry trade](/blog/trading/forex/the-carry-trade-getting-paid-to-hold-a-currency); for which currencies sit on each leg — the [funding currencies versus the high-yielders](/blog/trading/forex/funding-currencies-vs-high-yielders-jpy-chf-and-the-rest) — see its companion. Here, the point is structural: carry is *one* factor, with a known signature, that belongs in a diversified book.

## Value: betting on PPP reversion

If carry is "buy what pays the most," value is its opposite instinct: **buy what is cheapest, sell what is most expensive** — where "cheap" and "expensive" are measured against a fair-value anchor. In FX, that anchor is **purchasing power parity** (PPP): the idea that, in the very long run, a currency should buy roughly the same basket of goods everywhere, so a currency that is *undervalued* on PPP should tend to *appreciate* back toward fair value, and an *overvalued* one should drift down.

The ranking rule: estimate each currency's PPP fair value, compute the gap between the market rate and fair value (the *real exchange rate* misalignment), then go long the most undervalued currencies and short the most overvalued. The factor return is the slow pull of currencies back toward their PPP anchors.

![Big Mac index showing currencies cheap or dear versus the dollar in 2025](/imgs/blogs/fx-as-a-factor-zoo-carry-value-momentum-and-dollar-5.png)

The Big Mac index above is the friendliest possible PPP gauge: it compares the local price of an identical burger across countries to ask whether each currency is cheap or dear versus the dollar. In early 2025 the Swiss franc screened roughly 38% *overvalued* and the Norwegian krone about 20% over — those would be *short* candidates for a value basket. The Japanese yen screened about 41% *undervalued*, and a swathe of Asian currencies (the Indian rupee, Indonesian rupiah, Vietnamese dong) sat 45–52% cheap — those are *long* candidates. The factor goes long the cheap left side, short the dear right side, and waits for the gap to close. The full mechanics of PPP and the real exchange rate live in the [PPP and the real exchange rate](/blog/trading/forex/purchasing-power-parity-and-the-real-exchange-rate) post; here we only need the ranking.

The critical caveat — and it is enormous — is the *horizon*. PPP is a decades-long anchor, not a months-long signal. A currency can stay 40% cheap for years; the yen sat far below its OECD-PPP fair value for the entire 2022–2024 stretch even as it got *cheaper*. Value's Sharpe is the lowest of the four factors (around 0.35) precisely because it is *slow and noisy*: it is right eventually, but "eventually" can be a career. What value gives up in speed it pays back in *timing*: it tends to do well exactly when carry crashes, because crashes often slam the overvalued high-yielders that value was already short.

Practitioners refine the raw Big Mac signal in two ways, both worth knowing. First, the Big Mac index is a *crude* PPP gauge — a burger is not a representative basket, it is not traded across borders, and its price embeds local rents, wages, and taxes that have nothing to do with the exchange rate. Serious desks use the **real effective exchange rate** (REER) instead: a trade-weighted, inflation-adjusted measure of a currency's value against all its partners, which strips out the noise the Big Mac leaves in. The logic is the same — buy the cheap, sell the dear — but the signal is cleaner. Second, value is usually measured *relative to a currency's own history*, not against an absolute fair value. A currency that is two standard deviations below its ten-year average REER is "cheap" even if no burger-PPP model agrees, because the comparison is to its *own* normal. This sidesteps the awkward fact that no one truly knows a currency's "right" level — but everyone can measure how far it has strayed from its own recent range.

Why does the value premium exist at all? The honest answer is *overshooting*. Exchange rates, driven by capital flows and rate differentials, routinely push past their fundamental anchors and then take years to mean-revert — the classic Dornbusch overshooting story, developed in the [capital flows and the Dornbusch overshoot](/blog/trading/forex/capital-flows-and-the-dornbusch-overshoot) post. A currency with high rates attracts capital, appreciates *beyond* fair value, and eventually has to give it back when the rate advantage fades. Value harvests that giveback. The catch — and the reason value is so painful to hold — is that the overshoot can *extend* for years before it reverses. You are betting on gravity, and gravity always wins eventually, but "eventually" is exactly the word that bankrupts impatient traders.

#### Worked example: a value reversion trade on an undervalued pair

Suppose the yen screens 40% undervalued on PPP, with USD/JPY trading at 157 (157 yen per dollar) against an implied PPP fair value near 100. You build a value position: long \$10,000,000 of yen against the dollar (i.e., short USD/JPY), betting on reversion toward fair value. If the yen reverts even *partway* — say USD/JPY falls from 157 to 141, a 10% strengthening of the yen — your \$10,000,000 long-yen position gains roughly 10% × \$10,000,000 = \$1,000,000. (That, not coincidentally, is exactly the kind of move that happened in the August-2024 unwind: the most undervalued currency snapped back hard.) But note the catch: to capture that you had to be *short the carry trade* — short the very high-yielders that carry was long — and you may have waited years holding a losing position before the snap. *Value is the patient factor: it bleeds slowly, pays in lumps, and those lumps often arrive on the exact day carry blows up.*

## Momentum and trend: ride what is moving

Momentum is the factor that drives fundamentalists crazy because it has no story about fair value, rate gaps, or balance of payments. Its entire thesis: **what has been going up tends to keep going up, and what has been going down tends to keep going down — for a while.** The ranking rule is the recent return itself. Sort currencies by their trailing performance (commonly over the last 1–12 months), go long the winners, short the losers.

![Momentum ranks currencies by trailing trend then rides the winners](/imgs/blogs/fx-as-a-factor-zoo-carry-value-momentum-and-dollar-6.png)

There are two close cousins worth distinguishing. **Cross-sectional momentum** ranks currencies *against each other* and is long-short like the others — long the strongest, short the weakest in the basket. **Time-series momentum** (often called *trend-following*) looks at each currency *on its own*: if it has been rising, go long; if falling, go short; the net basket can end up net-long or net-short the dollar. CTAs (commodity trading advisors) and managed-futures funds are famous trend-followers, and FX trend is one of their core markets. Both forms earn a similar premium, with a momentum/trend Sharpe of roughly 0.45 — the second-best of the four.

Why does momentum work in a market full of sophisticated players? Two honest mechanisms. First, *information diffuses slowly*: a central bank's hawkish shift gets priced over weeks, not seconds, as different participants update at different speeds, so a trend persists. Second, *flows are sticky*: a sovereign reallocating reserves, or a wave of hedging by exporters, moves a currency the same direction for months, and trend-followers ride that flow. The factor is, in effect, monetising the slow adjustment of a huge, fragmented market.

Momentum's killer feature for a portfolio is its *timing*. Because trend goes *short* whatever has been falling, it is frequently already short the high-yielders *before* a carry crash finishes — the high-yielders that were rolling over were already in momentum's short basket. That makes trend a natural *crash hedge* for carry. The flip side: momentum's own weakness is *sharp reversals* — V-shaped bottoms where the loser you are short suddenly rips higher. It crashes too, just at different moments than carry.

There is a subtle and important relationship between momentum and the other two cross-sectional factors. Momentum and **carry are usually friends** in calm times: a high-yielding currency that is *also* rising rewards both factors at once, so the two pile into the same names — which is exactly why a carry-momentum crowd can become dangerously concentrated, and why their crashes amplify each other when the trend finally breaks. Momentum and **value, by contrast, are natural enemies in the short run**: value wants to buy the currency that has fallen *to* cheapness, while momentum wants to short that same falling currency until it bottoms. They literally take opposite sides of a falling-but-cheap currency. This is not a bug — it is the deepest reason the two diversify so well. When value is early and bleeding (the cheap currency keeps falling), momentum is profiting from that same fall; when momentum gets caught in a sharp reversal (the cheap currency snaps back), value is finally being paid. Their disagreement is the engine of their combined stability. A blend that holds both is, in effect, holding a long-horizon mean-reversion bet and a short-horizon trend bet at the same time, and letting them hand the baton back and forth.

A word on *implementation*, because trend in particular is sensitive to it. The lookback window matters: a 1-month signal catches fast moves but whipsaws in choppy markets; a 12-month signal is smoother but slower to flip. Most trend programs blend several lookbacks — fast, medium, and slow — so the position is a weighted vote across horizons rather than an all-or-nothing flip on a single window. They also size positions *inversely to volatility*: a calm trending currency gets a bigger position than a wild one with the same trend, so that each position contributes roughly equal risk to the book. That volatility-targeting is half of why professional trend-following has a far better Sharpe than a naive "buy whatever went up last month" rule.

#### Worked example: trend cushions a carry crash

Return to August 2024. Your carry leg is long \$10,000,000 of yen-funded high-yielders; the crash costs it roughly 12%, or −\$1,200,000. But your trend leg had ranked the yen a *winner* (it had been strengthening into the unwind) and the high-yielders *losers*, so it was already long yen / short the high-yielders. As the yen ripped 12% and the high-yielders fell, the trend leg *made* money — say +8% on a \$10,000,000 trend book, or +\$800,000. The blended loss is −\$1,200,000 + \$800,000 = −\$400,000 instead of −\$1,200,000. *Trend did not predict the crash; it was simply already leaning the right way, because the thing that crashes has usually been weakening first — and that timing is worth more than any forecast.*

## The dollar factor: the whole world against one currency

The fourth factor is different in kind. Carry, value, and momentum are *cross-sectional* — they rank currencies against each other and net out the common move. The **dollar factor** is the common move. It captures the simple, powerful fact that *the dollar trends against everything at once*, and that those broad dollar regimes are themselves systematic and (somewhat) harvestable.

![The dollar factor as one trade against the rest of the world](/imgs/blogs/fx-as-a-factor-zoo-carry-value-momentum-and-dollar-7.png)

The dollar deserves its own factor because it is not just another currency — it is the unit on roughly 88% of all FX trades, the world's invoicing and funding currency, the asset that the entire system is denominated against. When the dollar strengthens, it strengthens against the *whole basket* of other currencies more or less together; when it weakens, the same. That broad, correlated behaviour is exactly what a single-currency factor captures and what the cross-sectional factors deliberately throw away. The full story of why the dollar is special — the unit of account, the [dollar's special role in the world](/blog/trading/forex/the-dxy-and-the-dollars-special-role-in-the-world) — is its own post; here it is a factor.

![DXY year-close levels showing the dollar trending in long regimes](/imgs/blogs/fx-as-a-factor-zoo-carry-value-momentum-and-dollar-8.png)

The DXY history above shows why the dollar is *trendable*. It does not wander randomly; it moves in long, persistent regimes — the strong-dollar years into 2022 (the index touched roughly 114.8 in September 2022, its highest in two decades), then a softening into 2025. Those multi-year swings are what a dollar-factor strategy tries to capture, typically by tying its sign to *fundamentals* — the dollar tends to strengthen when US growth and rates outpace the rest of the world, and in genuine global risk-off panics (the famous "dollar smile"), and to weaken in the calm middle when the rest of the world is catching up. Because the dollar factor keys off a *different driver* — broad US-versus-world dynamics rather than the cross-section — it is the most diversifying of the four, even though its standalone Sharpe is the lowest at about 0.30.

The cross-asset view of the dollar as a single, gravity-bending asset is developed in [FX as the relative-value layer](/blog/trading/cross-asset/fx-currencies-the-relative-value-layer) and [trading the dollar via DXY, carry, and the dollar smile](/blog/trading/macro-trading/trading-the-dollar-dxy-carry-dollar-smile). For the factor zoo, the key property is its *orthogonality*: a long-dollar tilt does something different from carry, value, or momentum, so it earns its slot in the blend on diversification alone.

How is the dollar factor's *sign* actually chosen? Unlike the cross-sectional factors, the dollar factor cannot be a pure ranking — there is only one dollar, so you cannot sort it against itself. Instead, desks set its direction from a small set of conditioning variables. The most common is the *rate gap*: when US short rates are rising faster than the rest of the world's, the dollar tends to strengthen, so the factor goes long the dollar. A second is the *growth gap*: when US activity is outpacing the rest of the world, capital flows toward dollar assets. A third — and the most distinctive — is the **dollar smile**: the dollar tends to be strong at *both* extremes of the global cycle. It is strong when the US is booming and out-yielding everyone (the right side of the smile), *and* strong in a genuine global risk-off panic when the world scrambles for the safest, most liquid currency (the left side of the smile). It is weak only in the calm, synchronised-recovery middle, when capital fans out to chase growth elsewhere. A dollar-factor model that keys off the smile is *long the dollar in panics* — which is precisely the regime in which carry is crashing. That overlap is not redundancy; it is the dollar factor doing its most valuable job, paying out on the exact days the carry leg is being marked down.

The dollar factor also carries the system's plumbing inside it. Because dollar credit to borrowers *outside* the US runs to something like 13 trillion dollars, a stronger dollar tightens financial conditions for the entire non-US world — every offshore borrower's debt gets heavier in local-currency terms. That feedback loop, sometimes called the dollar "wrecking ball," is why a rising dollar reliably stresses emerging markets and why the dollar factor and EM-carry are so violently linked: when the dollar rips, EM currencies fall, and a long-dollar position is *long exactly the move that is crushing the EM-carry leg.* The dollar factor is, in this sense, the natural hedge for the riskiest version of carry.

#### Worked example: the dollar factor sizing in a blend

Suppose your model says the dollar should be *strong* (US rates and growth lead the world) and you allocate \$10,000,000 of risk to a long-dollar factor: long USD against an equal-weighted basket of the other G10. If the dollar index rises 5% over the quarter, the position makes roughly 5% × \$10,000,000 = \$500,000. The point is not the size of that gain — it is that this \$500,000 is being earned on a *different signal* than your carry, value, and momentum legs. Even if it only ran a 0.30 Sharpe alone, adding a return stream that zigs when the others zag raises the *combined* Sharpe. *The dollar factor earns its place not by being the best standalone trade, but by being the least correlated one.*

## How the four factors combine: the diversification engine

Now the payoff. Four factors, each with a modest standalone Sharpe and an ugly tail. Why bother running all four? Because **they crash at different times**, and a portfolio of imperfectly-correlated return streams has a higher Sharpe than any of its parts. This is the only genuinely free lunch in finance, and FX factors are one of its cleanest examples.

![Carry crashes while trend and value cushion the blended drawdown](/imgs/blogs/fx-as-a-factor-zoo-carry-value-momentum-and-dollar-9.png)

The before/after figure tells the story. In an isolated carry crash (the "before" state), the carry leg craters — recall the roughly 12% drop in August 2024 — and a carry-only book takes the full hit. In the blended book (the "after" state), the same crash hits carry, but *trend was already short the falling high-yielders* and *value was already short the overvalued ones*, so both of those legs *make* money in the same event. The blend's drawdown is a fraction of the carry-only drawdown. You did not avoid the crash; you arranged your portfolio so that the crash paid one pocket while it emptied another.

The mathematics is worth making explicit because it is the entire case for factor investing. Combine two strategies, A and B, with the same volatility σ and the same expected return, but a correlation ρ between them. The blended portfolio (half in each) has volatility σ_blend = σ × √((1 + ρ) / 2). If ρ = 1 (the factors are identical), σ_blend = σ — no benefit. If ρ = 0 (uncorrelated), σ_blend = σ / √2 ≈ 0.71σ — you cut risk by 29% for the *same* return, so the Sharpe rises by a factor of √2 ≈ 1.41. If ρ is *negative* — which carry-versus-trend approximates during crashes — the risk reduction is even larger. *That square-root-of-two is the diversification engine; everything else is finding factors with low enough correlation to feed it.*

#### Worked example: the Sharpe lift from blending a \$10,000,000 book

Take a \$10,000,000 book and split it across carry, value, and momentum. Suppose each leg has the same standalone volatility and the carry leg alone runs a Sharpe of 0.55. If the three factors were *perfectly correlated*, blending would do nothing — the combined Sharpe stays 0.55. But the real pairwise correlations are low, and carry-versus-trend is *negative* in stress. Using the formula above with an average pairwise correlation of roughly ρ = 0.0 across three equal legs, the combined volatility falls to about σ × √(1/3 × (1 + 2 × 0)) — the blend of three zero-correlated equal legs has volatility σ/√3 ≈ 0.58σ. Holding the return roughly constant, the combined Sharpe climbs from 0.55 toward 0.55 / 0.58 ≈ 0.95 — *nearly double the carry-only Sharpe*, purely from combining streams that do not move together. On the \$10,000,000 book, that is the difference between a strategy a risk committee tolerates and one it is happy to lever. *The Sharpe of the blend is the real product a factor desk sells; the individual factors are just ingredients.*

This is why the most sophisticated FX shops do not pitch "we run a carry fund." They pitch "we run a diversified style portfolio" — carry for the premium, trend for the crash timing, value for the slow anchor, dollar for the orthogonal regime bet — risk-weighted so no single factor can sink the book. The blend is the moat. Any amateur can put on a carry trade; building, sizing, and *combining* the factors so the whole survives an August-2024 is the craft.

How do you actually weight the four? The naive answer — equal dollars in each — is wrong, because the factors have very different volatilities and very different tails. Carry is quiet most of the time and then explosive; trend is moderately volatile but symmetric; value is slow and low-volatility; the dollar is its own beast. Equal *dollar* weights would let carry's rare crashes dominate the whole book. The professional answer is **risk parity at the factor level**: size each factor so it contributes *equal risk* (equal expected volatility) to the portfolio, then tilt from there based on conviction and on each factor's correlation to the others. A factor that diversifies the rest — like the dollar, with its low correlation — earns a *larger* allocation than its standalone Sharpe alone would justify, because its marginal contribution to portfolio risk is small. A factor that is highly correlated with the others gets *less*, even if its standalone Sharpe is high. This is the counterintuitive heart of portfolio construction: the value of a factor inside a book is not its own Sharpe, but how it *moves the whole book's Sharpe* — and that depends almost entirely on its correlation to what you already hold.

There is one more layer the best desks add: **dynamic risk scaling**. The factors' correlations are not constant — they rise toward one in a crisis, exactly when diversification fails you most. So sophisticated books cut *gross* exposure when volatility spikes and correlations climb, accepting smaller positions precisely when the diversification benefit is shrinking. This is the same lesson the whole series keeps returning to: [when correlations go to one in a crisis](/blog/trading/cross-asset/when-correlations-go-to-one-in-a-crisis), the protection you were counting on evaporates. The defence is to be *smaller* in those moments, not to assume the historical correlations will hold. A factor book that does not de-gross into a storm is one bad correlation spike away from discovering that its four "diversifying" factors were really one trade all along.

## Common misconceptions

**"Factors are just fancy names for the same momentum bet."** No — and the proof is in their correlations. If carry, value, momentum, and dollar were the same trade, blending them would not improve the Sharpe at all (the σ × √((1+ρ)/2) formula gives no benefit when ρ = 1). The fact that a blend roughly *doubles* the carry-only Sharpe is direct evidence that the factors are genuinely different return streams. Value is often *long* exactly what momentum is *short* (a cheap currency that is still falling), which is why the two can offset rather than reinforce.

**"Carry has the best Sharpe, so just run carry."** Carry's standalone Sharpe (about 0.55) is the highest of the four, but the Sharpe ratio hides carry's worst feature — its brutal negative skew. A book that is 100% carry hands you a steady drip and then a −12% week that can be career-ending with leverage. The whole point of the factor zoo is that you give up a little of carry's headline Sharpe to buy a much better *tail*. The blended Sharpe (toward 0.95) beats carry's 0.55 *and* has a far gentler left tail.

**"Value works because cheap currencies bounce back fast."** Value is the *slowest* factor — its Sharpe is the lowest (about 0.35) precisely because PPP reversion takes years, not months. The yen sat 40%+ undervalued on PPP for the entire 2022–2024 stretch while getting *cheaper*. Value earns its place not by being quick but by being *uncorrelated and well-timed*: its lumps tend to arrive on the exact days carry crashes, because both are positioned around the same overvalued high-yielders.

**"The dollar factor is redundant — it's already inside carry."** They overlap but are not the same. Carry's dollar exposure is a *side effect* of where rates happen to sit; the dollar factor is a *deliberate* bet on broad US-versus-world dynamics and the dollar smile. In a global risk-off panic the dollar can rip higher even as carry collapses — the dollar factor captures that smile, carry does not. The dollar factor's low standalone Sharpe (0.30) understates its portfolio value, which comes almost entirely from its orthogonality.

**"A factor is market-neutral, so it can't lose much."** Dollar-neutral is not risk-neutral. The long and short legs of a carry basket are *correlated in stress* — in a panic, your high-yield longs fall and your funder shorts rally *together* relative to safe havens, so both legs lose at once. That correlation going to one is exactly what a carry crash *is*. Neutrality protects you from the broad market move; it does *not* protect you from the factor's own crash.

**"Factor returns are free money the academics found in the data."** They are not free, and treating them that way is how books blow up. Every one of these premia is *compensation for bearing a specific risk* — carry for crash risk, value for the pain of holding a losing position for years, momentum for the whipsaw of sharp reversals, the dollar for being long the world's safe asset when you least want to be. The premia survive *because* the risks are real and unpleasant; if they were riskless, arbitrage would have erased them decades ago. The honest framing is not "I found an inefficiency" but "I am being paid to hold a risk that most investors prefer to avoid." That reframing changes how you size: you respect the tail instead of leveraging into it.

**"More factors are always better — load up on all of them."** Diversification has diminishing returns, and added factors bring added costs. Each leg you add carries trading costs, financing, and the risk that the factor is overfit — a pattern that worked in a backtest but not out of sample. The "factor zoo" got its mocking name precisely because researchers catalogued hundreds of candidate factors, most of which were statistical mirages that died once real costs and out-of-sample data were applied. In FX, the four in this post earn their keep; the fifth, sixth, and seventh candidates mostly do not, because they are too correlated with the first four or too thin to trade. Diversification is powerful, but only across *genuinely different and genuinely robust* return streams — quality over quantity.

## How it shows up in real markets

**August 2024 — the textbook factor event.** The yen-carry unwind is the cleanest recent illustration of the entire post. USD/JPY collapsed from roughly 161.9 on July 3 to about 141.7 by August 5 as the BoJ hiked and a weak US jobs print landed; the VIX spiked toward the mid-60s intraday. A stylised carry index gave back about 12%. But trend-followers were *already short* the falling high-yielders and *long* the strengthening yen, and value books had flagged those high-yielders as expensive months earlier. The desks running a diversified style portfolio took a fraction of the carry-only damage — and some trend-heavy CTAs printed their best week of the year. The lesson landed hard: the crash was not a forecasting failure, it was a *concentration* failure for anyone running carry alone.

**2014–2015 — the strong-dollar regime.** The dollar factor earned its keep when the DXY climbed from roughly 90 at the end of 2014 toward triple digits, a broad, persistent regime driven by the Fed moving toward tightening while the rest of the world eased. A cross-sectional carry book did not capture that move — it nets out the common dollar trend by construction. The dollar factor, keyed to US-versus-world fundamentals, did. This is the orthogonality in action: the regime that one factor is blind to is exactly the one another factor is built to ride.

**The 2022 carry-into-EM and the sudden stop.** Through 2021, hot money chased the highest-yielding emerging-market currencies — a carry book's dream. Then 2022 brought the Fed's fastest hiking cycle in decades, the dollar ripped (DXY to roughly 114.8 in September), and EM portfolio flows reversed by tens of billions. Carry into EM is carry with a worse tail — the same negative skew, more leverage, thinner liquidity. The funds that had paired their EM-carry longs with a *long-dollar* tilt and a *trend* overlay rode the reversal far better than the pure yield-chasers, because the dollar factor was *long* exactly the move that was crushing their carry leg. For the anatomy of these unwinds across 1998, 2008, and 2024, see [carry-trade unwinds: when leverage breaks](/blog/trading/macro-trading/carry-trade-unwinds-1998-2008-2024-when-leverage-breaks).

**The decades-long value anchor.** Over the full sample, value is the quietest of the four — long stretches of doing nothing, then a sharp reversion. The Swiss franc's persistent overvaluation (roughly 38% dear on the Big Mac index in 2025) and the yen's persistent cheapness (roughly 41% undervalued) are exactly the kind of multi-year misalignments value is built to fade. Value rarely tells you *when*, which is why it is dangerous alone — but inside a blend, its slow pull is uncorrelated insurance that costs little to carry and pays when the fast factors stumble.

**Emerging-market currencies as carry on steroids.** The factor zoo is usually described over the G10, but the same machinery runs across emerging markets — and there the carry premium is far larger and far more dangerous. In 2024, a high-yielder like the Mexican peso paid around 10.25%, the Brazilian real around 12.25%, and the Turkish lira an eye-watering 50% policy rate, against a yen funder at roughly 0.25%. A naive EM-carry book pockets enormous rate gaps; it also takes enormous tail risk, because EM currencies are thinner, more prone to sudden stops, and more sensitive to the dollar wrecking ball. The 2013 taper tantrum, the 2018 EM rout, and the 2022 outflows each saw EM portfolio flows reverse by tens or even ninety-plus billion dollars in a year, taking the highest-yielders down hardest. The factor lesson is identical but sharper in EM: a high standalone yield is *compensation for a worse tail*, and the only sane way to harvest it is inside a risk-weighted blend with a long-dollar and a trend overlay that profit when the carry leg breaks. EM carry is the purest demonstration that headline yield and true risk-adjusted return are not the same thing.

**The 1998 LTCM/Russia unwind — the original lesson.** Long before August 2024 there was October 1998. Russia defaulted, the hedge fund LTCM imploded, and the yen-funded carry trade unwound so violently that USD/JPY collapsed from about 136 to 112 in a *three-day* window — a roughly 18% move in the funder, mirroring the modern playbook almost exactly. The carry trade lost about 28% peak-to-trough. The funds that had only carry on were destroyed; the few running trend and a dollar tilt alongside it survived. Twenty-six years apart, the 1998 and 2024 unwinds rhyme so precisely because the underlying structure never changed: carry sells crash insurance, the crash eventually comes, and only a diversified style book lives through it.

## The takeaway: read FX as a portfolio of styles, not a pile of calls

The single most useful shift this post asks for is to stop reading a currency as a *call* — "I think the yen goes up" — and start reading it as a *factor exposure*. When you find yourself long a high-yielder, ask: which factor is this, and what else is it correlated with? If your "view" is just a leveraged carry bet wearing a story, you have one trade, one tail, and one bad week between you and a blow-up.

The professional alternative is humble and durable. Each factor on its own is mediocre — Sharpe ratios clustered around 0.3 to 0.55, every one of them with losing years and ugly tails. None is a money machine. But because carry, value, momentum, and the dollar *crash at different times*, a risk-weighted blend of them runs a Sharpe well above any single leg, with a far gentler left tail. The diversification — that √2 (and better) shrinkage of risk for the same return — *is* the edge. It is the one free lunch in markets, and FX factors lay it out more cleanly than almost any other asset class.

So when carry crashes — and it always eventually crashes — the question that separates the survivors from the casualties is not "did you see it coming?" Almost nobody does. The question is: *what else were you holding?* If the answer is "trend, already short the fallers; value, already short the dear; and a dollar tilt riding the panic," then the crash pays one pocket while it empties another, and you live to collect the carry premium another month. That, and not any single clever currency call, is what it means to trade FX like a factor desk.

The series spine, one last time, made richer: you never own a currency, you own a *spread* — and the deepest version of that idea is that you do not even own *one* spread, you own a *portfolio* of them, each ranking the same currencies a different way, deliberately arranged so that no single bad day can take you down. An exchange rate is the relative price of two monies; a factor book is the relative price of two monies, ranked four ways at once.
