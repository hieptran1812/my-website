> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The dollar index (DXY) is not "the dollar." It is a fixed basket of six rich-world currencies — 57.6% euro — frozen on a 1973 snapshot of who America traded with, so it is really a slow, leveraged short on EUR/USD. The dollar's actual importance has almost nothing to do with that index.
>
> - **The DXY is 57.6% euro.** When you watch "the dollar" on that ticker, you are mostly watching EUR/USD upside down. There is no China, no Mexico, no emerging market in it at all.
> - **It is not trade-weighted.** Its weights froze in 1973; the Fed's broad trade-weighted index, which actually re-weights to current trade, looks meaningfully different and is led by Mexico, China, and Canada.
> - **The dollar's real power lives elsewhere — in three jobs.** It is the *unit of account* the world prices oil and metals in, the *invoicing currency* on roughly half of all trade, the *funding currency* the world borrows in, and the *reserve* central banks save in.
> - **The one number to remember:** the dollar is on one side of about **88% of all FX trades.** It is the other side of almost everything — the structural reason dollar conditions leak into every market on earth.

On a normal trading morning a number scrolls across the top of every market screen and a thousand headlines get written about it: *"The dollar rose today."* The number is the DXY, the U.S. Dollar Index, and it sits next to oil and gold and the S&P 500 as if it were the single price of the dollar — the way a stock has one price. Traders cite it, central-bank watchers cite it, and Twitter threads about "de-dollarization" cite it.

Here is the uncomfortable thing almost nobody says out loud: the number on that ticker is mostly a bet against the euro. Fifty-seven point six percent of the DXY is the euro. Another quarter is the yen and the pound. There is no Chinese renminbi in it. No Mexican peso, even though Mexico is now America's largest trading partner. No emerging-market currency of any kind. The weights were set in 1973 from a snapshot of who America traded with back then, and they have barely moved since. So when a headline says "the dollar strengthened," what it usually means is "the euro weakened, and to a lesser extent the yen and pound did too." The index is a 50-year-old photograph being used as a live map.

This post is the anchor of the dollar track in this series, and it does two jobs. First, it takes the DXY apart so you stop confusing the index for the thing. Second — and this is the part that actually matters — it explains where the dollar's real power comes from, which is *not* the index at all. The dollar runs the world economy through four quiet roles: it is the unit prices are quoted in, the invoice trade is billed on, the debt the world borrows in, and the savings central banks hold. Those four jobs are why the dollar is on one side of 88% of every currency trade on the planet, and why a tightening of dollar conditions in New York can wreck a budget in Jakarta. Get those two things straight — what the index is, and what the dollar is — and you will read currency markets far more clearly than the people quoting the ticker at you.

![The dollar at the center of the global monetary system linking unit of account invoicing funding reserve and the DXY](/imgs/blogs/the-dxy-and-the-dollars-special-role-in-the-world-1.png)

Everything in this series rests on one idea: you never own *a currency* in isolation. Every position is a pair, a relative bet of one money against another, driven by the gap between two countries' interest rates and the flow of capital across their borders. The DXY is the purest expression of that idea — it is literally a basket of pairs — and the dollar's special role is what makes those pairs behave the way they do. Let's build both up from zero.

## Foundations: What the dollar index actually measures

Start with the most basic question, the one the ticker quietly answers wrong: **what is "the price of the dollar"?**

A currency does not have *a* price. The euro has a price in dollars, a different price in yen, a different price in pesos. The dollar, likewise, has a price against every other currency, and those prices do not move together. On a given day the dollar can rise against the euro and fall against the yen at the same time. So "the dollar went up" is, strictly, meaningless until you say *against what*. To turn the dollar into a single number — something you can chart and trade and put on a screen next to oil — somebody has to choose a basket of other currencies, decide how much each one counts, and average the dollar's price against all of them. That average is an *index*. The DXY is one particular choice of basket and weights.

**The base/quote refresher.** Every FX quote is two currencies: a *base* (the thing being priced) and a *quote* (the thing it is priced in). EUR/USD = 1.0800 means one euro costs 1.08 dollars — euro is the base, dollar is the quote. USD/JPY = 150 means one dollar costs 150 yen — now the dollar is the base. The DXY mixes these: it goes *up* when the dollar strengthens, so for the pairs where the dollar is the quote (EUR/USD, GBP/USD) the index moves *opposite* to the pair, and for the pairs where the dollar is the base (USD/JPY, USD/CAD) it moves *with* the pair. If that already sounds fiddly, that is the point — the DXY is a weighted geometric mash-up of six exchange rates, not a clean single price. (If base/quote and pips are new to you, the [reading an FX quote](/blog/trading/forex/base-quote-pips-and-how-to-read-an-fx-quote) post builds them from scratch.)

**The six currencies, and their weights.** The DXY contains exactly six currencies. Here they are, with the fixed weight each carries in the index:

![DXY basket weights horizontal bar euro 57.6 percent yen pound Canadian dollar krona Swiss franc](/imgs/blogs/the-dxy-and-the-dollars-special-role-in-the-world-2.png)

Read that chart slowly, because it is the whole argument in one picture. The euro is **57.6%** of the index — more than half. The Japanese yen is 13.6%, the British pound 11.9%, the Canadian dollar 9.1%, the Swedish krona 4.2%, and the Swiss franc 3.6%. That is it. Six currencies, all from the rich, developed, dollar-aligned world. The euro alone, plus the pound, plus the Swiss franc, plus the Swedish krona, means **roughly 77% of the index is European.** The DXY is, functionally, a measure of the dollar against Europe with a yen garnish.

Now the part that surprises people: **those weights are fixed.** They do not float. They are not updated as trade patterns change. The index was launched in 1973 by the U.S. Federal Reserve (later handed to ICE, which administers it today) with weights based on the share of U.S. trade with each country *at that time*. The only material change in fifty years came in 1999, when the dozen-odd European currencies in the original basket — the Deutsche mark, French franc, Italian lira, Dutch guilder, Belgian franc — collapsed into the single euro, which inherited their combined weight. That is how the euro ended up at 57.6%. Since then: frozen. A 1973 trade snapshot, lightly remodeled in 1999, still running the headline today.

**What that makes the DXY, mechanically.** Because the weights are fixed and the euro is dominant, the DXY behaves like a leveraged short position in EUR/USD with some smaller side bets. Roughly: about 58 cents of every dollar of "DXY exposure" is *short euro, long dollar*. The next 26 cents are short yen and short pound. The last 17 cents are short the Canadian dollar, krona, and franc. So when you hear "the Dollar Index hit a new high," your first instinct should be to look at EUR/USD — because more often than not, that is what moved.

#### Worked example: how a 10% euro move alone shifts the DXY

Suppose nothing happens to the yen, pound, Canadian dollar, krona, or franc — they sit perfectly still all day. Only the euro moves: EUR/USD falls 10%, from 1.0800 to 0.9720 (the euro weakens, the dollar strengthens against it). What happens to the DXY?

The DXY is a *weighted geometric* index, which means each currency's percentage move contributes in proportion to its weight. The euro carries a 57.6% weight, so a 10% dollar gain against the euro pushes the index up by roughly:

```
DXY change  ~  weight_euro  x  dollar move vs euro
            =  0.576  x  10%
            ~  5.8%
```

So a single 10% move in EUR/USD — with *every other currency unchanged* — moves the entire dollar "index" by about **5.8%.** If the DXY started at 100, it ends near **105.8**, and every headline declares broad dollar strength. But the dollar did not strengthen against the world. It strengthened against one currency, the euro, and the index dutifully reported more than half of that as if it were the whole picture. The takeaway: **more than half of any DXY move can come from EUR/USD alone, so the index tells you about Europe first and the world a distant second.**

**One more mechanical detail, because it explains the index's odd behavior.** The DXY is not a simple weighted *average* of six exchange rates; it is a weighted *geometric* mean. The official formula multiplies the six exchange rates together, each raised to the power of its weight, and scales the result against a 1973 base of 100. You do not need the algebra to use the index, but the geometric construction has two consequences worth knowing. First, it means a given *percentage* move in any currency contributes in rough proportion to its weight — which is exactly the approximation used in the worked example above, and it is accurate enough for reading the tape. Second, the geometric form makes the index slightly *asymmetric*: a currency that halves and one that doubles do not cancel perfectly, so over very long horizons the index has a faint downward drift baked into its math, separate from any real dollar trend. This is a small effect, but it is one more reason to treat the DXY as a directional gauge rather than a precise meter.

**Why was the basket frozen in the first place?** The fixed weights are not an oversight — they are a deliberate design choice that makes the DXY *tradeable*. A futures contract or an ETF needs a benchmark whose definition never changes, so that a price in 1985 and a price in 2025 are comparable and so that the contract's composition is predictable. An index that re-weighted every year would be a moving target: you could never be sure what you were holding. The cost of that stability is representativeness — by refusing to update, the DXY drifts further from the dollar's real trade exposure every year. The Fed's trade-weighted indices make the opposite trade-off: they re-weight annually and are therefore representative, but they are not designed as a clean tradeable instrument. Neither choice is wrong; they are tools built for different jobs, and the confusion arises only when people use the trading instrument to answer the economics question.

That is the foundation. The DXY is a fixed, euro-heavy, rich-world-only basket built as a geometric mean on 1973 weights — a useful, liquid, tradeable thing, but a narrow proxy that should never be mistaken for "the dollar's value against the world." With that nailed down, let's look at how it has actually behaved over the long sweep, because even as a narrow proxy it carries real information about regimes.

## The DXY across forty years: regimes, not noise

A fixed basket has one genuine virtue: because the weights never change, a long DXY chart is internally consistent. You are comparing the dollar-against-these-six-currencies in 1985 with the dollar-against-the-same-six-currencies in 2025. The level means the same thing across decades. And what it shows is that the dollar moves in long *regimes* — multi-year tides, not daily noise.

![DXY long history 1985 to 2025 line chart Plaza Accord 2001 high 2008 trough 2022 spike](/imgs/blogs/the-dxy-and-the-dollars-special-role-in-the-world-3.png)

Four moments anchor that history, and each one teaches something about what actually moves the dollar.

**1985 — the Plaza Accord peak (~123).** In the early 1980s, Paul Volcker's Federal Reserve had crushed inflation with brutally high interest rates, and money flooded into dollar assets to earn those rates. The DXY soared to about 123. The strong dollar was hammering American exporters, so in September 1985 the finance ministers of the G5 — the U.S., Japan, West Germany, France, and the U.K. — met at the Plaza Hotel in New York and *agreed* to push the dollar down through coordinated intervention. It worked, dramatically: the DXY fell from ~123 to ~85 over the next two years. That is the highest the index has ever been, and it took a coordinated act of the world's biggest governments to reverse it. The lesson: rate differentials drive the dollar (the high rates pulled it up), and sufficiently determined official action can move it (the Plaza Accord pushed it back down).

**2001 — the strong-dollar high (~117).** The late-1990s tech boom and a "strong dollar policy" out of the U.S. Treasury pulled global capital into American assets. The DXY climbed back near 117. Then the dot-com bust, the 2001 recession, and aggressive Fed rate cuts sent it into a long decline through the 2000s. Again: capital flows and the rate gap, working in the same direction.

**2008 — the GFC trough (~81).** By 2008 the dollar had ground down to ~81 as the Fed cut rates toward zero and the U.S. ran large deficits. Notice the paradox here, because it matters for the next post in this track: even though the *2008 financial crisis began in the U.S.*, the dollar spiked violently *during* the worst of the panic before settling low — the world scrambled for dollars precisely when American finance was the problem. That counterintuitive behavior is the [dollar smile](/blog/trading/forex/the-dollar-smile-why-the-dollar-wins-in-boom-and-in-panic), and it is exactly what you would expect from a currency that is the world's funding and reserve unit, not just America's domestic money.

**2022 — the hiking-cycle spike (~108, intraday 114.8).** When the Fed hiked interest rates faster than at any time in forty years to fight post-COVID inflation, the dollar rose against almost everything. The DXY hit 114.8 intraday in September 2022 — its highest since 2002. The euro fell below parity (under 1.00 dollars per euro for the first time in twenty years), the yen collapsed toward 152, and the pound briefly cratered to an all-time low of 1.035 during the U.K.'s mini-budget crisis. The driver, once more, was the rate gap: the Fed was hiking hard while the Bank of Japan held rates at zero and the European Central Bank moved more slowly.

Step back and the pattern is clear. In forty years the DXY has spent almost all its life inside a band of roughly **80 to 120.** It swings in long regimes that map cleanly onto the U.S. rate cycle and the global flow of capital. This is the proper use of the index: as a *regime gauge* for the dollar versus the developed world, not as a precise measure of the dollar's global value. For the formal mechanism linking policy rates to the dollar, the macro track owns that — see [how monetary policy moves currencies](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-monetary-policy-moves-currencies-rate-differentials); we lean on it here rather than re-deriving it.

There is a second, subtler lesson in that 80–120 band. The DXY looks *tame* — a 50% range over four decades — precisely because it measures the dollar only against five other rich, stable, freely-floating currencies. Against those peers, the dollar genuinely does swing in a moderate range; the euro, yen, pound, and friends are all serious currencies that hold their value reasonably well. But that tameness is an artifact of the basket, not a feature of the dollar's global reach. Over the same forty years, the dollar rose from 25 baht to over 50 baht in the 1997 Asian crisis, from a few thousand rupiah to nearly 17,000, from single-digit reais to 5-plus. Against the currencies that are *not* in the index — the emerging-market monies where the dollar's funding role bites hardest — the dollar's moves have been violent and one-directional. The DXY's calm is the calm of a club of equals; the dollar's real-world history outside that club is anything but calm. Whenever someone shows you a placid long-run DXY chart as evidence that "the dollar isn't really that strong," remember which currencies the chart politely leaves out.

#### Worked example: why "the dollar fell 5% this year" can be a half-truth

Say the DXY drops from 105 to 100 over a year — a clean 4.8% decline, splashed everywhere as "dollar weakness." Now suppose that underneath that headline, the euro rose 8% against the dollar while the Mexican peso *fell* 6% against the dollar over the same year. A U.S. importer buying goods from Mexico saw the dollar get *stronger* — their dollars bought more pesos. But the peso is not in the DXY at all. The index reports only the euro-led move and prints "dollar down 4.8%," while a huge slice of real-world dollar trade went the other way.

```
DXY view:      dollar fell ~4.8%  (euro-driven)
Reality check: dollar +6% vs peso (peso not in the index)
Net for a US-Mexico importer: a STRONGER dollar
```

The index and the importer's lived experience point in opposite directions. The takeaway: a DXY move describes the dollar against six rich currencies, so it can be flatly wrong about the dollar you actually face if your trade flows run through Mexico, China, or any emerging market.

## Fixed weights versus a true trade-weighted dollar

This is the single most important misconception to clear, so it gets its own section and its own figure. People use "the dollar index" and "the trade-weighted dollar" as if they were synonyms. They are not. A *trade-weighted* index weights each currency by how much your country actually trades with that country, and it *updates* those weights as trade patterns shift. The DXY does neither: its weights are frozen, and they reflect 1973's trade, not today's.

![Before and after fixed 1973 DXY weights versus a trade weighted dollar with Mexico China Canada](/imgs/blogs/the-dxy-and-the-dollars-special-role-in-the-world-4.png)

Look at the mismatch. America's top three trading partners today are Mexico, China, and Canada. The DXY contains *Canada* (at a modest 9.1%) but **no Mexico and no China at all.** The two countries that the U.S. trades with most, after Canada, are simply absent. Meanwhile the euro area — important, but nowhere near 58% of U.S. trade — dominates the index at 57.6%. The DXY is over-weighted to Europe by a wide margin and blind to the entire developing world and to North America's largest bilateral relationship.

The Federal Reserve maintains the alternative: the **Trade-Weighted U.S. Dollar Index (Broad)**, sometimes called the broad dollar or the "advanced + emerging" index. It covers roughly 26 currencies, re-weights them annually based on actual U.S. trade shares, and therefore puts large weights on the Mexican peso, the Chinese renminbi, and the Canadian dollar. When you want to know whether the dollar is genuinely strong or weak *for the U.S. economy* — for exporters, for import prices, for the trade balance — the Fed's broad index is the right tool. The DXY is the right tool when you want a liquid, tradeable, century-stable benchmark for the dollar against the major developed pairs, which is exactly why futures and ETFs are built on it.

Both are legitimate. They simply answer different questions. The error is using the DXY to answer the trade-weighted question — and that error is everywhere, including in serious commentary about "the dollar's value" and "de-dollarization."

#### Worked example: invoicing a \$1,000,000 trade in dollars between two non-US countries

Here is the fact that the DXY cannot see, and that explains the dollar's real power. A factory in Vietnam sells \$1,000,000 of furniture to a buyer in Nigeria. Neither party is American. Neither uses dollars domestically — the Vietnamese firm pays its workers in dong, the Nigerian buyer earns in naira. Yet the contract is written in **dollars**, the invoice says **\$1,000,000**, and the payment moves through the dollar system.

Why? Because the dollar is the *invoicing currency* — the neutral unit two strangers in different countries both trust and both can price. The Vietnamese exporter does not want to be paid in naira (hard to use, volatile). The Nigerian importer does not have dong. Both can get dollars, both understand a dollar price, and the global banking plumbing settles dollars fastest. So:

```
Contract value:        $1,000,000   (written in USD)
Vietnamese seller:     receives USD, converts dong<->USD as needed
Nigerian buyer:        pays USD, sources USD via naira<->USD
US involvement:        none -- yet the trade rides the dollar
```

This single transaction touches the dollar **twice** (the seller converting out of dollars, the buyer converting into them) and **never appears in the DXY**, because neither the dong nor the naira is in the basket. Multiply by the millions of such trades happening daily and you see the gap between the index and the reality. The takeaway: the dollar's grip on the world runs through invoicing and settlement between *third countries*, an effect the DXY is structurally blind to. This is the dollar's real job — so let's catalog all four of its jobs properly.

## The dollar's four jobs: the real source of its power

If the DXY is the dollar's *reflection in a narrow mirror*, the dollar's *substance* is a set of four functions it performs for the entire world economy — functions no other currency performs all at once. Economists call the resulting advantage "exorbitant privilege," a phrase coined by a French finance minister in the 1960s to describe how the U.S. gets to borrow cheaply, in its own currency, simply because everyone else needs dollars. Here are the four jobs.

![Matrix of the dollar's four jobs unit of account invoicing funding reserve with the number and who relies on it](/imgs/blogs/the-dxy-and-the-dollars-special-role-in-the-world-5.png)

**Job 1 — Unit of account.** The dollar is the ruler the world measures value with. Oil is priced in dollars per barrel. Gold is dollars per troy ounce. Copper, wheat, iron ore, ocean freight, semiconductors — quoted in dollars on the global market regardless of where they are dug up, grown, or shipped. When a Brazilian miner sells iron ore to a Chinese steelmaker, the price on the screen is in dollars even though neither is American. Being the unit of account means the dollar is the *common language of value*, and a language with a billion speakers is very hard to displace. (This is also why commodity exporters' currencies — the Australian, Canadian, and Norwegian dollars — track the dollar prices of their exports so tightly; see [terms of trade and the commodity currencies](/blog/trading/forex/terms-of-trade-and-the-commodity-currencies).)

**Job 2 — Invoicing currency.** Beyond commodities, roughly **half of all world trade** is invoiced in dollars — and the U.S. is involved in only about a tenth of world trade. That gap is the whole story. Research by economists including Gita Gopinath shows that countries price their exports in dollars even when selling to non-U.S. buyers, a phenomenon called "dominant currency pricing." Once your competitors invoice in dollars, you invoice in dollars too, so prices stay comparable — a self-reinforcing standard. The Vietnam-to-Nigeria furniture trade above is one of millions. Invoicing is why a strong dollar raises import costs *worldwide*, not just for Americans: when the dollar rises, every dollar-invoiced import gets more expensive for everyone paying in a local currency.

**Job 3 — Funding currency.** The world does not just price and invoice in dollars; it *borrows* in dollars. Governments, banks, and corporations outside the United States have issued trillions of dollars of dollar-denominated debt — bonds and loans they must repay in dollars they do not print. This is the offshore dollar system, the "eurodollar" market, and it is enormous: dollar credit to non-bank borrowers outside the U.S. runs well over \$13 trillion. The deep dive on this is the [eurodollar and offshore dollar system](/blog/trading/forex/eurodollars-and-the-offshore-dollar-system) post; the key point here is that being the funding currency makes the Federal Reserve the de facto central bank of the world. When the Fed raises rates or dollar liquidity tightens, every offshore dollar borrower feels it — which is the mechanism behind the dollar's role as a wrecking ball for emerging markets, covered in [the dollar as a wrecking ball](/blog/trading/forex/the-dollar-as-a-wrecking-ball-for-emerging-markets).

**Job 4 — Reserve currency.** Finally, the dollar is what central banks *save in*. When a country runs a trade surplus or wants a war chest to defend its own currency, it accumulates foreign-exchange reserves — and the majority of those reserves are dollars, parked mostly in U.S. Treasury bonds. We will quantify this below, but the headline is that the dollar is about 57.8% of all disclosed global reserves. Being the reserve asset is the store-of-value capstone: the world not only prices, invoices, and borrows in dollars, it keeps its rainy-day money in dollars too.

These four jobs reinforce one another. Because the world invoices in dollars, it needs dollars to settle trade; because it needs dollars, it borrows and holds dollars; because it holds dollars, dollar assets are deep and liquid, which makes them the natural unit to price and invoice in. It is a flywheel, and it has been spinning since 1944. None of it shows up on the DXY ticker.

**Where the flywheel came from.** The dollar's roles were not decreed by markets overnight; they were built, deliberately, at a 1944 conference in Bretton Woods, New Hampshire, where the Allied powers designed the postwar monetary order around the dollar. Under that system, other currencies were pegged to the dollar and the dollar was convertible to gold at \$35 an ounce — so the dollar became the hinge of the entire system, the one currency everyone else was defined against. When the U.S. ended gold convertibility in 1971 (the "Nixon shock") and the world moved to floating exchange rates, the gold anchor vanished but the *habit* of using the dollar did not. By then the plumbing was already laid: trade was invoiced in dollars, commodities were priced in dollars, reserves were held in dollars, and the deepest bond market on earth was the U.S. Treasury market. The 1971 break removed the dollar's link to gold but left every one of its network roles intact. That is the deep reason "de-dollarization" is so slow: you cannot vote a network standard out of existence, you can only build a better one and wait decades for everyone to switch — and no one has built a credible rival to the four-job machine.

**The privilege is also a responsibility — and a vulnerability.** Exorbitant privilege sounds purely like a gift, and in fiscal terms it is: the U.S. can run large deficits and finance them cheaply because the world *needs* to hold dollar assets, so demand for Treasuries stays strong even when supply balloons. But the flip side is that the U.S. is on the hook for the world's dollar plumbing. When global dollar funding seized up in 2008 and again in March 2020, it was the Federal Reserve — not any global body — that had to open emergency "swap lines," lending hundreds of billions of dollars to foreign central banks so the offshore dollar system would not collapse. The Fed acted as the world's lender of last resort because the dollar's four jobs make Fed dollars the only thing that can put out a global dollar fire. Privilege and obligation are two sides of the same coin: the currency that everyone uses is also the currency everyone runs to in a panic, and the institution that issues it cannot opt out of backstopping the system it anchors.

#### Worked example: the cost of a strong dollar to a non-American borrower

Take a corporation in Indonesia that borrowed \$100,000,000 in dollar bonds three years ago, when the Indonesian rupiah traded at 14,000 per dollar. Its revenue is in rupiah. At borrowing time, that debt was worth 1.4 trillion rupiah of local earnings. Now suppose the dollar strengthens and the rupiah falls to 16,000 per dollar — a roughly 14% move, the kind the 2022 cycle delivered to many emerging markets.

```
Debt:                 $100,000,000  (fixed, in dollars)
At 14,000 IDR/USD:    1,400,000,000,000 rupiah owed
At 16,000 IDR/USD:    1,600,000,000,000 rupiah owed
Extra burden:           200,000,000,000 rupiah (+14%)
```

The dollar amount of the debt never changed. But measured in the rupiah the company actually earns, the burden jumped by **200 billion rupiah — a 14% increase — purely from the exchange rate.** Nothing about the business changed; the dollar simply got stronger. This is why a rising dollar tightens financial conditions across the entire developing world simultaneously, and why dollar strength and emerging-market stress are joined at the hip. The takeaway: the dollar's funding role means a stronger dollar is a *tax on every dollar-borrower outside America*, transmitted instantly and globally.

## The 88% fact: the dollar is the other side of almost everything

Now we arrive at the single number that captures all four jobs at once, the one to commit to memory. According to the Bank for International Settlements (BIS), which surveys the global FX market every three years, **the U.S. dollar is on one side of about 88% of all foreign-exchange trades.**

![Bar chart share of FX trades by currency dollar 88 percent euro 30 yen pound renminbi](/imgs/blogs/the-dxy-and-the-dollars-special-role-in-the-world-6.png)

Read the chart carefully, including the quirk that the bars sum to 200%, not 100%. Every FX trade has *two* currencies — you are always swapping one for another — so each trade is counted once for each of its two currencies. That is why the shares total 200%. The dollar's share is **88.5%.** The euro, a distant second, is **30.5%.** The yen is 16.7%, the pound 12.9%, and the Chinese renminbi — the great supposed challenger — is just **7.0%.**

Sit with how lopsided that is. Nine out of every ten currency trades on earth have a dollar on one side. The dollar is not merely the *biggest* currency; it is the **vehicle** through which almost everything else trades. Here is why. Suppose a fund wants to swap Thai baht for Chilean pesos. There is essentially no liquid, tight market for THB/CHP directly — who trades that pair? Instead, the standard route is to sell baht for dollars, then sell dollars for pesos. Two dollar legs, executed in the deepest markets on the planet, to get from one minor currency to another. The dollar is the connecting hub of the entire FX network, the airport everyone connects through. Trades that have nothing to do with America are still, mechanically, dollar trades.

This is the deepest reason the dollar's power dwarfs America's economic size. The United States is about 24% of world GDP, but the dollar is on 88% of trades and is roughly 58% of reserves. The currency is *over-represented* relative to the economy by a huge margin, because its job is not to represent the U.S. economy — its job is to be the world's shared medium. A network's value comes from how many people use it, and almost everyone uses the dollar, which makes almost everyone want to use the dollar. That is the network effect, and network effects are extraordinarily sticky.

It is also why the DXY, for all its flaws, is still *useful*. Even though the index is a narrow euro-heavy basket, the dollar's hub role means that broad dollar strength tends to show up across many pairs at once — when the dollar rises against the euro, it is often rising against the peso and the rand and the real too, just not in the same proportion. So the DXY catches the *direction* of a broad dollar move even when it badly mismeasures the *magnitude*. Use it as a compass, not a ruler.

The hub role also explains a structural fact about *where* and *how* FX trades, which the BIS survey lays out in detail. Most "FX trading" is not what a beginner pictures — someone buying euros to take a view. The overwhelming majority of FX volume is *FX swaps*: short-term deals where two parties exchange currencies now and agree to reverse the exchange later, used by banks and corporations to manage their dollar funding and hedge their dollar exposure. Of the roughly \$7.5 trillion that trades every single day in the global FX market, FX swaps alone are over \$3.8 trillion, dwarfing the \$2.1 trillion of outright spot. And the swaps are overwhelmingly *against the dollar*, because the dollar is what the world needs to fund and hedge. So the 88% figure is not just about speculative bets — it is mostly about the global financial system rolling its dollar funding over and over, every day, through the dollar hub. The currency you see flashing on a retail screen is the visible tip of an enormous, mostly-invisible plumbing system whose job is to keep dollars flowing to everyone who owes them. (The instrument-by-instrument breakdown is the [inside the interbank FX market](/blog/trading/forex/the-biggest-market-on-earth-inside-the-interbank-fx-market) post; the point here is that the plumbing is dollar plumbing.)

There is one more consequence of the hub structure that matters for anyone trading minor currencies: liquidity and spreads. Because every minor currency trades *through* the dollar, the dollar legs are the deepest, tightest markets in the world, while direct "cross" markets between two minors are thin and expensive. The bid-ask spread on EUR/USD in normal conditions is a fraction of a pip; the spread on an exotic emerging-market pair can be hundreds of times wider. That is not because traders dislike the exotic — it is because the dollar hub concentrates all the liquidity onto the dollar legs, leaving the direct crosses starved. The 88% dominance and the liquidity advantage are the same phenomenon seen from two angles: everyone routes through the dollar, so the dollar legs are deep, so everyone routes through the dollar.

#### Worked example: routing a baht-to-peso trade through the dollar

A Thai exporter is paid 35,000,000 Thai baht and needs to pay a Chilean supplier in pesos. There is no deep direct THB/CLP market. So the bank routes it through the dollar in two legs. Assume USD/THB = 35.0 (35 baht per dollar) and USD/CLP = 950 (950 pesos per dollar).

```
Leg 1:  35,000,000 THB / 35.0   = $1,000,000   (sell baht for dollars)
Leg 2:  $1,000,000 x 950        = 950,000,000 CLP  (sell dollars for pesos)
Result: 950,000,000 Chilean pesos delivered
```

Two currencies that have nothing to do with the United States, and the trade still passes through \$1,000,000 of dollars in the middle. Both legs trade in tight, liquid markets precisely *because* they are dollar legs; the spread on a direct THB/CLP trade, if you could even find it, would be far wider. The takeaway: the dollar's 88% share is not an accident of American power — it is the equilibrium of a network that routes everything through its most liquid hub, and that hub is the dollar.

## Reserves: where the world keeps its savings

The fourth job — reserve currency — deserves its own numbers, because it is the most visible front in the "is the dollar losing its crown?" debate, and the data are more nuanced than either side claims.

![Bar chart global reserve mix 2024 dollar 57.8 percent euro yen pound renminbi other](/imgs/blogs/the-dxy-and-the-dollars-special-role-in-the-world-7.png)

The IMF tracks the currency composition of the world's disclosed official reserves in a dataset called COFER. As of late 2024, the dollar was **57.8%** of disclosed reserves. The euro was 19.8%, the yen 5.8%, the pound 4.7%, and the Chinese renminbi just **2.2%** — barely a rounding error despite a decade of headlines about the renminbi's rise. The dollar is roughly *three times* the euro, *ten times* the yen, and *twenty-six times* the renminbi as a reserve asset.

Two facts coexist here, and you need both. The first: the dollar's reserve share *has* drifted down — from about 71.5% in 2001 to 57.8% today. That is real, it is a roughly 14-percentage-point decline over two decades, and it is the strongest factual basis for the de-dollarization thesis. The second, and larger, fact: **none of that lost share went to a rival reserve currency.** It did not flow to the euro (roughly flat), the yen, or the renminbi (still trivial). It diffused into a scatter of smaller currencies — the Australian and Canadian dollars, the Swedish krona, the Korean won — and into gold. Central banks have been *diversifying at the margin*, trimming a little dollar exposure and spreading it thinly, not *defecting* to a new standard. There is no challenger. There is only mild, slow diversification away from a still-overwhelming dollar.

Why do central banks hold so many dollars in the first place? Three reasons, all downstream of the other three jobs. **Liquidity:** the U.S. Treasury market is the deepest, most liquid bond market on earth, so a central bank can park hundreds of billions and sell quickly in a crisis without crashing the price. **Settlement:** because trade is invoiced in dollars and debt is denominated in dollars, a central bank needs dollar reserves to *defend its own currency* — to sell dollars and buy its own money when it is under attack (the [wrecking ball](/blog/trading/forex/the-dollar-as-a-wrecking-ball-for-emerging-markets) post and the [game-theoretic central-bank game](/blog/trading/game-theory/the-central-bank-game-credibility-commitment-and-dont-fight-the-fed) both turn on this). **Safety:** in a global panic, the dollar and Treasuries are where everyone runs, so dollar reserves *gain* value exactly when a country most needs them. The dollar's reserve role is the store-of-value capstone on top of its medium-of-exchange dominance.

There is a deep structural reason the dollar's reserve role is so hard to dislodge, and it is worth naming: the world has a chronic *shortage of safe assets*. A reserve currency's bonds have to be safe (low default risk), liquid (sellable in size without moving the price), and available in *enormous* quantity (because the whole world wants to hold them). Very few assets clear all three bars. The euro area has safety and liquidity, but it lacks a single unified safe bond — there is no "Euro-Treasury," only German Bunds, Italian BTPs, French OATs, each a separate credit, and the safe ones (Bunds) are too scarce. Japanese and Swiss bonds are safe but the economies are too small and yields too low. The renminbi fails the liquidity-and-availability test outright because of capital controls. That leaves the U.S. Treasury market, which is the one place on earth that supplies trillions of dollars of a single, safe, liquid asset. The world's reserve managers do not hold dollars because they *prefer* America; they hold dollars because the U.S. Treasury market is the only pool deep enough to absorb their savings. To replace the dollar, a rival would first have to build a Treasury-sized safe-asset market — a multi-decade project no one has even started.

This also surfaces a famous tension known as the **Triffin dilemma.** For the world to have enough dollar reserves and dollar liquidity, the U.S. must supply dollars to the rest of the world — which it does by running persistent trade deficits, sending dollars abroad in exchange for goods. But running endless deficits to feed the world's appetite for dollars can, in principle, erode confidence in the dollar's value over the very long run. The reserve currency's issuer is caught between supplying enough of its currency (which means deficits) and maintaining its currency's soundness (which means restraint). The dollar has lived inside this tension for half a century without resolving it, which is itself a kind of answer: the demand for dollar safety has so far outweighed every worry about dollar supply. But it is the structural reason serious analysts keep one eye on the dollar's long-run privilege even while acknowledging there is no near-term challenger.

#### Worked example: how a central bank's reserve mix actually breaks down

Take a stylized central bank holding \$200,000,000,000 (\$200 billion) of reserves and applying the global average mix. Dollar-allocate it by the COFER shares:

```
US dollar  57.8%  ->  $115,600,000,000
Euro       19.8%  ->   $39,600,000,000
Yen         5.8%  ->   $11,600,000,000
Pound       4.7%  ->    $9,400,000,000
Renminbi    2.2%  ->    $4,400,000,000
Other       9.7%  ->   $19,400,000,000
```

Over \$115 billion of a \$200 billion war chest sits in dollars — mostly in U.S. Treasury bonds — versus just \$4.4 billion in renminbi. Even a central bank that *wanted* to de-dollarize faces a brutal constraint: there is no other market deep enough to absorb \$115 billion without moving prices against it, and no other currency a trade partner will reliably accept. The takeaway: the dollar's reserve dominance is held in place less by love of America than by the simple absence of a liquid alternative big enough to replace it.

## Common misconceptions

The DXY and the dollar's role attract more confident wrong opinions than almost any topic in markets. Five worth correcting, each with a number.

**Myth 1: "The DXY is the dollar's value against the world."** No — it is the dollar's value against *six rich currencies*, 57.6% euro, with no emerging market and no Chinese renminbi at all. The dollar's value against the world is better captured by the Fed's broad trade-weighted index, which puts heavy weight on the Mexican peso and the renminbi. When the two disagree — and they regularly do by several percent a year — the DXY is the *less* representative of the dollar's real-economy value.

**Myth 2: "The DXY going up means the dollar is broadly strong."** Mostly, but not reliably. Because the index is 57.6% euro, a DXY rally can be almost entirely a EUR/USD move while the dollar is *flat or weaker* against currencies outside the basket. In our worked example, the DXY fell 4.8% in a year when the dollar actually *rose* against the peso. The index gives you direction, not a faithful magnitude, and occasionally not even the right direction for the pairs that matter to you.

**Myth 3: "The renminbi is about to dethrone the dollar."** Not on the data. The renminbi is **7.0%** of FX trades (versus the dollar's 88.5%) and **2.2%** of reserves (versus 57.8%). China runs capital controls that make the renminbi hard to hold freely, and reserve managers will not park serious money in a currency they cannot move out of at will. The renminbi is rising — from near zero — but it is a long, long way from challenging, and its share of reserves has barely budged in five years.

**Myth 4: "De-dollarization is happening fast."** The dollar's reserve share *has* fallen from ~71% (2001) to ~57.8% (2024) — that part is true. But the lost share scattered into small currencies and gold, *not* into a rival reserve currency. There is diversification at the margin, not a regime change. The dollar's share of *trade invoicing* and *FX trades* has barely moved at all. "Slow marginal diversification" is accurate; "the dollar is collapsing as the reserve currency" is not.

**Myth 5: "The dollar is strong because the U.S. economy is strong."** Partly, but the deeper reason is structural, not cyclical. The dollar is on 88% of trades and 58% of reserves because it is the world's *network hub* — the unit, the invoice, the funding, the reserve — and networks are sticky regardless of the year's GDP print. The U.S. is 24% of world GDP but the dollar is wildly over-represented relative to that. The economy explains the *cyclical swings* (the rate cycle moving the DXY around); the network effect explains the *structural dominance* that persists across cycles.

## How it shows up in real markets

Knowing what the DXY is — and isn't — changes how you read the tape. Here is how the distinction plays out in practice.

**Reading a "dollar move" correctly.** When the DXY jumps and the headline says "dollar surges," your first move should be to pull up EUR/USD, because more than half of any DXY move is the euro. In 2022, the famous DXY spike to 114.8 was *substantially* a euro story — the European energy shock (gas prices exploding after Russia's invasion of Ukraine) crushed the euro toward and below parity at the same time the Fed was hiking. The DXY read it as "dollar strength," but a chunk of it was "euro weakness from an energy crisis." Both forces were real, but they are different stories, and only by splitting the index can you tell which is driving. The pair-by-pair habit — never trusting a single index number — is the core discipline this whole series teaches.

**The DXY as a cross-asset signal.** Because the dollar is the world's funding and invoicing currency, the DXY behaves as a *global risk and liquidity gauge*, not just an FX price. A rising dollar tends to coincide with falling commodity prices (they are priced in dollars, so a stronger dollar mechanically lowers the local-currency price), tightening emerging-market conditions, and risk-off sentiment. This is the "dollar as cross-asset gravity" effect — the cross-asset track owns the allocation angle in [the dollar cross-asset gravity](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-dollar-cross-asset-gravity), and the macro track covers trading it in [trading the dollar](/blog/trading/macro-trading/trading-the-dollar-dxy-carry-dollar-smile). For our purposes, the point is that the DXY is watched far beyond FX desks precisely *because* the dollar's four jobs make it a barometer for everything.

**The 2022 emerging-market squeeze.** When the dollar ripped higher in 2022, country after country felt the funding-role pain from the worked example above. Sri Lanka defaulted. Pakistan, Egypt, and Ghana hit IMF programs or restructurings. The 2022 cycle was a textbook demonstration that a strong dollar is a global tightening — transmitted through the dollar's funding role, not through the DXY's euro-heavy basket. The currencies that got crushed (the rupee, the cedi, the rupiah) are not even *in* the index. To see the stress you had to look past the DXY at the broad trade-weighted dollar and at individual emerging-market pairs.

**The Plaza Accord template.** Whenever the dollar gets *too* strong and starts damaging the global economy, the question of coordinated intervention resurfaces — a "Plaza 2.0." It has been floated repeatedly (most recently around the 2022 spike and again in 2025 debates about the dollar's level). The 1985 precedent shows it is *possible* but requires the world's major governments to agree and act together, which is rare. The DXY's all-time high of ~123 in 1985 stands as the marker of how far the dollar can run before the political will to reverse it appears.

**The discipline that follows from all of this.** Put the index and the institution together and a practical rule of reading emerges, one you can apply to every dollar headline you will ever see. When the story is *cyclical* — the Fed is hiking, the dollar is rising, risk assets are wobbling — the DXY is a perfectly good shorthand, because the cyclical swings really are dominated by the dollar-versus-developed-world rate gap that the index captures. But when the story is *structural* — "is the dollar losing its reserve status," "is de-dollarization here," "how strong is the dollar really" — the DXY is the wrong instrument, and you should reach instead for the BIS trade shares (88%), the IMF reserve mix (57.8%), the invoicing data (~50% of trade), and the Fed's broad trade-weighted index. Cyclical questions: use the index. Structural questions: use the four jobs. A reader who internalizes that one split will avoid the great majority of bad takes about the dollar, because almost every bad take comes from using the cyclical instrument to answer a structural question — pointing at a wiggling DXY chart to argue about the dollar's century-long role. The chart is real; it is simply answering a different question than the one being asked.

**Trading the index itself.** The DXY is genuinely tradeable — ICE lists DXY futures, and ETFs (most famously UUP) track it — which is part of why its fixed, century-stable basket survives despite being a poor trade-weighted measure. A liquid, unchanging benchmark is *useful for trading* even when it is *misleading for economics*. That tension — useful instrument, misleading thermometer — is the whole story of the dollar index in one line. For the broader system view of why the dollar runs markets, the macro track's [dollar system](/blog/trading/macro-trading/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy) post and the [petrodollar](/blog/trading/finance/petrodollar-and-dollar-dominance) history are the natural next reads.

## Reading the DXY: the stack that explains everything

Pull the threads together with the picture that makes the index's nature unmistakable. Stack the DXY's six currencies by weight, and one block towers over the rest.

![Stack of the DXY basket by weight euro 57.6 percent dominating yen pound Canadian dollar krona franc](/imgs/blogs/the-dxy-and-the-dollars-special-role-in-the-world-8.png)

That one tall block — the euro at 57.6% — is the index. Everything below it is a thin garnish. The way to read the DXY, then, is: *it is a euro index wearing a dollar costume.* When it moves, look to EUR/USD first, the yen and pound second, and assume nothing about the peso, the renminbi, or any emerging market, because they are not represented at all.

And then — this is the crucial mental separation — set the index aside and remember that the *dollar's* importance has nothing to do with that stack. The dollar matters because it is the unit the world prices in, the invoice the world trades on, the debt the world borrows in, and the reserve the world saves in. Those four jobs put a dollar on one side of 88% of all trades. The DXY is a narrow, tradeable, century-stable proxy that catches the dollar's *direction against Europe*; the four jobs are the dollar's *substance against the planet*. Confusing the two is the single most common mistake in currency commentary, and you no longer have to make it.

## The takeaway: index versus institution

Here is the distinction to carry out of this post, because it will sharpen everything else you read about the dollar.

**The DXY is an index. The dollar is an institution.** The index is a fixed, 57.6%-euro, six-currency basket frozen on a 1973 trade snapshot — useful as a liquid benchmark and a regime gauge against the developed world, misleading as a measure of the dollar's global value. Use it as a compass for direction, never as a ruler for magnitude, and always crack it open into EUR/USD plus the rest before you believe a "the dollar did X" headline.

The institution is the four-job machine: unit of account, invoicing currency, funding currency, reserve. Those jobs reinforce each other into a flywheel that puts the dollar on 88% of all FX trades despite the U.S. being only 24% of world GDP. That over-representation is the dollar's exorbitant privilege, and it is why dollar conditions in New York set financial conditions in Lagos, Jakarta, and São Paulo — none of which appear on the DXY.

This connects straight back to the spine of the series. You never own *a currency* in isolation: every position is a relative bet of one money against another, driven by the rate gap and capital flows. The DXY is that idea made into a single tradeable instrument — six relative bets bundled together, dominated by one. And the dollar's special role is *why* those relative bets behave the way they do: because almost every pair on earth is, in the end, a dollar pair. Read the index for the regime, read the four jobs for the gravity, and never let the ticker convince you it is the whole dollar. It is just the euro, in a costume.
