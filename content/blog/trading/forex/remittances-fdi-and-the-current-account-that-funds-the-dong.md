> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A managed-float emerging-market currency like the Vietnamese dong is held up not by a central bank's willpower but by a steady, structural stream of dollars flowing into the country every year.
>
> - Three big, sticky inflows fund the dong: remittances (about \$16 billion a year), disbursed foreign direct investment (about \$25 billion), and a goods trade surplus (around \$25 billion in recent years).
> - Those inflows more than offset the country's services and investment-income deficits, so on net dollars pile up — and the central bank can buy them, printing dong, to rebuild its reserve war chest.
> - The crucial distinction is **sticky money versus hot money**: remittances, FDI, and exports are dependable; portfolio flows chase yield and bolt at the first scare. A currency funded by sticky money is far harder to break.
> - The one number to remember: Vietnam ran a current-account surplus of roughly **+4.8% of GDP in 2024** — one of the largest in the world, and the deepest reason the dong's depreciation is a slow managed crawl rather than a crash.

On a normal Tuesday in mid-2022, a garment exporter in Bình Dương province shipped a container of t-shirts to a buyer in Ohio. Three weeks later, the dollars landed in the exporter's bank account in Ho Chi Minh City. The exporter did not want dollars — its workers, its landlord, and the tax authority all want dong. So the bank sold those dollars into the local market, and somewhere in the chain the State Bank of Vietnam bought a slice of them, handing over freshly created dong and tucking the dollars into the nation's reserves. Multiply that single container by millions of transactions a year — exports, factory investments, money wired home by Vietnamese working abroad — and you have the machinery that actually holds a currency up. No single trade matters; the *aggregate* of all of them, running in one direction year after year, is what sets the floor under the dong.

This is the part of the foreign-exchange story that gets least attention and matters most. Markets obsess over the interest-rate differential, the carry trade, the central bank's daily reference rate. But underneath all of that sits a far more boring, far more powerful force: the *balance of payments* — the running tally of every dollar that enters or leaves a country. For an emerging market, the balance of payments is the bedrock. A currency with a structural dollar surplus can be steered gently down a managed path for years. A currency with a structural dollar deficit is one bad headline away from a run.

The series spine says you never own "a currency" in isolation — every position is a relative bet, driven by the gap between two countries' interest rates *plus the flow of money across borders*. This post is about that second half: the flows. Specifically, the flows that fund an emerging-market currency from below. We will build Vietnam's balance of payments piece by piece, put real numbers on every flow, and show exactly how a country with no enormous interest-rate advantage nonetheless keeps its currency from falling apart. The answer, it turns out, is that the dong is funded by people sending money home, factories being built, and t-shirts crossing the Pacific.

![Vietnam remittances and FDI disbursed as grouped bars, 2018 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/remittances-fdi-and-the-current-account-that-funds-the-dong-1.png)

Look at that chart for a second before we go on. Two inflows, each running in the \$16–25 billion range, *every single year*, through a pandemic, through a global tightening cycle, through a domestic banking scare. That dependability is the whole story. Now let us take it apart.

## Foundations: The flows that fund a currency

Before we can talk about Vietnam, we need the plumbing. A currency is the local unit of account — dong in Vietnam, pesos in Mexico, rand in South Africa. The exchange rate is the price of that local money in dollars (or the price of a dollar in local money, which is the more common quote in emerging markets: "25,450 dong per dollar"). That price moves for two broad reasons. One is the *price of holding* the currency — the interest-rate differential, the carry, the thing that fixed-income and macro own and that we link out to rather than re-derive. The other is the *flow of the currency* — how many dollars are actually being bought and sold against the local money, and which way the net pressure runs. This post is about the second reason.

The accounting framework for cross-border flows is the **balance of payments** (BoP). It is a country's external bank statement: every transaction with the rest of the world, recorded as either a dollar coming in (a credit) or a dollar going out (a debit). It has two big halves.

The **current account** records flows tied to *current* economic activity — selling and buying goods and services, earning and paying investment income, and transfers like remittances. The **financial account** (older texts call its near-twin the capital account; we will use "financial account" for the modern flows) records *changes in ownership of assets* — foreigners building factories here, buying our bonds, or lending to our banks, and us doing the same abroad. The deep fact, almost a tautology, is that these two accounts must sum to the change in official reserves. Every dollar that comes in for one reason has to go *somewhere* — pay for an import, buy a foreign asset, or sit in the central bank's reserves. The books always balance. That is why it is called the balance of payments.

Let us define the four flows that will do all the work in this post.

**Remittances** are money sent home by people working abroad — a Vietnamese nurse in Japan, a construction worker in South Korea, a software engineer in California wiring money to family in Hải Phòng. These are *transfers*: nothing is sold, no asset changes hands, the dollars simply arrive as a gift to the household. They land in the current account.

**Foreign direct investment** (FDI) is a foreigner taking a lasting ownership stake in productive capacity here — Samsung building a phone factory in Thái Nguyên, a Japanese firm opening a logistics hub, a Singaporean fund buying a controlling stake in a Vietnamese company. The key word is *direct*: the investor is building or buying something physical and operational, not just parking money in a tradable security. FDI lands in the financial account. We care about *disbursed* FDI — dollars that actually arrived and were spent — not *registered* or *pledged* FDI, which is a press-release number that may never show up.

**The trade balance** is exports minus imports. When a country sells more goods abroad than it buys, it runs a *trade surplus* — a net inflow of dollars. Vietnam, as a manufacturing-export hub, runs a large and growing goods surplus. That surplus lands in the current account.

**The services and income deficit** is the offset. Vietnam imports more services than it exports (shipping, insurance, tourism in some years, software-as-a-service, consulting), and — crucially — it pays out a large stream of *investment income* to the foreigners who own all those factories. When Samsung earns a profit in Vietnam and sends a dividend home to Seoul, that is a dollar leaving the country. The more FDI a country has attracted, the bigger this income-outflow becomes over time. This deficit is a debit in the current account, partly offsetting the trade surplus.

Net them and you get the current-account balance. Add the financial account (FDI and portfolio flows) and you get the overall balance of payments, which lands as a change in reserves. Here is the whole stack as one picture.

![Stack of Vietnam dollar inflows and the deficits that offset them](/imgs/blogs/remittances-fdi-and-the-current-account-that-funds-the-dong-2.png)

The shape of that stack is the thesis of this entire post. Three fat green inflows — remittances, FDI, the trade surplus — pile up. One red bar — the services-and-income deficit — eats into them. What is left over is a net inflow of dollars, and *that* is what the central bank can buy to rebuild reserves and what ultimately holds the dong up. A country whose green bars outweigh its red bar has a currency funded from below. A country whose red bar swallows the green has a currency funded by the kindness of strangers — by foreigners willing to keep lending — and that kindness can vanish overnight.

If you want the formal accounting of the current account as a standalone topic — the exact line items, how a deficit is financed, why the US can run a permanent deficit and Vietnam cannot — that lives in the sibling post on [the balance of payments and the current account](/blog/trading/forex/the-balance-of-payments-and-the-current-account). Here we keep our eyes on one country and one question: what holds the dong up?

#### Worked example: Vietnam's balance of payments on the back of an envelope

Let us add up the structural inflows for a representative recent year and see what they imply for reserves. Take round numbers in the range the data supports:

- Remittances: **+\$16B** (workers abroad wiring money home).
- FDI disbursed: **+\$25B** (factories actually being built and funded).
- Goods trade surplus: **+\$25B** (exports minus imports of goods).

That is **+\$66B** of structural dollar inflow before we subtract anything. Now the offsets. Vietnam runs a sizeable *services* deficit (shipping, insurance, some travel) and, more importantly, a large *primary-income* deficit — the profits and dividends the foreign factory owners repatriate. Call the combined services-and-income drain **−\$30B** in a heavy year. Also subtract some net portfolio outflow and errors-and-omissions — call it **−\$15B** in a stress year when foreigners are pulling money out of Vietnamese stocks and bonds.

Net it out: \$66B − \$30B − \$15B = **+\$21B**. That is the overall balance-of-payments surplus — the net pile of dollars the system threw off this year. The central bank can buy roughly that amount, handing over newly created dong, and add it to reserves. In a *good* year (smaller income outflow, foreigners buying rather than selling Vietnamese assets), the surplus is even bigger; in a *bad* year (2022, when capital fled and the income drain was heavy), the net can flip negative and the central bank must *sell* reserves instead. The intuition: the three sticky green inflows are big enough that even a rough year only dents reserves rather than emptying them.

## Remittances: money sent home, the most loyal dollar inflow

Of the three structural inflows, remittances are the strangest and the most underrated. They are dollars that arrive with no strings, no expectation of return, and almost no sensitivity to the things that move markets. A Vietnamese worker in Osaka sends \$500 a month to her parents in Nghệ An because they are her parents, not because the Vietnamese ten-year bond yields 50 basis points more than it did last quarter. That insensitivity is exactly what makes remittances such powerful currency support: they are the least flighty dollars a country can have.

Vietnam is one of the world's top remittance recipients in absolute dollars and a standout relative to its economy. The flow has run steadily in the \$16–19 billion range for years. To put that in perspective, \$16 billion is roughly 4–5% of Vietnam's GDP arriving as a pure gift from the diaspora — money that requires no exports, no factory, no asset sale. It just shows up.

The mechanics of how a remittance supports the currency are worth slowing down on. When the nurse in Osaka sends \$500 home, the receiving family in Nghệ An does not spend dollars at the local market — they need dong. So the dollars are sold (through the bank, the money-transfer operator, or the informal channel) and dong are bought. Every remitted dollar is, at the margin, a small bid for dong and a small offer of dollars. Sixteen billion dollars a year of that pressure is a persistent, structural tailwind under the local currency. It is the opposite of a speculative attack: instead of millions of traders simultaneously *selling* the currency, you have millions of families steadily *buying* it.

#### Worked example: what \$16 billion of remittances does to the dong

Suppose, in a calm year, the only flows in the entire balance of payments were remittances — \$16 billion of dollars arriving to be converted into dong. At a rate of 25,450 dong per dollar, that is 16,000,000,000 × 25,450 ≈ **407 trillion dong** of demand for the local currency, met by an offer of \$16 billion. If nothing else were happening, that one-directional flow would push the dong *stronger* — fewer dong per dollar — until the central bank stepped in to buy the surplus dollars and stop the appreciation. The point is not the precise number; it is the direction. Sixteen billion dollars a year of remittance conversion is a standing bid for dong large enough that, on its own, it would tend to *strengthen* the currency. That is why countries with big diasporas — the Philippines, Mexico, Pakistan, Vietnam — have currencies that are structurally better supported than their interest rates or trade balances alone would suggest. The diaspora is a permanent buyer.

There is a subtlety markets sometimes miss. Remittances are not perfectly constant — they dipped in 2023 as Vietnamese workers abroad faced their own cost-of-living squeeze and as some flows shifted channels — but the *variation* is tiny compared to portfolio flows, which can swing by tens of billions in a single quarter. A 10% wobble in a \$16 billion remittance flow is \$1.6 billion. A risk-off episode can yank \$5–10 billion of hot money out of an emerging market in weeks. Remittances are the keel of the boat; hot money is the wind that heels it over.

There is also a *measurement* wrinkle that matters for anyone reading the official data. A meaningful share of remittances arrives through informal channels — hand-carried cash, money-transfer operators, hawala-style networks — that the official balance-of-payments statistics capture imperfectly. The headline ~\$16 billion is almost certainly an *undercount* of the true diaspora support, which means the structural inflow holding the dong up is, if anything, larger than the data shows. The same is partly true of the gold and dollar-cash hoarding that has long been a feature of Vietnamese household saving: a slice of remittance dollars never gets converted to dong at all, but sits as dollar cash or gold, which is its own form of demand for hard currency. For a reader, the lesson is that the *reported* remittance figure is a floor on the real support, not a ceiling — a reason to lean toward the optimistic side when judging the dong's structural funding.

The "so what" for reading the currency: when you assess any emerging-market currency, find the remittance number first. A country receiving 3–5% of GDP in remittances has a built-in shock absorber that a country with no diaspora simply does not. It is the cheapest, most reliable form of currency support on earth, and it costs the central bank nothing.

## Foreign direct investment: dollars that bolt themselves to the ground

If remittances are the loyal inflow, FDI is the *committed* one — and for Vietnam, it is the headline act. Disbursed FDI has climbed steadily and crossed roughly \$25 billion in 2024, the highest on record. This is the flow that built the Vietnam growth story: Samsung, LG, Foxconn, Intel, and a long tail of suppliers relocating manufacturing from China into Vietnam over the past decade, accelerated by the US–China trade tensions of the late 2010s. Each of those factories is a wall of incoming dollars — the foreign parent wires money in to buy land, pour concrete, install machines, and hire workers.

The word that makes FDI special is *direct*. A foreign portfolio investor buying \$100 million of Vietnamese stocks can sell them tomorrow and wire the dollars home; the position is liquid and the investor is a tourist. A foreign firm that has spent \$2 billion building a chip-assembly plant in Bắc Ninh cannot pack it up and leave when the VIX spikes. The capital is *bolted to the ground*. That stickiness is why FDI is treated as the gold standard of capital inflow: it funds the currency, it does not flee in a panic, and it brings jobs, technology, and exports as a bonus. A dollar of FDI is worth far more to currency stability than a dollar of portfolio inflow, even though they look identical on the day they arrive.

![Vietnam FDI disbursed bar chart shares the cover; trade surplus bars 2019 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/remittances-fdi-and-the-current-account-that-funds-the-dong-4.png)

There is a second-order effect that is easy to miss and that closes a loop in the balance of payments. FDI does not just bring dollars in; it *changes the future shape of every other flow*. Those Samsung and Foxconn factories are not built to serve the Vietnamese market — they are built to export. So today's FDI becomes tomorrow's trade surplus: the factories that foreign dollars built now ship phones and electronics to the world, earning yet more dollars. Electronics is now Vietnam's single largest export category, and it exists because of a decade of FDI. The inflows compound: FDI funds the factory, the factory funds the trade surplus, the trade surplus funds the reserves.

But the same FDI also *creates the offsetting outflow* we mentioned in the foundations. Once those factories are profitable, the foreign owners repatriate profits — dividends flowing back to Seoul and Tokyo. That is the primary-income debit in the current account, and it grows as the FDI stock grows. This is the quiet tax on being an FDI-funded economy: you import the capital cheaply, but you export the profits forever. It is a good trade — Vietnam keeps the jobs, the wages, the export base, and the technology transfer — but it means the *gross* FDI inflow overstates the net support, because some of it leaves again as income.

#### Worked example: FDI in, dividends out — the net dollar support

Suppose a year brings **+\$25B** of disbursed FDI. That is \$25 billion of fresh dollars entering the financial account. But the *existing stock* of foreign-owned factories — built up over fifteen years — earns profits, and say **\$12B** of those profits are repatriated as dividends (a primary-income outflow in the current account). The *net* dollar support from the foreign-investment relationship this year is \$25B − \$12B = **+\$13B**, not the headline \$25B.

Now extend it. As the FDI stock keeps growing, the dividend outflow grows with it. In a future year, suppose disbursed FDI is still \$25B but accumulated factories now repatriate \$18B. Net support falls to **+\$7B** even though the headline inflow is unchanged. The lesson: a maturing FDI-funded economy must keep *attracting new* FDI just to stand still against its rising income outflow — or replace it with a bigger trade surplus. The dong's structural support depends not on the FDI headline but on FDI *minus* the income it eventually sends home. This is why analysts watch the *primary-income balance*, not just the FDI press releases.

The takeaway for reading the dong: FDI disbursement is the single most important medium-term flow to track. Rising disbursed FDI means more factories, more future exports, and a deeper structural bid for the currency. A *stall* in FDI — if the China-plus-one relocation theme faded, or if a global recession froze capex — would be the first warning sign that the dong's foundation was weakening, long before it showed up in the spot rate. For how foreign equity flows specifically interact with the Vietnamese market and the index, see [foreign flows, ETFs, and the index effect in Vietnam](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/foreign-flows-etfs-and-the-index-effect-vietnam).

## The trade surplus: t-shirts and phones, converted into currency support

The third structural inflow is the goods trade surplus, and for Vietnam it has become enormous. After decades of running trade *deficits* — importing machinery and materials it could not yet make — Vietnam tipped into persistent surplus in the late 2010s, and the surplus has ballooned: roughly \$28 billion in 2023 and \$25 billion in 2024 on goods. That surplus is the export economy throwing off dollars faster than the country spends them on imports.

A trade surplus funds a currency through the most direct channel there is. Exporters earn dollars abroad; they must pay domestic costs — wages, rent, taxes, local suppliers — in dong; so they sell their dollar receipts and buy dong. A \$25 billion goods surplus means the export sector, *in aggregate and on net*, is a \$25 billion-a-year seller of dollars and buyer of dong. It is the same mechanical bid we saw with remittances, but sourced from factories instead of families.

What makes Vietnam's surplus distinctive — and a little fragile — is its concentration. A huge share of it is electronics and machinery produced by the foreign-owned factories that FDI built. This is the loop again: FDI built the factories, the factories generate the trade surplus, the trade surplus supports the dong. The strength is real — but it is *thin* in the sense that it rests heavily on a handful of multinational supply chains. If Samsung shifted a product line elsewhere, or a global electronics downturn hit, the trade surplus would contract fast. A surplus built on a thousand small domestic exporters would be more robust than one built on a few foreign giants. Vietnam's is the latter, which is why the surplus is large but its quality is debated.

#### Worked example: a trade surplus turned into reserves

Take a year with a goods trade surplus of **+\$25B**. That is \$25 billion of dollars earned abroad that must be converted to dong to pay domestic costs. At 25,450 dong per dollar, the export sector generates demand for 25,000,000,000 × 25,450 ≈ **636 trillion dong**, met by an offer of \$25 billion into the local FX market.

Now suppose imports of *services* run a \$10B deficit and the country pays \$12B of investment income abroad — total current-account drains of **−\$22B**. The *current-account* balance is then \$16B remittances + \$25B trade surplus − \$22B services-and-income = **+\$19B**. That is a current-account surplus of roughly 4–5% of GDP, squarely in the range the data shows for Vietnam. A current-account surplus is, definitionally, the country earning more dollars from current activity than it spends — a *net* seller of dollars, a *net* buyer of its own currency. The dong is being bought, in net, by the real economy, year after year. That is what "the current account funds the dong" means in one line.

The strategic read: the trade surplus is the most visible of the three inflows — it prints monthly in the customs data and gets headlines — but it is also the most cyclical. Exports rise and fall with global demand. In a worldwide slowdown, the trade surplus shrinks first, the income outflow keeps draining, and the current account narrows. That is exactly when an emerging-market currency comes under pressure: the structural bid thins out just as the global mood turns risk-off. The trade surplus is the inflow you most need to watch over the *cycle*, while remittances and FDI are the inflows that carry you *through* it.

## Where Vietnam sits: a current-account surplus most of the world envies

Pull the three inflows and the offsets together and you get the headline that captures everything: Vietnam runs a **current-account surplus of about +4.8% of GDP** (2024 estimate). That single number is the deepest reason the dong does not behave like a crisis currency.

![Current account as percent of GDP across countries, 2024, Vietnam highlighted](/imgs/blogs/remittances-fdi-and-the-current-account-that-funds-the-dong-6.png)

Set that against the company it keeps. The United States runs a current-account *deficit* of around −3.3% of GDP — it consumes more than it produces and imports the difference, financed by the world's appetite for dollar assets. The UK runs −2.7%. These deficits are sustainable only because foreigners keep choosing to hold those currencies' bonds and stocks. Vietnam is on the *other* side of the ledger entirely, in the surplus camp with Japan (+3.6%), the euro area (+2.9%), and Switzerland (+6.2%) — countries that, on net, *send* capital to the rest of the world.

That is a remarkable place for an emerging market to be. The textbook EM crisis — Thailand 1997, Turkey on repeat, Argentina forever — is a *deficit* story: the country spends more dollars than it earns, finances the gap with foreign borrowing and hot money, and then the financing dries up and the currency collapses. A country running a current-account *surplus* is, by construction, not in that trap. It is not dependent on foreigners' willingness to keep lending; it is generating its own dollars. The surplus is the structural moat around the dong.

Now we can connect this directly to the spine of the series. An exchange rate is set at the margin by the gap between the dollars being supplied and the dollars being demanded. A current-account surplus means the *real economy* is, on net, supplying dollars and demanding dong — a permanent, slow, structural bid under the local currency. The interest-rate differential (which fixed-income owns — see [interest rate differentials, the master variable of FX](/blog/trading/forex/interest-rate-differentials-the-master-variable-of-fx)) sets the speculative, fast-money pressure. The current account sets the structural, slow-money pressure. For Vietnam the two point in *opposite* directions: the rate differential is often *unfavorable* (dong rates are not high enough above dollar rates to compensate for depreciation risk, which pushes the dong down), but the current-account surplus is *favorable* (a structural bid, which holds it up). The managed crawl you observe — the dong sliding gently lower year after year rather than crashing or rallying — is the visible resolution of those two opposing forces.

#### Worked example: surplus versus deficit, the same shock

Picture two emerging markets hit by the same global shock — the Fed hikes hard and risk-off grips the world, pulling **\$10B** of portfolio money out of each.

Country A runs a current-account **deficit** of −5% of GDP. It was *already* relying on foreign capital to fund its overspending. The \$10B outflow removes financing it cannot replace; the central bank must sell reserves or let the currency fall, and with thin reserves it usually falls. This is the classic sudden stop.

Country B is Vietnam, running a current-account **surplus** of +5% of GDP — say **+\$20B** a year of structural inflow. The \$10B portfolio outflow is painful, but the structural surplus is still throwing off +\$20B; the *net* is +\$10B, still positive. The central bank can let reserves dip modestly to smooth the move, then rebuild as the structural inflows keep arriving. The currency bends, it does not break.

Same shock, opposite outcomes — and the only difference is the sign of the current account. That is why "+4.8% of GDP" is the one number to remember about the dong. For the mechanics of how that portfolio outflow becomes a crisis when there is *no* surplus to cushion it, see the sibling post on [the carry into emerging markets and the sudden stop](/blog/trading/forex/the-carry-into-emerging-markets-and-the-sudden-stop).

## How the inflows become reserves: the central bank's war chest refills

We have established that Vietnam, on net, earns more dollars than it spends. The question is: where do the surplus dollars go? The answer is the central bank's reserves — and the mechanism is the heart of how a managed-float currency actually works.

![Pipeline from exporters earning dollars to SBV adding to reserves](/imgs/blogs/remittances-fdi-and-the-current-account-that-funds-the-dong-3.png)

Follow the dollars step by step. Exporters and remittance-receiving families and FDI projects all end up holding dollars they do not want — they need dong. They sell those dollars to commercial banks. In aggregate, the banking system ends up with *more* dollars than its customers want to buy, because the country is running a surplus. The banks sell that surplus to the State Bank of Vietnam. The SBV buys the dollars and, to pay for them, creates dong. Those dollars go into the official reserves; the dong enters the domestic banking system. The reserve war chest refills.

This is the same operation a managed-float central bank runs the world over: it stands in the market and absorbs the structural dollar surplus to stop its currency from appreciating too fast, parking the dollars as reserves. (The full toolkit — how the SBV sets a daily reference rate, runs a trading band, and intervenes on both sides — is the subject of the sibling post on [USD/VND and the managed float, how the SBV runs the dong](/blog/trading/forex/usd-vnd-and-the-managed-float-how-the-sbv-runs-the-dong); the reserves-as-a-weapon angle is covered in [FX reserves and intervention in an emerging market](/blog/trading/forex/fx-reserves-and-intervention-in-an-emerging-market). Here we focus on where the reserves *come from*.) The point for this post is the direction of causation: the reserves are not a gift, a loan, or a magic trick. They are the *accumulated surplus of the balance of payments*. A country that earns a structural dollar surplus can build reserves out of thin air — well, out of the dong it prints to buy the surplus dollars. A country that runs a deficit must *borrow* its reserves, and borrowed reserves are a far weaker defense.

But reserves are not a one-way ratchet, and Vietnam's history shows both directions vividly.

![Vietnam FX reserves line chart 2016 to 2024 with the 2022 drawdown marked](/imgs/blogs/remittances-fdi-and-the-current-account-that-funds-the-dong-8.png)

Look at the arc. Reserves climbed from around \$41 billion in 2016 to roughly \$109 billion by 2021 — six years of the structural surplus doing its quiet work, the central bank mopping up the dollar inflows and stacking them away. Then 2022 happened: the Fed hiked at the fastest pace in forty years, the dollar surged globally (the DXY hit a two-decade high — see [the dollar as a wrecking ball for emerging markets](/blog/trading/forex/the-dollar-as-a-wrecking-ball-for-emerging-markets)), portfolio money fled emerging markets, and the dong came under heavy pressure. The SBV did the opposite of accumulation: it *sold* reserves to defend the dong, drawing the stockpile down from ~\$109 billion to ~\$87 billion in a single year. That is the war chest being spent in a crisis. Since then it has partially rebuilt, but it sits below its peak and, on a months-of-imports basis, around the low-but-not-comfortable line of roughly three months.

#### Worked example: the surplus refilling reserves, then a crisis draining them

In a normal year, suppose the overall balance of payments throws off a **+\$15B** surplus (structural inflows beating the offsets). The SBV buys roughly that \$15B of surplus dollars and reserves rise by about \$15 billion — from, say, \$95B to \$110B. The central bank prints the dong to pay for it; that dong creation is a side-effect it manages with its other tools. The war chest grows because the country earned the dollars.

Now a crisis year: the Fed hikes, **\$20B** of capital flees, and even the structural +\$15B inflow cannot cover it — the net balance of payments is +\$15B − \$20B = **−\$5B**, a deficit. Worse, the dong is under speculative attack, so to keep the slide orderly the SBV *sells* dollars from reserves to meet the excess demand. Suppose it sells \$22B over the year. Reserves fall from \$109B to roughly \$87B — exactly the 2022 drawdown. The lesson in one line: the structural surplus *fills* the war chest in good years and *cushions* the drain in bad ones, but a big enough shock can still force a defensive sale. Reserves are the buffer between the slow structural inflows and the fast speculative outflows — and the bigger the structural surplus, the rarely the buffer gets tested.

The strategic read on reserves: never look at the *level* in isolation. Look at the *flow* — is the structural surplus large enough to rebuild reserves faster than a typical shock can drain them? For Vietnam, the answer through the 2016–2021 build was clearly yes; the 2022 drawdown was the structural inflows being temporarily overwhelmed by a once-in-a-generation dollar surge; and the partial rebuild since is the surplus reasserting itself. A currency whose reserves *rebuild* after a shock is fundamentally sound. A currency whose reserves never recover is on a one-way path to a crisis.

## Sticky money versus hot money: the distinction that decides everything

We have used the words "structural" and "sticky" repeatedly. Now let us make the distinction precise, because it is the single most important lens for reading any emerging-market currency, and it is what separates a managed crawl from a crash.

![Before and after comparison of hot money flows versus structural inflows](/imgs/blogs/remittances-fdi-and-the-current-account-that-funds-the-dong-5.png)

On one side is **hot money** — portfolio flows. Foreigners buying Vietnamese stocks and bonds because the yield looks attractive relative to the dollar, the carry into the emerging market (a topic the carry posts own). This money is *liquid* and *opinionated*: it arrives when the EM story is hot and the carry is juicy, and it leaves the instant the story sours or the Fed makes dollars more attractive at home. It can reverse in days. A risk-off episode — a Fed surprise, a regional banking scare, a geopolitical shock — sends every hot-money manager toward the same exit simultaneously. The flow does not just stop; it reverses, turning a tailwind into a headwind in a single week. That synchronized reversal is the *sudden stop*, and it is the mechanism behind essentially every emerging-market currency crisis.

On the other side is **sticky money** — the three flows this post is about. Remittances arrive because of family, not yield; a nurse does not stop supporting her parents because the VIX spiked. FDI is bolted to the ground; a \$2 billion factory does not get wired home in a panic. The trade surplus comes from real exports of real goods that the world keeps buying through the cycle. None of these three flows reverses on a risk-off headline. They are the keel. They are why a country can ride out a storm that would capsize a currency funded by hot money alone.

The practical test, when you size up any emerging-market currency, is to ask: *what funds it?* Split the inflows into the sticky bucket and the hot bucket. A currency funded mostly by sticky money — Vietnam, with its remittances, FDI, and trade surplus — has a structural floor. A currency funded mostly by hot money — many higher-yielding EMs that lure in carry traders with double-digit rates — is living on borrowed time, dependent on the carry trade staying in fashion. The same headline current-account number can hide completely different risk: a +2% current account funded by sticky exports is a fortress; a −2% current account plastered over by hot portfolio inflows is a house of cards.

#### Worked example: same country, two funding mixes

Take two versions of an emerging market, each with a balance of payments that nets to **+\$10B** of inflow this year — identical on the surface.

*Version 1 (sticky-funded):* +\$16B remittances, +\$25B FDI, +\$25B trade surplus, −\$30B services-and-income, −\$26B... no — let us keep it clean: structural inflows net to +\$10B, and portfolio flows are roughly flat. When the Fed hikes and \$8B of any hot money present tries to leave, there is barely any hot money *to* leave; the structural +\$10B keeps arriving; the currency dips a percent or two and steadies. This is Vietnam's profile.

*Version 2 (hot-funded):* the country runs a current-account *deficit* of −\$15B, plastered over by +\$25B of portfolio inflows chasing a 12% local interest rate — netting to the same +\$10B. When the Fed hikes and the carry trade unwinds, that \$25B of hot money does not just stop; \$15–20B of it *reverses*. The net balance of payments swings from +\$10B to roughly **−\$10B or worse** in a quarter. The currency is in free fall, the central bank burns reserves, rates get jacked to 20% to defend, and a crisis is underway.

Same headline +\$10B, opposite fate — because one is sticky and one is hot. The composition of the funding matters more than the level. When you read an EM currency, read the *funding mix*, not just the balance. For the formal anatomy of how that hot-money reversal becomes a self-reinforcing attack, the game-theory treatment lives in [bank runs as coordination games](/blog/trading/game-theory/bank-runs-as-coordination-games-diamond-dybvig-and-svb).

## The balance-of-payments identity, drawn out

To close the foundations, let us make the accounting fully explicit, because the identity is what guarantees the books balance and it is where a lot of intuition comes from.

![Graph of Vietnam balance of payments components flowing into the change in reserves](/imgs/blogs/remittances-fdi-and-the-current-account-that-funds-the-dong-7.png)

Read the picture left to right. On the far left are the raw flows. Remittances (+\$16B) and the trade surplus (+\$25B) are credits; the services-and-income bill (−\$30B) is a debit. These three feed the **current account**, which nets to roughly +\$15B for Vietnam — a surplus. Separately, FDI disbursed (+\$25B) and the volatile portfolio-and-other flows feed the **financial account**. Both accounts then flow into the **change in reserves**, which is the leftover — the figure that makes the whole thing sum to zero.

That last point is the identity, and it is worth stating as a near-law: *the current account plus the financial account equals the change in official reserves* (with a statistical-discrepancy line for measurement error). It is true by construction. Every dollar that enters the country for any reason must end up somewhere — spent on an import, used to buy a foreign asset, or held by the central bank as reserves. There is no fourth option. So if you know two of the three pieces, you know the third. If a country runs a current-account surplus and is *not* attracting net financial inflows, then reserves *must* be rising — there is nowhere else for the surplus dollars to go. If a country runs a current-account deficit and reserves are stable, then it *must* be financing the deficit with net financial inflows (borrowing or selling assets) — there is no other way to pay for the imports.

This identity is the reason the balance of payments is such a powerful diagnostic. You can take any EM currency, find its current-account balance, find its net financial flows, and the change in reserves is determined. From the *signs* alone you can read the story: surplus-current-account plus reserve-accumulation is the strong-currency profile (Vietnam, China, the Asian surplus economies); deficit-current-account plus reserve-loss is the crisis profile (the country is spending dollars it does not earn and cannot finance, and the war chest is draining). The dong sits firmly in the first camp.

#### Worked example: solving for reserves from the identity

Suppose for a year you know two of the three pieces. The current account is a **+\$19B** surplus (we computed this earlier: +\$16B remittances + \$25B trade − \$22B services-and-income). The financial account nets to **−\$4B** this year because foreign portfolio investors pulled money out of Vietnamese stocks faster than FDI came in — say +\$25B FDI but −\$29B of net portfolio-and-other outflow in a stressed year.

By the identity, the change in reserves = current account + financial account = +\$19B + (−\$4B) = **+\$15B**. Reserves *still rise* by about \$15 billion, *despite* the hot-money outflow, because the current-account surplus was big enough to more than cover it. That is the whole point of being a surplus country: even a bad year on the financial account leaves you accumulating reserves, as long as the structural current-account surplus holds. Flip the current account to a deficit and the same financial-account outflow would force reserves *down* — the difference between a country that weathers a shock and one that gets a crisis. The identity does the bookkeeping; the *sign of the current account* decides the outcome.

## Common misconceptions

**"A strong central bank is what holds an EM currency up."** Backwards. The central bank can *steer* and *smooth*, but it cannot *create* dollars — it can only spend the reserves it has accumulated from the structural surplus. The SBV's ~\$87 billion of reserves in 2022 existed because of years of trade surpluses, FDI, and remittances; the central bank was the custodian of that surplus, not its source. A central bank defending a currency that has *no* structural inflows behind it is just burning a finite war chest, and the market knows it — see Black Wednesday 1992, the [Soros versus the Bank of England](/blog/trading/forex/soros-and-black-wednesday-breaking-the-bank-of-england-1992) episode, where a central bank with no fundamental support lost in a day. The flows fund the currency; the central bank only meters them.

**"FDI and portfolio inflows are basically the same dollars."** They are identical on the day they arrive and completely different in a crisis. A \$25 billion FDI factory cannot leave when the VIX spikes; \$25 billion of portfolio inflow can be wired out in a week. Treating the two as equivalent is the most expensive mistake in EM analysis. The relevant question is never "how much capital is coming in?" but "how much of it is *bolted down*?" Vietnam's inflows are dominated by the bolted-down kind — that is the entire reason the dong crawls rather than crashes.

**"A trade surplus always means a strong currency."** The trade surplus is *one* of three inflows, and it can be swamped by the others. In 2022 Vietnam ran a goods surplus and the dong *still* fell against the dollar — because the dollar was surging globally, hot money was fleeing, and the income outflow was draining. A surplus is a structural tailwind, not a guarantee of appreciation in any given month. Over years it sets the floor; over weeks the rate differential and the global dollar (see [the dollar smile](/blog/trading/forex/the-dollar-smile-why-the-dollar-wins-in-boom-and-in-panic)) dominate. Confusing the slow force with the fast one is how people mis-time the dong.

**"Remittances are small and don't matter for markets."** At \$16 billion a year — roughly 4–5% of GDP — Vietnam's remittances are larger than its portfolio inflows in most years and far steadier. They are a bigger, more reliable source of currency support than the flows markets actually watch. The reason they get ignored is that they do not show up on a trading screen; they arrive through banks and money-transfer operators, not exchanges. But the dollar that a family in Nghệ An converts to dong props up the currency exactly as much as the dollar an exporter converts. Ignoring remittances means missing the single most loyal bid under the currency.

**"Reserves of three months of imports are plenty."** The "three months of imports" rule of thumb is a floor, not a comfort zone — and Vietnam's reserves have at times sat right around or below it. Three months is the minimum to cover essential imports if all financing stopped; it leaves *no* buffer to defend the currency against a speculative attack. A country with three months of reserves and a structural surplus is fine because the surplus keeps refilling the tank. A country with three months and a *deficit* is one shock from a crisis. The level only means something in the context of the *flows* refilling it.

## How it shows up in real markets

The clearest real-market demonstration is the contrast between Vietnam's experience in 2022 and the textbook EM crisis it did *not* become.

**2022 — the dollar wrecking ball, and why the dong only bent.** Through 2022 the Fed hiked from near zero to 4.5%, the DXY surged to a two-decade high near 114 in late September, and the rate differential turned sharply *against* the dong. Capital fled emerging markets across the board. The dong, which had been remarkably stable, depreciated — USD/VND moved from around 23,000 at the start of the year toward 24,000+, and the SBV widened its trading band and sold reserves to keep the slide orderly. Reserves fell from ~\$109 billion to ~\$87 billion. By the standards of a *deficit* EM, this would have been a full-blown crisis: currency down sharply, reserves gutted, rates spiking. But the dong's move was a *managed depreciation of a few percent*, not a collapse. The reason was the structural surplus underneath: even as hot money fled and the dollar surged, the trade surplus (a record ~\$12 billion that year on the way to \$28 billion in 2023), the FDI (\$22 billion disbursed), and the remittances (\$19 billion) kept arriving. The structural bid never disappeared; it was just temporarily overwhelmed by the dollar's strength. When the dollar peaked and rolled over into 2023, the structural inflows reasserted themselves, the dong stabilized, and reserves began to rebuild. The currency bent and recovered — the signature of a sticky-funded EM.

**2023 — the surplus reasserts itself.** As the dollar eased off its 2022 peak, Vietnam's balance of payments did exactly what the structural story predicts. The goods trade surplus *ballooned* to roughly \$28 billion — the highest ever — as imports fell faster than exports in a soft global patch (a "recessionary surplus," but a surplus nonetheless). FDI disbursement kept climbing toward \$23 billion. Even with remittances dipping to ~\$16 billion, the current account swung to a comfortable surplus, and the SBV could begin rebuilding the reserves it had spent in 2022. The contrast with a deficit EM is stark: a deficit country coming out of a 2022-style shock has to *attract* foreign capital back to rebuild reserves, on the market's terms; Vietnam just had to let its own structural surplus keep flowing in.

**2024 — record FDI, the structural story intact.** Disbursed FDI hit a record ~\$25.4 billion in 2024, the China-plus-one relocation theme still firmly in place, electronics exports still the backbone of the trade surplus (~\$25 billion). The dong continued its gentle managed crawl — USD/VND drifting toward 25,450 — depreciating modestly as the rate differential stayed unfavorable, but with no hint of a crisis, because the structural funding never wavered. The market read the dong correctly: a currency on a slow, managed downward path, anchored by one of the world's larger current-account surpluses, not a currency at risk of breaking.

It is worth placing Vietnam alongside its surplus peers to see the pattern, because the dong is not unique — it is a member of a *club*. The Asian surplus economies — China, Taiwan, South Korea, Thailand in the years it runs a tourism-fed surplus, and Vietnam — all share the same deep structure: they earn more dollars than they spend, they accumulate reserves, and their currencies, while they can weaken in a strong-dollar year, do not suffer the kind of run that hits a deficit EM. The mirror image is the deficit cluster — Turkey, Argentina, South Africa in its weaker years, Egypt — where a structural shortfall of dollars must be financed with foreign borrowing and hot money, and where the currency periodically falls apart. The single best one-line predictor of which camp a currency sits in is the sign of its current account, and Vietnam has sat firmly on the surplus side since the late 2010s. When you compare the dong to, say, the Turkish lira, you are not comparing two emerging-market currencies of similar risk; you are comparing a surplus currency funded by sticky money to a deficit currency funded by hot money and high rates. They are different animals, and the balance of payments is what tells them apart.

The general pattern, across all three years: the *fast* forces (rate differential, global dollar, hot-money flows) drive the dong's *short-term* moves and the SBV's *tactical* defense; the *slow* forces (remittances, FDI, the trade surplus, the current-account surplus) set the *structural floor* and decide whether a shock is a wobble or a crisis. For the dong, the slow forces have consistently been strong enough to keep every shock a wobble. That is the market signature of a well-funded EM currency, and it is exactly what the balance of payments predicts.

There is a final real-market wrinkle worth naming: the *offshore signal*. Because the dong is not freely deliverable, foreign traders express views on it through non-deliverable forwards (NDFs). When the offshore 1-year NDF starts pricing the dong meaningfully *weaker* than the onshore spot, it is a tell that the market expects devaluation pressure — usually because it expects the structural inflows to weaken or the rate differential to worsen. Watching the onshore/offshore NDF gap is the trader's way of front-running a strain in the balance of payments. The mechanics of that instrument live in [non-deliverable forwards, trading uninvestable currencies](/blog/trading/forex/non-deliverable-forwards-trading-uninvestable-currencies), but the *signal* it carries is, at root, a market forecast of the flows this post has described.

## The takeaway: read the funding, not just the rate

The instinct, when you look at any currency, is to reach for the interest rate. What does it yield? What is the carry? That instinct is right for the *fast* layer — the speculative pressure, the hot money, the day-to-day moves. But for an emerging-market currency, it misses the bedrock. The dong is not held up by its interest rate; if anything its rate differential is a *headwind*. It is held up by a structural stream of dollars that arrives every year regardless of the carry: people sending money home, factories being built, t-shirts and phones crossing the ocean.

So the discipline this post asks of you is to read an EM currency from the *flows* first, the rate second. Concretely:

**Find the current-account balance and its sign.** A surplus is a structural bid under the currency; a deficit is a structural drain that someone must finance. Vietnam at +4.8% of GDP is a fortress; a country at −5% is a candidate for the next crisis. This one number tells you more about the currency's *durability* than any interest rate.

**Decompose the inflows into sticky and hot.** Remittances, FDI, and the trade surplus are sticky — they fund the currency through a storm. Portfolio flows are hot — they fund it only until the storm. A currency funded by sticky money bends; a currency funded by hot money breaks. The same headline balance can hide opposite fates, so always look at the *composition*, not just the level.

**Watch FDI minus its income outflow, not the FDI headline.** Gross FDI is a press release; net support is FDI minus the dividends the existing factory stock sends home. A maturing FDI economy must keep attracting new investment just to stand still against a rising income drain. A stall in *new* FDI is the earliest warning that the structural floor is weakening.

**Treat reserves as a flow, not a level.** Reserves are the accumulated surplus of the balance of payments. The question is never "how big is the war chest?" but "does the structural surplus refill it faster than a typical shock can drain it?" Vietnam's reserves fell hard in 2022 and rebuilt as the surplus reasserted — the signature of a currency that is fundamentally sound even when it is temporarily under pressure.

Tie it back to the spine of the series. An exchange rate is the relative price of two monies, moved by the gap between two countries' interest rates *plus the flow of money across borders*. For a developed-market major, the rate gap usually dominates and the flows are a footnote. For an emerging market, the order flips: the flows are the bedrock and the rate gap is the modulation on top. The dong's gentle managed crawl is the visible outcome of an unfavorable rate differential pushing down against a powerful current-account surplus pushing up — and the surplus, year after year, mostly wins. That is what it means to say the current account funds the dong. The currency is not held up by a central bank's resolve or a clever policy. It is held up by a country that earns more dollars than it spends — and keeps doing so, through every storm.
