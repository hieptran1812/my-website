> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — In 1998 a local Russian default detonated a *global* deleveraging because the same leveraged bets, the same lenders, and the same funding currency linked every desk on Earth. The cleanest scar it left on FX was a three-day collapse in USD/JPY as the yen carry trade unwound at once.
>
> - **An exchange rate is a relative price**, and a carry trade is a *short position in the low-yield funding currency*. Closing it means buying that currency back — which is why the yen rockets in a panic, not falls.
> - **Leverage is the multiplier that turned a small move into a wipeout.** Long-Term Capital Management ran at roughly 25-to-1; a ~4% adverse move erases the entire equity behind a position levered that hard.
> - **Correlation is the channel.** When everyone holds the same trade, financed the same way, a single shock makes "diversified" books move as one — and forced selling becomes a self-feeding loop.
> - **The one number to remember:** USD/JPY fell from around 147 to **112** in three trading days in October 1998 — a ~24-yen, roughly 15% move in a "boring" G3 currency pair, driven almost entirely by an unwind, not by Japan.

In the first week of October 1998, traders who were short the Japanese yen against the dollar — which, that year, was very nearly *everyone* running a carry book — watched the screen do something they had been told could not happen to a major currency pair. USD/JPY, the price of one US dollar in yen, did not drift. It fell off a table. From roughly 147 in early October it dropped to 131, then to 120, then to 112 in the space of three trading sessions. A move of that size in an emerging-market currency would be a crisis. In USD/JPY — the second-most-traded pair on the planet — it was a once-in-a-generation event, and almost none of it was about Japan.

To understand why the yen, of all currencies, exploded higher in the middle of a global panic, you have to follow a chain that started six thousand kilometres away, in Moscow. On August 17, 1998, Russia defaulted on its domestic debt and let the ruble go. That single act — a local emerging-market event — set off a sequence of margin calls that ran through a famous, doomed hedge fund called Long-Term Capital Management, and ended in the most violent currency-funding snap-back the modern G3 market had ever seen. The lesson of 1998 is not "Russia is risky" or "hedge funds blow up." It is a structural lesson about how money actually moves: **leverage plus correlation plus a single funding currency is a delivery system for contagion.** Pull any one of those three legs out and 1998 doesn't happen.

![USD JPY collapses from around 147 to 112 over three trading days in October 1998](/imgs/blogs/1998-russia-ltcm-and-the-fx-tremor-that-shook-the-world-1.png)

This post tells that story as a currency story, because that is where the cleanest scar is. We will build the mechanics from zero — what a carry trade actually *is* as a position, what leverage does to a number, why a funding currency snaps *up* and not down in a crisis — and then watch all three combine in the autumn of 1998. We lean on the broader macro and microstructure mechanics that other posts in this series and its neighbours own (rate differentials, the dollar system, the formal speculative-attack model); here we keep the narrative and the numbers.

## Foundations: How a local default went global

Start with the spine of this whole series: **an exchange rate is the relative price of two monies.** You never own "a currency" in isolation. Every FX position is a pair — long one money, short another. When you read "long USD/JPY," that is shorthand for *long dollars, short yen*: you are betting the dollar buys more yen tomorrow than it does today. The number, say 147, means one dollar buys 147 yen. If it rises to 150, the dollar strengthened and the yen weakened; if it falls to 112, the dollar weakened and the yen strengthened. Hold that mapping firmly — half of 1998's surprise is that people forget a falling USD/JPY *is* a strengthening yen, and a strengthening yen in a panic is the whole story.

### What a carry trade actually is

A **carry trade** sounds exotic but is just borrowing cheap and lending dear, *across currencies*. Money has a price in each country — the interest rate. In 1998 Japan's short-term interest rate was effectively zero; the dollar paid around 5%; high-yield emerging markets paid double digits. So a trader could borrow yen at almost nothing, sell those yen for dollars (or rubles, or Brazilian real), and park the proceeds in a higher-yielding asset. As long as the exchange rate didn't move against you, you pocketed the *interest-rate difference* — the "carry."

Here is the part that matters for what follows: **a carry trade is structurally a short position in the funding currency.** You borrowed yen. To repay the loan one day, you will have to buy yen back. While the trade is on, you are short yen — you *owe* yen — exactly as a short-seller of a stock owes shares. The carry is your reward for being short the world's cheapest money. The risk is that the cheap money suddenly gets expensive, or scarce, and you are forced to buy it back all at once. This is why the carry trade is often described as *picking up pennies in front of a steamroller*: small, steady gains, punctuated by rare, violent losses. Our sibling post on [carry crashes](/blog/trading/forex/carry-crashes-picking-up-pennies-in-front-of-a-steamroller) dissects that skew; here we watch the steamroller arrive.

Why was the yen the world's funding currency in 1998? Because Japan's interest rate was, uniquely, near zero. Japan had spent the early 1990s deflating the largest asset bubble in modern history; its central bank had cut rates toward zero to fight the deflation, and they would stay there for decades. A near-zero rate makes a currency the *cheapest thing to borrow on the planet*, which is precisely the qualification for being a funding currency. The mechanism that *pulls* the exchange rate — the gap between two countries' interest rates — is owned by the [rate-differential](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-monetary-policy-moves-currencies-rate-differentials) and [what-moves-exchange-rates](/blog/trading/macro-trading/what-moves-exchange-rates-rates-flows-carry) posts in the macro track; what you need here is just the consequence: when one major economy holds its rate at zero for years while others pay 5%, that economy's currency becomes the funnel through which the whole world's leverage is financed. And a currency that funds the world's leverage is a currency the whole world is short — a loaded spring.

The carry trade also has a quiet feature that makes it *feel* even safer than it is, which matters for why it gets so crowded. For long stretches, the high-yield, high-carry currencies *appreciate* rather than depreciate — the opposite of what economic theory (uncovered interest parity) predicts. The currency you bought drifts up while it pays you the rate gap, so you earn the carry *and* a capital gain. This "forward-premium puzzle" is well documented and it is a trap: the extra appreciation is compensation for the rare crash, paid out as a steady drip until the day it is all clawed back at once. Traders who measure the trade only by its calm-period Sharpe ratio see a beautiful, low-volatility, positive-return strategy and lever it hard. They are measuring the pennies and ignoring the steamroller, which is exactly the mistake 1998 punished.

### What leverage does

The second leg is **leverage** — using borrowed money to control a position larger than your own capital. If you have \$40 of your own money and you borrow \$960, you control a \$1,000 position. Your *leverage ratio* is 1,000 / 40 = 25-to-1. Leverage is a multiplier on both gains and losses: a 1% move on the \$1,000 position is \$10, which is 25% of your \$40 of equity. The position barely twitched; your capital lurched.

The fatal property of high leverage is that a small adverse move can wipe out your entire equity, at which point your *lender* — not you — decides what happens next. They issue a **margin call**: post more collateral now, or we liquidate your position at whatever price we can get. In a calm market that is an inconvenience. In a panic, when everyone gets the same call at the same time, it is the mechanism that turns a price decline into a rout, because forced sellers don't sell what they *want* to sell — they sell what they *can* sell, which is whatever still has a bid.

### The funding-currency snap-back

Now combine the two and you get the third leg, the one that makes FX special. When a carry trade is forced to close, the trader must **buy back the funding currency** — the yen — to repay the yen loan. Everyone running the same trade gets forced out at the same time. They all rush to buy yen at once. The yen, which had been drifting weaker for years as carry traders sold it, snaps violently higher. The funding currency *strengthens in the crisis* — the opposite of what an untrained eye expects. A weak, unloved, near-zero-rate currency becomes a wrecking ball precisely *because* so many people were short it.

![The 1998 chain from ruble default through margin calls to a global yen carry unwind](/imgs/blogs/1998-russia-ltcm-and-the-fx-tremor-that-shook-the-world-2.png)

Stack the three legs and you have the entire 1998 machine. A shock (Russia) creates losses on leveraged, correlated positions. Losses trigger margin calls. Margin calls force selling of whatever is liquid, including the closing of carry trades. Closing carry trades means buying back the funding currency, which snaps the yen higher. The yen spike deepens losses for anyone still short yen, triggering *more* margin calls. The loop runs until either the leverage is gone or someone with a bigger balance sheet — a central bank — steps in to break it. That is the shape of every modern deleveraging, and 1998 is its cleanest early specimen.

#### Worked example: the ruble default that started it

On August 17, 1998, Russia did three things at once: it widened (then abandoned) the ruble's trading band, declared a 90-day moratorium on private foreign-debt payments, and defaulted on its domestic ruble-denominated bonds, the **GKOs**. Consider a foreign fund holding \$100,000,000 face value of GKOs, which it had bought because they yielded an eye-watering rate — at moments in 1998, GKO yields ran well above 100% annualised, the market screaming that default was possible.

Before the default, the ruble traded near **6 per dollar**, so that \$100,000,000 position was backed by 600,000,000 rubles of bonds. After the default and devaluation, the ruble collapsed toward **21 per dollar**. The bonds were frozen and restructured at a fraction of face value, but even ignoring the haircut, the *currency* move alone gutted the dollar value: 600,000,000 rubles at 21 per dollar is about \$28,571,000. The currency translation alone turned \$100,000,000 of exposure into under \$29,000,000 — a ~71% loss before a single rouble of the restructuring haircut was applied. The lesson: in an emerging-market blow-up, the bond default and the currency default arrive together, and the currency leg is often the larger one.

![The ruble falls from 6 to 21 per dollar after the August 1998 default](/imgs/blogs/1998-russia-ltcm-and-the-fx-tremor-that-shook-the-world-3.png)

A ruble that goes from 6 to 21 per dollar has lost roughly 70% of its dollar value. For a Russian importer that is ruinous; for a leveraged foreign fund holding ruble assets it is a mark-to-market crater. And crucially, Russia in 1998 was a *small* part of the world economy — a rounding error in global GDP. The reason a small country's default mattered to the price of the yen is that the *positions* built on top of it were enormous, leveraged, and held by the same handful of players. That is the bridge from a local event to a global one, and it has a name: Long-Term Capital Management.

## The ruble break: why an obvious risk still detonated

It is tempting to say the GKO market was a reckless bet that any sober analyst would have avoided. The truth is more uncomfortable. The trade that hurt the most in 1998 was not "buy Russia and pray." It was **convergence** — a class of relative-value trades built on the assumption that two closely related prices, currently apart, will move back together. You buy the cheap one, short the expensive one, and earn the gap as it closes. Convergence trades are *designed* to be low-risk on any single position because the long and the short hedge most of the market's direction; the bet is only on the small spread between them. Lever a low-risk spread enough and you manufacture an attractive return.

The fatal assumption buried in every convergence trade is that the spread will *narrow*, or at worst stay put — that the two prices won't fly *apart*. In a panic they fly apart. A flight to safety means everyone sells the cheap, risky, illiquid thing and buys the expensive, safe, liquid thing — which *widens* every convergence spread on Earth at the same time. The trade that was supposed to be hedged and diversified turns out to be the *same* trade, over and over, expressed in different markets: long risk, short safety. When risk repriced in August 1998, every one of those spreads moved against the convergence funds simultaneously.

Russia was the trigger, not the cause. The cause was that the world's most sophisticated arbitrage desks had, without quite realising it, all put on a single gigantic bet — "spreads will narrow, volatility will fall, the cheap will catch up to the dear" — and financed it with enormous leverage. The ruble default was simply the match. As we will see, the same structure shows up in 2008, in 2015, and in the August 2024 yen unwind. The countries and instruments change; the architecture does not. Our [emerging-market carry and the sudden stop](/blog/trading/forex/the-carry-into-emerging-markets-and-the-sudden-stop) post tracks how this same flight-to-safety mechanic repeatedly traps hot money chasing EM yield.

#### Worked example: a convergence spread that "couldn't" widen

Suppose a fund holds a classic convergence pair: it is long \$1,000,000,000 of a slightly cheap, slightly riskier bond yielding 6.5%, and short \$1,000,000,000 of a safe benchmark bond yielding 6.0%. The yield *spread* is 0.5%, or 50 basis points. Because the two legs offset most interest-rate risk, the fund's models say the position's true risk is tiny — its value moves only with the 50-bp spread, which has been stable for years. So the fund posts only, say, \$20,000,000 of capital against the \$1,000,000,000 gross position: 50-to-1 leverage on the gross book.

Then Russia defaults and the world flees to safety. The spread doesn't narrow — it *blows out* to 150 basis points as everyone dumps the risky leg and buys the safe one. The fund's loss is roughly the spread change times the position's sensitivity. If the position has about 5 years of spread duration, a 100-bp widening is roughly 5 × 1% = 5% of \$1,000,000,000 = **\$50,000,000** of loss. Against \$20,000,000 of posted capital, the fund is not merely down — it is wiped out twice over and facing a margin call it cannot meet. A "tiny-risk" trade became a solvency event because the one thing the model ruled out — the spread *widening* in a panic — is exactly what panics do.

### The three channels that carried it everywhere

Why did a Russian default reach a German bond spread, a Brazilian equity, and the Japanese yen on the same afternoon? Because three distinct channels connected them, and 1998 happened to light up all three at once. Naming them precisely is what lets you tell a *contained* shock from a *contagious* one before the fact.

The **correlation channel** is the trade itself. When the same relative-value bet — long the cheap and risky, short the dear and safe — sits on dozens of desks across continents, those desks are not running independent strategies; they are running one strategy in many costumes. A repricing of risk anywhere moves all of them together. The **leverage channel** is the plumbing of who lent the money. The big dealer banks financed the leveraged community, often against the very positions that were now falling, and a bank that takes a loss on one client tightens credit to *every* client — so stress at one fund propagates to others through their shared lenders and shared collateral pools. The **funding channel** is the currency itself: because so much of the leveraged world had borrowed yen to finance its bets, a shock anywhere produced yen-buying everywhere, and that single shared liability stitched unrelated markets into one move.

![Three channels carry a local Russian default into every major market at once](/imgs/blogs/1998-russia-ltcm-and-the-fx-tremor-that-shook-the-world-7.png)

The practical test for whether a shock will stay local or go global is to ask, of each channel: is it loaded? A default in a country nobody is levered to, with no crowded correlated trade and no shared funding currency, stays local — it is news, not contagion. The same default, when the leveraged world is crowded into the correlated trade and financed in a single currency, is a system event. Russia in 1998 scored "loaded" on all three. That is why a country too small to dent world GDP could move the second-most-traded currency pair on Earth by 35 yen. Contagion is a property of the *positioning*, not of the trigger — a point our [emerging-market sudden-stop](/blog/trading/forex/the-carry-into-emerging-markets-and-the-sudden-stop) sibling makes from the EM side, and [cross-asset correlation](/blog/trading/cross-asset/when-correlations-go-to-one-in-a-crisis) makes from the allocator's side.

## LTCM's leverage: when genius failed

Long-Term Capital Management was the convergence trade made flesh. Founded in 1994 by John Meriwether, formerly of Salomon Brothers, its partners included Myron Scholes and Robert Merton — two of the three names on the Black-Scholes-Merton option-pricing model, both 1997 Nobel laureates. The fund's intellectual horsepower was, genuinely, the best on Wall Street. For its first three years it delivered spectacular returns — over 40% in 1995 and 1996 — by running thousands of convergence and relative-value trades across global bond, swap, and FX markets, each individually tiny, each hedged, all financed with staggering leverage.

By early 1998, LTCM controlled positions with a notional value in the *trillions* of dollars on an equity base of around \$4.7 billion to \$5 billion. On the balance-sheet positions alone the leverage was roughly **25-to-1**; counting the derivative and off-balance-sheet exposure, the effective leverage was far higher. The partners believed this was safe because the positions were hedged and diversified — long the cheap, short the dear, across dozens of uncorrelated markets. The models, calibrated on historical data, said a loss large enough to threaten the fund was a multi-standard-deviation event that should occur roughly once in the lifetime of the universe.

The models were wrong in a specific, instructive way. They estimated the risk of each position from how prices had behaved in *normal* times, when the markets really were largely uncorrelated. They could not see that the *correlations themselves change in a crisis* — that when liquidity evaporates, every "diversified" relative-value trade becomes the same flight-from-risk trade. When Russia defaulted, LTCM's supposedly independent bets all lost money together. The fund lost about \$550 million on a single day, August 21, 1998. Over the following five weeks it bled toward insolvency, its capital falling from roughly \$4.7 billion in January to around \$400 million by late September — a ~90% drawdown. Our neighbour post [LTCM 1998: when genius failed](/blog/trading/finance/ltcm-1998-when-genius-failed) tells the firm's full biography; here we care about what its leverage did to the *currency* market.

A crucial and underappreciated detail is that LTCM was not primarily a *currency* fund — its biggest bets were in bond and swap spreads, equity-volatility, and merger arbitrage. The FX exposure was partly incidental: the fund, like most leveraged players of the era, financed and hedged in low-rate currencies and held risk in higher-yielding ones, so its book carried an embedded short in the funding currencies. That is the point. The yen did not crash in 1998 because one fund had a giant yen bet; it crashed because the *entire* leveraged ecosystem — LTCM, its imitators, the banks' own proprietary desks, every fund running relative value — was financed in the same cheap currencies and had to repay them at once when the deleveraging hit. A bond-spread crisis became a currency crisis because the bond-spread trades were *funded* in yen. This is why you cannot understand an FX crash by looking only at FX positioning: the trigger often sits in a completely different asset class, connected to the currency only through the funding leg. To read the yen in a panic, you have to know what the leveraged world *borrowed* it to buy.

![A four percent move wipes out the equity behind a position levered twenty-five to one](/imgs/blogs/1998-russia-ltcm-and-the-fx-tremor-that-shook-the-world-4.png)

The reason LTCM's troubles became *everyone's* troubles is leverage math. When you are levered 25-to-1, you have almost no cushion. The arithmetic is brutal and worth doing slowly, because it is the entire reason the 1998 FX move was so violent.

#### Worked example: 25-to-1 leverage and a 4% move

Take a position of **\$1,000,000,000** controlled with **\$40,000,000** of your own equity — exactly 25-to-1 leverage, with \$960,000,000 borrowed. Now the market moves 4% against you. The loss is 4% × \$1,000,000,000 = **\$40,000,000**. That loss equals your *entire* equity. Your account is now worth zero. A 4% move — the kind of thing that can happen in a single bad afternoon in a stressed market — has wiped you out completely.

It gets worse, because the lender does not wait for zero. The moment your equity falls below the maintenance margin, you get a margin call. You must post more collateral *immediately* or be liquidated at the prevailing price — which, in a panic, is a falling, illiquid, gap-prone price. So you sell. But your "diversified" book is correlated, so selling one leg moves the others against you, deepening the loss on what you still hold, triggering the next margin call. At 25-to-1, you do not get to choose the timing of your exit. The market and your lender choose it for you, at the worst possible moment. A reader who internalises only one number from LTCM should keep this one: **at 25-to-1, a 4% move is a 100% loss.**

LTCM was not the only fund running this structure — it was the largest and most leveraged, but Wall Street's own proprietary trading desks held overlapping versions of the same convergence and carry bets. So when LTCM and its peers were forced to liquidate, they were all selling the same risky assets and *buying back the same funding currencies* into a market where every other holder of those positions was doing the identical thing. There were no buyers, only sellers. The price did not adjust smoothly; it gapped. And the single market where that gap was most dramatic was USD/JPY.

## The carry unwind: three days that broke the yen

By October 1998, the deleveraging had been grinding for weeks. Credit spreads had blown out, equity markets had fallen, and the Fed had already begun to respond. But the carry trade — short yen, long higher-yielding assets — was still substantially on the books across the global trading community. The yen had been a one-way bet for years: borrow it at zero, sell it, and watch it slowly weaken as everyone else did the same. USD/JPY had climbed from the low 100s toward 147 as the carry trade fed on itself. The trade was crowded, comfortable, and consensus.

Crowded, comfortable, and consensus is exactly the condition for a violent unwind. As losses mounted elsewhere in their books, leveraged players needed to raise cash and cut risk. One of the most reliable ways to cut risk was to close the yen carry trade — buy back the yen they were short. But because *everyone* held the trade, everyone tried to buy yen at the same moment. The yen, which had spent years being sold, now had a wall of forced buyers and almost no sellers. The price snapped.

![Closing a yen carry trade forces traders to buy back borrowed yen all at once](/imgs/blogs/1998-russia-ltcm-and-the-fx-tremor-that-shook-the-world-5.png)

Over October 6 to 9, 1998, USD/JPY collapsed from around 147 in early October — through 136, 131, 120 — to **112**. A roughly 24-yen, ~15% move in the second-most-liquid currency pair in the world, compressed into three trading days. There was no Japanese news to justify it. Japan's economy was, if anything, dreadful; nobody woke up suddenly bullish on Japan. The move was *pure positioning* — the mechanical consequence of thousands of short-yen carry trades being slammed shut at once. The yen strengthened not because of Japan, but because of how much the rest of the world owed in yen and had to repay all at once.

This is the single most important intuition in the whole episode, and it generalises far beyond 1998: **the funding currency is the crash currency.** Whatever currency the speculative community has borrowed to finance its bets — the yen in 1998 and again in 2007 and 2024, the dollar and Swiss franc at other times — that currency will *rally* hardest when the bets unwind, because closing the bet *is* a forced purchase of that currency. The pairs that "should" be calm in a crisis (low-yield, low-growth funders) are the ones that move the most.

It is worth slowing down on the *microstructure* of why three days, rather than three weeks. A normal market move is a negotiation: sellers and buyers meet at a price that clears, and if the price moves a little, new buyers appear and absorb the flow. A forced unwind is not a negotiation — it is a one-sided stampede. Every leveraged holder of the short-yen trade is trying to buy yen, and there is no natural seller of yen on the other side, because the people who *would* normally sell yen (other carry traders) are themselves trying to buy. The dealers who make markets in USD/JPY, seeing nothing but buy orders and a screen gapping against them, widen their spreads and pull their bids to protect themselves, which *removes* liquidity exactly when it is most needed. Each leg down triggers stop-loss orders and fresh margin calls, which generate *more* buying, which moves the price again. The move does not stop because the price reaches fair value; it stops when the forced sellers run out of positions to close. In October 1998 that took three days. In a thinner market or with more leverage, it can take three hours — which is roughly what happened to the Swiss franc in 2015.

There is also a reflexive cruelty in the timing. The carry trade had worked so well for so long *because* it was crowded — all that yen-selling is what pushed USD/JPY up to 147 in the first place. The crowding that manufactured the profit is the identical crowding that manufactures the crash. A trade cannot be simultaneously very profitable *and* uncrowded for long; the profit attracts the crowd, and the crowd is the fuel for the unwind. This is the deepest reason carry returns are skewed: the strategy contains, structurally, the seeds of its own periodic destruction. You are not paid the carry *despite* the crash risk; you are paid the carry *because* of it.

#### Worked example: a \$1,000,000 yen-carry position when USD/JPY drops 147 to 112

Suppose in mid-1998 you put on a classic yen carry trade with \$1,000,000 of capital, levered modestly at 5-to-1, so you control a \$5,000,000 position. You borrow the yen equivalent of \$5,000,000 at roughly 0.5% and convert it to dollars at USD/JPY = 147. That is 5,000,000 × 147 = **735,000,000 yen** borrowed and sold for \$5,000,000, which you invest in a US asset paying, say, 5%.

For months it works beautifully: you earn the ~4.5% rate gap on \$5,000,000, about \$225,000 a year, against \$1,000,000 of capital — a 22.5% carry return before any currency move. Then the unwind hits and USD/JPY falls to 112. To close, you must buy back your 735,000,000 yen. But now each dollar buys only 112 yen, so repaying 735,000,000 yen costs 735,000,000 / 112 = **\$6,562,500**. You sold the yen for \$5,000,000 and must buy it back for \$6,562,500 — a currency loss of **\$1,562,500**. Against your \$1,000,000 of capital, that is a *total wipeout plus a debt*. A year of fat carry — a few hundred thousand dollars — was erased many times over in three days. The carry giveth in pennies and taketh away in fortunes; the asymmetry *is* the trade.

Multiply that single \$1,000,000 trade by the thousands of leveraged desks holding the same position in 1998, and you can see why USD/JPY didn't drift lower — it cratered. Every one of those traders was simultaneously trying to do the same arithmetic, buy the same yen, and beat the others to the exit. The exit was too small for the crowd. The result was the cleanest, fastest carry unwind the G3 market had seen, and a template that markets would replay, almost beat for beat, in 2008 and 2024.

## The deleveraging cascade: how the loop feeds itself

Step back from the yen and look at the whole machine, because the yen move was one visible symptom of a system-wide seizure. The defining feature of a deleveraging is that it is a **positive feedback loop**: each step makes the next step worse, until something external breaks the cycle. Let us trace it carefully, because the same loop is the skeleton of every crisis in this series.

It begins with a **shock** that creates losses on leveraged positions — Russia's default. Losses reduce the equity cushioning the leverage. As equity falls, the leverage ratio *mechanically rises* even if you do nothing — a \$1,000,000,000 position that was 25-to-1 on \$40,000,000 becomes 50-to-1 if your equity halves to \$20,000,000. Higher leverage means less cushion, which means lenders demand **more collateral** — the margin call. To meet the call, you must raise cash, which means **forced selling**. But you sell into a market where every other leveraged holder is selling the same things, so prices fall further, which creates *more* losses, which erodes equity further, which triggers *more* margin calls. Round and round.

![A four-phase positive feedback loop of losses, margin calls, forced selling, and a yen spike](/imgs/blogs/1998-russia-ltcm-and-the-fx-tremor-that-shook-the-world-2.png)

Two amplifiers make the loop especially vicious. The first is **correlation breakdown**: in calm markets, a diversified book has offsetting positions that cushion each other, but in a deleveraging, *everything the leveraged community owns goes down together and everything it is short goes up together*, because the dominant force is no longer fundamentals — it is the shared need to raise cash. Diversification, the very thing that justified the leverage, evaporates exactly when you need it. Our cross-asset neighbour explains this directly: [when correlations go to one in a crisis](/blog/trading/cross-asset/when-correlations-go-to-one-in-a-crisis), the only number that matters is how levered you are.

The second amplifier is **the funding-currency feedback**. As we saw, forced selling includes closing carry trades, which buys back the funding currency and spikes it. But that spike *itself* creates new losses for anyone still short the funding currency — which is most of the leveraged world — triggering yet another round of calls and another round of yen-buying. The yen rally is not just a *symptom* of the deleveraging; it is one of the *engines*. This is why FX moves in 1998 were both an output of the crisis and an input to it.

#### Worked example: the margin spiral in numbers

Start with \$50,000,000 of equity supporting a \$1,000,000,000 levered book — 20-to-1, with a maintenance requirement that equity stay above 4% of the position (so above \$40,000,000). The book falls 3% on day one: loss of \$30,000,000, equity down to \$20,000,000. You are now below the \$40,000,000 floor and levered 50-to-1. The lender calls for \$30,000,000 to restore the cushion. You don't have \$30,000,000 in cash, so you sell \$400,000,000 of the position to raise it. But the whole market is selling, so your sale executes 2% below the prior mark, locking in another \$8,000,000 loss and pushing the *remaining* \$600,000,000 down with it. Equity falls again; the next call arrives. Within days, a 3% opening shock has compounded into a forced, near-total liquidation — not because the original positions were unsound, but because **leverage converts a price move into a liquidity event, and a liquidity event into insolvency.** The arithmetic, once it starts, runs without you.

The cascade has no natural brake. In a normal market, falling prices attract bargain hunters who step in and stabilise things. In a deleveraging, the would-be bargain hunters are the *same* leveraged players, equally starved of cash and equally forced to sell — so there is no marginal buyer. The only entity with a balance sheet large enough and a mandate broad enough to break the loop is a central bank. Which is exactly what happened next.

## The Fed steps in: breaking the loop

By late September 1998, LTCM was days from collapse, and its collapse threatened to be a system event, not a private one. The fund's counterparties — the major investment and commercial banks that had lent it money and traded against it — held the other side of its trillions in positions. If LTCM defaulted, those banks would have to seize and dump the collateral into an already-illiquid market, crystallising the very forced-selling cascade everyone feared, and possibly cracking the banks themselves. The risk was not that one clever fund would fail; it was that its failure would detonate the loop we just traced, across the entire financial system.

The **Federal Reserve Bank of New York**, under William McDonough, did something unusual: it did not lend public money, but it *convened* the rescue. On September 23, 1998, it gathered fourteen of LTCM's major creditor banks in a room and brokered a private bailout — a consortium that injected about **\$3.6 billion** in exchange for 90% of the fund, taking control of its positions and unwinding them in an orderly way over time rather than dumping them in a fire sale. The Fed put in no taxpayer cash; its contribution was the *coordination* — getting rivals who each wanted to sell first to instead act together. Game theory in a conference room: the [central-bank-as-coordinator move](/blog/trading/game-theory/the-central-bank-game-credibility-commitment-and-dont-fight-the-fed) that turns a run into an orderly wind-down.

Alongside the rescue, the Fed cut interest rates three times in quick succession in the autumn of 1998 — including an unscheduled inter-meeting cut on October 15 — to inject liquidity and confidence into a market that had frozen. The combination worked. The orderly unwind of LTCM's book removed the single largest source of forced selling; the rate cuts restored some appetite for risk; and crucially, the *coordination* broke the every-man-for-himself dynamic that drives a cascade. By the end of 1998, markets had stabilised, USD/JPY had clawed back some ground (settling in the 110s–120s), and the crisis was, remarkably, over within a few months of its peak.

![The VIX fear gauge in 1998 versus the GFC, COVID, and the 2024 yen unwind](/imgs/blogs/1998-russia-ltcm-and-the-fx-tremor-that-shook-the-world-8.png)

It is worth keeping the scale honest. By the standards of *later* crises, 1998 was a moderate panic — the fear gauge (volatility) spiked to the mid-40s, well above its long-run average near 20, but far below the 80-plus readings of 2008 and 2020. What makes 1998 instructive is not that it was the worst crisis ever; it is that it was the *purest* demonstration of the leverage-correlation-funding mechanism, uncontaminated by the banking-system insolvency that complicated 2008 or the real-economy shutdown of 2020. In 1998 you can see the machine naked: a leveraged bet, a correlation that went to one, a funding currency that snapped back, and a central bank that had to coordinate a rescue to stop the loop. Every later crisis is a variation on this theme.

The rescue worked, but it left a long shadow that is part of the lesson. By organising a bailout — even a private one — for a fund whose partners had grown rich on extreme leverage, the Fed arguably taught Wall Street that the system would catch it if a big enough, interconnected enough player fell. That perception of an implicit backstop is the textbook definition of **moral hazard**: if losses are yours but catastrophic losses are socialised, the rational move is to take more risk, not less. Critics drew a straight line from the LTCM rescue to the even larger leverage build-up of the 2000s that culminated in 2008 — the same structure, bigger, with the lesson "you will be rescued" now baked in. Whether the line is fair is debated, but the mechanism is real, and it is why central bankers agonise over *whether* to break a cascade at all: stopping today's loop can load tomorrow's slope. The [don't-fight-the-Fed coordination game](/blog/trading/game-theory/the-central-bank-game-credibility-commitment-and-dont-fight-the-fed) cuts both ways — credibility that you will intervene is exactly what encourages the risk-taking that makes intervention necessary.

There is a second, quieter lesson in *how* the rescue worked that is easy to miss. The Fed did not need a bigger model, a smarter forecast, or more capital. It needed to change the *game* the banks were playing. Left alone, fourteen creditors each faced a prisoner's-dilemma payoff: the bank that seized and sold LTCM's collateral *first* would get the best price, so each had an incentive to race for the exit, and the race itself would have crashed the market and harmed all fourteen. By putting them in one room and getting them to commit together, the Fed converted a destructive race into a cooperative wind-down — it changed the payoffs so that "wait and unwind slowly" became individually rational because everyone else had agreed to it too. The scarce resource in a cascade is coordination, and a central bank's deepest power is not its chequebook but its ability to make a credible promise that everyone will move together. That is a tool with no obvious limit and no obvious cost on the day — which is precisely why it is so tempting, and so prone to the moral-hazard trap.

## Common misconceptions

Even people who know the headline — "Russia defaulted, LTCM blew up" — usually carry at least one of these wrong models in their head. Each one matters for how you read the next crisis.

**Myth 1: "The yen fell in the crisis."** It did the opposite — the yen *rose* sharply, USD/JPY falling from ~147 to 112. The error comes from assuming a crisis hurts every currency equally. It doesn't. The funding currency — the one the speculative world borrowed — *strengthens* in a crisis, because closing the borrowed positions means buying it back. If you remember nothing else, remember that the carry funder is the crash-time winner: a near-zero-rate, unloved currency becomes the strongest thing on the screen precisely because so many were short it.

**Myth 2: "LTCM failed because its trades were bad."** Most of LTCM's individual trades were sound, and several of them, held to maturity, would actually have *made money* — the convergence the fund bet on did eventually happen. LTCM failed because of *leverage and liquidity*, not analysis. At 25-to-1 (and much higher counting derivatives), it could not survive the temporary, panic-driven *widening* of spreads long enough to collect the eventual narrowing. The fund was right about the destination and wrong about its ability to survive the journey. Being right but over-levered is indistinguishable from being wrong.

**Myth 3: "Russia was too small to matter, so this was an overreaction."** Russia *was* small — a tiny fraction of world GDP. But the crisis was never about Russia's economic weight; it was about the *size and concentration of the leveraged positions built on top of Russia and everything correlated with it*. A small trigger detonated a large, crowded, leveraged structure. This is the general lesson: contagion scales with *positioning*, not with the GDP of the country where the spark lands. A pebble can start an avalanche if the slope is loaded.

**Myth 4: "Diversification protected the diversified."** LTCM was as diversified as a fund could be — thousands of positions across dozens of markets and continents. Diversification failed because, in a deleveraging, correlations converge to one: the positions stopped being independent bets and became a single bet on "risk appetite." Diversification protects against *idiosyncratic* risk in normal times; it offers almost no protection against a *systemic* deleveraging, when the dominant driver is everyone's shared need to raise cash. The diversification was real in calm markets and illusory in the storm.

**Myth 5: "The Fed bailed out LTCM with public money."** The Fed put in *no* taxpayer money. Its role was purely to *coordinate* a private-sector rescue — to get fourteen rival banks to act together rather than racing to liquidate. The distinction matters: the scarce resource in a cascade is not capital, it is *coordination*. Everyone selling at once is the problem; getting them to stop selling at once is the cure. That is a very different intervention from writing a cheque, and it is the template the Fed and Treasury would reach for again.

## How it shows up in real markets

The 1998 machine is not a museum piece. It is a recurring structure, and once you can see it, you spot it forming in real time. Here is how the same three legs — leverage, correlation, funding-currency snap-back — have reappeared, and what they teach a currency reader.

**2008, the global financial crisis.** The same loop ran on a vastly larger scale. Leveraged positions in mortgage credit (instead of Russian bonds) created losses; correlations went to one as every credit spread blew out together; and the yen *again* snapped higher as the carry trade — even bigger in 2008 than 1998 — unwound. USD/JPY fell from the 110s toward the 80s over 2008. The carry-trade drawdown in 2008 was the worst on record, narrowly beating 1998. The difference was that 2008 added a *banking-system insolvency* on top of the deleveraging, which is why it took years rather than months to resolve. But the FX signature — funding currency rallies, high-yield currencies crater — was identical to 1998.

**January 2015, the Swiss franc.** A different flavour: not a carry unwind but a *peg break*, when the Swiss National Bank abandoned its EUR/CHF 1.20 floor and the franc spiked ~30% in minutes. The connection to 1998 is the *leverage* leg: retail and institutional traders short the franc against the floor — a "free carry" everyone believed was safe — were wiped out and pushed into negative equity in a single gap, exactly as LTCM was. Several brokers failed. The lesson rhymes: a crowded, leveraged, "this can't move" trade is the most dangerous trade there is, because when it moves, it gaps past every stop. Our [2015-to-2022 stress-cycle](/blog/trading/forex/2015-to-2022-the-franc-the-yen-and-the-em-stress-cycle) post walks that whole modern sequence.

**August 2024, the yen carry unwind redux.** This was 1998 in miniature, and recent enough that many readers lived it. The Bank of Japan finally hiked rates while the Fed signalled cuts, narrowing the US-Japan rate gap that had powered years of yen-funded carry. The crowded short-yen trade unwound, and USD/JPY fell from about 162 in early July to roughly 142 by August 5 — a ~12% move that spiked global volatility to a multi-year high and briefly cracked equity markets worldwide. It was smaller and faster-resolving than 1998, but mechanically the *same event*: a funding currency, snapping back as the carry trade closed all at once. If you understood 1998, you were not surprised by 2024. The macro-trading companion [carry-trade unwinds 1998, 2008, 2024](/blog/trading/macro-trading/carry-trade-unwinds-1998-2008-2024-when-leverage-breaks) lines all three up side by side.

#### Worked example: spotting the next one before it breaks

You don't need inside information to see a carry unwind coming — you need to read *positioning and the rate gap*. Two conditions load the slope. First, **a wide, stable rate differential** that has made a funding currency cheap to borrow for a long time: in 2024, the US-Japan short-rate gap sat above 5% for two years, which is the rate fuel. Second, **a crowded, complacent short** in that funding currency — visible in futures positioning data, in how one-sided the consensus is, in how low the *implied volatility* of the pair has fallen. When implied vol on USD/JPY is unusually low *and* the rate gap is starting to narrow (the BoJ hints at a hike, or the Fed at a cut), you are standing on a loaded slope. The trade that snaps it is rarely predictable; that the slope *can* snap is entirely predictable. The signal to watch is not the trigger — it is the *fuel and the crowd*. When both are extreme, respect the steamroller.

The practical takeaway for sizing is the one LTCM died for: in a crowded, leveraged trade, your true risk is not the daily volatility of the position in calm times — it is the *gap risk* when the crowd all heads for the same exit. Size for the gap, not the drift. A position that looks 2%-volatile on a normal day can move 15% in three days when the funding currency snaps. If your leverage assumes the calm-day number, the gap-day number is your epitaph.

**What the survivors changed.** 1998 rewired professional risk management in ways that still shape how desks run today. The first change was the death of the single-number, calm-calibrated risk model as a sufficient control: funds that lived through 1998 added *stress tests* — explicit "what if every spread widens together and correlations go to one" scenarios — alongside the statistical models, precisely because the statistical models had declared the actual event impossible. The second was a hard look at *liquidity-adjusted* sizing: rather than asking "how much can this position move?", the survivors asked "how long would it take me to exit this position without moving the price, and what happens to me in the meantime?" A position you can exit in a day at size is a different animal from one that takes three weeks to unwind, even if their daily volatilities are identical. The third was a structural humility about *crowding*: the recognition that a trade's popularity is itself a risk factor, because the more capital is in a trade, the more violent its unwind, regardless of how sound the underlying thesis is. None of these fixes is exotic; all of them are direct scar tissue from watching a fund full of Nobel laureates discover that being right is not the same as surviving.

**The signature is FX-readable.** For a currency trader specifically, the recurring tell across 1998, 2008, and 2024 is the same cross-asset pattern: a sharp move *up* in the funding currency (yen), coinciding with a spike in volatility, a blow-out in credit spreads, and a fall in high-yield and emerging-market currencies — all at once, all in the same hours. When you see those four things move together, you are not looking at four separate stories; you are looking at one deleveraging viewed from four windows. The currency window is often the clearest, because the funding-currency rally is the most mechanical and least ambiguous of the four. A yen that rips higher with no Japanese catalyst, while everything risky sells off, *is* the signature of a carry unwind, and it has meant the same thing for more than a quarter of a century.

## The takeaway: leverage, correlation, and a funding-currency snap-back

The deep lesson of 1998 is that **the danger in markets is rarely the asset you hold — it is the structure you hold it in.** Russian bonds defaulting was a knowable, even priced, risk; the GKO yields above 100% were the market shouting it. What turned a knowable default into a global tremor was the *structure* built on top: enormous leverage, positions that were correlated despite looking diversified, and a single funding currency that the entire speculative world had borrowed. Pull any one leg and the cascade doesn't run. Leave all three in place and a pebble starts an avalanche.

For how you read a currency, that resolves into a few durable habits. First, **always know what is the funding currency and how crowded the short is.** In a risk-off event, the funding currency is where the violence concentrates, and it moves *up*, not down — the opposite of the naive "crisis = currency falls" instinct. The yen at 147 going to 112 is not a Japan story; it is a *positioning* story, and positioning stories live in the funding currency. Second, **treat leverage as the real risk variable, not volatility.** A position's calm-day volatility tells you nothing about its gap risk when a correlated crowd unwinds; size for the day the exit is too small for everyone trying to use it.

Third, and most subtly, **distrust diversification that has never been stress-tested by a deleveraging.** Correlations that look low in normal times are a feature of *liquidity*, not of fundamentals; when liquidity vanishes, the correlations you were relying on vanish with it, and your diversified book reveals itself as a single levered bet on risk appetite. That is the trap LTCM's Nobel laureates walked into with their eyes open, and it is the trap every model that calibrates risk on calm-market data will keep setting.

There is a fourth habit that is less about defence and more about opportunity, because the other side of a forced unwind is one of the few genuinely free lunches in markets. When a funding currency snaps to an extreme on pure positioning — not on any change in fundamentals — it has, by definition, *overshot*. The forced buyers who drove the yen to 112 in 1998 were not buying because 112 was fair; they were buying because they had to. Once the forced selling exhausts itself, there is no fundamental force holding the currency at the overshoot, and it tends to retrace. A trader with dry powder and a strong stomach who can tell a *positioning* move from a *fundamental* move — and who sized small enough to still be solvent and clear-headed at the bottom — gets to buy what the forced sellers had to dump. The catch is in those two qualifiers: *dry powder* and *still solvent*. The leveraged crowd has neither at the bottom, which is exactly why the overshoot exists. Surviving the cascade with capital and composure intact is not just defence; it is what positions you to be the marginal buyer the market was missing. The capstone [FX-view playbook](/blog/trading/forex/building-an-fx-view-the-currency-trading-playbook-capstone) builds this distinction — positioning move versus fundamental move — into an actual decision process.

The spine of this series is that an exchange rate is the relative price of two monies, driven by the rate gap and the flow of money across borders. 1998 is the purest demonstration of the *flow* half of that sentence. For three days in October, the rate gap and the fundamentals didn't matter at all — what moved USD/JPY 35 yen was the desperate, mechanical, correlated flow of leveraged traders all buying back the same borrowed yen at once. When you next see a "boring" major currency move 15% in three days with no news, you will know exactly what you are looking at: not a change in the world, but a change in *who owns the trade* — and a funding currency snapping back to remind everyone what it was lending all along.
