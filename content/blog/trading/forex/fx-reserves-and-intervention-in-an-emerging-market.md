> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — FX reserves are the stash of foreign currency a central bank keeps so it can defend its exchange rate, pay for imports, and repay hard-currency debt — and that stash is finite ammunition, not an infinite shield.
>
> - Reserves do three concrete jobs: defend the currency, cover the import bill, and service dollars owed abroad. The same pile has to be ready for all three.
> - "Enough" is measured by yardsticks: months of imports (rule of thumb ~3), 100% cover of short-term external debt, and the IMF's ARA composite metric. Vietnam ran at roughly 2.9 months of imports in 2024 — just under the line.
> - Defending a currency means *selling your own reserves to buy your own currency back*. Every dollar sold props the rate today but shrinks the war chest, so the defence has a bottom.
> - The one number to remember: Vietnam's reserves fell from about **\$110 billion in early 2022 to roughly \$87 billion by year-end** — a ~\$23 billion drawdown spent slowing the dong's slide.

## A war chest, drained in one year

In January 2022, the State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) sat on a record stash of foreign currency — somewhere around **\$110 billion**, the most the country had ever held. By the December of the same year, that pile had shrunk to roughly **\$87 billion**. About **\$23 billion** of hard currency had left the building in twelve months. Nothing was stolen, no bank failed, and no headline-grabbing crisis hit the front pages. The dollars were *spent* — deliberately, in chunks, by the central bank itself, trying to slow the fall of the Vietnamese dong against a US dollar that had turned into a wrecking ball as the Federal Reserve hiked rates faster than at any time in forty years.

That sentence contains the whole subject of this post. A central bank can hold a mountain of foreign currency and still watch a fifth of it walk out the door in a single year, because foreign-exchange reserves are not a trophy you keep on a shelf — they are *ammunition*, and ammunition gets fired. The dong still depreciated about 4% over 2022 despite the spending. So the natural question a beginner asks is the right one: if you spend \$23 billion and the currency falls anyway, what exactly did the reserves *buy*? And the deeper question behind it: how does a managed-float central bank like Vietnam's actually use this war chest, how does it know when the chest is running low, and what happens when it does?

This is the third post in the Vietnam / emerging-market FX track of the series. The spine of the whole series is that an exchange rate is the *relative price of two monies* — you never own "a currency" in isolation; every position is a pair, a relative bet between two countries' interest rates and the flow of money across their borders. Reserves are where that abstract idea becomes a concrete, countable pile of someone else's money. We will build the concept from zero, walk through the three jobs reserves do, learn the yardsticks that tell you whether a country has "enough," and trace the exact mechanics of spending reserves to defend a level — anchoring every step in Vietnam's 2022 episode.

![Vietnam FX reserves bar chart 2016 to 2024 with the 2022 drawdown in red](/imgs/blogs/fx-reserves-and-intervention-in-an-emerging-market-1.png)

Look at that drawdown bar. The chart tells the story before any equation does: reserves were built patiently across years of trade surpluses and foreign investment, climbing from about \$41 billion in 2016 to \$109 billion by 2021, and then a single bad year erased two years of accumulation. That asymmetry — slow to build, fast to spend — is the central fact of reserve management in an emerging market, and it shapes every decision a central bank makes when its currency comes under pressure.

## Foundations: What FX reserves are and what they're for

Start with the most basic possible picture. You run a small import business in Ho Chi Minh City. You sell to local customers in dong, but every machine and component you buy comes from abroad and is invoiced in US dollars. So you keep a buffer of dollars in your bank account — enough to keep paying suppliers for a few months even if your dong revenue is lumpy or the exchange rate moves against you. That dollar buffer is *your* personal FX reserve. It exists for one reason: there are bills you cannot pay in your own currency, and you want to be sure you can always pay them.

A central bank's foreign-exchange reserves are the same idea scaled up to a whole country. **FX reserves are the stock of foreign currency and foreign-currency assets a central bank holds and controls** — mostly US dollars, but also euros, yen, pounds, gold, and a small allocation of IMF special drawing rights (SDRs). Critically, reserves are held in *liquid* form: short-dated US Treasury bills, bank deposits at other central banks, gold bars in a vault. The whole point is that they can be turned into spendable dollars at a moment's notice. A central bank does not hold its reserves in Vietnamese property or in a ten-year private loan; it holds them in instruments it can sell tomorrow morning at a known price.

A few terms before we go further, because we will use them constantly. The **exchange rate** is the price of one money in terms of another; for Vietnam we quote it as **USD/VND**, the number of dong it takes to buy one US dollar — about **25,450 VND per dollar** at the end of 2024. When that number *rises*, the dong is getting *weaker* (each dollar costs more dong); when it *falls*, the dong is getting *stronger*. **To depreciate** means the currency weakens; **to appreciate** means it strengthens. **Hard currency** means a globally accepted, freely convertible money — overwhelmingly the dollar — as opposed to a **soft** or local currency like the dong that the rest of the world does not generally want to hold. And a **managed float** (Vietnam's regime) means the exchange rate is allowed to move, but inside boundaries the central bank sets and defends — not pinned to one fixed number (a hard peg), and not left entirely to the market (a free float). We cover the mechanics of that regime in [how the SBV runs the dong](/blog/trading/forex/usd-vnd-and-the-managed-float-how-the-sbv-runs-the-dong); here we focus on the dollars behind the defence.

One more distinction that trips up almost everyone: **gross** versus **net** reserves. The headline number a central bank publishes — the one in our cover chart — is *gross* reserves: the total stock of foreign assets it holds. But some of those dollars are spoken for. If the central bank has promised to deliver dollars in the future (a forward sale), or has borrowed dollars through a swap line, those commitments reduce the dollars actually available to spend. *Net* reserves subtract those claims, and they can be materially smaller than the gross figure. The distinction is not academic — it is exactly what hid Thailand's true position before its 1997 collapse, when a huge forward book made the gross number look healthy while the spendable chest was nearly empty. Whenever a reserve number crosses your screen, the first question is always: gross or net?

### Where reserves actually come from

A central bank does not print dollars — only the US Federal Reserve can do that — so where does a country like Vietnam *get* \$80–110 billion of someone else's money? The answer is the **balance of payments**, the national ledger of every dollar that crosses the border. Reserves are the *residual* of that ledger: the dollars that flow in and are not immediately spent flowing back out end up on the central bank's books.

Three pipes feed the chest. The first is the **trade surplus**: Vietnam exports more goods (phones, textiles, electronics, furniture) than it imports, and the net difference arrives as dollars. The second is **foreign direct investment**: when Samsung or Intel builds a factory in Vietnam, the dollars to fund it come in from abroad and have to be converted into dong to pay local wages and land — and the central bank often absorbs those dollars into reserves as it supplies the dong. The third is **remittances**: the roughly \$16–19 billion a year that Vietnamese working overseas send home to their families, a steady, recession-resistant river of hard currency. When all three pipes run net positive, dollars pile up faster than the country spends them, and the central bank *accumulates* reserves — it buys the surplus dollars and pays out freshly created dong, which is why reserve accumulation is also a form of monetary easing unless sterilised. When the pipes reverse — exports slump, investors flee, the dollar surges — the chest drains. So reserves are not a savings decision made in isolation; they are the accumulated record of a country's dollar surpluses, and they breathe in and out with the balance of payments. We trace those flows in detail in [remittances, FDI and the current account that funds the dong](/blog/trading/forex/remittances-fdi-and-the-current-account-that-funds-the-dong).

### The three jobs reserves do

People often picture reserves as a single thing with a single purpose — "the money that protects the currency." That is one of three jobs, and lumping them together hides the most important constraint in the whole subject: the *same* pile of dollars has to stand ready for all three at once.

![Grid figure showing the three jobs of FX reserves defend pay imports service debt](/imgs/blogs/fx-reserves-and-intervention-in-an-emerging-market-2.png)

**Job one: defend the exchange rate.** When the dong is falling faster than the central bank wants, the SBV can step into the market and *sell dollars* — i.e. sell some of its reserves — *to buy dong*. That extra demand for dong, paid for with the reserve dollars, props up the dong's price. This is intervention, and it is the job most people mean when they talk about reserves. It is also the only one of the three jobs where the reserves are spent *against the market's wishes* — and therefore the one where they can run out.

**Job two: pay for imports.** A country that imports more than it earns in foreign currency needs a buffer to keep the goods flowing. Vietnam imports oil, refined fuels, semiconductors, machinery, and raw materials for its factories — all invoiced in dollars. If, for some reason, the normal dollar inflows (export revenue, foreign investment, remittances) dried up for a few months, reserves are what let the country keep buying the oil and chips it needs. This is why the headline adequacy measure is "months of imports" — it literally asks: *if dollar inflows stopped tomorrow, how many months could we keep paying for what we buy abroad?*

**Job three: service hard-currency debt.** Governments, banks, and companies in an emerging market often borrow in dollars because dollar loans carry lower interest rates than dong loans. But a dollar debt has to be repaid in dollars, regardless of where the exchange rate goes. When a big slug of external debt comes due — a sovereign bond maturing, a syndicated bank loan rolling off — the country needs dollars to pay it. Reserves are the national backstop that guarantees those payments can be made even if the country is temporarily shut out of borrowing more. This is why the second great adequacy yardstick is *short-term external debt cover*: can we repay everything due within a year, out of reserves, if no one will lend to us?

Hold those three jobs in mind, because the tension between them is the entire game. Every dollar the central bank fires at the exchange rate (job one) is a dollar no longer available to cover imports (job two) or repay debt (job three). The reserve pile is not three separate stashes; it is one stash wearing three hats. That is exactly why a central bank cannot simply "spend whatever it takes" to defend a currency — spending too aggressively on job one quietly erodes the cushion for jobs two and three, and the market knows it.

#### Worked example: How many dollars is "the war chest"?

Suppose at the start of 2022 Vietnam holds about **\$110 billion** in gross reserves. Now ask what claims sit against that pile. The country's annual import bill runs around **\$360 billion** of goods, or roughly **\$30 billion per month**. Short-term external debt — dollars owed and due within twelve months across the government, banks and companies — might be on the order of **\$25 billion** in a country Vietnam's size. So before the central bank has fired a single dollar at the exchange rate, the \$110 billion already has to cover months of imports *and* a year of short-term debt repayments. If you mentally earmark, say, \$25 billion for the short-term-debt backstop, only \$85 billion is "free" to serve as import cover and intervention ammunition combined. The takeaway: the headline reserve number is always larger than the amount actually available to defend the currency, because two other jobs have first claim on it.

## Months of imports: the oldest yardstick

So how does anyone judge whether a country has "enough" reserves? There is no single right number — a small open economy that imports half its GDP needs a far bigger buffer than a large, mostly self-sufficient one. Instead, practitioners use a handful of *yardsticks*, each answering a slightly different question. The oldest and simplest is **months of import cover**.

The arithmetic is plain. You take total reserves and divide by the monthly import bill. The result is the number of months the country could keep paying for its imports if every other source of dollars vanished. The traditional rule of thumb, dating back to the IMF's early reserve-adequacy work in the 1950s, is that a country should hold at least **three months of imports**. Three months is not magic — it is roughly the time it was thought to take for a country to adjust its policies and restore dollar inflows after a shock. But it has stuck as the floor below which analysts start to get nervous.

![Horizontal bar chart of months of imports across emerging markets with Vietnam at the adequacy line](/imgs/blogs/fx-reserves-and-intervention-in-an-emerging-market-3.png)

The chart puts Vietnam in context against its emerging-market peers. China, sitting on the world's largest reserve pile, holds well over a year of imports. India runs around eleven months, Thailand around eight, Indonesia and the Philippines six to eight. Vietnam sits down at the thin end, around **three months** — right on the traditional adequacy line, and a touch below it in 2024. Notice that "low" here does not mean "weak country." Vietnam runs a chronic *trade surplus* and steady foreign investment, so it earns dollars reliably; it simply chooses not to (and historically could not afford to) stockpile a Chinese-scale cushion. But the thin cushion is exactly why a single bad year like 2022 shows up so dramatically — there is less slack to absorb a shock.

#### Worked example: Vietnam's months-of-imports cover

Let us compute it the way an analyst would. Take end-2024 reserves of roughly **\$82 billion**. Vietnam's goods imports in 2024 ran about **\$340 billion** for the year, which is about **\$28.3 billion per month** (\$340B ÷ 12). The months-of-imports cover is then:

```
months of cover = reserves / monthly import bill
                = 82 / 28.3
                = 2.9 months
```

So Vietnam ended 2024 with about **2.9 months** of import cover — just *below* the three-month rule of thumb. The intuition: if every dollar coming in stopped tomorrow, Vietnam could keep paying for its imports for not quite three months before the buffer was gone. That is a real cushion, but a thin one — and it is precisely why the SBV manages reserves so carefully and lets the dong crawl weaker rather than burning the chest to hold a fixed level.

This single ratio explains a behaviour that confuses beginners. Why doesn't the SBV just defend a strong dong, spend reserves freely, and keep the currency from depreciating at all? Because at three months of cover, the central bank simply does not have the depth to fight the dollar to a standstill for long. A country with fourteen months of cover (China) can sell reserves for a year and still be comfortable; a country with three months cannot. The size of the war chest, measured against the import bill, dictates how aggressive a defence the country can even contemplate.

It is worth pausing on *why three months* specifically, because the number is more historical accident than law of nature. When the metric was first formalised in the 1950s, the world ran fixed exchange rates under the Bretton Woods system, capital barely moved across borders, and the main risk a country faced was a temporary shortfall in export earnings — a bad harvest, a collapse in a single commodity price. Three months was judged roughly long enough to ride out such a trade shock while policies adjusted. But the modern world is different in a way that *weakens* the import yardstick: the biggest danger today is rarely a slow trade squeeze; it is a *sudden capital flight* — foreigners and locals alike dumping local assets and rushing into dollars in a matter of days. The import-cover ratio says nothing about that channel at all, which is precisely why two more yardsticks were invented. So treat three months of imports as a *necessary but not sufficient* floor: a country below it is clearly thin, but a country comfortably above it can still be fragile if its debt or capital-flight exposure is large. Vietnam's saving grace is that its capital account is only partially open — the dong is not freely convertible, and there are real frictions on moving money out — which damps the capital-flight channel the import ratio ignores, and makes its thin import cushion less dangerous than the same number would be in a fully open economy like Turkey's.

## Reserves as ammunition: the IMF ARA metric and debt cover

Months of imports is intuitive but crude — it only looks at the import channel and ignores the debt channel entirely. So the toolkit has grown. Two more yardsticks matter, and together with months-of-imports they form the standard trio.

![Matrix figure of the three reserve adequacy yardsticks months imports debt cover IMF ARA](/imgs/blogs/fx-reserves-and-intervention-in-an-emerging-market-5.png)

**Short-term external debt cover (the Greenspan–Guidotti rule).** After the 1990s emerging-market crises — Mexico in 1994, Asia in 1997, Russia in 1998 — analysts realised that what actually breaks a country is not a slow drain on imports but a sudden *refusal to roll over debt*. When foreign lenders simultaneously decline to renew their short-term loans, a country must repay all of it at once, in dollars. The Guidotti–Greenspan rule, named for an Argentine deputy finance minister and the then-Fed chairman, says a country should hold reserves at least equal to **100% of its external debt falling due within one year** — a cover ratio of 1.0x or more. The idea is brutal and simple: you should be able to survive a full year with no new foreign borrowing at all. We unpack why short-term debt is the live wire in [emerging-market and sovereign debt](/blog/trading/fixed-income/emerging-market-and-sovereign-debt-yield-with-country-risk); the reserve angle is that this cover ratio is the second hard test of adequacy.

**The IMF ARA metric.** The IMF's modern *Assessing Reserve Adequacy* framework rolls several drains into one composite. Rather than looking at imports or debt in isolation, it builds a weighted risk measure from four potential sources of dollar outflow: short-term debt (the rollover risk), other portfolio liabilities (foreigners selling their stocks and bonds and taking the money home), the broad money supply M2 (residents fleeing the local currency into dollars — capital flight), and exports (a proxy for the income shock from a fall in global demand). Each gets a risk weight, the IMF sums them, and a country is judged "adequate" if its reserves sit at roughly **100% to 150%** of that composite. The ARA metric is the most complete of the three because it captures *all* the ways dollars can leave, not just the import channel.

So why keep three yardsticks instead of one? Because they fail in different ways. Months-of-imports flags a *trade-financing* squeeze. Debt cover flags a *rollover* crisis. The ARA metric flags *capital flight* and portfolio outflows. A country can pass one and fail another: a commodity exporter with no foreign debt might have huge import cover but be vulnerable to a portfolio stampede; a country with a small import bill but a mountain of short-term dollar debt looks fine on the import ruler and terrifying on the debt ruler. A serious analyst checks all three. Vietnam, with a thin import cushion (~2.9 months) but a manageable short-term debt load and capital controls that slow capital flight, scores *adequate* on the debt and ARA rulers while sitting *below* the line on the import ruler — which is exactly why the picture is "managed, not comfortable."

#### Worked example: the short-term debt cover ratio

Take a stylised but realistic snapshot. Suppose Vietnam holds **\$85 billion** in reserves and has **\$30 billion** of external debt falling due within the next twelve months. The Guidotti–Greenspan cover ratio is:

```
cover ratio = reserves / short-term external debt
            = 85 / 30
            = 2.83x
```

A ratio of 2.83x means Vietnam holds nearly three dollars of reserves for every one dollar of debt due within a year — comfortably above the 1.0x rule. The intuition: even if every foreign lender refused to roll a single loan, Vietnam could repay all of next year's maturing dollar debt out of reserves and still have most of the chest left over. This is the yardstick on which Vietnam looks *strong*, even though it looks *thin* on months-of-imports — and it is why one number is never enough to judge a country.

## The defence mechanic: how spending reserves props up a currency

Now the heart of it. We have said the central bank "defends the currency by selling reserves." What does that actually *do*, step by step, in the market? This is where most explanations wave their hands, so let us be concrete.

A currency's price, like any price, is set by supply and demand. The dong weakens (USD/VND rises) when more people want to sell dong and buy dollars than the reverse — importers needing dollars to pay suppliers, foreign investors pulling money out, locals worried about depreciation rushing to convert savings into dollars. That selling pressure pushes the price of a dollar *up* in dong terms. To lean against it, the central bank must do the opposite trade, and do it big enough to matter: it **sells dollars and buys dong**. Mechanically, the SBV instructs the commercial banks it deals with that it will sell them dollars at a set price (or sell into the interbank market), draining dollars from its reserves and pulling dong out of circulation.

![Pipeline figure of the defence mechanic currency pressure sell reserves buy own currency reserves fall](/imgs/blogs/fx-reserves-and-intervention-in-an-emerging-market-4.png)

Walk the pipeline left to right. Pressure builds on the dong. The SBV sells dollars out of its reserves. Those dollars buy dong in the market. That extra demand for dong supports its price — the rate holds, or at least falls more slowly than it otherwise would. And the cost, shown in the final box: reserves fall by exactly the dollars sold, so the ammunition shrinks with every round fired. There is a second, subtler effect worth naming. When the central bank buys dong, it is pulling dong *out* of the banking system — the opposite of printing money. That tightens dong liquidity, which tends to push *up* dong interest rates, which makes holding dong more attractive and discourages the rush into dollars. So intervention works through two channels at once: the direct demand for dong (the flow effect) and the tightening of dong money (the liquidity effect). We trace the general-purpose version of this in [how central banks intervene in the currency market](/blog/trading/forex/how-central-banks-intervene-in-the-currency-market); here we keep it grounded in the dong.

Two more refinements that separate a clued-in reader from a naive one. First, **sterilised versus unsterilised**. When the SBV sells dollars and absorbs dong, it can choose to *put the dong back* by other means — for example by injecting liquidity through its open-market operations — so that domestic money conditions are unchanged. That is *sterilised* intervention: it moves the exchange rate without tightening domestic policy. If it leaves the dong drained, that is *unsterilised*, and it doubles as a monetary tightening. In 2022 the SBV did some of both — it sold dollars *and* let dong rates rise *and* eventually hiked its policy rate, a combined defence. Second, **the futures/forward channel**. A central bank does not always have to spend spot dollars; it can sell dollars *forward* (promising to deliver them later) to relieve pressure without immediately draining reserves. But a forward sale is a deferred drain — the dollars still have to be delivered eventually — so it borrows ammunition from the future rather than creating it.

There is a third refinement that matters enormously for whether intervention *works*, and it is about scale. The dong's onshore FX market is shallow — it turns over a few billion dollars on a busy day — so a central bank selling, say, half a billion dollars in a single session is an overwhelming presence, the largest single counterparty in the market by far. That depth advantage is exactly why intervention can move the price at all: the SBV is not one voice among thousands but the whale in a small pond. The flip side is that in a *deep* market like the dollar–yen, where hundreds of billions trade daily, even a multi-billion-dollar intervention is a smaller fraction of the flow, so it works more by *signalling* — by telling the market "the central bank is now watching this level and will defend it" — than by sheer weight of money. For an emerging market with a thin market and a finite chest, intervention is mostly weight; for a developed market with a deep market and a vast chest, it is mostly signal. Vietnam plays the weight game, and the weight game is the one that runs out of ammunition.

And one piece of intervention that is invisible on a reserve chart but matters in practice: **verbal and regulatory intervention**. Before it spends a single dollar, the SBV can move the daily central reference rate, lean on commercial banks about their dollar positions, tighten or loosen the rules on who may buy dollars and for what, and signal its intentions to the market. A managed-float central bank that has cultivated credibility — that the market believes will act — can often steer the currency with the *threat* of intervention, conserving the actual reserves for when the threat alone no longer suffices. The reserves are the ultimate backstop behind a wall of cheaper tools, and a well-run central bank spends the cheaper tools first.

#### Worked example: what \$10 billion of intervention buys

Suppose the dong is under pressure and the SBV decides to sell **\$10 billion** of reserves over a stretch of weeks to slow the slide, at an average rate of about **24,000 VND per dollar**. Selling \$10 billion buys:

```
dong purchased = dollars sold × exchange rate
              = 10,000,000,000 × 24,000
              = 240,000,000,000,000 VND
              = 240 trillion dong
```

So \$10 billion of intervention injects **240 trillion dong of buying demand** into the market and pulls that many dong out of circulation. Against a daily onshore FX market that turns over a few billion dollars, \$10 billion spread over weeks is a heavy, sustained bid — enough to slow a depreciation meaningfully. But here is the discipline check: \$10 billion is more than a tenth of Vietnam's entire war chest, and it is roughly a *third of one month* of import cover gone. The intuition: intervention is powerful but expensive, and a central bank with three months of cover can only afford a handful of \$10 billion rounds before the chest is dangerously thin. That arithmetic — power per round versus rounds available — is what every FX reserve manager is really computing.

## Vietnam 2022: the case study, drawdown and all

Now put the pieces together on the real 2022 episode, because it is a textbook example of a managed-float defence under a once-in-a-generation dollar squeeze.

The trigger was external and had nothing to do with Vietnam's own economy. Through 2022 the US Federal Reserve raised interest rates from near zero to over 4% in the most aggressive tightening cycle in decades, fighting post-pandemic inflation. Higher US rates pull money toward the dollar — money flows to where it is paid the most, a mechanism we cover in [the carry into emerging markets and the sudden stop](/blog/trading/forex/the-carry-into-emerging-markets-and-the-sudden-stop) and in the fixed-income view of [rate differentials and currencies](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-monetary-policy-moves-currencies-rate-differentials). The dollar index (DXY) rose to a twenty-year high near 114 by late September 2022. For an emerging market like Vietnam, a surging dollar is a wrecking ball: it makes the dong's depreciation almost automatic, raises the dong cost of all those dollar-invoiced imports, and pulls foreign capital out toward higher US yields.

![Line chart of USD/VND year-end 2014 to 2025 with the 2022 defence year shaded](/imgs/blogs/fx-reserves-and-intervention-in-an-emerging-market-6.png)

The chart shows the level the SBV was defending. USD/VND had crawled gently weaker for years — the managed crawl — sitting around 22,790 at the end of 2021. Through 2022 the pressure intensified, and despite everything the SBV did, the dong slid to about **23,650** by year-end, a depreciation of roughly **4%**. Now, a 4% fall might sound like a failed defence. It was the opposite. In 2022, the euro fell about 6% against the dollar, the British pound about 11%, the Japanese yen about 14%, and the Korean won about 7%. A 4% dong move in the teeth of the strongest dollar in twenty years was one of the *most controlled* depreciations in the world. That control is what the \$23 billion of reserves bought: not a frozen exchange rate, but an *orderly* depreciation instead of a disorderly one — a 4% crawl rather than a 15% gap-down that would have panicked importers, blown up dollar-borrowers, and risked a run.

Here is the sequence the SBV ran, and it is the canonical managed-float playbook. First, it *sold reserves* — the \$23 billion drawdown — to meet dollar demand and slow the fall. Second, when selling alone was not enough and the chest was thinning, it *widened the trading band*, lifting the daily band around the central rate from ±3% to ±5% in October 2022, which let the dong depreciate more inside official boundaries without spending more dollars. Third, it *raised interest rates* — the SBV hiked its policy rates twice in late 2022 — to make holding dong more rewarding and discourage the rush into dollars, the carry channel of defence. Three tools, layered: spend the chest, vent the pressure through a wider band, and pay savers to hold the currency. We unpack the band and the policy-rate side of this in [Vietnam's monetary policy and the credit ceiling](/blog/trading/finance/vietnam-monetary-policy-state-bank-dong-credit-ceiling).

The band-widening step deserves a closer look, because it is the most elegant tool in the kit and the one beginners least understand. The SBV publishes a daily central reference rate and allows the market rate to trade within a band — ±5% — around it. When pressure builds, the central bank has a choice at the *ceiling* of that band: keep selling dollars to hold the market rate below the ceiling, or *raise the ceiling* by widening the band so the market rate can climb without the bank spending a cent. Widening the band from ±3% to ±5% in October 2022 instantly created room for the dong to weaken further inside official boundaries, relieving the pressure on reserves. It is the central bank choosing to *let some air out of the balloon* rather than holding the balloon's shape by force. The cost is that it concedes ground — the dong is allowed to fall further — but the benefit is that it conserves the chest for when it truly matters. A central bank that only ever sells dollars and never widens its band is fighting with one hand; the SBV fought with both, which is why its defence was so economical relative to the size of the dollar shock.

It is also worth being precise about how *good* this outcome was by global standards, because "the dong fell 4%" sounds like a defeat until you see the field. In the same 2022 dollar surge, the Japanese yen fell about 14% and required tens of billions of dollars of intervention from one of the richest central banks on earth; the British pound briefly touched an all-time low near 1.035 against the dollar amid a domestic fiscal crisis; the Korean won, Taiwan dollar, and Philippine peso all fell harder than the dong despite some of them holding far larger reserve cushions. Vietnam, with one of the *thinnest* cushions in the region — three months of imports — delivered one of the *smallest* depreciations. That is not luck. It is the product of three things working together: real structural dollar inflows that kept refilling the chest even mid-crisis, a managed crawl that never let the dong drift far from fair value (so there was no large overvaluation to defend), and a partly closed capital account that slowed the capital flight that hammered more open economies. The reserves were the visible tool, but they worked *because* the rest of the architecture was sound. A war chest defends a sound position cheaply and an unsound one expensively or not at all.

And then the part beginners miss: the war chest *refilled*. Once the Fed stopped hiking and the dollar rolled over in 2023, the pressure reversed. Vietnam's structural dollar inflows — its trade surplus, its foreign direct investment, and its remittances — went back to work, and the SBV could *buy* dollars again to rebuild reserves, which recovered toward \$90 billion in 2023. This is the rhythm of reserve management in a surplus economy: spend the chest to smooth a shock, then refill it when the storm passes.

![Grouped bar chart of Vietnam FDI and trade surplus that refill reserves 2019 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/fx-reserves-and-intervention-in-an-emerging-market-8.png)

The chart shows the two engines that refill the chest. Disbursed foreign direct investment — factories actually being built, dollars actually arriving — ran a steady \$20–25 billion a year through the whole period, *including* the crisis year. The goods trade surplus swung more but ballooned to \$28 billion in 2023 and \$25 billion in 2024 as exports recovered. Add remittances (Vietnamese abroad sending money home, around \$16–19 billion a year, covered in [remittances, FDI and the current account](/blog/trading/forex/remittances-fdi-and-the-current-account-that-funds-the-dong)) and you have the structural dollar surplus that makes Vietnam's thin reserve cushion *manageable* despite being thin. A country that earns dollars reliably can afford to run a smaller buffer, because the buffer refills itself. A country that runs chronic deficits has to hoard, because every dollar spent is gone for good. This is the deepest reason Vietnam's three-month cushion is not as fragile as the bare number suggests: the inflows behind it are real and recurring.

#### Worked example: the 2022 drawdown in import-cover terms

Translate the headline \$23 billion drawdown into the yardstick that matters. Start with about **\$110 billion** in early 2022 and a monthly import bill of roughly **\$30 billion**. That is about **3.7 months** of import cover at the start (\$110B ÷ \$30B). End the year at **\$87 billion** against the same import bill:

```
start cover = 110 / 30 = 3.7 months
end cover   = 87  / 30 = 2.9 months
cover spent = 3.7 − 2.9 = 0.8 months of imports
```

So the entire dramatic \$23 billion defence cost Vietnam about **0.8 months of import cover** — it took the cushion from a comfortable 3.7 months down to a thin-but-not-broken 2.9 months. The intuition: that single number, 2.9 months, is why the SBV could not simply keep selling. One more year like 2022 would have pushed cover toward two months, into genuine danger. The drawdown was sustainable *once*; it was not a strategy that could be repeated indefinitely. The chest has a bottom, and the SBV was watching it the whole time.

## When the war chest runs out

Which brings us to the hardest question: what happens when the ammunition runs low? Because reserves are finite, defending a currency by selling them alone is a strategy with a built-in expiry date — and the market knows the expiry date too.

![Graph figure showing options when reserves are drawn down sell hike widen band](/imgs/blogs/fx-reserves-and-intervention-in-an-emerging-market-7.png)

The figure lays out the branch points. Once reserves are drawing down, the central bank has three responses, and the art is in *combining* them rather than relying on any one. It can keep selling dollars — but that path, taken alone, leads straight to an empty vault, which is the worst outcome of all because a central bank seen to be running out of reserves invites a speculative attack (more on that below). It can raise interest rates to pay savers a higher carry to hold the local currency, attacking the demand for dollars at its source. Or it can let the trading band slide wider, venting the depreciation pressure as an orderly, official depreciation rather than spending dollars to hold a level the fundamentals no longer support. The good outcomes — pressure cooling off, an orderly depreciation — come from the rate-hike and wider-band branches, *not* from selling alone.

The reason selling-alone is so dangerous deserves its own paragraph, because it is the most important strategic idea in the post. When speculators see a central bank with falling reserves stubbornly defending a fixed level, they face a one-way bet. The currency cannot go *up* much — the central bank is capping it — but if the reserves run out, it will gap *down* hard. So the speculators pile in, selling the currency short, which forces the central bank to spend even more reserves to defend, which accelerates the drain, which makes the eventual break more certain. This is a coordination game with a self-fulfilling bad equilibrium, and it is exactly how the Bank of England lost to George Soros in 1992 and how the Asian pegs collapsed in 1997. We cover the formal version in [the central bank game: credibility and commitment](/blog/trading/game-theory/the-central-bank-game-credibility-commitment-and-dont-fight-the-fed); the lesson for reserves is blunt. **A finite war chest cannot defend an indefensible level against a determined market.** The reserves can buy *time* and *order* — they can turn a crash into a crawl — but they cannot repeal the fundamentals. If a currency is genuinely overvalued, reserves only postpone the adjustment, and postponing it expensively can make the eventual move worse.

There is a grim arithmetic to the empty-vault scenario that is worth spelling out, because it is what makes a speculative attack so hard to stop once it starts. A speculator who borrows local currency and sells it short faces a payoff that is wildly asymmetric in their favour. If the central bank successfully defends, the speculator loses only the borrowing cost — a few percent of carry. If the central bank fails and the currency gaps down 20% or 30%, the speculator pockets that entire move. So a bet that costs a little to be wrong and pays enormously to be right will attract more and more capital as the reserves visibly dwindle, which is the opposite of what the defending bank needs. The market does not have to *know* the chest will run out; it only has to suspect it might, and the cheapness of the bet does the rest. This is why the single most dangerous thing a central bank can do is to be seen publicly committing to defend a level while its reserves are obviously falling toward zero — it is advertising a one-way bet. The defending bank's only winning move in that position is to make the outcome genuinely uncertain: raise rates brutally, blur how much ammunition is left, or — best of all — never let the situation arise by keeping the currency near fair value in the first place.

This is precisely why Vietnam's managed float is *managed* and not *fixed*. The SBV never tries to hold a single line in the sand. It lets the dong crawl weaker continuously, so the currency is always close to where the fundamentals put it, and reserves are spent only to *smooth the path*, never to *stop the journey*. A central bank that defends a crawl is fighting with the fundamentals; a central bank that defends a fixed peg the market disbelieves is fighting against them, and against a market that can always print more selling pressure than the bank has reserves to absorb. The whole genius of the managed float, from a reserve-management point of view, is that it never lets the war chest become the *only* thing standing between the currency and a cliff.

## Common misconceptions

**"More reserves are always better."** No — reserves have a real *carrying cost*. A central bank that holds \$100 billion in US Treasury bills earning, say, 4% while the country could have used those dollars to repay domestic borrowing costing 7%, or to invest at home, is paying a spread for the insurance. Holding reserves means earning a low, safe foreign yield while the country forgoes higher-return uses of the capital — economists call this the *quasi-fiscal cost* of reserves, and for a big hoarder it can run to a percent or more of GDP a year. There is such a thing as too many reserves; the goal is *adequate*, not *maximal*. Vietnam's relatively lean cushion is partly a deliberate choice not to over-insure.

**"Reserves are the government's savings account."** Not really. Gross reserves are not free national wealth the government can spend on roads and schools — a large chunk is the *mirror image* of liabilities. When foreign investors bring dollars in and buy dong assets, the central bank often ends up holding those dollars as reserves, but the foreigner can take them back out. So part of the reserve pile is "borrowed" in the sense that it could be reclaimed. *Net* reserves — gross reserves minus forward sales and other claims — are the truer measure of firepower, and they can be meaningfully smaller than the headline gross number. A central bank that has sold dollars forward to defend its currency has a gross figure that flatters its real position. Always ask whether a quoted reserve number is gross or net.

**"If the SBV has \$80 billion, it can defend the dong forever."** We have now seen why not. Eighty billion sounds enormous, but against a country that imports \$28 billion a month and faces a global dollar squeeze, it is under three months of cover, with two other jobs (imports and debt) holding first claim on it. A determined depreciation can eat \$20 billion in a year, as 2022 showed. Reserves measured in months and debt-cover ratios, not in headline billions, are the only honest gauge of how long a defence can last.

**"Intervention always works."** It works when the central bank is leaning *with* the fundamentals — smoothing a temporary overshoot, slowing a move that is happening anyway. It fails when it leans *against* the fundamentals — trying to hold a currency above the level its trade balance, interest rates, and capital flows justify. The same \$23 billion that bought Vietnam an orderly 4% crawl in 2022, deployed instead to hold the dong at an unchanged peg, would have been gone faster and bought nothing but a delayed, larger break. Intervention is a tactic for *timing and order*, not a substitute for the right level.

**"A falling reserve number means a country is failing."** Falling reserves can mean a *successful* defence in progress — the central bank is doing its job, spending the chest exactly as intended to smooth a shock, and will refill it when the storm passes, as Vietnam did in 2023. What matters is not the direction of the change but the *level relative to the yardsticks* and whether the inflows behind the chest are intact. A drawdown from a comfortable cushion is prudent insurance being claimed; a drawdown toward two months of cover with the inflows collapsing is a crisis. The number alone does not tell you which.

## How it shows up in real markets

For anyone reading currencies, reserves are one of the most information-rich data series an emerging market publishes — if you read them the right way. Here is how they show up in practice.

**Reserves are a leading indicator of intervention.** When you see an EM central bank's monthly reserve figure drop sharply, you are very often looking at intervention — the bank sold dollars to defend its currency. Traders watch these releases closely because a falling reserve number under a managed float tells you the central bank is *spending to defend*, which tells you something about both the pressure on the currency and how much ammunition is left. A bank whose reserves have fallen from twelve months of cover to four months has far less room to keep defending than one still at ten — and the market prices that dwindling firepower into how hard it is willing to push.

**The 1997 Asian crisis was a reserves-and-pegs story.** Thailand spent the bulk of its usable reserves — including, fatally, a huge book of *forward* dollar sales that hid how empty the real chest was — defending the baht's peg in the first half of 1997. When the forward book was discovered and the spot reserves ran dry, the peg broke on 2 July 1997, the baht fell over 50% against the dollar, and the crisis swept through Korea, Indonesia and Malaysia. The lesson the whole region drew, and acted on for the next two decades, was *hold more reserves and avoid hiding the true position in forwards* — which is why Asian EMs, Vietnam included, became reserve hoarders after 1997. We cover the episode in [the Asian financial crisis](/blog/trading/finance/asian-financial-crisis-1997) and the speculative-attack mechanics in [the central bank game](/blog/trading/game-theory/the-central-bank-game-credibility-commitment-and-dont-fight-the-fed).

**Developed markets play the same game, just in reverse.** When the Japanese yen fell past 150 to the dollar in 2022 and again in 2024, Japan's Ministry of Finance and the Bank of Japan *sold dollars to buy yen* — the same defence mechanic, run by a rich country with effectively unlimited reserves, spending around \$60 billion in September–October 2022 alone. The difference is depth: Japan can sell dollars almost without limit, so its intervention is a credible threat that can turn a market on a single round, whereas an emerging market must husband every dollar. The mechanic is identical; the war chest's size changes how the market respects it.

**The dollar cycle drives the whole EM reserve cycle.** Because most reserves are dollars and most EM pressure comes from a rising dollar, the global dollar cycle (DXY) is the master variable. A strong-dollar phase — like 2022 — drains EM reserves across the board as central banks defend; a weak-dollar phase lets them refill. If you want to forecast when an EM central bank will be spending reserves versus rebuilding them, watch the dollar and the US rate cycle first. We treat the dollar as a single cross-asset force in [the dollar and cross-asset gravity](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-dollar-cross-asset-gravity); for reserves, the takeaway is that an EM's war chest breathes in and out with the dollar's tide.

#### Worked example: reading a reserve release like a trader

Suppose Vietnam reports reserves of **\$78 billion**, down **\$4 billion** from the previous month, while the import bill is about **\$28 billion a month**. Walk the read. The \$4 billion drop almost certainly signals intervention — the SBV sold dollars to defend the dong against pressure. Now check cover: \$78B ÷ \$28B is **about 2.8 months** of imports, just under the adequacy line and a touch thinner than before. The strategic question becomes: how many more \$4 billion months can the SBV run before cover falls toward two months? At this burn rate, perhaps two or three before it must lean harder on rate hikes and a wider band instead of selling. The intuition: a single reserve release, divided by the import bill, tells you both *that* the bank is defending and roughly *how long* it can keep doing it — which is exactly what you need to judge whether the currency's managed crawl is sustainable or about to be allowed to slide faster.

## The takeaway: reserves are timing, not invincibility

Strip everything down and one idea remains. **FX reserves are a finite stock of someone else's money that a central bank spends to control the *speed and order* of its currency's movement — not its *direction*.** They are ammunition for a defence that buys time, smooths a shock, and keeps a depreciation orderly; they are not a force field that can freeze a price the fundamentals have already condemned. Vietnam's 2022 episode is the clean illustration: \$23 billion bought a controlled 4% crawl in the year the dollar broke half the world's currencies — a triumph of *order* — and not a single dollar of it changed the fact that a stronger dollar means a weaker dong. The reserves bought the *how*, never the *whether*.

For reading any emerging market, that gives you a compact checklist. Never look at the headline reserve number alone — divide it by the monthly import bill to get months of cover, check it against the year's short-term external debt, run it through the IMF's ARA composite if you can, and always ask whether the figure is gross or net of forward sales and swap-line borrowing. A bank quoting a fat gross number while sitting on a mountain of forward dollar sales is the single most common way reserve strength gets overstated, and it is exactly the trap that hid Thailand's empty chest in 1997. A falling number can be a successful defence in progress or the first act of a crisis; what tells you which is the *level* relative to the yardsticks and whether the structural dollar inflows behind the chest — exports, FDI, remittances — are intact. And remember the strategic ceiling: a central bank that has to spend reserves to hold a level the market disbelieves is in a losing game, because the market can always generate more selling pressure than the bank holds in reserves. The central banks that win — Vietnam's managed crawl, Japan's deep-pocketed threat — are the ones that never let the war chest become the *only* thing between the currency and the cliff.

That connects straight back to the spine of this whole series. An exchange rate is the relative price of two monies, pushed around by the gap between two countries' interest rates and the flow of money across their borders. Reserves are where a country meets that flow head-on with a pile of the *other* money — and the size of that pile, measured honestly against imports and debt, tells you exactly how much the country can shape the relative price before the relative price shapes it instead. The next time you see an EM reserve number flash across a screen, you will not see a trophy on a shelf. You will see a war chest with a bottom, and you will know how to estimate where the bottom is.
