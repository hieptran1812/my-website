> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — When people say "the FX market," they usually picture someone clicking a button to swap dollars for yen at today's price. That is *spot*, and it is barely a quarter of the action. The real engine room is **forwards** (a price to exchange currencies on a future date) and **FX swaps** (a spot trade and a reversing forward, glued together). The single fact to carry away: an FX swap is not a currency bet — it is a *collateralised loan in two currencies*, and the rent you pay is set entirely by the gap between the two interest rates.
>
> - A forward price is not a forecast. It is spot, mechanically adjusted by the interest each currency earns over the period — nothing more.
> - An FX swap = a near leg (exchange now, at spot) + a far leg (exchange back later, at the forward). You end up where you started, having borrowed one currency against the other in between.
> - This is why swaps are roughly **half of all FX turnover** — about **\$3.81 trillion a day** in 2022, more than spot's \$2.10 trillion. They are how the world rolls hedges and funds dollar balance sheets.
> - The one number to remember: forward USD/JPY, spot 150.00, USD at 5.0% and JPY at 0.5%, one year out, is **143.57** — the yen is "stronger" forward by exactly the rate gap.

## A funding squeeze nobody saw on the spot screen

In the third week of September 2008, the spot price of EUR/USD looked almost calm. It drifted around 1.45, wobbling a cent here and there — nothing that would alarm a tourist changing money at the airport. Yet inside the banking system, a five-alarm fire was raging, and it was burning in a corner of the market most people never look at: the price of *borrowing dollars through an FX swap*.

A European bank with a pile of dollar assets and a balance sheet funded in euros had to get dollars *somehow* to keep the lights on. The clean way to do that is an FX swap: hand over euros today, take dollars today, and agree to reverse the trade in three months. In normal times that costs almost nothing beyond the interest-rate gap. By October 2008 it cost a fortune. The "cross-currency basis" — the extra premium on top of the rate gap that you paid to get dollars this way — blew out to roughly **minus 220 basis points** for dollars-against-yen. The spot screen said "calm." The swap market said "there are no dollars to be had at any sane price."

That episode is the whole point of this post. The exchange rate you read in the news — the spot price — is the *least* of what trades. Underneath it sits an enormous machine of forwards and swaps whose prices are set not by opinion about where the currency is going, but by the cold arithmetic of two interest rates. Once you can read that machine, you understand where most FX volume actually comes from, why a "currency crisis" often shows up first as a *funding* crisis, and why an exchange rate is never a thing you own in isolation — it is always a relative price, and a forward is just that relative price with interest baked in.

![Bar chart of FX turnover by instrument in 2022 showing swaps as the largest slice](/imgs/blogs/fx-forwards-and-swaps-the-real-workhorses-of-the-market-1.png)

Look at that chart and let it reset your mental model. FX swaps — **\$3.81 trillion a day** — are the single biggest instrument in the entire foreign-exchange market, bigger than spot's \$2.10 trillion, far bigger than outright forwards, options, and currency swaps combined. The market you thought was about *exchanging* money is mostly about *borrowing* it. The rest of this post explains why.

## Foundations: forwards and swaps from the rate gap

Before anything clever, let us nail down the three words that do all the work: spot, forward, and swap. We will build each one from a bank account, not a textbook.

**Spot** is the price for exchanging two currencies *now* — settlement is conventionally two business days later, but for our purposes "now." If USD/JPY is 150.00, that means one US dollar buys 150 Japanese yen today. The first currency (USD) is the **base**; the second (JPY) is the **quote**. The number tells you how many units of the quote currency one unit of the base costs. That is the only convention you need.

**A forward** is the price for exchanging the same two currencies on a *future* date — say, one year from now. Here is the move that trips up every beginner: the forward price is almost never equal to the spot price, and the difference has *nothing to do with anyone's forecast* of where the currency is heading. It is pure arithmetic, and it comes from a single source — the gap between the two currencies' interest rates.

Here is the everyday-money version. Suppose you have \$1,000,000 and you will need yen in exactly one year. You have two honest ways to get there:

1. **Wait and convert later.** Keep your dollars in a US deposit earning 5.0%. In a year you have \$1,050,000, and you convert at whatever spot is then.
2. **Lock it in today.** Convert to yen now at spot 150.00 (getting ¥150,000,000), park the yen in a Japanese deposit earning 0.5%, and in a year you have ¥150,750,000 guaranteed.

If a bank quotes you a one-year forward, it must make these two paths cost the same — otherwise someone with a calculator and a credit line earns a riskless profit by doing one and undoing the other. Setting the dollar path and the yen path equal and solving for the forward rate gives you a clean formula. The dollar, carrying the *higher* interest rate, must trade at a forward **discount**; the yen, carrying the lower rate, trades at a forward **premium**.

To see why the "free profit" threat is what holds the forward in place, suppose a careless dealer quoted the one-year forward at 148 instead of the fair 143.57 — too high, meaning the dollar is "too strong" forward. An arbitrageur pounces: borrow dollars at 5.0%, convert to yen at spot 150.00, deposit the yen at 0.5%, and *sell the yen forward at 148* to lock the round trip. Because 148 is richer than fair value, the arbitrageur ends the year with more dollars than the 5.0% they owe — a riskless gain, scalable to any size. Armies of traders doing exactly this would sell the forward down until it hit 143.57 and the free money disappeared. That relentless pressure is *why* the forward equals spot-times-the-rate-ratio: not because a regulator decreed it, but because any other number is an open invitation to be arbitraged. This is the bedrock that makes the forward a mechanical identity rather than a matter of opinion.

A note on units, since pips trip people up. A "pip" is the smallest conventional price increment. For most pairs it is the fourth decimal place (0.0001), but for yen pairs, because the numbers are large, it is the second decimal place (0.01 yen). So when the one-year forward USD/JPY of 143.57 is 6.43 yen below spot, that is 643 pips of "forward points." Dealers always quote the *points*, never the whole forward price, because spot is a moving target all day while the points — pinned by the rate gap — barely budge. You take whatever spot is at the moment of dealing and add the agreed points to it.

![Pipeline showing spot price adjusted by the two interest rates to produce the forward](/imgs/blogs/fx-forwards-and-swaps-the-real-workhorses-of-the-market-2.png)

That pipeline is the entire idea. Take spot, scale it by the ratio of the two growth factors — `(1 + r_jpy) / (1 + r_usd)` for a USD/JPY quote — and you have the forward. The mechanism is called **covered interest parity**, and it is one of the few relationships in finance that holds by construction rather than by hope, because violating it would hand out free money. (We lean on the rates themselves rather than re-derive them — for the curve mechanics and what forward *rates* imply, see the fixed-income companion linked at the end.)

**Forward points** are just the forward minus the spot, expressed in the smallest price increment ("pips"). For USD/JPY a pip is 0.01 yen. If spot is 150.00 and the one-year forward is 143.57, the forward points are −643. Dealers quote the points, not the whole price, because the points are the *stable* part — they move with the rate gap, while spot jumps around all day.

**An FX swap** is the instrument that ties spot and forward together. It is two trades agreed at the same moment: a **near leg** (exchange the two currencies now, at spot) and a **far leg** (exchange them *back* later, at the forward). Crucially, the two legs are equal and opposite in one currency. You hand over dollars today and get them back in a year; in between, the counterparty holds your yen. You have not taken a view on the exchange rate at all — you have *borrowed dollars and lent yen* for a year, and the price of that borrowing is the difference between the two legs, which is exactly the forward points. Hold that thought; it is the key that unlocks the whole market.

One more distinction matters, because the words sound alike and get muddled constantly. An **outright forward** is a *single* exchange on a future date with no offsetting leg — you genuinely want yen in a year and you lock the price now. An **FX swap** is the *pair* of legs, with the far leg reversing the near one, so your net currency position is unchanged. And a **currency swap** (also called a cross-currency swap) is a different, longer-dated beast entirely: it exchanges *streams of interest payments* in two currencies over years, not a single round trip. Notice from the opening chart how tiny currency swaps are — just \$0.12 trillion a day — versus FX swaps at \$3.81 trillion. When this post says "swap," it always means the FX swap: spot now, reverse later, agreed together. Keeping those three apart is the difference between reading the market and being confused by it.

It helps to know *why* settlement works the way it does. A spot trade settles in two business days (the convention is "T+2") because, historically, the back offices of two banks in two time zones needed time to confirm and move the actual cash. A forward settles on whatever future date you name. An FX swap has its two legs settling on those two different dates — the near leg at T+2, the far leg at maturity — but the *price relationship* between them is locked the instant you deal. That is the magic: you fix tomorrow's economics today, which is exactly what a borrower or hedger needs.

#### Worked example: pricing the one-year forward from the rate gap

Take the canonical numbers. Spot USD/JPY is **150.00**. The one-year US deposit rate is **5.0%**; the one-year Japanese deposit rate is **0.5%**. What is the one-year forward?

The dollar path: \$1 grows to \$1.050. The yen path: convert \$1 to ¥150 now, grow at 0.5% to ¥150.75. For no free lunch, \$1.050 in a year must buy ¥150.75 in a year, so the forward rate is:

`F = 150.00 × (1.005) / (1.050) = 150.00 × 0.957143 = 143.57 yen per dollar`

So the one-year forward USD/JPY is **143.57** — the dollar is worth *fewer* yen forward than spot, by **643 pips**. Notice what just happened: the currency with the higher interest rate (the dollar, at 5.0%) is the one that is *weaker* in the forward market. That feels backwards until you see why — you are being compensated for *not* earning the higher dollar rate while your money sits in yen. The forward isn't predicting a weaker dollar; it is pricing the interest you forgo.

And the magnitude is no accident: 643 pips on a 150 spot is about 4.3%, which is essentially the 4.5% annual rate gap (the small shortfall is the compounding done exactly rather than linearly). That is the tell that the forward is *all rate gap and nothing else* — change the spot and the points scale with it, change the gap and the points move one-for-one, but no opinion about the yen's future ever enters the number. Every forward you will ever see decomposes into exactly these two ingredients: today's spot, and the rate gap over the tenor.

### Pricing the forward points precisely: the rate gap, compounded over the tenor

The single forward number is just one point on a *curve*. The same arithmetic gives you a forward for every tenor — one month, three months, a year, two years — and the points grow steadily with maturity, because the rate gap compounds the longer you wait. The exact relationship for a USD/JPY quote is `F(t) = S × (1 + r_jpy)^t / (1 + r_usd)^t`, with `t` in years.

![Line chart of forward USD/JPY by tenor falling from spot as the rate gap compounds](/imgs/blogs/fx-forwards-and-swaps-the-real-workhorses-of-the-market-3.png)

Read the curve from left to right. At one month the forward is 149.45 — barely below spot, because one month of a 4.5-percentage-point gap is only a sliver of interest. At one year it is 143.57, the number we just computed. At three years it has slid to 131.53. The forward points are not a flat haircut; they are a *fan* that widens with time, and the width is set entirely by the size of the rate gap. Double the gap, and you roughly double the points at every tenor.

This is why traders talk about "the points" as a market of their own. When a US rate cut narrows the dollar's advantage, the forward points shrink even if spot does not move a single pip. When the gap widens, the points balloon. The points are a pure, tradeable expression of the *difference* between two yield curves — which is the cleanest possible illustration of this series' spine: you never own a currency in isolation, you own a *spread* between two countries' money.

The sign of the points tells you which currency is "expensive" to hold. Because the dollar's rate is higher here, USD/JPY forwards are at a *discount* — the dollar buys fewer yen forward. Flip the pair to a case where the quote currency has the higher rate and the points flip sign. For EUR/USD, where the dollar (the quote) often out-yields the euro, the euro trades at a forward *premium* — the forward EUR/USD is *above* spot. There is no contradiction: the higher-yielding currency always trades at a forward discount, and whether that shows up as points above or below spot just depends on which currency sits in the quote slot. Once you internalise "higher rate = forward discount," you can read the direction of the points off the rate gap in your head, for any pair, without touching a calculator.

It is also worth noting what happens at the extremes. If the two rates were *equal*, the forward would equal spot and the points would be zero — there would be no time value to differentiate the two currencies. If the gap *inverts* (the low-rate currency suddenly out-yields the other), the points flip sign and the previously-premium currency starts trading at a discount. This is not hypothetical: as the Bank of Japan inches off its zero policy while the Fed cuts, the US–Japan gap narrows, and the enormous yen forward premium that funded a decade of carry trades is slowly shrinking. The forward-points curve is a live readout of the relationship between two central banks — and it moves the moment either one of them does.

#### Worked example: reading the forward points off a quote

A dealer quotes you USD/JPY one-year forward as "spot 150.00, points −643." You don't need their pricing model to sanity-check it. The annual rate gap is 5.0% − 0.5% = **4.5%**. Apply that to spot: 150.00 × 4.5% ≈ 6.75 yen of interest differential, which at this spot translates to roughly 640–645 pips of discount once you do the exact `(1.005/1.050)` ratio. Their −643 lands right in the pocket.

Now suppose the next morning the Fed has cut and the US one-year rate is 4.0%. The gap is now 3.5%. Redo it: `F = 150 × 1.005 / 1.040 = 144.95`, so the points are now about **−505**. Spot hasn't moved, but the forward jumped 138 pips closer to spot. **The lesson: a change in the rate gap moves the forward even when nobody trades a single unit of spot** — the points are a rates instrument wearing an FX costume.

### What sets the rate gap, and why the points and the spot move together

If the forward points are dictated by the rate gap, then anything that moves the gap moves the points. And the same gap that prices the forward also exerts a powerful pull on *spot* itself — because the gap is what makes one currency more attractive to hold than the other. So the rate gap is doing double duty: it mechanically sets the forward, and it economically drags the spot. The cleanest way to see both at once is to plot a pair against the gap between its two countries' rates over time.

![Dual axis chart of USD JPY versus the US Japan two year rate gap from 2019 to 2025](/imgs/blogs/fx-forwards-and-swaps-the-real-workhorses-of-the-market-5.png)

The two lines move together with almost embarrassing obedience. In 2020 the US–Japan two-year rate gap collapsed to near zero as the Fed cut to the floor, and USD/JPY sat quietly around 103–108. Then from 2022 the Fed hiked hard while the Bank of Japan held its policy rate pinned near zero, and the gap exploded to about **4.35 percentage points**. USD/JPY went straight up with it — from 115 in 2021 to 131 in 2022 to 157 by 2024 — as the wider gap both widened the forward discount on the dollar *and* pulled capital toward the higher-yielding dollar. The forward points and the spot are two readings off the same underlying instrument: the difference between two yield curves. Master the gap and you have a hand on both.

A subtle but important consequence: because the gap sets the points, the *cost of hedging* a currency is the rate gap. A US investor buying Japanese stocks who hedges the yen is, through the swap, *paying away* the entire 4.5% rate gap every year for the privilege. A Japanese investor hedging US bonds is *earning* that 4.5% — which is why hedged-yield math flips depending on which direction you fund. The rate gap is not just a pricing curiosity; it is a real cash flow that decides whether a hedge is a cost or a windfall, and it is identical to the forward points you read off a quote. We will not re-derive *why* the gap itself moves — that is monetary policy, and the macro-trading post linked at the end owns it — but every time it moves, your forwards reprice.

## The FX swap mechanic: two legs, one trade, zero directional risk

Now we can dissect the instrument that dominates the volume chart. An FX swap is *not* a bet that the exchange rate will rise or fall. It is two exchanges, agreed together, that cancel out your currency exposure and leave behind a loan.

![Before and after diagram of an FX swap near leg at spot and far leg at the forward](/imgs/blogs/fx-forwards-and-swaps-the-real-workhorses-of-the-market-4.png)

Walk through the figure. On the **near leg** (today), you pay \$1,000,000 and receive ¥150,000,000 at the spot rate of 150.00. On the **far leg** (in one year), you do the reverse: you receive your \$1,000,000 back and return yen — but at the *forward* rate of 143.57, so you return ¥143,570,000. You start with dollars, you end with dollars; the same \$1,000,000 came back to you. What changed is the yen: you handed over ¥150,000,000 and only had to return ¥143,570,000, pocketing the ¥6,430,000 difference. That difference *is* the forward points, and it is exactly the interest you earned by holding yen instead of dollars for the year.

Flip the perspective and it is even clearer. From your counterparty's seat, they *received* your dollars on the near leg and *gave them back* on the far leg — they borrowed your dollars for a year. They *gave* you yen and *got more yen back* — they earned the rate gap as the price of that dollar borrowing. An FX swap is a two-sided loan: each party borrows the currency it needs and simultaneously lends the currency it has, and the interest is settled through the difference between the two exchange rates rather than through a separate cash payment.

Because both legs are locked in at the start, **you carry no exchange-rate risk for the life of the swap**. If USD/JPY rockets to 170 or craters to 120 over the year, it doesn't matter to your swap — your far-leg price was fixed at 143.57 on day one. That is the property that makes swaps the plumbing of the market: they let you move funding around the world without taking a currency view you didn't want.

This is worth dwelling on because it is the single most counterintuitive fact in FX. A swap moves enormous sums across currencies and yet leaves your *net* currency exposure exactly where it started. The dealer who quotes you the swap isn't quoting an exchange-rate view either — they are quoting the **swap points**, which are simply the far-leg price minus the near-leg price. In dealer-speak, "USD/JPY one-year is minus 643 points" tells you everything: spot is whatever it is, and the far leg is 643 pips below it. Two dealers can disagree wildly about where USD/JPY is *going* and still quote you the same swap points, because the points are pinned by the rate gap, not by their forecast. The swap points market is therefore a *rates* market in disguise — it tracks the spread between two money-market curves, and it barely flinches when spot lurches around on a payrolls number.

There is also a tidy way to settle a swap's profit without anyone moving the full principal twice: net it. In practice, much swap activity is **non-deliverable or net-settled** for liquidity-management purposes — but the classic, deliverable FX swap really does exchange the full notional on each leg, which is exactly what makes it a true collateralised loan rather than a side bet. When you *do* deliver both legs in full, the principal you post on the near leg is what secures the loan, and that is the property the next section turns into the most important idea in the post.

#### Worked example: a \$1,000,000 one-year FX swap, leg by leg

Set it up exactly. Spot 150.00, one-year forward 143.57.

- **Near leg, today.** You pay \$1,000,000; you receive ¥150,000,000.
- **Far leg, in one year.** You receive \$1,000,000; you return ¥143,570,000.

Net yen kept: ¥150,000,000 − ¥143,570,000 = **¥6,430,000**. Convert that back at the near-leg spot of 150 to put it in dollars: ¥6,430,000 / 150 ≈ **\$42,867**. That is your "interest" for lending yen / borrowing dollars for the year — and as a percentage of the \$1,000,000 you borrowed, it is about **4.3%**, which is, to within rounding, the 4.5% rate gap (the small gap is the compounding and the conversion-at-spot detail). **The swap's economics collapse, exactly, to the interest-rate differential — there is nothing else in there.** You did not bet on the yen; you rented dollars and paid the rate gap as the fee.

## The swap as a collateralised loan in two currencies

Here is the sentence to tattoo on the inside of your eyelids: **an FX swap is a fully collateralised loan**. It is the safest way two banks can lend to each other, because neither party is ever exposed to the other's default for more than the exchange-rate wiggle on a margin.

![Graph showing an FX swap as a secured loan with currency posted as collateral](/imgs/blogs/fx-forwards-and-swaps-the-real-workhorses-of-the-market-6.png)

Trace the logic in the figure. A bank needs dollars but holds yen. On the near leg it gets the dollars it needs and gives up yen. But notice what the yen *does*: it sits with the counterparty for the whole year as **collateral**. If the dollar-borrower vanishes and never shows up for the far leg, the counterparty simply keeps the yen — and the yen it holds was worth a full \$1,000,000 at the start. The loan is secured, principal-for-principal, in hard cash. Compare that to an *unsecured* loan, where the lender hands over dollars and prays the borrower is solvent in a year. The FX swap has none of that credit terror baked in, which is precisely why it survived 2008 as a funding channel when unsecured interbank lending froze solid.

This reframing changes how you should read the whole market. An FX swap rate is a *secured funding rate*. When that rate moves away from where the two interest rates say it should be, it is not telling you anything about the exchange rate's future — it is telling you that secured dollar funding has become scarce or abundant. That gap-from-fair-value has a name: the **cross-currency basis**, and it is one of the most important stress gauges in global finance. We will get to it.

Compare the FX swap to the two obvious alternatives and its appeal jumps out. Alternative one: the Tokyo bank borrows dollars *unsecured* in the interbank market — it asks another bank to simply lend it dollars on trust. That works in calm times and vanishes the instant anyone smells trouble, because the lender bears the full risk that the borrower fails. Alternative two: the bank sells its yen for dollars in the spot market and buys them back later — but then it is *exposed* to where USD/JPY moves in between, which defeats the purpose. The FX swap threads the needle: it gets the dollars *and* posts full collateral *and* removes the exchange-rate risk, all in one trade. It is, quite literally, the safest and cheapest way to move funding between two currencies, which is why it became the default and why its volume dwarfs everything else.

The collateral mechanic also explains a quiet but crucial detail: an FX swap economises on *credit lines*. Banks ration the unsecured credit they will extend to each other, and that ration shrinks to nothing in a panic. Because the FX swap is secured by full principal, it consumes far less of that scarce credit line — you are lending against cash, not against a name. That is why, in the worst weeks of 2008 and 2020, the FX swap channel stayed open (if expensive) while the unsecured channel slammed shut. The instrument's security is not a footnote; it is the reason the global funding system has a backstop pipe at all.

The one residual risk — the exchange rate moving on the collateral before maturity — is handled the way every secured loan handles it: with **margining**. Longer-dated swaps between dealers are typically marked to market daily, and if the collateral currency weakens enough that the posted principal no longer fully covers the loan, the borrower posts more (a "margin call"). This is exactly how a repo loan or a futures position is managed, and it shrinks the swap's effective credit risk from "a year of exchange-rate moves" down to "one day's move, topped up daily." A swap that is margined every day is about as close to a riskless secured loan as two private institutions can build. The reason this matters for reading the market: when you hear that funding "dried up" in a crisis, it is rarely the *secured* swap channel that fails first — it is the unsecured one. The swap, being collateralised, is the *last* pipe to clog, which is precisely why its price (the basis) is such a sensitive gauge — by the time it is screaming, the easier channels have already gone silent.

#### Worked example: what the lender keeps if the borrower defaults

Suppose the bank that borrowed \$1,000,000 against ¥150,000,000 of collateral goes bankrupt halfway through the year and never comes back for the far leg. The lender is holding ¥150,000,000. What is that worth in dollars now?

If USD/JPY is still near 150, the yen is worth ≈ \$1,000,000 — the lender is whole. The lender simply sells the yen, recovers the dollars it lent, and walks away. The *only* way the lender loses is if the yen has weakened sharply in the meantime — say to 165 — in which case ¥150,000,000 fetches only ≈ \$909,000, a shortfall of about **\$91,000**, or 9%. **That residual exchange-rate move on the collateral is the entire credit risk of an FX swap** — which is why swaps are margined daily in practice and why they kept flowing in crises that shut every unsecured market. A secured loan with a tiny, hedgeable tail is a very different animal from lending on trust.

## Why most "FX" volume is swaps, not spot

Now the headline fact gets its explanation. Return to that opening chart: swaps are \$3.81 trillion a day, spot only \$2.10 trillion. Why would *borrowing* dwarf *exchanging*? Three reasons, and they all come back to the same thing — swaps are how the world manages funding and hedges, not how it places directional currency bets.

![Matrix of who uses FX swaps showing corporate hedgers banks and central banks](/imgs/blogs/fx-forwards-and-swaps-the-real-workhorses-of-the-market-7.png)

Look at the three users in the figure and notice what they have in common: none of them is placing a directional bet on the exchange rate. The corporate hedger is *removing* currency risk, the bank treasury is *funding* a balance sheet, the central bank is *backstopping* its banking system. The exchange rate's direction is incidental to all three — what they care about is the rate gap (the cost) and the availability of dollars (the funding). That is the deepest reason swaps dominate: the largest, steadiest flows in FX come from people who explicitly do *not* want a currency view, and the swap is the one instrument that gives them everything except a view.

**First, rolling hedges.** A multinational that hedged its euro revenue with a three-month forward has to do *something* when that forward matures and the euros it was hedging still exist. It rolls the hedge: it does an FX swap to close the old forward and open a new one. Every hedge in the world that outlives its forward's maturity generates swap volume on each roll. Hedges are short-dated and the exposures are long-lived, so the same underlying exposure churns through the swap market again and again. A pension fund hedging a decade of foreign-bond holdings with rolling three-month swaps touches the swap market forty times over the life of that one position.

**Second, funding.** A bank in Tokyo that wants to hold US Treasuries doesn't have a natural source of dollars — its depositors give it yen. So it raises dollars by swapping yen for dollars, holds the Treasuries, and rolls the swap as it matures. This is the dominant use: banks and asset managers fund foreign-currency assets through the swap market because it is cheaper and safer than borrowing the foreign currency unsecured. Trillions of dollars of cross-border investment are funded exactly this way, every day, on tenors from overnight to a few months — and short tenors mean *constant* rolling, which means enormous turnover.

There is a risk hiding inside the mechanic, and it is worth naming because it shaped the entire plumbing. When the two legs of a swap (or any FX trade) settle, the two currencies move at slightly different times in different time zones — you might pay your dollars in New York hours before you receive your yen in Tokyo. For those hours, you have paid and not yet been paid. In 1974 a German bank, Bankhaus Herstatt, was shut down by regulators in the afternoon *after* it had received Deutsche marks but *before* it had paid out the corresponding dollars — and its counterparties lost the dollars entirely. That "Herstatt risk" haunted FX settlement for decades until the industry built **CLS**, a settlement system that exchanges both legs simultaneously ("payment versus payment") so neither side is ever exposed. Roughly 40% of global FX now settles through CLS. The lesson is that the swap's *economics* are clean, but its *settlement* needed an entire piece of infrastructure to make safe — more evidence that FX is, at bottom, a plumbing business.

**Third, the short tenors compound the volume.** Because so many swaps are overnight, one-week, or one-month, the *same* funding need shows up in the turnover statistics dozens of times a year. A \$1 billion dollar-funding book run on overnight swaps generates \$1 billion of "turnover" every single day — roughly \$250 billion a year of recorded volume from a single \$1 billion need. Spot, by contrast, is a one-and-done exchange. This is why the BIS turnover numbers are dominated by swaps: they count the *flow* through a revolving door, and the door spins fastest where tenors are shortest.

The shortest-tenor swaps even have their own jargon, because they are so routine. An **overnight** swap rolls funding from today to tomorrow; a **tom-next** ("tomorrow-next") swap rolls it from tomorrow to the day after; a **spot-next** rolls from the spot date to the next day. These one-day swaps are the heartbeat of the funding market — they are how a bank that is "long dollars today, short dollars tomorrow" smooths its cash position into perfect balance every single afternoon. They are also how a position trader who is long an outright forward but doesn't want to settle the spot leg yet "rolls" the position forward a day at a time. Each roll is a fresh swap in the statistics. Multiply a few thousand banks each rolling billions every day, and the \$3.81 trillion daily figure stops being surprising — it is the sound of the entire banking system breathing in and out, one day at a time.

There is a structural reason this concentration in swaps is *stable*, not a fluke of one survey. Spot turnover has actually drifted *down* as a share over the past two decades, even as total FX turnover climbed from \$1.2 trillion a day in 2001 to \$7.5 trillion in 2022. The growth has come from swaps and forwards — from the funding-and-hedging machine, not from directional speculation. As cross-border investment grew, the world accumulated ever more foreign-currency assets that need hedging and funding, and every one of those positions feeds the swap market on a loop. The plumbing grew because the building grew.

#### Worked example: how a single funding book inflates turnover

A Tokyo bank holds \$1,000,000,000 of US Treasuries funded by rolling overnight USD/JPY swaps. Each morning it swaps to extend the funding one more day. How much "FX turnover" does this one position generate in a year?

Each daily roll is, in BIS accounting, a fresh FX swap with a \$1,000,000,000 near leg. Over roughly 250 trading days, that is **\$250,000,000,000** of recorded annual swap turnover — from a position that, economically, never changed size and never took a currency view. Multiply that across thousands of funding books and you see why swaps are half the market. **Turnover measures churn, not exposure; swaps churn because funding is short-dated and exposures are long-lived.** The "\$3.81 trillion a day" is the revolving door spinning, not \$3.81 trillion of new directional bets.

## The funding angle: when the swap price dislocates from the rate gap

Everything so far assumed covered interest parity holds — that the swap price equals exactly what the two interest rates say it should. In calm markets it nearly does. In stress, it breaks, and the *way* it breaks is one of the most reliable early-warning signals in markets.

The dislocation is measured by the **cross-currency basis**: the extra yield (positive or negative) you must add to one side of the swap to make the two interest rates actually line up with the traded forward. When dollars are scarce, everyone wants to borrow dollars through swaps, and the price of doing so rises *above* what the rate gap alone would justify. That excess shows up as a *negative basis* — a premium you pay, on top of the interest gap, just to get your hands on dollars.

![Line chart of the cross currency basis for USD/JPY and EUR/USD spiking negative in crises](/imgs/blogs/fx-forwards-and-swaps-the-real-workhorses-of-the-market-8.png)

The chart tells a crisis history at a glance. In normal times the basis hugs zero — parity roughly holds. Then the spikes: **minus 220 basis points** for USD/JPY in October 2008, **minus 145** in March 2020 as the pandemic froze funding markets. Each spike is the swap market screaming "there are not enough dollars." Note that the spot exchange rate could be doing almost anything during these episodes — the *basis* is a separate signal, living inside the swap price, that measures dollar *funding* stress directly. (For why the dollar specifically becomes the world's funding bottleneck, and how the Fed's swap lines extinguish these fires, see the dollar-system and central-bank toolkit posts linked below — this post owns the instrument; those own the system.)

This is the deepest payoff of understanding swaps. A "currency crisis" in the headlines often *is* a funding crisis in disguise, and it shows up first in the cross-currency basis, days or weeks before the spot rate cracks. The swap market is where the plumbing leaks before the flood reaches the street. The cross-currency basis even has a dedicated companion post in this series — when covered parity breaks, that is its own deep subject — but you now have the instrument-level reason it breaks: scarce secured dollars repriced through the swap.

It is worth understanding *why* the basis stopped being zero, because it reveals how the post-2008 world actually works. Before the crisis, banks could arbitrage any basis away almost for free: if dollars were even slightly expensive via swaps, a bank would borrow dollars cheaply unsecured, lend them through the swap, and pocket the difference until the gap closed. After 2008, that arbitrage got *expensive* — new regulations made banks hold capital against the size of their balance sheets (the leverage ratio), so taking on a swap position to harvest a small basis now consumes scarce, costly balance-sheet capacity. The arbitrage that used to be free now has a real cost, so the basis can sit persistently negative — a standing premium for dollars that no one finds worth arbitraging all the way to zero. You can even see it pulse at quarter-ends, when banks shrink their balance sheets for regulatory reporting and the basis briefly gaps wider before snapping back in the new quarter.

So the basis carries two distinct signals layered on top of each other. The slow, persistent negative drift is the *structural* cost of dollar funding in a regulated, balance-sheet-constrained world. The violent spikes — the −220 of 2008, the −145 of 2020 — are *crisis* signals, the moment when the demand for dollars overwhelms the system's capacity to supply them and only the Federal Reserve, opening swap lines to foreign central banks, can put the fire out. Reading the basis means separating the two: a quiet −20 is just the cost of doing business; a lurch to −100 is the smoke alarm. Either way, the information lives inside the swap price, not the spot price — which is exactly why the people who run the world's funding watch the swap screen, not the exchange-rate ticker.

#### Worked example: the hedged yield that the basis can erase

A Japanese insurer buys a US corporate bond yielding **5.5%** and hedges the dollars with rolling three-month FX swaps. Its hedged yield is the bond yield minus the cost of funding dollars through the swap. In normal times, that funding cost is roughly the US three-month rate (say **4.5%**) plus a small basis of **−0.3%**, so the hedged yield is about 5.5% − 4.5% − 0.3% = **0.7%** — thin, but positive.

Now stress hits and the basis blows out to **−1.5%**. The funding cost jumps to 4.5% + 1.5% = 6.0%, and the hedged yield becomes 5.5% − 6.0% = **−0.5%**. The same "high-yield" bond now *loses* money on a fully hedged basis, purely because the swap market repriced dollars. **The cross-currency basis is no mere technicality for these investors — it is the line between a profitable hedged position and a losing one, and it can flip in a single stressed week.** Whole waves of Japanese demand for US bonds switch on and off with this number.

#### Worked example: the dollar premium hidden in a stressed swap

In calm conditions, the three-month USD/JPY basis sits near −35 bps — small frictions, regulation, balance-sheet costs. In March 2020 it gapped to **−145 bps**. What does that 110-bp move cost a Japanese bank funding \$1,000,000,000 of dollar assets via three-month swaps?

110 basis points is 1.10% per year. On \$1,000,000,000 for three months (a quarter of a year), the *extra* cost beyond the normal rate gap is:

\$1,000,000,000 × 1.10% × 0.25 = **\$2,750,000**

So that single quarter of dollar funding cost an extra **\$2.75 million** purely because the swap market dislocated — nothing to do with the level of USD/JPY, everything to do with dollar scarcity. **The cross-currency basis turns the swap into a thermometer for dollar funding stress, and you can read the fever in basis points before it shows up anywhere on a spot screen.**

## Common misconceptions

**"The forward price tells you where the currency is going."** No. The forward is spot mechanically adjusted by the rate gap — it is a *no-arbitrage* price, not a forecast. The one-year forward USD/JPY of 143.57 does not mean the market thinks the yen will strengthen to 143.57; it means that *if* you lock in today, the rate gap forces that number. Empirically, forwards are notoriously *bad* predictors of future spot — the "forward premium puzzle" is decades of evidence that high-rate currencies don't fall the way the forward implies, which is the entire reason the carry trade exists. The forward is an interest-rate identity, not a crystal ball.

**"An FX swap is a bet on the exchange rate."** The opposite — it is the instrument you use to *avoid* an exchange-rate bet while moving funding around. Both legs are fixed at inception, so the rate can do whatever it likes and your swap's economics don't change. If you want a directional view, you trade spot or an outright forward; if you want funding without a view, you swap.

**"Spot is the biggest part of the FX market."** Spot is only \$2.10 trillion a day — about 28% of turnover. Swaps are \$3.81 trillion, just over half. The market most people equate with "trading currencies" is mostly a funding-and-hedging machine, and the directional-bet part is a minority. If you only watch spot, you are watching the smallest engine in the room.

**"Forward points are a fee the dealer charges."** The points are not a markup — they are the interest-rate differential itself, the same number that makes the two funding paths cost the same. The dealer's actual profit is a thin spread *around* the fair points (a few pips for liquid pairs), not the points themselves. Confusing the rate gap with the dealer's cut leads people to think they are being gouged when they are simply paying for the time value of money in two currencies.

**"Covered interest parity always holds, so the basis is always zero."** It held cleanly before 2008 and has been *persistently* non-zero since, especially around quarter-ends and in crises, because post-crisis bank balance sheets are scarce and regulated. The basis is a real, tradeable dislocation — a standing premium for dollars that the textbook says shouldn't exist. Treating parity as an iron law rather than a stressed approximation is how you miss the single best funding-stress gauge in markets.

## How it shows up in real markets

**September–October 2008: the dollar funding heart attack.** As Lehman fell, every non-US bank holding dollar assets scrambled for dollars to fund them, and the only channel still open was the FX swap. The USD/JPY cross-currency basis blew out to roughly **−220 bps** and the EUR/USD basis to **−160 bps** — meaning a European or Japanese bank paid up to 2.2 percentage points a year *over* the rate gap just to secure dollars. The Federal Reserve ultimately extended **\$583 billion** of central-bank swap lines by December 2008 to flood the system with dollars and pull the basis back toward zero. The spot rates were volatile but not catastrophic; the *funding* was. This is the canonical case that an FX crisis is often a swap-market crisis first.

**March 2020: the pandemic dash for cash.** History rhymed. As COVID lockdowns hit, the dollar funding squeeze returned: the USD/JPY basis snapped to about **−145 bps** and the EUR/USD basis to **−85 bps** within days. The Fed re-opened and expanded its swap lines, drawing a peak of roughly **\$449 billion** in spring 2020, and the basis normalised within weeks. A Japanese bank funding \$1 billion of Treasuries through three-month swaps ate an extra ~\$2.75 million that quarter — a tax levied entirely by the swap market's dislocation, invisible on the spot screen.

**The everyday roll: a corporate treasurer's quiet swap habit.** Far from any crisis, a German exporter that books \$50 million of US sales a quarter hedges each batch with a three-month EUR/USD forward, then rolls it with an FX swap as it matures. No drama, no view — just the mechanical re-up of a hedge, four times a year, for years. Multiply that across every multinational, pension fund, and insurer on Earth and you have the steady, enormous base flow that makes swaps half the market. The dramatic crisis spikes ride on top of an ocean of boring, daily rolling.

**The pension fund that hedges a decade with three-month swaps.** A European pension fund holds \$2 billion of US equities for the long run but reports in euros and doesn't want a decade of dollar risk layered on top of its equity bet. So it hedges with rolling three-month FX swaps: short dollars forward, roll every quarter, repeat for ten years. That single \$2 billion position generates a \$2 billion swap roughly forty times over its life — and the *cost* of each roll is the EUR/USD rate gap at that moment, which can swing from a pickup to a drag as the Fed and ECB diverge. The fund's hedging cost is therefore a live function of the rate gap, re-struck every quarter, entirely through the swap market. This is the unglamorous reality behind the turnover statistics: trillions in long-term cross-border investment, each position quietly churning the swap market on a quarterly loop, none of it a directional currency bet.

**The funding arbitrage that funds half of Tokyo.** A Japanese life insurer wants the higher yield on US corporate bonds but must report in yen and cannot stomach the currency risk. So it buys the bonds and hedges the dollars with rolling FX swaps — earning the bond yield *minus* the cost of dollar funding through the swap. When the cross-currency basis is deeply negative, that funding cost spikes and the hedged yield evaporates, sometimes turning a "high-yield" US bond into a *negative* hedged return for a yen investor. Whole waves of Japanese demand for US bonds switch on and off with the basis — a direct, multi-hundred-billion-dollar consequence of where the swap market prices dollars. The plumbing isn't a sideshow; it routes the savings of a nation.

**Emerging markets: when the forward can't be delivered.** In a currency like the Vietnamese dong, you can't freely deliver the local currency offshore, so the forward turns into a *non-deliverable* forward — a swap or forward that cash-settles the difference in dollars rather than exchanging the local currency. The same rate-gap arithmetic applies, but the forward points now also encode *devaluation pressure* that the authorities are holding back onshore. When the offshore one-year dong forward prices the currency materially weaker than the onshore band allows, it is the market betting against the peg through the only instrument it can reach. The forward-points machine is universal — but in a managed currency, the points carry an extra signal the free-float pairs don't, which is exactly why the NDF gets its own post in this series.

**The 2024 yen carry unwind seen through the swap.** When the Bank of Japan finally hiked in mid-2024 and the great yen-funded carry trade unwound, USD/JPY fell from about 162 to 142 in a few weeks. But the *first* tremor was in funding: traders who had borrowed yen cheaply (via swaps) to buy higher-yielding assets suddenly faced a rising cost of rolling that yen funding, and the rush to close those swaps fed straight into the spot move. The carry trade and the swap market are the same plumbing viewed from two angles — the carry trader's "borrow low, invest high" *is* an FX swap held open and rolled, and the unwind is that swap being slammed shut all at once.

## The takeaway: read the points, not just the price

If you take one habit from this post, make it this: when you look at a currency, look past the spot price to the forward points and the swap rate. The spot price is the noisy, opinion-driven surface; the points are the structural truth underneath, and they are dictated by the gap between two countries' interest rates with almost no room for opinion. A forward is not a forecast — it is spot plus the rate gap. An FX swap is not a currency bet — it is a collateralised loan in two currencies, with the rate gap as its rent.

This is the cleanest expression of the series' spine. You never own a currency by itself; you own the *spread* between two monies, and the forward market prices that spread explicitly. When the dollar's rate advantage widens, the forward points balloon, the carry on funding trades grows, and the whole edifice of hedges and funding books reprices — all before spot necessarily moves at all. And when secured dollars go scarce, the swap price tears away from the rate gap, the cross-currency basis goes negative, and you get your earliest warning that a funding crisis is coming — read off an instrument most people don't even know is the biggest one in the market.

In practice, three habits follow from all this. First, when you see a forward quote, *decompose* it: spot plus the rate gap. If the points look bigger than the gap can justify, you are looking at a basis dislocation, not a forecast — and that is a funding signal worth understanding. Second, when you read about hedging costs — a fund "paying away yield to hedge the yen," or "earning a pickup by hedging the dollar" — recognise that the cost or pickup *is* the rate gap, identical to the forward points, and it flips sign depending on which way you fund. Third, when a currency starts to wobble, glance at the cross-currency basis before you glance at spot; the swap market reprices funding stress first, and a sharply negative basis is the early tremor of a quake the spot screen hasn't felt yet.

So the next time a headline shouts about a currency, ask the plumber's question, not the tourist's: *what is happening in the swap market?* Half the time, that is where the real story already is — priced into an instrument that is bigger than spot, governed by arithmetic rather than opinion, and quietly funding and hedging the entire cross-border financial system one rolling leg at a time.
