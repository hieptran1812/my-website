> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — An emerging-market currency funded by foreign capital is only as strong as the next dollar that wants to come in. The carry-into-EM trade pays beautifully while the flow continues and then reverses sign in a "sudden stop" — and because the currency, the reserves and the economy all break together, a flow reversal turns into a full-blown crisis.
>
> - The trade is simple: borrow cheap developed-market money, buy a high-yield EM bond, and earn the **yield plus** whatever the currency does. While money keeps flooding in, the currency *rises*, so the trade earns the yield *and* a capital gain — it looks like a miracle.
> - The miracle is the fragility. The same inflow that lifts the currency is what has to *keep arriving* to hold it up. The trade is a bet that a flow continues, and no flow continues forever.
> - A **sudden stop** (Guillermo Calvo's term) is when the flow doesn't just slow — it *reverses*. Foreigners sell the bond, sell the currency, and run for the exit at once. The currency craters, the central bank burns reserves trying to defend it, rates spike to beg the money to stay, and the real economy seizes up. All at the same time.
> - The two canonical cases: **1997 Asia**, where the Thai baht broke and the rupiah lost **83%** of its value against the dollar; and the **2013 taper tantrum**, where one sentence from the Fed flipped EM portfolio flows from a **+\$180bn** flood toward outflows almost overnight.
> - The one number to remember: in a **\$1,000,000** carry into a 10% EM yielder, a single year that earns the **10% yield** but loses **30%** on the currency hands you a **−20%** return — and that is *before* leverage turns the loss into a wipeout.

In late May 2013, Ben Bernanke, then chair of the US Federal Reserve, sat in front of a congressional committee and answered a question about the Fed's bond-buying programme. He said that in the next few meetings, "if we see continued improvement," the Fed might "take a step down in our pace of purchases." That was it. No rate hike. No policy change. Just a hint that the river of cheap dollars the Fed had been pumping out since the 2008 crisis might, someday, flow a little more slowly.

The reaction was not subtle. Over the following weeks the currencies of India, Indonesia, Brazil, Turkey and South Africa — the so-called "Fragile Five" — fell hard. The Indian rupee lost roughly a fifth of its value against the dollar by late summer. Bond yields in those countries jumped. Central banks scrambled to raise interest rates and sell reserves to slow the bleeding. The money that had spent four years pouring *into* emerging markets to escape near-zero developed-market yields suddenly wanted to come *home*. Markets gave the episode a name that captures exactly how little it took to set off: the **taper tantrum**.

Nothing had actually happened. The Fed had not tapered. It would not even begin until December. A *hint* about a *future slowdown* in the *rate of money creation* was enough to reverse billions of dollars of flows and shake half a dozen currencies. That sensitivity — that an emerging-market currency can be knocked over by a sentence about American monetary policy — is the subject of this post. It is the closing post of this series' Vietnam and emerging-market track, and it is the bridge into the crisis stories: 1997, 1998, and every EM stress since. To understand why a currency can be that fragile, you have to understand the trade that makes it fragile in the first place.

![Hot money floods into EM then reverses violently, net EM portfolio flows by year](/imgs/blogs/the-carry-into-emerging-markets-and-the-sudden-stop-1.png)

Look at the chart above before we go further, because it is the whole story in one picture. Each bar is the net amount of foreign portfolio money flowing into emerging-market stocks and bonds in a given year. Green bars are floods — 2012 and 2021 saw roughly a quarter-trillion dollars pour in. Red bars are the reversals: 2015, 2020, 2022. The flow is not a steady stream you can lean on. It is a tide that comes in for years and then goes out in months, and a currency that floats on that tide goes out with it. This series' spine says you never own a currency in isolation — every position is a relative bet on the gap between two countries' interest rates plus the flow of money across borders. The carry-into-EM trade is that spine in its rawest, most leveraged form, and the sudden stop is what happens when the flow half of the equation turns against you all at once.

## Foundations: The carry into EM and the sudden stop

Three ideas have to be built from zero before any of the crisis stories make sense: what the carry-into-EM trade actually *is*, why "hot money" is structurally fragile, and what a *sudden stop* means mechanically. Get these three and every crisis in the back half of this post reads as the same machine running.

### What the carry-into-EM trade actually is

Start with the plainest possible version. A **carry trade** is borrowing money where it is cheap and parking it where it pays more. In currencies, you borrow a **funding currency** with a low interest rate — historically the Japanese yen or Swiss franc, currencies whose central banks held rates near zero for two decades — and you use the proceeds to hold a **high-yield currency** that pays a fat rate. The gap between the two rates is your income. We build the mechanics of the basic trade from first principles in [the carry trade — getting paid to hold a currency](/blog/trading/forex/the-carry-trade-getting-paid-to-hold-a-currency); here we take the mechanics as given and push them into emerging markets, where the gap is enormous and the danger is real.

The "carry **into EM**" version replaces the high-yield leg with an emerging-market currency and, usually, an emerging-market government bond. Instead of holding US dollars at 4.5%, you hold Brazilian real at 12.25%, or Mexican peso at 10.25%, or — at the extreme — Turkish lira at 50%. To do it, a foreign investor sells dollars (or yen), buys the local currency, and uses it to buy a local-currency government bond yielding double digits. They now own two bets stacked on top of each other: the **yield** of the bond, and the **exchange rate** between the EM currency and their home currency.

Here is the crucial asymmetry. Your total return is *not* the yield. It is the yield **plus the spot move** — whatever the exchange rate does to you while you hold. The yield is large and steady. The currency move is also large, but it is *two-sided*: it can add to your return or it can erase it. A 10% yield is wonderful right up until the currency falls 30%, at which point you have lost a fifth of your money despite the bond paying out perfectly. That single fact — yield is steady, the currency move is the whole game — is what makes EM carry both lucrative and lethal.

![The carry-into-EM inflow phase pipeline from cheap DM rates to brilliant returns](/imgs/blogs/the-carry-into-emerging-markets-and-the-sudden-stop-2.png)

The figure above traces the inflow phase, and there is one feature of it that you must internalise: it is **self-validating while it runs**. Read the chain left to right. Developed-market rates are near zero, so yield-starved capital reaches for return. It chases EM yield by buying local bonds. To buy them, it sells dollars and buys the EM currency. That buying *pushes the EM currency up*. So the foreign investor's total return is the yield *plus* a currency gain — better than they expected. The trade looks brilliant, which draws *more* money in, which pushes the currency up *more*. The inflow manufactures its own evidence that it was a good idea. This is the trap. Nothing in the boom phase warns you, because the boom phase is the inflow congratulating itself.

#### Worked example: the carry into a 10% EM yielder while the flow runs

Put real numbers on the boom. You are a developed-market fund and you take a **\$1,000,000** position into a high-yield EM bond paying **10%** a year. You convert your dollars at a spot rate of, say, 20 local-currency units per dollar, so you hold **20,000,000** local units of bonds.

- **Yield earned over the year:** 10% of \$1,000,000 = **\$100,000** in local-currency coupons.
- **Currency move while the inflow continues:** suppose the wave of foreign buying lifts the EM currency **5%** against the dollar over the year. Your 20,000,000 local units are now worth more dollars when you convert back — a **\$50,000** currency gain on the principal (roughly 5% of \$1,000,000).
- **Total return:** \$100,000 + \$50,000 = **\$150,000**, or **+15%** on your million.

You earned 15% on a bet that, on paper, was "just" a 10% bond. The extra 5% came from the currency, and the currency rose *because of the inflow you were part of*. The lesson hiding in the arithmetic: in the boom, the flow pays you twice — once in yield and once in appreciation — which is precisely why the trade gets so crowded that the reversal becomes violent.

### Why "hot money" is structurally fragile

The capital that does this trade has a nickname: **hot money**. It is "hot" because it is fast, foreign, and footloose — portfolio flows into liquid stocks and bonds that can be sold and repatriated in days, as opposed to **foreign direct investment** (FDI), which builds a factory and cannot be yanked out overnight. Hot money is attracted by yield and momentum, and it leaves the instant either turns. The distinction matters enormously: a country financed by sticky FDI is far more robust than one financed by hot portfolio money, even if the dollar totals look similar.

Why is hot money fragile? Because of a structural feature of the inflow phase that the boom hides. When foreigners buy a country's bonds with borrowed dollars, they create a **currency mismatch** somewhere in the system. The foreign investor's *liability* (the borrowed yen or dollars) is in hard currency, but the *asset* they hold (the EM bond) is in soft local currency. Often the local borrowers — companies, banks — have the mirror-image problem: they borrowed cheap dollars and earn local-currency revenue. As long as the exchange rate is stable, nobody notices. The moment the local currency falls, every dollar of hard-currency debt suddenly costs more in local terms, and the whole chain of borrowers scrambles for dollars at once. The mismatch is a landmine laid quietly during the boom and stepped on during the bust.

The deeper fragility is simpler still: **the trade is a bet that the flow continues.** The currency is held up by the act of buying. To *keep* it up, money must *keep* arriving. The instant net new inflows stop — not even reverse, just *stop* — the upward pressure on the currency disappears. And because everybody in the trade knows everybody else is in the same trade (it is mechanical, obvious, and crowded), the first sign that the flow is slowing makes everyone reach for the exit at once. A flow that depends on its own continuation is the financial equivalent of a bicycle: stable while moving, and it falls over the moment it stops.

#### Worked example: the leverage that turns a flow reversal into a crisis

The boom example used unlevered cash. Real carry desks do not. The whole point of carry is that the per-trade return is modest, so players borrow against it to make it worth doing. Take the same **\$1,000,000** of capital, but run it at **5× leverage** — a \$5,000,000 EM bond position funded by \$4,000,000 of borrowed dollars.

- **In the boom:** the bond earns 10% on \$5,000,000 = \$500,000, minus, say, 1% borrowing cost on \$4,000,000 = \$40,000, plus the 5% currency gain on \$5,000,000 = \$250,000. Net ≈ **\$710,000** on \$1,000,000 of capital — a **+71%** year. Leverage made the miracle even more miraculous.
- **In the stop:** the currency falls **30%**. That is a \$1,500,000 loss on the \$5,000,000 position — *one and a half times your entire capital*. You are wiped out, and you still owe the \$4,000,000 you borrowed. The lender margin-calls you; you must sell the bond into a falling market to raise dollars, which pushes the currency *down further*, which margin-calls the next fund.

The same 5× that turned a 15% boom into a 71% boom turns a 20% loss into a *total wipeout plus a margin call*. Leverage is the multiplier that converts a flow reversal from "a bad year" into "a forced-selling cascade" — and a forced-selling cascade across an entire crowded trade *is* a currency crisis.

### What a sudden stop is, mechanically

The term **sudden stop** comes from the economist Guillermo Calvo, who borrowed a banker's adage: "it is not speed that kills, it is the sudden stop." A sudden stop is when capital inflows to a country don't gently taper — they abruptly halt and reverse. The current-account deficit that the inflows were quietly financing can no longer be financed, so it has to close, fast, the only way it can: through a collapse in the currency, a collapse in imports, and a collapse in domestic demand.

![The sudden stop doom loop from trigger to currency crisis](/imgs/blogs/the-carry-into-emerging-markets-and-the-sudden-stop-3.png)

The figure above is the machine. Some **trigger** — a Fed hike, a global risk-off shock, a wobble in the EM's own growth or politics — makes the inflows *stop and reverse*. Foreigners sell the bond, sell the currency, and head for the exit. The currency **falls** as capital exits. Now watch the doom loop, because this is what makes it a *crisis* rather than just a cheaper currency. The central bank tries to defend the currency two ways: it **sells reserves** (its hoard of dollars) to buy back its own currency, and it **hikes interest rates** brutally to beg the foreign money to stay. Both defences hurt the real economy — high rates choke credit, and the reserve drain signals weakness. The economy slides into recession. The recession makes the country look *worse*, which makes *more* foreigners leave, which weakens the currency *more*. The currency, the reserves, and the GDP all crater **together**, each one's collapse accelerating the others. That simultaneity is the definition of a currency crisis, and it is why a sudden stop is so much worse than the sum of its parts.

There is a deep connection here to bank runs, and it is worth naming because it explains the *self-fulfilling* quality of these crises. A country with short-term foreign debt and limited reserves is in exactly the position of a bank with demand deposits and illiquid loans: if everyone tries to withdraw at once, there isn't enough to go around, so the rational move for each individual lender is to run *first* — which makes the run happen. The formal coordination-game model of this is laid out in [bank runs as coordination games — Diamond–Dybvig and SVB](/blog/trading/game-theory/bank-runs-as-coordination-games-diamond-dybvig-and-svb); a sudden stop is a bank run on a *country*, where the "deposits" are foreign portfolio holdings and the "bank" is the central bank's reserve stack. The speculative-attack version of the same logic — where traders coordinate to *break* a peg deliberately — gets its own treatment in [currency crises and the anatomy of a speculative attack](/blog/trading/forex/currency-crises-and-the-anatomy-of-a-speculative-attack).

### The balance-of-payments accounting that makes it inevitable

One more foundation makes the sudden stop feel less like a mystery and more like arithmetic. Every country's external dealings are tracked in the **balance of payments**, which has two big halves that, by construction, sum to zero. The **current account** records the flow of goods, services, and income — roughly, whether the country is earning more from the world than it spends, or the reverse. The **capital and financial account** records the flow of money and assets — foreign money coming in to buy bonds, stocks, and factories, or domestic money going out.

Here is the iron rule: if a country runs a **current-account deficit** — it imports more than it exports, spending more abroad than it earns — that deficit *must* be financed by an equal and opposite **capital-account surplus**, money flowing in. There is no third option; the books must balance. So a country with a 5% -of-GDP current-account deficit is, every single year, dependent on foreigners sending in capital equal to 5% of its GDP. That capital is the carry-into-EM flow. The current-account deficit is not a side issue — it is the *reason* the country needs the hot money, and the size of the deficit is the size of the dependency.

Now you can see why the stop is a forced adjustment, not a choice. When the capital inflow stops, the current-account deficit it was financing *cannot continue* — the money to pay for the excess imports simply isn't there. The deficit has to close, immediately, the only way it can: the currency falls (making imports expensive and exports cheap), imports collapse, and domestic demand contracts until the country stops spending more than it earns. A sudden stop is the balance of payments violently re-balancing after its financing disappears. The current-account deficit that looked sustainable in the boom — because the financing was flowing — is revealed as a vulnerability the instant the financing stops. The full mechanics of the current account and how it pulls on a currency are in [the balance of payments and the current account](/blog/trading/forex/the-balance-of-payments-and-the-current-account); for our purposes, the key is that a current-account-deficit country financed by hot money has *pre-committed* to a crisis if the flow ever reverses.

This is also why the surplus countries — the ones that *earn* more abroad than they spend — are so much more robust. A country running a current-account surplus does not depend on foreign capital to balance its books; it is a net *lender* to the world, not a borrower, so a sudden stop has far less to bite on. Vietnam's large goods-trade surplus, which we return to at the end, is exactly this kind of structural protection: a country that earns its dollars rather than borrowing them is not at the mercy of the next foreign portfolio dollar.

## The inflow phase: why the boom feels so safe

The cruelty of the sudden stop is that the years before it are wonderful, and the wonder is what builds the vulnerability. To see why so much money piles in, look at the yields on offer.

![The EM yields that bait the carry trade, 2024 policy rates by currency](/imgs/blogs/the-carry-into-emerging-markets-and-the-sudden-stop-6.png)

The chart shows policy rates in 2024. On the left, in slate, are the funders and the major developed currencies: the yen at 0.25%, the franc at 0.50%, the dollar at 4.50%, the Aussie at 4.35%. On the right, in amber, are the EM high-yielders that bait the carry: the South African rand at 7.75%, the Mexican peso at 10.25%, the Brazilian real at 12.25%, and the Turkish lira at a staggering 50%. Borrow yen at a quarter of a percent and hold lira at fifty, and the *carry* — the raw rate gap — is nearly 50 percentage points a year. The size of that gap is the size of the bait.

But notice the warning hidden in the amber bars: **the bigger the yield, the riskier the country.** Turkey pays 50% not out of generosity but because its inflation is rampant and its currency has been falling for years; the high nominal rate is the market demanding compensation for expected depreciation. This is the textbook prediction of **uncovered interest parity** — the idea that a high-yielding currency should, on average, depreciate by exactly enough to cancel its yield advantage, so carry earns nothing in expectation. In practice the relation fails for long stretches (high-yielders hold up or even rise while the inflow runs), which is *why* carry is profitable most of the time — but it fails *systematically*, paying out its entire debt in the crash. The full story of why the parity relation breaks in the calm and reasserts itself in the storm is in [uncovered interest parity and why it fails — the forward puzzle](/blog/trading/forex/uncovered-interest-parity-and-why-it-fails-the-forward-puzzle); for our purposes, the high yield is not free money, it is a risk premium you are being paid to bear, and the risk is the sudden stop.

#### Worked example: the rate gap you bank into Brazil while the calm holds

Make the boom-phase yield concrete with a real-ish pair. In 2024, Brazil's policy rate (the Selic) was about **12.25%** and the Fed's was about **4.50%**. You borrow dollars and hold Brazilian real bonds.

- **Raw carry (rate gap):** 12.25% − 4.50% = **7.75%** a year, banked just for holding the position, before any currency move.
- On a **\$1,000,000** position, that is **\$77,500** a year in pure rate-gap income.
- Add a currency that holds steady or drifts up 3% on continued inflows, and your total is closer to **\$107,500**, or **+10.75%** — handsomely above anything a dollar bond pays.

The intuition: in the calm, the trade pays a fat, visible, daily-accruing premium that shows up on your screen as a smooth upward line. That smoothness is exactly what lulls the next investor in — and the more investors the smoothness attracts, the more crowded and fragile the position underneath it becomes.

### The currency that rises on its own demand

There is a reflexive quality to the inflow phase that deserves a name, because it is what makes the bust so abrupt. The EM currency is rising *because* people are buying it, and people are buying it *because* it is rising. The price action and the flow feed each other. A trader looking at a chart of, say, the Mexican peso strengthening steadily through a carry-friendly period sees confirmation that the trade is working — momentum and carry pointing the same way, the dream combination. Trend-followers pile in on the momentum; carry traders pile in on the yield; the two factors reinforce each other and the currency climbs.

This is why EM carry positions get so *crowded*. The same handful of high-yield currencies attract the same global pool of yield-hungry capital using the same logic at the same time. There is no diversity of opinion to absorb a reversal, because everyone arrived at the position by the same reasoning. When the music stops, every holder tries to exit the identical trade through the same narrow door simultaneously — and an EM currency's "door" (its daily FX liquidity) is a fraction of the size of the dollar's. The crowding that makes the boom smooth is the same crowding that makes the bust a stampede.

## The reversal: how the flow flips sign

Now the turn. What actually makes the flood become an outflow? The honest answer is that it usually takes very little, because the position is a coiled spring. But the triggers fall into recognisable categories.

The first and most common trigger is **the dollar funding side, not the EM side**. This is the single most counterintuitive fact about EM crises: they are usually *made in Washington, not in the capital that gets hit.* When the Fed raises rates or even hints at slowing its money printing — the taper tantrum — the *relative* attraction of EM yield shrinks and, more importantly, the cost and scarcity of the dollars that fund the trade rise. Global investors don't need to reach as far for yield, and the leverage that powered the trade gets more expensive. The carry-into-EM trade is, at its core, a short-dollar position; when the dollar gets stronger and scarcer, that position bleeds, and the bleeding forces the unwind. We treat the dollar's outsized, destabilising role in EM specifically in [the dollar as a wrecking ball for emerging markets](/blog/trading/forex/the-dollar-as-a-wrecking-ball-for-emerging-markets), and the broader dollar-funding mechanics in [the global dollar shortage and central-bank swap lines](/blog/trading/forex/the-global-dollar-shortage-and-central-bank-swap-lines).

The second trigger is a **risk-off shock** anywhere in the world. EM carry is a "risk-on" position — it works when investors are confident and reaching for yield. Any event that makes the global investor flinch — a banking scare, a geopolitical shock, an equity sell-off — sends capital home to the safety of the dollar regardless of what is happening in the EM itself. This is the "dollar smile": the dollar strengthens both when the US is booming *and* when the world is panicking, and EM gets hit in the panic case for reasons that have nothing to do with its own fundamentals. We cover that pattern in [the dollar smile — why the dollar wins in boom and in panic](/blog/trading/forex/the-dollar-smile-why-the-dollar-wins-in-boom-and-in-panic).

The third trigger is **a wobble in the EM's own story** — a current-account deficit that widens, an election that spooks markets, a credit-rating downgrade, a sudden realisation that the country's external debt is larger than thought. Once the market's attention turns to a country's vulnerabilities, the same numbers that were ignored in the boom become headlines. The numbers do not have to change; the *attention* changes. A current-account deficit that the market happily financed at 5% of GDP in the boom becomes, the morning after the trigger, the single statistic every analyst quotes as proof the currency must fall. Crises are as much a shift in narrative as a shift in fundamentals — the fundamentals were often there all along, ignored.

What links all three triggers is that they are *coordinating events*. The position is a coiled spring held by a crowd, and each member of the crowd is watching the others. A trigger does not have to be large; it has to be *common knowledge* — something every holder sees at once, which makes every holder suspect every other holder is about to sell. That shared focal point is what turns a slow drift into a stampede, and it is why a single sentence from a Fed chair can do what months of deteriorating data could not.

![The same trade in boom and bust, yield steady while the currency leg flips](/imgs/blogs/the-carry-into-emerging-markets-and-the-sudden-stop-4.png)

The before-and-after figure isolates the asymmetry that makes the reversal so damaging. On the left, the inflow boom: you earn the 10% yield, the currency adds 5%, total around +15%, and it feels easy. On the right, the reversal: you *still earn the 10% yield* (assuming no default), but the currency drops 30% in the stop, and your total swings to roughly −20% — and leverage makes it far worse. The point the figure proves is that **the yield leg barely changes between boom and bust; the entire swing is in the currency.** You are not really being paid 10% to own a bond. You are being paid 10% to be short a crash in the currency. The crash, when it comes, hands back years of that premium in weeks.

#### Worked example: the stop that turns 10% yield into a 20% loss

This is the headline arithmetic of the whole post, so let us do it cleanly. You hold a **\$1,000,000** unlevered carry into a 10% EM yielder. The flow reverses and the currency falls **30%** against the dollar over the episode.

- **Yield earned:** +10% on \$1,000,000 = **+\$100,000** (the bond still pays).
- **Currency loss on the principal:** −30% on the \$1,000,000 of currency exposure = **−\$300,000**.
- **Total return:** +\$100,000 − \$300,000 = **−\$200,000**, or **−20%** on the year.

A full year of a fat 10% yield was not remotely enough to cover the currency move; the spot loss was three times the income. And this is the *unlevered, no-default* case. Add 5× leverage and the −\$300,000 currency loss becomes −\$1,500,000 on a \$5,000,000 position — more than your capital. The intuition to carry forward: in EM carry, the yield is the small, certain thing and the currency is the large, sudden thing, so the trade's true risk lives entirely in the tail you cannot see while the calm holds.

## The sudden stop itself: when everything breaks at once

Once the flow reverses, the central bank is forced into a defence it usually cannot win, and the tools it reaches for are the very things that deepen the crisis. This is the part where the doom loop closes.

The first defence is **selling reserves**. A central bank's foreign-exchange reserves are its war chest of dollars (and euros, and gold). To defend the currency, it sells dollars and buys back its own currency, absorbing the foreigners' selling. The mechanics of this defence — and why it so often fails — are the subject of [how central banks intervene in the currency market](/blog/trading/forex/how-central-banks-intervene-in-the-currency-market). The trouble is that reserves are finite, and in a real sudden stop the outflow can dwarf them. Every dollar the central bank spends defending the currency is a dollar of ammunition gone, and the market can count.

![Reserves as ammunition, months of imports covered by FX reserves across EM in 2024](/imgs/blogs/the-carry-into-emerging-markets-and-the-sudden-stop-7.png)

The chart shows the standard yardstick for whether a country has enough ammunition: how many **months of imports** its reserves could cover. The rough adequacy rule of thumb is about three months (the dashed line). China, with 14 months, and India, with 11, are well armed. Thailand and the Philippines, at 8, are comfortable. But Turkey at 4, and Vietnam at 3, sit right at or below the line — fighting a sudden stop with a near-empty clip. A country with thin reserves cannot credibly defend its currency for long, and *the market knows it*, which means the defence often fails before it starts. Speculators, seeing a reserve stack that can be exhausted, are *encouraged* to attack precisely because the defence is visibly finite. The reserve adequacy of a specific EM and the politics of intervention deserve their own treatment, which is why the broader currency-crisis and reserves story threads through [currency crises and the anatomy of a speculative attack](/blog/trading/forex/currency-crises-and-the-anatomy-of-a-speculative-attack).

The second defence is **hiking interest rates** — sometimes brutally. If the currency is falling because money is leaving, the central bank can try to make the money stay by paying it more. Turkey, Brazil, and others have at times pushed overnight rates to 50% or higher to defend a currency. But this is a defence that wounds the patient: punishing rates choke domestic credit, push the economy into recession, and bankrupt local borrowers — especially the ones with the currency mismatch, who now face both a more expensive dollar debt *and* a domestic recession. The defence that saves the currency can kill the economy, which is why central banks often cannot hike enough to actually win.

The third constraint is the **impossible trinity**, and it is the deep reason no defence can fully work. A country cannot simultaneously have a fixed (or heavily managed) exchange rate, free movement of capital across its borders, and an independent monetary policy. It can pick two. During the boom, EM countries enjoyed all three at once — a stable currency, open capital flows, and their own interest rates — because the flow was cooperating. The sudden stop is the trinity collecting its debt: to defend the currency, the country must either close its capital account (stop the money leaving) or surrender its monetary policy (hike rates to whatever the defence requires). It cannot keep all three. We build this constraint from first principles in [the impossible trinity — pick two of three](/blog/trading/forex/the-impossible-trinity-pick-two-of-three); here, the trinity is the cage the central bank discovers it has been living in all along.

#### Worked example: the central bank burning reserves it doesn't have

Put the reserve drain in numbers. Suppose a mid-sized EM has **\$80bn** of reserves — about 3 months of imports — and faces foreign holders trying to pull out **\$120bn** of portfolio money in a sudden stop. The central bank starts selling dollars to defend the currency.

- It can absorb at most **\$80bn** of the selling before the cupboard is bare — and it will never spend the *last* dollar, because reserves below a critical level trigger a worse panic, so the usable defence is more like **\$50–60bn**.
- That leaves **\$60bn+** of selling with no buyer but the open market — which means the currency *must* fall to clear it. Say it has to fall **30%** to entice value buyers and choke off imports enough to balance the flows.
- Meanwhile, every billion of reserves sold *also shrinks* the country's import cover, so a defence that starts at 3 months of imports might end at 1.5 — moving the country from "fragile" to "in crisis" on the very metric the market watches.

The lesson: a central bank with reserves smaller than the outflow is not defending the currency, it is *rationing the decline* and burning its credibility doing so. The defence buys days, not safety, and the market prices the inevitable break in advance.

## Common misconceptions

**"A high yield means a strong currency."** Exactly backwards as a *forward-looking* statement. A currency yields 50% because the market expects it to *lose* value — the rate is compensation for expected depreciation, per uncovered interest parity. The yield is a danger sign, not a strength sign. Turkey's lira paid carry traders enormously in nominal terms over the 2010s and still *destroyed* them, falling from about 1.8 per dollar in 2010 to past 30 per dollar by the mid-2020s. The carry never came close to covering the currency collapse. High yield is the market quoting you the risk; it is not a gift.

**"The currency only falls when the country's fundamentals deteriorate."** Often false, and dangerously so. The taper tantrum was triggered by *US* monetary policy, not by anything that changed in India or Indonesia. EM currencies get hit by global risk-off events, dollar-funding squeezes, and contagion from *other* countries — forces entirely outside the EM's control. A country can do everything right and still get caught in a sudden stop because the Fed sneezed or a neighbour broke. This is why "my fundamentals are fine" is no defence: in a sudden stop, the market sells the whole asset class first and sorts out the details later.

**"A 30% currency drop is a 30% loss."** Only if you are unlevered and there is no default. With leverage, a 30% currency move can be a total wipeout — our 5× example turned a 30% drop into a loss larger than the entire capital. And if the currency collapse pushes the government or local borrowers into *default* on the bond itself, you lose the principal *and* the currency, stacking two losses. The 30% figure is the floor of the damage in a real crisis, not the ceiling. We treat the credit-risk leg — the chance the bond itself doesn't pay — in [emerging-market and sovereign debt — yield with country risk](/blog/trading/fixed-income/emerging-market-and-sovereign-debt-yield-with-country-risk).

**"Big reserves make a country safe."** They help, but reserves can be smaller than the hot-money stock they would have to redeem, and they can be drained faster than anyone expects. More subtly, *reported* reserves can overstate usable reserves — some are pledged, illiquid, or already committed in forward contracts (Thailand in 1997 had quietly sold most of its reserves *forward*, so its headline number was a fiction). Reserves are necessary but not sufficient; what matters is reserves *relative to* the short-term external liabilities that can run.

**"Floating the currency avoids the crisis."** Floating avoids the specific drama of a *peg break*, but it does not make the country immune to a sudden stop. A freely floating EM currency simply takes the adjustment continuously, in the price, rather than in one discrete snap — the rupee, the real, and the rand all float and all sold off hard in 2013. Floating changes the *shape* of the pain (a slide instead of a cliff), not the existence of it. The peg-versus-float spectrum and what each choice actually buys you is laid out in [pegs, bands and crawling pegs — the spectrum of FX regimes](/blog/trading/forex/pegs-bands-and-crawling-pegs-the-spectrum-of-fx-regimes).

## How it shows up in real markets

Now the cases. Each is the same machine — inflow boom, trigger, sudden stop, doom loop — running on different countries in different decades.

### 1997: the Asian financial crisis

The 1997 Asian crisis is the textbook sudden stop, and it is worth telling carefully because every element is there in its purest form. Through the early 1990s, Southeast Asian economies — Thailand, Indonesia, Korea, Malaysia, the Philippines — ran what looked like a miracle. High growth, stable currencies pegged to the dollar, and a flood of foreign capital chasing that growth. The pegs were the key: because the local currency was fixed to the dollar, foreign lenders and local borrowers alike *behaved as if there were no currency risk*. Local banks and companies borrowed dollars cheaply (because dollar rates were lower than local rates) and invested in local-currency assets — real estate, factories, stocks. The currency mismatch was enormous and invisible, because the peg made it feel safe.

The trigger had several parts. The US dollar strengthened in the mid-1990s, dragging the pegged Asian currencies up with it and hurting their exports. China had devalued in 1994, taking export share. Current-account deficits widened. And the foreign capital that had been pouring in began to notice the cracks. In Thailand, speculators — sensing the baht peg was unsustainable given the country's thin (and partly forward-sold) reserves — attacked it. The Bank of Thailand burned through its reserves defending the 25-baht peg, and on **July 2, 1997**, it gave up and let the baht float. It immediately collapsed.

![How one sudden stop became a contagion across Asia in 1997](/imgs/blogs/the-carry-into-emerging-markets-and-the-sudden-stop-8.png)

What happened next is the most important lesson of 1997: **contagion.** The baht's break did not stay in Thailand. It taught the market a general rule — *every economy with the same shape (a dollar peg, large dollar-denominated debt, a current-account deficit, hot-money financing) is fragile* — and the market re-priced all of them at once. The figure traces it: Thailand breaks, markets re-price all of pegged, dollar-borrowing Asia, and the stop jumps to Indonesia, Korea, and Malaysia in turn. Contagion is not magic; it is re-pricing by analogy. Once one country reveals the vulnerability, every similar country is sold next, regardless of whether its own situation had actually changed.

![1997 the pegs that broke fell 38% to 83% versus the dollar](/imgs/blogs/the-carry-into-emerging-markets-and-the-sudden-stop-5.png)

The damage was staggering, and the figure shows it. The Indonesian rupiah, the worst hit, fell **83%** against the dollar — from about 2,400 per dollar to nearly 16,800 at the trough. The Thai baht and Korean won each fell about **54%**. The Malaysian ringgit fell **47%**, the Philippine peso **38%**. For the carry traders and the local dollar-borrowers, these were catastrophic numbers. A foreign investor who had earned, say, 12% a year holding Indonesian assets lost over 80% of their *principal* in months — a decade of carry wiped out many times over. Local companies that had borrowed dollars saw their debt, in rupiah terms, multiply six-fold while their revenues collapsed in a deep recession. The IMF arrived with bailouts and harsh conditions; governments fell, including Indonesia's Suharto after three decades in power. The full narrative of the crisis, including its political aftermath, is told in [the Asian financial crisis of 1997](/blog/trading/finance/asian-financial-crisis-1997).

#### Worked example: the rupiah carry trader's decade-in-a-month wipeout

Put a carry trader inside the 1997 rupiah collapse to feel the asymmetry at full force. Suppose in early 1997 you put **\$1,000,000** into Indonesian rupiah money-market instruments paying a juicy **15%** a year, converting at the pre-crisis rate of about 2,400 rupiah per dollar — so you hold **2,400,000,000** rupiah.

- **Yield you would earn in a full year:** 15% of \$1,000,000 = **\$150,000**. Tempting.
- **What the currency did:** the rupiah fell from 2,400 to about 16,800 per dollar at the trough — an **83%** collapse in dollar terms. Your 2,400,000,000 rupiah, converted back, is now worth about **\$143,000**.
- **Total:** you started with \$1,000,000 and end with roughly **\$143,000** of principal plus a sliver of accrued yield — a loss of around **\$850,000**, or **−85%**, in months.

To earn back an 85% loss you need a 567% gain. No carry yield on earth covers that. The intuition: the rupiah trade had paid a fat 15% for years, and the sudden stop handed back more than five *decades* of that premium in a few months. This is negative skew in its most extreme historical form — and it is why the proverb calls carry "picking up pennies in front of a steamroller," a pattern we dissect in full in [carry crashes — picking up pennies in front of a steamroller](/blog/trading/forex/carry-crashes-picking-up-pennies-in-front-of-a-steamroller).

The deepest lesson of 1997 is about the peg itself. The fixed exchange rate was not a safety feature — it was the *thing that built the vulnerability*, by suppressing the currency risk that should have made everyone borrow more carefully. The peg lulled the system into the currency mismatch, and the peg break is what detonated it. A managed or pegged EM currency is calm right up until it isn't, and the calm is doing the damage.

### 1998: Russia, LTCM, and the carry unwind that crossed back

A year after Asia, the same machine ran again with a twist that matters for traders: the 1998 Russian crisis showed how an EM sudden stop can detonate the *funding* side of the carry trade and blow up sophisticated players far from the original country. Russia, financing large deficits with short-term ruble debt that paid eye-watering yields, was a magnet for carry capital. In August 1998 it did the thing that was supposed to be unthinkable for a country with nuclear weapons: it *defaulted* on its domestic debt and devalued the ruble, which collapsed from about **6 to 21 per dollar** — a fall of roughly 70%.

The interesting part is what happened next, thousands of miles away. The Russian default triggered a global flight to safety. Investors dumped risky positions and bought safe ones, which meant *unwinding carry trades everywhere*. Because so many of those trades were funded in yen — borrow cheap yen, buy higher-yielding everything — the unwind meant buying yen back en masse. The dollar/yen rate, which had been drifting around 136, collapsed to **112 in three trading days** in early October 1998 as the funded trades were violently closed. The hedge fund Long-Term Capital Management, running enormous leveraged convergence trades, was caught on the wrong side and had to be rescued in an organised bailout to prevent its forced liquidation from cascading through the system. The full LTCM story is told in [LTCM 1998 — when genius failed](/blog/trading/finance/ltcm-1998-when-genius-failed).

The lesson 1998 adds to 1997 is about *transmission*. A sudden stop in one EM does not stay contained to that EM or even to emerging markets at all. Because the carry trade is funded in a handful of global currencies and run with leverage by interconnected players, the unwind transmits through the *funding currency* and the *leverage chain* to hit positions that have nothing to do with the original country. The Russian default broke a Connecticut hedge fund through the yen. That is the carry-into-EM trade's deepest danger: its failures are not local, because its plumbing is global.

### 2013: the taper tantrum

If 1997 is the sudden stop in its full, peg-breaking, contagious horror, the 2013 taper tantrum is the *modern, floating-currency* version — gentler in mechanism but identical in cause. By 2013, most large EMs floated their currencies, so there was no peg to break dramatically. Instead, the adjustment came continuously through the price.

The setup was four years of post-2008 zero-rate policy in the developed world. With US, European, and Japanese rates near zero and central banks printing money through quantitative easing, yield-starved capital had flooded into EM bonds and currencies — the carry-into-EM trade at industrial scale. Then came Bernanke's May 2013 testimony, the single sentence about possibly slowing bond purchases. The reaction, as we saw, was instant and violent: the "Fragile Five" — India, Indonesia, Brazil, Turkey, South Africa — sold off hard, with the rupee falling roughly 20% by late summer.

Crucially, *nothing about these countries had changed* in May 2013. The trigger was entirely external — a hint about future US policy. The taper tantrum is the cleanest possible proof of this post's central claim: an EM currency held up by foreign inflows is hostage to the policy of the country those inflows come from. When the Fed even *gestures* toward tightening, the carry-into-EM trade unwinds, and the currencies that floated on it sink. Looking back at the flows chart from the top of this post, 2013's net inflow of **+\$180bn** was already well below 2012's **+\$250bn**, and it kept deteriorating into the outright outflow years that followed. The mechanism connecting Fed policy to EM currencies through the rate-differential and flow channels is laid out in [the carry-trade unwinds — 1998, 2008, 2024](/blog/trading/macro-trading/carry-trade-unwinds-1998-2008-2024-when-leverage-breaks).

### 2015, 2018 and 2022: the modern stops

The pattern keeps recurring because its causes are structural. **2015** saw a major EM outflow — the red bar of roughly −\$40bn in the flows chart — as the Fed prepared its first post-crisis rate hike and China devalued the yuan, hitting commodity exporters and EM broadly. **2018** was the "EM crisis that didn't quite happen" but came close: a strengthening dollar and rising US rates squeezed Argentina (which went back to the IMF) and Turkey (whose lira fell roughly 40% that year) in classic sudden-stop fashion, even as the broad EM flow stayed positive. The wrecking-ball dynamic of a strong dollar hitting EM specifically is the subject of [the dollar as a wrecking ball for emerging markets](/blog/trading/forex/the-dollar-as-a-wrecking-ball-for-emerging-markets).

**2022** was the biggest modern stop, with the flows chart showing a −\$80bn outflow. The Fed hiked rates at the fastest pace in four decades to fight inflation, the dollar surged to multi-decade highs, and EM currencies — and bonds, and equities — sold off together. Some EMs that had hiked *early* and aggressively (Brazil, Mexico) weathered it far better than the developed world, an interesting reversal of the usual story; but the broad EM asset class still suffered the textbook outflow as dollar funding tightened globally.

Across all of them, the through-line is the same. The carry-into-EM trade is a leveraged bet that the flow of foreign money continues. When the dollar strengthens, when US rates rise, or when global risk appetite turns, the flow reverses, the currency falls, the central bank is forced into a defence it usually cannot win, and the loss in the currency dwarfs years of yield. The only things that change from episode to episode are the names of the countries and the size of the leverage.

### Where Vietnam sits in this picture

Vietnam is a useful closing case because it shows the spectrum between "free-floating EM that takes the hit in the price" and "pegged EM that breaks." Vietnam runs a *managed crawl*: the State Bank of Vietnam sets a daily central reference rate and allows the dong to trade within a band (currently ±5%), letting the dong depreciate gradually and in one direction rather than floating freely or pegging hard. The dong fell from about 23,650 per dollar at end-2022 to roughly 26,300 by 2025 — a steady, controlled slide rather than a snap.

This managed approach has costs and benefits visible in the data. On the protective side, Vietnam runs a large goods-trade surplus, steady FDI inflows of \$20–25bn a year, and remittances around \$16bn — *sticky* sources of dollars, not hot money, which is what makes its currency far more robust than a portfolio-flow-financed economy. On the vulnerable side, its reserves sit at only about 3 months of imports (right at the adequacy line in our reserves chart), and it drew them down hard — from \$109bn in 2021 to about \$87bn in 2022 — defending the dong during the 2022 dollar surge. Vietnam is a managed-crawl EM that leans on FDI and remittances rather than hot money, which is precisely why it has never had a 1997-style sudden stop: its dollar financing is structural, not footloose. The interplay of Vietnam's foreign flows, its room limits, and the strategic games around them is explored in [the Vietnam case study on foreign flows and room limits](/blog/trading/game-theory/case-study-vietnam-foreign-flows-room-limits-and-manipulation-games).

## The takeaway: read the currency by reading the flow

Here is how to actually *use* all of this when you look at an emerging-market currency.

First, **the yield is the bait, not the edge.** When you see an EM currency paying 10%, 15%, 50%, do not read it as "free money." Read it as the market pricing in expected depreciation and crisis risk. The higher the yield, the more the market is warning you. Your real position when you hold EM carry is *short a crash in the currency*, and you are collecting the yield as the premium for selling that crash insurance. Price the insurance, not the coupon.

Second, **the currency is held up by a flow, so watch the flow, not the level.** An EM currency's strength in the boom is not evidence of safety — it is evidence that money is still arriving. The number that matters is not where the currency *is*, it is whether net foreign inflows are still positive and accelerating, or slowing. The flows chart at the top of this post is the single most important picture for an EM currency, because it shows the tide that the currency floats on. When the green bars start shrinking, the currency is already in danger, even if its price is still rising.

Third, **the trigger is usually external, so watch the Fed and the dollar.** The most reliable warning sign for an EM sudden stop is not in the EM at all — it is a strengthening dollar and a tightening Fed. The carry-into-EM trade is a short-dollar position; when the dollar turns, the trade bleeds, and the bleeding forces the unwind. If you hold EM and the dollar starts to rally, your clock is ticking regardless of how good the local story looks.

Fourth, **check the ammunition.** Before you trust a currency to be defended, count the reserves *relative to* the short-term external debt and hot-money stock that could run. A central bank with three months of import cover facing an outflow larger than its reserves is not defending the currency — it is rationing the decline. Reserves smaller than the runnable money are a fiction of safety.

Fifth, **distinguish the shape of the financing, not just its size.** Two EM countries can have identical headline foreign-capital inflows and completely different risk profiles depending on *what kind* of money it is. Sticky FDI that builds factories and stays for a decade is robust; hot portfolio money that can be sold in a morning is fragile. Long-term local-currency bonds held by patient pension funds are robust; short-term dollar-denominated debt rolled every ninety days is a time bomb. When you size up an EM, do not just ask "how much foreign capital is funding this?" — ask "how fast can it leave, and in what currency is it owed?" The country financed by a current-account *surplus* and FDI is a different animal entirely from the one financed by short-term hot money chasing a yield, even when the dollar totals match. The maturity and currency *composition* of the external liabilities is the real measure of fragility, and it is invisible in the headline flow number.

And finally, the deepest point, the one that ties this post back to the series' spine. You never own an EM currency in isolation; you own a *relative bet on a flow continuing*, financed by the gap between two countries' interest rates. The yield is what you are paid; the flow is what you are betting on; and the sudden stop is the moment the bet is called. The currency, the reserves, and the economy don't crater in sequence — they crater *together*, because they were never separate things. They were always three faces of the same flow, and when the flow reverses, all three faces break at once. That simultaneity is what separates a currency *move* from a currency *crisis*, and it is the reason an EM currency is only ever as strong as the next dollar that wants to come in.
