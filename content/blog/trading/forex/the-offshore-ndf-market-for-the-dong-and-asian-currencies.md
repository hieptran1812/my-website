> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The offshore non-deliverable forward (NDF) market is where the dong and its Asian peers trade *free of the onshore band*, and the gap between the two prices is the cleanest real-time gauge of devaluation pressure you can read.
>
> - An NDF lets anyone, anywhere, take a view on a controlled currency and **settle the difference in dollars** — no dong, won, or rupiah ever crosses a border.
> - The **onshore price** is what the central bank *permits*; the **offshore NDF price** is what the unrestricted world will *actually pay* for dollars. When they diverge, the offshore price is usually right.
> - A wide offshore premium — the 1Y NDF pricing dollars well above the onshore ceiling — is the market saying *"the official rate has to give."* For the dong, anything past roughly **2.5%** is a stress signal; it blew out in 2022.
> - The one number to remember: in 2022, the dong's 1Y offshore NDF priced a **~3.5% depreciation premium** over the onshore band — and within months the SBV had let the rate crawl, drained reserves, and widened the band to validate it.

In October 2022, a strange thing happened to the Vietnamese dong. On paper — the *onshore* paper that the State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) publishes every morning — the dong was depreciating in its usual orderly crawl, a few percent a year, nothing dramatic. But a Singapore trader looking at a screen most Vietnamese had never heard of saw something else entirely: the *offshore* one-year forward was pricing the dollar **3.5% richer** than anything the onshore band allowed. The official market said one thing. The market that the SBV could not touch said another. And the offshore market turned out to be right: within weeks the SBV had widened the dong's trading band from ±3% to ±5%, raised its policy rate twice, and sold down billions of its reserves to keep the slide orderly.

That screen was the **NDF market** — the non-deliverable forward. It is the venue where currencies you cannot freely buy or sell still trade, where every global bank and hedge fund parks its real opinion about a controlled currency, and where devaluation pressure shows up *before* it shows up in the official rate. If you only ever watch the onshore number, you are reading yesterday's news. The offshore NDF is the leading indicator.

This post is about that market across Asia — the dong, the renminbi (which has a fascinating twist), the Korean won, the Indian rupee, and the Indonesian rupiah. We will build the NDF up from zero, show exactly how the onshore/offshore spread becomes a pressure gauge, work the dollar math four times, and end on how traders and corporates actually use it. It is the companion to the general NDF primer in this series — here the focus is Asia, and the dong in particular.

## Foundations: The offshore NDF market in Asia

Start with the spine of this whole series: **an exchange rate is the relative price of two monies.** You never own "a currency" in isolation; every position is a pair, a bet on one money against another. USD/VND is the price of one dollar measured in dong — at the end of 2024, about **25,450 dong per dollar**. When that number rises, the dong is getting *weaker* (it takes more dong to buy a dollar); when it falls, the dong is getting *stronger*.

Now add the complication that defines this whole post: **you cannot freely trade the dong.** Vietnam, like most of emerging Asia at some point in its history, runs **capital controls** — legal restrictions on moving money across its border. A foreign hedge fund in London cannot simply call a bank, buy a billion dong, and wire it home, the way it could with euros or yen. The dong is, in the jargon, a **restricted** or **non-convertible** currency. And yet global investors hold Vietnamese stocks, Vietnamese exporters earn dollars, and importers owe them — all of these people have real, large exposure to USD/VND and a real need to hedge it. How do you trade a price you are not allowed to deliver?

It helps to be precise about *what* the controls actually block, because that is exactly what the NDF is engineered around. There are two flavors of convertibility. **Current-account convertibility** means you can exchange the currency for real trade — importing goods, paying for services, sending a remittance home. Vietnam allows that; an exporter can convert its dollar earnings into dong and an importer can buy dollars to pay a supplier. **Capital-account convertibility** means you can freely move money across the border for *investment* purposes — buying and selling financial assets, parking speculative cash, taking a leveraged position. *That* is what Vietnam restricts, and what most of emerging Asia restricted for decades. The dong is convertible for trade but not for speculation. The reason is defensive: an open capital account lets hot money flood in chasing yield and then flee all at once in a panic, which is precisely the mechanism that detonated the 1997 Asian financial crisis — see [the 1997 Asian financial crisis](/blog/trading/finance/asian-financial-crisis-1997) for the canonical case of pegs breaking under exactly that pressure. Capital controls are Vietnam's seatbelt against a repeat.

The NDF threads this needle elegantly. Because it never requires the restricted currency to actually cross the border — it settles a *dollar difference*, not a *dong delivery* — it sidesteps the capital-account restriction entirely while still giving the holder the full economic exposure of a real forward. It is a synthetic position in a currency you are not allowed to physically hold. That is not a loophole the authorities overlooked; it is a structure the global derivatives market built deliberately, and it is the reason a controlled currency like the dong has any tradeable forward curve at all.

### The non-deliverable forward, from zero

A **forward** is the simplest derivative there is: an agreement *today* to exchange two currencies at a *fixed rate* on a *future date*. If you agree today to buy \$1,000,000 worth of dong in three months at 25,450, you have locked that rate in regardless of where the market goes. A normal ("deliverable") forward ends with the actual currencies changing hands: you hand over dollars, you receive dong.

A **non-deliverable forward** strips out the one step the capital controls forbid — the delivery. Instead of swapping the actual currencies on the settlement date, the two sides simply look up an agreed **official reference rate** (the "fixing"), compare it to the rate they locked in, and **pay each other the difference in US dollars.** No dong ever moves. No border is crossed. No license is required. The contract lives entirely offshore — in Singapore, Hong Kong, London — in dollar bank accounts the SBV cannot reach. That single design choice is what makes the otherwise-untradeable dong tradeable by anyone in the world.

The detailed mechanics of a single NDF — the fixing source, the settlement convention, why a fund uses one — are the subject of the general NDF primer in this series, [non-deliverable forwards: trading uninvestable currencies](/blog/trading/forex/non-deliverable-forwards-trading-uninvestable-currencies). Here we build *on* that and zoom in on the structure that makes the NDF analytically useful: the fact that it creates a **second price** for the same currency.

One detail worth fixing in your mind, because it is the linchpin of everything that follows: the **fixing**. An NDF has to settle against *something* — an agreed, public, official number that both sides will accept as "the" exchange rate on the settlement date. For the dong, that reference is typically an SBV-published or interbank-survey rate; for the rupee it is the RBI reference rate; for the won, a Korean dealer fixing. The fixing is the umbilical cord between the offshore contract and the onshore reality. It is also a point of contention: because billions of dollars of NDFs settle against a single daily print, there is real money in *where that print lands*, which is one reason central banks pay close attention to (and sometimes try to control) the fixing source. The key thing is that the contract is *priced* offshore in a free market but *settled* against an onshore official number — and the distance the contract travels from that official number is the signal we are going to spend the rest of this post reading.

#### Worked example: a 1M USD/VND NDF settling against the SBV fixing

Suppose a Singapore fund believes the dong will weaken sharply over the next two days around a policy meeting. It buys a short-dated USD/VND NDF on a **\$1,000,000** notional at a contracted rate of **26,200 dong per dollar**. (Buying USD/VND means it profits if the dollar gets *more* expensive in dong — i.e. the dong weakens.)

On the fixing date the SBV's reference rate prints at **27,000 dong per dollar** — the dong has indeed weakened. The settlement is pure arithmetic:

- The rate moved **27,000 − 26,200 = 800 dong per dollar** in the buyer's favor.
- On a \$1,000,000 notional that gap is worth **\$1,000,000 × 800 = 800,000,000 dong**.
- But nobody delivers dong. We convert that dong P&L back into dollars *at the fixing rate*: **800,000,000 ÷ 27,000 = \$29,629.63**.

So the fund receives roughly **\$29,630**, wired to its offshore dollar account, and not a single dong ever changed hands. That is the whole trick: a fully economic bet on the dong, settled entirely in dollars. The figure below traces those exact steps.

### Two prices for one currency

Here is the conceptual heart of the post. Because the NDF lives outside the controls and the onshore market lives inside them, the same currency ends up with **two simultaneous prices**:

1. The **onshore price** — what the central bank *permits*. In Vietnam the SBV publishes a daily central reference rate and allows the spot market to trade within a band of **±5%** around it. At end-2024 the central rate was about 24,340, which set a hard **ceiling of 25,557** dong per dollar. Onshore, the dollar literally *cannot* trade above that ceiling; the SBV will sell reserves to defend it.

2. The **offshore NDF price** — what the unrestricted world will *actually pay*. The 1-year NDF has no band, no ceiling, no license. If global demand for dollars is intense, the NDF will quote a USD/VND rate *above* the onshore ceiling — pricing in a depreciation the onshore market is not yet allowed to express.

The onshore rate is an *administered* price. The offshore rate is a *market* price. When a free market and an administered market disagree, history says the free market is usually telling you where things are headed. The whole edge of watching the NDF is in that disagreement.

To make this less abstract, hold the two markets side by side. Onshore, the dong trades in a regulated interbank market staffed by licensed Vietnamese banks, supervised tick-by-tick, with the SBV ready to step in as buyer or seller of last resort the moment the rate approaches the band edge. The flow there is dominated by *real* commercial demand — exporters converting earnings, importers buying dollars, the SBV smoothing — plus whatever administered guidance the central bank applies. Offshore, the dong NDF trades in Singapore, Hong Kong, and London among global investment banks and their hedge-fund and corporate clients, with no band, no Vietnamese supervisor, and no lender of last resort. The flow there is dominated by *opinion* — the unconstrained, often leveraged, view of people who can take the other side of the SBV's intention. One market is built to be calm; the other is built to be honest. When they part ways, you are watching the difference between what the authorities want the dong to be worth and what the world thinks it is worth, and the second number is the one that predicts the first.

### Why the offshore price diverges

If both prices are quotes for the same dong, what makes them split apart? Three forces, in rough order of importance:

- **The interest-rate gap.** This is the boring, mechanical part and it is the foundation. A forward rate is not a forecast; it is the spot rate adjusted for the interest-rate differential between the two currencies, a relationship called **covered interest parity**. The rates themselves live in the fixed-income world — see [forward rates: what the market expects rates to be](/blog/trading/fixed-income/forward-rates-what-the-market-expects-rates-to-be) — so we will not re-derive the curve here; we just ride it. Because Vietnamese dong interest rates sit *above* US dollar rates, the dong trades at a forward *discount*: the NDF curve points to a weaker dong even with zero devaluation fear, purely from the rate gap.

- **Devaluation expectations.** On top of the mechanical forward, the offshore market adds a *risk premium* whenever it thinks the onshore peg/crawl is under threat. This is the part that matters for our gauge. If traders believe the SBV will be forced to let the dong slide faster than its usual crawl, they bid up dollars in the NDF *beyond* what interest parity alone implies — and the NDF rate punches above the onshore ceiling.

- **Dollar-funding stress and illiquidity.** The offshore market is thin and sometimes one-sided. When global dollar funding tightens — the same stress that shows up in the cross-currency basis — everyone wants dollars at once, and the NDF gaps wider than fundamentals justify. The thinness is itself a cost: the bid-ask spread on a USD/VND NDF runs around **40 pips**, versus a fraction of a pip in EUR/USD.

So the offshore premium = (mechanical forward from the rate gap) + (devaluation risk premium) + (funding/liquidity premium). The first piece is always there and roughly stable; it is the *second and third pieces blowing out* that signal stress. We will separate them carefully, because confusing "the dong is at a forward discount because its rates are higher" with "the market expects a devaluation" is the single most common mistake people make reading this market.

There is a fourth, slower force worth naming: **the structural balance of payments.** A currency that runs a persistent trade surplus and pulls in steady foreign direct investment has a structural tailwind — there is a natural, recurring supply of dollars wanting to be converted, which holds the currency up over years. Vietnam, helpfully, has that: a sizable goods-trade surplus, FDI disbursement that has climbed past \$25bn a year, and remittances around \$16bn a year. Those flows are why the dong's underlying crawl is *gentle* rather than a free fall — the structural dollar supply partly offsets the structural dollar demand. The offshore NDF prices the *marginal* pressure on top of that structural backdrop: when the marginal dollar buyer (a fleeing portfolio investor, a panicked importer) overwhelms the structural seller (the exporter, the FDI inflow), the premium widens. So reading the NDF well means holding two timescales at once — the slow structural support underneath, and the fast marginal pressure on top that the premium actually measures.

## The onshore/offshore spread as a devaluation-pressure gauge

Strip away the jargon and the spread is a beautifully simple instrument. The onshore ceiling tells you the **most depreciation the authorities will currently tolerate.** The offshore NDF tells you **how much depreciation the market is actually pricing.** Subtract one from the other and you get a number with a clear meaning:

> **Offshore premium = how far past the official ceiling the free market is willing to pay for dollars.** A small premium means the band is credible. A large, widening premium means the market expects the band itself to move.

When the offshore premium is small — say under 1.5% for the dong — it is essentially just the rate-gap carry, and the band is comfortable. When it climbs past **2.5%**, the market is no longer pricing carry; it is pricing a *policy change*. And when it spikes hard, you are watching a slow-motion bet that the central bank will blink. The graph below traces the causal chain from a dollar-scarcity shock to a widening premium to an official move that validates it.

#### Worked example: the offshore premium pricing a 3% devaluation on a 1,000,000 dollar notional

Let's quantify what "the NDF prices a 3% devaluation" actually costs and means. Take the dong with an onshore spot of **26,300** dong per dollar (roughly its 2025 level). The 1-year offshore NDF is quoting a **3% depreciation premium**, so it prints at:

- **NDF rate = 26,300 × 1.03 = 27,089 dong per dollar.**
- The premium itself is **27,089 − 26,300 = 789 dong per dollar**.

Now suppose a corporate treasurer at a Vietnamese importer needs to lock the cost of **\$1,000,000** of imports a year out. By trading the NDF she fixes her effective rate at 27,089. The *extra* cost she is paying, relative to today's spot, to insure against the dong weakening is:

- **789 dong/dollar × \$1,000,000 = 789,000,000 dong** of forward premium.
- Converted at the NDF rate: **789,000,000 ÷ 27,089 ≈ \$29,127** of carry cost baked into the one-year hedge.

That ~\$29,000 is the price of certainty — and it is also a *number you can read backwards*. If the NDF is charging a 3% premium and you know the rate-gap carry only justifies, say, 2%, then the market is pricing roughly **1% of pure devaluation risk** into the dong over the next year. The spread doesn't just cost money; it *tells you the market's forecast*, denominated in basis points of expected official depreciation.

It is worth pausing on *why* this read-backwards trick works, because it is the conceptual engine of the whole gauge. The NDF rate is a single number that the market has to agree on, and that number has to compensate the seller of dollars for everything that could happen to the dong over the contract's life. If the seller thought the dong would just crawl at its usual 2–3%, she would demand only the carry premium. If she fears a 5% step-devaluation, she demands more — she will not lock in dollars cheaply if she expects to be able to buy them far more cheaply soon. The equilibrium NDF rate is therefore a *weighted average of every scenario the market is pricing*, with the probabilities baked in. A 3% premium when carry justifies 1.4% is not the market saying "there will definitely be a 1.6% devaluation"; it is the market saying "the probability-weighted expected extra depreciation, plus the risk premium for bearing that uncertainty, equals 1.6%." That is a richer signal than a point forecast — it is the market's entire distribution of fear, collapsed into one tradeable price.

### Separating the carry from the fear

This is worth slowing down on, because it is where amateurs misread the signal. The offshore NDF is *always* above onshore spot for the dong, because dong rates are above dollar rates — that is just carry, and it is not bearish. The trick is to compare the offshore premium against the **carry-implied** forward and watch the *residual*.

#### Worked example: backing out the carry-implied NDF from the rate gap

Covered interest parity says the fair forward for a one-year USD/VND NDF is:

> **Forward = Spot × (1 + dong rate) ÷ (1 + dollar rate)**

Take spot **26,300**, a one-year dong interest rate of **4.5%**, and a one-year dollar rate of **4.5%** (roughly the 2024-25 backdrop — Vietnamese rates fell close to US rates as the Fed stayed high). Then:

- **Forward = 26,300 × (1.045 ÷ 1.045) = 26,300.** With equal rates, carry implies *no* premium at all.
- If instead dong rates are **6%** and dollar rates **4.5%**: **Forward = 26,300 × (1.06 ÷ 1.045) = 26,678**, a carry-implied premium of just **378 dong**, about **1.4%**.

So if the actual offshore NDF is printing 27,089 (a 3% premium) while carry only justifies 1.4%, the **residual ~1.6% is the market's devaluation-risk premium** — the fear, cleanly separated from the carry. *That residual* is the gauge. When dong and dollar rates are close, almost the entire offshore premium *is* fear, which is exactly why the 2022 blow-out was so alarming: rates were not the story, expected devaluation was.

This is also why the *direction* of the interest-rate gap matters when you read the signal. In a normal emerging market the local rate sits comfortably above the dollar rate, so there is a fat carry cushion and you have to clear a high bar before the residual turns into a real warning. But Vietnam spent 2024–25 in an unusual configuration: the Fed held US rates high while the SBV kept dong rates low to support domestic growth, so the carry cushion *shrank toward zero*. With almost no carry to absorb it, even a modest offshore premium was almost *entirely* fear — and worse, the thin carry cushion meant there was little reward for anyone to hold the dong and short dollars, so the natural stabilizing flow weakened. The lesson generalizes: the same headline NDF premium means very different things depending on the rate gap underneath it. A 2.5% premium on top of a 6% carry cushion is mostly mechanical; a 2.5% premium on top of a near-zero carry cushion is mostly fear. Always net out the carry before you panic — or before you relax.

### Reading the level

For the dong specifically, a rough field guide to the 1-year offshore premium:

- **Under 1.5%** — calm. This is carry plus noise. The band is credible; nobody is betting against it.
- **1.5% to 2.5%** — watchful. Carry is rising or a little fear is creeping in. Worth monitoring the SBV's reserve prints and the central-rate crawl.
- **Over 2.5%** — stress. The market is pricing a policy response: a faster crawl, a band widening, a rate hike, or visible intervention. This is where 2022 lived.

The headline chart of this post — figure 1, the cover — plots exactly this series from 2018 to 2025: a quiet 0.8% in the easy-money year of 2020, a spike to 3.5% in the 2022 dollar shock, and a settling back toward 2–2.8% as the SBV regained control. Read it as a fever chart for the dong.

## The dong NDF in close-up

Now make it concrete for Vietnam. The dong NDF market is **thin** — the dong is a small currency, capital controls are real, and onshore deliverable hedging through licensed banks handles much of the genuine commercial demand. But the offshore NDF is the only place the *speculative and the unconstrained* opinion can express itself, which is precisely what makes it informative. A thin market moved by conviction can be a better thermometer than a deep market clogged with administered flow.

What the dong NDF is pricing against is the **managed crawl** — the SBV's deliberate, one-directional, gentle depreciation of the dong, year after year, to keep Vietnamese exports competitive without inviting a disorderly run. The mechanics of that crawl — the daily central rate, the band, the credit ceiling, the whole SBV toolkit — are covered in [USD/VND and the managed float: how the SBV runs the dong](/blog/trading/forex/usd-vnd-and-the-managed-float-how-the-sbv-runs-the-dong) and, from the monetary-policy side, in [Vietnam monetary policy: the State Bank, the dong, and the credit ceiling](/blog/trading/finance/vietnam-monetary-policy-state-bank-dong-credit-ceiling). The NDF is the market's continuous referendum on whether that crawl is fast enough.

![USD VND onshore year-end level 2014 to 2025 with the 2024 band ceiling](/imgs/blogs/the-offshore-ndf-market-for-the-dong-and-asian-currencies-4.png)

Look at the onshore crawl above and you can see why the NDF matters. From 2014 to 2025 the dong slid from about 21,340 to 26,300 per dollar — a steady, *managed* depreciation of roughly 2–3% a year, with a sharp leg in 2022 when USD/VND jumped from ~23,650 to ~24,270 and the 2024 band ceiling of 25,557 came into play. The onshore line is smooth *by design*; the SBV smooths it. The offshore NDF is where the *pressure that the smoothing suppresses* accumulates and becomes visible. When the onshore line is being held flat by intervention while the NDF premium widens, you are watching a coiled spring.

The "coiled spring" framing matters because it explains the *asymmetry* of the dong's path. The SBV runs a one-directional crawl — it lets the dong weaken gently and almost never lets it strengthen meaningfully — because Vietnam's whole economic model depends on competitive exports. That means the onshore rate behaves like a ratchet: it ticks weaker, holds, ticks weaker, holds. Pressure that the ratchet doesn't release does not vanish; it accumulates offshore. So in calm times the NDF premium and the onshore crawl roughly agree, the spring stays loose, and nothing dramatic happens. In a dollar-up shock the onshore ratchet *can't keep up* — the SBV will not let the dong drop 5% in a week even if the market wants it to — so the offshore NDF runs ahead and the premium balloons. The gap between the smooth onshore ratchet and the jumpy offshore price *is* the coiled energy. When the SBV finally widens the band or accelerates the crawl, it is releasing that energy in a controlled way, and the NDF premium deflates. Reading the dong is largely reading the tension in that spring: how far has the offshore price run ahead of the onshore ratchet, and how much longer can the ratchet resist?

### Why the dong gap is hard to fake

A skeptic might say: the dong NDF is so thin that its price is noise. There is something to that — thinness means a single large hedger can move the quote, and the bid-ask is wide. But thinness cuts the other way too. Because there is *no* large administered flow offshore, the price is not muddied by intervention. Every dollar of demand in the dong NDF is someone with a real, unconstrained view: a global macro fund, a multinational treasury, a foreign holder of Vietnamese equities hedging repatriation risk. The signal-to-noise is actually *high* precisely because the market is small and unmanaged. You just have to read the *trend* in the premium, not any single tick.

#### Worked example: the cost of crossing the spread on a dong NDF roundtrip

Thinness has a price, and you should size it. The dealer spread on a USD/VND NDF runs about **40 pips** — here a "pip" is the last digit the market quotes, roughly 5 dong on a ~26,000 handle, so 40 pips ≈ **40 dong** of round-trip cost per dollar. On a **\$1,000,000** NDF:

- Crossing the spread once (to get in) costs about half of that, **~20 dong/dollar × \$1,000,000 = 20,000,000 dong**.
- Converted at ~26,300: **20,000,000 ÷ 26,300 ≈ \$760** to enter, and roughly the same to exit — call it **~\$1,500 round-trip** on a million-dollar position.

Compare that to EUR/USD, where the interbank spread is about **0.2 pips** and the same round-trip costs single-digit dollars. The dong NDF is **roughly 200× more expensive to trade** than a major. That cost is the toll for accessing an otherwise-closed market — and it is why the NDF is dominated by hedgers and patient macro players, not high-frequency flow. ## The CNH, won, rupee, and rupiah markets

The dong is one member of a family. Across Asia, restricted and managed currencies all have an offshore life, and each one teaches a slightly different lesson about how to read the onshore/offshore spread. The map below lays out the five and what their gaps signal; then we walk each one.

![The Asian NDF map of VND CNH KRW INR IDR and what each gap signals](/imgs/blogs/the-offshore-ndf-market-for-the-dong-and-asian-currencies-5.png)

### The renminbi: from NDF to CNH, a market that graduated

The renminbi is the most important and most instructive case, because it shows where this whole story can *go*. For years the only way to trade the Chinese currency offshore was the CNY NDF — a non-deliverable forward exactly like the dong's. Then, starting around 2010, Beijing did something the others have not: it created a **deliverable offshore renminbi**, traded in Hong Kong, with its own ticker — **CNH**. CNH is real, deliverable renminbi that lives outside the mainland's capital controls. You can actually hold it, settle it, and clear it offshore.

This gives China **two live renminbi prices at once**: the onshore **CNY**, tightly managed by the People's Bank of China around a daily fixing with a ±2% band, and the offshore **CNH**, which floats much more freely. The **CNH−CNY spread** is the renminbi's version of the dong's offshore premium — and it is watched obsessively. When CNH trades *weaker* than CNY (a positive USD/CNH minus USD/CNY gap), it means the offshore market is betting the PBoC will have to allow more depreciation than the onshore fixing admits. In August 2015, when China surprised the world with a renminbi devaluation, the CNH had been signaling pressure for weeks. The old CNY NDF still exists as a legacy hedging tool, but CNH is where the action moved. The lesson for the dong: an offshore *deliverable* market is the mature endpoint, and the spread stays informative even after a currency "graduates."

The CNH case also teaches a subtler point about *how* a central bank can fight its own offshore market. Because CNH is deliverable, the PBoC (working through Chinese state banks in Hong Kong) can intervene in it directly — it can buy CNH to make it more expensive, and it can squeeze the offshore funding market so that *borrowing* CNH to short it becomes punishingly costly. In January 2016 the overnight CNH HIBOR — the rate to borrow offshore renminbi — spiked toward an eye-watering 66% as Beijing deliberately drained offshore liquidity to crush the shorts. That is a weapon the dong's central bank does not have, precisely because the dong has no deliverable offshore pool to squeeze. With a pure NDF currency like the dong, the only ammunition is onshore reserves and the fixing; there is no offshore funding lever. So as you read the spectrum of Asian currencies, notice that the *type* of offshore market determines the *type* of defense available — a point that matters enormously for how durable each band really is.

### The Korean won: a deep, liquid NDF and a risk-off proxy

The Korean won (KRW) is restricted onshore — Korea limits non-resident won trading — but its **offshore NDF is one of the deepest and most liquid in the world**, far more so than the dong's. That depth changes how you read it. The won is a high-beta, export-heavy, semiconductor-cycle currency, and its NDF trades less as a pure devaluation gauge and more as a **real-time risk-off and global-growth proxy**. When global risk sours — a tech selloff, a China scare, a Fed shock — the KRW NDF weakens fast, often leading the rest of Asia. Korea also intervenes, and because the NDF is so liquid, the Bank of Korea's footprints (and its battles with offshore sellers) are visible in the spread. For the dong, the won is the picture of what a *liquid* version of the same market looks like: the signal is cleaner, but it is also more crowded with macro tourists.

The depth difference is not a minor footnote — it changes the meaning of the price. In the thin dong NDF, the premium is moved by *genuine* hedgers with skin in the game, so the level reflects domestic conviction. In the deep won NDF, the premium is also moved by global macro funds expressing a broad *Asia* or *risk* view that may have little to do with Korea specifically. A won NDF that weakens because a fund is selling all of Asia as a dollar-up trade is a different signal from a dong NDF that weakens because Vietnamese-specific dollar demand is spiking. The practical upshot: read the won as a *regional* and *risk-cycle* gauge, and read the dong as a *country-specific* gauge. When both move together, you have a real Asia-wide dollar squeeze; when only the dong moves, you have a Vietnam-specific story. Liquidity tells you which kind of information the price is carrying.

### The Indian rupee: a giant NDF and the onshore tug-of-war

The Indian rupee (INR) has an **enormous offshore NDF market** — at times the offshore rupee turnover rivaled or exceeded the onshore market, which deeply annoyed the Reserve Bank of India (RBI), because it meant the price of the rupee was being set in London and Singapore rather than Mumbai. India's response is the template for what worried authorities do: rather than just ban the offshore market, it tried to **pull the activity onshore**, opening rupee derivatives trading in the GIFT City international finance hub and to onshore banks, so the RBI could see and influence the flow. The RBI manages the rupee to a level (a "dirty float"), and the NDF is where the market tests how hard it will defend. The rupee shows the *politics* of the NDF: a central bank that resents the offshore price will try to onshore it, not abolish it.

There is a strategic logic to "onshoring" rather than banning. If you ban the offshore market, you do not kill the demand to hedge or speculate on the rupee — you just push it somewhere you cannot see and cannot influence, and you lose the information the price carries. If instead you let onshore banks trade in the same offshore market and you build a domestic venue with similar economics, you achieve two things: you let the RBI *participate* in the offshore price (so its interventions bite more directly), and you keep the activity inside a perimeter you can monitor. The deeper lesson for any managed-currency authority — including the SBV — is that the offshore NDF is not really a threat to be eliminated; it is a thermostat to be read, and ideally a market to be *joined* so your interventions land where the price is actually set. India learned that the hard way after years of trying to wall the offshore market off and watching it grow anyway.

### The Indonesian rupiah: NDF plus a homegrown backstop

The Indonesian rupiah (IDR) is the most outflow-sensitive of the group — Indonesia runs a current-account that swings, holds a lot of foreign portfolio money in its bond market, and gets hit hard in "sudden stop" episodes when global capital flees emerging markets (the dynamic covered in [the carry into emerging markets and the sudden stop](/blog/trading/forex/the-carry-into-emerging-markets-and-the-sudden-stop)). Bank Indonesia's clever response was to build its *own* onshore answer to the NDF: the **Domestic NDF (DNDF)**, a rupiah-settled non-deliverable forward that lets locals hedge without dollars leaving the country, giving the central bank a tool to absorb hedging demand and lean against the offshore market. The rupiah shows the *defensive innovation* end of the spectrum — when you cannot beat the offshore NDF, you build a domestic mirror of it.

The DNDF is worth understanding because it is the most likely model for where the dong eventually goes. The problem Bank Indonesia faced was this: in a stress episode, every hedger and speculator piles into the *offshore* NDF to short the rupiah, the offshore price gaps weak, and that weak offshore print drags the onshore spot down with it through arbitrage and sentiment. The DNDF lets a domestic bank or corporate hedge its dollar exposure *onshore*, in rupiah, settling against the local fixing — so the hedging demand that would have flooded the offshore market gets absorbed at home, where Bank Indonesia can see it and supply the other side. In effect the central bank manufactures its own hedging supply to compete with the offshore market and keep the two prices from diverging. It is a structural recognition that you cannot make hedging demand disappear; you can only choose whether it expresses itself in a market you control or one you do not. For Vietnam, watching the rupiah's DNDF is watching a preview of the tool the SBV may build if dong hedging demand ever outgrows what the onshore deliverable-forward market can handle.

### Where the dong sits

Against these, the dong is the **smallest and most tightly controlled**. Its NDF is thin, its band is administered, its crawl is deliberate. It has no CNH-style deliverable offshore market, no GIFT City, no DNDF — though Vietnam watches all three models. For now, the dong's offshore NDF premium remains the single best external read on whether the SBV's managed crawl is keeping pace with reality. The five currencies sit on a spectrum from "graduated to a free offshore float" (CNH) through "deep liquid NDF" (KRW, INR) to "thin but honest NDF" (VND), and where a currency sits on that spectrum tells you how to weight its signal.

That spectrum is also a rough development path. A currency tends to start as a pure thin NDF (where the dong is now), grow into a deep liquid NDF as foreign investment in the country deepens (the won and rupee), and eventually — if the authorities choose to open the capital account — graduate to a deliverable offshore market (the renminbi's CNH). Each step trades away some control for some credibility and some inward investment, and each step changes the defense toolkit, as we saw. None of it removes the basic logic of this post: as long as there is *any* gap between what the authorities permit onshore and what the market will pay offshore, that gap is a price, and that price is information. The instruments evolve; the signal does not. Whether you are reading a 66%-HIBOR squeeze in CNH or a 3.5% premium in the thin dong NDF, you are reading the same thing — the distance between an administered price and a free one — and that distance is always the market's verdict on whether the managed rate can hold.

## Common misconceptions

**"A positive offshore premium means a devaluation is coming."** No — most of the premium is *carry*, the mechanical forward discount from the dong's higher interest rates, and carry is not a forecast. As the worked example showed, when dong rates are 6% and dollar rates 4.5%, about **1.4%** of premium is pure carry and signals nothing. Only the *residual* above carry — and especially a *widening* residual — is the devaluation signal. People who scream "devaluation!" every time the NDF is above spot are misreading carry as fear.

**"The NDF is the 'black market' rate."** No. The offshore NDF is a regulated, institutional, dollar-settled derivative traded by global banks under standard ISDA documentation. It is not a back-alley cash rate. It can *diverge* from the onshore official rate, but it is a legitimate, deep (for the won and rupee, very deep) market — the place global institutions hedge, not a parallel street economy.

**"If the NDF spikes, the central bank has lost control."** Not necessarily. A central bank with ample reserves and credibility can let the NDF run hot for a while and then *validate* a portion of it with a measured move — a band widening, a faster crawl — which actually *narrows* the gap and reasserts control. The 2022 dong episode looked alarming in the NDF, but the SBV's response (rate hikes, reserve sales, band widening) brought the premium back down. A spike is a *demand* for a response, not proof the response will fail.

**"Thin means meaningless."** The dong NDF is thin, but as we argued, thinness can *raise* signal quality because there is no administered flow muddying the price. A thin market of conviction-driven hedgers is a better thermometer than a thick market full of mandated flow. You read the *trend*, not the tick.

**"Offshore and onshore can't diverge much because arbitrage links them."** They can and do, precisely because the capital controls *break* the arbitrage. In a free market, any gap between onshore and offshore would be arbitraged away instantly. But you cannot move dong across the border to close the gap — that is the whole point of the controls. So the two prices can persist far apart, and the persistence of the gap is itself the measure of how binding the controls are.

**"The NDF premium predicts the spot rate one-for-one."** No, and treating it as a point forecast is a recipe for bad trades. The premium is a *risk-adjusted, probability-weighted* number, and it embeds a risk premium that the actual outcome usually does not pay out in full — historically, across many emerging markets, the realized depreciation tends to be *less* than the forward premium implied, which is exactly why systematically *selling* the high-yielding currency forward (collecting the premium) has been a profitable carry strategy on average. The premium is a fear gauge and a probability map, not a calendar of certain devaluations. Read its *trend* and its *level relative to carry* for the signal; do not bet that the dong will fall by precisely the premium.

## How it shows up in real markets

**The dong in 2022.** The cleanest case. As the Fed hiked from near-zero to 4.5% through 2022, dollars drained out of emerging Asia, Vietnam's import bill ballooned, and the SBV burned through reserves — they fell from about \$109bn at end-2021 to roughly \$87bn by end-2022, a draw of more than \$20bn, which on a ~3-month import-cover base is a frightening rate of burn. The offshore 1-year NDF premium for the dong blew out to around **3.5%**, deep into the stress zone. The SBV responded across the autumn: it widened the trading band from ±3% to ±5% in October, hiked its policy rate twice (the refinancing rate went up a full two percentage points in a month), and let the central rate crawl faster. By 2023 the premium had eased back to ~2%. The NDF led; the policy followed; the gap validated and narrowed — exactly the chain in figure 3. The instructive detail is the *sequence*: the offshore premium widened *before* the band was widened. A trader watching only the onshore rate saw an orderly crawl right up until the band moved; a trader watching the NDF saw the pressure building for weeks and could position — or hedge — ahead of the policy response. That lead time is the entire practical value of the gauge.

**The renminbi in 2015–2016.** China's August 2015 devaluation was the moment the world learned to watch CNH. In the weeks before, USD/CNH had been trading persistently weaker than USD/CNY — the offshore market pricing depreciation the onshore fixing was suppressing. When the PBoC moved, the CNH had front-run it. Through early 2016 the CNH−CNY spread stayed wide as capital outflows pressured the renminbi, and the PBoC spent a chunk of its \$3-trillion-plus reserves and intervened directly in CNH to slam the spread shut. The episode is the canonical demonstration that the offshore price leads the onshore one. It is also a lesson in *cost*: defending the spread is not free. China's reserves fell by roughly a trillion dollars from their 2014 peak through 2016 as the PBoC sold dollars to fight the offshore market and the capital outflow it reflected. Even the largest reserve stockpile on Earth felt the strain. For a country with Vietnam's ~3-month buffer, the same kind of fight would be over in weeks, not years — which is precisely why the SBV manages the dong's crawl so conservatively and would rather validate the NDF early than burn reserves defending an indefensible level. The CNH episode shows the *whole* playbook: the offshore market pressures, the central bank either spends reserves or moves the rate, and reserves are the clock running down the whole time.

**The won and the rupee: two faces of the Asian NDF.** Beyond the dong and the renminbi, two markets show how different an NDF can be. The Korean won NDF is one of the deepest offshore markets in Asia — Korea runs a fairly open economy but historically kept the onshore won non-deliverable to non-residents, so a vast institutional NDF grew up in Singapore, Hong Kong, and London. It is liquid enough that a fund can move size; in the 1997 crisis and again in 2008 the won NDF was where the global market expressed its view on Korea, and the onshore rate followed. Liquidity is the difference: the won NDF is a thick market whose level you can trust on any given day, where the dong NDF is a thin one whose *trend* you trust but whose single ticks you do not.

The Indian rupee NDF is the cautionary tale of an offshore market growing so large it started to *drive* the onshore one. By the late 2010s the offshore rupee NDF, centered in Singapore and Dubai, had grown comparable in turnover to the onshore market, and the Reserve Bank of India grew uneasy that the offshore tail was wagging the onshore dog — that price discovery for the rupee was happening in a market the RBI could not touch. Its response, from 2020, was telling: rather than fight the NDF, the RBI *opened the door to it*, letting onshore Indian banks trade in the offshore NDF market so that domestic price-setters could compete in it and pull the two prices back together. It is the opposite move from a central bank that bans offshore trading — an admission that you cannot wish an NDF away, only narrow the gap by participating. For a treasurer, the lesson is that the depth and the regulatory posture of each market differ: a \$10,000,000 won hedge fills at a tight price any hour of the day, while the same size in dong NDF moves the market and the same size in rupee must now be read against an onshore rate the RBI has deliberately welded closer to the offshore one.

**The reserve backstop.** What ultimately decides whether a central bank can hold its band against a screaming NDF is its **reserves** — and on the standard yardstick, *months of import cover*, Vietnam is thin. The chart below ranks Asian EMs: China at ~14 months, India ~11, but Vietnam at **~3 months**, right at the bottom of the adequacy rule of thumb. That thin buffer is exactly why the dong NDF premium matters so much — there is less firepower to defend the band, so the market's expectation does more of the work, and the offshore gauge carries more weight.

**How a corporate uses it.** A Vietnamese importer with a large dollar payable does not speculate; it hedges. It watches the NDF premium to *time and size* its hedge: when the premium is cheap (under carry, calm band), it locks more of its dollar needs forward; when the premium is screaming (stress zone), it knows it is paying up for insurance and may stagger its hedging or use shorter tenors. The NDF is both the instrument it trades and the signal that tells it when to trade. A foreign equity fund does the same in reverse — it watches the dong NDF to decide whether to hedge the currency risk on its Vietnamese stock holdings, because a 3% dong slide can erase a year of equity gains.

There is a behavioral trap in this that is worth flagging, because it is where corporates lose the most money. The temptation is to hedge *most* when the premium is *highest* — because that is when fear is loudest and the dong looks most likely to fall. But the highest premium is also the most *expensive* insurance, and it often comes right before the SBV validates and the pressure deflates, leaving the corporate having locked in a punitive rate at the peak of fear. The disciplined treasurer does the opposite of the crowd: she builds her hedge *steadily* through calm periods when the premium is cheap, so that when the stress hits she is already largely covered and does not have to buy expensive insurance into a panic. The NDF premium, read this way, is not just a fear gauge — it is a *cost-of-hedging* gauge, and the two send opposite signals about when to act. The foreign equity fund faces the same trap when deciding whether to repatriate: selling Vietnamese stocks and converting dong to dollars through the NDF is cheapest when nobody else wants to, and most expensive in the rush. Knowing that the gauge measures both the danger *and* the price of avoiding it is what separates a treasurer who uses the NDF well from one who is whipsawed by it.

**How a macro fund uses it.** A global macro fund reads the cross-Asia NDF premiums as a *map of pressure*: which central bank is closest to blinking, where the carry-into-EM trade is most crowded, where a sudden stop would bite first. A widening dong premium alongside a widening rupiah premium and a soft CNH is the kind of confluence that says *the dollar is squeezing Asia* — the same regime that the broader [carry-trade unwinds](/blog/trading/macro-trading/carry-trade-unwinds-1998-2008-2024-when-leverage-breaks) and dollar-as-wrecking-ball dynamics describe. The reserves backstop tells the fund which currency has the least defense and is therefore the cleanest short.

## The takeaway: read the gap, not the headline

Here is what the offshore NDF market means for how you read an Asian currency.

The official, onshore rate is a *managed* number. It is smooth because someone is smoothing it. If you only watch the onshore print, you are watching the central bank's *intention*, not the market's *opinion* — and the gap between intention and opinion is where currencies break. The offshore NDF is that opinion, expressed by people the central bank cannot constrain, settled in a currency it cannot touch.

So the discipline is simple. Find the offshore NDF premium. Subtract the carry — the mechanical forward discount from the interest-rate gap — to isolate the residual. That residual, especially its *trend*, is the market's real-time forecast of devaluation pressure. For the dong, under ~1.5% is calm, over ~2.5% is stress, and a fast widening is the market demanding that the band give way. Cross-check it against the reserve backstop: a thin buffer (Vietnam's ~3 months) means the same premium carries more weight, because there is less firepower to defend the band.

And remember the spine of this whole series: an exchange rate is the relative price of two monies, driven by the gap between two countries' interest rates plus the flow of money across borders. The NDF makes both visible at once. The carry component *is* the rate gap. The residual premium *is* the flow pressure — the world's net demand for dollars against a currency it cannot freely buy. The offshore NDF is, in the end, the purest expression of this series' central idea: it strips a controlled currency down to the two forces that move every currency, and prices them in dollars on a screen, days before the official rate admits a thing.

Watch the gap. It usually knows first.
