Banks abroad create these dollars as credit, lending out dollars they were never given by the Fed, which means the world runs on a pool of dollars the Fed prices but does not control.
>

> - Offshore banks **create dollar credit** the same way domestic banks create domestic money: a loan is a new deposit. A London bank can book a **\$10,000,000** dollar loan without holding a single dollar at the Fed — it owes dollars, the borrower has dollars, and net new dollar credit just appeared outside US borders.
> - This makes the **Fed the world's central bank by accident**. Its policy rate prices roughly **\$13.4 trillion** of dollar credit owed by non-banks *outside* the US (2024), so a Fed hike tightens financial conditions in Jakarta and São Paulo just as hard as in Ohio.
> - The system's fatal flaw: offshore dollar banks **borrow short and lend long in a currency they cannot print**, with **no lender of last resort**. When the dollar funding scare comes, only the Fed's swap lines stand between the offshore system and collapse — **\$583B** drawn in December 2008, **\$449B** in the spring of 2020.
> - **The one number to remember:** the offshore dollar credit pile grew from about **\$6T in 2010 to \$13.4T in 2024**, and every dollar of it is a claim the Fed can squeeze but never see on its own balance sheet.

In the third week of March 2020, as the pandemic shut the world's borders, a strange thing happened to the price of money. The Federal Reserve had just slashed its policy rate to zero and was buying Treasuries by the hundreds of billions. By every domestic measure, dollars had never been cheaper or more plentiful. And yet, all over the rest of the planet, dollars suddenly went missing. A Korean bank that needed to roll over its dollar borrowing found nobody willing to lend. A Brazilian company with a dollar bond coming due could not find the dollars to pay it. The price of swapping euros or yen into dollars for three months — a plumbing rate almost no one outside a trading floor has ever heard of — blew out to levels last seen in the depths of 2008. The Fed had flooded *its* system with dollars, and the rest of the world was choking for them anyway.

How can the world be short of dollars at the exact moment the institution that prints them is creating them as fast as it can? The answer is the single most important and least understood fact about the modern monetary system: **most of the world's dollars are not in the Fed's system at all.** They live offshore, on the balance sheets of banks in London, Tokyo, Hong Kong, and Singapore — banks the Fed does not regulate, cannot see clearly, and has no obligation to rescue. These offshore dollars have a name that has confused students for sixty years. They are called *eurodollars*. And understanding them is the key that unlocks why the Fed is, like it or not, the central bank of the entire world — and why a dollar shortage is the recurring nightmare beneath every global financial crisis.

This is the deep plumbing of the dollar system, the layer beneath the headline exchange rate. Our series' spine says every currency position is a relative bet driven by the gap between two countries' interest rates and the flow of money across borders. The eurodollar market is where that flow of money *physically lives* — a vast, ungoverned ocean of dollar credit that sloshes between countries and decides, on the worst days, whether the whole machine seizes up. Here is that ocean, taken apart.

## Foundations: What a eurodollar actually is

Start from zero, and throw away the word "euro" immediately, because it is the single biggest source of confusion in this entire subject.

A **eurodollar** is a **US dollar deposit held at a bank outside the United States** — or, equivalently, a dollar loan made by a bank outside the United States. That's it. The "euro-" prefix has *nothing to do with the European single currency*, which did not even exist until 1999. The term was coined in the late 1950s, decades earlier, and "euro-" simply meant "held in Europe," then drifted to mean "held offshore, outside the home country of the currency." So a **eurodollar** is an offshore dollar; a **euroyen** is a yen deposit booked outside Japan; a **eurosterling** is a pound booked outside the UK. The naming is genuinely terrible — a euroyen deposit in Singapore has no euros and no Europe in it — but it is sixty years entrenched, so we live with it. Whenever you read "eurodollar," read **offshore dollar** in your head and you will never be confused again.

Why would a dollar live outside the US in the first place? Because the dollar is the world's money. When a German carmaker sells engines to a Saudi buyer, the invoice is in dollars even though neither party is American. When a Chinese shipping firm charters a Greek-owned tanker, the freight is paid in dollars. When a Nigerian importer buys wheat from Argentina, dollars change hands. The dollar is the currency of about half of all global trade and the unit of about 88% of all foreign-exchange trades — a number we unpack in [the DXY and the dollar's special role in the world](/blog/trading/forex/the-dxy-and-the-dollars-special-role-in-the-world) and in [the dollar system and why USD rules markets](/blog/trading/macro-trading/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy). All those non-American parties need somewhere to *keep* their dollars and somewhere to *borrow* dollars. They are not going to open accounts at a small-town bank in Kansas. They keep their dollars where they bank — in London, in Singapore, in Tokyo. And those dollars, sitting in non-US banks, are eurodollars.

### A dollar bank account that the Fed cannot see

Here is the mental model that makes everything click. Picture two ledgers. One is the set of accounts inside the United States — at JPMorgan in New York, at a community bank in Texas, ultimately settled across the Federal Reserve's own books. Every dollar in that ledger is *onshore*. The Fed can see it, regulates the bank holding it, insures it (via the FDIC), and stands behind it as lender of last resort. The other ledger is the set of dollar-denominated accounts at banks *outside* the US — at Barclays in London, at MUFG in Tokyo, at DBS in Singapore. Every dollar in *that* ledger is *offshore* — a eurodollar. The Fed does not regulate those banks, does not insure those deposits, and has no legal duty to rescue them.

![Before-and-after comparison of an onshore dollar inside the US safety net versus an offshore eurodollar outside it](/imgs/blogs/eurodollars-and-the-offshore-dollar-system-3.png)

The crucial thing is that *the dollar in the offshore ledger is a real, spendable, fully-fledged US dollar*. If you hold a \$1,000,000 deposit at a London bank, you can wire it to New York, pay a US supplier, buy a US Treasury bond — it spends exactly like an onshore dollar, because it *is* one. The only difference is *where the liability is booked*. Your million dollars is an IOU written by a London bank rather than a New York one. When you spend it, the London bank settles the payment through its own account at a US correspondent bank, and the dollar slides from the offshore ledger to the onshore one. The two ledgers are connected at the plumbing level, but they are governed by completely different rules — and that difference, as we will see, is where every crisis is born.

Why did this offshore world come into being? The standard origin story has two threads. In the 1950s, the Soviet Union and China held dollar reserves (you need dollars to trade globally even if you are a communist state) but feared the US government might freeze them if held in New York during the Cold War. So they parked their dollars at banks in London and Paris instead — offshore, beyond Washington's reach. That seeded the market. The second thread was regulation: through the 1960s the US capped the interest American banks could pay on deposits (Regulation Q) and taxed cross-border lending. London banks, free of those rules, could pay savers a little more and charge borrowers a little less, so dollar business migrated to London to escape American red tape. The market exploded. By the 1970s the "petrodollar" recycling of oil-exporter surpluses — covered in [the petrodollar and dollar dominance](/blog/trading/finance/petrodollar-and-dollar-dominance) — poured hundreds of billions of offshore dollars through London, and the eurodollar market became the beating heart of global finance. It has only grown since.

Notice what *kind* of innovation this was. The eurodollar market was not designed by a committee or chartered by a treaty. It grew because it solved a real problem — the world needed a place to keep and borrow dollars away from American regulation and American political reach — and because it was profitable for the banks that ran it. It is, in the truest sense, a *self-organising* market: nobody is in charge of it, nobody planned its size, and no single authority can shut it down. That ownerless quality is a feature in good times (it makes the market deep, flexible, and global) and a curse in bad ones (there is no one whose job it is to stand behind it). Every property of the offshore dollar system, including its fragility, descends from this founding fact: it is a private, stateless market in someone else's currency.

### The scale and shape of the offshore dollar world

It helps to anchor a sense of *how big* this is before we go further, because the numbers reframe what "the dollar" even means. The single cleanest measure — the one in our cover chart — is the BIS series for dollar credit owed by *non-bank* borrowers (companies and governments, excluding bank-to-bank lending) located *outside* the United States. That number is about **\$13.4 trillion** as of 2024, up from roughly **\$6 trillion** in 2010. To put \$13.4 trillion in perspective: it is larger than the entire annual economic output of any country on earth except the US and China. And it deliberately *excludes* the enormous web of dollar lending between offshore banks themselves, which would multiply the figure several times over. The dollar-denominated financial system that lives outside US borders is not a footnote to American finance — it is a parallel financial system, comparable in scale to the domestic one, running on the same currency but under different rules and with a different (mostly absent) safety net.

Who are the players in this offshore world? On the lending side, the biggest offshore dollar creators are large international banks headquartered *outside* the US — Japanese megabanks, European universal banks, Canadian and British banks, and the global branches of Chinese banks. These institutions take in dollar deposits and short-term dollar funding and lend dollars out worldwide. On the borrowing side sit multinational corporations financing global operations, emerging-market companies and governments that find dollar funding cheaper or deeper than their local market, commodity traders who must settle in dollars, and a thicket of non-bank financial firms. The unifying thread is that *almost none of these are American*, yet all of them are doing their borrowing and lending in dollars — which is precisely why a currency issued by one country has become the operating system of global credit. We will see in the next sections that this is no accident of habit; it is a self-reinforcing equilibrium that is extraordinarily hard to escape once a currency becomes the world's funding unit.

### One quick disclaimer: this is not the CME "eurodollar future"

Because the naming gods are cruel, there is a second thing called "eurodollar" that you must not confuse with this one. For decades the Chicago Mercantile Exchange listed a wildly popular futures contract called the **eurodollar future**, which let traders bet on the future level of three-month dollar interest rates (specifically, three-month LIBOR — the rate at which banks lent eurodollars to each other). At its peak it was one of the most traded contracts on earth. It was *named* after this offshore deposit market because the rate it tracked was the offshore dollar lending rate. But the contract is a *derivative on an interest rate*, not the deposit market itself, and after LIBOR was retired the CME transitioned it into the SOFR future. When we say "eurodollar" in this post, we mean the **offshore dollar deposit-and-loan market** — the actual dollars on actual offshore balance sheets — not the Chicago rate bet. Keep the two in separate boxes.

## How offshore banks create dollar credit

Now we reach the part that breaks most people's intuition, and it is the most important idea in this entire post. **Offshore banks do not just store dollars that already exist. They create new dollar credit out of nothing — the same way a domestic bank creates domestic money — and the Fed neither prints those dollars nor controls how many get made.**

To see this, you have to first un-learn a folk theory of banking. Most people think a bank is a warehouse: savers deposit money, the bank lends that same money to borrowers, and it can only lend what it has been given. That is not how banking works. When a bank makes a loan, it does **not** hand over someone else's deposit. It writes two new numbers on its own balance sheet *simultaneously*: a new **asset** (the loan — the borrower's promise to repay) and a new **liability** (a new deposit — the dollars it credits to the borrower's account). The borrower didn't have those dollars a second ago; now they do. The bank conjured them by typing them into existence, backed by the borrower's promise. **A loan creates a deposit.** Money is credit. We dig into the mechanics of this in the banking and money-creation literature, but the punchline is all you need: *lending is money creation, not money lending.*

Here is the kicker. **An offshore bank can do exactly the same thing — in dollars — while sitting in London.** A London bank does not need to hold dollars at the Fed to make a dollar loan. It books a dollar asset (the loan) and a dollar liability (a dollar deposit it credits to the borrower), and a brand-new offshore dollar has just been created on a balance sheet thousands of miles from Washington. The Fed didn't print it. The Fed can't see it directly. No one rang a bell. The world's stock of dollar credit just went up, created by a bank the Fed has no authority over.

![Pipeline showing a US export payment deposited in a London bank and re-lent as a new dollar loan that never touches the Fed](/imgs/blogs/eurodollars-and-the-offshore-dollar-system-2.png)

#### Worked example: a London bank books a \$10,000,000 dollar loan with no US deposit

Let's make this concrete, because it is the single fact that explains why the dollar system is so big and so fragile.

A shipping company in Singapore needs **\$10,000,000** to buy a vessel. It goes to a London-based bank. The London bank approves the loan. Watch what happens on the bank's balance sheet — and notice what does **not** happen.

```
London bank balance sheet  (the moment the loan is made)

ASSETS                              LIABILITIES
  Loan to shipping co.  +$10.0M       Deposit: shipping co.  +$10.0M
  ----------------------------------------------------------------
  net change            +$10.0M       net change             +$10.0M
```

The bank created a **\$10,000,000 asset** (the loan) and a **\$10,000,000 liability** (the new dollar deposit it just credited to the shipping company's account). The shipping company now has \$10,000,000 of spendable dollars it did not have a minute ago. And here is the point: **the London bank did not need \$10,000,000 of pre-existing dollars to do this.** It did not draw down a balance at the Fed (it may not even have one). It did not borrow \$10,000,000 from another bank first. It simply *issued a dollar liability against a dollar asset*, and net new dollar credit — \$10,000,000 of it — now exists, entirely outside the United States.

The only thing the bank must manage is *settlement*: when the shipping company actually spends the \$10,000,000 (wires it to the shipyard), the bank will need real dollars to settle that outgoing payment through its US correspondent. So the bank does need *funding* — but it raises that funding in the offshore market (it borrows dollars from another offshore bank, or takes in eurodollar deposits) at the moment of settlement, *after* the credit was already created. Creation comes first; funding comes second. *This is why the offshore dollar pile can grow without the Fed lifting a finger: every offshore dollar loan is a privately-created dollar, and the funding to settle it is found in a private market the Fed does not run.*

### The offshore dollar credit multiplier

Because every offshore dollar loan creates an offshore dollar deposit, and that deposit can be re-lent, the offshore system has its own **money multiplier** — a credit pyramid built on top of whatever base of "real" Fed dollars happens to be circulating offshore. This is the offshore credit stack, and it is taller than most people realise.

#### Worked example: the dollar credit multiplier offshore

Suppose **\$100,000,000** of genuine, Fed-created dollars leaks offshore — say, a US corporation parks \$100,000,000 in its London subsidiary's bank account. Trace what the offshore banking system can build on top of that \$100,000,000.

```
Round 0  London bank A receives deposit            +$100,000,000
Round 1  Bank A lends 90% to a borrower            +$90,000,000  (new offshore dollars)
         Borrower's $90M lands as a deposit in bank B
Round 2  Bank B lends 90% of that                  +$81,000,000  (new offshore dollars)
Round 3  Bank C lends 90% of that                  +$72,900,000
  ...   (geometric series continues)
-----------------------------------------------------------------
Total deposits created  =  $100M / (1 - 0.9)  =  $1,000,000,000
```

With a 90% lending rate at each step, \$100,000,000 of base dollars supports up to **\$1,000,000,000** of offshore dollar deposits — a 10× multiplier. In reality offshore banks hold *far* thinner buffers than this stylised example, because there is **no reserve requirement at the Fed on offshore dollars** — the Fed's reserve rules apply to onshore banks. So the offshore multiplier can run higher and with less of a safety cushion than the onshore one. The takeaway is stark: *a modest amount of Fed base money, leaked offshore, supports a towering pyramid of privately-created dollar credit — and the pyramid stands on funding that has to be rolled over constantly, with no central bank underneath it.*

This is why the headline number is so large. The Bank for International Settlements (BIS) tracks dollar credit owed by *non-bank* borrowers outside the US — companies and governments, not even counting bank-to-bank lending. That measure alone grew from about **\$6 trillion in 2010 to \$13.4 trillion in 2024**, the curve in our cover figure. Add interbank offshore dollar lending and the offshore-dollar financial system is many tens of trillions deep. *None of that was printed by the Fed.* It was created by the offshore banking system, lending dollars into existence the way any bank lends any money into existence — but in a currency none of those banks can issue.

### Where the offshore dollars come from in the first place

The multiplier needs a seed — some real dollars to leak offshore and form the base of the pyramid. Where do they come from? Four main spigots:

1. **The US trade deficit.** Every year the US buys more from the world than it sells, sending a net flood of dollars abroad — the mechanism we trace in [the balance of payments and the current account](/blog/trading/forex/the-balance-of-payments-and-the-current-account). Those dollars land in foreign hands and foreign banks. The US running a persistent current-account deficit is, in effect, the US *exporting dollar reserves* to the rest of the world.
2. **Petrodollar recycling.** Oil is priced in dollars, so oil exporters earn dollars, and they bank a big share of them offshore. The original 1970s eurodollar boom was largely recycled oil money.
3. **Foreign borrowing of dollars.** When a foreign company issues a dollar bond or takes a dollar loan to fund itself, it pulls dollar credit into the offshore system.
4. **Central-bank reserves.** Foreign central banks hold trillions of dollars as reserves — about 58% of all official FX reserves are in dollars — and some of that sits in the offshore market too.

The point is that the offshore dollar base is fed by the structural reality that the world *runs on dollars it must obtain from outside its own borders*. No foreign country can print dollars. They have to earn them (export), borrow them (issue dollar debt), or be given them (Fed swap lines). That dependency is the hinge on which the whole story turns.

### The price of an offshore dollar: LIBOR, then SOFR

Every market needs a price, and the offshore dollar market's price is an interest rate: what does it cost to borrow a dollar offshore for three months? For most of the market's history that price had a famous name — **LIBOR**, the London Interbank Offered Rate. LIBOR was, quite literally, the rate at which big banks said they could borrow *eurodollars* from each other in London. It became the reference rate for an astonishing share of global finance: trillions of dollars of loans, mortgages, and derivatives were priced as "LIBOR plus a spread." That is the deepest sign of how central the offshore dollar market is — for decades, the benchmark cost of money for half the planet was set by the offshore dollar lending rate, not by anything the Fed published directly.

LIBOR had a fatal weakness: it was based on banks' *self-reported* estimates of their borrowing costs rather than real transactions, which made it vulnerable to the manipulation scandals that eventually killed it. Regulators retired LIBOR and replaced it with **SOFR** (the Secured Overnight Financing Rate), which is built from actual transactions in the US repo market. The switch matters for our story in a subtle way: SOFR is an *onshore* rate, anchored in US money markets, whereas LIBOR was an *offshore* rate. By moving the world's benchmark from an offshore rate to an onshore one, the reform tied global dollar credit even more directly to US monetary plumbing — pulling the offshore world a little further into the Fed's gravitational field. But the core fact is unchanged: the price of an offshore dollar loan is set by US dollar interest rates, and US dollar interest rates are set by the Fed. The denomination is the leash, and the benchmark rate is how the Fed tugs it.

The spread *over* the benchmark is where the offshore market's own supply and demand for dollars shows up. In calm times a strong offshore bank borrows at a thin spread over SOFR; in a scare, that spread blows out as dollars get hoarded — which is precisely the stress that the cross-currency basis, our fever thermometer later in this post, measures from a different angle. So you can read the offshore dollar's price in two layers: the benchmark (set by the Fed) tells you the *level* of dollar funding costs worldwide, and the spread (set by offshore supply and demand) tells you how *stressed* the offshore plumbing is right now. Both layers matter, and a trader who watches the dollar reads both.

## Why the Fed is the world's central bank — whether it likes it or not

We can now state the central thesis cleanly. Because roughly **\$13.4 trillion** of dollar credit is owed by borrowers outside the US, and because every dollar of that credit is **priced off US interest rates** and ultimately **rescuable only by the Fed**, the Federal Reserve sets monetary conditions for the entire planet. It is the world's central bank — not by treaty, not by choice, but by the brute arithmetic of how much of the world's debt is denominated in its currency.

Walk through the transmission. The Fed sets the US policy rate. That anchors SOFR (the secured overnight financing rate, the successor to LIBOR) and the whole dollar money-market curve. Offshore dollar loans are *priced off that curve* — a London bank's dollar loan to the Singapore shipping company is set at "SOFR plus a spread," exactly like an onshore loan. So when the Fed hikes, the cost of *every* offshore dollar loan rises in lockstep, even though the borrower is in Singapore and the lender is in London and neither is in the Fed's jurisdiction. The Fed never voted to raise rates in Singapore. It doesn't have to. The denomination does the work.

![Graph showing a Fed rate decision repricing offshore dollar funding and forcing a global tightening, backstopped by Fed swap lines](/imgs/blogs/eurodollars-and-the-offshore-dollar-system-5.png)

The mechanism in [how monetary policy moves currencies](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-monetary-policy-moves-currencies-rate-differentials) explains how a rate gap drives an exchange rate. The eurodollar lens adds the missing piece: a Fed hike doesn't just make the dollar more attractive to hold (the rate-differential channel), it makes **dollar debt more expensive to service everywhere on earth at once** (the credit channel). These two channels reinforce each other in the same direction, which is what gives Fed tightening such outsized global bite. The rate-differential channel pulls capital *toward* dollars (raising the dollar's price), while the credit channel raises the cost of the dollar debt that foreigners must keep servicing — and a stronger dollar makes that debt heavier in local-currency terms. So a single Fed decision tightens the world through three doors at once: the cost of new dollar borrowing rises, the dollar gains against the currencies of those who owe dollars, and the local-currency burden of existing dollar debt swells. No domestic central bank facing a Fed hike can fully insulate its economy, because the squeeze arrives through the denomination of its companies' debts, not through its own policy rate. A company in Indonesia with a dollar loan feels the Fed's hike in its interest bill as directly as a company in Indiana — arguably more, because it has no offsetting dollar income and a local currency that tends to weaken against the dollar precisely when the Fed is tightening. This is the "dollar wrecking ball": a tightening Fed simultaneously raises the cost of the world's dollar debt and pushes the dollar up against the currencies of the countries that owe it, a double squeeze on emerging markets.

#### Worked example: a Fed hike adds \$2,000,000 to a foreign borrower's annual bill

Take an Indonesian property developer that borrowed **\$100,000,000** in the offshore market at **SOFR + 2%**. In early 2022, SOFR was near 0%, so its rate was about 2% and its annual interest was roughly **\$2,000,000**. Over 2022–2023 the Fed hiked SOFR to about 5.3%, covered in [the fastest hiking cycle](/blog/trading/macro-trading/carry-trade-unwinds-1998-2008-2024-when-leverage-breaks)-style episodes. The developer's rate became **5.3% + 2% = 7.3%**, and its annual interest jumped to about **\$7,300,000**.

```
Before (early 2022):  $100,000,000 x (0.0% + 2%)   = $2,000,000 / year
After  (late 2023):   $100,000,000 x (5.3% + 2%)   = $7,300,000 / year
-------------------------------------------------------------------
Extra annual cost from the Fed's hikes              = $5,300,000 / year
```

The Fed added **\$5,300,000 a year** to this Indonesian borrower's interest bill without ever holding a hearing about Indonesia. And it gets worse: over the same window the rupiah weakened against the dollar, so each dollar of interest also cost *more rupiah* to buy. *The Fed's domestic policy is a tax on the whole world's dollar debt, and the people who pay it most never get a vote — that is what "the Fed is the world's central bank" means in practice.*

### The reserve-currency burden runs both ways

There is a subtler consequence. Because so much of the world borrows in dollars it cannot print, foreign borrowers and their central banks are perpetually managing a currency-mismatch risk: their assets and revenues are in local currency, but a chunk of their debt is in dollars. When the dollar rises, that debt gets heavier in local-currency terms — a balance-sheet shock that has nothing to do with their own economy and everything to do with what the Fed and global risk appetite are doing. This is why a rising dollar is one of the most reliable stress indicators for emerging markets, a theme developed in [the dollar's cross-asset gravity](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-dollar-cross-asset-gravity). The dollar's role as the world's funding currency exports US monetary conditions — and US financial cycles — to economies that never asked for them.

### Why foreign central banks can't just opt out

A natural question is: if dollar dependence is so dangerous, why don't countries simply stop borrowing in dollars and use their own currencies? Some try, and the difficulty of escaping reveals just how entrenched the system is. The problem is a chicken-and-egg trap. A foreign company would happily borrow in its own currency — except that international lenders don't want to *hold* a small, volatile emerging-market currency, so loans in that currency are scarce, short, and expensive. Dollars, by contrast, are wanted by everyone, so dollar loans are deep, long, and cheap. So the borrower takes the dollar loan because it is the best deal *on offer*, and in doing so adds one more brick to the offshore dollar pile and one more notch of dollar dependence to the system. Each individual choice is rational; the aggregate result is a world that has bound itself ever tighter to a currency none of its members can issue. Economists call the inability of most countries to borrow abroad in their own currency "original sin," and it is the deep reason the eurodollar system keeps growing even as everyone agrees the dependence is dangerous.

The same logic locks in the dollar's reserve status. A foreign central bank that wants to be able to backstop *its own* banks' dollar funding needs a war chest of dollars — reserves. So it accumulates dollar assets (mostly US Treasuries), which deepens the demand for dollars and the dollar's centrality, which makes the next country's dollar dependence even more rational. The system is a self-reinforcing equilibrium: the more the world relies on dollars, the more it must hold and borrow dollars, the more central the dollar becomes. Breaking out would require a coordinated global shift that no single actor can lead, which is why the dollar's dominance has survived every prediction of its demise for half a century. The eurodollar market is both the symptom and the engine of that lock-in — covered from the macro side in [the dollar system, why USD rules markets](/blog/trading/macro-trading/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy).

## The funding fragility: borrowing short, lending long, with no backstop

We have built the system up. Now we take apart why it periodically blows up. The offshore dollar market has a structural flaw baked into its DNA, and it is the same flaw that destroys *every* bank that fails: it **borrows short and lends long, in a currency it cannot create, with no lender of last resort.** Onshore, the Fed plugs that gap. Offshore, nobody does — until the Fed chooses to.

Recall how an offshore bank funds itself. It creates a long-term dollar loan (say a 5-year loan to the shipping company), but it funds that loan with *short-term* offshore dollar borrowing — overnight and 3-month deposits and interbank loans that must be **rolled over** constantly. This is the classic banking maturity transformation: long assets, short liabilities. It is profitable in calm times because long rates exceed short rates. And it is lethal in a panic, because the moment lenders refuse to roll over the short funding, the bank is stuck holding a 5-year asset it cannot sell quickly and a pile of overnight liabilities it cannot repay. That is a bank run — the coordination-failure mechanism dissected in [bank runs as coordination games](/blog/trading/game-theory/bank-runs-as-coordination-games-diamond-dybvig-and-svb).

![Graph of the offshore funding-fragility loop where a dollar scare halts rollovers and forces fire sales with no domestic backstop](/imgs/blogs/eurodollars-and-the-offshore-dollar-system-6.png)

Now here is what makes the *offshore* version uniquely dangerous. When an onshore US bank faces a run, it can pledge its assets to the Fed's discount window and borrow freshly-printed dollars to meet the withdrawals — the Fed is the lender of last resort, and it can create dollars without limit. An offshore bank in a dollar run has **no such option**. It cannot print dollars (it's not the Fed). It does not have direct access to the Fed's discount window (it's not a US bank). Its own central bank — the Bank of England, the Bank of Japan — *can* print pounds and yen without limit, but it **cannot print dollars**, and dollars are exactly what the bank owes. So a domestic central bank, the natural rescuer, is powerless in precisely the currency that matters. The offshore bank is left to do the only thing it can: dump assets to raise dollars at any price — a fire sale that craters asset prices, which triggers more margin calls and more dumping, the doom loop that turns a funding scare into a crash.

What turns this from a single bank's problem into a system-wide seizure is that *everyone is in the same trade at the same time*. In calm markets, an offshore bank that needs dollars simply borrows them from another offshore bank that has a surplus — the interbank market redistributes dollars smoothly, and no one notices the underlying mismatch. But in a scare, every offshore bank simultaneously wants to *hoard* dollars and *stop lending* them, because each one fears it might need its dollars to meet its own withdrawals. The redistributive market that worked yesterday vanishes overnight. This is a coordination failure: it is individually rational for each bank to hoard, but the aggregate effect of everyone hoarding is that the dollars stop circulating and the whole system freezes — even though the *total* quantity of dollars hasn't changed. A funding market is only liquid as long as everyone believes it will *stay* liquid; the belief is self-fulfilling in both directions. The same self-fulfilling dynamic drives ordinary bank runs and even speculative attacks on currency pegs, which is why the patterns rhyme so closely across [bank runs as coordination games](/blog/trading/game-theory/bank-runs-as-coordination-games-diamond-dybvig-and-svb) and the [Asian financial crisis of 1997](/blog/trading/finance/asian-financial-crisis-1997).

There is one more twist that makes the offshore version worse than a textbook bank run. A domestic bank run is a run by *depositors* — retail savers pulling cash. An offshore dollar run is a run by *other banks and funds* in the wholesale market, and wholesale lenders are far faster and more skittish than retail depositors. They are professionals watching screens; the moment a counterparty looks shaky, they cut the line at the next rollover date, which might be tomorrow morning. So an offshore dollar funding run can go from "everything is fine" to "the bank is gone" in *days*, not weeks. That speed is why the Fed's swap-line response in 2008 and 2020 had to be measured in hours and hundreds of billions, not in a leisurely policy process — by the time a committee finished deliberating, the offshore system would already be on fire.

#### Worked example: a \$5,000,000,000 offshore bank that cannot roll its dollars

Consider a mid-sized European bank with a **\$5,000,000,000** offshore dollar loan book, funded by **\$4,500,000,000** of short-term dollar borrowing (3-month deposits and interbank funding) plus **\$500,000,000** of equity. In normal times it rolls the \$4,500,000,000 every quarter without a thought. Then a dollar scare hits — counterparties hoard dollars, no one wants to lend.

```
Funding to roll this quarter                    $4,500,000,000
Amount lenders will actually roll (panic)       $2,700,000,000   (60%)
-------------------------------------------------------------------
Dollar shortfall the bank must cover            $1,800,000,000
Dollars it can raise selling liquid assets       $900,000,000
-------------------------------------------------------------------
Remaining gap                                    $900,000,000
```

The bank is suddenly **\$900,000,000 short of dollars** it owes *this quarter*, against assets it cannot sell fast enough. Its own central bank can lend it unlimited euros — useless, because it owes dollars. To raise the last \$900,000,000 it must fire-sale its loan book at a discount, booking losses that eat into its \$500,000,000 of equity. If the discount is steep enough, the losses exceed the equity and the bank is insolvent — not because its loans were bad, but because it could not refinance dollars it never had the power to create. *This is the offshore system's original sin: it runs a dollar bank without a dollar central bank behind it, so every dollar scare is potentially terminal — and that is exactly why the Fed eventually had to step in.*

### The cross-currency basis: the offshore dollar-stress gauge

How do we *see* this stress before it becomes a headline? There is a beautiful, precise instrument: the **cross-currency basis**. Here is the idea without the jargon. There is a textbook law called **covered interest parity** (CIP) — explored in full in [the cross-currency basis, when covered parity breaks](/blog/trading/forex/the-cross-currency-basis-when-covered-parity-breaks) — that says you should be able to turn euros into dollars two ways for the same cost: borrow dollars directly, *or* borrow euros and swap them into dollars in the FX market. If those two routes cost the same, the "basis" is zero. When dollars get scarce offshore, the swap route gets more expensive than the direct route — people are willing to *pay a premium* to get dollars via the swap market because they can't get them directly. That premium is the **cross-currency basis**, and a *negative* basis means **dollars are scarce and expensive offshore**. It is the offshore dollar market's fever thermometer.

![Line chart of the EUR/USD cross-currency basis from 2008 to 2024 spiking negative in 2008 and 2020 crises](/imgs/blogs/eurodollars-and-the-offshore-dollar-system-4.png)

Look at the EUR/USD basis through history and you can read every dollar-funding crisis off it like a seismograph. In September 2008, as Lehman fell and the offshore dollar funding market seized, the 3-month EUR/USD basis blew out to about **−160 bps** — a punishing premium to get dollars. In the 2011 eurozone crisis it hit about **−150 bps** as European banks, huge offshore dollar borrowers, found their dollar funding evaporating. In March 2020 it spiked to about **−85 bps** in days. In calm periods (2019, end-2024) it sits near **−12 to −15 bps** — a small structural premium reflecting the permanent, low-grade scarcity of offshore dollars. The basis is, quite literally, the price of the dollar shortage, and it spikes negative every time the offshore system's funding fragility is exposed.

The yen basis tells the same story even more violently, because Japanese banks are among the largest offshore dollar lenders on earth — they take in cheap yen and swap it into dollars to lend abroad. When dollars get scarce, *their* swap cost explodes.

![Bar chart of the USD/JPY cross-currency basis from 2008 to 2024 with deep negative spikes in 2008 and 2020](/imgs/blogs/eurodollars-and-the-offshore-dollar-system-8.png)

In October 2008 the 3-month USD/JPY basis hit about **−220 bps**; in March 2020, about **−145 bps**. Those are enormous numbers — at −220 bps, a Japanese bank swapping yen into dollars was paying over two extra percentage points *just for the privilege of accessing dollars* it could not otherwise get. The basis is the cleanest single proof that the offshore dollar market is a real, separate funding system with its own supply-and-demand for dollars — a supply-and-demand the Fed does not directly control, but ultimately has to rescue.

### The Fed's grudging answer: swap lines

So who *does* rescue the offshore dollar system when it seizes? Only one institution can: the Fed, the sole issuer of dollars. But the Fed cannot lend directly to a bank in Seoul or Frankfurt — it has no relationship with them. So it invented a workaround: **central-bank liquidity swap lines.** The Fed lends dollars to a foreign central bank (say the ECB or the Bank of Korea), taking that bank's local currency as collateral; the foreign central bank then lends those dollars onward to *its* commercial banks, who use them to plug their offshore dollar funding gaps. The Fed becomes the lender of last resort to the offshore dollar system *at one remove*, through foreign central banks acting as its retail arm.

![Bar chart of Fed central-bank dollar swap lines drawn in crises peaking at 583 billion in 2008 and 449 billion in 2020](/imgs/blogs/eurodollars-and-the-offshore-dollar-system-7.png)

The numbers show just how essential this backstop is. In December 2008, foreign central banks drew about **\$583 billion** of Fed swaps — over half a trillion dollars of emergency offshore dollar liquidity. In the spring of 2020, swap drawings peaked near **\$449 billion** as the pandemic dollar scramble hit. In March 2023, during the regional-bank and Credit Suisse wobble, about **\$60 billion** was drawn. These spikes are not background noise — they are the offshore dollar system being kept alive on a Fed drip feed during each crisis. The full architecture of these lines is the subject of [the global dollar shortage and central-bank swap lines](/blog/trading/forex/the-global-dollar-shortage-and-central-bank-swap-lines), but the headline is this: **the offshore dollar system has no lender of last resort except the Fed, and the Fed provides it only in emergencies, only to a chosen club of central banks, and only at its own discretion.** The world's reserve currency comes with a backstop that is real but conditional — and that conditionality is the geopolitical heart of the dollar system.

The deep irony is that the Fed *has* to do this, even though it would rather not. If the offshore dollar market froze and a wave of foreign banks failed on their dollar debts, the damage would crash straight back into the US — through US banks' counterparty exposures, through collapsing global trade, through a soaring dollar that wrecks US exporters. The Fed cannot let the offshore dollar system fail without harming the onshore one, because the two ledgers are joined at the settlement layer. So the lender of last resort to the world is the Fed by *necessity*, not generosity. It is stuck with the job because it is the only one who can print the currency the world chose to borrow in.

## Common misconceptions

**"A eurodollar is a European currency / has something to do with the euro."** No — and this is the single most common error. A eurodollar is a US dollar held *offshore* (outside the US). The "euro-" prefix means "offshore," coined in the 1950s, decades before the euro currency existed. A euroyen deposit in Singapore is an offshore yen with no euros and no Europe in it. Read "eurodollar" as "offshore dollar" every time.

**"Only the Fed can create dollars."** False in the way that matters most. The Fed creates *base* dollars (reserves and cash). But the vast majority of dollars in the world are *credit* dollars — bank deposits created when banks make loans — and **offshore banks create dollar credit too**, lending dollars into existence on balance sheets the Fed neither owns nor regulates. The \$13.4 trillion of offshore dollar credit owed by non-banks was overwhelmingly *not* printed by the Fed; it was created by the offshore banking system. The Fed controls the *price* of dollars (the rate) far more than the *quantity* of offshore dollar credit.

**"The offshore dollar market is small and exotic."** The opposite. Dollar credit to non-bank borrowers outside the US alone is about **\$13.4 trillion** (2024) — larger than the GDP of every country except the US and China. Add interbank offshore dollar lending and the offshore-dollar financial system is tens of trillions deep. It is the *core* of global finance, not a sideshow. When it sneezes — as in 2008 and 2020 — the whole world catches the flu.

**"If the Fed floods the US with dollars, the world can't be short of dollars."** Demonstrably false — it is exactly what happened in March 2020. The Fed can create unlimited *onshore* dollars, but those dollars do not automatically reach an offshore bank in Seoul that needs to roll its dollar funding. Onshore liquidity and offshore liquidity are different pools connected by imperfect plumbing. A dollar glut onshore can coexist with a dollar famine offshore — which is *why the Fed had to build swap lines* to pipe dollars across the gap.

**"Foreign central banks can rescue their own banks in a dollar crisis."** No — and this is the fragility's crux. The Bank of Japan can print unlimited yen; it cannot print a single dollar. When a Japanese bank faces a dollar run, yen are useless, because the bank owes *dollars*. The only entity that can create the dollars the offshore system needs is the Fed. That is why a domestic central bank, the natural lender of last resort, is structurally powerless in an offshore dollar crisis — and why the Fed's swap lines, not the local central bank, are the real backstop.

## How it shows up in real markets

**The Lehman crisis (September–December 2008).** The 2008 crisis was, at its plumbing core, a global *dollar funding* crisis. European banks had built enormous offshore dollar books — by some estimates over \$1 trillion of dollar assets funded by short-term dollar borrowing — and when the interbank market froze, they could not roll that funding. The EUR/USD cross-currency basis blew out to **−160 bps** and the yen basis to **−220 bps**, the deepest readings on record. The Fed responded by opening unlimited swap lines to major central banks; by December 2008, **\$583 billion** of Fed dollars had been pushed out through those lines into the offshore system. This was the moment the Fed publicly, visibly became the world's central bank — lending dollars to the ECB, the Bank of England, the Bank of Japan, and a dozen others so *their* banks could survive a dollar run. The episode is woven through [when correlations go to one in a crisis](/blog/trading/cross-asset/when-correlations-go-to-one-in-a-crisis) and the [LTCM 1998 case study](/blog/trading/finance/ltcm-1998-when-genius-failed) as the template for how a funding squeeze becomes a fire sale.

**The March 2020 dash for dollars.** When the pandemic hit, every entity on earth with dollar liabilities scrambled for dollars at once — companies drawing credit lines, funds meeting margin, central banks defending currencies, exporters losing dollar revenue. The offshore dollar market seized within days. The cross-currency basis spiked to **−85 bps** (EUR) and **−145 bps** (JPY); even US Treasuries, the world's safest dollar asset, were dumped for cash dollars. The Fed responded faster and bigger than ever: it cut swap-line pricing, extended lines to more central banks, and set up a new repo facility (FIMA) so *any* foreign central bank could swap its Treasuries for dollars. Swap drawings peaked near **\$449 billion**. The dollar shortage, and the Fed's response to it, is the central case study of [the global dollar shortage and central-bank swap lines](/blog/trading/forex/the-global-dollar-shortage-and-central-bank-swap-lines). The lesson markets took away: in a true panic, *cash dollars beat everything*, including Treasuries, and the offshore system lives or dies on whether the Fed turns the swap lines on.

**The 2022–2023 tightening and the EM squeeze.** When the Fed hiked from near zero to about 5.3% in 2022–2023, the dollar surged (DXY hit about 114.8 in September 2022, its highest in two decades) and the global cost of dollar debt jumped in lockstep — the wrecking-ball dynamic. Emerging-market borrowers with offshore dollar loans saw their interest bills balloon (our worked example added \$5.3M a year to one Indonesian borrower) while their local currencies weakened against the rising dollar, a double squeeze. There was no acute funding freeze this time — the basis stayed contained (EUR around −45 bps in late 2022) because the stress was a slow tightening, not a sudden panic. But it showed the *other* face of the Fed-as-world-central-bank: even in an orderly cycle, a Fed hike is a global tax on \$13 trillion of dollar debt, transmitted instantly through the pricing of offshore dollar credit.

**The everyday basis: a permanent low-grade dollar shortage.** Even in calm markets the cross-currency basis sits slightly negative — around **−12 to −15 bps** for EUR/USD, **−35 bps** for USD/JPY at end-2024. That persistent negative basis is the market telling you that, in normal times too, there are structurally more people who *need* dollars offshore than there are dollars freely available — a chronic, low-grade dollar shortage that flares into an acute one whenever risk appetite collapses. The non-zero calm-time basis is the steady hum of the offshore dollar system's fundamental imbalance: a world that borrows in a currency it cannot print is *always* a little short of it, and sometimes catastrophically so.

## The takeaway: read the dollar as a debt the world owes

Here is the shift in how you should read a currency once you understand eurodollars. Most people see the dollar as *America's money* — a chip the US plays. The deeper truth is that the dollar is **the world's debt**: roughly \$13.4 trillion of dollar credit owed by non-Americans to a global banking system the Fed prices but does not control. When you hold that picture, three things click into place.

First, **the dollar is most powerful when everyone is short of it.** Because the world borrows in dollars it must keep refinancing, a scramble for dollars *strengthens* the dollar at the worst possible moment — risk-off, crisis, panic. This is the left side of the "dollar smile" (the dollar rises both when the US booms *and* when the world panics), and it is why the dollar is the ultimate safe haven: in a crisis, the world's dollar debtors all reach for dollars at once, and the price goes up. The eurodollar system *is* the engine of the safe-haven bid. We connect this to the trading playbook in [trading the dollar via DXY, carry, and the dollar smile](/blog/trading/macro-trading/trading-the-dollar-dxy-carry-dollar-smile).

Second, **the Fed's policy is the world's policy, and the cross-currency basis is your early-warning gauge.** When you watch the Fed, you are not just watching the rate gap that drives the exchange rate — you are watching the price of \$13 trillion of global debt and the tightness of the funding that rolls it. When you want to know if the offshore dollar system is under stress *right now*, you don't read the news; you read the basis. A basis grinding more negative is the offshore dollar market telling you the funding fragility is being tested — before the headlines arrive.

Third, **every "dollar shortage" is the same fragility wearing a new mask.** 1998, 2008, 2020, the next one — they rhyme because the structure is unchanged: a colossal offshore dollar credit pyramid, borrowing short and lending long in a currency its banks cannot print, with no lender of last resort except a Fed that helps only its chosen central banks, only in emergencies, at its own discretion. The exchange rate you trade sits on top of this. The rate gap and the cross-border flows that make up our series' spine are, at the deepest level, *flows of this offshore dollar credit* — created in London, priced in Washington, and capable of freezing the whole world solid on the day the rollover stops. Read the dollar not as a price but as a promise the world has made to keep finding dollars it cannot make — and you will understand why the Fed is the world's central bank whether it likes it or not.
