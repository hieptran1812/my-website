> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — In eighteen months Vietnam ran a complete policy cycle in fast-forward. In 2022, an anti-fraud crackdown on the corporate-bond market, an imported wave of global tightening, a defense of the dong, and two emergency rate hikes (+200bp) combined to drive the VN-Index from about 1,528 to about 874 — roughly a 33% loss for the year, one of the worst in Asia, led by property and banks. Then in 2023, with growth slowing, the State Bank of Vietnam cut its refinancing rate from 6.0% to 4.5% — easing *ahead* of a still-hiking Federal Reserve — and Vietnamese assets stabilized and recovered.
>
> - Policy reprices assets through the same machine everywhere: a lever (a crackdown, an FX defense, a rate) reaches an asset through a channel (the discount rate, the supply of credit, the currency) and shows up as a *change in what the asset is worth*. Vietnam 2022-23 is that machine run forward, then backward, in one clean cycle.
> - The 2022 shock was not mainly about interest rates. The dangerous lever was the **funding freeze**: when the primary corporate-bond market shut, property developers who relied on rolling their bonds could not refinance, and banks were capped by their **credit quota** from filling the gap. A solvent-on-paper developer could still be forced into a fire sale.
> - The 2023 pivot is the rarer, more instructive event: a small open economy *cut rates while the Fed was still raising them*, buying domestic relief at the cost of currency pressure. The single number to remember: the SBV refinancing rate went **6.0% → 4.5% in four cuts between March and June 2023**, while the Fed was climbing to a 5.50% peak.

In April 2022, Vietnam's stock market was making new highs. The VN-Index — the benchmark of the Ho Chi Minh City exchange, the index that for most Vietnamese savers simply *is* "the market" — touched about **1,528 points**, the top of a roaring post-pandemic bull run. Property developers were the darlings; their bonds were sold to retail savers as if they were bank deposits with a better yield; bank stocks rode the credit boom. Eighteen months later the same index had been to **874** and back, the most violent round trip in the market's modern history, and the cause was not a war, not a pandemic, and not a global crash. The cause was **policy** — a sequence of decisions made in Hanoi and, in part, in Washington.

This post is the case study of that cycle. It is the clearest example in the series of the thing we keep insisting on: that a government or central bank pulls a lever, the lever reaches an asset through a transmission channel, and the asset's *value* changes as a result. Vietnam 2022-23 is unusually clean because you can watch the machine run in both directions inside a year and a half. First the tightening: a crackdown that froze a funding market, a defense of the currency, and emergency rate hikes, all landing at once and dragging asset values down. Then the easing: four rate cuts that reversed the channel and let values recover. We will walk each lever to each asset with dated, real numbers, and we will anchor every Vietnamese-dong figure in US dollars so the magnitudes are legible to any reader.

![Graph of Vietnam's policy cycle showing four 2022 tightening levers flowing into the VN-Index drop then the 2023 cuts lifting property and bank stocks](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-policy-case-studies-2022-crackdown-to-2023-cuts-1.png)

The figure above is the whole argument on one page. On the left, four levers — the bond crackdown, imported Fed tightening, the FX defense, and the SBV's own emergency hikes — converge on a single outcome: the VN-Index falling from 1,528 to 874, a roughly 33% loss for 2022. In the middle sits the pivot: the 2023 decision to cut the policy rate from 6.0% to 4.5%, taken *ahead* of the Fed. On the right, that single cut fans back out to lift the assets the tightening had crushed worst — property stocks and bank stocks. Read left to right and you read eighteen months of Vietnamese market history as one causal chain. The rest of this post is that diagram, expanded with the arithmetic.

## Foundations: how policy reaches a Vietnamese asset

Before the case study, we need the machine, built from zero, because Vietnam's version has a few moving parts that the US version does not. Three ideas do almost all the work: the **discount rate**, the **supply of credit**, and the **currency**. Everything below is some combination of those three.

### The discount rate: why one interest rate prices everything

Start with the single most important idea in all of asset valuation: **a dollar — or a dong — in the future is worth less than one today**, because money today can be put to work and earn a return. If the going safe rate is 5%, then 100 dong today grows to 105 dong next year, so a *promise* of 105 dong next year is worth only 100 to you now. To turn any future cash flow into its value today, you shrink it by the going rate. That rate is the **discount rate**, and the shrinking is called discounting. The present value of a cash flow `CF` arriving `t` years out, discounted at rate `r`, is:

```
PV = CF / (1 + r)^t
```

and the value of a whole asset is the sum of the present values of every cash flow it will ever pay:

```
Value = CF1/(1+r) + CF2/(1+r)^2 + CF3/(1+r)^3 + ...
```

The crucial feature is that `r` sits in the *denominator*, raised to the power of time. Raise `r` and every term shrinks, and the far-future terms shrink fastest. A stock is a claim on profits that arrive for decades; a property is a claim on rent that arrives for decades; so both are dominated by far-out cash flows, and both are exquisitely sensitive to the discount rate. When a central bank raises its policy rate, it lifts the whole structure of discount rates in the economy, and every long-lived asset reprices down at once. This series' companion post on [the discount-rate channel](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-discount-rate-channel-how-rates-reprice-cash-flows) builds this mechanism in full; here we just need the one fact that the policy rate lives in the denominator of every valuation.

It is worth feeling *how big* this effect is, because it explains why Vietnamese property developers — the most rate-sensitive assets in the market — fell so much harder than the index. Consider a single cash flow arriving ten years from now. Discount it at 8% and it is worth about 46 cents on the dong today; discount the same cash flow at 10% (a 200bp rise, exactly the SBV's 2022 hike) and it is worth about 39 cents — a drop of roughly 16% from a 200bp move, on a *single* far-out cash flow. Now stack that effect across the dozens of years of future cash flows a developer's projects represent, and a "mere" 200bp rise in the policy rate compresses the fair value of a long-duration developer by far more than 200bp would suggest. The sensitivity of an asset's value to the discount rate has a name — **duration** — and the longer-dated and more leveraged the cash flows, the higher the duration. Property developers, financing decades of future apartment sales with borrowed money, are among the highest-duration assets in any market. That is why, in both 2022 and 2023, they moved the most in both directions. The discount rate is not a gentle dial for a developer; it is a lever with enormous mechanical advantage.

One more subtlety belongs here, because it is special to an emerging market. In a developed economy, the relevant discount rate is roughly the local risk-free rate plus an equity risk premium. In Vietnam, the discount rate that prices a local stock also carries a **country-risk premium** and is anchored, in part, to *global* conditions — when US rates rise and the dollar surges, global investors demand a higher return to hold any emerging-market asset, which lifts the discount rate on Vietnamese stocks even before the SBV touches its own policy rate. So the imported-tightening lever (lever two, below) reaches Vietnamese asset values through this same discount-rate channel, not only through the currency. A hawkish Fed raises the rate at which the world discounts Vietnamese cash flows. Keep that dual path in mind: global tightening hits Vietnam both through the dong *and* through the discount rate directly.

### The supply of credit: Vietnam's binding lever

Here is where Vietnam diverges from the textbook. In the United States, the Federal Reserve mostly works through *price* — it sets an interest rate and lets the banking system decide how much to lend at that price. The State Bank of Vietnam (the SBV, Vietnam's central bank) works through *price and quantity* both. Its most powerful tool is not a rate at all; it is the **credit growth quota**, often called "room" in Vietnamese financial press. Each year the SBV hands every commercial bank a ceiling on how much its loan book may grow — say 14% — and that ceiling is binding. A bank can have eager borrowers, ample deposits, and a healthy balance sheet, and *still* be unable to make another loan because it has used up its room.

Why does this matter so much? Because in an economy where companies fund themselves mostly through bank loans and bond issuance rather than equity, the *quantity* of credit available is the master variable for asset prices. When credit is flowing, developers build, buyers borrow, and property and bank stocks rise. When the quota is tight or the bond market is shut, the funding simply is not there, and asset values fall regardless of where the interest rate sits. The companion post [the SBV toolkit](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-sbv-toolkit-credit-quotas-rates-and-the-managed-dong) develops this in detail, and the legal scaffolding sits in [the SBV's monetary and banking law](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/sbv-monetary-and-banking-law-credit-quotas-and-the-dong). For our story, hold this: **in Vietnam, freezing the supply of credit is a more violent lever than raising its price.**

The reason a *quantity* lever is more violent than a *price* lever deserves a moment, because it is the single most important mechanical fact in this case study. When a central bank raises the price of credit — the interest rate — borrowing becomes more expensive, but it remains *possible*. A developer facing a higher rate can still refinance; it just pays more, and the discount-rate channel marks its equity down accordingly. The pain is continuous and bounded: a 200bp higher rate costs a knowable amount. But when a quantity lever slams shut — when the primary bond market closes and the banks are at their quota ceiling — refinancing becomes *impossible at any price*. The pain is discontinuous: a firm that was perfectly able to roll its debt at 12% one month finds that the next month there is simply no one to roll it with, at 12% or 20% or 30%. A price shock moves a firm down a smooth curve; a quantity shock pushes it off a cliff. Markets fear cliffs far more than slopes, which is why the 2022 funding freeze did damage out of all proportion to the 200bp of rate hikes that accompanied it.

There is also a feedback loop hiding in the quota system that amplified the 2022 shock. Banks are required to hold capital against their loans and to manage their bad-debt ratios. When property developers came under stress, banks grew worried about the loans they had *already* made to those developers — and a worried bank pulls back on *new* lending to protect its balance sheet, quite apart from any quota limit. So the credit supply tightened twice over: once because the quota was binding, and again because banks voluntarily retreated from a sector they suddenly feared. This is how a problem in one sector (property bonds) spreads to the whole credit system: the banks that funded the troubled sector tighten lending to *everyone*, and the quantity of credit available to the entire economy shrinks. The companion post [how policy moves credit spreads](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/how-policy-moves-credit-spreads-and-the-fed-put) develops this contagion mechanism in the language of credit markets.

### The currency: the managed dong and the FX band

The third lever is the exchange rate. Vietnam does not let the dong float freely against the dollar the way the US lets the dollar float against everything. Instead the SBV runs a **managed, crawling band**: it publishes a daily central reference rate for USD/VND and allows the market rate to move within roughly plus-or-minus five percent of it. The SBV defends that band with its foreign-exchange reserves — it *sells dollars and buys dong* when the dong is too weak, and *buys dollars* when the dong is too strong. This matters for asset prices in two ways. First, a weak dong imports inflation (everything Vietnam buys from abroad costs more dong), which can force the central bank to tighten. Second, defending the band costs reserves, and when reserves run low the central bank has to choose between letting the currency slide or raising domestic rates to make dong assets more attractive — exactly the bind Vietnam hit in 2022. The companion post [the currency channel](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-currency-channel-rate-differentials-and-carry) develops the mechanism; for now, hold that **defending the dong and defending asset prices can pull in opposite directions.**

The arithmetic of a managed band is worth making concrete, because it explains why a small economy cannot simply ignore the Fed. The reason capital wants to leave the dong when the Fed hikes is the **interest-rate differential**. Suppose a dollar deposit pays 5% and a dong deposit pays 6%. On its face the dong looks better — but a global investor holding dong bears the risk that the dong weakens against the dollar. If the market expects the dong to depreciate by, say, 3% over the year, then the *expected* dollar return from holding the dong is roughly 6% − 3% = 3%, which is *worse* than the 5% on a safe dollar deposit. So money flows out of dong and into dollars, pushing the dong down further — a self-reinforcing pressure. To stop it, the SBV must either raise the dong rate (widen the gap enough to compensate for the depreciation risk) or spend reserves to absorb the selling. In 2022, with the Fed hiking hard, the differential moved against the dong, the depreciation expectation grew, and the SBV was forced to do both. This is the precise channel by which a decision made in Washington compelled a rate hike in Hanoi.

A reserve war chest is the buffer that lets a central bank fight this pressure, but it is exhaustible, and the market watches the level. A central bank with \$100 billion of reserves can defend for a long time; one down to \$30 billion is vulnerable to a self-fulfilling run, because traders, seeing the reserves dwindle, bet against the currency *more* aggressively, accelerating the drain. This is why a reserve-spending FX defense is a race against time: every dollar sold buys a little stability now but brings the day of reckoning closer, and at some point the central bank must let the currency adjust or risk running the reserves to dangerous lows. Vietnam managed this in 2022 by combining reserve sales, a wider band, *and* the rate hikes — sharing the load across all three rather than relying on any one. But the episode is the textbook small-economy currency bind, and it is the same bind that, run to its catastrophic conclusion, produced the Asian financial crisis of 1997.

### The asset side: who gets hit

Two sectors dominate the VN-Index and both sit at the intersection of all three levers. **Property developers** borrow heavily — through bank loans and, crucially, through corporate bonds sold to the public — to fund land and construction, then sell apartments years later. They are leveraged claims on far-future cash flows: maximally sensitive to the discount rate *and* maximally dependent on the supply of credit. **Banks** are the plumbing: their earnings rise and fall with loan growth (governed by the quota), their stock trades on a multiple of book value that moves with the discount rate, and their balance sheets carry the credit risk of everyone else — including those property developers. So when policy tightens, property and banks lead the market down; when policy eases, they lead it back up. That is exactly the pattern we are about to watch.

With the machine assembled — discount rate, credit supply, currency, and the two sectors that feel all three — we can run the case study.

## The 2022 shock: four levers, one drawdown

The 2022 collapse was not a single event with a single cause. It was a *sequence* of policy shocks, each landing on an economy already weakened by the previous one, and the order matters. Let us take them in the order they arrived.

![Timeline of the dated 2022 tightening sequence from the April crackdown through the September and October hikes to the November low](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-policy-case-studies-2022-crackdown-to-2023-cuts-2.png)

The timeline above lays out the dated sequence. It begins in April with the bond crackdown, runs through the summer of imported Fed tightening, hits a second crisis in October with the Van Thinh Phat case, sees the SBV's emergency hikes in September and October, and bottoms in November with the VN-Index at 874. Five dated shocks; one drawdown. We will walk each.

### Lever one: the bond-market crackdown

The first and most damaging lever was a law-enforcement action, not a monetary one. For years, Vietnamese property developers had funded themselves by issuing **corporate bonds** — IOUs sold to investors, often ordinary retail savers, promising a fixed coupon and the return of principal at maturity. The market had grown enormous and, in places, abusive: bonds were sometimes sold through misleading prospectuses, secured by inflated collateral, or issued by shell entities. In April 2022, the government moved hard against the fraud. The high-profile **Tan Hoang Minh** case saw a major developer's bond deals cancelled and its chairman arrested over allegedly fraudulent issuance; the state cancelled bond lots worth trillions of dong. Then in October came the far larger **Van Thinh Phat / SCB** case — the arrest of the chair of a sprawling property group connected to Saigon Commercial Bank (SCB), in what became one of the largest fraud cases in the country's history, complete with a depositor scare at SCB that forced the SBV to step in and guarantee deposits.

The legal merits were real — this was a genuine clean-up of a market riddled with fraud, and the companion post [Vietnam's 2022 bond and property crackdown](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/vietnams-2022-bond-and-property-crackdown) covers the legal and regulatory story in full. But our concern is the *market* effect, and it was brutal and specific: the crackdown did not lower bond prices, it **shut the primary market**. New issuance collapsed as investors fled and regulators tightened the rules on who could issue and who could buy. And that is a far more dangerous thing than a price decline, because of how developers had been funding themselves.

![Pipeline showing why a developer with a maturing bond could not refinance when the primary market shut and was forced into asset sales or default](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-policy-case-studies-2022-crackdown-to-2023-cuts-4.png)

The figure above traces the trap. A developer that had financed long-dated projects with short-dated bonds was relying on being able to **roll** that debt — to issue a new bond and use the proceeds to repay the old one as it matured. That model works only as long as the primary market is open. When the crackdown shut it, a developer with a bond maturing in late 2022 suddenly had no way to refinance. It could not issue a new bond (no buyers). It could not lean on the banks, because the banks were capped by their credit quota and were themselves nervous about property exposure. So it was left with bad options: sell assets into a falling market (a fire sale), halt projects, or default. The fear that any given developer might be next is what repriced the *entire* property sector, solvent and insolvent alike — the market could not tell which developers had refinancing cliffs, so it discounted all of them. This is the funding-freeze channel, and it is the heart of the 2022 shock.

#### Worked example: a developer rolling a maturing bond when the primary market shuts

Take a mid-size developer with a corporate bond of **2,000 billion dong** (2 trillion VND) maturing in November 2022. At the period-end 2022 exchange rate of about 23,633 dong per dollar, that is roughly **\$84.6 million** of debt coming due.

- **The plan (normal times):** issue a new 2,000-billion-dong bond at, say, a 12% coupon, use the proceeds to repay the maturing bond, and carry on. The developer's projects are years from completion, so it never intended to repay out of cash flow — it intended to roll.
- **The reality (late 2022):** the primary market is shut. There are no buyers for a new property-developer bond at *any* coupon the developer can afford. The banks cannot lend — they are at their quota ceiling and wary of property. The developer must find 2,000 billion dong (\$84.6 million) in cash within weeks.
- **The forced choice:** it sells a land parcel or a partially built project at a distressed price — perhaps recovering 60 cents on the dong of its carrying value — or it asks bondholders to accept a restructuring (a maturity extension, sometimes a swap into apartments), or it defaults.

Notice what happened to the *equity*. Nothing about the buildings changed; the apartments are still there, the land is still there. What changed was the developer's access to funding, and that alone can wipe out the equity, because a leveraged firm that cannot refinance is worth far less than the same firm that can. *The crackdown destroyed property-developer equity not by lowering the value of their assets but by removing their ability to fund those assets — a pure funding-freeze repricing.*

### Lever two: imported global tightening

The second lever came from outside Vietnam entirely. Through 2022 the US Federal Reserve was running the fastest hiking cycle since Volcker — lifting its policy rate from near zero to a 4.50% upper bound by December (and on to 5.50% by July 2023). When the Fed raises rates, the dollar strengthens against almost everything, because dollar deposits suddenly pay much more. For a small open economy pegged loosely to the dollar, this is an imported tightening: to keep the dong from collapsing against a surging dollar, the SBV had to either spend reserves, raise its own rates, or both. The companion post [how global policy reaches Vietnam](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/how-global-policy-reaches-vietnam-the-fed-the-dollar-and-tariffs) develops this channel; the short version is that **a hawkish Fed exports tightening to the whole world, and Vietnam cannot opt out.** This is the second arrow into the VN-Index drop in our cover figure.

It is worth being precise about the *two distinct paths* by which the Fed's 2022 hiking reached Vietnamese asset values, because it is easy to collapse them into one. The first path is the currency, as just described: a stronger dollar pressures the dong, forcing the SBV toward reserve sales and rate hikes. The second path, which we flagged in the Foundations, is the discount rate directly: when US Treasury yields rose and the global risk-free rate climbed, the rate at which international investors discount *all* emerging-market cash flows rose with it, and the country-risk premium on a frontier market like Vietnam tends to widen in exactly these "risk-off" episodes. So even before the SBV raised a single domestic rate, the fair value of Vietnamese stocks had already been marked down by the rise in the global discount rate — and foreign investors, who are meaningful marginal buyers and sellers on the Ho Chi Minh exchange, sold emerging-market equities broadly as US rates rose, adding direct selling pressure to the discount-rate repricing. The 2022 drawdown was thus partly a *global* repricing of all emerging markets, not solely a Vietnamese story; the domestic crackdown then made Vietnam's version far worse than the regional average. Distinguishing the global component from the domestic one matters for the playbook: the global component reverses when the Fed pivots, while the domestic component (the funding freeze) only reverses when *Vietnamese* policy eases — which, as we will see, came first.

### Lever three: the FX defense

The first two levers collided in the currency market. The dollar was surging (Fed), and Vietnamese capital was nervous (crackdown), so the dong came under sustained downward pressure through 2022. The SBV defended its band the only ways it could: it sold dollars from reserves to buy dong, and — when that proved insufficient — it widened the trading band and let the central rate crawl weaker. The dong still ended 2022 down meaningfully against the dollar.

![Line chart of USD/VND period-end from 2020 to 2025 showing the managed crawl weaker with the 2022 FX-defense stress window highlighted](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-policy-case-studies-2022-crackdown-to-2023-cuts-5.png)

The chart traces USD/VND at year-end from 2020 to 2025. The dong was steady — even slightly stronger — through 2020-21 (one dollar bought about 23,100 dong, then 22,790). Then the 2022 stress hit: the year-end rate weakened to about 23,633, and the pressure continued for years afterward as the rate gap with the Fed stayed wide, reaching about 26,100 by end-2025. The annotation box anchors the magnitude for a saver: 100 million dong, worth about \$4,329 at the start of 2020, was worth only about \$3,831 by end-2025 — the slow tax a managed devaluation levies on dong savings.

#### Worked example: the FX defense and the reserve cost

Suppose at the height of the 2022 pressure the SBV wants to hold the dong at 23,500 against a market that wants to push it to 24,000. To do that it must stand in the market and *sell dollars, buy dong* — soaking up the selling pressure with its reserves.

- Say the SBV sells **\$1 billion** of reserves to defend the rate over a stressful month. At 23,500 dong per dollar, that billion dollars buys back **23,500 billion dong** (23.5 trillion VND) and takes it out of circulation, supporting the currency.
- But reserves are finite. If Vietnam holds, say, \$100 billion of reserves, then every \$1 billion spent is 1% of the war chest. Spend \$20 billion defending the band over a few stressful months and you have burned a fifth of your reserves — and the market knows it, which can make the run worse.
- The alternative to spending reserves is to **raise domestic rates**, making dong deposits pay enough more than dollar deposits that holders are willing to stay in dong. This is cheaper in reserves but it tightens domestic financial conditions — exactly when the bond crackdown has already frozen credit.

Here is the bind in one sentence: *defending the dong with reserves is finite, and defending it with higher rates deepens the domestic credit squeeze — so the FX defense forced the SBV toward the very rate hikes that hurt the stock market.* The currency lever and the rate lever were not independent; the first compelled the second.

### Lever four: the SBV's emergency hikes

So we arrive at the fourth lever, which the previous three made nearly unavoidable. In September and October 2022, facing a surging dollar, a pressured dong, and dwindling room to spend reserves, the SBV raised its policy rates by a total of **200 basis points** — two 100bp moves in quick succession. The refinancing rate (the SBV's benchmark policy rate, the rate at which it lends to commercial banks) went from 4.0% to 6.0%.

![Step chart of the SBV refinancing rate showing the plus 200 basis point hikes in 2022 followed by minus 150 basis points of cuts in 2023](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-policy-case-studies-2022-crackdown-to-2023-cuts-6.png)

The step chart shows the full rate path that frames the whole case study. On the left, the red zone: the refinancing rate held at 4.0%, then jumped to 5.0% and 6.0% in the September-October 2022 emergency hikes — a defensive tightening, taken mainly to protect the dong rather than to cool a domestic boom. On the right, the green zone is 2023, which we will come to. For now, focus on the spike: a +200bp hike, arriving on top of a frozen bond market and a falling currency, was the final weight on the VN-Index. Higher policy rates lifted the discount rate that prices every stock *and* raised the cost of the credit that was already scarce. The fourth arrow had landed.

### The result: the VN-Index falls 33%

Four levers — crackdown, imported tightening, FX defense, emergency hikes — all landing in the same nine months. The result was one of the worst equity-market years in Asia.

![Line chart of the VN-Index through 2022 falling from a high of 1528 to a low of 874 with the September and October SBV hikes marked](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-policy-case-studies-2022-crackdown-to-2023-cuts-3.png)

The chart traces the VN-Index through 2022. From the April high of about 1,528, the index slid through the summer, rallied weakly in August, then fell off a cliff through September, October, and into November as the Van Thinh Phat case broke and the hikes landed, reaching a low of about **874** in November — a peak-to-trough drawdown of about 42.8%. It recovered into year-end to close around 1,007, leaving the full-year return at about **-32.8%**, the headline number for 2022. The shaded amber band marks the September-October hikes, which coincide with the steepest leg of the fall. Property and bank stocks led the decline; many individual developer stocks fell far more than the index. This is the tightening machine at full force: every lever pushing the same way, every asset repricing down together.

#### Worked example: a 100-million-dong portfolio through the -33% year

Put yourself in the shoes of a Vietnamese retail investor who started 2022 with **100 million dong** (100M VND) in a broad portfolio of VN-Index stocks — a very common saver's position. At the start of 2022, with the dong near 23,100 per dollar, that 100 million dong was worth about **\$4,329**.

- **The full-year drawdown:** the VN-Index fell about 32.8% over 2022. A portfolio tracking the index ends the year at roughly 100M × (1 − 0.328) = **67.2 million dong**.
- **The currency on top:** the dong also weakened, from about 23,100 to 23,633 per dollar. So the 67.2 million dong is now worth 67,200,000 ÷ 23,633 ≈ **\$2,843**.
- **The total loss in dollars:** \$4,329 → \$2,843, a decline of about **34%** in dollar terms — slightly worse than the dong loss, because the currency depreciation compounds the equity loss for anyone measuring in dollars.
- **The peak-to-trough pain:** if the investor had bought at the April high and panic-sold at the November low, they would have realized the full -42.8% index drawdown — turning 100 million dong into roughly **57 million dong** before the dong adjustment.

*A single year of tightening policy — most of it not even aimed at the stock market — cut a typical Vietnamese equity saver's wealth by a third in dong and by more than a third in dollars.* That is the cost of being on the wrong side of a four-lever tightening, and it explains why the 2023 pivot, when it came, mattered so much.

## The 2023 pivot: easing ahead of the Fed

Now the machine runs backward. By early 2023 the picture had changed. Vietnamese inflation, which had been a worry in 2022, was coming back down. Growth was slowing sharply — the export sector, on which Vietnam depends, was weakening as global demand cooled, and the frozen property sector was a drag on the whole economy. The SBV faced a different problem than in 2022: not a currency under acute attack, but an economy that needed relief. And it made a decision that is genuinely rare for a small open economy: **it started cutting rates while the Fed was still raising them.**

### Why easing ahead of the Fed is hard — and what it bought

To see why this is bold, recall the bind from 2022. A small economy that cuts its rates while the US is hiking *widens the rate gap against the dollar*, which tends to weaken its currency. Lower dong rates make dong deposits less attractive relative to dollar deposits, so capital leans toward leaving, and the dong faces renewed pressure. Most emerging-market central banks, fearing exactly this, wait for the Fed to pivot first and only then ease — they import the Fed's stance because they fear the currency consequences of diverging from it.

The SBV chose differently. It judged that the domestic need — reviving credit, supporting the property sector, cushioning growth — outweighed the currency risk, and it cut. This is the green zone in the rate-path chart above: four cuts to the refinancing rate between March and June 2023, taking it from 6.0% back down to **4.5%** (in 50bp steps, effective in early April, late May, and mid-June, with the discount rate cut earlier in March). The SBV bought domestic relief and accepted that the dong would keep crawling weaker — which, as the USD/VND chart showed, it did. This is the defining choice of the case study: *a small economy used its own policy autonomy to ease into a slowdown rather than wait for permission from Washington, paying for it in currency depreciation.*

![Step chart comparing the US Fed funds upper bound rising through 2023 against the SBV refinancing rate falling with the 2023 divergence window highlighted](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-policy-case-studies-2022-crackdown-to-2023-cuts-8.png)

The figure above is the single most important picture in the post, because it shows the divergence directly. The grey staircase is the Fed funds upper bound, climbing relentlessly through 2022 and into 2023 to its 5.50% peak in July. The blue line is the SBV refinancing rate: it *also* rose in late 2022 (the emergency hikes), but then — in the amber-shaded window of 2023 H1 — it turned and fell while the Fed was still climbing. Two central banks, moving in opposite directions at the same moment. The Fed was fighting US inflation; the SBV was reviving the Vietnamese economy. That a small, trade-dependent economy could run a genuinely independent rate policy, even at the cost of a weaker currency, is the lesson of 2023. For the deeper mechanics of how rate cuts transmit to markets, the macro-trading companion [monetary-policy transmission](/blog/trading/macro-trading/monetary-policy-transmission-how-rate-changes-reach-markets) builds the channels from scratch.

### How the cut reached property and bank stocks

A rate cut reaches a stock through the same channels the hike used, in reverse. Lower the policy rate and you lower the discount rate, which lifts the present value of every future cash flow — the multiple expands. Lower the policy rate and, in Vietnam's quantity-driven system, you also pair it with looser credit conditions: the SBV raised banks' credit quotas through 2023, and a developer that could not refinance at any price in late 2022 could, by mid-2023, restructure its bonds and access bank credit again. So the cut hits property and bank stocks *twice*: once through the discount rate (the multiple), and once through the credit supply (the earnings).

![Before and after diagram showing how the 2023 cut re-rated a bank stock by lowering the discount rate and raising the credit quota](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-policy-case-studies-2022-crackdown-to-2023-cuts-7.png)

The before-and-after figure isolates the bank case. On the left, at 6.0% (early 2023): a high discount rate compresses the price-to-book multiple, the tight credit quota holds loan growth flat, and funding stress keeps bad-debt fears high — fair value is anchored low. On the right, at 4.5% (mid-2023): the lower discount rate lets the multiple re-rate up, the raised quota lets loan growth resume, and easier funding lowers provisioning — fair value lifts. The same rate cut moves both the multiple *and* the earnings, which is why the most rate-sensitive, credit-sensitive sectors (property and banks) led the recovery, just as they had led the fall. The companion post on [how policy prices real estate](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/how-policy-prices-real-estate-cap-rates-and-mortgages) develops the cap-rate version of this for property specifically.

#### Worked example: the 2023 cut transmitted to a bank stock's valuation

Take a Vietnamese bank stock and value it with the simplest professional shorthand: a bank trades at a multiple of its **book value per share** (its net assets per share), and that multiple, the **price-to-book (P/B)** ratio, depends on the discount rate and on how fast the bank can grow its (profitable) book. Suppose the bank has a book value of **20,000 dong per share** (about \$0.85 at the 2023 rate).

- **At 6.0% (early 2023):** with a high discount rate and a frozen credit quota, the market assigns the stock a depressed P/B of, say, **1.0×**. The stock trades near book: 1.0 × 20,000 = **20,000 dong** (about \$0.85).
- **At 4.5% (mid-2023):** the cut does two things. It lowers the discount rate, which alone lifts the fair multiple — for a long-duration asset like a growing bank, a 150bp lower discount rate is worth a meaningful multiple expansion. And it loosens the quota, so the market now believes the bank can grow its profitable loan book again, lifting expected earnings. Together, the market re-rates the stock to a P/B of, say, **1.25×**: 1.25 × 20,000 = **25,000 dong** (about \$1.06).
- **The repricing:** 20,000 → 25,000 dong is a **+25%** move in the stock, driven entirely by the policy cut — not by any change in the bank's current assets, which are unchanged at 20,000 dong of book per share. The cut changed the *rate* the market uses to value those assets, and the *growth* it expects from them.

*A 150bp policy cut lifted a bank stock about 25% by re-rating its multiple and reviving its earnings outlook — the mirror image of the 2022 collapse, where the same two channels in reverse had crushed it.* Multiply this across the bank-heavy, property-heavy VN-Index and you get the 2023-24 recovery.

### The recovery, with numbers

The rate cut was not the only easing lever in 2023; it was paired with two others that mattered just as much for the funding freeze specifically. First, the SBV **raised banks' credit quotas** through the year, loosening the quantity lever that had been binding — banks that had hit their ceiling in 2022 were given room to lend again, and crucially, room to lend *to the property sector* that needed refinancing. Second, the government issued decrees aimed directly at the bond-market cliff. The most important, a decree on corporate bonds issued in early 2023, gave issuers a legal pathway to **negotiate maturity extensions** with bondholders (typically up to two years) and to **repay bonds with other assets**, such as swapping a maturing bond for apartments in the developer's own projects. In plain terms, the state gave developers facing a refinancing cliff a way to *not* default — to push the maturity wall out by two years and buy time for the market to reopen and for the rate cuts to revive demand. This is the funding-freeze channel being deliberately reversed by policy: the same government that shut the primary market to clean up fraud then built a controlled off-ramp so the clean-up would not trigger a cascade of defaults.

The market recovered as these levers combined. From the November 2022 low of about 874, the VN-Index climbed through 2023 and 2024 as the rate cuts re-rated multiples, the quota loosening revived loan growth, and the maturity-extension decree let the property sector restructure rather than default. It was not a straight line — the recovery had setbacks, several large developers still went through painful restructurings, and the property sector's clean-up took years — but the direction was unmistakable: the easing reversed the channel, and asset values followed. The order of recovery mirrored the order of the fall: banks and the better-capitalized developers re-rated first (they could most credibly access the reopened credit), while the weakest developers, whose problem had tipped from funding toward genuine solvency, lagged or were restructured out. That divergence is itself the lesson of the playbook below — an easing reverses a *funding* freeze cleanly but cannot resurrect a firm whose assets are worth less than its debts.

The dong, meanwhile, kept crawling weaker, as the USD/VND chart showed, reaching about 24,260 at end-2023 and onward to about 26,100 by end-2025 — the price Vietnam paid, in currency terms, for easing ahead of the Fed and for the persistent rate gap that followed. It is worth stating plainly that this was a *choice*, and a defensible one: a managed, gradual depreciation of a few percent a year is a far cheaper price than the alternative of holding rates high to defend the currency and choking off the recovery the economy needed. The SBV traded a slowly weaker dong for a reviving domestic economy, and given the slowing growth and the frozen property sector it inherited from 2022, that was very likely the right trade.

## Common misconceptions

A cycle this fast breeds tidy but wrong stories. Five corrections, each with a number.

**Misconception 1: "The 2022 crash was caused by the rate hikes."** The +200bp hikes were real and they hurt, but they were the *fourth* lever, not the first, and arguably not the most damaging. The bond-market freeze did the deepest damage by shutting off developers' refinancing entirely — a quantity shock, not a price shock. A developer can survive a higher interest rate; it cannot survive having *no* access to funding when its bond matures. The hikes pushed the discount rate up by 200bp; the freeze pushed the supply of developer credit toward *zero*. The second is worse.

**Misconception 2: "The crackdown was a policy mistake that crashed the market."** The crackdown crashed the market in the short run, but it was a clean-up of a genuinely fraudulent corner of the bond market — the Van Thinh Phat case alone involved one of the largest frauds in the country's history. Conflating "this hurt asset prices" with "this was wrong" is exactly the error this series warns against: policy that is *correct* (rooting out fraud, protecting savers long-term) can still *reprice assets down hard* in the near term. The transmission is mechanical and indifferent to whether the policy was wise.

**Misconception 3: "Vietnam just followed the Fed."** In 2022, largely yes — the FX defense forced the SBV to hike alongside a hawkish Fed. But in 2023 it conspicuously did *not* follow: it cut from 6.0% to 4.5% while the Fed was still hiking to 5.50%. The divergence chart makes this undeniable. A small open economy *can* run an independent rate policy; the cost is currency depreciation, which Vietnam accepted.

**Misconception 4: "A weaker dong is always bad for Vietnamese stocks."** It is bad for a dollar-based investor and it imports inflation, but a managed, gradual depreciation also *supports* Vietnam's export-driven economy by keeping its goods competitively priced abroad. The dong fell steadily from 2022 through 2025 (23,100 → 26,100 per dollar) *while* the VN-Index recovered. The currency and the equity market are not rigidly linked; a slow crawl weaker is the price of monetary autonomy, not a verdict on stocks.

**Misconception 5: "The recovery proves the crisis was overdone."** The recovery proves the *policy reversed*, which reversed the channel — not that the 2022 fall was irrational. At the November 2022 low, the funding freeze was real, refinancing cliffs were real, and the risk of a cascade of developer defaults was real. The market priced a genuine danger; the danger then receded because policy eased and the government gave developers room to restructure. Both the fall and the recovery were policy-driven, and both were rational responses to the policy in force at the time.

## Case studies: the cycle in three dated snapshots

We have walked the levers; let us now pin three dated moments where you can see the machine most clearly, each with its real numbers.

### Snapshot one: April 2022 — the top, and the first crack

In April 2022 the VN-Index was at its high of about **1,528**. The same month, the Tan Hoang Minh bond deals were cancelled and its chairman arrested — the first crack in the funding edifice. At this moment the four levers had barely begun: the Fed had only just started hiking (its first move was March 2022), the dong was still strong (about 22,800-23,000 per dollar), and the SBV's refinancing rate was still 4.0%. The market did not yet understand what the bond crackdown would do to developer refinancing. *April 2022 is the lesson that a market top often coincides with the first, easily-dismissed policy crack — the danger is in the funding plumbing, not yet in the price.*

### Snapshot two: November 2022 — the bottom, all four levers at maximum

By November 2022 every lever was at maximum force. The Fed had taken its rate to 4.00% (on the way to 4.50% in December). The dong had been pushed to about 23,600 per dollar despite the SBV's reserve sales. The refinancing rate was at 6.0% after the +200bp hikes. And the Van Thinh Phat / SCB case had just broken, triggering a depositor scare that forced the SBV to guarantee SCB's deposits. The VN-Index hit **874** — down 42.8% from the April high. This is the machine at full tightening: four levers, all pushing down, the discount rate up and the credit supply frozen at once. *November 2022 is the textbook bottom — maximum policy pressure, maximum fear, the point of peak danger that, in hindsight, was also the point of maximum opportunity for anyone who could see the pivot coming.*

### Snapshot three: mid-2023 — the pivot, and the divergence

By June 2023 the SBV had completed its four cuts, taking the refinancing rate to **4.5%** — while the Fed was simultaneously hiking to its 5.50% peak in July. This is the divergence, dated and undeniable. The cuts re-rated the property and bank stocks that had led the fall; the VN-Index was well off its lows and climbing; and the property sector was beginning a multi-year restructuring under government decrees that let developers extend bond maturities and swap bonds for assets. The dong, meanwhile, kept weakening (to about 24,260 by year-end), the accepted cost of easing ahead of the Fed. *Mid-2023 is the rarest event in the cycle: a small economy buying its own relief, on its own schedule, by diverging from the world's central bank — and it worked, at the price of a weaker currency.*

### Putting the three snapshots together

Read in sequence, the three dated snapshots are the whole machine: April 2022, the top and the first funding crack; November 2022, the bottom with all four tightening levers at maximum; mid-2023, the easing pivot and the divergence from the Fed. The VN-Index went 1,528 → 874 → recovering, and at each turn the *cause* was a policy lever reaching an asset through a channel — the discount rate, the supply of credit, or the currency. Nothing about the buildings, the banks' branches, or the apartments changed at the speed the index moved. What changed was policy, and policy is the master input.

## What it means for asset values: the playbook

So what is the durable, transferable lesson — the part you can carry to the *next* Vietnamese cycle, or to any emerging market running the same machine?

**The funding freeze is the lever to fear most.** In a bank-and-bond-financed economy, the quantity of credit matters more than its price. Watch the *primary market* — can companies still issue bonds and access bank loans? — more than you watch the policy rate. A frozen primary market with developers facing refinancing cliffs is a far more dangerous signal than a couple of rate hikes. The 2022 collapse was a quantity shock dressed up as a rate cycle. The signal to watch: corporate-bond issuance volumes and the SBV's credit-quota decisions, not just the refinancing rate.

**The most rate-sensitive, credit-sensitive sectors lead in both directions.** Property and banks led the VN-Index down in 2022 and led it back up in 2023, because they sit at the intersection of all three levers — maximum discount-rate sensitivity, maximum credit dependence. They are the amplifier of the policy cycle. In a tightening, they fall hardest; in an easing, they re-rate fastest. The ~25% move in our worked bank example is the mechanism.

**A small economy's pivot can come before the Fed's — watch the domestic data, not just Washington.** The 2023 SBV cuts are the proof. An emerging-market central bank with enough reserves and enough domestic need can ease ahead of the Fed, accepting currency weakness as the cost. So do not assume the local central bank will simply wait for the Fed. Watch the *domestic* triggers — slowing growth, falling local inflation, a frozen credit sector that needs reviving — because those, not the Fed's dot plot, drive a small economy's pivot. The signal: when local growth and inflation roll over together, the local central bank may diverge.

**Price the currency cost into the equity recovery.** The 2023-24 equity recovery was real, but a dollar-based investor earned less than the index did, because the dong fell the whole time (23,100 → 26,100 per dollar from 2020 to 2025). Easing ahead of the Fed *means* a weaker currency; that is the trade-off, not a bug. When you measure a recovery in an easing-ahead-of-the-Fed economy, always convert to your home currency — the local index can rise while your dollar return lags.

**What would invalidate this read.** The pattern — tightening levers down, easing levers up, property and banks leading both ways — holds as long as the transmission machine is intact. It would break if the SBV lost the ability to ease (for instance, if a renewed currency crisis forced it to defend the dong at all costs, as nearly happened in 2022), or if a fraud clean-up went so deep that the property sector's *solvency*, not just its *funding*, was in question. In 2022 the danger was a funding freeze (a liquidity problem, curable by reopening credit); had it tipped into mass insolvency (a solvency problem), the recovery would have looked very different. The signal that the read is breaking: developer *defaults* rising even after credit reopens, which would mean the problem was solvency, not liquidity, all along.

**The transferable shape.** Strip away the Vietnamese specifics and the 2022-23 cycle is a template that recurs across emerging markets: a domestic policy shock (here, a bond-fraud crackdown) collides with an imported global tightening (here, the Fed), the currency comes under pressure, the central bank is forced to tighten defensively, and the most leveraged, longest-duration assets — property and banks — lead a sharp drawdown. Then, when domestic conditions soften, the central bank eases (sometimes ahead of the Fed, if it has the reserves and the nerve), and the same assets lead the recovery, with the currency paying the bill. You will see this shape again — in Vietnam's next cycle, and in any small open economy that finances itself through banks and bonds. The numbers will differ; the machine will not. When you can name the lever, trace it to the channel, and price the asset at the end, you are no longer surprised by the round trip — you are reading it as it happens.

The deepest lesson is the one this whole series turns on. A government cleaning up bond fraud, a central bank defending its currency, the same central bank later reviving its economy — none of these decisions were "about" the stock market. Yet each one reached into the present-value formula behind every Vietnamese asset and turned a dial: the discount rate, the supply of credit, the currency. The VN-Index's violent 18-month round trip was the sum of those turns. Learn to see the levers, trace them to the channels, and you can read the value of the assets — which is, in the end, the only thing the policy machine was ever doing.
