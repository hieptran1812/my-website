> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — In July 2012 the euro was pricing its own break-up, and one man stopped it with a sentence. Mario Draghi promised the European Central Bank would do "whatever it takes," backed the promise with an unlimited bond-buying program (OMT), and watched peripheral yields collapse — without ever buying a single bond. The lever was a credible promise; the channel was belief; the asset that repriced was the price of money across a quarter of the world economy.
>
> - A peripheral government's borrowing cost is not one risk. It is the safe rate **plus** a real-default risk **plus** a *redenomination premium* — the extra yield demanded for the chance that the euros you are owed come back as devalued pesetas or lire. In July 2012 that fear leg alone was worth roughly 4.8 percentage points of Spain's 7.6% yield.
> - A credible *unlimited* backstop does something no finite amount of bond-buying can: it makes the bad outcome **impossible**, so the fear that justified the high yield simply evaporates. OMT was designed to delete the bad equilibrium, not to fund the periphery.
> - The proof is that **OMT was never used — not once.** And yet Spain's 10-year yield fell from 7.6% to 1.6% by 2014, below what the United States paid at the time. The cheapest policy tool in history moved a benchmark yield 600 basis points for zero euros.
> - The one number to remember: **7.6% to 1.6%, and €0 spent.** A credible promise is the most powerful and cheapest instrument a central bank owns — which is also why losing credibility is the most expensive thing it can do.

On the morning of 26 July 2012, the bond market had reached a verdict on the euro, and the verdict was death. Spain was paying 7.6% to borrow for ten years. Italy was paying 6.6%. Those are not the borrowing costs of solvent, G7-sized economies — they are the yields a market charges when it has started to price, seriously, the possibility that the borrower defaults or walks away from the currency altogether. A self-reinforcing spiral was tightening its grip: higher yields made each country's debt look less sustainable, which justified still-higher yields, which made the debt look even worse. Capital was fleeing the periphery for the safety of German bunds, and every euro that fled made the fear it was fleeing more real.

Then Mario Draghi, the president of the ECB, stood up at an investment conference in London and said nineteen words, of which three did all the work. *"Within our mandate, the ECB is ready to do whatever it takes to preserve the euro. And believe me, it will be enough."* He bought nothing. He committed no specific sum. He did not even announce a program — the actual mechanism, Outright Monetary Transactions, would not be unveiled until September. And over the entire decade that followed, OMT was **never used, not a single time.** Yet Spanish and Italian yields began to fall that very afternoon and kept falling for two years, until Spain was paying 1.6% — a yield *below* the United States Treasury's.

![Whatever it takes mechanism: a credible backstop kills the redenomination premium and reverses the doom loop](/imgs/blogs/draghi-2012-whatever-it-takes-and-the-spread-machine-1.png)

This post is the definitive case study of the credibility channel — the strangest and most powerful of the four ways policy reaches asset prices. In every other channel the central bank *does* something physical: it cuts a rate, it buys a bond, it sells a currency. The action is the cause; the price move is the effect. Here there is no action. The cause is a change in *belief*, and the bank's only tool is its own accumulated credibility, spendable in an instant. We will take the magic apart and show you the gears: how a sovereign yield decomposes into a piece you can negotiate away with words, why an *unlimited* promise is categorically different from a large one, how the bank-sovereign "doom loop" turned a solvency scare into a self-fulfilling prophecy, and why a promise that was never kept — because it never had to be — remains the single most cost-effective intervention any central bank has ever made.

We have built the general *theory* of this channel in [the expectations channel: forward guidance and credibility](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-expectations-channel-forward-guidance-and-credibility). This post is its laboratory: one event, dated to the day, with the numbers fully worked. If the theory post tells you *why* words can be a policy lever, this one shows you the cleanest example in the historical record of words being one.

## Foundations: what a sovereign yield is actually made of

Start from zero, because the whole story lives inside one number — the yield on a government bond — and most people treat that number as a single thing when it is in fact a sum of several very different fears.

A **bond** is a loan you make to a borrower in tradeable form. You hand over money today; the borrower promises to pay you a fixed stream of *coupons* (interest) each year and to return your *principal* (the face value, usually \$100 or €100) at *maturity* (the end date). The **yield to maturity** is the single interest rate that makes the present value of all those promised payments equal to the bond's current market price. When the price falls, the yield rises, and vice versa — they are two ways of saying the same thing. A government that has to offer a 7.6% yield to find buyers is a government whose bonds are *cheap*, because few people want to hold them at any higher price.

Now the crucial decomposition. The yield a *risky* government pays is not arbitrary. It is built up from layers, each one a distinct compensation for a distinct fear:

1. **The risk-free rate.** What a lender would charge a borrower it considered completely safe, for the same maturity. In the euro area in 2012 the benchmark for "safe" was the German bund — Germany was the country no one believed would default or leave. Call this leg ~1.3% for a 10-year bund in mid-2012.
2. **The credit / fiscal-default premium.** The extra yield demanded for the genuine, ordinary risk that *this* borrower might not pay back what it owes, in the currency it owes it in — too much debt, too little growth, a budget that does not close. For Spain in 2012, a real but containable risk: call it ~1.5%.
3. **The redenomination premium.** This is the leg that made the euro crisis unique, and it is the leg this entire post is about. It is the extra yield a lender demands for the risk that the euros it is owed get paid back in a *different, devalued currency* — that Spain leaves the euro, brings back the peseta, and redenominates its debt into a currency worth far less. You are not being paid late or paid less in euros; you are being paid in something else entirely. In July 2012 the market was pricing this fear at roughly 4.8 percentage points of Spain's yield.

Add them up — 1.3% + 1.5% + 4.8% — and you get the 7.6% Spain actually paid. The first two legs are about whether Spain can *pay*. The third is about whether the euro *survives*. And the third leg is the one that can be moved by belief alone, because it is not a fact about Spain's finances — it is a fact about what everyone *expects everyone else to do*.

![Decomposing the Spanish 10-year into risk-free, credit, and redenomination legs before and after](/imgs/blogs/draghi-2012-whatever-it-takes-and-the-spread-machine-5.png)

### Why the third leg is special: self-fulfilling fear

Here is the property that makes a redenomination premium so dangerous, and so different from ordinary default risk. **It is self-fulfilling.** Suppose enough investors come to believe Spain will leave the euro. They sell Spanish bonds. Yields spike. High yields make Spain's debt genuinely harder to service — every refinancing happens at a punishing rate — which makes a default-or-exit *more* likely, which justifies the selling. The fear creates the conditions that vindicate the fear. There is no anchor pulling the system back to calm; left alone, it spirals.

Economists call a system like this one with **multiple equilibria.** Given the *same* underlying fundamentals — the same Spanish economy, the same debt load — the market can settle into a "good" outcome (everyone believes Spain stays, yields are low, the debt is serviceable, and so Spain does stay) or a "bad" outcome (everyone believes Spain leaves, yields are crushing, the debt becomes unserviceable, and so Spain is pushed toward leaving). Which one you get depends not on the fundamentals but on the *belief*. The economy did not change between the good and bad equilibrium. The story everyone told about it did.

This is the deepest reason the euro crisis could be ended with words. If the high yields had been pure, fundamentals-driven default risk — if Spain were simply, objectively insolvent — no speech could fix it; only money or default could. But a *self-fulfilling* premium is, by definition, not anchored in fundamentals. It is anchored in expectations. And expectations are exactly what a credible central bank can move. The problem was never that Spain could not pay. The problem was that everyone feared everyone else feared Spain could not pay. Break that loop and the premium vanishes — not because anything real changed, but because the fear that created it was always, in part, a prophecy that fulfilled itself.

![Two equilibria matrix: a credible backstop deletes the bad self-fulfilling outcome](/imgs/blogs/draghi-2012-whatever-it-takes-and-the-spread-machine-7.png)

### Why the euro made this premium possible at all

It is worth pausing on *why* a redenomination premium can exist in the euro area but not, say, in the United States or Japan, because the answer is the whole reason the crisis was structural rather than merely cyclical. The difference is who issues the currency you owe.

When the US government borrows, it borrows in dollars — a currency the Federal Reserve, an arm of the US state, can create without limit. A holder of a US Treasury bond therefore faces essentially no *redenomination* risk and no *forced-default* risk: in the absolute last resort, the Fed can always create the dollars to pay. (A US default is conceivable only as a *political* choice — a debt-ceiling standoff — never as a financial impossibility.) The same is true of the UK with sterling and Japan with the yen. These are *monetary sovereigns*: they print the money they owe.

A euro-area member is not a monetary sovereign. Spain borrows in euros, but euros are issued by the ECB in Frankfurt, an institution Spain does not control and which, in 2012, had no mandate and (the market feared) no willingness to act as the buyer of last resort for any single member's debt. So Spain could *run out of euros* the way a household runs out of cash — it had to find willing buyers for its bonds at every refinancing, and if buyers demanded a yield Spain could not afford, there was no domestic central bank standing behind it to break the impasse. That single structural fact — a sovereign borrowing in a currency it cannot print — is what made redenomination thinkable. If buyers vanished, the only way for Spain to pay its bills might be to leave the euro, reintroduce a national currency it *could* print, and pay its debts in that. The mere *possibility* of that escape route is what the redenomination premium prices. Monetary sovereigns do not have the escape route, so their bonds do not carry the premium. The euro's founders had created a currency union without a unified fiscal backstop, and the bond market, in 2012, finally noticed the gap.

This is also why Draghi's promise was so precisely the right key for this specific lock. The ECB *is* the monetary sovereign for the euro as a whole — it can create euros without limit. What it had refused, until 2012, was to *use* that power on behalf of any single distressed member. "Whatever it takes" was, in essence, the ECB announcing it would finally stand behind the periphery the way the Fed stands behind every US state. It supplied the missing monetary backstop the euro had been built without — and the moment it did, the escape-route fear that the missing backstop had created lost its reason to exist.

### How a yield becomes a price, mechanically

One more foundation, because the worked examples below lean on it. People say "yields fell" as if a yield were a thing that moves on its own, but a yield is a *consequence* of a price, and prices move because buyers and sellers move. Walk the actual market mechanics.

A bond's promised payments are fixed at issue: the 5.5% coupon and the €100 principal do not change. What changes is the *price* people will pay for that fixed stream, and the yield is just the rate that reconciles the two. When fear rises and investors dump Spanish bonds, the *price* falls — sellers accept less and less to get out. As the price falls, the same fixed coupons represent a higher and higher percentage return to whoever buys at the new low price: that higher implied return *is* the rising yield. So "the yield rose to 7.6%" and "the price fell to about €86" are not two events; they are one event described two ways. The market did not set a yield; it set a price by selling, and the yield fell out of the arithmetic.

This matters for understanding Draghi because it locates *where* the action happens. The ECB cannot decree a yield. What a credible backstop does is change the behavior of the *buyers and sellers*: it removes the incentive to sell (you no longer fear a break-up) and creates an incentive to buy (yields look attractive and the downside is now backstopped). Buying pushes the *price* up, and the yield falls out of that. The whole chain — promise → changed belief → changed trading behavior → higher prices → lower yields — runs through human decisions to buy and sell, not through any direct lever on the yield itself. The expectations channel is, at bottom, a channel that changes minds, and changed minds change bids.

#### Worked example: a Spanish 10-year bond's price as its yield collapses

Let me make the redenomination premium concrete in dollars, because the abstract "yield fell from 7.6% to 1.6%" hides an enormous, tangible transfer of wealth.

Take a representative Spanish 10-year bond: \$100 of face value, a 5.5% annual coupon (so it pays \$5.50 a year), ten years to maturity. (We use dollars here for a global reader; the euro mechanics are identical — read every \$ as the euro equivalent at roughly 1.2 to 1 in 2012.) Its price is the present value of those payments, discounted at the market yield.

At a **7.6% yield** (July 2012, the panic), discount each \$5.50 coupon and the final \$100 principal back at 7.6%:

- Price = \$5.50 × (present-value factor for years 1–10 at 7.6%) + \$100 ÷ (1.076)^10
- Working it through: price ≈ **\$85.65 per \$100 face.**

The bond trades *below par* — at a discount — because its 5.5% coupon is worth less than the 7.6% the market now demands. To hold a bond paying 5.5% when you could demand 7.6%, you only pay \$86 for \$100 of face.

Now the yield falls to **1.6%** (2014, after Draghi). Re-discount the identical cash flows at 1.6%:

- Price = \$5.50 × (present-value factor for years 1–10 at 1.6%) + \$100 ÷ (1.016)^10
- Working it through: price ≈ **\$135.78 per \$100 face.**

Now the bond trades *above par* — at a premium — because its 5.5% coupon is generous compared to the 1.6% the market will accept. The capital gain is (\$135.78 − \$85.65) ÷ \$85.65 ≈ **+58.5%.** A holder who bought the bond in the depths of the panic and held it to 2014 made roughly **59% on price alone**, on top of collecting a fat 5.5% coupon every year. No default occurred. No principal was lost. The borrower's finances improved only modestly. The entire gain came from the *redenomination premium evaporating* — from fear being repriced out of the bond by a promise. **The expectations channel does not just move yields on a screen; it moves the actual market value of trillions of euros of bonds, and the move is a transfer from those who panicked to those who held.**

## The crisis machine: how Europe got to 7.6%

To understand why the words worked, you have to understand the machine they jammed. The euro-area sovereign crisis of 2010–2012 was not one event but a slow-motion structural failure with a specific, vicious feedback loop at its core.

The euro had a design flaw baked in from birth. Nineteen countries shared one currency and one central bank, but each kept its own budget, its own debt, and — critically — its own banks. A country in the euro is in a strange position: it borrows in a currency *it does not control.* The United States, Japan, and the UK borrow in currencies their own central banks issue, so they can never be *forced* to default — in the last resort the central bank can always print the money to pay. A US Treasury default is a political choice, never a financial necessity. But Spain in 2012 borrowed in euros, and the euro was issued by the ECB in Frankfurt, which had no mandate (and, the market feared, no willingness) to be the buyer of last resort for any single member's debt. Spain could *run out of euros* the way a household can run out of dollars. That single fact is what made a redenomination premium possible at all: a country that can be forced to default can be forced to leave.

### The doom loop

On top of that structural flaw sat the mechanism that turned a Greek problem into an existential threat to the whole currency: the **bank-sovereign doom loop.** Follow the circle.

Banks hold their own government's bonds — a lot of them. They are the most liquid, supposedly-safest asset on a domestic bank's balance sheet, and banks are often nudged by regulation to hold them. So a Spanish bank holds piles of Spanish government bonds. Now suppose Spanish yields spike. The market value of those bonds *falls* (remember: yields up, prices down). The bank's assets shrink, eating into its capital — the cushion that stands between it and insolvency. A weakened bank lends less, which deepens the recession. A deeper recession means less tax revenue and more bailout spending for the government, which makes the government's *own* debt look worse, which pushes its yields *higher* — which marks down the bonds the bank holds even further. The sovereign drags down the bank; the bank drags down the sovereign; round and round, each turn tighter than the last.

![The bank-sovereign doom loop and how the OMT backstop breaks the first link](/imgs/blogs/draghi-2012-whatever-it-takes-and-the-spread-machine-3.png)

This is why the periphery's yields were not just high but *accelerating* in mid-2012, and why the fear was so hard to talk down with ordinary reassurances. The loop had its own momentum. Greece had already required two bailouts and a debt restructuring. Ireland and Portugal were in programs. Spain — the euro area's fourth-largest economy, far too big for the existing rescue funds to bail out — had just requested up to €100 billion to recapitalize its banks, and its 10-year yield punched through 7%, the level past which Greece, Ireland, and Portugal had each lost market access and needed rescuing. The market was pricing the next domino, and that domino was big enough to bring down the table.

#### Worked example: the doom loop on a bank holding \$10bn of its government's bonds

Make the loop concrete. Take a Spanish bank holding \$10 billion of *face value* of its own government's 10-year bonds — a 4% coupon, the kind of "safe, liquid" sovereign book a bank keeps for collateral and capital purposes. Watch what the yield spiral does to it.

**Before the crisis,** the yield is a benign 5.0%. The market price of a 4%-coupon 10-year at a 5% yield is about \$92.28 per \$100 face. The bank's book is worth 0.9228 × \$10bn ≈ **\$9.23bn.**

**At the July 2012 panic,** the yield has spiked to 7.6%. The same bonds now price at about \$75.40 per \$100 face. The book is worth 0.7540 × \$10bn ≈ **\$7.54bn.** The bank has taken a mark-to-market loss of roughly \$9.23bn − \$7.54bn ≈ **\$1.69 billion** — and it has done nothing wrong; it merely owned its own country's debt. That \$1.69bn comes straight out of capital. A bank that was comfortably capitalized is now scrambling, lending less, and feeding the recession that feeds the spiral. *This* is the channel through which a sovereign scare becomes a banking crisis becomes a worse sovereign scare.

**Then Draghi speaks,** and over the next two years the yield collapses to 1.6%. The same bonds now price at about \$122.02 per \$100 face. The book is worth 1.2202 × \$10bn ≈ **\$12.20bn** — a swing of roughly **+\$4.66 billion** from the panic low. The bank is recapitalized not by an injection of taxpayer money but by the *reversal of the doom loop.* Falling yields repaired the very balance sheets whose deterioration had been driving yields up. **A credible backstop did not just rescue governments; it ran the bank-sovereign loop in reverse, healing the banks for free — which is precisely why peripheral bank stocks were among the biggest winners of the whole episode.**

### Two years of interventions that did not work

Here is the part of the story that makes Draghi's success so striking: the ECB had already been *trying* to calm the periphery for two full years before July 2012, with real money, and it had not worked. Understanding why the earlier interventions failed is the best possible way to understand why the later promise succeeded.

The crisis arc began in late 2009, when Greece revealed its deficit was far larger than reported. By 2010 the contagion logic of the doom loop had spread the fear from Greece to Ireland, then Portugal, all three of which lost market access and entered bailout programs. In May 2010 the ECB launched its **Securities Markets Programme (SMP)** — it began *actually buying* peripheral government bonds in the secondary market to push their prices up and yields down. This was real bond-buying, the thing OMT would only ever promise. And it had a temporary, limited effect that faded every time, because of two fatal design choices.

First, the SMP was **finite and discretionary.** The ECB never committed to a size or a yield target; it bought in fits and starts, and the market could see the purchases were limited and reluctant. A speculator betting against a peripheral bond knew the ECB's buying could stop at any moment and was capped in size — so the bet stayed attractive, and yields kept drifting back up after each round of intervention. Second, the SMP came with a poisonous feature for private holders: as the ECB accumulated a country's bonds, the market began to fear that in a restructuring the ECB would be paid first (as a senior, preferred creditor) and private holders would absorb the losses. This **subordination fear** meant ECB buying could perversely *raise* the yield private investors demanded, because it made their slice of the debt riskier. The cure was feeding the disease.

The ECB also tried flooding the *banking system* with cheap cash. In December 2011 and February 2012, under Draghi's just-begun presidency, it ran two enormous **Long-Term Refinancing Operations (LTROs)**, lending banks more than €1 trillion combined at very low rates for three years. The hope was partly that banks would use the cash to buy their own governments' bonds (the "Sarkozy carry trade"), capping yields. It bought a few months of calm in early 2012 — and then the calm broke, because LTROs treated a *symptom* (banks short of funding) without touching the *cause* (the redenomination fear). Lending banks cheap money does not remove the fear that the currency itself might break; if anything, loading banks with more of their own sovereign's debt made the doom loop *tighter.* By the summer of 2012 the periphery was back in crisis, worse than before, and the ECB had spent two years and hundreds of billions of euros learning that *real money, deployed timidly, loses to a self-fulfilling panic.*

That is the context for 26 July. Draghi was not speaking into calm; he was speaking into the wreckage of every prior tool. The SMP had been finite and got tested. The LTROs had treated the wrong problem. What had never been tried was an *unlimited, unconditional-on-size* commitment aimed directly at the redenomination premium itself. The lesson the ECB had paid two years and a near-collapse to learn was the lesson that made "whatever it takes" the right words: against a self-fulfilling fear, *a large intervention loses and an unlimited promise wins.*

## The intervention: "whatever it takes" and the machinery behind it

Now we can see exactly what Draghi did, and why it was so precisely targeted at the machine described above.

The speech itself, on 26 July 2012, was the credibility shock. But credibility is not magic; it has to be backed by a believable *capacity to act.* A central banker who says "whatever it takes" with no instrument behind the words is bluffing, and markets eventually call bluffs. So in September 2012 the ECB unveiled the instrument: **Outright Monetary Transactions, or OMT.** It is worth understanding its design in detail, because the design is what made it work without ever being used.

OMT was a commitment by the ECB to buy the short-dated government bonds of a euro-area country in the secondary market, **in unlimited quantities,** subject to one condition: the country had to be in (or eligible for) a formal European bailout program with attached reforms. The two features that mattered were the word *unlimited* and the word *conditional.*

**Unlimited** is the heart of it, and the reason a finite war chest would have failed. Imagine the ECB had instead announced "we will buy up to €500 billion of peripheral bonds." A clever speculator does the arithmetic: €500 billion is a lot, but the periphery's debt is measured in trillions. The fund can be exhausted. So you keep shorting, betting you can outlast the firepower — and your bet *itself* keeps yields high, which is the bad equilibrium reasserting itself. A finite backstop can be tested, and anything that can be tested *will* be tested. But an *unlimited* backstop cannot be exhausted, by definition. There is no point at which the speculator wins. The moment you believe the ECB will buy without limit at a yield it deems unjustified, *betting against that yield becomes a guaranteed loss* — you would be selling a bond to a buyer with an infinite balance sheet and a stated price floor. Rational speculators do not take guaranteed losses, so they stop. And the instant they stop, the high yield they were causing disappears. The unlimited promise wins precisely *because* it makes itself unnecessary.

**Conditional** is what made the unlimited promise legally and politically survivable. By tying OMT to a reform program, the ECB answered the obvious objection — that it was simply financing profligate governments (which its mandate forbids). A country could only get the backstop if it submitted to budget discipline and reforms. This kept OMT inside the ECB's monetary mandate: it was framed as repairing a *broken transmission of monetary policy* (the redenomination premium was preventing the ECB's low policy rate from reaching Spanish borrowers), not as fiscal bailout. That distinction is what let OMT survive the legal challenges that followed — and it is a topic in its own right, which we treat in [the legal mandate of a central bank](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-legal-mandate-of-a-central-bank).

#### Worked example: the cost of OMT to the ECB

Here is the worked example that is also the punchline of the entire post, and it is the easiest arithmetic in the article.

How much did OMT cost the ECB? Add up every bond it purchased under the program, from its announcement in September 2012 through the entire decade that followed:

- Bonds bought under OMT: **€0.**
- Therefore total cost: **€0.**

OMT was *never activated.* No country ever applied for it; no purchase was ever made under it. The ECB committed to spend an unlimited amount and spent exactly nothing — and in doing so moved Spain's 10-year yield by **600 basis points** (from 7.6% to 1.6%) and Italy's by roughly **470 basis points** (from 6.6% to about 1.9%). The cost per basis point of yield reduction was zero, because the numerator was zero.

Contrast this with the alternative the ECB was implicitly being asked to do *before* OMT: actually buy peripheral bonds in size to cap yields, the way the Federal Reserve bought trillions of dollars of Treasuries in its COVID-era quantitative easing, or the way the Bank of England spent £65 billion of real money on emergency gilt purchases in 2022 to stop *its* doom loop. Those interventions worked, but they cost real money and ballooned central-bank balance sheets. OMT achieved a larger yield move than either, for free, because it operated through *belief* rather than *purchase.* **The most powerful thing a central bank can buy is the market's certainty that it will act — and certainty, unlike bonds, costs nothing to hold.**

## What repriced, and by how much

The headline is Spain's 10-year yield, but the credibility shock rippled across every asset whose price embedded euro-break-up risk. This is the "so what is it worth now" part — the valuation payoff that the whole series is built to deliver.

**Peripheral sovereign bonds** were the epicenter. Spain's 10-year fell from 7.6% (July 2012) to about 5.0% within a year and to 1.6% by 2014 — at which point Spain, a country the market had been pricing for break-up eighteen months earlier, was borrowing *more cheaply than the United States.* Italy's 10-year fell from 6.6% to under 2%. Portugal and Ireland, which had been in bailout programs, regained market access and saw their yields normalize. The redenomination leg of every peripheral yield — the self-fulfilling fear — was repriced toward zero.

**Peripheral bank stocks** were the second-order winners, through the doom loop running in reverse. As sovereign yields fell, the bonds on bank balance sheets recovered, capital was rebuilt, and the implied risk of a banking collapse receded. Spanish and Italian bank equity indices, which had been pricing a real chance of nationalization or failure, rallied hard. The worked example above — a \$4.66bn swing on a \$10bn bond book — multiplied across an entire banking system is the mechanism behind that rally.

**The euro itself** stabilized. A currency whose very existence is in question carries a discount; remove the existential question and the discount narrows. The euro, which had been sliding through the first half of 2012 on break-up fears, found a floor.

**Peripheral equities and credit** more broadly re-rated. A lower sovereign discount rate raises the present value of every domestic cash flow — the discount-rate channel, working through the credibility channel. When the risk-free anchor for an entire economy falls 600 basis points, every stock, every corporate bond, every property cash flow in that economy is worth more, mechanically. The repricing of one number — the sovereign yield — propagated into the valuation of everything priced off it.

To see how the propagation works, take the simplest valuation identity: an asset is worth the cash it throws off, divided by the rate you discount that cash at. A perpetual stream of €1 a year discounted at 8% is worth €1 ÷ 0.08 = €12.50. Drop the discount rate to 3% and the same €1 stream is worth €1 ÷ 0.03 = €33.33 — a *166% increase in value* with no change whatsoever to the underlying cash flow. The cash did not improve; the rate it was discounted at fell, and the present value exploded. This is not a peripheral curiosity of the episode; it is the central mechanism. The sovereign yield is the floor under every domestic discount rate — a Spanish company borrows at the government rate plus a spread, a Spanish property is valued at a cap rate built off the government rate — so when the sovereign rate falls 600 basis points, the discount rate on *everything* Spanish falls with it, and the present value of every Spanish cash flow jumps. The credibility shock entered through the sovereign bond and propagated, by arithmetic, into the valuation of an entire economy.

The timeline of the recovery is worth tracing because it shows the channel working in stages. The *fast* move was the redenomination premium — the pure-fear leg collapsed within months of the speech and the OMT announcement, because fear can reprice instantly. The *slow* move was the fundamental improvement — Spain's reforms, its return to growth, the gradual repair of its banks — which played out over the following two years and pulled the credit leg down further. So the 7.6%-to-1.6% path was not one motion but two superimposed: a sharp drop in 2012 as the panic was talked down, then a longer grind through 2013–2014 as fundamentals caught up to the calm the words had bought. Credibility bought the time; fundamentals filled it in.

The signal to watch, in any future episode, is the **spread** — the gap between a risky sovereign's yield and the safe benchmark (the periphery-over-bund spread in Europe). A spread is the market's running estimate of the redenomination-plus-credit premium. When a credibility shock lands, the spread *collapses*; when credibility frays, it *widens*. The spread is the thermometer of the credibility channel, and Draghi 2012 is the cleanest reading it has ever given.

### The credibility had to survive a courtroom

A promise is only as good as its durability, and OMT's durability was tested not in the market but in court — which is itself part of why it worked. A backstop the market believed might be struck down would have been a weaker backstop, so the legal fight matters to the economics.

OMT was challenged before the German Federal Constitutional Court by plaintiffs who argued it amounted to the ECB financing governments — *monetary financing* of fiscal deficits, which the EU treaties explicitly forbid, and which Germany in particular had insisted on banning when it gave up the Deutschmark. The German court, uneasy, referred the question to the European Court of Justice. In June 2015 the ECJ ruled that OMT fell *within* the ECB's mandate: because it was aimed at repairing the *transmission of monetary policy* (the redenomination premium was blocking the ECB's low policy rate from reaching peripheral borrowers) rather than at funding governments, and because it carried conditions and limits in *design* (only short maturities, only for countries under a reform program), it was a legitimate monetary tool, not fiscal financing. The German court, somewhat grudgingly, accepted the ruling with caveats.

The legal outcome ratified the *conditional* design choice we walked through earlier. Had OMT been an unconditional, open-ended pledge to fund any government at any maturity, it would almost certainly have been struck down as monetary financing — and a backstop the market expected to be illegal is no backstop at all. The conditionality was not a weakness bolted on to placate Germany; it was the feature that made the promise *legally credible*, and therefore *economically credible*. A central bank's credibility, it turns out, includes the credibility that its promises are lawful and will stand. The interaction between a central bank's economic mandate and its legal one is deep enough to deserve its own treatment, which is exactly what [the legal mandate of a central bank](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-legal-mandate-of-a-central-bank) provides.

### What it would have cost if the promise had been doubted

It is worth dwelling on the counterfactual, because it sharpens the "€0" punchline. Suppose the market had *not* believed Draghi — suppose it had treated "whatever it takes" as the kind of bluff that finite, timid SMP buying had revealed the ECB to be. What then?

The speculators would have kept selling. Yields would have stayed at or above 7%. To make good on the promise, the ECB would have been forced to *actually activate OMT* and buy peripheral bonds in genuinely unlimited size — potentially hundreds of billions or trillions of euros — to drag yields down by force. Its balance sheet would have ballooned, the subordination and monetary-financing objections would have detonated politically, and the German court challenge would have landed on a program that was *being used* rather than merely promised, a far harder thing to defend. The cost, financial and political, would have been enormous, and there is no guarantee it would have worked: a central bank forced to fund a member in unlimited size is one step from the fiscal-financing line its treaty forbids.

That counterfactual is the entire value of credibility expressed as a number. The €0 cost was not a property of OMT; it was the *dividend on credibility the ECB had accumulated* — credibility that the market, weighing the unlimited design and the institutional reality that the ECB really can create euros without limit, judged to be backed by genuine capacity and will. The promise was free *because* it was believed, and it was believed *because* it was credible, and it was credible *because* it was unlimited, conditional, and lawful. Pull any one of those legs away and the bill stops being zero.

## Common misconceptions

Five beliefs about this episode are widespread and wrong, and correcting each one sharpens the lesson.

**"The ECB bought a lot of bonds to bring yields down."** No — this is the single most important fact about the episode and the most commonly misremembered. OMT bought *zero* bonds. The ECB *did* run other bond-buying programs later (the Public Sector Purchase Programme, its quantitative easing, began in 2015), but the 2012 yield collapse — the 600-basis-point move that ended the acute crisis — happened *before* any of that, driven by the *promise* alone. The yields fell in 2012–2014; the actual buying started in 2015. The credibility did the work first, and most of it.

**"It worked because Draghi is charismatic / it was just rhetoric."** No — rhetoric without a believable instrument behind it is a bluff, and the market would have tested it. What made the words credible was the *unlimited, conditional* OMT design unveiled weeks later, plus the institutional reality that the ECB genuinely *can* create euros without limit. The speech was the trigger; the credible capacity to act was the gun. Words moved markets *because* they were backed by an instrument that could not be exhausted.

**"This proves a central bank can fix any crisis with words."** No — it proves a central bank can fix a *self-fulfilling* crisis with words, because the premium it is destroying was never anchored in fundamentals to begin with. If Spain had been *actually* insolvent — if the high yield reflected a real, unavoidable inability to pay rather than a fear-driven spiral — no promise could have helped; only money or default could. Draghi worked because the redenomination premium was, in large part, a coordination failure. Words cannot fix a solvency problem; they can dissolve a panic. Knowing the difference is the entire skill.

**"OMT cost nothing, so backstops are free."** No — OMT cost nothing *because it was never triggered,* which only happened because the market believed it *would* be triggered if needed. The ECB was genuinely on the hook for an unlimited, real commitment. Had the market called the bluff, the ECB would have had to buy, and the bill would have been very real. The zero cost is the *consequence* of credibility, not a property of backstops in general. A backstop that the market doubts gets used, and gets expensive.

**"It permanently solved the euro's problems."** No — it ended the *acute* crisis by removing the break-up premium, but the structural flaw (a shared currency with separate national budgets and banks) remained. The doom loop was eased, not abolished. The euro area spent the next decade building further institutions — banking union, a permanent rescue fund — to address what Draghi had only stabilized with a promise. A credible promise buys time and calm; it does not rebuild the foundations.

## Case studies: the credibility channel, dated and compared

The deep case is Draghi 2012 itself, walked above. Here are three shorter comparisons that locate it in the wider record of credibility shocks — what makes one succeed and another fail.

### Draghi 2012 (the success): unlimited beats large

The dated spine, in one place. **26 July 2012:** the speech; Spain 10Y at 7.6%, Italy at 6.6%, both having broken the 7% bailout-trigger zone. **6 September 2012:** OMT formally announced — unlimited, conditional, short-dated. **Through 2012–2014:** Spain's 10Y falls to 1.6%, Italy's to ~1.9%; peripheral bank stocks rally; the euro stabilizes. **The whole decade after:** OMT used zero times, cost €0. The lesson, stated as a rule: an *unlimited* backstop that the market believes is categorically more powerful than a *large* one it can test, because the unlimited one removes the bad equilibrium rather than merely leaning against it.

### The Bank of England, 2022 (a forced backstop): when credibility snaps first

The instructive contrast is the UK in September 2022. A new government announced £45 billion of unfunded tax cuts; the market lost confidence in British fiscal-monetary coherence essentially overnight; 30-year gilt yields jumped about 130 basis points in days, the pound fell to a record \$1.035, and a hidden doom loop in pension funds (liability-driven investment, or LDI, strategies that had to sell gilts *into* the rout to meet collateral calls) threatened a fire-sale spiral. The Bank of England was forced to intervene with **£65 billion of actual, real emergency gilt-buying** to stop it. Note the difference from Draghi: the BOE had to *spend real money* because the intervention came *after* credibility had already snapped — at that point words alone could not have held, so the bank had to put its balance sheet where its mouth was. Draghi acted from a position of credibility and paid nothing; the BOE acted from a position of lost credibility and paid £65bn. The full anatomy of that episode is in [the 2022 UK gilt and LDI crisis: when credibility snapped](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-2022-uk-gilt-and-ldi-crisis-when-credibility-snapped). The pairing is the whole lesson: **credibility spent in advance is free; credibility rebuilt after it is lost is ruinously expensive.**

### Currency defenses (the FX cousin): when the promise is *not* believed

The credibility channel has a darker history in the foreign-exchange market, where central banks have repeatedly promised to defend a currency peg and been overrun. The mechanism is the mirror image of OMT. A central bank defending a peg has a *finite* war chest — its foreign-exchange reserves — and speculators know it. So they test it, selling the currency until the reserves run dry, at which point the peg breaks and they collect.

The canonical example is Black Wednesday, 16 September 1992, when George Soros and others bet that the Bank of England could not hold the pound inside the European Exchange Rate Mechanism. The BOE's defense worked through *finite* tools: it spent billions of pounds of reserves buying its own currency and hiked its interest rate from 10% to 12% and announced a further hike to 15% — and it still lost, because the speculators correctly judged that the reserves were exhaustible and the rate hikes politically unsustainable. By the end of the day Britain crashed out of the ERM, and Soros reportedly made over a billion dollars. Five years later, in 1997, the same logic tore through Asia: Thailand, then Indonesia, then South Korea each promised to defend a dollar peg with finite reserves, each was tested, and each peg broke when the reserves ran low. In every case the structure was identical — a *finite* defender against a market that knew the defense could be exhausted, and therefore tested it to destruction.

The lesson sharpens the Draghi insight by exact contrast: a central bank promising to *defend* a level with *finite* resources can be beaten, but a central bank promising to *create* its own currency *without limit* cannot — because it can never run out of the thing it issues. The pound defenders in 1992 and the Asian central banks in 1997 were spending a resource that could run out; Draghi in 2012 was promising to supply a resource — euros — that the ECB can produce without limit. That is the whole difference between the side of the line that gets overrun and the side that wins without firing a shot. A defender of a peg sells a finite asset to buy its currency; the ECB offered to *print* an infinite one to buy peripheral bonds. The market tests the first and never bothers to test the second. We work through the FX version in detail in [currency policy and FX intervention](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/currency-policy-and-fx-intervention).

#### Worked example: decomposing a peripheral yield and what the words did to it

Let me close the loop on the decomposition with the exact arithmetic, because it shows *which leg* the words moved and which they left alone.

In **July 2012,** Spain's 10-year yield of 7.6% decomposed roughly as:

- German risk-free rate: **1.3%**
- Credit / fiscal-default premium: **1.5%**
- Redenomination premium: **4.8%**
- Total: 1.3 + 1.5 + 4.8 = **7.6%** ✓

By **2014,** Spain's 10-year yield of 1.6% decomposed roughly as:

- German risk-free rate: **0.6%** (fell ~70bp as ECB policy eased and bunds rallied)
- Credit / fiscal-default premium: **1.0%** (fell ~50bp as Spanish reforms and recovery reduced real default odds)
- Redenomination premium: **~0.0%** (fell ~480bp — *erased* by the backstop)
- Total: 0.6 + 1.0 + 0.0 = **1.6%** ✓

Of the total 600-basis-point collapse, the risk-free leg explains ~70bp and the credit leg ~50bp — real but modest improvements. The remaining **~480 basis points came entirely from the redenomination premium going to zero,** and that leg was destroyed by a promise, not by any change in Spanish fundamentals. **Four-fifths of the greatest sovereign-yield rally in modern European history was the price of a fear that a credible central bank simply talked out of existence.** That is the credibility channel in one calculation: it does not change what a country *is*; it changes what everyone *believes will happen to it*, and for a self-fulfilling premium, that is the same thing.

## What it means for asset values: the playbook

Strip the episode to its transferable lessons — the things that generalize to the next crisis, wherever it lands.

**The cheapest policy tool is a credible promise, and it is cheapest exactly when it is most needed.** A central bank with credibility can move a benchmark yield hundreds of basis points for nothing, because the market does its work for it. This is why credibility is the most valuable asset a central bank owns and why protecting it — through consistency, through never bluffing, through following words with capacity — is the core of the job. The corollary is brutal: the moment a central bank's promise is *doubted,* the same channel runs in reverse and reprices everything violently and at once, as the UK learned in days.

**Unlimited beats large; the size of a backstop matters less than whether it can be exhausted.** When you assess any future intervention — a sovereign backstop, a deposit guarantee, a market-maker-of-last-resort facility — the first question is not "how big is it?" but "can it run out?" A facility that can be exhausted invites a test; a facility that cannot, ends the test before it begins. Design the promise to be untestable and you may never have to honor it.

**A self-fulfilling premium is the one a central bank can destroy with words; a solvency premium is not.** Before you assume a crisis can be talked away, ask whether the high price reflects a *coordination failure* (everyone fears everyone else's fear, with fundamentals that could support a calm outcome) or a *fundamental insolvency* (the borrower genuinely cannot pay under any belief). The first is curable by credibility; the second is not. Draghi worked because Spain's premium was mostly the former. Reading which one you face is the whole skill, and getting it wrong in either direction is costly.

**The signal to watch is the spread, and the thing that moves it is the reaction function plus credibility.** A risky sovereign's spread over the safe benchmark is the live thermometer of the credibility channel. It collapses on a believed backstop and blows out on a credibility loss. When you see a spread spiking in a self-fulfilling way, the relevant question is whether the central bank has both the *will* and the *unlimited capacity* to backstop it — and whether the market believes both. If yes, the spread is a buying opportunity (the panic will be talked down); if no, it is a warning (the panic will be funded only by real money, or not at all).

**What would invalidate the read:** a credibility shock that *fails* — where the central bank promises and the market does not believe, forcing real intervention or a break. That is the UK-2022 scenario, and it is the proof that the channel is not magic. Credibility is earned over years and can be spent in an instant; a promise from a bank the market trusts is free, and a promise from a bank it doubts is worthless. The same nineteen words that saved the euro would have done nothing from a central banker the market did not believe. The lever is not the words. The lever is the belief behind them.

**The template became permanent.** The deepest evidence that Draghi 2012 was understood — by the central bankers themselves — as a *reusable mechanism* rather than a one-off rescue is that the ECB deliberately built a bigger version of it and kept it on the shelf. When COVID hit in March 2020 and peripheral spreads began to gap out again on the same redenomination logic, the ECB launched the **Pandemic Emergency Purchase Programme (PEPP)** with explicit *flexibility* to skew its buying toward whichever country's spread was widening — an OMT-style anti-fragmentation tool, this time pre-armed and unconditional on a bailout program. And in 2022, as rate hikes threatened to reopen the spread, the ECB unveiled the **Transmission Protection Instrument (TPI)**, a near-direct descendant of OMT: an unlimited, conditional commitment to buy the bonds of a member facing "unwarranted, disorderly" spread widening. Like OMT, the TPI has been a backstop the market prices but the ECB has barely had to use. The institution learned the lesson of 2012 and turned a speech into permanent machinery: *an unlimited, credible anti-fragmentation backstop is now a standing feature of the euro area,* precisely because the original proved that the threat to act can substitute almost entirely for the act itself.

The broader policy-machine lesson — the one that connects this case to the rest of the series — is that the *most* effective interventions are often the ones that leave no footprint in the data. A rate cut shows up in the fed-funds series; QE shows up in the balance sheet; a fiscal stimulus shows up in the deficit. The credibility channel shows up *nowhere* on the central bank's books, because its entire effect runs through private expectations and private trades. An analyst reading only the ECB's balance sheet in 2013 would conclude the ECB had done almost nothing to the periphery — and would be exactly wrong, having missed the single most consequential policy act of the decade because it cost nothing and bought nothing. The lever that moves markets most is sometimes the one that is invisible in every official series, because it lives in the one place no central bank can put on its balance sheet: belief.
