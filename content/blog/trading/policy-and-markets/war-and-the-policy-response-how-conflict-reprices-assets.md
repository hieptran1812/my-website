> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A war does not reprice assets by itself; it reprices them through the policy response it forces — sanctions, defense spending, the central bank's reaction to the inflation it causes, and the flight to safe assets.
>
> - There are four policy transmission paths: (1) **sanctions** — an FX/financial lever that froze ~\$300bn of Russian reserves and cut banks from SWIFT; (2) **defense fiscal** spending — a deficit-financed bid for the defense complex; (3) the **energy-to-inflation-to-rate-hike** chain that forces a central bank's hand; (4) **safe-haven flows** into gold, the dollar, and Treasuries.
> - In 2022 Brent spiked to \$139.13 (Mar 7), European gas hit ~€340/MWh (~10x normal), and that energy/food inflation fed **directly** into the fastest hiking cycle since Volcker (+525bp).
> - Russia's own policy response — a **20% policy rate** plus capital controls — reversed a ruble collapse (80 → 135 → ~55 per USD): a textbook small-economy crisis defense.
> - The one number to remember: a war's market impact is set less by the body count than by **which policy levers it pulls** — and not every war is a 1973, because US shale and spare OPEC capacity now cap the energy shock.

On the morning of February 24, 2022, Russian forces crossed into Ukraine. Within hours, oil was bid; within days, Brent crude printed \$139.13 a barrel — its highest since 2008. European natural gas, the fuel that heats homes and runs factories across the continent, would climb to roughly €340 per megawatt-hour by late summer, about ten times its normal level. The Russian ruble cratered from around 80 to the dollar to 135. And yet, if you had watched only the tanks and the headlines, you would have mispriced almost every one of these moves.

Because the tanks did not move the markets. **Policy did.** The oil spike was real, but what made it durable — what turned a one-week scare into a year-long inflation problem — was the collision between an energy supply shock and a Western financial counter-strike. The West froze about \$300 billion of the Russian central bank's foreign reserves and cut major Russian banks out of SWIFT, the messaging network that lets banks settle cross-border payments. Russia answered with a 20% policy rate and capital controls. The United States Federal Reserve, watching the inflation that the war helped ignite, embarked on the steepest tightening campaign in four decades. Every number that mattered — the ruble's path, the inflation rate, the level of interest rates, the price of gold — was the output of a policy lever, not of the war directly.

That is the lens of this post, and of this whole series: **a war is a shock, but the policy response is the transmission that actually reprices assets.** This is a policy series, not a geopolitics one. If you want the raw geopolitical-risk-premium framing — how conflict itself prices into a risk premium — read the companion piece in the law-and-geopolitics track. Here, we keep the policy machine front and center: which lever gets pulled, which channel it travels, and what the asset is worth on the other side.

## Foundations: the four policy paths from a war to a price

Before any case study, fix the machine in your head. A war is an input — a sudden, violent disruption of two things markets hate: **supply** (energy, food, metals, shipping lanes, factory output) and **certainty** (about borders, about contracts, about whether your counterparty will be sanctioned tomorrow). Neither of those is, by itself, a price. They become prices only when a government or central bank pulls a lever in response. There are four such levers, and they map cleanly onto the four channels this series cares about — the discount rate, the liquidity tide, the currency, and credibility.

Why insist on the policy lens when the war is right there on the front page? Because the front page is a trap for an investor. The intensity of a conflict — its casualties, its territory, its moral stakes — correlates only loosely with its market impact. A devastating regional war that touches no major commodity and triggers no sanctions can leave global asset prices essentially untouched, while a relatively contained conflict astride a critical energy chokepoint can reprice everything. The transmission is not the violence; it is the *economic and policy plumbing* the violence happens to touch. So the disciplined question is never "how terrible is this war?" but "which policy levers does it force, and what do those levers do to the rate I discount cash flows by, the currency I price assets in, and the credibility of the institutions I assumed were safe?" Get that mapping right and you will often find yourself positioned opposite the headlines — buying when the news is worst because the policy response will be benign, or selling into a rally because an energy shock is quietly forcing a central bank's hand.

**Path 1 — Sanctions: an FX and financial lever.** Modern war is fought with balance sheets as well as missiles. A coalition can freeze the target's foreign reserves (the hard-currency war chest a central bank uses to defend its own currency), cut its banks out of the global payment plumbing (SWIFT), seize the assets of named individuals and firms, and bar trade in key goods. This is the **weaponization of finance**, and its target is the currency channel and the target country's access to capital. When it works, the sanctioned currency loses the very tools that normally defend it.

**Path 2 — Defense fiscal spending.** War is expensive, and the money is borrowed. A surge in defense outlays is a **fiscal impulse**: it is government demand, deficit-financed, aimed at a specific corner of the economy. It bids up defense contractors' earnings, but it also enlarges the deficit and the supply of government bonds that must be sold — which, all else equal, pushes long yields up. This is the fiscal lever feeding both the equity multiple of one sector and the term premium of the whole bond market. (For the mechanics of how a deficit becomes a wall of Treasury supply, see the fiscal-toolkit post linked at the end.)

**Path 3 — The energy-to-inflation-to-rate-hike chain.** This is the path that does the most damage to a diversified portfolio, because it forces the central bank. A war that disrupts energy supply lifts the oil price; oil feeds into gasoline, freight, fertilizer, and ultimately the price of nearly everything; **headline inflation** rises; and a central bank with a price-stability mandate must respond by raising its policy rate. The war never lands on the central bank's desk directly — the **inflation** does. This is the discount-rate channel, triggered at one remove.

**Path 4 — Safe-haven flows.** When certainty collapses, capital runs to assets that are expected to hold value in a crisis: **gold** (no counterparty, no government), the **US dollar** (the world's reserve currency and the deepest pool of liquidity), and **US Treasuries** (the closest thing to a risk-free claim). These flows are not policy levers in the same sense — they are the market's reaction — but they are deeply shaped by policy, because whether the dollar and Treasuries actually behave as havens depends on whether the issuer's own policy is credible. Sometimes the haven fails.

It helps to be precise about *why* these three assets are havens, because each rests on a different foundation and each can be undermined by a different policy. Gold is a haven because it has **no counterparty and no government** — its value does not depend on anyone's promise to pay, so it cannot be frozen, defaulted on, or inflated away by a central bank printing more of it. The dollar is a haven for the opposite reason: it is the most *connected* asset on earth, the currency in which the world borrows, trades commodities, and holds reserves, so in a panic everyone needs dollars to meet obligations, and that scramble bids the dollar up. Treasuries are a haven because they combine the dollar's reach with the explicit backing of the US government and a market so deep that even a large investor can move size without crashing the price. Notice that two of the three — the dollar and Treasuries — are *credibility* assets: their haven status is a statement of faith in US policy and the US government's willingness to pay. Gold is the only one whose haven status is *unconditional*, which is precisely why it is the asset of last resort when even the credibility assets are in doubt. That distinction will matter enormously when we look at the 2025 episode, where US policy itself was the shock and the credibility havens failed while gold soared.

Hold those four in mind. The rest of this post is a tour of each, anchored in real, dated numbers, and then a gallery of historical cases that show the *range* of outcomes — because the single most important lesson is that not every war produces a 1973.

#### Worked example: an oil shock becomes a rate hike

Let us make Path 3 concrete with arithmetic, because the chain from a barrel of oil to a basis point of policy is more direct than people think. Suppose oil moves from \$70 to \$120 a barrel — roughly the scale of the 2022 move. Energy is about 7% of the US consumer price index basket (gasoline, utility gas, fuel oil, electricity). A move from \$70 to \$120 is a **71% increase** in the crude price. Gasoline at the pump is not 100% crude — refining, taxes, and distribution dilute it — but the pass-through from crude to the retail energy basket is roughly 60–70% over a few months. So the direct first-round hit to the energy component is about 0.71 × 0.65 ≈ a **46% rise** in energy prices.

Apply that to the 7% basket weight: 0.46 × 7% = **3.2 percentage points** added to headline inflation from the direct energy channel alone. Then add the second-round effects — diesel raises freight costs, fertilizer raises food costs, and workers seeing higher prices ask for higher wages — which historically roughly double the direct hit over a year. Call the total headline impulse **5–6 percentage points** of extra inflation.

A central bank running an inflation-targeting regime with something like a Taylor rule responds to inflation roughly one-for-one above target, and often more than one-for-one to re-anchor expectations. If inflation runs 5 points hot, the policy rate has to climb by *at least* that much, and usually more, to push the **real** rate (the policy rate minus inflation) into restrictive territory. That is exactly what happened: the energy/food shock helped push US headline CPI to about **9% in mid-2022**, and the Fed raised its target range by **525 basis points** in sixteen months. The intuition: a war that adds \$50 to a barrel of oil is, with a lag, a multi-hundred-basis-point tightening of monetary policy.

## How sanctions work as a financial weapon

Of the four paths, sanctions are the most distinctly *policy* — they are a deliberate lever, pulled by governments, with the explicit goal of moving prices and starving an adversary of capital. To understand why they hit so hard in 2022, you have to understand what a central bank's reserves are *for*.

A central bank holds foreign-exchange reserves — dollars, euros, gold, foreign government bonds — as a war chest. When its own currency is falling because everyone is selling it, the central bank can sell those reserves (sell dollars, buy rubles) to prop up the price, the same way a homeowner draws on savings in an emergency. Russia entered 2022 with roughly \$640 billion in reserves, one of the largest stockpiles in the world. It looked impregnable.

The catch: a reserve held *abroad* — in a New York custody account, a German bond, a Paris deposit — is only yours if the host country lets you use it. In the days after the invasion, the G7 and the EU **froze** the portion of Russia's reserves held in their jurisdictions: roughly \$300 billion, about half the total. Simultaneously, major Russian banks were cut off from SWIFT, so even the reserves Russia could theoretically access became hard to *move*. In one stroke, the war chest that defends the currency was cut in half and the plumbing to deploy it was clogged.

![The reserve freeze immobilizes half of Russia's war chest, the ruble crashes, and a 20 percent rate plus capital controls reverses it](/imgs/blogs/war-and-the-policy-response-how-conflict-reprices-assets-3.png)

This is why economists call it the **weaponization of finance**: the West turned the architecture of the global financial system — the reserve currencies, the custody banks, the payment messaging network — into a weapon. The legal mechanics (which authorities, which lists, which secondary-sanction reach) belong to the law-and-geopolitics track and are linked below. What *we* care about is the valuation consequence: a currency's value rests partly on the credibility of the reserves backing it, and freezing those reserves removes the backstop the market was pricing.

But 2022 also revealed the *limits* of the financial weapon, and the limits are themselves a policy story. Europe could not stop buying Russian energy overnight — roughly 40% of EU gas and a quarter of its oil came from Russia — so a full energy embargo would have inflicted as much pain on the buyer as the seller. That tension produced one of the most novel policy instruments of the decade: the **G7 oil price cap.** Rather than ban Russian oil outright (which would have spiked the global price and *raised* Russia's per-barrel revenue even as it sold fewer barrels), the G7 used its control over the world's shipping and insurance infrastructure to set a *ceiling*. Western insurers and shippers — who underwrite the bulk of seaborne oil — were permitted to handle Russian crude only if it sold below a capped price (set initially at \$60 a barrel). The design goal was surgical: keep Russian barrels flowing to the market (so the global oil price, and therefore global inflation, stayed contained) while squeezing the *margin* Russia earned on each one. This is a policy lever aimed not at the *quantity* of a commodity but at the *price the adversary receives* — a genuinely new tool, and one whose effectiveness is still debated because Russia built a "shadow fleet" of non-Western tankers to evade it. The point for an investor: when designing or reading a sanctions regime, ask whether it targets quantity (embargo — bullish the commodity, inflationary) or margin (price cap — designed to be neutral for the commodity, deflationary by intent). The two have opposite implications for what oil, and therefore inflation, and therefore your discount rate, will do.

#### Worked example: freezing \$300bn and what it does to a currency's backing

Think of a currency's exchange-rate defense as a bank run in reverse. The "depositors" are everyone holding rubles who would like to convert them to dollars and get out. The "vault" is the central bank's usable FX reserves. In a calm market, the central bank can meet redemptions: a holder sells rubles, the bank sells dollars from the vault to buy those rubles, and the price holds.

Russia entered the crisis with about \$640bn in reserves. Strip out the ~\$300bn frozen abroad and the portion held in gold and Chinese yuan that could not be quickly sold for hard currency to defend a falling ruble in Western markets, and the *usable* dollar-and-euro war chest collapsed to a small fraction of the headline number — call it well under \$100bn of genuinely deployable hard currency in the opening days. Now run the math of a panic: in a currency crisis, daily outflows can run into the tens of billions of dollars. A \$300bn vault might absorb a week or two of one-way selling; a \$60–80bn usable vault gets emptied in days. The market does this arithmetic instantly. The moment traders saw that the deployable reserves were a fraction of the headline \$640bn, the ruble was a one-way bet down — and it gapped from ~80 to ~135 per dollar. **The intuition: a reserve you cannot spend is not a reserve, and freezing it is mathematically equivalent to halving the price support under a currency.**

## How Russia defended: a textbook small-economy crisis playbook

Here is the twist that makes 2022 such a clean teaching case. The ruble did not stay at 135. By summer it had *over*-recovered to about 55 per dollar — stronger than before the war. Did the sanctions fail? No. The recovery was engineered by Russia's *own* policy response, and it is a flawless illustration of how a small, open economy defends its currency when the normal tool — selling reserves — has been taken away.

With reserves frozen, Russia could not buy rubles in the open market. So it reached for the two levers that remain when the FX war chest is gone: **the price of money** and **capital controls.**

On February 28, 2022, the Central Bank of Russia hiked its key policy rate from 9.5% to **20%** in a single move. A 20% rate does two things. First, it makes holding rubles enormously attractive — you earn 20% just for parking money in the currency — which slows the rush to sell. Second, and more importantly in a crisis, it chokes off the credit that would otherwise fund capital flight. Then came the controls: exporters (Russia still sold oil and gas, because Europe could not stop buying overnight) were *forced* to convert a large share of their hard-currency earnings into rubles, creating a captive, mandatory bid for the currency. Outflows were banned or heavily restricted, so the "depositors" could not get to the vault at all.

The combination worked, mechanically and predictably: forced demand from exporters, plus a wall blocking the supply of would-be sellers, plus a 20% reward for staying. The ruble did not just stabilize — with the exits welded shut and the export bid mandatory, it over-shot to the strong side. This is not a story about Russian financial genius; it is the standard emergency playbook for a small economy under a balance-of-payments attack, the same toolkit Malaysia used in 1998 and Iceland used in 2008. The lesson for asset valuation: **a managed exchange rate under capital controls is no longer a market price** — it tells you about the controls, not about the economy. A ruble at 55 in a controlled market was not a sign of strength; it was a sign that the market had been switched off.

#### Worked example: a 20% policy rate and capital controls reverse a currency collapse

Put numbers on the defense. Before the hike, the ruble's overnight rate was 9.5% and the currency was falling perhaps 5–10% a day. The cost of *shorting* the ruble — borrowing rubles to sell them — was cheap, so speculators piled on. After the hike to 20%, borrowing rubles to short them cost 20% annualized, roughly 0.055% a *day*, which on its own is a meaningful tax on a leveraged short held over weeks.

But the binding lever was the controls, not the rate. Russia's goods exports ran around \$50bn a *month* in 2022, dominated by energy sold for dollars and euros. Force exporters to convert, say, 80% of that into rubles, and you manufacture roughly \$40bn a month of mandatory ruble buying — a structural bid larger than almost any speculative selling flow. Simultaneously, ban residents from moving money abroad and you remove the largest natural seller. The net supply-demand picture flips from "everyone selling, no one buying" to "mandatory buyers, blocked sellers." A currency cannot fall when the only large flows are forced purchases. **The intuition: when you cannot defend a currency by spending reserves, you defend it by forcing demand (export-conversion rules) and blocking supply (outflow bans) — and a 20% rate pays holders to wait while you do.**

## The energy-to-inflation chain: how a war forces the Fed

Now the path that mattered most for a global investor who held neither rubles nor Russian assets. The 2022 war was, above all, an **energy and food supply shock** layered on top of an economy that was already running hot from pandemic stimulus. Russia was the world's largest natural-gas exporter and among the largest oil and wheat exporters; Ukraine was a breadbasket for wheat, corn, and sunflower oil. War and sanctions disrupted all of it.

Brent crude hit \$139.13 on March 7, 2022. European TTF gas — the benchmark that prices the gas burned across the continent — spiked toward €340/MWh, roughly ten times its pre-crisis norm, as Russia throttled pipeline flows and Europe scrambled for alternatives. Wheat jumped about 40%. These are not abstract commodity moves; they are the inputs to the consumer price index. Energy is in everything because everything has to be made, moved, and heated; food is the most visible line item in any household budget.

The European gas shock deserves a moment on its own, because it is where the energy channel and the *fiscal* channel collided. A €340/MWh gas price (against a norm closer to €20–30) is not just an inflation problem — it is an existential threat to energy-intensive industry (chemicals, fertilizer, steel, glass) and a cost-of-living crisis for households facing winter heating bills several times normal. European governments responded with a *fiscal* counter-move of staggering size: energy support packages — price caps on household bills, subsidies, and windfall taxes on energy producers — totaling on the order of €700–800 billion across the EU by late 2022. That is a war-driven fiscal impulse of a different kind than defense spending: it is a transfer to cushion the *energy* blow, financed by deficits, that simultaneously *supported demand* (and therefore kept inflation stickier) even as the European Central Bank was trying to tighten. This is the policy bind in miniature: the energy shock forced the central bank to hike to fight inflation, while it forced governments to spend to cushion the same shock — fiscal and monetary policy pulling in opposite directions, a tug-of-war that kept European inflation elevated longer than the US's. For an investor, the read was that European duration was even more exposed than US duration (two policy forces fighting), and that the euro itself was vulnerable — it briefly fell below parity with the dollar in 2022 for the first time in two decades, precisely the currency-channel symptom of an economy importing an energy shock it could not control.

![The war-to-rate-hike chain: a supply shock lifts oil, then headline inflation, then forces the central bank to tighten](/imgs/blogs/war-and-the-policy-response-how-conflict-reprices-assets-5.png)

The chain is mechanical. Higher oil and gas raised the energy component of CPI directly. Higher diesel raised freight, which raised the price of every shipped good. Higher gas raised fertilizer, which — together with the wheat shock — raised food. Headline US inflation, already elevated, pushed to roughly **9% by mid-2022**, a forty-year high. And a central bank cannot look at 9% inflation and do nothing, war or no war, because its legal mandate is price stability. So the Fed raised its target range from 0.25% to 5.50% — **525 basis points across eleven hikes in sixteen months**, the fastest cycle since Paul Volcker broke inflation in the early 1980s.

![The war-to-rate-hike chain in numbers: 2022 inflation and the Fed funds response on a dual axis](/imgs/blogs/war-and-the-policy-response-how-conflict-reprices-assets-8.png)

Here is the part that catches investors off guard. The war was bad for risk assets *not primarily because of the war*, but because of the **policy response to the inflation the war caused**. Higher rates raise the discount rate applied to every future cash flow, which lowers what stocks and long bonds are worth today. In 2022, US stocks fell about **19%** and the aggregate bond index fell about **13%** — the rare year when stocks and bonds fell *together*, wrecking the classic 60/40 portfolio (down roughly 16%). Diversification failed precisely because the same lever — a rate hike forced by war-driven inflation — was pushing both asset classes down at once. (The full anatomy of that 60/40 wreck is its own post in this series; linked below.)

The discount-rate mechanism is worth restating, because it is the heart of the series: an asset is worth the present value of its future cash flows, and the rate you discount by is set by policy. When the war forced the Fed to lift that rate, *everything priced off it* re-rated lower. For the full discount-rate channel mechanics, see the channel post linked at the end; the point here is that **war reached your portfolio through the Fed's reaction function, not through the front line.**

#### Worked example: the safe-haven rotation and its 2022 payoff

Now the defensive side. Imagine a US investor on February 23, 2022, the day before the invasion, holding a conventional balanced book. They make three policy-aware bets for the year ahead: rotate a slice into **gold** (the no-counterparty haven), tilt equity toward **defense and energy** (the fiscal and supply winners), and *shorten duration* on the bond side — that is, hold cash-like short Treasuries rather than long bonds, because the coming rate hikes will crush long-bond prices.

Score it over 2022. Gold ended the year roughly flat to modestly up in dollar terms (it touched ~\$2,070 in March on the haven bid, then gave much of it back as real rates rose — a crucial nuance we will return to), so call it ~0% versus a 19% loss on the S&P: a ~19-point relative win. The defense sector materially outperformed a market down 19%, as European rearmament and US supplemental spending bid up contractors; energy was *the* winning sector of 2022, up sharply while the index fell. And the duration call was the biggest one of all: long Treasuries lost on the order of 25–30% as the 10-year yield climbed from ~1.5% to ~4.3%, while short Treasuries lost almost nothing and then started *earning* 4–5% as rates rose. A book that shifted, say, 10% into gold, tilted equity toward defense/energy, and cut long-duration exposure would have lost a fraction of what the 60/40 lost. **The intuition: in a war-driven inflation shock, the winning trades are the ones that profit from the *policy response* — own the fiscal beneficiaries, own the no-counterparty haven, and get out of the duration the central bank is about to punish.**

![Gold is the safe-haven that holds in war: spot price from 2018 through the 2026 record run](/imgs/blogs/war-and-the-policy-response-how-conflict-reprices-assets-7.png)

A word of caution that the gold chart makes visible. Gold's haven reputation is real but conditional. In 2022 it *spiked* on the invasion and then *faded*, because the very same rate hikes that crushed stocks also raised the real yield — and gold, which pays no interest, competes with real yields. A higher real yield is a higher opportunity cost of holding metal, so gold struggled even as the war raged. The spectacular gold run that followed in 2024–26 (to an all-time high near \$5,589/oz in January 2026) was driven by a *different* policy story — central-bank buying and de-dollarization, a structural shift away from dollar reserves partly *triggered* by the 2022 reserve freeze itself. In other words, weaponizing the dollar gave every non-aligned central bank a reason to buy gold instead, which is policy reshaping a price years after the fact. The gold-and-real-rates mechanics get a full treatment in the linked commodities post.

This second-order effect is worth dwelling on, because it is the most important *long-run* consequence of the 2022 sanctions and it is invisible if you only watch the war headlines. For seventy years, a central bank's reflex when it accumulated a trade surplus was to park the proceeds in US Treasuries — the deepest, safest, most liquid asset on earth. The 2022 reserve freeze demonstrated, in public, that those Treasuries were only "safe" so long as the holder stayed on the right side of US foreign policy. For a central bank in Beijing, Riyadh, or Ankara, that was a revelation with a direct portfolio implication: a Treasury you can be locked out of is not, for *your* purposes, risk-free. The rational response is to diversify reserves *away* from claims that a rival government can freeze — and the only large, liquid reserve asset with **no counterparty and no government** is gold. Central banks bought gold at the fastest pace in decades after 2022, adding well over 1,000 tonnes a year, and that structural official-sector demand is the engine behind gold's run *despite* positive real yields — a combination that the old gold-versus-real-rates model said should not happen. The lesson is pure policy-and-markets: a financial weapon used once changes the *incentives* of everyone who might be targeted next, and those changed incentives reprice a multi-trillion-dollar asset class for years. When you weaponize the reserve currency, you teach the world to hold something else.

## The defense-fiscal path: war as a sector-specific stimulus

The third lever is the cleanest fiscal story in markets. A war is a demand shock for a specific industry, financed by the government. Defense outlays are deficit spending pointed at contractors, and they reprice that sector's earnings directly while enlarging the bond supply that the whole market must absorb.

![Defense spending is a war's fiscal footprint: US defense outlays as a share of GDP from WWII to today](/imgs/blogs/war-and-the-policy-response-how-conflict-reprices-assets-6.png)

The historical scale chart tells the story at a glance. US defense spending hit **37% of GDP in 1944** at the peak of World War II — a level of mobilization that bears no resemblance to anything since. Korea pulled it to ~14% in 1953, Vietnam to ~9% in 1968, the Reagan buildup to ~6% in 1986, and the post-Cold-War "peace dividend" let it drift down to a modern floor around **3.1–3.4% of GDP** by the 2020s. The 2022 war did not move the US number much — the US is not a combatant — but it triggered a structural rearmament in Europe, where Germany announced a €100bn special defense fund within days of the invasion and NATO members committed to lifting spending toward and beyond 2% of GDP. That is a multi-year fiscal impulse aimed squarely at the defense complex, and the European defense contractors re-rated higher on it.

The valuation logic is two-sided. On the equity side, a credible, sustained rise in defense budgets lifts the *earnings* of contractors and, because the market believes the higher spending will persist, often lifts their *multiple* too — both terms of the price go up. On the bond side, the same spending enlarges deficits and the supply of government bonds, which (per the fiscal-toolkit post) tends to lift the term premium and long yields. So defense spending is simultaneously bullish for one slice of equities and a modest headwind for long bonds — the fiscal lever pulling two channels in opposite directions on different assets.

The European rearmament after 2022 is the cleanest live example of why the *multiple*, not just the earnings, re-rates. Before the war, European defense was a sleepy, low-growth sector trading at modest multiples because investors assumed budgets would keep shrinking — the "peace dividend" was the consensus. The invasion shattered that assumption permanently. Germany's €100bn special fund, NATO's commitment to lift spending toward and past 2% of GDP (with talk of 3% and beyond by the mid-2020s), and a decade-long backlog of orders to replace stockpiles sent to Ukraine all signaled that defense budgets would now *grow* for a generation. A stock's price-to-earnings multiple is, at its core, a bet on the future growth and durability of its earnings; when the market shifted from "this sector is shrinking" to "this sector has a decade of guaranteed growth," the multiple expanded sharply even before the higher earnings actually arrived. Several European defense names doubled or tripled over 2022–2025. That is the policy mechanism in its purest form: a *credible, durable* change in government spending policy re-rated an entire sector's valuation, because valuation is a forecast and the forecast changed.

#### Worked example: a defense budget re-rating both terms of the price

Put the two-term move in numbers. Take a defense contractor earning \$5 per share, trading at a price-to-earnings multiple of 12 — so its stock is \$60. Now a war triggers a credible, sustained rearmament. Two things happen, and they compound. First, the order book swells, and over a few years earnings grow from \$5 to, say, \$7 per share — a 40% earnings increase. Second, because the market now believes defense spending will keep rising for a decade rather than shrink, it re-rates the *multiple* it is willing to pay from 12 to 18 (defense has become a growth story, not a value trap).

The new price is \$7 × 18 = **\$126**, up from \$60 — a **110% gain**. Notice that earnings rose only 40%; the other two-thirds of the return came from the *multiple expansion*, which is the market's verdict on the *durability of the policy*. That is the crucial asymmetry of a war-driven fiscal impulse: the biggest gains accrue not when the spending happens, but at the moment the market becomes convinced the spending will be *permanent*. **The intuition: a defense stock in a rearmament reprices on two levers at once — higher earnings (the spending) and a higher multiple (belief that the spending is durable) — and the multiple is the larger of the two, which is why you want to own the sector at the moment the policy regime changes, not after the budgets are already in the numbers.**

## Common misconceptions

**"War is bullish for stocks — markets climb a wall of worry."** Sometimes, not always, and the difference is *policy*. The 1990 Gulf War bottom came at the *start* of the war: the S&P fell ~20% into the August invasion, then rallied hard once the January air war went well and oil collapsed — "buy the invasion" worked because the energy shock proved brief and the Fed was *easing*. But 1973 was the opposite: the Yom Kippur War and the OPEC embargo quadrupled oil, the inflation forced a tightening bind, and the S&P fell ~48% over 1973–74. Same asset class, opposite outcomes — because in 1990 the policy backdrop was accommodative and the oil shock faded, while in 1973 the oil shock was durable and forced restrictive policy. The war is never the whole story; the policy response is.

**"Gold always goes up in a war."** Gold *spikes* on the initial haven rush, but its sustained path is governed by real interest rates, which are a policy variable. In 2022 gold touched ~\$2,070 in March and then *fell* through much of the year as the Fed's hikes pushed real yields up — the rate hikes that punished stocks also punished gold. Gold is a haven against *uncertainty and debasement*, not a haven against *high real rates*. Know which regime you are in.

**"Safe havens — the dollar and Treasuries — always work in a crisis."** They usually do, but a haven only works if the issuer's own policy is credible, and that can fail. In the 2025 tariff shock — a policy war rather than a shooting war — US Treasuries sold off *during* the risk-off episode (the 10-year yield rose ~50bp in a week while stocks were crashing), and the dollar fell more than 10% in the first half, its worst H1 since 1973. When the source of the shock is US *policy itself*, US assets stop acting as the refuge. The haven property is a statement about credibility, not a law of nature.

**"Sanctions don't really work — look at the ruble recovering."** The ruble's recovery to 55 was *manufactured* by capital controls and a 20% rate, not a sign that sanctions failed. A controlled exchange rate is not a market price. The sanctions did their damage elsewhere: they cut Russia off from Western technology and capital markets, forced a deep restructuring of its trade toward discounted sales to China and India, and — through the reserve freeze — triggered a global wave of central-bank gold buying and de-dollarization that is reshaping reserve management to this day. Judge a financial weapon by its second-order effects, not by the headline exchange rate.

**"Higher oil from a war is automatically a big inflation problem."** Only if supply cannot adjust. In 1973 and 2022, supply was tight and the shock fed straight into CPI. But the Middle-East flare-ups of 2023–25 produced only brief oil spikes that faded within days, because US shale production and spare OPEC capacity could fill the gap. The same trigger — conflict near oil — produced a durable inflation shock in one decade and a non-event in another, entirely because of the supply elasticity of the moment.

## Case studies: the range of war-to-policy outcomes

The deep case is 2022, which we have already dissected. Now place it in a gallery, because the whole point of the policy lens is that *the same kind of event produces wildly different prices depending on the policy backdrop.* Here are four anchors that span the range.

### 1973: the war that ended in Volcker

In October 1973, the Yom Kippur War triggered an Arab oil embargo against nations supporting Israel. The posted price of Arabian Light crude went from about \$3 to about \$12 a barrel — a **roughly fourfold** increase by 1974. This is the archetype, and the cleanest example of the energy-to-policy chain doing maximum damage. The oil shock collided with an economy already running loose monetary policy, and the result was **stagflation**: high inflation *and* stagnant growth at the same time, a combination that defeats the standard policy playbook (you cannot cut rates to fight the recession without feeding the inflation, and you cannot hike to fight the inflation without deepening the recession).

The S&P 500 fell about **48% from January 1973 to October 1974** — in real, inflation-adjusted terms, worse. Both stocks and bonds suffered; the only winners were hard assets. Gold, freed from its \$35 peg as Bretton Woods collapsed, embarked on the run that would take it to ~\$850 by January 1980. And the policy bind that the embargo opened did not close until Paul Volcker took rates to roughly **19%** in 1981 to finally break the back of inflation, triggering a double-dip recession but launching a forty-year bond bull market. The lesson: an energy shock that forces a durable inflation regime is the most dangerous kind of war for a portfolio, because the *cure* — restrictive policy held for years — is itself a multi-year headwind for risk assets. (Volcker's repricing of everything is its own post in this series.)

It is worth understanding *why* 1973 was so much worse than the raw oil number alone would suggest, because the answer is entirely about policy. The Fed of the early 1970s, under Arthur Burns, had been running an accommodative policy and was reluctant to inflict a recession to fight inflation — there is a long-running debate about how much political pressure shaped that reluctance. Inflation expectations had already become *unanchored*: workers and firms had come to expect rising prices and built them into wage demands and contracts, so the oil shock landed on an economy primed to pass it through rather than absorb it. That is the difference between a one-time price-level jump and a self-sustaining inflation. When the central bank lacks the credibility (or the will) to keep expectations anchored, an energy shock does not fade — it embeds, and then only a Volcker-scale shock to credibility can dislodge it. Contrast that with 2022: the Fed, for all its initial "transitory" misjudgment, still possessed forty years of hard-won anti-inflation credibility, and it spent that credibility decisively with 525bp of hikes. Inflation came back down to the 2–3% range by 2024–25 without a 1970s-style decade of stagflation. The same energy shock, applied to an economy with a credible central bank, produced a sharp-but-shorter inflation episode rather than a generational one. The policy variable — central-bank credibility — was the difference between a 48% bear market that took years to recover and a 19% drawdown that was erased within months of the pause.

### WWII: pegging the bond market to pay for a war

World War II is the template for how a great power *finances* a total war, and it is pure policy. Rather than let interest rates rise as the government issued an avalanche of debt, the Fed agreed to **peg** Treasury yields: it held the long government bond at **2.5%** and Treasury bills at **0.375%** from 1942 to 1951, buying whatever quantity of bonds was needed to keep yields at those levels. This is **financial repression** — capping the cost of government borrowing by force — and it let the US fund the war cheaply even as federal debt soared to about **106% of GDP by 1946**.

The catch fell on bondholders. With yields pinned at 2.5% while inflation ran much higher in the post-war years, the *real* return on holding government bonds was deeply negative for a decade — the debt was quietly eroded by inflation, transferring wealth from savers to the government. The peg ended only with the **1951 Treasury-Fed Accord**, which freed the central bank to set policy independently again. The valuation lesson is stark: when a government pegs yields to finance a war, *bondholders are the designated losers.* If you can see the peg coming, you do not want to be the one holding the long bond at a fixed low yield while inflation runs above it.

#### Worked example: how a yield peg erodes a bondholder in wartime

Make the WWII repression concrete. You hold a \$1,000 long Treasury bond pegged to yield **2.5%**, so it pays you \$25 a year. In a normal world, if inflation rose, the bond's price would fall and its yield would rise to compensate you — that is the market doing its job. But under the peg, the Fed stands ready to buy unlimited bonds to *prevent* the yield from rising above 2.5%. So your yield is frozen at 2.5% no matter what inflation does.

Now let post-war inflation run at, say, **8%** (it spiked even higher in 1946–47 as wartime price controls lifted). Your **real** return is the nominal yield minus inflation: 2.5% − 8% = **−5.5%** a year. In purchasing-power terms, \$1,000 of bonds compounding at −5.5% a year is worth, in real terms, about \$1,000 × (0.945)^10 ≈ **\$568 after a decade** — you have lost over 40% of your real wealth simply by holding the "safe" asset. Meanwhile the government, which *issued* that bond to pay for the war, sees the real value of its debt shrink by the same proportion: it borrowed in expensive dollars and repays in cheap ones. **The intuition: a wartime yield peg is a wealth transfer from bondholders to the government, executed not through default but through the quiet arithmetic of a fixed nominal yield minus a rising inflation rate — which is exactly why "is the central bank still independent, or is it being asked to finance the war?" is the single most important question a bond investor can ask in a conflict.**

This is not merely a historical curiosity. It is the policy template every heavily indebted government reaches for when it needs to finance a crisis cheaply, and it is why the phrase "fiscal dominance" — the central bank losing its independence to the financing needs of the treasury — is a recurring fear in modern markets carrying record peacetime debt loads. The 2020s arrived with US debt above 100% of GDP and deficits running over 6%; in a major war, the temptation to lean on the central bank to keep financing costs down would be enormous, and the bondholder would once again be the one asked to pay.

### Gulf War 1990: "buy the invasion"

The opposite of 1973. When Iraq invaded Kuwait in August 1990, oil roughly doubled from ~\$17 to ~\$36 a barrel and the S&P fell about **20%** into the invasion on fears of a 1973 repeat. But the repeat never came. The US-led coalition's January 1991 air campaign went swiftly, Saudi spare capacity and strategic reserves cushioned the supply hit, oil collapsed back down, and stocks rallied off the lows. The bottom, as traders put it, was the *start* of the war, not the end. Why the benign outcome? Two policy facts: the oil shock proved brief because spare capacity existed, and the Fed was in an *easing* posture, not a tightening one. With no durable inflation forced onto the central bank, the war became a buying opportunity rather than a regime change.

### The 2023–25 Middle-East premia: not every war is a 1973

The most recent and arguably most instructive cases are the ones where the chain *failed to fire.* The Israel-Hamas war that began on October 7, 2023, and the unprecedented direct Israel-Iran strikes of 2024–25 each produced sharp but **short-lived** oil spikes — moves of a few percent that faded within days. In October 2024, for instance, oil rose only from roughly \$72 to ~\$80 on the Israel-Iran exchange before settling back. There was no inflation surge, no forced tightening, no equity bear market from the conflict itself.

The reason is a structural change in the energy market: the US shale revolution turned America into the world's largest oil producer, and OPEC+ has carried meaningful spare capacity. When conflict threatens supply, the market now knows that barrels can be added quickly, so the risk premium is small and transient. The policy chain — oil to CPI to rate hike — simply does not engage when the oil move does not stick. **This is the single most important calibration for a modern investor: the war-to-inflation transmission depends entirely on energy-supply elasticity, and today that elasticity is much higher than it was in 1973.** The same headline ("missiles exchanged near oil infrastructure") was a regime-defining catastrophe in 1973 and a two-day blip in 2024 — because of policy and production capacity, not because the geopolitics were any less frightening.

There is a further modern wrinkle worth naming: not every "war" that reprices assets is fought with weapons. The 2025 tariff shock was a *policy* war — a trade conflict — and it produced one of the sharpest cross-asset repricings of the decade without a single shot fired. When the US announced sweeping "reciprocal" tariffs in April 2025, the S&P 500 fell nearly 19% from its February high into early April, and crucially the supposed safe havens *did not work the way they were supposed to*: the 10-year Treasury yield *rose* about 50 basis points in the very week stocks were crashing (instead of falling as havens do), and the dollar fell more than 10% over the first half of 2025, its worst first half since 1973. Why? Because the shock *was US policy itself*. When the source of the danger is the credibility of the issuer of the haven assets, those assets stop being a refuge — exactly the failure mode flagged in the Foundations section. Gold, the only *unconditional* haven, soared. The tariff war is its own case study in this series, but it belongs here too, because it is the purest demonstration of the post's thesis: assets reprice through policy, and the most violent repricings come not from the *size* of a shock but from its effect on the *credibility* of the institutions that markets had assumed were safe.

The oil-shock comparison chart makes the spread visible: 1973's fourfold move and 2022's surge to \$139 are in a different league from the modest, fading reactions of 2003, 1990's swift reversal, and 2024.

## What it means for asset values: the playbook

Pull the threads together into something you can act on. When a major conflict erupts, do not start with the war map — start with the **policy diagnosis**, because that is what sets the prices.

**First, gauge the energy-supply elasticity.** Is the world tight on oil and gas, or is there ample shale and OPEC spare capacity to fill a gap? This single question decides whether you are in a 1973 (durable shock, forced tightening, bear market) or a 2024 (brief spike, no policy response, non-event). In a tight market, position for inflation and rate hikes; in a slack one, fade the spike.

**Second, identify the sanctions vector.** Which currency, which reserves, which banks are exposed? A reserve freeze or SWIFT cutoff is a one-way wrecking ball for the target currency and a windfall for the financial-weapon-proof havens (gold, and increasingly non-dollar reserve assets). And watch the *second-order* effect: weaponizing the dollar accelerates de-dollarization, which is structurally bullish gold for years. Note too what sanctions do to the *target's own assets*: a sanctioned country's stocks and bonds can be marked to zero overnight for Western investors not because the underlying businesses vanished but because the *ability to transact* did — MSCI removed Russia from its emerging-market indices at a price of effectively zero in March 2022, a stark reminder that for a sanctioned asset, liquidity and legality are the value, and policy can erase both in a day.

To make the cross-asset picture concrete, here is the 2022 scorecard the policy lens predicts and the market delivered: **energy** was the year's best-performing sector (the supply shock); **defense** outperformed a falling market (the fiscal impulse); **gold** held roughly flat while equities fell ~19% (the haven, capped by rising real rates); **long bonds** lost 25–30% and the aggregate bond index ~13% (the rate-hike channel); **European equities and the euro** underperformed US peers (importing the energy shock); **emerging-market assets** broadly suffered from the strong dollar and risk-off; and **Russian assets** went to zero for Western holders (the sanctions vector). Every one of those moves traces back to a policy lever, not to the battlefield. That is the whole point: if you had the policy map, you had the scorecard.

**Third, map the fiscal impulse.** Who is rearming, and is the spending credible and sustained? Sustained defense budgets re-rate the defense complex's earnings and multiple, while enlarging deficits that pressure long bonds. Own the contractors; be wary of the duration.

**Fourth, check whether the havens are credible.** Gold works as a haven against uncertainty and debasement but struggles against high real rates — so know which regime you are in. The dollar and Treasuries work as havens *unless the shock originates in US policy itself*, as the 2025 tariff episode showed when both sold off in a risk-off move. A haven is a credibility bet, not a law.

There is a meta-lesson sitting on top of all four. Markets are forward-looking, so the move happens not when the war does, but when the *policy response becomes clear* — and that response is often the opposite of what the headlines would suggest. In 1990 the bottom was the invasion, because the market correctly anticipated that the policy backdrop (easing Fed, ample oil) would contain the shock. In 1973 the damage compounded for two years, because the policy response (a central bank unwilling to inflict a recession) let the inflation embed. The same structure recurs in every case: ask not "how bad is the war?" but "what policy will it force, is that policy credible, and has the market already priced it?" The conflict is the easy part to see and the wrong thing to trade. The policy reaction function — what the central bank *must* do given its mandate, what the fiscal authority *can* afford, whether the targeted currency *can* be defended — is the hard part to see and the right thing to trade. That is the entire discipline this series teaches, applied to the most violent inputs markets ever receive.

**The signals to watch:** the oil price *path* (spiking-and-holding versus spiking-and-fading), the inflation print in the months after the shock (this is what forces the central bank), the policy-rate trajectory (the actual transmission to your discount rate), the target currency's reserve and capital-control news (the sanctions scorecard), and gold's behavior relative to real yields (haven-on versus rate-pressured). **What would invalidate the read:** if oil spikes but fades within days, the whole inflation-and-hike chain never engages and you should not position for it; if a central bank under attack imposes capital controls, its exchange rate stops being informative and you should stop trading it as a market price.

The deepest takeaway of the whole post: a war's effect on your portfolio is not measured in territory or casualties. It is measured in **which policy levers the conflict forces governments and central banks to pull** — the sanction, the deficit, the rate hike, the flight to safety — and in whether the structure of the world (energy supply, reserve credibility, the central bank's reaction function) lets those levers actually bite. Read the policy, and you have read the price.
