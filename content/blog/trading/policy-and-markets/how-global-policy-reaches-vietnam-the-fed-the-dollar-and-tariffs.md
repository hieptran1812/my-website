> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Vietnam does not set the policy that prices its assets; it imports it. A Fed rate decision moves the dollar, the dollar pushes on the dong, the State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) is forced to choose between spending reserves and letting the currency slide, and the VN-Index reprices on the back of that choice — and on top of the financial channel sits a trade channel, where a US tariff lands directly on the margin of an export-led economy that sends ~29% of its goods to America.
>
> - The **financial channel**: Fed hike → stronger dollar (DXY up) → upward pressure on USD/VND → the SBV must defend the band (burn reserves, tighten) or let the dong weaken (import inflation). Either road tightens financial conditions in Vietnam.
> - The **impossible trinity**: a country can keep at most two of {stable currency, free capital flow, independent rate}. Vietnam keeps a managed band and controls capital — so its interest rate is only *half* its own; it has to track the Fed to hold the band.
> - The **trade channel**: the 2025 US tariff on Vietnam was announced at **46%** (the 4th-highest "reciprocal" rate), negotiated to **20%** by July, with **40%** on transshipped goods. On a thin export margin, a 20-point tariff is the difference between profit and break-even.
> - **The squeeze**: when the Fed hikes *and* the US raises tariffs at the same time, a small open economy is hit on both the financial channel and the trade channel at once. The one number to remember: **~29%** of Vietnam's exports go to the US, so the trade channel is not a side issue — it is a first-order shock.

In the first days of April 2025, two clocks were ticking in Hanoi at once. One was financial: the US dollar had spent the prior two years near multi-decade highs, and every basis point the Federal Reserve held at the top of its range was a basis point of gravitational pull on the Vietnamese dong. The other was a trade clock: on April 2, in a Rose Garden ceremony, the United States announced a "reciprocal" tariff schedule that put a **46% rate** on goods from Vietnam — the fourth-highest number on a board of fifty-seven trading partners. For an economy whose exports are roughly the size of its entire GDP, and where close to a third of those exports are bought by American consumers, that single number was an existential headline.

Neither of those levers was pulled in Vietnam. The Fed sets US rates to manage US inflation and US employment; the White House set the tariff to rebalance US trade. Vietnam was, in both cases, on the receiving end of a policy made several thousand miles away. And yet both reached deep into Vietnamese asset prices — the exchange rate, the cost of credit, the earnings of the country's biggest exporters, and the VN-Index itself. This is the defining feature of a **small open economy**: it does not get to write the rules of the global financial and trade system; it has to *react* to them. Vietnam imports policy the way it imports oil and semiconductors.

This post is a map of that import process. We trace two channels. The **financial channel** runs Fed → dollar → USD/VND → the SBV's forced reaction → the VN-Index. The **trade channel** runs US tariff → exporter margin → corporate earnings → the equity market and the trade balance. We will build each from first principles, ground each in a worked dollar example, and finish on the question this whole series keeps coming back to: *given the policy, what is the asset worth now, and why?*

## Foundations: what "importing policy" actually means

Start with the term that does all the work here: **small open economy**. "Open" means goods, services, and capital cross the border freely enough that the rest of the world's prices and interest rates show up inside the country. "Small" is the load-bearing word — it means the country is a *price-taker*, not a *price-setter*. When the United States changes its interest rate, the whole world's cost of dollars changes, and a country like Vietnam has to take that new dollar price as given and adapt. When a tiny country changes *its* rate, the world's cost of dollars does not move at all. The causality is one-directional: big economies export their policy; small economies import it.

Vietnam is a textbook case. Its economy is heavily trade-dependent — the value of its exports plus imports is roughly 1.7 to 2 times its GDP, one of the highest trade-to-GDP ratios of any large emerging market. Its largest export market by a wide margin is the United States, which buys close to **29%** of everything Vietnam ships abroad. Its currency, the dong (VND), is not freely floating; the SBV manages it inside a band. And the dong is not a global reserve currency — when investors get scared, they do not run *to* the dong, they run *from* it, toward the dollar. Every one of those facts makes Vietnam a receiver of other people's decisions.

Now the two channels, defined plainly:

- **The financial channel** is about the *price of money*. The Fed sets the price of dollars. Because dollars are the world's reserve currency — the unit in which oil is priced, in which most cross-border loans are written, in which central banks hold their reserves — the Fed's rate is the gravitational center of the entire global financial system. When that center tightens, money is pulled out of the periphery (emerging markets like Vietnam) and back toward the dollar. This shows up first in the **exchange rate**, then in **capital flows**, then in the **cost of domestic credit**, and finally in **asset prices**.

- **The trade channel** is about the *price of access to a market*. A tariff is a tax the importing country charges at its own border on goods coming in. A 20% US tariff on a Vietnamese shirt means that when the shirt crosses into the United States, someone pays the US government an extra 20% of its value. That "someone" is split, through negotiation, between the American buyer (who pays a higher price) and the Vietnamese seller (who accepts a lower one). For an export-led economy, the trade channel hits the *earnings* of its companies directly, without passing through the exchange rate first.

The crucial insight of this post is that these two channels are usually *correlated in the wrong direction* for Vietnam. A strong-dollar era (financial channel tightening) tends to coincide with a more protectionist, America-first policy posture (trade channel tightening). When both fire at once — as they did across 2022 to 2025 — a small open economy gets squeezed from both sides simultaneously, with limited tools to push back. Understanding the squeeze means understanding each channel, and then understanding the constraint that ties the SBV's hands: the impossible trinity.

If you want the deeper mechanics of *how* a central bank's rate change propagates through an economy in general, this series covers it in [the discount-rate channel](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-discount-rate-channel-how-rates-reprice-cash-flows) and [the currency channel](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-currency-channel-rate-differentials-and-carry); here we specialize all of it to the case of a country on the receiving end.

### Why the dollar, specifically, is the lever that matters

It is worth pausing on *why* the Fed — and not the European Central Bank or the Bank of Japan — is the policy-maker whose decisions reach Hanoi hardest. The answer is the special role of the dollar as the world's **reserve currency**, and it has three concrete parts that all point at Vietnam.

First, the dollar is the **invoicing currency** of global trade. The vast majority of cross-border transactions — including a large share of trade that does not involve the United States at all — are priced and settled in dollars. When a Vietnamese factory buys cotton from India or machinery from South Korea, the invoice is very often in dollars. So the cost of Vietnam's *imports* is, in part, a dollar cost; a stronger dollar raises that cost regardless of what the Indian rupee or Korean won is doing.

Second, the dollar is the **funding currency** of the global economy. A great deal of the world's cross-border lending — to companies, banks, and governments in emerging markets — is written in dollars. Vietnamese banks and corporations that borrowed in dollars to fund expansion owe those dollars back regardless of the dong's value. When the dollar strengthens, that debt becomes heavier in dong terms: the same \$100 million loan that cost 2.31 trillion dong to repay in 2020 costs 2.61 trillion dong in 2025, a 13% increase in the local-currency burden with no new borrowing. This is the "original sin" of emerging-market finance — borrowing in a currency you cannot print.

Third, the dollar is the **reserve asset** central banks hold. When the SBV defends the dong, the ammunition it spends is dollar reserves (mostly US Treasury securities). The country's entire crisis buffer is denominated in the very currency whose price the Fed controls. So the Fed is not one foreign central bank among many — it is the manager of the unit in which Vietnam invoices its imports, services its debt, and holds its reserves. That is why "global policy reaches Vietnam" is, first and foremost, a story about the Fed and the dollar.

## The financial channel, step by step

### Step 1: the Fed sets the price of dollars

The lever at the very top of the chain is the federal funds rate — the rate at which US banks lend reserves to each other overnight, which the Fed steers by setting a target range. It is the anchor for every other dollar interest rate, from the yield on a 3-month Treasury bill to the rate on a 30-year mortgage. When the Fed raises that range, holding a dollar (in the form of a US Treasury bill) pays more. When it lowers the range, dollars pay less.

The 2022–2025 cycle is the cleanest example in living memory. From a floor of 0.25% in early 2022, the Fed raised the upper bound of its target range to 5.50% by July 2023 — **525 basis points in sixteen months**, the fastest hiking campaign since Paul Volcker broke inflation in the early 1980s. Then, having held there through most of 2024 and the first three quarters of 2025 (waiting to see whether the new tariffs would push inflation up or jobs down), the Fed cut three times in late 2025, bringing the upper bound to 3.75%. The shape of that path — a steep climb, a long plateau, a gentle descent — is the entire backdrop against which Vietnam's currency and stock market moved.

![Step chart of the Fed funds target range upper bound from 2015 to 2025 showing the 2022 to 2023 hikes to 5.5 percent and 2025 cuts](/imgs/blogs/how-global-policy-reaches-vietnam-the-fed-the-dollar-and-tariffs-8.png)

Why does this matter for Vietnam at all? Because of the **rate differential**. Capital, all else equal, flows toward where it is paid more. If a US Treasury bill yields 5.5% and a Vietnamese government bill yields, say, 4%, a global investor holding dong-denominated assets is earning 1.5 percentage points less *and* taking currency risk on a non-reserve currency. The rational move is to sell the dong asset, buy the dollar asset, and pocket the higher, safer yield. Multiply that by millions of investors and you get a steady outward pull on the dong every time the Fed's rate climbs above what Vietnam can comfortably pay.

The 2023 episode shows both the freedom and the limit of Vietnam's position in one move. With the Fed still parked near its 5.25–5.50% peak, the SBV did something striking: it *cut* its refinancing rate from 6.0% to 4.5% in four steps between March and June 2023, easing into a domestic slowdown even as the Fed stayed tight. That is the partial monetary independence the trilemma allows — a small country *can* diverge from the Fed for a while. But look at what paid for that divergence: through 2023 the dong was allowed to drift weaker (USD/VND climbed from about 23,633 at the end of 2022 to 24,260 at the end of 2023), and the SBV leaned harder on its capital controls and credit quota to keep the easing from leaking out as an FX crisis. Vietnam bought monetary independence by spending it on the *other two* legs of the trilemma — a managed depreciation plus tighter capital throttling. That is the trilemma's iron logic: every choice has a price paid somewhere on the triangle.

### Step 2: the dollar (DXY) responds

The aggregate of all that capital being pulled toward dollars shows up in the **US Dollar Index (DXY)** — a measure of the dollar's value against a basket of major currencies (euro, yen, pound, and a few others). When the Fed hikes and US yields rise relative to the rest of the world, the dollar strengthens and DXY rises. When the Fed cuts, or when something dents confidence in US policy, DXY falls.

Here is where 2025 throws an instructive curveball. You would expect a high-rate, hawkish dollar to stay strong. Instead, DXY *fell more than 10% in the first half of 2025* — its worst first-half performance since 1973, the year the Bretton Woods system finally collapsed. It dropped from around 110 in January to a low near 98 in April, right as the tariff shock hit. This is the second-order lesson of the financial channel: the dollar is not just a yield story; it is also a *credibility* story. When the world worried that US trade and fiscal policy had become erratic — a 46% tariff one week, a 90-day pause the next — some of the safe-haven premium that normally lives in the dollar drained away. The dollar weakened *despite* high rates because policy uncertainty made dollar assets feel less safe.

![Line chart of the US Dollar Index in 2025 falling from about 110 in January to a low near 98 in April](/imgs/blogs/how-global-policy-reaches-vietnam-the-fed-the-dollar-and-tariffs-7.png)

For Vietnam, a *weaker* dollar in 2025 was a quiet relief on the financial channel — it took some pressure off USD/VND — even as the *trade* channel was tightening hard. That decoupling is exactly why you have to trace the channels separately. We will return to it in the case studies. But for the general mechanics, hold the standard case in mind: Fed hikes → dollar strengthens → pressure on the dong.

### Step 3: USD/VND comes under pressure

The exchange rate USD/VND is quoted as the number of dong it takes to buy one dollar. A *rising* USD/VND means the dong is *weakening* (it takes more dong to buy a dollar). When the dollar strengthens globally and capital is being pulled out of Vietnamese assets, the demand for dollars (to take money out) rises and the demand for dong falls — and USD/VND wants to climb.

The historical record shows a striking one-directional drift. The dong has weakened against the dollar almost every single year for the last half-decade. The chart below traces the period-end rate: from about 23,100 dong per dollar at the end of 2020 to roughly 26,100 by the end of 2025 — a cumulative depreciation of around 13%. There is no year in that window where the dong meaningfully *strengthened*. That is what a managed-but-crawling currency in a strong-dollar world looks like: the SBV does not let the dong collapse, but it lets it slide steadily, a controlled retreat rather than a rout.

![Line chart of USD VND from 2020 to 2025 climbing from 23100 to 26100 dong per dollar, the dong weakening about 13 percent](/imgs/blogs/how-global-policy-reaches-vietnam-the-fed-the-dollar-and-tariffs-3.png)

#### Worked example: a Fed hike, the rate differential, and the SBV's bill

Let us make the rate-differential channel concrete with dollars and dong. Suppose at the start of a year the Fed funds rate sits at 4.5% and a Vietnamese 1-year government bill yields 4.0%. The differential is a manageable 0.5 percentage points — small enough that the dong's modest expected depreciation roughly offsets it, and capital sits still.

Now the Fed hikes by 100 basis points to 5.5%, and Vietnam, for domestic reasons (it wants to support growth), keeps its bill at 4.0%. The differential blows out to **1.5 percentage points** in the dollar's favor. Consider a global fund holding \$100 million in Vietnamese government bills. Each year it now earns \$4.0 million in dong, versus the \$5.5 million it could earn risk-free in dollars — a \$1.5 million annual penalty for staying in dong, *before* any currency loss. If the fund also expects the dong to weaken ~3% over the year (the historical drift), that is another \$3 million of expected currency loss on its \$100 million, for a total expected drag of \$4.5 million versus the dollar alternative. The rational fund sells.

When enough funds make that calculation, USD/VND is bid up. To stop the dong from breaching the bottom of its band, the SBV steps in and *sells dollars from its reserves* to buy dong, propping up the dong's value. Say the SBV has to sell \$5 billion of reserves over the quarter to absorb the outflow. That \$5 billion is gone from the war chest. If Vietnam's total reserves are around \$90 billion (roughly three months of imports), spending \$5 billion is over 5% of the buffer in a single quarter — and reserves measured in months-of-imports are exactly what rating agencies and the IMF watch. **The intuition: a Fed hike that widens the rate differential by 100 basis points doesn't cost Vietnam an interest payment — it costs the SBV billions of dollars of reserves to keep the dong from falling, or it forces the SBV to raise Vietnamese rates and choke its own economy instead.**

### Step 4: the SBV's forced choice

This is the heart of the financial channel, and the reason a small open economy has so little freedom. When USD/VND is pushing up against the top of the band, the SBV has exactly two real options, and both are costly.

**Option A — defend the band.** The SBV sells dollar reserves to buy dong (propping the currency) and/or raises domestic interest rates (making dong assets more attractive again, narrowing the differential). Defending the band keeps the currency stable — which protects companies and banks that have borrowed in dollars, and keeps imported inflation in check — but it *drains reserves* (reducing the country's crisis buffer) and *tightens domestic credit* (a rate hike to defend the currency is a rate hike the domestic economy did not ask for). In effect, the SBV imports the Fed's tightening.

**Option B — let the dong slide.** The SBV widens the band or simply moves the central rate higher, letting the dong weaken. This *preserves reserves* and *avoids a domestic rate hike*, but a weaker dong makes every import — oil, machinery, raw materials for the very factories that drive exports — more expensive in dong terms. That is **imported inflation**, and for a country that imports most of its energy and a large share of its manufacturing inputs, it is a real cost that shows up in the price of everything.

Notice that both roads lead to the same place for the stock market. Defend the band, and you get higher domestic rates plus tighter credit — which lowers the value of Vietnamese equities (a higher discount rate compresses the multiple, and tighter credit slows the economy). Let the dong slide, and you get imported inflation plus a hit to dollar-borrowers' balance sheets — which also lowers equity values. The SBV's choice is between two flavors of pain; there is no costless option when the dollar is surging.

![Branching graph showing a strong dollar shock forcing the SBV to either defend the band by draining reserves or let the dong slide and import inflation, both ending in a lower VN-Index](/imgs/blogs/how-global-policy-reaches-vietnam-the-fed-the-dollar-and-tariffs-5.png)

For the institutional detail of *how* the SBV operates — its credit-growth quotas, refinancing and discount rates, open-market operations, and the mechanics of the managed band — see [the SBV toolkit](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-sbv-toolkit-credit-quotas-rates-and-the-managed-dong). The point here is narrower: whatever tools it uses, the SBV's freedom to use them is constrained by the dollar, and that constraint has a name.

#### Worked example: how long the SBV's reserves can hold the band

The reserve-defense option has a hard arithmetic limit, and putting numbers on it shows why "defend the band" is never an unlimited promise. Suppose Vietnam holds **\$90 billion** of FX reserves — a respectable buffer, but a key benchmark is *months of imports*, and with annual imports running around \$360 billion, that \$90 billion covers exactly **three months** of imports, right at the edge of what the IMF treats as an adequacy floor.

Now a strong-dollar episode triggers a persistent outflow, and the SBV decides to defend rather than let the dong slide. Say it has to sell \$3 billion of reserves a month to absorb the net dollar demand and keep USD/VND inside the band. After one month, reserves are \$87 billion (2.9 months of imports). After three months, \$81 billion (2.7 months). After six months of sustained defense, \$72 billion — down to **2.0 months of imports**, a level that starts flashing red on every sovereign-risk dashboard and can itself trigger a rating downgrade and *more* outflows (a doom loop).

So the SBV does the math the market does: defending the band at \$3 billion a month is only sustainable for a handful of months before the buffer itself becomes the problem. That is why, in a prolonged strong-dollar regime, the SBV cannot purely defend — it *must* eventually let the dong crawl weaker to conserve reserves, which is exactly the controlled-depreciation pattern the USD/VND chart shows. **The intuition: the SBV's "defend the band" option is a bucket of dollars with a measurable bottom — at a \$3 billion-a-month burn, a \$90 billion bucket buys roughly half a year, after which the only options left are a domestic rate hike or a managed depreciation. The band is defended with a finite resource, and the market knows the size of the bucket.**

## The impossible trinity: why Vietnam's rate is only half its own

The reason the SBV is *forced* to choose — rather than simply doing what is best for Vietnam regardless of the Fed — is one of the most durable results in international economics. It is called the **impossible trinity** (or the trilemma), and it says a country can have at most *two* of the following three things at once:

1. **A stable exchange rate** (a fixed or tightly managed currency).
2. **Free movement of capital** (money can flow in and out without restriction).
3. **An independent monetary policy** (the central bank sets its own interest rate for domestic conditions).

You cannot have all three. The logic is airtight. Suppose you want both a stable currency *and* free capital flow. Then if your interest rate differs from the world's, capital will flood in or out (chasing the yield gap) and break your currency peg — so to keep the currency stable, your rate *must* match the world's. You have lost monetary independence. Conversely, if you want both an independent rate *and* free capital flow, then whenever your rate diverges from the world's, your currency must move (float) to absorb the capital flows — you have lost the stable exchange rate. The only way to keep *both* a stable currency *and* an independent rate is to slam the door on capital flows, so the yield gap cannot trigger an exodus.

![Matrix showing the impossible trinity for Vietnam: it keeps a managed band and capital controls and gives up a fully independent interest rate](/imgs/blogs/how-global-policy-reaches-vietnam-the-fed-the-dollar-and-tariffs-2.png)

Vietnam's choice is clear from its behavior. It keeps option 1 — a managed USD/VND band (a roughly ±5% range around a daily central rate that the SBV moves in a slow crawl). And it gives up option 2 — Vietnam *restricts* capital flows. The dong is not fully convertible; foreign investors face quotas, registration requirements, and frictions moving large sums in and out; and the SBV's signature tool is the **credit-growth quota**, an annual ceiling it assigns each bank on how much its loan book may grow. That quota is, in effect, a capital control on the *domestic* credit channel — it lets the SBV throttle how much money the banking system creates regardless of where the global rate sits.

What does Vietnam get in return? It gets to keep *some* monetary independence — it can ease to support growth even when the Fed is on hold, as it did in 2023 when it cut its refinancing rate from 6.0% to 4.5% in four moves while the Fed was still near its peak. But the independence is partial, not full. Because the band is only managed (±5%), not slammed shut, and because capital is throttled but not sealed, the SBV cannot drift *too* far from the Fed without the dong taking strain. Its rate is, in a real sense, *half* its own: it has room to maneuver in the short run, but over any longer horizon it must track the gravitational pull of US rates to hold the band. That is the trilemma in action.

#### Worked example: what Vietnam gives up to hold the band

Put a number on the trade-off. Imagine the Fed is at 5.5% and Vietnam, left entirely to its own devices, would want a 3.0% policy rate to stimulate a soft domestic economy — a 2.5-point gap below the dollar.

If Vietnam had *fully free capital flow* and tried to run that 3.0% rate while holding the band, here is the mechanism that breaks it. A Vietnamese company or wealthy individual could borrow dong at ~3%, convert to dollars, and park the money in US Treasuries at 5.5%, pocketing a 2.5-point spread *with no currency risk as long as the band holds* — a near-riskless arbitrage. On \$1 billion of such "carry," that is \$25 million a year of free money, and capital would pour out of dong and into dollars to capture it. The outflow would force USD/VND up through the top of the band; to defend it, the SBV would burn reserves until it ran out — at which point the band would break and the dong would gap weaker, exactly the outcome the band exists to prevent.

So Vietnam does *not* allow that free flow. The credit quota and FX restrictions mean the company cannot freely borrow dong and ship \$1 billion offshore to harvest the carry. The capital control is the price Vietnam pays to run a 3.0% rate while the Fed is at 5.5% *and* keep the dong stable. **The intuition: the gap between the rate Vietnam wants (3.0%) and the rate the dollar implies (5.5%) is exactly the size of the capital-control wall the SBV must maintain — close the wall and the band breaks; widen the wall and you choke domestic finance. There is no free lunch in the trilemma.**

For the legal architecture underneath all of this — the statutes that give the SBV its mandate over the dong and bank credit — see the law-and-geopolitics series on [SBV monetary and banking law](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/sbv-monetary-and-banking-law-credit-quotas-and-the-dong).

## The trade channel: a tariff lands on the export margin

Now the second channel, which bypasses the exchange rate entirely and lands directly on corporate earnings. To see it, you have to understand what a tariff *is* and who actually pays it.

A **tariff** is a tax the *importing* country charges at its border on goods coming in. A 20% US tariff on Vietnamese furniture means that when a \$100 sofa arrives at a US port, the importer of record pays the US Treasury \$20. That \$20 has to come from somewhere, and where it comes from is settled by negotiation between the American buyer and the Vietnamese seller. If the US market is competitive and the buyer can switch suppliers, the seller has to eat more of it (cut their price) to keep the order. If the product is hard to source elsewhere, the buyer eats more of it (pays a higher shelf price). In practice the burden is *split*, and for a Vietnamese exporter the share they eat comes straight out of an already-thin margin.

### The 2025 tariff path: 46% to 20%, with a 40% transshipment trap

The 2025 episode is the case study the whole world watched, and Vietnam was near the center of it. The sequence, dated:

- **April 2, 2025** — "Liberation Day." The US announced a 10% universal tariff plus "reciprocal" rates on 57 partners. Vietnam's headline number was **46%** — the fourth-highest on the board, behind only Cambodia (49%), and ahead of Thailand (36%), China (34%), and Taiwan (32%). For an economy that sends ~29% of its exports to the US, a 46% wall was a five-alarm fire.
- **April 9, 2025** — after a brief, brutal market sell-off, the US announced a 90-day pause on the reciprocal rates for all partners except China, buying time to negotiate.
- **July 2, 2025** — the Vietnam deal: the rate was set at **20%**, with a **40% rate on transshipped goods** (products merely routed through Vietnam to disguise another country's origin — in practice, aimed at Chinese goods relabeled as Vietnamese).
- **Late 2025** — many other partner deals followed (EU 15%, Japan 15%), and a regional framework was folded into the October ASEAN Summit statement.

![Bar chart of Vietnam's 2025 tariff path: 46 percent April reciprocal rate, 20 percent July deal, 40 percent transshipment rate, against a 2.4 percent 2024 baseline](/imgs/blogs/how-global-policy-reaches-vietnam-the-fed-the-dollar-and-tariffs-4.png)

Three things deserve emphasis. First, even the *negotiated* 20% is enormous relative to the ~2.4% average US tariff that prevailed in 2024 — it is an order-of-magnitude increase in the cost of accessing Vietnam's single most important market. Second, the **40% transshipment rate** is a structural trap: a great deal of "Vietnamese" manufacturing is really final assembly of Chinese components, and the transshipment clause is designed to make sure those goods cannot dodge the China tariff by passing through Vietnam. Policing that distinction (what counts as "made in Vietnam" versus "routed through Vietnam") becomes a permanent compliance cost and a source of ongoing uncertainty. Third, the whole thing is *negotiated policy*, not a fixed law — which means it can move again, and uncertainty itself is a cost (we cover that dynamic in the dedicated post on [the 2025 tariff shock](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-2025-tariff-shock-policy-uncertainty-reprices-markets) and the general mechanics in [tariffs and trade policy as a market force](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/tariffs-and-trade-policy-as-a-market-force)).

The transshipment rate deserves its own moment, because it is where the trade channel and the geopolitics intertwine most tightly. The rules of origin that determine whether a product is "Vietnamese" turn on how much value was actually added inside Vietnam — typically a threshold like "at least 40% of the product's value must be local content." A solar panel whose cells were made in China and merely framed and boxed in Vietnam fails that test; a piece of furniture built from Vietnamese-grown rubberwood, cut and assembled by Vietnamese labor, passes it. The 40% transshipment tariff is the US saying: *we will tax the relabeled-Chinese goods at a punitive rate, and only genuinely Vietnamese value-add gets the 20% rate.* For Vietnamese policymakers, this creates a powerful incentive to push the economy *up the value chain* — to do more of the manufacturing, not just the final assembly — because deeper local content is now the difference between a 20% and a 40% tariff. The trade channel, in other words, does not just tax exports; it reshapes industrial policy. A country that wants to keep its US market access has to make more of each product at home, which is a slow, capital-intensive transformation that the tariff is effectively trying to force.

There is also a *macro* dimension to the trade channel that goes beyond any single exporter's margin. Exports are a direct component of GDP, and net exports (exports minus imports) feed the current account, which in turn affects the balance of payments and therefore the supply of dollars flowing into Vietnam. A tariff that slows exports does three things at the macro level at once: it directly subtracts from GDP growth (fewer goods sold abroad), it shrinks the trade surplus (fewer dollars earned), and — because fewer dollars are coming in — it adds *back* to the pressure on USD/VND. So the trade channel and the financial channel are not fully separate after all: a big enough tariff shock that dents the trade surplus loops back into the currency, tightening the financial channel too. This is the deepest version of the squeeze, and it is why a small open economy treats a tariff on a third of its exports as a macro event, not just a sector story.

#### Worked example: the 20% tariff's hit to a Vietnamese exporter's margin

Here is the trade channel in dollars, on a single \$100 product — say a Vietnamese-made jacket sold to a US retailer.

Before the tariff, the math is simple. The US retailer pays the factory **\$100**. The factory's costs — fabric, zippers, labor, overhead — come to **\$88**. The factory keeps a **\$12** net margin, a 12% margin that is healthy for contract garment manufacturing.

Now a 20% tariff lands. When the \$100 jacket enters the US, the importer owes the US Treasury **\$20**. Someone has to absorb that \$20. Suppose buyer and seller split it roughly evenly (a common outcome in competitive supply chains). The US retailer raises its shelf price and absorbs about **\$10** of the tariff (passing it to American consumers). The factory absorbs the other **\$10** by cutting its invoice price to **\$90** to keep the order — because if it doesn't, the retailer will move production to a country with a lower tariff.

Run the factory's new P&L. Revenue: **\$90**. Costs, unchanged in the short run: **\$88**. Net margin: **\$2** — a **2% margin**, down from 12%. The factory just lost **five-sixths of its profit** to a tax it never paid directly. If the split had gone worse — if the buyer had the power to push *all* \$20 onto the seller — the factory's revenue would fall to \$80 against \$88 of cost, and it would be running a **\$8 loss per jacket** and would have to either renegotiate, cut costs, or walk away from the US market.

![Before and after comparison of a Vietnamese exporter's margin on a 100 dollar product, showing net margin collapsing from 12 dollars to 2 dollars under a 20 percent tariff](/imgs/blogs/how-global-policy-reaches-vietnam-the-fed-the-dollar-and-tariffs-6.png)

**The intuition: a tariff is not a small tax on a big margin — for an export economy it is a big tax on a thin margin, and a 20-point tariff can erase most of a contract manufacturer's profit even when the buyer shares the burden. That is why ~29% of exports going to the US makes the tariff a first-order shock to corporate Vietnam, not a footnote.** And note what is *not* in this calculation: the exchange rate. The trade channel hits earnings directly. The only currency relief is indirect — if the dong weakens enough, the factory's dollar revenue converts to more dong, cushioning the blow in local-currency terms, which is one reason a small open economy under tariff pressure often *wants* a weaker currency.

## The two channels together: the small-open-economy squeeze

The single most important idea in this post is that the financial channel and the trade channel are not independent — they tend to fire *together*, and when they do, a small open economy has nowhere to hide. Walk through the squeeze logic:

When the US tightens monetary policy (Fed hikes), the dollar strengthens and capital is pulled out of emerging markets like Vietnam. That pushes USD/VND up and forces the SBV to choose between burning reserves and tightening domestic credit — either way, **financial conditions in Vietnam tighten**. Simultaneously, when the US turns protectionist (tariffs up), Vietnamese exporters' margins are compressed and the country's biggest growth engine — selling to American consumers — sputters. So **the real economy slows** at the very moment **the cost of money rises**.

That is a vise. A larger, more self-contained economy could lean against it — cut its own rates aggressively, run a big fiscal stimulus, let its currency float to absorb the shock. Vietnam's options are narrower on every front. It cannot cut rates too far without breaking the band (the trilemma). Its fiscal space is real but limited. And its currency is managed, so it cannot let the dong float freely to fully absorb the trade shock without abandoning the stability that its dollar-borrowers and importers depend on. The small open economy is squeezed precisely because it is small and open: it imports the tightening through the financial channel and the demand shock through the trade channel, and it lacks the heft to fully offset either.

The 2025 experience added one more wrinkle that is worth internalizing, because it shows the channels can also *partially cancel*. In 2025 the trade channel tightened violently (the 46% then 20% tariff) while the financial channel actually *loosened* — the dollar fell more than 10% as US policy credibility wobbled, which took pressure *off* USD/VND. So the dong's job in 2025 was relatively easy on the FX front even as corporate Vietnam absorbed a historic tariff. The lesson is not that the channels always reinforce — it is that you must trace them *separately*, because the dominant channel changes with the regime. The dangerous regime for Vietnam is the one where the dollar is *strong* (financial squeeze) *and* tariffs are *high* (trade squeeze) at the same time — the 2018–2019 and 2022 configurations, not the peculiar 2025 one.

It helps to lay the four possible regimes out as a 2×2, because knowing which quadrant you are in tells you almost everything about what Vietnamese assets are worth. **Weak dollar, low tariffs** is the goldilocks regime: capital flows into emerging markets, the SBV can ease, exporters have full market access, and the VN-Index re-rates up — this is the 2017 and parts-of-2021 setup. **Weak dollar, high tariffs** is the 2025 oddity: the financial channel is friendly but the trade channel bites, so the currency is calm while corporate earnings take the hit — a stock-pickers' market where US-exposed exporters lag and domestic names hold. **Strong dollar, low tariffs** is a financial-only squeeze: the currency and rates are under pressure but the export engine still runs, so the SBV's reserve buffer is the variable to watch. And **strong dollar, high tariffs** is the nightmare quadrant — both channels firing, the SBV pinned by the trilemma, the export engine sputtering, and no costless way out. The whole art of reading Vietnam from the outside is figuring out which quadrant the next year sits in, because the same VN-Index level can be cheap in one quadrant and expensive in another.

A final structural point about why the squeeze hurts a *small* economy more than a large one. A large economy like the United States or China can run an independent monetary policy *because* it is big enough that capital flows do not overwhelm it — the trilemma binds it far more loosely. A small economy has no such cushion: a flow that is a rounding error to global capital markets is a tidal wave relative to the size of its bond market and its reserves. The same \$10 billion outflow is noise in a \$25 trillion US bond market and a crisis in a \$100 billion Vietnamese one. Smallness is not just a label; it is the reason the policy made elsewhere arrives with so much force.

## Common misconceptions

**"Vietnam can just set its own interest rate to whatever its economy needs."** No — that is precisely what the impossible trinity forbids. As long as Vietnam wants to keep the dong stable inside its band, its rate is tethered to the Fed's. It has *some* room (it eased in 2023 while the Fed held), but not unlimited room. The 2023 cuts from 6.0% to 4.5% were possible partly *because* the SBV simultaneously tightened capital controls and the dong was allowed to drift weaker — Vietnam paid for its monetary independence on the other two legs of the trilemma. There is no free rate.

**"A tariff is paid by the foreign country, so Vietnam pays the US tariff."** Half-right in a way that misleads. The tariff is *charged* at the US border and *legally owed* by the US importer — so in the first instance Americans pay it. But through price negotiation the *economic burden* is split, and a chunk of it lands on Vietnamese exporters' margins (as the worked example showed). The truth is in the middle: a tariff is a tax on the *trade relationship*, and both sides bleed.

**"A weaker dong is always bad for Vietnam."** Not in the trade channel. A weaker dong makes Vietnamese exports cheaper in dollar terms, partially offsetting a tariff and supporting the export sector. The cost of a weaker dong shows up elsewhere — in imported inflation and in the local-currency burden of dollar debt. Whether a weaker dong is net good or bad depends on which channel dominates: for an exporter facing a tariff, a controlled depreciation is a cushion; for an importer of fuel or a company with dollar loans, it is a hit.

**"The dong's steady decline means Vietnam is in crisis."** No — it means the SBV is running a *managed crawl*, which is a deliberate policy, not a failure. A ~13% depreciation over five years (about 2.5% a year) is an orderly retreat that keeps Vietnamese exports competitive while avoiding the chaos of a free float. A crisis looks like the dong gapping 20% in a week. The slow crawl is the SBV successfully *avoiding* a crisis, not suffering one.

**"Foreign portfolio flows are too small to move the VN-Index."** They are a minority of daily turnover, but they are the *marginal* buyer and seller — and markets are priced at the margin. When foreigners sell persistently on a strong-dollar move, they set the price that domestic investors then react to, and the psychological signal of "foreigners are leaving" amplifies the move. The 2025 data is the proof: persistent foreign net selling coincided with the sharpest VN-Index drawdowns even though foreigners were a single-digit share of volume.

## Case studies: how the channels actually played out

### Case study 1 (the deep one): April 2025, the tariff shock and the global repricing

The cleanest demonstration of the trade channel — and of how a US policy shock ripples through every market at once — is the two weeks after Liberation Day. The numbers are dramatic.

When the 46% Vietnam rate (and the broader reciprocal schedule) hit on April 2, 2025, the US market itself convulsed. The S&P 500 had set an all-time high of 6,144 on February 19; by the April 8 trough it had fallen to 4,983 — a peak-to-trough drawdown of nearly **19%**, including a four-day drop of about 12% that was among the most violent on record. Then, on April 9, the moment the 90-day pause was announced, the S&P 500 ripped **9.5% higher in a single day** — one of the largest one-day gains in the index's history. The whole episode is a textbook of how policy *uncertainty* — not even the tariff itself, just the on-again-off-again uncertainty about it — reprices risk assets in days.

For Vietnam specifically, the April 2 shock was acute because of the 46% headline. A rate that high, if it had stuck, would have made a large fraction of Vietnam's US-bound exports uncompetitive overnight — and with ~29% of exports going to the US, the implied hit to GDP was large enough to threaten the country's growth model. Vietnamese equities and the dong both came under immediate pressure on the announcement. What pulled Vietnam back from the brink was the *negotiation*: the move from 46% to 20% by July 2 cut the implied damage by more than half and let the market re-rate Vietnamese exporters back up. The 20% that remained is still a heavy tax — but it is survivable in a way 46% was not.

The bond-market detail from this episode is worth flagging because it overturns an intuition. During the April risk-off, the US 10-year Treasury yield did *not* fall the way it "should" in a flight to safety — it actually *spiked* about 50 basis points in a week (from ~3.99% on April 4 to ~4.49% by April 11). That is a safe-haven *failure*: in a normal crisis, money runs into Treasuries and yields fall; here, the very policy uncertainty that caused the sell-off also made some investors question US assets, so Treasuries sold off alongside stocks. For a small open economy watching from outside, that is a sobering signal — the anchor of the whole system was wobbling, which is exactly why the dollar fell so hard in H1 2025.

### Case study 2: 2022, the strong-dollar squeeze and VN-Index −33%

For the *opposite* regime — the dangerous one where the financial channel dominates — rewind to 2022. This is the year both channels of pressure on Vietnam pointed the same way and the VN-Index paid the price.

The backdrop was the Fed's 525-basis-point hiking sprint, which drove the dollar to multi-decade highs and pulled capital out of every emerging market. USD/VND came under sustained pressure, and the SBV did exactly what the financial channel predicts: it *defended the band*. It raised its policy rate by 200 basis points in two moves (September and October 2022) and sold reserves to slow the dong's slide. That defense — an imported tightening Vietnam's domestic economy did not need — collided with a *home-grown* policy shock: a 2022 crackdown on fraud in the corporate-bond and property markets (the Tan Hoang Minh and Van Thinh Phat cases) that froze a major funding channel for Vietnamese developers.

The result was brutal. The VN-Index fell from a 2022 high near 1,528 to a low near 874 — a peak-to-trough drawdown of about **−33%**, one of the worst years in its history. This is the squeeze in its purest form: the global financial channel (strong dollar, imported rate hikes) and a domestic credit shock hit simultaneously, and a market with thin foreign-flow buffers had no cushion. The lesson for the framework: the financial channel is most dangerous to Vietnam precisely when the dollar is strong, *and* it compounds with any domestic vulnerability. (The legal and regulatory side of that 2022 crackdown is covered in the law series on [Vietnam's 2022 bond and property crackdown](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/vietnams-2022-bond-and-property-crackdown).)

### Case study 3: 2018–2019, the first trade war as the template

The 2025 tariff shock did not come from nowhere — it was a bigger, faster sequel to the 2018–2019 US–China trade war, and that earlier episode is the template for understanding the trade channel's *second-order* effects on Vietnam.

When the US put tariffs on Chinese goods in 2018–2019, something counterintuitive happened for Vietnam: it *benefited*, at least initially. Manufacturers shifting production out of China to dodge the China tariffs moved a great deal of capacity to Vietnam — the "China + 1" strategy — and Vietnamese exports to the US surged. Vietnam was a *winner* of the first trade war's reconfiguration of supply chains. But that very success planted the seed of 2025: Vietnam's bilateral trade surplus with the US ballooned (it became one of the largest in the world), and a large surplus is exactly the red flag that invites a "reciprocal" tariff. The 46% number in 2025 was, in part, the bill for being the biggest beneficiary of 2018. And the 40% transshipment rate was a direct response to the suspicion that some of Vietnam's export boom was really Chinese goods routed through Vietnamese ports. The template lesson: in the trade channel, today's winner from a supply-chain shift is tomorrow's target. (For the geopolitics of Vietnam caught between its two giant partners, see [Vietnam in the US-China squeeze](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/vietnam-in-the-us-china-squeeze).)

#### Worked example: foreign portfolio outflows on a strong-dollar move and the VN-Index

Tie the financial channel to the equity market with a flow example. Suppose foreign investors hold \$15 billion of Vietnamese stocks, and a strong-dollar episode prompts them to reduce that exposure by 10% over a quarter — a \$1.5 billion net sell.

Why does \$1.5 billion matter for a market whose total capitalization runs into the hundreds of billions of dollars? Because of where it hits and how it is priced. Foreign investors are concentrated in the large, liquid blue chips that anchor the VN-Index — the banks, the big developers, the consumer giants. Their \$1.5 billion of selling is not spread evenly; it lands on the 20–30 names that *drive the index*. And markets price at the margin: if the marginal seller is dumping VinGroup and Vietcombank, those stocks fall, and because they are heavily weighted in the VN-Index, the index falls with them — pulling down the value of *every* portfolio, including the domestic ones that never sold.

Put rough numbers on the multiple effect. Say the foreign selling, concentrated in index heavyweights, pushes the average price-to-earnings multiple on those names from 14× down to 12× — a 14% de-rating. On a slice of the market worth, say, \$120 billion, a 14% de-rating is roughly **\$17 billion** of market-cap evaporation, triggered by a \$1.5 billion foreign sell. **The intuition: foreign flows move the VN-Index far out of proportion to their size because they are the marginal price-setter in the most heavily-weighted stocks — a strong-dollar move that prompts even a modest foreign outflow can knock a double-digit percentage off the index by re-rating the names everyone else is forced to mark to market.** This is the last link in the financial chain: Fed → dollar → USD/VND → SBV's choice → foreign outflows → VN-Index.

## What it means for asset values: the Vietnam playbook

Pull the threads together into something you can actually use. The framework is: identify which channel is dominant, identify the SBV's likely response, and then ask what each asset is worth under that response.

**Watch the dollar (DXY) and the rate differential first.** The single most important external variable for Vietnamese assets is the trajectory of the dollar, which is downstream of the Fed. A *strengthening* dollar (Fed hiking or staying high, DXY rising) is the warning sign: it pulls capital out, pressures USD/VND, forces the SBV to tighten or burn reserves, and de-rates the VN-Index. A *weakening* dollar (Fed cutting, DXY falling) is the all-clear: it relieves USD/VND pressure, lets the SBV ease, and supports the multiple. In 2025, the falling dollar was the quiet good news beneath the tariff noise.

**Watch USD/VND and reserves for the SBV's stress level.** When USD/VND is pinned at the top of the band and reserves (measured in months of imports) are falling, the SBV is in defend mode — expect higher domestic rates and tighter credit, which is bad for Vietnamese equities and especially for rate-sensitive sectors (real estate, banks' net interest margins). When USD/VND has room and reserves are rebuilding, the SBV has freedom to ease — supportive for stocks. The exchange rate is the SBV's stress gauge, readable in real time.

**On the trade channel, watch the effective tariff and the sector mix.** A 20% tariff is survivable but it compresses exporter margins and slows the export engine. The exporters most exposed are the thin-margin contract manufacturers (textiles, footwear, furniture, electronics assembly) whose entire model is being a low-cost supplier to the US. Companies with pricing power, diversified end-markets (selling to the EU and within Asia, not just the US), or genuine domestic value-add (so they clear the transshipment test) are more insulated. The tariff re-rates the *export-heavy, US-concentrated, thin-margin* names down, and leaves *domestic-demand* names (consumer staples, domestic banks, utilities) relatively untouched.

**The direction and rough magnitude.** A strong-dollar-plus-high-tariff regime (the dangerous one) argues for caution on the VN-Index broadly, underweight the US-exposed exporters and the rate-sensitive real-estate and bank names, and a preference for domestic-demand defensives and companies with diversified export markets. A weak-dollar-plus-easing-tariff regime (the friendly one, like late 2025) argues for the opposite: the VN-Index can re-rate higher, foreign flows can return, the SBV can ease, and the beaten-down exporters can recover as the tariff overhang lifts.

**The signal that would invalidate the read.** Two things would flip the framework. First, a *break* in the managed band — if the SBV were forced to let the dong gap weaker by more than its usual crawl (say a sudden 5%+ move), that would signal the financial squeeze had overwhelmed the defense, and would be a genuine risk-off trigger for all Vietnamese assets. Second, a *re-escalation* of the tariff (a move back toward the 46% headline, or aggressive enforcement of the 40% transshipment rate) would re-open the trade-channel wound that the July 2025 deal had stitched. Either of those turns the friendly regime back into the dangerous one. The whole point of the framework — and of this series — is to know, when a policy lever moves in Washington, exactly which channel it travels through to reach Hanoi, and therefore what to do about it. For the cross-asset, cross-regime synthesis of all these levers, see [the policy-to-asset playbook capstone](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-policy-to-asset-playbook-capstone).
