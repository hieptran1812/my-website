> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The exchange rate is a policy lever: governments set, defend, and move it, and every move travels through the *currency channel* to reprice imports, exporter earnings, foreign-currency debt, and the value of every cross-border asset on the planet.
>
> - You cannot have everything at once. The **impossible trinity** says a country picks at most two of {free capital flow, a fixed exchange rate, an independent interest-rate policy}. A peg, a float, and a managed band are just three different answers to which one you give up.
> - **Reserves are a finite war chest.** A central bank defends a peg by selling hard currency and buying its own. The market trades ~\$7.5 trillion a day; the biggest one-day intervention on record was ~\$43 billion. Size alone never holds a level — the *interest rate* has to back it.
> - A currency move is not abstract. A 10% weaker home currency lifts an exporter's local-currency earnings, raises imported inflation, and makes foreign-currency debt heavier — all at once, on real balance sheets.
> - **The one number to remember:** when the Swiss National Bank abandoned its EUR/CHF 1.20 floor on 15 January 2015, the franc gapped ~30% in *minutes*. Pegs do not bend; they break.

On 22 September 1985, finance ministers and central bankers from the United States, Japan, West Germany, France, and the United Kingdom met at the Plaza Hotel in New York and agreed to do something governments almost never admit to doing: deliberately push their own currency down. The dollar had spent five years climbing to a level its trading partners considered ruinous — the US Dollar Index (DXY) had reached roughly 164 — and US manufacturers were screaming. The five governments announced they would intervene together to weaken it.

It worked, and then it worked too well. The dollar fell from about 239 yen to roughly 150 by the end of 1986 and to about 121 by 1987 — the yen *doubled* in value against the dollar in two years. The slide became so violent that the same governments met again at the Louvre in February 1987 to try to *stop* it. And the cheap money Japan used to cushion its now-uncompetitive exporters helped inflate one of history's great asset bubbles, which burst at the end of 1989 and put Japan into a decades-long deflation.

That is the whole story of currency policy in one episode. A government can move an exchange rate — sometimes with nothing but a coordinated press release. But the exchange rate is connected to everything: trade, inflation, debt, asset prices, the next country's policy. You pull the lever, and you do not fully control where the consequences land. This post is about that lever — how it is set, how it is defended, how it breaks, and how every move reprices the assets you hold.

![FX policy lever travels through the currency channel to reprice imports exporter earnings and foreign currency debt](/imgs/blogs/currency-policy-and-fx-intervention-1.png)

The mental model is the one above and it runs through this entire post. A government or central bank pulls an **FX lever** — it sets a peg, widens a band, buys or sells reserves, opens or closes a rate differential, or simply talks the currency up or down. That lever reaches markets through the **currency channel**: a cheaper currency makes imports cost more and exports earn more; a dearer one does the reverse. And it lands as a concrete **change in what assets are worth** — imported inflation reprices bonds, the exporter-importer split reprices equities, and a move in the currency rewrites the burden of foreign-currency debt. Plaza 1985, the Swiss franc floor, the Bank of Japan's yen-buying, China's daily yuan fix, and Vietnam's dong band are all the *same lever* pulled to different ends.

## Foundations: what an exchange rate actually is, and who controls it

Start from zero. An **exchange rate** is just a price — the price of one currency expressed in another. USD/JPY = 150 means one US dollar buys 150 Japanese yen. USD/VND = 26,100 means one dollar buys 26,100 Vietnamese dong. Like any price, it is set where supply meets demand: people and institutions who want to *buy* a currency (to buy that country's goods, bonds, stocks, or to repay debt in it) push it up; people who want to *sell* it push it down.

Here is the first thing to internalize, because the convention trips up beginners constantly. When we say a currency "weakens" or "depreciates," we mean it buys *less* of the other currency. If USD/VND moves from 25,000 to 26,100, the dollar got *stronger* (it buys more dong) and the dong got *weaker* (it takes more dong to buy a dollar). A bigger USD/VND number = a weaker dong. Keep that straight and half the confusion evaporates.

Now, who controls this price? In a purely free market, nobody — it floats on supply and demand, like the price of a stock. But most governments do not leave their currency entirely to the market, because the exchange rate is too important to too many things. So we get a spectrum of **regimes**, from "the government fixes the rate by force" to "the government never touches it."

### The regime spectrum

![FX regime spectrum from hard peg to free float showing what is fixed and how it breaks](/imgs/blogs/currency-policy-and-fx-intervention-4.png)

At one extreme is the **hard peg** (or its strictest form, a *currency board*). The government promises to exchange its currency for an anchor — the US dollar, gold, or the euro — at a fixed rate, and it backs that promise with reserves. Hong Kong has run a currency board pegging the Hong Kong dollar to the US dollar (~7.8) since 1983. Argentina ran a one-peso-one-dollar board from 1991 to 2002. A hard peg buys you stability and credibility, and it is wonderful for trade and for borrowing — until the day reserves run out and it breaks overnight.

In the middle is the **managed float** or **crawling band**: the central bank announces a central rate and a band around it (say ±5%), and lets the currency drift — usually steadily weaker — within that band, intervening at the edges. Vietnam runs the dong this way: a daily central rate set by the State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) with a ±5% band, crawling gently lower year after year. China manages the yuan with a daily "fix" plus a counter-cyclical adjustment factor. This is the most common real-world regime among emerging markets, because it gives you *some* exchange-rate stability and *some* monetary independence without the all-or-nothing fragility of a hard peg.

At the other extreme is the **free float**: the rate is whatever the market says, and the central bank does not target a level. The dollar, euro, yen, pound, and Swiss franc all float. A float gives you full control of your own interest-rate policy and an open capital account — but you accept that the exchange rate can swing hard, importing inflation when it falls and hurting exporters when it rises. Floating central banks intervene only rarely, in disorderly conditions, and almost never to defend a specific number.

### Reserves: the ammunition behind every regime

Before we get to the central idea, we need the one resource that makes the whole machinery possible — **foreign exchange reserves**. Reserves are a central bank's stockpile of assets it does *not* control the printing of: mostly US dollars (held as US Treasury bonds), euros, yen, gold, and a reserve asset at the IMF called Special Drawing Rights. They are the ammunition. A central bank can create *its own* currency without limit — that is its superpower — but it cannot create dollars or gold. So the direction of intervention you can sustain depends entirely on which way you're pushing.

This asymmetry is the most underappreciated fact in currency policy, so sit with it. To make your currency *weaker*, you print your own currency (unlimited) and buy foreign assets — you can do this forever, accumulating reserves without bound, because you're spending the one thing you can always make more of. China did exactly this through the 2000s, amassing over \$4 trillion in reserves to keep the yuan from rising. The SNB did it for 3.5 years defending its franc floor. The constraint here is *inflation and politics*, not ammunition — printing francs to buy euros eventually floods your own economy with money.

To make your currency *stronger*, you must do the reverse: sell foreign reserves and buy your own currency. Now you are spending the thing you *cannot* make more of. This is the hard, finite, dangerous direction — and it is the one every classic currency crisis is about. A country defending against a *falling* currency is fighting with a fixed magazine; when it's empty, the defense ends. A country defending against a *rising* currency has an infinite magazine and can hold indefinitely (until inflation or politics stops it). **Remember this asymmetry whenever you read about an intervention: defending a weak currency has a countdown clock; defending a strong one does not.**

How much is "enough" reserves? A common rule of thumb is that a country should hold enough to cover several months of imports and to cover its short-term foreign-currency debt (the "Greenspan-Guidotti rule": reserves ≥ short-term external debt due within a year). Vietnam, for instance, has run reserves around three months of imports — adequate but not lavish, which is part of why the SBV crawls the dong gently rather than trying to hold a hard line. A country with thin reserves and lots of short-term dollar debt is a currency crisis waiting for a trigger.

### Why can't you have all three?

Why can't a country have a stable currency *and* its own interest-rate policy *and* free movement of capital? That question has a famous, precise answer, and it is the single most important idea in currency policy. It rests on a relationship economists call **uncovered interest parity** (UIP): in a world of free capital, the expected return on holding any currency must be roughly equal once you account for expected exchange-rate moves. If Vietnamese deposits pay 3% more than US deposits, then in equilibrium the market must *expect* the dong to depreciate ~3% over the year — otherwise everyone would pile into the higher-yielding dong and the extra return would be a free lunch, which markets compete away.

UIP is why you cannot pin both the interest rate *and* the exchange rate when capital is free. If you fix the rate (no expected depreciation) but offer a lower interest rate than the anchor, UIP is violated — holding your currency is strictly worse — and capital floods out until something gives. The "something" is either the peg (it breaks), the rate (it's dragged back up), or the capital account (you close it). The trinity is just UIP stated as a policy constraint.

## The impossible trinity: pick any two of three

![Impossible trinity triangle showing a country can hold only two of fixed FX independent monetary policy and free capital flow](/imgs/blogs/currency-policy-and-fx-intervention-2.png)

The **impossible trinity** (also called the *trilemma*) states that a country can have at most **two** of these three things at the same time, never all three:

- **A** — an **independent monetary policy** (you set your own interest rates to suit your own economy);
- **B** — a **fixed exchange rate** (you peg the currency to an anchor);
- **C** — **free capital flow** (money can move in and out of the country without restriction).

Why is the third always impossible? Think it through with money chasing yield. Suppose Vietnam pegs the dong to the dollar (B), lets capital move freely (C), and *also* wants to cut its own interest rate to 2% while the US holds at 5% (A). The moment Vietnamese rates drop below US rates, every rational holder of dong sells dong, buys dollars, and parks the money in US deposits earning 3% more. That selling pressure pushes the dong down — but the peg says the dong cannot move. So the SBV has to *defend* the peg by selling dollars and buying dong, which drains its reserves and, in effect, drags the domestic interest rate back up. You wanted a 2% rate; the peg plus open capital *forces* you back toward the US rate. You cannot have all three. The market takes one away.

So every regime is a *choice* of which corner to surrender:

- **Keep A + B, give up C** (closed capital account): China for much of its history. You can peg *and* run your own rates, but only by controlling who moves money across the border. Capital controls are the price.
- **Keep A + C, give up B** (free float): the US, euro area, Japan, UK. You get your own rates and open capital, but you accept whatever exchange rate the market produces.
- **Keep B + C, give up A** (hard peg / currency board): Hong Kong. You get a rock-solid peg and open capital, but you *import* the anchor's monetary policy — Hong Kong's interest rates effectively track the Fed's, regardless of what Hong Kong's economy needs.

The managed float is a deliberate attempt to live *in the interior* of the triangle — to keep partial control of all three by surrendering a *little* of each. Vietnam allows the dong to crawl (partial B), keeps meaningful capital controls (partial C), and runs a somewhat independent rate policy via credit quotas (partial A). It is a balancing act, and the trinity is always pulling at it.

#### Worked example: the carry trade and why the trinity binds

Let's put numbers on the force that breaks the trinity — the **carry trade** — because it is the mechanism, and it is also one of the biggest trades in global markets.

A carry trade borrows in a low-yielding currency and lends in a high-yielding one, pocketing the rate difference. Suppose US dollar deposits pay 5% and Japanese yen deposits pay 0.25%. A trader borrows ¥150,000,000 (worth \$1,000,000 at USD/JPY = 150), converts it to \$1,000,000, and buys US deposits.

- Interest earned on the dollar leg: \$1,000,000 × 5% = **\$50,000** per year.
- Interest paid on the yen leg: ¥150,000,000 × 0.25% = ¥375,000 ≈ **\$2,500** per year.
- Net carry: \$50,000 − \$2,500 = **\$47,500** per year, or **4.75%** — pure rate differential, before any currency move.

That 4.75% is "free money" *only* if the exchange rate doesn't move against you. The risk is that the yen *strengthens*. If USD/JPY falls from 150 to 142.5 (a ~5% stronger yen) over the year, repaying the ¥150,000,000 loan now costs \$1,052,600 — a **\$52,600 currency loss** that more than wipes out your \$47,500 of carry. The carry trade is, in one line, *"picking up nickels in front of a steamroller"*: small steady gains, occasional violent reversals.

Now connect it back to the trinity. The carry trade is exactly the capital flow that punishes a country trying to hold a peg with the "wrong" interest rate. If you peg and keep capital open but try to set rates below the anchor, carry traders borrow your cheap currency and flee to the anchor's higher yield, forcing your hand. **The carry trade is the impossible trinity in action — it is the river of money that makes the third goal impossible.**

For the positioning side of carry and the dollar's role in it, see the macro-trading deep dive on [trading the dollar, the DXY, the carry trade, and the dollar smile](/blog/trading/macro-trading/trading-the-dollar-dxy-carry-dollar-smile), and within this series, [the currency channel: rate differentials and carry](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-currency-channel-rate-differentials-and-carry).

### Capital controls: the "give up C" lever in detail

The cleanest way to escape the trinity's bite is to surrender the third corner — free capital flow — and many emerging economies do exactly that. **Capital controls** are rules that restrict how money crosses a border. They come in several forms, and understanding them tells you which countries can hold a managed currency that an open economy never could.

The mildest controls are *quantity limits*: a cap on how much foreign currency a resident can buy per year, restrictions on foreigners buying local bonds, or limits on how quickly money can be repatriated. China runs a version of this — individuals face an annual \$50,000 foreign-currency conversion quota, and inflows and outflows of portfolio capital are channeled through tightly regulated programs. Harsher controls include outright bans on certain transactions, taxes on inflows (Brazil used a tax on foreign bond purchases to slow hot money in the 2010s), and minimum holding periods that penalize short-term speculative flows.

The point of all this is to *break the carry-trade pipe*. Recall the worked example: the carry trade is the river of money that punishes a country trying to hold a peg with the "wrong" rate. Capital controls dam that river. If a Vietnamese saver cannot freely convert unlimited dong into dollars and ship them abroad, then the SBV can run a lower interest rate than the US without triggering the full UIP outflow — the controls absorb part of the pressure. This is precisely why China can manage the yuan *and* set its own rates: it gave up corner C, so corners A and B coexist.

The cost is real, though. Capital controls deter foreign investment (investors hate not being able to get their money out), create black-market exchange rates (when the official rate diverges from what the street will pay), and invite corruption around the licensing of who gets to move money. They are a tool of partial sovereignty over the currency, paid for in efficiency and openness. Most managed-float economies — Vietnam included — run *partial* controls: open enough to attract foreign factories and portfolio money, closed enough to keep the carry trade from forcing a devaluation overnight.

## How a peg is defended — and how it breaks

A peg is a *promise*, and a promise about a price is only as good as your ability to enforce it. The central bank enforces a peg by standing ready to buy or sell its own currency at the fixed rate, using its **foreign exchange reserves** — its stockpile of dollars, euros, gold, and other hard assets.

When the currency is under **downward** pressure (everyone wants to sell it), the central bank does the opposite of the crowd: it *sells dollars from its reserves and buys its own currency*, soaking up the selling and holding the price up. The catch is brutal and simple: **reserves are finite.** Every day of defense burns through the war chest. When markets see the reserves running low, the defense becomes a one-way bet — speculators pile in to short the currency, knowing the central bank must eventually run out. That is the doom loop.

![Peg defense reserve burn doom loop from outflow pressure to reserves falling to the currency gapping](/imgs/blogs/currency-policy-and-fx-intervention-3.png)

The figure traces it. Outflow pressure forces the central bank to sell reserves and buy its own currency, *and/or* to hike interest rates to pay holders to stay (defending the "price" of holding the currency). Selling reserves drains the finite war chest. Hiking rates chokes growth, banks, and jobs, raising the *political* cost of the defense. Speculators, watching reserves fall, smell blood and short the currency one-directionally. Eventually one of two things happens: reserves approach zero, or the economic and political cost of the rate defense becomes unbearable. The central bank capitulates — and the currency gaps 20-40% in minutes, because all the pressure that was held back releases at once.

This is why a defended peg, paradoxically, often looks calm right up until it explodes. The defense suppresses the small daily moves that a float would have, so the pressure accumulates invisibly until the dam fails.

#### Worked example: defending a peg — how reserves burn per day

Put numbers on the war chest. Suppose a country runs a hard peg and holds **\$80 billion** in reserves. Capital is flooding out — say investors and locals are selling the currency at a pace that requires the central bank to buy **\$4 billion** of its own currency per day to hold the line (the central bank sells \$4 billion of reserves daily).

- Days of defense at \$4 billion/day: \$80,000,000,000 ÷ \$4,000,000,000 = **20 days.**

Twenty days. Now the speculators do this exact arithmetic. They know that if they sell harder — push the daily drain to \$8 billion — the war chest lasts only **10 days**. So they sell harder, which makes the drain worse, which shortens the runway, which invites more selling. The defense's own visibility is what kills it: the more obviously finite the reserves, the more attractive the short. This is precisely what happened to the Bank of England in 1992 (George Soros's famous trade against the pound in the European Exchange Rate Mechanism) and to dozens of emerging-market pegs in the 1990s Asian crisis.

The lesson in one line: **a peg defended only with reserves has a countdown clock that the whole market can read — so the defense must be backed by a credible interest-rate hike, or it is already lost.** Raising rates changes the *incentive* to hold the currency, which is sustainable; selling reserves only changes the *price* for a finite number of days, which is not.

## Intervention mechanics: the tools and how they actually work

"Intervention" is the catch-all for a central bank acting on its currency. It comes in four flavors, and the distinctions matter enormously for whether it works.

**Direct intervention** is the central bank itself buying or selling currency in the FX market — the literal transaction. To weaken its currency, it prints its own currency and buys foreign assets (this is what the SNB did for years, and what China did in the 2000s, accumulating trillions in reserves). To *strengthen* its currency, it sells foreign reserves and buys its own — the harder direction, because it is limited by the size of the reserve stockpile (you can print your own currency infinitely, but you cannot print dollars).

**Verbal intervention** — or **jawboning** — is talking the currency without trading. A finance minister says the currency is "overvalued" or "moving in a disorderly fashion" or that authorities are "watching with a sense of urgency," and the market moves on the *threat* of action. Japan's Ministry of Finance is the master of this: it issues escalating verbal warnings before it ever spends a yen, and often the words alone move the rate a percent or two. Draghi's "whatever it takes" in 2012 is the most famous jawbone in history — three words and a never-used backstop crushed a sovereign-debt panic.

Then there is the most technically important distinction, between **sterilized** and **unsterilized** intervention.

When a central bank sells dollars and buys its own currency to *defend* it, it is pulling its own currency out of circulation — which tightens domestic money supply and pushes domestic interest rates up. That monetary tightening is often *unwanted*: maybe the economy is weak and the central bank doesn't want higher rates. **Sterilized intervention** offsets the monetary effect: the central bank simultaneously does an open-market operation (buying domestic bonds, injecting the currency back) to keep the money supply and the policy rate unchanged. The FX intervention happens; the monetary stance does not change. **Unsterilized intervention** lets the monetary effect run — the FX intervention *and* the resulting tightening (or easing) both happen.

This matters because of a hard empirical truth: **sterilized intervention rarely works for long.** If you defend your currency by selling reserves but sterilize away the interest-rate effect, you have changed nothing about the *incentive* to hold the currency — you've just handed speculators cheap reserves to bet against. Unsterilized intervention works better precisely because it changes domestic rates, which changes the carry, which changes the incentive. But that is the same as giving up monetary independence — the trinity again. There is no free lunch.

### The two channels through which intervention works

When intervention *does* move the rate (beyond just the mechanical buying and selling), economists point to two channels. Understanding them tells you when to take an intervention seriously and when to fade it.

The first is the **portfolio-balance channel**. When a central bank sells dollars and buys its own currency, it changes the *supply* of each currency's assets that private investors must hold. If investors see the available stock of, say, yen-denominated bonds shrink relative to dollar bonds, they need a slightly better price to hold the new mix — and the exchange rate adjusts. This channel is *real* but *weak*: because the FX market is so enormous (\$7.5 trillion a day), the central bank has to shift a lot of supply to move the price much. The portfolio-balance channel alone explains why pure mechanical intervention against the trend fades fast.

The second, and far more powerful, is the **signaling channel**. Intervention is a *signal* about future policy. When the BOJ buys yen, the market reads it as "the authorities are uncomfortable here and may be willing to tighten policy to defend this level." If that signal is credible — if the market believes a rate response will follow — the rate moves on the *expectation*, not the trade. This is why Japan's Ministry of Finance escalates *verbal* warnings before spending a yen: it is firing the signaling channel for free. And it's why the *most* effective interventions in history (Plaza's five-government coordination, Draghi's "whatever it takes") moved markets enormously while spending little or nothing — they were pure, overwhelming signals about future policy. **Intervention's power lies far more in what it says about future rates than in the dollars it spends today.**

#### Worked example: when verbal intervention alone moves the rate

Quantify the signaling channel. Japan's authorities are famous for a graduated ladder of verbal warnings, and the market knows the ladder. Suppose USD/JPY is climbing toward 152 and the Ministry of Finance escalates from "watching closely" to "ready to take decisive action against excessive moves." History says actual intervention often follows within days.

- A carry trader is long USD/JPY (short yen) with \$50,000,000 of notional exposure, earning the ~4.75% carry from the earlier example — about \$2,375,000 a year, or roughly \$6,500 a day.
- On the verbal warning, USD/JPY drops 1.5% in an hour — say from 152.0 to 149.7 — on the *threat* alone, with no actual intervention yet.
- The trader's mark-to-market loss: \$50,000,000 × 1.5% = **\$750,000** in an hour.

That \$750,000 hit is roughly **115 days** of carry income (\$750,000 ÷ \$6,500) erased in sixty minutes — by *words*. No reserves were spent; no transaction occurred. The threat fired the signaling channel, and the leveraged carry book took the loss. This is why traders watch the verbal ladder as closely as the actual flows: **the cheapest, fastest intervention is the one that never trades — a credible threat that makes the market do the central bank's work.**

#### Worked example: intervention size versus the daily FX market

![Intervention size versus daily FX turnover showing the market dwarfs even the largest interventions](/imgs/blogs/currency-policy-and-fx-intervention-8.png)

Here is the chart every aspiring currency speculator should tattoo on their forearm. The global FX market trades roughly **\$7.5 trillion per day** (BIS Triennial Survey, 2022). Now look at the biggest interventions in history:

- The Bank of Japan's largest *single-day* yen-buying intervention on record (21 October 2022) was about **\$43 billion**.
- Japan's total intervention across April-May 2024 was about **\$62 billion** over several weeks.
- The Swiss National Bank's reserve buildup defending the franc floor from 2011 to 2015 reached roughly **\$700 billion** — but that took *3.5 years*.

Do the arithmetic. The single biggest one-day intervention ever (\$43 billion) is about **0.6%** of *one day's* market turnover:

- \$43,000,000,000 ÷ \$7,500,000,000,000 ≈ **0.0057**, i.e. **~0.6%**.

The market is roughly **170 times** bigger than the largest one-day intervention. This is why intervention against the *trend*, with reserves alone, almost always fails. You are a bucket against a tide. The only intervention that holds is the kind that changes the *flow itself* — by moving the interest rate so the carry trade reverses, or by being so credible (Plaza's five-government coordination, Draghi's backstop) that the market does the moving for you. **Size alone never holds a level; the interest rate, or overwhelming credibility, has to back it.**

## How a currency move reprices assets — the whole point

Everything above is the *machinery*. Here is where it lands: a currency move is not a number on a screen, it is a transfer of value across real balance sheets. Four channels do the work.

**1. Imported inflation.** A country imports goods priced in foreign currency. When the home currency weakens, those imports cost more in home-currency terms, and that feeds straight into consumer prices. A 10% weaker currency, in a country where imports are ~30% of the consumption basket, can add roughly 1.5-3 percentage points to inflation over a year (the "pass-through" depends on how much firms absorb in margins). Higher inflation forces higher interest rates, which reprices bonds *lower* (bond prices fall as yields rise) and pressures equity valuations. This is the channel that ties currency policy to the whole rest of the policy machine.

**2. Exporter versus importer earnings.** A weaker home currency is a gift to exporters and a tax on importers. An exporter sells in dollars and pays costs at home; when the home currency falls, those same dollar revenues convert into *more* home currency, and local-currency earnings jump. Importers face the mirror image: their dollar-priced inputs cost more in home currency, squeezing margins. So a currency move *splits* the equity market — exporters' shares reprice up, importers' down — even though the index level might barely move.

**3. Foreign-currency debt.** This is the dangerous one for emerging markets. If a Vietnamese company borrowed in dollars but earns in dong, a weaker dong makes that debt *heavier* in dong terms — the same dollar principal now requires more dong to repay. A 10% weaker currency raises the home-currency burden of dollar debt by ~10%, instantly, with no change in the underlying loan. Waves of EM crises (Asia 1997, many since) were currency depreciations that turned manageable dollar debt into unpayable dollar debt overnight.

**4. Cross-border valuation.** Every foreign asset you own is worth its local price *times* the exchange rate. A US investor holding Japanese stocks earns the stock return *plus or minus* the yen's move against the dollar. When the yen falls 15%, the Nikkei can rise 15% in yen and the US investor breaks even. Currency policy therefore silently reprices the entire universe of cross-border holdings. This channel also flows back into bond markets: when a country's currency is weak and falling, foreign holders of its government bonds face a currency loss on top of any yield, so they demand a higher yield to compensate — currency weakness raises borrowing costs for the government itself. A country that loses control of its currency often loses control of its bond market in the same move, because the two are priced together by the same foreign investors.

These four channels do not fire one at a time; they fire *together* and sometimes against each other. A weaker currency simultaneously helps exporters (good for some equities), hurts importers (bad for others), lifts inflation (bad for bonds), heavies foreign-currency debt (bad for leveraged firms), and revalues every cross-border holding. The *net* effect on a given economy depends on its structure: an export powerhouse with little dollar debt (think Japan or Germany) generally welcomes a weaker currency, while an import-dependent economy with heavy dollar debt (many emerging markets) can be wrecked by the same move. This is why there is no universal "good level" for a currency — the same depreciation is a tailwind for one country and a crisis for another.

#### Worked example: a 10% depreciation lifts an exporter's local-currency earnings

Make the exporter channel concrete. Take a Vietnamese electronics exporter that sells everything abroad in dollars and pays all its costs in dong.

Before any currency move, at USD/VND = 25,000:

- Annual revenue: \$200,000,000 in dollar sales → \$200,000,000 × 25,000 = **5,000,000,000,000 dong** (5,000 billion dong).
- Annual costs (wages, rent, local inputs, all in dong): **4,400,000,000,000 dong** (4,400 billion dong).
- Operating profit: 5,000 − 4,400 = **600 billion dong.**

Now the dong weakens 10% — USD/VND moves from 25,000 to 27,500. The dollar revenue is unchanged at \$200,000,000, but it now converts into more dong:

- New revenue: \$200,000,000 × 27,500 = **5,500,000,000,000 dong** (5,500 billion dong).
- Costs are still in dong, roughly unchanged: **4,400 billion dong** (assume the imported-input share is small).
- New operating profit: 5,500 − 4,400 = **1,100 billion dong.**

A 10% currency move lifted operating profit from 600 to 1,100 billion dong — an **83% jump in local-currency earnings** — purely from the exchange rate, with no change in units sold or prices charged. (In USD terms, the firm's profit went from \$24 million to \$40 million.) This is *operating leverage on the currency*: because costs are sticky in dong while revenue is in dollars, a small currency move swings profit hugely. The mirror case — a pure importer — would see its margin collapse by a similar magnitude. **A currency depreciation is a wealth transfer from importers to exporters, and the equity market prices it within hours of the move.**

This is also why, when a central bank weakens its currency to help exporters (a "competitive devaluation"), it is choosing winners and losers inside its own economy — for the legal-and-geopolitical dimension of currencies-as-weapons, see [currency wars and competitive devaluation](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/currency-wars-and-competitive-devaluation).

#### Worked example: imported inflation and the bond repricing

Trace the inflation channel all the way to a bond price, because that is where currency policy meets the rest of the policy machine. Suppose a country's imports are 30% of its consumption basket, all priced in dollars, and its currency weakens 10% against the dollar. Assume exchange-rate pass-through is incomplete — firms absorb half the move in their margins — so 50% of the import-cost increase reaches consumer prices.

- Direct price effect on imported goods: 10% currency move × 30% import share × 50% pass-through = **1.5 percentage points** added to the inflation rate over a year.

Now feed that into a central bank that follows a rough rule of thumb — for every extra point of inflation, it raises the policy rate by ~1.5 points (a "Taylor-rule" response). The 1.5pp of imported inflation could push the central bank to raise rates by ~2.25pp. Watch what that does to a long bond:

- A 10-year government bond has a duration of roughly 8.5 years, meaning its price falls about 8.5% for every 1 percentage point rise in its yield.
- If the yield rises by even 1.5pp (the bond market won't price the full Taylor response, but it will price a chunk): price change ≈ −8.5 × 1.5% = **−12.75%.**

A 10% currency depreciation, through the imported-inflation channel, just knocked roughly **13%** off the price of the country's long bonds — before the central bank has even met. **The currency channel and the discount-rate channel are not separate; a currency move *is* a bond move, routed through inflation and the central bank's reaction to it.** This is exactly the linkage the macro-correlations work measures empirically across rate paths and yields.

## Common misconceptions

**"A strong currency is always good for a country."** No — it's a trade-off. A strong currency makes imports cheap and tames inflation (good for consumers), but it makes exports expensive and uncompetitive (bad for producers and jobs). The whole point of Plaza 1985 was that the dollar had become *too* strong and was hollowing out US manufacturing. There is no universally "good" exchange rate; there is only one that suits *whose* interests you're prioritizing — savers and importers (who like a strong currency) versus exporters and borrowers (who like a weak one).

**"Central banks can set the exchange rate wherever they want."** Only within limits, and only for as long as the market lets them. Against the trend, with reserves alone, intervention fails — the market is ~170 times bigger than the biggest one-day intervention. A central bank can *lean against* a move, smooth it, or pick the direction with overwhelming credibility, but it cannot durably hold a level the fundamentals reject. The SNB learned this at a cost; see the case study below.

**"A peg means the currency is safe."** A peg means the currency is *stable until it isn't*. Pegs suppress small daily moves, which makes them feel safe — but that suppression lets pressure build invisibly, so when a peg breaks it breaks *violently*. The EUR/CHF floor went from rock-solid to a 30% gap in minutes. A float, for all its noise, never gaps 30% because it's always releasing pressure in small doses.

**"Intervention and interest-rate policy are separate things."** They are deeply linked through sterilization and the trinity. Unsterilized intervention *is* monetary policy — it changes the money supply and rates. Sterilized intervention tries to separate them and usually fails to last. You cannot move the currency hard without either moving rates or accepting that the move won't stick. This is the same machinery covered in [how monetary policy moves currencies through rate differentials](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-monetary-policy-moves-currencies-rate-differentials).

**"A weaker currency always boosts the economy."** It helps exporters and can boost growth — but it also imports inflation and makes foreign-currency debt heavier. For a country with lots of dollar debt, a depreciation can be *contractionary*: the debt-burden shock outweighs the export boost. The net effect depends on the country's trade structure and how much of its debt is in foreign currency.

## Case studies: five times policy met the currency

Theory is cheap. Here are five dated, real episodes — each a different point on the spectrum from "intervention worked spectacularly" to "the peg blew up in the central bank's face."

### Plaza Accord, 22 September 1985 — coordination at its most powerful

![Plaza Accord drove USD JPY from 239 to 121 in two years as five governments talked the dollar down](/imgs/blogs/currency-policy-and-fx-intervention-5.png)

The dollar had risen relentlessly from 1980 to 1985 — DXY peaked around 164 — driven by Volcker's sky-high US interest rates (the Fed funds rate hit 19% in 1981) that sucked global capital into dollar assets. By 1985 the strong dollar was devastating US exporters, and protectionist pressure in Congress was mounting. Rather than risk a trade war, the five governments chose coordinated devaluation.

The mechanics: the US, Japan, West Germany, France, and the UK agreed to *jointly* sell dollars and buy their own currencies, and — crucially — to publicly *announce* the policy so the market would do most of the work. It succeeded beyond expectation. USD/JPY fell from 239 at the time of the accord to about 150 by the end of 1986 and to about 121 by 1987 — a ~49% drop, the yen roughly *doubling* against the dollar.

But intervention's power is also its danger. The slide became disorderly, prompting the **Louvre Accord** in February 1987 to try to *halt* it. And to cushion its now-uncompetitive exporters, Japan slashed interest rates and flooded the system with cheap money — which inflated the Nikkei to 38,957 by December 1989 and Tokyo land prices to absurd levels, before the bubble burst into a thirty-year deflation. Plaza is the case study in *both* lessons: coordinated policy can move a currency hard, *and* the second-order consequences can dwarf the intended effect.

### The SNB franc floor, 2011-2015 — when a peg blows up

In September 2011, the Swiss National Bank did something extraordinary: facing a flood of safe-haven money into the franc during the euro crisis (a soaring franc was crushing Swiss exporters), it announced a **floor** of EUR/CHF 1.20 — it would *not allow* the euro to fall below 1.20 francs, and it would "buy foreign currency in unlimited quantities" to enforce it. This is the *opposite* of defending against a weak currency: the SNB was defending against a currency that was too *strong*, which is the one direction a central bank can sustain indefinitely, because it can print unlimited francs to buy euros.

And so it did — for three and a half years, the SNB printed francs and bought euros and other assets, accumulating roughly **\$700 billion** in reserves (enormous for a country of 8 million people — larger than Switzerland's entire annual GDP). The floor held. It looked permanent.

Then, on **15 January 2015**, with no warning, the SNB abandoned the floor. The reserve accumulation had become politically and financially untenable (the European Central Bank was about to launch massive QE, which would have forced the SNB to print *even more* francs). The result was one of the most violent moves in modern FX history: **EUR/CHF gapped roughly 30% in minutes**, the franc exploding higher. Several FX brokers went bankrupt; retail traders were wiped out; even institutions with stop-losses couldn't get out because there was no liquidity at any price between 1.20 and 0.85. The lesson is the one-number takeaway of this whole post: **pegs and floors do not bend, they break — and a defended level that "always holds" is exactly the one that gaps hardest when it finally goes.**

#### Worked example: the SNB gap in dollar terms

Quantify the 15 January 2015 shock for a trader who was short the franc (long EUR/CHF, betting the floor would hold) with leverage.

Suppose a trader held a €1,000,000 long EUR/CHF position at the floor, 1.2000, posting 2% margin (50× leverage) = €20,000 of equity.

- Position value at 1.2000: €1,000,000 → CHF 1,200,000.
- After the gap, EUR/CHF traded down to ~0.8500 (a ~29% drop). The position is now worth CHF 850,000 — a loss of CHF 350,000 ≈ **\$301,000** (at ~0.86 USD/CHF).
- The trader's equity was only **€20,000** (~\$23,000). The loss is roughly **13 times** the entire account.

Because the move *gapped* — there was no trading between 1.20 and ~0.85 — the broker's stop-loss couldn't fire at 1.18 or 1.10; it filled near 0.85 or not at all. The trader didn't just lose the \$23,000 account; they owed the broker a *negative balance* of roughly a quarter of a million dollars. This is why several brokers collapsed: their clients' losses exceeded their deposits, and the brokers were on the hook. **A gapping peg destroys the core assumption of leveraged trading — that you can exit near your stop — which is why a "stable" pegged currency can be the most dangerous thing on a leveraged book.**

### BOJ yen intervention, 2022 and 2024 — fighting the rate differential

By 2022, the Bank of Japan was the last major central bank pinning rates near zero (via yield-curve control) while the Fed hiked aggressively to ~5.5%. That enormous rate differential made the carry trade irresistible: borrow yen at ~0%, buy dollars at ~5%, and the yen sank — USD/JPY blew past 145, then 150, then toward 152. A weak yen helps exporters but it was importing painful inflation into Japan.

So Japan's Ministry of Finance, executing through the BOJ, intervened directly to *buy* yen — the hard direction, spending reserves. In October 2022 it conducted its largest single-day yen-buying intervention ever, about **\$43 billion**. In April-May 2024, with USD/JPY pushing past 160, it intervened again, spending roughly **\$62 billion** over several weeks.

Did it work? Only briefly. Each intervention bought a few percent and a few weeks, but the yen kept sliding because the *fundamental* driver — the 5-percentage-point rate gap — was untouched. This is the textbook illustration of the earlier worked example: \$43 billion against a \$7.5 trillion daily market is a bucket against a tide. The yen only durably stabilized when the *rate differential* began to close — the Fed cutting in 2024-25 while the BOJ finally hiked (ending negative rates in March 2024). **Intervention can smooth the path and buy time, but it cannot overpower the carry trade; only closing the rate gap does that.**

### The PBOC managed yuan — a daily fix and a counter-cyclical factor

China sits firmly in the "keep A + B, give up C" corner: it runs a managed yuan *and* an independent rate policy, paid for with capital controls. The mechanism is distinctive. Each morning the People's Bank of China publishes a **daily fix** — a reference rate for USD/CNY — and allows the currency to trade within a ±2% band around it. By controlling the fix, the PBOC controls the trend.

When markets push the yuan in a direction the PBOC dislikes, it deploys a **counter-cyclical adjustment factor** — an opaque add-on to the fix formula that lets it lean against market pressure without spending reserves directly. It also uses reserve-requirement ratios (RRR) on banks' foreign-currency deposits and "window guidance" to state banks to manage flows. The PBOC manages the yuan more with *quantity tools and the fix* than with a single policy rate, and it keeps the band narrow (the yuan has traded in a managed ~6.0-7.35 range against the dollar since 2015). The capital account is the release valve: because money can't freely flee China, the PBOC can hold the currency in a managed range that an open-capital economy never could. **China's regime is the trinity's "give up C" corner made operational — a managed currency and domestic rate control, bought with capital controls and an opaque daily fix.**

### The SBV ±5% band — Vietnam's deliberate crawl

![Vietnam managed crawl USD VND drifting steadily higher inside the SBV band from 2020 to 2025](/imgs/blogs/currency-policy-and-fx-intervention-6.png)

Vietnam runs the dong as a **crawling band**: the State Bank of Vietnam sets a daily central rate and lets USD/VND trade within ±5% of it, nudging the central rate gently weaker over time. The chart shows the result — a slow, deliberate slide from about 23,100 in 2020 to about 26,100 in 2025, roughly a 13% cumulative depreciation spread over five years. That is *managed* weakness: never a one-day devaluation, always a controlled crawl.

Why crawl the dong weaker? Vietnam is an export-led economy — the US alone takes roughly 29% of Vietnam's exports — so a gradually weaker dong keeps its exporters competitive. But the SBV cannot let the dong fall *too* fast, because a sharp drop would import inflation and spook foreign investors. So it threads the needle: a gentle crawl, defended at the band edges with reserves and, when needed, with its real binding lever — the **credit-growth quota** it sets for each bank (Vietnam's most powerful monetary tool, which substitutes for a single policy rate). When the dong is under pressure, the SBV can tighten credit room and raise refinancing rates to defend the band, all while keeping capital partly controlled. It is the managed-float regime in its purest form.

The 2025 tariff shock tested this directly: when the US announced a 46% "reciprocal" tariff on Vietnam in April 2025 (later negotiated to 20%), the dong faced both a growth threat and capital-flight risk, and the SBV had to defend the band while absorbing the hit. For the full SBV toolkit — credit quotas, rates, and how the managed dong actually works — see [the SBV toolkit: credit quotas, rates, and the managed dong](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-sbv-toolkit-credit-quotas-rates-and-the-managed-dong).

### The 2025 dollar decline — a partly policy-driven move

![DXY fell more than ten percent in the first half of 2025 the worst first half since 1973](/imgs/blogs/currency-policy-and-fx-intervention-7.png)

One more recent episode shows the currency channel working *in reverse* — a strong currency weakening not because a central bank intervened to push it down, but because *policy uncertainty* made the world want fewer dollar assets. In the first half of 2025, the US Dollar Index fell more than **10%** — from about 110 in January to a low near 98 in April — its **worst first half since 1973**.

The driver was not a Plaza-style coordinated intervention; it was the market re-rating US policy credibility amid the 2025 tariff shock and fiscal expansion. The tell was a rare and ominous pattern: during the April tariff panic, US stocks fell, US Treasury *yields rose* (bond prices fell), *and* the dollar fell — all at once. Normally, a US risk-off sends money *into* dollars and Treasuries as safe havens. When stocks, bonds, and the currency fall *together*, it signals that foreign capital is questioning the safe-haven status of US assets themselves — a *policy-credibility* move, not a growth move. The dollar partly recovered by year-end (back to ~99.5), but the episode was a reminder that **a currency can be moved by the market's judgment of a government's credibility just as powerfully as by deliberate intervention — and that judgment is the one lever a central bank cannot simply buy back.**

## What it means for asset values — the playbook

Tie it together. When you see a currency policy move — a peg break, an intervention, a band shift, a rate-differential change — here is how the assets reprice and what to watch.

**A currency weakens (deliberately or not):**
- *Exporters' equities up, importers' down.* The bigger the dollar-revenue-with-local-costs gap, the bigger the earnings swing (recall: a 10% move lifted our exporter's local-currency profit 83%). Watch the trade structure of each company, not the index.
- *Bonds reprice lower (yields up)* as imported inflation forces the central bank toward higher rates. The pass-through is biggest in economies with high import shares.
- *Foreign-currency debtors get squeezed* — a 10% depreciation is a ~10% instant increase in the home-currency burden of dollar debt. Screen for who borrowed in dollars but earns at home; that's where the crises start.
- *Gold and hard assets tend to rise* in the weakening currency, as residents hedge.

**A peg or band is under defense:**
- *Watch the reserve trend, not the spot rate.* The spot rate looks calm because it's pinned; the reserves are the real gauge. Falling reserves + a flat rate = pressure building. The break, when it comes, gaps.
- *The interest-rate response is the tell.* If the central bank is defending only with reserves (sterilized) and not hiking, the defense has a countdown clock. If it hikes hard (unsterilized), it's serious — but at the cost of growth.

**A coordinated or credible intervention (Plaza, Draghi):**
- *Move with it, not against it.* Coordinated multi-government action or a credible unlimited backstop can override the market — these are the rare interventions that stick, because they change incentives or carry overwhelming credibility.
- *Watch for the second-order consequence.* Plaza weakened the dollar and seeded Japan's bubble. The intended move is rarely the whole story.

**The handful of signals that matter most.** If you only track a few things to read currency policy, track these. First, the **rate differential** between the currency and its anchor — it is the carry-trade fuel and the single best predictor of medium-term direction; a widening gap pulls capital toward the higher yielder and pressures the lower one. Second, the **reserve trend** for any managed or pegged currency — a flat spot rate with falling reserves is a peg on a countdown clock, regardless of how calm it looks. Third, the **shape of the risk-off** — when a country's stocks, bonds, *and* currency fall together (as US assets did in April 2025), that's a credibility move, not a growth move, and it's far more dangerous than an ordinary sell-off. Fourth, the **verbal ladder** — for currencies like the yen, the escalating language of officials is itself the leading indicator of actual intervention. And fifth, the **import share and dollar-debt load** of any economy whose currency is moving — those two numbers tell you whether a depreciation is a tailwind (export powerhouse, little dollar debt) or a crisis (import-dependent, heavy dollar debt).

**What would invalidate the read:** a currency move that *isn't* backed by a rate differential or overwhelming credibility will fade — don't extrapolate a few weeks of intervention into a trend. And a "stable" pegged currency is the *most* dangerous thing on a leveraged book, because its calm is borrowed against a future gap. The signal to respect above all others is the rate differential: it is the river of money (the carry trade) that ultimately decides where a currency settles, and no amount of reserve-burning beats it for long.

**Putting it on a single spectrum.** Walk back through the five case studies and notice they line up exactly along the lever-strength axis. Plaza worked because it was the strongest possible signal — five governments, publicly coordinated, changing expectations about future policy across the board. The SNB floor held for years because it was pushing the *easy* direction (printing francs to cap a rising currency) and broke the instant that direction became politically impossible. The BOJ's 2022-24 yen-buying barely dented the trend because it was the *hard* direction (spending finite reserves) against an untouched rate differential. The PBOC holds its managed range only because it closed the capital account — it removed the carry-trade pipe rather than trying to out-spend it. And the SBV crawls the dong gently precisely because it knows it cannot defend a hard line with modest reserves, so it never promises one. The same physics — the trinity, the reserve asymmetry, the carry trade — explains every outcome. Once you see the machine, you can predict which interventions will stick and which are buckets against the tide.

The exchange rate, in the end, is the price at which a government's policy meets the rest of the world's judgment. A central bank can set it, defend it, and move it — but only within the bounds the impossible trinity draws, and only for as long as its reserves and credibility hold. When those run out, the market sets the price, all at once.
