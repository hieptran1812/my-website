> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — In February–March 2020 the S&P 500 fell 33.9% in 33 days, the fastest bear market in history; the policy response was *total* — the Fed cut to zero, launched unlimited QE, and backstopped corporate bonds, while Congress passed a \$2.2T CARES Act — and the combined monetary-plus-fiscal force reflated *everything*, doubling the S&P off its low by 2021 and leaving a money-supply surge that seeded the 2022 inflation.
>
> - When every lever fires at once, you stop debating which channel matters and watch *all* of them push the same direction: the discount rate collapsed, the liquidity tide rose by roughly \$3T, and the fiscal transfer put cash directly in hands — every asset that discounts a future cash flow or rides a liquidity wave repriced up.
> - The Fed balance sheet went from about \$4.2T (March 2020) to \$7.36T by year-end and a \$8.97T peak in April 2022 — the single largest and fastest balance-sheet expansion in its history.
> - The same policy that engineered the everything rally also pushed M2 from \$15.4T to \$19.1T in one year (+24%); roughly two years later, CPI hit 9.06% — the bill.
> - The one number to remember: **the S&P doubled in less than two years off the March 2020 low**, and it cost a \$3-trillion-plus balance sheet, ~\$5T of fiscal spending, and a 40-year-high inflation print to do it.

On February 19, 2020, the S&P 500 closed at an all-time high of 3,386. Thirty-three days later, on March 23, it closed at 2,237 — down 33.9%. That is not a typo, and it is not slow. The 2008 crash took over a year to fall that far; the 1929 crash took longer still. COVID did it in a month. By mid-March the machinery of markets was visibly breaking: the VIX — Wall Street's "fear gauge," which measures expected stock-market swings — spiked to 82.7, a level seen only once before (the depths of 2008). Even US Treasuries, the asset the whole world flees *to* in a panic, stopped working normally; the market that is supposed to be the safest, deepest pool of liquidity on earth seized up as everyone sold everything at once to raise cash.

And then, in the space of about a week, the United States government and its central bank did something no one had ever seen at that scale or speed. The Federal Reserve cut its policy rate to zero on March 15. On March 23 — the very day the market bottomed — it announced *unlimited* quantitative easing and, for the first time in its history, said it would buy corporate bonds. Within days, Congress was assembling a \$2.2T relief package, the CARES Act, the largest single piece of fiscal legislation in American history at the time. Every policy lever this series has described — the rate, the balance sheet, the deficit, the credit backstop — fired at the same moment, in the same direction, at maximum force.

This post is the case study where everything fired at once. It is the cleanest demonstration in modern markets of the series' core idea: **a policy lever reaches markets through a transmission channel and shows up as a change in what an asset is worth.** Normally you can only watch one or two levers at a time and argue about which channel did the work. In 2020 they *all* fired, they all pointed the same way, and the result was unmistakable — the everything rally, in which the S&P doubled, Bitcoin went up more than thirteen-fold, house prices took off, and the riskiest corporate bonds reflated, all off the same low. Then we will follow the consequence: the money-supply surge that, two years later, became the worst inflation in four decades. The lesson is simple and expensive: combined monetary and fiscal policy at maximum force reflates everything — and it leaves a bill.

## Foundations: the four levers and the channels they travel

Before we walk the case asset by asset, let's build the machine from zero, because the whole point of 2020 is that *four* levers fired together and you need to recognize each one.

A "policy lever" is just an action a government or central bank can take that ripples out into the price of money and the price of assets. Think of the economy as a bathtub of demand. The water level is how much spending is happening. Policymakers have a few different taps and drains they can open:

**Lever 1 — the policy interest rate.** The Federal Reserve sets a target for the *federal funds rate*, the overnight rate at which banks lend reserves to each other. It is the single most important price in finance because it anchors the entire chain of borrowing costs — your mortgage, a company's bond, the rate used to discount a stock's future profits. When the Fed cuts this rate, it is making money cheaper to borrow and less rewarding to hoard. The *channel* this lever travels is the **discount rate**: every asset is worth the present value of its future cash flows, and a lower interest rate makes those future dollars worth more today. (If you want the mechanics of how the Fed actually moves this rate, see [how the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates).)

**Lever 2 — quantitative easing (QE).** When the policy rate is already at zero, the Fed can still ease by *creating new bank reserves* and using them to buy bonds — Treasuries and mortgage-backed securities — in the open market. This is QE. It does not literally drop cash from helicopters, but it does two things: it pushes down longer-term interest rates (by buying bonds, the Fed raises their price and lowers their yield), and it floods the banking system with reserves. The channel here is the **liquidity tide** — there is simply more money sloshing around looking for a home, and a lot of it ends up bidding for stocks, credit, and other risk assets. (The deep mechanics of QE live in [quantitative easing explained](/blog/trading/finance/quantitative-easing-explained-printing-money) and the trader's-eye view in [QE vs QT: how balance-sheet policy moves markets](/blog/trading/macro-trading/qe-vs-qt-how-balance-sheet-policy-moves-markets).)

**Lever 3 — the corporate-bond backstop.** This one was nearly new in 2020. When companies cannot borrow — when no one will buy their bonds at any reasonable price — they cut jobs, default, and the recession deepens. In March 2020 the Fed announced it would *itself* buy corporate bonds (and even bond ETFs) through special vehicles. The channel is **credibility plus liquidity**: the promise that a backstop exists is often enough to make private buyers come back, because no one wants to be short an asset the Fed is willing to buy. We will see this is exactly what happened — the backstop barely had to spend money to work.

**Lever 4 — fiscal policy.** This is the government's tap, not the central bank's. The Treasury can spend money directly — send checks to households, top up unemployment benefits, lend to businesses — funded by issuing debt. This is the most *direct* lever of all: it puts money straight into the hands of people who spend it, rather than relying on cheaper credit to *encourage* spending. The CARES Act and its successors did this on a scale never attempted. (For the trader's framing, see [fiscal policy for traders](/blog/trading/macro-trading/fiscal-policy-for-traders-spending-deficits-demand) and the institutional view in [the fiscal toolkit](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-fiscal-toolkit-spending-taxes-and-deficits).)

Now the key insight. There are really only four channels through which *any* policy reaches asset prices: the **discount rate** (rates down lifts the present value of future cash flows), the **liquidity tide** (more money chasing the same assets lifts their prices), the **currency** (a weaker dollar reprices everything denominated in it), and **expectations/credibility** (what people believe about future policy can move prices before a dollar is spent). In a normal easing cycle, one or two of these channels do the work. In March 2020, all four opened at once. That is why we call it the everything rally: when the discount rate falls *and* the liquidity tide rises *and* fiscal cash floods in *and* the central bank credibly promises to catch any falling knife, there is no asset class left standing on the sidelines.

### The valuation engine in one equation (no math degree required)

To see why falling rates and rising liquidity lift asset prices, you only need one idea. The value of any asset is the sum of its future cash flows, each one shrunk down ("discounted") to what it is worth today. A dollar you will receive in ten years is worth less than a dollar today, because you could have invested today's dollar and grown it. The interest rate is the shrink factor.

In plain words: **price = cash flow ÷ (discount rate − growth rate)**, where the discount rate is roughly the safe interest rate plus a "risk premium" — the extra return investors demand for holding something risky instead of a safe Treasury. Two things happened in 2020 that both push price *up* in this formula: the safe interest rate collapsed toward zero (lowering the discount rate), and the flood of liquidity plus the Fed backstop made investors willing to accept a *smaller* risk premium (also lowering the discount rate). A lower denominator with the same numerator means a higher price. The everything rally is that sentence, applied to every asset at once.

There is a second, related idea hiding in that formula that explains *which* assets moved most: **duration**. Duration is just a measure of how far in the future an asset's cash flows lie. A short-term bond that pays you back next year has low duration; a 30-year bond has high duration; a money-losing tech company whose profits are all supposedly twenty years out has *very* high duration. The crucial property is that the higher an asset's duration, the more its present value swings when the discount rate changes — because a small change in the shrink factor, compounded over many years, makes an enormous difference to what those distant dollars are worth today. When the discount rate collapsed in 2020, the *long-duration* assets — long bonds, growth stocks, anything whose payoff is decades away — were repriced the hardest. Keep this word in mind; it is the thread that connects why the Nasdaq beat value stocks in the rally and why, in 2022, those same long-duration assets fell the furthest when rates rose.

### Why the safe haven broke: the dash for cash

One feature of March 2020 confused even seasoned investors and is worth understanding, because it explains *why* the Fed had to fire the QE lever so violently. In a normal panic, money flees risky assets (stocks) and pours *into* the safest asset on earth, US Treasuries, pushing Treasury prices *up* and yields *down*. That is the textbook "flight to quality." But for a few terrifying days in mid-March 2020, the opposite happened: Treasuries *sold off* alongside stocks. The 10-year yield, which had fallen to a record low of 0.31% on March 9 as the panic began, suddenly spiked back up — yields rose when they should have fallen.

What broke? A **dash for cash**. When everyone needs dollars *right now* — to meet margin calls, to fund redemptions, to hoard against the unknown — they sell whatever they can, and the most liquid thing to sell is Treasuries. So a panic severe enough flips the safe haven into a *source* of cash: people sold their safest, most sellable asset precisely *because* it was sellable. The world's deepest, most liquid market briefly stopped functioning; bid-ask spreads on Treasuries gapped wider than at any time since 2008. This is the nightmare scenario for a central bank, because the Treasury market is the foundation on which every other price in finance rests. The Fed's unlimited-QE announcement on March 23 was, first and foremost, a promise to *be the buyer* in the Treasury market — to absorb the selling and restore function — and only secondarily a stimulus program. Understanding the dash for cash is what makes the speed and the *unlimited* size of the response make sense: the Fed was not fine-tuning the economy, it was preventing the plumbing of the entire financial system from failing.

#### Worked example: how fast is a 33-day, −34% crash?

Let's make the speed concrete, because "fastest bear market in history" is a claim worth pricing. A *bear market* is conventionally defined as a fall of 20% or more from a peak. The historical record going back to 1929 contains about a dozen of them. The question: how does the COVID crash compare on *speed*?

The S&P peaked at 3,386 on February 19, 2020 and bottomed at 2,237 on March 23 — a drop of \$3,386 − \$2,237 = \$1,149 in index points, or 1,149 ÷ 3,386 = **−33.9%**. Now count the days: February 19 to March 23 is **33 calendar days**, or about 23 trading days.

Compare that to the historical norm. The 2007–09 bear market fell 56.8% but took **517 calendar days** to do it — about 1.7% per *month*. The 2000–02 dot-com bear fell 49% over roughly 929 days. The average post-war bear market takes somewhere around 12–18 months to reach its trough. So the typical pace of a bear is on the order of −1.5% to −3% per month.

The COVID crash fell −33.9% in roughly **one month**. That is on the order of **ten times faster** than a normal bear market. Put differently: it covered in 33 days a distance that historically takes a year or more. The intuition: this was not a slow repricing of fundamentals — it was a liquidity panic, where everyone tried to sell at once and there were no buyers, so prices gapped down. And a liquidity panic is precisely the kind of crash a central bank *can* reverse quickly by becoming the buyer of last resort. That is the setup for everything that follows.

## The response: every lever, at maximum force, in eight days

The speed of the policy response matched the speed of the crash. Here is the timeline, and it is worth reading slowly because nothing like it had happened before.

**March 3, 2020** — The Fed made an *emergency* inter-meeting rate cut of 50 basis points, taking the upper bound from 1.75% to 1.25%. Emergency cuts between scheduled meetings are rare; the last one had been in 2008. The market barely held.

**March 15, 2020** — On a *Sunday*, with the next scheduled meeting still days away, the Fed cut again, this time by a full 100 basis points, slamming the policy rate down to a range of 0–0.25%. In twelve days the Fed had gone from 1.75% to zero. The discount-rate lever was fully pulled. (For how rate changes propagate from the policy rate out to every asset, see [monetary policy transmission](/blog/trading/macro-trading/monetary-policy-transmission-how-rate-changes-reach-markets).)

But the market kept falling — because at zero, the rate lever is spent, and the problem in mid-March was not the *cost* of credit but its *availability*. Liquidity was vanishing. So the Fed reached for the other levers.

**March 23, 2020** — The Fed announced **open-ended, unlimited** asset purchases. Previous QE programs had a number attached — QE1 was about \$1.75T, QE2 about \$0.6T. This one had no number. The statement said the Fed would buy Treasuries and mortgage-backed securities "in the amounts needed to support smooth market functioning." It was a blank check. And in the same breath it announced it would, for the first time ever, buy *corporate* bonds — both newly issued and existing — through special-purpose vehicles, plus extend credit to support consumer and business lending. The discount-rate lever, the liquidity lever, and the credit-backstop lever all fired together. March 23 was the day the S&P bottomed. That is not a coincidence.

**March 27, 2020** — President Trump signed the **CARES Act**, a \$2.2T fiscal package: \$1,200 stimulus checks to most adults, a \$600/week federal unemployment top-up, \$349B (later expanded) in forgivable small-business loans through the Paycheck Protection Program, and hundreds of billions in aid to states, hospitals, and large employers. The fiscal lever — the most *direct* of the four — was now also fully pulled. Over 2020 and 2021, total US fiscal support reached roughly \$5T.

The fiscal leg matters more than its size alone suggests, and understanding *why* is the key to the whole episode. Monetary policy is *indirect*: the Fed makes credit cheaper and hopes that lower rates *encourage* people and businesses to borrow and spend. But you can lead a horse to water — in a panic, people may not want to borrow no matter how cheap it is. Fiscal policy is *direct*: the Treasury does not lower the cost of borrowing and hope; it puts money into your checking account, full stop. A \$1,200 check is \$1,200 of spendable money that exists whether or not you wanted to take out a loan. This directness is why the fiscal leg, and not the monetary leg, is what drove the money supply (M2) higher — and ultimately why 2020 produced consumer inflation when 2008's monetary-only response did not. The deficit financing — the government borrowed the \$5T by issuing Treasury bonds, many of which the Fed then bought with newly created reserves — is the mechanism by which the two levers fused into one. The Treasury created the spending; the Fed created the money to fund it; and the combination landed as cash in household accounts. This is as close as a modern economy gets to "helicopter money," and the M2 chart is its receipt.

Read that list again. The policy rate to zero. Unlimited QE. A corporate-bond backstop. \$2.2T (then ~\$5T) in direct fiscal transfers. **Four levers, four channels, all firing within about eight days, all pushing asset prices the same direction — up.** This is what "the bazooka" means: not one big policy, but every policy at once.

### The balance sheet: the single largest expansion in Fed history

The cleanest way to *measure* the monetary side of the bazooka is the Fed's balance sheet — the total value of the assets (mostly bonds) it holds. When the Fed does QE, it creates new reserves and buys bonds, so its balance sheet grows by exactly the amount of QE. The chart of that balance sheet is the chart of the liquidity tide.

Coming into the crisis, the Fed's balance sheet sat at about **\$4.17T** at the end of 2019, having been slowly *shrunk* from its post-2008 peak of \$4.5T during the 2017–19 "quantitative tightening." By the end of March 2020 it was already at **\$4.24T** and climbing fast. By the end of 2020 it had nearly *doubled* to **\$7.36T**. It kept climbing through 2021 and peaked at **\$8.97T** in April 2022. That is an expansion of roughly **\$4.8T** — about \$3T of it in the first nine months — added to the financial system's pool of liquidity. For scale, the entire QE1–QE3 program after 2008 added about \$3.6T over *six years*; COVID QE added more than that in *two*.

![Fed balance sheet line chart from 2007 to 2025 highlighting the COVID surge to 8.97 trillion dollars](/imgs/blogs/covid-2020-infinite-qe-fiscal-bazooka-and-the-everything-rally-3.png)

#### Worked example: what a \$3T liquidity injection does to a "fair" stock price

Here is the second worked example, and it is the heart of the everything rally — how the liquidity tide and the falling discount rate translate into a *higher fair value* for stocks.

Recall the valuation idea: a stock is worth its earnings divided by the gap between the discount rate and the growth rate. Let's value the S&P 500 with simple, round numbers before and after the policy bazooka.

**Before (early 2020, but pre-panic norms).** Say the 10-year Treasury yield — the "risk-free rate" that anchors the discount rate — is about 1.9%. Add an *equity risk premium* (the extra return investors demand for stocks over safe bonds) of about 5.0%, and assume long-run earnings growth of about 4.5%. The discount rate is 1.9% + 5.0% = 6.9%. The fair price-to-earnings multiple is roughly 1 ÷ (discount rate − growth) = 1 ÷ (6.9% − 4.5%) = 1 ÷ 0.024 ≈ **41.7×**. (This is a stylized "Gordon growth" P/E; real index P/Es are lower because growth isn't perpetual, but the *direction* of the change is what matters.)

**After (mid-2020, post-bazooka).** The 10-year yield collapsed to about 0.6%. And critically, the \$3T liquidity flood plus the Fed's credible backstop *compressed the risk premium* — with the Fed promising to catch falling knives, investors demanded less compensation for risk, say 4.5% instead of 5.0%. Growth assumption unchanged at 4.5%. New discount rate: 0.6% + 4.5% = 5.1%. New fair P/E: 1 ÷ (5.1% − 4.5%) = 1 ÷ 0.006 ≈ **166×**.

That number is absurdly large because the denominator (discount − growth) gets tiny and the formula goes hyperbolic — which is *exactly the point*. When the safe rate falls toward growth and the risk premium compresses at the same time, the present value of long-dated cash flows explodes. You don't need to believe a literal 166× P/E; you need to see that the *direction and force* are overwhelming. A drop in the 10-year from 1.9% to 0.6% (−1.3 points) and a 0.5-point cut in the risk premium together nearly *double* the justified multiple even on the same earnings. The intuition: **the policy bazooka didn't make companies more profitable — it made every dollar of future profit worth far more today.** That is the engine of the everything rally.

## The everything rally, asset by asset

Now let's walk the result. The phrase "everything rally" is not loose — almost literally every asset class that exists reflated off the March 2020 low. Let's take them in turn, because each one tells you which channel did the lifting.

![Horizontal bar chart of asset returns from the March 2020 low to the 2021 peak showing the everything rally](/imgs/blogs/covid-2020-infinite-qe-fiscal-bazooka-and-the-everything-rally-5.png)

### Stocks: the V that should have been an L

The S&P bottomed at 2,237 on March 23, 2020. By August 18, 2020 — just **150 trading days** later — it had made a *new all-time high*, erasing the entire crash. That is the fastest recovery from a bear market on record; the 2008 crash took over four years to make a new high. It kept going: the S&P closed 2020 up on the year despite the worst recession since the Depression, and closed 2021 at 4,766 — more than **double** its March 2020 low.

Which channel did it? Both the discount rate and liquidity, working together. Within stocks, the *most* rate-sensitive names led: the Nasdaq 100 (heavy in long-duration tech, whose value is mostly profits far in the future, and therefore most sensitive to the discount rate) rose even more than the broad index, up roughly 138% from the low into late 2021. This is the discount-rate channel made visible — when rates collapse, the assets with the most distant cash flows benefit most, because those distant dollars get re-valued the hardest. (For the sector mechanics, see [how monetary policy moves stocks](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-monetary-policy-moves-stocks-discount-rates-sectors).)

It is worth sitting with how strange this was *fundamentally*. The S&P made a new all-time high in August 2020 while the United States was in the deepest recession since the 1930s — unemployment had spiked above 14% in April, GDP had collapsed at an annualized rate of over 30% in the second quarter, and tens of thousands of businesses had shuttered. By every measure of the *real* economy, this was a catastrophe. Yet the stock market, which is supposed to reflect the discounted value of future corporate profits, hit a record. The reconciliation is the discount rate. The market was not saying "the economy is fine"; it was saying "future profits are now worth far more in present-value terms because the rate used to discount them has collapsed, and the Fed has removed the tail risk of a credit-market meltdown." Earnings barely moved; the *multiple* exploded. A stock market hitting records during a depression is not a paradox once you understand that policy reprices the *denominator*, not the *numerator*. This is the single most important lesson of the equity side of 2020: in a policy-driven rally, you are watching the discount rate fall, not the economy improve. The two can — and in 2020 did — point in opposite directions.

Within the market, the *dispersion* told the same story. The handful of mega-cap technology companies — the ones whose business models actually *benefited* from lockdowns (cloud, e-commerce, streaming) and whose valuations were most leveraged to low rates — drove the bulk of the index's gain. A narrow rally led by long-duration growth is the fingerprint of a discount-rate-driven move; a broad rally led by cyclical, economically-sensitive value stocks would have been the fingerprint of a genuine economic recovery. In 2020–21 it was the former, which is exactly what the policy story predicts.

### Corporate credit: the backstop that barely had to fire

Remember the corporate-bond backstop — Lever 3. Here is the remarkable part: it worked almost without spending money. The *high-yield* spread — the extra yield investors demand to hold risky "junk" corporate bonds over safe Treasuries, and the single best real-time gauge of credit fear — had blown out to about **1,087 basis points** (10.87 percentage points) in March 2020 as the market priced a wave of defaults. After the Fed announced it would buy corporate bonds, the spread collapsed. By mid-2021 it had tightened all the way to about **304 basis points** — *tighter* than before the crisis. The Fed ended up buying only a tiny fraction of the corporate-bond market; the mere *promise* that it would be a buyer was enough to bring private buyers roaring back. That is the credibility channel in its purest form, and it echoes the lesson of [Draghi's "whatever it takes"](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/bernanke-qe-2008-14-the-birth-of-balance-sheet-policy) — sometimes the threat of intervention does the work the intervention itself never has to.

#### Worked example: what a spread collapse does to a junk bond's price

Let's price what that spread move was worth to an actual bondholder, because "spreads tightened 783 basis points" is abstract until you turn it into dollars.

Take a high-yield corporate bond with 5 years to maturity and a 6% coupon, face value \$1,000. Its price depends on the *yield* the market demands, which is the safe rate plus the credit spread.

**At the March 2020 panic:** suppose the 5-year Treasury yield is 0.4% and the high-yield spread is 1,087 bp = 10.87%. The bond's market yield is 0.4% + 10.87% = **11.27%**. Discounting a 6% coupon (\$60/year) and \$1,000 principal at 11.27% over 5 years gives a price of roughly **\$806** — the bond trades at a deep discount because the market is pricing serious default risk.

**By mid-2021, after the backstop:** the spread has collapsed to 304 bp = 3.04%, and say the 5-year Treasury is now 0.8%. New market yield: 0.8% + 3.04% = **3.84%**. Discounting the same \$60 coupons and \$1,000 principal at 3.84% gives a price of roughly **\$1,098** — the bond now trades at a *premium*.

The price went from \$806 to \$1,098, a gain of \$292, or about **+36%** — on a *bond*, an asset class that in normal times might earn you 6% a year. That entire move came from the credit-spread channel: the policy backstop removed the default fear, the required yield collapsed, and the price jumped. The intuition: **when the central bank promises to be the buyer of last resort for credit, it doesn't just prevent defaults — it hands existing bondholders an enormous capital gain.** This is why the riskiest credit rallied hardest in the everything rally.

### Housing: the rate lever meets a supply shortage

The Fed's QE specifically targeted *mortgage-backed securities*, which pushed mortgage rates to record lows. The 30-year fixed mortgage rate fell from about 3.7% in early 2020 to **2.68%** by December 2020 — the lowest in the history of the series. A lower mortgage rate means a buyer can afford a bigger loan for the same monthly payment, which pushes home prices up. Combined with pandemic-driven demand for more space and a chronic shortage of housing supply, US home prices (Case-Shiller national index) rose roughly **45%** from early 2020 through their 2022 peak — one of the sharpest housing booms on record. This is the discount-rate channel operating on the most leveraged asset most households own.

The mortgage mechanism deserves a moment because it shows how a policy lever reaches an ordinary household. Most people do not buy a house at a *price*; they buy it at a *monthly payment* they can afford. So the binding constraint is not the sticker price but the mortgage rate. When the 30-year rate fell from 3.7% to 2.68%, the *same* monthly payment now financed a substantially larger loan. Take a household that can afford \$2,000 a month in principal and interest. At a 3.7% rate, that payment supports a loan of about \$434,000. At 2.68%, the same \$2,000 supports about \$494,000 — roughly 14% more borrowing power, for free, courtesy of the Fed's mortgage-bond buying. Multiply that across every would-be buyer in the country, all suddenly able to bid 14% more, against a housing stock that cannot grow quickly, and prices *have* to rise. The Fed did not set out to inflate house prices, but the mortgage-rate channel did it anyway — and it created the "lock-in" problem that would haunt the housing market later, when rates rose back above 7% and homeowners sitting on 2.7% mortgages refused to sell, freezing the market.

### Crypto: the pure liquidity play

If you want to see the liquidity channel with nothing else attached, look at Bitcoin. It has no earnings, no coupons, no cash flows to discount — its price is almost purely a function of how much speculative liquidity is sloshing around and what regime of real interest rates we're in. In March 2020 it crashed alongside everything else, down to about \$4,900. As the liquidity tide rose, it ran: to about \$29,000 by the end of 2020, and to a peak near **\$69,000** in November 2021 — a gain of more than **1,300%** off the low, by far the largest move of any major asset. Bitcoin is the high-beta expression of the liquidity tide; when policy floods the system with money, the most speculative asset moves the most. (When the tide went *out* in 2022, Bitcoin fell back to about \$15,600 — the same channel, in reverse.)

### Gold: the early winner, then a laggard

Gold, the classic monetary hedge, also rose — to a then-record of about \$2,070 in August 2020, up roughly 9% from where it started the year. It led early (when real interest rates plunged and fear was high) but then *lagged* the rally, because once the panic passed and stocks and crypto were doubling, money rotated *out* of the defensive hedge and into the high-beta risk assets. Gold's biggest move came later, in the 2025–26 record run — but that is a different policy story (central-bank buying and de-dollarization), covered in [the liquidity channel](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-liquidity-channel-qe-qt-and-the-everything-bid).

The reason gold led-then-lagged is itself a lesson in *which channel was dominant when*. Gold has essentially no cash flows, so it does not benefit from a falling discount rate the way a stock or a bond does. What gold responds to is the **real** interest rate — the interest rate after subtracting expected inflation — and to *fear*. When real rates plunged deeply negative in early 2020 (because the Fed crushed nominal rates while inflation expectations held up) and fear was at its peak, gold was the obvious hedge and it ran first. But as the policy bazooka turned fear into greed and the everything rally took hold, capital chased *return*, and gold — which offers no return, just preservation — got left behind by stocks and crypto that were doubling and quintupling. Gold is the asset that wins the panic and loses the party. Its 2020 arc, leading the fear phase and lagging the reflation phase, is a clean illustration that the everything rally was, underneath, a *rotation* — first into safety as the world broke, then out of safety and into risk as policy promised to fix it.

### The dollar and the international dimension

One more channel rounds out the picture: the **currency**. When the Fed floods the world with dollars, it tends to *weaken* the dollar, because there are simply more of them. And a weaker dollar is itself stimulative for risk assets, because so much of the world's debt and so many commodities are priced in dollars — a cheaper dollar makes it easier for everyone, everywhere, to service dollar debt and buy dollar-priced goods. The dollar index did soften through the back half of 2020 as the liquidity flood took hold, adding yet another tailwind to the rally. It also meant the everything rally was not just an American phenomenon: because the dollar is the world's reserve currency and the Fed is in effect the world's central bank, the COVID liquidity tide lifted assets *globally* — emerging-market stocks, foreign currencies, and commodities all reflated alongside US assets. The dollar channel is the mechanism by which one country's policy bazooka becomes a worldwide reflation.

The pattern across all of these is the same: **a policy lever pulled, a channel opened, and an asset repriced — and because every lever fired at once, every asset repriced up.** The only differences were *timing* (defensive assets led, risk assets followed) and *magnitude* (the more an asset depends on cheap money and distant cash flows, the more it rose). That is the everything rally.

## The bill: the M2 surge and the inflation that followed

Here is where the case study earns its lesson. The everything rally was not free. The same policy machine that doubled the S&P also did something to the *quantity of money* in the economy, and that something came due about two years later.

### What is M2, and why did it explode?

**M2** is a broad measure of the money supply — physical cash, checking and savings deposits, money-market funds: roughly the money that households and businesses can spend or quickly access. When the Fed does QE *and* the Treasury sends out stimulus checks *and* banks make PPP loans, all of that lands in someone's bank account, and M2 swells.

And swell it did. M2 went from about **\$15.4T** at the end of 2019 to **\$19.1T** at the end of 2020 — a jump of **\$3.7T**, or about **+24% in a single year**. There is nothing like it in the post-war record; in a normal year M2 grows 4–6%. By the end of 2021 it reached \$21.5T. The amount of spendable money in the US economy grew by nearly a quarter in one year and roughly 40% over two.

![Bar chart of M2 money supply showing the surge from 15.4 to 19.1 trillion dollars in 2020](/imgs/blogs/covid-2020-infinite-qe-fiscal-bazooka-and-the-everything-rally-6.png)

#### Worked example: the quantity-theory link from the M2 surge to 9% inflation

This is the third worked example, and it connects the money surge to the inflation bill using the oldest equation in monetary economics.

The *quantity theory of money* says: **money supply × how fast money is spent = the price level × the quantity of real goods produced.** In symbols, M × V = P × Q, where M is the money supply, V is "velocity" (how many times each dollar gets spent per year), P is the price level, and Q is real output. Rearranged, it says that inflation (the growth rate of P) roughly equals money growth plus velocity growth minus real-output growth.

Let's plug in COVID numbers as a back-of-envelope. From end-2019 to end-2021, M2 grew about **40%** total (\$15.4T → \$21.5T). Over the same two years, real output (Q) was roughly *flat to slightly up* — call it +2% cumulative, because the economy crashed in 2020 and recovered in 2021. Velocity (V) actually *fell* during 2020 (people sat on their stimulus cash rather than spending it immediately) and then *recovered* in 2021–22 as the economy reopened — call the net change roughly −15% over the window as the initial hoarding partly unwound later.

So the rough price-level pressure is: money growth (+40%) + velocity change (−15%) − real-output growth (+2%) ≈ **+23%** of cumulative upward pressure on prices over and above what output could absorb. Spread over the following two years or so, that is on the order of **8–11% per year** of inflationary pressure — and indeed, CPI inflation peaked at **9.06% in June 2022**, a 40-year high.

This is deliberately crude — velocity is unstable and the timing is fuzzy — but the *direction* is unmistakable and the *order of magnitude* is right. You cannot grow the spendable money supply by 40% against a roughly flat quantity of goods and *not* eventually get a burst of inflation, once the money that was initially hoarded starts chasing goods. The intuition: **the everything rally and the 2022 inflation were the same policy event, seen two years apart — first the money lifted asset prices, then, as it was spent, it lifted consumer prices.** The bill always comes; it just arrives late.

### The lag: why the bill took two years

Why did inflation not show up immediately? Because money does not instantly turn into spending. In 2020, the flood of money first went into *assets* — stocks, crypto, houses — and into savings accounts, while a locked-down population could not spend it on services and travel. CPI inflation actually *fell* to 0.1% in May 2020; there was a genuine *deflation* scare. The asset-price inflation came first (the everything rally), and the consumer-price inflation came later (2021–22), as the economy reopened, supply chains snarled, and the hoarded money finally started chasing a limited quantity of goods. The lag between the policy and the consumer-price bill was roughly **18 to 24 months** — long enough that by the time inflation arrived, many had concluded the bazooka had been "free."

There is a crucial distinction buried here that the word "inflation" obscures: **asset inflation and consumer inflation are different things, and they arrive at different times.** When the Fed floods the system with reserves but the money mostly stays in financial markets — as in 2008–14 — you get *asset* inflation (stocks and bonds go up) with little *consumer* inflation (the price of groceries and rent stays flat). When fiscal transfers put money directly into household hands and that money eventually gets spent on goods and services — as in 2020–22 — you get *consumer* inflation too. The reason 2008 and 2020 produced such different inflation outcomes is precisely this: 2008 was a monetary-only response that inflated assets, while 2020 was a monetary-*plus*-fiscal response that inflated assets *first* and then consumer prices *second*. The everything rally was the asset-inflation phase. The 9% CPI was the consumer-inflation phase. They were the same flood of money, observed at two different stops on its journey from the Fed's balance sheet to the supermarket checkout.

The reopening collision made it worse. When the economy unlocked in 2021, the hoarded savings — Americans had accumulated an estimated \$2T-plus in "excess savings" above their normal trajectory — were finally unleashed onto an economy whose *supply* could not keep up. Factories were still snarled, shipping containers were stuck in the wrong ports, and there were not enough workers, chips, or cars. So you had a wall of demand (the released money) hitting a wall of constrained supply, and the only thing that can give in that collision is *price*. The 9% CPI of June 2022 was the sound of \$2T of pent-up, policy-created demand meeting a supply chain that could not flex. The money had been created in 2020; it just took the reopening of 2021 to turn it loose.

![CPI year-over-year chart showing the path from a 2020 deflation scare to 9 percent inflation in 2022](/imgs/blogs/covid-2020-infinite-qe-fiscal-bazooka-and-the-everything-rally-7.png)

#### Worked example: what 9% inflation did to a "safe" bond portfolio

The fourth worked example closes the loop by pricing the *other* side of the bill — what the eventual inflation, and the rate hikes used to fight it, did to the supposedly safe part of a portfolio.

Imagine a retiree in early 2022 holding \$100,000 in a 10-year Treasury bond yielding 1.6% — the kind of "safe" holding that anchors a conservative portfolio. Then inflation hit 9% and the Fed responded with the fastest hiking cycle since Volcker, taking the policy rate from near zero to 5.5% and the 10-year yield up to about 4.3% by late 2022.

When market yields rise, existing bond *prices* fall — that is the core mechanic of fixed income. A 10-year bond's price moves by roughly its *duration* (here about 9 years) times the change in yield. The yield rose from 1.6% to about 4.3%, a jump of +2.7 percentage points. The price change is approximately −duration × Δyield = −9 × 2.7% ≈ **−24%**. Our retiree's \$100,000 "safe" bond was now worth about **\$76,000** in price terms — a \$24,000 paper loss.

And that is only the *nominal* hit. Layer on the 9% inflation eating purchasing power, and the *real* loss was worse still. This is why 2022 was the year both stocks *and* bonds fell hard at the same time — the classic 60/40 portfolio lost about 16% — something that almost never happens, because the thing protecting you (bonds) and the thing you own for growth (stocks) were *both* repriced by the same rising-rate force. The intuition: **the bill for the everything rally was not paid only at the gas pump; it was paid in the bond market, where the rate hikes that fight inflation destroy the value of the safe assets people hold for protection.** The full story of that reckoning is in [2021–23: the fastest hiking cycle and the 60/40 wreck](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/2021-23-the-fastest-hiking-cycle-and-the-60-40-wreck).

## Common misconceptions

Even with the case as clear as it is, several myths cling to the COVID episode. Each one is worth correcting with a number.

**Myth 1: "QE is just printing money, so it directly causes consumer inflation."** Not directly. QE creates *bank reserves*, which mostly sit at the Fed and do not directly become household spending money. The 2008–14 QE created trillions in reserves and produced almost *no* consumer inflation, because banks held the reserves rather than lending them out. What made 2020 different was the *combination* — QE plus *fiscal transfers* that put money straight into household checking accounts. M2, the spendable money, surged precisely because checks went to people, not just because reserves went to banks. The lesson: it was the *fiscal* leg, riding on top of the monetary leg, that drove the M2 surge and the eventual inflation.

**Myth 2: "The Fed spent trillions buying corporate bonds."** It barely spent anything on corporate bonds. The corporate-bond facilities were authorized for hundreds of billions but the Fed ended up buying only a small fraction — single-digit billions of corporate bonds and ETFs. The *announcement* did the work. Spreads collapsed from 1,087 bp to 304 bp mostly because private buyers returned, confident the Fed had their back. This is the credibility channel: the promise is the policy.

**Myth 3: "The stimulus checks caused the inflation."** Partly, but it is more subtle. The first round of checks in 2020 was largely *saved*, not spent — which is why inflation fell to 0.1% that year. The inflation came later, when the economy reopened, the saved money was unleashed, and it collided with snarled supply chains and a limited quantity of goods. It was the *cumulative* money overhang (40% M2 growth) meeting a reopening economy and constrained supply — not any single check — that produced 9% CPI.

**Myth 4: "The recovery proved the crash was an overreaction."** No — the recovery proves the *policy worked*, which is different. The crash was a real liquidity panic; without the bazooka, it plausibly becomes an L-shaped depression with a wave of corporate defaults. The V-shaped recovery is evidence that an overwhelming, fast policy response *can* reverse a liquidity panic — at the cost of the inflation bill. Both the crash and the recovery were real; the policy is what connected them.

**Myth 5: "An everything rally means you can't lose — just buy everything."** The everything rally lasted as long as the policy did. The moment the tide went *out* in 2022 — QE became QT, zero rates became 5.5% — the same assets that rose together *fell* together. Bitcoin fell 77%, the Nasdaq fell 33%, junk spreads doubled, and the 60/40 portfolio had its worst year in a century. The everything rally and the everything *selloff* are the same machine running forward and then backward. Buying everything works until the policy reverses.

**Myth 6: "The Fed and the government acted independently; it was a coincidence they moved together."** The opposite is true, and it is the most important institutional point of the episode. The two levers were *fused*. The Treasury issued roughly \$5T of new debt to fund the fiscal response; the Fed, through QE, bought trillions of Treasuries with newly created reserves. In effect, a large share of the fiscal spending was financed by the central bank's printing press, even if no law says so explicitly and the Fed insists it does not "monetize the debt." This fusion is exactly what made 2020 a maximum-force event rather than two separate, smaller ones — and it is exactly what made the inflation that followed both larger and more durable than the asset-only inflation of the prior decade. When monetary and fiscal policy pull together, they do not add — they *multiply*, because the central bank's money creation funds the government's direct transfers, and the combination reaches the real economy in a way neither lever does alone. The independence of the central bank is a real and important thing, but in a crisis of sufficient size, monetary and fiscal policy converge into a single bazooka. Recognizing when that convergence is happening is the most valuable signal in this entire playbook.

## Case studies: the bazooka, dated and numbered

We have woven the dates throughout, but let's pin down the case explicitly, because the power of 2020 is in the precise sequence and the precise numbers.

**The crash (Feb 19 – Mar 23, 2020).** S&P 3,386 → 2,237, −33.9% in 33 calendar days. VIX peaked at 82.7 on March 16. The high-yield credit spread blew out to 1,087 bp. The Treasury market — the world's safe haven — experienced a "dash for cash" so severe that even Treasuries sold off, prompting the Fed to step in as buyer. This was a liquidity panic, not a fundamental repricing, which is precisely why it was reversible by policy.

**The response (Mar 3 – Mar 27, 2020).** March 3: emergency −50 bp to 1.25%. March 15: emergency −100 bp to 0–0.25% (zero). March 23: unlimited QE + the first-ever corporate-bond backstop. March 27: the \$2.2T CARES Act. Four levers, ~8 days. Total 2020–21 fiscal support reached roughly \$5T; the Fed balance sheet went \$4.24T (March) → \$7.36T (December) → \$8.97T peak (April 2022).

**The everything rally (Mar 2020 – 2021).** S&P bottomed March 23, made a new high by August 18 (150 trading days, fastest on record), and closed 2021 at 4,766 — more than double the low. Nasdaq 100 +138%, Bitcoin +1,300%+ (to \$69,000), home prices +45%, high-yield spreads tightened to 304 bp (tighter than pre-crisis), gold to a then-record \$2,070. Every asset class reflated.

**The bill (2021 – 2023).** M2 \$15.4T → \$19.1T (2020) → \$21.5T (2021), +40% over two years. CPI fell to 0.1% in May 2020 (deflation scare), then climbed to 9.06% in June 2022 — a 40-year high. The Fed reversed: 525 bp of hikes in 16 months (the fastest since Volcker), QE became QT. In 2022 the S&P fell 19.4%, the aggregate bond index fell 13%, and the 60/40 portfolio fell ~16% — its worst year in a century. The bill came due, and it was paid across stocks *and* bonds at once.

Set against the other case studies in this series, COVID 2020 is the *maximal* case. [Bernanke's QE in 2008–14](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/bernanke-qe-2008-14-the-birth-of-balance-sheet-policy) was QE *without* a fiscal bazooka, and it produced an asset-price recovery with almost no consumer inflation — the reserves stayed in the banking system. 2020 added the fiscal leg, which is exactly why the money reached households, M2 surged, and consumer inflation followed. The contrast is the proof: **it was the *combination* — monetary *and* fiscal, at maximum force — that reflated everything and left the inflation bill.** Monetary policy alone inflates assets; monetary plus fiscal inflates *prices*.

## What it means for asset values: the playbook

So what is the durable, transferable lesson — the part you can use the next time policy goes to maximum force?

**When you see every lever firing in the same direction, expect every risk asset to reprice the same way.** The signal to watch is *breadth* of policy: not just a rate cut, but a rate cut *plus* QE *plus* fiscal *plus* a backstop. When you see all four, you are in an everything-rally regime, and the assets that benefit most are the most rate-sensitive and most liquidity-sensitive: long-duration tech, the riskiest credit, and pure-liquidity plays like crypto. The ranking of returns in 2020–21 — crypto > tech > broad stocks > housing > gold — was the ranking of *liquidity sensitivity*, and that ordering tends to repeat.

**The discount-rate channel reprices the *most distant* cash flows hardest.** When the safe rate collapses, an asset whose value is mostly profits twenty years out (a money-losing growth company, a long-dated bond) gains far more than an asset paying you cash now. This is why the Nasdaq beat the value index and why 30-year bonds rallied more than 2-year bills. In reverse — when rates rise — the same long-duration assets fall hardest, which is the entire story of 2022.

**The credibility channel means the *announcement* can be the trade.** The corporate-bond spread collapsed on the *promise* of Fed buying, before the Fed bought much of anything. When a central bank credibly commits to backstop an asset class, the repricing happens immediately and the actual intervention may never be needed. Watch the words, not just the dollars.

**The bill arrives with an 18–24 month lag, in the form of inflation and then rate hikes.** The single most important second-order lesson of 2020 is that maximum-force reflation that reaches households *will* produce a money surge, and a money surge against a roughly fixed quantity of goods *will* eventually produce inflation, and that inflation *will* force a reversal that punishes the very assets the policy first inflated. The thing that invalidates the everything-rally trade is the *turn*: when M2 growth rolls over, when inflation prints accelerate, when the central bank pivots from QE to QT and from cutting to hiking. The everything rally and the everything selloff are one machine; the playbook is to ride the reflation while every lever points up, and to respect the bill the moment the levers reverse. (For the positioning version of this — how to actually trade around these policy turns — see [the macro-trading take on the COVID crash and the everything rally](/blog/trading/macro-trading/qe-vs-qt-how-balance-sheet-policy-moves-markets) and the [central-bank toolkit](/blog/trading/macro-trading/central-bank-toolkit-rates-qe-qt-forward-guidance).)

The deepest takeaway is also the simplest. In 2020 we ran the largest controlled experiment in the history of policy-driven asset valuation: hold the world constant, fire every lever at maximum force, and watch what happens. The answer was that *everything* repriced up — proving, in real dollars, the central claim of this entire series, that a policy lever travels through a channel and shows up as a change in what an asset is worth. And then, two years later, the same experiment proved the corollary: there is no free reflation. The bill is just paid late.
