On 22 September 1985, the finance ministers and central-bank governors of the United States, Japan, West Germany, France, and the United Kingdom met at the Plaza Hotel in New York and did something governments almost never admit to doing on purpose: they agreed to push a currency — *their own*, in the US case — down. The dollar had spent five years climbing to a height its trading partners and its own manufacturers considered ruinous. The US Dollar Index had reached roughly 164, the strongest level in the index's history. American factories were closing, the trade deficit was exploding, and Congress was drafting protectionist tariff bills by the dozen. The five governments announced they would intervene together to weaken the dollar in an "orderly" fashion.

It worked, and then it worked too well. The dollar fell from about 239 yen to roughly 150 by the end of 1986 and to about 121 by 1987 — the yen *doubled* against the dollar in two years. The slide became so fast and disorderly that the same governments reconvened at the Louvre in Paris in February 1987 to try to *stop* the very decline they had started. And the cheap money Japan deployed to cushion its now-uncompetitive exporters helped inflate one of the largest asset bubbles in history, which burst at the end of 1989 and dropped Japan into decades of deflation.

That is the whole arc of currency policy in a single episode. A government can move an exchange rate — sometimes with little more than a coordinated press release. But the exchange rate is wired into everything: trade, corporate earnings, inflation, debt burdens, asset prices, and the next country's policy response. You pull the lever, and you do not fully control where the consequences come to rest. This post walks the Plaza Accord from first principles — what the lever was, how it reached markets, what it repriced, and why its second-order effects mattered far more than the first.

![Plaza lever runs through the currency channel to reprice cross-border assets and trigger second-order effects](/imgs/blogs/the-plaza-accord-1985-engineering-a-weaker-dollar-1.png)

The mental model above runs through the entire post and is the spine of this whole series. A government pulls a **lever** — here, a coordinated FX intervention by five governments. That lever reaches markets through a **transmission channel** — here, the *currency channel*: a weaker dollar makes US exports cheaper and imports dearer, and does the reverse for everyone trading with the US. And it lands as a concrete **repricing** of what assets are worth — exporter earnings, importer costs, the burden of cross-border debt, equity multiples, and the value of every asset priced across a border. Then come the **second-order effects**, which in Plaza's case rewrote a generation of Japanese economic history. Keep this chain — lever, channel, repricing, second-order — in your head; the rest of the post is just filling it in with real numbers.

### Why the dollar got so strong in the first place

You cannot understand why the governments wanted the dollar *down* in 1985 without understanding why it had gone so far *up*. And that story is itself a policy story — a different lever, the discount rate, pulled by Paul Volcker.

In 1979–82, Federal Reserve Chairman Paul Volcker broke the back of US inflation by pushing the federal funds rate to a peak above **19%** and the 10-year Treasury yield to nearly **16%** in 1981. Those extraordinary US interest rates did exactly what the currency channel predicts in reverse: they made the dollar irresistible to hold. If you could earn 12–15% risk-free on US Treasury bills while German and Japanese rates were far lower, you wanted dollars — and so did every global investor, pension fund, and central bank. Capital flooded into the United States, and the demand for dollars drove the currency relentlessly higher. The dollar index climbed from around 85 in 1980 to roughly 164 by early 1985, an appreciation of nearly **90%** in five years.

Layer on top of high rates a second pull: the early-Reagan fiscal expansion (big tax cuts and a defense build-up) widened the US budget deficit, which the Treasury financed by issuing a flood of bonds — bonds that, at those yields, sucked in even more foreign capital. The combination of *tight money and loose fiscal policy* is, in this series' framework, the classic recipe for a strong currency: the central bank's high rates and the government's heavy borrowing both bid up demand for the currency at once. (We have a whole post on that policy mix; the link is at the end.) By 1985 the dollar was, by most estimates, 30–40% overvalued against the yen and mark on any fundamental measure of purchasing power — and the bill was being paid by American factories that could no longer export and could no longer compete with cheap imports at home.

### The political pressure that forced the meeting

The Plaza Accord did not happen because economists elegantly diagnosed an overvalued dollar. It happened because the *politics* of the strong dollar had become unbearable. The US trade deficit had ballooned to record levels; the merchandise deficit with Japan alone was enormous; and the American manufacturing heartland — autos, steel, machine tools, agriculture — was in visible distress. By 1985, Congress had introduced literally hundreds of protectionist bills, the most dangerous being broad tariff measures aimed squarely at Japan. The Reagan administration, ideologically committed to free trade, faced a stark choice: either bring the dollar down through *coordinated currency policy*, or watch Congress bring imports down through *tariffs and quotas*.

Plaza, in other words, was partly an *anti-protectionist* maneuver — a way to relieve the pressure for trade barriers by addressing the exchange rate that was causing the deficit in the first place. This is a crucial and often-forgotten point: a currency lever was pulled specifically to *forestall* a trade-law lever. It is the mirror image of 2025, when trade-law levers (tariffs) were pulled directly and the currency moved as a *side effect*. Same two policy tools — the exchange rate and the tariff schedule — reached for in opposite order, with opposite intent.

## Foundations: what an exchange rate is, and what it means to "engineer" one

Start from absolute zero, because the whole story depends on getting one convention straight.

An **exchange rate** is just a price — the price of one currency expressed in another. **USD/JPY = 239** means one US dollar buys 239 Japanese yen. **USD/JPY = 121** means one dollar buys only 121 yen. Like any price, it is set where supply meets demand: everyone who wants to *buy* dollars (to buy US goods, US bonds, US stocks, or to repay dollar debt) pushes the dollar up; everyone who wants to *sell* dollars pushes it down.

Here is the convention that trips up every beginner, so fix it now. When the USD/JPY *number falls* — from 239 to 121 — the **dollar got weaker** (it buys fewer yen) and the **yen got stronger** (it takes fewer yen to buy a dollar, so each yen is worth more). A *smaller* USD/JPY number means a *stronger* yen. When people say "the yen doubled" between 1985 and 1987, they mean exactly this: at 239, you needed 239 yen to buy a dollar; at 121, you needed only about half as many, so each yen now bought roughly twice as many dollars as before. The yen's *value* doubled even though the *number* fell. Keep that straight and half the confusion in this post evaporates.

### Why a currency's level is a policy problem, not just a market price

In a purely free market, nobody sets the exchange rate; it floats on supply and demand, like a stock price. But governments rarely leave their currency entirely alone, because the level reaches into too many things they care about:

- **Trade competitiveness.** A *strong* home currency makes your exports expensive abroad and foreign imports cheap at home. Your exporters lose market share; importers and consumers win. A *weak* home currency does the reverse: your exporters get cheaper and gain share, while imports (and imported inflation) get more expensive.
- **Inflation.** A weaker currency raises the price of everything you import — oil, food, components — feeding into domestic inflation. A stronger currency imports disinflation.
- **Debt burdens.** If you owe money in a *foreign* currency and your own currency weakens, your debt gets heavier in your own money. If it strengthens, the debt gets lighter.
- **Asset values.** Every cross-border asset — a foreign stock, a foreign bond, an export-driven company's earnings — is repriced by a currency move, often violently.

Because so much rides on the level, governments keep a toolkit for nudging it. The strongest tools are *intervention* (the central bank directly buys or sells currency in the market using its reserves), *interest-rate differentials* (raising or cutting rates to make a currency more or less attractive to hold), and — the cheapest tool of all — *the credible threat to do either*, communicated through statements. The Plaza Accord used all three at once, multiplied by five.

### What "intervention" actually means mechanically

To make its own currency **weaker**, a central bank prints its own currency (which it can do without limit) and uses it to buy foreign assets — selling its own currency, buying foreign currency. It can do this almost indefinitely, because it is spending the one thing it can always make more of; the only constraint is domestic inflation.

To make its own currency **stronger**, it must do the reverse: sell its foreign reserves and buy its own currency back. Now it is spending the thing it *cannot* print — its finite stock of dollars, gold, and other reserves. When the reserves run out, the defense ends. This asymmetry matters for Plaza: the operation was about pushing the *dollar down*, which for the US means selling dollars (unlimited) and for Japan and Germany means selling *their* reserves of dollars to buy yen and marks. The heavy lifting on the buy side fell on the surplus countries, who had the dollar reserves to spend.

Now layer on the word **coordinated**. A *single* country intervening against a deep, liquid market like FX is usually swimming against the tide — the market is bigger than any one central bank's war chest. But when *five* governments announce they are all pushing the same way *and* signal they will keep pushing, they change something more powerful than the immediate flow: they change what every other trader in the market *expects*. Suddenly betting against the dollar isn't fighting the central banks — it's *joining* them. That expectations flip is the real lever, and it is why a press release moved a market that daily intervention alone could not.

### Sterilized vs unsterilized — why most intervention is weaker than Plaza

One more piece of plumbing, because it explains why coordinated intervention is rare and why Plaza was special. When a central bank intervenes, it can do so in one of two ways. An **unsterilized** intervention lets the operation change the domestic money supply: if the BOJ sells dollars and buys yen, it pulls yen *out* of circulation, which tightens domestic money and is effectively a monetary-policy move. A **sterilized** intervention reverses that side effect with an offsetting operation, leaving the money supply unchanged — so it is *purely* an FX trade with no monetary consequence.

Almost all routine intervention is sterilized, because central banks don't want their FX operations to hijack their interest-rate policy. But sterilized intervention is also *weak* — with the money supply unchanged, it relies entirely on the signaling and portfolio-balance effects, and a few billion dollars of sterilized trades rarely move a deep market for long. This is the academic puzzle Plaza partly resolved: how did a modest, largely sterilized intervention move the dollar so much? The answer is the *coordination and the credibility* — the signal was so loud and so unanimous that it shifted expectations far more than the trades themselves. A lone central bank doing sterilized intervention is whistling into a hurricane; five central banks doing it together, with a public communiqué, are conducting the hurricane.

### Reserves: who actually had the ammunition

Recall the asymmetry: pushing the *dollar down* means *buying* yen and marks, which the surplus countries do by *selling their dollar reserves*. This is why the heavy lifting fell on Japan and Germany. Through the early-1980s strong-dollar years, both had run large trade surpluses with the US and accumulated big stockpiles of dollar reserves. Plaza asked them to spend some of those dollars buying their own currencies back — the *unlimited* direction for them (selling a foreign reserve they held plenty of), and the politically *easy* direction for the US (which simply had to not resist a falling dollar, and to sell some of its own currency, which it can always create).

That alignment of *interests* and *ammunition* is what made Plaza feasible. Everyone wanted, or could at least tolerate, the same outcome, and the countries doing the buying had the reserves to do it. Coordinated interventions fail when this alignment is missing — when one country wants the move and another is quietly resisting, or when the country that needs to do the buying lacks the reserves. Plaza is the textbook case of *full alignment*; its rarity since is a measure of how unusual that alignment is.

## Common misconceptions

Before the deep dive, clear out five myths that obscure what Plaza actually did.

**Myth 1: "Plaza worked because the governments out-traded the market."** No. The coordinated dollar sales were roughly \$10 billion over about six weeks — a rounding error against the roughly \$150 billion *a day* that traded in FX in 1985. The intervention worked because it *signaled* a credible, unanimous policy direction and flipped market expectations. The flows followed the signal, not the other way around. (We size this precisely in a worked example below.)

**Myth 2: "A weaker dollar is simply good for America."** It relieved US exporters and the trade deficit, yes. But a weaker dollar also raises import prices and imported inflation, and it punished foreign holders of US assets. And the cure had a side effect the US barely noticed at the time: it dumped a brutal competitiveness shock onto Japan, whose policy response boomeranged through the global financial system (the 1987 crash) and seeded a thirty-year deflation. "Good for America" ignores that the world is connected.

**Myth 3: "The yen 'collapsed.'"** Exactly backwards. The *dollar* fell against the yen; the *yen rose*. USD/JPY going from 239 to 121 means the yen got *stronger*, not weaker. This is the convention trap from the Foundations section — and getting it backwards inverts the entire causal chain.

**Myth 4: "Once you start a currency lower, you can stop it whenever you like."** Plaza's own sequel disproves this. By early 1987 the same five governments — now joined by Canada — met at the Louvre to *halt* the dollar's slide, because it had overshot. Stopping a trend you have endorsed is far harder than starting it, because you have taught the market which way you want the currency to go. Momentum, once policy-blessed, is sticky.

**Myth 5: "The 1989 bubble and Japan's Lost Decades were unrelated to Plaza."** They were not the *only* cause, but the line is direct: the post-Plaza *endaka* (strong-yen) recession pushed the Bank of Japan to ease aggressively to protect exporters; that cheap money inflated Tokyo land and the Nikkei; the bubble burst at the end of 1989. Plaza did not *cause* every Japanese policy mistake, but it set the table.

## The lever: how a coordinated intervention moves a currency

Let's open up the mechanism. Why does a coordinated intervention work when a solo one usually doesn't?

![Layered diagram of how five governments coordinate a dollar-selling intervention into a market-wide signal](/imgs/blogs/the-plaza-accord-1985-engineering-a-weaker-dollar-5.png)

Trace the figure left to right. The five governments — the **US Treasury and Fed**, **Japan's Ministry of Finance and BOJ**, the **German Bundesbank**, and the **French and UK treasuries** — do two things at once. First, they issue a **communiqué**: a joint statement that "some further orderly appreciation of the main non-dollar currencies against the dollar is desirable" — diplomatic language for *the dollar is too high and we intend to bring it down*. That statement is the **signal**. Second, their trading desks actually **sell dollars and buy yen and Deutsche marks** in the market — perhaps \$10 billion over the following weeks. That is the **flow**.

Here is the part that matters. The flow alone is small. But the signal does the heavy lifting by changing **expectations**. A trader who was long the dollar now faces five governments publicly committed to pushing it down and willing to keep intervening. Fighting that is a losing bet. So the rational move is to *flip*: sell your dollars, get short, ride the policy wave. As thousands of traders, real-money investors, and carry-trade positions all reach the same conclusion, the selling that actually moves the market comes not from the central banks but from the *private flows the signal unleashed*. The central banks lit the fuse; the market supplied the explosion. The result: **USD/JPY fell from 239 toward 150 within fifteen months**, and the dollar index began its long decline from the 1985 peak.

This is the single most important mechanism in currency policy: a credible, coordinated signal recruits the market to do your work for you. It is why Plaza is studied to this day, and why the mere *rumor* of coordinated intervention can move FX before a single trade is placed.

#### Worked example: the intervention was tiny — coordination did the work

Let's size the firepower against the battlefield to prove the point.

- **The intervention:** the coordinated dollar sales after Plaza were on the order of **\$10 billion** spread over roughly **six weeks**.
- **The battlefield:** global FX turnover in 1985 was roughly **\$150 billion per day** (it is over \$7 trillion a day now, but FX was far smaller then).

So the entire six-week intervention equaled about **\$10B ÷ \$150B = 6.7% of a single day's** trading. Spread over six weeks (about 30 trading days), the intervention was roughly **\$10B ÷ (\$150B × 30) = 0.22% of the volume** that traded in that window. If moving a currency were a matter of out-trading the market, \$10 billion against \$4.5 trillion of six-week turnover would have been swept away like a pebble in a river.

It wasn't, because the intervention's job was never to *out-trade* the market — it was to *re-aim* it. Five governments publicly committed to one direction told every trader which side of the boat to stand on. **The takeaway: in FX, a credible coordinated signal is worth a hundred times its notional, because it conscripts the market's own capital to push your way.**

## The channel: a weaker dollar reprices the world

Now follow the currency channel. The dollar fell; the yen and mark rose. What does that actually *do* to real businesses and assets?

![Before and after comparison of an overvalued strong dollar versus the engineered weak dollar](/imgs/blogs/the-plaza-accord-1985-engineering-a-weaker-dollar-2.png)

The before/after above captures the whole repricing. *Before* Plaza, the strong dollar (DXY ~164, USD/JPY ~239) made imported cars, steel, and electronics cheap in America and US exports expensive abroad — the US trade deficit blew out and Congress reached for tariffs. *After* Plaza, the weak dollar (DXY heading toward ~92, USD/JPY ~121) reversed all of it: US exporters and importers got relief, the trade gap began to narrow — and the squeeze moved across the Pacific onto Japan's exporters, whose government then eased money so hard it inflated a bubble.

The cleanest way to feel the channel is from the perspective of the two businesses on opposite sides of a single trade: a Japanese exporter and an American importer.

### The exporter's side: a fixed-price export earns far less at home

Picture a Japanese carmaker selling cars in the United States. To stay competitive in the American showroom, it prices a car at a fixed **\$20,000**. That dollar price is sticky — raise it and you lose sales to Ford and GM. But the company's costs (factory, workers, steel) are all in *yen*. So what matters to its bottom line is how many *yen* it gets to keep when it converts that \$20,000 of revenue back home. And that number is set entirely by the exchange rate.

![Bar chart of yen revenue on a fixed-price export car falling as USD/JPY drops from 239 to 120](/imgs/blogs/the-plaza-accord-1985-engineering-a-weaker-dollar-7.png)

The bars tell the story. At USD/JPY = 239, the \$20,000 car brings home ¥4.78 million. By 1987, at USD/JPY = 121, the *same car at the same dollar price* brings home only ¥2.42 million. The exporter did nothing wrong — didn't cut its price, didn't lose a single sale — and its home-currency revenue still got cut nearly in half. This is the **endaka** (literally "strong yen") squeeze, and it is the engine of everything that followed.

#### Worked example: the Japanese exporter's ~49% revenue squeeze

Take the \$20,000 car and run it through the Plaza-era exchange rates.

- **1985, USD/JPY = 239:** yen revenue = \$20,000 × 239 = **¥4,780,000** per car.
- **1986, USD/JPY = 150:** yen revenue = \$20,000 × 150 = **¥3,000,000** per car.
- **1987, USD/JPY = 121:** yen revenue = \$20,000 × 121 = **¥2,420,000** per car.

From ¥4,780,000 to ¥2,420,000 is a fall of **(1 − 2,420,000 ÷ 4,780,000) = 49.4%**. The exporter lost roughly half its home-currency revenue on every car, with no change in what it sold or charged. To survive, it had three brutal options: raise the dollar price (and lose volume to American rivals), slash yen costs (lay off workers, squeeze suppliers), or get its government to cushion the blow. Japan's exporters leaned hard on the third — which is exactly how this currency policy turned into a monetary-policy and asset-bubble story. **The takeaway: a currency move is the one cost shock an exporter cannot manage away — it hits revenue directly, in full, the moment the rate moves.**

But there was a fourth, slower response that reshaped global manufacturing: build the factory *inside* the customer's currency zone. If your costs are in yen and your revenue is in dollars, a strong yen is a disaster — so move the costs into dollars too. This is precisely what Japanese automakers did after Plaza, accelerating a wave of US "transplant" factories: Honda in Ohio, Toyota's joint venture in California and later plants across the South, Nissan in Tennessee. By producing in America, a Japanese carmaker pays American wages in dollars and earns American revenue in dollars, and the exchange rate largely cancels out — the currency exposure is *hedged operationally* rather than financially. The strong-yen squeeze of the late 1980s is a major reason the American South is now dotted with Japanese auto plants. It is a beautiful illustration of the currency channel's reach: a policy lever pulled in a New York hotel rearranged the physical map of where cars get built, a change still visible on the ground forty years later. The lesson generalizes — when a currency move is large and policy-driven enough, businesses don't just adjust prices; they relocate production, which is why currency policy is, over long horizons, *industrial* policy by another name.

### The importer's side: the mirror-image gain and pain

Now flip to the American side. A US car importer (or a US consumer) buying a Japanese car priced at a *fixed ¥2,000,000* sees the exact opposite. The dollar cost depends on the same exchange rate, in reverse.

#### Worked example: the US importer's cost — the mirror image

Take a Japanese car priced at a fixed **¥2,000,000** in yen, and price it in dollars across the Plaza move.

- **1985, USD/JPY = 239:** dollar cost = ¥2,000,000 ÷ 239 = **\$8,368**.
- **1987, USD/JPY = 121:** dollar cost = ¥2,000,000 ÷ 121 = **\$16,529**.

The *same yen-priced car* went from \$8,368 to \$16,529 — about **98% more expensive** in dollars. For the US *importer of Japanese goods*, this is pain: imported Japanese cars roughly doubled in dollar terms, killing their price advantage. But for the US *domestic manufacturer* — Ford, GM, Chrysler — this is precisely the **relief** Plaza was designed to deliver: the imported competition that had been undercutting them on price suddenly cost nearly twice as much, handing market share back to American factories. The same exchange-rate move that squeezed Toyota's home-currency revenue also re-opened the American showroom to American cars. **The takeaway: a currency move is zero-sum across a border — one side's exporter squeeze is the other side's domestic-industry relief, dollar for dollar.**

### The carry trade: a leveraged bet that the dollar would *not* fall

The third asset Plaza repriced was a popular speculative position: the **dollar-funded carry trade**, or more precisely here, a *yen-funded long-dollar* trade. In the early 1980s, US interest rates were high (the post-Volcker era) and Japanese rates were low. So a natural trade was to *borrow cheap yen, convert to dollars, and park the money in high-yielding US Treasury bills*, pocketing the interest-rate difference. As long as the dollar stayed flat or rose, you earned the rate spread for free. The whole trade was a leveraged bet that the dollar would *not* fall — exactly the bet Plaza was engineered to break.

#### Worked example: a dollar-funded carry trade through the devaluation

Suppose in mid-1985 an investor borrows yen to go long the dollar, at USD/JPY = 239.

- **Borrow ¥239,000,000** (worth \$1,000,000 at the 239 rate) and convert it into **\$1,000,000** of US Treasury bills.
- The trade earns the *rate spread* — say US T-bills at ~8% versus yen funding at ~6%, a +2% annual carry, roughly **+4% over two years**.
- But the investor still **owes yen.** Two years later, the dollar has fallen to USD/JPY = 121. The \$1,000,000 of US assets is now worth only **\$1,000,000 × 121 = ¥121,000,000** when converted back to repay the yen loan.

The investor borrowed ¥239,000,000 and the dollar assets are now worth ¥121,000,000 — an **FX loss of ¥118,000,000, or −49.4%** on the position. Even adding back the +4% rate carry, the position is down roughly **−47% in yen terms**. The 2% annual interest pickup that made the trade look attractive was obliterated many times over by a single policy-driven currency move. **The takeaway: a carry trade is a short position on volatility and on a policy reversal — Plaza was the policy reversal, and it turned years of patient carry into a near-50% loss overnight.** This is the same mechanism that blew up the yen carry trade in August 2024; the asset class never forgets.

### What the weak dollar did to US bonds and foreign holders

There is a subtler repricing that the exporter/importer story misses, and it matters because it shows how a currency move reaches *every* asset, not just trade-exposed ones.

Think about a foreign investor — a Japanese life insurer, say — who had bought US Treasury bonds in 1984 to capture those juicy US yields. In 1985 they hold, let's say, \$10 million of US Treasuries, worth ¥2.39 billion at USD/JPY = 239. The bonds themselves might pay their coupons reliably and even rise a little in dollar price as US rates drift down. But by 1987, with the dollar at 121, that same \$10 million of bonds is worth only **¥1.21 billion** when measured back in the insurer's home currency — a roughly **49% loss in yen terms**, regardless of what the bonds did in dollars. The currency move *swamped* the bond return entirely.

This is the hidden cost of a deliberately weakened currency: it is a transfer *away from* the foreigners who financed you. The United States had spent the early 1980s borrowing heavily from the rest of the world at high yields; Plaza, by engineering the dollar down, handed those foreign creditors a large currency loss on their US holdings. Economists sometimes call this an *implicit default through depreciation* — you repay every dollar you owe, in full, but each dollar is now worth far less in the creditor's own money. It is one reason foreign appetite for US assets can sour after a coordinated devaluation: lenders learn that the currency they were paid in can be policy-targeted downward. Keep this mechanism in mind for the 2025 discussion at the end — a dollar that falls because policy *wants* it lower, or because creditors *doubt* it, both impose the same currency loss on the people holding your bonds.

#### Worked example: the foreign bondholder's currency loss

Put numbers on the transfer. Take that Japanese insurer holding **\$10,000,000** of US Treasuries through the Plaza move, and assume the bonds' *dollar* value is unchanged (the coupons are reinvested, the price round-trips).

- **1985, USD/JPY = 239:** the position is worth \$10,000,000 × 239 = **¥2,390,000,000** in the insurer's home currency.
- **1987, USD/JPY = 121:** the *same* \$10,000,000 of bonds is worth \$10,000,000 × 121 = **¥1,210,000,000**.

The insurer's home-currency wealth fell by **¥1,180,000,000**, a loss of **(1 − 1,210 ÷ 2,390) = 49.4%** — and the bonds never missed a payment. The entire loss came through the exchange rate. To even *break even* in yen, those Treasuries would have had to *rise 98% in dollar price* over two years, which government bonds simply do not do. **The takeaway: for a foreign holder, a deliberately weakened currency is a wealth tax collected through the exchange rate — every cross-border bond is also an unhedged bet on the issuer's currency policy.**

### The German half of the story

We have focused on Japan because that is where the cascade ran deepest, but West Germany was the other major surplus country at the table, and its experience is the instructive *contrast*. The Deutsche mark also appreciated sharply against the dollar after Plaza, and German exporters felt a version of the same squeeze. But Germany did **not** ease money the way Japan did. The Bundesbank, with its constitutional horror of inflation forged by the memory of the 1920s hyperinflation, kept policy far tighter and refused to flood its system with cheap money to offset the strong mark.

The result is the cleanest natural experiment in this whole episode: two surplus economies, the same currency shock, opposite monetary responses — and only Japan got a generational asset bubble. Germany absorbed the strong mark with a slower-growth economy but no Nikkei-style mania and no Lost Decades. The difference was not the currency lever; it was the *monetary response to the currency lever*. This is the single most important lesson for an analyst: **the asset-price consequence of a currency move depends overwhelmingly on how the affected central bank responds, not on the currency move itself.** Same shock, different policy reaction, wildly different outcome.

### The full path: USD/JPY 239 → 121

Step back and look at the currency move itself, which drove every one of those repricings.

![Line chart of USD/JPY falling from 239 in 1985 to 121 by 1987 after the Plaza Accord with the Louvre marker](/imgs/blogs/the-plaza-accord-1985-engineering-a-weaker-dollar-3.png)

The chart marks the three cited anchors in red — 239 just before the Plaza meeting, ~150 by the end of 1986, ~121 by the end of 1987 — with the path between them drawn as a stylized line (a full monthly series isn't in our curated data; only the anchors are precise cited levels). The shape is what matters: a steep, almost uninterrupted slide for the first fifteen months, then a brief stabilization around the **Louvre Accord** in February 1987, then a final leg down before the rate found a floor. By any historical standard this was an enormous, fast move in a major exchange rate — the yen *doubled* in value against the dollar in roughly two years — and it was, to a first approximation, *engineered by policy*.

### The dollar index: engineered down ~40%

The yen wasn't alone; the dollar fell against everything. The Dollar Index (DXY), which weights the dollar against a basket of major currencies, tells the broader story.

![Two panel chart comparing the 1985 to 1988 dollar slide with the 2025 dollar decline](/imgs/blogs/the-plaza-accord-1985-engineering-a-weaker-dollar-4.png)

The left panel shows the 1980–88 arc: the dollar climbed relentlessly from the early 1980s to a **1985 peak of about 164** (the one precise cited anchor; the surrounding path is stylized on documented levels), then slid roughly **40% by 1988** as Plaza did its work. That climb-then-collapse is the canonical picture of an "overvalued" currency that policy decided to correct.

The right panel is the modern contrast, and we'll return to it at the end: in 2025, the dollar index fell from about **110 in January to a low near 98** in April — its worst first half since **1973** — but this time there was no Plaza, no communiqué, no five-government meeting. It fell on its own, driven by policy *uncertainty* (the 2025 tariff shock) and questions about US policy credibility rather than a deliberate coordinated push. The visual rhymes; the mechanism is completely different, and the difference is the whole point of studying Plaza.

## Case studies: the second-order effects that dwarfed the first

Here is the deepest lesson of Plaza, and the reason it belongs in every policymaker's education: **the first-order effect was the easy part, and the chain it triggered was far larger than the problem it solved.** Plaza fixed (more or less) the US trade-competitiveness complaint. But to absorb the shock Plaza handed *them*, the Japanese authorities made a series of reasonable-looking decisions that compounded into a catastrophe.

![Layered diagram of the cascade from a weak dollar to Japan's bubble Louvre Accord and the Lost Decades](/imgs/blogs/the-plaza-accord-1985-engineering-a-weaker-dollar-6.png)

Trace the cascade above. The **weak dollar / strong yen** branches immediately: it gave **US factories price relief** (the intended effect, and the protectionist tariff bills in Congress duly faded), but it dumped the **endaka recession** on Japan's exporters, who lost pricing power overnight. To cushion them, the **Bank of Japan eased hard**, cutting its discount rate in steps to 2.5% by early 1987 and flooding the system with cheap money. That cheap money didn't just stabilize exporters — it **inflated Tokyo land and the Nikkei** into a historic bubble. Meanwhile the same governments, alarmed that the dollar had now fallen *too* far, met at the **Louvre in February 1987** to halt the slide — and the policy tension between supporting the dollar and fighting domestic inflation contributed to the **October 1987 crash**. The bubble peaked with the **Nikkei at 38,957 on 29 December 1989**, then burst — and Japan spent the next three decades in the **Lost Decades** of deflation, zero rates, quantitative easing, and yield-curve control.

### Mini-case 1: the *endaka* recession and the BOJ's fateful easing

The immediate consequence of the strong yen was an export recession. With home-currency revenue cut nearly in half (our ¥4.78M → ¥2.42M worked example), Japanese exporters cut investment and the economy slowed sharply in 1986 — the *endaka fukyō*, the "high-yen recession." The Bank of Japan responded the way any central bank cushions a downturn: it cut rates, taking the official discount rate down in five steps to **2.5% by February 1987**, the lowest in its postwar history at that point, and held it there for over two years.

This is the hinge of the whole story. The rate cuts were a *reasonable* response to a real recession. But they collided with a financial system that was rapidly deregulating, banks hungry to lend, and an economy that had just been told by the world's strongest governments that its currency was going up. Cheap, abundant money found its way not into productive investment — Japanese industry was already world-class — but into *asset prices*. Land and stocks became the destination, and the bubble inflated.

There is a second, subtler channel worth naming. A *strong* yen made everything priced in foreign currency cheaper for Japanese buyers — including foreign assets. Flush with a currency that now bought twice as much abroad, Japanese institutions and companies went on a famous overseas buying spree in the late 1980s: Rockefeller Center, Pebble Beach, Columbia Pictures, trophy real estate across the United States and Europe. The strong yen that hurt exporters simultaneously gave Japanese capital enormous *purchasing power abroad*, and much of that capital chased assets at the top of a global cycle — buying high, in many cases, and selling low years later when the domestic bust forced repatriation. So the currency move didn't just inflate *domestic* Japanese assets; it funded a wave of overseas purchases that became another chapter of the same boom-and-bust. A single exchange-rate lever reached into asset markets on two continents.

The numbers underneath the mania are worth pausing on, because they show how detached prices became from any fundamental. At the 1989 peak, the aggregate value of the Tokyo Stock Exchange exceeded that of the entire US stock market, despite Japan's economy being roughly half the size. Estimated price-to-earnings ratios on the Nikkei reached around 60 — versus a long-run norm closer to 15 — meaning investors were paying sixty years of current earnings for a share. Land was more extreme still: the total value of Japanese land was estimated at several times that of all the land in the United States, a country twenty-five times larger in area. None of this reflected a Japanese economy that had genuinely become four times more valuable than the rest of the developed world; it reflected a tidal wave of cheap money, summoned by the BOJ to offset the Plaza-induced strong yen, with nowhere productive left to go.

### Mini-case 2: the Louvre Accord — trying to stop a trend you started

By early 1987 the dollar had fallen so far that the Plaza signatories grew alarmed it would overshoot into a disorderly collapse, which would itself be destabilizing. So on **22 February 1987**, the same governments (plus Canada) met at the Louvre and agreed to *stabilize* exchange rates around then-current levels — to *stop* the very slide Plaza had set in motion. It is one of the most instructive reversals in policy history: having spent eighteen months teaching the market that the dollar should fall, the governments discovered they could not simply flip the switch to "stop."

The Louvre Accord largely failed to hold the line, and the tension it created — the US needing higher rates to defend the dollar at Louvre's agreed levels, while domestic conditions argued otherwise, and Germany tightening against US wishes — is widely cited as one of the contributing strains behind the **October 1987 stock-market crash** ("Black Monday," when the Dow fell 22.6% in a single day). The mechanism is worth spelling out, because it shows how a *currency* lever can detonate an *equity* market. After Louvre, the US was effectively committed to keeping the dollar from falling further, which meant it could not freely cut rates and risked having to *raise* them. When Germany then tightened in the autumn of 1987, the implication was that US rates would have to follow to defend the dollar at Louvre's levels — a headwind for a US stock market that had run up sharply all year. Bond yields backed up, the Treasury Secretary publicly feuded with Germany over its rate hike, and into that brittle, over-leveraged, portfolio-insurance-laden market came the trigger. The crash had many causes, but the *policy* cause was the Plaza–Louvre bind: the governments had boxed themselves into defending a currency level that fought their domestic needs, and the equity market paid the bill. The lesson is stark: **a coordinated intervention can start a currency trend far more easily than it can stop one, because the signal you sent becomes the expectation the market now trades on — and the contortions required to reverse it can break an entirely different market.**

There is a redemptive footnote here that itself became policy doctrine. The Fed's response to the 1987 crash — Alan Greenspan flooding the system with liquidity and promising to support markets — worked so cleanly that it became the template for the modern "central bank put": the expectation that policymakers will ease aggressively to cushion a market crash. So even the crash that Plaza's sequel helped cause taught a lever that central banks still pull today. The threads connect: a 1985 currency meeting, a 1987 equity crash, and a reflex that shapes how every risk asset is priced four decades later.

### The deep case: the bubble, the peak, and the Lost Decades

Now the centerpiece. The cheap money Japan deployed to survive the strong yen inflated one of the great asset bubbles in financial history.

![Line chart of the Nikkei 225 rising to a 38,957 peak in 1989 then falling for decades to a 2009 low](/imgs/blogs/the-plaza-accord-1985-engineering-a-weaker-dollar-8.png)

The Nikkei 225 climbed from around 12,500 in 1985 to a peak of **38,957 on 29 December 1989** — more than tripling in four years. Tokyo land values went parabolic; at the peak, the grounds of the Imperial Palace were said to be worth more than the entire state of California, and Japanese price-to-earnings ratios reached levels that made no arithmetic sense. This was not driven by Japanese fundamentals suddenly improving by 200%; it was driven by a tidal wave of cheap money looking for a home — money the BOJ had created to offset the Plaza-induced strong yen.

Then it burst. The BOJ, belatedly alarmed by the bubble, began hiking in 1989, and the Nikkei rolled over. From the **38,957 peak**, the index fell for years, eventually bottoming around **7,055 in 2009 — a drawdown of roughly 82%** from the peak. Tokyo land prices collapsed by similar magnitudes. Japanese banks, which had lent against inflated land collateral, were crippled for a decade, and the economy slid into a chronic deflation that policymakers have been fighting ever since with the entire modern unconventional-policy toolkit: **zero interest rates (ZIRP) from 1999, quantitative easing from 2001, negative rates and yield-curve control from 2016**, and only an exit from that regime in 2024. The Nikkei did not reclaim its 1989 high until **2024 — thirty-five years later.**

#### Worked example: the scale of Japan's bubble round-trip

Put concrete numbers on the cost of the cascade Plaza set off.

- **Peak:** Nikkei 38,957 on 29 December 1989.
- **Trough:** Nikkei ~7,055 in 2009.
- **Drawdown:** (7,055 ÷ 38,957) − 1 = **−81.9%** from peak to trough.
- **Recovery time:** the index did not durably reclaim 38,957 until **2024 — about 35 years** of round-trip.

Consider an investor who bought the Nikkei at the December 1989 peak and held. By 2009 they were down about 82%; to recover that, the index had to rise roughly **(38,957 ÷ 7,055) − 1 = +452%** off the low, and it took until 2024 to get there. A 35-year wait just to break even, *nominally* — ignoring inflation and opportunity cost — is the single most expensive consequence in this entire story. And it traces, through the BOJ's strong-yen easing, back to a meeting at a New York hotel in 1985. **The takeaway: the trade problem Plaza solved was measured in billions of dollars of deficit; the second-order Japanese cost was measured in a lost generation of asset returns. The side effect dwarfed the cure.**

To be precise about causation: Plaza was *not* the sole cause of Japan's bubble. Financial deregulation, lax bank supervision, a speculative mania, and the BOJ's own delayed response all played roles. But the strong yen was the trigger that called forth the easy money, and the easy money was the fuel. Plaza pulled a currency lever to fix a trade problem, and the lever's vibrations traveled through Japan's entire financial system for thirty years.

### The toolkit Japan invented to climb out

The most lasting legacy of the post-Plaza bust is the policy toolkit Japan was forced to invent, because the rest of the world later borrowed all of it. When the bubble burst, the BOJ cut rates conventionally — but with inflation turning to *deflation*, even a zero interest rate was too high in *real* (inflation-adjusted) terms, and conventional policy ran out of room. So Japan pioneered, in order: **zero interest-rate policy (ZIRP)** starting in 1999, the first time a major central bank deliberately pinned its policy rate at zero; **quantitative easing (QE)** from 2001, creating money to buy government bonds when the rate could go no lower; **negative interest rates (NIRP)** in 2016, charging banks to hold reserves; and **yield-curve control (YCC)** in 2016, pinning the 10-year government bond yield near zero by promising to buy unlimited quantities at that level. The BOJ ended up owning more than half of all Japanese government bonds and an enormous portfolio of stock ETFs — a central bank that had, in effect, nationalized large parts of its own asset markets to fight the deflation the bubble left behind.

Every one of those tools was later deployed by the Federal Reserve, the European Central Bank, and the Bank of England after the 2008 financial crisis and again in the 2020 pandemic. The unconventional monetary policy that defined the 2010s — QE, zero rates, balance sheets ballooning into the trillions — was, in a real sense, *invented in Japan in the wreckage of the post-Plaza bubble*. The currency lever pulled at the Plaza Hotel in 1985 did not just cost Japan a generation of asset returns; it wrote the playbook that the entire developed world would run a quarter-century later. When you read about the Fed's balance sheet or the ECB's bond-buying, you are reading a sequel to a story that began with five governments agreeing to weaken the dollar. (This series' Japan-at-the-zero-bound post, linked at the end, picks up exactly here.)

### What today's analyst should extract from the cascade

The transferable insight is not "Plaza caused a bubble." It is a way of *reading* any currency-policy event for its second-order risk. When a major currency moves sharply because of policy, run the chain forward: which economy's exporters or importers just got squeezed? How is *that* economy's central bank likely to respond? If the answer is "ease aggressively," then the cheap money has to land somewhere — and an asset bubble in that economy becomes a live risk that the original currency move did nothing to suggest on its face. The 2022–24 episode of a weak *yen* (the inverse of 1985) lifting the Nikkei to new highs while squeezing Japanese households on import costs is the same machinery running in reverse. The currency channel never stops transmitting; it only changes which way it points.

## What it means for asset values: the playbook

Strip Plaza down to its transferable lessons — the things that still tell you how a currency-policy lever will reprice assets today.

**1. A coordinated FX signal is worth a multiple of its notional.** When multiple major governments publicly commit to a currency direction *and* back it with even modest intervention, position *with* them, not against. The market will do the heavy lifting. The signal to watch is unanimity and credibility, not the dollar size of the intervention. What would invalidate the read: visible disagreement among the parties (one central bank tightening while another eases), which tells the market the coordination is hollow.

**2. A weaker home currency reprices exporters up and importers down — quantify it directly.** The magnitude is mechanical: a currency that falls X% lifts a fixed-foreign-price exporter's home revenue by roughly X% and raises a fixed-foreign-price importer's home cost by roughly X%. For an export-heavy equity index (Japan then, Germany or export-tilted EM now), a sharp home-currency *appreciation* is a direct earnings headwind; a *depreciation* is a tailwind. This is why a weakening yen has, in recent years, often *lifted* the Nikkei — the exact inverse of the 1985–89 squeeze.

**3. Watch the second-order channel: how does the *losing* side respond?** Plaza's biggest market consequence wasn't the dollar's fall — it was Japan's *reaction* to the dollar's fall. When a currency move hammers an economy's exporters, ask: will that country ease money to cushion them? If so, the cheap money has to go somewhere — often into that country's own asset prices. The currency lever in one country becomes an asset-bubble risk in another.

**4. Starting a currency trend is easy; stopping it is hard.** A government can talk a currency lower far more easily than it can later talk it back up, because the first signal becomes the standing expectation. The Louvre Accord's struggle to halt the dollar — and the strains that fed into the 1987 crash — are the warning. The signal to watch: a sudden policy *reversal* (Louvre after Plaza) is a tell that the trend has overshot and volatility is coming.

**5. Not every dollar decline is a Plaza.** This is the crucial contemporary lesson, and it brings us to 2025.

### Is the 2025 dollar decline a "reverse Plaza"?

In 2025, the dollar index fell sharply — from about **110 in January to a low near 98 in April**, a roughly **10.7% drop in the first half, the worst H1 for the dollar since 1973.** Naturally, comparisons to Plaza appeared, sharpened by open speculation about a so-called "Mar-a-Lago Accord" — a hypothetical modern arrangement to deliberately weaken the dollar to boost US manufacturing, sometimes floated in policy circles connected to the 2025 administration.

But look at the mechanism, not the chart. The 1985 dollar fell because **five governments agreed to push it down and signaled so unanimously.** The 2025 dollar fell for nearly the opposite reason: **policy *uncertainty* and questions about US credibility** in the wake of the April 2025 "Liberation Day" tariff shock, which simultaneously raised the average US tariff rate toward the highest since the 1930s and rattled confidence in US assets. There was no coordinated communiqué, no joint intervention, no five-government meeting. In fact, the dollar's 2025 slide came *alongside* a US Treasury-yield *spike* during the worst of the tariff sell-off — a "safe-haven failure" in which both the dollar *and* US bonds fell together, the opposite of the orderly, policy-led descent of 1985.

The distinction is the entire payoff of understanding Plaza:

- **Plaza (1985)** was a *deliberate, coordinated, credible* policy lever pulled in the same direction by five governments. The dollar fell *because policy wanted it to*, and the market amplified a clear signal.
- **2025** was a *market-driven, uncoordinated* repricing of US policy risk. The dollar fell *despite* (and partly because of) chaotic policy, with no coordinating signal to follow.

A genuine "reverse Plaza" would require what 1985 had and 2025 lacked: multiple major governments openly agreeing to move the dollar, with the credibility to make the market believe it. A unilateral wish to weaken the dollar, announced into a market that doubts your credibility, is far more likely to produce a *disorderly* decline — and a yield spike, and capital flight — than the *orderly* engineered descent of 1985. The lesson Plaza teaches is not "governments can always weaken a currency on demand." It is "governments can move a currency *when they coordinate credibly*, and even then they cannot control where the consequences land." Both halves of that sentence are doing work.

There is also a structural reason a modern Plaza is harder. In 1985, the FX market traded roughly \$150 billion a day and capital controls still shaped many economies; a coordinated push by the major economies was a large share of the relevant flow. Today the market trades over **\$7 trillion a day** — nearly fifty times larger — and capital moves instantly and globally. The notional firepower of any intervention is a vastly smaller fraction of the battlefield than it was. That makes the *signaling* and *credibility* channel even more dominant than in 1985: the only way to move a market this deep is to change what everyone in it expects, and that requires the kind of unanimous, believable commitment that is politically far harder to assemble among today's larger, more divided set of major economies. A communiqué that the market suspects is bluster — or that one major signatory privately opposes — moves nothing. Plaza worked because every word of it was credible and every signatory meant it; that is the bar, and it is a high one.

### The one-paragraph synthesis

If you remember nothing else, remember this. The Plaza Accord is the cleanest proof in financial history that a government can move a major exchange rate with coordinated, credible policy — the dollar fell ~40%, the yen doubled, and US factories got their relief. It is also the cleanest proof that the first-order success is the *easy* part and the second-order consequences are where the real story lives: to absorb the shock Plaza handed it, Japan eased money, inflated history's great bubble, and spent thirty-five years climbing back to the 1989 high while inventing the entire modern unconventional-policy toolkit along the way. The lever was pulled in one country to fix a trade deficit; the deepest consequences landed in another country's stock market, land prices, banking system, and monetary regime for a generation. **Intervention is powerful. Its consequences are not yours to direct. That is the whole of currency policy, and Plaza is its definitive case.**
