> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The State Bank of Vietnam runs monetary policy with a fundamentally different lever than the Fed: its binding tool is a **quantity** — an annual loan-growth ceiling it hands to each bank — not a single market price, so Vietnamese assets get repriced by *how much credit is available*, not just by *what money costs*.
>
> - The Fed sets one price (the federal funds rate) and lets banks lend as much as they like. The SBV sets a **credit-growth quota** ("room") per bank — a hard cap on how much each bank's loan book may grow this year — plus a refinancing rate, a discount rate, open-market operations, and a managed currency band.
> - In a **bank-dominated economy** with a thin corporate-bond market, banks *are* the transmission pipe. So the quota is the master valve: tighten it and property developers lose their funding, margin lending dries up, and the VN-Index loses its bid — regardless of where the policy rate sits.
> - The dong is a **crawling peg**: a daily central rate set by the SBV with a ±5% band around it, drifting gently weaker year after year (USD/VND went from ~23,100 in 2020 to ~26,100 in 2025). Defending the weak edge of that band spends finite reserves — the same countdown clock every managed-float central bank faces.
> - **The one number to remember:** in 2022 the SBV hiked +200bp, defended the dong band, and a bond-market crackdown choked credit all at once — and the VN-Index fell **−32.8%**, from 1,528 to 874. When the quantity of credit collapses in a bank-funded market, the index goes with it.

In early October 2022, a 67-year-old property tycoon named Truong My Lan was arrested in Ho Chi Minh City, and within days the Vietnamese stock market began to come apart. The VN-Index, which had touched 1,528 in early April, was on its way to 874 by mid-November — a third of the market's value gone in a year. American readers watching the same calendar saw a bad-but-ordinary bear market at home: the S&P 500 fell about 19% in 2022 as the Fed hiked. Vietnam's drop was almost twice as deep, and the reasons were not the same reasons.

What hit Vietnam in 2022 was not primarily the price of money. It was the *availability* of it. An anti-fraud crackdown froze the corporate-bond market that had been funding property developers; the State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) raised its policy rates +200 basis points to defend a sliding dong; and credit, which in Vietnam flows almost entirely through banks, simply stopped reaching the borrowers who needed to roll their debt. Three policy levers — a legal one, a rate one, and a currency one — pressed down on a market that lives and dies on credit. The index did what a bank-funded market does when the credit tap closes: it collapsed.

That is the whole thesis of this post. Vietnam's central bank reaches asset prices through a different door than the Fed does, because Vietnam's financial system is built differently. Understand the SBV's toolkit — the credit quota above all — and you understand why a property stock in Hanoi, a bank stock in Ho Chi Minh City, and the VN-Index itself respond to a *quantity* of credit that an American investor never has to think about.

![SBV pulls quantity and currency levers that travel through banks and the dong band to reprice property bank stocks and the VN-Index](/imgs/blogs/the-sbv-toolkit-credit-quotas-rates-and-the-managed-dong-1.png)

The mental model is the one above, and it runs through the entire post. The SBV pulls a **quantity lever** (the credit-growth quota that tells each bank how much it may lend) and a **currency lever** (the daily central rate and the ±5% band that hold the dong inside a managed crawl), with a **price lever** (the refinancing and discount rates) playing a supporting role. Those levers reach markets through the **banks** — because in a bank-dominated economy the banks *are* the transmission system — and they land as a concrete change in **credit availability**, which reprices property, bank earnings, and the liquidity that sets the VN-Index's multiple. The Fed's lever is a price; the SBV's binding lever is a quantity. Everything downstream follows from that one difference.

## Foundations: what the SBV is, and the four tools it actually uses

Start from zero. A **central bank** is the institution that issues a country's currency and steers the cost and quantity of money and credit in the economy. The Federal Reserve does this for the United States; the State Bank of Vietnam does it for Vietnam. But "steering money and credit" can be done with very different instruments, and the choice of instrument is not a detail — it is the whole personality of a monetary system.

The **State Bank of Vietnam** is unusual among modern central banks in one structural way: it is a *ministry-level body of the government*, not an independent agency in the Western sense. Its Governor sits in the cabinet, and major policy moves are coordinated with the broader economic plan set by the Party and the government. (For the legal architecture behind that — the law that gives the SBV its mandate and the credit-quota power specifically — see the companion deep dive on [the SBV's monetary and banking law: credit quotas and the dong](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/sbv-monetary-and-banking-law-credit-quotas-and-the-dong), and for how the SBV fits alongside the securities regulator and the exchanges, [Vietnam's market legal architecture: the SSC, the SBV, and the exchanges](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/vietnam-market-legal-architecture-ssc-sbv-and-the-exchanges).) That structure matters because it makes the SBV comfortable using *direct, administrative* tools — telling banks how much to lend — that an independent Western central bank would consider too heavy-handed.

The SBV's toolkit has four main instruments. Learn them in order of how much they actually bind:

**1. The credit-growth quota (the "room").** Once a year, the SBV gives each commercial bank a ceiling on how much its loan book may grow — a percentage. A bank told it has "14% room" may grow its outstanding loans by at most 14% that year, full stop. This is a *quantity* control, and it is the SBV's most powerful and most distinctive lever. There is nothing like it at the Fed.

**2. The refinancing rate and discount rate.** These are the *prices* at which the SBV lends short-term funds to commercial banks — the refinancing rate for ordinary collateralized lending, the (lower) discount rate for the SBV rediscounting banks' high-quality paper. They are the closest Vietnamese analogue to the Fed's policy rate, but they bind far less, because the quota already decides *how much* a bank can lend before the price of funding even enters the picture.

**3. Open-market operations (OMO).** Day to day, the SBV adds or drains short-term liquidity in the banking system by buying and selling securities (and via FX swaps), nudging interbank rates and managing the money the banks have on hand. This is the plumbing — the same plumbing every central bank runs — and it keeps the system's overnight rate near where the SBV wants it.

**4. The managed dong band.** The SBV publishes a **daily central rate** for USD/VND and allows the market rate to trade within a **±5% band** around it. The central rate crawls — it is nudged gently weaker over time — so the regime is a *crawling peg* or *managed float*, not a free float and not a hard peg. Defending the band's weak edge means selling dollar reserves; this is where the SBV's foreign-exchange firepower gets spent.

Notice what is *missing* from this list relative to the Fed: there is no single, famous "the rate" that the whole market watches the way the world watches the federal funds rate. Vietnamese policy is a *bundle* of quantity and price and currency controls, and the binding one — the one that decides whether credit flows — is the quantity. To see why that is, you have to understand what kind of financial system the SBV is steering.

### Why the toolkit is different: a bank-dominated economy

In the United States, a large share of credit is created *outside* banks — in the bond market. American companies borrow by issuing corporate bonds; American homeowners borrow through mortgages that are packaged into securities and sold to investors; even consumer credit gets securitized. The banking system is huge, but it is one channel among several. So when the Fed wants to tighten, it raises the *price* of money (the funds rate), that price ripples through the deep, liquid bond market, and the whole economy reprices off a single risk-free curve. The Fed never has to tell a bank how much to lend, because the bond market does most of the heavy lifting and a price moves all of it at once.

Vietnam is the opposite. Its **corporate-bond market is thin and young**, its **equity market is still developing**, and the overwhelming majority of credit in the economy is created by **banks making loans**. A Vietnamese property developer does not, for the most part, fund itself in a deep public bond market the way a US firm does — it borrows from banks (and, in the boom years before 2022, from a fragile and lightly regulated private-placement bond market that turned out to be a house of cards). A Vietnamese homebuyer gets a bank mortgage; there is no Fannie Mae securitizing it. When credit is overwhelmingly *bank credit*, the most direct way to control credit is to control the *banks' lending volume* — which is exactly what the credit quota does.

![Fed controls the price of money through a single market rate while SBV controls the quantity of credit through per-bank ceilings and a managed currency band](/imgs/blogs/the-sbv-toolkit-credit-quotas-rates-and-the-managed-dong-2.png)

The figure lays the two toolkits side by side. The Fed (left) sets a *price* — one target rate — and trusts a deep capital market to carry that price into every asset; the currency floats and there is no band to defend. The SBV (right) sets a *quantity* — a per-bank loan-growth ceiling — because banks are the pipe and the bond market is too thin to do the work; and it manages the dong inside a ±5% band, spending reserves to hold the edge. Same goal (steer money and credit to manage inflation and growth), opposite instrument. The Fed reaches assets through the cost of money; the SBV reaches them through the *availability* of credit.

This is not a quirk or a sign of backwardness — it is the rational tool for the system the SBV actually has. A price lever needs a deep market to transmit through. When you do not have that market, you reach for a quantity lever that works *directly on the banks*. China's central bank, the PBOC, does something similar for the same reason: it leans on reserve-requirement ratios and "window guidance" and quantity tools more than on a single policy rate, because China is also (historically) a bank-and-quantity-driven credit system. The credit quota is the emerging-market answer to a problem the Fed does not have.

## The credit quota: a quantity cap that is also an allocation tool

The credit-growth quota deserves a section of its own, because it is the single most important thing to understand about how Vietnam's policy reaches markets — and it is genuinely alien to anyone trained on Western monetary policy.

Here is the mechanism, concretely. Each year (and often adjusted mid-year), the SBV looks at the economy — how fast it wants nominal GDP and credit to grow, what inflation looks like, how stable the dong is — and decides on a *system-wide* credit-growth target. In recent years that has typically been in the **14–16%** range. It then allocates that total across the commercial banks, giving each bank an individual ceiling based on the bank's health: a well-capitalized bank with clean loan books and a low bad-debt ratio gets more room; a weak bank gets less. A bank that wants more room than its initial allocation has to *apply* for it, and the SBV grants extensions selectively through the year. The room is, in effect, a license to lend — and it is scarce.

The first thing to internalize is that this is a **hard ceiling on a quantity**, not a nudge on a price. A US bank facing a higher funds rate can still lend as much as it wants; it just lends at a higher rate, and the market sorts out how much borrowing happens. A Vietnamese bank that has used up its room *cannot make another loan* until it gets more room, no matter how creditworthy the borrower or how attractive the rate. The constraint is physical, not financial.

![Stacked bars showing existing system loan stock with the new credit a credit-growth quota allows at different room levels](/imgs/blogs/the-sbv-toolkit-credit-quotas-rates-and-the-managed-dong-5.png)

The figure makes the arithmetic vivid. Take a system loan stock of roughly 13,000 trillion dong (a round, illustrative figure for the worked example below). A +12% system quota allows about 1,560 trillion dong of *new* credit for the whole year; +14% allows about 1,820 trillion; +16% allows about 2,080 trillion. That blue slice — and nothing above it — is the credit the entire banking system is permitted to create. Demand for loans can be twice that; it does not matter. The quota is the lid.

#### Worked example: a +14% quota as a hard ceiling on the system

Let's put real numbers on the lid. Suppose the Vietnamese banking system's outstanding loans total **13,000 trillion VND** at the start of the year. (One US dollar buys roughly 26,000 dong in 2025, so 13,000 trillion VND ≈ **\$500 billion** of loans outstanding — a useful anchor for a reader thinking in dollars.) The SBV sets the system credit-growth quota at **+14%**.

- New credit the entire system may create this year: 13,000 trillion × 14% = **1,820 trillion VND**.
- In dollars: 1,820 trillion ÷ 26,000 ≈ **\$70 billion** of net new lending for the whole economy, all year.
- That is the ceiling *regardless of demand*. If borrowers want 2,500 trillion VND of new loans — property developers rolling debt, exporters financing inventory, families buying homes — the system can supply only 1,820 trillion. The remaining ~680 trillion of demand goes unmet at any interest rate.
- Per bank, it cascades. A bank with a 1,000-trillion-dong loan book and "14% room" may grow it to at most 1,140 trillion. Loan number 1,141-trillion-and-one does not get made this year.

The intuition: in Vietnam the binding question is never just "what does a loan cost?" — it is "is there any room left to make the loan at all?" That is a constraint no American banker has ever faced.

The third thing to know is *how the room is set and adjusted*, because the timing is itself a market signal. The SBV announces an initial system target and per-bank allocations near the start of the year, then **adjusts through the year** — granting extra room to healthy banks that have used theirs up, holding back weak ones, and occasionally lifting the whole system target if growth is undershooting or trimming it if credit is running too hot or the dong is under pressure. Each of these adjustments is a policy event the market reads closely: a mid-year room expansion is a green light for the credit-funded sectors; a freeze or a reallocation away from property is a brake. Because the room is allocated by *bank health*, it also rewards good behavior — a bank that keeps its bad-debt ratio low and its capital strong earns more room and can grow faster, which is part of why bank balance-sheet quality is watched so intently by Vietnamese equity analysts. The quota is simultaneously a macro tool (how much credit the whole economy gets) and a micro tool (which banks are allowed to grow).

A reasonable question at this point: why not just use the price lever harder, the way the Fed does, and skip the administrative quota entirely? The honest answer is that in a bank-dominated system the price lever is a *blunt and slow* instrument. Raising the refinancing rate enough to actually choke a credit boom would crush every borrower in the economy indiscriminately and take months to bite — and it would do nothing to stop a developer from raising money in the unregulated private-bond market that bypassed bank limits entirely (which is exactly what happened in the 2021–22 boom). The quota, by contrast, is *fast and surgical*: the SBV can tighten property credit specifically, this quarter, by closing the room and raising risk weights, without having to jack up the rate on every loan in Vietnam. The cost of that surgical power is the loss of market efficiency — the quota substitutes a bureaucrat's judgment about who deserves credit for the market's price-discovery — but for a central bank steering a thin, bank-heavy system, the trade has historically looked worth making.

The second thing to internalize is subtler and more powerful: **a quota does not just cap credit, it steers it.** When the total amount of new lending is fixed and scarce, *someone has to decide who gets the scarce room* — and that someone is the SBV, through its priority guidance and risk weights. This turns the quota from a brake into a steering wheel.

![Branching diagram showing a fixed credit quota rationed by SBV priorities into funded export and SME sectors and starved property and consumer credit](/imgs/blogs/the-sbv-toolkit-credit-quotas-rates-and-the-managed-dong-4.png)

The figure traces it. A fixed quota means only so much new credit exists. The SBV's priority list and risk weights then ration that scarce room: priority sectors — manufacturing, exporters, agriculture, small and medium enterprises — are protected and get cheap, available credit, while sectors the authorities consider speculative or risky — real estate, consumer and margin lending — see their room tightened first when the SBV wants to cool things down. The quota becomes industrial policy executed through the credit valve.

#### Worked example: why the quota steers which sectors get funded

Imagine the same +14% system quota, 1,820 trillion VND of new credit, but now the SBV is worried about a property bubble and wants to channel credit toward production and away from speculation. It does not need a separate law — it just shapes the room.

- It tells banks to *prioritize* manufacturing, exporters, agriculture, and SMEs, and applies **higher risk weights** to real-estate and consumer loans (so those loans consume more of a bank's capital, making them less attractive to write).
- A bank with 200 trillion VND of room now faces a choice: lend it to a priority exporter (favored, low risk weight, the SBV is pleased) or to a property developer (penalized, high risk weight, the SBV is watching). The bank rationally tilts toward the favored sectors.
- Net effect across the system: of the 1,820 trillion VND of new credit, perhaps 1,400 trillion flows to production and consumption and only 420 trillion to real estate — even though, at market rates, developers might have bid for far more.
- The developer's problem is now stark: it cannot get the loan *at any rate*, because the room has been pointed elsewhere. A project that pencils out at a 10% borrowing cost dies anyway, because there is no credit to be had.

The intuition: with a price lever, money flows to whoever will pay the rate; with a quantity lever, money flows to whoever the central bank *favors*. The SBV doesn't just set how much credit exists — it sets where it goes. That is why Vietnamese sector rotation is so sensitive to a single policy signal: when the SBV reprioritizes the room, an entire sector's funding can switch on or off.

This is the deepest reason Vietnam's markets behave differently. In the US, if you want to know whether real estate will get funded, you look at mortgage rates and credit spreads — *prices*. In Vietnam, you look at whether the SBV has opened or closed the room for property lending — a *quantity decision*. The cap rate on a Hanoi apartment block and the multiple on a Vietnamese property developer's stock both hinge on a policy choice about credit allocation that has no price-lever equivalent.

## The price levers: refinancing rate, discount rate, and OMO

The quota is the binding lever, but the SBV does have price levers, and they matter — especially for the *cost* of the credit that the quota allows. Let's build them up.

The **refinancing rate** is the interest rate at which the SBV lends short-term funds to commercial banks against collateral. The **discount rate** is a lower rate at which the SBV rediscounts banks' holdings of high-quality short-term paper (like government bills). Together they bracket the SBV's lending to banks: the refinancing rate is the ceiling-ish cost of central-bank funding, the discount rate the floor-ish cost. When the SBV wants to ease, it cuts both; when it wants to tighten, it raises both. These are genuine policy rates — they are Vietnam's nearest equivalent to the Fed funds rate — but they are *secondary* to the quota, because no rate cut helps a bank that has run out of room to lend.

**Open-market operations** are the daily plumbing. The SBV injects liquidity by buying securities from banks (or via FX swaps that put dong into the system) and drains it by selling. This keeps the interbank overnight rate — the rate banks charge each other for ultra-short funds — near the SBV's target, and it is the tool the SBV reaches for to smooth out short-term squeezes (a tax-payment date, a holiday cash demand, an FX-defense drain). OMO is how the SBV manages the *level* of system liquidity within the quota's *quantity* ceiling.

Here is how a price move actually transmits in a bank-dominated economy — which is, again, different from the textbook Fed story.

![Pipeline showing an SBV refinancing-rate cut lowering bank funding cost then deposit rates then lending rates and finally a borrower's payment](/imgs/blogs/the-sbv-toolkit-credit-quotas-rates-and-the-managed-dong-6.png)

The figure walks the chain. The SBV cuts its refinancing rate (as it did four times in 2023, taking the refinancing rate from 6.0% to 4.5%). That lowers banks' cost of borrowing from the SBV and makes OMO funding cheaper. Banks, now able to fund themselves more cheaply, let *deposit rates* drift down — savers earn less to hold dong. With deposit costs falling, banks then ease *lending rates*, though with a lag, because they guard their net interest margin (the spread between what they earn on loans and pay on deposits). Finally the *borrower's payment* falls: a cheaper loan, which frees up demand for housing, cars, and business investment. Notice that the whole chain runs *through the banks*. There is no bond market shortcut. The banks are every link.

#### Worked example: a refinancing-rate cut reaching a homebuyer

Let's price the chain with a concrete loan. A family in Ho Chi Minh City takes a **2,600,000,000 VND** mortgage (about **\$100,000** at 26,000 VND per dollar) on a 20-year term. Before the SBV's easing, their floating mortgage rate is **11%**.

- Monthly payment at 11% over 20 years ≈ 2,600,000,000 × [0.00917 × (1.00917)^240] ÷ [(1.00917)^240 − 1] ≈ **26,830,000 VND/month** (≈ **\$1,032**).
- The SBV cuts the refinancing rate from 6.0% to 4.5% across 2023. After deposit and lending rates follow with a lag, the family's mortgage rate resets to **9%**.
- Monthly payment at 9% over 20 years ≈ **23,390,000 VND/month** (≈ **\$900**).
- Monthly saving: ~3,440,000 VND (≈ **\$132**), or about **13%** off the payment — real disposable income freed up, and a meaningfully cheaper cost of owning the home.

The intuition: even though the *quota* decides whether the loan can be made at all, the *refinancing rate* decides what it costs once it is made — and in a floating-rate system like Vietnam's, an SBV cut flows fairly quickly into household payments through the banks. Price and quantity are two separate dials, and the SBV turns both.

One more piece of plumbing worth naming, because it ties the price and currency levers together: the **interbank market**, where banks lend dong to each other overnight and out to a few months. The interbank rate is the SBV's most immediate gauge of system liquidity — when it spikes, dong is scarce in the banking system; when it sinks toward zero, dong is abundant. And here the FX leg and the rate leg connect. When the SBV defends the dong by *selling dollars and buying dong*, it is pulling dong *out* of the banking system — every dollar of reserves sold soaks up dong, draining interbank liquidity and pushing the interbank rate up. So an FX defense is *automatically* a domestic tightening: defending the currency drains dong, which raises interbank rates, which tightens credit, all without a single announced rate hike. This is why a currency under pressure in a managed-float economy tends to bring a credit squeeze with it — the two are mechanically linked through the central bank's balance sheet. The SBV can offset the drain with OMO injections if it wants to (sterilizing the intervention), but doing so spends its own firepower and works against the defense. The tension between defending the dong and keeping domestic liquidity easy is one the SBV lives with constantly, and it is invisible to anyone watching only the policy rate.

The crucial point — and the reason this series puts Vietnam in a different mental box than the US — is that the price lever and the quantity lever can pull in *opposite* directions, and the quantity usually wins for asset prices. The SBV can cut the refinancing rate (making credit cheaper) while keeping the quota tight (keeping credit scarce). Cheaper money that you cannot get is not stimulus. Conversely, it can hold rates steady but *open* the room, and credit floods in even though nothing changed about the price. When you analyze Vietnamese policy, watch the quota first and the rate second.

## The managed dong: a crawling peg inside a ±5% band

The third leg of the toolkit is the currency, and it is where the SBV's reserves get spent. Recall that an exchange rate is just the price of one currency in another: **USD/VND = 26,100** means one US dollar buys 26,100 dong, and a *bigger* USD/VND number means a *weaker* dong (it takes more dong to buy a dollar). For the full machinery of how any central bank sets, defends, and moves an exchange rate — the regime spectrum, the impossible trinity, intervention mechanics — see the within-series deep dive on [currency policy and FX intervention](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/currency-policy-and-fx-intervention). Here we focus on Vietnam's specific design.

Vietnam runs the dong as a **crawling band**. Each day, the SBV publishes a **central rate** for USD/VND, and the market is allowed to trade within **±5%** of that central rate. The central rate is not fixed — the SBV nudges it gently weaker over time, so the dong *crawls* lower in a slow, deliberate slide rather than being pinned at a single number (a hard peg) or left to the market (a free float). This is the most common regime among export-driven emerging economies because it threads a needle: it gives exporters and foreign investors *enough* exchange-rate stability to plan, while leaving the SBV *some* room to run its own credit policy.

![Line chart of USD per VND period-end 2020 to 2025 inside an illustrative band showing the dong crawling steadily weaker](/imgs/blogs/the-sbv-toolkit-credit-quotas-rates-and-the-managed-dong-3.png)

The chart shows the crawl. USD/VND moved from about 23,100 at the end of 2020 to about 26,100 at the end of 2025 — the dong weakened roughly **13%** over five years, but in a smooth, managed glide, not a one-day devaluation. That smoothness is the point of the regime: a 2–3% drift per year is survivable for businesses and absorbable for foreign investors, where a sudden 13% gap (the kind a broken peg produces) would be a crisis. The shaded ±5% illustration around the path is a reminder that on any given day, the market rate has room to move within the band before the SBV must act.

Why does the SBV want the dong to crawl gently weaker rather than hold it flat? Because Vietnam's economy is **export-led**, and a slightly weaker dong keeps Vietnamese goods competitively priced abroad. But the SBV cannot let the dong fall *too* fast, because Vietnam *imports* most of its raw materials and energy, and a collapsing dong would import inflation and frighten the foreign investors whose factories drive the export machine. So the SBV is constantly balancing: weak enough to keep exports humming, stable enough to keep inflation and capital flight in check. The crawl is that balance made visible.

Now, the hard part: **defending the band costs reserves**, and reserves are finite. When the dong comes under downward pressure — say the Fed is hiking and dollars are flooding home, or foreign investors are pulling out — the market wants to push USD/VND up past the weak edge of the band. To hold the line, the SBV must do the opposite of the crowd: it **sells US dollars from its reserves and buys dong**, soaking up the selling pressure and keeping the rate inside the band. Every day of that defense spends down a finite war chest, because the SBV can print dong without limit but cannot print dollars.

#### Worked example: defending the weak edge of the dong band

Let's price an FX defense. Suppose the SBV's daily central rate implies a weak-edge ceiling of **26,500 VND per dollar**, and selling pressure is trying to push the market rate to 26,800 — through the band. The SBV decides to hold the edge.

- To absorb the pressure, the SBV sells dollars and buys dong. Suppose over a tense month it must sell **\$5 billion** of reserves to keep USD/VND from breaching 26,500.
- Vietnam's gross FX reserves have run on the order of **\$80–100 billion** in recent years — adequate, roughly three months of imports, but not lavish. Selling \$5 billion is **~5%** of the war chest in a single month of defense.
- The arithmetic of the countdown is brutal and simple. If pressure persists and the SBV burns \$5 billion a month, the defense has a *measured lifespan*: a few months of reserves at that pace before the cushion thins to the point where the SBV must either let the dong slide faster (widen the crawl), hike rates hard to pay holders to keep their dong, or do both.
- This is exactly what happened in 2022: facing Fed-driven dollar strength, the SBV spent reserves defending the dong, *and* hiked policy rates +200bp to raise the return on holding dong — the classic two-handed defense.

The intuition: defending a *weak* currency runs on a finite resource (dollar reserves) and therefore has a countdown clock; the SBV's gentle crawl is partly a way to *avoid* ever having to make a dramatic last stand, by letting the dong ease a little each year so pressure never builds to a breaking point. A managed crawl is a controlled retreat, not a fixed line in the sand.

How much reserve firepower does the SBV actually have, and is it enough? Gross reserves have run on the order of \$80–100 billion in recent years — adequate by the standard rules of thumb (roughly three months of import cover, and broadly sufficient against short-term external debt), but not lavish for an economy of Vietnam's size and openness. That "adequate but not abundant" reserve position is itself a *reason* the SBV crawls the dong gently rather than trying to hold a hard line: a central bank with a modest war chest cannot afford a dramatic, all-or-nothing peg defense, because a determined market could drain it. The gentle crawl is the strategy of a central bank that knows its ammunition is finite — let the dong ease a little each year so the pressure never compounds into a crisis that would demand reserves it does not have. Contrast this with a reserve superpower like China, which amassed over \$3 trillion and could (historically) overwhelm almost any market; the SBV plays a more conservative hand precisely because its hand is smaller. Reserve adequacy, in other words, *shapes the regime*: thin reserves push you toward a crawl and capital controls; deep reserves let you hold a firmer line.

This currency leg also explains a constraint the SBV lives under that the Fed does not. Because Vietnam wants *some* exchange-rate stability (a managed dong), *some* monetary independence (its own credit policy), and *some* openness to foreign capital (the factories and portfolio money that fund growth), it is trying to live in the *interior* of the famous **impossible trinity** — the rule that a country can fully have at most two of {a stable exchange rate, an independent monetary policy, free capital flow}. Vietnam keeps *partial* capital controls (you cannot freely ship unlimited dong abroad and convert them), which is precisely what lets the SBV run a lower interest rate than the US *and* manage the dong at the same time. The credit quota is part of that same machinery: it gives the SBV a tool to manage the domestic economy that does not require it to match US interest rates dollar-for-dollar. Take away the capital controls and the quota, and the trinity would force the SBV to choose.

## How it all reaches the VN-Index, property, and bank stocks

We have the levers; now the valuation payoff — the "so what is the asset worth?" that this series always ends on. In Vietnam the answer runs through credit availability, and it lands hardest on three things: property, bank stocks, and the VN-Index itself.

**Property.** Vietnamese real estate is the most credit-sensitive asset in the economy, on *both* sides. Developers fund their projects with bank loans (and, pre-2022, with private bond placements that were really just disguised bank-adjacent credit). Buyers fund their purchases with bank mortgages. So a property developer's fortunes depend almost entirely on whether the SBV has opened the room for real-estate lending. When the room is open and rates are low, developers can roll their debt, finish projects, and sell to mortgage-financed buyers — values rise. When the SBV tightens the room and raises risk weights on property loans (as in 2022), developers cannot refinance, projects stall, distressed selling begins, and values fall. The cap rate on a Vietnamese property doesn't move because of an abstract discount rate the way a US cap rate does — it moves because the *credit that funds the whole sector* gets switched on or off.

**Bank stocks.** Banks are the largest sector in the VN-Index, and their earnings are almost mechanically tied to the SBV's levers. A bank's profit is, roughly, the volume of loans it makes times the net interest margin on those loans, minus credit losses. The quota *directly* sets the volume — a bank with more room can grow its loan book faster and earn more. The refinancing rate and deposit-rate dynamics set the margin. And the credit cycle sets the losses (a tight-credit bust like 2022 raises bad debts, especially property-linked ones). So a bank stock in Vietnam is a leveraged bet on the SBV's policy stance: open room + healthy margin + benign credit = soaring bank earnings; tight room + squeezed margin + rising bad debt = collapsing ones. When you value a Vietnamese bank, you are really forecasting the SBV.

#### Worked example: a Vietnamese bank's earnings as a function of the quota

Let's value the quota's effect on a single bank, because it shows how directly the SBV's quantity lever lands on a stock. Take a mid-size Vietnamese bank with a **300,000-billion-VND** loan book (about **\$11.5 billion** at 26,000 VND/USD) and a net interest margin (NIM) of **3.5%** — the spread it earns on loans over what it pays for funding.

- Net interest income this year ≈ 300,000 billion × 3.5% = **10,500 billion VND** (≈ \$404 million), the bulk of the bank's revenue.
- Now the SBV's room decision. With **+14% room**, the loan book can grow to ~342,000 billion VND, and *next* year's net interest income (at the same 3.5% NIM) is ~342,000 × 3.5% ≈ **11,970 billion VND** — a **+14%** jump in the bank's core revenue, handed to it by the quota.
- With **+8% room** instead (a tightening year), the book grows only to ~324,000 billion, and net interest income is ~324,000 × 3.5% ≈ **11,340 billion VND** — only **+8%**. The difference between a generous and a stingy quota is ~630 billion VND (≈ \$24 million) of revenue the bank either does or does not earn, decided not by the market but by the SBV's allocation.
- And the NIM itself moves with the rate levers: if a tightening cycle squeezes the NIM from 3.5% to 3.0% *while* room is cut, both terms fall together and earnings can drop outright — the 2022 pattern.

The intuition: a Vietnamese bank's top line is almost mechanically loan volume × margin, and the SBV sets the dominant term (volume, via the quota) and heavily influences the other (margin, via rates). Buying a Vietnamese bank stock is, in large part, taking a view on what room the SBV will hand it next year.

**The VN-Index.** The broad index is, to a first approximation, a liquidity-and-credit machine. Vietnamese retail investors — who dominate daily turnover — often trade on **margin** (borrowed money from brokers, which is itself a form of credit). When credit is abundant and cheap, margin flows in, turnover rises, and the index's multiple expands. When the SBV tightens credit and defends the dong, margin contracts, retail flows reverse, and the multiple compresses — *on top of* the earnings hit from a slowing economy. That double-whammy is why the VN-Index can fall so much harder than a developed market in a tightening episode: the same policy move hits both the earnings (E) and the multiple (P/E) through the single channel of credit availability.

The throughline for all three: **in Vietnam, the master valuation variable is the quantity of credit the SBV permits, not the price of money the way it is in the US.** That is the one mental adjustment a Western-trained investor has to make.

## Common misconceptions

**"The SBV's policy rate is Vietnam's version of the Fed funds rate, so just watch the rate."** No — this is the single biggest error. The binding lever is the *credit quota*, a quantity, not the refinancing rate, a price. The SBV can cut rates while keeping the quota tight, and credit stays scarce; it can hold rates while opening the quota, and credit floods in. In 2023 the SBV cut the refinancing rate from 6.0% to 4.5% — four cuts — yet credit growth stayed sluggish for much of the year because demand was weak and banks were cautious after the 2022 property bust. The rate is the secondary dial. Watch the room.

**"A managed currency means the dong is basically fixed and safe."** No — a crawling band is *designed* to move, gently weaker, year after year. The dong lost about 13% against the dollar from 2020 to 2025 (USD/VND ~23,100 → ~26,100). That is not a fixed rate; it is a controlled, persistent depreciation. A foreign investor holding Vietnamese assets earns a local return *minus* that ~2–3%/year currency drag, which has to be in the math. The dong is managed, not pinned.

**"Vietnam can run any interest rate it likes because it controls its own currency."** No — the impossible trinity still binds. The only reason the SBV can hold a lower rate than the US *and* manage the dong is that Vietnam keeps *partial capital controls*. Remove those, open the capital account fully, and the SBV would be forced to choose between matching US rates (to stop capital flight) or letting the dong float. The quota and the capital controls are what buy Vietnam its partial monetary independence — and they have real efficiency costs.

**"The 2022 crash was just the global Fed-hiking cycle reaching Vietnam."** Only partly. Yes, Fed hikes pressured the dong and forced the SBV to defend and hike. But the *deepest* wound was domestic and quantity-driven: the bond-market crackdown that froze property developers' funding. The S&P fell ~19% in 2022 on the rate cycle; the VN-Index fell ~33% because, on top of the rate cycle, the *credit that funds its dominant property sector simply vanished*. Same global backdrop, far deeper local drawdown — because the transmission ran through a credit quantity, not just a price.

**"Opening more credit room is always bullish for the VN-Index."** Usually, but not unconditionally. Room only translates into asset prices if banks are *willing* to lend (after a bust, they hoard capital and ration credit even when room is available) and borrowers are *willing* to borrow. In a confidence shock, you can open the room and credit still does not flow — the same "pushing on a string" problem central banks face at the zero bound, in a Vietnamese key. The quota sets the *ceiling* on credit, not the *floor*; demand and bank appetite set how much of the room actually gets used.

## Case studies: the 2022 shock and the 2023 cuts

### The deep one: 2022, when three policy levers pressed at once

The 2022 episode is the cleanest demonstration in modern history of how Vietnam's quantity-and-currency toolkit transmits to asset prices — because all three levers pressed down simultaneously, and the result was nearly twice the drawdown of the US market under the same global backdrop.

The setup: by early 2022 Vietnam had ridden a credit-and-property boom. Developers had funded themselves heavily in a loosely regulated private corporate-bond market — bonds sold to retail investors with thin disclosure, often really just a way around bank-lending limits. The VN-Index touched **1,528** in early April 2022.

Then three things happened at once:

1. **The legal/credit lever.** Authorities launched an anti-fraud crackdown on the corporate-bond market. High-profile arrests — the Tan Hoang Minh chairman in April 2022, then the Van Thinh Phat and Truong My Lan case from October — froze the private-bond market that had been the developers' lifeline. New issuance collapsed; developers could not roll maturing bonds. The *quantity* of credit available to property evaporated, not because of any rate, but because the funding channel was shut down and banks pulled back from property risk.

2. **The rate/FX lever.** As the Fed hiked aggressively through 2022, the dollar surged and the dong came under heavy downward pressure. The SBV defended the band — selling reserves — and **hiked its policy rates +200bp** across September and October 2022 to raise the return on holding dong. Domestic credit got both scarcer *and* dearer.

3. **The liquidity lever.** Reserves spent on FX defense drained dong liquidity from the banking system, interbank rates spiked, and the credit squeeze deepened. Margin lending to retail investors — a big driver of VN-Index turnover — contracted hard.

The result: the VN-Index fell from **1,528** to **874** by mid-November 2022 — **−32.8%** on the year. Property developers' stocks fell far more. Bank stocks fell on the double hit of slower loan growth and rising property-linked bad debt. And the dong still weakened to about 23,633 per dollar by year-end despite the defense, because the SBV chose to let the band give a little rather than burn its entire war chest.

![Bar chart of the VN-Index 2022 high versus low showing a 32.8 percent fall driven by three stacked policy forces](/imgs/blogs/the-sbv-toolkit-credit-quotas-rates-and-the-managed-dong-7.png)

The figure stacks the story onto the price. The VN-Index's −32.8% in 2022 was not one shock but three policy forces pressing together: a credit/bond crackdown that cut off property funding (a *quantity* hit), an FX defense plus +200bp of hikes (a *currency-and-price* hit), and the liquidity drain that followed. In a bank-and-credit-funded market, when the credit available to the dominant sector collapses, the index collapses with it. This is the quantity lever in its most violent form — and it is the central case study the broader Vietnam-policy post returns to in detail (see [Vietnam policy case studies: 2022 crackdown to 2023 cuts](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/vietnam-policy-case-studies-2022-crackdown-to-2023-cuts)).

#### Worked example: the VN-Index drawdown versus a US benchmark in 2022

Let's quantify how much harder the same global cycle hit Vietnam, to make the transmission difference concrete.

- A foreign investor put **\$100,000** into a VN-Index tracker at the April 2022 peak (index 1,528) and held to the November low (index 874).
- Local-currency loss from the index: (874 / 1,528) − 1 = **−42.8%** peak-to-trough (the −32.8% headline is the *calendar-year* close-to-close; the peak-to-trough drawdown was deeper).
- *Plus* a currency loss: USD/VND went from ~22,800 (early 2022) toward ~24,500 at the worst (the dong weakened ~7% intra-year), so the dollar value of the holding fell further than the index alone.
- The same \$100,000 in an S&P 500 tracker over 2022 fell about **−19%** to ~\$81,000 — painful, but the Vietnamese position lost roughly twice as much *before* even counting the currency drag.

The intuition: the gap between a −19% US drawdown and a far deeper Vietnamese one in the *same* global tightening is the transmission difference made visible. The Fed's price lever reached US assets through a discount rate; the SBV's quantity-and-currency levers reached Vietnamese assets by *cutting off the credit that funds the market itself*. Quantity shocks are more violent than price shocks because there is no rate at which the missing credit reappears.

### The mirror image: 2023, when the levers reversed

The 2023 recovery is the same machinery run in reverse, and it confirms the model. Having broken the property-credit boom in 2022, the SBV spent 2023 *easing* — both price and quantity. It **cut the refinancing rate from 6.0% to 4.5%** in four moves (March through June 2023), pushed banks to lower lending rates, *opened* credit room, and rolled out targeted support packages for the property sector and homebuyers. The dong stabilized as Fed-hike pressure peaked and then reversed.

The asset response followed the credit, with the usual lag. The VN-Index recovered through 2023 as liquidity returned, margin lending came back, and the worst of the property fear faded — though the recovery was uneven, because *opening* the room does not force banks to lend or borrowers to borrow (the "pushing on a string" misconception above). Property took longest to heal, because confidence and project completion lag credit. But the direction was unambiguous: when the SBV reversed the quantity-and-price levers, the credit-funded market turned back up. This is the clean, dated proof that in Vietnam, the credit lever — not an abstract rate — is what moves the market.

### The structural backdrop: 2025 and the tariff overlay

One more, briefer, to bring it to the present. In 2025 Vietnam faced a *different* kind of policy shock — an external one. The US announced a headline "reciprocal" tariff of **46%** on Vietnamese goods in April 2025 (the fourth-highest rate announced on any partner), later negotiated down to **20%** in a July 2025 deal (with a 40% rate on transshipped goods). Because the US buys roughly **29%** of Vietnam's exports, this was an existential question for the export engine that drives the whole economy — and therefore for the SBV's currency calculus.

A tariff that threatens exports pressures the dong (weaker exports mean fewer dollars flowing in), which pressures the SBV's band defense, which feeds back into domestic credit and rates. It is a vivid reminder that Vietnam's small, open, export-led economy imports policy shocks from abroad through the currency channel — a theme the within-series post on [how global policy reaches Vietnam: the Fed, the dollar, and tariffs](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/how-global-policy-reaches-vietnam-the-fed-the-dollar-and-tariffs) develops in full. The SBV's response to an external shock always runs through the same toolkit: let the crawl ease a little to absorb the hit, defend the band if the slide gets disorderly, and manage domestic credit to cushion the affected sectors. The tools are the same; only the source of the shock changes.

## What it means for asset values: the playbook

Pull it together into something you can act on. When you analyze a Vietnamese asset through a policy lens, the questions — and the signals to watch — are different from the US.

**Watch the credit quota first, the rate second.** The single most important signal is the SBV's credit-growth target and, critically, *how it is being allocated*. A higher system quota with room directed toward property and consumption is broadly bullish for the VN-Index and especially for property and banks. A tightened quota — or one redirected away from real estate toward "priority production" sectors — is a warning, regardless of where the refinancing rate sits. The room is the master valve.

**Read the dong's crawl as a slow tax, and the band defense as a stress gauge.** Expect the dong to ease ~2–3% a year against the dollar in normal times; bake that into any foreign-investor return. When you see the SBV *defending the band hard* — selling reserves, hiking to support the dong — treat it as a flashing signal that external pressure (usually a strong dollar / Fed hiking) is about to tighten domestic credit and squeeze the market. The FX defense is the canary; the credit squeeze is the cat.

**Value Vietnamese banks as leveraged bets on the SBV's stance.** Bank earnings ≈ loan volume (set by the quota) × net interest margin (set by rate dynamics) − credit losses (set by the credit cycle). Open room + healthy margin + benign credit cycle = soaring bank earnings and a re-rating; tight room + squeezed margin + rising property bad debt = the opposite. Forecasting a Vietnamese bank stock *is* forecasting the SBV.

**Treat property as the most credit-sensitive asset in the market, both ends.** Developers depend on the room being open for real-estate lending; buyers depend on mortgage availability and rates. When the SBV cools property credit, the whole chain — developer solvency, project completion, buyer demand, secondary prices — moves together. Property leads the credit cycle down and lags it back up.

**What would invalidate this read.** The model assumes the quota remains the binding lever. If Vietnam's capital market deepens enough that a real corporate-bond market carries a meaningful share of credit (a stated long-term policy goal), the price lever would start to matter more and the quota less — the system would drift toward the US model. Watch for a credible, liquid, well-regulated bond market. Likewise, if Vietnam liberalized its capital account, the impossible trinity would force the SBV to give up either the managed dong or its independent credit policy — and the whole toolkit would change. Until then, the quantity lever rules, and credit availability is the master valuation variable.

The deepest lesson is the one the whole series keeps returning to, in a Vietnamese accent: a policy lever reaches asset prices through a *channel*, and the channel is set by the structure of the financial system. The Fed pulls a price lever that travels through a deep capital market; the SBV pulls a quantity lever that travels through banks. Same destination — the value of property, banks, and the broad index — opposite road. If you only know how to read the price lever, Vietnam will keep surprising you. Learn to read the room, and the surprises become signals.
