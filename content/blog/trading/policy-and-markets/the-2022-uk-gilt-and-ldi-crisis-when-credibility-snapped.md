> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — In September 2022 a new UK government announced about £45 billion of unfunded tax cuts, into the highest inflation in forty years, with no independent costing. The bond market rejected the plan in days: 30-year gilt yields jumped roughly 130 basis points, the pound fell to a record \$1.035, and a hidden leverage machine inside UK pension funds — liability-driven investment, or LDI — turned the sell-off into a self-reinforcing fire sale that the Bank of England had to halt with up to £65 billion of emergency gilt-buying. The lever was a fiscal decision; the channel was lost credibility; the asset that repriced was the entire price of UK government money.
>
> - A government bond yield is not just an interest rate — it is the market's *verdict on the borrower*. When that verdict turns in days, every long-dated asset in the economy reprices at once, because the discount rate underneath everything has moved.
> - The hidden accelerant was **leverage you could not see on any official series**. UK pension funds had used LDI to hedge their liabilities with roughly 2–4× levered gilt exposure; a fall in gilt prices triggered collateral calls, which forced them to *sell gilts*, which pushed prices lower still — a doom loop that fed on itself.
> - The Bank of England faced a genuine bind: it was *hiking* rates and *selling* gilts to fight inflation, yet it had to *buy* gilts to stop the fire sale. It was easing and tightening in the same week, for two different problems.
> - The one number to remember: **49 days.** That is how long the prime minister lasted after the mini-budget. Markets can discipline a G7 government faster than any election, and hidden leverage is what turns a policy mistake into a near-systemic event.

On the afternoon of Friday 23 September 2022, the UK's new chancellor of the exchequer, Kwasi Kwarteng, stood up in the House of Commons and delivered what the government called a "Growth Plan" and everyone else immediately called the mini-budget. It contained the largest package of tax cuts in half a century — the abolition of the 45% top income-tax rate, a reversal of a planned corporation-tax rise, a cut to stamp duty, the scrapping of a cap on banker bonuses — roughly £45 billion of giveaways, all of it unfunded, all of it to be paid for by borrowing, and none of it costed by the country's independent fiscal watchdog, the Office for Budget Responsibility, which the government had pointedly declined to involve. It was delivered into an economy already running inflation above 10%, with the Bank of England in the middle of the fastest rate-hiking cycle in three decades.

The market's verdict arrived almost instantly and was brutal. Over the next three trading sessions the yield on 30-year UK government bonds — gilts — leapt by about 130 basis points, one of the largest moves in the modern history of a G7 sovereign bond. Sterling, already weak, went into freefall and touched \$1.035 against the dollar in thin Asian trading on Monday 26 September — the lowest level *ever* recorded for the pound. And then, on Wednesday 28 September, something happened that almost nobody outside a few pension-fund risk desks had seen coming: the Bank of England announced emergency, "whatever-the-scale-required" purchases of long-dated gilts, up to £65 billion, to prevent a "material risk to UK financial stability." A central bank that was supposed to be tightening had just been forced to ease, mid-crisis, to stop its own bond market from collapsing.

![When credibility snapped: an unfunded mini-budget triggers a gilt sell-off, an LDI doom loop, and an emergency BOE backstop](/imgs/blogs/the-2022-uk-gilt-and-ldi-crisis-when-credibility-snapped-1.png)

This post is a case study in two things that rarely get studied together: **a credibility shock** — what happens when a government does something that makes the market doubt its competence or solvency — and **hidden leverage**, the financial plumbing that can turn a manageable repricing into a systemic event. The mini-budget was the spark. The LDI doom loop was the accelerant. The Bank of England's intervention was the fire brigade. And the lesson, which we will build piece by piece, is the one the title promises: a government's credibility is an asset it can lose in an afternoon, and when it goes, markets reprice everything that government touches — at a speed no ballot box can match.

We have built the general *theory* of the credibility channel in [the expectations channel: forward guidance and credibility](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-expectations-channel-forward-guidance-and-credibility), and we have seen its most triumphant example in [Draghi 2012: whatever it takes and the spread machine](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/draghi-2012-whatever-it-takes-and-the-spread-machine). This post is the dark mirror of Draghi. Where Draghi *spent* credibility he had and paid nothing, the Truss government *lost* credibility it needed and paid dearly — and the Bank of England had to spend real money cleaning up, precisely because the policy came after the credibility had already snapped.

## Foundations: what a gilt is, and why its yield is a verdict

Start from zero, because the whole crisis lives inside the behaviour of one financial instrument, and most people have never thought hard about what it actually is.

A **gilt** is a bond issued by the UK government. (The name comes from the gilt-edged certificates the original bonds were printed on — a promise from the Crown was considered as good as gold.) Like any bond, it is a loan in tradeable form: you hand the government money today, and in return it promises to pay you a fixed stream of interest payments — *coupons* — twice a year, and to return your *principal* (the face value, say £100) at a set *maturity* date. A "30-year gilt" is simply one whose principal comes back in thirty years. Gilts are how the British state borrows, and the total stock of them outstanding is the national debt in marketable form.

Now the crucial idea, the one everything else hangs on. The **yield** on a gilt is the single annual rate of return that makes the present value of all its promised future payments equal to its current market price. Price and yield are two descriptions of the same thing, and they move in *opposite* directions: when buyers are eager and bid the price up, the fixed coupons represent a smaller percentage return, so the yield falls; when sellers dump bonds and the price drops, those same fixed coupons become a larger percentage return to whoever buys at the lower price, so the yield rises. "Gilt yields jumped" and "gilt prices crashed" are not two events. They are one event, described two ways.

Here is why the yield is a *verdict*. The government's promised payments are fixed — the coupon and the principal do not change. So the only way the market can express a changed *opinion* about UK government debt is by changing the *price* it will pay, and therefore the yield. If the market grows nervous — about inflation eroding those fixed payments, about the government borrowing far more than expected, about whether the whole fiscal framework is sound — it pays less for gilts, and the yield rises. A rising gilt yield is the bond market saying, in the only language it has: *we now demand to be paid more to lend to you, because we trust you less.* For an in-depth look at how the whole curve of yields is set, see [how policy sets the bond market: the yield curve](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/how-policy-sets-the-bond-market-the-yield-curve).

### Why this yield is the most important price in an economy

A gilt yield is not just the government's borrowing cost. It is the *anchor* from which almost every other long-term price in the economy is derived. A bank pricing a 25-year mortgage starts from the gilt yield and adds a margin. A company issuing a corporate bond starts from the gilt yield and adds a credit spread. A pension fund valuing its promises to retirees *decades* in the future discounts them back at a rate built on the gilt yield. An equity analyst valuing a stock discounts its future earnings at a rate built on the gilt yield. The gilt yield is the risk-free rate — the floor under everything — for the entire sterling economy. (We unpack this mechanism in [the discount rate channel: how rates reprice cash flows](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-discount-rate-channel-how-rates-reprice-cash-flows).)

So when 30-year gilt yields jump 130 basis points in three days, it is not a niche event in a corner of the bond market. It is a 130-basis-point shock running through the foundation of every long-dated valuation in Britain at once: mortgages, corporate funding, pension solvency, house prices, equities. The discount rate underneath the whole economy moved, violently, in seventy-two hours. That is why a chancellor's speech became a national financial emergency.

### Duration: why long bonds are so dangerously sensitive

One more foundation, because it is the technical heart of why the *30-year* gilt in particular was the epicentre. A bond's **duration** measures how much its price moves for a given change in its yield — roughly, the percentage price change for a one-percentage-point move in yield. Crucially, duration rises with maturity: the further away your payments are, the more their present value is whiplashed by a change in the discount rate, because you are compounding that rate change over more years.

A 2-year gilt has a duration of about 2 — a 1% yield rise costs it about 2% of its price. A 30-year gilt, especially a low-coupon one, can have a duration of *18 to 20*. The same 1% yield rise costs it roughly 18–20% of its price. Long bonds are not a little more sensitive than short bonds; they are an order of magnitude more sensitive. This is the single fact that turns a sharp-but-survivable yield move into a price *crash* at the long end — and, as we will see, it is also exactly why the pension-fund hedges that blew up were concentrated in long gilts. (For the mechanics in full, see [how policy prices real estate: cap rates and mortgages](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/how-policy-prices-real-estate-cap-rates-and-mortgages), where the same duration logic drives mortgage rates.)

#### Worked example: a 30-year gilt's price crash from a +130bp move

Let me make the duration point concrete in money, because "yields jumped 130 basis points" sounds abstract until you see what it does to a price.

Take a representative long gilt of the kind UK pension funds held in size: \$100 of face value, a low 1.5% annual coupon (typical of a gilt issued in the low-rate 2010s and maturing in the 2050s), and 28 years to maturity. (We use dollars here so a global reader can follow the arithmetic; read every \$ as the pound equivalent — the mechanics are identical in sterling.) Its price is the present value of all those payments, discounted at the market yield.

**Before the mini-budget,** the 30-year gilt yields about 3.7%. Discounting the 28 annual coupons of \$1.50 and the final \$100 principal back at 3.7% gives a price of about **\$62.0 per \$100 face.** (The bond trades far below par because its 1.5% coupon is feeble against a 3.7% market yield — you only pay \$62 for \$100 of face to lift your return to 3.7%.)

**After the mini-budget,** the yield has jumped 130 basis points to about 5.0%. Re-discount the identical cash flows at 5.0%, and the price collapses to about **\$47.9 per \$100 face.**

The price change is (\$47.9 − \$62.0) ÷ \$62.0 ≈ **−22.9%.** A single 130-basis-point move in the yield wiped out nearly a *quarter* of the bond's market value — in three days. That is duration in action: a long bond's price is a coiled spring, and a yield shock at the long end is not a flesh wound but an amputation. **A 30-year gilt is one of the most rate-sensitive financial instruments in existence, and in late September 2022 the UK's were repricing by a fifth of their value over a long weekend.**

### What "credibility" means for a government, mechanically

We will use the word *credibility* constantly, so let me define it precisely, because it is not a vague reputational quality — it is a measurable wedge in the yield. The yield a government pays decomposes, just like any risky borrower's, into layers: a baseline real rate, compensation for expected inflation, a *term premium* for locking your money up for thirty years, and — the leg this case is about — a **credibility premium**: the extra yield the market demands when it doubts the borrower's competence, honesty, or commitment to sound finances. A government with deep credibility pays a credibility premium of roughly zero; the market extends it the benefit of the doubt. A government that has just done something the market reads as reckless pays a positive, sometimes large, credibility premium — extra basis points that have nothing to do with the real economy and everything to do with *trust*.

What makes the credibility premium uniquely dangerous is that it can move almost instantly, because it is a fact about *belief*, not about the economy. The UK's productive capacity, its tax base, its debt stock — none of those changed between Thursday 22 September and Monday 26 September 2022. What changed was the market's *estimate of how the government would manage them*. The fundamentals were identical; the credibility premium repriced from near-zero to large in a long weekend, and that repricing *was* the crisis. This is why a credibility shock is so much faster and more violent than ordinary monetary transmission: a rate hike works its way through the economy over quarters, but a collapse in trust reprices the credibility premium in *hours*, and it does so across every gilt at once.

The institution at the centre of this — the **Office for Budget Responsibility** — exists precisely to *hold the credibility premium down*. Created in 2010, the OBR is an independent body that publishes its own forecast of what any government fiscal plan will do to growth, borrowing, and debt. Its whole function is to let the market *outsource its trust*: instead of each investor having to judge whether the chancellor's numbers are honest, they can rely on an independent referee who has staked its reputation on the arithmetic. When the Truss government delivered £45 billion of tax cuts *without* an OBR costing, it did not merely skip a formality — it removed the very mechanism that keeps the credibility premium near zero. The market, deprived of the referee, had to price the trust itself, and it priced it harshly. Traders gave the extra yield a blunt nickname at the time: the "moron premium." It was the credibility premium, made suddenly visible, on a G7 sovereign.

## The spark: an unfunded mini-budget into a credibility vacuum

Now we can see why the mini-budget detonated. To the market, three things about it were alarming, and they compounded.

**First, the scale and the funding.** Roughly £45 billion of permanent tax cuts is a large number — comparable to about 1.5% of UK GDP — and the government proposed to pay for none of it with offsetting spending cuts or revenue. It would all be borrowed. That meant the government's debt issuance — the supply of new gilts the market would have to absorb — was about to balloon at exactly the moment the Bank of England was *withdrawing* as a buyer (more on that collision below). More supply, less demand: gilt prices had to fall, and yields had to rise, just to clear the market. This is the mechanical, supply-and-demand layer of the shock.

**Second, and more corrosively, the *process*.** A British chancellor delivering a fiscal package of this size would normally publish an independent costing from the Office for Budget Responsibility — the body created in 2010 precisely to give markets confidence that the government's numbers were honest and its debt path sustainable. The Truss government deliberately bypassed the OBR. To the market, refusing the independent referee was not a procedural footnote; it was a signal that the government either did not know or did not care what its plan would do to the public finances. The mini-budget arrived *uncosted, unchecked, and unanchored.* That is what turned a large-but-quantifiable fiscal loosening into a *credibility* event. The market was no longer just pricing more borrowing; it was pricing a government it could no longer trust to manage the borrowing.

**Third, the *contradiction with monetary policy*.** The whole package was designed to *boost demand* — put more money in people's pockets, stimulate spending — at the precise moment the Bank of England was trying to *crush demand* to bring inflation down from above 10%. The government was pressing the accelerator while the central bank stamped on the brake. A market watching the fiscal and monetary arms of the state pull in opposite directions, with no coordinating plan, concluded that one of two things had to give: either the Bank would have to hike *much* harder to offset the fiscal stimulus (bad for bonds), or inflation would run away (also bad for bonds). Either way, gilts were worth less.

![Matrix of the fiscal-monetary collision: the Treasury loosening while the Bank tightens, and what the gilt market saw](/imgs/blogs/the-2022-uk-gilt-and-ldi-crisis-when-credibility-snapped-4.png)

### The currency told the same story

Sterling's collapse to \$1.035 was the same verdict expressed in a different market. A currency's value reflects, among other things, foreign investors' willingness to hold a country's assets. When those investors saw an uncosted fiscal blowout, a credibility breach, and a policy at war with the central bank, many concluded UK assets had become riskier — and they sold the pound to get out, or demanded a cheaper entry point to come in. A falling currency *and* rising bond yields *at the same time* is a particularly ominous combination: normally, higher yields attract foreign capital and *support* a currency. When yields rise and the currency falls *together*, it means the higher yield is not attracting capital — it is *compensation for fear*, and capital is fleeing anyway. That twin move is the classic signature of a genuine confidence crisis, the kind usually seen in emerging markets, not G7 economies. (We dissect this mechanism in [the currency channel: rate differentials and carry](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-currency-channel-rate-differentials-and-carry).)

![Cable falls to an all-time low of 1.035 dollars around the September 2022 mini-budget](/imgs/blogs/the-2022-uk-gilt-and-ldi-crisis-when-credibility-snapped-6.png)

#### Worked example: the credibility premium, costed in real money

Let me put a number on the "moron premium" — the credibility leg the market bolted onto UK borrowing costs — because it shows that a loss of trust is not a vibe; it is a measurable, recurring cash cost to the taxpayer.

The cleanest way to isolate it is to compare the UK's borrowing cost to a credible benchmark *of the same maturity* before and after the shock. Before the mini-budget, the UK 10-year gilt yielded roughly the same as, or a touch above, comparable safe sovereigns once you adjusted for inflation expectations — call the UK-specific credibility wedge effectively *0 basis points*. In the days after the mini-budget, UK gilts cheapened sharply *relative* to those benchmarks: the gap widened by roughly **70–100 basis points** of pure UK-specific risk that had not been there the week before — the part the market was charging for *the UK government specifically*, after stripping out the global rate moves everyone faced.

Now cost it. The UK had roughly **£2.4 trillion** of government debt. A persistent credibility premium of even **0.5 percentage points** across that stock, as it rolls over and is refinanced, is 0.005 × \$2,400,000,000,000 = **\$12 billion per year** in extra interest — money the taxpayer pays not for any service, not for any spending, but purely as a fine for lost trust. (Not all of the debt reprices at once — much is locked in at old rates until it matures — so the bill builds gradually as bonds roll. But the *flow* cost of a half-point credibility premium on a debt stock that size is on the order of ten billion dollars a year.) **That is the tangible price of a credibility breach: a government that loses the market's trust does not just suffer a bad week of headlines; it pays a recurring, multi-billion-dollar tax on its own borrowing until the trust is rebuilt — which is exactly why reversing the mini-budget, fast, was worth far more than the £45 billion it nominally saved.**

#### Worked example: the pound's devaluation on an importer's costs

Let me show what a currency move from \$1.12 to \$1.035 actually *does* to a real British business, because the abstract "the pound fell" hides a concrete squeeze.

Take a UK manufacturer that imports \$1,000,000 of components from abroad each month, priced in US dollars (as most globally traded inputs — energy, metals, electronics — effectively are). The firm earns in pounds and pays in dollars, so the exchange rate sets its real cost.

**Before the crisis,** with the pound at \$1.12, buying \$1,000,000 of components costs the firm \$1,000,000 ÷ 1.12 = **£892,857.**

**After the crisis,** with the pound at \$1.035, the *same* \$1,000,000 of components now costs \$1,000,000 ÷ 1.035 = **£966,184.**

The firm's import bill has risen by £966,184 − £892,857 = **£73,327 per month — an 8.2% increase** — for buying exactly the same goods. Nothing about the components changed; only the currency did. Across a year that is nearly £880,000 of extra cost, straight off the bottom line or passed on to customers as higher prices. **A currency devaluation is an across-the-board tax on every imported input, and because it raises the price of the energy and goods a country buys from abroad, it feeds *back* into the very inflation the central bank was already fighting** — which is exactly why a credibility-driven currency slide makes the central bank's job harder, not easier.

## The accelerant: the LDI doom loop

Here is where the story stops being a familiar tale of a bad budget and becomes something more dangerous and more instructive. The gilt sell-off, on its own, was a serious but survivable repricing. What turned it into a near-systemic event was a piece of financial plumbing that almost no one outside the pensions industry had heard of, and that appeared on no official statistic: **liability-driven investment**, or LDI.

### What LDI is, and why it exists

To understand LDI you have to understand the problem it was built to solve. A *defined-benefit* pension fund — the old-fashioned kind that promises retirees a fixed income for life — has a peculiar balance sheet. Its **liabilities** are a long stream of payments stretching decades into the future. The present value of those liabilities behaves *exactly like a very long bond*: when interest rates fall, the discounted value of those distant promises *rises* (you discount them at a lower rate), and the fund's liabilities balloon. When rates rise, the liabilities shrink. In duration terms, a pension fund's liabilities have a duration of 15–20 years or more — they are, financially, a giant long-bond *short position*.

Through the 2010s, with rates grinding ever lower, pension funds watched their liabilities swell and their funding positions deteriorate, even as their asset portfolios did fine. The fix, recommended by consultants and adopted across the industry, was LDI: hold assets whose value *also* rises when rates fall, to offset the rising liabilities. The natural such asset is — long gilts, and interest-rate derivatives (swaps and repo) that behave like long gilts. Match the duration of your assets to the duration of your liabilities, and rate moves no longer blow a hole in your funding level. It is, in principle, a *prudent* hedge. The fund is reducing risk, not taking it.

### The leverage hidden inside the hedge

But there is a catch, and the catch is the whole story. A pension fund's liabilities might have a duration of 20, but the fund does not have enough cash to buy 20-duration's worth of physical gilts to match them — most of its money is in equities and other return-seeking assets it needs for growth. So to get enough interest-rate *exposure* from a limited pot of cash, LDI strategies used **leverage**: through repo (borrowing against gilts) and interest-rate swaps, a fund could get, say, £3 of gilt exposure for every £1 of cash actually committed. That is roughly 3× leverage. The hedge worked beautifully as long as rates moved gently — but leverage cuts both ways, and it comes with a tripwire called a *collateral call*.

When you hold a leveraged position, your counterparty (the bank on the other side of the swap or repo) requires you to post **collateral** — a cash or gilt cushion — to cover potential losses. If your position *loses* value, the counterparty demands *more* collateral, immediately, to keep the cushion intact. This is a **margin call** or collateral call. If you cannot post it in time, the counterparty closes out your position — sells your gilts — to protect itself. The LDI funds had sized their collateral buffers for *normal* rate moves: a buffer that could absorb maybe a 100-basis-point move in yields before a call was triggered. A 100bp buffer is generous by the standards of a calm market. It was nowhere near enough for what was coming.

![Before-and-after of an LDI fund's collateral: a 130bp move wipes out a buffer built for 100bp and triggers a cash call](/imgs/blogs/the-2022-uk-gilt-and-ldi-crisis-when-credibility-snapped-3.png)

### How the leverage was actually built: repo and swaps

It is worth opening the hood on *how* a pension fund gets £3 of gilt exposure for £1 of cash, because the two mechanisms it used are exactly the ones that generated the collateral calls. The first is **repo** — a repurchase agreement. The fund buys a gilt, then immediately uses that gilt as collateral to borrow cash from a bank (it "repos out" the gilt), and uses the borrowed cash to buy *another* gilt, which it repos again, and so on. Each turn of the loop adds gilt exposure on borrowed money. The fund ends up owning a large pile of gilts financed mostly by short-term borrowing secured against those same gilts. The catch: if the gilts fall in value, the lender's collateral is now worth less than the loan, so the lender issues a **margin call** — post more cash or more gilts to top up the collateral — and if the fund cannot, the lender sells the gilts to recover its money.

The second mechanism is the **interest-rate swap**. Here the fund does not buy a physical gilt at all; it enters a contract with a bank to *receive* a fixed long-term interest rate and *pay* a floating short-term rate, on a large notional amount. Economically, receiving fixed on a swap behaves almost exactly like owning a long gilt — its value rises when rates fall and falls when rates rise — but it ties up far less cash up front. The swap, too, is collateralised: as it moves against the fund (when long rates *rise*, the receive-fixed position loses value), the bank on the other side demands variation margin — *post cash now to cover the loss*. So whether the leverage came through repo or swaps, the structure was the same: a small amount of cash controlling a large gilt-like exposure, with a contractual obligation to find *more cash, immediately,* the moment that exposure lost value. In a gentle market this was invisible and harmless. In a 130-basis-point three-day move it became a synchronised, industry-wide demand for cash that no one had.

### The loop closes

Now assemble the machine and watch it run. Gilt yields jump 130 basis points — *more* than the 100bp buffer the LDI funds had set aside. Their leveraged gilt and swap positions lose value far beyond the cushion. Counterparties issue collateral calls: *post more cash, now, or we close you out.* But the funds do not have piles of spare cash — that was the whole reason they used leverage in the first place. So to raise the cash, they sell the most liquid asset they own: **gilts.** And not just any gilts — *long* gilts, the very instruments whose prices were already crashing. The forced selling pushes gilt prices *lower still*, which raises yields *further*, which inflicts *bigger* losses on every other LDI fund's positions, which triggers *more* collateral calls, which forces *more* gilt sales. The hedge had inverted into its opposite: a strategy designed to *reduce* risk had become a mechanism for *forced selling into a falling market*, and the selling was feeding the fall.

This is a **doom loop** — a self-reinforcing spiral with no natural floor. Each turn makes the next turn worse. Left alone, it does not converge to a new equilibrium; it accelerates toward a disorderly collapse of the long-gilt market, taking the solvency of a large slice of the UK pension system with it. The Bank of England later estimated that without intervention, a number of LDI funds would have been forced into insolvency within hours, and the long end of the gilt market — the foundation of UK pension and mortgage pricing — would have ceased to function.

![The LDI doom loop: falling gilt prices trigger collateral calls that force gilt sales that push prices lower, until a bigger buyer caps it](/imgs/blogs/the-2022-uk-gilt-and-ldi-crisis-when-credibility-snapped-2.png)

#### Worked example: the LDI doom loop on a 3×-levered pension hedge

Let me run the numbers on a single, representative LDI fund, because the spiral is only frightening when you see how fast the cash call exceeds what the fund can pay.

A pension scheme has \$100 million of fund assets. To hedge liabilities with a duration of about 18, it uses LDI to hold **\$300 million of long-gilt exposure** — 3× leverage — through a mix of physical gilts, repo, and swaps. The modified duration of that gilt exposure is about 18, meaning a 1-percentage-point rise in yields costs about 18% of the exposure's value.

**The shock:** 30-year gilt yields rise 130 basis points (1.30 percentage points). The mark-to-market loss on the \$300 million of exposure is approximately:

- Loss ≈ duration × yield change × exposure = 18 × 0.0130 × \$300,000,000 ≈ **\$70 million.**

That \$70 million loss must be met with collateral *now*. The fund had set aside a buffer sized for a ~100bp move — about 18 × 0.0100 × \$300m ≈ \$54 million. The buffer is **wiped out and then some**: the \$70 million call exceeds the \$54 million buffer by \$16 million, and the fund's *entire remaining liquid cash* is a fraction of its \$100 million in assets, most of which is tied up in equities it cannot sell instantly at fair value. To meet the call, the fund must sell gilts into a market that is already in free fall — and every other LDI fund in the country is doing exactly the same thing, at exactly the same moment, for exactly the same reason.

Here is the punchline. The \$70 million loss came from a 130bp move. But the fund's *forced selling* — and that of every peer — pushes yields up *another* 40 basis points, say to +170bp total. Now the loss is 18 × 0.0170 × \$300m ≈ **\$92 million**, a *fresh* \$22 million call lands, and the fund must sell *more* gilts, which pushes yields up again. **The selling caused by the call generates the loss that triggers the next call. That is the doom loop in one calculation: leverage turned a one-time 130bp repricing into a self-feeding spiral, and no individual fund could stop it by acting rationally — selling to meet its own call only deepened everyone else's.**

## The fire brigade: the Bank of England's bind

A doom loop with no natural floor can only be stopped by a buyer big enough that the loop cannot exhaust it — a buyer who is not subject to collateral calls and cannot be forced to sell. There is exactly one such actor in any economy: the central bank, which can create the currency without limit. On Wednesday 28 September 2022, the Bank of England became that buyer.

The Bank announced it would carry out *temporary*, *targeted* purchases of long-dated gilts "on whatever scale is necessary" — with a stated envelope of up to £65 billion (£5 billion per day for the planned 13-working-day operation) — explicitly "to restore orderly market conditions" and head off a "material risk to UK financial stability." The effect was immediate: with an unlimited buyer standing in the market, the forced sellers had somewhere to sell *without* crashing the price further; yields fell back sharply on the day; collateral calls eased; and the LDI funds were given a window — those 13 days — to rebuild their cash buffers and reduce their leverage in an orderly way. The doom loop was broken not by fixing the budget but by inserting a buyer the loop could not overwhelm.

### Why this was so awkward

But step back and look at what the Bank had just done, because the contradiction is the most instructive thing in the whole episode. In September 2022 the Bank of England was in the middle of the most aggressive tightening campaign in its modern history: it was *raising* its policy rate to fight inflation, and it had just announced **quantitative tightening** — a plan to actively *sell* gilts from its balance sheet (the £838 billion stock accumulated during years of quantitative easing) to drain liquidity from the system. Tightening means *removing* monetary stimulus: higher rates, fewer gilts held by the central bank, less money sloshing around.

And now, in the same week, the Bank was *buying* gilts on an emergency basis — which is the textbook *easing* move, the core action of quantitative easing. It had to delay the start of its QT gilt sales. To an outside observer it looked as if the Bank had reversed course and gone back to money-printing. The Bank insisted, correctly, that the two operations had different *purposes*: the rate hikes and QT were *monetary policy*, aimed at inflation over the medium term; the emergency gilt-buying was *financial-stability policy*, aimed at a specific market dysfunction, time-limited, and to be unwound. But the *instrument* — buying gilts — was the same in both cases, and the optics were genuinely uncomfortable: a central bank easing and tightening at once. This is the deepest tension in the case, and it is worth a worked example. (We cover the general distinction between the two uses of the balance sheet in [the liquidity channel: QE, QT, and the everything bid](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-liquidity-channel-qe-qt-and-the-everything-bid).)

#### Worked example: the Bank's bind — easing and tightening in the same week

Let me make the contradiction concrete with the actual policy moves stacked side by side, in the same seven-day window.

In the week of 22–28 September 2022, the Bank of England was simultaneously committed to:

- **Tightening, for inflation.** Its policy rate had been hiked to 2.25% on 22 September (a 0.5 percentage-point rise), with the market pricing a path toward 5–6%. And it had confirmed plans to begin **selling £80 billion of gilts** over the coming year via QT — actively shrinking its \$895-billion-equivalent balance sheet to drain liquidity. Direction: *remove* money from the system.
- **Easing, for stability.** On 28 September it announced **up to £65 billion of emergency gilt purchases** — *adding* money to the system by buying the very bonds it had just said it would sell. Direction: *add* money to the system.

Net the two against each other and the absurdity is plain: the Bank planned to *sell* £80 billion of gilts to fight inflation while *buying* up to £65 billion of gilts to prevent a crash — nearly offsetting flows, in opposite directions, justified by two different mandates, in the same fortnight. The reconciliation is that **the two operations targeted different problems at different horizons**: the QT/rate-hike track was about the *price level* over the next two years, while the emergency buying was about *market functioning* over the next two weeks. A central bank can, in principle, separate "the price of money should be higher" from "this specific market must not seize up." But the episode exposed how fragile that separation is in practice. **The deepest lesson is that financial-stability emergencies can force a central bank to act *against* its own monetary stance — and a government that creates such an emergency hands the central bank an impossible choice between fighting inflation and preventing a crash.** The Bank chose, correctly, to stop the crash first; you cannot run monetary policy through a broken bond market.

### Credibility spent in advance vs credibility rebuilt after it is lost

The contrast with Draghi is the heart of the matter, and it is exact. In 2012, Mario Draghi *announced* an unlimited backstop (Outright Monetary Transactions) from a position of credibility — and never had to spend a single euro, because the mere existence of a credible unlimited buyer deleted the panic that justified the high yields. The promise did the work; the money was never needed.

In 2022, the Bank of England also became an unlimited buyer — but it had to *actually spend* (it ultimately bought about £19 billion of gilts during the operation), because it was acting *after* credibility had already snapped. By then, words alone could not hold the market; only real purchases could. The reason is the sequencing. Draghi acted *pre-emptively*, converting trust he already had into a free backstop. The BOE acted *reactively*, into a market that had already lost trust in the government's fiscal path, so it had to put its balance sheet where its mouth was. **Credibility spent in advance is free; credibility rebuilt after it is lost is ruinously expensive.** That single comparison — the same instrument, opposite cost, because of *when* it was deployed relative to the credibility break — is the most transferable lesson the two cases teach together. The full anatomy of the triumphant version is in [Draghi 2012: whatever it takes and the spread machine](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/draghi-2012-whatever-it-takes-and-the-spread-machine).

### The gilt yield jump, dated

It is worth pinning the market move to the calendar, because the *speed* is the whole point. The 30-year gilt yield went from roughly 3.7% before the mini-budget to about 5.0% at the intraday peak on 27–28 September — a move of about 130 basis points in three trading days, and an intraday surge on the 28th that was halted only by the Bank's announcement. For context, a 130bp move in a long sovereign yield would ordinarily take *months* to play out in a developed market; it happened here in *days*, and the final, vertical leg on the 28th was the doom loop, not the budget — forced selling, not fresh fundamental news. The Bank's intervention is visible as the sharp reversal: the moment an unlimited buyer appeared, the yield fell back, because the *forced* component of the selling had a place to go.

## Common misconceptions

**"The Bank of England's bailout caused the crisis / proved QE never stopped."** No. The Bank's purchases were the *cure*, not the disease, and they were carefully designed to be temporary and self-reversing — the Bank sold the gilts back into the market over the following months and the operation ultimately turned a small *profit* for the taxpayer. The cause was the unfunded mini-budget colliding with hidden LDI leverage. Conflating the emergency stabilisation with the monetary-policy QE of the 2010s misreads both: one was open-ended stimulus, the other was a 13-day financial-stability backstop with an exit built in from day one.

**"It was all about the £45 billion of tax cuts."** The number mattered, but the *credibility breach* mattered more. Plenty of governments run large deficits without a gilt crisis — the US ran a deficit of about 6% of GDP in 2022 with no such event. What made the UK different was *uncosted* cuts, the *bypassed* independent watchdog, the *contradiction* with monetary policy, and the *hidden leverage* waiting to amplify the shock. Identical-sized cuts, properly costed and phased, would not have produced a doom loop. It was the *manner*, not just the magnitude.

**"LDI was a reckless bet that blew up."** LDI was, in its purpose, the *opposite* of a bet — it was a hedge, designed to *reduce* a pension fund's interest-rate risk. The failure was not the strategy's intent but its *liquidity design*: collateral buffers sized for normal volatility, leverage that turned forced collateral calls into forced selling, and a systemic blind spot in which every fund would be selling the same asset at the same moment. The lesson is about leverage and liquidity buffers, not about hedging being foolish.

**"Markets overreacted; the budget would have been fine."** The market's verdict was *vindicated* with unusual speed. Within weeks the government reversed almost the entire package, the chancellor was sacked, and the prime minister resigned — the policy was abandoned by its own authors. When a government undoes its plan in three weeks, the market that rejected it in three days was not overreacting; it was *forecasting*. (This is the disciplining role of markets we explore in [the fiscal toolkit: spending, taxes, and deficits](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-fiscal-toolkit-spending-taxes-and-deficits).)

**"This can't happen in a country with its own central bank — only in the euro area."** A common misreading of the Draghi case is that monetary sovereigns (a country that issues its own currency, like the UK) are immune to bond-market discipline. The UK *is* a monetary sovereign — the Bank can always create the pounds to pay — yet it still suffered a violent gilt crisis. The reason is that monetary sovereignty protects you from *forced default*, not from *inflation risk* or *credibility loss*. The market wasn't pricing a UK default; it was pricing higher inflation and a loss of fiscal discipline, and it expressed that by demanding higher yields and a weaker currency. Sovereignty is not a shield against being repriced — only against being *forced to miss a payment*.

## Case studies: the 49 days, and its echoes

The mini-budget crisis is itself the central case study; let me walk its timeline, then place it beside two episodes that illuminate it by contrast.

### The deep case: 23 September to 25 October 2022

**Friday 23 September.** Chancellor Kwarteng delivers the mini-budget: ~£45 billion of unfunded tax cuts, no OBR costing. Gilt yields rise sharply and sterling falls on the day — the market's first verdict.

**Monday 26 September.** With no reassurance over the weekend, the rout accelerates. Sterling crashes to a record \$1.035 in thin Asian trading. 30-year gilt yields climb toward 4.5–5.0%. The move is now self-evidently disorderly.

**Wednesday 28 September.** As LDI-driven forced selling threatens to seize the long-gilt market entirely, the Bank of England announces emergency purchases of up to £65 billion of long-dated gilts and delays the start of its QT sales. Yields reverse sharply on the day. The doom loop is capped.

**Monday 3 October.** The government U-turns on its most totemic measure, the abolition of the 45% top tax rate — the first crack in the plan.

**Friday 14 October.** The emergency gilt-buying operation ends as scheduled (13 working days). The same day, Kwarteng is sacked as chancellor after just 38 days in office; Jeremy Hunt replaces him.

**Monday 17 October.** Hunt reverses *almost the entire* mini-budget, restoring fiscal credibility by abandoning the policy that broke it.

**Thursday 20 / Tuesday 25 October.** Liz Truss resigns as prime minister on 20 October and formally leaves office on 25 October, **49 days** after taking the job — the shortest premiership in British history. Rishi Sunak, who had warned during the leadership campaign that the plan would spook markets, becomes prime minister, and gilt yields and sterling stabilise.

The whole arc — spark, doom loop, backstop, policy reversal, leadership change — played out in under five weeks. The numbers to keep: **£45 billion unfunded, +130bp on the 30-year, \$1.035 record low, £65 billion BOE envelope, 49 days.** It is the fastest, cleanest example in the modern record of a bond market disciplining a G7 government — and of hidden leverage turning a policy error into a stability emergency.

It is worth naming the real-economy damage, because the gilt market is not a distant ledger — it sets the price of everything long-dated. The most direct casualty was the mortgage market. UK fixed-rate mortgages are priced off gilt yields (specifically off the *swap* rates that track them), so when 30-year gilt yields jumped 130 basis points, mortgage rates followed almost immediately. Lenders, unable to price risk in the chaos, *pulled* roughly 40% of available mortgage products off the shelf within a week, and when deals returned they were sharply more expensive: the average two-year fixed rate climbed past 6% in the weeks after the mini-budget, up from around 4.7% just before it. A household remortgaging a £250,000 loan saw its annual interest cost rise by thousands of pounds — a direct, personal transmission of a chancellor's speech, through the gilt market, into a family's monthly budget. This is the point of the whole series made painfully concrete: a policy lever (the mini-budget) reached an asset (gilts) through a channel (lost credibility), and the repriced asset reset the cost of money for millions of people who had never heard the word "LDI."

### Contrast 1: the United States, same era, no crisis

Place the UK beside the US in the same period and the *credibility* point sharpens. In 2022 the US ran a federal deficit of about 5.3% of GDP and was itself in the fastest Fed hiking cycle since Volcker (525 basis points). US Treasury yields rose substantially that year — the 10-year reached about 4.3% — but there was *no* gilt-style crisis: no record-low currency on a confidence break (the dollar *rose*), no forced-selling doom loop, no emergency central-bank reversal. Why the difference? The US fiscal path, whatever its long-run problems, arrived through the normal, costed, congressional process; the dollar is the world's reserve currency and a *destination* for safe-haven flows in a panic (so it strengthens in stress rather than weakening); and US pension and insurance leverage was not concentrated in the same fragile LDI structure. Same global tightening shock, opposite outcome — because the credibility and the plumbing were different. (The US tightening case is in [Volcker 1979–82: how 20-percent rates repriced everything](/blog/trading/finance/paul-volcker-1980-rate-shock-killing-inflation), and the broader hiking cycle in [how policy moves credit spreads and the Fed put](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/how-policy-moves-credit-spreads-and-the-fed-put).)

### Contrast 2: Draghi 2012 — the same instrument, free instead of ruinous

We have drawn this contrast already, but it belongs in the case file because it is the cleanest natural experiment in the credibility literature. Both the ECB in 2012 and the BOE in 2022 deployed the *same* instrument — an unlimited central-bank backstop for the government bond market. The ECB deployed it *before* losing credibility, converting trust into a free, never-used promise that crushed Spanish yields from 7.6% to 1.6% for €0. The BOE deployed it *after* the government had lost credibility, so it had to spend real money (about £19 billion bought) just to stop a fire sale, and even then the underlying problem was only fixed when the *fiscal policy itself* was reversed. The instrument was identical; the cost and the outcome were opposite; the only difference was the *sequencing relative to the credibility break*. That is the single most important thing a policymaker — or a market analyst — can take from putting the two cases side by side.

## What it means for asset values: the playbook

Strip the episode to the transferable lessons — the things that generalise to the next country, the next budget, the next hidden-leverage blow-up.

**A government's credibility is a balance-sheet asset, and the bond market is its auditor.** A fiscal plan that is large but *credible* (costed, phased, coordinated with the central bank) can be absorbed; an identical plan that is *not* credible can break the market. The single most important variable is not the size of the deficit but whether the market believes the government has a coherent, honest plan to fund it. When you assess any fiscal announcement, the first question is not "how big?" but "is it costed, is it coordinated with monetary policy, and does it have an independent stamp of credibility?" The mini-budget failed all three tests, and the market graded it in days.

**Hidden leverage is the difference between a repricing and a crisis.** The same 130bp yield move, in a system *without* the LDI doom loop, would have been a painful but orderly repricing of long bonds. *With* the leverage, it became a near-systemic fire sale. The lesson for anyone watching markets: the dangerous shocks are not the ones that move prices, but the ones that trigger *forced* selling — margin calls, collateral calls, redemptions — because forced sellers do not sell at fair value, they sell at *any* value, and that is what turns a move into a spiral. After a shock, always ask: *who is forced to sell here, and at what price does their forced selling stop?* If the answer is "no one, until a central bank shows up," you are looking at a doom loop.

**The signal to watch is the *pace and breadth* of the move, not just its direction.** A gilt yield drifting up over months on rising inflation is ordinary monetary transmission. A gilt yield jumping 130bp in three days, *with the currency falling at the same time*, is a credibility break — a different animal, requiring a different response. The twin signature — yields up *and* currency down together — is the tell that the higher yield is fear compensation, not carry attraction, and that capital is fleeing rather than arriving. When you see that combination in any sovereign, you are watching a confidence crisis, and the relevant question becomes whether the central bank will be forced to choose between its inflation mandate and financial stability.

**What would invalidate the read:** a large, unfunded fiscal package that the market *absorbs* calmly — which happens when the country has reserve-currency status, deep and un-levered demand for its bonds, and a track record of credibility deep enough to extend the benefit of the doubt (the US in 2022 is the example). Credibility is a buffer: a country with a lot of it can do things that would sink a country with little. The UK had spent decades building fiscal credibility and discovered, in 2022, that it could be lost in an afternoon and was not nearly as deep as assumed. The lever was never the £45 billion. The lever was *belief* — and the asset that repriced, in the end, was the market's faith that the UK government knew what it was doing.

**The aftermath rewired regulation.** The deepest evidence that the episode was understood as a *systemic* failure, not a one-off, is what came after. UK regulators (the Bank's Financial Policy Committee and The Pensions Regulator) forced LDI funds to hold permanently larger collateral buffers — sized to survive a yield move of around 250 basis points, not 100 — precisely so that a future shock could no longer outrun the cushion and trigger forced selling. In other words, the system was re-engineered so that the *next* gilt shock cannot become a doom loop, by making sure the funds can meet a much larger collateral call without selling gilts into a falling market. That is the macroprudential lesson made concrete: the way you prevent a hidden-leverage spiral is not to ban the hedge but to make the buffers deep enough that no one is ever *forced* to sell. The general framework for this kind of system-level regulation is in [macroprudential and regulatory policy](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/macroprudential-and-regulatory-policy).

The broadest policy-machine lesson — the one that ties this case to the rest of the series — is that the most dangerous risks are the ones that live *off* the official series. A rate hike shows up in the policy-rate data; QE shows up on the balance sheet; a deficit shows up in the fiscal accounts. But LDI leverage showed up *nowhere* an outside analyst would routinely look — not in the government's debt statistics, not in the central bank's balance sheet, not in any published market metric. It was embedded in the collateral terms of private swaps and repo, invisible until the moment it forced selling and became, suddenly, the most important fact in the financial system. The thing that turned a bad budget into a crisis was a piece of leverage that no official series tracked — which is the most unsettling lesson of all, because it means the next one is probably hiding in exactly the same kind of place.
