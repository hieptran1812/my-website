> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Gold is the one asset with no earnings, no coupon, and no cash flow, so there is no business to value. Its price is set almost entirely by **policy**, through three doors: the **real interest rate** (gold's opportunity cost), the **dollar** (gold is priced in USD), and the **policy-credibility / debasement** story (deficits, de-dollarization, central-bank buying). Read any gold move by asking which door policy just opened.
>
> - The cleanest macro relationship in markets is gold against the **10-year real yield** — roughly a **−0.8 correlation**. Gold pays you nothing, so the real yield on a bond is the rent you give up to hold it. High real rates (Volcker's 16% in 1980, the 2022-23 hikes) crush gold; negative real rates (2011, 2020-21) set it on fire.
> - Gold is quoted in dollars, so a weaker dollar mechanically lifts the price even when nothing about gold changes. The dollar index fell more than 10% in the first half of 2025 — its worst H1 since 1973 — and that alone was a tailwind.
> - In 2025 gold ran **+64%**, breached \$3,000 and \$4,000 for the first time, and hit an all-time high of **\$5,589/oz** in January 2026 — and it did so *despite positive real yields*, because the driver had shifted to **record central-bank buying and de-dollarization**. That is a policy-regime change you can read in one price.
> - **The one number to remember:** central banks have bought roughly **1,000 tonnes of gold a year since 2022** — about a third of all mine supply — a structural, policy-driven bid that broke the old real-rate rule.

For most of modern financial history there was one relationship in markets you could set your watch by. Plot the price of gold against the inflation-adjusted yield on a 10-year US Treasury bond, and the two lines move like a seesaw: when the real yield falls, gold rises; when the real yield climbs, gold falls. No earnings season, no management call, no product cycle — just a clean, mechanical inverse that held for decades. Traders called it the cleanest macro relationship in the world, and it ran at roughly a −0.8 correlation, tight enough that you could trade gold as a bet on real rates and not be far wrong.

Then 2025 happened. The Federal Reserve held rates high through the first half of the year, real yields stayed firmly *positive* — around +2% — and by the old rule gold should have been dead money or worse. Instead gold went vertical. It rose 64% on the year. It punched through \$3,000 an ounce for the first time in March, through \$4,000 in October, and by the end of January 2026 it printed an all-time high of \$5,589 an ounce. The seesaw had broken. Real yields and gold rose *together*, which is not supposed to happen.

![Diagram showing four policy-driven levers — the real interest rate, the dollar, central-bank buying, and the debasement hedge — all feeding into the gold price.](/imgs/blogs/how-policy-prices-gold-and-commodities-real-rates-and-the-dollar-1.png)

This post is about why both things are true: why gold obeyed the real-rate rule for forty years, and why a *policy* change — central banks buying gold by the thousand tonnes and the world quietly diversifying away from the dollar — overrode it. Gold is the purest policy asset there is, because it is the one thing on the board with no cash flow to anchor it. A stock has earnings; a bond has a coupon; a building has rent. Gold has none of those. So when you ask "what is gold worth?", the honest answer is: whatever the real interest rate, the dollar, and the world's trust in paper money say it is worth. That makes gold a near-perfect lens for this whole series — a price set entirely by the policy machine, with no business getting in the way.

## Foundations: why a thing with no cash flow still has a price

Start with the question that trips up every beginner: *if gold pays no interest, no dividend, and produces nothing, why is it worth anything at all — and why does the price move?*

Every other asset you can value with the same idea. A share of stock is a claim on a stream of future profits; a bond is a claim on a stream of future coupons and a final repayment; an apartment is a claim on a stream of future rents. To value any of them you do the same thing: estimate the future cash, then **discount** it — shrink it to today's value because a dollar arriving in ten years is worth less than a dollar in your hand now. The discount rate is the engine of the whole series, and we built it from zero in [the discount-rate channel post](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-discount-rate-channel-how-rates-reprice-cash-flows). Higher rates mean a steeper discount, so the same future cash is worth less today; lower rates mean a gentler discount, so it is worth more.

Gold breaks this machine in one specific way: **there is no future cash to discount.** An ounce of gold today is an ounce of gold in ten years. It throws off no coupon, no rent, no dividend. So you cannot run a discounted-cash-flow valuation on it, because the cash flow is zero. This is not a bug in gold; it is the entire point of gold. Precisely because it promises nothing and depends on no one's solvency, it cannot default, cannot be diluted, and cannot be inflated away by a central bank printing more of it. There is a roughly fixed above-ground stock of about 215,000 tonnes, and mine supply adds only ~1.5% a year. Gold is, in a phrase, **money that no government can print.**

That last sentence hides a number worth dwelling on, because it is what makes gold different from every ordinary commodity, and it is the key to understanding the 2025 run. Most commodities are **flow** markets: the world produces and consumes a barrel of oil or a tonne of wheat in a continuous stream, very little is stored, and the price clears the *flow* — this year's production against this year's demand. Gold is the opposite. Almost all the gold ever mined still exists, sitting in vaults, central-bank reserves, jewelry, and coins — that is the ~215,000-tonne *above-ground stock*. New mine production adds only about 3,600 tonnes a year, or roughly 1.5% on top of that stock. So gold is overwhelmingly a **stock** market, not a flow market: the price is set not by this year's mining against this year's consumption, but by whether the holders of that enormous existing pile want to hold *more* or *less* of it relative to paper money. This is why a stat like "central banks bought 1,000 tonnes" matters so much: against a flow of 3,600 tonnes a year it is a third of new supply, and against the desire of stock-holders to add or shed bullion it is a signal that the world's official sector wants *more* gold and *less* dollars. The stock-to-flow nature of gold is exactly why it behaves like money rather than like copper.

#### Worked example: why the above-ground stock makes gold a monetary asset

> The world holds roughly 215,000 tonnes of above-ground gold and mines about 3,600 tonnes a year — a stock-to-flow ratio of about 60. Compare that to oil: the world consumes about 35 billion barrels a year and stores maybe a few weeks of it, a stock-to-flow ratio near zero.
>
> Now ask what happens if demand jumps 10%. For oil, a 10% demand surge with fixed near-term supply sends the price soaring, because there is no buffer — you are bidding for the *flow*. For gold, a 10% rise in *new investment demand* (say, an extra 360 tonnes) is trivial against the 215,000-tonne stock — about 0.17% of it. The price doesn't move because new buyers cleared the flow; it moves because *existing holders* of the 215,000-tonne pile decided gold was worth more relative to paper, and re-rated the whole stock at once.
>
> That re-rating is what a falling real yield, a weaker dollar, or a central-bank buying program *does* — it changes how the holders of the entire existing stock value bullion against money. A move from \$3,500 to \$5,589 isn't 2,000 tonnes of new gold being scarce; it is 215,000 tonnes being repriced because the *monetary* case for holding it strengthened.
>
> **The intuition:** gold's price is a referendum on paper money taken by the holders of a giant existing stock — which is exactly why it answers to monetary *policy* and not to mine output or industrial demand.

So if you cannot discount a cash flow, what *does* set the price? Three things, and all three are policy variables.

**1. The real interest rate — gold's opportunity cost.** This is the deepest one, so we will spend the most time on it. The "opportunity cost" of holding any asset is what you gave up to hold it. If you hold gold, you are choosing not to hold a safe interest-bearing asset like a Treasury bond. The bond pays you a yield; gold pays you nothing. So the cost of holding gold is *the yield you forfeited.* But not the nominal yield — the **real** yield, the yield after subtracting expected inflation, because gold itself roughly holds its purchasing power against inflation. The real yield is what a bond pays you *in real, after-inflation terms* for sitting still. When that real yield is high, holding gold is expensive — you are giving up a lot of real income for the privilege. When the real yield is zero or negative, holding gold costs nothing, or actually beats the alternative, because the "safe" bond is quietly losing purchasing power. Gold has no yield, so the real yield *is* its hurdle rate.

**2. The dollar.** Gold has one global value, but it is quoted in US dollars — the screen says "USD per ounce." So the dollar's own strength feeds straight into the printed price. When the dollar weakens against other currencies, the same ounce of gold simply costs more dollars, even if nothing about gold changed. This is mechanical, not psychological, and we will work through the arithmetic below. The dollar's strength is itself a policy outcome — set by the Fed relative to other central banks and by the world's confidence in US policy — which we cover in [the currency channel post](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-currency-channel-rate-differentials-and-carry).

**3. Trust in paper money — the debasement and de-dollarization story.** This is the slow, structural one. Because gold is no one's liability, it is the asset you hold when you stop trusting the issuers of paper money — when deficits look unsustainable, when inflation runs hot for years, or when you are a foreign central bank worried that your dollar reserves could be frozen or inflated away. This is the door that swung wide open in 2025.

Hold those three in your head, because the entire post is just walking through each door and watching gold reprice. And notice what they have in common: not one of them is about gold *the metal.* They are about **rates, the dollar, and trust** — three things set in Washington, in Beijing, in the world's central banks. Gold is a mirror held up to monetary policy.

## The real-rate channel: gold's opportunity cost

Let's make the opportunity-cost idea concrete, because it is the spine of gold valuation and the place beginners get the most confused.

Imagine you have a pile of savings you want to keep safe. You have two safe-ish options. Option A: buy a 10-year Treasury bond. Option B: buy gold and lock it in a vault. The bond pays you a yield every year; gold pays you nothing and even costs a little to store and insure. So on the face of it, the bond wins every time — why would anyone hold gold?

The answer is **inflation.** The bond's yield is *nominal* — a headline number. If the bond pays 4% but inflation is running at 4%, your real return is zero: your money grows 4% but everything you want to buy also got 4% more expensive, so you are standing still. If the bond pays 4% but inflation is 6%, your real return is *negative two percent* — the "safe" bond is quietly making you poorer in purchasing-power terms every year. Gold, by contrast, has historically held its purchasing power against inflation over long stretches. So the right comparison is not "bond yield vs zero," it is **"bond's real yield vs gold's zero real yield."**

That single comparison is the whole channel. Subtract expected inflation from the nominal bond yield and you get the **real yield**. When the real yield is clearly positive, the bond beats gold on the one thing that matters — real income — and money rotates out of gold into bonds, pushing gold down. When the real yield is zero or negative, the bond's advantage vanishes or reverses, and money rotates into gold, pushing it up. Gold's price is, to a first approximation, an inverse bet on the real yield.

#### Worked example: the opportunity cost of holding gold

> Suppose you are deciding what to do with \$3,500 of savings, and you can buy exactly one ounce of gold at \$3,500 or put the \$3,500 in a 10-year Treasury.
>
> **Scenario A — real yields high (a 5% real yield).** The bond pays you \$175 a year in *real* terms (5% of \$3,500). If you hold gold instead, you earn \$0. So holding the gold *costs you \$175 a year* in forgone real income — and that is before storage and insurance. Over ten years that is roughly \$1,750 of real income you walked away from, more than a third of your principal. At a 5% real yield, gold has to *appreciate* meaningfully just to break even against the bond. No surprise that gold struggles when real rates are this high.
>
> **Scenario B — real yields at zero.** Now the bond's real yield is 0% — it pays 4% but inflation is also 4%, so in purchasing-power terms it pays you nothing, exactly like gold. Holding gold now costs you *nothing* in forgone real income. The bond's one advantage has evaporated. Gold and the bond are even on income, and gold wins on the things the bond can't offer — no default risk, no issuer, a hedge if inflation surprises higher.
>
> **Scenario C — real yields negative (a −1% real yield, like 2021).** The bond now pays 3% while inflation runs 4%, so its real yield is −1%: the "safe" asset *loses* \$35 a year of purchasing power on your \$3,500. Gold, at a flat 0% real, now *beats* the bond by a full percentage point a year just by standing still. Money floods toward the asset that at least doesn't go backward.
>
> **The intuition:** gold's opportunity cost *is* the real yield, so gold loves low and negative real rates and hates high ones — at a 5% real yield holding an ounce costs you \$175 a year, and at 0% it costs you nothing.

This is why the gold–real-yield correlation is so tight and so reliable: it is not a statistical accident, it is an accounting identity about opportunity cost. Plot the two and you can see the seesaw with your own eyes — and you can see the exact moment in 2025 when it broke.

![Dual-axis chart of gold price against the 10-year real yield inverted, showing a tight inverse relationship that breaks down in 2025 as both rise together.](/imgs/blogs/how-policy-prices-gold-and-commodities-real-rates-and-the-dollar-3.png)

In the chart the real yield is drawn on an *inverted* axis, so the inverse relationship reads as the two lines moving together. From 2020 through 2024 they track beautifully: real yields collapsed to −1.1% in 2021 and gold ran; real yields spiked toward +2% in 2022-23 and gold stalled around \$1,800. Then the green band: from late 2024 into 2026, real yields stayed *high and positive* — and gold, instead of falling, doubled. The seesaw didn't just slow down; it reversed. Something other than the real yield had grabbed the wheel. We come back to what, in the case studies. The statistical version of this relationship — the betas, the rolling correlation, and how stable it has been across regimes — lives in the macro-correlations series, in [the real-yield story of gold](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/inflation-and-gold-the-real-yield-story); here we own the *mechanism* and the policy story behind it.

The cleanest way to hold the rule in mind is as a seesaw between two regimes.

![Before and after comparison of gold's behavior under high real rates versus low or negative real rates.](/imgs/blogs/how-policy-prices-gold-and-commodities-real-rates-and-the-dollar-4.png)

On the left, the high-real-rate regime: bonds pay real money to wait, gold's opportunity cost is high, money leaves gold. This is Volcker in 1980-82 and the Fed in 2022-23. On the right, the low-or-negative-real-rate regime: cash and bonds lose to inflation, gold's opportunity cost is zero, money floods in. This is 2011 and 2020-21. For forty years, knowing which side of the seesaw you were on was most of what you needed to know about gold.

## The dollar channel: priced in greenbacks

The second door is more mechanical and easier to see once it clicks. Gold has *one* value in the world — an ounce in London is the same ounce in Shanghai or Mumbai, fungible and arbitraged to a single global price within a tiny spread. But that one value is *quoted in US dollars*. The number on the screen is dollars per ounce. So the dollar's own strength is baked into the price.

![Pipeline showing how a weaker dollar mechanically raises the dollar price of gold through five steps.](/imgs/blogs/how-policy-prices-gold-and-commodities-real-rates-and-the-dollar-5.png)

Walk the chain. Gold has one global value. It is quoted in dollars. Now suppose the dollar weakens — the Fed eases relative to other central banks, or the world loses some confidence in US policy, and the dollar index (DXY, the dollar's value against a basket of major currencies) falls. A foreign buyer — say, an importer in Europe paying in euros — now needs *fewer* euros to buy the same ounce, because their currency goes further against the dollar. To keep one global value consistent across currencies, the *dollar* price has to rise to compensate. More dollars are now required per ounce. The screen price goes up — and notice that nothing about gold the metal changed. The shift happened entirely in the currency it is measured in.

#### Worked example: a 10% dollar decline lifts the gold price mechanically

> Start with gold at \$3,500 an ounce and the euro at \$1.10 (so one ounce costs a European buyer €3,182). Now the dollar weakens 10% against the euro, so the euro rises to roughly \$1.22.
>
> Gold's value *in euros* hasn't changed — it is still worth €3,182 to that European buyer, because nothing about gold or about Europe shifted; only the dollar moved. But €3,182 now converts to a different number of dollars: €3,182 × \$1.22 = **\$3,882** an ounce. The dollar price rose from \$3,500 to about \$3,882 — roughly **+11%** — purely because the dollar fell 10%.
>
> The arithmetic is just the reciprocal: if the dollar buys 10% less, it takes about 1 ÷ 0.90 = 1.11, or 11% more dollars, to buy the same thing. A 10% dollar decline is a roughly +11% mechanical tailwind to the USD gold price.
>
> **The intuition:** a chunk of any big gold move is not gold doing anything — it is the dollar moving, because gold is just the dollar's reflection in a mirror that has one fixed global value.

This is why the dollar index matters so much for gold, and why 2025 was such a strong year on this channel alone. The dollar index started 2025 near 110, fell below 98 by April, and finished the first half of the year down more than 10% — its worst first half since 1973. By the mechanics above, a 10%+ dollar decline is, on its own, a 10-11% lift to the dollar gold price before any other driver does anything. And the dollar's slide was itself a policy story: markets read the 2025 tariff chaos, the swelling deficit, and the pressure on the Fed's independence as a credibility problem, and a falling currency is the market's verdict on credibility. The currency channel — how rate differentials and confidence set the dollar — is its own deep topic, built out in [the currency-channel post](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-currency-channel-rate-differentials-and-carry) and in [currency policy and FX intervention](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/currency-policy-and-fx-intervention).

Two important refinements. First, the real-rate channel and the dollar channel are not independent — they are usually two faces of the same policy. When the Fed cuts aggressively, real yields fall *and* the dollar tends to weaken, so both doors swing open for gold at once. That correlation is exactly why gold can move so violently around a dovish Fed pivot: the levers reinforce. Second, the dollar channel is symmetric. A *strengthening* dollar is a headwind: in 2022, the dollar index hit a two-decade high near 115, and that strength capped gold even as inflation fear ran hot. Gold spent 2022 going almost nowhere — pinned between an inflation hedge bid pulling up and a strong-dollar, rising-real-rate vice pushing down.

## The credibility channel: debasement and de-dollarization

The third door is the one that explains 2025, and it is the most important shift in the gold story in two generations. To understand it, go back to what gold *is*: an asset that is no one's liability. A dollar is a liability of the Federal Reserve. A Treasury bond is a liability of the US government. A bank deposit is a liability of a bank. Every form of paper money is a promise by some institution — and a promise is only as good as your trust in the institution and your ability to actually access it. Gold is the asset you hold when that trust frays.

There are two distinct flavors of fraying, and both lift gold.

**Debasement — the slow inflation of paper claims.** When a government runs very large deficits for a long time, and the central bank keeps money cheap to help finance them, the implicit message to savers is: the value of paper money will be allowed to erode. The classic policy template for this is *financial repression* — pinning interest rates below inflation so that inflation slowly erodes the real value of the debt, with bondholders footing the bill. The US did exactly this after World War II, pegging long Treasury yields at 2.5% while inflation ran higher, until the 1951 Treasury-Fed Accord freed monetary policy. In any debasement regime, gold is the escape hatch: it cannot be printed, so its supply does not inflate alongside the paper. The US fiscal picture in the mid-2020s — deficits near 6-7% of GDP in a non-recession, debt held by the public crossing 100% of GDP, and the 2025 tax law (OBBBA) adding trillions more — is precisely the kind of backdrop that makes savers want an asset no one can dilute. We cover the fiscal machine itself in [the fiscal toolkit post](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-fiscal-toolkit-spending-taxes-and-deficits); here the point is its *effect on gold*: big, persistent deficits are a structural bid.

**De-dollarization — the loss of trust in the dollar system itself.** This is the sharper, faster flavor, and it is what changed gold's behavior in 2025. For decades, foreign central banks parked their reserves overwhelmingly in US dollars and Treasuries — it was the safest, most liquid asset in the world. But a Treasury bond held by a foreign central bank is still a *claim on the US government*, accessible only through the dollar plumbing the US controls. In 2022, after Russia invaded Ukraine, the US and its allies *froze roughly \$300 billion of the Russian central bank's dollar reserves* — immobilized them at the click of a switch. That was a watershed. Every central bank in the world that was not a close US ally suddenly understood that dollar reserves are safe only as long as you stay on the right side of US policy. Gold sitting in your own vault has no such risk: no one can freeze it, sanction it, or inflate it. So central banks — led by China, Russia, India, Turkey, Poland and others — began buying gold at a pace not seen in modern history, deliberately shifting reserves out of dollars and into the one reserve asset that is nobody's liability.

This is why the real-rate rule broke. The old rule assumed the marginal buyer of gold was a Western investor weighing gold against a Treasury bond — for whom the real yield is the whole calculation. But the new marginal buyer is a *foreign central bank pursuing a policy of de-dollarization*, and that buyer does not care what the 10-year real yield is. It is not trading; it is reallocating reserves for geopolitical reasons. When your biggest buyer is price-insensitive and policy-driven, the price can rise even as real yields rise. The seesaw didn't break because the physics changed; it broke because a new, larger force showed up on one side.

![Stacked bar chart showing central-bank net gold buying against annual mine supply, with buying crossing 1,000 tonnes from 2022 onward.](/imgs/blogs/how-policy-prices-gold-and-commodities-real-rates-and-the-dollar-7.png)

Look at the scale of it. From 2018 to 2021, central banks bought 250-650 tonnes a year — meaningful but not dominant, around 7-18% of mine supply. Then in 2022, the year of the reserve freeze, official-sector buying jumped above 1,000 tonnes and *stayed there* — roughly 1,080 tonnes in 2022, 1,040 in 2023, 1,045 in 2024. That is close to a third of all the gold pulled out of the ground each year, bought by buyers who don't sell and don't care about the real yield. New mine supply only grows the above-ground stock about 1.5% a year, so when a price-insensitive buyer soaks up a third of the *annual flow*, the price has to clear higher to ration the rest. This is the single most important fact in the modern gold market.

#### Worked example: central-bank buying versus mine supply

> Annual gold mine production runs about 3,600 tonnes. Suppose central banks buy 1,000 tonnes of that — about 28% of the year's new supply — and, crucially, they buy it to *hold*, not to trade. They are taking it off the market permanently.
>
> That leaves only ~2,600 tonnes a year of new supply for everyone else: jewelry demand, industrial and electronics use, retail bars and coins, and investment funds. But those other buyers haven't shrunk — jewelry and investment demand have if anything grown. So you have *roughly the same private demand chasing ~28% less available new supply.* Basic scarcity: when a third of the new flow is bought by someone who never sells, the price the remaining buyers must pay to clear the market rises.
>
> To put a number on the stock effect: 1,000 tonnes is about 32 million ounces. At \$3,500 an ounce, central banks are spending roughly **\$112 billion a year** buying gold — a deliberate, budgeted reserve-policy decision, repeated year after year, by buyers who are explicitly trying to *reduce* their dollar holdings. That is a structural bid, not a trade.
>
> **The intuition:** when a price-insensitive, policy-driven buyer permanently removes ~28% of annual mine supply, the real yield stops being the marginal price-setter — and that is exactly why gold could double while real yields stayed positive.

It helps to be concrete about what "de-dollarization" actually means, because the word gets thrown around loosely. It does *not* mean the dollar is about to be replaced as the world's reserve currency — the dollar still makes up the majority of global reserves and the overwhelming share of trade invoicing, and no rival is close. What it means is a slow shift at the *margin*: foreign central banks gradually trimming the share of their reserves held in dollars and Treasuries and adding gold instead. The dollar's share of disclosed global reserves has drifted down from around 71% in 2000 to under 59% by the early 2020s — not a collapse, but a clear, decade-long erosion. The mechanism is reserve diversification: a central bank that holds, say, \$400 billion of reserves and wants to cut its dollar exposure from 70% to 65% has to find something else to hold, and gold — liquid, universally accepted, and nobody's liability — is the natural destination. Multiply that decision across dozens of central banks and you get the 1,000-tonne-a-year flow. The 2022 reserve freeze didn't start this trend, but it dramatically accelerated it, because it converted an abstract worry ("could the dollar lose value?") into a concrete one ("could my reserves be seized?"). Gold answers the concrete worry perfectly, which is why the post-2022 buying has been so persistent and so price-insensitive.

A modern wrinkle worth addressing: isn't Bitcoin the new "digital gold," and doesn't it compete for this bid? Bitcoin shares one property with gold — a capped, un-inflatable supply, no issuer — which is why it draws some of the same debasement-hedge demand. But for the *policy* story that drives gold, the two are not interchangeable. A foreign central bank diversifying out of dollars for geopolitical safety wants an asset that is deep, liquid, has a 5,000-year monetary track record, and can be held physically in its own vault; gold fits, and Bitcoin — far more volatile, far younger, and itself dependent on infrastructure — does not yet fit that institutional reserve role. So while Bitcoin and gold can rise together when the broad theme is "distrust of paper money and easy policy," the specific, structural, central-bank bid that broke gold's real-rate rule in 2025 is a gold-only phenomenon. Bitcoin answers to the liquidity tide and risk appetite more than to reserve policy.

This third channel is also why gold is correctly described as a *monetary asset* rather than a commodity. Copper's price is set by how many factories and power grids and electric cars the world is building — by industrial demand. Gold's price is set by how much the world trusts paper money — by monetary policy and geopolitics. Less than 10% of gold demand is industrial; the rest is store-of-value. When you buy gold you are not betting on the economy; you are betting on the *policy* that governs the economy's money.

### Gold's cousins: miners and silver

Two close relatives of gold deserve a quick word, because they are how many people actually express a gold view, and both behave as *leveraged* versions of the same policy story.

**Gold miners** are companies that dig gold out of the ground, and their stocks are a geared bet on the gold price. The reason is operating leverage. A miner's cost of production is roughly fixed — say it costs \$1,500 to extract an ounce. At a \$2,000 gold price the miner earns \$500 an ounce; if gold rises 25% to \$2,500, the miner now earns \$1,000 an ounce — a *doubling* of profit on a 25% move in the metal. Because the cost base is sticky, every dollar of gold-price upside drops almost straight to the bottom line, so miner profits swing far more than the gold price. The flip side is that miners are still *stocks*, so they also answer to the equity discount rate (the very thing covered in [how policy prices equities](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/how-policy-prices-equities-the-multiple-and-the-earnings)) — high rates that compress all equity multiples can drag miners even when gold itself is firm. So a miner is a bet on gold *and* a bet on the equity multiple, which is why miners can lag physical gold in a high-rate environment like 2022-23 and then violently outperform it when rates fall and gold rises together, as they began to in 2024-25.

**Silver** is gold's hyperactive little sibling. It shares the monetary, store-of-value role — so it rises and falls with the same real-rate and dollar drivers — but a much larger share of silver demand is *industrial* (solar panels, electronics, electric vehicles), so it also carries a growth-cycle component gold lacks. The combination makes silver more volatile in both directions: in gold bull markets silver typically outruns gold, and in gold bear markets it falls harder. Traders watch the **gold-silver ratio** (the number of ounces of silver that buys one ounce of gold) as a regime gauge: a very high ratio (silver cheap relative to gold) often marks a fear-driven, monetary-only rally where investors crowded into gold and ignored silver, while a falling ratio (silver catching up) often signals a broadening, growth-friendly phase. None of this changes the core lesson — both are still priced by the real rate, the dollar, and the monetary story — but it explains why a single gold view can be expressed at three different volatilities: physical gold (1x), silver (~1.5-2x), and miners (~2-3x).

## Common misconceptions

**"Gold is an inflation hedge, so it should rise whenever inflation rises."** This is the single most common error, and the data routinely embarrasses it. Gold hedges inflation only *over very long horizons and through the real-rate channel* — not mechanically with the CPI print. In 2022, US inflation hit a 40-year high of 9% and gold went *nowhere* on the year, because the Fed was hiking aggressively, real yields were spiking from −1% to +1.7%, and the dollar was at a two-decade high. Inflation was high but the policy *response* to inflation crushed gold's opportunity cost. The correct statement is: gold loves *high inflation that the central bank is not fighting* (so real yields stay low), and hates *high inflation that the central bank is aggressively fighting* (so real yields soar). It is the real yield, not the CPI, that gold tracks.

**"Gold goes up in a crisis."** Sometimes, eventually — but not reliably *during* the acute phase. In the worst days of the 2008 crash and the March 2020 COVID panic, gold initially *fell*, because terrified investors sold everything liquid, including gold, to raise cash and meet margin calls. Gold's big rallies came *after* the panic, once central banks slashed rates to zero and flooded the system with liquidity — that is, once the *policy response* drove real yields down. Gold is a bet on the policy response to a crisis, not on the crisis itself.

**"A weak dollar always means high gold, full stop."** The dollar channel is real but it is one of three, and the others can dominate. In 2025 the dollar fell *and* real yields rose; the dollar tailwind helped, but the central-bank bid did the heavy lifting. Conversely, you can have a flat dollar and surging gold (when the de-dollarization bid is the driver) or a falling dollar and flat gold (when rising real rates offset it). Always ask which of the three doors is open, not just one.

**"Gold and bonds are both safe assets, so they move together."** They are both havens, but they answer to *opposite* policy regimes. Bonds love disinflation and falling nominal yields; gold loves negative *real* yields and currency debasement. In a financial-repression world — yields pinned below inflation — the bondholder is the *loser* (negative real returns, as in 1942-51) while the gold-holder wins. They are havens for different fears.

## Case studies: four times policy set the gold price

Now the payoff. Four real, dated episodes, each one a different door opening — and together they are the whole gold-valuation machine in motion.

![Line chart of the gold price from 2018 to January 2026 rising to an all-time high of 5,589 dollars per ounce.](/imgs/blogs/how-policy-prices-gold-and-commodities-real-rates-and-the-dollar-2.png)

The chart shows the price arc the case studies live on: years of consolidation around \$1,200-\$1,800, then the 2024 break higher, then the near-vertical run through \$3,000, \$4,000, and to \$5,589 by January 2026. Each milestone has a policy story behind it.

### Case 1 — 1980: Volcker's 16% real rate crushes the \$850 top

The first modern gold mania ended on the door we opened first: the real rate. Through the 1970s, two oil shocks and a decade of loose policy had let US inflation run wild — it peaked at 14.8% in March 1980 — while the Fed kept rates too low for too long, so *real* yields were deeply negative for years. Gold did what gold does when real yields are negative and trust in money is collapsing: it went parabolic, from \$35 an ounce in the early 1970s (when it was still officially pegged) to a peak of about **\$850 an ounce in January 1980**.

Then Paul Volcker, who had taken over the Fed in August 1979, did the one thing guaranteed to kill gold: he drove the *real* rate sharply positive. He pushed the fed funds rate to a peak of **19.1%** in mid-1981, with the 10-year Treasury yielding nearly 16%. With inflation finally falling, that meant real yields of roughly +5% to +8% — an enormous opportunity cost for an asset that pays nothing. By the worked-example logic above, holding an ounce of gold now cost you something like 6% of its value a year in forgone real income. The result was brutal and immediate: gold fell from \$850 toward \$300 over the next few years and did not see \$850 again for *twenty-eight years*. Volcker's rate shock — which we cover in depth in [the Volcker 1980 case study](/blog/trading/finance/paul-volcker-1980-rate-shock-killing-inflation) — is the cleanest demonstration in history that high real rates are gold's kryptonite. Policy lever (rate hikes) → channel (real yield turns sharply positive) → valuation (gold collapses).

### Case 2 — 2011: real yields go deeply negative, gold tops \$1,900

Fast-forward thirty years to the mirror image. After the 2008 financial crisis, the Fed cut rates to zero and launched round after round of quantitative easing — buying bonds to push yields down and flood the system with liquidity. Inflation was modest, but the Fed was deliberately holding nominal yields *below* inflation, so the **real** yield went negative. By the worked-example logic, holding gold now cost *less* than holding a Treasury, because the Treasury was losing purchasing power. Money rotated hard into gold, which ran from under \$1,000 in 2009 to an intraday peak above **\$1,900 in September 2011**.

The trigger for the final leg was pure policy credibility: in August 2011, in the middle of the debt-ceiling standoff, S&P downgraded the US sovereign credit rating from AAA for the first time ever. The world's "risk-free" asset had just been declared slightly less risk-free, the Fed was holding real rates negative, and gold — the asset that *is* no one's liability — caught a credibility bid on top of the real-rate bid. Both the real-rate door and the credibility door were open at once. Notice the symmetry with 1980: same metal, opposite policy regime, opposite price. That is the seesaw in action across thirty years.

### Case 3 — 2022: high inflation, but gold goes nowhere

This case earns its place precisely because it *violates* the naive "inflation hedge" story, and the violation is instructive. 2022 had the highest US inflation in 40 years — a 9% CPI peak in June. By the misconception, gold should have soared. It didn't; it ended the year roughly flat around \$1,824, after a brief spike to \$2,070 right after Russia invaded Ukraine that fully faded by autumn.

Why? Because all three doors were *shut* or pushing the wrong way. The Fed responded to the inflation with the fastest hiking cycle since Volcker — 525 basis points in 16 months — which drove the **real** yield from −1% at the start of the year to +1.7% by October. That is gold's worst enemy. Simultaneously, the rate hikes drove the **dollar** to a two-decade high near 115 on the dollar index — a strong-dollar headwind. The only door open was the credibility/safe-haven door, briefly, around the invasion, and even that faded as the dollar and real-yield vice tightened. The lesson: gold doesn't track inflation; it tracks the *policy response* to inflation. When the central bank fights inflation hard, real yields rise and the dollar strengthens, and both crush gold even with the CPI at a 40-year high.

### Case 4 — 2025-26: the record run *despite* positive real yields

And now the case that motivated the whole post — the one where the old rule broke. By 2025, real yields were firmly positive, around +2%. By the Volcker-and-2022 logic, gold should have been flat to down. Instead it ran 64% on the year, breached \$3,000 in March and \$4,000 in October, and printed **\$5,589 an ounce by late January 2026** — an all-time high, and nearly seven times the inflation-adjusted 1980 peak.

What changed was that the *marginal buyer* changed, and with it the door that mattered. Three things stacked up. First, the **dollar** fell more than 10% in the first half of 2025 — its worst H1 since 1973 — as markets read the tariff chaos, the ballooning deficit, and the political pressure on the Fed as a credibility problem; that alone was a double-digit mechanical tailwind. Second, the **fiscal/debasement** backdrop got worse, not better: the 2025 OBBBA tax law added trillions to projected deficits, debt crossed 100% of GDP, and savers reached for the one asset no one can dilute. Third and decisively, the **de-dollarization** bid went into overdrive. The 2022 reserve freeze had taught every non-aligned central bank that dollar reserves carry political risk; they responded by buying gold at over 1,000 tonnes a year and shifting reserves out of Treasuries and into bullion. That buyer is price-insensitive and policy-driven — it does not consult the 10-year real yield. So gold and real yields rose together, the −0.8 correlation snapped, and the price went vertical.

This is not the real-rate rule failing; it is a *regime change in who sets the gold price.* For forty years the marginal gold buyer was a Western investor for whom the real yield was the whole story. In 2025 the marginal buyer became a foreign central bank executing a multi-year policy of getting out of dollars. When the dominant force on one side of the seesaw changes, the seesaw's behavior changes — and you can read the entire shift in one price chart. For the trader's-eye view of how to *position* around this regime (rather than the valuation mechanics we own here), see the macro-trading series on [how monetary policy moves commodities, real rates, and gold](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-monetary-policy-moves-commodities-real-rates-gold).

## From gold to the rest of the commodity complex

Gold is the purest policy asset, but the same machinery — real rates, the dollar, and policy shocks — prices the whole commodity complex, with one crucial difference: most commodities *do* have a cash flow of sorts, in the form of industrial demand and a cost of carry. So policy reaches them through extra doors that gold doesn't have.

**The dollar door is universal.** Almost every globally traded commodity — oil, copper, wheat, soybeans — is priced in dollars, for exactly the reason gold is. So a weaker dollar lifts the whole complex mechanically, and a stronger dollar caps it. This is why "the dollar" and "commodities" are two of the most reliably inverse macro pairings, and why a Fed easing cycle (weaker dollar) is usually a commodity tailwind across the board.

**The real-rate door works through carry and inventory.** Holding a physical commodity costs money: storage, insurance, and the interest you forgo on the cash tied up. When real rates are high, that carry cost is high, so traders and producers hold *less* inventory and demand a lower spot price; when real rates are low, carrying inventory is cheap, supporting prices. It is the same opportunity-cost logic as gold, just applied to a barrel of oil or a tonne of copper instead of an ounce.

**But commodities have a demand door gold lacks.** Copper is famously "Dr. Copper" — its price is a real-time read on industrial and construction activity, because it goes into everything that gets built. Oil tracks transport and manufacturing demand. So commodities respond to the *fiscal and growth* side of policy too: a big infrastructure program or a Chinese stimulus package lifts industrial metals directly through demand, in a way it never lifts gold. This is why copper and gold can diverge sharply — gold rising on a debasement bid while copper falls on a growth scare, or vice versa. They are answering different policy questions.

The clearest way to see the demand door is the **copper-gold ratio**, a favorite of macro traders. Copper is pure growth; gold is pure monetary fear. So the ratio of the two strips out the parts they share (the dollar, the real rate, which lift or cap both) and isolates *growth optimism versus monetary fear*. When the ratio rises — copper outperforming gold — the market is pricing a strong, building, growth-on world; when it falls — gold outperforming copper — the market is pricing a slowdown or a debasement scare. The copper-gold ratio even tracks the 10-year Treasury yield reasonably well, because both are reading the same growth-and-inflation signal. The point for policy: a fiscal stimulus aimed at *building things* (infrastructure, manufacturing, the energy transition) shows up in copper first and gold barely at all, while a *monetary* loosening or a fiscal program that just inflates the deficit shows up in gold. The two metals are two different policy thermometers.

Two more wrinkles separate the commodity complex from gold. First, commodities have a **cost curve**: there is a price below which producers shut in supply (a marginal oil well, a high-cost copper mine), which puts a rough floor under the price that gold — with no production cost driving its value — doesn't have. Second, commodities can fall into **backwardation or contango** — the futures curve sloping down or up — which encodes the carry story directly: when real rates are high and inventories are ample, the curve tends toward contango (future prices above spot, because you are paid to wait), and when supply is tight the curve flips to backwardation (spot above futures, because you'll pay up to have the barrel *now*). Reading the shape of the futures curve is reading the real-rate-and-scarcity story in one picture. These are mechanics gold mostly escapes precisely because it is a monetary stock, not a consumed flow.

**And commodities have a supply door driven by war and intervention.** This is the most violent one, and it is where commodities differ most from gold. A geopolitical shock that threatens *supply* — a war in an oil-producing region, an export ban, a sanction regime — can spike a commodity's price overnight in a way that has nothing to do with rates or the dollar. War reaches markets through policy: sanctions are a financial lever, defense spending is a fiscal lever, and an energy-price spike forces the central bank's hand.

![Grouped bar chart of oil price reactions to major conflicts, showing pre-event prices and conflict peaks for 1973, 1990, 2003, 2022, and 2024.](/imgs/blogs/how-policy-prices-gold-and-commodities-real-rates-and-the-dollar-6.png)

The chart catalogs the war-to-oil link across five conflicts. The 1973 Yom Kippur War and the OPEC embargo roughly *quadrupled* oil from \$3 to \$12 a barrel; the 1990 Gulf War doubled it from \$17 to \$36; the 2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine spiked Brent from \$94 to a near-record \$139. But notice the right side of the chart: the 2024 Israel-Iran strikes barely moved oil, from \$72 to \$80. The difference is *spare supply.* In 1973 the world had no buffer; in 2024, US shale and OPEC spare capacity capped the shock. The lesson for the modern era: not every war is a 1973, because the supply cushion has grown — but when supply is genuinely threatened, the oil spike is the fastest way policy and geopolitics reach the inflation rate and force a central-bank response.

#### Worked example: an oil shock feeds CPI and forces the policy response

> Suppose a geopolitical shock drives oil from \$70 to \$120 a barrel — a 71% jump, in the range of the 2022 Ukraine spike. Trace it through to policy.
>
> **Step 1 — into the inflation rate.** Energy is directly and indirectly a meaningful share of the consumer basket (gasoline, heating, and the transport cost embedded in every good). As a rough rule of thumb, a sustained doubling of oil adds on the order of 1.5-2.5 percentage points to headline CPI over the following year, both directly at the pump and indirectly as shipping and production costs pass through. So a 71% oil jump might push a 3% CPI toward 4.5-5%.
>
> **Step 2 — into the policy response.** Now the central bank faces the worst kind of inflation: a *supply shock*, which raises prices while *hurting* growth (consumers spend more on energy and less on everything else). It can either fight the inflation with rate hikes — risking recession — or look through it and risk inflation expectations un-anchoring. In 2022 it chose to fight, hard.
>
> **Step 3 — back to gold and commodities.** The rate hikes that fight the oil-driven inflation drive real yields *up*, which (per the first worked example) is a headwind for gold even as the inflation scare is a tailwind — exactly the cross-currents that pinned gold flat in 2022. Meanwhile oil itself, and energy equities, are the assets that *win* directly from the shock.
>
> **The intuition:** an oil shock is a policy event in disguise — it lands first on CPI, then forces the central bank's hand, and the *rate response* is what ultimately reprices gold, bonds, and stocks, often more than the oil move itself.

The deepest modern version of this is the 2022 Russia-Ukraine case, where the West weaponized finance itself — freezing \$300 billion of Russian reserves and cutting major Russian banks off from the dollar payment system — while the energy shock fed the global inflation that forced the fastest hiking cycle in 40 years. Defense, energy, and gold won; bonds, European equities, and emerging markets lost. Russia's own policy response — an emergency hike to 20% and hard capital controls — is itself a textbook of how a sanctioned economy defends its currency. That sanctions-as-a-financial-weapon story sits at the border of this series and the legal one; the rulebook side is in [law and geopolitics on the 2018-19 trade war](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-2018-19-us-china-trade-war) and the broader sanctions literature.

## What it means for asset values: the playbook

Pull it together into something you can actually use the next time policy moves. For gold and the commodity complex, value is set by three policy variables and one supply wildcard. Here is how to read each.

**The real interest rate is the master gauge for gold.** Watch the 10-year TIPS real yield (FRED series DFII10). Falling real yields — whether from rate cuts or rising inflation expectations — are a gold tailwind; rising real yields are a headwind. The rough magnitude: historically, every 1 percentage-point fall in the 10-year real yield was associated with a high-teens-to-20% rise in gold, though the 2025 regime shift has loosened that link. The signal that the rule is *holding* is gold and real yields moving inversely; the signal that something structural has taken over is gold and real yields moving *together* — which is your cue that the de-dollarization bid is in charge.

**The dollar is the universal multiplier.** A weaker dollar lifts gold and the whole commodity complex roughly one-for-one (a 10% dollar decline is ~11% of mechanical price lift); a stronger dollar caps them. Watch the dollar index. A Fed easing cycle usually weakens the dollar and is therefore a commodity tailwind; a Fed hiking cycle usually strengthens it and is a headwind. The dollar is also your read on US policy credibility — a falling dollar amid large deficits is the market pricing a debasement risk that gold loves.

**The credibility/de-dollarization bid is the slow structural force, and it is the new regime.** Watch central-bank gold purchases (the World Gold Council's quarterly Gold Demand Trends) and the fiscal trajectory (the deficit and the debt-to-GDP path). When official-sector buying runs above 1,000 tonnes a year and the deficit runs 6%+ of GDP in a non-recession, there is a persistent, price-insensitive bid under gold that can override the real-rate signal. This is what makes the 2020s gold regime different from the 1980s and 2010s: the marginal buyer is a policy-maker, not a trader.

**For commodities, add the growth and supply doors.** Industrial metals like copper respond to the fiscal/growth side — infrastructure programs, Chinese stimulus — in a way gold does not, so a growth-driven commodity rally can coexist with flat gold. And the supply wildcard — war, export bans, sanctions — can spike energy and agriculture overnight, which then feeds CPI and forces the central-bank response that reprices everything else.

**What would invalidate the read.** For gold, the bearish case is a regime where real yields rise *and* the central-bank/de-dollarization bid fades — a world where the US restores fiscal discipline, the Fed re-establishes hard credibility, the dollar strengthens, and foreign reserves rotate back toward Treasuries. In that world the old real-rate rule reasserts and gold gives back a chunk of the 2025 run. The bullish case is the continuation of the current mix: sticky deficits, a soft dollar, and central banks who keep buying because the geopolitical reason they started has not gone away. As of early 2026, the policy mix still favors the structural bid — which is why gold sits at a record rather than retracing.

The single most important takeaway is the one the whole series keeps returning to: **you cannot value gold by looking at gold.** It has no earnings to grow, no coupon to discount, no business to analyze. Its price is a mirror held up to monetary and fiscal policy — the real rate, the dollar, and the world's trust in paper money. When you understand the policy machine, you understand gold. And when gold does something the textbook says it shouldn't — like running to a record while real yields are positive — it is not gold breaking; it is policy telling you the regime just changed.
