> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A tariff is a tax on imports, and like every policy lever it reaches markets through a transmission channel and ends as a change in what an asset is *worth*. The unusual thing about a broad tariff is that it pulls *four* channels at once — it raises inflation (so rates stay higher-for-longer), it can weaken the currency, it squeezes corporate margins, and above all it injects uncertainty that fattens the risk premium on every asset.
>
> - **A tariff is paid at the border by the importer of record, then shared** — between the consumer (higher prices), the foreign exporter (lower prices), and the domestic firm (thinner margins). Who eats it depends on elasticities, not on who writes the check.
> - **The 2025 tariff shock** took the average US tariff rate from \~2.4% to the mid-teens — the highest since the 1930s Smoot-Hawley era. The S&P 500 fell \~19% peak-to-trough, then ripped **+9.5% in a single day** on the April 9 pause.
> - **The tell that this was a credibility shock, not an ordinary growth scare:** the 10-year Treasury yield *spiked* \~50 bp during the crash (a safe-haven *failure*), the dollar fell \~10% over the first half (its worst since 1973), and gold ran to records.
> - **The number to remember:** a broad tariff can make stocks, bonds, *and* the dollar fall together — a "sell-America" move — and that simultaneity is the rarest, most important thing a tariff can do to markets.

On the evening of April 2, 2025, after US markets had closed, the White House released Executive Order 14257. It imposed a 10% tariff on imports from almost every country on Earth, plus higher "reciprocal" rates on 57 trading partners — 34% on China, 46% on Vietnam, 20% on the European Union. The administration called it "Liberation Day." Markets called it something else.

When trading opened on April 3, the S&P 500 began a four-day slide that erased roughly 12% of its value — one of the worst stretches since the 2020 pandemic crash and the 2008 financial crisis. By the close on April 8 the index sat near 4,983, down almost 19% from its February high. Then, on April 9, the administration announced a 90-day pause on the reciprocal rates for every country except China. The S&P 500 jumped **9.5% in a single session** — one of the ten best days in the history of the index. Within a few hours, a tax decision had wiped out and then restored more than four trillion dollars of equity value.

That round-trip — a tax announcement, a crash, and a one-day rip on the pause — is the cleanest demonstration you will ever see of the thesis of this entire series: **a policy lever reaches markets through a transmission channel and ends as a change in what an asset is worth.** A tariff is that lever in its most vivid form. This post takes it apart: what a tariff actually *is*, who really pays it, the four channels it travels, and why a broad tariff can do the one thing markets fear most — make stocks, bonds, and the dollar fall *together*.

## Foundations: what a tariff is and who pulls the lever

A **tariff** is a tax on an imported good. When a container of steel, or sneakers, or auto parts, or pharmaceutical ingredients arrives at a US port, the **importer of record** — the company bringing the goods in, almost always a domestic business — must pay a percentage of the declared value to US Customs and Border Protection before the goods clear. A 25% tariff on a \$1,000 shipment means \$250 to the Treasury, due at the dock. The foreign seller does not write that check. The US importer does.

That single fact — *the importer pays, at the border, in dollars* — is where most public arguments about tariffs go wrong, and where the market analysis has to start. A tariff is mechanically identical to a sales tax that applies only to foreign-made goods. It is a **tax**, collected by the government, paid in the first instance by a domestic company. The question of who *ultimately* bears the cost — the consumer, the foreign exporter, or the domestic producer — is a separate question called **incidence**, and we will spend a whole section on it because it is where the valuation effects live.

### Who pulls the lever

In the United States, the Constitution gives Congress the power to "lay and collect Taxes, Duties, Imposts and Excises" and to "regulate Commerce with foreign Nations." For most of US history, tariffs were set by Congress through legislation — the infamous **Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act of 1930** was an act of Congress. But over the twentieth century, Congress delegated large chunks of tariff authority to the President through a series of trade statutes, each of which lets the executive raise tariffs under specific conditions:

- **Section 232** of the Trade Expansion Act of 1962 — tariffs on national-security grounds (this is the legal basis for the steel, aluminum, and auto tariffs).
- **Section 301** of the Trade Act of 1974 — tariffs to retaliate against "unfair" foreign trade practices (the basis for the 2018-19 China tariffs).
- **Section 122** of the same act — temporary tariffs (up to 15%, for up to 150 days) to address balance-of-payments deficits.
- The **International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA)** of 1977 — emergency economic powers during a declared "national emergency."

The 2025 Liberation Day tariffs were imposed under IEEPA, on the theory that persistent trade deficits constitute a national emergency. That choice of statute mattered enormously, because IEEPA had never been used to impose broad tariffs before, and its legality was contested from day one. We will return to how that legal question became a *market* question.

For our purposes, the key point is this: a modern US President can change the effective tax rate on a huge slice of the economy's inputs **by executive order, overnight, without a vote.** That is what makes a tariff such a potent — and such an *uncertain* — market force. A rate hike from the Federal Reserve is telegraphed weeks in advance through a careful communications process; the central bank works hard to be *predictable*, because predictability is itself stabilizing. A tariff can arrive after the market close on a Wednesday. The contrast in *predictability* — not just the contrast in the level of the tax — is a huge part of why tariffs hit markets harder than their direct economic cost would suggest.

### The kinds of tariff, and why the breadth matters

Not all tariffs are the same animal, and the distinction matters enormously for how markets react. A **targeted, sectoral tariff** — say a 25% duty on imported steel — hits a handful of industries and a few stocks. Steelmakers (domestic) may even *benefit*; steel-using industries (autos, appliances, construction) are hurt. The market can analyze it sector by sector, the winners offset the losers in a broad index, and the macro effect is small. Markets digest sectoral tariffs in a day or two.

A **broad, universal tariff** — a flat rate on *everything* from *everywhere* — is a different beast entirely. It is no longer a sector story; it is a *macro* story. It raises the cost of inputs across the whole economy, lifts the general price level, and forces the central bank to react. There are no clean "winner" sectors to offset the losers, because almost every company imports *something*. This is why the April 2025 universal tariff produced an index-level crash while the February 2025 sectoral steel-and-auto tariffs produced barely a ripple. **Breadth is what turns a tariff from a sector trade into a market force.**

A third distinction is the **retaliation dimension.** A tariff the US imposes is one shock; the *retaliatory* tariffs trading partners impose in response are a second, and they hit US exporters — farmers, aircraft makers, machinery producers — directly. The escalation spiral, where each side raises rates in response to the other, is the mechanism that turned a 34% US rate on China into 145% in a matter of weeks in 2025. Markets price the *risk of escalation*, not just the announced level, which is why a single retaliatory headline can move an index.

### The trade-deficit logic — and why economists dispute it

The stated rationale for the 2025 "reciprocal" tariffs was the US **trade deficit** — the fact that America imports far more goods than it exports, running a persistent gap of roughly \$1 trillion a year. The reciprocal rates were calculated, controversially, from each country's bilateral trade surplus with the US rather than from that country's actual tariff rates on American goods — which is why the formula produced 46% for Vietnam (a country that runs a large surplus with the US) even though Vietnam's actual tariffs on US goods are far lower.

Most economists dispute the premise that a bilateral trade deficit is a problem to be taxed away. A trade deficit is, by accounting identity, the mirror image of a *capital surplus* — the same dollars that flow out to buy imports flow back in to buy US assets (Treasuries, stocks, real estate). The US runs a trade deficit in part *because* the dollar is the world's reserve currency and foreigners want to hold US assets, which keeps the dollar strong and imports cheap. Tariffs don't address that underlying capital flow; they just tax the goods leg of it. This is not a political point — it is the analytical reason markets treated the tariffs as a *distortion* to be priced as risk, rather than as a fix for a real problem. When the rationale for a policy is contested by the people who price assets for a living, the policy carries a higher risk premium.

### How a tariff is actually measured, and why the average rate matters

When you read that "the average US tariff rate jumped from 2.4% to the mid-teens," it pays to understand what that number is, because it's the cleanest single gauge of the macro shock. The **average effective tariff rate** is total customs duties collected divided by the total value of imports. In 2024 the US collected duties equal to roughly 2.4% of the value of everything it imported — a low number, the product of decades of trade liberalization. A jump to 16-17% means the government is now skimming roughly a sixth of the value of imported goods at the border. Multiply that by the \$3-trillion-plus the US imports each year and you get a tariff *take* on the order of \$300-400 billion annually — a tax increase of historic size, landing on inputs that feed straight into consumer prices and corporate costs.

The reason the *average* matters more than any single country's rate is that markets care about the aggregate macro effect — the total drag on growth and the total push on inflation — not the bilateral detail. A 145% rate on China sounds terrifying, but if China is 13% of US imports, its contribution to the *average* is bounded. Conversely, a 10% universal tariff that touches *everything* moves the average a lot precisely because it is universal. This is the quantitative version of the breadth argument: the average rate is the scalar that compresses the whole tariff schedule into the one number the macro channels respond to.

### The everyday-money version

Before any of the market machinery, hold onto a simple analogy. Suppose your town imposes a 20% tax on every bag of groceries bought from out-of-town stores, payable by the local shop that ships them in. Three things can happen, and usually all three happen at once: the shop raises its shelf prices (you, the shopper, pay), the out-of-town supplier cuts its wholesale price to keep the shop's business (the foreigner pays), or the shop simply earns less per bag (the shop pays). On top of all of that, *nobody in town knows how long the tax will last or whether it will rise to 40% next month* — so the shop stops signing long supply contracts, delays hiring, and sits on cash. That last effect, the freeze, is the one that does the most damage to the town's economy, and it is the one markets price most violently. Keep the grocery tax in mind; every channel below is a more precise version of it.

## The incidence question: who actually pays a tariff?

Here is the most common misconception about tariffs, stated plainly so we can dismantle it: *"China pays the tariff."* Mechanically, no — the US importer pays it at the border. But economically, the *burden* can land anywhere along the chain, and figuring out where is the single most important analytical step, because it determines which assets reprice and by how much.

The burden splits according to **elasticity** — a measure of how much buyers and sellers change their behavior when the price changes. The side of the transaction that *cannot easily walk away* eats the tax.

![Who actually pays a tariff depends on demand and supply elasticity](/imgs/blogs/tariffs-and-trade-policy-as-a-market-force-7.png)

Read the matrix as two questions. First, *does the buyer have substitutes?* If American buyers genuinely need this specific imported good and have nowhere else to get it (inelastic demand — think specialized pharmaceutical ingredients or rare-earth magnets), the importer can raise the price and the consumer pays, because the consumer can't refuse. If buyers have easy substitutes (elastic demand — generic T-shirts available from a dozen countries), the importer can't raise the price without losing the sale, so the tax has to be absorbed somewhere else.

Second, *does the foreign supplier have pricing power?* If the foreign exporter is one of many interchangeable producers selling a commodity input, that exporter will cut its price to keep the American customer rather than lose the order — the foreigner eats part of the tax. If the foreign supplier is essential and irreplaceable, it won't budge, and the burden stays on the American side.

#### Worked example: a 20% tariff on a \$100 imported good

Take a concrete shipment. A US retailer imports a good that costs **\$100** landed (before tariff). The government imposes a **20% tariff**, so the importer owes **\$20** at the border. Where does that \$20 go?

**Case A — fully passed to the consumer (inelastic demand).** The retailer keeps its dollar margin and raises the shelf price. The good that sold for, say, \$150 now sells for \$170. The consumer pays the entire \$20. Inflation rises; the retailer's earnings are unchanged; the foreign exporter's revenue is unchanged.

**Case B — fully absorbed by the foreign exporter (elastic demand, replaceable supplier).** The retailer refuses to pay more than \$100 all-in. The foreign exporter cuts its wholesale price from \$100 to about \$83, so that \$83 + 20% tariff ≈ \$100 lands. The consumer pays nothing extra; the retailer's margin is intact; the *foreigner* bore the \$20 (really \$17 of price cut, since the tariff is on the lower base). Inflation barely moves.

**Case C — split, the realistic case.** Studies of the 2018-19 round found that the tariffs were passed *almost entirely* into US import prices — meaning American buyers, not Chinese exporters, bore the bulk. Suppose a realistic split: the consumer pays \$12 (shelf price up), the exporter cuts price by \$5, and the retailer eats \$3 of margin. Now *three* assets move at once: consumer prices tick up (an inflation impulse), the retailer's earnings per share falls (an equity repricing), and the foreign exporter's home-country economy weakens (a currency effect).

**Intuition:** who writes the check (always the importer) tells you nothing about who *bears* the cost; the side with no substitutes always pays, and in the 2018-19 and 2025 episodes the empirical answer was overwhelmingly *the American consumer and firm*, not the foreign exporter.

This is why the political slogan and the market reality diverge so sharply. If a tariff were truly paid by foreigners, it would be a free lunch — revenue for the Treasury at no domestic cost, and markets would shrug. The reason markets *don't* shrug is that the burden falls mostly at home, as higher prices and thinner margins. That is the bridge from "a tariff is a tax" to "a tariff is a market force."

There is one more wrinkle that the incidence matrix hides: **time.** In the short run, demand is *inelastic* for almost everything — a company that built its supply chain around a specific Chinese supplier can't re-source overnight, so it pays the tariff and passes it on. That means the *short-run* incidence falls heavily on the American consumer and firm, exactly as the data show. Over *years*, supply chains adjust — production moves to Vietnam, or Mexico, or back home — and the long-run incidence can shift. But markets discount the near-term cash flows most heavily, and the near-term incidence is domestic. This is why the equity repricing is fast and the supply-chain "reshoring" benefit, if it comes at all, is slow and uncertain — a mismatch that markets resolve in favor of the immediate, visible cost.

#### Worked example: the exporter and the retaliation hit

The incidence story has a second half that the headline misses — what happens to the foreign *exporter*, and to the US *exporter* facing retaliation. Take a US farm exporter — say a soybean producer — selling \$10 billion of soybeans a year, of which \$4 billion goes to China.

When China retaliates against US tariffs with a 25% duty on American soybeans, Chinese buyers face a higher landed cost and switch to Brazilian soybeans. The US producer doesn't pay the Chinese tariff — but it *loses the sale*, or must cut its price to compete. Suppose it cuts its price 15% to retain half its China volume and loses the other half. Revenue from China falls from \$4 billion to about **\$1.7 billion** (\$2 billion of volume at an 85% price), a roughly \$2.3 billion hit. The producer's earnings fall, its stock reprices, and — critically — this damage was caused not by the US tariff but by the *retaliation* to it.

**Intuition:** a tariff is a two-sided weapon: the side that imposes it taxes its own consumers, and the retaliation taxes its own exporters — which is why a tariff war is negative-sum and why the agricultural, aircraft, and machinery sectors (heavy US exporters) sold off on *retaliation* headlines in both 2018-19 and 2025, even though they import little themselves.

## The four channels: how a tariff reaches asset prices

A single rate hike from the Fed travels essentially one channel — it raises the discount rate. A tariff is messier and more dangerous precisely because it travels *four* channels simultaneously, and they don't all push the same direction. Let's take them one at a time, then see how they collide.

### Channel 1: the inflation impulse and the discount rate

A tariff raises the price of imported goods and of every domestic good that uses imported inputs. That is, by construction, an **inflation impulse** — a one-time uplift to the price level, and potentially a persistent one if firms keep raising prices and workers demand higher wages to compensate.

Why does inflation hit asset values? Because a stock or a bond is worth the **present value of its future cash flows**, and present value is computed by *discounting* those future dollars at a rate that includes expected inflation. Higher expected inflation means a higher discount rate, which means every future dollar is worth less today, which means the asset is worth less. (We build this discount-rate machinery from scratch in [the discount-rate channel post](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/how-policy-sets-asset-prices-the-transmission-map); here we just use the result.)

But the deeper mechanism runs through the **central bank's reaction function.** If a tariff pushes inflation up, the Federal Reserve faces a dilemma: cut rates to support growth (because tariffs also slow the economy), or hold rates high to fight the inflation? In 2025 the Fed chose to *wait* — it held the funds rate at 4.25-4.50% from January through August, explicitly because it wanted to see whether the tariffs would show up as inflation or as job losses before moving. That delay is itself a market force. "Higher-for-longer" rates are the discount-rate channel in action.

#### Worked example: a tariff's CPI uplift and the Fed's delay

Suppose imports are about **14% of US GDP**, and a broad tariff raises the average tariff rate by **12 percentage points** (roughly the 2025 move from \~2.4% to the mid-teens). If the full tariff passed into prices and applied to all imports, the first-round price-level effect would be about **14% × 12% ≈ 1.7 percentage points** added to the price level. In practice, pass-through is partial and phased, so estimates clustered around **+0.5 to +1.0 percentage point** added to year-over-year CPI inflation over the following year.

Now follow the Fed's reaction. Before the tariff, suppose the Fed was on track to cut from 4.50% to about 3.50% over the year — four cuts — as inflation drifted toward its 2% target. A +0.7-point inflation impulse pushes the inflation path *away* from target, so the Fed delays: instead of cutting in March, it holds until September, and delivers perhaps three cuts instead of four. The front end of the yield curve reprices to that slower path. For a long-duration asset — say a growth stock whose fair value is sensitive to the discount rate — even a quarter-point-higher rate held for an extra six months trims the present value by **1-2%**.

**Intuition:** a tariff doesn't just raise prices once; it changes the *path of the policy rate* by forcing the central bank to keep money tighter for longer, and that "higher-for-longer" path is what reprices long-duration assets — the exact reason "tariffs keep rates higher for longer" became a 2025 market refrain.

There is a genuinely hard problem buried here, and it's worth naming because it explains the Fed's paralysis in 2025. A tariff is *both* an inflation shock *and* a growth shock at the same time. It pushes prices up (the Fed's instinct: raise rates) *and* it slows the economy (the Fed's instinct: cut rates). This is the central bank's nightmare — a **supply shock**, sometimes called "stagflationary," because it pushes inflation and unemployment in opposite directions from the Fed's two goals. The Fed's tools can address one or the other, not both. Faced with this, the Fed's 2025 choice was to *wait and see* which effect dominated — and waiting, with rates held high, is itself a tightening relative to the cuts the market had expected. The supply-shock nature of a tariff is precisely why it is so much harder for markets to price than a demand shock: there is no clean policy response that fixes it.

### Channel 2: the currency

A tariff *should*, in textbook theory, *strengthen* the home currency. The logic: tariffs reduce imports, which reduces demand for foreign currency to pay for those imports, which pushes the dollar up. For most of trade-war history this held — during the 2018-19 US-China tariffs, the dollar broadly firmed.

In 2025, the opposite happened, and the reason it happened is the most important lesson in this entire post. The dollar *fell* — the Dollar Index (DXY) dropped from about 110 in January to under 98 by April, a decline of roughly 10.7% over the first half of the year, the worst first-half performance since **1973**, when the post-war fixed-exchange-rate system collapsed.

Why did the dollar fall when the theory said it should rise? Because the tariff wasn't read as a *trade* policy; it was read as a *credibility* policy. Foreign investors who hold trillions of dollars of US assets — Treasuries, equities, real estate — concluded that US policy had become erratic and that the rule-of-law foundations of the dollar's reserve status were less certain than they had assumed. They sold dollar assets. That selling overwhelmed the textbook trade-flow effect. We will see this same logic — credibility, not flows — drive the bond market in the next channel.

A weaker dollar is itself a transmission channel into asset values. It raises the dollar price of imported goods (reinforcing the inflation impulse), it raises the dollar value of foreign earnings for US multinationals (a partial offset for large-cap equities), and it raises the dollar price of dollar-denominated commodities like gold and oil.

The reserve-currency angle deserves emphasis because it is the deepest part of the channel. The dollar is special: it is the unit in which most global trade is invoiced, most cross-border debt is issued, and most central-bank reserves are held. That special status rests on an implicit promise — that US assets are the safest, most liquid, most rule-governed store of value in the world. A tariff regime imposed by overnight executive order, reversible at will, and challenged in court chips at *that promise*, not just at trade flows. When foreign central banks and sovereign funds begin, even at the margin, to diversify *away* from the dollar — buying gold, euros, or yuan instead — the effect on the currency dwarfs the trade-flow arithmetic. The 2025 dollar decline was, at its core, a small repricing of the *reserve premium* the dollar had enjoyed for 80 years. Small repricings of an 80-year premium are enormous in dollar terms.

#### Worked example: the dollar move and an unhedged foreign holder

Put the reserve-diversification logic in numbers from a single foreign investor's seat. A European pension fund holds **\$1 billion** of US equities, unhedged, when DXY is at 110 and the euro/dollar rate makes \$1 = €0.91. The fund's holding is worth **€910 million**.

Now the tariff shock hits. US stocks fall 15% *and* the dollar falls 10% against the euro. In dollars, the holding is worth \$850 million. In euros — the currency the fund actually reports in — it is worth \$850M × (0.91 × 0.90) ≈ **€696 million**, a loss of about **23.5%**, far worse than the 15% a US-based investor saw. The currency loss *stacked on top of* the equity loss.

Faced with that double hit, the rational European fund trims its US exposure — selling both the stock *and* the dollar — which pushes the dollar down further, which deepens the loss for the next foreign holder, who then sells too. **Intuition:** for a foreign holder, a falling dollar turns a stock-market loss into a *compounded* loss, which is exactly why a credibility-driven dollar decline becomes self-reinforcing — the currency channel and the equity channel feed each other in a loop that a domestic-only analysis completely misses.

### Channel 3: corporate margins and earnings

A stock is a claim on a company's future earnings. A tariff hits earnings two ways. First, directly: if the company imports inputs and can't fully pass the tariff into its prices (the incidence question again), its **margins** compress — the same revenue, higher cost of goods, lower profit. Second, indirectly: if the tariff slows the broader economy, *demand* falls, and the company sells fewer units. Lower margin times fewer units is a double hit to earnings, and earnings are the numerator of every equity valuation.

#### Worked example: a tariff's hit to a retailer's EPS and fair value

Take a hypothetical importer-retailer. It earns **\$5.00 per share** in profit. Its goods cost **\$60 billion** to import annually, and it imports heavily from a country now facing a **20% tariff**. Suppose the company can pass two-thirds of the tariff into prices but must eat one-third.

The tariff bill is **20% × \$60B = \$12B**. The company passes \$8B into prices (consumers pay) and absorbs **\$4B** of margin. If the company has 1 billion shares outstanding and a 25% tax rate, the after-tax hit to profit is **\$4B × (1 − 0.25) = \$3B**, or **\$3.00 per share**. Earnings per share fall from \$5.00 to **\$2.00** — a 40% earnings cut.

Now reprice the stock. If the market was paying a **20× price-to-earnings multiple** on \$5.00 of earnings, the stock was worth **\$100**. Hold the multiple constant and apply it to the new \$2.00 of earnings, and fair value falls to **\$40** — a 60% decline. In reality the multiple would also compress (higher discount rate, higher uncertainty), so the repricing could be even larger. This is why import-heavy retailers, automakers, and consumer-goods companies were among the hardest-hit stocks in April 2025.

**Intuition:** a tariff doesn't have to wipe out a company to halve its stock — it just has to take a bite out of the *margin*, because the stock price is the earnings times a multiple, and a 40% earnings cut at a constant multiple is a 40% price cut before the multiple even moves.

### Channel 4: the risk premium and the uncertainty shock

This is the channel that matters most, and the one that purely-economic models miss. Every asset price embeds a **risk premium** — extra return investors demand for bearing uncertainty. The higher the uncertainty, the higher the premium investors demand, the lower the price they will pay today.

A tariff — especially one imposed by executive order, subject to pauses, escalations, country-by-country negotiation, and pending court challenges — is an **uncertainty machine.** A company trying to decide whether to build a factory, sign a five-year supply contract, or hire 500 workers faces a policy environment that can flip in a week. The rational response is to *wait*. That waiting — the freeze in the grocery-store analogy — slows investment and hiring across the whole economy, independent of where the tariff rate finally settles.

Researchers measure this with indices of "economic policy uncertainty," and these indices spiked to record highs in 2025. Markets price uncertainty directly through the risk premium: when the range of possible outcomes widens, the price of every risky asset falls even if the *expected* outcome hasn't changed, because investors discount the wider distribution more heavily.

The one-day +9.5% rip on April 9 is the proof. *Nothing about the economy improved that day.* The tariffs that had been announced were merely *paused* for 90 days. But the *uncertainty* collapsed — the worst-case scenario was taken off the table for three months — and the risk premium snapped back, lifting the entire market 9.5% in hours. When uncertainty is the dominant channel, the market trades the *distribution of policy outcomes*, not the policy itself.

It is worth being precise about *why* uncertainty alone lowers prices, because it is counterintuitive. Compare two worlds with the *same* expected tariff rate — say, an average of 15%. In World A, everyone knows the rate will be exactly 15% forever. In World B, the rate is equally likely to be 5% or 25%, averaging 15%, but nobody knows which. A risk-averse investor pays *more* for the asset in World A even though the *expected* cash flow is identical, because the certainty is worth something. The wider the range of outcomes, the larger the discount the investor demands. A tariff policy that swings from 10% to 145% to 30% inside two months is World B in the extreme — the *range* is enormous, so the discount is enormous, even before anyone knows where the rate settles. This is the formal reason that "uncertainty" is not hand-waving: it is a measurable widening of the outcome distribution, and the risk premium is the price the market charges for it.

The corollary cuts the other way too. Because the damage comes from the *width* of the distribution, *any* narrowing of that width — a pause, a deal, a court ruling that caps the worst case — produces an immediate relief rally, regardless of whether the *central* expectation improved. This is why tariff-era markets are so headline-driven and so prone to violent reversals: every announcement reshapes the distribution, and prices reprice the *whole distribution* in real time.

### How the four channels interact — and why the net effect is ambiguous

A reader who has followed the four channels closely will notice something uncomfortable: they don't all push asset prices the same direction, and some of them partly cancel. The inflation channel pushes yields *up*; the growth-scare side of the same shock pushes yields *down*. The trade-flow currency effect pushes the dollar *up*; the credibility effect pushes it *down*. This is not a flaw in the framework — it is the single most important thing to understand about tariffs, because *which channel dominates* is exactly what determines the market outcome, and it depends on context.

Three contextual factors decide the dominant channel. First, **breadth**: a narrow tariff barely touches inflation or the currency, so its effect is a clean sector-earnings story; a universal tariff lights up all four channels. Second, **the inflation backdrop**: a tariff that lands when inflation is already above target (as in 2025) makes the inflation channel dominant, because the central bank can't ease to cushion the growth hit — it's pinned by its inflation mandate. A tariff that lands in a low-inflation, slack economy lets the growth channel dominate, and the central bank can cut. Third, and most important, **the credibility interpretation**: if the market reads the tariff as a normal, bounded trade action, the safe-haven reflexes hold; if it reads it as a sign that policy itself has become erratic and rule-of-law is in question, the credibility channel overwhelms everything and you get the "sell-America" outcome below.

The practical upshot is that you cannot predict a tariff's market effect from the tariff *alone*. You have to ask: how broad, what's the inflation backdrop, and how is credibility being read? The same 10% tariff is a yawn in one context and a crisis in another. That context-dependence is why this framework is built around *channels* rather than a single rule — the rule changes with the regime.

## The rare and dangerous case: stocks, bonds, and the dollar fall together

Now we can assemble the channels into the thing that makes a broad tariff genuinely scary for markets — and genuinely unusual.

In a *normal* risk-off event — a recession scare, a banking wobble, a geopolitical shock — three things happen together, and they *cushion* each other:

1. Stocks fall (risk-off).
2. Treasury bonds *rally* — their yields *fall* — because frightened money flees to the safest asset, US government debt. This is the **safe-haven** or "flight-to-quality" reflex.
3. The dollar *strengthens*, because global money rushes into dollar assets for safety.

The rallying bond and the rising dollar are the *automatic stabilizers* of a US financial crisis. When stocks fall, bonds rise, so a 60/40 portfolio is cushioned; and a stronger dollar tightens conditions abroad but signals confidence in US assets. A diversified investor is partially protected.

A broad tariff that reads as a *credibility* shock breaks all three reflexes at once. Watch what actually happened in April 2025:

![The 2025 safe-haven failure where Treasury yields rose while the dollar fell](/imgs/blogs/tariffs-and-trade-policy-as-a-market-force-6.png)

Look at the red line. As stocks crashed in early April, the 10-year Treasury yield did not fall — it *spiked*, rising about 50 basis points in a single week, from under 4.0% to about 4.5%. Bonds were *selling off* during an equity crash. The safe haven failed. And the blue line shows the dollar falling hard over the same window, not strengthening.

Why does this happen? When the shock is the *US government's own policy credibility*, US assets are no longer the safe haven — they are the *source* of the risk. Foreign holders sell Treasuries (yields up) and sell dollars (DXY down) at the same time they sell stocks. The three legs that normally cushion each other instead pile on. There is nowhere to hide inside dollar assets. Money flees to the one asset that is nobody's liability — gold.

This pattern has a name in markets: the **"sell-America" trade.** It is the same dynamic that disciplined the United Kingdom in September 2022, when an unfunded fiscal package sent gilt yields surging *and* the pound crashing *and* UK stocks falling together, forcing the Bank of England into emergency intervention and ending Prime Minister Truss's tenure in 49 days. (We cover that episode in the fiscal-credibility section of [the fiscal toolkit post](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-fiscal-toolkit-spending-taxes-and-deficits).) A credibility shock is the one macro event that can make *every* domestic asset fall at once.

What makes this pattern genuinely dangerous — as opposed to merely painful — is that it breaks the machinery of diversification that the entire wealth-management industry is built on. The standard "60/40" portfolio (60% stocks, 40% bonds) works because, in *most* downturns, the two legs are negatively correlated: when stocks fall, bonds rise, so the portfolio loses less than stocks alone. That negative correlation is the free lunch of modern portfolio construction. A credibility shock turns the correlation *positive* — stocks and bonds fall together — and the free lunch vanishes exactly when you need it most. The 60/40 portfolio had its worst year in a generation in 2022 for precisely this reason (a different cause — runaway inflation forcing the Fed to hike — but the same correlation flip). In the April 2025 tariff window, the same flip recurred in miniature: a balanced portfolio was hit on both legs at once. The lesson for portfolio construction is that bonds hedge *growth* shocks but not *inflation* or *credibility* shocks — and a broad tariff is the latter two, not the former.

This is also why **gold and other non-dollar stores of value** earn their place in a portfolio. They are the only large, liquid assets whose value does not depend on the US policy machinery that is the source of the risk. In a credibility shock, the correlation of gold to the dollar goes sharply negative — gold rises *because* the dollar falls — making it the one diversifier that actually works when the others fail. The 64% gold gain in 2025, against the dollar's worst first half since 1973, is that relationship written large.

#### Worked example: the term-premium and dollar effect of a credibility shock

This is the subtlest channel, so let's put numbers on it. A 10-year Treasury yield can be split into two parts: the *expected average short-term rate* over the next decade, plus a **term premium** — extra yield investors demand for the risk of holding a long bond instead of rolling short ones.

In a normal risk-off, the expected-rate component *falls* (the market expects the Fed to cut to fight the slowdown), which pulls the 10-year yield *down* — the safe-haven rally. In April 2025, the expected-rate component did fall somewhat, but the **term premium rose more.** Foreign investors, less sure that US debt was a riskless asset, demanded extra compensation to hold it. Suppose the expected-rate path fell by 20 bp but the term premium rose by 70 bp — the *net* yield rises 50 bp, exactly the spike we observed.

The dollar side compounds it. If foreign holders of, say, \$8 trillion in Treasuries decide to trim their US exposure by even 3%, that is **\$240 billion** of selling — selling both the bond (yields up) *and* the dollars the bond is denominated in (DXY down). A US-based investor holding the same Treasury loses on the price *and* would lose on the currency if measured in euros or yen, but a domestic investor at least keeps the dollar. A foreign investor takes both losses — which is exactly why they sell, in a self-reinforcing loop.

**Intuition:** when policy itself becomes the risk, the safe asset and the safe currency stop being safe — the term premium and the currency move *the same direction as* the stock-market loss instead of against it, and that is the precise mechanism by which a tariff can sink stocks, bonds, and the dollar together.

## Case studies: tariffs as a market force, dated and real

Theory is only as good as the tape. Here are three real episodes — one a generation apart, one recent, one current — that show the channels firing.

### Case 1: Smoot-Hawley, 1930 — the cautionary tale

The **Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act**, signed in June 1930, raised US tariffs on more than 20,000 imported goods to some of the highest levels in American history — an average rate that pushed well above 40% on dutiable imports. It was passed *into* the early Great Depression, ostensibly to protect American farmers and workers.

It is remembered as a catastrophe, and the mechanism is the channels in this post run in reverse and amplified. Trading partners retaliated with their own tariffs; global trade collapsed by roughly two-thirds between 1929 and 1934. The tariff didn't *cause* the Depression — the monetary contraction and banking collapse did far more damage — but it deepened and globalized it by strangling the trade channel and signaling that the world's largest economy had turned inward. The lesson markets absorbed, and have never forgotten, is that broad tariffs invite *retaliation*, and a tariff war is negative-sum: every economy ends up smaller. When commentators in 2025 said the average US tariff rate had reached "the highest since the 1930s," Smoot-Hawley is the comparison they meant, and it is why the initial market reaction was so violent.

There is a subtler lesson in the Smoot-Hawley story that is easy to miss. The Act became law in June 1930, but the stock market had already begun discounting it during the legislative debate in late 1929 — over a thousand economists signed a public letter begging President Hoover to veto it. Markets *anticipate* trade policy; they don't wait for the goods to clear customs. The price damage is done when the *policy* changes, not when the first tariff is collected. This is why, in 2025, the S&P 500 fell on the *announcement* of April 2, before a single dollar of the new reciprocal tariffs had been paid. The market discounts the expected future cost, instantly. The corollary, which we'll see in the playbook, is that the *relief* is also instant — the +9.5% pause-day rip happened before any tariff was actually un-collected.

### Case 2: the 2018-19 US-China trade war — the modern template

The first Trump administration's trade war with China, fought from 2018 to 2019 mostly under Section 301, is the modern template for how markets digest tariffs. The US imposed tariffs on hundreds of billions of dollars of Chinese goods in escalating tranches; China retaliated. The episode taught markets several durable lessons that shaped the 2025 reaction:

- **Pass-through was overwhelmingly to American buyers.** Academic studies (including widely-cited work by Amiti, Redding, and Weinstein) found the 2018-19 tariffs were passed almost entirely into US import prices — confirming the incidence answer above, that the *importer and consumer*, not the foreign exporter, bore the cost.
- **The uncertainty channel dominated the rate channel.** Equity markets gyrated not with the *level* of tariffs but with the *headlines* — a tweet hinting at escalation could drop the S&P 500 2-3% in a session; a hint of a deal could lift it as much. The market was trading the distribution of outcomes.
- **The dollar firmed,** consistent with the textbook trade-flow effect, because the shock was read as a *trade* dispute, not a credibility crisis. This is the crucial contrast with 2025.

The market path tells the story. The S&P 500 fell sharply in the fourth quarter of 2018 as the trade war escalated alongside Fed rate hikes — a roughly 20% peak-to-trough drop into late December. It recovered through 2019 on hopes of a deal, then lurched lower in May and August 2019 on escalation headlines, before the "Phase One" deal in January 2020 brought a truce. Crucially, the *cumulative* economic damage was modest — estimates put the GDP drag at a few tenths of a percent — yet the *market volatility* was large, precisely because the uncertainty channel was doing the work. The contrast with 2025 is the whole point: in 2018-19 the dollar *firmed* and Treasuries *rallied* on the risk-off days (the safe haven worked), because the conflict read as a contained trade dispute. In 2025 the dollar *fell* and Treasuries *sold off* — because the conflict read as a credibility shock. *Same lever, opposite cross-asset signature, because the market's interpretation differed.*

We give this episode a full treatment, including the legal mechanics of Section 301, in the law-and-geopolitics series — see [the 2018-19 US-China trade war](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-2018-19-us-china-trade-war) and the related [export controls and the chip war](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/export-controls-and-the-chip-war), which covers the move from *tariffs* (taxing trade) to *export controls* (banning it) — a sharper, more durable weapon.

### Case 3: the 2025 tariff shock — the credibility break

The flagship case, and the reason this post exists. Let's walk the timeline, because the *sequence* is the story.

![The 2025 tariff shock from Liberation Day to the Supreme Court strike](/imgs/blogs/tariffs-and-trade-policy-as-a-market-force-2.png)

It began in February-March 2025 with targeted tariffs — fentanyl-related duties on China, Mexico, and Canada, then 25% on steel, aluminum, and autos. The average US tariff rate crept from \~2.4% toward 3%. Markets were wary but stable.

Then came **Liberation Day, April 2** — EO 14257, the 10% universal tariff plus "reciprocal" rates on 57 partners. The reciprocal rates were the shock: 34% on China, 46% on Vietnam, 24% on Japan, 20% on the EU, and higher still on smaller economies like Cambodia (49%). The market's read was not "this is a trade negotiation"; it was "the rules just changed, by executive order, overnight, and we don't know where this stops." The four-day, \~12% S&P 500 plunge followed.

The escalation with China was breathtaking: tit-for-tat retaliation drove the US rate on Chinese goods to **145%** and China's rate on US goods to **125%** — numbers so high they amounted to a near-total trade embargo between the world's two largest economies. At a 145% rate, a \$100 Chinese good costs \$245 landed; trade in many categories simply stopped, because no price made sense. China also reached for a sharper weapon than tariffs: it restricted exports of **rare-earth elements and magnets**, inputs the US defense and technology sectors cannot easily source elsewhere. That move — escalating from *taxing* trade to *cutting off* a critical input — is the bridge to the export-control playbook, and it is why the chip-and-rare-earth supply chain became the strategic center of the conflict. (We trace that escalation in [export controls and the chip war](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/export-controls-and-the-chip-war).)

There was also a *fiscal* twin to the tariff story that markets had to integrate. Three months after Liberation Day, on July 4, 2025, the **One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA)** was signed — a large tax-cut package that permanently extended the 2017 individual tax brackets and added new cuts, at an estimated cost to the deficit of roughly \$3.4-4.9 trillion over a decade. The combination is the defining policy mix of the period: **tight money** (the Fed holding rates high to fight tariff-driven inflation) layered on **easy fiscal** (big tax cuts widening an already-large deficit). That mix — large deficits financed at high interest rates — is itself a source of upward pressure on long-term yields and term premia, and it compounded the credibility concerns the tariffs created. A government cutting taxes *and* raising tariffs *and* running a 6%-of-GDP deficit, while the central bank holds rates high, is asking bond investors to absorb a lot of supply at a moment of maximum policy uncertainty. (The fiscal half of this picture is the subject of [the fiscal toolkit post](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-fiscal-toolkit-spending-taxes-and-deficits).)

![S&P 500 through the 2025 tariff shock with the drawdown and the pause-day rip](/imgs/blogs/tariffs-and-trade-policy-as-a-market-force-5.png)

The chart shows the round-trip. The index fell from its February 19 high of 6,144 to a trough of 4,983 on April 8 — a 19% drawdown. Then, on **April 9**, the 90-day pause for all countries except China. The +9.5% single-day rip is the green arrow. By mid-May the index had erased its year-to-date loss entirely; by year-end it printed new highs above 6,600. The *crash and the full recovery happened in the same year* — the signature of an uncertainty shock, not a fundamental one. When the policy distribution narrowed, the risk premium normalized and prices snapped back.

But underneath the equity round-trip, the *cross-asset* damage told the deeper story. We already saw the safe-haven failure — the 10-year yield spiking to \~4.5% mid-crash and the dollar falling \~10% over the first half. And the asset that *won* was gold.

![Gold record run through the policy credibility shock](/imgs/blogs/tariffs-and-trade-policy-as-a-market-force-8.png)

Gold is the asset that has no issuer, no earnings, and no policy committee — it is nobody's liability. When the risk *is* US policy credibility, gold is the only large, liquid hedge that doesn't depend on the thing that broke. It crossed \$3,000/oz for the first time in March 2025, \$4,000 in October, and pushed to a record near \$5,589 by January 2026 — a gain of roughly 64% in 2025 alone, its best year in decades. Central banks, especially in Asia, were heavy buyers, partly as a deliberate move *away* from dollar reserves. (For the full monetary-hedge mechanism, see the gold series; here the point is that gold's record run is the *mirror image* of the dollar's worst first-half since 1973.)

### The climb-down and the legal reversal

The story didn't end with the pause. Over the following months, the administration negotiated bilateral deals that brought the headline rates down sharply.

![Reciprocal tariff rates announced on Liberation Day versus where they settled after deals](/imgs/blogs/tariffs-and-trade-policy-as-a-market-force-3.png)

The chart shows the climb-down. Vietnam's 46% became 20% (with a 40% rate on transshipped goods to stop China routing exports through Vietnam); China's 34% reciprocal rate — after the 145% spike and de-escalation — settled around 30%; the EU and Japan landed at 15%; South Korea at 15%. The country-by-country negotiation was itself a source of running uncertainty: every deal was a fresh headline, and markets traded each one.

![Average US tariff rate stepping to the highest level since the 1930s](/imgs/blogs/tariffs-and-trade-policy-as-a-market-force-4.png)

The net effect, even after the climb-down, was historic. The step chart shows the average effective US tariff rate jumping from \~2.4% in 2024 into the mid-teens through 2025 — settling around 16-17% at the peak before easing slightly. That is the **highest average US tariff rate since the 1930s.** A structural change of this size in the cost of imported inputs is a permanent feature of corporate cost structures and the inflation outlook, not a passing headline.

Then the twist that made trade lawyers and bond traders the same audience. On **February 20, 2026**, the Supreme Court ruled that **IEEPA does not authorize the imposition of these tariffs** — the emergency-powers statute used for Liberation Day was the wrong legal tool. The reciprocal tariffs were struck down. Within days, the administration re-invoked a global 10% tariff under **Section 122** (the balance-of-payments statute, capped at 15% for 150 days) to maintain a baseline while it sought a more durable legal footing. The legal question had become, definitively, a *market* question: the entire tariff edifice rested on a contested statute, and that contingency was a live source of uncertainty priced into every affected asset until the Court ruled.

## Common misconceptions

**"Tariffs are paid by foreign countries."** Mechanically false — the US importer pays at the border, in dollars. Economically, the *burden* splits by elasticity, and the empirical evidence from both 2018-19 and 2025 is that American consumers and firms bore the overwhelming majority. The slogan describes who you'd *like* to pay; the data describe who actually does.

**"A tariff just raises prices once, so it's a one-time blip."** The price-level effect may be one-time, but the *market* effects are not. The inflation impulse changes the central bank's rate path (higher-for-longer), the uncertainty freezes investment for as long as the policy is unsettled, and the credibility damage can persist in a structurally higher term premium and weaker currency. A "one-time" tax that lifts the average tariff rate to a 90-year high is a permanent change to cost structures.

**"Tariffs strengthen the currency."** They *can*, through the trade-flow channel, and historically did — the dollar firmed in 2018-19. But when the tariff reads as a *credibility* shock rather than a trade policy, the capital-flow channel (foreigners selling US assets) overwhelms the trade channel, and the currency *falls*. The dollar's \~10% first-half 2025 decline — its worst since 1973 — is the counterexample that proves the rule.

**"If stocks fall, my bonds will protect me."** Usually true; not in a credibility shock. The April 2025 safe-haven failure — Treasury yields *rising* while stocks crashed — meant a 60/40 portfolio got hit on both legs at once. The diversification that works for ordinary risk-off events *inverts* when the US government's own policy is the source of the risk.

**"The tariff level is what markets trade."** Markets trade the *uncertainty* far more than the level. The +9.5% pause-day rip happened with the tariffs still announced and merely paused — the level barely changed, but the *distribution* of outcomes narrowed, and that's what reprices the risk premium. Watch the headlines and the policy-uncertainty indices, not just the rate schedule.

**"At least the tariff revenue helps the budget."** The revenue is real — hundreds of billions a year — but it is not free money, and the worked example below shows why. A tariff raises revenue *and* destroys economic value at the same time, because it distorts choices: buyers shift to more expensive domestic goods they wouldn't have chosen at undistorted prices, and some trades simply don't happen. That destroyed value — the "deadweight loss" — is a real cost to the economy that doesn't show up as Treasury revenue. And because the burden falls mostly on domestic consumers and firms, the tariff is, in budget terms, a tax increase on Americans dressed up as a tax on foreigners.

#### Worked example: tariff revenue versus deadweight loss

Put numbers on the "it helps the budget" claim. Suppose the US imports \$100 billion of a good and imposes a 20% tariff. Imports don't stay at \$100 billion — the higher price reduces demand, say to \$80 billion. The government collects **20% × \$80B = \$16 billion** in revenue. That's the visible benefit.

But two costs hide behind it. First, the \$16 billion is paid by *American* importers and consumers (the incidence result), so it's a transfer *from* US households *to* the Treasury, not new wealth — it nets to zero for the country, just moving money from one American pocket to another. Second, the \$20 billion of trade that *stopped* represents transactions that were mutually beneficial at undistorted prices and now don't happen; the lost value to buyers and sellers — the deadweight loss — might be on the order of **\$2 billion** (the triangle between the old and new quantities). That \$2 billion is pure destruction: nobody collects it; it simply vanishes from the economy.

**Intuition:** a tariff's revenue is mostly a transfer from American consumers to the American government, not a payment from foreigners — and on top of that transfer sits a genuine deadweight loss that makes the whole economy smaller, which is the deepest reason markets treat a broad tariff as a *negative* for aggregate corporate earnings and not a fiscal windfall.

## What it means for asset values: the playbook

Pull the four channels into a usable read. When a broad tariff lands, ask which channel dominates, because that tells you which assets reprice and how.

**Equities.** Import-heavy sectors — retail, autos, consumer discretionary, industrials with global supply chains — take the direct margin hit (Channel 3) and reprice fast and hard. Large-cap multinationals get a partial offset from a weaker dollar (foreign earnings translate to more dollars). Domestic-services and utilities are more insulated. Across the board, the multiple compresses as the discount rate (Channel 1) and risk premium (Channel 4) rise. *Direction: down, with import-exposure determining the magnitude. The signal to watch: the gap between announced and negotiated rates — every climb-down is a relief rally.*

A useful way to *rank* sector exposure is to ask three questions of any company: (1) *How much of its cost of goods is imported, and from where?* — a retailer sourcing 60% of inventory from a 46%-tariff country is far more exposed than one sourcing domestically. (2) *Can it pass the tariff through, or is its demand elastic?* — a discount retailer with price-sensitive customers can't raise prices, so it eats the margin; a luxury brand with inelastic demand can pass it on. (3) *How much does it export into the line of retaliation?* — an aircraft maker or farm-equipment producer can be hurt by *retaliation* even if it imports nothing. Companies that score badly on all three — import-heavy, price-sensitive, and export-exposed — are the ones that fall the most, and they fell the most in April 2025: apparel retailers, automakers, and globally-sourced consumer-electronics names led the decline, while domestic utilities, healthcare services, and telecoms held up far better.

**Bonds.** The key question is *which* channel wins. If the inflation impulse dominates, yields rise (higher-for-longer). If the growth scare dominates, yields fall (safe-haven rally). If the *credibility* channel dominates — the dangerous case — yields rise *during* the equity sell-off as the term premium blows out and foreigners sell. *The tell: watch whether the 10-year yield falls or rises on a risk-off day. A rising yield during an equity crash is the credibility-shock signature, and it means your bonds are not hedging your stocks.* There is also a *curve* dimension: a tariff-driven inflation impulse hits the front end (the Fed's reaction function) and the term premium (the long end) differently. In 2025 the curve steepened — long yields rose more than short yields — because the inflation impulse and the term-premium blowout both concentrated at the long end while the Fed's held-but-eventually-cutting path anchored the front. A steepening curve during a risk-off is itself a credibility tell, distinct from the bull-steepening (front-end falling on rate-cut expectations) you'd see in an ordinary growth scare.

**The dollar.** Read the *type* of shock. A trade-flow shock firms the dollar; a credibility shock sinks it. *The signal: a falling dollar during a US-policy crisis is the "sell-America" tell, and it changes everything downstream — it amplifies imported inflation, lifts gold, and removes the currency cushion for foreign holders.*

**Gold and stores of value.** A tariff that damages policy credibility is the perfect environment for gold, which is nobody's liability and doesn't depend on the institution that broke. *The signal: gold making new highs while the dollar falls is the cleanest single confirmation that the market is pricing a credibility shock, not an ordinary growth scare.* The structural buyer underneath gold's 2025 run was not just retail or hedge funds — it was *central banks*, especially in Asia, deliberately shifting reserves out of dollars and into a neutral asset. That is the slow-moving, hard-to-reverse part of the story: a reserve manager who has decided the dollar carries more policy risk than assumed doesn't reverse that decision on a single good headline. It is the difference between a *tactical* gold rally (which fades when the scare passes) and a *structural* re-rating (which persists), and the central-bank bid is what made 2025 the latter.

### A practitioner's checklist when a tariff lands

When a tariff headline hits, run this sequence rather than reacting to the rate alone. First, *breadth* — is this a sector tariff or a universal one? Sector tariffs are a stock-picking problem; universal tariffs are a macro problem. Second, *incidence* — who bears it, given the elasticities? That tells you whether the hit lands on consumer prices (inflation), foreign exporters (currency), or domestic margins (earnings). Third, *the inflation backdrop* — is the central bank free to ease, or pinned by an inflation mandate? That decides whether bonds rally or sell off. Fourth, and most important, *the credibility read* — is the market treating this as a contained trade action or as a sign that policy itself is erratic? Watch the dollar and the long bond on the risk-off day: if both fall *with* stocks, you are in a credibility shock and ordinary diversification has failed. Fifth, *the reversibility* — is this anchored in durable law or a reversible executive order subject to courts and deals? The more reversible, the fatter the uncertainty premium, and the more violently the market will reprice on every headline. Run those five questions and you will understand a tariff's market effect better than any single number — including the tariff rate itself — could tell you.

**What would invalidate the read.** A credible, durable, legislated trade framework — one that takes the executive-order overnight-reversal risk off the table — would collapse the uncertainty channel and let the other three channels resolve in a more orderly way. The 2025 episode's defining feature was that *no such durability existed*: the tariffs were executive orders, subject to pauses, deals, and a Supreme Court challenge that ultimately struck them down. Until trade policy is anchored in something more stable than an emergency-powers statute, the uncertainty channel stays live, and the risk premium stays fat.

### The legal endgame is a market variable

It is worth dwelling on the most unusual feature of the 2025 episode, because it generalizes: **the legal basis of a policy became a tradable variable.** Normally, markets treat the legal authority for a policy as settled background — a Fed rate decision is not going to be overturned by a court. But the Liberation Day tariffs rested on IEEPA, an emergency-powers statute that had never been stretched this far, and from day one trade lawyers argued it didn't authorize broad tariffs. That meant every affected asset carried an extra, unusual contingency: *the entire tariff regime might simply be voided by the judiciary.*

This created a strange options-like structure in prices. An importer facing a 20% tariff had a non-trivial probability of getting that tariff *refunded* if the courts struck it down — so the present value of the tariff cost was the rate times the probability it survived legal challenge. As the case worked through the courts, every ruling and every oral argument moved that probability, and prices moved with it. When the Supreme Court ruled on February 20, 2026 that IEEPA does *not* authorize the tariffs, it resolved one source of uncertainty — but the administration's immediate Section 122 re-invocation (a 10% global tariff, legally cleaner but capped and temporary) replaced it with another. The lesson is durable: when a policy's legal foundation is contested, *the litigation calendar becomes a market calendar*, and the risk premium prices the probability distribution over legal outcomes, not just economic ones.

### Macroprudential and FX-intervention responses

The fourth lever in this series' framework — FX and macroprudential policy — is the *defensive* counterpart to the offensive tariff. When a tariff (or the credibility shock it triggers) sends a currency tumbling, the affected country's central bank can intervene: selling foreign-currency reserves to buy its own currency, raising rates to defend it, or imposing capital controls. Vietnam's State Bank, for instance, manages the dong within a band and leans against excessive depreciation using reserves and its policy rates; a US tariff that weakens the dong's trade position forces exactly this kind of defensive response. The interaction matters for valuation because a central bank defending its currency is *tightening* domestic financial conditions — raising rates or draining liquidity — which reprices that country's bonds and equities downward, a second-order hit layered on top of the direct tariff damage. A tariff, in other words, doesn't just tax the exporter's goods; it can force the exporter's central bank into a defensive tightening that taxes its whole asset market.

For Vietnam and other export-led economies, the stakes are even higher than for the US itself — with roughly 29% of Vietnam's exports going to the US, a 46% reciprocal rate was an existential threat to its growth model, and the negotiated 20% deal was a national-priority outcome. The arithmetic is stark: if exports are roughly 90% of Vietnam's GDP and the US takes nearly a third of them, a tariff that priced Vietnamese goods out of the US market would have threatened a double-digit share of the entire economy. That is why Vietnam moved fastest of any partner to negotiate, accepting a 20% rate (with a punitive 40% on transshipped Chinese goods routed through Vietnam to dodge the China tariffs) to preserve access. For a Vietnamese investor, the lesson is that a US executive order can reprice the VN-Index more violently than any domestic policy — the tariff channel reaches an export-led economy through its largest customer's tax decisions. We trace exactly how a US tariff decision propagates through to Vietnamese equities, the dong, and the State Bank's response in [how global policy reaches Vietnam](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/how-global-policy-reaches-vietnam-the-fed-the-dollar-and-tariffs).

## The one thing to carry

Strip everything else away and keep this. A tariff is a tax on imports — but unlike an ordinary tax, it pulls four channels at once: it raises inflation (so rates stay higher-for-longer), it can move the currency, it squeezes corporate margins, and it injects uncertainty that fattens the risk premium on everything. Most of the time these channels partly offset, and a tariff is a moderate, sector-specific drag. But when a *broad* tariff reads as a *credibility* shock, the channels stop offsetting and start compounding — and you get the rarest, most dangerous outcome in macro: stocks, bonds, and the dollar falling *together*, with only gold left as a haven. That simultaneity is what the 2025 tariff shock taught a generation of investors, and it is the single most important thing to remember about tariffs as a market force.
