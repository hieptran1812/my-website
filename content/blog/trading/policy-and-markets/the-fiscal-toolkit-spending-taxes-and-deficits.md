> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The government's fiscal levers reach asset prices through two doors: spending and tax cuts add demand that flows into corporate earnings (so equities reprice up), while the deficit those policies create becomes bond *supply* that the Treasury must sell, pushing the term premium and long yields up.
>
> - **Three levers, not one.** Spending (transfers and government purchases), taxes (income, corporate, capital gains), and the deficit (spending minus revenue) are distinct tools with distinct transmission paths. The central bank sets the *price* of money; the government decides how much it *spends, taxes, and borrows*.
> - **Demand channel → earnings → equities.** A dollar of well-targeted fiscal spending can add more than a dollar of GDP (the multiplier), and that demand shows up as corporate sales, then earnings, then a higher equity multiple. The 2017 corporate tax cut lifted S&P 500 earnings per share by roughly a fifth with no change in the underlying businesses.
> - **Supply channel → term premium → yields.** Every dollar of deficit is a dollar of new Treasury bonds someone must buy. More supply means a lower bond price and a higher yield — the "term premium." This is why the 2025 mix of *high rates plus big deficits* keeps pressure on the long end and the dollar.
> - **The one number to remember:** the One Big Beautiful Bill Act, signed July 4, 2025, was scored at **+\$3.4 trillion (CBO/JCT) to +\$4.9 trillion including interest** over ten years — a tax cut financed entirely by borrowing, colliding with a Treasury that already has to issue a wall of debt.

On July 4, 2025, the President signed the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA) into law. It made permanent the individual tax cuts from the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act, which were set to expire at the end of 2025, and it layered on new cuts — no federal tax on tips and overtime, an expanded state-and-local-tax deduction, and full immediate expensing of business investment. To a household, it read as a tax cut. To the bond market, it read as a promise that the government would borrow several trillion dollars more over the next decade than it otherwise would have.

Those two readings are the same policy. That is the puzzle this post solves. A tax cut is, simultaneously, more money in households' and companies' pockets *and* a bigger hole in the government's budget that has to be filled by selling bonds. The first effect lifts corporate earnings and tends to push equities up. The second effect adds to the supply of Treasury bonds and tends to push their yields up. The same signature does both at once.

This collision is the defining macro story of 2025 and 2026. The Treasury must issue a historically large volume of debt to fund deficits running above six percent of GDP. The Federal Reserve, having hiked rates to a two-decade high and only just begun cutting, is *not* buying that debt — it is letting its holdings run off. So the bond market has to absorb the supply on its own, at a time when the policy mix (loose fiscal, still-tight money) is exactly the combination that historically lifts long-term yields and strains the currency. Understanding fiscal policy means understanding both doors at once.

![Diagram of the fiscal toolkit showing spending and tax cuts routing to earnings and equities, and deficits routing to bond supply, term premium, and long yields](/imgs/blogs/the-fiscal-toolkit-spending-taxes-and-deficits-1.png)

This is the map for the whole post. The government pulls a lever on the left. Spending adds demand; tax cuts leave more cash in private hands; both lift corporate earnings and reprice equities upward. But spending minus revenue is a *deficit*, and the deficit becomes bond supply, which raises the term premium and pushes long yields up. Every section below is a walk down one of these two paths.

## Foundations: what fiscal policy is and who pulls the levers

Before any of the market mechanics, we need a clean definition. **Fiscal policy is the government's use of its budget — what it spends, what it taxes, and how much it borrows — to influence the economy.** It is the deliberate, political counterpart to monetary policy. If you have read [the central bank toolkit](/blog/trading/macro-trading/central-bank-toolkit-rates-qe-qt-forward-guidance), you already know the monetary side: the Federal Reserve sets the short-term interest rate and manages the size of its balance sheet. Fiscal policy is the *other* hand. It is run by the elected government — in the United States, by Congress (which writes the spending and tax laws) and the Treasury (which collects the taxes, cuts the checks, and borrows the difference).

The cleanest way to keep the two apart is by what each one *controls*. The central bank controls the **price of money** — the interest rate, and through it the discount rate that every asset is valued against. The government controls the **quantity of money it puts into and takes out of the economy** — how many dollars of spending flow out, how many dollars of taxes are pulled back, and how big the gap between them is. The central bank works on the cost of capital; the government works on the flow of cash through the real economy.

Think of the national economy as a giant household budget, only the household is the federal government. Money comes in (taxes); money goes out (spending). When out exceeds in, the household runs a **deficit** for the year and must borrow to cover the shortfall. The pile of borrowing accumulated over all the years is the **debt**. A family that overspends puts the gap on a credit card; the U.S. government puts the gap on Treasury bonds, which are simply IOUs the government sells to investors, promising to pay them back with interest. That single act — selling bonds to cover the gap — is where fiscal policy meets the bond market, and it is the engine behind the entire "supply channel" we will spend the second half of this post on.

### The three levers, defined

**Lever one: spending.** Government spending splits into two very different buckets. **Discretionary spending** is the part Congress votes on every year — defense, infrastructure, research, the agencies. **Mandatory spending** is set by existing law and runs on autopilot — Social Security, Medicare, Medicaid, and interest on the debt. Mandatory spending is roughly two-thirds of the federal budget and growing, which matters because the part of the budget that policymakers can actually *adjust* in a downturn (the discretionary part) is the smaller slice. Within spending, **transfers** are a special category: payments the government makes to people without buying anything in return — unemployment benefits, stimulus checks, Social Security. Transfers do not directly add to GDP the way a government purchase of a bridge does; they add to GDP only when the recipient *spends* the money.

**Lever two: taxes.** Taxes are the government pulling money *out* of the private economy. The major federal taxes are the **individual income tax** (on wages and salaries, the largest single source of federal revenue), the **corporate income tax** (on company profits), the **payroll tax** (funding Social Security and Medicare), and the **capital gains tax** (on profits from selling assets like stocks and property). Each tax has two effects we will separate carefully later: a **demand-side effect** (a tax cut leaves people with more money to spend, lifting demand) and a **supply-side effect** (a tax cut changes the incentive to work, invest, or hire, potentially lifting the economy's productive capacity). The political fight over tax policy is almost always a fight over which of these two effects dominates.

**Lever three: the deficit.** The deficit is not really a separate lever — it is the *arithmetic consequence* of the first two. Spend more than you tax, and you run a deficit. But the deficit deserves its own line because it is the part that touches the bond market directly. The deficit must be *financed*, and financing means the Treasury sells bonds. The change in the deficit from one year to the next — whether fiscal policy is adding stimulus or pulling it back — is called the **fiscal impulse**, and it is one of the most-watched numbers in macro.

### The anatomy of the budget: where the dollars actually go

It helps to put rough numbers on the buckets, because the *composition* of the budget constrains what fiscal policy can do. Federal spending runs around \$6.5-7 trillion a year. Of that, roughly **two-thirds is mandatory** — Social Security (~\$1.4 trillion), Medicare and Medicaid (~\$1.8 trillion combined), and *interest on the debt* (now over \$1 trillion and climbing). Only about a third is **discretionary**, split roughly evenly between defense and everything-else-non-defense. On the revenue side, federal receipts run around \$4.9-5 trillion: the individual income tax is the largest piece (~50% of revenue), payroll taxes are next (~35%), and the corporate income tax is a surprisingly small slice (~10%). The gap between the ~\$6.5T out and ~\$4.9T in is the deficit — roughly \$1.8 trillion a year before OBBBA's additions.

Two facts fall out of this anatomy, and both matter for markets. First, **the fastest-growing line item is now interest** — a direct consequence of the supply channel feeding back on itself, which we develop later. Second, the part of the budget Congress can actually *adjust* year to year (discretionary spending) is the *smaller* part, and it is shrinking as a share of the total. This is why so much fiscal action happens on the *tax* side rather than the spending side: changing the rules of a giant, automatic tax base is the highest-leverage lever a government has. A one-percentage-point change in the effective income-tax rate, applied across a multi-trillion-dollar wage base for ten years, is itself a trillion-dollar decision. That leverage is exactly why OBBBA's permanent extension of the 2017 individual cuts carries a multi-trillion-dollar price tag.

### Automatic stabilizers: fiscal policy on autopilot

One more foundational idea. Not all fiscal policy is a deliberate vote. Parts of the budget move *automatically* with the economy in a way that smooths the cycle. In a recession, tax revenue falls (people earn less, so they owe less) and spending on benefits rises (more people claim unemployment). Both push the deficit wider *without any new law* — and both put money into the economy exactly when it is weak. In a boom, the reverse happens: revenue surges and benefit spending falls, narrowing the deficit and cooling things off. These are **automatic stabilizers**, and they are why the deficit naturally balloons in every recession (2020's 14.7% of GDP) and shrinks in expansions, even before Congress lifts a finger. When you read a deficit number, part of it is policy and part of it is just the economy breathing.

## The demand channel: from spending to corporate earnings

Now the first transmission path. Start with the simplest case: the government spends a dollar. Where does it go, and how does it reach an equity price?

When the government buys something — pays a contractor to build a road, sends a benefit check to a household, funds a research grant — that dollar becomes someone's income. The contractor pays wages; the worker buys groceries; the grocer restocks and hires. The original dollar of government spending circulates, becoming income again and again as it passes from hand to hand. The total increase in GDP from one dollar of government spending is therefore *more* than a dollar — that ratio is the **fiscal multiplier**.

#### Worked example: the fiscal multiplier on GDP

Suppose the government spends an extra \$100 billion on infrastructure, and households spend 70 cents of every additional dollar of income they receive (economists call this the *marginal propensity to consume*, here 0.7). The first round adds \$100 billion to GDP. The workers paid by that spending then spend \$70 billion (70% of \$100B), which becomes someone else's income. That someone spends 70% of \$70 billion, or \$49 billion. And so on. The geometric series sums to:

\$100B × 1 / (1 − 0.7) = \$100B × 3.33 = **\$333 billion** of total GDP.

So the multiplier in this stylized case is **3.33** — every fiscal dollar produces \$3.33 of GDP. Real-world multipliers are much smaller (more on that below), but the mechanism is exactly this: government spending does not just add itself to the economy; it *cascades*, because one person's spending is the next person's income.

In practice, multipliers are nowhere near 3.33 — money leaks out at every round into saving, imports, and taxes, and the Federal Reserve may offset fiscal stimulus by tightening. The Congressional Budget Office and the academic literature put realistic multipliers in the range of roughly 0.3 to 1.7, and — crucially — the size depends almost entirely on *where* the dollar lands.

![Horizontal bar chart of fiscal multipliers by policy type, with transfers to low income highest and corporate tax cuts lowest](/imgs/blogs/the-fiscal-toolkit-spending-taxes-and-deficits-6.png)

The pattern is consistent across studies. A dollar handed to a low-income household — which spends nearly all of it because it is cash-constrained — has a multiplier above 1.0. A dollar of government purchases (a road, equipment) is similar, because the whole dollar is spent by definition. But a dollar of tax cut aimed at high-income households or corporations multiplies *less*, often well below 1.0, because the recipients save or invest a large share rather than spend it immediately. **The same number of fiscal dollars can produce very different amounts of demand depending on who receives them.** This single fact explains why a stimulus check to households (2020-2021) jolts demand fast, while a corporate tax cut (2017) works through a slower channel — earnings and investment — and shows up more in asset prices than in immediate consumption.

![Comparison matrix of fiscal levers showing who receives the money first, the demand multiplier, and the asset most affected](/imgs/blogs/the-fiscal-toolkit-spending-taxes-and-deficits-8.png)

This matrix is the demand channel in one frame. Read across each row: a *transfer* to low-income households reaches the cash-constrained first, multiplies high, and shows up in consumer stocks. *Government purchases* flow to contractors and states, multiply moderately-to-high, and lift industrials and materials. An *income tax cut* spreads thinly across all wage earners with a medium multiplier and a broad consumer effect. A *corporate tax cut* lands directly on shareholders, multiplies low into the real economy, but reprices equities the most — because it flows straight to earnings and buybacks. The lever you pull decides both how much GDP you get and which assets move.

### From earnings to the equity price

Here is the link to asset value. A stock is worth the present value of the cash flows the company will pay its owners over time. The two biggest drivers of that value are how *much* the company earns and what *multiple* the market puts on those earnings. Fiscal demand works on the first. When government spending lifts overall demand, companies sell more, which (with fixed costs spread over more units) lifts profits *more than proportionally* — operating leverage. Higher profits, the same multiple, mean a higher stock price. This is why the equity market cares intensely about the fiscal impulse: a widening deficit driven by spending is, all else equal, a tailwind to corporate earnings and therefore to the index.

The operating-leverage point is worth a number, because it is why fiscal demand hits earnings harder than it hits sales. Take a company with \$100 of sales, \$70 of fixed costs, and \$20 of variable costs, leaving \$10 of profit. Now fiscal demand lifts its sales 10%, to \$110. Variable costs rise with sales (to \$22), but fixed costs stay at \$70. Profit becomes \$110 − \$70 − \$22 = **\$18** — a *jump of 80%* from a *10% rise in sales*. That asymmetry is operating leverage, and it is why a fiscal demand impulse that nudges nominal GDP up a few percent can swing index earnings by double digits. The effect is strongest for high-fixed-cost, cyclical businesses (industrials, materials, autos) and weakest for asset-light services — which is why fiscal-stimulus rallies are led by cyclicals, not defensives.

Crucially, fiscal works on the *numerator* of the valuation (earnings) while monetary works on the *denominator* (the discount rate the market applies to those earnings). A fiscal expansion that lifts earnings 10% is unambiguously good for the stock *if* the discount rate is unchanged — but if the same expansion's bond-supply channel raises long yields and therefore the discount rate, the two effects fight. We connect this to the discount-rate side in [how monetary policy moves stocks](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-monetary-policy-moves-stocks-discount-rates-sectors) — keeping the numerator and denominator straight is the whole trick to reading a fiscal package's net effect on equities.

## The tax channel in detail: income, corporate, and capital gains

Taxes deserve their own deep section, because the tax thread is where the most current and most consequential policy fights live. A tax change is the government adjusting the *wedge* between what a worker earns and what they take home, between what a company earns and what it keeps, and between an investment's gross and net return. Each of the three major taxes works differently.

### The individual income tax: the demand-side lever

The income tax is the biggest single source of federal revenue, so a cut to it is the most direct demand-side lever the government has. Lower the rates or widen the brackets, and every wage earner keeps more of each paycheck. The demand effect is exactly the multiplier story above: more take-home pay, more spending, more corporate sales. But the *distribution* matters enormously. A flat dollar of income-tax cut spread across all earners has a medium multiplier (some is saved). A cut concentrated on lower earners spends faster; a cut concentrated on the highest earners is largely saved or invested, so its demand multiplier is small even though its dollar cost is large.

The 2017 TCJA cut individual rates across the board and nearly doubled the standard deduction. Those individual provisions were *temporary* — scheduled to expire at the end of 2025 — which set up the entire OBBBA fight. Making them permanent (which OBBBA did) is a multi-trillion-dollar decision precisely because the income tax is so large: small rate changes, multiplied across the whole wage base over ten years, add up to trillions.

One subtlety trips up almost everyone: the difference between the **marginal rate** and the **effective rate**. The marginal rate is the tax on your *next* dollar of income — the headline bracket number. The effective rate is the tax on your *whole* income, which is always lower because the U.S. income tax is *progressive*: the first slice of income is taxed at a low rate, and only income above each threshold is taxed at the higher bracket. When a politician says "the top rate is 37%," that does not mean a top earner pays 37% of everything — they pay 10% on the first slice, 12% on the next, and so on, with 37% applying only to income above the top threshold. This matters for markets because the *demand* effect of a tax cut depends on the *effective* rate change (how much more cash people actually keep), while the *incentive* effect depends on the *marginal* rate (the tax on the next dollar of effort). A cut that lowers marginal rates a lot but effective rates only a little has a big incentive story and a small demand story — and vice versa.

There is also a fourth major tax that gets less attention but is enormous: the **payroll tax**, the flat levy on wages (split between employer and employee) that funds Social Security and Medicare. It raises about a third of all federal revenue — nearly as much as the income tax — and because it is flat and applies from the first dollar of wages, it is the most *regressive* major federal tax (it takes a larger share of low incomes). A payroll-tax cut, like the temporary one in 2011-2012, is therefore an unusually high-multiplier demand lever: it puts cash directly into every worker's paycheck, weighted toward the cash-constrained who spend it fast. Cutting it is rare because it directly drains the Social Security trust fund, but when it happens, it is one of the purest demand jolts in the fiscal toolkit.

### The corporate income tax: the supply-side and asset-price lever

The corporate tax is where the asset-price effect is most direct and most measurable. A company's earnings per share — the number equity investors value — is its *after-tax* profit divided by its shares. Cut the corporate tax rate, and after-tax profit rises mechanically, with no change to the underlying business at all. This is the cleanest fiscal-to-equity link there is.

![Before-and-after comparison showing a corporate tax cut from 35 percent to 21 percent lifting after-tax EPS from sixty-five to seventy-nine dollars](/imgs/blogs/the-fiscal-toolkit-spending-taxes-and-deficits-3.png)

#### Worked example: a corporate tax cut lifting S&P EPS

Take a company earning \$100 of pre-tax income per share. Under the old 35% corporate rate, it paid \$35 in tax and kept **\$65** of after-tax earnings per share. The 2017 TCJA cut the statutory rate to 21%. Now the same \$100 of pre-tax income is taxed at 21%, leaving **\$79** of after-tax EPS.

The lift: (79 − 65) / 65 = **+21.5%**.

In reality, the effective tax rate companies actually pay is lower than the statutory rate (thanks to deductions and credits), so the realized EPS bump for the S&P 500 was smaller — analysts estimated roughly a **+8% to +10%** one-time boost to index earnings in 2018. But the direction and the mechanism are exactly this: a rate cut hands equity holders a step-up in earnings for doing nothing differently. That is why the equity market rallied into the TCJA's passage and why corporate tax policy is the single most watched fiscal lever for stock investors.

The corporate tax cut also has a *supply-side* argument attached: lower taxes on profit raise the after-tax return on investment, so companies should invest more, expanding the economy's capacity over time. The evidence here is mixed and slower-moving — the immediate, certain effect is the EPS step-up; the longer-run investment effect is real but smaller than its advocates claim and harder to isolate. OBBBA's "100% bonus depreciation" provision — letting companies immediately deduct the full cost of new equipment — is a direct attempt to juice that investment channel, front-loading the tax benefit of capital spending.

The investment-incentive logic is worth making precise, because it is the supply-side argument's strongest form. A company decides whether to undertake a project by comparing its *after-tax* return to its cost of capital. Cutting the corporate rate raises the after-tax return on *every* project, so projects that were marginal-rejects become marginal-accepts.

#### Worked example: a tax cut and the hurdle for investment

Suppose a company is weighing a project that returns 9% pre-tax, and its cost of capital is 7%. Under a 35% tax rate, the after-tax return is 9% × (1 − 0.35) = **5.85%** — *below* the 7% hurdle, so the project is rejected. Cut the rate to 21%, and the after-tax return becomes 9% × (1 − 0.21) = **7.11%** — now *above* the 7% hurdle, so the project goes ahead.

The same project, the same business, flips from rejected to approved purely because the government took a smaller share of its return. Multiply that across thousands of marginal projects and you have the supply-side investment channel. The catch is that most corporate cash from the 2017 cut went to *buybacks and dividends* rather than new projects, because the binding constraint was not the tax rate but the lack of attractive projects — you cannot tax-cut a company into investing if it has nothing profitable to build. That is why the realized investment response was modest: the channel is real but it only bites on firms that *have* good projects sitting just below the hurdle.

There is also a ceiling on the "cut taxes to raise revenue" argument, captured by the **Laffer curve**: at a 0% rate the government collects nothing, and at a 100% rate it also collects nothing (no one works or reports income), so somewhere in between is a revenue-maximizing rate. Cutting *from above* that peak can raise revenue; cutting *from below* it loses revenue. The empirical consensus is that U.S. rates have been well *below* the peak, which is why tax cuts here lose revenue rather than pay for themselves — the "self-financing" claim only holds on the far right side of the curve, where the U.S. does not sit.

### The capital gains tax: the asset-holder's lever

The third tax touches investors most directly. **Capital gains tax** is owed on the profit when you *sell* an asset for more than you paid. Two features make it special. First, it is only owed on *realization* — you can defer it indefinitely by simply not selling, which is why a capital gains rate cut tends to trigger a wave of selling (people realize gains they had been sitting on) and a rate *hike* tends to trigger selling *ahead* of the increase. Second, because it applies to the after-tax return on capital, the capital gains rate feeds directly into the fair value of every asset.

#### Worked example: a tax change and a stock's fair value

Consider a stock you expect to pay you cash flows worth a gross return of 8% a year. If the tax on those returns is 20%, your after-tax return is 8% × (1 − 0.20) = **6.4%**. Now suppose the capital gains rate falls to 15%. Your after-tax return rises to 8% × (1 − 0.15) = **6.8%**.

To see the valuation effect, flip it around. If investors require a 6.4% after-tax return and the stock pays \$6.40 of after-tax cash per \$100 of price, the stock is fairly priced at \$100. Cut the tax so the *same* gross cash flow now yields \$6.80 after tax, and investors — still content with a 6.4% after-tax return — will bid the price up until \$6.80 / price = 6.4%, i.e. price = \$106.25.

A 5-percentage-point cut in the capital gains rate raised the asset's fair value by roughly **6%** in this stylized case, purely by leaving more of each dollar of return in the investor's hands. Taxes are not just a cost on the income statement; they are a term in the valuation equation itself.

## The supply channel: from deficits to bond yields

Now the second door — the one most people miss. We have followed the demand channel from spending and tax cuts through to earnings and equities. But every dollar of those tax cuts and spending increases that is *not* covered by revenue creates a deficit, and the deficit has to be financed. This is where fiscal policy reaches the bond market, and it is the channel that dominates the 2025-2026 story.

### Why a deficit is bond supply

Recall the household analogy: spend more than you take in, and you borrow the difference. The government borrows by having the Treasury auction bonds — Treasury bills (under a year), notes (2 to 10 years), and bonds (20 to 30 years). When the government runs a \$2 trillion deficit, the Treasury must sell roughly \$2 trillion of *new* net debt that year (on top of rolling over the maturing old debt). Someone — pension funds, foreign central banks, banks, households, the Federal Reserve — must buy every dollar of it.

The mechanics matter more than people realize. The Treasury sells debt through regular **auctions**, and it chooses *where on the curve* to issue — more bills (short) or more notes and bonds (long). That choice, set by the Treasury's quarterly refunding announcements, is itself a market-moving lever: shifting issuance toward the long end floods the market with duration and steepens the curve, while leaning on bills concentrates supply at the front. In 2023-2024, the Treasury deliberately tilted toward bills to avoid overwhelming the long end — a move some called "stealth stimulus" because it kept long-term yields lower than the deficit alone would imply. The *gross* issuance number is even larger than the deficit, because every maturing bond must be *refinanced* (rolled over) on top of funding the new gap. With roughly a third of the \$28 trillion-plus public debt maturing within a year, the Treasury is constantly auctioning trillions just to stand still — and refinancing maturing low-rate debt at today's higher rates is itself a force widening the interest bill.

The *buyer mix* is the other half of the supply story. Historically, three big blocs absorbed Treasury supply: the **Federal Reserve** (via QE), **foreign official buyers** (central banks parking reserves, led by China and Japan), and **domestic price-sensitive investors** (banks, pensions, funds, households). The first two have stepped back. The Fed is in QT, shrinking its holdings rather than adding. Foreign official demand has been flat-to-falling as reserve managers diversify away from the dollar and as some, like China, run down their Treasury holdings. That leaves *price-sensitive* domestic buyers — hedge funds, money managers, households — to absorb the marginal bond. And price-sensitive buyers, by definition, will only take more bonds at a *better price* — a higher yield. When the inelastic official buyers retreat and the elastic private buyers are the marginal demand, every extra dollar of supply has to clear at a higher yield. This is the structural reason the term premium has re-emerged in the 2020s after a decade near zero.

![Pipeline diagram showing a deficit becoming Treasury issuance, then bond supply buyers must absorb, then a falling price, rising term premium, and higher long yields](/imgs/blogs/the-fiscal-toolkit-spending-taxes-and-deficits-5.png)

This is the supply channel in one line. A bond's price and its yield move *inversely* — that is the most important relationship in fixed income, and we build it from scratch in the [fixed-income foundations](/blog/trading/fixed-income/why-bond-prices-and-yields-move-in-opposite-directions). When the Treasury floods the market with new bonds, basic supply-and-demand applies: to get buyers to absorb a larger pile, the *price* of bonds has to fall, which means their *yield* has to rise. The extra yield that investors demand to hold the increased supply of long-dated bonds — over and above what short rates would imply — is the **term premium**. It is the compensation for the risk of holding a long bond when the supply keeps growing.

#### Worked example: a deficit's bond supply and the term premium

Suppose the deficit widens by \$2 trillion in a year, all of it financed by new Treasury issuance. Empirical estimates from Federal Reserve staff and academic studies cluster around **3 to 4 basis points of extra 10-year term premium per \$100 billion of net supply** the market has to absorb (a basis point is one-hundredth of a percentage point).

Using the middle of that range, ~3.5 bp per \$100B:

\$2,000B / \$100B × 3.5 bp = 20 × 3.5 = **+70 basis points** of term premium.

That is 0.70 percentage points of extra 10-year yield from the supply alone — enough to move a 4.0% yield toward 4.7%, with everything else (inflation, the Fed's path) held constant. Of course these elasticities are uncertain and the effect is partly anticipatory (the market prices expected future deficits, not just this year's), but the direction is unambiguous: **a bigger deficit is a heavier supply of bonds, and a heavier supply needs a higher yield to clear.**

![Horizontal bar chart of implied ten-year term premium added by different deficit sizes, rising from eighteen to eighty-eight basis points](/imgs/blogs/the-fiscal-toolkit-spending-taxes-and-deficits-4.png)

The sensitivity scales roughly linearly: a modest \$0.5 trillion deficit implies a small term-premium add, while a 2025-class \$2.5 trillion of net issuance implies close to a full percentage point of extra term premium. This is the arithmetic behind every headline worrying about "the bond market having to digest the deficit." The macro-trading desk's version of this — how to position for it — lives in [deficits, debt, and bond supply](/blog/trading/macro-trading/deficits-debt-bond-supply-why-issuance-moves-yields); here we own the *valuation mechanism* itself.

### Crowding out versus crowding in

The supply channel raises a classic question: does government borrowing *crowd out* private investment? The argument goes like this. There is a finite pool of savings. When the government borrows heavily, it competes for those savings, bidding up interest rates, which makes it more expensive for companies and households to borrow — so private investment falls, "crowded out" by the public borrowing. Higher yields from heavy issuance are the mechanism: the same rate rise that compensates bondholders for the supply also raises the cost of every corporate loan and mortgage.

The counterargument is **crowding in**. In a depressed economy with idle resources — high unemployment, factories running below capacity — government spending does not compete for scarce savings; it puts idle resources to work, raising incomes and *generating* new savings. In that environment, fiscal stimulus can *raise* private investment by lifting demand and business confidence. The crowding-out worry is strongest when the economy is already at full employment (so the government is competing for genuinely scarce resources), and weakest in a deep recession (when resources are idle). This is why the *same* deficit can be benign in 2020 (deep slump, crowding in) and worrying in 2025 (full employment, crowding out) — the context decides which force dominates.

### The debt service spiral

There is a second-order effect that makes the 2025 mix especially uncomfortable. Interest on the debt is itself *mandatory spending*. When yields rise — whether from the Fed's rate hikes or from the supply pressure we just described — the government's interest bill rises, which *widens* the deficit, which means *more* issuance, which can push yields *higher* still. At low debt levels this loop is harmless; at 100% of GDP and rising, with rates back to two-decade highs, interest costs have become one of the fastest-growing lines in the entire federal budget. The deficit and the yield are no longer independent — they feed each other.

The arithmetic of the spiral is sobering. With public debt over \$28 trillion, every 1-percentage-point rise in the *average* interest rate the government pays — phased in as old low-rate debt matures and is refinanced at higher rates — eventually adds on the order of \$280 billion a year to the interest bill. That \$280 billion is pure deficit, requiring \$280 billion more issuance, which (via the supply elasticity) nudges yields up a touch more. The loop is slow — it takes years for the average rate to fully reset as debt rolls over — but it is self-reinforcing in a way that did not exist when debt was 40% of GDP and rates were near zero. Interest costs have already overtaken defense spending in the budget. The danger is not a sudden crisis but a *quiet ratchet*: each year, a larger share of the budget goes to servicing the debt rather than to anything productive, and the deficit gets harder to close. This is the mechanism behind the term "fiscal dominance" — the point at which the debt load constrains what monetary policy can do, because higher rates feed straight back into a bigger deficit.

![Dual-axis chart of US federal deficit as percent of GDP in bars and debt held by the public as percent of GDP as a line, both elevated through 2026](/imgs/blogs/the-fiscal-toolkit-spending-taxes-and-deficits-2.png)

The picture is stark. The deficit spiked to 14.7% of GDP in the 2020 pandemic, then — unusually — never normalized the way it did after past recessions. It has settled in the 6%+ range *during a full-employment expansion*, a peacetime, non-recession deficit with few precedents. Meanwhile debt held by the public has climbed past 100% of GDP and is still rising. This is the "loose fiscal" half of the 2023-2025 policy mix: wide deficits funding a steady wall of new Treasury supply, regardless of the cycle.

## The fiscal impulse and the policy mix

To trade or invest on fiscal policy you need two summary concepts. The first is the **fiscal impulse** — the *change* in the structural deficit from one year to the next, stripped of the automatic-stabilizer effect of the cycle. A widening structural deficit is *stimulus being added*; a narrowing one is stimulus being withdrawn (called *fiscal drag*). The level of the deficit tells you how much support is in place; the impulse tells you the *direction* of the push. A deficit can be large and yet *shrinking* — that is restrictive at the margin even though the level looks loose.

The second concept is the **policy mix** — the combination of the fiscal stance and the monetary stance. There are four quadrants, and each has a signature for asset prices:

- **Easy money + easy fiscal** (2020-2021): both pedals down. Reflation, risk-on; stocks, credit, crypto, and gold all rally. The "everything rally."
- **Easy money + tight fiscal** (the 2010s): the central bank does the heavy lifting via QE while the government pulls back. A financial-asset boom on a weak-demand base — great for stocks, soft for wages.
- **Tight money + easy fiscal** (2023-2025, *today*): the dangerous quadrant for bonds. High rates *and* big deficits. The Fed is restraining demand while the Treasury floods the market with supply — sticky long yields, a term premium that will not compress, and a currency that is strong on rate differentials but vulnerable on credibility.
- **Tight money + tight fiscal** (Volcker, early 1980s): both brakes on. Disinflation or recession; favor defensives and duration.

The current quadrant is the whole point of the OBBBA story. A large deficit-financed tax cut is *easy fiscal*, layered on top of a Fed that is still restrictive. That combination pushes the long end up through the supply channel even as it supports equities through the demand channel — exactly the split-screen we opened with.

The split-screen is not just rhetorical; it nets out to a number, and the number can go either way.

#### Worked example: the net equity effect of a deficit-financed tax cut

A simple stock-valuation rule says price ≈ earnings ÷ (discount rate − growth). Start with a stock earning \$5, a discount rate of 8%, and a growth rate of 3%: price = \$5 / (0.08 − 0.03) = **\$100**.

Now apply a deficit-financed corporate tax cut. The *demand channel* lifts earnings 8%, to \$5.40. The *supply channel* raises long yields 0.5 percentage points, lifting the discount rate to 8.5%. New price = \$5.40 / (0.085 − 0.03) = \$5.40 / 0.055 = **\$98.18**.

Earnings up 8%, and yet the stock is *down* ~2%, because the higher discount rate more than offset the earnings lift. Flip the inputs — a smaller yield move of 0.2 points instead of 0.5 — and the same earnings lift makes the stock *rise* to \$5.40 / 0.052 = \$103.85. **The net sign of a deficit-financed tax cut on equities is genuinely indeterminate**; it depends on how hard the bond-supply channel pushes yields relative to how much the demand channel lifts earnings. That indeterminacy is why "the market loves tax cuts" is too glib — the market loves the earnings, fears the yields, and the winner is decided in the bond market, not the stock market.

## Common misconceptions

**"Government spending always crowds out private investment."** Only when resources are scarce. In a slump with idle capacity, fiscal spending crowds *in* — it puts unemployed workers and idle factories to work, raising incomes and savings. The 2020-2021 stimulus did not raise yields meaningfully at first because the economy was deeply slack; the problem came later, when demand recovered and the deficits *stayed* wide into a full-employment economy.

**"A tax cut pays for itself through faster growth."** Almost never fully. For a tax cut to pay for itself, the growth it sparks must generate enough new revenue to replace what was cut — which requires implausibly large growth effects. The empirical record on the 2017 TCJA is that it added meaningfully to the deficit; growth offset only a fraction of the cost. OBBBA was scored at +\$3.4 trillion *net of* any growth feedback the official scorekeepers credited. Tax cuts can be good policy for other reasons, but "they pay for themselves" is not supported by the evidence.

**"Deficits don't matter because we can always print money."** This conflates two institutions. The Treasury borrows; only the central bank can create reserves. When the central bank is buying the debt (QE), supply pressure on yields is muted — but when the central bank is *tightening* (QT, as in 2022-2025), the market absorbs the supply on its own, and the term premium does the adjusting. The 2025 mix is precisely the case where "we can print" is *not* operative: the Fed is shrinking its balance sheet, not buying the deficit. Supply matters most exactly when the central bank steps back.

**"The stock market loves any tax cut."** Equities love a *corporate* tax cut, because it flows straight to EPS. But a deficit-financed cut that pushes long yields up is a *headwind* to the equity multiple (higher discount rate) even as it lifts earnings. The net effect depends on which force wins — earnings up versus discount rate up. A corporate cut that raises EPS 8% but lifts the 10-year yield 50 basis points can be a wash for the index even as it reshuffles which sectors win.

**"More debt means a default is coming."** A government that borrows in its *own* currency does not face the same default risk as a household or a company — it can always create the currency to pay. The real risk is not default but *inflation and currency debasement*: financing deficits with newly created money, or losing the confidence of bond buyers, shows up as a weaker currency and higher inflation, not a missed payment. The 2022 UK gilt crisis (below) is the cautionary tale — markets disciplined a G7 government not by forcing a default but by repricing its bonds and currency in days.

## Case studies: TCJA 2017, CARES 2020, and OBBBA 2025

Three real, dated fiscal episodes show the demand channel, the supply channel, and the collision of both.

### Case 1: The 2017 TCJA — a corporate tax cut and the EPS boom

The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act, signed in December 2017, cut the **corporate income tax rate from 35% to 21%** — its centerpiece. As the EPS worked example showed, that rate cut handed S&P 500 companies an immediate, mechanical lift to after-tax earnings, estimated at roughly **8-10%** for the index in 2018. The market had front-run it: equities rallied through late 2017 in anticipation of the cut flowing to earnings.

The TCJA also included a one-time tax break to encourage companies to bring home — *repatriate* — the roughly \$2.5 trillion in profits they had parked overseas. The law taxed those foreign profits once at a reduced rate and then exempted future foreign earnings, removing the incentive to keep cash abroad. The result was a wave of repatriation, much of which funded a record **share buyback boom** in 2018 — companies used the returning cash to buy back their own stock, which mechanically raised EPS further (fewer shares dividing the same earnings) and supported the equity market. The buyback channel was the most visible use of the windfall; investment rose too, but more modestly than advocates had projected. The deeper legal and corporate-finance mechanics of the repatriation trade are covered in [the 2017 TCJA and the repatriation trade](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-2017-tcja-and-the-repatriation-trade).

The fiscal cost: the TCJA was scored at roughly **\$1.5 trillion over ten years** on a static basis. This is the demand/asset channel in its purest form — a corporate tax cut that lifted earnings and equity prices directly, financed by a wider deficit whose bond-supply consequences were muted at the time because yields were still historically low and the economy had room.

### Case 2: The CARES Act 2020 — transfers and the demand surge

The Coronavirus Aid, Relief, and Economic Security (CARES) Act, signed in March 2020, was a **roughly \$2.2 trillion** package — the largest single fiscal action in U.S. history at the time — and total COVID-era fiscal support across 2020-2021 reached an estimated **\$5.0 to \$5.5 trillion**. Crucially, this was *transfer*-heavy: direct stimulus checks to households, vastly expanded unemployment benefits, and forgivable loans to small businesses (the PPP). Recall the multiplier matrix — transfers to cash-constrained households spend fast. That is exactly what happened.

#### Worked example: the CARES transfer and the demand jolt

The CARES Act sent direct payments of \$1,200 per adult (plus \$500 per child) to most households, alongside an extra \$600/week of unemployment benefit. Consider \$2.2 trillion of fiscal support landing on an economy where the cash-constrained share spends most of it quickly — assume an effective average multiplier of ~1.0 for the transfer-heavy package:

\$2.2T × 1.0 ≈ **\$2.2 trillion** of demand pumped into a roughly \$21 trillion economy — over 10% of GDP — in a matter of months.

The demand surge was so large, and supply chains so constrained, that it contributed to the 2021-2022 inflation spike. But for asset prices the immediate effect was a *boom*: combined with the Fed's zero-rate-plus-unlimited-QE response, the everything rally took the S&P 500 from its March 2020 low to a double by 2021. The CARES Act is the demand channel at full throttle — transfers landing on cash-constrained households, multiplying through the economy, lifting corporate sales and earnings, and (with the monetary bazooka alongside) repricing every risk asset up. The trader's playbook for this fiscal-spending impulse is in [fiscal policy for traders](/blog/trading/macro-trading/fiscal-policy-for-traders-spending-deficits-demand).

Notably, the bond-supply consequence of CARES was *muted in real time* — long yields fell, not rose, through 2020 — because the Fed was buying the debt (unlimited QE) and the economy was deeply slack (crowding in, not out). This is the cleanest possible illustration of the misconception above: supply pressure on yields appears only when the central bank steps back. It did, eventually, and the bill came due in 2022.

### Case 3: OBBBA 2025 — the deficit math and the bond-supply collision

Which brings us to the present. The One Big Beautiful Bill Act, signed July 4, 2025, is the collision of both channels in a single law. On the demand/equity side, it is a large tax cut: it permanently extended the 2017 TCJA individual brackets and the doubled standard deduction (which were about to expire), and added new cuts — no federal tax on tips and overtime, an expanded SALT deduction, and 100% bonus depreciation for business investment. Those provisions support household after-tax income and corporate earnings, the demand channel.

But the supply side is the headline. OBBBA was scored at **+\$3.4 trillion (CBO/JCT)** added to the deficit over 2025-2034, rising to **+\$4.9 trillion including the extra interest** on the additional borrowing. The gross revenue reduction was around **\$4.5 trillion** before any spending offsets.

![Bar chart of OBBBA's ten-year fiscal cost showing a gross revenue cut of four point five trillion, a net deficit add of three point four trillion, and four point nine trillion including interest](/imgs/blogs/the-fiscal-toolkit-spending-taxes-and-deficits-7.png)

#### Worked example: OBBBA's added supply and the term premium

Spread \$3.4 trillion of additional deficit over ten years and it is roughly **\$340 billion of extra net Treasury supply per year** — on top of a baseline deficit already running above \$1.8 trillion. Using the supply elasticity from earlier (~3.5 bp of term premium per \$100B of net supply), the *incremental* OBBBA supply alone implies:

\$340B / \$100B × 3.5 bp = 3.4 × 3.5 ≈ **+12 basis points** of extra 10-year term premium per year of issuance, layered on top of the term premium the baseline deficit already commands.

Now stack the context. The total deficit is funding **\$2 trillion-plus of net issuance a year**, the Fed is in QT (shrinking its balance sheet, *not* buying the debt), and short rates, though falling, are still elevated — the Fed cut three times in late 2025 to a 3.50-3.75% target range but remains restrictive. That is the **tight money + easy fiscal** quadrant in its purest form. The market must absorb the supply on its own, and the term premium is the price of absorption. The 10-year yield, which sat near 4.6% in early 2025, has stayed sticky in the 4.0-4.5% range *despite* Fed rate cuts — the cuts pull the front end down, but the supply keeps the long end up. The curve steepens; the long end refuses to follow the Fed down.

And the dollar tells the credibility side of the story. The DXY dollar index fell more than 10% over the first half of 2025 — its worst first half since 1973 — as foreign investors questioned whether they wanted to keep funding ever-wider U.S. deficits at the prevailing yields. A loose-fiscal, tight-money mix can keep a currency strong on rate differentials *or* weak on credibility concerns; in 2025 the credibility worry won, at least for a while. This is the OBBBA collision in full: equities supported by the earnings channel, long yields pinned up by the supply channel, and the dollar caught in between. The legal and policy backdrop to the 2025 tax fight is in [tax law as a market force](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/tax-law-as-a-market-force).

### A counter-case: the 2022 UK gilt crisis

For the supply channel's tail risk, look across the Atlantic. In September 2022, the UK government announced an unfunded package of roughly **£45 billion** in tax cuts with no plan to pay for them. The bond market's reaction was violent: 30-year gilt yields jumped about **130 basis points** in days, the pound fell to an all-time low near \$1.035, and a forced-selling doom-loop in pension funds (which had borrowed against their gilt holdings) threatened the financial system. The Bank of England had to intervene with £65 billion of emergency gilt-buying, and the Prime Minister resigned after 49 days.

The lesson is the supply channel without a credible plan: markets will discipline even a G7 government when fiscal policy collides with the bond market's willingness to fund it. It is the cautionary backstop to every "deficits don't matter" argument — they matter exactly when the buyers say so, and they can say so in days.

## What it means for asset values

Pull the two channels together into a playbook. Fiscal policy reaches assets through demand (numerator: earnings) and supply (denominator and term premium: yields), and the net effect on any asset depends on which channel dominates for *that* asset and *that* policy.

**Equities.** A corporate tax cut is the most direct positive — it lifts EPS mechanically, as the 2017 TCJA's ~8-10% index earnings bump showed. Spending-driven demand lifts cyclical earnings (industrials, materials, consumer). But a deficit-financed cut that pushes long yields up is a headwind to the *multiple*, especially for long-duration growth stocks valued on far-off cash flows. Watch the split: does the earnings lift outweigh the discount-rate drag? The signal to watch is the 10-year yield's reaction to fiscal news — if a "stimulative" tax cut sends yields *up* sharply, the bond market is telling you the supply channel is winning, and the equity multiple is at risk.

**Bonds.** The cleanest fiscal trade is the supply channel: wider deficits, heavier issuance, higher term premium, steeper curve. When fiscal loosens while the Fed holds or tightens (the 2025 quadrant), the long end is pinned up regardless of front-end cuts — a *steepener*. The signal: net Treasury issuance versus the Fed's balance-sheet path. When the Treasury is issuing heavily and the Fed is in QT, the market absorbs supply alone and the term premium rises. We hand the positioning to [how policy sets the bond market](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/how-policy-sets-the-bond-market-the-yield-curve); here the takeaway is that the deficit *is* the supply, and supply needs yield.

**The dollar.** A loose-fiscal, tight-money mix is ambiguous for the currency. The rate differential (high U.S. yields versus the rest of the world) supports it; the credibility worry (can the U.S. keep funding these deficits?) weakens it. In 2025 the credibility side won a round — the DXY's worst first half since 1973. The signal: when long yields rise *and* the dollar falls *together*, that is the market pricing a credibility problem, not a growth story — a tell worth respecting.

**What would invalidate the read.** The supply channel weakens dramatically if the Fed returns to buying the debt (a pivot back to QE), if the deficit narrows materially (a credible consolidation), or if a recession swings the economy back into "crowding in" territory where supply is absorbed by a flight to safety. The demand channel weakens if tax cuts are concentrated where they are saved rather than spent (low multiplier), or if the Fed offsets the fiscal stimulus by tightening. The frame holds; the *which-channel-dominates* answer changes with the policy mix and the cycle. We cover the trade-policy cousin of this — tariffs as a fiscal-and-price lever — in [tariffs and trade policy as a market force](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/tariffs-and-trade-policy-as-a-market-force).

The deepest point is the one we opened with. A single fiscal action — OBBBA's signature on July 4, 2025 — is *simultaneously* a tailwind to corporate earnings and a flood of new bond supply. It supports equities through one door and pressures long yields through the other. Whether the net is bullish or bearish for a balanced portfolio depends entirely on which channel the market is focused on that week. The skill is not picking a side; it is knowing which door the policy is walking through, and watching the yield curve and the dollar to see which one the market believes.
