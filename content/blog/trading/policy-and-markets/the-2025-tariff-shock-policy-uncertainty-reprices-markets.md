> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — In 2025 a single policy lever — the US import tariff — swung from 2.4% to the mid-teens in a matter of days, and markets repriced every asset class in weeks; the repricing engine was not the tariff *level* but the *uncertainty* around it, which showed up as a rare "sell-America" move where stocks, bonds, and the dollar all fell together.
>
> - **The lever moved 90 years' worth of distance in a quarter.** The average effective US tariff rate jumped from \~2.4% (2024) to \~17% (Aug 2025), the highest since the Smoot-Hawley era of the 1930s.
> - **The tell was the combination, not any single move.** The S&P 500 fell \~19% peak-to-trough, but the 10-year Treasury yield *spiked* to \~4.5% and the dollar *fell* \~8% at the same time — the opposite of a normal flight-to-quality. That signals a credibility/term-premium shock, not a growth scare.
> - **Uncertainty was the engine.** Markets can price a known 17% tariff; they cannot price a rate that changes by executive order every week. The April 9 90-day pause produced a +9.5% S&P day — one of the best ever — because it removed uncertainty, not because tariffs were cut to zero.
> - **The one number to remember:** a \$100,000 S&P position bought at the Feb 19 high was worth \~\$81,100 at the April 8 trough, and back near \$95,800 by mid-May — a full round trip in twelve weeks, driven entirely by a policy headline.

On the evening of April 2, 2025, after US markets had closed, the President stood in the Rose Garden and held up a chart. The chart listed dozens of countries and a tariff number next to each one. The policy was Executive Order 14257 — branded "Liberation Day" — and it did two things at once: it imposed a flat 10% tariff on nearly all imports into the United States, and it stacked steep "reciprocal" tariffs on top of that for 57 trading partners, with rates running from 10% all the way to 49%.

S&P 500 futures were already falling as he spoke. By the close on April 8 — four trading days later — the index had dropped about 19% from its February high, one of the fastest non-recession, non-pandemic drawdowns in modern history. And then something strange happened that does not happen in a normal panic: instead of money flooding into US Treasury bonds and the dollar as safe havens, the 10-year Treasury *yield spiked* (bonds *sold off*) and the dollar *fell hard*. Gold, meanwhile, ran to record after record.

This post is the case study. The companion post, [tariffs and trade policy as a market force](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/tariffs-and-trade-policy-as-a-market-force), explains the *mechanism* — what a tariff is, who pays it, and the channels through which any tariff reaches asset prices. Here we trace one specific, dated episode tick by tick, because the 2025 tariff shock is the cleanest recent demonstration of a deep principle in this series: **a policy lever reprices assets not only through its level, but through the *uncertainty* it injects — and when uncertainty attacks a government's credibility, even its own bonds and currency can become the thing investors sell.**

## Foundations: what a tariff shock actually is, and the four ways it reaches prices

Before the blow-by-blow, let us build the machine from zero, the way the rest of this series does: a **policy lever** reaches markets through a **transmission channel** and shows up as a **change in what an asset is worth.**

**What is a tariff?** A tariff is a tax on an imported good, paid at the border by the company importing it. If a US retailer imports a \$100 pair of shoes from Vietnam and the tariff on Vietnamese goods is 20%, the retailer pays the US government \$20 at customs. That is the whole mechanism. The retailer then has three choices: eat the \$20 (lower profit margin), pass it to the customer (the shoes now cost \$120 — that is inflation), or stop importing from Vietnam and find a different supplier (supply-chain disruption). In practice it does some of each. This is why a tariff is simultaneously a tax (revenue for the government), an inflation impulse (higher consumer prices), and a margin squeeze (lower corporate profits) — three things a market has to reprice at once.

**Who pulls this lever, and how fast?** In the United States, the Constitution gives the power to levy tariffs to *Congress*. But over the last century Congress has delegated large chunks of that power to the President through a stack of trade statutes — Section 232 (national security), Section 301 (unfair trade practices), Section 122 (balance-of-payments), and most controversially the International Emergency Economic Powers Act of 1977 (IEEPA), which lets the President act on a declared national emergency. The 2025 tariffs leaned heavily on IEEPA, and that legal choice is exactly what got them struck down in 2026 — more on that below. The key point for a market is this: a tariff set by *executive order* under an emergency statute can change *overnight*, by one person's decision, with no vote and little warning. That is the source of the uncertainty. A tax passed by Congress is slow and visible; a tariff by decree is fast and unpredictable. We cover the legal plumbing in [the 2018-19 US-China trade war](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-2018-19-us-china-trade-war) and [tax law as a market force](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/tax-law-as-a-market-force); here we care about the consequence: *the speed and reversibility of the lever is itself a market variable.*

**The four channels.** This whole series tracks four ways a policy lever reaches asset values: the **discount rate** (what rate we use to value future cash flows), the **liquidity tide** (how much money is sloshing through the system), the **currency** (the exchange rate), and **expectations/credibility** (what people believe policymakers will do, and whether they trust them). A tariff shock is unusual because it pulls on *all four at once*:

- **Discount rate.** Tariffs are inflationary, and inflation pushes up the yields investors demand on bonds (the discount rate), which lowers the present value of every future cash flow — a direct hit to stock and bond prices. See [the discount rate channel](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-discount-rate-channel-how-rates-reprice-cash-flows).
- **Currency.** A tariff is, in textbook theory, dollar-positive (it shrinks the trade deficit). In 2025 the dollar did the *opposite* — it fell — because the credibility channel overwhelmed the textbook channel.
- **Expectations and credibility.** This is the channel that dominated 2025. When a policy is erratic and reversible, investors cannot form a stable expectation, so they demand a *premium* to hold US assets at all. We unpack this in [the expectations channel](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-expectations-channel-forward-guidance-and-credibility).
- **Liquidity** played a supporting role through the Fed's reaction (it *held* rates rather than cutting into the inflation risk).

**The crucial distinction: level vs uncertainty.** A market can price a *known* tariff. If you tell the market "the tariff on China is 30%, permanently, starting Monday," analysts will cut earnings estimates by some amount, the affected stocks will fall by that amount, and the market will move on. What a market *cannot* price is a tariff that is 34% on Wednesday, 145% on Thursday, paused on the following Wednesday, and might be struck down by a court a year later. That is not a number you can put into a discounted-cash-flow model. The rational response to un-pricable uncertainty is to demand compensation for bearing it — a higher *risk premium* and, for the bond market, a higher *term premium* — and to sell the assets most exposed to it. That is the entire story of April 2025 in one sentence, and the figure above is its map.

**What is a risk premium, and why does uncertainty raise it?** The value of any asset is the present value of its future cash flows. To turn future dollars into today's dollars, you *discount* them by a rate, and that rate has two parts: a risk-free rate (what a perfectly safe loan would pay) plus a *risk premium* (extra return demanded for bearing uncertainty). The higher the uncertainty about those future cash flows, the bigger the risk premium, and a bigger discount rate means a *smaller* present value. Concretely: a stock whose earnings you can forecast with confidence might trade at 20 times earnings; the same stock, if you suddenly cannot forecast its earnings because policy might tax its supply chain by an unknown amount, might trade at 16 times — a 20% repricing with *zero change* in this year's actual profit. That is uncertainty acting directly on valuation, and it is most of what happened to US equities in the first week of April 2025.

**What is the term premium, and why is it the bond-market version of the same thing?** When you lend money to the US government for ten years instead of rolling overnight loans, you take on extra risk: inflation might erode your repayment, or the government might do something unpredictable. The *term premium* is the extra yield you demand for that. For most of the 2010s the term premium was near zero or even negative — investors were so confident in low, stable inflation that they barely charged anything for holding duration. A tariff shock that raises *both* expected inflation *and* policy unpredictability pushes the term premium back up, and a rising term premium pushes long bond yields up *independently of what the Fed does to short rates*. This is precisely why, in April 2025, the 10-year yield could spike even as the Fed held the short rate steady: the move was in the *premium*, not the policy rate. We develop the credibility side of this in [the expectations channel](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-expectations-channel-forward-guidance-and-credibility).

**Uncertainty is itself a measurable variable.** It is tempting to treat "uncertainty" as a vague mood, but economists track it with hard indices — the most cited is the Economic Policy Uncertainty index, which counts how often newspapers pair words like "economic" and "policy" with "uncertain." That index spiked to record highs in April 2025, above even its COVID-2020 peak. And there is a clean empirical regularity behind the whole thesis of this post: when policy-uncertainty indices rise, equity risk premia rise, corporate investment falls (companies defer capital spending until the rules are clear), and the bond-market term premium rises. Uncertainty is not a metaphor for the selloff; it is a measurable input that mechanically raises the discount rate on every future cash flow in the economy. The 2025 episode is what that regularity looks like when it fires all at once and at maximum amplitude — which is exactly why it is worth studying tick by tick. With the machine built — lever, channels, risk premium, term premium, and uncertainty as a measurable driver — we can now run the episode.

## The lever: from 2.4% to a 90-year high in a quarter

Let us put a number on how far the lever actually moved, because the magnitude is the first half of the story.

For most of the post-war era, the average effective US tariff rate — total customs duties collected divided by total imports — sat in a narrow band of roughly 1.5% to 3%. The United States was, by the standards of its own history, an extraordinarily open economy; the punishing tariffs of the Smoot-Hawley Act of 1930 (which deepened the Great Depression and triggered a global trade war) had been steadily dismantled over ninety years of trade liberalization. As recently as 2024, the average effective rate was about **2.4%**.

The 2025 program changed that number more in a single quarter than the previous several decades combined.

![Average effective US tariff rate stepping to a 90-year high](/imgs/blogs/the-2025-tariff-shock-policy-uncertainty-reprices-markets-7.png)

The step function above tells the story. The rate ticked up to \~3% in early 2025 as the first "fentanyl" tariffs on China, Mexico, and Canada took effect. Then Liberation Day blew it open: by April the effective rate had jumped to \~12%, by May \~15%, and by August it peaked around **17%** — the highest the United States had imposed since the 1930s. To a first approximation, the country went from charging two-and-a-half cents on the dollar of imports to charging seventeen cents, in months.

Why does this matter for asset values specifically? Because \~17% on the roughly \$3.3 trillion of goods the US imports each year is, mechanically, a tax of around \$500 billion a year on the US economy — split, in some proportion, between foreign exporters (who cut prices to keep market share), US importers (who eat the cost in margins), and US consumers (who pay higher prices). Every one of those three is a line item a market has to reprice: foreign-exporter pain hits emerging-market equities and currencies; margin compression hits US corporate earnings; and consumer price increases hit the inflation outlook, which hits bond yields. A \$500 billion-a-year shift in who-pays-what is not a rounding error. It is a macro event.

#### Worked example: sizing the tariff as a tax on the S&P 500's earnings

Let us make the earnings hit concrete. Suppose, for round numbers, that US importers and the companies in the S&P 500 end up absorbing one-third of a \$500 billion annual tariff bill in compressed margins — about \$165 billion a year of pre-tax profit, before any offsetting price increases or supply-chain shifts.

The S&P 500's aggregate annual earnings in 2025 ran on the order of \$2.2 trillion. A \$165 billion margin hit is therefore roughly **7.5% of total earnings** (\$165B ÷ \$2,200B ≈ 0.075).

Now apply a simple valuation rule of thumb: in a first-order discounted-cash-flow model, if a permanent shock cuts earnings by 7.5% and nothing else changes, the fair value of the index falls by about that same 7.5%. The S&P fell *far more* than 7.5% at the trough (\~19%) — which is the tell that something beyond the earnings arithmetic was at work. The extra \~11.5 percentage points of decline was the market *raising the risk premium* it demanded to hold stocks at all, because the policy was un-pricable.

**Intuition:** the tariff level explains maybe a third of the drawdown; the *uncertainty premium* explains the rest — which is why removing the uncertainty (the April 9 pause) recovered the market faster than any tariff cut could have.

## The timeline: a policy headline reprices everything in weeks

Now the blow-by-blow. The 2025 episode is best understood as a sequence of policy pulses, each one immediately answered by markets.

![The 2025 tariff shock week by week timeline](/imgs/blogs/the-2025-tariff-shock-policy-uncertainty-reprices-markets-2.png)

**February 2025 — the opening salvo.** The first tariffs of the year were "fentanyl" tariffs imposed under IEEPA on China, Mexico, and Canada, justified by the flow of the drug across borders. These nudged the average effective rate to \~3%. Markets shrugged; the S&P was still grinding to new highs, peaking at **6,144 on February 19**.

**March 2025 — sector tariffs.** 25% tariffs on steel and aluminum took effect, and 25% tariffs on imported autos were announced. These are "Section 232" national-security tariffs, a more conventional and legally durable basis. Auto stocks and steel-consuming industrials began to wobble, but the broad market held in the mid-5,000s.

**April 2, 2025 — Liberation Day.** EO 14257 lands after the close. A 10% universal tariff plus reciprocal rates on 57 partners. The headline rates were enormous and, to most analysts, came in *higher* than the worst-case scenarios that had been priced. This is the crucial detail: markets had braced for a tough but negotiable opening bid; what they got looked like a maximalist, formula-driven number (the "reciprocal" rates were computed from bilateral trade deficits, not from other countries' actual tariff rates, which markets read as arbitrary). The S&P closed April 2 at 5,671 — and then the floor gave way.

**April 3-8 — the crash.** Over the next four trading days the S&P fell about **12%**, one of the worst four-day stretches outside of 2008 and 2020. China retaliated, the US escalated, and by April 9 the tariff on Chinese goods had been ratcheted to an almost theatrical **145%** (China's counter-tariff settled around 125%) — a rate so high it amounts to a near-embargo on bilateral trade. The S&P bottomed at **4,983 on April 8**, down \~19% from the February high. This is where the *unusual* cross-asset behavior — bonds and the dollar selling off alongside stocks — became impossible to ignore, and we will dissect it in its own section below.

**April 9, 2025 — the pause and the rip.** With the bond market visibly stressed (the 10-year yield was spiking, not falling), the administration announced a **90-day pause** on the reciprocal tariffs for every country *except* China — reverting most partners to the flat 10% while negotiations proceeded. The S&P 500 rose **9.5% in a single day**, one of the largest one-day gains in the index's history. Read that sequence carefully: the pause did *not* cut tariffs to zero. The 10% universal tariff stayed; China went *up* to 145%. And yet the market staged a historic rally — because the pause replaced "we have no idea what the rate will be next week" with "there is a 90-day window and a negotiation." The market rallied on the *removal of uncertainty*, which is the thesis of this entire post in one trading session.

**May 12, 2025 — de-escalation with China.** The US and China reached a 90-day truce, cutting the bilateral tariffs from 145%/125% down to roughly **30% (US) / 10% (China)**. The relief rally continued; by **May 13 the S&P had erased its entire year-to-date loss**, closing around 5,886. The round trip — peak to trough to recovery — was essentially complete in twelve weeks.

**July-August 2025 — the country deals.** A wave of bilateral agreements landed. **Vietnam** went from its threatened 46% reciprocal rate to a negotiated **20%** (with a 40% rate on goods deemed to be transshipped through Vietnam to dodge China tariffs). The **EU** and **Japan** both settled around **15%**. These deals converted the chaotic April uncertainty into a set of known, if elevated, numbers — and the market continued higher, ending 2025 around **6,680**, comfortably above the pre-shock high.

**July 4, 2025 — the fiscal twin.** Worth noting because it compounds the policy picture: the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA) was signed, a large tax-cut-and-deficit package we cover in [the fiscal toolkit](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-fiscal-toolkit-spending-taxes-and-deficits). OBBBA permanently extended the 2017 tax cuts and added new ones, at a cost (per CBO/JCT) of roughly \$3.4 trillion added to the deficit over a decade — and the US was already running deficits above 6% of GDP with debt held by the public crossing 100% of GDP. This matters for the *bond* side of the tariff story: a market already nervous about fiscal sustainability (more bonds to be issued, more supply for investors to absorb) is a market with a *higher term premium to begin with* — and that elevated baseline made the April Treasury selloff more violent than it would have been against a clean fiscal backdrop. So 2025 ran *tight monetary, loose fiscal, and protectionist trade* all at once. That specific combination — high rates *and* big deficits *and* tariffs — is the "tight money / easy fiscal" quadrant of the policy mix, and it is precisely the mix that produces sticky-high yields and a currency that is strong on rate differentials until credibility cracks, at which point it weakens. We return to the full policy-mix framework in [the policy-to-asset playbook capstone](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-policy-to-asset-playbook-capstone).

**February 20, 2026 — the legal reckoning.** Almost a year later, the Supreme Court ruled that IEEPA does *not* authorize tariffs — the emergency-powers statute the Liberation Day reciprocal tariffs rested on was the wrong legal tool, and those tariffs were struck down. Four days later, on February 24, the administration re-invoked a flat 10% global tariff under **Section 122** of the Trade Expansion Act (a balance-of-payments provision that allows a 15% tariff for up to 150 days without new legislation). The lesson for markets: even the *legal foundation* of a policy lever is a source of uncertainty, and the ground can shift under a position a full year after the original shock.

## The unusual reaction: stocks down, yields UP, dollar DOWN

Here is the heart of the case study, and the part that makes 2025 a teaching example rather than just another selloff.

In a *normal* risk-off event — a growth scare, a recession fear, a pandemic — money behaves predictably. Investors sell risky assets (stocks) and buy safe ones. The safest asset in the world is the US Treasury bond, so its price rises and its yield *falls*. And because foreign investors repatriate cash into dollars to buy those Treasuries, the dollar *rises*. This is the "flight to quality" or "flight to safety," and it is so reliable that a generation of portfolio construction — the 60/40 stock/bond portfolio — was built on the assumption that when stocks fall, bonds rise to cushion the blow.

April 2025 broke the pattern.

![Flight-to-quality versus sell-America comparison of two selloffs](/imgs/blogs/the-2025-tariff-shock-policy-uncertainty-reprices-markets-6.png)

As the figure above lays out, in April 2025 all three moved the *wrong* way relative to a normal panic: stocks fell, the 10-year yield *rose*, and the dollar *fell*. When the world's reserve asset (Treasuries) and the world's reserve currency (the dollar) sell off *together, during a stock-market crash*, it is no longer a flight to safety. It is a flight *away from* the country at the center of the storm. Traders gave it a name: the **"sell-America" trade.**

Let us take the three moves one at a time, with the data.

### Stocks down — the part that was normal

![S&P 500 round trip around the 2025 tariff shock](/imgs/blogs/the-2025-tariff-shock-policy-uncertainty-reprices-markets-3.png)

The equity move was the conventional half of the story. The S&P fell from 6,144 to 4,983, a peak-to-trough decline of about **19%**, with the bulk of it (\~12%) compressed into the four days after Liberation Day. As we showed in the worked example above, only about a third of that drop is explained by the earnings hit from the tariff level; the rest is risk-premium expansion. The chart shows the full round trip: the sharp collapse into April 8, the +9.5% pause-day spike on April 9, and the recovery that erased the year's losses by mid-May. Equities, in other words, behaved like equities in a policy shock — they fell on the bad news and recovered on the good. It was the *bond and dollar* reaction that was anomalous.

### Yields up — the part that was alarming

![The 10Y Treasury yield spiked as stocks crashed](/imgs/blogs/the-2025-tariff-shock-policy-uncertainty-reprices-markets-4.png)

This is the chart that frightened policymakers. As the stock market was crashing in early April, the 10-year Treasury yield first dipped to **3.99% on April 4** (a small, normal flight-to-safety move) — and then *reversed violently*, climbing about **50 basis points in a single week to 4.49% by April 11**. Bonds were *selling off* in the middle of an equity crash. That is the macro equivalent of a fire alarm: the asset that is supposed to be the refuge had become a source of the loss.

Why would Treasuries sell off in a panic? Several forces, all pointing the same way:

- **A higher inflation outlook.** Tariffs are inflationary. A 17% average tariff feeds directly into consumer prices, so the market raised its expected path of inflation, and higher expected inflation means investors demand higher bond yields to be compensated.
- **A higher *term premium*.** This is the subtle and important one. The term premium is the extra yield investors demand to hold a long-term bond rather than rolling short-term ones — compensation for the *uncertainty* of holding duration over many years. When fiscal policy looks unsustainable (big deficits from OBBBA) and trade policy looks erratic, the term premium rises. Investors are saying: "I will lend the US government money for ten years, but I want to be paid more for the risk that policy does something unpredictable to my real return."
- **Forced selling and a credibility flight.** There were also mechanical pressures — leveraged "basis trades" unwinding, and reports of foreign holders trimming Treasury exposure. The common thread is that, for the first time in a long time, the safest asset in the world was being treated as *not entirely safe* because of the policy of its own issuer.

A bond's price moves *opposite* to its yield, so a yield spike from 3.99% to 4.49% means the long bond *fell in price* — the supposed hedge was deepening the loss instead of cushioning it. The 60/40 portfolio's central assumption was failing in real time, exactly as it had (for a different reason — inflation) in 2022, which we cover in [the discount rate channel](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-discount-rate-channel-how-rates-reprice-cash-flows).

### Dollar down — the part that confirmed it

![The US dollar fell during the selloff: DXY down 8% in H1 2025](/imgs/blogs/the-2025-tariff-shock-policy-uncertainty-reprices-markets-5.png)

The final confirmation came from the currency. Trade theory says a tariff should *strengthen* the dollar: tariffs shrink imports, which shrinks the trade deficit, which (all else equal) means fewer dollars sold abroad and a stronger currency. That is the textbook prediction. Instead, the US Dollar Index (DXY) fell from about **110 in January to under 98 by late April**, and finished the first half down roughly **8-10% — its worst first half since 1973**, the year the post-war fixed-exchange-rate system finally collapsed.

A falling dollar *during* a US-centered crisis is the single clearest signal that this was a credibility event. Foreign capital was not rushing *into* dollars for safety (the normal reflex); it was rotating *out* of US assets entirely. The currency channel — covered in depth in [currency policy and FX intervention](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/currency-policy-and-fx-intervention) and [the currency channel](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-currency-channel-rate-differentials-and-carry) — usually amplifies a flight to safety; in April 2025 it ran in reverse, which only happens when the market is questioning the policy regime of the issuer itself.

#### Worked example: reading the cross-asset combination as a credibility shock, not a growth scare

Suppose you are a portfolio manager on the morning of April 9, 2025, and you can only see three numbers. You want to diagnose *what kind* of shock this is, because the right hedge depends entirely on the diagnosis.

- **Number 1:** S&P 500 down \~19% from the high. (A selloff — but selloffs come in two flavors.)
- **Number 2:** 10-year Treasury yield *up* \~50bp in a week, to 4.49%.
- **Number 3:** DXY *down* \~8% for the year.

Now run the two hypotheses:

- **If this were a growth scare** (a recession fear), you would expect yields *down* (bonds bid as a haven) and the dollar *up* (cash coming home). Both of those are the *opposite* of what you see. Hypothesis rejected.
- **If this were a credibility / term-premium shock** (the market is repricing the *risk* of holding US assets, not the *growth* of the US economy), you would expect exactly what you see: stocks down (higher risk premium), yields up (higher term premium), and the dollar down (capital leaving US assets). Hypothesis confirmed.

The practical consequence is huge. In a *growth scare* you hedge equities by *buying duration* (long Treasuries) — bonds rise as stocks fall. In a *credibility shock* that hedge *fails* — bonds fall too — and the assets that actually protect you are **gold, foreign currencies, and inflation-protected securities**, not nominal Treasuries.

**Intuition:** the three-asset combination is a diagnostic test. Stocks-down-bonds-up-dollar-up says "growth problem, buy bonds." Stocks-down-bonds-down-dollar-down says "credibility problem, buy gold" — and in April 2025 the market wrote the second answer in 72-point type.

### Gold — the safe haven that actually worked

![Gold's record run, the safe haven that worked in 2025](/imgs/blogs/the-2025-tariff-shock-policy-uncertainty-reprices-markets-9.png)

If Treasuries and the dollar were *not* the safe haven in 2025, what was? Gold. Gold ran from about \$2,624/oz at the end of 2024 to **\$3,500 by April 2025** — straight through the tariff shock — and kept going, finishing the year up roughly **64%**, one of its best years on record. (It pushed past \$4,000 in October 2025 and on to new records into 2026.)

Gold's logic in a credibility shock is precisely why it earns the "monetary hedge" label, and we develop the full mechanism in [how policy prices gold and commodities](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/how-policy-prices-gold-and-commodities-real-rates-and-the-dollar). The short version: gold is the one major asset with *no issuer* and *no counterparty*. A Treasury bond is a promise from the US government; a dollar is a liability of the Federal Reserve. When the market is specifically worried about the *credibility of US policy*, the assets that are *claims on the US* (Treasuries, the dollar) lose their haven status, and the asset that is a claim on *no one* (gold) inherits it. The 2025 run was reinforced by heavy central-bank buying and a structural "de-dollarization" trend — foreign central banks diversifying reserves away from the dollar — which is itself a slow-moving response to exactly the kind of policy unpredictability April 2025 put on display.

## The Fed's role: why it held rates while the market burned

A natural question: where was the central bank in all this? In a normal crash, the Fed cuts rates to support markets. In 2025 it *held* — keeping the funds rate at **4.50%** (upper bound) from the end of 2024 all the way through August 2025 — and only began cutting in September. Why the hold?

Because the tariff shock put the Fed in a genuine bind, and that bind is itself a policy-to-asset story.

A central bank has one main lever (the policy rate) and, in 2025, two problems pointing in opposite directions:

- **The inflation problem** said *don't cut, maybe even hike.* Tariffs are a price shock; they push CPI *up*. The Fed's models suggested the 2025 tariffs would add somewhere on the order of 0.5 to 1 percentage point to inflation. If the Fed had cut rates into a tariff-driven inflation impulse, it would have risked un-anchoring inflation expectations — the cardinal sin in central banking.
- **The growth problem** said *cut.* The tariff shock and the equity crash threatened to slow the economy and hurt employment, which is the other half of the Fed's dual mandate.

Faced with a shock that is *inflationary and contractionary at the same time* — a "stagflationary" shock, the hardest kind for a central bank — the Fed did the only defensible thing: it *waited*. It held rates to see whether the tariffs would show up first in the inflation data or first in the jobs data. By September, with inflation behaving and the labor market softening, it judged the growth risk to be the bigger one and began cutting (three cuts in late 2025, taking the upper bound to 3.75%). The mechanics of how the Fed sets and signals this are in [how the Fed sets interest rates](/blog/trading/finance/how-the-fed-sets-interest-rates) and [the monetary toolkit](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-monetary-toolkit-rates-qe-qt-and-forward-guidance).

#### Worked example: why a tariff CPI uplift forced the Fed to hold

Let us walk the Fed's arithmetic. Going into 2025, suppose core inflation was running around 2.8% — above the Fed's 2% target but trending down. The Fed's base case was to cut rates gradually through 2025 as inflation normalized.

Now layer on the tariff shock. If a 17% average tariff passes through to consumer prices and adds, say, **0.7 percentage points** to inflation over the following year, the inflation path shifts from "2.8% and falling toward 2%" to "potentially 3.3-3.5% and *re-accelerating*."

Here is the decision the Fed faces, in numbers:

- **If it cuts** (say, to 4.00%) into a re-accelerating inflation path, it risks letting inflation expectations drift up. A rise in long-run expected inflation from 2% to 3% would, through the discount-rate channel, *raise* long bond yields by roughly that 1 percentage point — making financial conditions *tighter*, not easier. The cut would backfire.
- **If it holds** at 4.50%, it keeps policy credibly anti-inflation, anchors expectations, and buys time to see whether the tariff is a one-time price-level bump (which the Fed can "look through") or a persistent inflation source (which it cannot).

The Fed chose to hold. And holding had a direct asset-value consequence: it kept the front end of the yield curve anchored near 4.5% all through the crisis, which is part of *why* the 10-year could spike on term-premium fears without the Fed "rescuing" the bond market by cutting.

**Intuition:** a tariff is a *supply* shock dressed as a demand problem — it raises prices *and* slows growth, so the central bank's single rate lever can't fix both, and "hold and wait" becomes the least-bad policy. The Fed's hold is itself a lever that kept short rates high while long yields rose.

## Second-order effects: credit spreads and the dispersion underneath the index

The headline indices — S&P down 19%, dollar down 8%, gold up 64% — are the *average* of the shock. Underneath them, the repricing was wildly uneven, and the dispersion is where the mechanism shows its fingerprints. Two layers are worth opening up.

**Credit spreads — the corporate-bond stress gauge.** A credit spread is the extra yield a company must pay to borrow over what the US government pays for the same maturity; it is the market's price of the company's default risk. When stress hits, spreads *widen* (companies look riskier), and they widen *most* for the weakest borrowers. During the April 2025 shock, investment-grade (the safest corporate) spreads widened from about 83 basis points at the end of 2024 to roughly **121bp** in April; high-yield (junk, the riskiest) spreads blew out from about 292bp to roughly **461bp** — a far larger move. High-yield is the canary: it sells off hardest in a policy stress because the companies that issue it (leveraged, cyclical, supply-chain-exposed) are exactly the ones a tariff shock hurts most. As the deals landed and uncertainty fell, spreads compressed almost all the way back by year-end (IG to \~85bp, HY to \~300bp), tracing the same round trip as equities. The full mechanism of how policy moves credit spreads — and the "Fed put" that usually backstops them — is in [how policy moves credit spreads](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/how-policy-moves-credit-spreads-and-the-fed-put).

**Sector dispersion — not every stock is a tariff stock.** The index fell 19%, but a globally-sourced consumer-electronics retailer that imports nearly everything fell far more, while a domestic utility that imports almost nothing barely moved. The repricing sorted companies by their *import intensity* and their *pricing power*: a company that imports a lot of its inputs and cannot raise prices (it competes on price) got crushed from both ends — higher input costs *and* no ability to pass them on. A company that sources domestically and has pricing power was nearly untouched, and a few even *benefited* (a domestic steel producer competing against newly-tariffed imports). This is the discount-rate and earnings channels acting at the single-stock level, and it is why "the market fell 19%" badly understates what happened to the most exposed names and overstates what happened to the least exposed. The general mechanism — how policy reprices equities through the multiple and through earnings — is in [how policy prices equities](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/how-policy-prices-equities-the-multiple-and-the-earnings).

The takeaway from the dispersion is a refinement of the whole thesis: a policy shock does not reprice "the market" uniformly; it reprices *exposure* to the policy, and the index move is just the cap-weighted average of a huge range of individual repricings. The companies and bonds with the most policy exposure carry the most uncertainty premium — and therefore both fall hardest in the shock and recover hardest in the relief.

## The country deals: from threat to settled number

The second act of the story — and the part that drove the recovery — was the conversion of April's chaotic threats into negotiated, knowable numbers.

![Reciprocal tariff rates announced versus negotiated by partner](/imgs/blogs/the-2025-tariff-shock-policy-uncertainty-reprices-markets-8.png)

The chart above is the single best picture of *why* markets recovered. On April 2, the announced "reciprocal" rates were maximalist: China 34% (before the escalation to 145%), Vietnam 46%, the EU 20%, Japan 24%, India 26%, Thailand 36%, Cambodia 49%. By late 2025, after a summer of negotiation, the *settled* rates were materially lower and, crucially, *fixed*: China around 30%, **Vietnam 20%**, the EU and Japan both 15%, South Korea 15%.

Two things made this bullish for assets even though the final rates were still historically high:

1. **The deals replaced uncertainty with certainty.** A known 15% EU tariff is something an analyst can model — earnings estimates can be cut by a specific amount and the stock repriced once. A *threatened* 20% that might be 46% or might be 10% next week cannot be modeled at all. Removing the uncertainty compresses the risk premium even if the level rises.
2. **The settled levels came in below the April threats.** The market had priced something close to the maximalist scenario at the April 8 trough; the deals were a positive surprise relative to that anchor.

The Vietnam case is the cleanest illustration and a headline example for this series. Vietnam is an export-led economy with roughly 29% of its exports going to the United States — its single largest market — so the threatened **46% rate** (the fourth-highest reciprocal rate on the April 2 list) was an existential threat to its growth model. To grasp the stakes: if nearly a third of your economy's exports suddenly face a 46% tax in your largest market, the affected goods become uncompetitive overnight, factories slow, and the currency comes under pressure as the trade outlook deteriorates. The negotiated drop to **20%** in the July 2 deal (with a 40% rate on goods deemed *transshipped* — Chinese products routed through Vietnam to dodge the China tariffs) was a massive relief, converting an existential threat into a manageable, known cost.

Why did the deal matter so much for *asset* values specifically? Because the path from a tariff to Vietnamese stock prices runs through several links: a higher tariff means lower export volumes, which means lower factory output and weaker corporate earnings, which means lower equity values; it also means a worse trade balance, which pressures the dong (USD/VND drifted from \~25,450 at the end of 2024 toward \~26,100 in 2025 as the State Bank of Vietnam managed a controlled depreciation), and a weaker currency raises the local cost of the dollar debt many Vietnamese firms carry. The 46%→20% deal short-circuited the worst of that chain. It is a textbook example of how a *single* policy number in Washington reprices an entire foreign economy's assets — and of why an export-dependent emerging market is among the most policy-sensitive asset classes in the world. We trace how this specific deal flows through to Vietnamese assets and the dong in [how global policy reaches Vietnam](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/how-global-policy-reaches-vietnam-the-fed-the-dollar-and-tariffs), and the legal machinery of the State Bank in [SBV monetary and banking law](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/sbv-monetary-and-banking-law-credit-quotas-and-the-dong).

#### Worked example: the April 9 pause rip on a \$100,000 position

Let us put the historic pause-day rally in dollar terms, because the magnitude is genuinely hard to believe.

Imagine you held a \$100,000 S&P 500 index position through the crash and into the morning of April 9, 2025. By the April 8 close, your position had fallen with the index to its trough. On April 9, the index rose **9.5%** in a single session on the pause announcement.

- A 9.5% one-day gain on a \$100,000 position is **+\$9,500** — in *one day*.
- For scale: the average *annual* return on the S&P 500 over the long run is about 10%. You earned, in a single session, nearly a full year's average return.
- And note what triggered it: not a tariff *cut* to zero, but a 90-day *pause* that kept the 10% universal tariff in place and pushed China *up* to 145%. The market paid \$9,500 per \$100,000 not for lower tariffs, but for *less uncertainty about the path of tariffs*.

**Intuition:** the single best day of the year was a *removal-of-uncertainty* day, not a good-news-on-the-level day — which is the cleanest possible proof that uncertainty, not the tariff level, was the asset being priced. When the market got a 90-day window to think instead of a weekly coin-flip, it repriced the risk premium back down instantly.

#### Worked example: the full round trip on a \$100,000 S&P position

Finally, let us trace the complete journey of a single \$100,000 position from the February high through the trough and back, because the round trip is the whole thesis in one number.

Using the index levels: peak 6,144 (Feb 19) → trough 4,983 (Apr 8) → recovery to \~5,886 (May 13).

- **Buy at the peak:** \$100,000 at 6,144.
- **At the April 8 trough (4,983):** the position is worth \$100,000 × (4,983 ÷ 6,144) = **\$81,100**. You are down \$18,900, a \~19% drawdown, in seven weeks.
- **At the May 13 recovery (5,886):** the position is worth \$100,000 × (5,886 ÷ 6,144) = **\$95,800**. You are down only \$4,200 from the peak — and *up* from where the index started the year (the recovery erased the full year-to-date loss).
- **By year-end (6,680):** the position is worth \$100,000 × (6,680 ÷ 6,144) = **\$108,700** — a new high, \$8,700 *above* the pre-shock peak.

So the complete arc for a buy-and-hold investor was: down \$18,900 at the worst, back to roughly flat by mid-May, and up \$8,700 by year-end. A \$28,000 swing (trough to year-end) driven almost entirely by the path of a single policy variable.

**Intuition:** a policy-uncertainty shock is, for a long-term holder, a *volatility* event more than a *value-destruction* event — the fundamental damage (the tariff level's earnings hit) was real but modest, while the *price* swing was enormous, because the market was repricing the *uncertainty premium* up and then back down. The investor who panic-sold at the trough locked in the uncertainty premium as a permanent loss; the one who understood it was a premium, not a fundamental, rode it out.

## Common misconceptions

**"Tariffs are paid by foreign countries, so they don't hurt US assets."** No. Tariffs are paid at the US border by the US company importing the good. Studies of the 2018-19 trade war and the 2025 round both found that the overwhelming majority of the cost was borne by US importers and consumers, not foreign exporters. That is exactly why US corporate margins and US consumer prices — and therefore US stocks and bonds — repriced so hard. A tariff is, first and foremost, a tax on the country imposing it.

**"A tariff strengthens the dollar."** In textbook trade theory, yes — and over a long enough horizon, maybe. But in April 2025 the dollar *fell* \~8%, its worst first half since 1973, because the *credibility* channel overwhelmed the *trade-balance* channel. When markets question the predictability of a country's policy, capital leaves that country's currency regardless of what the trade math says. The 2025 dollar move is a permanent reminder that *credibility beats arithmetic* in a crisis.

**"The 19% drop means tariffs destroyed 19% of corporate value."** No. As the earnings worked example showed, the tariff *level* explained only about a third of the drawdown (\~7.5% of earnings ≈ \~7.5% of value). The other \~11.5 points were the market raising its *risk premium* in response to *uncertainty* — and that part was recovered in weeks once the uncertainty was reduced. Confusing the fundamental hit with the uncertainty premium is the single most expensive mistake an investor could make in this episode, because it turns a temporary volatility spike into a permanent panic-sale.

**"Bonds always protect you when stocks fall."** This is the assumption the 60/40 portfolio is built on, and 2025 broke it — for the second time in three years (2022 broke it via inflation). When the shock is *inflationary* (2022) or a *credibility/term-premium* event (2025), bonds and stocks fall *together*, and nominal Treasuries are not a hedge. The hedge in those regimes is gold, real assets, and foreign currency — a lesson the 2025 "sell-America" move taught in brutal clarity.

**"The Fed should have cut to support the market."** Cutting into a tariff-driven inflation impulse risked un-anchoring inflation expectations, which (through the discount-rate channel) would have *raised* long yields and *tightened* financial conditions — the opposite of the intended rescue. The Fed's hold was not negligence; it was the correct response to a stagflationary shock, where the single rate lever cannot fix inflation and growth at the same time.

## Case studies: 2025 against its closest historical cousins

The 2025 shock is most illuminating when set against the episodes it rhymes with. Each one is a policy-credibility story.

**The 2018-19 US-China trade war (the dress rehearsal).** The first Trump-era trade war was the template: tariffs by executive action, tit-for-tat escalation with China, and equity volatility that tracked the headlines. The S&P fell sharply in Q4 2018 (partly trade, partly Fed) and again in mid-2019 on tariff escalations. But the 2018-19 round was *smaller* (the average effective rate rose to perhaps 3% from 1.5%, not to 17%) and — critically — did *not* produce the "sell-America" bond-and-dollar reaction, because the scale was not large enough to threaten US policy credibility. The 2025 round was the 2018-19 playbook turned up to a magnitude that finally cracked the safe-haven status of US assets. The legal and geopolitical detail of the first war is in [the 2018-19 US-China trade war](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-2018-19-us-china-trade-war).

**The 2022 UK gilt crisis (the same disease, a different government).** In September 2022, the UK government announced £45 billion of *unfunded* tax cuts. The market response was a near-perfect small-scale version of April 2025: UK government bonds (gilts) *crashed* (30-year yields jumped \~130bp in days), the pound *fell* to a record low near \$1.035, and a doom-loop in pension-fund leverage forced the Bank of England into emergency bond-buying. Within 49 days the Prime Minister had resigned. The mechanism was identical: a fiscal-policy lever that the market judged un-credible triggered a simultaneous bond *and* currency selloff — the bond market *disciplining* a G7 government in real time. The 2022 gilt crisis is the clearest historical proof that *the same combination* (bonds down + currency down on a policy shock) is a credibility tell, not a growth scare — and that even a major developed-market sovereign is not immune.

**The 2022 Fed hiking cycle (when bonds first stopped hedging).** 2025 was the *second* time in three years that the 60/40 portfolio failed. In 2022, the Fed's fastest hiking cycle since Volcker (525bp in 16 months) sent *both* stocks (-19%) *and* bonds (-13%, the worst year for Treasuries in modern history) down together, wrecking the classic 60/40 (-16%). The cause was different — inflation, not credibility — but the lesson for portfolio construction was the same: there are regimes where stocks and bonds are *positively* correlated, and a hedge that works in a growth scare fails in an inflation or credibility shock. 2022 broke the 60/40 via the discount rate; 2025 broke it via the term premium. We trace the 2022 mechanics in [how policy moves credit spreads](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/how-policy-moves-credit-spreads-and-the-fed-put).

**The deep case — April 2025 itself, start to finish.** Putting the pieces together, the full April 2025 sequence is the most complete modern example of policy-uncertainty repricing in the historical record: a lever that moved 90 years' worth of distance in a quarter; a four-day, \~12% equity crash; the breaking of the safe-haven reflex (yields *up*, dollar *down*); gold inheriting the haven role; a central bank boxed into holding by a stagflationary shock; a historic +9.5% rally on the *removal* of uncertainty rather than a cut in the level; and a near-full recovery once the uncertainty was converted into negotiated, knowable numbers. Every channel in this series fired at once, and the dominant one was credibility/expectations. If you want a single episode to understand how policy uncertainty — not just policy *level* — sets asset prices, this is it.

## What it means for asset values: the playbook

Stepping back from the narrative, here is the durable, transferable read for how a tariff/policy-uncertainty shock prices assets — the part you can carry to the *next* episode.

**Separate the level from the uncertainty.** The tariff *level* hits asset values through earnings (margin compression) and inflation (higher discount rates) — that part is permanent and modelable, and for the 2025 S&P it was worth roughly the 7-8% earnings hit. The tariff *uncertainty* hits asset values through the risk premium and term premium — that part is large, fast, and *reversible*. The whole art of trading a policy shock is distinguishing the two, because the uncertainty premium is the part that round-trips.

**Watch the cross-asset combination, not any single asset.** The diagnostic is the *pattern*: stocks-down + bonds-up + dollar-up is a growth scare (hedge with duration); stocks-down + bonds-down + dollar-down is a credibility shock (hedge with gold, foreign FX, and inflation protection). The single most valuable skill the 2025 episode teaches is reading those three numbers together. The statistical version of this — how these correlations shift across regimes — is in [the fed funds path and front-end correlation](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/the-fed-funds-path-and-front-end-correlation) and [real yields and the cleanest macro correlation](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/real-yields-and-the-cleanest-macro-correlation).

**Magnitude and direction by asset, in a credibility-style tariff shock:**

- **US equities:** down (\~15-20% peak-to-trough in 2025), with most of the drop being a *recoverable* risk premium; the most import-exposed and highest-multiple sectors fall hardest. Recovers fast on uncertainty removal.
- **Long Treasuries:** *yields up* (term premium and inflation premium rise), so prices *down* — they fail as a hedge. In 2025, +50bp in a week mid-crash.
- **The dollar:** *down* in a credibility shock (the opposite of textbook), \~8-10% in H1 2025.
- **Gold:** *up* — the issuer-less, counterparty-less haven inherits the safety bid; +64% in 2025.
- **Emerging-market / export-economy assets** (Vietnam, etc.): acutely exposed to the *announced* rate, then relieved by the *negotiated* deal — high volatility, deal-headline-driven.

**The signal to watch:** the *path* of the policy, not the level. The market rallied 9.5% on a *pause*, not a cut. So the things that move assets are the events that change the *uncertainty*: a pause, a deal, a court ruling, a missed deadline. Track the policy calendar, not just the policy number.

**What would invalidate this read:** if a future tariff shock produced the *normal* flight-to-quality (yields down, dollar up), it would mean the market judged it a growth scare within a still-credible policy regime — and the right hedge would flip back to duration. The 2025 "sell-America" pattern is specific to shocks large and erratic enough to threaten *credibility*; it is not the default response to every tariff. The positioning playbook for trading these events — sizing, timing, which instruments — lives in the macro-trading section (see [trading the FOMC](/blog/trading/macro-trading/trading-the-fomc-statement-presser-dot-plot) for the analogous policy-event framework); this series owns the *why*, and the synthesis lives in [the policy-to-asset playbook capstone](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-policy-to-asset-playbook-capstone).

**A note on what the 2026 SCOTUS ruling teaches.** When the Supreme Court struck the IEEPA tariffs in February 2026 and the administration re-invoked a 10% rate under Section 122 four days later, the market reaction was muted compared to April 2025 — and that muted reaction is itself the final lesson. By 2026 the *level* of tariffs was largely known and priced; the legal reshuffle changed the statutory basis but not the economic substance, so there was little new uncertainty to price. It confirms the thesis from the other direction: markets move on *changes in uncertainty*, not on policy events per se. A dramatic-sounding headline (the Supreme Court voids the President's tariffs) produced a small market move because it resolved into roughly the same economic outcome — while a 90-day *pause* a year earlier produced one of the largest one-day rallies in history because it slashed uncertainty at the moment of peak fear. Same kind of asset (US equities), opposite-sized reactions, because the variable that prices them is the *change in uncertainty*, not the apparent drama of the headline.

The deepest lesson of 2025 is the one this whole series is built around: **an asset's value is the present value of its future cash flows, discounted at a rate that includes a premium for uncertainty — and a policymaker who makes the future un-pricable raises that premium on every asset at once.** The tariff level mattered. But the *unpredictability* of the policy mattered more, and it repriced stocks, bonds, the dollar, and gold simultaneously, in weeks, on nothing but headlines. That is policy uncertainty as a market force, in its purest modern form.
