> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Two assets with no cash flows — Bitcoin and a currency — are priced almost entirely by policy: crypto trades on net liquidity and real rates as the highest-beta read on the easing/tightening regime, while an exchange rate is set by the *difference* between two central banks' paths.
>
> - **Crypto is the long-duration extreme.** With no earnings and no yield, Bitcoin has no cash flow to anchor a price, so it is a near-pure read on the liquidity tide (QE/QT) and real rates — the same two channels that move every other asset, amplified to the maximum.
> - **FX is the divergence trade.** An exchange rate is a *relative* price: it moves on the gap between two policy paths, not on either one alone. Long the hiking central bank's currency versus the cutting one is the cleanest way to express a policy view.
> - **Together they are the high-octane layer.** Crypto plus high-beta FX rip first and hardest when liquidity is added and break first when it is drained — the most leveraged expression of the policy regime.
> - **The one number to remember:** Bitcoin went from \$4,900 at the COVID liquidity bottom (March 2020) to \$69,000 at the QE peak (November 2021), back to \$15,600 after a year of QT and hikes (November 2022), and on to roughly \$126,000 in 2025 as liquidity and rate cuts returned. A no-cashflow asset, drawn entirely by the policy cycle.

In March 2020 you could buy a Bitcoin for about \$4,900. By November 2021 — after the Federal Reserve had cut its policy rate to zero and bought roughly \$4.8 trillion of bonds — the same Bitcoin cost about \$69,000. Then the Fed reversed: the fastest hiking cycle since the early 1980s, plus the start of quantitative tightening, drained the tide. By November 2022 — the same month the crypto exchange FTX collapsed in a leverage flush — Bitcoin had fallen to about \$15,600, a drop of roughly 75%. And as rate-cut expectations and liquidity returned through 2024 and 2025, it climbed back to record highs above \$95,000, touching roughly \$126,000.

Nothing about Bitcoin itself changed across that round trip. It paid no dividend at \$69,000 and pays none at \$126,000. It has no earnings to grow, no coupon to discount, no factory, no customers. And yet its price traced the policy cycle almost perfectly — up with easy money, down with tight money, back up with easing. That is the whole thesis of this post: an asset with *no cash flows* is the cleanest read on policy you can buy, because there is nothing else holding its price up. Strip away earnings and yield and all that is left is the discount rate and the liquidity tide — the two channels we have built throughout this series — running at maximum sensitivity.

Foreign exchange is the same idea from a different angle. A currency has no cash flow either; an exchange rate is just a *relative* price — how many dollars one euro buys. And a relative price is set by the *difference* between two central banks. When the Fed is hiking and Europe is cutting, the dollar is the higher-yielding currency and it strengthens; when that gap narrows, the dollar weakens. Crypto reads the *level* of the policy regime; FX reads the *divergence* between two of them. This post takes both, builds them from zero, and shows — with dated, real numbers — how policy alone prices the two assets that have nothing else to price them.

![No-cashflow asset map showing policy levers flowing through liquidity and rate channels to crypto and FX outcomes](/imgs/blogs/how-policy-prices-crypto-and-fx-liquidity-and-divergence-1.png)

This is the last stop in the per-asset valuation track. We have priced [equities through the multiple and earnings](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/how-policy-prices-equities-the-multiple-and-the-earnings) and walked [the discount-rate channel that reprices any cash flow](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-discount-rate-channel-how-rates-reprice-cash-flows). Crypto and FX are where that machinery runs without the brakes that cash flows provide.

## Foundations: what it means to be an asset with no cash flows

Start with the most basic question in finance: why is anything worth anything? The textbook answer is that an asset is worth the present value of the cash it will hand you over its life. A bond hands you coupons and your principal back. A stock hands you a share of the company's future profits — dividends now or buybacks and growth later. A rental property hands you rent. To value any of these, you forecast the cash flows and then *discount* them: a dollar arriving in ten years is worth less than a dollar today, and exactly how much less depends on the interest rate you use to discount it. That discount rate is set, at its foundation, by the central bank's policy rate. (We built this engine in full in [the discount-rate channel](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-discount-rate-channel-how-rates-reprice-cash-flows) — here we only need the conclusion.)

Now ask the uncomfortable question: what is a thing worth if it has *no* cash flows at all? Bitcoin pays you nothing for holding it. There is no coupon, no dividend, no rent. The same is true of gold and of a foreign banknote sitting in a drawer. The discounted-cash-flow machine has nothing to chew on — zero in, zero out, present value of zero. By the strict textbook, these assets should be worth nothing.

They are not worth nothing, of course, because the textbook is incomplete. An asset with no cash flows is still worth whatever someone else will pay for it, and that price is set by two forces that have nothing to do with cash flows:

- **The opportunity cost of holding it** — what you give up by parking money in a non-yielding asset instead of one that pays. That opportunity cost *is* the real interest rate (the policy rate minus inflation). When real rates are deeply negative, holding cash or bonds *loses* you purchasing power, so a non-yielding asset costs you nothing in relative terms and looks attractive. When real rates are high and positive, every dollar in Bitcoin is a dollar not earning 2% real in a Treasury — an expensive choice.
- **The amount of money sloshing around looking for a home** — the liquidity tide. When central banks create reserves through quantitative easing (QE) and rates are at zero, there is a vast pool of cash earning nothing, and some of it flows into the highest-octane non-yielding assets. When central banks drain reserves through quantitative tightening (QT) and pay 5% on cash, that pool shrinks and the flow reverses. (The liquidity machine is built in full in [the liquidity channel](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-liquidity-channel-qe-qt-and-the-everything-bid).)

So a no-cashflow asset is not unpriced — it is priced by *exactly the two policy channels we already know*, with the cash-flow channel removed. That removal is the whole point. For a stock, a policy move competes with earnings: the Fed cuts, but if profits are also falling, the stock can go nowhere. For Bitcoin there are no earnings to compete. The policy signal arrives undiluted. That is why crypto is the cleanest, loudest read on the liquidity regime you can find — and also the most violent.

### How a no-cashflow asset finds a price at all

It is worth pausing on a question that bothers careful beginners: if there are no cash flows, how does the *level* get set? Why \$69,000 and not \$690 or \$6.9 million? The honest answer is that a non-yielding asset's price is a **coordination point**, not a calculation. Economists call this a monetary premium, and the cleanest way to see it is John Maynard Keynes's beauty-contest metaphor for markets: you are not trying to pick the asset you think is most valuable, you are trying to pick the asset *everyone else* will agree to hold. Gold has held a monetary premium for five thousand years not because it pays a coupon but because everyone *expects* everyone else to keep valuing it — a self-fulfilling equilibrium, a Schelling point that the whole market converges on without being told to.

This matters for our thesis because a coordination-point price is *more* sensitive to the policy regime, not less. When real rates are negative and liquidity is abundant, the marginal participant's willingness to coordinate on a high price *rises* — there is cash looking for a home and no penalty for holding a non-yielding store of value, so the equilibrium ratchets up. When real rates turn positive and liquidity drains, the willingness to coordinate *falls* — every holder now has a yielding alternative, the marginal buyer steps back, and the equilibrium ratchets down, sometimes violently because there is no cash-flow floor to catch it. A monetary premium is a *policy-sensitive* premium. That is why the level can move ten-fold across a cycle while nothing about the asset itself changes: what changed is the regime that the coordination is conditioned on.

The practical takeaway: do not look for a "fair value" of Bitcoin the way you would for a bond. There isn't one in the cash-flow sense. There is only the policy regime and the market's willingness to coordinate on a price *given* that regime — which is exactly why the regime variables (net liquidity, real rates) are the only inputs that consistently matter across cycles.

#### Worked example: why a zero-cashflow asset is the longest-duration asset

Duration measures how sensitive a price is to a change in the discount rate. A 2-year bond has low duration: most of its cash arrives soon, so a higher discount rate barely dents its present value. A 30-year bond has high duration: its big principal payment is decades away, so a higher rate slashes that distant payment's present value hard. The rule of thumb: the *further out* an asset's cash flows sit, the more a rate change moves its price.

Now push that logic to its limit. A growth stock with no profits today but a promise of huge profits in fifteen years has very high duration — its value lives almost entirely in the distant future. So when rates rose in 2022, unprofitable tech fell far more than the broad market.

Bitcoin is the limit of this process. Its "cash flows" are not in 15 years; they are *never*. The entire value is terminal — it is whatever someone pays at some unspecified future date. In duration terms, a cash flow that never arrives sits infinitely far out, which makes Bitcoin the **longest-duration asset there is** — effectively *infinite duration*. And the most basic fact about duration is that the longer it is, the more a change in the real rate moves the price.

Work the comparison in dollars. Suppose the real discount rate rises by 1 percentage point. A 2-year Treasury (duration ~2) loses about 2% of its value — \$100 becomes ~\$98. A 30-year Treasury (duration ~18) loses about 18% — \$100 becomes ~\$82. A long-duration growth stock might lose 25-35%. Bitcoin, with no cash flow to anchor it, can lose 50-75% on the *same* shift in the real-rate regime — which is precisely what it did in 2022 when the 10-year real yield went from −1.0% to +1.7%. **A zero-cashflow asset has infinite duration, so it suffers the maximum repricing when real rates move.** That single sentence explains both the 2021 melt-up and the 2022 collapse.

## The crypto thesis: Bitcoin as the highest-beta liquidity asset

Let us make "highest-beta" precise, because *beta* is the word that does all the work here. In markets, beta measures how much an asset moves for a given move in some driver. A stock with a beta of 1.5 to the market rises 1.5% when the market rises 1%. We can borrow the same idea for *policy*: how much does an asset move for a given change in the liquidity tide?

Bitcoin's beta to liquidity is the highest of any liquid asset, and the chart below is the cleanest way to see it. Lay the Fed's balance sheet — the direct measure of how much money the central bank has created — on one axis, and Bitcoin on the other. They rise together through QE and fall together through QT. The relationship is not perfect month to month (crypto has its own manias and crashes layered on top), but the *regime* match is unmistakable: the tide goes out, Bitcoin goes down; the tide comes in, Bitcoin goes up.

![Dual-axis chart of the Fed balance sheet and Bitcoin rising together in QE and falling in QT](/imgs/blogs/how-policy-prices-crypto-and-fx-liquidity-and-divergence-3.png)

Why is the beta so high specifically for Bitcoin, rather than, say, for the broad stock market? Three reinforcing reasons:

- **No cash-flow anchor.** As we built above, there is no earnings stream to hold the price up when sentiment fades. The price is pure flow-of-funds. When the marginal buyer's wallet shrinks, there is nothing underneath.
- **It sits at the far end of the risk ladder.** When a central bank adds liquidity, that money does not all rush into the riskiest asset at once. It cascades: first into the safest assets (cash equivalents, short bonds), then out the curve into longer bonds, then into equities, and finally — the last and most aggressive stop — into the highest-octane speculative assets. Bitcoin is at the end of that line. So it gets the *last* and *largest* push of an easing wave, and it is the *first* thing sold when the wave reverses and investors retreat back down the ladder toward safety.
- **Leverage and reflexivity.** Crypto markets carry enormous embedded leverage — perpetual futures, lending protocols, leveraged funds. When liquidity is ample and prices rise, leverage builds on leverage (rising collateral lets you borrow more, which buys more, which raises prices). When liquidity drains and prices fall, that same leverage unwinds violently — forced liquidations cascade, which is exactly what the FTX collapse in November 2022 detonated. Leverage turns a high beta into a *non-linear* one: small liquidity moves produce small price moves, but a regime change produces a crash or a melt-up.

### The risk-asset beta ladder

The ladder image is worth making concrete, because it is the single most useful mental model for where crypto sits in the policy machine. Picture every asset class arranged by how much cash flow anchors its price — and therefore how much policy beta it carries.

![Matrix of asset classes from cash to crypto showing rising policy beta as cashflow anchoring falls](/imgs/blogs/how-policy-prices-crypto-and-fx-liquidity-and-divergence-4.png)

At the bottom sits **cash and Treasury bills** — repriced daily to the policy rate itself, so they are the rate. Beta to the rate is essentially the whole asset; beta to *risk sentiment* is zero. One rung up, **bonds** carry fixed coupons and a return of principal, so duration sets their sensitivity, but they still have a cash-flow anchor. Up another rung, **equities** have earnings, which both anchor the price *and* respond to policy through the discount rate and the economic cycle — mid-to-high beta, and the swings come from the multiple expanding and contracting. At the very top sits **crypto** — no yield, no earnings, nothing to anchor it — pure net-liquidity and real-rate sensitivity, the highest beta on the ladder.

The ladder is not just a ranking; it is a *transmission sequence*. A liquidity add enters at the bottom and percolates up; a liquidity drain starts the retreat from the top. That is why crypto leads both directions.

### Net liquidity, precisely: the two drains that can offset QT

We have been saying "net liquidity" loosely; let us make it exact, because the precision is what separates a real read from a hand-wave. The Fed's balance sheet (the WALCL series) is the *gross* amount of money the central bank has created. But not all of that money is sloshing around in markets — two large accounts can lock it up even while the headline balance sheet is flat or rising.

- **The Treasury General Account (TGA)** is the government's checking account at the Fed. When the Treasury issues a flood of bills and lets cash pile up in the TGA, that money is pulled *out* of the banking system and parked at the Fed — a drain on market liquidity even though the Fed's balance sheet hasn't shrunk. When the Treasury *spends* the TGA down, the cash floods back in — an injection.
- **The overnight reverse-repo facility (RRP)** is where money funds park spare cash at the Fed overnight to earn the policy rate. A high RRP balance means liquidity is sitting *idle* at the Fed instead of chasing assets; a falling RRP balance means that money is being released back into markets to hunt for yield.

So the liquidity that actually reaches the risk ladder is roughly **Fed balance sheet − TGA − RRP**. This is why crypto sometimes ran *during* QT in 2023-24: the headline balance sheet was shrinking, but the RRP was draining fast (over \$2 trillion released as money funds rotated out), which *added* net liquidity to markets even as the gross balance sheet fell. The lesson for reading the crypto regime: track *net* liquidity, not the headline. When net liquidity is rising — whatever the gross balance sheet is doing — the top of the ladder tends to bid.

### The on-chain liquidity gauge: stablecoin supply

Crypto offers a second, native liquidity gauge that traditional assets lack: the supply of **stablecoins** — tokens like USDT and USDC that are pegged to the dollar and act as the cash leg of crypto markets. Stablecoin supply is, in effect, the amount of "dry powder" sitting on exchanges ready to buy. When the macro liquidity regime is easy and capital is flowing into crypto, stablecoin supply *expands* (people mint stablecoins to deploy); when the regime tightens and capital retreats, supply *contracts* (people redeem stablecoins for dollars and leave). The aggregate stablecoin market grew from under \$10 billion in early 2020 to over \$160 billion at the 2021-22 peak, contracted through the 2022 drain, and re-expanded past \$200 billion into the 2024-25 recovery — tracing the same policy cycle as Bitcoin itself, one layer closer to the plumbing. It is the clearest example of how the macro liquidity tide shows up *inside* the crypto system, not just around it.

#### Worked example: Bitcoin's beta to net liquidity

Let us put a number on the beta with the actual cycle data. Between March 2020 and April 2022, the Fed's balance sheet grew from about \$4.2 trillion to about \$9.0 trillion — a liquidity injection of roughly \$4.8 trillion. Over that same window, Bitcoin went from about \$4,900 to its November 2021 peak of about \$69,000. That is a gain of roughly 1,300% — a 14-fold increase — riding a balance-sheet expansion of about 114%.

Now run it in reverse. From the April 2022 peak of \$9.0 trillion, QT pulled the balance sheet down toward \$6.85 trillion by the end of 2024 — a drain of roughly \$2.1 trillion, about a 24% contraction. Over the heart of that drain (the 2022 portion), Bitcoin fell from \$69,000 to \$15,600, a drop of about 77%.

So the rough arithmetic: a *24% drain* of central-bank liquidity coincided with a *77% crypto drawdown* — a beta on the order of 3 (the crypto move was roughly three times the percentage liquidity move), and far higher than that against the *net* liquidity measure that also strips out the Treasury's cash account and the Fed's reverse-repo facility. Translate to dollars for a position: a \$10,000 Bitcoin holding at the April 2022 regime turn was worth about \$2,300 by the November 2022 low. **A central-bank liquidity swing of a couple trillion dollars maps onto a multi-fold crypto move — the highest policy beta in the liquid markets.** That is what "no cash-flow anchor" buys you: leverage to the policy tide with no floor underneath.

### The real-rate read

Net liquidity is one half of the crypto thesis; the *real rate* is the other, and the two are deeply related. The real rate is the policy rate minus expected inflation — the true reward for holding safe, yielding assets after inflation eats its share. It is the opportunity cost of holding something that yields nothing, like Bitcoin or gold.

When the 10-year real yield is deeply negative — as it was through 2021, bottoming near −1.1% — holding cash or Treasuries *loses* you purchasing power every year. In that world a non-yielding asset gives up nothing relative to the alternatives, and the highest-octane non-yielding asset, Bitcoin, runs. When the real yield swings hard positive — as it did in 2022-2023, reaching +1.7% and beyond — every dollar in a no-yield asset is now a dollar foregoing a genuine, inflation-beating return in a Treasury. The opportunity cost of holding Bitcoin went from *negative* to *expensively positive*, and the asset cratered.

![Dual-axis chart of the inverted 10-year real yield against Bitcoin tracking the same regime](/imgs/blogs/how-policy-prices-crypto-and-fx-liquidity-and-divergence-6.png)

This is the same mechanism that drives gold, which has roughly a −0.8 correlation to the 10-year real yield. Crypto is gold's high-beta cousin in the real-rate trade: same sign, much bigger amplitude. For the precise statistical relationship — the rolling betas and correlations — see [the real-yields correlation post](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/real-yields-and-the-cleanest-macro-correlation); what we own here is the *why*: a no-cashflow asset's only competitor is a yielding safe asset, so its price is the mirror image of that asset's real return.

## The FX thesis: exchange rates as the policy-divergence trade

Now switch assets, but keep the principle. A currency has no cash flow — a euro banknote pays no coupon. So what sets its price against the dollar? Not its own central bank's policy *alone*, but the *difference* between two central banks' policies. This is the single most important idea in foreign exchange, and beginners miss it constantly: an exchange rate is a **relative price**, so it is driven by a **relative** force.

Here is the intuition before any mechanism. Suppose you have \$1,000 and you can park it in dollars earning 5% or in euros earning 2%. All else equal, the dollar is more attractive — you earn 3 percentage points more per year just for holding it — so money flows toward dollars, bidding the dollar up against the euro. The thing that drove the flow was not the 5% by itself and not the 2% by itself; it was the **3-point gap**. If the European Central Bank then hiked to 4% while the Fed held at 5%, the gap would shrink to 1 point, and the pressure on the dollar would *ease*, even though the Fed never moved. FX trades on the *change in the gap*.

This "relative price" property is what trips up beginners, so it is worth dwelling on. With a stock, you can ask "is this company doing well?" and get a useful answer about the price. With a currency, the question "is the US doing well?" is almost meaningless on its own — well *compared to whom*? A currency has no absolute value; its price is always a ratio, the dollar *per euro* or the yen *per dollar*. And a ratio can move because the numerator changed, because the denominator changed, or because expectations about *either* changed. The dollar can strengthen because the US economy improved *or* because Europe's deteriorated — the chart looks identical. This is why FX is the purest *relative* asset in markets: there is no way to price it except as a comparison, and the comparison that dominates is the comparison of the two central banks' policy paths, because that is the comparison that determines where global capital earns the most for the least risk.

That gap is the **rate differential**, and chasing it is the **carry trade** — borrow in the low-yielding currency, hold the high-yielding one, and pocket the difference. We build the full carry mechanism, including its hidden risks, in [the currency channel](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-currency-channel-rate-differentials-and-carry); here the point is narrower and sharper: because an exchange rate is set by a *difference* between two policy paths, FX is the purest way to express a **divergence** view — a bet not on what one central bank does, but on what one does *relative* to another.

There is a deeper relationship hiding underneath the carry trade, and it is worth surfacing because it pins down *exactly* how the rate differential is priced into the currency. It is called **covered interest parity**, and it says: the difference between the spot exchange rate and the forward exchange rate (the rate you can lock in today for a future date) must equal the interest-rate differential between the two currencies. If it didn't, you could borrow in one currency, convert, lend in the other, and lock the future conversion rate for a risk-free profit — and arbitrageurs would close that gap in seconds. So the rate differential is not a vague pressure; it is *mechanically embedded* in the forward FX price. The market prices the policy divergence into the currency curve the way it prices a yield into a bond.

#### Worked example: how the rate differential is priced into the forward

Make covered interest parity concrete. Suppose the dollar earns 5% for one year and the euro earns 2%, and the spot rate is 1 euro = \$1.10. Covered interest parity says the one-year forward rate must make the two paths break even. Start with \$1.10: lend it in dollars and you have \$1.10 × 1.05 = \$1.155 in a year. Alternatively, convert the \$1.10 to 1 euro, lend it at 2%, and you have 1.02 euros in a year. For those to be equal, the forward rate must be \$1.155 ÷ 1.02 = about \$1.1324 per euro.

So the forward rate (\$1.1324) is *higher* than the spot (\$1.10) — the euro trades at a forward *premium* of about 3%, exactly the rate differential. The higher-yielding currency (the dollar) trades at a forward *discount*. This is the mathematical fingerprint of the policy divergence: the entire 3-point gap shows up as a ~3% forward adjustment. When you "earn the carry," you are earning that 3% — but you are also betting the *spot* doesn't move against you by more than the forward already implies. **The rate differential is not a soft pressure on the currency; covered interest parity bakes the full 3-point policy gap into the forward FX rate, so a divergence view is literally a bet on whether spot will beat the forward the market has already priced.** That is what makes FX the most exact expression of a relative-policy view in all of markets.

#### Worked example: an FX-divergence trade

Make the divergence trade concrete with dollars. It is 2022. The Fed is hiking aggressively — the policy rate is climbing through 3% and 4% on its way to 5.5%. The Bank of Japan is doing the opposite: it has pinned its short rate near −0.1% and is capping its 10-year yield near zero (yield-curve control). The rate differential between the dollar and the yen is gaping wide and *getting wider* every meeting — exactly the divergence FX loves.

The trade: borrow yen (cheap, near 0%), convert to dollars, and hold them earning ~4%. You collect the ~4-point carry. And because the differential is *widening*, capital floods the same way you are positioned, pushing the dollar up against the yen. In 2022 the dollar rose from about ¥115 to about ¥150 — a roughly 30% move in the dollar's favor over the year. On a \$1,000,000 notional position, you earned roughly \$40,000 in carry *and* a six-figure currency gain as the yen weakened.

Now the warning the worked example must carry: the carry is collected in small daily drips, but it can be wiped out in a single violent session when the divergence reverses. When the BOJ finally started normalizing in 2024 and the Fed started cutting, the gap *narrowed*, the carry trade unwound, and the yen snapped back — a several-percent move in days that liquidated leveraged carry positions worldwide (the August 2024 unwind). **Long the hiking central bank's currency versus the cutting one earns the rate differential as carry and rides the divergence — but the trade lives and dies on the *gap*, and a narrowing gap can give back a year of carry in a week.** FX is policy divergence with a fat tail.

### The dollar smile and the safe-haven exception

The rate differential explains most FX moves most of the time, but the dollar has a second mode that every FX trader must hold in mind: the **dollar smile**. Picture a U-shaped curve. The dollar tends to be strong at *both* extremes — when the US economy is booming and the Fed is the highest-yielding central bank (the right side of the smile, pure rate-differential strength) *and* when the world is in crisis and capital flees to safety (the left side of the smile, safe-haven strength). The dollar is weakest in the *middle*, when global growth is steady and risk appetite is healthy, so capital fans out to higher-yielding currencies abroad.

The safe-haven side is the important exception to "trade the differential," because it is the one regime where the rate gap stops mattering. In a genuine risk-off panic — a 2008, a March 2020 — investors do not ask which currency yields more; they ask which currency they can *always* sell into, and the answer is the dollar, because it is the world's reserve currency and the denominator of global trade and debt. So the dollar can *rise* even as the Fed slashes rates to zero, because the flight-to-safety flow overwhelms the differential. This is why the dollar's relationship to risk assets is regime-dependent: most of the time a weak dollar goes with risk-on (good for crypto and high-beta FX), but in a crisis the dollar and risk assets *both* get the safe-haven flow backwards — the dollar bids and everything else sells. The April 2025 episode was unusual precisely because it *broke* the safe-haven reflex: amid a tariff-driven risk-off, the dollar *fell* and Treasury yields *rose*, a "safe-haven failure" that signaled the credibility discount we discuss in the case studies.

### Crypto's cousins: the high-beta currencies

To see why crypto and FX belong in the same post, look at the currencies that behave most like crypto. The **commodity and emerging-market currencies** — the Australian dollar, the Norwegian krone, the Mexican peso, the South African rand, the Brazilian real — are the high-beta layer of the FX world. They share crypto's two defining traits: they are *risk-on* assets (they rise when global liquidity is ample and the dollar is weak) and they often carry *high local interest rates* (so they are the targets of the carry trade). When the global policy regime is easy and risk appetite is high, capital borrows cheap dollars or yen and pours into these high-yielding currencies — they rip, just like crypto. When the regime tightens and the dollar bids, that carry unwinds and they crack — first and hardest, just like crypto. An Australian dollar and a Bitcoin are not as different as they look: both are leveraged bets on the global liquidity tide, one with a cash-flow anchor (the rate differential it pays) and one without.

### The 2025 dollar decline as a divergence (and credibility) trade

The cleanest recent FX case runs the divergence in reverse — and adds a second policy channel, *credibility*. Coming into 2025, the dollar was strong because the Fed was the highest-yielding major central bank, holding its rate at 4.25-4.50% while peers had begun cutting. The US Dollar Index (DXY), which measures the dollar against a basket of major currencies, started the year around 110.

![Line chart of the US Dollar Index falling through 2025 as policy divergence narrowed](/imgs/blogs/how-policy-prices-crypto-and-fx-liquidity-and-divergence-5.png)

Then two things happened. First, the *divergence narrowed* in the market's mind: as the year wore on, traders priced the Fed eventually joining the cutting club (which it did, three cuts in the back half of 2025), shrinking the dollar's yield advantage. Second — and this is the part that makes 2025 special — the dollar fell *despite* still offering a yield premium, which points past the rate differential to the **expectations and credibility channel**. The April 2025 tariff shock and the questions it raised about US policy stability made foreign capital less willing to hold dollar assets at *any* given yield. The DXY fell more than 10% in the first half of 2025 — its worst first half since 1973 — bottoming near 98 in April. That is a currency repriced not by a single rate move but by a *narrowing differential plus a credibility discount*. (For how a fiscal-monetary collision can discipline a currency in *days* rather than months, the 2022 UK gilt crisis is the textbook case — covered in the case studies below.)

## Combining them: crypto plus high-beta FX as the high-octane layer

Put the two assets side by side and a pattern jumps out: crypto and high-beta currencies are the **same trade expressed twice** — both are leveraged reads on the policy regime, the first things to rip when liquidity is added and the first to break when it is drained.

![Before-after diagram of the tightening regime breaking high-beta assets and the easing regime ripping them](/imgs/blogs/how-policy-prices-crypto-and-fx-liquidity-and-divergence-7.png)

Think of the high-beta currencies — the commodity and emerging-market currencies (the Australian dollar, the Mexican peso, the South African rand, the Brazilian real) — as crypto's cousins in the FX world. They benefit when global liquidity is ample and the dollar is weak (risk-on, carry-friendly), and they get crushed when liquidity drains and the dollar bids (risk-off, carry-unwind). Crypto and high-beta FX move *together* because they are driven by the same master variable: the global liquidity and real-rate regime. In an easing turn, both run; in a tightening break, both crack — and the leveraged ones crack first.

#### Worked example: the risk-beta ladder cascade

Trace a single \$1 trillion liquidity add as it cascades up the ladder, to see why crypto and high-beta FX sit at the loud end.

The central bank creates \$1 trillion of reserves through QE and parks the policy rate at zero. Step one: that \$1 trillion lands first in the *safest* assets — money funds and short Treasury bills. Because cash now yields nothing, holders start reaching for *any* yield. Step two: money flows out the curve into longer Treasuries and investment-grade credit; yields fall a little, prices rise a little — a modest move, because these assets have cash-flow anchors that limit how far they can re-rate. Step three: the search for return pushes into equities; the discount rate has fallen, so the multiple expands and stocks rise more — a bigger move, because the cash-flow anchor is looser. Step four, the end of the line: the last and most aggressive dollars chase the highest-octane assets — speculative growth stocks, high-beta currencies, and crypto. Here there is *no* cash-flow anchor, so the move is largest of all.

Put rough magnitudes on the cascade from a \$1 trillion add: T-bills, basically flat (they *are* the rate); 10-year Treasuries, maybe +2-3% on a 25-30 basis-point yield fall; the S&P, perhaps +5-10% as the multiple expands; high-beta FX, +5-15%; Bitcoin, +50-100% or more if the add is part of a sustained easing regime. **The same \$1 trillion produces a flat move in cash and a multi-fold move in crypto — the liquidity wave amplifies as it climbs the ladder, and crypto stands at the top catching the largest, last, and most leveraged push.** Reverse the flow and the retreat starts at the top: crypto and high-beta FX are sold first, and the cascade runs back down toward the safety of cash.

## Common misconceptions

**"Bitcoin is digital gold / an inflation hedge, so high inflation should lift it."** This conflates inflation with the policy *response* to inflation. In 2022, US inflation hit a 40-year high of 9.1% — and Bitcoin fell 65% that year. Why? Because high inflation *forced the Fed to hike hard*, driving real rates from −1% to +1.7%, and the real rate is what prices a no-yield asset. Bitcoin does not hedge inflation; it reads the *real-rate regime that inflation provokes*. When inflation rises but the central bank stays easy (negative real rates), Bitcoin runs; when inflation rises and the central bank tightens (positive real rates), it falls. The hedge story gets the sign exactly backwards in a tightening cycle.

**"Crypto has decoupled from macro — it's its own asset class now."** Decoupling is what people say at the top, right before the macro reasserts itself. Crypto's correlation to liquidity and real rates *varies* — it can run hot on its own narratives (a halving, an ETF approval, a memecoin mania) for months, which looks like decoupling. But across every full cycle since 2020, the regime has reasserted itself: the 2021 top came as the Fed signaled tapering; the 2022 bottom came as the hiking cycle peaked; the 2024-25 highs came as cuts and liquidity returned. The short-run noise is idiosyncratic; the *cycle* is policy.

**"A strong economy means a strong currency."** Not directly — a currency is priced by the *relative policy path*, not the absolute economy. A booming economy matters for FX only insofar as it makes the central bank *hike relative to others*. In 2025 the US economy was solid and the dollar still fell more than 10% in the first half, because the *differential* narrowed and credibility was questioned. Growth reaches the currency through the policy reaction function, not on its own.

**"FX is a zero-sum sideshow — real money is in stocks."** FX is the *largest* market in the world by volume (trillions of dollars a day) and the channel through which every other policy trade ultimately settles. More to the point for this series: a currency is the purest single-variable expression of a *divergence* view. If you believe the Fed will out-hawk the ECB, you do not need to pick stocks — long the dollar against the euro and you have isolated the policy bet with no earnings risk attached.

**"QT is just QE in reverse, so it should hurt crypto symmetrically."** The *direction* is symmetric but the *violence* is not, because of leverage and reflexivity. QE builds positions slowly as confidence accretes; QT can trigger a *forced* unwind where falling prices liquidate leverage, which lowers prices further, which liquidates more — the FTX-style cascade. A no-cashflow asset with embedded leverage falls faster than it rose. The 2022 drawdown was sharper and more abrupt than the 2020-21 climb for exactly this reason.

## Case studies: five turns of the policy cycle

The chart that opened this post is the spine of all four cases. Here we walk each turn with the dated numbers, because the case studies are where this series earns its keep — the policy lever, the channel, and the repriced asset, all real.

![Bitcoin price cycle from 2017 to 2025 annotated with the four policy-regime turning points](/imgs/blogs/how-policy-prices-crypto-and-fx-liquidity-and-divergence-2.png)

### Case 1 — 2020-21: zero rates and QE drive \$4,900 to \$69,000

On March 15, 2020, with the COVID shock cratering markets, the Fed cut its policy rate to zero, and on March 23 it announced *unlimited* quantitative easing — open-ended bond buying with no cap. Over the next two years the balance sheet roughly doubled, from about \$4.2 trillion to about \$9.0 trillion. Real yields plunged to roughly −1.0% and kept falling toward −1.1% by early 2021. Every condition that prices a no-cashflow asset went maximally favorable at once: liquidity flooding in, real rates deeply negative, the opportunity cost of holding a non-yielding asset wiped out.

Bitcoin's response was the textbook high-beta read. From its March 2020 low near \$4,900, it cleared \$29,000 by the end of 2020 and peaked near \$69,000 in November 2021 — a roughly 14-fold gain. Nothing fundamental about Bitcoin changed; the *policy regime* changed, and the highest-beta asset on the ladder caught the largest, last push of the easing wave. This is the easing-rip half of our before-after figure, drawn in real numbers. For the trader's positioning version of this exact regime, see [how monetary policy moves crypto](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-monetary-policy-moves-crypto-liquidity-real-rates).

It is worth noting how *broad* the easing wave was, because it confirms the ladder mechanism. The same policy regime that took Bitcoin up 14-fold also doubled the S&P 500 off its March 2020 low by 2021, drove unprofitable growth stocks and SPACs to manic highs, and inflated everything from used cars to suburban housing. Bitcoin was simply the *loudest* expression of a wave that lifted the entire risk ladder — it caught the largest move because it sits at the top, with no cash-flow anchor to limit how far the coordination point could ratchet. The breadth is the tell: when *every* risk asset moves together, the driver is not any single asset's story, it is the common factor — the policy tide — and crypto is the asset that registers that common factor with the least dilution.

### Case 2 — 2022: QT plus the fastest hiking cycle and a −75% drawdown

The regime flipped in 2022, and it flipped hard. The Fed delivered the fastest hiking cycle since the Volcker era: 525 basis points in 16 months, from 0.25% to 5.50% across 11 increases. At the same time it began quantitative tightening, letting the balance sheet run off from its \$9.0 trillion April peak. The 10-year real yield swung from about −1.0% to +1.74% by October 2022 — a brutal repricing of the opportunity cost of holding anything that yields nothing.

![Dual-axis chart of the 2022 hiking cycle stepping up while Bitcoin falls 75%](/imgs/blogs/how-policy-prices-crypto-and-fx-liquidity-and-divergence-8.png)

Bitcoin did what the highest-duration, highest-beta asset must do when real rates swing positive and liquidity drains: it collapsed. From \$69,000 it fell to about \$15,600 by November 2022 — a drawdown of roughly 77%. And here the second amplifier showed its teeth: the embedded leverage in crypto markets turned a bad year into a cascade. The November 2022 collapse of FTX was the leverage flush made visible — a forced unwind that liquidated positions across the ecosystem and marked the cycle low. Note the symmetry with traditional markets: 2022 was also the year stocks and bonds *both* fell (the 60/40 portfolio lost about 16%), because the same rate shock repriced every duration-sensitive asset at once. Crypto, with infinite duration, simply lost the most. The mechanics of the balance-sheet drain are detailed in [QE vs QT](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-liquidity-channel-qe-qt-and-the-everything-bid).

The *timing* within 2022 is the part that confirms the ladder runs both directions. Crypto did not crash at the bottom alongside everything else; it *led*. Bitcoin had already broken below \$30,000 by mid-June 2022 — months before the S&P 500 found its October low and well before the worst of the bond rout. The top of the ladder retreats first because the leveraged, no-anchor assets are the first thing sold when the tide is expected to drain. The order of the carnage in 2022 reads top-down: the speculative growth stocks and crypto cracked in the first half, the broad equity market bottomed in the autumn, and the credit canary (high-yield spreads) widened last. That sequence is not a coincidence; it is the ladder running in reverse, and crypto's early break was a *leading* signal that the regime had turned hostile — exactly the property the playbook below trades on. The same year's UK gilt crisis (a fiscal-monetary collision that forced an emergency central-bank intervention within days) showed the *credibility* version of the same lesson: when policy loses the market's confidence, the repricing is measured in days, not quarters.

### Case 3 — 2024-25: the easing turn drives a recovery to records

As inflation cooled and the Fed pivoted, the regime turned a third time. The Fed began cutting in September 2024 (down to 4.50% by year-end) and cut three more times in 2025 to 3.50-3.75%. Liquidity expectations improved, real-rate pressure eased off its highs, and risk appetite rebuilt from the bottom of the ladder up toward the top.

Bitcoin tracked the turn precisely. It recovered through 2023 (to about \$42,000), broke its old high in 2024 (above \$73,000, then above \$95,000 by year-end as a spot ETF channeled fresh demand), and pushed to roughly \$126,000 in 2025. The point is not the ETF narrative — that is the idiosyncratic noise layered on top. The point is that the *regime* turned from draining to adding, and the highest-beta asset on the ladder responded, as it always does, first and largest. Three turns of the policy cycle, three textbook moves in an asset with no cash flows to muddy the signal. For the structural framing of crypto as a macro liquidity asset across the whole cycle, see [crypto as a macro liquidity asset](/blog/trading/macro-trading/crypto-as-a-macro-liquidity-asset) and [the liquidity correlation](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/crypto-as-a-macro-asset-the-liquidity-correlation).

A fair objection: doesn't the Bitcoin *halving* — the roughly four-year event that cuts the rate of new supply in half — explain the cycle just as well as policy? The halvings (2012, 2016, 2020, 2024) do line up loosely with the cycle tops that followed roughly a year later, and supply mechanics are real. But two things argue that policy is the dominant driver. First, the *magnitude* of the moves tracks the liquidity regime, not the supply cut: the 2020-21 melt-up coincided with the largest liquidity injection in history, and the 2022 collapse coincided with the sharpest tightening in 40 years — supply changed by a fixed schedule, but the *amplitude* was set by policy. Second, the drawdowns are timed to *policy* turns, not supply events: the 2021 top came as the Fed signaled tapering, and the 2022 bottom came as the hiking cycle peaked — neither is a halving date. The cleanest reading is that the halving sets a loose *rhythm* but the policy regime sets the *amplitude and the turns*. When the two align (a halving into an easing wave, as in 2020 and 2024), the move is enormous; when they fight (a halving into tightening), policy wins.

### Case 4 — 2025: the dollar's decline as a divergence trade

The FX case runs alongside the crypto recovery and shows the *other* no-cashflow asset reading policy — through divergence rather than level. Entering 2025 the dollar was strong because the Fed was the highest-yielding major central bank, and the DXY started near 110. Through the first half of 2025, two forces pulled it down: the *expected* convergence of the Fed toward its cutting peers (the differential narrowing), and a *credibility discount* after the April tariff shock raised questions about US policy stability. The DXY fell more than 10% in the first half of 2025 — the worst first half since 1973 — bottoming near 98 in April before stabilizing into year-end around 99.5.

This is the divergence trade in a single year: a currency repriced not by one central bank's move but by the *difference* between paths narrowing, with a policy-credibility discount stacked on top. It is the FX mirror of the crypto cases — same machine (policy with no cash flow to dilute it), different lever (the gap between two policies rather than the level of one). Notice also how the *expectations channel* did work that the rate differential alone could not: the dollar fell while still offering a yield premium, because credibility is its own price.

### Case 5 — 2013: the taper tantrum and the high-beta FX flush

One more case sharpens the FX-divergence point by running it through the *emerging-market* currencies — crypto's cousins. In May 2013, Fed Chair Ben Bernanke merely *hinted* that the Fed might slow (taper) its bond buying. No rate hike, no actual reduction — just the suggestion that the liquidity tide might stop rising. The reaction was violent and instructive: the 10-year Treasury yield jumped from about 1.63% to 2.99% over the summer, the dollar strengthened, and the assets at the top of the risk ladder were hit hardest.

The high-beta currencies took the worst of it. The "Fragile Five" — the currencies of countries running large external deficits that depended on cheap global capital (the Indian rupee, Brazilian real, Turkish lira, Indonesian rupiah, South African rand) — sold off sharply as carry trades unwound and capital fled back toward the dollar. These currencies had ripped during the post-2008 easing wave for the same reason crypto rips: they were the high-beta end of the global liquidity trade. And they cracked on the *expectation* of tightening for the same reason crypto cracks: when the tide is expected to drain, the leveraged top of the ladder retreats first. The taper tantrum is the cleanest pre-crypto template for the whole thesis of this post — the highest-beta read on the policy regime, this time in the FX market, reacting not even to a policy *change* but to a change in the *expected path*. The expectations channel, isolated.

## What it means for asset values: the playbook

Pull the threads into something you can act on. The two no-cashflow assets give you the cleanest, highest-beta read on the policy regime — and the highest-beta read is the most dangerous if you misjudge the turn.

**For crypto, watch two variables and only two: net liquidity and the real rate.** Net liquidity is the Fed balance sheet *minus* the Treasury General Account *minus* the reverse-repo facility (the two drains that can offset QT). When net liquidity is rising and the 10-year real yield is falling or deeply negative, the regime favors the top of the ladder — crypto and high-beta FX should run, and crypto should run hardest. When net liquidity is draining and the real yield is rising through positive territory, the regime is hostile — the top of the ladder breaks first, and leverage makes the break violent. The signal that invalidates a bullish crypto read is a *real-rate regime shift*: if the 10-year real yield is climbing hard and the central bank is draining, the highest-duration asset on the planet is the wrong place to be, no matter how compelling the narrative.

**For FX, trade the differential, not the level.** The currency to own is the one whose central bank is hiking *relative* to others; the currency to short is the one whose central bank is cutting *relative* to others. The magnitude that matters is the *change in the gap*, not either rate alone — a Fed hold while peers cut strengthens the dollar just as a Fed hike would. The signal to watch is the *convergence or divergence of policy paths*: when the gap is widening, carry and trend both pay; when it is narrowing, the trade can give back a year of carry in days (the August 2024 yen unwind). And remember the credibility overlay: a currency can fall *despite* a yield advantage if the market discounts the issuer's policy stability, as the dollar did in 2025.

**For the two together, they are your regime barometer and your risk dial.** Crypto and high-beta FX are the first assets to confirm an easing turn (they rip before the safer rungs) and the first to confirm a tightening break (they crack before the safer rungs). Use them as a leading read on where the policy regime is heading — but size them as the leveraged tail they are. The same property that makes them the cleanest read — no cash-flow anchor, infinite duration, maximum policy beta — makes them the assets that hand back the most when you are early or wrong. They are the high-octane layer: the most reward when the regime turns your way, the most pain when it turns against you, and always the loudest signal of which way the tide is running.

### Sizing the high-octane layer

The corollary of "highest beta" is "smallest position." Because crypto can move 50-75% on a single regime shift — and high-beta currencies 10-20% — the *same dollar* of crypto carries several times the risk of the same dollar of equities and many times that of bonds. Risk is measured in volatility, not in dollars at face value, so a portfolio that wants equal *risk* contributions from each rung of the ladder must hold a *much smaller* face position at the top. A useful discipline: decide how much you are willing to lose if the regime turns against you, divide by the plausible drawdown (assume 60-75% for crypto, 15-20% for high-beta FX), and let *that* set the position — not a round-number dollar allocation. The leveraged tail is a feature when the regime is with you and a portfolio-ender when it isn't, and the only defense is to size it as the tail it is.

The second discipline is to *watch the leverage*. The most reliable warning that the top of the ladder is about to break is not a macro forecast; it is the build-up of leverage in the system — record funding rates on perpetual futures, record open interest, a stretched stablecoin supply. Leverage is the accelerant that turns a tightening *drift* into a *crash*. When the regime is easy and leverage is record-high, the asymmetry has flipped against you even if the tide hasn't turned yet: the upside is rationed by how stretched positioning already is, and the downside is amplified by how much there is to liquidate. The FTX flush in 2022 was not caused by the Fed; it was *detonated* by the Fed and *amplified* by the leverage that the easy years had built. Read the regime for direction; read the leverage for how violent the move will be.

### Reading the regime in real time

Translate all of this into a short watch-list you could actually run. For the *liquidity* read: track net liquidity (Fed balance sheet minus the TGA minus the RRP) and the trend in stablecoin supply — rising net liquidity and expanding stablecoins favor the top of the ladder. For the *real-rate* read: track the 10-year TIPS yield — falling or negative favors crypto and gold; rising hard through positive territory is the single most reliable bearish signal for a no-cashflow asset. For the *FX divergence* read: track the *change* in the rate differential between the two currencies you care about (and the forward points that embed it), plus the regime of the dollar smile — is the world in a rate-differential regime (most of the time) or a safe-haven regime (a crisis)? And for the *credibility* overlay: watch whether the currency moves *with* or *against* its yield advantage — a currency falling despite a yield premium, or a "safe-haven failure" where the dollar and Treasuries sell off together, is the market pricing a credibility discount that no rate differential can offset. Run that list and you are reading the same machine from four angles — which is all the no-cashflow assets ever respond to.
