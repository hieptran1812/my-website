> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A central bank or regulator can move credit and property valuations without ever touching the policy rate, by pulling a non-rate lever — a capital rule, a borrower cap, a liquidity rule, a credit quota, or an emergency backstop — that changes the price and supply of bank credit.
>
> - **Capital rules are a tax on lending.** Raising a bank's required equity ratio from 8% to 12% cuts how many loan-dollars each capital-dollar can support from 12.5x to ~8.3x — about a third less credit per dollar of capital, before a single rate moves.
> - **Borrower caps price houses directly.** An LTV (loan-to-value) cap from 80% to 60% cuts a buyer's maximum house price by a quarter for the same down payment — a quantity lever aimed straight at property valuation.
> - **Backstops reprice an asset class overnight.** The March 2023 BTFP and the 2020 corporate-bond SPV both compressed an entire market on the *announcement*, before the Fed bought much of anything — credibility, not the checkbook, did the work.
> - **The one fact to remember:** regulation is monetary policy by other means. The policy rate sets the *price* of money; the non-rate levers set the *quantity and the terms* — and in places like Vietnam, the quantity lever (the credit quota) is the binding one.

On the morning of Friday, March 10, 2023, Silicon Valley Bank — the sixteenth-largest bank in the United States, the banker to half of America's venture-backed startups — was placed into receivership. The day before, depositors had tried to pull \$42 billion in a single day, the fastest bank run in modern history. Over the weekend, Signature Bank failed too. The fear was not subtle: every regional bank in the country held bonds bought in the zero-rate era that were now worth less than face value, and every uninsured depositor in the country could do the arithmetic. A classic run was metastasizing into a system-wide one.

The Federal Reserve did not cut interest rates. It had just hiked them to a 4.50–4.75% range and was about to hike again. Instead, on Sunday March 12, it announced the Bank Term Funding Program (BTFP): any eligible bank could pledge its underwater Treasuries and agency bonds and borrow against them **at par** — at the bonds' original face value, not their crashed market value — for up to a year. Alongside it, the discount window threw open. Within days, banks drew hundreds of billions; the Fed's balance sheet, which had been *shrinking* under quantitative tightening, jumped by roughly \$300 billion in a fortnight. The run stopped. No general rate cut, no QE program for the bond market — just a backstop, a non-rate lever, that re-anchored the price of an entire asset class (regional-bank deposits and the bonds behind them) practically overnight.

This is the part of policy that the rate-watchers miss. The federal funds rate is the lever everyone stares at, but it is one lever among many. Sitting beside it is an entire machinery — capital rules, reserve and buffer requirements, loan-to-value caps, liquidity ratios, deposit insurance, emergency lending facilities, and in some countries outright credit quotas — that moves the *supply, price, and terms* of credit directly. These are the **macroprudential and regulatory levers**, and the thesis of this post is simple: they are monetary policy by other means. Pull one, and you change what credit and property are worth, through the **bank-lending channel**, without the policy rate moving a single basis point.

## Foundations: how a non-rate lever reaches an asset price

Start from the thing the whole post is about: a **bank**. A bank is, at bottom, a leveraged bet on loans. It funds a big book of assets — loans, mortgages, Treasuries — almost entirely with *other people's money*: deposits, bonds, and short-term borrowing. The sliver it funds with its *own* money — the shareholders' stake — is called **capital** or **equity**. That sliver is the loss-absorber: if loans go bad, the losses eat into equity first, and only when equity is wiped out do depositors and bondholders take a hit. Capital is the shock absorber that stands between a bad loan and a bank run.

Here is the figure to keep in your head for the rest of this post: a bank's balance sheet, with the assets it owns on one side and the funding it used on the other, and capital as the thin green slice at the bottom of the funding stack.

![Bank balance sheet with assets on the left balancing deposits, debt, buffers, and a thin equity slice on the right](/imgs/blogs/macroprudential-and-regulatory-policy-2.png)

Now define the four objects a regulator can reach for.

**1. Capital requirements.** A rule that says: for every dollar of (risk-weighted) loans you hold, you must fund at least *X cents* of it with your own equity. Raise X and the bank either has to raise more equity (expensive) or hold fewer loans (less lending). This is the headline lever, and the modern global rulebook for it is **Basel III** — and its in-progress final tranche, nicknamed the **"Basel endgame"** in the US and **Basel IV** elsewhere.

**2. Borrower-based caps.** Rules aimed at the *borrower*, not the bank: a **loan-to-value (LTV)** cap says you may not lend more than, say, 80% of a property's value; a **debt-service ratio (DSR)** or **debt-to-income (DTI)** cap says a borrower's monthly debt payments may not exceed, say, 40% of their income. These caps put a ceiling on how big a mortgage anyone can get — and therefore, as we'll see, a ceiling on how much they can pay for a house.

**3. Liquidity rules.** Rules about how *survivable* the bank's funding is: the **Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR)** says you must hold enough cash-like assets to survive a 30-day stress run; the **Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR)** says you must fund long-term assets with stable, long-term money rather than flighty overnight borrowing. These don't change how much you can lend so much as how *expensively* you must fund it.

**4. Quantity and backstop levers.** At one extreme, a **credit quota** (or "credit room") simply tells each bank the maximum percentage by which its loan book may grow this year — a hard cap on quantity, used heavily in Vietnam and historically in many emerging markets. At the other extreme sit the **backstops**: **deposit insurance**, the **lender of last resort**, and **emergency facilities**, which don't restrict lending at all but instead *guarantee* the banking system against a run, putting a floor under the whole thing.

The common thread — the reason these all belong in one post — is the **transmission channel**. Unlike the policy rate, which works mostly through the *discount rate* (changing the rate at which all future cash flows are valued), these levers work through the **bank-lending channel**: they change how much credit banks can and will extend, and on what terms. Less credit, or pricier credit, means fewer buyers can bid for houses and riskier borrowers can't roll their debt — so property prices soften and credit spreads (the extra yield risky borrowers must pay) widen. More credit, or a credible backstop, means the opposite. The chain always ends at the same question this series keeps asking: *so what is the asset worth now, and why?*

If you want the policy-rate version of this story — how a change in the funds rate reaches markets through the discount-rate channel — that's the companion mechanism, and it's covered in [monetary-policy transmission](/blog/trading/macro-trading/monetary-policy-transmission-how-rate-changes-reach-markets). This post is the *other* channel.

### The bank-lending channel, step by step

It's worth slowing down on exactly *how* a rule about a bank's balance sheet ends up changing the price of a house or the yield on a corporate bond, because the chain is what makes regulation a market force rather than an accounting detail. The bank-lending channel has four links.

**Link one: the rule changes a constraint on the bank.** A higher capital ratio, a tighter LTV cap, a new liquidity buffer, a lower credit quota — each one tells the bank it may lend less, or must hold more low-yielding safety assets, or may only lend on stricter terms. The bank's *lending capacity* or its *cost of lending* moves.

**Link two: the bank passes the change into the price and availability of credit.** A bank doesn't absorb a new constraint out of charity; it protects its return on equity. If capital just got more expensive to hold against a loan, the bank raises the loan's interest rate (to earn back the extra capital cost) or simply declines to make it. So the *supply curve of credit* shifts in: less credit is offered at every rate, and the rate charged for risky credit rises. This is the step where a balance-sheet rule becomes a *price* — the price of borrowing.

**Link three: borrowers and asset markets feel the tighter credit.** A would-be homebuyer who can no longer get as large a mortgage bids less for the house. A speculative property developer who can't roll its bank loan or its bond is forced to sell assets or default. A leveraged firm that depended on cheap bank credit must pay a wider spread to refinance. Credit-dependent demand — for houses, for commercial property, for the bonds of marginal borrowers — falls.

**Link four: the asset reprices.** Lower credit-fueled demand means lower clearing prices for the assets that credit was buying. House prices soften. Cap rates rise (commercial property values fall). Credit spreads widen, because the marginal risky borrower is now closer to distress. Bank-equity multiples compress, because the banks themselves earn less on a smaller, safer book. And in the reverse direction — when a backstop is announced — every link runs the other way: the constraint loosens, credit flows, demand returns, and the asset reprices *up*, often violently and on the announcement alone.

The key property of this channel, and the reason it deserves a whole post separate from the rate channel, is that it can be aimed. A rate cut runs every link of the *price-of-money* channel for the entire economy at once. A risk-weight increase on commercial real estate runs the *bank-lending* channel for one sector. That selectivity is the whole point — and the reason the levers in this post can reprice one asset class while leaving the rest of the market untouched.

### Why a regulator would ever do this instead of moving rates

A natural question: if the central bank wants less lending, why not just raise rates? Because the policy rate is a blunt, economy-wide instrument. It cools *everything* — business investment, consumer spending, the labor market — to address a problem that might be concentrated in one place, like a housing bubble or a build-up of risky corporate credit. Macroprudential levers are *targeted*. An LTV cap cools the housing market without throttling factory investment. A higher capital charge on commercial real estate loans cools that one sector. A countercyclical buffer leans against a credit boom without forcing a recession. Economists call this having "the right tool for the right job": rates for the macroeconomy, macroprudential tools for financial stability. In practice the two interact, but the point stands — these levers exist precisely *because* the policy rate is too crude for some jobs.

## The capital lever: how a higher requirement shrinks and prices credit

Capital requirements are the most powerful non-rate lever because they sit at the very root of how much a bank can lend. To see why, we need two ideas: **risk-weighted assets** and the **capital ratio**.

A regulator doesn't make a bank hold equity against every dollar of assets equally. A loan to a blue-chip government is treated as near-riskless; a loan to a speculative property developer is treated as very risky. So each asset is multiplied by a **risk weight** — 0% for cash and many government bonds, 20% for high-grade banks, 50% for prime mortgages, 100% for ordinary corporate loans, and as high as 150% for the riskiest exposures. Sum it all up and you get **risk-weighted assets (RWA)**. The **capital ratio** is then simply:

> capital ratio = CET1 capital ÷ risk-weighted assets

where **CET1** ("Common Equity Tier 1") is the highest-quality capital — basically common shares and retained earnings, the purest loss-absorber. Basel III sets a *minimum* CET1 ratio of 4.5%, then piles required buffers on top: a 2.5% **capital conservation buffer**, an extra surcharge for the biggest "globally systemic" banks (G-SIBs), and the **countercyclical buffer** we'll meet shortly — so the effective floor for a large bank lands around 9–13%. The Basel endgame would push the effective requirements higher still for big US banks, which is exactly why the industry fought it so hard: a higher required ratio is, mechanically, a constraint on how much they can lend per dollar of capital.

Here is the arithmetic that makes capital a lever at all.

#### Worked example: raising the capital ratio from 8% to 12%

Suppose a bank has \$10 billion of CET1 capital. Its constraint is that capital must be at least the required ratio times its risk-weighted assets, so the *maximum* RWA it can carry is:

> max RWA = capital ÷ required ratio

At an **8%** required ratio, max RWA = \$10bn ÷ 0.08 = **\$125 billion**. That \$10 billion of capital supports \$125 billion of risk-weighted loans — a lending multiple of **12.5x**.

Now the regulator raises the requirement to **12%**. Max RWA = \$10bn ÷ 0.12 = **\$83.3 billion** — a multiple of just **8.3x**. The same \$10 billion of capital now supports about **\$42 billion less** lending — roughly a **33% cut** in credit capacity, and the policy rate hasn't moved.

The bank has only three ways out, and each tightens credit or raises its price. It can *raise new equity* — but equity is expensive (shareholders demand a high return, often 10%+), so the cost gets passed into loan pricing. It can *shrink its loan book* — directly cutting credit supply. Or it can *shift toward lower-risk-weight assets* — pulling back from the high-weight loans (speculative property, leveraged corporates) and crowding into government bonds. **A higher capital requirement raises the price of credit and shrinks its supply, especially for the riskiest borrowers — the exact borrowers whose spreads then widen.**

![Bar chart of maximum lending multiple per dollar of capital at capital ratios from 6 to 14 percent](/imgs/blogs/macroprudential-and-regulatory-policy-7.png)

The figure above makes the lever visible: the lending multiple is just 1 ÷ the capital ratio, so as the required ratio climbs from the pre-2008 6% world (16.7x) to a buffered 12% (8.3x), each dollar of capital does far less lending. The 2008 crisis happened in the 16x–30x world; the whole point of Basel III was to drag that multiple down — which is the same thing as permanently tightening credit supply.

It's worth being precise about *why* the bank can't simply shrug off a higher requirement by issuing more shares. Equity is the most expensive form of funding a bank has. Deposits cost almost nothing; senior bonds cost a few percent; but shareholders demand a double-digit return on the equity they put at risk — historically banks have targeted a return on equity (ROE) around 10–15%. So every extra dollar of equity the rule forces a bank to hold is a dollar that must earn that high required return, and the only place to earn it is by charging more on loans. A rough rule of thumb used in the industry: each additional percentage point of required capital raises loan pricing by something on the order of 5–15 basis points, because the bank has to spread the higher cost of its now-larger equity cushion across its loan book. Multiply that across a multi-point increase like the Basel endgame and you get a measurable, permanent widening in the cost of bank credit — a tightening delivered entirely through the regulatory channel.

There is also a direct effect on **what a bank is worth as a stock**, which is the valuation question this series cares about. A bank's equity value is driven by its ROE relative to its cost of equity: a bank that earns 15% on equity is worth a premium to book value; one that earns 8% trades at a discount. Raising the required capital ratio does two things to that calculation, both negative for the multiple. It *enlarges the denominator* — the bank now runs on more equity, so the same profit is spread over a bigger capital base, mechanically lowering ROE. And it *shrinks the numerator's growth* — a smaller, safer loan book generates less profit growth. This is exactly why bank stocks rallied when the US Basel endgame was watered down in 2024–2025 and sold off when the tougher version looked likely: the market was repricing future ROE, and therefore the price-to-book multiple, off the expected capital requirement. The capital lever moves bank-equity valuations as directly as an earnings revision.

There is a second-order effect worth naming, because it shows up in market prices. Because risk weights differ by asset, capital rules don't just shrink credit — they *steer* it. When the Basel endgame proposed sharply higher weights on certain mortgage and operational exposures, banks signaled they'd pull back from exactly those areas. So a capital rule is also a relative-value lever: it makes some kinds of lending more capital-expensive than others, repricing the spread between, say, jumbo mortgages and corporate loans. For the full legal and regulatory anatomy of how these rules are written and negotiated, see the law-and-geopolitics companion, [Basel III/IV: bank capital rules and the price of credit](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/basel-iii-iv-bank-capital-rules-and-the-price-of-credit). This post owns the *valuation* side: what the rule does to spreads and bank-equity multiples.

### Reserve requirements and the countercyclical buffer

Two close cousins of the capital lever deserve their own paragraph, because they're the levers a central bank reaches for to lean *against the cycle* rather than to set a permanent floor.

**Reserve requirements** are the oldest macroprudential tool: the central bank tells banks they must hold a minimum fraction of their deposits as reserves at the central bank, unavailable for lending. Raise the requirement and you drain lendable funds from the system; cut it and you flood them back in. In the US, the Fed cut the reserve requirement to *zero* in March 2020 and now manages liquidity through interest on reserves instead — so the classic reserve-ratio lever is dormant in America. But it is alive and central in **China**: the PBOC's **reserve requirement ratio (RRR)** is one of its main tools, cut by 50 basis points in both February and September 2024 to ease credit, each cut releasing roughly a trillion yuan of lendable funds. When the PBOC wants to ease without obviously cutting its policy rate, the RRR is how it does it quietly.

**The countercyclical capital buffer (CCyB)** is Basel III's purpose-built cycle lever. The idea is elegant: in a credit boom, when lending is racing ahead of the economy and risks are building, the regulator *raises* an extra capital buffer — typically 0 to 2.5% on top of the normal requirement — which leans against the boom by making credit more capital-expensive exactly when it's overheating. Then, when the cycle turns and a bust threatens, the regulator *releases* the buffer, freeing capital so banks can keep lending into the downturn instead of slamming the brakes. The Bank of England, for instance, has actively dialed its CCyB up and down across the cycle. It is the clearest statement of the macroprudential philosophy: lean against the credit cycle directly, with a capital dial, rather than waiting for the policy rate to do it bluntly.

#### Worked example: how a credit quota of +14% caps aggregate loan growth

The most extreme quantity lever skips capital ratios entirely and just *rations* credit. This is Vietnam's signature tool. The State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) sets each commercial bank an annual **credit-growth quota** — the maximum percentage by which its loan book may grow that year — and the system-wide quota becomes a hard ceiling on aggregate credit.

Suppose the banking system starts the year with **\$300 billion** of outstanding loans, and the SBV sets a **+14%** system-wide credit-growth target. Then the maximum *new* net lending the whole system may do all year is:

> max new credit = \$300bn × 14% = **\$42 billion**

That \$42 billion is the entire pool of new credit the economy gets — for mortgages, for business loans, for everything. If property developers want a bigger share, businesses get less. And here is the lever's teeth: the SBV doesn't just set the total; it can *steer* it. It can instruct banks to keep real-estate lending under a sub-cap, or to prioritize manufacturing and agriculture — quantity *and* allocation, in one move. A bank that hits its quota in October simply *stops lending* until January, no matter how creditworthy the next borrower. **A credit quota makes the quantity of credit a policy variable directly: the central bank decides how much new lending happens and, increasingly, which sectors get it — a far more direct grip on property valuations than any interest rate.** We'll see the 2022 version of this bite hard in the case studies. The SBV's full toolkit gets its own treatment in [the SBV toolkit: credit quotas, rates, and the managed dong](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-sbv-toolkit-credit-quotas-rates-and-the-managed-dong).

## The borrower lever: capping the house price, not just the loan

Capital and quota rules act on the *bank*. Borrower-based tools act on the *loan* — and they are the most surgically precise lever for one specific asset: residential property. The mechanism is almost embarrassingly direct, which is exactly why regulators love it.

A buyer's maximum purchase price is set by two things: how much cash they have for a **down payment**, and how big a **loan** the rules let them take. The loan-to-value (LTV) cap controls the second directly. If the cap is 80%, the loan can be at most 80% of the house value, which means the down payment must be at least 20%. Flip that around: for a *given* down payment, the maximum house price is the down payment divided by the required down-payment fraction. Tighten the LTV cap, and the maximum house price falls — for the same buyer, with the same savings.

#### Worked example: an 80% → 60% LTV cap cuts the maximum house price

Take a buyer who has saved **\$100,000** for a down payment.

**Under an 80% LTV cap**, the loan can be up to 80% of the price and the down payment must cover the other 20%. So the \$100,000 down payment is 20% of the maximum price:

> max house price = \$100,000 ÷ 0.20 = **\$500,000**

The buyer can bid up to half a million dollars (\$400,000 loan + \$100,000 down).

**Now the regulator tightens the cap to 60%** — a common move in overheating markets like Hong Kong or Korea. The down payment must now cover 40% of the price:

> max house price = \$100,000 ÷ 0.40 = **\$250,000**

The same buyer, with the same \$100,000, can now bid only **\$250,000** — half as much. Their *maximum loan* fell from \$400,000 to \$150,000, but the effect on what they can *pay* is even more dramatic because the leverage works both ways. **Tightening the LTV cap from 80% to 60% chopped this buyer's purchasing power in half — a quantity lever aimed straight at property valuation, with no rate change and no new tax.**

![Before and after of an 80 percent versus 60 percent LTV cap on a buyer's maximum house price](/imgs/blogs/macroprudential-and-regulatory-policy-3.png)

Now scale this up. If *every* buyer's maximum bid falls, the marginal bidder at every auction is weaker, and house prices soften across the board. This is why LTV and DSR caps are the textbook tool for cooling a housing bubble without inducing a recession: they reach the one asset class you're worried about and leave the rest of the economy alone. Korea and Hong Kong have for years run *tiered* caps — lower LTVs in hot districts, on second homes, on luxury price bands — dialing the screw exactly where prices are frothiest. The DSR/DTI version works the same way but binds through *income* rather than savings: if your monthly debt payments can't exceed 40% of income, then at a given interest rate there's a hard ceiling on your total mortgage, and therefore on your bid. Notice the interaction: when rates *rise*, a fixed DSR cap automatically shrinks the allowable loan (because more of each payment is interest), so the borrower lever and the rate lever reinforce each other on the way down. For how these property levers connect to cap rates and mortgage valuation more broadly, see [how policy prices real estate: cap rates and mortgages](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/how-policy-prices-real-estate-cap-rates-and-mortgages).

## The liquidity lever: LCR, NSFR, and the cost of being run-proof

The third lever is subtler and rarely makes headlines, but it sits right at the heart of the SVB story. After 2008, regulators concluded that capital alone wasn't enough — a bank could be perfectly solvent on paper and still die in a weekend if its funding ran faster than it could sell assets. So Basel III added **liquidity** rules.

The **Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR)** requires a bank to hold enough **high-quality liquid assets** (cash, central-bank reserves, top-grade government bonds) to cover the net cash it would lose over a hypothetical 30-day stress scenario. The intuition: you must be able to survive a month-long run on your own, without help. The **Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR)** attacks the other side — it requires that long-term, illiquid assets (like a 30-year mortgage) be funded with *stable* money (long-term deposits and bonds) rather than flighty overnight borrowing, so the bank isn't constantly rolling short-term funding to hold long-term assets.

These rules don't cap lending the way capital rules do; they make the *funding mix* more expensive. Holding a big buffer of low-yielding liquid assets, and funding with stable-but-pricier long-term money, both eat into a bank's **net interest margin** (NIM) — the gap between what it earns on assets and pays on funding. So the liquidity lever shows up in markets as a drag on bank profitability and a tilt toward holding government bonds, which is itself a price signal in the funding markets.

To make the NIM drag concrete: suppose a bank earns 6% on its loans and pays 2% on its deposits, for a gross margin of 4 percentage points. An LCR rule that forces it to park, say, 15% of its assets in government bonds yielding only 4% mechanically dilutes the blended asset yield — those 15 cents of every asset-dollar now earn 4% instead of 6%, knocking roughly 0.3 percentage points off the bank's overall asset yield and therefore off its margin. That doesn't sound like much, but on a large balance sheet it is hundreds of millions of dollars of lost profit a year, and it compounds with the capital drag. The market reads it directly: liquidity rules are part of why post-2008 bank ROEs settled structurally lower than the pre-crisis era, and that lower ROE is baked into the lower price-to-book multiples banks have traded at ever since. The lever doesn't restrict *how much* a bank lends; it taxes the *profitability* of the whole operation, and that tax sits in the equity price.

A subtle but important consequence: liquidity rules also push banks to hoard exactly the assets — government bonds and central-bank reserves — that the rules deem "high-quality and liquid." That structural demand for safe collateral is itself a market force. It compresses the yield on the safest government bonds (more forced buyers) and it can create scarcity of collateral in funding markets, occasionally showing up as spikes in repo rates when the supply of pristine collateral runs short. So even a rule that looks like pure prudential plumbing leaves fingerprints on the price of the world's safest assets.

The cruel irony of SVB is that it was *exempt* from the full LCR (US regulators had loosened the rules for mid-sized banks in 2018–2019), and it had loaded up on long-dated Treasuries funded by uninsured deposits that could leave overnight — exactly the maturity mismatch the NSFR was designed to forbid. When rates rose, the bonds fell in value and the deposits ran. The liquidity rules existed; they just hadn't been applied to SVB. That regulatory choice — who is *inside* the rule and who is exempt — was itself a lever, and it's a large part of why a mid-sized bank could blow up the way it did.

## The backstops: deposit insurance, the lender of last resort, and the bank put

Now we arrive at the most powerful non-rate lever of all — and the one that reprices assets fastest. The capital, borrower, and liquidity rules are all about *preventing* trouble by constraining lending in normal times. The backstops are about *stopping* a panic once it starts, and they work through a completely different mechanism: not by changing the quantity of credit, but by changing what everyone *believes* about the safety of the system.

Begin with the disease they cure: the **bank run**. A bank funds long-term loans with deposits that can be withdrawn on demand. As long as depositors trust the bank, only a few withdraw at a time and the bank is fine. But if enough depositors fear the bank might fail, the rational move for each individual is to withdraw *first*, before the cash runs out — and that very rush is what *causes* the failure. A bank that would have been perfectly solvent if everyone stayed calm is forced to dump assets at fire-sale prices to meet withdrawals, crystallizing losses and confirming the fear. It is a self-fulfilling prophecy, and it has two equilibria: everyone stays (the bank lives) or everyone runs (the bank dies). The whole purpose of the backstops is to *eliminate the bad equilibrium* — to make staying always the rational choice.

**Deposit insurance** does this for small depositors. A government agency (in the US, the FDIC) guarantees deposits up to a limit — \$250,000 per depositor in America. If your money is guaranteed, you have no reason to run, so insured depositors simply don't. Here is the run arithmetic that shows why the guarantee is so cheap and so powerful.

#### Worked example: how deposit insurance stops a run

Imagine a small bank with **\$1 billion** in deposits, of which **\$700 million** is insured (under the \$250k limit) and **\$300 million** is uninsured (big corporate accounts above the limit). The bank is fundamentally solvent — its loans are good — but a rumor spreads.

*Without insurance*, every depositor faces the same logic: if even a fraction of the others withdraw, the bank may run out of cash, and the last in line gets nothing. So the dominant strategy is to withdraw *now*. All \$1 billion is "runnable," and the bank, forced to fire-sell loans worth \$1 billion to raise cash immediately, might only get \$850 million for them — turning a solvent bank into an insolvent one. The run *created* a \$150 million loss that didn't exist before.

*With insurance*, the \$700 million of insured depositors have no reason to move — their money is guaranteed regardless. Only the \$300 million of *uninsured* deposits is runnable. The bank now needs to find at most \$300 million of liquidity, not \$1 billion — and it can pledge its good loans to the central bank to borrow that, rather than fire-selling them. **Deposit insurance shrank the runnable base from 100% of deposits to 30%, and converted a solvency-destroying fire sale into a manageable liquidity event — the guarantee did its work without the government spending a dollar, precisely because depositors believed it.**

But deposit insurance only covers small depositors, and modern runs (like SVB's) are runs of *uninsured* money — venture funds with \$50 million in the bank, far above the \$250k cap. That's where the **lender of last resort** comes in. The classic prescription, written by Walter Bagehot in 1873, is timeless: in a panic, the central bank should **lend freely, against good collateral, at a penalty rate**. The logic is exactly the run logic in reverse. If every solvent bank knows it can *always* turn its good assets into cash by borrowing from the central bank, then no solvent bank can be forced into a fire sale, so depositors have no reason to run on a solvent bank, so the runs stop. The mere *existence* of a credible lender of last resort prevents most of the runs it would ever have to fund.

![Backstop sequence from bad news and a deposit run through a fire-sale threat to a central-bank backstop and a calmed system](/imgs/blogs/macroprudential-and-regulatory-policy-5.png)

This is why the BTFP worked so fast in March 2023. By letting banks borrow against their underwater bonds *at par* — at face value, ignoring the market loss — the Fed told every regional bank: you will never be forced to sell these bonds at a loss to meet a withdrawal, because you can always pledge them here for full value instead. That promise removed the reason to run. The actual money lent mattered, but the *announcement* mattered more: it changed the equilibrium. Markets call this kind of guarantee a **"put"** — by analogy to a put option that protects you against a price falling below a floor. There is a "Fed put" for the stock market (the belief the Fed will ease if stocks crash badly enough) and, more concretely, a **Fed put for the banking system**: the standing promise that the lender of last resort will not let a solvent bank fail for lack of liquidity. The full legal anatomy of these backstops — what deposit insurance is, how the discount window and emergency facilities are authorized — is in the law-and-geopolitics companion, [deposit insurance, lender of last resort, and the anatomy of a bank run](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/deposit-insurance-lender-of-last-resort-and-the-anatomy-of-a-bank-run).

The same logic scales up from banks to whole asset classes, which is the 2020 corporate-bond story we'll come to next. And it connects directly to credit spreads: a credible backstop is, in effect, a cap on how wide spreads can blow out, because it removes the tail risk of a liquidity-driven collapse. That spread mechanism is the subject of [how policy moves credit spreads and the Fed put](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/how-policy-moves-credit-spreads-and-the-fed-put).

## Common misconceptions

**"Only interest rates are 'monetary policy'; capital rules are just paperwork."** This is the central error the post is written to correct. A capital requirement that cuts a bank's lending multiple from 12.5x to 8.3x removes a third of its credit capacity — a tightening at least as real as a rate hike, just aimed through the quantity-of-credit channel instead of the price-of-money channel. In countries with a credit quota, the quantity lever is often *more* binding than the policy rate. Treating these as administrative trivia is how analysts get blindsided by a credit crunch that the rate path never predicted.

**"Higher capital requirements just make banks safer; they don't affect markets."** They make banks safer *and* they tighten credit — those are two sides of one coin. The safety comes precisely *from* the bank lending less aggressively and funding more conservatively. When the Basel endgame was proposed, US bank stocks and credit markets repriced on the expectation of lower lending volumes and lower returns on equity. Safety and the credit-supply effect are inseparable; you can't have one without the other.

**"Deposit insurance and the lender of last resort cost taxpayers a fortune."** Usually they cost almost nothing, because they *prevent* the very losses they would otherwise have to cover. Deposit insurance stops runs before they start, so the insurance rarely pays out for solvency reasons. Bagehot lending is *secured* against good collateral at a penalty rate, so the central bank typically gets its money back with interest — the BTFP, in fact, *earned* the Fed interest and was fully repaid. The backstop's main "cost" is the moral-hazard incentive it creates (banks may take more risk knowing they're backstopped), which is exactly why the *constraining* levers — capital, liquidity, borrower caps — exist as the price of admission.

**"An LTV cap only affects people taking out new loans, so it can't move the whole market."** It moves the whole market through the *marginal buyer*. Property prices are set at auction by whoever is willing and able to bid the most. If the LTV cap lowers the maximum bid of *every* leveraged buyer, the entire bid stack shifts down, and prices clear lower — even for all-cash buyers, who now compete against weaker rivals. The cap doesn't have to touch you to reprice your house.

**"Reserve requirements are obsolete."** They're dormant in the US (cut to zero in 2020) but central in China, where the PBOC's RRR moves are among its most-watched policy signals. "Obsolete in my market" is not "obsolete everywhere" — and the moment you're looking at Chinese credit, the RRR is the lever to watch.

**"Backstops and constraints are separate, unrelated policies."** They're a matched pair, and you can't understand one without the other. A backstop creates **moral hazard**: if banks know the lender of last resort will catch them, they have an incentive to take more risk and hold less of their own capital. The constraining levers — capital requirements, liquidity rules, borrower caps — exist precisely to *price* that hazard, forcing banks to internalize the cost of the safety net they enjoy. So the right way to read the whole machinery is as a bargain: society offers the banking system a backstop (deposit insurance plus a lender of last resort) in exchange for accepting the constraints (capital, liquidity, supervision). When one side of the bargain weakens — when constraints are loosened, as they were for mid-sized US banks in 2018 — the backstop side ends up doing more work later, as the 2023 BTFP showed. The two are a single system, and a complete read watches the balance between them, not either piece alone.

## Case studies: when a non-rate lever repriced an asset class

Theory is cheap. Here are four real, dated episodes where a non-rate lever moved markets — sometimes overnight.

### Case 1: March 2023 — SVB, the BTFP, and the discount-window surge

We opened with this one; now the numbers. Silicon Valley Bank failed on March 10, 2023, after a \$42 billion deposit run in a single day; Signature Bank followed on March 12. The trigger was the maturity mismatch the liquidity rules were meant to prevent: SVB had funded a portfolio of long-dated Treasuries with uninsured, flighty deposits, and the 2022 hiking cycle (525 basis points, the fastest since Volcker) had driven those bonds well below face value. When depositors realized the bank was sitting on large unrealized losses, the run logic took over.

The policy response is the lesson. The Fed did **not** cut rates — it raised them again on March 22. Instead it deployed two non-rate backstops. The discount window saw record borrowing — roughly \$150 billion in a single week, the most ever. And the brand-new **BTFP** let banks pledge underwater bonds at par. Together they injected enough liquidity that the Fed's balance sheet, then shrinking under QT, *rose* by roughly \$300–400 billion within two weeks before resuming its decline.

![Fed balance sheet from 2007 to 2025 showing the COVID 2020 backstop spike and the 2023 bank-stress bump](/imgs/blogs/macroprudential-and-regulatory-policy-4.png)

The chart shows the fingerprint clearly: the giant 2020 hump (the COVID backstop, which we'll come to) and, on the downslope of QT in 2023, a visible bump — the discount-window-plus-BTFP surge. That bump is a non-rate lever made of pure liquidity. It re-anchored the price of regional-bank deposits and the bonds behind them within days, with the policy rate moving the *opposite* direction. If you only watched the funds rate, you would have completely missed the most important policy action of the quarter.

There's a regulatory back-story that makes SVB a perfect illustration of the whole post, because it shows the *absence* of the constraining levers as clearly as the presence of the backstop. In 2018–2019, US lawmakers and regulators rolled back parts of the post-2008 rulebook for banks in SVB's size class (between roughly \$100 billion and \$250 billion in assets): they were freed from the toughest stress tests, the full liquidity coverage ratio, and the requirement to count unrealized bond losses against their capital. So the very rules designed to catch a bank funding long-dated bonds with flighty deposits — the LCR, the NSFR, the stress scenarios — had been switched off for exactly this tier of bank. SVB then did precisely the thing those rules forbid, and when rates rose, the unrealized losses that didn't count against its capital became the run that killed it. The episode is therefore a two-part lesson: a *constraining* lever that was removed (the liquidity and capital rules SVB was exempted from) let the vulnerability build, and an *emergency* lever (the BTFP) was then needed to contain the fallout. Who is inside a rule and who is outside it is itself one of the most consequential policy choices a government makes — and it's invisible to anyone watching only the rate.

### Case 2: March 2020 — the corporate-bond backstop and the announcement effect

The single cleanest demonstration that a backstop reprices on *credibility*, not on the checkbook, came in spring 2020. As COVID hit, the corporate-bond market seized: spreads on investment-grade bonds blew out to crisis levels as everyone fled to cash, and even healthy companies couldn't roll their debt. On **March 23, 2020**, the Fed announced something it had never done before — it would buy corporate bonds (and even, later, some high-yield ETFs) through special-purpose vehicles, backstopped by the Treasury.

Here is the punchline. **Investment-grade credit spreads peaked the very day of the announcement and began compressing immediately — before the Fed had bought a single corporate bond.** The facilities took weeks to set up; by the time the Fed actually started buying in mid-May, spreads had already tightened dramatically. The Fed ultimately bought a tiny fraction of what it had authorized. The *promise* to be the buyer of last resort was enough: it told every investor that the downside tail — a liquidity-driven collapse in credit — had been removed, so it was safe to step back in. The announcement *was* the policy.

#### Worked example: what the spread compression was worth to a bondholder

Put numbers on why that announcement repriced the asset class. Investment-grade corporate spreads (the extra yield over Treasuries) had blown out to roughly **3.7%** (370 basis points) at the March 23 peak; over the following weeks they compressed back toward **1.5%** (150 basis points) — a tightening of about **220 basis points**.

For a bond, price moves opposite to yield, and the sensitivity is the **duration**. Take a representative investment-grade corporate bond with a duration of about **8 years**. The price change from a yield move is approximately:

> price change ≈ −duration × yield change = −8 × (−2.2%) = **+17.6%**

So a 220-basis-point spread tightening was worth roughly a **17–18% gain** on that bond's price — an enormous move for high-grade credit, which normally trundles along at low single-digit annual returns. **A single backstop announcement, requiring almost no actual Fed buying, handed corporate-bond holders a double-digit price gain by removing the collapse tail — the cleanest possible proof that for these levers the credible promise, not the purchase, reprices the asset.** This is the credit-spread version of the Fed put, and it's the textbook case of why, for these levers, **the threat is the weapon**. Mario Draghi's "whatever it takes" in 2012 did the same thing to European peripheral spreads with three words and a backstop (OMT) that was *never actually used*. Credibility, not bond-buying, did the work first.

### Case 3: China 2020–2021 — the "three red lines" and the developer crunch

The borrower-and-balance-sheet lever can also be turned the *hard* way — to deliberately deflate a bubble — and China's "three red lines" policy is the defining modern example. By 2020, Chinese property developers had borrowed colossally, and Beijing decided to force a deleveraging. In **August 2020**, regulators imposed the **"three red lines"**: developers were sorted by three balance-sheet metrics — a liability-to-asset ratio below 70%, a net-gearing ratio below 100%, and a cash-to-short-term-debt ratio above 1 — and a developer that breached all three was *forbidden from growing its debt at all*. Breach fewer, and you got a capped growth rate.

This was a capital-style constraint aimed at one sector, and it bit immediately. Developers that had grown on a treadmill of new borrowing suddenly couldn't roll their debt. The largest, **Evergrande**, with over \$300 billion in liabilities, slid toward default through 2021 and formally defaulted on offshore bonds that December. The pain spread across the sector — Country Garden and others followed — and Chinese high-yield property bonds lost the vast majority of their value. The lesson cuts both ways: a borrower/balance-sheet lever, applied forcefully, can reprice an entire asset class downward just as fast as a backstop can reprice one up. China used a regulatory dial, not an interest rate, to pop a property bubble — and the resulting credit event was one of the largest in history. This sits alongside the *easing* version of China's toolkit — the RRR cuts and the September 2024 stimulus package — as proof that the PBOC governs through quantity and balance-sheet rules far more than through a single policy rate.

### Case 4: Vietnam 2022 — the credit quota as the binding lever

Vietnam is the purest live laboratory for the quantity lever, and 2022 was its stress test. Three non-rate forces hit at once. First, the SBV's **credit quota** kept overall loan growth on a tight leash even as demand surged. Second — and this is the dramatic part — an **anti-fraud crackdown** on the corporate-bond market, triggered by the arrests in the Tan Hoang Minh and Van Thinh Phat cases, abruptly froze a funding channel that property developers had leaned on heavily. Overnight, developers who had been rolling bond debt found the market shut. Third, to defend the dong against a surging dollar, the SBV **hiked its policy rates by 200 basis points** in September and October and spent reserves — tightening domestic liquidity further.

The result was a market repricing on the scale of a crash. The **VN-Index fell roughly 33% in 2022**, from a high near 1,528 in April to a low around 874 in November — one of the worst years in its history — driven overwhelmingly by the property and financial sectors that depended on the credit and bond channels that had just been throttled. The dong still weakened, with USD/VND pushing from about 23,100 at end-2021 toward 23,633 by end-2022 despite the defense.

![Vietnam 2022 showing the VN-Index high to low collapse and the USD per dong depreciation](/imgs/blogs/macroprudential-and-regulatory-policy-6.png)

Notice what is *absent* from this story: there was no dramatic rate-cutting or rate-hiking cycle by Western standards. The 200bp of hikes mattered, but the *binding* levers were the credit quota and the bond-market crackdown — pure quantity controls. They decided how much credit flowed and to whom, and the market repriced the assets that depended on that credit. This is the cleanest possible illustration of the post's thesis: in a quantity-managed system, the non-rate levers *are* monetary policy, and they move asset valuations directly. The macro mechanics behind Vietnam's tools are detailed in [the SBV toolkit: credit quotas, rates, and the managed dong](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-sbv-toolkit-credit-quotas-rates-and-the-managed-dong).

## What it means for asset values: the playbook

Pull the four case studies together and a clean playbook emerges for reading the non-rate levers — and for knowing which asset reprices, in which direction, when one moves.

![Matrix of macroprudential levers against what they raise, what they cut, and the asset repriced](/imgs/blogs/macroprudential-and-regulatory-policy-8.png)

The toolkit matrix above is the one-page reference. Read it as: *which lever bites which valuation*. Here's how to use it in practice.

**When capital requirements rise (Basel endgame, a higher G-SIB surcharge):** expect bank-equity multiples to compress (lower return on equity), credit spreads on the riskiest, highest-risk-weight borrowers to widen (capital gets steered away from them), and overall credit growth to slow. The signal to watch is the *proposed risk weights*: where the weights rise most is where credit pulls back hardest. What would invalidate the read: a long phase-in period or a watered-down final rule (which is largely what happened to the US endgame), which blunts the effect.

**When a countercyclical buffer is raised:** read it as a deliberate lean against a credit boom — modestly bearish for cyclical credit and property, bullish for financial stability. When it's *released* in a downturn, read the opposite: the regulator is freeing banks to keep lending, a stabilizing signal for credit. The CCyB level is a published number; watch it directly.

**When borrower caps (LTV/DSR) tighten:** this is the most targeted property-bearish signal there is. Maximum bids fall, the marginal buyer weakens, and home prices soften in the affected segments — with the tightest caps (and the sharpest price effect) usually aimed at second homes, hot districts, and luxury bands. The magnitude is roughly proportional to the leverage cut: an 80%→60% cap is a far bigger shock than 80%→75%. What would invalidate it: a strong all-cash buyer base (luxury, foreign money) that doesn't borrow and so ignores the cap.

**When a credit quota is set or cut (Vietnam, emerging markets):** the quota *is* the credit forecast. A low quota directly caps how much new lending — and therefore how much property and equity-market fuel — the year gets, with the sectoral sub-caps deciding who's starved. Watch the headline number and the sectoral guidance together; they tell you which parts of the market get oxygen.

**When a backstop is announced (BTFP, a corporate-bond facility, "whatever it takes"):** this is the fastest and most violent repricing of all, and it goes the *bullish* way for the backstopped asset. Spreads compress on the announcement, often before any money is deployed — so the time to react is *immediately*, not after the buying starts. The 2020 corporate-bond case and Draghi's OMT both repriced on credibility alone. What would invalidate it: a backstop the market doubts is credible or large enough (a half-hearted facility can fail to move the equilibrium), in which case spreads keep widening despite the announcement.

One more framing makes the levers easier to read together: think of policy as a *mix* of price-of-money settings and quantity-of-credit settings. Through the 2023–2025 period, the United States ran a notably mixed stance — high policy rates (tight on the price of money) alongside large fiscal deficits and a regulatory pendulum that swung toward *loosening* the Basel endgame (looser on the quantity and cost of bank credit). The net effect on credit was far gentler than the headline rate suggested, precisely because the non-rate levers were leaning the other way. Reading only the funds rate, you'd have forecast a credit squeeze that the eased regulatory path partly offset. The lesson generalizes: the rate and the regulatory levers can reinforce each other or cancel out, and you only see the true stance of credit policy when you watch both dials at once.

The unifying lesson is the one we started with. The policy rate sets the *price* of money; the non-rate levers set its *quantity, its terms, and its safety net*. A complete read of policy watches both. An analyst who tracks only the funds rate will be repeatedly surprised — by a credit crunch the rate path didn't predict, by a property market that cooled with no hike, by a spread that compressed with no cut, by a 33% equity drawdown driven by a quota and a crackdown. Regulation is monetary policy by other means, and the levers in this post are how a government reprices credit and property without ever touching the rate that everyone is watching.
