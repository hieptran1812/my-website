> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Governments and central banks do not set stock prices, bond yields, or the gold price directly; they pull a *lever*, that lever travels through one of four *channels*, and the channel changes what a future stream of cash is worth today. Learn the map once and every headline — a rate cut, a bond-buying program, a deficit, a tax cut, a tariff — becomes legible.
>
> - The four levers: **the policy rate**, **the balance sheet (QE/QT)**, **fiscal policy** (spending, taxes, tariffs), and **FX & macroprudential rules**.
> - The four channels: **the discount rate** (the price of time), **the liquidity tide** (how much cash is chasing assets), **the currency** (the exchange rate), and **expectations/credibility** (whether the market believes the policymaker).
> - The outputs that reprice: **equity multiples, bond yields, credit spreads, property cap rates, gold, and exchange rates.**
> - The one number to remember: for a long-duration asset, a **+1 percentage-point** move in the discount rate can cut its fair value by **~15-20%** with no change in its cash flows at all. Policy moves prices mostly by moving that one number.

In the spring of 2022 the Federal Reserve raised its policy rate by 525 basis points — from a floor near zero to a range of 5.25-5.50% — in just sixteen months, the fastest tightening since Paul Volcker in the early 1980s. Nothing about the businesses in the S&P 500 changed that fast. Apple still sold iPhones; Exxon still pumped oil. And yet a classic "balanced" portfolio of 60% stocks and 40% bonds fell about 16% on the year, its worst result in a generation, because *both* stocks and bonds dropped together. The lever was monetary. The damage was universal.

Three years later, in April 2025, a completely different lever produced an eerily similar machine. On April 2 — branded "Liberation Day" — the U.S. announced a 10% universal import tariff plus steep "reciprocal" rates on dozens of trading partners, the highest average U.S. tariff since the 1930s. The S&P 500 fell almost 19% from its February peak to its April 8 trough; the 10-year Treasury yield, instead of falling the way it "should" in a panic, *spiked* roughly 50 basis points in a week to about 4.5%; the dollar dropped more than 8%; and gold tore to record highs. Then, on April 9, a 90-day pause was announced and the S&P ripped +9.5% in a single session. The lever this time was a tax on trade — fiscal, not monetary. The damage, again, was universal, and it ran through the very same channels.

That is the whole point of this series, and of this opening post. Two shocks, two completely different levers — a rate hike and a tariff — and yet both repriced *every* asset class because both reached markets through the same small set of channels. If you can hold that map in your head, you stop reacting to headlines one at a time and start seeing the machine. The figure below is the map; the rest of this post teaches you to read it.

## Foundations: levers, channels, and what an asset is worth

Before we trace a single shock, we need three plain-English ideas. Take them slowly; everything else in this series is built on them.

**Idea one: an asset is a claim on future money.** A share of stock is a claim on a slice of a company's future profits and dividends, stretching out for decades. A bond is a claim on a fixed schedule of interest payments plus your principal back at the end. An apartment building is a claim on future rent. Gold is the odd one out — it pays no cash at all — but even gold is priced *relative* to the future money you could have earned by holding something else. So almost every asset is, at heart, a promise of cash that arrives *later*.

**Idea two: money later is worth less than money now, and the "discount rate" is the exchange rate between them.** If someone offers you \$100 a year from now, you will not pay a full \$100 for it today, because you could put money in a safe place — a bank, a Treasury bill — and earn interest in the meantime. Suppose that safe return is 5%. Then \$100 a year from now is worth \$100 ÷ 1.05 ≈ \$95.24 today. That divisor, 5%, is the **discount rate**: it is the price of time. Crucially, the discount rate is built on top of what the central bank charges for short-term money. When the policy rate goes up, the discount rate on *everything* tends to go up, and the present value of *all* those future-money claims goes down. Hold this thought — it is the single most important mechanism in the entire series.

**Idea three: policymakers cannot set prices, but they can pull levers that move the discount rate and three other things.** A central bank does not phone the stock exchange and announce that Apple should trade at \$190. What it *can* do is change the cost of overnight money, buy or sell trillions in bonds, or change the rules banks operate under. A government does not set the gold price either, but it can run a deficit, cut a tax, or impose a tariff. Each of these is a **lever**. And each lever, no matter how different it looks, reaches markets through one of four **channels**. Let us name all four levers and all four channels precisely, because the rest of the series is organized around them.

### The four levers (who pulls what)

A **lever** is a concrete policy action a central bank or a government can take. There are four families, and almost every real-world policy is one of them or a blend.

1. **The policy rate.** The central bank sets the interest rate on overnight money between banks (in the U.S., the federal funds rate). This is the most direct lever and the one markets watch minute to minute. Raising it makes all borrowing more expensive and pushes the discount rate up; cutting it does the reverse. The Monetary Toolkit post in this series — [the monetary toolkit](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-monetary-toolkit-rates-qe-qt-and-forward-guidance) — takes this lever apart in full.

2. **The balance sheet (QE and QT).** When the policy rate is already near zero and the central bank wants to ease further, it *creates new money* and uses it to buy bonds — **quantitative easing (QE)**. This floods the system with cash and pushes longer-term yields down. The reverse, letting those bonds roll off and shrinking the balance sheet, is **quantitative tightening (QT)**. This lever works mainly through the *liquidity* channel, and we devote a whole post to it.

3. **Fiscal policy: spending, taxes, and tariffs.** This is the government's lever, not the central bank's. When the government spends more than it taxes, it runs a **deficit** and must borrow the difference by issuing bonds — which affects bond supply and yields. When it changes **taxes**, it changes companies' after-tax earnings and households' spending power directly. And a **tariff** is simply a tax on imports: it raises the cost of foreign goods, which is both a tax on importers' earnings and a one-time push to consumer prices. The 2025 tariff shock is the freshest example of this lever, and it gets its own deep-dive. See [the fiscal toolkit](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-fiscal-toolkit-spending-taxes-and-deficits) and [tariffs as a market force](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/tariffs-and-trade-policy-as-a-market-force).

4. **FX and macroprudential rules.** Some governments and central banks intervene directly in the currency market — buying or selling their own currency to move the exchange rate (Japan and China do this; the U.S. rarely does). And regulators set *macroprudential* rules: how much capital banks must hold, how much credit can grow, reserve requirements, loan-to-value caps on mortgages. These are quieter levers, but in places like Vietnam — where the central bank sets each bank's annual loan-growth quota — they are the *binding* lever, more powerful than the policy rate. The [law-and-geopolitics post on the SBV](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/sbv-monetary-and-banking-law-credit-quotas-and-the-dong) covers the legal side.

### The four channels (how the lever reaches the price)

A **channel** is the route a lever travels to reach an asset's price. There are four, and a single lever can travel through more than one at once — which is exactly why big policy shocks reprice everything.

- **The discount-rate channel.** The lever changes the rate at which future cash is discounted to today. Higher discount rate → lower present value → lower prices for stocks, bonds, and property. This is the channel for the policy rate, and we explore it in [the discount-rate channel](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-discount-rate-channel-how-rates-reprice-cash-flows).

- **The liquidity-tide channel.** The lever changes *how much cash is sloshing around looking for a home*. QE adds cash; QT and rate hikes drain it. When the tide is high, money pours into stocks, credit, and speculative assets and lifts their prices even when earnings haven't changed. When it goes out, the same assets sag. "A rising tide lifts all boats" is almost literally the mechanism.

- **The currency channel.** The lever changes the exchange rate. A weaker currency makes a country's exports cheaper abroad and its imports dearer, lifts the local-currency value of foreign earnings, and — critically — changes the value of gold and dollar-priced commodities. A *credibility-driven* currency move (a currency falling because the world doubts the policymaker) is one of the most important and underappreciated signals in markets.

- **The expectations/credibility channel.** This is the subtle one. Markets price not just what a policymaker *does* but what they are *expected* to do and whether the market *believes* them. Mario Draghi moved European bond markets enormously in 2012 with three words — "whatever it takes" — and a backstop he never had to use. Credibility is an asset; squander it and the currency channel and discount-rate channel both turn against you, as the UK discovered in 2022.

### From channel to a number: what "repricing" actually means

When we say a policy lever "reprices an asset", we mean something exact, not vague. We mean the *fair value* of that asset — the present value of its future cash — moves. For a stock, that shows up as a change in its **price-to-earnings multiple** (the P/E) and therefore its price. For a bond, it shows up as a change in its **yield** (and yields move opposite to prices). For property, it shows up in the **cap rate** (the yield a building offers; a higher cap rate means a lower price for the same rent). For gold and currencies, it shows up directly in the quoted price. Throughout this series we always finish on the same question: *so what is the asset worth now, and why?*

One more framing will pay off repeatedly. Notice that three of the four channels — discount rate, liquidity, currency — are things you can *measure directly*: a yield, a balance-sheet size, an exchange rate. The fourth, credibility, you have to *infer* from how the first three behave together. That is why, throughout this series, we keep returning to one master signal: the relationship between a country's bond yields and its currency. When they move in the *normal* relationship (yields up, currency up, because higher rates attract capital), policy is working through the mechanical channels and the market trusts it. When they *decouple* — yields up while the currency falls — the credibility channel has taken over, and that is the moment to pay the closest attention. Keep this in your pocket; it is the thread that ties the whole map together.

With the vocabulary in place, let us walk each lever and watch it travel through the channels to a number — starting with the most direct one of all.

## The policy rate: the lever you can see

The federal funds rate is the interest rate banks charge each other to borrow reserves overnight. It sounds technical and remote, but it is the foundation stone of every other interest rate in the economy. Mortgage rates, corporate borrowing costs, the yield on your savings account, the rate used to discount a company's future profits — all of them sit on top of the overnight rate the central bank controls. Move the foundation and the whole structure shifts.

The chart below is the U.S. policy rate over the last decade. Read it as a sequence of deliberate lever-pulls: near-zero through the 2010s and the 2020 COVID shock, then the violent 525-basis-point climb of 2022-23, then three cuts in 2025 as the Fed waited to see whether tariffs would show up as higher inflation or weaker jobs.

![Fed funds target rate step chart from 2015 to 2025](/imgs/blogs/how-policy-sets-asset-prices-the-transmission-map-2.png)

How does this single number reach a stock price? Through the discount-rate channel. Recall idea two from the Foundations: a stock is a claim on decades of future profits, and each year's profit must be discounted back to today. The discount rate is roughly the safe interest rate plus a risk premium. When the Fed lifts the policy rate, the safe rate climbs, the discount rate climbs, and every one of those future profits is now divided by a bigger number. The further out the cash flow, the harder it is hit — which is why fast-growing technology companies, whose value lies mostly in profits ten and twenty years away, fall hardest when rates rise. They are the *longest-duration* equities.

Let us make this concrete with the kind of arithmetic that runs underneath every analyst's model.

#### Worked example: a +1 point discount rate and a long-duration stock

Imagine a mature company expected to pay \$5 per share in cash next year, growing 4% a year forever. A standard way to value such a perpetual, growing stream is the Gordon growth formula: fair value = next year's cash ÷ (discount rate − growth rate).

At a discount rate of 8%: fair value = \$5 ÷ (0.08 − 0.04) = \$5 ÷ 0.04 = **\$125 per share.**

Now the central bank tightens and the discount rate rises by one point to 9%, with the company's cash flows completely unchanged: fair value = \$5 ÷ (0.09 − 0.04) = \$5 ÷ 0.05 = **\$100 per share.**

A single percentage point on the discount rate cut the stock's fair value from \$125 to \$100 — a **20% decline** — even though next year's dividend, the growth rate, and the business itself never moved. The intuition: when the gap between the discount rate and the growth rate narrows or widens, a long stream of cash is extraordinarily sensitive to it, because that small gap is the denominator dividing an infinite future.

![Before and after a one point discount rate increase on a stock](/imgs/blogs/how-policy-sets-asset-prices-the-transmission-map-5.png)

That \$125 → \$100 move *is* the discount-rate channel. It is why a hawkish central-bank meeting can knock 2% off the stock market in an afternoon without a single earnings report being released. The earnings did not change; the rate at which we value them did. (The macro-correlations series quantifies how tightly stocks track this front-end rate path — see [the Fed funds path and front-end correlation](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/the-fed-funds-path-and-front-end-correlation).)

The same channel runs through bonds even more mechanically. A bond's price is just the present value of its fixed coupons and principal. Raise the discount rate and that present value falls — which is the entire reason bond prices drop when yields rise. And it runs through property: an apartment building throwing off \$60,000 of rent a year, valued at a 5% cap rate, is worth \$1.2 million; push the cap rate to 6% (because safe rates rose and investors demand more) and the same building is worth \$1.0 million. One lever, three asset classes, one channel.

#### Worked example: a rate move repricing an apartment building

Property is the asset class where the discount-rate channel is easiest to *see*, because real estate is quoted in a number that is almost literally a discount rate: the **cap rate**, which is annual net rent divided by the building's price. A 5% cap rate means the building costs 20 times its annual rent; a 6% cap rate means it costs about 16.7 times.

Take a small apartment building generating \$60,000 a year in net rent. At a 5% cap rate — typical when safe interest rates are low — its value is \$60,000 ÷ 0.05 = **\$1,200,000.**

Now safe rates rise (the policy rate went up, lifting Treasury yields), and property investors, who could now earn more in risk-free bonds, demand a higher yield from buildings too. The cap rate rises to 6%. With the rent unchanged at \$60,000, the building is now worth \$60,000 ÷ 0.06 = **\$1,000,000.**

A one-point move in the cap rate — driven entirely by the policy rate, not by anything happening to the tenants or the rent — cut the building's value by \$200,000, about **17%**. The intuition: real estate is a bond made of bricks; its price is the present value of future rent, and when the discount rate built into the cap rate rises, the price falls just as a bond's does. This is why commercial real estate struggled badly through the 2022-23 tightening even where rents held up: the *denominator* moved.

### Why "duration" decides who gets hurt most

There is one more idea inside the discount-rate channel that explains *which* assets move the most, and it is worth a moment because it makes a lot of otherwise-confusing market behavior obvious. The idea is **duration** — not the calendar life of an asset, but how far into the future its cash flows are weighted. The further out an asset's money sits, the more times it gets divided by the discount rate, and the more violently its present value reacts when that rate moves.

Think about two stocks. The first is a slow, cash-rich utility that pays most of its value out as dividends in the next few years. The second is a young software company that pays nothing now and is valued almost entirely on profits investors expect in 2035 and beyond. When the discount rate rises by a point, the utility's near-term dividends are barely touched — they get divided by a slightly bigger number, but only once or twice. The software company's distant profits get divided by that bigger number raised to the tenth or fifteenth power, which is a much larger haircut. This is exactly why, in 2022, the high-growth, no-profit technology names fell 50-80% while staid dividend payers fell single digits. Same lever, same channel, wildly different damage — and duration is the dial that explains the difference.

The same logic applies across asset classes, which is why it is so powerful. A 30-year Treasury bond has far more duration than a 2-year note, so the long bond's price falls much more for the same rise in yields. A growth-stage venture investment has more duration than a mature dividend stock. A trophy office tower bought at a low cap rate (priced for decades of rent growth) has more duration than a cheap building bought for immediate cash yield. Whenever a policy lever moves the discount rate, ask one follow-up question — *how long-duration is the asset?* — and you will know, before the prices even print, which corner of the market is about to take the worst of it.

## The balance sheet: the liquidity tide

When the policy rate is already at zero, the central bank cannot cut further in the ordinary way. So it reaches for a second lever: it creates new money and buys bonds in size. This is **quantitative easing**. The Fed's balance sheet — the total value of bonds and other assets it holds — tells the story of this lever directly. It sat near \$0.9 trillion before the 2008 crisis, climbed to \$4.5 trillion through three rounds of QE in the 2010s, then exploded from about \$4.2 trillion to a peak of nearly \$9 trillion during the COVID response of 2020-22, before QT began grinding it back down toward \$6.5 trillion by 2025.

QE works mainly through the *liquidity* channel rather than the discount-rate channel, and the distinction matters. When the central bank buys a trillion dollars of Treasuries from investors, those investors now hold a trillion dollars of cash instead of bonds. Cash earns little, so they go looking for something with a return — corporate bonds, stocks, real estate, riskier assets. That wall of money chasing a roughly fixed pile of assets pushes prices up. Earnings need not improve at all; the *multiple* people are willing to pay expands because the alternative — sitting in cash — is so unrewarding. This is why the QE decade saw stock valuations climb even when corporate profit growth was modest.

#### Worked example: QE lifts the multiple without lifting earnings

Take a company earning \$5 per share, trading at a price-to-earnings multiple of 16. Its price is 16 × \$5 = **\$80 per share.**

Now the central bank launches a large QE program. The flood of cash drives investors out of bonds and into stocks; the *required return* on equities falls because the safe alternative pays so little, and the market re-rates the whole asset class. The company's earnings are still exactly \$5 — not a cent more — but investors are now willing to pay a multiple of 20. Its price becomes 20 × \$5 = **\$100 per share.**

The stock rose 25%, from \$80 to \$100, on *zero* earnings improvement. Every dollar of that gain came from multiple expansion, and the multiple expanded because the liquidity tide came in. The intuition: QE doesn't make companies more profitable; it makes investors pay more for the same profits, because cash has become a worse place to wait.

This is also why QE is double-edged. The gains are real on paper, but they are *valuation* gains, not *earnings* gains — and they can reverse just as fast when the tide goes out. That reversal is precisely what QT and rate hikes do, and it is a major reason 2022 was so brutal: the discount-rate channel and the liquidity channel turned against stocks *simultaneously*. The macro-trading series details how traders position around this in [QE vs QT: how balance-sheet policy moves markets](/blog/trading/macro-trading/qe-vs-qt-how-balance-sheet-policy-moves-markets).

The liquidity tide has a second, quieter output that is one of the best early-warning signals in markets: the **credit spread**. A credit spread is the extra yield a company must pay to borrow above what the government pays for the same maturity — it is the market's price for the risk that the company might not repay. When the liquidity tide is high, investors are hungry for yield and will lend to risky borrowers for very little extra; spreads compress, and even shaky companies can refinance cheaply. When the tide goes out — rate hikes and QT drain the cash — investors get choosy, demand much more to lend, and spreads blow out. A company that could roll its debt at a 2-point spread suddenly faces a 6-point spread, its interest bill balloons, and the weakest borrowers can't refinance at all. This is the channel through which tight policy eventually *breaks* something: the March 2023 regional-bank stress and the steady drip of corporate defaults in a tightening cycle are spreads doing their work. Watch credit spreads, not just stock prices — they often turn first, because they price the *availability* of the liquidity tide directly.

## Fiscal policy: deficits, taxes, and the tariff lever

Now we cross from the central bank to the government, whose levers are spending, taxes, and tariffs. These reach markets through different channels — sometimes the discount rate (via bond supply), sometimes earnings directly (via taxes), sometimes credibility (when the deficit looks unsustainable).

Start with **deficits**. When a government spends more than it collects in tax, it borrows the gap by selling bonds. More bonds for sale, all else equal, means their price has to fall — and a lower bond price is a higher yield — to attract enough buyers. Economists call the extra yield investors demand for holding all that supply the **term premium**. So a bigger deficit can push up long-term yields, which then feeds back into the discount rate for everything else.

#### Worked example: a deficit, bond supply, and the term premium

Suppose the government must finance an extra \$2 trillion of borrowing this year on top of what was already planned. The bond market can absorb it, but only at a price. Investors who were content to hold 10-year Treasuries at a 4.0% yield now face a much larger pile of supply, and they demand compensation — say an extra 0.3 percentage points of term premium — pushing the 10-year yield to 4.3%.

That 0.3-point rise is not confined to government bonds. The 10-year Treasury yield is the benchmark off which corporate borrowing, mortgage rates, and the equity discount rate are all set. So a long-duration stock that was fairly valued at a discount rate built on a 4.0% safe rate now faces a discount rate built on 4.3%. Using the Gordon math from earlier — cash \$5, growth 4% — fair value falls from \$5 ÷ (0.085 − 0.04) = \$111 at an 8.5% discount rate to \$5 ÷ (0.088 − 0.04) = \$104 at 8.8%, roughly a **6% repricing**, all triggered by the government's borrowing, not by the central bank at all. The intuition: a deficit is a supply shock in the bond market, and the bond market is the foundation everything else is priced on.

The 2025 **One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA)**, signed July 4, 2025, is the live example. It permanently extended the 2017 tax cuts and added new ones, and the Congressional Budget Office and Joint Committee on Taxation estimated it would add roughly \$3.4 trillion to deficits over a decade (some conventional estimates including interest run closer to \$4.9 trillion). Bigger deficits, more bond supply, upward pressure on the term premium — the chain runs exactly as the worked example describes. The fiscal-toolkit post takes OBBBA apart in detail.

Now **taxes**, which can hit earnings directly. A corporate tax cut raises after-tax profits dollar for dollar; the 2017 cut from a 35% to a 21% corporate rate did exactly this and was a meaningful tailwind for U.S. earnings. A tax *increase* does the reverse. And the most market-relevant tax of 2025-26 has been a tax most people don't even call a tax: the **tariff**.

A tariff is a tax on imports. When a U.S. company imports \$1,000 of components and a 20% tariff applies, it pays \$200 to the government at the border. That \$200 is a direct cost. The company has three bad choices: eat the cost (margins fall), raise its price (it sells less, or stokes inflation), or find a new supplier (slow and costly). In every case, earnings per share take a hit — and since a stock's price is its multiple times its earnings, the price falls even if the multiple holds.

The chart below shows the tariff lever moving in 2025. The average effective U.S. tariff rate jumped from about 2.4% in 2024 to roughly 12% by April 2025 and peaked near 17% — the highest since the Smoot-Hawley era of the 1930s.

![Average US tariff rate step chart 2024 to 2026 highest since the 1930s](/imgs/blogs/how-policy-sets-asset-prices-the-transmission-map-6.png)

#### Worked example: a tariff as a tax on an importer's EPS

Consider a retailer that imports most of its goods, earning \$5.00 per share and trading at a price-to-earnings multiple of 18, so its stock is 18 × \$5.00 = **\$90 per share.**

A 20% tariff lands on its imported inputs. Suppose, after the cost passes through its supply chain, the net effect is to cut earnings per share by 12%, from \$5.00 to **\$4.40** (the company can't pass the full cost to customers without losing sales, so it eats part of it). If investors still apply the same 18 multiple — they are not panicking about the *quality* of the business, just its near-term earnings — the new price is 18 × \$4.40 = **\$79.20**, call it \$79.

The stock fell from \$90 to \$79, about **12%**, purely because a tax raised its costs. The intuition: a tariff is a tax that shows up in the cost line, and because price equals multiple times earnings, anything that cuts earnings cuts the price even when the multiple never moves.

![Tariff as a tax on an importer earnings per share pipeline](/imgs/blogs/how-policy-sets-asset-prices-the-transmission-map-7.png)

But the 2025 tariff shock did something more than dent importers' earnings — it travelled through the *credibility* channel too, which is why it repriced bonds, the dollar, and gold all at once. We will come to that in the case studies.

## FX and macroprudential rules: the currency channel

The fourth lever family is the quietest in the U.S. but dominant elsewhere. A central bank or finance ministry can intervene directly in the currency market, and regulators can set the rules that govern how much credit flows. Both reach markets through the currency channel and, often, the credibility channel.

The classic example of *intentional* currency policy is the 1985 Plaza Accord, when the U.S., Japan, West Germany, France, and the UK agreed to coordinate and push down an overvalued dollar; the yen roughly doubled against the dollar over the next two years. The classic example of *defensive* currency policy is China's daily management of the yuan within a band, or Japan's occasional interventions to slow the yen's fall. And the most important macroprudential lever for an emerging market like Vietnam is the **credit growth quota** — the State Bank of Vietnam literally tells each bank how much its loan book may grow each year, a more binding lever on the economy than any single interest rate.

Why does the currency channel reprice assets? Three reasons. First, a weaker currency lifts the local-currency value of a multinational's foreign earnings, helping exporters and hurting importers. Second, it changes the price of dollar-denominated commodities and gold for foreign buyers. Third — and most importantly for this series — a currency that falls *because the world is losing faith in the policymaker* is a credibility signal that bleeds into every other channel. When a currency drops at the same time as its government's bond yields *rise*, that is the market refusing to fund the policy at the old price. It is the single clearest tell that the credibility channel has turned, and it is exactly what we saw in both the UK in 2022 and the U.S. in 2025.

For an export-led economy, this lever is not a sideshow — it is the main event. Take Vietnam, which sends nearly 30% of its exports to the United States. When the 2025 tariffs hit, Vietnam faced one of the highest "reciprocal" rates announced — 46% — before negotiating it down to 20% in a July 2 deal. For a country whose growth runs through its export factories, a tariff that size is a direct shock to the earnings of its largest companies *and* a force on its currency: the State Bank of Vietnam manages the dong within a band around a daily central rate, and a trade shock pressures that band. The SBV's most powerful domestic lever, meanwhile, is not its policy rate at all but the **credit growth quota** — the ceiling it sets on how much each bank's loan book may grow each year. That quota is a macroprudential lever with no direct U.S. equivalent, and it moves the Vietnamese economy and its stock market more than any single interest-rate decision. We devote a full post later in the series to the Vietnam track, because it shows the same map running with a different set of dominant levers.

### The credibility channel: the one that breaks the others

The first three channels — discount rate, liquidity, currency — are mechanical. You can write the equation, plug in the lever, and read off the price. The fourth channel, **expectations and credibility**, is different, and because it is harder to quantify it is the one most beginners underweight. It deserves its own treatment, because when it turns, it overrides the other three.

Credibility is whether the market *believes* the policymaker will do what is needed and *can* do it. A central bank with credibility can move markets with words alone, before it spends a dollar. The cleanest demonstration in history is Mario Draghi, then president of the European Central Bank, on July 26, 2012, at the depth of the euro crisis. Spanish 10-year yields had blown out to about 7.6% and Italian yields to 6.6% — levels that, if sustained, would have bankrupted those governments and broken the euro apart. Draghi said the ECB would do "whatever it takes to preserve the euro", and announced a bond-buying backstop (Outright Monetary Transactions). The backstop was *never actually used* — not a single bond was bought under it. And yet Spanish yields fell from 7.6% toward 1.6% over the next two years. Three words and a credible promise did the work that trillions of euros might otherwise have required. That is the credibility channel running in the *helpful* direction.

Now run it in reverse, and you see why it is the dangerous one. When a government does something the market judges reckless — large unfunded tax cuts, a tariff regime that threatens to reignite inflation, a deficit that looks unsustainable — the market does not wait politely. It *reprices the policymaker*. And the tell is unmistakable: the country's bond yields *rise* (investors demand more to fund the policy) *at the same time* as its currency *falls* (capital leaves) and its stocks *fall* (the real economy is threatened). In a normal shock, at least one of those moves the other way — bonds rally when stocks fall, or the currency holds when bonds sell off. When *all* of them move against the country together, the credibility channel has turned, and no amount of the other three channels will save you until trust is restored. This is the single most important pattern in this entire post to recognize, and it is exactly what played out in the UK in 2022 and the U.S. in 2025 — both of which we trace in the case studies below.

That brings the four channels together into a single, powerful framework: the policy mix.

## The policy mix: when levers combine

In the real world, the monetary lever and the fiscal lever are pulled at the same time, and the *combination* — easy or tight money, easy or tight fiscal — defines the regime markets live in. The 2×2 below is worth memorizing, because it tells you, at a glance, what the same set of assets tends to do in each world.

![Policy mix quadrants of monetary and fiscal stance](/imgs/blogs/how-policy-sets-asset-prices-the-transmission-map-3.png)

- **Easy money + easy fiscal (top-left): reflation.** The central bank is cutting or doing QE *and* the government is spending freely. This is the most powerful risk-on regime: stocks, credit, gold, and speculative assets all rise. 2020-21 was the textbook case — zero rates, unlimited QE, and trillions in fiscal support produced the "everything rally".

- **Easy money + tight fiscal (top-right): a financial-asset boom on weak demand.** The central bank is supportive but the government is austere. Money pours into financial assets — multiples expand, as we saw in the QE worked example — but the real economy stays sluggish. Much of the 2010s looked like this.

- **Tight money + easy fiscal (bottom-left): the 2023-25 mix.** High policy rates *and* large deficits at the same time. This is an unusual, tense combination: the discount-rate channel pushes asset prices down while the fiscal channel pumps demand and bond supply up. The result tends to be sticky long-term yields and a strong dollar at first — but, if the deficits start to look unsustainable, the credibility channel can turn and the currency can weaken even with high rates, which is part of what happened in 2025.

- **Tight money + tight fiscal (bottom-right): disinflation and recession risk.** Both levers restrictive. This is the Volcker regime of the early 1980s: brutal for risk assets and the economy in the short run, but it breaks inflation and sets up the long bull market in bonds that follows. Defensive stocks and long-duration bonds are the assets that work.

The mix is not academic. Knowing which quadrant you are in tells you which channel is dominant and therefore which assets are most exposed. The capstone of this series — [the policy-to-asset playbook](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-policy-to-asset-playbook-capstone) — turns this quadrant map into a working checklist.

## Common misconceptions

A few beliefs sound reasonable but get the transmission map exactly wrong. Each is worth correcting with a number.

**"The central bank sets the stock market, so a rate cut always lifts stocks."** Not reliably. A cut works through the discount-rate channel, which *should* help — but if the cut signals that the economy is falling apart (the credibility/expectations channel), stocks can drop on a cut. In 2007-08 the Fed cut aggressively and stocks fell anyway, because the cuts confirmed how bad things were. The lever and the channel both matter; never read the lever alone.

**"Treasuries always rally in a crisis, so bonds are a safe hedge."** Usually, but not when the crisis *is about policy credibility*. In April 2025, during a sharp equity selloff, the 10-year Treasury yield *rose* about 50 basis points in a week — bonds fell *with* stocks — because the shock was a self-inflicted policy shock that raised inflation fears and dented faith in U.S. assets. The "flight to safety" failed precisely because the policy was the source of the fear. A hedge that depends on a channel can break when that channel is the problem.

**"Money printing (QE) must cause runaway inflation."** Over 2008-2019 the Fed's balance sheet grew roughly five-fold and inflation stayed *below* the Fed's 2% target most of the time. QE primarily lifts *asset* prices through the liquidity channel; whether it lifts *consumer* prices depends on whether the money reaches the real economy and on capacity. The 2021-22 inflation that did arrive came from a *combination* of QE *and* enormous direct fiscal transfers *and* supply shocks — not from QE alone.

**"A tariff is paid by foreign exporters, so it doesn't hurt domestic markets."** The tariff is paid at the importer's border, by the domestic company bringing the goods in — as the EPS worked example showed, it lands squarely on domestic earnings (and often on domestic consumer prices). The 2025 tariff shock knocked nearly 19% off U.S. stocks at the trough; that is not a foreigner's problem.

**"Deficits don't matter because the central bank can always buy the bonds."** They can, and sometimes do — but doing so risks the credibility channel. The UK in 2022 is the cautionary tale: when a government announced large unfunded tax cuts, the bond market revolted in *days*, and the central bank had to step in with emergency buying just to prevent a pension-fund doom loop. Deficits are constrained by what the market will fund at an acceptable price.

## Case studies: two shocks, two levers, one machine

Now we run the machine on real, dated events. Both of these get full deep-dives later in the series; here we use them to *see the map work* end to end.

### 2022: the monetary lever (the 60/40 wreck)

Through 2021, U.S. consumer-price inflation climbed from under 2% to a 40-year high of about 9% by mid-2022 — the direct hangover of the 2020-21 reflation regime, where zero rates, unlimited QE, and trillions in fiscal transfers met pandemic supply shocks. The Fed, having been "behind the curve", responded with the fastest tightening since Volcker: from a near-zero floor in March 2022 to a 5.25-5.50% range by July 2023, 525 basis points in sixteen months across eleven hikes. The sequence is itself a lesson in the machine: the *easy*-money/easy-fiscal lever of 2020-21 produced the inflation that forced the *tight*-money lever of 2022, which then repriced everything back down. Levers cause levers.

Trace it through the map. The **lever** was the policy rate. The dominant **channel** was the discount rate. Higher policy rate → higher discount rate on every future cash flow → both stocks and bonds reprice *down at the same time*. This is the crucial and counterintuitive part: in a normal year, bonds rise when stocks fall, so the 60/40 portfolio is cushioned. But 2022 was not a growth scare — it was a *discount-rate* shock, and the discount rate is the denominator for *both* asset classes. So they fell together: the S&P 500 dropped about 19.4%, the aggregate bond index fell about 13%, and the classic 60/40 portfolio lost roughly 16% — one of its worst years on record. The 10-year Treasury yield rose from under 2% to about 4.3%. And in March 2023, the same higher rates that repriced everything also cracked Silicon Valley Bank, whose long-dated bond portfolio had lost value exactly as the bond-pricing channel predicts.

The lesson of 2022 is the purest demonstration of the discount-rate channel in the dataset: when the *denominator* moves, diversification across asset classes that all share that denominator does not save you. We deep-dive this in [the discount-rate channel](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-discount-rate-channel-how-rates-reprice-cash-flows).

### 2025: the fiscal/trade lever (the tariff shock)

Three years later, a different lever, the same machine. The setup: through early 2025 the Fed was *on hold*, having cut to a 4.25-4.50% range in late 2024 and then waiting. The shock came from the government, not the Fed.

The timeline is worth having in front of you, because the market's reaction tracked the policy headlines almost tick for tick.

- **February 2025:** the first new tariffs (on China, Mexico, Canada) push the average U.S. tariff rate up toward 3%.
- **April 2, 2025 — "Liberation Day":** a 10% universal tariff plus steep "reciprocal" rates on 57 partners are announced, the highest average U.S. tariff since the 1930s. The S&P had already slipped from its February 19 record of 6,144.
- **April 3-8:** markets crater. The S&P falls to 4,983 by April 8 — down 18.9% from the February peak, including a four-day drop of about 12%. But here is the tell: the 10-year Treasury yield, instead of falling in the panic, *rose* — climbing toward 4.5%, up roughly 50 basis points in a week. And the dollar *fell* more than 8% over the first half of the year, its worst start since 1973. Gold, meanwhile, ran to records.
- **April 9:** a 90-day pause on the reciprocal tariffs (for all partners but China) is announced. The S&P rips **+9.5% in a single session**, one of the largest one-day gains in history.
- **May-July 2025:** de-escalation with China, then a series of partner deals (Vietnam 46% → 20% on July 2; the EU and Japan to 15%). The S&P erases its year-to-date loss by mid-May and closes 2025 around 6,680.

![S&P 500 2025 drawdown and recovery around the tariff shock](/imgs/blogs/how-policy-sets-asset-prices-the-transmission-map-4.png)

Now trace it through the map, channel by channel, because 2025 is the rare event that lit up *all four at once*:

- **The discount-rate / earnings channel.** Tariffs are a tax on importers' earnings (the EPS worked example) and a one-time push to consumer prices, which raises inflation fears and the discount rate. Equity multiples *and* earnings expectations fell together. Stocks down.
- **The liquidity channel** stayed roughly neutral — the Fed didn't flood or drain — which is why this was a *valuation and earnings* shock rather than a liquidity one.
- **The currency channel.** The dollar fell more than 8%. Some of that is the ordinary effect of a growth scare, but the *scale* and the simultaneity with rising yields point to something more.
- **The credibility/expectations channel.** This is the signature of 2025 and the reason it looked so strange. Normally, in a U.S. risk-off event, money floods *into* Treasuries and the dollar *rises* — the classic flight to safety. In April 2025 the opposite happened: Treasury yields rose and the dollar fell *at the same time as* stocks fell. That combination — sell the stocks, sell the bonds, sell the dollar, buy gold — is the market questioning the policy itself, not just the growth outlook. Gold's record run (below) is the same message from a different instrument: when faith in policy-managed paper assets wobbles, the asset with no policymaker behind it bid up.

![Gold record run to all-time highs USD per ounce](/imgs/blogs/how-policy-sets-asset-prices-the-transmission-map-8.png)

Gold gained about 64% in 2025, broke \$3,000 in March, \$4,000 in October, and went on to a record near \$5,589 by January 2026. No company got more profitable; no coupon was paid. Gold rose because the *other* assets were repriced by a policy whose credibility the market was, for a stretch, unwilling to take on faith. That is the credibility channel in its purest form. The [2025 tariff shock deep-dive](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-2025-tariff-shock-policy-uncertainty-reprices-markets) walks the whole episode in detail.

### 2020: both levers at full power (the reflation regime)

It helps to have a case where the levers pushed assets *up*, not down, because it proves the machine runs in both directions. In March 2020, as COVID shut down the global economy, the S&P 500 fell about 34% in roughly a month — one of the fastest crashes on record. Then policy responded with everything at once. The Fed cut the policy rate to zero on March 15, 2020, and on March 23 announced *unlimited* QE plus, for the first time, programs to buy corporate bonds directly. Simultaneously, the government deployed the largest fiscal package in history — the \$2.2 trillion CARES Act, part of roughly \$5 trillion in total 2020-21 fiscal support.

That is the top-left quadrant of the policy mix — easy money *and* easy fiscal — at maximum intensity, and you can read the result straight off the map. The discount-rate channel: rates to zero lowered the discount rate, lifting the present value of every future cash flow. The liquidity channel: unlimited QE plus trillions in fiscal transfers flooded the system with cash that poured into stocks, credit, housing, and speculative assets. With both channels pushing the same way, the S&P 500 not only recovered its 34% crash but *doubled* off the low by 2021, even as the real economy was still healing. The reflation regime is the most powerful risk-on setting there is, and 2020-21 was its purest modern expression. (It also seeded the 2021-22 inflation that forced the 2022 tightening — the levers, pulled hard enough, write the next chapter's problem.)

### 2022 again, abroad: the UK gilt crisis (credibility in 48 hours)

One more mini-case, because it shows the credibility channel disciplining a wealthy G7 government in *days*, not years. On September 23, 2022, the new UK government announced a "mini-budget" of about £45 billion in unfunded tax cuts — fiscal stimulus with no plan to pay for it, at a moment when inflation was already high. The market's verdict was instant and brutal. The 30-year gilt yield jumped roughly 130 basis points; the pound collapsed to an all-time low near \$1.035 on September 26. Yields up, currency down, together — the unmistakable signature of the credibility channel turning. Worse, the yield spike triggered a doom loop in UK pension funds using a strategy called LDI, forcing the Bank of England into £65 billion of emergency gilt-buying on September 28 just to stop the unraveling. The prime minister who launched the budget lasted 49 days. The lesson is stark: a deficit is constrained not by economics in the abstract but by what the bond market will fund at an acceptable price — and that constraint can bind in 48 hours.

Put the case studies side by side and the thesis of this series is complete. 2020: both levers easy, discount-rate and liquidity channels, every asset *up* (reflation). 2022 (US): monetary lever, discount-rate channel, stocks and bonds down together. 2022 (UK): fiscal lever, credibility channel, yields up while the currency collapsed in days. 2025 (US): fiscal/trade lever, all four channels — earnings, discount rate, currency, and credibility — and stocks, bonds, *and* the dollar down together while gold soared. Four episodes, different levers, one machine; in every case, the lever travelled through channels and ended as a change in what assets were worth.

## What it means for asset values: the playbook

Here is how to turn the map into something you can actually use. When a policy headline lands, run it through four quick questions — and you will know which assets to watch and in which direction.

**1. Which lever was pulled?** Rate, balance sheet, fiscal (spending/tax/tariff), or FX/rules. The first sort tells you which institution acted and roughly how fast it transmits. Rate moves and balance-sheet moves hit markets in minutes; fiscal moves can take quarters (a tax change) or hours (a tariff announcement).

**2. Which channel does it travel through?** Discount rate, liquidity, currency, or credibility — often more than one. A rate hike is mostly discount-rate. QE/QT is mostly liquidity. A tariff is earnings *plus*, potentially, credibility. A big unfunded deficit is bond-supply (discount rate) *plus* credibility. The number of channels lit up tells you how broad the repricing will be: one channel hits one or two asset classes; four channels hit everything.

**3. Which assets reprice, and in which direction?** Use the table you've now built:
- *Discount rate up* → equity multiples down (long-duration tech worst), bond prices down/yields up, property cap rates up/prices down.
- *Liquidity in* → multiples up across risk assets even without earnings (and the reverse when liquidity drains).
- *Currency down* → exporters and foreign earnings up, importers down, gold and dollar-commodities up in local terms.
- *Credibility lost* → the dangerous one: yields rise *and* the currency falls *and* stocks fall together, and gold runs. Watch the bond-and-currency combination; when they move the "wrong" way together, the credibility channel has turned.

**4. What would invalidate the read?** A rate hike is bearish for stocks *unless* it's accompanied by a credible message that growth is fine (then multiples can hold). A tariff is bearish for importers *unless* it's quickly paused or negotiated down (as April 9, 2025 showed — +9.5% in a session on a pause). Always name the condition that would flip your conclusion; policy is a negotiation, and the headline is rarely the last word.

The rough magnitudes to carry in your head: a **+1 point** move in the discount rate is worth roughly **−15% to −20%** on a long-duration stock and a similar order on long bonds; a **large QE program** can add several multiple-points to the market's P/E with no earnings change; a **tariff** of the 2025 scale knocked about **19%** off equities at the trough before the pause; and the clearest *danger* signal in all of markets is a currency falling while its own government's bond yields rise.

One last habit worth building: distinguish a *re-rating* from an *earnings* move, because they behave differently and recover differently. When policy moves the discount rate or the liquidity tide, it changes the *multiple* — investors pay more or less for the same earnings — and these moves can reverse fast when the policy reverses (the +9.5% pause-day rip in April 2025 was a multiple snapping back). When policy moves *earnings* — a tariff cutting an importer's profits, a tax cut raising them — the change is stickier, because it takes real business adjustments to undo. A useful question on any policy headline: *is this changing what the company will earn, or only what investors will pay for those earnings?* The first is slow to reverse; the second can turn on a single subsequent announcement. Getting that distinction right is most of what separates a panicked reaction from a considered one.
