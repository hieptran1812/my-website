> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — "Who is allowed to pull the lever" is itself a price. The Fed sets the price of money, the Treasury decides how much debt to sell and in what form, and Congress controls taxes and spending — and when the market starts to doubt that the Fed is truly independent of the other two, it reprices the term premium and the currency before any policy has actually changed.
>
> - **Three buildings, three levers.** The Fed owns monetary policy (the rate and the balance sheet); the Treasury owns debt issuance and the maturity mix; Congress owns taxes, spending, and the debt ceiling. The President owns tariffs and sanctions — until a court says otherwise.
> - **Independence is a valuation input, not a slogan.** A central bank that markets believe will defend a 2% inflation target gets a low term premium. One markets suspect will be told to cut rates to fund deficits pays that suspicion back in higher long-bond yields and a weaker currency.
> - **The 2025-26 episode is the cleanest example in a generation:** political pressure on the Fed, a tariff order struck down by the Supreme Court in February 2026, the dollar down ~11% in the first half of 2025, and gold up 64% on the year — all of it a market verdict on who controls which lever.
> - **The one number to remember:** a 1 percentage-point rise in the term premium knocks roughly **18-20%** off the price of a 30-year zero-coupon bond. That is the size of the bill markets send when they doubt independence.

In the spring of 2025, traders spent more time reading the *masthead* of policy than the policy itself. The questions on every desk were not "will the Fed cut?" but "can the President fire the Fed chair?", not "what is the tariff rate?" but "is the President even allowed to set that tariff?" Those are constitutional questions, not economic ones — and yet they moved the 10-year Treasury yield, the dollar, and gold more than any data release.

That is the subject of this post. Most market commentary treats "policy" as a single dial that "the government" turns. It is not. Policy is a set of levers held in different hands, under different rules, with different checks. The Fed can cut the overnight rate but cannot pass a tax cut. Congress can run a \$2 trillion deficit but cannot print the money to fund it. The President can announce a 46% tariff on Vietnam — and then watch the Supreme Court rule, in February 2026, that he never had the authority to impose it under the law he invoked.

Understanding *which institution holds which lever* is not civics trivia. It is the foundation of how markets price policy, because the market is constantly asking a second-order question: **is the institution that holds this lever allowed to use it, and is it the one that's supposed to?** When the answer wobbles — when a court might strike a tariff, when a chair might be pressured, when a debt ceiling might not be raised — the asset reprices the *institution*, not just the policy. This post maps the levers, then shows you, with dated examples and dollar math, how the credibility of who-holds-the-lever turns into a yield, a price, and an exchange rate.

![Matrix showing which of the Fed, Treasury, Congress, and Executive controls each policy lever and whether each is independent](/imgs/blogs/who-actually-sets-policy-fed-treasury-and-congress-1.png)

Read the matrix one row at a time. The short-term rate and the balance sheet (QE/QT) belong to the Fed alone. Debt issuance — the *form* of borrowing, bills versus long bonds — is the Treasury's. Taxes and spending are Congress's, written into statute. Tariffs are the Executive's, delegated by old trade statutes. And the bottom row is the one the market watches hardest: only the Fed sits in the "independent" column. Everyone else is elected, or part of an elected government. That single asymmetry is what the rest of this post is about.

## Foundations: the four levers and who holds them

Before we can talk about pricing, we need to define the machinery from zero. There are, in the United States, four big policy levers that reach asset prices. Each lives in a different institution. Think of the whole system like a household with four different family members, each in charge of one thing: one sets the interest rate on the family's credit card, one decides how much to borrow and for how long, one decides what to spend and how much to tax the kids' allowances, and one negotiates with the neighbours. They share a kitchen table but not a wallet.

**The Fed — the price of money.** The Federal Reserve is the central bank. Its job, set by the *Federal Reserve Act* (and updated by the 1977 *dual mandate* amendment), is to pursue **maximum employment** and **stable prices**, which it defines as 2% inflation over the long run. Its primary lever is the **federal funds rate** — the overnight interest rate at which banks lend reserves to each other. The Fed does not literally "set" this rate by decree; it steers it. The modern tool is **IORB**, the *interest on reserve balances* — the rate the Fed pays banks on the reserves they park at the Fed. Because no bank will lend reserves to another bank for less than it can earn risk-free from the Fed itself, IORB acts as a floor that drags the whole overnight market into the Fed's target range. Its second lever is the **balance sheet**: buying bonds to add reserves (quantitative easing, QE) or letting them roll off to drain reserves (quantitative tightening, QT). We go deep on these in [the monetary toolkit](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-monetary-toolkit-rates-qe-qt-and-forward-guidance); here, the point is just *who holds it* — a seven-member Board and a twelve-member rate-setting committee, the FOMC, not the President and not Congress.

**The Treasury — the supply and shape of debt.** The Treasury Department is part of the executive branch, run by a Secretary the President appoints. It does *not* decide how much to borrow — that is downstream of Congress's tax and spending choices. But it decides the **form**: how much of the government's borrowing comes as short-term *bills* (under a year) versus long-term *notes and bonds* (2 to 30 years). That maturity-mix choice is a genuine market lever, as we'll see. The Treasury also runs the **Exchange Stabilization Fund (ESF)**, the legal vehicle for U.S. currency intervention, and it administers the **sanctions plumbing** through the Office of Foreign Assets Control. The Treasury, crucially, is *not* independent — it answers to the President.

**Congress — the purse.** Under Article I of the Constitution, Congress holds the "power of the purse." It writes **tax law** (rates, brackets, credits) and it **appropriates spending**. The gap between the two is the **deficit**, and the running total of deficits is the **debt**. Congress also sets the **debt ceiling** — a statutory dollar cap on how much the Treasury may borrow, a peculiarly American device we'll examine in detail. Congress is the most powerful fiscal actor and the least nimble: its levers move through legislation, which takes months and a majority. We unpack the fiscal side in [the fiscal toolkit](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-fiscal-toolkit-spending-taxes-and-deficits).

**The Executive — trade and sanctions.** The President's direct economic levers are mostly *external*: **tariffs** (taxes on imports) and **sanctions**. The President cannot raise income taxes or write a budget alone, but Congress has, over the decades, delegated broad tariff authority through statutes like **Section 232** (national security), **Section 301** (unfair trade practices), and the **International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA)** of 1977. Those delegations are the legal basis for the 2025 tariff shock — and the legal question the Supreme Court answered in 2026.

![Tree showing US policy powers branching into money, the purse, and foreign trade, each with its constitutional source and check](/imgs/blogs/who-actually-sets-policy-fed-treasury-and-congress-2.png)

The tree makes the separation of powers concrete. Each lever traces back to a different source of authority — the Federal Reserve Act, Article I, Article II — and each comes with a different **check**. The Fed's check is that its governors can only be removed "for cause," not at the President's whim, which is the legal core of its independence. Congress's check on itself is the debt ceiling, a self-imposed brake that can stall its own borrowing. The Executive's check is the courts, which can strike a tariff or a sanction that exceeds delegated authority. Hold onto that word — *check* — because every market repricing in this post happens when a check is tested.

### Why "who holds the lever" is a market question

Here is the conceptual leap that makes this series distinct. A policy lever only moves asset prices if the market believes the holder will *use it as expected*. The expectation is doing the work. And the expectation depends entirely on *who* holds the lever and what constrains them.

Consider the same action — "cut interest rates by 1 percentage point" — taken by two different institutions:

- If an **independent central bank** cuts because its own models say inflation is heading below 2%, the market reads it as the system working: lower rates, anchored inflation, a stronger case for risk assets. Bond yields fall, stocks rise.
- If a **central bank under political control** cuts because the government wants cheaper financing for its deficits, the market reads it as the inflation anchor breaking. *Long-term* yields can actually **rise** even as the *short* rate falls, because investors demand more compensation for holding bonds in a currency they no longer trust to keep its value.

Same lever, same direction, opposite market reaction — purely because of *who pulled it and why*. This is the single most important idea in the post: **the identity and independence of the lever-holder is itself a priced variable.** We'll quantify it shortly.

### The dual mandate is a built-in tension between the branches

It's worth pausing on *why* the Fed and the elected branches so often pull in opposite directions, because it isn't an accident — it's wired into the Fed's job description. Congress gave the Fed a **dual mandate**: maximum employment *and* stable prices. Most of the time those two goals point the same way. But in a supply shock — a war that spikes oil, a pandemic that snarls supply chains, a tariff that raises import costs — they split. Prices rise *and* employment falls at the same time. The Fed then has to choose: fight the inflation (raise rates, which costs jobs) or protect the jobs (hold or cut rates, which lets inflation run). There is no answer that satisfies both halves of the mandate.

This is exactly the bind the Fed faced in 2025, and it's why it *held* through the first half of the year instead of cutting on the political timetable. A tariff is a one-off price-level shock that *could* either pass through to persistent inflation (argue for higher rates) or crush demand and hiring (argue for cuts). An independent committee, looking at its own data, chose to wait and see — three meetings of holding before it began cutting in September. A *captured* central bank, told to support the administration's agenda, would have cut immediately to cushion the tariff's drag on growth, accepting the inflation risk. The market watched which behavior it got, and read the answer as a real-time test of independence.

Here is the deeper point that connects the mandate to valuation. The elected branches face an election clock; the Fed, by design, does not. A politician's time horizon is the next election; the cost of inflation shows up *later*, often after they've left office, while the benefit of cheap money and a hot economy shows up *now*. That asymmetry is precisely why the Fed was made independent in the first place — to put the long-term cost of inflation on the same desk as the short-term temptation to print. When markets believe that desk is staffed by people insulated from the election clock, they trust the currency over decades. When they suspect the clock has reached into the building, every long-dated asset reprices for the possibility that someone will choose the *now* over the *later*. Independence, in other words, is the market's name for "someone in the room is paid to care about thirty years from now."

## The Fed's lever in detail: the price of money

The Fed's overnight rate is the taproot of every other price in the economy, because it is the price of the safest, shortest loan there is — and every other interest rate is built on top of it by adding term and credit risk. When the Fed moves the funds rate, it moves the *discount rate* that every future cash flow in the economy is discounted at. A stock is worth the present value of its future profits; a bond is worth the present value of its coupons; a building is worth the present value of its rents. Raise the discount rate and all of those present values fall. That is the discount-rate channel, covered in depth in [how monetary policy moves stocks](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-monetary-policy-moves-stocks-discount-rates-sectors) and [monetary policy transmission](/blog/trading/macro-trading/monetary-policy-transmission-how-rate-changes-reach-markets).

The Fed pulls this lever with remarkable force when it wants to. The chart below is the single most consequential line in modern macro: the federal funds target over the last decade.

![Step chart of the federal funds target upper bound from 2016 to 2026 showing the COVID cut and the 2022 hiking cycle](/imgs/blogs/who-actually-sets-policy-fed-treasury-and-congress-3.png)

Three regimes jump out. In March 2020, the Fed cut to 0.25% in days as COVID hit. Then, starting March 2022, it ran the **fastest hiking cycle since Volcker**: 525 basis points (5.25 percentage points) in 16 months, eleven hikes, taking the upper bound from 0.25% to 5.50%. And in 2025, having *held* through the first half of the year to see whether tariffs would show up as inflation or as job losses, it cut three times to a 3.50-3.75% range. Each of those moves was a deliberate pull on the discount-rate lever — and notice that the holder never changed. The same committee, under the same mandate, made every one of those calls. That continuity is the independence the market prices.

#### Worked example: how a 1-point rate cut lifts a long-duration stock

Suppose a growth company is expected to earn \$10 of free cash flow per share next year, growing 4% a year forever, and the market discounts that stream at 9%. The value of a growing perpetuity is the next cash flow divided by (discount rate − growth rate): \$10 / (0.09 − 0.04) = \$10 / 0.05 = **\$200 per share**.

Now the Fed cuts, and the discount rate falls 1 point to 8%. Re-run it: \$10 / (0.08 − 0.04) = \$10 / 0.04 = **\$250 per share**. A single point off the discount rate lifted fair value by **25%**, with no change in the company's actual profits.

Why so violent? Because the gap between the discount rate and the growth rate is the *denominator*, and a long-lived cash-flow stream is dominated by its far-future payments — exactly the ones most sensitive to the rate you discount them at. The intuition: the longer an asset's cash flows stretch into the future, the more its price is really a bet on the level of interest rates, and the Fed sets that level.

### The Fed's second lever: the liquidity tide

The funds rate is the *price* of money; the balance sheet is the *quantity* of it. When the Fed buys bonds (QE), it pays for them by creating new bank reserves — cash that has to go somewhere. That cash sloshes into financial assets, lifting prices and compressing yields and spreads across the board. When the Fed lets bonds roll off without replacing them (QT), reserves drain, and the tide goes out. The Fed's balance sheet swung from about \$0.9 trillion before 2008 to a peak of \$8.97 trillion in April 2022 — an enormous liquidity injection — and has been draining since under QT. We treat the mechanics in [QE vs QT: how balance-sheet policy moves markets](/blog/trading/macro-trading/qe-vs-qt-how-balance-sheet-policy-moves-markets); for our purposes, the key fact is that this lever, too, sits *only* in the Fed's hands. Congress cannot order QE; the Treasury cannot conduct it. It is monetary, and it is independent.

The liquidity lever is also where the Fed and the Treasury can quietly *collide*. If the Fed is running QT — pulling reserves out of the system — while the Treasury floods the market with bills to fund a large deficit, the two are fighting over the same pool of short-term cash. In late 2019 a version of this collision (shrinking reserves meeting heavy bill issuance) caused the overnight repo market to seize, with rates briefly spiking to nearly 10%, and forced the Fed to start adding reserves again. Two independent institutions, each pulling its own lever correctly, produced a plumbing accident neither intended. That is the cost of separated powers: no single hand is steering the whole machine.

#### Worked example: how QE compresses a credit spread

Liquidity reprices risk assets through *spreads*, not just the risk-free rate. Suppose a corporate bond yields 6.0% while the matching Treasury yields 4.0% — a credit spread of 2.0 percentage points (200 basis points), the extra compensation for default risk. Now the Fed launches QE and floods the system with reserves. Investors holding cash that earns nothing reach for yield, bidding up corporate bonds. Say the spread compresses to 1.2% (120 basis points) as a result.

What did that do to the corporate bond's price? Take a 10-year corporate bond with a 6% coupon. At a 6.0% yield it trades around par, \$1,000. If its yield falls to 5.2% (Treasury 4.0% + the new 1.2% spread), the price rises to roughly \$1,000 × [the present value of an 80-basis-point yield drop over 10 years], which for a 10-year bond with ~7.5-year duration is about \$1,000 × (1 + 0.008 × 7.5) = **\$1,060** — a 6% gain, purely from the Fed adding liquidity and investors chasing yield. The intuition: the Fed's quantity lever doesn't just lower the risk-free rate, it pushes investors *out the risk curve*, compressing the very spreads that are supposed to price default — which is wonderful in a crisis and dangerous when it inflates risk in calm times.

## The Treasury's lever: the maturity mix is a policy choice

The Treasury is the quietest of the three institutions and one of the most underrated. It cannot decide *whether* the government borrows — Congress's deficits decide that. But it decides the **maturity composition** of that borrowing, and that choice has a real effect on bond yields and on financial conditions.

Here's the mechanism. Long-term bonds carry a **term premium**: extra yield investors demand for locking their money up for years, exposed to the risk that inflation or rates surprise them. Short-term **bills** carry almost no term premium because you get your money back quickly. So when the Treasury chooses to fund the deficit by issuing *more bills and fewer long bonds*, it is, in effect, taking duration risk *off the market's plate* — which can hold long-term yields down. Issue *more* long bonds, and the market has to absorb more duration, which pushes long yields up. This is sometimes called "activist Treasury issuance," and in 2023-24 it became a live debate, with critics arguing the Treasury was leaning on bills to suppress long yields — a quasi-monetary act by a non-monetary institution.

#### Worked example: the maturity mix as a hidden easing lever

Say the Treasury must raise \$1 trillion this quarter. Suppose the term premium on a 10-year note is currently about 0.50% (50 basis points), and bills carry essentially zero term premium.

- **Plan A — fund it all with 10-year notes.** The market must absorb \$1 trillion of duration. To clear that supply, the 10-year yield has to rise. A rough rule of thumb from issuance studies: roughly every \$300 billion of *extra* 10-year-equivalent supply nudges the 10-year yield up by about 5-7 basis points. A \$1 trillion long-bond slug might lift the 10-year yield by **15-20 basis points** versus the bills alternative.
- **Plan B — fund it all with 3-month bills.** Almost no duration hits the market. The 10-year yield is left roughly untouched; the cost shows up only in *rollover risk* — the Treasury must keep re-auctioning those bills every three months and is exposed if short rates jump.

So the Treasury, by tilting toward bills, can shave ~15-20 basis points off the long bond *without the Fed lifting a finger*. The intuition: the Treasury doesn't set the price of money, but by choosing how much *duration* to dump on the market, it nudges the price of *time* — a real lever hidden inside a plumbing decision.

This is also why the Treasury's lever and the Fed's lever can work *against* each other. If the Fed is running QT (selling long bonds to tighten) while the Treasury is shortening its issuance (buying back duration, effectively), the two arms of the government are pulling in opposite directions on the same long-yield. Coordination is not guaranteed, because they are different institutions with different mandates — which is exactly the point.

### The Treasury's other levers: the dollar and the sanctions rail

The maturity mix is the Treasury's market-facing lever, but it holds two more that reach asset prices through the *currency* channel. The first is **FX intervention** through the Exchange Stabilization Fund — a pool of dollars, euros, and yen the Treasury can deploy to buy or sell currency and lean against the dollar's level. The U.S. rarely intervenes, precisely because *not* intervening is itself a signal that the dollar floats freely. But the legal authority lives with the Treasury, not the Fed — so when you hear "the U.S. wants a weaker dollar," the institution that can actually act on it is the Treasury Secretary, and markets listen to that office for currency cues far more than they listen to the Fed. The most famous use of this lever was the 1985 **Plaza Accord**, a coordinated G5 intervention to push down an overvalued dollar; the yen roughly doubled against the dollar over the following two years, from about 239 to 121 by 1987 — a currency move engineered by treasuries, not central banks.

The second is the **sanctions rail**. Because the dollar is the world's reserve currency and most cross-border trade clears through dollar-based systems, the Treasury — through its Office of Foreign Assets Control — can effectively cut a country, company, or individual off from the global financial system. This is a foreign-policy lever with enormous market consequences: a sanctioned entity's assets can be frozen, its bonds can become untradeable, and the threat of secondary sanctions reshapes who is willing to trade with whom. The legal mechanics belong to [law and geopolitics](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/who-writes-the-rules-legislatures-regulators-central-banks-courts); the point for *this* map is that the Treasury, despite being the quietest of the four institutions, controls two of the most powerful levers on the *external* value of the dollar — and it does so as an arm of the executive, with no independence at all.

## Congress's lever: deficits, debt, and the ceiling

Congress holds the biggest lever and the bluntest one. Through tax law and appropriations, it sets the **fiscal impulse** — whether the government is, on net, injecting money into the economy (running a bigger deficit) or pulling it out (shrinking the deficit). The 2025 *One Big Beautiful Bill Act* (OBBBA), signed July 4, 2025, permanently extended the 2017 tax cuts and added new ones; the Congressional Budget Office and Joint Committee on Taxation scored it as adding roughly \$3.4 trillion to deficits over ten years (about \$4.9 trillion including interest, on some conventional scorings). That is a fiscal lever with a price tag, and the market reads it through the lens of [tax law as a market force](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/tax-law-as-a-market-force).

![Dual-axis chart of US federal deficit as a percentage of GDP as bars and debt held by the public as a line from 2019 to 2026](/imgs/blogs/who-actually-sets-policy-fed-treasury-and-congress-4.png)

The chart shows the two halves of Congress's lever. The red bars are the **deficit** each year — the flow Congress controls directly through tax-and-spend choices. The blue line is **debt held by the public** as a share of GDP — the stock that piles up. Notice the COVID spike (a 14.7%-of-GDP deficit in 2020, the CARES Act and the rest), and then notice what's unusual about 2023-2026: deficits of 6%+ of GDP *while the economy was at full employment and rates were high*. That combination — big deficits plus high rates — is historically rare, and it matters for valuation because the debt stock crossing 100% of GDP raises the question of how that debt gets financed, and at what yield.

#### Worked example: how a 1-point rise in the term premium re-prices a long bond

This is the worked example to internalize, because it puts a number on what "the market doubts policy" actually costs. Take a **30-year zero-coupon bond** — a bond that pays nothing until maturity, then pays \$1,000. Its price is the \$1,000 discounted back 30 years.

At a 4.5% yield, the price is \$1,000 / (1.045)³⁰ = \$1,000 / 3.745 = **\$267**.

Now suppose the market's confidence in U.S. fiscal and monetary credibility slips, and it tacks an extra **1 percentage point** onto the long-bond yield purely as term premium — the yield goes to 5.5%. New price: \$1,000 / (1.055)³⁰ = \$1,000 / 4.984 = **\$201**.

The price fell from \$267 to \$201 — a drop of **24.7%** — even though nothing about the bond's promised \$1,000 changed. The only thing that changed was the market's required compensation for holding long-dated U.S. debt. The intuition: at the very long end, a small rise in required yield compounds across thirty years into a brutal price hit, which is why "credibility" is not a soft concept — it is the difference between a 30-year bond worth \$267 and one worth \$201.

### The debt ceiling: a self-imposed check that the market prices

The debt ceiling is the strangest of Congress's levers because it is a brake on the government's ability to pay for spending *Congress already approved*. When borrowing hits the statutory cap, the Treasury can't issue new net debt. It buys time with **extraordinary measures** — accounting maneuvers like pausing contributions to certain federal pension funds — but those run out on a date markets call the **X-date**: the day the government would run out of cash and risk missing a payment. The 2023 standoff is the cleanest recent case.

![Graph of how a debt-ceiling standoff flows from the limit being hit through extraordinary measures to an X-date that splits the bill market into bills that reprice and bills that stay normal before resolution](/imgs/blogs/who-actually-sets-policy-fed-treasury-and-congress-5.png)

Follow the flow. The limit is hit (January 2023); extraordinary measures begin; the Treasury's cash burns down toward an X-date (early June 2023). Then comes the part traders watch: the bill market *bifurcates*. Bills maturing *right around* the X-date carry default risk — you might not get paid on time — so their yields spike. Bills maturing *before* or *well after* the X-date stay normal. When Congress finally raises the ceiling (the deal was signed June 3, 2023), the kink in the bill curve unwinds and those yields snap back. The lever here is Congress's; the channel is a precisely dated default risk priced into a specific slice of the yield curve.

#### Worked example: how an X-date prices into a single T-bill

Imagine a Treasury bill maturing the week of a projected X-date. Normally a 1-month bill might yield, say, 5.0% (annualized) when the funds rate is around there. But now there's a perceived 10% chance that the bill is paid two weeks late, during which you earn nothing and bear headline risk.

Investors price that. Roughly, they demand extra yield to compensate for the expected delay. If the worst case is a two-week delay (about 1/26 of a year) with a 10% probability, the expected "lost time" is about 0.1 × (2/52) ≈ 0.0038 of a year. To be compensated for that on a 1-month (1/12-year) bill, the annualized yield has to rise by roughly 0.0038 / (1/12) ≈ **4.6 percentage points** in the extreme — which is why X-date bills have, in real standoffs, traded **50 to 80+ basis points cheap** to their neighbors even when actual default was considered unlikely. In 2023, yields on bills maturing in the danger window rose visibly above bills maturing just on either side.

The intuition: a debt ceiling doesn't change the creditworthiness of the United States in the long run — it injects a *dated* timing risk that the market surgically prices into the exact bills exposed to it, then erases the moment Congress raises the cap.

## The Executive's lever: tariffs, and the limit the courts impose

The President's most direct market lever in 2025 was the **tariff** — and it became the defining case study of "who is allowed to pull the lever." Tariffs are taxes on imports. They raise the cost of foreign goods, which can feed into domestic inflation, squeeze corporate margins, and — because trading partners retaliate or currencies adjust — ripple through the dollar and global equities. We cross-reference the trade-war mechanics in [the 2018-19 US-China trade war](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-2018-19-us-china-trade-war).

What made 2025 different was the *legal basis*. The administration invoked **IEEPA** — the emergency-powers statute — to impose a 10% universal tariff plus steep "reciprocal" rates on dozens of partners. The chart traces the escalation.

![Step chart of the average effective US tariff rate from 2024 to 2026 rising through Liberation Day and falling after the Supreme Court strikes the IEEPA tariffs](/imgs/blogs/who-actually-sets-policy-fed-treasury-and-congress-6.png)

The average effective U.S. tariff rate went from about 2.4% in 2024 to a peak near 17% in late 2025 — the highest since the Smoot-Hawley era of the 1930s. The single biggest step was **Liberation Day**, April 2, 2025, when Executive Order 14257 announced the universal-plus-reciprocal regime. Then comes the part this post is really about: on **February 20, 2026, the Supreme Court ruled that IEEPA does not authorize tariffs at all.** The President had pulled a lever he did not legally hold. The administration re-invoked a narrower 10% tariff under a different statute (Section 122 of the Trade Expansion Act, which is explicitly time-limited to 150 days), but the headline was unmistakable: a **court** had just disciplined the executive policy lever.

This is the cleanest illustration of the post's thesis. The *economic* content of a tariff is one thing; the *question of authority* is another, and markets price both. When the legality of a policy is in doubt, the policy's market impact is itself uncertain — you cannot price a tariff that might be struck down next quarter. Businesses can't plan inventory, importers can't sign contracts, and the dollar carries a discount for the unpredictability. The court ruling didn't just change a tariff rate; it re-drew the boundary of what the President is *allowed* to do, and that boundary is a market input.

## Central-bank independence: the variable markets price hardest

Now to the heart of it. Of the four institutions, only the Fed is designed to be **independent** — insulated from the elected branches so it can take unpopular actions (like raising rates into an election year) that serve price stability over political convenience. The legal architecture of that independence — fixed terms, removal only "for cause," budgetary autonomy — is the subject of [the legal mandate of a central bank](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-legal-mandate-of-a-central-bank). The *market* question is simpler: does the market *believe* the independence is real?

Because here is the thing about independence — it is worth nothing as a statute and everything as a *belief*. If markets are certain the Fed will defend 2% inflation regardless of who is President, they will lend to the U.S. government for thirty years at a low term premium, because they trust the dollar to hold its value. The moment that certainty cracks — the moment a President credibly threatens to install a chair who will cut rates to fund deficits — the market stops trusting the inflation anchor and demands more yield to compensate. That extra yield *is* the price of doubted independence.

![Before and after comparison of a captured central bank where a politician sets the rate versus an independent central bank where a committee defends a 2 percent target](/imgs/blogs/who-actually-sets-policy-fed-treasury-and-congress-7.png)

The two columns are the same machine with one part swapped. On the left, the politician sets the rate, cuts it to fund deficits, inflation expectations un-anchor, the term premium climbs by a point or more, and long bonds and the currency fall. On the right, a committee sets the rate to hit the 2% target, expectations stay anchored, the term premium stays low, and long bonds and the currency are stable. The *only* difference between the two columns is who holds the lever — and that difference is worth, as our earlier worked example showed, roughly a quarter of a long bond's price.

#### Worked example: how independence doubt re-prices inflation expectations and the long bond

Suppose markets currently expect inflation to average 2.2% over the next ten years, and the 10-year Treasury yields 4.5%. That yield can be split roughly into: a real yield of about 1.8%, plus expected inflation of 2.2%, plus a term premium of about 0.5%.

Now suppose a credible signal arrives that the Fed will be pressured into easy policy regardless of inflation — say, the appointment of a chair who has promised to slash rates. Investors revise their ten-year inflation expectation up from 2.2% to 3.0% (the anchor slips) *and* demand an extra 0.5 point of term premium for the added uncertainty (now 1.0%). The new 10-year yield is roughly 1.8% + 3.0% + 1.0% = **5.8%**, up 1.3 points.

What did that do to the long bond? Using our 30-year-zero math, a 1.3-point yield rise from 4.5% to 5.8% takes the price from \$267 to \$1,000 / (1.058)³⁰ = \$1,000 / 5.49 = **\$182** — a **32% loss**. And the dollar would weaken in tandem, because foreign investors holding those bonds face the double hit of a lower bond price *and* a currency losing purchasing power. The intuition: doubting the Fed's independence doesn't require the Fed to actually do anything — the mere *expectation* that it might be captured re-prices the entire long end and the currency, instantly.

This is not hypothetical arithmetic. It is, in compressed form, what happened in 2025.

## Common misconceptions

**"The Fed controls long-term interest rates."** No. The Fed controls the *overnight* rate directly and influences expectations of future overnight rates. But the 10- and 30-year yields are set by the market and depend heavily on the **term premium** and inflation expectations — which the Fed influences only through *credibility*, not by decree. In April 2025, the Fed's policy rate was unchanged while the 10-year yield *spiked* 50 basis points in a single week during a risk-off panic, the opposite of what you'd expect if the Fed "controlled" long rates. Long yields answer to who-holds-the-lever-and-do-we-trust-them, not to the Fed's dial.

**"A bigger deficit always means higher yields."** Not mechanically. Deficits raise the *supply* of bonds, which all else equal pushes yields up — but "all else" is rarely equal. In 2020, deficits exploded to 14.7% of GDP and 10-year yields *fell* to record lows, because the Fed was buying bonds (QE) and a global panic drove a flight to safety. The deficit is one input; the Fed's balance sheet, the term premium, and global risk appetite are others. The lever Congress pulls interacts with the lever the Fed pulls.

**"The President sets economic policy."** The President sets *some* of it — tariffs, sanctions, the Treasury Secretary, Fed nominations — but not the big domestic dials. The President cannot raise income taxes (Congress), cannot set the funds rate (the Fed), and, as February 2026 showed, cannot even impose tariffs without a statute that actually grants the power. The President proposes a budget; Congress disposes.

**"Central-bank independence is a legal fact, so it's settled."** Independence is a *belief that has to be continuously re-earned*. The statute can say governors are removable "only for cause," but if markets believe a President will find a pretext, or pack the Board, or install a compliant chair, the *de facto* independence — the only kind that matters for pricing — erodes. The 2025 episode showed that the market prices the *probability* of capture continuously, not just the legal status.

**"If the debt ceiling isn't raised, the U.S. defaults the next day."** Not quite. Hitting the ceiling triggers *extraordinary measures* that buy weeks or months; the genuine risk is at the *X-date*, when cash runs out. And even then, the Treasury has discretion over *which* payments to prioritize. The market doesn't price a certain default; it prices a small, dated probability of a *delayed payment* on specific bills — which is why the repricing is surgical, not catastrophic, right up until it isn't.

## Case studies: who pulled the lever, and what repriced

### Case 1 — The 2023 debt-ceiling standoff (Congress's check on itself)

In January 2023, federal borrowing hit the statutory debt limit. The Treasury began **extraordinary measures**, and through the spring the projected **X-date** crept toward early June. As it approached, the bill market did exactly what theory predicts: yields on Treasury bills maturing in the early-June danger window rose sharply above bills maturing just before or after — a visible kink, with the worst-exposed bills trading dozens of basis points cheap. The major rating agencies flagged downgrade risk; one had already cut the U.S. from its top rating in a prior episode, and in August 2023, after this standoff, Fitch did downgrade the U.S. from AAA to AA+. The deal — the Fiscal Responsibility Act — was signed June 3, 2023, days before the X-date. The kink unwound within hours.

The lesson maps cleanly to our framework: the lever was Congress's (its own self-imposed ceiling); the channel was a *dated default-timing risk*; and the asset that repriced was the precise slice of the bill curve exposed to the X-date. No long-run change in U.S. creditworthiness, just a temporary, surgical premium for a calendar risk that Congress manufactured and then resolved. For how traders actually position around this, see [the fiscal toolkit](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-fiscal-toolkit-spending-taxes-and-deficits).

### Case 2 — The 2025 tariff order and the 2026 Supreme Court strike (the executive lever, checked by the courts)

This is the case study the whole post builds toward, because it is the cleanest "who-is-allowed-to-pull-the-lever" episode in modern markets. The timeline:

- **April 2, 2025 — Liberation Day.** EO 14257 announced a 10% universal tariff plus "reciprocal" rates on 57 partners (China 34%, Vietnam 46%, EU 20%, Japan 24%, and more), invoking IEEPA.
- **April 3-8, 2025 — the market reaction.** The S&P 500 fell about 12% in four trading days, from a February all-time high of 6,144 to a trough of 4,983 on April 8 — a peak-to-trough drawdown of roughly **18.9%**. Critically, the 10-year Treasury yield *rose* mid-crash (from ~3.99% on April 4 to ~4.49% by April 11) instead of falling — a **safe-haven failure** that told you the market was repricing U.S. policy *credibility*, not just growth.
- **April 9, 2025 — the pause.** A 90-day pause on most reciprocal tariffs (except China, briefly pushed to 145%) sparked a **9.5% one-day rally** in the S&P 500, one of the largest single-day gains in history.
- **February 20, 2026 — the strike.** The Supreme Court ruled IEEPA does not authorize tariffs. The President's lever was, in legal fact, never his to pull under that statute.

The macro footprints were enormous. The **dollar fell about 10.7% in the first half of 2025**, its worst first half since 1973 — a direct market verdict on U.S. policy credibility. And **gold rose 64% over 2025**, breaking above \$3,000 in March, \$4,000 in October, and eventually printing an all-time high above \$5,500 by early 2026 as the monetary hedge of choice when faith in policy institutions wobbles.

![Dual-axis chart of the US Dollar Index falling while gold rises through 2025 as a market verdict on policy credibility](/imgs/blogs/who-actually-sets-policy-fed-treasury-and-congress-8.png)

The chart is the thesis in two lines. The dollar (blue) fell hard through the first half of 2025; gold (amber) ran the other way. When markets doubt the institutions — when a tariff might be illegal, when the Fed might be pressured — they sell the *currency* of those institutions and buy the asset that no institution can print. The lever was the Executive's; the *check* was the courts; and the channel was the currency and the monetary hedge. We connect gold's behavior to policy in detail in the [gold](/blog/trading/gold) track; here it's the cleanest credibility barometer there is.

### Case 3 — Fed independence under pressure: Volcker (1979-82) and 2018-19

To see why independence is priced, look at the two times it was most visibly tested.

**Volcker, 1979-82.** When Paul Volcker took the Fed chair in August 1979, inflation was running near 14.8% (peaking March 1980). To break it, he pushed the federal funds rate to an extraordinary **19.1%** by mid-1981, sending the 10-year Treasury to a **15.84%** peak and unemployment to **10.8%** by late 1982. This was savagely unpopular — homebuilders mailed him two-by-fours, farmers blockaded the Fed — and a *captured* central bank would never have done it. Volcker had just enough political cover (and the institutional independence) to take the country through a double-dip recession to kill inflation. The payoff was a 40-year bond bull market: the 10-year yield fell from 15.8% in 1981 to 0.5% by 2020. Independence, exercised, bought the U.S. four decades of cheap long-term borrowing. The full story is in [the Volcker rate shock](/blog/trading/finance/paul-volcker-1980-rate-shock-killing-inflation).

**2018-19.** During the Fed's last hiking cycle before COVID, the chair faced sustained public pressure from the White House to stop raising rates and to cut. The Fed hiked anyway through 2018 (to a 2.50% upper bound), then pivoted to cuts in 2019 — but framed the cuts as *its own* call on a slowing global economy, not as compliance. The market mostly accepted that framing, which is *why* the term premium stayed contained. The contrast with 2025 is instructive: in 2018-19, the pressure was loud but the *de facto* independence held, so the credibility premium never blew out. In 2025, the pressure came bundled with a broader assault on policy institutions, and the credibility premium showed up in the dollar and gold.

The through-line across all three cases: **markets do not price the policy in isolation; they price the institution that holds the lever and whether it will be allowed to act as designed.** A debt ceiling is a Congressional check that prices into bills. A struck-down tariff is a judicial check that prices into the dollar. A defended (or doubted) inflation target is the independence variable that prices into the entire long end. For the global analogs and the statistical betas behind these moves, see [the legal mandate of a central bank](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/the-legal-mandate-of-a-central-bank) and [the fed funds path and front-end correlation](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/the-fed-funds-path-and-front-end-correlation).

## The global map: same machine, different wiring

The U.S. structure is one instance of a universal pattern. Every major economy has a central bank, a treasury, and a legislature — but the *degree of independence* and the *division of levers* differs, and markets price each accordingly.

- **The ECB (Governing Council).** The most independent central bank by statute, serving 20 countries with one currency but *no* shared treasury — a fiscal-monetary mismatch that nearly broke the euro in 2012 until Mario Draghi's "whatever it takes" backstop restored credibility (Spain's 10-year fell from 7.6% in July 2012 toward 1.6% by 2014 on three words and a never-used program). The ECB's independence is its core asset; its missing fiscal counterpart is its core fragility.
- **The Bank of England (Monetary Policy Committee).** Independent since 1997. The 2022 mini-budget crisis showed the *fiscal* lever (an unfunded £45 billion tax cut) colliding with the *monetary* lever: 30-year gilt yields jumped ~130 basis points in days, the pound hit a record low of \$1.035, and the BOE had to intervene with emergency gilt-buying to stop a pension doom-loop. Markets disciplined a G7 government in 49 days — the prime minister resigned. It is the textbook case of the bond market enforcing fiscal credibility.
- **The Bank of Japan (Policy Board).** Independent, but a 30-year case study in policy at the zero bound — ZIRP, QE, yield-curve control, negative rates — ending its negative-rate experiment only in March 2024. The BOJ owns more than half of all Japanese government bonds, which raises its own question: when the central bank *is* the bond market, what does "independence" even price?
- **The PBOC (under the State Council).** *Not* independent — China's central bank operates under the government. It manages the yuan through a daily fix and uses reserve-requirement and quantity tools more than a single policy rate. Markets price the yuan partly as a *policy choice* rather than a free float, which is a different valuation regime entirely.
- **The State Bank of Vietnam (under the government).** Like the PBOC, the SBV is an arm of the government, not independent. Its binding lever is the **credit-growth quota** — it sets each bank's annual loan-growth ceiling — plus the refinancing rate and a managed FX band (±5% around a daily central rate). When Vietnam faced a 46% reciprocal tariff in 2025 (negotiated down to 20% by July), with ~29% of its exports going to the U.S., the policy sensitivity ran straight through an export-led economy whose central bank answers to the same government negotiating the trade deal. We cover this in [the SBV's monetary and banking law](/blog/trading/law-and-geopolitics/sbv-monetary-and-banking-law-credit-quotas-and-the-dong).

Two of these deserve a closer look, because they are the sharpest "who-holds-the-lever" lessons outside the U.S.

The **euro crisis of 2012** is the purest demonstration that credibility, not action, does the work. The euro area has a single independent central bank but no shared treasury — so when markets feared that Spain or Italy might leave the euro and redenominate their debt into devalued local currency, the bonds of those countries blew out: Spain's 10-year hit 7.6% and Italy's 6.6% in July 2012, levels that would have made their debt unpayable. The lever that fixed it was not a bond purchase. It was a sentence. On July 26, 2012, ECB president Mario Draghi said the bank would do "whatever it takes to preserve the euro," and backed it with a never-actually-used program called Outright Monetary Transactions. Spreads collapsed — Spain's 10-year fell toward 1.6% by 2014 — on the *credible threat* of unlimited buying, not a single euro of it. That is the independence variable in its clearest form: a central bank markets *believed* could and would act didn't have to. The threat alone was worth hundreds of basis points of spread.

The **UK gilt crisis of 2022** is the mirror image: what happens when the *fiscal* lever is pulled recklessly and the bond market enforces discipline in days. A new government announced an unfunded £45 billion package of tax cuts — a fiscal lever with no funding plan — and the market refused to absorb it. Thirty-year gilt yields jumped about 130 basis points in days, the pound crashed to a record low of \$1.035, and a hidden plumbing problem in UK pension funds (leveraged liability-driven investing) threatened a forced-selling doom-loop. The independent Bank of England had to step in with emergency gilt-buying — a monetary lever deployed to clean up a fiscal mistake — and within 49 days the prime minister had resigned. It is the textbook case of markets disciplining a G7 government almost overnight, and it shows the flip side of the credibility coin: when the elected branch pulls its lever without regard for what the bond market will bear, the bond market sets the limit, fast and brutally.

The lesson generalizes: the *more* a central bank's independence is doubted (PBOC, SBV by design; the Fed if pressured), the more its currency and bonds carry a policy-risk premium — and the *less* a government's fiscal lever is constrained by credibility, the harder the bond market will eventually constrain it for them. Independence and fiscal discipline are, everywhere, priced variables. The U.S. just happens to have the deepest, most liquid market in which to price them, and the reserve-currency status that has, so far, given it more room than any other country to test the limits.

## What it means for asset values: the playbook

Pull this together into something you can actually use. When you see a policy headline, the first question is not "what does this do to the economy?" It is **"who pulled this lever, were they allowed to, and is it the institution that's supposed to hold it?"** The answer tells you which asset reprices and in which direction.

- **A Fed move (the rate or the balance sheet)** reprices through the **discount rate** and the **liquidity tide**. A cut lifts long-duration equities most (our 25%-on-a-1-point example); a hike compresses them. Signal to watch: the dot plot and the press conference, covered in [trading the FOMC](/blog/trading/macro-trading/trading-the-fomc-statement-presser-dot-plot). What invalidates the read: if the cut is seen as *captured* rather than *justified*, long yields can rise even as the short rate falls.
- **A Treasury issuance shift (more bills vs more bonds)** reprices the **long end** quietly. More bills hold long yields down; more bonds push them up. Signal: the quarterly refunding announcement. Magnitude: tens of basis points, not hundreds.
- **A Congressional fiscal move (a tax law, a spending package, the deficit path)** reprices through **bond supply** and, at the extreme, **credibility**. Bigger structural deficits at full employment lift the term premium over time. Signal: CBO scores and the auction tails. What invalidates: a Fed willing to absorb the supply (QE) can mask it — temporarily.
- **A debt-ceiling standoff** reprices a **surgical slice of the bill curve** around the X-date, and nothing else, until resolution. Signal: the X-date estimate and the bill curve kink. It reverses fast once the ceiling is raised.
- **An Executive tariff or sanction** reprices through the **currency**, **inflation expectations**, and **affected sectors** — *and* through the legality question. Signal: the legal basis cited (IEEPA vs Section 232 vs 301) and any pending court challenge. What invalidates: a court strike (as in February 2026) can erase the policy's market footprint overnight.
- **A credibility shock to independence** reprices the **entire long end and the currency at once** — the most violent and most fundamental of all (our 32% long-bond example). Signal: chair-succession news, removal-power challenges, "shadow chair" talk. What invalidates: the institution visibly holding the line, as in 2018-19.

A useful habit is to read every policy headline twice. The first reading asks the obvious question — what does this lever *do* to growth, inflation, or the deficit? The second reading asks the question this post is really about — *who pulled it, were they allowed to, and was it the institution that's supposed to hold it?* The first reading tells you the economic content. The second tells you the credibility content, and in the moments that matter most — a debt-ceiling X-date, a tariff struck down by a court, a Fed chair under pressure — the credibility content is the larger market force. A tariff that might be illegal is priced differently from one that is settled law. A rate cut from a trusted committee is priced differently from the same cut by a captured one. Train yourself to separate the two, and most of the apparently confusing market reactions to policy resolve into the same simple frame.

The deepest takeaway is the one we opened with. Markets do not just price *policy*; they price the *machine that makes policy* — who holds each lever, whether they're allowed to use it, and whether the one institution meant to be independent actually is. When that machine is trusted, the U.S. borrows for thirty years at a low term premium and the dollar holds its place at the center of the system. When it is doubted — as in 2025-26 — the term premium widens, the dollar falls, and gold runs to records. The institution itself is the asset. Price it accordingly.
