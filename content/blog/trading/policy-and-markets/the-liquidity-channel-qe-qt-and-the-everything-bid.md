> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Quantitative easing reprices assets through *quantity*, not the discount rate: the central bank removes safe assets from the market, floods the banking system with reserves, and forces every investor one rung down the risk ladder until the bid reaches stocks, credit, and crypto. The result is a higher *multiple* on the same earnings — and quantitative tightening runs the whole machine in reverse.
>
> - The lever is the **central-bank balance sheet**; the channel is the **portfolio-rebalancing (or "portfolio-balance") effect**; the valuation outcome is a **compressed risk premium and a higher fair P/E** — a re-rating that needs no improvement in profits.
> - Watch **net liquidity** — the Fed balance sheet *minus* the Treasury General Account *minus* the reverse-repo facility — not the headline balance sheet. A full balance sheet can still drain markets if cash is piling up in the TGA and the RRP.
> - 2020-21 is the textbook everything bid: the Fed balance sheet went from \$4.2T to **\$8.97T**, M2 grew **40%** (\$15.4T to \$21.5T), and *every* asset reflated — the S&P doubled off its low, gold and crypto and housing and junk credit all ran — **before** earnings had recovered.
> - 2022 is the reverse: the fastest **\$95B-a-month** drain in history pulled the multiple back down; the S&P fell **19.4%** and "safe" bonds fell **13%** *together*. The one number to remember: QT removes about **\$1.14 trillion of reserves per year** at full speed.

In the spring of 2020 the world's economy was closing down, corporate earnings were collapsing toward their worst quarter since the Great Depression, and the S&P 500 had just fallen 33.9% in 33 days — one of the fastest crashes on record. By the logic every beginner is taught — that a stock is worth the present value of the profits a company will earn — stocks should have stayed in the basement for as long as the profits were missing. Instead, from the low on March 23, 2020, the S&P 500 *doubled* over the next eighteen months. It reached new all-time highs while unemployment was still in the double digits. Bitcoin went from under \$5,000 to nearly \$69,000. House prices set records. Even the riskiest junk-rated corporate bonds — debt issued by companies most likely to default in a recession — rallied hard. Almost nothing fell. People started calling it **the everything bid**.

What changed between March 23 and the rest of 2021 was not earnings; earnings were still a wreck. What changed was that on March 23, 2020, the Federal Reserve announced it would buy bonds in *unlimited* quantity, and over the following two years it grew its balance sheet from about \$4.2 trillion to a peak of **\$8.97 trillion** — it roughly doubled the size of the most powerful balance sheet on Earth. That is the subject of this post. The discount-rate channel — the one the previous post in this series covered, where a central bank changes the *rate* at which we discount future cash flows — was also at work (the Fed had cut to zero). But the discount rate cannot explain the *whole* everything bid, because rates had been near zero for a decade in the 2010s without producing a doubling of stocks in eighteen months. Something else was happening, and that something is the **liquidity channel**: policy moving prices through *quantity*.

This is the channel that beginners and even many professionals misunderstand most, because it does not work the way intuition expects. The Fed does not buy a single share of Apple. It does not lend money to a homebuyer or a crypto exchange. It buys two things — government bonds and government-backed mortgage bonds — from banks and dealers, and somehow the bid shows up everywhere. Understanding *how* a purchase of the world's safest asset becomes a bid on the world's riskiest one is the literacy this post is built to give you. By the end you will be able to look at a chart of the Fed's balance sheet, subtract two numbers most people have never heard of, and read the liquidity tide that is lifting or draining every market at once.

This is the map for the whole post, and it follows the spine of the entire series: a policy **lever** (the balance sheet) travels through a transmission **channel** (the liquidity tide and the portfolio rebalance) and lands as a change in **valuation** (a higher multiple, a tighter spread, a reflated risk asset). We will walk it left to right. If you want the *trader's positioning* playbook for these tools — how to actually trade a QE announcement or a QT taper — that lives in the macro-trading post on [QE vs QT and how balance-sheet policy moves markets](/blog/trading/macro-trading/qe-vs-qt-how-balance-sheet-policy-moves-markets); here we own the machinery and the valuation math.

## Foundations: reserves, the balance sheet, and what QE actually is

Start from zero, because the popular phrase for QE — "money printing" — is both the most common description and one of the most misleading.

**Bank reserves.** Banks keep accounts at the Federal Reserve, just as you keep an account at a bank. The balances in those accounts are called **reserves**. Reserves are a special kind of money: they exist only on the Fed's books, banks use them to settle payments with each other, and ordinary people and companies can never hold them directly. When you hear that the Fed "created money," what it almost always created is *reserves* — a deposit in a bank's account at the Fed, conjured with a keystroke. This matters enormously, because reserves are not the dollars in your wallet or your checking account; they are a wholesale settlement asset that sits *inside* the banking system. A trillion new reserves does not mean a trillion new dollars chasing groceries. It means a trillion of fresh, perfectly safe, perfectly liquid balances sitting in bank accounts at the Fed, looking for something to do.

**The central-bank balance sheet.** Like any institution, the Fed has a balance sheet: assets on one side, liabilities on the other. Its assets are mostly the bonds it has bought — US Treasuries and agency mortgage-backed securities (MBS). Its liabilities are mostly two things: the physical cash in circulation (those Federal Reserve notes in your wallet are literally a liability of the Fed) and the reserves banks hold. The defining feature of a central bank is that it can expand both sides of its balance sheet *at will*: it can buy a bond (an asset) and pay for it by crediting the seller's bank with new reserves (a liability) that it creates from nothing. No taxpayer money, no borrowing — just an expansion of the balance sheet. This is the deepest sense in which a central bank is different from a household or even a government: it is the one entity whose IOUs *are* money.

**What QE actually is.** Quantitative easing is simply the Fed deciding to buy a *large quantity* of bonds — to expand its balance sheet deliberately, as a policy tool, rather than just enough to keep the plumbing running. When it does QE, the Fed buys Treasuries and MBS in the open market, mostly from large dealer banks. The bank that sells a bond loses the bond and gains reserves. Two things have now happened in the wider system at once: (1) the total quantity of reserves in the banking system has gone *up* by the purchase amount, and (2) the total quantity of that safe bond available to private investors has gone *down* by the same amount. Hold those two facts; they are the entire engine. QE *adds reserves* and *removes safe assets*, simultaneously, by construction.

**Why "money printing" is the wrong mental model.** The phrase implies the Fed is handing dollars to people who will rush out and spend them on goods, driving up prices in shops. That is the inflation story, and it is a *different channel* — and an unreliable one, because the reserves stay trapped inside the banking system. Through the 2010s the Fed did trillions of QE and consumer inflation stayed *below* its 2% target the entire decade; the printing-press story flatly failed to predict that. What QE reliably does is not push money into the grocery aisle but push *asset prices* up, through the mechanism we are about to build. The 2021-22 inflation, when it finally came, was driven mostly by the *fiscal* bazooka (cash sent directly to households) colliding with supply shortages — a story told in the [fiscal-toolkit post](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-fiscal-toolkit-spending-taxes-and-deficits). QE's signature is the price of assets, not the price of bread.

Two terms you will need throughout. **The risk premium** is the extra return investors demand for holding a risky asset instead of a safe one — the compensation for the chance you lose money. **The multiple**, or **P/E ratio**, is the price of a stock divided by its earnings; a P/E of 20 means investors pay \$20 for every \$1 of annual profit. These two are joined at the hip: when the risk premium falls, investors accept a lower expected return, which means they are willing to pay a *higher* price for the same future earnings — a higher multiple. The entire liquidity channel, when it reaches stocks, works by squeezing the risk premium and lifting the multiple. Keep those two ideas close; the rest is arithmetic.

**One more piece of plumbing — why the Fed *wants* QE to work this way.** A central bank has a problem at the **zero lower bound**: once it has cut the policy rate to zero, it has run out of room to cut further, but the economy may still need more support. The conventional lever (the rate) is jammed at the floor. QE is the unconventional lever it reaches for next — a way to keep *easing* after the rate hits zero, by working on the *quantity* of money and the *term premium* instead of the overnight rate. That is the original 2008 rationale, straight from the Bernanke Fed: when you cannot push the price of money any lower, push on the quantity. Everything in this post follows from that one design choice — the Fed deliberately built a second lever that bites even when the first is stuck, and that second lever happens to land squarely on asset prices. So the "everything bid" is not an accident or a side effect; it is the *intended transmission* of a tool designed to ease financial conditions when rates alone cannot. Understanding that intent is half the battle: the Fed is *trying* to make you reach for risk.

## The lever and the tide: how a bond purchase becomes liquidity

Let us trace the mechanics one step at a time, because the magic is in the plumbing.

The Fed announces a QE program and starts buying, say, \$80 billion of Treasuries and \$40 billion of MBS a month. Each morning the New York Fed's trading desk transacts with primary dealers. A dealer (or the pension fund or insurer behind it) sells a 10-year Treasury to the Fed. The Fed pays by crediting the dealer's bank with new reserves. The dealer's bank now has more reserves and one fewer safe bond on the books. Multiply this across thousands of transactions and you get the two macro facts from the Foundations: **reserves in the system rise** and **the float of safe bonds held by the public shrinks**.

Here is the first crucial insight: the seller did not *want* cash. A pension fund holds a 10-year Treasury because it needs a long-dated, safe, yield-bearing asset to match a long-dated liability (the pensions it owes retirees decades from now). When the Fed buys that bond away, the pension fund is left holding reserves or a bank deposit earning almost nothing — which does *not* match its liability. It is now under-invested in exactly the kind of asset it needs. So it goes looking for a replacement. There are fewer safe bonds available (the Fed just removed a chunk), and the ones that remain now yield less (more buyers, fewer bonds, prices up, yields down). So the pension fund reaches for the *next* best thing: maybe a high-grade corporate bond, or a longer Treasury, or a stock that pays a steady dividend. Whoever sells *that* asset to the pension fund now has cash and the same problem, and reaches one rung further out. The bid marches outward through the entire risk ladder. This is the **portfolio-rebalancing channel** — also called the **portfolio-balance effect** — and it is the heart of how QE moves prices.

![Fed balance sheet area chart with QE waves and QT drains](/imgs/blogs/the-liquidity-channel-qe-qt-and-the-everything-bid-2.png)

The chart shows the whole history of the lever. The Fed's balance sheet sat under \$0.9 trillion before the 2008 crisis. Three rounds of QE (QE1 in 2008, QE2 in 2010, QE3 in 2012) drove it to about \$4.5 trillion. A first attempt at quantitative tightening (QT1, 2017-19) trimmed it modestly before the September 2019 repo crisis forced a halt. Then COVID hit, the Fed went to unlimited QE, and the balance sheet exploded to its **\$8.97 trillion** peak in April 2022. Since then, QT2 has been grinding it back down toward \$6.5 trillion. Every green band is a period when the lever was *adding* liquidity; every amber band is when it was *draining*. Keep this shape in your head — most of the asset-price history of the last fifteen years rhymes with it.

**Why the tide lifts the boats it does.** The portfolio rebalance does not affect every asset equally; it affects the ones closest to the bonds the Fed bought *first and most*. Treasuries are the very asset being removed, so their prices rise (yields fall) most directly. The next ring is high-grade credit — corporate bonds rated investment grade — which behaves a lot like a Treasury plus a small spread; as Treasuries get scarce and expensive, that spread compresses too. Then equities, where the falling discount rate and falling risk premium lift the multiple. Then the most speculative assets — high-yield ("junk") bonds, emerging markets, crypto, anything where the appeal is "reach for yield" or "reach for return." The further out a buyer is pushed, the riskier the asset they end up holding, and the more its price rises relative to its fundamentals. That is why, in a big QE wave, the *junkiest* assets often rise the most in percentage terms: they are at the end of the chain, and by the time the bid reaches them it has been amplified by every rebalancing investor ahead of them.

#### Worked example: \$1 trillion of QE and the broad money supply

Let us put a number on the tide. Suppose the Fed runs \$1 trillion of QE over a year, and look at what it does to the broad money supply. When the Fed buys a bond from a *non-bank* (a pension fund, an insurer, a money fund), it pays by crediting the seller's *bank* with reserves, and the bank in turn credits the seller's deposit account. So a purchase from a non-bank creates *both* a new reserve (the bank's asset at the Fed) *and* a new bank deposit (broad money the public can spend or invest). That is how QE leaks into the broad money supply, M2.

In 2020-21 this happened on a massive scale, amplified by the fiscal transfers that also landed in bank accounts. M2 went from **\$15.4 trillion at the end of 2019 to \$21.5 trillion at the end of 2021** — a jump of **\$6.1 trillion, or about 40%**, the fastest money growth since the 1940s. Not all of that is QE — much is the fiscal cash — but the two arrived together and pooled in the same bank accounts. The intuition: in two years, two of every five dollars of broad money in the United States were *new*, and that wall of money had to be parked somewhere — overwhelmingly, it was parked in financial assets, which is why their prices rose so far ahead of the real economy.

![M2 money stock bar chart with the 2020 to 2021 surge highlighted](/imgs/blogs/the-liquidity-channel-qe-qt-and-the-everything-bid-4.png)

The bars make the surge unmistakable: M2 had crept up gently for years, then leapt in 2020-21 (the green bars) and actually *shrank* in 2022-23 as QT and the end of fiscal transfers pulled money back out — the first sustained contraction of US money in modern history, and a warning that the everything bid can run in reverse.

## Net liquidity: the number that actually matters

Here is where most people reading a balance-sheet chart go wrong. They watch the headline Fed balance sheet — the blue line above — and assume that whenever it is high, markets are flush, and whenever it falls, markets drain. That is roughly true but dangerously incomplete, because *not all of the balance sheet reaches markets*. Two large pools of cash can sit *inside* the Fed's liabilities doing nothing for asset prices, and to read the real tide you have to subtract them. The result is called **net liquidity**, and it is the single most useful number in this entire post.

The formula traders actually watch is:

**Net liquidity = Fed balance sheet − Treasury General Account − Overnight Reverse Repo.**

Let us define the two things being subtracted.

**The Treasury General Account (TGA).** The US Treasury — the part of the government that taxes and spends — keeps its checking account at the Fed. That account is the TGA. When the Treasury collects taxes or sells bonds to the public, money flows *out* of private bank accounts and *into* the TGA, where it sits as a Fed liability doing nothing for markets. When the Treasury spends — sends out a stimulus check, pays a contractor, makes a Social Security payment — money flows *out* of the TGA and *into* private bank accounts, adding to reserves and liquidity. So the TGA is a *drain* on net liquidity when it is filling up and a *source* when it is running down. A Treasury that rebuilds its cash balance from \$0.1T to \$0.8T after a debt-ceiling fight has, all else equal, pulled \$0.7T of liquidity *out* of markets — even though the Fed's balance sheet never moved.

**The Overnight Reverse Repo facility (ON RRP).** This is a facility where money-market funds and other non-banks can park spare cash *at the Fed* overnight, fully collateralized, earning a set rate. Cash sitting in the RRP is, by definition, *not* chasing risk assets — it has chosen the safest possible home. So a *rising* RRP balance is liquidity being sterilized (pulled out of markets and parked at the Fed), and a *falling* RRP is liquidity being released back into the system to chase assets. In 2021-22 the RRP swelled to over \$2 trillion as money funds had nowhere better to put cash; as it drained back toward zero in 2023-24, that released liquidity helped cushion markets *even while QT was shrinking the headline balance sheet.* This is exactly why net liquidity, not the headline, is the number that predicts asset prices.

![Net liquidity stack Fed balance sheet minus TGA minus RRP](/imgs/blogs/the-liquidity-channel-qe-qt-and-the-everything-bid-3.png)

The stack shows the subtraction visually. The gross balance sheet (blue) is the big number everyone quotes. The TGA and the RRP (amber) are the two pools that sterilize liquidity — cash that exists on the Fed's books but is *out* of the market. What is left over (green) is net liquidity: the pool that actually rebalances into risk. A reader who watches only the blue bar will be blindsided when the green pool moves the other way.

#### Worked example: computing net liquidity

Let us do the arithmetic with round, representative recent numbers. Suppose at a given moment:

- Fed balance sheet = **\$6.8 trillion**
- Treasury General Account = **\$0.7 trillion**
- Overnight Reverse Repo = **\$0.5 trillion**

Then net liquidity = \$6.8T − \$0.7T − \$0.5T = **\$5.6 trillion**. The number that matters for markets is \$5.6T, not the \$6.8T headline — \$1.2 trillion of the balance sheet is sterilized inside the TGA and the RRP, doing nothing for asset prices.

Now watch what happens *without any change in the Fed's balance sheet*. Say the Treasury runs down its cash from \$0.7T to \$0.3T to pay bills (the TGA falls by \$0.4T) and money funds pull \$0.3T out of the RRP to buy Treasury bills instead. The balance sheet is unchanged at \$6.8T, but net liquidity rises to \$6.8T − \$0.3T − \$0.2T = **\$6.3 trillion** — a \$0.7 trillion *injection* into markets that never shows up on the headline balance-sheet chart. The intuition: the Fed can be standing still while the Treasury and the money funds are quietly flooding or draining the system — which is why "don't fight the Fed" really means "don't fight net liquidity."

This is also the resolution of a paradox that confused many people in 2023-24: the Fed was doing QT (shrinking the balance sheet), yet stocks rallied hard. How? Because the RRP was draining *faster* than QT was shrinking the balance sheet, so *net* liquidity was flat-to-rising even as the headline fell. The RRP acted as a buffer tank — QT drained the tank, not the market. Once the RRP buffer was nearly empty, QT started biting the actual reserves that matter, which is exactly when the Fed began slowing its pace. The plumbing details — IORB, the RRP floor, how the Fed pins the rate at any balance-sheet size — are laid out in the macro-trading post on the [central-bank balance sheet, net liquidity, reserves, RRP and TGA](/blog/trading/macro-trading/central-bank-balance-sheet-net-liquidity-reserves-rrp-tga).

## The term premium: why QE works on long-dated everything

There is a second mechanism, complementary to the portfolio rebalance, that explains why QE has such an outsized effect on *long-duration* assets — long bonds, growth stocks, real estate, crypto — and it is worth its own section because it is where the channel's force concentrates.

Recall **duration**: roughly how many years' worth of price an asset loses for each 1-percentage-point rise in its discount rate. A 2-year note has duration near 1.9; a 10-year bond near 8; a 30-year bond near 18. The same idea applies to equities: a profitable, slow-growing utility has most of its value in near-term dividends (short duration), while a fast-growing tech company has most of its value in profits expected a decade or two out (long duration). The longer the duration, the more sensitive the price is to the discount rate — and the more a *fall* in that rate lifts it.

Now the key fact about the discount rate on a long bond. A 10-year yield is, in theory, the market's expectation of the *average* overnight rate over the next ten years, *plus* a **term premium** — extra compensation for the risk of locking your money up for a decade when rates could surprise you. QE attacks both pieces. By committing to hold rates low and buying long bonds directly, it lowers the *expected* path of short rates (forward guidance plus action), and — crucially — by removing a huge quantity of long-dated bonds from the market, it compresses the *term premium*. When the Fed owns trillions of long Treasuries, the private market has fewer of them to hold, so it demands less extra compensation for the duration risk that remains. The term premium can even go *negative*, as it did for stretches of the 2010s and 2020s.

This is the **duration extraction** view of QE: the Fed is not just adding money, it is pulling *interest-rate risk* out of private hands and onto its own balance sheet, which lets every long-duration asset re-price as if the world had less risk in it. That is why growth stocks, long bonds, and the most speculative assets — all long-duration — are the biggest beneficiaries of QE and the biggest victims of QT. When the Fed reverses and *gives the duration back* (QT plus no buyer for new issuance), the term premium rebuilds, long yields rise, and the longest-duration assets fall hardest. The 2022 collapse of long Treasuries (down ~31%) and growth tech (Nasdaq down ~32.5%) was duration being handed back to a market that suddenly had to price it.

#### Worked example: a falling term premium lifts a long bond

Take a 10-year Treasury with duration of about 8.5. Suppose QE compresses its yield by 50 basis points — partly by lowering the expected rate path, partly by squeezing the term premium as the Fed soaks up the float. The price change is approximately duration × yield change = 8.5 × 0.50% = **+4.25%** on the bond's price, for a safe government bond, from a policy that touched no fundamental about the borrower.

Now apply the same 50bp discount-rate fall to a long-duration *growth stock* with an effective equity duration of, say, 25 (most of its value is in far-future profits). The lift is roughly 25 × 0.50% = **+12.5%** — three times the bond's move, from the same policy nudge, purely because the cash flows sit further out in time. The intuition: QE's force is proportional to duration, so the assets with the most distant payoffs — long bonds, growth equities, crypto — feel the tide most, which is exactly why they lead both the everything bid and its reversal.

## Why QE lifts the P/E without lifting earnings

Now we connect the tide to a *valuation*, which is what this series is ultimately about. The cleanest place to see it is equities, because a stock's price decomposes neatly into two parts: the earnings, and the multiple investors pay for those earnings.

Price = Earnings × Multiple. Or, written the way a finance text would: a stock's value is its expected future cash flows discounted back at a rate that has two pieces — the *risk-free rate* (what you could earn on a safe Treasury) plus the *equity risk premium* (the extra return demanded for bearing stock-market risk). QE pushes on *both* discount-rate pieces. It lowers the risk-free rate directly (by buying Treasuries and pushing their yields down — that is the discount-rate channel, covered in [the discount-rate channel post](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-discount-rate-channel-how-rates-reprice-cash-flows)). And — this is the part unique to the liquidity channel — it lowers the *equity risk premium*, because the portfolio rebalance means there is a wall of money that *must* find a home in risk assets, and that competition for risk assets bids their prices up and their forward returns (hence the premium demanded) down.

The result is a higher multiple on *unchanged* earnings. The company is not more profitable. It will not sell more widgets because the Fed bought a bond. But the price investors are willing to pay for each dollar of its profit has gone up, because the alternatives (safe bonds) now yield almost nothing and there is too much money chasing too few risk assets. This is why, in a QE wave, you see the strange spectacle of stocks ripping to records while earnings are flat or falling: it is a **re-rating**, a change in the multiple, not a change in the fundamentals. And it is why valuations look "expensive" by historical standards through long QE periods — the multiple is being held up by the liquidity tide, not by superior growth.

![Balance sheet direction and the S and P 500 outcome bar chart](/imgs/blogs/the-liquidity-channel-qe-qt-and-the-everything-bid-6.png)

The bars line up the *sign* of the liquidity lever with the *sign* of the equity outcome across the big modern episodes. When the balance sheet was expanding hard (2020-21 COVID QE), the S&P roughly doubled. When liquidity was draining (2022 QT plus hikes), it fell about 19%. And the two big crashes — 2008 and the March 2020 plunge — both happened *before* the QE response, in the brief window when liquidity was collapsing and the Fed had not yet flooded the system. The relationship is not mechanical to the decimal point — earnings, valuations, and shocks all matter too — but the direction is unmistakable, and it is the empirical backbone of "don't fight the Fed."

#### Worked example: how \$1 trillion of QE lifts the fair P/E

Let us quantify the re-rating with a simple, transparent model. A common shorthand for the fair P/E of the market is the inverse of the required return: if investors require a total return of `r` on stocks, the fair earnings yield is `r`, and the fair P/E is `1 / r`. Suppose before QE:

- Risk-free rate (10-year Treasury) = **4.0%**
- Equity risk premium = **4.0%**
- Required return r = 4.0% + 4.0% = **8.0%**, so fair P/E = 1 / 0.08 = **12.5×**

Now the Fed runs a big QE program. Say it pulls the risk-free rate down by 1 percentage point (to 3.0%) and the portfolio rebalance compresses the equity risk premium by half a point (to 3.5%). The new required return is 3.0% + 3.5% = **6.5%**, so the new fair P/E = 1 / 0.065 = **15.4×**.

The earnings have not changed by a penny. But the fair multiple rose from 12.5× to 15.4× — a **+23%** re-rating of the entire stock market, driven entirely by a lower discount rate and a compressed risk premium. If the index was at 4,000 on \$320 of earnings (a 12.5× multiple), the *same* \$320 of earnings now supports an index of 320 × 15.4 = **4,930** — a 23% rally with zero improvement in profits. The intuition: QE does not make companies better; it makes the *price of safety* so unattractive that investors pay up for everything else, and the multiple does all the work.

## The everything bid: the tide lifts more than stocks

The reason the phenomenon earned the name "everything bid" is that the portfolio rebalance does not stop at stocks. It pushes outward through *every* risk asset at once, which is why so many seemingly unrelated markets rise together during a QE wave. Let us walk the rungs.

![Portfolio rebalancing flow pushed down the risk ladder](/imgs/blogs/the-liquidity-channel-qe-qt-and-the-everything-bid-5.png)

The flow makes the marching bid concrete. The Fed buys the safest asset (Treasuries and MBS). The bond holder who sold is pushed to rung 1 (cash earning nothing), then rung 2 (investment-grade credit), then rung 3 (high-yield and equities). At each rung the seller of *that* asset inherits the cash and the problem, so the bid keeps moving outward — and the outcomes on the right (spreads compress, multiples rise, crypto and housing and junk all reflate) are simply the footprints of that bid arriving at each asset class in turn.

**Credit.** As Treasuries get scarce and their yields fall, the *spread* — the extra yield a corporate bond pays over a Treasury — gets squeezed, because yield-hungry investors pushed off Treasuries pile into credit instead. Investment-grade and then high-yield spreads compress, which means the price of corporate bonds rises and the cost of borrowing for companies falls. Cheap borrowing lets companies refinance, buy back stock, and fund deals — a second-order boost to equities.

**Gold.** Gold pays no yield, so its great enemy is a high *real* interest rate (a safe asset that pays you to wait). When QE crushes real yields and floods the system with new money, the opportunity cost of holding gold falls and its appeal as a hedge against currency debasement rises. Gold is one of the purest expressions of the liquidity tide.

![Gold spot price chart with COVID QE leg and record run](/imgs/blogs/the-liquidity-channel-qe-qt-and-the-everything-bid-7.png)

Gold rose 25% in 2020 alone (the green COVID-QE leg, \$1,517 to \$1,898) as the liquidity bazooka fired, then — after a digesting pause — went on to a record run past **\$5,500/oz** by early 2026, propelled by a second leg of money growth, deficits, and de-dollarization. The deeper why of gold's run belongs to the [gold series](/blog/trading/gold/why-gold-is-money-the-monetary-history-of-gold), but the liquidity-channel point is simple: when the tide of money rises, the asset that cannot be printed rises with it.

**Crypto.** Bitcoin and the broader crypto complex are the *farthest-out* rung of the risk ladder — the most speculative, longest-duration, most liquidity-sensitive asset class in existence. So they tend to amplify the liquidity tide in both directions: they ran ferociously in the 2020-21 QE flood and crashed hardest in the 2022 drain. The mechanism by which policy liquidity and the dollar reach crypto and FX is covered in the companion post on [how policy prices crypto and FX](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/how-policy-prices-crypto-and-fx-liquidity-and-divergence); here, just file crypto as the high-beta tail of the everything bid.

**Housing.** QE includes buying *mortgage*-backed securities, which directly pushes mortgage rates down and frees up bank balance sheets to lend. Cheaper mortgages plus a wall of liquidity is rocket fuel for house prices, which is why US home prices set records through 2020-21 even in a recession. The empirical correlation structure that ties all these markets together when liquidity moves is mapped in the macro-correlations post on [global liquidity and the everything correlation](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/global-liquidity-and-the-everything-correlation).

The unifying point: these are not five independent bull markets that happened to coincide. They are one bid — the portfolio rebalance — arriving at five doors. That is the everything bid, and it is why a single number (net liquidity) can move the whole complex at once.

## Global liquidity and the direct-purchase contrast

Two refinements turn the everything bid from a US story into a more complete one.

**It is *global* liquidity, not just the Fed.** Capital flows across borders, so the tide that matters for any given market is the *sum* of the major central banks' balance sheets, adjusted for exchange rates. When the Fed, the European Central Bank, the Bank of Japan, and the People's Bank of China are all expanding at once — as in 2020-21 — the global liquidity wave is enormous and every market on Earth feels it. When they diverge — one easing while another tightens — the picture is muddier, and the currency channel (covered in the [currency-policy post](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/currency-policy-and-fx-intervention)) starts to dominate. A subtle but important case: even while the Fed ran QT in 2023-24, the Bank of Japan was still pinning its yield curve and expanding, and Chinese stimulus added liquidity too, so *global* net liquidity drained far less than the US headline suggested — another reason the de-rating was gentler than the QT pace implied. The reader who tracks only the Fed will misjudge the global tide; the empirical structure of that cross-border correlation is mapped in [global liquidity and the everything correlation](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/global-liquidity-and-the-everything-correlation).

**The Bank of Japan shows what *direct* purchases do.** The Fed buys only Treasuries and MBS; its lift to stocks is entirely indirect, through the rebalance. The Bank of Japan went further and bought *equity ETFs* directly for over a decade, becoming one of the largest owners of the Japanese stock market. That is the portfolio-rebalancing channel taken to its logical extreme — the central bank itself standing at the far rung of the risk ladder, buying the risk asset outright. It worked to support prices, but it also illustrated the channel's deepest problem: an *exit*. Once a central bank owns a huge book of long bonds or stocks, shrinking it risks reversing the very rebalance that lifted prices, which is why both the BOJ and the Fed have shrunk their balance sheets so cautiously. The everything bid is easy to start and treacherous to unwind — the asset prices it lifted have come to *depend* on the liquidity staying, which is the uncomfortable subtext of every QT campaign.

There is one more second-order effect worth naming: QE quietly transfers risk and reward in ways that have political consequences. Because the liquidity tide lifts *asset* prices first and most, and assets are owned disproportionately by the already-wealthy, a long QE era tends to widen wealth gaps even as it does little for wages — a critique that has shadowed every QE program since 2008. That is not a markets point so much as a reminder that the liquidity channel is a *distributional* lever as well as a valuation one, and the politics of "the Fed inflates asset prices for the rich" is a real constraint on how freely future policymakers can use it.

## The reverse: QT and the de-rating

Everything above runs in reverse. **Quantitative tightening (QT)** is the Fed shrinking its balance sheet, which it does not by *selling* bonds (that would be disruptive) but by letting bonds it owns *mature without reinvesting the proceeds*. When a Treasury the Fed holds matures, the Treasury pays the Fed back, the Fed simply extinguishes the reserves it receives, and both sides of the balance sheet shrink. Reserves leave the system; the float of safe bonds the public must absorb *grows* (because the Treasury must now sell new bonds to the public instead of to the Fed). The portfolio rebalance runs backward: there are *more* safe bonds to hold and *fewer* reserves chasing risk, so the marginal investor rotates *up* the risk ladder, out of stocks and credit and back toward safety. Spreads widen, the equity risk premium rises, the multiple compresses. This is a **de-rating**: the same earnings, a lower price.

![QT drains reserves 95 billion a month chart](/imgs/blogs/the-liquidity-channel-qe-qt-and-the-everything-bid-8.png)

The chart shows the mechanical pace. The Fed's QT2 plan capped runoff at **\$95 billion a month** (\$60B of Treasuries plus \$35B of MBS) at full speed. The arithmetic is relentless: twelve months of an uninterrupted cap removes about **\$1.14 trillion** of assets and reserves from the system — a slow, grinding drain rather than a crash, which is exactly why QT's effect on markets is steady pressure rather than a single dramatic event.

#### Worked example: \$95 billion a month, draining reserves over a year

Let us put the drain in dollar terms and trace it to a valuation. The cap is \$95B/month. Over twelve months that is 12 × \$95B = **\$1.14 trillion** of reserves removed. Suppose the system started with \$3.3 trillion of reserves (a representative recent level). After a year of full-speed QT, reserves fall to about \$3.3T − \$1.14T = **\$2.16 trillion** — a roughly one-third reduction in the cushion of liquidity sitting in the banking system.

Now connect that to the multiple. If the liquidity flood lowered the equity risk premium by half a point on the way up (our earlier worked example), QT pushing it *back* up by half a point reverses the re-rating: a required return rising from 6.5% back toward 7.0% takes the fair P/E from 15.4× down to 1 / 0.07 = **14.3×** — a roughly **7% de-rating** on unchanged earnings. Run the risk-free rate back up too (QT and the absence of a buyer push Treasury yields higher) and the compression is larger. The intuition: QT does not crash the market in a day; it leans on the multiple month after month, which is why the de-rating tends to be a grinding bear market rather than a one-day plunge — and why "don't fight the Fed" cuts *both* ways.

The full 2022 episode, where this de-rating met the discount-rate channel head-on, is the deep case study below.

## Common misconceptions

**"QE is money printing that causes consumer inflation."** Through the entire 2010s the Fed ran trillions of QE and US consumer inflation stayed *below* 2% — the printing-press story failed for a decade. Reserves are trapped in the banking system; they reliably lift *asset* prices, not grocery prices. The 2021-22 consumer inflation came mainly from *fiscal* transfers (cash to households) hitting supply shortages, not from QE. Mixing the two channels is the single most common error in this whole subject.

**"The bigger the Fed's balance sheet, the more liquid markets are."** Not necessarily — watch *net* liquidity. A \$6.8T balance sheet with \$0.8T sitting in the TGA and \$1.5T in the RRP is *less* market-liquid than a \$6.0T balance sheet with both pools near empty. The headline can rise while net liquidity falls, and vice versa. In 2023-24 the Fed was doing QT yet stocks rallied, because the draining RRP more than offset the shrinking balance sheet.

**"QE directly buys stocks and props them up."** The Fed (unlike, say, the Bank of Japan, which famously bought equity ETFs) does not buy stocks. It buys Treasuries and MBS. The lift to stocks is *indirect*, through the portfolio rebalance — which is precisely why understanding the rebalancing chain matters: there is no direct equity purchase to point at, only a marching bid.

**"QT is the same size as the prior QE, so it should be symmetric."** In practice QT is far gentler — a slow runoff capped at \$95B/month rather than the unlimited buying of a QE flood — and it is buffered by the RRP. So the de-rating is a grind, not a mirror-image crash, and the Fed can pause or slow it the moment funding markets show stress (as it did in 2019 and effectively in 2024).

**"You can read the liquidity tide from the balance sheet alone."** You cannot. You need three numbers — balance sheet, TGA, RRP — and their *direction*. A trader who watches only the blue line will be repeatedly surprised; a trader who watches net liquidity will not.

## Case studies: the everything bid, the de-rating, and the rescues

### 2020-21: the everything rally

This is the textbook case, and it is worth walking slowly because every part of the machine fired at once. In late February and March 2020 the COVID shock hit; the S&P 500 fell **33.9%** in 33 days, the fastest crash of that depth in history, as liquidity evaporated and even Treasuries briefly traded badly. On **March 15, 2020** the Fed cut to zero; on **March 23, 2020** it announced *unlimited* QE — it would buy "in the amounts needed" — plus, for the first time, facilities to buy *corporate* bonds through special vehicles. That was the bottom. The market turned *that day*, not when the virus was beaten or earnings recovered, but when the liquidity bazooka fired.

Over the next two years the Fed grew its balance sheet from about \$4.2T to **\$8.97T**, M2 grew 40%, and the portfolio rebalance did its work across every market. The S&P 500 *doubled* off the March low by 2021 and made new highs while unemployment was still elevated. Gold rose 25% in 2020. Bitcoin went from under \$5,000 to nearly \$69,000. Investment-grade and high-yield spreads collapsed back to pre-crisis levels within months. House prices set records. The everything bid was the portfolio-rebalancing channel running at maximum volume, amplified by the fiscal cash landing in the same bank accounts. The lesson for valuation: stocks were not "predicting" a fast recovery; they were *re-rating* on liquidity, which is why the rally so badly outran the real economy.

One detail of March 2020 deserves emphasis because it shows the channel working faster than any actual purchase could. When the Fed *announced* it would buy corporate bonds — through new facilities (the PMCCF and SMCCF) — corporate-bond spreads began collapsing *immediately*, before the Fed had bought a single corporate bond. The mere credible promise that a deep-pocketed, never-defaulting buyer stood ready was enough to make every private investor want to own the bonds *before* the Fed did, which bid them up on its own. This is the liquidity channel fused with the **credibility channel** (the fourth channel in this series): a believed commitment to provide liquidity can move prices as powerfully as the liquidity itself — the same lesson Mario Draghi taught in 2012 with three words ("whatever it takes") and a backstop he never had to use. The portfolio rebalance, it turns out, responds to the *promise* of liquidity, not only its arrival, which is why announcement day is so often the turning point.

### 2022: QT, the hikes, and the de-rating

Then the machine reversed. With inflation at a 40-year high of **9.06%** (June 2022), the Fed both hiked aggressively (525 basis points, the fastest since Volcker — see [the monetary toolkit post](/blog/trading/policy-and-markets/the-monetary-toolkit-rates-qe-qt-and-forward-guidance)) *and* began QT in June 2022, ramping to the \$95B/month cap. Both channels pushed the same way: the discount rate rose (hurting long-duration cash flows) *and* liquidity drained (lifting the risk premium and compressing the multiple). The result was a textbook de-rating: the S&P 500 fell **19.4%** in 2022, and — the part that shocked everyone — the "safe" Bloomberg US Aggregate bond index fell **13%** at the same time, the worst bond year in modern history. A classic 60/40 portfolio lost about **16%**. Stocks and bonds fell *together* because the thing repricing was not any single asset's fundamentals but the *price of money and liquidity itself*, which sits underneath everything. The longest-duration, highest-beta assets fell most: the Nasdaq 100 dropped about 32.5% and crypto fell roughly 65% from its peak — the far end of the risk ladder, draining hardest, exactly as the rebalancing model predicts.

### The liquidity adds that rescue markets

The third case study is the most important for understanding *why* "don't fight the Fed" became gospel: when the liquidity tide turns *up*, even briefly and even in the middle of a tightening campaign, markets stop falling. Two episodes make the point.

**September 2019, the repo crisis.** QT1 had quietly drained reserves to the point where the banking system was short of the cushion it needed. On September 17, 2019, the overnight repo rate — the cost of borrowing cash against Treasuries — spiked from around 2% to nearly 10% intraday, because there were not enough reserves to go around. The Fed rushed in, injected liquidity, and within weeks restarted balance-sheet *growth* (buying T-bills — officially "not QE," but it added reserves all the same). Stocks, which had wobbled, resumed climbing into early 2020. The lesson: reserves can become *scarce* even at a large balance sheet, and when they do, the Fed has to add liquidity whether it wants to or not — and markets cheer.

**March 2023, SVB and the BTFP.** In the middle of the 2022-23 tightening, Silicon Valley Bank collapsed on **March 10, 2023**, felled by the very rate shock the Fed had engineered (it had loaded up on long bonds that lost value when rates rose). To stop a banking panic, the Fed created the Bank Term Funding Program, which lent banks cash against their underwater bonds *at par* — an emergency liquidity add. The Fed's balance sheet, which had been shrinking under QT, jumped roughly \$300 billion in a week as banks tapped the facility. And markets *rallied*: the de-rating paused, tech stocks led a sharp bounce, and the rest of 2023 was a strong year for equities — in part because that liquidity bump, plus the draining RRP, kept *net* liquidity from collapsing even as headline QT continued. The lesson that traders internalized: the Fed will always backstop the system in a crisis, and the backstop is a liquidity add, and a liquidity add is bullish. That asymmetric reaction function — drain slowly, but flood fast at the first sign of breakage — is a large part of why every dip in the QE era was bought.

There is a deeper structural point hiding in these rescues. The financial system, after fifteen years of QE, has come to *need* a large and growing pool of reserves simply to function — to clear payments, to meet intraday spikes in demand for cash, to satisfy the post-2008 regulations that require banks to hold mountains of high-quality liquid assets. That means the Fed cannot shrink its balance sheet back to its pre-2008 size even if it wanted to; the floor on reserves is far higher than it used to be, and pushing below it breaks something (as 2019 and 2023 both showed). The practical implication for asset values is profound: the *baseline* level of liquidity in the system is structurally elevated and likely to stay that way, which puts a structurally higher floor under asset valuations than existed before 2008. QT can trim the excess, but the everything bid's tide never fully goes out, because the plumbing of a modern financial system now requires the water to stay high. The liquidity channel, once opened, is very hard to close.

## What it means for asset values

Pull the threads together into a practitioner's read.

**The signal to watch is net liquidity and its direction.** Track three numbers — the Fed balance sheet, the TGA, and the RRP — and compute the trend in (balance sheet − TGA − RRP). Rising net liquidity is a tailwind for *every* risk asset; falling net liquidity is a headwind for all of them at once. The headline balance sheet alone will mislead you; the net number will not.

**The valuation effect is on the multiple, not the earnings.** QE and liquidity adds lift the *price* investors pay per dollar of profit — the P/E, the credit spread, the cap rate — without improving the underlying cash flows. So in a liquidity-flooded market, do not mistake high prices for strong fundamentals; the multiple is being held up by the tide. When the tide goes out (QT), expect the multiple to compress *first*, often before earnings even wobble.

**The magnitudes, roughly.** A big QE wave that pulls the risk-free rate down ~1 point and the equity risk premium down ~0.5 point can lift the fair market P/E by ~20% — a major bull market on unchanged earnings (our worked example: 12.5× to 15.4×). Full-speed QT removes about \$1.14 trillion of reserves a year and tends to grind the multiple back down by several percent annually, more if it coincides with rate hikes (as in 2022).

**The cross-asset order.** The bid arrives first at Treasuries (yields fall), then high-grade credit (spreads compress), then equities (multiple rises), then the speculative tail — high-yield, gold, crypto, frontier assets — which move the most in percentage terms and reverse the hardest. In a drain, that order runs backward: the speculative tail bleeds first and worst.

**What would invalidate the read.** The liquidity channel is powerful but not omnipotent. It can be *overwhelmed* by a discount-rate shock (2022: even a draining-then-flat net liquidity could not save markets while the Fed hiked 525bp), by a credit event that freezes the rebalancing chain (a true panic where no one will hold the next rung at any price), or by an inflation problem that *forces* the Fed to keep draining regardless of asset prices (the Fed's "put" disappears when inflation is the binding constraint, as it was in 2022). The clean liquidity-up-equals-everything-up relationship holds best when inflation is *not* the binding problem — which is the whole reason it worked so cleanly in the disinflationary 2010s and broke down in the inflationary 2022.

**"Don't fight the Fed," precisely stated.** The famous maxim is usually heard as "when the Fed eases, buy; when it tightens, sell." That is directionally right but sloppy. The precise version, after everything above, is: *do not position against the direction of net liquidity unless you have a specific reason the channel is jammed.* The reasons it jams are the invalidators just listed — a binding inflation constraint, a credit freeze, an offsetting discount-rate shock. So the disciplined read is two-step: first establish the direction of net liquidity (the default prior for every risk asset), then ask whether any invalidator is active (which can flip or neutralize the prior). In 2020-21 net liquidity was surging and nothing was jammed, so the everything bid was the right and obvious trade. In 2022 net liquidity was draining *and* inflation forced the Fed's hand *and* the discount rate was rising — three forces aligned downward, and fighting them was ruinous. The maxim is not magic; it is a reminder that the liquidity tide is the strongest single current in markets, and you swim against it only with a very good reason.

The deepest single takeaway: the liquidity channel teaches you to separate *price* from *value*. QE does not make a company worth more; it makes the *price of safety* so unattractive that capital floods into everything else and pays up for it. That re-rating is real money — it is most of the return of the last fifteen years of bull markets — but it is a re-rating, and re-ratings reverse. Learn to read the tide, and you can tell the difference between a market that is rising because the world is getting better and one that is rising because the Fed is removing safe assets faster than the world can produce risky ones.
