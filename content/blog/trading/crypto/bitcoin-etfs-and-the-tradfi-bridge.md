> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — After a decade of refusing, U.S. regulators let Wall Street wrap Bitcoin in a familiar, regulated product called the spot exchange-traded fund (ETF), and that single wrapper pulled enormous institutional money into the asset while tying Bitcoin's fate ever more tightly to traditional finance.
>
> - An **ETF** is a basket you can buy and sell like a stock; a *spot* Bitcoin ETF holds **actual Bitcoin**, so its price tracks Bitcoin closely through a quiet arbitrage machine called **creation and redemption**.
> - The machine needs two unglamorous parts: an **authorized participant** (a big trading firm that swaps cash for shares) and a **custodian** (a firm that holds the real coins, in most cases Coinbase Custody).
> - For ten years the SEC said no. **Grayscale**, whose closed-end trust GBTC had swung from a fat premium to a roughly **-40%** discount, sued and won — and that court loss, not a change of heart, forced approval.
> - In **January 2024**, eleven spot Bitcoin ETFs launched at once. BlackRock's **IBIT** gathered tens of billions of dollars faster than almost any fund in history; spot **Ether** ETFs followed in July 2024.
> - The prize is "demand on tap": pensions, registered investment advisers, and 401(k)-style money can now buy Bitcoin through the same brokerage they already use — which deepens liquidity but also tightens Bitcoin's correlation to macro markets.
> - The irony: Bitcoin was built so you could hold your own keys and need no bank, and its biggest demand engine is now a fund where a custodian holds the keys for you and a money manager charges you a fee.

Here is a fact that would have sounded absurd in 2013. The largest single buyer pushing money into Bitcoin today is not a cypherpunk, not a hedge fund founder tweeting at 3 a.m., and not a teenager mining in a dorm room. It is, increasingly, a retiree in Ohio who clicked "buy" on a ticker called IBIT inside the same retirement account that holds an S&P 500 fund, and who has no idea that somewhere a firm called Coinbase quietly bought real Bitcoin on her behalf and locked it in cold storage. The diagram above is the mental model for the whole post: demand from ordinary brokerage accounts flows through a money manager, through a specialist trading firm, through a custodian, and finally lands as a real purchase of Bitcoin on the open market — a pipeline that did not exist before 2024 and that has rewired who owns crypto.

![Pipeline of an ETF creation and redemption flow with an authorized participant and a custodian](/imgs/blogs/bitcoin-etfs-and-the-tradfi-bridge-1.png)

This is one of the strangest reversals in modern finance. Bitcoin was designed in 2008 as a deliberate rejection of Wall Street — no banks, no intermediaries, hold your own keys, be your own bank. For its first fifteen years, the financial establishment mostly returned the disdain, calling it a fraud, a tulip, a tool for criminals. And then, very quickly, the establishment did the thing establishments do when they cannot kill something: it absorbed it. It built a regulated wrapper, slapped a familiar label on it, charged a modest fee, and sold it back to the world through the plumbing it already controlled.

This post is about that wrapper — what it is, the machinery that makes it work, the ten-year fight to get it approved, what changed the day it launched, and the uncomfortable irony at the center of it. We will build everything from zero. If you do not know what an ETF is, what "spot" means, what a custodian does, or why a fund can trade for less than the stuff inside it, you are exactly the reader this is written for. Every term is defined the first time it appears, every abstraction is grounded in a worked example with real dollar figures, and only then do we climb up to the big question: did Wall Street tame Bitcoin, or did Bitcoin capture Wall Street?

## Foundations: funds, ETFs, NAV, custody, and the creation machine

Let us start with the smallest piece and build up. By the end of this section you will understand every word in the phrase "spot Bitcoin exchange-traded fund," plus the arbitrage loop that makes the whole thing tick.

### What a fund is, and what an ETF adds

A **fund** is a pool of money from many investors used to buy a basket of assets. Instead of you buying ten different things one at a time, you put money into the pool, the pool buys the things, and you own a slice of the pool. Funds exist because picking and managing assets is hard, and because pooling lets small investors own a piece of something they could never buy alone.

An **ETF**, or *exchange-traded fund*, is a particular kind of fund with one defining trick: its shares trade on a stock exchange all day long, exactly like a company's stock. You can buy an ETF share at 10:14 a.m. and sell it at 2:37 p.m. through any ordinary brokerage account, at whatever price the market is quoting that second. That is different from an old-fashioned **mutual fund**, where you can only buy or sell once per day at a single price struck after the market closes. The "exchange-traded" part — the ability to trade it like a stock, in your normal account, with no special paperwork — is the entire reason ETFs matter for this story. It means a Bitcoin ETF can be bought by anyone who can buy Apple stock, including the giant pools of money that are *legally forbidden* from touching crypto directly but are perfectly free to buy an SEC-registered fund.

### Net asset value: what the basket is actually worth

Every fund has a **net asset value**, or **NAV**: the total market value of everything the fund holds, divided by the number of shares outstanding. If a fund owns Bitcoin worth \$1,000,000,000 and has issued 20,000,000 shares, its NAV per share is \$1,000,000,000 ÷ 20,000,000 = \$50.00. NAV is the "fair" or "fundamental" value of one share — what it would be worth if you could instantly liquidate the basket and split the cash.

The crucial question for any fund is: **does the share price on the exchange match the NAV?** For a well-built ETF the answer is "almost exactly, almost always," and the reason is the arbitrage machine we will build in a moment. For the older structure Bitcoin first arrived in — a closed-end fund — the answer was a resounding *no*, and that gap is the source of one of the great cautionary tales in crypto.

### Spot versus futures: holding the thing versus betting on its price

A **spot** Bitcoin ETF holds *actual Bitcoin*. When the fund takes in \$10 million, it buys roughly \$10 million of real coins (minus fees), and those coins sit in storage. The fund's value moves with Bitcoin's price because it literally owns Bitcoin. "Spot" just means "the thing itself, at today's price," as opposed to a contract about its future price.

A **futures** Bitcoin ETF does not hold Bitcoin at all. It holds **futures contracts** — standardized agreements traded on a regulated exchange (in the U.S., the CME) to buy or sell Bitcoin at a set price on a future date. Futures track Bitcoin's price loosely, but they expire, so the fund must constantly sell expiring contracts and buy later-dated ones. This "rolling" usually costs money, because later-dated contracts often trade higher than near-dated ones (a condition called *contango*). That roll cost is a steady drag that makes a futures ETF a poor long-term proxy for owning Bitcoin. We will see why this distinction was politically decisive: the SEC approved a futures ETF years before it allowed a spot one, even though the futures version is, for a buy-and-hold investor, the worse product.

### Custody: why institutions cannot just hold their own keys

Bitcoin is controlled by **private keys** — secret numbers that prove you own a given pile of coins. Whoever holds the keys controls the coins; lose them and the coins are gone forever, with no bank to call. For an individual, "be your own bank" is empowering. For a regulated fund managing tens of billions of other people's dollars, it is a nightmare. A fund cannot have an employee memorize a seed phrase. It needs auditors, insurance, segregated accounts, disaster recovery, and a legal entity that can be sued if the coins vanish.

That role is filled by a **custodian**: a regulated firm whose entire job is to hold assets safely on behalf of clients. In traditional finance, custodians (like State Street or BNY Mellon) hold trillions of dollars of stocks and bonds for funds, keep them segregated from the custodian's own balance sheet, and are examined by regulators. For Bitcoin, the dominant custodian became **Coinbase Custody**, which holds the coins for most of the spot ETFs in **cold storage** — keys kept on devices never connected to the internet, often split into pieces so no single person or location can move funds (a scheme called multi-signature or sharded keys), spread across multiple geographies, with institutional insurance and audit trails.

Why can't the funds just hold the keys themselves? Because the rules that govern regulated funds were written for a world of stocks and bonds held at custodians, and they effectively *require* a qualified custodian for client assets. A money manager that lost a seed phrase, got phished, or had an employee abscond with the keys would have no recourse and no insurance and would have breached its fiduciary and regulatory duties. The custodian exists to make the asset *fit the rulebook*: it provides the segregation, the insurance, the audit trail, and crucially a named, regulated, suable entity that the fund's lawyers can point to when a pension board asks "and who, exactly, is responsible if the Bitcoin disappears?" That is the real unlock — not technology, but accountability that satisfies a compliance department. We will return, near the end, to the irony that this reintroduces exactly the trusted intermediary Bitcoin was built to remove.

### The closed-end fund, and how it can trade away from NAV

Before spot ETFs existed, the main way a U.S. brokerage account could get Bitcoin exposure was a **closed-end fund** — specifically Grayscale's GBTC. A closed-end fund is a fund with a *fixed* number of shares. It issues shares once (or in occasional batches), and after that the shares just trade hand to hand on a market. Critically, an ordinary investor cannot redeem a share back to the fund for its slice of the underlying assets, and new shares are not created continuously to meet demand.

That fixed supply breaks the link between price and NAV. If everyone wants in and no new shares can be made, the share price gets bid *above* NAV — a **premium**. If everyone wants out and no one will redeem the underlying, the price falls *below* NAV — a **discount**. A closed-end fund can trade at a 30% premium one year and a 40% discount the next, even though the Bitcoin inside never changed. This is the single most important contrast in the post: **a closed-end fund lets price and NAV drift apart; a properly built ETF nails them together.** The mechanism that does the nailing is creation and redemption.

### The creation and redemption machine

Here is the engine that makes an ETF track its NAV, and it is the most important idea in this entire post. An ETF has special partners called **authorized participants**, or **APs** — large trading firms (think Jane Street, Virtu, big banks) licensed by the issuer to do one privileged thing: swap baskets of the underlying asset for blocks of ETF shares, and vice versa, directly with the fund, all day long.

Watch what this does. Suppose demand pushes the ETF's market price *above* its NAV. The shares are now overpriced relative to the Bitcoin they represent. An AP pounces: it buys Bitcoin (or delivers cash that the fund uses to buy Bitcoin), hands it to the fund, receives newly **created** ETF shares at NAV, and immediately sells those shares into the hungry market at the higher price — pocketing the gap. That selling pushes the price back down toward NAV. The opposite runs in reverse: if the ETF trades *below* NAV, an AP buys cheap shares, **redeems** them with the fund for the underlying value, and sells the Bitcoin — pushing the price back up. The result: any gap between price and NAV is an arbitrage opportunity, APs compete it away in seconds, and the ETF price stays glued to NAV. No central planner sets the price; greed does.

![Pipeline showing buyer demand, an authorized participant, a custodian, and new ETF shares](/imgs/blogs/bitcoin-etfs-and-the-tradfi-bridge-1.png)

This is also why the ETF actually buys real Bitcoin. When you click "buy IBIT" and demand rises, the creation process means an AP ultimately causes more real coins to be acquired and locked at the custodian. The brokerage click and the on-chain purchase are connected by this loop. That is the "demand on tap" we will keep coming back to.

One subtlety worth knowing, because it caused a real fight during the launch: there are two flavors of creation, **in-kind** and **in-cash**. In an *in-kind* creation, the AP delivers the actual underlying asset — real Bitcoin — to the fund and receives shares; in an *in-cash* creation, the AP delivers cash and the fund itself does the buying. For most commodity ETFs, in-kind is standard, because it keeps the fund out of the trading business and is more tax-efficient. But the SEC, still wary of letting broker-dealers handle Bitcoin directly, pushed the spot Bitcoin ETFs into a **cash-create** model at launch: APs hand over dollars, and the fund (through its trading desk and custodian) buys the coins. This sounds like a technicality, and for a holder it is invisible, but it matters for the plumbing — it means the fund, not the AP, sits closest to the spot purchase, and it adds a small layer of trading friction and cost. Some issuers later worked toward in-kind creation as regulators grew more comfortable. The takeaway for a beginner: whether the AP brings coins or cash, the *result* is the same — new shares only exist if matching value entered the fund — and that one-to-one backing is what makes the wrapper trustworthy.

Two more terms round out the machine. The **spread** is the small gap between the price at which you can buy a share and the price at which you can sell it at the same instant; in a heavily traded ETF like IBIT it is a fraction of a penny, which is part of why volume matters so much. And **tracking error** is how far the fund's return drifts from the return of pure Bitcoin over time; for a well-run spot ETF it is tiny and comes almost entirely from the yearly fee, whereas for a futures ETF it can be large because of the roll cost. When you hear that one Bitcoin product "tracks better" than another, this is the number being discussed.

#### Worked example: the arbitrage that keeps price at NAV

Suppose a spot Bitcoin ETF has a NAV of exactly \$50.00 per share. A wave of buyers piles in and bids the *market* price up to \$50.40. The share is now trading \$0.40 above what its Bitcoin is worth.

An authorized participant sees this. It assembles \$50.00 worth of value per share (cash the fund uses to buy Bitcoin), delivers it to the fund, and in return receives newly created shares valued at NAV, \$50.00 each. It then sells those shares into the market at \$50.40. On a creation block of, say, 50,000 shares, the AP's gross profit is 50,000 x (\$50.40 - \$50.00) = 50,000 x \$0.40 = \$20,000, minus its trading and creation costs.

But here is the self-correcting part: by *selling* 50,000 new shares into the market, the AP increases the supply of shares, which pushes the market price back down toward \$50.00. The moment the gap closes, the profit disappears and the AP stops. **The intuition: the ETF tracks the value of its Bitcoin not because anyone enforces it, but because any deviation is free money that traders race to grab, and the grabbing closes the gap.**

#### Worked example: an AP arbitraging a tiny ETF-vs-NAV gap

The gaps in a liquid ETF are usually far smaller than 40 cents. Say IBIT's NAV is \$60.00 and it briefly trades at \$60.50 — a gap of just \$0.50, less than 1%. A creation unit might be 40,000 shares.

The AP creates 40,000 shares at NAV (\$60.00) and sells them at \$60.50: gross profit 40,000 x \$0.50 = \$20,000. Suppose its all-in costs — buying the underlying, exchange fees, the creation fee the issuer charges (often a few hundred to a couple thousand dollars per creation), and the risk of price moving during the trade — come to \$8,000. Net profit: \$12,000 on a single creation that might take minutes. Do this many times a day across many funds and you see why firms like Jane Street fight to be APs. **The intuition: APs are not charities; they are paid the spread, and that payment is precisely what disciplines the ETF's price to stay within pennies of NAV.**

### Why a familiar wrapper unlocks giant pools of money

Now the punchline of the foundations. The wrapper itself — not the Bitcoin inside — is what unlocked institutional demand. A vast amount of the world's investable money is managed under rules that forbid buying "crypto" directly, but that permit buying any SEC-registered, exchange-listed security. Consider three pools:

- **Registered investment advisers (RIAs)** manage money for individuals and families. Many have compliance policies that bar them from sending a client's money to an unregulated crypto exchange and bar them from holding private keys, but allow them to allocate to any listed ETF. The spot ETF turned Bitcoin into something an RIA could put in a client's portfolio with one line on a statement.
- **Retirement and pension money** lives inside accounts (401(k)s, IRAs, pension plans) whose menus are built from registered funds. You cannot easily put a hardware wallet inside a 401(k); you can put an ETF inside it.
- **Institutional treasuries, endowments, and model portfolios** want auditable, custodied, regulated exposure, not a hot wallet a CFO has to safeguard.

For all of these, the obstacle was never "do we want Bitcoin exposure." The obstacle was "can our rules, auditors, and custodians accommodate it." The ETF wrapper answered yes. That is why a *wrapper* — a piece of legal and operational packaging that contains nothing new — could move tens of billions of dollars that the underlying asset, sitting right there on exchanges for fifteen years, never could.

It is worth dwelling on how mundane and decisive this is, because it is easy to underrate. Picture a financial advisor managing \$500,000,000 for a few hundred families. She might personally believe Bitcoin belongs in a small slice of a modern portfolio. Before 2024, acting on that belief meant opening accounts at a crypto exchange, arranging custody, satisfying her firm's compliance committee that none of it violated the rules she operates under, and explaining to a regulator how client assets sitting on an exchange were protected. The friction was enormous and the career risk real, so almost no advisor did it. After January 2024, acting on the same belief means adding a 1% or 2% allocation to a ticker that already appears on her firm's approved-product list, prints on the client's statement next to their index funds, and is custodied and audited by entities her compliance department already trusts. The *belief* did not change; the *path* did. Multiply that single advisor by the tens of thousands who manage the bulk of household wealth in the U.S., and you see how a piece of packaging that contains nothing new can unleash a flood. The wrapper did not create demand from nothing — the demand was latent all along. It removed the friction that had kept the demand bottled up.

![Before and after comparison of holding Bitcoin directly versus through an ETF wrapper](/imgs/blogs/bitcoin-etfs-and-the-tradfi-bridge-2.png)

The figure above lays out the trade you are making. Holding Bitcoin directly gives you full sovereignty and no fee, but you carry the risk of losing your keys. The ETF wrapper hands the keys to a custodian and charges you a yearly fee, but it slots into your existing brokerage account and lets the institutions in. Neither is "better" in the abstract; they optimize for opposite things, and that tension runs through the rest of the post.

## The long rejection: a decade of SEC "no"

To appreciate January 2024, you have to feel how long the door was nailed shut.

The first serious attempt came in **2013**, when Cameron and Tyler Winklevoss — the twins famous from the early Facebook saga — filed to launch a spot Bitcoin ETF. The U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (the **SEC**, the federal regulator that decides which securities can be sold to the public) sat on it and ultimately rejected it in 2017. Over the following years, a parade of issuers filed, and the SEC rejected essentially all of them, often citing the same worry: the **spot Bitcoin market** — the actual market where coins trade — was, in the SEC's view, too vulnerable to **manipulation** and fraud, and there was no large, regulated market it could partner with to **surveil** for that manipulation. In plain terms: the SEC was not confident the price feeding the fund's NAV was clean, so it would not bless a product that put that price in front of ordinary investors.

There is a real argument buried in this. A spot ETF's NAV depends on a reliable Bitcoin price, and Bitcoin's price is set across dozens of exchanges of wildly varying quality, some offshore, some with thin volume, some with documented wash trading (fake volume created by trading with yourself to inflate the apparent activity). If a bad actor could nudge the reference price, they could distort the fund — for instance, by pushing the price up just before a NAV strike to mint shares cheaply, or down to redeem them richly. The SEC's job is investor protection, and "we are not sure the underlying price is trustworthy" is not a crazy thing for that regulator to say.

The technical hinge of the SEC's standard was the phrase **"surveillance-sharing agreement"** — a deal between the exchange listing the product and a large, regulated market trading the underlying, so that if someone manipulated the price the two could share data and catch them. For stocks and commodities this is routine. For Bitcoin, the SEC kept insisting no adequate surveillance-sharing partner existed, because the spot exchanges were not regulated to its satisfaction. Issuers spent years trying to satisfy this: proposing to share data with Coinbase, building reference rates from multiple venues, commissioning studies arguing that the regulated CME futures market *led* the spot market in price discovery (so that watching the futures was enough to detect manipulation). The SEC rejected each attempt. This is the part that made the eventual court loss so sharp — the agency had drawn a line that its own approval of futures ETFs could not logically defend.

But there was also a glaring inconsistency, and it became the crowbar that pried the door open. In **October 2021**, the SEC *allowed* a Bitcoin **futures** ETF — the ProShares fund trading as **BITO** — to launch, and it gathered roughly a billion dollars in its first days, proving the appetite was real. Futures, the SEC reasoned, traded on the CME (the Chicago Mercantile Exchange), a large regulated exchange overseen by the SEC's sister regulator the CFTC, so there was a surveillance partner and the manipulation concern was addressed. Fine. But here is the catch: CME Bitcoin futures *settle to a price derived from the very same spot markets the SEC called untrustworthy.* If the spot price was clean enough to settle a futures contract that the SEC approved, on what logic was it too dirty to value a spot ETF? That contradiction would prove fatal to the SEC's position in court.

It is worth dwelling on why a futures ETF is, for an ordinary long-term holder, the *worse* product despite getting approved first. BITO does not own Bitcoin; it owns near-dated CME futures and must "roll" them forward as they expire — sell the contract about to mature and buy a later one. In the common condition called *contango*, the later contract costs more than the expiring one, so every roll quietly bleeds value. Over months and years that drag can cause a futures ETF to meaningfully *underperform* the very Bitcoin it is supposed to track. So the regulatory sequence had a perverse shape: the SEC first blessed the version that tracks Bitcoin *badly* (futures), and resisted for two more years the version that tracks it *well* (spot) — purely on the surveillance-partner technicality, not on which served investors better.

## Grayscale, GBTC, and the discount that launched a lawsuit

The most important character in this drama is not BlackRock. It is Grayscale, and its product GBTC, the **Grayscale Bitcoin Trust**.

Grayscale launched GBTC in 2013 as a closed-end trust — the fixed-share structure from the foundations. For years it was the *only* easy way for a U.S. brokerage or fund to get Bitcoin exposure without touching a crypto exchange. And because demand was huge and supply was effectively capped (new shares could be created by big investors but not redeemed by ordinary ones, and newly created shares were locked up for six months before they could be sold), GBTC traded at a *premium* to its NAV — sometimes 20%, 30%, even higher. People were paying \$1.30 for a dollar's worth of Bitcoin, simply because GBTC was the only door.

That premium was, for a while, a money machine — and it set up one of the most destructive trades in crypto history, which we will dissect in the real-markets section. But premiums built on scarcity are fragile. As more ways to get Bitcoin appeared (futures ETFs, easier exchanges, competing products), the scarcity value of GBTC's wrapper evaporated. The premium shrank, hit zero in early 2021, and then flipped hard. By late 2022, in the wreckage of that year's crypto collapse, **GBTC traded at a discount of roughly 40% to its NAV** — meaning a share representing a dollar of Bitcoin changed hands for about sixty cents. Billions of dollars of value seemed to be "trapped": holders could see the Bitcoin backing their shares, but the closed-end structure gave them no way to redeem it and capture the difference.

#### Worked example: GBTC at a -40% discount to its Bitcoin NAV

Suppose GBTC's NAV is \$15.00 per share — that is, each share represents \$15.00 of actual Bitcoin held by the trust. But because it is a closed-end fund and the mood is grim, the market price is \$9.00 per share.

The discount is (\$9.00 - \$15.00) ÷ \$15.00 = -\$6.00 ÷ \$15.00 = **-40%**. A buyer at \$9.00 is purchasing \$15.00 of Bitcoin for sixty cents on the dollar. If GBTC could be *converted* to an ETF — turning on redemption so APs could arbitrage the gap — that \$6.00 per share would snap back to the holder. On, say, 700,000,000 shares outstanding, a -40% discount represents 700,000,000 x \$6.00 = **\$4,200,000,000** of value separated from its owners by nothing but the wrapper's structure.

**The intuition: in a closed-end fund the wrapper itself can be worth dramatically less (or more) than its contents, because nothing forces the two together — which is exactly the flaw the ETF's creation/redemption machine fixes.**

That trapped \$4 billion-plus is what motivated the lawsuit. Grayscale had repeatedly asked the SEC to let it **convert** GBTC from a closed-end trust into a spot ETF — which would switch on redemption, invite APs, and collapse the discount to near zero. The SEC refused, again citing manipulation. In **2022**, Grayscale sued the SEC in federal court.

In **August 2023**, a three-judge panel of the U.S. Court of Appeals for the D.C. Circuit ruled for Grayscale, and the reasoning was precisely the contradiction noted earlier. The court found the SEC's denial **arbitrary and capricious** — a legal term meaning the agency had failed to explain itself rationally. The SEC had approved Bitcoin *futures* ETFs while rejecting *spot* ones, even though both relied on the same underlying spot price. The agency, the court said, had not given a coherent reason to treat two nearly identical products differently. The SEC could not have its cake and eat it too.

This is the part that matters for the thesis: **the spot Bitcoin ETF was not approved because the SEC decided Bitcoin was good. It was approved because a court told the SEC its rejection was legally indefensible.** Wall Street did not so much open the door as have it knocked off the hinges by a judge. After the ruling the SEC chose not to appeal, and the path to approval was clear for everyone — Grayscale's conversion and the dozen fresh applications waiting behind it.

## January 2024: eleven ETFs launch at once

On **January 10, 2024**, the SEC approved a batch of spot Bitcoin ETFs simultaneously, and they began trading the next day. Approving them all at once was deliberate: the SEC did not want to hand any single issuer a head start, so roughly eleven funds went live together in a single, unprecedented launch.

The lineup read like a roll call of the financial establishment that had spent years deriding Bitcoin. **BlackRock**, the largest asset manager on earth, launched the **iShares Bitcoin Trust (IBIT)**. **Fidelity** launched **FBTC**. **Ark/21Shares**, **Bitwise (BITB)**, **VanEck**, **Invesco/Galaxy**, **Franklin Templeton**, **WisdomTree**, and others launched their own. And **Grayscale's GBTC** finally converted from its closed-end trust into a spot ETF, switching on the redemption mechanism that erased its discount overnight — the lawsuit's whole point.

The symbolism was hard to miss. Larry Fink, BlackRock's chief executive, had in 2017 called Bitcoin "an index of money laundering." By 2023 he was describing it as a flight-to-quality asset and putting his firm's iShares brand on a Bitcoin fund. That reversal — from the most powerful skeptic to the most powerful distributor — is the human-scale version of the whole thesis: the establishment did not defeat Bitcoin, it decided to sell it. When the biggest asset manager in the world concludes there is more money in wrapping a thing than in dismissing it, the wrapping happens fast.

The competition was, from the first hour, a **fee war**. The yearly **management fee** (also called the *expense ratio*) is the slice of your assets the issuer keeps each year for running the fund. The new entrants set fees astonishingly low to grab assets — many around **0.20% to 0.25%** per year, with temporary waivers to zero for the first months or first few billion dollars. The lone holdout was converted GBTC, which kept a fat **1.50%** fee, betting that its existing holders would be too inert (or too tax-averse, since selling triggers taxes) to leave.

#### Worked example: a \$1,000,000,000 inflow buying X Bitcoin at spot

To feel how ETF demand becomes on-chain demand, follow a single billion-dollar day. Suppose, across all the spot ETFs, net new money on a given day is \$1,000,000,000, and Bitcoin's spot price that day is \$60,000 per coin.

Net of the small spread and creation fees, the funds must acquire roughly \$1,000,000,000 ÷ \$60,000 = **16,666.7 BTC** to back the new shares. Those coins are bought on the spot market through the APs and delivered to the custodians.

Now compare that to supply. After Bitcoin's 2024 halving (the roughly four-year event that cut the new-issuance reward), miners produce about **450 BTC per day** of brand-new supply. A single \$1 billion ETF inflow day demands 16,666.7 BTC — about **37 times** the entire day's new mined supply. The coins do not come from miners, of course; they come from existing holders willing to sell at some price. But the arithmetic shows why sustained ETF inflows are a powerful, persistent bid: the wrapper converts a brokerage click into relentless real buying pressure against a fixed, slow issuance schedule. **The intuition: the ETF is "demand on tap" — it pipes ordinary-account money straight into spot Bitcoin purchases, and the daily flow can dwarf the daily new supply.**

![Graph showing how ETF demand flows from brokerages through APs and custodians to spot Bitcoin](/imgs/blogs/bitcoin-etfs-and-the-tradfi-bridge-5.png)

The graph above is the demand-transmission map. Money enters from two broad sources on the left — RIAs and retirement accounts, and retail brokerages — converges on the ETF shares, triggers a creation through an authorized participant, and ends as a real spot purchase by the custodian that adds to Bitcoin's price and liquidity. Every arrow is a forward step; there is no magic. The click at the top genuinely moves coins at the bottom.

### How fast IBIT gathered assets

The headline of 2024 was the speed. BlackRock's IBIT did not just succeed; it set records. Within weeks it had crossed several billion dollars in **assets under management** (AUM — the total value of everything the fund holds). Within its first year it had gathered on the order of **\$50 billion** (approximate, and growing thereafter), making it one of the fastest-growing ETFs ever launched, in *any* asset class, beating funds that track stocks and bonds with decades of head start. (All AUM and flow figures here are approximate and as-of 2024–2025; they move daily.)

Why IBIT and not a cheaper rival like Bitwise's 0.20% BITB? Three reasons, none of them about the fee. First, **distribution**: BlackRock's iShares brand sits on every advisor's platform, in every model portfolio, with sales relationships everywhere. An advisor does not go shopping for a Bitcoin ETF; they reach for the name already on their approved list, and iShares is on every list. Second, **trust as a feature**: for a compliance officer signing off, "BlackRock" is a sentence-ender; the marginal basis points of fee are noise next to the comfort of the biggest name in asset management standing behind the product. Third, **liquidity begets liquidity**: the fund that trades the most has the tightest spreads, which attracts large traders and options markets, which deepens the volume further — a flywheel that is self-reinforcing and very hard for a latecomer to break. Once IBIT became the most liquid Bitcoin ETF, institutions that care about getting in and out in size *had* to use it, which made it more liquid still. The lesson, familiar from every ETF launch in history, is that in this business *distribution and brand usually beat price* — the cheapest fund is rarely the biggest one.

The growth also fed a second-order milestone: options on IBIT. Once the shares were liquid enough, exchanges listed **options** (contracts giving the right to buy or sell IBIT at a set price), which let institutions build hedged and leveraged Bitcoin strategies entirely inside the regulated brokerage system — no crypto exchange, no wallet, no key. That is the bridge widening into a highway: not just spot exposure on tap, but a full derivatives toolkit wrapped around it, all in the familiar venue. Each such addition deepens the integration and makes the ETF, rather than the underlying coin, the place where a growing share of Bitcoin price discovery and risk-transfer actually happens.

#### Worked example: the 0.25% management fee on \$50 billion of assets

Take IBIT at roughly \$50,000,000,000 of assets and a 0.25% yearly fee. The annual revenue is \$50,000,000,000 x 0.0025 = **\$125,000,000 per year**. That is \$125 million a year for operating a fund whose hard work — actually safeguarding the Bitcoin — is outsourced to the custodian.

Compare GBTC: at roughly \$20,000,000,000 of (shrinking) assets and a 1.50% fee, its revenue is \$20,000,000,000 x 0.015 = **\$300,000,000 per year** — more than double IBIT's, on less than half the assets. That gap is exactly why GBTC bled assets to the new funds: rational money does not pay 1.50% for the same Bitcoin it can hold at 0.25%, so billions flowed out of GBTC and into IBIT and FBTC over 2024. **The intuition: a management fee is a small percentage that becomes an enormous, recurring business at scale — and an indefensibly *high* fee is a slow-motion exodus, because the product inside the wrapper is identical.**

## The plumbing, layer by layer

It is worth pausing to see the full stack of what you actually own when you buy a share of a spot Bitcoin ETF, because the layers are where both the convenience and the irony live.

![Stack of ETF wrapper layers from shares to trust to custodian to Bitcoin](/imgs/blogs/bitcoin-etfs-and-the-tradfi-bridge-6.png)

At the top, what you hold in your brokerage is an **ETF share** — a security, an entry in your account, redeemable (by APs, not you) and tradable like a stock. Below that sits the **trust or fund**, the legal entity that is the actual owner of the Bitcoin and that issues the shares. Below *that* is the **custodian** — for most of these funds, Coinbase Custody — which physically (well, cryptographically) holds the private keys in cold storage. And at the very bottom is the thing it is all about: **Bitcoin on-chain**, real coins at real addresses on the actual blockchain.

Notice what each layer does. Each one adds convenience — a familiar security, a regulated legal owner, a professional key-holder — and each one moves *you* one more step away from the coin. In direct self-custody, you *are* all the middle layers: you are the legal owner, you are the custodian, you hold the key. In the ETF, every middle layer is someone else, and the chain of trust runs through a money manager, a trust, and a custodian before it reaches a single satoshi.

This stack also concentrates a quiet new risk. Because most of the spot ETFs use the *same* custodian — Coinbase Custody holds the coins for the bulk of them — a very large share of all ETF-held Bitcoin sits with one firm. If that single custodian were ever compromised, hacked, insolvent, or legally frozen, it would touch many funds at once. The funds and regulators are aware of this concentration; it is disclosed in the filings. But it is the kind of single point of failure that Bitcoin's design was specifically meant to avoid, now reintroduced at the heart of Bitcoin's largest demand engine.

## The Ether ETFs, and the precedent

The Bitcoin door, once forced open, did not stay shut for the next asset. In **July 2024**, the SEC approved and launched spot **Ether** ETFs — funds holding **ETH**, the native token of the Ethereum blockchain, the second-largest cryptocurrency. The mechanics are identical: creation/redemption, authorized participants, a custodian holding the coins, shares trading on an exchange.

The Ether launch mattered for two reasons. First, it confirmed that the Bitcoin ETF was not a one-off exception but a **template**: once the legal and operational pattern existed, it could be stamped onto the next asset that cleared the bar. Second, it sharpened a debate the Bitcoin approval had dodged. Ether, unlike Bitcoin, can be **staked** — locked up to help secure the Ethereum network in exchange for a yield, somewhat like interest. The initial Ether ETFs were approved *without* staking, meaning holders gave up that native yield in exchange for the wrapper. That is a real cost the ETF imposes, and a reminder that the wrapper is never free: it can strip away features (self-custody, staking yield, the ability to actually *use* the token on-chain) that direct ownership keeps.

The Ether ETFs gathered assets far more slowly than Bitcoin's, partly because Ether's institutional story was less developed and partly because giving up staking yield made the wrapper a worse deal relative to holding the coin. But the precedent was set: the bridge built for Bitcoin now carries other traffic.

## What institutionalization actually changed

Step back from the plumbing and ask the bigger question: what does it *mean* that Bitcoin now flows mostly through this regulated wrapper? Three changes stand out, and each cuts two ways.

**Deeper liquidity, at a price.** The ETFs brought enormous volume and tight spreads. **Liquidity** is the ease of buying or selling without moving the price; a deep, liquid market absorbs large orders with little impact, a thin one lurches. The ETFs made Bitcoin exposure far more liquid: it is now trivial for a large institution to get in and out in size, during market hours, through familiar rails, with a market maker on the other side. That depth is genuinely good for the asset's maturity — it narrows the cost of trading and makes Bitcoin usable by players who could never have touched a thin offshore exchange. But it also concentrates a lot of "Bitcoin" trading into a wrapper that only trades when the *stock market* is open. Bitcoin itself trades 24 hours a day, 365 days a year; the ETF does not. So a growing share of price discovery now pauses on nights, weekends, and U.S. holidays, which can leave gaps when the ETF reopens Monday morning and snaps to wherever the 24-hour coin has wandered — occasionally a jarring jump for ETF holders who did not realize the thing they own takes nights and weekends off while the asset behind it never sleeps.

**Tighter macro correlation.** This is the deepest change, and the one most in tension with Bitcoin's founding story. Bitcoin was sold for years as an *uncorrelated* asset — a hedge, "digital gold," something that zigs when stocks zag. **Correlation** here just means the tendency of two things to move together: high correlation means when one falls the other tends to fall too. The pitch was that Bitcoin had *low* correlation to stocks, so adding a little of it could improve a portfolio without adding the same risk. But once Bitcoin lives inside the portfolios of institutions who run it through the same risk models as their stocks and bonds, it starts to *behave* like those holdings. When a macro shock hits — a surprise interest-rate move, a credit scare, a liquidity squeeze — big funds cut risk across the board, and they sell Bitcoin alongside everything else, because to them it is now just another line in a risk model, sold first precisely because it is the most volatile and most expendable. Institutionalization, in other words, partly *destroys* the very uncorrelation that was part of Bitcoin's pitch. The asset's fate becomes more tightly bound to interest rates, liquidity conditions, and the same macro forces that move the S&P 500. You buy the wrapper to get Bitcoin into your portfolio; the wrapper, in turn, makes Bitcoin move more like the rest of your portfolio. The thing that made Bitcoin attractive as a diversifier is partly eroded by the very channel that made it easy to buy.

**"Demand on tap."** The phrase captures the structural shift. Before the ETF, getting institutional money into Bitcoin required each institution to solve custody, compliance, and operations on its own — a tall barrier. After the ETF, that demand is *on tap*: any advisor can add it with a click, any model portfolio can include a slice, any 401(k) menu can list it. The barrier dropped from "build a crypto operation" to "type a ticker." That is a profound widening of the funnel — and it means a much larger share of Bitcoin's demand now depends on the moods, rules, and flows of traditional finance rather than on the crypto-native faithful.

There is a quieter consequence, too: a slow drift of Bitcoin's effective control toward a handful of large institutions. Every coin held inside an ETF is a coin that is *not* circulating, *not* being self-custodied, and whose on-chain governance weight (in the rare cases Bitcoin holders are polled, or in any future fork dispute) is exercised — if at all — by the custodian and issuer, not the underlying investor. A growing fraction of all Bitcoin now sits, immobile, behind the wrapper. That is not inherently sinister; locked-up supply has been part of every asset's maturation. But it does mean the asset that was designed so *no one* could be a gatekeeper now has a small set of gatekeepers controlling its largest, fastest-growing pool.

## How it makes money, and how it can fail

It is worth being precise about both the business and the risks, because an INSTITUTION is best understood by following its incentives and its failure modes.

**How the issuer makes money** is simple and recurring: the yearly management fee on assets, as the worked example above showed. There is no performance fee, no spread capture (that goes to the APs and market makers), no lending of the coins in the base products. The entire business is *assets times fee*, which is why the launch was an asset-gathering land grab: in a fee war where the product inside the wrapper is identical Bitcoin, the only thing to compete on is how many dollars you can attract and keep. A few extra basis points of fee are worth tens of millions a year only if you can hold the assets, and you can only hold the assets if your distribution and brand keep money from wandering to a cheaper rival. That dynamic — gather first, defend on brand, accept thin margins per dollar — is why a giant like BlackRock could win even while charging more than a nimble competitor.

**The custodian risk** is the one most specific to crypto. Because the coins are real and bearer-like — whoever holds the keys holds the money, with no chargebacks and no recovery — the custodian is the load-bearing wall. A hack, an insider theft, a botched key ceremony, a bankruptcy, or a court order freezing assets at the custodian would hit the fund directly, and because most spot ETFs use the *same* custodian, it would hit many funds at once. Issuers mitigate this with cold storage, insurance, audits, and proof-of-reserves disclosures, but the concentration is structural and disclosed as a risk factor. This is the single point of failure Bitcoin was built to eliminate, reintroduced at the center of its largest demand engine.

**The wrapper-versus-coin mismatch** is a subtler operational risk. The ETF trades only during stock-market hours, while Bitcoin trades nonstop. If the price moves violently over a weekend, the ETF opens Monday with a gap, and in those first minutes the share price can briefly diverge from a stale NAV until APs catch up. In a true market panic — when even APs pull back and creation/redemption slows — an ETF can trade at a small premium or discount for longer than usual. It will not behave like the old closed-end GBTC, which had *no* redemption at all, but the arbitrage that disciplines the price is not magic; it depends on APs being willing and able to trade, and that willingness is itself a function of market stress.

**Counterparty and concentration risk in the flows** rounds out the picture. A large share of creations and redemptions runs through a small number of APs and market makers. If those firms retrench during stress, the arbitrage loop loses horsepower exactly when it is most needed. And because so much demand now arrives through model portfolios and advisor platforms, a single large allocator changing its model — adding or removing a Bitcoin sleeve — can move flows in size, mechanically forcing the fund to buy or sell real coins. The wrapper made demand convenient; it also made demand *coordinated* in a way that fifteen years of scattered, idiosyncratic crypto buyers never were.

## Common misconceptions

**"A spot Bitcoin ETF means you own Bitcoin."** Not directly. You own a *share* of a fund that owns Bitcoin held by a custodian. You cannot withdraw the coins, you cannot move them on-chain, you cannot spend them, and you do not hold the keys. For exposure to the price, that is fine. But if your reason for wanting Bitcoin is self-sovereignty — money no one can freeze or seize — the ETF gives you essentially the opposite: an asset held by a custodian, inside a fund, inside a brokerage, all of which are subject to courts, regulators, and intermediaries. The price exposure is real; the "be your own bank" property is gone.

**"The ETF can print fake Bitcoin or be fractionally reserved."** The whole structure is built to prevent this. Creations require the underlying value to be delivered *before* shares are minted, the coins sit with a regulated custodian, and the holdings are published (most spot ETFs disclose their Bitcoin holdings and addresses, and are audited). It is not a bank taking deposits and lending them out; it is a warehouse that must hold one unit of stuff for every claim it issues. The risks are real — custodian failure, operational error, the wrapper trading briefly off NAV in a stressed market — but "the ETF secretly holds less Bitcoin than it owes" is not the live risk; the structure is designed around exactly that fear.

**"The ETF trades at whatever price, disconnected from Bitcoin."** That was true of the *closed-end* GBTC, which is precisely why it swung to a 40% discount. It is *not* true of the spot ETFs, because creation/redemption and authorized participants arbitrage any gap away within pennies. Conflating the old closed-end behavior with the new ETF behavior is the single most common error in this area. The wrapper structure is the whole difference: one lets price drift, the other glues it to NAV.

**"BlackRock won because it had the lowest fee."** It did not have the lowest fee — Bitwise and others undercut it. BlackRock won on distribution, brand trust, and the liquidity flywheel. In the ETF business, the cheapest fund rarely wins; the best-distributed, most-trusted, most-liquid one usually does. The fee matters at the margin, but it is not why IBIT gathered \$50 billion.

**"The ETF approval was the SEC endorsing Bitcoin."** No regulator endorsed Bitcoin. The SEC approved the products because a federal court ruled its refusal arbitrary and capricious — it lost in litigation and chose not to appeal. Approval was a legal defeat managed into an orderly launch, not a blessing. The SEC's own statements at the time were pointedly grudging.

**"Institutional adoption makes Bitcoin safer and less volatile."** It deepens liquidity, which can dampen some volatility, but it also welds Bitcoin to macro markets. When institutions de-risk in a downturn, they now sell Bitcoin alongside stocks, which can *amplify* drawdowns rather than cushion them. "Safer" and "more correlated to a market crash" are not the same thing, and institutionalization buys you the second while only partly delivering the first.

## How it shows up in real markets

**The GBTC premium, the arbitrage trade, and the 3AC-era unwind.** GBTC's old premium was not just trivia — it powered one of the most catastrophic trades of the last cycle. Big players (most infamously the hedge fund **Three Arrows Capital**, or **3AC**) ran a trade: borrow Bitcoin from a crypto lender, deposit it to create GBTC shares at NAV, wait out the six-month lockup, then sell those shares into the market at the *premium*, pocketing the gap before repaying the borrowed coins. While GBTC traded at, say, a 20% premium, this looked like a near-riskless 20% on every turn of the trade. It became so popular that the borrowing-and-creating itself helped sustain the premium for a while — a self-reinforcing loop. But the trade depended on the premium *staying positive*. As competing products eroded GBTC's scarcity, the premium collapsed through zero in early 2021 and flipped to a deep discount by 2022. Now the shares 3AC and others held — often pledged as **collateral** (assets posted to back a loan) for yet more borrowing — were worth far *less* than the Bitcoin owed, and the closed-end structure offered no redemption to escape. As the discount widened toward -40%, lenders issued margin calls, the collateral could not cover the debts, and the unwind helped sink 3AC and cascaded through the crypto lenders (Celsius, Voyager, BlockFi, and others) that had financed the trade. The closed-end wrapper's flaw was not abstract; the inability to redeem at NAV, combined with leverage, vaporized real firms and billions of dollars.

**The January 2024 launch flows.** The launch itself was a live demonstration of "demand on tap." In the opening weeks, the new ETFs (led by IBIT and FBTC) took in net new money even as *converted GBTC bled assets* — holders fleeing its 1.50% fee for the cheaper funds, with GBTC's outflows partly offsetting the new funds' inflows. Watching the daily flow data became a market obsession: net positive flows read as institutional accumulation; the GBTC outflow was the counter-current. The episode showed both the power of the new funnel and the way a single high-fee legacy product could leak for months.

**IBIT's record-setting growth.** IBIT's ascent to tens of billions in AUM within a year was, by the standard metrics, among the fastest in ETF history. It became a case study (cited across the asset-management industry) in how brand and distribution, fused to a genuinely novel asset, can compound an ETF's assets at a speed that decades-old stock and bond funds never matched. It also became the single largest pool of "institutional Bitcoin," concentrating influence over flows in one issuer's product.

**Outflows during drawdowns.** The flip side of "demand on tap" is that the tap runs in reverse. During sharp Bitcoin drawdowns, the ETFs have seen multi-day stretches of *net outflows* — APs redeeming shares, which forces the funds to sell real Bitcoin, which adds selling pressure on the way down just as inflows added buying pressure on the way up. This is the mechanical proof that the wrapper does not only support the price; it transmits both directions. Institutionalized demand can become institutionalized supply the moment risk appetite turns, and because that appetite is now tied to macro conditions, the outflows tend to cluster with broader market stress.

**The Ether ETF approval.** When the spot Ether ETFs launched in July 2024, the market got to watch the template applied to a second asset — and got a quieter result. Ether's inflows were modest next to Bitcoin's, partly because of the stripped-out staking yield and a thinner institutional narrative. The episode is the clearest evidence that the *wrapper* is now a reusable piece of financial infrastructure, while also showing that the wrapper alone does not guarantee the same demand: the asset inside, and what the wrapper takes away from it, still matter.

**GBTC's discount snapping shut at conversion.** The cleanest natural experiment in the whole saga: the day GBTC converted from a closed-end trust to a spot ETF, its persistent discount collapsed to roughly zero, because redemption switched on and APs could finally arbitrage it. The trapped value the lawsuit was fought over was released by nothing more than changing the wrapper's structure from closed-end to open-ended ETF. If you ever want a one-event proof that "the wrapper, not the Bitcoin, was the problem," that is it.

## The irony at the center

It is worth saying plainly, because it is the heart of the story. Bitcoin's original promise, set out in its 2008 white paper and carried by the cypherpunk movement that built around it, was *removal of the trusted intermediary*. No bank to hold your money, no institution that could freeze or seize it, no middleman taking a cut — you hold your keys, you control your coins, you transact peer to peer. That ethos is explored in [Bitcoin and the cypherpunk vision](/blog/trading/crypto/bitcoin-and-the-cypherpunk-vision), and it is genuinely radical.

The spot Bitcoin ETF is, structurally, the negation of that promise. In the ETF, a **money manager** runs the fund, a **custodian** holds the keys, a **brokerage** holds your shares, and the whole thing is overseen by the very regulators Bitcoin was meant to route around. You have traded sovereignty for convenience. And the firms now wielding the most influence over Bitcoin's largest demand channel are the same index-fund and asset-management giants that already dominate ownership of public companies — the world examined in [the Big Three: how BlackRock, Vanguard, and State Street quietly own Corporate America](/blog/trading/finance/big-three-blackrock-vanguard-state-street). The asset built to escape Wall Street's institutions is now most easily bought through one of them.

None of this means the ETF is bad. It is wildly convenient, it is appropriate for a great many investors who want price exposure and not a hardware wallet, and it has plausibly accelerated Bitcoin's acceptance by years. But it is honest to name what was given up. The bridge that Wall Street built to Bitcoin carries traffic in both directions: it brought institutional money on-chain, and it brought Bitcoin into the embrace of exactly the intermediated, regulated, custodied financial system it was designed to make optional. For a fuller map of the institutions on the other side of that bridge, see the [field guide to financial institutions](/blog/trading/finance/field-guide-to-financial-institutions); and for the crypto-native venues the ETFs partly displaced and partly rely on for price, see [centralized crypto exchanges: Binance and Coinbase](/blog/trading/crypto/centralized-crypto-exchanges-binance-coinbase).

## When this matters to you / further reading

If you are deciding *how* to hold Bitcoin, the ETF-versus-direct choice is the practical version of everything above. The ETF wins on convenience, tax-account compatibility (it fits in an IRA or 401(k)), and freedom from key-management risk — at the cost of a yearly fee, no self-custody, no on-chain use, and a custodian counterparty. Direct self-custody wins on sovereignty and zero fee — at the cost of carrying the full, unforgiving burden of not losing your keys. Neither is universally right; they optimize for opposite things, and the right answer depends on whether you want *exposure to the price* or *the thing Bitcoin was actually built to be*.

If you are trying to *read the market*, watch the ETF flow data, but read it carefully. Net inflows are real buying pressure; net outflows are real selling pressure; and because that money is now institutional, it tends to move with macro conditions — rates, liquidity, risk appetite — rather than independently of them. The flows are a genuine signal, but they are a signal about *Wall Street's* appetite for Bitcoin, which is not the same as the crypto-native demand of earlier cycles.

And if you care about the bigger arc, hold onto the central tension. A wrapper is never neutral. It can release trapped value (GBTC's discount snapping shut) or trap it (the closed-end years). It can democratize access (a retiree's 401(k) can now hold Bitcoin) while concentrating control (in a handful of issuers and one dominant custodian). It can deepen an asset's liquidity while welding it to the very system it was meant to escape. The spot Bitcoin ETF did all of these at once. That is what it looks like when Wall Street finally swallows something it spent a decade trying to spit out: not a rejection and not an endorsement, but an absorption — the asset survives, transformed, on the system's own terms.
