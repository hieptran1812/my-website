> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — An option's quoted price is not what you trade at. Every fill gives up part of the bid-ask spread, you pay it on the way in *and* the way out, and it compounds with every leg you add — so liquidity, not direction, is what quietly decides whether your edge survives.
>
> - Read four numbers off the chain before you trust a strike: open interest, today's volume, spread width (in dollars **and** as a percent of price), and the depth sitting on the quote.
> - The spread is tiny near the money — about **2%** of a \$2.45 at-the-money call — and brutal in the wings, blowing past **50%** of price on far-OTM strikes. You pay it twice on a round trip.
> - Each leg has its own spread, crossed entry and exit: a single option = 2 crossings, a vertical = 4, a four-leg iron condor = **8**. On our condor that is **\$48 of spread against a \$102 credit** — nearly half the credit gone before the trade can work.
> - The one rule: default to a **marketable limit near the mid**, never a market order into a wide quote, and never enter a strike you cannot exit.

It is a quiet Tuesday and you are short an iron condor on a mid-cap name that has drifted nowhere for a week. Theta is bleeding in your favor, the position is up a little, and you decide to take it off and book the win. You click "close," the platform routes a market order on all four legs, and your fill comes back. You stare at it. The trade that the screen said was worth a \$0.95 credit just cost you \$1.40 to buy back. You were *right* about the stock, right about the volatility, right about the range — and you still lost money on the trade. Nobody moved the stock against you. The spread ate you.

This is the part of options trading that almost no introductory material covers honestly, because it is not glamorous and it does not fit on a payoff diagram. The payoff diagram assumes you transact at one clean price. Real markets quote you two prices — a **bid** (what someone will pay you to sell) and an **ask** (what someone will charge you to buy) — and the gap between them, the **bid-ask spread**, is a cost you pay on every single fill. It is invisible because it never shows up as a line item. There is no "spread fee" on your statement. It is baked into the price you transact at, and over a year of trading it can be larger than every commission you pay combined.

This post is about that hidden tax: where it comes from, how to read whether a strike is liquid enough to trade, why the spread is a cost you pay *twice* and that *compounds* across multi-leg structures, why you almost never fill at the "mid," and how the whole bundle of frictions — spread plus commissions plus the market-maker's edge plus slippage — can turn a backtest that looked like free money into a live account that bleeds. We end on a playbook. By the time we are done, you should never again click "market order" on an option, and you should be able to look at a chain and tell, in five seconds, whether a strike is a place you can actually trade or a trap you can get into but never out of.

![Bid-ask spread as a percent of option price across strikes](/imgs/blogs/liquidity-bid-ask-spreads-and-getting-filled-the-hidden-tax-1.png)

That chart is the whole post in one image, so sit with it. The amber line is the bid-ask spread expressed as a *percent of the option's own price*, walking from in-the-money strikes on the left, through at-the-money in the middle, out to far-out-of-the-money strikes on the right. Near the money it hugs the floor — around 2% of a \$2.45 option. Step a few strikes out and it climbs: 5%, then 11%, then 24%, and by the time you reach a strike worth a quarter, the spread is **55% of the price** and still rising. The dollar spread barely changed — it went from a nickel to maybe fifteen cents. But that nickel is trivial against a \$2.45 option and ruinous against a \$0.28 one. The hidden tax is not a flat fee. It is a percentage, and the percentage explodes exactly where beginners love to shop: the cheap, far-out lottery tickets.

## Foundations: what the bid, the ask, and the spread actually are

Before we can talk about the cost, we have to be precise about the prices. An option, like a stock, does not have *a* price during trading hours. It has a **quote**, and a quote is a pair: the highest price any buyer is currently willing to pay (the **bid**) and the lowest price any seller is currently willing to accept (the **ask**, also called the offer). If the bid is \$2.40 and the ask is \$2.50, the option is "\$2.40 bid, \$2.50 offered," and the **spread** is the \$0.10 gap between them. The **mid-price** — \$2.45 here — is just the arithmetic midpoint, and it is a useful reference, but, as we will hammer repeatedly, it is *not a price you can demand to trade at*.

Why does the gap exist at all? Because the person on the other side of your trade is, most of the time, a **market maker** — a firm whose business is to stand ready to buy from anyone who wants to sell and sell to anyone who wants to buy, continuously, on thousands of strikes at once. They do not have a directional view on your stock. They make money on the spread: buy at the bid, sell at the ask, pocket the difference, and hedge away the risk in between. The spread is their compensation for three things: the *inventory risk* of holding a position they did not choose, the *adverse-selection risk* that the person hitting their quote knows something they don't, and the *operational cost* of running the quoting machine. We have a whole post on [how an options market maker thinks](/blog/trading/options-volatility/how-an-options-market-maker-thinks-the-other-side-of-your-trade) — the short version is that the spread is the price of immediacy, and you, the impatient trader who wants to transact *now*, are the one who pays it.

A few terms you need so the rest of the post reads cleanly. To **cross the spread** (or "pay the spread," or "lift the offer" when buying / "hit the bid" when selling) means to transact immediately at the other side's price rather than waiting for someone to come to you. A **tick** is the minimum price increment — for most US options it is \$0.05 below \$3.00 and \$0.10 above, though many liquid names quote in pennies. The **multiplier** is 100: an option controls 100 shares, so a quoted price of \$2.45 means \$245 per contract, and a \$0.10 spread means \$10 per contract of cost. If the multiplier mechanics are fuzzy, the [options-chain-and-contract-mechanics post](/blog/trading/options-volatility/the-options-chain-and-contract-mechanics-multiplier-expiry-settlement) builds them from zero; here we just use the fact that every per-share number gets multiplied by 100 to become a per-contract dollar.

There is one more idea hiding in that definition that the rest of the post leans on, so it is worth slowing down for. A quote is not a price; it is a *promise to trade in one direction*. The bid is a promise to *buy*, the ask is a promise to *sell*, and those promises are made by different participants (or the same maker on both sides) at different prices. When you transact, you do not "meet at the mid" — you accept one of those standing promises, which means you accept *their* price, not the average of the two. The mid-price is a statistic computed from two promises that point in opposite directions; nobody ever literally offered to trade there. That is the whole reason the mid is a fiction for execution purposes, and it is why the cover chart measures the spread, not the mid: the spread is the real, transactable gap, and the mid is the comfortable lie in between.

It also matters *why* the spread is the price of immediacy specifically. If you are willing to *be* the patient side — to post a resting bid and wait for an impatient seller to come hit it — you can sometimes *earn* part of the spread instead of paying it, just as the market maker does. Retail traders rarely have the speed or the queue priority to do this reliably, but the mental model is important: the spread is a fee for *demanding* a trade right now, and the more you can shift toward *supplying* liquidity (resting limit orders, no urgency) instead of *demanding* it (market orders, chasing a move), the less of the spread you hand over. Almost everything practical in this post is a way of moving a notch toward the patient, liquidity-supplying side without losing the fill entirely.

#### Worked example: the round-trip spread as a percent of premium

Let's make the cover chart concrete with one strike. Take an at-the-money 30-day call on a \$100 stock with 20% implied volatility. The Black-Scholes model prices it at a mid of **\$2.45** per share, or \$245 per contract. Suppose the market is \$2.40 bid, \$2.50 offered — a \$0.10 spread, which is a perfectly normal, *tight* quote for a liquid ATM option.

You buy one contract. You don't fill at the \$2.45 mid; you lift the offer at **\$2.50**. That is \$0.05 per share above mid — half the spread — or \$5 on the contract. Now suppose, immediately, you change your mind and sell it back. You don't get \$2.45; you hit the bid at **\$2.40**, another \$0.05 below mid, another \$5. You have transacted nothing of substance — bought and instantly sold — and you are down \$0.10 per share, **\$10 per contract**. As a fraction of the \$245 you put up, that round trip cost **4.1%**. The stock has to move enough for the option to gain 4.1% just to get you back to flat. That is the spread tax on the *most liquid* kind of option you can trade. Now reread the cover chart and notice that 4% is the *good* end.

The intuition to carry forward: the spread is not a one-time toll, it is a percentage drag on the capital you cycle, and that percentage is set entirely by *how liquid the thing you're trading is* — not by how right you are.

One more piece of background before the mechanics, because it explains *why* the spread behaves the way it does across strikes. The market maker on the other side of your quote is not setting that \$0.10 width arbitrarily. They size it to cover their risk, and their risk is larger where the option is harder to hedge and harder to offload. At the money, the option is the most heavily traded contract on the board — order flow is two-sided, the maker can lay off inventory in seconds, and competition between makers compresses the quote toward a few cents. Out in the wings, the option trades rarely, the maker may sit on the inventory for days, and the only people buying far-OTM strikes are often the ones who turn out to be informed about a tail event — so the maker pads the quote for adverse selection. That is the deep reason the cover chart slopes the way it does: the spread-as-a-percent isn't a quirk of arithmetic, it's the price of the maker's risk, and their risk is concentrated exactly where you, the bargain-hunter, want to shop.

## The double cross: you pay the spread on the way in and the way out

Here is the single most under-appreciated fact about trading costs: the spread is not paid once. It is paid *twice* — once when you open the position and again when you close it. This is so important that it deserves its own picture.

![Frictionless versus real fill P&L for a long ATM call, with the break-even shifted higher by the round-trip spread](/imgs/blogs/liquidity-bid-ask-spreads-and-getting-filled-the-hidden-tax-2.png)

The blue curve is the textbook payoff of a long call at expiry — the one in every options book. It assumes you bought at the mid (\$2.45) and, implicitly, that you'll be valued at the mid. The red dashed curve is the *real* payoff: you bought at the ask (paying half the spread above mid going in) and, when you eventually close or get marked, you give up the other half of the spread coming out. The entire red curve is shifted *down* relative to the blue one by the full round-trip spread, and that shift drags your break-even to the right. The amber arrow marks the gap: the real break-even sits **\$0.10 per share higher** than the frictionless one. The stock doesn't just have to clear the strike plus premium; it has to clear the strike plus premium *plus the round-trip spread*. You have to be right by an extra dime per share before you make a cent.

Notice what this does *not* depend on. It does not depend on whether the trade wins or loses. The spread comes out whether you exit at a profit, exit at a loss, or get assigned — every leg that opened has to close somehow, and closing crosses the spread again. The only way to pay the spread *once* instead of twice is to let the option expire worthless or be exercised at intrinsic value at expiry, in which case there is no closing trade. That is a real and underrated reason to hold defined-risk options to expiry when you can: you skip the exit spread entirely. But any time you actively manage — roll, adjust, take profit early, cut a loser — you are paying the spread again.

#### Worked example: how much more right you have to be

Same ATM call: \$100 stock, \$2.45 mid premium, \$0.10 spread. Frictionless, your break-even at expiry is strike plus premium: \$100 + \$2.45 = **\$102.45**. The stock has to close above \$102.45 for the trade to make money in the textbook.

Now pay the spread for real. You buy at the ask, \$2.50. Your cost basis is \$2.50, so even ignoring the exit, break-even is \$100 + \$2.50 = \$102.50. And if you intend to *sell* the option before expiry rather than exercise, you'll sell into the bid, giving up another \$0.05 against mid — so to net zero on a sale you'd need the option's mid to reach \$2.55, which corresponds to the stock being meaningfully higher still. Rolled all the way through, the round-trip spread tax pushes your true break-even to about **\$102.55** — a full \$0.10 per share, **\$10 per contract**, beyond the textbook line. On a stock that moves \$1 a day, that's a tenth of a day's range you've handed over before the trade can work in your favor. Small per trade; relentless across hundreds of trades a year.

The intuition: the payoff diagram is drawn for a frictionless world; the real one lives a spread-width below it, on both the entry and the exit, and the distance between break-evens *is* the tax.

## More legs, more spread: how the cost compounds with structure

If a single option crosses the spread twice, what happens when you trade a structure made of several options at once? Every leg has its own bid-ask spread, and you cross *all of them* on entry and *all of them* again on exit. The crossings multiply.

![Round-trip spread cost versus premium at stake for single, vertical, and iron condor structures](/imgs/blogs/liquidity-bid-ask-spreads-and-getting-filled-the-hidden-tax-3.png)

The chart stacks three structures. A **single option** is one leg: 2 crossings, and at a representative \$0.10 per-leg spread that is \$0.10 × 2 × 100 = **\$20 per contract** of round-trip spread, against \$245 of premium at stake — about 8%. A **vertical spread** ([debit or credit, defining your risk](/blog/trading/options-volatility/vertical-spreads-debit-and-credit-defining-your-risk)) is two legs: 4 crossings, \$32 of spread against a \$174 debit — roughly 18%. A **four-leg iron condor** ([selling the range](/blog/trading/options-volatility/iron-condors-and-credit-spreads-selling-the-range)) is four legs: **8 crossings**, \$48 of round-trip spread against a \$102 credit — and now the spread cost is nearly **half of the entire credit you collected**. The red bars climb relative to the blue ones as you add legs, because the cost scales with the number of legs while the premium or credit you're working with does not grow nearly as fast.

This is the structural punchline of the whole post for anyone who likes multi-leg trades. The seductive thing about an iron condor is the high win rate and the defined risk. The ugly thing is that you are managing *four* spreads, and if you trade them frequently and manage them actively — rolling untested sides, closing early at 50% of max profit, adjusting tested sides — you are crossing eight spreads, then eight more, then eight more. The reason experienced condor traders obsess over only trading the most liquid index and ETF options (think the big, penny-wide names) is precisely this: on a wide-quoted underlying, the eight-crossing tax is not a rounding error, it is *the* dominant cost, and it can be larger than the edge the structure is supposed to harvest.

#### Worked example: the iron condor's spread tax against its credit

Build the condor from the model on our \$100 stock, 30 days, 20% vol: sell the 95 put and 105 call, buy the 90 put and 110 call as wings. The net credit, priced off Black-Scholes, is **\$1.02 per share**, or **\$102 per contract**. That is the most you can make and it is your buffer against being wrong.

Now the spreads. Representative per-leg full spreads: \$0.05 on the 90 put, \$0.07 on the 95 put, \$0.07 on the 105 call, \$0.05 on the 110 call — total \$0.24 per share across the four legs. You cross all four going in and all four coming out: \$0.24 × 2 = \$0.48 per share, **\$48 per contract**. So the spread tax is \$48 against a \$102 credit — **47% of the credit gone to the spread on a single round trip.** You collected \$102, you will hand back \$48 in spread just to open and close, and that's before commissions. The trade has to work *cleanly*, and you have to *not over-manage it*, or the structure that "wins 80% of the time" loses money on the cost line. The intuition: every leg you add buys you a payoff feature and sells you two more spread crossings — count both.

A subtle and important corollary: the compounding gets worse the *less liquid* each leg is. Our condor used representative \$0.05-\$0.07 spreads, which are realistic only on a genuinely liquid underlying. Move the same four-leg structure onto a thin single-name where each leg is \$0.20 wide, and the round-trip spread becomes \$0.20 × 4 × 2 × 100 = \$160 per contract against roughly the same credit — the trade is dead on arrival before the stock has done anything at all. This is why "which underlying" is a bigger execution decision than "which structure," and it is the bridge to the next question: how do you actually *measure* whether a leg is liquid enough before you commit to it?

## The four numbers that tell you if a strike is tradeable

"Is this option liquid?" is not a yes/no question and it is not answered by a single number. Four separate readings on the chain each tell you a different piece of the story, and a strike can look fine on one and be a trap on another. You read all four before you click.

![Four liquidity readings on the options chain: open interest, volume, spread width, depth](/imgs/blogs/liquidity-bid-ask-spreads-and-getting-filled-the-hidden-tax-4.png)

**Open interest (OI)** is how many contracts of that exact strike and expiry *exist* — the count of open positions that have not yet been closed or expired. High OI means an established, crowded strike: lots of other traders hold it, which usually (not always) means there is a real two-sided market. Low OI means thin — you might be one of the only participants, and getting filled at a fair price will be a fight. Read OI as: *can a position even be built here?* Note the failure mode: OI is a *stock* measure that accumulates and lingers. A strike can carry huge OI from a fund's hedge that was put on weeks ago and sits untouched, which tells you a position *exists* but says nothing about whether anyone is willing to trade it *today*. That is why OI alone is never sufficient.

**Volume** is how many contracts *traded today*. This is the freshness signal, the *flow* that complements OI's stock. A strike can carry big open interest from a position someone put on weeks ago and have zero volume today, meaning the quote you see is stale and possibly untradeable at that level. High volume means active, fresh, two-sided flow — people are transacting *right now*, so the quote is real and the makers are competing to be on it. Zero volume on a strike with big OI is the classic stale-quote trap: the price on the screen is a courtesy, not a commitment. Read volume as: *is anyone actually trading this at the moment?*

**Spread width** is the gap between bid and ask, and you must read it *both* ways: in absolute dollars (a \$0.05 spread) **and** as a percent of the option's price (is that \$0.05 sitting on a \$2.45 option, where it's 2%, or on a \$0.20 option, where it's 25%?). Spreads are tight near the money where flow is heavy and blow out as you move into the wings — exactly the shape of the cover chart. The percent reading is the one that matters for cost, because, as we'll keep seeing, it is the spread *as a fraction of price* that you pay, not the raw dollar gap. Read spread width as: *what does a round trip cost me?*

**Depth** is the size resting on the quote — how many contracts you can actually buy at the ask or sell at the bid before the price moves. A "\$2.40 bid, \$2.50 offered" looks identical whether there are 100 contracts on each side or 5. But if you want to trade 20 and there are only 5 on the offer, your order eats through the visible quote and *walks the price* — you fill 5 at \$2.50, then the next tranche at \$2.60, and so on. Depth is the reading that turns a "tight" spread into a mirage: the quote is only tight *for the size shown*, and your size may be larger than the quote can absorb. Read depth as: *will the quote survive my order?*

The trap is mistaking one green light for all four. A strike can show a tight \$0.05 spread that is real for *one* contract and a mirage for twenty. It can show fat open interest that is all stale, with zero volume today. The liquid strike has all four: high OI, real volume today, a tight spread in both absolute and percent terms, and depth that can absorb your size. Anything less, you size down or stay away — and you treat far-OTM wings, dead names, and any quote that vanishes when the market is stressed as the trap they are. We have a full post on [how the options chain is laid out](/blog/trading/options-volatility/the-options-chain-and-contract-mechanics-multiplier-expiry-settlement); the point here is that the chain is a liquidity dashboard, and you read all four gauges before you commit.

#### Worked example: same dollar spread, opposite verdict

Two calls, same underlying, same \$0.05 quoted spread. Call A is the ATM 30-day strike, mid \$2.45, OI 18,000, volume today 4,200, and the quote is 50-up (50 contracts each side). Call B is a far-OTM strike eight points out, mid \$0.28, OI 600, volume today 11, quote 3-up.

For Call A, the \$0.05 spread is \$0.05 / \$2.45 = **2.0%** of price, the volume says people are trading it now, and 50-up depth swallows any retail order. Tradeable. For Call B, the *same* \$0.05 spread is \$0.05 / \$0.28 = **17.9%** of price — and that is if you even see a \$0.05 spread, which on a strike this thin you usually won't; it'll be \$0.25 bid, \$0.40 offered, a 54% spread. Volume of 11 means the quote is barely alive, and 3-up depth means a 10-lot walks the book. Untradeable for anything but a tiny, hold-to-expiry punt. Note that the \$0.28 mid on Call B matches the far-right end of the cover chart, where the spread-as-a-percent has already run past 50% — the chain reading and the chart are telling the same story from two angles.

The lesson: a dollar spread is meaningless without the price it sits on, the flow behind it, and the depth under it. The chain gives you four numbers because you need all four.

## The full hidden tax: spread plus commission plus the maker's edge plus slippage

The bid-ask spread is the biggest piece of the hidden tax, but it is not the whole bill. Four costs ride on every options trade, and they stack:

1. **The bid-ask spread** — the half-spread you give up on each fill, paid going in and coming out, compounded across legs. Everything above.
2. **Commissions and fees** — the explicit per-contract charge from your broker (often \$0.50 to \$0.65 per contract, sometimes zero on a fee-rebate model), plus tiny regulatory and exchange fees. Visible on your statement, usually the *smallest* of the four for an active trader, which is exactly why people fixate on it and ignore the spread.
3. **The market-maker's edge** — even when you fill "at mid," the mid itself is not the option's fair value; the maker has shaded the quote so that the midpoint sits slightly in their favor given the order flow they expect. You are, on average, trading against a price built to win over many trades. This is subtle and you can't see it directly, but it's why even mid fills are not free.
4. **Slippage** — the gap between the price you *expected* and the price you *got*, from the quote moving while your order travels, from your size walking the book through thin depth, and from latency. On liquid options slippage is small; on thin ones or in fast markets it can dwarf the spread.

For a long-term position trader who puts on a handful of trades a month, this whole bundle is a manageable drag. For anyone trading *often* — scalping, day-trading 0DTE, running a high-turnover systematic strategy — the bundle is existential, because it is paid *per trade* and the number of trades is the thing they're cranking up. Here is the picture every aspiring high-frequency options trader needs tattooed somewhere visible.

![Gross versus net annual P&L as trading frequency rises, with net peaking at low frequency then going negative](/imgs/blogs/liquidity-bid-ask-spreads-and-getting-filled-the-hidden-tax-5.png)

The blue line is **gross** annual P&L — what the backtest shows — as you trade more often. It rises with frequency: more trades, more total edge harvested, the curve marches up and to the right and looks fantastic. The red line is **net** annual P&L after costs, and it tells the true story. Because the *gross edge per trade shrinks* as you trade more often (you exhaust the few great setups and start taking marginal ones), while the *cost per round trip stays roughly fixed* (spread + commission + slippage), the net curve *peaks at a surprisingly low frequency* — here around 20 trades per year — and then turns *down* and plunges negative. Past the crossover, every additional trade has a gross edge smaller than its cost, so each one *subtracts* from your P&L. The amber gap between the curves is the hidden tax, and it widens with every trade you add.

This is why so many strategies that backtest beautifully die in live trading: the backtest used mid-prices (or worse, last-traded prices) and ignored the spread, the commission, and the slippage. It modeled the blue line and traded the red one. The cruelest version is the strategy whose *gross* edge is real and even decent, but whose *per-trade* edge is thin enough that the fixed cost per round trip eats it — and the trader, seeing the gorgeous gross backtest, cranks the frequency up, racing further down the wrong side of the red curve.

#### Worked example: a "profitable" edge eaten by costs

A systematic options strategy shows a gross edge that declines with how often you trade — roughly \$60 per contract divided by the square root of the number of trades per year (a common shape: the more you trade, the thinner the average opportunity). Your all-in cost per round trip is **\$13.30**: \$10 of round-trip spread, \$1.30 of commissions, and \$2 of slippage.

At **20 trades a year**, gross edge per trade is \$60 / √20 = \$13.42. Net per trade is \$13.42 − \$13.30 = \$0.12 — barely positive. Annualized net: \$0.12 × 20 = about \$2.40 per contract slot. Thin, but green.

Crank to **100 trades a year** to "scale it up." Now gross edge per trade is \$60 / √100 = \$6.00. But the cost is still \$13.30. Net per trade is \$6.00 − \$13.30 = **−\$7.30** — you *lose* money on every trade. Annualized net: −\$7.30 × 100 = **−\$730 per contract slot.** Meanwhile the *gross* backtest at 100 trades shows \$6.00 × 100 = \$600 of profit, up from \$268 at 20 trades — so the backtest says scaling up *tripled* your profit while live trading turned it into a \$730 loss. The break-even frequency, where gross edge per trade exactly equals the \$13.30 cost, is about **20 trades a year**; past it you're paying to play. The intuition: costs are a fixed tax per trade, so the more marginal each additional trade is, the faster the tax overtakes the edge — and "trade more to make more" is exactly backwards once you're near the line. This is the practical heart of [backtesting done right](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/backtesting-done-right-quant-research): model the spread, the commission, and the slippage, or your equity curve is fiction.

If costs are a fixed tax per trade, then two of the four levers are inside your control on every single fill — the *spread* you give up and the *slippage* you suffer — and both of those are set by *how you send the order*. The maker's edge and the commission you mostly negotiate by choosing what and where to trade; the spread and slippage you negotiate at the moment of execution. That is where order types come in.

## The mid-price reality: you do not fill at the mid

Beginners anchor on the mid-price because it's the number platforms display largest and it feels "fair." It is fair in the sense that it's the midpoint of two real prices. It is unfair in the sense that *nobody is obligated to trade with you there*. The mid is a reference, not a right. So how do you actually get filled, and what are you choosing between when you decide how to send an order?

You have three basic instruments, and they trade off **fill quality** against **certainty of execution**. There is no free lunch among them — every order type pays for better fills with worse certainty, or buys certainty with worse fills — and knowing which to reach for is most of execution skill.

![Three option order types comparing fill price, certainty, and when to use each](/imgs/blogs/liquidity-bid-ask-spreads-and-getting-filled-the-hidden-tax-6.png)

A **mid-price limit order** says "I'll trade, but only at (or near) the mid." Best possible fill price — you give up little or none of the spread. But low certainty: a market maker only fills you at mid if it suits their inventory, and on a directional move the price can run away while your order sits unfilled. You may never trade, and "the move left without me" is a real cost even though it never shows up on a statement. Use it on liquid names when you have no hurry and can wait, or *walk* the order — start at mid and improve your price one tick at a time toward the other side until you get hit. Walking the order is the single most useful retail execution habit: you discover the true clearing price one tick at a time instead of paying the full spread up front.

A **marketable limit order** is the workhorse and should be your default on options. You place a limit at or just past the offer (when buying) or the bid (when selling). It fills fast — essentially immediately, like a market order — but with a hard cap: it will *never* fill worse than your limit. You typically give up about half the spread, the same as crossing once, but you are protected from the catastrophe of an air-pocket quote. The fill price has a *known worst case*, and a known worst case is the whole game when the alternative is an open-ended quote.

A **market order** says "fill me now at whatever is quoted." Certainty is total — you *will* trade. And that total certainty is exactly the danger. On a wide option quote, "whatever is quoted" can be far past the last mid you saw, especially if depth is thin and your order walks the book. A market order on an illiquid option is how the trader in our opening lost forty cents buying back a condor: the platform took the worst available price on each leg, and on wide quotes the worst available price is genuinely awful. Use market orders almost never on options — only on the deepest, penny-wide names where the quote is so tight the distinction barely matters.

#### Worked example: market order versus marketable limit on a wide quote

A far-OTM call you want to exit is quoted \$0.40 bid, \$0.70 offered — a brutal but not unusual wing spread. Mid is \$0.55. You are *selling*, so you'll hit the bid side.

Send a **market order to sell**: you take the bid, \$0.40 — but if your size exceeds the 3 contracts resting at \$0.40, the next ones fill at \$0.35, then \$0.30, as you walk down the thin book. Say you average \$0.36 on a 10-lot. Against the \$0.55 mid you *thought* the position was worth, you gave up \$0.19 per share, **\$190 on 10 contracts** — 35% of the position's mid value, vaporized on the exit alone.

Now send a **marketable limit to sell at \$0.40** instead. You fill what you can at \$0.40 and the rest *does not trade* below your limit — you stay in for the remainder rather than getting walked down. Better still, work a limit at \$0.50 (between mid and bid) first: on a wing this wide, a market maker will often meet you partway because they price the option below mid anyway, and you might fill the whole lot at \$0.50 — saving \$0.14 per share, **\$140**, versus the market-order disaster. The intuition: on a wide quote the *order type* is worth more dollars than your directional view; never hand the market a blank check.

**A note on legging risk.** On multi-leg structures you have a second choice: send the whole structure as *one* net-debit/credit limit order (the platform fills all legs together at your net price, or none), or "leg in" by trading each option separately to chase a better price on each. Legging in *can* save you a few cents per leg when quotes are favorable, but it exposes you to **legging risk**: you fill one leg, the underlying moves before you fill the next, and now you're holding a naked, unintended position at a worse price — sometimes much worse. For anyone not running a fast execution desk, the right default is a single net-price limit on the whole structure, worked from the natural mid toward the market. You give up the chance to scalp a few cents per leg in exchange for never waking up accidentally naked. Professionals who do leg in have automated, sub-second execution and treat legging risk as a managed cost; retail traders who leg in by hand are usually just paying *more* spread while feeling clever. The same patience-versus-immediacy trade is what professional [execution algorithms like VWAP and TWAP](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/execution-algorithms-vwap-twap-pov-quant-research) automate at scale: slice the order, work it into the natural liquidity, and refuse to pay the full spread for the privilege of trading right this second.

## Liquidity traps: the strikes you can get into but not out of

The most dangerous form of illiquidity is not a wide spread you can see. It's the *disappearance* of liquidity at the exact moment you need it — and there are two classic versions.

**The far-OTM trap.** Cheap, far-out-of-the-money options are catnip: a few cents of premium for a lottery-ticket payoff. The problem isn't the entry; you can usually buy a few. The problem is the *exit*. Those strikes carry thin open interest and near-zero volume, so the bid is a token \$0.05 or there is *no bid at all*. You bought a call for \$0.15 hoping to sell it for \$0.60 on a move; the move happens, the option is "worth" \$0.60 at the mid — and the actual bid is \$0.30, or nobody is bidding and your "60-cent" option can only be sold for a quarter. You are right and you still can't realize it. Far-OTM strikes are easy to enter and brutal to exit, which is why the cover chart shades the wings red: the spread there isn't a 4% tax, it's a 50%-plus tax, and in the deepest wings it's "no exit at any reasonable price."

**The liquidity mirage in a crash.** This is the one that wrecks people, because the liquidity you measured in calm markets is *not the liquidity you get in a panic*. When volatility spikes, market makers widen their spreads dramatically — a name that was \$0.05 wide goes \$0.50 wide — and pull their depth, because their inventory risk and adverse-selection risk just exploded. The four green lights you checked on a quiet Tuesday all go red simultaneously the moment you most want to hedge or exit. Spreads blow out, depth vanishes, and the "liquid" put you were counting on to protect you trades at a price that makes the protection nearly worthless to monetize. The liquidity you see in calm is the *maximum* you'll ever get; assume it shrinks toward zero exactly when you need it most. This is also why a market order in a fast market is suicidal: you're taking "whatever is quoted" precisely when the quote is at its worst.

The defensive principle from both traps is the same and it is non-negotiable: **never enter a position you cannot exit.** Before you put on any trade, ask not "can I get filled now?" but "if I'm right and need to take profit, or wrong and need to cut, will there be a real bid/offer on the other side?" If the answer depends on calm markets persisting, you don't have a position, you have a hope.

## Common misconceptions

**"I fill at the mid."** No. The mid is the midpoint of two prices, neither of which has your name on it. You fill near the mid only with a patient limit order on a liquid name, and even then a maker fills you at mid only when it suits them. On our \$2.45 / \$0.10-spread call, the *realistic* assumption is that you pay the ask (\$2.50) buying and hit the bid (\$2.40) selling — that's the \$10-per-contract round-trip tax, not zero. Plan every trade assuming you give up about half the spread per fill; treat any better fill as a bonus, not a baseline.

**"Commissions are my main trading cost, so a zero-commission broker means cheap trading."** Almost never. For an active options trader the bid-ask spread dwarfs commissions. On the iron condor above, commissions might be \$2.60 round trip (eight legs at ~\$0.32) while the *spread* was **\$48** — the spread is roughly 18× the commission. A "free" broker that routes your order to whoever pays them the most for it may be saving you a dollar of commission while costing you ten dollars of worse fill. Optimize the spread and the routing, not the headline commission.

**"A cheap option is a cheap trade."** The opposite. A \$0.20 far-OTM option with a \$0.10 spread costs you **50% of the price** just to round-trip the spread — versus 4% on the \$2.45 ATM call with the *same* \$0.10 dollar spread. Cheap options have the *widest* spreads as a percent of price, the thinnest depth, and the worst exits. "Cheap" measures the premium, not the cost; the cost is the spread-as-a-percent, and on cheap options it's enormous.

**"More legs make the trade safer, so they're better."** More legs define your risk, yes — but they also multiply your spread crossings. A four-leg iron condor crosses **8** spreads round-trip versus 2 for a single option. On our condor that's \$48 of spread eating 47% of a \$102 credit. Defined risk is a real benefit; just price the four-fold spread cost into the trade and don't over-manage, or the "safe" structure bleeds out through the cost line.

**"My backtest is profitable, so the strategy works."** Only if the backtest charged you the real spread, commissions, and slippage on every fill. Most don't — they use mid or last prices and assume frictionless entry and exit. Our example strategy showed \$600 of gross profit at 100 trades a year and *lost* \$730 net after a \$13.30-per-trade cost. The gross curve is a fantasy; the net curve, after the full hidden tax, is the only one you can spend. A backtest that ignores costs isn't optimistic, it's wrong.

## How it shows up in real markets

These patterns are the texture of every real trading day, not theory. A few concrete ones.

**SPX and SPY versus a thin single-name.** Index and big-ETF options (SPX, SPY, QQQ) are the most liquid options on earth: penny-wide or near it on the front-month at-the-money strikes, enormous depth, volume in the millions of contracts. You can trade size and barely move the quote. Step to a thinly-traded single-name a few strikes out and the same notional gets you a \$0.30-wide quote, three contracts of depth, and a fill that walks the book. The *first* execution decision in options is the underlying you choose; the most liquid name with a tradeable structure beats a "better" setup on a name you can't get out of. Concretely: an at-the-money SPY put might quote \$3.00 / \$3.02 — a two-cent spread, 0.7% of price — while the analogous strike on a small-cap with the same percentage move priced in quotes \$2.80 / \$3.20, a forty-cent spread, **13% of price**, and 5-up depth. Same view, same notional, the second one taxes you nearly twenty times harder on every fill. The chain you choose sets your cost floor before you have done anything else.

**Earnings and the vanishing exit.** Far-OTM options on a stock into earnings look tantalizing — lots of implied move priced in, cheap-looking tickets. Traders pile into wing calls and puts. Then earnings print, the stock gaps, implied vol collapses, and the trader who's "right" goes to sell — into a bid that's a fraction of the mid, on a strike with almost no depth, because the makers widened up around the event. The position was right and unmonetizable. (The mechanics of pricing and trading these events live in [the expected move](/blog/trading/options-volatility/the-options-chain-and-contract-mechanics-multiplier-expiry-settlement) and adjacent event posts; the *liquidity* lesson is that the exit is the trade.)

**The 2018 and 2020 vol spikes.** In both Volmageddon (February 2018) and the COVID crash (March 2020, VIX closing 82.69), option spreads on even normally-liquid names blew out by multiples and depth evaporated. Traders holding short-vol structures who needed to cover found the cost of covering had exploded along with the volatility — the liquidity mirage in its purest form. The lesson reprices every "tail" position: the protection or the exit you're counting on costs the most exactly when you need it.

**The high-frequency edge that wasn't.** The single most common story in systematic options trading is a strategy that backtested with a Sharpe of 2 and went flat-to-negative live. Nine times in ten the culprit is costs: the backtest filled at mid, the live account paid the spread twice on every leg, and the per-trade edge — real on paper — was thinner than the per-trade cost. The fix is never "trade more"; it's model the spread honestly, trade the liquid stuff, trade *less* often at higher per-trade edge, and respect the net curve. This is the same discipline behind [execution algorithms like VWAP and TWAP](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/execution-algorithms-vwap-twap-pov-quant-research): the whole point of a good execution algo is to *not* pay the full spread by spreading an order out and trading patiently against liquidity, rather than demanding immediacy and getting taxed for it. And it's the mirror image of [how market makers and HFT firms make their living](/blog/trading/finance/market-makers-and-high-frequency-trading) — your spread cost is their revenue.

**Delta-hedging drag.** If you run a delta-hedged options book, every hedge adjustment in the underlying crosses *that* market's spread too, and frequent re-hedging racks up real cost — the subject of [delta hedging in practice](/blog/trading/options-volatility/delta-hedging-in-practice-the-cost-and-slippage-of-staying-neutral). The same principle: immediacy is taxed, and the more often you demand it, the more you pay.

#### Worked example: the full hidden-tax bill on one round trip

Tie the four costs together on a single trade so the bundle stops being abstract. You buy ten of the ATM call from the foundations example and close the position later the same week. The mid was \$2.45, the spread \$0.10, and the contract multiplier is 100.

The *spread* tax: \$0.05 per share giving up half the spread on entry plus \$0.05 on exit equals \$0.10 per share, \$10 per contract, **\$100** across ten contracts. The *commission* tax: at \$0.60 per contract per side, that is \$0.60 × 10 × 2 = **\$12** round trip. The *maker's edge* you cannot see directly, but call it a conservative half-cent per share baked into the mid you trade around — \$0.005 × 100 × 10 × 2 ≈ **\$10**. *Slippage* on a liquid ATM strike with this size is small; budget another \$0.02 per share one way on the exit if the market ticks while you work the order — \$0.02 × 100 × 10 ≈ **\$20**. Add them: \$100 + \$12 + \$10 + \$20 = **\$142** of all-in cost to round-trip a position whose total premium was \$2,450. That is **5.8%** of the capital deployed, gone before the stock moves a penny — and notice the spread (\$100) is by far the largest piece, almost eight times the commission everyone obsesses over. The intuition: the hidden tax is real money with four line items, the spread dominates it, and you must subtract the whole \$142 from any edge you think you have.

## The playbook

The hidden tax is not avoidable, but it is *controllable*. You pay it on purpose, sized into the edge, instead of by accident in the fills. Five habits do almost all the work.

![Five-habit liquidity playbook for controlling the bid-ask tax](/imgs/blogs/liquidity-bid-ask-spreads-and-getting-filled-the-hidden-tax-7.png)

**1. Trade liquid strikes.** Before you click, read all four numbers: open interest, today's volume, spread width (dollars *and* percent of price), and depth on the quote. Stay near the money and in the front months where quotes are tight; treat the wings and dead names as off-limits unless you have a specific, exit-aware reason. The single highest-leverage decision is the *underlying* — prefer the most liquid name that still expresses your view. A worse setup you can trade beats a better setup you can't exit.

**2. Use limit orders, near the mid.** Default to a marketable limit, never a naked market order into a wide quote. Start your limit at the mid and *walk* it one tick at a time toward the market until you fill; on liquid names you'll often get filled well inside the spread. On multi-leg structures, send the whole thing as one net-debit/credit limit and work it from the natural mid — don't leg in by hand and don't let the platform fire market orders on each leg.

**3. Count the spread on every leg, both ways.** Before you put on a structure, total the round-trip spread: sum the per-leg spreads, multiply by 2 (entry and exit) and by 100 (the multiplier). One leg = 2 crossings, a vertical = 4, a four-leg condor = 8. Compare that number to the premium or credit at stake. If the spread is a large fraction of the credit — like the 47% on our condor — either the underlying is too illiquid for this structure or the structure is too leg-heavy for this underlying. Fix it before you trade, not after.

**4. Size the cost into the edge.** Subtract spread + commission + slippage from your *gross* edge before you trust any backtest or expectancy estimate. A thin per-trade edge that looks great gross will die at high frequency once the fixed per-trade cost overtakes it. The cure is almost always to *trade less often at a higher per-trade edge*, not more. Respect the net curve, not the gross one — they diverge exactly where it hurts. Position sizing has to start from net expectancy, which is why this connects directly to [position sizing and risk of ruin](/blog/trading/options-volatility/position-sizing-and-risk-of-ruin-in-options-trading): you can't size a strategy whose true edge you've overstated by ignoring costs.

**5. Never enter what you cannot exit.** Before any trade, plan the exit: if you're right and want to take profit, or wrong and need to cut, will there be a real bid/offer on the other side? Avoid far-OTM lottery strikes and thin names you'd be forced to hold to expiry. And assume the comfortable liquidity you see in calm markets vanishes in a crash — size and structure so that a wide-spread, no-depth exit doesn't ruin you.

A practical routine ties these together at the moment of the trade. Before you click: glance at the chain and confirm all four liquidity gauges are green for the size you intend, not for one contract. Set your order as a limit at the natural mid and decide in advance how far you will walk it — one tick at a time, with a hard stop a tick or two past mid, never an open-ended chase. For a structure, send it as a single net-price ticket and let the platform fill all legs together at your net or not at all. Write down, even mentally, the round-trip cost in dollars (sum the per-leg spreads, times two, times one hundred) and confirm it is a small fraction of the edge you expect — if it is half the credit, the trade is wrong before it starts. And name the exit: the price you will take profit at and the price you will cut at, both checked against whether a real bid or offer will be there. Done consistently, this turns the hidden tax from a recurring surprise into a known, budgeted line item that you have already subtracted from your edge.

The deepest version of the lesson is that liquidity is a *risk*, not a convenience. A position is only as good as your ability to get out of it, and that ability is not a fixed property of the contract — it is a function of the regime, the strike, the size, and the order type, every one of which can move against you at the worst moment. The traders who survive are not the ones with the best forecasts; they are the ones who never confuse a quote with a fill, never confuse a mid with a price, and never enter a strike whose exit they have not already paid for in their heads.

The one sentence to carry out the door: **liquidity is not free and it is not constant.** Price the spread as a real, recurring cost — the tax on every fill, paid going in and coming out, compounded across legs — and most of the ways options traders quietly lose money simply stop happening to you.
