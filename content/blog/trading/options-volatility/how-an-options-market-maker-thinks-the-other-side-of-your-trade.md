> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A market maker (MM) is not betting against your direction. They quote a two-sided market, earn the bid-ask spread plus a small edge, instantly delta-hedge the position your trade hands them, and warehouse the leftover gamma and vega for the variance risk premium. You are not trading against a gambler with a view — you are renting liquidity from a hedged book, at its price.
>
> - **The MM earns the spread regardless of which way you're right.** Sell you a \$2.45 call at the \$2.50 ask, buy 53 shares to hedge, and the next day's P&L is roughly flat-plus-edge across ordinary moves — about +\$5 to +\$9 per contract whether the stock rose or fell. Direction is hedged out; the spread and edge are not.
> - **Your fill price already contains their model and their inventory.** The quote is fair value off a fitted vol surface, skewed by what the desk is already too long or too short. When you "got filled instantly," you paid the price the MM was *delighted* to give.
> - **Retail pays the spread and the vol — twice.** You cross the bid-ask spread (about +\$5/contract on a liquid name), then you pay the variance risk premium baked into implied vol (about +3.7 vol points, near +\$42/contract over a month on the same option), plus the skew you cross and any information your order leaks.
> - **The one rule to remember:** you will never out-trade the MM on the spread — you only win on the *view*. Trade liquid strikes, use limit orders, don't lift offers in size, and be right on direction or vol by *more* than the edge you paid.

## The fill that arrived too fast

A retail trader I'll call Dan was sure a chip stock would pop on earnings. He pulled up the options chain, found a weekly call about 5% out of the money, and clicked Buy. The fill came back in well under a second: 20 contracts, done, at the asking price. Dan felt a small thrill — the market was *liquid*, his order was *important enough* to fill instantly, the trade was *on*. He had crossed the threshold from spectator to participant in the blink of an eye.

What Dan didn't feel was the other side of that millisecond. Somewhere, a market maker's system had been continuously posting a bid and an ask on that exact contract, updating both dozens of times a second as the underlying ticked. When Dan's order hit the ask, the MM's machine sold him 20 contracts, and in the same breath bought a slug of the underlying stock to neutralize the delta, logged the new gamma and vega onto a book that already held thousands of other positions, and went right back to quoting. The MM did not pause to wonder whether Dan was right about earnings. It did not take a view. It took the *spread*, hedged the *direction*, and moved on. The instant fill that thrilled Dan was, from the other side, a routine harvest — the MM was delighted to sell at that price, not because it disagreed with Dan, but because the price already contained everything the MM needed to make money no matter what the stock did.

That asymmetry — Dan playing a directional hand, the MM running a hedged machine indifferent to his hand — is the subject of this post. If you trade options and you don't understand who is on the other side and how they think, you are negotiating against a counterparty whose business you've never studied. This is the study. We will build the market maker from zero: what their job actually is, why they quote two sides, how they hedge what you hand them, what their edge really comes from, when they widen and why, and — most usefully — what all of it means for the prices *you* see and the way *you* should trade. We will lean on the rest of this series for the machinery (delta hedging, the net Greeks, the variance risk premium, the vol surface) and assume you've met the [Black-Scholes price as the cost of hedging an option](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/black-scholes). Here we put on the MM's headset.

![Pipeline of the market maker loop: quote two sides, customer trades, delta-hedge, warehouse and lay off Greeks, bank the spread and edge, then requote](/imgs/blogs/how-an-options-market-maker-thinks-the-other-side-of-your-trade-1.png)

The loop above is the whole job on one screen. The MM posts a two-sided quote (blue), a customer lifts the ask or hits the bid (lavender), the MM instantly delta-hedges the fill in the underlying (amber), warehouses the leftover gamma and vega, lays off what it can in other options, banks the spread plus a sliver of edge plus — over the book's life — the variance risk premium (green), and then requotes. Around and around, thousands of times a day, with no view on where any single name is going. Read the loop once now; the rest of the post is just each box in detail.

## Foundations: what a market maker's job actually is

Strip away the technology and a market maker does one thing: it stands ready to **buy and sell the same instrument at the same time**, posting a price it will buy at (the **bid**) and a slightly higher price it will sell at (the **ask** or **offer**). The difference between them is the **bid-ask spread**. That is the entire pitch. The MM is a shopkeeper for risk: it keeps the shelves stocked so that anyone who wants to trade can trade *now*, and in exchange it charges the spread. It is paid for **immediacy** — for being the always-available counterparty so you don't have to wait for a natural buyer to appear at the exact moment you want to sell.

This is worth sitting with, because it is the opposite of how most people frame an options "dealer." A casino takes the other side of your bet and roots for you to lose. A market maker takes the other side of your *trade* and roots for nothing — it hedges away the part it can't predict (where the stock goes) and keeps the part it can charge for (the spread, plus a structural premium we'll come to). The MM's profit does not depend on you being wrong. It depends on **flow** — on a steady stream of customers crossing the spread in both directions — and on the MM being able to hedge what that flow hands it for less than the edge it collected.

Let's define the few terms the rest of the post leans on.

- **Two-sided quote.** A simultaneous bid and ask. "2.40 at 2.50, 50 up" means: I'll buy 50 contracts at \$2.40 and sell 50 at \$2.50. A *one-sided* quote (only a bid, or only an offer) is a different animal — it's a request to do one specific trade, not a market.
- **Fair value (theoretical / "theo").** The MM's model price for the option, computed from the underlying, the strike, time to expiry, rates, and — crucially — the **implied volatility** the MM reads off its fitted [vol surface](/blog/trading/options-volatility/reading-the-vol-surface-like-a-trader-the-3d-map-of-fear). The bid sits below theo and the ask above it.
- **Edge.** The amount, per contract, by which the MM expects to come out ahead on a trade — selling a hair above fair value, buying a hair below, plus the structural premium for warehousing risk. Edge is the MM's expected profit; the spread is the visible part of it.
- **Delta hedge.** Immediately after a fill, the MM trades the underlying to cancel the option's first-order directional exposure (its **delta**). After hedging, a small move in the stock barely changes the MM's combined value. The mechanics and frictions live in [delta hedging in practice](/blog/trading/options-volatility/delta-hedging-in-practice-the-cost-and-slippage-of-staying-neutral); here we just use the result: *the MM does not keep your directional bet.*
- **The book.** The MM's running inventory of every option position it holds, netted into a single set of [Greeks](/blog/trading/options-volatility/the-net-greeks-of-a-position-building-your-risk-dashboard): net delta, net gamma, net vega, net theta. The book is the MM's actual risk, and it shapes every quote.

So the job, precisely stated: **quote a two-sided market, get hit by flow, hedge the delta you inherit, warehouse and manage the higher-order Greeks, and earn the spread plus the structural edge — without ever taking a directional view.** Everything below is mechanism on top of that sentence.

### Why quote two sides at all?

Because a one-sided position is a bet, and a market maker is in the business of *not* betting. If the MM only sold options, it would accumulate a giant short-options book and live or die on whether vol went up or down — exactly the directional risk it's trying to avoid. By quoting both sides, the MM lets the *flow* be roughly balanced: some customers buy, some sell, and the net position the MM has to manage is smaller than the gross volume it trades. The spread is collected on *every* contract that crosses, in both directions, while the risk is only the *net* it can't immediately offset. High volume, low net risk, spread on all of it — that is the shape of a healthy market-making business.

When flow is *not* balanced — when customers overwhelmingly buy puts for protection, say — the MM ends up with a one-sided net book it must warehouse or lay off, and now it *is* carrying risk. That's where the variance risk premium and the skew come in, and it's the heart of why your option prices look the way they do. Hold that thought; we'll build to it.

### Why the MM hedges *now*, not later

A retail trader who sells a call often sits on the naked short for hours or days, exposed to every tick — that *is* a directional bet. The MM does the opposite: it hedges the delta in the same breath as the fill, often automatically, within milliseconds. The reason is the whole business model. The MM's edge is the spread plus the structural premium, not a view on the next print, so any unhedged directional exposure it carries is *uncompensated risk* — variance it isn't paid for, that only adds noise to a P&L built on collecting small edges at high volume. Holding the delta would be like a casino betting its own float on the roulette wheel between customers: pure added variance, no added edge. So the desk strips the direction off immediately and keeps only the risks it's actually paid to warehouse — the gamma and vega and the premium that comes with them. The speed isn't showmanship; it's the discipline of a business that makes its money on the *law of large numbers*, where a single unhedged bet can swamp a thousand carefully-collected spreads.

## The spread and the edge: the visible and invisible profit

Start with the simplest version of the trade and make it concrete. The MM has a model. It feeds in the spot, the strike, the time, the rate, and the implied vol from its surface, and out comes a fair value. Then it posts a bid *below* fair and an ask *above* fair. The whole spread straddles theo.

![Two-sided quote around theoretical fair value: the bid below and ask above the model price, the shaded spread the market maker earns](/imgs/blogs/how-an-options-market-maker-thinks-the-other-side-of-your-trade-2.png)

The chart shows it for our running option — a \$100 stock, a \$100-strike call, 20% implied vol, a 4% rate, 30 days to expiry. The blue line is the MM's fair value across nearby spots; the green line below is the bid, the red line above is the ask, and the amber band between them is the spread. A customer who *buys* trades on the red line; a customer who *sells* trades on the green line. The MM, in aggregate, captures the amber band. Note that the band rides up and down *with* fair value as the spot moves — the MM isn't quoting a fixed price, it's quoting a fixed *margin around its model*, and the model updates continuously.

#### Worked example: the spread and the edge on one quoted call

Take the running option and price it from this series' Black-Scholes model:

- **Fair value:** `bs_price(100, 100, 30/365, 0.04, 0.20, kind="call")` = **\$2.4513** per share, or **\$245.13** per 100-share contract.
- **Delta:** **0.5343** (the MM will hold about 53 shares per contract to hedge).
- The MM posts a **5-cent half-spread** — a typical width on a liquid name: **bid \$2.40, ask \$2.50** (rounding the \$2.4013 / \$2.5013 to the penny tick).

Now the money. When a customer *buys* one contract by lifting the ask at \$2.50, they pay **\$250.13**, which is **\$5.00 above fair value** — that \$5.00 is the MM's edge on this trade, locked in the instant of the fill. When a *different* customer later *sells* one contract by hitting the bid at \$2.40, they receive **\$240.13**, which is **\$5.00 below fair value** — another \$5.00 of edge to the MM. If the MM both bought (at the bid) and sold (at the ask) one contract, it captured the full round-trip spread of **\$10.00 per contract** while ending flat. The intuition: the spread is the MM's fee for immediacy, charged on *every* crossing in *both* directions, and on a liquid option it's a few dollars per contract that the customer pays simply for the privilege of trading right now.

Two subtleties hide in that example, and they matter.

First, the **half-spread is the edge per one-sided trade**, but the MM rarely gets a clean round trip on the *same* contract. Real flow is lumpy: the MM might sell ten contracts at the ask and only later buy back six, leaving it short four it has to manage. So the "spread" it actually keeps is a blend — full spread on the matched part, and on the unmatched part it keeps only the edge versus fair value while taking on the risk of holding the position. This is why a market maker's quoted spread is *not* its profit margin; the profit is the spread minus the cost of hedging and warehousing the net inventory the flow leaves behind.

Second, the MM **skews** the quote. The fair value in our example sat in the middle of the bid-ask, but a real desk routinely posts an *asymmetric* market — say bid 2.41 / ask 2.50 — when it wants to *attract* one side and *discourage* the other. If the desk is already too short this option, it raises both the bid and the ask so it's eager to buy (to flatten) and reluctant to sell (to avoid getting shorter). The customer who trades against a skewed quote is, without knowing it, helping the MM manage inventory. **Your fill price already contains the MM's model and the MM's current position.** You are not seeing "the price"; you are seeing *this desk's price, adjusted for what it's carrying right now.*

### Inventory skew: how the book bends the quote

The inventory effect deserves its own example, because it's the single most under-appreciated reason your fill is what it is. A market maker that is already carrying a big net-short position in some risk does not want more of it — so it shifts its *whole* two-sided market in the direction that flattens the book. The shift is small per contract, but it is *systematic*, and over thousands of fills it steers the desk's net exposure back toward neutral while quietly charging the customers who push it the wrong way.

#### Worked example: the desk skews its quote to shed inventory

The desk is short 800 of our \$100 calls from a morning of customer buying, and its risk system flags the net delta and vega as uncomfortably large. Fair value is still **\$2.4513**. Instead of quoting the symmetric **2.40 / 2.50**, the desk shifts the *whole* market up by 3 cents to **bid \$2.43 / ask \$2.53**:

- A customer who now *sells* the desk a call hits the **\$2.43 bid** — 3 cents *above* the old bid, so the seller gets a *better* price, and the desk is *eager* to fill because buying calls flattens its short. It gives up 3 cents of edge to attract the flow it needs.
- A customer who now *buys* lifts the **\$2.53 ask** — 3 cents *worse* than before — paying the desk **\$8.00 over fair value** instead of \$5.00. The desk *discourages* more buying by charging extra, and if someone buys anyway, the desk banks **+\$3.00 of additional edge** per contract precisely *because* the trade made its position worse.

Same option, same model, same instant — two different customers see two different deals, both bent by the 800 calls already on the book. The intuition: the MM's quote is not a neutral readout of fair value; it is fair value *tilted by inventory*, paying you to flatten its book and charging you to extend it — which is exactly why the same option can fill a hair cheap for a seller and a hair rich for a buyer at the very same second.

## The hedge: why the MM doesn't care which way you're right

Here is the part that surprises people most. Sell a call to a customer and you are now *short* a call — you lose if the stock rallies. That sounds like a directional bet against the customer. It would be, if the MM stopped there. It doesn't. The instant the trade prints, the MM buys delta shares of the underlying to cancel the directional risk, and from that moment its P&L barely depends on where the stock goes.

#### Worked example: the MM sells at the ask, delta-hedges, and earns the spread either way

Sell one of our \$100 calls at the \$2.50 ask and immediately buy **53 shares** (the delta of 0.5343, on a 100-share contract, rounded) at \$100. Now let one day pass and the stock move, with implied vol unchanged, and re-price the option from the model. The MM's per-contract P&L is the premium it collected minus the new option value, plus the gain or loss on the 53 hedge shares:

| Stock move over the day | New option value | Share-hedge P&L | Option P&L | Net MM P&L / contract |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| −\$3.00 | \$1.124 | −\$160.28 | +\$137.73 | **−\$22.55** |
| −\$1.00 | \$1.909 | −\$53.43 | +\$59.21 | **+\$5.79** |
| 0 | \$2.407 | \$0.00 | +\$9.39 | **+\$9.39** |
| +\$1.00 | \$2.976 | +\$53.43 | −\$47.47 | **+\$5.96** |
| +\$3.00 | \$4.311 | +\$160.28 | −\$180.99 | **−\$20.71** |

Look down the middle of the table. For *ordinary* daily moves — a dollar in either direction — the MM nets **+\$5.79 to +\$9.39 per contract regardless of whether the stock rose or fell.** The directional P&L on the option is almost exactly cancelled by the hedge shares; what's left is the spread/edge it banked plus a day's worth of time decay it collects as the short-option holder. The MM did *not* root for the stock to fall. It made roughly the same money either way. The intuition: **delta-hedging converts "a bet against the customer" into "a fee for taking the other side," and the fee is the spread plus the decay, not the direction.**

![Hedged market-maker profit and loss after one day across a range of underlying moves, roughly flat plus the captured edge for ordinary moves and bending down only on large moves](/imgs/blogs/how-an-options-market-maker-thinks-the-other-side-of-your-trade-3.png)

The curve makes the table visual. Across small moves the hedged P&L sits *above zero* by the edge the MM banked (the green dashed line marks the +\$5 edge), nearly flat — the MM wins a similar amount whether spot ticks up or down. Only on *large* moves does the curve bend below zero, into the red. That downside bend is the catch, and it has a name: **gamma**.

### The catch: short gamma

When the MM sold the call and hedged the delta, it did *not* hedge the *curvature*. The option's delta changes as the stock moves — that rate of change is **gamma** — and a short option is **short gamma**. Practically: as the stock rises, the call the MM is short gets *more* sensitive (its delta climbs toward 1), so the MM's hedge of 53 shares becomes too few, and it must buy more shares *after* the move — buying high. As the stock falls, the call's delta drops, the MM is now over-hedged, and it must sell shares *after* the fall — selling low. Buy high, sell low, over and over: that is the cost of being short gamma, and it's exactly the downside bend in the chart. The bigger the move, the worse the bend, because gamma P&L scales with the *square* of the move. (The full anatomy is in [gamma, the Greek that bites](/blog/trading/options-volatility/gamma-the-greek-that-bites-curvature-convexity-and-the-toxic-short).)

So the MM's real position after hedging is: **flat on direction (delta-hedged), short gamma (it bleeds on big moves and on choppy rehedging), short theta-from-the-customer's-side (it *collects* decay), and exposed to vega (the implied vol of the option it's now short).** The spread it pocketed has to be enough to pay for the expected cost of all that hedging — the discrete-rehedging slippage, the bid-ask it pays on its own hedge trades, and the gap risk it can't hedge at all. This is why the spread and the edge exist *and need to be as wide as they are*: they're not a markup for nothing, they're the MM's compensation for warehousing a short-gamma, vega-exposed position it can never perfectly neutralize.

## The Greek warehouse: how the book actually accumulates risk

One trade is easy to picture. A market maker does tens of thousands a day, across hundreds of strikes and expiries on a single name, and *every* fill drops a little delta, gamma, vega, and theta onto one shared book. The MM does not manage these position by position — it manages the *net*. The risk system continuously sums every contract's Greeks into a single dashboard: the desk's net delta in shares, net gamma, net vega per vol point, net theta per day. That netted number is the MM's actual exposure, and it's what every quote is trying to steer.

![Greek warehouse diagram: customer buys and sells of puts and calls flow into one net risk book, which the market maker hedges by delta in shares and lays off gamma and vega in other options](/imgs/blogs/how-an-options-market-maker-thinks-the-other-side-of-your-trade-4.png)

The figure shows the netting. On the left, four kinds of customer flow pour in: protection buyers lifting puts, upside buyers lifting calls, yield programs selling covered calls, dip-buyers selling cash-secured puts. All of it collapses (center) into one **net risk book** — a single net delta, a net gamma, a net vega. On the right, the MM disposes of that net risk three ways: it **hedges the delta in shares** (cheap, liquid, immediate), it **lays off gamma and vega by trading other options** (buying back optionality in different strikes and expiries to flatten the net), and it **warehouses the residual** — carries it deliberately, because carrying it is how it earns the variance risk premium. The note at the bottom is the punchline for you: the desk *skews its quotes* to shed inventory it's already too long or short, so your fill reflects not just the model but the book.

Why can't the MM just delta-hedge everything and call it a day? Because **delta is the only Greek you can hedge cheaply with the underlying.** Buying or selling shares changes your delta and nothing else (shares have no gamma, no vega, no theta). To change your *gamma* or *vega*, you must trade *another option* — and that option has its own spread, its own theta, its own everything. So the MM can flatten direction for the cost of a share trade, but to flatten curvature or vol exposure it has to go back into the options market and cross *someone's* spread. Often the cheapest move is to *not* fully hedge the gamma and vega — to warehouse them — and get paid the premium for holding them. The decision of how much to lay off versus warehouse is the core craft of the desk.

### Customers mostly buy options — so the MM is mostly short vol

Here's the structural fact that shapes everything downstream: **end users are net buyers of options.** They buy calls for upside, they buy puts for protection, they buy straddles into earnings. Yes, some sell covered calls and cash puts, but in aggregate — especially in index options — the customer base is a net *buyer* of optionality, because options are mostly bought as *insurance and lottery tickets*, and the natural direction of insurance is for the public to buy it and a dealer to underwrite it. That means the market maker, taking the other side, is structurally **net short options** — short gamma and short vega across its book.

Being net short vol is risky: the book loses when realized volatility comes in high (short gamma bleeds) and when implied volatility spikes (short vega bleeds). The MM does it anyway, because it gets *paid* to — and that payment is the **variance risk premium**.

#### Worked example: warehousing vega from a wave of put buying

Suppose, over a morning, customers buy **1,000** of our 30-day at-the-money puts as portfolio insurance. Price the put and its vega from the model:

- **Put fair value:** `bs_price(100, 100, 30/365, 0.04, 0.20, kind="put")` = **\$2.1230** per share.
- **Vega per vol point:** **0.1140** per share, i.e. **\$11.40 per contract** for a one-point move in implied vol.

The MM is now **short 1,000 puts**, so it's **short vega**: its book loses if implied vol rises. The total vega exposure is `1,000 × 0.1140 × 100 ≈` **\$11,400 of P&L per vol point.** If implied vol jumps **2 points** (a modest move in a nervous market), the MM's mark-to-market loss is `11,400 × 2 =` **\$22,790**, before any delta or gamma effects. To neutralize that, the MM would have to *buy* vega back — buy 1,000 puts' worth of vega in other strikes and expiries — and pay the spread to do it, surrendering much of the edge it collected. So instead it warehouses some of the short vega and lives with the exposure, *because* it expects to be paid the variance risk premium for holding it. The intuition: customer insurance demand makes the MM short vol whether it likes it or not, and the only reason that's a business and not a slow death is that implied vol is, on average, set high enough to pay the underwriter.

## Where the edge really comes from: four stacked sources

If the spread were the MM's only edge, market making would be a thin, brutal business — and competition does compress the spread toward the cost of providing it. The reason the business survives, and the reason your option costs what it costs, is that the MM's edge has *four* layers stacked on top of fair value. You, the customer, pay all four.

![Stacked layers of market-maker edge: fair value at the base, then bid-ask spread, variance risk premium, order-flow information, and skew, summing to what the customer pays](/imgs/blogs/how-an-options-market-maker-thinks-the-other-side-of-your-trade-6.png)

The stack reads bottom-up. At the base is **fair value** — the honest model price (blue). On top of it sit the four edges (amber), and the total (red, right) is what the buyer pays; the green block on the left is what the MM keeps after its own costs. Let's take the four in turn.

**1. The bid-ask spread.** Already covered: the visible fee for immediacy, about **+\$5 per contract** at a 5-cent half-spread on our liquid example, **+\$10 captured on a round trip.** It's the most certain edge — collected the instant of the fill — but also the most competed-away, because any MM can quote a penny tighter and steal the flow. On the most liquid options (SPY, QQQ, the megacaps) the spread is razor-thin precisely because dozens of MMs compete for the flow. On illiquid options it's wide because few will quote them.

**2. The variance risk premium (VRP).** This is the deep one, and the reason being net short vol is a *business*. On average, the implied volatility embedded in option prices prints *above* the realized volatility that actually follows. Across the S&P 500, 30-day implied vol has historically averaged near **19.5 vol points** against subsequent 30-day realized near **15.8** — a gap of about **+3.7 vol points** that option *sellers* earn and *buyers* pay. (The why is its own post: [the variance risk premium, why selling vol pays until it doesn't](/blog/trading/options-volatility/the-variance-risk-premium-why-selling-vol-pays-until-it-doesnt).) For our option, with a vega of \$11.40 per contract per vol point, a 3.7-point premium is worth roughly `11.40 × 3.7 ≈` **\$42 per contract** over the life of the option — far larger than the \$5 spread. The MM, structurally short vol because customers buy insurance, is the natural collector of this premium. *You pay it in the price of every option you buy*, embedded in an implied vol set above what will likely be realized.

**3. Order-flow information.** Not every order is uninformed. When a flurry of buying hits a specific out-of-the-money strike right before an event, that flow may *know something*, and an MM that fills it blindly gets run over (this is **adverse selection**, the next section). MMs read their flow: they distinguish dumb flow (a retail account buying a round lot of front-month calls) from sharp flow (an institution sweeping size across strikes), and they *price differently* for each. Filling benign flow at a tight spread and widening or fading toxic flow is itself an edge — the MM is, in effect, paid for sorting the customers. Payment for order flow (more below) is the retail-broker side of this: wholesalers pay brokers for the *right* to fill retail orders precisely because retail flow is, on average, uninformed and therefore profitable to take the other side of.

**4. The skew the customer crosses.** The vol surface isn't flat: out-of-the-money puts trade at higher implied vols than at-the-money or out-of-the-money calls — the [volatility smile and skew](/blog/trading/options-volatility/the-volatility-smile-and-skew-why-otm-puts-cost-more). When a customer buys a downside put, they're buying on the *richest* part of the surface, paying up for the very protection everyone else also wants. The MM, quoting off its fitted surface, charges the skew. A protection buyer crosses the spread *and* pays the elevated skew vol *and* feeds the VRP — three of the four edges at once. This is the precise mechanism behind "retail pays the spread and the vol twice": once in the bid-ask, again in the rich implied vol (VRP), and a third time in the skew if they're buying the wings.

The sum is sobering. On a downside put bought into a nervous tape, a customer can pay the spread *plus* a few extra vol points of skew *plus* the structural VRP — easily several percent of premium in stacked edge before the trade has any chance to be right on direction. **This is why directional option buying is so hard: you must overcome a multi-layered edge just to break even.** It's the same lesson as [why the pure directional long usually loses](/blog/trading/options-volatility/long-calls-and-puts-the-pure-directional-bet-and-why-it-usually-loses), now seen from the underwriter's side of the ledger.

## Adverse selection: the risk that makes the MM widen

The MM's whole model rests on its flow being, on average, uninformed — on customers crossing the spread for reasons (hedging, speculation, rebalancing) that don't systematically predict the next move. The threat to that model is **adverse selection**: the risk that the specific person trading against you *knows more than you do*, so that the trades you fill are systematically the wrong side. If informed traders preferentially lift your offer right before the stock jumps, your "edge" evaporates — you sold cheap to someone who knew it was cheap.

The MM's only defense is to **widen** — to make the spread big enough that the edge on the *benign* flow covers the losses to the *informed* flow. The wider the quote, the more the uninformed customers subsidize the MM's losses to the sharp ones, and the less attractive it is for an informed trader to cross at all. So the spread is not a fixed cost of doing business; it's a *dial* the MM turns up exactly when the probability of trading against information rises.

![Quote width versus event proximity and liquidity: the market maker half-spread balloons as an earnings print approaches and ordinary delta hedging can no longer protect against the jump](/imgs/blogs/how-an-options-market-maker-thinks-the-other-side-of-your-trade-5.png)

The chart shows the dial turning. Far from any event, on a liquid name, the half-spread sits near the calm 5-cent floor (green dashed). As an earnings print approaches (time runs right-to-left toward the event), the MM widens the half-spread — to 16, 34, 60 cents — because two things happen at once: more of the flow is likely *informed* (people position before earnings), and the **jump risk can't be delta-hedged** (the stock will gap on the print, and the MM's short gamma will lose through the gap with no chance to rehedge along the way). The red dotted line marks the vega cost of being just **one point** wrong on implied vol — \$11.40 per contract — to scale how fast a misjudged event quote can hurt. (The event-vol mechanics are in [trading event vol: earnings, FOMC, and the vol crush](/blog/trading/options-volatility/trading-event-vol-earnings-fomc-and-the-vol-crush).)

#### Worked example: why the MM widens into an earnings print

Our \$100-strike call is worth **\$2.4513** at the calm 20% implied vol. As earnings approach, the market bids implied vol up to price the expected jump — say to **35%**. Re-price:

- **Call at 35% IV:** `bs_price(100, 100, 30/365, 0.04, 0.35, kind="call")` = **\$4.1611** — the same option now costs **\$1.71 more**, purely from the higher implied vol.
- **Vega per point (near the money):** about **\$11.40 per contract**, so a misjudgment of just **1 vol point** in the MM's event quote is a **\$11.40** swing per contract.

Now put yourself in the MM's seat. Across the print, the stock will gap — maybe ±8% — and the MM's short gamma will lose `0.5 × gamma × (move)²` with no path to hedge through it. On top of that, anyone buying the calls right before earnings might *know* something. So the MM faces (a) un-hedgeable jump risk, (b) elevated adverse selection, and (c) the chance it has the event vol wrong by several of those \$11.40 points. A 5-cent half-spread cannot cover that. The MM widens to 30, 40, 60 cents — and on the most uncertain single names, dollars wide. The intuition: **the spread is the MM's insurance premium against trading with the informed and against gaps it can't hedge; when both risks spike, the premium — your cost to trade — spikes with them.**

The same logic explains **illiquid strikes**. A deep out-of-the-money put on a thin name trades rarely, can't be easily hedged or laid off, and is exactly the kind of contract a sharp trader picks when they have a specific view. So the MM quotes it dollars wide — not from greed, but because the only flow it's likely to see there is either nonexistent or dangerous. This is the forward bridge to [liquidity, bid-ask spreads, and getting filled: the hidden tax](/blog/trading/options-volatility/liquidity-bid-ask-spreads-and-getting-filled-the-hidden-tax), which dwells on exactly how that width taxes your trades.

### A word on payment for order flow

You can't discuss who's on the other side of a retail option trade without the **payment-for-order-flow (PFOF)** ecosystem. When a retail trader clicks Buy on a zero-commission app, the order usually doesn't go to a public exchange; it's routed to a **wholesaler** — a large electronic market maker — that pays the broker for the right to fill it. The wholesaler fills the retail order (often at or slightly inside the public quote) and keeps the edge: the spread, minus a rebate to the broker, on flow that is, in aggregate, uninformed and therefore profitable to internalize. The retail trader gets "free" commissions and a fast fill; the broker gets paid by the wholesaler; the wholesaler gets a clean stream of benign flow to harvest. It is the four-edge stack, applied at industrial scale to the retail crowd. None of it is sinister — it's the same market-making economics in this post, organized so the most uninformed flow finds the MMs most eager to take it. (For the broader plumbing of who quotes and why, see [market makers and high-frequency trading](/blog/trading/finance/market-makers-and-high-frequency-trading).) The practical reading for you: your "instant free fill" is instant and free *because* you are exactly the customer the system is built to serve — at its price.

## Common misconceptions

**"The market maker is betting against my direction."** No — the MM hedges your direction away within milliseconds and is essentially indifferent to it. In the worked example, the MM sold a call and was *up* roughly +\$5.79 to +\$9.39 per contract whether the stock rose \$1 or fell \$1 the next day, because the 53 hedge shares cancelled the directional P&L. The MM is short *gamma* and short *vega*, not short *you*. It loses on big moves and vol spikes regardless of which way they go, and it makes the spread regardless of which way you're right. If you think you're in a duel with the dealer over direction, you've misread the trade — you're in a duel with everyone else trading the same vol, and the MM is the toll booth.

**"A tight spread means the market maker isn't making much."** The spread is only the *first* of four edges. On a liquid name the bid-ask might be a penny or two — about \$5 per contract round-tripped — but the MM also collects the variance risk premium (about \$42 per contract over the life of our option, more than 8× the spread), the skew the customer crosses, and the value of sorting informed from uninformed flow. A penny-wide market on SPY options is not charity; it's a high-volume harvest of the VRP with the spread competed down to the cost of providing immediacy. The visible spread understates the edge — sometimes by an order of magnitude.

**"I got filled instantly at a good price, so I beat the market maker."** An instant fill is the *opposite* of a victory — it means you traded at a price the MM was *delighted* to give. The MM's machine quoted, you crossed, it hedged, it banked the edge, all before you blinked. A fast fill at the touch tells you the MM faced no adverse selection from your order and was happy to take it. The trades the MM *doesn't* want — the ones from someone who knows something — are the ones it *widens* against or refuses to fill cheaply. If you sailed through, you were benign flow. Beating the MM doesn't look like a fast fill; it looks like being right on the *view* by more than the stacked edge you paid.

**"The market maker can be squeezed if I trade big size."** Backwards — *size is what makes the MM widen against you*, because a large order is exactly the signature of informed flow. Lift the offer for 500 contracts and you've told the MM "I have conviction," and its system will raise the offer, fade you, or fill you in pieces at progressively worse prices (market impact). The retail intuition that "being a bigger fish" gets you a better deal is exactly inverted in market making: small, patient, fragmented orders look benign and trade near mid; large, urgent, lift-the-offer orders look toxic and pay up. You don't squeeze the MM with size; you *signal* with it, and pay for the signal.

**"The variance risk premium is free money, so I should just sell options like the dealers."** The VRP is *real* but it is not free, and "like the dealers" is doing heavy lifting. The MM earns it while running an industrial hedging operation — instant delta hedging, gamma and vega laid off across the surface, deep capital, and the ability to warehouse risk through a storm. A retail seller of naked options collects the same premium but *without the hedge*, which means they're long the VRP and short the tail with no defense. As the [VRP post](/blog/trading/options-volatility/the-variance-risk-premium-why-selling-vol-pays-until-it-doesnt) shows, selling vol "pays until it doesn't," and the "doesn't" is a short-gamma blowup that can erase years of premium in a day. The MM survives the storm because it hedges and diversifies across thousands of names; the naked seller doesn't. The premium is compensation for a risk you must actually be able to bear.

## How it shows up in real markets

**The earnings vol-crush, from the dealer's chair.** The week before a single-name earnings report, the options market maker watches implied vol climb as customers buy straddles and calls to bet on the move. The MM, taking the other side, gets shorter and shorter vol into the print, and *widens its quotes* as both adverse selection and un-hedgeable jump risk rise — exactly the chart above. On the print, the stock gaps, the event is resolved, and implied vol *collapses* (the vol crush) because the uncertainty it was pricing is gone. The MM, short that elevated vol, *profits from the crush* — it sold expensive insurance against an event and the insurance expired worthless-rich. But it earned that profit by warehousing genuine gap risk through the report; in the years a name gaps far enough, the short gamma overwhelms the vol it collected. The dealer's edge into earnings is real on average and brutal in the tail — which is precisely why the quote is so wide going in.

**The August 2024 vol shock.** On Monday, August 5, 2024, a yen-carry unwind gapped global equities down hard and the VIX spiked to **38.57** intraday — one of the largest single-day vol shocks on record. Market makers across the Street were, as always, net short gamma and short vega (because customers are net long options). The gap blew past every delta hedge — there was no continuous path to rehedge along between Friday's close and Monday's open — and the vol spike marked down every short-vega book at once. Desks that had *reduced* their short gamma into the weekend (paying the spread to buy back optionality) lost far less than those that held the full short position to collect the last of the weekend theta. This is the MM's recurring nightmare made concrete: the spread and the VRP it collects on a thousand quiet days can be handed back in a single gap, because the one risk it cannot hedge — the jump — is the one that defines the tail.

**The 0DTE gamma grind.** Same-day-expiry options have enormous gamma in their final hours. A market maker short 0DTE options must rehedge furiously as spot oscillates around the strike — buying high and selling low on every wiggle through it — and the cost of all that rehedging is the realized version of being short gamma. The MM charges for this in the option's price (0DTE premiums embed the expected hedging cost of that violent gamma), and on a choppy afternoon the realized rehedging cost can exceed even those rich premiums. The customer buying a 0DTE lottery ticket sees a cheap-looking absolute price; the MM sees a contract whose gamma is five times a 30-day's and whose hedging cost it has carefully baked into the quote. The grind is the [delta-hedging-in-practice](/blog/trading/options-volatility/delta-hedging-in-practice-the-cost-and-slippage-of-staying-neutral) cost, dialed to its maximum, passed straight to the buyer.

**Index dispersion and the dealer's skew book.** In index options, the structural net-short-vol position is at its most extreme, because pensions, insurers, and structured-product desks are relentless buyers of index puts for protection. Dealers warehouse a vast short-skew, short-vega index book and lay off what they can in single-name options — the [dispersion trade](/blog/trading/options-volatility/dispersion-and-correlation-trading-index-vol-vs-single-name-vol) is partly the dealer community managing exactly this inventory. The persistent richness of index-put skew — the reason out-of-the-money index puts are so expensive — is in large part the price dealers charge to keep underwriting the protection the world keeps buying. When you buy an index put, you are the millionth customer in a long line of insurance buyers, paying a skew that exists *because* of that line.

**Competition is what sets the spread you actually pay.** The four-edge stack tells you how the MM *could* profit; competition decides how much it *keeps*. On the most liquid options — index ETFs, the megacaps — a dozen electronic desks quote the same contract, each willing to shave a penny to win the flow, and the spread collapses toward the bare cost of providing immediacy. The VRP is still there (no one competes it away, because it's compensation for real tail risk everyone shares), but the bid-ask narrows to almost nothing. On illiquid single-name options, the opposite: one or two desks bother to quote, there's no penny-shaving pressure, and the spread stays dollars wide. So the spread you pay is a near-direct readout of *how many MMs are fighting for your order*. The practical consequence is blunt: trade where the competition is fierce (liquid, front-month, near-the-money) and the spread is small; trade where it's thin and you hand the lone MM its full edge. The desk isn't choosing to gouge you on the illiquid strike — there's simply no second quoter to undercut it.

**The market maker's daily P&L, in one sentence.** Add it up and a liquid options MM's day is: the spread captured on all the flow, plus the variance risk premium earned on the net short-vol book, minus the slippage of hedging that book delta-neutral thousands of times, minus whatever the day's realized moves cost its short gamma, minus whatever it lost to informed flow. On a calm day the spread and premium beat the costs and the desk makes money on the grind. On a gap day the short gamma and short vega can erase weeks of grind in an hour. The MM has no view on direction — it has a view on whether the edge it's charging covers the cost of the risk it's warehousing. When it doesn't think it does, it widens, and your cost to trade goes up.

## The playbook: how to trade against a hedged book

Everything above collapses into a handful of habits. You are not going to out-trade the market maker on the spread — it quotes faster, hedges better, and is capitalized to warehouse risk you can't. You can only win on the *view*. So your job is to pay the *least possible edge* getting your view on, and to make sure your view is big enough to clear what edge remains.

![Trader takeaways for facing a hedged book: trade liquid strikes, use limit orders, do not lift offers in size, and treat an instant fill as a warning](/imgs/blogs/how-an-options-market-maker-thinks-the-other-side-of-your-trade-7.png)

The diagram is the routine on one screen — what feeds the MM (red), what protects you (green), and the four takeaways below it. Walk them in order.

**Trade liquid strikes — liquidity is the tax rate.** The spread you pay is set by how many MMs compete for that contract. Front-month, at-the-money options on SPY, QQQ, and the megacaps are pennies wide because dozens of desks quote them; deep out-of-the-money options on thin single names are dollars wide because almost none will. Before you trade, look at the bid-ask: if it's wide, *that width is your cost*, and on the round trip you'll pay it twice. Prefer the liquid expiry and the strike near the money unless your view specifically requires the wing — and if it does, know you're buying on the richest, widest part of the surface.

**Use limit orders, not market orders.** A market order says "fill me now at any price," which on a wide or fast market means paying the full ask (or worse, through it on size). A limit order near the mid says "I'll trade at *this* price" and asks the MM to give up some edge to get your flow. You won't always get filled at mid, but you'll never pay *through* your limit, and on anything wider than a penny the difference between mid and the touch is real money. The discipline: decide the price the trade is worth to you *before* you click, and let the limit hold the line — don't chase a fill by lifting the offer just because the screen is moving.

**Don't lift offers in size — work it in pieces.** A big, urgent order is the signature of informed flow, and the MM's system will widen against it, fade it, or fill it at progressively worse prices. If you need size, work it: smaller clips, patience, limit orders that let the MM fill you near mid as natural flow appears. Lifting 500 contracts at the touch tells the desk you have conviction and lets it charge you for the privilege. Looking benign — small, patient, fragmented — is how you trade nearest to fair value.

**Treat an instant fill at the touch as information, not a triumph.** If you market-buy and get filled in a blink at the ask, the MM saw nothing dangerous in your order and was delighted to take it. That's fine for a small, deliberate position — but recognize what it tells you: you paid the full edge, and the desk would happily do it again. The fills you'd *want* (the ones where you're trading with information) are the ones the MM widens against. A frictionless fill means you were the easy customer.

**Budget the stacked edge into the trade — and require a view that clears it.** Before you put on a directional option, add up what you're paying: the spread (visible in the bid-ask), the VRP baked into the implied vol (is implied rich versus what you expect to realize?), and the skew if you're buying the wings. If you're buying a call into earnings at 35% implied when you think realized will be 20%, you are paying the MM the VRP *and* the event premium, and you need the stock to move far enough to overcome *all* of it. If you're *selling* premium to collect that edge, understand you're stepping into the MM's shoes *without the MM's hedge* — you're long the VRP and short the tail, and you must be sized to survive the gap the MM warehouses against. The [implied-versus-realized trade](/blog/trading/options-volatility/implied-vs-realized-volatility-the-trade-at-the-heart-of-options) is fought on the *edge-adjusted* spread, never the raw price on the screen.

**Read the quote as the MM's model plus its inventory.** A skewed market — bid closer to fair than the ask, or vice versa — is the desk telling you which side it wants. A market that's wider than usual on a name is the desk pricing event or adverse-selection risk. You can't see the MM's book, but you can read its *quote* as a signal: when the spread is unusually wide, the desk is nervous, and trading into that nervousness is expensive. Patience near the mid, on liquid strikes, in calm conditions, is how you keep the edge you surrender to a minimum.

**The single habit to build:** before every option trade, name the edge you're paying and the view that has to beat it. Write down the spread (from the bid-ask), the VRP (implied versus your realized estimate), and the skew (are you buying the wings?). If your directional or vol view doesn't clear the sum of those three, you don't have a trade — you have a donation to a hedged book that is, as Dan learned in the first second of his earnings bet, *delighted* to take the other side at its price.
