> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Every options trade is, underneath the direction and the Greeks, a bet on the gap between *implied* volatility (the vol the market's prices charge you today) and *realized* volatility (the vol the underlying actually delivers afterwards).
>
> - **Realized vol is an outcome you measure; implied vol is a price you trade.** Realized vol is the annualized standard deviation of past log returns — a fact about what happened. Implied vol is the number you back out of an option's market price — a forecast bundled with a risk premium, set by supply and demand.
> - **Buying options is being long realized vol and short the implied-vol level; selling is the reverse.** A long straddle wins if the stock realizes *more* movement than its implied vol charged; a short straddle wins if it realizes *less*.
> - **On average, implied prints above the realized that follows — the variance risk premium.** Over the long run S&P 500 one-month implied vol has averaged about 19.5 vol points against roughly 15.8 realized, a structural **+3.7** edge for sellers. It is not free money; it is compensation for carrying crash risk.
> - **The number to remember:** sell vol when implied is rich versus its own history *and* versus realized, buy when it is cheap, and never forget the +3.7 gap is the market paying you to hold a grenade.

A trader looks at a quiet, range-bound stock and decides its options are absurdly cheap. Implied volatility is sitting at 15% — the lowest it has been all year — and the trader is sure the calm cannot last. So they buy a one-month at-the-money straddle, paying about \$3.44 per share for the call and the put together, betting the stock will break out. The breakout never comes. Day after day the stock drifts in a tighter and tighter range, realizing only about 10% volatility. At expiry the straddle is nearly worthless. The trader was "long cheap vol" — and the vol was cheap for a reason. It stayed cheap, the stock stayed calm, and the position bled out at roughly \$1.14 per share, the difference between what 15% cost and what 10% was worth.

Across town, a different trader sells a one-week straddle into a company's earnings report when implied volatility is screaming at 45%. The premium is fat — about \$4.97 per share — because the whole market is bracing for a big move. The report comes out, the surprise is mild, and the next morning implied vol collapses back to 20% with the stock barely changed. The straddle the second trader sold for \$4.97 is now worth about \$2.05. They buy it back and pocket roughly \$2.92 per share overnight. They didn't predict the news. They predicted that the *price* of the move — the implied vol — was higher than the move that would actually print.

These two traders took opposite sides of the *same trade*. One was long the spread between implied and realized vol; the other was short it. Neither was making a pure directional bet on the stock. Both were trading the difference between **what volatility was priced at** and **what volatility turned out to be**. That spread — implied minus realized — is the single most important quantity in options, and this post is about understanding it, measuring it, and putting yourself on the right side of it.

![Two lines from 2004 to 2024 with implied volatility above a realized volatility proxy and the gap between them shaded as the variance risk premium](/imgs/blogs/implied-vs-realized-volatility-the-trade-at-the-heart-of-options-1.png)

In the [vega post](/blog/trading/options-volatility/vega-your-exposure-to-implied-volatility-and-the-vol-of-vol) we met implied volatility as one of the five inputs into an option's price, and we learned that being long an option means being long vega — exposed to the *level* of implied vol. This post takes the next step. It is not enough to know you are exposed to implied vol; you have to know whether that vol is *expensive or cheap*, and the only honest yardstick is the realized vol that follows. The cover figure above is the whole thesis in one picture: the blue implied-vol line, year after year, tends to sit above the green realized-vol line, and the amber band between them is the edge that gets fought over every single day in the options market. Let us build that picture from the ground up.

## Foundations: two different things that both get called "volatility"

The word "volatility" hides two completely different objects, and the entire post hinges on keeping them apart. One is a fact about the past. The other is a price about the future. They share a name, a unit, and a symbol, which is exactly why beginners conflate them and lose money.

Start with the everyday version. Suppose you want to know how "bumpy" a car ride was. You could measure it after the fact: strap an accelerometer to the seat, record the whole trip, and compute how much the readings jumped around. That number describes a ride that already happened — it is a *measurement*. Alternatively, before the trip, an insurer could quote you a price to cover any whiplash injury, and that price would embed the insurer's guess about how bumpy the ride *might* be, plus a margin for the risk that they are wrong. That price is *not* a measurement of bumpiness. It is a forecast wearing a price tag. Realized volatility is the accelerometer reading. Implied volatility is the insurance quote.

### Realized (historical) volatility: the measurement

**Realized volatility** — also called historical volatility — is the annualized standard deviation of an asset's returns over some past window. It answers the question: "how much did this thing actually wiggle?" It is computed entirely from price history, with no options involved. Here is the standard recipe, the close-to-close estimator:

1. Take a series of closing prices and turn them into **log returns**: for each day, the return is the natural log of today's close divided by yesterday's close, `r = ln(P_today / P_yesterday)`. Log returns are used instead of simple percentage changes because they add up cleanly over time and are roughly symmetric, which matches how the pricing model treats them.
2. Compute the **standard deviation** of those daily log returns — the typical size of a day's wiggle around its average.
3. **Annualize** by multiplying by the square root of the number of trading days in a year, conventionally `sqrt(252)`. The square-root scaling comes from the fact that variance (the square of standard deviation) grows linearly with time, so standard deviation grows with the square root of time — the same square-root-of-time rule that governs how option premiums scale with tenor.

The output is a single percentage, like "the stock realized 24% volatility over the last ten days." That 24% is an annualized figure even though it was measured over ten days; annualizing puts every volatility number — one-week, one-month, one-year — on the same comparable scale.

Two choices in that recipe deserve a moment, because they are where most of the confusion hides. The first is **why log returns instead of plain percentage returns**. A log return has a property that a percentage return lacks: it adds up across periods. If a stock goes up 10% one day and down 10% the next, the percentage returns are +10% and −10%, but the stock is not back where it started — it is at `1.10 × 0.90 = 0.99`, down 1%. Log returns fix this: `ln(1.10) + ln(0.90) = +0.0953 − 0.1054 = −0.0101`, which is exactly the −1.01% the stock actually realized over the two days. Because they sum cleanly, a multi-day variance is just the sum of daily variances, which is precisely what makes the annualization step legitimate. The second choice is **whether to subtract the mean**. The textbook standard deviation subtracts the average return before squaring; many practitioners *do not*, on the argument that over a short window the true drift is tiny and noisy, and assuming a zero mean gives a less jittery vol estimate. For a ten-day window the difference is in the third decimal place, but it is another reason two desks can quote slightly different realized vols for the same stock — they made different assumptions about a number that barely matters.

The annualization factor of `sqrt(252)` deserves its own line because it is the same square-root-of-time rule that governs the entire options world. Variance accumulates *linearly* in time: the variance of a two-day return is twice the variance of a one-day return, because the days are (approximately) independent and variances of independent things add. Standard deviation, being the square root of variance, therefore grows with the *square root* of time. So a daily vol of 1.5% becomes an annual vol of `1.5% × sqrt(252) ≈ 23.8%`, and a one-week vol is `sqrt(5)` times the daily, not five times. This is exactly why a 30-day option's break-even move is roughly `sigma × sqrt(30/365)` of spot rather than `sigma × 30/365` — vol scales with the square root of horizon, never linearly, and internalizing that single fact prevents a whole category of sizing mistakes.

A subtle but important point: **realized vol is backward-looking and known only after the fact.** You cannot know today what volatility the next month will realize, because the prices that determine it have not happened yet. Realized vol over a future window is exactly the thing an option buyer is hoping to capture and an option seller is hoping stays small. It is the *outcome* in the trade.

There are fancier estimators than close-to-close, and they exist because the closing price throws away most of the day's information. The **Parkinson** estimator uses the high and the low of each day instead of just the close, on the logic that a stock that ranged from 98 to 105 and closed at 100 was clearly more volatile than one that ground sideways from 99.8 to 100.2 and also closed at 100 — yet close-to-close would score the second day's *open-to-close* identically if both opened at 100. The **Garman-Klass** estimator goes further and blends the open, high, low, and close, squeezing more efficiency out of each day so you need fewer days of history for the same precision. These range-based estimators can be several times more efficient than close-to-close, but they have blind spots — they understate volatility when most of the move happens in overnight gaps, because the gap occurs while the market is closed and never shows up in the intraday range. For the purposes of this post the close-to-close number is what matters; just know that "realized vol" is a family of estimators, not a single canonical figure, and that two desks can quote slightly different realized vols for the same stock because they used different estimators or windows.

### Implied volatility: the price

**Implied volatility** is the volatility number you would have to feed into the Black-Scholes pricing model to make the model spit out the option's *actual market price*. It is not measured; it is *backed out*. The market quotes a premium, and you run the pricing model in reverse, asking "what volatility assumption makes this premium fair?" That answer is the implied vol.

This works because of a clean mathematical fact: **an option's price is a strictly increasing function of volatility.** Hold the strike, spot, time, and rate fixed, and the higher you set the volatility input, the higher the model price — every single time, with no exceptions and no plateaus. A function that always increases is invertible: given a price, there is exactly one volatility that produces it. That one-to-one map between price and volatility is what *defines* implied vol. We are not estimating a hidden parameter from noisy data; we are reading a price off a curve.

![Call price rising smoothly with implied volatility, with a market price of 2.45 dollars mapped back to a 20 percent implied vol](/imgs/blogs/implied-vs-realized-volatility-the-trade-at-the-heart-of-options-2.png)

The figure above shows the map for a 30-day at-the-money call on a \$100 stock. As implied vol climbs from 5% toward 60%, the call's price climbs smoothly and monotonically. Pick any price on the vertical axis — say \$2.45 — draw a horizontal line, and it strikes the curve at exactly one place, which projects down to 20% on the horizontal axis. That is the inversion: a market price of \$2.45 *is* an implied vol of 20%. Quote the price, read off the vol. (The numerical procedure to find that crossing point — Newton's method or a bisection search — is mechanical; we do not derive Black-Scholes itself here, see the [Black-Scholes deep-dive](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/black-scholes) for the pricing math, and the [volatility surface](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/volatility-surface) post for why different strikes invert to different implied vols.)

Because implied vol is a *price*, it behaves like a price, not like a measurement. It is set by supply and demand. When everyone scrambles to buy crash protection, the price of puts rises, and the implied vol you back out of those puts rises with it — whether or not the stock has actually become more volatile yet. Implied vol can spike on fear alone, ahead of any realized movement, and it can stay stubbornly high or low for reasons that have nothing to do with a forecast and everything to do with who is buying and who is selling. This is the deepest distinction in the whole subject:

> **Realized volatility is an outcome you measure after the fact. Implied volatility is a price you trade before the fact. The first is a number; the second is a market.**

#### Worked example: realized vol from a ten-day price series

Let us compute a realized vol by hand so the recipe is concrete. Take eleven consecutive closing prices for a stock: \$100.00, \$101.50, \$100.20, \$102.00, \$101.00, \$99.50, \$100.80, \$102.40, \$101.10, \$103.00, \$101.80. That gives ten daily log returns. The first is `ln(101.50 / 100.00) = +1.489%`; the second is `ln(100.20 / 101.50) = -1.289%`; and so on through the series, giving returns of roughly +1.49%, −1.29%, +1.78%, −0.99%, −1.50%, +1.30%, +1.58%, −1.28%, +1.86%, −1.17%.

Now take the standard deviation of those ten daily returns. Their average is close to zero (the stock ended near where it started), and the typical deviation works out to about **1.512% per day**. That is the daily volatility. Annualize it by multiplying by `sqrt(252)`: `1.512% × 15.875 ≈ 24.0%`. So this stock realized about **24% annualized volatility** over these ten days.

Notice what we did and did not use. We used *only prices*. No options, no model, no forecast — just the record of what already happened. **Realized vol is the stock confessing how much it actually moved; it has no opinion about tomorrow.**

## The core trade: you are long or short the spread

Here is the sentence the entire series is built around, now made fully concrete: **when you trade an option, you are taking a position in the spread between implied volatility and realized volatility.** Everything else — the strike, the expiry, the directional lean — is detail layered on top of that fundamental bet.

When you **buy** an option, you pay a premium that embeds today's implied vol. You then profit if the underlying *realizes more volatility* than that implied vol charged you for. You are **long realized vol and short the level of implied vol you paid.** A long straddle is the purest expression: you buy a call and a put at the same strike, you have no directional view (the deltas cancel near the money), and you simply need the stock to *move* — to realize more than the implied vol baked into the premium you paid. If it realizes more, the gamma gains on your long options outrun the premium you spent. If it realizes less, you bleed.

When you **sell** an option, you receive the premium — you collect today's implied vol — and you profit if the underlying *realizes less volatility* than that implied vol promised. You are **short realized vol and long the level of implied vol you received.** A short straddle wins when the stock sits still: you keep the fat premium and pay out little, because the stock never moved enough to put your short options in the money.

![Before and after framing showing implied vol traded before the move and realized vol revealed after, with profit and loss equal to the gap](/imgs/blogs/implied-vs-realized-volatility-the-trade-at-the-heart-of-options-3.png)

The figure above is the cleanest way to hold this in your head: two clocks. *Before* the move, on the left, the future is unresolved and the only thing you can trade is implied vol — the price the market quotes. You take a side: buy options to be long the realized vol that is coming, or sell them to be short it. *After* the move, on the right, the path has actually happened and realized vol is no longer a forecast but a fact you can measure. Your profit and loss is the gap: realized above implied and the buyer wins; realized below implied and the seller wins. The whole trade lives in the space between those two clocks.

This reframing is liberating because it cuts through a confusion that paralyzes new options traders: "I think the stock is going up, so I should buy calls." Maybe. But the call's price already contains a volatility forecast, and if that forecast is too rich, you can be right on direction and still lose — exactly the vega trap from the [vega post](/blog/trading/options-volatility/vega-your-exposure-to-implied-volatility-and-the-vol-of-vol), where a trader nailed a 3% earnings rally and *still* lost money because implied vol crushed from 60% to 30% overnight. The direction was right; the volatility *price* was wrong. Once you see options as a vol trade first and a direction trade second, you start asking the right question before every trade: *not* "where is the stock going?" but "is the volatility I am about to pay or receive cheap or expensive relative to what is likely to actually happen?"

### Isolating the pure vol bet by hedging the delta

A naked long call mixes two bets: a directional bet (delta) and a vol bet (the IV-versus-RV spread). If you want the *pure* vol trade — the thing this post is about — you strip out the direction by **delta-hedging**. Buy the call, then short enough shares to make the position's net delta zero, so a small move in the stock leaves the position roughly unchanged. What remains, once direction is neutralized, is exactly the gamma-and-theta machine: you make money when the stock moves a lot (realizes high vol) and lose when it sits still (realizes low vol), regardless of *which way* it moves. The delta hedge converts a messy directional-plus-vol position into a clean bet on realized versus implied. This is why professional vol traders almost never hold un-hedged options for a vol view — the un-hedged delta is just noise drowning out the signal they actually want, which is the spread.

The decomposition is worth making explicit because it is the cleanest way to see what you are really exposed to. Over a short interval, the change in a long option's value is approximately `delta × (move in spot) + 0.5 × gamma × (move)² + vega × (change in implied vol) − theta × (time elapsed)`. The first term is direction, and a delta hedge zeroes it. The second and fourth terms are the gamma-versus-theta tug-of-war — the realized-vol bet, paid out daily. The third term is the mark-to-market on the implied-vol *level* — your exposure to the price moving while you hold. A delta-hedged long option, then, is long the gamma-theta machine (long realized vol) *and* long the vega mark (long the implied-vol level). You profit if the stock realizes more than you paid, and you also profit if implied vol rises while you hold even before any realizing happens. Selling is the mirror: short both. Holding this decomposition in your head is what lets you answer, for any position, the only two questions that matter — "am I long or short realized vol?" and "am I long or short the implied-vol level?"

#### Worked example: backing implied vol out of a market price

Let us run the inversion that defines implied vol. Suppose you see a 30-day at-the-money call on a \$100 stock (strike \$100, rate 4%) quoted at \$1.882. What implied vol does that price correspond to? You search for the volatility that reproduces the price. Try 10%: the model says the call is worth \$1.313 — too low, so the market is pricing more vol than that. Try 20%: \$2.451 — too high. The answer is between. A bisection search converges quickly to **15.0%**: at 15% vol the model price is exactly \$1.882, matching the quote.

Now do it for two more quotes on the same option. A market price of \$2.451 inverts to exactly **20%** implied vol. A market price of \$3.591 inverts to exactly **30%** implied vol. Each price maps to one and only one vol, because the price-versus-vol curve only ever rises. **The market gives you a dollar price; you hand back the one volatility number that price is secretly quoting — that number is the implied vol, and it is the thing you are really trading.**

## The variance risk premium: why sellers have a structural edge

If implied vol were a *pure forecast* of realized vol, then on average the two would be equal — the market's prediction would be right on average, the buyer and seller would each break even over many trades, and there would be no structural edge to either side. That is not what the data shows. **Implied vol systematically prints above the realized vol that follows.** The market, on average, charges more for volatility than volatility turns out to cost. That persistent gap is the **variance risk premium** (VRP).

The cover figure showed it across two decades: the blue implied line sits above the green realized line in most years, and the amber band between them is the premium. Now look at it as a single number.

![Bar chart comparing average implied volatility of 19.5 against average subsequent realized volatility of 15.8 with the 3.7 point gap labeled as the variance risk premium](/imgs/blogs/implied-vs-realized-volatility-the-trade-at-the-heart-of-options-5.png)

Over the long run, S&P 500 one-month implied volatility has averaged roughly **19.5 vol points**, while the realized volatility that actually followed averaged about **15.8 vol points**. The difference is about **+3.7 vol points** — and crucially it is *positive*: implied minus subsequent realized is, on average, a gain to whoever was *short* vol. This is one of the most robust empirical facts in derivatives, documented across decades and across markets (Carr & Wu; Bollerslev, Tauchen & Zhou; and the long literature on the VIX versus subsequent realized). The option seller, on average, collects a vol they never have to pay back in full.

Why does this premium exist, and why does it persist instead of being arbitraged away? Because **selling volatility is selling insurance, and insurance is supposed to be profitable on average.** When you sell a straddle or a put, you are taking the other side of someone's crash protection. Most of the time nothing terrible happens, and you keep the premium — that is the +3.7. But occasionally the market gaps down 8% in a day, the protection you sold pays out enormously, and you hand back years of accumulated premium in a single session. The VRP is the *compensation* for bearing that crash risk. It is structurally positive precisely *because* the payoff is structurally lopsided: small steady gains punctuated by rare catastrophic losses. Investors are willing to overpay for protection against the catastrophe, the way homeowners overpay (relative to expected losses) for fire insurance, and that overpayment is the seller's edge.

Why doesn't the premium get competed away? If selling vol is profitable on average, you would expect enough sellers to pile in to drive implied down to realized and erase the +3.7. Three forces keep it alive. First, **persistent demand for protection**: pension funds, insurers, and corporates are structurally *long* the underlying and want downside hedges, so there is a steady bid for puts that someone must sell, and the seller demands a premium to do it. Second, the **awful shape of the payoff** deters capital: a strategy whose returns are years of small gains and occasional ruinous losses is hard to hold, hard to risk-manage, and lethal to a leveraged book — so the capital that *would* arbitrage the premium stays away or gets blown out before it can. Third, **leverage and margin constraints**: harvesting the premium efficiently requires selling a lot of vol per dollar of capital, and the margin rules (and the gap risk) cap how much any one player can do. The premium is the price the market pays to coax a limited pool of willing sellers into bearing a risk most participants actively want to shed. That is why it is durable but not infinite — it widens when sellers get scared off (after a blow-up) and compresses when too many pile in (right before one).

This is the thread that runs to the [variance risk premium post (C6)](/blog/trading/options-volatility/the-variance-risk-premium-why-selling-vol-pays-until-it-doesnt), whose title says it all: *selling vol pays until it doesn't*. The +3.7 is real, but it is not a free lunch — it is a wage for standing in front of a train that mostly does not come. Any vol-selling strategy that ignores the train will look brilliant for years and then blow up in an afternoon, which is exactly the story of [Volmageddon in February 2018](/blog/trading/cross-asset/volatility-as-an-asset-owning-fear), when a wave of short-vol products imploded as the VIX doubled overnight.

#### Worked example: a vol seller's edge over a year

Let us turn the +3.7 into dollars. Suppose you sell a one-month at-the-money straddle on a \$100 stock every month for a year, always pricing it at the average implied of 19.5% and always letting the average realized of 15.8% determine what it was "really" worth.

Price the straddle at the implied vol you sell it at: a one-month (T = 1/12) at-the-money straddle at 19.5% vol is worth about **\$4.49 per share** by the model — that is what you collect. But the realized vol that follows averages only 15.8%, and a straddle priced at 15.8% would have been worth about **\$3.64**. The difference, **\$0.85 per share per month**, is your average edge — the premium you collected minus the fair value of the movement that actually showed up.

Repeat that twelve times in a year and the edge sums to roughly **\$10.18 per share** — on a \$100 stock, about a 10% gross harvest from selling vol alone, before transaction costs and before the inevitable bad month. That is the variance risk premium made tangible. **The seller's structural edge is real and sizeable, which is exactly why it lures so many people into selling vol without respecting the crash month that eventually arrives and takes a chunk of it back.**

## How to measure it and act: IV rank, the IV–RV spread, and vol cones

Knowing that implied tends to exceed realized is the strategic backdrop. The tactical question is: *right now, for this specific underlying, is implied vol rich or cheap?* The long-run +3.7 average does not tell you whether today's 35% implied vol on a single stock is a screaming sell or a fair price. You need to compare today's implied vol to two reference points: its own history, and the realized vol around it.

### IV rank and IV percentile

**IV rank** answers "where is implied vol within its own recent range?" Take the highest and lowest implied vol over the last year. If implied vol ran between 12% and 48% and sits at 30% today, the IV rank is `(30 − 12) / (48 − 12) = 50%` — exactly halfway up its annual range. An IV rank near 100% means implied vol is near its yearly high (options are expensive relative to their own history, lean toward selling); near 0% means near its yearly low (options are cheap, lean toward buying). **IV percentile** is a close cousin that asks instead "what fraction of days over the last year had a *lower* implied vol than today?" — it is less sensitive to a single outlier spike than IV rank, because one freak VIX-of-80 day inflates the range and depresses every subsequent rank reading, whereas percentile just counts days.

Both metrics turn the abstract "is this expensive?" into a number between 0 and 100 that you can act on consistently. They are the bread and butter of how systematic premium-sellers decide *when* to put on a trade: many will only sell when IV rank is above some threshold (say 50), refusing to sell cheap vol just because the strategy is "sell vol."

The difference between the two is more than academic, and it bites in exactly the regimes that matter. Suppose a stock spent eleven months with implied vol pinned between 18% and 24%, then had one panic day where implied vol spiked to 70%. The one-year range is now 18% to 70%. Back at a normal 24%, IV rank reads `(24 − 18) / (70 − 18) = 12%` — it makes a perfectly ordinary 24% vol look *cheap*, because the lone 70% spike stretched the denominator. IV *percentile*, which just counts how many days were below today, would correctly report that 24% is near the *top* of the everyday distribution (the panic day was a single observation). The lesson: in markets with occasional violent spikes, IV rank is biased downward and will talk you out of selling vol that is genuinely rich, while IV percentile is the steadier read. Many desks watch both and treat a large divergence between them as a flag that a recent outlier is distorting the picture.

### The IV–RV spread

IV rank compares implied vol to *itself*. The IV–RV spread compares it to *reality*: subtract the recent realized vol from the current implied vol. If implied is 25% and the stock has been realizing 15%, the spread is +10 — implied is richly above what the stock is actually doing, a setup that favors sellers. If implied is 18% and the stock has been realizing 28% (it has been moving violently while options stayed cheap), the spread is −10 — implied is *below* realized, a rare and juicy setup that favors buyers, because you are paying for less vol than the stock is currently delivering.

The IV–RV spread is the most direct read on the core trade, because it lines up exactly the two quantities you are trading. The catch is that the realized vol you can measure is *trailing* — it tells you what the stock has done, not what it will do — so a low IV–RV spread is suggestive, not conclusive. A stock can realize 28% for a month and then go dead quiet; selling its "cheap" vol because trailing realized was high would have been a trap. You combine the spread with IV rank and with a view on whether the recent realized regime is likely to persist.

### The vol cone

A **vol cone** puts both ideas together visually across tenors. For each expiry tenor — ten days, one month, three months, a year — you compute the distribution of *realized* vol over many historical rolling windows of that length, and plot the minimum, the 25th percentile, the median, the 75th percentile, and the maximum. Plotted against tenor, these bands form a cone that narrows as the tenor lengthens, because long-window realized vol is an average of many days and therefore less variable than short-window realized vol, which can be dominated by a single wild day.

![Vol cone showing realized volatility percentile bands narrowing with tenor and today's implied vol curve sitting near the top of the cone](/imgs/blogs/implied-vs-realized-volatility-the-trade-at-the-heart-of-options-4.png)

The figure above shows the cone with today's implied-vol curve overlaid on top. The trade signal is geometric: where does today's implied vol sit relative to the historical realized cone for that tenor? When implied vol pokes up near the *top* of the cone — above the 75th percentile of what the stock has historically realized at that tenor — options are rich and you lean toward selling. When it sags toward the *bottom*, options are cheap and you lean toward buying. The cone is just IV-versus-RV and IV rank fused into one model you can read at a glance, tenor by tenor. The whole tactical mantra collapses to one line:

> **Sell vol when implied is rich versus its own history and versus realized; buy vol when it is cheap — and size every trade for the gap you cannot see coming.**

#### Worked example: the break-even move on a long straddle

Suppose you buy that one-month at-the-money straddle at the cheap 15% implied — paying about **\$3.44 per share** (call \$1.88 + put \$1.55). For the trade to break even at expiry, the stock has to finish far enough from the strike that one leg's intrinsic value covers the *whole* \$3.44 you paid for both legs. That means the stock must move at least **\$3.44 in either direction** — a **3.44% move** on a \$100 stock — by expiry, just to get your money back.

Now compare that to selling the same straddle at a rich 45% implied. There you collect about **\$10.27 per share**, and the stock would have to move more than **\$10.27 — a 10.27% move** — before the buyer who took the other side breaks even and you, the seller, start losing. The implied vol *is* the break-even move expressed as a price: a one-month straddle's premium is roughly the size of the move the market is charging for. **Buying a straddle is a bet that the realized move beats the break-even the premium implies; selling it is a bet that the move falls short — which is just the IV-versus-RV trade dressed up as a price level.**

## The break-even link to gamma and theta

There is a beautiful mechanical reason the IV-versus-RV trade resolves the way it does, and it lives in the two Greeks we covered earlier: gamma and theta. When you hold an option and hedge out its directional exposure (sell enough stock to neutralize delta), you are left with a clean vol bet, and that bet pays off through a daily tug-of-war between gamma gains and theta losses.

If you are **long** the option (long gamma, short theta), every day you collect a **gamma gain** proportional to the *square* of the stock's actual move — big moves help you a lot because of the squaring — and you pay a fixed **theta cost** for the day's time decay. The gamma post called this "renting convexity"; the theta post called it "paying rent on the clock." Put them together and the long position's daily profit and loss is, very roughly, `0.5 × gamma × (move)² − theta`. The position makes money on any day the move is large enough that the gamma gain exceeds the theta rent, and loses on quiet days when the rent exceeds the gain.

![Gamma profit and loss rising as a parabola crossing a flat theta cost at the break-even move of 1.12 dollars equal to 17.8 percent realized vol](/imgs/blogs/implied-vs-realized-volatility-the-trade-at-the-heart-of-options-6.png)

The figure above plots that tug-of-war for a delta-hedged 30-day at-the-money call at 20% implied vol. The green parabola is the daily gamma gain as a function of the size of the day's move; the amber dashed line is the flat daily theta cost. They cross at a break-even move of about **\$1.12** — a 1.12% daily move. Below that move the seller wins (you paid more theta than you collected in gamma); above it the long-gamma holder wins. And here is the punchline that ties the whole post together: convert that break-even *daily* move into an annualized vol — `1.12% × sqrt(252)` — and you get about **17.8%**, which is essentially the **20% implied vol you paid** (the small gap is carry and the discreteness of a one-day step). That is not a coincidence. The break-even realized vol for a delta-hedged option *is* its implied vol.

This is the deepest statement of the trade. **A delta-hedged long option breaks even exactly when realized vol equals implied vol.** Realize more than implied and the long-gamma scalper wins; realize less and the short-gamma seller wins. The gamma-versus-theta tug-of-war is the IV-versus-RV trade playing out one day at a time — which is precisely the mechanism behind [gamma scalping (E1)](/blog/trading/options-volatility/gamma-scalping-turning-a-long-straddle-into-a-vol-harvest), where a trader buys a straddle and harvests the realized vol by repeatedly re-hedging the delta, banking the gamma gains as long as the stock realizes more than the implied vol they paid.

#### Worked example: realized must beat implied for the long scalper

Take the delta-hedged 30-day at-the-money call on a \$100 stock at 20% implied. From the model, the gamma is about **0.0693 per share** and the daily theta decay is about **\$0.044 per share per day**. Set the daily gamma gain equal to the daily theta cost: `0.5 × 0.0693 × move² = 0.044`, which solves to a break-even daily move of about **\$1.12**.

If the stock realizes daily moves *bigger* than \$1.12 on average, the long scalper's gamma gains outrun theta and the position makes money. If the stock's daily moves come in *smaller* than \$1.12, theta eats the position and the seller on the other side wins. Annualize the \$1.12 break-even move and it maps to about 17.8% realized vol — essentially the 20% you paid in implied. **You bought 20% vol; you need the stock to deliver about 20% vol to break even; everything you realize above that is profit and everything below is loss — the gamma-theta machine is just the implied-versus-realized bet, settled daily.**

## Common misconceptions

The IV-versus-RV framing clears up a set of errors that cost beginners real money. Each one below is corrected with a number.

**"High implied volatility means the stock is going up (or down)."** No. Implied vol is *directionless* — it measures the expected *size* of the move, not its sign. A high implied vol says the market expects a big move *in either direction*; it says nothing about which way. A call and a put on the same strike have the *same* vega and rise together when implied vol rises. When earnings push a stock's implied vol from 25% to 60%, it is not a bullish or bearish signal — it is the market pricing a wider cone of outcomes around an uncertain event. Reading high IV as a directional tell is the single most common rookie error, and it is why people buy "cheap" calls into earnings and lose even when the stock rallies: they bought *direction* and got handed a *vol crush*. See the [event-volatility deep-dive](/blog/trading/event-trading/event-volatility-implied-vs-realized-and-the-vol-crush) for the full anatomy of that crush.

**"If I buy options when implied vol is low, I'm getting a good deal."** Only if realized vol comes in *higher* than the low implied you paid. Low implied vol is cheap *relative to history*, but it can still be *expensive relative to the future* if the stock then goes dead quiet. Recall our opening trader who bought the 15% straddle for \$3.44 and watched the stock realize only 10%, harvesting back the equivalent of just \$2.30 of movement — a bleed of about \$1.14 per share. The vol was low and it stayed low. "Cheap" is meaningless without a view on what realizes; the only thing that makes implied vol genuinely cheap is realized vol exceeding it.

**"Selling options is free money because implied is usually above realized."** The +3.7 variance risk premium is real, but it is the *average* outcome, not the *every-time* outcome. The distribution of a vol seller's profit and loss is steady small gains punctuated by rare catastrophic losses. Our worked example showed a seller harvesting about \$10.18 per share over a calm year — and a single month where the stock gaps and realized vol blows through implied can hand back most of that in days. The +3.7 is a *wage for risk*, and the risk is precisely the tail that the average conceals. "Sell vol, collect premium, repeat" is a strategy that works until the afternoon it catastrophically doesn't.

**"Implied volatility is the market's forecast of realized volatility."** Close, but the gap matters enormously. Implied vol is a forecast *plus a risk premium* — a price, not a pure prediction. If it were a pure forecast it would be right on average and the +3.7 gap would not exist. The reason implied systematically exceeds realized is that the price includes compensation for bearing crash risk on top of any genuine forecast. Treating implied vol as an unbiased forecast of realized vol leads you to think the market is "wrong" every time implied exceeds realized — when in fact it is *supposed* to, by about 3.7 points on average, as payment for insurance.

**"Realized volatility is a single, objective number."** It depends on the estimator and the window. Close-to-close, Parkinson, and Garman-Klass can give meaningfully different figures for the same stock over the same period — range-based estimators are more efficient but blind to overnight gaps, while close-to-close captures gaps but throws away the intraday range. A stock that gaps every morning and grinds flat all day will look calm to Parkinson and wild to close-to-close. When someone quotes "the realized vol," ask *which* realized vol; the IV–RV spread you compute depends on the answer. The window matters just as much as the estimator: a 10-day realized vol reacts instantly to a single wild session and decays it out ten days later, while a 60-day realized vol barely flinches at the same session but takes two months to forget it. Comparing a 30-day implied vol to a 10-day realized is apples to oranges — match the realized window to the option's tenor, or you will manufacture an IV–RV spread that is an artifact of mismatched horizons rather than a real edge.

## How it shows up in real markets

The IV-versus-RV trade is not a textbook abstraction; it is the daily reality of every options desk, and it shows up in a few recognizable patterns.

**Earnings and the vol crush.** The cleanest, most repeatable instance. In the days before a company reports, implied vol on its options balloons as traders bid up protection and lottery tickets around the binary event. The morning after the report, the uncertainty resolves and implied vol *collapses* — the "vol crush." The realized move on the announcement is usually *smaller* than the implied vol charged for it, which is why systematically *selling* the inflated pre-earnings vol (with defined risk) is a known premium-harvesting strategy. Our second opening trader lived this exactly: sold a 45% straddle for \$4.97 the day before earnings, bought it back at 20% for \$2.05 the morning after with the stock flat, and pocketed \$2.92 — the entire profit coming from the implied vol being higher than the realized move that printed. The [expected-move post](/blog/trading/event-trading/the-expected-move-pricing-event-risk-with-options) shows how to read the straddle price as the market's implied move and compare it to history.

**The VIX and the long-run premium.** The VIX index is, in effect, the S&P 500's 30-day implied vol, and its long-run average sits around 19.5 — meaningfully above the roughly 15.8 average realized vol that has followed. That structural gap is the index-level variance risk premium, and it is the engine behind an entire industry of systematic vol-selling: covered-call funds, put-write strategies, short-VIX-futures products, and "volatility as an asset class" allocations all monetize the +3.7 in one form or another. The [volatility-as-an-asset post](/blog/trading/cross-asset/volatility-as-an-asset-owning-fear) walks through how those products turn the premium into a return stream — and how they periodically detonate.

**Crash episodes — when the premium gets paid back.** The other side of the structural edge is the rare day the seller's nightmare arrives. In the curated record of VIX spikes, the index closed at 80.86 at the GFC peak in November 2008 and 82.69 at the COVID peak in March 2020 — days when realized vol exploded far through any implied vol that had been sold beforehand, and short-vol positions that had been quietly collecting the +3.7 for years gave it all back and then some. February 2018's "Volmageddon" was a concentrated version: the VIX more than doubled in a day, and a clutch of short-vol exchange-traded products lost the overwhelming majority of their value essentially overnight. These episodes are not anomalies that break the VRP thesis — they *are* the VRP thesis. The premium exists *because* these days exist; sellers are paid the +3.7 in calm times precisely to be on the hook in these moments.

**Index versus single-stock.** The variance risk premium is generally richer and steadier in *index* options than in single names, because index implied vol carries an extra premium for correlation risk — the fear that in a crash everything falls together. Single-stock realized vol can also spike idiosyncratically on company-specific news that no index move predicts. Practitioners often *sell* index vol (harvesting the steady correlation-rich premium with defined risk) while being more selective in single names, where the IV–RV relationship is noisier and a single earnings miss can blow through the premium.

**The shape of vol across tenors carries its own signal.** Implied vol is not one number; it is a *term structure* — a different implied vol for each expiry. In calm markets the structure is in **contango**, sloping gently upward: a representative quiet day might show one-month implied around 14.5, two-month around 15.5, rising to about 17.6 by six months. The front is cheap because the next month looks calm; the back is dearer because uncertainty compounds over longer horizons and the long end mean-reverts toward the long-run average. In a panic the structure *inverts* into **backwardation**: front-month implied spikes far above the back, because the fear is *right now*. A stressed day might show one-month implied at 38 collapsing to 24 by six months — the market is screaming about the next few weeks while expecting the storm to pass. The slope itself is a trade: when the front is unusually cheap relative to the back (steep contango), the realized vol that the front will face is often closer to the back's level, making front-month options a cheap long; when the front is screaming above the back (backwardation), the front is usually overpaying for near-term fear, favoring sellers of front-month vol against longer-dated hedges. The [volatility surface post](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/volatility-surface) treats the full surface — vol across *both* strike and tenor — as a single no-arbitrage object.

## The playbook

Here is how to actually trade the implied-versus-realized spread, end to end.

![Decision map reading implied vol against its history and realized vol, branching to sell when rich and buy when cheap, both routing through a crash-risk and sizing node](/imgs/blogs/implied-vs-realized-volatility-the-trade-at-the-heart-of-options-7.png)

**The read.** Before any trade, measure three things. First, **IV rank or IV percentile** — where is implied vol in its own one-year range? Second, the **IV–RV spread** — is implied above or below the realized vol the stock has actually been delivering? Third, the **vol cone** — where does today's implied vol sit relative to the historical realized distribution for the tenor you are trading? The decision map above fuses these into a single flow: read implied vol, judge whether it is rich or cheap, and branch accordingly — with both branches routing through a mandatory crash-risk and sizing check before you put anything on.

To make that concrete, here is the read as a numeric checklist. Pull today's 30-day implied vol — call it 35%. Pull the trailing 20-day realized vol — say 22%. The **IV–RV spread is +13**, comfortably positive: the market is charging well above what the stock has lately delivered. Now pull the **IV rank**: the year's implied ranged 18% to 46%, so `(35 − 18) / (46 − 18) = 61%` — implied is in the upper third of its own range. Cross-check **IV percentile** to make sure no single spike is distorting the rank; suppose it reads 68%, consistent with the rank, so no outlier flag. Finally place 35% on the **vol cone** for the 30-day tenor: if the historical 75th percentile of 30-day realized vol is around 28%, then 35% implied sits *above* the cone's upper band — rich by every measure. All four readings agree: implied is expensive versus its own history *and* versus reality. That is a sell-vol setup with conviction. When the four disagree — high IV rank but a *negative* IV–RV spread, say, because the stock is realizing more than the elevated implied — you have no clean edge and the right move is to pass. **The discipline is to require agreement across the readings before you commit; a single rich-looking metric is a coincidence, not a trade.**

**When implied is rich (high IV rank, IV above RV): lean short vol.** Sell premium — but with *defined risk*, not naked. Prefer a short spread (a sold straddle hedged with wings into an iron condor, or a credit spread) over a naked short option, because the naked option is the position that hands back years of the +3.7 in one gap. You are now short vega, short gamma, long theta: you profit as time passes and the stock realizes less than the implied you sold. Your invalidation is a realized-vol regime change — if the stock starts realizing well above the implied you sold, the trade is wrong and you cut it; do not "average down" into a vol expansion.

**When implied is cheap (low IV rank, IV near or below RV): lean long vol.** Buy premium — a long straddle or a debit spread — and plan to *harvest* the realized vol rather than wait for expiry, by delta-hedging and [gamma scalping (E1)](/blog/trading/options-volatility/gamma-scalping-turning-a-long-straddle-into-a-vol-harvest) the moves as they come. You are long vega, long gamma, short theta: you profit if the stock realizes more than the cheap implied you paid. Your invalidation is the clock: if the stock stays dead quiet, theta grinds you down, and a long-vol trade that hasn't worked by the time decay accelerates (the last third of the option's life) is usually a trade to close.

**Sizing and the caveat.** Whatever side you take, size for the gap you *cannot* see in the cone. The +3.7 variance risk premium is compensation for crash risk, which means the short-vol side carries a fat left tail that no IV rank will warn you about in advance. Cap the loss explicitly — buy the wing, define the spread, size the position so a limit-down day is survivable rather than fatal. The whole edge of vol selling is real and worth harvesting; it is also the edge most likely to be confused for skill right up until the day it isn't. For the deeper treatment of why selling vol pays until it doesn't, and how to structure the harvest to survive the tail, see the [variance risk premium post (C6)](/blog/trading/options-volatility/the-variance-risk-premium-why-selling-vol-pays-until-it-doesnt); for *why* different strikes carry different implied vols — and why the puts you buy as crash protection cost more — see the [volatility smile and skew post (C2)](/blog/trading/options-volatility/the-volatility-smile-and-skew-why-otm-puts-cost-more).

**The one rule.** Strip everything else away and the trade is this: *implied vol is the price, realized vol is the outcome, and you make money trading the spread between them.* Sell when the price is rich versus history and versus reality; buy when it is cheap; and never forget the +3.7 is the market paying you to hold a grenade — so always know where the pin is.
