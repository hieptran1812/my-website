> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — An option is not a leveraged bet on direction; it is a bet on *how much* a stock moves (volatility) before a *deadline* (time), and you survive by managing the Greeks. You buy a right, not an obligation, and you pay a premium for the privilege.
>
> - A **call** is the right to *buy* at a fixed price by a deadline; a **put** is the right to *sell*. The buyer pays a premium and can walk away; the seller collects the premium and is on the hook.
> - The buyer's payoff is **asymmetric**: loss is capped at the premium, while the upside can be many multiples of it. That convexity — small fixed cost, large variable payoff — is the entire reason options exist.
> - Options do three jobs: **insurance** (hedge a position), **leverage** (a defined-risk directional bet), and **income** (sell premium to collect cash for taking risk).
> - The one rule to remember: **you can be completely right on direction and still lose money** if volatility falls or time runs out. That sentence is the whole series.

On the afternoon of a recent earnings report, thousands of retail traders bought call options on a popular tech stock. The logic felt airtight: the company was about to report blowout numbers, the stock would jump, and a cheap call would multiply. The numbers came out, they *were* a blowout, and the stock opened the next morning up almost 3%. The call buyers had been right about everything — the direction, the catalyst, the size of the move. And many of them still lost money.

Here is how that happens. The night before earnings, the option's price was swollen with **implied volatility** — the market's nervous, expensive guess at how far the stock might lurch. A 14-day at-the-money call that would normally cost around \$2.50 was trading near \$4.76, because the implied volatility baked into it was something like 60% annualized instead of the usual 20%. The moment the report dropped and the uncertainty resolved, that implied volatility collapsed back toward 25%. The 3% pop in the stock added value to the call; the vol collapse subtracted more. Net of the two, the call that cost \$4.76 was worth about \$3.88 the next morning. The trader was right and lost roughly 18% of their money. Traders have a name for this: the **vol crush**. It is the single most expensive lesson in options, and almost everybody pays for it at least once.

That story is the reason this series exists, and it is the reason its first sentence is the most important one you will read: **an option is a bet on volatility and time, not just direction.** Once that clicks, the rest of the subject — the Greeks, the vol surface, the structures with names like "iron condor" and "calendar spread" — stops being a bag of tricks and becomes a single coherent thing: you are trading the price of risk. This post builds the whole foundation from zero. No finance background assumed; every term defined on first use. By the end you will know exactly what a call and a put are, why their payoff is shaped the way it is, the three reasons anyone trades them, and why the price moves with volatility and time. We will *not* re-derive the pricing math here — that lives in the companion posts ([Black-Scholes](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/black-scholes), [risk-neutral pricing](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/risk-neutral-pricing-martingale-measure-quant-interviews)) — because this series owns the *practice*: reading the Greeks live, building positions, trading volatility, and not blowing up.

![Diagram of an option contract showing buyer, seller, premium flow, the right, the obligation, strike, and expiry](/imgs/blogs/what-is-an-option-the-right-not-the-obligation-1.png)

Look at that figure for a moment before we go further, because it is the mental model the entire post hangs on. There are two parties. The buyer hands over a premium, once, up front, and in return holds a *right* — they may act, or not, entirely as they please. The seller pockets that premium and accepts the mirror-image *obligation* — if the buyer chooses to act, the seller must deliver. Off to the sides sit the two things that make this more interesting than a coin flip: at expiry the right is worth something only if it pays to exercise, and *before* expiry the premium lives and breathes, moving with volatility and the clock. Hold that picture. Everything below is detail on it.

## Foundations: what a call and a put actually are

Strip away the jargon and an option is a contract between two people about a future transaction at a price agreed today. The thing the contract is *about* — the stock, the index, the barrel of oil — is called the **underlying**. The fixed price written into the contract is the **strike**. The deadline is the **expiration** (or **expiry**). And the price the buyer pays the seller to enter the contract is the **premium**.

There are exactly two basic kinds.

A **call option** gives its holder the **right to buy** the underlying at the strike, on or before expiry. You buy a call when you want exposure to the price going *up*. If you own a \$100-strike call and the stock rises to \$130, you can exercise: buy at \$100 (the strike), and the shares are instantly worth \$130. You have captured the \$30 difference. If instead the stock falls to \$80, you simply do nothing — you are not obligated to buy anything at \$100 when the market price is \$80. You let the call expire worthless and you are out only what you paid for it.

A **put option** gives its holder the **right to sell** the underlying at the strike, on or before expiry. You buy a put when you want to profit from, or protect against, the price going *down*. If you own a \$100-strike put and the stock falls to \$70, you can exercise: sell at \$100 even though the market is at \$70, capturing the \$30. If the stock instead rallies to \$120, you do nothing — why sell at \$100 when you can sell in the market at \$120? — and the put expires worthless.

The two words that do all the work are **right** and **obligation**. The buyer of an option holds the right and has no obligation: they will exercise only when it benefits them and walk away otherwise. The **seller** (also called the **writer**) of that same option has the obligation: they collected the premium up front, and in exchange they are bound to honour the contract if the buyer exercises. The seller of a call must sell the stock to the buyer at the strike, however high the market has gone. The seller of a put must buy the stock from the buyer at the strike, however far the market has fallen.

A few more terms you will see everywhere, defined now and used for the rest of the series:

- **In the money (ITM):** the option would have positive value if exercised right now. A call is ITM when the stock is above the strike; a put is ITM when the stock is below the strike.
- **Out of the money (OTM):** the option would be worthless if exercised now. A call is OTM below the strike; a put is OTM above it.
- **At the money (ATM):** the stock is sitting right at the strike.
- **Intrinsic value:** the part of the premium you would capture by exercising immediately — for a call, the stock price minus the strike (floored at zero); for a put, the strike minus the stock price (floored at zero).
- **Time value (or extrinsic value):** everything in the premium *above* intrinsic value. It is the market charging you for the possibility that the option moves further into the money before expiry. Time value is where volatility and the clock live, and it is the single most misunderstood part of an option.

One contract on a US-listed equity option almost always controls **100 shares** of the underlying. So a premium quoted as "\$4.49" is per share; the actual cash that changes hands for one contract is \$4.49 × 100 = \$449. We will quote per-share premiums in the worked examples (that is how they are screened and priced) and remind you of the ×100 whenever the dollar total matters.

It helps to see where these contracts came from, because the history explains the shape. Options are ancient — there are records of Thales of Miletus reportedly buying the rights to olive presses ahead of a harvest, and of options on tulip bulbs in 1630s Amsterdam — but the modern, standardized, exchange-traded option dates to 1973, when the Chicago Board Options Exchange opened and, in the same year, Fischer Black, Myron Scholes, and Robert Merton published the pricing model that made a liquid market possible. Standardization is the quiet hero: before the CBOE, an option was a bespoke handshake between two parties, hard to price and harder to sell on. Once strikes, expiries, and contract sizes were standardized and centrally cleared, options became a tradable instrument with a continuous price — and that price, set by supply and demand against the model, is what carries the implied-volatility information this whole series is about. When you buy a call today, you are not negotiating with one farmer; you are lifting an offer in a deep market whose price encodes the crowd's expectation of future movement.

The other foundational idea is what an option is *not*. It is not a share of the company; it confers no ownership, no vote, no dividend (this is why dividends complicate call pricing — the call holder does not receive them, so a looming dividend lowers a call's value and raises a put's). It is a *derivative*: its value is derived entirely from the behaviour of the underlying. That derivation is the reason a \$2.39 call can give you exposure to \$100 of stock — you are renting the price movement, not buying the asset — and it is also the reason your entire premium can go to zero while the stock barely moves. You own a claim on movement, with an expiry date stamped on it.

#### Worked example: pricing a fair call and reading its parts

Take a stock trading at \$100. We want a 3-month (T = 0.25 years) call struck at \$100, in a market where the stock's annualized volatility is 20% and the risk-free rate is 4%. Feeding those into the standard option-pricing model gives a fair premium of about **\$4.49** per share.

Now decompose it. The stock is at \$100 and the strike is \$100, so the **intrinsic value is \$0** — exercising right now buys at \$100 something worth \$100, a wash. That means the *entire* \$4.49 is **time value**. You are paying \$4.49 per share, or \$449 for the contract, purely for the chance that the stock travels far enough above \$100 over the next three months to be worth it. If the stock never moves at all and just sits at \$100 until expiry, that \$4.49 melts to zero and you lose every cent of it — even though you were never "wrong" about direction. *Intuition: an at-the-money option is 100% a bet on movement; if nothing moves, the buyer loses by default.*

## The asymmetric payoff, and why convexity is the whole point

Now we draw the picture that defines the entire field. Put the stock's price at expiry on the horizontal axis and the buyer's profit or loss on the vertical axis. For a call, the result is a hockey stick.

![Long 100-strike call payoff diagram at expiry showing capped loss and rising upside](/imgs/blogs/what-is-an-option-the-right-not-the-obligation-2.png)

Read it left to right. Below the strike of \$100, the call expires worthless and the buyer loses the whole premium — but *only* the premium. The line is flat at −\$4.49 no matter how far the stock falls. Whether the stock closes at \$95 or at \$5, the call buyer's loss is identical: the \$4.49 they paid. That flat floor is the **capped loss**, and it is the feature people fall in love with.

Above the strike, the option starts paying. At the strike the line begins to climb, dollar for dollar with the stock. It crosses zero — the **breakeven** — at \$104.49, which is the strike plus the premium (\$100 + \$4.49). Below breakeven the call buyer is still down; above it, they are in profit, and that profit grows without any ceiling. If the stock goes to \$130 the call is worth \$30 of intrinsic value, against a \$4.49 cost, for a profit of \$25.51 per share. If it goes to \$150, profit is \$45.51. There is no upper bound.

That shape — a fixed, known maximum loss and an open-ended, unbounded gain — is called **convexity**, and it is the reason options are not just "leveraged stock." A leveraged stock position loses faster as the stock falls and gains faster as it rises, symmetrically. An option *bends*: it refuses to lose more than the premium on the downside while keeping the full upside. You have paid for that bend. The premium is the price of convexity.

It is worth dwelling on why convexity is so valuable, because it is the single most important property in derivatives and it is genuinely counterintuitive. With a symmetric instrument like stock, a sequence of equal-sized up and down moves roughly cancels — up 10% then down 10% leaves you slightly below where you started. With a convex instrument, the asymmetry means big moves are *good for you regardless of direction* once you strip out the cost: the upside contribution from a large rise exceeds the downside contribution from an equally large fall, because the fall is capped. This is why option holders are said to be "long volatility": they profit from large moves and from the *uncertainty* of moves, not just from being right about direction. The flip side is that this beautiful property is not free — the market charges you the time-value premium for it, and that premium decays every day. Convexity you bought too dear, or held too long, is convexity that loses money. The entire craft of buying options is acquiring convexity when it is cheap relative to the movement that follows.

The put is the mirror image, sloping the other way.

![Long 100-strike put payoff diagram at expiry showing profit as the stock falls](/imgs/blogs/what-is-an-option-the-right-not-the-obligation-3.png)

A \$100-strike put bought for a fair premium of about **\$3.49** loses that \$3.49 and no more if the stock stays above \$100 (the flat floor on the right of the chart). Below \$100 it gains as the stock falls. Its breakeven is the strike *minus* the premium, \$100 − \$3.49 = \$96.51, and its maximum gain is capped only because a stock cannot fall below zero: if the stock went all the way to \$0, the put would be worth \$100 of intrinsic value, for a profit of \$96.51. Same convex bend, pointed downward.

(Notice the put's fair premium, \$3.49, is *lower* than the matching call's \$4.49 even though both are at-the-money with the same volatility and time. That gap is not arbitrary — it is exactly the cost of carrying the position at the 4% interest rate, and the precise relationship between a call's price and a put's price at the same strike is a no-arbitrage identity called **put-call parity**. We will lean on it constantly; the full proof lives in the [put-call parity post](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/put-call-parity-no-arbitrage-quant-interviews) so we will not re-derive it here.)

#### Worked example: splitting a premium into intrinsic and time value across moneyness

The hockey-stick payoff is the option's value *at expiry*. Before expiry the value is a smooth curve that sits *above* the kinked payoff, and the gap between them is time value. Pin this down with three calls on the same \$100 stock, all 3-month, all 20% IV. An **in-the-money** \$90 call is worth about \$11.5: of that, \$10 is intrinsic (the stock at \$100 is \$10 above the \$90 strike) and only about \$1.5 is time value. An **at-the-money** \$100 call is worth \$4.49, of which \$0 is intrinsic and the entire \$4.49 is time value. An **out-of-the-money** \$110 call is worth about \$1.1, of which \$0 is intrinsic and all \$1.1 is time value.

Two lessons fall out. First, time value is largest, in dollar terms, at the money — that is where the most uncertainty about the outcome lives, so that is where the option is most a pure bet on movement. Second, the OTM option is "cheap" precisely because it is *all* time value with low odds of becoming intrinsic; it is the purest play on volatility and the fastest to decay to nothing. *Intuition: an option's premium is intrinsic value (what you would get exercising now) plus a time-value premium for future movement, and the at-the-money option is the one made almost entirely of that movement bet.*

#### Worked example: the insurance vs lottery framing, done honestly

People reach for two analogies for option buying, and both are *half* right, which is exactly why they mislead.

The **insurance** analogy fits a put. Suppose you own 100 shares of a \$100 stock — \$10,000 of exposure — and you are nervous about a drop. You buy one \$95-strike put for a fair premium of about **\$1.60** per share, so \$160 for the contract. That put is a deductible insurance policy. If the stock crashes to \$70, your shares lost \$3,000, but the put is now worth \$25 per share (\$95 strike − \$70 market), or \$2,500, clawing back almost all of it. Your net loss is capped: shares down \$30, put up \$25, minus the \$1.60 you paid, for a worst realistic loss of about \$6.60 per share below \$95 instead of an open-ended fall. Like real insurance, you paid a premium you hope to "waste," and you are perfectly happy if the stock rises and the put expires worthless. *Intuition: a long put is a deductible — you pay \$160 so a \$3,000 loss becomes a roughly \$660 loss.*

The **lottery** analogy fits a far-out-of-the-money call. Pay a tiny premium for a strike well above the current price, and most of the time you lose the whole (small) ticket — but occasionally a big move pays many multiples. The honest part the lottery framing *hides* is the base rate. A lottery ticket has a transparent, fixed, terrible expected value. An OTM call's expected value depends on whether the implied volatility you paid was higher or lower than the volatility that actually shows up — and on average, as we will see, implied volatility is priced *above* realized, which means the buyer is, on average, slightly overpaying. The convexity is real; the "free money" is not. Honest version: **a long option is convex insurance or a convex lottery ticket whose price you should always check against the volatility you expect.**

## The two sides of every contract: long, short, and zero-sum

Every option contract has two people on it. For every buyer there is a seller; for every right there is a matching obligation. This is not a detail — it is the structure of the market, and it is where a beginner's intuition usually breaks.

When you **buy** an option you are **long** it. You paid the premium; you hold the right; your loss is capped at the premium and your gain is open-ended (for a call) or large (for a put). When you **sell** (or **write**) an option you are **short** it. You *received* the premium; you hold the obligation; your gain is capped at the premium you collected and your loss can be enormous. The short call seller, in particular, has theoretically unlimited loss: if the stock rockets, they must keep selling it at the strike no matter how high it goes.

Put the two sides of the same call on one chart and the relationship is exact.

![Buyer versus seller profit and loss for the same call option shown as mirror images](/imgs/blogs/what-is-an-option-the-right-not-the-obligation-5.png)

The blue line is the buyer; the red line is the seller. They are perfect reflections across the horizontal axis. At every possible stock price, the dollar the buyer makes is precisely the dollar the seller loses, and vice versa. The seller's best case is the flat ceiling on the left: keep the entire \$4.49 premium when the call expires worthless. The buyer's worst case is the flat floor: lose exactly that same \$4.49. Past the \$104.49 breakeven the lines diverge — the buyer climbs into profit, the seller sinks into loss — and they do so by identical amounts.

This makes options, gross of fees, a **zero-sum** game. Not negative-sum like a casino and not positive-sum like the broad stock market over decades; literally zero-sum. The total profit across both sides of any option contract is exactly zero before transaction costs, and slightly negative after you pay the bid-ask spread and commissions. This has a sharp implication: **there is no structurally "winning side."** Buyers do not beat sellers, or sellers buyers, as a class. What determines who wins is whether the premium that changed hands was too high or too low relative to what actually happened — which is, once more, a question about volatility.

#### Worked example: the short put as the seller's mirror

Suppose you are on the other side and you *sell* a 30-day \$100-strike put on that \$100 stock, collecting a premium of about **\$2.12** per share, or \$212 for the contract. You now have the obligation: if the stock is below \$100 at expiry, the buyer will exercise and you must buy 100 shares at \$100.

Three outcomes. If the stock finishes at \$100 or above, the put expires worthless, you keep the full \$212, and your annualized return on the cash you set aside is attractive — this is the income trade. If the stock finishes at \$98, the put buyer exercises; you buy at \$100 a stock worth \$98, losing \$2 per share, but you keep the \$2.12 premium, so you net +\$0.12 — barely a winner. Your true breakeven is \$100 − \$2.12 = \$97.88. If the stock craters to \$80, you must buy at \$100 a stock worth \$80, a \$20 loss per share, against your \$2.12 premium, for a net loss of \$17.88 per share — \$1,788 on the contract. *Intuition: the put seller trades a capped \$212 gain for a tail risk of nearly \$10,000 if the stock goes to zero; they are paid to be the insurer, and like all insurers they are fine until the rare big claim.*

## Why options exist: insurance, leverage, and income

If options are zero-sum and the buyer is, on average, slightly overpaying, why does anyone trade them at all? Because the *shape* of the payoff does jobs that a plain stock position cannot. There are three of them, and almost every options trade in the world is one of these three wearing a costume.

![Tree diagram of the three reasons to use options: insurance, leverage, and income, each with a dollar example](/imgs/blogs/what-is-an-option-the-right-not-the-obligation-4.png)

**Insurance (hedging).** This is the original purpose of derivatives. A farmer locks in a price for next season's wheat; an airline locks in a price for jet fuel; a fund manager buys puts to cap the downside on a portfolio they want to keep holding. The defining feature is that you already have an exposure and you are paying a premium to cap its worst case. You *want* the insurance to expire worthless, the same way you want your house not to burn down. The protective put in the worked example above — \$160 to turn a potential \$3,000 loss into a roughly \$660 one — is the canonical hedge.

**Leverage (a defined-risk directional bet).** This is what draws most retail traders in, and it is genuinely powerful when respected. Instead of buying \$10,000 of stock to bet on a rise, you buy a call for a few hundred dollars. Your maximum loss is that premium — *defined* in advance, unlike margin or futures where a sharp move can wipe out more than you posted. If you are right and the move is big enough and fast enough, the percentage return dwarfs holding the stock. The trap, which we will hammer throughout the series, is that "big enough and fast enough" is doing enormous work in that sentence: the move has to beat the breakeven *and* arrive before time decay and any vol crush eat the premium.

**Income (selling premium).** This is the seller's business. You sell options — covered calls against stock you own, cash-secured puts, spreads — and collect the premium as income. You are now the insurer. You win the small premium most of the time and lose big occasionally, and your edge, if you have one, comes from the structural tendency of implied volatility to be priced above the volatility that actually shows up. This is a real, documented edge (the variance risk premium, below), but it is an edge you can blow up an entire account chasing if you size it wrong, because the losses are fat-tailed.

There is a fourth job, subtler than these three, that the professional side of the market does and that this series eventually reaches: **relative-value and volatility arbitrage.** Here the trader has no directional view at all and is not seeking income in the naive sense; they are betting that the *implied volatility* of one option is mispriced relative to another, or relative to the realized volatility they forecast. They might buy a cheap-vol option and sell an expensive-vol one, hedging away the directional exposure (the delta) so that only the volatility view remains. This is the purest expression of the series thesis — trading the gap between implied and realized vol as an isolated, hedged position — and it is where market-makers and vol funds live. We mention it now only so the spine is complete: insurance, leverage, income, and relative-value all reduce to the same underlying object, the price of risk, viewed from different angles.

#### Worked example: same view, three different option trades

You are mildly bullish on a \$100 stock over the next three months. Watch how the same view becomes three completely different risk profiles.

As **leverage**, you buy one \$105-strike call for a fair premium of about **\$2.39** per share (\$239 for the contract). Your max loss is \$239. Your breakeven is \$105 + \$2.39 = \$107.39. If the stock finishes at \$115, the call is worth \$10 intrinsic, a profit of \$7.61 per share, or \$761 — a **218% return** on the \$239 risked, versus a 15% return for someone who just held the stock from \$100 to \$115. That is the leverage. If the stock finishes anywhere below \$107.39, you lose money even though you were "right" that it went up.

As **insurance**, suppose you instead *own* the 100 shares and buy a \$95 put for \$160 to protect them. You have spent \$160 to cap your downside; your upside on the shares is untouched.

As **income**, you sell a 30-day \$100 put and collect \$212, betting the stock holds up and you keep the premium. Same bullish lean, but now your max gain is \$212 and your tail risk is large.

*Intuition: "I think it goes up" is not a trade. The structure — long call, protective put, or short put — decides whether you are paying for convexity, paying for protection, or selling both; the Greeks below tell you what you are really exposed to.*

## A first, gentle look at why price moves with volatility and time

We now arrive at the idea the whole series is built on, and we will reach it without a single formula. Why does an option's premium depend on volatility and time at all? Because an option's value comes from *uncertainty about the future*, and volatility and time are the two things that govern how much uncertainty there is.

Start with **time**. Picture two identical \$100-strike calls on the same \$100 stock. One expires tomorrow; one expires in a year. Which is worth more? Obviously the one-year call. It has eleven extra months of chances for the stock to climb above \$100. More time means more opportunity for a favourable move, and since the downside is capped at the premium either way, more opportunity is worth more money. Hence: **more time to expiry, higher premium.** And the flip side — the one that bites buyers — is that as the clock runs, that extra opportunity shrinks. Every day that passes with the stock sitting still, the option is worth a little less. This bleed is called **time decay**, and the Greek that measures it is **theta**. The option seller earns theta; the option buyer pays it. An option is, in part, a melting ice cube, and the buyer is holding it.

Now **volatility**. Picture two \$100-strike calls, same time to expiry, on two different \$100 stocks: one a sleepy utility that barely moves 1% a month, one a biotech that routinely swings 15% on news. Which call is worth more? The biotech's, by a lot. A wildly volatile stock is far more likely to make a big move above the strike — and again, the downside is capped at the premium no matter how wild the swings get. Volatility is *good* for the option holder precisely because of the asymmetry: big up moves pay off hugely, big down moves cost nothing extra beyond the premium already at risk. Hence: **higher expected volatility, higher premium.**

Here is the crucial twist that makes options a *market* rather than a calculation. The volatility that matters for the premium is not the volatility that has already happened (that is **realized volatility**, computed from past price moves). It is the volatility the market *expects* over the life of the option — and because that is an expectation about the future, it has a price, set by supply and demand. That priced-in expectation is **implied volatility (IV)**. When everyone is scared before an earnings report or a Fed meeting, demand for options spikes, IV rises, and premiums get expensive — exactly the swollen \$4.76 call from the opening story. When the event passes and uncertainty resolves, IV collapses and premiums deflate, regardless of which way the stock moved. That collapse is the vol crush.

This is the engine of the whole series, so let me state it as plainly as possible. You can be perfectly right about *direction* and still lose, because the premium you paid was loaded with implied volatility that then evaporated, or because time decay ground the option down before your move arrived. Conversely, an option seller can be *wrong* about direction and still win, if the implied volatility they sold was higher than the volatility that actually materialized. **You are not betting on where the stock goes. You are betting on how far it travels, relative to how far the market already charged you for.** That gap — implied volatility versus realized volatility — is where options traders make and lose money.

![Pipeline diagram of the series spine: direction is not enough, volatility, time, the implied versus realized edge, and the Greeks](/imgs/blogs/what-is-an-option-the-right-not-the-obligation-6.png)

That figure is the roadmap for everything that follows. Direction alone is not enough; volatility is the price; time bleeds the premium; the edge lives in the gap between implied and realized vol; and the Greeks are the dials that tell you what you are actually long or short. We will spend the series on each pillar. For the pricing machinery that connects them — how the model turns volatility, time, strike, and rate into a single premium — see the [Black-Scholes deep-dive](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/black-scholes) and the [options pricing fundamentals](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/options-theory) posts; we will use their outputs without re-deriving them.

#### Worked example: right on direction, wrong on the trade (the vol crush in numbers)

Return to the opening story and price it out. A stock is at \$100 the day before earnings. You buy a 14-day \$100-strike call. Because the market is bracing for a big report, the implied volatility priced into it is 60% (annualized), versus a normal 20%. At 60% IV, that 14-day call costs about **\$4.76** per share — nearly double its "calm" value — and \$476 for the contract.

Earnings come out, they are great, and the stock gaps up 3% to \$103. You were right. But the uncertainty is now resolved, so implied volatility collapses from 60% back to 25%. The next morning your call has 13 days left, the stock is at \$103, and IV is 25%. Repricing it gives about **\$3.88** per share. You paid \$4.76 and you can sell for \$3.88: a loss of \$0.88 per share, **−18%**, despite a correct, profitable-looking 3% move in the stock.

What happened to the \$0.88? The 3% rally added intrinsic and delta value to the call — that part helped. But the IV crush from 60% to 25% stripped out far more time value than the rally added, and one day of theta took a sliver on top. Net, the vol you bought (60%) was crushed below the vol that showed up in the price (the 3% move annualizes to far less than 60%), so you overpaid. *Intuition: buying an option into a known event means buying inflated implied volatility; unless the move beats what the IV already priced in, you lose even when you are right on direction.*

## The Greeks, in one breath: the dials of every position

The playbook below leans hard on a word we have used loosely — the **Greeks** — so let us define them properly now, at the level a beginner needs, and go deeper on each across the series. The Greeks are simply the *sensitivities* of an option's premium to the things that move it. Each one answers "if this changes by a little, how much does my premium change?" There are four that matter for survival, and you can read them off any broker's option screen.

**Delta** is sensitivity to the stock's price. It answers: if the stock moves up \$1, how much does my option's premium change? Delta runs from 0 to 1 for a long call and from 0 to −1 for a long put. A delta of 0.50 means the option gains about \$0.50 for every \$1 the stock rises — it behaves, locally, like holding half a share. Delta is also, conveniently, a rough estimate of the probability the option finishes in the money: a 0.50-delta call is roughly a coin-flip to expire ITM. Delta is your *directional* exposure, and it is the only Greek a beginner usually notices.

**Gamma** is the sensitivity of delta itself — the rate at which your directional exposure changes as the stock moves. A position with high gamma sees its delta swing fast: a long option's delta ramps toward 1 (or −1) as it goes in the money and fades toward 0 as it goes out. Gamma is highest for at-the-money options near expiry, which is why short-dated ATM options are so twitchy and why the "0DTE" (zero-days-to-expiry) trades you may have heard about are gamma bombs. Long options are *long gamma* (their convex curvature works for you); short options are *short gamma* (the curvature works against you). Gamma is why the seller's risk accelerates.

**Vega** is sensitivity to implied volatility. It answers: if IV rises by one volatility point, how much does my premium change? This is the Greek the vol crush lives in. A long option is *long vega* — it gains when IV rises and loses when IV falls — which is exactly why the earnings call lost money: its vega was large, and IV dropped 35 points. A short option is *short vega*, profiting when IV falls. When we say a trader is "long volatility" or "short volatility," vega is the Greek we mean.

**Theta** is sensitivity to the passage of time — the daily bleed of time value we met earlier. It answers: with everything else held still, how much does my premium fall as one day passes? Long options have *negative* theta (you pay it); short options have *positive* theta (you collect it). Theta and gamma are eternal enemies: the buyer pays theta to own gamma, and the seller collects theta in exchange for being short gamma. Most of options trading is a negotiation over that one trade-off.

#### Worked example: reading the four Greeks on one at-the-money call

Take our 3-month \$100-strike call at 20% IV, premium about \$4.49. Its Greeks, computed from the same pricing model, come out roughly as follows. **Delta ≈ 0.56:** the call gains about \$0.56 for each \$1 the stock rises, and is a touch better than a coin-flip to finish ITM. **Gamma ≈ 0.039:** for each \$1 the stock rises, that 0.56 delta itself climbs by about 0.04, so a \$5 rally lifts delta from 0.56 toward roughly 0.76 — the position gets more bullish as it works, which is the convexity again, in dial form. **Vega ≈ 0.20 per vol point:** if IV jumps from 20% to 21%, the premium rises about \$0.20 — and if IV instead fell from 60% to 25% as in the earnings trade, that 35-point drop alone would knock roughly \$7 off a similarly sized option, which is why the crush dominates. **Theta ≈ −\$0.027 per day:** the call bleeds about 2.7 cents of value every calendar day it sits still, accelerating as expiry nears.

Now read them as a dashboard. You are long delta (bullish), long gamma (you get more bullish as it rises and less bearish as it falls — good), long vega (you want IV to rise or hold), and short theta (time is your enemy, about \$0.027/day). That single line — *long delta, long gamma, long vega, short theta* — is what you are *actually* holding, and it explains every way the trade can win or lose far better than "I bought a call because I'm bullish." *Intuition: the Greeks turn a vague directional hunch into an explicit four-part risk position you can monitor, hedge, and size.*

## A few mechanics that trip up beginners

Before the misconceptions, three pieces of plumbing that are not glamorous but cause real losses when ignored.

**American vs European exercise.** Most US equity options are **American-style**: the holder may exercise any time up to expiry. Most index options (and all the textbook math) are **European-style**: exercise only *at* expiry. In practice, early exercise of a call is almost never optimal — you would throw away the remaining time value — so the distinction matters mostly around dividends (where exercising a call early to capture a dividend can occasionally pay) and for short put sellers, who can be assigned early. For trading intuition, treat options as European; for risk management of a short book, remember assignment can land before expiry.

**Assignment is the seller's surprise.** When an option buyer exercises, an exchange process randomly **assigns** the obligation to one of the sellers. If you are short a put and the stock is below your strike near expiry, you may wake up owning 100 shares per contract at the strike price, with the cash debited from your account. This is not a glitch; it is the obligation you sold, arriving. The income trader has to be ready — in cash and in temperament — to take delivery, which is why the responsible version of the trade is the *cash-secured* put: you hold enough cash to buy the shares if assigned.

**Settlement and the multiplier.** Equity options settle into shares (you get or deliver 100 shares per contract); many index options are *cash-settled* (the difference is paid in cash, no shares change hands). And every premium and Greek you read is per share — multiply by the 100-share contract multiplier to get the dollars actually at stake. A \$4.49 premium is \$449 of cash; a 0.56 delta is really 56 share-equivalents per contract. Forgetting the ×100 is how beginners accidentally put on positions ten times larger than they intended.

## Common misconceptions

Five beliefs that sound reasonable, cost beginners real money, and each break against a number.

**"Buying calls is cheap leverage on a stock I like."** Cheap in dollars, expensive in odds. A long call has to clear *three* hurdles the stock does not: the move must be big enough to beat the breakeven, it must arrive before time decay erodes the premium, and it must outrun any drop in implied volatility. Recall the \$105 call for \$2.39: the stock has to rise past \$107.39 — a **7.4% move in three months** — *just to break even*, while the stockholder is already in profit at \$100.01. Calls are not cheap stock; they are a wager that the stock moves more, and sooner, than the market's priced-in expectation.

**"Out-of-the-money options are basically free."** They are *cheap*, which is not the same as free or fair. A far-OTM call costs little because the model thinks it will probably expire worthless — and most of the time the model is right and you lose 100% of the (small) premium. The honest test is not the dollar price; it is whether the implied volatility embedded in that cheap option is higher or lower than the volatility you actually expect. A \$0.30 lottery call on 60% IV before earnings is *expensive* on the only axis that matters, even though \$0.30 sounds like nothing.

**"The option seller has unlimited risk, so selling is reckless and buying is safe."** The seller's tail risk is real and must be respected, but framed as "always reckless" it is simply wrong. The *probabilities* run the other way: the seller wins the small premium the majority of the time, while the buyer of an OTM option loses the whole premium the majority of the time. On the data, the structural edge (the variance risk premium below) sits with the disciplined, well-sized seller — which is why "selling is reckless, buying is safe" is exactly backwards as a blanket statement. Neither side is free money; the seller's job is sizing the tail, the buyer's job is not overpaying for vol.

**"If the stock goes my way, my option makes money."** Only if it goes your way *enough*, *fast enough*, and *while IV holds up*. The opening worked example is the entire rebuttal: a correct 3% rally produced an 18% loss because implied volatility crushed and a day of theta passed. Direction is necessary but nowhere near sufficient. Write it on your monitor: a right call on direction is a losing trade if you overpaid for volatility.

**"Implied volatility is a forecast of where the stock is going."** It is not a directional forecast at all. Implied volatility is the market's expectation of how *far* the stock will travel — its expected magnitude of movement, up or down combined — not which way. A 60% IV says "the market expects big swings," not "the market expects a rise." You can be long volatility (you profit if the stock moves a lot in *either* direction) and have no directional view whatsoever; that is exactly what a straddle is, and it is one of the first structures we build later in the series.

## How it shows up in real markets

The abstractions above are not academic. They are visible in market data, in named events, and in the structural pricing of options every single day.

**Volatility is a real, tradable quantity — and it moves a lot.** The clearest evidence is the **VIX**, the CBOE index that distills the implied volatility of S&P 500 options into a single annualized number, often called the market's "fear gauge." It is not a price you can hold directly, but it is computed from real option premiums, and its history makes the case that volatility is a thing in its own right.

![Bar chart of average VIX by year from 2004 to 2024 with the long-run average marked](/imgs/blogs/what-is-an-option-the-right-not-the-obligation-7.png)

The annual averages tell the story. In the calm years — 2017 averaged about **11**, 2013 and 2014 about **14** — options were cheap because little movement was expected, and option sellers were getting thin premiums. In crisis years the gauge exploded: 2008 averaged near **33** during the global financial crisis, and 2020 averaged about **29** through the COVID shock, with single-day closes spiking to **82.69** in March 2020 and **80.86** in November 2008. The long-run average sits around **19.5**. A trader who is "long volatility" wants this number to rise; one who is "short volatility" wants it to fall or stay low. Entire strategies, products, and blowups (the February 2018 "Volmageddon," when an inverse-VIX product lost about 96% in a day) live on this single axis. *Source: CBOE VIX, annual average of daily close.*

**The variance risk premium is the seller's structural edge.** Across decades, S&P 500 one-month implied volatility has, on average, printed *above* the realized volatility that subsequently shows up — a long-run gap of roughly **+3 to +4 vol points** (implied around 19.5, subsequent realized around 15.8). In plain terms: option buyers, as a group, slightly overpay, and option sellers, as a group, are slightly overpaid. This is the **variance risk premium (VRP)**, and it is the closest thing to a free lunch in the options world — except it is not free, because it is compensation for bearing crash risk. Sellers collect a small, steady edge for years and then occasionally hand a chunk back in a vol spike. It is an insurance business, with insurance-business economics. We devote a whole strand of the series to harvesting it without going broke; for the macro view of vol as something you can own, see [volatility as an asset](/blog/trading/cross-asset/volatility-as-an-asset-owning-fear).

**The vol crush is a calendar event you can see coming.** Implied volatility predictably ramps into known catalysts — earnings, central-bank meetings, drug-trial readouts, major economic prints — and predictably collapses the moment the news lands. This is so regular that the *expected move* an option chain is pricing for an event can be read straight off the premiums, and the post-event IV crush is one of the most reliable patterns in the market. It is why naive "buy a call before earnings" trades bleed out: you are systematically buying the most expensive volatility of the cycle. The mechanics of reading that expected move, and of the crush itself, get their own dedicated posts: [the expected move](/blog/trading/event-trading/the-expected-move-pricing-event-risk-with-options) and [implied vs realized and the vol crush](/blog/trading/event-trading/event-volatility-implied-vs-realized-and-the-vol-crush).

**Selling volatility blows up when realized beats implied.** The flip side of the variance risk premium is its tail, and it has a date: 5 February 2018, remembered as "Volmageddon." For years, traders had earned the VRP by being short volatility, including through exchange-traded products that were effectively short VIX futures. The trade had worked so reliably — collecting that 3-to-4-point edge month after month — that it had become crowded and badly sized. On 5 February, the S&P 500 fell about 4% and the VIX more than doubled in a day, closing at 37.32 after years of teens-and-low-twenties readings. The realized move dwarfed the implied volatility that the short-vol crowd had sold. One popular inverse-VIX product lost roughly **96% of its value in a single session** and was shortly wound down. The lesson is exactly the seller's-mirror worked example, scaled to billions: the short-vol seller wins a small premium most of the time and is wiped out by the rare session where realized volatility blows past implied. The edge was real; the sizing was suicidal. *Source: CBOE VIX close, 2018-02-05.*

**Retail can move option markets — and pay for it.** The 2021 meme-stock episode showed the convexity and the vol dynamics at scale. Crowds bought enormous quantities of short-dated OTM calls; the dealers who sold those calls had to buy the underlying stock to hedge, mechanically pushing the price up (a feedback loop we will dissect under "dealer gamma" later in the series); implied volatility on those names exploded to triple-digit levels; and when the frenzy faded, the vol crush and time decay vaporized the late call buyers even when the stock was still elevated. Every theme in this post — convexity, leverage, the zero-sum two sides, IV, the crush, dealer hedging — was on display in a single, very public month.

#### Worked example: a year of harvesting the variance risk premium, and the day it bites

Suppose you sell one 30-day at-the-money put each month on a \$100 index proxy, collecting around \$2.12 in premium each time (\$212 per contract), in a market where implied volatility averages a touch above realized. In eleven quiet months out of twelve, the index drifts and you keep most of each premium — call it \$180 net after the occasional small assignment, so about \$1,980 banked over eleven months. That steady drip is the variance risk premium made concrete: you were paid for insuring others against moves that mostly did not come.

Then the twelfth month delivers a 12% drop. Your \$100 put is now \$12 in the money; you are assigned and effectively lose \$12 − \$2.12 = \$9.88 per share, \$988 on the contract. Tally the year: +\$1,980 from eleven months, −\$988 in the twelfth, for +\$992 net — still positive, *because you sold one contract at a time*. Now suppose you had been seduced by the easy money and sold ten contracts: the eleven good months bank \$19,800, but the bad month costs \$9,880, and a *worse* tail — a 25% crash — would cost roughly \$22,880 on ten contracts, more than the whole year's income. *Intuition: the VRP is a genuine edge, but it is insurance-company economics; the entire game is sizing so that the inevitable bad month is a dent, not a death.*

## The playbook: how to think and trade from here

This is the series intro, so the playbook is less "here is a trade" and more "here is the operating mentality every later post assumes." Internalize these and the rest of the series will feel like elaboration rather than a list of tricks.

**Always name the trade's job before you put it on.** Is this insurance (you hold an exposure and want to cap its downside), leverage (a defined-risk directional bet), or income (you are selling premium to collect the variance risk premium)? The same vague view — "I'm bullish" — becomes a long call, a protective put, or a short put, and those three have wildly different risk profiles. If you cannot say which job your trade is doing, you do not yet have a trade.

**Price the volatility, not just the direction.** Before buying any option, ask whether its implied volatility is high or low relative to the volatility you actually expect and relative to that name's own history. Buying into a known event means buying inflated IV; you are accepting the vol crush unless your move beats what is priced in. Selling into a known event means collecting that inflated IV, with the matching tail risk. The decision to buy or sell should hinge on the **implied-versus-realized** gap, not on your directional hunch alone.

**Respect time decay as a running cost.** A long option is a melting ice cube; you pay theta every day you hold it. That means long-premium trades need a *thesis with a deadline* — the move must arrive in time. If your view is "this drifts up over a year," options are usually the wrong, expensive vehicle; if it is "this gaps in the next two weeks," they can be the right one. The seller's mirror: theta is your income, and your enemy is the gap risk that can overwhelm a year of collected premium in a day.

**Size for the tail, not the average.** Sellers especially: the worked short-put example clears \$212 most of the time and risks \$1,788 (or worse) in the tail. Position sizing — not cleverness — is what keeps a premium-selling book alive across a vol spike. We lean on the discipline covered in [position sizing and the Kelly criterion](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/position-sizing-and-kelly-criterion); the single most common way to die in options is to size as if the tail never arrives.

**Learn to read the Greeks as a dashboard, not as calculus.** Every position has a **delta** (sensitivity to the stock's direction), a **gamma** (how fast that delta changes), a **vega** (sensitivity to implied volatility), and a **theta** (the daily time bleed). Those four numbers tell you, at a glance, what you are *actually* long or short — and managing them is how professionals survive moves that wipe out people who only watched the stock price. The next posts in this series take each Greek in turn and show it live on real positions.

**Invalidation is mechanical, and it includes vol.** A long-premium trade is invalidated not only when the stock goes the wrong way, but when implied volatility falls below where you bought it or when enough time passes that theta has eaten your thesis. A short-premium trade is invalidated when realized volatility blows past the implied you sold. Write the invalidation — price level, IV level, and date — before you enter, because in options "I was right but I lost" is not bad luck; it is an un-priced volatility or time bet you did not realize you were making.

If you remember nothing else: **an option is the right, not the obligation, to transact at a fixed price by a deadline — and its price is a bet on volatility and time, settled in the gap between the vol the market charged you and the vol that actually arrives.** Everything else in this series is detail on that sentence.
