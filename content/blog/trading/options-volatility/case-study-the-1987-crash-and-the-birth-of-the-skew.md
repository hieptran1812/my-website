> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — On Monday 19 October 1987 the S&P 500 fell about 20.5% and the Dow about 22.6% in a single session — a 16-standard-deviation event that a Black-Scholes lognormal world said should happen roughly once every 10^57 years. Portfolio insurance, a dynamic-hedging strategy that sold futures as the market fell, turned the decline into a self-feeding short-gamma spiral. The lasting legacy is in every options screen: equity-index puts have traded at a permanently higher implied vol than calls ever since.
>
> - **A continuous-hedging strategy met a discontinuous market.** Portfolio insurance replicated a put by selling index futures on the way down; when the market gapped through every hedge level at once, the replication broke exactly when it was needed.
> - **The crash taught the market that the left tail is real and fat.** Before 1987 equity-index options priced off a roughly flat vol curve; afterward, out-of-the-money puts permanently bid up — the skew, or "smirk."
> - **The skew is a price you pay or collect on every trade.** In the representative post-crash shape, a 10%-out-of-the-money put trades at 22% vol versus 17% at-the-money — roughly five times the dollar cost it would carry under flat vol.
> - **The number to remember:** a ~20% one-day drop is about a 16-sigma event under a normal model; the lognormal probability is ~10^-59, far smaller than one over the age of the universe. The skew is the market's permanent admission that the model was wrong about the tail.

The phone calls started before the New York open. It was Monday, 19 October 1987, and the selling had already rolled around the planet — Hong Kong had closed limit-down, Europe was bleeding, and the futures in Chicago were pointing to an open well below Friday's close. On the trading desks running *portfolio insurance*, the models were doing exactly what they had been built to do: the market was lower, so the synthetic put they were maintaining required them to sell more stock-index futures. The instruction was unambiguous. Sell. And so, into a market that was already falling and a futures book that was already thinning out, the programs sold.

The problem was that the buyers had left. Portfolio insurance was not the only seller — but it was a *mechanical* seller, one that got hungrier the lower the market went, and on that morning it was selling into a vacuum. Each block of futures it dumped pushed the price down further, which told the models to sell still more, which pushed the price down again. By the close the Dow had fallen 508 points to 1,738.74 — a 22.6% loss in one day, the largest one-day percentage decline in the index's history, and one it has never come close to repeating. The S&P 500 fell about 20.5%. The strategy that was supposed to be insurance had, in aggregate, helped light the fire it was meant to put out.

This post is a case study of that day and its single most durable consequence. We are not here to retell the macro history for its own sake; we are here because 19 October 1987 is the day the options market changed its mind about how the world is shaped, and the change is still priced into every option you will ever trade. Before the crash, equity-index options were quoted off a roughly flat volatility curve — the clean, symmetric world of the Black-Scholes lognormal model. After the crash, out-of-the-money puts permanently traded at a higher implied volatility than at-the-money options and far higher than out-of-the-money calls. That tilt is the **skew**, and it was born on Black Monday. To understand why it is permanent — why nearly forty years later the put wing is still bid — you have to understand exactly what broke that day, and why the model the market had been trusting said the whole thing was impossible.

![Implied volatility by strike before and after October 1987: a roughly flat pre-crash curve versus the post-crash put skew rising on the downside](/imgs/blogs/case-study-the-1987-crash-and-the-birth-of-the-skew-1.png)

The figure above is the whole story in one chart. The dashed gray line is the world before October 1987: implied volatility roughly flat across strikes, a faint symmetric smile, every option priced off about the same number. The solid blue line is the world after: the same at-the-money level, but the put wing pulled sharply up and the call wing pushed down. The red wedge between them is the implied volatility the crash *added* to the downside and never gave back. Everything in this post is an explanation of that wedge.

## Foundations: portfolio insurance and the flat-vol world before 1987

To see what broke, we need two things clear before the crash: what portfolio insurance actually was, and what the options market believed about the distribution of returns. Both turned out to be wrong in the same way, for the same reason.

### What portfolio insurance was

Portfolio insurance, as practiced in the mid-1980s — the strategy most associated with the firm Leland O'Brien Rubinstein Associates — was a way to get the payoff of a protective put *without buying a put*. The promise was seductive: protect a large equity portfolio against a crash, but skip the premium, the bid-ask spread, and the limited size of the listed options market by *manufacturing* the protection synthetically.

The mechanism rests on an idea this series has already built up in detail: **delta hedging**. A protective put has a delta — a sensitivity to the underlying — that becomes more negative as the market falls and less negative as it rises. (Delta is the option's first derivative with respect to the underlying; we covered it as the hedge ratio, and the practice of trading it in [delta hedging in practice](/blog/trading/options-volatility/delta-hedging-in-practice-the-cost-and-slippage-of-staying-neutral).) A put you *own* has that delta automatically. But you can also *replicate* it: hold your stock portfolio and, alongside it, maintain a short position in stock-index futures whose size you adjust to match the put's delta. As the market falls, the put you are imitating wants a more negative delta, so you sell more futures. As the market rises, it wants a less negative delta, so you buy futures back. Do this continuously and — in theory — the combination of stock plus the dynamic futures hedge has the same payoff as the stock plus a real put. You have built a put out of trading, not out of premium.

This is exactly the Black-Scholes replication argument run as a strategy. Black-Scholes derives an option's price by showing that a continuously rebalanced position in the underlying can replicate the option's payoff; the no-arbitrage price of the option is the cost of running that replication. (The full derivation lives in [the Black-Scholes post](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/black-scholes), which this series does not re-derive.) Portfolio insurance simply took that argument literally and *was the replication* — selling and buying futures to track a put's delta over time.

#### Worked example: the synthetic put that has to sell more as it falls

Take a stylized \$100 index portfolio of 1,000 units (a \$100,000 book) that wants the protection of a six-month put 10% out of the money — a 90-strike put. At a calm 18% volatility, that put's delta might be around −0.20 when the market is at \$100: mildly negative, because the put is well out of the money and only modestly sensitive to the spot. To replicate it, the manager sells futures equivalent to about 0.20 × 1,000 = **200 units** of index exposure — a small initial hedge.

Now the market falls to \$95. The 90 put is closer to the money, so its delta steepens — say to −0.38. The replication now requires a short of about 380 units. The manager must **sell another 180 units of futures** just to stay matched. Fall to \$90 and the delta might be −0.55: short 550 units, another 170 sold. Each leg down forces another sale, and the sales get *larger* per unit of move as the put nears the money — that is the curvature, the gamma, of the put.

The intuition: a synthetic put is a *sell-low* machine by construction — it is supposed to sell more exposure precisely as the market falls. In a calm, continuous market that is just disciplined rebalancing. In a one-day crash it is gasoline.

That gamma — the rate at which delta itself changes as the market moves — is the hinge of the whole story, and we covered it directly in [gamma, the Greek that bites](/blog/trading/options-volatility/gamma-the-greek-that-bites-curvature-convexity-and-the-toxic-short). A portfolio insurer running a synthetic put is, in Greek terms, *short gamma* in their hedging behavior: to hold a long-put-like exposure synthetically, they must trade against themselves, selling into weakness and buying into strength. Holding a real put would make them long gamma — the option does the convex work for them. Replicating it makes their *hedging* short the very convexity they were trying to own. Hold that thought; it is the engine of the spiral.

### The flat-vol world the options market believed

The second thing to set up is what the options market thought returns looked like. Before October 1987, the dominant mental model — and the dominant pricing convention — was the Black-Scholes lognormal. The assumption is clean and, for a decade, it was treated as close enough to true: the underlying's returns are normally distributed, with a single volatility that applies to every strike. Feed one sigma into the model and you get a fair price for every option on the name at once.

If that were true, then running the model in reverse — taking the market prices of options across strikes and backing out the volatility each one implies — would hand you the *same* number at every strike. A flat line. And in equity-index options before 1987, the curve was indeed roughly flat: a faint, near-symmetric smile, with the wings only slightly above the middle. There was no systematic premium on the downside. A 10%-out-of-the-money put and a 10%-out-of-the-money call were priced off close to the same volatility, because the market genuinely believed the distribution was close to symmetric and thin-tailed. Crash protection was *cheap*, because nobody was bidding for it. The lognormal said a 20% one-day drop was essentially impossible, so why pay up to insure against it?

It is worth being precise about what "implied volatility" is, because the skew is best understood as a deviation from a flat one. Black-Scholes is a function that maps a volatility to a price: feed it a sigma and it returns one dollar value for a given strike and expiry. That function is *monotonic* in sigma — a higher sigma always produces a higher option price, because more volatility means a wider cone of outcomes and an option only captures the favorable side. Monotonic functions are invertible, so for any market price there is exactly one sigma that reproduces it, and finding it is a simple one-dimensional root-find. The answer is the implied volatility of that strike. Nothing in that procedure forces the answer to be the same across strikes; it simply reports, strike by strike, the vol consistent with each observed price. Before 1987 the market handed back nearly the same number at every strike, so the curve was flat. After 1987 it handed back a rising number on the downside, so the curve became a skew. The model never changed; the prices the market was willing to pay did.

Two more pieces of the pre-1987 picture matter. First, **put-call parity** ties the put and call at the same strike together: a call minus a put at one strike equals a deterministic combination of the forward and the strike, with no volatility in it. (The proof is in [put-call parity and no-arbitrage](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/options-theory).) That arbitrage relationship forces a call and a put at the *same* strike to share one implied vol — so the skew is not a put-versus-call story at a single strike; it is a story about how vol changes *across* strikes. Second, the demand was simply not there. The big institutional crash-protection bid that exists today — pension funds, insurers, and risk-parity desks all systematically buying index puts — did not exist in 1987, in part because the people who wanted crash protection were *replicating* it through portfolio insurance instead of buying it. The synthetic-put crowd was a substitute for the put-buying crowd, which is a quiet irony: by manufacturing their own protection they kept the listed put market from developing the standing bid that would later define it. When their strategy failed in the crash, the survivors and everyone who watched switched to buying the real thing, and that permanent demand is part of what holds the skew up to this day.

This is the deep setup. Portfolio insurance assumed prices move *continuously* enough to rebalance through. The flat-vol options market assumed returns are *thin-tailed* enough that a crash is negligible. These are the same assumption wearing two hats — the assumption that the market is smooth and Gaussian — and on one Monday morning both hats came off at once.

## The run-up and the global cascade

The crash did not come from a clear blue sky. Through the first eight months of 1987 the U.S. market ran up hard — the Dow rose roughly 44% to a peak of 2,722.42 on 25 August 1987. Valuations were stretched, interest rates had been climbing through the autumn, the dollar was under pressure, and there was friction in trade and currency policy among the major economies. By early-to-mid October the market had rolled over and was weakening.

The week *before* Black Monday primed the charge. Wednesday through Friday, 14–16 October, the Dow fell roughly 3.8%, 2.4%, and 4.6% — a cumulative drop of more than 10% in three days. This matters mechanically: by Friday's close, the portfolio-insurance models were already carrying large short futures positions and were poised, if the market opened lower on Monday, to sell a great deal more. The selling was queued up over the weekend.

Then geography did its work. Markets open west to east across the globe, so the trading day begins in Asia. On Monday morning Hong Kong fell hard (and would soon suspend trading for the rest of the week); the selling rolled into Europe — London down sharply — and by the time New York opened, the U.S. market was staring at a wall of global selling and a futures market already deeply in the red. There was no calm open to ease into. The cascade had a running start, and the mechanical sellers were positioned to chase it down.

We have a sibling case study on an earlier and even larger collapse — the [1929 crash and the Great Depression](/blog/trading/finance/wall-street-crash-1929-great-depression) — and the rhyme is worth noting: a long run-up, a fragile market structure, and a self-reinforcing wave of forced selling. The 1987 version had a new and specifically modern accelerant, though, one that 1929 could not have had because the tools did not exist: a popular, well-funded strategy that was *programmed* to sell more the more the market fell.

## The accelerant: a short-gamma feedback loop

Here is the loop that turned a bad day into a historic one. It is worth walking slowly, because the same structure recurs in every later vol-driven crash, and recognizing it is most of what this case study is for.

![Portfolio-insurance feedback loop: market falls, the model says sell futures, selling pushes the market down, which forces more selling](/imgs/blogs/case-study-the-1987-crash-and-the-birth-of-the-skew-3.png)

Read the spiral from the top. The market falls a few percent into the Monday open. The portfolio-insurance models, replicating their synthetic puts, compute that the put's delta has become more negative and instruct the desks to sell index futures to match. The desks sell — into a book that is already thin because the natural buyers have stepped back. That selling *pushes the futures price down*, which the model reads as a further decline, which makes the delta more negative still, which instructs the desks to sell *more*. Each turn of the wheel feeds the next. This is a positive feedback loop, and its sign is what matters: the hedge is amplifying the move it is supposed to insure against.

In the language of the Greeks, the aggregate of all the portfolio insurers behaved like one enormous **short-gamma** position being forced to hedge in the worst possible direction. A short-gamma hedger must buy as the market rises and sell as it falls — they trade *with* the move, adding momentum. (This is the same dealer-gamma dynamic that drives modern intraday vol; we built the mechanics in [gamma, the Greek that bites](/blog/trading/options-volatility/gamma-the-greek-that-bites-curvature-convexity-and-the-toxic-short), where short gamma is described as the toxic position precisely because of this forced sell-low, buy-high behavior.) On a normal day this is a minor headwind, dwarfed by ordinary two-way flow. On 19 October 1987 the short-gamma flow was large relative to the available liquidity, one-directional, and self-reinforcing — and the natural buyers who would normally absorb it had vanished.

The phrase that captures the failure is "**selling insurance to yourself**." A protective put bought from a dealer transfers the crash risk to a counterparty who is paid to hold it and who will pay you in the crash. A *synthetic* put transfers nothing: you remain the only party in the trade, and you must produce the protective payoff by your own trading. As long as the market is liquid and continuous, that works. But the entire payoff depends on being able to sell at the levels the model assumes, when the model assumes them. When everyone running the same strategy needs to sell at the same time, and the buyers are gone, the price at which you can actually sell collapses away from the price the model used. You are insuring yourself against a crash by promising to sell — and the crash is exactly the moment the selling becomes impossible at any reasonable price. The insurance evaporates precisely when the claim comes due.

The Brady Commission, the official body that investigated the crash, identified this interaction — portfolio insurance selling, compounded by index arbitrage linking the futures and cash markets — as a central mechanical driver of the cascade. It did not *cause* the underlying decline; macro fragility did that. But it converted an ordinary sharp sell-off into a one-day collapse of historic size.

## The gap that cannot be hedged

Why did the replication break so completely? The answer is the single most important practical lesson of the day, and it goes to the heart of what dynamic hedging quietly assumes.

![Continuous-hedging assumption versus the 1987 gap: a smooth path rebalanced at every level on the left, a single jump through every level on the right](/imgs/blogs/case-study-the-1987-crash-and-the-birth-of-the-skew-6.png)

Dynamic replication assumes the market moves **continuously** — that prices trace a smooth path so you can rebalance the hedge at every level along the way. The left panel shows the theory: the price drifts from 100 to 99 to 98 to 97, and at each level the insurer sells a small slice of futures, paying a small cost, and the synthetic put tracks tightly. The math of Black-Scholes is built on this assumption — the underlying follows a continuous diffusion, and the replicating portfolio is rebalanced over infinitesimally small moves. (The continuous-path machinery is the stochastic calculus behind the model; see [delta hedging in practice](/blog/trading/options-volatility/delta-hedging-in-practice-the-cost-and-slippage-of-staying-neutral) for how that assumption frays even on ordinary days, through transaction costs and discrete rebalancing.)

The right panel is what actually happened. The market did not drift from 100 to 90 through every level in between. It **gapped** — opened far below the previous close and kept lurching down in jumps, with the in-between levels barely trading or not trading at all. You cannot rebalance at 97 if 97 never prints. The futures the model needed to sell at 97 could only be sold at, say, 90, deeply below fair value, after the move had already happened. Through the gap, the portfolio insurer was simply *unhedged* — carrying the full downside exposure the synthetic put was supposed to remove — and then realized the loss by selling into the hole at the bottom. The replication did not merely cost more than expected; over the gap it did not exist.

This is the gap risk that no continuous-hedging strategy can engineer away, and it is the precise reason a *real* put and a *replicated* put are not the same instrument. A real put pays its full intrinsic value at expiry regardless of how the underlying got there — smooth path or violent gap, the holder of a 90-strike put collects the same payoff at \$70 either way, because the contract is a promise, not a trading program. A synthetic put is only as good as your ability to trade through the move, and a gap is exactly the move you cannot trade through. The whole appeal of portfolio insurance was that it was cheaper than buying real puts. October 1987 revealed why: it was cheaper because it was not actually insurance against the one scenario — the gap — that you most needed insurance against.

#### Worked example: the gap the hedge missed

Suppose an insurer's model, with the market at \$100 on Friday's close, holds a synthetic short of 200 units (delta −0.20 against a 1,000-unit book). The model's plan for a decline to \$80 is to sell gradually as the market falls, ending around a short of 800 units, so that the *average* sale price across the path is maybe \$90 — the cost of the protection.

Monday the market gaps straight to \$80 at the open. The model now wants a short of 800 units but is only short 200. It must sell **600 units at \$80** — not spread across \$95, \$90, \$85 as planned, but all at the bottom. Against a book that fell from \$100,000 to \$80,000 — a \$20,000 loss — the synthetic put was supposed to offset most of the drop. Instead the hedge was effectively absent through the \$100-to-\$80 gap; the 600 units sold at \$80 lock in the realization at the worst level rather than averaging the insurer out on the way down.

The intuition: the synthetic put's protection lives in the *path*, and a gap deletes the path. A real 90-strike put would have paid \$10/unit at \$80 no matter what the path looked like — \$10,000 of protection delivered as a contractual fact. The replication delivered a fraction of that and a forced sale at the lows.

## When price discovery broke down

There is one more layer to why the day was as bad as it was, and it explains why the gap was not a single clean jump but a chaotic, all-day disintegration: the machinery the market uses to *find* a price stopped working. Understanding this matters because it is the reason the short-gamma spiral could not self-correct — normally, a falling price attracts buyers, and the spiral burns out. On 19 October 1987 the buyers could not even see a reliable price to bid against.

Start with the link between the futures and the cash market. The S&P 500 index is computed from the last traded prices of its constituent stocks. The S&P 500 futures contract is a separate instrument that trades on a different exchange. In normal times these two are tied together by **index arbitrage**: if the futures trade below the cash index, arbitrageurs buy the cheap futures and sell the underlying stocks (or vice versa), and that arbitrage keeps the two prices in line. The two markets are supposed to be two views of the same thing.

On Black Monday the link snapped. The portfolio-insurance selling hit the futures market hardest, because futures are the cheapest, fastest way to sell a large notional of equity exposure. So the futures gapped down well below the cash index. Index arbitrageurs should have stepped in — sell stock, buy the cheap futures — but selling the underlying stocks ran into a wall: many individual stocks could not be sold because there were no buyers, the specialists who made markets were overwhelmed, and a large number of stocks simply did not open on time or traded only in fits. With the cash leg of the arbitrage jammed, the arbitrage that normally re-links futures and cash could not function. The futures kept falling on their own, untethered, and because portfolio-insurance models often keyed off futures prices, the unmoored futures fed even more selling instructions back into the loop.

The deeper problem was **stale and missing prices**. When a stock has not traded in twenty minutes, its last print is meaningless — yet that print is what the index is computed from and what every model reads. Traders genuinely did not know what things were worth. A market maker asked to buy in that environment faces a brutal asymmetry: bid too high and you catch a falling knife against sellers who know more than the tape shows; bid low enough to be safe and you are quoting a price so far below the last print that it looks like a different universe. The rational response of the people whose job is to provide liquidity was to widen their quotes enormously or step away entirely. That is what "liquidity vanished" actually means in mechanical terms — not that buyers were emotionally scared, but that the price-discovery process had degraded to the point where no one could quote a tight, reliable market, so the cushion that normally absorbs selling was simply not there.

This is the loop's fatal property. A self-reinforcing sell-off ends when falling prices attract enough buyers to absorb the selling. But that requires functioning price discovery — buyers need to believe the price they see is real. When the index is stale, the futures are unmoored, individual stocks are not trading, and the arbitrage link is broken, the price a would-be buyer sees is not trustworthy, so the buyers stay out, so the selling has nothing to push against, so it keeps going. The short-gamma spiral and the price-discovery breakdown were mutually reinforcing: the spiral overwhelmed the liquidity, and the loss of liquidity removed the only thing that could have stopped the spiral. This precise interaction — mechanical forced selling colliding with a market that has lost its ability to price itself — is the signature of every modern flash crash and vol shock, and it is why the regulatory fix that followed was aimed squarely at the price-discovery layer.

#### Worked example: what the arbitrage disconnect cost

Suppose mid-morning the cash S&P index reads 250 (stale — built from last prints, many an hour old) while the futures, where the live selling is, trade at the equivalent of 230 — a **20-point, ~8% disconnect** between two instruments that are supposed to be the same thing. A portfolio insurer hedging in futures is realizing exposure at 230. A trader who wants to buy the "cheap" futures and sell the index to capture the 8% must short the basket of 500 stocks — but if a third of the basket will not trade, the short cannot be executed, so the 8% gap cannot be closed and the futures are free to keep falling.

The intuition: the 8% gap between futures and cash was not free money, it was a measurement of broken plumbing. In a working market that gap is arbitraged to near zero in minutes; on Black Monday it persisted because the arbitrage could not be done, and a market where the two prices of the same thing disagree by 8% is a market that has, for practical purposes, stopped functioning. No buyer rebuilds a cushion under a price they cannot trust.

## The birth of the skew

Now the consequence that outlived the crash by decades. When the dust settled and the options market reopened to a world that had just witnessed a 20% one-day drop, traders repriced what downside protection was worth — and they never repriced it back.

Before October 1987, recall, the implied-vol curve across strikes was roughly flat: puts and calls equidistant from the money traded at similar vols. After October 1987, a persistent **downward skew** appeared: out-of-the-money puts traded at a structurally higher implied volatility than at-the-money options, which in turn traded higher than out-of-the-money calls. The curve tilted, the put wing lifted, and it stayed lifted. Mark Rubinstein — one of the architects of portfolio insurance, which gives the observation a certain irony — documented the phenomenon in the 1990s; the literature sometimes calls it the "1987 smirk" or, more evocatively, "crashophobia." This is the same skew we built from the trading side in [the volatility smile and skew](/blog/trading/options-volatility/the-volatility-smile-and-skew-why-otm-puts-cost-more); this post is the story of where it came from.

Why did the crash do this? Because it taught the market a fact about the *shape* of the return distribution that the lognormal model denied: the left tail is fat. Real equity returns are not the tidy bell curve Black-Scholes assumes. They have rare but genuinely possible catastrophic down-moves — moves so far out in the tail that the normal distribution assigns them essentially zero probability, yet which actually occur. Once you believe the left tail is fat, out-of-the-money puts are worth more than the lognormal says, because they pay off precisely in that fat tail. And the way the market expresses "this put is worth more than the lognormal says" is to bid up its implied volatility — because implied vol is just the number you plug into Black-Scholes to reproduce the price you are actually willing to pay. A higher willingness to pay for downside protection *is* a higher implied vol on the puts. The skew is the lognormal model's flat curve bent to fit a fat-tailed reality.

![Risk-neutral return distribution before and after 1987: a thin-tailed lognormal bell versus a distribution with a fat left tail](/imgs/blogs/case-study-the-1987-crash-and-the-birth-of-the-skew-2.png)

The figure makes the shape explicit. The dashed gray bell is the Black-Scholes lognormal: symmetric, thin-tailed, with almost no probability mass beyond a few percent in a day. The blue curve is the distribution implied by the post-1987 skew: most days still look normal — the two curves overlap in the middle — but the left tail is dramatically heavier. The red shaded region is the "crash mass" the lognormal model simply leaves out: the probability of a large down-move that the bell curve says is negligible but the market, post-1987, insists on pricing. The skew on the vol curve and the fat tail on the distribution are the same object seen two ways. You cannot have one without the other; a steep put skew *is* a statement that the left tail is fat, and vice versa.

There is a deep reason the skew is *permanent* and not just a temporary scar. The flat-vol world was an equilibrium held up by a belief — the belief that returns are thin-tailed. That belief was an empirical claim, and on 19 October 1987 the data falsified it in the most violent way imaginable. You cannot un-see a 20% day. Every risk manager, every options market maker, every model since has had to carry the knowledge that the tail is real, and the cleanest way to carry that knowledge in a price is to keep the put wing bid. The skew is, quite literally, a permanent memorial to Black Monday — a feature of every equity-index options screen that exists because of one specific Monday morning almost forty years ago.

But "the tail is real" is only half of why the skew persists; the other half is who is on each side of the trade, and that supply-and-demand structure has only hardened over forty years. On the **demand** side, there is a deep, price-insensitive, standing bid for index puts. Pension funds and insurers have liabilities that a crash would impair, so they buy downside protection as a matter of policy, not opportunism. Risk-parity and volatility-target funds mechanically de-risk into falling markets, which is itself a form of put-like demand. And every fund manager who lived through a crash carries a career incentive to be visibly hedged. On the **supply** side, the dealers and vol sellers who write those puts are, in effect, selling crash insurance, and they price it the way any insurer prices a catastrophe policy: with a fat margin, because the rare loss is enormous and the capital to warehouse that risk is expensive. The result is a structural imbalance — persistent demand for downside protection meeting a supply that charges a premium to provide it — and that imbalance is exactly what a steep put skew looks like in price. It does not need a fresh crash to stay steep; it only needs the standing demand and the cost of supplying the insurance, both of which are permanent features of how large pools of capital manage equity risk.

There is also a reflexive twist that makes the skew self-stabilizing. Because the skew is steep, anyone who *wants* crash protection finds it expensive, which nudges some of them toward selling upside calls or buying put spreads instead of outright puts — flows that themselves reinforce the shape of the curve. And because selling the put wing carries that fat premium, a steady population of yield-seeking sellers shows up to harvest it, which would tend to flatten the skew — except that those sellers periodically get carried out in the next vol spike, which re-steepens it and reminds everyone why the premium exists. The skew breathes: it flattens in long calm stretches as sellers crowd in, then snaps steeper in every shock as those sellers are forced to cover. But it never returns to the flat, pre-1987 line, because the belief that anchored that line — that a 20% day cannot happen — is gone for good. The 1987 crash did not just add a premium; it installed a floor under the steepness of the put wing that no amount of calm has since been able to fully erode.

#### Worked example: the crash premium on a 10%-out-of-the-money put

Price the same put two ways. Spot \$100, 30 days to expiry (T = 30/365 ≈ 0.082 years), risk-free rate 4%, a 90-strike put.

**Priced off a flat 1987-style 17% vol** — the world before the crash, where the put was just another option on a symmetric distribution — Black-Scholes gives about **\$0.0207 per share**: roughly two cents, \$2.07 per 100-share contract. Crash protection was nearly free.

**Priced off today's skew** — where the 90 strike trades at **22%** vol, not 17% — Black-Scholes gives about **\$0.1033 per share**: about ten cents, \$10.33 per contract. The same option, the same spot, the same expiry, **roughly five times** the price. The entire difference is the vol input: 22% versus 17%.

That extra eight cents per share is the **crash premium** — the price 1987 permanently added to downside protection. The intuition: you are not paying more because the stock is "more volatile below 90"; you are paying more because the market now refuses to price the put as if a crash were impossible, and the higher implied vol is how that refusal shows up in dollars.

![Out-of-the-money put prices under flat vol versus skew vol across three strikes, showing the multiple added by the post-1987 skew](/imgs/blogs/case-study-the-1987-crash-and-the-birth-of-the-skew-4.png)

The bar chart generalizes the worked example across the put wing. At every out-of-the-money strike, the post-1987 skew price (blue) towers over the flat-vol price (gray), and the multiple *grows* the further out you go. The 95 put (5% out of the money) goes from about \$0.31 at flat vol to \$0.45 at its 19% skew vol. The 90 put (10% out) jumps from \$0.02 to \$0.10 — the five-fold move from the worked example. And the 85 put (15% out) is the most striking: nearly worthless at \$0.0004 under flat 17% vol, but \$0.0313 at its 26% skew vol — about **80 times** more. The deeper into the tail you look, the more violently the skew reprices the option, because the deep wing is exactly where the fat-tail belief diverges most from the lognormal. The crash premium is not a uniform markup; it is concentrated in the far downside, which is the part of the distribution the crash taught the market to fear.

## The day the bell curve said was impossible

It is worth quantifying just how far outside the model the crash sat, because the magnitude is what makes the permanence of the skew rational rather than superstitious.

![A 20% one-day move against the normal model's bell curve, marking the S&P decline at about 16 standard deviations](/imgs/blogs/case-study-the-1987-crash-and-the-birth-of-the-skew-5.png)

A volatility is just an annualized standard deviation of returns. In a calm regime the S&P might run around 20% annualized vol, which converts to a daily standard deviation of about 20% ÷ √252 ≈ **1.26%** per day (there are roughly 252 trading days in a year). Now ask the bell curve how unusual a 20.5% one-day drop is: 20.5% ÷ 1.26% ≈ **16.3 standard deviations**. The Dow's 22.6% works out to about 17.9 sigma. The figure plots the normal daily-return bell and marks the S&P move far off in the left tail, where the curve has long since flattened to the axis.

A 16-sigma event under a normal distribution has a probability of roughly **10^-59**. To put that in human terms: if the market traded every day, a 16-sigma down-day would be expected about once every **10^57 years**. The universe is about 1.4 × 10^10 years old. The model did not say the crash was *unlikely*; it said the crash was so far beyond unlikely that it should not have occurred even once in a span of time longer than the age of the universe raised to a meaningful power. And yet it happened on an ordinary Monday, well within living memory.

This is the cleanest possible demonstration of what is wrong with assuming Gaussian returns, and it is why the skew is not paranoia. When a model assigns a 10^-59 probability to something that demonstrably happens, the rational response is not to trust the model's tail at all. The market's response was to stop pricing the tail off the lognormal and start pricing it off experience — and experience says the down-tail is fat enough to be worth real money. The skew is the price of that lesson, paid forward on every put, in perpetuity.

#### Worked example: how much fatter the skew makes the implied tail

The skew does not just raise put prices; it changes the *probability* the market assigns to a crash. Under Black-Scholes, the risk-neutral probability that the underlier finishes below a strike K is N(−d2) — a direct output of the model for a given vol. Take the 90 strike, 30 days, spot \$100, r = 4%.

**At the flat 17% vol:** the implied probability of finishing below \$90 is about **1.37%**.

**At the 90-strike skew vol of 22%:** the implied probability rises to about **4.54%** — roughly 3.3 times fatter.

Go further out, to the 85 strike: at flat 17% the implied chance of finishing below \$85 is about **0.04%** — four hundredths of one percent, essentially "never." At the 85-strike skew vol of 26%, it is about **1.44%** — roughly **36 times** fatter. The intuition: the steep put skew is the market quoting a far higher chance of a large down-move than the flat lognormal admits, and the deeper the tail, the wider the gap between what the model says and what the price says.

## Common misconceptions

**"Portfolio insurance protected investors in the 1987 crash."** It largely did not, and in aggregate it made things worse. The strategy's protection depended on selling futures continuously as the market fell; when the market gapped and liquidity vanished, the selling could only be executed far below the model's assumed levels, so the realized hedge was a fraction of the planned one. Worse, because so much capital ran the same strategy and all of it needed to sell at once, the selling itself drove prices down — a short-gamma feedback loop the Brady Commission singled out as a mechanical accelerant of the cascade. Investors who bought *real* puts were protected; investors who replicated them were, through the gap, effectively unhedged and helped deepen the hole. The lesson, in one line: a hedge that depends on the liquidity that disappears in a crash is not a crash hedge.

**"The skew means traders think the market will crash."** No. The skew is a *risk-neutral* pricing object, not a forecast. A steep put skew says the market is willing to pay up for downside protection — because the tail is fat *and* because investors are more averse to losses than indifferent to them — not that a crash is imminent or even probable. In fact the variance and skew you pay for tend, on average, to exceed what is realized: sellers of the put wing collect a premium over time precisely because buyers overpay for insurance relative to the actual frequency of crashes. The skew is the price of fear, and fear is persistently a bit overpriced, which is the whole basis of [the variance risk premium](/blog/trading/options-volatility/the-variance-risk-premium-why-selling-vol-pays-until-it-doesnt). Reading the skew as a directional crash call is a category error.

**"Black-Scholes was proved wrong in 1987, so traders abandoned it."** The opposite happened. Traders kept the Black-Scholes formula but stopped feeding it a single flat volatility; instead they feed it a *different* implied vol at every strike — the skew. The model survives as a quoting convention: a monotonic, invertible map between price and a single comparable number, vol. What 1987 killed was not the formula but the *assumption* of one flat sigma and a thin-tailed distribution. The market agreed to keep using the formula while structurally disagreeing with it, strike by strike, and the shape of that disagreement is the skew. Black-Scholes is the language; the skew is what the market actually says in that language.

**"A 20% one-day move is a once-in-a-lifetime, never-again event."** Treat that belief as expensive. Under a normal model a 20% day is a 16-sigma impossibility — and yet it happened, which is the entire point: the tail is fatter than the model, so "the model says it can't happen" is worthless reassurance. Markets have gapped violently many times since: the 2010 flash crash, the 2018 short-vol blowup, the 2020 COVID open, and others. The right posture is not "it will never happen again" but "it can happen at any time, I cannot hedge through the gap, and I must be sized to survive it." That is the difference between the people who blew up in later crashes and the people who were still standing.

**"If I delta-hedge carefully enough, I can replicate any payoff and never need to buy options."** This is precisely the portfolio-insurance fallacy, restated. Continuous replication is a theoretical limit that assumes you can trade at every price level as the market passes through it. Real markets gap, and a gap is the one move you cannot rebalance through. A replicated long-put position is long the convexity *only if the path is smooth*; over a gap it delivers a fraction of a real put's payoff and forces you to trade at the worst level. If you actually need protection against the tail, you must *own the option*, not promise to manufacture it. The premium you "save" by replicating is the price of the gap risk you are quietly keeping.

## How it shows up in real markets

The skew born in 1987 is not a historical curiosity; it is a live feature of every equity-index options market, and the short-gamma spiral has recurred, in new clothes, in nearly every vol shock since.

**The skew is on your screen right now.** Pull up SPX options today and the put wing trades at a meaningfully higher implied vol than the call wing — the same shape as the blue line in the first figure. This reprices everything you build. A put spread costs more than the symmetric intuition suggests because the put you buy is richer than the put you sell on a vol basis. A collar — long a put, short a call to finance it — is *favorably* priced for the hedger because you buy the cheap-vol call... no, you *sell* the cheap-vol call and buy the rich-vol put, so the skew works against the collar buyer and the structure is less of a free lunch than it looks; the trade-off is exactly what we dissect in [trading skew with risk reversals and collars](/blog/trading/options-volatility/trading-skew-risk-reversals-collars-and-the-shape-of-fear). The point is that you cannot price any downside structure off a single at-the-money number; the 1987 skew is baked into every quote.

**The short-gamma spiral keeps coming back.** The specific vehicle changes — portfolio insurance in 1987, leveraged inverse-VIX products and short-vol strategies in 2018, dealer gamma in the 2020 COVID crash and the 2024 yen-carry unwind — but the structure is identical: a large, concentrated, mechanically short-gamma position that is forced to trade *with* the move, into thinning liquidity, amplifying it. The 2018 episode is so direct a sequel that this series treats it as its own case study; we will pick up the short-vol blowup in [Volmageddon 2018](/blog/trading/options-volatility/case-study-volmageddon-2018-and-the-short-vol-blowup), where the VIX more than doubled in a day and a popular short-vol product was destroyed by exactly the feedback dynamic 1987 invented. If you understand the 1987 spiral, you already understand 2018; only the instrument changed.

**Correlations go to one, and the hedge you counted on isn't there.** A second 1987 lesson that recurs every crisis: in a crash, the diversification you relied on evaporates. Markets that normally move independently all fall together, liquidity dries up everywhere at once, and the careful cross-hedges in a portfolio stop offsetting. This is the "correlation goes to one" phenomenon, which we cover from the cross-asset side in [when correlations go to one in a crisis](/blog/trading/cross-asset/when-correlations-go-to-one-in-a-crisis). For an options trader it means a hedge built on the assumption that A and B move oppositely can fail exactly when you need it, because in the tail everything is the same trade. The skew prices index puts richly in part because index puts are the one hedge that *does* pay when everything correlates down — the cleanest insurance against the moment diversification dies.

**Market structure changed permanently too.** The most visible regulatory legacy of Black Monday is the **circuit breaker**. Before 1987 there were no market-wide trading halts; the 22.6% Dow drop ran without any mechanical pause. In direct response, exchanges introduced circuit breakers in 1988 — predefined declines (today, 7%, 13%, and 20% intraday on the S&P 500) that trigger temporary halts to let liquidity regroup and break the feedback loop. The premise is precisely the 1987 lesson: a self-reinforcing sell-off needs a circuit breaker because the participants themselves will not stop — the models tell them to keep selling. Whether circuit breakers help or simply delay the inevitable is debated, but their existence is a monument to the specific failure mode this post describes.

## The playbook: what 1987 teaches a modern options trader

The crash is almost forty years old, but its lessons are operational, not historical. Here is how a working options trader should carry them.

![A matrix of the four lasting lessons of 1987 and what each one means for an options trade today](/imgs/blogs/case-study-the-1987-crash-and-the-birth-of-the-skew-7.png)

**Respect the skew as a permanent, tradable price — not a quirk.** Every put you buy or sell carries the 1987 crash premium in its implied vol. Price each option off *its own* strike's vol, never a single at-the-money number, or you will be shocked at the fill on any downside structure (the 90 put is five times the flat-vol estimate, the 85 put eighty times). On the other side, the persistence of the skew is *why* selling the put wing has a structural edge: the tail is priced richer than it realizes on average, so disciplined, well-sized put-side selling collects the variance and skew premium — the engine of [the variance risk premium](/blog/trading/options-volatility/the-variance-risk-premium-why-selling-vol-pays-until-it-doesnt). The catch is the same one 1987 taught: that edge is paid for by occasionally taking the full tail, so it only works if your sizing assumes the crash will come.

**Own the option when you actually need the tail; never replicate it.** The single sharpest lesson of the day: a synthetic put built from dynamic hedging is not insurance against a gap, because a gap is the one move you cannot rebalance through. If your hedge has to *work* in a crash — if a drawdown would force a sale, breach a limit, or trigger a margin call — buy the real option and pay the premium. The premium you would "save" by replicating is exactly the gap risk you are keeping. This is the bright line between a protective put and portfolio insurance, and it maps directly onto the hedging toolkit in [hedging a portfolio with options](/blog/trading/options-volatility/hedging-a-portfolio-with-options-protective-puts-collars-and-tail-risk): the real put pays through the gap; the delta replication does not.

**Watch the gamma of the whole market, not just your book.** The danger in 1987, 2018, and 2020 was not any single trader's position — it was the *aggregate* short-gamma exposure of a crowded strategy, forced to sell into a falling, illiquid market. As a modern trader you should know which way the dealer-gamma flow leans and where the crowded short-vol positioning sits, because that flow tells you whether a sell-off will be cushioned (dealers long gamma, buying the dip) or amplified (dealers and funds short gamma, selling the dip). The short-gamma spiral is a feature of market microstructure, not a one-time event; reading it is part of [how a market maker thinks](/blog/trading/options-volatility/delta-hedging-in-practice-the-cost-and-slippage-of-staying-neutral) about the other side of your trade.

**Size for the gap, assume the hedge slips, and never trust a thin-tailed risk number.** The final lesson is the most boring and the most important. A 16-sigma day happened; it can happen again; you cannot hedge through it in real time. So your position sizing must assume that one day the market gaps 20%, your stop does not fill, and your dynamic hedge slips badly. If a 20% gap day would end your account, you are too big — full stop, regardless of what your value-at-risk says, because VaR is built on the same thin-tailed distribution that said 1987 was impossible. The skew on your screen is the market's standing reminder that the tail is fat and the model is optimistic about the downside. Trade as if you believe it, because the one day you don't is the day it costs everything.

A concrete way to operationalize that last point: before you put on any short-option or short-vol position, run the position through a deliberate gap scenario — not a one-standard-deviation move, but a 1987-scale jump that ignores your stops and assumes your dynamic hedge fills well away from where you wanted it. Mark the loss. If that number is survivable, the position is sized correctly; if it ends you, no amount of expected edge justifies it, because the variance and skew premium you are harvesting is *paid for* precisely by the occasional catastrophe, and a single uncovered catastrophe wipes out a decade of premium collection. The traders who compound through the years are not the ones who never take a loss on a gap day — everyone short the tail eventually does — but the ones who sized so the gap day is a bruise rather than a death. That discipline is the entire practical inheritance of Black Monday: assume the impossible move, because the options market has been telling you, in the shape of its own prices for almost forty years, that it is not impossible at all.

The deepest takeaway is that the options market is, in a sense, *smarter* than its model. It kept the Black-Scholes formula because the formula is a useful language, but it overrode the model's most dangerous assumption — a thin, symmetric tail — by bending the vol curve into a skew that prices the crash the model denies. That override was written in a single day in October 1987, and the market has never erased it. Every time you price a put off its own rich vol, every time you choose to buy real protection instead of replicating it, every time you size so a gap day cannot end you, you are trading on a lesson that a generation of traders learned the hard way on one Monday morning. The skew is their memorial, and reading it correctly is the closest thing options trading has to learning from someone else's blow-up instead of your own.
