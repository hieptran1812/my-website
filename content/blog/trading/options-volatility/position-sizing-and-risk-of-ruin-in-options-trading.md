> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — An option's risk is not its premium and not its notional; it is its **max loss** and the **Greeks** it warehouses, and the only thing that keeps you alive is sizing to the bad day, not to the carry.
>
> - **Notional lies.** The same naked put can show a \$87 premium, a \$9,300 notional, and a \$9,213 max loss — three numbers, only the last two are real. Size off the bad-day scenario and the dollar-gamma/dollar-vega you carry, never off the premium.
> - **A small edge with a fat left tail can still ruin you** if you size to the carry: the same positive-expectancy short-vol strategy can have a near-zero or a near-certain risk of ruin depending only on how big you bet.
> - **Full Kelly is too aggressive for short vol.** The Gaussian Kelly formula assumes a symmetric distribution and over-bets the fat tail; use a fraction of an honestly-skew-aware Kelly, and size to survive the worst plausible move.
> - The one rule to remember: **set your maximum tolerable tail loss first, then size the whole book to survive it — assume the tail prints next month.**

Two traders run the same strategy. Both sell one-month put options on a basket of large-cap stocks, collecting premium, betting that implied volatility is richer than what will actually realize — the structural edge documented in [the variance risk premium](/blog/trading/options-volatility/the-variance-risk-premium-why-selling-vol-pays-until-it-doesnt). Both have the *exact same edge*: the same win rate, the same average premium, the same skill at picking strikes. They start the year with \$100,000 each.

The first trader sizes to survive. She decides up front that no single bad month may cost her more than 10% of capital, and she sells a number of contracts small enough that even a market-wide crash stays inside that budget. The second trader sizes for maximum return. He looks at the carry — the premium rolling in month after month — and reasons that since the strategy is profitable, more contracts simply means more profit. He sells four times as many.

For eleven months they look identical, except the second trader's account is climbing faster. His equity curve is steeper, his returns are the envy of the group chat, his Sharpe ratio looks superhuman. Then the twelfth month arrives — a single gap, a Volmageddon, a COVID Monday, the inevitable tail that every short-vol book eventually meets. The first trader takes a dent: down 10%, annoyed, still in the game, and now able to sell richly-priced insurance into the panic. The second trader is down 130% of his account. He is not down 130% on paper and recovering; he received a margin call he could not meet, his positions were liquidated at the worst possible prices, and the account is gone. Same edge. Same trade. One survived because of a single decision made before either of them put on a position: **how big**.

![Risk of ruin rising sharply as position size grows for a positive-edge short-vol strategy](/imgs/blogs/position-sizing-and-risk-of-ruin-in-options-trading-1.png)

That single decision is the entire subject of this post, and the chart above is its thesis. Both axes describe the same positive-edge short-vol strategy — the average month makes money — but the horizontal axis is *size* (how much of your capital the worst plausible month would cost) and the vertical axis is the probability of ruin over three years. A conservatively-sized book has essentially zero chance of ruin. A Kelly-ish size carries a real but survivable risk. An oversized book is more likely than not to be wiped out, *with the identical underlying edge*. Edge gets you nothing if you do not survive to compound it. Sizing is not a footnote to a trading strategy; for options, where the left tail is fat and the leverage is built in, sizing **is** the strategy.

## Foundations: what "risk" actually means for an option

Before we can size anything, we have to be ruthlessly precise about a word that gets used three different ways, often in the same sentence: *risk*. When a trader says "I have \$10,000 of risk on," they could mean three completely different numbers, and confusing them is the root of most blow-ups. Let us define each from zero.

**Premium** is what you pay (if you buy) or receive (if you sell) to put on the option. It is a cash amount that changes hands today. For a buyer, the premium is the most you can lose — a long call or long put cannot cost you more than what you paid. That is the *one* case where premium equals max loss, and it is why long options feel safe (and why people overtrade them). For a seller, the premium is what you *collect*, and it has almost nothing to do with what you can *lose*. Treating the premium you collect as your risk is the single most common and most lethal sizing error in options.

**Notional** is the dollar value of the underlying the option controls. An equity option contract typically controls 100 shares, so a put struck at \$93 on a \$100 stock has a notional of `93 × 100 = \$9,300`. Notional matters for understanding leverage — a \$87 premium controlling \$9,300 of stock is more than 100-to-1 leverage — but notional is not your max loss either. You will not lose the whole notional on a put unless the stock goes to exactly zero, and on a call your loss is capped differently. Notional overstates the risk of some positions and understates it for others. It is a leverage gauge, not a risk measure.

**Max loss** is the honest answer to "what is the worst this single position can cost me?" For a *defined-risk* structure — a spread where you both buy and sell options so the downside is bounded — max loss is a hard, knowable number you compute before you trade. For an *undefined-risk* (naked) structure, max loss is theoretically enormous (a naked put loses everything down to the strike; a naked call is unbounded), so in practice you define it by the **worst plausible move**: how far can the underlying realistically gap in the holding period, and what does the position lose there? Sizing off max loss is the foundation of survival.

**The Greeks** are the position's *current* sensitivities — how its value changes as the underlying moves (delta and gamma), as volatility changes (vega), and as time passes (theta). For a short-vol book, two Greeks define the day-to-day risk you are warehousing: **dollar-gamma** (how violently your delta — and therefore your P&L — accelerates as the market moves) and **dollar-vega** (how much you lose per point that implied volatility rises). A position with a small premium can carry large dollar-gamma and dollar-vega, which is exactly why the premium tells you nothing. We build these on top of the single-Greek definitions in [delta, direction exposure, and the hedge ratio](/blog/trading/options-volatility/delta-direction-exposure-and-the-hedge-ratio) and [gamma, the Greek that bites](/blog/trading/options-volatility/gamma-the-greek-that-bites-curvature-convexity-and-the-toxic-short); the dashboard that aggregates them across a whole book is [the net Greeks of a position](/blog/trading/options-volatility/the-net-greeks-of-a-position-building-your-risk-dashboard).

It is worth pausing on *why* the Greeks, and not just the max loss, belong in a sizing discussion — after all, the max loss already bounds the worst case. The answer is that the Greeks describe the risk you carry *between now and the worst case*, on every ordinary day, and that risk is what generates the margin calls and forced covers that turn a survivable max loss into an actual ruin. A short option with a large negative gamma loses money at an *accelerating* rate as the market moves against it: the first 1% move costs you a little, the second 1% costs you more, the third costs you more still, because gamma is the rate at which your directional exposure grows against you. By the time the underlying has moved enough to approach your max loss, your daily mark-to-market losses are violent and your margin requirement has ballooned — and that combination, not the terminal max loss, is what forces you out at the worst price. The dollar-vega adds a second blade: in a crash, implied volatility spikes at the same time the underlying gaps, so a short-vega position is hit *twice* — once by the price move (gamma) and once by the vol spike (vega) — in the same session. Sizing by max loss alone, ignoring the Greeks, assumes you can calmly hold to expiry; the Greeks are what tell you whether you will still be solvent and un-margin-called long enough to *get* to expiry. Both belong in the size.

### Why the premium is a liar

Make it concrete with a single naked put, the workhorse income trade of retail and professional sellers alike. The intuition is that one number — what hits your account — gets mistaken for the risk, while the dangerous numbers stay hidden until the bad day. The chart below puts all four "risks" side by side for the *same* position.

![Premium, notional, max loss, dollar-vega, and dollar-gamma for the same naked put as bars](/imgs/blogs/position-sizing-and-risk-of-ruin-in-options-trading-2.png)

The bars are computed from the Black-Scholes model (we do not re-derive it here — see [Black-Scholes](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/black-scholes); we use it as a calculator). The position is one short put struck at \$93 on a \$100 stock, one month to expiry, 30% implied volatility, 4% rates — a roughly 18-delta out-of-the-money put, the kind a premium-seller writes for income. Look at the spread of numbers describing one identical contract:

- **Premium received: \$87.** This is what lands in your account. It feels like the trade.
- **Notional: \$9,300.** The stock value the put controls.
- **Max loss: \$9,213.** What you lose if the stock goes to zero (strike minus the premium you kept, times 100).
- **Dollar-vega: \$7.54 per vol point.** Lose this much for every point implied vol rises.
- **Dollar-gamma: \$302 per 1% move.** The acceleration in your P&L per 1% move in the stock — and this *grows* as the stock falls toward the strike.

The premium is **0.9% of the notional and 0.9% of the max loss.** If you size your book by the premium you collect — "I'm only risking \$87 per contract, so I'll sell 100 of them for \$8,700 of income" — you have unknowingly put on \$921,300 of max loss and \$30,200 of dollar-gamma against, very possibly, a \$100,000 account. The premium told you a comforting lie. The max loss and the Greeks told the truth, and you did not listen.

#### Worked example: the premium versus the max loss on a naked put

Price the position exactly. Stock at \$100, sell the one-month \$93-strike put at 30% implied vol, 4% rate. The Black-Scholes pricer gives a put value of about **\$0.8736 per share**, so per 100-share contract you collect a premium of `0.8736 × 100 = \$87.36`. The notional is `93 × 100 = \$9,300`.

Now walk the downside. If the stock simply drifts to \$93 (the strike) by expiry, the put expires worthless and you keep the whole \$87.36 — a clean win. If instead the stock gaps down to \$75 — a 25% drop, well within what a single name does on a bad earnings report or a market shock — the put is \$18 in the money at expiry. Your loss is `(93 − 75 − 0.8736) × 100 = \$1,712.64`. You collected \$87 and you are out \$1,713 — a loss **nineteen times** the premium you collected, on a single ordinary gap. If the stock crashes to \$60 (a 40% drop, a crisis move), the put is \$33 in the money and you lose `(93 − 60 − 0.8736) × 100 = \$3,212.64` — **thirty-seven times** the premium. The premium you "risked" was \$87; the position risked thousands.

The intuition: when you sell an option, the premium is the rent you collect, and the max loss is the house that can burn down. You size to the house, never to the rent.

## Risk of ruin: the math of a small edge with a fat tail

Now we can state the central danger precisely. Short premium is a strategy with **positive expectancy** (you make money on average — that is the variance risk premium, real and durable) but a **negative skew** payoff: you win small, often, and lose big, rarely. This combination — positive edge, fat left tail — is exactly the combination where *risk of ruin* becomes the binding constraint, because the strategy's average return tells you nothing about whether you survive the path.

**Risk of ruin** is the probability that a sequence of bets drives your capital below a level from which you cannot recover — a margin call you can't meet, a drawdown so deep that the compounding math can never climb back, or literally zero. It is a *path* property, not an *average* property. Two strategies with the identical expected return can have wildly different risks of ruin, because ruin depends on the worst point of the path, not the endpoint.

The classic intuition comes from the gambler's-ruin problem. Imagine a gambler making a long sequence of bets, each with a small positive edge, against a house with effectively infinite capital. Even with a positive edge per bet, if the gambler bets too large a fraction of a *finite* bankroll, a run of bad luck can hit zero before the edge has time to compound — and zero is absorbing. You cannot bet your way back from zero. The mathematics is brutal and counterintuitive: a positive edge does **not** guarantee survival; only a positive edge *combined with sufficiently small bet sizing* does. This is the deep result that connects gambling theory to options sizing, and it is developed rigorously in [the Kelly criterion and sequential betting](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/kelly-criterion-sequential-betting-quant-interviews).

Short-vol makes the gambler's-ruin problem worse in two specific ways. First, the loss is not a fixed bet size — it is a *random variable with a fat tail*, so a single bad outcome can be many times larger than your "expected" loss. Second, the losses are *correlated with leverage and forced selling*: the bad month is exactly the month margin requirements spike and you are forced to cover at the worst prices. The textbook gambler can always make the same-size bet next round; the over-sized option seller often *cannot*, because the tail event itself shrinks his capital and raises his margin simultaneously.

There is a third, subtler reason short vol is more ruin-prone than a clean coin-flip game: the *drawdown asymmetry* of compounding. Losses and gains do not cancel symmetrically. A 50% loss requires a 100% gain to recover; a 75% loss requires a 300% gain; a 90% loss requires a 900% gain. The deeper the drawdown, the more violently disproportionate the recovery required, which is the mathematical reason a single deep tail can effectively end a strategy even if it technically leaves you with a positive balance. A short-vol book that loses 80% in a Volmageddon is not "down 80% and recovering" — it now needs to *quintuple* just to get back to even, and it must do so with the smaller capital base that the tail left it, while the richest premiums (which arrive right after the shock) are being harvested by the survivors who were sized to stay whole. Ruin, in practice, is not only the literal zero; it is any drawdown deep enough that the recovery math becomes hopeless. That threshold is far above zero — which is why serious sizing targets a *maximum drawdown*, not merely a non-zero balance.

This is the precise sense in which risk of ruin is the binding constraint and expected return is a distraction. A negatively-skewed strategy can post a beautiful expected return and a beautiful Sharpe ratio over any sample that does not happen to contain the tail — the Sharpe ratio measures the small wiggles of the quiet months and is structurally blind to the cliff, which is why short-vol products routinely advertise Sharpe ratios above 2 right up until they detonate. The expected return is computed across the *full* distribution including the tail, but if you size to the expected return you are implicitly assuming you will be present and solvent for enough repetitions that the average asserts itself — and the whole point of ruin is that you might not be. Sizing is the discipline of guaranteeing you survive enough repetitions for your positive edge to become your realized return rather than a statistic computed over a path you did not live to see.

#### Worked example: the risk of ruin of 1 versus 10 versus 40 naked contracts

Hold the strategy fixed and vary only the size. Account: \$100,000. Each month you sell `n` of the \$93-strike naked puts from the previous example, collecting \$87.36 per contract. Assume eleven ordinary months where the puts expire worthless (you keep the premium) and **one** tail month where the stock gaps to \$75, costing \$1,712.64 per contract. This is a deliberately *mild* tail — a 25% gap, not a crash — and the strategy still has positive premium income across the eleven good months.

| Contracts | Carry (11 good months) | One tail month | Net for the year | Tail as % of account |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | +\$961 | −\$1,713 | −\$752 | 1.7% |
| 10 | +\$9,610 | −\$17,126 | −\$7,517 | 17% |
| 40 | +\$38,439 | −\$68,506 | −\$30,067 | 69% |

At one contract, the tail month costs 1.7% of the account — survivable, a rounding error. At ten, the tail costs 17% — a serious drawdown but recoverable. At forty, a *single ordinary gap month* costs 69% of the account. And remember this is the *mild* tail. Re-run the forty-contract case with a crash to \$60 instead of a gap to \$75: the loss per contract is \$3,212.64, so forty contracts lose `3,212.64 × 40 = \$128,506` — **more than the entire account.** That is ruin in the literal sense: the position can lose more money than you have, which means a margin call, forced liquidation, and possibly a debit balance you owe the broker.

The intuition: the edge (the \$961 of annual carry per contract) scales *linearly* with size, but so does the tail loss — and once the tail loss crosses 100% of capital, no amount of edge saves you, because ruin is absorbing. Size sets which side of that line you live on.

### Three sizes, one edge, three fates

The table above is a single year with a single tail month, which makes the arithmetic clean but hides the *path*. Compound the strategy over several years and the difference between sizes stops being a percentage and becomes a fork in the road: one path compounds, one survives, one dies. The intuition is that the oversized book looks like the *best* performer right up until the month it ceases to exist. The figure below runs the identical monthly return stream — same edge, same luck, same sequence of good and bad months — at three leverage multiples.

![Three equity curves at conservative, full-Kelly, and oversized sizes with the oversized one blowing up](/imgs/blogs/position-sizing-and-risk-of-ruin-in-options-trading-3.png)

The three curves share *everything* except size. The green conservative book (0.35× the unit position) grinds steadily upward; the amber full-Kelly book (1.0×) is a roller-coaster but ends ahead; the red oversized book (2.2×) climbs the fastest and highest — it is the star of the group chat for two years — and then meets the scheduled tail month at month 24, a single −42%-at-unit-size move, and collapses from roughly two-and-a-half times its starting equity down to less than a quarter of it. An 80%-plus drawdown in one month. The oversized book does not just give back its gains; it falls so far that the compounding math cannot recover, because climbing back from a 20-cents-on-the-dollar account requires a 400% gain just to break even. Meanwhile the conservative book absorbed the *identical* tail as a modest dip and kept compounding. The cruel irony is on full display: for twenty-three of twenty-four months the oversized trader was winning, and his "edge" was identical to the survivor's. The only difference was size, and size is what killed him.

Notice also that the oversized book was *the most attractive* for the entire pre-crash period. This is why oversizing is so seductive and so common: the punishment is invisible until the exact moment it is total. There is no warning shock, no gradual deterioration that lets you reduce in time — the negative-skew payoff delivers its entire downside in a single print. You do not get to learn from a small version of the disaster; the first time you experience the tail at oversized leverage, it is already fatal. This is the structural reason you must size for a tail you have *not yet seen*, using the worst plausible move rather than the worst move in your track record.

### Why "high win rate" is not "safe"

The most seductive lie in option selling is the win rate. A short-premium strategy can genuinely win 85%, 90%, even 95% of the time — most months the options expire worthless and you keep the premium. New sellers see a 90% win rate and conclude the strategy is safe. It is not. Win rate and expectancy are *different things*, and for negatively-skewed payoffs they can point in opposite directions.

![Expectancy of a 90 percent win-rate strategy turning negative as the tail loss grows](/imgs/blogs/position-sizing-and-risk-of-ruin-in-options-trading-6.png)

Conceptually, the figure holds the win rate fixed at 90% — you win one unit in nine months out of ten — and asks: how big does the tenth month's loss have to be before the *expectancy* (the average outcome) turns negative? The math is one line. With a 90% chance of winning +1 and a 10% chance of losing −L, expectancy is:

```
E  =  0.90 × (+1)  +  0.10 × (−L)  =  0.9 − 0.1·L
```

This crosses zero at `L = 9`. So any tail loss bigger than nine times the typical win makes a *90%-win-rate strategy* a guaranteed money-loser over time — the green region (positive expectancy, small tail) flips to the red region (negative expectancy) the moment the tail grows past nine. And nine-times-the-win is not exotic for short vol: we just computed a naked put that collects \$87 and loses \$1,713 on an ordinary gap — that is a tail nearly **twenty times** the win, deep in the red region, *with a win rate well above 90%*. The win rate is high precisely *because* the strategy is short the tail; the rare loss is large precisely *because* it is the insurance claim. High win rate and large tail loss are not in tension — they are the *same feature* of the payoff seen from two angles.

This is why the win rate is worse than useless as a safety gauge: it is highest for the strategies that are most dangerous. A coin-flip directional bet wins 50% of the time and has no tail; a naked-put income strategy wins 92% of the time and has a catastrophic tail. The higher win rate is the *more* dangerous strategy. Stop looking at win rate. Look at expectancy, and then look at the tail that produces the expectancy, and then size for the tail.

### The naked-premium ruin: the one gap that wipes the account

There is a specific death that deserves its own treatment because it is the most common end for retail option sellers and the recurring villain of every short-vol post-mortem: **selling unsized naked options.** Naked means uncovered — you sell a put or a call without owning the offsetting position or buying a protective wing, so your downside is, for practical purposes, unbounded. The trade is seductive because the income is real and frequent and the losses are absent for long stretches, which trains the seller to scale up. Each month of premium that arrives without incident is, perversely, evidence in favor of selling *more*, until the account is one gap away from zero and the seller does not know it.

Trace the exact mechanism of the wipe-out. A seller with a \$100,000 account has sold naked puts steadily for two years, collecting the \$87.36-per-contract premium from our running example, and — emboldened by twenty-four clean months — has scaled to 50 contracts, which feels fine because "I'm only collecting \$4,368 a month and the puts are 18-delta, they almost never get touched." Then a Monday opens with the underlying gapped down 30% on a sector shock. The puts that were 18-delta on Friday are now deep in-the-money and screaming toward their max loss; the seller's mark-to-market loss is `50 × \$1,712.64 ≈ \$85,632` if the move is a gap to \$75, wiping out 86% of the account in a single session. But it is worse than the mark, because the gap *also* spikes implied volatility, so the seller is hit by dollar-vega on top of the price loss, and the broker — seeing the position now massively underwater and far more volatile — raises the margin requirement at the exact moment the equity collapsed. The seller cannot meet the call. The broker liquidates the puts at the worst intraday prices, locking in the loss near the bottom. Twenty-four months of patient premium, gone in one open, and possibly a debit balance owed on top.

The lesson is not "never sell naked" — disciplined desks sell naked premium profitably for decades. The lesson is that naked options have *no built-in max loss*, so the entire burden of bounding the downside falls on *your* sizing, and the only honest size is one where the worst plausible gap is a dent. If 50 contracts means an 86% account loss on a single gap, the position was never 50 contracts of income; it was a 50-contract bet that no gap would arrive before the seller got out, and that bet has a fixed, eventual expiry date called "the next crash." The defined-risk alternative — buy a protective wing to convert the naked put into a spread, capping the loss at a knowable number — gives up a few cents of premium to make the max loss finite and therefore sizeable. After living through one naked wipe-out, every survivor decides the wing was the cheapest insurance they ever bought.

## Kelly for options: why full Kelly will kill a short-vol book

If sizing is the whole game, is there an optimal size? There is a famous answer — the **Kelly criterion** — and understanding both what it says and where it breaks for options is essential. We will not re-derive Kelly from scratch (that is done in [position sizing and the Kelly criterion](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/position-sizing-and-kelly-criterion) and the [sequential-betting](/blog/trading/quantitative-finance/kelly-criterion-sequential-betting-quant-interviews) treatment); we will adapt it honestly to negatively-skewed option payoffs.

Kelly answers a precise question: what fraction `f` of your capital should you bet each round to maximize the *long-run geometric growth rate* of your wealth? The key word is geometric, not arithmetic. Maximizing your average *return* would tell you to bet everything; maximizing your *compound growth* — which is what actually builds wealth over a sequence of bets — tells you to bet much less, because a single total loss zeroes the compound forever. Kelly maximizes `E[log(1 + f·X)]`, where `X` is the per-unit payoff. The log is the mathematical expression of "ruin is unrecoverable": `log(0)` is negative infinity, so the formula refuses to risk total loss.

For a simple symmetric or near-symmetric bet, Kelly has the clean approximation `f* = μ / σ²` — the optimal fraction is the mean return divided by the variance. This is the "Gaussian Kelly," and it is what most people reach for. **It is wrong for short vol**, and dangerously so, for one reason: it keeps only the mean and the variance, which is to say it treats the payoff distribution as if it were symmetric. A short-vol payoff is the opposite of symmetric — it has a fat left tail and a capped right side. The Gaussian formula does not see the tail, so it recommends a bet size that the tail will not survive.

![Long-run growth rate versus bet fraction for an asymmetric short-vol payoff against the naive Gaussian Kelly](/imgs/blogs/position-sizing-and-risk-of-ruin-in-options-trading-5.png)

The figure makes the gap visible. Both curves plot the long-run growth rate `g(f)` against the bet fraction `f` for the same short-vol payoff — win +0.4 units with probability 0.85, lose −2.0 units with probability 0.15 (a thin positive edge with a fat left tail). The blue curve uses the *true* payoff; the amber dashed curve is the Gaussian approximation that keeps only the mean and variance. Two things matter. First, the true optimum (the peak of the blue curve, the true Kelly fraction) sits at a *tiny* `f* ≈ 0.05` — Kelly is already telling you to risk only 5% of capital on this thin-edge, fat-tail bet. Second, the amber Gaussian curve sits *above* the blue one for every over-bet and keeps recommending you push bigger, because it cannot see the cliff. The blue curve falls off a cliff just past its peak — the red-shaded ruin zone — because over-betting a fat tail destroys compound growth violently. The Gaussian approximation is blind to that cliff. Follow it and you bet into the red zone thinking you are still near optimal.

And here is the part that even Kelly purists get wrong for options: **even the true full-Kelly fraction is too aggressive in practice.** Full Kelly is optimal only if you know the true probabilities *exactly* and the distribution is *stationary*. For short vol, you do not know the tail probability (how often is the next Volmageddon?) and the distribution is emphatically not stationary (correlations and vol regimes shift). Full Kelly also produces gut-wrenching drawdowns — its expected maximum drawdown is enormous even when it never ruins you — and worse, *over-estimating your edge with full Kelly pushes you past the peak into the ruin zone*. The standard professional response is **fractional Kelly**: bet a half, a quarter, or even less of the Kelly fraction. Half-Kelly captures about 75% of the growth with far less than half the drawdown; quarter-Kelly is more conservative still. For a fat-tailed, non-stationary, edge-uncertain strategy like short vol, **quarter-Kelly or smaller is the honest default.**

#### Worked example: a fractional-Kelly size for a short-vol edge

Take the short-vol bet from the figure: each "unit" of the strategy wins +0.4 with probability 0.85 and loses −2.0 with probability 0.15. First, confirm the edge is real. Expectancy per unit is `0.85 × 0.4 + 0.15 × (−2.0) = 0.34 − 0.30 = +0.04` — a thin but genuinely positive edge. Now compute Kelly.

The naive Gaussian Kelly uses `f* = μ / σ²`. The mean is μ = 0.04. The variance is `σ² = 0.85 × (0.4 − 0.04)² + 0.15 × (−2.0 − 0.04)² = 0.85 × 0.1296 + 0.15 × 4.1616 = 0.110 + 0.624 = 0.734`. So the Gaussian recommendation is `f = 0.04 / 0.734 = 0.054`, or 5.4% of capital.

The true skew-aware Kelly maximizes `E[log(1 + f·X)]` over the actual two-state payoff, which (solving numerically) peaks at **f* ≈ 0.050** — slightly smaller than the Gaussian, and the curve drops off a cliff for any larger `f`. Now apply fractional Kelly. Quarter-Kelly is `0.050 / 4 ≈ 0.0125`, or **1.25% of capital risked per unit of this strategy.** That is the honest size: it captures most of the growth, it sits far to the left of the ruin cliff, and it leaves room for the very real possibility that your estimate of the 15% tail probability is too optimistic.

The intuition: Kelly tells you the *most* you could bet; survival tells you to bet a *fraction* of that, because you do not know the tail as well as the formula pretends you do.

## Sizing rules in practice: from a single trade to the whole book

Theory says "size to survive the tail." Practice needs concrete, daily rules. Here is the framework professionals actually use, built from the single trade outward to the portfolio.

![Sizing decision framework with max loss, Greek limits, and reserve for the tail](/imgs/blogs/position-sizing-and-risk-of-ruin-in-options-trading-4.png)

The framework is three gates, and a position size is the *smallest* size that passes all three — you take the binding constraint, not the average. The intuition is that any one gate can be the one that kills you, so the safe size is whichever is tightest, never a compromise between them.

**Gate 1 — Max loss per trade.** Cap the loss on any single trade at a small fraction of capital, typically 1–2%. For a defined-risk structure this is trivial: `max loss = strike width − net credit`, a hard number. For a naked structure, you have to *define* the max loss by a bad-day scenario (a 2- or 3-standard-deviation gap) and size off that. This gate alone forces you toward defined-risk structures, because a naked position's honest max loss is so large that the 1–2% rule permits very few contracts.

**Gate 2 — Portfolio-level Greek limits.** A single trade can pass Gate 1 and still blow up the book if the book is already loaded with the same exposure. So you set hard caps on the *aggregate* Greeks: a maximum net vega (total dollars lost per vol point across all positions) and a maximum net gamma (total P&L acceleration per 1% market move). A new trade is only allowed if the book still fits under both caps after adding it. This is the gate that stops you from quietly accumulating a concentrated short-vol position one "small" trade at a time. The dashboard that aggregates these is [the net Greeks of a position](/blog/trading/options-volatility/the-net-greeks-of-a-position-building-your-risk-dashboard).

**Gate 3 — Reserve for the tail.** Even a book that passes Gates 1 and 2 day-to-day can be destroyed by a true tail event, because the Greeks are *local* — they describe small moves, and gamma means the losses on a big move are far worse than the linear Greeks predict. So you hold an explicit reserve: cash you do not deploy, and/or tail hedges (far-OTM puts) bought on top of the book. The test is concrete: assume a Volmageddon-scale gap prints *next month*, and confirm the book survives it without a forced cover.

#### Worked example: sizing a defined-risk spread by max loss versus by percent of capital

Compare the two sizing logics on a defined-risk trade. You sell a one-month put credit spread: short the \$95 put, long the \$90 put, on a \$100 stock at 20% vol. From the Black-Scholes pricer the \$95 put is worth \$0.518 and the \$90 put is worth \$0.063, so the net credit is `0.518 − 0.063 = \$0.455` per share, or **\$45.47 per spread** (×100). The strike width is \$5, so the **max loss per spread** is `(5 − 0.455) × 100 = \$454.53` — a hard, knowable number, because the long \$90 put caps the downside.

Now size it two ways on a \$100,000 account. **By premium** (the wrong way): "I collected \$45 per spread, I want \$4,500 of income, so I'll sell 100 spreads." That puts on `100 × \$454.53 = \$45,453` of max loss — 45% of the account at risk on one trade. **By max loss / percent of capital** (the right way): "I will risk at most 2% of capital, or \$2,000, on this trade. Each spread risks \$454.53 of max loss, so I can sell `2,000 / 454.53 ≈ 4 spreads`." Four spreads, not a hundred. The premium-based size was **25 times too large.** The defined-risk structure made the max loss knowable; the percent-of-capital rule turned that knowable max loss into a position size. The premium never entered the calculation, which is exactly right.

The intuition: a defined-risk structure hands you the max loss for free; your only job is to divide your risk budget by it.

#### Worked example: a portfolio vega limit and how many contracts it permits

Now size by Gate 2. Suppose your risk policy says the *whole book* may lose at most \$5,000 if implied volatility across your positions rises by 10 vol points at once (a plausible single-day vol shock). That is a net-vega cap of `5,000 / 10 = \$500 per vol point`. Each naked \$93-strike put from earlier carries a dollar-vega of \$7.54 per vol point (and as the seller you are *short* vega, so a vol rise hurts you). How many can you hold? `500 / 7.54 ≈ 66 contracts` — but only if vega is your binding constraint.

Check it against Gate 1. Sixty-six naked puts have a combined max loss (to a crash at \$60) of `66 × \$3,212.64 = \$212,034` — more than twice a \$100,000 account. Gate 1 (max loss) is far tighter than Gate 2 (vega) here, so Gate 1 binds: the naked-put book is limited to a handful of contracts by its catastrophic max loss, long before the vega cap matters. This is the framework working as designed — you compute all three gates and take the *smallest* permitted size. For a naked book, max loss almost always binds; for a defined-risk book where max loss is capped, the Greek limits often become the binding constraint instead.

The intuition: the gates are not alternatives, they are a minimum — the safe size is whichever gate screams first.

### Correlation: why a book of short puts is one bet

The most dangerous hidden risk in a short-vol book is *correlation*. Sell a put on Apple, another on Microsoft, another on Nvidia, another on a bank ETF, another on the S&P 500 itself — it *feels* diversified, twelve small independent positions on different underlyings. It is not. In a crash, equity correlations go to one: everything falls together, every put goes in-the-money together, and your "diversified" book reveals itself as one enormous short-vol, short-correlation bet.

![Many short puts on correlated names collapsing into one short-vol position in a crash](/imgs/blogs/position-sizing-and-risk-of-ruin-in-options-trading-7.png)

In calm markets the names wiggle somewhat independently and the book genuinely looks like the left side of the figure — eight small, separate bets. But the diversification is an illusion conditional on calm, because every one of those puts is *short the same market*. When the crash comes (the amber node — correlation goes to one) the book collapses into the single red position on the right: all puts in-the-money at once, net gamma and net vega *stacking* rather than canceling. This is the precise mechanism by which a "diversified" book of short premium becomes a concentrated short-correlation bet exactly when you can least afford it. The same single-name-versus-index correlation dynamic is the entire subject of [dispersion and correlation trading](/blog/trading/options-volatility/dispersion-and-correlation-trading-index-vol-vs-single-name-vol) — selling a book of single-name puts is, whether you intend it or not, a short-dispersion, short-correlation position.

The practical rule: when you sum your Greeks for Gate 2, do *not* assume your positions diversify. Stress the whole book as if every correlated underlying moves together, because in the tail it will. Real diversification for a short-vol book means spreading across genuinely different *asset classes* (equity vol, rates vol, commodity vol, FX vol — things that do not all crash on the same day) and across *expiries* (so a single event window does not catch your entire book), or holding an explicit index tail hedge on top. Selling puts on twelve tech stocks is not diversification; it is the same bet twelve times.

The deeper point is that correlation itself is a *regime variable*, not a constant, and it moves against you exactly when it matters. In calm markets the pairwise correlation among single names might be 0.3 or 0.4, low enough that a naive risk model — one that plugs in the average correlation — reports a comfortably diversified book. But correlation is not stationary: in a crash it converges toward 1, because in a true panic investors sell *everything* indiscriminately to raise cash, and idiosyncratic stories stop mattering. So the very risk model that reassured you in the calm under-states your tail risk by precisely the amount correlation rises in the crash — it tells you the book is diversified using the correlation that holds when you don't need diversification, and it is silent about the correlation that holds when you do. This is why stressing the book at correlation-equals-one is not paranoia; it is using the correct number for the scenario you are sizing against. A short-vol book's worst case is, by construction, a correlated-crash scenario, and in that scenario the diversification benefit you penciled in simply is not there.

There is also a Greek-stacking subtlety worth making explicit. When you sum dollar-vega and dollar-gamma across positions, *correlated short positions add rather than offset*. Two short puts on names that move together contribute vega and gamma in the same direction, so the book's net short-vega and net short-gamma are the simple sum — there is no hedging benefit from holding "different" names that are really the same bet. A model that allowed correlation-based netting would let you carry a larger book under the same Greek caps; using correlation-equals-one in the tail correctly *forbids* that netting, which is the whole point. The honest book-level Greeks for sizing purposes are the un-netted, everything-moves-together sums, and they should be checked against your caps before every new trade. Genuine offset only comes from positions that are *structurally* opposite — a long-vol tail hedge against a short-vol book — not from spreading the same short-vol exposure across correlated tickers.

## Common misconceptions

**"My risk is the premium I collected."** No — for a seller the premium is what you *receive*, and it is almost unrelated to what you can *lose*. We priced a naked \$93 put that collects \$87.36 and loses \$1,712.64 on an ordinary 25% gap and \$3,212.64 in a crash — a loss 19 to 37 times the premium. Sizing by premium ("I'm only risking \$87") is how traders end up with a million dollars of max loss against a hundred-thousand-dollar account. Your risk is the max loss and the Greeks; the premium is the rent, not the house.

**"A 90% win rate means the strategy is safe."** A high win rate is the *signature* of a fat-tailed, negatively-skewed payoff, not evidence of safety. We showed that a 90%-win-rate strategy turns negative-expectancy the instant the tail loss exceeds nine times the typical win — and short-vol tails routinely run twenty times the win. The strategies with the highest win rates (naked premium selling) are precisely the ones with the most dangerous tails. Win rate is highest exactly where it is most misleading. Track expectancy and the size of the tail, never the win rate.

**"My strategy has positive expectancy, so I can't go broke — I'll just compound."** Positive expectancy is necessary for survival but nowhere near sufficient. Risk of ruin is a *path* property: a positive-edge strategy bet too large hits a margin call or an unrecoverable drawdown before the edge compounds, and zero is absorbing. We saw the identical positive-edge short-vol strategy go from ~0% risk of ruin (conservative size) to majority-likely ruin (oversized) with *no change to the edge*. Expectancy tells you the destination; sizing decides whether you survive the journey.

**"Full Kelly is the mathematically optimal size, so I should use it."** Full Kelly is optimal only under assumptions short vol violates: known probabilities and a stationary, well-behaved distribution. The Gaussian Kelly formula `μ/σ²` keeps only mean and variance and is blind to the fat left tail, so it over-bets — it sits above the true growth curve and pushes you toward the ruin cliff. Even the *true* skew-aware Kelly produces brutal drawdowns and punishes any over-estimate of your edge. Professionals use fractional Kelly — quarter-Kelly or less for fat-tailed strategies — which captures most of the growth with a fraction of the drawdown.

**"Selling puts on lots of different stocks diversifies my risk."** In a crash, equity correlations go to one and every short put goes in-the-money together; a book of single-name short puts is one big short-vol, short-correlation bet that detonates as a unit. When you set portfolio Greek limits, stress the book as if everything moves together. Genuine diversification means spreading across asset classes and expiries, or buying a tail hedge — not selling the same equity-beta bet twelve times under twelve tickers.

## How it shows up in real markets

**February 2018 — Volmageddon.** The canonical case of size, not edge, killing traders. Through 2017 the VIX averaged just **11.1**, its lowest year on record, and capital crowded into short-volatility products that had paid pennies steadily for years. The edge was real; the carry was extraordinary; the tail had not printed. On February 5, 2018, a modest equity sell-off spiked the VIX to a **37.32** close — roughly doubling intraday. Products that were *mechanically* short vol had to buy it back as it rose, and that forced covering pushed vol higher still, a self-amplifying loop. One inverse-VIX exchange-traded note lost more than 90% of its value overnight and was wound down. The traders who blew up were not wrong about the edge — short vol had genuinely paid for years. They were wrong about the size: they had sold so much premium that the inevitable tail exceeded their capital. The detailed post-mortem is in the [Volmageddon 2018 case study](/blog/trading/options-volatility/case-study-volmageddon-2018-and-the-short-vol-blowup); the sizing lesson is that the same trade was a dent for the survivors and a death for the oversized.

**March 2020 — the COVID crash.** The largest VIX print in the data — an **82.69** close on March 16, 2020. Realized volatility exploded: the S&P 500 had multiple single-day moves above 9%, each one a gap that crushed any short-gamma book. Short-vol positions carried into the crash with no tail reserve were destroyed in the first two weeks. But the survivors — those sized so the crash was a survivable dent — then harvested one of the richest short-vol windows of the decade, because after the shock implied vol stayed elevated and richly bid while realized began to calm. The cruel arithmetic of the trade is that the fattest premiums appear right after most short-vol capital has been wiped out. Surviving the tail is the precondition for collecting the premium that follows it.

**August 2024 — the yen carry unwind.** A milder rerun. On August 5, 2024, an unwind of the yen carry trade spiked the VIX to a **38.57** close before subsiding within days. The difference between a dent and a death was usually not the size of the spike but whether the position was sized to *hold through* it without a forced cover. Books that could ride out the gap recovered as vol normalized; over-leveraged books that were forced to cover locked in the loss at the bottom. Same shock, opposite outcomes — decided before the event, by size.

**The long quiet between.** It is worth emphasizing the years that never make the news. Across 2013, 2014, 2017, 2019, 2021, 2023, and 2024, selling vol paid steadily and the carry was real. A *correctly sized* short-vol book compounded handsomely across all of it — including the 2018, 2020, and 2024 hits — precisely because the hits were dents and not deaths. The strategy is not doomed; it is an insurance business, and an insurance business that reserves properly for catastrophe is one of the most durable franchises in finance. The ones that go bankrupt are the ones that wrote more policy than their capital could cover.

## The playbook: how to size so you survive the tail

This is where mechanism becomes a daily discipline. If you are going to warehouse the variance risk premium — and it is a legitimate, durable edge — here is the sizing playbook that separates the survivors from the case studies.

**The position.** Start defined-risk, always. An iron condor or a put credit spread, not a naked strangle, so your max loss is a hard number you divide your risk budget by — see [iron condors and credit spreads](/blog/trading/options-volatility/iron-condors-and-credit-spreads-selling-the-range). The gentlest naked entry is the cash-secured put, because your worst case is owning the stock lower, a purchase you may have wanted anyway — covered in [cash-secured puts](/blog/trading/options-volatility/cash-secured-puts-getting-paid-to-buy-lower). Graduate to less-defended structures only as your capital, your hedging discipline, and your scar tissue grow.

**Define risk by max loss and Greeks, never by premium.** Before every trade, write down three numbers: the premium you collect, the max loss (computed for defined-risk, scenario'd for naked), and the dollar-gamma and dollar-vega the position adds. Size off the second and third, never the first. If you cannot state your max loss in dollars, you cannot size the trade and you should not put it on.

**Set the tail budget first, then size to it.** Decide up front the worst single month you can absorb without a forced cover or a breach — say 10–15% of capital. Then size the *whole book* so a Volmageddon-scale move stays inside that budget. Assume the tail prints *next month*, not in five years, and verify you survive it. If the honest answer is "I'd be down 70% and margin-called," you are too big regardless of how good the carry looks. The math of how drawdowns destroy compound growth is in the [tail-risk and extreme value theory](/blog/trading/math-for-quants/tail-risk-extreme-value-theory-math-for-quants) treatment.

**Use fractional Kelly, not full.** Kelly tells you the most you could bet; bet a fraction of it — quarter-Kelly or less for a fat-tailed, non-stationary, edge-uncertain strategy like short vol. The Gaussian Kelly formula is blind to the tail and will over-bet; the true Kelly produces unbearable drawdowns; fractional Kelly captures most of the growth with a fraction of the pain and leaves room for your edge estimate to be wrong. The full derivation is in [position sizing and the Kelly criterion](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/position-sizing-and-kelly-criterion).

**Cap the book's Greeks and stress correlations.** Set hard portfolio limits on net vega (per vol point) and net gamma (per 1% move), and refuse any trade that breaches them. When you sum the Greeks, stress the book as if every correlated underlying moves together, because in the tail it will — a book of single-name short puts is one short-correlation bet. Build the live dashboard from [the net Greeks of a position](/blog/trading/options-volatility/the-net-greeks-of-a-position-building-your-risk-dashboard).

**Reserve for the tail explicitly.** Hold cash you do not deploy and/or far-OTM tail hedges on top of the book. Yes, the hedge costs carry — that is the point; you are buying the right to survive. A fully naked book with no reserve is not a more profitable version of a hedged book; it is a hedged book that will eventually be liquidated.

**The invalidation.** Your sizing is invalidated the moment the book can no longer survive the tail you budgeted for — because the position grew, because correlations tightened, or because realized vol started overshooting the implied you sold (the regime where short vol has no edge). When the tail budget is breached, *reduce*, even though reducing mid-shock feels most painful. Doubling down into a vol spike to "earn it back" is the classic terminal mistake — it is selling more insurance as the hurricane makes landfall. The discipline of when to roll, adjust, or just take the loss is the subject of [managing a trade](/blog/trading/options-volatility/managing-a-trade-rolling-adjusting-and-when-to-just-take-the-loss).

**The mindset.** Run it like an insurance company, because it is one. Collect steady premiums, reserve explicitly for the catastrophe, diversify across genuinely uncorrelated risks, never let one event window dominate the book, and build the bad month into the plan rather than praying it stays away. Your job is not to avoid the tail — the tail is the price of admission to the premium. Your job is to make sure that when it arrives, it is a dent your book absorbs and recovers from, not the death that ends the strategy. Get the sizing right and short vol is one of the most durable edges in markets. Get it wrong, with the very same edge, and you are the second trader from the hook — the one who sized for maximum return and blew up on the inevitable tail.
