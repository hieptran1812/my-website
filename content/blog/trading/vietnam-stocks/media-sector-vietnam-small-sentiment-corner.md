> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Media is the smallest and most speculative corner of VN-Index: a handful of small, illiquid, story-driven names whose prices are driven far more by *sentiment, low free float and narrative* than by steady, growing earnings.
>
> - It is roughly **half a percent of VN-Index market cap** — versus about 34% for banks. The whole sector is smaller than a single mid-cap bank, so it barely moves the index, and the index barely supports it: there is almost no institutional buyer of last resort underneath these stocks.
> - The names split into two very different animals. **Traditional media and advertising** (small broadcasters and ad firms like Yeah1, YEG) sell *ad spend × audience* — a discretionary, cyclical business where revenue and profit swing hard with the economy. The **internet/platform names** (the VNG / VNZ group: games, Zalo, cloud, AI) have big revenue but are *often loss-making by design*, funding growth, and are genuinely hard to value with normal tools.
> - The reason prices are *violent* is mechanical, not mystical: small market cap **plus** low free float **plus** a story means a little buying or selling moves the price a lot, in both directions. A stock with a 10% float reacts to an order roughly six times harder than one with a 60% float.
> - The number to remember: **size it at 1-2% of your portfolio, never more.** A violent low-float name can halve on a single bad print; at a 1% position a 50% drop costs you 0.5% of your capital, at a 10% position it costs you 5%. The playbook here is *small, high-risk theme trade with a hard invalidation — not a core holding you marry.*

In the spring of 2024, a small media name on the Ho Chi Minh Stock Exchange (HOSE) did something that looked, for a few weeks, like genius. The company had spent years as a forgotten micro-cap — a thin, sleepy ticker that traded a few hundred million dong a day, the kind of stock most institutional desks do not even have a model for. Then a story arrived. Depending on the week it was a content partnership, a pivot into short-form video, a rumored tie-up with a bigger platform, a whisper about a new shareholder. The narrative did not need to be true, or even checkable; it only needed to be *exciting*. And on that excitement, with almost no shares actually available to buy, the stock went up in a near-vertical line — doubling in a matter of weeks, ceiling-locked (hitting the daily upper price limit) day after day, the message boards euphoric, the chart a thing of beauty.

Then it collapsed. The story did not pan out, or the early buyers simply decided to ring the register, and the same thin float that had carried the price up with so little buying now carried it down with so little selling. The stock gave back the entire move and then some, on volume that vanished as fast as it had appeared, leaving a fresh crowd of late buyers holding a position they could not exit without crushing the price themselves. Nobody's earnings had changed. The "value" the market had discovered and then un-discovered was never about cash flow at all — it was about *sentiment meeting scarcity*. That round trip, from forgotten to euphoric to abandoned in a single quarter, is the single most honest portrait of Vietnam's media sector you will ever see.

This post is about that corner of the market: why it behaves the way it does, what is actually inside it, and how a sensible investor should treat it. We will build the media business model from zero — how traditional media really makes money (advertising times audience, and why that is discretionary and cyclical), and the very different internet/platform model where a company can have enormous revenue and still lose money on purpose. We will get mechanical about *why* low float plus low liquidity makes prices violent, because that mechanism — not the stories — is the real driver. We will look honestly at how you even try to value a loss-making platform versus a cyclical ad firm. And then, as this series always does, we will land on a concrete playbook: how to size, when to engage, and exactly what tells you the trade is wrong.

![Flow diagram showing small market cap plus low free float plus a story leading a little buying or selling to move the price a lot in both directions](/imgs/blogs/media-sector-vietnam-small-sentiment-corner-1.png)

A quick word on money, because we will use it throughout. Vietnam trades in dong (VND), and every market cap, order size and price level you will meet here is quoted in dong. As a reference for readers who think in dollars, the exchange rate in mid-2026 is roughly **\$1 = 25,400 VND**. So a 2 billion dong buy order is about **\$79,000**; a 5 trillion dong company is about a **\$197mn** market cap; a small ad firm doing 800 billion dong of revenue is doing about **\$31mn**. We will keep the primary figures in dong, because that is how the market quotes them, and drop a dollar anchor whenever it helps you feel the size. Holding the dollar figures in mind also makes one thing immediately clear: by the standards of a global market, *every name in this sector is tiny.* The biggest of them would be a small-cap anywhere else; most are micro-caps that a single foreign fund could not build a meaningful position in without becoming the market.

## Foundations: how media actually makes money, and why it is two businesses

Before we touch a single price chart, we have to be precise about what these companies *do*, because the word "media" hides two completely different businesses that happen to share a sector label. One is old, cyclical and earnings-driven; the other is new, growth-driven and frequently loss-making. Confusing them is the single most common mistake beginners make in this corner, so let us build each from zero.

### Traditional media: advertising times audience

Start with the business that has existed for a century: **traditional media** — television channels, radio, newspapers, magazines, outdoor billboards, and the production companies that make the content that fills them. Ask how any of these makes money and the answer, almost always, is the same two words: **advertising**. A TV channel does not really sell television to viewers; it sells *viewers to advertisers*. The content — the drama, the game show, the news — is the bait that gathers an audience, and the audience is the product that gets sold. The same is true of a billboard (it sells the eyeballs of everyone who drives past), a newspaper (it sells the attention of its readers), and a YouTube channel or TikTok account (it sells the attention of its followers).

This gives traditional media a deceptively simple revenue formula. Revenue is, roughly, **audience size × advertising rate × volume of ads sold**. A channel with a bigger or more attractive audience can charge a higher rate per advertising slot and sell more slots. An advertising agency — a firm that *buys* ad space on behalf of brands and takes a cut, or *produces* the ads themselves — earns a fee or margin on the total *ad spend* it handles. Either way, the entire industry's fortunes rise and fall with one number: **total advertising spending in the economy** (Vietnamese: *chi tiêu quảng cáo*).

And here is the crucial property of that number, the one that determines everything about how the sector trades: **advertising spend is discretionary and cyclical.** It is *discretionary* because, for any individual company, the advertising budget is one of the easiest things to cut. When a consumer-goods firm or a bank or a property developer has a great year, it floods the airwaves and the feed with ads to grab market share. When it has a bad year — when the economy slows, when its own sales soften, when it needs to protect profit — the marketing budget is among the first lines slashed, because cutting ads does not immediately break anything the way cutting production or staff does. So advertising is *pro-cyclical* on steroids: it does not just follow the economy up and down, it *amplifies* the swing, because budgets balloon in good times and get gutted in bad ones.

That amplification is the heart of the traditional-media business and the reason its earnings are so volatile. We will quantify it with a worked example shortly, but hold the intuition now: an ad firm's revenue swings *more* than the economy, and its *profit* swings more than its revenue, because media businesses carry meaningful fixed costs — a channel still has to pay its staff and its broadcasting costs whether ad revenue is up 20% or down 20%. High fixed costs plus a volatile, discretionary top line is a recipe for *operating leverage*, the property whereby a modest move in revenue produces an outsized move in profit. That is why a traditional-media stock can look like a steady cash cow in a boom and a near-bankruptcy in a bust, on a revenue line that only moved a fraction as much.

You can see the cyclicality directly in the data. Vietnamese advertising spend does not grow in a smooth line; it lurches — strong growth in expansion years, an outright *contraction* in downturns, and a sharp snapback in recovery. Trace the path: solid double-digit growth in 2019, a *decline* in the 2020 shock, a tepid recovery in 2021, a strong bounce in 2022 as the economy reopened, another *dip* in the 2023 slowdown, then a return to mid-single-digit growth in 2024-2025. That is not the shape of a stable utility; it is the shape of a discretionary line item that brands flood in good times and gut in bad ones. And remember the amplification: a *negative* ad-spend year does not hit the ad firm's revenue by a few percent — it hits *profit* by a multiple, because the fixed costs do not fall with the revenue. The chart below is the macro backdrop; the operating-leverage worked example later turns it into an earnings collapse.

![Line chart of approximate Vietnam advertising spend growth from 2019 to 2025 showing positive years interrupted by declines in 2020 and 2023](/imgs/blogs/media-sector-vietnam-small-sentiment-corner-4.png)

It is worth dwelling on *why* advertising is so much more cyclical than the economy as a whole, because the same logic explains why media earnings are nearly impossible to forecast a year out. Advertising is what economists call a *second-derivative* business: it depends not on the *level* of the economy but on how *confident* companies feel about the *next* few quarters. A firm sets its marketing budget on the expectation of future sales, so when confidence wobbles even slightly — before any actual sales decline shows up — the budget gets trimmed pre-emptively. This makes ad spend a *leading* indicator that turns *down* before the broader slowdown is visible and *up* before the recovery is confirmed, and it makes the swings larger than the underlying economy because they are driven by *sentiment about the future*, which is always more volatile than the present. The traditional-media investor is therefore exposed to a double dose of sentiment: the company's *own* stock price is sentiment-driven through the thin-float mechanism, and its *earnings* are sentiment-driven through the advertising cycle. Two sentiment layers stacked on top of each other is a large part of why these names are so hard to hold.

### The internet/platform model: revenue without profit, by design

Now the other animal entirely. Sitting under the same "media" or "technology-media" umbrella in Vietnam is a very different kind of company: the **internet platform**. The flagship — really the only one of scale — is **VNG Corporation**, whose shares trade under the ticker **VNZ**. VNG is not a broadcaster; it is a sprawling internet company that runs **online games** (its original and still largest cash engine), **Zalo** (Vietnam's dominant messaging and super-app, used by tens of millions of people daily), a **cloud-and-data-centre** business, a **fintech/payments** arm (ZaloPay), and an increasingly visible push into **AI**. In scale of *revenue* and *users*, VNG dwarfs every traditional-media name in the country combined.

But here is what trips people up: **a platform like this can have huge revenue and still report a net loss — on purpose.** The logic is the opposite of a cash-cow broadcaster. A platform's core bet is that *future* profit is worth more than *current* profit, so it deliberately spends its present cash — and often more — on growth: building cloud infrastructure, subsidizing payments to win users, funding AI research, paying to acquire and retain customers. These are real costs that hit the income statement *now*, while the payoff they are buying — a dominant position in a market that might be enormous in five or ten years — shows up only *later*, if at all. So the company runs at a loss not because it is failing, but because it is *choosing* to convert profit into growth. This is the famous **loss-funding-growth** model, and it is the standard playbook of internet platforms worldwide, from the early years of Amazon to nearly every venture-backed tech company you have heard of.

It helps to see *where* the money goes, because that is what separates a loss-by-design platform from a company that is simply unprofitable. A platform's spending splits into two buckets. The first is *keeping the lights on* — running the games, the messaging app, the servers — and on its own, the mature parts of the business (games, in particular) typically generate cash. The second bucket is the *land grab*: building data centres before the cloud demand has fully arrived, subsidizing payments to pull users onto ZaloPay, funding AI research whose revenue is years away, and absorbing the early losses of new ventures. It is the *second* bucket that pushes the consolidated company into a net loss, and the key insight is that the company *could* turn profitable almost immediately by simply *stopping* the land grab — but it chooses not to, because it believes the land is worth more than the near-term profit it is forgoing. That is the difference between a loss-funding-growth platform and a failing business: the platform's losses are *discretionary investments in the future*, not a sign the core cannot pay its way. The catch, for an investor, is that you are being asked to *trust* that those investments will pay off — and that trust is exactly what sentiment grants and withdraws.

The consequence for an investor is profound and worth stating plainly: **you cannot value this kind of company with the tools you would use on a bank or an ad firm.** The standard yardstick, the **price-to-earnings ratio** (P/E — the stock price divided by earnings per share, i.e. how many dong you pay for each dong of annual profit), is *undefined* when there are no earnings. Divide by a loss and the number is negative or meaningless. So the whole valuation conversation has to shift to fuzzier, more forward-looking, more *faith-based* measures — revenue multiples, user counts, per-user values — every one of which requires you to *guess* about a future that has not happened. We will work through exactly how fragile that is later. For now, lock in the distinction:

- **Traditional media / advertising** (Yeah1/YEG, small broadcasters, ad agencies): sells *ad spend × audience*, earnings-driven, cyclical and discretionary, valued on a (volatile) P/E.
- **Internet platform** (VNG/VNZ): sells *games, messaging, cloud, payments, AI*, growth-driven, often loss-making by design, valued — if at all — on revenue multiples and user metrics.

### Why this is the smallest corner of the index

There is one more foundational fact, and it shapes everything: **media is a tiny share of VN-Index.** When you add up the market value of every listed media and advertising name and weigh it against the whole index, you get a sliver — on the order of **half a percent** of total VN-Index market capitalization as of 2026. Compare that to the giants: banks are roughly **34%** of the index, real estate about **17%**, food and beverage about **6%**, securities/brokers about **5%**, information technology about **3%**. Media sits at the very bottom of the list, smaller than almost anything else you can name.

![Horizontal bar chart of approximate VN-Index sector weights showing banks largest near 34 percent down to media smallest near half a percent](/imgs/blogs/media-sector-vietnam-small-sentiment-corner-2.png)

Why does this smallness matter so much? Two reasons, and they compound each other. First, **the sector barely moves the index** — you will never see "media" leading or lagging VN-Index the way banks or property do, because its weight is a rounding error. So sector rotation, the engine of this whole series, mostly *passes media by*; capital flows between banks, property, brokers, steel, but rarely makes a deliberate, index-level allocation into media. Second, and more important for how the stocks behave, **the index barely supports media.** In big sectors there is a deep bench of institutional money — funds, ETFs, insurance portfolios — that owns the names structurally and provides a floor of demand. In media there is almost none of that. These stocks are too small for most funds to bother with (a fund cannot put 2% of its assets into a company whose entire float is worth a few tens of millions of dollars), too illiquid to enter and exit cleanly, and too story-driven to fit a disciplined mandate. So there is no institutional buyer of last resort underneath them. When sentiment turns, there is nothing to catch the fall — which brings us to the mechanism that makes this corner *violent*.

The foreign-investor angle sharpens the point. Much of the structural, price-insensitive demand in Vietnamese large-caps comes from foreign funds and the ETFs that track the index — the flows we cover in the [foreign flows and the index effect post](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/foreign-flows-etfs-and-the-index-effect-vietnam). But that money *cannot reach media*, for a simple mechanical reason: a foreign fund managing hundreds of millions of dollars cannot meaningfully own a company whose entire tradable float is worth, say, \$8mn — it would *become* the float, unable to buy a position without doubling the price or sell one without halving it. The index effect, which lifts a stock when it enters a tracked index and forces passive money to buy it, also largely skips media, because these names are too small to enter the headline indices in the first place. So media is shut out of the two biggest sources of patient, structural, valuation-anchored demand in the entire market — foreign active funds and passive index money — leaving it to be priced almost entirely by domestic retail sentiment. That exclusion is not a minor footnote; it is *the* reason media trades like a casino while a bank trades like an institution. Strip a stock of every patient owner and all that remains to set its price is the most impatient, sentiment-driven money in the market.

## Why low float plus low liquidity makes prices violent

This is the most important mechanical idea in the entire post, and it has nothing to do with stories or sentiment per se — it is pure market microstructure. Once you understand it, the wild behavior of media stocks stops looking mysterious and starts looking *inevitable*.

Begin with two definitions. The **free float** of a stock is the portion of its shares that is actually available to trade in the open market — total shares outstanding *minus* the chunks locked up by founders, the state, strategic partners, and long-term insiders who never sell. A company can have a billion shares outstanding, but if the founder and a couple of strategic holders own 85% of them and never trade, the *free float* — the shares that can actually change hands on any given day — is only 15%. **Liquidity** is the related but distinct idea of how much you can buy or sell *without moving the price* — measured by daily trading value (how many dong of the stock trades each day) and by **depth** (how many shares are resting in the order book near the current price, waiting to be hit).

Now the mechanism. Imagine the order book of a stock as a staircase of resting sell orders: a few thousand shares offered at the current price, a few thousand more a tick higher, more a tick higher still. When you send a buy order, you "walk up" that staircase, consuming the resting offers and pushing the price up as you go. **The thinner the staircase — the fewer shares resting at each level — the further a given buy order walks the price up.** A stock with a deep order book absorbs a large order with barely a flicker; a stock with a thin order book lurches several percent on the same order, because your buying alone exhausts the available supply.

And here is the link to free float: **a low free float almost always means a thin order book.** If only 10% of a company's shares can trade, there are simply fewer shares around to rest in the order book at any moment, so each price level holds less, so the staircase is steep, so any given order moves the price more. The same order that a high-float stock shrugs off, a low-float stock *amplifies* — in both directions. A little buying spikes it; a little selling collapses it. This is *price impact*, and it scales inversely with available float and depth.

![Before and after diagram contrasting a thin 10 percent free float that moves a lot on one buy order with a deep 60 percent free float that barely moves on the same order](/imgs/blogs/media-sector-vietnam-small-sentiment-corner-3.png)

This is the whole secret of why media stocks are violent. They are *small* (so a modest amount of money is large relative to the company), they have *low float* (so the order book is thin), they have *low liquidity* (so depth is shallow and daily value is small), and they are *story-driven* (so sentiment, which is the input that triggers buying and selling, is itself volatile and herd-like). Stack those four properties — small cap, low float, low liquidity, sentiment — and you get a stock where a tiny shift in narrative produces a tiny shift in net buying, which the thin float then *magnifies* into a violent price move. The amplifier is mechanical. The trigger is psychological. The result is the doubling-and-collapsing chart from our opening.

It is worth naming the cruel asymmetry built into this. On the way *up*, low float is intoxicating: a trickle of FOMO buying, with no supply to meet it, sends the price ceiling-locked for days, and the chart looks unstoppable. But that same thin float is a trap on the way *down*, because **the very illiquidity that let you in cheaply will not let you out cheaply.** When you decide to sell — or when everyone decides at once — there is no depth on the bid to absorb the selling, so the price gaps down floor-locked (hitting the daily *lower* limit), and you cannot exit at all on the worst days: there is simply no buyer. The position that felt like a triumph on the way up becomes a position you are *trapped inside* on the way down. Liquidity, the saying goes, is a coward — it is there when you do not need it and gone the moment you do. Nowhere is that truer than in a low-float media micro-cap.

#### Worked example: the price impact of a single buy order on a thin float

Make the mechanism concrete. Suppose a small media stock has **10 million shares of free float** and trades around **20,000 dong** a share, so the entire tradable float is worth `10mn × 20,000 = 200 billion dong`, about **\$7.9mn** — the whole *floatable* company is the size of a nice apartment building. On a typical day maybe 5% of that float changes hands, so daily trading value is around `5% × 200bn = 10 billion dong`, about **\$394,000**. The order book is correspondingly thin: at any instant, perhaps a few hundred million dong of shares rest within a percent or two of the price.

Now you decide to buy **2 billion dong** of the stock — about **\$79,000**, a perfectly ordinary position for a serious retail investor. But 2 billion dong is *one-fifth of the entire day's normal trading value*, and it is many times the depth resting near the price. To fill it, your order has to walk up the staircase, consuming offer after offer, and by the time it is done you have pushed the price up several percent — say from 20,000 to 21,000 dong, a 5% move *caused entirely by your own buying.* You did not get the 20,000 price you saw on the screen; your average fill is higher, and you have *announced your interest to the whole market* by moving the tape. The same order in a large-cap bank — where 2 billion dong is a rounding error against tens of billions of daily turnover — would not move the price at all. The intuition: in a thin float, *you are the market*, and the price you trade at is a price *you created*, not one you found — which is exactly why entering and (worse) exiting these names is so treacherous.

## How to (barely) value the two kinds of media company

Valuation is where the two animals diverge most sharply, and where beginners get hurt by reaching for the wrong tool. Let us take each in turn, honestly, including *why* each method is fragile.

### The ad firm: P/E on cyclical earnings (and why the E is the problem)

A traditional ad firm or broadcaster *does* have earnings, so in principle you value it the normal way: a **P/E ratio**, price divided by earnings per share. If the stock is 30,000 dong and it earns 3,000 dong per share, the P/E is 10 — you are paying 10 years of current earnings for the stock. Simple enough. The trap is not the method; it is the *input.* Because ad earnings are wildly cyclical, **the "E" you plug in is a moving target that can swing by a multiple from year to year**, and the market's favorite way to lose money here is to put a high multiple on a *peak* earnings number.

Think about what that does. At the top of an advertising boom, the firm earns 3,000 dong a share, the story is great, and the crowd happily pays a P/E of 15 — a price of 45,000. Then the ad cycle turns, revenue falls, operating leverage does its work, and earnings collapse to 500 dong a share. Now even if the market grudgingly keeps the *same* 15x multiple, the price is `15 × 500 = 7,500` — an 83% wipeout, driven not by any change in the multiple but purely by the *earnings* falling off a cliff. This is the cyclical's curse: a P/E looks *cheapest* exactly when earnings are at a peak and about to fall (low P/E, high E), and *most expensive* at the trough when earnings are depressed and about to recover (high P/E, low E). For a cyclical ad firm you have to value it across the *whole cycle* — on normalized or mid-cycle earnings — not on whatever the trailing print happens to be. We covered this trap in depth in the [sector valuation post](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/valuation-by-sector-pe-pb-nav-ev-ebitda); media advertising is one of its sharpest examples.

### The platform: EV/sales and per-user, and why both are guesswork

Now the hard case. VNG/VNZ has no earnings to divide into, so P/E is off the table entirely. The analyst's fallback is to value the company on its *revenue* and its *users* instead — and to understand how fragile this is, you have to watch it being built.

The most common revenue-based yardstick is **EV/sales** — *enterprise value* divided by annual sales (revenue). Enterprise value (EV) is the total value of the business to *all* its funders: market capitalization (the equity) plus net debt (what it owes minus its cash). Dividing EV by sales tells you how many dong of total business value the market is assigning per dong of annual revenue. A profitable, slow grower might trade at 1-2x sales; a fast-growing platform the market is excited about might trade at 5x, 8x, even 10x sales. And *that multiple is the entire ballgame*, because it is not anchored to anything concrete — it is a pure expression of *how much the market believes in the future*. The exact same revenue line is worth twice as much at 8x sales as at 4x, and the only thing that determines whether it is 4 or 8 is *sentiment about a future that has not arrived.* That is why platform valuations are so volatile: a small change in the mood about "the AI story" or "the cloud story" re-rates the multiple, and the price moves enormously on a revenue number that did not change at all.

The other family of yardsticks is **per-user metrics**: value the company by how many users it has and what each user might eventually be worth. You take the enterprise value and divide by monthly active users to get an *implied value per user*, then argue about whether that is reasonable versus comparable platforms, or you forecast a future *average revenue per user* and a future margin and discount it back. Every step of that is a guess: how many users will stay, how much each will eventually pay, what margin the business will *someday* earn once it stops spending for growth. You are not measuring value; you are *assuming* a future and back-solving a price from it. Change any assumption — slower user growth, lower eventual margin, a longer wait for profitability — and the "value" moves by a factor. We will work an example shortly to see just how flimsy this is.

The honest summary: **valuing a loss-making platform is less a calculation than a bet on a narrative, dressed up in a spreadsheet.** That is not a criticism of the companies — it is genuinely how early-stage platforms must be assessed — but it is exactly why their stocks are sentiment machines. When the tools you must use are "how much do you believe the story," the price *is* the story, and the story changes with the wind.

#### Worked example: the operating leverage of an ad-cycle downturn

Quantify why ad earnings swing so violently. Take a small broadcaster that in a good year does **800 billion dong of revenue** (about **\$31mn**) against a cost base of **720 billion dong**, leaving **80 billion dong of operating profit** — a 10% margin. Crucially, suppose most of that cost is *fixed*: say 600 billion dong of fixed cost (staff, broadcasting infrastructure, content commitments, rent) and only 120 billion dong that varies with revenue.

Now the ad cycle turns and revenue falls a modest **10%**, from 800 to 720 billion dong. The variable cost falls proportionally, from 120 to 108 billion, but the *fixed* 600 billion does not budge. New cost base: `600 + 108 = 708` billion. New operating profit: `720 − 708 = 12` billion dong. So a **10% fall in revenue** turned 80 billion of profit into 12 billion — an **85% collapse in profit.** The intuition: when most of your costs are fixed, every dong of lost revenue falls almost straight through to the bottom line, so a small, ordinary swing in discretionary ad spend produces a violent swing in earnings — and, through the P/E, a violent swing in the stock. This is operating leverage, and it is why a "cheap" ad firm at the top of the cycle is a trap.

#### Worked example: trying to value a loss-making platform, and watching it wobble

Now feel how fragile platform valuation is. Suppose VNZ-type company does **9 trillion dong of revenue** (about **\$354mn**) but reports a **net loss** — so P/E is useless. The market currently values its enterprise at **45 trillion dong** (about **\$1.77bn**), which is `45 / 9 = 5x` EV/sales. Is that cheap or expensive? You literally cannot say from the multiple alone; it depends entirely on what you believe about the future.

So you try the per-user route. Say the platform has **30 million monthly active users** across its apps. An enterprise value of 45 trillion dong implies `45,000bn / 30mn = 1.5 million dong per user`, about **\$59 per user**. Is a Vietnamese internet user worth \$59 of enterprise value? Maybe — if each user will *eventually* generate, say, 200,000 dong a year of profit, then \$59 is about 7.5 years of future per-user profit, which sounds plausible *if you believe the company will ever earn that margin.* But now flex one assumption: suppose the market's mood sours and it decides the platform deserves only **3x sales** instead of 5x. Enterprise value drops to `3 × 9 = 27` trillion dong — a **40% fall** — on a revenue line that *did not change at all.* The per-user value falls to 900,000 dong, about **\$35**, and the same analyst who called \$59 reasonable now calls \$35 reasonable, with an equally straight face. The intuition: nothing about the *business* changed; only the *multiple* did, and the multiple is just sentiment with a decimal point — which is precisely why platform stocks lurch on narrative, not numbers.

## How a sentiment-and-theme corner actually behaves

Put the pieces together — two odd businesses, tiny weight, thin float, faith-based valuation — and a characteristic *behavior* emerges that you will see again and again. Media trades in **theme waves** (Vietnamese: *sóng ngành* applied to a story rather than a real cycle). A narrative catches — "AI," "short-form video," "data centres," "a strategic foreign partner," "a content licensing deal" — and the relevant names go vertical together, not because earnings moved but because the *story* gives a reason to buy and the thin float magnifies the buying. The wave peaks when the marginal buyer runs out, the early entrants take profit, and the same thin float that powered the spike now powers the collapse. The whole arc — ignition, melt-up, exhaustion, unwind — can play out in weeks, far faster than any real-economy cycle.

![Line chart of a stylized media small-cap price path indexed at 100 spiking to 260 then collapsing back below 90 over ten months](/imgs/blogs/media-sector-vietnam-small-sentiment-corner-6.png)

The reason these waves recur — rather than the corner simply being dead between stories — is that thin float and a story are a *renewable* combination. The float never deepens (the founders and strategic holders still own most of the company), the daily liquidity stays low, and there is always *some* narrative available to a creative imagination. So the same stock can run the spike-and-collapse arc multiple times over a few years, each time on a fresh story, each time trapping a fresh crowd of late buyers. This is structurally different from a cyclical sector like steel or property, where the *driver* (the steel spread, the property cycle) is a real-economy variable that takes years to turn. A media theme wave needs no real-economy turn at all — it needs only a story and a thin float, both of which are perpetually in stock. That renewability is why you cannot "wait out" a media position the way you might patiently hold a cyclical through its trough: there is no fundamental cycle underneath to mean-revert to, only the next story, which may never come for *your* particular name.

Three behavioral signatures are worth naming because they help you recognize what you are looking at:

**Sentiment-driven spikes with no earnings to back them.** When you see a media micro-cap ceiling-locked for a week with no profit release, no contract announcement, no fundamental catalyst — only a *rumor* or a *theme* — you are watching pure sentiment-times-scarcity, not a re-rating of value. The tell is that the move is *disconnected from any number*: there is no new earnings figure that justifies a doubling, because nothing about the cash flow changed.

**High-price, low-float swings in the platform names.** VNZ in particular trades at a *very high nominal price per share* with a *very low free float* (a large majority of the company is held by founders and strategic partners; only a thin slice floats). That combination produces enormous percentage swings on modest volume — the stock can move tens of percent on turnover that would not register in a liquid large-cap, precisely because there is so little float to set the price. A high-price, low-float name is the purest expression of the violence mechanism: there is barely any stock to argue over, so the price the few available shares change hands at can be almost anything sentiment decides.

**Ad-cycle hits in the traditional names.** The broadcasters and ad firms, meanwhile, take their lumps from the *real* economy: when consumer demand softens and brands cut marketing, ad revenue falls, operating leverage amplifies it into an earnings collapse, and the stock de-rates on the falling E — a slower, more fundamental decline than the sentiment names, but just as brutal because of the leverage.

## Common misconceptions

Three beliefs reliably separate the people who lose money in this corner from the people who treat it sensibly. Each is corrected with a number.

**Misconception 1: "The stock doubled, so the market found real value in it."** This is the most expensive belief in small-cap media. A doubling on a thin float tells you almost *nothing* about value and almost *everything* about scarcity. Recall the mechanism: when only 10 million shares float and a little FOMO buying meets no supply, the price ceiling-locks and doubles on volume that, in absolute dong, is trivial. In our worked example, 2 billion dong of buying — about **\$79,000**, a single determined retail account — moved the price 5% *by itself.* Multiply that across a few excited buyers over a few days and you have a double, with not one dong of changed earnings underneath it. The price went up because there was nothing to sell into the buying, *not* because the business got more valuable. The proof is the round trip: the same move reverses completely when the buying stops, which a genuine re-rating of value does not.

**Misconception 2: "VNG is profitable like FPT — it's Vietnam's big tech compounder."** No. FPT and VNG are opposite financial animals, and conflating them is dangerous. FPT, the subject of our [information-technology post](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/information-technology-sector-vietnam-fpt-structural-growth), is a *profit machine* — it earned roughly **11 trillion dong of pre-tax profit** in 2024 (about **\$435mn**), compounding profit ~20% a year with a defined, valuable P/E. VNG is a *growth bet that often loses money* — big revenue, frequently a net loss as it funds games, cloud, payments and AI, with *no* usable P/E. FPT you value on earnings; VNG you value on a narrative-driven revenue multiple that can halve on a mood swing, as our platform worked example showed (5x sales to 3x sales is a 40% drop on unchanged revenue). Both are "tech," but one is a compounder you can own and the other is a speculative platform you can only *bet on* — treating the second like the first is how people pay a compounder's price for a loss-maker's risk.

**Misconception 3: "A small-cap media name is an early-stage compounder — I'm getting in before it gets big."** Occasionally true, mostly a fantasy. The base rate for a tiny, illiquid, story-driven media micro-cap is *not* "the next FPT"; it is "a thin ticker that spikes on a narrative and gives it all back." Most of these names have no recurring, compounding earnings engine of the kind that actually produces a multi-year winner — they have a cyclical ad business or a single speculative story. "Early-stage compounder" describes a company growing real, recurring profit off a small base; "small, low-float, sentiment-driven micro-cap" describes a *price* that is easy to push around. They are not the same thing, and mistaking the second for the first is how you end up holding the late stage of the price path in our chart — the part after 260 that goes back below 90.

## How it shows up on VN-Index

Pull the threads into the concrete market behavior you will actually observe. But first it helps to lay the handful of actual listed plays side by side, because the sector's whole character is that its members are *not* alike — they differ in what they are, whether they make money, how liquid they are, and what kind of risk they carry. The matrix below sorts them into the three groups you will meet on the exchange.

![Matrix of listed Vietnam media plays comparing internet platform broadcast advertising and publishing across what it is profitability liquidity and risk](/imgs/blogs/media-sector-vietnam-small-sentiment-corner-7.png)

Read across the rows and the sector's split personality jumps out. The **internet/platform** row (VNZ) is big-revenue, loss-making, very-low-float and narrative-risk — a venture bet. The **broadcast/advertising** row (YEG and small broadcasters) is small-revenue, cyclical-and-thin, with ad-cycle-and-sentiment risk — a leveraged play on the economy and the story. The **publishing** row (small printers and content houses) is tiny, low-growth and mostly illiquid — sleepy until a theme wakes it. No single valuation tool, no single thesis, and no single risk covers all three; the only thing they truly share is *smallness and thin float*, which is exactly why they all spike and collapse on sentiment despite being such different businesses underneath.

**The sector is invisible at the index level and violent at the stock level.** Because media is roughly **0.6%** of VN-Index, you will never see it as a leading or lagging *sector* in a rotation map — it is too small to register. But that index-level invisibility is exactly *why* the individual stocks are so wild: with no structural institutional ownership underneath them, the names live and die on retail sentiment and thin float, untethered from the index's gravity. The lesson for the rotation framework in this series — which we develop in [sector rotation explained](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/sector-rotation-explained-leaders-and-laggards) — is that media is a corner rotation *skips*: capital rotates between the big sectors and only occasionally, on a theme, sloshes a little into media before sloshing back out.

**The VNZ high-price, low-float swing is the signature.** Watch VNZ and you will see the violence mechanism in its purest form: a stock priced at a very high nominal level, with a thin float (founders and strategic holders own the great majority), swinging tens of percent on volume that would not move a liquid large-cap a fraction as far. When you see that pattern — big percentage moves on small absolute turnover — you are not looking at a re-rating of a known value; you are looking at a tiny number of shares repricing on sentiment because there is barely any float to anchor them. The same property that makes it explosive up makes it brutal down.

**The ad-cycle hit is the traditional-media signature.** The broadcasters and ad firms show a different, more fundamental pattern, tied to the real economy. When consumer demand and corporate confidence soften — the kind of slowdown that also hits [retail](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/retail-sector-vietnam-the-consumption-story) and consumer names — advertising budgets get cut first, ad revenue falls, and operating leverage turns a modest revenue dip into an earnings collapse, exactly as our 10%-revenue-to-85%-profit example showed. The stock then de-rates on falling earnings. This is the cyclical face of media: slower than a sentiment spike, but mechanically just as severe.

**And underneath all of it sits the liquidity-and-margin cycle.** Media's behavior is amplified by the same margin-debt dynamics that drive the whole small-cap end of the market, which we cover in [liquidity and the margin cycle](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/liquidity-and-the-margin-cycle-vietnam). When margin lending is abundant and risk appetite is high, the speculative, low-float, story-driven names — and media is the archetype — get the most fuel, because leveraged retail money chases exactly this kind of high-beta lottery ticket. When margin tightens and risk appetite collapses, these names are sold first and hardest, because they are the most speculative thing in the book and the first thing a margin call liquidates. So media tends to be a *late-cycle* outperformer (the froth at the top) and an *early-bust* casualty (the first thing dumped), an exaggerated version of the small-cap cycle.

## The playbook: how to trade it

Everything above resolves into a single, disciplined stance. Media is *not* a core holding and was never meant to be one; it is a small, high-risk, theme-trading allocation that you treat with respect for its violence. Here is the concrete playbook.

**Decide first whether you should be here at all.** For most investors building a long-term Vietnamese portfolio, the honest answer is *barely, if at all.* Media has no structural compounder you can own and forget (that is FPT's job, in IT), no defensive cash flow, no dividend anchor — it offers volatility and stories. So treat any media position as *explicitly speculative money*, mentally walled off from your core, sized as if you could lose all of it. If you cannot afford to lose the position to zero, you do not have a media position; you have a problem.

**Size it tiny — 1 to 2% of the portfolio, hard cap, per name.** This is the single most important rule, and it follows directly from the violence. A low-float media name can halve on one bad print and you may not be able to exit on the worst day. So you size such that a 50% drop is an annoyance, not a wound. At a **1% position**, a 50% loss costs you **0.5% of your capital** — a rounding error. At a **10% position**, the same 50% loss costs you **5%** — a serious dent that takes real work to recover. The asymmetry is the whole argument: because the *downside* is violent and the *exit* is unreliable, you control your risk on the *entry size*, where you have total control, rather than hoping to control it on the *exit*, where you have almost none.

#### Worked example: why a 1-2% cap protects you from the violence

Quantify the sizing rule on a 1 billion dong portfolio (about **\$39,000**). You like a thin media name's story and consider two sizings. **Sizing A:** a disciplined 1% position — 10 million dong, about **\$394** — in the speculative name. **Sizing B:** an undisciplined 10% position — 100 million dong, about **\$3,940** — because you are *sure* about the story. Now the violence arrives: the narrative fails and the stock falls 50%, floor-locked, and by the time you can actually exit you are down 50% on the position.

Under Sizing A you lose `50% × 10mn = 5 million dong`, about **\$197** — **0.5%** of your portfolio. You barely feel it; the rest of your book carries on. Under Sizing B you lose `50% × 100mn = 50 million dong`, about **\$1,970** — **5%** of your portfolio, from a single thin micro-cap. To recover that 5% you need the *rest* of your portfolio to do meaningful work, all because you let one lottery ticket get too big. The intuition: in a corner where a 50% drawdown is a normal outcome and the exit can be locked against you, position size is the *only* risk control you fully command — so you spend it cheaply, capping each violent name at 1-2% so that being wrong is survivable by design.

**If you engage, trade the theme, not the "value" — and define the invalidation before you enter.** When you do take a media position, be honest that you are trading a *narrative wave*, so trade it like one: enter early in a theme while liquidity is rising and the float is starved, and have a *pre-committed exit*. The invalidation is not a fundamental thesis-break (there is rarely a fundamental thesis); it is *price and liquidity behavior.* Concretely: you are out when the **volume that powered the spike dries up** (the marginal buyer is gone — the wave is over), when the stock **breaks the trend of higher highs** that defined the melt-up, or simply when it falls a hard, pre-set percentage from your entry. Set that stop *before* you buy, while you are calm, because the thin float means that once the unwind starts you may not get a graceful exit — you take the first exit you are offered, not the one you wish for.

**Never average down a collapsing low-float name.** The instinct to "buy more cheaply" is lethal here, because the thing that is falling has no fundamental floor to catch it — no institutional buyer of last resort, no earnings anchor, just thin float collapsing on vanished sentiment. Averaging down turns a capped 1% mistake into an uncapped disaster. The discipline is the reverse: a low-float name that has broken down is *more* dangerous as it falls, not less, because the exit liquidity is disappearing exactly when you most need it. Respect the floor-lock; do not try to be a hero in the one corner of the market specifically engineered to trap heroes.

**Mind the liquidity at the exit, not just the entry.** The most underappreciated risk in this corner is not that you will pick the wrong story — it is that you will be *right about the story being over* and still be unable to act on it. When a low-float name reverses, the daily trading value can collapse to a fraction of its already-thin normal, and on floor-locked days there is *no bid at all* — you place a sell order and it simply does not fill, day after day, while the price keeps marking lower. This is why your position size must be set assuming you might have to exit over *several days* into vanishing liquidity, not in one clean trade. A practical rule: never hold a media position so large that your intended exit is more than a *small fraction of the stock's normal daily volume*, because anything bigger means *you* are the thing crushing the price on the way out. If you cannot exit your whole position in a day or two without moving the price meaningfully, the position is too big — and that constraint, not your conviction in the story, should set the size. Liquidity risk is the risk that the door is too narrow for the crowd trying to get through it, and in media the door is always narrow.

**Treat VNG/VNZ as a separate decision — a venture bet, priced like one.** If you want exposure to the internet-platform story, recognize that you are making a *venture-style bet on a loss-funding-growth company*, not buying a compounder. Size it the same tiny way, accept that its valuation is a sentiment-driven multiple that can halve on a mood swing, and understand that you are paying for a *future that may or may not arrive* — its current losses are a feature of the model, but they also mean there is no earnings floor under the price. If the AI-and-cloud narrative cools, the multiple compresses and the high-price, low-float swing works violently against you. Own it only as the speculative sliver it is.

The honest bottom line: media is the small, sentiment-driven corner where the *mechanism* (small cap + low float + thin liquidity) and the *trigger* (story, theme, narrative) combine to make prices move violently in both directions, untethered from steady earnings. Respect it for what it is — a place to make a small, disciplined, hard-stopped theme trade — and never mistake a thin-float doubling for the discovery of value or a loss-funding platform for a proven compounder. Size it at 1-2%, define the invalidation before you enter, and let the rest of your portfolio live in the sectors that actually compound.
