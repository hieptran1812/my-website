## A broker that doubled, then halved

In the spring of 2021, a mid-tier Vietnamese securities firm — pick any of the listed names that traded the retail boom — was a one-way bet. New brokerage accounts were being opened at record monthly rates, HOSE turnover was climbing past 20 trillion dong a day, and the firm's margin loan book was maxed out and being expanded with fresh share issues. Its stock price did what broker stocks do in that environment: it more than doubled inside a single year. Quarterly profit reports came in showing brokerage commissions up, margin interest up, and proprietary trading gains up — all three of the firm's engines firing at once. Investors who had bought the broker as "a leveraged way to play the bull" looked like geniuses. By early 2022, with VN-Index brushing 1,500 and every liquidity gauge at a record, the broker sector was one of the most crowded, most loved corners of the market.

Then the loop reversed. Through 2022 the State Bank hiked rates, a corporate-bond crisis (*trai phieu doanh nghiep*) froze a key funding channel, and VN-Index fell more than 40% from its peak to a low near 874 in November. The broker sector did not fall 40% — it fell harder. The same three engines that had fired in unison now stalled in unison: turnover collapsed (commissions down), clients deleveraged and the margin book shrank (interest income down), and the firm's own proprietary book took mark-to-market losses (prop gains turned to prop losses). The stock that had doubled in 2021 roughly halved in 2022. Same firm. Same business model. The only thing that changed was the direction of the market it was levered to.

This is the defining feature of the securities sector on VN-Index, and the reason it deserves its own deep-dive: **a broker is not a normal financial company — it is an amplifier of the market itself.** Banks have a spread business that grinds out earnings through most of the cycle; consumer-staples firms sell the same noodles in a boom and a bust; utilities collect regulated cash flows regardless. A securities firm has none of that ballast. Every dong it earns is geared to how much the market trades, how much it borrows, and which way the index is pointing. That makes brokers the **highest-beta sector** on the exchange — the lever you push hardest when you want maximum exposure to a liquidity upturn, and the one you pull back first when the fuel gauge reads "full." This post builds the broker revenue model from zero, explains why the beta is structurally high, walks the margin-lending engine that drives the profit cycle, shows how to value a broker on price-to-book, surveys the market-share war and the zero-fee disruption, and ends with a concrete playbook for trading the sector as a leveraged index proxy.

![Broker revenue engine graph with four geared lines feeding one amplified profit node](/imgs/blogs/securities-brokers-sector-vietnam-highest-beta-1.png)

The figure above is the whole thesis in one picture. Four inputs on the left — market turnover, the margin balance plus the lending rate, the index level, and deal flow — each feed one revenue line, and all four revenue lines pour into a single profit node that is amplified relative to any of them. The point of the picture is that there is no offsetting line: nothing on the left moves *against* the market, so when the market booms, every input rises together and the profit explodes, and when it busts, every input falls together and the profit collapses. Everything between here and the playbook is an elaboration of those four lines and the gearing that stacks them.

## Foundations: what a securities firm actually does

Before any beta math, we need to be precise about what a securities firm is and how it makes money. The Vietnamese term is *cong ty chung khoan* — literally "securities company" — and in English the same entity is called a broker, a brokerage, or a securities firm. The big listed names you will hear are **SSI** (SSI Securities), **VND** (VNDirect), **HCM** (HSC, Ho Chi Minh City Securities), **VCI** (Vietcap, formerly VCSC), **MBS** (MB Securities), **VIX**, **SHS**, and the privately held but dominant **VPS** and **TCBS** (Techcom Securities). These firms do four distinct things, and it pays to separate them clearly because each carries different economics and a different sensitivity to the cycle.

### Line one: brokerage — a cut of every trade

The original business of a broker is **execution**: it gives retail and institutional clients access to the exchange and takes a commission on every buy and sell. When you place an order through your trading app, your broker routes it to HOSE or HNX and charges you a fee — historically a fraction of a percent of the trade value, on each side. The commission is small per trade but enormous in aggregate, because Vietnam's market is retail-dominated (roughly 85-90% of volume) and retail investors trade *a lot*.

The key property of brokerage revenue is that **it scales directly with market turnover.** If the whole market trades 10 trillion dong a day, and your firm has 10% market share, you are executing 1 trillion dong of turnover a day and clipping a fee on every dong of it. If turnover doubles to 20 trillion a day — as it did in the 2021 boom — your brokerage revenue roughly doubles too, with almost no extra cost (the trading platform is already built, the staff already hired). Brokerage is a high-operating-leverage business: most of the incremental revenue in a boom drops straight to profit. That is the first reason broker earnings are so explosive on the way up.

A homely analogy before the numbers: **a broker is a tollbooth on a highway.** It does not care much *where* the cars are going (which stocks you buy) — it collects a toll on every car that passes. In a boom the highway is jammed bumper to bumper and the tollbooth mints money; in a bust the road empties out and the same booth, with the same fixed cost, collects almost nothing. The toll rate barely changes; what changes violently is the *traffic*. Market turnover is the traffic, and it is wildly cyclical.

#### Worked example: brokerage fee revenue from turnover

Suppose the market's average daily trading value (ADTV) is **20 trillion dong/day**, and your firm holds a **10% brokerage market share**. That means your firm executes 20 × 10% = **2 trillion dong of client turnover a day**. Vietnamese retail brokerage fees have historically run around **0.15% per side** (it varies by broker and tier, and the zero-fee disruptors have pushed it lower, but use 0.15% for a worked number). Your gross brokerage revenue per day is 2,000 billion × 0.15% = **3 billion dong/day** — about **\$118,000 per day** at \$1 = 25,400 VND. Annualize over roughly 250 trading days and that single line generates 3 × 250 = **750 billion dong a year** (~\$29.5mn). The intuition: brokerage revenue is just *market turnover × your share × the fee rate*, and because turnover swings by a factor of three across the cycle while your share and fee rate barely move, this whole line breathes in and out with the market's volume.

### Line two: margin lending — interest on borrowed money

The second and increasingly the *most important* line is **margin lending** — *cho vay ky quy*. A broker lends clients money to buy more stock than their cash allows, holding the purchased shares as collateral, and charges interest on the loan. (The mechanics of margin from the borrower's side — leverage, maintenance ratios, margin calls — are covered in depth in the companion post on [liquidity and the margin cycle](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/liquidity-and-the-margin-cycle-vietnam); here we look at it from the *lender's* side, because that lender is the broker, and margin interest is now a huge slice of broker profit.)

The economics are a classic **leveraged carry**: the broker funds its lending from its own equity capital plus short-term borrowing (bank credit lines, bonds, interbank money), at one rate, and lends to clients at a higher rate. The gap between the two — the **net interest spread** — is the broker's margin-lending profit. Vietnamese brokers have historically charged clients in the low-to-mid teens annually (call it ~11-14%) and funded themselves a few points cheaper, so the spread runs roughly 4-6 percentage points on a very large book.

The size of that book is the swing factor. The total system margin balance went from ~52 trillion dong in 2019 to ~195 trillion at the 2021 peak, fell to ~122 trillion in the 2022 crash, and pushed past ~370 trillion dong by Q3 2025. A broker's net interest income tracks *its share of that balance*, so this line, too, breathes with the cycle — and it has grown to rival or exceed brokerage commissions at many firms, especially the ones that compete on near-zero execution fees and make their money on the loan book instead.

#### Worked example: the margin-lending spread

Suppose your firm carries a **20 trillion dong margin loan book** (~\$787mn at \$1 = 25,400 VND), lends it to clients at an **11% yield**, and funds it at a **6% cost of funds**. The net spread is 11% − 6% = **5 percentage points**. On a 20 trillion dong book, that is 20,000 billion × 5% = **1 trillion dong of net interest income a year** — about **\$39.4mn**. Notice how large that is relative to the brokerage line from the previous example (750 billion dong): at a big firm, the loan book can be the *biggest* profit center. And notice the gearing: if a bull market lets the book grow from 20 to 30 trillion dong, the same 5% spread now earns 1.5 trillion dong — a 50% jump in this line from the loan book alone. The intuition: margin interest is *spread × book size*, and because the book size doubles and halves across the cycle while the spread is relatively stable, the margin balance chart is essentially a chart of broker interest profit.

![Total broker margin balance area chart from 2019 to 2025 showing the broker profit engine](/imgs/blogs/securities-brokers-sector-vietnam-highest-beta-3.png)

The chart above is the broker profit engine drawn as one curve. The total system margin balance climbed from ~52 trillion dong in 2019 to ~195 trillion at the 2021 peak (more than tripling), collapsed to ~122 trillion through the 2022 deleveraging, then rebuilt to ~180 trillion (2023), ~245 trillion (2024), and pushed to a fresh record past ~370 trillion dong (~\$14.6bn) by Q3 2025. Because each broker earns a spread on its slice of this balance, the shape of this curve is the shape of broker interest income — and a large part of the shape of broker profit overall.

### Line three: proprietary trading — the firm's own book

The third line is **proprietary trading** (*tu doanh*), where the firm trades its own capital in stocks, bonds, and other instruments for its own account. This is the most volatile line of all, because it is a *direct* bet on market direction with the firm's own money. In a rising market, the prop book marks up and generates gains; in a falling market, it marks down and generates losses. There is no spread, no fee, no toll — just the raw profit-and-loss of holding securities through the cycle.

Prop trading is what makes broker earnings *very* high beta rather than merely high beta. A bank's loan book does not get marked to market every quarter on the price of equities; a broker's prop book does. When VN-Index rallies 30%, a broker holding a big equity prop book can book large gains that flatter its earnings; when the index falls 40%, the same book can swing to a loss that turns a profitable year into a loss-making one. The composition of the prop book matters enormously — some firms run conservative books heavy in bonds and term deposits (lower beta), others run aggressive equity books (very high beta) — and it is one of the most important things to check when you analyze an individual broker.

### Line four: investment banking — deal flow

The fourth line is **investment banking and advisory** (*ngan hang dau tu* / IB): underwriting initial public offerings (IPOs), arranging corporate bond issues, advising on mergers and acquisitions, and structuring deals. The broker earns fees for bringing capital-raising and transactions to market. (For the full mechanics of how an IB makes money — underwriting, advisory, the league-table game — see [inside an investment bank: how they make money](/blog/trading/finance/inside-an-investment-bank-how-they-make-money).)

IB is geared to **deal flow**, which is itself cyclical but on a slightly different rhythm. Companies want to IPO and issue bonds when markets are buoyant and valuations are high (more deals, more fees); in a bear market the IPO window slams shut and bond issuance freezes (the 2022 corporate-bond crisis is the extreme example — issuance collapsed). So IB revenue, while lumpier and harder to predict than the other lines, still leans pro-cyclical: it booms when the market booms and dries up when it busts. It is generally the smallest of the four lines for most Vietnamese brokers, but it can be material for the firms that dominate underwriting.

There is also a hidden tail risk inside the IB line specific to Vietnam: **corporate-bond distribution**. Several brokers — TCBS most prominently, but others too — built large businesses *distributing* corporate bonds (*trai phieu doanh nghiep*) to retail investors, earning arranging and distribution fees. That looked like a stable, growing fee line right up until the 2022 bond crisis, when high-profile issuer defaults and a regulatory crackdown froze the channel, triggered redemption pressure, and exposed the brokers that had distributed paper that subsequently soured. The reputational and sometimes financial fallout hit the most aggressive distributors hardest. The lesson is that the IB line is not just "lumpy and small" — it can carry a concentrated, hard-to-see risk that materializes precisely in a bust, which is one more way the sector's downside is fatter than a casual look at the four lines suggests.

#### Worked example: the IB and bond-distribution line in a boom versus a bust

Suppose a broker arranges and distributes **5 trillion dong** of corporate bonds in a buoyant year (~\$197mn of issuance) and earns an arranging-plus-distribution fee of roughly **1%**, booking **50 billion dong** of fee income (~\$2.0mn). That is a clean, attractive fee in a boom. Now run the bust: the bond market freezes, new issuance falls to near zero, so the fee income collapses to a trickle — *and*, worse, a slug of the bonds the broker distributed in prior years defaults, sparking client complaints, forced buybacks, or reputational damage that costs far more than the 50 billion the line ever earned. The intuition: the IB and bond line earns small, steady-looking fees in the boom but can carry an asymmetric, bust-concentrated liability — so it amplifies the sector's downside rather than cushioning it, just like every other broker line.

### Putting the four lines together: beta defined

Now the central concept of this whole post: **beta**. Beta measures how much a stock or a sector moves relative to the overall market. A sector with a beta of 1.0 moves roughly in line with VN-Index — up 10% when the index is up 10%, down 10% when it is down 10%. A beta of 2.0 means it moves *twice* as much in both directions; a beta of 0.5 means it moves *half* as much (a defensive sector). Beta is not about being good or bad — it is about *amplitude*: high-beta sectors give you a magnified version of the index, low-beta sectors give you a muted version.

The broker sector has a structurally high beta — historically on the order of 1.5x to 3x depending on the period and how you measure it — and now we can see exactly *why* it is high rather than just *that* it is high. It is high because **all four revenue lines are geared to the market in the same direction, with no offsetting line.** Brokerage rides turnover (up in a boom); margin interest rides the loan book (up in a boom); prop rides the index (up in a boom); IB rides deal flow (up in a boom). There is no consumer-staples line, no regulated-utility line, no counter-cyclical hedge anywhere in the business. When you stack four pro-cyclical lines on top of each other, you get a profit number that swings far more than the market — and a stock price that swings even more, because the *valuation multiple* moves too (we will get to that). That stacking of four same-direction lines is the mechanical source of the high beta.

![Broker sector return versus VN-Index line chart 2021 to 2024 showing high beta both ways](/imgs/blogs/securities-brokers-sector-vietnam-highest-beta-2.png)

The chart above shows the beta in the actual record. In 2021 the broker sector returned roughly **+110%** while VN-Index rose about **+36%** — the sector delivered three times the index's gain. In 2022 the sector fell about **−58%** against the index's **−33%** drop — falling hardest. In 2023 the sector rebounded roughly **+75%** against the index's ~+12%, and in 2024 both did about +12% in a quieter, less directional year. The pattern is unmistakable: in big up years and big down years, the broker line is a magnified version of the index line; in flat years, the magnification fades because there is less directional move to amplify. That is high beta drawn as two curves.

#### Worked example: beta in action

Take 2021. VN-Index returned roughly **+36%** on the year. The broker sector returned roughly **+110%**. The implied beta is simply the ratio of the two moves: 110 ÷ 36 ≈ **3.05** — call it 3x. That means a broker portfolio gave you about three times the index's return in that bull year. Now run it backward through 2022: VN-Index fell **−33%**; at a 3x beta you would expect the broker sector to fall ~99%, and it fell about **−58%** — not the full 3x (beta is never perfectly constant, and the down-leg compressed somewhat), but still far harder than the index, and enough to roughly halve a broker portfolio. The intuition: a beta of ~3x is a promise that works in both directions — it triples your upside in a bull and multiplies your downside in a bear, so the sector is a *return amplifier*, not a free lunch.

## The margin-lending engine and the regulatory cap

Of the four lines, margin lending deserves the closest look, because it is the one that most tightly *drives the cycle* and because it is governed by a hard regulatory ceiling that shapes how the whole sector behaves near a top. We saw the spread economics above; now we examine the constraints.

### The per-account cap: up to 2x gross exposure for the client

On the client side, regulation limits how much an individual can borrow relative to their own equity. The initial margin requirement has historically meant a client can borrow up to roughly 1:1 against eligible collateral — put up about 50%, borrow about 50% — giving up to ~2x gross exposure. Brokers can be more conservative on specific stocks, and each broker publishes a **margin list** of eligible tickers with a permitted loan ratio for each. Blue-chip large-caps with deep liquidity get generous ratios; thin, volatile small-caps get low ratios or are excluded; newly listed and warned stocks are typically barred. This per-stock list matters for the *whole market*, because when a broker cuts a hot stock's margin ratio after a sharp run, it pulls fuel and can puncture the move on its own.

### The firm-level cap: margin loans ≤ 200% of equity

The constraint that matters most for the *sector* is the firm-level one: **a securities firm's total outstanding margin loans cannot exceed 200% of its own owners' equity** — twice its capital. This is the systemic ceiling on how big the leverage machine can get, and it has a profound consequence for how brokers behave in a bull market. When demand for margin is insatiable and a firm's loan book bumps against its 2x cap, the firm cannot lend any more — so to keep growing the book (and the interest income), it must **raise fresh equity capital**, usually through a rights issue. A wave of broker rights issues and capital raises is therefore a classic *late-cycle* signal: it means the sector has run its margin books to the regulatory ceiling and is scrambling to expand capacity right when the cycle is most stretched.

This cap also explains a subtle dilution risk in broker stocks. A broker that keeps issuing shares to grow its margin book is growing its *earnings* but also growing its *share count*, so per-share earnings and book value grow more slowly than the headline. When you analyze a broker, you have to watch the share count, not just the profit — a firm can double its loan book and its net profit while diluting existing holders enough that the stock barely moves. The cap forces this trade-off: grow the book, dilute the holder.

#### Worked example: how margin gears the broker's own ROE

Here is the lender-side mirror of the leverage math. A broker has its own equity of, say, **10 trillion dong**. At the 200% cap it can run a margin book of up to **20 trillion dong** (~\$787mn) — twice its equity. Suppose it lends that 20 trillion at an 11% yield, funds it at a 6% cost, and earns the 5% spread = **1 trillion dong of net interest** (from the earlier example). That 1 trillion dong of profit is earned on just 10 trillion of the firm's *own* equity, because the other half of the book is debt-funded. So this line alone contributes 1 ÷ 10 = **10% to return on equity (ROE)** before any brokerage, prop, or IB profit is added on top. Stack the other three lines on in a bull market and the firm's total ROE can leap from ~10% to ~20% or more. The intuition: the broker is itself running a leveraged carry on its own balance sheet, so its ROE — and therefore its stock — is geared to the size of the book it can fund, which is exactly why brokers re-rate so violently when the cycle turns.

![Before and after panels showing how a margin book spread gears broker return on equity](/imgs/blogs/securities-brokers-sector-vietnam-highest-beta-5.png)

The figure above traces that gearing. On the left, the raw economics of the book: a 20 trillion dong margin book lent at 11% against a 6% cost of funds. On the right, what that does to the firm: a 5% net spread earns 1 trillion dong a year, but because it sits on a thin, debt-funded equity base, it gears the firm's ROE — which leaps in a bull market when the book is full and collapses in a bear when clients deleverage and the book shrinks. The book is the lever; ROE is what the lever moves.

## Proprietary book risk: the hidden swing factor

If margin lending is the engine that drives the cycle, the **proprietary book** is the wild card that decides how violent any single firm's swing will be. Two brokers with identical brokerage and margin businesses can post wildly different earnings in the same year purely because of how they ran their prop books.

The reason is that the prop book is the one line with *no buffer*. Brokerage has the fixed-rate toll; margin has the contractual spread; IB has the fee; but prop is just the mark-to-market profit-and-loss of whatever securities the firm chose to hold. A firm that loaded up on equities going into a bull market books spectacular gains; a firm that did the same going into a bear market books spectacular losses — sometimes large enough to swamp all three of the other, more stable lines. In the 2022 crash, several brokers swung from profit to loss substantially because of prop-book write-downs on equity holdings, even as their brokerage and margin lines merely shrank rather than reversed.

This is why, when you analyze an individual Vietnamese broker rather than the sector, the **composition of the prop book** is one of the first things to check. A conservative firm runs a prop book heavy in bonds, certificates of deposit, and term deposits — low-beta, income-generating, stable. An aggressive firm runs a prop book heavy in listed equities and even pre-IPO or illiquid positions — very high beta, capable of huge gains and huge losses. The same sector label hides very different risk profiles: one broker's "high beta" is a steady spread business with a bond-heavy treasury, another's is a turbo-charged equity fund wearing a brokerage license. The prop book is where that difference lives.

#### Worked example: the prop book turns a good year bad

Suppose a broker earns a steady **800 billion dong** from brokerage and margin combined in a given year (~\$31.5mn) — its "boring" lines held up. But it ran an aggressive equity prop book of **3 trillion dong** that fell 30% in a market crash, booking a **−900 billion dong** mark-to-market loss. The two boring lines made 800 billion; the prop book lost 900 billion; the firm posts a net **−100 billion dong loss** for the year despite a perfectly healthy core franchise. Now imagine the opposite firm with the same 800 billion core but a *bond-heavy* prop book that merely earned a flat ~150 billion in interest: it posts a **+950 billion dong profit** in the same crash year. Same brokerage, same margin, opposite outcome — entirely because of the prop book. The intuition: the prop book is the swing factor that can flip a broker's whole year, so it is the single most important thing distinguishing a "high-beta but profitable" broker from a "high-beta and dangerous" one.

## The market-share war: VPS, SSI, TCBS, and the rest

Step back from a single firm to the competitive landscape, because the structure of the sector shapes how individual brokers fare through the cycle. Vietnamese brokerage is a **fragmented, intensely competitive war for market share**, and the battle lines have shifted dramatically over the past few years.

For a long time the incumbents — SSI, HCM (HSC), VND, VCI (Vietcap) — dominated brokerage by value, competing on research, institutional relationships, and full-service offerings. Then the disruptors arrived. **VPS** stormed to the number-one position by trading volume by competing aggressively on near-zero execution fees and a fast retail app, capturing a large share of the high-frequency retail flow. **TCBS** (Techcom Securities), backed by Techcombank, built a powerful digital-first, fee-light model and a large bond-distribution and wealth franchise. The result is a market where the volume leader (VPS) makes relatively little per trade and monetizes through scale and margin lending, while the traditional houses defend higher-margin institutional and advisory business.

![Horizontal bar chart of HOSE brokerage market share by value around 2024](/imgs/blogs/securities-brokers-sector-vietnam-highest-beta-4.png)

The chart above shows roughly where brokerage market share by value sat around 2024: **VPS ~18%**, **SSI ~9.3%**, **TCBS ~7%**, **VND ~6%**, **HCM ~5.5%**, **Mirae (MAS) ~5%**, **VCI ~5%**, **MBS ~5%**, with the long tail of remaining firms making up about **39%**. The headline is fragmentation: even the number-one firm holds under a fifth of the market, and no single house dominates. This matters for trading the sector because share is *contestable* — a firm can gain or lose several points of share in a year through pricing and product, which adds a company-specific layer on top of the sector beta.

The competitive structure also produces a recurring trap, which we will return to in the misconceptions: **market share is not the same as profit.** VPS leads on volume but earns little per trade by design; a smaller firm with a fat margin book and a conservative prop treasury can be far more profitable per share than the volume leader. When you pick a broker to express a sector view, you are choosing among very different business mixes wearing the same sector label.

## Valuing a broker: price-to-book and ROE

How do you put a value on a securities firm? The headline metric for brokers — as for banks and most financials — is **price-to-book (P/B)**, not the price-to-earnings (P/E) ratio you would use for an industrial. (The full toolkit of valuation ratios by sector is laid out in [valuation by sector: P/E, P/B, NAV, and EV/EBITDA](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/valuation-by-sector-pe-pb-nav-ev-ebitda); here we focus on why P/B is the right lens for a broker specifically.)

### Why P/B, not P/E

P/B compares the stock's market value to its **book value** — the net assets (equity) on the balance sheet. For a financial firm whose business *is* its balance sheet — the margin loan book, the prop book, the capital — book value is a meaningful anchor, and earnings are too volatile and cyclical to make P/E reliable. A broker's P/E can look absurdly low at a cyclical earnings peak (high earnings in the denominator) and absurdly high or even meaningless at a trough (near-zero or negative earnings). P/B smooths through that because book value does not swing nearly as violently as earnings.

The right way to think about a broker's P/B is to tie it to **ROE** — return on equity. A firm that earns a high, sustainable ROE deserves to trade at a higher multiple of its book value; a firm that earns a low ROE deserves a lower multiple. In rough terms, a broker earning an ROE around its cost of equity trades near 1.0x book; a broker earning a much higher ROE can justify 1.5x, 2x, or more. Because a broker's ROE is itself wildly cyclical — it can leap from ~10% in a normal year to ~20% in a bull and collapse toward zero or negative in a bust — the *justified* P/B moves with it.

### The double whammy: earnings and the multiple re-rate together

Here is the mechanism that makes broker stocks move even more than broker *earnings*: in a bull market, a broker's earnings rise **and** its P/B multiple expands, at the same time, in the same direction. This is the **double whammy** — the stock price is the product of two things (book-value-per-share growth and the P/B multiple), and in a broker both rise together in a boom and both fall together in a bust. That is why a broker stock can triple in a bull even though the underlying business "merely" doubled its profit: the doubling of earnings drives book value up, *and* the re-rating of the P/B multiple from, say, 1.0x to 1.8x compounds on top. The same two factors compound on the way down, which is why brokers halve when the market merely falls a third.

#### Worked example: the P/B re-rate

Suppose a broker trades at **1.0x book value** with a normal-year ROE of **10%**. A liquidity bull arrives. Its loan book fills, brokerage volumes surge, the prop book marks up, and its ROE jumps to **20%**. Two things now happen. First, the higher ROE grows book value faster (more retained profit). Second, the market re-rates the multiple: a 20% ROE justifies a much higher multiple of book, so the stock re-rates from 1.0x to, say, **1.8x book**. If book value per share rose ~15% over the year from the fat profit *and* the multiple went from 1.0x to 1.8x, the stock price moves by roughly 1.15 × 1.8 = **2.07x** — it more than doubles, even though book value only rose 15%. The intuition: in a broker, earnings and the valuation multiple re-rate in the same direction at once, so the stock amplifies the business — and that double compounding is a second source of beta layered on top of the operating gearing.

## The zero-fee and tech-broker disruption

A structural force is reshaping broker economics, and it bears directly on how you value the sector: the **collapse of brokerage fees toward zero**, driven by tech-first disruptors. VPS led the charge by competing on near-zero execution costs to win retail volume; TCBS and others pushed digital-first, low-fee models. The State has at times even discussed or implemented changes to the fee structure that compress the floor further. The direction is unmistakable: the pure execution commission — line one of the four — is being competed toward zero.

What does this do to the sector? It does *not* kill brokers, but it **changes which line carries the profit.** As the commission shrinks, the relative importance of **margin-lending interest** (line two) rises — the firms that win are the ones that can fund a large, cheap loan book and monetize the cheap-execution retail flow through *lending* rather than *commissions*. This is the same playbook the global discount brokers ran: give away the trade, make the money on the financing (and on the float, and on order flow where permitted). The Vietnamese version tilts the sector's profit mix further toward the margin book, which has two consequences. First, it makes the sector *even more* geared to the margin cycle, because the biggest profit line is now the most cyclical balance-sheet line. Second, it favors firms with cheap, deep funding — bank-backed brokers (TCBS behind Techcombank, MBS behind MB) and the largest, best-capitalized houses — over small firms that cannot fund a big book cheaply.

For an investor, the disruption means you cannot value a broker on brokerage commissions alone, and you must increasingly ask: *how big and how cheap is the loan book, and how cheaply can this firm fund it?* The zero-fee world rewards balance-sheet scale and cheap funding, not execution share — which is precisely why the volume leader (VPS) and the most profitable firms are not always the same.

## Common misconceptions

Several widely held beliefs about the broker sector get investors hurt. Each is corrected with a number.

**Misconception 1: "Brokers are safe financials, like banks."** They are not. Banks earn a spread on a vast, diversified loan book that grinds out earnings through most of the cycle, backed by deposits and regulated to be stable (the bank sector is the subject of the companion post on [the banking sector as the heartbeat of VN-Index](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/banking-sector-vietnam-the-heartbeat-of-vn-index)). A broker has none of that ballast: every line is geared to market activity, there is no counter-cyclical buffer, and the prop book is marked to market on equity prices every quarter. The numbers make the point — in 2022 the broker sector fell ~58% while the bank sector fell far less; brokers are *high-beta financials*, the opposite of defensive. Treating a securities firm like a stable financial utility is a category error.

**Misconception 2: "A high margin balance is always bullish for brokers."** A *rising* margin balance off a low base is genuinely good for broker interest income — the loan book is growing, the spread is earned on a bigger base. But a margin balance at an **all-time record**, with the system stretched and brokers raising fresh capital to lend more, is a *late-cycle* warning, not a green light. The record ~370 trillion dong margin balance of 2025 means broker interest income is high *now*, but it also means the fuel tank is nearly full — and a full tank can only drain. A record margin balance is simultaneously the source of today's broker profit and the measure of tomorrow's deleveraging risk. The level and context matter, not just the direction.

**Misconception 3: "Market share equals profit."** It does not, and the clearest counterexample is the volume leader itself. VPS holds the largest brokerage market share (~18%) but earns relatively little per trade by design, because it won that share with near-zero fees. A smaller firm with a fat, well-funded margin book and a conservative prop treasury can be substantially *more profitable per share* than the volume leader. Share is contestable and visible; profit depends on the *mix* — how much of the franchise is cheap-execution volume versus high-spread lending versus prop. When you pick a broker, you are choosing a business mix, and the highest-share firm is not automatically the most profitable one.

**Misconception 4: "If the market goes up, all brokers win equally."** They do not, because the prop book differentiates them. Two firms with identical brokerage and margin businesses can post wildly different earnings in the same year depending on how aggressively they ran their proprietary equity books. In a crash, the bond-heavy conservative firm merely earns less while the equity-heavy aggressive firm swings to a loss; in a bull, the aggressive firm books outsized prop gains. The sector beta is an *average*; individual broker beta ranges from "high" to "dangerously high" depending on the prop book.

**Misconception 5: "Brokers are cheap when their P/E is low."** This is a classic value trap in cyclical financials. A broker's P/E looks lowest exactly at the *peak* of the cycle, when earnings are at a cyclical high in the denominator — and that is precisely the worst time to buy, because earnings are about to fall. A broker's P/E looks highest (or goes meaningless) at the *trough*, when earnings are crushed — which is often the best time to buy. P/B, tied to ROE, is the better lens, and even then you want to buy when ROE is *depressed and about to recover*, not when it is at a record. Low P/E in a broker is usually a warning, not a bargain.

## How it shows up on VN-Index

Theory is cheap. Here is the broker sector in the actual record of VN-Index, with dates and numbers — the boom, the bust, and the recovery.

### The 2021 liquidity boom: +110%

After the COVID crash of March 2020, the State Bank cut rates aggressively and a wall of new retail money flooded into the market. HOSE ADTV climbed from ~7.4 trillion dong/day in 2020 to ~21.6 trillion in 2021; new brokerage accounts hit records; and the total margin balance more than doubled from ~90 trillion dong at end-2020 to a ~195 trillion peak. For brokers, every one of the four lines fired at once: brokerage commissions surged on the turnover, margin interest surged on the doubling loan book, prop books marked up on the rising index, and IB boomed as companies rushed to issue and list into the buoyant market. Broker net profits roughly **doubled or more** across the sector, ROEs leaped from normal levels toward 20%+, and P/B multiples re-rated up — the double whammy. The broker sector returned roughly **+110%** on the year against the index's ~+36%, an implied beta near 3x. It was the textbook leveraged-index melt-up: brokers led the market up because their earnings re-rated *before and faster than* the rest of the market.

### The 2022 forced-deleveraging crash: −58%

Then the loop reversed. Global central banks hiked through 2022, the State Bank followed, and a domestic corporate-bond crisis (*trai phieu doanh nghiep*) erupted in late 2022, freezing a key funding channel and bleeding straight into equities. VN-Index fell from ~1,500 in early 2022 to a low near 874 in November — more than 40% off the peak. The broker sector did not fall 40%; it fell about **−58%**, the hardest-hit major sector. All four lines reversed at once: turnover collapsed (commissions down), clients deleveraged and the margin book shrank ~37% from ~195 to ~122 trillion dong (interest income down), prop books took mark-to-market losses on the falling index (some firms swung from prop gains to prop losses), and the IB window slammed shut (no deals). ROEs collapsed, P/B multiples de-rated, and the double whammy ran in reverse: falling earnings *and* a falling multiple, compounding the decline. The same firms that doubled in 2021 roughly halved. Brokers led the market down for the same reason they led it up — they are the leveraged proxy.

### The 2023-2024 recovery: +75%, then a quieter year

The system rebuilt. Through 2023, falling rates and recovering confidence pulled the margin balance back up toward ~180 trillion dong and lifted turnover, and the broker sector roared back roughly **+75%** against the index's ~+12% — once again amplifying the recovery, because the same gearing that crushed brokers in the bust now powered them off the bottom. The sectors that fall hardest in a deleveraging crash typically bounce hardest off the bottom, and brokers are the archetype: the leverage that crushed them amplifies the recovery. In 2024, a quieter and less directional year, both the broker sector and the index returned roughly +12% — the amplification fades when there is less directional move to amplify, which is itself a useful tell: brokers shine when the market *trends*, and they offer no special edge when it chops sideways.

![Grouped bar chart of broker sector net profit index by year showing boom and bust](/imgs/blogs/securities-brokers-sector-vietnam-highest-beta-6.png)

The chart above renders the boom-bust in profit terms. Set the broker sector's net profit at 100 in 2020. It leaped to ~230 in the 2021 bull (the +130% boom), collapsed to ~95 in the 2022 crash (back below the 2020 base — nearly halved from the 2020 level and far below the 2021 peak), then re-accelerated to ~180 in 2023 and ~210 in 2024 as the cycle turned up. The shape is the shape of the four geared lines moving together: profit more than doubles in a boom and roughly halves in a bust, and the stock price — through the double whammy of earnings and multiple — moves even more than the profit.

### Watch the four lines together

The reason this matters in real time is that the four lines move *together*, so you do not need to wait for the earnings report to see the cycle turning. Rising ADTV tells you brokerage revenue is rising; a rising system margin balance tells you interest income is rising; a rising index tells you prop books are marking up; a busy IPO and bond calendar tells you IB is busy. When all four are improving off a low base, broker earnings are about to inflect up — and the stocks usually move ahead of the print. When all four are at records simultaneously, broker earnings are near a cyclical peak — and the stocks usually peak ahead of the eventual earnings decline. The four-line model is not just an explanation; it is a real-time dashboard.

![Matrix of four broker revenue lines mapped to their driver and cyclicality](/imgs/blogs/securities-brokers-sector-vietnam-highest-beta-7.png)

The matrix above is that dashboard in one figure. Each row is one of the four revenue lines; the first column names what drives it, and the second column rates its cyclicality. Brokerage is driven by turnover (high cyclicality — turnover triples in a bull); margin interest by the loan book and rates (high — the book doubles then halves); prop trading by the index (very high — gains turn to mark-to-market losses); IB by deal flow (medium — deals freeze in a bear). The visual claim is that there is no row marked "low" or "counter-cyclical": every line is geared to the market in the same direction, which is the whole reason the sector beta stacks so high.

## The playbook: trading the highest-beta sector

Everything above is diagnosis. Here is what to *do* with it. The core idea is simple: **treat the broker sector as a leveraged proxy for the index, and use it to amplify a market-timing view you already hold.** You do not buy brokers because you have a special insight into brokerage commissions; you buy them because you are bullish on the *market* and you want a magnified version of that view. The sector is a beta amplifier — a way to dial up exposure to a liquidity upturn — and the entire skill is knowing when to dial it up and when to dial it back.

### When to own brokers (the entry signal)

Own the broker sector when you are bullish on the market *and* the conditions favor the four lines inflecting up. Concretely, the entry signal is an **early-cycle liquidity upturn**: rates falling with a supportive State Bank, ADTV rising off a low base, the total margin balance recovering off a trough (not at a record), and new account openings picking up. In that configuration, all four broker lines are about to improve from a low base, ROEs are about to recover, and P/B multiples are about to re-rate up from a depressed level — the double whammy is poised to work *for* you. This is the highest-conviction time to overweight brokers, because you are buying depressed earnings *and* a depressed multiple, both about to rise.

The deepest version of this entry is to **buy the deleveraged bottom**: after a crash, when the margin balance has collapsed (the system has de-risked), margin-to-equity has reset to slack, and ADTV has bottomed and started to tick up. That is the mirror image of the late-cycle warning, and brokers — having fallen hardest — typically bounce hardest off it. The 2023 +75% rebound is the template.

There is also a slower, structural reason to hold a core broker position through the cycle rather than purely trading it: the Vietnamese market is on a long-term growth path that lifts all four broker lines over time. The penetration of retail investing is still low relative to developed peers, account openings keep trending up across cycles, and an eventual market-status upgrade (the much-discussed move toward emerging-market classification by the major index providers) would pull in a wave of foreign passive money, lifting turnover and the margin base structurally. A broker is the most direct listed proxy for that secular rise in market activity, not just the cyclical swings. The discipline, then, is two-layered: hold a *core* broker position for the structural growth in trading activity, and *trade a tactical sleeve* on top of it that you dial up in early-cycle upturns and dial down at stretched tops — the structural floor under the sector is real, but it does not protect you from the ~58% cyclical drawdowns, so the tactical overlay still matters most for your returns.

#### Worked example: sizing the broker sleeve through the cycle

Say you run a 1 billion dong portfolio and your tactical "high-beta sleeve" tops out at 30% — 300 million dong (~\$11,800 at \$1 = 25,400 VND) — of which brokers are the core. **Early cycle** (margin slack, ADTV rising off a low, rates falling): you run the full 300 million in high-beta names, brokers first, because the reflexive loop is working for you and brokers give you ~2-3x the index's upside. Suppose the index then rallies 30% and brokers (at ~2.5x beta) rally ~75%: your 300 million sleeve gains ~225 million dong while a same-sized index position would have gained ~90 million — the broker tilt added ~135 million dong (~\$5,300) of extra gain. **Late cycle** (margin at a record, brokers raising capital, account openings euphoric): you cut the broker sleeve hard, because the same ~2.5x beta now points down. If you trim from 300 to 100 million before a crash and the sleeve falls 55%, your loss is ~55 million instead of ~165 million — you saved ~110 million dong. The intuition: brokers are the lever, and the whole game is pushing the lever in an early-cycle upturn and pulling it before a stretched top, because the same beta that triples your gain triples your loss.

### When to trim or avoid brokers (the exit signal)

Trim the broker sector when the market is stretched and the four lines are at or near a cyclical peak: **ADTV at a record, the total margin balance at an all-time high, brokers announcing rights issues to expand their loan books (they have hit the 2x cap), new account openings euphoric, and the index extended.** This is the same late-cycle configuration that signals "size down" for the whole market, but it signals it *louder* for brokers, because brokers carry the highest beta — they will fall hardest in the cascade. The crucial discipline is that this exit signal fires when broker earnings look *best* (peak ROE, low P/E) — which is exactly the value trap. You are not selling because the business looks bad; you are selling because the business looks too good to last and the stock is about to suffer the double whammy in reverse.

Avoid brokers entirely in a confirmed downturn: once the deleveraging cascade has begun (floor-price clusters in high-beta names, margin balance falling, brokers cutting margin ratios), brokers are the worst place to be, because all four lines are reversing and the prop book is marking down. Wait for the leverage to actually come out — the signal to re-enter is "the margin balance has stopped falling and started to tick up," not "the index fell a lot."

### Picking the broker within the sector

Once you have decided to own the sector, the company choice turns on the prop book and the funding. For maximum beta to a bull, an equity-heavy prop book and an aggressive loan book give you the most amplification — but the most downside in a bust. For a more durable franchise, favor firms with **cheap, deep funding** (bank-backed houses, the largest best-capitalized firms) and a **conservative prop book**, because in the zero-fee world the profit increasingly comes from the margin loan book, and the firms that can fund a big book cheaply will out-earn the volume leaders. Always check the **share count**: a broker diluting heavily to grow its book at the 2x cap can grow profit while the stock stagnates. And remember that market share is not profit — the volume leader is not automatically the best stock.

### The invalidation: when this view is wrong

A disciplined view names what would prove it wrong. The "own brokers as a leveraged index proxy" thesis is **invalidated when the market is rolling over, not turning up.** If rates are rising, ADTV is falling, the margin balance is contracting, and the index is breaking down, then the broker beta points the wrong way — owning brokers in that regime is the single fastest way to amplify a loss, and being long the sector is the error. Symmetrically, the "avoid brokers" stance is invalidated when the cycle is *early and slack* — margin recovering off a trough, rates falling, ADTV rising from a low — because then the high beta works in your favor and refusing to own the sector means missing the most amplified leg of the recovery.

The skill is not a permanent view on brokers; it is reading **which way the market is about to move and pressing the broker lever in that direction with conviction, then having the discipline to release it before a stretched top.** Get that one judgment right and the broker sector becomes the most powerful market-timing amplifier the Vietnamese exchange offers: ~3x the index when you are right about the direction, which is exactly why it is the highest-beta bet on the market itself.
