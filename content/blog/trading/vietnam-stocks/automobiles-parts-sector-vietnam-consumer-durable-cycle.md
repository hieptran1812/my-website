> **TL;DR** — A car is the classic *deferrable big-ticket consumer durable*: you do not have to buy one this quarter, so demand swings violently with income, financing rates and the government's on-off 50% registration-fee cut — and that makes auto sales and auto-sector earnings far more cyclical than the smooth GDP line they sit inside.
>

VinFast is the opposite: a pure growth bet valued on EV/sales, not earnings, that hit roughly **175,000 EV deliveries in Vietnam in 2025** (~29% market share).
> - The number to remember: Vietnam's annual car sales swung from about **510,000 units in 2022** to roughly **340,000 in 2024**, a fall of about a third — that one-third swing in volume is what makes the whole sector a cyclical you trade off the rate and policy cycle, not a buy-and-hold compounder.

In the third quarter of 2020, something happened in Vietnamese car showrooms that looked, from the outside, like a contradiction. The country was in the middle of a pandemic, the economy was bruised, household incomes were under pressure — and yet dealers could barely keep cars on the floor. Showroom traffic spiked, waiting lists lengthened, and domestic-assembled models in particular flew out the door. The cause was not a sudden burst of national optimism. It was a single line in a government decree: from late June 2020, the **registration fee** on domestically assembled cars was cut by 50%. Overnight, the cash a buyer had to hand over to put a car on the road dropped by tens of millions of dong, and a wave of people who had been *thinking* about buying a car — someday — decided that someday was now.

That episode is the whole sector in miniature, and it repeated almost exactly in the second half of 2023 and into 2024 when the 50% cut was switched back on to support a weak market. A car is not like a bag of rice or a bottle of fish sauce. You buy rice every week whether the economy is good or bad. A car you can *defer* — your old one still runs, you can take a Grab, you can wait. That deferrability is the defining property of a **consumer durable**, and it is why auto demand does not track the smooth GDP line; it swings, hard, with anything that changes the *timing* of a purchase you were always going to make eventually: your income and job confidence, the interest rate on the car loan, the price of fuel — and, in Vietnam most of all, whether the registration fee is currently being cut.

This post is about how to think in that category. We will build the consumer-durable demand cycle from zero — why deferrability is the engine of the swing — and then map it onto the genuinely unusual structure of the Vietnamese listed auto sector, where there is no pure carmaker to buy and the cleanest exposure is a holding company called VEA that you own essentially for its dividend. We will work through the registration-fee math that drives the surges, the VEA dividend-proxy model, the rise of VinFast and the EV transition, and how to value each of the very different things that hide under the label "auto stock". And we will end where this series always ends: a concrete playbook for trading the sector off the policy and rate cycle — when to buy ahead of a fee-cut-plus-rate-cut demand wave, and exactly what would prove the thesis wrong.

![Flow chart showing rising income, low rates and a registration-fee cut pulling a deferrable car purchase forward into a sales surge that lifts dealers, parts makers and the VEA joint-venture dividend](/imgs/blogs/automobiles-parts-sector-vietnam-consumer-durable-cycle-1.png)

A quick word on money, because we will use it throughout. Vietnam trades in dong (VND), and the sector's primary numbers — car prices, sales volumes, dividends, market caps — are all quoted in dong. As a reference for readers who think in dollars, the exchange rate in mid-2026 is roughly **\$1 = 25,400 VND**. So an 800 million dong car is about **\$31,500**; a VEA market cap of 53 trillion dong is about a **\$2.1bn** market cap; a foreign net buy of 200 billion dong is about **\$7.9mn**. We will keep the primary figures in dong (that is how the market quotes them) and drop a dollar anchor whenever it helps you feel the size — and because the *inputs* to the business, from imported components to the global vehicle platforms the JVs assemble, are partly dollar-priced, dollars are a natural sanity-check unit too.

## Foundations: why a car is a deferrable big-ticket purchase

Forget the stock for a moment and think like a household. You earn a salary, you have monthly expenses — food, rent, school fees, a motorbike that gets you to work — and somewhere on your list of wants is a car. A car is two things at once that no weekly purchase is: it is **expensive** (it costs a large multiple of your monthly income, so you almost certainly finance part of it with a loan), and it is **durable** (once you own one, it lasts years, so you do not need to buy another for a long time). Those two properties — *big-ticket* and *durable* — combine into the single most important behavioural fact about the product: the purchase is **deferrable**.

Deferrable means you have discretion over the *timing*. You will probably buy a car at some point in the next few years if your income rises and your family grows. But you do not have to buy it *this* quarter. If money feels tight, if the loan rate looks high, if you are nervous about your job, if the price feels like it might fall — you simply wait. Your existing motorbike or older car keeps working. The demand does not disappear; it gets *pushed into the future*. And just as easily, if something makes buying suddenly more attractive — a pay rise, a rate cut, a fee cut, a new model you love — demand that was sitting in the future gets *pulled into the present*. This is **pulling demand forward**, and it is the master mechanism of the entire consumer-durable cycle. Auto sales are not a steady stream; they are a reservoir of deferrable purchases that fills up in bad times and floods out in good times.

Contrast this with a **consumer staple** — food, household goods, basic personal-care products. You consume those continuously and you cannot defer them; you have to eat this week regardless of the economy. So staples demand is *smooth*: it grows slowly with population and incomes and barely wobbles in a downturn. (We cover that sector in its own post.) Durables are the mirror image. Because the purchase is deferrable, durables demand is *volatile* — it amplifies the cycle rather than smoothing it. When you put the cyclical durable and the smooth staple on the same chart, the durable swings around the staple's steady grind, and that contrast is the cleanest way to *see* what "cyclical" means.

So what, concretely, moves the timing decision? Four drivers, and you should hold all four because the sector's quarters are explained by which combination is firing.

The **first driver is income and confidence.** A car is bought out of household income and savings, so when incomes rise and people feel secure in their jobs, more households cross the threshold where a car is affordable and sensible, and they buy. When incomes stall or job fear rises, they defer. This is why auto sales are a *coincident-to-lagging* read on the consumer cycle: they confirm that households feel rich enough to commit to a multi-year purchase.

The **second driver is the financing rate.** Most cars are bought partly on credit — a car loan from a bank or a finance company. What the buyer actually feels is not the sticker price but the **monthly payment**, and the monthly payment depends heavily on the interest rate. When the State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) cuts policy rates and bank lending rates fall, the monthly payment on the same car drops, more buyers qualify for the loan, and demand rises. When rates are high — as they were in late 2022 and much of 2023 — the monthly payment balloons, marginal buyers are priced out, and demand sags. This is why the auto sector is rate-sensitive in the same direction as property and brokers: it is a *credit-financed* purchase, so it loves a rate-cutting cycle.

It is worth feeling how big the rate effect is, because it is easy to underestimate. Take a buyer financing **600 million dong** of a car over five years. At a lending rate of **9%**, the monthly payment is roughly 12.5 million dong. Now push the rate to **14%** — roughly what happened when the SBV tightened hard in late 2022 — and the monthly payment on the same loan jumps to about 14 million dong, an extra ~1.5 million dong every month for five years. For a household budgeting carefully, that swing is the difference between "we can afford this" and "let's wait" — and the *bank* sees it too, because a higher rate means the buyer qualifies for a *smaller* loan at the same income, so the affordable car shrinks. The rate cycle therefore works on demand through two channels at once: it changes the monthly payment on a given car, and it changes how much car a given income can finance. Both push the same way, which is why a rate-cutting cycle is such a powerful tailwind for autos and a hiking cycle is so brutal.

The **third driver, and the one most specific to Vietnam, is registration-fee policy.** When you buy a car in Vietnam you do not just pay the sticker price; you pay a **registration fee** (lệ phí trước bạ) to put the car legally on the road, calculated as a percentage of the car's value — typically around 10-12% depending on the locality and vehicle type. That is a large, lumpy, *cash* cost on top of the price. Periodically, to support the domestic auto-assembly industry and stimulate demand, the government issues a decree cutting that fee by **50%** for domestically assembled cars, usually for six months at a time. Because the fee is a percentage of a big number, halving it saves the buyer a large, visible chunk of cash, exactly at the moment of purchase — and because the cut is *temporary and announced*, it creates a deadline that pulls demand forward in a rush. This policy lever has been pulled in 2020, in 2021-22, and again in 2023H2-2024, and each time it produced a visible surge.

The **fourth driver is fuel and total-cost-of-ownership economics**, increasingly the *EV* economics. The running cost of a car — fuel, maintenance, the resale value — feeds into the decision, and the rise of cheap electric vehicles (charging cheaper than petrol, fewer moving parts) is reshaping that calculation in Vietnam faster than almost anywhere, because of one company we will spend real time on: VinFast. The total-cost-of-ownership argument for an EV is simple: even if the up-front price is similar, the *running* cost is much lower — electricity to charge is cheaper per kilometre than petrol, and an electric drivetrain has far fewer moving parts to service. For a buyer who drives a lot, that lower running cost can tip the deferral decision toward "buy now, and buy electric", which both *adds* to total demand at the margin and *shifts* the mix away from the petrol cars sold through the traditional JVs and dealers. The fuel/EV driver is therefore doing two jobs at once: it nudges the timing of the purchase, and it reshuffles *which* part of the listed sector benefits.

There is also a slow, structural tailwind sitting underneath all four cyclical drivers, and it matters for how you frame the sector over years rather than quarters: Vietnam is still a *low-motorisation* country. Far more households own a motorbike than a car, and car ownership per thousand people is a fraction of the level in richer neighbours like Thailand or Malaysia. As incomes rise over the coming decade, a growing share of Vietnamese households will cross the threshold from motorbike to first car — a structural, multi-year increase in the *baseline* number of car buyers. This is the long-run growth story the cyclical swings ride on top of: the trend is up (more cars as the country gets richer), but the *path* is violently cyclical (deferrable demand swinging with income, rates and the fee cut). A good auto investor holds both ideas at once — a rising long-term floor, and a volatile cyclical path around it — and uses the cyclical troughs as entry points into a structurally growing market, rather than mistaking a cyclical air-pocket for the end of the growth story.

Put these four together and you have the consumer-durable demand cycle. Income and confidence set the *baseline* of how many households are ready to buy; the financing rate sets the *monthly affordability*; the registration-fee policy sets the *timing trigger*; and fuel/EV economics increasingly shape *which* car they choose. When three or four line up favourably — rising income, falling rates, an active fee cut — deferrable demand floods forward and you get a sales surge. When they line up against you — flat income, high rates, no fee cut — demand drains into the reservoir and sales fall off a cliff.

#### Worked example: the registration-fee cut, in cash

Make the master lever concrete, because the whole sector trades off it. Take a mid-range domestically assembled car with a sticker price of **800 million dong**, about **\$31,500** at \$1 = 25,400 VND. The registration fee is, say, **10%** of value — **80 million dong** of cash the buyer must pay to register the car, on *top* of the 800 million for the car itself. So the real "out-the-door" cost is about **880 million dong**.

Now the government switches on the 50% cut. The fee drops to **5%** — **40 million dong** instead of 80 million. The buyer saves **40 million dong** of up-front cash, about **\$1,575**, and the out-the-door cost falls to about **840 million dong**. Forty million dong is real money — roughly two to three months of a comfortable urban salary — and crucially it is *cash you save right now*, not a vague future benefit. A household that was on the fence, planning to "maybe buy next year", suddenly has a concrete reason to buy *before the cut expires*. The intuition: the fee cut does not create new car buyers out of thin air; it *pulls forward in time* purchases that were going to happen anyway, which is exactly why a fee cut produces a sharp surge followed by an air-pocket once it lapses.

![Two column before and after comparison of an eight hundred million dong car showing a ten percent registration fee versus a halved five percent fee cutting forty million dong off the out the door price](/imgs/blogs/automobiles-parts-sector-vietnam-consumer-durable-cycle-4.png)

That figure is the demand lever in one picture. On the left, the normal 10% fee adds 80 million dong of cash cost and the buyer defers; on the right, the 50%-cut 5% fee adds only 40 million, saving 40 million dong, and the buyer acts now. **Nothing about the car changed.** The fee changed, and the fee is the timing trigger. This is why, in Vietnam more than in most markets, you can practically set your watch by the auto sector: when a fee-cut decree lands, the surge is coming.

## What actually drives auto-sector earnings

Now connect the demand cycle to *profit*, because that is what the stocks track. The sector's earnings are driven by **volume** above all — how many cars (and motorbikes, and tyres, and parts) actually get sold — because almost every business in the auto chain has high fixed costs and earns its money by spreading them over volume. When the deferrable-demand reservoir floods forward and volume surges, the fixed-cost base gets covered many times over and profit explodes; when volume drains away, the same fixed costs eat the thin remaining margin and profit collapses. This is **operating leverage**, and it is what turns a one-third swing in unit sales into a much larger swing in earnings.

Look at the real volume history. Vietnam's annual car sales (on the VAMA-members-plus-listed-brands basis, which excludes some direct-sales brands) ran around 410,000 units in 2019-2021, surged to roughly **510,000 in 2022** on pent-up post-COVID demand and an active fee cut, then fell hard to about **370,000 in 2023** and roughly **340,000 in 2024** as high interest rates and weak income bit — before recovering toward **380,000 in 2025**. That is a peak-to-trough swing of about a third in just two years.

![Bar chart of Vietnam car sales volume by year from 2019 to 2025 with the registration fee cut years highlighted showing a 2022 peak near 510 thousand units and a 2024 trough near 340 thousand](/imgs/blogs/automobiles-parts-sector-vietnam-consumer-durable-cycle-2.png)

The chart above is the demand cycle made visible. Notice two things. First, the **fee-cut years are marked** — 2020, then 2023H2-2024 — and they coincide with demand being *supported* against an otherwise weak backdrop; the policy lever is doing visible work. Second, look at the shape of 2022 to 2024: a 510,000-unit peak collapsing to 340,000 in two years. That collapse was not caused by the cars getting worse or the companies doing anything wrong. It was the deferrable-demand reservoir draining as rates spiked and incomes stalled — exactly the consumer-durable mechanism we built in the Foundations section, playing out in the data.

Now, the demand cycle hits each *type* of auto business differently, and this is where the unusual Vietnamese structure matters enormously. Let me lay out the four ways you can own the sector, because they are genuinely different animals with different drivers and different valuations.

### The four listed auto plays

Here is the fact that surprises everyone who comes to the Vietnamese auto sector expecting to "buy a carmaker": **there is no pure carmaker listed on HOSE.** The big-volume brands in Vietnam — Toyota, Honda, Ford, Hyundai, Kia, Mazda — are sold through *joint ventures* and assemblers that are either foreign-owned, privately held, or held inside conglomerates, not listed as standalone carmaker equities you can buy on the Ho Chi Minh exchange. So you cannot simply "buy the carmaker". You own the sector through four very different vehicles.

![Matrix of the four listed auto plays VEA dealers parts and tyres and VinFast across columns for what it is main driver and dividend versus growth](/imgs/blogs/automobiles-parts-sector-vietnam-consumer-durable-cycle-7.png)

The matrix above is the map of the sector. Read it row by row.

The **first play is VEA** (Vietnam Engine and Agricultural Machinery Corporation, ticker VEA on the UPCoM market). VEA is the closest thing to "owning the carmakers", but indirectly: it holds large *minority* stakes in the Honda Vietnam, Toyota Vietnam and Ford Vietnam joint ventures. It does not run them, and it does not consolidate their revenue onto its own income statement. Instead it books its *share* of their profit as **equity income**, and because those JVs are mature, cash-generative and require little reinvestment from VEA, VEA accumulates cash and pays most of it out as a **high dividend**. VEA is, in effect, a *dividend proxy* for the profits of Honda, Toyota and Ford in Vietnam. We will model this in detail because it is the cleanest and most-owned way institutions get auto exposure.

The **second play is the dealers** — companies like **HAX** (Haxaco, the largest Mercedes-Benz dealer in Vietnam) and **Savico** (ticker SVC, a large multi-brand auto retail group). A dealer does not make cars; it *sells* them, taking a gross margin on each car of typically **4-6%** plus service and parts revenue. Dealers are the *highest-operating-leverage* play on the demand cycle: their margin is thin, their costs (showrooms, staff, floorplan financing) are largely fixed, so a volume surge drops straight to the bottom line and a volume slump devastates it. Dealers are how you make a *geared* bet on the sales cycle itself.

The **third play is parts and tyres** — makers like **DRC** (Danang Rubber Company, tyres) and **CSM** (Casumina / Southern Rubber, tyres). These businesses sell into both the new-vehicle build (original equipment) and the replacement market (every car needs new tyres eventually), and they are exposed to *input costs* — natural and synthetic rubber, steel, carbon black — as well as to export demand, since they ship tyres abroad. They are a *mid-cyclical* play: more stable than dealers because the replacement market provides a base, but geared to the vehicle cycle and to commodity input prices.

The *replacement market* is the key to why the parts-and-tyre makers are less violently cyclical than the dealers, so it is worth a beat. A dealer's revenue depends almost entirely on *new-car* sales, which are the most deferrable part of the whole picture — you can put off buying a *new* car for years. But a tyre wears out whether or not the economy is good; a car already on the road *needs* new tyres after a certain mileage regardless of the cycle. So a tyre maker's revenue has two layers: a *cyclical* layer that rises and falls with new-vehicle production, and a *replacement* layer that grows steadily with the *installed base* of vehicles already on the road — and that installed base only ever grows (cars accumulate; they are scrapped slowly). The replacement layer puts a sturdier floor under the parts makers' earnings than the dealers have, which is precisely why they sit one notch lower on the beta scale. The flip side is that their margin is squeezed by *input-cost* volatility: when rubber and steel prices spike, the cost of making each tyre rises and the margin compresses, independent of how many tyres they sell. So you trade the parts makers off *two* cycles at once — the vehicle cycle on the demand side and the commodity cycle on the cost side — which makes them a genuinely different animal from the pure-volume dealers.

The **fourth play is VinFast** (ticker VFS), and it is unlike the other three in every way. VinFast is the EV arm of the Vingroup conglomerate, it is **listed in the United States, not on HOSE**, and it is not a dividend or value story at all — it is a *pure growth bet* on Vietnam's (and eventually global) EV transition, burning cash to gain share, valued on **EV/sales** rather than earnings because it does not yet make a profit. VinFast is the disruptor, and it is reshaping the whole demand picture.

#### Worked example: a dealer's thin, geared margin

Make the dealer model concrete, because "4-6% gross margin" is abstract until you put dong on it. Suppose a dealer sells a car with a sticker price of **1 billion dong** (about **\$39,400**) at a gross margin of **5%**. The gross profit on that single car is 1,000 million × 5% = **50 million dong**, about **\$1,970**. That is the gross profit *before* the dealer pays for its showroom, its sales staff, its floorplan financing (the interest on the inventory of cars sitting on the lot), and its overhead.

Now see why this is so geared. Suppose the dealer's fixed costs run to 30 million dong per car at normal volume. Net profit per car is 50 − 30 = **20 million dong**. Sales are strong, the dealer is busy, life is good. Now a downturn hits and volume falls by a third (exactly the 510k-to-340k swing we saw in the data). The fixed costs do not fall — the showroom rent and the staff are still there — so fixed cost per car *rises* (say to 45 million as the same costs spread over fewer cars), and net profit per car collapses to 50 − 45 = **5 million dong**, a 75% fall in profit from a 33% fall in volume. The intuition: a dealer's thin gross margin sits on a fixed-cost base, so volume is everything — which is precisely why dealers are the highest-beta way to play the auto demand cycle, and why you buy them *ahead* of a demand surge, not during the slump.

## VEA: the dividend proxy, in detail

VEA deserves its own section because it is the most important and least understood name in the sector, and because it is a beautiful example of a holding-company *dividend proxy* — a structure you will meet again and again in markets.

Here is the mechanism. VEA owns large minority stakes in three joint ventures: roughly **30% of Honda Vietnam** (which dominates the motorbike market and also sells cars), around **20% of Toyota Vietnam** (the best-selling car brand in the country), and about **25% of Ford Vietnam** (strong in SUVs and pickups). These are *associates* in accounting terms — VEA does not control them, so it does not put their full revenue on its books. Instead, each year VEA recognises its *proportional share* of each JV's net profit as a single line: **equity income from associates**. Because Honda Vietnam in particular is a motorbike cash machine (Vietnam buys millions of motorbikes a year, and Honda dominates), that equity income is large and reliable.

Crucially, VEA's *own* business — engines and agricultural machinery — is small and not very profitable; the value of VEA is almost entirely the JV stakes. And because the JVs are mature and self-funding, VEA does not need to plough the equity income back in. So the cash piles up on VEA's balance sheet, and VEA pays it out as a **high cash dividend**. That is the whole investment case: you buy VEA not as a carmaker, not for growth, but as a *cash-cow dividend proxy* for the steady profits of Honda, Toyota and Ford in Vietnam.

![Flow chart showing VEA holding minority stakes in the Honda Toyota and Ford joint ventures booking their profit as equity income building cash and paying a high dividend to holders](/imgs/blogs/automobiles-parts-sector-vietnam-consumer-durable-cycle-5.png)

The figure traces the proxy. Three JV stakes feed *equity income* into VEA; the equity income, needing little reinvestment, builds *cash*; the cash is paid out as a *high dividend*. Notice the lavender JV boxes — they are **counterparties**, not part of VEA itself; VEA is just a claim on their profits. This is the load-bearing distinction: VEA is not a carmaker. If you value it as a carmaker, on revenue and growth, you will get it wrong. You value it the way you value any cash-cow holding company: on its dividend yield and the durability of the underlying JV profits.

#### Worked example: the VEA dividend yield

This is the case in numbers. Suppose VEA trades at about **40,000 dong** per share, and in a typical year it pays a cash dividend of about **5,000 dong** per share out of its JV equity income. The dividend yield is 5,000 / 40,000 = **12.5%**. To a beginner that number looks almost too good — a 12% yield in a market where bank deposits pay maybe 5-6% — so let me explain why it can be real and what the catch is.

It can be real because VEA pays out *almost all* of its earnings (it has little need to retain cash for growth), and because the underlying JV profits — especially Honda's motorbike profits — are genuinely large and stable. So the high yield is partly just a high *payout ratio* on a mature cash cow, not a distress signal. The catch is twofold. First, VEA's profit is *not* growing much — it is a yield play, not a compounder, so you are buying the cash flow, not capital appreciation. Second, the yield is only as safe as the JV profits: if Honda's motorbike sales or Toyota's car sales fall in a downturn, the equity income falls and the dividend can be cut. The intuition: VEA hands you a fat ~12% cash yield in exchange for almost no growth and full exposure to the JVs' cyclical profits — a bond-like cash cow with equity-like downside, which is exactly how you should size it.

This dividend-proxy structure is also why VEA is *less* cyclical than the dealers. The motorbike business inside Honda Vietnam is more staple-like than the car business — a motorbike in Vietnam is closer to a necessity than a deferrable luxury — so VEA's equity income has a sturdier floor than a Mercedes dealer's profit. VEA gives you auto-and-mobility exposure with a defensive, income tilt; the dealers give you the geared, cyclical upside. Knowing which one you want is half the game.

## The EV transition and VinFast's rise

No discussion of the Vietnamese auto sector in 2026 is complete without VinFast, because it is doing something no incumbent is: it is *disrupting* the demand picture from the electric side, and doing it fast.

VinFast is the automotive arm of Vingroup, Vietnam's largest private conglomerate. It pivoted hard from internal-combustion to **all-electric**, and it has pursued share in its home market with a strategy that combines aggressive pricing, a dense and rapidly expanding charging network (built by an affiliated company), and a battery-leasing model that lowers the up-front price. Each of those three levers attacks a specific barrier to EV adoption, and it is worth understanding them because together they explain the steepness of the curve. *Aggressive pricing* attacks the up-front-cost barrier head-on, pricing models to undercut comparable petrol cars and accepting losses to win share. The *charging network* attacks "range anxiety" — the fear that you will be stranded with a flat battery — by blanketing the country with chargers so that an EV becomes practical for everyday use, not just a city novelty. And the *battery-leasing model* attacks the single scariest number for an EV buyer: the battery is the most expensive component and the one whose long-term life is least understood, so letting the buyer *lease* the battery (pay a monthly fee) rather than buy it outright slashes the sticker price and removes the fear of a ruinous future replacement bill. Stack those three together against a backdrop of cheap running costs and a patriotic "buy Vietnamese" pull, and you get one of the fastest national EV-share grabs anywhere. The result has been a genuinely steep adoption curve. VinFast's EV deliveries *in Vietnam* went from about **7,000 units in 2022** to roughly **35,000 in 2023**, about **87,000 in 2024**, and approximately **175,000 in 2025** — at which point it represented roughly **29% of the entire Vietnamese passenger-vehicle market**. That is one of the fastest national EV-share grabs anywhere in the world.

![Bar chart of VinFast electric vehicle deliveries in Vietnam rising from seven thousand units in 2022 to about one hundred seventy five thousand in 2025](/imgs/blogs/automobiles-parts-sector-vietnam-consumer-durable-cycle-3.png)

The chart above is the disruptor curve, and it is worth sitting with because it changes how you read the whole sector. When a single new entrant takes ~29% share in three years, the *total* car-sales numbers we looked at earlier start to mean something different: a chunk of Vietnam's demand is shifting from the ICE brands sold through the JVs and dealers toward EVs sold largely direct by VinFast. That is both a *demand-mix* story (some of the cars being bought are now EVs) and a *channel* story (VinFast sells partly direct, bypassing the traditional dealer model). For the incumbents — the JVs behind VEA, the multi-brand dealers — VinFast's rise is a competitive threat to *share*, even as the overall fee-cut-and-rate cycle still drives *total* demand.

But here is the critical point for an investor, and it is the source of the biggest misconception about the name. **VinFast is not a value or dividend play; it is a growth bet, and growth bets are valued completely differently.** VinFast does not yet make a profit — it is burning cash to build factories, fund the charging network, and price aggressively for share. So you cannot value it on P/E (there are no E to speak of) or dividend yield (it pays none). It is valued on **EV/sales** (enterprise value divided by revenue) — a multiple of its *revenue*, on the bet that today's growth and share gains turn into tomorrow's profits at scale. That makes VFS a high-risk, high-reward, sentiment-driven stock whose price can move violently on delivery numbers, funding announcements and the broader EV mood — and one that, being US-listed, is not even on HOSE and behaves on a different clock from the Vietnamese sector names.

#### Worked example: VinFast's sales surging while profit stays negative

This is the worked example that kills the most common mistake — confusing VinFast's *sales* with *profit*. Suppose VinFast delivers about 175,000 vehicles in Vietnam in a year at an average price of, say, 700 million dong (about **\$27,600**). That is revenue of roughly 175,000 × 700 million = about **122,500 billion dong**, on the order of **\$4.8bn** of vehicle revenue. A beginner sees that enormous number and assumes the company must be hugely profitable. It is not. After the cost of building each car, the operating expenses, the cost of standing up factories and a charging network, and the aggressive pricing taken to win share, VinFast runs at a substantial *net loss* — it is spending more than it earns, on purpose, to grab the market before competitors do.

So you have a company with billions of dollars of *revenue* and *negative* profit, growing deliveries at triple-digit rates. The intuition: **sales is not profit, and for a growth-stage disruptor the two can move in opposite directions** — revenue up, losses up — because the company is investing ahead of profitability. That is why VinFast is valued on a multiple of *sales* and on the *credibility of the path to profit*, not on current earnings, and why owning it is a fundamentally different decision from owning the cash-cow VEA. One is a bond-like yield; the other is a venture-like bet.

## Valuing the auto plays: different tools for different animals

By now the central lesson should be obvious: "the auto sector" is not one thing, so there is no single right valuation tool. You match the tool to the animal.

For **VEA**, you value it as a **dividend cash cow**: the **dividend yield** is the headline (is the ~12% yield well-covered by sustainable JV equity income?), and a **P/E** sanity check on the equity income (a mature, no-growth cash cow should trade at a *low* P/E — call it high single digits — because you are paying for current cash flow, not growth). If VEA's yield is fat and the underlying Honda/Toyota/Ford profits look durable, it is cheap; if the JV profits are rolling over, the yield is a trap.

For the **dealers** (HAX, Savico), you value them as **cyclical, operating-levered retailers**. P/E is treacherous here for the same reason it is treacherous for any cyclical (which we covered in depth in the steel post): the P/E looks *lowest* at the top of the cycle, when earnings are peaking, and *highest* at the bottom, when earnings are depressed. So you think in terms of *normalised* (mid-cycle) earnings and you watch the *volume cycle* directly — you buy the dealers when volume is troughing and a demand surge is coming, not when the trailing P/E looks optically cheap on peak earnings.

For **parts and tyres** (DRC, CSM), you blend the two: a **P/E** on normalised earnings, an eye on the **input-cost cycle** (rubber, steel) that drives the margin, and the **dividend** they often pay. They sit between the dealers' high beta and VEA's defensiveness.

For **VinFast**, you use **EV/sales** and a *qualitative* judgement on the path to profitability and the funding runway, because there are no earnings or dividends to anchor on. This is the hardest and most speculative valuation in the sector, and it is the one where you must size smallest and accept that you are buying a story whose proof is years away.

We covered the general toolkit — P/E, P/B, dividend yield, EV/EBITDA, NAV — sector by sector in the dedicated valuation post; the auto-specific point is simply that **a single sector spans a 12%-yield cash cow and a money-losing growth disruptor**, so you cannot reach for one ratio and call it a day.

#### Worked example: why P/E lies for a cyclical dealer

This is the trap, in numbers, because it catches beginners every cycle. Suppose a dealer earns **20 million dong** of net profit per car at the *peak* of the cycle (high volume, fixed costs well-covered), and on its share count that works out to earnings per share of 8,000 dong. At a share price of 64,000 dong, the trailing P/E is 64,000 / 8,000 = **8x** — optically cheap. A beginner sees "8x P/E" and buys, thinking value.

Then the cycle turns. Volume falls a third, fixed costs do not, and net profit per car collapses to **5 million dong** (the dealer math from earlier). Earnings per share fall to 2,000 dong. Even if the share price falls to 50,000, the P/E is now 50,000 / 2,000 = **25x** — optically *expensive*, at the exact moment the stock is closest to its bottom and about to recover. The intuition: a cyclical's P/E is **lowest at the top and highest at the bottom**, the precise inverse of when you want to buy — so for dealers you anchor on mid-cycle earnings and the *volume* cycle, never the trailing P/E. (This is the same lesson the steel sector teaches; cyclicals share one valuation logic.)

## Common misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Auto sales track GDP, so they grow smoothly with the economy."** No — auto sales *amplify* the cycle, they do not track it smoothly. Because a car is a deferrable big-ticket purchase, demand swings far more than GDP: Vietnam's GDP grew every year from 2019 to 2025, but car sales went 410k → 510k → 370k → 340k → 380k over 2021-2025, a roughly one-third peak-to-trough collapse *inside* a growing economy. The deferrability is the whole point — durables are the volatile thing that swings around the smooth staple line, as the cyclicality chart shows.

![Line chart contrasting an autos earnings index swinging from 100 to 130 to 80 against a staples earnings index grinding steadily from 100 to 116 over 2019 to 2025](/imgs/blogs/automobiles-parts-sector-vietnam-consumer-durable-cycle-6.png)

That figure is the misconception corrected in one image. The amber autos line swings — up to 130 on the 2022 surge, down to 80 in the 2024 air-pocket — while the green staples line grinds steadily up from 100 to 116, barely noticing the cycle. Same economy, completely different earnings shapes. (The numbers are illustrative indices, normalised to 2019 = 100, to show the *shape* of cyclical-versus-defensive earnings, not a single company's results.) If you treat autos as a smooth grower, you will buy them at the 130 peak and sell at the 80 trough, which is exactly backwards.

**Misconception 2: "VEA is a carmaker, so value it on revenue and growth."** No — VEA does not make or sell cars on its own income statement; it holds *minority stakes* in the Honda, Toyota and Ford JVs and books their profit as equity income, paying most of it out as dividends. It is a **dividend proxy / cash-cow holding company**, valued on its ~12% yield and the durability of the JV profits, not on revenue growth. Mistaking the proxy for the operator leads you to the wrong valuation model and the wrong expectations (you will wait for growth that the structure is not designed to deliver).

**Misconception 3: "VinFast's huge and rising sales mean it must be hugely profitable."** No — sales is not profit. VinFast grows *revenue* at triple-digit rates while running a substantial *net loss*, because it is investing ahead of profitability — building factories, a charging network, and pricing aggressively for share. Billions of dollars of revenue can sit on top of negative earnings; the two move independently for a growth-stage disruptor. You value VinFast on EV/sales and the credibility of the path to profit, not on a profit it does not yet make.

**Misconception 4: "A low headline P/E means a dealer is cheap."** No — for a cyclical, the trailing P/E is *lowest at the cycle top* (peak earnings) and *highest at the trough* (depressed earnings), so a low P/E on a dealer often signals you are buying at the peak. You anchor on normalised mid-cycle earnings and the volume cycle, not the optical trailing multiple — exactly as the worked example showed.

**Misconception 5: "The registration-fee cut creates lasting new demand."** No — the fee cut mostly *pulls forward* demand that would have happened anyway. It produces a sharp surge while the cut is active, followed by a *payback* air-pocket once it lapses and the pulled-forward buyers are no longer in the market. So a fee-cut quarter is a *timing* signal, not a permanent uplift — you trade the surge, but you respect the payback that follows.

## How it shows up on VN-Index

Now let us walk the real history, because the auto sector's behaviour on the Vietnamese market is a clean illustration of every mechanism above. We will hit the fee-cut surges, the 2022-23 air-pocket, and VinFast's share grab.

**The 2020 fee-cut surge.** When the 50% registration-fee cut on domestically assembled cars went live in late June 2020, the demand response was immediate and visible. Despite a pandemic-bruised economy, showroom traffic and domestic-assembled sales jumped through the second half of the year — the classic *pull-forward*. For investors, the read was straightforward: the dealers and the JV-exposed names (and therefore VEA's equity income) got a demand tailwind precisely because the policy lever lowered the out-the-door cost. This was the first clear demonstration in the data of how powerful the fee lever is.

**The 2022 demand peak.** 2022 was the high-water mark — about **510,000 units** — driven by pent-up post-COVID demand colliding with still-loose financial conditions early in the year and a supportive fee environment. This was the *good* configuration: income recovering, rates still low (early 2022), demand flooding forward. Auto-sector earnings and the dealer names rode the volume up; VEA's equity income was strong on robust JV sales. If you owned the geared dealers into that surge, you were rewarded by the operating leverage working *for* you.

**The 2022-23 air-pocket.** Then the configuration flipped, hard. In late 2022 Vietnam's corporate-bond crisis hit, the SBV jacked up policy rates to defend the dong, and bank lending rates spiked. Suddenly the *monthly payment* on a car loan ballooned, household confidence cracked, and the deferrable-demand reservoir went into reverse — buyers deferred en masse. Sales fell from 510k (2022) to about **370k in 2023** and roughly **340k in 2024**. This is the consumer-durable cycle in its painful phase: high rates plus weak income plus the payback from earlier pull-forward, all at once. Dealer earnings collapsed (operating leverage working *against* them), and the sector was a place to *avoid*, not own, through the worst of it.

**The 2023H2-2024 fee-cut rescue.** To support the weak market, the government switched the 50% fee cut back on in the second half of 2023 and through 2024. It did what it always does — *cushioned* demand against the rate-and-income headwind, pulling some buyers forward and keeping volumes from falling further. But note the lesson of Misconception 5: a fee cut into a high-rate environment supports demand but cannot fully overpower a brutal financing backdrop, which is why 2024 volumes (~340k) were still well below the 2022 peak even *with* the cut active. The fee lever is powerful but it is not all-powerful; it works best when the *rate* cycle is also turning your way.

#### Worked example: the cyclical earnings hit from a one-third volume fall

Quantify the air-pocket on a fixed-cost base, because this is what made the sector un-ownable in 2023. Take the sector's volume falling from **510,000 units (2022) to 340,000 (2024)** — a fall of 170,000 units, or about **−33%**. Now apply it to a stylised dealer-or-parts business with high fixed costs. Suppose at 510k industry units the business earns, on its slice of that volume, a gross profit pool of 100 billion dong against fixed costs of 60 billion dong — so net profit is **40 billion dong** (about **\$1.6mn**).

Now volume falls 33%. The gross profit pool, which scales roughly with volume, falls to about 67 billion dong. But the fixed costs *do not fall* — they are still 60 billion. So net profit collapses to 67 − 60 = **7 billion dong**, a fall of about **83%** from a 33% drop in volume. The intuition: on a high-fixed-cost base, a one-third fall in volume can wipe out the bulk of profit, because the fixed costs eat the shrinking gross margin — which is exactly why auto-sector earnings (and the dealer stocks) fell so much harder than the headline volume did in 2023-24, and why you must respect operating leverage on the way down as much as you enjoy it on the way up.

**VinFast's share grab, in parallel.** Running *underneath* this whole cyclical story is the structural disruption: VinFast climbing from 7k to ~175k EV deliveries (2022→2025) and roughly 29% market share. This is a different axis entirely — not the cyclical up-and-down of *total* demand, but a *structural* shift in *who* captures it and *what kind* of car gets bought. For the incumbent-exposed names (VEA's JVs, the multi-brand dealers), VinFast is a slow competitive pressure on share even as they ride the same cyclical waves. For investors in VFS itself, it is a US-listed growth bet on a different clock. The two stories — the cyclical demand wave and the structural EV share-grab — are both true at once, and you have to hold both.

## The playbook: how to trade the auto sector

Everything above resolves into a concrete playbook. The auto sector is a *cyclical consumer durable* whose timing is set by policy and rates, so you trade it off the policy-and-rate cycle, not buy and hold it.

**The core setup: buy ahead of a fee-cut-plus-rate-cut demand wave.** The single best configuration for the cyclical auto names (especially the geared dealers) is when *two* levers are about to turn favourable at once: the SBV is *cutting* policy rates (so car-loan monthly payments are falling and credit is easing) **and** a 50% registration-fee cut is active or clearly coming (so the out-the-door cash cost is dropping and demand is being pulled forward). When you see rates rolling over *and* a fee-cut decree on the table, you are looking at a demand surge forming, and the operating-leveraged dealers will gear that surge into outsized earnings. That is the time to lean into the cyclical names — *ahead* of the volume surge showing up in the prints, because by the time monthly sales numbers confirm the surge, the stocks have already moved.

**Position by what you actually want.** This sector lets you dial your exposure precisely:

- For *income and defence* with auto exposure, own **VEA**: the ~12% dividend yield, anchored on Honda's staple-like motorbike profits, gives you a cash-cow with a sturdier floor than the dealers. Buy it when the yield is fat and the JV profits look durable; it is the *low-beta* way to be in the sector.
- For *geared cyclical upside* on the demand surge, own the **dealers** (HAX, Savico): thin margin on a fixed-cost base means a volume surge drops straight through. Buy them at the *trough* of the volume cycle when a fee-cut-plus-rate-cut wave is forming — never on a low trailing P/E at the peak.
- For a *mid-cyclical* blend with some yield and commodity-input exposure, own **parts and tyres** (DRC, CSM), watching the rubber and steel input cycle.
- For a *structural EV growth bet*, own **VinFast (VFS)** — but size it *small*, treat it as a venture-like position valued on EV/sales and the path to profit, and accept it runs on a different (US-listed, sentiment-driven) clock.

**Sizing.** The dealers are *high beta* to the demand cycle — size them like the cyclical bets they are, smaller than a core holding, and be willing to take them off when the cycle turns. VEA you can size larger and hold for the yield, treating it as a quasi-defensive income position. VinFast you size *smallest* of all, because the dispersion of outcomes is enormous. Never let the sector as a whole become a large undiversified bet, because it is *all* geared to the same two macro levers (rates and the fee cut) — when those turn against you, every name except VEA suffers together.

That last point deserves emphasis because it is the single most common portfolio mistake in this sector: thinking you are *diversified* across four auto names when you are really making one concentrated bet on the demand cycle. Owning HAX *and* Savico *and* DRC *and* CSM feels like four positions, but they are driven by the same underlying variable — Vietnamese new-vehicle volume — so in a downturn they fall *together*. The genuine diversifier inside the sector is VEA, precisely *because* its earnings rest on Honda's staple-like motorbike profits rather than on deferrable car-buying, so it has a lower correlation to the cyclical names and a sturdier dividend floor. A sensible sector sleeve therefore is not "four equally-weighted dealers and parts makers"; it is a *barbell* — a core VEA position held for the defensive yield, plus a smaller, tactical, cyclically-timed allocation to the geared dealers that you scale up only when the fee-cut-plus-rate-cut wave is forming and scale back to nothing when it is not, plus a tiny optional VinFast sliver as a structural EV call. The barbell gives you income through the cycle from VEA and geared upside from the dealers exactly when the timing is right, instead of one big leveraged bet on the cycle that gets cut in half when rates turn up. Think of position *count* and position *correlation* as two different things: four names that move together are one bet, not four.

**The invalidation — what proves the thesis wrong.** Be disciplined about exit. The bull case on the cyclical auto names *fails* if: (1) the SBV moves to *hiking* rates (rising car-loan payments crush the marginal buyer, as in 2022-23); (2) the fee cut *lapses with no renewal* into a weak market, triggering the payback air-pocket as pulled-forward demand evaporates; (3) the monthly sales prints *roll over* after a surge, signalling the pull-forward is spent; or (4) household income and confidence deteriorate (rising unemployment, falling real wages) so that deferral sets in regardless of policy. Any of those flips the operating leverage from tailwind to headwind, and the geared dealers will fall faster than the market — so when you see rates turning up *and* the fee cut expiring, you reduce the cyclical names and rotate toward VEA's defensive yield or out of the sector entirely. For VEA specifically, the invalidation is different: it is a *cut in the dividend*, which would signal the underlying JV profits (especially Honda's motorbikes) are rolling over — that is the moment the cash-cow case breaks.

**The one-line version.** Buy the geared auto names (dealers first) ahead of a fee-cut-plus-rate-cut demand wave, hold VEA for its ~12% defensive yield through the cycle, treat VinFast as a small structural EV bet on a different clock, and get out of the cyclicals the moment rates turn up or the fee cut lapses — because a car is a deferrable purchase, and deferral is exactly what a high-rate, no-fee-cut quarter produces.
