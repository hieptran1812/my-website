> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Foreigners are only about 10-15% of VN-Index turnover, but because their money concentrates in a narrow set of bank, large-cap and "Diamond" names, their flows move those sectors and set the market's risk-on/risk-off tone far beyond their share of volume.
>
> - **Foreign room** (*room ngoai*) is a legal cap on how much of a company foreigners may own — 30% for banks, 49% (or a company-set limit) elsewhere. When room is full, new foreign buyers must pay a premium or wait, so the cap itself shapes price.
> - **ETFs buy and sell baskets mechanically.** When money flows into VanEck VNM, Fubon FTSE Vietnam or the VNDiamond ETF, an authorized participant has to buy the whole basket of constituents — that is the "index effect," and it hits the same banks and large caps every time.
> - **Foreign flow is tone, not gospel.** In 2024 foreigners net-sold a record ~92.5 trillion dong (~\$3.6bn) on HOSE and the VN-Index *still rose ~12%* because domestic retail bought even harder. Read the flow as sentiment, size it against domestic money, and never assume net selling must drag the index down.
> - **The one number to remember:** an emerging-market upgrade by FTSE could pull an estimated **\$1.5-2bn** of passive money into Vietnam — most of it landing on bank and large-cap weights.

On the last trading day of 2024, a Vietnamese retail investor scrolling the foreign-flow ticker would have seen a frankly alarming statistic. Across the whole year, foreigners had net-sold roughly **92.5 trillion dong** of stock on the Ho Chi Minh exchange (HOSE) — about **\$3.6bn** at the mid-2026 rate of around \$1 = 25,400 VND. It was the heaviest foreign net-selling year on record, dwarfing even the 58 trillion dong they dumped in 2021. If you believe the old market folklore that "foreigners are the smart money and you sell when they sell," you would have spent 2024 in cash, terrified.

You would also have missed a roughly 12% rise in the VN-Index. The benchmark closed 2021 at 1,498, crashed to 1,007 by the end of 2022, recovered to 1,130 in 2023, and finished 2024 around 1,266 — climbing through the single largest foreign exodus the market has ever printed. The lesson is not that foreign flows do not matter. They matter enormously, in specific places, at specific times. The lesson is that they are a *minority* force whose influence is concentrated, not universal — and learning *where* that concentration sits is one of the highest-value things a sector-minded investor in Vietnam can do.

This post is the sixth and final foundation in *The Vietnam Sector Playbook*. We will build, from zero, the full machinery of foreign participation: what foreign room is and why banks are capped at 30%, how ETFs mechanically buy and sell whole sectors, what the "index effect" really means, why the looming FTSE/MSCI emerging-market upgrade is a multi-billion-dollar catalyst, and — most importantly — how to read foreign flow as a sector signal without letting it run your portfolio. By the end you should be able to look at a single day's foreign net-buy print and know which sectors it actually touched, and whether to care.

![Flow map of foreign capital concentrating into a few VN-Index sectors](/imgs/blogs/foreign-flows-etfs-and-the-index-effect-vietnam-1.png)

Before we open the machinery, look at the raw record of foreign behavior, year by year, because the pattern is the whole argument in one picture. Foreigners net-bought a healthy 44 trillion dong in 2018 and a thin 7 trillion in 2019; they flipped to modest net selling in 2020 (-16 trillion) as the pandemic pulled global money home; they net-sold a then-record 58 trillion in 2021 into the biggest bull market the index had ever had; they swung back to net *buying* (+27 trillion) into the 2022 crash; and then they sold persistently in 2023 (-23 trillion) and at a record pace in 2024 (-92.5 trillion). If you try to draw a line from "foreigners bought" to "market went up," you cannot — the relationship keeps inverting. That single failure of correlation is the most important thing this post will teach you, and everything else is the explanation for *why* a force this visible can be this often disconnected from the index.

![Foreign net trading on HOSE by year bar chart 2018 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/foreign-flows-etfs-and-the-index-effect-vietnam-2.png)

What the bar chart cannot show — and what the rest of this post supplies — is that the *same dong* of foreign flow does very different things depending on *where* it lands. A trillion dong of foreign buying smeared across 300 illiquid small caps barely registers. The same trillion concentrated into two banks and a Diamond name at full room can move a whole sector and reset the market's mood for a week. The aggregate number on the ticker hides this. To read foreign flow well, you have to disaggregate it — and to disaggregate it, you first have to understand the rules that herd foreign money into the same few pens every single day. That herding rule is foreign room, so that is where we start.

## Foundations: how foreign ownership works in Vietnam

Start with the single most important fact, because almost everything else flows from it: **Vietnam legally limits how much of a listed company foreigners may own.** This limit is called the *foreign ownership limit*, or in the language every Vietnamese investor uses, *room ngoai* — literally "foreign room." It is the amount of headroom foreigners have to keep buying before they hit the legal ceiling.

Think of a listed company as a theatre with a fixed number of seats. Domestic Vietnamese investors can buy any seat freely. Foreign investors, though, are told: "You may collectively occupy at most this many seats, and not one more." Once the foreign section is full, a foreigner who wants in has to buy a seat from another foreigner who is leaving — they cannot simply buy a fresh seat from a domestic holder at the screen price. That collective ceiling is the foreign room, and the number of empty foreign seats remaining is the *available room*.

### Why the caps exist, and why they differ by sector

The caps are a deliberate policy choice. Vietnam wants foreign capital — it brings dollars, liquidity, and credibility — but it does not want strategic national industries handed to foreign control. So the limit is set by law and by sector sensitivity:

- **Banks are capped at 30%.** Banking is a strategic, systemically important industry, and the State Bank of Vietnam guards control of it tightly. This 30% cap is the single most consequential foreign-room rule in the market, because banks are by far the heaviest sector in the VN-Index — and we will come back to why that matters again and again.
- **Most other companies cap at 49%** under the general foreign-ownership rule, though many sectors and individual firms set their own *lower* limits in their charters, and some "conditional" business lines are capped tighter still.
- **A company can also voluntarily *raise* its room** toward the legal maximum, or in some cases unlock a higher limit — a corporate action that itself becomes a flow event, because it suddenly creates fresh foreign seats to be filled.

The key intuition for a sector investor: **the foreign-room cap is not the same across sectors, and that asymmetry is structural.** Banks, the index's biggest sector, have the *tightest* foreign cap. That means foreign demand for the index's most important sector is permanently rationed — and when that demand is strong, the rationing shows up as a price premium rather than as ever-larger foreign ownership.

#### Worked example: the room math on a single bank

Take a hypothetical mid-sized Vietnamese bank with **1,000 million shares** outstanding. Its foreign-room cap is the banking-sector limit, **30%**. The maximum number of shares foreigners may collectively hold is:

```
max foreign shares = 1,000 million × 30% = 300 million shares
```

Suppose the share price is **24,000 dong**. The total value of the foreign-room "bucket" is:

```
300 million × 24,000 dong = 7,200,000 million dong
                          = 7.2 trillion dong
```

At \$1 = 25,400 VND, that bucket is worth about **\$283mn** — and not a dong more, no matter how much foreign demand exists. Now suppose foreigners already hold 298 million of those 300 million shares. The *available room* is just 2 million shares. A foreign fund that wants to put **\$50mn** to work in this bank — roughly 53 million shares — physically cannot. It can buy 2 million shares at the screen and then must either pay a premium to a departing foreign holder or simply give up and buy something else. **The intuition: when a quality bank's room is full, foreign demand stops translating into foreign ownership and starts translating into price pressure and frustration.**

This is what traders mean when they talk about the "room wall." Picture two stocks side by side. The first is a top-tier bank, capped at 30% by law, and that 30% is fully taken. A foreigner staring at the order book sees a price, sees liquidity, and sees a "buy" button that is, for them, effectively disabled — every offer they could lift is reserved for domestic buyers. To get in, they must find another foreigner willing to sell, often on a negotiated **premium board** at a price several percent above the screen. The second stock is a Diamond name that has *also* run its room to the limit, but for a different reason: structural index demand keeps it pinned full. Here too there is no headroom, so any incremental foreign demand cannot be satisfied by new ownership — it can only be expressed as a higher price. The room wall converts demand into re-rating.

![Room wall before and after capped bank versus full room Diamond stock](/imgs/blogs/foreign-flows-etfs-and-the-index-effect-vietnam-5.png)

The practical, sector-level consequence is subtle but powerful. In a normal stock, strong demand shows up as *more shares bought* — volume and ownership rise together. In a full-room name, strong foreign demand shows up as *price*, because the quantity is fixed. So two banks with identical fundamentals can trade at meaningfully different valuations purely because one has open room and the other is walled off and coveted — the walled one carries a "scarcity premium." When you compare valuations across the banking sector, you must adjust for room status, or you will mistake a scarcity premium for a quality premium and buy the wrong name.

### The "Diamond" stocks: full-room quality names

This room-scarcity problem created a clever workaround that every Vietnam investor should understand: the **VNDiamond index**. HOSE built the VN Diamond index specifically to bundle together the large, high-quality stocks whose foreign room is *full or nearly full* — the names foreigners most want but cannot freely buy. The "Diamond" label signals exactly that: these are the gems that are sold out to foreigners.

Why does a sold-out-to-foreigners index matter? Because it gave the asset-management industry something to package. The **DCVFM VNDiamond ETF** tracks this basket, and crucially, *domestic* investors and *new foreign* money can buy the ETF even when the underlying stocks' direct foreign room is shut. The ETF becomes a side door into full-room names. The result is a self-reinforcing loop: Diamond names attract structural demand, that demand keeps their room pinned full, the full room keeps them in the Diamond index, and the Diamond ETF keeps channeling money toward them.

### NVDR-style workarounds and how foreigners actually trade

When direct room is closed, foreigners reach for alternatives. The cleanest is simply to **buy the ETF** instead of the stock — the side door we just described. Others include:

- **Buying from a departing foreigner on the "premium board"** — a negotiated, often higher-priced transaction between two foreign accounts that does not touch domestic supply. The premium can run several percent above the screen price for a coveted, fully-capped name.
- **Waiting for room to open** when a domestic event (a dividend reinvestment, a placement, a foreigner exiting) frees a seat — foreign desks literally queue for these openings.
- **Depositary-receipt and participatory structures.** Vietnam does not yet run a deep Non-Voting Depositary Receipt (NVDR) regime like Thailand's — where a special instrument lets foreigners get the economics of a capped stock without the voting rights — but the *concept* of "get the economic exposure without consuming voting room" is exactly what the Diamond ETF and various structured notes approximate.

Mechanically, foreigners trade through the same HOSE order book as everyone else; their orders are simply tagged by nationality so the exchange can enforce room and publish the daily foreign net-buy/net-sell figure. That daily figure — *khoi ngoai mua rong / ban rong* — is the data print this entire post teaches you to read. It is reported every session, by ticker and in aggregate, and it is one of the most-watched numbers in the Vietnamese market.

### Reading the published room and flow data

The exchange and every broker app publish two related numbers you need to keep straight. The first is **foreign room remaining** — the available foreign seats, shown per stock as a percentage and a share count. When it is near zero, the name is "full room" (*het room*) and you know fresh foreign demand can only express itself as price. When a company raises its limit or a foreigner exits, the room number ticks up, and that opening is itself a tradeable event because waiting foreign buyers rush the new seats. The second number is the **daily foreign net value** — buy value minus sell value — published in aggregate and per ticker. A disciplined reader never looks at the aggregate alone; they pull up the per-ticker breakdown and ask: *is this flow concentrated or spread?* A 500-billion-dong net buy that is 80% one bank is a sector signal about banks; the same 500 billion spread across forty names is barely a signal at all.

There is a subtlety that trips up beginners. The published foreign-flow figure mixes two very different kinds of money: **discretionary active funds** making real decisions, and **mechanical ETF and index flows** that are pure plumbing. On a day when the Fubon ETF sees a wave of redemptions, the foreign net-sell print balloons — but no human decided Vietnamese banks were a bad investment; an authorized participant simply had to liquidate a basket because Taiwanese retail investors sold the ETF for reasons that may have nothing to do with Vietnam at all. Learning to mentally separate "someone chose to sell Vietnam" from "a basket got unwound because of flows in a fund half a world away" is most of the skill in reading the print.

### The active-fund channel: the other half of foreign money

ETFs get the headlines, but a large share of foreign capital in Vietnam is *active*, run by discretionary managers who pick stocks and sectors. The biggest names are part of the market's furniture: **Dragon Capital** (manager of the long-running Vietnam Enterprise Investments fund and a suite of others), **VinaCapital**, and a roster of regional and global emerging-frontier funds. These managers do not mechanically track an index; they overweight the banks, large caps, and Diamond names they like and underweight the rest. But here is the key point for a sector investor: **they concentrate in almost exactly the same place the ETFs do.** Their mandates demand liquidity and governance, which funnels them into the same large-cap, full-room quality complex. So whether foreign money arrives through the passive door or the active door, it lands on the same sectors — which is precisely why foreign concentration is so reliable, and why a sector that foreigners structurally ignore stays ignored through both channels.

The difference that matters is *behavior under stress*. Passive ETF money is price-insensitive and flow-driven: it buys when the fund gets inflows, sells when it gets outflows, regardless of valuation. Active foreign money is the opposite — it is the cohort that bought the 2022 bottom while everyone else panicked, because a discretionary value manager *can* lean against the tape. When you read a foreign net-buy print during a crash, the encouraging interpretation is that active managers are stepping in; when you read a net-sell print driven by a strengthening dollar, the likely culprit is passive outflows and repatriation. Same headline number, opposite meaning — and the only way to tell them apart is to know which funds are moving.

## ETFs and the index effect

We have mentioned ETFs three times now without defining them properly. Let us fix that, because the *mechanics* of how an ETF buys and sells are the heart of the "index effect."

An **ETF** (exchange-traded fund) is a fund whose shares trade on the exchange like a stock, but whose job is to hold a basket of underlying stocks that tracks an index. If you buy one share of the VNDiamond ETF, you are indirectly buying a slice of every Diamond constituent in its index weight. The magic — and the part that creates mechanical sector flows — is how new ETF shares get *created* and *destroyed*.

### Creation and redemption: the mechanism that forces basket buying

ETFs do not print new shares from thin air when demand rises. Instead they use a wholesale mechanism involving a special intermediary called an **authorized participant** (AP) — typically a large broker. Here is the loop:

1. **Demand for the ETF rises.** Investors buy ETF shares on the exchange faster than they sell. The ETF's market price starts trading slightly *above* its net asset value (NAV) — the true value of the basket it holds.
2. **The AP arbitrages the gap.** The AP goes into the market and **buys the entire underlying basket** of stocks — every bank, large cap, and Diamond name in the index, in exactly the right weights.
3. **Creation.** The AP delivers that basket to the fund and receives, in exchange, a block of newly created ETF shares. It sells those ETF shares on the exchange, pocketing the small premium, which pushes the ETF price back toward NAV.

The result: **money flowing into the ETF mechanically forces buying of every constituent.** Nobody decided "let's buy banks today" — the buying happened automatically because investors wanted ETF exposure and the AP had to assemble the basket to satisfy them.

**Redemption** is the exact reverse. When investors sell the ETF faster than they buy, its price drifts *below* NAV; the AP buys cheap ETF shares, hands them back to the fund, receives the underlying basket, and **sells that basket into the market** — mechanically pressuring every constituent down. Critically, when a *foreign-domiciled* ETF like VanEck or Fubon sees outflows, those basket sales show up in the daily foreign net-sell print, even though no foreign investor made a discretionary "I dislike Vietnamese banks" decision. A chunk of what looks like foreign conviction is really plumbing.

### The main Vietnam ETFs

A handful of funds carry most of the passive firepower aimed at Vietnam. Knowing them by name lets you anticipate which sectors a flow will hit:

- **VanEck Vietnam ETF (VNM)** — the original US-listed Vietnam ETF. It actually tracks a broader Vietnam-exposure index (including some offshore-listed names), so its basket is not a pure VN-Index clone, but its flows are watched as a foreign-sentiment gauge. AUM roughly **\$450mn**.
- **Fubon FTSE Vietnam ETF** — a Taiwan-listed fund that became one of the largest single foreign vehicles into Vietnam. It tracks the FTSE Vietnam index, heavy on large-cap and financials. AUM in the region of **\$700mn**, and its creation/redemption swings have at times been the single biggest mover of daily foreign flow.
- **DCVFM VNDiamond ETF** — the domestic Diamond-basket fund described above; the side door into full-room quality names. AUM around **\$700mn**.
- **VN30 ETFs** — several funds (DCVFM VN30, SSIAM VN30, MAFM VN30, and others) tracking the VN30 index of the 30 largest, most liquid stocks. Combined AUM on the order of **\$300mn**.
- **SSIAM VNFIN Lead ETF** — tracks a financials-tilted basket; smaller, around **\$150mn**, but a pure-play on the bank/broker/insurer complex.

![Approximate assets under management of major Vietnam ETFs](/imgs/blogs/foreign-flows-etfs-and-the-index-effect-vietnam-4.png)

The total is on the order of a couple of billion dollars of explicitly index-tracking money — small against the market's total capitalization, but large *relative to the daily liquidity of any single constituent*, which is exactly why a rebalance can shove a sector around.

### The index effect: how a rebalance forces mechanical sector trading

The **index effect** is the price impact that occurs when an index changes its constituents or weights and every fund tracking it must trade to match. Indices like VN30, VNDiamond, and FTSE Vietnam rebalance on a schedule — quarterly or semi-annually. When the rebalance is announced, the new basket is known in advance, and on the effective day every tracking ETF must buy the additions and sell the deletions, in size, often near the close.

Two things make this powerful in Vietnam:

1. **The funds are large relative to single-stock liquidity.** A \$700mn ETF re-weighting a position by a few percent can demand more stock in a day than the name normally trades.
2. **The trades are concentrated and predictable.** Because the new weights are public, *active* traders front-run the rebalance — buying additions before the funds must, selling them the effective day — which amplifies the move around the event and then often reverses it.

#### Worked example: the rebalance demand on a single addition

Take a Vietnam ETF with **\$700mn** of AUM that, at a rebalance, adds a new stock at a **3%** target weight. The dollar amount it must buy is:

```
required purchase = $700mn × 3% = $21mn
```

In dong, at \$1 = 25,400 VND:

```
$21mn × 25,400 = 533,400 million dong ≈ 533 billion dong
```

Now compare that to the stock's liquidity. Suppose the name trades an average of **80 billion dong** per day. The ETF alone needs to buy the equivalent of:

```
533 billion ÷ 80 billion per day ≈ 6.7 days of normal volume
```

— and it typically wants most of it executed around one rebalance date. If two or three other funds track the same index, the combined demand is a multiple of that. No wonder the stock gaps up into the event. **The intuition: an index addition is a forced buyer with a deadline, and a forced buyer with a deadline pays up — which is the entire mechanism behind the index effect.**

### Front-running and the reversal

Because the new basket is published *before* the effective date, the index effect has a predictable two-act structure. In **act one**, in the days between the announcement and the rebalance, smart active traders buy the additions and short or sell the deletions, knowing the ETFs *must* trade them later. This front-running pushes the addition up before the funds even arrive. In **act two**, on the effective day, the ETFs execute their forced trades — often dumping a large order into the closing auction — and the front-runners sell their pre-bought stock *to* the funds, taking profit. The classic pattern is a run-up into the rebalance, a spike on the day, and a partial **reversal** in the days after, as the forced buying ends and the front-runners are gone. A sector investor who does not know this calendar can be whipsawed: buying a large cap "because it's strong" right before a rebalance reversal, or selling a deletion into the very dip the index effect created.

The honest takeaway is that a meaningful share of short-term, large-cap price action around these dates is *mechanical* — it reflects index plumbing, not a change in the business. The fundamentals of a bank do not improve because the VNDiamond index added 30 basis points to its weight. So when you see a large-cap name move sharply and the date is near a known rebalance, your first question should be "is this the index effect?" before you reach for a fundamental story. Treat rebalance-driven moves as temporary distortions to fade, not trends to chase — and treat the *deletion* dip as a potential opportunity if the business is sound and only the index membership changed.

#### Worked example: the premium a full-room name commands

Suppose a coveted, fully-capped bank trades at **40,000 dong** on the screen, but its foreign room has been shut for months. A foreign fund that *must* own it — say, to match a benchmark after an upgrade — cannot buy on the screen, so it negotiates a block from a departing foreign holder on the premium board. If the scarcity premium is **5%**, the fund pays:

```
premium price = 40,000 × 1.05 = 42,000 dong
```

On a **\$10mn** order, that 5% premium is an extra:

```
$10mn × 5% = $0.5mn = $500,000  (~12.7 billion dong)
```

— half a million dollars of pure scarcity cost, paid simply because the room wall blocked the screen. Multiply that across every full-room name a large mandate must hold, and the scarcity premium becomes a structural, sector-wide feature of Vietnamese banks and Diamond stocks. **The lesson: foreign room scarcity is not a technicality — it is a real, payable premium that raises the effective price of the market's most-wanted sectors.**

## Where foreign money concentrates

Foreigners are a minority of turnover — roughly 10-15% of HOSE value on a typical day, sometimes more on a heavy-flow session, often less in a retail frenzy. If that money were spread evenly across 400 listed names it would be almost invisible. It is not spread evenly. It piles into a short list of sectors, and *that* is what gives it outsized influence.

### Banks: heavy index weight, tight room, foreign favorite

Banks are the gravitational center. They are the **largest sector in the VN-Index** by a wide margin — often around a third of the index's weight when you add the big listed lenders together. They are also the sector foreigners most want to own as a leveraged bet on Vietnam's growth: a bank's earnings rise with credit growth, which rises with GDP. So you have maximum foreign appetite meeting the *tightest* foreign cap (30%). The collision produces three reliable effects:

- **The biggest banks run with full or near-full foreign room**, so fresh foreign demand bids the price up rather than expanding ownership.
- **Bank flow dominates the aggregate foreign print.** When the headline says "foreigners net-bought 500 billion dong today," a large slice of that is usually one or two banks.
- **Banks set the index's tone**, so foreign buying of banks reads as risk-on for the whole market, and foreign dumping of banks reads as risk-off — a signal far broader than the banks themselves.

### Large-cap quality and the Diamond basket

Beyond banks, foreign money clusters in **large-cap, liquid, governance-clean names** — the consumer staples leader, the industrial conglomerate, the top retailer, the tech champion. These are the stocks an offshore fund can actually build a position in without moving the price for weeks, and the ones that screen well on the quality and liquidity filters foreign mandates impose. Many of them are precisely the **Diamond** names whose room is full.

### Why their flows move those sectors most: the free-float problem

There is a deeper mechanical reason foreign flow hits these specific sectors hardest, and it is worth understanding because it explains the *magnitude* of the impact, not just the location. Most Vietnamese large caps have a large *strategic* shareholder — a founding family, a state holding company, a partner bank — who sits on a big block and never trades. The shares that actually change hands day to day are the **free float**, which can be far smaller than the total share count. Foreign and ETF buying chases that thin free float, not the whole company.

So when a price-insensitive ETF needs 533 billion dong of a name whose free float trades only 80 billion a day, the demand is not measured against the company's full market cap — it is measured against the sliver of stock that is genuinely available. That is why a few hundred billion dong of mechanical buying can shove a multi-trillion-dong company several percent in a session: the buyer is competing for a small, fixed pool of tradeable shares. The same dynamic in reverse explains why ETF redemptions can dump a large cap so violently — there simply are not enough natural buyers of the free float to absorb a forced basket sale at the current price. **Foreign concentration plus thin free float is the combination that turns a minority of turnover into a majority of the price impact in the index's biggest sectors.**

### Where foreign flow barely registers

The mirror image matters just as much for a sector investor. Walk down the list of the 19 ICB sectors and you find whole corners where foreign ownership is negligible: small-cap chemicals, most of media, much of construction and household goods, the thinly-traded utilities. In these sectors the daily foreign print is near zero, room is wide open and untouched, and a big foreign-flow day on HOSE leaves them completely unmoved because the flow never reaches them. This is a *feature* for the analyst: it tells you exactly which sector calls you can make on foreign-flow grounds (the bank/large-cap/Diamond complex) and which you must make on purely domestic grounds (the small-cap and unloved sectors). Trying to read foreign flow into a sector foreigners ignore is reading tea leaves — there is no signal there because there is no flow.

### The practical consequence

Pulling it together: **the sectors foreigners can move are the sectors where they are concentrated** — banks, the large-cap quality complex, and the Diamond basket — and the *reason* they can move them is the collision of concentrated, price-insensitive demand with a thin free float and, for banks, a hard legal cap. A small-cap chemicals or media name with negligible foreign ownership will barely twitch on a big foreign-flow day. If you want to use foreign flow as a sector signal, you watch the sectors foreigners actually trade — and you ignore it for the corners they do not.

## The FTSE/MSCI upgrade catalyst

Here is the single largest *potential* flow event hanging over the Vietnamese market, and the reason foreign-flow literacy is worth real money over the next few years.

### Frontier vs emerging: the classification ladder

Global index providers — chiefly **FTSE Russell** and **MSCI** — sort the world's stock markets into tiers: **developed**, **emerging**, and **frontier**, with sub-tiers in between. The tier matters enormously because the giant pools of passive money are benchmarked to *emerging-market* indices, not frontier ones. Vietnam today sits in the **frontier** tier. Only a thin slice of frontier-tracking money flows its way; the vast emerging-market pools largely ignore it because, by definition, Vietnam is not in their benchmark.

An **upgrade from frontier to (secondary) emerging** changes that. The moment Vietnam joins an emerging-market index, every fund that tracks that index must buy Vietnamese stocks to match the benchmark — at the new index weight, mechanically, whether the fund manager personally likes Vietnam or not. It is the index effect, but at the scale of an entire country.

### What it takes, and where the flow lands

Vietnam has been on **FTSE Russell's watchlist for a possible upgrade since 2018**, and the wait has dragged because reclassification has hard criteria around market access and settlement:

- **Settlement and pre-funding.** Vietnam historically required investors to pre-fund trades (cash in the account before buying), which global custodians dislike; reforms toward a smoother, T+2-style, no-pre-funding settlement are central to clearing the bar.
- **Market access and foreign-room transparency.** The room system itself, and clear real-time disclosure of available room, are part of what reviewers scrutinize.
- **Trading and clearing infrastructure**, including the long-awaited central counterparty (CCP) clearing model.

When the upgrade comes — and the consensus expectation is FTSE first, with an MSCI emerging-market review following later on a longer timeline — the passive inflow is estimated in the billions. A commonly cited figure is **\$1.5-2bn** of FTSE-tracking passive money on the initial reclassification, with potentially much more over time if MSCI follows and active emerging-market managers add Vietnam to their universe.

### Why FTSE comes first and MSCI is harder

FTSE Russell and MSCI run *separate* classification processes with *different* bars, and that gap is the entire reason traders talk about "FTSE first." FTSE's frontier-to-secondary-emerging criteria are more mechanical and access-focused — settlement, custody, the pre-funding question — which Vietnam's reforms (the move toward no-pre-funding trading and a central counterparty) are directly aimed at. MSCI's review weighs those same plumbing issues but leans harder on *qualitative* market-accessibility judgments: how open the foreign-room regime really is, how easy it is to register and repatriate as a foreign investor, how reliable the information flow is in English. Those are slower to fix because they are partly cultural and regulatory, not just technical. So the realistic sequence is: FTSE reclassification triggers the first, smaller, mechanical passive wave; MSCI follows on a longer, less certain timeline and, because the MSCI emerging-market pool is far larger, would eventually dwarf the FTSE flow if it lands. A sector investor positions for the *FTSE* event with conviction and treats the *MSCI* event as a larger, later optionality.

The reason both events land so hard on the same sectors is the one we have built throughout this post: index weights in any Vietnam basket are dominated by banks and large-cap quality, exactly the names with the tightest room and thinnest float. So the upgrade is not a broad lift for all 19 sectors — it is a concentrated re-rating of the index's heavyweight financials and blue chips, funded by price-insensitive passive buyers hitting a capped, scarce supply. Knowing this lets you front-position years ahead: the question "what is my bank and large-cap weight relative to the upgrade basket?" is answerable today, long before the reclassification date is set.

### A note on what an upgrade does *not* do

It is worth being honest about the limits, because upgrade hype gets overdone. An emerging-market upgrade does not change a single company's earnings; it changes *who is forced to own the shares*. The mechanical flow is real and re-rates valuations, but it is a one-time (or staged) repricing, not a permanent tailwind to growth. Once the passive money is in and the new weight is established, the ongoing flow reverts to normal — and the names that ran up on upgrade anticipation can give back ground if the fundamentals do not grow into the higher multiple. The upgrade is a flow catalyst to *trade*, not a substitute for the domestic earnings story that ultimately drives each sector. Treat it as a powerful, datable event with a beginning and an end, not as a reason to own Vietnamese banks forever.

![Timeline of the FTSE and MSCI emerging-market upgrade path](/imgs/blogs/foreign-flows-etfs-and-the-index-effect-vietnam-7.png)

#### Worked example: mapping an upgrade inflow onto sectors

Suppose an upgrade attracts **\$1.5bn** of passive money, and the new Vietnam basket in the emerging-market index is weighted roughly the way the domestic large-cap indices are: call it about **35% banks**, **30% other large-cap quality** (consumer, industrials, tech, retail), and the rest spread across smaller weights. The mechanical buying lands as:

```
banks:            $1.5bn × 35% = $525mn
large-cap quality: $1.5bn × 30% = $450mn
everything else:   $1.5bn × 35% = $525mn
```

Now consider that **\$525mn** of forced bank buying, in dong at \$1 = 25,400 VND, is about **13.3 trillion dong** — concentrated into a sector where the biggest names already run near full foreign room. The buyers are mechanical and price-insensitive; the supply is capped by law. **The intuition: an emerging-market upgrade points a price-insensitive multi-billion-dollar firehose at a small set of room-constrained sectors, which is exactly the recipe for a sustained re-rating of banks and large-cap quality.** This is why the upgrade is the single biggest structural flow story in the market — and why "what's my bank exposure into the upgrade?" is a question worth asking years ahead of the event.

## Reading flows honestly

Now the discipline. Everything above could tempt you to over-weight foreign flow — to treat the daily net-buy print as the market's verdict. That is the most common and most expensive mistake retail investors make with this data. Foreign flow is **tone, not gospel.** Here is how to keep it in proportion.

### Foreign flow as tone, not a trade trigger

Use the foreign print the way a sailor uses the wind direction: it tells you the prevailing mood — risk-on if foreigners are buying banks and large caps, risk-off if they are dumping them — but it does not by itself tell you to buy or sell. A single day of net selling in a strong uptrend is noise. A *multi-month, broad-based* shift in foreign positioning is a regime signal worth respecting. The skill is distinguishing the two, and never letting one red print override a trend that domestic money is driving.

### The dollar driver behind the 2024 selling

Much of the persistent foreign selling in 2023-2024 was not a verdict on Vietnamese companies at all — it was a **currency and global-rates story**. When the US dollar strengthens and US interest rates are high, global capital flows *out* of emerging and frontier markets and back into dollar assets, because a stronger dollar erodes the home-currency value of foreign holdings and high US yields offer a safe alternative. The Vietnamese dong came under depreciation pressure against the dollar through this period, and a foreign investor watching their dong-denominated gains shrink in dollar terms has every reason to sell *regardless* of how the underlying business is doing. This is the single most important thing to internalize about reading the foreign print: **a lot of foreign selling is a macro/FX trade about the dollar, not a micro judgment about the stock.** When you see heavy, broad foreign net selling, check the dollar and US yields before you conclude anything about Vietnamese fundamentals — the cause is often offshore. The corollary is bullish: when the dollar finally turns and US rates fall, the same mechanism reverses, and the foreign flow that left can come back as fast as it went — landing, as always, on banks and large-cap quality first.

### Domestic retail can overwhelm it

This is the crux, and the data settles it. Vietnam's market is **dominated by domestic retail investors** — individuals trading from phones, who routinely supply 80-90% of daily turnover. When this army of domestic buyers is in a buying mood, it can absorb essentially unlimited foreign selling and still push the index up. That is precisely what happened in 2024.

#### Worked example: who bought what foreigners sold in 2024

In 2024, foreigners net-sold roughly **92.5 trillion dong** (~\$3.6bn) on HOSE, and yet the VN-Index *rose* from about 1,130 to 1,266 — a gain of roughly **12%**. For the index to rise while a seller is unloading 92.5 trillion dong of stock, some other group had to *buy at least* 92.5 trillion dong *more* than they sold — and then keep buying hard enough to lift prices. That group was domestic investors, overwhelmingly retail.

Put a number on the offset: every share a foreigner sold had to be bought by a domestic investor. So domestic investors net-*bought* the entire **92.5 trillion dong** (~\$3.6bn) the foreigners net-sold, and they did it while bidding the index up 12%. In rough terms, domestic money did not merely absorb a \$3.6bn foreign exodus — it absorbed it *and* had enough conviction left over to re-rate the market higher.

```
foreign net sell (2024):    -92.5 trillion dong  (~ -$3.6bn)
domestic net buy (offset):  +92.5 trillion dong  (~ +$3.6bn) at minimum
index result:               +12% (1,130 -> 1,266)
```

**The intuition: in a retail-dominated market, foreign flow is one current in a much larger sea; when domestic conviction runs the other way, it wins.** Anyone who shorted Vietnam in 2024 "because foreigners are selling" was reading a minority vote as the final tally.

![VN-Index level versus foreign net flow 2021 to 2024 showing divergence](/imgs/blogs/foreign-flows-etfs-and-the-index-effect-vietnam-6.png)

### The 2022-2024 divergence

Zoom out and the divergence is stark. In 2021 foreigners net-*sold* 58 trillion dong into a roaring bull market that ended the year at 1,498 — selling into strength, and wrong about the top only in the sense that the crash came from domestic deleveraging, not foreign flow. In 2022 foreigners turned net *buyers* (+27 trillion dong) right as the bond crisis smashed the index down to 1,007 — buying the bottom while domestic margin calls forced everyone else out. Then 2023 and 2024 saw persistent, eventually record, foreign selling into a recovering and then rising market. Across this whole stretch, **the correlation between annual foreign flow and the index's direction was weak-to-negative.** Foreign flow explained sentiment and specific sector moves; it did not explain the index.

## Common misconceptions

**"Foreigners are the smart money — just copy their trades."** They are *informed*, *patient*, *long-horizon* money with strong research, and their concentrated buying genuinely moves banks and large caps. But "smart" does not mean "right about the index's next move." Foreigners net-bought the 2022 bottom (good call) and net-sold the 2021 top (also reasonable) — but they also net-sold a record amount in 2024 straight through a 12% rally. Copy the *sectors* they concentrate in if you like; do not copy the *timing* as if it were prophecy.

**"Net selling means the market must fall."** It does not, and 2024 is the clean counterexample: ~92.5 trillion dong (~\$3.6bn) of net selling, index up ~12%. Foreign flow is roughly 10-15% of turnover. The other 85-90% — domestic retail — sets the direction far more often than the foreign minority does. Net selling is a headwind for the *specific sectors* foreigners are leaving, and a tone signal; it is not a sell signal for the index.

**"ETFs are passive, so they don't move price."** Exactly backwards. *Because* they are passive, ETF flows are mechanical and price-insensitive — the authorized participant *must* buy or sell the whole basket regardless of price, and *must* trade the index changes on the rebalance date regardless of how stretched the stock is. A discretionary fund can wait for a better price; a passive ETF cannot. That forced, deadline-driven, price-insensitive buying is *more* impactful per dollar than discretionary flow, not less — it is the entire engine of the index effect.

**"Foreign room is a fixed, permanent number."** Room utilization changes every session as foreigners buy and sell, and the *cap* itself can change: a company can raise its limit, a sector rule can shift, or a stock can move in or out of the Diamond/VN30/FTSE baskets. A name going from "open room" to "full room" — or a company unlocking new room — is itself a flow event you can anticipate.

**"The FTSE/MSCI upgrade is priced in already."** Partly, in the most obvious names, by the most sophisticated funds. But the *mechanical* passive inflow only happens on the actual reclassification date, and the *active* emerging-market money typically arrives later, as managers who were never benchmarked to frontier add Vietnam to their universe. A multi-year, multi-stage inflow is not "priced in" by a watchlist announcement; it is a process, not a single event.

## How it shows up on VN-Index

Theory is cheap. Here is the foreign-flow story written across four real chapters of recent VN-Index history.

### 2021: a record foreign net sell into a screaming bull market

Through 2021, foreigners net-*sold* roughly **58 trillion dong** on HOSE — a record at the time. If foreign flow drove the index, 2021 should have been a bad year. Instead the VN-Index *soared*, closing the year at **1,498**, an all-time high, powered by an explosion of new domestic retail accounts and cheap margin (the margin cycle we cover in a sibling post). Foreigners spent the entire melt-up selling into strength; domestic newcomers bought everything they let go. The takeaway is the foundational one: **a record foreign sell coincided with the best year the index ever had.** Foreign flow was a footnote to a domestic story.

There is a sector lesson buried in 2021 that the headline obscures. While foreigners were dumping the large-cap, full-room banks and Diamond names they had long held, domestic retail money was chasing an entirely *different* set of sectors — mid- and small-cap brokers, steel, real estate, and speculative theme stocks (*song nganh*, the sector "waves" we explore later in the series). The foreign-favored blue chips actually *lagged* in 2021 precisely because their main marginal buyer was selling, while the domestically-driven, low-foreign-ownership sectors ran the hardest. So the 2021 divergence was not just "foreigners sold, index rose" — it was a clean demonstration that **foreign flow and domestic flow can favor opposite sectors at the same time**, and that the sectors foreigners abandon can underperform even in a roaring bull if domestic enthusiasm is parked elsewhere. Read the foreign print as information about the *foreign-favored* sectors specifically, not about the whole market.

### 2022: foreigners net-buy the November bottom

2022 inverted everything. A corporate-bond and real-estate credit crisis (the *trai phieu doanh nghiep*, or corporate-bond, blow-up) triggered a brutal sell-off; domestic margin calls cascaded; the index cratered from 1,498 to a low near 870 before closing the year at **1,007**. And in the depths of that panic — particularly the November washout — **foreigners turned aggressive net buyers**, ending the year net-positive at around **+27 trillion dong**. While domestic retail was being forcibly liquidated, patient foreign capital and ETF inflows stepped in to buy quality banks and large caps at distressed prices. This is the cleanest example of foreign flow as a genuine *value* signal: when domestic forced-selling overshoots, foreign buying can mark a sector bottom. The Diamond and bank names foreigners accumulated in late 2022 led the subsequent recovery.

#### Worked example: the ETF inflow that helped mark the 2022 low

Around the late-2022 bottom, the Fubon FTSE Vietnam ETF saw a wave of creations as Taiwanese investors bet on a Vietnam rebound. Suppose, illustratively, the fund took in **\$200mn** of fresh money over a few weeks. At \$1 = 25,400 VND that is about **5.1 trillion dong**, and — by the creation mechanism — essentially *all* of it had to be spent buying the FTSE Vietnam basket: banks and large caps, in index weight, mechanically, into a falling market where domestic sellers were desperate. A price-insensitive **5.1 trillion dong** buyer arriving while everyone else is panic-selling is exactly the kind of force that puts a floor under a sector. **The intuition: passive inflows are most powerful precisely when discretionary money has fled, because the AP buys regardless of how scary the tape looks.**

### 2023-2024: persistent, then record, net selling into a recovery

2023 brought a recovery to **1,130** and modest foreign net selling (around -23 trillion dong). 2024 brought the record: roughly **-92.5 trillion dong** (~\$3.6bn) of net selling, driven by a stronger US dollar pulling money back to dollar assets, profit-taking after the rebound, and ETF outflows from the foreign-domiciled funds. And the index *rose* to **1,266**. The mechanism is now familiar: domestic retail, flush with savings and starved of yield elsewhere, bought everything foreigners sold and then some. For a sector investor, the signal in 2024 was not "sell" but "watch which sectors the foreign selling is *concentrated* in" — because those specific names underperformed the broad index even as the index climbed.

### ETF rebalance-day moves

Finally, the micro-event: the rebalance day. On the effective dates of the VN30, VNDiamond, and FTSE Vietnam rebalances, you can routinely watch additions spike and deletions dump in the closing auction, as every tracking ETF executes its forced basket trades at once. A name newly added to the Diamond or VN30 basket can gap several percent into and on the rebalance, then partially give it back as the front-runners take profit. These moves are predictable enough that a whole cottage industry of *rebalance arbitrage* exists in Vietnam — trading the additions before the funds must buy them. For the sector investor the lesson is narrower: **a chunk of any given day's move in a large-cap name can be pure index-effect plumbing, not fundamentals** — so do not read a rebalance-day spike as a fundamental signal.

## The playbook: using foreign and ETF flows

Pull it together into something you can actually run. Foreign and ETF flows are a *secondary* input — a tone and concentration signal layered on top of your primary, domestically-driven sector view. Here is how to use them without over-weighting them.

### Track four things

1. **Net flow, by sector, with a moving average.** Watch the daily foreign net-buy/net-sell print, but smooth it — a 5-day or 20-day average filters out single-session noise and surfaces the *regime*. Most importantly, break it down by sector: a 500-billion-dong net buy that is all banks tells you something very different from one spread thinly across the market. The signal lives in the concentration.
2. **Foreign room, by name.** For the banks and Diamond names you care about, know whether room is open or full. A near-full room means foreign demand will show up as price premium and re-rating, not as ownership growth; an *opening* room (a company raising its limit, a foreigner exiting) is a fresh-seat event that can pull in a wave of waiting foreign buyers.
3. **Upcoming rebalances.** Mark the VN30, VNDiamond, and FTSE Vietnam rebalance calendars. Know the expected additions and deletions *before* the effective date, because that is when ETFs become forced, deadline-driven traders and the index effect is at its strongest.
4. **The upgrade timeline.** Track the FTSE (and longer-term MSCI) reclassification news flow — the settlement reforms, the CCP rollout, the review dates. The upgrade is the market's biggest structural flow catalyst, and it lands hardest on banks and large-cap quality.

### The signal

**Risk-on tone:** sustained (multi-week) foreign net buying *concentrated in banks and large caps*, room tightening on quality names, ETF creations running. This confirms — it does not by itself create — a constructive view on the bank-led, large-cap end of the market, and it is the configuration you want to see heading into a possible upgrade.

**Risk-off tone:** sustained foreign net selling concentrated in the same names, ETF redemptions, a strengthening dollar pulling money home. Treat this as a *headwind for the specific sectors foreigners are leaving* and a caution flag for market-wide risk appetite — not as a market-timing sell signal, because domestic flow can and does override it.

**Event setups:** an opening foreign room, a known index addition into a rebalance, or a concrete upgrade milestone are the moments when flow is most *mechanical* and therefore most tradeable — a forced buyer with a deadline is the most reliable flow there is.

### Sizing and the invalidation

Size foreign flow as a **tilt, not a thesis.** Let it nudge your sector weights — lean a little more into banks and large caps when foreign tone is constructive and rooms are tightening, lean a little out of the names foreigners are concentratedly dumping — but never let a foreign print override your primary, domestically-grounded sector model. A sensible rule of thumb: foreign flow can move a sector's weight in your book by a few percentage points, not flip it from overweight to underweight on its own.

**The invalidation is explicit and it is 2024:** if you ever find yourself *short the index, or in cash, "because foreigners are selling,"* while domestic retail turnover is rising and the index is grinding up, your read is wrong. Foreign net selling that the domestic market is happily absorbing is not a bearish signal — it is a transfer of ownership from patient foreign hands to enthusiastic domestic ones, and the price can rise the entire time. Respect the flow for what it is: a concentrated minority current that moves specific sectors and colors the market's mood, sitting inside a much larger domestic sea that usually sets the tide.

To make the discipline concrete, hold three rules in your head whenever the foreign print flashes red or green. First, **disaggregate before you react** — pull the per-ticker breakdown and ask whether the flow is concentrated in the foreign-favored sectors or spread into noise. Second, **separate plumbing from conviction** — check whether an ETF creation or redemption is doing the work before you read a human decision into the number, and check the dollar before you read a Vietnam decision into a sell. Third, **size it as a tilt and define the kill-switch up front** — decide in advance that a rising domestic turnover into a rising index invalidates any bearish foreign-flow thesis, so you cannot be talked out of a good trend by a minority of the volume. Do those three things and foreign flow becomes what it should be: a sharp, sector-specific instrument in a toolkit, not the whole dashboard.
