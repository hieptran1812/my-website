> **TL;DR** — A listed retailer is the cleanest stock-market bet on Vietnam's rising middle class and the slow migration from traditional wet markets to modern trade — but it is a *high-operating-leverage, thin-margin* business, so its profit does not track consumption smoothly; it collapses when demand dips and explodes when it recovers.

> - The proof is **Mobile World (MWG)**, Vietnam's biggest retailer: its net profit fell from about **4.1 trillion dong in 2022 to roughly 168 billion dong in 2023** — almost nothing — then rose about **22-fold to ~3.73 trillion dong in 2024**. The structural driver under all of them is **modern-trade penetration** rising from the low teens toward 20%+ of retail — a long runway.
> - The number to remember: that **22-fold profit jump in 2024** off a near-zero 2023 base. It is the operating-leverage signature, and it tells you exactly how to trade the sector — you buy the consumption-cycle *trough* when store economics are turning, not the headline growth story at the top.

In the spring of 2024, anyone reading Mobile World's annual results for the first time could be forgiven for thinking there had been a typo. The company — which runs The Gioi Di Dong phone stores, the Dien May Xanh electronics chain and the Bach Hoa Xanh grocery format, and which by revenue is the largest retailer in Vietnam — reported that its net profit had risen roughly **twenty-two-fold** year on year. Not 22%. Twenty-two *times*. A business that had earned about **168 billion dong** in 2023 reported roughly **3.73 trillion dong** in 2024. To a newcomer, a 22-fold jump in a single year looks like either an accounting trick or a company that has discovered something miraculous. It was neither. It was the most ordinary thing in the world for a retailer: a thin net margin that had been crushed to almost nothing in a weak consumer year *un-crushing* in a better one.

That episode is the entire retail sector in one number, and learning to read it is the point of this post. A retailer is not like a bank, whose earnings grind with credit growth, or a property developer, whose profit lumps in when projects complete. A retailer sells a huge volume of goods at a *thin* gross margin, and it carries a *large and mostly fixed* cost of running its stores — the rent, the staff, the electricity, the logistics. Subtract the fixed store cost from the thin gross margin and what is left, the net margin, is a *sliver* — often just a few percent of revenue, sometimes barely one. And because that sliver is the small difference between two big numbers, when the big numbers move even a little, the sliver moves enormously. A few percent dip in sales does not cut profit by a few percent; it can wipe profit out. A few percent recovery does not lift profit by a few percent; it can multiply it. That is **operating leverage**, and it is why Mobile World's profit fell to almost nothing in 2023 and then rose 22-fold in 2024.

This post is about how to think in that category. We will build the retail business model from zero — revenue times a thin margin, minus the fixed store cost, equals the sliver — and then show exactly why that structure makes profit swing far harder than sales. We will separate the two engines of a retailer's growth (selling *more* through the *same* stores, versus opening *new* stores), walk the four big listed formats and their very different economics (electronics, grocery, jewellery, pharmacy), explain why *modern-trade penetration* is the slow structural tailwind under all of them, and lay out how to value a business whose reported earnings lie to you at the top and the bottom of the cycle. And we will end where this series always ends: a concrete playbook for trading the consumption cycle — when to buy the trough as store economics turn, and exactly what would prove the thesis wrong.

![Flow chart showing retail revenue from volume passing through a thin gross margin then losing a large fixed store cost to leave a tiny net profit that swings hard up or down](/imgs/blogs/retail-sector-vietnam-the-consumption-story-1.png)

A quick word on money, because we will use it throughout. Vietnam trades in dong (VND), and the sector's primary numbers — store revenues, gross margins, net profits, market caps — are all quoted in dong. As a reference for readers who think in dollars, the exchange rate in mid-2026 is roughly **\$1 = 25,400 VND**. So a store doing 30 billion dong of annual revenue is about a **\$1.2mn** store; MWG's roughly 134 trillion dong of 2024 revenue is about **\$5.3bn**; a foreign net buy of 250 billion dong is about **\$9.8mn**. We will keep the primary figures in dong — that is how the market quotes them — and drop a dollar anchor whenever it helps you feel the size. Now let us build the model.

## Foundations: how a retailer actually makes money

Forget the stock for a moment and think like a shopkeeper. You buy goods wholesale and sell them at a markup. The difference between what you sell for and what the goods cost you is your **gross profit**, and as a percentage of the sale it is your **gross margin**. If you buy a phone for 9.5 million dong and sell it for 10 million, your gross margin is 0.5 / 10 = **5%**. That is the first number in the model, and the crucial fact about it for most retail is that it is *thin*. Retailers compete on price, customers comparison-shop, and the goods themselves are often commodities (a phone, a litre of milk, a bag of rice), so nobody can charge a fat markup for long. A typical Vietnamese mass retailer earns a gross margin somewhere in the *teens* of percent across its basket — call it 15-20% — and on the most commoditised, lowest-margin lines (raw electronics, staple groceries) it can be much lower.

Now the second number. Earning that gross margin is not free. To sell the goods you have to *run stores*, and running stores costs money continuously whether or not the goods are flying off the shelves. You pay **rent** on the store space. You pay **staff** — the people on the floor, at the till, in the warehouse. You pay for **electricity, logistics, marketing, depreciation** on the fit-out. Add it all up and you get the **store operating cost** (in accounting terms, selling, general and administrative expense, or SG&A). The defining property of this cost — and the single most important fact in the whole sector — is that it is **largely fixed in the short run**. The rent on a store is the same in a busy month and a slow one. The staff are still on payroll when traffic falls. You cannot switch a store off for a quiet quarter. So the store cost is a big, heavy, *mostly fixed* number that sits there every period regardless of how much you sell.

Put the two together and you have the retail model: **net profit = revenue x gross margin − fixed store cost**. The revenue times the gross margin is the gross profit *pool* — the money you make on the goods. Out of that pool you pay the fixed store cost, and whatever is left is your **net profit**. As a percentage of revenue, that net profit is your **net margin**, and here is the punchline: because the store cost eats most of the gross profit pool, the net margin that survives is *tiny* — frequently just **1-4%** of revenue for a mass retailer, sometimes less. A retailer is a business that handles an enormous flow of money and keeps a sliver of it.

That sliver is the whole game, and the reason is *arithmetic*, not bad management. The net margin is the small difference between two large numbers — the gross profit pool and the fixed store cost. When you take a small difference between two big numbers, tiny percentage moves in either big number produce large percentage moves in the small difference. This is the engine of everything that follows, so it is worth seeing it slowly with real dong.

#### Worked example: the store-economics sliver

Make the model concrete with a single store, because "thin net margin" is abstract until you put dong on it. Suppose one store does **30 billion dong** of revenue a year — about **\$1.2mn** at \$1 = 25,400 VND — at a gross margin of **18%**. The gross profit pool is 30,000 million × 18% = **5.4 billion dong**. That is the money the store makes on the goods before paying to run itself.

Now subtract the fixed store cost. Suppose running that store — rent, staff, electricity, logistics, its share of head office — costs **4.8 billion dong** a year. The net profit is 5.4 − 4.8 = **0.6 billion dong**, about **\$23,600**. On 30 billion dong of revenue, that is a net margin of 0.6 / 30 = **2%**. Two percent. The store moves 30 billion dong of goods and keeps 600 million. The intuition: a retailer's net profit is a *thin sliver* left after a big fixed store cost eats most of a thin gross margin — and because it is the small gap between two large numbers, it is about to prove extraordinarily sensitive to anything that moves either one.

Hold that store in your mind, because the next section is about what happens to that 0.6 billion dong sliver when sales wobble. The answer is the reason the whole sector behaves the way it does.

## Operating leverage: why retail profit swings so hard

Here is the mechanism that turns a steady-looking consumer economy into a violently cyclical earnings stream. Take the store from the worked example: 30 billion dong of revenue, 18% gross margin, 5.4 billion gross profit pool, 4.8 billion fixed cost, 0.6 billion net profit. Now let sales fall — not by a lot, just by **10%**, the kind of dip a soft consumer year produces. Revenue drops to 27 billion dong. The gross profit pool, which scales with sales, falls to 27,000 × 18% = **4.86 billion dong**. But the fixed store cost *does not fall* — the rent and the staff are still there — so it is still **4.8 billion**. Net profit collapses to 4.86 − 4.8 = **0.06 billion dong**. A 10% fall in sales caused a *90% fall in profit*. The sliver almost vanished.

Now run it the other way. Let sales *rise* 10% from the original base, to 33 billion dong. The gross profit pool rises to 33,000 × 18% = **5.94 billion dong**, the fixed cost stays at 4.8 billion, and net profit jumps to 5.94 − 4.8 = **1.14 billion dong** — nearly *double* the original 0.6 billion, off a 10% rise in sales. This asymmetry, where a small sales move produces a large profit move *in either direction*, is **operating leverage**, and it is the defining feature of a high-fixed-cost, thin-margin business. The fixed store cost is the *fulcrum*: the larger the fixed cost relative to the gross margin, the longer the lever, and the more violently profit swings for a given wobble in sales.

This is why you cannot read a retailer's profit the way you read its revenue. Revenue is the *smooth* line — it rises and falls gently with consumption, which itself rises and falls gently with the economy. Profit is the *amplified* line — it takes the gentle wobble in revenue and magnifies it into a savage swing, because the fixed cost stands still while the gross pool moves. A retailer's revenue might fall 8% in a bad year and its profit fall 80%; the next year its revenue might recover 8% and its profit rise tenfold. Same stores, same staff, same business — a thin sliver collapsing and un-collapsing on a fixed-cost fulcrum.

![Two column before and after comparison showing a small same store sales drop wiping out a thin net margin while a small recovery multiplies the profit on a fixed cost base](/imgs/blogs/retail-sector-vietnam-the-consumption-story-3.png)

The figure above is the lever in one picture. On the left, a small same-store-sales drop knocks the gross pool down past the fixed-cost line and the net profit nearly vanishes; on the right, a small recovery lifts the gross pool back up and the profit multiplies. **Nothing about the store changed** — not the rent, not the staff, not the products. Sales wobbled a little, and the thin sliver between the gross pool and the fixed cost swung enormously. This is the single most important picture in the sector, and once you see it, the 22-fold Mobile World jump stops looking like a typo and starts looking inevitable.

#### Worked example: a 10% same-store-sales swing on a 5% net margin

Generalise the lever to a whole company, because this is the move that explains MWG's 22-fold print. Suppose a retailer does **100 trillion dong** of revenue at a **20% gross margin** — a gross profit pool of **20 trillion dong** — against fixed store costs of **15 trillion dong**, leaving a net profit of **5 trillion dong**, a **5% net margin**. Healthy, normal year.

Now same-store sales fall **10%**. Revenue drops to 90 trillion, the gross pool falls to 90 × 20% = **18 trillion**, the fixed cost stays at 15 trillion, and net profit collapses to 18 − 15 = **3 trillion dong** — a **40% fall in profit** from a 10% fall in sales. Push the sales fall to a brutal **−15%** and the gross pool falls to 85 × 20% = 17 trillion; net profit is 17 − 15 = **2 trillion**, *down 60%*. Now imagine costs also crept up and the gross margin compressed to 18% in the slump (discounting to move stock): the pool is 85 × 18% = 15.3 trillion against 15 trillion fixed, leaving **0.3 trillion** — a profit *down 94%*, almost gone, on a sales fall of 15%. That is precisely the shape of MWG's 2023. The intuition: on a thin net margin sitting atop a big fixed cost, a single-digit-to-low-teens fall in sales can erase almost all the profit — and the same lever, run in reverse the next year, is what produces a 22-fold rebound off the depressed base.

That last clause is the key to the whole sector. The 22-fold jump was not 22 times more business. It was the *same* lever, working in reverse: a depressed near-zero base (0.168 trillion in 2023) recovering to a normal-ish level (3.73 trillion in 2024) as sales recovered a little and, crucially, as the company also cut costs and closed weak stores so the fixed-cost fulcrum itself came down. When you start from almost zero, *any* recovery looks like a multiple, because you are dividing a normal number by a tiny one. This is the arithmetic trap that makes retail profit growth rates meaningless at the turn — and the opportunity, if you know to buy the trough.

Look at the actual profit path, because the shape of it tells you everything about the sector's personality. Mobile World's net profit ran around **4.9 trillion dong in 2021** and **4.1 trillion in 2022** — two good consumer years — then collapsed to roughly **168 billion dong in 2023**, before rebounding to about **3.73 trillion dong in 2024**. Plotted year by year, that is a deep, sharp **V**: a near-vertical drop into the trough and a near-vertical climb out of it. Crucially, the *revenue* line over those same years did *not* trace a V — revenue dipped only modestly and recovered modestly. It was the *profit* line that cratered and exploded, because the thin sliver between the gross pool and the fixed cost is what does the swinging. The V is the operating-leverage signature, and once you can recognise it you can recognise the same shape across every operating-levered retailer in any market.

![Bar chart of Mobile World MWG net profit by year showing about 4.9 trillion dong in 2021 falling to 168 billion in 2023 then rebounding to 3.73 trillion in 2024](/imgs/blogs/retail-sector-vietnam-the-consumption-story-2.png)

The chart above is the operating-leverage signature made visible: the 2023 bar is almost invisible next to its neighbours, a profit crushed to a sliver, and the 2024 bar springs back to the level of the good years. The depth of that V is *not* a measure of how much the business changed — it changed only modestly in revenue terms — it is a measure of how *long the lever is*, of how much fixed cost sits beneath a thin margin. A business with no operating leverage would show a gentle dip and recovery; MWG shows a near-total collapse and a 22-fold rebound. The lever is the personality, and the V is its fingerprint.

## The two engines of growth: same-store sales and the store rollout

Now separate a retailer's growth into its two genuinely different engines, because confusing them is the most common analytical mistake in the sector. A retailer can grow its revenue in exactly two ways: by selling *more through the stores it already has*, or by *opening new stores*. These are completely different in their economics, their quality, and what they tell you, and a good retail investor watches them separately.

The first engine is **same-store sales** — often abbreviated SSS, and sometimes called *like-for-like* sales. This measures the growth in revenue from stores that have been open for at least a full year, stripping out the effect of new openings. It answers the question: *is each existing store selling more this year than last year?* Same-store-sales growth is the **highest-quality** growth a retailer can produce, because it is almost pure *operating leverage in your favour*: selling more through a store whose fixed cost is already paid drops almost straight to profit. A retailer growing same-store sales is becoming *more profitable per store*, which is the best thing that can happen to a thin-margin business. Conversely, *negative* same-store sales — each store selling less than last year — is the most dangerous signal in retail, because it means the fixed-cost lever is working against you store by store. MWG's 2023 collapse was, at bottom, a story of negative same-store sales across its electronics formats as the consumer pulled back.

The second engine is **new-store growth** — the **rollout**. The retailer takes its profits (and often debt or equity capital) and *opens more stores*, each of which adds revenue. Rollout growth is genuine and can be enormous — a chain going from 1,000 to 1,500 stores grows revenue 50% from openings alone — but it is *lower quality* and *more dangerous* than same-store growth, for two reasons. First, each new store carries its *own* fixed cost from day one but takes time to build the traffic that covers that cost, so a fast rollout can actually *depress* near-term margins as immature stores drag on profit. Second, a rollout consumes *capital* — fit-out, inventory, deposits — so it burns cash even as it grows revenue. The graveyard of retail is full of companies that grew revenue gloriously by rolling out stores faster than the new stores could mature, until the cash ran out or the immature-store drag swamped the profit. MWG itself learned this the hard way with Bach Hoa Xanh, which it rolled out aggressively, then had to *close hundreds of underperforming stores* to fix the unit economics before the format turned profitable.

So the two engines tell you different things. *Same-store-sales growth* tells you the existing estate is getting healthier — the highest-quality signal, pure margin leverage. *New-store growth* tells you the company is investing in future scale — necessary for a growth retailer, but it must be funded and the new stores must mature, or it destroys value. The healthiest retailer grows *both*: positive same-store sales (the estate is improving) *plus* a disciplined rollout into stores that reach profitability quickly. The most dangerous one grows *only* through the rollout while same-store sales are flat or negative — that is a company papering over a sick core with new openings, and it is exactly the configuration that precedes a profit collapse.

![Flow chart showing the store rollout flywheel where opening a store builds scale and buying power which lowers prices and draws more traffic funding the opening of more stores](/imgs/blogs/retail-sector-vietnam-the-consumption-story-6.png)

There is a virtuous version of the rollout, though, and the figure above traces it — it is why the best retailers compound. Open more stores, and you buy in *larger volumes*; larger volumes give you *more buying power* with suppliers and lower your cost of goods; lower costs let you offer *better prices*; better prices draw *more traffic*; more traffic funds *more stores*. This is the **scale flywheel**, and it is the deep reason retail tends toward a few large winners. A bigger chain can negotiate cheaper goods than a small one, pass some saving to customers, win share, get bigger still, and negotiate harder again. It is why Vietnam's modern-trade retail is consolidating around a handful of names — MWG in electronics and grocery, PNJ in jewellery, FPT Retail's Long Chau in pharmacy — rather than fragmenting. The flywheel is also why the rollout, *done with discipline*, is not just revenue growth but a *moat*: every store you add makes the whole chain a little cheaper to supply, which is a structural advantage a new entrant cannot easily match.

#### Worked example: the value of a disciplined pharmacy rollout

Put dong on the rollout, because this is how the best Vietnamese retail story of the 2020s — Long Chau — actually compounded. Suppose a pharmacy chain opens **374 new stores** in a year (a realistic Long Chau-scale year), and each mature store does about **1.2 billion dong of revenue per month**. Annualised, a mature store does 1.2 × 12 = **14.4 billion dong** a year. So 374 new stores, *once they mature*, add 374 × 14.4 = roughly **5.4 trillion dong** of incremental annual revenue — about **\$213mn** of new sales conjured from a single year's openings.

But notice the two catches the model warns about. First, those stores do not hit 14.4 billion dong on day one — they ramp over many months, so the *first-year* contribution is a fraction of the mature 5.4 trillion, and the immature stores carry their full fixed cost while building traffic. Second, opening 374 stores costs real capital — fit-out, inventory, deposits — perhaps 1.5-2 billion dong each, so several hundred billion dong of cash goes out the door before the revenue arrives. The intuition: a disciplined rollout into a format with proven unit economics is a money machine — 374 stores becoming 5.4 trillion dong of revenue is enormous — but only if the stores actually *mature* to that 1.2 billion-dong-a-month level and the company can *fund* the gap; a rollout into unproven economics, as Bach Hoa Xanh was early on, burns cash and drags margin until it is fixed.

## The four listed formats and their economics

"Retail" is not one business any more than "auto" was — it is a handful of genuinely different animals hiding under one label, each selling different goods at different margins to different parts of the consumer cycle. Let me lay out the four big listed formats, because matching the format to its economics is most of the analysis.

![Bar chart comparing the scale of Vietnamese retail formats in 2024 with MWG total revenue Bach Hoa Xanh revenue and the Long Chau and PNJ store counts](/imgs/blogs/retail-sector-vietnam-the-consumption-story-4.png)

The chart above sizes the formats so you can feel the shape of the listed sector — MWG's total revenue towering over the others, Bach Hoa Xanh's grocery revenue growing fast, and the pharmacy and jewellery chains measured in store counts. Now walk them one by one.

The **first format is electronics** — MWG's two flagship chains, **The Gioi Di Dong** (TGDD, "the mobile world", phones) and **Dien May Xanh** (DMX, "the green electronics machine", large appliances and consumer electronics). This is the *original* MWG, the business that made it Vietnam's largest retailer, and it is the **most cyclical** of the formats. Phones and especially big-ticket appliances — a fridge, a washing machine, a television — are *deferrable consumer durables*, exactly like the cars we met in the auto post: you do not have to replace your phone or buy a new TV this quarter, so when incomes soften, you wait. Electronics retail therefore swings hardest with the consumer cycle, and because the gross margin on raw electronics is thin and the store cost is high, the operating leverage is brutal. The 2023 collapse and 2024 rebound were *concentrated* in these electronics formats — they are the high-beta heart of the consumption trade.

The **second format is grocery** — MWG's **Bach Hoa Xanh** (BHX, "the green grocery"), a chain of mini-supermarkets selling fresh food and daily staples. Grocery is the mirror image of electronics on the cycle: food is a *non-deferrable staple*, so grocery revenue is far *steadier* and *less cyclical* — people eat in good times and bad. But grocery has its own challenge: the gross margin on fresh food and staples is *even thinner* than electronics, and the operational complexity (fresh supply chains, spoilage, dense store networks) is high, so the path to profitability is hard. BHX is the great *structural* growth story inside MWG — the bet that Vietnamese shoppers will keep shifting from wet markets to modern mini-marts — but it took years and the closure of hundreds of weak stores before its unit economics turned. By 2024 BHX revenue was running around **41 trillion dong**, growing roughly **30%** year on year, and it had finally crossed into profitability — the rollout discipline paying off.

The **third format is jewellery** — **PNJ** (Phu Nhuan Jewellery), the dominant branded gold-and-jewellery retailer. This is a *different beast* again: PNJ sells *aspirational, branded* product (wedding jewellery, gold ornaments, fashion pieces) at a *much fatter gross margin* than commodity electronics or groceries, because brand and craftsmanship command a markup that a phone never can. PNJ is partly a *consumer-discretionary* play (jewellery purchases rise with rising middle-class wealth and fall when belts tighten) and partly tied to *gold* (it sells gold bars and its inventory is gold-priced). With around **400-430 stores** by 2024 and a premium-brand position, PNJ is the *highest-quality-margin* name in the sector — less of a pure operating-leverage rollercoaster than electronics, more a branded-consumer compounder riding the wealth-effect of a growing middle class.

The **fourth format is pharmacy** — **FPT Retail's Long Chau** chain, the breakout story of the sector. FPT Retail (ticker FRT) historically sold phones (a smaller TGDD competitor), but its growth engine became **Long Chau**, a modern pharmacy chain that has rolled out explosively to roughly **1,900 stores** by 2024-25 and is racing toward and beyond 2,000. Pharmacy is a *beautiful* retail format for a structural reason: medicine is a *staple-with-a-trust-premium* — non-deferrable like groceries (you fill your prescription regardless of the cycle), but with *better margins* than groceries because of the trust, advice and branded-OTC element, and a *huge* runway as Vietnamese consumers shift from informal, unbranded street pharmacies to trusted modern chains. Long Chau is the purest expression of the *modern-trade-penetration* thesis in the whole sector: a fragmented, traditional category being consolidated by a trusted modern chain, store by store.

![Matrix of the four Vietnamese retail formats electronics grocery jewellery and pharmacy across columns for ticker main driver margin and growth stage](/imgs/blogs/retail-sector-vietnam-the-consumption-story-7.png)

The matrix above is the map of the sector — read it row by row, because the four formats want completely different things from you as an investor. *Electronics* (MWG) is the high-beta cyclical: thin margin, deferrable durables, the operating-leverage rollercoaster you trade off the consumer cycle. *Grocery* (BHX inside MWG) is the structural staple play: thinnest margins, hardest operations, but a long modern-trade runway and now-turning economics. *Jewellery* (PNJ) is the branded-discretionary compounder: fat margins, wealth-effect-driven, plus a gold tilt. *Pharmacy* (Long Chau inside FRT) is the rollout growth darling: staple-like demand, decent margins, an explosive store expansion into a fragmented category. One label, four animals — and your job is to know which one you are buying and at what point in its cycle.

## Modern-trade penetration: the structural driver under everything

Step back from the cycle for a moment, because there is a slow, powerful tailwind sitting *underneath* all four formats, and it is the reason the sector is a *growth* story and not just a cyclical one. That tailwind is **modern-trade penetration** — the gradual shift of Vietnamese shopping from *traditional trade* (wet markets, street stalls, tiny family-run shops, informal pharmacies and goldsmiths) to *modern trade* (organised chains, supermarkets, branded retail with barcodes, fixed prices, supply chains and receipts).

To see why this matters, you have to understand how Vietnamese people have traditionally shopped. For most of modern Vietnamese history, the default was the **wet market** (chợ) for fresh food, the corner *tạp hóa* shop for daily goods, the street pharmacy for medicine, and the family goldsmith for jewellery. These are *traditional trade*: fragmented, informal, often cash-only, with no brand, no fixed price, no supply-chain scale. As recently as the late 2010s, *traditional trade still dominated* — modern, organised retail was only around **11% of total retail sales in 2018**, meaning roughly nine out of every ten dong of retail spending still went through the informal traditional channel.

Now watch the migration. As incomes rise, cities densify, a young middle class grows up comfortable with malls and chains, and trust in branded, hygienic, fixed-price retail builds, shoppers steadily move from traditional to modern trade. Modern-trade penetration climbed from about **11% in 2018** to roughly **13% in 2020**, around **16% in 2022**, about **19% in 2024**, and toward **21% in 2025**. Each of those points is *years* of structural share moving from the wet market and the street shop to the organised chain — and the listed retailers are precisely the chains *capturing* that shift. When a shopper switches from a wet market to a Bach Hoa Xanh, from a street pharmacy to a Long Chau, from a goldsmith to a PNJ store, that is *new revenue* for the listed sector that did not exist in the formal economy before.

![Line chart of Vietnam modern trade penetration rising from 11 percent of retail in 2018 to about 21 percent in 2025 showing the long structural runway](/imgs/blogs/retail-sector-vietnam-the-consumption-story-5.png)

The chart above is the structural story in one line, and the most important thing about it is not how far penetration has *come* but how far it has *to go*. At ~21% in 2025, Vietnam's modern-trade penetration is still *far* below the levels of richer regional neighbours — in Thailand, Malaysia or South Korea, modern trade is a *majority* of retail, often 50%+. That gap is the *runway*: as Vietnam grows richer over the coming decade, modern trade can plausibly double its share of the retail pie, and the listed chains are the vehicles that capture it. This is why retail is a *growth* sector and not merely a cyclical one — there is a multi-year, structural increase in the *baseline* of organised-retail revenue, on top of which the consumer cycle adds its violent swings. A good retail investor holds both ideas at once: a rising structural floor (modern-trade share climbing for a decade) and a violently cyclical path around it (operating leverage swinging profit with the consumer cycle), and uses the cyclical troughs as entry points into a structurally growing market.

#### Worked example: what one point of penetration is worth

Put a number on the structural runway, because it is easy to wave at "rising penetration" without feeling its size. Vietnam's total retail sales run on the order of **6,000 trillion dong** a year (roughly **\$236bn**). If modern trade is **21%** of that, the modern-trade pie is about 6,000 × 21% = **1,260 trillion dong**. Now suppose penetration rises just **one percentage point**, to 22%, with the total retail market flat. The modern-trade pie grows to 6,000 × 22% = **1,320 trillion dong** — an extra **60 trillion dong** (about **\$2.4bn**) of organised-retail revenue, *purely* from share shifting out of the wet market, before the overall consumer economy grows at all.

And penetration has been rising roughly *two points a year*, while the total retail market *also* grows with incomes. The intuition: a single point of modern-trade penetration is worth tens of trillions of dong of revenue migrating into the listed sector, and with a decade of runway from ~21% toward regional norms of 50%+, the structural tailwind under the cyclical swings is enormous — which is why you treat retail's cyclical troughs as *buying* opportunities in a growth market, not as the end of a story.

## Valuing a retailer: when the P/E lies

Now the hard part — valuation — because a retailer's reported earnings *lie to you* at exactly the moments you most want to act, and for the same operating-leverage reason we have been building all post. The naive tool is the **price-to-earnings ratio** (P/E): the share price divided by earnings per share, the number of years of current profit you are paying for the stock. For a stable business, a low P/E means cheap and a high P/E means expensive. For a high-operating-leverage retailer, the P/E is *treacherous*, and you must understand exactly why before you ever use it.

The problem is that the denominator — earnings — is the *amplified*, swinging line, not the smooth one. At the *bottom* of the consumer cycle, when profit has collapsed to a sliver (MWG's 168 billion dong in 2023), the P/E looks *enormous* — you are dividing a normal share price by a near-zero profit, so the ratio explodes to triple digits and the stock looks *insanely expensive*. But that is the exact moment the stock is closest to its *trough* and about to recover, because the depressed earnings are about to un-collapse. Conversely, at the *top* of the cycle, when profit is peaking, the P/E looks *modest* — a normal price divided by peak earnings — and the stock looks reasonable just as the earnings are about to roll over. So the headline P/E, read naively, tells you to *avoid* retail at the trough (when you should buy) and *buy* it near the peak (when you should be cautious). It is precisely backwards, for the same reason it is backwards for any operating-levered cyclical.

The fix is to value the retailer on **normalised earnings** — a *mid-cycle* estimate of what the business earns in a *normal* consumer year, neither the collapsed trough nor the peak — and to apply a sensible multiple to *that*. You ask: across a full cycle, what does this retail estate earn in an average year, given its store count, its same-store-sales trend and its mature-store margins? Then you pay a reasonable multiple of that normalised number, ignoring the optically terrifying trough P/E and the optically cheap peak P/E. For MWG in 2023, the *normalised* earnings were never 168 billion dong — that was a collapsed trough; the normalised number was something like the 3-4 trillion dong the business earns in a normal year, which is roughly what 2024 delivered. An investor who valued MWG on *normalised* earnings in the depths of 2023 saw a cheap stock; an investor who looked at the trailing P/E saw an impossibly expensive one and missed the rebound.

There is a second, complementary tool for the *growth* part of the story: **EV/sales** — enterprise value divided by revenue. This matters most for a format in *rollout* mode, like Long Chau, where current earnings are *deliberately* depressed by the cost of immature stores and the bet is on *future* scale. For a fast-rolling-out chain, current profit understates the value of the estate being built, so you look at the *revenue* the rollout is generating (which is real and growing fast) and value it on a multiple of sales, on the judgement that the maturing stores will turn that revenue into profit. EV/sales is to a rollout retailer what it was to VinFast in the auto post: the tool you reach for when current earnings cannot anchor the valuation because the company is investing ahead of profitability.

#### Worked example: why the trough P/E screams "expensive" at the bottom

Make the P/E trap concrete with MWG's actual numbers, because it is the most important valuation lesson in the sector. Suppose MWG's market value sat around **75 trillion dong** through the 2023 trough — about **\$3bn**. In 2023 it earned **168 billion dong** of net profit. The trailing P/E was 75,000 / 168 = roughly **446x**. Four hundred and forty-six times earnings. To a beginner screening for value, "446x P/E" screams *wildly overvalued, avoid* — at the precise moment the stock was at its cyclical floor.

Now run 2024. The same business earned **3.73 trillion dong**. On the same ~75 trillion dong of value, the P/E is 75,000 / 3,730 = about **20x** — a perfectly ordinary multiple for a quality growth retailer. *Nothing about the company's long-run earning power changed between those two snapshots.* The 446x was an artefact of dividing by a collapsed trough profit; the 20x reflects normalised earnings. The intuition: for an operating-levered retailer, the trailing P/E is *highest at the trough* (near-zero earnings in the denominator) and *lowest near the peak* — the exact inverse of when you want to buy — so you anchor on *normalised* mid-cycle earnings and the *store-economics* trend, never the trailing multiple. The investor who understood this bought MWG when its P/E looked absurd and was right.

## Common misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Retail tracks consumer spending smoothly, so a steady economy means steady retail profit."** No — retail *revenue* tracks consumption fairly smoothly, but retail *profit* does not, because operating leverage amplifies the gentle revenue wobble into a savage profit swing. Vietnam's consumer spending grew through 2022-2024 in nominal terms, yet MWG's net profit went 4.1 trillion → 0.168 trillion → 3.73 trillion dong over those three years — a near-total collapse and a 22-fold rebound *inside* a broadly growing consumer economy. The fixed store cost is the fulcrum: a small dip in same-store sales drops the thin gross pool past the fixed-cost line and the net profit nearly vanishes. If you expect retail profit to be as smooth as retail sales, you will be blindsided every cycle.

**Misconception 2: "More stores always means more profit, so a fast rollout is always good."** No — a new store carries its *full* fixed cost from day one but takes months to build the traffic that covers it, so a fast rollout can *depress* near-term margins as immature stores drag on profit, and it burns *cash* (fit-out, inventory) before the revenue arrives. MWG learned this with Bach Hoa Xanh, which it rolled out aggressively and then had to *close hundreds of underperforming stores* before the format's unit economics turned profitable. A rollout creates value only if the new stores *mature* to healthy unit economics and the company can *fund* the gap; rolling out faster than stores can mature destroys value, however gloriously revenue grows.

**Misconception 3: "A 22-fold profit jump means the business got 22 times better."** No — the 22-fold jump was the *same* business, with its thin net margin un-collapsing off a depressed near-zero base. When 2023 profit is crushed to 168 billion dong, *any* recovery to a normal level (3.73 trillion in 2024) shows up as a huge multiple, because you are dividing a normal number by a tiny one. The growth *rate* is meaningless at the turn; what matters is that normalised earnings returned to normal. Reading the 22x as a 22x improvement leads you to extrapolate a miracle that is really just a sliver un-crushing — and to overpay at the top because you mistook a recovery for permanent acceleration.

**Misconception 4: "All retail is the same thin-margin commodity business."** No — the formats are genuinely different animals. Commodity *electronics* earns a thin gross margin and is brutally cyclical; *grocery* is even thinner-margin but staple-steady; branded *jewellery* (PNJ) earns a *fat* margin on aspirational product and rides the wealth effect; *pharmacy* (Long Chau) earns decent margins on a staple-with-trust and is in explosive rollout. Treating PNJ's branded-margin compounder the way you treat MWG's high-beta electronics cyclical — or vice versa — will get the valuation and the cycle-timing wrong. You match the tool and the cycle-call to the *format*.

**Misconception 5: "Modern-trade penetration is already high, so the structural growth is mostly over."** No — at roughly 21% of retail in 2025, Vietnam's modern-trade penetration is still *far* below regional peers where modern trade is a *majority* (50%+) of retail. The runway is a *decade* of share migrating from wet markets and street shops to organised chains, worth tens of trillions of dong per point of penetration. The structural growth story is in its *middle*, not its end — which is exactly why you treat the cyclical troughs as buying opportunities in a growing market rather than as the end of the story.

## How it shows up on VN-Index

Now walk the real history, because the retail sector's behaviour on the Vietnamese market over 2022-2025 is the cleanest possible illustration of every mechanism above. We will hit the 2023 consumption air-pocket, the 2024 operating-leverage rebound, and the Bach Hoa Xanh and Long Chau rollouts running underneath.

**The 2022 high-water mark.** Coming out of the post-COVID reopening, 2022 was a strong consumer year early on. MWG's electronics formats were selling briskly, and the company earned around **4.1 trillion dong** of net profit. This was the *good* configuration: incomes recovering, consumer confidence high, deferrable durables (phones, appliances) selling well, and the operating leverage working *for* the company — volume covering the fixed store cost many times over. The stock and the sector were market darlings.

**The 2023 consumption air-pocket.** Then the configuration flipped, hard. In late 2022 Vietnam's corporate-bond crisis hit, the State Bank of Vietnam jacked up policy rates, property stalled, and a wave of caution swept through households. Consumers stopped buying deferrable big-ticket electronics — exactly the most cyclical part of MWG's business. Same-store sales at the electronics formats went *negative*, and the operating leverage went into reverse: the gross profit pool fell while the fixed store cost stood still, and MWG's net profit collapsed from 4.1 trillion to about **168 billion dong** — a fall of roughly *96%*. This is the consumer-durable cycle in its painful phase, amplified by retail's thin margin: a single-digit-to-low-teens fall in same-store electronics sales erased almost all the profit. The sector was a place to *avoid* through the worst of it — and the trailing P/E, ballooning toward 400x+, *told* naive investors to avoid it, for the wrong reason.

**The 2024 operating-leverage rebound.** Then the lever reversed again. Through 2024, consumer confidence stabilised, rates came down, same-store sales at the electronics formats turned *positive*, and — crucially — MWG had spent the downturn *cutting costs and closing weak stores*, which lowered the fixed-cost fulcrum itself. The combination of recovering sales *and* a lower fixed-cost base produced an explosive profit rebound: net profit rose from 168 billion to roughly **3.73 trillion dong** — that **~22-fold jump**. The business did not get 22 times better; the thin sliver un-collapsed off a depressed base *and* the company had structurally lowered its cost line. The investor who bought the trough on *normalised* earnings — ignoring the absurd trailing P/E — was rewarded by the operating leverage working in reverse.

**The structural rollouts running underneath.** Below the cyclical drama, the structural story compounded the whole time. **Bach Hoa Xanh**, after years of aggressive rollout and then a painful closure of hundreds of weak stores, finally turned profitable and grew revenue toward **41 trillion dong** (+~30%) in 2024 — modern-trade penetration in groceries paying off. And **Long Chau** rolled out relentlessly toward **1,900 pharmacies**, becoming FPT Retail's growth engine and the purest expression of the traditional-to-modern shift in the whole sector. These rollouts are a *different axis* from the cyclical swing — not the up-and-down of total consumption, but a *structural* capture of share from the informal economy — and both were running, quietly, even through the 2023 air-pocket.

#### Worked example: the 2024 rebound decomposed

Quantify the rebound, because seeing where the 22x came from kills the "miracle business" misconception. MWG's profit went from **168 billion (2023) to 3,730 billion dong (2024)** — a jump of about 3,562 billion dong, or **22.2x**. Decompose it. Revenue rose modestly — from roughly **118 trillion to 134 trillion dong**, up about **14%** — as same-store sales turned positive and BHX grew. A 14% revenue rise is *not* a 22x story on its own; the rest is operating leverage and cost-cutting.

Watch the lever. Suppose the gross margin was steady around 19%. On 118 trillion of revenue the gross pool was about 22.4 trillion; on 134 trillion it was about 25.5 trillion — an extra **3.1 trillion dong** of gross profit from the 14% revenue rise. Almost *all* of that extra 3.1 trillion dropped to the bottom line, because the fixed store cost barely rose (indeed MWG had *cut* it by closing weak stores). So a 14% revenue rise, landing on a thin sliver above a flat-to-lower fixed-cost base, multiplied profit roughly 22-fold. The intuition: the 22x was a 14% revenue recovery *geared* by operating leverage off a depressed base and a lowered cost line — not a 22-times-better business — which is exactly why you buy retail at the consumption trough when same-store sales are turning and management is fixing the cost base, and why the rebound looks like a miracle only to someone who never saw the lever.

## The playbook: how to trade the consumption cycle

Everything above resolves into a concrete playbook. The retail sector is a *high-operating-leverage bet on the consumption cycle* riding a *structural modern-trade tailwind*, so you trade it by buying the cyclical trough as store economics turn — not by chasing the growth story at the top.

**The core setup: buy the consumption-cycle trough as the store-economics turn confirms.** The single best configuration for the operating-levered retail names (especially MWG's electronics) is the *trough* of the consumer cycle, *just as* the leading indicators of a turn appear. Concretely, you are looking for three things to line up: (1) the macro backdrop easing — the SBV *cutting* rates, consumer confidence stabilising, the property and credit cycle bottoming, so deferrable big-ticket demand starts to thaw; (2) **same-store sales turning from negative to flat-to-positive** — the single highest-quality signal, because it means the operating leverage is about to work *for* the company again store by store; and (3) management having spent the downturn *fixing the cost base* — closing weak stores, cutting SG&A — so the fixed-cost fulcrum is *lower* going into the recovery, which amplifies the rebound. When you see rates rolling over, same-store sales inflecting up, and a cleaned-up cost line, you are looking at a profit explosion forming — and you buy *ahead* of it, because by the time the 22-fold profit print lands, the stock has already moved.

**Position by what you actually want.** This sector lets you dial your exposure precisely across the four formats:

- For *geared cyclical upside* on the consumption recovery, own the **electronics** leader (MWG): thin margin on a big fixed-cost base means a same-store-sales turn gears straight into the bottom line. Buy it at the *trough* of the consumer cycle when same-store sales are inflecting and the cost base is fixed — never on a low trailing P/E at the peak, and never let the absurd trough P/E scare you out at the bottom.
- For a *structural staple-growth* play with a long runway, own the **grocery** rollout (Bach Hoa Xanh inside MWG): steadier demand, the modern-trade-penetration tailwind in food, now-turning unit economics — a lower-beta way to own the consumption shift.
- For a *branded-margin compounder* riding the middle-class wealth effect, own the **jewellery** name (PNJ): fat margins, aspirational discretionary demand, a gold tilt — less of a pure operating-leverage rollercoaster, more a quality compounder you can hold through the cycle.
- For *rollout growth* into a fragmented category, own the **pharmacy** story (Long Chau inside FRT): staple-like demand, decent margins, an explosive store expansion — valued partly on EV/sales while the rollout matures, the purest modern-trade-penetration bet.

**Sizing.** The electronics cyclical (MWG) is *high beta* to the consumption cycle — size it like the cyclical bet it is, scale it up at the trough and be willing to trim as the cycle matures and the easy operating-leverage gains are banked. PNJ you can size larger and hold for the branded-compounder quality and the wealth effect. The rollout names (BHX inside MWG, Long Chau inside FRT) you size for the structural growth, accepting that current earnings understate the value of the estate being built and that the rollout must remain disciplined. Never let the sector become one big undiversified bet on consumption, because the cyclical formats are *all* geared to the same consumer cycle — when confidence cracks, electronics and discretionary fall together.

That last point deserves emphasis, because it is the most common portfolio mistake in the sector: thinking you are *diversified* across retail names when you are really making one concentrated bet on the consumer cycle. The genuine diversifiers *inside* retail are the *staple-like* formats — grocery and pharmacy — whose demand is non-deferrable and whose revenue grows with modern-trade penetration rather than swinging with confidence. So a sensible retail sleeve is a *barbell*: a structural-growth core in the staple rollouts (Long Chau, BHX) and the branded compounder (PNJ) held for the multi-year penetration story, plus a smaller, *tactical*, cyclically-timed allocation to the high-beta electronics name (MWG) that you scale up only at the consumption trough when same-store sales are turning and scale back as the rebound is banked. The barbell gives you structural growth through the cycle *and* geared upside exactly when the timing is right, instead of one big leveraged bet on consumption that gets halved when confidence cracks.

**The invalidation — what proves the thesis wrong.** Be disciplined about exit. The bull case on the cyclical retail names *fails* if: (1) **same-store sales roll back over into negative** after a recovery — the clearest signal the operating leverage is about to reverse against you again; (2) the SBV moves to *hiking* rates and consumer confidence cracks (as in late 2022), so deferrable big-ticket demand freezes; (3) a rollout *outruns its funding or its unit economics* — stores opening faster than they mature, immature-store drag swamping profit, cash burning (the early Bach Hoa Xanh failure mode); or (4) gross margins compress structurally from a price war, so even flat sales no longer cover the fixed cost. Any of those flips the operating leverage from tailwind to headwind, and the geared names will fall faster than the market — so when you see same-store sales rolling over *and* rates turning up, you reduce the cyclical electronics exposure and rotate toward the staple rollouts or out of the sector. For the rollout names specifically, the invalidation is different: it is *new stores failing to mature* to healthy unit economics, which would mean the rollout is destroying value rather than compounding it — that is the moment the growth case breaks.

**The one-line version.** Buy the operating-levered electronics name (MWG) at the consumption trough when same-store sales are turning and the cost base is fixed, hold the staple rollouts (Long Chau, BHX) and the branded compounder (PNJ) for the multi-year modern-trade-penetration story, ignore the trailing P/E that screams "expensive" at the bottom and "cheap" at the top, and get out of the cyclicals the moment same-store sales roll back over — because a retailer's profit is a thin sliver on a fixed-cost fulcrum, and that sliver collapses and explodes for the same reason, just in opposite directions.
