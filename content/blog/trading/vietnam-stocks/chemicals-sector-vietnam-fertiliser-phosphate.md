> **TL;DR** — Vietnam's chemicals sector is two cyclical commodity plays: yellow phosphorus and phosphate at Duc Giang (ticker DGC, a mine-to-chemical exporter), and gas-fed urea fertiliser at Dam Phu My (DPM) and Ca Mau (DCM). In both, profit is a *spread* — the output price minus the input cost — multiplied by volume, and that spread swings violently with global prices.
>
> - The whole sector is one equation: profit per ton equals the chemical's selling price minus the input cost, times tons sold. Because the output price moves far more than the input, the spread is more volatile than either, and profit is the most volatile thing of all.
> - Duc Giang's net profit exploded to a record **6.0 trillion dong in 2022** (about **\$236mn**) on a yellow-phosphorus price spike, then mean-reverted to roughly **3.1 trillion** in 2024 — the same plants, a different price. That round trip *is* the sector.
> - For urea makers (DPM, DCM), the spread is the urea price minus the cost of natural gas, their main feedstock. When gas rises while urea falls, the margin collapses; the gas-cost link is the single most important number for the fertiliser names.
> - The number to remember: DGC's profit went 2.5 -> 6.0 -> 3.1 trillion dong inside three years on flat plants. A low P/E at the 2022 peak was a *sell* signal, not a bargain. Value these on mid-cycle EV/EBITDA, not peak P/E — buy the spread trough plus a supply catalyst, sell the euphoria.

In the first half of 2022, Duc Giang Chemicals — ticker **DGC**, Vietnam's dominant maker of yellow phosphorus and one of the most-watched industrial stocks on the Ho Chi Minh exchange (HOSE) — printed the most extraordinary set of numbers in its history. The world price of yellow phosphorus, the elemental product (chemists call it **P4**) that Duc Giang smelts out of phosphate rock, had gone vertical. China, which makes most of the world's phosphorus, had throttled its own production on energy and environmental grounds, just as global demand for everything that contains phosphorus — fertiliser, lithium-iron-phosphate battery material, semiconductors, detergents — was surging. Duc Giang sat on its own apatite mine in the northern mountains of Lao Cai, smelted the rock into P4 at a cost that barely moved, and sold that P4 into a world that was suddenly desperate for it at prices nobody had ever seen. The company earned **6.0 trillion dong** of net profit in 2022 — about **\$236mn** at the mid-2026 rate — more than double its 2021 result and roughly six times what it had earned just two years earlier.

Then the cycle did what cycles do. By 2023, China's phosphorus output had recovered, the battery-material and fertiliser demand spike had cooled, and the phosphorus price normalised hard. The same company that printed 6.0 trillion dong in 2022 earned about **3.24 trillion** in 2023 and **3.11 trillion** in 2024 — a near-halving of profit on the *same* mine, the *same* furnaces, the *same* tonnage. Anybody who bought DGC in the euphoric weeks of mid-2022, looking at a single-digit P/E built on record earnings and concluding that a great company was "cheap," spent the next two years learning the oldest lesson in commodity investing: a chemicals maker's headline P/E is *lowest* exactly when its earnings are about to fall.

That round trip — a windfall, then mean reversion — is not a Duc Giang quirk. It is a *chemicals* story, and it is the same story in two flavours. This post is about how to think in that category. We will build the chemical maker's profit model from zero — the spread between what it sells and what it buys, times volume — and show why that model makes earnings (and the stock) so violently cyclical. We will split the sector into its two halves: the **phosphorus / phosphate** play (Duc Giang, mine-to-chemical, integrated) and the **urea fertiliser** play (Dam Phu My and Ca Mau, gas-fed, less integrated), and see how their spreads are driven by different global prices. We will see why vertical integration is the moat that decides who survives the trough. We will work through why the P/E ratio actively *lies* at the cycle's extremes and what to use instead. And we will end where this series always ends: a concrete playbook for trading these cyclicals on VN-Index — when to buy, when to sell, and what would prove the whole thesis wrong.

![Flow chart showing apatite coal and gas as inputs feeding a process into phosphorus or urea output sold at a price minus input equals spread times volume into profit and the DGC stock](/imgs/blogs/chemicals-sector-vietnam-fertiliser-phosphate-1.png)

A quick word on money, because we will use it constantly. Vietnam trades in dong (VND), and the chemicals sector's primary numbers — profit, capacity, market caps — are all quoted in dong. As a reference point for readers who think in dollars, the exchange rate in mid-2026 is roughly **\$1 = 25,400 VND**. So 6.0 trillion dong of profit is about **\$236mn**; a company worth 60 trillion dong is about a **\$2.4bn** market cap; a foreign net buy of 300 billion dong is about **\$11.8mn**. We will keep the primary figures in dong (that is how the market quotes) and drop a dollar anchor whenever it helps you feel the size — especially because the *prices* that drive this sector — yellow phosphorus, urea, natural gas — all trade in dollars on world markets, so dollars are genuinely the natural unit for the output and cost sides of the business.

## Foundations: what these companies actually make, and how they earn

Forget the stock for a moment and think about the two businesses as if you ran them. They look different on the factory floor but they earn money in exactly the same way, and seeing that shared structure is the whole point.

**The phosphorus business (Duc Giang, DGC).** You own an **apatite mine** — apatite is the phosphate-bearing rock that holds the element phosphorus. You dig out the rock, and then, in an enormous electric furnace running at brutal temperatures, you reduce it (using coke, a carbon source, and electricity) into **yellow phosphorus**, the pure elemental product the trade calls **P4**. P4 is a versatile industrial chemical: you can sell it as is, or refine it one step further into **phosphoric acid** (the acid that goes into fertilisers, food additives, and — increasingly — the cathode material for lithium-iron-phosphate batteries), or turn it into industrial chemicals and detergent ingredients. Duc Giang sells P4 and phosphoric acid into export markets all over the world, priced in dollars. The two raw inputs that matter are the **apatite rock** (which Duc Giang largely owns, so its cost barely moves) and the **electricity and coke** that power and feed the furnace (energy, which does move).

**The urea business (Dam Phu My DPM, Ca Mau DCM).** You take **natural gas** — methane — and, through a chemical process, you first make ammonia and then combine it with carbon dioxide to make **urea**, the white granular nitrogen fertiliser that farmers spread on rice and crops. Urea is the most widely used nitrogen fertiliser on earth. The dominant input cost, by far, is the **natural gas** itself: gas is the feedstock *and* much of the energy, and it can be more than half the cost of a ton of urea. So a urea maker is, at heart, a machine that converts gas into fertiliser, and its fortunes ride on the gap between the urea price (what it sells) and the gas price (what it buys). Dam Phu My (a unit of the state oil-and-gas group, PetroVietnam) and Ca Mau are the two big Vietnamese urea producers; they also make NPK (a blended fertiliser of nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium) and other products, but urea is the core.

Now here is the entire business model of *both* companies in one sentence, and it is worth tattooing on the inside of your eyelids if you want to understand the chemicals sector: **a chemicals maker's gross profit per ton is the price it sells its product for, minus the cost of the inputs that went into that ton — and its total profit is that per-ton spread multiplied by the number of tons it sells.**

That is it. Profit = (sell price − input cost) × volume. Everything else — the financing, the depreciation, the tax — is downstream of that one equation. Let us name the parts precisely, because the precision is where the insight lives.

The **sell price** is the world price of the chemical — yellow phosphorus for DGC, urea for DPM and DCM. Crucially, these companies are **price-takers**, not price-setters. A ton of P4 is a ton of P4 whether it comes from Duc Giang, from a Chinese smelter, or from a Kazakh one; a ton of urea is a ton of urea wherever it is made. Buyers shop on price, and the world price is set by *global* supply and demand — overwhelmingly by China, which dominates the supply of both phosphorus and a large share of the world's fertiliser. Duc Giang does not get to decide what phosphorus costs any more than a farmer decides what rice costs. It wakes up each morning and discovers the price the world has set. This single fact — *price-taker* — is the root of everything cyclical about the sector.

The **input cost** is the raw materials and energy. For DGC it is apatite (which it owns, so cheap and stable), plus electricity and coke for the furnace (energy, which moves). For urea makers it is overwhelmingly the **natural gas** feedstock. The crucial point, in both cases, is that **the input cost does not move in lockstep with the output price.** Duc Giang's apatite cost is nearly *fixed* — it owns the mine — so when the phosphorus price spikes, the cost stays put and the spread blows wide open. For urea makers the gas cost *does* move, but on its own energy cycle, not in time with the urea price; the two can diverge badly.

The **volume** is how many tons you actually sell, which depends on capacity (your furnaces, your reactors) and demand (how much the market absorbs). A chemicals plant has high *fixed* costs — the furnace, the reactor, the depreciation — so the more tons you spread them over, the lower your cost per ton. This is **operating leverage**, and it is the second engine (after the price-taker spread) that makes chemicals earnings so violent.

#### Worked example: the phosphorus spread, ton by ton

Make the equation concrete for Duc Giang. Suppose DGC sells yellow phosphorus at **\$5,000 per ton** (its sell price, set by the world). Suppose its all-in cost per ton — the owned apatite, the coke, the electricity, the labour, and depreciation — is **\$2,500 per ton**. The gross spread is \$5,000 − \$2,500 = **\$2,500 per ton**. That is a fat spread, because Duc Giang owns its cheap rock. Now multiply by volume: say it sells **70,000 tons** of phosphorus-equivalent product in the year. Gross profit is \$2,500 × 70,000 = **\$175mn**. Convert to dong at \$1 = 25,400 VND: about **4.4 trillion dong** of gross profit. From that you still subtract overhead and tax to get net profit, but the *driver* — the thing that makes net profit huge or tiny — is that \$2,500 spread. The intuition: Duc Giang is a machine that mints the spread, one ton at a time, and the spread is the only number that really matters.

Now break it — deliberately — to show why the same machine mints a fortune one year and far less the next. Suppose the phosphorus price doubles to **\$6,000 per ton** in a supply squeeze (China cuts output, the world scrambles), while Duc Giang's cost stays flat at \$2,500 because it owns its apatite and nothing about its mine changed. The new spread is \$6,000 − \$2,500 = **\$3,500 per ton** — a 40% jump in the spread from a 20% move in the price, with the entire increase dropping straight through to profit because the cost did not rise at all. On the same 70,000 tons, gross profit jumps from \$175mn to **\$245mn**, about **6.2 trillion dong**. The intuition: when your input cost is fixed (you own the rock), every dollar the output price rises is a dollar of pure spread — and that is precisely why Duc Giang's profit exploded in 2022. The price did the work; the cost just sat there.

Hold that example, because it is the whole post. The same logic, run with a *gas* cost that does move, governs the urea makers.

## Why the spread is so volatile: the price-taker squeeze

Here is the part that confuses people new to cyclicals. The phosphorus *price* is volatile, sure — but how does net profit swing by 6x? The answer is that the **spread** — the *difference* between the output price and the input cost — is far more volatile than either alone, and **profit, which is the spread times volume, is the most volatile of all.**

Think about what happens when the phosphorus price rises while Duc Giang's cost barely moves. Start from our \$2,500 spread (\$5,000 sell, \$2,500 cost). The price rises 60% to \$8,000 in a genuine squeeze. The cost ticks up a little — say to \$2,700 — because energy rose a touch. The new spread is \$8,000 − \$2,700 = **\$5,300** — more than *double* the original \$2,500 spread, from a 60% move in the price. A large move in the sell price produced an even larger move in the spread, because the cost barely budged and the spread is what is *left over* after you subtract a nearly fixed number from a moving one. This is the mathematical heart of why Duc Giang's earnings explode in a price upcycle: the spread is what remains after subtracting a stable cost from a soaring price, so the spread soars even harder than the price.

Now run it the other way, which is the part that hurts. The phosphorus price falls 45% (from \$8,000 back to \$4,400) as China's supply returns and demand cools, while the cost stays near \$2,500. The new spread is \$4,400 − \$2,500 = **\$1,900** — barely a third of the \$5,300 peak spread, from a 45% drop in the price. The spread *collapsed* on a price normalisation, and this thinner spread is multiplied across tens of thousands of tons, so profit collapses with it. That collapse — from a \$5,300-ish peak spread down toward \$1,900 — is exactly the path from 6.0 trillion dong in 2022 to ~3.1 trillion in 2024.

The chart above is the stylised shape of the yellow-phosphorus price across the cycle (indexed to 100 in 2020). Look at it: a steady base in 2020, a climb through 2021, an explosion to roughly *three times* the 2020 level in 2022 as China cut output and demand surged, then a fall back through 2023 and 2024 toward a level well above the start but far below the peak. That single line — the price — drives Duc Giang's entire profit, because the price drives the spread and the spread drives everything. When you understand that DGC's earnings are essentially a *leveraged bet on that one line*, you understand the stock.

![Bar chart of Duc Giang net profit after tax by year from 2020 to 2024 in trillion dong showing a 2022 peak of six and normalisation toward three](/imgs/blogs/chemicals-sector-vietnam-fertiliser-phosphate-2.png)

And here is the profit that line produced. Duc Giang's net profit after tax: **0.95 trillion dong in 2020**, climbing to **2.5 trillion in 2021** as the price began to rise, exploding to **6.0 trillion in 2022** (the peak, about **\$236mn**), then settling to **3.24 trillion in 2023** and **3.11 trillion in 2024** as the price normalised. Lay this chart next to the price chart above and they are the same shape, because profit is a leveraged echo of the price. That spike-then-settle shape — a sharp peak and a return toward a higher-than-before but far-below-peak base — is the visual signature of a commodity-spread cyclical, and it is what no amount of "structural battery-material demand growth" smooths out.

#### Worked example: the Duc Giang profit swing, and why P/E misleads at the peak

Let us reconstruct the actual Duc Giang cycle with the spread model, because it makes the swing feel inevitable rather than mysterious. In 2021, the phosphorus price was climbing and Duc Giang earned **2.5 trillion dong** on a healthy but not extraordinary spread. In 2022, the price spiked to roughly three times its 2020 level while Duc Giang's owned-apatite cost stayed flat — the spread blew wide open, and on similar volume the profit more than doubled to **6.0 trillion dong**, about **\$236mn**. Then in 2023-24 the price normalised, the spread compressed back toward its earlier range, and profit settled near **3.1 trillion dong** — still well above the 2020-21 base, but a near-halving from the peak.

Now the trap. At the 2022 peak, with 6.0 trillion dong of earnings, DGC traded at a *low* trailing P/E — earnings were enormous, so price-divided-by-earnings looked small, and the stock "looked cheap." But that low P/E was built on a peak number the cycle would not sustain. As earnings mean-reverted toward 3.1 trillion — roughly *halving* — the same stock at the same price re-rated to *double* the P/E, and the price itself fell because earnings were falling. A buyer who anchored on the "cheap" 2022 P/E bought the top of the spread cycle. The intuition: you did not need the company to do anything wrong; the spread normalisation alone, multiplied across the tonnage, halved the profit — and the P/E that looked cheapest was the warning, not the invitation.

![Two column comparison of a wide spread year 2022 versus a squeezed spread year 2023 with price index numbers showing the same plants earning very different profit](/imgs/blogs/chemicals-sector-vietnam-fertiliser-phosphate-5.png)

That figure is the whole post in one image. Same mine, same furnaces, same tonnage on both sides. On the left (2022), a fat spread as the phosphorus price spiked to an index of 320 while the owned-apatite input stayed near 110 — and profit explodes to 6.0 trillion dong. On the right (2023), a thin spread after the price normalised to 170 on the same flat input — and profit halves to 3.2 trillion dong. **Nothing about the company changed. The spread changed, and the spread is everything.** This is what "cyclical" means in its purest form: the earnings are a function of an external price the company does not control, and that price runs in cycles.

## Vertical integration: why owning the mine is the moat

If every chemicals maker is a price-taker selling at the same world price, then the *only* thing that separates a great chemicals company from a doomed one is **cost**. The company with the lowest cost per ton makes money at a price where its higher-cost rivals are bleeding. And the most powerful way to be the low-cost producer is **vertical integration** — owning the steps of the chain that you would otherwise have to buy from someone else at the market price.

Duc Giang's entire edge is integration. Most phosphorus smelters around the world *buy* their phosphate rock on the market, so their input cost rises and falls with the rock price — they have a moving cost they do not control. Duc Giang **owns its apatite mine** in Lao Cai. It digs the rock out of its own ground at its own cost, which barely moves. Then it smelts the rock into yellow phosphorus, and refines a chunk of that into phosphoric acid, and exports the result. It owns the chain from the rock in the ground to the acid on the ship.

![Four stage pipeline showing Duc Giang from apatite mine to yellow phosphorus to phosphoric acid to exports with owning the chain giving cost control](/imgs/blogs/chemicals-sector-vietnam-fertiliser-phosphate-4.png)

Walk the chain in the figure. **Apatite mine:** Duc Giang owns the rock, so its base input cost is low and stable — the single biggest source of its cost advantage. **Yellow phosphorus (P4):** the furnace smelts the rock into the elemental product, the core of the business. **Phosphoric acid:** a refining step that turns some of the P4 into a higher-value product, capturing more of the chain's margin instead of selling raw P4. **Exports:** the finished product is sold into world markets at the dollar price. Because Duc Giang owns *every* link, when the output price spikes its cost does not chase the price up — and that is exactly why its spread (and its profit) exploded in 2022 while a rock-buying competitor's would have widened far less.

This integration is also the **moat** that protects Duc Giang from new competition. A new entrant cannot cheaply replicate the chain: you would need a mining licence for a quality apatite deposit (scarce, slow to permit), the capital for an enormous electric smelter, the power supply to run it, and the refining and export capability on top. That is years and a fortune. The barrier protects the incumbent, which is why Duc Giang dominates Vietnamese phosphorus the way Hoa Phat dominates steel — the integrated, low-cost leader takes share from weaker rivals every cycle.

The trade-off, and you must hold both halves of it, is that this same scale and integration create **operating leverage** that amplifies the cycle. The mine, the smelter, and the refinery cost a fortune to build, so Duc Giang carries large fixed costs (depreciation, and the energy to run the furnace, much of which is fixed-ish). Those costs do not shrink when the phosphorus price falls. So in a good year the fat spread flows over those fixed costs and a torrent reaches the bottom line; in a normalised year the thinner spread still covers the fixed costs comfortably (Duc Giang's owned-rock cost is so low it stays very profitable) but far less reaches the bottom line than at the peak. Integration is what lets Duc Giang *survive and thrive* through the cycle — but the leverage is what makes its *earnings* swing so violently within it.

#### Worked example: integration as a cost wall in a downcycle

This is the mechanism that makes integration a survival weapon, so it is worth doing carefully. Compare two phosphorus makers in a price downcycle where the output price has fallen to **\$3,500 per ton**. Duc Giang owns its rock, so its all-in cost is **\$2,500 per ton** — it earns a spread of \$3,500 − \$2,500 = **\$1,000 per ton**, a thinner but still healthy margin. A non-integrated rival *buys* its phosphate rock on the market; when phosphorus was booming, rock prices rose too, and the rival's all-in cost is **\$3,300 per ton**. Its spread at the same \$3,500 output price is just \$3,500 − \$3,300 = **\$200 per ton** — barely above water. If the price falls one more notch, to \$3,200, Duc Giang still earns \$700 per ton while the rival is *losing* \$100 per ton on every sale and must idle its furnace.

The intuition: in a downcycle, the integrated producer earns a comfortable spread at a price where the rock-buyer is bleeding, so the rock-buyer shuts down first — which removes supply, helps the price recover, and hands the survivor the next upcycle. Owning the mine is not just a margin perk in the good years; it is what keeps you alive and gaining share through the bad ones.

## The urea half: how the gas-cost link drives DPM and DCM

Everything so far has been Duc Giang and phosphorus. Now turn to the other half of the chemicals sector — the **urea fertiliser** makers, **Dam Phu My (DPM)** and **Ca Mau (DCM)** — because they earn money the same way (a spread times volume) but the *inputs* and the *price drivers* are different, and that difference matters for how you trade them.

For a urea maker, the **output** is urea (and NPK), and the **input** is overwhelmingly **natural gas** — the feedstock and most of the energy. Gas can be more than half the cost of a ton of urea. So the urea maker's spread is the **urea price minus the gas cost**, and the single most important relationship to watch is the gap between those two. Both move, on their own cycles, and when they diverge the margin swings hard.

![Dual axis line chart showing urea price index above gas cost index from 2020 to 2024 with the widest gap in 2022 marking peak profit](/imgs/blogs/chemicals-sector-vietnam-fertiliser-phosphate-6.png)

The chart above is the urea maker's whole business in one picture (both indexed to 100 in 2020). The green line is the urea price (the output, the money-in) and the red line is the gas cost (the input, the money-out). The vertical *gap* between them is the spread — the profit pool. Look at 2022: the urea price spiked to an index of 230 (a global fertiliser crunch as energy prices soared and supply was disrupted) while gas rose only to 150, so the gap was at its widest and urea-maker profits peaked. By 2023-24 the urea price fell back toward 120 while gas held near 120-130, so the gap narrowed sharply and the margin compressed. The two lines moving *apart* is a fat-margin year; the two lines closing *together* is a margin squeeze. That gap is the entire story of DPM's and DCM's profit.

Notice the contrast with Duc Giang. Duc Giang's input cost (owned apatite) is nearly *flat*, so its spread is driven almost entirely by the *output* price — the phosphorus line moving up and down against a stable floor. A urea maker's input cost (gas) *moves*, so its spread is squeezed from *both* ends: the urea price can fall *and* the gas price can rise at the same time, pincering the margin from both directions. That makes urea makers, in some ways, more exposed than Duc Giang — they do not own their key input the way DGC owns its rock. A urea maker's worst configuration is falling urea prices *and* rising gas costs together; its best is rising urea *and* cheap gas.

The gas-cost link also ties the urea makers to the **oil-and-gas sector** and to government policy. In Vietnam, much of the gas that feeds Dam Phu My and Ca Mau comes through the PetroVietnam group's gas infrastructure, and the gas price the urea makers pay is partly set by formulas linked to oil and to negotiated arrangements with the state. So a urea investor must watch not just the urea price but the *gas price and the gas-pricing policy* — a change in how gas is priced to the fertiliser plants can shift their cost base and their margin overnight, independent of what the urea price does. This is why the urea makers belong in the same mental basket as the broader PetroVietnam chain: their cost is a gas cost, and gas is an oil-and-gas-sector variable.

#### Worked example: a urea maker's margin collapse when gas rises and urea falls

Make the gas-cost squeeze concrete. Suppose a urea maker sells urea at **\$500 per ton** in a strong year, and its gas-and-other cost to make that ton is **\$300 per ton**. The spread is \$500 − \$300 = **\$200 per ton** — a healthy margin. On 800,000 tons of urea, that is a gross spread pool of \$200 × 800,000 = **\$160mn**, about **4.1 trillion dong**.

Now run the squeeze. The urea price falls 30% to **\$350 per ton** as the global fertiliser spike unwinds and cheap supply returns — *and at the same time* the gas cost rises 20% to **\$360 per ton** because energy prices climbed and the gas-pricing formula reset higher. The new spread is \$350 − \$360 = **−\$10 per ton**. The maker is now *losing* money on every ton — the margin did not just shrink, it went negative, because the squeeze hit from both ends at once. Even a milder version — urea at \$400 and gas at \$330, a spread of just \$70 per ton — cuts the gross pool from \$160mn to \$56mn, about **1.4 trillion dong**, a two-thirds collapse, on the *same* volume. The intuition: for a urea maker the margin is pincered between two moving prices it does not control, so its profit can collapse far faster than the output price alone would suggest — the gas-cost link is the number that decides everything, and when gas and urea move the wrong way together, the spread can vanish entirely.

## The global price drivers: China, energy, and the harvest

We have leaned hard on "the price drives the spread." But the price itself is set by supply and demand, so to *anticipate* the price — which is what trading the sector requires — you have to understand what drives it. Three forces dominate, and they are different for phosphorus and urea even though they overlap.

**Driver one: China supply and export policy.** China is the swing producer of both phosphorus and a large share of the world's fertiliser. When China *cuts* its output — for energy reasons (phosphorus smelting is hugely power-hungry, and China sometimes curbs it during power shortages), for environmental crackdowns, or by *restricting fertiliser exports* to keep domestic farm costs down — the world supply tightens and the price spikes. The 2022 phosphorus windfall was, more than anything, a *China supply* story: Beijing throttled phosphorus production and limited phosphate exports, and the world price went vertical, handing Duc Giang its record year. So the single most powerful signal for the phosphorus and fertiliser price is **what China is doing with its supply and its export quotas.** A Chinese export restriction is, indirectly, a profit windfall for Duc Giang and the Vietnamese urea makers.

**Driver two: energy.** Both halves of the sector are energy plays in disguise. Phosphorus smelting is electricity-intensive, so the power price feeds Duc Giang's cost and, more importantly, feeds *China's* decision to curb its own smelters when power is short. Urea is even more direct: natural gas *is* the feedstock and the energy, so the gas price is the urea maker's dominant cost, and the global gas price (driven by oil, by LNG markets, by geopolitics) sets the cost base for urea makers everywhere. When energy prices soar — as they did in 2022 after the Russia-Ukraine shock spiked European gas — fertiliser costs soar with them, and the high-cost producers around the world get squeezed out, tightening supply and lifting the price. Energy is both a *cost* (input) and a *supply* (it knocks out high-cost rivals) lever.

**Driver three: agricultural demand and the harvest cycle.** Fertiliser demand is ultimately about *farming*. When crop prices are high, farmers earn more and can afford to fertilise heavily, so fertiliser demand rises; when crop prices are low or a planting season is poor, farmers cut back and fertiliser demand softens. There is also a seasonal rhythm — demand peaks around the major planting seasons (in Vietnam, the rice crops), so urea makers have a within-year cycle on top of the multi-year commodity cycle. And there is a strategic dimension: governments treat fertiliser as a food-security input, so policy (export curbs to protect domestic farmers, subsidies, strategic stockpiling) sits on top of the raw supply-demand picture. For Duc Giang, the demand side is broader than farming — phosphorus also feeds *batteries* (lithium-iron-phosphate cathode material) and *semiconductors* and *detergents* — so DGC has a demand tailwind from the battery and tech build-out that pure fertiliser makers lack.

Put the three together and you have the demand-and-supply side of the price. The most bullish configuration for the sector is **China cutting supply *and* energy prices high (squeezing out high-cost rivals) *and* agricultural or battery demand strong** — all at once, which is roughly the 2022 setup that produced the windfall. The most bearish is **China supply returning *and* energy cheap *and* demand soft** — which is roughly the 2023-24 normalisation. Most of the time you are somewhere in between, reading which way the three forces are tilting.

## Valuing a cyclical: why P/E lies and EV/EBITDA mid-cycle is the answer

This brings us to the single most expensive mistake in cyclical investing, and the reason so many retail investors buy chemicals stocks at exactly the wrong time: **the P/E ratio actively lies about a cyclical at the extremes of its cycle.** (P/E is price-to-earnings — the share price divided by earnings per share. A *low* P/E normally suggests a stock is cheap relative to its profits. For a stable company that intuition works fine. For a cyclical it is dangerously backwards.)

Here is why. The P/E uses *current* (or trailing) earnings in the denominator. For a cyclical, current earnings are at their *highest* at the top of the cycle and their *lowest* at the bottom. So at the top — when the phosphorus price is peaking, the spread is fattest, and profit is at a record — the denominator is huge, which makes the P/E look *low*, which makes the stock look *cheap* exactly when it is most dangerous. And at the bottom — when the price has normalised, the spread is thin, and profit has fallen — the denominator is smaller, which makes the P/E look *higher*, which makes the stock look *expensive* exactly when it may be the better bargain. **For a cyclical, a low P/E can be a sell signal and a high P/E can be a buy signal.** Getting it backwards is how people bought DGC at the 2022 top because it "looked cheap on a single-digit P/E" built on 6.0 trillion dong of peak earnings.

So if you cannot trust the P/E, what do you use? The standard answer for cyclicals is **EV/EBITDA valued on mid-cycle (normalised) earnings.** Let us unpack that. **EV** is *enterprise value* — the market value of the equity *plus* the net debt. We use EV rather than just the market cap because we want to value the *whole business*, debt and equity together. **EBITDA** is earnings before interest, tax, depreciation, and amortisation — roughly, the cash operating profit before the financing and accounting layers. EV/EBITDA, then, is "what is the whole business worth relative to its cash operating profit?" — a cleaner cyclical gauge than P/E because it is less distorted by debt and the heavy depreciation of a capital-intensive chemicals plant.

But the *real* trick is the second word: **mid-cycle**. You do not value a cyclical on its peak EBITDA (that flatters it) or its trough EBITDA (that punishes it). You estimate a **normalised, through-the-cycle average EBITDA** — what the company earns in an *average* year across the whole cycle — and you value the business on *that*. This is the discipline that protects you from both traps: it stops you paying up at the top (because mid-cycle EBITDA is far below peak EBITDA) and it stops you panicking at the bottom.

#### Worked example: mid-cycle EV/EBITDA versus the peak-earnings trap

Make it concrete with round numbers for Duc Giang. Suppose DGC's enterprise value (equity plus net debt) is **40 trillion dong**, about **\$1.6bn**. Now we need an EBITDA to value it against, and the whole lesson is *which* EBITDA.

Take a sensible **mid-cycle EBITDA of 5 trillion dong** — an average-year number, between the fat 2022 and the leaner 2024. EV/EBITDA = 40 / 5 = **about 8.0x**. That is a fair multiple for a low-cost, integrated chemicals leader — a reasonable place to own it.

Now make the classic mistake. Suppose it is the *top* of the cycle (2022) and trailing EBITDA is a **peak 8 trillion dong**. If you value the *same* 40 trillion EV against peak EBITDA, you get 40 / 8 = **about 5.0x** — which screams "incredibly cheap!" and lures you in at the worst possible moment. But that 5.0x is an illusion: it is dividing by a peak number the cycle will not sustain. As EBITDA mean-reverts toward 5 trillion, the *same stock at the same price* re-rates to 8.0x — and if the price falls (which it will, because earnings are falling), you lose money even though you "bought it cheap." Conversely, at a *trough* with depressed EBITDA of, say, **3.5 trillion**, you get 40 / 3.5 = **about 11.4x**, which screams "expensive!" and scares you out — even though, valued on the 5 trillion mid-cycle number, the business is the same fair 8.0x it always was.

The intuition: a cyclical's *true* value is roughly constant across the cycle (it is the same mine, the same furnaces, the same cost position), but its *trailing multiple* oscillates wildly because the denominator oscillates. Anchor on mid-cycle EBITDA and you see through the oscillation; anchor on trailing P/E or trailing EBITDA and the oscillation tricks you into buying high and selling low. A simple rule falls out: **when a chemicals cyclical's headline P/E hits a multi-year low, treat it as a warning, not an invitation** — a 5x P/E on DGC almost always means peak earnings about to fall, not a bargain.

## Common misconceptions

Chemicals cyclicals attract more wrong intuitions than almost any other kind of stock, precisely because the normal rules of thumb run backwards. Here are the four that cost the most money, each corrected with a number.

**Misconception 1: "A cheap P/E at the profit peak means a cheap stock."** This is the big one, and we have already dismantled it, but it is worth restating because it is so seductive. In 2022, at the very top, DGC traded at a single-digit trailing P/E on its 6.0 trillion dong of peak earnings — it *looked* cheap. It was the opposite of cheap; it was at the top of the spread cycle with earnings about to fall by nearly half. A buyer who anchored on that low P/E bought the peak. **For a cyclical, the lowest P/E of the cycle often marks the top, not a bargain.** The correction: value it on mid-cycle EV/EBITDA (we got ~8x on a 5 trillion mid-cycle number), and the "cheap" 2022 stock was fully valued once you normalised the earnings.

**Misconception 2: "Fertiliser is a defensive sector — people always need food, so it is stable."** There is a kernel of truth: the *world* always needs fertiliser, and long-run demand grows with population and farming. But fertiliser *makers* are not defensive at all — their *profit* is a spread (urea price minus gas cost) that swings violently, even when *volume* is stable. People kept eating right through 2023-24, yet the urea makers' margins compressed sharply as the urea price fell and the gas cost held. **Stable demand for the product does not mean stable profit for the producer, because the profit is a spread, not a volume.** The correction: treat the fertiliser names as *cyclicals* whose margin rides the urea-gas spread, not as defensives whose earnings are steady — the steady thing is the tonnage, not the profit.

**Misconception 3: "Domestic Vietnamese demand sets the price these companies get."** Demand at home matters for *volume*, but the *price* of phosphorus and urea is set *globally*, dominated by China. You can have perfectly healthy Vietnamese farm demand and a *falling* urea price at the same time, because China's supply returning floods the world market and crushes the global price. In 2023-24, Vietnamese agricultural demand was fine, yet fertiliser prices ground lower because the *global* supply-demand balance had loosened. **The local demand story does not override the global price.** The correction: watch the *global* price and especially *China's supply and export policy*, not just the domestic demand narrative — Duc Giang and the urea makers are price-takers on a world price, and the world is bigger than Vietnam.

**Misconception 4: "Duc Giang's owned mine means its profit is stable — it controls its cost."** Owning the apatite mine *does* stabilise Duc Giang's *cost*, which is a genuine and powerful advantage. But it does *not* stabilise its *profit*, because profit is the spread, and the spread is driven by the *output* price, which Duc Giang does *not* control. In fact, owning the cheap mine makes the profit swing *more* sensitive to the output price, not less: with a fixed low cost, every dollar the phosphorus price moves drops straight into the spread. That is exactly why DGC's profit *tripled* into 2022 and *halved* out of it — the stable cost amplified the output-price swing rather than damping it. **A fixed cost makes the profit *more* geared to the output price, not less.** The correction: integration is a survival and share-taking moat, but it makes Duc Giang a *purer* leveraged bet on the phosphorus price, so you must watch that price as closely as ever.

## How it shows up on VN-Index

Now let us ground all of this in the actual behaviour of the sector on the exchange, because the abstractions only matter if they help you read the tape.

The defining episode is the **2022 commodity windfall and the 2023-24 normalisation**, and Duc Giang is the cleanest example on the index. Through the first half of 2022, as the phosphorus price went vertical on China's supply cuts and the global energy and fertiliser crunch, DGC was one of the standout industrial performers on HOSE — the stock ran hard as investors front-ran the record earnings, and the company became a retail favourite, the "phosphorus stock" everyone was talking about. The earnings delivered: 6.0 trillion dong of net profit, about **\$236mn**, a record. The stock, naturally, traced the same shape — climbing into the 2022 peak as the spread blew open.

Then the normalisation. Through 2023, as China's phosphorus output recovered and the battery-material and fertiliser demand spike cooled, the phosphorus price fell back, the spread compressed, and DGC's earnings settled toward 3.1-3.2 trillion dong. The stock did what the earnings did — it gave back a large part of its 2022 run, even though the company remained highly profitable and still dominated Vietnamese phosphorus. Anybody who bought the euphoria, anchored on the "cheap" peak P/E, sat in a long drawdown while the spread mean-reverted. The urea makers (DPM, DCM) traced a similar arc: a fat-margin 2022 as the urea price spiked and the urea-gas gap widened, then a 2023-24 margin squeeze as the urea price fell back toward the gas cost.

That violent up-then-down is the *normal* behaviour of a commodity-spread cyclical, and it is exactly why chemicals stocks reward trading the cycle and punish buy-and-hold-and-forget. The biggest leader of one year became a laggard the next, inside twelve to eighteen months — not because the companies got worse, but because the spread normalised.

![Matrix table of the two chemical sub sectors phosphorus DGC and urea DPM DCM across input output key price driver and integration](/imgs/blogs/chemicals-sector-vietnam-fertiliser-phosphate-7.png)

The matrix above is the sector on one screen, and it is the map you trade from. The **phosphorus** row (Duc Giang, DGC) buys apatite and coal, sells yellow phosphorus and phosphoric acid, is driven by China supply plus energy, and is *highly integrated* — it owns the mine. The **urea** row (Dam Phu My DPM, Ca Mau DCM) buys natural gas, sells urea and NPK, is driven by the gas cost plus farm demand, and is *less integrated* — it buys its gas in. Reading across the rows tells you *what to watch* for each name: for DGC, the phosphorus price and China's export policy; for the urea makers, the urea-gas spread and the gas-pricing arrangements. They rotate together when the whole commodity cycle turns, but they answer to different prices, so the trade expression matters — a China phosphorus-export cut is a DGC catalyst, while an energy-price spike that lifts urea more than gas is a DPM/DCM catalyst.

#### Worked example: sizing the 2022-24 round trip in your account

Make the sector cyclicality personal, because that is what enforces discipline. Suppose at the 2022 euphoria you put **250 million dong** (about **\$9,800**) into DGC, anchored on its "cheap" single-digit P/E built on the record 6.0 trillion dong of earnings, right near the top. As the phosphorus price normalised and earnings fell toward 3.1 trillion through 2023-24, the stock gave back a large part of its run — call it a 40%+ peak-to-trough decline — taking that position toward **145-150 million dong** (about **\$5,800**) at the lows, a paper loss of roughly **\$4,000**, sitting in a drawdown for the better part of a year and a half. Now suppose instead you had the discipline to *buy* the normalised stock once the phosphorus price had based and a fresh China supply catalyst appeared — deploying **150 million dong** (about **\$5,900**) into the gloom when the P/E looked "expensive" on the lower earnings — and rode the next spread upturn. The same cyclicality that punished the top-buyer rewarded the trough-buyer. The intuition: in a chemicals cyclical, *when* you buy relative to the spread cycle matters more than *what* you buy — the same dominant company is a wealth-destroyer at the peak spread and a wealth-builder at the trough, and the cost of getting the timing backwards is a multi-quarter drawdown.

## The playbook: how to trade the chemicals sector

Everything above exists to support this section. Here is how you actually position in the sector on VN-Index, with concrete signals, the trade, the sizing, and the invalidation.

**The core stance: buy the spread trough plus a supply catalyst; sell the euphoria.** You are trading the cycle, not the company. Duc Giang is an excellent, integrated, low-cost business and will be excellent in a decade — but its *stock* will be much cheaper at some points in that decade than others, and your job is to own it when the spread cycle is turning up and to be light or out when it is turning down. This is the opposite of how you treat a steady compounder, and you have to internalise that or the sector will whipsaw you.

**The signals to buy (the trough-and-catalyst).** Look for a *cluster* of these, not any one alone: (1) the **output price** (phosphorus for DGC, urea for the fertiliser names) has been falling for a while and is *basing or turning up* off a multi-year low — the price is the master signal because it drives the spread; (2) a **supply catalyst is appearing** — and this is the most powerful one for this sector — China cutting phosphorus output or *restricting fertiliser exports* (which tightens the world market and spikes the price), or an energy shock squeezing out high-cost rivals; (3) the **headline P/E looks high or earnings are at a normalised low** — counterintuitively bullish, because it means earnings are at a level they can recover from; (4) for urea specifically, the **gas cost is falling or the urea-gas spread is widening** — the gap reopening is the urea maker's profit returning. When the output price is basing, a China or energy supply catalyst is appearing, earnings are at a cyclical low, and (for urea) the spread is reopening, that is the window to accumulate.

**The signals to sell (the peak-and-euphoria).** Again, look for the cluster: (1) the **output price** has run a long way up and feels *parabolic* — phosphorus or urea spiking vertically the way phosphorus did in 2022; (2) the **headline P/E looks very low** on peak earnings — the classic "it looks so cheap" trap, which is your sell signal; (3) the **narrative is loudest and most crowded** — "structural battery-material demand," "permanent fertiliser shortage," retail piling into the "phosphorus stock," the name is the talk of every coffee table (as DGC was in 2022); (4) the **supply catalyst that drove the spike is about to reverse** — China's phosphorus output recovering, its export curbs being lifted, energy prices falling. When the price is extended, the P/E looks fake-cheap, the narrative is euphoric, and the supply squeeze is unwinding, that is the moment to trim or exit into the strength.

#### Worked example: the round trip a spike-buyer versus a trough-buyer actually earns

Put two investors side by side across the same 2022-24 cycle with the same **200 million dong** (about **\$7,900**) to deploy in DGC, and let the spread model do the rest. The **spike-buyer** buys near the 2022 euphoria, anchored on the "cheap" single-digit P/E built on the peak 6.0 trillion dong of earnings, just as the phosphorus price is going parabolic. As China's supply returns and the price normalises, earnings fall toward 3.1 trillion and the stock gives back 40%+; his 200 million dong becomes about **115-120 million dong** at the lows — a loss of roughly **\$3,300**, held through a long drawdown, and even the next recovery only claws part of it back because his entry was so far above the trough. The **trough-buyer** waits, buys once the phosphorus price has based and a fresh China export-restriction catalyst appears (the contrarian buy signal, when the P/E looked "expensive" on the lower earnings), and rides the next spread upturn as the price spikes again and earnings climb. As profit re-expands off the trough and the stock re-rates with it, her 200 million dong grows toward **280-300 million dong** — a gain of roughly **\$3,500** on the same name. Same company, same cycle, opposite outcomes — separated entirely by *when* each one bought relative to the spread cycle. The intuition: in a chemicals cyclical the entry point is not a detail, it *is* the trade; the spread cycle hands the spike-buyer a loss and the trough-buyer a strong gain, and the only thing they did differently was act with the euphoria versus against it.

**The position and sizing.** Express the core phosphorus view through **Duc Giang (DGC)** as the integrated, low-cost anchor — it survives every normalisation and dominates the recoveries. For a urea-and-fertiliser view (a bet on a global fertiliser-price spike or a widening urea-gas spread), use **Dam Phu My (DPM)** or **Ca Mau (DCM)**, sizing them with awareness that their margin is pincered between two moving prices (urea and gas), so they can swing even harder. Because the sector is so volatile, **size it as a cyclical bet, not a core holding** — a position you are willing to see swing 30-50%, which means a slice of the portfolio you can hold through that amplitude without being forced to sell at the bottom. Sizing discipline *is* the strategy: the cycle will test you, and an oversized position forces you to capitulate at exactly the wrong moment.

**The invalidation — what proves you wrong.** A buy thesis at the trough is invalidated if the **output price breaks to a new low** after you bought (the basing was false, the downcycle has further to run) *and* the **supply catalyst fails to materialise** — China keeps its phosphorus output high and its exports open, energy stays cheap, no squeeze appears. If both the price and the supply catalyst keep deteriorating after your entry, you were early and the spread cycle has not turned; respect that and cut, because catching a falling knife in a commodity downcycle can mean a much deeper drawdown. Conversely, a sell thesis at the top is invalidated if the price keeps rising on a *genuine, supply-constrained* shortage (a real, lasting China supply cut, not just demand euphoria) — but be skeptical, because "this time the shortage is permanent" is what people say at every top.

The deeper discipline tying it all together: **trade the spread and the cycle, not the company's quality.** Duc Giang's quality — the owned mine, the integration, the low cost — is what lets you *hold* it through a normalisation without fearing for its survival; it is the integrated leader. But its quality does nothing to protect you from buying it at the top of the spread cycle. Watch the output price (the spread's driver), watch China's supply and export policy and the energy price (the spread's catalysts), watch the urea-gas gap for the fertiliser names, value the stocks on mid-cycle EV/EBITDA rather than trailing P/E, and act against the mood — buy the trough plus a supply catalyst, sell the euphoria of the spike. Do that, and the same violent cyclicality that destroys the crowd becomes the thing you harvest.
