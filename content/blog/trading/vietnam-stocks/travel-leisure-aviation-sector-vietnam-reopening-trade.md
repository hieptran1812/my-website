> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Travel and leisure (airlines **Vietjet (VJC)** and **Vietnam Airlines (HVN)**, the airport monopoly **ACV**, plus hotels and resorts) is the purest **high-operating-leverage cyclical** on VN-Index: tiny swings in how full the planes are, or how many tourists arrive, swing profit from huge loss to record profit.
>
> - **Airlines have enormous fixed costs** (planes, crew, slots) and earn on the *gap* between a high break-even **load factor** and the actual load factor — a few points of fullness is the entire difference between losing money and printing a record. They also carry **fuel** (~30% of cost) and **USD aircraft leases and debt**, so a weak dong hits them twice.
> - **The 2020-2024 arc is the cleanest cycle in the market**: international arrivals collapsed from **18 million in 2019** to **0.16 million in 2021**, then the reopening drove a violent V — Vietnam Airlines posted its **first profitable quarter since COVID in early 2024** and a **record profit by 2025**.
> - **ACV is a toll booth on passengers** — it collects a fee on every traveler through the airport and earns fat margins — but it carries large **USD debt**, so a weak dong can turn an operationally great year into a reported loss via FX revaluation.
> - The number to remember: a flight that *loses* money at a **70% load factor** can *make* money at **82%** — same plane, same cost, twelve points of fullness flipping the sign. That single fact is why you buy this sector on the recovery *turn* and sell it into peak-cycle euphoria, not the other way around.

In the first quarter of 2021, you could have walked through Noi Bai International Airport in Hanoi at what should have been peak Lunar New Year travel season and heard your own footsteps. Vietnam, having closed its borders against COVID-19, welcomed roughly **160,000 international visitors in all of 2021** — a number so small it rounds to **0.16 million** — against the **18 million** who had poured in during 2019. An entire industry that runs on people moving had its lifeblood cut off at the source. Vietnam Airlines, the flag carrier, bled losses so deep that its accumulated deficit threatened to wipe out its equity and get the stock delisted; Vietjet, the scrappy low-cost upstart, survived on cargo and financial engineering; the airport operator ACV watched its passenger throughput evaporate; and the hotels and resorts of Da Nang, Nha Trang, and Phu Quoc sat empty.

Then the borders reopened in March 2022, and the recovery was as violent as the collapse. Arrivals clawed back to **3.66 million in 2022**, vaulted to **12.6 million in 2023**, and reached **17.6 million in 2024** — back to the 2019 peak in barely two years. The earnings followed with a lag and then a vengeance: **Vietnam Airlines posted its first profitable quarter since the pandemic in the first quarter of 2024**, and by **2025 it had swung all the way to a record annual profit** of roughly **8.45 trillion dong** on **123 trillion dong** of revenue. Vietjet grew profit past **2 trillion dong**, up more than **50%**. The same stocks that had been left for dead in 2021 had, by 2025, completed one of the cleanest round-trips in the history of the exchange.

![Flow chart of the airline operating leverage engine showing high fixed costs of planes and crew feeding into load factor times ticket price to make revenue, with a small load factor change swinging profit hugely and fuel plus USD leases as cost shocks](/imgs/blogs/travel-leisure-aviation-sector-vietnam-reopening-trade-1.png)

That round-trip is the whole subject of this post, and it teaches the single most important idea in the sector: **travel and leisure is a high-operating-leverage cyclical, which means small changes in demand produce enormous changes in profit.** A hotel's rooms, an airline's planes, an airport's terminals — these are fixed, expensive things that cost almost the same to run whether they are full or empty. When demand collapses, the fixed costs stay and the losses are catastrophic; when demand recovers, the extra revenue drops almost entirely to the bottom line and the profits are spectacular. This is what makes the sector simultaneously the most dangerous and the most rewarding thing to trade on a *cycle* — and why the people who bought Vietnam Airlines and Vietjet in the dark days of 2021-2022, when the planes were empty but the borders were about to open, made multiples, while the people who bought at the 2025 euphoria, when every plane was full and there was nothing left to recover, are the ones holding the bag.

A word on money first, because we will use both currencies. Vietnam trades in dong (VND), and the sector's headline numbers — revenue, profit, market caps — are quoted in dong. As a reference point for readers who think in dollars, the mid-2026 exchange rate is roughly **\$1 = 25,400 VND**. So 8.45 trillion dong of profit is about **\$333mn**; a company worth 100 trillion dong is about a **\$3.9bn** market cap; a foreign net buy of 500 billion dong is about **\$20mn**. Aviation is special in one way that will matter throughout: its two biggest *costs* — **jet fuel** and **aircraft leases and debt** — are priced and owed in **US dollars**, even though the airlines sell most of their tickets in dong. That currency mismatch is a recurring character in this story, so we will keep company figures in dong with a dollar anchor, and quote fuel and lease obligations in dollars, which is how the world quotes them.

## Foundations: airline economics, the airport toll booth, and operating leverage

Forget the stock market for a moment and think about what it actually costs to fly an airplane from Hanoi to Ho Chi Minh City. You have to own or lease the aircraft — a single narrowbody jet costs on the order of **\$100mn** to buy or commits you to lease payments of perhaps **\$300,000-400,000 a month**. You have to pay the pilots and cabin crew, who are trained, certified, and on the payroll whether the plane is full or empty. You have to pay for the airport slot, the ground handling, the maintenance, the insurance, the financing. Almost all of this cost is *fixed*: it is the same whether you sell 100 seats or 200 seats on that flight. The only cost that really varies with how many passengers you carry is a little extra fuel for their weight, the meals, and the booking commissions — a small fraction of the total. **An airline is, to a first approximation, an enormous pile of fixed costs that you try to spread across as many paying passengers as possible.** Everything that makes airlines a wild, cyclical, boom-and-bust business flows from that single structural fact.

Because the costs are fixed, the central number in airline economics is the **load factor** — the percentage of available seats that are actually filled by paying passengers. A flight with 200 seats that sells 150 has a load factor of 75%. The load factor is the lever that moves everything, because the revenue rises with each filled seat while the cost barely moves. There is a particular load factor — call it the **break-even load factor** — at which the revenue from the filled seats exactly covers the (mostly fixed) cost of the flight. Below it, the airline loses money on that flight; above it, it makes money; and because the cost is fixed, *every passenger above break-even is almost pure profit, and every empty seat below break-even is almost pure loss.* This is the engine of the whole sector, so let us make it concrete immediately.

#### Worked example: the load-factor break-even

Take one flight in isolation. Suppose it costs **400 million dong** to operate that flight — fuel, crew, the slice of the aircraft lease and crew salary allocated to it, airport fees, the lot — and this cost is essentially fixed regardless of how many people are on board. The plane has **200 seats**, and the average fare is **4 million dong** per seat (about **\$157**). How full does the plane have to be just to break even?

The break-even is the load factor at which ticket revenue exactly equals the fixed cost: seats × *L* × fare = cost. Solving, *L* = cost ÷ (seats × fare) = 400,000,000 ÷ (200 × 4,000,000) = 400,000,000 ÷ 800,000,000 = **50%**. So this flight breaks even at a **50% load factor** — it needs 100 of its 200 seats filled just to cover its cost. Below 100 passengers it loses money; above 100 it makes money. Now watch the leverage above break-even. At a **60% load factor** (120 seats), revenue is 120 × 4,000,000 = 480,000,000 dong, so profit is 480 − 400 = **80 million dong**. Push to an **80% load factor** (160 seats): revenue is 160 × 4,000,000 = 640,000,000 dong, so profit is 640 − 400 = **240 million dong**. A load factor that rose by a third (60% → 80%) *tripled* the profit (80 → 240 million dong), because the 400 million dong cost did not move — every extra seat sold was almost pure profit. The intuition: because the cost is fixed, profit is *geared* to the load factor — a modest rise in fullness produces an outsized rise in profit, and the same gearing runs in reverse on the way down.

That gearing — profit moving much more than revenue because the costs are fixed — is called **operating leverage**, and airlines have more of it than almost any other business on the exchange. The break-even load factor for a real airline typically sits somewhere in the **70-80%** range once you load in all the corporate overhead, financing, and a normal fare environment. That means the *entire difference* between a money-losing airline and a record-profit airline is often just **five to fifteen points of load factor** — a sliver of fullness. This is why a recovering travel cycle, which lifts load factors across the whole industry at once, can flip airlines from deep loss to record profit in the space of a few quarters, exactly as it did for Vietnam Airlines between 2021 and 2025.

It is worth pausing on *why* the break-even sits so high, because it is the root of everything dangerous about the business. A factory that makes shoes can lay off workers and idle a line when demand falls, shrinking its costs to match. An airline largely cannot: the plane is leased on a multi-year contract whether it flies or sits, the pilots are certified and expensive to re-hire if you let them go, the airport slots must be used or lost, and the debt that bought the fleet keeps accruing interest. So when demand drops 30%, an airline's costs might fall only 10% — the other 20% of the revenue shortfall comes straight out of profit. The cost base is *sticky downward*, which is the defining feature of a high-fixed-cost business and the reason airlines do not simply lose a *proportional* amount in a downturn; they lose a *geared* amount. The flip side, the part that makes the recovery trade so lucrative, is that the cost base is equally sticky *upward*: when demand returns, the airline does not need to add 30% more cost to carry 30% more passengers — the planes, crew, and slots are already there, so the recovering revenue lands almost entirely as profit. The same stickiness that makes the trough catastrophic makes the recovery spectacular.

This also explains a feature of airline behavior that confuses newcomers: airlines will fly flights that *lose* money on a fully-allocated basis, and they are often right to. Because so much of the cost is fixed and already sunk, the relevant question for *one more flight* is not "does this flight cover its share of the plane's lease and the head office?" but "does the fare from these passengers cover the *marginal* cost of flying them — the extra fuel, the meals, the handling?" As long as the answer is yes, the flight contributes something toward the fixed costs that are being paid regardless. This *contribution-margin* logic is why airlines discount empty seats heavily at the last minute (a seat flown empty earns nothing; a seat sold for any price above marginal cost helps), and it is another face of the same operating-leverage coin: when the bulk of your cost is fixed, the economics of filling that last seat are radically different from the economics of the average seat.

![Before and after comparison of one flight showing 70% load factor producing a loss versus 82% load factor producing a profit on the same fixed flight cost, with the twelve point fullness difference flipping the sign of the result](/imgs/blogs/travel-leisure-aviation-sector-vietnam-reopening-trade-3.png)

Now add the two cost shocks that make airlines even wilder than their operating leverage alone would suggest, and both of which fall especially hard on a Vietnamese carrier. The first is **jet fuel**. Fuel is typically **25-35%** of an airline's total operating cost — call it **30%** as a round number — and it is priced globally in US dollars per barrel, tied to the crude oil price. When oil spikes, an airline's single largest variable cost spikes with it, and because the margins are thin to begin with, a fuel shock can erase a whole year's profit. The airline can hedge some of this with fuel-price contracts, but hedging is itself a bet, and an unhedged or wrongly-hedged airline is fully exposed to the oil price. So an airline is, among other things, a *leveraged short position on jet fuel* layered on top of a leveraged long position on travel demand.

#### Worked example: the fuel shock

Put numbers on the fuel exposure. Suppose an airline has total annual operating costs of **100 trillion dong**, of which fuel is **30%**, or **30 trillion dong** (about **\$1.18bn**). Now jet fuel prices jump **20%** — the kind of move a geopolitical oil shock delivers. The fuel bill rises by 20% × 30 trillion = **6 trillion dong**, which is **6% of total cost** (because fuel was 30% of cost and 30% × 20% = 6%). That does not sound catastrophic until you remember the margins. If this airline was running a net profit margin of, say, **4%** of revenue, and revenue roughly equals the 100 trillion dong cost base plus the thin profit, then a 6 trillion dong cost increase — about **6%** of revenue — completely swamps the **4%** margin and pushes the airline into a loss, unless it can raise fares or had hedged. The intuition: because fuel is ~30% of cost and airline margins are razor-thin, a fuel-price move of 20% is not a 20% problem — it is a *whole-year-of-profit* problem, which is why oil shocks and airline-stock crashes so often arrive together.

The second cost shock is **foreign exchange**, and this is where Vietnamese carriers carry a special burden. Aircraft are bought and leased in **US dollars**, and the long-term debt and lease obligations that finance a fleet are denominated in dollars. But the airline collects most of its revenue — domestic tickets, much of its costs paid locally — in **dong**. So a Vietnamese airline has a structural **currency mismatch**: dollar liabilities against largely dong revenue. When the dong *weakens* against the dollar (which it has done steadily, drifting from around 23,000 to over 25,000 per dollar across recent years), two bad things happen at once. First, the airline's *dollar costs* — fuel, leases, maintenance contracts, parts — cost more dong. Second, and more violently for the reported numbers, the airline's stock of *dollar debt* is revalued upward in dong terms, and that **unrealized FX loss** flows straight through the income statement as a real reported loss, even though no cash has left the building. A weak dong therefore hits a Vietnamese airline twice: once on its operating costs, and once — often the bigger hit on the headline number — on the revaluation of its dollar debt.

#### Worked example: the FX hit on USD aircraft leases

Make the currency mismatch concrete. Suppose an airline owes the equivalent of **\$500mn** in US-dollar aircraft leases and long-term debt. At an exchange rate of 25,400 dong per dollar, that obligation is worth 500,000,000 × 25,400 = **12,700 billion dong**, or **12.7 trillion dong**, on its books. Now the dong weakens by **5%**, so the rate moves to about 26,670 dong per dollar. The same \$500mn obligation is now worth 500,000,000 × 26,670 = **13,335 billion dong** — an increase of **635 billion dong** (about **\$25mn**). That **635 billion dong** appears as a foreign-exchange loss on the income statement, dragging reported profit down by that amount, even though the airline has not paid a single extra dollar of cash — it is purely the revaluation of an existing dollar liability into a weaker dong. The intuition: a Vietnamese airline's reported profit carries a hidden bet on the dong, and a 5% currency move can swing the headline number by hundreds of billions of dong, which is why FX commentary fills these companies' earnings calls and why you read the operating result *separately* from the reported result.

Now turn from the airlines to the **airport**, because it is the other half of aviation and it behaves completely differently. In Vietnam, almost all the country's airports — Noi Bai in Hanoi, Tan Son Nhat in Ho Chi Minh City, Da Nang, Cam Ranh, and the giant new Long Thanh under construction — are operated by a single company, **ACV (Airports Corporation of Vietnam)**, which trades on the over-the-counter UPCoM market. ACV is, in the purest sense, a **toll booth on passengers**. It does not own planes, take load-factor risk, or buy fuel. Instead, it collects a fee on essentially every passenger who passes through its terminals — a **passenger service charge** built into the price of every ticket — plus rents from the shops, lounges, and restaurants in the terminals (**concessions**), plus aircraft landing and parking charges from the airlines. These fees are high-margin: once the terminal is built, the cost of processing one more passenger through it is tiny, so almost every extra fee dollar drops to the bottom line. ACV is therefore a *quasi-monopoly* with operating leverage of its own — but instead of betting on load factors, it is a clean bet on **total passenger volume** through the system.

#### Worked example: the ACV passenger fee toll booth

Size the toll booth. Suppose ACV handles **100 million passengers** in a year across all its airports (a realistic post-recovery throughput, combining domestic and international), and the average **passenger service charge** it collects per departing passenger works out to, say, **120,000 dong** — higher for international departures, much lower for domestic, blended down. That is 100,000,000 × 120,000 = **12,000 billion dong**, or **12 trillion dong** (about **\$472mn**) of passenger-fee revenue alone, before concessions and aircraft charges. And because the airports are already built and staffed, the *marginal* cost of that 100-millionth passenger is close to zero — almost the entire fee is margin. The intuition: ACV is a machine that mints a high-margin fee on every traveler, so its earnings track *passenger count* directly, which makes it the cleanest pure-play on the *volume* of the travel recovery — no load-factor risk, no fuel, just bodies through the gate.

But ACV has its own poison pill, and it is the same one that bites the airlines: **US-dollar debt.** ACV borrowed heavily in foreign-currency loans (notably Japanese yen and US dollars) to fund airport expansion and the enormous Long Thanh project. So just like the airlines, ACV carries a stock of foreign-currency debt that gets revalued every time the dong moves, and in years when the dong weakened sharply, ACV reported FX losses large enough to dent or even dominate its headline profit despite a thriving operating business. The lesson repeats across the whole sector: **the operating story (passengers, load factors) and the reported number (after FX revaluation) can diverge sharply, and you must read both.**

Finally, the third leg of the sector: **hotels and resorts.** Vietnam has a large, listed and unlisted hospitality industry — beach resorts in Da Nang, Nha Trang, Phu Quoc and Ha Long, city hotels, and integrated resort developers, some of them arms of the big property groups (Vingroup's Vinpearl, Sun Group's resorts, and listed names in the leisure space). Hotels are the *same business model* as airlines in disguise: a fixed, expensive asset (the building, the staff, the financing) that you try to fill with as many guests as possible. The hotel's equivalent of load factor is the **occupancy rate** (the percentage of rooms filled), and its revenue per available room (**RevPAR**, occupancy × average room rate) is the lever that everything turns on. Like airlines, hotels have brutal operating leverage — an empty resort still costs nearly as much to run as a full one — so they collapse when tourism dies and mint money when it floods back. The hospitality sub-sector is the most *property-like* leg (it carries land and buildings on the balance sheet) and the least liquid on the exchange, but it moves on exactly the same travel-cycle driver as the airlines and the airport.

There is one extra wrinkle that makes the hotel leg distinct, and it matters for how you bet on it. A hotel is half *operating business* (filling rooms each night, the occupancy game) and half *real-estate asset* (the land and building, which can appreciate independently of how many guests show up). The big Vietnamese resort developers — Vinpearl, the Sun Group resorts, and the listed leisure-property names — are therefore a *blend* of the travel cycle and the property cycle, and the two cycles do not always move together. In the COVID trough, the *operating* side of a resort was destroyed (no guests) even as the *land* underneath it held much of its value; in the recovery, both legs lifted. So a hotel-and-resort stock gives you the travel-cycle recovery *plus* a property-cycle dimension and a chunk of property-style debt — which is why this leg is best understood alongside the property sector, and why its risk profile (more balance-sheet, more debt, less daily-traffic sensitivity than an airline) sits between aviation and real estate proper.

#### Worked example: hotel occupancy and RevPAR leverage

See the same operating leverage in a resort. Take a 300-room beach resort whose fixed annual cost — staff, maintenance, financing, the building — is **120 billion dong**, essentially the same whether the rooms are full or empty. The average room rate is **2.5 million dong** a night (about **\$98**). In a depressed year the resort runs at **40% occupancy**: that is 300 rooms × 365 nights × 40% = 43,800 room-nights, at 2.5 million dong each = **109.5 billion dong** of room revenue — *below* the 120 billion dong cost, so the resort loses about **10.5 billion dong**. Now the travel cycle recovers and occupancy climbs to **70%**: 300 × 365 × 70% = 76,650 room-nights × 2.5 million = **191.6 billion dong** of revenue, against the same 120 billion dong cost — a profit of about **71.6 billion dong**. Occupancy rose by three-quarters (40% → 70%) and the result swung from a 10.5 billion dong loss to a 71.6 billion dong profit. The intuition: a hotel is an airline that doesn't fly — the same fixed-cost gearing means a recovering occupancy rate flips the result from loss to fat profit, which is why resort operators are a leveraged play on exactly the same tourism cycle.

So the whole sector — airlines, airport, hotels — shares one master driver (**how many people are travelling**) and one master mechanism (**high fixed costs, so profit is geared to volume**). What differs is *where each leg takes its risk*: airlines take load-factor, fuel, and FX risk; the airport takes pure volume risk plus FX; hotels take occupancy risk plus property risk. We will lay all three side by side at the end of the foundations, but first we need to understand *why* this combination produces such extreme cyclicality, and how you value a business whose profit is geared this hard.

## Why the sector has extreme operating leverage, and how you value it

We have met operating leverage flight by flight; now see why it dominates the *whole* sector and the *whole* cycle at once. The danger in a high-fixed-cost business is not just that one flight's profit is geared — it is that *every* flight, *every* hotel room, and *every* airport gate is geared the same way and they all move *together*, because they share a single demand driver (the travel cycle). When the travel cycle turns down, load factors fall, occupancy falls, and passenger counts fall *across the entire sector simultaneously*, so every company's geared losses compound at the same time — there is nowhere to hide. And when the cycle turns up, the reverse: every company's geared profits explode at the same time. This is what makes travel and leisure a **pure cyclical** — its fortunes are dictated almost entirely by where you are in the demand cycle, not by company-specific execution. The implication for an investor is profound: in this sector, *getting the cycle right matters far more than getting the stock right.* A mediocre airline bought at the bottom of the cycle beats a great airline bought at the top.

The extreme operating leverage also explains why **conventional valuation breaks down** for this sector, and why beginners who reach for a price-to-earnings ratio get badly misled. The price-to-earnings (P/E) ratio — the stock price divided by earnings per share — is the standard way most people value a stock, and it works fine for a stable business with steady earnings. But for a high-operating-leverage cyclical, the earnings *E* in the denominator is wildly unstable: it swings from a huge negative number (deep loss at the cycle trough) through zero (break-even) to a huge positive number (record profit at the peak). When earnings are near zero — which they are precisely at the recovery turn, the most interesting time to buy — the P/E ratio is *meaningless*: a tiny earnings number in the denominator makes the P/E explode to absurd hundreds, or go negative when the company loses money. So a recovering airline at the moment you most want to buy it will show either a nonsensical 300x P/E or a negative one, and a naive screen that "avoids expensive stocks" will reject the single best entry point. Worse, the *lowest* P/E for a cyclical typically appears at the *peak* of the cycle, when earnings are at their maximum — exactly when you should be selling. P/E for a cyclical is a contrarian trap: high (or negative) at the bottom when you should buy, low at the top when you should sell.

So how *do* you value an airline? The professional answer is to value it on the **enterprise value to EBITDAR** multiple, where the "R" — rentals/leases — is what makes it airline-specific. **Enterprise value (EV)** is the market value of the equity plus the net debt: it values the *whole business* regardless of how it is financed, which matters enormously for airlines because some own their planes (more debt) while others lease them (less debt but big lease payments). **EBITDAR** is earnings *before* interest, taxes, depreciation, amortization, *and aircraft rent* — it strips out the financing and lease structure so you can compare an airline that owns its fleet against one that leases it on a like-for-like basis. EV/EBITDAR therefore measures what you are paying for the airline's *core cash-generating capacity* before the noise of how it financed its planes, the FX revaluations, and the cyclical swings in the bottom line. It is far more stable across the cycle than P/E, because EBITDAR stays positive even in a bad year when net income has gone negative. The professional buys a cyclical airline when its EV/EBITDAR is *high relative to normal* (because EBITDAR is temporarily depressed at the trough) and sells when it is *low* (EBITDAR inflated at the peak) — the mirror image of how you would read P/E for a stable stock, and the opposite of the naive instinct.

#### Worked example: why P/E lies at the recovery turn

Watch P/E fail at the exact moment you care about. Take an airline whose stock has a market value of **30 trillion dong** (about **\$1.18bn**). In the trough year it loses **3 trillion dong**, so its P/E is 30 ÷ (−3) = **−10x** — negative, which most screens treat as "no earnings, avoid". In the *first* recovery year it scrapes a tiny **200 billion dong** profit, so its P/E is 30,000 ÷ 200 = **150x** — which a naive screen reads as "insanely expensive, avoid". Yet this is precisely the moment the cycle has turned and earnings are about to explode. By the *peak* year the airline earns **6 trillion dong**, so its P/E is 30,000 ÷ 6,000 = **5x** — which a naive screen reads as "cheap, buy" — at exactly the top, with nothing left to recover. The intuition: for a geared cyclical, P/E is highest (or negative) at the bottom you should buy and lowest at the top you should sell, so you must value it on through-cycle cash flow (EV/EBITDAR, or normalized earnings) and read the *cycle position*, not the trailing P/E.

There is a final structural force layered underneath the cycle, and it is the reason this sector is more than just a repeating boom-bust: **Vietnam's travel demand is in long-run structural growth.** Strip out the COVID collapse and the recovery, and the underlying trend of both *outbound* Vietnamese travellers (a young, fast-growing middle class taking its first flights) and *inbound* international tourists (a country that is cheap, safe, beautiful, and increasingly well-connected) has been rising for two decades. Air travel in emerging Asia grows several times faster than the economy as a whole, because flying is a *luxury* that ordinary people buy more of as incomes cross key thresholds — the first family holiday by plane, the first trip abroad. So the travel cycle in Vietnam oscillates *around an upward-sloping trend*, not a flat one. This is what makes the sector different from a pure commodity cyclical like steel: each recovery tends to carry the sector to a *new high* above the previous peak, not merely back to it. Vietjet's passenger count reaching ~30 million in 2025, *above* its ~25 million pre-COVID peak, is exactly this structural growth showing through the cyclical recovery. The investing implication is subtle but important: the *cycle* tells you *when* to buy and sell, but the *structural trend* tells you which names (especially the growth-oriented low-cost carrier and the airport with its expanding capacity) are worth holding across more than one cycle — and which (the heavily-indebted legacy carrier) are pure trades.

With the economics, the valuation logic, and the structural backdrop in hand, we can now meet the actual companies and watch the cycle play out on the exchange.

## The aviation leaders: Vietjet versus Vietnam Airlines, and ACV

Vietnam's listed aviation space is dominated by two airlines with opposite business models, and understanding the contrast is most of what you need to trade the sector. **Vietnam Airlines (ticker HVN)** is the state-controlled **flag carrier** — a full-service, legacy airline with the widebody long-haul fleet, the international routes, the premium cabins, and the heavy fixed-cost, heavily-indebted structure that comes with all of that. Because it flies the long international routes, HVN was the most *exposed* to the COVID collapse (international travel died first and recovered last) and carried the heaviest **dollar debt** load, which is why it bled the deepest losses and flirted with delisting. But for the same reasons, it had the most operating leverage to the *recovery*: when the long-haul international routes came back, HVN's geared losses flipped to geared profits with stunning speed. Its **first profitable quarter since COVID arrived in the first quarter of 2024**, and by **2025 it had swung to a record profit** of around **8.45 trillion dong** on **123 trillion dong** of revenue — the single most dramatic earnings recovery on the exchange.

**Vietjet (ticker VJC)** is the **low-cost carrier (LCC)** — the budget airline built on a single-aircraft-type narrowbody fleet, high-density seating, ancillary fees, rapid aircraft turnaround, and a famously aggressive sale-and-leaseback financing model (it buys planes and sells them to lessors, booking gains, then leases them back). Vietjet's cost structure is leaner and more domestic-and-regional-focused, so it weathered COVID better than HVN (domestic travel recovered first, and Vietjet pivoted hard into cargo), and it returned to growth faster. In **2025 Vietjet's profit grew past 2 trillion dong** (we will use **2.12 trillion dong**, up more than **50%**) on roughly **82 trillion dong** of revenue, powered especially by a surging **international** route network as it expanded across Asia. The contrast is the classic LCC-versus-flag-carrier divide the world over: Vietjet is leaner, more domestic, faster to recover, and financially more aggressive; Vietnam Airlines is heavier, more international, more leveraged, and therefore both the bigger COVID casualty *and* the bigger recovery winner.

![Grouped bar chart of Vietnam aviation leaders in 2025 showing Vietnam Airlines revenue 123 trillion dong and profit 8.45 trillion dong versus Vietjet revenue 82 trillion dong and profit 2.12 trillion dong](/imgs/blogs/travel-leisure-aviation-sector-vietnam-reopening-trade-4.png)

And sitting behind both of them, collecting a fee on every passenger either airline carries, is **ACV** — the airport operator. ACV is the *only* way to bet on the travel recovery *without* taking load-factor or fuel risk: whether the passenger flies HVN or VJC, full fare or discount, ACV gets its passenger service charge either way. In the recovery, ACV's passenger throughput and fee revenue surged and its operating business was extremely profitable. But — to repeat the sector's recurring lesson — ACV's *reported* profit in any given year is hostage to its large foreign-currency debt: in years the dong weakened sharply, ACV booked FX losses that ate a large slice of an otherwise excellent operating result. So even the "safe" toll-booth leg of the sector carries the FX bet that runs through everything in Vietnamese aviation.

Watch the passenger recovery directly, because passenger count is the cleanest read on the whole sector's fortunes, and Vietjet's traffic shows the V-shape with textbook clarity.

![Line chart of Vietjet approximate passengers carried by year from 25 million in 2019 down to 5 million in 2021 then recovering to 30 million by 2025 showing the V shaped travel recovery](/imgs/blogs/travel-leisure-aviation-sector-vietnam-reopening-trade-6.png)

Vietjet carried roughly **25 million passengers in 2019**, collapsed to about **5 million in 2021** as the country locked down, then recovered explosively: **21 million in 2022**, **25.3 million in 2023**, **27 million in 2024**, and around **30 million by 2025** — comfortably above the pre-COVID peak, with the *international* leg the engine of the latest growth as the airline pushed routes across Asia. That curve is the sector in one picture: a near-total collapse, a violent V-shaped recovery, and a new high driven by the structural growth of Vietnamese travel. Every earnings number in this post is downstream of that passenger curve.

Before we map the cycle onto VN-Index, lay the three sub-sectors side by side so the differences in risk are explicit.

![Matrix table comparing the three travel and leisure sub-sectors airlines airports and hotels across their main demand driver fixed cost intensity foreign exchange exposure and cyclicality](/imgs/blogs/travel-leisure-aviation-sector-vietnam-reopening-trade-7.png)

The matrix makes the trade-offs legible: **airlines** (HVN, VJC) take the most risk — load factor, fuel, *and* FX — and have the highest cyclicality, so they are the highest-octane recovery play and the most dangerous at the top. **Airports** (ACV) take pure passenger-volume risk plus FX, with no fuel and no load-factor risk, so they are the cleaner, slightly-lower-beta volume play. **Hotels and resorts** take occupancy risk plus property risk, with the property leg adding a land-and-debt dimension the others lack, and the least liquidity on the exchange. All three share the travel-cycle driver, but you pick *which* leg by how much fuel and FX risk you want bundled with your bet on bodies-in-motion.

## Common misconceptions

Three beliefs cost retail investors money in this sector, and each one inverts the truth in a way the operating leverage explains.

**Misconception 1: "Airlines are a good long-term buy-and-hold."** This is perhaps the most expensive myth in all of investing, and it is wrong for a precise reason. Across a *full cycle*, airlines as an industry have historically destroyed capital — the operating leverage that produces spectacular profits at the peak produces equally spectacular losses at the trough, and the losses tend to win over time because price competition (especially from low-cost carriers) competes away the peak profits while the fixed costs and debt never go away. Vietnam Airlines is a vivid case: a buy-and-hold investor who bought it in 2019 at the pre-COVID peak rode it down through near-delisting losses and, even after the record-2025 recovery, may not have recovered their entry. Airlines are a *trade* — a thing you buy at the trough and sell at the peak — **not** a hold. The discipline of the sector is *exit*, not patience.

**Misconception 2: "The reopening recovery means a permanent boom."** When arrivals were vaulting from 3.6 million to 17.6 million between 2022 and 2024, it was tempting to extrapolate the line forever. But a *recovery* is not the same as *permanent growth*. Most of the 2022-2024 surge was simply travel *returning to its pre-COVID level* — recovering the 18 million of 2019, not growing beyond it. Once arrivals reach and pass the old peak (around **2024-2025**), the easy recovery is *over*, and from there the sector grows only at the slower *structural* rate of Vietnamese and regional travel demand — strong, but nothing like the V-shaped snap-back. The investor who bought the *recovery* (cheap, geared, lots of slack to fill) made multiples; the investor who bought the *permanent boom* thesis at the 2025 peak bought a fully-recovered sector with the easy gains already banked and only the slow structural growth ahead — and paid a peak-cycle valuation for it. The reopening is a one-time recovery, not a new normal of perpetual acceleration.

**Misconception 3: "ACV is risk-free because it is a monopoly toll booth."** ACV's operating model genuinely is a beautiful, high-margin quasi-monopoly — but "monopoly on operations" does not mean "no risk on the reported number." Two things can hurt ACV that have nothing to do with its monopoly. First, **FX**: its large foreign-currency debt means a weak dong can turn an excellent operating year into a thin or even loss-making *reported* year through revaluation, as it has more than once. Second, **the capex cycle**: ACV is funding the gigantic **Long Thanh** airport, a multi-billion-dollar project that consumes cash, adds debt and depreciation, and will pressure near-term returns before it (eventually) adds capacity. A monopoly that is in the middle of a vast, debt-funded construction program is not risk-free — it is a great long-term franchise carrying a heavy financing and FX load in the meantime. Read the operating result and the reported result separately, always.

## How it shows up on VN-Index

Now watch the whole cycle play out on the exchange, because the travel-and-leisure stocks trace the demand cycle with a clarity that makes them a kind of textbook for cyclical investing. The driver is the arrivals curve, so start there.

![Line chart of Vietnam international tourist arrivals by year from 18 million in 2019 collapsing to 0.16 million in 2021 then recovering through 12.6 million in 2023 to 20 million by 2025 forming a V shape](/imgs/blogs/travel-leisure-aviation-sector-vietnam-reopening-trade-2.png)

**The 2020-2021 collapse.** When COVID closed the borders, international arrivals fell off a cliff — from **18 million in 2019** to **3.8 million in 2020** to **0.16 million in 2021**. The aviation stocks were destroyed: Vietnam Airlines' losses were so deep its accumulated deficit threatened its listing, and even the leaner Vietjet was kept alive by cargo and financial engineering. ACV's throughput cratered, and the hotels emptied. For an investor with cash and nerve, this was the *setup*: a high-operating-leverage sector trading at a trough, with the entire upside contingent on one binary event — the borders reopening. The risk was real (the airlines could have gone insolvent), but the asymmetry was extraordinary, because operating leverage that crushes you on the way down lifts you violently on the way back.

**The 2022 reopening and the recovery turn.** Vietnam reopened its borders in **March 2022**, and arrivals began the climb back: **3.66 million in 2022**. But here is the subtle, crucial point about *timing* that separates the people who made money from the people who chased it. The stocks did not wait for the *earnings* to recover — they moved on the *reopening news* and the *expectation* of recovery, often *while the airlines were still reporting losses*. Earnings lag the cycle (load factors take time to fill, and the FX and accumulated losses linger on the books), so if you wait for the clean profit print before buying, you have missed the first and biggest leg of the move. The recovery *turn* — the moment the cycle inflects from collapse to recovery — is bought on the *leading indicator* (arrivals, flight schedules, border policy), not the *lagging* one (reported profit). This is the heart of the playbook.

**The 2023-2025 recovery and the record.** Arrivals vaulted to **12.6 million in 2023**, **17.6 million in 2024**, and around **20 million in 2025** — through and past the 2019 peak. The earnings followed: load factors climbed back above break-even, the geared losses became geared profits, and **Vietnam Airlines posted its first profitable quarter since COVID in early 2024**, then swung to a **record profit by 2025**. Vietjet rode its surging international network to profit growth past **50%**. This was the *payoff* phase — but also, critically, the phase in which the *easy* money had already been made. By the time the headlines screamed "record airline profits!" and "tourism fully recovered!" in 2025, the sector had completed its recovery: arrivals were back above the old peak, load factors were high, and there was little *slack* left to fill. The geared upside that had been there at the 2022 trough was now mostly *realized*, and what remained was the slower structural growth — at a peak-cycle valuation.

**VJC versus HVN through the cycle.** The two airlines tell slightly different versions of the story, and the difference is instructive. HVN — heavier, more international, more indebted — fell *harder* (deepest losses, delisting risk) and recovered *more violently* (the record swing), so it was the higher-beta, higher-drama recovery play, best bought at maximum pessimism and most dangerous at the euphoric top. VJC — leaner, more domestic-then-regional, financially nimbler — fell *less* and recovered *faster and more steadily*, with the international expansion giving it a structural-growth leg that outlives the pure recovery. So a recovery-trade investor might lean toward HVN for maximum leverage to the *turn*, while an investor who wants the recovery *plus* ongoing structural growth might prefer VJC's expanding international franchise. Both are the same cycle; they differ in how much leverage and how much structural growth you want bundled in.

**The FX overlay across the cycle.** Layer the dong on top of the arrivals curve and you see why the *reported* numbers were even more dramatic than the operating story. Through the recovery years, the dong drifted weaker against the dollar — from the low-23,000s toward the mid-25,000s per dollar — which means that in the very same years aviation's operating business was healing, the FX revaluation of its dollar debt was working *against* the reported result. Vietnam Airlines, with the heaviest dollar-debt load, took the largest FX hits; ACV, with its yen and dollar borrowings for airport expansion, took its own. The effect is that a reader watching only the headline net-profit line saw a *noisier, more volatile* recovery than the clean operating recovery underneath, and in some quarters an excellent operating result was masked by a large FX revaluation loss. This is why disciplined analysts of this sector strip the FX revaluation out and track the *operating* profit (or EBITDAR) separately — it is the cleaner read on where the cycle actually is, because the FX line is a non-cash revaluation that can reverse the moment the dong stabilizes. The lesson for the playbook: a *stabilizing or strengthening dong* is a double tailwind near the recovery — it relieves the operating cost of fuel and leases *and* it removes (or reverses) the FX drag on the headline number, so the reported profit can leap even faster than the operating profit when the currency turns supportive.

**Why the whole sector moves together — and when it doesn't.** Because all three legs share the travel-cycle driver, they tend to move as a *pack*: when arrivals inflect up, HVN, VJC, ACV, and the resort names all catch a bid roughly together, which is the textbook behavior of a sector with a single dominant driver (a *sóng ngành*, a sector "wave," in local trading parlance). But the legs *de-synchronize* on their leg-specific risks, and spotting that is where the edge sharpens. A fuel spike hurts the airlines (fuel is their cost) while leaving ACV largely untouched (the airport buys no fuel) — so in an oil shock, ACV outperforms the airlines within the same recovering sector. A sharp dong devaluation hurts everyone with dollar debt (HVN and ACV) more than a leaner, less-indebted name. And the *domestic-versus-international* split matters for timing: domestic travel recovered first after COVID (Vietnamese moving within their own country), so the more-domestic Vietjet inflected before the more-international Vietnam Airlines, whose long-haul routes recovered last. So the *sector* gives you the big cyclical move, but reading *which leg* leads or lags on fuel, FX, and the domestic/international mix is how you pick the right horse within the pack.

## The playbook: how to trade it

Everything above converges on a single, disciplined way to trade this sector, and it is almost the opposite of how most people instinctively approach it. Here is the playbook.

**The signal to buy — the recovery turn, on leading indicators.** You buy travel and leisure when the cycle is at or near its *trough* and a *catalyst* is turning it up — not when the earnings are good. Concretely, the buy setup is: (1) the sector has been crushed by a demand shock (a pandemic, a recession, a travel collapse) and the stocks are pricing despair, often with negative or nonsensical P/E and depressed valuations; (2) a *leading indicator* turns — border reopenings, a jump in flight schedules and bookings, a recovery in the **monthly arrivals** print, rising load factors, a falling oil price (which relieves the fuel cost), or a stabilizing dong (which relieves the FX drag); and (3) crucially, you act on the *leading* indicator while the *reported earnings are still bad*, because the earnings lag the cycle and the stock moves first. The 2022 reopening was the textbook buy: borders opening, arrivals inflecting up, airlines still reporting losses — the maximum-asymmetry moment.

**What to watch, month by month.** The most important data print for this sector is the **monthly international arrivals** figure from the Vietnam National Authority of Tourism — it is the leading edge of the whole sector's earnings. Alongside it: the airlines' **load factors** and **passenger counts** (in their monthly operating updates), the **jet fuel / Brent crude** price (a falling oil price is a tailwind, a spiking one a warning), and the **USD/VND** exchange rate (a stabilizing or strengthening dong relieves the FX drag on both airlines and ACV; a sharply weakening dong is a headwind to the reported numbers). When arrivals are inflecting up, oil is calm, and the dong is stable, the operating leverage is working *for* you and the sector is in its sweet spot.

**The position and sizing.** Size this as the *high-volatility cyclical* it is. The geared upside is real, but so is the downside — these stocks can halve in a demand shock, and the airlines carry genuine insolvency risk at the extreme trough. So you take a *cyclical* position size, not a *core* one: meaningful enough to matter on the recovery, small enough to survive being early or wrong. If you want the maximum leverage to the *turn*, lean toward the heaviest, most-beaten-down name (Vietnam Airlines at the trough); if you want recovery *plus* structural growth with somewhat less drama, lean toward Vietjet's expanding international franchise; if you want the cleanest *volume* bet without fuel and load-factor risk, take ACV (accepting its FX and Long-Thanh-capex baggage). You can spread across the three to capture the cycle while diversifying the leg-specific risks.

**The exit — peak-cycle euphoria.** This is the hardest and most important part, because the sector punishes the buy-and-hold instinct. You *sell* travel and leisure into the *good news*: when arrivals have recovered to and past the old peak, when load factors are high and there is little slack left to fill, when the headlines are celebrating "record profits" and "full recovery," and when the trailing P/E has fallen to a low number (the classic peak-cycle signal for a geared cyclical). At that point the geared upside has been *realized*, the easy recovery is *over*, and what remains is slower structural growth at a peak valuation — an unfavorable risk-reward. The discipline is to take the cyclical gain when the cycle is hot and *not* to hold a high-operating-leverage cyclical through the top hoping for more, because the same leverage that lifted you will crush you when the cycle eventually rolls over.

**The invalidation — what would prove the trade wrong.** Every position needs a line that says "I was wrong." For the *recovery* trade, the thesis is invalidated if the **leading indicators reverse**: if monthly arrivals *roll back over* (a fresh demand shock — a new pandemic scare, a regional recession, a geopolitical event that kills travel), if **oil spikes** sharply and stays high (a fuel shock that erases the recovering margins), or if the **dong weakens violently** (an FX shock that drags the reported numbers back into loss and, for the airlines, raises the spectre of the debt problem again). Any of those breaks the recovery thesis and is the cue to cut — because in a high-operating-leverage sector, a reversing cycle compounds against you just as fast as it compounded for you. The trade lives and dies on the *direction of the cycle*, so you exit the moment the cycle turns, regardless of how good last quarter's earnings looked.

**A note on the legacy carrier's special risk.** One more invalidation deserves its own line because it is specific to Vietnam Airlines and to legacy flag carriers generally: **balance-sheet solvency.** At the depth of the COVID trough, HVN's accumulated losses had eroded its equity to the point that delisting rules were genuinely in play, and only state support and capital raises kept it on the exchange. A high-operating-leverage company carrying heavy dollar debt does not merely have *earnings* risk in a prolonged trough — it has *existence* risk, because sustained geared losses can eat through equity entirely and trigger forced dilution (a rescue capital raise that crushes existing shareholders) or worse. So when you buy the heaviest, most-indebted name for maximum recovery leverage, you are also taking the most *solvency* risk if the recovery is delayed — which is precisely why you buy it at the *turn*, when the catalyst is already in motion, rather than catching the falling knife earlier when the trough could still deepen. The leverage cuts both ways: it is why HVN was the biggest recovery winner, and why it would be the biggest casualty if the next shock arrives before its balance sheet has fully healed.

**Position the legs to your macro view.** A practical refinement: once you have decided the cycle has turned, *which* leg you weight should follow your *macro* read. If you also believe oil is heading lower and the dong is stabilizing, lean hardest into the airlines (HVN, VJC) — they get the load-factor recovery *plus* the fuel and FX tailwinds, a triple win. If you are worried about an oil spike but still want the volume recovery, tilt toward ACV, which carries no fuel cost and gives you the cleanest passenger-volume bet (accepting its own FX exposure). If you want the recovery with a property-cycle kicker and can tolerate illiquidity, add a resort name. This is the sector-rotation logic applied *within* travel and leisure: the same travel cycle drives all three legs, but the fuel and FX macro dials tell you which leg to overweight at any given moment, turning a single sector bet into a more precise expression of your whole macro view.

In sum: travel and leisure is the market's purest geared bet on the travel cycle. You buy the *recovery turn* on the *leading* indicators while earnings are still bad, you size it as the volatile cyclical it is, you ride the operating leverage up, and you *sell into the peak-cycle euphoria* before the same leverage turns against you. The 2020-2025 arc — collapse to 0.16 million arrivals, then a V-shaped recovery to a record airline profit — is the cleanest worked example of that discipline the Vietnamese market has ever produced, and the next demand shock will write the same script again.
