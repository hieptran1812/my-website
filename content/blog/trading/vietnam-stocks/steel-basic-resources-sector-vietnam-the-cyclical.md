> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Steel is the textbook cyclical: a price-taker whose profit is the *spread* between the steel it sells and the raw materials (iron ore plus coking coal) it buys, multiplied by volume — and that spread is geared to construction demand and public investment, so earnings and the stock swing violently.
>
> - The whole sector is one equation: profit per ton equals steel price minus input cost, times tons sold. Because price moves and inputs lag, the spread is far more volatile than either, and profit is the most volatile thing of all.
> - Hoa Phat (HPG) is the bellwether — roughly 37% of domestic construction steel and 25% of steel pipe — and its net profit swung from **35.3 trillion dong in 2021** to **8.4 trillion in 2022** to **6.8 trillion in 2023** and back to **12.0 trillion in 2024**. Same company, a 5x swing in four years.
> - P/E *misleads* at the extremes of a cyclical: it looks cheapest at the top (peak earnings) and most expensive at the bottom (trough earnings). Value a cyclical on mid-cycle EV/EBITDA, not on trough or peak P/E.
> - The number to remember: in 2021 the steel sector returned roughly **+75%** on VN-Index, and in 2022 it fell about **−52%**. Buy the trough, sell the euphoria, and never confuse a low headline P/E for a cheap cyclical.

In the first half of 2021, Hoa Phat — ticker **HPG**, the largest steelmaker in Vietnam and one of the heaviest single weights on the Ho Chi Minh exchange (HOSE) — did something that made it the most talked-about stock in the country. Adjusted for the dividends and bonus shares it paid out, the stock roughly tripled from its 2020 lows into its 2021 peak. Retail investors who had never owned an industrial stock in their lives were suddenly steel experts at the coffee table. The story was irresistible and, for once, true: the world was flooded with cheap money, China was building, construction at home was recovering, and the price of steel was going vertical. Hoa Phat was selling everything it could roll out of its giant new Dung Quat complex, and it was selling it at the fattest margins in its history. The company earned more in 2021 than in any year before it: **35.3 trillion dong** of net profit, about **\$1.39bn** at the mid-2026 rate.

Then the cycle did what cycles do. By the second half of 2022, the steel price had rolled over hard, the property market that consumes most of Vietnam's steel had seized up in a bond crisis, and the same company that printed 35 trillion dong in 2021 printed just **8.4 trillion** in 2022 — a profit collapse of more than three-quarters on a roughly *similar* tonnage. The stock did what the earnings did: it halved, and then some, peak to trough. Anybody who bought HPG in the final euphoric weeks of 2021, convinced that a great company at a "low P/E" was a bargain, spent the next year learning the single most important lesson in cyclical investing — that a steelmaker's headline P/E is *lowest* exactly when its earnings are about to fall off a cliff.

That round trip — triple, then halve — is not a Hoa Phat story. It is a *steel* story, and steel is the purest example of a category of stocks that behaves unlike anything else on the exchange: the **cyclical**. This post is about how to think in that category. We will build the steelmaker's profit model from zero — the spread between what it sells and what it buys, times volume — and show *why* that model makes earnings (and the stock) so violently cyclical. We will see why scale and self-sufficiency (Hoa Phat's blast-furnace cost advantage at Dung Quat) decide who survives the trough. We will work through why the P/E ratio actively *lies* at the cycle's extremes, and what to use instead (mid-cycle EV/EBITDA). And we will end where this series always ends: a concrete playbook for trading the sector off the steel price and the demand cycle on VN-Index — when to buy, when to sell, and what would prove the whole thesis wrong.

A quick word on money, because we will use it constantly. Vietnam trades in dong (VND), and the steel sector's primary numbers — profit, capacity, market caps — are all quoted in dong. As a reference point for readers who think in dollars, the exchange rate in mid-2026 is roughly **\$1 = 25,400 VND**. So 35.3 trillion dong of profit is about **\$1.39bn**; a company worth 250 trillion dong is about a **\$9.8bn** market cap; a foreign net buy of 500 billion dong is about **\$19.7mn**. We will keep the primary figures in dong (that is how the market quotes) and drop a dollar anchor whenever it helps you feel the size, especially because the *inputs* to steel — iron ore, coking coal, the global steel price itself — all trade in dollars on world markets, so dollars are genuinely the natural unit for the cost side of the business.

## Foundations: how a steelmaker actually makes money

Forget the stock for a moment and think about the business as if you ran it. You own a giant facility that takes two raw materials — **iron ore** (the rock that contains iron) and **coking coal** (a special high-carbon coal that you burn to both heat the furnace and chemically strip the oxygen off the iron ore) — and turns them, through enormous heat in a *blast furnace*, into molten iron and then steel. You roll that steel into the shapes your customers want: long products like construction **rebar** (the ribbed bars buried in every concrete column in Vietnam) and the flat product **HRC** (hot-rolled coil, the wide sheet that gets cut and bent into everything from pipes to car panels to appliances). You sell those products to construction companies, to fabricators, and into export markets.

Now here is the entire business model in one sentence, and it is worth tattooing on the inside of your eyelids if you want to understand cyclicals: **a steelmaker's gross profit per ton is the price it sells steel for, minus the cost of the raw materials and conversion that went into that ton — and its total profit is that per-ton spread multiplied by the number of tons it sells.**

That is it. Profit = (sell price − input cost) × volume. Everything else — the financing, the depreciation, the tax — is downstream of that one equation. Let us name the parts precisely, because the precision is where the insight lives.

The **sell price** is the price of steel. Crucially, a steelmaker is a **price-taker**, not a price-setter. Steel is a global commodity. A ton of rebar is a ton of rebar whether it comes from Hoa Phat, from a Chinese mill, or from a Korean one; buyers shop on price, and the world price is set by global supply and demand — overwhelmingly by China, which produces and consumes more than half the world's steel. Hoa Phat does not get to decide what steel costs any more than a farmer decides what rice costs. It wakes up each morning and discovers the price the world has set, and it sells at roughly that price minus a small local adjustment. This single fact — *price-taker* — is the root of everything cyclical about the sector. The company controls its costs and its volume; it does not control its price.

The **input cost** is mostly the two raw materials, iron ore and coking coal, plus the *conversion cost* of turning them into steel (energy, labor, maintenance, depreciation of the plant). Iron ore and coking coal are themselves globally traded commodities priced in dollars, so the cost side moves too — but, and this is the key, **it does not move in lockstep with the steel price.** Input costs are stickier. A mill buys ore and coal on contracts and holds weeks or months of inventory, so when the *selling* price of steel suddenly drops, the mill is often still working through expensive raw materials it bought earlier. The sell price can fall faster than the input cost, which squeezes the spread from both ends.

The **volume** is how many tons you actually sell, which depends on two things: how much you *can* make (your capacity — your furnaces, your rolling mills) and how much the market will *absorb* (demand). A steelmaker wants to run its furnaces flat-out, because a blast furnace is hugely expensive to build and hates being throttled — its costs are mostly *fixed*, so the more tons you spread them over, the lower your cost per ton. This is **operating leverage**, and we will come back to it because it is the second engine (after the price-taker spread) that makes steel earnings so violent.

It is worth opening up that input cost a little more, because the *composition* of the cost is what makes the spread move the way it does. Of the roughly \$560 it costs to make a ton of steel in our example, the lion's share is the two raw materials: iron ore (call it \$110-130 of contained-iron cost per ton of steel, since you need more than a ton of ore to make a ton of steel) and coking coal (call it \$150-250 per ton of steel, and notice how *large* and *volatile* that coal number is — coking coal is the single most violent input, and a coal spike alone can crush a mill's spread even when the steel price is steady). The rest is *conversion*: the energy to run the furnace, labor, maintenance, and the depreciation of the plant. The two raw materials are *globally traded commodities* that move on their own supply-demand cycles (Australian and Brazilian ore, Australian coal), so the cost side is not one number you control — it is two volatile world prices plus a fixed-ish conversion layer. That is why a steelmaker is squeezed not just when steel prices fall, but also when ore or coal prices *rise* faster than steel — a "cost-push" squeeze that thins the spread from the input side. The most painful configuration of all is a falling steel price *and* a rising coal price at the same time, which pincers the spread from both ends; the most blissful is a rising steel price *and* falling input costs, which blows the spread wide open.

#### Worked example: the spread, ton by ton

Make the equation concrete. Suppose Hoa Phat sells HRC at **\$700 per ton** (its sell price, set by the world). Suppose its all-in input cost per ton — iron ore plus coking coal plus the energy, labor, and depreciation to convert them — is **\$560 per ton**. The gross spread is \$700 − \$560 = **\$140 per ton**. Now multiply by volume: say it sells **8 million tons** in the year. Gross profit is \$140 × 8,000,000 = **\$1.12bn**. Convert to dong at \$1 = 25,400 VND: about **28.5 trillion dong** of gross profit. From that you still subtract interest, tax, and overhead to get net profit, but the *driver* — the thing that makes net profit huge or tiny — is that \$140 spread. The intuition: the steelmaker is a machine that mints the spread, one ton at a time, and the spread is the only number that really matters.

Hold that example, because we are about to break it — deliberately — to show you why the same machine can mint a fortune one year and almost nothing the next.

## Why the spread is so volatile: the price-taker squeeze

Here is the part that confuses people who are new to cyclicals. The steel *price* is volatile, sure — but it does not move by 80% in a year very often. The input cost is volatile too, but it is stickier. So how does net profit swing by 5x? The answer is that the **spread** — the *difference* between two volatile numbers — is far more volatile than either one alone, and **profit, which is the spread times volume, is the most volatile of all.**

Think about what happens when the steel price rises 20% while input costs rise only 5%, which is roughly what a tightening commodity cycle does. Start from our \$140 spread (\$700 sell, \$560 cost). The sell price rises 20% to \$840. The input cost rises 5% to \$588. The new spread is \$840 − \$588 = **\$252** — a jump of 80% in the spread from a 20% move in the price. A modest move in the sell price produced a violent move in the spread, because the cost barely budged and the spread is what is *left over* after you subtract a nearly fixed number from a moving one. This is the mathematical heart of why steel earnings explode in a price upcycle: the spread is the small difference between two big numbers, so a small move in the big number is a big move in the small one.

Now run it the other way, which is the part that hurts. The steel price falls 15% (from \$700 to \$595) while input costs, sticky and bought earlier, fall only 5% (from \$560 to \$532). The new spread is \$595 − \$532 = **\$63** — less than half the original \$140, from a 15% drop in the sell price. The spread *collapsed* on a moderate price decline, because the cost did not fall fast enough to protect it. And remember, this thinner spread is multiplied across millions of tons, so profit collapses with it.

![Line chart of China HRC steel price annual average in US dollars per tonne from 2019 to 2025 showing a 2021 spike then a long decline](/imgs/blogs/steel-basic-resources-sector-vietnam-the-cyclical-3.png)

The chart above is the real history of the steel price (using China's HRC price as the global proxy, because China sets the world price). Look at the shape: a steady ~\$500 in 2019-2020, an explosion to ~\$830 in 2021 as cheap money and post-pandemic stimulus collided with supply constraints, then a long grind back down through 2022, 2023, 2024 to roughly where it started. That single line — the price — drives the entire sector's profits, because the price drives the spread and the spread drives everything. When you understand that the steel sector's earnings are essentially a *leveraged bet on that one line*, you understand the sector.

#### Worked example: margin compression — this is 2021 versus 2022

Let us reconstruct the actual Hoa Phat cycle with the spread model, because it makes the 5x earnings swing feel inevitable rather than mysterious. In the fat-spread year (2021), the steel price was spiking toward \$830 while Hoa Phat was still partly working through cheaper raw materials and running its new low-cost Dung Quat furnaces — call the realized spread roughly **\$140 per ton** on the year (averaging the early fat months with the cooling later ones). On about 8 million tons, that is a gross spread pool that supports a 35 trillion dong net profit.

Now the squeezed-spread year (2022). The steel price has rolled over and the domestic property market has frozen, so realized prices fall — but the iron ore and coal Hoa Phat is feeding into its furnaces were contracted earlier, at higher prices, so input cost stays stubbornly high. The realized spread compresses to roughly **\$60 per ton**. On a *similar* ~7-8 million tons of volume, the spread pool is less than half of the 2021 pool — and because fixed costs (interest, depreciation on the giant new plant) do not shrink, what is left at the *net* line collapses by more than the spread did: from 35.3 trillion dong to 8.4 trillion. Same furnaces, same company, roughly the same tonnage — and a 76% profit collapse, driven almost entirely by the spread halving. The intuition: you did not need volume to fall or the company to do anything wrong; the spread compression alone, multiplied across millions of tons, did all the damage.

![Two column comparison of a wide spread year 2021 versus a squeezed spread year 2022 with per ton numbers showing the same volume earning very different margins](/imgs/blogs/steel-basic-resources-sector-vietnam-the-cyclical-6.png)

That figure is the whole post in one image. Same eight million tons on both sides. On the left (2021), a fat \$270-ish gross spread before conversion overhead, and profit explodes. On the right (2022), a thin \$60-ish spread after the price rolled over but inputs stayed sticky, and profit collapses. **Nothing about the company changed. The spread changed, and the spread is everything.** This is what "cyclical" means in its purest form: the earnings are a function of an external price the company does not control, and that price runs in cycles.

## The cost curve: why scale and self-sufficiency win the trough

If every steelmaker is a price-taker selling at the same world price, then the *only* thing that separates a great steel company from a doomed one is **cost**. The company with the lowest cost per ton makes money at a steel price where its higher-cost rivals are bleeding. This is why, in any commodity business, you obsess over the **cost curve** — the ranking of every producer from cheapest to most expensive. When the price falls, it falls down the cost curve, knocking out the high-cost producers first (they shut furnaces or go bankrupt), which eventually shrinks supply and lets the price recover. The low-cost producer at the bottom of the curve survives every trough and emerges to dominate the next upcycle.

Hoa Phat's entire strategy is to be at the bottom of that cost curve, and it gets there through two things: **scale** and **integration**. Scale comes from the **Dung Quat** complex in central Vietnam — a massive integrated steel facility built around large blast furnaces. A big blast furnace has a lower cost per ton than a small one (the fixed costs spread over more tons, and the thermal efficiency is better), so size alone pushes Hoa Phat down the curve. Integration means Hoa Phat makes steel from *raw materials* via the blast-furnace route (the "BF-BOF" route, blast furnace then basic oxygen furnace), rather than melting scrap steel in electric arc furnaces (the "EAF" route) the way many smaller mills do. The blast-furnace route is cheaper per ton at scale and is less exposed to the price of scrap metal, but it requires enormous up-front capital — which is exactly why it is a barrier that protects the incumbent. A new entrant cannot cheaply build a Dung Quat.

The trade-off, and you must hold both halves of it, is that this same scale and integration create **operating leverage and financial leverage** that amplify the cycle. Building Dung Quat cost a fortune and was financed partly with debt, so Hoa Phat carries large fixed costs: depreciation on the plant and interest on the debt. Those costs do not shrink when the steel price falls. So in a good year, the fat spread flows over those fixed costs and a torrent reaches the bottom line; in a bad year, the thin spread barely covers the fixed costs and almost nothing reaches the bottom line. The low-cost position is what lets Hoa Phat *survive* the trough that kills weaker mills — but the leverage is what makes its *earnings* (and its stock) swing so violently within the cycle. Low cost is a survival advantage, not a volatility cure.

#### Worked example: operating leverage doubles EBITDA on a 10% price move

This is the mechanism that makes the stock so geared, so it is worth doing carefully. Take a simplified Hoa Phat ton. Sell price **\$700**, variable input cost (ore, coal, energy that scales with output) **\$520**, so the variable spread is **\$180 per ton**. But the company also carries **\$120 per ton** of fixed cost (depreciation and interest, spread over its normal volume). So EBITDA-equivalent profit per ton, *after* fixed costs, is \$180 − \$120 = **\$60 per ton**.

Now raise the steel price by just 10%, to **\$770**. Variable cost barely moves (say it ticks to \$525). The variable spread jumps to \$770 − \$525 = **\$245**. Subtract the same \$120 of fixed cost and profit per ton is now \$245 − \$120 = **\$125 per ton** — more than double the \$60 you started with, from a 10% price move. The fixed cost acts as a fulcrum: because it does not rise with the price, every extra dollar of spread above it drops straight through. The intuition: operating leverage means a small move in the steel price produces a *large* move in profit, which is precisely why the stock can triple on a price rally that "only" doubled the headline steel price — and why it can halve just as fast on the way down.

So now you have the two engines of steel's cyclicality stacked on top of each other. Engine one: the spread is the small difference between two big volatile numbers, so it swings more than either. Engine two: operating leverage means fixed costs act as a fulcrum, so the spread's swings get *amplified* again before they reach net profit. Multiply those two amplifiers together and you get a 10-20% move in an input price turning into a 5x swing in annual earnings. That is not a bug in the business. That *is* the business.

There is a third amplifier that finishes the job: **financial leverage**. The debt Hoa Phat took on to build Dung Quat means the interest bill is a fixed claim that sits *below* the operating line, so the same logic that magnifies operating profit magnifies the *net* profit that reaches shareholders one more time — a fat operating year easily covers the interest and floods the bottom line, while a lean year has the interest eating a much larger share of a much smaller operating profit. Stack the price-taker spread, operating leverage, and financial leverage in series, and you have built a three-stage amplifier that turns a modest move in a global commodity price into the violent earnings swings — and the tripling-then-halving stock — that define the sector. When people call steel "high beta," this three-stage amplifier is the machinery they are describing, even if they have never named the parts.

## What drives demand: construction, public investment, and exports

We have talked a lot about the price, because the price drives the spread. But the price itself is set by supply and demand, so to anticipate the price — which is what trading the sector requires — you have to understand what drives *demand* for steel. In Vietnam, three demand engines matter, and they are the same engines that drive the whole mid-cycle, construction-heavy part of the economy.

**Engine one: private construction and property.** The single biggest consumer of Vietnamese steel is building — apartments, offices, factories, houses. Rebar goes into every concrete structure. So steel demand is tightly coupled to the property cycle: when developers are launching projects and breaking ground, they pour concrete and buy rebar; when the property market freezes (as it did in the 2022-2023 corporate-bond crisis, which strangled developer financing), construction stops and steel demand craters. This is why the steel sector and the property sector are joined at the hip on VN-Index — a steel investor *must* watch property, because property is where most of the rebar goes. When you read about presales (the cash developers collect from buyers before a building is finished) collapsing, you are reading about steel demand about to fall.

**Engine two: public investment** (*đầu tư công*). This is the government's own spending on infrastructure — highways, airports, bridges, the North-South expressway, metro lines. Public investment is enormously steel-intensive and, crucially, it is *counter-cyclical by policy*: when the private economy and property slow, the government often *accelerates* public investment to support growth, which props up steel demand exactly when private demand is weak. This is why "public investment acceleration" is one of the most powerful bullish narratives for Vietnamese steel and construction stocks, and why steel investors watch the state budget's disbursement rate (how fast the planned public-investment money is actually being spent) like a hawk. A government that announces a big infrastructure push is, indirectly, announcing demand for Hoa Phat's rebar.

**Engine three: exports.** Hoa Phat does not only sell at home; it exports HRC and other products into regional and global markets. Exports matter for two reasons. First, they let the company run its furnaces flat-out (maximizing that operating-leverage benefit) even when domestic demand is soft, by finding buyers abroad. Second, they tie Hoa Phat's fortunes to the *global* steel cycle and to trade policy — anti-dumping duties, tariffs, and the health of Chinese steel exports (which compete with Vietnamese steel in third markets) all swing the export leg. When China is flooding the world with cheap steel, it depresses prices everywhere and squeezes Hoa Phat's export margins; when trade barriers go up against Chinese steel, it can actually *help* Vietnamese exporters by opening space.

Put the three together and you have the demand side of the price. The most bullish configuration for steel is private construction recovering *and* public investment accelerating *and* exports finding open markets, all at once — that is a demand boom that lifts the price, fattens the spread, and (through operating leverage) explodes the profit. The most bearish is property frozen *and* public investment stalled in red tape *and* China dumping cheap steel into export markets — demand collapses, the price falls, the spread is crushed, and profit evaporates. Most of the time you are somewhere in between, reading which way the three engines are tilting.

## The cycle: how steel manufactures its own booms and busts

Now we can assemble the full picture: the commodity cycle. Steel does not just *respond* to a cycle; through the behavior of producers, it *manufactures* its own cycle, and understanding that self-reinforcing loop is what lets you anticipate the turns instead of being surprised by them.

![Five stage flow of the steel commodity cycle from high price and fat margin to capacity building to oversupply to falling price to healing and restart](/imgs/blogs/steel-basic-resources-sector-vietnam-the-cyclical-7.png)

Walk the loop. **Stage one: high price, fat spread, record profit.** Demand is strong, the steel price is high, the spread is wide, and steelmakers are printing money and piling up cash. **Stage two: everyone invests.** Flush with cash and convinced the good times will last, producers around the world (especially in China) announce new capacity — new blast furnaces, new mills. This is the fatal flaw of commodity industries: the signal to build (high prices and high profits) arrives at the *top* of the cycle, and the new capacity is committed precisely when it is most dangerous. **Stage three: the new supply lands.** A blast furnace takes two to three years to build, so all that capacity committed at the top arrives *together*, a couple of years later — often just as demand is cooling. **Stage four: oversupply crushes the price.** Now the world has more steel than it needs, so the price falls, the spread thins, and profit collapses. **Stage five: the healing.** Low prices and losses force the high-cost producers (at the top of the cost curve) to shut furnaces or go bankrupt; capacity investment stops cold; supply gradually shrinks until it falls back below demand — at which point the price can rise again and the whole loop restarts.

The brutal beauty of this loop is that **the boom causes the bust.** The fat margins of the good years are exactly what fund the capacity that floods the market and ends the good years. This is why steel (and copper, and oil, and every commodity) is cyclical rather than secular-growth: success sows the seeds of failure, mechanically, every cycle. And it is why you should be *most* nervous about the sector when everything looks best — when margins are fattest, when everyone is announcing expansion, when the narrative is "structural demand growth." That is stage two, the top, the moment the seeds of the next bust are being planted.

For a trader, the loop is a clock. You want to *buy* in stage four-to-five (price at a trough, weak producers exiting, capacity stopping, demand starting to turn) and you want to *sell* in stage one-to-two (price at a peak, margins euphoric, everyone expanding). The hardest part, psychologically, is that this is the opposite of what feels safe: stage five looks terrifying (losses, bankruptcies, gloom) and stage one looks wonderful (records, optimism, expansion). The cyclical investor's entire edge is the discipline to act against that feeling — to buy the gloom and sell the euphoria — because the cycle, not the mood, sets the future earnings.

## Valuing a cyclical: why P/E lies and EV/EBITDA mid-cycle is the answer

This brings us to the single most expensive mistake in cyclical investing, and the reason so many retail investors buy steel at exactly the wrong time: **the P/E ratio actively lies about a cyclical at the extremes of its cycle.** (P/E is price-to-earnings — the share price divided by earnings per share. A *low* P/E normally suggests a stock is cheap relative to its profits; a *high* P/E suggests it is expensive. For a stable company, that intuition works fine. For a cyclical, it is dangerously backwards.)

Here is why. The P/E uses *current* (or trailing) earnings in the denominator. For a cyclical, current earnings are at their *highest* at the top of the cycle and their *lowest* at the bottom. So at the top — when the steel price is peaking, the spread is fattest, and profit is at a record — the denominator is huge, which makes the P/E look *low*, which makes the stock look *cheap* exactly when it is most dangerous. And at the bottom — when the steel price has crashed, the spread is thin, and profit has collapsed — the denominator is tiny, which makes the P/E look *high*, which makes the stock look *expensive* exactly when it is the best bargain. **For a cyclical, a low P/E is a sell signal and a high (or even negative) P/E is often a buy signal.** This inverts the entire normal reading, and getting it backwards is how people buy HPG at the 2021 top because it "looks cheap on a 6x P/E" and sell it at the 2023 bottom because it "looks expensive on a 30x P/E."

So if you cannot trust the P/E, what do you use? The standard answer for cyclicals is **EV/EBITDA valued on mid-cycle (normalized) earnings.** Let us unpack that. **EV** is *enterprise value* — the market value of the equity *plus* the net debt. We use EV rather than just the market cap because steel companies carry a lot of debt (remember Dung Quat was debt-financed), and we want to value the *whole business*, debt and equity together, not just the equity slice. **EBITDA** is earnings before interest, tax, depreciation, and amortization — roughly, the cash operating profit before the financing and accounting layers. EV/EBITDA, then, is "what is the whole business worth relative to its cash operating profit?" — a cleaner cyclical gauge than P/E because it is less distorted by the debt and depreciation that swamp a capital-heavy steelmaker's net line.

But the *real* trick is the second word: **mid-cycle**. You do not value a cyclical on its peak EBITDA (that flatters it) or its trough EBITDA (that punishes it). You estimate a **normalized, through-the-cycle average EBITDA** — what the company earns in an *average* year across the whole cycle, not a peak or trough year — and you value the business on *that*. This is the discipline that protects you from both traps: it stops you paying up at the top (because mid-cycle EBITDA is far below peak EBITDA, so the business looks expensive when the trailing P/E says cheap) and it stops you panicking at the bottom (because mid-cycle EBITDA is far above trough EBITDA, so the business looks reasonable when the trailing P/E says expensive).

#### Worked example: EV/EBITDA on mid-cycle versus peak

Make it concrete with round numbers. Suppose Hoa Phat's enterprise value (equity plus net debt) is **250 trillion dong**, about **\$9.8bn**. Now we need an EBITDA to value it against, and the whole lesson is *which* EBITDA.

Take a sensible **mid-cycle EBITDA of 35 trillion dong** (an average-year number, between the fat and lean years). EV/EBITDA = 250 / 35 = **about 7.1x**. That is a fair, even slightly cheap, multiple for a low-cost cyclical leader — a reasonable place to own it.

Now make the classic mistake. Suppose it is the *top* of the cycle and trailing EBITDA is a **peak 60 trillion dong**. If you value the *same* 250 trillion EV against peak EBITDA, you get 250 / 60 = **about 4.2x** — which screams "incredibly cheap!" and lures you in at the worst possible moment. But that 4.2x is an illusion: it is dividing by a peak number that the cycle will not sustain. As EBITDA mean-reverts toward 35 trillion, the *same stock at the same price* re-rates to 7.1x — and if the price falls (which it will, because earnings are falling), you lose money even though you "bought it cheap." Conversely, at the *bottom*, trailing EBITDA might be a depressed **15 trillion**, giving 250 / 15 = **about 16.7x**, which screams "expensive!" and scares you out — even though, valued on the 35 trillion mid-cycle number, the business is the same fair 7.1x it always was.

The intuition: a cyclical's *true* value is roughly constant across the cycle (it is the same furnaces, the same cost position, the same long-run earning power), but its *trailing multiple* oscillates wildly because the denominator oscillates. Anchor on mid-cycle EBITDA and you see through the oscillation; anchor on trailing P/E or trailing EBITDA and the oscillation tricks you into buying high and selling low.

A simple sanity rule falls out of this: **when a cyclical's headline P/E hits a multi-year low, treat it as a warning, not an invitation.** A 5x P/E on a steelmaker almost always means peak earnings that are about to fall, not a bargain. And when the P/E is sky-high or the company is making a small loss, that is often the *trough* — the moment when, if the demand cycle is about to turn, you want to be buying.

## "Basic resources" is more than steel: the wider sector

This series and most exchange classifications file Hoa Phat under **"basic resources"** (sometimes "basic materials"), and it is worth knowing that the bucket is broader than steel, because the same cyclical logic applies to everything in it — they are all price-taker commodity producers geared to a global price they do not control. On VN-Index, "basic resources" steel dominates, but the wider category includes mining and other raw-material producers.

The members worth knowing: alongside Hoa Phat in steel sit **Hoa Sen Group (HSG)** and **Nam Kim (NKG)**, which specialize in *coated and galvanized* steel sheet and pipe (more flat-product and export-oriented than Hoa Phat's broad mix). These are smaller and, importantly, *less integrated* — they often buy HRC (sometimes from Hoa Phat itself, sometimes imported) and process it, which makes their spread the difference between coated-product prices and HRC prices rather than between steel and raw ore. That makes them a *different* and often even more volatile bet: when HRC prices fall fast, a processor's input cost (HRC) falls with it, which can actually *help* their margin briefly, before falling end-product prices catch up. Beyond steel, the basic-resources bucket reaches into base-metal and mineral miners and other raw-material producers, all of which share the price-taker, cost-curve, capacity-cycle DNA we have built up. If you understand steel, you understand the structure of all of them.

The practical point for a sector investor is that within basic resources you can express different views. Want the purest, lowest-cost, most-integrated play on the Vietnamese steel and construction cycle? That is Hoa Phat. Want a higher-beta, more export- and processing-leveraged bet that moves even harder (in both directions)? That is HSG or NKG. Want exposure to a specific commodity rather than steel? That is the relevant miner. They rotate together when the whole commodity cycle turns, but they have different cost structures and different sensitivities, so the trade expression matters.

![Horizontal stacked bar chart showing Hoa Phat holds 37 percent of construction steel and 25 percent of steel pipe in the domestic market](/imgs/blogs/steel-basic-resources-sector-vietnam-the-cyclical-4.png)

The chart above is why Hoa Phat *is* the sector for most purposes: roughly **37%** of the domestic construction-steel market and about **25%** of the steel-pipe market. No other Vietnamese producer is close. That dominance is itself a product of the cost-curve logic — Hoa Phat's low-cost Dung Quat scale let it survive every trough and take share from weaker rivals who could not, so each cycle has tended to *consolidate* the market toward Hoa Phat. When you trade "Vietnamese steel," you are, to a first approximation, trading HPG and using the smaller names as higher-beta satellites.

## Common misconceptions

Cyclicals attract more wrong intuitions than almost any other kind of stock, precisely because the normal rules of thumb run backwards. Here are the three that cost the most money, each corrected with a number.

**Misconception 1: "A low P/E cyclical is cheap."** This is the big one, and we have already dismantled it, but it is worth restating because it is so seductive. In 2021, at the very top, HPG traded at a single-digit trailing P/E on its 35.3 trillion dong of peak earnings — it *looked* cheap. It was the opposite of cheap; it was at the top of the cycle with earnings about to fall 76%. A buyer who anchored on that low P/E bought the peak. **For a cyclical, the lowest P/E of the cycle marks the top, not a bargain.** The correction: value it on mid-cycle EV/EBITDA (we got ~7x on a 35 trillion mid-cycle number), and the "cheap" 2021 stock was actually fully or over-valued once you normalized the earnings.

**Misconception 2: "Steel is a growth stock — Vietnam is industrializing, so demand grows forever."** There is a kernel of truth here: Vietnam *is* industrializing, and long-run steel demand *does* grow with the economy. But that secular trend is *swamped* by the cycle in any window shorter than a decade. Hoa Phat's profit in 2023 (6.8 trillion dong) was *below* its 2020 profit (13.5 trillion dong) — three years of "industrialization" and the earnings went *down*, because the cycle overwhelmed the trend. **Treating a cyclical as a buy-and-hold growth stock means riding the full amplitude of every bust.** The correction: respect the trend for your *decade* view, but trade the *cycle* for your one-to-three-year view, because the cycle is bigger than the trend over that horizon.

**Misconception 3: "Demand alone sets the steel price, so if Vietnamese construction is strong, steel must go up."** Demand matters, but the steel price is set *globally*, dominated by China, and *supply* (the capacity cycle) matters just as much as demand. You can have strong domestic Vietnamese demand and a *falling* steel price at the same time, if China is dumping a flood of cheap steel into the world market and crushing the global price. In 2022-2024, Vietnamese public-investment demand was supposed to be supportive, yet the steel price ground lower because the global supply-demand balance (Chinese oversupply, weak Chinese property) was bearish. **The local demand story does not override the global price.** The correction: watch the *global* price and the Chinese supply situation, not just the domestic demand narrative — Hoa Phat is a price-taker on a world price, and the world is bigger than Vietnam.

**Misconception 4: "The trough is the dangerous time to buy, because the company might go bankrupt."** This is the fear that scares people out at exactly the bottom, and for *most* steel companies it is a legitimate fear — high-cost producers at the top of the cost curve really do go bankrupt in troughs, which is how the cycle heals. But it inverts the truth for the *low-cost leader*. Hoa Phat sits at the *bottom* of the cost curve, so it is the *last* mill to lose money, not the first; even at the brutal 2023 trough it stayed firmly profitable (6.8 trillion dong of net profit, about **\$268mn** — a bad year, not a loss). The trough is dangerous for the marginal producer and *safest* for the survivor, because the survivor is buying market share from the casualties. **The thing that makes a trough scary — bankruptcies and shut furnaces — is precisely the supply-side healing that sets up the survivor's next upcycle.** The correction: the trough is the *right* time to own the low-cost leader, and you can tell the low-cost leader from the rest because it is still making money when everyone else is bleeding.

## How it shows up on VN-Index

Now let us ground all of this in the actual behavior of the sector on the exchange, because the abstractions only matter if they help you read the tape.

![Bar chart of Hoa Phat net profit after tax by year from 2020 to 2024 in trillion dong showing a 2021 peak and a 2023 trough](/imgs/blogs/steel-basic-resources-sector-vietnam-the-cyclical-2.png)

Start with the earnings, because the earnings are the story. The bar chart shows Hoa Phat's net profit after tax: **13.5 trillion dong in 2020**, exploding to **35.3 trillion in 2021** (the peak, about **\$1.39bn**), collapsing to **8.4 trillion in 2022**, bottoming at **6.8 trillion in 2023** (about **\$268mn** — less than a fifth of the peak), and recovering to **12.0 trillion in 2024**. That shape — a sharp spike and a deep trough within four years — is the visual signature of a cyclical, and it is what no amount of "industrialization" smooths out. The stock, naturally, traced the same shape: roughly tripling into the 2021 peak and roughly halving into the 2022-2023 trough, before recovering with earnings in 2024.

![Dual axis chart with steel price as bars and Hoa Phat net profit as a line from 2020 to 2024 showing profit peaks when the steel price peaks](/imgs/blogs/steel-basic-resources-sector-vietnam-the-cyclical-5.png)

The dual-axis chart proves the causal link we built from first principles: the bars are the steel price and the line is Hoa Phat's profit, and they move *together*. The profit line peaks in 2021 exactly when the price bars peak, bottoms in 2023 as the price grinds down, and turns up in 2024 with the price. This is the spread model made visible across real years — profit is a leveraged echo of the steel price, because profit is the spread (driven by price) times volume, amplified by operating leverage. When you see the price chart, you have seen the profit chart, lagged and amplified.

Now the sector behavior on the index. In **2021**, the steel and basic-resources sector returned roughly **+75%** on VN-Index — among the best-performing groups in a roaring bull market, as the spread exploded and the operating leverage delivered the record earnings. In **2022**, the same sector fell about **−52%** — among the *worst*-performing groups, as the steel price rolled over, the property bond crisis froze the biggest domestic customer, and the spread collapsed. The biggest leader of one year became one of the biggest losers of the next, inside twelve months. By **2024**, with the steel price stabilizing and public investment supporting demand, the sector recovered alongside its recovering earnings. That violent up-down-up is the *normal* behavior of a cyclical sector, and it is exactly why steel rewards trading the cycle and punishes buy-and-hold-and-forget.

The 2022 episode deserves a closer look because it shows how the demand engines interact with the global price. Vietnam's 2022-2023 was dominated by the **corporate-bond crisis** (*khủng hoảng trái phiếu doanh nghiệp*) — a freeze in the corporate-bond market that strangled property developers' financing. Property developers are the largest domestic consumer of steel, so their financing freeze meant projects stalled, construction stopped, and domestic steel demand cratered — *at the same time* as the global steel price was falling on Chinese weakness. Both the local demand engine and the global price engine failed together, which is why 2022 was so brutal for steel: it was not one headwind but two, compounding. The lesson for a sector investor is that steel sits *downstream* of property and *downstream* of the global commodity cycle, so it can be hit from both directions at once — and you must watch both upstream engines, not just the steel price in isolation.

#### Worked example: sizing the 2022 drawdown in your account

Make the sector cyclicality personal, because that is what enforces discipline. Suppose at the 2021 euphoria you put **300 million dong** (about **\$11,800**) into HPG, anchored on its "cheap" single-digit P/E, right near the top. Through the 2022-2023 trough, a roughly 50%+ peak-to-trough decline in the stock takes that position to around **140-150 million dong** (about **\$5,800**) at the bottom — a paper loss of roughly **150 million dong**, about **\$5,900**, more than half your stake, sitting in a drawdown for the better part of two years. Now suppose instead you had the discipline to *buy* near the 2022-2023 trough — say **150 million dong** (about **\$5,900**) deployed into the gloom when the P/E looked "expensive" on collapsed earnings — and rode the 2024 recovery as earnings climbed from 6.8 back toward 12.0 trillion. The same cyclicality that destroyed the top-buyer rewarded the trough-buyer. The intuition: in a cyclical, *when* you buy matters more than *what* you buy — the same great company is a wealth-destroyer at the top and a wealth-builder at the bottom, and the cost of getting the timing backwards is a multi-year drawdown of more than half your stake.

## The playbook: how to trade the steel and basic-resources sector

Everything above exists to support this section. Here is how you actually position in the sector on VN-Index, with concrete signals, the trade, the sizing, and the invalidation.

**The core stance: buy at the price-trough and demand-turn; sell at peak-margin euphoria.** You are trading the cycle, not the company. The company (Hoa Phat) is excellent and will be excellent in a decade — but its *stock* will be much cheaper at some points in that decade than others, and your job is to own it when the cycle is turning up and to be light or out when the cycle is turning down. This is the opposite of how you treat a steady compounder, and you have to internalize that or the sector will whipsaw you.

**The signals to buy (the trough-and-turn).** Look for a *cluster* of these, not any one alone: (1) the **steel price** has been falling for a long time and is *flattening or turning up* off a multi-year low — the price is the master signal, because it drives the spread; (2) the **headline P/E looks high or the company is near break-even** on collapsed trough earnings — counterintuitively bullish, because it means earnings are at a low they can recover from; (3) **capacity is being cut** globally — Chinese mills shutting furnaces, weak producers exiting — which is the supply side healing; (4) a **demand catalyst is appearing** — public-investment disbursement accelerating (*đầu tư công* picking up), the property market thawing as developer financing reopens, or interest rates being cut (cheaper money revives construction). When the price is basing, earnings are depressed, supply is healing, and a demand catalyst is appearing, that is the stage-four-to-five window where you want to be accumulating.

**The signals to sell (the peak-and-euphoria).** Again, look for the cluster: (1) the **steel price** has run a long way up and is **extended**, with the move feeling parabolic; (2) the **headline P/E looks very low** on peak earnings — the classic "it looks so cheap" trap, which is your sell signal; (3) **everyone is announcing expansion** — Hoa Phat, the Chinese mills, everyone committing new capacity at the top, which guarantees the future oversupply; (4) the **narrative is loudest and most crowded** — "structural steel super-cycle," retail piling in, the stock is the talk of every coffee table (as it was in 2021). When the price is extended, the P/E looks fake-cheap, capacity is being added, and the narrative is euphoric, that is stage one-to-two, and you trim or exit into the strength.

#### Worked example: the round trip a top-buyer versus a trough-buyer actually earns

Put two investors side by side across the same 2021-2024 cycle with the same 200 million dong (about **\$7,900**) to deploy, and let the spread model do the rest. The **top-buyer** buys near the 2021 euphoria, anchored on the "cheap" single-digit P/E built on peak 35.3 trillion dong earnings. As earnings collapse 76% to the 2023 trough and the stock roughly halves, his 200 million dong becomes about **95-100 million dong** at the bottom — a loss of roughly **\$4,000**, more than half his stake, held in a drawdown for the better part of two years; even the 2024 recovery only claws part of it back, because his entry was so far above the trough. The **trough-buyer** waits, buys near the 2023 bottom when the headline P/E looked *expensive* on collapsed earnings (the contrarian buy signal), and rides earnings recovering from 6.8 toward 12.0 trillion dong in 2024. As profit nearly doubles off the trough and the stock re-rates with it, her 200 million dong grows toward **300+ million dong** — a gain of roughly **\$4,000** on the same name, over a shorter window. Same company, same cycle, opposite outcomes — separated entirely by *when* each one bought relative to the spread cycle. The intuition: in a cyclical, the entry point is not a detail, it *is* the trade; the spread cycle hands the top-buyer a multi-year loss and the trough-buyer a fast double, and the only thing they did differently was act with the mood versus against it.

**The position and sizing.** Express the core view through **Hoa Phat (HPG)** as the low-cost, most-integrated anchor — it survives the troughs and dominates the recoveries. If you want more torque on a confirmed upcycle, add a *satellite* in the higher-beta processors (**HSG** or **NKG**), which move harder in both directions, but size them smaller because their volatility cuts both ways. Because the sector is so volatile, **size it as a cyclical bet, not a core holding** — a position you are willing to see swing 30-50%, which means it should be a slice of the portfolio you can hold through that amplitude without being forced to sell at the bottom. Sizing discipline *is* the strategy here: the cycle will test you, and an oversized position forces you to capitulate at exactly the wrong moment.

**The invalidation — what proves you wrong.** A buy thesis at the trough is invalidated if the **steel price breaks to a new low** after you bought (the basing was false, the downcycle has further to run) *and* the **demand catalyst fails to materialize** (public investment stalls in red tape, property stays frozen, China keeps dumping). If both upstream engines — the global price and domestic demand — keep deteriorating after your entry, you were early and the cycle has not turned; respect that and cut, because catching a falling knife in a commodity downcycle can mean a much deeper drawdown. Conversely, a sell thesis at the top is invalidated if the steel price keeps rising on a genuine, *supply-constrained* shortage (not just demand euphoria) — but be skeptical, because "this time the super-cycle is real" is what people say at every top.

The deeper discipline tying it all together: **trade the spread and the cycle, not the company's quality.** Hoa Phat's quality is what lets you *hold* it through a trough without fearing bankruptcy — it is the survivor, the low-cost leader. But its quality does nothing to protect you from buying it at the top of the spread cycle. Watch the steel price (the spread's driver), watch the two demand engines upstream (property and public investment), value it on mid-cycle EV/EBITDA rather than trailing P/E, and act against the mood — buy the gloom of the trough, sell the euphoria of the peak. Do that, and the same violent cyclicality that destroys the crowd becomes the thing you harvest.
