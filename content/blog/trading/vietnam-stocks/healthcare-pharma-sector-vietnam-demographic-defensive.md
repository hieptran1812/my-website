The one number to remember: pharma carries roughly **half** the index's beta and pays roughly **2x** its dividend yield — you give up the bull's torrid upside and keep getting paid through the bust.

## The drawdown where the medicine stocks held

In the first half of 2022, VN-Index collapsed from a euphoric peak near 1,530 in April to a panicked low near 870 by mid-November — a fall of more than 40% in seven months. The trigger was a credit and corporate-bond crisis, but the lived experience for a retail investor was brutal in a very specific pattern: the stocks that had led the 2021 bull led the way down, and they fell the hardest. Brokers that had tripled gave back two-thirds. Steel halved. Bond-funded property developers fell 60–70% and some never came back.

Now look at what a Vietnamese drug maker did over the same window. DHG Pharmaceutical (DHG), the largest listed pharma name and majority-owned by Japan's Taisho, fell too — everything falls in a 40% index drawdown, because margin-financed retail and foreign funds sell what they *can*, not what they *want*. But DHG fell maybe 15–20% from its high, kept paying a cash dividend out of steady operating profit, and recovered while the brokers were still bleeding. Imexpharm (IMP), the company that had bet hardest on EU-GMP factory lines, did something even more interesting: across 2023–2024, as its newly certified plants began winning higher-tier hospital tenders, its revenue and profit kept grinding upward through a flat-to-weak market, and the stock quietly re-rated on the back of that ETC-channel growth — a fundamental catalyst that had nothing to do with where the index was. The medicine stocks won the year that mattered, and one of them won on a reason all its own.

![Pharma value engine flow diagram from branded generics through ETC hospital tenders and OTC pharmacies to steady margin and dividends with demographic demand underneath](/imgs/blogs/healthcare-pharma-sector-vietnam-demographic-defensive-1.png)

The figure above is the whole sector in one picture, and we will spend the next several thousand words unpacking every box. But hold the shape in your head. A pharma company *manufactures* branded generic drugs, *sells* them through two very different channels — ETC into hospitals and OTC across pharmacy counters — earns a *steady margin* on that volume, and pays much of it out as a *dividend*. Underneath all of it, a slow tectonic force pushes demand up year after year: an aging population that needs more medicine. That is the engine. The rest of this post is about which parts of it actually create value, which parts are a commodity trap, and how a Vietnamese investor should own the sector.

It is worth saying plainly what this post is *not* arguing, so the thesis stays honest. It is not arguing that pharma is where you make your biggest gains — over a roaring liquidity bull, drug stocks will badly lag the brokers, property and steel names that double and triple, and if maximum upside in a rising market is your only goal, you would deliberately underweight them. The argument is narrower and more useful: pharma is the *defensive growth* corner of VN-Index — a place that compounds slowly but reliably on a demographic tailwind, cushions you in a crash, pays you to wait, and occasionally hands you a real catalyst in the form of a GMP-driven re-rating or a foreign takeover bid. It is a specific tool for a specific job, and the rest of this post is about understanding the tool well enough to know exactly when to reach for it.

## Foundations: how a pharma company makes money

Before any trading view, you need to understand how a drug maker earns, because the *shape* of its earnings — and especially the *channel* it sells through — is what makes the sector both defensive and slow. We will build this from zero, defining every term on first use.

### The everyday analogy: the bakery with two very different customers

Picture a bakery that makes a few reliable, well-known loaves — not exotic experimental pastries, just dependable bread people buy every week. That is a generic drug maker. It is not inventing new molecules (that is the multi-billion-dollar, decade-long, mostly-American business of *original* pharmaceutical research). It is taking medicines whose patents have long expired — paracetamol, amoxicillin, common cardiovascular and diabetes drugs — and manufacturing them well, consistently, and under its own brand. These are **branded generics**: off-patent formulas sold under a company name people trust.

Now the bakery sells its bread to two completely different kinds of customer, and this is the single most important thing to understand about the whole sector. The first customer is a giant institutional canteen — a hospital — that buys in bulk through a formal purchasing contest and will keep buying the same supplier for years once chosen. The second customer is the walk-in retail crowd at the corner shop, who pick whichever loaf has the nicest packaging and the most advertising. Same bread, two utterly different sales games. In pharma those two games have names: **ETC** and **OTC**.

### The two channels: ETC versus OTC

This split is the spine of the sector, so define it carefully.

**ETC** stands for *ethical* drugs — historically, medicines dispensed on a doctor's prescription. In the Vietnamese market the term has come to mean the **hospital channel**: drugs a manufacturer sells *into* the public hospital system. Crucially, hospitals do not just phone a supplier; by law they buy through a **tender** (a public procurement auction, *đấu thầu* in Vietnamese). A drug maker submits a bid — a price and a dossier proving the drug's quality and the factory's certification — and competes for the right to supply a hospital or a whole province's hospital network for a contract period. Win the tender and you have a large, predictable, recurring order that is hard for a rival to dislodge mid-contract. The catch — and it is the whole game — is that the tender system **grades drugs into quality tiers**, and which tier you are allowed to bid in depends on your *factory certification*, not your marketing. We will get to that.

**OTC** stands for *over-the-counter* — the **pharmacy channel**. These are drugs a customer can buy at the corner pharmacy without a prescription, or that a pharmacist recommends across the counter. Here there is no tender and no certification gate to clear; here you win on **brand, distribution and marketing**. You need your product on the shelf of tens of thousands of pharmacies, you need the pharmacist (who heavily influences what the customer walks out with) to favour you, and you need enough advertising and trade promotion that the consumer asks for your name. It is a classic fast-moving-consumer-goods fight, with FMCG-style costs: a big sales force, big promotion budgets, thin and contested margins, and constant competition from cheaper rivals and imports.

Here is the asymmetry that drives everything. The OTC channel is *open* — anyone can compete — so it is *commoditised*: margins get competed down, and you spend heavily on marketing just to hold share. The ETC channel is *gated* — only certified factories can bid in the high tiers — so it is *protected*: once you are inside the gate, volume is large, the relationship is sticky for the contract's life, and you are competing against a much smaller set of qualified rivals. **The strategic prize in Vietnamese pharma is to move revenue from the open OTC fight into the gated ETC channel.** Almost every interesting story in the sector — Imexpharm's whole strategy, the re-rating of GMP upgraders, the logic of the foreign takeovers — is a version of that one move.

#### Worked example: the same drug, two channels, two economics

Take a single product — say a box of a common antibiotic — and follow it down both channels.

Sold **OTC**, the box leaves the factory, goes through a distributor, and reaches a pharmacy shelf where it competes against a dozen near-identical boxes. To get it onto that shelf and into the customer's bag, the company spends on a sales rep visiting the pharmacy, on trade discounts to the pharmacy, and on consumer advertising. Suppose the box sells for 30,000 dong (about \$1.18 at \$1 = 25,400 VND), of which the factory's true product cost is 12,000 dong; the gross spread is 18,000 dong, but selling-and-marketing cost eats roughly 10,000 dong of that, leaving an operating margin of maybe 8,000 dong — and that margin is *contested*, because next quarter a rival cuts price and you must respond. Worse, the customer has no loyalty: she buys whatever the pharmacist hands her.

Sold **ETC**, the *same* box is supplied to a hospital under a tender the company already won. There is no per-box marketing — the order is a bulk contract for, say, 200,000 boxes over the contract period. The tender price might be lower per box, say 26,000 dong, but the company spends almost nothing on selling each one, so on a true cost of 12,000 dong the operating margin can be *higher*, perhaps 10,000–11,000 dong per box, and it is *not* contested — the contract is locked, the hospital keeps reordering, and a rival cannot undercut you until the tender re-runs. The volume is large and the cash flow is predictable.

Same physical product; the channel changed the *quality* of the earnings — lower marketing drag, stickier volume, and a competitor set narrowed by certification rather than open to all comers.

### How a hospital tender actually works

Because the ETC channel is the whole prize, it pays to understand the *mechanics* of a tender rather than treating it as a black box. In Vietnam, public hospitals are funded substantially through social health insurance (*bảo hiểm y tế* — the national health-insurance scheme that pays for much of the population's hospital drugs), and they are legally required to procure their medicines through competitive bidding rather than buying ad hoc. A tender (*đấu thầu thuốc*, literally "drug bidding") is the formal auction that results.

Periodically — often annually, sometimes centralised at the provincial or even national level for high-volume drugs — the procurement authority publishes a list of the drugs it needs and the quantities, and invites manufacturers to bid. A manufacturer's bid has two parts. The first is the **technical dossier**: proof that its product is what it claims to be and, critically, *which GMP tier the manufacturing line holds*. The second is the **price**. The authority then evaluates bids *within the appropriate technical group* and generally awards to the qualifying low bidder, locking in a supply contract for the period.

The load-bearing detail — the one that creates the entire moat — is that drugs are sorted into **technical groups** (commonly numbered, with the lower-numbered groups being the higher quality). The top groups are explicitly reserved for drugs manufactured on **stringently certified lines** — EU-GMP, Japan-GMP, or equivalent — and, in many cases, drugs that also clear bioequivalence standards. A WHO-GMP-only drug is *administratively ineligible* to bid in those top groups; it can only compete down in the crowded lower groups where price is the only differentiator and margins are thin. So when a manufacturer upgrades a line to EU-GMP, it is not buying a marketing badge — it is buying *legal eligibility* to compete for a class of orders that were previously off-limits, against a much smaller field of similarly certified rivals. That is why "GMP tier" is the first number a serious analyst checks on a Vietnamese pharma name, ahead of revenue, margin, or multiple.

There is a second-order benefit too: tenders are **sticky across renewals**. A manufacturer that wins a hospital's business, supplies reliably, and clears the quality audits builds a track record that makes it the incumbent — and incumbency, plus the switching friction of re-qualifying a new supplier, means won tenders tend to *stay* won. The ETC channel is not just higher-margin; it is *lower-churn*, which is why ETC-heavy revenue deserves a higher multiple than OTC revenue of the same size.

### Branded generics, not biotech: the slow, steady nature of the business

It is worth pausing on what these companies are *not*, because mis-modelling this is the single most common mistake newcomers make. A Vietnamese pharma company is **not** a biotech. It does not spend hundreds of millions on a decade-long gamble to discover a new molecule that might be worth tens of billions or might be worth zero. It makes known, off-patent drugs. That means three things for the investor.

First, **growth is slow and steady, not explosive.** Revenue grows roughly with the market — a few percent of volume growth plus mix improvement — not in the way a tech platform or a successful broker grows. A great year for a pharma name is high-single-digit to low-teens revenue growth; a 50% revenue jump would be extraordinary and usually means an acquisition.

Second, **earnings are predictable and cash-generative.** People take their blood-pressure pills in a recession. Demand for essential medicines barely flexes with the economy — that *inelasticity* is exactly what makes the sector defensive (low beta), a property we will quantify shortly.

Third, **capital goes into factories, not labs.** The big capital decision a Vietnamese pharma company makes is not "which molecule to research" but "which factory line to upgrade to which GMP standard." That single decision — covered next — is where almost all of the sector's value creation and re-rating potential lives.

### The GMP-upgrade moat: a certification you can bid behind

**GMP** stands for *Good Manufacturing Practice* — a set of factory standards (cleanliness, process control, documentation, quality assurance) that a drug plant must meet to be allowed to make medicine. The crucial point for an investor is that GMP is **tiered**, and Vietnam's hospital-tender system maps those tiers directly onto which drugs you are allowed to bid to supply.

At the base is **WHO-GMP**, the World Health Organization standard. Nearly every legitimate Vietnamese plant holds it; it lets you make and sell drugs domestically, including into the *lower* tender groups. But WHO-GMP is table stakes — everyone has it, so a WHO-GMP line competes in the crowded, lower-priced tiers.

Above it sit the **stringent** standards: **EU-GMP** (the European Union standard) and **Japan-GMP** / PIC-S-equivalent certifications. These are far harder and far more expensive to achieve — they require building or rebuilding a factory line to European or Japanese specifications, passing audits by foreign inspectors, and maintaining that standard continuously. The payoff is regulatory and direct: under Vietnam's drug-procurement rules, hospital tenders are split into **technical groups (the "tender groups")**, and the *highest* groups — the ones with the best prices and the least competition — are **reserved for drugs made on EU-GMP / Japan-GMP-certified lines**. A drug made on a mere WHO-GMP line is *legally barred* from bidding in those top groups.

Read that again, because it is the whole moat. Spend the capital to get a line certified to EU-GMP, and you unlock a tier of high-priced, low-competition hospital-tender revenue that your WHO-GMP-only competitors **physically cannot bid for, no matter how good their marketing or how low their price.** The certification is the gate, and you bought the key. This is a *structural* advantage, not a marketing one — it does not erode when a rival runs a promotion, and it lasts as long as you keep the certification current.

#### Worked example: the GMP-upgrade payoff

A company is weighing whether to build a new EU-GMP injectable line. The cost is about 500 billion dong (500,000,000,000 VND, roughly \$19.7mn at \$1 = 25,400 VND) for the plant and the certification programme.

Before the upgrade, its products can only bid in the lower tender groups, where the price per unit is competed down and the company captures, say, an incremental 200 billion dong of low-margin OTC-and-low-tier revenue from those products. After certification, the *same* products can be enrolled in the top tender groups. Suppose this unlocks bidding for high-tier hospital orders worth 400 billion dong a year of *ETC* revenue at, say, a 25% operating margin — that is 100 billion dong of operating profit a year that simply did not exist before, on volume a WHO-GMP rival cannot touch.

On a \$19.7mn (500 billion dong) investment, generating on the order of 100 billion dong (about \$3.9mn) of new annual operating profit is a payback of roughly five years and an ongoing high-teens-to-20%-plus return on the capital deployed — *and* it is durable, because the gate stays shut behind you. That is the moat ROI: you are not buying growth, you are buying a *certified position* in a channel competitors are locked out of, and the market re-rates you for the stickiness of that position.

### The demographic and health-spend tailwind

Now the slow tectonic force underneath the whole sector: demand grows, structurally, for decades. Two drivers stack on top of each other.

The first is **aging**. Vietnam's population is getting older — the share of people over 60 is rising steadily as life expectancy climbs and birth rates fall. Older people consume *far* more medicine per head: chronic conditions (diabetes, hypertension, cardiovascular disease) require daily, lifelong drug regimes. As the population ages, drug consumption per capita rises mechanically, independent of the economic cycle.

The second is **rising health spend per capita**. As incomes grow, a richer Vietnam spends a larger absolute amount on healthcare — more doctor visits, more diagnoses, more prescriptions filled, more willingness to pay for branded and higher-quality drugs rather than going without. This is the same income effect that drives the rest of the consumer story, applied to medicine.

Stack the two and you get a domestic pharmaceutical market that has compounded at roughly **7–8% a year**, from about \$6bn in 2019 to about \$8.5bn in 2025, with forecasters expecting that pace to continue well into the 2030s. Critically, this growth is *not cyclical* — it does not surge in a bull and collapse in a bust the way property or steel demand does. It is a slow, reliable escalator that lifts the whole sector a few percent every single year.

#### Worked example: riding the demographic escalator

Suppose the domestic drug market sits at about \$8bn and compounds at 7% a year. A leading manufacturer that simply *holds* its market share — does nothing clever, just keeps its slice of the pie — grows its revenue at that same 7%.

Run it forward: \$8bn growing at 7% reaches roughly \$8.6bn after one year, about \$11.2bn after five years, and roughly \$15.7bn after ten years — the market nearly doubles in a decade purely from the escalator. A manufacturer holding share rides that doubling without needing a single brilliant strategic move. Now layer on the GMP play: a company that not only holds share but also *shifts* its mix from low-margin OTC into high-margin EU-GMP ETC grows its *profit* faster than its revenue, because each dong of revenue migrates to a higher margin. Demographics give you the steady 7% top-line escalator for free; the GMP upgrade is the lever that turns that into faster bottom-line compounding. That combination — a reliable tailwind plus a self-help margin lever — is the entire bull case for a well-run Vietnamese pharma name.

### The cost side: imported APIs and the gross-margin lever

One more piece of the business model matters for understanding margins, and it is the cost side. A generic drug is a *formulation*: the manufacturer takes an **active pharmaceutical ingredient (API)** — the actual molecule that does the work, like the paracetamol or the amoxicillin itself — and combines it with binders, coatings and fillers into a finished tablet, capsule or vial. Vietnam makes very few of its own APIs; the great majority are **imported**, overwhelmingly from China and India, the world's API factories.

This has two consequences an investor should hold in mind. First, **input cost is partly out of the company's control and partly priced in foreign currency**, so a spike in global API prices or a weaker dong squeezes gross margin — a genuine, if usually modest, cost risk. Second, and more importantly, it reframes where the value-add is. A Vietnamese pharma company's edge is *not* cheap chemistry — the molecule is a globally traded commodity anyone can buy. The edge is *formulation quality, brand, distribution, and above all certification*. That is precisely why the GMP/ETC strategy is the value driver: when the raw input is a commodity that every competitor can import at a similar price, the only durable way to earn a fat, protected margin is to manufacture on a certified line and sell into a channel competitors cannot reach. The cost side, in other words, *reinforces* the thesis — it explains why the moat has to come from certification and channel, not from the chemistry.

#### Worked example: the API-cost squeeze on gross margin

Take a finished drug that sells for 30,000 dong (about \$1.18) a box, where the imported API accounts for, say, 9,000 dong of cost and the rest of the manufacturing and overhead adds another 6,000 dong, for a gross margin of 15,000 dong (50%).

Now suppose global API prices jump 20% and the dong weakens, pushing the imported-input cost from 9,000 to about 11,000 dong a box. If the company cannot raise its price — and in the *tender* channel, the price was locked at the bid — the gross margin compresses from 15,000 to 13,000 dong, a drop from 50% to about 43%. That is a real, if bounded, earnings risk you carry by owning the sector. The company's defences are scale (buying API in bulk), a higher-value mix (the EU-GMP ETC drugs carry enough margin to absorb input swings), and pricing power on the OTC brands. The lesson: a pharma name with a low-value, import-heavy, OTC-commodity mix is *most* exposed to this squeeze, while a high-GMP, high-ETC-margin name has the cushion to ride it out — yet another reason the GMP tier is the number that matters.

### Foreign strategic ownership: the takeover theme

A defining feature of Vietnamese pharma — and a recurring source of upside — is that **foreign strategic buyers want in**. Japanese and Korean drug majors face stagnant, aging home markets and see Vietnam as a fast-growing, under-penetrated one with a credible manufacturing base. So they buy controlling or large stakes in the listed leaders.

The two canonical deals frame the theme. Japan's **Taisho Pharmaceutical** built a majority stake in **DHG Pharmaceutical (DHG)**, Vietnam's largest listed drug maker, turning DHG into a Japanese-controlled, dividend-rich, well-governed franchise. Korea's **SK Group** took a large strategic stake in **Imexpharm (IMP)**, the company most aggressively pursuing the EU-GMP / high-tier-ETC strategy — a foreign partner explicitly backing the GMP-upgrade thesis with capital. Other names (Domesco/DMC with Abbott's involvement historically, and ongoing interest in Traphaco/TRA and Bidiphar/DBD) round out a sector where foreign ownership is the norm at the top, not the exception.

Why does this matter for *your* return? Three reasons. First, a strategic buyer pays a **control premium** — well above the screen price — to get a majority, so a stock that becomes a takeover target can re-rate sharply on the deal. Second, foreign ownership usually *raises governance and capital discipline*: Japanese and Korean parents tend to enforce clean accounting, generous and reliable dividends, and disciplined capex — which is exactly what you want in a defensive holding. Third, the *scarcity* of free float (once a foreigner owns 51%+) and the constant possibility of further buying or a full takeover put a floor under valuation and a call option on top of it. The flip side, which we will not gloss over: once a foreign strategic owns the majority, **liquidity dries up** and the free float shrinks, so these are not stocks you trade in size — they are stocks you *hold*.

### Valuation: P/E, dividend yield, and the takeover premium

How should you value a defensive, slow-growing, cash-generative pharma name? Not on dreams of explosive growth — there are none — but on **earnings, dividends, and the embedded takeover option**.

The first lens is **P/E** (price-to-earnings — the share price divided by earnings per share, i.e. how many years of current profit you are paying for). Because growth is modest and steady, pharma names typically trade at *middling* P/E multiples — usually richer than a deep cyclical like steel (which the market refuses to pay up for because next year's profit is a coin-flip) but cheaper than a high-growth retailer. A reasonable mental anchor is a P/E somewhere in the low-to-mid teens for a healthy, GMP-upgrading leader, with the multiple drifting *up* if the ETC mix improves and earnings quality rises. (For the full sector-by-sector multiple toolkit, see the valuation post linked at the end.)

The second lens — and arguably the more important one for a defensive holding — is **dividend yield** (the annual cash dividend per share divided by the share price). A well-run pharma name pays a large, reliable fraction of its profit out as cash, because it does not need to hoard capital for risky research. A dividend yield around 4% on a stock with a beta near 0.6, in a market whose index yields under 2%, is the core of the *defensive income* case.

The third lens is the **takeover premium** — the part of the price that reflects the possibility a foreign strategic buys the rest of the company. This is real but unquantifiable; treat it as a call option you get for free on top of the dividend, not as the reason to own the stock.

Putting the three lenses together gives you a practical valuation discipline. You *anchor* on the dividend yield (the cash you are guaranteed to receive), you *sanity-check* on the P/E (are you overpaying for the modest growth?), and you treat the takeover premium as upside you do not pay up for. A pharma name yielding 4% at a low-teens P/E with an improving ETC mix is the sweet spot: you are paid a fat, safe dividend, you are not overpaying for the earnings, and the GMP re-rating plus the takeover option are both free. Contrast that with a name yielding 2% at a high-teens P/E because the market has already priced in a takeover that may not come — there you are paying *for* the option, which is the wrong way round. The discipline is simple: buy pharma for the dividend and the moat, and let the catalyst be a bonus you did not pay for.

#### Worked example: the dividend defensive

Take a pharma name trading at 60,000 dong a share (about \$2.36) that pays a cash dividend of 2,500 dong per share for the year.

The dividend yield is 2,500 ÷ 60,000 = 4.17% — call it about 4%. Compare that with VN-Index, whose blended dividend yield sits near 1.8%. So this single stock pays you **more than twice the index's yield** in hard cash, every year, regardless of where the market goes. Now add the second leg: this stock has a beta near 0.6, meaning that in a 40% index drawdown it would *tend* to fall around 24% rather than 40% — it loses materially less. Put the two together and you have the defensive bargain in one position: you are paid roughly \$0.10 per \$2.36 of price each year in dividends (that 4% yield), and you lose meaningfully less than the market when it crashes. You give up the bull's torrid upside; in exchange you get income while you wait and a shallower hole to climb out of when it breaks. That trade-off — less upside, more income, smaller drawdown — is the entire defensive case for the sector in one stock.

## Common misconceptions

A few myths trap newcomers in this sector. Each one is wrong, and each is wrong in a way you can attach a number to.

### Myth 1: "Pharma is high-growth biotech — buy it for explosive upside"

This is the costliest misread. Vietnamese pharma is a **branded-generics manufacturing business**, not drug discovery. There is no molecule lottery ticket, no patent cliff windfall, no 10x. Top-line growth tracks the market at roughly 7–8% a year, and a great operational year is high-single-digit to low-teens revenue growth. If you buy these names expecting a broker-like or tech-like run, you have bought the wrong sector for the wrong reason and you will be disappointed even when the company executes perfectly. The correct expectation is *slow compounding plus a dividend plus an occasional catalyst* — not explosive growth.

### Myth 2: "All drug makers are basically the same"

They are not, and the difference is precisely the *channel mix* and the *GMP tier*. A company that has invested in EU-GMP / Japan-GMP lines and shifted its revenue into the high-tier ETC channel is a *structurally* better business — stickier volume, protected margins, a competitor set narrowed by certification — than a peer of identical size stuck fighting in the open OTC market with WHO-GMP-only lines. Imexpharm's EU-GMP-led ETC growth versus a marketing-heavy OTC peer is the textbook contrast: same sector, same products, very different earnings quality. When you analyse a pharma name, the first questions are *how much of revenue is ETC versus OTC?* and *which GMP tiers are its key lines certified to?* — not the headline P/E.

### Myth 3: "Defensive means there is no catalyst — it just sits there"

Wrong on both the upside and the timing. Pharma absolutely has catalysts; they are just *different* from a cyclical's. A new EU-GMP line passing certification and winning its first high-tier tenders is a fundamental re-rating event that can lift earnings and the multiple at the same time, often in a *flat* market (as IMP showed in 2023–2024). A foreign takeover bid is a discrete, sometimes large re-rating. A dividend hike signals confidence and pulls in income buyers. "Defensive" describes the stock's *beta and drawdown behaviour*, not the *absence of news* — the catalysts are real, they just tend to be company-specific and regulatory rather than macro and cyclical.

### Myth 4: "The huge demographic tailwind means the stocks must outperform"

This is the same trap that catches utility investors. A *market* growing 7–8% a year does not mechanically make the *stocks* outperform, for two reasons. First, much of that growth accrues to the *whole* market — including imports, multinationals, and new entrants — not just to your listed name. Second, a company can grow revenue with the market yet see margins squeezed (if it stays stuck in the OTC fight) so profit *doesn't* keep pace. The demographic escalator is the *floor* under the sector, not a guarantee of stock outperformance. The outperformance comes from the *self-help* lever — the GMP upgrade and ETC shift — layered on top of the escalator. Buy the tailwind *and* the lever, not the tailwind alone.

### Myth 5: "Foreign ownership is pure upside — just buy the takeover targets"

Foreign strategic ownership is genuinely a positive — better governance, reliable dividends, a takeover option — but it is not *free*. The cost is **liquidity**. Once a Taisho or an SK owns 51%+ of a company, the free float (the shares actually available to trade) shrinks dramatically; daily volume thins out, and you can no longer build or exit a position in size without moving the price. That has three practical implications most newcomers miss. First, these are *accumulate-slowly* stocks — you buy them patiently with limit orders over weeks, not in one market order. Second, the thin float makes them *poor* vehicles for active trading; the bid-ask spread and the price impact eat any short-term edge. Third, the takeover "option" can take *years* to pay off, or never fully crystallise if the strategic is content with majority control and does not bid for the rest. Foreign ownership is a quality signal and a slow-burn upside option, not a quick arbitrage — treat the illiquidity as the real, ongoing cost of owning a high-quality defensive franchise.

## How it shows up on VN-Index

Translate all of this into how the sector actually *behaves* on the screen — the patterns you will see and the prints that drive them.

### The low-beta hold: pharma in the 2022 drawdown

The clearest signature is **shallow drawdowns**. In the 2022 bear (index −40%+ peak to trough), the high-beta book — brokers, bond-funded property, steel — was halved or worse, while pharma leaders like DHG fell a fraction of that and kept paying dividends throughout. This is the *defensive* behaviour the low beta predicts: when forced sellers liquidate, they dump the liquid, high-beta names first; the illiquid, foreign-owned, dividend-paying pharma names are sold *less* and bought back *sooner*. If you held a sleeve of pharma into that crash, your blended drawdown was meaningfully shallower and your account recovered faster — the whole point of owning the sleeve.

![Grouped bar chart of Vietnamese pharma leaders 2024 revenue and net profit in VND billion DHG IMP and DBD](/imgs/blogs/healthcare-pharma-sector-vietnam-demographic-defensive-2.png)

The chart above sizes the listed leaders. DHG is the largest — roughly 4,885 billion dong of 2024 revenue and 779 billion dong of net profit (about \$192mn and \$31mn respectively at \$1 = 25,400 VND) — followed by Imexpharm (IMP) at about 2,205 / 320 billion dong and Bidiphar (DBD) at roughly 1,800 / 270 billion dong. Notice two things. First, the *absolute* size is small: even the largest pharma name is a fraction of a big bank or a HPG, which is why the sector is a *small* weight in VN-Index and never leads it. Second, profit margins are healthy and similar across the leaders (net margins in the 12–16% range), which is exactly what you expect from a stable, branded-generics business — and DHG's profit being proportionally large reflects its Taisho-driven discipline and ETC mix.

### The GMP-upgrade re-rating: Imexpharm 2023–2024

The clearest *upside* signature is the **GMP-driven re-rating**. When a company brings new EU-GMP / Japan-GMP lines online and they start winning high-tier hospital tenders, ETC revenue steps up at a higher margin, earnings grow faster than the market, and — because the *quality* of the earnings has improved (stickier, more protected) — the market is willing to pay a higher multiple. Imexpharm across 2023–2024 is the case study: its EU-GMP-led ETC channel grew through a soft market, profit compounded, and the stock re-rated on a fundamental, company-specific catalyst that was independent of the index. This is the pattern to *hunt* for: a pharma name in the middle of a credible GMP-upgrade-and-ETC-shift programme, where the re-rating has further to run.

![Line chart of Vietnam approximate pharmaceutical market size in USD billion from 2019 to 2025 rising steadily](/imgs/blogs/healthcare-pharma-sector-vietnam-demographic-defensive-4.png)

The chart above is the tailwind that underwrites all of this: the domestic drug market climbing steadily from about \$6.0bn in 2019 to about \$8.5bn in 2025 — a smooth, non-cyclical ~7–8% annual rise. Note how *un-dramatic* the line is. It does not surge in the 2021 bull or collapse in the 2022 bear; it just goes up, year after year, because people get older and sicker on a schedule the business cycle cannot touch. That smoothness is the sector's defining feature — it is the demand-side reason the stocks are defensive.

### The M&A premium: Taisho/DHG and SK/Imexpharm

The third on-screen signature is the **M&A premium and the float squeeze**. Once a foreign strategic (Taisho in DHG, SK in IMP) takes a large or controlling stake, two things happen to the stock's behaviour. The *float shrinks* — fewer shares trade freely — so liquidity drops and the stock becomes harder to move in size, which deters short-term traders and dampens volatility (reinforcing the low beta). And a *premium* gets embedded: the price reflects both the strategic's clean-up governance and the standing possibility of further buying or a full takeover. You will see these names trade with low daily volume, a steady bid, and the occasional pop on deal news — the signature of a stock whose register is dominated by a long-term strategic holder rather than by momentum money.

![Horizontal bar chart comparing pharma beta and dividend yield versus VN-Index showing pharma lower beta and higher yield](/imgs/blogs/healthcare-pharma-sector-vietnam-demographic-defensive-6.png)

The chart above puts numbers on the defensiveness. Pharma's beta sits around 0.6 versus the index's 1.0 — it tends to move about 60% as much as the market, so it falls less in a crash and rises less in a boom. And its dividend yield sits around 4.0% versus the index's ~1.8% — more than double the cash income. That two-bar contrast is the sector's investment identity in one picture: *half the volatility, double the income.* When you reach for pharma, this is the trade you are making.

### Why pharma never leads the index — and why that is fine

A pattern worth internalising: pharma is *never* the sector that leads a VN-Index bull, and you should not wait for it to be. The reason is arithmetic. The sector's total market value is small — even DHG, the largest name, is a tiny fraction of a single large bank like Vietcombank or a Vingroup — so pharma's *weight* in the index is minor. A few low-beta names that, by construction, rise *less* than the market in a boom cannot, mathematically, lead a rally. When the liquidity bull is running, the leadership is in the highest-beta, highest-float corners — brokers, property, steel — precisely the opposite of pharma.

This is not a flaw; it is the *whole point*. You do not own pharma to lead the index up; you own it to lag the index *down*. The behaviour you want — and the behaviour you reliably get — is the inverse of leadership: a sector that quietly underperforms while everyone is euphoric, then quietly outperforms (by falling less and recovering sooner) when the euphoria breaks. An investor who measures pharma against the leaders in a bull and concludes "this sector is bad" has fundamentally misunderstood what they bought. The correct benchmark for a defensive sleeve is not the index's *upside* but its *drawdown* — and on that benchmark pharma earns its place.

### The regulatory two-sided coin

The single most important thing to understand about pharma's behaviour on the index is that it is a **regulated sector**, and regulation is a two-sided coin. On the upside, regulation *creates* the moat: the GMP-tiered tender system is a government-built barrier to entry that protects certified manufacturers' margins. No equivalent barrier exists in the OTC fight, which is exactly why ETC is the better business. On the downside, regulation can *take the moat away*: a change to the procurement rules, a tightening of drug-price ceilings, or a restructuring of the technical groups could compress the very margins the system currently protects. So the sector's earnings are unusually exposed to *policy* rather than the *cycle* — which is what makes them defensive (policy does not swing with GDP) but also introduces a distinctive risk (a stroke-of-the-pen change can re-price the whole sector). When you read the news flow on a pharma holding, the headlines that matter are not macro prints but procurement-policy and price-control announcements.

## The playbook: how to trade it

Everything above resolves into a position. Here is how a Vietnamese investor actually owns healthcare and pharma.

### The role in the portfolio: defense plus a catalyst

Pharma is a **core defensive sleeve with embedded upside options**, not a trading vehicle. Own it for three jobs at once: (1) *defense* — the low beta cushions a drawdown; (2) *income* — the ~4% dividend pays you to hold; and (3) *catalyst* — a GMP-upgrade re-rating or a foreign takeover provides the occasional jump. You are not buying it to time the cycle; you are buying it to *survive* the cycle while getting paid and to capture a company-specific re-rating when one comes.

Sized correctly, this is a *minority, permanent* sleeve. Because the whole sector is a small weight in VN-Index and the leaders are illiquid (foreign-owned, low float), you cannot — and should not — build a huge position fast. A modest, patient allocation accumulated on weakness, held for years, is the right shape. This is the opposite of how you trade the broker sector (the highest-beta, most-traded corner of the market, covered in the rotation posts linked below): pharma is the part of the book you *don't* touch much.

### When to lean in: the rotation and catalyst signals

Two distinct triggers tell you to *add* to the pharma sleeve.

The first is **late-cycle / defensive rotation**. When the liquidity-and-momentum bull is clearly mature — margin balances stretched, brokers and property have run hard, the macro is turning — you rotate *toward* defensives, and pharma (with utilities and other low-beta staples) is where you go. The signal set: deteriorating breadth, the high-beta leaders losing relative strength, and a defensive sector quietly making new relative highs while the index stalls. (The mechanics of reading which sector is leading are in the relative-strength and rotation posts linked at the end.)

The second is the **company-specific catalyst**, which you can buy *regardless* of the cycle. Concretely: a pharma name that has just (or is about to) bring new EU-GMP / Japan-GMP lines online and is starting to win higher-tier hospital tenders — the start of a GMP re-rating like IMP's. Or credible signs of foreign strategic accumulation (a Japanese or Korean major building a stake) that points to a takeover premium. These catalysts are *fundamental and regulatory*, so they reward research over timing: read the tender results, the GMP-certification announcements, and the foreign-ownership disclosures.

### The position and the sizing

Concretely, the playbook position is: a *basket* of two or three of the GMP-upgrading, dividend-paying leaders — anchored by a Taisho/SK-style foreign-owned franchise (DHG, IMP) for the governance-and-dividend floor, plus a credible self-help GMP upgrader (IMP or DBD) for the re-rating optionality — held as a permanent defensive sleeve and *added to on weakness*, not chased on strength. Because liquidity is thin, you accumulate patiently with limit orders, not market orders, and you accept that you will hold for years, collecting the dividend, waiting for the catalyst.

Why a *basket* rather than a single name? Because the two upside legs — the defensive dividend floor and the GMP/M&A catalyst — do not always live in the same stock. The most foreign-owned, highest-governance, fattest-dividend name (a DHG) gives you the cleanest *defense* but, with its takeover already largely consummated, less *catalyst* left to capture. The most aggressive GMP upgrader (an IMP, or a DBD building specialist oncology and dialysis ETC capacity) gives you the *catalyst* but may pay a thinner dividend while it reinvests in plant. Holding two or three across that spectrum gives you both legs and diversifies the single-name regulatory and tender risk — if one company loses a key tender or sees a margin shock, the sleeve as a whole still does its defensive job. The basket *is* the position; the individual name is a component of it.

A note on the *foreign-room* mechanic, because it bites in this sector specifically. Vietnamese listed companies carry a **foreign-ownership limit** (the "foreign room" — the maximum share of the company foreigners may collectively own), and in pharma that room is often *already full or near-full* because a strategic and other foreign funds have taken it up. The practical effect for a foreign investor: you frequently *cannot* buy the most attractive names on the open market at all, or only at a premium "negotiated" price, because there is no room left. For a domestic investor this is less of a problem, but it is the reason the highest-quality pharma franchises trade with a persistent scarcity premium — the foreigners who want them most are partly locked out, and the few shares that change hands clear at a level that reflects that scarcity.

#### Worked example: sizing the sleeve against the whole book

Suppose you run a Vietnamese equity book of the dong equivalent of \$100,000 and you want a defensive ballast that will still be standing after a crash. A sensible defensive allocation might be 15% of the book — \$15,000 — split between pharma and utilities, say \$8,000 to a two-name pharma basket and \$7,000 to utilities.

What does that \$8,000 pharma sleeve do for the *whole* book? On a 4% yield it throws off about \$320 a year in dividends, a steady cash drip into the account regardless of the market. In a 40% index crash, while your high-beta cyclical book (say the other \$70,000 in brokers, property and steel at a ~1.3 beta) falls roughly 52% — a \$36,400 loss — your \$8,000 pharma sleeve at 0.6 beta falls about 24%, a loss of roughly \$1,900, and keeps paying the dividend through the fall. The sleeve is small, so it does not *rescue* the book — nothing small can — but it does two things that matter: it bleeds far less than its share of the book would otherwise, and it leaves you with *dry powder* and income at the bottom, exactly when the cyclicals are cheapest and you most want cash to redeploy. That is the role of a defensive sleeve: not to win the bull, but to keep you solvent and liquid through the bust so you can buy the recovery.

#### Worked example: building the defensive income sleeve

Suppose you want a defensive sleeve that pays you and protects you. You allocate, say, the dong equivalent of \$10,000 across two pharma leaders averaging a 4% dividend yield and a 0.6 beta.

The income: a 4% yield on \$10,000 is about \$400 a year in cash dividends, versus roughly \$180 if the same money sat in an index-yield basket — you are paid \$220 a year *more* for owning the defensive sleeve. The protection: in a hypothetical 40% index crash, a 0.6-beta sleeve tends to fall about 24%, so your \$10,000 would notionally drop to about \$7,600 rather than the \$6,000 an index-beta position would hit — you preserve roughly \$1,600 of capital relative to the market, *and* you kept collecting that \$400 dividend through the fall. Then, if one of your two names is mid-GMP-re-rating or gets a takeover bid, you get an additional, uncorrelated upside leg on top. The sleeve does exactly the job you bought it for: pays you to wait, loses less when it breaks, and leaves a catalyst option open.

### The invalidation: when the thesis is wrong

A disciplined view names what would *break* it. The pharma-defensive thesis is invalidated, and you should cut or avoid, if:

- **The GMP/ETC story stalls or reverses.** If a company's EU-GMP lines fail to win the expected high-tier tenders, or it loses a major hospital tender to a rival, the ETC-growth re-rating thesis is dead and you are left holding a slow OTC business at a premium multiple. Watch the tender results.
- **A regulatory or price-control change compresses tender margins.** The ETC channel's profitability depends on the procurement rules. If the government tightens drug-price controls or restructures the tender groups in a way that squeezes manufacturer margins, the whole sector's earnings quality drops. This is the sector's biggest *structural* risk — its protection comes from regulation, so a regulatory change is the thing that can take it away.
- **The dividend is cut.** A pharma name's defensive case *is* its reliable dividend. A cut signals either a profit problem or a shift to capital-hungry expansion, and it removes the income leg that justified the low-growth holding.
- **The macro flips decisively risk-on and you want maximum upside.** This is not an invalidation of the *business* but of the *timing*: in the early, explosive phase of a liquidity bull, pharma will badly lag the high-beta leaders, and a return-maximising investor should be *underweight* it. Owning a defensive when you should own a cyclical is a real cost — just a different kind of one.
