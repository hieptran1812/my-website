> **TL;DR** -- A leading sector still contains both winners and losers, so the sector call is not the stock call. Run a six-filter funnel -- relative strength within the sector, then quality, liquidity, foreign room, valuation versus peers, and balance-sheet safety -- to narrow a hot industry down to the one or two names worth owning.
>

> - **Screen in order, and let each filter kill candidates.** Relative strength inside the sector finds the names the flow already favors; quality (ROE, margin, growth) finds the ones that earn it; liquidity asks whether you can actually get out; foreign room asks whether there is a buyer above you; valuation versus peers asks what you are paying for the quality; balance-sheet safety is the thing that decides who survives the next downturn.
> - **The two traps that kill returns inside a winning sector are the value trap** (the cheapest stock is cheap *because* its assets, ROE, or balance sheet are bad) **and the illiquidity trap** (a thin name you cannot exit in a selloff without crushing your own price). Both are avoidable with the funnel.
> - The one number to remember: a bank at **0.8x** book with **6%** ROE and rising bad loans is not a bargain next to one at **1.5x** book with **20%** ROE -- the cheap one is cheap for a reason. This is educational, not financial advice.

In the spring of 2024, two banks listed on the Ho Chi Minh exchange (HOSE) sat inside the same sector at the same time. Banking was leading VN-Index -- the headline market-cap-weighted benchmark of the stocks on HOSE -- and a dashboard scanning sector relative strength would have flashed financials green for weeks. So far, so good: the sector call was correct. Money was rotating into banks, and a reader who owned "banks" in any form was on the right side of the rotation.

But "banks" is not a stock you can buy. You buy *a* bank. And the two banks in this story, both members of the same leading sector, both swept up in the same wave of buying, did not behave the same way at all. One of them -- a high-return-on-equity, well-capitalized lender trading at a premium to book value -- ran roughly 55% over the leg. The other -- optically the *cheaper* of the two, trading below book, the one a naive value screen would have flagged as the better buy -- managed barely 10% over the same window, and gave most of that back when the sector cooled. Same sector. Same rotation. Same correct top-down call. Opposite outcomes for the investor, separated by a factor of five, decided entirely by *which name inside the sector you chose*.

That gap is the subject of this post. Getting the sector right is necessary, and the rest of this series is largely about how to do exactly that -- read relative strength, map the cycle, watch the flows, build a dashboard. But the sector call is the easy half. The hard half is what comes after the dashboard turns green: going from a hot industry to the specific one or two names worth owning, while avoiding the two ways the inside of a winning sector eats your returns. We will build a concrete, repeatable screening funnel for that -- six filters, run in order, each one allowed to kill candidates -- and we will be honest about the traps it exists to dodge.

![Screening funnel diagram from hot sector down to one or two stocks through six filters relative strength quality liquidity foreign room valuation balance sheet](/imgs/blogs/from-sector-to-stock-screening-leaders-in-a-hot-industry-1.png)

Look at the funnel in the figure. It starts wide -- a hot sector might hold twenty or thirty names -- and narrows through six filters to the one or two stocks at the bottom. Read it top to bottom: a stock has to *pass each stage to reach the next*. Relative strength within the sector finds the names the flow already favors. Quality asks whether they earn that favor with real return on equity and margins. Liquidity asks whether you can get out without crushing your own price. Foreign room asks whether there is a buyer waiting above you. Valuation asks what you are paying for the quality you found. Balance-sheet safety is the last filter, the one that decides who survives a downturn. By the bottom of the funnel, what is left is not "the sector" -- it is the leader of the sector, and a short list you can actually own with conviction. The entire post is a walk down this funnel, one filter at a time, with the two traps marked along the way.

## Foundations: why the sector call is not the stock call

Start with the single fact that makes this whole post necessary: **a sector is a basket, and the names inside a basket do not move as one.** When we say "banking is leading," we mean the *average* bank, weighted by size, is outperforming. But an average is built from a distribution, and the distribution of returns inside even the hottest sector is wide -- often shockingly wide. The leader and the laggard share an industry, a regulator, a macro tailwind, and a cycle phase, and they still end up at opposite ends of the scoreboard.

Why? Because a sector index aggregates companies that share a common *economic driver* -- banks all earn on the spread between loan and deposit rates (net interest margin, or NIM) and on credit growth; brokers all earn on trading turnover and margin lending; property developers all live and die on land banks and presales. That shared driver is what makes a sector move together *on average*. But each company sits on top of that shared driver with its own balance sheet, its own management, its own asset quality, its own franchise, and its own starting valuation. The shared driver lifts everyone; the company-specific factors decide *by how much*, and sometimes *whether at all*. A rising tide lifts all boats, the saying goes -- but some boats have holes in them, and a rising tide just means they sink more slowly.

Here is the everyday analogy before any of the screening machinery. A hot sector is like a school that just got famous for sending students to the best universities. The school's reputation -- the sector tailwind -- is real, and on average its graduates do better than the average student elsewhere. But "the school" is not a person you can be; you have to pick a *particular student* to bet on. And the students inside that famous school are not identical: some are genuinely brilliant and hard-working (the high-ROE leaders), some are coasting on the school's name (the low-quality laggards floating on the tide), some are at the school on a scholarship that runs out next year (the fragile balance sheets), and some are so popular that you cannot get near them (full foreign room, a queue of buyers). The reputation gets you into the right school; it does not tell you which student to back. The screening funnel is how you tell the brilliant student from the coaster *inside* the famous school -- and the value trap is betting on the student who is *cheap to back* precisely because everyone else has already noticed they are coasting.

The dispersion has a second, less obvious cause that matters for screening: **the flow itself concentrates.** When money rotates into a sector, it does not spread evenly across every name. Institutional buyers, foreign funds, and index trackers concentrate into the *liquid, large, high-quality* names they can actually deploy size in -- which pushes those names up faster and *widens* the gap to the small, thin, low-quality names that the big money cannot or will not touch. So a strong sector is not a gentle, uniform lift; it is a concentration event, and the funnel is your map of where the concentration is going. This is also why the dispersion tends to be *widest* exactly when the sector is hottest: peak flow means peak concentration into the leaders, which is the moment the gap between owning the leader and owning the laggard is largest.

![Bar chart of illustrative same-sector stock returns over one leg showing leader plus sixty percent down to laggard plus five percent](/imgs/blogs/from-sector-to-stock-screening-leaders-in-a-hot-industry-2.png)

The chart makes the dispersion concrete. These are illustrative returns for five stocks *inside one leading sector* over a single leg up. The leader does +60%. The next name does +45%, then +30%, then +18%, and the laggard limps in at +5%. The sector index -- a size-weighted average of these -- might print something like +30%, which is the number a dashboard shows you. But almost nobody actually earns the index return: you earn the return of *the names you picked*. If you bought the leader you are thrilled; if you bought the laggard you are wondering why you bothered, even though you were *right about the sector the whole time*. The point of the screening funnel is to push your pick toward the left end of this distribution and away from the right.

This dispersion is not a flaw in the market; it is the *reason* stock-picking can add value at all. If every stock in a hot sector returned the same amount, you would just buy the cheapest exchange-traded fund (ETF) tracking the sector and be done. The dispersion is the opportunity -- and it is also the danger, because the same width that lets a good pick beat the sector lets a bad pick lag it by just as much. The funnel exists to make that width work *for* you instead of against you.

### The screening funnel, stage by stage

Here is the funnel in words, in the order you run it. The order matters: each filter is cheap to apply relative to the next, and each one removes candidates so that you spend your scarce attention only on names that have survived. Think of it like airport security with successive checkpoints -- a name that fails an early filter never reaches the expensive, careful examination at the end.

**Filter 1 -- Relative strength within the sector.** Most of this series talks about a sector's relative strength *versus VN-Index*. Inside the sector, you do the same thing one level down: rank the individual stocks by their performance *relative to the sector index itself*. The names whose price is rising *faster than the sector basket* are the ones the flow is concentrating into. This is your first, cheapest cut: it is pure price, it needs no fundamentals, and it tends to surface the genuine leaders before the news explains why. A stock outperforming a sector that is itself outperforming the market is a double-positive on flow -- that is exactly where leadership lives.

**Filter 2 -- Fundamental quality (ROE, margin, growth).** Relative strength tells you where the money is going; quality tells you whether it *should* be. Return on equity (ROE) -- net profit divided by shareholders' equity -- is the single most important quality number for a Vietnamese financial or industrial name: it measures how much profit the company squeezes out of each dong of capital its owners have left in the business. A bank earning 20% ROE is compounding shareholder capital at 20% a year *before* you pay anything for it; a bank earning 6% is barely beating the cost of that capital. Add operating margin (how much of each dong of revenue survives to profit) and a multi-year earnings growth rate, and you have a quality profile. Filter 2 keeps the names that *earn* their leadership and drops the ones that are merely floating up on the sector tide.

**Filter 3 -- Liquidity (can you actually get out).** This is the filter beginners skip and professionals never do. Liquidity is the average daily trading value of the stock -- how much money changes hands in it per day, usually quoted in dong (billion dong per day, *tỷ đồng*). It matters for one brutal reason: **a position you cannot exit is not an investment, it is a hostage situation.** In a selloff, the names that hold up are the liquid ones, where there is always a bid; the thin names gap down with no buyers, and your sell order *becomes* the down-move. Filter 3 asks a single question -- if I needed to sell my whole position in two days, could I, without moving the price against myself? -- and removes every name where the answer is no.

**Filter 4 -- Foreign room (is there a buyer above you).** Vietnam caps foreign ownership in most listed companies (commonly at 49%, lower for banks at 30%). The remaining headroom is called *room ngoại*, or **foreign room** -- the amount of a company foreigners are still allowed to buy. This filter is uniquely Vietnamese and uniquely powerful. When a quality stock is at *full* foreign room (foreigners own the maximum allowed and more want in), there is a wall of frustrated demand sitting *above* the current price -- buyers who will pay up the moment any room opens, often at a premium. That is structural support. When a stock has *open* room and foreigners are *selling* into it, the demand-above-you is gone and you are the one absorbing the supply. Filter 4 favors the names where the foreign bid is a buyer waiting, not a seller leaving.

**Filter 5 -- Valuation versus sector peers.** Only now, after you have a liquid, quality, flow-favored, foreign-supported name, do you ask what it costs. And you ask it *relative to its sector peers*, never in the abstract. The two workhorse multiples are price-to-earnings (P/E -- price divided by annual profit per share) and price-to-book (P/B -- price divided by net asset value per share); for banks P/B is king, for growth names P/E. The goal here is *not* to find the cheapest stock -- that is the value trap, which we will dismantle below. The goal is to find a *fair* price for *demonstrated* quality: a high-ROE name at a reasonable multiple, where you are paying up modestly for something genuinely better, not paying a bargain price for something genuinely broken.

**Filter 6 -- Balance-sheet safety.** The last filter is the one that decides who is still standing after the cycle turns. For a bank it is capital adequacy and the non-performing-loan (NPL) ratio -- the share of loans that have gone bad. For a developer it is the debt load and the maturity wall of corporate bonds (*trái phiếu doanh nghiệp*) coming due. For an industrial it is net debt versus earnings. The principle is universal: **leverage amplifies the good times and kills you in the bad ones.** A name that screens beautifully on the first five filters but carries a fragile balance sheet is a name that wins in the bull and disappears in the bear. Filter 6 removes the fragile survivors so that what passes the funnel can be held through a downturn, not just rented during the rally.

Why this *exact* order, and not some other? Because the funnel is sequenced by two principles at once: **cost of analysis** and **what kills you fastest.** The early filters (relative strength, then quality) are cheap -- relative strength is pure price, available instantly for free, and quality is a handful of ratios off a financial statement -- so they go first and do the bulk of the culling, shrinking a thirty-name sector to a handful before you spend real effort. The middle filters (liquidity, foreign room) are structural facts about the *tradability* of what remains -- there is no point doing deep valuation work on a name you cannot buy in size or exit at all. Valuation comes second-to-last on purpose: it is the filter most prone to seducing you, and you only want to look at price *after* you have already confirmed the quality and tradability behind it, so that a low price reads as "cheap relative to good" rather than "cheap, full stop." Balance-sheet safety is last because it is the *veto*: a name can pass all five prior filters and still be uninvestable if its balance sheet is one bad quarter from a forced capital raise or a bond default. Putting the veto last means it is the final word, the thing that can override an otherwise perfect score. Reorder the funnel -- put valuation first, say -- and you systematically walk into the value trap, because you start from the cheapest name and rationalize the rest. The order *is* the discipline.

### The leader-versus-laggard tell

Run those six filters across a sector and a pattern emerges that is so consistent it is almost a law: **the leader and the laggard separate on the same handful of axes every time.** The leader has rising relative strength, high ROE, deep liquidity, a foreign bid waiting above it, a fair-but-not-cheap multiple, and a clean balance sheet. The laggard has falling relative strength, low ROE, thin liquidity, open foreign room with foreigners leaving, a "cheap" multiple, and a stretched balance sheet. The two are not random draws from the sector -- they are systematically different animals, and the funnel is precisely the instrument that tells them apart.

![Before and after diagram comparing a sector laggard with weak relative strength low ROE and high debt against a sector leader with strong relative strength high ROE and a clean balance sheet](/imgs/blogs/from-sector-to-stock-screening-leaders-in-a-hot-industry-4.png)

The before/after figure lays the two profiles side by side. On the left is the laggard: weak relative strength (it is up, but lagging its own sector), 6% ROE, a thin float you cannot exit, open foreign room with foreigners selling, an optically cheap 0.8x book that *looks* like a bargain, and a stretched balance sheet with rising bad loans. On the right is the leader: strong relative strength (outpacing the sector), 20% ROE, deep liquidity, full foreign room with a buyer waiting, a fair 1.5x book, and a clean balance sheet. Read across and you can see why the leader ran +55% while the laggard managed +10%: the leader is *better on every axis that matters*, and the one axis where the laggard "wins" -- the lower multiple -- is exactly the axis that lies to you. Which brings us to the traps.

### The value trap and the illiquidity trap

Two specific failure modes account for most of the damage investors do to themselves *after* getting the sector right. Both feel like smart moves at the time. Both are the funnel's reason for existing.

![Two traps diagram showing the value trap cheap for a reason with bad assets and low ROE and the illiquidity trap cannot exit in a selloff](/imgs/blogs/from-sector-to-stock-screening-leaders-in-a-hot-industry-6.png)

The figure puts the two traps side by side, because they are the twin reasons a correct sector call still loses money. On the left, the value trap: a low multiple draws you in, but it is low *because* the assets are bad, the ROE is thin, and the franchise is shrinking -- so instead of rerating up, the cheap stock stays cheap or gets cheaper while the leader compounds away from you. On the right, the illiquidity trap: a thin small-cap rockets in the hot sector and tops your relative-strength screen, but the same thin float that lifted it means there is no bid when the sector turns -- your own selling becomes the down-move, and you are trapped at exactly the moment you most need to leave. Both traps are *seductive on a single number* (a low price, a big move) and *fatal on the full picture* -- which is precisely what the six-filter funnel forces you to see.

The **value trap** is buying a stock *because* it is the cheapest in the sector, when it is cheap *for a reason*. A low P/B or low P/E is not inherently a bargain -- it is the market's *price* for the stock, and the market is usually pricing in something real: declining ROE, deteriorating asset quality, a structural loss of franchise, a balance sheet that is quietly rotting. The trap is mistaking a *low multiple* for a *margin of safety*. A bank at 0.8x book with 6% ROE and rising NPLs is not trading cheap because the market is wrong; it is trading cheap because its book value is suspect and its earnings are thin. Buy it and you do not get the rerating you imagined -- you get a name that stays cheap, or gets cheaper, while the high-ROE leader you passed on compounds away from you. Valuation is filter *five*, not filter one, precisely so you never reach for the cheapest name before you have checked the quality and the balance sheet behind the price.

The **illiquidity trap** is owning a position you cannot exit. It is seductive because thin small-caps can rocket in a hot sector -- low float means a little buying moves them a lot, so they often top the relative-strength screen. But the same thin float that lifts them on the way up *traps you* on the way down. When the sector cools and everyone reaches for the exit at once, a thin stock has no bid: your sell order is the marginal trade, and every share you dump pushes the price lower, so you are simultaneously the seller *and* the cause of the loss. A liquid name lets you leave at roughly the screen price; an illiquid one charges you a brutal toll to leave at all, and in a real selloff sometimes will not let you leave for days. Liquidity is filter *three*, before valuation and before you fall in love with a fundamental story, because no fundamental story is worth owning if you cannot act on the exit.

Both traps share a root: they treat one attractive number -- a low multiple, a big move -- as the whole picture, when it is one filter out of six. The funnel's discipline is that *no single filter can pass a name on its own.* A stock has to clear all six, in order, and the two filters that catch the traps (liquidity at stage three, valuation-in-context at stage five, balance sheet at stage six) sit deliberately *after* the seductive ones so the trap never gets to the front of your attention unchecked.

There is a deeper reason the two traps are so dangerous specifically in a *hot* sector, as opposed to a quiet one. In a hot sector, both traps come dressed as opportunities. The value trap looks like "the one name that has not run yet" -- the laggard everyone else has noticed but you, the cheap stock in an expensive group, the apparent catch-up trade. It is genuinely tempting precisely because the rest of the sector has run and this one has not, and the human instinct is to buy what is cheap relative to its now-expensive peers. But the reason it has not run is usually that the flow examined it and *declined* -- the smart money already ran the funnel and dropped it on quality or balance sheet, which is why it is the cheap laggard in the first place. The illiquidity trap looks like "the rocket" -- the small-cap that is up the most in the whole sector, the name on every retail watchlist, the one whose chart looks the best. It is tempting because it has the biggest move, and a big move feels like the strongest signal. But the move is *because* of the thin float, not despite it, and the same thinness guarantees you cannot get the move back out at the end. A hot sector manufactures both temptations at industrial scale, which is exactly why a hot sector is when the funnel matters most, not least.

## How the funnel scores a real shortlist

The funnel is most useful when you stop thinking of it as prose and start thinking of it as a *scorecard*. Put your three or four candidate stocks in rows, the six filters in columns, and grade each cell plus, zero, or minus. The leader lights up plus across the row; the laggard collects minuses; the in-between names show you exactly which filter is the problem. The scorecard is the single most practical artifact in this whole post -- it turns a vague "which one do I like" into a structured, repeatable comparison you can run in fifteen minutes per sector.

![Scorecard matrix with three candidate stocks as rows and six filters relative strength ROE liquidity foreign room valuation balance sheet as columns with plus zero and minus cells](/imgs/blogs/from-sector-to-stock-screening-leaders-in-a-hot-industry-3.png)

The matrix shows three candidates in the same sector scored across the six filters. Candidate A -- the leader -- is plus on relative strength, plus on ROE, plus on liquidity, plus on foreign room, zero on valuation (fairly but not cheaply priced), and plus on the balance sheet. Candidate B is the laggard: minus on relative strength, minus on ROE, minus on liquidity, minus on foreign room, *plus* on valuation (it is the cheapest -- the value-trap bait), and minus on the balance sheet. Candidate C is the in-between case: plus on relative strength and ROE, but minus on liquidity, which is the filter that knocks it out for any position size you actually care about. Read the rows and the decision makes itself: own A, avoid B despite its tempting cheapness, and keep C on a watchlist for if its liquidity ever deepens. **One green-on-valuation cell does not redeem a row full of minuses** -- that is the value trap written in a single line of the table.

Notice how the scorecard *immediately* exposes the value trap. Candidate B's only plus is the valuation column -- it is the cheapest name in the sector. A naive value investor sorts the sector by P/B ascending and buys the top of that list, which is exactly Candidate B, the worst stock in the group. The scorecard refuses to let one column carry a decision: B is cheap *and* a falling-relative-strength, low-ROE, illiquid, foreign-abandoned, fragile-balance-sheet stock, and the cheapness is the *consequence* of all those minuses, not a counterweight to them.

### What each filter actually measures and its pass test

To run the funnel you need to know, for each filter, the *one number* you look at and the *one question* it answers. Vague filters produce vague decisions; a concrete metric and a concrete pass test make the funnel mechanical enough to run the same way every time.

![Matrix of the six funnel filters with what to measure and the pass test for relative strength quality liquidity foreign room valuation and balance sheet](/imgs/blogs/from-sector-to-stock-screening-leaders-in-a-hot-industry-7.png)

The matrix lays out the six filters as rows, with what you measure in the middle column and the pass test on the right. Relative strength: measure the stock's return *versus the sector index*; pass if it is outperforming its own sector, not just the market. Quality: measure ROE, operating margin, and earnings growth; pass if ROE is high (for a Vietnamese bank, think 18%-plus) and stable. Liquidity: measure average daily trading value in billion dong; pass if you could exit your full position in roughly two days of normal volume. Foreign room: measure *room ngoai* (the open foreign headroom) and the direction of the foreign tape; pass if room is tight with foreigners buying, fail if room is open with foreigners selling. Valuation: measure P/E or P/B *versus sector peers*; pass if you are paying a fair multiple for demonstrated quality, not the cheapest price in the abstract. Balance sheet: measure NPLs (banks), the bond-maturity wall (developers), or net debt to earnings (industrials); pass if leverage is conservative and there is no near-term maturity it cannot meet. Six rows, six metrics, six pass tests -- that is the entire funnel reduced to a card you can keep next to your screen.

The discipline the matrix encodes is that every filter has a *test you can fail*, not just a vibe you can feel. "Good company" is not a filter; "ROE above 18% and rising" is. "Liquid enough" is not a filter; "I can sell my whole position in two days without moving the price" is. The value of making each filter falsifiable is that it removes your own optimism from the loop -- the stock either clears the bar or it does not, and the funnel, not your hope, decides.

### Worked examples: running the funnel on real numbers

Prose and scorecards build the intuition; numbers make it operational. Here are four worked examples, one for each of the filters where beginners most often go wrong, using realistic Vietnamese-market figures anchored to the dong (with USD reference points at the mid-2026 rate of about **\$1 = 25,400 VND**).

#### Worked example: relative strength within the sector

Two banks, both up over the same three-month leg, both members of a banking sector that is itself leading VN-Index. Bank Leader rises from 22,000 to 30,800 dong, a gain of (30,800 - 22,000) / 22,000 = **+40%**. Bank Lagger rises from 18,000 to 21,600 dong, a gain of (21,600 - 18,000) / 18,000 = **+20%**. Both are green; a daily-color glance says both are winning. Now bring in the sector: the banking index over the same window rose **+28%**. Compute each stock's relative strength versus the *sector* (not the market): Leader's relative performance is roughly 40% - 28% = **+12 points** of outperformance versus its own sector; Lagger's is 20% - 28% = **-8 points**, *underperforming the very sector it belongs to*. The relative-strength filter picks Leader instantly: it is winning a larger and larger share of the sector's own move, while Lagger is up only because the whole group is up. **Inside a leading sector, the stock that beats the sector index -- not just the market -- is the one the flow is truly concentrating into.**

#### Worked example: the value trap

Two banks again, now compared on valuation and quality. Bank Cheap trades at a price-to-book (P/B) of **0.8x** -- its market price is 80% of its stated net asset value per share -- with an ROE of **6%** and a non-performing-loan (NPL) ratio that has climbed from 1.5% to **3.8%** over the last year. Bank Quality trades at **1.5x** book, with ROE of **20%** and a stable NPL ratio near 1.2%. A naive screen sorts the sector by P/B and buys Bank Cheap as "the bargain." But work the economics: at 6% ROE, Bank Cheap compounds its book value at 6% a year, and with NPLs rising, even that stated book is suspect -- some of those "assets" are loans that will not be repaid, so true book is *below* the 0.8x suggests. Bank Quality compounds book at 20% a year on a clean balance sheet. In roughly three and a half years, Bank Quality's compounding (1.20^3.5 ~ 1.84x) doubles its book while Bank Cheap's barely grows (1.06^3.5 ~ 1.22x) -- and the market knows this, which is *why* one trades at 1.5x and the other at 0.8x. The 0.8x is not a discount waiting to close; it is the market correctly pricing a weak, possibly shrinking franchise. **The cheapest name in a sector is usually cheap for a reason the funnel's quality and balance-sheet filters can name out loud.**

#### Worked example: the liquidity test

Two stocks in the same hot sector, and you want to build a position worth **5 billion dong** (about **\$197,000** at 25,400 VND to the dollar). Stock Thin trades an average of **2 billion dong** per day (~**\$79,000**); Stock Deep trades **50 billion dong** per day (~**\$1.97mn**). To buy 5 billion dong of Stock Thin without dominating the tape, you would need to spread the purchase over many days -- your position is two and a half *full days* of the stock's entire turnover. Now imagine the sector cools and you need out. In Stock Deep, 5 billion dong is one-tenth of a normal day's volume -- you sell into a deep bid and leave near the screen price. In Stock Thin, 5 billion dong is 2.5x a normal day's *entire* volume; there is no bid that size, so your selling *is* the down-move, and in a real selloff (when everyone wants out at once and daily volume itself collapses) you may be locked in for days while the price gaps lower beneath you. The position in Stock Thin is not 5 billion dong of value -- it is 5 billion dong of *hope you never have to sell in a hurry*. **Liquidity is the difference between a position and a trap; size every name against the volume you would need to exit, not just to enter.**

#### Worked example: the foreign-room edge

Two quality stocks, identical on the first three filters, separated only by foreign room. Stock Full is at its foreign-ownership cap -- *room ngoại* of zero, foreigners own the maximum 49% allowed, and there is a visible queue of foreign buyers placing orders that cannot fill because no room exists. Stock Open has plenty of open room -- foreign ownership sits at 30% against a 49% cap, leaving 19% of headroom -- but the foreign tape shows them *net selling*, trimming the position week after week. The structural read is opposite. In Stock Full, there is a wall of frustrated demand *above* the current price: any foreigner who wants in must buy from a *departing* foreigner, often at a premium, and any opening of room (a buyback, a cap increase) releases that pent-up bid into the price. In Stock Open, the marginal foreign flow is supply, not demand -- you are buying into a stock foreigners are leaving, and you are the one absorbing their selling. A foreign net outflow of even **\$20mn** over a quarter in a mid-cap can cap the price for months. The funnel favors Stock Full: full room with a queue is a buyer waiting above you; open room with foreigners selling is a seller you have to out-wait. **In Vietnam, where foreigners are price-setters at the margin, the direction of the foreign bid often matters more than the size of the open room.**

#### Worked example: valuation as a fair price for quality, not a cheapness contest

Now combine quality and valuation the way filter five actually wants you to. In one sector you have two finalists that both passed filters one through four. Stock Quality trades at a P/E of **12x** -- you pay 12 dong of price for each dong of annual profit per share -- and grows earnings at **20%** a year with a 22% ROE. Stock Cheap trades at a P/E of **7x** and grows earnings at **4%** a year with an 8% ROE. The naive read says Stock Cheap is "57% cheaper" (7 versus 12) and therefore the better buy. But valuation is meaningless without the growth and quality behind it. A standard way to put them on one scale is the price-to-earnings-to-growth ratio (PEG -- the P/E divided by the growth rate): Stock Quality's PEG is 12 / 20 = **0.6**; Stock Cheap's PEG is 7 / 4 = **1.75**. On a growth-adjusted basis, the "expensive" stock is *nearly three times cheaper* than the "cheap" one -- you are paying far less per unit of future growth. Put a dong figure on it: if Stock Quality earns 2,000 dong per share today (about **\$0.079** at 25,400 VND to the dollar) and grows that at 20%, in four years it earns roughly 4,150 dong per share; Stock Cheap earning 2,000 dong today at 4% growth reaches only about 2,340 dong. The premium multiple on Stock Quality is the market paying, correctly, for a profit stream that nearly doubles while the cheap one barely moves. **Filter five is not "find the lowest multiple" -- it is "find the lowest multiple *per unit of quality and growth*," which is almost never the optically cheapest name.**

## Common misconceptions

A few beliefs about picking inside a sector are so common, and so costly, that they deserve to be named and corrected with numbers.

**Misconception 1: "Buy the cheapest stock in the sector."** This is the value trap in slogan form, and it is wrong because *a low multiple is a price, not a discount.* The market sets a stock at 0.8x book or 6x earnings for reasons -- low ROE, bad assets, a shrinking franchise, balance-sheet risk -- and sorting a sector by cheapness ascending buys you exactly the names carrying those reasons. The correction: a high-ROE name at 1.5x book that *earns* 20% on equity is mathematically a better asset than a low-ROE name at 0.8x that earns 6%, because the first compounds your capital three times faster, and the multiple gap is the market correctly pricing that difference. Cheapness is only a virtue *after* quality and balance-sheet safety have passed -- which is why valuation is filter five, not filter one.

**Misconception 2: "The biggest name in the sector is always the best."** Size is not quality. The largest company by market cap is the most *index-weighted* -- it moves the sector index most -- but the relative-strength leader inside the sector is frequently a smaller, higher-ROE name that the flow is concentrating into precisely *because* it is growing faster off a smaller base. A 15 trillion dong (~**\$590mn**) mid-cap compounding ROE at 22% can easily out-return a 200 trillion dong (~**\$7.9bn**) mega-cap compounding at 14%, even though the mega-cap dominates the index. The correction: rank by relative strength and ROE, not by market cap. Size buys you liquidity and index relevance (real virtues -- filters three and four lean on them), but it does not buy you the best *return*. The leader of a sector is the best *stock*, which is rarely the same thing as the biggest one.

**Misconception 3: "A great sector lifts all boats equally."** The whole post is the refutation, but the number is worth stating plainly: in the dispersion chart above, a sector that returned roughly +30% on the index contained names from +60% down to +5% -- a *twelve-to-one* spread between best and worst. The tide lifts the average; it does nothing to close the gap between the leader and the laggard, and in fact a strong sector often *widens* the gap, because the flow concentrates into the genuine leaders and starves the laggards of attention. The correction: being right on the sector earns you the *index* return only if you buy the index (the ETF). To earn the *leader's* return, you still have to run the funnel -- the sector call gets you into the right room, but it does not pick the right person in it.

**Misconception 4: "High relative strength alone is enough to buy."** Relative strength is filter *one* for a reason: it is necessary but not sufficient. The thinnest, most speculative small-cap in a hot sector frequently tops the relative-strength screen, because low float means a little buying moves it a lot -- and that same thinness is the illiquidity trap waiting to spring. The correction: a name that screens strong on relative strength must still pass liquidity (can you exit?), quality (does it earn the move?), and balance-sheet safety (does it survive the turn?). Relative strength tells you where the money *is going*; the other five filters tell you whether you should follow it. Buying on relative strength alone is how you end up holding the sector's most fragile name at the exact moment the sector tops.

## How it shows up on VN-Index

The leader-versus-laggard gap is not a textbook abstraction in Vietnam; it is one of the most reliable patterns on HOSE, and it shows up the same way in every leading sector.

**In banks**, the gap is mostly an ROE-and-asset-quality story. Across the 2023-2025 cycle, the high-ROE, well-capitalized private banks -- the names earning north of 18-20% on equity with NPL ratios held near 1-1.5% -- consistently traded at a premium to book and consistently led the sector on the way up. The lower-ROE banks, often carrying heavier exposure to property-developer loans and corporate bonds, traded below book and lagged, and during the 2022 corporate-bond stress they were the ones whose "cheap" 0.7-0.9x book multiples *kept falling* as their NPLs climbed. A reader who simply owned "the banking sector" caught the average; a reader who ran the funnel and owned the high-ROE leader caught multiples of it, and a reader who bought the cheapest bank on the screen caught the value trap in its purest form.

![Scatter chart of illustrative same-sector ROE versus price-to-book showing the high ROE leader at a fair multiple and the low ROE value trap at a low multiple](/imgs/blogs/from-sector-to-stock-screening-leaders-in-a-hot-industry-5.png)

The scatter plots four banks from one sector on ROE (horizontal) against P/B (vertical) -- the single most useful picture in bank-picking. There is a clear upward relationship: higher-ROE banks trade at higher multiples, which is the market *correctly* paying more for banks that earn more on equity. Stock A sits top-right -- ROE 21%, P/B 1.6x -- the high-quality leader, expensive but earning it. Stock D sits bottom-left -- ROE 6%, P/B 0.8x -- the value trap: it *looks* cheap on P/B, but it earns so little on equity that the low multiple is deserved, not a bargain. The trap is reaching for the bottom-left corner because it is "cheap"; the edge is buying the *upper-right* (paying a fair multiple for genuine quality) or, on a good day, a name that is high-ROE but momentarily *under* the fitted line (quality the market has temporarily mispriced cheap). The point the scatter proves: **low P/B and low ROE travel together for a reason, and that reason is why the cheapest bank is so rarely the best one.**

**In brokers (securities companies)**, the gap is about balance-sheet scale and margin-lending capacity. The sector is the highest-beta group on the exchange -- it rips in a bull market on surging trading turnover and margin balances, and it is brutal in a bear. But inside it, the leaders are the well-capitalized brokers with the biggest margin-lending books and the deepest liquidity; the laggards are the thinly-capitalized small brokers that *look* cheaper and move *faster* on the way up but cannot fund the margin growth that drives broker earnings, and that you cannot exit when the sector turns. The illiquidity trap lives most dangerously in the small-broker corner of this sector.

**In property (real estate)**, the gap is almost entirely a balance-sheet story, and it is the most lethal of the three. Developers all share the presales-and-land-bank driver, but their survival is decided by leverage and the corporate-bond maturity wall. In the 2022-2023 bond crisis, the leading developers -- conservatively geared, with clean bond maturities and real presales -- held up and recovered; the laggards, the ones who had funded aggressive land banks with short-dated corporate bonds, saw their "cheap" valuations collapse toward zero as the bonds came due and could not be rolled. Here the balance-sheet filter (filter six) is not a tiebreaker -- it is the difference between a stock that survives and a stock that does not exist a year later. A reader running the funnel on property in early 2022 would have been pushed *away* from exactly the over-leveraged names that the crisis erased, no matter how cheap they screened.

The thread across all three: the funnel's filters are not equally binding in every sector. In banks, ROE and asset quality dominate; in brokers, liquidity and balance-sheet scale dominate; in property, the balance-sheet and bond-maturity filter dominates above everything. The funnel is the same six stages everywhere, but *which filter does the killing* depends on what actually decides survival in that industry -- and knowing that is the difference between a checklist and a judgment.

It is worth dwelling on how the *index effect* interacts with all of this, because it is a peculiarly Vietnamese amplifier. VN-Index is market-cap weighted, and so are the big ETFs that foreign money uses to access Vietnam. When a sector leads, the largest, most liquid names in it get a *double* bid: the active flow chasing leadership, plus the passive flow that index trackers must buy to keep their weights. That passive bid is mechanical and price-insensitive -- it buys the big liquid leader because it *has* to, not because it ran a funnel -- and it reinforces exactly the concentration the funnel points you toward. The laggards, often smaller and less index-relevant, get none of this mechanical support. So in Vietnam the leader/laggard gap is partly a *structural* feature of how passive money is forced to deploy, not just a fundamental one -- which is another reason the liquidity and foreign-room filters (three and four) carry more weight on HOSE than they would on a deeper, more domestically-owned market. A name at full foreign room in a leading sector is sitting in front of both the active and the passive foreign bid; a thin laggard with open room is sitting in front of neither.

A second cross-sector lesson: the leader does not stay the leader forever, and the funnel is a *repeated* measurement, not a one-time verdict. The high-ROE bank that led the 2023-2024 leg can become the laggard of the next one if it over-extends its loan book into a property downturn and its NPLs climb -- at which point the funnel, re-run, drops it on quality and balance sheet and points you to a *different* leader inside the same sector. This is the discipline beginners most often lack: they pick the leader once, fall in love, and ride it down through its own deterioration because "it is the best name in the sector." The funnel does not let you do that. Each re-run scores the *current* numbers, and when the leader's relative strength rolls over against its own sector while a peer's turns up, the funnel has already told you to rotate before the loss gets large. Leadership is a state, not a permanent attribute, and the funnel measures the state, not the memory of it.

## The playbook: how to go from sector to stock

Pull it together into something you can run every time a dashboard turns a sector green.

**The signal that starts the funnel:** your sector dashboard flags an industry as leading -- rising relative strength versus VN-Index, in an index uptrend, with confirming breadth and flow. That is the trigger, and it is covered in the rest of this series. The funnel begins the moment that signal fires and *not before* -- you do not screen for stocks in a sector that is lagging, because even the best name in a lagging sector is fighting a headwind.

**Run the six filters in order, and let each one kill candidates.** Start with the full sector basket. Filter 1 (relative strength within the sector): keep the names outperforming the sector index, drop the laggards -- this alone usually halves the list. Filter 2 (quality): keep the high-ROE, healthy-margin, growing names, drop the low-ROE ones floating on the tide. Filter 3 (liquidity): drop every name you could not exit a full position in within two days -- this kills the small-cap rockets, and that is the point. Filter 4 (foreign room): favor the names at or near full room with a foreign bid waiting; flag the ones foreigners are abandoning. Filter 5 (valuation versus peers): rank what survives by quality-adjusted valuation -- a fair price for demonstrated quality, never the cheapest name in the abstract. Filter 6 (balance-sheet safety): drop the fragile survivors -- high NPLs for a bank, a bond-maturity wall for a developer, high net debt for an industrial. What is left at the bottom of the funnel is your shortlist: one or two names, the leaders of the sector.

**The position:** own the leader, or split a position across the top two if they are close on the scorecard. Size to liquidity -- never build a position larger than something you could exit in a couple of days of normal volume, because filter three is not just an entry test, it is the rule that governs how big you are allowed to be. A name that passes liquidity at a 2 billion dong position fails it at 20 billion; the filter and the sizing are the same decision.

**A note on splitting versus concentrating.** If the top two names on the scorecard are genuinely close -- both plus across the board, separated only by a coin-flip on valuation -- splitting between them diversifies away the single-stock risk (a fraud, a surprise capital raise, a regulatory shock specific to one name) without giving up the sector leadership you screened for. But do not over-split: a hot sector usually has *one* clear leader, and spreading a position thin across five "pretty good" names just rebuilds the sector ETF at a higher cost and throws away the dispersion edge you did all this work to capture. The funnel exists to *concentrate* into the leader, not to launder the sector index into a hand-picked basket that returns the average anyway. One or two names is the target; if your shortlist is five, you have not finished running the funnel -- some of those five are failing a filter you have not been strict enough about. Push the bar up (ROE above 18% *and* full foreign room *and* top-quartile liquidity) until the list is short, because a short list is the entire point.

**Avoid the two traps, explicitly.** Before you buy, ask two questions out loud. First: *am I buying this because it is good, or because it is cheap?* If the honest answer is "cheap," check ROE and the balance sheet -- you may be reaching for the value trap. Second: *if I needed to sell my whole position the day after a bad print, could I, at roughly this price?* If the honest answer is "no," you are reaching for the illiquidity trap, and the position is too big or the name is too thin. Two questions, asked every time, catch the two errors that do the most damage.

**The invalidation -- when you are wrong about the stock even if the sector is still right.** This is the part beginners forget: the sector call and the stock call invalidate *separately*. Your stock thesis is broken when (a) the name's relative strength *versus its own sector* rolls over -- it starts lagging the very group it belongs to, the flow is leaving it specifically; (b) the quality deteriorates -- ROE falls, margins compress, NPLs climb -- so the reason you owned it is gone; or (c) the balance sheet cracks -- a bond maturity it cannot roll, a capital raise it has to do at a bad price. Any one of these invalidates the *stock* even while the *sector* is still leading. The discipline is to sell the broken name and rotate to the next leader *inside the same hot sector*, rather than holding the laggard out of loyalty to a sector call that was right about the room and wrong about the person. The sector being right does not make the stock right; only the funnel, re-run, keeps you in the leader.

A final word on honesty, in the spirit of this whole series. The funnel does not predict the future and it does not guarantee the leader keeps leading -- leadership rotates, and today's leader can be tomorrow's laggard if its quality or balance sheet turns. What the funnel *does* is stack the odds: it pushes your pick toward the left end of the dispersion (the +60% names, not the +5% ones), it keeps you out of the two traps that do the most damage, and it gives you a repeatable, falsifiable process instead of a hunch. In a market as dispersed as Vietnam's -- where the gap between the best and worst name in a hot sector is routinely twelve-to-one -- a disciplined funnel is not a small edge. It is the difference between earning the leader's return and earning the laggard's, on the exact same correct top-down call.
