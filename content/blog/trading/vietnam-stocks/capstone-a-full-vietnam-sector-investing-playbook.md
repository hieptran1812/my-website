In March 2020 the VN-Index was a falling knife. COVID had taken it from 990 down to a panic low of **660** on 24 March — a 33% crash in six weeks. Brokerage forums were a wall of red. And yet, by the close of that same week, the State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) had begun cutting its policy rate, eventually all the way to a record-low **4.0%**, and lifting the credit ceiling that governs how fast banks may lend. A reader running the playbook in this series would not have needed to *call the bottom*. They would have noticed one thing — the macro dial had flipped from tightening to easing — and let the process do the rest.

What followed was a clean, textbook trip around the cycle. Cheap money flooded in. The financial chain lit up in sequence: banks first, then brokers (the highest-beta sector on the exchange), then property, then steel and construction riding the credit boom. The index ran from 660 to a record **1,498** by January 2022. Then inflation forced the SBV to hike back to 6.0%, a corporate-bond crisis detonated under the property developers, and the index collapsed to **874** by November 2022 — a 42% drawdown that wiped out two years of gains in eleven months. Then a slow, *uneven* recovery: the index clawed back to 1,130 by the end of 2023 and 1,266 by the end of 2024, but the leadership had completely changed. The old high-beta darlings lagged; technology (FPT) and selective retail led.

A buy-and-hold investor lived all of that volatility for a mediocre five-year return. A playbook investor — someone who read the cycle, rotated sectors as the clock turned, sized by risk, and reviewed weekly — caught most of the bull, sidestepped most of the crash, and stayed positioned for the divergence. The difference was never a crystal ball. It was a **process**. This capstone is that process, every framework from the series pulled into one master playbook, with a sector cheat-sheet you can keep on your desk.

## Foundations: The playbook in five steps

Everything in this series — nineteen sector deep-dives, the macro dials, the financial chain, the rotation framework, the case studies — exists to serve one repeatable loop. The loop is **top-down**: you start from the biggest, slowest-moving force (the macro cycle) and work down to the smallest, fastest decision (which exact ticker to buy and how much). Each step narrows the field. By the time you are choosing a stock, the cycle and the sector have already done most of the filtering for you.

Here are the five steps. The rest of this section walks each one and points back to the post in the series where you can go deep.

**Step 1 — Read the cycle.** Locate Vietnam on the investment clock. **Step 2 — Select sectors.** Translate that cycle phase into the two or three sector groups that phase historically rewards, confirmed by relative strength. **Step 3 — Screen the stock.** Inside each chosen sector, find the leader: strongest relative strength, best quality, enough liquidity, acceptable valuation and balance sheet, and foreign room you can actually buy. **Step 4 — Size by risk.** Set position sizes inversely to each sector's beta so every sleeve contributes equal swing, and cap the portfolio beta. **Step 5 — Review and rotate.** Re-score the dashboard weekly; trim what is failing, add to what is leading, and act on your invalidation rules without emotion.

That is the whole thing. It is deliberately boring and deliberately repeatable. The edge is not in any single brilliant insight; it is in running the same disciplined loop while everyone around you is improvising on fear and greed.

### Step 1 — Read the cycle

The economy does not move in a straight line; it moves in a cycle of four phases — **recovery, expansion, overheat, slowdown** — and each phase hands market leadership to a different group of sectors. This is the single most important idea in the series, because it sets the stage for everything else. Get the cycle phase right and you are fishing in the right pond; get it wrong and even a perfect stock pick swims upstream.

In a developed market the cycle is usually read off GDP and inflation. In Vietnam the dominant dial is **credit and rates**, controlled by the SBV through two levers: the **policy rate** (the price of money) and the **credit ceiling** (*room tín dụng*, the quota that caps how fast each bank may grow its loan book). When the SBV cuts the rate and lifts the ceiling, cheap and plentiful credit flows into the economy and into the stock market via margin lending; the cycle accelerates. When inflation forces the SBV to hike and tighten the ceiling, the fuel is cut and the cycle rolls over. You can locate Vietnam on the clock with five free prints: GDP growth, CPI (inflation), system-wide credit growth, the SBV policy rate, and PMI (the manufacturing pulse).

The clock turns clockwise. **Recovery** (rates cut, credit reaccelerating, the economy still weak) favors the most rate-sensitive, highest-beta sectors: banks, brokers, steel. **Expansion** (growth broadening, earnings rising, rates still low) favors property, industrials, and construction materials. **Overheat** (inflation climbing, the SBV starting to hike) favors hard assets — energy, materials. **Slowdown** (rates high, growth cooling, credit tightening) favors the defensives that do not need a strong economy: utilities, consumer staples, healthcare.

Why does the order go this way? The logic is the *transmission lag* between money and earnings. When the SBV cuts and lifts the credit ceiling, the very first things to move are the assets whose value is *directly* a function of the interest rate and of trading liquidity — bonds, then bank net-interest margins, then the brokers, whose entire business is leverage on market activity. These react in *weeks*. Then, as cheap credit actually reaches borrowers and gets spent, it shows up in *real* activity: property developers launch projects, factories run hotter, steel and cement get ordered. These react in *quarters*. Finally, when all that demand has been pulling on capacity for a year or more, prices rise — inflation climbs, commodities and energy run, and the SBV is forced to hike, which starves the front of the chain first. The clock is just the calendar of how money turns into earnings, sector by sector, with a built-in delay at each link. That delay is your edge: because the sequence is reliable, you can be *early* into each phase's leaders rather than chasing them after they have already run.

One Vietnam-specific nuance is worth flagging, because it trips up readers who learned the clock on a Western textbook. In the US the cycle is fairly synchronized with the Fed's dual mandate; in Vietnam the *credit ceiling* is a discretionary administrative lever the SBV adjusts mid-year, which can make the clock jump forward suddenly when the ceiling is lifted, or stall the cycle when the ceiling is held even as rates are low. So you watch *both* the price of money (the policy rate) and the *quantity* of money (the credit ceiling and the actual system credit-growth print). A rate cut with the ceiling held is a weaker easing signal than a rate cut *plus* a ceiling lift; the second is what fires the financial chain.

The clock tells you which sectors *can* lead. But you should never trade the clock alone, because the clock is a slow, lagging read — it tells you the season, not the day. You confirm the season's read with **relative strength**: the actual price evidence of which sectors the market is already buying. More on that in Step 2.

### Step 2 — Select sectors

Once you know the cycle phase, you have a shortlist of two or three sector groups that phase favors. Now you confirm with two more lenses, both built earlier in the series.

The first is the **financial chain** — the causal sequence through which credit flows into the real economy and lights up sectors in order. Credit is created by the banks; cheap credit inflates the value of land, which is the collateral and the inventory of the **property** developers; property activity feeds **construction and steel**; and the whole boom is amplified by the **brokers**, who lend margin to retail investors chasing all of it. When the SBV eases, this chain ignites front-to-back; when the SBV tightens, it fails back-to-front, and the most leveraged link — property and its corporate bonds — breaks first. Knowing the chain tells you not just *which* sectors lead but in *what order*, so you are early into each one rather than chasing.

For the chain, see [The Financial Chain: Banks, Property, Brokers, Steel, Construction](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/the-financial-chain-banks-property-brokers-steel-construction).

![Financial chain credit to banks to property to brokers to steel and construction with collateral feedback](/imgs/blogs/capstone-a-full-vietnam-sector-investing-playbook-4.png)

The second lens is the **relative-strength (RS) dashboard** — the price evidence of which sectors are *actually* leading right now. RS is brutally simple: for each sector index, compute its return relative to the VN-Index over a trailing window (say 13 and 26 weeks). Sectors outperforming the index have positive RS and are leaders; sectors lagging have negative RS and are laggards. Leadership *persists* — a sector that has been leading for a quarter tends to keep leading for a while — so RS turns the slow, lagging clock into a fast, confirming signal. You buy where the cycle says you *should* lead AND where RS says you *are* leading. When the two disagree, you wait.

The reason you need *both* the clock and RS, rather than trusting either alone, is that they fail in opposite ways. The clock is a *fundamental* read: it is right about the direction over quarters but useless about timing — it will have you "early" for months, bleeding patience, before the move starts. RS is a *price* read: it is precise about timing but has no memory and no fundamentals — it will flip you long at the exact top if the top happens to have strong recent momentum, and it whipsaws on every two-week wobble. Used together they cover each other's blind spots: the clock keeps you from chasing a momentum spike into an over-extended late-cycle sector, and RS keeps you from sitting on a fundamentally-right idea that the market simply isn't ready to reward yet. The rule "act only when cycle and RS agree, wait when they disagree" is not a hedge — it is the whole point. The disagreements are where most retail money is lost: buying a "cheap" early-cycle sector while RS is still falling (catching a knife), or chasing a high-RS sector the clock says is already late (buying the top).

For RS and the dashboard, see [Relative Strength: Reading Which Sector Leads VN-Index](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/relative-strength-reading-which-sector-leads-vn-index) and [Building a Sector-Rotation Dashboard for VN-Index](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/building-a-sector-rotation-dashboard-vn-index).

#### Worked example: a full top-down decision in one walkthrough

It is early 2020. Run the chain end to end.

**Read the cycle (Step 1).** The SBV has just cut the policy rate toward 4.0% and signaled it will lift the credit ceiling. CPI is benign. Credit growth is set to reaccelerate. That places Vietnam firmly in **recovery** — the early-cycle phase that favors banks, brokers, and steel.

**Select the sector (Step 2).** The clock says banks/brokers/steel. The financial chain says the highest-beta link — the one that moves *most* when cheap credit and margin flood in — is the **brokers** (*công ty chứng khoán*), because their revenue is leveraged twice over: brokerage commissions rise with turnover, and their own margin-lending book grows with the boom. The RS dashboard confirms it: as the index turns up, the brokers' sector index is already outpacing the VN-Index. Cycle, chain, and RS agree. **Buy the brokers.**

**Screen the stock (Step 3).** Inside the brokers, you want the leader: the largest, most liquid name with the strongest RS and a clean balance sheet. That points to a name like SSI — biggest market cap, deepest liquidity, top-tier RS. Say it trades at **20,000 dong** per share (≈ **\$0.79** at \$1 = 25,400 VND).

**Size by risk (Step 4).** Brokers carry a beta near 1.8 — the highest on the exchange. On a 1bn-dong book (≈ **\$39,400**) you do not give it a full 20% slot; you size it down to ~10% (100mn dong, ≈ **\$3,900**) so its 1.8 beta contributes roughly the same risk a 1.0-beta sector would at 18%.

**Set the invalidation (Step 5).** Your thesis is "early-cycle, easing-driven broker leadership." It is wrong if the SBV reverses to hiking, if credit growth stalls, or if the brokers' RS rolls over below the index. Any of those is your exit. You did not predict anything — you read the cycle, followed the chain, confirmed with RS, screened the leader, sized by beta, and pre-committed your exit. **The whole playbook fits in one trade.**

### Step 3 — Screen the stock

You have your sectors. Now you find the one stock in each that you will actually own. The reason sector comes before stock is the single most-cited number in this series: **roughly half of any Vietnamese stock's variance is its sector moving together.** A stock is part company, part sector, part market. Pick the right sector and you have already won half the battle; a great company in a sinking sector usually still sinks.

Inside the chosen sector, screen on six filters. They are listed worst-to-skip first:

1. **Relative strength within the sector.** Buy the *leader*, not the laggard hoping to catch up. The strongest stock in a strong sector is where institutional money concentrates.
2. **Quality.** Durable return on equity, real earnings (not one-off gains), competent management, a moat. In Vietnam, prefer names with transparent disclosure over opaque conglomerates.
3. **Liquidity.** You must be able to get in and out. A name that trades 50bn dong (≈ **\$2.0mn**) a day is investable for a retail book; one that trades 2bn dong a day is a trap you cannot exit in a panic.
4. **Foreign room** (*room ngoại*). The foreign ownership limit caps how many shares foreigners may hold; when room is full, foreign buyers — a major source of demand — are locked out, and you may pay a premium to buy from another foreigner. Check that room exists.
5. **Valuation, by the lens that fits the sector.** Banks on price-to-book (P/B); property on net asset value (NAV) of the land bank; cyclicals like steel on EV/EBITDA; growth and consumer names on P/E. Using the wrong multiple is one of the most common errors — a 6× P/E steel name at the top of the cycle is not "cheap."
6. **Balance sheet.** Especially for cyclicals and property: how much debt, when does it mature, how much is short-term corporate bonds (*trái phiếu doanh nghiệp*) coming due? In 2022 the balance sheet was the *only* filter that mattered.

The order matters: you apply these filters as a *funnel*, cheapest-to-check first, so you eliminate most names before doing the slow work. Liquidity and foreign room are quick yes/no gates — if a name fails them, stop, you cannot own it at size regardless of how good the story is. Then RS-within-sector ranks the survivors. Only on the top two or three do you do the genuine work: read the quality, pick the right valuation lens, and stress the balance sheet. Most retail investors run the funnel backwards — they fall in love with a story, then a chart, and only check liquidity and balance sheet *after* they are already holding, which is exactly when it is too late.

A word on the Vietnamese specifics, because two of these filters are local quirks a Western screen does not have. **Foreign room** (*room ngoại*) exists because Vietnam caps foreign ownership of most listed companies (often 49%, lower for banks and some "conditional" sectors). When a popular name's room is *full*, the marginal foreign buyer is locked out of the order book and can only buy from another foreigner, sometimes at a premium negotiated off-exchange. For you as a domestic investor this matters two ways: a name with no room has lost a major demand engine (the ETFs and foreign funds cannot add), and a name where foreigners are *net selling* into thin room is one where a big, price-insensitive seller is overhanging the tape. **Liquidity** matters more than in a deep market because VN-Index small-caps can trade by appointment — a 2bn-dong-a-day name (≈ **\$79,000** of turnover) looks fine until you try to sell 500mn dong of it into a falling market and find no bid. The playbook's liquidity floor is not fussiness; it is the difference between a position and a trap. See [Foreign Flows, ETFs, and the Index Effect in Vietnam](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/foreign-flows-etfs-and-the-index-effect-vietnam) for how room and flows actually move prices.

For the full screen, see [From Sector to Stock: Screening Leaders in a Hot Industry](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/from-sector-to-stock-screening-leaders-in-a-hot-industry) and the valuation framework in [Valuation by Sector: P/E, P/B, NAV, EV/EBITDA](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/valuation-by-sector-pe-pb-nav-ev-ebitda).

#### Worked example: screening the leader inside a hot sector

It is mid-2021. The cycle and RS both say property is leading. Three property names are on your list. Name A trades **45bn dong** (≈ **\$1.77mn**) a day, has foreign room available, the strongest 26-week RS of the three, presales accelerating, and net debt at a manageable ~0.4× equity. Name B has a *better* story — a famous mega-project — but trades only **3bn dong** (≈ **\$118,000**) a day, room is full, and it has 4tn dong of corporate bonds (≈ **\$157mn**) maturing inside twelve months against thin cash. Name C is the laggard — cheapest on paper at a low price-to-NAV, but its RS is negative and its land bank is in a province with no infrastructure catalyst.

Run the funnel. Name B fails on liquidity and balance sheet — the bond wall is a 2022-shaped landmine and you could never exit 500mn dong of it in a panic; *eliminate it regardless of the story.* Name C fails on RS — "cheap and falling" is a value trap, not a bargain; *skip it until RS turns.* Name A wins every filter that matters: liquid enough to trade, room to buy, leading on RS, presales (the key driver) accelerating, and a balance sheet that survives a credit squeeze. You buy **Name A** at, say, 60,000 dong (≈ **\$2.36**) a share. Notice you never even valued Names B and C — the funnel killed them before the slow work. **The screen's job is to spend your attention only on the names that already passed the gates that get you killed.**

### Step 4 — Size by risk

Now the part most retail investors get exactly backwards. The natural instinct is to bet *biggest* on the most exciting, fastest-moving name — the high-beta broker, the speculative property play. The playbook does the opposite: it sizes those *smallest*.

**Beta** is a sector's sensitivity to the VN-Index. A beta of 1.8 means that when the index moves 10%, that sector tends to move ~18%; a beta of 0.6 means it moves ~6%. High beta is not "better" — it is *more*, in both directions. The error is treating equal dong as equal risk. Put 100mn dong in brokers (beta 1.8) and 100mn in utilities (beta 0.6) and the broker position contributes roughly three times the swing; your "diversified" book is really one big broker bet in disguise.

**Risk-budgeting** fixes this. Decide how much risk each position may contribute, then size *inversely to beta* (and volatility) so each sleeve adds the same amount of swing. The exciting high-beta name gets a *smaller* position, not a bigger one. And because your **portfolio beta is just the weighted average of your positions' betas**, you can cap it deliberately: a beta-1.4 book falls ~42% in a 30% index drop, while a beta-0.9 book falls ~27%. Cap the portfolio beta and you cap the drawdown.

The final sizing input is **correlation**. Many Vietnamese sectors move together — banks, brokers, property, and steel are all links in the same financial chain, so they spike and crash *together*. Owning all four is not diversification; it is one leveraged bet on credit. True diversification means pairing high-beta cyclicals with genuinely uncorrelated defensives (utilities, staples, healthcare), so that when the credit chain breaks, part of your book holds.

For sizing and correlation, see [Sizing by Sector Beta: How Much to Bet on Each Industry](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/sizing-by-sector-beta-how-much-to-bet) and [Sector Correlations and Diversification in Vietnam](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/sector-correlations-and-diversification-vietnam).

#### Worked example: building the portfolio sized by beta

You have a **1bn-dong** book (≈ **\$39,400** at \$1 = 25,400 VND). It is recovery/early-expansion, so you want a cyclical tilt — but sized so no single sleeve dominates the risk. You set a target portfolio beta of about **1.1** (a modest cyclical tilt, not a reckless one) and size each sleeve so that higher beta means fewer dong.

| Sleeve | Sector beta | Allocation (dong) | Allocation (\$) | Beta contribution (weight × beta) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Brokers (high beta) | 1.8 | 120mn (12%) | \$4,720 | 0.216 |
| Banks (mid-high) | 1.2 | 250mn (25%) | \$9,840 | 0.300 |
| Steel (cyclical) | 1.5 | 130mn (13%) | \$5,120 | 0.195 |
| Industrials | 1.0 | 200mn (20%) | \$7,870 | 0.200 |
| Utilities (defensive) | 0.6 | 150mn (15%) | \$5,910 | 0.090 |
| Cash | 0.0 | 150mn (15%) | \$5,910 | 0.000 |

Portfolio beta = 0.216 + 0.300 + 0.195 + 0.200 + 0.090 + 0.000 = **1.001** — call it ~1.0, a measured cyclical tilt. Note what the discipline did: the *most exciting* sleeve (brokers, beta 1.8) is the *second-smallest* position, while the boring banks sleeve is the biggest. The 15% cash is dry powder for the weekly review. **You decided how much risk to take first, then let beta dictate the dong — not the other way around.**

### Step 5 — Review and rotate

A playbook you set once and forget is not a playbook; it is a guess. The fifth step is the engine that keeps the other four honest: a **weekly review** in which you re-score the dashboard, compare it to your positions, and make small, mechanical adjustments.

Every weekend you do four things. **(1) Re-read the cycle**: did any of the five macro prints change the phase? **(2) Re-score the RS dashboard**: which sectors gained or lost relative strength this week? **(3) Compare to your book**: are you overweight the leaders and underweight or out of the laggards? **(4) Act on invalidations**: any position that hit its exit rule gets trimmed or cut — no debate, no "it'll come back."

The discipline is to make the adjustment *small* and *rules-based*. When a sector slips from leading to neutral on your dashboard, you trim it from overweight to neutral — you do not panic-sell the whole sleeve, and you do not double down to "average in" out of pride. The weekly cadence is slow enough to ignore noise and fast enough to catch a real regime change before it costs you the whole drawdown.

#### Worked example: the review loop catching a rotation early

It is mid-2022. Your dashboard has, for two quarters, shown brokers and property as the clear leaders, and you are overweight both. This weekend's re-score shows something different: CPI has jumped, the SBV has started *hiking*, and on the RS dashboard both brokers and property have slipped from strong-positive to *negative* relative strength for the second week running. Meanwhile utilities and staples are quietly turning positive.

The cycle has turned from expansion toward slowdown. The disciplined move is small and immediate: trim the brokers sleeve from overweight (say 18% of the book, ≈ **\$7,090** on a 1bn-dong book) down to neutral (~6%, ≈ **\$2,360**), trim property similarly, and rotate the freed dong into utilities and cash. You are not calling a crash; you are simply moving your weight from sectors the dashboard says are *failing* to sectors it says are *strengthening*.

Contrast the emotional response: a non-playbook investor, anchored to the gains they made all year, holds the brokers and property "for the rebound," adds on the first dip to lower their average, and rides both straight into the bond crisis that takes property down ~48% and brokers down ~58% that year. The disciplined re-score cost you a small, unglamorous adjustment. The emotional overreaction — refusing to adjust — cost half the book. **The review loop is where process quietly beats prediction.**

## The master sector cheat-sheet

This is the keep-it page — the centerpiece of the playbook. For each of the twelve sectors you will actually trade on VN-Index, it records four things: the **cycle phase that favors it**, its **beta** (how much it moves vs the index), the **valuation lens** that fits it, and the **key driver** that determines its earnings. Tape it to your monitor; it is the lookup table behind Steps 2, 3, and 4.

![Sector cheat sheet twelve sectors with cycle phase beta valuation lens and key driver](/imgs/blogs/capstone-a-full-vietnam-sector-investing-playbook-2.png)

Read the cheat-sheet down the cycle, not across the rows, and a story emerges. The **early-cycle** column — banks, brokers, steel — is where the recovery starts: rate-sensitive, high-beta, the first to move when the SBV eases. The **mid-cycle** column — property, construction, industrials — is the broad expansion, where the financial chain feeds the real economy. The **late-cycle** column — oil and gas, and to a degree steel again — is the inflation/overheat trade on hard assets. And the **defensive** column — utilities, F&B, retail staples, pharma, plus structural-growth IT — is where you hide when the cycle rolls over. IT (FPT) is the special case: it is a *structural grower* whose earnings are less tied to the domestic cycle than to global software demand, which is exactly why it led the 2023-2024 divergence while the cyclicals languished.

The beta column is your sizing input (Step 4). The valuation-lens column is your screening input (Step 3): a bank without a P/B is unscreened; a property developer without a NAV of its land bank is unvalued; a steel name priced on P/E at the top of the cycle is a trap. The key-driver column is your *invalidation* input (Step 5): when the driver turns — credit growth stalls for banks, presales freeze for property, the hot-rolled-coil price rolls over for steel — the thesis is broken regardless of what the chart is doing.

A few rows reward a second look because they are the ones beginners get wrong. **Banks** carry a deceptively high beta for a "safe" sector: they are the heart of VN-Index by weight, and because their earnings lever directly off credit growth and their net-interest margin (NIM) widens and narrows with the rate cycle, they move *more* than people expect — and they are valued on P/B precisely because their assets (loans) and equity are the whole business, not their reported P/E, which can be distorted by provisioning. **Property** is valued on the NAV of the land bank rather than current earnings because a developer's profit is lumpy — it books revenue when projects complete, not when value accrues — so the right question is "what is the land worth and how much debt sits against it," which is exactly why the 2022 bond wall, not the income statement, was the thing that mattered. **Brokers** are the purest cyclical amplifier: their revenue is leveraged twice (commissions rise with turnover *and* their margin book grows with the boom), which is why they are the highest-beta row and why they led both the 2021 melt-up and the 2022 collapse. **IT** is the row that breaks the cyclical pattern: its driver is global software and digital-transformation demand, not the domestic credit cycle, so it can lead when everything cyclical is dead — the FPT-led 2023-2024 divergence is the cheat-sheet's "structural growth" row doing exactly what the table says it should.

## Putting it together across a full cycle

Steps 1-5 are not five separate decisions; they are one continuous loop that turns as the cycle turns. The power of the playbook is that the *same* routine produces *different* positioning in each phase — automatically, without you having to override it on conviction. Walk one full cycle and watch the loop do its work.

**At the trough (recovery).** The SBV cuts; credit reaccelerates. Step 1 says recovery. Step 2 points to banks, brokers, steel, confirmed as their RS turns up. Step 3 screens the leaders. Step 4 sizes them with a modest cyclical tilt (portfolio beta ~1.1) — high-beta brokers sized *down*, not up. You are long the right sectors, early, but not recklessly. This is the climb from 660 to the 1,100s.

**Into the boom (expansion).** Growth broadens. Step 1 says expansion. Step 2 rotates weight toward property, industrials, materials as their RS overtakes the early-cycle leaders. Step 3 re-screens. Step 4 lets portfolio beta drift up modestly as the trend confirms — but caps it. The weekly review (Step 5) keeps you in the leaders. This is the run toward the 1,498 record.

**At the top (overheat → early slowdown).** CPI climbs; the SBV starts hiking. Here is where the playbook earns its keep. Step 1 flags overheat turning to slowdown. The RS dashboard (Step 2) shows the high-beta cyclicals *losing* relative strength. Step 5's weekly re-score forces the trim — brokers and property out, defensives and cash in — *before* the crash, not after. You do not call the top; you simply rotate out of failing sectors. Portfolio beta falls toward 0.7.

**Into the bust (slowdown/crisis).** The bond crisis breaks property and the financial chain fails back-to-front. Because you already rotated to defensives and raised cash, your book falls far less than the index's 42%. Utilities, staples, and healthcare hold; your cash is dry powder.

**Into the recovery (selective).** The crisis passes but leadership has *changed*. Step 2's RS dashboard now points not to the old cyclical darlings but to structural growth (IT/FPT) and selective consumption (retail). Step 3 re-screens to the new leaders. You rotate again — and you participate in the 2023-2024 recovery in the *right* sectors, not the ones that led the last cycle. This is the single hardest thing for a human to do, and the playbook does it for you, because RS does not care which sector "used to" lead.

Step back and notice what just happened across that walk. At no point did you have to *predict* anything. You did not forecast the 1,498 top, the bond crisis, or the IT-led recovery. You read a handful of free macro prints each weekend, re-scored a dashboard, and made small rotations when the cycle and RS agreed that leadership had shifted. The positioning changed enormously — from 80% cyclical at the trough to defensive-plus-cash at the top to structural-growth in the recovery — but *the routine never changed*. That is the entire claim of this series compressed into one paragraph: the market's narrative is unpredictable, but your *process* can be invariant, and an invariant process applied to a changing market is what produces adaptive positioning without requiring a crystal ball. The investor who tried to *predict* each of those four turns needed to be right four times in a row and would almost certainly have been wrong at least once, fatally. The investor who *ran the loop* needed only to keep running it.

It is also worth being honest about where the loop is *hard*, because a playbook you cannot actually execute is worthless. The two hardest moments are both at the extremes. At the top, the loop tells you to trim your best-performing sectors precisely when they feel unstoppable and everyone is mocking caution — this is where most people abandon the rules and ride the high-beta names into the crash. At the bottom, the loop tells you to *not* buy back the old leaders just because they are down 60% and "must be cheap" — this is where people anchor to the last cycle and miss the new one. Both failures are emotional, not analytical, which is exactly why the playbook front-loads the rules: the trim is mechanical (RS rolled over → trim), and the re-entry is mechanical (buy where RS is positive *now*, not where it used to be). You will still *feel* the pull to override the rules at both extremes. The discipline is to let the dashboard, not the feeling, place the trade.

#### Worked example: the cycle round-trip vs buy-and-hold

Take the full 2020-2024 arc and compare two investors with the same 1bn-dong (≈ **\$39,400**) starting book.

**Buy-and-hold** buys the VN-Index at 660 in March 2020 and never moves. By the 1,498 peak they are up 127% (book ≈ 2.27bn dong). By the 874 trough they have given most of it back, sitting at ~1.32bn dong — still up, but they lived a 42% peak-to-trough drawdown and the stress that comes with it. By end-2024 at 1,266 they are at ~1.92bn dong (≈ **\$75,600**), a fine five-year result earned by simply not flinching.

**The playbook investor** runs the loop. They are ~80% invested in early-cycle leaders off the trough, ride the climb at a capped beta, and — critically — the mid-2022 weekly re-score rotates them out of brokers and property and into defensives plus 40% cash *before* the worst of the crash. Their peak-to-trough drawdown is roughly half the index's, say ~22% instead of 42%, because the defensive book and cash cushioned it. They redeploy the cash into the 2023-2024 leaders (IT, selective retail) as RS confirms. The exact end figure depends on execution, but the shape is the point: **similar or better terminal wealth with roughly half the drawdown and far less white-knuckle stress.** The playbook did not beat buy-and-hold by being smarter about the destination; it beat it by being disciplined about the *path*. Lower drawdown is what lets a real human stay invested through a full cycle without capitulating at the bottom.

## The discipline: process over prediction

Every framework in this series is downstream of one philosophy: **you cannot reliably predict the market, but you can reliably run a process.** The playbook works not because it forecasts the future but because it reacts to the present faster and more dispassionately than your emotions can. Three disciplines hold it together.

**Score, do not predict.** Replace every "I think the market will…" with "the dashboard scores this sector as…". Prediction invites ego and anchoring; scoring invites adjustment. When your job is to read a number off a dashboard each week and act on it, you stop arguing with the market and start following it. The discipline loop is: **score → tilt → size → review → repeat**, with emotions removed at each step.

**Pre-commit your exits.** The single most expensive habit in retail investing is the absence of a sell rule. Every position you open gets an invalidation *before* you buy it: the macro condition that would break the thesis (the SBV reverses), the price condition (RS rolls below the index), and the fundamental condition (the key driver turns). When one fires, you are out — not after a meeting with yourself about whether it'll come back. The 2022 investors who survived had exits; the ones who got buried did not.

**Let sizing protect you from yourself.** Risk-budgeting is not just math; it is a behavioral guardrail. By forcing the exciting high-beta name into a small position, sizing-by-beta prevents the one trade that does the most damage: the oversized bet on the most volatile thing at the most euphoric moment. The portfolio-beta cap is a hard limit on how much pain a single regime change can inflict. Discipline you build into the *position sizes* does not depend on you being calm in a crisis — it works even when you are not.

There is a reason this works that is worth internalizing: the playbook converts decisions you would otherwise make *in the moment*, under emotional duress, into decisions you make *in advance*, calmly. The exit rule is written before you buy, when you have no money on the line and no ego invested. The position size is set by a formula, when the high-beta name is just a number in a beta column, not the screaming green candle you are watching at 2pm. The weekly review is a scheduled, mechanical re-score, not a panicked reaction to a headline. Every place where a human normally injects fear or greed, the playbook has already pre-loaded a rule made in a cool hour. That is the real edge — not that you are smarter than the market, but that you front-loaded your discipline so that your worst, most-emotional self has the smallest possible say in your outcomes. Markets do not pay you for being right; they pay you for being consistent, and consistency is something you can *engineer* with rules even when you cannot *feel* it in a crisis.

A subtle corollary: this same discipline protects you on the *upside*, not just the downside. The hardest thing in a roaring bull market is to *not* abandon the playbook — to keep the high-beta sleeve sized down while it is the best performer and your neighbor is bragging about doubling their money in brokers. The rules that feel pointless at the top (why am I only 12% in the best sector?) are the exact rules that save you at the bottom. Discipline is not something you turn on when it hurts; it is something you keep running when it is boring, so that it is already running when it matters.

## Common misconceptions

**"A playbook guarantees returns."** It does not, and any process that claims to is selling you something. A playbook does something more useful and more durable: it gives you a **repeatable edge with bounded downside**. You will still have losing trades — the cycle read will sometimes be early or late, a sector leader will sometimes underperform. What the playbook guarantees is that no *single* mistake is fatal, that you rotate before a drawdown compounds, and that your behavior is consistent across the emotional extremes where most investors do their worst damage. Over many cycles, a disciplined process with bounded losses beats a brilliant prediction with unbounded ones. In 2022 the playbook did not avoid *all* losses; it turned a potential 42% catastrophe into a survivable ~22% one. That is the realistic promise: not invincibility, but survivability and a positive expectancy.

**"You can skip the macro and just pick good stocks."** This is the most expensive misconception in the series, because it ignores the half of a stock's return that comes from its sector and the cycle. A wonderful company in a sector the cycle has turned against usually still falls — the best-run property developer in Vietnam still lost half its value in 2022 when credit froze, because the *driver* of the whole sector reversed. Bottom-up stock-picking without the top-down cycle read is fishing in a pond that's being drained. The macro read is Step 1 for a reason: it decides which ponds have water. You do not need to be a macroeconomist; you need to read five free prints and locate the clock.

**"One signal is enough."** No single indicator carries the load. The clock alone is too slow and lagging; it tells you the season, not the day. Relative strength alone is too fast and whippy; it will flip you on every two-week wobble. Valuation alone is a value trap waiting to happen — a steel name on a 6× P/E at the top of the cycle is "cheap" right up until earnings collapse. The playbook works because it stacks *confirming* signals: the cycle says a sector *should* lead, RS says it *is* leading, the chain says it leads *in this order*, and valuation says you are not overpaying. You act only when the signals agree, and you wait when they disagree. Any one of them, traded alone, will eventually wreck you. For the cheapest single mistake — using the wrong valuation lens — revisit [Valuation by Sector](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/valuation-by-sector-pe-pb-nav-ev-ebitda).

**"Rotation means trading constantly."** Rotation is not day-trading. The cycle turns over *quarters and years*, not minutes. The weekly review exists to catch real regime changes, and most weeks the right action is to do *nothing* — to confirm your positioning still matches the dashboard and move on. Over-trading bleeds you on spreads, taxes, and bad fills, and the Vietnamese T+2 settlement (you receive shares two business days after the trade) means you cannot churn intraday anyway. A typical year on the playbook might involve two or three meaningful sector rotations, not two hundred trades.

**"High beta is the way to make money in a bull market."** Half-true and dangerous. High-beta sectors *do* move most in a bull market — but they also move most in the crash, and the crash is where the un-disciplined lose everything they made. The playbook owns high-beta sectors when the cycle and RS both favor them, but it *sizes them down* so a single high-beta sleeve never dominates the book, and it *rotates out of them* the moment RS rolls over. The investors who blew up in 2022 were not wrong that brokers led in 2020-2021; they were wrong to oversize them and to hold them as RS collapsed.

## How it shows up on VN-Index: the full 2020-2024 arc

Now run the whole playbook against the real record. The chart below is the proof that the framework maps to reality: the actual annual total returns of seven key sectors across 2021-2024. Read it left-to-right and you can see the cycle turn — and you can see the playbook's calls land.

![Sector annual total return heatmap 2021 to 2024 for seven sectors green positive red negative](/imgs/blogs/capstone-a-full-vietnam-sector-investing-playbook-5.png)

**2021 — the liquidity bull, late expansion.** The whole column is green, but unevenly so. Securities (brokers) returned roughly **+110%**, steel about **+75%**, retail **+55%** — the high-beta cyclical chain delivering exactly as the early/mid-cycle column of the cheat-sheet predicted. Banks and property, having led earlier, were up more modestly (~+28% each) as leadership had already rotated past them. A playbook investor was long this chain, sized by beta, riding the trend with the weekly review keeping them in the leaders. The index ran to its 1,498 record.

**2022 — the bond crisis, the bust.** The entire column is deep red. Securities **−58%**, steel **−52%**, real estate **−48%** — the exact high-beta links of the financial chain, breaking back-to-front as the SBV hiked to 6.0% and the corporate-bond market seized. This is the column that destroys un-disciplined investors and vindicates the playbook. The Step-5 weekly re-score, watching RS roll over and the cycle turn to slowdown, rotated weight out of these very sectors into utilities (only **−10%** that year) and cash *before* the worst of it. The cheat-sheet's defensive column — the sectors with low beta and non-cyclical drivers — is exactly where the playbook hides, and it held.

**2023 — selective recovery, leadership changes.** Now the column is mixed, and the *mix* is the lesson. IT led with about **+55%**, securities bounced **+75%** off the deep base, but real estate managed only **+5%** and banks a tepid **+12%**. The old leaders did not lead the recovery. A playbook investor, following RS rather than memory, rotated toward the new leadership (IT/FPT) instead of buying the bombed-out property names hoping for a return to the old regime.

**2024 — the divergence widens.** IT ran another **+80%** as FPT's structural growth compounded, and banks finally caught a bid (**+28%**) on a recovering credit cycle, while property stayed *negative* (**−3%**) — still working off the bond overhang. The dispersion between the best sector (IT, +80%) and the worst (property, −3%) was enormous. In a year like this, *which sector you own is almost the entire return*, and the playbook's whole job — get the sector right first — is worth more than ever.

The four columns together are the empirical proof of the series' central claim. Look at any single stock and you will see noise — a good year, a bad year, an earnings surprise. Look at the *sectors*, year by year, and you see a clean, legible cycle: the high-beta financial chain leading the 2021 bull as one block, breaking as one block in 2022, and then *not* leading the recovery while a structural-growth sector took over. The co-movement is real, the rotation is real, and the cycle phase predicts the rotation. That is everything the cheat-sheet encodes, validated against four very different years. A reader who internalizes just this heatmap — that sectors move in blocks, that the blocks rotate with the cycle, and that the leadership of the *last* cycle is not the leadership of the next — already has most of the edge the playbook is built to capture.

Notice too what the heatmap says about *dispersion*, which is the quiet argument for why sector selection matters so much in Vietnam specifically. In 2024 the gap between the best and worst sector was over 80 percentage points. In a market with that much sector dispersion, a stock-picker who gets the *company* right but the *sector* wrong can still badly underperform a mediocre picker who simply owned the right sector. Vietnam's VN-Index, dominated by a handful of large sectors and prone to single-sector manias (*sóng ngành*, the sector "waves"), runs hotter dispersion than a broad developed index — which is precisely why a *sector-first* process, rather than a pure bottom-up one, is the right tool for this market. The dispersion is the opportunity; the playbook is how you harvest it without getting caught on the wrong side of it.

#### Worked example: reading one cell of the record as a playbook call

Take the single most dramatic cell: Securities, 2022, **−58%**. In dong terms, a 100mn-dong (≈ **\$3,940**) broker position held naively through 2022 ended the year worth about **42mn dong** (≈ **\$1,650**) — a loss of 58mn dong, more than half the capital, on what had been the *best*-performing sector the year before.

Now the playbook version. The mid-2022 weekly re-score (the Step-5 worked example earlier) flagged brokers' RS rolling negative and the cycle turning to slowdown, and trimmed that 100mn-dong broker sleeve to a neutral ~33mn dong, moving the freed ~67mn dong to utilities and cash. Utilities returned roughly **−10%** that year, and cash returned ~0% (in fact it earned deposit interest of ~5-6%). Blend it: the trimmed broker stub lost ~58% on 33mn (≈ −19mn dong), utilities lost ~10% on ~33mn (≈ −3mn dong), and cash on ~34mn was flat-to-positive. Total ≈ **−22mn dong** versus the naive **−58mn dong**. The single disciplined rotation, triggered by a number on a dashboard, saved roughly **36mn dong** (≈ **\$1,420**) on this one sleeve alone. **One cell of the historical record is one playbook call, and the call was worth more than a third of the position.**

## The playbook checklist

Print this. It is the operating manual — the weekly routine, the per-trade routine, and the invalidation rules, in the order you run them.

![Per trade decision flow where in the cycle which sectors lead best stock size by beta set invalidation](/imgs/blogs/capstone-a-full-vietnam-sector-investing-playbook-6.png)

**The weekly routine (every weekend, ~30 minutes).**

1. **Re-read the cycle.** Check the five prints — GDP, CPI, credit growth, SBV policy rate, PMI. Did the phase change? Note recovery / expansion / overheat / slowdown.
2. **Re-score the RS dashboard.** For each sector index, recompute 13- and 26-week relative strength vs VN-Index. Rank leaders to laggards.
3. **Compare to your book.** Are you overweight the current leaders and out of the laggards? Mark every mismatch.
4. **Act on invalidations.** Any position that hit its macro, price, or fundamental exit gets trimmed or cut — no debate.
5. **Make the smallest adjustment that fixes the mismatch.** Trim overweights that slipped to neutral; add to leaders you are underweight; keep dry powder. Most weeks: do nothing.

**The per-trade routine (before every new position).**

1. **Where in the cycle?** Confirm the phase favors this sector group (Step 1 + cheat-sheet).
2. **Which sectors lead?** Confirm RS is positive and the financial-chain order makes this sector early, not late (Step 2).
3. **Best stock in the sector?** Run the six-filter screen — RS, quality, liquidity, foreign room, the *right* valuation lens, balance sheet (Step 3).
4. **Size by beta.** Set the position inversely to the sector's beta; check it keeps portfolio beta under your cap (Step 4).
5. **Set the invalidation.** Write down, before buying, the macro / price / fundamental conditions that mean you are wrong and out (Step 5).

**The invalidation rules (the exits, pre-committed).**

- **Macro exit:** the cycle phase flips against the sector (e.g., the SBV pivots from cutting to hiking while you hold early-cycle cyclicals).
- **Price exit:** the sector's relative strength rolls from positive to negative vs VN-Index for two consecutive weeks.
- **Fundamental exit:** the sector's key driver turns — credit growth stalls (banks), presales freeze (property), the steel price rolls over (steel), software demand softens (IT).
- **Risk exit:** the position or portfolio beta breaches your cap, or a single sleeve grows past its size limit — trim back to target.

Any one of these firing is an exit, not a discussion. The exits are what convert a good framework into a survivable one.
