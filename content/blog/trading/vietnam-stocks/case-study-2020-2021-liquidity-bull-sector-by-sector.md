> **TL;DR** — The 2020-2021 bull is the cleanest case study in liquidity-driven sector rotation Vietnam has ever produced: from the COVID trough near 660 in March 2020, VN-Index more than doubled to roughly 1,500 by January 2022, powered not by an earnings boom but by a flood of cheap money and new retail accounts that rotated through sectors in a textbook sequence.
>
> - **Liquidity, not earnings, was the engine.** The State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) cut its policy rates to a record-low 4.0%, household savings piled up during lockdowns, over a million new retail trading accounts opened, and total broker margin balances tripled from ~52 trillion dong to a ~195 trillion peak. That wall of money lifted everything.
> - **Leadership rotated in a readable order**: brokers (the highest-beta sector) led, roughly +110% in 2021; then steel and materials, ~+75%; then property and the speculative penny midcaps; and the whole thing peaked together in January 2022.
> - **Every framework in this series called it in real time**: the business cycle said early-cycle (rates falling, recovery ahead), the liquidity/margin gauge said fuel was abundant and rising, and relative strength said brokers were leading — three independent green lights pointing the same way.
> - **The one number to remember**: VN-Index went 660 → ~1,500, about **+127%** in under two years; a 500 million dong (~\$19,700) index position became roughly 1.14 billion dong — and the same trade in a broker, up ~3x the index, was the high-beta payoff for buying the trough.

## A trough nobody wanted to buy

It is late March 2020. VN-Index has just printed a multi-year low around 660 points, down roughly 34% from its January high in a matter of weeks. The headlines are uniformly grim: a novel coronavirus is shutting down the global economy, Vietnam has closed schools and is about to enter a nationwide social-distancing order, airlines are grounded, factories are idling, and nobody knows how long any of it will last. Foreign investors are selling Vietnamese stocks every single session — the ETFs are bleeding, *room ngoại* (the foreign-ownership room) is opening up on names that were chronically full — and the financial press is openly asking whether VN-Index could break below 600 and keep going. Buying anything feels like catching a falling knife in the dark.

That trough, near 660, was the single best entry point Vietnamese equities offered in a decade. Within nine months VN-Index would reclaim 1,100. Within a year and a half it would touch roughly 1,500 — more than double the trough. The people who bought brokers and steel in the second quarter of 2020, while the news was still apocalyptic, made multiples on their money. The people who waited for the news to get good bought in late 2021, at the top, and rode the subsequent crash down. The gap between those two outcomes is the entire subject of this series, and the 2020-2021 bull is the purest illustration of it we have.

What makes this episode such a perfect teaching case is that it was *not* about earnings. Corporate profits in 2020 were, for most sectors, flat to down — factories were disrupted, retail footfall collapsed during lockdowns, banks were bracing for bad debt. The market doubled anyway, because the market does not trade earnings; it trades the *flow of money into stocks*. And the flow exploded: rates were cut to records, locked-down households saved instead of spent, a generation of new retail investors discovered the market, and brokers pyramided cheap margin loans on top of all of it. This post walks that bull sector by sector — the engine that drove it, the timeline it followed, the precise order in which leadership rotated, how each framework in this series flagged it as it happened, and the warning signs that flashed at the top. The goal is not nostalgia. It is to extract the *repeatable* pattern, so that next time the engine starts, you recognize it on the way up instead of on the way down.

## Foundations: what actually drove the 2020-2021 bull

Before we walk the sectors, we have to be precise about the engine, because the whole lesson of this case study collapses if you misattribute the cause. Four forces combined, and they reinforced each other. Let me define each from zero, because they are the same four dials this entire series keeps coming back to.

### Rate cuts: the price of money fell to a record low

The most important single driver was **monetary policy**. The State Bank of Vietnam — the central bank — responded to the COVID shock the way central banks everywhere did: it cut interest rates, hard and repeatedly. Through 2020 the SBV reduced its key policy rates in several moves, pulling the headline refinancing rate down to **4.0%**, the lowest level in the modern history of the Vietnamese market. (For the mechanics of how the SBV sets rates and steers credit, see the monetary-policy deep dive linked at the end.)

Why does the price of money move stock prices? Two channels. First, **the discount-rate channel**: a stock is worth the present value of its future cash flows, and you discount those future cash flows at a rate tied to interest rates. Lower rates mean a lower discount, which mechanically raises the present value of every future dong of profit — so the *same* earnings are worth a higher price. Second, and far more powerful in a retail market like Vietnam, **the alternative-yield channel**: when a bank deposit (*tiền gửi tiết kiệm*) pays 8-9% a year, sitting in cash is comfortable. When deposit rates fall toward 4-5%, that comfort evaporates. Savers go hunting for return, and the most accessible high-return option a Vietnamese household has is the stock market. Cheap deposits *push* money out of the bank and *toward* equities. This push was the deepest current under the entire bull.

### COVID savings: locked-down households had cash and nowhere to spend it

The second force was a strange byproduct of the pandemic itself. During lockdowns and social-distancing periods, ordinary spending collapsed — no restaurants, no travel, no shopping, no entertainment. But many households kept their incomes (especially salaried urban professionals working from home). The arithmetic is simple: income roughly steady, spending sharply down, so **savings shot up**. Across the world this produced a pool of "excess savings," and Vietnam was no exception. Combine that pool of fresh savings with collapsing deposit rates, and you have a large block of money actively looking for a home that pays more than 4%. A booming, doubling stock market was the obvious magnet.

### The "F0" generation: a flood of new retail accounts

The third force is the most Vietnam-specific, and it is the one that gives this bull its signature violence. Lockdowns gave people *time*. Brokers had digitized account opening so you could fund an account from your phone in minutes. And a rising market is the best advertisement there is. The result was an unprecedented wave of brand-new retail investors — Vietnamese commentators coined the term **"F0"** (borrowing the epidemiological label for a first-generation case) to describe the millions of first-time traders who poured in. New account openings ran at record monthly rates through 2020 and especially 2021; the count of new retail accounts opened in 2021 alone was larger than in several prior years combined. (For why a retail-dominated market behaves so differently — narrative-driven, momentum-chasing, prone to *sóng ngành* or sector "waves" — see the liquidity post linked below.)

This matters for *sectors* because retail money does not behave like institutional money. It is not diversified across the index; it concentrates, chases what is moving, and rotates as a herd. That herding is exactly what produces clean, readable sector rotation — and what makes the rotation overshoot in both directions.

It is worth pausing on *why* the F0 wave gives the bull its violence, because it is the structural feature that distinguishes Vietnam from a developed market. An institutional market is full of professionals who run risk limits, diversify, and rebalance against momentum — they sell what has run and buy what has lagged, which *damps* rotation. A retail-dominated market does the opposite: new investors with no risk framework pile into whatever is going up, which *amplifies* rotation. When a sector starts to lead, retail money chases it, which makes it lead harder, which attracts more retail money — a momentum loop with no professional counterweight. The same loop runs in reverse on the way down: when a leader cracks, retail panic-sells, which makes it crack harder. This is why Vietnamese sector waves (*sóng ngành*) are so much larger than the equivalent moves in developed markets, and why the 2020-2021 rotation was so unusually clean and readable — there were millions of momentum-chasing F0 accounts and almost no professional money leaning against them. The market's structure *is* the rotation engine.

### Margin: leverage tripled the firepower

The fourth force is **margin** — *ký quỹ*, borrowing money from your broker to buy more stock than your cash allows, using your stock as collateral. Margin is leverage: put up 100 million dong of your own cash, borrow another 100 million from the broker, and you control 200 million dong of stock. Every percent the stock moves now lands on a smaller equity base, so gains and losses are both magnified.

In a rising market, margin is a flywheel. Prices go up, your collateral is marked up, the broker extends you *more* borrowing room, you buy more, prices go up again. Multiply that loop across millions of accounts and you get the defining statistic of the bull: **total broker margin balance roughly tripled, from about 52 trillion dong at the end of 2019 to a peak near 195 trillion dong in 2021.** That is not a small number on top of the cash flows above — it is a multiplier on all of them, borrowed money pyramided onto the rate-cut money, the savings money, and the F0 money. (The full reflexive mechanics of the margin cycle — including how the same flywheel spins violently in reverse — are the subject of the liquidity post linked at the end; here we focus on its role as accelerant.)

### Beta: why some sectors amplify the move and others damp it

One more foundation is needed before the rotation makes sense: **beta**. Beta is a measure of how much a stock or sector *amplifies* the market's move. A sector with a beta of 1.0 moves roughly in line with VN-Index — up 10% when the index is up 10%. A beta of 2.0 means it tends to move *twice* as much — up 20% when the index is up 10%, and down 20% when the index falls 10%. A beta below 1.0 (a *defensive* sector) moves *less* than the market in both directions.

Why do sectors have such different betas? It comes down to **operating leverage** and **flow sensitivity**. A broker's revenue is geared directly to turnover and margin balances, both of which swing wildly with the market itself, so its earnings are enormously amplified by a bull — high beta. A steel mill has high *operating* leverage: large fixed costs (the furnace runs whether or not steel prices are high), so a swing in the steel price drops almost entirely to the bottom line — high beta for a fundamental reason. A food and beverage company, by contrast, sells roughly the same noodles and beer whether the index is at 660 or 1,500; its earnings barely notice the bull, so its stock barely moves — low beta, defensive.

Beta is the organizing principle of the rotation. In a liquidity bull, money flows *toward* beta, because high-beta sectors deliver the most amplified return on the rising tide — and it flows *away* from beta near the top, when the smart money wants to reduce its exposure to the coming reversal. Reading the rotation is, to a first approximation, reading the *beta ladder*: who is at the top of it (the leaders, high beta, early) and who is at the bottom (the laggards, low beta, late and defensive). (For how to size positions by sector beta — the practical question of how much to bet on a high-beta leader — see the sizing post in this series.)

#### Worked example: the bull math

Here is the headline trade in numbers. VN-Index troughed near **660** in March 2020 and peaked near **1,500** in January 2022. That is a gain of (1,500 − 660) / 660 ≈ **+127%** — the index *more than doubled*. Suppose, in April 2020 with the news still terrible, you put **500 million dong** (about **\$19,700** at \$1 = 25,400 VND) into a basket that simply tracked VN-Index. By the January 2022 peak that position is worth 500 million × (1 + 1.27) ≈ **1.14 billion dong** (about \$44,900) — a gain of roughly 640 million dong (~\$25,200) on an index fund, for doing nothing but buying the trough and holding. The lesson in one line: in a liquidity bull you do not need to pick the perfect stock; the rising tide alone roughly doubles a plain index position, and the entire game is just *being positioned for the tide*.

## The liquidity mechanics: why the tide lifted everything

It is worth dwelling on *why* a wall of money lifts the whole market roughly together, before we get to why some sectors lift more than others, because beginners often assume the index rises because "the economy got better." In 2020 it manifestly did not — GDP growth slowed sharply, profits were flat to down. The index rose because the **supply of stock is roughly fixed in the short run while the demand for stock exploded**.

Think of it as a simple supply-and-demand market for shares. On any given day there is a certain quantity of shares people are willing to sell at various prices (supply), and a certain amount of money chasing shares at various prices (demand). The four fuel sources above did nothing to supply — companies did not suddenly issue a flood of new shares in 2020 — but they massively increased demand. New F0 accounts brought new buyers. Rate cuts pushed deposit money toward those buyers. COVID savings funded them. Margin let each buyer bid for two or three times their cash. More money chasing the same shares can only do one thing to price: push it up. That is the entire mechanism. Earnings are a *long-run* anchor for where prices belong; *liquidity* is the *short-run* force that moves them, and in 2020-2021 the short-run force dwarfed the anchor.

The proof is in the turnover. **Average daily trading value (ADTV)** — the total dong amount of stock changing hands per day on HOSE — is the cleanest single gauge of how much money is actually in the game. It went from roughly **4.7 trillion dong a day in 2019** to about **7.4 trillion in 2020** to an astonishing **21.6 trillion a day in 2021** — more than a fourfold increase in two years. When ADTV quadruples, it is not because earnings quadrupled; it is because the *participation* quadrupled. Money is the story, and turnover is the money made visible.

There is a counterintuitive wrinkle worth flagging here, because it surprises people who assume foreigners drive Vietnamese stocks. **Foreigners were net *sellers* through most of the bull.** Foreign investors and the big VN-focused ETFs sold Vietnamese equities heavily in 2020 and 2021 — net outflows ran into the hundreds of millions of dollars, with some windows seeing foreign net selling on the order of \$1bn or more across the period. And yet the index doubled. How? Because *domestic retail money overwhelmed foreign selling by a wide margin*. The F0 wave was so large that it absorbed everything the foreigners sold and then bid prices up regardless. This is itself a structural lesson about VN-Index: it is a *domestic-liquidity-driven* market far more than a foreign-flow-driven one, and in a domestic liquidity bull, foreign selling is a headwind the local tide simply blows past. (The mechanics of foreign flows, *room ngoại*, and the index effect are covered in the foreign-flows post in this series.)

The other thing turnover tells you is *where in the market the money is concentrating*. ADTV is not spread evenly across sectors; in a bull it pools first in the leaders. In 2021, the securities and steel tickers consistently topped the most-actively-traded lists, while defensive sectors like utilities and staples saw their share of turnover *shrink* even as the total grew. Turnover, broken down by sector, is therefore a real-time map of the rotation — the sectors hogging the day's value are the sectors the money is rotating into, often *before* the price move is obvious on a chart. A trader who watched the daily turnover league table in 2021 had a leading indicator of the rotation, not a lagging one.

A news-driven rally spikes and fades; a money-driven rally grinds higher for as long as the money keeps coming. The money kept coming for almost two years.

When you see these two series surging together, you are not in an earnings bull; you are in a liquidity bull, and the playbook is different.

#### Worked example: margin tripling as a money multiplier

Let us make the leverage concrete. Say in early 2020 the market's investors collectively held **100 trillion dong of their own cash** committed to stocks, plus the ~52 trillion of margin they had borrowed — call it ~152 trillion of total buying power. By 2021, the *cash* base had grown (from F0 inflows and savings), but the margin balance had grown faster, to ~195 trillion. Suppose the cash base roughly doubled to ~200 trillion as new accounts piled in; total buying power is now 200 + 195 ≈ **395 trillion dong**, versus ~152 trillion before — buying power more than doubled, and a large chunk of that increase (the jump from 52 to 195 trillion in margin, about **143 trillion dong**, ~\$5.6bn) is *borrowed* money amplifying the same underlying cash. Leverage means the same household dong bids for stock two-and-a-half or three times over. The lesson: in a margin-fueled bull, the visible price rise overstates how much *real* money arrived, because each real dong was multiplied — which is exactly why the unwind is so dangerous, since the borrowed multiple has to be repaid when prices fall.

## The timeline: trough to peak in four acts

The bull is best understood as four distinct acts, each with a different character. Knowing which act you are in is the first job of the playbook.

**Act 1 — The trough (March 2020).** Capitulation. VN-Index bottoms near 660 amid maximum fear: nationwide social distancing, foreign selling every session, headlines forecasting worse. This is the moment of maximum opportunity and maximum discomfort — by construction, because the lowest price is the price at which the most people have given up. Almost nobody buys here; the few who do will make the most.

**Act 2 — The recovery (April-December 2020).** The SBV's rate cuts land, lockdowns ease, and the index climbs steadily — 660 in March, ~830 by mid-year, back above **1,100 by December 2020**, reclaiming nearly all the COVID losses in nine months. This act is driven by the *rate-cut and reopening* story; valuations are still reasonable, the move feels like a relief rally, and most participants are skeptical ("it's just a bounce, it'll roll over"). The F0 wave is building but not yet euphoric.

**Act 3 — The melt-up (2021).** This is where it goes vertical in participation. New account openings hit records, ADTV explodes to 21.6 trillion dong a day, margin balances march toward 195 trillion, and VN-Index climbs from ~1,100 to ~1,408 by mid-2021 and ~1,498 by year-end. This is the *liquidity* act — the move is now driven less by improving fundamentals and more by the sheer weight of money and leverage. Sector rotation is at its most violent and readable here. Euphoria sets in; everyone has a friend who got rich.

**Act 4 — The peak (January 2022).** VN-Index prints its all-time high around **1,530** in early January 2022. By now margin balances are stretched near their structural ceiling, valuations on the hot sectors are extended, and the marginal new buyer is getting scarce. The index goes sideways-to-down, and within months the whole flywheel reverses into the 2022 crash (the subject of the next case study). The top is not a single day; it is a roughly month-long distribution where the smart money sells to the last wave of F0 buyers.

The critical insight is that **the same four-act structure recurs in every liquidity cycle**, and your returns are determined almost entirely by which act you buy in. Buy in Act 1 or early Act 2 and you ride the whole thing. Buy in Act 3 and you might catch the last 20%. Buy in Act 4 and you are the exit liquidity. The frameworks in this series exist to tell you which act you are in *while it is happening*, not in hindsight.

### The warning signs at the top

Act 4 deserves a closer look, because the top is where fortunes were kept or lost, and the warning signs were genuinely visible at the time to anyone watching the right gauges. Six signs clustered in late 2021 and early 2022, and you did not need all six — any three together were a loud alarm.

First, **margin balances stalling at a record near the ceiling.** The margin book had climbed to ~195 trillion dong, and brokers were bumping against the regulatory cap that limits margin lending to a multiple of the broker's own equity. When the *flow* of new margin flattens, the marginal fuel that lifts a leveraged market is gone, even though the level looks healthy.

Second, **breadth narrowing.** Early in a bull, almost everything goes up together — broad breadth. Near a top, the index keeps making new highs but on the back of fewer and fewer names, while more stocks quietly roll over beneath the surface. By late 2021, the *headline* index was still pushing higher while an increasing share of individual stocks had already peaked — a classic divergence.

Third, **leadership rotating into junk.** When the highest-quality leaders (brokers, blue-chip steel) stop leading and the speculative penny midcaps take over the most-active lists, the market is telling you the smart money has finished buying the good stuff and only the last, least-discriminating money remains. The penny mania of late 2021 was this sign in neon.

Fourth, **valuation stretch on the hot sectors.** The brokers and the frothy midcaps were trading at multiples that only made sense if the bull ran forever. When a sector's price-to-book triples while its book value barely moves, you are paying for *liquidity*, not value, and liquidity is the most fickle thing to pay up for.

Fifth, **euphoric sentiment.** When taxi drivers and office colleagues are swapping tips, when "everyone" is opening an account, when losing money seems impossible — that universal confidence *is* the signal, because it means the last marginal buyer has already bought. A market needs a steady supply of new buyers to keep rising; euphoria means the supply is nearly exhausted.

Sixth, **the macro dials beginning to turn.** Rates had bottomed; the recovery was maturing; inflation pressures were stirring globally. The very early-cycle conditions that launched the bull were quietly expiring, and a maturing cycle eventually forces rates back *up*, which is poison for a market that rose on rate cuts.

None of these six told you the exact day of the top. Together, they told you the bull was *old* and the risk/reward had flipped — which is all a trim signal needs to do. The job of the top is not to be timed perfectly; it is to be *respected* while everyone else is celebrating.

## The sector rotation sequence: who led, in what order

Now the heart of the case study. A liquidity bull does not lift all sectors at once or equally. Money rotates through them in a sequence driven by *beta* (how much a sector amplifies the market's move) and by the *cycle logic* of which businesses benefit first. The 2020-2021 sequence was about as textbook as Vietnam has ever produced.

**First: brokers (securities/financial services).** The securities sector led, and led hard — roughly **+110% in 2021**, against an index up about +36% that year. Why brokers first? Because a broker is the *highest-beta bet on the market itself*. Brokers make money three ways that all explode in a bull: brokerage fees (on exploding turnover), margin-lending interest (on the tripling margin book), and proprietary trading gains (on their own holdings). Every one of those revenue lines is directly geared to *rising prices and rising volume* — exactly what a liquidity bull delivers. When you are bullish on liquidity itself, the purest expression is to own the businesses that sell liquidity: the brokers. They are the first to run and the highest to fly. (The full case for why brokers are the highest-beta sector on VN-Index is the dedicated post linked below.)

**Second: steel and basic resources.** Next to run was steel, roughly **+75% in 2021**. Steel is the classic early-to-mid-cycle cyclical: its earnings are levered to construction activity, public-investment infrastructure spending, and global steel prices, all of which were turning up as the world reopened and Vietnam pushed public investment to support the recovery. Steel is high-beta but for a *fundamental* reason (commodity-price and volume operating leverage) rather than a purely flow reason — so it tends to lead once the recovery story has legs, just behind the pure liquidity plays.

**Third: property (real estate).** Property came next. Real estate is rate-sensitive (cheaper mortgages and cheaper developer funding), it is a natural beneficiary of the search for yield (land as an inflation hedge in the Vietnamese mindset), and it tends to lead the *mid* part of the cycle. Property also has the largest index weight among the cyclicals, so when it moves it drags the index meaningfully. The property run in 2020-2021 was real, but it also planted the seeds of the 2022 crisis: developers funded the boom heavily with corporate bonds (*trái phiếu doanh nghiệp*), and that leverage became the fault line a year later.

**Alongside, the banks: the heavyweight that lifted the index level.** A word on the banks, because they sit oddly in the sequence. Banking is the largest sector by index weight on VN-Index — the "heartbeat" of the index — but in 2021 the bank sub-index returned only about **+28%**, roughly in line with the index, far behind brokers and steel. Banks are *moderate* beta: they benefit from a bull (more credit, fee income, trading gains, lower provisioning as the economy recovers) but they are too large and too regulated to triple. Their role in the bull was not to lead but to *carry the index level* — because of their enormous weight, the banks' steady +28% did more to lift the *headline VN-Index number* than the brokers' +110% did, even though the brokers were the better trade. This is a crucial distinction for a sector trader: the sector that moves the *index* most is not the sector that makes *you* the most money. The index is a weighted average; your portfolio is whatever you chose to own. Owning the high-beta leaders beat owning the heavyweight that merely tracked. (The banks' role as the index's heartbeat is its own deep dive in this series.)

**Fourth: the penny midcaps (speculative *sóng ngành*).** The final, frothiest leg of the bull was the explosion of small and mid-cap penny stocks — the speculative *sóng ngành* (sector waves) where narrative and momentum, not fundamentals, set prices. By late 2021, with the high-quality leaders extended, the F0 money rotated down the quality curve into low-priced, high-story midcaps that could double on a rumor. This is *always* the last act of a liquidity bull: when the leaders are tired, the money does not leave, it descends into junk. The penny mania is the clearest top signal there is, because it means the marginal buyer has run out of good things to buy and is now buying anything that moves.

**Why this particular order?** The sequence — brokers, then steel, then property, then penny — is not arbitrary; it follows from combining the *beta ladder* with the *cycle logic* of which businesses benefit first. At the very trough, the only thing you actually know is that liquidity is turning up; the purest bet on *that* is the brokers, who sell liquidity, so they lead. As the recovery firms and you gain confidence the *real economy* is turning, the early cyclicals with the most operating leverage to that recovery — steel — run next. As rates stay low and the recovery matures into the mid-cycle, the big rate-sensitive, mid-cycle sector — property — takes the baton. And finally, when the quality names are all extended and the bull is old, the leftover money with nowhere good to go floods into junk — the penny mania. Each handoff corresponds to the market gaining confidence about a later, more fundamental part of the recovery story, which is exactly why the order is *forecastable*: you are watching confidence migrate from "liquidity is turning" to "the economy is turning" to "rates will stay low" to "there is nothing left to buy but stories."

#### Worked example: broker amplification, the high-beta payoff

Here is why the *order* matters for your money. In 2021, VN-Index returned about **+36%**. A broker stock returned roughly **+110%**. The ratio is 110 / 36 ≈ **3.05x** — owning the leading sector amplified the index move roughly three-fold. Put numbers on it: invest **200 million dong** (~\$7,900) in an index tracker at the start of 2021 and you end the year with ~272 million dong (a +72 million dong gain). Invest the same 200 million in a basket of brokers and you end with ~420 million dong (a +220 million dong, ~\$8,700, gain) — roughly *three times* the profit on the same capital and the same year. This is the high-beta payoff for being early and correct about the liquidity regime. The catch, of course, is symmetric: the same 3x beta that tripled your gain on the way up roughly triples your loss on the way down — which is precisely what happened to anyone still holding brokers into the 2022 crash. High beta is a tool you point at the regime, not a permanent allocation.

## How the frameworks called it: three independent green lights

The reason this series spends so many posts on frameworks — the business cycle, the liquidity/margin gauge, relative strength, the financial chain — is that those frameworks would have put you in this bull, early, *while the news was still terrible*. Let us check each one against what actually happened, because a framework that only works in hindsight is worthless; the test is whether it fired *in real time* at the trough.

**The business cycle / investment clock said: early-cycle, go.** The investment clock framework reads the cycle through growth and inflation and tells you which sectors lead in each phase. In Q2 2020 the readings were unambiguous: rates were being *cut* aggressively, growth was bottoming, and policy was maximally supportive — the textbook definition of the *early-cycle* phase that follows a recession trough. The early-cycle playbook says: own the rate-sensitive, high-beta recovery leaders — financials (brokers and banks) and early cyclicals (steel). That is *exactly* the rotation that played out. The clock was not guessing; it was reading the dials, and the dials said go. (See the early-cycle-leaders post for the full mapping.)

**The liquidity/margin gauge said: fuel is abundant and rising, size up.** The liquidity framework tells you to track ADTV, total margin balance, and the margin-to-equity ratio as a regime gauge. Through 2020 and into 2021, all three were *rising from a low base* — ADTV climbing off 4.7 trillion, margin climbing off 52 trillion, plenty of headroom before any structural ceiling. A rising-liquidity regime with headroom is a green light to be aggressive and to favor high-beta sectors, because the fuel that lifts them is plentiful and getting more so. The gauge said the tank was filling, not draining.

**Relative strength said: brokers are leading, follow the leader.** Relative strength (RS) measures which sectors are outperforming the index — leadership made visible. From mid-2020, the broker sector's RS line was turning up and pushing to new highs against VN-Index well before the penny mania. RS does not tell you *why* a sector leads; it just tells you *that* it leads, early and objectively. A trader watching RS would have seen brokers take leadership and would have followed, without needing a thesis. (The RS-reading post linked below shows exactly how to build and read these lines.)

![Framework convergence flowchart rate cut and margin up and broker relative strength leading to an early cycle green light](/imgs/blogs/case-study-2020-2021-liquidity-bull-sector-by-sector-6.png)

The figure above shows the convergence. Three independent signals — a rate cut (the cycle dial), a rising margin balance (the liquidity dial), and broker RS turning up (the leadership dial) — all pointed the same direction in mid-2020: **early-cycle green light, own the high-beta leaders.** The power of using multiple frameworks is exactly this convergence: any one signal can mislead, but when the cycle, the liquidity gauge, and relative strength *all* agree, the probability that you are reading the regime correctly is high. They agreed, loudly, at the trough — the hard part was acting on them while the headlines screamed the opposite.

#### Worked example: the exit signal

The frameworks did not just call the entry; they called the *exit*, and this is where most people failed. By late 2021, two of the three gauges had rolled over even as the index made new highs. First, the **margin balance hit its structural ceiling**: it had climbed to ~195 trillion dong and brokers' own capital limited how much more they could lend (regulators cap margin lending relative to broker equity), so the *flow* of new margin — the marginal fuel — was flattening even though the *level* was at a record. A liquidity engine runs on the *change* in fuel, not the level; when the change stops, the engine stalls. Second, **broker relative strength rolled over**: the very sector that had led the bull began *underperforming* the index in late 2021 — leadership was rotating into the frothy penny midcaps, the classic sign of a late-stage top. Put numbers on the warning: if your trim rule was "reduce high-beta exposure when margin growth stalls *and* the leading sector's RS rolls over," you would have started cutting in late 2021, near 1,450-1,500 — and avoided the bulk of the roughly **−40%** drawdown that followed into late 2022 (the index fell from ~1,530 to under 900). The lesson: the same gauges that scream "buy" at the trough whisper "trim" at the top, and the whisper is the one that protects the gains.

## Common misconceptions

The 2020-2021 bull spawned a set of comforting myths that hurt people in 2022. Each is worth correcting with a number.

**Misconception 1: "It was about earnings — the economy was booming."** It was not. Vietnam's 2020 GDP growth slowed sharply (to one of its lowest rates in decades), and aggregate corporate earnings were flat to down through much of 2020 as lockdowns disrupted everything. VN-Index *more than doubled* against that backdrop. If the bull had been about earnings, the index would have been flat. It doubled because the *price investors paid per dong of earnings* (the P/E multiple) expanded enormously, and multiple expansion is a *liquidity* phenomenon, not an earnings one. Believing it was earnings made people hold too long, because earnings looked fine right up until the liquidity left.

**Misconception 2: "Everyone got rich."** Most F0 investors did not, and many lost money. Here is the brutal arithmetic of a liquidity bull: the *index* doubled, but the average new account opened *during* the bull — many in the euphoric 2021 melt-up, near the top — bought extended leaders and frothy midcaps at high prices, often on margin, and then rode them down in 2022. The money in a bull is made by the people positioned *before* the crowd arrives; the crowd that arrives at the party pays for the drinks. The +127% index gain is a measure of the *opportunity*, not of the *typical investor's outcome*. The typical late-arriving, leveraged F0 account did far worse than the index, because it bought high and was margin-called low.

**Misconception 3: "A bull this strong would last for years."** It lasted under two. Liquidity bulls are *self-limiting* by construction: they run on rising fuel (rising ADTV, rising margin), and fuel cannot rise forever. Margin hits regulatory and capital ceilings; the supply of new F0 accounts exhausts; rates eventually have to rise again as the economy recovers and inflation returns. The very forces that powered the bull all had natural caps, and when they hit those caps simultaneously in early 2022, the bull did not just slow — it reversed, because the leverage that pyramided up had to unwind down. The strength of a liquidity bull is *inversely* related to its durability: the more violent the melt-up, the more leverage is stacked, and the harder the unwind.

**Misconception 4: "Buying the dip always works."** It worked spectacularly in 2020-2021 because every dip was bought by the rising tide of new liquidity. The exact same reflex got people destroyed in 2022, when each "dip" was the start of a leg down in a deleveraging cascade. "Buy the dip" is not a strategy; it is a *regime-dependent tactic* that works in a rising-liquidity regime and is suicidal in a falling-liquidity one. The frameworks exist precisely to tell you which regime you are in — and in early 2022 they had flipped from green to red while the dip-buyers were still running the 2021 playbook.

**Misconception 5: "The foreigners were buying, so it must be a quality bull."** The opposite was true: foreigners were net *sellers* through most of 2020 and 2021, dumping on the order of \$1bn-plus into the rally. Many domestic investors took false comfort from the idea that "smart money" foreign institutions were validating the move; they were not — they were exiting into retail strength. This is a recurring trap on VN-Index: the market can rip higher on pure domestic liquidity *while* foreigners sell, which means foreign flows are a *contrary* signal as often as a confirming one in a retail-driven bull. Do not outsource your conviction to what the foreigners are doing; in 2020-2021 they were wrong on direction precisely because they underestimated the domestic tide.

## What the playbook would have done

Now the payoff. Walk the bull again, but this time as a disciplined sector-rotation trader using the tools in this series. What does the playbook actually *do* at each stage?

**At the trough (Act 1, March-April 2020): position early-cycle, against the news.** The cycle dial reads early-cycle (rates being cut, growth bottoming). The liquidity gauge reads rising-from-a-low-base (ADTV and margin turning up with huge headroom). The playbook does not wait for confirmation from the headlines — by the time the news is good, the easy money is gone. It begins accumulating the early-cycle leaders: brokers first (highest beta on the liquidity story), then banks and steel. Position sizing leans into the high-beta names *because the regime supports them* — this is the one time you *want* maximum beta, when the cycle, liquidity, and (soon) RS all point up. The invalidation is clear: if the liquidity gauge fails to rise — if ADTV and margin keep falling — the thesis is wrong and you cut.

**Through the recovery and melt-up (Acts 2-3, 2020-2021): ride the rotation, do not fight it.** As brokers lead and then steel joins, the playbook *follows relative strength* rather than guessing. When RS shows brokers leading, you overweight brokers. When steel's RS turns up, you add steel. When property's RS turns up mid-bull, you rotate some capital there. The discipline is to hold the leaders while they lead and rotate into the next sector as leadership passes, *using RS as the objective referee* rather than your opinion. You let the winners run and you do not prematurely sell a leader just because it "feels high" — in a liquidity bull, things go higher than feels reasonable, and RS keeps you in until leadership actually rolls over.

**At the margin-balance ceiling (Act 4, late 2021-January 2022): trim, do not be a hero.** This is where the playbook earns its keep. When margin balances stall near their ceiling *and* the leading sector's RS rolls over *and* the speculative penny mania is in full bloom, the playbook *trims high-beta exposure and raises cash* — not all at once, but steadily, taking the table-money off as the signs accumulate. It does not try to pick the exact top; it sells *into strength* during the distribution, accepting that it will leave some upside on the table in exchange for not riding the −40% down. The penny mania in particular is treated as a *late-bull alarm*, not an invitation: when the money has descended into junk, the good part of the bull is over.

The discipline that makes this work is having the trim rules written down *in advance*. A workable rule set, decided cold before the euphoria, might be: cut high-beta exposure by a third when margin growth stalls for two consecutive months; cut another third when the leading sector's RS makes a lower high while the index makes a higher high (the breadth divergence); and cut the rest, into cash, when the penny mania is unmistakable and your own positions are extended on valuation. Notice this is *mechanical* — it does not require you to feel bearish or to call the top. It just requires you to honor the gauges you set up at the bottom. The reason to write it down is psychological: in the middle of a melt-up, when your account is at an all-time high and everyone is making money, every cell in your body will tell you to hold and add. The pre-committed rule is the only thing that overrides that, and it is the single most valuable habit a sector-rotation trader can build.

A note on what the playbook does *not* do, because over-trading kills returns too. It does not try to short the top, it does not exit fully on the first wobble, and it does not abandon the bull because a single gauge flickered. Through Acts 2 and 3, plenty of scary-looking pullbacks came and went; the playbook held through them because the *regime* gauges (rising liquidity, leadership intact) stayed green. The trim only began when *multiple* structural gauges flipped together. The art is distinguishing a *pullback within a bull* (buy or hold) from a *regime change* (trim), and the regime gauges — not the daily price action — are what tell them apart.

#### Worked example: what disciplined sizing saved

Make the playbook's value concrete. Suppose you ran **1 billion dong** (~\$39,400) through the whole bull. The buy-and-hold-everything approach: fully invested in high-beta leaders from the trough, you ride 660 → 1,500 (and your brokers do ~3x the index), so by January 2022 your 1 billion might be ~2.5-3 billion dong on the leveraged leaders — but you hold them all the way down through 2022 and give back the bulk of it, ending perhaps near ~1.5-1.7 billion after the −40%+ drawdown crushes the high-beta names hardest. The playbook approach: you ride the same way up, but when the trim signals fire in late 2021 you cut high-beta exposure from, say, 90% to 30% and raise ~1.5 billion dong to cash near the highs. You sacrifice maybe the last 5-10% of the melt-up, but you *keep* the gains through the crash, ending well above the buy-and-hold-everything investor — and, crucially, with dry powder to buy the *next* trough. The lesson: in a leveraged bull, the exit discipline is worth more than the entry timing, because the entry only made money you could lose, while the exit is what lets you keep it.

## The lessons

Strip the case study down to its repeatable core and three lessons remain. These are the ones to carry into the next cycle.

**Lesson 1: Liquidity leads; earnings follow (or don't).** The single biggest error a beginner makes is waiting for good earnings before buying. The 2020-2021 bull doubled the index *before* and largely *without* an earnings boom, on liquidity alone. Money moves prices in the short run; earnings only anchor them in the long run. Watch the money — ADTV, margin, account openings, rate cuts — and you will be early. Watch earnings and the news, and you will be late, because by the time those confirm, the liquidity that does the actual lifting is already in the price. The four-dial framework (rates, credit, FX, commodities) and the liquidity gauge exist to make you watch the money.

**Lesson 2: Rotation is the game, not stock-picking.** The dispersion across sectors in 2021 — securities +110% versus food and beverage +5% — was vastly larger than the dispersion you would typically achieve by picking individual stocks *within* a sector. Getting the *sector* right was worth more than getting the *stock* right. And the sectors led in a readable order (brokers → steel → property → penny), driven by beta and cycle logic, which means the rotation was *forecastable* with the frameworks. The whole point of thinking in *nhóm ngành* rather than single tickers is that the big money is in catching the right sector at the right phase, not in agonizing over which broker to buy once you know to buy brokers.

**Lesson 3: Exits matter more than entries in a leveraged bull.** Anyone could make money on the way up in 2020-2021; the index doubled and high-beta tripled, so being *anywhere* in the high-beta leaders worked. The people who *kept* the money were the ones who recognized the top signals — margin at its ceiling, leadership RS rolling over, penny mania peaking — and trimmed before the −40% unwind. The asymmetry is structural: leverage that pyramids up on the way to the top *must* unwind on the way down, and the same high-beta names that flew highest fall hardest. A great entry with no exit plan, in a leveraged bull, is just a round trip. Decide your trim rules *before* you are euphoric, because you will not invent discipline in the middle of a melt-up.

![Lessons matrix what happened versus the lesson for liquidity rotation margin ceiling and exits](/imgs/blogs/case-study-2020-2021-liquidity-bull-sector-by-sector-7.png)

The matrix above pins the four lessons to what actually happened. Liquidity led — the index doubled with flat earnings — so the lesson is *watch the money, not the news*. Rotation was the game — securities +110% versus food and beverage +5% — so the lesson is *get the sector right before the stock*. The margin ceiling capped the fuel near 195 trillion dong, so the lesson is *watch the change in fuel, not the level*. And exits mattered more than entries — the −40% unwind erased careless gains — so the lesson is *decide your trim rules before euphoria*. Each row is a fact from this bull on the left and a repeatable rule on the right; the rules are the part that carries to the next cycle.

There is a fourth, quieter lesson worth naming, because it underwrites the other three: **respect the structure of the market you are in.** VN-Index is a retail-dominated, margin-fueled, domestic-liquidity-driven market. That is not a bug to be wished away; it is the terrain. It means the moves are bigger than a developed-market intuition expects, the rotations cleaner, the tops sharper, and the unwinds faster. A strategy imported from a slow institutional market — buy quality, hold forever, ignore the noise — would have left enormous money on the table in 2021 and then given it all back in 2022. The strategy that *fits* VN-Index is the one this series teaches: think in sectors, read the liquidity regime, follow the leaders by relative strength, and have your exits decided before you need them. The terrain dictates the strategy, and the terrain here rewards the rotation player.

The 2020-2021 bull is the clearest case study Vietnam has given us of every framework in this series working together: the cycle dial called the early-cycle entry, the liquidity gauge tracked the fuel from full to empty, relative strength named the leaders in order, and the same gauges flipped to warn of the exit. The bull was not a mystery and it was not luck. It was liquidity-driven sector rotation, running its textbook sequence, readable in real time by anyone holding the right gauges — and the next one will run the same playbook, which is exactly why studying this one pays off.
