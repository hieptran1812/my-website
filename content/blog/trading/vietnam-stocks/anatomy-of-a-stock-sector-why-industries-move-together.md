On a quiet trading morning in early March 2024, the VN-Index opened flat and a casual observer might have assumed nothing was happening. By the close it was up almost 1%, a solid green day. But the green was not spread evenly across the board. If you scrolled the price tables, you would have seen something strange: nearly every bank ticker on the Ho Chi Minh exchange (HOSE) was up 2% to 4% — VCB, BID, CTG, TCB, MBB, VPB, all of them — while large swathes of the rest of the market barely twitched, and a few sectors were actually red. Steel was sleepy. Consumer names went nowhere. It was, in the language traders use, a *banking day*. The index rose not because "stocks went up" but because one industry caught fire and that one industry happens to weigh more than a third of the whole index.

This pattern is not a coincidence and it is not rare. Vietnamese market days have a strong tendency to be *sector days*: a day when banks run, or steel runs, or securities brokers run, while the rest of the tape is quiet. The companies inside a sector behave like a school of fish — turning together, accelerating together, scattering together. A reader who only watches individual tickers will find this baffling. Why would Vietcombank, a state-controlled giant, and a small private bank you have never heard of, move up the same 3% on the same day, when their managements have never met and their businesses are run completely differently?

The answer is the subject of this entire series, and this opening post lays the foundation. We are going to take a stock sector apart and look at the machinery inside — what binds the companies together, why that binding is so much stronger in some sectors than others, how Vietnam officially defines and labels sectors, why VN-Index is dominated by a tiny handful of them, and, most importantly, how to *use* this knowledge. By the end you should be able to look at any green or red day on VN-Index and ask the right first question: not "which stock moved?" but "which **sector** moved, and why?"

## Foundations: what a stock sector actually is

Let us start from absolute zero, because every term in this series is built on this one.

A **stock** is a fractional ownership slice of a single company. Own one share of Hoa Phat Group (ticker HPG) and you own a tiny piece of Vietnam's largest steelmaker. Own one share of Vietcombank (ticker VCB) and you own a tiny piece of Vietnam's most valuable bank. Each of these is one company, one business, one share price.

A **sector** is a *group* of companies that do fundamentally the same kind of business. All the banks form the banking sector. All the steelmakers and miners form the basic-resources sector. All the property developers form the real-estate sector. In Vietnamese, this grouping is called a *nhom nganh* — literally an "industry group." When market commentators on Vietnamese TV say "*nganh ngan hang*" (the banking industry) ran today, they mean the whole group of banks moved together.

So far this is just a filing system: you could sort companies by the first letter of their name, or by their city, or by the color of their logo. What makes a *sector* special — what makes it worth an entire series — is that companies sorted into the same sector do not just *look* similar, they *behave* similarly in the stock market. Their prices move together. That co-movement is the whole point, and the rest of this post explains where it comes from.

### Sector vs industry: the words people use loosely

In everyday talk, "sector" and "industry" are used interchangeably, and for our purposes that is fine. Formally there is a hierarchy: an *industry* is broad (Financials), and within it sit narrower *sectors* or *sub-industries* (Banks, Insurance, Securities brokers). For this series, when we say "the banking sector" we mean the co-moving group of bank stocks, and we will not fuss about whether the technically correct label is "industry" or "sector." The thing that matters is the group that moves together.

### How Vietnam classifies its stocks: the ICB system

Someone has to decide which company goes in which box, and they cannot do it by vibes. Vietnam's exchanges and the data providers that serve them (FiinTrade, the index committees behind VN-Index, most brokers' research desks) use the **ICB** system — the **Industry Classification Benchmark**. ICB is a global standard, the same framework used to classify stocks in London and other markets, adapted to Vietnam's listed universe.

ICB is a four-level ladder. At the top is the broadest bucket, the **Industry**. Below it is the **Supersector**. Below that is the **Sector** — this is the level that matters most for co-movement, the level at which sector indices are usually built. At the bottom is the individual company, the listed **ticker**. Every company on HOSE and the Hanoi exchange (HNX) is filed down this ladder exactly once.

Worked through two real examples: Vietcombank is filed under Industry = Financials, Supersector = Banks, Sector = Banks, and the ticker is VCB. Hoa Phat is filed under Industry = Basic Materials, Supersector = Basic Resources, Sector = Industrial Metals & Mining (which in the Vietnamese market is colloquially "thep," steel), ticker HPG. The figure below traces both ladders side by side so you can see how a broad industry narrows down to the single sector index that actually drives the stock's day-to-day moves.

![Tree diagram of ICB four-level classification ladder mapping VCB and HPG to their sectors](/imgs/blogs/anatomy-of-a-stock-sector-why-industries-move-together-3.png)

In practice, Vietnamese market data is usually presented at the level of roughly **19 ICB sectors** covering the listed market: Banks, Real Estate, Food & Beverage, Securities (brokers), Industrial Goods & Services, Basic Resources (steel and mining), Utilities, Oil & Gas, Retail, Information Technology, Chemicals, Construction & Materials, Insurance, Health Care, Travel & Leisure, Media, Automobiles & Parts, Personal & Household Goods, and Telecommunications. The exact count and naming vary a little between data vendors — one provider's "Construction & Materials" might be split where another lumps it — but the working number Vietnamese investors carry in their heads is "about 19 sectors," and the dominant handful is what we care about most.

### Where the stocks live: HOSE, HNX, and a word on the plumbing

Two terms will recur throughout the series, so let us pin them down now. **HOSE** (the Ho Chi Minh Stock Exchange) is the larger, more liquid of Vietnam's two main exchanges; it is home to VN-Index and to the heavyweight names — VCB, HPG, VIC, and most of the blue chips. **HNX** (the Hanoi Stock Exchange) is the smaller venue, with its own index, generally hosting smaller companies. There is also UPCoM, a third board for companies not yet fully listed. For sector analysis you treat the whole listed universe as one pool and sort it by ICB sector regardless of which board a name trades on, but it is worth knowing that the biggest, most sector-defining companies almost all sit on HOSE.

One more piece of plumbing matters because it shapes how co-movement plays out intraday: **T+2 settlement.** When you buy a Vietnamese stock today, the shares only land in your account two business days later — that is what "T+2" means — and you generally cannot sell them until they have settled. This rule slows down the feedback loop: a sector move cannot be fully ridden and exited within a single session the way it could in a same-day-settlement market, which tends to *stretch* sector moves over several days rather than compressing them into one. It is a small detail, but it is one reason Vietnamese sector trends often unfold as multi-day waves (*song nganh*, a sector "wave") rather than single-day spikes.

### How a stock actually gets assigned to a sector

The assignment is made by classification providers — the index committees and data vendors — based on where the company earns the *majority of its revenue*. A firm that makes most of its money lending is a bank; one that makes most of its money selling steel is a basic-resources name. The "majority of revenue" rule is simple in principle and messy in practice, because plenty of Vietnamese companies are conglomerates that earn meaningful revenue across several sectors. When that happens, the classification picks the single largest revenue line and files the whole company there — which is exactly why ICB labels can mislead for conglomerates, a trap we return to in the misconceptions section. For the large, focused companies that dominate VN-Index, though, the assignment is clean and stable: VCB is unambiguously a bank, HPG is unambiguously steel, and their sector membership tells you almost everything about how their stocks will trade.

### Sector indices: how the group's move is measured

When someone says "the banking sector was up 2.5% today," they are quoting a **sector index** — a market-cap-weighted average of the prices of all the banks in the group, exactly the way VN-Index itself is a market-cap-weighted average of all the stocks on HOSE. ("Market-cap-weighted" simply means a company counts in proportion to its total market value: a \$19bn bank moves the bank index far more than a \$1bn bank.) A sector index is the heartbeat of the group. When we talk about "the sector moving," this is the number we mean, and learning to read whether a sector index is outperforming or underperforming VN-Index is a skill we develop across the whole series.

So a sector is: a group of companies, doing the same kind of business, filed into the same ICB box, whose collective price is tracked by a sector index. The mystery is why that collective price moves as one. To that we now turn.

## Why companies in a sector move together: the three shared forces

Here is the central claim of this series, stated plainly: **companies in the same sector co-move because they share three things — one demand driver, one discount rate, and one narrative.** Each of these forces, on its own, would pull the group's prices in the same direction. Together they make a sector behave like a single organism. Let us take them one at a time, using a running contrast between two very different Vietnamese sectors — banks and steel — to keep it concrete.

### Force 1: the shared demand driver (the same earnings engine)

A stock's price, stripped to its essence, is the market's guess at the company's future profits. If two companies' profits are driven by the *same underlying force*, then any news about that force moves both companies' expected profits the same way — and so their prices move together.

Banks are the cleanest example in Vietnam. A bank's core profit engine is **credit growth** — how much new lending it does — multiplied by its **net interest margin** (NIM), the gap between the interest rate it charges borrowers and the rate it pays depositors. (NIM is one of the most important numbers in this whole series; remember it as "the bank's gross profit margin on lending.") Every bank in Vietnam lends into the *same economy*. When the State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) loosens its grip and lets total system credit grow faster — when it raises the **credit ceiling**, the annual cap on how much each bank may grow its loan book — *every* bank gets to lend more. When the property market is booming and developers and homebuyers want mortgages, *every* bank with property exposure benefits. The demand driver is shared, so the earnings surprise is shared, so the price move is shared.

Steel is just as clean but driven by a completely different force. A steelmaker's profit engine is **construction and manufacturing demand** — tonnes of steel sold — multiplied by the **spread** between the steel price and the cost of iron ore and coking coal. When Vietnam is in a building boom (infrastructure spending, a property construction wave), steel volumes rise for HPG and for every smaller steel name at once. When global steel prices spike or ore costs collapse, the spread widens for the whole group simultaneously. Different driver from banks, but the same logic: one shared engine, one shared earnings surprise, one shared price move.

This is why a "banking day" and a "steel day" are usually *different* days. Banks respond to credit and rates; steel responds to construction and commodity spreads. The two engines run on different fuel, so the two sectors light up at different times — which is the entire foundation of *sector rotation*, the subject of much of this series.

#### Worked example: two banks, one rate cut

Suppose the SBV cuts its policy rate by 50 basis points (0.50%). Lower policy rates feed through to cheaper funding for banks and, over time, faster credit growth — both good for bank earnings. But the cleaner, faster effect is on **valuation**, through the discount rate (Force 2, below). Take two very different banks. Vietcombank is a giant: roughly 500 trillion VND of market value, which at the mid-2026 exchange rate of \$1 = 25,400 VND is about \$19.7bn. A mid-cap bank might be worth 30 trillion VND, about \$1.2bn — sixteen times smaller. Their managements, balance sheets, and customer bases are nothing alike.

Yet a back-of-envelope valuation model values both as a stream of future profits discounted at a rate tied to the policy rate. If that discount rate falls from, say, 13% to 12.5%, the present value of a long stream of profits rises by a roughly similar *percentage* for both — call it about +4% each, holding everything else fixed. So on the day of the cut, both the \$19.7bn giant and the \$1.2bn mid-cap pop a few percent, in the same direction, by a similar magnitude, for the same reason. **The intuition: a shared discount rate means a single macro lever re-prices the entire sector in one motion, no matter how different the individual companies are.**

### Force 2: the shared discount rate (the same valuation lever)

That worked example smuggled in the second force, so let us make it explicit. To value any company, you take its expected future profits and *discount* them back to today — future money is worth less than money now, and the rate at which you mark it down is the **discount rate**. That discount rate is built on top of the economy-wide interest rate, which the central bank controls. When rates fall, every future profit becomes worth more today, so almost every stock's fair value rises; when rates rise, the reverse.

Crucially, *the same interest rate discounts every company in a sector at the same moment.* There is one SBV policy rate, one set of Vietnamese government bond yields, one cost of money for the whole market. When that lever moves, it moves under all the banks at once, all the property developers at once, all the brokers at once. Sectors whose value is especially sensitive to the discount rate — **long-duration** sectors, where most of the profit is expected years in the future, like real estate and high-growth technology — swing the most when rates move. Sectors whose profits are nearer-term and more stable, like utilities and consumer staples, swing less. But within any one sector, the rate move hits every member, which adds another layer of co-movement on top of the shared demand driver.

There is a subtle but important consequence here: **two different sectors can move in opposite directions on the same rate news.** A rate cut is unambiguously good for the present value of a property developer's far-future profits, so real estate often rallies on cuts. But a rate cut can *compress* a bank's NIM if loan rates fall faster than deposit rates, so banks sometimes wobble on the very same news. Same lever, opposite sign, because the two sectors' earnings respond differently. We will see this tension play out repeatedly across the series.

This is also why the *direction* of a rate move tells you which sectors to favor, not just whether to be bullish or bearish overall. A falling-rate regime tends to reward long-duration, credit-hungry sectors — property, growth, brokers — because their value is most leveraged to the discount rate and to cheap money. A rising-rate regime tends to reward the opposite: cash-rich, short-duration, defensive sectors that do not depend on borrowing to grow, and that may even *earn more* on their cash as rates climb. The discount rate is therefore not just a level that lifts or sinks everything together; it is a *rotation engine* that quietly reshuffles which sectors lead. Reading the rate trend is, in large part, reading the next sector to lead — which is exactly the link between the macro posts and the sector posts later in this series.

### Force 3: the shared narrative and flows (the same buyers)

The third force is not about fundamentals at all — it is about *who is doing the buying*. Large investors in Vietnam — domestic funds, foreign institutions, exchange-traded funds (ETFs) that track Vietnamese indices — increasingly trade *themes*, not individual tickers. A foreign fund that wants exposure to "Vietnam's banking growth story" does not agonize over VCB versus CTG; it buys a basket of the big banks in one program trade. An ETF that tracks a Vietnam index, when it receives a fresh \$50mn inflow, must buy every constituent in proportion, lifting the whole index — and disproportionately the heaviest sectors — in a single coordinated push.

This flow-driven co-movement is amplified by **narrative**. Markets run on stories: "banks are cheap and credit is reopening," "steel is bottoming as construction returns," "the property market is finally clearing its bond problems." When a narrative takes hold, money chases the *sector*, and the buying pressure lifts the laggards along with the leaders, because the buyers are not discriminating finely between names — they are expressing a view on the group. This is why even a weak bank can ride a banking rally: the tide of theme-driven flow lifts it whether it deserves it or not.

In Vietnam specifically, two flow channels matter enormously and we will return to them throughout the series. The first is **foreign room** (*room ngoai*) — the regulatory cap on how much of a Vietnamese company foreigners may collectively own. When foreigners pile into a sector, the most-wanted names hit their room limit and the flow spills over into the rest of the group, spreading the move. The second is **margin** (*ky quy*) — borrowed money that domestic retail investors use to buy stocks. When margin balances across the brokerage system swell, that leverage tends to flood into whatever sector the current narrative favors, exaggerating its move both up and, brutally, down.

So: one demand driver, one discount rate, one narrative-and-flow. Three independent forces, each pushing the members of a sector in the same direction at the same time. Stack them and you get co-movement strong enough that a Vietnamese trading day is better described as a *sector day* than a *market day*.

### Why the three forces are not independent at all

There is a deeper layer worth surfacing, because it explains why sector co-movement is so much *stronger* than you would naively expect from three separate forces. The three forces are not independent — they *reinforce* each other, which is why a sector move, once it starts, tends to snowball.

Trace the chain. Suppose the SBV cuts rates (the discount-rate force fires). Lower rates make banks' future profits worth more *today* (valuation), and over the following months they also encourage faster credit growth, which lifts banks' actual *earnings* (the demand-driver force fires too, with a lag). Meanwhile, the moment the rate cut is announced, the *narrative* shifts — "the easing cycle has begun, banks are the play" — and theme-driven money starts rotating into the sector (the flow force fires). One macro event lit all three fuses at once. The forces are correlated by construction: the same macro lever that moves the discount rate also moves the demand driver and the narrative, because in a credit-driven economy like Vietnam's, rates, lending, and sentiment are three faces of the same cycle.

This is the mechanical reason a "banking day" is rarely a one-day affair. The valuation re-rate happens instantly; the earnings improvement and the flow build over weeks. So a genuine sector move has a *front edge* (the snap re-rate on the news) and a *body* (the grinding follow-through as earnings confirm and flows accumulate). Learning to tell a real sector trend — one with all three forces engaged — from a one-day flash that fades is a core skill of the rotation playbook later in this series.

### How the three forces differ across sectors

The three forces apply to *every* sector, but their *weights* differ, and that difference is what gives each sector its personality. Some sectors are valuation-driven (the discount-rate force dominates); some are demand-driven (the earnings engine dominates); some are flow-and-narrative driven (sentiment dominates). Knowing which force dominates a given sector tells you what to watch.

- **Banks** are roughly balanced between the demand driver (credit growth, NIM) and the discount rate (rate-sensitive funding costs and valuations), with a strong flow component because they are the heaviest, most-traded sector and the default vehicle for foreign "Vietnam growth" money.
- **Real estate** is the most *discount-rate-sensitive* sector in Vietnam, because a developer's value is dominated by far-future profits from projects not yet sold; a small change in the rate used to discount those distant cash flows swings the valuation a lot. Property is also acutely sensitive to *credit availability*, since both developers and homebuyers run on borrowed money. That double rate-sensitivity is why property is the high-beta, high-drama end of the Vietnamese market.
- **Steel and basic resources** are the most *demand-driven*: the swing factor is construction tonnage and the commodity spread, which march to a global and construction-cycle drum that is largely independent of Vietnamese interest rates. Steel can rally while banks are flat and vice versa precisely because its dominant force is a different one.
- **Consumer staples and utilities** are the most *defensive*: stable near-term earnings, low rate-sensitivity, low beta. Their dominant characteristic is the *absence* of a strong cyclical force, which is exactly why money rotates *into* them when investors want to hide from the cycle.

Carry this mental table with you. When you look at a sector, ask "which of the three forces is the dominant one here?" — and then watch that force. For property, watch rates and credit. For steel, watch construction and spreads. For banks, watch all three. This is the difference between memorizing a list of sectors and actually understanding how each one breathes.

## Co-movement vs correlation: what "the sector beta" really means

We have been using the word "co-movement" loosely. Let us sharpen it, because the precise version is a tool you will use constantly.

**Correlation** is a statistical measure, between −1 and +1, of how reliably two things move in the same direction. Two stocks with a correlation of +0.9 almost always move together; a correlation near 0 means their moves are unrelated; −0.9 means they reliably move *opposite*. Within a Vietnamese sector, pairwise correlations between member stocks are typically high — often 0.6 to 0.85 over daily returns — precisely because of the three shared forces. Across *different* sectors, correlations are lower. That gap is the statistical fingerprint of a sector.

**Beta** is a related but distinct idea: it measures how much a stock moves *for each 1% the market (or the sector) moves*. A bank with a beta of 1.3 to the banking index tends to move 1.3% when the bank index moves 1%. A "sector beta" decomposition splits any stock's move into two parts: the part it shares with its sector (the tide) and the part unique to itself (the swimmer). For most stocks in a strongly co-moving sector, the sector component dominates day to day — which is the quantitative heart of why this whole series exists. If 70% of a bank stock's daily variance comes from the banking sector index, then most of the time you are, whether you realize it or not, *making a bet on the sector* when you buy that stock.

It is worth being precise about why high correlation and the cap-weighting of the index are *not the same thing*, because beginners conflate them. Cap-weighting is about how much a stock *counts in the index*; correlation is about how *reliably stocks move together*. A sector could be heavily weighted but internally incoherent (its members scattered by different drivers), or lightly weighted but tightly co-moving (a small, homogeneous group). Vietnamese banks happen to be *both* heavily weighted and tightly co-moving, which is the worst possible combination for an index investor hoping for diversification: when banks have a bad day, a large slice of the index falls, and it falls *together*, with no internal offset. Diversification only protects you when the things you own move *independently*; inside a strongly co-moving sector, owning five banks instead of one barely reduces your risk, because the five share the same tide. This is one of the most underappreciated facts about the Vietnamese market: holding "a diversified basket of bank stocks" is far less diversified than it sounds, because you have bought five copies of the same sector bet.

The flip side is the opportunity. Because cross-sector correlations are *lower* than within-sector correlations, real diversification in Vietnam comes from holding *different sectors*, not different stocks. A portfolio of one bank, one steel name, one consumer staple, and one utility is genuinely more diversified than a portfolio of four banks, even though the second one "owns more stocks," because the four sectors march to different drivers. This is the practical, money-saving consequence of understanding co-movement: it tells you that *the unit of diversification is the sector, not the stock.*

### Intra-sector dispersion: the swimmer inside the tide

Co-movement does not mean *identical* movement. Inside every sector there is **dispersion** — leaders that run ahead and laggards that drag. The sector tide lifts all boats, but some boats are faster. In Vietnamese banking the dispersion axes are well known: state-owned banks (VCB, BID, CTG) versus private banks (TCB, VPB, MBB, ACB); high-NIM lenders versus thin-margin ones; clean loan books versus those nursing problem property loans. A reader who understands both the tide *and* the swimmer can do better than just buying the sector: pick the right sector for the *macro* (the co-movement bet) and then the right leader *within* it (the dispersion bet).

#### Worked example: the same tide, two different swimmers

Imagine a banking rally where the bank sector index rises 10% over a quarter. A high-beta private bank with a sector beta of 1.4 might rise about 14%; a defensive state-owned giant with a beta of 0.8 might rise about 8%. Both went up — the tide lifted both — but the gap between them is 6 percentage points, which on a 100 million VND (about \$3,900) position is roughly 6 million VND, about \$236, of difference from the *dispersion* alone. **The intuition: getting the sector right is most of the battle, but within a strong sector the choice of leader versus laggard still adds or subtracts a meaningful slice of return.**

Notice the figure below in the next section makes the same point at the index level — but first we need to understand the shape of VN-Index itself, because that shape is what makes sector selection the single most powerful decision a Vietnamese investor makes.

## The shape of VN-Index: why banks and real estate dominate

VN-Index is the headline index of the Ho Chi Minh exchange, and like most national indices it is **market-cap-weighted**: each company's influence on the index is proportional to its total market value. This single design choice has an enormous consequence, because Vietnam's listed market value is wildly concentrated in a few sectors.

The chart below shows the approximate sector weights. Banks are around 34% of VN-Index market capitalization. Real estate is around 17%. Together those two sectors are roughly **51% — more than half the entire index.** Add the next tier — food & beverage, securities brokers, industrial goods, basic resources (steel), utilities — and you have accounted for the overwhelming majority of the index, leaving the remaining dozen-plus sectors to split a thin slice.

![Horizontal bar chart of VN-Index sector weights showing banks and real estate above half](/imgs/blogs/anatomy-of-a-stock-sector-why-industries-move-together-2.png)

For an index investor — anyone holding a fund or ETF that tracks VN-Index — this concentration is the most important fact about the product they own. When you buy "the Vietnamese stock market," you are, mathematically, buying a portfolio that is roughly one-third banks and one-sixth property. Your returns will be dominated by what happens to those two sectors. A great year for Vietnamese technology or retail will barely register in your index return if banks are flat; a bad year for banks will sink the index almost regardless of how everything else does. This is not a flaw — it reflects the real economic weight of those sectors in Vietnam — but it means *an index investor in Vietnam is, like it or not, primarily a banks-and-property investor.*

#### Worked example: sector-weight arithmetic on an "up" day

Here is the mechanism behind a banking day, with the actual arithmetic. Suppose on a given day banks (34% weight) rise 3%, while every other sector is exactly flat (0%). The index move is the weighted average of the sector moves:

```
index move = 0.34 * (+3%) + 0.66 * (0%)
           = +1.02% + 0
           = +1.02%
```

So a 3% move in a single sector — banks — drags the whole VN-Index up more than 1%, with literally nothing else participating. That is the "banking day" from the opening anecdote, reduced to one line of arithmetic. Now flip it: if banks fall 3% and everything else is flat, the index drops about 1% and the financial press writes "stocks fell" — even though two-thirds of the market did nothing. **The intuition: because of cap weighting, a move in the heaviest sector masquerades as a move in "the market," so reading the index without reading its sectors will routinely mislead you.**

#### Worked example: putting a Vietnamese market cap on a global scale

Vietnamese figures are quoted in dong, and the numbers are huge, so a global reader needs an anchor. The mid-2026 rate is about \$1 = 25,400 VND. Vietcombank, the largest bank, carries a market value of roughly 500 trillion VND. Convert it: 500,000,000,000,000 / 25,400 is about 19.7 billion, so VCB is around \$19.7bn — a genuinely large company, comparable in scale to a mid-sized regional bank anywhere in Asia. Now take a typical mid-cap bank at about 30 trillion VND: 30,000,000,000,000 / 25,400 is about \$1.2bn. The giant is roughly sixteen times the mid-cap. Yet both are *banks*, both ride the same sector tide, and both will pop on the same rate cut. The whole banking sector's market value, summed, is what produces that 34% slice of VN-Index. **The intuition: a handful of \$10bn-plus banks, converted to dollars, makes the absolute scale concrete — and explains why the banking sector alone can steer an entire \$200bn-plus market.**

There is a second-order point hiding here for the index investor. Because banks and real estate are so heavy, *their valuations effectively set the valuation of the whole index.* When commentators say "VN-Index is cheap at a P/E of 12" (P/E, the price-to-earnings ratio, is the price you pay per unit of annual profit), they are mostly saying *banks and property are cheap*, because those two sectors dominate the aggregate earnings. If you want to understand whether the index is expensive or cheap, you have to understand whether its two biggest sectors are — which, again, is sector thinking, not stock thinking.

We have argued that same-sector stocks co-move and cross-sector stocks do not. The chart below shows it directly with a stylized illustration: two bank-like price paths (a VCB-like and a CTG-like line) tracking each other almost step for step, against a steel-like path that wanders off on its own schedule. These are not historical quotes — they are a clean illustration of the *pattern* — but the pattern is real and you will see it on live charts constantly once you start looking for it.

Look at the two bank lines: they dip together in month 3, rally together into month 5, pull back together, and grind up together to the end. The steel line, meanwhile, surges early while the banks are quiet, then collapses while the banks rally, then recovers on its own timing. Same exchange, same currency, same economy — but a different demand driver and a different cycle phase, so a different path. That single picture is the thesis of this series rendered as a chart.

## Sector vs single stock: why the sector usually matters more

Now we arrive at the practical payoff of everything above. There are two broad ways to choose a stock. **Bottom-up** investing starts with the individual company — its management, its products, its balance sheet — and picks the best businesses regardless of sector. **Top-down** investing starts with the big picture — the economic cycle, interest rates, which sector the macro favors — picks the right *sector* first, and only then chooses a stock within it.

Both have merit, and the best investors blend them. But there is a structural reason top-down deserves to come *first*, especially in a strongly co-moving market like Vietnam: **most of your return, most of the time, is determined by the sector you are in, not the specific stock you picked inside it.** If 70% of a stock's daily variance is its sector component, then choosing the wrong sector is an error you cannot stock-pick your way out of. The best-run steel company in Vietnam will still fall in a steel downturn; a mediocre bank will still rise in a banking bull market. The sector is the current; the stock is your stroke. You can swim hard, but you cannot beat the current by much.

This does not mean stock selection is worthless — the dispersion is real money, as the worked example above showed, and in a sideways market for the sector, the swimmer is *all* you have. But it means the *order of operations* matters: get the sector right first, because it is the larger and more reliable lever, and refine with stock selection second.

#### Worked example: top-down vs bottom-up payoff

Two investors each put 200 million VND (about \$7,900 at \$1 = 25,400 VND) to work for a year. Investor A is top-down: she identifies that the macro favors banks, buys a simple basket of the leading banks, and the banking sector rises 25% on the year. Her position grows by about 50 million VND, to roughly 250 million VND — about \$9,840, a gain near \$1,970. Investor B is bottom-up: he finds what he believes is a brilliantly managed small company, but it sits in a sector the cycle is working against, and despite the company executing well the sector falls 8% on the year and drags his stock down 5%. His 200 million VND shrinks to about 190 million VND, a loss of roughly 10 million VND, about \$394. The gap between them is around 60 million VND — about \$2,360 — and almost none of it came from how good the companies were. **The intuition: being in the right sector with an average stock beat being in the wrong sector with a great stock, because the sector tide was larger than the stock-picking edge.**

## Common misconceptions

Sector thinking is powerful, but it is easy to over-apply or misunderstand. Here are the four traps that catch beginners most often, each corrected with a number.

**Myth 1: "Stocks in a sector always move together."** They move together *more than chance*, not *in lockstep*. Pairwise correlations inside a Vietnamese sector are typically 0.6–0.85 — high, but not 1.0. On any given day a sector leader can be up 4% while a laggard in the same sector is flat or even red, because of the dispersion we discussed (a bad earnings report, a specific scandal, a stock-specific catalyst). Co-movement is a strong *tendency*, not a law. Treat "the sector moved" as the base case and always check whether your specific stock actually participated.

**Myth 2: "The biggest sector is the safest."** Size is not safety. Banks are 34% of VN-Index, but that weight makes the *index* more fragile, not the banks safer — it means a problem in banking (a bad-debt cycle, a NIM squeeze) hits more than a third of the index at once. In the 2022 corporate-bond crisis, the heaviest sectors (property, and the banks exposed to property debt) were precisely the ones that fell hardest and dragged VN-Index down roughly 33% from its peak. The biggest sector is the biggest *concentration of risk*, not a safe harbor.

**Myth 3: "A cheap sector is automatically a buy."** A sector trading at a low P/E or P/B (price-to-book, the price you pay per unit of net assets) is often cheap *for a reason* — the market is pricing in falling earnings ahead. Vietnamese banks sometimes trade near a P/B of 1.0 or below not because they are a bargain but because investors fear a coming bad-debt wave that will erode book value. "Cheap" is a starting question, not an answer; you have to ask *why* it is cheap and whether the cycle is about to make it cheaper. A falling knife in a whole sector is still a falling knife.

**Myth 4: "ICB labels are precise and objective."** The boundaries are fuzzier than they look. A conglomerate that does property *and* retail *and* manufacturing gets filed under one ICB sector, but its stock may co-move with several. Vingroup-style conglomerates are the classic Vietnamese headache: the label says one thing, the business is many things, and the co-movement does not respect the box. Always sanity-check whether a stock *actually* trades like its label says it should, rather than trusting the classification blindly.

The common thread running through all four myths is the same error: treating a *tendency* as a *certainty*. Sectors co-move, but not perfectly; the biggest sector concentrates risk, it does not dispel it; cheapness is a question, not an answer; labels are a useful first cut, not ground truth. The reader who holds sector thinking as a *strong prior* — the default expectation, to be confirmed or overridden by the specifics of the day and the stock — uses it correctly. The reader who treats it as a mechanical rule will eventually be punished by the exception. Used as a prior, sector thinking is the most reliable edge a Vietnamese investor has; used as a law, it becomes a way to be confidently wrong.

## How it shows up on VN-Index

Theory is cheap. Here are two concrete patterns, drawn from how the Vietnamese market actually behaves, that show sector co-movement carrying — or sinking — the whole index.

### Case 1: banks carrying the index

Because banks are about 34% of VN-Index, there are long stretches where *the index is the banks and the banks are the index.* In the 2020–2021 bull market, as the SBV held rates low and credit growth ran hot, the banking sector led VN-Index from below 700 to over 1,500 — and on the strongest up days, the banks alone supplied the bulk of the index's gain while broad swathes of the market merely tagged along. An investor who, in early 2021, simply recognized "banks are leading, money is flowing into banks, the credit cycle is loose" and bought a basket of the big banks captured most of the index's move without ever doing detailed single-stock work. The sector call *was* the trade.

The reverse is equally instructive. When the banking sector stalls — when credit-ceiling worries or bad-debt fears set in — VN-Index struggles to make progress no matter how well smaller sectors do, because a flat 34% of the index is a heavy anchor. You can have a wonderful year in Vietnamese technology or retail and watch the headline index go nowhere because banks sat out. Reading "are the banks participating?" is, more than any other single question, how you read whether a VN-Index rally has legs.

The arithmetic of why this is so unforgiving is worth spelling out. For VN-Index to gain 10% with banks flat, the *other 66%* of the market would have to rise about 15% on its own to do the work banks are not doing — a heroic ask, since that "other 66%" includes plenty of sleepy, defensive sectors that rarely move 15% in a stretch. So in practice a flat banking sector caps how far the index can run, almost mechanically. Conversely, a 10% rise in banks alone, with everything else flat, already delivers about a 3.4% index gain (0.34 × 10%) for free. The heaviest sector is the index's accelerator and its brake at the same time. This is why seasoned VN-Index watchers glance at the bank sector index *first* every morning: it is the single most informative number about where the headline is likely headed that day.

### Case 2: a steel up-cycle dragging the whole materials group

The second pattern shows co-movement *inside* a smaller sector. When Vietnam's construction cycle turns up and global steel spreads widen, the steel sector lights up as a group. HPG, the giant, leads, but the smaller steel and basic-resources names — the ones most retail investors could not name — rise right alongside it, because the *shared demand driver* (construction tonnage) and the *shared spread* (steel price minus ore cost) lifted the whole group's earnings outlook at once. In a strong steel up-cycle, it is common to see the entire basic-resources sector index outrun VN-Index for months, with HPG and a clutch of lesser names all posting big gains in a tight cluster. A trader who spotted "construction is turning, steel spreads are widening" and bought the *group* would have ridden the co-movement; one who agonized over picking the single best steel stock would have captured most of the same move with far more effort, because the tide did the heavy lifting.

There is a useful contrast buried in these two cases. The banking case shows a *heavy* sector whose co-movement matters because of its sheer index weight — it moves the whole market. The steel case shows a *light* sector (around 4% of the index) whose co-movement matters not for the index but for the *trader who is in it* — the basic-resources sector can have a roaring up-cycle that barely nudges VN-Index, yet hands a focused investor a large gain. This is a crucial distinction the rest of the series develops: a sector can be important to the *index* (banks, real estate) or important to *your return* (any sector you happen to overweight), and those are not the same thing. The index investor cares mostly about the heavy sectors; the active sector-rotator cares about whichever sector is *leading right now*, heavy or light. Both, however, are practicing sector thinking — they are just applying it to different ends.

Both cases teach the same lesson from opposite ends of the size spectrum: the sector is the unit of motion. Whether it is the 34% banking behemoth steering the entire index, or a 4% steel sector moving as a tight pack, *the group moves first and the index (or your portfolio) follows.*

## The playbook: how to use sector thinking

This is the payoff, and it is the workflow that the rest of the series fills in. Sector thinking is not a trade by itself — it is the *first layer* of analysis, the lens you look through before you ever pull up a single company's financials. Here is the top-down checklist, in order.

**Step 1 — Read the macro regime.** Start with the biggest dials: Is the SBV easing or tightening? Is the credit ceiling being raised (more lending capacity) or held tight? Is the policy rate falling (good for long-duration sectors like property and growth) or rising (good for short-duration, cash-rich, and value sectors)? Is the economy early-cycle (favor financials, cyclicals) or late-cycle (favor defensives, real assets)? You do not need to forecast the macro perfectly — you need to know roughly *which way the wind is blowing*, because that determines which sectors have the wind at their backs.

**Step 2 — Pick the sector the regime favors.** Translate the macro read into a sector tilt. Loosening credit and falling rates? Banks and real estate get the tailwind. A construction and infrastructure build cycle? Steel, construction, and materials. A consumption recovery? Retail and food & beverage. This is where the shared-demand-driver knowledge from earlier in this post earns its keep: you are matching the macro driver to the sector whose earnings engine runs on that driver.

**Step 3 — Confirm leadership before you commit.** A sector you *like* fundamentally is not enough; you need the sector to actually be *leading*. Pull up the sector index versus VN-Index: is the sector outperforming the index (rising relative strength) or lagging it? Is money actually flowing in — are foreign flows and margin balances pointing into this sector? A great fundamental story with no relative strength and no flow is a value trap in waiting. **No relative strength, no trade.** This relative-strength confirmation step is important enough that a later post in this series is devoted entirely to reading which sector leads VN-Index.

**Step 4 — Only now pick the stock.** Inside the leading, confirmed sector, choose the best individual name: the bank with the highest NIM and cleanest book, the steelmaker with the best cost position, the developer with the most presold inventory (*presales* = units sold but not yet handed over and booked as revenue — a forward indicator of a developer's future earnings). This is the dispersion bet, and it is where bottom-up work finally enters — *after* the sector decision, not before.

**Step 5 — Size and manage the risk to the sector thesis.** Position for the sector beta (a high-beta name in a sector you are confident in; a defensive name if you want the sector exposure with less swing). Set your stop where the *sector thesis* breaks, not at an arbitrary stock-level number — if the reason you own the stock is "the banking sector is leading," then your invalidation is "the banking sector stops leading," and you should be watching the sector index, not just your ticker. And cap any single-name weight so a stock-specific blow-up cannot sink the whole thesis.

Every later post slots into one of these steps: the macro posts feed Step 1, the individual sector deep-dives feed Step 2, the relative-strength and rotation posts feed Step 3, and so on.

**The three mistakes that wreck the top-down approach.** Knowing the steps is not enough; most beginners fail at the same three points, so name them and guard against them. The first is **skipping straight to Step 4** — falling in love with a single company and buying it regardless of what its sector is doing. That is bottom-up masquerading as conviction, and the sector tide will overrule it more often than not. The second is **confusing a cheap sector with a leading sector** — buying a beaten-down group *because* it is cheap, before any relative strength confirms the turn, which is how you catch a falling knife (Step 3 exists precisely to stop this). The third is **mis-setting the stop** — placing it at a stock-specific level when the actual thesis is a sector thesis, so you get shaken out by stock-level noise while the sector trend is still intact, or worse, you hold a losing stock long after the *sector* has rolled over because you were watching the ticker instead of the sector index. Each mistake is a failure to keep the sector as the primary unit of analysis. The discipline is simple to state and hard to keep: the sector decision comes first, the sector confirmation comes second, and the sector thesis defines the exit.

**What to watch, concretely, as a Vietnamese investor.** Keep a short dashboard: the SBV policy rate and any credit-ceiling news; the relative strength of the top sectors (banks, real estate, securities, steel) versus VN-Index; foreign net buying or selling by sector (quoted in \$mn in most broker reports); and total market margin balance (rising margin = leverage flooding into the leading sector, which exaggerates both the up move and the eventual unwind). When the macro dial turns, the leading sector usually turns with it, and your job is to be early to the *sector*, not clever about the *stock*.

**What invalidates the whole approach.** Sector thinking fails you in two situations, and it is honest to name them. First, in a stock-specific event — a fraud, a single-company scandal — the sector lens tells you nothing; that is pure idiosyncratic risk and only stock-level diligence catches it. Second, in a *correlation-breakdown* moment, like a forced deleveraging or a liquidity crisis, sectors that normally diverge can all crash together as everyone sells everything at once; in those moments the only thing that co-moves is *everything*, and sector rotation stops working until the panic clears. Knowing when the regime is "normal" (sectors rotate, the playbook works) versus "crisis" (everything correlates to 1, cash is the only sector) is itself a skill — and one this series returns to in its case-study posts.

Start every stock decision at the top of that pipeline, not the bottom. Before you ask "is this a good company?", ask "is this a good *sector*, right now, in this regime, and is it actually leading?" Get those answers first and you have done the larger half of the work — because, as the whole of this post has argued, a Vietnamese industry moves as one, and that one motion is usually the biggest single thing that happens to your money.
