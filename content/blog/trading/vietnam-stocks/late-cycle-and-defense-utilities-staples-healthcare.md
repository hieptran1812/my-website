> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — When the cycle overheats and rolls into slowdown, market leadership rotates one final time out of the high-beta cyclicals and into the defensives: utilities, consumer staples (food and beverage), healthcare and pharma, and telecom. These sectors lag in the bull but lose far less in the bust, and they keep paying you a dividend while you wait for the next early cycle.
>
> - **Defensives defend because their demand is inelastic and a lot of their cash is contracted or regulated.** People keep buying electricity, water, instant noodles, fish sauce, and medicine in a recession. That stability shows up as a low beta — roughly 0.5 to 0.7 against the VN-Index, versus 1.4 to 1.8 for steel, property, and brokers.
> - **The signal of the late-cycle turn is a cluster, not a single print.** CPI rising and broadening, the State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) turning hawkish, credit growth decelerating, and relative-strength leadership narrowing to fewer and fewer names — when these line up, you trim the cyclicals into defensives *before* the drawdown, not after it.
> - **The yield is the second half of the trade.** A defensive book yielding 4-6% versus the index's ~1.8% keeps compounding through a flat or falling tape. In a 40% index drawdown a 0.6-beta name falls roughly 24% on price but still pays its dividend; a 1.5-beta cyclical falls roughly 60% and pays nothing.
> - **The one number to remember:** defensive beta ~0.6 and yield ~5% versus index beta 1.0 and yield ~1.8% — that gap *is* the defensive trade.

In late 2021 the VN-Index was a party. The benchmark had run from under 660 in the depths of the 2020 crash to almost 1,500 by the first week of January 2022 — more than a double in eighteen months. Margin balances at the brokers had ballooned to record levels, new retail accounts were opening by the hundreds of thousands a month, and the loudest stories were all cyclical: securities firms up two and three times, steel names like Hoa Phat (HPG) riding a global commodity spike, property developers presold out for years. If you owned a sleepy power utility or a fish-sauce maker through that run, you felt slow. Everyone around you was making more, faster, in the high-beta names.

Then 2022 happened. Inflation that had been quiet all through 2021 started to broaden as global energy and food prices fed through. The SBV, which had held policy easy through the pandemic, pivoted hawkish — raising operating rates twice in the autumn and effectively letting deposit rates climb sharply. Credit growth, which the central bank rations with an annual ceiling (*room tín dụng*), got tight. And then the corporate bond market — the funding lifeline of the property developers — froze after a high-profile arrest. The VN-Index fell from roughly 1,530 in early April to about 875 by mid-November, a drawdown near 43%. The cyclical leaders that had led the bull led the bust too, only down: brokers fell ~58%, steel ~52%, property ~48%.

But not everything fell like that. The boring names you felt slow holding in the bull — the power generators, the water utilities, the instant-noodle and dairy makers, the pharma distributors, the telecoms — fell far less, kept printing roughly the same earnings, and kept paying their dividends straight through the wreckage. That is the whole point of this post. The last rotation of a cycle is into *defense*, and defense is not about being clever or fast. It is about owning the things whose demand does not go away, getting paid to wait, and losing a fraction of what the crowd loses when the cycle finally rolls over.

![Late cycle rotation flow from overheating into defensive sectors and yield](/imgs/blogs/late-cycle-and-defense-utilities-staples-healthcare-1.png)

This is the eleventh — and in many ways the final — move on the rotation clock. Earlier posts in this series walked the clock forward: early-cycle leaders (brokers, banks, steel) when the SBV first eases and credit turns up; mid-cycle expansion (industrials, property, materials) when growth is humming; and now the overheat-and-roll into defense. If you have read [the business-cycle investment clock for Vietnam](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/business-cycle-investment-clock-vietnam), this is the clock's six-o'clock-to-nine-o'clock arc. The job of this post is to make you fluent in what *late cycle* and *defense* actually mean, why these specific sectors defend, the signals that tell you the turn is coming, and the concrete playbook for rotating before the drawdown rather than after it.

## Foundations: What late cycle and defense actually mean

Let me build this from zero, because the two words doing all the work — "late cycle" and "defense" — get thrown around loosely, and the looseness is where people lose money.

### The business cycle, in four phases

An economy does not grow in a smooth straight line. It breathes. Think of it as four phases that repeat:

1. **Early cycle (recovery).** The economy was in or near recession; the central bank has cut rates and loosened credit to stimulate. Growth is low but accelerating. This is when you want the things most sensitive to cheap money and a turn in sentiment — brokers, banks, steel. Covered in [early-cycle leaders](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/early-cycle-leaders-vietnam-brokers-banks-steel).
2. **Mid cycle (expansion).** Growth is strong and broad. Companies are investing, building, hiring. Industrials, property, and materials lead. This is the longest phase and feels the most comfortable.
3. **Late cycle (overheat).** Growth is still positive but *decelerating*, and the economy is bumping against its limits. Inflation rises. The central bank, worried about overheating, stops easing and starts tightening. Credit gets expensive and scarce. The stock market often makes its high *during* this phase — prices peak before earnings do, because the market is a discounting machine looking 6-12 months ahead.
4. **Recession (slowdown / bust).** Growth turns negative or near-zero. Earnings fall, the weakest companies fail, and the central bank eventually pivots back to easing — which sets up the next early cycle.

"Late cycle" is phase 3 rolling into phase 4. It is the *overheat-then-rollover* — the part of the ride where the cyclicals that led the bull are most expensive, most loved, and most exposed, and where the smart move is to rotate the portfolio into something that will lose less when the music stops.

#### Worked example: dating the late cycle on the four-dial dashboard

Suppose you track [the four macro dials](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/the-four-macro-dials-rates-credit-fx-commodities) — rates, credit, FX, commodities. In mid-2021 the reading was clearly early-to-mid: the SBV's refinancing rate sat at a record-low 4.0%, system credit was growing well into double digits, the dong was stable, and the market was making new highs on broadening participation. By autumn 2022 the same dials read late-to-recession: the SBV had hiked the refinancing rate to 6.0% in two moves, credit growth was capped and decelerating, the dong had weakened past 24,000 per dollar forcing the SBV to sell reserves, and commodity-driven CPI was running near 4%. Reading those dials, a disciplined investor did not need to call the exact top; the *cluster* told them the cycle had aged from early to late, and that is the trigger to rotate. The lesson: you date the cycle from a dashboard of dials turning together, not from any single print.

### What "defensive" means, precisely

A **defensive** sector is one whose revenues and earnings hold up relatively well when the economy slows, because the demand for what it sells does not collapse in a downturn. The technical word for this is **inelastic demand**: when times get hard, you cut the new car, the second apartment, the discretionary travel — but you do not stop paying your electricity bill, you do not stop drinking water, you do not stop buying instant noodles and fish sauce, and you certainly do not stop taking your blood-pressure medication. Demand for those things is *inelastic* with respect to the economy.

Contrast that with a **cyclical** sector, whose demand swings hard with the cycle — a steel mill sells far less rebar when nobody is building, a broker earns far less commission when trading volume dries up, a property developer sells far fewer units when credit is tight and confidence is low.

Two more properties make a sector defensive beyond just inelastic demand:

- **Contracted or regulated cash flows.** A power generator often sells its output under a long-term power purchase agreement (PPA) at a regulated or contracted price; a water utility sells at a tariff set by the province. That cash is not at the mercy of the spot market the way a steel mill's revenue is. The income is *visible and durable*, which is exactly what investors crowd toward when the future looks uncertain.
- **Strong, stable balance sheets and high payout.** Defensive companies tend to be mature, generate more cash than they need to reinvest, and return the surplus as dividends. So they carry **high dividend yields** — typically 4-6% in the Vietnamese defensive names versus ~1.8% for the index as a whole.

Put those properties together and they produce the single most important quantitative signature of a defensive: a **low beta**.

### Beta: the one number that captures "how much it moves"

**Beta** measures how much a stock or sector tends to move relative to the whole market. A beta of 1.0 means it moves roughly one-for-one with the VN-Index: index up 10%, the stock up ~10%. A beta of 1.5 means it tends to move 1.5x as much — index up 10%, stock up ~15%; index down 10%, stock down ~15%. A beta of 0.6 means it moves only ~0.6x as much — index down 10%, stock down only ~6%.

Defensive sectors have low betas, typically 0.5 to 0.7. Cyclical sectors have high betas, 1.4 to 1.8. That single number is the mechanical reason a defensive book loses less in a drawdown.

![Beta by sector horizontal bar chart with defensives lowest](/imgs/blogs/late-cycle-and-defense-utilities-staples-healthcare-2.png)

The chart above is the heart of the matter, and it is worth sitting with. On the high-beta end you have securities firms (~1.8), steel (~1.5), real estate (~1.4) — the early-and-mid-cycle leaders that amplify every move of the index in both directions. On the low-beta end sit water (~0.5), utilities and pharma (~0.6), and food-and-beverage and telecom (~0.7). Banks (~1.1) and IT (~1.0) sit near the market. The defensives are not a vague idea; they are a measurable cluster at the bottom of the beta distribution, and that is precisely why they "defend."

#### Worked example: turning beta into a drawdown estimate

Say the VN-Index falls 40% in a bust — roughly what it did from the April 2022 high to the November low. A back-of-envelope estimate of how much a stock falls is *beta x index move*. A 1.5-beta cyclical, like a steel name, would be expected to fall about 1.5 x 40% = **60%**. A 0.6-beta defensive, like a power generator, would be expected to fall about 0.6 x 40% = **24%**. And here is the second half: the cyclical pays no dividend in a bust (earnings collapse, the board cuts the payout), while the defensive keeps paying — say a 5% yield. So the defensive's *total* drawdown is closer to 24% minus the 5% you collected, about 19%, while the cyclical eats the full 60%. At a USD/VND rate of about \$1 = 25,400 dong, if you held 1 billion dong (about \$39,000) in each, the cyclical book is worth ~\$15,700 at the bottom and the defensive book ~\$31,600 including the dividend. The lesson: a low beta plus a paid dividend is the difference between a flesh wound and an amputation.

### The defensive sectors and their roles on VN-Index

Not all defensives are identical. Each plays a slightly different role, and on the Vietnamese market they map to specific, recognizable groups:

- **Utilities — power, water, gas.** The purest defensives. Power generators (POW, NT2, REE's power arm, PPC) sell electricity under PPAs to EVN, the state offtaker; water utilities (BWE, TDM) sell under provincial tariffs; gas distributors sell a regulated necessity. Demand barely moves with the cycle, cash is contracted, yields are high. This is the anchor of any defensive book. Covered in depth in [the utilities sector post](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/utilities-power-water-gas-sector-vietnam-defensive-cash).
- **Consumer staples — food and beverage.** Vinamilk (VNM) in dairy, Masan Consumer (MCH) in fish sauce, soy sauce, and instant noodles, Sabeco (SAB) in beer. People keep eating and drinking the basics in a recession; these are brands with pricing power and fat margins. Covered in [the food-and-beverage post](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/food-beverage-sector-vietnam-defensive-staple).
- **Healthcare and pharma.** Drug distributors and manufacturers (DHG, IMP, DBD, TRA), hospital operators. Demand for medicine is the most inelastic of all — you take your prescription regardless of the GDP print — and Vietnam's aging demographics add a structural growth tailwind on top of the defensiveness. Covered in [the healthcare and pharma post](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/healthcare-pharma-sector-vietnam-demographic-defensive).
- **Telecom.** A mobile data plan and a fixed broadband line are now necessities, not luxuries. Telecom names (the listed proxies and the FPT Telecom arm) carry telecom-like low betas and steady, subscription cash flows. People do not cancel their phone plan in a downturn.

Each of these has a different growth profile — pharma has the demographic tailwind, telecom rides data growth, staples grow with population and premiumization, utilities grow with the grid and new capacity — but they share the defensive signature: inelastic demand, durable cash, low beta, high yield.

## How each defensive sector actually earns its money

"Defensive" is a label; the durability has to come from somewhere specific in each business. If you do not understand *why* a sector's cash holds up, you cannot tell a genuine defensive from a cyclical wearing a defensive costume. So let me open the hood on each of the four, because the mechanics differ and the differences matter when you build the book.

### Utilities: contracted volume at a regulated price

A power generator in Vietnam — POW, NT2, PPC, the power arm of REE — does not sell electricity into a free market the way a steel mill sells rebar. It sells under a **power purchase agreement (PPA)**, a long-term contract with EVN, the state-owned single buyer of electricity. The PPA fixes, or formula-links, the price and often guarantees an offtake volume. That structure does two things at once: it makes the *price* far less volatile than a commodity price, and it makes the *volume* far more predictable than discretionary demand. The plant's revenue is therefore close to an annuity — a stream of contracted payments — rather than a bet on the spot market.

There is a real risk that a steel mill does not have: a power plant's earnings depend partly on the spread between the contracted electricity price and the cost of its fuel (gas or coal). When fuel costs spike, a gas plant's margin can compress even though its revenue is contracted. So utilities are defensive on *demand* but carry a *cost* risk — which is exactly the kind of nuance the amber "risk/cost" color in our figures is meant to flag. The water utilities (BWE, TDM) are even cleaner: they sell water under provincial tariffs to a captive population, with minimal fuel-cost exposure, which is why water sits at the very lowest beta (~0.5) and the highest yield (~6%) of the whole defensive set.

#### Worked example: a power plant's contracted annuity

A gas-fired generator sells 4 billion kilowatt-hours a year under a PPA at an average 1,800 dong per kWh — about 7,200 billion dong (roughly \$283 million) of contracted revenue. In a recession when the broader economy contracts 2%, national electricity demand might dip 1% or even keep growing, because air conditioners and factories' base load do not switch off; the plant's offtake holds near its contracted volume. Compare that to a steel mill whose volumes can fall 30-40% in the same recession because construction stops. The utility's revenue line barely flinches while the steel mill's collapses. The lesson: a contracted volume at a regulated price is what turns a power plant into a bond-like annuity that a slowdown cannot easily break.

### Consumer staples: inelastic baskets and pricing power

Vinamilk's dairy, Masan Consumer's fish sauce and instant noodles, Sabeco's beer — these are the things in every Vietnamese kitchen. The demand is inelastic because they are cheap, frequently purchased necessities and habits: a family does not stop buying milk for the children or fish sauce for dinner because GDP growth slowed from 7% to 4%. Unit volumes are remarkably stable across the cycle.

On top of inelastic demand, staples have **pricing power**: a dominant brand can raise prices modestly to pass through cost inflation without losing many customers, because the customer's alternative is an unbranded substitute they trust less. That pricing power is what protects *margins* in an inflationary late cycle, which is precisely when input costs (milk powder, wheat, packaging) are rising. A staple that can pass through a 5% cost increase with a 4% price increase keeps its margin roughly intact while a price-taking cyclical sees its margin crushed. The trade-off is that staples are *not* immune to input-cost spikes in the short run — there is usually a lag between the cost rising and the price catching up, so margins can wobble for a quarter or two. But across the cycle, the combination of stable volumes and pricing power is what makes the earnings line so smooth, and a smooth earnings line is the foundation of a low beta.

### Healthcare and pharma: the most inelastic demand plus a demographic tailwind

Medicine is the textbook inelastic good. A diabetic does not skip insulin in a recession; a hypertensive does not stop the blood-pressure pill because the index fell 40%. Demand for essential pharmaceuticals is almost completely decoupled from the economic cycle, which makes pharma distributors and manufacturers (DHG, IMP, DBD, TRA) about as defensive as a sector can be. Hospital operators share the property, though private-hospital *elective* procedures can be deferred and carry a little more cyclicality than drug demand.

What makes Vietnamese healthcare special is that the defensiveness comes *with* a structural growth tailwind, which most defensives lack. Vietnam's population is aging and its middle class is expanding, so per-capita healthcare spending is on a long upward trajectory regardless of the cycle. That means a pharma name can be defensive in the bust *and* compound earnings through the recovery — a rare combination. It is the closest thing to a "growth defensive" on the market, which is why it deserves a permanent core slot rather than only a late-cycle tactical one.

### Telecom: subscription cash and a utility-like floor

A mobile data plan and a home broadband line have crossed the line from luxury to necessity. People do not cancel their phone in a downturn; if anything, data usage rises as discretionary spending on outside entertainment falls and people stay home. Telecom revenue is **subscription-based** — recurring monthly payments from a huge base of customers — which gives it a utility-like steadiness. Churn ticks up modestly in a recession but the base is sticky, and the average revenue per user is resilient. The result is a low beta (~0.7) and steady cash flow that funds a reliable dividend. Telecom is the quietest of the defensives and rarely the most exciting, but it does its job: it does not fall much, and it keeps paying.

Across all four, the common thread is now explicit: the *source* of the durability is a contract (utilities, telecom), a habit (staples), or a biological necessity (pharma). Wherever the durability comes from a *contract, a habit, or a necessity*, you have a real defensive. Wherever it comes from a story about why "this time the cyclical won't fall," you do not.

### Why the high yield is the second half of the trade

The low beta tells you the price falls less. The **dividend yield** tells you that you get paid to hold through the wait. Yield is just the annual dividend divided by the price: a stock paying 3,000 dong per share with a 60,000-dong price yields 5%.

![Dividend yield by defensive sector versus the index horizontal bar chart](/imgs/blogs/late-cycle-and-defense-utilities-staples-healthcare-3.png)

The defensive sectors cluster at 4-6% yield — water utilities near 6%, power near 5%, telecom and food-and-beverage near 4.5%, pharma near 4% — versus the VN-Index's ~1.8%. That gap is not a rounding error. Over a flat or falling year, a 5% yield is 5% of return you collect regardless of what the price does, while the index investor collects 1.8%. Compounded over the slow, frustrating late-cycle-into-recession stretch when prices go nowhere or down, that yield is most of your total return.

#### Worked example: the yield cushion that pays you to wait

You buy a power utility at 60,000 dong a share. It pays a cash dividend of 3,000 dong a share a year — a 5.0% yield. Now suppose the late cycle plays out and the share price slips 10% to 54,000 over the next year as the whole market drifts down. Your price loss is 6,000 dong, but you collected 3,000 dong in dividend, so your *net* loss is only 3,000 dong, or 5%. Hold a second year at the lower price and collect another 3,000 dong, and the dividend has now offset the entire price dip — you are roughly back to even on total return while the index is still down. In dollar terms, at \$1 = 25,400 dong, a 10,000-share position is about \$23,600; the 3,000-dong dividend pays you about \$354 a year to sit and wait. The lesson: in a sideways-to-down market, a high-yield defensive *pays you to be patient* while the cyclical holder bleeds with nothing coming in.

### The signals of the late-cycle turn

Rotating to defense is only useful if you do it *before* the drawdown. After the crash, the cyclicals have already fallen and the defensives have already outperformed — the trade is done. So the whole skill is reading the signals of the turn. They come as a cluster:

1. **CPI rising and broadening.** Headline inflation ticking up is the first warning, but the more important tell is *breadth* — when price pressure spreads from one volatile category (say energy) into core goods and services, the central bank can no longer look through it.
2. **The central bank turning hawkish.** The SBV pivoting from easing to tightening — raising operating rates, draining liquidity through open-market operations, tightening the credit ceiling — is the single most reliable late-cycle marker. The SBV's mechanics are covered in [Vietnam monetary policy](/blog/trading/finance/vietnam-monetary-policy-state-bank-dong-credit-ceiling). When the SBV stops being your friend, the cycle has aged.
3. **Credit growth decelerating.** Vietnam runs on credit. When the year-over-year growth of system credit rolls over — especially when the SBV deliberately caps the *room tín dụng* — the fuel for the cyclical sectors is being throttled. This is covered in [the liquidity and margin cycle](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/liquidity-and-the-margin-cycle-vietnam).
4. **Relative-strength leadership narrowing.** In a healthy bull, many sectors participate. As the cycle ages, leadership narrows to fewer and fewer names — the index makes new highs while the number of stocks doing so shrinks. This breadth deterioration, readable through [relative strength](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/relative-strength-reading-which-sector-leads-vn-index), is the market's own confession that the move is tiring.

No single one of these is decisive. CPI ticks up and back down all the time; one rate hike does not make a recession. But when they *cluster* — CPI up and broadening, the SBV hawkish, credit decelerating, breadth narrowing — that is the late-cycle signal. That is when you trim the cyclicals into the defensives.

There is a deeper reason the rotation must lead the data, and it is worth understanding because it is counterintuitive. The stock market is a **discounting machine**: prices reflect what investors expect 6-12 months ahead, not what is happening today. That is why the index often makes its *high* during the late-cycle phase, while the economic data still look fine — the market is already pricing the slowdown it sees coming. By the time the recession shows up in the GDP and earnings prints, the market has usually been falling for months. So if you wait for confirmation in the hard data, you are systematically late. The signal cluster — CPI, rates, credit, breadth — leads the hard data, which is exactly why you trade on the cluster and not on the eventual GDP number. The cluster is the market's early-warning system; the GDP print is the obituary.

There is also a lead-lag *within* the rotation. The highest-beta cyclicals turn down first — brokers roll over before steel, steel before property's reported earnings, because the market discounts each one's earnings collapse ahead of the actual collapse. So when you rotate, you trim in roughly beta order: brokers first (highest beta, first to fall), then steel and the materials, then the later-cycle industrials. The defensives are the destination, and within them you can lead with the lowest-beta water and pharma names, which will fall the least if your timing is slightly early.

### Cash as the ultimate defensive

There is one more defensive asset that is not a sector at all: **cash.** Holding cash (or short-term deposits, which in Vietnam pay a real interest rate) has a beta of zero and a guaranteed positive nominal return. It is the most defensive position there is — it cannot fall in nominal terms, and in a hawkish late cycle the deposit rate you earn on it is actually *rising*. Cash is also **dry powder**: capital you preserve through the drawdown that you can deploy into the early-cycle leaders at the bottom, when the cyclicals are cheapest and the next cycle is about to begin.

The defensive sectors and cash are complements, not substitutes. The defensive equities keep some upside exposure and pay a higher yield than a deposit; cash keeps zero downside and maximum flexibility. A late-cycle book typically blends them — overweight defensive sectors, with a meaningful cash buffer for the dry powder.

### How defensives fit a whole-cycle portfolio

It helps to step back and see where defensives sit in the bigger picture, because they are not only a late-cycle tactical trade — they are also a permanent ballast in a balanced book. One useful mental model is the **barbell**: a portfolio split between a high-beta sleeve (the cyclicals and growth names that drive returns in the bull) and a low-beta sleeve (the defensives and cash that protect capital in the bust), with the *weights* shifting across the cycle. In the early and mid cycle you tilt the barbell toward the high-beta end; in the late cycle you shift weight to the defensive end. The defensives are always there; what changes is how much of the book sits on that side.

This matters because the alternative — being all-cyclical and trying to time a clean exit to cash before every drawdown — is a fantasy. Nobody times the top consistently. A permanent defensive ballast means that even when your timing is wrong and the bust catches you partly cyclical, a chunk of the book is already in the things that lose 24% instead of 60%, and that chunk is paying you a yield the whole time. The defensive sleeve is your *forgiveness* for imperfect timing. The late-cycle rotation is simply the moment you make that sleeve bigger, on purpose, because the signals say the odds have shifted from reward to risk. Thinking of it as a barbell whose tilt you adjust — rather than an all-or-nothing switch you have to flip at exactly the right instant — is what makes the whole approach survivable for a real human investor who will, inevitably, sometimes be early and sometimes be late.

## Common misconceptions

The defensive trade is simple in principle, which is exactly why it attracts simple-but-wrong beliefs that cost people money. Three are worth dismantling with numbers.

### Misconception 1: "Defensives never fall"

They fall. The word is *defensive*, not *bulletproof*. A 0.6-beta defensive in a 40% index drawdown still falls about 24% on price. In an outright systemic panic — a 2008-style event or the March 2020 COVID crash — *everything* gets sold, including defensives, because investors raise cash indiscriminately and margin calls force liquidation of whatever can be sold. Defensives are about losing *less*, not losing *nothing*.

The number that matters is the *relative* drawdown. In 2022, while brokers fell ~58% and property ~48%, the defensive baskets fell roughly 20-30% — a real loss, but a fraction of the cyclical wreckage, and one cushioned by dividends. If you expected your utility to be flat through that, you misunderstood the trade. The right expectation is: in a 40% index drop, my defensives are down maybe 25% on price but paying me 5%, so my real pain is ~20%, versus the cyclical investor's 50-60%.

### Misconception 2: "You can just wait for the crash and then rotate"

This is the most expensive mistake, because it confuses *seeing* the crash with *profiting* from the rotation. By the time the crash is obvious — the index already down 30%, the headlines screaming — the cyclicals have already fallen and the defensives have already outperformed. The relative trade is over. Worse, at the bottom the *correct* move is often to rotate *out* of defensives and *into* the beaten-down cyclicals for the next early cycle, the opposite of what panic tells you to do.

The defensive rotation only pays if you do it on the *signals* — CPI up, SBV hawkish, credit decelerating, breadth narrowing — while the index is still near its high and the cyclicals are still expensive. You are trading a deteriorating setup, not a confirmed crash. That requires acting when it feels early and slightly foolish, which is why most people do not do it.

#### Worked example: rotating on the signal, not the crash

It is October 2022. CPI has been climbing for months and is now broadening into core. The SBV has just hiked its refinancing rate to 6.0% — the second hike in a few weeks — and the dong has weakened past 24,000. Credit growth is decelerating against a capped ceiling, and on your relative-strength dashboard, fewer and fewer sectors are making new highs; the move has narrowed to a handful of large caps. That is four signals clustering. You do not need the crash to be obvious. You sell your steel and broker positions — say 600 million dong (about \$23,600) of high-beta cyclicals — and you rotate that capital into a power utility, a water name, and a dairy staple. Over the following six weeks the index falls another ~20% into the November low; your cyclicals would have fallen ~30% and paid nothing, your defensives fall ~12% and keep paying. You preserved roughly 18 percentage points of that 600 million — about 108 million dong, or \$4,250 — by acting on the signal instead of the headline. The lesson: the rotation is paid for in advance, on the cluster of signals, never after the crash is on the front page.

### Misconception 3: "High yield equals safe"

A high dividend yield is attractive, but a *very* high yield is often a warning, not a gift. Yield is dividend divided by price, so a yield spikes when the *price* collapses — which usually means the market is pricing in a dividend cut, an earnings collapse, or a balance-sheet problem. A stock yielding 12% is rarely a safe 12%; it is usually a market saying "we do not believe this dividend will be paid."

The defensives that genuinely defend yield a *moderate*, *sustainable* 4-6%, backed by stable earnings and a payout the company can actually fund from cash flow. The check is not "how high is the yield" but "is the dividend covered" — does the company earn enough, and generate enough cash, to keep paying it? A 5% yield with 2x earnings coverage from a regulated utility is safe. A 10% yield from a leveraged cyclical whose price just halved is a trap. The skill is telling sustainable yield from a falling-knife yield, and the difference is in the cash-flow statement, not the yield number.

### Misconception 4: "Defensives are dead money — you give up too much upside"

The complaint is real but the framing is wrong. Yes, a defensive book lags badly in a roaring bull — a utility yielding 5% does nothing exciting while brokers triple. If you measure success only by upside in the best year, defensives look like dead money. But that is the wrong scorecard. The right scorecard is *compounded return through the full cycle*, and through the full cycle the math flips. The cyclical that triples in the bull and then falls 60% in the bust has a brutal compounding problem: a stock that goes up 200% and then down 60% ends at 1.0 x 3.0 x 0.4 = 1.2, a 20% gain over the round trip, with terrifying volatility. A defensive that goes up 30% in the bull and down 24% in the bust, while paying 5% a year, ends at roughly 1.0 x 1.30 x 0.76 x (dividends) ≈ 1.05 plus several years of collected yield — a smoother path to a similar or better place, with a fraction of the drawdown and the income arriving the whole way. Defensives are not "dead money"; they are *low-volatility compounders* whose advantage only shows up when you measure across the whole cycle, not the best year of it. The investors who blow up are almost always the ones who judged the cycle by its best year.

## How it shows up on VN-Index

Theory is cheap. Let me show you the defensive trade in the actual VN-Index data, because the 2022 cycle is one of the cleanest defensive case studies the Vietnamese market has produced.

### The 2022 defensive outperformance

Recall the setup. The VN-Index peaked near 1,530 in early April 2022 and fell to about 875 by mid-November — a drawdown of roughly 43%. That is the *index*, which is a blend of everything. Underneath it, the dispersion was enormous:

- **Securities firms** fell roughly 58%. They are the highest-beta sector on the market, mechanically geared to trading volume and margin balances, both of which collapsed.
- **Steel** fell roughly 52%, hit by a global price reversal and the freezing of domestic construction.
- **Real estate** fell roughly 48%, the epicenter of the bond-market freeze.
- The **defensive baskets** — power, water, food-and-beverage, pharma — fell roughly 20-30%, with the most regulated utilities at the shallow end.

![Stylized defensive over cyclical relative performance line through a late cycle and bust](/imgs/blogs/late-cycle-and-defense-utilities-staples-healthcare-6.png)

The line above shows the *relative* performance of a defensive basket against a cyclical basket through a stylized late-cycle-into-bust window. Early on the ratio is flat or even falling — defensives lag while the cyclicals are still running and the bull is intact. Then, as the overheat turns into the bust around the middle of the window, the line climbs hard: the defensives are falling far less than the cyclicals, so the *ratio* rises sharply. By the trough of the bust the defensive book has outperformed the cyclical book by a wide margin — not because the defensives went up, but because they went down so much less. That shape — lag in the bull, win in the bust — is the entire signature of the defensive trade, and it is visible in essentially every Vietnamese cyclical drawdown.

#### Worked example: the two books through the 2022 drop

Start January 2022 with two 1-billion-dong books (about \$39,400 each at \$1 = 25,400 dong). Book A is cyclical — steel, brokers, property, average beta ~1.5. Book B is defensive — power, water, dairy, pharma, average beta ~0.6, average yield ~5%. The index falls ~43% from its high to the November low. Book A, at 1.5 beta, falls about 1.5 x 43% = ~64%, ending near 360 million dong (~\$14,200), and it paid essentially nothing as the cyclicals slashed dividends. Book B, at 0.6 beta, falls about 0.6 x 43% = ~26% on price, ending near 740 million dong on price, *plus* it paid roughly 50 million dong of dividends along the way (5% on a falling balance), so it ends near 790 million dong (~\$31,100). The defensive book preserved about 430 million dong — roughly \$16,900 — more than the cyclical book through the same drawdown. The lesson: the defensive rotation is not about catching upside; it is about how much of your capital is still standing when the cycle turns back up.

### The yield cushion in a flat year

The 2022 example is the dramatic version — a sharp bust. But the defensive yield also earns its keep in the quieter, more common scenario: a *flat* or grinding year where the index goes roughly nowhere. In a year when the VN-Index is up 2% with white-knuckle volatility, a defensive book yielding 5% delivers a ~7% total return (price plus yield) with far less stress, while the index investor's 2% is all the volatility and almost none of the income. Over the long, dull stretches that make up most of market history, the yield is not a sweetener; it is the main course.

This is why allocators describe defensives as the sectors that "keep paying you to wait." You are not waiting for the defensive to soar — it will lag the next bull. You are collecting a 4-6% yield through the slow part of the cycle, preserving capital, and keeping dry powder, so that you arrive at the next early cycle with your stake intact and ready to rotate back into the high-beta leaders.

### The earlier dress rehearsals

2022 was the cleanest example, but it was not the first. The Vietnamese market has run this play before, and seeing the pattern repeat is what gives you the confidence to act early. In the 2007-2008 cycle, the VN-Index ran to a euphoric peak above 1,170 in early 2007 on a wall of foreign and retail money, then collapsed to under 240 by early 2009 — a drawdown above 75% as the global financial crisis met an overheated domestic bubble. The high-beta financials and property names that had led the mania led the collapse; the few defensive staples and utilities that existed at the time held up far better in relative terms. The lesson the survivors took from 2008 was exactly the one this post teaches: the things that go up the most in the mania go down the most in the bust, and the boring inelastic-demand businesses are where capital hides.

The March 2020 COVID crash is the instructive *exception* that proves the rule about systemic panics. In that episode the VN-Index fell from about 990 in January 2020 to roughly 660 by late March — a ~33% drop in weeks. It was so fast and so liquidity-driven that *everything* sold off together, defensives included, because investors and margined accounts raised cash indiscriminately. For a few weeks, beta stopped protecting you. But notice two things: first, even in that panic the defensives fell less than the brokers and the travel-and-aviation names that were directly hit; second, the defensives recovered their footing fast once the SBV slashed rates and liquidity returned. The takeaway is the honest version of Misconception 1 — in a true liquidity panic, defensives still fall, just less, and they are first to stabilize. They are a shock absorber, not a force field.

### What the dispersion is telling you

The single most important fact in the 2022 numbers is the *dispersion*: the index fell 43%, but the range underneath it ran from roughly minus 58% (brokers) to roughly minus 20% (the most regulated utilities). That spread of nearly 40 percentage points between the worst and best sectors in the same drawdown is the entire opportunity. Index-level thinking — "the market fell 43%" — hides it. Sector-level thinking — "which part of the market fell 58% and which part fell 20%, and could I have known in advance from beta?" — is what lets you position. The dispersion is not random; it is ordered by beta, and beta is knowable ahead of time. That is the whole reason this series teaches you to think in sectors rather than in the index: the index is an average, and you do not have to own the average. You can own the part of it that loses 20% instead of the part that loses 58%, and the beta chart tells you which is which before the drawdown ever starts.

## The playbook: how to trade it

Everything above resolves into a concrete, repeatable playbook. Here is how a Vietnamese investor — retail or institutional — actually rotates to defense.

### Step 1: Watch for the late-cycle signal cluster

You do not act on a single print. You act when the cluster lines up. Keep a simple late-cycle checklist and rotate when most of it is flashing.

![Late cycle signal checklist matrix of what to watch and what it confirms](/imgs/blogs/late-cycle-and-defense-utilities-staples-healthcare-7.png)

The four signals, with what to watch and what each confirms:

- **CPI up** — watch the monthly CPI print and especially core CPI breadth. Confirms inflation is broadening, which forces the central bank's hand.
- **Rates turning** — watch the SBV's operating rates, deposit rates, and open-market operations. Confirms the central bank has pivoted from your friend to your headwind.
- **Credit tightening** — watch system credit growth versus the ceiling, and interbank rates. Confirms the fuel for the cyclicals is being throttled.
- **RS narrowing** — watch how many sectors and stocks are making new highs. Confirms the bull is tiring and leadership is concentrating into fewer names.

When three of these four are flashing, the cycle has aged into late, and the rotation window is open. You are still early enough that the cyclicals are expensive and the defensives are cheap — exactly the right side to trade from.

### Step 2: Trim the cyclicals, build the defensive core

Rotate, do not flip. You are not selling everything and buying utilities in a day. You scale: trim the highest-beta cyclicals first — brokers, then steel, then property — because they fall hardest and fastest. Take that capital and build a defensive core: an anchor of utilities (power and water for the most regulated, contracted cash), a layer of consumer staples (a dairy or fish-sauce brand with pricing power), a healthcare or pharma name for the demographic tailwind plus defensiveness, and a telecom for the subscription cash flow. Spread across the defensive sectors so you are not concentrated in one regulatory regime.

![Cyclical book versus defensive book through the same forty percent index drawdown](/imgs/blogs/late-cycle-and-defense-utilities-staples-healthcare-4.png)

The before-and-after above is the whole reason you do this. The *same* 40% index drawdown hits a cyclical book and a defensive book completely differently: the cyclical book, average beta ~1.5, is down ~60% and paying nothing; the defensive book, average beta ~0.6, is down ~24% on price and still paying its ~5% dividend. Same market, same drawdown, radically different outcome — and the only difference is what you owned going in.

How you build the defensive core differs a little by who you are. A **retail investor** keeps it simple: four to six liquid large-cap defensives spread across the sectors — a power name and a water name for the utility anchor, a dairy or fish-sauce staple, a pharma distributor, and a telecom proxy — sized roughly equally, with a cash buffer on top. You do not need to over-engineer it; the whole point of defense is robustness, not precision. An **institution** running real size has two extra concerns. The first is *liquidity*: some of the best defensives are tightly held (high state or strategic ownership, low free float), so a large fund cannot move in or out quickly without moving the price, which means it must rotate earlier and more gradually than a retail investor. The second is the **foreign room** (*room ngoại*) — the cap on foreign ownership in a given stock. Several quality defensives are at or near their foreign limit, so a foreign fund may not be able to buy them at all and has to express the defensive view through the names where room is still available. These are practical frictions, not reasons to skip the rotation, but they are why an institution starts the rotation while the retail crowd is still chasing the late-cycle melt-up.

#### Worked example: sizing the rotation in beta terms

Think about the rotation not as "sell stock A, buy stock B" but as *reducing the beta of the whole book*. Say your 1-billion-dong portfolio (about \$39,400) runs at an average beta of 1.3 in the bull — heavy in brokers, steel, and property. On the late-cycle signal you want to take the book's beta down to about 0.7. You sell enough of the high-beta names and buy enough defensives that the *weighted-average* beta drops from 1.3 to 0.7. Concretely, if you move 60% of the book from a 1.6-beta cyclical sleeve into a 0.6-beta defensive sleeve and hold 10% cash (beta 0), the new book beta is roughly 0.4 x 1.6 + 0.5 x 0.6 + 0.1 x 0 ≈ 0.94, still a touch high; push the cyclical sleeve down to 25% and the defensive sleeve to 65% and you land near 0.4 x ... — the point is you *target a beta*, then solve for the weights. In a subsequent 40% index drop, a 0.7-beta book falls ~28% versus the ~52% a 1.3-beta book would have eaten — preserving about 240 million dong, roughly \$9,400. The lesson: the cleanest way to think about defensive rotation is as a dial on portfolio beta, and you size each trade by how much it moves that dial.

### Step 3: Anchor on the yield

The dividend yield is your patience-payment and your downside cushion. Build the defensive book around *sustainable* 4-6% yields, and check coverage — that the company earns and generates the cash to fund the payout, ideally with 2x earnings coverage. The yield does two jobs: it pays you through the flat-to-down stretch so your total return stays positive while the index drifts, and it puts a soft floor under the price (a stock that yields 7% after a dip attracts buyers chasing the income, which limits how far it falls). Avoid the falling-knife yields — the 10-12% yields that scream "dividend cut coming." Sustainable moderate yield is the trade; spectacular yield is the trap.

### Step 4: Keep meaningful cash as dry powder

Defense is not only equities. Hold a real cash buffer — a deposit earning a rising late-cycle rate, with a beta of zero. It preserves capital absolutely and gives you dry powder to deploy into the cheap cyclicals at the bottom, when the SBV finally pivots back to easing and the next early cycle begins.

#### Worked example: cash as defense and dry powder

You run a 1-billion-dong portfolio (about \$39,400). On the late-cycle signal you move it to 70% defensive equities and 30% cash — 700 million in defensives, 300 million in a deposit. The index then falls 40%. Your defensive equities (beta ~0.6) fall ~24% to about 532 million dong; your cash sits flat at 300 million and earns ~6% interest, growing to about 318 million. Your portfolio bottoms near 850 million dong (~\$33,500) — down only ~15%, versus the ~40% a fully-invested index book would have lost (down to ~600 million, ~\$23,600). You preserved roughly 250 million dong — about \$9,800 — relative to staying fully invested in the index. And now, at the bottom, you have 318 million dong of cash ready to buy the cheapest cyclicals for the next early cycle. The lesson: cash is the defensive that both protects capital in the drawdown and arms you to lead the recovery.

### Step 5: Know the invalidation

A trade without an exit is a religion, not a strategy. The defensive rotation is invalidated — and you rotate back toward cyclicals — when the late-cycle signals reverse: CPI rolls over and the SBV pivots back to easing (cutting rates, loosening the credit ceiling), credit growth turns back up, and breadth broadens as more sectors start making new highs again. That cluster reversing is the early-cycle signal, the bottom of the clock, and the cue to rotate the defensive book and the cash *into* the beaten-down high-beta leaders for the next bull. Holding defensives too long into a new early cycle is its own mistake — you will lag badly as the cyclicals scream off the bottom while your utility plods. Defense wins the bust; it loses the recovery. Rotate out of it on the same kind of signal discipline you used to rotate in.

There is a second way the defensive thesis can be invalidated, and it is a single-name risk rather than a macro one: the dividend gets cut. The whole second half of the defensive trade is the yield, so if a holding slashes its payout — because its own earnings deteriorated, its balance sheet got stretched, or a regulator changed a tariff or a PPA in its disfavor — then that name has stopped being the defensive you bought. The discipline is to watch dividend coverage on each holding, not just the macro signals. A utility whose fuel-cost spread has collapsed, or a pharma name that lost a key distribution contract, can cut its dividend even while the macro still says "stay defensive." When a holding's dividend coverage erodes toward 1x, you replace it with a healthier defensive rather than reaching for its now-spiking yield. The macro signal tells you *whether* to be defensive; the coverage check on each name tells you *which* defensives are still doing their job.

The timing of the rotation back is genuinely hard, and it is worth being honest about that. You will not catch the exact bottom, and you should not try. The cyclicals bottom *before* the economic news bottoms — again, because the market discounts ahead — so the rotation back into early-cycle leaders happens while the headlines are still terrible and it feels reckless. The signal discipline is what carries you through that discomfort: when the SBV cuts, credit turns up, and breadth broadens, you begin scaling out of defense and into the leaders even though it feels too early, because by the time it feels safe the first leg of the recovery is gone. The dry powder you preserved is what makes this possible — you cannot buy the cheap cyclicals at the bottom if you rode the whole drawdown fully invested in them. Defense, in other words, is not just a way to lose less; it is the thing that *funds* your aggression at the next bottom.

This is the last move on the rotation clock and it loops straight back to the first. You rotated to defense on the overheat; you rotate back to early-cycle leaders on the pivot. The full loop is laid out in [sector rotation explained](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/sector-rotation-explained-leaders-and-laggards) and timed against the cycle in [the business-cycle investment clock](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/business-cycle-investment-clock-vietnam). Master both ends — when to get defensive and when to get aggressive again — and you have the core of cyclical sector investing on the VN-Index.

Why do defensives defend at all? It comes back to the chain we started with, and it is worth seeing it as one picture.

![Why defensives defend chain from inelastic demand to shallow drawdown](/imgs/blogs/late-cycle-and-defense-utilities-staples-healthcare-5.png)

Inelastic demand and contracted-or-regulated cash mean earnings hold through the slowdown. Earnings holding produces a low beta and supports a steady dividend. Low beta plus dividend produces a shallow drawdown — the whole point. The defensive sectors are not magic; they are the mechanical consequence of selling things people cannot stop buying, under contracts that do not break when the cycle does. That is why, when the cycle overheats and rolls over, leadership rotates one last time into utilities, staples, healthcare, and telecom — the sectors that lose less and pay you to wait.

And that is the quiet wisdom of the last move on the rotation clock. The early-cycle trade is about courage — buying the beaten-down high-beta names when the news is darkest. The late-cycle trade is about discipline — selling the loved, expensive, high-beta winners while everyone is still celebrating, and accepting that you will lag for a while in exchange for keeping your capital intact through the bust. Both are hard for opposite reasons, and mastering both is what separates an investor who compounds across decades from one who gives back every bull-market gain in the next bear. Defense does not feel heroic in the moment. But when the cycle finally rolls over, the investor who rotated early — into the sectors that lose 24% instead of 60%, while collecting a 5% yield and holding dry powder — is the one still standing, and still buying, when the next early cycle begins.
