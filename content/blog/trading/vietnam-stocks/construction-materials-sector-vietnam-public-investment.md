> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Construction (the contractors who build) and building materials (cement, stone, asphalt, steel) are the *most direct* listed play on Vietnam's public-investment super-cycle — the North-South Expressway, Long Thanh airport, Tan Son Nhat Terminal 3 — but it is a brutal, low-margin business that punishes investors who confuse a big order book for a big profit.
>
> - The whole sector is downstream of one government number: the **public-investment disbursement rate** (how fast the planned *dau tu cong* budget is actually spent). When disbursement accelerates, contractor backlogs convert to revenue and materials volumes rise; when it stalls in red tape, the sector starves.
> - Contractors run a **cash trap**: they spend to build long before they get paid, so a fat backlog comes with heavy working capital and receivables risk. A net margin of **2-3%** is normal, which means a single unpaid project can wipe out a year of profit — this is exactly how Hoa Binh Construction (HBC) went from blue-chip builder to a distressed, debt-restructuring penny stock.
> - Materials makers (cement especially) are **price-takers with chronic oversupply**: Vietnam can make far more cement than it consumes, so prices stay capped and margins thin even when volumes are good.
> - The number to remember: planned public investment ran near **790 trillion dong** for 2025 (about **\$31bn**), up from 540 trillion in 2022 — but the sector only pays off when that money is *disbursed*, not just *announced*. Buy disbursement acceleration *plus* balance-sheet quality; sell the announcement.

In early 2023, the Vietnamese government did something that lit a fire under a whole corner of the stock market. Facing a property market that had frozen solid in the corporate-bond crisis of late 2022, with developers unable to refinance and private construction grinding to a halt, the state reached for the one lever it fully controls: its own spending on infrastructure. The Prime Minister set an aggressive public-investment target for the year, demanded that ministries and provinces *spend the money instead of sitting on it*, and pushed hard on the flagship projects — above all the North-South Expressway, a multi-phase highway running the spine of the country. The message to the market was unmistakable: when the private economy stalls, the state will build.

The stocks that move on that message are construction and building-materials companies. Contractor names like Vinaconex (VCG) and Coteccons (CTD), stone and aggregate suppliers, asphalt makers, and the giant cement producers — these are the companies whose revenue *is*, more or less directly, the government's infrastructure budget turned into roads and runways. Through 2023, as the disbursement narrative built, many of these names ran hard off their lows, and "*dau tu cong*" (public investment) became one of the most repeated phrases at the retail coffee table. The story was real: 2023 ended as a record disbursement year, with roughly 95% of an enormous plan actually spent — the highest execution rate in years.

But here is the cruelty that this post is really about. The *same* public-investment wave that was lifting the sector's narrative was, at that very moment, *destroying* one of its most famous members. Hoa Binh Construction Group (HBC), for years one of the two dominant high-rise contractors in the country, was collapsing under a mountain of receivables — money it was owed by property developers who could no longer pay — and was sliding toward losses, dilution, and a desperate debt restructuring. One contractor was a buy on public investment; another contractor, in the same sector, was a near-bankruptcy. *Public investment lifts the sector* and *contractors are dangerous* were both true at the same time, and the entire skill of investing in this sector is holding both of those facts at once.

![Pipeline flow from state budget to public investment plan to contractors to building materials to the finished asset such as an expressway or airport](/imgs/blogs/construction-materials-sector-vietnam-public-investment-1.png)

This post builds the sector from the ground up. We will start with what public investment actually *is* and how the money flows from the state budget all the way to the job site, because that flow is the sector's only real demand engine. We will then build the two business models that sit on that flow — the **contractor** (who builds) and the **materials maker** (who sells the inputs) — and show *why* contractors run on thin margins and heavy working capital while materials makers are price-takers drowning in oversupply. We will work through how you value each (asset-light contractors on price-to-book, asset-heavy materials makers on EV/EBITDA), why the receivables-and-payment-delay risk is the thing that actually kills companies here, and how the sector has behaved across real episodes on VN-Index — including the HBC distress. And we end, as this series always does, with a concrete playbook: how to trade construction and materials off the disbursement cycle, what to buy, what to avoid, and what would prove the whole thesis wrong.

A note on money, because we will use it throughout. Vietnam trades in dong (VND), and every primary number in this post — budgets, backlogs, profits, market caps — is quoted in dong. As a reference point for readers who think in dollars, the exchange rate in mid-2026 is roughly **\$1 = 25,400 VND**. So a 790 trillion dong public-investment plan is about **\$31bn**; a contractor with a 10 trillion dong backlog has roughly a **\$394mn** order book; a foreign net buy of 500 billion dong is about **\$19.7mn**. We will keep the primary figures in dong (that is how the market quotes) and drop a dollar anchor whenever it helps you feel the size.

## Foundations: what public investment is and how it reaches the sector

Start with the demand engine, because nothing else in this sector makes sense without it. **Public investment** — in Vietnamese, *dau tu cong* — is the government spending its own money to build physical infrastructure: highways, airports, bridges, seaports, metro lines, irrigation works, public hospitals and schools. It is distinct from the government's *current* spending (salaries, pensions, running the bureaucracy); public investment is specifically the *capital* spending that creates long-lived assets. In Vietnam it is a huge number — running in the hundreds of trillions of dong every year — and it is one of the three classic engines of the country's GDP growth, alongside exports and private consumption.

Why does the government do this, and why does it matter so much for the cycle? Two reasons. First, Vietnam genuinely *needs* the infrastructure: a fast-growing manufacturing economy with chronically congested roads, an overloaded main airport in the south, and ports that need deeper water cannot keep growing without highways, a new airport, and better logistics. So there is a long structural pipeline of projects — the North-South Expressway, the Long Thanh international airport outside Ho Chi Minh City, the new Terminal 3 at Tan Son Nhat, ring roads around the two big cities, and more. Second — and this is the part that makes it tradable — public investment is **counter-cyclical by policy**. When the private economy slows (property freezes, exports soften, credit tightens), the government can *accelerate* its own infrastructure spending to support growth. It is the state's most direct growth lever, and it gets pushed hardest exactly when the private sector is weakest. That is why a property bust (bad for almost everything) can be *good* for public-investment-geared construction: the same downturn that freezes private building is the trigger for the state to build more.

It helps to anchor the scale of these flagship projects, because their sheer size is what makes the public-investment narrative so powerful for the stock market. The **North-South Expressway** is not a single road but a multi-phase national highway program intended to run the length of the country, broken into dozens of component packages awarded to different contractors — a years-long, multi-hundred-trillion-dong construction effort that consumes vast quantities of stone, sand, asphalt, and structural steel. **Long Thanh International Airport**, being built east of Ho Chi Minh City to relieve the overloaded Tan Son Nhat, is a generational project — a brand-new airport with runways, a huge terminal, and all the supporting infrastructure, again a multi-year build worth on the order of hundreds of trillions of dong across its phases. **Tan Son Nhat Terminal 3** is a major new terminal at the existing southern airport. Add ring roads around Hanoi and Ho Chi Minh City, provincial expressways, and seaport expansions, and you have a *pipeline of demand* that, if disbursed, keeps the sector busy for a decade. This is why the structural bull case is genuinely real — the projects exist, they are needed, and they are enormous. The catch, every time, is the word *if*: the demand only reaches the sector when the money is actually disbursed, and Vietnamese public investment has a long history of plans that bog down in execution.

Now the flow. Money does not jump from the budget to the job site; it moves through a chain, and each link in the chain is where a different listed company earns its revenue. The chain runs: **state budget → public-investment plan → contractors → building materials → the finished asset.** Let us walk it.

The **state budget** funds the whole thing, from tax revenue and government borrowing (bond issuance). Each year the National Assembly approves a **public-investment plan** — a target figure (call it the plan) broken down by project, ministry, and province. Crucially, *approving the plan is not the same as spending it*. The money is then **disbursed** — actually paid out — as projects clear their paperwork, acquire land, and proceed. The gap between the plan and the disbursement is the single most important variable in this entire sector, and we will return to it again and again: **a big announced plan does nothing for earnings until it is disbursed.**

From the disbursed budget, the state (or its project-management boards) **awards contracts** to **contractors** — the construction companies that actually build the highway or the airport terminal. The contractor is the general builder; it may subcontract pieces, but it carries the contract. To build, the contractor **buys building materials** — cement, crushed stone and aggregates, sand, asphalt (bitumen) for road surfaces, and structural steel — from the materials makers. Those materials are consumed on site, and the result is **the asset**: a stretch of expressway, a runway, a terminal building, a bridge. That asset, once it opens, generates economic value for decades (lower logistics costs, more capacity) — but the *listed companies* earned their money on the way to building it, not from owning the finished road.

So the sector splits cleanly into two business models sitting on the same flow: the **contractor** (sells *labor and construction services* — turning materials and money into a finished structure) and the **materials maker** (sells *physical product* — the cement, stone, asphalt, steel that goes into the structure). They have completely different economics, balance sheets, and risks, and you value them completely differently. We will build each in turn.

Before we do, one more piece of the flow deserves emphasis: the **timing mismatch** between when money leaves the budget and when it reaches each link. The budget is approved at the start of the year, but disbursement ramps up unevenly — historically it has been slow in the first half (projects clearing paperwork, land acquisition, the dry-season versus rainy-season construction calendar) and rushed in the fourth quarter as ministries race to spend before year-end. This *back-loaded* pattern matters for trading, because the sector's revenue and the disbursement data both tend to be lumpy and seasonal, with a heavy fourth quarter. A weak first-half disbursement number does not necessarily kill the year (the Q4 rush can save it), and a strong one is doubly encouraging. The materials makers feel this first (they sell the inputs as work begins), the contractors recognize revenue as they build, and the cash actually arrives last of all (after billing and the wait). So even within a single good year, the *cash* lags the *spending* lags the *plan* — and that cascade of lags is the working-capital problem we are about to build, expressed across the calendar.

### The contractor model: backlog, revenue, and a brutally thin margin

A contractor is, at its core, a **service business that builds things to order**. It does not own the road it builds or the building it erects; it is hired to construct them, hands them over, and gets paid. Its economics run on four concepts you must understand: backlog, revenue recognition, the thin margin, and working capital.

**Backlog** (the order book, sometimes called the *backlog* or *signed contract value*) is the total value of contracts the company has won but not yet finished building. It is the contractor's headline "pipeline" number, and it is what gets investors excited — "Vinaconex won a 5 trillion dong package on the North-South Expressway!" A big backlog *looks* like a big future. But backlog is **contract value, not profit** — it is the total amount the contractor will *bill*, out of which it must pay for all the materials, all the labor, all the subcontractors, all the equipment, and only a sliver is left as profit. Holding the difference between backlog and profit in your head is the single most important discipline in this sector, so we will hammer it with a worked example shortly.

**Revenue recognition.** A highway takes years to build, so a contractor does not wait until handover to book revenue; it recognizes revenue *as it builds*, by the **percentage-of-completion** method. If a 5 trillion dong project is 30% built by the end of the year, the contractor books roughly 1.5 trillion dong of revenue that year (and the matching share of cost). So revenue is, roughly, *backlog being burned down* — the faster the work proceeds (driven by disbursement), the faster backlog converts to revenue.

**The thin margin.** This is the heart of the contractor's pain. Construction is fiercely competitive and largely *price-bid*: public projects are awarded by tender, and the lowest qualified bid usually wins. When you win by being the cheapest, you win on a thin margin by construction. Net profit margins for Vietnamese contractors typically run around **2-3%** — sometimes less, occasionally a touch more on specialized work. That is razor-thin. For comparison, a bank might earn a 30% net margin and a consumer-goods company 12%; the contractor scrapes 2-3%. The implication is severe: on a 2% net margin, a contractor must *flawlessly* execute on price, cost, and collection, because there is almost no cushion. A small cost overrun, a delayed payment, or a financing-cost increase can turn that 2% into zero — or negative.

![Grouped bar chart comparing approximate net profit margins by sector with contractors near 2 percent cement 5 percent steel 8 percent consumer 12 percent and banks 30 percent](/imgs/blogs/construction-materials-sector-vietnam-public-investment-4.png)

The chart above ranks net margins across sectors, and the contractor bar — about **2%** — is the shortest on the board by a wide margin. A bank earns roughly fifteen times the net margin of a contractor on each dollar of revenue; even a cement maker, no one's idea of a high-margin business, earns more than double. This is not a temporary state of affairs that competition will fix; it is the *structural* condition of a price-bid construction industry, and it is why the entire analytical weight in this sector falls on *execution and balance sheet* rather than on growth. A 30%-margin bank can absorb a mistake; a 2%-margin contractor cannot. When you look at that tiny bar, internalize what it means: there is no room for error, so the only contractors worth owning are the ones that do not make errors — clean costs, clean collection, clean balance sheet. Everything else in the contractor analysis is downstream of how thin that bar is.

**Working capital and receivables.** And here is the killer, the thing that makes the thin margin even more dangerous than it looks. A contractor has to *spend cash to build before it gets paid*. It mobilizes (pays crews, rents equipment, buys materials), it builds for months, it bills the owner in stages — and then it *waits* to be paid. The money it is owed but has not yet collected is its **receivables** (in Vietnamese accounting, the large "*phai thu*" line). On public projects, payment can be slow — tied to the disbursement bureaucracy, to inspection and acceptance of completed work, to the owner's own cash flow. On private projects (like building apartment towers for a developer), payment depends on the *developer's* solvency, which is exactly what evaporated in the 2022 bond crisis. So the contractor is perpetually financing a giant pile of work-in-progress and uncollected bills, usually with debt, and that debt carries interest that eats directly into the already-thin margin.

![Pipeline of the contractor cash trap from winning the bid to mobilizing cash to building to billing to waiting for payment to a thin net margin](/imgs/blogs/construction-materials-sector-vietnam-public-investment-3.png)

That figure is the whole contractor problem in one line. Cash goes *out* at the start (mobilize, build) and only comes *back* much later (after billing and a long wait), and the gap is financed. The order book looks huge, but by the time you subtract the materials, the labor, the subcontractors, the financing cost of carrying all that work-in-progress and receivables, and the occasional bad debt, you are left with that sliver of net margin — and sometimes you are left with nothing.

#### Worked example: why a fat backlog is not a fat profit

Make the backlog-versus-profit gap concrete, because it is the most common trap in this sector. Suppose a contractor announces a **10 trillion dong backlog** — a genuinely large, headline-grabbing order book, about **\$394mn**. Retail investors see "10 trillion dong" and imagine a windfall. Now apply the contractor's real economics. At a net margin of **3%** (a *good* margin for this business), the *entire* 10 trillion dong of work, built out over the several years it takes to complete these projects, throws off **300 billion dong** of net profit in total — about **\$11.8mn**, spread across the whole life of the projects. That is the profit on the *entire backlog*, not per year. If the work takes three years, it is roughly 100 billion dong (about \$3.9mn) of profit a year from a 10 trillion dong order book. The intuition: backlog flatters because it is a *revenue* number on which a 2-3% sliver is profit — a contractor's order book can be ten times its market value and still not make it rich, because almost all of that backlog flows straight back out as cost.

#### Worked example: the working-capital bite eats the margin

Now show how working capital turns the thin margin into a *negative* one. Take that same contractor and suppose it has **2 trillion dong of receivables** stuck — money it has billed but not collected, sitting on the balance sheet for a full year because a project owner is slow to pay. It is financing that 2 trillion dong with debt at, say, a **10%** annual interest rate (Vietnamese corporate borrowing rates in this range are normal). The carrying cost is 2,000 × 10% = **200 billion dong** a year — about **\$7.9mn** — just to *finance the money it is owed but cannot collect*. Compare that to the profit: on a 10 trillion dong backlog burning down at perhaps 3-4 trillion dong of revenue a year, a 3% margin is only about 90-120 billion dong of net profit. The 200 billion dong financing cost on stuck receivables is *larger than the entire year's profit*. The intuition: in a thin-margin, high-working-capital business, the financing cost of uncollected receivables can exceed the profit on the work itself — which is exactly how a contractor with a full order book reports a *loss*.

### The materials model: price-takers swimming in oversupply

The other half of the sector makes the *stuff* — cement, crushed stone and aggregates, sand, asphalt, and structural steel. (Steel gets its own treatment in the [steel and basic resources](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/steel-basic-resources-sector-vietnam-the-cyclical) post; here we focus on the construction-specific materials, with cement as the central example, because cement is the purest illustration of the materials maker's curse.)

A materials maker has the *opposite* balance sheet from a contractor. Where a contractor is **asset-light** (it owns relatively little — its value is its people, its know-how, and its order book), a cement or stone producer is **asset-heavy**: it owns enormous, expensive fixed assets — a cement plant with kilns, a limestone quarry, grinding mills, a stone quarry with crushers. Building a cement plant costs a fortune and the assets last decades. This single difference — asset-light contractor versus asset-heavy materials maker — drives *everything else*, including how you value them, which we will get to.

The materials maker's curse has two parts, and cement shows both perfectly.

**Part one: they are price-takers.** Cement is a commodity. A ton of standard Portland cement is a ton of standard Portland cement; buyers shop on price (and on freight cost, because cement is heavy and expensive to transport, so it sells regionally). No cement maker gets to *set* the price; the market sets it, and individual producers take it. This is the same price-taker logic that makes steel cyclical — the company controls its costs and volume, not its price. Cement's main costs are *energy* (it takes enormous heat to turn limestone into clinker, the intermediate product, so coal and electricity are huge cost lines) and *clinker* itself for grinders who buy it in. When energy prices spike, the cement maker's costs rise but it cannot simply pass them through, because the price is set by a competitive, oversupplied market — which brings us to part two.

**Part two: chronic oversupply.** Here is the structural problem that hangs over Vietnamese cement specifically. The country has built far *more* cement-making capacity than its domestic market consumes. Through years of investment (much of it debt-financed), total cement capacity climbed past 100 million tons a year, while domestic demand has hovered far below that — often in the low-60-million-ton range. That gap is **massive chronic oversupply**. When you can make 120 million tons and your home market only wants 60 million, the producers are locked in a permanent fight for volume, prices stay capped, and the only escape valve is *exports* — selling the surplus abroad (to Bangladesh, the Philippines, elsewhere), often at thin margins and at the mercy of global freight and demand. Oversupply is why cement margins stay structurally thin even in a good construction year, and why a public-investment boom helps cement *volumes* far more than it helps cement *prices*. The wave lifts how much they sell; it does not lift the price much, because there is always idle capacity ready to undercut.

![Line chart of Vietnam cement capacity rising from about 105 to 124 million tons against flat domestic demand near 60 to 65 million tons showing a wide and growing oversupply gap](/imgs/blogs/construction-materials-sector-vietnam-public-investment-6.png)

The chart above is the cement maker's curse drawn out over time: the *capacity* line climbs steadily — from roughly 105 million tons in 2020 toward 124 million by 2025 — while *domestic demand* barely moves, sitting in the low-60-million-ton band the whole time. The vertical gap between the two lines is the oversupply, and it is enormous and *growing*: the industry kept building kilns even as demand flatlined. That widening gap is the single most important fact about Vietnamese cement as an investment. It tells you, before you look at any individual company, that the sector has a structural pricing problem: with capacity nearly double domestic demand, no producer can sustainably raise prices, because there is always a rival with idle kilns happy to take the volume at the going price. The only way to use that excess capacity is to export it — which is why Vietnamese cement makers became major exporters, and why their fortunes are now tied not just to domestic construction but to global freight rates and the demand of importing countries. When you hear "public investment will boom, so buy cement," look at this gap and remember: the boom fills some of that idle capacity (good for volume), but it cannot close the gap or unlock pricing power (so margins stay thin). The structural oversupply is the ceiling on every bull case for the sector.

It is worth being precise about *why* this oversupply is so sticky, because it is not a temporary glut that demand growth will absorb. A cement kiln is a massive, expensive, long-lived asset financed largely with debt; once it is built, the owner will run it to cover its fixed costs even at a thin spread, because shutting it down means defaulting on the debt and writing off the investment. So capacity, once added, does not leave — it just sits there pressuring price. New capacity gets *added* in good years (when a few fat-margin quarters tempt someone to build) but almost never *removed* in bad years, so the supply curve ratchets up and rarely down. This is the same trap that afflicts every capital-intensive commodity business — steel has it too — but cement's freight-sensitivity makes it regional and even harder to rationalize: you cannot simply ship surplus southern cement to a tight northern market cheaply, because transport eats the margin. The result is a permanently oversupplied, regionally fragmented, price-capped industry where the *best* a producer can do is be the lowest-cost, best-located maker and grind out volume — which is exactly why you value these companies on EV/EBITDA and obsess over their cost position and plant location rather than dreaming of pricing power.

#### Worked example: the cement spread, and why oversupply caps it

Build the cement maker's economics like we built the steelmaker's, because the logic is identical: profit per ton is the spread between the sell price and the cost, times volume. Suppose a cement maker sells a ton of cement at **1.2 million dong** (its sell price, about **\$47**), and its all-in cost per ton — energy (coal and power to fire the kiln), limestone, labor, depreciation of the plant — is **1.05 million dong** (about **\$41**). The spread is 1.2 − 1.05 = **0.15 million dong per ton**, roughly **\$6** — a thin spread, maybe 12-13%. On, say, 5 million tons of volume, that is 5,000,000 × 150,000 = **750 billion dong** of gross spread (about **\$29.5mn**), out of which still come interest and overhead. Now watch what oversupply does. In a year when the public-investment wave lifts volumes, the maker would *love* to raise the sell price to 1.3 million dong and fatten the spread — but it can't, because a competitor with idle capacity will hold price at 1.2 to win the volume. So the maker gets *more tons at the same thin spread*, not a wider spread. The intuition: oversupply means the public-investment boom flows to materials makers as **volume, not price** — the spread stays pinned thin, and only the ton-count grows.

#### Worked example: the disbursement leverage on a materials supplier

Now show why volume is still worth a lot to a materials maker, by quantifying the disbursement lever. Suppose public investment rises from **540 trillion dong** (the 2022 level) to **676 trillion dong** (the 2023 level) — a jump of about **+25%**. A meaningful share of public-investment spending on roads and airports goes into materials: cement, stone, asphalt. If a stone-and-aggregate supplier near a major expressway package sees its volumes rise roughly in line with the regional infrastructure spend — call it +25% on volume — and its thin per-ton spread holds, then its *gross* profit rises about 25% too, before any operating leverage. But materials makers, like steelmakers, carry heavy *fixed* costs (the quarry, the crushers, the depreciation), so a 25% volume rise spread over those fixed costs lifts *net* profit by *more* than 25% — that is operating leverage. A supplier earning, say, 200 billion dong of net profit (about **\$7.9mn**) at the lower volume could plausibly earn 300+ billion dong (about **\$11.8mn**) at the higher one. The intuition: even though oversupply pins the *price*, the *volume* lift from a disbursement acceleration is geared by fixed costs, so the right materials supplier — one sitting next to the actual spending — can see profit jump far more than the headline 25%.

## Valuation: price-to-book for contractors, EV/EBITDA for materials

You cannot value a contractor and a cement maker the same way, because they have opposite balance sheets, and getting this wrong is how people overpay. The series covers the full toolkit in [valuation by sector](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/valuation-by-sector-pe-pb-nav-ev-ebitda); here is the sector-specific version.

**Contractors: price-to-book (P/B) and an eye on receivables quality.** A contractor is asset-light and earns a thin, *volatile*, sometimes-negative margin. That makes its **P/E** (price-to-earnings) almost useless — in a bad year earnings can be zero or negative, so P/E is undefined or meaningless, and in a good year a contractor can look deceptively cheap on a low P/E right before a receivables problem detonates. Instead, the market often values contractors on **price-to-book** (P/B) — the share price relative to the company's net asset value (book equity) per share. The logic: a contractor's *equity* is the buffer that absorbs losses and the base it builds on, so what you are really buying is a claim on that equity and on the company's ability to win and execute work. But — and this is critical — a contractor's book value is **only as good as its receivables**. If a chunk of those receivables is uncollectible (a developer that will never pay), the true book value is lower than reported, and the "cheap" P/B is an illusion. So for contractors, the right discipline is: value on P/B, but **discount the book for receivables risk** — ask how much of the receivables pile is from shaky counterparties, how old it is, and whether it is already being written down.

**Materials makers: EV/EBITDA.** A cement or stone producer is asset-heavy and debt-heavy (those plants and quarries were financed). For an asset-heavy, leveraged, capital-intensive business, the right tool is **EV/EBITDA** — enterprise value (market cap *plus* net debt) divided by EBITDA (earnings before interest, tax, depreciation, and amortization). Why? Because EV/EBITDA values the *whole business* (debt and equity together) against its *operating* cash generation *before* the distortions of depreciation (which is huge for a plant-heavy company and is a non-cash accounting figure) and financing (which varies with how much debt the company carries). This lets you compare two cement makers with different debt loads on a like-for-like operating basis — exactly what P/E cannot do when depreciation and interest are large and variable. The same EV/EBITDA logic applies to the asset-heavy steel sector, which is why this metric recurs across the cyclical, capital-intensive corners of VN-Index.

The neat summary: **asset-light, thin-margin, receivables-exposed contractor → price-to-book (discounted for bad receivables); asset-heavy, debt-heavy, depreciation-heavy materials maker → EV/EBITDA.** Use the wrong tool and you will think a contractor with rotten receivables is cheap, or that a cement maker drowning in debt is expensive when its operations are fine — both expensive mistakes.

#### Worked example: why P/B on a contractor must be discounted for receivables

Make the receivables-adjusted book value concrete, because it is where a "cheap" contractor turns out to be expensive. Suppose a contractor reports book equity (net asset value) of **3 trillion dong** (about **\$118mn**) and trades at a price-to-book of **0.8** — apparently a 20% discount to book, which looks like a bargain. Now open the balance sheet. Of its assets, **4 trillion dong** is receivables, and suppose **1 trillion dong** of that (about **\$39mn**) is owed by property developers in financial distress who realistically will never pay in full. If you write that 1 trillion dong down to zero, the *true* book equity is not 3 trillion but 3 − 1 = **2 trillion dong**. Against the same share price, the *real* price-to-book is no longer 0.8 but 0.8 × (3 / 2) = **1.2** — a 20% *premium* to honest book value, not a 20% discount. The "cheap" contractor was never cheap; the reported book was inflated by receivables that were not really worth their carrying value. The intuition: a contractor's book value is only as solid as the collectibility of its receivables, so you must mentally write down the shaky receivables *before* you compute price-to-book — otherwise the ratio lies in exactly the direction that tempts you to buy.

## The risk that actually kills companies: receivables and payment delay

Every sector has a characteristic way that its companies die. For banks it is bad loans; for property developers it is bond maturities they cannot roll; for contractors it is **receivables and payment delay**. This is the single most important risk in the whole sector, and it deserves its own treatment because it is *not obvious* — it is precisely the companies with the biggest, most exciting order books that are most exposed to it.

Here is the mechanism, step by step. A contractor wins lots of work (big backlog, exciting story). To build it, it spends cash up front and finances the work-in-progress with debt. It bills the owners as it completes stages. But the owners — especially private property developers — are slow to pay, or *cannot* pay. The receivables pile grows and grows. The contractor, still building and still bidding for new work to keep its crews busy, keeps spending, so it takes on *more* debt to bridge the gap. Now it is carrying a huge receivables balance financed by a huge debt balance, paying interest on debt to finance money it is owed but cannot collect. As long as the receivables eventually turn into cash, this is merely expensive (the working-capital bite from our earlier example). But if the counterparties *default* — if the developers cannot pay at all — the receivables must be **written off**, turning into outright losses. On a 2-3% margin business, writing off even a fraction of a large receivables pile produces a *loss*, which erodes equity, which (since the company is leveraged) can push it toward insolvency. The order book that looked like a fortune becomes the very thing that sinks the company, because the bigger the book, the bigger the receivables it spawned.

![Before and after comparison of a healthy contractor with low receivables and debt versus a distressed contractor with huge receivables and drained cash](/imgs/blogs/construction-materials-sector-vietnam-public-investment-5.png)

That figure is the difference between the two contractors from our opening. On the left, a *healthy* contractor: receivables are modest and getting paid on schedule, cash on hand covers payroll, debt is light, and even a slow year leaves it solvent and ready to win the next contract. On the right, a *distressed* contractor: receivables have ballooned to many times the cash, the cash is drained to near zero, debt has piled up, interest is crushing, and the company is forced into losses, share dilution (issuing new shares to raise cash, which hurts existing holders), and a debt restructuring. **Both contractors can show the same revenue and the same backlog.** The difference that matters is on the balance sheet — receivables versus cash versus debt — not on the income statement. This is why, in this sector, you analyze the *balance sheet first* and the order book second. The order book tells you the story the company wants you to hear; the receivables-to-cash-to-debt picture tells you whether the story ends well.

The concrete embodiment of the right-hand panel is **Hoa Binh Construction (HBC)**. For years it was, with Coteccons, one of the two giants of Vietnamese high-rise construction — a builder of landmark towers, a name every investor knew. Its downfall was textbook: it built extensively for property developers, accumulated an enormous receivables balance from those developers, financed it with growing debt, and when the property and bond crisis of 2022-2023 left those developers unable to pay, HBC's receivables turned bad. It slid into heavy losses, its equity was eroded, it had to restructure debt and issue shares, and its stock collapsed from a respected blue chip to a distressed small cap. The lesson is brutal and specific: HBC did not fail because it lacked work. It failed because the work it had done was owed to it by counterparties who could not pay. *Backlog was never the problem. Collection was.*

There is a deeper, structural point in the HBC story that generalizes to the whole sector: a contractor's risk is *not its own*; it is *inherited from its customers*. A contractor building for the state inherits the disbursement bureaucracy's slowness (annoying, but the money usually comes). A contractor building for private property developers inherits *the developers' solvency* — and in Vietnam, that solvency depends on the corporate-bond market and the property cycle, both of which can seize up suddenly, as they did in 2022. So when you analyze a contractor, you are really analyzing the credit quality of *whoever it builds for*. A clean-looking contractor with a great order book is only as safe as the weakest large customer on its receivables list. This is why the same disbursement wave can lift a public-infrastructure contractor while burying a private-property contractor: they have the *same job* (building things) but *opposite counterparties* (a solvent state versus distressed developers), and in a thin-margin, high-leverage business, the counterparty is destiny. The single most useful question you can ask about any contractor is not "how big is the backlog?" but "who owes them the receivables, and can those people pay?"

## Common misconceptions

This sector generates more confident-but-wrong takes than almost any other on VN-Index, precisely because its surface story (public-investment boom!) is so simple and its underlying economics (thin margins, receivables, oversupply) are so unforgiving. Here are the four that cost people money.

**Misconception 1: "A big backlog means a big profit."** It does not, and this is the most expensive error in the sector. As our worked example showed, a 10 trillion dong (about \$394mn) backlog at a 3% net margin is only 300 billion dong of profit *over the entire life of the projects* — and that is *before* any receivables go bad. Backlog is a *revenue* number on a sliver-thin margin; it flatters relentlessly. Worse, a bigger backlog often means *bigger working capital and bigger receivables*, so an aggressively growing order book can actually *raise* the risk of distress rather than signal strength. Read the backlog, then immediately ask: at what margin, with what working-capital intensity, owed by whom?

**Misconception 2: "Public investment lifts every name in the sector."** It does not. Public-investment spending is *specific* — it flows to particular project packages, in particular provinces, for particular kinds of work (highways need a lot of stone, asphalt, and earthworks; an airport terminal needs a lot of structural and finishing work). A contractor or materials supplier *sitting next to the actual spending* benefits enormously; one whose work is mostly private property, or whose plants are far from the projects (and cement is freight-sensitive, so distance matters), benefits little or not at all. The "*dau tu cong* wave" is not a tide that lifts all boats — it is a set of specific contracts won by specific companies. You have to identify *which* names actually capture the spend.

**Misconception 3: "Contractors are safe — they have guaranteed government work."** Government work is *steadier demand*, but it is not *safe profit*. Public projects are won on the lowest bid (thin margins by construction), payment is tied to the slow disbursement-and-acceptance bureaucracy (heavy working capital), and a thin-margin, high-working-capital business is *fragile*, not safe. A contractor can have a full book of government work and still report a loss because financing the receivables on that work costs more than the margin on it. "Guaranteed work" and "guaranteed profit" are entirely different things, and the gap between them is where contractors go bankrupt.

**Misconception 4: "A public-investment boom fattens cement margins."** Mostly it fattens cement *volumes*, not *margins*. Because Vietnamese cement is in chronic oversupply (capacity far above domestic demand), there is always idle capacity ready to undercut any attempt to raise prices, so the spread per ton stays pinned thin even when demand is strong. The boom is real and valuable — but it shows up as *more tons sold at a similar thin spread*, geared by fixed costs into a decent profit rise, *not* as a fat pricing windfall. Expecting cement makers to behave like a pricing-power business in a boom is how you overpay for them.

## How it shows up on VN-Index

Now connect the mechanics to the tape — how the sector has actually traded, and what the disbursement cycle does to it. The general pattern is that construction and materials are a **public-investment-acceleration trade**: they run when the *rate of change* of disbursement is improving and the narrative is "the state is building," and they fade when disbursement stalls in red tape or the boom is fully priced.

![Bar chart of Vietnam public investment disbursement by year in trillion dong from 2020 to 2025 showing a rise to a record in 2023 and a higher 2025 plan](/imgs/blogs/construction-materials-sector-vietnam-public-investment-2.png)

The chart above is the demand engine in one picture: planned/disbursed public investment by year, climbing from around **470 trillion dong** in 2020 to **676 trillion dong** in 2023 — and 2023 was the record execution year, with roughly 95% of an enormous plan actually disbursed — to a **790 trillion dong** plan for 2025 (about **\$31bn**). That rising staircase is the structural bull case for the sector. But notice the texture: the line is not smooth, and *disbursement* (actual spending) has historically lagged the *plan* badly — for years, only 70-80% of the planned money got spent because of land-clearance delays, paperwork, and provincial bottlenecks. So the trade is not "public investment is big" (it is always big); the trade is "**public investment is being disbursed faster than expected**," which is what 2023 delivered and what every public-investment rally is really pricing.

**The 2023 disbursement-acceleration trade.** This is the cleanest example. Entering 2023, the property market was frozen by the bond crisis, private construction was dead, and the government responded by pushing public investment hard — an aggressive plan, relentless pressure on provinces to spend, and a sprint on the North-South Expressway. As the disbursement numbers came in strong through the year (ending near that 95% record), the public-investment names — Vinaconex (VCG) as the listed contractor most associated with the expressway, stone and aggregate suppliers feeding the highway packages, asphalt makers — were among the better performers in an otherwise difficult market. The trade worked because the *rate of change* of disbursement was clearly improving and visible in the monthly data. This is the template: a year where the government *needs* to lean on public investment (because the private economy is weak) and *executes* on disbursement is the year the sector outperforms.

**The contractor dispersion: VCG and CTD versus HBC.** But the same period showed why you cannot buy the sector as a blob. While the public-investment contractors ran, **HBC** — exposed to *private* property developers, not public infrastructure — was collapsing under bad receivables, as we have described. And **Coteccons (CTD)**, the other high-rise giant, spent the period working through its own challenges and a strategic pivot toward industrial and public work, with a much cleaner balance sheet than HBC. Three famous contractors, three completely different outcomes in the same window — because the thing that mattered was *which end of the demand chain each was exposed to* (public infrastructure versus private property) and *the quality of its balance sheet* (clean receivables versus a toxic pile). The sector index can be up while a marquee name is being delisted-adjacent. **Stock selection inside this sector is not optional; it is the entire game.**

**Why the sector is a high-beta, sentiment-driven trade.** Layer on the fact that these are mostly mid- and small-cap stocks with thin, volatile earnings, and you get a sector that the retail crowd treats as a *narrative* vehicle. When "*dau tu cong*" is the hot phrase, the names can run far and fast on the story, often well ahead of any actual earnings improvement (which lags disbursement by quarters, since revenue is recognized as work proceeds and profit only shows up after collection). When the narrative cools — disbursement disappoints, a quarter comes in weak, a receivables problem surfaces — they can fall just as hard. This is a sector where the *price* moves on the *expectation* of disbursement long before the *earnings* move on the *reality* of it, which is both the opportunity (you can be early) and the danger (the story can be fully priced before the profits ever arrive).

## The playbook: how to trade construction and building materials

This is the payoff. Everything above tells you *what the sector is*; this tells you *how to position*. The thesis is simple to state and hard to execute: **construction and materials are the most direct play on the public-investment cycle, so you buy them on a disbursement acceleration — but only the names with the balance sheet to survive — and you sell them when the boom is fully priced or disbursement stalls.**

**The core buy signal: disbursement acceleration, not just a big plan.** The sector's single best entry is when the *rate of change* of public-investment disbursement is improving — when the monthly and quarterly numbers from the Ministry of Finance and the General Statistics Office show the money actually going out the door *faster than last year and faster than expected*. Watch three things: (1) the **disbursement rate** (actual spending as a percent of plan, reported through the year — a rate climbing toward and past 80-90% is the bullish tell); (2) **flagship-project progress** (visible momentum on the North-South Expressway, Long Thanh airport, Tan Son Nhat T3 — groundbreakings, package awards, construction milestones); and (3) the **policy stance** (when the government is publicly pushing public investment as a growth lever, which it does hardest when the private economy is weak — a property bust or soft exports is, paradoxically, the setup for this trade). A big *announced* plan with no acceleration in *disbursement* is not a buy; the market has seen big plans underdeliver for years. You are buying the *acceleration*, not the *announcement*.

**The non-negotiable filter: balance-sheet quality.** Because this sector kills companies through receivables, you *never* buy the cheapest or most exciting name blindly — you buy the one that can survive. Before buying any contractor, check three balance-sheet items: **receivables** (how big relative to revenue and equity, how old, and from *whom* — public owners are slower but safer than shaky private developers), **net debt** (how much leverage is financing that working capital, and can the company service the interest in a thin-margin year), and **cash** (enough to cover near-term obligations without forced dilution). A contractor with clean, collectible receivables, modest debt, and real cash can ride the public-investment wave for years; one with a toxic receivables pile and heavy debt will detonate on the first counterparty default, no matter how good the order-book story sounds. For materials makers, the balance-sheet filter is similar — watch debt (these are leveraged, asset-heavy businesses) — plus the *location* filter: is the plant or quarry actually near the spending? (Cement is freight-sensitive; a plant far from the projects captures little of the wave.)

![Matrix of public investment beneficiaries showing contractors materials makers and property adjacent names rated on how direct the benefit margin and balance sheet risk](/imgs/blogs/construction-materials-sector-vietnam-public-investment-7.png)

The matrix above is the position-selection map. **Contractors** are the *most direct* beneficiaries (their revenue *is* the budget turned into work) but carry the *thinnest margins* (2-3%, lowest bid wins) and the *highest balance-sheet risk* (receivables, payment delay) — so they are the highest-reward, highest-risk way to play the theme, and balance-sheet selection is everything. **Building materials** makers (cement, stone, asphalt) are *indirect* (they sell the inputs, so their volume follows the builds) with *middling margins* (5-12%, but price-takers in oversupply) and *moderate* risk (asset-heavy, leveraged, oversupply) — a steadier, lower-beta way to play the same theme, with location and debt the key filters. **Property-adjacent** names (developers whose land near new roads gains value) are the *least direct* (the benefit is a slow lift in land value) with *project-based, cycle-dependent* economics and *bond-and-leverage* risk — a second-order play, more about the property cycle than the disbursement cycle. The map tells you the trade-off: the more directly a name touches the public-investment money, the thinner its margin and the heavier its balance-sheet risk. There is no free lunch in this sector — the purest exposure is also the most fragile.

**Sizing and the materials-versus-contractor barbell.** Because the contractors are the highest-beta, highest-risk expression, size them modestly and demand the cleanest balance sheets; lean the larger part of a sector position on the steadier materials makers (and the asset-heavy ones with real pricing discipline and proximity to the projects) and on the highest-quality contractor you can find. A sensible structure is a *barbell*: a core in steady, well-located materials and one clean-balance-sheet contractor, plus a smaller, higher-risk satellite in the most direct public-investment contractor — sized so that a receivables blow-up in the satellite is survivable. Never let the exciting-story contractor become the position; let the balance sheet decide the weight.

**The sell discipline.** You sell — or at least take the position down — on any of three triggers. First, **the boom is fully priced**: the stocks have run hard on the disbursement narrative while *earnings* (which lag) have not yet caught up, and valuations (P/B for contractors, EV/EBITDA for materials) are stretched against any realistic profit. The price moves on expected disbursement long before the profit arrives, so the danger is buying the story after it is fully in the price. Second, **disbursement stalls**: the monthly numbers roll over, the rate-of-change turns down, land-clearance or budget bottlenecks reappear, the policy push fades. The trade is the *acceleration*; when the acceleration stops, the trade is over. Third, **a balance-sheet red flag**: receivables balloon, a counterparty (a developer the contractor builds for) gets into trouble, debt rises faster than collections — any sign that the receivables risk is turning real. In this sector, a balance-sheet red flag is a *sell*, not a dip to buy, because the failure mode (a receivables write-off on a 2% margin) is catastrophic and fast.

**The invalidation — what proves the thesis wrong.** Be explicit about what would tell you the whole public-investment trade has failed, because a thesis without an invalidation is just hope. The construction-and-materials thesis is invalidated if: **disbursement repeatedly undershoots the plan despite a strong announced budget** (the classic Vietnamese failure — big plans that bog down in execution, so the demand never actually reaches the sector); or **the public-investment push is real and fast but the names you own do not capture it** (their work is private, or their plants are far from the projects — the wave is happening but flowing past you); or **a receivables crisis spreads** (a wave of developer defaults turns contractors' order books into write-offs, as 2022-2023 did to HBC — at which point "contractors are a public-investment play" is overwhelmed by "contractors are a credit-risk play"). Concretely: if disbursement is stalling, your specific names are not winning the packages, or receivables are deteriorating, the trade is wrong regardless of how big and exciting the headline public-investment number is. *A big budget that does not get spent, or gets spent past the companies you own, or gets spent on work that never gets paid for, is not a thesis — it is a press release.*

Tie it back to the macro. The public-investment lever is the government's most direct growth tool, and it gets pulled hardest when the other engines stall — so the construction-and-materials trade often sets up exactly when the rest of the market looks worst (a property bust, soft exports, a weak private cycle). That counter-cyclical timing is what makes the sector a useful piece of a full rotation framework: it can lead precisely when the cyclical and financial sectors are struggling, because its demand engine is *policy*, not the private cycle. Read the disbursement data, demand balance-sheet quality, size for survival, and let the rate-of-change of *spending* — not the size of the *plan* — be your signal.
