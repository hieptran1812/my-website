> [!important]
> **TL;DR** -- A sector rotation dashboard turns five scattered signals into one repeatable weekly read. You score each VN-Index sector on relative strength, cycle phase, liquidity and margin, foreign flow, and valuation-versus-its-own-history, sum the scores into a composite, and let that composite set an overweight / neutral / underweight tilt. The whole thing fits in a spreadsheet.
>
> - **Five inputs, one number.** Relative strength (is the sector beating VN-Index?), cycle phase (do rates and credit favor it?), liquidity and margin (is fuel rising?), foreign flow (are the big funds buying?), and valuation versus the sector's own history (is it cheap or stretched for itself?). Each scores +1, 0, or -1. Sum to a composite from -5 to +5.
> - **The composite sets the tilt, not the trade.** A composite of +2 or more leans overweight; -2 or less leans underweight; the noisy middle stays neutral. You size *toward* high composites and *away* from low ones -- you do not trade every wiggle.
> - **Refresh weekly, act monthly.** Recompute on the weekend; only change the tilt when a *sign* changes (a +1 becoming a -1), not when a +2 becomes a +1. That discipline is the single biggest source of return the dashboard gives you, because it stops you trading noise.
> - **The dashboard describes, it does not predict.** It tells you where leadership *is* and where it is *bending* right now -- a faster, less emotional read than the headlines. It is not a crystal ball, and a perfect backtest is not a perfect future.
> - The one number to remember: with five inputs each scoring **-1 to +1**, every sector lands on a **-5 to +5** composite, and the gap between the top and bottom sector is your rotation. This is educational, not financial advice.

In late 2021, with VN-Index grinding toward its all-time high near 1,500, a quiet thing was happening underneath the index that the front pages were not reporting. Brokerage stocks -- SSI, VND, VCI, HCM -- had been the loudest leaders of the liquidity boom all year. But by November, if you had been keeping a simple weekly scorecard, three of their five inputs had quietly flipped. Their relative strength against VN-Index had stopped making new highs. Margin balances at the brokers, the fuel that had powered them, were brushing against regulatory ceilings and could not grow much more. And their valuations, measured against their *own* five-year history, were stretched to the top of the range. The cycle and the flow still looked fine. But three of five had turned, and the composite for brokers had slipped from a roaring +4 to a wobbling +1.

A dashboard would have flashed that before the headlines did. It would not have told you "sell tomorrow" -- nothing honest does. What it would have told you is that the leadership which had defined 2021 was thinning, that the fuel tank was nearly empty, and that the smart move was to stop adding to brokers and start watching who was next. The investors who were reading their sectors that way trimmed into strength. The ones reading the headlines were still buying the top.

This post is where the entire Vietnam Sector Playbook series comes together. Across the series we built every piece in isolation -- relative strength, the investment clock, the liquidity and margin cycle, foreign flows, valuation by sector. Each is a single lens. A dashboard is the thing that holds all five lenses up at once, scores them on a common scale, and gives you one number per sector and one repeatable routine per week. Let us build it from zero, learn to score and weight each input, turn the composite into a tilt, and -- most importantly -- learn the discipline that keeps the dashboard from turning into a churn machine.

## Foundations: why a dashboard beats gut feel

Start with the problem the dashboard solves, because if you do not feel the problem you will not respect the discipline.

Here is how most people actually decide which sector to buy. They read a headline ("banks rally on credit growth"), they hear a friend mention a hot ticker, they glance at a chart that looks like it is going up, and they form a *feeling*: this sector is good right now. That feeling is real information -- it usually contains a grain of truth -- but it has three fatal flaws. It is **inconsistent** (you would score the same sector differently on a good-mood day and a bad-mood day). It is **unweighted** (the loudest signal, usually price going up, drowns out the quieter ones, like valuation being stretched). And it is **unauditable** (next month you cannot reconstruct *why* you thought banks were good, so you cannot learn from being wrong).

A dashboard fixes all three. It is, at heart, nothing more than a checklist with arithmetic. You write down the signals that actually matter -- not all of them, just the handful that move sectors -- you score each one the same way every week, and you add them up. The output is a number you can defend, compare across sectors, and look back on. The dashboard does not make you smarter than your gut. It makes you *consistent*, which over a full cycle beats smart-but-erratic almost every time.

Think of it the way a doctor uses vital signs. A doctor does not diagnose by overall vibe; they take temperature, blood pressure, heart rate, and oxygen, each on a known scale, and read them together. Any one number can mislead -- a high heart rate alone means little. Together they form a picture, and crucially, the same four numbers get taken every visit so the *trend* is visible. Your five sector inputs are the vital signs of an industry. None of them alone is a verdict. Read together, week after week, they tell you which sectors are healthy and getting healthier, and which are sick and getting sicker.

### The five inputs, and why these five

A good dashboard is ruthlessly short. The temptation is to add every indicator you have ever read about -- twenty signals, surely, beat five. We will see later why that is wrong. For now, here are the five inputs this dashboard uses, each one a full post earlier in this series, compressed to a single line:

1. **Relative strength (RS)** -- is the sector *beating* VN-Index, or lagging it? RS is the sector index divided by VN-Index; a rising RS line means the sector is winning a larger share of the market's move. This is the *price-confirmation* input: it tells you the rotation is already underway, not just that you think it should be. (See [Relative Strength: Reading Which Sector Leads VN-Index](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/relative-strength-reading-which-sector-leads-vn-index).)

2. **Cycle phase** -- does the current part of the economic cycle *favor* this sector? Early cycle (rates falling, credit loosening) favors brokers, banks, and steel; mid cycle favors industrials, property, and materials; late cycle and slowdown favor utilities, staples, and healthcare. This is the *why-now* input: it tells you whether the macro wind is at the sector's back. (See [The Business Cycle and the Investment Clock in Vietnam](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/business-cycle-investment-clock-vietnam).)

3. **Liquidity and margin** -- is the *fuel* rising? Vietnamese equities run on liquidity: system credit growth, average daily trading value on HOSE, and especially **margin** (*ký quỹ*, money borrowed from brokers to buy stocks). When margin balances expand, the highest-beta sectors get the most lift. This is the *fuel-gauge* input. (See [Liquidity and the Margin Cycle in Vietnam](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/liquidity-and-the-margin-cycle-vietnam).)

4. **Foreign flow** -- are the big foreign funds *buying* or *selling* the sector? Foreign investors (through direct buying and ETFs) move large, slow, trend-setting money. Net foreign buying into a sector is a tailwind; persistent selling is a headwind, especially for the large-cap names foreigners can actually own given **foreign room** (*room ngoại*, the ownership cap for non-Vietnamese holders). This is the *big-money* input. (See [Foreign Flows, ETFs, and the Index Effect in Vietnam](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/foreign-flows-etfs-and-the-index-effect-vietnam).)

5. **Valuation versus its own history** -- is the sector cheap or stretched *for itself*? Not cheap versus other sectors (banks always look cheaper than tech on P/E; that is structural), but cheap versus where *this* sector has traded over its own five-year range of P/E, P/B, or P/NAV. This is the *margin-of-safety* input: it tells you how much good news is already in the price. (See [Valuation by Sector: P/E, P/B, NAV, EV/EBITDA](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/valuation-by-sector-pe-pb-nav-ev-ebitda).)

Why exactly these five? Because they are *the* five forces, and they are *independent*. RS captures price. Cycle captures the macro tailwind. Liquidity captures the fuel. Foreign flow captures the marginal big buyer. Valuation captures the price you are paying. Each answers a different question, so they do not double-count. Add a sixth input that is really just "price going up in a different costume" and you have not added information, you have added weight to a signal you already counted. The skill in dashboard design is choosing inputs that are *orthogonal* -- each one tells you something the others cannot.

### A quick anchor in real money

Vietnam trades in dong, but it helps non-VN readers (and satisfies the habit of thinking in a stable unit) to anchor in dollars now and then. The exchange rate as of mid-2026 is roughly **\$1 = 25,400 VND**. So a 500 million dong equity sleeve is about **\$19,700**; a sector with an 85 trillion dong daily turnover spike is about **\$3.3bn**; a 2 trillion dong net foreign buy in a month is about **\$79mn**. Throughout this post the primary numbers are in dong -- that is what your account and the exchange quote -- and the dollar figures are reference anchors. The dashboard itself is unit-free: it works on scores, not currency.

### The dashboard itself: one grid, one read

Before we get into scoring rules, look at the artifact you are building, because the whole post is in service of this one grid. A sector rotation dashboard is a table: sectors down the side (the rows), the five inputs across the top (the columns), and a final column for the composite. Every cell holds a score -- a plus, a zero, or a minus. You read it the way a pilot reads a cockpit: not by staring at any one dial, but by scanning the whole panel for the pattern.

![Sample sector rotation scorecard grid with six sectors scored on five signals plus a composite column](/imgs/blogs/building-a-sector-rotation-dashboard-vn-index-2.png)

That grid above is the centerpiece of everything in this post. Six representative VN-Index sectors -- Banks, Brokers, Steel, Property, Utilities, IT -- run down the rows. The five inputs (RS, Cycle, Liquidity, Foreign, Valuation) run across, and the rightmost column carries the composite. Scan it the way you would in practice. The top rows light up green: Banks and Brokers have plusses across most of the panel, and their composites are the highest on the board. The bottom rows show red: Utilities is mostly minuses, and its composite is negative. Steel, Property, and IT sit in the muddy middle, with mixed cells and composites that hover near zero.

That visual -- a panel of green at the top and red at the bottom, with a noisy middle -- *is* the rotation, captured in a single image. You do not have to remember twelve headlines about six sectors. You have one grid, refreshed weekly, that tells you at a glance where leadership is concentrated and where it is draining away. Notice too that no sector is *all* one color: Banks have a stretched-valuation minus even amid their greens, and Utilities have a cheap-valuation plus amid their reds. That is the five-input design working -- each sector's score is a genuine *blend* of independent forces, not a single momentum read wearing five hats. The rest of this post is simply the detail of how each cell in that grid gets its plus, zero, or minus, and how you turn the composite column into the only thing that touches your money: a tilt.

The grid also makes one structural truth visible that prose hides. The columns are *not* equally lit across all sectors. The Liquidity column tends to move *together* -- when the fuel is rising, most sectors get a plus in that column at once, because liquidity is a market-wide tide. The Foreign column, by contrast, is sharp and selective -- it lights up only for the large caps foreigners can own, and stays grey for the small-cap corners they never touch. RS and Valuation are the most *sector-specific* columns: they are where one sector genuinely diverges from another. Learning to read which columns are moving in unison and which are diverging is half the skill of reading the dashboard, and you cannot see it at all until you lay the scores out as a grid instead of a list.

## How to score each input: from a messy signal to +1, 0, or -1

The heart of the dashboard is turning each fuzzy signal into a clean, discrete score. We use the simplest scale that works: **+1, 0, -1**. Plus one means the signal is a tailwind for the sector. Zero means it is neutral or ambiguous. Minus one means it is a headwind. Three states, no false precision.

Why three states and not, say, a continuous 0-to-100 score? Because the inputs are *noisy*, and a coarse scale is honest about that noise. The difference between an RS score of 71 and 68 is meaningless -- it is measurement jitter, not signal. The difference between "RS clearly rising" (+1) and "RS clearly falling" (-1) is real. A three-state score throws away the fake precision and keeps the real signal. It also makes the dashboard *fast*: you can eyeball five charts and assign five scores in a few minutes, every week, without agonizing.

There is a deeper statistical reason to prefer the coarse scale, and it is worth understanding because it explains why simple dashboards beat elaborate ones. Every signal you measure is a mix of *signal* (the real, persistent tendency of the sector) and *noise* (random week-to-week jitter that means nothing). A fine-grained score -- 0 to 100 -- spends its resolution faithfully recording *both*. It records the noise just as precisely as the signal, and because there is usually more noise than signal in any single week, a fine score mostly records noise. The three-state score is a deliberate, brutal filter: by collapsing everything into +1 / 0 / -1, you force the measurement to ignore small moves and only register changes large enough to plausibly be signal. You are trading away precision you cannot use for robustness you can. In the language of estimation, you are accepting a little bias to kill a lot of variance -- and on noisy financial data, that trade almost always pays.

The practical upshot is that the *width* of your 0 band is the single most important design choice in the whole dashboard. Set it too narrow and every input flickers between +1 and -1 on noise; set it too wide and the input never registers a real change. The right width is "wide enough that ordinary weekly wiggle stays at 0, narrow enough that a genuine three-month trend escapes it." For RS, that means requiring a move of a few percent over a quarter, not a few tenths of a percent over a week. We will see the same principle govern the *time* dimension later, where the deadband becomes a sign-change-only trading rule.

![How to score one signal relative strength rising above one scores plus one flat scores zero falling below one scores minus one](/imgs/blogs/building-a-sector-rotation-dashboard-vn-index-4.png)

Look at the scoring figure. It shows the rule for one input -- relative strength -- but the *pattern* is the same for all five. There is a clearly-good state (score +1), a clearly-bad state (score -1), and an in-between zone that scores 0. The art is in defining the bands so that most weeks a signal sits cleanly in one of the three, and the 0 band absorbs the genuine ambiguity. Here is the scoring rule for each of the five inputs.

**Relative strength.** Compute the sector index divided by VN-Index, normalized to 1.00 at a start date a year or so back. Score **+1** if the RS line is *above* its level three months ago *and* sloping up (the sector is winning share and accelerating). Score **-1** if RS is below its three-month level and sloping down. Score **0** if it is flat or whipsawing. A faster reader can use the 13-week relative return directly: positive and improving is +1, negative and worsening is -1.

The thing to watch with RS is that it scores the *direction of leadership*, not the level of price. A sector can be down in absolute terms and still score +1 if it is falling *less* than the index -- in a bear market, the sector losing you the least money is the leader, and RS catches that when raw price does not. The opposite trap is more common in Vietnam's frequent liquidity rallies: a sector can be up a roaring 20% and still score -1 if the index is up 30%, because it is losing share even while it makes you money. Score the *ratio's* slope, never the price's. This is exactly why RS is the price-confirmation input: it confirms that the rotation you suspect from the fundamentals is actually showing up in where the money is going, which no fundamental input can tell you.

**Cycle phase.** Locate where the economy is on the investment clock using three macro prints: the State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) policy rate direction, system credit growth, and CPI. Then ask whether *this* sector is favored in that phase. Brokers and banks score **+1** in early cycle (rates falling, credit reaccelerating); utilities and staples score **+1** in late cycle and slowdown; and so on. A sector gets **0** if the cycle is neither clearly for nor against it, and **-1** if the cycle is actively working against it (e.g. a high-beta broker in a rate-hiking slowdown).

There are really two steps hiding inside this one score, and beginners conflate them. Step one is *locate the phase* -- a single, market-wide judgment about where Vietnam's economy sits, made the same way for every sector. You read the direction of the SBV policy rate (cutting = easing toward early cycle; hiking = tightening toward late cycle), whether credit growth is accelerating or being throttled by the credit ceiling (*room tín dụng*, the SBV's cap on how much each bank may lend), and whether CPI is tame or climbing. Those three prints put you somewhere on the clock: early cycle, mid cycle, late cycle, or slowdown. Step two is *map the phase to the sector* -- a fixed lookup you can memorize. Early cycle favors the high-beta financials (brokers, banks, then steel); mid cycle favors the builders (industrials, property, construction materials); late cycle and slowdown favor the defensives (utilities, staples, healthcare). The phase is judged once for the whole market; the +1/0/-1 is just reading that one phase off the lookup for each sector. The most common mistake is reading the prints *backward* -- treating rising rates as good for banks because "banks earn more on loans," when in Vietnam a hiking cycle usually means a throttled credit ceiling and a falling stock market that drags the high-beta banks down with it. Read the cycle for *risk appetite*, not for a single line of the income statement.

**Liquidity and margin.** Score the *direction* of the fuel. **+1** if margin balances and average daily trading value are rising month-over-month and credit growth is accelerating. **-1** if margin is contracting (brokers tightening, or balances near regulatory caps and falling) and turnover is shrinking. **0** if flat. This input is mostly a *market-wide* read -- it lifts or sinks all sectors together -- but it lifts the high-beta sectors (brokers, small-cap property) the most, so you can tilt the score up by one notch for the highest-beta sectors when liquidity is clearly expanding.

Liquidity matters more in Vietnam than in most markets, and the reason is structural. The market is dominated by retail investors trading with borrowed money: *margin* (*ký quỹ*) is money you borrow from your broker against your existing shares to buy more shares, and when margin balances across the brokerage industry are expanding, there is simply more buying power chasing the same number of shares -- prices rise on flow, not fundamentals. The catch is that margin has a hard ceiling: regulations cap how much a broker can lend relative to its equity, so in a hot market the brokers' margin books fill up and *cannot grow further* no matter how much demand there is. When you see margin balances brushing those caps, the fuel gauge is near empty even if the tank still looks full -- that is a quiet -1 in the making. The watch items are three: industry margin balance (rising or capped?), average daily trading value on HOSE (a turnover spike from 18 to 24 trillion dong, about **\$945mn**, is fuel arriving), and system credit growth, which feeds the whole thing upstream. Because this input is a tide, it tends to set the *baseline* for the whole board at once; the sector-specific work is remembering that the same tide lifts a high-beta broker twice as much as a low-beta utility.

**Foreign flow.** Look at net foreign buying or selling of the sector over the trailing month or quarter. **+1** for clear net buying (or a sector heavily owned by an ETF that is seeing inflows). **-1** for persistent net selling. **0** for roughly balanced flow. Be careful: foreigners are concentrated in large caps with foreign room, so this input is sharpest for banks, large property, and the index heavyweights, and nearly mute for sectors foreigners barely touch (small-cap media, for instance) -- score those 0 unless there is a clear reason.

The reason foreign flow gets its own input -- rather than being folded into liquidity -- is that foreign money behaves *differently* from domestic margin money. It is large, slow, and trend-following on a quarterly horizon, set by global fund managers and the index funds that track Vietnam. When the big foreign ETFs see inflows, they must buy the large-cap names in proportion to their weights, and that buying is a steady, mechanical tailwind for a handful of heavyweights regardless of the domestic mood. The complication unique to Vietnam is **foreign room** (*room ngoại*): each stock has a legal cap on how much of it non-Vietnamese investors may own, and the best names (some banks especially) are *full* -- foreigners cannot buy more even if they want to. A sector where the marquee names are at full room cannot get a foreign-flow tailwind through those names; the flow has to find the second-tier stocks or go elsewhere. So when you score this input, ask two questions: is foreign flow into the sector net positive this month, and is there *room* for it to continue? A sector that foreigners love but cannot buy because room is full scores a soft 0, not a +1 -- the desire is there but the mechanism is blocked.

**Valuation versus its own history.** Place the sector's current P/E (or P/B for banks, P/NAV for property) on its own five-year percentile. Score **+1** if it is cheap *for itself* -- in the bottom third of its historical range. Score **-1** if it is stretched -- top third. Score **0** in the middle. The crucial discipline is the words *for itself*: a bank P/B of 1.5x might be the *cheap* end for banks and the *expensive* end for a utility; you compare each sector only to its own history.

The reason you compare each sector only to its own history -- rather than ranking all sectors on one valuation table -- is that different sectors *deserve* different multiples permanently. Banks structurally trade on P/B (price-to-book) because their value is their balance sheet; property trades on P/NAV (price to net asset value) because their value is land they have not yet sold; a fast-growing IT name like FPT trades on a high P/E because earnings are compounding. Comparing FPT's P/E to a bank's P/E tells you nothing -- one is a growth stock and one is a balance-sheet machine, and the gap between them is permanent structure, not a signal. What *is* a signal is FPT's P/E versus *FPT's own* five-year range, or the banks' P/B versus the banks' own range. That self-comparison strips out the permanent structural premium or discount and leaves only the part that mean-reverts: how expensive is this sector *right now relative to how expensive it usually is*. Use the right multiple per sector (P/B for banks and insurers, P/NAV for property, P/E for the rest, EV/EBITDA for capital-heavy industrials), and always read the percentile, never the raw number.

#### Worked example: scoring brokers in an early-cycle week

Walk one sector through all five inputs on a single weekend, so you can feel the machine turn. Suppose it is early in a new cycle: the SBV has just cut its policy rate from 6.0% toward 4.5%, credit is reaccelerating, and CPI is tame near 3.3%. You sit down with the securities (brokers) sector.

- **RS:** the brokers index divided by VN-Index has risen from 1.00 to 1.07 over the past quarter and is still sloping up. Brokers are beating the index. Score **+1**.
- **Cycle:** rates falling and credit reaccelerating is the textbook *early-cycle* setup, and brokers are the highest-beta early-cycle leader. The macro wind is squarely at their back. Score **+1**.
- **Liquidity / margin:** margin balances across the brokers have grown about 12% month-over-month and average daily HOSE turnover has jumped from roughly 18 trillion to 24 trillion dong (about **\$945mn**). Fuel is rising, and brokers are the most fuel-sensitive sector. Score **+1**.
- **Foreign flow:** foreigners are roughly flat on the brokers this month -- some buying of SSI, some trimming of VCI -- no clear trend. Score **0**.
- **Valuation:** the brokers' P/B sits at the 75th percentile of its own five-year range. Not bubble territory, but not cheap; a lot of the early-cycle good news is already priced. Score **-1**.

Sum: +1 +1 +1 +0 -1 = **+2**. A composite of +2 is a clear overweight. The intuition: four of five forces favor brokers, and only the price you are paying argues against -- exactly the profile of an early-cycle leader you want to own *but not chase*, because the valuation score is your reminder that you are buying strength, not value.

## Weighting: are all five inputs equal?

The simplest dashboard weights all five inputs equally -- each contributes +1, 0, or -1, and the composite is a straight sum from -5 to +5. That is a perfectly good starting point, and I recommend a beginner start exactly there. Equal weighting has a hidden virtue: it is robust. Because you do not really *know* the true importance of each signal, pretending you do (by assigning fancy weights) usually adds error, not accuracy. Equal weights are the honest default.

But the five inputs are not, in truth, equally important for every purpose, and once you have run the equal-weight version for a few cycles you may want to tilt the weights. Here is the reasoning, not a recipe to copy blindly.

![Weighting scheme for the five dashboard inputs showing each input its weight and what a high score means](/imgs/blogs/building-a-sector-rotation-dashboard-vn-index-5.png)

The weighting figure lays out a sensible *starting* set of weights and what a high score on each input is telling you. The logic behind it:

- **Relative strength and cycle phase deserve the most weight** because they are the two signals most directly tied to *whether the rotation is actually happening and why*. RS is the market voting in real time; cycle is the fundamental reason the vote is happening. When these two agree, the rotation has both a cause and a confirmation. A common choice is to give each of them a weight of, say, 1.5 instead of 1.0.

- **Liquidity and foreign flow are the *fuel and the marginal buyer*** -- powerful but mostly *market-wide* rather than sector-specific. Liquidity lifts everything; foreign flow concentrates in a few large caps. They earn a full weight of 1.0 each but rarely more, because they tell you about the tide more than about which boat.

- **Valuation deserves the *least* weight as a timing signal** -- not because it does not matter (it matters enormously for your eventual return) but because it is a *terrible* timer. Cheap sectors stay cheap for years; expensive sectors get more expensive in a mania. Valuation's job in the dashboard is not to time the rotation but to *modulate your conviction and size*: a +3 composite on a cheap sector is a higher-conviction overweight than a +3 on a stretched one. Many practitioners give valuation a weight of 0.5 in the timing composite and then use it separately to cap position size. This is the subtle point most beginners miss.

If you do apply weights, the composite becomes a weighted sum. With weights of 1.5 (RS), 1.5 (cycle), 1.0 (liquidity), 1.0 (foreign), 0.5 (valuation), the range becomes -5.5 to +5.5, and the brokers example above scores 1.5 + 1.5 + 1.0 + 0 - 0.5 = **+3.5** -- a stronger overweight than the equal-weight +2, because the two heaviest inputs (RS and cycle) were both positive. But I cannot stress this enough: **do not start here**. Start equal-weight, run it through a full cycle, see where it fooled you, and only then adjust. Weights tuned on your own painful experience beat weights copied from a blog.

#### Worked example: comparing two sectors head to head

The dashboard earns its keep when you stop scoring sectors in isolation and start *ranking* them. Take the same early-cycle week and score utilities (power, water, gas) alongside the brokers from before.

- **RS:** the utilities index divided by VN-Index has *fallen* from 1.00 to 0.96 over the quarter -- utilities are lagging the rising index. Score **-1**.
- **Cycle:** early cycle, with rates falling and risk appetite rising, is the *worst* phase for defensive utilities; capital is leaving safety for beta. Score **-1**.
- **Liquidity / margin:** rising liquidity does lift utilities a little, but they are low-beta, so the lift is muted -- neither clearly helped nor hurt. Score **0**.
- **Foreign flow:** foreigners are modest net buyers of a couple of large-cap power names for the dividend. Score **+1**.
- **Valuation:** utilities' P/E sits at the 30th percentile of its own range -- genuinely cheap for itself, because everyone has rotated *out*. Score **+1**.

Sum: -1 -1 +0 +1 +1 = **0**. Now put them side by side. Brokers at **+2**, utilities at **0**. The gap of two points *is the rotation*: in an early-cycle week, the dashboard says tilt toward brokers and away from utilities, even though utilities are the cheaper sector. The intuition: cheapness without a catalyst is a value trap in the wrong phase, and the composite captures that by letting cheap-but-unloved utilities net out to a flat zero while brokers, riding four tailwinds, pull ahead.

## From composite to tilt: overweight, neutral, underweight

You now have a composite score per sector, somewhere between -5 and +5 (or -5.5 to +5.5 weighted). The last step is the only one that touches your money: turning each composite into a **tilt** -- a decision to hold more, the same, or less of that sector than your neutral baseline.

The mapping is deliberately coarse, because the composite is noisy and you do not want to react to every one-point wiggle:

- **Composite ≥ +2: overweight.** The sector has a clear majority of tailwinds. Hold *more* than your baseline weight.
- **Composite ≤ -2: underweight.** A clear majority of headwinds. Hold *less* than baseline (or nothing).
- **Composite between -1 and +1: neutral.** The signals roughly cancel. Hold your baseline weight and do nothing.

The wide neutral band in the middle is the whole point. It is a *deadband* -- a deliberate zone of inaction that absorbs noise. A sector flickering between +1 and -1 week to week is not telling you anything actionable; it is telling you the forces are balanced. The deadband stops you from churning your portfolio every time a single input twitches. Most weeks, most sectors sit in the deadband, and you do nothing. The dashboard's value is concentrated in the few sectors that climb to +3 or sink to -3 and *stay there*.

What does "overweight" mean in practice? It means relative to a baseline. Decide your neutral allocation first -- maybe you would normally hold each of, say, six sectors at roughly equal weight. An overweight sector gets pushed above its baseline (you put more of your sleeve into it); an underweight gets pushed below or to zero. The composite *score* tells you the direction and rough magnitude of the tilt; it does not give you an exact percentage. We make that concrete next.

It is worth pausing on *why* the tilt is relative to a baseline rather than an absolute buy/sell instruction, because this is what makes the dashboard a portfolio tool rather than a stock-tip generator. A baseline is your neutral, opinion-free allocation -- what you would hold if you had no view at all, usually something close to the sectors' weights in the index or a simple equal split. The dashboard's job is not to replace that baseline; it is to *tilt* it. An overweight does not mean "go all in on banks"; it means "hold somewhat more banks than your neutral, funded by holding somewhat less of the underweights." This framing has two huge benefits. First, it bounds your risk: even a +5 sector only earns a *tilt*, not a concentration, so a single wrong call cannot blow up the portfolio. Second, it keeps you diversified by construction: because every overweight is funded by an underweight, you are always holding a spread of sectors, just leaning. The dashboard tunes the lean; the baseline keeps you honest. A reader who treats a high composite as a green light to bet the farm has misunderstood the entire instrument -- the composite sets the *size of a lean*, and the lean is deliberately modest because a five-signal score, however disciplined, supports only a modest edge.

#### Worked example: turning the tilt into a dong allocation

You run a 500 million dong equity sleeve -- about **\$19,700** -- that you want to spread across six sectors by their composite scores. This weekend's scores come out:

| Sector | Composite | Tilt |
|---|---|---|
| Brokers | +2 | overweight |
| Banks | +2 | overweight |
| Steel | +1 | neutral |
| Property | 0 | neutral |
| IT | +1 | neutral |
| Utilities | -2 | underweight |

A clean way to allocate is to shift each sector's weight from a 1/6 baseline (about 83mn dong each) by its composite. Give the two **+2 overweights** an extra share, hold the **+1 and 0 neutrals** near baseline, and cut the **-2 underweight** toward zero, redistributing what you take from it to the overweights.

A concrete split: Brokers and Banks at 110mn dong each (about **\$4,330** each, overweight); Steel, Property, and IT at 85mn dong each (about **\$3,350**, baseline); Utilities at 25mn dong (about **\$985**, underweight). That sums to 110 + 110 + 85 + 85 + 85 + 25 = 500mn dong. The two overweights together hold 220mn dong, **44%** of the sleeve, versus the 33% they would hold equal-weighted -- that 11-point tilt *is* your active bet. The intuition: the dashboard did not tell you to bet the farm on brokers; it told you to lean about a tenth of your sleeve toward the strongest sectors and away from the weakest, which is exactly the size of edge a coarse five-signal score can honestly support.

## How often to refresh, and how to avoid overtrading

Here is where most dashboards go to die. A new investor builds a beautiful scorecard, refreshes it daily, and discovers that scores wiggle constantly -- and starts trading every wiggle. Within a month they have paid more in transaction costs and given up more to bid-ask spreads and bad timing than the dashboard could ever earn. The dashboard did not fail; the *cadence* did.

The rule is simple and it is the most important rule in this post: **refresh weekly, act on sign changes only.** Recompute all five scores once a week, on the weekend, when the market is closed and your head is clear. But change your actual positions *only* when a score changes *sign* in a way that moves the composite across a tilt boundary -- a sector crossing from neutral (+1) into overweight (+3), or from overweight back down through the deadband. A composite drifting from +3 to +2 is *not* a trade; both are overweight. A composite flipping from +2 to -2 *is* a trade; the tilt has genuinely reversed.

This is the deadband doing its job across *time* as well as across the score range. The combination -- a wide neutral band and a sign-change-only trigger -- is what separates a disciplined rotation strategy from a hyperactive churn machine. The dashboard becomes a slow, deliberate instrument: it nudges your tilt a few times a year, when leadership genuinely rotates, and otherwise tells you to sit still. Sitting still is a position, and most weeks it is the right one.

![Gut feel investing scattered and emotional versus dashboard driven one repeatable read](/imgs/blogs/building-a-sector-rotation-dashboard-vn-index-7.png)

The before/after figure captures the whole philosophy. On the left is gut-feel investing: a tangle of headlines, tips, fear, and a chart that looks like it is going up -- scattered inputs, an emotional verdict, a different answer every day. On the right is the dashboard: the same world, but funneled through five fixed signals into one number and one decision. Same market, same information. The difference is that the right-hand process is *repeatable* -- you will reach the same conclusion on a good-mood day and a bad-mood day -- and *auditable* -- next month you can see exactly why you tilted, and learn if you were wrong.

#### Worked example: the cost of trading the noise

Quantify why the deadband matters, because "avoid overtrading" is easy to say and hard to feel. Suppose round-trip transaction costs in Vietnam -- brokerage fee both ways, the 0.1% sale tax, and a realistic slice of bid-ask spread and slippage -- total about **0.5%** of the traded value each time you rotate a position. That is roughly 50,000 dong per 10 million dong moved.

Now suppose your brokers composite is genuinely hovering around neutral and flickering +1, 0, +1, -1, 0 week to week -- pure noise. If you *trade every flicker* on a 110mn dong broker position, moving it in and out, you might churn that position eight times in a quarter. Eight round trips at 0.5% on 110mn dong is 8 × 0.5% × 110mn = **4.4mn dong** (about **\$173**) in costs in three months -- and you have *nothing to show for it*, because the underlying score never actually changed its tilt. Annualized, that is roughly 17.6mn dong, about **3.5%** of your entire 500mn dong sleeve, vaporized on noise. A sign-change-only rule would have triggered *zero* of those trades, because the composite never crossed a tilt boundary. The intuition: in a five-signal dashboard, the deadband is not a nicety -- it is worth several percent a year, which is often more than the rotation edge itself, so protecting it is protecting your returns.

## Common misconceptions

Three beliefs will quietly ruin a dashboard. Each is seductive, and each is wrong.

**"More signals make a better dashboard."** This is the most common and most damaging mistake. Adding inputs feels like adding rigor, but most extra signals are *correlated* with ones you already have -- a sixth "momentum" indicator is just RS in a costume, a seventh "sentiment" gauge is just liquidity again. Correlated inputs do not add information; they *double-count* a signal you already counted, secretly overweighting it and unbalancing the composite. Worse, more inputs mean more noise: with twenty signals, several are flickering at random every week, so your composite jitters and you trade more. The honest finding from decades of quantitative work is that a *handful* of independent signals beats a kitchen sink of correlated ones. Five orthogonal inputs is not a compromise; it is close to optimal. If you must add a sixth, it has to answer a question the first five cannot -- and almost none do.

**"The dashboard predicts the future."** It does not, and treating it as a forecast is how you get hurt. The dashboard is a *description of the present*: it tells you where leadership *is* and where it is *bending right now*, faster and less emotionally than the headlines. That is genuinely valuable -- a clear read of the present is rarer than it sounds -- but it is not prophecy. A sector at +4 today can be at -1 next month if the cycle turns or liquidity reverses. The composite changes *as the world changes*; it does not see around corners. Use it to stay aligned with current leadership, not to predict where leadership will go. The honest framing is: the dashboard keeps you reading the market *as it is*, which is most of the battle, but it will never tell you tomorrow's news today.

**"A perfect backtest means a perfect future."** Suppose you tune your weights and thresholds until the dashboard would have produced spectacular returns over the last five years of VN-Index data. That number is almost entirely *fiction*. You have **overfit**: you bent the rules to fit the exact wiggles of the past, including the random ones, and those random wiggles will not repeat. A backtest that requires precisely 1.5 weight on RS and a +2.3 threshold to look good is a backtest that has memorized noise. The dashboards that survive contact with the future are the *robustly simple* ones -- equal weights, round thresholds, a wide deadband -- precisely because they did not chase the past's randomness. If your backtest looks too good, be suspicious, not pleased. The goal is a dashboard that would have worked *roughly* across many different periods, not *perfectly* across one.

## How it shows up on VN-Index

Let us read a real-style rotation through the dashboard, the way you would on an actual weekend, using the kind of sector picture VN-Index throws off in an early-cycle recovery.

![Sample sector relative strength snapshot heatmap of thirteen week relative performance versus VN-Index by sector](/imgs/blogs/building-a-sector-rotation-dashboard-vn-index-3.png)

Start with the RS input, because it is the fastest to read. The heatmap above ranks eight sectors by their 13-week relative performance against VN-Index in a snapshot. At the top, Brokers (+8%) and IT (+9%) are winning share hand over fist; Banks (+6%) and Steel (+3%) are solidly ahead; Retail (+2%) is roughly tracking the index; and at the bottom, Property (-3%), Utilities (-4%), and F&B (-5%) are bleeding share -- they are up in price in this rising market, but losing the race. In one glance, the RS column of your dashboard is filled in: +1 for Brokers, IT, Banks, Steel; 0 for Retail; -1 for Property, Utilities, F&B.

Now layer the other four inputs on the leaders. For **Brokers**: cycle is early (+1), liquidity is expanding and they are the most fuel-sensitive sector (+1), foreign flow is mixed (0), and valuation is stretched after the run (-1). Composite: +1 +1 +1 +0 -1 = **+2**, overweight. For **Banks**: RS +1, cycle early and favorable (+1), liquidity helps (+1), foreigners are net buyers of the large-cap banks they can own (+1), and bank P/B is still only mid-range historically (0). Composite: +1 +1 +1 +1 +0 = **+4**, a *strong* overweight -- the highest-conviction sector on the board, because banks have RS, the cycle, the fuel, *and* the big foreign buyer all pointing the same way, with valuation not yet a brake.

![Investment clock cycle phase input SBV policy rate and CPI inflation from 2021 to 2024 locating the phase](/imgs/blogs/building-a-sector-rotation-dashboard-vn-index-6.png)

The cycle-phase input deserves its own look, because it is the one most people get wrong by reading it backward. The figure plots two macro series that locate the phase: the SBV policy rate and CPI inflation, 2021 through 2024. Read the *direction*, not the level. The rate falling from 6.0% (2022) toward 4.5% (2023-24) while CPI stays tame near 3.3% is the signature of an *early-cycle* setup -- easing into low inflation, exactly the wind that favors the high-beta financials at the top of the RS heatmap. Had the chart shown rates *rising* into *climbing* inflation (the 2022 setup), the cycle scores would invert: the same brokers and banks would lose their +1 cycle tailwind, and defensives would gain it. The cycle input is a *phase locator*, and these two lines are where you locate it.

Reading the two figures together is the dashboard in action. The heatmap tells you *who is already leading* (price). The clock tells you *whether the leadership makes sense for this phase* (why). When the price leaders and the phase favorites are the *same* sectors -- as they are here, with financials leading an early-cycle easing -- the rotation has both confirmation and a cause, and your highest composites (Banks +4, Brokers +2) sit exactly where theory says they should. That agreement is the dashboard telling you the rotation is real, not a head-fake.

Now play it forward the way late 2021 actually went, to see the dashboard *bend*. Months later, suppose the SBV has begun hiking, credit is throttled by the ceiling, a corporate-bond scare has frozen property funding, and margin balances are slammed into their caps and falling. Re-score the board. Brokers: RS rolls from +1 to -1 (they stop leading), cycle flips to -1 (hiking is poison for beta), liquidity flips to -1 (margin contracting), foreign turns to -1 (de-risking), valuation finally cheapens to +1. New composite: -1 -1 -1 -1 +1 = **-3**, a clear underweight. The sector that was +2 in the easing is -3 in the tightening. *That* swing, from +2 to -3, is the dashboard catching a full rotation -- and it caught it input by input, weeks before any single headline declared the bull market over.

The crucial thing to notice is *how* the dashboard bent: not all at once, but one column at a time. RS rolled over first, while everyone was still cheering the price highs. Then liquidity, as margin balances hit the ceiling. Then the cycle score, as the SBV's hiking became undeniable. Then foreign flow, as the global funds de-risked. Each input is a separate early-warning light, and they did not all flash on the same week -- they cascaded over a couple of months, and the composite slid down with them, from +2 to +1 to -1 to -3. An investor reading the headlines saw nothing actionable until the bond crisis was front-page news and the property names had already halved. An investor reading the five-column grid saw the greens turn amber turn red, column by column, and had every opportunity to trim before the crowd. That is the entire value proposition of the dashboard in one paragraph: it does not predict the storm, but it watches five different barometers at once, so you feel the pressure dropping long before the rain.

## The playbook: the weekly routine, the tilt rules, the invalidation

Here is the entire dashboard distilled into something you can run every Sunday evening with a spreadsheet and a cup of coffee.

**The weekly routine (about 30 minutes).**
1. **Pull five inputs for each sector you track** (start with the six to eight biggest: Banks, Brokers, Steel, Property, Utilities, IT, Retail, F&B). For RS, the 13-week relative return versus VN-Index. For cycle, the latest SBV rate move, credit-growth print, and CPI. For liquidity, the month-over-month change in margin balances and average daily HOSE turnover. For foreign flow, the trailing-month net buy/sell by sector. For valuation, each sector's current P/E or P/B against its own five-year percentile.
2. **Score each input +1 / 0 / -1** using the rules above. Be decisive; the 0 band is for genuine ambiguity, not for cowardice.
3. **Sum to a composite** (equal weight to start). You now have one number per sector, -5 to +5.
4. **Map composites to tilts:** ≥ +2 overweight, ≤ -2 underweight, the rest neutral.
5. **Compare this week's tilts to last week's.** Change a position *only* where a composite crossed a tilt boundary.

**The tilt rules.**
- **Overweight (composite ≥ +2):** hold the sector above your baseline weight; within it, favor the leaders (the highest-RS names with room for foreigners). Size up most aggressively when valuation is *not* the input dragging the score -- a +2 built on cheapness has more room than a +2 built on stretched momentum.
- **Neutral (-1 to +1):** hold baseline, do nothing. This is most sectors most weeks.
- **Underweight (composite ≤ -2):** cut toward or to zero. Do not short on the dashboard alone; the dashboard is a *relative* tool, and an underweight in a rising market can still rise.
- **Position cap from valuation:** even on a +4 sector, if valuation is at the top of its range, cap the position size -- let valuation veto *size*, not direction.

**The invalidation -- when to stop trusting the dashboard.**
- **A sign change, not a drift, moves you.** If a composite slips within the same tilt band, sit still. Only a boundary crossing acts.
- **If the whole board flips at once** (every sector's liquidity and cycle scores turn together), the *market regime* changed, not a single rotation. Reduce gross exposure first, re-tilt second -- a regime change is a market-level event the sector dashboard cannot fully capture.
- **If your composites stop matching reality** -- the +4 sectors keep underperforming the -2 sectors for a couple of months -- something is broken: an input is mis-scored, your data is stale, or the market is in a phase your five signals do not read well (a pure liquidity mania, say, where valuation and cycle are simply ignored). Stop, audit each input against what actually happened, and fix the broken one before trusting the composite again.
- **The dashboard never overrides risk management.** Position sizing, diversification, and your stop discipline sit *above* the dashboard. The composite tells you *which* sectors to lean toward; it never tells you to bet more than you can afford to lose on any of them.

Run this for one full cycle. You will mis-score inputs, you will be early and late, and the composite will occasionally point at a sector that promptly falls over. That is fine and expected -- the dashboard is not a prophet. What it gives you is a *consistent, auditable, unemotional* read of where VN-Index leadership is and where it is bending, refreshed every week, acted on only when it genuinely turns. Over a full cycle, consistent-and-disciplined beats smart-but-erratic, and the dashboard is how you become the first kind of investor instead of the second.
