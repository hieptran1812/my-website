> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Vietnamese utilities (power generators like POW, REE, NT2, GEG, HDG, PC1; water like BWE, TDM; gas distribution) earn regulated or contracted cash flows, so they lag in a liquidity-driven bull but cushion the bust — they are the defensive end of VN-Index.
>
> - A utility's earnings are simple: a capacity payment for being available, plus dispatch (MWh sold) times tariff, minus fuel cost. Two of those legs are contracted, which is why cash flows are stable and beta is low (utilities ~0.6, water ~0.5 vs the index at 1.0).
> - The structural tailwind is enormous: Vietnam's electricity demand has grown near 9% a year, from 192 TWh in 2018 to ~300 TWh in 2025, and the PDP8 master plan calls for roughly \$136bn of new build, tilting the mix hard toward solar and wind by 2030.
> - But high demand growth does not mean high stock returns — the cash gets reinvested into capex, not always handed to you. The investable edge in utilities is the dividend yield (water ~6%, power ~5%, vs the index at ~1.8%) and the low beta, not torrid earnings growth.
> - The one number to remember: utilities and water carry roughly **half** the index's beta and pay roughly **3x** its dividend yield. That trade-off — give up upside, keep income, lose less in the crash — is the whole sector in one line.

## The drawdown where the boring stocks won

In the first half of 2022, VN-Index went from a euphoric peak near 1,530 in April to a panicked low near 870 by mid-November — a fall of more than 40% in seven months. The trigger was a credit and corporate-bond crisis (we cover the mechanics in the real-estate post linked below), but the experience for a retail investor was simpler and more brutal: the stocks that had led the 2021 bull led the way down, and they fell the hardest.

A broker like SSI or VND, which had tripled in 2021, gave back two-thirds of its value. Steel names like HPG halved. Property developers that had run on bond-funded land banks fell 60–70%, and a handful never recovered. The high-beta book — the very stocks that made you feel like a genius in 2021 — was a wealth-destruction machine in 2022. If you were 100% in cyclicals and growth, your account did not just dip; it was cut in half.

Now look at what a Vietnamese power generator did over the same window. Nhon Trach 2 (NT2), a gas-fired plant with a long-term power purchase agreement, fell too — everything falls in a 40% index drawdown, because foreign funds and margin-financed retail sell what they can, not what they want. But NT2 fell maybe 15–20% from its high, kept paying a cash dividend out of contracted cash flow, and by the time the dust settled it had given back a fraction of what the brokers gave back. A water utility like Binh Duong Water (BWE) behaved the same way: a shallow drawdown, an uninterrupted dividend, a near-vertical recovery once the panic passed. The boring stocks won the year that mattered. This post is about *why* — the business model that makes utilities defensive, the structural demand growth that underwrites them, and the playbook for using them when the cycle turns.

![Utility cash machine flow diagram capacity payment plus dispatch times tariff minus fuel cost to stable cash flow and dividends](/imgs/blogs/utilities-power-water-gas-sector-vietnam-defensive-cash-1.png)

The figure above is the entire sector in one picture, and we will spend the next several thousand words unpacking each box. But hold the shape in your head: two paid revenue legs (capacity and energy), one big variable cost (fuel), and what is left over is a steady stream that turns into dividends. That stability is the product. A broker sells volatility; a utility sells the absence of it.

It is worth saying plainly what this post is *not* arguing, so the thesis is honest. It is not arguing that utilities are the best stocks to own — over a full bull market they will badly lag the brokers, steel and property names that double and triple, and if your only goal is maximum return in a rising market, you would underweight them on purpose. The argument is narrower and more useful: utilities are the most *reliable* part of a Vietnamese portfolio, the part that turns regulated and contracted cash into a steady dividend regardless of the cycle, and the part most likely to still be standing after a crash. They are a tool — a specific tool for a specific job (defense, income, late-cycle rotation, dry powder for the bust) — and the rest of this post is about understanding the tool well enough to know exactly when to reach for it and when to leave it in the drawer.

## Foundations: what a utility actually earns

Before any trading view, you need to understand how a utility makes money, because the *shape* of its earnings — not just the level — is what makes it defensive. We will build this from zero.

### The everyday analogy: the toll bridge and the rented apartment

A power plant works much like a toll bridge that someone built and now operates. The bridge cost a fortune to build (the capex), but once it is up, the cost of letting one more car cross is almost nothing. The owner makes money two ways. First, in many setups the government or the grid pays the bridge owner a fixed fee just to keep the bridge *available* and maintained, whether or not traffic is heavy that month — a **capacity payment**. Second, the owner collects a toll per car that actually crosses — the **energy payment**, which is volume times price.

A water utility is even simpler: it is a rented apartment building with a captive tenant base. The pipes are in the ground, every household and factory in the service area is connected, and people pay their water bill every month because they cannot exactly switch to a competitor's pipes. The revenue is recurring, the customer cannot leave, and the price is set by a regulator. That is about as close to a contractual annuity as a listed company gets.

### The earnings identity, in one line

For a power generator, strip away the accounting and you get:

```
profit  =  capacity_payment
         + (MWh_dispatched x energy_tariff)     <- the energy leg
         - (MWh_dispatched x fuel_cost_per_MWh)  <- the only big variable cost
         - fixed_O&M
         - depreciation
         - interest_on_debt
```

Two features of this identity drive everything that follows.

**First, the capacity payment is fixed and contracted.** A plant with a power purchase agreement (PPA) — the long-term contract under which it sells power to the single buyer, Vietnam Electricity (EVN) — gets paid a capacity charge designed to cover its fixed costs and give a regulated return on the capital invested, *regardless of how much power it actually sells that month.* This is the floor under a utility's cash flow. When the economy slows and a steel mill's earnings collapse because nobody is buying steel, the power plant still collects its capacity payment.

**Second, the energy leg has a built-in hedge.** Look at the two energy terms: revenue is `MWh x tariff` and the big variable cost is `MWh x fuel_cost`. For a thermal plant (coal or gas), the PPA tariff is usually structured so that the fuel cost is *passed through* — when coal or gas gets more expensive, the contracted tariff rises to compensate. The plant keeps a relatively stable spread per MWh. It is not perfectly hedged, and timing mismatches bite, but the structure means a thermal generator is far less exposed to commodity prices than, say, a steel mill, which eats the full swing in iron-ore and coal prices with no pass-through.

> [!note]
> **Jargon, defined once.** *PPA (power purchase agreement)*: the long-term contract to sell electricity, usually to EVN. *EVN (Vietnam Electricity)*: the state-owned single buyer and grid operator — it is the counterparty to almost every generator. *Tariff*: the price per kWh, set by contract and regulation. *Dispatch*: how much the grid actually instructs your plant to generate, hour by hour. *Capacity factor / utilization*: the fraction of a plant's maximum possible output it actually produces over a year. *kWh / MWh / GWh / TWh*: units of energy, each 1,000x the last (1 GWh = 1,000 MWh = 1,000,000 kWh; a TWh is a billion kWh).

### The single-buyer market and the merit order

There is one more piece of structure you need before any of this clicks: in Vietnam, electricity is not sold into a free market with many buyers. There is one buyer, EVN, which owns the grid and dispatches every plant in the country. A generator does not get to decide how much to produce; EVN decides, plant by plant, hour by hour, in what is called the **merit order** — the grid stacks every available plant from cheapest marginal cost to most expensive and dispatches them in that order until demand is met.

This single-buyer, merit-order design is the hidden engine behind everything in this post. It explains *why* hydro earns so well in a wet year (free water puts it first in the merit order, so it runs flat-out), *why* coal and gas fill the gap (they sit further up the cost curve and only get called when cheaper sources are exhausted), and *why* the capacity payment exists at all (a plant that sits idle most of the year still needs to be paid to stay available for the peak, or nobody would build it). When you own a Vietnamese power stock, you are really owning a position in this dispatch stack — and your returns depend on where your plant sits in the merit order and how often EVN calls on it.

It also explains the sector's single biggest counterparty risk: every generator's cash flow ultimately depends on EVN's ability and willingness to pay. EVN is state-owned and systemically critical, so it does not default — but it can be slow, and it can be squeezed when retail electricity prices are held below cost for social reasons while EVN's own purchase costs rise. A few times in the past decade EVN has run large losses precisely because the government held the retail tariff down during a cost spike. When EVN is financially stretched, payment timing to generators can slow and receivables build up on the generators' balance sheets — a real, recurring risk that a government bond simply does not carry. The defensiveness of a Vietnamese utility is real, but it is the defensiveness of a contracted supplier to a single, occasionally-cash-strapped state buyer, not the defensiveness of sovereign credit.

### Why the capacity payment is the floor

It is worth dwelling on the capacity payment, because it is the most under-appreciated part of a utility's defensiveness and the part that most distinguishes a contracted generator from a merchant one. A merchant plant — one that sells purely into a spot market with no contract — lives or dies on volume and price; in a bad year it can lose money outright. A contracted Vietnamese generator with a capacity charge in its PPA is different: even if EVN dispatches it for only a few hundred hours in a soft year, the capacity payment covers the fixed costs and a regulated return on the capital. That payment is what turns "a plant that might or might not run" into "an annuity with a floor." When you read that NT2 or a similar contracted gas plant held up in a drawdown, the capacity payment is a big part of the reason: a chunk of its revenue does not move with dispatch at all. The closer a generator's revenue mix is to capacity-heavy and contracted, the more bond-like — and the more defensive — the stock behaves.

### Why cash flows are stable and beta is low

Put the two features together and you see why a utility's earnings barely move with the economic cycle. The capacity payment is fixed. The energy spread is partly hedged. Water tariffs are regulated and the demand is non-discretionary — people use roughly the same amount of water in a recession as in a boom. So a utility's revenue is *inelastic* to the business cycle in a way that a broker's revenue (which depends entirely on how much people are trading) or a developer's revenue (which depends on whether anyone is buying apartments) simply is not.

That cycle-insensitivity shows up directly in **beta** — the statistic that measures how much a stock moves when the index moves. A beta of 1.0 means the stock moves in lockstep with VN-Index; a beta of 1.8 means it tends to move 1.8% for every 1% in the index (amplifying both up and down); a beta of 0.5 means it moves only half as much. Utilities sit at the low end.

![Bar chart of approximate beta to VN-Index by sector with securities highest and water lowest](/imgs/blogs/utilities-power-water-gas-sector-vietnam-defensive-cash-4.png)

The chart makes the defensive case visually. A securities broker has a beta near 1.8 — it is a leveraged bet on the index itself. Steel and real estate sit around 1.4–1.5, banks near 1.1. Utilities come in around 0.6 and water around 0.5. When VN-Index falls 40%, a 0.5-beta water stock that drops ~20% (beta is a tendency, not a law, and the foreign-flow mechanics below add their own kick) is doing exactly what it is supposed to do: losing *less*. The whole point of a defensive stock is not that it never falls; it is that it falls less, and that the difference compounds in your favor over a full cycle.

#### Worked example: the toll-bridge plant's gross margin

Let's price a single power plant to make the identity concrete. Take a 1,200 MW thermal plant running at a 60% capacity factor. Over a year it produces:

```
1,200 MW x 8,760 hours/year x 0.60  =  ~6,300,000 MWh  =  ~6,300 GWh  =  ~6.3 billion kWh
```

Now apply the spread. Say the contracted energy tariff is 1,200 dong/kWh and the fuel cost is 800 dong/kWh, leaving a gross spread of 400 dong/kWh:

```
400 dong/kWh x 6,300,000,000 kWh  =  2,520,000,000,000 dong  =  ~2,520 billion dong
```

At roughly \$1 = 25,400 VND, that is about **\$99 million** of gross energy margin a year, before the capacity payment, fixed O&M, depreciation and interest. The plant also collects a capacity payment on top, which is the part that does not move with dispatch. The intuition: a utility's gross profit is a wide, slow river — a spread times a large, stable volume — not the flash flood of a cyclical's good year.

## The generation mix: hydro, thermal and the renewable wave

"Power utility" is not one thing. The three big buckets behave very differently, and knowing which is which is the difference between owning a defensive annuity and owning a leveraged bet on the weather.

### Hydro: cheapest power, but it depends on rain

Hydroelectric plants — names like REE's portfolio of hydro assets, or HDG's hydro arm — are the cheapest source of power once built, because their "fuel" (falling water) is free. A hydro plant's marginal cost is close to zero, so when the reservoirs are full, the grid dispatches them first and they earn a fat spread. The catch is in the word "when." Hydro output depends entirely on rainfall. A wet year fills the reservoirs and the plant runs flat-out at near-zero fuel cost; a dry year starves it, and a hydro generator's profit can swing 30–50% from one year to the next purely on the weather. Hydro is defensive in the sense that its *price* is stable (no fuel-cost shock), but its *volume* is the least predictable in the sector.

### Thermal: coal and gas, the workhorses

Thermal plants burn coal (large coal fleets in the north) or natural gas (NT2, and POW's gas-fired plants in the south). They are dispatchable — the grid can call on them any time, day or night, rain or shine — which makes them the backbone that fills the gap when hydro and renewables fall short. Their economics are the spread we modeled above: tariff minus fuel cost, with fuel partly passed through in the PPA. The risk is fuel-price timing and, increasingly, policy: coal is the source the PDP8 plan wants to *shrink*, so a coal-heavy generator faces a structural headwind even if its current cash flow is fine.

### Renewables: solar and wind, the policy-driven build

Solar (GEG, and solar arms of many names) and wind (PC1, which both builds the turbines as a contractor and owns wind farms) are the growth end. Their fuel is free, like hydro, but their output is intermittent and their economics depend heavily on the feed-in tariff (FiT) — the guaranteed price the government set to attract the build. The Vietnamese solar boom of 2019–2020 happened because the FiT was generous; when it lapsed and the new framework lagged, the build stalled. Renewables are where the structural growth is, but they are also where *policy risk* is highest: a stock can be made or broken by a single tariff decree.

### The names, and what kind of bet each one is

It helps to put faces on the tickers, because the brief's list — POW, REE, NT2, GEG, HDG, PC1, BWE, TDM — spans the full spectrum from pure cyclical to pure annuity. Reading each one correctly is most of the job.

- **POW (PetroVietnam Power)** is the largest listed power generator, primarily gas-fired in the south plus some coal. It is a contracted generator, so it is reasonably defensive, but it carries fuel-cost-timing risk (gas supply from the PVN chain) and some exposure to how much EVN dispatches it. Think contracted thermal annuity with a fuel wrinkle.
- **NT2 (Nhon Trach 2)** is the textbook defensive: a single, efficient gas-fired plant with a long PPA, a clean balance sheet, and a history of paying out most of its cash as dividends. As its debt amortized over the years, it became close to a pure dividend annuity — the most bond-like generator on the exchange.
- **REE (Refrigeration Electrical Engineering)** is a hybrid: an electrical-and-water-engineering business that has built a large portfolio of *stakes* in hydro, thermal, solar, wind and water companies, plus an office-leasing arm. Owning REE is owning a diversified utility-and-infrastructure fund inside one ticker — which dampens the single-driver risk (one dry year does not sink the whole thing) but also dilutes the pure-play exposure.
- **HDG (Ha Do Group)** pairs a property-development arm with a growing hydro, wind and solar portfolio — so its "utility" cash flow is partly a hedge against the cyclicality of its property side.
- **GEG (Gia Lai Electricity)** is a renewable owner — hydro, solar and wind — and trades much more like a growth-and-policy play than a defensive annuity; its fortunes swing with feed-in tariffs and the pace of the renewable build.
- **PC1 (Power Construction No. 1)** is the odd one out: it is mainly a *contractor* that builds transmission lines and renewable projects, plus some owned generation and even a nickel-mining venture. The contracting revenue is cyclical — it rises and falls with the national capex cycle — so PC1 behaves more like an industrial than a defensive utility, even though it sits under the "power" label.
- **BWE (Binh Duong Water)** and **TDM (Thu Dau Mot Water)** are the water utilities — regional concessions with regulated tariffs and captive customers, the most defensive cash flows in the list. BWE is the larger, more acquisitive operator; TDM is the steadier, higher-yield holding (and the two are cross-linked through ownership, so they often move together).

The single most useful habit when you hear "buy utilities" is to ask *which kind* — because owning PC1 for "defense" in a downturn would be a category error (it is a cyclical contractor), while owning NT2 or TDM is exactly the defense you wanted.

![Stacked bar chart of Vietnam power capacity mix 2024 actual versus 2030 PDP8 target showing shift to solar and wind](/imgs/blogs/utilities-power-water-gas-sector-vietnam-defensive-cash-3.png)

The chart shows where the mix is and where the PDP8 plan wants it to go. As of 2024 the installed capacity is roughly 28% hydro, 32% coal, 10% gas, 18% solar, 6% wind and 6% other. The 2030 target shrinks coal's *share* (even as absolute capacity may hold) and grows solar to ~22% and wind to ~18% — a dramatic renewable tilt. For an investor, the message is twofold: the pie is growing fast (so even a shrinking *share* of coal can mean stable coal volume), and the growth dollars are flowing to renewables, which is where the contractor-and-owner names like PC1 and the solar/wind owners like GEG have their runway.

## The structural tailwind: demand that doubles

Here is the single most important fact about Vietnamese utilities, and the reason the sector is more than a sleepy dividend play: electricity demand grows relentlessly, and it is barely cyclical.

![Line chart of Vietnam national electricity consumption 2018 to 2025 rising from 192 to 300 TWh](/imgs/blogs/utilities-power-water-gas-sector-vietnam-defensive-cash-2.png)

National consumption went from 192 TWh in 2018 to roughly 300 TWh in 2025 — growth of about 8–9% a year, and notice how it barely flinched even through the 2020–2021 pandemic. Vietnam is industrializing, urbanizing, and air-conditioning all at once. Factories relocating from China need power. A growing middle class buys refrigerators and air conditioners. Data centers are arriving. Electricity demand in Vietnam grows roughly in line with, and often faster than, nominal GDP, and the official planning assumption is that it keeps compounding at a high-single-digit rate, so total demand roughly *doubles* by around 2030.

That is the structural underpinning. A sector whose volume grows 8–9% a year regardless of the business cycle is a sector with a permanent tailwind. It is why utilities can be both *defensive* (low beta, stable cash) and *growing* (the pie expands every year) — a combination most defensive sectors cannot offer.

#### Worked example: demand growth in capacity terms

Make the tailwind concrete. Demand of 277 TWh growing 9% adds:

```
277 TWh x 0.09  =  ~25 TWh of new demand in a single year
```

To supply an extra 25 TWh, roughly how much new plant do you need? A plant running at, say, a 50% average capacity factor produces about `1,000 MW x 8,760 h x 0.50 = 4,380 GWh = ~4.4 TWh` per gigawatt of capacity per year. So 25 TWh of new annual demand needs on the order of:

```
25 TWh / 4.4 TWh per GW  =  ~5.7 GW of new capacity  -  every single year
```

Several gigawatts of new plant, year after year, for a decade. That is the PDP8 build in miniature, and it is why the plan's headline capital figure is so large. The intuition: a country adding the equivalent of a handful of large power plants annually is a country where utility capex — and the contractors and owners who supply it — has a runway measured in years, not quarters.

### PDP8 and the \$136bn build-out

PDP8 is Vietnam's eighth national Power Development Plan — the master roadmap for how much of each kind of plant gets built through 2030 and beyond. The headline number is enormous: on the order of \$136bn of investment in generation and grid over the plan horizon, with the explicit policy goal of tilting the mix toward renewables and away from coal. For the listed utility sector, PDP8 is the supply-side bookend to the demand growth above: the country *will* build, the only questions are which technologies, at what tariff, and on what timeline. Contractors that build transmission and renewables (PC1), developers that own the new wind and solar (GEG, REE's expanding portfolio), and the gas-and-LNG names positioned for the gas build all have a policy-backed runway. The risk, as always, is execution and tariff: a plan is not a guaranteed cash flow until the decree sets the price.

There is a subtle and important distinction inside PDP8 that separates the durable opportunity from the hype. The plan grows the *grid* and the *renewable fleet*, but it also makes a hard bet that liquefied natural gas (LNG) will replace coal as the dispatchable backbone — because you cannot run a modern economy on solar and wind alone (the sun sets and the wind drops), you need something that can be switched on at the evening peak. Solar and wind add cheap energy but not reliable *capacity*; LNG and remaining gas add the dispatchable capacity that keeps the lights on when renewables are not producing. That is why the gas-and-LNG names are a quieter but arguably more durable PDP8 play than the headline solar build: their output is dispatchable and contracted, which is exactly the capacity-heavy, defensive profile this whole post is about. The flashy renewable-developer stocks get the headlines; the unglamorous dispatchable-gas names get the capacity payments.

A second nuance: the grid itself is a bottleneck and an opportunity. Building gigawatts of solar in the sunny south is useless if the transmission lines cannot carry that power to the industrial and population centers, and Vietnam has repeatedly run into grid-congestion limits that forced renewable plants to curtail (throttle back) output they could have sold. PDP8's transmission spend is meant to fix this — which is precisely the business of a contractor like PC1. So the build-out creates two distinct kinds of listed exposure: the *owners* of new generation (defensive once contracted, risky until the tariff is set) and the *builders* of the grid and plants (cyclical, riding the capex wave). They are different trades with different risk profiles, and confusing them is the most common mistake in playing the PDP8 theme.

### Gas distribution: the third leg

The brief's "utilities" also include gas distribution, and it deserves its own note because it behaves differently again. A gas distributor (the LPG and natural-gas distribution names in the PV Gas orbit) sits between the upstream gas supply and the end user — power plants, factories, and households. Its economics are a *volume-and-spread* business much like a water utility: it buys gas at one price, distributes it, and earns a regulated or contracted margin on the throughput. The defensiveness comes from the same place as water — recurring demand, regulated margin, captive infrastructure (the pipeline network is a natural monopoly) — but the cash flow is more exposed to the absolute level of gas and oil prices, because the input cost is a global commodity and the margin can compress when prices spike faster than the regulated tariff can follow. Gas distribution is defensive, but a notch less so than water, which is why the income chart below places its yield between power and the index. For the deeper picture of how the gas supply itself is produced and priced — the upstream end that feeds both the gas generators and the distributors — the oil-and-gas post linked at the end walks the full PVN chain.

## Valuation: how to price a utility

You do not value a utility the way you value a growth stock. A utility is a cash-flow asset, so the metrics that matter are the ones that capture cash and capital, not the ones that capture growth optionality.

- **EV/EBITDA** is the workhorse. Because utilities carry a lot of debt (the plant was built with borrowed money) and a lot of depreciation (the plant is a giant depreciating asset), the equity-only P/E can be misleading. Enterprise value over EBITDA looks through the capital structure and the non-cash depreciation to ask: what are you paying for the actual cash-generating engine? Mature, contracted generators often trade around the high-single-digit-to-low-double-digit EV/EBITDA range; the lower the multiple, the more cash you are buying per dong invested.
- **Dividend yield** is the metric most relevant to *why you own the stock*. A utility's appeal is the income, so the yield — annual dividend per share divided by price — is the headline. We will return to this; it is the anchor of the whole investment case.
- **P/B (price-to-book)** matters because a utility's book value is mostly its plants, which are real, tangible, cash-generating assets — not goodwill or intangibles. A regulated utility earning a steady return on a real asset base can be sensibly anchored to its book value in a way a tech firm cannot.

The mistake to avoid is applying a high-growth lens. A utility that "only" grows earnings 5–8% a year is not a disappointment — it is doing exactly what a utility does, and you are paid in dividends for the slow growth. Judge it on cash returned and the stability of the asset base, not on whether it 10x'd.

There is one more lens worth adding for a generator specifically: the **debt-amortization story**. A power plant is built almost entirely with borrowed money, and in its early years a huge slice of its cash flow goes to paying interest and principal. But debt amortizes — each year the loan balance shrinks, the interest bill falls, and more of the same operating cash flow becomes free to pay out to shareholders. This is why a contracted generator can look mediocre when young (heavy debt, modest dividend) and become a cash gusher in its later years (debt nearly gone, almost all cash flow available for dividends). NT2's evolution into a high-payout name followed exactly this arc. For an investor, a generator with a stable PPA and a rapidly amortizing loan is a *rising-dividend* story hiding inside a flat-earnings facade — the earnings barely grow, but the cash available to *you* grows as the bank gets paid off.

#### Worked example: EV/EBITDA on a contracted generator

Put numbers on the valuation. Suppose a contracted gas generator has a market capitalization of 12,000 billion dong and net debt of 4,000 billion dong, so its enterprise value (EV) is:

```
EV  =  equity value + net debt  =  12,000 + 4,000  =  16,000 billion dong
```

If the plant generates EBITDA (earnings before interest, tax, depreciation and amortization — a proxy for the plant's pre-financing cash engine) of 2,000 billion dong a year:

```
EV / EBITDA  =  16,000 / 2,000  =  8.0x
```

At \$1 = 25,400 VND, that 16,000-billion-dong enterprise value is about **\$630 million**, and you are paying 8x the annual cash engine for it. Compare two generators on this multiple, not on P/E, because the one with more debt and more depreciation will look artificially expensive on P/E while EV/EBITDA puts them on equal footing. The intuition: EV/EBITDA asks "what am I paying for the cash-generating machine itself," which is the only question that matters for an asset whose whole value is the cash it throws off.

## Water: the most defensive corner of the market

If utilities are the defensive end of VN-Index, water is the defensive end of utilities — the closest thing the Vietnamese market has to a bond that happens to trade as a stock.

A water company like BWE (Binh Duong Water) or TDM (Thu Dau Mot Water) operates a regional water-supply concession: it treats and distributes water to households and industry in a defined area, with a regulated tariff and a customer base that physically cannot switch suppliers. Demand is almost perfectly non-discretionary — water consumption barely moves in a recession — and grows steadily with population and industrialization. The result is the most stable cash flow in the listed market: predictable volume, regulated price, captive customers, and a tariff that ratchets up over time. That stability is exactly why water carries the lowest beta in the sector and supports the highest, most reliable dividend yield.

Why is water *even more* defensive than power? Three reasons stack up. First, there is no fuel-cost swing — a water utility's main inputs are electricity (to pump and treat) and chemicals, which are small relative to the regulated tariff, so it has none of the commodity exposure that bites a thermal generator and none of the weather exposure that bites a hydro generator. Second, the demand is the least discretionary in the entire economy: a household will cut its restaurant spending, its travel, even its electricity use before it cuts the water it drinks and washes with, and a factory in an industrial park must have water to operate at all. Third, the customer base is a genuine local monopoly — there is exactly one set of pipes into a given district, and a regulated tariff that the local authority adjusts upward over time to keep the operator's return intact. The combination of "no fuel risk, non-discretionary demand, and a regulated local monopoly" is why water is the closest thing on VN-Index to a corporate bond that grows with the population.

The catch — because there is always a catch — is that a water utility's *growth* is slow and capex-heavy. To grow volume it must extend pipes to new areas and build new treatment plants, which costs money and takes years, and the regulated tariff caps the return on that investment. So a water stock is the ultimate "income, not growth" holding: you buy it for the 5–6% dividend and the rock-steadiness, not for capital appreciation. In a roaring bull it will be one of the worst performers in your portfolio on a price basis — and that is exactly the point. It is the ballast, and ballast is supposed to sit at the bottom of the boat being boring while the sails do the exciting work.

![Horizontal bar chart of approximate dividend yield by sub-sector water power gas versus VN-Index average](/imgs/blogs/utilities-power-water-gas-sector-vietnam-defensive-cash-6.png)

The chart lays out the income case. Against a VN-Index average dividend yield of roughly 1.8%, a water utility can yield around 6%, power around 5%, and gas distribution around 4%. Those are illustrative figures, but the ranking and the *multiple* are the point: utilities pay roughly two-to-three times the market's yield. In a market obsessed with capital gains, that consistent income is the part most retail investors underrate — and it is the part that does the heavy lifting in a flat or falling market.

#### Worked example: the water utility's dividend yield

Make the income case concrete. Suppose a water utility trades at 33,000 dong per share and pays a cash dividend of 2,000 dong per share for the year:

```
dividend yield  =  2,000 dong / 33,000 dong  =  6.06%  ~  6%
```

Compare that to the index's ~1.8%: this single stock pays roughly **3.3x** the market's yield. In dollar terms, if you held \$10,000 of this stock, you would collect about \$600 a year in dividends just for owning it — and that dividend is funded by a regulated tariff on water people must buy, not by a hoped-for capital gain. The intuition: a water utility pays you to wait, and the waiting is the strategy.

## Common misconceptions

The defensive label invites a few comfortable errors. Each costs money.

### Misconception 1: "Utilities are risk-free bonds"

They are not. They are *bond-like*, which is different. A utility carries real risks a government bond does not: drought can gut a hydro generator's volume (we will see exactly how below), a tariff decree can change the economics overnight, fuel-cost pass-through can lag and squeeze a thermal plant's spread for a few quarters, and the heavy debt that funds the plants means rising interest rates raise the interest bill and the discount rate at once. NT2 fell 15–20% in the 2022 drawdown — a real bond would not have. The correct mental model is "low-beta, dividend-paying equity with regulated cash flows," not "a bond." You get most of a bond's stability, but you keep equity's tail risks.

### Misconception 2: "High demand growth means high stock returns"

This is the most expensive error, because the premise is true and the conclusion is false. Yes, electricity demand grows 8–9% a year. No, that does not flow through to your total return, for two reasons. First, the growth requires enormous capex — building those 5+ GW a year costs money, and a utility plowing cash into new plants is *not* handing that cash to shareholders; it is converting it into more depreciating assets at a regulated return. Second, the regulated/contracted nature of the cash flow caps the upside: the return on each new plant is set by the tariff, so you cannot earn supernormal profits the way a monopoly in an unregulated industry might. Demand growth keeps the sector healthy and the dividend safe; it does not make utilities a high-return growth play. Confusing "the industry grows fast" with "the stock returns will be high" is how people overpay for utility capex stories.

### Misconception 3: "All power stocks are the same"

They could not be more different. A hydro generator is a leveraged bet on rainfall. A coal plant is a structurally challenged asset facing PDP8's de-emphasis of coal. A gas plant like NT2 is a contracted-cash-flow annuity with fuel-price-timing risk. A solar or wind owner is a policy-driven growth play hostage to the next feed-in-tariff decree. A power contractor like PC1 is, in part, a *cyclical* industrial that lives off the capex cycle, not a defensive cash machine at all. Lumping them together as "utilities" and buying the basket misses that you are choosing between assets with completely different risk drivers — weather, policy, fuel, and the capex cycle. The defensiveness lives in the *contracted* names, not automatically in everything with "power" in the name.

### Misconception 4: "The dividend is guaranteed"

A 5–6% yield looks like a sure thing, but the dividend is a *decision*, not a contract, and three things can break it. First, a capex cycle: a utility that decides to build a big new plant may cut the dividend to fund the construction, converting your income stock into a growth stock without asking — REE and HDG, with their expansion ambitions, are more prone to this than a fully-built name like NT2. Second, a regulated-tariff freeze: if the authority holds a water or power tariff flat through a period of rising costs (often for social or political reasons), the cash that funds the dividend gets squeezed. Third, an EVN payment slowdown: if the single buyer is stretched and slow to pay, a generator's cash can be tied up in receivables even though its accounting profit looks fine, and a board may trim the payout to protect the balance sheet. None of these is likely in a given year, but "unlikely" is not "impossible" — so the defensive investor checks dividend *coverage* (is the payout comfortably funded by free cash flow, or is the company borrowing to pay it?) rather than chasing the highest headline yield. A yield that looks too good often reflects a market that doubts the dividend's safety, not a free lunch.

## How it shows up on VN-Index

Theory is one thing; let's watch the sector behave on the actual market across a few regimes.

### Defensive outperformance in the down years

The cleanest evidence is the 2022 drawdown we opened with. As VN-Index fell from ~1,530 to ~870 (down >40%), the high-beta book — brokers, steel, leveraged property — fell 40–70%, while contracted utilities like NT2 fell roughly 15–20% and kept paying dividends. *Relative* to the index, utilities had a strong year even though their absolute return was negative, because losing 18% while the index loses 40% is a 22-point outperformance. Over a full cycle, that down-year cushion is what makes a utility sleeve worth holding: it is not there to win the bull, it is there to lose less in the bust so you have capital left to redeploy.

It is worth being honest about *why* even the most defensive utility falls at all in a crash, because it reveals a Vietnam-specific mechanic. A large share of VN-Index trading is margin-financed retail (investors borrowing from brokers to buy stocks, a practice called *ký quỹ*) and foreign funds. When the index falls hard, two forced-selling waves hit *everything*, defensive or not. Margin calls force leveraged retail investors to sell whatever they own to raise cash, and they often sell their most *liquid* holdings — which can include the large-cap utilities — precisely because those are the easiest to dump. Foreign funds facing redemptions sell their whole book proportionally, defensives included. So in the depths of a panic, a low-beta water stock can fall more than its fundamentals justify, simply because it is liquid and someone needs the cash. The lesson is double-edged: the indiscriminate selling means defensives are not *immune* in the panic, but it also means they often get *oversold* relative to their stable cash flow — which is exactly the moment to buy them, because the dividend did not change even though the price did. The 2022 lows in names like NT2 and BWE were, in hindsight, a gift to the patient holder collecting a fat yield on a depressed price.

![Before and after diagram of a cyclical portfolio versus a utility portfolio in a bull year and a bust year](/imgs/blogs/utilities-power-water-gas-sector-vietnam-defensive-cash-5.png)

The figure crystallizes the trade-off across two years. In the bull (left), the cyclical book is up 80–120% and the utility book is up only 10–20% — utilities look boring and you will be tempted to sell them. In the bust (right), the cyclical book is down 40–60% while the utility book holds, down only 5–15%, with the dividend cushioning the fall. Same two portfolios, opposite relative outcomes. The defensive investor's edge is the discipline to hold the boring book *through* the bull so it is there for the bust — and to rotate toward it precisely when everyone else is bored of it.

### El Nino and the hydro swing

The most utility-specific driver on VN-Index is the El Nino / La Nina cycle — the swing in Pacific weather that determines whether Vietnam has a wet year or a dry one. This is the single biggest non-market force on the power sub-sector's earnings, and it splits the sector cleanly.

![Diagram of the El Nino dry year squeezing hydro margins and La Nina wet year expanding them](/imgs/blogs/utilities-power-water-gas-sector-vietnam-defensive-cash-7.png)

The mechanism runs as the figure shows. In an El Nino (dry) year, rainfall falls well below normal, reservoirs run low, and the cheap hydro plants simply cannot generate as much — fewer MWh sold at the low end of the cost curve. The grid has to fill the gap with the more expensive option: thermal coal and gas. So in a dry year, hydro generators see their volume and profit drop, while thermal generators get *more* dispatch and earn more. In a La Nina (wet) year the opposite happens: full reservoirs let the near-zero-fuel-cost hydro plants run flat-out, their margins expand, and thermal gets crowded out. The drought years of recent El Nino cycles squeezed Vietnamese hydro names hard while lifting the gas and coal generators — a clean, recurring rotation *within* the utility sector that a sharp investor can position for by watching the weather forecasts and reservoir levels, not the income statement.

The 2023 episode made this painfully concrete. A strong El Nino drove a severe drought across northern Vietnam in mid-2023, reservoir levels at major hydro plants fell to critical lows, and for a stretch of weeks the north actually faced rolling power shortages and forced outages because there was not enough generation to meet the summer-peak air-conditioning load. Hydro-heavy holdings saw their earnings gutted that year, while the thermal generators that could be dispatched to plug the gap saw their utilization and profit jump. For an investor, the signal was available *months* ahead in the meteorological forecasts and the published reservoir levels — this is one of the rare cases where a fundamental earnings swing is genuinely forecastable from public weather data before it shows up in the financials. The tradeable view: when a strong El Nino is forecast, tilt the utility sleeve away from hydro and toward contracted thermal and gas; when La Nina is on the way and reservoirs are filling, do the reverse. It is the closest thing the sector has to a recurring, mechanical edge.

#### Worked example: the hydro plant's wet-year profit jump

Quantify the swing. Take a hydro plant that in a normal year runs at a 40% capacity factor. In a wet La Nina year, full reservoirs let it run 20% more hours — call it a 48% capacity factor. Because hydro's fuel cost is essentially zero, almost every extra MWh drops straight to gross profit. If the plant is 300 MW:

```
normal year:  300 MW x 8,760 h x 0.40  =  ~1,051 GWh
wet year:     300 MW x 8,760 h x 0.48  =  ~1,261 GWh
extra output: ~210 GWh  =  210,000,000 kWh
```

At a hydro tariff of, say, 1,100 dong/kWh and near-zero marginal cost, that extra 210 GWh is worth about `1,100 x 210,000,000 = 231,000,000,000 dong`, roughly **\$9 million** of almost-pure incremental gross profit — in a single good-rain year, with no change to the plant, the tariff, or anything but the weather. The intuition: a hydro stock's earnings are a bet on the monsoon, and the wet-year-versus-dry-year gap can be the difference between a great year and a poor one.

### Renewable-policy swings

The third VN-Index-specific force is policy. The 2019–2020 solar boom — driven by a generous feed-in tariff — sent solar developers and the contractors building the farms (PC1, GEG and others) on a tear, and the subsequent lapse and uncertainty around the new tariff framework cooled them just as fast. For renewable-tilted utility names, a single decree from the Ministry of Industry and Trade can move the stock more than a year of demand growth. This is the opposite of defensive — it is a policy-driven growth play wearing a utility's clothes — and it is why "all power stocks are the same" is such a costly assumption.

## The playbook: how to trade it

Everything above is mechanism. Here is how to actually use the utility sector in a Vietnamese portfolio.

### When to rotate in: late-cycle and risk-off

The utility sleeve is a *late-cycle and defensive* position. You rotate toward it when the evidence says the liquidity-driven bull is mature and the risk of a drawdown is rising. The concrete triggers, drawing on the rotation framework linked below:

- **Late in the cycle**, when the State Bank of Vietnam has stopped easing and is tightening — credit growth slowing, deposit rates ticking up, the margin/liquidity cycle rolling over. This is the regime where high-beta leadership (brokers, then property) tends to top out and defensive leadership begins. Shift weight from the 1.5–1.8-beta book toward the 0.5–0.6-beta book.
- **When relative strength rolls over** — when brokers and property stop making new relative highs against the index and utilities' relative-strength line turns up. The market often rotates into defensives *before* the headline index peaks; watching the relative-strength leadership (covered in the relative-strength post) is how you see it early.
- **In an outright risk-off event** — a bond scare, a global shock, a sharp foreign-outflow episode. Utilities will still fall (everything does), but the low beta and the dividend mean you lose less and have dry powder.

### Yield as the anchor

The reason to *hold* utilities, as opposed to trade them, is the income. A water-and-power sleeve yielding 5–6% is paying you roughly 3x the index to wait, and that dividend is funded by regulated and contracted cash flow, not by a hoped-for capital gain. In a flat or falling market, the dividend is most of your total return — and it compounds. Size the position as the *ballast* of the portfolio: the part you do not touch in the bull, that funds your buying in the bust. A common allocator's frame is a barbell — high-beta cyclicals for the upside in the bull, a utility-and-water core for the income and the downside cushion — rebalanced across the cycle.

The mechanics of why the ballast works are worth spelling out, because they are not intuitive until you have lived through a cycle. Suppose you run a barbell: half the portfolio in high-beta cyclicals, half in a utility-and-water core yielding 5%. In the bull, the cyclical half doubles and the utility half rises 15% — your blended return is strong, dragged down only modestly by the boring half. Now the bust comes: the cyclical half halves (down 50%), and the utility half falls only 12% but keeps paying its 5% dividend. The blended drawdown is roughly `(−50% + −12%)/2 = −31%` instead of the −50% you would have taken fully in cyclicals. That 19-point smaller hole is the whole game, because of the brutal arithmetic of recovery: a portfolio down 50% must *double* (+100%) just to get back to even, while a portfolio down 31% only needs +45%. The defensive sleeve does not just feel safer; it mathematically shortens the climb back, and it hands you dividend cash *during* the bust that you can use to buy the wrecked cyclicals at the bottom. Ballast is not a drag you tolerate — it is an active source of dry powder and a shorter recovery, paid for by giving up some upside in the good years.

A practical sizing note for a Vietnamese retail investor: because utilities are not where the explosive gains are, the temptation in a bull is to sell the boring book and chase the brokers — and that is exactly the mistake the barbell is designed to prevent. Pre-commit to a defensive weight (say, 20–40% of equity depending on how late in the cycle you judge things to be) and rebalance *mechanically*: when cyclicals run and the utility weight drifts below your target, the discipline is to *top up* the boring book, not abandon it. The whole edge of the defensive sleeve is behavioral as much as financial — it forces you to take some chips off the table near the top, when it feels worst to do so.

### Picking within the sector

Match the name to the regime and your view:

- For pure defense and income, the **contracted gas generators** (NT2-style) and the **water utilities** (BWE, TDM) — lowest beta, most reliable dividend.
- For a **wet-year tilt**, lean toward **hydro** (REE's hydro portfolio, HDG) when the forecasts point to La Nina and reservoirs are filling; reverse toward **thermal** in an El Nino.
- For **structural growth with policy risk**, the **renewable owners and contractors** (GEG, PC1, REE's expanding portfolio) — but size these as growth, not as ballast, because the policy risk makes them more volatile than the defensive core.

A diversification point that is easy to miss: because the sector's risk drivers are *different* across sub-types — weather for hydro, fuel-and-dispatch for thermal, tariff policy for renewables, regulated tariff for water — a basket of utilities is genuinely more robust than any single name, in a way that a basket of, say, brokers is not (all brokers rise and fall together on market turnover). A holding split across a contracted gas generator, a water utility, and a diversified name like REE is hedged against any one driver going wrong: a drought that guts hydro lifts thermal; a tariff decree that hurts a renewable name does not touch water. The single best structural choice for a hands-off defensive investor is often the *diversified* utility holding — REE-style exposure to many drivers at once — precisely because it converts a collection of single-driver bets into one steady, all-weather cash stream. The more concentrated single-name bets (a pure hydro play before a wet year, a thermal play before a drought) are for the investor with a specific, time-stamped view on weather or policy.

### The invalidation

Every position needs a line that says "I was wrong." For the utility sleeve, the defensive thesis is invalidated when:

- **A new liquidity-driven bull is clearly underway** — the SBV is cutting, credit is accelerating, margin balances are expanding, and high-beta leadership is broadening. In that regime, utilities will *lag*, and holding them is a (deliberate) drag; if your goal is maximum upside, you underweight them here. Holding a defensive book in a roaring bull is not a mistake, but expecting it to keep pace is.
- **The dividend is cut or the cash flow breaks** — a regulated-tariff freeze that fails to keep up with costs, a sustained multi-year drought that guts a hydro-heavy holding, or a balance sheet stretched too far by capex so the dividend is no longer covered. The whole reason to own the name is the cash return; if that breaks, the thesis breaks with it.
- **A specific policy or fuel shock** hits your holding — an adverse tariff decree for a renewable name, a fuel-cost pass-through that the regulator refuses to honor for a thermal name. These are name-specific, which is exactly why you diversify across hydro, thermal, water and renewables rather than concentrating in one driver.

Hold the boring book through the bull, collect the yield, and let it do its job when the cycle turns. The utility sector will never make you feel like a genius — but it is the part of a Vietnamese portfolio most likely to still be standing when the genius trades have blown up.
