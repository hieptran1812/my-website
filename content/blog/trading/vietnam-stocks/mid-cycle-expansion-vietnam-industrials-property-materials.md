> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Once the early-cycle financial rebound matures — GDP is strong, credit is flowing, confidence is back — leadership on VN-Index rotates out of the high-beta brokers and banks and into the *real economy*: industrials riding foreign direct investment, real-estate developers launching projects into recovering demand, and materials feeding the building boom and the public-investment push.
>
> - Financials lead early because they re-rate the *instant* the cost of money turns; they hand off in mid cycle because the cheap re-rating is done and the next leg of returns lives where capital is actually being *spent* — factories, ports, apartments, steel and cement.
> - The three mid-cycle engines are foreign direct investment (factories and the industrial parks, ports and logistics that serve them), recovering housing demand (developers' presales turning back up), and the building boom funded by both private construction and government public investment (steel and cement volumes).
> - The signal that the handoff has begun is a *relative-strength rotation*: the brokers' and banks' relative strength rolls over and breaks below 1.0 while industrials, property and materials push their relative strength up through 1.0 — confirmed by credit broadening out of financials and by mid-cycle earnings finally catching up to the price the market paid a year earlier.
> - The number to remember: disbursed foreign direct investment into Vietnam rose from roughly **\$19.7bn in 2021 to about \$28bn in 2025**, and public-investment disbursement climbed from **420 trillion dong to roughly 790 trillion dong** over the same span — that rising tide of *spending* is the fuel the mid-cycle sectors burn.

## The handoff nobody rings a bell for

In the spring of 2021, the loudest trade on VN-Index was the broker trade. SSI, VND, HCM, VCI — the securities houses — had run for months. Margin balances were exploding, new accounts were opening at a pace nobody had seen, and every retail group chat was full of broker tickers. The banks had run too; credit was accelerating, net interest margins were fat, and the financial complex as a whole had carried the index off its pandemic lows. If you had bought the early-cycle leaders in the second half of 2020, by mid-2021 you were sitting on gains that, in some names, had more than doubled.

And then, quietly, something changed. The brokers stopped making new relative highs against the index even as the index itself ground higher. The banks went sideways. Meanwhile, names you had been ignoring started to move with conviction: the industrial-park operators with land in the FDI corridors, the developers finally launching projects they had sat on through the slow years, the steel and cement companies as construction sites reopened and the government leaned harder on public investment. Nobody rang a bell. There was no headline that said *leadership has rotated*. But the money had already started to move, and the investors who were still adding to brokers in the back half of 2021 — because brokers had "always" worked — were buying the top of a trade that the market had already begun to leave.

That handoff is the subject of this post. The early-cycle leaders — brokers, banks, the high-beta financials — are the sprinters off the start line; we covered them in the [early-cycle leaders post](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/early-cycle-leaders-vietnam-brokers-banks-steel). This post is about what happens *next*: why, as the expansion matures, leadership hands off from financials to the real economy, what actually drives each of the mid-cycle sectors, how to read the handoff while it's happening rather than a year later, and how to rotate your portfolio with the cycle instead of clinging to the leader that's already cooling.

![Mid-cycle handoff from early leaders to industrials real estate and materials on VN-Index](/imgs/blogs/mid-cycle-expansion-vietnam-industrials-property-materials-1.png)

We will build it from the ground up. If you have never heard the phrase "mid cycle" before, that's fine — the next section defines every term from zero. By the end you will have a concrete checklist: the relative-strength prints that flag the handoff, the FDI and public-investment numbers that confirm the mid-cycle engines are running, and the exact rotation — out of cooling brokers, into rising industrials, property and materials — with the level that invalidates the view.

## Foundations: what "mid cycle" actually means

Before we can talk about *rotating into* the mid-cycle sectors, we have to be precise about what part of the economic cycle we are in, because the whole argument rests on it.

### The business cycle, in one paragraph

An economy does not grow in a smooth straight line. It moves in waves: a downturn (recession), a recovery off the bottom, an expansion that runs for years, and eventually an overheating-then-cooling phase that tips back into the next downturn. We slice that wave into four phases — **early cycle** (the recovery off the bottom), **mid cycle** (the long expansion), **late cycle** (the overheating top), and **recession** (the contraction). This is the "investment clock" framing, and it is the spine of the whole [business-cycle and investment-clock post](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/business-cycle-investment-clock-vietnam). Each phase favors a different group of sectors, because each phase changes what's scarce, what's cheap, and where the next dollar of profit comes from.

**Early cycle** is the recovery: the economy has bottomed, the central bank has cut rates to the floor, credit is just starting to thaw, and the stocks that move first are the ones most sensitive to the *cost of money* — the high-beta financials. ("Beta" is just how much a stock moves relative to the whole market; a high-beta stock that moves 1.5% for every 1% move in the index amplifies the cycle. Brokers, whose earnings swing wildly with trading volumes and margin lending, are about the highest-beta thing on VN-Index — see the [securities brokers post](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/securities-brokers-sector-vietnam-highest-beta).)

**Mid cycle** — our subject — is the *expansion*. The recovery has matured into a self-sustaining boom. Concretely, mid cycle means:

- **GDP growth is strong and broad.** Vietnam in a good mid-cycle year runs real GDP growth in the 6.5–7.5% range, and it's not just one sector carrying it — manufacturing, construction, services, and exports are all contributing.
- **Credit is flowing and accelerating.** The State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) has stopped cutting and credit growth is running hot — system loan growth of 14–16% a year is a mid-cycle print. Crucially, the credit is broadening: it's no longer just feeding bank balance sheets and margin loans, it's funding factories, property projects, and construction. (For how the SBV's credit ceiling — the *room tín dụng* it hands each bank — gates this, see the [monetary-policy post](/blog/trading/finance/vietnam-monetary-policy-state-bank-dong-credit-ceiling).)
- **Confidence is back.** Businesses invest, hire, and expand capacity because they believe the demand will be there. Consumers buy durable goods and, critically for Vietnam, apartments. Foreign manufacturers commit to building factories.

That last point — confidence translating into *spending on real things* — is the hinge of this entire post.

Here's an everyday analogy to lock the phases in before we go further. Picture a town the morning after a long storm. *Early cycle* is the moment the rain stops: the first thing that happens is the banks reopen and people who'd hoarded cash start lending and borrowing again — the *financial* system thaws first, fast, because money can move at the speed of a phone call. *Mid cycle* is the weeks that follow: now the actual rebuilding happens — the builders show up, the lumber yards and cement plants run flat out, the trucks haul materials, new shops break ground. That rebuilding is *slow* (you can't pour a foundation with a phone call) but it runs for a long time and employs the whole town. The financiers had their burst of activity on day one; the *real economy* — builders, materials, logistics — carries the recovery for the long middle stretch. Early cycle is the banks reopening; mid cycle is the town rebuilding. Different actors, different timing, and the stock market pays you in the same sequence: financials first, the real economy next.

### Why leadership rotates *out* of financials in mid cycle

Here is the question that confuses most beginners: if the economy is booming and banks and brokers are making more money than ever, why would their *stocks* stop leading?

The answer is the single most important idea in sector rotation, so slow down here. **A stock's price already contains the market's expectations.** The early-cycle financial rally is overwhelmingly a *re-rating* — the market repricing financials from "the economy is dying, these banks might take huge loan losses, nobody is trading so brokers earn nothing" to "the economy is fine, loans are good, volumes are exploding." That swing in *expectations* is enormous and it happens *fast*, in the first months of the recovery. A bank that traded at 0.8× book value in the depths of the downturn might trade at 1.6× book a year later — the stock doubled mostly because the *multiple* the market is willing to pay doubled, not because earnings doubled.

But a multiple can only re-rate once. Once the market has accepted that the financial system is healthy, the cheap re-rating is *done*. From there, a financial stock can only grind higher on actual earnings growth, which is much slower than a re-rating. The explosive part of the financial trade is behind you.

Meanwhile, the *spending* that the recovery unleashed is just getting started. A factory takes two years to build. An industrial park signs tenants over many quarters. A property project moves from land to launch to handover across years. Public-investment disbursement ramps over the whole expansion. So the earnings of the real-economy sectors — industrials, real estate, materials — don't spike on day one of the recovery the way a broker's do; they *build* through the mid cycle as the spending actually happens and converts into volumes, presales and profit. That is why leadership hands off: the next leg of returns lives where the cash is being *spent*, not where the cost of money was *re-rated*.

Put bluntly: **early cycle pays you for the change in the price of money; mid cycle pays you for the change in the volume of real activity.** Different engine, different sectors.

It's worth being precise about *why the financial re-rating can only happen once*, because this is the mechanism most people feel but can't articulate. A stock's price can be decomposed, very roughly, into *earnings × the multiple the market pays for those earnings*. The early-cycle financial rally is mostly the *multiple* expanding — from a fearful 0.8× book to a confident 1.6× book in our bank example. That multiple expansion is a one-time event: once the market has decided the banks are healthy, it won't keep paying *more and more* multiples of book indefinitely; there's a ceiling to how confident sentiment can get. After that ceiling, the *only* source of further return is earnings growth — and a bank's earnings grow at maybe 15–20% a year in a good year, not the doubling the re-rating delivered. So the financial stock's return *rate* slows dramatically the moment the re-rating is spent, even though the bank is doing fine. The mid-cycle sectors are the mirror image: they didn't re-rate much (nobody pays a euphoric multiple for a steel mill), so almost *all* their return comes from the earnings *volumes* growing — and those volumes grow for *years* as the spending plays out. A slow-but-long earnings engine beats a fast-but-spent multiple engine over the middle of the cycle. That's the handoff, stated as arithmetic.

### The mid-cycle sectors and what drives each one

Three groups take leadership in mid cycle. Each one is wired to a different part of the real-economy expansion.

![Early to mid rotation map money flowing from financials into industrials property and materials](/imgs/blogs/mid-cycle-expansion-vietnam-industrials-property-materials-2.png)

**1. Industrials — riding foreign direct investment (FDI).** "Foreign direct investment" is foreign companies building real operations in Vietnam: Samsung's phone factories, Foxconn's assembly lines, the textile and electronics supply chains relocating out of China under the "China+1" trend (companies diversifying their manufacturing so they aren't 100% dependent on China). Those factories need *somewhere to go* — serviced industrial land, with power, water, roads and customs nearby — and that land is sold or leased by **industrial-park operators** like Kinh Bac (KBC), IDICO (IDC), Becamex (BCM), Sonadezi (SNZ) and Long Hau (LHG). The factories also need *things moved* — raw materials in, finished goods out to the ports — which is the business of the **ports and logistics** companies like Gemadept (GMD) and the Hai Phong port operators. When FDI accelerates, industrial-park land absorption rises, port throughput rises, and this whole complex — what the index calls "industrial goods and services" — leads. We go deep on it in the [industrial goods and services post](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/industrial-goods-services-sector-vietnam-ports-logistics).

**2. Real estate — developers launching into recovering demand.** A property developer's economics are simple to state and brutal in timing: they buy land (a "land bank") years in advance, then launch projects — apartments, townhouses — and book revenue as buyers put down deposits and stage payments (**presales**, the leading indicator of a developer's future revenue). In the downturn, demand evaporates and developers sit on their land, launching nothing. In mid cycle, with credit flowing, mortgage rates lower, confidence back and incomes rising, *demand recovers* — and developers finally launch the inventory they've been holding. Presales turn back up, and with the long lag between presale and recognized revenue, the earnings stream is *visible and rising* through the expansion. The big names are Vinhomes (VHM), Nam Long (NLG), Khang Dien (KDH), Dat Xanh (DXG). The mechanics — land banks, presales, and the corporate-bond plumbing (*trái phiếu doanh nghiệp*) that funds developers — are in the [real estate sector post](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/real-estate-sector-vietnam-land-banks-presales-bonds).

**3. Materials — feeding the building boom and public investment.** All that building — factories, apartments, and the roads, bridges and airports the government funds — consumes physical inputs: **steel** (Hoa Phat / HPG dominates), **cement**, **construction stone and aggregates**, paving, pipes. Materials are the most cyclical of the three because they're pure *volume* plays: a steel mill's profit swings violently with how many tonnes it ships and the spread between selling price and input cost. Two mid-cycle demand sources stack on top of each other for materials: private construction (the property launches above) *and* **public investment** — the government's spending on infrastructure, *đầu tư công*. When the government accelerates disbursement on highways, the long-haul North–South expressway, ring roads and the new Long Thanh airport, steel and cement volumes get a second leg of demand that doesn't depend on the property cycle at all. The construction-materials-meets-public-investment story is its own deep dive: the [construction materials post](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/construction-materials-sector-vietnam-public-investment).

So the mid-cycle leadership trio is: **industrials (FDI), real estate (demand recovery), materials (building boom + public investment).** Hold that triangle in your head — the rest of the post is about *when* it takes over and *how* you trade the handoff.

### The signals of the early-to-mid handoff

How do you *know* the handoff is happening, in real time, rather than reading about it a year later? Three signals, in roughly this order of reliability:

**Signal 1 — relative-strength rotation.** This is the cleanest, most objective signal, so it gets the most space. "Relative strength" (RS) is simply a sector's price divided by the index, tracked over time. If a sector is *outperforming* the index, the ratio rises; if it's *lagging*, the ratio falls. You don't need the absolute level — you need the *direction and the trend*. The handoff shows up as a **rotation in RS**: the brokers' and banks' RS lines, which had been rising all through early cycle, *roll over* and start falling — their RS breaks below its rising trend and crosses below 1.0 (meaning they've gone from leading to lagging the index) — while industrials', property's and materials' RS lines, which had been flat or falling, *turn up* and push *through* 1.0 from below. Two trends crossing: financials' RS down, real-economy RS up. That crossover *is* the handoff. We dedicate a whole post to reading these lines: [relative strength: which sector leads VN-Index](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/relative-strength-reading-which-sector-leads-vn-index).

**Signal 2 — credit broadening.** Watch *where* the credit is going, not just how much there is. This deserves unpacking because it's the macro engine under everything. The SBV controls how much credit the banking system can extend via the **credit ceiling** (*room tín dụng*) — an annual growth cap it hands to each bank (covered in depth in the [monetary-policy post](/blog/trading/finance/vietnam-monetary-policy-state-bank-dong-credit-ceiling)). In early cycle, the credit that *does* flow is dominated by financial activity — margin lending into the broker boom, interbank, securities. The real economy is still cautious; factories aren't expanding, developers aren't launching, so they're not borrowing. As the expansion matures and confidence returns, that changes: businesses borrow to build capacity, developers borrow to fund launches, contractors borrow to deliver public-investment projects. So the SBV's credit data *broadens* — loan growth to manufacturing, construction and real-estate development accelerates relative to consumer and financial lending. When you see the *composition* of credit tilt toward the real economy — manufacturing and construction loans growing faster than the system average — the fuel for the mid-cycle sectors is confirmed at the source. This is the *cause* of which the FDI absorption, presales recovery and materials volumes are the *effects*. (The [liquidity and margin-cycle post](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/liquidity-and-the-margin-cycle-vietnam) covers how to read the margin and credit data.)

**Signal 3 — earnings catching up to price.** The early leaders ran on re-rating, so their price ran *ahead* of their earnings — they got *expensive* on trailing numbers. The mid-cycle sectors run the other way: the market paid up for the *anticipated* expansion, and as the FDI lands, the presales come through, and the public-investment volumes ship, the *earnings actually arrive* and grow into the price. When you see industrials, property and materials posting accelerating year-on-year earnings growth that *justifies* the prices they're already trading at, the mid-cycle thesis is no longer a forecast — it's confirmed by the income statements.

When all three line up — RS rotating, credit broadening, earnings catching price — the handoff isn't a guess anymore. It's the highest-conviction part of the whole rotation. Now let's go deeper, with numbers.

## How FDI actually drives the industrials trade

The industrials trade is, at its core, a bet on a single national statistic: **disbursed FDI** — not *registered* FDI (the headline pledge number, which is noisy and includes projects that may never break ground), but *disbursed* FDI, the money that actually shows up and gets spent building factories. That's the number that converts into industrial-land absorption, port volumes, and ultimately the earnings of the industrial complex.

![FDI disbursement and public investment in Vietnam rising together 2021 to 2025](/imgs/blogs/mid-cycle-expansion-vietnam-industrials-property-materials-3.png)

Disbursed FDI into Vietnam has been on a steady mid-cycle climb. The Ministry of Planning and Investment (MPI) figures show roughly **\$19.7bn in 2021, \$22.4bn in 2022, \$23.2bn in 2023, \$25.4bn in 2024, and about \$28.0bn in 2025** — a grind higher year after year, exactly the kind of multi-year ramp that makes industrials a *mid-cycle* trade rather than a quick early-cycle pop. (Throughout this post I anchor Vietnamese figures to US dollars at roughly **\$1 = 25,400 dong**, the mid-2026 USD/VND rate, so the numbers mean something to a non-VN reader.)

Why does this number lead the industrials sector so reliably? Because the chain from FDI to earnings is mechanical:

1. A foreign manufacturer decides to build a factory in Vietnam (the China+1 decision).
2. It needs serviced land, so it leases a plot in an industrial park — *cash to the park operator now, recognized over the lease.*
3. It builds and staffs the factory — construction demand, jobs, local supply chains.
4. It imports inputs and exports product — *throughput for the ports and logistics firms.*

Every step is revenue for some part of the industrials complex, and the whole chain scales with disbursed FDI. Let's make it concrete.

#### Worked example: FDI rises and an industrial-park operator fills more land

Suppose disbursed FDI rises from **\$23bn to \$25bn** in a year — a roughly **+9%** increase (2 ÷ 23 ≈ 0.087). That extra ~\$2bn of factory-building has to go *somewhere*, and a chunk of it lands as new tenants in industrial parks.

Take a stylized industrial-park operator with **500 hectares** of leasable land, of which **300 ha** are already leased going into the year. In a normal year it might absorb (lease out) **40 ha**. But with FDI accelerating, tenant demand is strong and it absorbs **70 ha** instead — nearly double its baseline. Say the average lease price is **2.5 million dong per square metre** (a representative industrial-land rate). One hectare is 10,000 m², so 70 ha is 700,000 m². At 2.5 million dong/m², that's:

700,000 × 2,500,000 = **1,750 billion dong** of lease value signed.

At \$1 = 25,400 dong, that's about **\$69 million** — roughly **\$0.07bn** — of new lease value for a *single* mid-sized operator from one year of accelerating FDI. Multiply across a dozen listed park operators and you see why the sector's order book swells when disbursed FDI ramps.

Here's the part that matters for *timing*: the market often pays *up* for the park operators on the *expectation* of this absorption — the stocks run before the leases are signed. Then, through the mid cycle, the leases *actually get signed* and the rental revenue *actually shows up* on the income statement. **The earnings catch up to the price the market already paid** — Signal 3 from above, made concrete. The investor who bought industrials at the *start* of the handoff, when their RS first turned up, is paid as that earnings stream arrives.

The same logic runs through ports. More FDI factories means more containers in and out, which means rising throughput for Gemadept and the Hai Phong operators, which means rising terminal earnings. The industrials trade is, fundamentally, *leverage on the FDI number* — and FDI is a slow, grinding, multi-year series, which is precisely why industrials is a *mid-cycle* leader, not an early-cycle sprinter.

There's a second-order effect worth understanding, because it's what makes industrials a *durable* mid-cycle leader rather than a flash. When a foreign manufacturer commits to a factory, it doesn't arrive alone. Samsung doesn't just build a phone plant; it pulls in a whole *supply chain* — component makers, packaging, logistics, local subcontractors — each of which needs land, labour and movement of goods. Economists call this the *cluster effect*: one anchor tenant seeds a dozen suppliers nearby. So a single big FDI commitment ripples through the industrial complex for years: the anchor factory in year one, the supplier park filling in years two and three, the rising port volumes as the cluster reaches full production in years three to five. This staggered, multi-year ripple is *why* the industrials earnings stream builds through the whole mid cycle instead of spiking and fading. It's also why the industrial-park operators with land *in the right corridors* — the established FDI zones around Bac Ninh, Bac Giang, Hai Phong in the north and Binh Duong, Dong Nai, Ba Ria–Vung Tau in the south — command the leadership: they own the scarce, serviced land exactly where the clusters want to land.

One more nuance for the careful investor: not all FDI is equal for the industrials trade. *Manufacturing* FDI — factories, the kind that needs industrial land and ports — is the fuel. FDI into real estate or financial services is a different animal and doesn't feed the industrial-park-and-ports complex the same way. So when you read the MPI's monthly FDI release, look past the headline total to the *manufacturing and processing* share, which is the slice that actually drives the sector. In Vietnam's good mid-cycle years that share has consistently been the dominant chunk of disbursed FDI — which is exactly why the industrials leadership has been so reliable.

## How public investment drives the materials trade

If FDI is the engine behind industrials, **public investment** — *đầu tư công*, the government's own spending on infrastructure — is the engine behind a big chunk of materials. And unlike FDI, it has a property a trader loves: it's *counter-cyclical policy*, which means the government tends to *accelerate* it precisely when it wants to support growth, often layering it right on top of a private-construction boom.

The public-investment series has ramped hard. Disbursement (the money actually spent, not just budgeted) ran roughly **420 trillion dong in 2021, 540 trillion in 2022, 676 trillion in 2023, around 660 trillion in 2024, and a planned ~790 trillion in 2025** (Ministry of Finance / MPI figures). At \$1 = 25,400 dong, 790 trillion dong is about **\$31bn** of government infrastructure spending in a single year. That is a colossal, growing demand source for steel, cement, stone and construction services that runs *independently* of whether the property market is hot.

Why does materials respond so violently to this? Because materials companies are **pure volume-and-spread plays**, the most operationally levered of the three mid-cycle groups. A steel mill has huge fixed costs (the blast furnace runs whether it's at 60% or 95% utilization). So when volumes rise, the *incremental* tonne is hugely profitable — revenue rises while fixed costs barely move, and margins expand fast. The same operating leverage that makes steel the deepest *loser* in a downturn (we covered that cyclicality in the [steel and basic resources post](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/steel-basic-resources-sector-vietnam-the-cyclical)) makes it the most explosive *winner* when mid-cycle volumes accelerate.

#### Worked example: public investment up 25% lifts steel and cement volumes

Take the 2022→2023 jump: public-investment disbursement rose from **540 trillion to 676 trillion dong**, a **+25%** increase (676 ÷ 540 − 1 ≈ 0.252). At \$1 = 25,400 dong, that's an extra **136 trillion dong ≈ \$5.4bn** of government construction demand stacked on in one year.

A rough rule of thumb among construction analysts is that **steel is on the order of 10–15% of a typical infrastructure project's cost** and cement another large slice. Take the low end, 10%, on the *incremental* 136 trillion dong of public spending: that's roughly **13.6 trillion dong ≈ \$0.5bn** of *additional* steel demand from public investment alone, before you count a single private apartment tower. Spread across the listed steel makers — with Hoa Phat the dominant volume player — that incremental demand lands on income statements with that violent operating leverage: at, say, a **2 million dong per tonne** margin on construction steel, 13.6 trillion dong of revenue at a representative steel price of ~14 million dong/tonne is on the order of **~1 million tonnes** of extra volume, and ~2 trillion dong of incremental gross profit flows from it.

The intuition: **public investment is a demand source the government can dial up at will, and materials' operating leverage turns each extra trillion dong of infrastructure spending into outsized profit growth** — which is exactly why materials leadership often *outlasts* the property cycle, riding the public-investment leg even when private launches cool.

## How recovering demand drives the property trade

The third mid-cycle leg, real estate, is the trickiest to time and the most rewarding when it works — so it deserves its own treatment. Where industrials runs on the FDI number and materials runs on volumes, property runs on a slower, more psychological variable: **the recovery of housing demand**, which depends on credit, mortgage rates, confidence and the reopening of the developers' funding market.

Recall the developer's economics. A developer buys land cheaply, years ahead — its *land bank*. Then it launches projects in stages, and books revenue not when the apartment is finished but as buyers commit and pay in instalments — the **presales** (*doanh số bán hàng*), the single most important leading indicator of a developer's future earnings. There's a long lag between a presale and the revenue hitting the income statement (often two to three years, as the project is built and handed over). That lag is *the whole game*: when presales turn up in mid cycle, you can *see* the earnings coming years in advance, which is why the market re-rates developers on rising presales long before the profit lands.

Why does demand recover in mid cycle specifically? Stack the conditions: credit is flowing and *broadening* into mortgages, so buyers can get loans; the SBV's easing has pulled mortgage rates *down* from their downturn highs, so monthly payments are affordable again; incomes are rising in the expansion; and — critically for Vietnam — confidence returns, so households stop sitting on cash and start committing to apartments again. On the *supply* side, the **corporate-bond market** (*trái phiếu doanh nghiệp*) reopens. Vietnamese developers fund their launches heavily with corporate bonds; when that market froze in the 2022 crisis, developers couldn't fund construction and launches collapsed. When the bond market thaws in mid cycle, developers can fund launches again — and the presale engine restarts. So the property leg needs *both* blades of the scissors: demand returning *and* the funding market reopening. That dual dependency is why property's timing is the most variable of the three mid-cycle sectors.

#### Worked example: presales recovery and the developer re-rating

Take a stylized developer sitting on a large land bank, whose presales cratered in the downturn. In the bad year it presold only **2,000 units** at an average price of **3 billion dong** per unit — presales of 6,000 billion dong (6 trillion dong, about **\$236mn** at \$1 = 25,400 dong). The stock got crushed because the future earnings stream visibly dried up.

Now mid cycle arrives. Mortgage rates have fallen, the bond market has reopened so the developer can fund construction, and demand is back. The developer launches the inventory it sat on and presells **4,500 units** at **3.2 billion dong** each (prices firmed too): presales of 14,400 billion dong (14.4 trillion dong, about **\$567mn**). Presales have gone from 6 to 14.4 trillion dong — a **+140%** jump (14.4 ÷ 6 − 1 = 1.4).

Here's the timing insight: those presales won't be *recognized* as revenue for two to three years, as the projects are built and handed over. But the market doesn't wait — it re-rates the developer's stock *now*, on the visible presale recovery, pricing in the earnings stream it can already see coming. So the property stock leads on the *leading* indicator (presales) while the *lagging* indicator (recognized revenue) is still depressed. The intuition: **in property you buy the presale turn, not the earnings print — by the time recognized earnings recover, the easy gains are gone.** This is also why property is the leg you size up *last* in the rotation: you wait for presales to confirm the turn and the bond market to confirm the funding, because either one failing leaves the developer stuck on its land.

## Reading the relative-strength handoff

Now the single most actionable skill in this whole post: reading the RS handoff *while it's happening*. Everything else — FDI, public investment, earnings — confirms the thesis fundamentally. RS is what lets you *act* before the fundamentals are obvious to everyone.

![Stylized relative strength handoff brokers rolling over while industrials rise through one](/imgs/blogs/mid-cycle-expansion-vietnam-industrials-property-materials-4.png)

Recall the definition: a sector's relative strength is *(sector index) ÷ (VN-Index)*, tracked over time. Normalize it so that 1.0 means "performing exactly in line with the index." Above 1.0 and rising = leading. Below 1.0 and falling = lagging. The *level* matters less than the *trend* and the *crossing*.

In the chart above, two stylized RS lines tell the whole story. The brokers' RS starts high — say **1.20** — because they've been the early-cycle leader and have crushed the index for months. But it's *rolling over*: 1.20, 1.18, 1.14, 1.08, 1.02, **0.98**, 0.95, 0.93, 0.92, 0.91, 0.90. It breaks its rising trend, then breaks *below 1.0*: the early leader has gone from outperforming to *underperforming* the index. Simultaneously the industrials' RS starts *below* the index — **0.92** — and turns up: 0.92, 0.94, 0.97, **1.00**, 1.04, 1.08, 1.11, 1.13, 1.15, 1.16, 1.17. It pushes *through* 1.0 from below. The point where the two lines cross — brokers falling through, industrials rising through — is the **relative-strength handoff**. That crossover is your signal that mid-cycle leadership has begun.

#### Worked example: the RS crossover, with the ratio math

Let's do the arithmetic so it's not hand-waving. Say VN-Index is at **1,300**. Brokers' sector index is at **1,560**, so brokers' RS = 1,560 ÷ 1,300 = **1.20** — they're 20% ahead of the index, the early-cycle leader. Industrials' sector index is at **1,196**, so industrials' RS = 1,196 ÷ 1,300 = **0.92** — lagging by 8%.

Now run the tape forward several months. The index grinds up to **1,400** (a healthy mid-cycle market). But the brokers' sector index only crawls to **1,260** — it lagged. Brokers' RS = 1,260 ÷ 1,400 = **0.90**. The brokers *rose* in absolute terms (well, fell slightly here) but *collapsed* relative to the index. Meanwhile industrials' sector index surges to **1,638**: industrials' RS = 1,638 ÷ 1,400 = **1.17**. The two ratios have *crossed* — brokers 1.20 → 0.90, industrials 0.92 → 1.17.

The dollar consequence: \$10,000 (about 254 million dong) parked in the broker basket at the start would be worth roughly \$8,070 in *relative* terms versus the index, while \$10,000 in industrials would be worth about \$12,700 relative to the index. The intuition: **you don't need to call the absolute top in brokers — you only need to notice that their *relative* trend has rolled over while the next group's has turned up, and rotate on the cross.** That's the entire skill.

A crucial caution: RS *rotations* are real but RS *whipsaws* are common, especially on VN-Index where retail flows are violent. One down-week in brokers' RS is noise; a *sustained* roll-over below the trend, confirmed by the next group sustaining a move *above* 1.0, is signal. Wait for the trend, not the tick.

## How the mid-cycle sectors line up: drivers, confirmation, and the metric to watch

Let's consolidate the three sectors into one reference you can keep next to you. Each mid-cycle sector has a *driver* (the macro engine), a *what-confirms-leadership* tell (how you know it's actually working, not just hoping), and a *key metric* (the one number to track).

![Mid-cycle sectors and their drivers what confirms leadership and the key metric](/imgs/blogs/mid-cycle-expansion-vietnam-industrials-property-materials-5.png)

- **Industrials.** Driver: *disbursed FDI* and the China+1 factory relocation. Confirms leadership: industrial-park land absorption rising and port throughput climbing. Key metric: **disbursed FDI (\$bn)** plus park occupancy.
- **Real estate.** Driver: *recovering housing demand* — credit flowing, mortgage rates lower, confidence and incomes rising. Confirms leadership: developers' *presales* turning back up after the downturn freeze. Key metric: **presales growth** (and the *trái phiếu doanh nghiệp* corporate-bond market reopening, which is the developers' funding lifeline).
- **Materials.** Driver: the *building boom* — private construction *plus* public investment (*đầu tư công*). Confirms leadership: steel and cement *volumes* rising and selling-price-minus-input *spreads* widening. Key metric: **construction-steel volume** and the public-investment disbursement run-rate.

Notice the structure: each sector has a *different* leading indicator, so you can track them independently and watch *which* engine fires first. Often it's industrials (FDI is the most reliable mid-cycle series), then materials as public investment and construction ramp, with property's timing the most variable because it depends on the bond market reopening and demand actually returning. The point of the matrix is that you don't treat "the real economy" as one blob — you watch three distinct meters.

## What a mid-cycle leadership year looks like on the tape

It helps to see the *shape* of a mid-cycle year in sector returns. In a clean mid-cycle year, the real-economy trio leads and the early-cycle financials cool — not necessarily *falling*, just *lagging* as the leadership passes.

![Illustrative mid-cycle year sector returns industrials property materials leading banks securities cooling](/imgs/blogs/mid-cycle-expansion-vietnam-industrials-property-materials-6.png)

The directional, illustrative pattern of a mid-cycle leadership year looks like this: **Industrials +30%, Real Estate +25%, Materials +22%, Banks +15%, Securities +8%.** (These are *illustrative and directional* — the point is the *ordering and the spread*, not precise forecasts; the actual numbers vary by year.) Read the shape, not the digits: the three mid-cycle sectors cluster at the top, while the two early-cycle financials cluster at the bottom — *still positive* (it's a bull market, everything's up) but clearly *lagging*. That spread — the mid-cycle trio beating the financials by 10–20 points — is the *quantitative footprint of the handoff*. When you see that ranking emerging in the trailing 6-month sector returns, the rotation is on.

This is also why "the handoff is obvious in real time" is a myth (we'll hit it again in the misconceptions): in the moment, brokers are *still up 8%* and bank holders are *still up 15%*. Nothing is screaming "sell." The handoff is visible only in the *relative* ranking and the *RS trend* — which is exactly why most retail accounts miss it and keep holding the cooling leader.

## Common misconceptions

Three beliefs reliably destroy mid-cycle returns. Each one feels reasonable in the moment, which is why it's dangerous.

### Misconception 1: "Stay in the early leaders forever — brokers always work"

The early-cycle financials are the most seductive trap on VN-Index precisely because they work *so well, so fast* off the bottom that they teach retail a false lesson: *brokers always go up*. They don't. They go up *explosively in early cycle* and then *hand off* — and because they're the highest-beta group, when they stop leading they often don't just lag, they *fall hard* into the next downturn.

The number that should be tattooed on every Vietnamese trader's screen: in 2021 the broker sector returned roughly **+110%**; in 2022 the same group fell about **−58%**. The biggest leader became the biggest loser inside twelve months. Staying in the early leaders "forever" means riding that round-trip all the way back down. The whole *point* of understanding mid cycle is to rotate *off* the early leaders while they're cooling but *before* they crash — to hand the baton along with the market.

### Misconception 2: "Mid cycle means just keep buying everything — it's a bull market"

In a roaring mid-cycle expansion, it's tempting to think stock-picking is pointless: everything's going up, so just buy the index, or buy whatever's already run. Both are subtly wrong. *Buy-the-index* leaves the mid-cycle alpha on the table — the spread in our snapshot (industrials +30 vs securities +8) is 22 points of relative performance you capture by being *in the right sectors*. And *buy-whatever's-run* means buying the cooling early leaders at the top, the exact mistake of Misconception 1.

"It's a bull market so just keep buying everything" also ignores that mid cycle is *internally rotational*. Leadership is moving *within* the bull market — out of financials, into the real economy. The money is conserved (a fixed-ish pool chasing returns; see the [sector-rotation post](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/sector-rotation-explained-leaders-and-laggards)), so buying the *next* leader is funded by selling the *current* one. Mid cycle rewards *rotating*, not blanket-buying.

### Misconception 3: "The handoff is obvious in real time"

It is not. As we just saw, at the moment leadership rotates, the early leaders are *still positive* — brokers up 8%, banks up 15% in our snapshot. No headline declares the handoff. The fundamental confirmation (earnings catching price) arrives *quarters later*, in the income statements. The only real-time signal is the *relative-strength rotation* — a quiet, gradual crossing of two ratio lines that most retail investors never even chart.

This is why the discipline matters: you can't wait for the handoff to be *obvious*, because by the time it's obvious in the returns, you've missed the early part of the new leadership and you're still overweight the old one. You have to act on the *RS trend* while it still feels uncomfortable — selling brokers that are still green to buy industrials that haven't yet broken out hard. That discomfort is the *price of being early*, and being early is the entire edge.

## How it shows up on VN-Index

Two episodes show the mid-cycle handoff concretely on the Vietnamese tape.

### The 2021 mid-cycle rotation

The pandemic-recovery bull that began in the second half of 2020 ran in textbook sequence. *Early cycle* (late 2020 into the first half of 2021) was the financials' show: brokers and banks led as the SBV slashed rates, retail accounts flooded in, margin balances exploded, and the financial complex re-rated off its lows. Then, through the back half of 2021, leadership *broadened and rotated*. With FDI grinding higher (\$19.7bn disbursed in 2021), credit flowing into the real economy, and a building boom underway, leadership passed to the mid-cycle sectors: steel (Hoa Phat had a monster run as global and domestic steel demand surged), property developers launching into recovering demand, and the industrial and construction names. The brokers' *relative* leadership faded even as the index made new highs — the classic RS roll-over.

What made the 2021 rotation so instructive is how *quiet* the handoff was while it happened. Through the middle of 2021 the brokers were *still going up* in absolute terms — new accounts kept opening, margin kept climbing — so the retail crowd kept piling in, convinced the trade was eternal. But under the surface the brokers' *relative strength* had already rolled over: they were no longer making new highs *against the index*, even as the index pushed higher on the back of steel, property and the industrials. That is the RS roll-over described in the abstract, playing out on a real tape. The investors who were watching the *relative* lines rotated; the ones watching only the *absolute* price of their broker stocks saw nothing wrong until it was far too late.

The cruel epilogue confirms the whole framework: the same brokers that led in 2021 (+110%) were the *deepest losers* in the 2022 downturn (−58%) when the corporate-bond crisis hit, the SBV reversed to hiking, and the cycle rolled over into recession. The investors who rotated *off* the early leaders into the mid-cycle real economy in late 2021 — and then *out* of cyclicals into defensives as the cycle aged — preserved enormous gains. The ones who stayed in brokers "because they always work" gave it all back. A \$10,000 broker position from early 2021 that wasn't rotated rode +110% to about \$21,000 and then −58% back to roughly \$8,800 — a *loss* over the round trip, despite owning "the best sector" at the start. Rotation isn't a refinement; it's the difference between keeping the gain and giving it back.

### The 2024–25 industrial and public-investment leadership

The more recent episode is the FDI-and-public-investment leadership of 2024–25. As the economy recovered from the 2022–23 shock, the mid-cycle engines fired hard. *Disbursed FDI* climbed to \$25.4bn in 2024 and roughly \$28bn in 2025 — the China+1 relocation in full swing — and the industrial-park operators (Kinh Bac, IDICO, Becamex), the ports (Gemadept), and the logistics names led on that real, grinding FDI ramp. Simultaneously, *public investment* was a deliberate policy lever: with the property market still healing from the bond crisis, the government leaned on infrastructure disbursement (climbing toward ~790 trillion dong / ~\$31bn in 2025) to carry growth — the North–South expressway, ring roads, and the Long Thanh airport mega-project — handing materials (steel, cement, construction stone) a powerful public-investment leg that ran *independently* of the still-recovering private property cycle.

That 2024–25 leadership is the purest demonstration of the post's thesis: **when financials' early-cycle re-rating is done, the next leg of returns lives in the real economy — and within the real economy, you follow the *spending*: FDI for industrials, public investment for materials, and recovering demand for property as and when it comes back.**

It's also a useful illustration of how the three legs can fire *out of sync*. In 2024–25 the industrials and materials legs led cleanly — FDI was grinding higher and public investment was a deliberate policy throttle — while the *property* leg lagged, because the corporate-bond market was still healing from the 2022 crisis and developer funding was constrained. An investor mechanically "buying the mid-cycle trio" in equal weight would have done fine on two legs and waited on the third. This is exactly why the matrix from earlier insists you track each sector's *own* leading indicator rather than treating "the real economy" as one trade: FDI confirmed industrials, public-investment disbursement confirmed materials, but presales and the bond market hadn't yet confirmed property — so a disciplined rotator overweighted the two confirmed engines and sized property up only as its signals turned. The mid-cycle handoff is not a single switch; it's three meters you read independently.

#### Worked example: the rotation return — rotating vs staying in brokers

Put a number on the cost of *not* rotating. Two investors each start with **\$20,000** (about 508 million dong) at the moment of the RS handoff.

**Investor A stays in brokers.** The brokers are the cooling early leader. Over the next year they lag — say the broker basket returns **+8%** (our snapshot number). A's \$20,000 grows to **\$20,000 × 1.08 = \$21,600.** A gain, yes — it's still a bull market — but a *lagging* one.

**Investor B rotates into the mid-cycle trio.** B sells the brokers and splits the \$20,000 across industrials, property and materials — roughly the +30%, +25%, +22% of our snapshot, an average of about **+25.7%**. B's \$20,000 grows to **\$20,000 × 1.257 ≈ \$25,140.**

The gap: **\$25,140 − \$21,600 = \$3,540**, or about **18%** of the starting capital, *earned in a single year purely by rotating on the handoff* — selling a still-green leader to buy the next one. At \$1 = 25,400 dong, that \$3,540 edge is about **90 million dong** of outperformance on a half-billion-dong account. The intuition: **the cost of clinging to the cooling early leader isn't a loss — it's the much bigger gain you forfeit by not rotating into the new one.** Over a multi-year cycle, compounding that handoff edge is the difference between a good decade and a mediocre one.

## The playbook: how to trade the mid-cycle handoff

Here's the payoff — the concrete, do-this-not-that playbook. The structure is: the *setup* (what you're watching for), the *trigger* (when to act), the *position* (what to hold), and the *invalidation* (when you're wrong).

![Mid-cycle portfolio shift from heavy in brokers to rotated into industrials property and materials](/imgs/blogs/mid-cycle-expansion-vietnam-industrials-property-materials-7.png)

**The setup — what you're watching.** You're in a confirmed expansion: GDP strong (6.5%+), credit accelerating (14%+ system loan growth) and *broadening* into the real economy, confidence back. You've ridden the early-cycle financials and they've worked. Now you watch the three handoff signals: (1) brokers' and banks' *RS rolling over* below its trend toward and through 1.0; (2) credit *broadening* — the SBV data tilting toward manufacturing, construction and property lending; (3) the mid-cycle sectors' *earnings starting to accelerate*, with FDI and public-investment series confirming the engines are running.

**The trigger — when to act.** The cleanest trigger is the **RS crossover**: brokers'/banks' RS breaking *below 1.0* on a sustained basis (not a one-week wobble) *while* industrials', property's or materials' RS pushes *above 1.0* from below. That cross is your go signal. Don't wait for the move to be obvious in the trailing returns — by then you're late. Act on the RS trend while the early leaders are still green; that discomfort is the edge.

**The position — what to rotate into.** Trim the cooling early-cycle financials (brokers first — highest beta, fastest to roll over — then banks) and rotate the proceeds into the mid-cycle trio:

- **Industrials** — the industrial-park operators (KBC, IDC, BCM) and ports/logistics (GMD) levered to *disbursed FDI*. Lead here when the FDI series is grinding higher and park absorption is rising.
- **Materials** — steel (HPG above all), cement, construction stone, levered to the *building boom and public investment*. Lean here when public-investment disbursement is accelerating; remember the violent operating leverage cuts both ways.
- **Real estate** — the developers (VHM, NLG, KDH) levered to *recovering presales*. Add here once presales turn up *and* the corporate-bond market is open enough to fund launches — property's timing is the most variable of the three, so let presales confirm before you size up.

**Sizing and sequencing.** You don't dump everything at once. Rotate *as the RS confirms each engine*: typically industrials first (most reliable signal), materials as public investment ramps, property last (wait for presales). Scale into the new leaders as their RS sustains above 1.0; trim the old leaders as their RS sustains below it. This is a *rotation*, not a single trade.

A word on *how much* to put in each leg, because the three mid-cycle sectors are not equally risky. Materials — steel above all — carries the most operating leverage, which means it's the most explosive on the way up *and* the most brutal if volumes disappoint or input costs spike; size it as the high-octane sleeve, not the core. Industrials is the steadiest of the three, because the FDI series is the most persistent and the park-and-port earnings build slowly and visibly; it can carry the largest, most patient allocation. Property is the highest *optionality* leg — when the presale turn and the bond-market reopening both come through, the developer re-rating is enormous, but the timing is the least reliable, so it earns a starter position that you add to only on confirmation. A reasonable mid-cycle frame might be a larger core in industrials, a meaningful but risk-budgeted sleeve in materials, and a scaling position in property — each sized to its *reliability*, not just its upside. And keep the early-cycle financials on a *trim, don't dump* discipline: you reduce them as their RS rolls over, but you don't have to be out completely on day one — the rotation is gradual on both sides, funded by the cooling leaders as the new ones confirm.

There's also a *time-stop* worth keeping. Mid cycle is the longest phase, but it isn't forever; eventually the expansion overheats into late cycle and the cyclicals you rotated *into* become the leaders you'll need to rotate *out of*. So even a perfectly executed mid-cycle rotation has a shelf life. The same RS discipline that got you in gets you out: when the mid-cycle sectors' RS finally rolls over — and late-cycle leaders like energy, and then defensives like utilities and staples, start turning their RS up — you hand the baton along again. Holding the mid-cycle cyclicals into the *next* downturn repeats the brokers' 2022 mistake one phase later. The playbook is never "buy and forget"; it's "ride the leader until its relative strength rolls over, then pass the baton."

**The invalidation — when you're wrong.** You're wrong if the *expansion itself* is failing rather than maturing. The view invalidates if: (1) the **RS crossover reverses** — the mid-cycle sectors' RS fails to hold above 1.0 and the financials' RS turns *back up* (the rotation was a head-fake, and you respect the tape); (2) **credit growth rolls over** — system loan growth decelerating sharply means the fuel is being cut, often by the SBV tightening, which kills the real-economy trade; (3) the **FDI and public-investment series stall or fall** — if disbursed FDI flattens or public-investment disbursement is *cut* (not just slowed), the mid-cycle engines lose their fuel and you should stand down. And note the *other* exit: as the cycle ages toward *late* cycle, even a successful mid-cycle rotation eventually hands off again — out of these cyclicals into late-cycle energy and then defensives — but that's the next chapter in the rotation, covered in the [sector-rotation post](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/sector-rotation-explained-leaders-and-laggards).

The discipline underneath the whole playbook is the same one that makes the early-cycle financial trade work and the same one that destroys the buy-and-hold-the-leader crowd: **leadership is a relay, not a sprint.** You get paid for handing the baton along *with* the market — rotating off the cooling early leaders into the rising real economy on the RS handoff — not for clinging to whatever ran last.
