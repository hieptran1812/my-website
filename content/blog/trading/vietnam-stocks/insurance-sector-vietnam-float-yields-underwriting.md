> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A Vietnamese insurer makes money two ways: a thin underwriting profit (premiums minus claims and costs) and, the far bigger lever, investment income on the **float** — the giant pile of premiums it holds before claims come due — which it parks mostly in bank deposits and government bonds.
>
> - Because that float is invested in deposits and bonds, an insurer is really a **leveraged play on interest rates**: when deposit and bond yields rise, investment income rises directly on the same float, with no extra premium written.
> - Underwriting is measured by the **combined ratio** — claims plus expenses divided by premiums; under 100% the insurer makes an underwriting profit, over 100% it loses on underwriting and leans on investment income to stay profitable.
> - **Non-life and life are different animals**: non-life (cars, property, health, cargo) rebounded around 10% in 2024, while life premiums *fell* two straight years on a 2023 mis-selling scandal that broke public trust in bancassurance.
> - The one number to remember: for a float-heavy insurer, a **2-percentage-point move in yields** can swing profit far more than a year of premium growth — which is why you own insurers when rates are high or rising, and why the rate cycle, not the premium headline, is the real driver.

In the second half of 2022, the State Bank of Vietnam did something it had not done in years: it raised its policy rates twice in quick succession, pushing the refinancing rate from 4.0% up to 6.0% to defend a sliding dong and fight imported inflation. For most of the stock market this was bad news — higher rates choke property developers, squeeze brokers, and cool the whole index. But one corner of the market quietly loved it. Vietnam's listed insurers, led by PVI Holdings (PVI) and Bao Viet Holdings (BVH), were sitting on tens of trillions of dong of premiums they had collected but not yet paid out in claims — and almost all of that money was sitting in bank deposits and government bonds. When deposit rates jumped, the interest those piles threw off jumped with them. The insurers had written no new policies to earn that windfall; they simply rolled their maturing deposits into fresh ones at a much higher rate, and their investment income climbed quarter after quarter.

That is the single most important and most misunderstood fact about the insurance sector: an insurer is not really an underwriting business with some investments on the side. In Vietnam, it is closer to a *bond-and-deposit portfolio with an insurance business bolted on the front to source cheap money.* The premiums are the funding; the investment book is where the profit lives. And because that investment book is overwhelmingly deposits and government bonds, the whole sector behaves like a leveraged bet on the level of interest rates. When you understand that, the insurance sector stops looking like a sleepy defensive corner and starts looking like what it is: one of the cleanest rate-sensitive trades on VN-Index.

This post takes the whole thing apart from absolute zero. We will build up what an insurer actually is, what the *float* is and why it is the key asset, how to read the **combined ratio** that measures underwriting, why life and non-life insurance are almost different industries, how the float makes the sector a leveraged play on yields, what the 2023 mis-selling scandal did to life premiums, how to value an insurer, and finally how to trade the whole sector as it moves through the interest-rate cycle and shows up on VN-Index.

## Foundations: how an insurer actually makes money

Start with the only thing insurance really is: a business that **collects money now in exchange for a promise to pay later if something bad happens.** Everything else — float, combined ratio, embedded value — is detail built on top of that one sentence.

### The basic deal: premiums now, claims later

When you buy car insurance from Bao Minh (BMI) or PVI, you hand over a **premium** — say two million dong for a year of coverage — and in return the insurer promises that if you crash, it will pay to fix or replace your car. Most years, you do not crash. The insurer keeps your two million dong. Across millions of customers, a few crash and the insurer pays out big claims to them; the vast majority do not, and the insurer keeps their premiums. The whole business rests on the **law of large numbers**: while any one driver's accidents are unpredictable, the *average* accident rate across a huge pool of drivers is remarkably stable and forecastable. The insurer's job is to set the premium high enough that the pooled premiums comfortably cover the pooled claims, plus its costs, plus a profit.

This is **risk pooling**: thousands of people each pay a small, certain amount (the premium) to be protected against a large, uncertain loss (the claim). The insurer sits in the middle, collecting many small certain payments and making a few large uncertain ones. Its skill is in *pricing the risk* — charging a premium that reflects how likely and how large the claims will be — and in *holding enough capital* to survive a bad year when claims come in higher than expected.

There is a useful everyday analogy. Picture a neighborhood of a thousand families who agree that whenever one family's house burns down, everyone chips in to rebuild it. Each family pays a small annual contribution into a common pot; when a fire happens (rarely), the pot pays to rebuild. As long as the pot is big enough and fires stay rare, every family is protected against a catastrophe they could never afford alone, for a price they barely notice. An insurer is just this neighborhood pot run as a business — it sets the contribution (the premium), manages the pot (the reserves and float), pays the claims, and keeps any surplus as profit. The genius of the arrangement is that the *individual* risk (will *my* house burn?) is terrifying and unknowable, but the *pooled* risk (how many of a thousand houses burn in a year?) is steady and predictable. Insurance converts an individual's unbearable uncertainty into a group's manageable statistic, and it gets paid for doing the conversion.

The insurer protects itself, in turn, with two more layers. The first is **diversification** — writing many *uncorrelated* risks so that a bad year in one line (say a wave of motor claims) is offset by a normal year in others (property, health, cargo). The second is **reinsurance** — buying insurance on its own book from a bigger, global reinsurer, so that a single catastrophic event (a typhoon flooding a province, a factory fire claiming a huge property policy) does not wipe out the insurer's capital. Vietnamese insurers cede a meaningful share of their large industrial and catastrophe risks to international reinsurers and to the domestic reinsurer Vinare; PVI even runs its own reinsurance arm. Reinsurance smooths the insurer's results and caps its worst-case loss, at the cost of handing over some premium — yet another reason underwriting profit alone is thin, and the float is where the real money compounds.

But notice the timing. The premium arrives *first.* The claim, if it ever comes, arrives *later* — sometimes much later. For car insurance, the gap might be months. For a life-insurance policy, the gap between collecting premiums and paying the death benefit can be *decades.* During that entire gap, the insurer is *holding other people's money.* That held money has a name, and it is the most important word in this whole post.

### What "float" is, and why it is the key asset

The **float** is the pool of money an insurer holds between the moment it collects a premium and the moment it pays out the corresponding claim. It is money the insurer does not own — it belongs, in a sense, to future claimants — but that the insurer gets to *hold and invest* in the meantime. Warren Buffett, who built Berkshire Hathaway on exactly this insight, calls float "money we hold but don't own," and he treats it as the crown jewel of the insurance business.

Here is why the float is so powerful. A normal company that wants to invest needs to either earn profits and reinvest them, or borrow money and pay interest on the loan. An insurer gets something better than a loan: it gets a giant pile of cash to invest, and instead of *paying* interest to borrow it, it often gets *paid* (via the premium margin) to hold it. The float is like a loan with a negative interest rate — or, at worst, a very cheap one. And because new premiums keep flowing in as old claims get paid out, the float does not shrink; for a growing insurer, the float *grows year after year,* a permanent and expanding pool of investable money.

In Vietnam, that float is enormous relative to the size of the listed insurers, and — this is the crucial part — it is invested *conservatively.* Vietnamese insurers, by regulation and by habit, keep the overwhelming majority of their investment book in **bank term deposits** and **Vietnamese government bonds** (*trai phieu chinh phu*), with only a small slice in equities, corporate bonds, or real estate. So the float is not a hedge fund; it is, in effect, a giant savings account and bond ladder. The income it throws off — interest on the deposits and coupons on the bonds — is **investment income**, and for most Vietnamese insurers, investment income is *larger* than underwriting profit. That single fact reorganizes everything you thought you knew about insurance.

Why so conservative? Two reasons, and both reinforce the rate-sensitivity thesis. The first is *regulation:* Vietnam's insurance law and the supervisory regime cap how much of an insurer's reserves can sit in risky assets and effectively push the bulk of the float into deposits and government bonds, the safest paper available. The regulator's job is to make sure the insurer can always pay claims, and the surest way to guarantee that is to keep the float in instruments that will not blow up. The second reason is *liability matching:* an insurer's promises (the claims it will owe) are denominated in dong and fall due on a schedule, so the natural asset to hold against them is a dong-denominated, income-producing instrument with a predictable maturity — exactly what a term deposit or a government bond is. The upshot is that the float is, by design, a near-pure interest-rate instrument. There is very little equity-market risk or credit risk muddying the picture. When you own a Vietnamese insurer, you are buying, more than anything else, a leveraged claim on the yield of Vietnamese deposits and government bonds — wrapped in an underwriting business that sources the money cheaply.

It helps to see the *scale* of the float relative to everything else. A typical Vietnamese non-life insurer might write 8-10 trillion dong of premiums a year, carry equity (book value) of perhaps 5-10 trillion dong, and yet sit on an investment book — the accumulated float plus its own capital — of 20-30 trillion dong or more. The float, in other words, is several times the annual premium *and* several times the equity. That is the lever. A one-percentage-point shift in the yield on a 25-trillion-dong float is 250 billion dong; the same one point applied to a year's worth of new premiums would be a tenth of that. The float is where the size is, and the size is where the leverage to rates lives.

#### Worked example: investment income on the float

Take a non-life insurer holding **20 trillion dong** of float — premiums collected but not yet paid out as claims — invested mostly in bank deposits and government bonds. At a blended yield of **5%**, that float throws off 20tn x 5% = **1.0 trillion dong** a year of investment income. Now the rate cycle turns and the State Bank lifts policy rates; the insurer rolls its maturing deposits and bonds into new ones yielding **7%**. The same 20tn float now earns 20tn x 7% = **1.4 trillion dong** a year — an extra **400 billion dong**, or about **\$15.7mn** at roughly \$1 = 25,400 VND, dropping almost straight to pre-tax profit. The insurer wrote *not one extra policy* to earn that 400bn dong; it simply held the same float at a higher yield. The intuition: an insurer's float is a leveraged claim on the level of interest rates, and a 2-point move in yields can move profit more than a whole year's worth of new premiums.

### The combined ratio: scoring the underwriting

The float and its investment income are one engine. The other engine is **underwriting** — the core insurance act of collecting premiums and paying claims — and the way you score it is the **combined ratio.**

The combined ratio answers one question: *of every 100 dong of premium the insurer collects, how many dong go out the door in claims and running costs?* It has two parts. The **loss ratio** is claims paid (plus reserves set aside for claims not yet paid) divided by premiums — the share of every premium dong eaten by actual claims. The **expense ratio** is the insurer's operating costs — commissions to agents, salaries, marketing, administration — divided by premiums. Add the two together and you get the **combined ratio**:

The rule is simple and binary. A combined ratio **below 100%** means the insurer collected more in premiums than it paid out in claims and costs — it made an **underwriting profit**, money earned *before* any investment income. A combined ratio **above 100%** means claims plus costs *exceeded* premiums — the insurer made an **underwriting loss**, and it must rely on investment income to end the year in the black. A combined ratio of exactly 100% is the break-even line: underwriting contributes nothing, and every dong of profit comes from the float.

This is the single most important operating metric in non-life insurance, and it is worth dwelling on what it implies. Many Vietnamese non-life insurers, in competitive lines like motor and health, run combined ratios *right around or even above 100%.* They write the policy essentially at break-even — or at a small underwriting loss — *on purpose,* because the policy is not really how they make money. The policy is how they *source the float.* The profit comes later, from investing that float. An insurer that runs a combined ratio of 102% but earns a fat investment yield on a huge float can be wildly profitable; an insurer that runs a combined ratio of 95% but has a tiny float and earns nothing on it can be mediocre. You cannot judge an insurer on underwriting alone, and you cannot judge it on investments alone — you have to see both engines together.

![Before and after comparison of a combined ratio below 100 percent and above 100 percent](/imgs/blogs/insurance-sector-vietnam-float-yields-underwriting-5.png)

#### Worked example: reading the combined ratio

Take a non-life insurer that writes **10 trillion dong** of premiums in a year. It pays **6.5 trillion dong** in claims (a loss ratio of 6.5 / 10 = **65%**) and spends **3.2 trillion dong** on commissions, staff, and overhead (an expense ratio of 3.2 / 10 = **32%**). The combined ratio is 65% + 32% = **97%.** Because that is below 100%, the insurer kept 3% of its premiums as underwriting profit: 10tn x 3% = **300 billion dong** of profit earned *before* a single dong of investment income — about **\$11.8mn**. Now imagine a harder year: claims rise to 7.1tn (71% loss ratio) and costs hold at 32%, pushing the combined ratio to **103%.** Now the insurer *loses* 3% on underwriting: a **300 billion dong underwriting loss** that its investment income on the float must cover before the company shows any profit at all. The intuition: the combined ratio tells you whether the core insurance business made or lost money this year, and for many Vietnamese insurers it hovers near 100% because the real profit lives in the float, not the underwriting.

### Reserves: the liability that funds the float

One more piece of plumbing makes the float concrete. When an insurer collects premiums, it does not get to call all of that money profit. It must set aside **technical reserves** (*du phong nghiep vu*) — a liability on its balance sheet representing the claims it expects to pay in the future. For non-life, the biggest reserve is the **unearned premium reserve** (you paid for a full year of coverage up front, but the insurer only "earns" that premium day by day as the year passes) plus a **claims reserve** for accidents that have happened but not yet been paid. For life insurance, the reserve is far larger and longer-dated: a **mathematical reserve** representing the present value of all the future benefits the insurer has promised to policyholders, sometimes stretching out forty or fifty years.

These reserves *are* the float. On the balance sheet, the reserves sit on the liability side (money the insurer owes to future claimants), and the matching pile of deposits and bonds sits on the asset side. The insurer's job is to invest those assets to earn more than it has promised, while keeping enough liquid to pay claims as they fall due. The bigger and longer-dated the reserves, the bigger the float — which is exactly why **life insurance**, with its decades-long reserves, generates a far larger float per dong of premium than **non-life**, with its one-year reserves. That difference drives almost everything about how the two sub-sectors behave.

## Why insurers are a leveraged play on interest rates

Now we can state the central thesis precisely. Because the float is invested overwhelmingly in **bank deposits and government bonds**, an insurer's investment income moves almost mechanically with the level of **interest rates.** Higher deposit and bond yields lift investment income directly, on the same float, with no new premiums required. Lower yields cut it. The insurer is, in effect, a *leveraged long position on Vietnamese interest rates* — and that is the key to trading the sector.

### The transmission: from the SBV policy rate to insurer profit

Walk the chain. The State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) sets policy rates — the refinancing rate and the discount rate — which anchor the whole short-term rate structure. When the SBV raises those rates (as in 2022) or holds them high, commercial banks raise the deposit rates they offer to attract funding, and the government must offer higher yields to sell new bonds. An insurer's float is parked in exactly those instruments: term deposits at commercial banks and government bonds. As the insurer's existing deposits mature and its existing bonds are rolled, the new ones it buys carry the higher yields. Quarter by quarter, the *book yield* of the whole float drifts up toward the new market rate, and investment income climbs.

The reverse happens when the SBV cuts. In 2020-21, the SBV slashed the refinancing rate to 4.0% to support the economy through COVID, and deposit and bond yields collapsed with it. Insurers watched their maturing deposits roll into new ones at painfully low rates, and investment income sagged even though the float kept growing. So the sector's earnings are not really driven by how many policies it sells; they are driven, to first order, by where the SBV has set rates and where deposit and bond yields sit.

![Line chart of the SBV refinancing rate from 2019 to 2025](/imgs/blogs/insurance-sector-vietnam-float-yields-underwriting-3.png)

There is an important nuance: the link works *with a lag.* An insurer's float is invested in deposits and bonds of varying maturities — some mature this quarter, some in two or three years. When market rates jump, only the maturing slice gets reinvested at the new high rate immediately; the rest keeps earning its old, lower coupon until *it* matures. So the float's book yield does not snap to the new market rate overnight — it *drifts* toward it over a year or two as the portfolio rolls. This is why insurer investment income tends to lag the deposit-rate cycle: rates peaked in early 2023, but the full benefit to insurer book yields showed up through 2023 and into 2024 as more of the portfolio rolled into high-rate paper. For a trader, that lag is a feature, not a bug — it means the earnings tailwind from a rate spike persists for several quarters after the spike, and the headwind from a rate cut persists too.

### Why this is "leverage": the float is a multiple of equity

The reason a move in yields swings profit so violently is that the **float is several times the size of the insurer's equity.** Just as a bank controls assets worth ten to fifteen times its equity, an insurer's investment book — funded by reserves, i.e. the float — is a large multiple of its shareholders' capital. So when yields move, the extra (or lost) investment income is measured against the big float, but the *profit and ROE* it produces are measured against the much smaller equity. A modest move in the yield, applied to a float that is, say, four or five times equity, becomes a large move in return on equity. That is the mathematical definition of leverage, and it is why a sleepy-looking deposit-and-bond portfolio can produce surprisingly cyclical earnings.

#### Worked example: a 200bp yield move versus a year of premium growth

Take a float-heavy insurer with **25 trillion dong** of float (deposits and bonds) and **8 trillion dong** of premiums a year, running a combined ratio of 99% — so underwriting contributes a slim 1% of premiums, or **80 billion dong** of underwriting profit. Now run two scenarios. **Scenario A: premiums grow a strong 10%.** Next year's premiums are 8.8tn; at the same 99% combined ratio, underwriting profit rises to 88bn — a gain of just **8 billion dong.** **Scenario B: yields rise 200 basis points** (2 percentage points), lifting the float's book yield from 5% to 7%. Investment income rises from 25tn x 5% = 1.25tn to 25tn x 7% = 1.75tn — a gain of **500 billion dong**, about **\$19.7mn.** The yield move was worth *sixty times* the premium growth. The intuition: for a float-heavy insurer, the rate cycle dwarfs the premium cycle — a 200bp move in yields swings profit far more than even a banner year of new-policy growth, which is exactly why you trade the sector off rates, not off premium headlines.

### Duration: why the rate sensitivity differs across the book

Not all of the rate sensitivity is good. There is a subtlety that separates the insurers who simply *earn more* when rates rise from those who can also take a *capital hit.* It comes down to **duration** — how long, on average, the insurer's bonds are locked in.

When market interest rates rise, the *price* of an existing fixed-rate bond *falls* (because new bonds pay more, so nobody will pay full price for an old low-coupon bond). An insurer that holds long-dated government bonds will see the mark-to-market value of those bonds drop when rates rise — a hit to its book value and, depending on accounting, its reported equity. The flip side is that higher rates lift the *income* the insurer will earn as it reinvests. So a rate rise is a tug-of-war: bad for the *price* of the bonds the insurer already holds (a one-time capital mark), good for the *income* it earns going forward (a recurring earnings boost). For most Vietnamese non-life insurers, whose books are dominated by short-dated deposits and shorter bonds, the income effect dominates and rising rates are unambiguously good. For life insurers with long-dated bond portfolios, the picture is more nuanced — but in Vietnam, where most of the float is in deposits and short-to-medium government bonds, the sector as a whole is a *positive* play on rising rates. Hold that distinction; it matters for which insurer you pick.

The reason the *income* effect dominates for the non-life names is that a term deposit and a short bond have almost no price risk. A one-year deposit cannot fall in mark-to-market value the way a fifteen-year bond can — it simply matures at par in twelve months and rolls into a fresh deposit at whatever the new rate is. So a portfolio stuffed with deposits and short government bonds captures almost the entire *upside* of rising rates (higher reinvestment yield) while suffering almost none of the *downside* (price marks). This is precisely why the float being parked in deposits, rather than long bonds, is a *feature* for a trader betting on rising rates: it is the rate exposure with the price risk stripped out. The accounting also helps — much of the deposit-and-held-to-maturity book is carried at cost rather than marked to market, so rising rates flow through as higher interest income on the income statement without an offsetting hit to reported equity.

For the **life** names the calculus flips in an important way. Because a life insurer holds long-dated bonds to match its decades-long promises, a rate rise *does* mark down the price of those bonds, and if the accounting forces some of that book to fair value, reported equity can take a hit even as future income improves. There is also a deeper, opposite worry for life insurers in a *low*-rate world: if a life insurer sold long-dated savings policies promising customers, say, a 5-6% return, and then market yields fall to 3-4%, the insurer can be locked into paying more than it can earn — a **negative spread** that erodes the life book over time. This is the classic life-insurance trap that crippled Japanese and European life insurers in their low-rate decades. Vietnamese life insurers have so far avoided it, partly because Vietnamese rates have stayed relatively high, but it is the reason life insurers, unlike non-life ones, genuinely *fear* a long era of low rates and benefit doubly from high ones — high rates both lift their float income and ease the burden of their guarantees.

## Life versus non-life: almost two different industries

The Vietnamese insurance market splits into two sub-sectors that share a name but behave very differently: **non-life** (also called general insurance) and **life.** They differ in what they cover, how long their reserves last, what drives their premiums, and how sensitive they are to interest rates. A trader has to know which one a given ticker actually is.

![Comparison matrix of life versus non-life insurance across four attributes](/imgs/blogs/insurance-sector-vietnam-float-yields-underwriting-7.png)

### Non-life: short-tail, fast-turning, motor-and-health-driven

**Non-life insurance** (*bao hiem phi nhan tho*) covers property and casualty: motor vehicles, health, fire and property, cargo and marine, aviation, engineering, and liability. Its defining feature is that the policies are **short-tail** — usually one-year contracts, with claims paid out within months of an accident. Because the reserves are short-dated (a year of unearned premium plus near-term claims), the float per dong of premium is *smaller* than in life, and the bonds backing it are short-dated, so the rate sensitivity is mostly about *reinvestment yield*, not bond-price risk.

What drives non-life premiums is the *real economy.* More cars and motorbikes on the road means more compulsory motor insurance. More factories, warehouses, and apartment blocks means more property and fire insurance. More trade flowing through Vietnam's ports means more cargo and marine cover. So non-life premium growth tracks GDP, vehicle sales, construction, and trade — it is a **cyclical, economy-linked** business. When Vietnam's economy is growing and incomes are rising, non-life premiums grow steadily; when the economy slows, growth flattens. In 2023, non-life growth cooled to around 5% as the economy stumbled through the property and bond crisis; in **2024, non-life rebounded to roughly 10%** as growth recovered, cars sold again, and trade picked up.

The non-life market is also fragmented and intensely competitive. Dozens of insurers fight for the same motor and health customers, which keeps premium rates low and combined ratios near 100%. The discipline of *not* chasing market share by underpricing is what separates a profitable non-life insurer from a value-destroying one — and it is why the float-and-investment engine matters so much, because underwriting itself often barely breaks even.

It is worth seeing how the non-life book breaks down by *line*, because the lines behave differently. **Motor** (car and motorbike insurance, including the compulsory third-party cover every vehicle must carry) is the largest line and the most competitive — high volume, thin margins, combined ratios that drift around or above 100% in the price wars. **Health and personal accident** has grown fast as a rising middle class buys private medical cover, but it is claims-heavy and hard to price as healthcare costs rise. **Property and fire** insurance on factories, warehouses, and buildings is lumpier — mostly benign, but exposed to occasional large losses (a single factory fire can blow up a quarter), which is why it is heavily reinsured. **Cargo and marine** tracks trade volumes through Vietnam's ports. **Aviation, energy, and engineering** are specialist, large-ticket lines where PVI in particular is strong thanks to its PetroVietnam heritage. The mix matters: an insurer weighted toward stable, well-priced commercial lines and away from the motor price war tends to run a better combined ratio than one chasing retail motor volume. When you analyze a non-life name, the line mix tells you how disciplined — and how cyclical — its underwriting is likely to be.

There is also an **underwriting cycle** layered on top of the economic cycle, and it is worth naming because it gives the post its title. Non-life pricing moves in multi-year waves between "soft" and "hard" markets. In a **soft market**, insurers have plenty of capital, competition is fierce, premium rates are driven down, and combined ratios creep up toward (or past) 100% — underwriting profit evaporates. Eventually a run of large losses or a capital squeeze forces insurers to raise rates; the market "hardens," premium rates climb, combined ratios fall back below 100%, and underwriting profitability recovers — until the good profits attract capital and competition again, and the cycle softens anew. Vietnam's non-life market has spent much of the last decade in a *soft* phase, with motor and health price competition keeping combined ratios stubbornly near 100%. For a trader, the underwriting cycle is the slower, second-order signal beneath the faster rate cycle: when the market is soft and combined ratios are near 100%, *all* of the profit is coming from the float, and the rate cycle is the only thing that matters; a hardening market that pushes combined ratios down adds a second, independent tailwind to underwriting profit.

### Life: long-tail, savings-driven, scandal-scarred

**Life insurance** (*bao hiem nhan tho*) is a different beast. A life policy is a **long-tail** contract — often 10, 20, or more years — and in Vietnam most life products are *savings-and-protection hybrids* (endowment and unit-linked policies) where the customer pays premiums for years, accumulates a savings balance, and gets a payout on death or at maturity. Because the insurer holds those premiums for decades before paying out, the **reserves and float per dong of premium are huge** — a life insurer is a long-duration savings institution wearing an insurance label. That long float, invested in long-dated government bonds, makes life insurers more sensitive to bond *prices* (duration risk) than non-life insurers, even as it gives them a bigger float to earn on.

What drives life premiums is *household savings and confidence.* Life insurance competes with bank deposits, gold, and property as a place for a Vietnamese family to park long-term savings. When confidence is high and incomes are rising, families buy more life policies; when confidence is shaken, they stop. And confidence was *badly* shaken in 2023.

### The 2023 life mis-selling scandal

For years, Vietnamese life insurers grew premiums at a blistering 20%-plus pace, and the engine of that growth was **bancassurance** — selling life policies through bank branches. A customer would walk into a bank to take out a loan or open an account, and a bank staffer would steer them into a life-insurance policy, often bundled with the loan. The banks earned fat commissions; the insurers got cheap distribution; premiums soared.

The problem was that a large share of those sales were **mis-sold.** Customers were told the product was a high-yield savings account, or that buying the policy was required to get their loan approved, when neither was true. Many did not understand they were locking money into a multi-year policy with heavy penalties for early surrender. In early 2023, the complaints exploded into a public scandal; a government inspection of several major life insurers found that the majority of bancassurance policies were *surrendered within the first year* — a damning sign that customers never wanted them. Regulators cracked down hard, banning the practice of bundling insurance with loans and tightening sales rules.

The result was a collapse in new life premiums. Trust in the channel evaporated, the bancassurance pipeline seized up, and **life premiums fell roughly 12% in 2023 and a further ~5% in 2024** — two straight years of decline in a market that had only ever known rapid growth. This is the single most important recent fact about Vietnamese life insurance, and it explains why the life and non-life lines diverged so sharply: while non-life rebounded with the economy, life kept shrinking under the weight of broken trust.

![Line chart comparing non-life and life premium growth from 2020 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/insurance-sector-vietnam-float-yields-underwriting-4.png)

#### Worked example: premium decline versus profit growth

Here is the puzzle the scandal sets up, and it captures the whole thesis of this post. In 2023, a Vietnamese life-and-composite insurer sees its **life premiums fall 12%** — a clear top-line shock. And yet its *profit can still rise.* How? Because the same 2022-23 period brought the SBV rate hikes, and the insurer's enormous float — built up from years of prior premiums — kept earning, now at much higher yields. Suppose the float is **30 trillion dong** and the book yield rises from 4.5% to 6.0% as deposits roll over: investment income rises from 30tn x 4.5% = 1.35tn to 30tn x 6.0% = 1.80tn — a gain of **450 billion dong**, about **\$17.7mn.** That investment-income gain swamps the modest hit from lower new-premium underwriting, because *this year's new premiums are a small slice of a float built from a decade of past premiums.* The intuition: premium growth is *not* the whole story — an insurer can shrink on the top line and still grow profit if the rate cycle lifts the yield on its giant existing float. Watch the rate cycle, not just the premium headline.

## Valuation: price-to-book, ROE, and embedded value

Once you understand the two engines, valuing an insurer follows naturally. Because an insurer — like a bank — is a balance-sheet business whose assets (the float) are marked close to fair value, the natural lens is **price-to-book value (P/B)** read against **return on equity (ROE).**

### P/B against ROE: the core lens

**Book value** is the insurer's equity — its assets (the investment book plus everything else) minus its liabilities (the reserves and debt). **Price-to-book** is the market value of the company divided by that equity; it tells you how many dong investors will pay for each dong of the insurer's net worth. **Return on equity** is annual profit divided by equity; it tells you how much profit the insurer squeezes out of each dong of net worth.

The link between them is the same as for banks: *a company that earns a higher ROE deserves a higher P/B.* An insurer earning a 15% ROE is compounding its book value fast and deserves to trade above book; one earning 8% is barely keeping up with its cost of capital and deserves to trade near or below book. Crucially, because an insurer's ROE is driven heavily by **investment yields**, its "deserved" P/B *rises and falls with the rate cycle.* When rates are high and the float is earning fat yields, insurer ROEs climb and the sector re-rates higher; when rates are low and yields are thin, ROEs sag and the sector de-rates. This is the valuation expression of the same thesis: insurers are a rate trade, and even their multiples breathe with the rate cycle.

#### Worked example: P/B and ROE through the rate cycle

Take an insurer with **10 trillion dong** of equity (book value). In a low-rate year, its float earns thin yields and it makes **800 billion dong** of profit — an ROE of 800 / 10,000 = **8%.** With an 8% ROE, the market might pay around 1.0x book, valuing it at 10tn dong, roughly **\$394mn.** Now the rate cycle turns: yields rise, investment income jumps, and the same insurer earns **1.5 trillion dong** — an ROE of 1,500 / 10,000 = **15%.** With a 15% ROE, the market is willing to pay perhaps 1.5x book, valuing it at 15tn dong, about **\$591mn.** The share price did not rise just because earnings rose — it rose *twice over,* because higher earnings *also* earned a higher multiple. The intuition: an insurer's valuation is geared to the rate cycle on both axes at once — earnings and multiple move together — which makes the sector a powerful (and two-sided) rate trade.

### Embedded value: the right lens for life

For *life* insurers, P/B alone undersells the business, because a life book contains decades of *future* profit that has not yet shown up in this year's earnings or this year's book value. The industry's answer is **embedded value (EV)** — a measure that adds the insurer's current net assets to the present value of the future profits locked into the existing book of policies. Think of it this way: a life insurer that has sold a million long-dated policies has effectively booked a stream of future premium payments and investment margins that will trickle in for decades. Embedded value tries to put a present-value number on that stream today, so you can see the "true" economic worth of the franchise rather than just this year's accounting snapshot.

In practice, embedded value is most relevant for the pure-life and composite insurers (like Bao Viet, which has a large life arm). For the non-life-dominated names, P/B against ROE is the workhorse. The key takeaway is that life and non-life require *different valuation lenses* — short-tail non-life on P/B and combined ratio, long-tail life on embedded value and the trajectory of new-business growth — and confusing the two leads you to misjudge both.

Embedded value also explains why the 2023 scandal hit the life names harder than a single year's premium drop would suggest. Embedded value grows from two sources: the profit emerging from the *existing* book, and the **value of new business** written each year — the present value of future profit from this year's fresh policies. When bancassurance seized up, the value of new business collapsed, so even though the existing book kept emerging its embedded value, the *growth* engine stalled. A life insurer is valued not just on what it has but on its ability to keep adding profitable new policies, and the scandal struck exactly at that growth engine. That is why a life-heavy name can see its float earn beautifully through a rate spike and *still* de-rate, because the market is pricing a broken new-business pipeline, not just this year's earnings. The lesson generalizes: for life insurers, watch the value of new business and the health of the distribution channel as closely as you watch the float's yield.

## Common misconceptions

Three myths trip up almost everyone who looks at the insurance sector for the first time. Each one is corrected by a number.

### Myth 1: "Insurers make money mainly from underwriting"

This is the most common and most damaging mistake. People assume an insurer's profit comes from the gap between premiums collected and claims paid — that it is fundamentally an underwriting business. For many Vietnamese non-life insurers, that gap is *close to zero.* They run combined ratios right around 100%, meaning underwriting contributes almost nothing to the bottom line. The profit comes from **investment income on the float.** Recall the worked example: a 200bp move in yields was worth sixty times a year of premium growth. If you analyze an insurer by staring at its underwriting and ignoring its float and book yield, you will misjudge it completely. The correct mental model is *float-and-investment first, underwriting second.*

### Myth 2: "Insurance is defensive, like consumer staples"

Because insurance sells protection and people pay premiums in good times and bad, investors lump it in with defensives — steady, low-beta, recession-proof. This is half-right and dangerously misleading. The *demand* for insurance is fairly stable, yes. But the *earnings* are anything but defensive, because they are dominated by investment income, which swings with the rate cycle. An insurer's profit can double or halve as yields move 200-300 basis points — that is not defensive behavior, that is a leveraged macro trade. And worse, the life side proved it can suffer a two-year *premium* collapse from a confidence shock (the 2023 scandal), which no true staple would. Treat insurers as **rate-cyclicals**, not defensives. They are most defensive precisely when rates are high and stable; they are vulnerable when rates fall.

### Myth 3: "Premium growth equals profit growth"

Headlines lead with premium growth — "life premiums up 20%," "non-life up 10%" — and investors treat it as the score. But premium growth is just *this year's* new business, a small slice of a float built from many prior years of premiums. As the 2023 example showed, a life insurer's premiums can *fall 12%* while its profit *rises,* because the rate cycle lifted the yield on its giant existing float. Conversely, an insurer can grow premiums fast by *underpricing* — writing policies at a loss to win market share — which inflates the top line while *destroying* profit through a worsening combined ratio. Premium growth is a top-line vanity number; the profit comes from the combined ratio (is underwriting making or losing money?) and the book yield (what is the float earning?). Watch those two, not the premium headline.

## How it shows up on VN-Index

The insurance sector is a modest weight in VN-Index — far smaller than banks or property — but it has two listed names worth knowing, and its behavior through the rate cycle is a clean, readable signal.

### The listed insurers: PVI and Bao Viet

The two heavyweight listed insurers are **PVI Holdings (PVI)** and **Bao Viet Holdings (BVH).** PVI is the largest non-life insurer in Vietnam — born out of the PetroVietnam group, with deep roots in energy, industrial, and large-corporate insurance, plus a reinsurance arm. It is a relatively pure **non-life-and-investment** play: a big float, short-tail underwriting, and earnings geared hard to deposit and bond yields. When rates rose in 2022-23, PVI was a textbook beneficiary, and its shares and dividends reflected the higher investment income.

**Bao Viet Holdings** is the giant of the sector and a *composite* — it owns both a major life insurer and a major non-life insurer, plus a securities arm and fund management. That makes BVH a blend: a huge long-tail life float (very rate-sensitive, on the embedded-value lens) plus a non-life book plus capital-markets businesses. BVH carried the brunt of the 2023 life mis-selling scandal on its life side, even as its enormous float benefited from higher yields — a vivid illustration of the two engines pulling in opposite directions in the same company. Beyond these two, several non-life insurers like **Bao Minh (BMI)**, **PTI (Post and Telecommunication Insurance)**, **PGI (PJICO)**, and the bank-linked **MIC (Military Insurance, part of the MB group)** trade on HOSE and HNX, all sharing the same float-and-rate sensitivity.

![Grouped bar chart of approximate non-life insurance market share by company](/imgs/blogs/insurance-sector-vietnam-float-yields-underwriting-6.png)

### The 2022-23 rate spike: the sector's best window

The cleanest case study is the 2022-23 rate cycle. As the SBV hiked the refinancing rate from 4.0% to 6.0% in the second half of 2022 and deposit rates spiked toward 9-10% at some banks, insurers' investment income began to climb as their floats rolled into higher-yielding deposits and bonds. Through 2023, even as the broader market wrestled with the property and corporate-bond crisis, insurers' book yields kept drifting up (remember the lag), and the non-life names in particular posted strong investment-income-driven profits. For a sector-aware investor on VN-Index, the insurers were one of the few places where *rising rates were unambiguously good news* — a natural hedge to hold against the rate-sensitive losers (property, brokers) elsewhere in the index.

### The 2020-21 low-rate trough and the life decline

The mirror image came in 2020-21, when the SBV cut the refinancing rate to 4.0% and held it there to support the COVID-hit economy. Deposit and bond yields collapsed; insurer book yields drifted *down* as floats rolled into low-rate paper; investment income sagged. And then 2023-24 layered the life-premium collapse on top: while the non-life names rode the rate spike up, the life-heavy Bao Viet had to absorb a two-year premium decline from the mis-selling scandal even as its float benefited from higher yields. The net effect on VN-Index was that the insurance sector *diverged internally* — pure non-life names outperformed the composite life names — a reminder that "the insurance sector" is really two trades, and you have to pick the right engine for the rate environment.

## The playbook: how to trade it

Everything above resolves into a small set of concrete rules. The insurance sector is a rate trade with an underwriting overlay, and you trade it off the interest-rate cycle.

### The core position: own insurers when rates are high or rising

The central signal is the **interest-rate cycle**, read off the SBV policy rate and the deposit/bond-yield trend. **When rates are high or rising** — the SBV is hiking or holding rates elevated, deposit rates are climbing, government-bond yields are up — *own the insurers,* especially the float-heavy non-life names like PVI and Bao Minh. Their investment income climbs as floats roll into higher-yielding paper, ROEs rise, and the sector re-rates. This is the high-conviction window, and it often coincides with a period when *the rest of the rate-sensitive market is struggling,* making insurers a valuable internal hedge.

**When rates are falling** — the SBV is cutting to stimulate, deposit rates are dropping — *lighten or avoid the sector.* The same float that was a tailwind becomes a drag: maturing deposits and bonds roll into lower-yielding paper, book yields sag, investment income falls, and the sector de-rates. Falling rates are good for property, brokers, and growth stocks; they are *bad* for insurers. The sector is the natural other side of the rate trade from the brokers, which are the highest-beta beneficiaries of *falling* rates and rising liquidity.

The timing nuance from earlier is a trader's edge here. Because the float's book yield *lags* the market rate as the portfolio rolls, the earnings tailwind from a rate spike keeps building for several quarters *after* rates have peaked. That means the best window to own insurers is often not at the very moment the SBV hikes — by then property and brokers are already selling off and the market knows rates are up — but in the *quarters that follow,* as the higher yields steadily feed through into rising reported investment income and the insurers print improving earnings. Conversely, when the SBV starts cutting, the drag on insurer earnings also arrives with a lag, so the de-rating can be slower and gives you time to exit. Read the *direction* of the rate cycle for the position, and use the *lag* to time the entry and exit.

### The secondary signals: combined ratio and book yield

Once you are in the sector for the rate reason, two operating signals tell you *which* insurer and whether the underwriting is healthy. First, watch the **combined ratio.** A name whose combined ratio is creeping *up* — toward or past 100% — is either facing rising claims (a soft underwriting cycle, intense price competition) or underpricing to chase share; that is a quality warning even if investment income is strong. A name holding a combined ratio comfortably below 100% is earning on *both* engines, the higher-quality position. Second, watch the **book yield on the investment portfolio** and the share of the float in deposits versus longer bonds — the higher the reinvestment yield and the faster the float rolls, the more leverage to a rate rise. For life-heavy names, also watch **new-business value and embedded value** trends, and whether the bancassurance channel is recovering from the scandal.

### Sizing and the invalidation

Size the position as a **rate-cycle satellite,** not a core holding — the insurance sector is small in the index, so it is a tactical overweight you put on when the rate setup is right, sized smaller than your bank or property positions. Pair it as a hedge: long insurers (rate-up winners) against the rate-down losers when you expect rates to stay high, or simply hold it to dampen a book that is otherwise long the rate-down trade.

One more practical note on *which* name to own. The cleaner the rate trade you want, the more you should tilt toward the pure non-life, float-heavy names (PVI being the archetype): they capture the rising-rate income with minimal bond-price risk and carry none of the life-premium-scandal overhang. If you want the bigger float and are willing to accept the life complications, the composite Bao Viet gives you a larger, longer-duration float — more leverage to rates, but also more exposure to the life-premium recovery (or lack of it) and to bond-price marks. And if you are using insurers purely as a *hedge* against a rate-down book elsewhere, a basket of the liquid non-life names is the most reliable expression. The point is that "buy the insurance sector" is not one trade — it is a menu, and the rate environment tells you which item to order.

The **invalidation** is clean and follows directly from the thesis. The whole case for owning insurers is that *yields are high or rising.* The moment that reverses — the SBV pivots to cutting, deposit rates roll over, and government-bond yields trend down — the core reason to own the sector is gone, and you should exit, regardless of how cheap the P/B looks. A second invalidation is *underwriting deterioration:* if a name's combined ratio blows through 100% and keeps climbing on rising claims or aggressive underpricing, the underwriting engine is now *destroying* value and even a strong float may not save the return on equity. And for the life names specifically, a *failure of the bancassurance channel to recover* — premiums staying in decline, embedded value stagnating — invalidates any thesis built on a life-premium rebound. Get the rate direction wrong, or ignore a combined ratio breaking 100%, and the float that was your engine becomes your anchor.
