> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The ICB "Personal and Household Goods" group on VN-Index is a label that hides two opposite businesses with opposite economics: branded domestic durables (plastic-pipe makers like BMP and NTP, branded home and consumer products) that own real *pricing power* and earn fat, stable margins; and export garments and textiles (TNG, MSH, TCM, STK) that are *price-taking*, FX-exposed, thin-margin order-takers riding US and EU consumer demand.
>
> - Pricing power is the whole game. A branded durable can raise price without losing volume, so a price increase drops almost straight to margin; a garment cut-make-trim factory takes the price the foreign buyer dictates, so it competes on cost and volume and earns a razor-thin spread.
> - The two halves sit at opposite ends of the margin map: a branded pipe maker can run an **18%** net margin that barely moves across the cycle, while a garment exporter runs a **3-7%** margin that swings with the order book and the dong.
> - The export-garment model is USD revenue against VND costs: a factory takes a dollar order, makes it in a dong-cost plant and ships it, so a weaker dong is a tailwind and a US demand slump is a body blow — Vietnam's textile and garment exports fell from about **\$44bn in 2022** to about **\$40bn in 2023** before recovering, and a fixed-cost factory's profit collapses far faster than its revenue.
> - The number to remember: pricing power, not size or fame, separates a margin compounder you can hold for the dividend from a cyclical order-taker you trade on the order cycle — own the first, trade the second, and never confuse a famous brand for a pricing-power moat.

In the same calendar quarter in 2023, two companies inside the same VN-Index sector group reported results that looked like they came from different planets. One was a maker of branded plastic pipes — the unglamorous white and grey tubes that run water through every new apartment and house in Vietnam. Its revenue barely moved, its net margin sat near the high-teens it had earned for years, and it declared yet another large cash dividend, as it had done almost every year for a decade. The other was a garment exporter — a factory floor of sewing machines stitching shirts and jackets for American and European brands. Its revenue had fallen sharply, its already-thin margin had been squeezed to almost nothing, its workers were on reduced shifts because the order book had thinned, and there was no dividend story to speak of, only the hope that US retailers would start re-ordering.

Both companies are filed under the same heading. The Industry Classification Benchmark — the standard taxonomy that index providers use to slot every listed company into a sector — puts both of them in a group called **Personal and Household Goods**. On paper they are the same sector. In reality they are mirror images: one is a *branded durable* with a moat and pricing power, the other is a *contract manufacturer* with no brand, no pricing power, and an income statement that lives or dies on whether shoppers in Texas and Germany are feeling flush. If you buy "the Personal and Household Goods sector" without knowing which of these two animals you are buying, you have not made an investment decision — you have made a coin flip.

This post is about telling them apart. We will build the single most important idea in consumer investing — **pricing power**, the ability to raise your price without losing your customers — from zero, and learn how to spot it. Then we will dissect the two halves of this sector in detail: the branded-durable model, where a brand and a distribution network create a moat that defends a fat, stable margin; and the export-garment model, where a factory takes dollar orders, makes them with dong-cost labour, and competes on price in a global race to the bottom. We will work through the FX mechanics that make a weaker dong a tailwind for exporters, the cut-make-trim margin math that explains why garment profit is so thin, and the order-book cyclicality that makes it so violent. And we will end, as this series always does, with a concrete playbook: own the pricing power, trade the garments on the order cycle, and know exactly what invalidates each view.

![Two column matrix contrasting branded durables with pricing power fat margin and dividends against export garments that are price takers with thin margin and FX exposure](/imgs/blogs/personal-household-goods-sector-vietnam-brands-pricing-power-1.png)

A quick word on money before we begin, because we will use it throughout. Vietnam trades in dong (VND), and the sector's primary numbers — product prices, factory revenues, dividends, market caps — are quoted in dong. As a reference for readers who think in dollars, the exchange rate in mid-2026 is roughly **\$1 = 25,400 VND**. So a 60,000 dong pack of pipes is about **\$2.40**; a company with a 25 trillion dong market cap is about a **\$980mn** company; a foreign net buy of 150 billion dong is about **\$5.9mn**. The export half of this sector is special because its *revenue* is literally priced in dollars — a garment factory signs contracts in USD and gets paid in USD, while it pays its workers in dong — so for the exporters, dollars are not just a convenience unit, they are the actual currency the business runs on. We will lean on that fact hard when we get to FX.

## Foundations: what pricing power means and how to spot it

Forget the stocks for a moment and run a simple thought experiment about any business you can think of. Imagine the business raises its price by 10% tomorrow morning. What happens to the number of units it sells? The answer to that one question is the difference between a great business and a mediocre one, and it has a name: **pricing power**.

Pricing power is the ability to raise your selling price without losing your customers. A business with strong pricing power can push its price up — to pass through higher input costs, or simply to earn more — and its customers grumble but keep buying, because they have no good alternative, or because the product is a small part of their budget, or because they trust the brand and switching feels risky. A business with weak or no pricing power cannot do this: the moment it lifts its price above what a rival charges for the same thing, customers walk, because the product is a *commodity* — interchangeable with the next supplier's version — and the buyer's only loyalty is to the lowest price.

This single property cascades into everything that matters about a company's economics. Think about what happens to profit. A company sells something for a price; it costs the company something to make; the gap between the two, as a share of the price, is the **margin**. If a company has pricing power, it can keep its price comfortably above its cost, and when costs rise it simply raises the price to match, so its margin stays fat and *stable*. If a company has no pricing power, its price is pinned down by competition to just a hair above cost — because if it charged more, a rival would undercut it — so its margin is thin, and when costs rise the company often cannot pass them through fast enough, so the margin gets *squeezed*. Pricing power is, in effect, the thing that protects a margin from competition.

Where does pricing power come from? It comes from a **moat** — some durable barrier that stops competitors from offering the customer an identical product at a lower price. In consumer businesses, the moat usually comes from one or more of a few sources. There is the **brand**: years of advertising, consistent quality, and familiarity have made customers trust a name and willing to pay a premium for it over an unbranded equivalent. There is the **distribution network**: the company's products are physically present in every shop, every hardware store, every corner of the country, so it is simply the most *available* option, and a new entrant cannot match that reach without spending years and a fortune building it. There is **switching cost or specification lock-in**: a builder who has always used one brand of pipe, whose plumbers know its fittings, whose blueprints specify it, will not casually switch to save a few percent. And there is **trust in a category where failure is expensive**: nobody wants the cheap pipe that bursts inside a wall, so for a product where the downside of a failure is large relative to its price, customers pay up for the reliable brand.

Now look at the other side. A business has *no* pricing power when its product is a commodity made to someone else's specification, when the customer is a large sophisticated buyer who can switch suppliers in a heartbeat, and when there are dozens of interchangeable suppliers competing for the same order. That is the world of **contract manufacturing**: you do not sell a product to consumers under your own name; you make a product to a customer's exact specification, under the customer's brand, and you compete for the contract against every other factory that can do the same job. In that world, the customer holds all the cards — they dictate the price, the quality standard, the delivery date — and you, the manufacturer, are a *price-taker*: you accept the price the market sets, because if you ask for more, the buyer simply takes the order to a cheaper factory in Bangladesh or Cambodia.

So how do you *spot* pricing power from the outside, before you have bought the stock? Three tells, and you can read all three off the financial statements and the business description. The first is the **margin level and its stability**: a business with pricing power earns a fat margin (well into double digits for a consumer product) that *barely moves* across good years and bad — a flat, high margin line through a recession is the fingerprint of a moat. The second is **the ability to pass through input costs**: watch what happens to the margin when a key raw material (resin, fabric, oil) spikes — a pricing-power business holds its margin because it raises price to match, while a price-taker's margin caves because it cannot. The third is **who the customers are**: many small, fragmented customers (households, plumbers, builders) who each buy a little give the seller leverage, while a few large, sophisticated buyers (global retailers, sourcing agents) who each buy a lot hold the leverage themselves. Fat-and-stable margin, cost pass-through, fragmented customers — three checks, and they sort this entire sector cleanly into its two halves.

This is the fundamental split inside the Personal and Household Goods group on VN-Index, and it is worth stating as bluntly as possible. One half of the sector — the branded domestic durables — has pricing power, because it sells *its own brand* to *fragmented customers* through a *distribution moat* in a category where *reliability matters*. The other half — the export garment and textile makers — has essentially *no* pricing power, because it does *contract manufacturing* of *commodity garments* for *large foreign buyers* who can switch factories at will. Same sector label. Opposite economics. The entire rest of this post is about understanding each half on its own terms and never mixing them up.

#### Worked example: an 8% price rise with flat volume versus a price you cannot move

Make pricing power concrete in dong, because it is the engine of the whole sector split. Take a branded pipe maker that sells **100 billion dong** of pipes in a quarter, about **\$3.9mn**, at a net margin of 18% — so it keeps **18 billion dong** of profit. Now suppose input resin costs creep up, and the company, because it has pricing power, raises its selling price by **8%** across its range. Customers grumble but keep buying — volume is flat — because the brand is trusted, the pipes are specified in the blueprints, and 8% on a pipe that is a tiny fraction of a building's cost is not worth switching supplier over.

What happens to profit? Revenue rises 8% to **108 billion dong**. The *cost* of making those pipes did rise too — that is why the company raised price — but a price increase with flat volume adds revenue without adding much cost beyond the resin, so a large share of that extra **8 billion dong** of revenue drops to the bottom line. Even if half of it is eaten by the higher resin cost, the company keeps roughly **4 billion dong** of extra profit on the same volume of pipes. That is the magic of pricing power: a price increase is almost pure margin, because you are not selling more units (which would cost more to make), you are simply charging more for the same units.

Now run the same scenario for a garment cut-make-trim factory. It sells **100 billion dong** of shirts in a quarter at a 5% margin — **5 billion dong** of profit. Its resin-equivalent — fabric, labour, thread — also creeps up. Can it raise its price 8% to compensate? No. The price is set by the foreign buyer in the contract that was signed months ago, and at the next negotiation the buyer will benchmark the factory against rivals in three other countries. If our factory asks for 8% more, the buyer moves the order. So the factory *eats* the cost increase: its margin gets squeezed from 5% toward 3%, and its **5 billion dong** of profit falls toward **3 billion**. The intuition: pricing power decides who absorbs a cost increase — the company with it passes the cost to the customer and protects its margin; the company without it eats the cost and watches its thin margin get thinner.

The figure above is that contrast in one picture. On the left, the branded pipe maker raises its price 8%, keeps its volume, and watches the increase fall to a fat, defended margin — because nobody walks away over a trusted, specified product. On the right, the garment maker faces the same cost pressure but cannot move its price; the buyer dictates it, so the factory eats the cost and its already-thin margin gets thinner. **Same cost shock, opposite outcomes** — and the only thing that differs between the two columns is whether the company owns pricing power. Every other difference in this sector flows from that one.

## The branded-durable model: a moat that defends a fat margin

Let us build out the first half of the sector — the one with pricing power. The cleanest, most concrete example on the Vietnamese market is the plastic-pipe industry, dominated by two listed names: **BMP** (Binh Minh Plastics, the southern market leader) and **NTP** (Tien Phong Plastics / Nhua Tien Phong, the northern leader). These companies make the PVC and HDPE pipes and fittings that go into every building, every water-supply project, every drainage system in the country. It sounds like the dullest possible business — and that dullness is exactly why it is such a good one.

Here is why a pipe maker has pricing power despite selling something as basic as a plastic tube. Start with the **brand and the trust premium**. A pipe is buried inside a wall or under a floor, and if it fails — if it cracks, leaks, or bursts — the cost of fixing it is enormous: you have to tear open the wall, find the leak, replace the pipe, and repair everything around it. The pipe itself might cost a tiny fraction of that repair bill. So a builder, a plumber, or a homeowner is extremely reluctant to save a few percent by buying an unknown cheap pipe that might fail. They pay up for BMP or NTP because those brands have decades of reliability behind them, and the *downside of a failure is huge relative to the price of the pipe*. That asymmetry — small saving, large potential loss — is the foundation of the trust premium.

Now layer on **distribution**. BMP and NTP have spent decades building dense networks of distributors, dealers, and hardware stores across their regions. When a plumber in the Mekong Delta needs pipes today, BMP's are the ones physically on the shelf at the local store; when a contractor in Hai Phong needs fittings, NTP's are the ones available. A new entrant cannot replicate that reach cheaply — you would have to sign up thousands of dealers, hold inventory across the country, and earn their trust, all while a well-capitalised incumbent defends its turf. Availability *is* a moat: the most available product wins a huge share of purchases simply by being there when the buyer needs it, no decision required.

Then add **specification and habit lock-in**. Large construction projects write their bills of materials around specific brands; engineers specify "BMP pipe" or an equivalent, and the fittings of one brand are sized and threaded to work together, so a contractor who mixes brands risks compatibility headaches. Plumbers learn one brand's system and stick with it. This is a switching cost: even a slightly cheaper rival has to overcome the inertia of "we have always used this and it works".

Put brand, distribution, and lock-in together and you get a business that can raise prices to pass through higher input costs — the main input is **PVC resin**, a petrochemical whose price moves with oil — and largely make those increases stick. The result is the economic signature of a pricing-power business: a **fat, stable margin**. BMP has, in good years, earned net margins in the high teens — around 18% — and even in tougher years stayed comfortably in double digits. That is extraordinary for something as commoditised-sounding as a plastic tube, and it is entirely a function of the moat. Because the business is mature, capital-light, and cash-generative — you build a pipe extrusion line once and it runs for years — the company throws off cash it does not need to reinvest, and so, like many pricing-power businesses, it pays a **large, consistent dividend**. BMP in particular has a long history of paying out most of its profit, which makes it a favourite of income-oriented and foreign investors who want a steady-compounding, dividend-paying consumer durable rather than a volatile cyclical.

![Brand moat loop showing strong brand and distribution leading to pricing power then fat margin then reinvestment in the brand and a deeper moat](/imgs/blogs/personal-household-goods-sector-vietnam-brands-pricing-power-7.png)

The figure above is the branded-durable flywheel. A strong brand and a wide distribution network create **pricing power**; pricing power produces a **fat, stable margin**; that margin generates cash the company can **reinvest in the brand and distribution** (advertising, dealer incentives, product quality), which deepens the moat further; and a deeper moat protects the pricing power for the next cycle. It is a self-reinforcing loop, and it is why a good branded durable tends to *compound* — the moat gets wider over time rather than narrower. Notice that the loop has nothing to do with selling *more* units in any given year; the volume can grow slowly with the construction cycle, but the *margin* is the engine, and the margin is protected by the moat. This is what a margin compounder looks like.

It is worth being precise about what the branded-durable business is *not*, because the same logic that makes it stable also caps its excitement. It is not a high-growth story. A pipe maker's volume grows roughly with construction activity and the property cycle — when new housing and infrastructure are being built, more pipe gets used; when construction slows, pipe demand softens. So the *cyclical* exposure of a branded durable is to the domestic construction and property cycle, not to global trade. But the key point is that even when volume wobbles with construction, the *margin* stays fat because the pricing power is intact. The branded durable is a steady, dividend-paying, margin-stable business whose main risk is a prolonged domestic construction slump or a resin-cost spike it cannot fully pass through — not a buyer in another country cancelling an order. Hold that distinction; it is the entire contrast with the other half of the sector.

#### Worked example: the trust premium on a buried pipe

Quantify why a buyer pays up for the branded pipe even though a cheaper one exists. Suppose a homeowner is plumbing a new house and needs **5 million dong** of pipe, about **\$197**. An unbranded import is 15% cheaper — it would save **750,000 dong**, about **\$30**. Tempting. But now price the downside: if a cheap pipe fails inside a wall in two years, the repair — breaking open the wall, finding the leak, replacing the pipe, re-tiling and repainting — easily runs to **20 million dong**, about **\$790**, plus the misery of water damage.

So the buyer is weighing a *certain* saving of 750,000 dong against a *possible* loss of 20 million dong. Even if the cheap pipe only fails 1 in 10 times, the expected cost of that failure is 2 million dong — far more than the 750,000 saved. A rational buyer pays the premium for the trusted brand, and an irrational, risk-averse one pays it even more readily. The intuition: pricing power in this category is manufactured by the *asymmetry* between a small price saving and a large failure cost — which is precisely why a cheap competitor cannot win on price alone, and why BMP and NTP can defend a fat margin selling something as basic as a tube.

## The export-garment model: cut-make-trim, FX and the order book

Now cross to the other half of the sector — the one with no pricing power. Here the listed names are garment and textile exporters: **TNG** (TNG Investment and Trading, a large garment maker in Thai Nguyen), **MSH** (Song Hong Garment), **TCM** (Thanh Cong Textile and Garment, one of the few vertically integrated players that spins yarn and weaves fabric as well as sewing), and **STK** (Century Synthetic Fibre, a yarn maker). These companies are the visible face of one of Vietnam's biggest export industries: textiles and garments, which bring in tens of billions of dollars a year and employ a huge share of the country's industrial workforce. But the business they run is the economic opposite of a pipe maker's.

Start with what they actually do, because the term hides the economics. The dominant model in Vietnamese garment export is **CMT — cut, make, trim**. In a CMT arrangement, the foreign buyer (a brand like a big US or European retailer, or its sourcing agent) provides the *design* and often even specifies or supplies the *fabric*; the Vietnamese factory's job is purely the manufacturing labour — to **cut** the fabric to the pattern, **make** (sew) the garment, and **trim** it (buttons, zips, finishing) — and then ship the finished garments back. The factory is selling *labour and conversion*, not a product of its own. It does not own the design, the brand, or usually even the fabric. It is, in the most literal sense, a contract sewing operation.

This is why a CMT garment maker has no pricing power. It sells an interchangeable service — sewing — into a global market where dozens of countries (Bangladesh, Cambodia, Myanmar, India, Indonesia) and thousands of factories compete to offer the cheapest reliable conversion. The buyer holds all the leverage: it sets the price, the quality standard, the delivery deadline, and it can move its orders to another country next season if Vietnamese labour gets too expensive or another country offers a better deal. The factory is a pure **price-taker**. Its only levers are *cost* (run the factory efficiently, keep labour productive) and *volume* (win more orders, keep the lines full). It cannot raise its price; it can only try to lower its cost faster than its rivals.

A step up from CMT is **FOB — free on board**, where the factory takes on more of the value chain: it sources the fabric and trims itself (rather than receiving them from the buyer), manages more of the production process, and sells the buyer a more finished product at a higher price. FOB earns a slightly fatter margin than CMT because the factory adds more value and takes on more risk (it now carries fabric inventory and sourcing risk). The most advanced players go further still toward **ODM** — original design manufacturing — where the factory even contributes design. The further up this ladder a company climbs (CMT → FOB → ODM), the more value it captures and the better its margin. But — and this is the crucial point — even an FOB or ODM garment maker is *still a manufacturer selling to a brand*, still largely a price-taker, still competing on cost against other countries. Climbing the ladder lifts the margin from razor-thin toward merely thin; it does not create the kind of pricing power a branded consumer product enjoys. The brand value, and the fat margin, lives with the Western retailer whose label goes on the shirt — not with the factory that sewed it.

The economic signature of this model is the exact inverse of the pipe maker's: a **thin, volatile margin**. CMT margins are often in the low single digits — 3% to 6% net is typical — because the factory's selling price is competed down to just above its cost. FOB nudges that up toward the high single digits. Either way, the absolute level is thin, which means two things follow. First, the business is intensely **volume-driven**: with a thin margin, profit comes from running large volumes through the factory and covering the fixed costs (the building, the machines, the supervisory staff) many times over. Second, the business has high **operating leverage**: because so much of the cost is fixed, a swing in volume produces a much larger swing in profit. When the order book is full, the fixed costs are spread thin and the margin holds; when orders dry up, the same fixed costs eat the thin margin and profit can vanish or turn into a loss. This is what makes garment exporters cyclical order-takers rather than steady compounders.

![Pipeline of the export garment FX model from a USD order to a dong cost factory then shipment then USD revenue with a weaker dong helping and a demand slump hurting](/imgs/blogs/personal-household-goods-sector-vietnam-brands-pricing-power-3.png)

The figure above traces the export-garment model end to end. A foreign buyer places a **USD order** at a price the buyer dictates; the factory makes it in a plant whose costs — labour, electricity, rent — are paid in **dong**; the finished goods are **shipped** abroad; and the factory collects **USD revenue**. Two arrows out of that chain matter enormously: a **weaker dong helps** (we will do the math in a moment), and a **demand slump hurts** (when the buyer's end-market — US and EU shoppers — pulls back, the orders thin and the operating leverage works in reverse). Hold both of those, because they are the two macro forces that drive the entire export half of the sector, and neither has anything to do with a brand or a moat.

### The FX mechanics: USD revenue against VND costs

The single most distinctive feature of the export-garment model is its currency mismatch, and it is worth slowing down to feel it, because it is the opposite of what most people assume about a "Vietnamese" company. A garment exporter earns its **revenue in US dollars** — its contracts are priced and paid in USD — but pays most of its **costs in dong**: the factory workers' wages, the electricity, the rent, the local logistics are all dong expenses. This mismatch makes the company a structural beneficiary of a *weaker dong*.

Here is the logic, step by step. Suppose the factory ships a garment order worth a fixed **\$1mn** to a US buyer. When the company converts that dollar revenue back into dong to pay its workers and report its profit, the amount of dong it gets depends on the exchange rate. If the dong is at 24,000 to the dollar, the \$1mn order converts to 24 billion dong of revenue. If the dong weakens to 25,400 to the dollar, the *same* \$1mn order now converts to 25.4 billion dong — about **1.4 billion dong more revenue** on exactly the same physical order, with no change in costs (the dong wages did not rise). That extra dong revenue flows almost entirely to profit, because the costs were already fixed in dong. So a weaker dong is a direct, mechanical tailwind to a garment exporter's reported earnings.

This is why, when the State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) lets the dong depreciate — usually because the US dollar is strong globally or because Vietnam needs to stay export-competitive — the export-garment stocks often get a lift, even if their underlying order volume has not changed at all. It is a pure FX translation gain. The flip side, of course, is that a *strengthening* dong is a headwind: the same dollar order converts to fewer dong, squeezing the already-thin margin. And there is a second-order subtlety: some inputs (imported fabric, imported machinery) are also dollar-priced, which partly offsets the benefit for a less-integrated CMT factory; the more *local* a company's cost base (more dong, less imported input), the more cleanly it benefits from a weak dong. But the headline is clear and load-bearing: garment exporters are *long the dollar and short the dong*, and that FX exposure is a core part of their investment case — one that has nothing to do with the quality of their business and everything to do with the macro.

This connects the sector directly to Vietnam's monetary and FX backdrop, which we cover in the macro-dials post in this series. The dong's path against the dollar is set by the interplay of the SBV's policy, the US Federal Reserve's rate cycle, and Vietnam's trade balance — and whatever moves the dong moves the translated earnings of every garment exporter on the exchange. An investor in this sector is, whether they realise it or not, taking a view on the dong.

#### Worked example: a \$1mn order and a weakening dong

Put a number on the FX tailwind, because it is one of the two forces that drive the export half. A garment factory ships a **\$1mn** export order — the price is fixed in dollars in the contract. The factory's costs to make it — fabric, labour, overhead — are, say, **22 billion dong**, paid locally.

Scenario one: the dong is at **24,000** to the dollar. The \$1mn converts to **24 billion dong** of revenue. Subtract the 22 billion dong of cost, and the factory keeps **2 billion dong** of profit — about an 8% margin on the dong revenue.

Scenario two: nothing about the order changes, but the dong weakens to **25,400** to the dollar over the production period. Now the same \$1mn converts to **25.4 billion dong**. The costs are unchanged at 22 billion dong (the wages were always dong). So profit jumps to **3.4 billion dong** — up about **1.4 billion dong**, a 70% increase in profit, purely from the currency moving. The intuition: because revenue is in dollars and costs are in dong, a 6% dong depreciation drops a 1.4 billion dong gain almost straight to the bottom line — the FX move is a free profit lever the factory does not control, and it can swamp the operating performance in either direction.

### The order book: where the cyclicality lives

If FX is the first force, the **order book** is the second, and it is the one that makes garment exporters genuinely cyclical. An order book is exactly what it sounds like: the stack of confirmed orders the factory has from its buyers, stretching out over the coming months. A garment factory does not sell to walk-in customers; it produces against orders placed in advance by a handful of large foreign buyers. So the order book *is* the business's forward revenue, and its thickness is the single best leading indicator of the next two or three quarters of earnings.

The order book swings with one thing above all: **end-consumer demand in the US and Europe**. Vietnamese garment exporters sit at the far end of a long supply chain that begins with a shopper in Houston or Hamburg deciding whether to buy a new jacket. When US and EU consumers are confident and spending, retailers re-stock aggressively, place big orders, and the factories' order books fill up. When those consumers pull back — in a recession, an inflation squeeze, or simply when retailers are stuck with too much unsold inventory from last season — retailers slash their orders, and the factories' order books thin out. This is **inventory destocking**, and it transmits a consumer slowdown thousands of miles upstream to a Vietnamese factory floor with a brutal lag and amplification.

The 2022-2023 episode is the textbook case, and we will return to it in detail in the VN-Index section. In short: after the post-COVID spending boom of 2021, US and EU retailers had over-ordered and were sitting on bloated inventories. Through 2022 and into 2023 they stopped placing new orders while they worked down those inventories, and Vietnamese garment exporters saw their order books collapse. Revenue fell, factories ran below capacity, and because of operating leverage, *profit fell far faster than revenue*. It was not that Vietnamese factories did anything wrong; it was that their customers' customers had stopped buying, and the order-book channel transmitted that all the way back to Thai Nguyen and Nam Dinh.

![Vietnam textile and garment exports by year line chart from 2019 to 2025 showing a 2022 peak near 44 billion dollars a 2023 dip and a recovery](/imgs/blogs/personal-household-goods-sector-vietnam-brands-pricing-power-2.png)

The chart above is the export driver in one line. Vietnam's textile and garment exports climbed from about **\$39bn in 2019**, dipped in the 2020 pandemic year to about **\$35bn**, recovered to **\$39bn in 2021**, then surged to roughly **\$44bn in 2022** on the post-COVID boom — before the destocking cycle dragged them down to about **\$40bn in 2023**, after which demand recovered toward **\$44bn in 2024** and about **\$47bn in 2025**. That 2023 dip is the order-book cyclicality made visible: a roughly 10% fall in national export value, which — because of operating leverage at the factory level — translated into far larger falls in the profits of individual exporters. When you look at this chart, you are looking at the demand pulse of American and European shoppers, transmitted into the revenue of the export half of the sector.

#### Worked example: cut-make-trim margin on a single shirt

Take the margin all the way down to one garment to feel how thin it is. A factory sews a shirt and sells it FOB to a US buyer for **\$5.00**. What does it cost to make? The fabric is roughly **\$2.80**, the labour to cut and sew is about **\$1.20**, and overhead — electricity, rent, supervision, depreciation on the machines, thread and trims — is about **\$0.65**. Add those up: **\$4.65** of cost.

So the factory keeps **\$0.35** on a \$5.00 shirt — a **7% margin**. Thirty-five cents. And that is an FOB shirt where the factory sourced the fabric; on a pure CMT shirt where the buyer supplied the fabric, the factory's slice is even thinner relative to the order value. The intuition: at 35 cents a shirt, the only way to make real money is *volume* — sew millions of shirts and keep every line running at full capacity — and the only way to lose money is to have the lines sit idle while the fixed costs (rent, supervisors, machine depreciation) keep running. That razor-thin per-unit margin is exactly why the order book matters so much, and why a demand slump that idles the lines is so devastating.

#### Worked example: a 15% order drop and operating leverage

Show why thin margins plus fixed costs make garment profit collapse in a downturn. A factory does **500 billion dong** of revenue, about **\$19.7mn**, at a 6% net margin — **30 billion dong** of profit. Of its 470 billion dong of costs, suppose **300 billion dong** are *variable* (fabric, the labour for goods actually produced) and **170 billion dong** are *fixed* (rent, machine depreciation, supervisory and admin staff that you pay whether the lines run or not).

Now US orders fall **15%**, so revenue drops to **425 billion dong**. The variable costs fall roughly in proportion — 15% off 300 billion is a 45 billion saving, leaving **255 billion dong** of variable cost. But the fixed costs *do not fall*: still **170 billion dong**. Total cost is now 425 billion dong — exactly equal to revenue. Profit has gone from 30 billion dong to **zero**. A 15% drop in revenue wiped out 100% of the profit, because the fixed costs could not shrink with the order book. The intuition: this is operating leverage in reverse, and it is the core risk of any thin-margin contract manufacturer — the same fixed-cost base that magnifies profit when the lines are full magnifies losses when the orders disappear, which is why you trade garments on the order cycle and never treat them as a steady hold.

## Valuation: P/E for the brand, P/B and the order book for the garment

Because the two halves of the sector are such different businesses, you value them with different tools — and getting the tool wrong is one of the most common mistakes in this group. We cover valuation in depth in the sector-valuation post in this series, but here is how it specialises for these two animals.

For the **branded durable**, the right lens is **earnings-based — the P/E ratio** (price-to-earnings, the share price divided by earnings per share, telling you how many years of current earnings you are paying for). Why earnings? Because the business has a *stable, predictable margin* protected by its moat, its earnings are reliable and recurring, so the market is willing to pay a multiple of those earnings — and often a premium multiple, because the stability and the dividend are valuable. You also lean heavily on the **dividend yield**: for a cash cow like BMP that pays out most of its profit, the dividend is a large part of the total return, and a rising yield (because the price fell) can be a buy signal. The key analytical question for a branded durable is: *is the moat intact and the margin stable?* If yes, the earnings are durable and the P/E premium is justified; if the moat is eroding (a new competitor breaking the distribution lock, or a resin-cost spike the company cannot pass through), the margin and the P/E should both compress.

For the **export garment maker**, the earnings are too volatile and too cyclical for a simple P/E to mean much — a P/E calculated on peak-cycle earnings will look deceptively cheap right before the order book rolls over, and a P/E on trough earnings will look absurdly expensive right at the bottom. So you lean on **P/B — price-to-book** (the share price relative to the company's net asset value per share), which anchors to the factory's tangible assets rather than its swinging earnings, and you read it together with the **order book**. The analytical questions for a garment exporter are: *how full is the order book for the next two or three quarters* (the forward-revenue read), *which way is the dong moving* (the FX tailwind or headwind), and *where are we in the US/EU consumer cycle* (destocking or restocking)? You buy a garment exporter cheap on P/B when the order book is bottoming and the consumer cycle is about to turn up — not when its trailing P/E looks low at the top of the cycle.

The deeper point is that the two valuation approaches reflect the two business models. You pay a *premium earnings multiple* for pricing power because pricing power makes earnings durable. You pay a *discount asset multiple* for a price-taker because its earnings are not durable — they are a leveraged bet on someone else's demand cycle. If you find yourself applying a fat, stable-business P/E to a garment exporter because last year's profit was high, you have made the sector's signature error: you have priced a cyclical order-taker as if it were a margin compounder.

## Common misconceptions

A few beliefs about this sector are widespread, intuitive, and wrong. Each one comes from collapsing the two halves into one, or from confusing a famous name with a strong business. Correcting them is most of the edge.

**Misconception 1: "All consumer companies are defensive."** The intuition is that people always need clothes and household goods, so consumer companies must be stable. But "consumer" is not one thing. A branded domestic *staple or durable* with pricing power can be genuinely defensive — its margin holds through the cycle. But an export garment maker is the *opposite* of defensive: it is a leveraged, cyclical bet on US and EU consumer spending, with thin margins and high operating leverage. Calling a garment exporter "defensive" because it makes clothes is a category error. The 2022-2023 episode proved it: garment exporters' profits fell far harder than the broad market, while a branded pipe maker's margin barely moved. Defensiveness comes from *pricing power*, not from the product category — and the two halves of this sector sit on opposite sides of that line.

**Misconception 2: "Garment exports are a structural growth play."** Vietnam's garment exports have grown over decades, and the headline numbers are large and rising, so it is tempting to treat the exporters as a long-term growth story. But the *national export total* growing does not mean the *listed factories' profits* grow durably, for two reasons. First, the growth is volume growth at a thin, price-taken margin — more shirts at 35 cents each — not margin expansion, so profit grows slowly and swings violently around the order cycle. Second, Vietnam's cost advantage is not permanent: as Vietnamese wages rise, the lowest-margin work migrates to cheaper countries (Bangladesh, Cambodia, Myanmar), exactly as it once migrated *to* Vietnam from China. The structural force can run the other way. The right frame is not "buy and hold the structural growth" but "trade the cyclical order-taker around its order book and the dong" — and own the value-chain *upgraders* (the firms climbing from CMT toward FOB and ODM, capturing more value per garment) rather than the bottom-rung CMT factories most exposed to the next low-cost country.

**Misconception 3: "A famous brand means pricing power."** People equate brand recognition with a moat. But fame is not the same as pricing power. A garment factory might make shirts for a globally famous *retailer's* brand — the shirt has a famous label on it — yet the factory itself has no pricing power at all, because *the brand belongs to the buyer, not the factory*. The factory is just the anonymous contract sewer; all the brand value and the fat margin sit with the Western retailer. Conversely, an utterly un-famous, dull plastic-pipe maker that most Vietnamese consumers could not name has *enormous* pricing power in its niche, because of the distribution moat and the trust premium on a buried pipe. The lesson: pricing power lives where the *moat* is — brand trust you own, distribution you control, switching costs you create — not where the famous logo is. Always ask *whose* brand it is and *who* captures the margin.

**Misconception 4: "FX is just noise; focus on the business."** For most companies FX is a minor translation effect, so investors learn to ignore it. But for a garment exporter, the FX exposure is *structural and load-bearing*, not noise: revenue is literally in dollars and costs in dong, so the dong's path can swing reported profit by tens of percent in a year — as the worked example showed, a 6% dong move flipped factory profit by 70%. Ignoring the dong when you analyse a garment exporter is like ignoring the oil price when you analyse an airline. You do not have to predict the dong, but you must know that you are taking a dong view when you own the sector, and size the position accordingly.

## How it shows up on VN-Index

Now watch the two models play out in real episodes on the Vietnamese market, with dates and numbers. This is where the abstractions become trades.

**BMP's dividend compounding — the branded durable in action.** Over the 2010s and into the 2020s, BMP did something quietly remarkable: it kept its net margin in the high teens through construction booms and busts, generated cash it did not need to reinvest, and paid most of that cash out as dividends year after year. For a foreign or income-oriented investor, BMP became a textbook *steady compounder* — you collected a fat, reliable dividend yield (often in the high single digits relative to the share price), and you owned a business whose margin the property cycle could dent at the volume line but never broke at the margin line, because the pricing power was intact. When the Thai building-materials group SCG took a controlling stake in BMP, it was buying exactly this: a pricing-power cash cow in a growing market. The lesson for the sector reader is that the branded-durable half rewards *patience and the dividend*, not trading — its return comes from compounding a stable, well-defended margin, not from catching a cycle.

**The 2022-2023 garment order slump — the price-taker in action.** The contrast could not be sharper. After the 2021 post-COVID spending boom, US and EU retailers had over-ordered and entered 2022 with bloated inventories. Through 2022 and into the first half of 2023, they slashed new orders to work those inventories down. Vietnam's national textile and garment exports fell from roughly **\$44bn in 2022** to about **\$40bn in 2023** — and at the individual-factory level, the hit to *profit* was far larger because of operating leverage. Listed exporters reported revenue down double digits and profit down far more; some saw quarterly profit fall by half or worse, factories ran below capacity, and workers faced reduced hours. None of this was a failure of the Vietnamese factories — it was the order-book channel transmitting a US and EU consumer pullback upstream. A reader who understood the model could have seen it coming by watching US retail inventory data and the exporters' own order-book commentary, and could have avoided the value trap of a "cheap" trailing P/E on 2021's peak earnings. Then, as the destocking ended and orders recovered into 2024 and 2025 (national exports back toward **\$44bn** then **\$47bn**), the same operating leverage worked in reverse and exporter profits rebounded — the cyclical's round trip.

![Grouped bar chart of approximate net profit margins showing branded durables near eighteen percent versus garment cut make trim near five percent and FOB near seven percent](/imgs/blogs/personal-household-goods-sector-vietnam-brands-pricing-power-5.png)

The chart above is the margin gap that the two episodes above produce, laid side by side. A branded durable (BMP-type) runs around an **18%** net margin; a diversified plastics maker without the same brand strength runs nearer **10%**; a garment CMT factory runs around **5%**; an FOB garment maker around **7%**. That descending staircase *is* the pricing-power gradient — the more of the value chain and brand a company owns, the fatter and more defended its margin; the more it is a pure contract sewer, the thinner and more exposed. When you look at a name in this sector, your first question should be: where does it sit on this staircase? That placement tells you almost everything about how to own it.

![Line chart comparing a branded durable steady net margin against a garment maker swinging net margin from 2019 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/personal-household-goods-sector-vietnam-brands-pricing-power-6.png)

And the chart above is the same idea through time, which is even more telling than the level. The branded durable's net margin (the steady line) sits around 16-18% and barely moves from 2019 to 2024 — through COVID, through the rate spike, through the construction wobble, the margin holds, because the pricing power holds. The garment maker's net margin (the swinging line) lurches from 7% in 2019 down to 5% in 2020, up to 8% in 2021's boom, then collapses to 3% in the 2023 destocking trough before recovering to 5% in 2024. Same sector group, two completely different risk profiles: one is a flat, reliable line you hold for the compounding; the other is a sawtooth you trade around its cycle. If you only take one image from this post, take this one — it is the entire thesis in two lines.

**The FX swings.** Layered on top of the order cycle, the dong's moves added their own pulse to exporter earnings. In periods when the dollar was strong globally and the SBV allowed the dong to depreciate, exporters got a translation tailwind that flattered their reported profit even when volumes were soft; in periods of dong strength, the same translation became a drag. An investor watching only the order book without the dong, or only the dong without the order book, would have been repeatedly surprised. The two forces — the order cycle and the FX rate — interact, and reading the export half of the sector means holding both at once.

## The playbook: how to trade it

Everything above resolves into a simple, two-track playbook, because the sector is two businesses and you treat them differently.

**Track one: own the pricing power, for the compounding and the dividend.** For the branded-durable half — BMP, NTP, and any genuine branded-domestic name with a distribution moat — the strategy is *ownership, not trading*. You want to hold the moat and collect the compounding margin and the dividend through the cycle. The right entry is on *weakness driven by the volume cycle, not the margin* — a domestic construction slowdown that dents pipe volumes and drags the share price down, while the *margin and the moat stay intact*, is a gift, because you are buying a permanently good business at a cyclically depressed price and getting paid a rising dividend yield to wait. Size it as a core, long-term holding. The signal to *watch* is the margin and the dividend, not the quarter's volume: as long as the net margin stays fat and the payout continues, the thesis is intact and a volume dip is noise.

**Track two: trade the garments on the order cycle and the dong.** For the export-garment half — TNG, MSH, TCM, STK — the strategy is *cyclical trading around the order book and the currency*. You want to *buy* the exporters when three things line up: the order book is bottoming (watch the companies' own order-visibility commentary and US/EU retail inventory data — when retailers have finished destocking, re-orders come fast), the US/EU consumer cycle is turning up (rate cuts, easing inflation, improving consumer confidence in the export markets), and the dong is weak or weakening (the FX tailwind). That combination — empty order books about to refill, consumers about to spend, a weak dong translating dollar orders into more dong — is when operating leverage works hugely in your favour off a low base. You *sell or avoid* the exporters at the opposite configuration: a full order book at the top of the consumer cycle (the destock is coming), a strengthening dong, and a trailing P/E that looks deceptively cheap on peak earnings. Size these as *tactical, cyclical* positions, not core holds, because the same operating leverage that makes the upside large makes the downside brutal.

**The invalidation — for each track.** For the *branded durable*, the thesis is invalidated not by a weak volume quarter but by **margin erosion**: if the net margin starts a sustained slide — because a new competitor is breaking the distribution lock, because a resin-cost spike is one the company genuinely cannot pass through, or because the dividend gets cut — then the moat is weakening and the whole reason to own it (the durable, defended margin) is gone; exit, regardless of how "cheap" the P/E looks. For the *export garment trade*, the thesis is invalidated when the **order-book-plus-cycle setup breaks**: if you bought for a recovery and the order book fails to refill (US/EU consumers keep retrenching, retailers keep destocking) or the dong reverses and strengthens hard against you, the operating leverage you were renting turns against you fast — cut the position, because a thin-margin cyclical going the wrong way does not give you time. The discipline that ties both tracks together is the one idea this whole post has been building: *know which animal you own*. Own the pricing power for the margin and the dividend; rent the price-taker for the cycle; and never, ever confuse the two.
