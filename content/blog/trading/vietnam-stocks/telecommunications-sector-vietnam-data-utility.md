## A "boring" stock that quietly tripled its profit

In early 2024, if you scanned the Vietnamese market for the names that had quietly compounded the hardest over the prior two years, one of the biggest surprises was not a hot broker or a property developer — it was a telecom. Viettel Global (ticker VGI on the UPCoM board), the overseas arm of the state-owned Viettel group, reported pretax profit of roughly 5.5 trillion dong for 2024, up about 41% on the year, after climbing from a small loss in 2021 to 1.6 trillion in 2022 and 3.88 trillion in 2023. The stock went from a sleepy line item that most retail investors ignored to one of the best-performing large caps on the market, multiplying several times over in price across 2023–24.

That is a strange thing for a *telecom* to do. Telecom is supposed to be the dictionary definition of a boring stock: a regulated-ish utility selling phone and data plans to a saturated market, throwing off a dividend, and otherwise going nowhere. In most developed markets that caricature is roughly accurate — a US or European mobile carrier is a low-growth, high-payout bond proxy. But VGI broke the caricature, and understanding *why* is the key to the whole Vietnamese telecom sector. The boring utility model is real and it is the floor under these stocks. The growth came from somewhere the model does not usually reach: frontier overseas markets in Africa and Asia where mobile and data are still penetrating, layered on top of the same recurring-subscriber engine that makes telecom defensive at home.

This post is about holding both ideas at once. Telecom is, at its core, a **data utility** — you build a network once, then sell access to it forever at near-zero marginal cost, and the cash flow is stable enough to pay a steady dividend through the cycle. That is the defensive thesis, and it is why telecom cushions a drawdown the way utilities and consumer staples do. But the Vietnamese listed names add a growth twist that a generic utility does not have: overseas expansion (VGI), the build-out of physical infrastructure (CTR), the migration from copper to fibre broadband (FOX), and the coming 5G capex cycle. The job of this post is to teach you the utility engine cold, then show you exactly where the growth kicker comes from and how to own the sector for yield *plus* that kicker.

![Telecom utility engine flow diagram build network then ARPU times subscribers to recurring revenue and dividends](/imgs/blogs/telecommunications-sector-vietnam-data-utility-1.png)

The figure above is the entire sector in one picture, and the next several thousand words unpack each box. Hold the shape in your head: a big up-front network capex, then a recurring revenue stream equal to ARPU times subscribers, flowing at near-zero marginal cost into steady cash and dividends. That recurring stream is the product. A broker sells you volatility and a steel mill sells you the commodity cycle; a telecom sells you the absence of both — a subscription that renews every month whether the index is up or down.

It is worth saying plainly what this post is *not* arguing, so the thesis is honest. It is not arguing that telecom is the best sector to own in a roaring bull market — over a liquidity-driven rally the brokers, steel and property names will run two or three times while a telecom plods along paying its dividend, and if maximum upside is your only goal you would underweight it. The argument is narrower: telecom is one of the most *reliable* cash engines on VN-Index, the part of a portfolio that keeps paying regardless of the cycle, and in Vietnam specifically it comes with a growth option attached that most defensive sectors lack. It is a tool for a specific job — income, defense, and a frontier-growth kicker — and the rest of this post is about understanding the tool well enough to know when to reach for it.

## Foundations: how a telecom makes money

Before any trading view, you need to understand how a telecom earns, because the *shape* of its earnings — not just the level — is what makes it a defensive data utility. We build this from zero.

### The everyday analogy: the toll road and the gym membership

A mobile network works much like a toll road that someone spent a fortune to build. Laying the road — the towers, the spectrum, the fibre backbone, the switching equipment — cost an enormous amount of money up front. But once the road exists, the cost of letting one more car drive on it is almost nothing. The owner makes money by collecting a toll from every car that uses the road, month after month, and the road keeps earning long after it is paid for. The whole game is: spend a mountain of capital once, then harvest small recurring payments from a huge number of users forever.

A fixed-broadband (fibre-to-the-home) business is even closer to a **gym membership**. The provider runs a fibre line to your apartment block once, then bills you a fixed amount every month for access. You barely think about it; the charge just recurs. Some months you stream all day, some months you are travelling and barely use it — the provider gets paid the same either way. That recurring, sticky, low-thought monthly bill is the most utility-like revenue in the whole technology world. It is why telecom belongs in the same mental bucket as power and water: a network in the ground, a captive subscriber base, a bill that renews on autopilot.

### The earnings identity, in one line

Strip away the accounting and a telecom's operating profit reduces to:

```
operating_profit  =  ARPU x subscribers          <- the recurring revenue base
                   - network_opex                 <- run the network, power, maintenance
                   - SG&A and customer_acquisition
                   - depreciation                 <- the network wearing out
                   - interest_on_debt
```

Three features of this identity drive everything that follows.

**First, the revenue is the product of two numbers: ARPU and subscribers.** ARPU stands for *average revenue per user* — the average monthly bill across the whole base. Multiply it by the number of subscribers and you have the recurring revenue. Grow either one and revenue grows; this is the lever every telecom pulls. In a saturated market subscribers barely move, so growth has to come from ARPU (selling more data, bundling fibre and TV, upselling 4G then 5G) or from new markets entirely (which is exactly what VGI does overseas).

**Second, most of the cost is fixed, and the marginal cost of a subscriber is near zero.** Running the network costs roughly the same whether you have nine million customers or ten million on it. The depreciation, the tower leases, the spectrum fees, the core network team — these are fixed. So when you add a subscriber, almost their entire ARPU drops straight to the operating line. This is **operating leverage**, and it is the single most important fact about telecom economics: once the network is built and paid for, incremental revenue is incredibly profitable.

**Third, the customer cannot easily leave.** A mobile or fibre subscriber faces switching friction — porting a number, re-wiring an apartment, breaking a bundle — so the base is sticky. The rate at which customers leave is called **churn**, and low churn is what makes the recurring revenue *durable*. A 1% monthly churn means you keep about 89% of your base over a year even if you sign up nobody new; a 3% monthly churn means you bleed roughly a third of your base a year and have to spend heavily on acquisition just to stand still. Churn is the leak in the bucket, and a well-run telecom keeps it low.

The churn number deserves a moment more, because it quietly drives the economics of the whole base. Think of the subscriber base as a bucket of water with a tap pouring in (new sign-ups, *gross adds*) and a hole in the bottom (churn). The level of the bucket — your net subscriber count — only rises when the tap runs faster than the hole drains. In a saturated market the tap is barely running (almost everyone who wants a phone already has one), so the *only* way to keep the bucket full is to shrink the hole: cut churn. This is why mature telecoms obsess over retention — bundles that make leaving painful (mobile plus fibre plus TV on one bill), loyalty pricing, family plans — far more than over acquisition. Every percentage point of churn you avoid is a subscriber you do not have to spend money re-acquiring, and in a saturated market the *cost to acquire a customer* (often a subsidised handset or a discounted first months) can take a year or more of ARPU just to pay back. Low churn is not a soft metric; it is the difference between a base that funds dividends and a base that funds a marketing department.

There is a second-order point hidden in churn that explains why fibre is structurally more defensive than mobile. A fibre line is *physically* wired into your home and usually shared across a household; switching means an engineer visit, possible downtime, and an argument with everyone who streams. Mobile is a SIM you can port in an afternoon. So fibre churn runs structurally *lower* than mobile churn, which is one reason FOX's recurring revenue is so reliable and one reason the mix-shift toward fibre makes the *whole* sector stickier over time, not just bigger.

> [!note]
> **Jargon, defined once.** *ARPU (average revenue per user)*: the average monthly revenue per subscriber. *Subscribers*: the active customer count (mobile SIMs or fibre lines). *Churn*: the monthly percentage of customers who leave. *Penetration*: subscribers as a share of the population (mobile penetration above 100% just means many people carry two SIMs). *Marginal cost*: the cost of serving one more subscriber once the network exists (near zero here). *Operating leverage*: the way fixed costs make incremental revenue very profitable. *Capex (capital expenditure)*: the up-front spend on towers, spectrum and fibre. *FTTH (fibre-to-the-home)*: a fixed fibre broadband line into a household. *Spectrum*: the licensed radio frequencies a mobile operator needs; bought from the state, a major cost.

### Why telecom is a *data* utility, not a *voice* one

A decade ago a telecom's ARPU was mostly voice and SMS — people paid per minute and per text. That world is dead. Messaging and calling migrated to over-the-top apps (Zalo, Messenger, WhatsApp) that ride for free on the data connection, and voice/SMS revenue collapsed. What replaced it is **data**: the monthly data plan, the mobile-internet bundle, the fibre line. Today a telecom is fundamentally selling *connectivity and bandwidth*, not minutes. This matters for the thesis because data demand is still growing fast even where subscriber counts are flat — people consume more gigabytes every year (video, social, cloud, AI), so a telecom can grow ARPU by selling bigger data buckets and faster speeds even into a saturated subscriber base. The utility is a *data* utility, and data volume is the one thing in this sector that reliably goes up.

This data-not-voice shift is also what makes the sector quietly *anti-fragile* to the very apps that seemed to threaten it. When Zalo and WhatsApp killed paid SMS, the lazy conclusion was that telecoms were being "disrupted" by the internet. The opposite happened. Every one of those apps runs on the telecom's data pipe; the more indispensable video calls and social feeds and cloud backups become, the more bandwidth they consume, and the telecom sells that bandwidth. The over-the-top apps did not bypass the telecom — they made the telecom's product (data) non-negotiable. A household will cut almost any discretionary spend in a downturn before it cuts the internet connection the children need for school and the parents need for work. That non-discretionary quality is exactly what makes a utility a utility: demand that does not flex much with the business cycle. Data is now closer to electricity and water in a Vietnamese household's hierarchy of bills than to a luxury.

A useful way to see the data tailwind concretely is to separate the two ways ARPU can move. **Volume ARPU** rises when a subscriber consumes more data and buys a bigger bucket to cover it — this is the structural, almost mechanical tailwind, because data consumption per user climbs every year as video resolution rises and new data-hungry apps arrive. **Mix ARPU** rises when the operator moves a subscriber up the value ladder — from a basic mobile plan to a mobile-plus-fibre bundle, from a 4G plan to a 5G one, from a consumer line to an enterprise contract with cloud and security attached. A well-run telecom grows both: it rides the volume tailwind it gets for free and engineers the mix shift it has to sell. In a saturated subscriber market, *all* the revenue growth has to come from these two ARPU levers, which is why management quality in telecom is mostly about ARPU and churn, not about chasing a subscriber count that cannot grow.

#### Worked example: the recurring revenue base, ARPU times subscribers

Take a large Vietnamese mobile operator with 50 million subscribers paying an average of 90,000 dong a month. The recurring revenue base is simply the product: 50,000,000 times 90,000 dong equals 4.5 trillion dong **per month** of mobile service revenue, or 54 trillion dong a year. At roughly \$1 = 25,400 VND, that monthly figure is about \$177mn a month, near \$2.1bn a year, from one product line. Notice what makes this powerful: it does not depend on anyone walking into a store today, on the index level, or on whether the economy is booming — it is 50 million bills that renew on autopilot. *That recurring base, ARPU times subscribers, is the floor under every telecom and the reason the cash flow is stable enough to pay a dividend through the cycle.*

### The penetration-then-ARPU lifecycle

Every telecom market moves through the same lifecycle, and knowing where a market sits tells you where the growth is. **Phase one is penetration**: a young market where most people do not yet have a phone, and the operator grows by signing up new subscribers — the subscriber count is the lever, and growth is fast and capacity-driven. **Phase two is saturation**: nearly everyone has a phone, subscriber growth flattens, and the only levers left are ARPU (sell more data and faster speeds to the same people) and cost discipline. **Phase three is consolidation and yield**: a mature, saturated market where two or three operators share a stable base, compete on quality rather than land-grab, and return cash to shareholders as dividends.

Vietnam's domestic mobile market is firmly in phase two — saturated, ARPU-driven, low subscriber growth. Its fixed-broadband market is still in late phase one to early phase two — fibre is still penetrating households, so the subscriber count is still genuinely growing. And the frontier overseas markets VGI operates in (parts of Africa, Laos, Cambodia, Myanmar, Haiti, Peru and others) are in *phase one* — low penetration, fast subscriber growth — which is precisely why VGI can grow like a young telecom while a developed-market carrier cannot. The same company can be a saturated utility at home and a fast-growing land-grab abroad. That split is the heart of the Vietnamese telecom story.

![Line chart mobile subscribers flat versus fixed broadband index rising 2019 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/telecommunications-sector-vietnam-data-utility-3.png)

The chart shows the split directly. The mobile-subscriber line is nearly flat — indexed to 100 in 2019, it sits around 105 by 2024, the signature of a saturated phase-two market. The fixed-broadband line, indexed the same way, climbs to roughly 165 over the same period — fibre is still penetrating Vietnamese households, so broadband is the part of the *domestic* business that genuinely grows the subscriber count, not just ARPU. When you own a domestic telecom, you are mostly owning a saturated mobile utility with a growing fibre business bolted on; the mix shift toward data and fibre is where the home-market growth lives.

## The marginal-cost magic: why the network is the moat

The single most important number in telecom is the one that is nearly zero: the marginal cost of a subscriber. Understanding it explains the entire profit profile of the sector — why the first years of building a network are painful and capital-hungry, why the later years are a cash gusher, and why an incumbent with a built-out network is so hard to dislodge.

When an operator enters a market, it must build the network *before* it has the subscribers to pay for it. Towers, spectrum licences, fibre backbone, transmission, switching, billing systems — billions of dollars of capex go in before a single bill is collected. The first subscriber on that network is, in effect, the most expensive subscriber in the world: they have to "pay" for the entire network. The operator runs losses for years while it scales the base up to cover the fixed costs. This is exactly what VGI's early overseas years looked like — heavy investment, accumulated losses, negative pretax profit as late as 2021 — the painful left side of the curve.

But once the network exists and is paid for, the picture inverts completely. The millionth subscriber, and the ten-millionth, costs almost nothing to add — the network is already there, with spare capacity engineered in. Their ARPU drops almost entirely to profit. The same fixed cost is now spread over a vast base, so unit economics get better with every subscriber. The business crosses from "bleeding capex" to "gushing cash," and the transition is sharp. This is operating leverage at its most extreme, and it is why telecom profits, once a market matures, can compound fast off a built network — which is exactly the inflection VGI hit in 2022–24.

![Before and after diagram first subscriber expensive on new network millionth subscriber nearly free](/imgs/blogs/telecommunications-sector-vietnam-data-utility-4.png)

The figure contrasts the two states. On the left, the early network: huge capex sunk, few subscribers, the cost of each one enormous, losses. On the right, the mature network: the same fixed cost now spread over millions, each new subscriber nearly free, ARPU dropping to the margin, cash gushing. The whole investment question in telecom is *which side of this curve is the company on* — and VGI is interesting precisely because different overseas markets sit on different parts of it, so the group as a whole is past the inflection and into the cash-gushing phase while individual frontier markets are still climbing.

#### Worked example: the operating leverage of one million new subscribers

Suppose a telecom has its network fully built and is already profitable, with fixed costs comfortably covered by its existing base. Now it adds one million net new subscribers at an ARPU of 90,000 dong a month. The incremental revenue is 1,000,000 times 90,000, or 90 billion dong a month — about 1.08 trillion dong a year, roughly \$42mn. Because the network already exists with spare capacity, the *incremental* cost to serve these subscribers is small — say only 20% of the revenue goes to extra opex — so roughly 80% of that 1.08 trillion, about 860 billion dong, flows straight to operating profit. Compare that with a manufacturer, where a million more units means a million more units of raw material and labour and the margin barely moves. *Once the network is built, adding subscribers is closer to printing margin than to making a product — that is the operating leverage that turns a mature telecom into a cash machine.*

### Spectrum and towers: the physical moat

Why can a built network not simply be copied by a well-funded rival? Two reasons make the moat physical and durable. **Spectrum is scarce and licensed** — the radio frequencies a mobile operator needs are auctioned by the state, there is a finite amount, and a new entrant cannot conjure more. **Towers and fibre are slow and expensive to build** — you cannot rent a national tower network into existence overnight; permits, land, and construction take years and enormous capital. An incumbent that already holds the spectrum and owns (or has long leases on) the towers and fibre sits behind a moat that money alone cannot quickly breach. This is why mature telecom markets settle into stable oligopolies of two or three players rather than fragmenting — and why the *infrastructure* layer (the towers themselves) is its own investable business, which is exactly where CTR comes in.

The moat has a financial signature worth recognising, because it shows up in the accounts as something that looks scary but is actually the source of the defensiveness: a lot of **depreciation**. A telecom's income statement carries a huge depreciation charge — the accounting recognition that the network is wearing out and will need replacing. This drags reported earnings (net profit) down and makes the P/E look high, which is one reason naive screens dismiss telecoms. But depreciation is a *non-cash* charge — no money left the building this quarter; it is the bookkeeping echo of capex spent years ago. The cash the business actually generates (EBITDA, and free cash flow after the *current* year's capex) is far larger than the reported net profit suggests. This gap between accounting earnings and cash generation is precisely why telecoms are valued on EV/EBITDA rather than P/E, and why a telecom that looks "expensive" on earnings can be a generous cash payer. The moat is built out of sunk capex; the dividend is paid out of the cash that capex no longer consumes.

A subtle competitive point follows from the moat: because the network is the barrier, the competitive battleground in a mature market is *quality and bundling*, not price warfare to the death. Three rational oligopolists who each own an irreplaceable network have far more to lose from a price war than two desperate start-ups in a fragmented market — a price war just destroys everyone's margin on an asset that cannot be un-built. So mature telecom markets tend toward disciplined competition: each player defends its base with better coverage, faster speeds, and stickier bundles rather than slashing ARPU to zero. This discipline is what keeps ARPU (and therefore the dividend) stable, and it is a structural reason telecom cash flows are more reliable than, say, airline or commodity cash flows, where the product is undifferentiated and price wars are endemic.

## The Viettel ecosystem: where the growth kicker lives

Here is where the Vietnamese telecom sector stops being a generic utility and becomes something more interesting. The listed names are not one undifferentiated "phone company" — they are different layers of a single ecosystem, each with a different growth profile, and you can choose which layer you want exposure to.

The anchor is **Viettel**, the state-owned military-run telecom group that dominates Vietnamese mobile. Viettel itself is not directly listed, but several pieces of its ecosystem are, and they are where the public-market opportunity sits. **Viettel Global (VGI)** is the overseas arm — Viettel's investments in mobile networks across Africa, Asia and Latin America, run under local brands. This is the growth engine: those markets are in the penetration phase, so VGI grows like a young telecom even as the domestic market saturates. **Viettel Construction (CTR)** builds and operates the *physical infrastructure* — towers, fibre, network deployment — and increasingly leases towers as a tower-company (towerco) model, plus does construction and technical services. As 5G rolls out and tower-sharing grows, CTR is a direct play on the capex cycle without owning the subscriber risk. Alongside the Viettel pieces, **FPT Telecom (FOX)** — part of the FPT group, not Viettel — is the listed fixed-broadband champion, growing fibre-to-the-home and data-centre/cloud services, the cleanest pure-play on the domestic data-utility growth.

Stack these together and you get something a single developed-market carrier cannot offer: a **growth-plus-yield stack**. The mature domestic mobile business is the utility floor (steady cash, dividends). FOX adds fibre-broadband growth at home. CTR adds an infrastructure/capex play. And VGI adds frontier-overseas growth on top. You are not forced to choose between a boring dividend and a growth story — the ecosystem lets you assemble the mix you want.

![Viettel ecosystem graph overseas telecom plus towers plus fibre plus digital into growth and yield stack](/imgs/blogs/telecommunications-sector-vietnam-data-utility-5.png)

The figure lays out the stack. VGI provides overseas telecom growth, CTR provides tower and infrastructure exposure, FOX-type fibre provides domestic broadband growth, and the digital-services layer (cloud, data centres, fintech) sits on top — all converging into a combined growth-plus-yield profile that a plain saturated carrier cannot match. The point of the figure is not the individual boxes but the *combination*: this is why Vietnamese telecom is not just a defensive utility but a defensive utility with a real growth option attached.

#### Worked example: the overseas growth kicker, VGI's pretax profit

Trace VGI's pretax profit through the inflection. In 2021 it was roughly negative 0.2 trillion dong — still in the heavy-investment, accumulated-loss phase for several frontier markets. In 2022 it turned firmly positive at about 1.6 trillion dong. In 2023 it more than doubled again to roughly 3.88 trillion. And in 2024 it reached about 5.48 trillion dong — up roughly 41% on the year — which at \$1 = 25,400 VND is about \$216mn of pretax profit. From a small loss to over five trillion dong in three years is not utility behaviour; it is a young telecom climbing the penetration curve in markets that are still adding subscribers fast, with the currency and dividend repatriation tailwinds that come when frontier operations mature. *That trajectory — a loss in 2021 to roughly 5.5 trillion dong in 2024 — is the growth kicker bolted onto a utility, and it is what made a "boring" telecom one of the market's best performers.*

![Bar chart Viettel Global pretax profit 2021 to 2024 turnaround to 5.5 trillion dong](/imgs/blogs/telecommunications-sector-vietnam-data-utility-2.png)

The chart shows the turnaround as a picture: a small loss in 2021, then 1.6, 3.88 and 5.48 trillion dong across 2022–24. The shape — a J-curve from loss to surge — is the signature of a network business crossing its operating-leverage inflection. The early losses were the cost of building networks in frontier markets; the surge is those networks now scaled past their fixed-cost base and into the cash-gushing phase, exactly the marginal-cost magic from the previous section, playing out at the group level.

It is worth being precise about *why* VGI specifically can do this when a developed-market carrier cannot. Three forces stack. **Penetration** — VGI's markets are young, so it adds subscribers the way a Vietnamese telecom did fifteen years ago, and each subscriber drops onto a network with spare capacity at near-zero marginal cost. **Operating leverage at the inflection** — many of those markets were built years ago and ran at a loss while scaling; now that the base has grown past the fixed-cost hump, the same revenue that once lost money is hugely profitable, so growth accelerates *non-linearly*. And **currency and repatriation** — as frontier operations mature and stabilise, the ability to recognise and repatriate profits (and favourable local-currency moves in some periods) can add a tailwind on top of the operating growth. None of these forces is available to a saturated carrier; all three are available to VGI at once, which is why its profit can triple in two years while the entire developed-market telecom sector grows low single digits. The flip side — and the reason this is a *growth* bet not a *utility* bet — is that every one of those forces can reverse: penetration eventually saturates, currencies in frontier markets can crash, and political risk in some of these countries is real. The growth kicker is real and it is large, but it is not a dividend stream.

### The same business, two completely different stocks

Hold the two halves of the ecosystem side by side and the investment logic snaps into focus. The *domestic* mobile incumbent is a saturated phase-two utility — flat subscribers, ARPU-and-cost story, dividend payer, low beta, valued on yield and EV/EBITDA. VGI is a phase-one frontier grower — fast subscriber growth, J-curve earnings, valued on a growth premium, higher risk and thinner liquidity. They run on the *same* underlying engine (build a network, harvest ARPU times subscribers at near-zero marginal cost), but they sit on opposite ends of the penetration lifecycle, so they trade like completely different animals. This is the single most important thing to internalise about Vietnamese telecom: "the telecom sector" is not one trade, it is a *menu* — a utility floor and a growth option that happen to share a business model — and your job is to decide how much of each you want, not to buy "telecom" as a monolith.

## 5G and the capex cycle

Every decade or so, telecom goes through a **capex cycle**: a wave of investment to roll out the next network generation (3G, then 4G, now 5G). These cycles matter enormously for how the sector behaves, and they are widely misunderstood.

A capex cycle is, in the short run, *bad* for free cash flow. Rolling out 5G means buying new spectrum, installing new radios on tens of thousands of towers, and upgrading the backbone — a multi-year spending wave that depresses free cash flow and can pressure dividends while it runs. This is the left side of the marginal-cost curve all over again: you spend ahead of the revenue. The *promise* of 5G is higher ARPU (faster speeds, new enterprise and fixed-wireless services, IoT and industrial use cases) and lower cost-per-bit, but that revenue arrives slowly and unevenly. The mistake investors make is treating 5G as an instant earnings driver; in reality it is a capex headwind first and an ARPU tailwind much later, if at all.

For Vietnam specifically, the 5G cycle has a clear winner and a clear group to watch. The winner on the way *up* the capex curve is **CTR** — more 5G means more towers, more network deployment, more technical services, all of which is CTR's revenue regardless of whether the operators ever earn back the ARPU. CTR sells picks and shovels into the build, so it benefits from the *spending* itself. The operators (Viettel, and the listed pieces exposed to domestic mobile) carry the capex burden and hope to earn the ARPU back later. This is the classic split in any infrastructure cycle: the contractor gets paid during the build; the operator is paid back only if the demand shows up.

There is a structural reason CTR's position is getting *better*, not just busier, and it is worth understanding because it is the cleanest growth story in the domestic sector. The traditional model had each operator build and own its own towers — three operators, three parallel sets of towers, enormous duplicated capex. The modern model is **tower-sharing**: an independent tower company (a *towerco*) owns the steel and the land lease and rents space on each tower to multiple operators. This is a far better business than building towers once and walking away, because a shared tower earns *recurring* rent from several tenants — the towerco itself becomes a mini-utility, with the same build-once-harvest-forever economics as the telecom it serves, but one layer down and diversified across operators. As CTR builds out a towerco model on top of its construction work, it converts one-off build revenue into recurring tenancy revenue, and 5G (which needs *many* more small cells and towers than 4G because higher frequencies travel shorter distances) is the demand wave that fills those towers. The picks-and-shovels play is quietly turning into a recurring-rent play, which is why CTR is a growth name and not merely a cyclical contractor.

Note the timing asymmetry this creates across the ecosystem. At the *front* of a 5G cycle, capital flows *out* of the operators (capex up, free cash flow down, dividend under pressure) and *into* the contractors and tower companies (revenue up). At the *back* of the cycle, if the ARPU shows up, the flow reverses — operators harvest the higher-ARPU services while the build slows. So the same 5G wave is bullish for CTR early and bullish for the operators (and their dividends) late. Knowing where in the capex cycle you are tells you which layer of the ecosystem to own — another reason "buy telecom" is too blunt an instruction, and the per-name map matters.

#### Worked example: the dividend yield, the income case

Here is the income side of the thesis in numbers. Suppose a domestic telecom trades at 60,000 dong a share and pays a cash dividend of 3,000 dong a share. The dividend yield is simply the dividend divided by the price: 3,000 / 60,000 = 5%. Set that against VN-Index, whose dividend yield runs around 1.8%, and the telecom pays roughly **2.8 times** the market's yield. Now add the beta — the telecom moves about 0.7% for every 1% in the index, versus the index's 1.0 — so you are collecting nearly three times the income while taking only about 70% of the market's swing. At \$1 = 25,400 VND, a 60,000-dong share is about \$2.36, and the 3,000-dong dividend is about \$0.12 per share per year. *A roughly 5% yield at a 0.7 beta is the income case in one line: more cash, less volatility, paid out of a recurring subscriber base that does not care what the index does this month.*

## Valuation: how to price a data utility with a growth option

Telecom is valued differently from a bank or a developer, and getting the framework right keeps you from over- or under-paying. Three lenses matter.

**EV/EBITDA is the workhorse multiple.** Telecom carries a lot of debt (networks are capital-intensive) and a lot of depreciation (the network wears out on paper faster than in reality), so earnings-based multiples like P/E can be noisy. *EV/EBITDA* — enterprise value (market cap plus net debt) divided by earnings before interest, tax, depreciation and amortisation — strips out the financing and the non-cash depreciation, and is the cleanest way to compare telecoms. A mature, saturated carrier might trade around 5–7x EV/EBITDA; the market pays *more* for a telecom with genuine growth. The reason to use enterprise value rather than market cap is that two telecoms with identical operations can look very different on P/E purely because one carries more debt — debt shifts profit between interest and equity holders without changing the underlying cash the network generates. EV/EBITDA puts the debt back into the numerator (enterprise value includes net debt) and strips the non-cash charges out of the denominator, so you are comparing the actual cash-generating engine, not the accident of how each company happened to finance its towers. For a sector this capital-intensive and this debt-laden, that adjustment is not optional — it is the difference between comparing businesses and comparing balance sheets.

**Dividend yield prices the utility floor.** For the mature, slow-growth part of the sector, the dividend yield *is* the thesis: you are buying a bond-like income stream, and the yield (relative to deposit rates and the index) tells you whether you are being paid enough. A ~4–5% yield at a 0.7 beta, against ~5% bank deposit rates, is a competitive income proposition with equity upside attached.

**The growth premium is where VGI breaks the mould.** A pure utility deserves a utility multiple. But VGI is not a pure utility — it is a frontier-growth telecom — so the market awards it a growth premium far above what a saturated carrier earns, the same way it pays up for FPT's software growth or a fast-growing fibre business. The danger is over-paying for the growth (a frontier turnaround can re-rate hard, then stall), so the discipline is to anchor the *utility* parts on yield and EV/EBITDA and treat the VGI growth premium as a separate, riskier bet you size deliberately. Mixing the two — paying a growth multiple for the whole sector, or a utility multiple for VGI — is how investors misprice telecom.

![Horizontal bar chart telecom dividend yield 4 percent and beta 0.7 versus index 1.8 percent and 1.0](/imgs/blogs/telecommunications-sector-vietnam-data-utility-6.png)

The chart puts the defensive case side by side with the index. Telecom's dividend yield sits around 4.0% versus the index near 1.8% — more than double the income. Telecom's beta sits around 0.7 versus the index's 1.0 — roughly 70% of the market's volatility. The two bars together *are* the defensive trade: more income, less swing. The VGI growth premium is the part this chart deliberately leaves off, because it is a separate bet stacked on top of this defensive base — yield and low beta from the utility, upside from the overseas growth.

### Mapping the listed plays

Because "telecom" is a menu rather than a monolith, the practical question is *which name expresses which view*. The four exposures map cleanly onto the lifecycle and the ecosystem. The **domestic mobile incumbent** is the saturated utility — its driver is ARPU and cost discipline, it sits firmly on the yield side, and the metric to watch is the dividend yield against deposit rates. **VGI** is the overseas grower — its driver is frontier penetration, it sits hard on the growth side, and the metric is overseas pretax-profit growth. **FOX** is domestic fibre — its driver is broadband penetration and data-centre/cloud upsell, it sits in the middle (growth *and* a dividend), and the metric is fibre subscriber and ARPU growth. **CTR** is the infrastructure/capex play — its driver is the tower and 5G build, it leans growth via the towerco model, and the metric is tower count and deployment backlog. Lay them out as a grid and you can see, at a glance, exactly what you are buying with each ticker.

## Common misconceptions

The telecom sector is widely misunderstood, and the misunderstandings are exactly where mispricing lives. Three myths, each corrected with a number.

**Myth 1: "Telecom is a pure no-growth utility."** This is the lazy developed-market caricature, and in Vietnam it is simply false. Yes, the domestic mobile business is a saturated utility — but VGI grew pretax profit from a small loss in 2021 to about 5.48 trillion dong in 2024, FOX grows fibre into still-penetrating households, and the data-volume tailwind lifts ARPU even where subscriber counts are flat. The correct frame is *utility floor plus growth option*, not *no growth*. Treating the whole sector as a dead utility means you miss the single best-performing piece of it.

**Myth 2: "More subscribers always means more profit."** In a *penetrating* market (VGI's overseas markets), more subscribers does mean more profit, because each one drops ARPU onto a network with spare capacity. But in a *saturated* market, chasing more subscribers can *destroy* profit: the marginal subscriber is low-value, often won with discounts and acquisition spend, and high churn means you pay to acquire customers who leave. A saturated carrier that brags about subscriber growth while ARPU and margins fall is running on a treadmill. The number that matters is not subscribers alone — it is ARPU times subscribers minus the cost to acquire and retain them. Growth in the right phase is gold; growth in the wrong phase is a value trap.

**Myth 3: "5G is an instant earnings driver."** 5G is a capex *cost* first and an ARPU *benefit* much later, if at all. Rolling it out depresses free cash flow for years (new spectrum, new radios, backbone upgrades) while the higher-ARPU services (enterprise, fixed-wireless, IoT) ramp slowly. In every prior generation (3G, 4G) the spending came years before the payoff, and several developed-market carriers spent heavily on 5G with disappointing ARPU uplift. The clean way to play the 5G build in Vietnam is *not* the operator hoping for ARPU — it is CTR, which gets paid for the towers and deployment during the build regardless of whether the ARPU ever shows up. Confusing the capex winner (the contractor) with the speculative ARPU winner (the operator) is the most common 5G mistake.

**Myth 4: "A high P/E means a telecom is expensive."** Telecoms carry enormous non-cash depreciation, which depresses reported net profit and inflates the P/E — a saturated carrier can show a P/E of 15–20x while generating far more cash than that multiple implies. Judging a telecom on P/E is like judging a paid-off apartment building on its accounting profit after a big depreciation charge: you miss the rent it actually collects. The right multiple is EV/EBITDA (often a modest 5–7x for a mature carrier), and the right cash check is the dividend yield, which a 15x-P/E telecom can still pay at ~4–5% precisely because the cash is bigger than the earnings. Dismissing telecom on P/E throws away one of the most reliable income streams on the market.

**Myth 5: "Over-the-top apps are killing telecoms."** This was the 2010s fear — that free messaging and calling apps would gut telecom revenue. They gutted *voice and SMS* revenue, yes, but they did it by moving everyone onto *data*, and the telecoms own the data pipe. The apps did not disintermediate the network; they made it indispensable. A telecom that completed the voice-to-data transition came out the other side selling a more essential, stickier, faster-growing product than the one the apps "disrupted." The threat that was supposed to kill the sector is the tailwind that now defines it.

## How it shows up on VN-Index

Abstractions are easy; here is how telecom has actually behaved on the Vietnamese market, with dates and numbers.

**The VGI surge of 2023–24 was the headline event.** For most of its listed life VGI was an ignored, illiquid line on the UPCoM board — a sleepy overseas-telecom holding that retail investors skipped. Then the earnings inflected: 1.6 trillion dong pretax in 2022, 3.88 trillion in 2023, and about 5.48 trillion in 2024 (up ~41%). As the market woke up to a state-backed telecom compounding at frontier-growth rates, the stock re-rated violently, multiplying several times in price across 2023–24 and becoming one of the best-performing large caps on the whole market. The lesson is the one this post keeps returning to: a *telecom* delivered that return, because the growth came from the penetration-phase overseas markets, not the saturated home market. A reader who dismissed the sector as a dead utility missed it entirely.

**FOX is the steady-compounder counterpoint.** FPT Telecom does not surge; it grinds. Year after year it adds fibre subscribers, grows ARPU through bundles and data-centre/cloud services, and compounds revenue and profit at a steady mid-teens kind of pace, paying a dividend along the way. It is the cleanest expression of the domestic data-utility-with-growth thesis: not exciting in any single quarter, but reliable in a way that the saturated mobile incumbents are not, because fibre penetration still has room to run. In a market full of cyclical booms and busts, a stock that just goes up a little every year is rarer and more valuable than it looks.

**The dividend appeal shows up in drawdowns.** When VN-Index fell more than 40% across 2022, the high-beta book — brokers, steel, bond-funded property — was a wealth-destruction machine, with brokers giving back two-thirds and developers falling 60–70%. A telecom with a 0.7 beta and a steady dividend fell too (everything falls in a 40% drawdown as foreign funds and margin-financed retail sell what they can), but it fell *less*, kept paying its dividend out of recurring subscriber cash, and recovered faster. That is the defensive utility behaving exactly as designed — the same role utilities, staples and pharma play, with the added VGI growth option that those sectors lack.

**CTR is the quiet beneficiary of every operator's capex.** While VGI grabbed the headlines, Viettel Construction compounded in the background by doing the unglamorous work of building and operating the towers and networks the whole group needs. It is a useful template for how to think about infrastructure plays: CTR's revenue does not depend on guessing whether 5G ARPU materialises — it depends on whether towers get built, and towers get built regardless, because the operators have no choice but to densify their networks as data traffic climbs. As CTR shifted toward a tower-leasing model, a slice of its revenue became recurring rent rather than one-off construction, which the market rewards with a higher multiple. The lesson for a sector reader: in any capex cycle, the contractor that converts build revenue into recurring tenancy revenue is structurally more valuable than one that just builds and moves on.

**The state-ownership and liquidity caveat is real.** Most of the Vietnamese telecom names sit on UPCoM rather than the main HOSE board, and the largest (Viettel's pieces) are majority state-owned with limited free float, so liquidity can be thin and price moves can be jumpy. VGI's re-rating was partly a function of a small float meeting suddenly large demand — which cuts both ways: it can run hard, and it can fall hard, on modest flow. The defensiveness of the *business* is real; the *stock's* tradability is more constrained than a liquid bank or broker, and position sizing has to respect that. It also means the sector behaves differently from the deep, liquid bank-and-broker complex that drives most of VN-Index: a telecom position is something you build patiently and hold, not something you trade in size on a day's notice, and the dividend is part of what compensates you for accepting that lower liquidity.

## The playbook: how to trade it

This is the payoff. Telecom is a defensive data utility with a growth kicker, so the playbook owns it for yield *plus* the overseas/digital growth — and knows exactly what would break the thesis.

**The core position is the utility floor: own the sector for yield and low beta.** The base case for telecom is income and defense. You buy the recurring-subscriber cash engine for its ~4–5% dividend yield and ~0.7 beta, the same way you would own utilities or staples, as the part of the book that keeps paying through the cycle and cushions a drawdown. This is the position you can hold through almost any environment — it does not depend on the cycle turning, the index rising, or a catalyst landing. It just collects subscriber bills and pays you a dividend.

**The growth kicker is a separate, deliberately-sized bet: VGI (overseas), CTR (capex), FOX (fibre).** On top of the utility floor, you add the growth layer you want exposure to. VGI is the highest-octane piece — frontier-overseas growth that re-rated several times over — but also the riskiest (thin float, frontier-market and currency risk, a multiple that already prices in growth). CTR is the picks-and-shovels play on the 5G capex cycle, earning from the build regardless of operator ARPU. FOX is the steady domestic-fibre compounder. The discipline is to *size the growth kicker as growth* (smaller, risk-aware) and the *utility floor as income* (larger, hold-through), and not to confuse the two — do not pay a growth multiple for the whole sector, and do not treat VGI as a low-risk dividend stock.

**When to lean in.** Telecom earns its place in the book in two environments. First, **late-cycle and defensive rotations** — when the liquidity bull is tiring, leadership is narrowing, and you want to rotate out of high-beta brokers and steel into cash-paying defensives, telecom (with utilities, staples and pharma) is where you go for income and a softer landing. Second, **when the growth kicker has a fresh catalyst** — an earnings inflection in VGI's overseas markets, an acceleration in fibre penetration for FOX, or the front of a 5G capex cycle for CTR — you add the growth layer ahead of the catalyst, sized as a growth bet.

A note on *how the two legs interact through the cycle*, because it is what makes telecom unusually flexible. In a roaring liquidity bull, the utility floor lags (you accept that — it is income, not torque), but the growth kicker can still run if VGI's overseas earnings are inflecting, so the sector is not dead money even in a bull. In a late-cycle wobble or an outright drawdown, the utility floor is exactly where you want to be (low beta, dividend, soft landing), while the growth kicker is the part you trim first because its thin float and growth multiple make it the volatile leg. So the same sector gives you a knob to turn: dial up the growth kicker when you want torque and a catalyst is live, dial it down and lean on the utility floor when you want defense. Very few Vietnamese sectors let you adjust your risk *within* the sector like that — most are all-cyclical (steel, brokers) or all-defensive (water, pharma). Telecom is both, and the playbook is mostly about choosing the blend for the regime you are in.

**The position, concretely.** A reasonable expression for a Vietnamese investor: a core defensive allocation to the dividend-paying telecom/fibre names for the ~4% yield and 0.7 beta, held through the cycle as part of the income sleeve; plus a smaller, deliberately-sized satellite in the growth kicker (VGI for overseas torque, CTR for the capex cycle, FOX for steady fibre growth), sized for its higher risk and thinner liquidity. The yield funds the wait; the growth kicker provides the upside.

**The invalidation — what would break the thesis.** Be honest about what kills each leg. The *utility floor* breaks if the dividend gets cut — which happens if a heavy 5G capex cycle or a price war collapses free cash flow, or if ARPU rolls over as data finally commoditises; watch free cash flow and the payout ratio, and if the dividend is no longer covered, the defensive thesis is gone. The *VGI growth kicker* breaks if the overseas earnings momentum stalls — if pretax profit growth decelerates sharply or reverses (frontier-market currency crises, political risk, market saturation abroad), the growth premium re-rates *down* hard, and a stock that ran several times over can give much of it back; watch the quarterly overseas pretax trend and the currency/repatriation environment. And the whole sector's *tradability* is capped by thin float and state ownership, so size positions for liquidity, not just conviction. When the dividend is cut or VGI's overseas growth stalls, the trade is over — not when the price wobbles.
