In late 2023 and through 2024, something quietly unusual happened on the Ho Chi Minh Stock Exchange (HOSE). The VN-Index spent those months doing what it usually does — lurching with the property-bond aftershocks, the on-off rate cuts, the worries about foreign selling — and most of the big cyclical sectors went with it. Steel makers swung from boom to bust to tentative recovery. Brokers spiked and gave it all back. Property developers stayed in the deep-freeze. And through all of it, one large-cap simply kept climbing in a near-straight line, hitting fresh all-time highs while the headlines were about everything that was going wrong. That stock was **FPT** — the country's largest information-technology company — and watching it compound through the noise was the cleanest illustration of a distinction this whole series is built on: the difference between a *cyclical* and a *structural compounder*.

Most of the VN-Index is cyclical. Banks, brokers, steel, property, chemicals, oil and gas — their earnings rise and fall with the credit cycle, the commodity cycle, the rate cycle. You make money in those sectors by trading the *turn*: buying the trough, selling the peak, and respecting that the up-and-down is the nature of the thing. Information technology is the rare exception. FPT's earnings do not whipsaw with the domestic cycle, because most of them are not even *earned* in the domestic cycle — they come from software contracts with Japanese, American and European corporations, paid in dollars, that renew year after year. The business compounds. And a business that compounds at 20% a year is a fundamentally different animal from one that swings around a flat trend: you do not trade its turn, you *own it and let it run*.

This post is about how to think about that animal. We will build the software-export model from zero — what FPT actually sells, why outsourced software earnings *recur* and *compound* in a way that steel tonnage never will, and where the famous labor-cost arbitrage actually shows up in the margin. We will lay out the AI and digital-transformation tailwind that is re-rating the stock, and the strange, very Vietnamese feature of a great company whose **foreign room is permanently full** — so foreigners pay a premium just to get in. Then we will do the part that trips up every beginner who has only ever owned cyclicals: how to *value a grower*, why a "high" P/E can still be cheap, and what the PEG idea really means. And we will end, as this series always does, with a concrete playbook — how to own a compounder in a cyclical market, when to add, and exactly what would prove the thesis wrong.

A quick word on money, because we will use it throughout. Vietnam trades in dong (VND), and FPT's headline numbers — revenue, profit, market cap — are all quoted in dong. As a reference for readers who think in dollars, the exchange rate in mid-2026 is roughly **\$1 = 25,400 VND**. So FPT's 62.8 trillion dong of 2024 revenue is about **\$2.47bn**; its 11.1 trillion dong of pre-tax profit is about **\$435mn**; a market cap of, say, 200 trillion dong is about a **\$7.9bn** company. The dollar lens is more than a convenience here — it is *structural*, because FPT's biggest business literally earns its revenue in dollars and yen and euros while paying its costs in dong. We will keep the primary figures in dong (that is how the market quotes them) and drop a dollar anchor whenever it helps you feel the size.

## Foundations: what FPT actually sells, and why software earnings compound

Forget the stock for a moment and think about what the company *does*. People hear "FPT" and "information technology" and reach for the wrong mental model — they picture a hardware maker, or a phone-shop chain, or a telecom utility. FPT touches some of those, but the engine that drives the stock, and the reason it is a compounder, is something more specific and more powerful: it sells **software-engineering labor to foreign corporations**. To understand the investment case you have to understand that business first, so let us build it from zero.

A large company in Japan, the United States or Germany — a bank, an insurer, a carmaker, a retailer — runs on software. It has aging systems that need maintaining, new systems that need building, websites and apps and back-office platforms that need writing and rewriting constantly. It needs *software engineers* to do all of this, and lots of them. But hiring engineers in Tokyo, San Francisco or Frankfurt is brutally expensive, and there are not enough of them. So the company does what manufacturers figured out decades ago with physical goods: it **outsources** the work to a place where skilled labor is cheaper. For software, two of the classic destinations are India and Vietnam, and FPT is Vietnam's flagship in that trade.

This is **software outsourcing**, sometimes called **IT services** or **software export** (Vietnamese: *xuất khẩu phần mềm*). FPT signs a contract with, say, a Japanese insurance company to build and maintain a chunk of its software. FPT staffs the project with Vietnamese engineers — sitting in Hanoi, Da Nang, Ho Chi Minh City, or on-site in Tokyo — who do the actual work. The Japanese client pays FPT in yen (or, for global contracts, often in dollars), and FPT pays its engineers in dong. The company is, in essence, an *exporter*: it ships engineering hours across the border and brings hard currency back, exactly the way a textile factory ships shirts and brings back dollars — except the "product" is human software talent, which is far higher-margin and far more scalable than thread.

It helps to be precise about *how* the work is sold, because the contract structure is part of why the revenue recurs. There are broadly two shapes. The first is **time-and-materials**, where the client effectively rents a team of FPT engineers for a period and pays for their time — this is the bread-and-butter, and it is *inherently* recurring because the team stays engaged month after month, quarter after quarter, as long as the client has work (and a large enterprise always has work). The second is **fixed-price project delivery**, where FPT quotes a price to build a defined system and delivers it — lumpier, higher-risk, but higher-margin when run well. Over time a healthy outsourcing relationship migrates from a small fixed-price pilot ("prove you can do this") into a large, standing time-and-materials engagement ("you now run this part of our IT permanently"). That migration — pilot to embedded partner — is the *land-and-expand* dynamic that turns a one-off contract into a compounding annuity, and it is exactly what FPT has done with its anchor Japanese clients over twenty-plus years. Japan, in particular, is FPT's single largest export market, and the reason is partly cultural: Japanese enterprises prize long, trust-based supplier relationships and are notoriously slow to switch vendors, which makes a Japanese contract, once won, extraordinarily durable.

That export software business is FPT's crown jewel, and within the company it sits inside the segment usually reported as **Global IT services** (or "technology / software outsourcing"). But FPT is not *only* that, and you need the full shape to value it. The company runs on roughly four legs:

The **first and most important leg is global IT services — the software export engine.** This is the outsourcing business just described: contracts with foreign clients, delivered by Vietnamese engineers, paid in foreign currency. It is the fastest-growing leg, the highest-quality leg (recurring, dollar-billed, structurally tailwinded by AI), and the one that makes FPT a compounder rather than a cyclical. Roughly **45%** of revenue comes from here, and it is growing faster than the rest, so its share keeps rising.

The **second leg is domestic IT.** FPT also sells software and IT services *inside* Vietnam — to Vietnamese banks, government agencies and enterprises digitizing their operations, plus system integration and hardware distribution. This is a solid, growing business riding Vietnam's own digital-transformation wave, but it is more competitive and lower-margin than the export work, and it is exposed to the domestic budget cycle. Call it about **15%** of revenue.

The **third leg is telecom.** FPT Telecom is one of Vietnam's large broadband and internet service providers, delivering fixed-line internet to homes and businesses across the country. This is a *utility-like* business: steady subscriber revenue, recurring monthly bills, defensive cash flow, but slow-growing and capital-intensive (you have to lay and maintain fiber). It is roughly **30%** of revenue and acts as a ballast — it does not compound at 20%, but it throws off reliable cash and rarely lurches.

The **fourth leg is education and everything else.** FPT runs a large private education arm — FPT University and a network of schools and training programs — that, not coincidentally, trains the very engineers the export business needs. Plus there are smaller ventures. Education is high-margin and fast-growing off a small base, and strategically it is the *supply line* for the whole model: it manufactures the low-cost engineers. Call it about **10%** of revenue.

Hold that shape: **~45% global IT services, ~15% domestic IT, ~30% telecom, ~10% education and other.** The single most important thing to notice is that the *fastest-growing, highest-quality leg is also the largest and is gaining share.* That is what makes the consolidated company a compounder: its mix is steadily tilting toward its best business.

![Bar chart of FPT 2024 revenue mix split into global IT services about 45 percent, telecom about 30 percent, domestic IT about 15 percent, and education and other about 10 percent](/imgs/blogs/information-technology-sector-vietnam-fpt-structural-growth-3.png)

Now to the heart of it: **why do software-export earnings recur and compound, when steel tonnage does not?** This is the question that separates a compounder from a cyclical, and there are three distinct reasons, each worth holding separately.

**Reason one: labor-cost arbitrage.** A senior software engineer in Tokyo or San Francisco costs the employer a large multiple of what an equally capable engineer costs in Hanoi. FPT's whole business model is to capture the *gap*. It bills the Japanese client at a rate that is a discount to what the client would pay locally — so the client saves money and is happy — while paying its Vietnamese engineer a wage that is excellent *by Vietnamese standards*. The difference between the dollar/yen billing rate and the dong cost is the gross margin, and because Vietnamese engineering wages are far below Western ones, that margin is structurally fat. Crucially, this arbitrage does not depend on the domestic Vietnamese economic cycle — it depends on the *gap* between Vietnamese and Western wages, which closes only very slowly over decades. So the margin is durable in a way a steel spread (which can vanish in a single quarter) never is.

**Reason two: the revenue is recurring and sticky.** When a Japanese insurer outsources a piece of its core software to FPT, it does not switch vendors every quarter. Software relationships are *sticky*: the FPT team learns the client's systems, builds institutional knowledge, becomes embedded in the client's operations, and switching to a competitor means re-explaining everything and risking a botched migration. So contracts renew, and renew, and grow — a client that started with one small project hands over more work each year as trust builds. This is the opposite of selling a ton of steel, which is a one-off transaction at a spot price with no relationship and no recurrence. Recurring revenue is what lets a business *compound*: this year's revenue is largely next year's *floor*, and the new work stacks on top.

**Reason three: dollar revenue against dong costs.** Because FPT bills in hard currency and pays in dong, it is naturally *long the dollar and short the dong* on its export business — and the dong has a long history of gently depreciating against the dollar (a few percent a year on average). That slow depreciation, which is a *headwind* for an importer or a dollar-borrower, is a quiet *tailwind* for FPT: as the dong weakens, the same dollar contract translates into *more* dong of revenue, padding the margin further. It is a structural feature that works in the company's favor over time, the mirror image of the currency risk that hurts so many other Vietnamese businesses.

Put those three together — fat arbitrage margin, recurring sticky revenue, a currency that drifts in your favor — and you have a business that *grows on top of itself*. Each year's recurring base is bigger; the company reinvests its profits into hiring and training more engineers (which is why the education arm matters), wins more contracts, and the whole thing ratchets up. That is the literal meaning of *compounding*: growth stacking on a growing base, year after year, without needing a fresh cyclical upturn to bail it out.

It is worth pausing on *why* this particular business model compounds so cleanly, because not every "growth" company actually does. The deep reason is that FPT's growth is **reinvestment-driven and capital-light**. Software outsourcing barely needs factories or machines; its main input is *people*, and people can be hired and trained at a roughly constant cost per head. So when FPT earns a profit, it can plough that profit straight back into hiring the next cohort of engineers and winning the next batch of contracts, and each new engineer is roughly as profitable as the last. There is no diminishing return from building an ever-bigger steel mill, no commodity price that caps your margin, no domestic market that saturates — the addressable market is the entire global enterprise software-services spend, which is measured in the *trillions* of dollars and which FPT has captured a rounding-error sliver of. A company that can reinvest its earnings at a high return, into an enormous and growing market, *with* recurring revenue underneath, is the textbook setup for multi-decade compounding. That is the structural difference from a cyclical: a steel maker's profit depends on a spread it does not control and a market that is fixed in size; FPT's profit depends on how fast it can hire and embed engineers into a market that is effectively unlimited.

There is a flip side worth naming honestly, because it is the one genuine constraint on the model: the binding limit is **talent supply.** FPT can only grow as fast as it can recruit and train capable engineers, and good engineers are scarce everywhere, Vietnam included. This is precisely why the *education* arm is strategically load-bearing rather than a side business — FPT University and its training network are the company's way of *manufacturing its own raw material*, partially escaping the talent bottleneck that constrains rivals. When you read that FPT is opening new campuses or expanding its training capacity, you are watching it widen the pipe that feeds the compounding engine. The talent constraint is also the honest cap on the growth rate: it is part of why FPT compounds at ~20% rather than the 50%+ a pure software-product company can manage — services growth is gated by headcount in a way that selling more copies of a finished product is not.

#### Worked example: the labor-arbitrage margin on a Japanese contract

Make the arbitrage concrete, because it is the engine of the whole model. Suppose a Japanese insurer hires FPT to staff a software project with **20 Vietnamese engineers** for a year, and FPT bills the client a blended rate of **\$40 per engineer-hour**. At roughly 1,800 billable hours a year per engineer, that is `20 × 1,800 × $40 = $1.44mn` of revenue — bill it in dollars, and at \$1 = 25,400 VND that is about **36.6 billion dong** of revenue for the project.

Now the cost side, paid in dong. A capable FPT engineer might cost the company, fully loaded (salary, benefits, overhead, the project manager's slice), on the order of **600 million dong a year**, about **\$23,600**. Twenty engineers cost roughly `20 × 600mn = 12 billion dong`, about **\$472,000**. So the project earns about 36.6 billion dong of revenue against roughly 12 billion dong of direct delivery cost — a gross margin on the order of **65-67%** before the company's own overhead. The intuition: FPT is selling the *same engineering hour* for a Western-discounted dollar price while buying it at a Vietnamese dong cost, and the wedge between those two — the labor arbitrage — is the durable, repeatable margin that makes software export a compounder rather than a one-off trade.

### The AI and digital-transformation tailwind

The labor-arbitrage model is the *base*. On top of it sits the thing that has re-rated the stock over the last couple of years: the **AI and digital-transformation wave.** This deserves its own treatment because it is the difference between a company that compounds at a steady 18-20% and one the market is willing to pay a premium multiple for in the belief that the growth rate could *accelerate*.

Start with the demand side. Every large corporation in FPT's client base — banks, insurers, manufacturers, retailers in Japan, the US and Europe — is in the middle of a years-long, expensive scramble to *digitize*: move legacy systems to the cloud, build mobile apps, modernize core platforms, and now, on top of all that, *adopt AI*. None of them have enough engineers to do it themselves, and the AI wave has made the engineer shortage worse, not better, because everyone wants to build AI features at once. That demand has to go *somewhere*, and a large slice of it goes to outsourcing partners like FPT. The digital-transformation budget at a Japanese megabank is not a cyclical line item that gets cut in a downturn — it is a multi-year strategic imperative the board has committed to — which is exactly why FPT's revenue is structural rather than cyclical.

Then layer AI on top. AI does two things for FPT at once, and they pull in the same direction. First, it *adds demand*: clients now want FPT to build AI capabilities — model integration, data pipelines, AI-powered applications — which is new, high-value work on top of the existing maintenance and build contracts. Second, AI makes FPT's *own* engineers more productive: a software engineer using AI coding tools can deliver more per hour, which (if FPT keeps its billing rates) *widens the margin*, or lets the company take on more work with the same headcount. The market looks at that combination — more demand *and* higher productivity — and concludes the growth rate might step up, which is the re-rating thesis driving the premium multiple. FPT has leaned into this explicitly, investing in AI capability, partnering with chipmakers and AI firms, and positioning itself as Vietnam's AI champion.

There is a third, slower AI angle that matters for the years ahead: **AI infrastructure and sovereign AI.** As governments and corporations across Asia decide they want their own AI capability rather than renting it from a handful of Western providers, there is demand for data centers, AI compute, and the engineering to run it — and FPT has moved to position itself in that supply chain, investing in AI factories and partnering with global chipmakers to bring GPU compute to the region. This is more capital-intensive and lower-margin than the pure software-services work, so it will not carry the same compounding economics, but it deepens FPT's strategic relevance and gives it a seat at the table as AI spending scales. For a valuation, treat it as optionality on top of the core compounder, not as the core itself.

A word of caution that we will return to in the playbook: the AI tailwind is *real*, but the market's *enthusiasm* about it is what makes the stock occasionally run ahead of itself. The business compounds at ~20% whether or not AI is in the headlines; the *multiple* the market pays expands and contracts with AI hype. The discipline is to own the compounding and refuse to chase the hype spikes — a distinction we will quantify later. The clearest tell that you are in a hype phase rather than a fundamentals phase is when the *share price* is rising much faster than *earnings* — when a year's stock move is 60-80% but the profit only grew the usual ~20%, the other 40-60% is pure multiple expansion on sentiment, and multiple expansion is exactly the part that can reverse.

### The foreign-room-full premium: when quality is so obvious foreigners pay extra to own it

Now a feature that is specific to Vietnam and central to how you actually own this stock: the **foreign ownership limit**, and what happens when it is permanently maxed out.

Vietnamese law caps how much of most listed companies foreigners are allowed to own collectively — the *foreign ownership room*, universally called **room ngoại** or just **foreign room**. For many sectors the cap is 49%; for some (banks especially) it is lower. FPT's foreign room is **full** — foreign investors collectively own right up to the legal limit, and have for years. There is, in effect, a permanent queue of overseas money that *wants* to own FPT and *cannot*, because there are no shares available inside the room.

This creates a strange and telling market structure. Because the official room is full, a foreigner who wants in cannot simply buy on the exchange at the screen price — they have to find another foreigner willing to *sell* their slice of the room, and that trade happens off-exchange, on what is informally called the **foreign-to-foreign board** or the **negotiated board**. And here is the key fact: because demand from foreigners exceeds the fixed supply of room, foreigners typically pay a **premium** over the on-screen price to acquire those shares — often on the order of **5-10%** above the regular market price. The premium is the market's way of rationing a permanently scarce asset.

What does a full-and-premium foreign room *tell* you? It is a loud, expensive vote of confidence. It says the quality of the company is so widely recognized by sophisticated global investors that they have bid the foreign allocation to its legal ceiling and are paying *extra* on top to get any. This is the opposite of a hidden bargain. FPT is not a stock you discover; it is a stock the whole world already agrees is excellent. That recognition is *why* the P/E is a premium — and it is also why, as we will see, the upside from here comes from continued *compounding of earnings*, not from a re-rating of an under-appreciated stock, because there is nothing left to discover.

There is a useful mental model for the foreign room as a *supply-and-demand* picture. The supply of room is *fixed by law* — a hard ceiling that cannot expand unless the government raises the cap (which it occasionally debates but rarely does for a stock like FPT). The demand from foreigners, meanwhile, *grows* as the company compounds and attracts more global quality-investors. Fixed supply meeting rising demand has exactly one outcome: a rising *price of access*, which is what the negotiated-board premium is. So the premium is not a quirk — it is the predictable result of a permanently scarce asset (room in a great company) facing permanently growing demand. For the domestic investor this is genuinely good news: you sit on the *supply* side of that imbalance. You can buy the same compounder at the unpremiumed on-screen price, and if Vietnam is ever upgraded from "frontier" to "emerging" market status by the major index providers — a long-running prospect that would pull a wave of new foreign passive money into HOSE — the demand for FPT's already-full room would intensify further, widening the premium and, indirectly, supporting the on-screen price you paid. The full foreign room is thus both a *quality signal today* and a *latent demand reservoir* for the future.

#### Worked example: what the foreign-room premium costs you in practice

Suppose FPT trades at **120,000 dong** a share on the regular HOSE board, about **\$4.72**. A foreign fund that wants to build a position cannot buy on the open exchange because the room is full, so it goes to the negotiated board and finds a seller willing to part with room-eligible shares at a **7% premium** — about **128,400 dong** a share, roughly **\$5.05**. On a \$10mn position, that 7% premium is about **\$700,000** of extra cost just for the privilege of owning the stock at all.

What does paying that premium *imply*? It implies the fund expects the compounding to more than cover it — and at ~20% earnings growth, a 7% one-time entry premium is recovered in well under a year of fundamental growth. The intuition: the foreign-room premium is not a tax on a bad investment, it is the market *pricing the scarcity of a recognized compounder* — and the fact that disciplined global funds happily pay it is itself a signal of how durable they think the growth is.

### Valuing a grower: why a "high" P/E can still be cheap

This is the conceptual hurdle every beginner who learned valuation on cyclicals has to clear, so we will take it slowly. The instinct, drilled in by the banks and steel makers, is: *low P/E = cheap, high P/E = expensive.* FPT trades at a P/E in the low-to-mid 20s while a Vietnamese bank trades at 8-10x. The naive reading is "FPT is two to three times more expensive than the bank." That reading is wrong, and understanding *why* is the key to owning growth.

First, define the term. The **price-to-earnings ratio (P/E)** is the share price divided by earnings per share — equivalently, the company's market value divided by its annual profit. A P/E of 10 means you pay 10 dong of price for each 1 dong of *current* annual earnings; a P/E of 22 means you pay 22 dong for each 1 dong of current earnings. So far the naive reading holds: 22 is a bigger number than 10. But the P/E is built on *current* earnings, and current earnings are not what you are buying. You are buying the *whole future stream* of earnings — and for a compounder, the future earnings are vastly larger than today's.

Here is the crux. The bank growing at 8% a year will, in five years, earn about `1.08^5 ≈ 1.47` times today's profit — a bit under half again as much. FPT growing at 20% a year will, in five years, earn about `1.20^5 ≈ 2.49` times today's profit — nearly *two and a half times* as much. So the "1 dong of current earnings" you are buying from FPT is going to grow into 2.49 dong in five years, while the bank's stays near 1.47. You are paying a higher multiple of *today's* earnings precisely because *tomorrow's* earnings are going to be so much bigger. The P/E is high because the **E** in the denominator is about to grow.

The tool that makes this precise is the **PEG ratio** — the P/E divided by the growth rate (in percent). The PEG asks: *how much are you paying per unit of growth?* A P/E of 22 on a 20% grower has a PEG of `22 / 20 ≈ 1.1`. A P/E of 9 on an 8% grower has a PEG of `9 / 8 ≈ 1.1`. *They are the same.* Per unit of growth, you are paying *exactly the same price* for FPT's 20% as for the bank's 8% — the "expensive" grower is not actually dearer at all once you account for what you are getting. A PEG around 1 is the rough fair-value benchmark; meaningfully below 1 is cheap-for-the-growth, meaningfully above 1 is dear-for-the-growth. This is the single most important idea in this post: **a high P/E on a high grower can be cheaper than a low P/E on a no-grower, and PEG is how you see it.**

#### Worked example: PEG makes the "expensive" grower and the "cheap" bank identical

Put two stocks side by side. Stock A is **FPT** at a P/E of **22x**, growing earnings **20%** a year. Stock B is a **Vietnamese bank** at a P/E of **9x**, growing earnings **8%** a year. The bank *looks* less than half the price.

Now compute PEG. FPT: `22 / 20 = 1.1`. Bank: `9 / 8 = 1.13`. They are essentially **identical** — about 1.1 each. Per unit of growth, you are paying the same for both. Push it forward to *see* it: in five years FPT's earnings are `1.20^5 = 2.49×` today's, so on *forward* earnings the P/E collapses to `22 / 2.49 ≈ 8.8x` — it has become as cheap as the bank is *today*, on earnings two and a half times larger. The bank in five years earns `1.08^5 = 1.47×`, so its forward P/E is `9 / 1.47 ≈ 6.1x`. The intuition: a P/E is a snapshot of *today's* earnings, but you own the *future* stream — and once you divide by the growth rate with PEG, the "expensive" 22x compounder and the "cheap" 9x bank turn out to cost almost exactly the same per unit of the thing you actually want, which is growth.

There is one honest caveat to keep the framing fair: PEG treats one year of 20% growth as worth the same as one year of 8% growth, but a grower is only worth its premium *if the growth persists.* The bank's 8% is highly reliable; FPT's 20% requires the export pipeline, the AI tailwind and the margin to all keep delivering. The premium multiple is a bet that the compounding is *durable* — and the whole foundations section above (recurring revenue, sticky clients, structural labor arbitrage, full-and-premium foreign room) is the evidence that it is. That is why FPT, specifically, earns its premium where a random high-P/E story stock would not.

It is worth understanding the *deeper* reason a faster grower deserves a higher multiple, beyond the mechanical PEG arithmetic, because it explains why the premium is rational rather than just a convention. The value of any company is, in principle, the present value of *all* its future cash flows. For a no-growth company, that future stream is flat — this year's earnings, repeated forever — so its fair multiple is essentially just the inverse of the discount rate (roughly, one over the required return). For a grower, the future stream *rises* every year, and the further out you look, the larger the cash flows become; discounting them back, that rising stream is worth far more *today* than a flat one, even though the first year's earnings are identical. The faster and more durable the growth, the more of the company's value sits in those big future years — which is exactly why a small change in the *assumed* long-run growth rate moves a grower's fair P/E so much, and why the market re-rates a compounder hard when it becomes convinced the growth will persist (or accelerate, as with the AI thesis). The premium is not sentiment; it is the math of discounting a rising stream. The risk in a premium multiple, correspondingly, is not that the math is wrong but that the *assumed* growth fails to materialize — which is why the entire investment case rests on the *durability* evidence, not on the growth rate alone.

## Common misconceptions

The IT sector — and FPT specifically — attracts a particular set of beginner errors, mostly because people pattern-match it to either the cyclicals they already know or to the volatile tech stocks they have heard about abroad. Each myth is corrected with a number.

**Myth 1: "A high P/E means the stock is expensive, so FPT is overpriced versus the banks."** This is the big one, and we just dismantled it. FPT's P/E of ~22x on 20% growth has a **PEG of about 1.1** — *identical* to a 9x bank growing 8% (PEG ~1.13). Per unit of growth, FPT is not more expensive than the bank at all. A high P/E is a statement about the *growth rate*, not about *cheapness or dearness*; the only valid way to compare a grower to a no-grower is on PEG or on *forward* earnings, and on both, FPT's premium is justified by its compounding. The naive "low P/E good, high P/E bad" rule is a rule for *cyclicals*, where a low P/E often signals peak earnings about to fall — it actively misleads you on a structural grower.

**Myth 2: "IT is tech, and tech is volatile and risky, so the IT sector swings like a meme stock."** This conflates *price* volatility with *earnings* volatility, and it imports the behavior of speculative Western tech onto a very different business. FPT's *earnings* are among the most stable and predictable on the entire VN-Index — it has grown revenue and pre-tax profit at roughly **20% a year for the better part of a decade**, through the 2020 pandemic, the 2022 bond crisis and the 2023 slowdown, while steel profits swung **5x** and broker profits swung even more. The *share price* can get volatile when AI hype inflates and deflates the multiple, but the *business* is the opposite of volatile — it is the steadiest compounder in the index. The risk in FPT is paying too high a multiple, not earnings collapsing.

**Myth 3: "FPT is really a hardware or telecom company — a phone-shop and broadband play."** FPT did historically have retail and distribution arms, and it does own a large broadband business (FPT Telecom, ~30% of revenue), which is where this myth comes from. But the *engine* of the stock — the part that compounds at 20% and drives the re-rating — is **global IT services**, the software-export business, at roughly **45% of revenue and growing its share every year.** Telecom is the *ballast* (steady, defensive, slow), not the growth driver. Valuing FPT as a telecom utility would be like valuing a software company at a utility multiple — you would badly under-price the compounding software core. The thing you are buying is the software exporter; the telecom and education are the supporting cast.

**Myth 4: "Vietnam is a cheap-labor outsourcer like dozens of others, so FPT has no moat and the margin will get competed away."** Labor-cost arbitrage *alone* would indeed be fragile — anyone can hire cheap engineers. But FPT's moat is not just cheap labor; it is **scale plus relationships plus a captive talent pipeline.** It has tens of thousands of engineers (hard for a rival to assemble quickly), decades-deep sticky relationships with Japanese clients in particular (where trust and long partnership are decisive), and its own university system *manufacturing* the engineers it needs. That combination — a large low-cost base that is *also* embedded with clients and self-supplying its talent — is why the margin has stayed fat for years rather than being competed to zero, and why FPT, not a random new entrant, keeps winning the contracts.

**Myth 5: "FPT has already grown so much that the big returns are behind it — it is too late to own a compounder this large."** This is the most expensive myth of all, because it has been wrong for the entire history of the stock. The error is thinking of growth as a tank that empties — as if a company that has already grown a lot must have "used up" its growth. But compounding does not work that way: a business growing 20% a year off a *bigger* base produces *bigger* absolute gains than it did when it was small. FPT's profit going from 11tn to a doubled ~22tn dong over the next ~3.8 years is a far larger absolute increase than the same percentage move was a decade ago — the compounding is *accelerating* in absolute terms even as the *rate* holds steady. The relevant question is never "has it grown a lot already?" but "is the *runway* still long?" — and with a global enterprise software market measured in the trillions of dollars and FPT holding a microscopic share, the runway is essentially uncapped for the foreseeable future. "Too late" is the reflex of someone anchoring on the *price*, not the *runway*.

## How it shows up on VN-Index

Now make it concrete on the actual market — the price action, the board structure, the re-rating — because the abstractions above leave fingerprints you can see in the data.

**The steady outperformance through the cycles.** The cleanest way to *see* that FPT is a compounder is to watch it through the chaos of 2020-2024. Across those five years, FPT's revenue climbed from about **29.8 trillion dong** to **62.85 trillion dong**, and pre-tax profit from about **5.26 trillion dong** to **11.07 trillion dong** — both more than *doubling*, both rising every single year, including the pandemic year and the bond-crisis year. There was no down year. Now overlay a cyclical like Hoa Phat (steel), whose profit went 13.5 → 35.3 → 8.4 → 6.8 → 12.0 trillion dong over the same period — a violent boom-bust-recovery. The IT line is a smooth up-ramp; the steel line is a roller-coaster. That contrast *is* the sector thesis, and it is why FPT kept making all-time highs in 2023-24 while the index churned. You can quite literally trade the difference: in a market full of things that go up and down, FPT mostly goes up.

![Grouped bar chart of FPT revenue and pre-tax profit each year from 2020 to 2024, both rising every year, revenue from about 30 to 63 trillion dong and pre-tax profit from about 5 to 11 trillion dong](/imgs/blogs/information-technology-sector-vietnam-fpt-structural-growth-2.png)

**The foreign-room premium board.** The second fingerprint is structural. Because FPT's foreign room is permanently full, the stock has a *two-tier* market: the on-screen HOSE price that domestic investors trade, and the negotiated foreign-to-foreign board where overseas funds pay a premium of often **5-10%** to acquire room-eligible shares. When you read that foreign investors are "buying FPT", they frequently cannot do it on the open exchange at all — they are doing it off-board at a premium. For a domestic investor this is a useful signal: a persistently full room with an active premium board is the market telling you, in cash, that global quality-investors regard this as a must-own. It also means a chunk of the usual *foreign-flow* dynamics this series covers elsewhere simply do not apply to FPT — you cannot read FPT through the foreign net-buy/sell prints the way you read the rest of the index, because the foreigners are transacting off-screen.

**The AI re-rating of 2023-2025.** The third fingerprint is the multiple. For most of the 2010s FPT traded at a fairly pedestrian P/E for a 20% grower — sometimes in the low-to-mid teens, a PEG comfortably *below* 1, which in hindsight was cheap-for-the-growth. As the AI wave arrived and FPT positioned itself as Vietnam's AI champion — partnering with global chip and AI firms, investing in AI capability, signing AI-flavored contracts — the market *re-rated* the multiple upward into the low-to-mid 20s. That re-rating is the market shifting its *expected* growth rate up: a higher P/E on the same ~20% delivery is the market pricing in some probability that AI *accelerates* the growth. The honest reading is that part of the move was earnings compounding (the durable part) and part was multiple expansion on AI optimism (the part that can give back). Distinguishing the two is the core of the playbook.

You can decompose any year's stock return into exactly these two pieces, and doing so is the single most useful discipline for owning FPT. A stock's total return over a period is, to a good approximation, the *earnings growth* plus the *change in the multiple* (plus the dividend, which for FPT is modest). So if in a given year the earnings grew ~20% and the stock rose ~25%, then roughly 20 points came from the durable engine (earnings) and only ~5 points from multiple expansion — a healthy, sustainable year. But if the earnings grew ~20% and the stock rose ~70%, then ~50 points came from *multiple expansion alone* — the market paying a higher P/E for the same earnings — and that 50 points is the fragile, reversible part. When you see that gap blow out, you are watching sentiment, not fundamentals, and the playbook says: stop adding. When the gap is small or negative (the stock lagging its earnings growth, multiple compressing), the fundamentals are quietly carrying the load and the crowd is handing you a discount — which is when you add. Reading FPT through this earnings-versus-multiple lens is how you stay disciplined in a stock whose *business* is calm but whose *price* periodically is not.

#### Worked example: the compounding doubling rule, in dong and dollars

Make the compounding tangible with FPT's own profit. Pre-tax profit in 2024 was about **11.07 trillion dong**, roughly **\$435mn** at \$1 = 25,400 VND. Grow it at **20% a year** and watch it stack: year 1 ≈ 13.3tn, year 2 ≈ 15.9tn, year 3 ≈ 19.1tn, year 4 ≈ 23.0tn, year 5 ≈ **27.5 trillion dong** — about **\$1.08bn**. In five years the profit roughly **2.5×**'s, from ~\$435mn to ~\$1.08bn.

There is a shortcut for *when* a compounder doubles: the **rule of 72** — divide 72 by the growth rate to get the doubling time in years. At 20%, that is `72 / 20 ≈ 3.6 years` (the exact figure is ~3.8 years). So FPT's profit doubles roughly every **3.8 years** at its historical rate; over a decade it does that nearly three times, turning ~11tn into the rough neighborhood of ~68tn dong of pre-tax profit. The intuition: cyclicals make you money by catching a *turn* and getting out; a compounder makes you money by *doing nothing* while the earnings double, and double again — which is why you own it rather than trade it.

## The playbook: how to own a compounder in a cyclical market

This is the payoff. Owning a structural grower in a market built for trading cyclicals requires *un-learning* some of the reflexes the rest of this series teaches. The signals, the position, the sizing and the invalidation are all different from how you would trade steel or brokers.

**The core stance: own it, do not trade it.** The single biggest mistake retail investors make with FPT is treating it like a cyclical — trying to trade the wiggles, selling after a 15% run "to take profit", and then never getting back in as it compounds away above them. The whole point of a compounder is that *time in the position* is where the money is made: the earnings double every ~3.8 years, and your job is to *be there* while that happens, not to be clever about the entry to the nearest 5%. The base case for FPT is a long-term core holding you accumulate and hold, sized so you can sit through a 20-30% drawdown without flinching, because there *will* be drawdowns when the AI multiple deflates — and those drawdowns are not the thesis breaking, they are the multiple normalizing around an earnings stream that keeps growing.

**The buy signal: add on cyclical and sentiment pullbacks, not on AI hype.** Because the *business* compounds steadily but the *multiple* swings with AI sentiment, the disciplined way to build a position is to add when the multiple is *cheap relative to the growth* — when a broad market sell-off (a bond scare, a rate shock, foreign de-risking) drags FPT down with everything else despite its earnings being untouched, or when AI enthusiasm cools and the P/E drifts back toward the high teens. Concretely: watch the **PEG**. When FPT's forward PEG sags toward **0.8-0.9** (a P/E in the high teens on ~20% growth), you are being handed a quality compounder at a discount to its growth, and that is when you add aggressively. When the PEG runs up past **1.3-1.5** on an AI-hype spike (a P/E in the high 20s or low 30s), you stop adding and let the existing position ride — you do *not* chase. The asymmetry is the edge: you accumulate the same great business more cheaply when the crowd is fearful or distracted.

**Sizing: a core position, scaled to conviction, not a trade.** Because this is the index's cleanest compounder, it deserves a *larger and more permanent* allocation than a cyclical, which you would size small and trade. A reasonable frame for a growth-tilted Vietnamese equity portfolio is to make FPT a meaningful *core* holding — the anchor you build around — while the cyclicals (steel, brokers, property) are the *tactical satellites* you trade around the core. The position is sized so that the long-term compounding does the heavy lifting and short-term price noise is irrelevant to the thesis.

**The accumulation mechanic: average in, let the compounding overwhelm the entry.** Because the hardest part of owning a compounder is psychological — the price always *feels* too high after it has been compounding — a useful practical discipline is to build the core position by *averaging in* over time rather than agonizing over a single entry. Set a base monthly or quarterly contribution that buys FPT regardless of the headline, and then *add extra* on the pullbacks when the PEG sags toward 0.8-0.9. Over a multi-year holding period, your average cost is dominated not by whether you nailed the bottom but by the relentless ~20% earnings growth underneath, which makes almost any reasonable entry look cheap in hindsight. This is the inverse of the cyclical discipline, where entry timing is *everything* because the earnings will mean-revert; here the earnings *run away upward*, so getting *invested* matters far more than getting the perfect price. A worked feel for it: if you buy FPT at a P/E of 22x and simply hold while earnings compound 20% a year, after about 3.8 years the *forward* P/E on your unchanged cost basis has effectively halved to ~11x as the earnings doubled — the business grew into and past your entry valuation, which is the entire magic of holding a compounder rather than trading it.

**The one structural caveat: the full foreign room.** If you are a *foreign* investor, remember that you may have to pay the **5-10%** premium on the negotiated board to get in at all, which raises your entry cost and slightly lowers your forward return — though at 20% compounding the premium is recovered in under a year. If you are a *domestic* investor, you have an edge here: you can buy at the on-screen price with no premium, owning the same compounder the global funds are paying extra to access.

**The invalidation: what would prove the thesis wrong.** A disciplined position needs a clear "I was wrong" trigger, and for a compounder it is *not* a falling price — a falling price on unchanged earnings is a *buying* opportunity. The thesis breaks if the *compounding itself* breaks. Watch for: (1) **the growth rate rolling over** — two or three consecutive quarters where global-IT-services revenue growth decelerates sharply from the ~20% trend, signaling the export pipeline is drying up or competition is biting; (2) **the margin compressing** — if the labor arbitrage starts closing fast (Vietnamese engineering wages rising toward Western levels, or pricing pressure from Indian rivals forcing billing rates down) and gross margins fall materially, the compounder math degrades; (3) **the foreign room *opening up* and staying open** — if the permanent premium board collapses and foreigners are suddenly *able* to buy on-screen and *choosing not to*, that is global quality-investors voting with their feet, a serious warning that the quality story has cracked. Any of those, sustained, means the premium multiple is no longer justified and you reduce. Absent them, a 30% price drop is an invitation to add, not a reason to sell — which is the exact inverse of how you treat a cyclical.

To make the contrast unmistakable, hold the two animals side by side: a cyclical like steel rewards you for *timing the turn* and punishes you for buying-and-holding through the bust; a compounder like FPT rewards you for *holding through everything* and punishes you for trading the wiggles. Same market, opposite playbooks.

![Side by side comparison showing FPT earnings rising steadily every year from 2020 to 2024 versus a steel maker profit swinging up sharply then crashing then partly recovering over the same years](/imgs/blogs/information-technology-sector-vietnam-fpt-structural-growth-4.png)

The deeper reason FPT can run this playbook — and almost nothing else on the VN-Index can — comes back to the moat we built in the foundations: a large, low-cost Vietnamese engineer base, sticky Japanese and US clients who renew and expand year after year, and dollar billing against dong costs. That trio is what converts a one-off cost advantage into a *durable, compounding* margin, and it is worth seeing as a single structure.

![Flow chart showing a large low-cost engineer base, sticky Japanese and US clients, and dollar billing against dong costs together producing a durable software-export margin](/imgs/blogs/information-technology-sector-vietnam-fpt-structural-growth-5.png)

And the last piece — the one that decides whether the premium multiple is a fair price or a trap — is the valuation discipline: laying the grower next to a no-growth value stock and checking that the *higher* P/E really is cheaper per unit of growth, on PEG and on forward earnings, with the foreign-room signal as a tiebreak.

![Comparison grid with rows for P/E, growth rate, PEG, and foreign room showing FPT as a premium-multiple high-growth full-room compounder versus a no-growth value stock with a low multiple and open room](/imgs/blogs/information-technology-sector-vietnam-fpt-structural-growth-7.png)

Own the compounder. Add when the crowd hands it to you cheap relative to its growth. Refuse to chase the AI-hype spikes. Size it as a core, not a trade. And measure your "I was wrong" against the *earnings*, never the price. That is how you own the one genuine structural grower in a market built for cyclicals — and it is the discipline that, over a decade of ~20% compounding and ~3.8-year doublings, quietly outperforms all the clever cyclical trading around it.
