> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Beyond the slow, earnings-driven sector rotation, VN-Index runs on fast, violent *sóng ngành* ("sector waves"): speculative pumps where a narrative plus retail FOMO plus margin leverage on low-float midcaps drives a whole sub-sector up 50-200% in weeks, then collapses just as fast — and most retail buys near the top and loses.
>
> - **A theme wave is narrative-driven, not earnings-driven.** A story (public investment, a commodity spike, an index-upgrade or "Diamond" theme, a penny real-estate dream) is the fuel; cash flow has nothing to do with the price for the duration of the move.
> - **The ingredients are always the same four**: a believable story, a low free float (so a little buying moves the price a lot), retail FOMO (fear of missing out), and margin leverage (borrowed money that amplifies both legs). Remove any one and the wave fizzles.
> - **The anatomy has six stages** — catalyst, accumulation, markup plus FOMO, margin peak, distribution, collapse — and the brutal arithmetic is that the crowd buys in stages 3-5 and gets sold to by the people who bought in stage 2.
> - **The one number to remember**: a stock that runs +150% and then falls -60% from the top ends roughly back where it started — the early buyer who got in at 120 and out at 230 nearly doubled, while the late buyer who got in at 240 and rode it to 120 lost half their money. Same stock, opposite outcomes, decided entirely by *when* you bought.

## A six-week melt-up, then a cliff

Picture a small construction-and-real-estate ticker — call it a midcap in the lower reaches of HOSE, the kind of name most institutional investors have never modelled because its float is tiny and its earnings are erratic. In a quiet autumn, a story catches: a new master-plan rezones a district, a wave of public-investment headlines hits the wires, and a few popular online "teachers" (the Vietnamese retail scene calls them *thầy*, with stock-tip rooms on Zalo and Facebook running into the tens of thousands of members) start naming this ticker as the next big public-investment play. The stock is at 10,000 dong. Over six weeks it does not drift — it *runs*. 12,000. 15,000. 18,000. Each green session pulls in more buyers; each ceiling print (the +7% daily up-limit on HOSE, the *trần*) is screenshotted and shared. By week six it has touched 25,000 dong, a clean +150%, on turnover ten times its sleepy baseline. Every account that owns it is up huge; the rooms are euphoric; the *thầy* who called it is a hero.

Then the tape changes. One morning it opens green, fades, and closes red on the heaviest volume of the entire run. The next day it gaps down. The day after, it is locked at the floor (-7%, the *sàn*) with millions of shares queued to sell and nobody bidding underneath. For the next month it bleeds: 22,000, then 18,000, then 14,000, then back to 10,000 — a -60% collapse from the 25,000 top, landing roughly where it started. The fundamentals never changed. No earnings report explained either the rise or the fall. What happened was a *wave*: a self-contained speculative cycle that inflated on a narrative and deflated when the last buyer ran out, transferring wealth from the people who bought late to the people who sold into them.

This post is the honest anatomy of that wave. It is the fast, dangerous cousin of the slow [sector rotation we covered earlier](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/sector-rotation-explained-leaders-and-laggards): where rotation moves capital between sectors over quarters as the economic cycle turns, a theme wave moves it in *weeks* on pure story and leverage. We will define exactly what a theme wave is, name its four ingredients, walk its six stages one at a time, do the arithmetic of who wins and who loses, show how it has actually played out on VN-Index (the 2021 penny-stock mania and the 2022 unwind), correct the three lies people tell themselves while riding one, and end with a concrete playbook: how to recognize the stage you are in so you can carefully ride a wave, or — far more often the right call — stand aside and not get run over.

![Theme wave engine narrative low float retail FOMO margin pump then collapse](/imgs/blogs/theme-waves-song-nganh-how-speculative-pumps-work-1.png)

The figure above is the whole post in one picture. On the left, the four ingredients — a narrative, a low free float, retail FOMO, and margin leverage — feed into a single engine. That engine pumps a low-float sub-sector up fast, far beyond anything earnings justify. And on the right, when the marginal buyer runs out, the same leverage that powered the climb reverses into forced selling and the wave collapses. Everything between here and the playbook is an elaboration of how those arrows actually fire, in what order, and where the money goes.

## Foundations: what a theme wave actually is

Before any mechanism, get the core distinction right, because it is the thing most retail traders never internalize.

### Narrative-driven, not earnings-driven

A normal investment thesis says: this company will earn more money, so its shares are worth more, so I buy and wait for the earnings to show up and the price to follow. That is **fundamentals**. A theme wave runs on a different engine entirely. The price moves because a *story* makes more people want to own the stock *right now*, and there are only so many shares, so the price has to rise to clear the buying. Whether the company actually earns anything is, for the duration of the wave, irrelevant. The wave is a phenomenon of *supply and demand for the shares*, driven by a narrative, not a phenomenon of *the value of the business*.

This is why you cannot value a wave with a spreadsheet. If you build a discounted-cash-flow model on a penny construction stock and it says the shares are worth 8,000 dong, that model is correct and completely useless for trading the wave, because the wave does not care. The stock can go to 25,000 dong on a story and back to 8,000 when the story dies, and your model was "right" the whole time while you either missed a triple or got run over, depending on which side you took. The honest framing: **a theme wave is a game of psychology and positioning layered on top of a stock, and for the weeks it lasts, the psychology is the fundamental.**

A useful everyday analogy before we go deeper. A theme wave is a **crowded nightclub on a one-way street.** The narrative is the rumour that this is the hot club tonight; everyone rushes in, the place fills, it *feels* amazing precisely because it is full. But there is one door, the street is narrow, and the moment the vibe turns — a fight, a rumour the police are coming, the headliner cancels — everyone tries to leave through that single door at once. The same crowd that made it feel exclusive on the way in becomes a crush on the way out. Low float is the narrow street; FOMO is the rush in; margin is everyone having pre-paid a tab they now have to settle. The wave is the whole evening compressed into six weeks.

### The four ingredients

Every theme wave on VN-Index is assembled from the same four parts. Take any one of them away and you do not get a wave — you get, at most, a sleepy drift.

- **A believable story (the narrative).** It has to be *plausible enough that a smart amateur would repeat it*. Public investment ramping up and lifting construction and materials. A global commodity spike (steel, fertiliser, hog prices) flowing to a Vietnamese producer. A regulatory or index event — a "Diamond" ETF inclusion, an FTSE/MSCI upgrade theme, a state-divestment story. A penny real-estate "land bank" dream where a tiny company supposedly sits on hugely valuable land. The story does not have to be *true*; it has to be *repeatable*.
- **A low free float.** The **free float** is the slice of shares actually available to trade — total shares minus the big blocks locked up by founders, the state, and strategic holders. A company can have a billion shares outstanding but only 15% genuinely floating. The smaller the float, the less buying it takes to move the price, because the price is set at the margin by whoever is willing to trade *today*. Low float is the lever; a story on a huge-float blue chip barely moves it, but the same story on a low-float midcap detonates.
- **Retail FOMO.** **FOMO** is *fear of missing out* — the visceral pull a retail investor feels watching a stock rip higher every day while their own portfolio sits flat. On a market that is roughly 85-90% retail by volume, FOMO is not a fringe emotion; it is the dominant flow. The *thầy* rooms, the green-candle screenshots, the friend who "made 80% in three weeks" — these are FOMO's delivery system. FOMO is what converts a quiet markup into a stampede.
- **Margin leverage.** **Margin** (*ký quỹ*, borrowed money from your broker to buy more stock than your cash allows) pours accelerant on both legs. On the way up, rising prices mark up the collateral, which unlocks more borrowing room, which funds more buying. On the way down, falling prices breach the maintenance floor and trigger forced selling. We covered this flywheel in detail in [the liquidity and margin-cycle post](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/liquidity-and-the-margin-cycle-vietnam); here it is the thing that makes a wave *violent* rather than merely fast.

Hold these four in your head, because the entire rest of the post is just watching them interact across six stages.

### Why VN-Index is unusually wave-prone

It is worth pausing on *why* these waves are so much more frequent and violent on the Ho Chi Minh and Hanoi exchanges than on a developed market like the United States or even most of Vietnam's regional peers. Three structural features stack the deck. First, the **retail share of volume** is extraordinarily high — roughly 85-90% of daily turnover comes from individual investors, versus a small minority on developed markets where institutions, pensions, and index funds dominate. Retail flow is more emotional, more momentum-chasing, and far more susceptible to social proof than a pension fund rebalancing on a schedule, so the FOMO ingredient is structurally amplified. Second, the **margin culture** is pervasive and the leverage is fast: a large share of retail accounts run on broker credit, the maintenance triggers are tight, and the daily price-limit bands (±7% on HOSE) mean that a stressed name can only fall 7% a session, which sounds protective but actually *prolongs* the agony — a wave that needs to fall 50% takes many consecutive floor-locked sessions to get there, with sellers trapped the whole way. Third, the **low-float midcap universe** is enormous: a great many HOSE and HNX names have most of their shares locked up by founders, the state, and strategic blocks, leaving a thin tradeable slice that a modest amount of coordinated buying can light up. Stack a momentum-prone retail majority on top of pervasive leverage on top of a deep bench of low-float vehicles, and you have an ecosystem almost purpose-built to manufacture theme waves. This is not a moral failing of Vietnamese investors; it is the predictable output of the market's microstructure, and recognizing it as *structural* is the first step to not being its victim.

### Who actually creates a wave

A wave does not assemble itself by accident every time. While some are genuinely organic — a real catalyst plus a real crowd — many of the most explosive low-float pumps have an *operator* behind them, a coordinated group (the Vietnamese retail scene calls such an actor an *đội lái*, literally a "driving team") that deliberately engineers the arc. The mechanics are simple and old: quietly accumulate a low-float name over stage 2; seed and amplify a narrative through the *thầy* rooms and friendly media in stage 3; let FOMO and margin do the markup for free; and then distribute the accumulated inventory into that manufactured demand in stage 5 before letting it collapse. The operator's edge is not insight into the business — it is *control of the float plus control of the narrative timing*. You do not need to prove any specific case to understand the lesson: when you are buying the loud, parabolic part of a low-float wave, you should assume that *somebody* on the other side knows far more about the supply of shares than you do, and is selling to you on purpose. That assumption is uncomfortable, and it is also the single most protective belief a retail trader can hold about speculative midcaps.

### The anatomy, stage by stage

A theme wave is not a random pump; it follows a recognizable arc. Naming the stages is the single most useful skill for surviving one, because your odds of profit are completely different in stage 2 than in stage 5. The six stages:

1. **Catalyst.** A specific event lights the fuse: a policy headline, a commodity print, an index-event rumour, a master-plan announcement, an unusually large block trade. Before the catalyst, the stock is quiet and cheap. The catalyst is the spark; it does not, by itself, guarantee a wave.
2. **Accumulation (smart money).** Quietly, before the crowd notices, informed buyers — the people closest to the story, sometimes including insiders and the operators who *create* waves for a living — accumulate shares. Price drifts up on rising-but-not-spectacular volume. There are no headlines yet. This is the only stage where you can buy with a real edge, and almost no retail trader is in it, because there is nothing exciting to see.
3. **Markup plus FOMO.** The price breaks out, ceiling prints start appearing, and the move becomes *visible*. Now the media and the *thầy* rooms pick it up. Each up-day pulls in more buyers; the rising price is itself the advertisement. Volume explodes. This is the loud, euphoric phase everyone remembers — and the phase where most retail finally buys, because this is when it finally *feels* safe.
4. **Margin peak.** As the price runs, leveraged buyers pile in to chase it, and total margin against the stock (and the sub-sector) hits its maximum. The book is fully stretched — *căng margin*. This is the point of maximum vulnerability: everyone who was going to buy has bought, and they bought with borrowed money, so the marginal buyer is exhausted exactly when leverage is highest.
5. **Distribution.** The smart money that accumulated in stage 2 now *sells* — into the eager FOMO and leveraged demand of stages 3-4. The price can still be high or even making marginal new highs while this happens, which is precisely what makes distribution so deceptive: heavy volume with the price *not really going anywhere* is the tape's signature of large holders handing shares to the crowd. The float quietly changes hands from strong to weak holders.
6. **Collapse.** The marginal buyer is gone, the smart money is out, and the first real down-day breaches margin floors. Forced selling begets lower prices, which breach more floors, which beget more forced selling — the flywheel in reverse. The low-float, low-liquidity feature that made the markup so easy now makes the collapse brutal: there is no depth to absorb the selling, so the stock gaps and locks at the floor with no bids. Within weeks it round-trips most or all of the move.

We will draw this arc explicitly in a moment. The crucial, uncomfortable truth embedded in it: **the wealth in a wave flows from the people who buy in stages 3-5 to the people who bought in stage 2.** The wave does not create value; it *redistributes* it, from late and leveraged buyers to early and patient sellers.

### Why retail systematically buys late

It is tempting to think "I will just buy early next time." Almost nobody does, and the reasons are structural, not a matter of willpower.

- **Recency bias.** Humans extrapolate the recent past. After six green days, the brain's pattern-matcher screams "this goes up," precisely when the move is most extended. The evidence that feels most compelling (a long run of up-days) is the evidence that the wave is *late*, not early.
- **Social proof.** When a stock is quiet and cheap (stage 2), nobody is talking about it, so there is no social signal to buy. By the time twenty thousand people in a Zalo room are posting their gains (stage 3-4), the social proof is overwhelming — and the stock is already up 80-120%. Social proof peaks exactly when the risk-reward is worst.
- **Fear of missing out.** The pain of watching others get rich while you sit out is acute and immediate; the pain of a future loss is abstract and deferred. So the emotional ledger pushes you to buy *now*, at the top, rather than wait. FOMO is not stupidity; it is a well-documented bias that the wave's whole machinery is built to exploit.

Put these together and you get the iron regularity of speculation: the crowd's buying is heaviest in stages 3-5, which is exactly where the expected return is negative. The wave does not need to trick anyone; it just needs human psychology to run its default program, which it reliably does.

There is a second-order version of the same problem that traps people on the *way down*, not just the way up: **the disposition effect and anchoring.** Once you have bought a wave, your brain anchors to your entry price and to the highs you watched it hit. When the stock falls from 25,000 back to 18,000, the loss feels temporary — "it was just at 25, it'll come back" — and the prospect of selling at a loss is so aversive that you hold, hoping. This is why the late FOMO buyer so rarely cuts at a small loss and so often rides the whole collapse to the bottom: the same anchoring that made the top feel safe makes the down-leg feel like a buying opportunity rather than an exit. The disciplined trader's pre-committed hard stop exists precisely to override this instinct, because in the heat of a collapse the instinct is always to hold, and the instinct is always wrong.

### The role of margin: amplifying both legs

Margin is worth its own paragraph because it is what turns a fast move into a violent one and a correction into a crash. On the way up, leverage is reflexive in your favour: a rising price marks up your collateral, your broker extends more buying power, you buy more, the price rises further. A sub-sector can therefore run *further* than unleveraged demand alone would carry it, because the leverage manufactures additional demand out of paper gains. But the same mechanism is merciless in reverse. When the price falls, your equity (the market value of the position minus the fixed loan) takes the *entire* loss first, because the loan does not shrink. A modest price drop produces a large equity drop; if equity falls through the maintenance floor (commonly around 30% in Vietnam), the broker issues a **margin call** and force-sells. Across a whole sub-sector running on margin, millions of maintenance floors are bunched together, so one real down-day trips them en masse, and the resulting forced selling is itself the next down-day. Leverage does not just make the wave bigger; it makes the collapse *self-accelerating*. We will do the arithmetic of this trap in a worked example below.

### The float arithmetic: why a little money moves a lot of price

It is worth making the low-float ingredient quantitative, because the intuition that "small companies move more" understates how extreme the effect is. Price on any exchange is set at the *margin* — by whoever is willing to transact at the moment, not by the average opinion of all shareholders. The people who own a block and are not selling exert *zero* pressure on the price today; only the actively-traded float matters. So the relevant denominator is not the company's total market value but the *value of shares that actually change hands in a day relative to the value people want to buy*. When the float is thin, a given amount of incoming demand chases a tiny supply, and the price has to gap up to find sellers.

#### Worked example: float versus blue-chip — the same money, different price impact

Take 50 billion dong of fresh buying (~\$1.97mn at \$1 = 25,400 VND) arriving over a week. Point it at a blue chip with, say, 80 billion dong of average daily trading value: the 50 billion is spread across the week, never more than a fraction of any day's turnover, and it barely nudges the price — maybe a percent or two, absorbed by the deep order book. Now point the *same* 50 billion dong at a low-float midcap whose normal daily turnover is 5 billion dong. That buying is *ten times a normal day's volume* arriving at once; there are simply not enough willing sellers near the current price, so the order walks up through empty levels, printing ceilings, and the stock can rip 30-50% in that week on the identical amount of money. The intuition: low float is a price *amplifier* — the thinner the tradeable supply, the more violently a fixed amount of demand moves the quote, which is exactly why operators and narratives target thin-float names rather than blue chips. The same property that makes the markup easy makes the collapse merciless, because the amplifier runs in both directions.

### The honest odds

So what actually happens to the typical retail participant in a wave? The honest answer, supported by the structure above and by the broad evidence on retail speculation worldwide: **most late buyers lose.** Not because they are stupid, but because the geometry is against them. The early accumulators have a real edge and mostly win. The disciplined trend-followers who get in during the early markup and have the discipline to exit can win. But the bulk of the volume — the FOMO buyers of stages 3-5 — are buying from the winners, near the top, with leverage, into a stock with no liquidity to escape through. The wave is, in expectation, a transfer of wealth from this group to the first two. Riding a wave can be done, but it is a discipline game played against the odds, not a free lottery ticket. Everything in the playbook section is about tilting those odds — or, far more often, recognizing that they cannot be tilted enough and standing aside.

## How a wave looks on the tape

Here is the stylized shape of the price path through the six stages, so you can recognize it in the wild.

![Stylized theme wave price path indexed showing accumulation markup distribution collapse](/imgs/blogs/theme-waves-song-nganh-how-speculative-pumps-work-2.png)

The chart traces a stylized wave indexed to 100 at the start. Through the early weeks the price grinds up modestly (accumulation), then accelerates into a steep, near-vertical markup as FOMO and leverage pile in, tops out around 260 (a +160% move), churns sideways-to-down on heavy volume (distribution), and then falls off a cliff back toward 90 (collapse, a round-trip and then some). Two features are diagnostic. First, the *acceleration*: a healthy uptrend is a steady slope, but a wave goes parabolic — the angle steepens until it is nearly vertical, which is the tape screaming that demand has become reflexive and unsustainable. Second, the *symmetry of the down-leg*: because the float is thin and the buyers were leveraged, the collapse is roughly as fast as the markup, often faster. A genuine value re-rating decays slowly; a wave round-trips violently. If you find yourself in a chart that looks like this and you cannot clearly say which stage you are in, the safe default is to assume you are late.

#### Worked example: the wave math

A midcap starts at **10,000 dong** and runs to **25,000 dong** — a clean **+150%** — then collapses **-60% from the top** back to 10,000. Watch how *timing*, not the stock, decides everything.

- **The early/trend buyer** gets in at **12,000** (during the early markup, after the breakout confirmed) and sells at **23,000** (into the strength, not at the exact top). Gain per share = 23,000 − 12,000 = **11,000 dong**, or **+91.7%**. On a 200 million dong position (~\$7,874 at \$1 = 25,400 VND), that is **+183 million dong**, about **+\$7,200**.
- **The late FOMO buyer** gets in at **24,000** (chasing the ceiling prints in stage 4) and finally capitulates at **12,000** on the way down. Loss per share = 24,000 − 12,000 = **12,000 dong**, or **−50%**. On the same 200 million dong position, that is **−100 million dong**, about **−\$3,937**.

Same stock, same wave, same six weeks. The early buyer nearly doubled; the late buyer halved. Nothing about the *company* explains the difference — only *when each one bought and sold*. This is the entire game in two numbers, and it is why "which stage am I in?" is the only question that matters in a wave.

The figure below puts the two buyers side by side so the divergence is unmistakable.

![Who wins versus who loses early trend buyer doubles late FOMO buyer halves](/imgs/blogs/theme-waves-song-nganh-how-speculative-pumps-work-4.png)

On the left, the early/trend buyer enters at 120 (early markup, after the breakout confirmed) and exits at 230 (into strength, before the obvious top) — a near-double, shown in green because it is a gain. On the right, the late FOMO buyer enters at 240 (chasing ceiling prints at the margin peak) and exits at 120 (capitulating on the way down) — a halving, shown in red because it is a loss. The two buyers traded the *same stock in the same wave*; the only thing that differed was their entry and exit timing, and that timing was driven by *when each one felt safe enough to act*. The early buyer acted when it was quiet and scary; the late buyer acted when it was loud and comfortable. The market systematically rewards the first behaviour and punishes the second, which is the deepest and least intuitive truth about speculation: the moment a wave feels safest to buy is usually the moment it is most dangerous.

## The six stages, drawn out

The timeline below lays the six stages end to end so you can map any real wave onto it.

![Six stage timeline of a theme wave catalyst accumulation markup margin peak distribution collapse](/imgs/blogs/theme-waves-song-nganh-how-speculative-pumps-work-3.png)

Reading left to right: the **catalyst** lights the fuse; **accumulation** is the quiet, edge-rich phase where smart money builds positions on rising-but-calm volume; **markup plus FOMO** is the loud, parabolic, media-fuelled climb where most retail buys; the **margin peak** is the moment of maximum leverage and maximum vulnerability; **distribution** is the deceptive churn where the early holders sell into the crowd's demand while the price stalls near the highs; and **collapse** is the self-accelerating unwind back toward the start. The colours encode the honest risk: the early stages are blue (the setup), the FOMO and margin-peak stages are amber (rising risk), and distribution and collapse are red (the loss zone for the crowd). Notice where the volume and the headlines peak — stages 3-5 — and notice that that is exactly where the expected return turns negative. The whole skill of surviving a wave is locating yourself on this line *honestly*.

#### Worked example: margin in a wave — the leverage trap

Suppose you buy the top of a wave on **2x margin**: you put up **100 million dong** of your own cash and the broker lends you another **100 million**, so you hold **200 million dong** of stock (~\$7,874 at \$1 = 25,400 VND). Now the wave collapses and the stock falls **30%** from where you bought.

- The position value drops 30% × 200 million = **−60 million dong**, to **140 million dong**.
- The loan is fixed at **100 million dong** — it does not shrink. So your **equity** = 140 − 100 = **40 million dong**, down from your original 100 million. A 30% *stock* drop wiped out **60% of your equity**.
- Your equity ratio = 40 ÷ 140 ≈ **28.6%**, below the ~30% maintenance floor. That triggers a **margin call**: post cash immediately or the broker force-sells — and in a collapsing, illiquid wave, the force-sell happens at the floor price, locking in the loss and adding to the downward pressure.

A 30% move against you cost you 60% of your money *and* the choice of when to exit. That asymmetry — losses landing entirely on your thin equity, then a forced sale at the worst possible moment — is the leverage trap, and it is why buying a wave's top on margin is the single most reliable way to be wiped out on VN-Index.

## Common misconceptions

Three lies people tell themselves while a wave is running. Each one feels true in the moment and is corrected by a number.

### Misconception 1: "The wave found real value — that's why it's up"

The seductive story is that the market is *discovering* something the spreadsheets missed, and the price is just catching up to true worth. Sometimes a kernel of the narrative is real (public investment really is ramping; the commodity really did spike). But the *magnitude* of a wave is almost never justified by fundamentals. A construction midcap whose earnings might grow 20-30% in a good year does not become worth 150% more in six weeks because of an earnings change — it becomes 150% more *expensive* because of a flow change. The tell: ask "what cash-flow number changed by enough to justify this price?" In a wave, there is no such number. The price moved on demand for the shares, not on the value of the business, and demand for shares is exactly the thing that reverses.

### Misconception 2: "I can always get out in time"

Everyone planning to ride a wave assumes they will sell near the top. The structure makes this far harder than it sounds, for two reasons. First, the same low float and thin liquidity that made the markup so easy mean there is *no depth to sell into* when everyone heads for the exit at once — the stock locks at the floor (-7%) with no bids, and you cannot get filled at any price you would accept. Second, *everyone* in the wave believes they will get out in time, which is logically impossible: the late buyers, by definition, are the exit liquidity for the early sellers, and somebody has to be holding when it collapses. If you are not clearly early, you are statistically the exit liquidity. The honest version: in an illiquid collapsing midcap, "I'll get out in time" is a plan that fails for the majority precisely because it is the majority's plan.

### Misconception 3: "Huge volume means it's safe — lots of people can't be wrong"

Heavy volume feels like confirmation: so many people trading it, surely it is solid. But volume cuts both ways, and *where in the wave* the volume appears is everything. Rising volume during the early markup can confirm a trend. But the heaviest volume of the entire wave typically prints during **distribution** — because that is when large holders are unloading their entire positions into the crowd. Climactic volume near the highs, with the price churning sideways rather than making clean new highs, is not a safety signal; it is the *opposite* — it is the signature of smart money handing the bag to the crowd. "Lots of people are trading it" near the top means "lots of people are being sold to," not "lots of people can't be wrong."

### Misconception 4: "It's already up so much, it must be a great company"

There is a quiet cognitive trap in watching a stock triple: the price *becomes* the evidence. "It went up 200% — the market clearly sees something special." This reverses cause and effect. In a theme wave, the price did not rise *because* the company is good; it rose because the four ingredients aligned and demand for the shares outran the thin supply. The 200% is evidence about *positioning and float*, not about *business quality*. Plenty of waves run on companies with deteriorating fundamentals, related-party accounting, or no real business at all — the worse the fundamentals, often the *easier* the wave, because a low-quality company tends to have a smaller, more concentrated float and a more outlandish (hence more shareable) story. The discipline: never let the price action substitute for an independent judgment of what you are buying. If your entire bull case is "the chart is up," you do not have a case; you have a position you cannot defend the moment the chart turns.

## How it shows up on VN-Index

Theme waves are not a textbook abstraction on the Ho Chi Minh and Hanoi exchanges; they are a recurring feature, and the clearest case study is the 2021 penny-stock mania and its 2022 unwind.

### The 2021 penny-stock waves

Through 2021, with interest rates low, a flood of new retail accounts opening (the *F0* generation — first-time investors who entered during the pandemic), and broker margin expanding fast, VN-Index ran one of the great retail bull markets in its history. The headline blue chips rose, but the *spectacular* moves were in the speculative small-caps and penny stocks. Whole baskets of low-float real-estate, construction, and "land-bank" names ran several-hundred-percent in months on narratives — public-investment ramps, master-plan rezonings, and the catch-all dream that a tiny company sat on undervalued land. Stock-tip rooms on Zalo and Facebook swelled into the tens of thousands of members, the *thầy* phenomenon went mainstream, and ceiling-locked penny names became a national pastime. This was the markup-plus-FOMO stage playing out across an entire tier of the market at once. The narrative did not need to be individually true for each ticker; the *meta-narrative* — "small caps go up, just buy the dip" — was self-fulfilling as long as new money kept arriving.

### The flavours of theme wave

Not every wave wears the same costume, and recognizing the *type* helps you read how durable the narrative is. The Vietnamese market has produced at least four recurring flavours. The **public-investment wave** — construction, materials, and stone/aggregate names that run whenever the government signals an infrastructure push — has a genuine fundamental kernel (public spending really does flow to these sectors) but the *price* runs far ahead of the actual contract awards, which arrive slowly and lumpily. The **commodity wave** — steel, fertiliser, hog producers, oil-and-gas service names — runs when a global commodity spikes; here the kernel is real and fast (a fertiliser price doubling really does lift a producer's margins), but it is also mean-reverting, so the wave tops when the commodity tops, often before the headline earnings even print. The **index/upgrade wave** — names expected to be added to a major ETF basket, the "Diamond" inclusion theme around foreign-ownership-capped large caps, or the perennial FTSE/MSCI emerging-market upgrade story — runs on a one-time mechanical buyer (the fund that *has* to buy on inclusion), which means the catalyst is a known date and the wave often "sells the news" when that date arrives. And the **penny real-estate / land-bank wave** — the purest speculation, a tiny developer supposedly sitting on undervalued land — has the weakest fundamental kernel and therefore the most violent round-trip, because there is essentially nothing underneath the price when the story dies. Knowing which flavour you are looking at tells you where the "real" ceiling is: a commodity wave is tethered (loosely) to the commodity, an index wave to the inclusion date, but a land-bank wave is tethered to nothing but belief, and belief is the most fragile collateral there is.

### The role of margin at the 2021-22 top

The fuel under all of it was margin, and the broker margin balance is the cleanest proxy for how stretched the market got.

![Broker margin balance VND trillion 2020 to 2022 peaking with the penny wave top](/imgs/blogs/theme-waves-song-nganh-how-speculative-pumps-work-5.png)

The chart shows total broker margin balance climbing from roughly 90 trillion dong in 2020 to a peak around 195 trillion dong in 2021 — more than doubling — before falling to about 122 trillion dong through the 2022 crash. The 2021 margin peak coincided almost exactly with the penny-stock top: leverage was maxed out across the market right as the speculative names were making their final highs. That is the margin-peak stage made visible at the index level — the whole market *căng margin* at once. When the turn came, the deleveraging was the mirror image: forced selling cascaded through the most leveraged, lowest-float names first, which were precisely the penny speculations that had run the hardest. The same balance that powered the melt-up powered the collapse, in reverse and at speed.

### The 2022 unwind

In 2022 the regime flipped. Global rates rose, domestic policy tightened, and — critically for the speculative names — a corporate-bond and confidence crisis hit several large property developers, puncturing the meta-narrative that small-cap property "land banks" were a one-way bet. The penny waves collapsed in textbook fashion: distribution gave way to forced selling, low float meant no liquidity to escape through, and basket after basket of speculative midcaps locked at the floor on consecutive sessions. Many round-tripped their entire 2021 gains and then some, exactly as the stylized price path predicts — a violent down-leg roughly as fast as the markup. The investors who were hurt most were the F0 cohort who had bought the markup with margin in 2021 and were still holding (or had averaged down) into 2022: late buyers, leveraged, in illiquid names, which is the precise recipe for the worst possible outcome. The 2022 unwind is the collapse stage written across a whole market, and it is the single best lesson on VN-Index in *why the stage you buy in decides your fate*.

One detail from the unwind deserves emphasis because it generalizes to every wave collapse: the **price-limit bands turned the exit into a multi-day trap.** On HOSE, a stock can fall at most 7% in a single session before it locks at the floor. That band sounds like a circuit-breaker that protects investors, but for a holder in a collapsing low-float name it does the opposite — it *guarantees* you cannot get out fast. When sellers vastly outnumber buyers, the stock simply opens at the floor, locks there with millions of shares queued to sell and no bids underneath, and closes at the floor, day after day. A name that "needs" to fall 50% to clear cannot do it in one capitulatory session the way a US stock might; it grinds down 7% a day across many sessions, with every holder watching their account bleed and unable to transact. The limit band converts a sharp, cathartic crash into a slow, agonizing bleed, and it concentrates the damage on exactly the people who waited for confirmation before selling — which is, again, the late FOMO crowd. The lesson is brutal and simple: in a low-float wave, the time to decide to sell is *before* the floor-locks start, because once they start, the decision is no longer yours to make.

#### Worked example: the distribution wealth transfer

Put concrete numbers on who got what. Suppose the early/smart money accumulated **5 million shares at an average of 12,000 dong** during stage 2 — a cost of 5,000,000 × 12,000 = **60 billion dong** (~\$2.36mn at \$1 = 25,400 VND). During distribution, they sell those same 5 million shares into FOMO demand at an average of **24,000 dong** — proceeds of 5,000,000 × 24,000 = **120 billion dong**, about **\$4.72mn**.

- The early money's profit = 120 − 60 = **60 billion dong**, about **+\$2.36mn**, a clean double.
- That 60 billion dong of profit did not come from the company — it came *out of the pockets of the FOMO buyers* who paid 24,000 for shares that, weeks later, were worth 10,000. Those buyers collectively lost on the order of 5,000,000 × (24,000 − 10,000) = **70 billion dong** as the stock round-tripped.

The wave produced no new value; it *moved* roughly 60-70 billion dong from the late, leveraged crowd to the early accumulators. That is the wealth transfer in numbers — and it is why understanding distribution is not academic. When you buy the markup, you are very often buying the exact shares the smart money is selling.

### Why retail keeps ending up on the wrong side

The same dynamic repeats every cycle, and the figure below shows the loop that keeps producing it.

![Why retail buys late price rises media social proof FOMO retail piles in at top distribution](/imgs/blogs/theme-waves-song-nganh-how-speculative-pumps-work-6.png)

The loop is self-reinforcing and self-defeating. A rising price generates media coverage and social proof (the *thầy* rooms, the green-candle screenshots, the friend's bragged-about gains). That social proof triggers FOMO. FOMO drives retail to pile in — and because they pile in *after* the price has already risen, they pile in near the top, into the very shares the early holders are distributing. The loop's cruel feature is that each step *feels* like increasing safety (more coverage, more participants, more confirmation) while it is actually increasing risk (later stage, higher leverage, thinner remaining upside). The crowd is not irrational at any single step; it is following social proof and momentum, which are usually sensible heuristics. They just happen to be exactly wrong at the top of a low-float speculative wave, which is the one environment built to exploit them.

#### Worked example: position sizing — the only sane defense

Why does professional risk management obsess over *position size* rather than stock-picking when it comes to speculation? Because size is the one variable you fully control, and in a wave it dominates the outcome. Compare two traders, each with a **1 billion dong** portfolio (~\$39,370 at \$1 = 25,400 VND), each of whom buys a wave that subsequently collapses **-60%**.

- **Trader A sizes it as a 1% position**: 10 million dong in the wave. The -60% collapse costs 60% × 10 million = **6 million dong**, or **−0.6%** of the portfolio. A bruise. They survive easily and live to trade the next setup.
- **Trader B sizes it as a 10% position**: 100 million dong in the wave. The same -60% collapse costs 60% × 100 million = **60 million dong**, or **−6%** of the portfolio. Ten times the damage from the *identical* trade — and if it was on margin, far worse.

Same stock, same -60% wipeout, same entry timing. The only difference is size, and size is entirely your choice. The intuition: in a game where you *know* the average late participant loses and the collapse can be -60% or worse, the only sane way to play a wave at all is *tiny* — small enough that a total loss is a footnote, not a wound. Sizing is not a detail; it is the whole risk-management strategy in a single decision.

## The playbook: how to recognize and trade a wave

You will not stop theme waves from happening, and you cannot reliably value them. What you *can* do is locate the stage honestly, decide whether you have an edge, and size so that being wrong is survivable. Here is the concrete playbook.

### Step 1: Recognize the stage before you act

Everything starts with an honest read of *where in the six stages you are*. The signals:

- **Accumulation (early):** quiet, cheap, rising-but-calm volume, no media, no *thầy* chatter. This is the only stage with a buyer's edge — and it is psychologically the *least* tempting, because nothing exciting is happening. If you are not close enough to the story to have real conviction here, you are not early; you are guessing.
- **Markup plus FOMO (mid):** breakout, ceiling prints, exploding volume, media and rooms lit up. A disciplined trend-follower can play this *with a plan and a stop*, but understand you are now buying what the early money will soon sell to you.
- **Margin peak and distribution (late):** parabolic acceleration, climactic volume with the price churning sideways near the highs, *căng margin* talk everywhere, the *thầy* rooms at peak euphoria. This is the danger zone. The right action here is almost always to *not buy* — and if you are holding, to take profits into the strength rather than wait for the obvious top.
- **Collapse (terminal):** the first heavy red day off the highs, then floor-locks with no bids. The action here is to be already out. Trying to "buy the dip" in a collapsing low-float wave is catching a falling knife with no liquidity to catch it.

### Step 2: Only the disciplined early or trend player wins — be honest about which you are

The uncomfortable self-assessment: the people who make money in waves are (a) the early accumulators with a genuine information or conviction edge, and (b) the disciplined trend-followers who get in during the early markup, ride with a stop, and *actually exit* into strength rather than rounding-trip the whole thing. If you are neither — if you are buying because the chart is up and the room is excited — you are the third group, the FOMO crowd, and the odds say you lose. There is no shame in concluding "I have no edge here," and it is the single most profitable conclusion most retail traders never reach. Standing aside is a position, and in late-stage waves it is usually the winning one.

### Step 3: Size tiny — the position rule

If you do play a wave, size it so that a total loss is a footnote. The position-sizing worked example above is the rule: a 1% position that goes to zero costs you 1%; a 10% position that goes to zero costs you 10% and may end your year. Given that the realistic downside of a late wave is -50% to -100% and the collapse is fast and illiquid, your position should be small enough that you can lose *all* of it and barely flinch. If you cannot bring yourself to size it that small, that is a signal you are too emotionally invested in the narrative — which is itself a reason to stand aside.

### Step 4: Hard stops, and never on margin late

Two non-negotiable rules for anyone who insists on riding. First, a **hard stop**: a pre-decided price at which you sell no matter what, set *before* you enter, and honoured mechanically — because in a collapse there is no time to deliberate and no liquidity to negotiate. Second, **no margin on a late-stage wave.** Margin is the mechanism that turns a -30% drawdown into a -60% wipeout and a forced sale at the floor (see the leverage-trap example). Riding a wave with borrowed money near its top is not aggressive; it is a near-guaranteed way to hand your equity to a margin call. Cash only, tiny size, hard stop — those three together are the difference between a survivable speculation and a portfolio-ending one.

### Step 5: Know the invalidation

Finally, define what tells you the wave is over and the thesis is dead, *in advance*:

- **Parabolic blow-off then a heavy reversal day** (a big up-move that fails and closes red on record volume) is the classic top signal — the markup has exhausted itself.
- **Climactic volume with the price stalling** near the highs is distribution; the smart money is leaving.
- **The narrative going mainstream** — your non-investing relatives asking about the ticker, the *thầy* rooms at maximum euphoria — is a contrarian top signal: when everyone who could buy has bought, there is no marginal buyer left.
- **The first floor-lock with no bids** is the collapse confirming; if you are not already out, you are now trapped.

The ride-or-avoid decision compresses into a short checklist, shown below.

![Ride or avoid checklist by stage float margin level and your edge](/imgs/blogs/theme-waves-song-nganh-how-speculative-pumps-work-7.png)

Read each row as a filter. **Stage:** ride only if you are genuinely early or in a confirmed early trend; avoid if you are at the margin peak or later. **Float:** a low float that is starting to turn up can be rideable *early*, but a low float at the top is a trap with no exit liquidity. **Margin level:** if market-wide margin is already stretched (*căng margin*), the fuel for further upside is gone and the downside is primed — avoid. **Your edge:** if you have a real information or trend edge *and* the discipline to size tiny and honour a stop, you may carefully ride; if your only reason is "it's going up and everyone's buying," that is the textbook reason to avoid. The honest summary of the whole checklist: you ride a wave early, small, and with a stop, or you do not ride it at all — and most of the time, *not riding* is the correct, profitable choice.

### A note on reading the order book and the tape

Beyond the price chart, two finer-grained signals help you locate the stage on a low-float name, and both are about *how* the price moves rather than the level. The first is the **shape of the order book**. In a healthy early markup, you will see genuine two-way interest — buyers and sellers both stacking orders, the price grinding up as demand wins narrowly. Near a distribution top, the book often shows a peculiar signature: large sell orders absorbing wave after wave of market buying without the price advancing much, then those sell walls quietly refilling. That is a large holder feeding inventory to the crowd at a price they have decided is good enough; the price stalls precisely because supply is now unlimited at that level. The second signal is **how the stock behaves on red days**. In accumulation and early markup, dips are bought quickly and shallowly — the stock barely gives ground. As distribution sets in, the dips get deeper and the bounces weaker; the character of the pullbacks deteriorates even while the headline price is still high. When you start seeing the first dip that *does not* get bought — the one that closes on its lows on heavy volume — the wave's internal demand has broken, regardless of where the price closes that week. These are not infallible signals, and on a manipulated low-float name the tape can be deliberately painted to mislead, which is one more reason to size tiny: you are reading an adversarial tape with imperfect information, and the only fully reliable defense is a position small enough that being fooled does not hurt.

### A worked checklist you can run in thirty seconds

Before buying anything that is up sharply, run five questions. *What is the catalyst, and is it still ahead or already priced?* If the news is out and everyone knows it, you are probably late. *What is the float?* If it is a thin-float midcap that has already tripled, the easy markup is behind you and the trap is ahead. *Where is market-wide margin?* If brokers are at record balances and *căng margin* is the talk of every room, the fuel is spent. *What is my actual edge?* If the honest answer is "it's going up," you have none. *Can I size this at 1% and honour a hard stop?* If you cannot bring yourself to size it that small or to pre-commit an exit, you are emotionally captured and should not touch it. Five questions, thirty seconds, and most of the time the honest answers route you straight to "stand aside" — which, across a full cycle of waves, is the most profitable answer there is.

### The one-paragraph version

If you remember nothing else: a theme wave is a narrative-plus-leverage pump on a low-float midcap that runs 50-200% in weeks and then round-trips, and the money flows from the late, leveraged FOMO crowd to the early accumulators. Your entire edge is locating the stage honestly and sizing for survival. Buy early with conviction or trend-follow with a hard stop and tiny size — or, far more often, recognize you are late and stand aside. The wave does not care about your spreadsheet; it cares only about *when* you bought, and the difference between getting in at 120 and getting in at 240 is the difference between doubling your money and halving it.
