> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Sector rotation is the observable fact that market leadership keeps handing off from one industry to the next as the cycle, the liquidity, and the story all change, and on VN-Index that handoff is faster, more retail-driven, and more violent than almost anywhere else.
>
> - A fixed pool of money chasing returns *forces* rotation: to buy the next leader, somebody has to sell the current one. Money is conserved, so leadership is a relay, not a free-for-all.
> - Three engines drive it — relative earnings momentum, relative liquidity and flows, and the narrative or positioning. When all three line up behind one group, that group leads.
> - The canonical sequence is early-cycle (brokers, banks) into mid-cycle (steel, property) into late-cycle (energy, industrials) into defensives (utilities, staples). Vietnam runs this clock on fast-forward.
> - The number to remember: in 2021, Vietnamese brokers returned roughly **+110%**; in 2022 the same group fell about **−58%**. The biggest leader became the biggest loser inside twelve months. Chasing the last candle is how retail accounts get destroyed.

In the autumn of 2021, if you sat in a Hanoi coffee shop and listened to the table next to you, every other sentence was about *chứng khoán* — securities firms. Brokers like SSI, VND, and HCM were the hottest tickers on the Ho Chi Minh exchange (HOSE). They had doubled, then doubled again. New retail accounts were opening at a record pace — hundreds of thousands a month — and a huge share of that fresh money piled straight into the broker stocks, because brokers literally make more money when trading volume explodes, and trading volume was exploding. For a few weeks it felt like the only trade in Vietnam was "buy a broker and hold."

Then, almost without anyone ringing a bell, the conversation changed. The brokers stopped going up. The new money started flowing somewhere else — into steel (Hoa Phat, ticker HPG, the giant of the group), into real estate, into the mid-cap construction and materials names riding a public-investment story. The brokers chopped sideways and then started to leak lower while steel and property printed fresh highs. Anyone who kept holding only brokers watched the rest of the market run without them. Anyone who *bought* brokers in those final euphoric weeks was about to learn what the word "drawdown" means, because 2022 was coming.

That handoff — money pouring out of one hot group and into the next over a span of weeks — is **sector rotation**. It is one of the most important things a Vietnamese investor can learn to *see*, because the market does not go up or down as one blob. Underneath the VN-Index, leadership is constantly moving from industry to industry, and the investors who do well are usually not the ones who pick the single best stock. They are the ones who notice *which group is being handed the baton* and position before the crowd finishes the trade.

![Ring of Vietnamese sector groups with arrows showing money handing off clockwise from brokers to steel to property to industrials to energy to defensives](/imgs/blogs/sector-rotation-explained-leaders-and-laggards-1.png)

This post is about that relay. We will define exactly what rotation is and why a fixed amount of money chasing returns *forces* it to happen; the three engines that drive which group leads; the canonical early-mid-late-defensive sequence and how it maps onto Vietnamese sectors; why Vietnam's version is faster and more extreme than the textbook; how to spot a handoff early instead of buying the last leader at the top; and a concrete playbook for trading it. By the end you should be able to look at the VN-Index, point at the group that is leading, name the group most likely to take the baton next, and know what would prove you wrong.

A quick note on money, because we will use it constantly. Vietnam trades in dong (VND). As a reference point for readers who think in dollars, the exchange rate in mid-2026 is roughly **\$1 = 25,400 VND**. So a 500 million VND retail account is about **\$19,700**; a 1 billion VND account is about **\$39,400**; a company worth 250 trillion VND is about a **\$9.8bn** market cap. We will keep the primary numbers in dong (that is how the market actually quotes) and drop a dollar anchor whenever it helps you feel the size.

## Foundations: what sector rotation actually is

Start with the simplest possible picture. The VN-Index is not one thing; it is a basket of hundreds of companies, and those companies belong to *industries* — banks, brokers, real estate developers, steel mills, power producers, retailers, food and beverage makers, and so on. In Vietnamese these groups are called *nhóm ngành*, "industry groups," and the exchange classifies them using the ICB system (Industry Classification Benchmark) into about 19 sectors. The whole premise of this series is that you should think in those groups first and individual tickers second, because the stocks inside a group tend to move *together*: when the steel story is hot, HPG, HSG, and NKG all rise; when it cools, they all fall.

**A leader is a sector that is outperforming the index. A laggard is one that is underperforming it.** That is the entire vocabulary you need to start. Note the word *out*performing — it is always relative. In a roaring bull market, a sector can be up 20% and still be a laggard if the index is up 40%. In a brutal bear market, a sector can be down 10% and be a *leader* if the index is down 30%. Leadership is never about the raw price; it is about the price *relative to the market*. This is the single most important mental shift in the whole post, so hold onto it: rotation is a story about relative strength, not absolute price.

The tool that makes this concrete is the **relative-strength ratio**, sometimes written RS. You take the sector's index (or a representative stock) and divide it by the VN-Index. If that ratio is rising, the sector is leading. If it is falling, the sector is lagging. The ratio strips out "the whole market went up" and leaves only "did this group beat the market or not." When the ratio for brokers is climbing and the ratio for steel is falling, brokers are the leader and steel is the laggard — full stop, regardless of whether both are technically up in price.

#### Worked example: reading a relative-strength ratio

Suppose on January 1 the VN-Index sits at 1,200 and the securities sector index sits at 1,800. The ratio is 1,800 / 1,200 = **1.50**. Three months later the VN-Index has risen to 1,300 and the securities index has risen to 2,275. The new ratio is 2,275 / 1,300 = **1.75**. The ratio went from 1.50 to 1.75, a rise of about **+16.7%**. Even though *both* the index and the sector went up, the sector beat the market by that 16.7%: it is leading.

Now do the same for steel. On January 1 the steel index is at 1,000, so its ratio is 1,000 / 1,200 = 0.833. Three months later steel sits at 1,040, so its ratio is 1,040 / 1,300 = 0.800. The steel ratio *fell* from 0.833 to 0.800, about **−4%**, even though steel rose 4% in raw price. Steel is lagging. The intuition: the ratio answers "if I owned this instead of the index, would I be ahead?" — and that is the only question rotation cares about.

Before we get to *why* leadership rotates, it is worth being precise about *why a sector moves as a block in the first place*, because that is the thing that makes "thinking in groups" valid at all. Three forces bind a sector together. First, **shared inputs and outputs**: every steel company buys iron ore and coking coal and sells rolled steel, so when ore prices or construction demand move, the whole group's margins move the same direction at the same time. Second, **shared macro sensitivity**: every bank earns a spread on lending, so when the SBV changes the credit ceiling or the policy rate, every bank's NIM responds together. Third, and most important in Vietnam, **shared classification and flows**: when retail decides "I want exposure to property," they do not run a discounted-cash-flow model on one developer — they buy *the property group*, spreading the same wave of money across DXG, NLG, KDH, and the rest at once. The first two forces are fundamental and slow; the third is behavioral and fast, and it is why a Vietnamese sector can move 8% in a day as one body even when only one company in it reported news. If sectors did not move together, rotation would be a meaningless concept — you would just have a few thousand uncorrelated stocks. They *do* move together, which is exactly what makes leadership a property of *groups* and rotation a thing you can see and trade.

So why does leadership rotate at all? Why doesn't the best sector just keep winning forever? The deep answer is the one that gives this whole phenomenon its inevitability: **money is conserved.** At any moment there is a roughly fixed pool of money in the Vietnamese stock market — call it the float available to chase returns. (It is not perfectly fixed; new accounts add money, margin lending inflates it, foreigners add or withdraw it. But over a span of weeks it is approximately fixed.) That fixed pool is constantly hunting for the best return. When a group has already run a long way, two things happen: its expected forward return falls (it is no longer cheap, the easy money is made), and the marginal buyer starts looking elsewhere. The only way that marginal buyer can fund a purchase of the *next* group is to sell something — and the obvious thing to sell is the group that has already run. So money flows out of the old leader to fund the new one. Leadership is a relay because the baton (the money) can only be in one set of hands at a time.

#### Worked example: the fixed-money rotation

Take a retail investor — call her Lan — with a 500 million VND account (about **\$19,700**), fully invested in broker stocks after a great run. Lan now believes steel is the next leader. Here is the hard constraint: she has no spare cash. Her account is 500 million VND of brokers and 0 VND of cash. To buy 200 million VND (about **\$7,900**) of steel, she *must first sell* 200 million VND of brokers, because that is the only place the money can come from. The act of rotating into steel is simultaneously an act of rotating *out of* brokers. Multiply Lan by a few hundred thousand retail accounts all making the same decision in the same few weeks, and you get exactly what we saw in 2021: brokers stop rising (their buyers have become sellers) at the precise moment steel takes off (the sellers' money becomes steel's buyers).

The intuition is brutal and clarifying: in a market with a roughly fixed money pool, *every* new leader is funded by selling an old one. Rotation is not a choice the market makes; it is arithmetic.

That is the foundation. Rotation exists because (1) sectors move as groups, (2) leadership is relative, measured by the relative-strength ratio, and (3) a roughly fixed money pool chasing returns forces money out of stretched leaders and into the next group. Everything else in this post is about *which* group gets the baton next, and *when*.

## Why rotation happens: the three engines

"Money rotates" tells you *that* leadership moves. It does not tell you *where* it moves. To predict the next leader you need to understand the forces that decide which sector the marginal money flows toward. There are three of them, and when all three point at the same group, that group leads hard. When they conflict, leadership is murky and you should size down. Think of them as three engines that can be in gear or in neutral.

**Engine 1: relative earnings momentum.** The most durable reason a sector leads is that its profits are accelerating faster than everyone else's. A sector is not just a story; it is a stream of earnings, and the stock market is, over any horizon longer than a quarter, a weighing machine for those earnings. When steel prices and construction demand are both rising, a steel company's profit can double — its margin expands at the same time as its volume. That earnings acceleration is *relative*: what matters is not "steel earnings are up" but "steel earnings are up *more than banks', brokers', and retailers'*." The market constantly re-ranks sectors by who is growing fastest, and the fastest-accelerating group attracts the money that wants the highest forward return.

**Engine 2: relative liquidity and flows.** Earnings explain the durable, fundamental leg of rotation. Liquidity explains the violent, fast leg — and in Vietnam this engine is enormous. "Flows" means where the money is physically going: retail buying, foreign buying or selling (*room ngoại*, the foreign ownership room), margin lending (*ký quỹ*, borrowed money used to buy more stock), and ETF rebalancing. A sector with light liquidity and a small free float can be *moved* by a relatively small amount of money — a few hundred billion dong of buying can lift a small-cap group 20% in a week. The same buying barely nudges a giant like Vietcombank. So flows chase not just the best earnings but the best *bang per dong*: where can this money push price the most? That is why small and mid-cap "theme" groups can lead so explosively even when their fundamentals barely justify it. Liquidity is the accelerant.

**Engine 3: narrative and positioning.** Markets are stories told with money. A sector leads partly because a *narrative* takes hold — "public investment is about to surge, so construction wins"; "the State Bank is cutting rates, so brokers and property win"; "El Niño is coming, so power and water utilities benefit." The narrative gives the money a reason to move *now* and a reason to *believe* the move will continue, which is what turns a one-day pop into a multi-week trend. Positioning is the flip side: once a narrative is fully owned (everybody who was going to buy has bought), there is no marginal buyer left, the story is "priced in," and the group rolls over even if the narrative is still technically true. The most dangerous moment to buy a sector is when the narrative is loudest, because that is usually when positioning is most crowded.

Here is the practical synthesis. The most powerful, most reliable rotations happen when all three engines fire together: earnings are accelerating (engine 1), money is flooding in via retail and margin (engine 2), and a clean story explains why (engine 3). The 2021 broker run was all three at once — trading volume exploded so broker *earnings* genuinely soared, retail money flooded in on heavy margin, and the narrative ("a new generation of retail investors is here to stay") was irresistible. When only one engine fires — say a narrative with no earnings behind it — you get a *sóng ngành*, a "sector wave": a fast speculative pump that can be very profitable to ride and very dangerous to hold, because it has no fundamental floor when liquidity leaves.

It helps to see how the three engines *sequence* over a single cycle, because they do not fire simultaneously — they fire in a relay of their own, and that relay is what produces the orderly handoff. At the bottom of a cycle, engine 2 (flows) moves *first*: the SBV cuts rates, liquidity returns, and money rushes back into the market looking for the highest-beta home before any earnings have actually improved. That is why the first leg of a recovery is often a "liquidity rally" with no fundamental confirmation — brokers and banks ripping on flows alone. Then, a quarter or two later, engine 1 (earnings) catches up: the recovery shows up in real profits, and leadership shifts to the groups whose earnings are *actually* accelerating (steel, property), which gives the move a durable foundation. Engine 3 (narrative) runs alongside both, loudest near the top, when the story is most fully believed and most fully priced. The danger zone is the very end, when engine 1 has rolled over (earnings can no longer accelerate from a high base) but engines 2 and 3 are still roaring — flows and story with no earnings underneath. That configuration is the *sóng ngành* penny mania, and it is the market screaming "the cycle is ending" in the only language it has. Learning to hear that scream — flows and narrative without earnings — is worth more than any single stock pick.

#### Worked example: relative earnings momentum re-ranks the leader

Make engine 1 concrete with two sectors and their forward earnings. Suppose steel companies in the group are collectively expected to earn 20 trillion VND this year (about **\$790mn** in aggregate net profit) and, on a strong construction cycle, 30 trillion VND next year — earnings growth of **+50%**. Banks in the group are expected to earn 120 trillion VND this year and 138 trillion VND next year — growth of **+15%**. Banks earn far more in absolute terms, but rotation cares about the *acceleration*, and steel's +50% growth dwarfs banks' +15%. The marginal dong chasing the highest forward return tilts toward steel, even though banks are the bigger, "safer" group, because a stock's price tends to follow the *rate of change* of its earnings, not the level. The intuition: engine 1 ranks sectors by how fast profits are accelerating relative to each other, so a smaller group with rapidly accelerating earnings can out-lead a giant with steady ones.

#### Worked example: when the engines conflict

Imagine power utilities. Engine 1 (earnings): a drought is squeezing hydropower output, so utility earnings are actually *falling* — engine in reverse. Engine 2 (flows): retail finds utilities boring and low-beta, so flows are thin — engine in neutral. Engine 3 (narrative): there is a vague "defensive rotation" story, but it is weak. Now value that against brokers in an early-cycle recovery: earnings accelerating, retail flooding in, a loud rate-cut narrative — all three engines firing. The math is not subtle. Money worth 100 billion VND (about **\$3.9mn**) deciding between these two will flow toward brokers, because that is where all three engines point. Utilities only lead when the engines *flip* — when growth rolls over (brokers' earnings collapse), flows flee risk, and the defensive narrative finally gets loud. The intuition: don't ask "is this sector good?" Ask "are its three engines in gear *relative* to the alternatives right now?"

## The canonical sequence: early, mid, late, defensive

Because rotation is driven by the business cycle and by liquidity, leadership tends to move in a *recognizable order*. This is the famous "rotation clock" — the idea that as the economy and monetary policy move through their phases, leadership marches from one type of sector to the next in a roughly predictable sequence. It is not a perfect law (we will spend a whole section on why it is messier than it looks), but it is a powerful default map, and it is worth knowing cold. The version that matters for Vietnam looks like this.

![Pipeline of four stages early mid late defensive with the Vietnamese sector group and tickers in each stage node](/imgs/blogs/sector-rotation-explained-leaders-and-laggards-3.png)

**Early cycle — the recovery.** The economy is coming out of a slowdown. The State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) has cut interest rates, credit is starting to open up, and confidence is returning. The first sectors to lead are the *most rate-sensitive and highest-beta* ones, because they benefit immediately and most violently from cheaper money and rising risk appetite: **securities brokers** (their trading volumes explode and their own proprietary books gain) and **banks** (lower funding costs, recovering loan demand, expanding net interest margin — NIM, the spread between what a bank earns on loans and pays on deposits). Brokers are usually the very first movers because they are pure leverage on market activity: when volume turns up, their revenue turns up faster than anyone's. This is why "brokers lead the bull" is a Vietnamese market cliché — and clichés in markets are usually compressed truths.

**Mid cycle — the expansion.** Growth is now broad and self-sustaining. Demand for physical things is rising — buildings, factories, durable goods — and so leadership shifts toward the sectors that *make* and *finance* real-world expansion: **steel and basic resources** (HPG and friends, riding both volume and rising commodity prices), **real estate developers** (cheap credit plus rising presales — the cash a developer collects from buyers before a building is finished — funds a land-bank-fueled boom), and **construction and materials** (especially when public investment, *đầu tư công*, accelerates). This is the meat of the cycle, where the most durable earnings-driven leadership lives, because here engine 1 (earnings) is at full throttle.

**Late cycle — the overheat.** Growth is still strong but inflation is rising and the SBV starts to lean against it (tightening credit, nudging rates up). Now the leadership shifts again, this time toward sectors that *benefit from rising prices and tight supply* rather than cheap money: **oil and gas** (the PVN chain — GAS, PVD, PVS — wins when energy prices are high), **commodities and chemicals**, and **industrials and retail** that can pass higher prices through to customers. Late-cycle leadership is shorter and more nervous, because everyone knows the tightening will eventually bite.

**Defensive — the downturn.** Growth rolls over, the SBV is cutting again, and risk appetite collapses. Now money does not want excitement; it wants *safety and steady cash*. Leadership goes to the **defensives**: utilities (POW, REE — regulated, steady demand for power and water), consumer staples (VNM and food and beverage — people keep buying milk and instant noodles in a recession), and healthcare and pharma (demand for medicine does not depend on the cycle). These sectors usually *fall less* than the market in a bear phase, which — remember — makes them the *leaders* on a relative-strength basis even while everything is red.

Then the SBV cuts enough, the recovery begins, and brokers lead again. The clock has gone around once. The whole sequence is a clockwise relay: cyclicals and financials early, real assets mid, energy and commodities late, defensives in the downturn — and back to financials.

The crucial caveat, which we will hammer in the misconceptions section: this clock tells you the *order* leadership tends to follow, not the *timing*. The phases do not last equal lengths; some get skipped; the market often anticipates the economy by months. Use the clock to know *what comes next in the relay*, never to set your watch by it.

## Vietnam's version: faster, narrower, more violent

Everything above is the textbook, and the textbook was mostly written about big, liquid, institution-dominated markets like the United States. Vietnam runs the same fundamental physics — money is still conserved, the engines still drive leadership — but the *parameters* are cranked to extremes. Four structural features make Vietnamese rotation faster, narrower, and more violent than the textbook describes. If you only learn the textbook clock and apply it naively to VN-Index, you will be repeatedly run over.

**Retail dominance.** On most days, retail investors — individuals trading from their phones — account for the large majority of trading value on HOSE, often around 85–90%. This is the opposite of a market like the US, where institutions dominate. Retail money is faster, more emotional, and more herd-like than institutional money. It chases winners harder and dumps losers faster. The practical consequence: when a sector starts to lead, retail piles in *en masse* and the move becomes self-reinforcing and overshoots; when it starts to lag, retail flees *en masse* and the move undershoots. Rotation that might take six months in an institutional market can happen in six weeks here.

**Margin.** Vietnamese retail uses a lot of *ký quỹ* — margin, borrowed money from the broker used to buy more stock than your cash allows. Margin is rocket fuel for rotation, and it works in both directions. On the way up, margin lets a fixed amount of real money command a much larger amount of buying power, so leadership moves accelerate. On the way down, margin *forces* selling: when prices fall, brokers issue margin calls, and investors who cannot post more cash are sold out automatically — *giải chấp*, forced liquidation. That forced selling is why Vietnamese drawdowns are so sharp and so synchronized: a falling leader triggers margin calls, the calls trigger more selling, which triggers more calls. The 2022 collapse was, mechanically, a margin-call cascade as much as a fundamental repricing.

**A narrow index.** The VN-Index is dominated by a handful of huge stocks — Vietcombank (VCB) alone can be a high single-digit percentage of the index, and the top ten names plus the Vingroup complex swing the headline number enormously. Banks and real estate together are a massive share of the index weight. This narrowness means the *headline index* can be doing one thing while *most stocks* are doing another. The index can grind sideways because the big banks are flat, while a furious rotation rages through the mid-caps underneath. You cannot read rotation from the index level alone; you have to look *under* it, at breadth and at sector ratios.

**Theme and penny waves — sóng ngành.** Because of all of the above, Vietnam is famous for *sóng ngành*, "sector waves": fast, narrative-driven pumps that sweep through a whole industry group — often low-priced, low-quality "penny" stocks — and then collapse. A theme wave is engine 3 (narrative) plus engine 2 (liquidity) firing hard with little or no engine 1 (earnings) underneath. These waves can deliver 30–50% in two weeks and give it all back in one. They are real rotations — money genuinely moves into and out of the group — but they are the most dangerous kind to hold, because there is no earnings floor. Recognizing a *sóng ngành* for what it is (a liquidity-and-story wave, not an earnings-driven leadership change) is one of the most valuable skills a Vietnamese investor can develop.

There is a fifth, more practical feature worth naming because it shapes *how you read* the rotation: the settlement and data rhythm. Vietnam settles on **T+2** — when you buy a stock, it does not arrive in your account to sell until two trading days later. This sounds like a technicality, but it slows the *exit* relative to the entry: a retail crowd can pile into a sector instantly (buying is immediate) but cannot all sell instantly (the stock they bought two days ago is only just becoming sellable), which contributes to the overshoot-then-air-pocket pattern of Vietnamese sector waves. On the data side, the things you watch to *see* rotation are mostly available: daily sector index values (so you can compute relative-strength ratios), daily trading value by group, total market margin balances (published by the larger brokers and aggregated quarterly), and foreign net buy/sell by ticker (the *room ngoại* flows, watched obsessively because foreign buying of a few **\$10mn** in a thin sector is a real signal). You do not need exotic data to trade rotation here; you need the sector ratios, the volume, and the breadth — and the discipline to read them instead of the headline index.

Put these four together and you get the defining feature of VN-Index rotation: it is *fast, narrow, and overshooting in both directions.* The same sector can be the best performer in the market one year and the worst the next, by an enormous margin. The data make this almost cartoonishly clear.

![Heatmap of approximate annual total return by Vietnamese sector for 2021 through 2024 with green positive and red negative cells](/imgs/blogs/sector-rotation-explained-leaders-and-laggards-2.png)

Read that grid as a story of rotation, not a table of numbers. The leftmost column (2021) is the liquidity bull: brokers roughly **+110%**, steel about **+75%**, retail about **+55%** — the high-beta, early-and-mid-cycle groups led by a mile. The second column (2022) is the bond crisis and the margin-call cascade: *every* sector is red, but the ones that led hardest fell hardest — brokers about **−58%**, steel about **−52%**, real estate about **−48%**, while the defensives (utilities about −10%, food and beverage about −18%) fell least and were therefore the relative leaders. The third column (2023) is the broker-led recovery: securities back to roughly **+75%** as the SBV cut rates and volumes returned. The fourth column (2024) shows divergence — IT (FPT's structural-growth story) about **+80%** and retail about **+35%** leading, while real estate stayed weak. The same nine sectors, four wildly different leadership orders, in four consecutive years. That is rotation, and that is why owning one sector forever is not a strategy in Vietnam — it is a coin flip on which year you happened to pick.

## Spotting a handoff: new leaders, breadth, and the laggard tell

If rotation is inevitable and violent, the entire game becomes *seeing the handoff early* — recognizing that leadership is about to pass *before* the crowd finishes the trade, so you rotate into the new leader near the start of its run instead of buying the old leader near the end of its. This is hard, because the human instinct is exactly backwards: we are drawn to whatever just went up the most, which is precisely the group most likely to hand off the baton soon. Here are the tells that actually work.

**New highs in price vs new leaders in relative strength.** The most important distinction. A sector can keep making new *price* highs while it is already losing *relative-strength* leadership — its price ratio to the VN-Index peaks and starts to roll over *before* its absolute price does, because the rest of the market is now rising faster. The relative-strength ratio (the sector divided by the index) tops out first. That divergence — price still rising, ratio already falling — is the single earliest tell that a leader is about to hand off. Watch the ratio, not the price.

![Line chart of two relative-strength ratios crossing over with the leader rolling down and the challenger rising past it](/imgs/blogs/sector-rotation-explained-leaders-and-laggards-4.png)

The figure shows the handoff in its purest stylized form. Leader A's ratio to the VN-Index rises, peaks around month 4, and then rolls over — it is still a "good stock" in absolute terms for a while, but it is no longer beating the market. Challenger B's ratio grinds higher the whole time and crosses above Leader A around month 8. *That crossover is the handoff.* The investor watching ratios sees it coming for months: Leader A's ratio was deteriorating long before B crossed it. The investor watching only price sees nothing until B is obviously the new leader and the easy money is gone. This crossover is exactly what played out in late 2021 as the broker ratio rolled over while the steel and property ratios climbed.

**Breadth.** Breadth measures how *many* stocks are participating, as opposed to how the index level is doing. In a healthy rotation, as the old leader fades, breadth *broadens* — more sectors and more stocks make new highs, because the money leaving the old leader is spreading into new groups. In an *unhealthy* top, breadth *narrows* — the index is held up by fewer and fewer big names while most stocks are already falling. Narrowing breadth at a high is a warning that the rotation has run out of fresh groups to rotate *into*, which often precedes a broad decline rather than another handoff. On VN-Index, where the headline number is dominated by a few giants, watching breadth (the count of advancers vs decliners, the percentage of stocks above their moving averages) is essential precisely because the index can lie.

**The laggard-becomes-leader tell.** The most actionable signal. The next leader is very often a sector that has been a quiet laggard — beaten down, unloved, cheap — that suddenly starts *outperforming on rising volume*. The first sign of a handoff is not the new leader making headlines; it is a previously-ignored group's relative-strength ratio quietly turning up while volume picks up. This is the inverse of the price-high trap: instead of chasing the obvious winner, you are watching the unloved groups for the first flicker of relative life. When a laggard's ratio bottoms and turns while volume expands, the smart money has started rotating in before the story is obvious.

**A concrete weekly routine.** Tells are only useful if you actually check them, so here is the minimal weekly scan that operationalizes all of the above. Once a week, for the eight or nine sectors you track, compute each one's relative-strength ratio (sector index ÷ VN-Index) and note whether it is making higher highs, rolling over, or bottoming-and-turning. Rank them: the ones with rising ratios are your current and emerging leaders; the ones with falling ratios are fading; the ones bottoming on rising volume are your handoff candidates. Glance at breadth (advancers vs decliners, percent of stocks above their 50-day average) to know whether the market is broadening or narrowing. And note *where in the quality ladder* the leadership sits — quality blue-chips, or speculative penny waves. That single scan, fifteen minutes a week, tells you which group holds the baton, which group is reaching for it, and whether the whole relay is healthy or about to break. You are not predicting; you are *reading the relay as it happens*, which is the only edge that survives Vietnam's speed.

**The one thing to avoid: chasing the last candle.** "Chasing the last candle" is buying a sector *because* it just went up the most — buying the green candle after the move, with no thought to where the group sits in its relative-strength cycle. It feels safe (you are buying the strongest thing!) and it is the single most expensive habit in retail investing, because the thing that went up the most is usually the thing closest to handing off. The discipline is to buy *relative strength that is turning up*, not relative strength that is already extended.

![Before-after panels comparing chasing the last leader near the top in red versus rotating into the new leader in green](/imgs/blogs/sector-rotation-explained-leaders-and-laggards-5.png)

The left panel is the chaser: sees brokers up 110%, FOMOs in near the top, rides the group down −58% in 2022, and sits trapped in a 50%-plus drawdown hoping for a recovery. The right panel is the rotator: notices the broker ratio rolling over, notices steel's ratio turning up on rising volume, *sells the old leader and uses that exact cash to buy the new one* (money conserved, redeployed), and rides the fresh trend. ## Common misconceptions

Rotation is intuitive enough that people overlearn it and then get hurt by the parts that are not true. Three myths in particular cost real money.

**Myth 1: "Rotation is predictable like clockwork."** The rotation *clock* is a map of the typical *order* of leadership; it is emphatically not a calendar. The phases do not last equal lengths — a mid-cycle expansion might run two years while a late-cycle overheat lasts three months. Phases get skipped: in 2022 there was barely an orderly "late cycle to defensive" rotation, because the bond crisis collapsed *everything* almost at once. And the market routinely *anticipates* the economy by six to nine months — brokers can start leading before GDP data confirm the recovery, because the market is forecasting, not reporting. If you trade the clock as if each phase arrives on schedule, you will be early, then wrong, then stopped out. The correct use is "given that brokers are leading now, steel and property are the *likely next* baton-holders — so watch their ratios for the turn," not "it is month nine, therefore buy energy."

**Myth 2: "The strongest sector keeps winning."** This is the chasing instinct dressed up as a strategy, and the heatmap demolishes it. The strongest sector in year one was, repeatedly, the *weakest* sector in year two. Brokers led 2021 by a mile (+110%) and were among the worst in 2022 (−58%). Strength is mean-reverting at the sector level precisely because of the fixed-money-pool logic: the more a group has run, the more stretched its valuation and the more crowded its positioning, which *lowers* its forward return. "It's the strongest, so I'll hold it forever" is the exact thought that puts you in the old leader at the moment of handoff. Strength tells you what *has* led; the relative-strength *turn* tells you what *will*.

#### Worked example: the cost of chasing the last leader

Suppose in November 2021 our investor puts 500 million VND (about **\$19,700**) into the broker group at the top, chasing the +110% move. Over 2022 the group falls about 58%. Her position is now worth 500 million × (1 − 0.58) = **210 million VND** (about **\$8,300**). She has lost 290 million VND, roughly **\$11,400**, more than half her capital, by buying the last leader.

Now the counterfactual. Instead of chasing brokers, she rotates that same 500 million VND into a defensive group — say utilities and staples — that falls only about 12% in 2022. Her position is 500 million × (1 − 0.12) = **440 million VND** (about **\$17,300**), a loss of 60 million VND (about **\$2,400**). The difference between chasing the last leader and rotating to where the cycle was heading is 440 − 210 = **230 million VND**, about **\$9,100**, on a 500 million VND account — nearly half the account, decided entirely by *which group* she held, not by stock-picking skill. The intuition: in a down year, the sector decision dwarfs the stock decision.

There is a quieter fourth myth worth puncturing: **"a sector that is cheap will lead because it is cheap."** Cheapness (a low price-to-earnings or price-to-book ratio) is not a catalyst. A laggard can stay cheap and lagging for years — Vietnamese real estate was "cheap" for most of 2022–2024 and kept underperforming because the bond hangover meant none of the three engines were firing. Valuation tells you the *size of the prize if* a sector leads; it does not tell you *when* it will, and rotation is fundamentally about the *when*. The handoff is triggered by relative strength turning up on volume (one or more engines re-engaging), not by a sector simply being inexpensive. Buy cheap-*and*-turning, never cheap-and-falling; "it's too cheap to fall further" is how value investors become bag-holders in a market that can stay irrational longer than they can stay solvent.

**Myth 3: "Rotation means the market is fine."** Investors comfort themselves during a top by saying "money isn't leaving, it's just rotating." Sometimes that is true — healthy rotation broadens, with money flowing into fresh groups. But late in a cycle, "rotation" can be the *symptom of distribution*: the money is rotating into fewer and fewer, lower-quality groups (the *sóng ngành* penny waves) precisely because the high-quality leaders have topped and there is nowhere good left to go. When the rotation is *down* the quality ladder — out of banks and into speculative small-caps — and breadth is *narrowing*, that is not a healthy market quietly recycling its money; it is the last stage before a broad decline. "It's just rotation" is comforting and sometimes a trap. Check the *direction* of the rotation (up or down the quality ladder) and the *breadth* before you relax.

## How it shows up on VN-Index

Abstractions are cheap; the value is in seeing rotation play out in the actual history of the market. Three episodes tell the whole story.

**2021 — the liquidity bull and the textbook rotation.** This is the cleanest rotation Vietnam has produced in recent memory, and it ran almost exactly to the clock, just on fast-forward. Coming out of the COVID slowdown, the SBV had cut rates hard and liquidity was abundant. The market rallied from around 1,100 on the VN-Index in early 2021 to a peak near 1,500 in early 2022. Underneath that rally, leadership relayed group to group: **brokers led first** (early-cycle, +110% on the year, all three engines firing — volumes and so broker earnings exploded, retail flooded in on margin, the "new retail era" narrative was deafening), then **steel** took the baton (mid-cycle, +75%, riding both record volumes and globally high steel prices), then **real estate and the mid-cap construction names** (riding cheap credit, presales, and the public-investment story), and finally a frenzy of small-cap *sóng ngành* — penny stocks of every theme ripping 30–50% in days on pure liquidity and story. That final penny-wave phase, with money rotating *down* the quality ladder into the junkiest names, was the tell that the cycle was ending. It was.

**2022 — the everything-down and the limits of rotation.** Then came the reckoning. A corporate-bond crisis (*trái phiếu doanh nghiệp* — corporate bonds — especially in real estate, where developers had funded land banks with short-term bonds they suddenly could not roll over) collided with a global tightening cycle and a domestic crackdown. The VN-Index fell from near 1,500 to under 900 — a roughly 40% peak-to-trough collapse. The crucial lesson for rotation: in a true systemic shock, *rotation breaks down* and almost everything falls together, but the magnitudes still ranked by the engines. The biggest prior leaders fell hardest (brokers −58%, steel −52%, real estate −48%) because they had the most margin, the most crowded positioning, and the most stretched valuations; the defensives fell least (utilities −10%, F&B −18%). There was no clean "rotate into the late-cycle winners" phase — the margin-call cascade was too fast. The lesson: rotation is a within-uptrend phenomenon; when the whole market enters a margin-driven crash, the only "rotation" that helps is the one *out of the market* (into cash and the most defensive groups) before the cascade.

**2023 — the broker-led recovery.** As 2023 progressed, the SBV pivoted back to cutting rates, liquidity slowly returned, and the cycle started over — and look at *which group led the recovery*: brokers again, roughly +75% on the year. This is the clock completing its circle. The same early-cycle logic that made brokers the first leaders in 2021 made them the first leaders again in 2023: when the SBV cuts and volumes turn up, the highest-beta, most rate-sensitive group moves first and hardest. An investor who knew the relay — "the SBV is cutting, so watch brokers to lead the recovery" — had a clean, repeatable, *evidence-backed* roadmap, not a guess. Then in 2024 leadership diverged again, with IT (FPT's structural-growth story, +80%) and retail (+35%) leading while real estate stayed mired in its bond hangover — the cycle's recovery rewarding different groups than the previous one did, exactly as the engines (this time, structural earnings momentum in IT) dictated.

![Grouped bar chart comparing 2021 and 2022 returns for brokers steel real estate banks and food and beverage](/imgs/blogs/sector-rotation-explained-leaders-and-laggards-6.png)

The grouped bars make the brutal symmetry impossible to miss: the height of the green 2021 bar predicts the depth of the red 2022 bar almost rank-for-rank. Brokers led up the most and led down the most; steel and real estate followed; banks and food and beverage, lower-beta groups, both rose less and fell less. This is the fixed-money-pool logic and the margin cascade rendered as a single picture: *the groups that absorbed the most leveraged, crowded money on the way up were the groups that disgorged it most violently on the way down.* If you take one image from this post into your trading, make it this one — because the chaser's mistake is to look at the tall green bars and conclude "those are the strong sectors to own," when the very same chart says those are the sectors with the most downside when the cycle turns.

#### Worked example: catching the handoff vs missing it

Put concrete numbers on the 2021-into-2022 handoff. Investor X holds 500 million VND (about **\$19,700**) in brokers through the whole period and never rotates: +110% in 2021 takes the account to 1,050 million VND (about **\$41,300**), then −58% in 2022 takes it to 1,050 × (1 − 0.58) = **441 million VND** (about **\$17,400**). Round trip: she is *below* where she started, having ridden a double up and a collapse down.

Investor Y rides brokers up in 2021 (to 1,050 million VND) but *catches the handoff* — she sees the broker ratio rolling over in late 2021 and rotates the full 1,050 million into defensives for 2022, which fall only about 12%: 1,050 × (1 − 0.12) = **924 million VND** (about **\$36,400**). The simple return difference between catching the handoff and not is 924 − 441 = **483 million VND**, roughly **\$19,000** — about the size of the *entire starting account* — earned not by picking better stocks but by rotating *out of* the topping leader and *into* the next cycle's relative winner at the handoff. The intuition: over a full rotation, when you exit the leader matters far more than which leader you picked.

## The playbook: trading the rotation

Everything so far is the *why* and the *what*. This is the *how* — a concrete, repeatable procedure for trading rotation on VN-Index. It is not a magic system; it is a discipline that keeps you buying turning-up relative strength instead of chasing the last candle. Five steps, then the invalidation.

**Step 1 — Confirm new leadership, do not guess it.** Do not rotate on a story alone. Require evidence: the candidate sector's relative-strength ratio (sector index ÷ VN-Index) has *turned up* and is making higher highs, *and* the move is on *rising volume* (real money, not a thin drift), *and* ideally at least one of the three engines is clearly behind it (accelerating earnings, a flow surge, or a fresh narrative). One engine plus a turning ratio on volume is a tradeable handoff; a story with a flat ratio is not. The point of demanding confirmation is to stop yourself from front-running a rotation that never comes — most "obvious next leaders" never actually take the baton.

**Step 2 — Ride it; let the leader lead.** Once you are in a confirmed new leader, *hold it while its relative strength keeps rising.* The mistake that mirrors chasing is *over-rotating* — jumping out of a healthy leader too early because you are nervous, then watching it run without you. The whole edge of rotation is that leadership *trends*: a group that has just taken the baton usually holds it for weeks or months. As long as the ratio is making higher highs, the trend is your friend. Do not sell a leader because it "went up a lot"; sell it because its *relative strength broke down* (step 4).

**Step 3 — Size by where you are in the relay (and by beta).** Position size should reflect both conviction and the violence of the group. Early-cycle broker-and-bank leadership, with all three engines firing, justifies a larger, more aggressive position — but brokers are the highest-beta group in the market, so a "large" position there still needs to be smaller in dong terms than the same conviction in a low-beta defensive, because it will move twice as hard against you if you are wrong. A *sóng ngành* penny wave (engine 3 plus engine 2, no engine 1) gets the *smallest* size and the *tightest* leash, because it has no earnings floor and can round-trip in days. Size is how you survive being wrong, and in a margin-heavy market, surviving is the whole game.

**Step 4 — Rotate out on a relative-strength breakdown, not a price high.** The exit signal is the mirror of the entry: when the leader's relative-strength ratio *stops making higher highs and turns down* — when it starts *lagging* the index even if its price is still drifting up — leadership is handing off, and it is time to rotate. Crucially, the sell decision funds the next buy: you sell the topping leader and use that exact cash to buy the next confirmed leader (money conserved, redeployed — the fixed-money-pool logic working *for* you instead of against you). You are not market-timing the whole index; you are continuously holding *whatever group currently has turning-up relative strength*, and rotating as the baton passes.

**Step 5 — Watch breadth and the quality ladder for the regime change.** The procedure above keeps you in the leading group through a healthy bull. The thing that breaks it is the *end* of the cycle, and the warnings are breadth narrowing (fewer stocks making new highs) and rotation moving *down* the quality ladder (out of quality leaders into speculative *sóng ngành* penny stocks). When you see leadership reduced to junk and breadth collapsing, the right rotation is not into the next sector — it is *out of risk*, into cash and the most defensive groups, ahead of the margin-call cascade. The 2022 lesson is that the most important rotation of an entire cycle is the one *out of the market*, and its tell is the rotation down the quality ladder that precedes it.

![Decision tree checklist asking whether the leader makes new relative highs and whether a laggard breaks out on volume leading to hold rotate or wait](/imgs/blogs/sector-rotation-explained-leaders-and-laggards-7.png)

The decision tree compresses the whole playbook into two questions you can ask every week. *Q1: is the current leader still making new relative highs (ratio rising)?* If yes, **hold** — the trend is intact, let the winner run. If no, ask *Q2: is a laggard breaking out on rising volume (a new ratio turning up)?* If yes, **rotate** — sell the old leader, buy the breakout with that cash. If no, **wait** in cash or the index — the old leader is fading but no new home has confirmed, so do not force a trade. (And if Q1 is "no" *and* Q2 is "no" while breadth is collapsing and rotation is down the quality ladder, that "wait in cash" is the regime-change exit, not a pause.) Two questions, four outcomes, repeated relentlessly. That is the entire discipline.

A word on *transaction costs and taxes*, because rotation is not free and over-trading it will quietly bleed your account. Each round trip in Vietnam costs a brokerage commission (roughly 0.10–0.15% per side at a discount broker) plus a 0.10% sell-side transaction tax, so a full rotation — sell the old leader, buy the new one — costs on the order of 0.3–0.4% of the traded amount, before any slippage in a thin small-cap. On a 500 million VND account (about **\$19,700**), one full rotation costs roughly 1.5–2.0 million VND (about **\$60–80**). That is trivial against catching a 30% handoff and ruinous if you "rotate" every week chasing noise. The lesson is not "don't rotate" — it is "rotate on confirmed relative-strength turns, not on every wiggle," because the costs reward patience and punish churn. The trader who rotates four well-confirmed times a year keeps almost all of his edge; the one who rotates forty times on noise hands a meaningful slice of it to commissions and the spread.

**The invalidation.** A rotation thesis is wrong, and you exit, when the relative-strength ratio you entered on *breaks back down* below where it turned up — the breakout that confirmed the new leader fails, the ratio rolls over, and the group goes back to lagging on falling volume. That is your line in the sand: it is objective, it is visible on the ratio chart, and it protects you from the failure mode of "I was sure steel was the next leader" turning into a months-long bag-hold. If the ratio that got you in turns down through its breakout level, the handoff did not happen — take the small loss and go back to the decision tree. The investors who survive Vietnam's violence are not the ones who are right most often; they are the ones whose *wrong* trades are small because they had an invalidation level and used it.

To bring the whole post back to one sentence: leadership on VN-Index is a relay where a fixed, retail-driven, margin-fueled pool of money hands the baton from group to group as the three engines re-rank the sectors — and your job is not to predict the order from a clock, but to *watch the relative-strength ratios*, hold the group whose ratio is turning up, rotate as the baton passes, and recognize the rotation *down the quality ladder* that signals it is time to leave the field entirely. Do that, and you stop being the person who buys brokers at +110% in November. You become the person holding the baton.
