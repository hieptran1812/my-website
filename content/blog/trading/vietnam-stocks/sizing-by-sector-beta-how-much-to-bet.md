## A "diversified" book that was really one broker bet

In late 2021, a retail investor I will call Minh was, by his own account, beautifully diversified. He had spread 600mn dong across six sectors — 100mn each in a broker (*cong ty chung khoan*), a steelmaker, a property developer, a bank, a consumer-staples name, and a utility. Six tickers, six industries, equal slices: textbook diversification, the kind every beginner's guide tells you to aim for. His pie chart looked balanced. He felt safe. When friends asked what he owned, he said "a bit of everything."

Then 2022 happened. The State Bank hiked rates, a corporate-bond crisis (*trai phieu doanh nghiep*) froze a key funding channel, and VN-Index fell more than 40% from its January peak to a November low near 874. Minh's book fell *much* harder than the index — and when he finally opened the statement he had been avoiding, the damage was not spread evenly across his six "equal" slices. The broker slice had cratered roughly 58%. The steel slice was down about 50%. The property slice was down 45%. The bank was down maybe 30%. The staples name was down 15%, the utility barely 10%. His "balanced" six-way book had behaved like a portfolio that was mostly broker, steel, and property — the three highest-beta things he owned — with the defensive slices contributing almost nothing to offset them. He had diversified the *names* but not the *risk*. In equal-dollar terms he owned six sectors; in equal-risk terms he owned about two and a half cyclicals and a rounding error of defense.

That is the trap this entire post exists to defuse. **Picking the right sector is only half the job; sizing it correctly is the other half — and the half almost every beginner skips.** Equal dollars feels fair and looks balanced, but it secretly hands the loudest sectors a megaphone. A high-beta sector and a defensive sector are not the same kind of risk per dong, so giving them the same number of dong does not give them the same vote in your outcome — it gives the high-beta sector three or four votes and the defensive one a fraction of a vote. The fix is to size by *risk*, not by dong: decide how much swing each position is allowed to add, then make the high-beta names *small* and the low-beta names *large* so every sleeve pulls its own weight and no single industry can wreck the book on its own. This post builds beta from zero, shows why equal dollars is not equal risk, teaches risk-budgeting and portfolio beta, and ends with a concrete sizing playbook you can run on your own VN-Index book.

![Risk budget concept graph showing same risk per position turning high beta into a small slice and low beta into a big slice](/imgs/blogs/sizing-by-sector-beta-how-much-to-bet-1.png)

The figure above is the whole idea in one picture. Start from a single rule on the left — *every position should contribute the same risk* — and follow it to the right: because the high-beta broker sector swings hard per dong, equal risk forces it into a *small* position; because the low-beta utility sector barely swings, equal risk lets it grow into a *big* position. The exciting name shrinks, the boring name grows, and the book ends up genuinely balanced in the only currency that matters in a drawdown — risk. Everything between here and the playbook is an elaboration of that one move.

## Foundations: what beta is and how to read it

Before any sizing math, we need to be precise about the central number: **beta**. Beta measures how sensitive a sector (or a stock) is to the overall market — here, to VN-Index. It answers one question: *when the index moves, how much does this thing tend to move?*

Formally, beta is the slope you would get if you plotted the sector's daily (or weekly) returns on the vertical axis and VN-Index's returns on the horizontal axis, then drew the best-fit line through the cloud of dots. A slope of 1.0 means the sector moves one-for-one with the index. A slope of 1.8 means that, on average, when the index moves 10%, this sector moves about 18% — it *amplifies* the market. A slope of 0.6 means that when the index moves 10%, this sector moves about 6% — it *dampens* the market. That single slope is the sector's beta.

A homely analogy before any formula. **Beta is the gearing on a bicycle.** The market (VN-Index) is how hard you pedal; the sector is the wheel. A high-beta sector is a high gear: one turn of the pedals sends the wheel spinning fast — exhilarating downhill, brutal uphill. A low-beta sector is a low gear: the same pedaling barely moves the wheel — slow on the flats, but you will not be thrown over the handlebars on the descent. Crucially, the gear does not decide which way you are going; *you* (the market) do. The gear only decides how *much* of your motion reaches the wheel. Beta is gearing on the market's motion, nothing more.

Three things follow immediately, and they matter for everything below:

1. **Beta is two-sided.** A beta of 1.8 means the sector tends to rise ~18% when the index rises 10% *and* fall ~18% when the index falls 10%. High beta is not "aggressive growth" or "good stock" — it is *more market*, in whichever direction the market goes. The thing that makes brokers thrilling in a bull is the exact same thing that makes them devastating in a bear.
2. **Beta is a tendency, not a guarantee.** It is an average slope over many days. On any single day a high-beta sector can move *less* than the index (its own news dominates) and a low-beta sector can move *more*. Beta describes the central pull, not the outcome of any one session. Over a long, market-driven move — a broad bull or a broad crash — the tendency dominates and beta becomes a very good predictor; over a quiet week of stock-specific noise, it can be drowned out.
3. **Beta is roughly constant only within a regime, not forever.** A sector's beta drifts with its business and its leverage. A broker that loads up on margin lending and a proprietary book becomes *more* sensitive to the market; a utility that signs long fixed-price power contracts becomes *less*. Beta is a slow-moving property, not a fixed constant — a point the misconceptions section will sharpen.

#### Worked example: reading a sector's beta off the index

Suppose VN-Index falls **10%** over a rough month, and you want to know what to expect from two sectors you own. The broker sector has a beta of **1.8**; the utility sector has a beta of **0.6**. The first-order estimate is just *beta x index move*. Brokers: 1.8 x (−10%) = **−18%**. Utilities: 0.6 x (−10%) = **−6%**. So on a 100mn dong position, the broker sleeve is expected to lose ~18mn dong (~\$709 at \$1 = 25,400 VND) while the utility sleeve loses ~6mn dong (~\$236) — three times the loss from the same number of dong. The intuition: beta is a multiplier on the market's move, so before you ever size a position you can read its rough swing straight off the index by multiplying by its beta.

### Why a whole sector has a beta (not just a stock)

You may wonder why we talk about a *sector's* beta rather than each stock's. The reason is the entire premise of this series: a sector is a bundle of companies that share the same fundamental drivers, so they move together, and that shared movement is what beta captures cleanly. Brokers all live or die on market turnover and the margin balance, so the whole broker sector has a high, stable beta. Utilities all collect regulated, contracted cash flows that barely care about the index, so the whole utility sector has a low, stable beta. (The full machinery of *why* industries move together is built up in the companion post on the [anatomy of a stock sector](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/anatomy-of-a-stock-sector-why-industries-move-together); here we take the co-movement as given and ask how to *size* it.) Using the sector beta — rather than chasing a single stock's noisier, less stable beta — gives you a more reliable number to size against, because the sector average washes out the stock-specific quirks.

### Beta versus volatility: two ways to measure "how much it swings"

Beta is not the only risk number you could size against. The other common one is **volatility** — the size of a sector's own price swings, measured as the standard deviation of its returns, regardless of which way the index is moving. The two are close cousins but they answer slightly different questions. Beta asks *how much does this sector move when the market moves?* — it captures only the part of the swing that is driven by the index. Volatility asks *how much does this sector move, period?* — it captures the market-driven swing *plus* the sector's own idiosyncratic noise (a sector-specific scandal, a commodity shock, a regulatory change).

For sizing a sector book on VN-Index, beta is usually the better tool, for one reason: in a real drawdown, almost everything that hurts you is *market-driven*. The crashes that wreck portfolios are broad — the index falls, and every cyclical falls with it according to its beta. Idiosyncratic volatility tends to wash out across a diversified book (one sector's bad news offsets another's good news), but market beta does not wash out — it stacks, because every position is exposed to the *same* market. So when you are worried about the drawdown that actually matters — the systemic one — beta is the number that governs it, and sizing inversely to beta is sizing against your real enemy. Volatility-based sizing (size inversely to each sector's volatility) gives a similar answer for most sectors, because high-beta sectors are usually high-volatility too; the two methods rank the sectors the same way and differ only at the margin. A beginner can use either and be far better off than sizing by dong — but if you size by one number, make it beta, because it targets the systemic risk that compounds across the whole book.

A second reason to prefer beta: it is *directional in a useful way*. Volatility is sign-blind — it counts an up-swing and a down-swing equally — but beta tells you that the high-beta sector will be your worst performer in the *down* market specifically, which is exactly the scenario you are sizing to survive. When you cap portfolio beta, you are capping your exposure to the one event — a broad index fall — that turns a portfolio of good stocks into a blown-up account.

The chart above ranks the major VN-Index sectors by their approximate beta, from the highest-beta corner at the top to the most defensive at the bottom. Securities (brokers) sit at the extreme, around 1.8 — the purest leveraged bet on the market, as the [brokers deep-dive](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/securities-brokers-sector-vietnam-highest-beta) explains. Steel, real estate, and construction cluster in the high-1.4-to-1.5 band — the heavy cyclicals. Read this chart as a menu of *how much market* each sector serves per dong — and notice that the spread from top to bottom is more than 3x, which is exactly why equal dollars cannot mean equal risk.

### Equal dollars versus equal risk

Here is the conceptual core of the whole post, stated as plainly as possible. **Equal dollars is not equal risk.** When you put the same number of dong into two sectors with different betas, you are *not* taking the same amount of risk in each — you are taking risk in proportion to each sector's beta. The high-beta sleeve, dong for dong, swings more, so it contributes more of your portfolio's total movement.

Make it concrete. Two positions, 100mn dong each: brokers (beta 1.8) and utilities (beta 0.6). The *dollars* are equal — 100mn and 100mn. But the *risk* is not: the broker sleeve moves three times as much per unit of index move (1.8 versus 0.6 is a 3:1 ratio). So if you measure each position's contribution to your portfolio's swings, the broker is contributing three units of risk for every one unit the utility contributes. Your "50/50 by dong" book is really "75/25 by risk." The picture looks balanced; the behavior is lopsided. That gap between how a book *looks* (by dollars) and how it *behaves* (by risk) is the single most important idea a beginner can internalize about position sizing.

![Before and after comparison of equal dollar versus risk balanced sizing for a broker and utility book](/imgs/blogs/sizing-by-sector-beta-how-much-to-bet-3.png)

The figure above puts the two framings side by side. On the left, the naive "equal-dollar" book: 100mn dong in brokers and 100mn in utilities — equal slices that *look* balanced but where the broker, at beta 1.8, dominates the actual risk. On the right, the risk-balanced book: less money in the high-beta broker and more in the low-beta utility, so that each position contributes the same swing. The left side is what most beginners build; the right side is what the risk actually requires. The whole craft of sizing is moving from the left picture to the right one.

#### Worked example: the equal-dollars trap

You hold **100mn dong in brokers** (beta 1.8) and **100mn dong in utilities** (beta 0.6) — 200mn dong total (~\$7,874 at \$1 = 25,400 VND), split exactly in half by money. Now suppose VN-Index falls 10%. The broker sleeve falls 1.8 x 10% = 18%, a loss of 18mn dong. The utility sleeve falls 0.6 x 10% = 6%, a loss of 6mn dong. Total loss: 24mn dong, of which the broker accounts for 18mn and the utility 6mn — a **75/25 split of the damage from a 50/50 split of the money**. The broker, dong for dong, contributed exactly 1.8 / 0.6 = **3x** the risk of the utility. The intuition: your "diversified, equal" book is, in risk terms, three-quarters a broker bet — you diversified the labels on the slices but not the swing inside them.

### Risk contribution: where your swing actually comes from

The cleanest way to see the gap between dollars and risk is to compute each position's **risk contribution** — the share of your portfolio's total movement that comes from that one sleeve. It is not the share of your money; it is the share of your *swing*. And in an equal-dollar book it is wildly skewed toward the high-beta names, because risk contribution scales with beta (and with volatility) while the dollars are flat. A position that holds a quarter of your money but contributes 60% of your swing is not a quarter-position — it is a controlling stake in your outcome, and you would never know it from the pie chart.

Computing risk contribution exactly requires correlations and volatilities, which is more machinery than a beginner needs. But the first-order intuition is simple and correct: in a broad market move — the kind that actually causes drawdowns — each sleeve's contribution to your loss is roughly *its dong weight x its beta*. Sum those up and divide each by the total, and you have each position's risk share. Do it for an equal-dollar book and the high-beta names swallow the pie; do it for a risk-balanced book and the shares flatten out to the equal split you actually wanted.

![Grouped bar chart of risk contribution share for an equal dollar book versus a risk balanced book across brokers banks and utilities](/imgs/blogs/sizing-by-sector-beta-how-much-to-bet-4.png)

The chart above puts the two books side by side in risk terms. On the equal-dollar book, three positions that hold the same money contribute *wildly* different risk: the broker contributes ~60% of the swing, the bank ~25%, and the utility just ~15% — the "equal" book is two-thirds a broker bet. On the risk-balanced book, the same three sectors each contribute roughly a third — 33%, 34%, 33% — which is what "diversified" is supposed to mean. The bars on the left are what you *own*; the bars on the right are what you *meant to own*. Sizing by beta is the move that turns the left chart into the right one.

#### Worked example: reading the risk-contribution pie

Take a three-sector equal-dollar book: 100mn dong each in brokers (beta 1.8), banks (beta 1.1), and utilities (beta 0.6), 300mn dong total (~\$11,811 at \$1 = 25,400 VND). The first-order risk weights are dong x beta: 100 x 1.8 = 180, 100 x 1.1 = 110, 100 x 0.6 = 60, summing to 350. So the risk shares are brokers 180 / 350 = **51%**, banks 110 / 350 = **31%**, utilities 60 / 350 = **17%**. The broker, holding a third of the money, owns *half* the risk; the utility, holding a third of the money, owns a sixth of the risk. The intuition: in an equal-dollar book the risk pie is just the beta-weighted dollar pie, so the highest-beta sector always eats a share of your swing far larger than its share of your money — which is precisely the imbalance risk-budgeting exists to erase.

### Risk-budgeting: size inversely to beta

If equal dollars gives unequal risk, the fix is to flip the logic: decide how much risk each position is *allowed* to contribute, then back out the dong that delivers that risk. This is **risk-budgeting** (sometimes called risk parity when every position gets an equal share). The recipe is mechanical once you accept the premise:

- Choose a **risk budget per position** — say, "no single position should contribute more than 1% of my portfolio's value in a typical bad move." This is your unit of risk, the same for every sleeve.
- For each sector, estimate its risk per dong. The cleanest proxy is *beta x the index's typical move* (or, more directly, the sector's own volatility). A high-beta sector has high risk per dong; a low-beta sector has low risk per dong.
- **Size inversely**: position size = risk budget / risk-per-dong. The high-beta sector, with high risk per dong, gets a *small* position; the low-beta sector, with low risk per dong, gets a *large* position. The two end up contributing the *same* risk despite holding very different amounts of money.

The mantra is one line: **to equalize risk, size inversely to beta.** The exciting, high-beta name does not get more money because it is exciting — it gets *less* money precisely because each dong of it carries more risk. The boring, low-beta name gets more money because each dong of it is gentle, and you need more of them to make the position matter. This feels backwards to a beginner, who wants to load up on the thrilling sector — and that instinct is exactly what blows books up.

There is a deeper symmetry worth pausing on. Risk-budgeting does not make your book *safe* — it makes your book *honest*. An equal-dollar book lies to you: it shows a balanced pie chart while behaving like a concentrated bet. A risk-budgeted book tells the truth: the pie chart looks lopsided (a big utility slice, a small broker slice) precisely because the *risk* underneath is balanced. You are trading a comforting-but-false picture for an uncomfortable-but-true one, and in a drawdown the true picture is the only one that pays. The lopsided-looking weights are the *feature*, not a bug — they are what equal risk looks like when you draw it in dong.

### Where the beta numbers come from

A fair question at this point: where does a retail investor actually *get* a sector's beta? You have three practical options, in increasing order of effort. First, **use a published estimate**: many Vietnamese brokers and data providers publish sector betas, and the ladder in the chart above (brokers ~1.8, banks ~1.1, utilities ~0.6) is stable enough that you can size against round numbers without computing anything. Second, **eyeball it from history**: pull the sector index and VN-Index over the last big up-and-down move, and compare the percentage swings — if the sector moved ~1.5x the index in both directions, its beta is roughly 1.5. Third, **compute the regression slope** if you are comfortable with a spreadsheet: put the sector's weekly returns and the index's weekly returns in two columns and use the slope function; that slope is the beta. For sizing purposes you do not need three decimal places — the difference between beta 1.7 and 1.9 barely changes the position size, while the difference between beta 0.6 and 1.8 changes it threefold. Get the *tier* right (high, mid, low) and you have captured almost all the benefit; chasing precision past that is false rigor.

A practical shortcut: because high-beta sectors are also high-volatility, you can often skip the beta estimate entirely and just *rank* your sectors from "swings the most" to "swings the least," then size in inverse rank order. A book that simply owns more of the calm sectors and less of the wild ones — even with no numbers at all — captures most of what risk-budgeting buys you. The numbers sharpen the sizing; the ranking alone already saves you from the equal-dollars trap.

![Inverse sizing graph turning a target risk per position divided by sector beta into a position size](/imgs/blogs/sizing-by-sector-beta-how-much-to-bet-5.png)

The figure above shows the inverse-sizing rule as a flow. Two inputs feed it: a single target risk per position (the same for every sleeve) and the sector's beta (different for each). Divide the first by the second and you get the position size: a high beta in the denominator produces a small position, a low beta produces a large one. The whole engine is one division, and the minus sign of intuition — *more risk per dong means fewer dong* — is built into it. This is the operational heart of risk-budgeting.

#### Worked example: risk-budget sizing in dong

You run a **1,000mn dong** book (~\$39,370 at \$1 = 25,400 VND) and set a risk budget of **1% of the portfolio per position in a typical 10% index move** — that is, no single sleeve should swing more than 10mn dong when the index moves 10%. For the broker (beta 1.8), the swing per dong in a 10% index move is 0.18, so the position that swings exactly 10mn dong is 10 / 0.18 = **~55.6mn dong** (~\$2,189). For the utility (beta 0.6), the swing per dong is 0.06, so the position is 10 / 0.06 = **~166.7mn dong** (~\$6,562). Same 10mn-dong risk from each — but the utility position is **three times larger in money** than the broker position. The intuition: to make a gentle, low-beta sector contribute the same risk as a violent high-beta one, you simply have to own three times as much of it — risk-budgeting hands the bigger dong slice to the *calmer* sector, the exact opposite of what excitement wants.

### Portfolio beta: the weighted average that governs your drawdown

Once each position is sized, the single most useful number you can compute about your whole book is its **portfolio beta** — the weighted average of your positions' betas, weighted by the dong in each. If half your money is in a beta-1.8 sector and half in a beta-0.6 sector, your portfolio beta is 0.5 x 1.8 + 0.5 x 0.6 = **1.2**. That one number tells you, to a first approximation, how your *entire* book will move when the index moves: a portfolio beta of 1.2 means a 10% index drop costs you roughly 12%, a 30% index drop costs you roughly 36%.

Portfolio beta is the bridge from individual sizing to your actual drawdown — the peak-to-trough fall you will live through in a bad market. And it is *additive in the obvious way*: high-beta positions pull it up, low-beta positions pull it down, and the weights decide who wins. This is why capping portfolio beta is the master control. You do not need to forecast the market to protect yourself; you only need to decide how much market exposure you are willing to carry, then keep the weighted-average beta under that ceiling by trimming the high-beta sleeves and adding the low-beta ones.

![Bar comparison of index drop versus a high beta book and a low beta book in a thirty percent market fall](/imgs/blogs/sizing-by-sector-beta-how-much-to-bet-6.png)

The chart above makes the stakes concrete. In a 30% VN-Index drop, the index by definition falls 30%. A book with portfolio beta 1.4 falls roughly 42% (1.4 x 30%); a book with portfolio beta 0.9 falls roughly 27% (0.9 x 30%). That 15-percentage-point gap — 42% versus 27% — is the entire difference between a drawdown you recover from in a year and one that takes three, and it comes *purely* from the portfolio beta you chose, not from any stock-picking skill. Cap the beta and you cap the bar on the left; let it drift up and the bar grows with it.

#### Worked example: portfolio beta and the drawdown it implies

You build a book that is **50% brokers** (beta 1.8), **30% banks** (beta 1.1), and **20% utilities** (beta 0.6). The portfolio beta is the weighted average: 0.5 x 1.8 + 0.3 x 1.1 + 0.2 x 0.6 = 0.90 + 0.33 + 0.12 = **1.35**. Now suppose VN-Index falls 30%. Your expected book loss is 1.35 x 30% = **~40.5%** — on a 1,000mn dong book (~\$39,370), about 405mn dong gone (~\$15,945). If you wanted to cut that to a 30% book drawdown, you would need to bring portfolio beta down to 1.0, which means trimming the broker sleeve and adding to utilities until the weighted average drops by 0.35. The intuition: your drawdown in a crash is roughly *portfolio beta x the index fall*, so the single lever that controls how bad your worst day gets is the weighted-average beta — and you set it deliberately, in advance, by how you size.

### Conviction is not size

One more foundation before the misconceptions, because it is where most beginners go wrong. **Conviction and size are two different decisions, and you must keep them separate.** Conviction is *whether* and *which way* — your view that a sector will outperform, your reason to be in the trade at all. Size is *how much* — the number of dong, governed by risk, not by enthusiasm. Beginners collapse the two: "I'm really sure about brokers, so I'll put a lot in brokers." But your certainty about *direction* tells you nothing about the *risk per dong*, and it is the risk per dong that should set the size.

The discipline is this: let conviction decide the *direction and the priority* (which sectors make the book at all, which gets the marginal dong when budgets are close), but let beta and the risk budget decide the *size*. You can be maximally convinced about a high-beta broker rally and *still* size the broker position small, because its risk per dong is high — your conviction is expressed by *owning it at all* and by being willing to add on the right signal, not by over-stuffing it. Separating the two decisions is what lets you hold a strong view without letting that view quietly turn into a concentration that one bad print can destroy.

There is a useful way to think about *how* conviction should enter the sizing without overriding it. Conviction adjusts the risk *budget* you are willing to spend on a sleeve, not the risk-to-dong translation. If you have a strong, well-reasoned view on brokers, you might grant the broker sleeve a *larger risk budget* than the others — say, 1.5% of the portfolio rather than 1% — and *then* run the inverse-beta math on that larger budget. The broker still ends up smaller in dong than a low-beta sector with the same budget, because the inverse-beta step is untouched; your conviction simply earned it a bigger slice of the *risk* pie, which is the honest currency to express a view in. What you must never do is skip the inverse-beta step and translate conviction straight into dong, because that is how a "high-conviction broker bet" becomes a 40% position that one bad quarter erases. Conviction sizes the *risk*; beta sizes the *dong*; keep that ordering and a bold view stays survivable.

#### Worked example: expressing conviction through the risk budget

Your base risk budget is **1% of a 1,000mn-dong book** per position — 10mn dong of swing per 10% index move. You have high conviction on brokers, so you grant that sleeve a **1.5% budget** (15mn dong of swing) while keeping banks and utilities at 1%. Running inverse-beta on the broker's larger budget: 15mn / 0.18 = **~83mn dong** (~\$3,268 at \$1 = 25,400 VND), versus the 1% broker of ~56mn dong from before. Your conviction grew the broker position by half — but it is *still* smaller in dong than the 1%-budget utility at ~167mn dong, because the inverse-beta step never went away. The intuition: a high-conviction high-beta name can be sized up *relative to its risk-neutral size* and still remain a small dong position — conviction buys it more risk budget, not a license to skip the beta math that keeps it from dominating the book.

## Common misconceptions

Sizing has a handful of seductive myths, each of which feels like common sense and each of which has blown up real books. Here are the four that matter most, corrected with numbers.

### "Size by conviction alone — put the most money where you're most sure"

This is the default beginner instinct and it is exactly backwards for risk. Conviction is a statement about *direction*; it says nothing about *risk per dong*. If you are very sure brokers will rise and you express that by putting 40% of your book in brokers (beta 1.8), you have built a position whose portfolio-beta contribution alone is 0.4 x 1.8 = 0.72 — more than two-thirds of a full-market beta from one sleeve. Your conviction might be right and you can *still* be ruined if the timing is wrong, because the position is sized to the strength of your feeling rather than to the risk it carries. The correction: conviction sets *direction and priority*; risk sets *size*. Be as sure as you like — then size the high-beta name *down*, not up, and express the conviction by holding it and adding on signal, not by over-weighting it from the start.

### "High beta just means buy a little less — knock 10% off and you're fine"

People who have heard "high beta is risky" often apply a vague haircut — own a *bit* less of the spicy sector — and think they are done. But the required adjustment is not a gentle trim; it is *proportional to the full beta ratio*. To equalize risk between a beta-1.8 broker and a beta-0.6 utility, you do not buy 10% less broker — you buy **three times less** broker (the inverse of the 1.8 / 0.6 ratio). A 10% haircut leaves the broker contributing 2.7x the risk of the utility instead of 3x; you have barely moved the needle. The correction: the sizing adjustment scales with the *ratio of betas*, which is often 3:1 or worse from the top to the bottom of the beta ladder — so equalizing risk means cutting the high-beta position to a *fraction*, not shaving a token slice off it.

### "Beta is a fixed constant you can look up once"

Beta drifts. A sector's sensitivity to the index changes as its business and its leverage change, and it can change a lot inside a single cycle. A broker that loads up its margin book and its proprietary trading becomes *more* sensitive to the market — its beta can climb well above 1.8 in a frothy, high-margin top, which is exactly when it is most dangerous. Betas also tend to *converge toward 1* in a genuine panic: in the worst days of a crash, correlations spike and almost everything sells off together, so a "defensive" beta-0.6 sector can briefly trade like a beta-1.0 sector when forced liquidation hits everything at once. The correction: treat beta as a *slow-moving estimate to update*, not a constant — re-check it each cycle, assume it rises in froth and converges toward 1 in a panic, and leave yourself a margin of safety rather than sizing to a single stale number.

### "Diversification by number of names is the same as diversification of risk"

Owning ten tickers feels diversified, but if eight of them are high-beta cyclicals, your risk is concentrated in one factor — the market — no matter how many names carry it. Minh from the opening owned six sectors and was, in risk terms, a broker-steel-property bet. The correction: diversification that matters is across *risk contributions and betas*, not across ticker symbols. A two-position book of one beta-0.6 utility and one beta-0.6 pharma name, sized equally, is *more* genuinely diversified in a crash than a ten-name book where every name is a beta-1.5 cyclical — because the first book's portfolio beta is 0.6 and the second's is 1.5. Count your risk, not your tickers.

## How it shows up on VN-Index

The principles above are not academic. They are written in the scar tissue of every VN-Index investor who lived through 2022, and they decide who survived the drawdown and who did not.

### The over-sized broker book of 2022

Through 2020 and 2021, the easiest money on VN-Index was in the high-beta corner — brokers, steel, property. The index roughly doubled off its 2020 low, but the broker sector ran far harder: the brokers deep-dive notes the sector returned on the order of +110% in 2021 against an index up ~36%, an *implied beta near 3x* in that boom. So a generation of new accounts, opened during the retail surge, learned a dangerous lesson: the high-beta sectors *go up the most*, so put your money there. They sized by recent return, which is sizing by beta in disguise — and they sized it *up*, loading the book toward the sectors that had paid the most.

Then 2022 reversed the loop. The same gearing that turned +36% into +110% on the way up turned −33% into roughly −58% on the way down for the broker sector. Books that were over-weighted in brokers, steel, and property did not fall with the index's ~33% (peak-to-low closer to ~40%) — they fell with their portfolio beta, which for a cyclical-heavy book was 1.4 or higher, so they lost 45-55% and worse. The investors who had built the "obvious" high-beta book — the one that felt smartest in 2021 — took the deepest drawdowns of the cycle. Nothing about their stock-picking was wrong; their *sizing* was the wound. They had let the highest-beta sectors take the biggest slices, so when the market turned, those slices did the most damage.

### The blow-up math of a single over-sized position

The cleanest way to feel the danger is to watch one over-sized high-beta position do its damage in isolation.

#### Worked example: the 2022 broker blow-up

Suppose your book is **40% in a single broker sleeve** (beta ~1.8) — a concentration that felt justified in early 2022 after the sector's 2021 run. In 2022 the broker sector fell roughly **58%**. From that one 40% sleeve alone, your portfolio loss is 0.40 x (−58%) = **−23.2%**. On a 1,000mn dong book (~\$39,370 at \$1 = 25,400 VND), that is 232mn dong gone (~\$9,134) *from one position* — before the other 60% of the book has lost a single dong. Add the rest of a cyclical-heavy book and you are past a 45% drawdown easily. The intuition: a high-beta sector sized like a normal position is a hidden leverage bomb — one 40% sleeve in a beta-1.8 sector that halves can torch a quarter of your entire book by itself, which is the whole case for *capping* how big any single high-beta position can get.

### The defensive book that barely flinched

The mirror image is the investor who sized by risk. Picture a book capped at portfolio beta 0.9: heavy in utilities, pharma, and consumer staples, with only a small, deliberately-sized broker sleeve held for the eventual recovery. In the same 2022 drawdown where the index fell ~33% and cyclical books fell 45-55%, this beta-0.9 book fell roughly 0.9 x 33% = ~30% — painful, but recoverable, and shallow enough that the investor could keep the small high-beta sleeve and even add to it near the lows instead of being forced to sell everything at the bottom. The defensive book did not *predict* the crash. It simply *carried less market*, by design, so the same crash did less damage. That is what a capped portfolio beta buys you: not foresight, just a smaller hole to climb out of. (The mechanics of why the defensive sectors hold up — regulated, contracted cash flows that ignore the index — are built up in the [utilities deep-dive](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/utilities-power-water-gas-sector-vietnam-defensive-cash).)

The contrast between Minh's six-way "diversified" book and the beta-0.9 book is the entire post in one comparison. Both investors owned defensive sectors. Both owned cyclicals. The difference was not *what* they owned — it was *how much* of each. Minh sized by dong and let beta concentrate his risk into the high-beta corner without his knowledge; the disciplined investor sized by risk and let the low-beta corner carry the weight it was supposed to carry. Same menu, opposite outcomes, and the only variable was the sizing. This is why the series insists that sector selection is half the job: you can pick every sector correctly — be right that brokers will lead the recovery, right that utilities are the ballast — and *still* blow up if you size them by dong instead of by risk. The selection earns you the right sectors; the sizing decides whether owning them helps you or ruins you.

### Why the margin cycle makes this worse

There is a second-order amplifier specific to Vietnam: margin (*ky quy*). The high-beta sectors are also the ones retail investors most love to buy *on margin*, which stacks broker-level leverage on top of an already-high beta. A margined position in a beta-1.8 broker can behave like a beta-3 or beta-4 position, because a forced margin call sells it at the worst possible moment, converting a paper drawdown into a realized one. This is why the high-beta corner falls *even harder* than its raw beta suggests in a real deleveraging — the margin balance collapses exactly when these sectors are falling, forcing sales that drive them down further. (The full feedback loop of margin, turnover, and the cycle is the subject of the [liquidity and the margin cycle](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/liquidity-and-the-margin-cycle-vietnam) post.) The sizing lesson is blunt: the higher a sector's beta, the *less* of it you should hold on borrowed money, because margin and beta multiply.

### The 2021 mirror: high beta in a bull

It would be wrong to leave the impression that high beta is simply bad. High beta is *more market*, and in a bull market more market is more gain — which is exactly why the discipline is hard. In 2021, the equal-dollar mistake *rewarded* you: the over-weighted broker, steel, and property sleeves were the ones up 80-110%, so the cyclical-heavy book that would later get crushed was, for a year, the genius book. This is the cruel part of sizing — the wrong sizing looks brilliant in the up phase, which is precisely when investors lock it in as a lesson. They learn "load the high-beta sectors" at the top of the cycle and then apply that lesson into the crash. Risk-budgeting asks you to *give up some upside in the bull* — your capped broker sleeve will lag the over-weighted one while the market rises — in exchange for a far smaller hole in the bear. The trade is real and it costs you bragging rights at the top. But the investor who keeps their drawdown to 30% while the cyclical crowd takes 55% does not need to be right about timing to come out ahead over a full cycle; they simply have less to recover. A 30% drawdown needs a ~43% gain to get back to even; a 55% drawdown needs a ~122% gain. The math of recovery is the whole case for sizing the high-beta corner down even when it hurts to do so in the bull.

#### Worked example: the recovery math of two drawdowns

The risk-budgeted, beta-0.9 investor takes a **30% drawdown** in 2022; the equal-dollar, beta-1.5 investor takes a **55% drawdown**. To get back to even, the first needs the book to rise by 1 / (1 − 0.30) − 1 = **~43%**; the second needs 1 / (1 − 0.55) − 1 = **~122%**. On a 1,000mn dong book (~\$39,370 at \$1 = 25,400 VND), the first investor is climbing from 700mn back to 1,000mn — a 300mn-dong (~\$11,811) recovery; the second is climbing from 450mn back to 1,000mn — a 550mn-dong (~\$21,654) recovery, from a smaller base. The intuition: drawdowns are asymmetric — the deeper the hole, the disproportionately larger the gain you need to escape it — so the few points of bull-market upside you surrender by capping beta are repaid many times over by the shallower hole you fall into.

## The playbook: how to size by sector beta

Everything above earns its keep here. This is the operational routine — five rules and an invalidation — for sizing a VN-Index book so that each industry pulls its own weight and no single sector can wreck you.

### Rule 1 — Set a risk budget per position, in advance

Before you buy anything, decide your unit of risk: *how much of my portfolio is any single position allowed to swing in a typical bad move?* A common, sane choice is **1% of the portfolio per 10% index move** — that is, no single sleeve should move more than 1% of your book when the index moves 10%. This is a number you set in calm times and refuse to renegotiate in the heat of a rally. It is the constant that every sizing decision divides into.

### Rule 2 — Size inversely to beta

For each sector you want to own, compute its risk per dong as *beta x the index move you are budgeting for*, then set the position size to *risk budget / risk per dong*. High-beta sectors get small positions; low-beta sectors get large ones. Run the division for every sleeve and you get a book where each position contributes the same risk by construction. Resist the pull to round the exciting sector up — the whole point is that it stays small.

![Sizing rules matrix with rows for high mid and low beta and columns for target weight max single position and role](/imgs/blogs/sizing-by-sector-beta-how-much-to-bet-7.png)

The matrix above is the playbook compressed into a table. The rows are the three beta tiers — high-beta (brokers, steel, property), mid-beta (banks, retail, industrials), and low-beta (utilities, pharma, staples) — and the columns give each tier a target weight, a hard cap on any single position, and its role in the book. High-beta names get *small* weights and *tight* caps and play the role of the geared upside you add on signal; low-beta names get *large* weights and *loose* caps and play the role of the ballast that keeps the book afloat in a drawdown. Size from the table, not from your enthusiasm.

#### Worked example: building a risk-balanced three-sector book

You have **1,000mn dong** (~\$39,370 at \$1 = 25,400 VND) and want exposure to brokers (beta 1.8), banks (beta 1.1), and utilities (beta 0.6), each contributing equal risk. Using risk per dong proportional to beta, the inverse weights are 1/1.8, 1/1.1, 1/0.6 = 0.556, 0.909, 1.667, which sum to 3.13. Normalizing: brokers 0.556 / 3.13 = **17.8%** (178mn dong, ~\$7,008), banks 0.909 / 3.13 = **29.0%** (290mn dong, ~\$11,417), utilities 1.667 / 3.13 = **53.2%** (532mn dong, ~\$20,945). The portfolio beta is 0.178 x 1.8 + 0.290 x 1.1 + 0.532 x 0.6 = 0.32 + 0.32 + 0.32 = **0.96** — and notice each sleeve contributes exactly 0.32 of the beta, which is the whole point. The intuition: equal *risk* across three sectors means the utility holds three times the dong of the broker, and the resulting book carries just under one unit of market beta with no single industry able to dominate it.

### Rule 3 — Cap the portfolio beta

Compute your portfolio beta — the dong-weighted average of your positions' betas — and hold it under a ceiling you choose for the regime. A reasonable default is **portfolio beta <= 1.0 in a normal market**, dropped toward **0.7-0.8 when you are defensive** (late cycle, stretched valuations, a frothy margin balance) and allowed up toward **1.2-1.3 only when you are deliberately aggressive** early in a liquidity upturn. The cap is your master drawdown control: at beta 1.0 a 30% index drop costs you ~30%; at beta 1.3 the same drop costs you ~39%. You set how bad your worst case gets by setting this one number, and you enforce it by trimming high-beta sleeves whenever the weighted average drifts above the ceiling.

### Rule 4 — Cap any single high-beta position

Independent of the risk budget, put a hard ceiling on how large any one high-beta position can be — a common rule is **no single high-beta sleeve above ~10-15% of the book**, with mid-beta names allowed to ~20% and low-beta ballast allowed to ~25-30%. This cap is what stops the 2022 blow-up math: a 40% broker sleeve that halves costs you 23% of the book, but a capped 12% broker sleeve that halves costs you only ~7% — survivable. Caps are not about the average case; they are about ensuring no single industry can detonate the whole book when its beta turns against you.

### Rule 5 — Let conviction pick direction, never size

Use your sector view — your read on the [rotation](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/sector-rotation-explained-leaders-and-laggards), the cycle phase, relative strength — to decide *which* sectors make the book and *which way* you lean, and to allocate the marginal dong when two budgets are close. But never let conviction override the size that risk dictates. A strong broker view earns the broker a place in the book and a willingness to add on the right signal — not a fat starting weight. Keep the two decisions separate and you can hold a bold view without it metastasizing into a fatal concentration.

### Rule 6 — Rebalance back to the risk budget

Sizing is not a one-time act; the market un-sizes you every day. When the high-beta sectors rally, they grow as a share of your book — a broker sleeve you sized to 12% can drift to 18% after a strong run, and now your portfolio beta has crept up exactly when valuations are stretched and the cycle is late. The discipline is to **rebalance back toward your risk budget on a schedule** — quarterly is a sane default — trimming the sleeves that have grown past their target and topping up the ones that have shrunk. This is mechanically *selling the winners and buying the laggards*, which feels wrong every single time and is right almost every single time, because it forces you to take profit on the high-beta names while they are loved and add to the defensives while they are cheap. Rebalancing is how the risk budget stays a budget instead of a one-day snapshot that the market erodes within a month. Note the elegant side effect: because the high-beta sectors are the ones that run hardest in a bull, rebalancing automatically trims your most dangerous exposure right as it becomes most dangerous — you are de-risking into froth without having to forecast the top.

#### Worked example: rebalancing a drifted book

You sized a broker sleeve to **120mn dong** out of a 1,000mn-dong book (~\$39,370 at \$1 = 25,400 VND) — a 12% target. After a strong quarter the broker rallies 50% to **180mn dong** while the rest of the book is flat, so the book is now 1,060mn dong and the broker is 180 / 1,060 = **17%** of it — well above target, and your portfolio beta has crept up with it. Rebalancing back to 12% means trimming the broker to 0.12 x 1,060 = **127mn dong**, selling ~53mn dong (~\$2,087) of the winner and redeploying it into the laggard defensives. The intuition: a rally silently inflates your highest-beta sleeve past its risk budget, so periodic rebalancing is the unglamorous act that keeps the high-beta corner capped — you bank the gain and restore the budget in the same trade.

### The invalidation

The whole framework rests on the betas you used being roughly right, so the invalidation is a *beta* invalidation, not a price one. **Re-estimate your sector betas each cycle, and re-size if they have moved materially.** If brokers' beta has climbed from 1.8 to 2.5 because the sector loaded up on margin and prop risk at a market top, your old position size is now carrying far more risk than budgeted, and you must cut it to restore the budget. If correlations are spiking and everything is converging toward beta 1 in a panic, your "defensive" sleeves are temporarily not defensive, and your real-time portfolio beta is higher than your spreadsheet says — derisk accordingly. The view is invalidated not when the market falls (you sized *for* that) but when the *betas themselves shift* under your positions and your book is quietly carrying more market than you signed up for. Watch the beta, re-size, and the rest of the playbook keeps working.
