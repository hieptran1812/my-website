> **TL;DR** — The industrial-goods and services sector is the cleanest *listed proxy* for Vietnam's FDI and export story: industrial-park developers buy and clear cheap land and lease it to foreign factories at a fat, recurring margin, while ports and logistics firms move the goods those factories make — so the whole group is geared to two numbers, **FDI inflows** and **trade volume**, both supercharged by the China+1 supply-chain shift.
>
> - An industrial park is *not* a normal property company. It buys raw land at maybe **\$30 per square metre**, spends to clear and service it, and leases serviced land to FDI tenants at **\$100-130 per square metre** on 50-year terms — a huge gross spread, booked as recurring cash, on land it never has to sell to a fickle homebuyer.
> - A port is a fixed-cost machine: most of its cost is the quay, cranes, and yard that are already built, so once it clears its break-even throughput, almost every extra container handled drops straight to profit. That is **operating leverage**, and it makes port earnings swing with trade volume.
> - The driver is FDI and trade, and the supercharger is **China+1** — multinationals diversifying factories out of China and into Vietnam. Disbursed FDI rose from about **\$20bn in 2019 to \$28bn in 2025**, and seaport container throughput from about **19 to 30 million TEU** over the same window. That structural uptrend is the sector's tailwind.

Headlines about a big FDI pledge are *not* immediate profit; the land and the containers show up in earnings over years.

In the spring of 2023, a Bac Ninh province official stood in front of a freshly cleared field of red earth north of Hanoi and announced that a global electronics supplier had signed to build a factory there. The field had been rice paddy a few years earlier. Now it had roads, a power substation, a water plant, and a perimeter fence — and a foreign company had just agreed to pay tens of millions of dollars to lease a slice of it for fifty years. The local press ran the story as an FDI win, a jobs win, a Vietnam-is-winning-the-supply-chain-war win. All of that was true. But buried in the announcement was the part that mattered for an investor: the company that *owned and cleared that field*, the company collecting the lease, was a listed industrial-park developer whose stock you could buy on the Ho Chi Minh exchange that same morning.

That is the whole sector in one scene. While the headlines talk about Samsung, Foxconn, LG, and the parade of multinationals moving factories to Vietnam, there is a quieter, listed business sitting underneath all of it — the companies that *sell the foreign factories their land* and the companies that *move the goods those factories export*. Industrial-park developers like **Kinh Bac (KBC)**, **Becamex (BCM)**, **IDICO (IDC)**, **Sonadezi Chau Duc (SZC)**, and **Viglacera (VGC)** lease cleared, serviced land to the incoming FDI tenants. Ports and logistics names like **Gemadept (GMD)**, **Viconship (VSC)**, and **Hai An (HAH)** handle and ship what those factories produce. Together they make up the **industrial goods and services** sector — the most direct way to own Vietnam's FDI and manufacturing story on the exchange.

This post builds that sector from zero. We will learn the industrial-park land-lease model — why it is high-margin, capital-heavy, and *recurring* rather than the boom-bust of a homebuilder. We will learn the port and logistics model — throughput times rate, on a mostly fixed cost base, which gives ports their operating leverage. We will see *why* the whole group is geared to FDI inflows and trade volume, and what the China+1 shift does to both. We will learn how to value the two halves differently (RNAV and land value for the parks, EV/EBITDA for the ports). And we will end where this series always ends: a concrete playbook for trading the sector off FDI, occupancy, and throughput — and the one signal that would prove the whole thesis wrong.

![Flow chart showing FDI inflow leading to a factory needing land, an industrial park leasing the land, goods produced for export, and ports plus logistics handling the containers](/imgs/blogs/industrial-goods-services-sector-vietnam-ports-logistics-1.png)

A quick word on money, because we will use it throughout. Vietnam trades in dong (VND), and the sector's primary numbers — market caps, land bank values, lease revenue — are quoted in dong. But this is the one sector where dollars are genuinely the *native* unit on the demand side, because FDI is foreign money, lease prices are quoted in US dollars per square metre, and container-handling rates are dollar rates set against global shipping. The reference rate in mid-2026 is roughly **\$1 = 25,400 VND**. So a park worth 30 trillion dong is about a **\$1.18bn** market cap; a 500 billion dong land bank is about **\$19.7mn**; a foreign net buy of 800 billion dong into the sector is about **\$31.5mn**. We will keep the company-level figures in dong where that is how they are reported, and use dollars for lease prices, FDI flows, and handling rates, because that is how the people running these businesses actually quote them.

## Foundations: what an industrial park actually does

Forget the stock ticker for a moment and imagine you run an industrial-park company. Your business is brutally simple to describe and surprisingly hard to do: you **acquire raw land, clear and service it, and lease the serviced land to factories** — and because the factories are foreign manufacturers setting up in Vietnam, your customers are FDI tenants paying in dollars on long leases.

Let us walk the cycle slowly, because every later insight hangs off it.

**Step one: acquire raw land.** You buy (or get assigned by the province) a large tract of land — often former agricultural land, hundreds of hectares at a time, in a province near a port, an airport, or a labour pool: Bac Ninh, Bac Giang, Hai Phong, and Hung Yen in the north; Binh Duong, Dong Nai, Long An, and Ba Ria-Vung Tau in the south. Raw agricultural land is *cheap* — call it **\$20-40 per square metre** all-in including the site-clearance compensation you pay the farmers. This is the single hardest and most valuable part of the whole business, and we will come back to it: **getting the land** (the legal clearance, the compensation, the zoning as an industrial zone) is the real moat, because it is slow, political, and capital-heavy. A company with a big, cleared, ready-to-lease **land bank** is sitting on the scarce thing.

**Step two: clear and build the infrastructure.** Raw land is useless to a factory. You have to level it, build internal roads, lay water and waste-water treatment, run a power substation, sometimes build the standard-issue factory shells (ready-built factories, or RBFs, that smaller tenants rent rather than build). This costs money — add maybe **\$15-25 per square metre** of infrastructure spend on top of the land — and it takes years. When you are done, you have *serviced industrial land*: a fenced, powered, connected plot a foreign factory can move into.

**Step three: lease it to an FDI tenant.** Now you sell the *use* of that land. Crucially, in Vietnam an industrial park does not usually *sell the freehold* — it grants a **land-use right** for the remainder of the zone's term (often up to 50 years). The tenant — a Samsung supplier, a furniture maker, a textile exporter — pays you for that right. The headline lease price for good serviced land near Hanoi or in the southern industrial belt runs **\$100-130 per square metre** for the full term (it varies a lot by province and by year; northern hotspots have pushed past \$130, while newer southern zones are lower). That is the number that makes the business: you bought and serviced the land for maybe **\$40-60 per square metre** all-in, and you lease it for **\$100-130** — a gross spread of **\$60-80 per square metre** before overhead.

**Step four: recognize the cash over years.** Here is the subtle accounting point that confuses beginners and is essential to understanding the stock. When a tenant signs a 50-year lease and pays a large upfront sum, the park does *not* book all of that as profit on day one. Under the accounting most of these companies use, the revenue is **recognized over the life of the lease** (or over the period the obligations are met). So a single big signing fills the *backlog* — the contracted-but-not-yet-recognized revenue — which then flows into reported earnings over many years. This is why a park can sign a blockbuster deal and yet report only modestly higher profit that quarter: the cash is real, but the income statement releases it slowly. We will use this directly in the playbook, because **signed leases and occupancy are leading indicators; reported profit is the lagging one.**

![Pipeline showing the industrial park land cycle: buy raw land, clear and build roads power water, lease to FDI tenant, recognize cash over lease years](/imgs/blogs/industrial-goods-services-sector-vietnam-ports-logistics-3.png)

That four-step cycle — buy, clear, lease, recognize — is the entire industrial-park business. Notice what it is *not*. It is not a homebuilder selling apartments to local buyers on a mortgage, exposed to presales and the corporate-bond cycle (that is the residential property sector, which we cover separately). The park's customer is a *foreign factory* signing a *long lease* paid largely *upfront in dollars*. The demand is driven by FDI, not by domestic mortgage rates. The product is *land*, the scarcest input of all. That difference is why the industrial-park model deserves its own mental model, and why it behaves so differently on VN-Index from the residential developers it sits near in the sector lists.

### The real moat: getting the land in the first place

It is tempting to assume the hard part of an industrial park is the construction — the roads and the power and the waste plant. It is not. Building infrastructure is something any competent contractor can do for money. The genuinely hard, slow, and *protective* part is **acquiring and clearing the land in the first place**, and understanding why is understanding the whole sector's barrier to entry.

In Vietnam, land is ultimately owned by the state and allocated through a layered process of provincial planning, zoning, and land-use-right assignment. To create an industrial zone, a developer has to get the land *re-designated* as industrial in the provincial master plan, get the project approved, then carry out **site clearance** — physically compensating and relocating the farmers and households living on what is usually agricultural land, plot by plot, household by household. This is the step that takes *years* and breaks the patience (and the balance sheets) of most would-be entrants. Compensation is negotiated, sometimes contested; a single holdout family can stall a zone; the political relationships with the provincial authorities matter enormously. A company that has *already done this* — that holds hundreds of hectares of *cleared*, *zoned*, *legally clean* land ready to lease — owns something a competitor cannot quickly replicate even with unlimited capital, because the bottleneck is not money but *time and clearance*.

This is why the *quality* of a park's land bank is measured not just in hectares but in **cleared and ready** hectares. A company may report a thousand hectares of "land bank" but if half of it is still tangled in clearance, that half cannot be leased to a factory that needs to break ground this year. The investor's job is to look past the headline hectares to the *leasable* hectares — the cleared, serviced land that can absorb an incoming tenant tomorrow. The parks with the deepest moats — **Becamex (BCM)** with its enormous, decades-assembled holdings across Binh Duong; **IDICO (IDC)** with mature zones already substantially cleared; **Kinh Bac (KBC)** in the northern electronics belt; **Viglacera (VGC)** spread across north and central provinces; **Sonadezi Chau Duc (SZC)** in the south — are valuable precisely because the slow, painful clearance work is *already done*. Their land bank is not just cheap land on the books; it is *years of clearance already paid for*, and that is the part the RNAV captures and the casual P/E misses entirely.

The flip side of this moat is its cost: clearance is *capital that goes out years before revenue comes in*, and it is *lumpy and unpredictable* in timing. A park might spend heavily clearing a new zone for two years with no lease income from it, then suddenly fill it with FDI tenants and watch the backlog explode. That mismatch between *when the money goes out* (clearance, infrastructure) and *when it comes back* (leases, recognized over decades) is the structural reason the parks carry so much capital and need patient funding — and the reason their stocks can look expensive on near-term earnings while sitting on a land bank worth a multiple of the market cap.

### The land-lease economics: high margin, capital-heavy, recurring

Let us put numbers on why this business is attractive — and where its catch is.

The **margin** is the headline. A gross spread of \$60-80 on land that cost \$40-60 is a gross margin in the *40-60% range* on land sales, far fatter than a residential developer earns on apartments, and fatter than almost any industrial business. The reason is simple: the park is monetizing a *scarce, appreciating* asset (cleared, zoned, ready industrial land near infrastructure) that it acquired cheaply and that gets *more* valuable as FDI demand rises and nearby land fills up. The park is, in effect, a *land bank with a leasing operation bolted on*.

The **capital intensity** is the catch. You have to pay for the land and the infrastructure *years before* the lease revenue arrives. Buying 300 hectares and servicing it can tie up trillions of dong before a single tenant signs. So industrial parks carry a lot of capital on the balance sheet — land inventory, work-in-progress infrastructure, receivables on signed leases — and they often fund it with debt and equity raises. This is the reason the stocks can be volatile despite the recurring revenue: the *value* is in a land bank that the market re-prices constantly, and the *funding* is geared. When credit is cheap and FDI is flooding in, the parks can fund expansion and the market values the land bank generously; when credit tightens or FDI stalls, the same land bank is suddenly hard to fund and the market marks it down.

The **recurring** quality is the underrated part. Because leases are long and revenue is recognized over years, a park with a filled zone has a stream of contracted income flowing for a long time — plus the *services* it sells the tenants forever: water, waste treatment, power distribution, factory-shell rent, management fees. A mature park is half land-developer, half utility. That recurring services layer is lower-margin than the land sale but it is sticky and annuity-like, and it is why the better parks (Becamex's vast Binh Duong holdings, IDICO's zones, Viglacera's spread across north and central Vietnam) are valued as long-duration assets, not as one-off land flippers.

#### Worked example: the lease spread, square metre by square metre

Make the model concrete with the brief's numbers. Suppose a park buys raw land at **\$30 per square metre** and, after clearing and infrastructure, leases serviced land at **\$120 per square metre** on a 50-year term. The gross spread is \$120 − \$30 = **\$90 per square metre** (we will fold the infrastructure cost into overhead for simplicity here; in reality the all-in cost is closer to \$50, leaving a \$70 spread, which the figure above uses). Now scale it: the park leases **50 hectares** in a year. Fifty hectares is 500,000 square metres. The gross value created is \$90 × 500,000 = **\$45mn**. Convert to dong at \$1 = 25,400 VND: about **1.14 trillion dong** of gross value from a single year's leasing on one zone. The intuition: the park is a machine that mints a per-square-metre spread, and because it leases land by the *hectare*, even one good year of signings on one zone moves the whole company — the unit is small but the volume is enormous.

Hold that \$45mn, because the very next thing a beginner gets wrong is assuming it all hits profit this year. It does not — it fills the backlog and releases over the lease life. The *signing* is the event; the *earnings* trickle.

### The ports and logistics model: throughput times rate, on a fixed cost base

Now flip to the other half of the sector. If industrial parks are where the factories *land*, ports and logistics are how the goods those factories make *leave*. Vietnam is an export economy — it runs a large trade surplus built on electronics, textiles, footwear, furniture, and increasingly machinery — and almost all of that trade moves in **containers** through seaports. The listed names here are port operators and container shippers: **Gemadept (GMD)**, the largest listed port-and-logistics group; **Viconship (VSC)** in Hai Phong; **Hai An (HAH)**, which both operates terminals and owns a container-ship fleet.

A port's business model is almost the mirror image of the park's in one important way: where the park sells a *scarce asset* (land) at a fat spread, the port sells a *service* (handling a container) at a thin per-unit rate — but it sells that service over and over, on a cost base that is mostly *fixed*. Let us name the parts.

The **throughput** is the number of containers the port handles, measured in **TEU** — twenty-foot equivalent units, the standard container-counting unit (one standard 20-foot box is 1 TEU; a 40-foot box is 2 TEU). A port's volume is the number of TEU that cross its quay in a year, and that number is driven by *trade volume* — how much the country imports and exports — and by the port's share of it.

The **rate** is what the port charges to handle each TEU: the **handling tariff** (loading and unloading the ship), plus storage, plus various ancillary fees. Vietnamese handling rates have historically been *low* by regional standards (the government has kept tariffs capped, which compresses port margins versus, say, Singapore), and a recurring bull thesis for the sector is that *tariff increases* are coming as the government lets rates rise toward regional levels. Revenue, then, is roughly **throughput × rate**: TEU handled times dollars per TEU.

The **cost base** is the key to the whole thing: it is **mostly fixed**. A container terminal is a giant capital asset — the quay wall, the deep-water berth, the gantry cranes, the yard, the reach-stackers — almost all of which is built and paid for (or being depreciated) regardless of how many ships call. The *variable* cost of handling one more container (a bit of crane fuel, a bit of labour) is small relative to the fixed cost of having the terminal at all. This is the source of the port's **operating leverage**: once throughput passes the level that covers the fixed costs (the break-even), almost every additional container's revenue drops to the bottom line, because the fixed costs are already paid. Below break-even, the port bleeds; above it, profit explodes. That is why port earnings swing with throughput, and why throughput swings with trade.

Logistics — trucking, warehousing, freight forwarding, depot services — sits alongside the ports and is the *connective tissue*: it moves the container from the factory gate to the quay and from the quay to the inland depot. Pure logistics is more competitive and lower-margin than port operation (anyone can buy trucks; not anyone can build a deep-water berth), but it grows with the same driver: trade volume. The integrated players (Gemadept again is the model) own both the terminals *and* the logistics chain, which is why they capture the most of each container's journey.

There is a third layer worth separating out, because it carries its *own* violent cycle: **container shipping**. Some listed names — **Hai An (HAH)** is the clearest example — do not just operate terminals; they *own the ships*, running container vessels on domestic and regional routes. Owning ships exposes you to the **charter rate** and **freight rate** — what it costs to hire a vessel and what a shipper pays to move a box on it — and those rates are some of the most volatile prices in the entire economy. During the pandemic, global container freight rates spiked to roughly *ten times* their normal level as ports clogged and ships waited weeks to berth; a shipowner's earnings exploded. Then, as the congestion cleared and new ships ordered at the peak arrived, rates *collapsed* back toward normal, and the same shipowner's earnings cratered. So a company like HAH has *two* cyclical engines stacked: the terminal's throughput-and-utilization cycle *and* the ship's freight-rate cycle, which makes its earnings (and its stock) swing harder than a pure terminal operator's. The lesson for the sector investor is to know *which* engine a given port-and-logistics name is most exposed to: a pure landlord-style terminal operator is geared to local throughput; a shipowner is additionally geared to global freight rates, which warn earliest and swing most.

The other structural distinction within ports is **deep-water versus river**. Vietnam's older ports sit up rivers (the Saigon River, the Cam River at Hai Phong) and can only take smaller feeder ships, which means a container bound for the US or Europe has to be carried first to a hub like Singapore or Hong Kong and *transshipped* onto a larger mother vessel — an extra handling, an extra cost, an extra delay. The newer **deep-water clusters** — Cai Mep-Thi Vai in the south, Lach Huyen at Hai Phong in the north — can take the largest ocean-going container ships *directly*, so a box can sail straight from Vietnam to Los Angeles without transshipping. Deep-water capacity is therefore worth more per container and is the strategic high ground of the sector: it captures the higher-value direct trade and the transshipment volume that used to leak to Singapore. When a port operator builds a deep-water berth, it is buying a position on that high ground — at the cost of a multi-year, capital-heavy, ramp-risk bet that the trade will come to fill it.

### Why the whole sector is geared to FDI and trade — and what China+1 does

Step back and look at the sector as one machine, because the unifying insight is that *both halves run on the same two fuels*. The parks run on **FDI** (foreign factories needing land). The ports run on **trade** (those factories exporting goods). And FDI and trade are *linked* — a factory built by FDI exists in order to *export*, so the FDI that fills the parks becomes the trade that fills the ports, with a lag of a year or two while the factory is built and ramped. Follow the chain: FDI dollars arrive → a factory needs cleared land → the industrial park leases it → the factory is built and starts producing → the goods are exported → the ports and logistics handle them. The first figure in this post drew exactly that chain, and it is the spine of the whole sector.

So the sector's master variable is the strength of Vietnam as a *manufacturing and export destination*. When that strengthens, parks fill and ports get busier. When it weakens, both stall. And the single biggest force strengthening it over the past decade is the **China+1** strategy — multinationals deciding they cannot have *all* their manufacturing in one country (China), and adding a second base to diversify cost, tariff, and geopolitical risk. Vietnam, sitting on China's border with cheaper labour, a young workforce, a stable government, and a web of free-trade agreements (the CPTPP, the EU-Vietnam FTA, RCEP), has been the *plus-one* of choice for electronics, footwear, textiles, and increasingly higher-value assembly. China+1 is not a one-year trade; it is a multi-year *relocation of supply chains*, and every factory that relocates needs land (park revenue) and exports goods (port revenue).

Why Vietnam specifically, and not one of a dozen other low-cost countries? The reasons compound, and they are worth naming because they are the durable part of the thesis. **Geography**: Vietnam shares a land border with southern China, so a company can keep its component suppliers in China and move only *final assembly* across the border — a short, cheap supply-chain hop rather than a wholesale relocation. **Cost**: Vietnamese manufacturing wages have run at a meaningful discount to coastal China for years, and the workforce is young, large, and increasingly skilled in electronics assembly. **Stability**: a single-party state that has been consistently pro-business and pro-FDI for decades gives multinationals the predictability they need to sink billions into fixed plant. **Trade access**: Vietnam has signed more major free-trade agreements than almost any peer, so goods assembled there enter the EU, Japan, and the CPTPP bloc at low or zero tariffs — which is the entire point of building there to *export*. And **incumbency**: once Samsung built its giant phone plants in the north, its component suppliers followed, and once a cluster of suppliers exists, the *next* manufacturer finds a ready-made ecosystem. That clustering effect is self-reinforcing, and it is why the FDI line keeps climbing rather than plateauing.

The investor's takeaway is that China+1 is *structural*, not cyclical. A cyclical tailwind comes and goes with the global economy; a structural one is a multi-year reallocation that continues even through soft patches. The 2023 trade slowdown did not stop factories from relocating to Vietnam — it slowed the *exports* of factories already there. The relocation kept going, which is why disbursed FDI rose even in the soft-trade years. When you own the sector, you are betting that this structural reallocation has years left to run — and the thing that would end the bet is not a soft quarter but a *reversal* of the relocation itself, which we come back to in the playbook.

![Bar chart of disbursed FDI into Vietnam by year from 2019 to 2025 in US dollars billion showing a structural uptrend from about twenty to twenty eight billion](/imgs/blogs/industrial-goods-services-sector-vietnam-ports-logistics-2.png)

The chart above is the demand side of the parks: disbursed FDI into Vietnam. "Disbursed" is the number that matters — it is money actually spent building factories, not just *registered* pledges (which are larger and lumpier and often don't fully convert). Look at the shape: roughly flat around **\$20bn through 2019-2021** (the pandemic years, when travel bans and supply-chain chaos slowed new projects), then a clear structural climb — **\$22.4bn in 2022, \$23.2bn in 2023, \$25.4bn in 2024, \$28.0bn in 2025**. That rising line is China+1 made visible: more foreign capital, every year, building more factories, needing more land. When you own an industrial park, *that is the demand curve for your product.*

#### Worked example: FDI up 13% lifts the parks' demand

Tie the FDI number to park demand directly. Suppose disbursed FDI rises from **\$22.4bn (2022) to \$25.4bn (2024)** — a jump of about **+13%** over two years (and the 2025 print of \$28.0bn extends it further). A chunk of that incremental \$3bn is *manufacturing* FDI that needs physical factory space. Industry rules of thumb put new manufacturing investment at very roughly **\$3-5mn of capital per hectare of factory land** absorbed (it varies enormously by industry — chip packaging is land-light and capital-heavy; furniture is the reverse). Take the conservative end: if even **\$1.5bn** of the incremental FDI is land-hungry manufacturing at \$5mn per hectare, that is **300 hectares** of new industrial-land demand showing up over the period — directly into the parks' leasing pipelines, and enough to lift a tight market's occupancy by several percentage points. The intuition: you don't have to forecast FDI perfectly; you only have to see the *direction*, because rising FDI is rising land absorption, and rising absorption is rising occupancy and pricing power for the parks.

![Line chart of approximate Vietnam seaport container throughput in million TEU from 2019 to 2025 rising from about nineteen to thirty with a dip in 2023](/imgs/blogs/industrial-goods-services-sector-vietnam-ports-logistics-4.png)

And here is the demand side of the ports: container throughput. The same structural climb is there — from about **19.4 million TEU in 2019 to 30 million in 2025** — with one instructive wobble. Notice the **2023 dip** (throughput eased to ~24.7mn from 25.1mn in 2022). That was the year global trade slowed: rich-world consumers stopped buying so many goods after the pandemic splurge, export orders for Vietnamese factories fell, and ships called less full. That dip is the sector's *cyclicality* showing through the *structural* uptrend — and it is exactly the kind of signal a trader watches, because port stocks fell hard in 2022-23 as throughput and freight rates softened, then recovered as 2024 volumes rebounded. The structural China+1 story is the tide; the trade cycle is the waves on top of it.

#### Worked example: port operating leverage on a 15-point utilization swing

This is the mechanism that makes port stocks move, so let us do it carefully. Take a terminal with a fixed cost base of **\$40mn a year** (depreciation on the berth and cranes, plus the fixed labour and maintenance to keep it open). Suppose at **75% utilization** it handles **1.5 million TEU** and earns a handling rate net of variable cost of **\$30 per TEU** of *contribution* (revenue per TEU minus the small variable cost per TEU). Contribution is 1.5mn × \$30 = **\$45mn**; subtract the \$40mn fixed cost and EBITDA is **\$5mn**.

Now trade picks up and the same terminal runs at **90% utilization**, handling **1.8 million TEU** — a 20% volume rise. The variable cost per container barely changes, so contribution rises to 1.8mn × \$30 = **\$54mn**; subtract the *same* \$40mn fixed cost (it didn't grow — the terminal was already built) and EBITDA is now **\$14mn**. A 20% rise in throughput produced almost a **3x rise in EBITDA** (\$5mn to \$14mn). The intuition: above break-even, the fixed cost is a fulcrum — every extra container's contribution drops nearly whole to profit, so a modest volume swing produces a violent earnings swing. That is operating leverage, and it is why ports are cyclical in earnings even though their *revenue* is far smoother than their profit.

#### Worked example: throughput times rate, and why volume beats rate

Put the port revenue equation together. A terminal handles **1.5 million TEU** at an all-in handling rate of **\$60 per TEU** (handling plus storage plus ancillaries). Revenue is 1.5mn × \$60 = **\$90mn** — about **2.29 trillion dong** at \$1 = 25,400 VND. Now compare two ways to grow it 10%. A **10% rate increase** (to \$66) on flat volume adds \$9mn of revenue, almost all of which drops to profit (rate increases are nearly pure margin). A **10% volume increase** (to 1.65mn TEU) on flat rate also adds \$9mn of revenue — but because of the operating leverage above, the *profit* impact of the volume rise is amplified by the fixed-cost fulcrum, while the rate rise is "only" its own \$9mn. Both help; the volume rise compounds harder once you account for fixed costs, which is why throughput growth (driven by trade) is the port investor's first variable and tariff increases are the cherry on top. The intuition: ports make money two ways — handle *more* boxes, or charge *more* per box — and in Vietnam the structural story is mostly the *more boxes* (trade growth), with tariff reform as an optional kicker.

### Valuing the two halves: RNAV for parks, EV/EBITDA for ports

Because the two halves of the sector are such different businesses, you value them with different yardsticks — and getting this wrong is the most common analytical mistake in the sector.

For **industrial parks**, the right lens is the **land bank**, valued at today's prices. The technique is **RNAV** — *revalued net asset value*. You take the company's land bank (hectares of cleared, leasable land it owns or controls), value each plot at the *current* market lease price (not the cheap historical cost it sits on the books at), subtract the debt and the cost still to spend on infrastructure, and you get the revalued equity value. The insight is that a park's accounts *understate* its worth, because the land sits at historical cost while its market value has risen with FDI demand. A park trading below its RNAV is, in effect, selling you cleared industrial land for less than you could lease it for — the classic value setup in the sector. P/E is nearly useless here because the lumpy, recognized-over-time revenue makes a single year's earnings a poor guide; the *asset* (the land bank) is the value, and RNAV is how you price the asset.

For **ports and logistics**, the right lens is **EV/EBITDA** — enterprise value divided by earnings before interest, tax, depreciation, and amortization. Why EBITDA and not earnings? Because ports are capital-heavy and carry large depreciation and often significant debt, so net profit (which is *after* depreciation and interest) understates the cash the terminal actually throws off. EBITDA strips out the depreciation of the already-built berth and looks at the operating cash the assets generate, and EV (which adds debt to the market cap) measures the whole enterprise you'd have to buy to own that cash. On mid-cycle throughput, EV/EBITDA tells you whether a port is cheap or dear relative to the cash its terminals produce — and crucially, you want to value it on *mid-cycle* throughput, not on a boom or a trough, for exactly the same reason you value a steel cyclical on mid-cycle EV/EBITDA rather than peak or trough P/E. (We unpack EV/EBITDA and RNAV in full in the [valuation-by-sector post](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/valuation-by-sector-pe-pb-nav-ev-ebitda); this is the sector where *both* of those tools earn their keep.)

![Before and after comparison of industrial park economics per square metre showing raw land cost about thirty dollars versus lease price about one hundred twenty dollars and the gross spread](/imgs/blogs/industrial-goods-services-sector-vietnam-ports-logistics-5.png)

The figure above is the park half of the valuation story in one image: cheap land going in on the left, an expensive lease coming out on the right, and the spread in between that — multiplied across a land bank of hundreds of hectares — *is* the RNAV. When you revalue a park, you are doing exactly this calculation across every plot it owns: what did it cost, what can it lease for today, and how many square metres does it have. The bigger the gap and the bigger the bank, the bigger the value the accounts are hiding.

#### Worked example: revaluing a park's land bank (RNAV)

Let us do a stripped-down RNAV so the technique is concrete. Suppose a park owns **300 hectares** of cleared, leasable land — 3,000,000 square metres. It sits on the books at historical cost of about **\$40 per square metre**, so the *accounting* value of the land is 3,000,000 × \$40 = **\$120mn**. But the *current* market lease price for that land is **\$110 per square metre**, so the market value of the bank is 3,000,000 × \$110 = **\$330mn**. The revaluation gain — the value the accounts are hiding — is \$330mn − \$120mn = **\$210mn**. Now finish the RNAV: take that revalued land value, *add* the company's other assets and cash, and *subtract* its debt and the infrastructure cost still to spend (say net \$90mn of debt and remaining capex), and you arrive at a revalued equity value of roughly **\$240mn**, or about **6.1 trillion dong**. If the stock's market cap is only **4 trillion dong**, it trades at a ~35% discount to RNAV — you are buying cleared industrial land for two-thirds of what the market will pay to lease it. The intuition: RNAV reprices the land bank at today's lease prices instead of yesterday's purchase cost, and the gap between the two is the entire reason a profitable park can look "expensive" on P/E yet cheap on its assets.

It is worth saying a word about where this sector sits in the *index* as a whole, because that shapes how it trades. Industrial goods and services is not one of the giant index weights the way banking or the largest property names are — the listed parks and ports are mostly mid-cap rather than mega-cap. That has two consequences. First, the sector moves *more on its own theme* (FDI headlines, throughput data, China+1 sentiment) than on broad index flows, so it can lead or lag the VN-Index meaningfully. Second, because the leaders are mid-caps, foreign and institutional flows can move them sharply — a wave of foreign buying into the China+1 theme lands hard on a relatively small float. So the sector behaves like a *thematic mid-cap basket* geared to a structural story, which is a different animal from the index-anchoring banks and the bond-sensitive residential developers it shares the sector lists with.

## Common misconceptions

The industrial-goods sector attracts a specific set of beginner errors, each of which costs money. Let us correct the four most expensive.

**Misconception 1: "Industrial parks are just property companies."** This is the single most common error, and it leads people to value parks like homebuilders and to lump them in with the residential-property sector and its bond crisis. They are *not* the same. A homebuilder sells apartments to *domestic* buyers funded by *mortgages*, books revenue on *presales*, and is exposed to the *corporate-bond* cycle that nearly broke the residential developers in 2022 (the story in the [real-estate sector post](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/real-estate-sector-vietnam-land-banks-presales-bonds)). An industrial park leases *land* to *foreign factories* on *long dollar leases*, driven by *FDI*, not domestic mortgages. The two react to *different* macro variables: residential property lives and dies on the credit ceiling and the bond market; industrial parks live and die on FDI inflows and trade. In the 2022 bond crisis, residential developers collapsed while several industrial parks held up far better, precisely because their demand came from foreign factories, not domestic homebuyers. Putting them in the same bucket is the most expensive mistake in the sector.

**Misconception 2: "Ports are utilities — safe, steady, defensive."** Ports *feel* like infrastructure, and infrastructure feels defensive. But a port is a *fixed-cost machine with high operating leverage*, which makes its earnings *cyclical*, not steady. When trade slows — as it did in 2023 — throughput falls, and because the cost base is fixed, profit falls *faster* than volume. We saw it in the operating-leverage worked example: a 20% throughput swing moved EBITDA nearly 3x. A genuine defensive utility (a water company, a power distributor) has stable demand; a port has demand that swings with the global trade cycle. Ports are *cyclical infrastructure*, and treating them as bond-proxy defensives will get you run over when trade rolls over.

**Misconception 3: "A big FDI pledge means immediate profit for the parks."** When the headlines blare that a multinational has *registered* a multi-billion-dollar project, the park stocks often pop — and impatient buyers get disappointed when the next quarter's earnings barely move. Three reasons. First, *registered* FDI is a pledge, not spent money; *disbursed* FDI is what actually builds factories, and it lags registration by quarters or years, and a chunk of registered FDI never fully disburses. Second, even when a park *signs* a lease, the revenue is **recognized over the lease life**, not booked upfront — so the profit trickles for years. Third, the factory has to be *built and ramped* before it exports anything, so the port and logistics benefit lags even further. The headline is real; the *timing* is slow. Trade the direction over quarters, not the press release over days.

**Misconception 4: "Whoever wins the most FDI headlines is the best stock."** Not necessarily. The value in a park is its *land bank* — cleared, zoned, ready-to-lease land near infrastructure — and a company can be busy with announcements while *running out of* leasable land, or sitting on a huge bank that the market hasn't revalued. Two parks with the same FDI tailwind can be worth wildly different amounts depending on how many hectares of *ready* land each controls and at what cost. The right question is not "who's in the news" but "who has the biggest, cheapest, best-located, *cleared* land bank relative to its market cap" — i.e. who trades at the steepest discount to RNAV. The headlines are noise; the land bank is signal.

## How it shows up on VN-Index

The sector's behaviour on the exchange follows the FDI-and-trade machine, and a few episodes make the pattern concrete.

**The 2020-21 liquidity bull and the FDI-wave trade.** In the great liquidity-driven bull market of 2020-21 (covered in the [liquidity and margin cycle post](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/liquidity-and-the-margin-cycle-vietnam)), the industrial-park stocks ran hard alongside everything else — but they ran on a *story* as much as on liquidity: the China+1 narrative was catching fire, manufacturers were publicly diversifying out of China, and Vietnam was the obvious beneficiary. **Kinh Bac (KBC)**, with its big land bank in Bac Ninh and Hai Phong, became a favourite FDI-wave trade — the stock multiplied as investors priced in years of land absorption from the supply-chain shift. The lesson of that run is that industrial parks are a *thematic, story-driven* trade as much as an earnings trade: the market pays *up front* for a multi-year FDI tailwind, then waits (sometimes impatiently) for the leases and earnings to catch up. When the theme is hot, the parks lead; when it cools, they de-rate even if the underlying land bank is intact.

**The 2022-23 trade slowdown and the port pullback.** The mirror image played out in ports. As global trade slowed in 2022 and into 2023 — rich-world consumers retrenched, export orders fell, container freight rates that had spiked to absurd pandemic highs collapsed — the port and shipping names felt the operating leverage in reverse. **Hai An (HAH)**, which owns container ships and so is doubly exposed (terminal *and* freight rate), saw its earnings and stock swing violently as charter rates fell from the pandemic peak. **Viconship (VSC)** and the Hai Phong port complex felt softer throughput. The episode is the operating-leverage worked example in real life: when throughput and rates both soften, port profit falls faster than volume, and the stocks anticipate it. Then, as 2024 volumes recovered and the throughput chart turned back up, the same names re-rated — the cyclical recovery trade.

**GMD and the port-expansion story.** The structural side of the port thesis is *capacity expansion*. **Gemadept (GMD)**, the sector bellwether, spent years building out deep-water terminal capacity — most visibly its **Gemalink** deep-water port in the Cai Mep-Thi Vai cluster in the south, the kind of berth that can take the largest container ships calling directly from Vietnam to the US and Europe (rather than transshipping through Singapore). A deep-water terminal is a *multi-year, capital-heavy* bet on rising trade, and when it fills, the operating leverage we described kicks in hard — a brand-new berth running from low utilization to high utilization is the purest expression of the fixed-cost fulcrum. GMD's stock has long been the way institutional investors play "Vietnam's trade volume grows for a decade", because GMD captures both the terminal and the logistics chain around it. When you see a port operator announcing a new deep-water berth, you are watching it pre-position for the next leg of the trade cycle — and taking on the financing risk and ramp risk that come with it.

![Grouped bar chart of three industrial sub-sector gauges, industrial park occupancy about eighty percent, port utilization about seventy five percent, and logistics growth about twelve percent](/imgs/blogs/industrial-goods-services-sector-vietnam-ports-logistics-6.png)

The chart above is the dashboard a sector investor watches, in three bars. **Industrial-park occupancy** (around 80% in the tight northern hotspots) tells you how much *ready* land is still available to lease — high occupancy means pricing power for the parks and scarcity value in the land bank, but it also means the easy growth is being used up and the next leg needs *new* zones cleared. **Port utilization** (around 75%) tells you how much operating-leverage headroom the terminals have — utilization climbing toward 90% is the bullish operating-leverage setup; utilization falling is the bearish one. **Logistics growth** (around 12%) tracks the connective-tissue layer's expansion with trade. Together these three gauges are the sector's vital signs, and watching them turn is how you front-run the earnings.

![Matrix of the three sub-sectors, industrial parks, ports, and logistics, with columns for main driver, margin profile, and cash pattern](/imgs/blogs/industrial-goods-services-sector-vietnam-ports-logistics-7.png)

The matrix above pins down how the three sub-sectors differ even though they share a driver. **Industrial parks**: driven by FDI land demand, *very high* gross margin on the land spread, but *lumpy* cash because big leases sign irregularly and recognize over years. **Ports**: driven by trade volume, *high fixed-cost* margins that swing with utilization, and *recurring* throughput revenue. **Logistics**: driven by the same trade volume but *thin, competitive* margins and *recurring, low-margin* cash. Same two fuels (FDI and trade), three different earnings shapes — which is why you size and time them differently, the subject of the playbook.

## The playbook: how to trade it

Here is the payoff. Trading the industrial-goods sector well comes down to watching the right *leading* indicators, owning the right *part* of the chain for the part of the cycle you're in, and knowing the one thing that invalidates the whole thesis.

**Watch the leading indicators, in this order.** The sector's master variables print on a known schedule, and they *lead* the earnings.

1. **Disbursed FDI** (monthly and quarterly, from the Ministry of Planning and Investment / GSO). This is the parks' demand curve. Rising disbursed FDI — especially *manufacturing* FDI — is rising land absorption. Watch the *trend*, not one month; the structural climb from \$20bn to \$28bn is the tailwind, and a sustained acceleration (or a stall) is the signal.
2. **Industrial-park occupancy and new lease signings** (company disclosures, provincial data). Occupancy is the parks' inventory gauge; signings fill the backlog that becomes future revenue. A park signing big leases is *front-running* its own reported profit by years — that is your edge, because the market often waits for the lagging earnings.
3. **Container throughput and trade data** (monthly customs/port data; exports and imports). This is the ports' demand curve. Rising throughput plus rising export orders is the bullish port setup; a throughput dip like 2023 is the warning.
4. **Port tariffs and freight rates.** A government move to *raise* port tariffs toward regional levels is nearly pure margin for the operators — a structural re-rating catalyst. Freight rates (for the shippers like HAH) swing the most and warn earliest.

**Own the right part of the chain for the cycle.** The two halves lead and lag differently. **Industrial parks** are the *early* play on an FDI upturn — they sign the leases that *precede* the factories, so they benefit first when the China+1 narrative reaccelerates and capital floods in. **Ports and logistics** are the *later* play — the goods only flow once the factories are built and ramped, so port throughput rises *after* the FDI does, with a one-to-two-year lag. So in the early innings of an FDI wave, lean toward the parks (the land sellers); as the wave's factories come online and export volumes climb, rotate toward the ports (the goods movers). Within each half, prefer the names with the scarce asset: for parks, the biggest *cheap, cleared* land bank at the steepest discount to RNAV (this is where the Becamex / IDICO / Kinh Bac / Viglacera choice gets made); for ports, the deep-water capacity and integrated logistics that capture the most of each container (Gemadept's model).

**Size for what the stocks actually are.** Industrial parks carry land-bank *valuation* risk and funding gearing, so they de-rate hard when credit tightens or FDI stalls even if the land is intact — size them as a geared thematic, not a bond proxy. Ports carry *operating* leverage, so their earnings swing more than their revenue — size them as cyclicals, smaller in late cycle, larger off a trade trough. Neither is the defensive utility it superficially resembles; both are *geared plays on Vietnam's manufacturing and export story*, and a manufacturing-and-export story is, at bottom, a *global growth* bet (covered in the [business-cycle clock post](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/business-cycle-investment-clock-vietnam)) — they do best in a global upcycle and worst when world trade contracts.

**The invalidation — the one signal that ends the thesis.** The entire sector rests on a single structural premise: *that Vietnam keeps winning manufacturing FDI and growing its trade*. So the thing that would prove the bull case wrong is **sustained falling disbursed FDI and falling container throughput at the same time** — not one soft quarter (the 2023 dip was a wave, not a tide-change), but a multi-quarter reversal of the structural climb. That could come from China+1 stalling (multinationals deciding Vietnam is no longer the cheap, stable plus-one — perhaps from rising wages, a tariff shock against Vietnamese exports, or infrastructure bottlenecks), or from a deep global trade recession. If you see disbursed FDI rolling over *and* throughput declining for several quarters *together*, the parks' land banks stop appreciating and the ports' operating leverage runs in reverse — and the whole sector de-rates regardless of how cheap it looks on a single year's earnings. Until that happens, the base case is the structural climb the two demand charts show, with the trade cycle as tradeable waves on top of it.

**Watch for the tariff-reform catalyst on the port side.** There is one sector-specific catalyst worth keeping a standing eye on: Vietnamese seaport handling tariffs have historically been *capped low* by the government, well below regional peers, which compresses port-operator margins. Periodically the authorities allow those tariffs to step up toward regional levels. Because a port's cost base is fixed, a tariff increase is *nearly pure margin* — recall the worked example where a 10% rate rise added \$9mn of almost-all-profit revenue. So a regulatory move to raise port tariffs is a structural re-rating event for the operators, distinct from the throughput cycle: it lifts the *price* leg of throughput-times-rate without needing a single extra container. When you read that the regulator is reviewing or lifting the tariff cap, that is a fundamental catalyst for GMD, VSC, and the terminal operators — independent of where trade volume sits in its cycle. It is the kind of slow-moving policy signal that the market underweights because it is undramatic, which is exactly why it is worth watching.

**A note on foreign flows.** Because this sector *is* the FDI story, it tends to attract foreign portfolio money that wants the manufacturing-relocation theme but in liquid listed form (the mechanics of foreign room and ETF flows are in the [foreign-flows post](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/foreign-flows-etfs-and-the-index-effect-vietnam)). When global investors get bullish on Vietnam's supply-chain story, the industrial-park and port names — the cleanest listed proxies for it — are among the first they buy, which adds a flow tailwind on top of the fundamental one. Watch foreign net buying in the sector's leaders (a sustained foreign net buy of, say, **800 billion dong**, about **\$31.5mn**, into the group) as a confirming signal that the theme is back in favour, and foreign net selling as an early warning that it is going out of fashion. The flow and the fundamental usually move together — foreign money chases the same China+1 story that fills the parks and the ports — so when foreign buying, rising FDI, climbing occupancy, and recovering throughput all line up at once, that confluence is the sector's strongest long signal, and when they diverge (price rising while throughput and FDI soften) it is the warning to trim.

Put it all together and the sector is refreshingly legible: own Vietnam's FDI and export story through the companies that *sell the factories their land* and *move the goods those factories make*; watch FDI, occupancy, and throughput as your leading gauges; play the parks early and the ports late in an FDI wave; value parks on RNAV and ports on mid-cycle EV/EBITDA; and stay long the structural China+1 climb until disbursed FDI and container throughput roll over together.
