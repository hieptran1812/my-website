> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — There is no single "cheap" or "expensive" in the stock market; the right valuation lens depends on what drives the sector, and a 10x P/E means opposite things for a bank, a steelmaker, and a software firm.
>
> - **Banks and brokers live on P/B** (price-to-book), because their balance sheet *is* the business and the multiple is earned by how far return on equity beats the cost of equity.
> - **Property developers live on NAV/RNAV** (land at market value minus net debt), because their reported earnings are lumpy and arrive years after the land was bought.
> - **Cyclicals like steel live on EV/EBITDA at mid-cycle earnings** — never trough or peak — because a trailing P/E is *lowest* exactly when profits have peaked, the deadliest trap in the market.
> - **Stable growers (consumer, IT) live on P/E**, and **utilities on EV/EBITDA plus dividend yield**, because their cash flows are steady enough for a simple earnings multiple to mean something.
> - The one number to remember: a bank at **P/B 0.8x** can be a screaming buy or a value trap depending entirely on its return on equity and the quality of its loan book — the multiple alone tells you nothing.

On a grey Tuesday in mid-2023, two Vietnamese stocks sat on a retail investor's watchlist with what looked like an identical verdict. A large bank traded at roughly nine times its trailing earnings — a P/E of 9. A large steelmaker also traded at about nine times its trailing earnings — a P/E of 9. Two heavyweight names, two "single-digit P/Es," two stocks that any screener would flag as cheap. The investor, reasonably enough, treated them as the same kind of opportunity and split his money between them.

Within a year the two had done completely different things, and not because one company was run better than the other. The bank's nine-times earnings turned out to be a fair, even slightly rich, price for a steady stream of profits — the stock drifted sideways. The steelmaker's nine-times earnings turned out to be a *mirage*: that P/E of 9 was computed on a year of bumper, top-of-cycle profits that were already collapsing. By the time the next set of accounts came out, the same share price sat on a P/E of 30, because the "E" — the earnings — had cratered. The stock had looked cheapest at the exact moment it was most dangerous.

This is the single most important idea in equity valuation, and almost no beginner is taught it: **the same multiple means different things in different sectors.** A P/E of 9 is reassuring for a bank and a warning siren for a cyclical. A P/B of 0.8 is a gift for a well-run lender and a death sentence for a badly-run one. Picking the wrong valuation lens for a sector is like measuring temperature with a ruler — you will get a number, it will feel precise, and it will be meaningless. This post teaches you which lens belongs to which sector on VN-Index, why, and how to avoid the two traps — the cyclical trap and the value trap — that punish investors who use the wrong one.

## Foundations: what a valuation multiple actually is

Let us start from absolute zero, because every term in this post is built on this one idea.

A **valuation multiple** is a ratio. On the top is the **price** the market is paying — either the share price, or the whole company's value. On the bottom is some **fundamental** the company produces — its earnings, its book value, its assets, or its cash flow. The multiple answers one question: *how many units of the fundamental am I paying one unit of price for?* When a stock trades at "P/E of 10," you are paying ten dong of price for every one dong of annual earnings. When it trades at "P/B of 1.5," you are paying 1.5 dong of price for every one dong of accounting net worth.

Why bother with a ratio at all? Because a raw price is meaningless on its own. A share that costs 200,000 dong is not "more expensive" than one that costs 20,000 dong — the first company might earn ten times as much per share. To compare companies of different sizes and different share counts, you have to divide price by *something the company produces*. The multiple is that division. It turns a price into a *price per unit of value*, which you can compare across companies, across time, and against history.

Here is the part beginners miss. **The fundamental you choose to divide by is not neutral — it encodes a theory of what makes the company valuable.** Dividing by earnings (P/E) says "this company is worth its profit stream." Dividing by book value (P/B) says "this company is worth the net assets on its balance sheet." Dividing by cash flow (EV/EBITDA) says "this company is worth the cash its operations throw off." Each is a different bet on *where the value lives*. And value lives in different places in different sectors — in a bank's book of loans, in a developer's land, in a steelmaker's cash flow, in a software firm's earnings. Choose the divisor that matches where the value actually lives, and the multiple is informative. Choose the wrong one, and it lies to you.

Let us build the four main lenses one at a time, then assign each to the sectors it fits.

### Price-to-earnings (P/E): the default lens, and its blind spot

The **P/E ratio** is price per share divided by earnings per share (EPS) over the last twelve months. (EPS is just the company's total net profit divided by the number of shares.) A P/E of 15 means you pay fifteen dong for each dong of annual profit; flipped over, it means the company's current earnings are a 1/15 = 6.7% "earnings yield" on your purchase price.

P/E is the world's default valuation lens because it is intuitive: profit is what an owner ultimately gets, so paying a price relative to profit feels right. And for a company whose earnings are **stable and predictable**, it works beautifully. If a consumer-goods firm earns roughly the same profit every year and grows it slowly, then last year's earnings are a fair guide to next year's, and "fifteen times earnings" genuinely tells you how many years of profit you are paying for.

P/E has one fatal blind spot, and it is the source of half the disasters in this post: **it assumes the current "E" is representative.** The moment a company's earnings swing wildly from year to year — boom one year, loss the next — the trailing P/E becomes worse than useless. It will be tiny when earnings are at a peak (a big denominator) and huge when earnings are at a trough (a tiny denominator), which is the *exact opposite* of what a useful valuation signal should do. Hold this thought; it is the cyclical trap, and we devote a whole section to it.

It helps to see P/E flipped on its head. The reciprocal of P/E — one divided by the multiple — is the **earnings yield**, the company's current profit as a percentage of the price you pay. A P/E of 10 is a 10% earnings yield; a P/E of 20 is a 5% earnings yield; a P/E of 25 is a 4% earnings yield. Viewing it this way connects stocks to everything else you could own. If a Vietnamese ten-year government bond yields, say, 4%, then a stock at a P/E of 25 offers the *same* 4% starting yield with far more risk — which is only worth it if that earnings stream is going to *grow*. A stock at a P/E of 10 offers a 10% starting yield, a fat premium over the bond, which is generous *unless* those earnings are about to fall. So the earnings-yield view immediately reframes the central question: a low P/E (high earnings yield) is only a bargain if the earnings will *hold*, and a high P/E (low earnings yield) is only justified if the earnings will *grow*. The multiple alone cannot tell you which; the *sector* and the *cycle* tell you which. This is the bridge from "a number on a screener" to "a judgment about a business," and the rest of the post is teaching you to cross it.

### Price-to-book (P/B): the lens for balance-sheet businesses

The **P/B ratio** is price per share divided by **book value per share**. Book value (also called shareholders' equity or net asset value in the accounting sense) is simply *everything the company owns minus everything it owes*, as recorded on its balance sheet, divided by the share count. If a company's assets are worth 100 and its debts are 70, its book value is 30. P/B of 1.0 means you are paying exactly the accounting net worth; P/B of 2.0 means you are paying twice it.

Why would you ever value a company on its book rather than its profit? Because for some businesses, **the balance sheet *is* the business.** A bank is the cleanest example. A bank's assets are its loans and securities; its liabilities are its deposits and borrowings; the difference — its equity, its book value — is the capital base on which it earns. A bank doesn't own much in the way of factories or brands; it owns financial claims, marked at something close to their stated value. So its book value is a real, meaningful number, and what the bank does is earn a *return on that book*. That return has a name — **ROE, return on equity** — and it is the single most important number in bank valuation, as we will see in a moment.

### Net asset value (NAV / RNAV): the lens for asset-heavy, lumpy-earnings firms

**NAV** stands for **net asset value**: the market value of everything a company owns, minus its debt. For a property developer the refined version is **RNAV — revalued net asset value** — where you re-price the company's biggest asset (its land bank) at *current market value* rather than the historical cost on its books, then subtract net debt. The result is your estimate of what the company's assets would fetch if liquidated today, which for an asset-holding business is a far better anchor than its erratic reported profit.

NAV is the right lens precisely when **earnings are a bad guide to value** — when profit arrives in unpredictable lumps that have little to do with the steady accumulation of value underneath. A developer buys land for years, builds for years, and then books a huge chunk of profit all at once when a project hands over. Its P/E swings violently for reasons that have nothing to do with whether the business is healthy. But the *land it sits on* is accumulating value the whole time, whether or not any profit has been booked. NAV looks straight through the earnings noise to the assets.

### Enterprise value to EBITDA (EV/EBITDA): the lens for capital-heavy and cyclical firms

This one has the scariest name and the simplest idea. **EV — enterprise value** — is the value of the *whole company to all its funders*, not just shareholders: market value of equity **plus** net debt. (You add debt because if you bought the entire firm, you would inherit its debts; EV is the true "takeover price.") **EBITDA** is **earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization** — a rough proxy for the raw cash the operations generate before financing and accounting choices muddy it. **EV/EBITDA** asks: for the whole company, how many years of operating cash flow am I paying?

Why use it? Two reasons. First, because it is **capital-structure-neutral.** P/E can be flattered or wrecked by how much debt a company carries; EV/EBITDA puts a heavily-indebted steelmaker and a debt-free one on the same footing, because EV already includes the debt and EBITDA is measured before interest. For capital-heavy sectors — steel, utilities, anything with big plants and big borrowings — this matters enormously. Consider two identical steel plants earning the same operating cash flow; one is financed entirely by equity, the other half by debt. Their P/Es will look completely different (the indebted one pays interest, lowering net profit and per-share earnings) even though the underlying business is identical. EV/EBITDA sees through this: both have the same enterprise value relative to the same operating cash flow, so both get the same multiple. When you are comparing companies that have made different financing choices — which is the norm in capital-heavy sectors — this neutrality is indispensable. Second, because **EBITDA is steadier than net profit** for these firms: it strips out the depreciation of those big plants (a non-cash accounting charge) and the interest bill, so it wobbles less than the bottom line. For a cyclical, that relative steadiness is exactly what you want to anchor to — and crucially, you anchor it to *mid-cycle* EBITDA, not the peak or the trough.

The honesty note from the misconceptions section applies here too, and it is worth stating up front: EBITDA's exclusions are a feature *for comparison* and a danger *for valuation*. A steelmaker's depreciation is not a fiction — its blast furnaces genuinely wear out and must be rebuilt with real cash every couple of decades. Its interest is real money leaving the company. EV/EBITDA is the right tool for asking "is this cyclical cheap *relative to other cyclicals and to its own history*"; it is not a license to pretend that the capital intensity and the debt service don't exist. The disciplined practitioner uses EV/EBITDA to compare and then checks that the free cash flow *after* the real capital spending and interest is positive over a full cycle.

### A word on enterprise value versus market cap

Because EV trips up nearly every beginner, it earns its own paragraph. **Market capitalization** is just share price times share count — the value of the *equity*, what the shareholders' slice is worth. **Enterprise value** adds net debt on top: EV = market cap + total debt − cash. The reason is the takeover thought-experiment. If you wanted to buy the *entire* company outright, paying off the shareholders is not enough — you also inherit its debts (you must repay or assume them), but you get to keep its cash (you can use it to pay down those debts). So the true all-in price of acquiring the whole business is equity value plus debt minus cash, which is exactly EV. A company with a 50-trillion-dong market cap, 30 trillion of debt, and 5 trillion of cash has an EV of 50 + 30 − 5 = **75 trillion dong** — half again its market cap. This is why two firms with the same market cap can have very different EVs, and why EV/EBITDA can rank them differently than P/E does: the debt-laden one carries a bigger EV for the same operating cash flow, so it is *more* expensive on EV/EBITDA even if its P/E looks lower. For capital-heavy sectors where debt levels vary widely, ignoring this is how you mistake a leveraged company for a cheap one.

### Dividend yield: the lens for cash cows

**Dividend yield** is the annual dividend per share divided by the share price — the cash return you get just for holding, before any price move. A stock paying 2,000 dong a year on a 25,000 dong price yields 8%. For sectors whose appeal *is* the cash they hand back — mature utilities, some staples — yield is a primary lens, because investors there are buying an income stream, much like a bond. We will treat it as a co-lens for utilities alongside EV/EBITDA.

So: five lenses — P/E, P/B, NAV, EV/EBITDA, dividend yield — each encoding a different theory of where value lives. The whole craft is matching the lens to the sector. The figure at the top of this post is the cheat sheet; the rest of this section earns each highlighted cell.

## Banks and brokers live on P/B — and ROE sets the multiple

Open any Vietnamese broker's report on a bank and the headline number is almost never the P/E. It is the **P/B**. This is not a stylistic quirk; it is forced by what a bank is.

Recall that a bank's whole business is earning a return on its equity capital. Regulators require it to hold a minimum cushion of equity against its loans (the capital-adequacy ratio), and the bank's job is to earn as much profit as it safely can on that cushion. So the natural question for an owner is not "how many years of *this year's* profit am I paying?" but "how much am I paying for each dong of the *capital base* that generates the profit, and how good is the bank at compounding that base?" That is precisely what P/B measures.

But P/B on its own is incomplete, and here is the rule that ties it together — the most important sentence in bank valuation:

**A bank's justified P/B is driven by the gap between its return on equity (ROE) and its cost of equity (CoE).**

Walk through the logic. **ROE** is the profit a bank earns each year as a percentage of its book value — if book value is 100 and profit is 18, ROE is 18%. **Cost of equity** is the annual return shareholders *demand* for taking on the risk of owning the bank — call it 13% in Vietnam, reflecting the risk-free government bond rate plus a risk premium. Now the intuition:

- If a bank earns **exactly** its cost of equity (ROE = CoE = 13%), it is creating no extra value for shareholders beyond what they demanded. Its fair P/B is **1.0** — you pay exactly book value.
- If a bank earns **more** than its cost of equity (ROE 18% > CoE 13%), every dong of book is being compounded faster than shareholders require. That excess is worth paying *above* book for. Fair P/B is **above 1.0**.
- If a bank earns **less** than its cost of equity (ROE 6% < CoE 13%), it is *destroying* value — each dong of book earns less than shareholders want. Fair P/B is **below 1.0**, and the stock deserves to trade at a discount to book.

A common simplification (the Gordon-growth form) gives justified P/B ≈ (ROE − g) / (CoE − g), where g is the long-run growth rate; with modest growth it collapses to roughly ROE / CoE. The point is not the formula — it is the relationship: **higher ROE earns a higher P/B; an ROE below the cost of equity earns a P/B below one.** When you see a Vietnamese bank at P/B 2.0, the market is saying "this bank's ROE is well above its cost of equity and will stay there." When you see one at 0.7, the market is saying "this bank earns less than its cost of equity, or its book value is not what it claims."

![Flow diagram of how return on equity versus cost of equity sets a bank justified price to book](/imgs/blogs/valuation-by-sector-pe-pb-nav-ev-ebitda-3.png)

There is a subtlety in bank book value that the P/B lens forces you to confront, and it is where most value traps hide: **a bank's book value is only as honest as its loan-loss provisioning.** A bank's biggest asset is its loan book, carried on the balance sheet at face value *minus* a provision for loans it expects to go bad. If management under-provisions — sets aside too little for souring loans — the stated book value is inflated, and every multiple computed on it is wrong. This is why a P/B of 0.8x can be a trap: the market may be pricing in a haircut to a book value it does not believe. Two banks with identical 1.0x P/B can be worlds apart if one has conservatively provisioned a clean book and the other is sitting on a pile of under-provisioned property loans. The lens (P/B) is correct for both; the input (book value) is trustworthy for only one. Reading a bank, therefore, means reading two numbers behind the P/B: the **ROE** (does the bank earn its keep on that book?) and the **NPL and provisioning trend** (is the book real?). A high, stable ROE on a conservatively-provisioned book earns a premium P/B; a low ROE on a suspect book earns — and deserves — a discount.

Brokers (securities firms, the *cong ty chung khoan*) get valued on P/B for a related reason: like banks, their balance sheet is the business. A broker's assets are its margin loans to clients (*ky quy*, the leverage retail investors borrow to buy more stock) and its proprietary trading book; its equity is the capital backing those. Their earnings are even more volatile than banks' — huge in a bull market when trading volumes and margin balances explode, thin in a bear market — so a P/E on broker earnings is almost meaningless year to year. A broker can trade at a single-digit P/E at the top of a bull market (peak earnings, the cyclical trap again) and a triple-digit P/E in a bear market (collapsed earnings), all while its book value barely moves. P/B, anchored to the steadier capital base, is the sane lens; brokers are the highest-beta financials in Vietnam and are covered in their own post in this series.

#### Worked example: a bank's P/B versus its justified P/B

Take a large Vietnamese bank with a book value of **50,000 dong per share** and a market price of **75,000 dong per share**. Its actual P/B is 75,000 / 50,000 = **1.5x** — the market pays one and a half times book.

Is that fair? Apply the ROE-vs-CoE rule. Suppose the bank's sustainable ROE is **18%** and its cost of equity is **13%**, with negligible long-run real growth assumed for simplicity. The rough justified multiple is ROE / CoE = 18 / 13 ≈ **1.4x**. So the market's 1.5x is a touch *above* the justified 1.4x — the stock is slightly rich, pricing in either a higher ROE or faster growth than our conservative inputs assume.

Now anchor it in dollars so the scale is concrete. If this bank has 14 billion shares, its market cap is 14bn × 75,000 = 1,050 trillion dong; at the mid-2026 rate of \$1 = 25,400 VND, that is about **\$41bn** — a genuine heavyweight. A move from a 1.5x to a 1.4x multiple, holding book constant, is roughly a 7% lower price — about **\$2.9bn** of market value riding on a half-turn of P/B. **The intuition: for a bank, the multiple is *earned* by ROE; pay above the ROE-justified P/B only if you believe ROE will rise or the bank will grow faster than its cost of equity.**

## Property developers live on NAV/RNAV — because earnings lie

Now switch sectors and watch the right lens change completely. A property developer's reported earnings are some of the most misleading numbers in the whole market, and valuing one on P/E will get you hurt in both directions.

Here is why earnings lie for a developer. Vietnamese accounting books a project's profit largely at **handover** — when finished apartments are delivered to buyers and revenue is recognized. But the *work* and the *value creation* happened over the preceding three-to-five years: buying the land, getting permits, building. So a developer can spend four years quietly accumulating value with almost no reported profit, then book an enormous one-year profit spike when a big project hands over, then drop back to near-zero the next year. Its P/E is a rollercoaster that has nothing to do with the health of the business — sky-high in the quiet years (tiny E), comically low in the handover year (huge E). (The related term you will hear is *presales* — apartments sold off-plan before completion, cash the developer collects early but cannot book as profit until handover; presales are a far better health gauge than reported earnings, and they get their own treatment in the real-estate post.)

So we throw out earnings and value the *assets*. The lens is **RNAV** — revalued net asset value:

1. Take the developer's **land bank** and re-price it at *current market value* (not the historical cost sitting on the books, which is often a fraction of today's value for land bought years ago).
2. **Subtract net debt** (total borrowings minus cash). Developers are heavy borrowers, often funded by *trai phieu doanh nghiep* — corporate bonds — so net debt is large and matters enormously.
3. The result is **NAV**: what the equity would be worth if you marked the land to market and paid off the debt. Divide by shares for **NAV per share.**

Then you compare the share price to NAV per share. Trading **below** NAV is a discount; **above** NAV is a premium. Vietnamese developers very often trade at a *discount* to RNAV — sometimes a steep one — because the market discounts execution risk, the risk that land never gets permitted or built, and (after 2022) the risk that the debt funding the land bank cannot be rolled over.

Why a *discount* rather than a premium, almost as a rule? Three deductions the market applies to a developer's gross land value, none of which appears on the balance sheet:

- **Permitting and legal risk.** A great deal of Vietnamese development land is not fully cleared — it lacks the red-book land-use certificate, the planning approval, or the cleared site needed to actually build. Land you cannot yet build on is worth less than land you can, and the gap between "land on the books" and "land that can become apartments" is the single biggest reason RNAV estimates differ between analysts. A conservative analyst values un-permitted land at a fraction of permitted land; an aggressive one marks it near full value. Same company, very different NAV.
- **Time and the discount rate.** Land that will be developed in eight years is worth less today than land being built now, because value far in the future is discounted back. A huge raw land bank that will take a decade to monetize does not deserve full market value today.
- **Funding fragility.** If the debt financing the land bank is short-dated bonds that must be refinanced, the developer is one frozen credit market away from a forced sale. The market prices that fragility as a discount — and in a crisis, as we will see, the discount can become a wipeout.

This is why "trading at a discount to NAV" is never, by itself, a buy signal for a developer. The right question is always *which* of these deductions the discount is compensating you for, and whether it is enough. A 10% discount on a developer with un-permitted land and bond debt maturing next quarter is not cheap; a 40% discount on a developer with clean, permitted land and long-dated funding might be a gift.

#### Worked example: a developer's RNAV and discount

A developer's **land bank** is independently valued at **80 trillion dong** at current market prices. It carries **net debt of 30 trillion dong**. So its **NAV = 80 − 30 = 50 trillion dong** — at \$1 = 25,400 VND, about **\$2bn** of net asset value.

Say it has **1 billion shares** outstanding. NAV per share = 50 trillion / 1 billion = **50,000 dong per share.** Now look at the market price: **35,000 dong.** The stock trades at 35,000 / 50,000 = 0.70 of NAV — a **30% discount to RNAV.**

Is a 30% discount a buy? Only if you trust the two inputs. If the 80-trillion land valuation is honest and the land is genuinely permitted and saleable, a 30% discount is a real margin of safety. But if a chunk of that "land bank" is tied up in projects with no legal clearance, the true NAV is lower and the discount is illusory — and if the 30 trillion of debt includes bonds maturing next quarter that cannot be refinanced, the equity could be wiped out entirely regardless of the land's paper value. **The intuition: NAV looks through lumpy earnings to the assets, but it is only as good as your honesty about what the land is really worth and whether the debt can be rolled.**

## Cyclicals like steel live on EV/EBITDA at mid-cycle — and beware the P/E trap

This is the section that would have saved our mid-2023 investor. A **cyclical** is a company whose profits swing dramatically with the economic cycle — steel, chemicals, shipping, commodities. For these, the trailing P/E is not just unhelpful; it is *actively dangerous*, because it sends precisely the wrong signal at precisely the wrong time.

Here is the mechanism, and it is worth slowing down for. A steelmaker's share price tends to be *forward-looking and relatively smooth* — the market knows steel is cyclical and looks through any single year. But its **earnings are violently cyclical** — huge at the top of the construction-and-commodity cycle, near-zero or negative at the bottom. Now compute P/E = price / earnings:

- At the **earnings peak**, the denominator is huge, so the P/E is **low**. The stock screens as cheap — single-digit P/E! — at the exact moment its profits are about to fall.
- At the **earnings trough**, the denominator is tiny, so the P/E is **high**. The stock screens as expensive at the exact moment its profits are about to recover.

The trailing P/E of a cyclical moves *inversely* to where you are in the cycle. It is lowest when you should be most cautious and highest when you should be most interested. This is the **cyclical trap**, and it is the most expensive single mistake a Vietnamese retail investor makes with steel names.

![Line chart showing a steelmaker earnings peak in 2021 with the P/E lowest at the peak and highest at the trough](/imgs/blogs/valuation-by-sector-pe-pb-nav-ev-ebitda-5.png)

The chart above tells the whole story with a stylized steelmaker. Earnings (the blue line) peak hard in 2021 — Vietnam's steel sector had a banner year as global prices spiked — then collapse through 2022–2023. The P/E (the red dashed line) does the opposite: it sits at its *lowest* in the 2021 peak year, luring in the value screeners, then balloons as earnings crater. Anyone who bought "the cheap single-digit P/E steel stock" in early 2022 bought the top.

So how *do* you value a cyclical? Two fixes, used together:

1. **Use EV/EBITDA, not P/E.** EBITDA wobbles less than net profit (it excludes depreciation and interest), and EV is capital-structure-neutral, which matters for debt-heavy steelmakers. But EBITDA still cycles — so you apply the second fix.
2. **Normalize to mid-cycle earnings.** Don't value the company on peak EBITDA or trough EBITDA. Estimate what the firm earns in an *average* year across a full cycle — mid-cycle EBITDA — and apply your multiple to that. This deliberately ignores the current year, whether it is a boom or a bust, and anchors to the through-the-cycle reality.

The combination — EV/EBITDA on mid-cycle earnings — is immune to the trap. It refuses to be fooled by a peak year's flattering numbers or a trough year's frightening ones.

How do you actually estimate "mid-cycle" EBITDA, given that no single reported year *is* mid-cycle? Three practical methods, in rough order of rigor:

- **Average across a full cycle.** Take the company's EBITDA over the last seven-to-ten years — long enough to span at least one peak and one trough — and average it. The average of a boom and a bust is a serviceable proxy for a normal year. For Vietnamese steel, a window that includes the 2021 peak and the 2022–2023 trough gives a far more honest center than either endpoint.
- **Normalize the margin, not the profit.** Estimate a *through-the-cycle* EBITDA margin (cash profit as a percentage of revenue) and apply it to a normalized revenue or sales-volume level. This separates the two things that cycle — volume and margin — and lets you reason about each. Steel margins, driven by the spread between steel prices and ore-and-coal costs, are the volatile part; volumes, driven by construction demand, are steadier.
- **Mid-cycle ROIC.** For the most rigorous version, estimate a normal return on invested capital across the cycle and back out the EBITDA it implies on the company's asset base. This is what sell-side analysts mean by a "normalized" model.

You will never get mid-cycle exactly right, and that is fine — the goal is not precision, it is to *refuse to anchor on the current year* when the current year is plainly a peak or a trough. Even a crude full-cycle average beats a pinpoint-accurate peak-year multiple, because the peak-year multiple is precisely accurate about the wrong thing.

#### Worked example: steel on EV/EBITDA, peak versus mid-cycle

A steelmaker has an **enterprise value of 120 trillion dong** (equity market cap plus net debt). Its **mid-cycle EBITDA** — a normal-year estimate across the full cycle — is **20 trillion dong.** So EV/EBITDA on mid-cycle earnings is 120 / 20 = **6.0x** — a reasonable, even cheap, multiple for a quality cyclical.

Now watch the trap. Suppose in the 2021 peak year the same company earned **40 trillion dong** of EBITDA — double the mid-cycle figure. Computed on *that* peak number, EV/EBITDA is 120 / 40 = **3.0x.** Three times cash flow! That looks absurdly cheap, and a naive buyer pays up — right as the cycle turns. The next year EBITDA falls back toward 20 and below, the multiple "re-rates" to 6x and higher purely from the denominator shrinking, and the stock falls even if the EV is unchanged. Convert for scale: that 120 trillion dong EV is about **\$4.7bn** at \$1 = 25,400 VND, so the difference between buying at a real 6x and a fake 3x is billions of dollars of misjudged value. **The intuition: for a cyclical, always ask "what multiple is this on *mid-cycle* earnings?" — a multiple computed on peak-year numbers is a trap dressed up as a bargain.**

## Consumer, IT, and utilities: where the simple lenses work

Not every sector is a minefield. For companies with **stable, predictable, growing earnings**, the humble P/E does its job, because this year's earnings really are a fair guide to next year's.

**Consumer staples and food & beverage** — the companies selling things Vietnamese households buy every week regardless of the economy — have steady volumes and pricing power. Their earnings grow at a measured pace and rarely crater. P/E works, and these names typically command *higher* P/Es (the market pays up for predictability and brand strength), which is why our data shows food & beverage and retail at P/Es of 18–20 against the index's 12.

The chart below makes the cross-sector spread concrete: it plots the approximate trailing P/E and P/B for eight VN-Index sectors side by side, with the index's own averages (P/E ~12.1, P/B ~1.9) drawn as reference lines. Read it carefully, because it is the visual proof of this whole post's thesis. Banks sit at the *lowest* P/E (~9) and a low P/B (~1.5) — but as we have seen, that low P/E is not the right lens for them at all; their P/B against ROE is. IT sits at the *highest* of both (P/E ~22, P/B ~4.5) — which looks expensive until you remember the growth that justifies it. Food and beverage and retail carry P/Bs of 3–3.5 because their value is in brands, not on the balance sheet, so a high price-to-book is normal and tells you little. The chart does *not* say "banks are cheap and IT is expensive"; it says "every sector trades in its own range, on its own appropriate lens, and you cannot rank them by a single raw multiple." That is precisely the discipline the rest of the post is teaching.

![Grouped bar chart of approximate trailing P/E and P/B by VN-Index sector with index reference lines](/imgs/blogs/valuation-by-sector-pe-pb-nav-ev-ebitda-2.png)

**Information technology** — led by Vietnam's structural-growth IT champion — is valued on P/E too, but a *forward* P/E that prices in growth. A software-and-services firm growing earnings 20%+ a year deserves a high multiple: a P/E of 22 on a company doubling its profit every four years is not expensive in the way a P/E of 22 on a no-growth utility would be. (The growth-adjusted version, the PEG ratio — P/E divided by the growth rate — formalizes this, but the instinct is enough: pay a higher P/E only for genuine, durable growth.)

**Utilities** — power generators, water companies — sit in a different spot again. Their earnings are stable (regulated or contracted), but they are *capital-heavy and often debt-funded*, so the same logic as cyclicals applies on the structure side: **EV/EBITDA** is the cleaner lens because it neutralizes the heavy debt. And because mature utilities pay out most of their cash as dividends, **dividend yield** is a co-primary lens — investors buy them substantially for the income, comparing the yield to government bond yields the way they would a bond. A utility yielding 8% when the ten-year government bond yields 4% is offering a real premium for its (modest) extra risk.

The dividend-yield lens deserves a moment, because it is the one valuation tool that connects a stock directly to the bond market and to the cost of money. A mature utility's appeal is that it behaves like a bond with a growing coupon: it generates steady, regulated cash and hands most of it back. So you value it the way you would a bond — by its yield relative to the alternative. When government bond yields *rise*, a utility's dividend yield must rise to stay competitive, which (with the dividend roughly fixed) means its price must *fall*; when bond yields fall, money chases the utility's higher yield and its price rises. This is why utilities and other high-yield "bond-proxy" sectors are interest-rate-sensitive in the opposite direction to banks: rising rates help bank margins but hurt the bond-proxy's price. A Vietnamese power generator on a 9% dividend yield against a 4% government bond is offering five percentage points of extra yield to compensate for the (small) risk that the dividend gets cut — a wide spread that looks attractive *if* the cash flow is genuinely stable. The trap on the yield lens is a yield that is high because the dividend is about to be cut, not because the stock is cheap — the income-investor's version of the value trap.

This is why the cheat-sheet matrix at the top lights up *different* cells for different rows: P/B for banks and brokers, NAV for property, EV/EBITDA at mid-cycle for steel, P/E for consumer and IT, EV/EBITDA-plus-yield for utilities. There is no universal "cheap." There is only "cheap *on the right lens for this sector*."

## Matching the lens to the sector: the decision

Step back and the whole framework reduces to one branching question — *what drives this company's value?* — with four answers and four lenses.

- If the **balance sheet is the engine** (a bank or broker earning a return on its capital base), use **P/B**, and judge the multiple against ROE versus cost of equity.
- If the company holds **lumpy assets whose earnings arrive in unpredictable bursts** (a developer sitting on a land bank), use **NAV/RNAV**, land at market value minus net debt.
- If it is a **commodity cyclical** whose profits swing with the cycle (steel, chemicals), use **EV/EBITDA on mid-cycle earnings**, never trailing P/E.
- If it is a **stable grower** with predictable earnings (consumer, IT), use **P/E**, growth-adjusted for the fast growers; for capital-heavy steady payers (utilities), **EV/EBITDA plus dividend yield**.

![Decision tree for choosing between P/B, NAV, EV/EBITDA, and P/E based on what drives the company](/imgs/blogs/valuation-by-sector-pe-pb-nav-ev-ebitda-7.png)

The decision tree above is the field guide. Notice that it routes on the *nature of the business*, not on a screener's output. You never start from "this has a low P/E"; you start from "what kind of company is this," pick the lens, and only then read the multiple. This ordering is the entire defense against both traps.

One more layer makes the framework practical: **compare a sector's multiple to its own history, not to other sectors.** It is meaningless to say "banks at P/B 1.5 are cheaper than IT at P/B 4.5" — they are different businesses with different right multiples. It is very meaningful to say "banks at P/B 1.5 are below their own five-year average of 2.0, so banks are cheap *relative to how banks usually trade*." Every sector has its own normal range; cheapness is a deviation from a sector's own history, which is why the next figure plots each sector against its own past rather than against the others.

## Common misconceptions

**"A low P/E always means a stock is cheap."** The single most expensive belief in the market. For a cyclical, a low trailing P/E often means earnings are at a *peak* about to fall — the stock is cheapest exactly when it is most dangerous. Our stylized steelmaker had its lowest P/E in its best year, 2021, right before earnings halved. Always ask *which* sector and *where in the cycle* before reading a low P/E as good news.

**"A bank below book value (P/B under 1.0) is a bargain."** Sometimes — and sometimes a value trap. A P/B below 1.0 is *justified* when ROE is below the cost of equity. A bank earning 6% ROE against a 13% cost of equity *should* trade below book; it is destroying value. If the low P/B also comes with rising non-performing loans (NPLs — loans that have stopped being repaid), the stated book value is itself overstated, because those bad loans will have to be written down. Cheap-looking and cheap-for-a-reason look identical on a screener.

**"You can compare P/Es across sectors to find the cheapest one."** No. A 9x P/E bank and a 22x P/E software firm are not "the bank is cheaper." They have different right multiples because their earnings have different quality, stability, and growth. Comparing raw P/Es across sectors is comparing a temperature to a weight. Compare each sector to *its own* history.

**"NAV is the 'true' value, so a developer below NAV is always cheap."** Only if the NAV is honest. RNAV depends on (1) the land being valued correctly and (2) the land being legally clear and saleable. A developer at a 30% discount to a *fantasy* NAV — land that can't be permitted, valued at prices nobody will pay — is not cheap at all. And the net-debt subtraction is unforgiving: enough un-rollable bond debt can take the equity to zero whatever the land is "worth."

**"EBITDA is real profit."** EBITDA deliberately excludes interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization — useful for comparing operating cash generation, dangerous if you forget that a steelmaker's depreciation reflects real plant that genuinely wears out and must be replaced, and its interest is real cash leaving the building. EV/EBITDA is the right *comparative* lens for capital-heavy cyclicals; it is not a claim that depreciation and interest don't matter.

## How it shows up on VN-Index

The framework is not academic — it has decided winners and losers in every recent VN-Index cycle.

**The 2021 steel peak and the cyclical trap, live.** In 2021, Vietnam's steel sector posted record profits as global steel prices spiked. The largest names traded at single-digit trailing P/Es at the top — textbook "cheap." Retail investors piled in on the low P/E. Through 2022 the cycle turned: steel prices fell, demand softened, and earnings collapsed; some quarters of 2022–2023 showed losses. The trailing P/E that had been ~6x at the 2021 peak ballooned as the "E" vanished, and the share prices fell sharply. Anyone valuing those names on **mid-cycle EV/EBITDA** instead of peak P/E would have seen them as fully-valued-to-expensive at the 2021 top, and avoided the trap. This is the section's whole thesis playing out in real money.

**The 2022 bond crisis and developer NAV.** When the corporate-bond market froze in late 2022, property developers that looked "cheap on NAV" were re-rated brutally. The market suddenly cared far more about the *net-debt* term in the NAV calculation than the *land* term: a developer with a huge land bank but un-rollable bonds maturing was not cheap at a discount to NAV — its NAV was at risk of going *negative* if it had to fire-sell land to meet bond payments. Investors who had anchored only on the land value, ignoring the debt and the rollover risk, were badly hurt. The episode is a permanent lesson in why the debt subtraction in NAV is not a footnote — it is half the calculation. (The bond crisis gets its own case-study post in this series.)

**Banks' P/B compression after 2022.** Vietnamese banks spent 2022–2023 trading at unusually low P/Bs — several large banks dipped toward or below 1.0x — as the market worried about property-loan exposure and rising NPLs eating into book value. The right question was never "are banks cheap because P/B is low?" but "is the *book value* real, and will ROE hold up?" Banks with clean books and sustained high ROE that traded down to ~1.0x P/B were genuine bargains; banks with hidden property-related NPLs at the same 1.0x were value traps, because their true book was lower than reported. Same multiple, opposite verdict — decided entirely by ROE and asset quality, exactly as the framework predicts.

**Brokers' P/E whipsaw across the 2020–2022 round trip.** Vietnam's securities brokers are the purest demonstration that P/E is the wrong lens for a balance-sheet business with violently cyclical earnings. In the 2020–2021 liquidity boom, retail trading volumes and margin balances exploded, broker profits soared, and at the top the leading brokers traded at modest, even single-digit, trailing P/Es on those peak earnings — the cyclical trap wearing a financial-sector costume. Then 2022 arrived: volumes collapsed, margin balances shrank, proprietary trading books took losses, and broker earnings fell off a cliff, sending trailing P/Es to absurd highs even as the *stocks* fell hard. An investor anchoring on P/B instead would have seen the same brokers at elevated price-to-book at the 2021 top (correctly expensive) and depressed price-to-book in the 2022 trough (correctly cheap), reading the cycle right side up while the P/E read it upside down. Same stocks, same dates — the lens decided who saw clearly.

**IT's persistent premium.** Throughout, Vietnam's structural-growth IT leader has held a P/E far above the index — often 20x+ against the index's ~12x — and the market has, broadly, been right to pay it. Twenty-plus times earnings on a firm compounding profit 20%+ a year is not the same expensive as twenty-plus times on a no-growth name. Investors who dismissed it as "too expensive on P/E" while ignoring the growth left a lot on the table. The lens for IT is growth-adjusted P/E, and the growth justified the multiple.

#### Worked example: the value trap, made concrete

Put the bank misconception into numbers. A bank trades at **P/B 0.8x** — a 20% discount to its stated book value of, say, 40,000 dong per share, so a price of 32,000 dong. A screener flags it as the cheapest bank in the sector. But its **ROE is just 6%** against a **cost of equity of 13%**, and its NPL ratio is climbing as property loans sour.

Apply the framework. Justified P/B ≈ ROE / CoE = 6 / 13 ≈ **0.46x.** The bank "should" trade at *less than half* of book given how little it earns on that book — so even at 0.8x it is arguably *expensive*, not cheap, relative to what its ROE deserves. Worse, the rising NPLs mean the stated 40,000-dong book is overstated: write down the bad loans and the real book might be 32,000 dong, making the "discount" vanish entirely. A bank with 2 billion shares at 32,000 dong is a ~64-trillion-dong, roughly **\$2.5bn** company that looks like a bargain and is a trap. **The intuition: a low P/B is only cheap if ROE is healthy and the book is real; a low ROE *justifies* a low P/B, and rising NPLs mean the book itself is fiction.**

## The playbook: how to value a stock by its sector

Here is the whole framework as a repeatable process you can run on any VN-Index name.

**1. Identify the sector and pick the lens first — before looking at any number.** Bank or broker? P/B, judged against ROE versus cost of equity. Developer? NAV/RNAV — land at market value minus net debt. Commodity cyclical (steel, chemicals)? EV/EBITDA on mid-cycle earnings, never trailing P/E. Stable grower (consumer, IT)? P/E, growth-adjusted. Utility? EV/EBITDA plus dividend yield. Picking the lens *before* the number is the single discipline that defeats both traps.

**2. Read the multiple, then immediately ask the lens-specific follow-up.** A multiple alone is never the answer; each lens has a mandatory second question:
- **P/B (bank):** what is the ROE, and is it above or below the cost of equity? Is the book value real (what is the NPL trend)?
- **NAV (developer):** is the land valued honestly and legally clear? Can the net debt be rolled?
- **EV/EBITDA (cyclical):** is this multiple on *mid-cycle* or *peak* earnings? (If peak, recompute on mid-cycle before deciding anything.)
- **P/E (grower):** is the growth real and durable enough to justify the multiple?

**3. Compare to the sector's own history, not to other sectors.** Pull the sector's five-year average multiple and see whether today's reading is a discount or a premium *to itself*. Banks at P/B 1.5 below their own 2.0 average are cheap-for-banks; that is a real signal. Banks at P/B 1.5 "versus IT at 4.5" is noise. The horizontal-bar figure above is exactly this comparison — each sector against its own past.

**4. Size the position to the lens's reliability.** A developer's NAV has wide error bars (land valuations and rollover risk are genuinely uncertain), so size smaller and demand a bigger discount — 30%+ to NAV, not 5%. A consumer staple's P/E is reliable, so a modest discount to its own history is enough to act on. Match your conviction — and your position size — to how trustworthy the lens is for that sector.

**5. Know the invalidation.** Every valuation thesis has a kill switch:
- The **bank** thesis dies if ROE deteriorates or NPLs spike (the book was fiction).
- The **developer** thesis dies if bonds can't be rolled or land turns out unsaleable (the NAV was a fantasy).
- The **cyclical** thesis dies if you discover your "cheap" multiple was on peak earnings (you bought the top).
- The **grower** thesis dies if growth slows and the high P/E has nothing left to justify it (the multiple compresses).

State the invalidation *before* you buy, in one sentence — "I own this bank because ROE 18% > CoE 13% justifies 1.4x; I am wrong if ROE drops below 13% or NPLs cross X%." If you cannot write that sentence, you do not understand the valuation, only the number.

The discipline is simple to state and hard to keep: **let the sector choose the lens, never let a screener's single number choose the stock.** A 10x P/E is a green light for a bank and a red flag for a steelmaker; a 0.8x P/B is a gift from a high-ROE lender and a curse from a low-ROE one; a 30% discount to NAV is a margin of safety if the land is real and a mirage if it is not. Get the lens right, ask the follow-up, compare to the sector's own history, and the same number that fools everyone else will tell you the truth.
