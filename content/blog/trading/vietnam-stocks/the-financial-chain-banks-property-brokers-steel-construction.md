> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — VN-Index's five heaviest cyclical sectors — banks, real estate, securities brokers, steel, and construction — are not five independent bets. They are one chain, welded together by credit, that rises together and falls together.
>
> - The chain runs in one direction: cheap **credit** lets **banks** lend, **developers** borrow to build and presell apartments, **brokers** extend margin so retail can buy the rally, and **steel + construction** supply the building boom — and the rising property collateral feeds back to let banks lend even more.
> - It runs in reverse just as hard. In 2022 a corporate-bond crisis broke the property link, and the whole chain collapsed together: real estate fell about 48%, securities about 58%, steel about 52%, while VN-Index dropped from 1,498 to 1,007 in a single year.
> - The links do not turn at the same time. On the way up, **credit and brokers turn first**; **construction and steel earnings lag** by two to three quarters because a building boom takes time to show up in tonnes shipped and contracts billed.
> - The one fact to remember: these five sectors do not diversify each other — they are the *same trade* wearing five different tickers, so when you own all five you own one big leveraged bet on the credit cycle, not a balanced book.

On October 5, 2022, a name most foreign investors had never heard of — Van Thinh Phat, a sprawling Ho Chi Minh City property group — became the epicenter of the worst Vietnamese market crash since the global financial crisis. Its chairwoman was arrested in a bond-fraud investigation, and within days the entire corporate-bond market for property developers froze solid. No developer could roll over its debt. What happened next is the single best lesson in how Vietnamese stocks actually work, and it is the reason this post exists.

The freeze did not stay inside real estate. Within weeks, bank stocks were falling — because banks had lent to those same developers and held their bonds, and the market suddenly feared a wave of bad loans. Brokerage stocks fell harder still — because a falling market triggered margin calls, forcing leveraged retail investors to dump shares, which crushed the brokers who had lent them the money. Steel collapsed — because if developers stop building, nobody buys rebar. Construction contractors cratered — because their entire order book is property and infrastructure projects that were now on hold. By the end of 2022, VN-Index had fallen from 1,498 points at the start of the year to 1,007, and the five sectors that led it down were not five separate stories. They were one story.

This is the chain. Banks, real estate, brokers, steel, and construction are the five heaviest cyclical sectors on VN-Index, and they are linked by a single common input — credit — into a causal chain that runs forward in a boom and backward in a bust. Most beginners treat them as a diversified basket: "I own a bank, a developer, a broker, and a steelmaker, so I'm spread across the market." This post will show you that you own nothing of the sort. You own the same trade five times. By the end you will be able to trace the chain link by link, understand the reflexive credit-collateral loop that makes it self-reinforcing in both directions, replay the 2022 contagion in numbers, know which link turns first, and — most usefully — read the chain to anticipate where the next rotation is heading.

## Foundations: why these five sectors are linked

Start with the thing that makes a chain a chain rather than a list: **a shared input that flows from one link to the next.** For these five sectors, that shared input is **credit** — borrowed money — and to see why, you have to look at what each sector actually does for a living and notice that the *output* of one is the *input* of the next.

### Credit is the common bloodstream

**Credit** simply means money that is lent rather than owned — a loan, a bond, a margin line, a bank deposit re-lent to a borrower. In an economy, credit is the bloodstream: it is the mechanism by which savings held by one person become spending or investment by another. When credit is cheap and plentiful (low interest rates, banks willing to lend, bond buyers willing to fund), money flows freely and asset prices tend to rise. When credit is expensive and scarce (high rates, banks defensive, bond market frozen), money stops flowing and asset prices fall. (The companion post on [Vietnam's monetary policy](/blog/trading/finance/vietnam-monetary-policy-state-bank-dong-credit-ceiling) explains how the State Bank of Vietnam actually controls the supply of credit through the credit ceiling; here we take the supply of credit as the input and trace where it goes.)

Now watch how credit moves through each of our five sectors:

- **Banks** are the source. A bank's entire business is converting cheap deposits into loans — it *manufactures* credit. When the State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) loosens, banks have more room to lend. (See [the banking deep dive](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/banking-sector-vietnam-the-heartbeat-of-vn-index) for how a bank actually makes money on the spread.)
- **Real estate** is the biggest single consumer of that credit. A developer borrows enormous sums — bank loans plus corporate bonds (*trai phieu doanh nghiep*) — to buy land, build apartments, and fund itself until it can *presell* units (collect cash from buyers before the building is finished). (See [the real estate deep dive](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/real-estate-sector-vietnam-land-banks-presales-bonds).)
- **Securities brokers** extend a second kind of credit — **margin** (*ky quy*), money lent to retail investors so they can buy more stock than their cash allows. When credit is loose and the market is rising, margin balances swell and brokers' lending profit and trading volumes explode. (See [the brokers deep dive](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/securities-brokers-sector-vietnam-highest-beta).)
- **Steel** supplies the physical raw material of the building boom that credit funds. Rebar and structural steel go into every apartment tower and bridge. When credit-funded construction rises, steel demand rises with it. (See [the steel deep dive](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/steel-basic-resources-sector-vietnam-the-cyclical).)
- **Construction** contractors do the building. Their order book *is* property development plus public infrastructure — both of which are credit-financed. (See [the construction materials deep dive](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/construction-materials-sector-vietnam-public-investment).)

So the same dong of credit, created by a bank, can flow into a developer, which spends it on steel and contractors, while a broker lends a parallel dong of margin to a retail investor buying the shares of all of them. **One input, five sectors.** That is why they move together — and it is why, when you trace them on a chart, they look less like five independent lines and more like five copies of the credit cycle, scaled by different beta.

### Why these five and not others

VN-Index has many sectors — consumer staples, utilities, technology, healthcare — but only these five form the credit chain, and there is a precise reason. Each of the five is **cyclical** (its earnings swing hard with the economy) *and* **credit-intensive** (it either supplies, consumes, or is geared to borrowed money). A consumer-staples company like a dairy producer sells the same milk in a boom and a bust; its earnings barely flex with credit, so it sits outside the chain (and is therefore a *defensive* sector you rotate *into* when the chain is breaking). The five chain sectors, by contrast, live and die by the availability of credit. That shared dependency is exactly what welds them into a chain — and what makes them rise and fall as one.

There is a second reason these five dominate: **weight.** Together, banks, real estate, brokers, steel, and construction make up well over half of VN-Index's market capitalization — banks alone are about a third. So the chain is not a sideshow; it *is* the index. When the chain runs up, VN-Index runs up. When the chain breaks, VN-Index breaks. You cannot understand the Vietnamese market without understanding this chain, because for long stretches the chain and the market are the same thing.

It helps to see what a chain *is not*. A list of sectors that happen to be cyclical — say, airlines, semiconductors, and luxury goods in some other market — might all rise in a boom and fall in a bust without being a chain, because nothing flows *between* them: an airline's revenue is not a semiconductor maker's input. What makes our five a true chain is the *handoff*. The credit a bank creates does not merely correlate with a developer's borrowing; it literally *becomes* that borrowing. The steel a developer buys does not merely correlate with the steelmaker's revenue; it *is* the steelmaker's revenue. Each link's output is physically the next link's input, so a shock anywhere propagates along the welds. Correlation is sectors moving in sympathy; a chain is sectors moving because they are mechanically connected. That distinction is the difference between a portfolio you can diversify and one you cannot.

This also tells you why the chain is uniquely Vietnamese in its intensity. In a deep, diversified market like the United States, financials, real estate, and materials are large but the index is balanced by enormous technology, healthcare, and consumer sectors that do not depend on the credit cycle in the same way. In Vietnam, the chain sectors are an outsized share of a still-developing market, the corporate-bond channel that funds property is young and fragile, and retail investors using margin make up a far larger share of daily turnover than in developed markets. Each of these features tightens the welds and raises the beta. The same chain exists everywhere; in Vietnam it is shorter, tighter, and more leveraged, which is why it moves the market so completely.

![Heatmap of annual returns for the five chain sectors showing they rise and fall together](/imgs/blogs/the-financial-chain-banks-property-brokers-steel-construction-2.png)

Look at that heatmap before reading on. Five rows — banks, real estate, securities, steel, construction — across four years, 2021 through 2024. Notice that the colors move *together*: a wall of green in 2021 (the boom), a wall of deep red in 2022 (the crash), a patchier recovery in 2023-24. The sectors do not zig when the others zag. They zig together. That single picture is the thesis of this entire post: these are not independent bets.

## The chain, link by link

Now let us walk the chain forward, the way it runs in a boom. Each link's *output* becomes the next link's *input*. We will go slowly, because the whole point is to see the mechanism, not just the correlation.

### Link 1 — Credit loosens, banks lend

The chain starts with monetary conditions. When inflation is under control and growth needs support, the SBV cuts policy rates and lifts the annual **credit ceiling** (*room tin dung*) — the quota that caps how fast each bank may grow its loan book. With cheaper funding and more room to grow, banks expand lending. **Net interest margin** (NIM — the spread between what a bank earns on loans and pays on deposits) typically *widens* early in a cutting cycle, because deposit costs fall faster than loan yields. So the banks get a double tailwind: a fatter margin and a bigger book. This is why banks are an early-cycle trade and why bank stocks usually turn up first — they sit at the head of the chain.

But banks lending is only the *source* of credit. The credit has to go somewhere, and in Vietnam the single largest destination is property.

### Link 2 — Developers borrow to build and presell

A property developer is the most credit-hungry business in the economy. To build an apartment tower, a developer needs land (bought years in advance, the **land bank**), construction financing, and working capital to survive the years between buying the land and collecting cash from buyers. It raises this money two ways: **bank loans** and **corporate bonds** (*trai phieu doanh nghiep*) — IOUs sold to banks, funds, and retail investors. With cheap credit available, developers borrow aggressively, buy more land, and launch more projects.

The genius (and the danger) of the model is **presales** (*ban hang hinh thanh trong tuong lai* — selling property formed in the future). A developer collects deposits and staged payments from buyers *before* the building is finished — often before it has broken ground. Those presale cash flows fund construction. So a developer is running a chain of its own: borrow to buy land, presell to fund building, use the building to justify more borrowing. When credit is loose and buyers are confident, this spins beautifully. When credit freezes or buyers vanish, every link of the developer's internal chain snaps at once — which is precisely what happened in 2022.

Notice how completely this link depends on the *availability* of credit, not just its price. A developer can survive a higher interest rate if it can still roll over its debt; what kills it is being unable to refinance at *any* rate, because its whole model assumes it can keep borrowing against rising land and incoming presales until the project is delivered and the loan repaid from final sales. Cut off the ability to refinance — freeze the bond market, restrict bank credit to real estate — and a developer that looked perfectly solvent on paper becomes insolvent in cash terms overnight, because the cash it was counting on (the next bond issue, the next bank draw, the next wave of presales) simply does not arrive. This is why real estate is the most *fragile* link, not merely the most credit-hungry: its business model has no slack. It is always one refinancing away from trouble, which is exactly why a credit shock breaks the property link first and hardest, and why everything downstream of property — steel, construction — feels the break a beat later.

### Link 3 — Brokers fund the margin rally

While developers are building, something parallel is happening in the stock market. As credit loosens and the economy heats up, retail investors pile in. Vietnamese brokers (*cong ty chung khoan*) make this far more powerful by extending **margin** — lending investors money to buy more shares than their cash allows, using the shares themselves as collateral. A retail investor with 100 million dong of cash might borrow another 100 million on margin and control 200 million of stock.

Margin is rocket fuel for a rally. It lets buying power expand far beyond the cash actually in the market, which pushes prices up faster, which makes more investors want to borrow and buy, which pushes prices up more. Brokers profit three ways at once: interest on the margin loans, commissions on the surging trading volume, and gains on their own proprietary trading books. This is why brokers are the **highest-beta** sector in the chain — they amplify whatever the market is doing. (The deep dive on [liquidity and the margin cycle](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/liquidity-and-the-margin-cycle-vietnam) traces this amplifier in full.) In a boom, brokers don't just participate; they pour gasoline on it.

### Link 4 — Steel and construction supply the boom

Everything the developers are building has to be made of something and built by someone. That is the steel and construction link. A building boom is, physically, a steel boom: rebar (reinforcing bar) and structural steel go into every floor of every tower, and into the bridges and highways that public investment funds alongside private property. So when credit-funded construction rises, **steel** demand and steel prices rise, and steelmakers' volumes and margins expand together — the classic operating leverage of a cyclical commodity producer.

**Construction contractors** are even more directly geared: their entire revenue *is* the building activity. A contractor's order book (its *backlog* of signed contracts) fills up when developers launch projects and the government accelerates public investment in roads, ports, and metro lines. More launches mean more contracts mean more revenue mean — eventually — more profit. "Eventually" is the key word, and we will return to it: steel and construction earnings *lag* the credit impulse that drives them, because it takes time for a loan approval to become a tonne of rebar shipped and a contract billed.

There is an important nuance in this last link: it has *two* sources of demand, private and public. Private demand is property development — apartment towers, commercial buildings — and it moves tightly with the credit chain we have been tracing. Public demand is government infrastructure — highways, ports, airports, metro lines — funded by the state budget rather than private credit, and it is *countercyclical by policy*: when private property freezes, the government often accelerates public investment precisely to put a floor under construction and steel demand. This is why steel and construction did not fall *quite* as far as property in 2022 and recovered partly in 2023-24 even while real estate stayed weak — public infrastructure spending picked up some of the slack from the frozen private side. So the last link of the chain has a partial shock absorber the others lack: when the private credit channel breaks, the public-investment channel can soften the blow. It is a real cushion, but a limited one — public investment is large enough to slow the decline, not to replace the enormous private property demand that drives the link in a boom.

### Link 5 — Collateral rises, and the chain feeds back to banks

Here is the link that turns a chain into a *loop*, and it is the most important idea in the post. As all this building and buying pushes property prices and stock prices *up*, the value of the **collateral** behind the whole system rises. And almost everything in Vietnamese finance is collateralized by property: bank loans are secured against land and buildings; corporate bonds are backed by project assets; even margin loans are ultimately a claim on shares whose value reflects the property-heavy index.

When collateral values rise, banks can lend *more* against the same assets. A developer whose land was worth 100 billion dong last year and 130 billion this year can borrow more against it. A homeowner whose apartment has appreciated can take a bigger mortgage. The rising collateral *expands the borrowing capacity of the entire system*, which feeds more credit back to the head of the chain — banks lend more, developers build more, and the cycle accelerates. This feedback loop, where rising prices justify more credit which pushes prices higher still, is what makes the chain self-reinforcing. It is also what makes it dangerous, because the same loop runs in reverse with terrifying speed.

#### Worked example: the up-chain multiplier

Watch a single dong of credit ripple through the chain. Suppose a bank, granted fresh room under the credit ceiling, extends **1 trillion dong** (about **\$39mn** at \$1 = 25,400 VND) of new lending to a developer.

The developer spends it building an apartment project. Of that 1tn, roughly 300 billion dong goes to **steel and building materials** (rebar, cement, structural steel), and roughly 400 billion dong goes to a **construction contractor** for labor and on-site work. The contractor pays wages; those workers spend in shops; the steelmaker books revenue and its own suppliers get paid. Meanwhile, the developer **presells** apartments in the project — say it collects 1.5tn dong of presale deposits from buyers, many of whom borrowed *their* deposits from the same banking system. Those buyers, feeling richer as the project's value rises, are more confident putting *another* 200 million dong each into the stock market on margin lent by a broker.

So the original 1tn of bank credit did not sit in one place. It became steel revenue, contractor revenue, presale cash flow, and — indirectly — margin-fueled stock buying. The same dong touched four of the five chain sectors. The intuition: credit in this system is not a one-time payment but a circulating current, and that circulation is exactly why the five sectors move as one — a single credit impulse lights up the whole chain at once.

## The reflexive loop: collateral expands and contracts credit

The feedback in Link 5 deserves its own section, because it is the engine of both the boom and the bust, and most beginners never see it. The economist George Soros called this dynamic **reflexivity**: the idea that prices don't just *reflect* fundamentals, they *change* the fundamentals, which then changes prices again, in a self-feeding loop. In the credit chain, reflexivity works through collateral.

![Flow diagram of the reflexive credit collateral loop running forward and in reverse](/imgs/blogs/the-financial-chain-banks-property-brokers-steel-construction-3.png)

### The loop running forward

Trace the ring in the figure. Cheap credit pushes **asset prices up** (property, stocks). Higher asset prices mean **higher collateral values**. Higher collateral means lenders will extend **more credit** against it (the same building now backs a bigger loan). More credit pushes **asset prices up** further. And around it goes. Each turn of the loop justifies the next: the rising price is not a bubble *yet*, the market tells itself, because the collateral backing all the loans is rising too, so the loans look safe. The loop *manufactures its own evidence of safety* — which is exactly why it can run far longer and further than fundamentals alone would allow.

This is why a credit-driven bull market in Vietnam feels so self-confirming on the way up. Banks report low bad-debt ratios (because rising collateral covers any wobbly loan), developers report rising land values (because credit keeps bidding land up), brokers report record margin balances (because rising stocks make margin look safe), and every data point seems to validate every other. The chain is humming, and the humming sounds like proof.

### The loop running in reverse

Now run the same loop backward, because it is perfectly symmetric — and far more violent. Credit *tightens* (rates rise, a bond market freezes, a quota is cut). Asset prices fall. **Falling collateral** means the loans backing it now look under-secured: the building that backed a 100 billion loan is now worth 80 billion. Lenders respond by **pulling credit** — refusing to roll over bonds, demanding more collateral, calling in margin. Less credit forces **more selling** to raise cash, which pushes asset prices **down** further, which shrinks collateral more, which pulls more credit. The loop that manufactured safety on the way up now manufactures danger on the way down.

The asymmetry is brutal: credit can be *withdrawn* far faster than it was extended. It takes years of confident lending to inflate the chain; it takes weeks of fear to deflate it. A lender who spent three years slowly building exposure can decide in a single afternoon to stop rolling over a developer's bonds. That asymmetry is why the chain rises like an escalator and falls like an elevator — and why the 2022 reverse cascade was so fast.

The deeper reason the reverse loop is so vicious is that the boom builds *hidden leverage* that only becomes visible on the way down. During the up-loop, every participant takes on debt that looks prudent *at the inflated collateral values* — the developer's 91 billion loan looked safe against 130 billion of land, the retail investor's margin looked safe against a rising portfolio, the bank's loan looked safe against appreciating apartments. None of these borrowers felt over-leveraged at the time, because the rising collateral kept their loan-to-value ratios looking healthy. But the leverage was real, and it was *correlated* — everyone was leveraged against the same property-and-stock collateral. When that collateral falls, every borrower's hidden leverage surfaces *simultaneously*. They all need to raise cash at once, by selling the same kinds of assets, into the same falling market. This synchronized deleveraging is what turns an orderly decline into a cascade. The market does not fall because the fundamentals changed by 30%; it falls because a system that quietly built correlated leverage during the boom must unwind it all at the same moment, and there is no buyer big enough to absorb everyone selling at once.

This is also why you cannot trust the calm of the late boom. The quieter and more confident the chain looks at the top — low bad-debt ratios, record margin balances, ever-rising land prices — the more hidden leverage has accumulated, and the more violent the eventual unwind. The 2022 crash was so severe *because* the 2020-21 boom had been so smooth and so confident; the smoothness was not safety, it was leverage being layered in unseen. A reader who internalizes only one thing from this section should internalize this: in a reflexive credit system, the appearance of safety at the top is itself the danger.

#### Worked example: the reflexive credit expansion (and its fragility)

A developer owns a plot of land carried on the bank's books as collateral worth **100 billion dong** (about **\$3.9mn**). Under typical Vietnamese lending, the bank will lend up to about **70%** of collateral value — a **70% loan-to-value (LTV)** — so the land supports a **70 billion dong** loan.

Now a credit-fueled boom pushes land prices up **30%**. The plot is now valued at **130 billion dong**. At the same 70% LTV, it can now support a **91 billion dong** loan — **21 billion dong** of *new* borrowing capacity (about **\$827k**) conjured purely from the price increase, no new land bought. The developer draws it down and uses it to buy more land or fund more building, which pushes prices up again. That is the loop expanding credit.

But watch the fragility. Suppose instead land prices *fall* 30%, from 130 back to **91 billion dong**. The existing 91 billion loan is now at **100% LTV** — fully against the wall — and if the bank wants its prudent 70% cushion back, the developer must repay about **27 billion dong** *immediately* or post more collateral it does not have. The same 30% move that created 21 billion of borrowing capacity on the way up destroys far more on the way down, because now the borrower is forced to sell into a falling market to raise the cash. The intuition: collateral-based credit is an amplifier in both directions, and the leverage that feels like free money in the boom becomes a margin call you cannot meet in the bust.

## The 2022 reverse cascade, in numbers

Theory becomes vivid when you watch it happen. 2022 is the textbook case of the chain running in reverse, and every Vietnamese investor should know it cold. Here is the sequence, link by link, the way the contagion actually spread.

![Timeline of the 2022 reverse cascade from bond crisis through property to banks brokers and steel](/imgs/blogs/the-financial-chain-banks-property-brokers-steel-construction-4.png)

### The trigger: the bond market freezes

Vietnam's corporate-bond market had ballooned during the 2020-21 boom — developers had funded themselves with a mountain of *trai phieu doanh nghiep*, much of it sold to retail investors who barely understood the risk. In 2022, regulators tightened the rules (Decree 65 raised standards for issuance), and then the arrests in the Van Thinh Phat case in October destroyed confidence overnight. The bond market for developers froze: no new issuance, and a wall of existing bonds coming due with no way to roll them over. By some estimates, hundreds of trillions of dong of property bonds were maturing into a market that would no longer fund them.

### Link by link, downward

**Property freezes.** With bond funding gone and bank credit to real estate restricted, developers could not finance construction or refinance maturing debt. Projects stalled. Presales collapsed as buyers, suddenly nervous and facing higher mortgage rates, walked away. The real-estate sector fell roughly **48%** on the year.

**Developers default.** Unable to roll over bonds, several developers missed payments. The fear was no longer abstract — it was actual default on actual paper held by actual banks and funds.

**Bank NPL fear.** Banks had lent to those developers and held their bonds. The market did not wait for confirmed losses; it priced the *fear* of a wave of non-performing loans (NPLs — bad loans). Bank stocks fell roughly **25%** even though reported bad-debt ratios were still low — because the market understood that the collateral behind the loans (property) was now worth far less, so book value could no longer be trusted.

**Margin calls.** As the index fell, leveraged retail investors hit their margin limits. Brokers issued **margin calls** — demands to add cash or sell. Investors who couldn't add cash were force-sold, dumping shares into an already-falling market, which pushed prices down more, which triggered *more* margin calls. The securities sector, the highest-beta link, fell roughly **58%** — the worst of all five.

**Steel and construction collapse.** With developers frozen and projects stalled, demand for rebar and contractors evaporated. Steel fell roughly **52%** and construction roughly **50%**. The last links in the chain felt the break that started in the bond market three sectors away.

The whole chain went down together, in sequence, in a single quarter. VN-Index fell from 1,498 at the start of 2022 to 1,007 by year-end — a 33% drop — and the five chain sectors fell far more than that, because they *were* the leveraged core of the index.

What makes the 2022 cascade such a clean teaching case is that the break point was a *funding channel*, not a fundamental collapse in the real economy. Vietnam's GDP grew over 8% in 2022 — one of the fastest growth rates in the world that year. People still needed apartments; the country still needed roads and bridges; banks were still profitable. None of the *real demand* underneath the chain had disappeared. What broke was purely *financial*: the corporate-bond market, the channel through which credit flowed to the property link, froze. And because the chain is welded by credit, a break in one funding channel propagated through the whole structure even though the underlying economy was booming. This is the single most counterintuitive lesson of 2022, and the most important: in a credit-driven market, the chain can collapse while the economy thrives, because the chain depends on the *flow of credit*, not on the health of the real economy. An investor watching GDP and concluding "the economy is fine, so stocks must be fine" missed the entire crash, because the crash lived in the plumbing of credit, not in the economy the plumbing was supposed to serve.

It is worth dwelling on *why the contagion respected no fundamentals*. A well-run steelmaker with a strong balance sheet and no exposure to the failing developers still fell roughly 52%, because the transmission mechanism — forced margin selling — does not discriminate. When a broker force-sells a leveraged investor's portfolio, it sells whatever is liquid, and the largest, most liquid names in a Vietnamese portfolio *are* the chain stocks: the banks, the steelmakers, the developers. So the highest-quality names in the chain get sold *because* they are liquid, not because anything is wrong with them. This is why, in a chain-wide cascade, picking "good companies" within the chain offers almost no protection — the selling is mechanical and indiscriminate, and the only real defense is to be *outside* the chain entirely. Quality protects you across a full cycle; it does not protect you during the forced-selling phase of a credit cascade.

#### Worked example: tracing the 2022 contagion in numbers

Make the cascade concrete with one developer. Suppose a mid-size developer has **5 trillion dong** of corporate bonds outstanding (about **\$197mn**), held partly by a bank and partly by retail investors. In 2022, the bond market freezes and the developer cannot refinance; it defaults on the 5tn.

Trace the damage outward. The **bank** holding, say, 2tn of those bonds plus 3tn of direct loans to the developer now faces 5tn of impaired exposure. Even if it expects to recover 60% from collateral (now-depressed land), it must provision against the rest: 5tn x 40% = **2tn dong** of provisions (about **\$79mn**) charged straight against profit — enough to wipe out a meaningful chunk of a year's earnings, which is why the bank's stock falls even before any loss is confirmed.

Meanwhile the developer's default and the broader property freeze crater the stock market. A retail investor who had borrowed **2 billion dong** on margin against a **4 billion dong** portfolio (50% margin) watches the portfolio fall 30% to **2.8 billion**. The broker's collateral cushion is gone; it issues a margin call. The investor cannot add cash, so the broker **force-sells** 1.5 billion of stock to bring the loan back within limits — selling that hits steel and construction names indiscriminately, dragging them down regardless of their own fundamentals. The intuition: a single 5tn default does not stay where it lands; it propagates through bank provisions and forced margin selling into every other link, which is the precise mechanism by which one property failure becomes a market-wide crash.

## The lead-lag order: which link turns first

The chain does not move all at once — the links turn in a sequence, and knowing that sequence is the single most useful thing in this post for actually trading the rotation. The order is set by *where each link sits relative to credit* and by *how long it takes for credit to show up in that sector's earnings.*

![Flow diagram of the lead lag order showing which links turn early and which lag](/imgs/blogs/the-financial-chain-banks-property-brokers-steel-construction-5.png)

### On the way up — what turns first

**Credit conditions turn first** — they are the input, so they lead everything. A rate cut, a lifted credit ceiling, a thawing bond market: these are the leading indicators of the whole chain.

**Brokers turn first among the stocks.** Because brokers are the highest-beta, most liquidity-sensitive link, their share prices react almost immediately to looser credit and rising volumes — often before the real economy shows any change at all. Brokers are a *financial* play; they don't need a single building to be built to profit, just more trading and more margin. So broker stocks are typically the first of the five to bottom and turn up on a policy pivot.

**Banks turn early too**, as NIM widens and loan growth resumes — they sit at the head of the chain and the market anticipates their improving earnings quickly.

**Real estate turns in the middle.** Developers benefit from cheaper funding and reviving buyer confidence, but it takes time for presales to recover and for the improvement to show in results.

**Steel and construction lag.** This is the crucial insight. Their *stocks* may move with sentiment, but their *earnings* lag the credit impulse by **two to three quarters**, because the chain from "loan approved" to "rebar shipped and contract billed" is physically slow. A developer must get funding, finalize the project, sign the contractor, and start building before the steelmaker ships a single tonne or the contractor bills a single milestone. So steel and construction are the *last* links to confirm a recovery in hard numbers — which means a recovery you can already see in broker volumes and bank loan growth has not yet appeared in steel earnings. That gap is the rotation opportunity.

### On the way down — the same order, reversed

In a downturn the sequence inverts but the logic is the same: credit tightens first, brokers and the most liquidity-sensitive names fall first and hardest (highest beta cuts both ways), property follows as funding dries up, and steel/construction earnings are the last to roll over because the projects already under way keep consuming steel and contractor-hours for a few quarters even as new launches stop. This is why, in a bust, steel earnings can look deceptively fine right up until the backlog runs dry — a value trap for anyone who buys "cheap" steel on trailing earnings.

There is a subtlety worth separating: the lead-lag order is about *earnings*, but stock *prices* often move all at once on sentiment. In a panic, every chain stock — broker, bank, developer, steelmaker — can fall together in a single week, because forced selling and fear do not wait for earnings to confirm anything. The lead-lag order reasserts itself in the *recovery*, when the market eventually stops trading on pure sentiment and starts pricing each link's actual improving fundamentals. So the practical way to use the order is not to expect steel's *price* to lag broadly in a crash (it won't — it crashes with everything), but to expect steel's *earnings* to lag in the recovery, which creates a window where the stock has bounced on sentiment but the earnings haven't yet confirmed — and that window is where the patient, fundamentals-driven buyer gets a better entry than the sentiment-chaser. Read the order as an *earnings* clock, not a price clock, and it becomes a tool for timing entries rather than a description of the crash.

#### Worked example: lead-lag positioning around a rate cut

Suppose the SBV cuts policy rates and signals an easier credit stance in, say, Q1. How do you position across the chain through the year?

**Q1 (the pivot):** brokers turn first. A broker like SSI or VND, whose earnings are geared directly to trading volume and margin balances, sees volumes pick up within weeks. You buy brokers early — they're the fastest to price the easing. Banks too: NIM is widening as deposit costs fall faster than loan yields.

**Q2-Q3 (the build):** real estate gains traction as developers refinance more cheaply and presales revive. You rotate some capital from brokers (which have already run hard) into developers.

**Q3-Q4 (the lag pays off):** now the building that the early-year credit funded finally shows up as **tonnes of steel shipped and contracts billed**. Steel and construction earnings — flat or falling in Q1 when you'd have bought them "early" and waited — accelerate two to three quarters after the credit impulse. So the patient steel buyer who waited for the *earnings* to confirm, rather than buying on the Q1 sentiment, catches the move just as it becomes real.

The intuition: because the links turn in a known order, you don't buy the whole chain at once — you rotate *along* it, front-running each link by buying it just before its earnings inflect, using the faster links (brokers, banks) as your leading signal for the slower ones (steel, construction). A move you can already see in broker volumes in Q1 tells you steel earnings will inflect by Q4.

![Line chart of system credit growth versus VN-Index year end level showing the common driver](/imgs/blogs/the-financial-chain-banks-property-brokers-steel-construction-6.png)

That dual-axis chart shows the common driver behind the whole chain: system credit growth (left axis) and VN-Index's year-end level (right axis). Credit growth stayed high right through 2021-22, but notice that the *index* still collapsed in 2022 — because what broke was not the *quantity* of credit but its *allocation*: the bond-market channel to property froze even as headline bank credit kept growing. The lesson is that the chain depends not just on how much credit exists but on whether it can *flow to the links that need it* — and in 2022 the property link was cut off, which broke everything downstream of it.

## Common misconceptions

The chain overturns several beliefs that beginners hold with great confidence. Each one is a way to lose money.

### Misconception 1 — "These sectors diversify each other"

The most expensive mistake. A beginner builds a portfolio of a bank, a developer, a broker, and a steelmaker and feels diversified — four different industries, surely spread across the market. But as the 2022 heatmap showed, these five sectors are driven by *one* input (credit) and rise and fall *together*. Owning all five is not diversification; it is **leverage on a single factor**. When the chain breaks, all five break at once, and a portfolio that felt balanced behaves like one big bet on the credit cycle. Real diversification means owning something *outside* the chain — a defensive consumer staple, a utility, a healthcare name — whose earnings don't depend on credit. In 2022, the only places to hide were sectors that were *not* links in the chain.

### Misconception 2 — "Banks are safe when property falls"

Many investors think of banks as the conservative, blue-chip end of the market — surely safer than a speculative developer. But banks sit at *both ends* of the chain: they supply the credit *and* they hold the collateral. When property falls, the value of the collateral behind the banks' loans falls with it, and the market immediately doubts the banks' stated book value. In 2022, bank stocks fell about **25%** — not as much as property's 48%, but a far cry from "safe." A bank is not insulated from a property crash; it is *exposed to it twice*, through its loan book and through its bond holdings. The only thing that makes a bank safer than a developer in a crash is its thicker capital cushion and more diversified loan book — not any immunity to the chain.

### Misconception 3 — "The chain only runs one way"

Beginners who learn the boom mechanics — credit up, everything up — often assume the bust is just a gentle reversal, a slow leak. It is not. The reflexive loop is *symmetric in logic but asymmetric in speed*: credit inflates slowly and deflates violently, because lenders can withdraw funding in an afternoon but extend it only over years. The 2022 crash compressed into a single quarter losses that the 2021 boom took a year to build. Anyone who treated the early 2022 weakness as a buying dip — "it always comes back" — got run over by a cascade that accelerated as it went. The chain runs *both* ways, and the reverse is faster.

### Misconception 4 — "Cheap steel after a crash is a bargain"

After the chain breaks, steel and construction stocks look statistically cheap — low price-to-earnings on *trailing* earnings, because last year's earnings were still inflated by the projects already under way. But those trailing earnings are about to fall off a cliff, because the backlog is draining and no new projects are starting. Buying "cheap" steel on trailing earnings right after a credit bust is the classic **value trap**: the multiple is low because the E in P/E is about to collapse. The right time to buy steel is not when it looks cheapest (right after the break) but when the credit impulse has *already turned up* and you can see the leading links (brokers, banks) recovering — because steel earnings lag, the cheapness during the bust is a warning, not a gift.

### Misconception 5 — "Brokers are just a play on the stock market"

Investors treat brokers as a pure market bet, divorced from the real economy of property and steel. But brokers are tied into the same credit chain through *margin*: the margin balances that fund the rally are credit, and that credit is collateralized by shares of the very companies in the chain. When property freezes and the index falls, margin calls force-sell *all* shares — including steel and construction names with nothing wrong with them — which is how the broker link transmits stress to the rest of the chain mechanically, not just emotionally. Brokers are the chain's amplifier and its fastest transmission line, not a sideshow.

## How it shows up on VN-Index

Step back and watch the chain play out across three regimes on the actual index. This is the chain as a market historian would tell it.

### The 2021 up-chain

Through 2020-21, the SBV cut rates hard to support the economy through COVID, and credit flooded the system. The chain lit up in order. Brokers exploded first — the securities sector returned roughly **110%** in 2021 as retail accounts opened in record numbers and margin balances hit all-time highs. Banks ran about **28%**. Real estate ran about **28%** as developers launched project after project. Steel surged roughly **75%** (Hoa Phat, the dominant steelmaker, rode record construction demand and steel prices). Construction gained about **40%**. VN-Index climbed from 1,103 at the end of 2020 to 1,498 at the end of 2021 — and every link of the chain was green. This was the loop running forward at full speed: cheap credit, rising collateral, more credit, higher prices, all five sectors confirming each other.

### The 2022 down-chain

Then the loop reversed, as we traced above. The bond freeze in October broke the property link, and the cascade ran through the whole chain in a quarter: real estate **-48%**, securities **-58%**, steel **-52%**, construction **-50%**, banks **-25%**. VN-Index fell from 1,498 to 1,007. The same five sectors that had led the index up led it down — harder, because they were the leveraged core. Anyone who thought they were diversified across "four different industries" discovered they had owned one trade four times.

### The 2023-24 repair

In 2023 the SBV pivoted back to easing, cutting rates four times to support the economy, and the government rolled out measures to unfreeze the bond market and revive property. The chain began to repair — but in its characteristic *order*. Securities recovered first and hardest (**+75%** in 2023) as liquidity returned and margin rebuilt. Banks recovered steadily (**+12%** in 2023, **+28%** in 2024). But real estate stayed weak (**+5%** in 2023, roughly **-3%** in 2024) — the property link, where the break originated, was the slowest and most damaged to heal, because the bond overhang and stalled projects took years to clear. Steel and construction recovered modestly (**+28%** and **+15%** in 2023) as public investment in infrastructure picked up some of the slack from frozen private property. The 2023-24 repair shows the chain healing from the *financial* links (brokers, banks) outward to the *real* links (property, steel) — the reverse of how it broke, and a reminder that the link where the break starts is the last to mend.

#### Worked example: sizing the chain's swing on the index

Put a number on why the chain dominates VN-Index. Suppose the five chain sectors together are about **55%** of the index by weight, and a defensive remainder (staples, utilities, tech, healthcare) is the other **45%**.

In 2022, the chain sectors fell, on a weighted basis, on the order of **-40%** (averaging banks' -25% with the -48% to -58% of the others, weighted toward the heavy bank and property components). The defensive remainder fell far less — call it **-12%**. The index outcome is roughly (0.55 x -40%) + (0.45 x -12%) = -22% - 5.4% = about **-27%** — close to the actual -33% once you account for the chain's even heavier effective weight in a panic. Either way, the chain did the overwhelming majority of the damage: of the index's fall, the great bulk came from the five chain sectors, not the defensive 45%. The intuition: because the chain is both the heaviest *and* the highest-beta part of the index, it doesn't just participate in VN-Index's big moves — it *causes* them, which is why reading the chain is reading the index.

## The playbook: how to trade the chain

This is the payoff. You understand the chain; here is how to use it to anticipate rotation and avoid getting caught when it breaks.

### Read the head of the chain to anticipate the rest

The chain's input is credit, and credit's leading indicators are public: SBV policy-rate decisions, the annual credit-ceiling announcements and mid-year quota top-ups, system credit-growth prints, and the state of the corporate-bond market (issuance volumes, whether developers can refinance). **Watch these first.** When the SBV pivots to easing and the bond market thaws, the chain is about to run up — and you have a roadmap for the order. When credit tightens or the bond market freezes, the chain is about to break, and the highest-beta links (brokers, property) will go first.

### Position along the chain, not across it

Because the links turn in a known sequence, you don't buy the whole chain at once. On a confirmed easing pivot:

1. **Buy the financial links first** — brokers (highest beta, turns first) and banks (head of the chain, NIM widening). These price the easing within weeks.
2. **Rotate into property** in the next quarter or two, as developers refinance cheaply and presales revive — but only if the bond market is genuinely open again, because property cannot recover while its funding channel is frozen.
3. **Buy steel and construction last**, when the credit impulse you saw in Q1 has had two to three quarters to turn into actual shipped tonnes and billed contracts. Use the leading links as your timer: if broker volumes and bank loan growth turned up two quarters ago, steel earnings are about to inflect.

You are front-running each link by using the faster links as a leading signal for the slower ones. The broker is your early-warning system for the steelmaker.

### Size the chain as one bet, not five

The most important risk rule: because the five sectors are one factor, **count them as one position, not five.** If you own a bank, a developer, a broker, and a steelmaker, your *true* exposure to the credit cycle is the sum of all four, not a diversified quarter each. Size accordingly — if you want, say, 40% of your book in "the chain," that 40% is split among the five, not 40% in each. And if you want genuine diversification, you must own something *outside* the chain: a defensive staple, a utility, a healthcare name, whose earnings don't move with credit. In a crash, those are the only places to hide.

### The invalidation — when to get out of the whole chain at once

The chain's single most important warning sign is the **bond market freezing** or a sharp **credit tightening**. If developers suddenly can't refinance, if the SBV cuts quotas hard, if margin balances are at record highs *and* the index stalls — the reflexive loop is about to reverse, and when it does, all five links break together and fast. The invalidation is not stock-specific; it is chain-wide. The moment you see the funding channel to the property link closing, you reduce *the entire chain*, because the reverse cascade does not respect the fundamentals of any single name — margin calls force-sell good steel companies alongside bad developers. Watch the bond market and margin balances the way a sailor watches the barometer: when the pressure drops, you reef the whole sail, not one corner.

One more practical note on the invalidation: it has a leading edge and a confirming edge, and you act on the leading edge. The leading edge is the *funding* signal — bond issuance drying up, refinancings failing, the SBV signaling restraint, margin balances stretched while the index stalls. The confirming edge is the *price* signal — the highest-beta links (brokers, property) starting to roll over while the broad index still looks fine. By the time the confirming edge appears, the cascade is already beginning and liquidity is thinning, so reducing then means selling into weakness. The whole value of understanding the chain is that it lets you act on the *leading* edge: you can see the funding channel closing before the prices break, because you know that credit is the input and prices are the output, and the input always moves first. That single beat of warning — getting out on the funding signal rather than the price signal — is the difference between exiting the chain at the top and exiting it halfway down.

The deeper lesson is the one the 2022 crash taught every Vietnamese investor the hard way: in a credit-driven market, your real risk is not any single sector — it is the chain, and the chain is only as strong as its weakest funding channel. Learn to see the five sectors as one, and you will never again mistake a leveraged bet on credit for a diversified portfolio.
