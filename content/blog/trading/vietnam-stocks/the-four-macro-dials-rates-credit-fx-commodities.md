> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Four macro variables explain most of the sector rotation you see on VN-Index: the SBV policy rate, credit growth and the credit ceiling, the USD/VND exchange rate, and the prices of a few key commodities. Learn to read these four dials and you can usually tell which sectors should be leading before the charts confirm it.
>
> - **Rates** are the discount rate for every stock. When the State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) cuts, banks, brokers and property re-rate first; when it hikes, those same high-beta sectors fall hardest.
> - **Credit growth and the credit ceiling** (*room tin dung*) are the lifeblood of banks and property. The ceiling literally caps how much the banking system — and therefore the property developers that borrow from it — can grow each year.
> - **USD/VND** splits the market: a weaker dong helps exporters (textile, seafood, electronics) who sell in dollars and hurts importers (steel, plastics, pharma) who buy inputs in dollars — and it ties the SBV's hands when it would rather cut.
> - **Commodities** (oil, steel, chemicals) set the revenue line for price-taking producers and the cost line for everyone who uses them. The one event to remember: in **late 2022** all four dials turned against equities at once — rates hiked to 6.0%, the dong toward ~24,800, the bond market frozen, commodities rolling over — and VN-Index fell from ~1,500 to ~874.

In October 2022, almost everything on the Ho Chi Minh exchange (HOSE) was falling at the same time. That is unusual. In a normal market some sectors zig while others zag — banks up while exporters are down, property weak while energy is strong. That is the whole premise of *sector rotation*, the idea that leadership passes from one group of industries to the next, and it is what makes a diversified Vietnamese portfolio safer than a single bet. But in October 2022 the rotation stopped working. Banks, brokers, property, steel, consumer names — they fell together, day after day, until the VN-Index had given back almost a third of its value in a few weeks.

What broke? Not one thing. Four things, all at once. The SBV had just hiked its policy rate from a record-low 4.0% back to 6.0% in a matter of weeks, draining the cheap money that had inflated the boom. The dong was sliding toward 24,800 against the dollar as the US Federal Reserve hiked aggressively and capital fled emerging markets. A corporate-bond scandal had frozen the credit that property developers lived on. And commodity prices, after a 2022 spike, were rolling over, taking the steel and chemical producers' earnings with them. Each of these is one of what I call the **four macro dials**. Most of the time they move one at a time, and you can trade the rotation they cause. Once in a while they all turn the same way at once, and you get a *perfect storm*.

This post is about those four dials. We will build each one from zero — what it is, what number to watch, why a Vietnamese company's earnings move when the dial moves — and then put them together to see how they interact. By the end you should be able to look at four free data prints and form a view on which sectors of VN-Index should be leading, and, just as importantly, what would prove you wrong.

## Foundations: what a "macro dial" is and why sectors feel different ones

Let me start with the word *macro*. **Macroeconomics** is the study of the whole economy at once — the total amount of money, the average level of prices, the exchange rate, the overall pace of growth — as opposed to *micro*, the study of one company or one market. A **macro dial**, in the sense I mean it here, is a single macroeconomic variable that (a) you can watch as a published number, and (b) reliably moves the earnings or the valuation of whole groups of stocks in the same direction. It is a "dial" because the central bank, the global market, or the government can turn it, and when they do, the sectors wired to that dial respond.

Here is the everyday version. Picture a sound mixing board with a handful of sliders. Each slider controls a different group of instruments — one for the drums, one for the bass, one for the vocals. A sound engineer who knows the board can shape the whole song by moving four or five sliders, even though there are forty instruments playing. The VN-Index has hundreds of stocks, but you do not need to track hundreds of variables. Four sliders — rates, credit, FX, commodities — control most of what you hear. The rest is detail.

Why do *sectors* respond to these dials, rather than individual stocks responding randomly? Because companies in the same industry share the same wiring. Every bank in Vietnam makes money the same way: it borrows from depositors at one rate and lends to borrowers at a higher rate, so every bank's profit moves with interest rates and with how much it is allowed to lend. Every steel maker buys iron ore priced in dollars and sells steel at a global reference price, so every steel maker's margin moves with the dong and with the steel price. The shared wiring is *why* a sector moves together, and it is also why each sector is sensitive to a *different* dial. A bank lives and dies on rates and credit; it barely notices the oil price. An airline lives and dies on the oil price and the dong; it does not care much about the credit ceiling. Sort every sector by which dials it is wired to, and the rotation map almost writes itself.

### The discount-rate idea, in one paragraph

There is one concept underneath all four dials, and it is worth slowing down on because it explains *why* a macro print moves a stock price even when the company itself has not changed. A stock is worth the present value of the company's future profits — all the money it will ever pay you, added up, but with money far in the future counted as worth less than money today. The rate at which you "count down" future money is the **discount rate**. When the discount rate rises, the same stream of future profits is worth less today, so the price falls; when it falls, the price rises. The single most important driver of the discount rate is the interest rate set by the central bank. That is why rates are the first dial: a rate change re-prices *every* stock at once, before any company reports a single number. The other three dials work mostly through *earnings* — they change how much a sector will actually make — but rates work through the discount rate, which is faster and broader.

### Some Vietnamese-market vocabulary you will need

Because this is a Vietnam-specific guide, a few local terms will recur. Let me define them once here so the later sections read cleanly.

- **SBV** — the State Bank of Vietnam, the central bank. It sets the policy rate and, unusually, it also sets the credit ceiling.
- **Room tin dung / credit ceiling** — the maximum percentage by which each commercial bank is allowed to grow its loan book in a given year, handed out by the SBV. This is a quantity control on lending that most developed markets do not use, and it is one of the most powerful dials in Vietnam.
- **NIM (net interest margin)** — the gap between the rate a bank earns on loans and the rate it pays on deposits, as a percentage of its loans. It is the core of a bank's profitability.
- **Foreign room / room ngoai** — the maximum percentage of a company that foreign investors are collectively allowed to own. When foreigners want to buy and the room is full, the stock can trade at a premium; when they sell, the flow can dominate a thin market.
- **USD/VND** — the exchange rate, quoted as how many dong it takes to buy one US dollar. A *higher* number means a *weaker* dong. As of mid-2026 the rate is around 26,000, so for the worked examples in this post I will use a round anchor of \$1 = 25,400 VND where I convert dong figures to dollars.
- **ASP (average selling price)** — the average price a producer gets for its output. For a price-taking commodity producer, the ASP is set by the global market, not by the company.

With the vocabulary in place, let me take the four dials one at a time, in the order in which they tend to matter for the broad market: rates, then credit, then FX, then commodities.

## Dial 1 — Rates: the discount rate for every stock

The first dial is the **SBV policy rate**. In Vietnam the headline policy rate is the *refinancing rate* — the rate at which the SBV lends to commercial banks against collateral — with a *discount rate* (a slightly lower rate for the highest-quality collateral) moving alongside it. When commentators say "the SBV cut rates," they usually mean this cluster of official rates came down together. These rates set the floor under everything else: the rate banks pay each other overnight, the rate they pay depositors, and ultimately the rate at which a Vietnamese company can borrow.

Here is the chart of where that dial has been set over the past several years.

![SBV refinancing rate year-end 2018 to 2025 as a step line](/imgs/blogs/the-four-macro-dials-rates-credit-fx-commodities-2.png)

Notice the shape: the rate does not drift, it *steps*. The SBV holds it flat for long stretches and then moves it in deliberate jumps — down to a record-low 4.0% through the 2020-2021 COVID easing, then sharply back up to 6.0% in the 2022 rate shock, then back to 4.5% where it has sat since 2023. Each of those steps is a regime change for the market, not a footnote.

### How a rate cut lifts banks, brokers and property

Walk through *why* lower rates lift the rate-sensitive sectors, because the mechanism is different for each.

**Banks** benefit through two channels. First, when the SBV cuts, deposit rates fall fast — a bank can reprice the rate it pays savers within months — while the rate it earns on existing loans falls more slowly, because many loans are fixed for a period. That widens the NIM for a while, and a wider NIM is pure profit. Second, lower rates mean fewer borrowers default, so the bank sets aside less money for bad loans, which also flows to profit. Both channels push bank earnings up when rates come down.

**Brokers** (securities companies) benefit even more violently. A broker makes money two ways: commissions on trading, and interest on *margin* — money it lends to investors to buy more stock than their cash allows. When rates fall, deposit returns become unattractive, a flood of retail investors opens brokerage accounts, trading volume explodes, and margin balances swell. The broker earns more commission *and* more margin interest at the same time, on top of the gains on its own stock holdings. This is why brokers are the highest-beta bet on falling rates: in the 2020-2021 easing, the leading securities names rose several-fold.

**Property** benefits through the buyer's wallet. Most Vietnamese apartments are bought with a mortgage, and a developer's sales — its *presales*, the units sold before the building is finished — depend on whether buyers can afford the monthly payment. When the mortgage rate falls, the same buyer can afford a bigger loan, demand for apartments rises, and the developer can launch projects and book revenue. Property is also heavily indebted itself, so its own interest bill falls when rates fall. Lower rates help property on both the demand side and the cost side.

#### Worked example: a rate cut re-rates a bank stock

Take a bank trading at 12,000 dong a share. Suppose the market values it at a price-to-earnings ratio (P/E — price divided by annual earnings per share) of 8, which implies earnings of 1,500 dong per share. Now the SBV cuts its policy rate by 1.5 percentage points. Two things happen. First, the lower discount rate alone makes investors willing to pay a higher multiple for the same earnings — say the P/E drifts from 8 to 9.5 as the whole market re-rates on cheaper money. Second, the wider NIM and lower provisions lift next year's earnings per share from 1,500 to 1,700 dong. Multiply the two effects: the new fair price is 9.5 times 1,700, which is 16,150 dong, up from 12,000. That is a 35% move in the stock from a single dial turning, and the company has not done anything differently — it is the same bank, re-rated by cheaper money and a wider margin. At \$1 = 25,400 VND, the stock went from about \$0.47 to \$0.64 a share. The intuition: a rate cut hits a bank twice — once through the discount rate, once through the margin — which is why financials lead rate-driven rallies.

### Why the rate dial hits some sectors harder than others

Not every sector feels a rate move equally, and the reason is worth a paragraph because it explains why the *same* cut produces a 35% move in a broker and barely a wiggle in a utility. Go back to the discount-rate idea. The present value of a company's profits is more sensitive to the discount rate the *further out* those profits sit. A water utility earns roughly the same steady cash this year, next year, and in twenty years; a large share of its value is near-term cash that is barely discounted, so a change in the discount rate moves its value only a little. A property developer or a high-growth broker, by contrast, has most of its value in profits that are years away — the apartments not yet built, the trading volumes not yet earned — and money that far out is heavily discounted, so a change in the discount rate swings its value a lot. In the language of bonds, the rate-sensitive sectors have a long *duration*; the defensives have a short one. This is the deep reason the rate dial rotates leadership: a cut lifts the long-duration sectors (property, brokers, anything priced on future growth) far more than the short-duration defensives (utilities, staples), and a hike crushes them far more. When you hear that "high-growth stocks are rate-sensitive," this duration mechanic is exactly what is meant.

There is a Vietnam-specific amplifier on top of the duration effect: **liquidity flow**. In a market where retail investors are the dominant force and most household savings sit in bank deposits, a rate cut does not just lower the discount rate — it physically moves money. When the 12-month deposit rate falls from 8% to 5%, leaving cash in the bank suddenly feels like a waste, and a wave of savers opens brokerage accounts and pours money into stocks. That wall of liquidity bids up the most-talked-about, highest-beta sectors first, regardless of any careful valuation. So in Vietnam the rate dial works through *three* channels at once — the discount rate, the bank's own margin, and the flow of household savings into the market — which is why Vietnamese rate-driven rallies are so much more violent than the textbook discount-rate effect alone would predict. For the mechanics of how that liquidity and the margin cycle feed each other, see [liquidity and the margin cycle on VN-Index](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/liquidity-and-the-margin-cycle-vietnam).

### What to watch and how it shows up

The print to watch for this dial is the SBV's own announcements of the refinancing and discount rates, plus the cheaper daily proxy of **interbank rates** (the rate banks lend each other overnight, reported by the SBV and the press) and **12-month deposit rates** at the big banks. When deposit rates at the large state banks fall below roughly 5%, you are in cheap-money territory and the rate-sensitive sectors have a tailwind. When they climb above roughly 7-8%, money is being pulled out of stocks and back into the bank, and the high-beta sectors are fighting a headwind. The 2020-2021 episode — policy rate at 4.0%, deposit rates near 4-5%, brokers and property leading — and the 2022 reversal — policy rate back to 6.0%, deposit rates spiking toward 10% at some banks, those same sectors collapsing — are the two cleanest examples in recent memory.

## Dial 2 — Credit and the ceiling: the lifeblood of banks and property

The second dial is **credit** — the total amount of money the banking system lends into the economy — and the SBV's unusual tool for controlling it, the **credit ceiling**. This dial is more important in Vietnam than in almost any developed market, because in Vietnam the central bank does not just influence lending through the price of money (the rate); it controls the *quantity* of lending directly.

Here is how the ceiling works. At the start of each year the SBV announces a target for total system credit growth — typically somewhere around 14-16% — and then hands each commercial bank an individual *room*, a cap on how much that specific bank may grow its loan book that year. A healthy, well-capitalized bank might get 14%; a weaker one might get 8%; a strong one might be granted an extra few percentage points mid-year if it has used up its initial room. No bank may exceed its room, full stop. This is a hard quantity control, and it makes the credit ceiling one of the most powerful and most distinctly Vietnamese dials on this list.

![Vietnam system credit growth bar chart 2018 to 2025](/imgs/blogs/the-four-macro-dials-rates-credit-fx-commodities-4.png)

The chart shows system credit growth landing in a fairly narrow band most years — low-to-mid teens — with a dip in 2020 as COVID froze demand and a notable acceleration to 18.0% in 2025 as the SBV pushed credit hard to support growth. That band is not an accident of the market; it is largely *set* by the ceiling. The SBV decides roughly how fast the economy's debt may grow, and the banks fill that quota.

### Why credit is the lifeblood of two sectors

**Banks** are the most obvious. A bank's revenue is, to first order, its loan book times its NIM. If the SBV grants the system 14% credit growth, the banking sector's interest income can grow roughly 14% — no more, because the loans simply cannot be made. The ceiling therefore puts a hard cap on how fast bank earnings can grow in a given year. When the SBV is generous with room, bank earnings have a tailwind; when it tightens the ceiling to fight inflation or defend the dong, bank earnings growth is throttled regardless of how much demand for loans exists.

**Property** is the second, and the link is tighter than most beginners realize. Property developers are among the largest borrowers in the system, and their *buyers* borrow too. When the ceiling is loose and rates are low, mortgage credit flows, apartments sell, and developers can borrow to fund the next project — a virtuous cycle. When the SBV tightens the ceiling, *both* ends seize up at once: developers cannot roll their own debt and buyers cannot get mortgages. This is exactly what happened in 2022, when the bond-market freeze and a tighter stance left property developers unable to refinance — several came within weeks of default. Credit is not just *a* driver of property; it is the artery.

#### Worked example: the ceiling caps a bank's loan growth

Take a bank with a loan book of 400 trillion dong. The SBV grants it 14% room for the year. The most it can add is 14% of 400 trillion, which is 56 trillion dong of new loans. At \$1 = 25,400 VND, that 56 trillion dong is about \$2.2bn of fresh lending — and not one dong more, no matter how many creditworthy borrowers line up at the door. If the bank's NIM is 3.5%, that incremental loan book can add at most 56 trillion times 3.5%, which is about 2.0 trillion dong of extra annual interest income (around \$77mn). That is the ceiling acting as a hard cap on the sector's earnings growth: the SBV has, in effect, set the upper bound on what every bank can earn this year before the year has even started. The intuition: in Vietnam you can forecast a bank's revenue ceiling from a single number the central bank publishes in January.

### What to watch and how it shows up

For this dial, watch three prints. First, the SBV's **annual system credit-growth target**, announced around the new year — that is the size of the quota. Second, the **mid-year credit-growth figure** the SBV and GSO (General Statistics Office) report: if the system is running well below target by mid-year, the SBV will usually grant extra room in the second half, a tailwind for banks and property; if it is running hot, expect tightening. Third, **individual bank room grants** reported in the financial press, which tell you *which* banks have headroom to grow. A bank that has used up its room is done growing for the year, however strong demand is; a bank just granted extra room has a fresh runway. When you hear "the SBV has loosened the credit ceiling," read it as a green light for banks and property; when you hear "the SBV is rationing room to defend the dong," read it as the opposite — and notice that this sentence ties Dial 2 directly to Dial 3.

For the deeper mechanics of the credit ceiling and how the SBV uses it as a policy tool, see the companion post on [Vietnam's monetary policy, the State Bank, the dong and the credit ceiling](/blog/trading/finance/vietnam-monetary-policy-state-bank-dong-credit-ceiling).

## Dial 3 — FX: the USD/VND rate that splits the market in two

The third dial is the **exchange rate**, USD/VND — how many dong it takes to buy one US dollar. This dial is different from the first two in an important way: rates and credit move the *whole* market in one direction, but FX *splits* the market, helping one set of sectors at the exact moment it hurts another. To read this dial you have to know which side of the dollar each sector sits on.

![USD/VND exchange rate annual average 2018 to 2025 line chart](/imgs/blogs/the-four-macro-dials-rates-credit-fx-commodities-3.png)

The chart tells a clear story. For years the dong was remarkably stable — barely 23,000 to the dollar from 2018 through 2021, a deliberate policy of a managed, slowly-crawling exchange rate. Then the dam started to give: a jump in 2022 (with an intraday spike toward 24,800 during the global rate shock), a step up in 2023, a sharp move to 24,900 in 2024, and on to 26,007 by 2025. That is a roughly 13% depreciation of the dong over a few years — small by emerging-market standards, but large for a currency the SBV works hard to keep stable, and enough to matter a great deal for the sectors wired to it.

### Why a weak dong helps exporters and hurts importers

Start with the simplest case. A **textile exporter** sews shirts in Vietnam, pays its workers and rent in dong, and sells the shirts to American buyers in dollars. When the dong weakens, each dollar of sales converts into *more* dong, while the dong costs are unchanged. The exporter's revenue and margin, measured in dong, go up purely because of the exchange rate. Seafood exporters, electronics assemblers and other dollar-earning, dong-spending businesses are in the same happy position. A weak dong is a tailwind for exporters.

Now flip it. A **steel maker** that buys iron ore and coking coal abroad pays for those inputs in dollars, while it sells much of its steel domestically in dong. When the dong weakens, each dollar of imported input costs *more* dong, squeezing the margin. The same is true for plastics makers buying imported resin, pharmaceutical firms importing active ingredients, and airlines paying for jet fuel and aircraft leases in dollars. A weak dong is a headwind for importers and dollar-cost businesses.

There is a third, subtler effect that hits the whole market: **foreign flows**. Foreign investors who hold Vietnamese stocks measure their returns in dollars. If the dong is expected to weaken, their dong-denominated gains shrink when converted home, so a weakening dong gives foreigners a reason to sell — and foreign selling, in a market where foreigners are a meaningful share of large-cap volume, weighs on the index and especially on the big, liquid names that foreigners own. This is why a sliding dong often coincides with weeks of foreign net selling. For how those flows work and why they can dominate a thin market, see [foreign flows, ETFs and the index effect on VN-Index](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/foreign-flows-etfs-and-the-index-effect-vietnam).

### Why a weak dong ties the SBV's hands

Here is the connection that makes FX so important for the *other* dials. When the dong weakens sharply, the SBV faces a dilemma. To defend the dong it wants to *raise* rates (higher dong rates make holding dong more attractive) and to *tighten* credit (less dong sloshing around to chase dollars). But raising rates and tightening credit are exactly what *hurts* banks, brokers and property — Dials 1 and 2 turning the wrong way for equities. So a weak dong does not just hurt importers directly; it *forces* the SBV toward a stance that hurts the rate-sensitive sectors too. In 2022 this is precisely what happened: the dong came under pressure as the Fed hiked, and the SBV, with its back to the wall, hiked the policy rate and rationed credit room to defend the currency. The FX dial pulled the rate and credit dials with it. That is the single most important interaction on this whole list, and we will return to it.

### Why the dong moves the way it does: the managed crawl

To read the FX dial you need to know that the dong is not a freely floating currency like the dollar or the euro. The SBV runs a **managed float**: each morning it publishes a *central rate*, and the commercial-bank rate is allowed to move only within a band of a few percent on either side. For years the SBV's strategy was a slow, deliberate *crawl* — letting the dong weaken a percent or two a year, predictably, to keep Vietnamese exports competitive without spooking anyone. That is why the chart looks so flat from 2018 to 2021 and then breaks: a managed crawl produces a calm line until the pressure exceeds what the band can absorb, at which point the SBV either widens the band, moves the central rate, or burns reserves defending the old one.

This matters for trading because it tells you *what kind* of dong move you are watching. A slow crawl — the dong drifting weaker by a percent or two a year — is the normal, healthy state: a gentle tailwind for exporters and a non-event for the broad market, because it is too gradual to force the SBV's hand on rates. A *disorderly* move — the dong slamming against the top of the band, the SBV visibly selling reserves, the central rate jumping — is the dangerous state, because it is exactly what drags the rate and credit dials against equities. The same direction (a weaker dong) is benign at one speed and a market threat at another, and the band and the reserve trend are how you tell which one you are in.

The foreign-flow channel deserves one more turn, because it is where FX bites the index hardest. Foreign investors who buy Vietnamese stocks are running an implicit bet on two things at once: the stock going up *and* the dong holding. If the dong is expected to weaken 5%, a foreign investor needs the stock to rise 5% just to break even in dollars. So when the dong starts sliding, the math turns against every foreign holder simultaneously, and the rational response — sell before the dong falls further — produces concentrated foreign net selling in exactly the large, liquid names foreigners own (the big banks, the index heavyweights). Those names then underperform not because their businesses changed but because the FX dial turned a marginal holder into a forced seller. This is why a sliding dong shows up first in the foreign-net-sold figures and in the relative weakness of foreign-favorite large caps, often before it shows up anywhere else.

#### Worked example: a 5% dong move, two opposite sectors

Suppose the dong weakens 5%, from 23,400 to 24,600 to the dollar.

Take a **steel importer** that buys \$100mn of iron ore a year. Before the move, that ore costs 100mn times 23,400, which is 2,340 trillion dong — no, let me keep the units clean: \$100mn at 23,400 is 2.34 trillion dong. After the 5% move, the same \$100mn of ore costs 100mn times 24,600, which is 2.46 trillion dong. The input bill rose by 0.12 trillion dong — about 120 billion dong, or roughly \$4.9mn at the new rate — with no change in the amount of ore bought. If the importer cannot pass that cost on (and a price-taker often cannot), it comes straight out of profit.

Now take a **textile exporter** that sells \$100mn of shirts a year, with its costs all in dong. Before the move, that revenue converts to 2.34 trillion dong; after, to 2.46 trillion dong. The exporter's dong revenue rose by 120 billion dong (about \$4.9mn) with no extra work — pure FX windfall, most of which drops to the bottom line because costs did not move. The intuition: the same 5% dong move that costs the importer \$4.9mn of margin hands the exporter \$4.9mn of margin, which is why FX is the dial that rotates leadership *within* the market rather than lifting or sinking all of it.

### What to watch and how it shows up

Watch the **USD/VND rate** itself (quoted daily, both the official SBV central rate and the freer commercial-bank rate), the **SBV's foreign-exchange reserves** (when the SBV is selling dollars to defend the dong, reserves fall — a sign of stress), and the **US dollar index (DXY)** and Fed policy, since the dong's pressure usually comes from a strong dollar abroad rather than anything domestic. When the dong is stable or strengthening, FX is a non-event and you can ignore this dial; when it is sliding through prior records — as it did in 2022 and again in 2024-2025 — tilt away from importers and foreign-heavy large caps and toward exporters, and brace for the SBV to lean hawkish on Dials 1 and 2.

## Dial 4 — Commodities: the price-takers' revenue and everyone's cost

The fourth dial is **commodity prices** — chiefly crude oil, steel (and its inputs, iron ore and coking coal), and industrial chemicals. This dial works differently from the other three. Rates and credit are domestic, set by the SBV; FX is partly managed. Commodity prices are set on global markets that Vietnam cannot influence at all. For the Vietnamese companies wired to them, this means they are **price-takers**: the world sets the price, and the company simply lives with it.

![Brent crude and China HRC steel prices dual line chart 2019 to 2025](/imgs/blogs/the-four-macro-dials-rates-credit-fx-commodities-5.png)

The chart shows two of the most important commodity dials for Vietnam — Brent crude oil and Chinese hot-rolled-coil (HRC) steel, the regional reference price that sets Vietnamese steel — over recent years. Both tell the same broad story: a COVID collapse in 2020, an enormous spike in 2022 (Brent near \$99 a barrel, steel near \$720 a tonne) as the world reopened and war disrupted supply, and a roll-over since. That shared 2022 spike is the commodity leg of the perfect storm, and the roll-over since is why steel and energy earnings have been under pressure.

### Two ways the dial works: revenue for producers, cost for users

The trick to reading this dial is to ask, for each company, *is this commodity its revenue or its cost?*

For a **producer** — an oil and gas company pumping crude, or a steel mill selling steel — the commodity price *is its revenue line*. When the price rises, every barrel or tonne it sells fetches more, and because much of its cost base is fixed (the rig, the blast furnace), the extra revenue drops almost entirely to profit. This is *operating leverage*, and it makes commodity producers' earnings swing far more than the commodity price itself. A 25% rise in the oil price might double an oil producer's profit. The same leverage works in reverse on the way down, which is why these are violent, cyclical stocks.

For a **user** — an airline burning jet fuel, a plastics maker buying resin, a fertilizer blender buying gas — the same commodity is its *cost line*. When the price rises, its costs rise, its margin shrinks, and unless it can pass the cost on (which competitive, price-taking users usually cannot), profit falls. The oil price that is a windfall for the producer is a tax on the user. So the commodity dial, like the FX dial, can split the market — though more often it lifts a cluster of producers (energy, materials) while taxing a cluster of users (transport, consumer, chemicals).

There is also a second-order effect: commodities feed **inflation**. When oil and food prices spike, Vietnam's CPI rises, which can force the SBV to hike rates to keep inflation in check — turning Dial 1 against equities. So a commodity spike is rarely just a sector story; it can become a whole-market story by routing through inflation into rates.

#### Worked example: an oil producer's revenue uplift per barrel

Take an oil and gas producer selling crude at the Brent price. Suppose Brent rises from \$80 to \$99 a barrel — exactly the 2022 move. That is \$19 of extra revenue on *every* barrel produced, and the producer's lifting cost (what it pays to get a barrel out of the ground) did not change — say it is \$45 a barrel. So the producer's profit per barrel went from \$80 minus \$45, which is \$35, to \$99 minus \$45, which is \$54. That is a 54% jump in profit per barrel from a 24% rise in the oil price — operating leverage in one line. If the producer lifts 100,000 barrels a day, the extra \$19 a barrel is \$1.9mn of additional revenue *per day*, nearly all of it profit. At \$1 = 25,400 VND that daily uplift is about 48 billion dong. The intuition: a commodity producer is a *leveraged* bet on its commodity, so a modest move in the price is a large move in the stock.

### The commodity-to-inflation-to-rate chain

The most important thing a commodity spike can do is not move a producer's stock — it is to turn Dial 1. Vietnam imports most of its oil products and a great deal of its raw materials, and energy and food are heavy weights in the consumer price index (CPI), the measure of inflation the SBV watches. When oil and food prices spike on world markets, they flow through into Vietnamese fuel and food prices within weeks, CPI rises, and if it rises far enough the SBV is forced to hike rates to keep inflation anchored — turning the rate dial against the *entire* market. So a commodity spike has two faces: a sector story (good for producers, bad for users) and, if it is large and broad enough, a whole-market story routed through inflation into rates. The investor who watches only the sector face misses the bigger one.

#### Worked example: an oil spike feeds into inflation and the rate dial

Suppose Brent jumps from \$80 to \$110 a barrel and stays there — a 38% rise. Domestic petrol prices, which track world oil with a short lag, rise sharply. Energy is roughly a tenth of Vietnam's CPI basket, so a large fuel-price rise on its own can add the better part of a percentage point to headline inflation, before you count the knock-on effect on transport and food. If CPI was running near 3.5% and this pushes it toward 4.5% or above — near the SBV's comfort ceiling — the SBV's calculus shifts from "hold or cut" to "hold or hike". Now trace the consequence: the oil producers you might have bought on the spike are up, but the rate-sensitive banks, brokers and property across the rest of your book are now facing a *hike* — Dial 1 turning red because Dial 4 turned up. The net effect on a diversified portfolio can be negative even though the headline event was "oil went up." The intuition: a big enough commodity move stops being a sector trade and becomes a rate trade, because it pulls inflation and the SBV along with it.

### What to watch and how it shows up

Watch the **Brent crude price** (for oil and gas, and as an inflation input), the **Chinese HRC steel price and iron-ore price** (for the steel sector, since China sets the regional benchmark), and **global fertilizer, urea and gas prices** (for the chemicals and fertilizer names). When a commodity is trending up, tilt toward its producers and away from its heavy users; when it rolls over — as steel and oil did after 2022 — the producers' earnings deflate and you want to be out of them. And keep half an eye on the inflation channel: a broad commodity spike, especially in oil and food, is an early warning that the SBV may have to hike, which would turn Dial 1 against the whole market.

## How the four dials interact: the 2022 perfect storm

So far I have treated the four dials one at a time, which is how you should learn them. But the reason 2022 is the case study every Vietnamese investor remembers is that the dials are *not* independent — they are linked, and once in a while they all turn the same way at once.

![The 2022 perfect storm with rate hikes, weak dong, bond crisis and falling commodities each hitting a sector](/imgs/blogs/the-four-macro-dials-rates-credit-fx-commodities-7.png)

Trace the chain. In 2022 the US Federal Reserve hiked rates at the fastest pace in forty years. That made the dollar strong and the dong weak — **Dial 3 turned**. A weak dong forced the SBV to defend the currency the only way it could, by hiking the policy rate from 4.0% to 6.0% and rationing credit room — **Dials 1 and 2 turned**, dragged by Dial 3. Meanwhile a corporate-bond scandal — issuers caught misusing bond proceeds — froze the *trai phieu doanh nghiep* (corporate bond) market that property developers relied on to refinance, an extreme tightening of credit on top of the official one. And commodity prices, after their mid-2022 spike, rolled over — **Dial 4 turned** — taking the steel and energy earnings that had been the bull market's bright spot.

All four dials, against equities, at the same time, each pulling the next. The result was that the usual rotation broke down: there was no sector to rotate *into*, because every dial was pushing nearly every sector down at once. Banks and brokers fell on the rate hike and the foreign selling; property collapsed on the credit freeze and the rate hike; steel fell on the commodity roll-over and the weaker dong's import-cost hit. The VN-Index went from near 1,500 in January 2022 to an intraday trough near 874 in November — a fall of about 42% in eleven months. That is what a perfect storm looks like: not one dial, but four, and the linkages between them turning a sector problem into a market problem.

The lesson is not that you should fear the dials, but that you should watch *all four together*. Most of the time they disagree — one is a tailwind while another is a headwind — and the disagreement is the rotation you trade. The rare, dangerous moments are when they *agree*. When you see the dong sliding, the Fed hiking, the SBV forced to follow, and commodities rolling over all at once, you are not looking at a rotation; you are looking at a storm, and the right response is to cut risk across the board, not to hunt for the next leader.

## Common misconceptions

A few beliefs about these dials are widespread and wrong, and each one costs Vietnamese investors money.

**"Lower rates are always good for stocks."** Not quite. Lower rates lift the *rate-sensitive* sectors — banks, brokers, property — but the SBV usually cuts rates *because* growth is weak, and weak growth hurts the earnings of cyclical and consumer sectors. The discount-rate tailwind and the earnings headwind can offset. The cleanest gains come when rates are falling *and* growth is recovering, not when rates are falling *because* the economy is in trouble. A rate cut in the middle of a recession is a different animal from a rate cut at the start of a recovery.

**"A weak dong is bad for the stock market."** It is bad for *importers* and for *foreign flows*, but it is good for *exporters*, who are a large and growing part of Vietnam's economy. The dong weakened materially from 2022 to 2025 — from roughly 23,400 to 26,007 — and over much of that stretch the export-heavy and the broad market did fine, because a gentle, managed depreciation is a tailwind for the export engine. The danger is not a weak dong; it is a *fast, disorderly* dong move that forces the SBV to hike. Direction matters less than speed.

**"The credit ceiling is just a technicality."** Far from it. In a developed market, central banks influence lending only through the price of money. In Vietnam the SBV controls the *quantity* directly, which means a single administrative decision — granting or withholding room — can throttle the earnings of the two largest sectors on the index, banks and property, regardless of how strong loan demand is. Ignoring the ceiling is ignoring the most Vietnamese feature of the whole market.

**"Commodity producers are a safe way to play a strong economy."** They are the *opposite* of safe. Operating leverage means their earnings swing far more than the commodity price, in both directions, so a steel or oil producer is one of the most volatile things on the index. They can double in a commodity upcycle and halve in the down-leg. They are a *leveraged*, *cyclical* bet, not a steady holding — useful as a tactical tilt when a commodity is trending, dangerous as a buy-and-forget.

**"Only the SBV's headline rate matters."** The headline policy rate is the slow signal; the faster, more tradeable signals are the *deposit rates* at the big banks and the *interbank rate*, which move ahead of and around the policy rate and tell you in real time whether money is getting cheaper or dearer. By the time the SBV announces a policy-rate change, the deposit and interbank rates have often already told the story.

## How it shows up on VN-Index

Let me ground all of this in three concrete episodes, because the dials are abstract until you have seen them turn real prices.

**2020-2021: three dials aligned for the bulls.** Rates fell to a record-low 4.0% (Dial 1, tailwind). Credit was loosened to support the COVID recovery, and system credit grew in the mid-teens (Dial 2, tailwind). The dong was rock-stable near 23,200 (Dial 3, neutral-to-friendly, no pressure on the SBV). Commodities recovered off their 2020 lows but had not yet spiked into a problem (Dial 4, neutral). Three of four dials were tailwinds and none was a serious headwind, and the result was one of the great VN-Index bull runs: the index more than doubled from its March 2020 trough near 660 to near 1,500 by January 2022, with brokers and property — the most rate- and credit-sensitive sectors — leading by a wide margin. This is the picture of dials in alignment to the upside.

**Late 2022: the perfect storm.** As traced above, all four dials turned against equities at once, the linkages amplified each other, and the index fell from ~1,500 to ~874. The instructive detail is *which* sectors fell hardest: precisely the ones that had led on the way up. Brokers, which had risen several-fold on falling rates and a margin boom, fell the most when rates reversed and margin was force-unwound. Property, which had ridden cheap credit, collapsed when credit froze. The high-beta leaders of the rate-and-credit boom were the high-beta losers of the rate-and-credit bust — the same wiring, run in reverse.

**2024-2025: the dials disagree.** This is the more normal, more instructive regime for learning to trade rotation. The dong slid hard, from roughly 23,900 to 26,007 (Dial 3, headwind for importers and foreign flows, but a tailwind for exporters). The SBV held the policy rate at 4.5% — not cutting, because the weak dong tied its hands, but not hiking either (Dial 1, neutral). Credit was pushed hard, accelerating to 18.0% in 2025 as the SBV prioritized growth (Dial 2, strong tailwind for banks and property). Commodities were soft and range-bound (Dial 4, headwind for producers). Four dials, four different signals — and the right read was a *rotation*, not a market call: tilt toward exporters and credit-driven banks, away from importers and commodity producers. When the dials disagree, you trade the disagreement; when they agree, you trade the market.

## The playbook: how to read the four dials and set the tilt

Here is how to turn the four dials into an actual portfolio decision, with the signals, the position, and the invalidation.

**Step 1 — Read the four dials, in order.** Once a month (or whenever a major print lands) write down the state of each dial as a simple plus, minus, or neutral for equities.

- **Rates** — is the SBV cutting, holding, or hiking? Proxy: 12-month deposit rates at the big state banks. Below ~5% is a plus; above ~7-8% is a minus.
- **Credit** — is the SBV loosening or tightening the ceiling? Proxy: the annual system credit-growth target and the mid-year run-rate. Running above target with extra room granted is a plus; rationed to defend the dong is a minus.
- **FX** — is the dong stable, gently weakening, or sliding fast? Proxy: USD/VND versus its recent record. Stable or gentle is neutral; a fast break to new records is a minus for the broad market and a plus for exporters specifically.
- **Commodities** — are oil and steel trending up, down, or flat? Proxy: Brent and Chinese HRC. Trending up is a plus for producers and a minus for users; rolling over is the reverse.

**Step 2 — Set the sector tilt from the reading.** The tilt follows mechanically from which dials are tailwinds.

- **Rates and credit both green** (cutting, loose ceiling): overweight banks, brokers and property — the rate-and-credit complex. This is the highest-conviction tilt because two dials reinforce each other.
- **Dong weak, SBV forced to hold or hike**: overweight exporters (textile, seafood, electronics), underweight importers (steel, plastics, pharma) and foreign-heavy large caps. Trade the split, not the level.
- **A commodity trending up**: a tactical overweight in its producers (oil and gas on a Brent uptrend, steel on a steel uptrend), funded by underweighting its heavy users — but keep it tactical, because operating leverage cuts both ways.
- **All four dials green**: lean into the high-beta, rate-and-credit leaders (brokers, property) and run more risk — this is the 2020-2021 setup.
- **All four dials red**: cut risk across the board and raise cash. This is the perfect-storm setup, and there is no clever rotation that escapes it — the only winning move is less exposure. Defensive names (utilities, staples) fall least, but "falls least" is not "rises".

#### Worked example: sizing a rate-and-credit tilt

Suppose you run a 1 billion dong portfolio (about \$39,000 at \$1 = 25,400 VND) and the dials read: rates green (SBV cutting), credit green (ceiling loosened), FX neutral, commodities flat. Two dials are firmly green and pointing at the same sectors. You decide to tilt 35% of the book — 350 million dong, roughly \$13,800 — into the rate-and-credit complex, split across two large banks and one broker, leaving the rest in your core holdings. You are not betting the farm on a macro call; you are over-weighting the sectors the two green dials favor, sized so that if you are wrong the damage is bounded. The intuition: the dials tell you *which* sectors to tilt toward, and position sizing — not the macro view — is what keeps a wrong call survivable.

**Step 3 — Write the invalidation before you put the trade on.** Every dial-based tilt needs a line that, if crossed, says you were wrong. The discipline is to write it *before* you trade, when you are calm.

- For a **rate-and-credit overweight**: the invalidation is the SBV signaling a *hike* (or deposit rates climbing back above ~7%), because that flips Dials 1 and 2 from tailwind to headwind. If that happens, the high-beta financials you bought become the first casualties — cut the tilt.
- For an **exporter overweight on a weak dong**: the invalidation is the dong *stabilizing or reversing*, which removes the FX tailwind. The trade was the depreciation, not the company; if the depreciation stops, so does the edge.
- For a **commodity-producer tilt**: the invalidation is the commodity *rolling over*. Operating leverage means the stock will fall faster than the commodity, so this invalidation has to be tight — a clear break in the Brent or steel trend is your exit, no negotiation.
- For the whole framework: the invalidation of *any* single-dial view is the other three dials lining up against it. One green dial is a tilt; four green dials is a market call; one green dial fighting three red ones is a trap. Always read all four before you size anything.

The deepest point in the whole playbook is this: you are not trying to forecast the dials, which is hard and often impossible. You are trying to *read* them — to look at four published numbers, state whether each is a tailwind or a headwind today, and tilt the portfolio toward the sectors the tailwinds favor, with an invalidation ready for when a dial turns. Reading is far easier than forecasting, and it is enough. The investor who simply knows, each month, the state of these four dials and which sectors each one favors will be early to most rotations and out of the way of most storms — which, over a full cycle, is most of the game.
