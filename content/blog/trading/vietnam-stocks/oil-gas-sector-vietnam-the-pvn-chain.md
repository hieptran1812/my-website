> **TL;DR** — Vietnam's oil and gas sector is one state family, **PetroVietnam (PVN)**, arranged along a value chain, and the price of crude oil moves each link *differently* — so "buying oil stocks" because oil is going up is a beginner's mistake.
>
> - **Upstream services** (PVD drilling, PVS offshore construction) *win* when crude is high, because high oil triggers more drilling activity, higher rig day-rates, and higher utilization — their earnings are a bet on *activity*, not on the oil price itself.
> - **Midstream gas** (GAS) is **take-or-pay** and volume-driven — buyers commit to take a contracted volume at a formula price, so GAS's revenue is far steadier than crude, more a utility than a commodity play.
> - **Downstream** (BSR refinery, PLX/OIL fuel retail) lives and dies on the **crack spread** (the margin between refined products and crude) and on **inventory** — and a *fast* crude spike with sticky pump prices can actually *hurt* them. Petrolimex earns roughly **3.2 trillion dong** of pretax profit on **213 trillion dong** of revenue — a razor-thin **~1.5%** margin where one inventory loss wipes a quarter.
> - The number to remember: in 9 months of 2024, profit growth split *by link* — upstream **PVD +34%**, refining **BSR +16.6%**, retail **PLX +11.5%** — same sector, three different engines. Match the sub-sector to your oil *view*, and watch **Block B-O Mon** as the upstream catalyst.

In the spring of 2022, crude oil did something it had not done in a decade: Brent, the global benchmark, vaulted past **\$120 a barrel** as Russia's invasion of Ukraine tore a hole in global energy supply. On the Ho Chi Minh exchange (HOSE), retail investors did the obvious thing. Oil is up, they reasoned, so buy oil stocks — and they bought the whole "dầu khí" (oil and gas) basket indiscriminately: the drillers, the gas pipeline operator, the refinery, the petrol retailer. They were half right and half catastrophically wrong, and the half they got wrong taught the single most important lesson in this sector.

Because the stocks did *not* all go up together. **PVD** — PV Drilling, the offshore rig operator — eventually soared, because a world starved of oil scrambles to drill more of it, and more drilling means more demand for PVD's rigs at higher day-rates. That is a clean, if delayed, win. But **BSR**, the company that runs Vietnam's flagship Dung Quat refinery, had a far rockier time of it: a refinery *buys* crude as its raw material, and when crude spikes faster than the company can pass the cost through to the pump, its margin gets pincered and the expensive inventory sitting in its tanks can take a paper loss. And **Petrolimex (PLX)**, the giant that owns half the country's petrol stations, found that selling fuel at *regulated* prices while its purchase cost ran away from it is a recipe for a thin, sometimes negative, quarter — the government literally sets a price ceiling, and a fast-rising crude cost squeezes the retailer against it.

So the same crude spike that was rocket fuel for one PVN company was a headwind for another. That is the entire thesis of this post, and it is why the sector confuses people: **oil and gas is not one trade, it is a value chain, and crude oil flows through each link differently.** This post will build that chain from zero — what each link does, how the crude price hits it, the all-important **crack spread** that governs refining, and the **take-or-pay** economics that make gas behave like a utility. We will meet the listed PVN family one ticker at a time (PVD, PVS, GAS, BSR, PLX, OIL), look at the **Block B-O Mon** mega-project that is the sector's biggest upstream catalyst, kill the three misconceptions that cost retail investors money, walk through how the sector actually behaved on VN-Index across the 2022 spike and the 2024-25 record-profit run, and end — as this series always does — with a concrete playbook for matching the sub-sector to your oil view, and the signal that would prove you wrong.

![Flow chart of the PetroVietnam value chain from crude oil price through upstream drillers PVD and PVS into midstream gas GAS then downstream refinery BSR and fuel retailers PLX and OIL, with high oil helping upstream and hurting downstream](/imgs/blogs/oil-gas-sector-vietnam-the-pvn-chain-1.png)

A word on money first, because we will use both currencies. Vietnam trades in dong (VND), and the sector's headline numbers — revenue, profit, market caps — are quoted in dong. As a reference point for readers who think in dollars, the mid-2026 exchange rate is roughly **\$1 = 25,400 VND**. So 3.2 trillion dong of profit is about **\$126mn**; a company worth 100 trillion dong is about a **\$3.9bn** market cap; a foreign net buy of 500 billion dong is about **\$20mn**. But oil and gas is special: its single most important *input*, the price of crude, trades globally in **dollars per barrel** (\$/bbl), and the refining margin is quoted in \$/bbl too. So unlike most of this series, the natural unit for the *driver* of this sector is the dollar, even though the listed companies report in dong. We will keep company figures in dong with a dollar anchor, and keep crude and margins in \$/bbl, which is how the world quotes them.

## Foundations: the oil value chain, and how crude hits each link

Forget the stock market for a moment and follow a molecule of oil from the ground to your motorbike's fuel tank. That journey is the **value chain**, and the oil industry the world over is organized into three stages along it: **upstream**, **midstream**, and **downstream**. Every oil and gas company on earth — and every listed PVN company in Vietnam — sits in one of these stages, and *which* stage it sits in determines almost everything about how it behaves when the crude price moves. Get the stages straight and the whole sector snaps into focus.

**Upstream** is the *finding and extracting* business: exploring for oil and gas, drilling the wells, and pumping the hydrocarbons out of the ground (or, in Vietnam's case, mostly out from under the seabed of the continental shelf). This is the riskiest, most capital-hungry, most technically demanding part of the chain — you spend a fortune drilling holes, many of which find nothing. In Vietnam, the actual *ownership* of the oil and gas in the ground belongs to the state through PetroVietnam and its production arm, but the listed companies in the upstream space are mostly **oilfield services** firms — they do not own the oil, they sell *services and equipment* to whoever is drilling for it. The two big listed names are **PVD** (PV Drilling, which owns and operates offshore drilling rigs and rents them out by the day) and **PVS** (PetroVietnam Technical Services, which builds and installs offshore platforms, lays subsea pipelines, and runs support vessels). Think of them as the *picks-and-shovels* sellers of a gold rush: they do not strike oil themselves, they get paid every time someone *looks* for it.

**Midstream** is the *transporting, storing, and processing* business: moving the oil and gas from the wellhead to where it is used. In many countries this means long-haul pipelines and storage terminals. In Vietnam, the dominant listed midstream company is **GAS** (PV Gas), which gathers natural gas from the offshore fields, pipes it ashore, processes it, and sells it — overwhelmingly to **power plants** (gas-fired electricity) and to **industrial customers** (fertilizer plants, factories). GAS is the single largest and most valuable company in the whole listed oil-and-gas space, often a top-five weight on the entire HOSE. And crucially, as we will see, GAS does *not* behave like a commodity bet at all — it behaves like a regulated utility, because of how its contracts are written.

**Downstream** is the *refining and selling* business: taking crude oil and turning it into the finished fuels people actually buy — petrol (gasoline), diesel, jet fuel, fuel oil — and then distributing and retailing those fuels. This stage splits into two listed pieces in Vietnam. First, **refining**: **BSR** (Binh Son Refining) operates the Dung Quat refinery, which takes in crude and distills it into products. Second, **retail and distribution**: **PLX** (Petrolimex), the dominant fuel retailer with roughly half the country's petrol stations, and **OIL** (PV Oil), the number-two retailer. The refiner *makes* the fuel; the retailers *sell* it at the pump. And here is the twist that catches everyone out: for the downstream companies, **crude oil is a cost, not a product.** They buy it (refiners) or buy the products made from it (retailers). So a high crude price is a *higher cost of goods*, and whether that is good or bad depends entirely on whether they can pass it through — which, as we will see, they often cannot, fast enough.

Now lay the crude price across the chain and watch it hit each link differently. This is the heart of the sector, so go slowly.

For the **upstream services** companies (PVD, PVS), a *high and rising* crude price is good — but not directly, and not instantly. PVD does not sell oil; it rents out drilling rigs. What it cares about is *drilling activity*: how many rigs are working, at what day-rate, at what utilization. And drilling activity is driven by the oil price *with a lag*. When crude is high, exploration and production companies (the people who actually own and pump oil) decide it is worth spending money to drill more wells, so they hire more rigs, and they hire them at higher prices because rigs are suddenly scarce. So PVD's revenue rises because *activity* rises and *day-rates* rise — both downstream consequences of a high oil price, but the *transmission* runs through activity, not through PVD selling a barrel of oil. This is why upstream services is a *delayed*, *amplified* bet on oil: delayed because drilling plans take quarters to ramp, amplified because both the number of rigs working *and* the price per rig move together (we will quantify this operating leverage shortly).

For **midstream gas** (GAS), the crude price matters mainly through *contract formulas* — Vietnamese gas sale prices are often linked to a basket of oil prices (commonly fuel oil) — but the *volume* is locked in by **take-or-pay** contracts, which we will define in a moment. The short version: GAS's volumes barely move with the oil price, and while its *prices* have some oil linkage, the dominant feature is stability. GAS is the *least* oil-sensitive link in the chain by design.

For the **downstream refiner** (BSR), the relationship is subtle and is the most misunderstood thing in the sector. A refinery does not really care about the *level* of crude — it cares about the **crack spread**, the difference between the value of the refined products it sells and the cost of the crude it buys. A refinery can be wildly profitable with crude at \$60 *or* at \$100, as long as the spread is wide. What hurts a refiner is (a) a *thin* crack spread, and (b) a *fast spike* in crude, which inflicts an **inventory loss** — the refinery is holding crude and products it bought at yesterday's lower price, and if the math turns, that inventory is suddenly worth a different amount on the books. A *fast* crude move is the refiner's enemy regardless of direction, but a fast *spike* combined with a regulated, sticky downstream price is the particular squeeze that hit BSR in 2022.

For the **downstream retailer** (PLX, OIL), crude is the cost of the fuel they sell, and their selling price is *regulated* — the government sets a base retail price for petrol and diesel, adjusted on a schedule. So a retailer's margin is essentially a *fixed fee per liter* that the regulator builds into the formula, multiplied by *volume*, plus or minus *inventory effects*. When crude rises fast, the retailer is buying expensive fuel and selling at a price that lags, squeezing the margin; when crude is stable, the retailer earns its steady regulated cut on a big volume. PLX is therefore far more a *volume and logistics* business — a fuel-distribution utility — than an oil-price play. This surprises people, and it is the single biggest misconception in the sector, so we will give it a whole section.

#### Worked example: the crack spread, barrel by barrel

Make the refiner's economics concrete. A refinery buys a barrel of crude at, say, **\$80 per barrel**. It distills that barrel into a slate of products — petrol, diesel, jet fuel, fuel oil — and sells the lot. Suppose the products that come out of one barrel are worth, in total, **\$90 per barrel** at market prices. The **crack spread** is simply the value of the products minus the cost of the crude: \$90 − \$80 = **\$10 per barrel**. That \$10 is the refiner's gross refining margin per barrel, before its own operating costs. Now multiply by volume: Dung Quat processes on the order of **6.5 million tons of crude a year**, roughly **48 million barrels**. At a \$10 crack spread, that is \$10 × 48,000,000 = **\$480mn** of gross refining margin a year, about **12.2 trillion dong** before operating costs, depreciation, and tax. The intuition: a refiner is a machine that mints the *crack spread*, one barrel at a time — and the level of crude barely matters, because both sides of the spread move with it; what matters is the *gap*.

Now break it the way 2022 broke BSR. Crude spikes from \$80 to \$110 in a few weeks. Product prices *should* follow, but Vietnam's pump prices are regulated and adjusted on a lag, so the products the refinery can realize are worth, say, \$112 — a crack spread that compressed to **\$2 per barrel** on new volume, *and* the refinery is sitting on tanks of products it valued at the old, lower price, which now must be re-marked. The same machine that minted \$10 a barrel is now minting \$2 a barrel and taking an inventory hit on top. Profit collapses even though the *oil price went up* — exactly the opposite of what a naive "oil is up, buy oil" investor expected.

## How the crude price actually transmits to each link

We have the static picture; now make the *dynamics* precise, because the timing and direction of each link's response is the whole edge. The crude price is the one external variable, and it threads through the chain along four different mechanisms.

**Upstream services: oil price → drilling capex → activity → day-rate × utilization.** The chain here has two amplifiers stacked on top of each other, and stacking them is what gives upstream its violent operating leverage. When oil rises and stays high, exploration-and-production budgets swell, the count of rigs under contract rises (utilization), *and* the price per rig-day rises (because rigs become scarce). A driller's revenue is roughly **day-rate × utilization × days**, so when both the rate and the utilization rise together, revenue rises *multiplicatively*, not additively. And because a rig's *costs* are mostly fixed (the crew, the maintenance, the financing on the steel), a higher day-rate drops almost entirely to the bottom line. That is **operating leverage**: a moderate rise in the day-rate produces an outsized rise in profit. The catch is the *lag* — drilling programs are planned quarters ahead, so PVD's earnings respond to the oil price of six-to-eighteen months ago, not today's. Upstream is a delayed, leveraged echo of the oil cycle.

#### Worked example: the drilling rig day-rate and operating leverage

Put numbers on PVD. Suppose one of its offshore jack-up rigs commands a day-rate of **\$90,000 per day**. If that rig works 330 days in a year (utilization is never a full 365 — there is maintenance, mobilization, gaps between contracts), it earns \$90,000 × 330 = **\$29.7mn** of revenue, about **754 billion dong**. Now suppose the rig's *cash operating cost* — crew, fuel, maintenance, insurance — is **\$60,000 per day**, which is mostly fixed whether the day-rate is high or low. At a \$90,000 day-rate, the daily cash margin is \$30,000; over 330 days, that is **\$9.9mn**, about **251 billion dong**.

Now raise the day-rate to **\$120,000** — a 33% increase, the kind of move you see when oil has been high for a year and rigs are scarce. The cost stays near \$60,000. The daily cash margin jumps to \$60,000 — it *doubled* from a 33% rise in the rate, because the fixed cost did not move. *And* in a tight market, utilization climbs too, say from 330 to 350 days. Now the rig earns \$60,000 × 350 = **\$21mn**, about **533 billion dong** — more than *double* the original margin from a 33% day-rate rise plus a small utilization gain. The intuition: a driller's profit is geared to the day-rate because its costs are fixed, and the day-rate is geared to the oil price — two amplifiers in series, which is why PVD's earnings can multiply in a sustained oil upcycle and why the stock is the sector's highest-octane play on a *durable* high-oil view.

**Midstream gas: oil-linked price formula × take-or-pay volume.** GAS sells gas to power plants and industry under long contracts. The price in those contracts is typically a formula linked to oil (often a percentage of a fuel-oil benchmark plus a fixed transport fee), so GAS's *revenue per unit* has some oil sensitivity. But the *volume* is governed by **take-or-pay**: the buyer commits to *take* a minimum contracted volume, and if it takes less, it *pays* for it anyway. That single clause transforms the economics. It means GAS's volume is not at the mercy of the spot market or of how much gas a power plant happens to want this quarter — it is contracted. So even when the oil price swings violently, GAS's revenue is anchored by guaranteed volumes at formula prices, which is why GAS trades and behaves like a *utility* with a commodity kicker, not like a commodity stock. It is the ballast of the sector.

#### Worked example: take-or-pay gas economics

See why take-or-pay makes GAS steady. Suppose a power plant signs a contract to take **2 billion cubic meters of gas a year** at a formula price that works out to, say, **9 dong per cubic meter equivalent unit** of margin to GAS (a stylized number — real contracts are more complex). On full volume, that is steady, predictable revenue. Now a bad quarter hits: electricity demand softens and the plant only *wants* 1.6 billion cubic meters. Under take-or-pay, the plant must still *pay* for a contracted minimum — say 90% of the 2 billion, or 1.8 billion — whether it burns it or not. So GAS's revenue falls only to the contracted floor, not to actual offtake. Compare that to PVD, whose revenue would simply *vanish* if no one hired its rigs. The intuition: take-or-pay converts a commodity business into something close to an annuity — GAS gets paid for capacity, not just consumption — which is exactly why its earnings are the steadiest in the sector and why it screens as a defensive, dividend-paying utility rather than an oil bet. (For the same reason GAS sits comfortably alongside the names in the [utilities sector](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/utilities-power-water-gas-sector-vietnam-defensive-cash).)

**Downstream refining: the crack spread, plus inventory.** We built the crack spread above. The refiner's profit is **crack spread × throughput**, and the killer is that the crack spread can be wide or thin *independent* of the crude level, while a fast crude move inflicts a one-off inventory gain or loss. So BSR's quarter is a function of (a) the global refining margin environment — wide cracks in 2022-2023 when refining capacity was tight after years of underinvestment, thinner cracks when capacity is ample — and (b) the *direction and speed* of crude within the quarter, through inventory. A slow, steady crude environment with a healthy crack is BSR's paradise; a violent crude spike or crash is its nightmare regardless of which way it goes.

**Downstream retail: a regulated fee × volume, plus inventory.** PLX and OIL earn a regulated margin per liter set by the state's price formula, multiplied by the *liters they move*. So their earnings are dominated by **volume** (which grows with the economy, vehicle ownership, and travel) and by *how well the regulated formula tracks their actual cost*. When crude is stable, the formula tracks well and the retailer earns its steady cut on rising volume — a lovely, boring, growing utility business. When crude spikes fast, the regulated price lags the cost and the per-liter margin gets squeezed, sometimes to nothing; an inventory loss can make a quarter outright bad. So the retailer is *hurt* by a fast crude rise and *helped* by stability — the precise opposite of a naive "oil play".

There is a deeper subtlety in that word *inventory*, and it is worth pausing on because it is the mechanism that turns a "good news" headline (oil up) into a "bad news" earnings print, and it operates on *both* the refiner and the retailer. Every company in the downstream holds a large physical stock of crude and products at all times — crude in the refinery's feed tanks, finished fuel in the retailer's terminals and the pipelines and ships between them. That inventory was *bought at the price prevailing when it was purchased*, which might be weeks ago. Accounting requires the company to value that inventory at the *lower* of cost or net realizable value, and to recognize gains or losses as prices move. So when crude *falls fast*, the inventory the company is holding is suddenly worth less than it paid, and it books an **inventory write-down** — a real, reported loss that has nothing to do with how well the business is running, purely a timing artifact of holding physical stock through a price drop. When crude *rises fast*, the reverse can happen (an inventory gain), but for the retailer this "gain" is more than offset by the *margin squeeze* of selling at a regulated price that lags the now-higher cost of replacing the fuel. The asymmetry is brutal: a *fast crude fall* gives the retailer an inventory loss, and a *fast crude rise* gives it a margin squeeze — so *any* fast move, in either direction, is bad, and only a *stable* crude lets the regulated margin be earned cleanly. This is the precise opposite of the commodity-producer intuition, where you root for your product's price to rise. The downstream company roots for crude to *sit still*.

#### Worked example: an inventory write-down turns "oil up" into a loss

Trace one fast move through BSR's books to feel the mechanism. Suppose, at the start of a quarter, Dung Quat holds about **one million barrels** of crude and products in its tanks and pipelines, bought when crude was around **\$95 per barrel** — a carrying value of roughly \$95mn, about **2.4 trillion dong**. Now crude *falls* sharply mid-quarter, from \$95 to **\$80** per barrel, a not-unusual 16% move. The market value of that same inventory drops to about \$80mn — a paper loss of **\$15mn**, roughly **381 billion dong**, that BSR must recognize *this quarter*, regardless of how its refining operations performed. If the refinery's *underlying* operating profit for the quarter was, say, 600 billion dong on a decent crack spread, that single inventory write-down erases nearly two-thirds of it, and the headline number disappoints even though the *business* was fine. The intuition: a downstream company is always carrying a tank full of yesterday's price, so a fast move in crude lands on its income statement as a one-off gain or loss that swamps the operating result — which is exactly why you cannot read a downstream quarter off the *direction* of oil. You have to know whether crude was *calm* or *violent*, and which way the inventory was caught.

That figure is the sector in one image: the *same* high-oil quarter, two opposite outcomes. On the left, upstream PVD — crude is high, drilling activity picks up, more rigs go to work at higher day-rates, operating leverage kicks in, profit rises. On the right, downstream BSR — that same high crude is a *higher feedstock cost*, the regulated pump price is sticky and cannot pass it all through, the crack spread thins, an inventory loss lands on top, and profit falls. ## The PVN family: who is who on the exchange

Vietnam's oil and gas sector is unusually clean to map, because almost every listed name is part of one corporate family: **PetroVietnam (PVN)**, the state-owned national oil and gas group, which holds majority or strategic stakes across the chain. Learning the sector is largely learning six tickers and where each sits.

**PVD — PV Drilling (upstream services).** Owns and operates offshore drilling rigs (jack-ups and a semi-submersible) and rents them by the day. The purest listed play on the *oil-services* and *day-rate* cycle. Highest beta to a durable high-oil view; most operating leverage; the most lag. When oil has been high for a year and the rig market is tight, PVD's day-rates and utilization climb together and earnings can multiply. When activity dries up, idle rigs bleed cash. PVD is the *upstream accelerator*.

**PVS — PetroVietnam Technical Services (upstream services / EPC).** Builds and installs offshore platforms, lays subsea pipelines, operates support and survey vessels, and increasingly bids for *offshore wind* construction work. PVS is an **EPC** (engineering, procurement, construction) contractor — its earnings are driven by its *order backlog*, the pipeline of awarded projects. A wave of new field developments (above all Block B, below) fills that backlog. PVS is less about the spot oil price and more about *the project cycle* — when big upstream projects get sanctioned, PVS gets the construction contracts.

**GAS — PV Gas (midstream).** The gas gatherer, processor, and seller; the largest and most valuable listed name in the sector, a perennial top-five HOSE weight, and a major dividend payer. Take-or-pay volumes and oil-linked formula prices make it the *utility* of the group — steady, defensive, the ballast. GAS is also the company most exposed to Vietnam's pivot toward **LNG** (imported liquefied natural gas) as domestic gas fields deplete, which is a long-term volume story.

**BSR — Binh Son Refining (downstream refining).** Runs the Dung Quat refinery, processing crude into fuels. Earnings = crack spread × throughput, plus inventory effects. Cyclical with the *global refining margin*, not simply with the oil price. Wide cracks (2022-2023) made it a record-profit machine; thin cracks or a violent crude move squeeze it.

**PLX — Petrolimex (downstream retail).** The dominant fuel retailer, roughly half of Vietnam's ~17,000 petrol stations, plus distribution infrastructure. A regulated-margin, volume-driven *fuel-distribution utility*. Huge revenue, razor-thin margin. Helped by stable crude and rising fuel demand; hurt by fast crude spikes against the regulated price.

**OIL — PV Oil (downstream retail).** The number-two fuel retailer and distributor. Same economics as PLX — regulated margin, volume-driven — at smaller scale, often with a bit more retail-network growth story.

So the family runs left-to-right across the chain: PVD and PVS *find and build* (upstream), GAS *moves and processes* gas (midstream), BSR *refines* and PLX/OIL *sell* the fuel (downstream). And the crude price, the one input, lifts the left, steadies the middle, and can squeeze the right.

One more structural feature shapes how these stocks *trade*, and it is specific to Vietnam: **state ownership and foreign room**. Because PetroVietnam holds large majority stakes across the family (often well over 50%, and in some cases far more), the *free float* — the shares actually available to trade — is smaller than the headline market cap suggests, and the state's hand matters for things like dividend policy and the pace of any *equitisation* (Vietnam's term for partial privatisation, where the state sells down a stake). The flip side is **room ngoại** ("foreign room"): the legal cap on how much of a company foreign investors may own collectively. Several of these names sit at or near their foreign-ownership limit, which means that when global funds or the big Vietnam ETFs want to add oil-and-gas exposure, they can run into a *wall* — there are simply no more shares foreigners are allowed to buy — and that scarcity can put a premium on the available float. So two companies with identical fundamentals can trade differently because one has open foreign room and the other is capped out. For a foreign-flow-aware investor this is a real, tradeable feature (it connects directly to the mechanics in [foreign flows, ETFs, and the index effect](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/foreign-flows-etfs-and-the-index-effect-vietnam)); for a domestic retail investor it mostly means understanding *why* a name might move on foreign buying that suddenly stops at the room limit. Either way, the ownership structure is part of the sector's character, not a footnote.

![Flow chart of the crack spread where a barrel of crude enters a refinery and exits as petrol, diesel, and jet fuel whose summed value minus the crude cost equals the refining margin](/imgs/blogs/oil-gas-sector-vietnam-the-pvn-chain-5.png)

The crack-spread figure makes the refiner's whole business visible. Crude goes in at one end (a cost, in amber); the refinery splits it into a slate of products — petrol is the biggest cut, then diesel, then jet fuel and others; those products have a summed market value; and the refining margin, the *crack spread*, is simply that summed product value minus the cost of the crude. Everything about BSR's profitability is whether that green gap is wide or thin. Notice what is *not* on this diagram: the *level* of crude. A refiner can thrive at \$60 or \$100 crude — what it needs is a wide, stable crack.

## Block B-O Mon: the upstream mega-catalyst

If you follow this sector, one phrase comes up more than any other: **Block B-O Mon** (often just "Block B"). It is the single biggest upstream catalyst in Vietnamese oil and gas, and understanding why tells you a lot about how the chain creates earnings *upstream-first*.

Block B is a giant gas field complex in the shallow waters off Vietnam's southwest coast. The full **Block B-O Mon gas-to-power chain** is one of the largest energy projects in the country's history: it develops the offshore gas field, builds an **offshore-to-onshore pipeline** to bring the gas to the mainland, and feeds a cluster of **O Mon power plants** near Can Tho that will burn that gas to make electricity. The whole thing is a multi-billion-dollar, multi-year program — total investment across the upstream field, the pipeline, and the power plants runs to roughly **\$10bn** equivalent — and after years of delay it reached **final investment decision (FID)** and broke ground in the mid-2020s.

Here is why it electrifies the *upstream-services* stocks specifically. A project this size means a tidal wave of *construction and drilling work*: platforms to fabricate and install, a long pipeline to lay, wells to drill. That work flows straight into the order backlogs of **PVS** (the platform and pipeline EPC contractor) and **PVD** (the drilling rigs to develop the field), and into a swathe of smaller PVN service companies. So Block B is not primarily a bet on the *oil price* at all — it is a bet on a *named, sanctioned, multi-year stream of work* that fills upstream backlogs regardless of where Brent trades next quarter. This is a crucial distinction: the upstream services companies have *two* drivers — the oil-price/day-rate cycle (cyclical, volatile) and the *project-award cycle* (lumpy, visible, backlog-driven). Block B is the biggest single item in the second driver, and it is why analysts talk about a multi-year *earnings visibility* for PVS even if oil itself goes sideways.

Why does this distinction matter so much for how you trade the upstream names? Because the *two drivers have different shapes*, and confusing them is how investors get the timing wrong. The oil-price/day-rate driver is a *cyclical wave* — it crests and troughs with global crude over a multi-year horizon, it is volatile, and it is largely outside Vietnam's control. The project-backlog driver is a *staircase* — a contract gets awarded, revenue gets recognized over the two-to-four years the project takes to build, and the company has *visible, contracted* work that does not evaporate if oil dips for a quarter. A PVS investor who only watches the crude price will be baffled when the stock holds up during an oil dip (the backlog is carrying it) or fails to fly during an oil rally (the backlog is already full and priced in). The right mental model is to track *both dials separately*: the day-rate/utilization cycle for the *cyclical* component, and the contract-award news flow (Block B milestones, new field FIDs, offshore-wind tenders) for the *visibility* component. When both point up at once — a high-oil environment *and* a fresh wave of sanctioned projects — that is the upstream sweet spot, and it is roughly the configuration that drove PVS and PVD's strong 2024 run.

There is also a slower, structural story braided through the midstream that a sector investor should hold in mind: **the LNG transition**. Vietnam's domestic offshore gas fields are *depleting* — the easy gas that GAS has piped ashore for two decades is running down — even as the country's appetite for gas-fired power keeps growing. The bridge is **imported LNG** (liquefied natural gas brought in by tanker, regasified at new coastal terminals, and fed into the grid). GAS is the company building and operating those import terminals, which turns a *depletion* problem into a *volume-growth* opportunity: instead of shrinking as the old fields run dry, GAS pivots to importing and distributing LNG, keeping the take-or-pay utility model intact but on a larger, import-fed volume base. For the *upstream* names, the depletion of old fields is precisely *why* new developments like Block B get sanctioned — the country needs the gas — so the LNG-and-depletion story is the structural reason the upstream project pipeline keeps refilling. It is a slow story, measured in years not quarters, but it is the backdrop that makes GAS a *growth* utility rather than a melting ice cube and keeps the upstream backlog alive.

#### Worked example: downstream thinness — why PLX is not an oil play

Now the example that breaks the "PLX is an oil-price stock" intuition for good. Look at Petrolimex's scale in the first nine months of 2024: revenue of roughly **213 trillion dong** (about **\$8.4bn**) and pretax profit of roughly **3.2 trillion dong** (about **\$126mn**). Divide: the pretax margin is 3.2 / 213 = **~1.5%**. One and a half cents of pretax profit on every dollar of fuel sold. That is a *distribution* margin, not an *oil* margin — it is the regulated cut for the logistics of getting fuel from the refinery to the pump, almost identical to the razor-thin margins of a grocery chain or a parcel carrier.

Now feel how fragile that is. Suppose, in a fast crude spike, PLX takes an *inventory loss* on the fuel sitting in its tanks and pipelines of just **2%** of a quarter's revenue. A quarter's revenue is about 71 trillion dong; 2% of that is about **1.4 trillion dong** — which is *more than the entire 1.07 trillion dong of pretax profit* an average quarter throws off. A single 2% inventory hit can wipe out a quarter's profit entirely. The intuition: a business earning ~1.5% has almost no cushion, so its quarter is dominated not by the *level* of oil but by whether crude was *stable* (margin earned cleanly on big volume) or *spiking* (the regulated price lags and inventory bites). PLX is a volume-and-stability stock wearing an oil-stock costume.

## Common misconceptions

This sector breeds more bad intuitions than any other in the market, and each one has cost retail investors real money. Three in particular are worth killing with a number.

**Misconception 1: "Oil is up, so all oil stocks go up."** This is the original sin of the sector, and it is simply false. We have seen why: upstream services (PVD, PVS) are *helped* by a sustained high-oil environment because it drives drilling activity; the downstream refiner (BSR) and retailers (PLX, OIL) can be *hurt* by a *fast* crude spike because crude is their *cost* and their selling price is regulated and sticky. In the 2022 spike, the divergence was stark: the upstream names eventually ran hard while PLX and BSR struggled with squeezed margins and inventory losses in the worst of the spike. The correction: an oil *rise* is not one signal for "oil stocks" — it is a *positive* for upstream activity (with a lag), roughly *neutral-to-mild* for take-or-pay gas, and potentially *negative* for downstream margins if it is fast. You must specify *which link*.

**Misconception 2: "Petrolimex is a play on the oil price."** Also false, and we just did the math: PLX earns a regulated ~1.5% distribution margin on enormous volume. It is a fuel-*logistics* utility, far more sensitive to *fuel demand* (the economy, vehicle ownership, travel) and to crude *stability* than to the crude *level*. If your thesis is "oil is going to \$100", PLX is one of the *worst* ways to express it — a fast rise to \$100 would squeeze PLX, not reward it. PLX rewards a view that fuel *volumes* will grow steadily and crude will be *calm*. The correction: value PLX as a volume-driven distribution business (think margin per liter × liters), not as a leveraged oil bet.

**Misconception 3: "When crude is high, the refinery is printing money."** Tempting, and sometimes coincidentally true, but the logic is wrong. The refinery's profit is the **crack spread**, not the crude level. BSR can earn a fortune at \$60 crude (if the crack is wide) and almost nothing at \$100 crude (if the crack is thin or crude spiked fast and inflicted an inventory loss). What actually drove BSR's record 2022-2023 profits was not high crude per se — it was an unusually *wide global refining margin* (years of refinery closures had left product capacity tight) *plus* favorable inventory timing. The correction: to forecast BSR, forecast the *crack spread* (global refining margins) and the *speed* of crude, not the crude level. A high crude price with a *collapsing* crack is a disaster for a refiner.

## How it shows up on VN-Index

Now watch the chain behave on the actual market across three episodes, because the theory only pays off if you can read it on the tape.

**The 2022 crude spike and the upstream-downstream divergence.** When Brent vaulted past \$120 in early 2022, the naive trade — buy the whole oil-and-gas basket — split apart within months. The *upstream* logic took time to play out (drilling budgets and day-rates respond with a lag), but the *downstream* pain was immediate: BSR and PLX faced a crude cost running away from regulated and sticky selling prices, and they ate inventory losses and margin compression in the teeth of the spike. So the early move was almost counterintuitive — the part of the sector closest to the spiking commodity (downstream) struggled, while the part that benefits from the *consequences* of high oil (upstream activity) had to wait for the activity cycle to turn. By late 2022 and into 2023, as the high-oil environment translated into a tightening rig market and a recovering project pipeline, PVD and PVS found their footing. The lesson printed on the chart: in a *spike*, fade the reflex to buy downstream; the durable beneficiary is upstream, and it pays *late*.

![Line chart of Brent crude oil annual average price in US dollars per barrel from 2019 to 2025 showing the 2020 collapse, the 2022 spike near 99 dollars, and the cooling to 70 by 2025](/imgs/blogs/oil-gas-sector-vietnam-the-pvn-chain-2.png)

The Brent chart is the master variable for the whole sector. See the shape: a steady ~\$64 in 2019, the pandemic collapse to ~\$42 in 2020, the recovery to ~\$71 in 2021, the war-driven spike to a ~\$99 *annual average* in 2022 (with intra-year prints well above \$120), then a cooling to ~\$82 in 2023, ~\$80 in 2024, and ~\$70 in 2025. That one line drives the entire chain — but, and this is the whole point of the post, it drives each *link* with a different sign and a different lag. The upstream stocks ride the *high plateau* of 2022-2024 with a lag (sustained high oil → more drilling). The refiner rides the *crack spread*, which was unusually wide in 2022-2023. The retailers want the line to be *flat*, not high. Same chart, four different readings.

**The 2024-2025 record profits and the split by link.** As the dust settled, the listed PVN family put up a strong run, and — critically — the profit growth *split by link*, exactly as the chain predicts. In the first nine months of 2024, profit growth came in around **PVD +34%** (upstream services riding higher day-rates and utilization as the global rig market tightened), **BSR +16.6%** (refining on a still-healthy crack and better throughput), and **PLX +11.5%** (retail on steady volume growth and a calmer crude tape that let the regulated margin be earned cleanly). Three different engines, three different growth rates, all inside one "sector." If you had owned the sector as a single blob you would have done fine; if you had *understood the split*, you would have known to overweight the upstream accelerator (PVD) when your view was "oil stays high and rigs stay tight," and to lean on the steadier downstream and gas names when your view was "oil drifts and the economy grows."

![Grouped bar chart of nine month 2024 year on year profit growth by oil and gas sub sector showing PVD at plus 34 percent, BSR at plus 16.6 percent, and PLX at plus 11.5 percent](/imgs/blogs/oil-gas-sector-vietnam-the-pvn-chain-4.png)

That bar chart is the thesis made quantitative: the *same* nine months, the *same* sector, three very different growth numbers — and the ordering (upstream fastest, retail slowest) is exactly what the chain predicts when oil has been high and the rig market is tight. The dispersion *within* the sector is the opportunity. Buying "oil and gas" gets you the average; reading the chain gets you the leader.

It is worth being precise about *why* the ordering came out that way, because the reasoning is the transferable skill, not the specific numbers. PVD led at +34% because by 2024 it was sitting in the *sweet spot* of its two drivers at once: years of elevated crude had finally tightened the global offshore-rig market, so day-rates *and* utilization were rising together (the operating-leverage amplifier we built earlier), and the project pipeline was refilling behind Block B. BSR came in the middle at +16.6% because the global crack spread, while off its extraordinary 2022 highs, was still healthy, and crude was calm enough that inventory effects were not doing much damage — a *good* refining environment, but not the once-in-a-decade one of 2022. PLX brought up the rear at +11.5% precisely *because* it is the steady one: its growth is the slow, dependable compounding of fuel-volume growth times a regulated margin, and a calm crude tape let that margin be earned cleanly — exactly the boring, reliable profile you *want* from the downstream utility, but not the kind of number that makes a stock fly. So the very feature that makes PLX a safe holding (low sensitivity) is the feature that caps its upside in an upcycle, and the very feature that makes PVD dangerous (high leverage) is what hands it the leadership when the cycle is in its favor. That trade-off — beta versus stability — is the choice you are actually making when you pick a link.

**The 2025 cooling and the rotation within the sector.** As Brent cooled toward ~\$70 in 2025, the internal logic rotated. A *falling* oil price is a headwind for the upstream accelerator (drilling budgets get trimmed, day-rate momentum fades), roughly neutral for take-or-pay GAS, and — counterintuitively — often *helpful* for the downstream retailers, because a calmer, lower crude lets the regulated margin be earned cleanly on growing volume without inventory shocks. So a thoughtful sector investor in 2025 would have been *reducing* the high-beta upstream exposure built up during the high-oil years and *rotating* toward the steadier downstream and gas names — selling the accelerator into the cooling, holding the ballast. That is the sector-rotation discipline this series keeps returning to: the leadership *within* a sector rotates with the cycle, not just leadership *across* sectors (see [sector rotation explained](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/sector-rotation-explained-leaders-and-laggards) and [which sector leads VN-Index](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/relative-strength-reading-which-sector-leads-vn-index)).

![Two bar chart of Petrolimex nine month 2024 scale showing revenue near 213 trillion dong against pretax profit near 3.2 trillion dong, the razor thin downstream distribution margin](/imgs/blogs/oil-gas-sector-vietnam-the-pvn-chain-6.png)

The Petrolimex scale chart hammers the downstream point home visually: a towering revenue bar (213 trillion dong, ~\$8.4bn) next to a tiny profit bar (3.2 trillion dong, ~\$126mn). That ~1.5% gap *is* the business. It is why PLX is a volume-and-stability stock, why a fast crude spike is dangerous to it, and why it is one of the worst vehicles for expressing a "crude is going up" view. The picture of a giant revenue bar and a sliver of profit is the picture of a distribution utility, not an oil bet.

## The playbook: how to trade it

Everything above converges on one discipline: **do not trade "oil," trade the link that matches your specific oil view.** Here is how to operationalize it on VN-Index.

**Step 1 — form a *specific* oil view, not just a direction.** "Oil is going up" is not actionable here, because up *how* matters. Distinguish three views: (a) *oil stays structurally high and the rig market stays tight* (a multi-quarter plateau); (b) *oil spikes fast* (a sharp, short move); (c) *oil drifts or falls, economy grows* (calm, lower crude). Each view points at a different link.

**Step 2 — match the sub-sector to the view.**
- If your view is **(a) durable high oil, tight rigs** → overweight **upstream services (PVD, PVS)**. This is the accelerator: high oil for long enough drives drilling activity, day-rates, and utilization, and operating leverage multiplies it into earnings. Add the **Block B / project-backlog** angle for PVS — it gives earnings visibility even if oil wobbles. Accept the *lag*: you are paid late.
- If your view is **(b) a fast crude spike** → this is the trickiest. Avoid the reflex to buy downstream (the spike *hurts* BSR and PLX through inventory and squeezed regulated margins). A fast spike can be a *trading* positive for upstream sentiment but the fundamentals lag; the cleanest expression is often *to do nothing in downstream and wait*. If the spike is driven by a wide *crack* (refining tightness), BSR can work — but that is a crack-spread call, not a crude-level call.
- If your view is **(c) calm or lower crude, growing economy** → lean on the *steady* links: **GAS** (take-or-pay utility, dividend, defensive) and the **downstream retailers (PLX, OIL)**, which earn their regulated margin cleanly on rising fuel volume when crude is calm. Reduce the high-beta upstream exposure — a falling oil price trims drilling budgets and fades the day-rate momentum.

**Step 3 — size by beta.** PVD is the highest-octane name (most operating leverage, most lag, biggest swings) — size it *smaller* and treat it as a high-conviction cyclical bet, not a core holding. GAS is the ballast — it can anchor a sector position because its take-or-pay revenue is steady. PLX/OIL are volume utilities — size them as steady compounders that you *avoid adding to* into a fast crude spike. A balanced sector book might core in GAS, satellite in PVD/PVS when your oil view is durable-high, and hold PLX as a volume play, tilting the weights as the cycle turns.

**Step 4 — watch the right signals.** For upstream: the *global offshore rig count and day-rates*, PVD's *contracted backlog*, and **Block B** milestones (FID, contract awards to PVS). For gas: GAS's *contracted volumes* and the LNG import ramp. For refining: the *global crack spread* (Singapore refining margins are the regional proxy) and Dung Quat's *throughput and turnaround schedule*. For retail: *domestic fuel consumption growth* and the *cadence of the regulated price adjustments*. The crude price itself is the backdrop; the *link-specific* signal is what actually moves each stock.

**Step 5 — know the invalidation.** Each link has a clean disproof. The upstream thesis (own PVD/PVS for durable high oil) is **invalidated** if oil rolls over *and stays down* for a couple of quarters — drilling budgets get cut, day-rates fade, backlogs thin, and the operating leverage works in reverse (a *falling* day-rate against fixed costs crushes the margin just as fast as it built it). The gas thesis is invalidated if take-or-pay contracts are renegotiated unfavorably or if domestic gas depletion outruns the LNG ramp (a volume problem). The downstream-retail thesis is invalidated by a *fast, sustained* crude spike that the regulated formula cannot keep up with (margin squeeze + inventory losses) — the very thing a naive investor *buys* PLX expecting to benefit from. If you find yourself buying the *whole* basket because "oil is up," stop: you have reverted to the misconception this entire post exists to cure.

The deepest point: the oil and gas sector is the clearest case in this whole series of why you must think in *links*, not in *themes*. "Oil" is a theme; the PVN chain is four different businesses with four different relationships to that theme. The investor who trades the *link* — and matches PVD, GAS, BSR, or PLX to a *specific* oil view with a *specific* invalidation — has a real edge over the crowd that buys "dầu khí" because the price of crude is on the news. The crude price is the same one line for everyone; what you do with it depends entirely on which link you own. Master that one idea — that a value chain refracts a single price into different fortunes — and you will never again buy "oil stocks" because oil is on the news; you will buy the *link* that your specific view of crude actually rewards, with the invalidation already written down. (For the macro backdrop that drives crude and the broader commodity dial, see [the four macro dials](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/the-four-macro-dials-rates-credit-fx-commodities); for how to value these very different businesses, see [valuation by sector](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/valuation-by-sector-pe-pb-nav-ev-ebitda).)

That dispersion — green and red living side by side under the *same* oil price — is the entire reason this sector rewards thinking in links instead of themes.
