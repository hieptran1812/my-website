> **TL;DR** — Food and beverage is VN-Index's classic defensive consumer staple: people eat and drink in any cycle, so volumes barely move with the economy, earnings are steady, beta is low (~0.7 vs the index at 1.0), and the best names pay reliable dividends backed by real brand pricing power. > - But "defensive" is not "winner." The old "national stocks" Vinamilk (VNM) and Sabeco (SAB) have gone ex-growth — VNM's profit has sat flat near 9-11 trillion dong for years — while Masan Consumer (MCH) compounded packaged-food earnings from ~2.25tn dong in 2017 to ~7.92tn in 2024 and overtook VNM by market cap (~158tn dong, roughly \$6.2bn).

## The bull that left the boring stocks behind, and the bust that proved them right

Run the tape back to 2021. VN-Index roared from around 1,100 at the start of the year to a euphoric peak near 1,530 by early 2022 — a liquidity-fuelled bull in which a securities broker like SSI tripled, steel names like HPG doubled, and property developers running on bond-funded land banks ran straight up. If you owned that high-beta book, 2021 made you feel like a genius. And if you owned Vinamilk — the bluest of Vietnam's blue chips, the dairy company every retail investor's parents trusted — you spent the entire bull *underperforming*. VNM drifted sideways and even down while everything around it doubled. By the top, the joke on retail forums was that VNM had become a "widow-and-orphan" stock: safe, dull, and going nowhere.

Then 2022 happened. From that April peak near 1,530, VN-Index fell to a panicked low near 870 by mid-November — a drop of more than 40% in seven months, triggered by a credit and corporate-bond crisis. The high-beta heroes of 2021 led the way down and fell the hardest: the broker that tripled gave back two-thirds, the steel name halved, the property developers fell 60-70% and a few never recovered. The book that made you a genius in 2021 was a wealth-destruction machine in 2022. And Vinamilk? It fell too — in a 40% index drawdown everything falls, because foreign funds and margin-financed retail sell what they *can*, not what they *want* — but it fell maybe half as much, never stopped paying its cash dividend, and was back near its old range while the brokers were still nursing 60% holes. The boring stock won the year that mattered.

That two-year sequence is the whole sector in miniature, and it is why food and beverage deserves a careful, honest treatment. F&B is the textbook **defensive consumer staple**: it lags badly when the index is ripping, and it cushions you when the index breaks. This post is about *why* — the staple business model that makes earnings inelastic to the cycle, the brand moat and pricing power that are the real edge, the defensive role the sector plays in a portfolio, and the uncomfortable truth that "defensive" and "best-performing" are not the same word. We will also be brutally honest about the growth trap: the most famous F&B brand on VN-Index has been flat for half a decade, and the sector's biggest winner over that span was a name most foreign investors had barely heard of.

![Food and beverage staple engine flow diagram inelastic demand to steady volume and brand to pricing power feeding stable margin reliable profit and dividend](/imgs/blogs/food-beverage-sector-vietnam-defensive-staple-1.png)

The figure above is the engine of the entire sector in one picture, and we will spend the next several thousand words unpacking each box. Hold the shape in your head: inelastic demand gives you steady volume, a strong brand gives you pricing power, and the two together produce a stable margin that turns into reliable profit and a dependable dividend. A broker sells you volatility; a staple sells you the absence of it. The rest of this post is about understanding that machine well enough to know exactly when to reach for it — and, just as importantly, when to leave it in the drawer.

## Foundations: what makes a consumer staple defensive

Before any trading view, you need to understand what a "consumer staple" actually is and why its earnings barely move with the economy. We will build this from zero, because the *shape* of a staple's earnings — not just the level — is what makes it defensive.

### The everyday analogy: the grocery list that never changes

Think about your own household spending in a recession. When money gets tight, what do you cut first? The holiday trip, the new phone, eating out, the upgrade to a bigger car. Those are *discretionary* — nice-to-haves you can defer. Now think about what you do *not* cut: milk for the kids, cooking oil, fish sauce, instant noodles, the morning coffee, the bottle of water. You buy roughly the same amount of those whether the economy is booming or in a slump, because they are not luxuries — they are the things you consume to live. That is the definition of a **consumer staple**: a product whose demand is *inelastic* to income and to the business cycle, because people buy it out of habit and necessity rather than choice.

A consumer staple company is one that sells those grocery-list items: dairy, packaged food, cooking oil, condiments, bottled water, beer, instant noodles, coffee. The defining feature of the sector is that the top line — total revenue — barely contracts in a downturn. A car maker's sales can fall 30% in a recession because people delay big purchases; a fish-sauce maker's sales fall maybe a few percent, because households keep cooking. This cycle-insensitivity is the entire reason the sector is called *defensive*: its earnings defend themselves against the economy.

### The three pillars of defensiveness

A staple is defensive for three structural reasons, and it helps to name them separately because each one shows up differently in the numbers.

**First, inelastic demand.** "Inelastic" is an economics term meaning quantity barely responds to price or income changes. If milk gets 10% more expensive, you do not stop buying milk — you grumble and pay. If your income drops 10% in a recession, you do not stop feeding your family — you cut the restaurant meals instead. So a staple's *volume* (units sold) is remarkably stable across the cycle. Compare that to a "cyclical" sector like steel or autos, where volume can swing 30-40% peak-to-trough, and you see the difference immediately: the staple's revenue floor is far higher.

**Second, repeat purchase.** A staple is consumed and re-bought constantly — milk weekly, instant noodles weekly, water daily. This is the opposite of a car (bought once a decade) or an apartment (bought once a lifetime). Repeat purchase means the revenue is *recurring*: it arrives in a steady stream rather than in lumpy one-off transactions, and a company that sold you milk last week has a very high probability of selling you milk again this week. Recurring revenue is predictable revenue, and predictable revenue is what lets a staple commit to a steady dividend.

**Third, brand and habit.** People do not buy "milk" in the abstract; they buy *Vinamilk*. They do not buy "beer"; they reach for *Bia Saigon* or *333*. They do not buy "fish sauce"; they buy *Nam Ngu* or *Chin-su*. A trusted brand is a habit, and a habit is a moat: a competitor cannot easily steal a customer who has bought the same brand of milk for twenty years and trusts it to be safe for their children. The brand is what converts a commodity (milk is milk) into a product with pricing power (Vinamilk milk commands a premium). We will spend a whole section on this, because it is the single most important and most under-appreciated part of the sector.

> [!note]
> **Jargon, defined once.** *Consumer staple*: a company selling everyday non-discretionary goods (food, drink, household basics) whose demand is inelastic to the cycle. *Defensive stock*: one whose earnings and price hold up relatively well in a downturn — low beta, steady dividend. *Inelastic demand*: quantity barely changes when price or income changes. *Pricing power*: the ability to raise price without losing much volume. *Beta*: how much a stock moves for a 1% move in VN-Index (1.0 = lockstep, 0.7 = 70% as much, 1.8 = amplified). *Dividend yield*: annual dividend per share divided by price, as a percent — the cash income you earn just for holding. *FMCG*: fast-moving consumer goods, the industry term for low-cost, high-turnover staples. *SKU*: a single stock-keeping unit, one specific product variant. *Gross margin*: revenue minus the cost of goods sold, as a percent of revenue.

### Why beta is low and the dividend is reliable

Put the three pillars together and you see why a staple's earnings barely move with the economic cycle, and why that translates into a low beta and a dependable dividend. Volume is stable (inelastic demand), revenue is recurring (repeat purchase), and the brand protects the margin (pricing power). So the bottom line — profit — is one of the most *predictable* in the market. A predictable profit stream has two consequences that matter to an investor.

The first is **low beta**. Beta measures how much a stock moves when the index moves; a beta of 1.0 means lockstep, 1.8 means a leveraged bet that amplifies both up and down, 0.7 means the stock moves only 70% as much. Because a staple's earnings do not collapse in a recession the way a broker's or a developer's do, its stock does not need to be repriced as violently when the economy turns. Vietnamese F&B names cluster around a beta of 0.7 — well below the index's 1.0 and far below the brokers near 1.8. When VN-Index falls 40%, a 0.7-beta staple tends to fall around 28%, doing exactly what it is supposed to: losing *less*.

The second is a **reliable dividend**. Because the cash flow is steady and the business is mature (more on this below), a staple does not need to plough every dong back into growth — there is no giant factory it urgently must build. So it returns a large chunk of profit to shareholders as a cash dividend, year after year, through booms and busts alike. That dividend is the income engine of the sector, and for a no-growth staple it is the *main* reason to own it. We will quantify it shortly, but the headline is that the best Vietnamese staples yield roughly 3x the index — a serious income stream you collect regardless of whether the stock price goes anywhere.

## The staple business model: volume x price x margin

Now strip away the brand romance and look at how a staple actually makes money, because the trading view falls out of the mechanics. For any food or beverage company, profit is built from a deceptively simple identity:

```
revenue  =  volume (units sold)  x  average_price_per_unit
profit   =  revenue  x  gross_margin
            - selling_and_marketing
            - admin_overhead
            - interest_on_debt
            - tax
```

Three levers drive everything: **volume**, **price**, and **margin**. The whole defensive thesis is that all three are unusually stable for a staple — and the whole growth question is which of the three a given company can still expand.

**Volume** is units sold — litres of milk, cases of beer, packets of noodles. For a staple in a mature market, volume growth is slow and tied to two things: population growth (more people, more mouths) and per-capita consumption (each person consuming more, e.g. Vietnamese dairy consumption rising as incomes rise toward regional norms). In a *developing* market, per-capita consumption can rise fast and volume grows nicely; in a *mature* category, per-capita consumption plateaus and volume growth flattens to roughly population growth. This distinction — developing versus mature category — is the heart of the growth trap we cover below.

**Price** is the average selling price per unit. Here is where pricing power lives: a strong brand can raise price faster than input costs rise, lifting revenue even with flat volume. A weak brand cannot — if it raises price, customers switch to a cheaper rival, so its price is effectively capped at the commodity level. Pricing power is the difference between a *premium staple* (Vinamilk, Chin-su) and a *commodity staple* (a no-name fish sauce that competes only on price).

**Margin** is what is left after the cost of making the product (milk, sugar, packaging, energy) and after the cost of selling it (marketing, distribution). A staple's gross margin is driven by the spread between selling price and input cost. The risk is **input-cost inflation**: when milk powder, sugar, malt or packaging gets more expensive, margin compresses *unless* the company can pass the cost through by raising price — which loops us straight back to pricing power. A brand with pricing power passes the cost through and protects its margin; a brand without it eats the cost and watches margin shrink.

### The distribution moat

There is a fourth lever that does not show up in the identity but quietly determines who wins: **distribution**. A staple lives or dies on being *physically present* at the point of purchase. In Vietnam that means being on the shelf of hundreds of thousands of tiny family-run grocery stores (the traditional trade, which still dominates), plus the modern trade (supermarkets, convenience chains like Bach Hoa Xanh, WinMart, Circle K). A company that has spent decades building relationships with that fragmented retail base — getting its milk into the cooler of the corner shop in every district of every province — has a moat a new entrant cannot replicate with money alone. Vinamilk's distribution network of hundreds of thousands of points of sale is arguably a deeper moat than its brand: even a competitor with a great product and a big marketing budget struggles to get onto the same shelves at the same time. Distribution is the unglamorous, capital-and-relationship-heavy foundation under the whole sector, and it is why incumbents are so hard to dislodge.

### The channel shift: traditional trade versus modern trade

One structural force is reshaping the distribution moat in real time, and it is worth understanding because it is where the next decade of competition is being fought. Historically, the overwhelming majority of Vietnamese groceries were sold through the **traditional trade** — the network of small, family-run shops, wet markets and street vendors that still accounts for the bulk of the country's grocery spend. The incumbents (Vinamilk, Masan, Sabeco) built their moats on dominating this fragmented channel: tens of thousands of distributor relationships, refrigerated coolers placed in corner shops, sales reps who visit each store on a route. That distribution machine took decades and enormous capital to build, and it is exactly why a well-funded new entrant cannot simply buy its way onto the same shelves overnight.

But the **modern trade** — supermarkets, convenience chains (Circle K, GS25), and especially the mini-mart formats like Bach Hoa Xanh and WinMart/WinMart+ — has been growing fast as urbanisation and incomes rise. Modern trade changes the competitive dynamics in two ways. First, it *concentrates* purchasing power: instead of negotiating with thousands of tiny shops, a brand now negotiates with a handful of large chains that can demand better terms, squeezing the brand's margin. Second, it *lowers* the distribution barrier for newcomers: a new product only needs to win a listing with a few big chains to get national shelf presence, rather than rebuilding the incumbent's thousands of relationships. The Masan group's ownership of WinCommerce (the WinMart chain) is no accident in this light — it is a vertical play to *control* the modern-trade channel through which its own MCH products flow, blending the distribution moat with the brand moat. For an investor, the channel shift is a slow-moving variable that gradually erodes the pure-distribution moat of the old incumbents while rewarding the brands that adapt to and control the new channels.

#### Worked example: pricing power drops straight to the bottom line

Take a staple with strong brand pricing power and inelastic demand — call it a dairy leader on a 60 trillion dong revenue base (roughly \$2.4bn at \$1 = 25,400 VND). Suppose input costs (milk powder, packaging) rise, and the company raises its average price 5% across the board. Because demand is inelastic and the brand is trusted, volume stays essentially flat — customers grumble and pay. What happens to profit?

A 5% price rise on a 60tn revenue base, with volume flat, is `0.05 x 60tn = 3tn dong` of extra revenue — about \$118mn. Crucially, that extra revenue arrives with almost no extra cost: the company sold the same number of litres, so the cost of goods barely changed; it just charged more for each. The 3tn is nearly pure incremental gross profit, most of which drops to the operating line. On a company earning ~9.5tn in net profit, a clean 3tn of incremental gross profit (before tax and any cost offset) is a material lift — the kind of move that, repeated annually, is how a premium staple grows earnings even when it sells no extra volume. **The lesson: in a staple, a price increase that demand absorbs is the highest-quality earnings growth there is, because it costs almost nothing to produce.** That is pricing power made concrete.

## Brand pricing power: the real edge

Everything above hinges on one thing, so it deserves its own section: the **brand moat**. In a commodity business — and food is, at the molecular level, a commodity, since one company's milk is chemically much like another's — the only thing that lets a company earn an above-commodity margin is a brand that customers trust, prefer, and will pay extra for. The brand is the edge. Without it, a food company is just a low-margin processor competing on price; with it, the same company earns a premium margin and the pricing power to defend that margin against inflation.

### Why a brand is a moat

A brand becomes a moat through repetition and trust over time. A Vietnamese mother who has fed her children Vinamilk for fifteen years, never once worried about safety, and watched them grow up healthy, is not going to switch to a cheaper unknown milk to save a few thousand dong — the downside risk (a safety scare with her kids) vastly outweighs the saving. That trust is *sticky*: it does not transfer to a competitor just because the competitor is cheaper. The result is that the incumbent can price above the commodity level and keep the volume, because the customer is buying trust, not just calories. The same logic runs through beer (Saigon Beer is a 150-year-old habit you reach for without thinking), fish sauce (Nam Ngu and Chin-su are kitchen defaults), and coffee. The older and more trusted the brand, the deeper the moat — which is precisely why these are called "national stocks": the brand is woven into the country's daily life.

### The pricing-power loop

The most important thing to understand about a strong brand is that it is *self-reinforcing*. It creates a virtuous loop that keeps the moat deep, and once you see the loop you understand why brand leaders are so durable.

![Brand pricing power loop diagram strong brand to raise price to protected margin to marketing spend back to stronger brand](/imgs/blogs/food-beverage-sector-vietnam-defensive-staple-5.png)

Walk the loop in the figure. A **strong brand** gives the company the ability to **raise price** with demand — customers absorb the increase because they trust the product. The price increase, landing on inelastic volume, **protects the margin** against input-cost inflation (the pricing-power point from the worked example). The protected, fat margin throws off cash that the company can **reinvest in marketing and distribution** — TV spots, sponsorships, getting into more stores, refreshing the packaging. That marketing investment, in turn, **strengthens the brand** further, which lets it raise price again next cycle. Round and round: strong brand → pricing power → fat margin → marketing budget → stronger brand. A new entrant trying to break in faces a competitor who out-spends them on marketing *because* they already have the fat margin the entrant lacks. That is why brand leadership, once established, is so hard to overturn — and why the moat is worth a premium valuation.

The loop also explains the sector's defensiveness from a different angle. In a downturn, the brand leader can hold or even raise price (its customers are loyal and the demand is inelastic) while a weak competitor is forced to discount to move volume. So in a recession the gap between the brand leader's margin and the also-ran's margin *widens* — the strong get stronger. This is the opposite of a cyclical sector, where a downturn squeezes everyone toward zero. A staple downturn is a market-share gift to the brand leader.

#### Worked example: the margin-protection a brand buys you

Two fish-sauce makers each sell at a 30tn dong revenue scale. Input costs (anchovies, salt, glass, energy) rise 8% across the industry in a bad inflation year. The branded leader, Chin-su-style, raises its average price 8% to fully pass the cost through; demand is inelastic and the brand is trusted, so volume holds — its gross margin is *unchanged*. The commodity also-ran cannot raise price (its customers shop on price and would switch), so it eats the full 8% cost increase; if its gross margin was 25tn cost on 30tn revenue and costs rise to ~27tn while price is stuck, its gross profit falls from 5tn to ~3tn — a 40% collapse in gross profit (about \$79mn of lost gross profit), purely from lacking pricing power. Same products, same inflation, opposite outcomes. **The lesson: pricing power is not abstract — in an inflation year it is the difference between a flat margin and a margin that nearly halves, and that difference is exactly what the brand premium pays for.**

## Valuation: P/E, dividend yield, and why staples trade at a premium multiple

If staples are no-growth and defensive, you might expect them to trade *cheap*. In fact the best ones trade at a *premium* multiple — a higher price-to-earnings ratio than the market average — and understanding why is essential to not misreading the sector.

### The two valuation tools that matter for staples

The **P/E ratio** (price-to-earnings) is price per share divided by earnings per share — how many years of current earnings you are paying for the stock. A P/E of 15 means you pay 15 dong of price for every 1 dong of annual profit. A higher P/E means the market is paying up, usually for either growth or *quality* (predictable, low-risk earnings). The **dividend yield** is the annual cash dividend per share divided by the price, as a percent — the income you collect just for holding. For a no-growth staple, the dividend yield is the *primary* return, so it is the number you anchor on.

### Why a defensive staple deserves a premium P/E

Here is the part that confuses beginners: a slow-growing staple often trades at a *higher* P/E than a fast-growing cyclical, and that is rational, not a mistake. The reason is that the P/E you should pay for a stock depends on the *quality and durability* of the earnings, not just their growth rate. A cyclical's earnings are volatile and unpredictable — this year's bumper profit could vanish next year — so the market refuses to pay a high multiple for them; it knows the number is unreliable. A staple's earnings are steady, predictable, and likely to still be there in ten years, so the market is willing to pay more *per dong of profit* for that certainty. You are paying a premium for the *bond-like reliability* of the earnings stream, not for growth.

This is the "quality premium," and it is why Vinamilk historically traded at a P/E in the high teens to low twenties even while growing earnings barely at all — the market valued the certainty. The danger, which we cover in the misconceptions section, is paying *too high* a premium: if a no-growth staple's P/E inflates to 25-30x, you are paying a growth multiple for a no-growth business, and the only way that ends is a de-rating (the multiple compressing back to fair value) that costs you money even if earnings hold flat.

### The dividend yield is the floor under the valuation

For a mature staple, the dividend yield acts as a *floor* under the valuation, and this is the most useful way to think about price. If a stock yields 6% and pays a reliable, growing-with-inflation dividend, income investors will step in to buy it whenever the price falls far enough to push the yield up to an attractive level — because a 6-7% reliable income stream is genuinely valuable in a country where bank deposit rates and government bonds may yield less. That buying support is part of why staples fall *less* in a crash: as the price drops, the yield rises, and yield-hungry buyers create a cushion of demand that does not exist under a no-dividend growth stock. The dividend is not just income; it is downside support.

#### Worked example: the dividend case for a no-growth stock

Suppose Vinamilk trades around 64,000 dong per share and pays a cash dividend of roughly 3,850 dong per share for the year. The dividend yield is `3,850 / 64,000 = 6.0%`. Now compare that to the alternatives a Vietnamese saver faces: VN-Index's overall dividend yield is around 1.8%, and a bank deposit or short government bond might pay a few percent. A 6% reliable cash yield — paid out of a stable, branded, cash-generative business — is roughly *3x the index's yield* and competitive with or better than fixed income, with the bonus that the dividend tends to grow with inflation over time as the company raises prices. So even if VNM's share price goes *nowhere* for five years (which it roughly did), an investor who bought for the yield collected ~6% a year in cash — about \$2,400 of income on a \$40,000 position annually — and did fine, while an investor who bought it expecting capital gains was disappointed. **The lesson: for a no-growth staple, the dividend yield is the return; you buy it as a quasi-bond with inflation protection, not as a growth stock, and you judge it on whether the income is reliable and attractive versus the alternatives.**

## The growth trap versus the compounders

Now we reach the most important — and most honest — part of the post. The fatal mistake retail investors make with staples is assuming that a famous, defensive, high-quality brand *must* be a good investment. It is not automatically. A staple's stock return depends entirely on whether the business can still *grow*, and many of Vietnam's most famous F&B names have run out of growth. Defensive and ex-growth are not contradictions — they are the default end-state of a mature staple.

### Vinamilk: the plateau

Vinamilk is the cautionary tale. For most of the 2010s it was a genuine growth stock: Vietnamese dairy consumption was rising fast as incomes climbed toward regional norms, VNM had the brand and the distribution, and its profit compounded year after year. The market rightly paid a premium multiple. But somewhere around the end of the decade, the domestic dairy *category* matured — per-capita milk consumption stopped rising as fast, the easy volume growth was gone, and competition (foreign brands, local rivals like TH True Milk) chipped at market share. VNM's profit stopped growing.

![Line chart of Vinamilk net profit 2020 to 2024 showing the plateau near nine to eleven trillion dong](/imgs/blogs/food-beverage-sector-vietnam-defensive-staple-2.png)

Look at the numbers. Vinamilk's net profit ran 11.24 trillion dong in 2020, 10.63tn in 2021, then *fell* to 8.58tn in 2022 (squeezed by milk-powder input-cost inflation it could not fully pass through fast enough), recovered to 8.97tn in 2023 and 9.45tn in 2024. Over five years the profit went *sideways to slightly down* — from 11.2tn to 9.5tn — a plateau, not a growth curve. The defensiveness was real: through a 40% index crash in 2022, VNM's earnings held in a tight band and it kept paying a big dividend. But the *growth* was gone. And a market that had paid a premium multiple for growth re-priced VNM lower as it dawned on everyone that the growth was structurally over — a **de-rating**, where the P/E falls back toward a no-growth fair value. A de-rating on flat earnings is a falling price: that is why VNM, a wonderful defensive business, was a poor *investment* for anyone who bought it expecting the old growth to continue.

This is the **growth trap**: a mature staple in a saturated category, with a great brand and a fat dividend, whose earnings have plateaued and whose multiple is grinding down to match. It is still defensive — you will not lose much in a crash and you collect the dividend — but you will not make capital gains either, and if you overpaid for the premium multiple you can lose money even as the business chugs along just fine.

### Masan Consumer: the compounder

Now the counter-example, and it is instructive precisely because it was *not* the famous name. While Vinamilk plateaued, **Masan Consumer (MCH)** — the packaged-food and condiments arm of the Masan group, maker of Chin-su and Nam Ngu sauces, Omachi and Kokomi noodles, Wake-Up and Vinacafé coffee — compounded its earnings hard. MCH's net profit went from around 2.25 trillion dong in 2017 to ~7.92 trillion in 2024 (about \$312mn) — more than tripling over seven years while VNM went sideways. By 2024, MCH had quietly become the largest F&B company on VN-Index by market capitalisation, around 158 trillion dong (roughly \$6.2bn), overtaking Vinamilk.

How did MCH grow when VNM could not? Three reasons, and they map straight onto the volume-price-margin levers. First, **category**: MCH operates in *multiple* packaged-food categories (sauces, noodles, coffee, processed meat, beverages) rather than one mature one, and several of those categories were still developing — premiumisation (selling a more expensive, better version of the same product to the same customer) had a long runway. Second, **premiumisation and price**: MCH relentlessly traded customers up from basic SKUs to premium ones (basic fish sauce → premium Chin-su; basic noodles → premium Omachi), lifting average price and margin without needing more volume. Third, **innovation cadence**: a steady stream of new products and line extensions kept volume growing in categories where the incumbent was complacent. The result was a staple that was *both* defensive *and* growing — the rare combination that re-rated *upward* (its multiple expanded) instead of down.

![Grouped bar chart of Vietnam food and beverage leaders 2024 revenue and net profit for Vinamilk Masan Consumer and Sabeco](/imgs/blogs/food-beverage-sector-vietnam-defensive-staple-3.png)

The chart lays the three leaders side by side on 2024 numbers. Vinamilk is still the revenue king — 61.8tn dong of revenue producing 9.45tn of profit — the biggest by sales, the slow giant. Masan Consumer did 30.9tn of revenue but converted it to 7.92tn of profit — *half* VNM's revenue but more than 80% of its profit, because MCH's premium-condiment margins are structurally fatter than dairy's. Sabeco (the beer leader) did 31.9tn of revenue and 4.5tn of profit. The punchline is in the contrast: VNM has the scale but the flat trajectory; MCH has the lower revenue but the higher-margin, still-growing trajectory that took it past VNM by market value. **Defensive does not tell you which one to own — growth does.** Two equally defensive, equally branded staples produced opposite shareholder outcomes over the same five years, and the only thing that separated them was whether the earnings could still compound.

#### Worked example: the compounder versus the plateau, in dong

Put real numbers on both paths. An investor who bought Masan Consumer's earnings in 2017 owned a business making 2.25tn dong of net profit; by 2024 that business made 7.92tn dong (~\$312mn) — earnings grew roughly 3.5x, a compound annual growth rate of about `(7.92/2.25)^(1/7) - 1 ≈ 19.7%` a year. An investor who bought Vinamilk over a comparable span owned a business making 11.2tn in 2020 that made 9.5tn in 2024 — earnings *shrank* at roughly `(9.45/11.24)^(1/4) - 1 ≈ -4.2%` a year. Now layer on the multiple: a business growing earnings ~20% a year *re-rates up* (the market pays a higher P/E as it recognises the growth), so MCH's price benefited from both rising earnings *and* a rising multiple — a double tailwind. A business shrinking earnings ~4% a year *de-rates* (the multiple falls toward no-growth fair value), so VNM's price suffered from both flat-to-falling earnings *and* a falling multiple — a double headwind. **The lesson: in staples, the earnings trajectory is destiny. Two defensive brands diverged completely in stock return because one compounded and re-rated up while the other plateaued and de-rated down — and no amount of brand fame or dividend reliability changes that arithmetic.**

## Common misconceptions

The food and beverage sector attracts more lazy thinking than almost any other, precisely because the brands are household names and "everyone knows" they are good companies. Here are the myths that cost retail investors money, each corrected with a number.

**Misconception 1: "Defensive means it delivers the best returns."** This is the central error. Defensive means *low beta and steady earnings* — it falls less in a crash and barely moves in a recession. It does *not* mean it goes up the most; over a full bull market a defensive staple will badly *lag* the brokers, steel and property names that double and triple. In the 2021 bull, VNM was flat while SSI tripled. Defensive is about *not losing*, not about *winning big*. If your goal is maximum return in a rising market, you would *underweight* staples on purpose. The correct mental model is insurance, not a lottery ticket: you hold staples for what they do in the bad year, accepting that they cost you upside in the good years.

**Misconception 2: "A famous brand always keeps growing."** Vinamilk is the refutation. It is the most famous, most trusted dairy brand in the country, with a deep distribution moat and a fat dividend — and its profit went from 11.2tn in 2020 to 9.5tn in 2024, *sideways to down*, because its core category matured. Brand fame is a *moat* (it protects margin and market share), not an *engine* (it does not by itself create new growth once the category saturates). A famous brand in a mature category is a defensive cash cow, not a growth stock — and confusing the two is exactly how you overpay.

**Misconception 3: "A high-P/E staple is overpriced."** Not necessarily — a premium multiple can be entirely rational for a high-quality, predictable staple, because you are paying for the *certainty and durability* of the earnings, not for growth. A staple at 18-20x P/E with bond-like earnings and a 5% dividend can be fairly valued; a cyclical at 8x P/E with earnings that could halve next year can be *expensively* valued despite the lower multiple. The P/E in isolation tells you nothing — you have to ask what quality of earnings you are buying. *That said*, the inverse trap is real: a *no-growth* staple at a *growth* multiple (25-30x) genuinely is overpriced, because there is no growth to justify the premium and the only path forward is a de-rating. The skill is distinguishing "premium for quality" (fine) from "premium for growth that does not exist" (a trap).

**Misconception 4: "Staples are immune to commodity prices."** They are *less* exposed than a steel mill, but not immune. A dairy company's biggest input is milk powder; a beer company's is malt, aluminium and packaging; a noodle maker's is wheat flour and palm oil. When those inputs spike, margin compresses *unless* the company can pass the cost through with a price increase — which depends on pricing power. Vinamilk's 2022 profit *fell* to 8.58tn partly because milk-powder costs spiked faster than it could raise prices. The pricing-power moat is what *limits* commodity exposure, but a bad inflation year still bites the margin of even a strong staple — and it crushes the margin of a weak one.

**Misconception 5: "The dividend is guaranteed."** A staple's dividend is *reliable*, not *guaranteed*. It is paid out of earnings, and if earnings fall hard enough (a major safety scandal, a regulatory shock, a debt-fuelled acquisition that strains the balance sheet), the dividend can be cut. The reliability comes from the *stability of the underlying cash flow*, which is real — but it is a probabilistic reliability, not a contractual one like a bond coupon. You judge the safety of the dividend by the **payout ratio** (dividend as a share of earnings — a staple paying out 50-70% of earnings has plenty of cushion; one paying out 100%+ is stretching) and the balance sheet (low debt = safe; high debt = at risk in a downturn).

## How it shows up on VN-Index

Abstract mechanics are one thing; the trades and de-ratings that actually happened on VN-Index are another. Here is how the food and beverage thesis has played out in real, dated episodes.

### The VNM and SAB de-rating

The clearest sector story of the last half-decade is the slow de-rating of the two "national stocks," Vinamilk (VNM) and Sabeco (SAB). Both were market darlings in the mid-2010s — VNM was for years the single largest stock in VN-Index by weight, SAB came to market in a blockbuster 2017 privatisation in which Thai conglomerate ThaiBev paid an eye-watering price (around 320,000 dong per share, valuing SAB at roughly \$4.8bn) for a controlling stake, a price that implied years of continued growth. Then both ran into category maturity and external shocks (covered next), their earnings flattened, and the market spent years grinding their premium multiples down. VNM's profit going from 11.2tn to 9.5tn while its multiple compressed produced a multi-year drift lower in the share price even as the business stayed perfectly healthy. SAB's experience was worse, because beer got hit by a regulatory shock on top of maturity. The lesson for a VN-Index investor: a stock can be a wonderful, defensive, dividend-paying business *and* a poor investment at the same time, if you bought it at a growth multiple it could no longer justify.

### The Decree-100 hit to beer

Beer deserves its own paragraph because it shows that "defensive" has exceptions. Beer is normally a textbook staple — Vietnamese beer consumption is enormous and habitual. But on 1 January 2020, the government implemented **Decree 100**, which imposed severe penalties for drink-driving (a near-zero blood-alcohol limit with heavy fines and licence suspensions, enforced aggressively). The effect on beer volume was immediate and brutal: Vietnamese consumers, facing real penalties for driving home after drinking, simply drank less when out. Sabeco's volume and revenue dropped sharply in early 2020 — and then COVID-19 arrived and closed the on-premise channel (bars, restaurants) where much beer is consumed, compounding the blow. SAB's revenue and profit fell hard, and the stock de-rated further. The lesson: a staple's demand is *inelastic to the economic cycle* but not *immune to a regulatory or behavioural shock* aimed directly at the product. Decree 100 was a structural, permanent hit to a category that everyone had assumed was bulletproof — a reminder that "defensive" describes resilience to the *business cycle*, not to *policy*.

### Where new growth comes from in a mature sector

It is worth pausing on *why* MCH could grow when VNM could not, because it points to where an investor should look for the next compounder. In a mature staple sector, organic volume growth is gone — the population grows slowly and per-capita consumption of the core product has plateaued. So growth has to come from somewhere other than "sell more litres of the same milk to the same people." There are four real sources, and a company that can tap them is a growth staple rather than a plateaued one.

The first is **premiumisation** — trading the same customer up to a more expensive version of the same product (basic fish sauce to premium Chin-su, basic milk to organic or fortified milk, basic noodles to premium Omachi). This lifts average price and margin with no extra volume, and it has a long runway in a country whose incomes are still rising toward regional norms. The second is **category expansion** — entering adjacent categories (a condiment maker moving into noodles, then coffee, then processed meat, then beverages), so that even as each individual category matures, the company keeps adding new ones. MCH's multi-category model is precisely this; VNM's single-category dairy focus is precisely the lack of it. The third is **export and overseas** — selling the brand abroad (Vinamilk does have a meaningful and growing export business and overseas operations, which is one of its few remaining growth legs). The fourth is **mergers and acquisitions** — buying a smaller brand and plugging it into your distribution machine, instantly adding revenue. The screen for a growth staple, then, is simple: does the company have premiumisation headroom, multiple categories, an export leg, or a credible M&A engine? If yes, it can compound; if it is a single mature category with none of these, it is a plateau, no matter how good the brand.

### MCH's rise to the top

The happiest VN-Index F&B story is Masan Consumer's quiet ascent. While VNM and SAB de-rated, MCH compounded earnings ~20% a year and re-rated *up*, and by 2024 it had overtaken Vinamilk to become the largest F&B name on the exchange by market cap, around 158tn dong (~\$6.2bn). What is striking is how *under-the-radar* this was for foreign investors: VNM was the famous index heavyweight every global fund owned, while MCH was a less-liquid name (much of it held within the Masan group structure) that many foreigners barely tracked. The investor who understood that the *growth* — not the *fame* — was what mattered, and who looked past the obvious national champion to the less-glamorous compounder, captured a multi-bagger while the crowd held the plateauing blue chip. This is the single most actionable lesson in the sector: when you screen F&B, screen for the *earnings trajectory*, not the brand recognition.

### The 2022 crash: defensiveness in action

Finally, the bust that proved the defensive case. In the 2022 drawdown — VN-Index from ~1,530 in April to ~870 in November, down more than 40% — the F&B staples did exactly what defensives are supposed to do. Everything fell (forced selling spares nothing in a panic), but VNM fell roughly half as much as the high-beta brokers and developers, kept paying its dividend uninterrupted, and recovered quickly once the panic passed. An investor who had rotated *into* staples in late 2021 as the bull got frothy — trading some of their broker and property exposure for VNM and consumer names — would have given up the last leg of the rally but dodged the worst of the crash and kept collecting a dividend the whole way down. That is the defensive playbook working as designed, and it is the subject of the final section.

![Before and after comparison of staple versus cyclical performance in a bull year and a bust year on VN-Index](/imgs/blogs/food-beverage-sector-vietnam-defensive-staple-4.png)

The figure captures the whole trade-off in two panels. In the **bull year** (left), the cyclicals — brokers, steel — race ahead while the staple lags, sitting there paying its dividend while everything around it doubles; owning the staple *cost* you upside. In the **bust year** (right), the cyclicals crater 60-70% while the staple gives back only a modest slice and keeps paying; owning the staple *saved* you. Same stock, opposite roles, depending on the regime. The defensive investor accepts the lag on the left to buy the cushion on the right — and over a *full* cycle, losing less in the bust can matter more for compound returns than winning more in the bull, because you do not have to climb out of a 60% hole.

#### Worked example: the defensive math of a drawdown

Make the cushion concrete. Suppose VN-Index falls 40% in a bust. A food and beverage staple with a beta of 0.7 tends to fall around `0.7 x 40% = 28%` — and on top of price, it keeps paying its ~6% cash dividend, so your *total* loss net of income is closer to `28% - 6% ≈ 22%` over the year. Now take a securities broker with a beta of 1.8: it tends to fall around `1.8 x 40% = 72%`, pays little or no dividend (brokers reinvest and their earnings vanish in a bear market), so your loss is roughly 70%. The difference is not subtle: a 22% drawdown versus a 70% drawdown. And the recovery math is even more lopsided — to climb back from a 22% loss you need a ~28% gain, but to climb back from a 70% loss you need a *233%* gain. On a 1,000,000,000 dong portfolio (roughly \$39,000), the staple holder is down to ~780mn dong and needs a 28% rally to recover; the broker holder is down to ~300mn dong and needs the index to *more than triple* to get whole. **The lesson: defensiveness is not about the year you lose less — it is about the years afterward you do not have to spend digging out of a hole, because avoided losses compound in your favour just as surely as gains do.**

## The playbook: how to trade the defensive staple

Everything above resolves into a concrete way to use the sector. Food and beverage is a *tool* — a specific tool for a specific job — and the playbook is about knowing when to reach for it, how much to hold, and what would prove the thesis wrong.

### The core position: own staples for defense and yield

The base-case role of F&B in a Vietnamese portfolio is the **defensive, income-producing ballast** — the part of your book that does not blow up in a crash and pays you cash to wait. You hold a core position in the highest-quality names (the brand leaders with deep distribution and a reliable, well-covered dividend) *through the cycle*, sized as the stabiliser of the portfolio rather than its engine. In a typical allocation, staples might be 10-20% of a long-term equity book — enough to materially dampen the drawdown and supply a steady dividend stream, not so much that you give up all the upside of Vietnam's structural growth. You are buying these for the *income* (a 5-6% yield, ~3x the index) and the *low beta* (~0.7), not for capital gains, and you judge them on the reliability of those two things.

### The rotation: overweight late-cycle and into risk-off

The active use of the sector is **defensive rotation**. The right time to *increase* your F&B weight is when the cycle is late and frothy or when risk is rising — when VN-Index has run hard on liquidity, valuations are stretched, the brokers and property names have doubled, and the macro signals (credit tightening, rising rates, a turning liquidity cycle — see the linked rotation and liquidity posts) say the bull is in its final innings. That is when you *sell some beta* (trim brokers, steel, property) and *rotate into staples*, accepting that you will lag if the bull runs longer, in exchange for being defensively positioned when it breaks. The signal to rotate *out* of staples (back into cyclicals) is the mirror image: a fresh easing cycle, a liquidity surge, the early innings of a new bull — when you want beta, not ballast, and staples will be the laggards again. Staples are where you *hide* late-cycle and in risk-off, and where you *underweight* early-cycle in a fresh bull.

### Within the sector: screen for growth, not fame

The critical sub-decision, once you have decided to own F&B, is *which* names — and the playbook here is the hardest-won lesson of the whole post: **screen for the earnings trajectory, not the brand fame.** Sort the sector by which businesses can still compound earnings (a developing category, premiumisation runway, innovation cadence, multiple categories) versus which have plateaued (a single mature category, flat volume, a multiple that is de-rating). The compounders (the MCH-style names) can deliver *both* defense *and* capital gains and deserve the premium multiple; the plateaued national champions (the VNM/SAB-style names) deliver defense and dividend but no growth, and you only want them at a price where the *dividend yield* compensates for the lack of upside — i.e. when they have de-rated to a genuinely cheap, high-yield level, not when they still carry a growth multiple they cannot justify.

### The invalidation: what proves the thesis wrong

Every position needs a line that says "I was wrong." For the defensive-staple thesis there are three.

First, **the dividend is cut or the payout ratio blows out.** If a staple you own for income cuts its dividend, or pushes its payout ratio above 100% (paying out more than it earns, funding the dividend from the balance sheet), the income thesis is broken — the cash flow is no longer covering the payout, which means either earnings have deteriorated structurally or the balance sheet is being raided. That is a sell signal regardless of how good the brand is.

Second, **the moat erodes — margin and market share fall together.** The whole thesis rests on pricing power. If you see gross margin *and* market share declining at the same time (the company is losing both the price war and the volume war — usually to a sharper competitor or a shift in consumer preference), the brand moat is cracking, and a staple without a moat is just a low-margin commodity processor that does not deserve a premium multiple. Margin falling while share holds (a temporary input-cost squeeze) is survivable; margin falling *and* share falling together is the moat dying.

Third, **a structural demand shock to the category** — a Decree-100-style regulatory blow, a permanent shift in consumer behaviour (health trends away from sugar or alcohol), or a disruptive new channel/format that the incumbent cannot adapt to. A staple is defensive against the *cycle*, not against a *structural* attack on the category itself. When the category's long-run demand curve bends down permanently, the defensiveness does not save you — beer holders learned this with Decree 100.

If none of those three fire, the defensive thesis is intact: hold the core for income and stability, rotate the weight up late-cycle and into risk-off, screen within the sector for growth over fame, and accept — cheerfully — that you will lag the next bull. That is the deal a defensive staple offers, and for the right slice of a portfolio it is a very good deal: less upside, more income, smaller losses, and the staying power to still be standing when the cycle turns.

The matrix above is the sector at a glance for positioning. Dairy (VNM) — a deep moat, low remaining growth, a fat dividend: the classic plateaued cash cow you own for yield. Sugar and agri (the upstream input names) — a thin moat, cyclical and commodity-exposed, a variable dividend: not really a staple at all, more a cyclical wearing a staple's coat, and the part of the sector to treat with the most caution. That is what you are buying when you buy the sector, and it is also exactly what you are *not* buying: index-beating upside. Own food and beverage for the stability and the yield, rotate its weight with the cycle, pick growth over fame within it, and you will have used the most reliable tool on VN-Index for precisely the job it was built to do.
