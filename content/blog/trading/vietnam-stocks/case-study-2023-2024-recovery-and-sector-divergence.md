> **TL;DR** -- The 2023-2024 recovery was not a tide that lifted all boats. It was a *selective* recovery where the sector you owned decided almost your entire outcome, even as the index itself climbed steadily back.
>
> - **VN-Index recovered, but the gains were lopsided.** From the November 2022 trough near 874, the index climbed to about 1,130 at the end of 2023 and about 1,266 at the end of 2024 -- but securities brokers jumped roughly +75% in 2023 while real estate barely moved, and in 2024 IT and FPT surged while property actually *fell*. Beta was not the trade; selection was.
> - **This was a divergent recovery, not a liquidity bull.** Unlike the 2020-2021 everything-rally fueled by a flood of cheap margin and reopening euphoria, 2023-2024 was powered by rate cuts that *repaired confidence* without flooding the system with leverage. No flood means no broad lift -- only the genuinely improving sectors ran.
> - **Leadership rotated in a clean sequence.** Brokers led the 2023 bounce (highest beta, first to price the rate-cut turn), then banks led 2024 (about +26% versus the index's +12%) and IT/FPT surged (about +80%) on a structural growth story. The handoff was readable in relative strength weeks before it was obvious in price.
> - **The index rose while foreigners sold a record amount.** In 2024, foreign investors net-sold roughly 92.5 trillion dong (about \$3.6bn) -- the largest annual foreign outflow on record -- and VN-Index *still* rose about 12%. Domestic retail absorbed the entire wave. Foreign selling did not sink the market; it just changed who owned it.
> - The one number to remember: in 2024, IT returned roughly **+80%** while real estate returned about **-3%**. An 80-point spread inside the same rising index is the whole lesson. This is educational, not financial advice.

On the final trading days of December 2024, two facts sat side by side on every Vietnamese broker's terminal, and they looked like they could not both be true. VN-Index -- the market-cap-weighted benchmark of the stocks listed on the Ho Chi Minh exchange (HOSE) -- had closed the year near 1,266, up about 12% on the year and more than 40% above the panic low it had printed two years earlier. And foreign investors, the institutional money that domestic retail traders have spent two decades watching like a weather vane, had just finished net-selling the largest annual amount in the market's history: roughly 92.5 trillion dong, about \$3.6 billion at the mid-decade rate of about \$1 to 25,400 dong.

A retail investor who had learned one rule -- "follow the foreigners, they know something you don't" -- would have spent all of 2024 fighting the tape and losing. The foreigners sold relentlessly, month after month, and the index went *up* anyway. Meanwhile a different investor, one who had simply asked "which sectors are actually leading?", would have ridden information technology to roughly +80% and banks to roughly +26% while quietly avoiding real estate, which finished the year slightly *down* even as the index it belonged to rose by double digits.

This is the case study that ties the whole Vietnam Sector Playbook together, because it breaks the two laziest mental models a beginner brings to a recovering market. The first lazy model is *"a recovery lifts everything, so just buy the index."* The second is *"foreign selling sinks the market, so get out when they get out."* The 2023-2024 recovery proved both wrong in the same two years. Let us reconstruct exactly what happened, why this recovery was structurally different from the 2021 boom that preceded it, and how the frameworks from the rest of this series -- relative strength, sector rotation, the foreign-flow read, the valuation trap -- would have navigated it.

![Flow diagram showing rate cuts repairing confidence but no liquidity flood, leading winners brokers banks IT to diverge from laggard property](/imgs/blogs/case-study-2023-2024-recovery-and-sector-divergence-1.png)

Look at the figure. It is the entire post in one picture. The State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) cut its policy rates, which *repaired confidence* -- the panic of late 2022 drained out of the market. But -- and this is the hinge of the whole story -- the cuts did **not** produce a liquidity flood the way 2020-2021 did. There was no torrent of fresh margin debt washing every stock higher. So instead of a broad rally, the market *diverged*: the sectors with a genuine earnings or structural reason to rise (brokers first, then banks and IT) pulled sharply ahead, while the sectors still nursing real wounds (above all real estate, still buried under its corporate-bond overhang) stayed flat to down. A repaired-but-not-flooded market is, by its nature, a divergent market. That single distinction is what made selection -- not beta -- the trade of 2023-2024.

## Foundations: what a "divergent recovery" actually is, and why this one was

Before we trace the year-by-year story, we need to build one concept cleanly from zero, because everything else hangs off it: the difference between a **liquidity bull market** and a **divergent recovery**. They both involve the index going up. They are otherwise almost opposites, and confusing them is how a beginner gets caught buying the wrong sector at the wrong time.

### Two ways an index can rise

Start with the most basic mechanical fact. VN-Index is a weighted average of its constituent stocks. There are exactly two ways for a weighted average to rise:

1. **Almost everything rises together** (a broad, correlated move). The average goes up because nearly every component goes up. This is what a *liquidity bull market* looks like.
2. **A handful of components rise sharply while others stay flat or fall** (a narrow, divergent move). The average still goes up -- but only because the winners outweigh the losers. This is what a *divergent recovery* looks like.

Both produce a rising line on the chart. But they demand completely different behavior from an investor. In case 1, owning *anything* works; you can close your eyes, buy the index, and win -- selection barely matters because correlation is near 1.0 (everything moves together). In case 2, owning the *wrong* thing loses even as the index wins -- selection is *everything*, because correlation has collapsed and the sectors have decoupled.

Here is the everyday analogy before we go deeper. Picture a rising tide in a harbor versus a few speedboats accelerating across a flat lake. In the harbor, the tide comes in and every boat -- yacht, dinghy, rowboat -- floats higher by the same amount; you do not need to pick the right boat, you just need to *be in the water*. That is a liquidity bull. On the flat lake, the water level is not changing at all; the average position of the boats only rises because two or three speedboats are roaring ahead while the rest sit still. If you are in a speedboat, you fly; if you are in a parked rowboat, you go nowhere -- even though the *average boat position* is moving forward. That is a divergent recovery. The 2021 boom was the tide. The 2023-2024 recovery was the speedboats.

### How you actually measure divergence: breadth and correlation

The analogy is intuitive, but you can put real numbers on "broad versus divergent," and a serious investor should. Two measurements do the job.

The first is **breadth** -- the fraction of stocks (or sectors) participating in the move. In a broad liquidity bull, breadth is high: on a typical up day, 80-90% of stocks rise, and over a quarter, almost every sector posts a positive return. In a divergent recovery, breadth is low: the index can grind to a new high while only a minority of stocks are actually advancing, and several whole sectors are flat or falling. A simple breadth gauge any retail investor can watch is the *advance-decline line* (the running count of advancing minus declining stocks) or, at the sector level, *how many of the major sectors are beating the index* in a given window. In 2024, that second number was small -- a handful of sectors (IT, retail, banks) carried almost the entire index move while the rest, especially real estate, dragged. Narrow breadth *is* divergence, measured.

The second is **correlation** -- how tightly the sectors move together. Correlation runs from +1.0 (everything moves in lockstep) down toward 0 (sectors move independently). In the 2021 liquidity bull, cross-sector correlation was very high, near 1.0: when the index rose, essentially every sector rose with it, so a diversified basket and a concentrated bet earned nearly the same thing. In 2023-2024, correlation *fell* -- sectors decoupled, IT and property could move in opposite directions in the same month, and diversification across sectors stopped protecting you because the sectors were no longer doing the same thing. (We devote a whole post to this: [sector correlations and diversification](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/sector-correlations-and-diversification-vietnam).) The practical punchline is brutal and simple: *high correlation rewards owning the market; low correlation rewards picking the sector.* When correlation collapses, beta stops being a strategy and selection becomes the only one that matters.

### What makes a recovery divergent rather than broad

The deciding variable is **where the money comes from and how much of it there is**. A broad liquidity bull needs a *flood* of new buying power that has to go *somewhere* and ends up bidding up everything indiscriminately. A divergent recovery happens when confidence returns but fresh liquidity does *not* flood in -- so the limited money that does come back is *selective*, flowing only toward sectors with a real reason to own them.

The single best proxy for that "flood" in Vietnam is **margin debt** -- the borrowed money (*ký quỹ*, literally "margin deposit") that retail investors use to buy more stock than their cash allows. When margin balances explode, you have a liquidity flood and a broad bull. When margin grows only modestly, you have, at most, a selective recovery. We have a whole post on this dynamic ([the margin cycle](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/liquidity-and-the-margin-cycle-vietnam)); the one-line version is that margin is the accelerant, and 2023-2024 simply did not have a margin explosion on the 2021 scale. Confidence came back faster than leverage did, and that gap is precisely what made the recovery selective.

#### Worked example: the difference between beta and selection in a divergent market

Take two investors at the start of 2024, each with 254 million dong to invest -- about \$10,000 at \$1 = 25,400 dong. Investor A believes in beta: "the market is recovering, I'll just buy a broad basket and ride the index up." Investor A earns roughly the index return for 2024, about +12%, ending the year with about 284 million dong -- a gain of about 30 million dong, or about \$1,200.

Investor B believes in selection: she reads relative strength, sees IT and banks leading, and concentrates there. Say she splits evenly between an IT position (up roughly +80% on the year) and a banks position (up roughly +26%). Her blended return is about +53%, ending the year with about 389 million dong -- a gain of about 135 million dong, or about \$5,300.

Same market, same starting capital, same twelve months. The selection investor ended up with more than *four times* the dollar gain of the beta investor. **In a divergent recovery, the sector call is not a refinement on top of your return -- the sector call basically *is* your return.** (And note the symmetry: had Investor B concentrated in real estate, down about -3%, she would have *lost* money in a year the index rose 12%. Selection cuts both ways, which is exactly why it matters.)

### Why 2023-2024 specifically was set up to diverge

Three conditions made this recovery selective rather than broad, and you should hold all three in your head as we walk through the timeline:

**First, the rate cuts repaired confidence but did not create a leverage flood.** The SBV cut its refinancing rate from 6.0% in 2022 down to 4.5% across 2023 and held it there through 2024. That matters enormously -- falling rates lift the present value of future earnings, make deposits less attractive than stocks, and signal that the central bank is supporting growth. (For the full mechanism of how the SBV's rate and credit tools move the market, see [Vietnam monetary policy](/blog/trading/finance/vietnam-monetary-policy-state-bank-dong-credit-ceiling).) But a rate cut is *permission* to take risk, not a *flood* of money forcing risk to be taken. Confidence returned; the speculative leverage of 2021 did not.

**Second, one sector was still genuinely broken and could not participate.** Real estate -- about a fifth of the market by influence and the emotional heart of Vietnamese retail investing -- was still working through the corporate-bond crisis that had detonated in late 2022 (we cover it in its own post: [the 2022 bond crisis](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/case-study-2022-bond-crisis-property-bank-contagion)). Developers had over-borrowed in the bond market (*trái phiếu doanh nghiệp*, corporate bonds), several large issuers had defaulted or restructured, presales had collapsed, and the legal/funding plumbing was still being repaired. A sector with a broken balance sheet and frozen sales cannot rally just because rates fell -- it has to *heal* first, and healing takes years. So the single biggest weight in the market sat out the recovery, which mechanically forced the gains to be narrow.

**Third, the genuine earnings and structural stories were concentrated in a few sectors.** Brokers (securities firms) are the highest-beta play on a market turn -- their revenue is brokerage commissions plus margin-lending interest plus proprietary trading gains, all of which boom when volumes and prices recover. Banks had survived the bond scare, were lending into a recovering economy, and traded cheap. And information technology -- above all FPT -- had a *structural*, non-cyclical growth engine in software exports and IT services that had nothing to do with the domestic credit cycle at all. (FPT's structural story gets its own deep dive: [the IT sector and FPT](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/information-technology-sector-vietnam-fpt-structural-growth).) Three real stories, one broken giant, and no flood to lift the rest -- that is the recipe for divergence.

![Line chart of VN-Index from the 2022 trough near 874 climbing to about 1130 end 2023 and about 1266 end 2024](/imgs/blogs/case-study-2023-2024-recovery-and-sector-divergence-2.png)

The figure traces the headline recovery so you can anchor the dates. The market bottomed near 874 in November 2022 -- the depth of the bond-crisis panic. It rebounded sharply into the end of 2022 (back above 1,000 by December as the worst-case fears faded), spent 2023 grinding higher to about 1,130 by year-end, and then advanced again through 2024 to close near 1,266. That is a clean, steady recovery line -- roughly +45% from trough to the end of 2024. Nothing in that single line tells you it was divergent. The divergence only shows up when you break the index into its sectors, which is exactly what we do next.

## The recovery setup: rate cuts, repaired confidence, no flood

Let us put dates and numbers on how the recovery actually started, because the *sequence* matters as much as the magnitude.

### The trough and the turn (late 2022 into 2023)

By November 2022, VN-Index had fallen to about 874 -- down roughly 40% from its early-2022 peak above 1,500. The proximate cause was the corporate-bond crisis: a regulatory crackdown on improperly issued developer bonds, high-profile arrests, and a wave of bond defaults and early redemptions that triggered a confidence collapse and a margin-call cascade (forced selling as leveraged positions got liquidated). The fear was contagion -- that bad property debt would sink the banks that had lent to developers and bought their bonds.

That worst-case contagion did not fully materialize, and once the market sensed that, the panic began to drain. But the *real* turn -- the thing that converted a relief bounce into a sustained recovery -- was monetary policy. Through 2023, the SBV cut its key policy rates repeatedly, taking the refinancing rate from 6.0% down to 4.5%. This was a deliberate pivot from defending the dong (*tỷ giá*, the exchange rate) and fighting imported inflation toward supporting domestic growth.

#### Worked example: why a rate cut lifts stock values even with no new buyers

A stock is worth the present value of its future earnings. Present value means you discount future money back to today using a rate -- the higher the rate, the less a future dollar is worth now. Suppose a company will earn a steady stream worth, in simplified terms, 25,400 dong (about \$1) per share per year forever. At a discount rate of 12%, that perpetual stream is worth about 25,400 / 0.12 ≈ 211,700 dong per share. Now the central bank cuts rates and the appropriate discount rate falls to 9%. The *same* earnings stream is now worth 25,400 / 0.09 ≈ 282,200 dong per share -- a jump of about 33% with no change in the business at all.

That is the pure mechanical lift from falling rates: the future is discounted less harshly, so today's value rises. **A rate cut can re-rate the whole market higher without a single new buyer showing up -- which is exactly why confidence can recover before liquidity does.** The 2023-2024 market got this re-rating engine; what it did not get was the second engine, the leverage flood.

### Repaired confidence versus a liquidity flood -- the crucial distinction

Here is where you must not confuse 2023-2024 with 2020-2021. In 2020-2021, the market had *both* engines running at full power. Rates were slashed in response to the pandemic *and* a generation of new retail accounts opened during lockdowns *and* margin debt exploded to record highs *and* reopening euphoria created a fear-of-missing-out feedback loop. Money flooded in with nowhere to go but stocks, and it bid up *everything* -- penny stocks, blue chips, real estate, brokers, steel, the lot. Correlations went to nearly 1.0; the whole market moved as one. That is a textbook liquidity bull.

In 2023-2024, only the first engine ran. Rates fell and confidence repaired -- but the new-account boom was over, margin debt grew only moderately (nothing like 2021's vertical ramp), and there was no euphoric reopening narrative. The economy was recovering, not booming. So the money that came back was *picky*. It went to brokers because brokers are the leveraged play on a volume recovery. It went to banks because banks were cheap survivors lending into a recovery. It went to IT because IT had real, structural, dollar-denominated export growth. It did *not* flood into property, because property's fundamentals were still broken. No flood, no broad lift -- just selective accumulation in the sectors that earned it.

## The sequence: brokers led 2023, then banks and IT led 2024

The single most useful thing to understand about this recovery is that leadership rotated in a clean, almost textbook sequence. It is the rotation we describe in the abstract in [sector rotation explained](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/sector-rotation-explained-leaders-and-laggards) and [early-cycle leaders](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/early-cycle-leaders-vietnam-brokers-banks-steel) -- and here it is in the flesh.

### Phase 1 -- brokers bounce first (2023)

When a beaten-down market turns, the securities-broker sector almost always moves first and hardest. The reason is structural: a broker's earnings are a *triple-geared* bet on the market itself. Brokers earn (1) commissions on trading volume, (2) interest on margin loans to clients, and (3) profit or loss on their own proprietary holdings. When the market recovers, all three improve at once -- volumes rise, margin balances grow, and the prop book gains. So a broker's earnings do not just recover; they *accelerate* off the bottom. That is what "highest beta" means in practice (we devote a full post to it: [the brokers sector](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/securities-brokers-sector-vietnam-highest-beta)).

In 2023, the broker sub-sector returned roughly +75% -- by far the strongest sector of the year. Names like SSI, VND, VCI, and HCM ran hard as volumes recovered from the dead lows of late 2022 and traders bet, correctly, that the rate-cut cycle would reignite turnover. This is the *early-cycle leadership* signature: brokers print the first, biggest move because they are the purest leveraged proxy for "the market is coming back."

There is a deeper reason brokers lead off the bottom, and it is worth making explicit because it generalizes to every market turn, not just Vietnam's. The broker move is *reflexive* -- it feeds on itself. As the market recovers, trading volumes rise; rising volumes lift broker commissions *and* let brokers extend more margin loans (more leverage in the system); more margin buying pushes prices higher still, which lifts the brokers' own proprietary holdings *and* draws in more traders, which lifts volumes again. Each link reinforces the next. That reflexive loop is why broker earnings do not recover linearly with the market -- they recover *convexly*, accelerating as the recovery gathers pace. A 20% rise in the index can drive a far larger rise in broker profits, which is precisely what justifies (and then over-justifies) a 75% sector move. The same reflexivity, run in reverse, is why brokers are the first sector to break when the cycle turns down: volumes dry up, margin gets called, prop books bleed, all at once. High beta means high in *both* directions -- you ride brokers off the bottom and you must be willing to *leave* them before the top, which is exactly the discipline the rotation framework is built to enforce.

A note on *when* in 2023 the broker move happened, because the timing is instructive. The bulk of the broker run came in the first three quarters of 2023, tracking the SBV rate cuts almost in real time -- each cut was a fresh signal that turnover would recover, and the brokers, as the leveraged proxy for turnover, priced it immediately. By the fourth quarter, with the rate-cut cycle essentially complete (the refinancing rate having settled at 4.5%) and the easy bounce already in the price, the broker trade was running out of *new* fuel. That is the early warning that an early-cycle leader is maturing: the catalyst that drove it (here, falling rates) has played out, and the next leg needs a *different* catalyst that favors a *different* sector. Which is exactly what 2024 delivered.

#### Worked example: a broker's triple-geared bounce in money terms

Suppose at the late-2022 trough you put 254 million dong (about \$10,000) into a broad broker position. Over 2023 the sector returns about +75%. Your position grows to about 254 × 1.75 ≈ 445 million dong -- a gain of about 191 million dong, or roughly \$7,500, in a single year. Compare that to the same 254 million dong placed in the *index*, which in 2023 rose from about 1,007 (end-2022) to about 1,130 (end-2023), roughly +12%: that index position would be worth about 284 million dong, a gain of about \$1,200.

The broker bet returned about six times the dollar gain of the index bet in the same year. **The reason is leverage to a single variable -- market turnover -- and that is exactly why brokers lead the *first* leg and why you want them when the turn is fresh, not after it is obvious.** (The same gearing works in reverse, which is why brokers also fall first and hardest when the cycle rolls over -- a point the rotation framework never lets you forget.)

### Phase 2 -- banks and IT lead 2024

By 2024 the early-cycle broker trade had largely played out -- the easy bounce was priced, and the broker sector cooled to roughly +12% for the year, now lagging the leaders. Leadership *handed off*. The new leaders were banks and information technology, and they led for two completely different reasons, which is itself instructive.

**Banks** led for a classic mid-cycle reason. By 2024 the economy was genuinely recovering, credit growth was picking up, the worst of the bond-crisis contagion fears had passed without the feared bank collapses, and banks were still trading cheap relative to their book value and earnings. As an economy expands, banks lend more, their net interest margin (*NIM* -- the spread between what they earn on loans and pay on deposits) stabilizes, and their cheap valuations re-rate upward. The banking sector returned roughly +26% in 2024, comfortably beating the index's roughly +12%. Banks are the heaviest weight in VN-Index, so when banks lead, the index has real support beneath it (their full role is in [the banking sector](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/banking-sector-vietnam-the-heartbeat-of-vn-index)).

**Information technology** led for a reason that had nothing to do with the domestic credit cycle at all. FPT -- the dominant Vietnamese IT and software-export company -- was compounding revenue and earnings at 20%+ rates on the back of a global software-services and offshore-development boom, much of it dollar-denominated and entirely insulated from Vietnam's property mess. The IT sub-sector returned roughly +80% in 2024, the single best-performing major sector of the recovery. This is *structural* growth, not cyclical recovery -- IT did not need the economy to recover; it had its own secular tailwind (we trace it fully in [the IT sector and FPT](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/information-technology-sector-vietnam-fpt-structural-growth)).

Notice what the two 2024 leaders teach you together: a divergent recovery can be led *simultaneously* by a cyclical-value story (banks, cheap and recovering) and a structural-growth story (IT, compounding regardless of the cycle). They have nothing in common except that both had a *real* reason to rise -- and in a selective market, a real reason is the only ticket to outperformance.

It is worth slowing down on *why the banks could lead in 2024 when they were at the center of the bond-crisis fear in 2022.* The bear case on banks during the crisis was contagion: banks had lent to developers and held developer bonds, so if property collapsed, the banks' loan books would be impaired and their capital eroded. That fear was reasonable in late 2022. But through 2023, the feared mass impairment *did not arrive* -- the SBV and regulators engineered a managed work-out (extending bond maturities, allowing restructuring, avoiding fire sales), bad-debt formation rose but stayed contained, and the banks kept earning. By 2024, the market could see that the banks had *survived the worst case*, were lending into a recovering economy, and were still trading at a discount to book value because the crisis-era fear had not fully washed out of their multiples. A survivor trading at a fear-discount, with earnings recovering, is a textbook re-rating candidate -- and that is exactly the +26% the banks delivered. The lesson embedded here is subtle: the sector that was the *epicenter of the prior crisis* can become a *leader of the recovery* once the feared catastrophe is confirmed not to have happened, precisely because its valuation is still carrying the discount of a catastrophe that never came.

The IT story rewards a different kind of attention, because it is the one leader whose move had *nothing to do with the rate cut or the domestic recovery at all.* FPT's growth engine is software services and offshore development sold to overseas clients -- Japanese, American, European enterprises buying Vietnamese engineering talent at a fraction of onshore cost. That revenue is largely dollar-denominated, growing with a *global* IT-services and digital-transformation cycle, and almost completely decoupled from Vietnamese interest rates, credit growth, or property. So while every other sector's 2024 fate was tied in some way to the domestic recovery, IT was running on a separate, structural, multi-year track. This is the single most important thing a sector investor can learn from FPT: in a divergent market, the most powerful position is often a sector with a growth driver that is *orthogonal* to whatever is gating the rest of the market. When property and the domestic credit cycle were the things holding everyone else back, IT simply did not care -- and that independence is exactly why it was the top performer. A structural grower does not wait for the cycle; it is its own cycle.

![Grouped bar chart comparing sector returns in 2023 versus 2024 for securities steel retail banks real estate and IT](/imgs/blogs/case-study-2023-2024-recovery-and-sector-divergence-3.png)

The figure puts the whole divergence on one canvas. In 2023, securities led at about +75%, with steel (+28%), retail (+18%), banks (+12%), and real estate (+5%) trailing. In 2024 the leadership board completely reshuffled: IT vaulted to about +80%, retail to about +35%, banks to about +28%, while securities cooled to about +12%, steel to about +8%, and real estate went *negative* at about -3%. Run your eye across the two clusters and you see the rotation as a physical fact: the 2023 leader (securities) is mid-pack in 2024, and a sector that barely registered in 2023 (IT) tops the board in 2024. Real estate is the only sector that fails to lead in *either* year -- the structural laggard.

## The laggards: property still healing, and the bond overhang

Every divergent recovery has its laggards, and in 2023-2024 the headline laggard was unmissable: **real estate**. It barely rose in 2023 (about +5%, less than the index) and actually *fell* in 2024 (about -3%, while the index rose 12%). For the single biggest emotional sector in Vietnamese retail investing to go *down* in a year the market went up is the clearest possible signal that this was no broad bull. Understanding *why* property could not participate is essential, because "buy the cheap laggard" is one of the most expensive instincts a beginner has.

### The bond overhang, explained simply

Vietnamese developers funded their land banks and projects substantially with corporate bonds -- short-dated, high-yield debt sold heavily to retail investors. When the 2022 crackdown and defaults froze that market, developers lost their primary funding channel *and* faced a wall of bond maturities they could not easily roll over (*roll over* = issue new debt to repay old debt). At the same time, presales -- the cash buyers pay upfront before a project is finished, which is how developers fund construction -- collapsed because buyer confidence had evaporated and mortgage rates had spiked. So developers were simultaneously cut off from bond funding *and* from presale funding, while owing large maturities. (The full property-sector mechanics -- land banks, presales, the bond reliance -- are in [the real estate sector](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/real-estate-sector-vietnam-land-banks-presales-bonds).)

A company in that position cannot re-rate just because the central bank cut rates. Lower rates *help* at the margin -- cheaper mortgages eventually, a bit of breathing room on refinancing -- but they do not conjure presale cash or repair a broken balance sheet overnight. The sector needed *time*: time for bond maturities to be restructured, for the legal framework to be repaired, for buyer confidence to slowly return, for inventory to clear. Time is not something a rate cut provides. So while brokers and banks and IT were running, property sat in the corner, healing, and its stocks went nowhere or fell.

### The value-trap problem

Here is the part that catches beginners. As property stocks stayed cheap or got cheaper through 2023-2024, their valuation multiples looked irresistible. Many large developers traded at a price-to-book ratio (*P/B* -- market price divided by net asset value per share) around 1.0x or even below -- meaning the market valued the company at roughly the stated accounting value of its land and buildings, or less. To a naive value investor, that screams "bargain": *you are buying the assets at cost!*

But a low P/B is only a bargain if the book value is *real* and the company can *earn* on those assets. In a sector where the land bank's true clearing value is uncertain (could those projects actually be sold, and at what price?), where presales are frozen, and where the funding to finish projects is in doubt, the "book value" is exactly the number you cannot trust. The stock is cheap *because the market doubts the book*. That is the definition of a value trap: a stock that looks cheap on a backward-looking multiple but is cheap *for a reason* the multiple does not capture. (We unpack this exact failure mode in [valuation by sector](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/valuation-by-sector-pe-pb-nav-ev-ebitda).)

#### Worked example: the laggard trap -- buying "cheap" property versus leading banks/IT in 2024

Start 2024 with 254 million dong (about \$10,000). Investor C is a deep-value bargain hunter: "property is at 1.0x book, banks are at 1.5x book, IT is at 5x book -- I'll buy the cheapest." She buys property. Over 2024, property returns about -3%, so her 254 million dong becomes about 246 million dong -- a *loss* of about 8 million dong, roughly \$300, in a rising market.

Investor D ignores the multiple and buys what is *leading*: an even split of banks (+26%) and IT (+80%), a blended +53%. Her 254 million dong becomes about 389 million dong -- a gain of about 135 million dong, roughly \$5,300.

The "expensive" leaders beat the "cheap" laggard by about 143 million dong -- about \$5,600 -- in a single year. **Cheap stayed cheap because the cheapness was *priced risk*, not a *gift*; the higher multiples on banks and IT reflected real, improving earnings that the property multiple could not match.** In a divergent recovery, the laggard's low valuation is a warning label, not a discount sticker.

## The foreign paradox: a record sell, and the index rose anyway

Now to the fact that opened this post -- the one that most violates a beginner's intuition. In 2024, foreign investors net-sold roughly 92.5 trillion dong of Vietnamese stocks (about \$3.6bn) -- the largest annual foreign outflow in the market's history -- and VN-Index *rose* about 12% across the same year. Foreign selling, the thing retail investors are taught to fear above all else, simply did not sink the market. To understand why, you need to understand who was on the other side of every one of those sell tickets.

### Every sale has a buyer

This sounds obvious but it is the entire resolution. When a foreign fund net-sells 92.5 trillion dong of stock over a year, that stock does not vanish -- *someone bought all of it*. In 2024, that someone was overwhelmingly **domestic retail and domestic institutions**. The Vietnamese individual investor base had grown enormously since the 2020-2021 account boom, sat on substantial cash, was being pushed out of low-yielding bank deposits by the rate cuts, and was increasingly confident in the recovery. So as foreigners handed over their shares, domestic buyers took them, at prices that *rose*. The composition of ownership shifted -- more domestic, less foreign -- but the *price* is set at the margin by whoever is more eager, and through 2024 the domestic buyer was more eager than the foreign seller was desperate.

#### Worked example: quantifying the offset -- who absorbed 92.5 trillion dong?

For VN-Index to *rise* about 12% while foreigners net-sold about 92.5 trillion dong (about \$3.6bn), domestic investors did not merely match the selling -- they out-bid it. Net-net, domestic flows had to absorb the *entire* 92.5 trillion dong of foreign supply *and* provide the marginal extra demand that pushed prices up 12%. So the domestic net *buy* was at least the roughly 92.5 trillion dong (about \$3.6bn) the foreigners sold, plus enough additional appetite to lift the index.

Put it in a single retail account's terms. If the average active domestic account deployed an extra, say, 25.4 million dong (about \$1,000) of net new buying into stocks over the year, it would take only about 3.6 million such accounts -- well within Vietnam's multi-million-account retail base -- to absorb the entire foreign exit. **The "record foreign sell" was real, but it was a transfer of ownership from foreign to domestic hands, not a withdrawal of money from the market -- and a transfer at *rising* prices is, definitionally, the buyers winning.** The lesson: foreign flow tells you about *one* group of participants, not about the *price*, which is set by the balance of *all* participants.

### Why foreigners were selling (and why it didn't matter for price)

It helps to know the foreigners were not selling because they had uniquely bad information about Vietnam. The 2024 outflow had largely structural and global drivers: a strong US dollar and high US interest rates made emerging markets less attractive across the board (capital rotated *home* to dollar assets), Vietnam's frontier-market classification and the perennial wait for an emerging-market upgrade kept some big funds on the sidelines, the dong was under depreciation pressure (which erodes a foreign investor's returns when converted back to dollars), and some funds were simply rebalancing or facing redemptions at home. None of that is a verdict on Vietnamese corporate earnings -- it is mostly *global macro and currency*, not *Vietnamese fundamentals*. (The full mechanics of foreign flows, ETF effects, and the index-inclusion story are in [foreign flows and the index effect](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/foreign-flows-etfs-and-the-index-effect-vietnam).)

That distinction is exactly why the selling did not sink the market: the domestic buyer, focused on *domestic earnings and the rate cut*, correctly judged that the foreign exit was about *the dollar and currency risk*, not about whether FPT or the banks were making more money. Two groups, two different scorecards, and in 2024 the larger, more confident group set the price.

There is one more layer worth adding, because it explains *which stocks* the foreign selling hit and connects back to the divergence theme. Foreign investors are concentrated in the large, liquid, index-heavy names -- the blue chips that are easy to buy and sell in size and that sit in the ETFs and the foreign-accessible baskets. So the foreign selling fell disproportionately on those big names, including some banks and large caps. But the *domestic* buyer was not selling those names back to the foreigners at lower prices; the domestic base was *accumulating* across the leading sectors and chasing the structural and mid-cap stories. The result was a market where foreign supply was being soaked up at rising prices even in the names foreigners were exiting, while the domestically-favored leaders (IT especially, where FPT's run was substantially a domestic-conviction story) ran hardest of all. The foreign exit and the sector divergence were not separate phenomena -- they were the *same* phenomenon viewed from two angles: a market whose price was being set by a domestic buyer with a domestic scorecard, rotating into the sectors *that buyer* believed in, regardless of what the departing foreign money thought.

#### Worked example: would following the foreigners have worked?

Suppose at the start of 2024 you adopted the rule "do what the foreigners do" and sold your stocks to match their net selling, sitting in cash (or a bank deposit yielding, after the cuts, only a few percent) for the year. The opportunity cost is stark. A 254-million-dong (about \$10,000) portfolio that followed the foreigners into cash earned essentially the deposit rate -- call it about 4%, ending around 264 million dong, a gain of about \$400. The same 254 million dong simply *held in the index* earned about +12%, ending around 284 million dong (about \$1,200), and *held in the leaders* (banks and IT) earned about +53%, ending around 389 million dong (about \$5,300). Following the foreigners out cost the index-follower about \$800 of forgone gain and cost the leader-follower nearly \$5,000. **The "smart money" foreign flow was a *terrible* trading signal in 2024 precisely because it was driven by currency and global allocation, not by a bearish view on Vietnamese earnings -- and price follows earnings and domestic demand, not the passport of the seller.**

The figure makes the paradox concrete. The bars are annual foreign net flow: a positive net buy of about +27 trillion dong in 2022, a net *sell* of about -23 trillion in 2023, and a record net sell of about -92.5 trillion in 2024 -- a clear, worsening outflow. The overlaid line is VN-Index at each year-end: about 1,007, then 1,130, then 1,266 -- rising the whole time. The two series go in *opposite directions* in 2023 and 2024: foreigners sold harder and the index climbed higher. If foreign flow drove price, these lines would mirror each other. They don't, because domestic flow was the dominant force, and that single mismatch is the most important thing a flow-watching retail investor can internalize.

![Step line chart of the SBV refinancing rate falling from 6.0 percent in 2022 to 4.5 percent in 2023 and held in 2024](/imgs/blogs/case-study-2023-2024-recovery-and-sector-divergence-5.png)

And here is the fuel underneath all of it. The figure steps the SBV refinancing rate down from 6.0% in 2022 to 4.5% in 2023, held flat at 4.5% through 2024. That single staircase is the engine of the whole recovery: it re-rated the discount applied to every future earnings stream (the perpetuity example from earlier), it pushed savers out of newly unattractive bank deposits and toward stocks, and it gave the domestic buyer both the *reason* and the *cheaper money* to absorb the foreign selling. The step shape matters too -- the cuts happened in 2023 (driving that year's bounce), and the *hold* through 2024 kept the supportive backdrop in place while leadership rotated to banks and IT. Rates were the tide that came in once; the sectors were the speedboats that did the rest.

## Common misconceptions

The 2023-2024 recovery is a near-perfect machine for dismantling the three errors that cost beginners the most money in a recovering market. Each one is "obvious," and each one is wrong in a way this period proves with numbers.

**Misconception 1: "A recovery lifts all sectors -- so just buy the cheapest one and wait."** This is the liquidity-bull mental model applied to a divergent recovery, and it is exactly backwards. In 2024, the index rose about 12% while real estate *fell* about 3% -- a 15-point gap between the benchmark and its laggard. A recovery does *not* lift all sectors when it is divergent; it lifts the sectors with a real reason to rise and leaves the broken ones behind. The corrective number: an 80-point spread between the best major sector (IT, about +80%) and the worst (real estate, about -3%) in the *same* rising year. "Buy anything, it'll come back" is a 2021 strategy that would have lost you money in property in 2024.

**Misconception 2: "Foreign selling sinks the market -- when they sell, get out."** The single cleanest refutation in the whole series. In 2024 foreigners net-sold a *record* roughly 92.5 trillion dong (about \$3.6bn) and the index rose about 12%. Foreign flow is *one* group's behavior, not the price; the price is the balance of all participants, and in 2024 the domestic buyer dominated. An investor who sold every time the foreigners sold would have spent the entire best year of the recovery on the sidelines. Watch foreign flow for *what it tells you about currency and global risk appetite*, never as a sell signal for the index.

**Misconception 3: "Buy the cheapest laggard -- it has the most room to recover."** The value trap, dressed up as common sense. Property at about 1.0x book in 2024 looked like the bargain of the decade and returned about -3%, while "expensive" banks (about 1.5x book) and IT (about 5x book) returned about +26% and +80%. Cheap is not the same as undervalued: a stock can be cheap because the market correctly doubts its book value, its earnings, or its funding. In a divergent recovery the laggard is usually a laggard *for a reason the multiple does not show*, and the leaders' higher multiples are usually *earned* by real, improving earnings. The corrective rule: in a divergent market, buy *strength with a reason*, not *cheapness without one*.

**Misconception 4: "The 2021 boom is the template -- this'll be just like that."** The most dangerous anchor of all, because the 2021 playbook (buy everything, max margin, chase the hottest penny stock) was *catastrophically* wrong for 2023-2024. 2021 was a twin-engine liquidity bull (rate cuts *plus* a leverage flood) where correlation was near 1.0 and beta was the trade. 2023-2024 was a single-engine divergent recovery (rate cuts *without* the flood) where correlation collapsed and selection was the trade. Same direction on the index, opposite optimal strategy. Knowing which kind of market you are in -- by watching whether margin debt is *flooding* or merely *recovering* -- is the meta-skill this case study teaches.

## What the playbook would have done

Now let us run the recovery through the actual frameworks from the rest of this series and see how they would have positioned an investor in real time. This is the payoff: not "with hindsight you should have bought IT," but "here is the *repeatable process* that would have put you in the leaders and out of the laggard, using signals available at the time."

### Step 1 -- read the regime: divergent, not broad

The first move is to identify *which kind of recovery* you are in, because it dictates everything else. The tell is liquidity. Through 2023, the playbook investor watches margin balances and overall turnover: are they *flooding* (2021-style vertical ramp) or merely *recovering off the lows*? They were recovering, not flooding -- margin grew, but nothing like the 2021 explosion. That single read flips the strategy from "buy beta, own the index" to "this is a stock-picker's recovery, *selection matters, lead with relative strength.*" Getting this regime call right at the start is 80% of the outcome.

### Step 2 -- rotate early-cycle into brokers (2023)

With the regime read as a divergent recovery off a deep low, the rotation framework points straight at the early-cycle leader: brokers. The signal is the combination of (a) the index trend turning up off the trough, (b) volumes recovering, and (c) brokers showing the strongest relative strength as the market turns. The playbook investor concentrates in brokers for the first leg of 2023 and rides the roughly +75% move. This is not a guess -- it is the textbook early-cycle position (the brokers-first logic is laid out in [early-cycle leaders](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/early-cycle-leaders-vietnam-brokers-banks-steel)).

### Step 3 -- watch for the relative-strength handoff (late 2023 into 2024)

The critical, hard-to-do move is *getting out of the prior winner*. By late 2023, the broker trade has run a long way and is getting crowded. The relative-strength framework gives the exit-and-rotate signal: brokers' RS line *peaks and rolls over* even as their price is still elevated, while banks' and IT's RS lines *start rising*. This RS divergence -- the old leader losing relative ground while new leaders gain it -- is the handoff tell, and it appears *weeks before* the absolute price ranking makes the rotation obvious. (The full mechanics of reading this handoff are in [relative strength](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/relative-strength-reading-which-sector-leads-vn-index).)

#### Worked example: the RS-driven rotation versus staying in the prior winner

Investor E rides the broker bounce in 2023 (about +75%) and then *stays* in brokers through 2024 out of loyalty to the winner. Brokers return only about +12% in 2024, so a 254-million-dong position (about \$10,000) entering 2024 grows to only about 284 million dong -- a gain of about \$1,200, barely matching the index.

Investor F rides the same broker bounce but *acts on the RS handoff* in late 2023, rotating into the new leaders (an even split of banks +26% and IT +80%, blended +53%). His 254 million dong becomes about 389 million dong -- a gain of about 135 million dong, roughly \$5,300.

The rotation earned Investor F about 105 million dong -- about \$4,100 -- more than staying in the prior winner, on the *same* starting capital and the *same* 2023 bounce. **The hardest discipline in rotation is selling a winner that is still going up in price but is losing the relative race; RS gives you the signal to do it before the absolute price ranking catches up.** Loyalty to last year's leader is one of the most expensive habits in a divergent market.

### Step 4 -- avoid the property value trap

Throughout 2023-2024, the playbook investor stays *out* of real estate despite its cheapness, because the framework's rule is *buy improving relative strength, not low valuation*. Property's RS line was flat-to-falling the entire period -- it was a persistent laggard, never showing the rising RS that signals real accumulation. The low P/B was not a buy signal; it was the market correctly pricing broken fundamentals. The playbook's discipline -- *only buy a laggard once its relative strength is actually turning up, never just because it is cheap* -- kept the investor out of the one large sector that lost money in a rising market.

### Step 5 -- ignore the foreign-sell noise (hold through 2024)

Finally, when the foreign-sell headlines screamed "record outflow" through 2024, the playbook investor does *not* panic-sell. The framework treats foreign flow as a *currency-and-global-risk* signal, not a price signal, and notes that domestic flow was absorbing everything at rising prices. Relative strength in banks and IT kept *rising* through the foreign selling -- the leaders were leading regardless of who was selling them. The discipline of weighting the *price and RS evidence* over the *flow headline* kept the investor invested in the leaders through the single best year of the recovery.

![Before and after comparison of the 2021 broad liquidity bull versus the 2023 to 2024 selective divergent recovery](/imgs/blogs/case-study-2023-2024-recovery-and-sector-divergence-6.png)

The figure contrasts the two recovery types side by side, because the whole playbook hinges on telling them apart. On the left, the 2021 broad bull: twin engines (rate cuts *plus* a leverage flood), correlation near 1.0, everything rises together, and the right move is *own the index / buy beta* -- selection barely matters. On the right, the 2023-2024 selective recovery: one engine (rate cuts, *no* flood), correlation collapsed, winners and laggards diverge sharply, and the right move is *pick the leading sector or miss out* -- selection is everything. The same rising index demands opposite strategies depending on which side of this picture you are on, and reading the liquidity backdrop is how you know which side you are on *in real time*.

## The lessons: selection beats beta, RS is king, mind the laggard trap

Step back from the dates and numbers and four durable lessons remain -- the ones worth carrying into the *next* recovery, whenever it comes.

**Lesson 1 -- in a divergent market, selection beats beta.** When the index rises because a few sectors run while others stall, owning the index gets you the *average* -- and the average badly understates what the leaders did and overstates what you would have made in the laggards. In 2024 the index rose 12%; IT rose 80% and property fell 3%. The gap between "buy the index" and "buy the leader" was enormous, and it was *all* selection. The first question in any recovery is therefore *not* "is the market going up?" but "is this broad or divergent?" -- because only the answer to the second question tells you whether selection or beta is the trade.

**Lesson 2 -- relative strength is king for navigating the rotation.** Every correct move in the playbook above -- into brokers, then the handoff to banks and IT, the avoidance of property, the holding through foreign selling -- was readable in relative strength. RS strips out the index move and shows you which sector is *actually* winning the flow, and crucially it shows the *handoff* (old leader's RS rolling over, new leaders' RS rising) before the absolute price ranking does. In a divergent recovery, RS is not one tool among many; it is the primary instrument, because it directly measures the one thing that matters -- *who is leading.*

**Lesson 3 -- mind the laggard trap.** The cheapest sector is cheap for a reason, and in a divergent recovery that reason is usually *real impairment the multiple cannot see*. Property at 1.0x book in 2024 was not a bargain; it was a market correctly doubting the book. Never buy a laggard for its low valuation alone -- wait for its *relative strength to turn up*, which signals that real money has decided the impairment is healing. Cheapness is a reason to *watch*, never a reason to *buy*.

**Lesson 4 -- domestic flow can fully offset foreign flow, so flow is not price.** The record 2024 foreign sell (about 92.5 trillion dong, about \$3.6bn) taught the whole market that foreign flow is *one group's* behavior, not the price. When the domestic base is large, cash-rich, and confident, it can absorb any foreign exit *and* push prices higher. Read foreign flow for what it genuinely signals -- currency risk, global risk appetite, the EM-classification story -- but never mistake it for a verdict on the index. In 2024, the foreigners left and the index had its best year of the recovery; both were true because the buyers were domestic and the buyers won.

![Matrix of four lessons selection beats beta RS is king laggard trap domestic offsets foreign with what happened and the lesson](/imgs/blogs/case-study-2023-2024-recovery-and-sector-divergence-7.png)

The figure consolidates the four lessons into a what-happened / what-to-learn grid so the whole case study collapses into one reference card. *Selection beats beta*: IT +80% versus property -3% in the same +12% index year -- so in a divergent market, the sector call *is* the return. *RS is king*: the broker-to-banks/IT handoff showed up in relative strength first -- so rank and rotate on RS, not on price color. *Laggard trap*: property at 1.0x book still fell -- so cheap is a warning, not a buy. *Domestic offsets foreign*: a record 92.5-trillion-dong foreign sell, index still up 12% -- so flow is one group's behavior, never the price. Read the right column as the four rules you keep; read the left as the 2023-2024 proof that they work.

To close where we began: the most counterintuitive image of the whole recovery -- foreigners dumping a record amount of stock while the index climbed and IT investors made 80% -- is not a paradox once you hold the two ideas this case study is built on. *This was a divergent recovery, not a liquidity bull,* so selection beat beta; and *flow is one group's behavior, not the price,* so a record foreign sell could coexist with a rising market that domestic buyers were powering. Hold those two ideas and 2023-2024 stops being a puzzle and becomes a template -- the clearest example in modern VN-Index history of a market where being *right about the sector* mattered more than being right about the market.
