> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Sectors take turns leading the market because the economy moves in a cycle, and each phase of that cycle rewards a different group of industries. Adapt the classic "investment clock" to Vietnam, where the cycle is driven by credit growth, the State Bank's credit ceiling and policy rate, and FDI/exports, and you can locate where you are with five free prints.
>
> - The four phases are **recovery, expansion, overheat, slowdown**. Each one hands leadership to a different sector group as earnings and interest rates take turns being the driver.
> - In Vietnam the clock turns on **credit and rates**, not just GDP: the SBV cuts the policy rate and lifts the credit ceiling, credit accelerates, property and brokers boom, CPI eventually forces hikes, and the cycle rolls over.
> - The phase map for VN-Index: **recovery -> banks, brokers, steel; expansion -> property, industrials, materials; overheat -> energy, materials; slowdown -> utilities, staples, healthcare.**
> - The one number to remember: in **2020-2022** Vietnam made one full trip around the clock, from a COVID trough with the policy rate cut to a record-low **4.0%**, to a VN-Index peak near **1,500**, to a Nov-2022 trough near **874** after rates were hiked back to **6.0%**.

In March 2020 the VN-Index, the main stock index on the Ho Chi Minh exchange (HOSE), fell to around 660 points. COVID had just shut the world down, factories in the south were idle, and nobody wanted to own a Vietnamese stock. The State Bank of Vietnam (SBV, the central bank) reacted the way central banks do: it slashed its policy rate to a record-low 4.0% and quietly let banks lend more by loosening the *room tin dung* — the annual credit ceiling each bank is allowed to grow its loan book by. Money got cheap. Deposit rates fell so far that a new generation of retail investors opened brokerage accounts and bought stocks instead. Over the next twenty-two months the index more than doubled, peaking near 1,500 in January 2022.

Then the clock kept turning. Inflation crept up, the US Federal Reserve hiked aggressively, the dong came under pressure, and a corporate-bond scandal blew open a hole in the property sector. The SBV reversed course in September and October 2022, hiking the policy rate back to 6.0% in a matter of weeks. The same cheap money that had inflated the boom drained out. By November 2022 the index had collapsed to an intraday trough near 874 — a 42% fall from the peak in eleven months. One round trip around the clock, start to finish, in under three years.

Here is the part most beginners miss. Through that entire round trip, **the sectors that led changed at every turn.** On the way up, banks and brokers and steel went first, then property and construction, then the speculative theme stocks. On the way down, the high-beta names that led the boom fell hardest and first, while a handful of boring utilities and consumer-staples names barely moved. If you had owned the right sector at each phase, you made the round trip look easy. If you owned last phase's leader, you got run over. This post is about reading the clock so you are early, not late.

## Foundations: what the business cycle is and why the clock works

Before we get to Vietnam-specific dials, we need the engine underneath. The **business cycle** is the repeating rhythm of an economy speeding up and slowing down. It is not a calendar — it does not arrive every four years on schedule — but it does move through the same four phases in the same order, because the same forces (demand, capacity, inflation, and the central bank's reaction) interact the same way each time.

Think of the economy like a car on a long road with a speed limit. **Recovery** is when the car has been stopped (a recession), the driver eases off the brake, and it starts to roll forward — slow at first, but accelerating. **Expansion** is cruising comfortably below the speed limit: fast, smooth, everyone relaxed. **Overheat** is when the car pushes past the speed limit — too fast, the engine (inflation) running hot, and a cop (the central bank) about to pull it over. **Slowdown** is the driver hitting the brakes hard, the car decelerating, sometimes all the way back to a stop. Then the cycle begins again.

### The four phases, defined

Let me pin each phase to what is actually happening in the real economy, because the sector map flows directly from these definitions.

- **Recovery (early cycle).** Growth is low but turning up from a bottom. Inflation is still low and often falling, because there is slack everywhere — idle factories, unemployed workers, unsold inventory. The central bank is *cutting* rates or holding them low to stimulate. This is the phase of cheap money and improving growth at the same time — the single best combination for stocks.
- **Expansion (mid cycle).** Growth is now solid and self-sustaining. Companies hire, invest, and build. Inflation is creeping up but still tame. The central bank is neutral — done cutting, not yet hiking. This is the longest phase and the most comfortable.
- **Overheat (late cycle).** Growth is still positive but decelerating, while inflation is now the problem. Capacity is tight: factories are full, labor is scarce, commodity prices rise. The central bank shifts to *hiking* rates to cool things down. Earnings are still good but the discount rate (more on that below) is rising against them.
- **Slowdown (recession).** Growth turns negative or near-zero, inflation finally rolls over, and the central bank — having hiked too far — starts to ease again at the end. This is the painful phase, but it is also where the next recovery is seeded.

### Why earnings and rates take turns being the driver

A stock's price is, at its core, the present value of the company's future profits. Two things move that value: **how big the future profits are** (earnings), and **how much you discount them** to today (the interest rate, the "discount rate"). The investment clock works because these two forces peak at *different* times in the cycle.

In **recovery**, earnings are still depressed but rising fast, and rates are low — both forces push prices up. In **expansion**, earnings are the main driver; rates are stable. In **overheat**, earnings are still high but rates are now rising fast, fighting the earnings — the two forces pull against each other, which is why late-cycle rallies feel grudging. In **slowdown**, earnings collapse and that dominates — even when the central bank cuts rates, falling profits win, and prices fall. The whole point of sector rotation is that different industries are sensitive to *different* forces — some live or die on rates, others on the earnings cycle — so as the dominant force rotates, so does sector leadership.

It is worth slowing down on *why* sectors differ in their sensitivity, because this is the deep reason the clock works rather than a coincidence. A company's earnings sensitivity to the cycle is mostly a function of two things: how much of its cost base is fixed versus variable, and how discretionary its product is. A steel mill has enormous fixed costs — the blast furnace runs whether orders are high or low — so when demand swings, its profits swing far more, a property called *operating leverage*. When steel demand rises 10% in an expansion, steel profits might rise 40%; when demand falls 10% in a slowdown, profits can vanish. A water utility is the opposite: people use roughly the same amount of water in a boom or a bust, so its earnings barely move. Now layer on *financial* leverage — how much debt the company carries. A property developer funded heavily with bank loans and corporate bonds has its earnings amplified by both operating *and* financial leverage, which is why property is the most violent sector in both directions. A bank's earnings are sensitive to a third thing entirely — the interest-rate spread and the credit cycle — which is why financials lead the *rate-driven* recovery rather than the *demand-driven* expansion. Sort every sector by these sensitivities and the clock's phase map falls out almost mechanically: rate-sensitive financials lead when rates drive the market, demand-sensitive cyclicals lead when earnings drive it, and cycle-proof defensives lead when nothing good is driving anything.

There is one more force worth naming, because it is especially loud in Vietnam: **liquidity**, meaning the sheer quantity of money sloshing around looking for a home. In a developed market, the discount-rate and earnings channels mostly capture what matters. In a retail-dominated, bank-financed market like Vietnam, the *flow* of money — out of bank deposits and into brokerage accounts when rates fall — is a third driver that can lift the whole market regardless of any single company's earnings. When the SBV cuts rates and loosens credit, the resulting wall of liquidity does not politely wait for earnings to justify higher prices; it bids up the highest-beta, most-talked-about sectors first. This is why Vietnamese recoveries feel less like a careful re-rating and more like a flood, and why the brokers — the most direct beneficiaries of trading-volume and margin-lending booms — are so often the explosive early leader.

### What the "investment clock" means

The **investment clock** is a single picture that arranges the four phases as the four quadrants of a clock face, and writes the leading sectors inside each quadrant. The original version was popularized by Merrill Lynch in the 2000s for developed markets, sorting sectors by their sensitivity to growth and inflation. We are going to keep the four-quadrant skeleton but rewire the *engine* for Vietnam, because what turns Vietnam's clock is not quite the same set of dials that turns the US clock. In the US the dominant dial is the Fed's reaction to inflation and the labor market. In Vietnam, as we will see, the dominant dial is **credit** — specifically how much the SBV lets the banking system lend.

If you remember only one sentence from this section: **the cycle moves earnings and rates in a fixed order, and each sector is tuned to a different one of those forces, so leadership rotates predictably as the cycle turns.**

## The Vietnamese dials: what actually moves the cycle here

Now the part that makes this the *Vietnamese* edition. If you tried to read Vietnam's cycle using the same dashboard a US investor uses — non-farm payrolls, core PCE, the Fed funds rate — you would be looking at the wrong instruments. Vietnam's cycle is driven by a smaller, more concentrated set of dials, and the most important one is credit.

### Dial 1: credit growth and the credit ceiling (*room tin dung*)

Vietnam runs a bank-dominated financial system. There is no deep corporate-bond market for most companies and no broad equity-issuance culture; when a Vietnamese business or homebuyer needs money, it borrows from a bank. That makes the *quantity* of bank lending the master variable for the whole economy. When credit grows fast, property developers build, consumers spend, and corporate earnings rise. When credit dries up, everything seizes.

Here is the uniquely Vietnamese twist: the SBV does not just set the price of credit (the interest rate), it sets the **quantity** directly. Every year the central bank hands each commercial bank an annual loan-growth quota — the *room tin dung*, or credit ceiling. A bank with a 14% room is allowed to grow its loan book by at most 14% that year, full stop, regardless of how many good borrowers walk in the door. The SBV adds the bank-by-bank quotas to a system-wide target (around 14-16% in recent years), and adjusts it through the year. **This is a throttle on the entire economy's earnings.** When the SBV is generous with credit room, the engine revs; when it tightens room to defend the currency or fight inflation, the engine stalls.

Why does Vietnam use a quota at all, when most central banks just move the price of money and let the market sort out the quantity? Two reasons, both worth understanding because they tell you how the throttle behaves. First, Vietnam's interest-rate transmission is weak: because the financial system is shallow and bank-dominated, moving the policy rate alone does not reliably control how much banks lend, so the SBV controls quantity directly as a blunt but effective backstop. Second, the SBV uses the ceiling as a *steering wheel* as well as a brake — it can hand bigger quotas to well-capitalized banks and smaller ones to weak banks, and it can lean against speculative lending (into property, say) by tightening room for property-heavy lenders. The practical consequence for an investor: the credit ceiling is not just a macro dial, it is a *policy preference* signal. When the SBV publicly nudges credit *toward* manufacturing and *away* from property, that is a direct sector signal you can trade — it tells you which sector's earnings the regulator is about to feed and which it is about to starve.

The mechanics of how this ceiling propagates into the real economy are worth tracing once, because the whole clock runs through it. The SBV sets the system credit target in early in the year. Banks, eager to use their full quota (lending is how they make money), front-load lending when they have room and slam the brakes when they hit the cap — which is why you sometimes see credit grind to a halt in the fourth quarter of a hot year as banks run out of room and wait for the next year's allocation. That lending becomes deposits in *other* banks (one bank's loan is another's deposit), which expands the money supply through the familiar money-multiplier mechanism. The new money funds property purchases, factory expansions, and consumer spending — which becomes the revenue and earnings of the listed companies you might own. Squeeze the ceiling and you squeeze the top of this whole chain; loosen it and the chain fills with money. *Nothing in the Vietnamese cycle is upstream of the credit ceiling.*

#### Worked example: the credit ceiling caps a bank's earnings

Suppose a mid-sized Vietnamese bank starts the year with a loan book of **400 trillion VND** (about \$15.7bn at \$1 = 25,400 VND). The SBV grants it a 14% credit room for the year. The most the bank can grow loans is:

```
400 trillion x 14% = 56 trillion VND of new loans (~$2.2bn)
```

If the bank's net interest margin (NIM, the spread it earns between lending and deposit rates) is 3.5%, that 56 trillion of new loans adds at most:

```
56 trillion x 3.5% = ~1.96 trillion VND of new annual interest income (~$77mn)
```

Now suppose mid-year the SBV, worried about the dong, cuts the system credit target and the bank's effective room falls to 10%. New loans are capped at 40 trillion instead of 56 trillion — 16 trillion VND (~\$630mn) of lending, and the earnings it would have produced, simply does not happen. The bank did nothing wrong; the regulator turned the throttle. **The takeaway: in Vietnam, a bank's earnings growth is rationed by a policy quota, so the credit ceiling is a sector-wide earnings dial that you can watch directly.**

![Vietnam credit growth 2018 to 2025 bar chart](/imgs/blogs/business-cycle-investment-clock-vietnam-5.png)

The chart shows why this matters: credit growth ran a tight 12-15% band for years, dipped to 12.2% in the COVID-slowed 2020, and was pushed toward an unusually loose ~18% in 2025 as the government leaned on credit to hit an 8%+ GDP target. Every one of those moves was a *policy decision*, not a market outcome — which is exactly why credit growth is the first dial to watch.

### Dial 2: the SBV policy rate

The SBV's main policy rate is the **refinancing rate**, the rate at which it lends to commercial banks. It is the closest Vietnamese equivalent to the Fed funds rate, and it sets the floor under deposit and lending rates across the system. When the SBV cuts the refinancing rate, banks can fund themselves more cheaply, deposit rates fall, and two things happen at once: borrowing gets cheaper (good for property and any leveraged business), and cash in the bank earns less, which pushes savers toward stocks.

That second channel — savings flowing out of low-yielding deposits and into the stock market — is enormous in Vietnam, where the retail investor dominates daily trading. When a one-year deposit yields 9%, why bother with the volatility of stocks? When it yields 5%, suddenly stocks look attractive, and the retail money floods in. The deposit rate is the *opportunity cost* of owning equities, and the policy rate sets the deposit rate.

It helps to think of the deposit rate as the *hurdle* every stock has to clear. A Vietnamese saver with money in the bank earning a risk-free 8% will only move into stocks if she expects meaningfully more than 8% — that 8% is the bar. Drop the deposit rate to 5% and the bar drops with it: now a stock yielding a 6% dividend, or offering plausible double-digit upside, clears the hurdle easily, and the saver rotates. Multiply that decision across millions of Vietnamese households — who hold an enormous share of national wealth in bank deposits and gold rather than financial assets — and you get the tidal flows that move VN-Index. The 2020-21 boom was, at its core, this rotation: deposit rates fell so far that parking money in the bank felt like losing to inflation, and a generation discovered the brokerage account. The reverse happened in late 2022, when deposit rates spiked back toward 9-10% and the same households yanked money *out* of stocks and back into the safety of high-yield deposits, draining the market of its fuel. The policy rate, through the deposit rate, is therefore not just a discounting input — it is a valve controlling the single biggest flow of money into and out of Vietnamese equities.

There is a subtlety here that catches people out: the SBV's *headline* refinancing rate and the *market* deposit and lending rates do not always move one-for-one. In stress periods, banks compete for deposits and market rates can spike above what the policy rate would imply — as they did in the late-2022 liquidity crunch, when 12-month deposit rates touched double digits even though the refinancing rate was "only" 6.0%. So watch both: the policy rate tells you the SBV's *intent*, while the market deposit rate tells you what savers are actually being offered, and therefore what the real hurdle for stocks is at that moment.

![SBV refinancing policy rate 2018 to 2025 step chart](/imgs/blogs/business-cycle-investment-clock-vietnam-3.png)

The step chart tells the whole policy story of the last cycle. The refinancing rate sat at 6.25% in 2018, was cut hard through 2020 to a record-low 4.0% (the easy-money fuel for the 2020-21 boom), spiked back to 6.0% in the September-October 2022 emergency hikes (the trigger for the bust), then was cut to 4.5% through 2023 to engineer the recovery, where it has held. **Read this chart top-to-bottom and you are reading the engine that turns Vietnam's clock.**

#### Worked example: a rate cut re-rates a stock's fair price

Here is the mechanism that connects the policy rate directly to share prices. A stock's fair price-to-earnings ratio (P/E) rises when the discount rate falls, because future earnings are worth more in today's money. A rough rule of thumb: fair P/E is loosely anchored to the inverse of a required return that moves with rates.

Take a Vietnamese stock trading at **12,000 dong** per share, earning **1,000 dong** of profit per share — a P/E of 12. Suppose the policy rate falls from 6% to 4.5%, dragging the market's required return down by roughly 1.5 percentage points. If that lifts the stock's justified P/E from 12 to, say, 14 (a 17% re-rating, modest for such a move), then with unchanged earnings of 1,000 dong:

```
new fair price = 1,000 dong x 14 = 14,000 dong
gain from re-rating alone = (14,000 - 12,000) / 12,000 = +17%
```

The company earned not one extra dong. The stock rose 17% purely because the discount rate fell. In dollar terms, a position worth \$10,000 became worth \$11,700 on the re-rating alone. **The takeaway: in the recovery phase you get paid twice — once when earnings recover and again when falling rates re-rate the multiple — which is why early-cycle returns are the fattest of the whole cycle.**

### Dial 3: FDI, exports, and PMI

Vietnam is a small, hyper-open economy bolted onto global manufacturing supply chains. Foreign direct investment (FDI) builds the factories; exports — electronics, textiles, furniture, increasingly semiconductors-adjacent assembly — are a huge share of GDP. When global demand is strong and FDI is flowing in, the export-and-industrial side of the economy booms regardless of what domestic credit is doing. When global demand cracks (as it did for electronics in 2023), exports fall and the industrial sectors feel it first.

The single best high-frequency read on this is the **Purchasing Managers' Index (PMI)** — a monthly survey of factory managers, scaled so that 50 is the dividing line between expansion (above 50) and contraction (below 50). A PMI of 54 means manufacturing is humming; a PMI of 46 means factories are cutting. Because it is a survey of intentions and new orders, PMI *leads* the hard export and GDP data by a month or two. It is the fastest dial on the dashboard.

The reason PMI leads is structural, not magic. A factory manager surveyed about new orders is reporting on demand she is seeing *right now* for product that will ship in one to three months and be paid for after that — so her answer this month foreshadows next quarter's revenue, which foreshadows the following quarter's GDP print, which foreshadows the earnings reports that move stocks. By the time the GDP number is published, it is describing a quarter that ended weeks ago and that the PMI flagged months earlier. For an export-driven economy this is doubly important: Vietnam's manufacturing PMI is effectively a read on *foreign* demand — the orders coming from US retailers and European brands — so a falling PMI is often the first sign that an external shock (Quirk 4) is about to override the domestic clock. When the global electronics cycle cracked in 2023, Vietnam's PMI fell below 50 months before the export and GDP weakness fully showed up in the headline data, and the industrial and export-linked sectors weakened on cue.

FDI deserves its own note because it operates on a *slower* clock than the rest of the dashboard and therefore tells you about the *structural* trend rather than the cyclical one. A factory takes years to plan, permit, and build, so FDI commitments today become production and exports two or three years from now. A surge in FDI registration — semiconductor-adjacent assembly relocating from China under the "China plus one" supply-chain shift, for instance — is a multi-year tailwind for industrial parks, ports, logistics, and construction, largely independent of where the credit clock sits this quarter. So read FDI as the slow structural current beneath the faster cyclical waves: it tells you which sectors have a durable demand floor even in a domestic slowdown.

### Dial 4: property

Real estate is not just a sector in Vietnam; it is a macro force. Property is where households store wealth, where banks concentrate their loan collateral, and where local governments raise revenue through land. A property boom pulls steel, cement, construction, banking, and brokers up with it; a property bust — as in 2022-2023 — drags all of them down through the collateral and corporate-bond channels. When you watch property *presales* (units sold before completion) and developer bond maturities, you are watching a load-bearing wall of the whole index.

The reason property is so central — and so dangerous — is the web of connections radiating out from it. Start with the banks: a large share of bank loans in Vietnam are secured against real estate, so when property prices fall, the *collateral* behind the banking system's loan book falls too, raising the risk of bad debts (*nợ xấu*) and forcing banks to set aside provisions that hit their earnings. That is the channel that turns a property problem into a banking problem. Next, the corporate-bond channel: Vietnamese developers fund themselves heavily through *trái phiếu doanh nghiệp* — corporate bonds, often sold to retail investors and other companies. When a wave of those bonds comes due and the developer cannot refinance (because the SBV has tightened credit room and rates have risen), the developer faces a funding cliff. That is exactly what detonated in late 2022. Finally, the supply-chain channel: every apartment tower is a stack of orders for steel, cement, construction materials, contractors, and interior goods, so property volumes drive an entire chain of cyclical sectors. Pull on the property thread and the banks, the brokers (who lent margin against property stocks), the steel mills, and the cement plants all come with it. This is why a Vietnamese cycle so often *is* a property cycle wearing a macro costume — and why the property and bank weights make the index move as one in the violent phases.

## Reading the clock in real time: the dashboard and the order it turns

You now have five dials: **GDP, CPI, credit growth, the policy rate, and PMI.** The skill is not just reading each one — it is reading them *together*, and knowing the order in which they turn, because the cycle reveals itself as a sequence, not a snapshot.

![Vietnam GDP growth versus CPI inflation 2018 to 2025 dual line chart](/imgs/blogs/business-cycle-investment-clock-vietnam-2.png)

Look at the GDP-versus-CPI chart and you can already see the cycle breathe. GDP cratered to 2.91% in 2020 and 2.58% in 2021 (the COVID trough), then snapped back to 8.02% in 2022 (the reopening boom), normalized to 5.05% in the 2023 slowdown, and re-accelerated to 7.09% and 8.02% in 2024-2025. CPI inflation, crucially, stayed tame the whole time — never above 3.7% — which is *why* the SBV had room to cut rates and stay easy. The relationship between these two lines is the master clock: when GDP is rising and CPI is low, the central bank can be friendly; when CPI climbs toward its 4-4.5% comfort ceiling, the friendliness ends.

### The order the dials turn

This is the part that turns a dashboard into a forecast. The dials do not all move at once — they turn in a reliable sequence:

1. **PMI turns first.** Factory managers feel new orders changing before it shows up anywhere else. A PMI crossing back above 50 is the earliest whisper of recovery; a PMI rolling under 50 is the earliest warning of slowdown.
2. **Policy rate and credit room turn next.** The SBV reacts to the growth-and-inflation picture by cutting (and loosening credit) or hiking (and tightening credit). This is the *cause* of the next leg, so when you see a rate cut, you are watching the starting gun for recovery.
3. **Credit growth and the stock market move together, and early.** Because cheap money flows straight into both lending and the stock market, the index often turns *before* the real economy does. The market is discounting the recovery that the rate cut will produce.
4. **GDP and corporate earnings turn last.** By the time GDP prints a strong number, the easy money is months old and the market has already moved. This is why "the data is great" is often a *late*-cycle signal, not an early one.

The practical consequence: **do not wait for good GDP to buy.** By the time GDP confirms the recovery, the recovery-phase sectors have already run. You position off the early dials — the rate cut, the credit loosening, the PMI cross — and you let the lagging confirmation come to you.

A useful way to internalize the ordering is to label each dial as *leading*, *coincident*, or *lagging* relative to the stock market. The **leading** dials — PMI, the policy-rate decision, the credit-room signal — move *before* the market and tell you what is coming. The **coincident** dials — credit growth itself, deposit-rate moves — move roughly *with* the market. The **lagging** dials — GDP, reported corporate earnings — confirm *after* the market has already moved. Beginners instinctively over-weight the lagging dials because they feel the most "real" and authoritative (a GDP number is hard data; a PMI is a survey), but that is exactly backwards for positioning. The hard, authoritative data is hard and authoritative *because* it is final and historical — and historical is too late. The squishy, forward-looking survey is what gives you the edge precisely because it is forward-looking. Train yourself to act on the leading dials and merely *confirm* with the lagging ones, and you will stop buying tops and selling bottoms.

There is also an art to reading *divergences* between the dials, because the most valuable signals often come when they disagree. When PMI is rising but GDP is still weak, you are early in a recovery the hard data has not caught up to — a buy signal. When GDP is printing strong but PMI has rolled under 50, you are late in an expansion the market is about to discount — a warning to trim. When credit growth is still high but the SBV has started hiking, the throttle is being eased off even though the engine still sounds loud — the overheat-to-slowdown turn. Each of these divergences is the clock catching itself in the act of turning, and catching the turn early is the entire game.

#### Worked example: locating the clock from the dials

Suppose it is some month and your dashboard reads: GDP rising off a low base, CPI a tame 3.1%, the SBV just cut the refinancing rate from 6.0% to 4.5% and lifted the system credit target, credit growth accelerating from 9% toward 14%, and PMI back above 51. Where are you on the clock?

Walk the logic. Growth rising + inflation low + central bank *cutting* + credit *accelerating* + PMI *expanding* is the textbook signature of **early expansion / recovery**. Every dial points the same way: cheap money meeting improving growth. The clock says: tilt toward the recovery-phase leaders — **banks** (NIM bottoming, loan growth resuming), **brokers** (the highest-beta play on rising volumes and retail inflows), and **steel** (the first cyclical to turn as construction restarts).

What would *invalidate* this read? A CPI print jumping toward 4.5%, or the SBV pausing cuts, or PMI rolling back under 50. Any of those means the clock is turning faster than expected and you should not chase. **The takeaway: you locate the clock by checking whether the dials agree — when growth, rates, credit, and PMI all point the same direction, the phase is unambiguous and you position with conviction; when they conflict, you wait.**

## The four phases mapped to Vietnamese sectors

Now we put the engine and the dials together into the map that actually pays the bills: which sectors lead in each phase, and *why*. The "why" matters more than the list, because Vietnam's quirks (covered in the next section) mean the list is a guide, not a law.

![Phase by sector leadership grid for VN-Index](/imgs/blogs/business-cycle-investment-clock-vietnam-7.png)

The matrix is the whole post compressed into one grid. Read each row as a phase and each column as a sector group, and the green cells trace a leadership band that walks diagonally down and around as the cycle turns. Let me unpack each phase.

### Recovery (early cycle): banks, brokers, steel

The rate cut has just happened. Cheap money is flooding the system, but the real economy has not visibly recovered yet — GDP is still soft. This is the phase of maximum re-rating, and it rewards the sectors most geared to *cheaper money and rising volumes*.

- **Banks** lead because the policy rate cut bottoms out their funding costs and their NIM, loan demand is about to resume, and — critically in Vietnam — the credit room is being loosened, lifting the cap on their earnings. Banks are the heaviest weight in VN-Index (financials are roughly a third of the index), so when banks lead, the whole index moves.
- **Brokers / securities firms** (*công ty chứng khoán*) are the purest high-beta play on the recovery. Their revenue is trading commissions plus margin-lending interest plus proprietary gains — all three explode when retail money floods in chasing the recovery. Brokers routinely move 2-3x the index in both directions; they are the accelerator pedal.
- **Steel and basic resources** are the first *cyclical* to turn, because falling rates and reviving credit mean construction and property are about to restart, and steel demand leads the build. Steel has huge operating leverage (the blast furnace is a fixed cost), so when volumes turn, profits turn violently, and the stocks anticipate it.

Why these three and not, say, retail or healthcare? Because the recovery phase is driven by *cheaper money and liquidity*, not yet by real demand, and these three are the most direct beneficiaries of falling rates and reviving credit. Banks profit from the credit-room loosening and the bottoming of their funding costs; brokers profit from the wall of retail liquidity flowing into the market; steel profits from the leading edge of the construction restart. A retailer or a hospital, by contrast, sees no special benefit from cheaper money until real consumer demand actually picks up — which is the *next* phase. The recovery is the rate-and-liquidity phase, so the rate-and-liquidity sectors lead. Get this logic and you never have to memorize the list; you can re-derive it from "what does cheap money help first?"

### Expansion (mid cycle): property, industrials, materials

Growth is now solid and self-sustaining; credit is accelerating through its band. The driver shifts from "cheap money re-rating everything" to "real earnings growth," and leadership moves to the sectors that earn the most from a *building, hiring, investing* economy.

- **Real estate / property** leads the expansion because this is when presales boom, land values rise, and developers' deferred revenue converts to recognized profit. Property is the great mid-cycle earnings engine — and the great late-cycle risk.
- **Industrials and construction** ride the capex and public-investment wave; ports, logistics, and contractors see volumes peak.
- **Materials** (cement, construction materials) ride the same build-out.

### Overheat (late cycle): energy, materials, real-asset names

CPI is now rising, the SBV is shifting to hikes, and the discount rate is climbing against still-good earnings. The sectors that lead late are the ones whose earnings *benefit* from inflation rather than suffer from it.

- **Oil, gas, and energy** lead because their revenue is tied to commodity prices that rise with inflation, and their earnings are relatively insensitive to the domestic credit squeeze.
- **Materials with pricing power** can pass rising input costs to customers.
- Crucially, this is the phase where the recovery leaders — banks and brokers — start to *fade* even though their reported earnings still look great, because the market begins to discount the coming slowdown and the rising rate that hurts their valuations.

### Slowdown (recession): utilities, staples, healthcare

Rates are high, credit is tightening, growth is rolling over, and earnings are about to fall across most of the economy. The only sectors that lead — and "lead" here mostly means "fall less" — are the **defensives**, whose earnings barely move with the cycle.

- **Utilities** (power, water, gas) sell a product people buy regardless of the economy, often with regulated or contracted prices. Their cash flows are the most cycle-proof in the market.
- **Food and beverage staples** sell things people eat and drink in any economy.
- **Healthcare and pharma** sell things people need regardless of GDP, with the added tailwind of Vietnam's aging demographics.

These defensives are boring in the boom and beautiful in the bust. Owning them in the slowdown is how you preserve capital to redeploy into banks and brokers when the next rate cut starts the clock again.

One caution specific to Vietnam: the "defensive" label is relative, not absolute. In a deep, retail-driven panic — the kind Quirk 2 produces — *everything* falls, including utilities and staples, because forced selling and margin calls do not respect sector fundamentals. What you are buying with defensives is not immunity but *relative* protection: they fall a third or a half as much, they recover faster, and crucially they let you sleep at night so you are still in the game to buy the recovery. A defensive that falls 12% while the brokers fall 55% has done its job, even though it is down. The goal of the slowdown tilt is not to make money; it is to lose little enough that you have the capital and the composure to act decisively when the dials flip back to recovery.

#### Worked example: the re-rating tailwind reverses in the slowdown

The recovery worked example showed how a rate *cut* re-rates a stock upward. The slowdown is the same lever in reverse, and it explains why late-cycle holders of high-beta stocks get hurt twice. Take the same stock — now trading at **18,000 dong** after a boom, on earnings of **1,200 dong** per share (a P/E of 15) because both the earnings and the multiple expanded in the good times. Now the slowdown arrives: the SBV hikes from 4.5% back to 6.0%, the discount rate jumps, and the justified P/E compresses from 15 back to 11. At the same time, the slowdown cuts the company's earnings from 1,200 to 1,000 dong. The new fair price is:

```
new fair price = 1,000 dong x 11 = 11,000 dong
fall from 18,000 = (11,000 - 18,000) / 18,000 = -39%
```

The stock falls 39% from the *combination* of falling earnings and a compressing multiple — the mirror image of getting paid twice. A 254-million-VND (\$10,000) position becomes ~155 million VND (\$6,100). **The takeaway: the same double-barreled mechanism that makes recovery returns so fat makes slowdown losses so deep, which is precisely why rotating *out* of high-beta names before the hikes — not after — is the highest-value defensive move in the cycle.**

## Where the cycle misleads: Vietnam's quirks

If sector rotation were as clean as the matrix, everyone would be rich. It is not, because Vietnam's market has four quirks that distort the textbook clock. Knowing them is the difference between using the framework and being fooled by it.

### Quirk 1: the cycle is policy-driven, so it turns on a decision, not a trend

In a big, market-driven economy, the cycle turns gradually as millions of decisions accumulate. In Vietnam, the cycle can turn on a *single SBV announcement* — a rate decision, a credit-room adjustment, a circular on property lending. The September-October 2022 hikes were not a slow drift; they were an emergency reversal that flipped the entire market in weeks. This means you cannot just watch slow-moving economic trends; you have to watch policy headlines, because the throttle is held by a small group of people who can move it overnight.

### Quirk 2: the market is retail-dominated, so it overshoots both ways

Roughly 85-90% of daily trading on HOSE is domestic retail investors. Retail money is momentum-driven and emotional: it piles in late in the boom (chasing the recovery's gains into the overheat) and panics out late in the bust. This means VN-Index *overshoots* the fundamentals in both directions — it runs hotter in overheat and falls harder in slowdown than the underlying cycle alone would justify. The 42% drawdown in 2022 was bigger than the earnings damage warranted; it was a retail unwind on top of a real tightening.

### Quirk 3: the index is narrow and bank-heavy

A handful of large-cap banks, plus Vingroup-family property and consumer names, dominate the index weight. When financials are roughly a third of the index, "the market" and "the banks" are nearly the same thing. A sector that is tiny by weight — media, say — can have a fantastic move that barely registers on the index. This is why the recovery phase, led by banks, tends to produce the strongest *index* moves: the leading sector and the heaviest sector are the same.

### Quirk 4: external shocks can override the domestic clock

Because Vietnam is so export- and FDI-dependent, a global shock can override the domestic cycle entirely. A US recession that craters electronics demand, a Fed hiking cycle that pressures the dong and forces the SBV to tighten defensively, a China slowdown that hits commodity-linked names — any of these can stop Vietnam's clock or spin it backward regardless of where the domestic dials point. In 2022, the SBV was forced to hike *defensively* to protect the dong against a surging US dollar, not because Vietnam's own inflation demanded it. The domestic clock said "still expanding"; the external shock said "tighten anyway."

## Common misconceptions

### Misconception 1: "The clock is a calendar — each phase lasts about the same time"

No. Phases vary enormously in length, and Vietnam's policy-driven cycle can compress a phase into weeks. The 2020-2022 expansion-to-overheat transition that might take a US cycle two years happened in Vietnam in roughly six months once inflation and the dong forced the SBV's hand. The clock tells you the *order* of phases and the *order* sectors lead; it tells you nothing reliable about *duration*. Trying to trade the clock on a calendar — "it's been 18 months, time for the slowdown" — gets you run over by a policy decision that arrives early or late.

### Misconception 2: "GDP leads the market, so wait for good GDP to buy"

Backwards. The market leads GDP by months, because the market discounts the future and GDP reports the past. In the 2020 recovery, VN-Index bottomed in March 2020 and had already risen ~50% by the time the strong GDP rebound printed. If you waited for the 8.02% 2022 GDP number to confirm the boom, you bought near the January 2022 *peak* and rode it down 42%. The strong GDP print was a *late*-cycle signal, not a green light. Position off the early dials (the rate cut, the credit loosening, PMI), and treat strong GDP as a reason to start watching for the exit, not the entry.

### Misconception 3: "The cycle is global, so just copy the US clock"

Partly true, dangerously incomplete. Vietnam's cycle is *correlated* with the global cycle through exports and FDI, but its domestic engine — the credit ceiling and the SBV's reaction function — runs on its own logic. In 2023 the US was still fighting inflation with high rates while Vietnam was *cutting* to engineer a recovery, because Vietnamese inflation was tame (3.25%) and the priority was domestic growth. An investor who copied the US "stay defensive, rates are high" stance missed a Vietnamese recovery that the domestic clock clearly signaled. Watch the global cycle as an *override risk* (Quirk 4), but read the domestic dials for the base case.

### Misconception 4: "When the SBV cuts rates, buy everything"

Too blunt. A rate cut is bullish for the *recovery-phase* sectors — banks, brokers, steel — but it is bullish *because* it signals where you are on the clock, not as a blanket buy. Cutting into a slowdown (the late-slowdown cuts of 2023) is an early signal to *start* rotating toward early-cycle leaders, not to buy the late-cycle defensives that have already had their run. The cut is information about the phase; you still have to buy the sector that the *next* phase rewards, not the one the last phase rewarded.

## How it shows up on VN-Index: the 2020-2022 round trip

Theory is cheap. Let me walk the most important Vietnamese cycle of the last decade — the 2020-2022 round trip — phase by phase, with the numbers, because it is the cleanest textbook example of the clock you will ever get.

![Timeline of the 2020 to 2022 round trip from COVID trough to peak to bust](/imgs/blogs/business-cycle-investment-clock-vietnam-6.png)

### Phase 1 — Recovery (March 2020 to mid-2021)

The COVID crash bottomed VN-Index near 660 in March 2020. The SBV cut the refinancing rate in three steps to a record-low 4.0% and loosened credit room. Deposit rates collapsed, and a wave of new retail accounts — the "F0" generation — opened brokerages and bought stocks. **The recovery leaders led exactly as the clock says:** banks rallied on the credit-room loosening and bottoming NIM, and brokers went vertical on exploding volumes and margin lending. Many securities-firm stocks rose 3-5x off the lows. This is the phase where you got paid twice — earnings recovering *and* the multiple re-rating on cheap money.

### Phase 2 — Expansion (mid-2021 to late 2021)

Credit accelerated, GDP began its snap-back toward the 8.02% it would print in 2022, and leadership broadened from financials into **property and construction**. Land prices ran, developers reported booming presales, and steel and materials rode the build-out. The index pushed through 1,400 toward its peak. Retail euphoria built; the *sóng ngành* — sector "waves" — rolled through one industry after another as money chased momentum.

### Phase 3 — Overheat and the turn (Q4 2021 to January 2022)

VN-Index peaked near 1,498 in early January 2022. By now the early leaders — banks and brokers — were already topping even as the headlines were most bullish, exactly the late-cycle fade the matrix predicts. Inflation pressure was building globally, the Fed was about to hike aggressively, and the dong was about to come under pressure. The clock had reached the top.

### Phase 4 — Slowdown and the bust (2022)

This is where Vietnam's quirks turned a normal slowdown into a crash. The SBV hiked the refinancing rate back to 6.0% in the September-October 2022 emergency moves — partly to defend the dong against a surging dollar (Quirk 4, the external override). Simultaneously, a corporate-bond scandal froze the property sector's funding (Quirk 1, policy-driven), and retail panic (Quirk 2) amplified the fall. VN-Index collapsed to an intraday trough near 874 in November 2022 — down 42% from the peak. **The high-beta recovery leaders fell hardest:** brokers and property gave back almost their entire boom, while a few utilities and staples names fell a fraction as much. The defensives "led" the slowdown by losing the least.

#### Worked example: the phase return gap, recovery basket vs slowdown basket

Put concrete numbers on why the sector choice dominated the index choice. Imagine two equal baskets, each worth **254 million VND** (\$10,000 at \$1 = 25,400 VND), held through a single phase.

In the **recovery** of 2020-21, a basket of brokers and steel might have returned roughly **+120%** as the high-beta names re-rated — turning 254 million VND into ~559 million VND (\$22,000). A defensive basket of utilities and staples might have returned a modest **+25%** in the same window — to ~318 million VND (\$12,500). The phase made a ~95-percentage-point difference in *return* between two portfolios held over the *same months*.

Now flip to the **slowdown** of 2022. The broker-and-steel basket might have fallen **-55%** (254 million VND down to ~114 million, or \$4,500), while the utility-and-staples basket fell only **-10%** (to ~229 million, \$9,000). Same index, same months, a 45-percentage-point gap — and this time the defensives won by a mile.

```
recovery phase: brokers/steel +120%  vs  utilities/staples +25%   -> gap +95 pts
slowdown phase: brokers/steel -55%   vs  utilities/staples -10%   -> gap +45 pts
```

**The takeaway: in both directions the sector you owned mattered far more than whether you owned "the market" — getting the phase-to-sector map right is the single highest-leverage decision in Vietnamese investing.**

### The 2023-2024 recovery

The clock kept turning. Through 2023 the SBV cut the refinancing rate back to 4.5% as inflation stayed tame (3.25%) and the priority shifted to growth — a textbook late-slowdown easing that started the clock over. GDP normalized to 5.05% in 2023, then re-accelerated to 7.09% in 2024. VN-Index recovered from the 874 trough back through 1,130 (end-2023) to 1,266 (end-2024) — and, true to the clock, **banks and brokers led the early recovery again**, just as they had in 2020. Different cycle, same phase map.

The 2023-2024 recovery is also a clinic in the divergence-reading discussed above. In early 2023, the lagging dials looked frightening: property developers were defaulting on bonds, bad-debt fears stalked the banks, and GDP had cooled to 5%. An investor anchored to that hard data stayed in cash and missed the turn. But the leading dials had already flipped: the SBV was cutting (the rate fell from 6.0% toward 4.5%), it was *loosening* credit room to support growth, and inflation was tame enough to give it room to keep easing. The clock said "early recovery" even while the headlines screamed "crisis." Those who read the policy pivot — not the property headlines — caught banks and brokers off the bottom. This is the recurring lesson of the Vietnamese clock: the best entries feel the *worst*, because the leading dials turn while the news is still dark, and the news only brightens after the easy money has already been made.

It is worth contrasting the 2023 Vietnamese pivot with what the US was doing at the same time, because it is the cleanest refutation of "just copy the US." Through 2023 the US Federal Reserve was still *hiking* and holding rates at multi-decade highs to crush a stubborn 4-6% inflation. A Vietnamese investor who imported that stance — "rates are high everywhere, stay defensive" — would have sat out a domestic recovery driven by Vietnamese rates *falling*. The two clocks were running in opposite directions because the two inflation pictures were completely different: tame in Vietnam, hot in the US. Read your own dials.

![VN-Index year-end close 2017 to 2024 line chart](/imgs/blogs/business-cycle-investment-clock-vietnam-4.png)

The index chart is the round trip in one line: the climb to ~1,498 in 2021, the collapse to a ~1,007 close (874 intraday) in 2022, and the recovery to 1,266 by 2024. Every one of those turns lines up with a turn of the policy-rate step chart above. The clock is not a metaphor; it is a mechanism you can trace print by print.

## The playbook: how to position around the clock

Here is the payoff — the concrete process for trading the clock on VN-Index, not just understanding it.

### Step 1: locate the clock from the five dials

Every month, read the dashboard in the order the dials turn: **PMI** (above or below 50, and which way), the **SBV policy rate** (cut, hold, or hike, and the credit-room signal), **credit growth** (accelerating or decelerating), **CPI** (relative to the ~4-4.5% comfort ceiling), and **GDP** (rising or falling, but treat it as confirmation, not signal). Write down which phase the dials *agree* on. If they conflict, you are at a turn — size down and wait for agreement.

### Step 2: set the sector tilt for the phase

Translate the phase into a tilt, not an all-in bet:

- **Recovery:** overweight **banks, brokers, steel.** This is the highest-conviction, fattest-return tilt of the cycle — you get earnings recovery *and* multiple re-rating. Brokers are the leveraged way to express it.
- **Expansion:** rotate toward **property, industrials, materials.** Keep some financials; the index needs them to lead.
- **Overheat:** rotate toward **energy and materials with pricing power**; begin trimming the high-beta recovery leaders even while the headlines are bullish. This is the hardest discipline in the cycle.
- **Slowdown:** overweight **utilities, staples, healthcare**; raise cash. The goal here is capital preservation so you can buy the next recovery.

### Step 3: size by phase conviction and sector beta

The early-recovery tilt deserves your largest sizing because the dials agree and the payoff (earnings + re-rating) is the fattest. The overheat-to-slowdown transition deserves the smallest gross exposure, because that is where Vietnam's retail overshoot (Quirk 2) makes drawdowns brutal. Remember brokers move 2-3x the index — size them as the high-beta instrument they are, not as a normal stock.

#### Worked example: sizing the recovery tilt

Suppose you run a **2.54 billion VND** portfolio (\$100,000 at \$1 = 25,400 VND) and the dials all say early recovery. A disciplined recovery tilt might put 60% in the recovery leaders, split by beta:

```
banks (lower beta, index anchor)     30%  =  762 million VND  ($30,000)
brokers (high beta, the accelerator) 20%  =  508 million VND  ($20,000)
steel (cyclical turn)                10%  =  254 million VND  ($10,000)
cash / defensives (dry powder)       40%  = 1,016 million VND ($40,000)
```

You keep 40% in reserve because the clock can turn on a policy decision (Quirk 1), and you want powder to add on the first pullback or to defend if a CPI print forces the SBV's hand. The brokers slot is deliberately smaller than the banks slot despite higher conviction, *because* its 2-3x beta means 20% in brokers carries roughly the index-move risk of 40-60% in banks. **The takeaway: size the tilt by the phase's conviction, then scale each sector's weight down by its beta so the high-beta names do not secretly dominate your risk.**

### Step 4: define the invalidation

The view is wrong — and you cut the tilt — when the dials stop agreeing. For a recovery tilt, the invalidations are: a **CPI print jumping toward 4.5%** (the SBV's comfort ceiling, which would force a hike), the **SBV pausing or reversing cuts**, **credit growth decelerating** instead of accelerating, **PMI rolling back under 50**, or an **external shock** (a Fed hiking surprise that pressures the dong, a global demand crack that hits exports). Any one of these means the clock is turning faster than your thesis; trim the high-beta names first, raise cash, and re-read the dashboard. The invalidation is not a stop-loss on a single stock — it is a regime signal that the *phase* has changed and the whole tilt should rotate.

### The one-paragraph version

Read five free prints — PMI, policy rate, credit growth, CPI, GDP — in the order they turn. When they agree on a phase, tilt toward that phase's leading sectors (recovery: banks/brokers/steel; expansion: property/industrials/materials; overheat: energy/materials; slowdown: utilities/staples/healthcare), sized down by beta. When they disagree, you are at a turn — raise cash and wait. Respect the four quirks: the clock turns on policy decisions, retail overshoots both ways, the index is bank-heavy, and external shocks can override everything. Do that, and the next round trip around the Vietnamese clock looks a lot more like an opportunity than an ambush.
