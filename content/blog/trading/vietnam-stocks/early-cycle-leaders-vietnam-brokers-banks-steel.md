## A rebound that started while the news was still bad

In the back half of 2022, the Vietnamese stock market was a wreck. A corporate-bond crisis (*trai phieu doanh nghiep*) had frozen a major funding channel, the State Bank of Vietnam had hiked its policy rate sharply to defend the dong, and VN-Index had fallen more than 40% from its early-2022 peak to a low near 874 in mid-November. The financial press was full of bankruptcies, frozen bond redemptions, and developers begging for liquidity. Margin balances at brokers had collapsed as clients deleveraged. By every measure of *sentiment*, this was the worst the market had felt in years — and the economic data was still deteriorating, with GDP growth slowing and the property market locked up.

And then, quietly, the market bottomed — months before the news got better. Through the first half of 2023 the SBV pivoted hard, cutting its policy rate four times between March and June. Liquidity began to return: the system margin balance, which had bottomed near 122 trillion dong in late 2022, started climbing again. And the stock market did what it always does at the trough — it led the recovery, not lagged it. Over 2023 the securities/broker sector returned roughly **+75%**, leading every other sector by a wide margin. Banks came up next, re-rating off rock-bottom valuations. Steel and the early cyclicals joined as restocking began. The investors who waited for the GDP print to confirm a recovery missed the first and biggest leg, because the first leg was already over by the time the data turned.

This is the single most valuable pattern a Vietnamese investor can learn to read, and it is the subject of this post: **at the bottom of a cycle, leadership rotates in a specific order, and that order is knowable in advance.** It is not random which sectors lead the rebound. Brokers lead, then banks, then steel and early cyclicals — and each of them leads *for a mechanical reason* tied to what turns first in a recovery. Liquidity turns before earnings; earnings turn before demand; demand turns before the data confirms it. Map each sector onto which of those it is geared to, and you have a roadmap for positioning at the trough — which is exactly when it is hardest to buy, because the news is still terrible. This post builds that roadmap from zero: what "early cycle" means, why each sector leads in the order it does, the signals that mark the turn, the misconceptions that keep people on the sidelines, how it has played out on VN-Index in two real recoveries, and the concrete playbook for trading it.

Everything between here and the playbook is an elaboration of why this order is mechanical rather than coincidental — and how you read the signals that tell you the sequence has started.

## Foundations: what "early cycle" actually means

Before we can talk about which sectors lead, we have to be precise about *where in the cycle we are*. Markets and economies move in cycles — long, irregular waves of expansion and contraction — and the sectors that lead change depending on which phase you are in. The early-cycle phase is the one that matters most for the rebound trade, so let us define it carefully and build the intuition from the ground up.

### The four phases of a cycle, in one paragraph

Think of the economic cycle as having four rough phases. In **early cycle** (the trough into recovery), the economy is at or just past its low point: the central bank is *cutting* interest rates to stimulate, credit is starting to flow again, liquidity is returning to markets, and confidence is fragile but improving. In **mid cycle** (expansion), growth is solid and self-sustaining, earnings are rising broadly, and the central bank is roughly neutral. In **late cycle** (the top), the economy is running hot, inflation is rising, the central bank is *hiking* to cool things down, and the easy gains are behind you. In **recession** (the downturn), activity contracts, earnings fall, defaults rise, and the central bank eventually pivots back to cutting — which sets up the next early cycle. The cycle is covered in full in the companion post on [the business cycle and the investment clock for Vietnam](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/business-cycle-investment-clock-vietnam); here we zoom in on the single most lucrative transition: the turn from recession into early cycle.

A homely analogy first. Picture the economy as a car that has stalled at the bottom of a hill. Late cycle was the car overheating as it strained up the slope; recession was it stalling and rolling backward; early cycle is the moment the engine catches again and the car *just* starts to move forward — slowly, still near the bottom, but accelerating. The first thing to respond is not the speed of the car (the economy) but the *fuel flowing to the engine* (liquidity and credit). And the sectors that lead are the ones geared to the fuel, not the speed. That is the entire intuition: **in early cycle, you buy the sectors levered to liquidity and credit, because those turn before the economy does.**

### What turns first: the order of a recovery

Here is the crucial sequencing that the whole post hangs on. A recovery does not happen all at once — different things turn in a specific order, and that order determines which sectors lead.

1. **Policy turns first.** The central bank cuts rates. This is a *decision*, and it can happen in a single meeting. In Vietnam, the SBV cutting its refinancing and discount rates is the starting gun.
2. **Liquidity turns next.** Lower rates make cash less attractive and borrowing cheaper, so money flows back into riskier assets. In the stock market specifically, the *margin balance* (money clients borrow from brokers to buy stock, *ky quy*) starts climbing again. This happens within weeks-to-months of the cuts.
3. **Credit turns after that.** Banks start lending more as demand for loans recovers and the SBV loosens the credit ceiling (*room tin dung*). Credit growth accelerates — but this takes a quarter or two to show up in the data.
4. **Earnings turn after credit.** With cheaper funding and reviving activity, corporate earnings bottom and start rising. This shows up two-to-three quarters into the recovery.
5. **The real economy turns last.** GDP growth, employment, and consumer demand recover slowest of all, and the official data confirms it last — often six months to a year after the market has already bottomed.

The sectors that lead the rebound are the ones geared to the *earliest* links in this chain. Liquidity turns almost immediately, so the most liquidity-sensitive sector — brokers — leads. Credit turns next, so banks follow. Real demand turns last of the three, so the early cyclicals geared to restocking — steel — join after that. **The leadership order is just the turning order of the recovery, read off the chain.**

This is worth pausing on, because it is the central insight that makes the rest of the post predictable rather than mysterious. People tend to think of a stock-market recovery as one undifferentiated event — "the market went up." But a recovery is a *sequence*, and the sequence has a definite order because the links in the chain are causally connected: the rate cut *causes* the liquidity return, which *enables* the credit recovery, which *supports* the earnings turn, which eventually *shows up* in the economy. Each link lags the one before it by a predictable interval — weeks for liquidity, a quarter or two for credit, two-to-three quarters for earnings, six months to a year for the economy. Because the links are ordered in time, and because each sector is geared to a specific link, the *sectors* are ordered in time too. Leadership rotation in early cycle is not a fashion or a sentiment shift — it is the literal mechanical consequence of the recovery chain unwinding link by link. Once you see it that way, "which sector leads" stops being a guess and becomes a deduction.

### Why the defensives lag: the mirror image

The flip side of "buy the liquidity-geared sectors" is "sell the defensives," and it pays to understand *why* the defensive sectors lag the early-cycle rebound, because it is the same logic in reverse. Defensive sectors — utilities (power, water, gas), consumer staples (food, beverage), healthcare — have *low* beta by design: their cash flows are stable across the cycle, because people buy electricity and noodles in a boom and a bust alike. That stability is exactly what you want in a recession, when you are trying to *preserve* capital and the high-beta sectors are collapsing. But it is exactly what you do *not* want at the trough, when the whole point is to *capture* the rebound with maximum gearing. A utility's earnings barely move when the cycle turns, so its stock barely moves; while brokers are running +75%, the utility is grinding out +3%. The defensives are not "bad" — they are *phase-appropriate*, and their phase is the recession, not the recovery. Holding them through the early-cycle leg is a phase-mismatch: owning the recession portfolio in the recovery. The rotation *out* of defensives and *into* the high-beta financials is the single most important move you make at the trough.

### Why brokers lead: pure leverage on returning liquidity

Securities brokers (*cong ty chung khoan*) are the highest-beta sector on VN-Index, and the reason is covered in depth in the dedicated post on [why brokers are the highest-beta sector](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/securities-brokers-sector-vietnam-highest-beta). The short version: every one of a broker's revenue lines — brokerage commissions, margin-lending interest, proprietary trading, and investment banking — is geared to market activity in the same direction, with no counter-cyclical line to cushion it. When turnover, the margin balance, and the index all turn up together, broker profits explode; when they turn down together, broker profits collapse.

That gearing is *exactly* what you want at the trough, because liquidity is the very first thing to turn. The moment rates are cut and money starts flowing back, two of a broker's four engines fire immediately: turnover rises (more commissions) and the margin balance climbs (more interest income). The broker does not need the economy to recover, earnings to grow, or demand to turn — it just needs *more trading and more borrowing against stock*, which is precisely what returning liquidity delivers first. So the broker sector is a pure, leveraged proxy for the earliest link in the recovery chain. It leads because it is geared to the thing that turns first.

There is a second, subtler reason brokers lead, and it is worth spelling out because it explains the *violence* of the move. A broker stock re-rates *twice* in an upturn. First, its *earnings* rise — turnover, margin interest, and prop gains all turn up together. Second, the *valuation multiple* the market is willing to pay for those earnings expands, because investors stop pricing the broker as a dying business in a frozen market and start pricing it as a leveraged play on a recovering one. When earnings and the multiple expand at the same time, the stock price move is the *product* of the two, not the sum — a 50% earnings recovery paired with a 50% multiple re-rating is not a 100% move but a 1.5 × 1.5 = 2.25x, a +125% move. This double re-rating is the mechanical reason the broker sector can return +75% in a year when the index is up only +12%: you are stacking an earnings recovery and a multiple expansion on top of an already-high-beta business. It is the most leveraged way to be long the return of liquidity itself.

One more nuance: not all brokers are equally geared, and the early-cycle leader within the sector is usually the one with the *biggest margin book relative to equity* and the *most aggressive proprietary book*, because those are the two engines most directly levered to the liquidity turn. A broker that funds a huge margin loan book on a thin equity base, and runs a large equity prop book, will see its return on equity (ROE) swing the most violently off the trough — and its stock with it. This is why, in the first leg of a rebound, the highest-beta broker names tend to outrun the conservative, bond-heavy ones. The sector *as a whole* leads, but within it, beta is a spectrum, and the trough rewards the top of that spectrum.

![Why brokers lead graph returning liquidity to turnover and margin demand to broker earnings explode first](/imgs/blogs/early-cycle-leaders-vietnam-brokers-banks-steel-6.png)

The figure above traces the mechanism. Returning liquidity (the rate cut plus money flowing back) drives two things at once: more market turnover and more margin demand. Those two together make broker earnings explode first — before banks, before steel, before the economy. The broker is the highest-beta link because both of its biggest engines are geared to liquidity directly, and liquidity turns before anything else in the chain.

### Why banks follow: NIM bottoms and cheap valuations re-rate

Banks are the heartbeat of VN-Index — they make up roughly a third of the index by weight, and the sector's mechanics are covered in the dedicated post on [the banking sector and the heartbeat of VN-Index](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/banking-sector-vietnam-the-heartbeat-of-vn-index). For our purposes, what matters is *why* banks lead second in the rebound, just behind brokers.

A bank's core profit is its net interest margin (NIM) — the spread between what it charges on loans and what it pays on deposits — multiplied by the size of its loan book. In a downturn, NIM gets squeezed: the SBV is hiking, funding costs spike, asset quality deteriorates, and loan growth stalls. When the cycle turns, three things improve in sequence. First, **NIM bottoms and starts widening** as deposit costs fall faster than loan yields once the SBV cuts (deposits reprice quickly; loans reprice with a lag). Second, **loan growth resumes** as credit demand recovers and the SBV loosens the credit ceiling. Third — and this is the big one for the stock price — **the valuation re-rates from cheap toward fair.** In a crisis, banks trade well below book value (P/B under 1.0x) because the market is pricing in bad-debt losses and a frozen credit cycle. As the recovery becomes visible, that fear unwinds, and the multiple expands back toward and above book.

That re-rating is why banks deliver large returns in early cycle *before their earnings even grow much*. The move is driven mostly by the multiple, not the profit — the market simply stops pricing the bank as if it is about to take huge losses, and re-rates it as a going concern with recovering ROE. Banks lead second rather than first because credit turns *after* liquidity: a bank needs the SBV cut to feed through to its funding costs and to the credit ceiling, which takes a quarter or two, whereas a broker benefits the instant the margin balance ticks up. So the order is mechanical — brokers (geared to liquidity, which turns first), then banks (geared to credit, which turns next).

It helps to be concrete about the NIM mechanism, because it is the hidden engine of the bank leg. A Vietnamese bank funds itself largely from customer deposits, which reprice *quickly* — a six-month or twelve-month term deposit rolls over at the new, lower rate within months of an SBV cut. Its loan book reprices *slowly* — many loans are fixed for a period, and the bank is in no hurry to cut the rate it charges borrowers. So in the window right after the SBV cuts, the bank's *cost of funds falls faster than its loan yield*, and the spread between them — the NIM — *widens*. This is a tailwind that arrives early in the recovery, ahead of any loan-growth recovery, and it is one reason bank earnings bottom and turn up faster than the broad economy. Add the second tailwind — the SBV loosening the credit ceiling (*room tin dung*) so the bank can grow its loan book again — and you have rising NIM on a growing book, which is the formula for accelerating profit. But again, the *stock* moves before the profit, because the re-rating front-runs the earnings.

There is also an asset-quality dimension that sharpens the re-rating. In a downturn, the market fears a wave of non-performing loans (NPLs) — borrowers defaulting, the bank forced to write off loans and raise provisions, which eats into book value. That fear is what pushes the P/B below 1.0x. When the cycle turns, lower rates ease the debt-service burden on borrowers, the property market thaws (collateral values stop falling), and the feared NPL wave often turns out smaller than priced. As that tail risk recedes, the discount-to-book unwinds — the stock re-rates not because anything *new* has happened to earnings, but because a feared *bad outcome* has been taken off the table. This is the single most important thing to understand about the bank leg: a large part of the early-cycle bank return is the *removal of a fear that was over-priced at the trough*, and fear is removed early in a recovery, not late.

### Why steel and early cyclicals join: restocking as demand turns

Steel (*thep*) is the textbook early cyclical, and its sector mechanics are covered in [the steel and basic-resources sector deep-dive](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/steel-basic-resources-sector-vietnam-the-cyclical). Steel demand is geared to *real* construction and industrial activity — building apartments, infrastructure, factories, cars, and appliances. That is genuine end-demand, and it turns later than liquidity or credit, because real projects take time to restart.

But steel does something special at the turn that makes it an *early* cyclical rather than a late one: it benefits from **restocking.** Here is the mechanism. Through a downturn, every link in the steel chain — distributors, fabricators, construction firms — runs its inventory down to the bone, because nobody wants to hold steel whose price is falling. This is *destocking*, and it makes the downturn in steel demand look even worse than the underlying drop in construction, because real demand falls *and* the pipeline empties. When the cycle turns, the reverse happens with a vengeance: as soon as demand stops falling and prices stabilize, the whole chain has to *rebuild* its depleted inventory. Restocking demand stacks on top of recovering end-demand, so steel orders snap back hard — faster than the underlying construction recovery alone would suggest. That is why steel is an early cyclical: the restocking impulse front-loads its recovery.

There is a second amplifier specific to steel: **operating leverage at the producer level.** A steel mill like Hoa Phat (HPG) has enormous fixed costs — blast furnaces, plants, financing — that do not change with volume. When demand and prices fall in a downturn, revenue drops but the fixed costs stay, so margins get crushed and the producer can swing to a loss. When demand and prices recover, the same fixed cost base is spread over rising volume *and* a rising price, so margins expand far faster than revenue — a modest recovery in steel prices and volumes can take a producer from a loss back to a fat profit in a couple of quarters. That operating leverage means steel *earnings* recover violently once the turn comes, which is why the steel sector can return +28% in an early-cycle year and far more in a strong one. Stack the restocking impulse (orders snap back) on top of the operating leverage (margins expand fast), and you get a sector whose earnings inflect hard at the turn.

Steel joins *after* brokers and banks because it needs real demand to turn, not just liquidity or credit — but it joins *early* among the cyclicals because restocking front-loads the move. The same logic applies to other early cyclicals: chemicals, some industrial materials, and the construction-materials names geared to the same restart. They are all levered to the moment real activity stops contracting, which is later than the financial turn but earlier than the broad economy. The practical implication is that steel is the *third* leg you add, not the first — you lead with the financials geared to liquidity and credit, then rotate into steel as the real-demand and restocking signals confirm.

## How the leadership order stacks up: the three sectors side by side

Let us make the order concrete with the actual 2023 numbers, because the magnitudes are as important as the sequence. In the 2023 early-cycle recovery, with the SBV cutting and liquidity returning, the rough sector total returns ran roughly like this: securities/brokers up about +75%, steel up about +28%, banks up about +12%, real estate up about +5%, and utilities up about +3%. Brokers led by a mile, the cyclicals (steel) and financials (banks) followed, and the defensives (utilities) lagged badly — exactly what the framework predicts.

The chart above ranks the 2023 sector returns and makes the leadership visceral. The bar for securities towers over everything else — that is the highest-beta sector exploding on returning liquidity. Steel and banks deliver solid double-digit gains as the cyclical and credit recovery feeds through. And the defensives — utilities, with real estate still nursing its bond-crisis wounds — bring up the rear, because there is no reason to own low-beta cash-flow stability when the whole point of early cycle is maximum leverage to the upturn. The figure is directional and approximate, but the *ordering* is the lesson: this is the early-cycle leadership ladder, and it repeats.

#### Worked example: broker beta off the trough

Beta measures how much a sector moves relative to the market. In 2023, VN-Index returned roughly +12% while the broker sector returned roughly +75%. The implied beta off the trough is 75 / 12 ≈ **6.3x** — meaning the broker sector moved more than six times as much as the index. Put a number on it: if you had put a 100mn-dong sleeve (about \$3,900 at \$1 = 25,400 VND) into a broker basket at the 2023 trough, a +75% return turns it into 175mn dong (about \$6,890), a gain of 75mn dong (about \$2,950). The same 100mn dong in an index fund returns +12%, ending at 112mn dong — a gain of just 12mn dong (about \$470). The intuition: at the trough, the broker sector is not a slightly-better version of the index — it is a *multiple* of it, because both of its biggest engines (turnover and margin) are geared straight to the liquidity that turns first.

One caution this worked example also teaches: the beta cuts both ways. A 6x beta off the trough means the broker sector will give back its gains roughly six times as fast if the recovery aborts and the index rolls over. The high beta that makes brokers the leader on the way up makes them the *worst* place to be if you are wrong about the turn — which is why the early-cycle playbook later in this post is so insistent on the invalidation signals. You lead with brokers *because* of the beta, and you watch them like a hawk *because* of the same beta.

## Common misconceptions

The early-cycle rebound is one of the most counter-intuitive trades in markets, which is exactly why it works — most people cannot bring themselves to do it. Let us correct the three myths that keep investors on the sidelines at the bottom, each with a number.

### Misconception 1: "Wait for the economy to confirm before you buy"

This is the single most expensive mistake in early-cycle investing. The instinct is sensible-sounding: don't buy until you see GDP growth recover, earnings turn up, and the data confirm a recovery. But the data confirms the recovery *last* — recall the turning order: policy, then liquidity, then credit, then earnings, then GDP. By the time the GDP print improves, the market has already priced the recovery in, and the biggest leg of the rebound is over.

Put a number on it. In 2023, VN-Index bottomed in November 2022 and the broker sector ran +75% over the following year — but Vietnam's GDP growth actually *slowed* to about 5.05% in 2023 from 8% in 2022. The economic data was *worse* in the year the market boomed, because the market leads the economy, not the other way around. If you had waited for the GDP number to improve before buying, you would have bought *after* the +75% move, not before it. The market is a discounting machine — it prices the future, not the present — so the early-cycle trade is precisely the trade you make *while the news is still bad.*

### Misconception 2: "Banks need a strong economy first"

People assume banks are a late-cycle, "buy when the economy is humming" sector, because banks lend into the real economy. But the bank *stock* leads the bank *earnings* by a wide margin, and the reason is the re-rating. At the trough, banks trade below book value because the market is pricing in a wave of bad debts and a frozen credit cycle. The stock does not need the economy to be strong — it just needs the market to stop pricing in catastrophe.

Here is the number. A bank trading at P/B 0.9x is priced at a 10% *discount* to its book value — the market is saying the equity is worth less than its accounting value, which only makes sense if you expect losses to erode that book. As the cycle turns and that fear unwinds, the same bank can re-rate to P/B 1.4x — a perfectly ordinary multiple for a bank earning a healthy ROE. That move from 0.9x to 1.4x is +55% *from the multiple alone*, before a single dong of extra earnings. The bank stock leads precisely because it re-rates on the *removal of fear*, which happens early, not on earnings growth, which happens late.

#### Worked example: the bank re-rating math

Take a bank with a book value per share of 20,000 dong. At the crisis trough it trades at P/B 0.9x, so the share price is 20,000 × 0.9 = **18,000 dong** (about \$0.71). The market is pricing it below book because it fears bad-debt losses. Now the cycle turns: the SBV cuts, NIM bottoms, loan growth resumes, and the fear of mass defaults unwinds. The bank's ROE recovers toward 18% — healthy enough that the market re-rates it to P/B 1.4x. The new price is 20,000 × 1.4 = **28,000 dong** (about \$1.10). That is a gain of 10,000 dong per share, or **+55.6%**, *entirely from the multiple expanding* — book value per share has not even grown yet in this calculation. Layer in some earnings growth (book value compounding at the recovered ROE) and the total return is higher still. The intuition: most of a bank's early-cycle return comes from the valuation re-rating off a distressed multiple, not from earnings — which is why the stock leads the economy.

### Misconception 3: "The rebound is gradual, so there's no rush"

The third myth is that recoveries are slow and gentle, so you can ease in over months without missing much. The data says the opposite: the early-cycle leg is *fast and front-loaded.* The highest-beta sectors do most of their work in the first few months off the trough, when the re-rating and the liquidity return hit together.

The number: in the 2020 post-COVID recovery, VN-Index fell to a low near 660 in late March 2020 and then rallied roughly 60% by the end of the year — and a large chunk of that came in the first few months. In 2023, the broker sector's +75% was similarly concentrated in the bursts right after each SBV rate cut. The rebound is not a gentle ramp you can stroll up; it is a violent snap-back that rewards being positioned *before* the turn confirms. "There's no rush" is how you end up buying the highest-beta sectors *after* they have already doubled.

## How it shows up on VN-Index

The framework is clean in theory; let us pressure-test it against two real Vietnamese recoveries. Both show the same leadership order — brokers, then banks, then cyclicals — triggered by the same signal: the SBV pivoting to cuts and liquidity returning.

### The 2020 post-COVID rebound

In late March 2020, as COVID-19 lockdowns hit, VN-Index crashed to a low near 660 — down roughly 34% from its January level in a matter of weeks. The panic was total. But the SBV responded fast, cutting policy rates multiple times through 2020 to support the economy, and the government flooded the system with liquidity. The result was a textbook early-cycle rebound: VN-Index rallied roughly 60% off the March low to close 2020 near 1,100, and then kept going through 2021 to a peak near 1,500.

The leadership was exactly as the framework predicts. Brokers led — the securities sector exploded as turnover surged (a wave of new retail accounts, the *F0* phenomenon, flooded into the market) and the margin balance ballooned from around 52 trillion dong toward 195 trillion at the 2021 peak. Banks followed, re-rating off cheap COVID-trough valuations as the feared wave of bad debts never fully materialized and credit growth resumed. Steel joined and then *roared* — this was the period of Hoa Phat's (HPG) huge run as global steel prices spiked and domestic construction restarted, with the restocking impulse amplifying the move. The defensives — utilities, staples — lagged the whole way up, exactly as the early-cycle script dictates.

It is worth flagging what was *unusual* about 2020, because it teaches the limits of the framework. The 2020 trough was triggered by an *external shock* (a pandemic) rather than a domestic policy-tightening cycle, so the rebound was supercharged by global factors: central banks worldwide slashed rates to zero and flooded the world with liquidity, global steel prices spiked on a synchronized recovery, and a flood of locked-down retail savers opened brokerage accounts. The leadership *order* was identical to the framework — brokers, then banks, then steel — but the *magnitude* was extreme because the liquidity wave was global, not just Vietnamese. The lesson: the framework tells you the *order* reliably; the *size* of each leg depends on how big the liquidity and credit impulse is, which varies from cycle to cycle. A domestic-only easing (like 2023) produces a solid rebound; a synchronized global easing (like 2020) produces a monster one.

### The 2023 post-bond-crisis rebound

The 2023 recovery is the cleaner case study, because it was a *domestic* liquidity cycle rather than a global shock. The setup: through 2022 the SBV hiked to defend the dong, a corporate-bond crisis froze funding, and VN-Index fell over 40% to a low near 874 in November 2022. Then the pivot: between March and June 2023, the SBV cut its policy rate four times, taking the refinancing rate from 6.0% down to 4.5%. Liquidity returned, and the margin balance turned up off its ~122 trillion dong trough toward ~180 trillion by year-end.

The leadership order played out precisely. Brokers led with roughly +75% on the year — the purest play on the returning liquidity. Steel returned roughly +28% as restocking and a tentative construction recovery fed through. Banks returned roughly +12%, re-rating off distressed valuations as the bad-debt fears that the bond crisis had stoked began to unwind. Real estate, still nursing its bond-crisis hangover and frozen presales, lagged at roughly +5%, and utilities brought up the rear at roughly +3%. The whole leadership ladder — brokers > steel > banks > real estate > utilities — is the early-cycle framework rendered in real returns.

The 2023 case is the more instructive of the two precisely *because* it was unglamorous. There was no pandemic-stimulus euphoria, no global liquidity tsunami — just the SBV, having over-tightened to fight a currency and bond-market scare in 2022, quietly reversing course and cutting four times. The recovery was *driven by the domestic policy pivot alone*, which is the cleanest possible test of the framework. And the framework held: the sectors geared to the earliest links (brokers to liquidity, then banks to credit) led, the cyclical geared to real-demand-plus-restocking (steel) came in strong, and the sectors that needed a fully-healed economy (real estate, with its frozen presales and bond overhang) lagged badly even as the index rose. Real estate's lag is itself a lesson: a sector can be cheap and beaten-down and *still* lag the early-cycle rebound if it is geared to a link in the chain — real demand and unfrozen funding — that has not turned yet. Cheapness is not the same as being early-cycle-geared. Brokers were not the cheapest sector at the trough; they were the most *liquidity-geared*, and that is what made them the leader.

Notice, too, that the *banks lagged steel* in 2023 (+12% vs +28%), which looks like it contradicts the "banks follow second" ordering. It does not — the framework orders the *timing* of when each leg *starts*, not the *total annual return*. Banks turned up before steel did (the credit signal preceded the real-demand signal), but steel's operating leverage and restocking impulse made its *full-year* return larger once it got going. The ordering is about *sequence of leadership*, not magnitude; a later-starting leg with more operating leverage can post a bigger annual number than an earlier-starting one. Keep the two ideas separate: the *order* of the turn, and the *size* of each leg, are different questions.

![SBV refinancing rate step line chart 2022 to 2024 the cuts that triggered early cycle](/imgs/blogs/early-cycle-leaders-vietnam-brokers-banks-steel-3.png)

The chart above is the starting gun for the 2023 rebound. The SBV's refinancing rate held at 6.0% through the 2022 tightening, then stepped down to 4.5% across the 2023 cuts, where it held into 2024. Each downward step is a policy decision that turned the first link in the recovery chain — and the market began leading within weeks of the cuts, long before the economic data improved. The step shape matters: rate policy moves in discrete decisions, and each step down is a signal you can act on the day it prints.

![System margin balance line chart 2022 to 2025 the liquidity signal turning up off the trough](/imgs/blogs/early-cycle-leaders-vietnam-brokers-banks-steel-4.png)

The chart above is the liquidity signal that confirms the turn. The system margin balance — the total amount clients have borrowed from brokers to buy stock — bottomed near 122 trillion dong in 2022, then climbed to ~180 trillion (2023), ~245 trillion (2024), and pushed past ~370 trillion dong by 2025. The *turn* off the 2022 trough is the early-cycle confirmation: when margin starts rising again, liquidity is returning, and the broker-led leg is underway. This is the single cleanest, most actionable liquidity gauge a Vietnamese retail investor can watch — brokers and FiinTrade publish it, and its inflection marks the bottom in real time.

#### Worked example: positioning at the trough versus staying defensive

Suppose you have a 500mn-dong sleeve (about \$19,700 at \$1 = 25,400 VND) and the cycle is at the trough — the SBV has just cut, the margin balance is ticking up. Two choices. **Choice A: stay defensive** — keep the sleeve in utilities and staples, which returned roughly +3% to +5% in 2023. Call it +4% blended: the sleeve grows to 520mn dong, a gain of 20mn dong (about \$790). **Choice B: tilt early-cycle** — split the sleeve 40% brokers / 35% banks / 25% steel. Using the 2023 returns (brokers +75%, banks +12%, steel +28%), the blended return is 0.40 × 75% + 0.35 × 12% + 0.25 × 28% = 30% + 4.2% + 7% = **+41.2%**. The sleeve grows to 706mn dong, a gain of 206mn dong (about \$8,110). The gap between the two choices is 186mn dong (about \$7,320) on a 500mn-dong sleeve — in a single year. The intuition: at the trough, the cost of staying defensive is not "missing a little upside" — it is forgoing the single biggest, most reliable leg of the entire cycle, the one that rewards the highest-beta tilt precisely when it is scariest to take it.

## The signals that mark the turn

The framework is only useful if you can *recognize the trough in real time* — which is hard, because the trough feels like the worst possible moment to buy. The trick is to stop watching sentiment (always terrible at the bottom) and start watching a specific stack of signals that turn in the recovery order. When enough of them line up, the early-cycle green light is on. Here are the four to watch.

### Signal 1: rate cuts

The first and most important signal is the SBV pivoting from hikes (or a hold) to **cuts.** This is the policy turn — the first link in the chain. In Vietnam, watch the SBV's refinancing rate and discount rate, and watch open-market operations and the credit ceiling for loosening. A single cut is a hint; a *sequence* of cuts (as in the four cuts of 2023) is a clear pivot. The rate cut is the starting gun because it makes cash less attractive, lowers funding costs for banks and brokers, and signals that policy is now working *for* the market rather than against it. Cross-reference the dedicated post on [Vietnam's monetary policy, the State Bank, and the credit ceiling](/blog/trading/finance/vietnam-monetary-policy-state-bank-dong-credit-ceiling) for the full mechanics.

### Signal 2: the margin balance turning up

The second signal is the **system margin balance turning up off its trough** — the cleanest liquidity gauge in the Vietnamese market. When clients start borrowing again to buy stock, liquidity is returning, and the highest-beta sectors (brokers first) are about to lead. Watch the brokers' published margin figures and FiinTrade's aggregate. A turn from the trough — say, margin up 10-20% off its low and rising — is the confirmation that the broker-led leg is underway. This is the most *actionable* early signal because it inflects almost immediately after the rate cuts, weeks before any earnings or GDP data. The mechanics of the margin cycle are in [liquidity and the margin cycle in Vietnam](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/liquidity-and-the-margin-cycle-vietnam).

### Signal 3: credit acceleration

The third signal is **credit growth accelerating** — banks lending more, the SBV loosening the credit ceiling, total system credit ticking up month over month. This is the link that turns banks from "cheap and re-rating" into "cheap, re-rating, *and* growing the loan book." Credit acceleration is a slightly later signal than the margin turn — it shows up in the monthly credit-growth data and the SBV's credit-ceiling announcements — but it confirms that the recovery is broadening from pure liquidity into real lending. When credit accelerates, the banks' leg of the rebound is on firm ground.

### Signal 4: the relative-strength flip

The fourth signal is technical: **relative strength (RS) leadership flipping** to the early-cycle sectors. Relative strength measures how a sector is performing *relative to the index* — a sector with rising RS is outperforming, a sector with falling RS is lagging. At the trough, you watch for the broker and bank sectors' RS lines to flip from falling to rising — that is the market *confirming* the rotation with real money, not just the macro setup implying it. The RS flip often *leads* the fundamental data, because price moves first. The full method is in [reading relative strength to see which sector leads VN-Index](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/relative-strength-reading-which-sector-leads-vn-index). When brokers and banks start outperforming the index on a sustained basis, the rotation has begun.

The matrix above is the early-cycle checklist in one frame. Each row is one signal — rate cuts, margin balance up, credit accelerating, RS flip — paired with what to watch (the concrete data series or print) and what it confirms (which link in the recovery chain has turned). Read top to bottom, the rows are roughly in the order the signals fire: policy first, then liquidity, then credit, then the price confirmation. When the top two are lit, you are early and aggressive; when all four are lit, the rotation is established and you are confirming, not predicting.

#### Worked example: the signal stack confirming the turn

Suppose it is early 2023. You are watching the stack. **Signal 1:** the SBV cuts its refinancing rate from 6.0% to 5.5%, then to 5.0% — a clear pivot to cutting. Green. **Signal 2:** the system margin balance, which bottomed near 122 trillion dong, ticks up to 146 trillion — up about 20% off the trough and rising. Green. **Signal 3:** monthly credit growth, which had stalled, accelerates as the SBV loosens the credit ceiling. Green. With three of four signals lit, the early-cycle green light is on — and crucially, *the GDP data is still bad and the news is still about the bond crisis.* You position into brokers and banks here, while it is uncomfortable. By the time the fourth signal (the RS flip) is unambiguous and the news has turned positive, the broker sector is already well into its +75% run. The intuition: the signal stack lets you confirm the *mechanical* turn (liquidity and credit) before the *narrative* turn (good news and good data), which is the entire edge of the early-cycle trade.

## The playbook: how to trade the early-cycle rebound

Now the payoff — the concrete playbook for positioning at the trough. This is where the framework becomes a set of decisions: what to buy, in what order, on what signals, and what invalidates the view.

### The core position: brokers, then banks, then steel

The position mirrors the leadership order. **Lead with brokers** — they are the highest-beta proxy for the returning liquidity that turns first, and they deliver the biggest move. Overweight the liquid broker names (SSI, VND, HCM, VCI) as a basket; the sector move dominates single-name selection in the first leg. **Add banks** as the credit signal confirms — overweight the cheap, well-capitalized names trading below or near book value, because the re-rating off distressed multiples is the engine of the bank leg. **Add steel and early cyclicals** (HPG the obvious anchor) as the restocking and demand signals turn — this is the third leg, geared to real activity rather than pure liquidity.

A reasonable early-cycle tilt for an aggressive investor at the trough might be something like 35-40% brokers, 30-35% banks, 20-25% steel/cyclicals, with the rest in cash to add on confirmation. The exact weights matter less than the *order and the tilt*: you are deliberately overweight the highest-beta, most liquidity-geared sectors, and deliberately underweight the defensives (utilities, staples) that lag the rebound. The defensives are wrong for the turn — they are what you owned in the recession to preserve capital, and the early-cycle move is precisely when you rotate *out* of them.

The figure above contrasts the two portfolios at the trough. On the left, the defensive-heavy book — utilities, staples, cash — that felt safe through the recession but is *wrong for the turn*, because it has almost no gearing to the rebound and lags the whole way up. On the right, the early-cycle-tilted book — brokers, banks, steel — built to capture the leadership order. The lesson of the before/after is that the portfolio that kept you safe in the recession is the exact portfolio you must *abandon* at the trough; holding the defensive book through the early-cycle leg is how you watch the biggest move of the cycle go by from the sidelines.

### The entry signals: when to pull the trigger

Enter when the signal stack lights up, *not* when the news turns. Concretely: the trigger is a **rate cut (or a sequence of cuts) plus the margin balance turning up off its trough** — that is the minimum green light, and it lets you lead with brokers. Add to banks as **credit accelerates and the SBV loosens the credit ceiling.** Add to steel as **steel prices stabilize and restocking signals appear** (distributor inventories rebuilding, order books filling). Confirm the whole rotation with the **RS flip** — brokers and banks sustainably outperforming the index. You do not need all four signals to start; the first two (rate cut + margin turn) are enough to begin building the broker leg, because waiting for full confirmation means buying after the first and biggest move.

### Sizing and staging

Stage the entry. Because the trough is genuinely uncertain — you cannot know the *exact* low, and there can be a final flush — build the position in tranches rather than all at once. A reasonable approach: deploy perhaps a third of the intended early-cycle allocation when the first two signals (rate cut + margin turn) fire, another third as credit accelerates and the RS flip confirms, and the final third on a pullback within the established uptrend. This staging respects the reality that you are buying into fear and the bottom is a *process*, not a point — while still ensuring you are positioned before the bulk of the move.

Make it concrete. Say your intended early-cycle allocation is a 300mn-dong sleeve (about \$11,800 at \$1 = 25,400 VND), tilted brokers/banks/steel. You stage it in three 100mn-dong tranches. **Tranche 1 (100mn dong)** goes in when the SBV's first cut prints and the margin balance ticks up off its trough — this is the scary one, bought while the news is still about the crisis, and it captures the start of the broker leg. **Tranche 2 (100mn dong)** goes in a quarter later, when credit growth accelerates and the broker/bank RS lines have flipped to rising — you are now *confirming* the trend, paying a bit more but with much higher conviction, and you weight this tranche toward banks as the credit signal matures. **Tranche 3 (100mn dong)** goes in on the first real pullback within the now-established uptrend, weighted toward steel as the restocking signal confirms — you are adding to a winning position on a dip, not chasing. The averaged entry sits well below where the sector ends the year, you were positioned for the bulk of the move, and you never had to call the exact bottom. The staging is what lets you act on the framework *despite* the uncertainty of the trough — you trade the sequence, not the single perfect entry.

### The invalidation: when you are wrong

Every trade needs a line that says "I was wrong; get out." For the early-cycle thesis, the invalidation is the recovery chain *breaking* before it completes. Concretely: if the **SBV reverses and starts hiking again** (a renewed inflation or currency shock forcing policy back to tightening), or if the **margin balance rolls back over** and resumes falling after a tentative turn, or if **credit fails to accelerate** despite the cuts (a credit-crunch where banks won't lend even at lower rates — a "liquidity trap"), then the early-cycle setup has failed and you cut the high-beta exposure. The brokers that lead hardest on the way up fall hardest if the recovery aborts, so the discipline is symmetric: the same signals that gave you the green light, reversing, are your stop. The most dangerous failure mode is a *false dawn* — a bounce that fades because the policy pivot was not real or the credit channel stayed frozen — and the margin balance rolling back over is your earliest warning of it.

### Putting it together

The early-cycle trade is, at its heart, a bet that the recovery chain turns in its usual order — policy, liquidity, credit, earnings, economy — and that the market leads the economy rather than lagging it. You position into the sectors geared to the *earliest* links (brokers for liquidity, banks for credit) before the *later* links (earnings, GDP) confirm. You watch the signal stack — rate cuts, margin turning up, credit accelerating, RS flipping — to confirm the mechanical turn before the narrative turn. And you hold the discipline to buy when it is scariest and to cut if the chain breaks. Done well, it captures the single biggest and most reliable leg of the entire cycle. The whole game is summarized in the rotation framework covered in [sector rotation explained: leaders and laggards](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/sector-rotation-explained-leaders-and-laggards) — early cycle is the first and most lucrative station on that wheel.

A final word on the *psychology*, because it is the real obstacle and no amount of framework fixes it for you. Every signal in the stack will be flashing green at the exact moment your gut is screaming "don't buy." The rate cut comes because the economy is in trouble; the margin balance is turning up off a trough it reached because the market crashed; the news is full of the bankruptcies and frozen markets that *caused* the cut. The framework asks you to buy the highest-beta sectors in the market — brokers, with their 6x beta — into that fear. This is genuinely hard, and most investors cannot do it; they wait for the news to feel safe, and by then the first leg is gone. The discipline that makes the early-cycle trade work is the willingness to trust the *mechanism* over the *mood*: to act on the signal stack rather than the sentiment, knowing that the sentiment is *designed* to be worst exactly when the opportunity is best. The market bottoms when the last seller is exhausted and the news is still terrible — which is precisely the moment the framework tells you to lead with brokers. Learn to read the stack, size with tranches so you never have to be precisely right about the bottom, keep the invalidation signals ready in case the recovery aborts, and you turn the scariest moment in the cycle into the most rewarding trade in the playbook.
