January 2026. A Turkish steel mill ships 50,000 tonnes of rebar to Hamburg. The vessel clears customs, the bill of lading is stamped, and then — something new happens. The German importer receives a demand from the EU CBAM registry: purchase carbon certificates covering the embedded emissions of that shipment.

The math is brutal in its simplicity. Fifty thousand tonnes of rebar, carbon intensity of approximately 1.8 tCO2 per tonne of steel, multiplied by the EU ETS carbon price of roughly EUR 65 per tonne CO2. That is 90,000 tonnes of CO2, times EUR 65, equals EUR 5.85 million. On one shipment. In addition to all existing freight, insurance, and commercial markups. For a product that, under old rules, crossed the border with zero explicit carbon cost attached.

This is the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism — CBAM — and it represents the most consequential reimagining of international trade finance since the World Trade Organization's founding in 1995. It is simultaneously a genuine climate instrument, a formidable industrial policy weapon, and the flashpoint for a new era of geopolitical confrontation between the EU and the developing world.

> [!important]
> **TL;DR — Carbon Border Adjustment in Five Points**
>
> 1. **What it is**: CBAM is a carbon tariff that forces non-EU exporters to pay for the embedded CO2 in their goods when selling into the EU market, matching what EU producers pay under the Emissions Trading System.
> 2. **Who pays most**: Steel, aluminum, cement, fertilizers, and electricity exporters from high-carbon-intensity economies — China, India, Turkey, Russia — face the largest bills.
> 3. **The political flashpoint**: Developing nations call it climate colonialism; India has filed WTO disputes; China is accelerating its domestic carbon market partly to gain deductibility credits.
> 4. **The market transmission**: EU ETS carbon prices directly set CBAM certificate costs, making ETS futures a tradeable proxy for emerging-market steel competitiveness.
> 5. **The timeline**: Full payment phase began January 2026; free allowances phase out at 10% per year through 2034, meaning the full EUR bite compounds annually through the decade.

## Foundations: The Carbon Leakage Problem and Why Economists Demanded a Border Tax

To understand CBAM, you first need to understand the problem it was designed to solve. And that problem — carbon leakage — is not a hypothetical. It is a mathematically inevitable consequence of any unilateral carbon pricing regime.

### The EU ETS: A Carbon Price Without a Border

The European Union Emissions Trading System, launched in 2005, is the world's largest mandatory carbon market. Covered industries — power generation, heavy manufacturing, aviation — must hold EU Allowances (EUAs) equivalent to their annual CO2 emissions. Total allowances are capped and reduced each year, creating scarcity and thus a price. As of 2025, that price hovers around EUR 60–70 per tonne of CO2.

For European industry, this is a real cost. A steel mill running blast furnace technology emits approximately 2.1 tonnes of CO2 per tonne of steel produced. At EUR 65/tonne, that is EUR 136.50 in carbon cost baked into every tonne of European steel — before raw materials, labor, or energy. A German steelmaker competes with a Turkish or Indian mill that pays no such charge at home (or pays a much smaller one).

The rational economic response is predictable: if Europe imposes carbon costs but its trading partners do not, European manufacturers either pass costs to consumers, relocate production abroad, or lose market share to cheaper carbon-unpriced imports. In every scenario, the net global emissions effect is weak or negative. European production declines, foreign production — often dirtier — rises to fill the gap. CO2 molecules do not respect national borders.

Economists have understood this since the 1990s. The phrase "carbon leakage" entered policy lexicon to describe the phenomenon: every tonne of CO2 eliminated from European industry is partly offset by CO2 increases elsewhere as market share shifts. Estimates of carbon leakage rates in sectors like steel and cement range from 5% to 25% without border corrections — meaning that for every 100 tonnes Europe reduces domestically, 5 to 25 tonnes flow back through trade channels.

### What CBAM Is Designed to Do

CBAM's theoretical elegance is in its symmetry. If EU producers must pay EUR 65/tonne for their embedded carbon, importers selling into the EU must also pay EUR 65/tonne for their embedded carbon — minus any carbon price already paid in the country of origin. The playing field is leveled not by raising tariffs on foreign goods, but by ensuring that carbon costs are reflected regardless of where production occurs.

This symmetry is also CBAM's primary legal defense at the WTO. Unlike a conventional tariff, which discriminates by national origin, CBAM in theory discriminates only by carbon intensity. A clean Turkish steel mill — if one produces at EU-level carbon efficiency — would face minimal CBAM costs. A dirty German steelmaker (hypothetically) importing its own steel from a third country would face the same calculation. The mechanism is designed to be carbon-neutral rather than trade-protectionist, though the distinction matters enormously for WTO compatibility.

### Embedded Carbon: The Technical Heart of the System

The technical concept of "embedded carbon" (sometimes called "embodied carbon" or "cradle-to-gate emissions") is the foundation on which CBAM rests. It measures the total CO2 equivalent emitted in producing a good, from raw material extraction through the final production stage, expressed in tonnes of CO2 per tonne of output.

For steel, the embedded carbon calculation covers iron ore processing, coking coal consumption in blast furnaces (or electricity in electric arc furnaces), and direct process emissions. For cement, it includes the highly problematic calcination reaction — limestone heated to release CO2 — which accounts for roughly 60% of cement's embedded carbon regardless of energy source. For fertilizers, it covers the Haber-Bosch process for ammonia synthesis, which currently depends almost entirely on natural gas reforming.

CBAM allows importers to use either declared values (the actual measured emissions from the specific production facility, verified by an accredited third-party verifier) or default values (EU-determined averages by country and product that err on the higher side). In the early years, most exporters from non-EU countries will default to default values, since building third-party verification infrastructure takes years. Default values are intentionally punitive — they use the average emissions intensity of the worst-performing EU producers as a baseline, which for countries like China in steel is actually favorable, while for countries like India in aluminum it can be significantly worse than actual performance.

### CBAM Certificate Mechanics

CBAM certificates are purchased through a national authority in each EU member state and priced weekly based on the average EU ETS auction price. Unlike EUAs (which can be traded on secondary markets), CBAM certificates cannot be sold — they are surrendered upon filing the annual CBAM declaration, or cancelled if the underlying import falls through. Unused certificates purchased in a given year can be repurchased by the national authority at the purchase price.

The surrender deadline is May 31 of the year following import. So goods entering the EU in calendar year 2026 require a CBAM declaration and certificate surrender by May 31, 2027. This creates a one-year lag between the import event and the cash outflow for the certificate, giving importers and exporters roughly a year to assess exposure before payment.

Crucially, the CBAM deduction mechanism is real but bureaucratically demanding. If an exporting-country producer can demonstrate that they have already paid a carbon price in their home jurisdiction — for example, a Chinese steel mill covered by China's national ETS — that paid price (converted to EUR) is deductible from the CBAM certificate obligation. If China's ETS price is, say, EUR 10/tonne and the EU ETS is EUR 65/tonne, the Chinese producer owes the EUR 55 difference. This mechanism is the diplomatic escape valve the EU built into CBAM: countries that develop credible carbon pricing get rewarded; countries that do not pay the full tariff.

## The EU ETS Carbon Price: The Beating Heart of CBAM

You cannot understand CBAM without understanding EU ETS price dynamics, because every euro change in the ETS carbon price flows directly into CBAM certificate costs for importers.

![EU ETS carbon price 2018 to 2025 showing surge and stabilization](/imgs/blogs/carbon-border-adjustment-the-new-trade-weapon-2.png)

The EU ETS price trajectory from 2018 to 2025 tells a story in three acts. Act One (2018–2020): the market was recovering from a decade of oversupply. The EU had accumulated a massive surplus of allowances from the 2008 financial crisis, when industrial output collapsed but allowances continued to be issued. The Market Stability Reserve — enacted in 2019 — began removing surplus allowances from the market, driving the price from EUR 4-5 in 2017 to EUR 25 by late 2019.

Act Two (2021–2023): the carbon price exploded. The combination of surging post-COVID industrial demand, natural gas price spikes driving coal-to-gas switching dynamics in power generation, and EU policy ambition (Fit for 55 package announced in July 2021 with plans to accelerate the cap reduction trajectory) sent ETS prices from EUR 25 at the start of 2021 to EUR 85 by February 2023. This was the period when CBAM went from a theoretical concept to an acute commercial threat for steel and aluminum exporters globally.

Act Three (2023–2025): correction and consolidation. EU industrial output weakened, gas prices normalized after the initial Russia-Ukraine shock, and carbon prices retreated to EUR 55–70, settling around EUR 65 by 2025. This is the price range that defines the current CBAM baseline.

The ETS price also reveals an important structural feature: it is highly volatile in the short term (20–30% swings within a quarter are common) but shows a clear long-term upward trend driven by the declining cap. For exporters building CBAM cost projections, hedging ETS exposure is becoming a new dimension of financial risk management — one that most emerging-market steel mills have no existing infrastructure to handle.

## The Covered Sectors: Who Pays, and How Much

CBAM in its current form covers six sectors chosen for their combination of carbon intensity, trade volume, and carbon leakage risk: steel and iron, aluminum, cement, fertilizers, electricity, and hydrogen.

![CBAM sector coverage matrix by carbon intensity and EU import volume](/imgs/blogs/carbon-border-adjustment-the-new-trade-weapon-3.png)

Steel is the flagship sector. The EU imports roughly 35–40 million tonnes of steel products annually, of which approximately 15–18 million tonnes come from countries now covered by CBAM. China is the largest non-EU steel source, followed by India, Turkey, South Korea, and Japan. The carbon intensity of steel production varies enormously by technology:

- **Blast furnace / basic oxygen furnace (BF/BOF)**: 2.0–2.4 tCO2 per tonne of crude steel, using coking coal as both fuel and reducing agent. This is the dominant technology in China (~90% of production), India (~55%), and Turkey (~60%).
- **Electric arc furnace (EAF)**: 0.4–0.7 tCO2 per tonne using scrap steel and electricity, with emissions heavily dependent on the electricity grid's carbon intensity. EAF is dominant in Turkey's coastal mills (60–70% of coastal capacity), the US, and increasingly Europe.
- **Direct reduced iron + EAF (DRI-EAF)**: 0.6–1.2 tCO2 per tonne using natural gas for reduction. This is ArcelorMittal's chosen green steel transition path and is becoming the benchmark for "low-carbon" primary steel production.

Aluminum faces an even more dramatic CBAM exposure due to the extraordinarily high electricity intensity of primary aluminum smelting. Primary aluminum produced from bauxite via the Hall-Héroult electrolysis process consumes approximately 13–15 MWh of electricity per tonne. When that electricity comes from coal-fired power (as in China and India), the embedded carbon reaches 10–15 tCO2 per tonne. At EUR 65/tonne CO2, the CBAM liability on a tonne of Chinese primary aluminum could reach EUR 650–975. This makes aluminum one of the most dramatically affected commodities under CBAM.

Cement faces a unique challenge: approximately 60% of its embedded carbon comes from the calcination reaction (CaCO3 → CaO + CO2) which is a chemical process not reducible by switching energy sources. Even "clean" cement made with renewable electricity still emits roughly 0.5 tCO2/tonne from calcination alone. Full decarbonization of cement requires either carbon capture and storage (CCS) or clinker substitutes — neither of which is yet commercially viable at scale.

Fertilizers — specifically urea, ammonia nitrate, and related nitrogen fertilizers — are produced from ammonia synthesized via the Haber-Bosch process, which in current commercial practice uses natural gas as both a hydrogen source and energy input. The embedded carbon of gray ammonia is approximately 2.4 tCO2 per tonne, making nitrogen fertilizer CBAM costs significant for exporters from Russia, Egypt, and the Gulf states.

## The Political Calculus: Green Deal Meets Geopolitical Confrontation

CBAM did not emerge from a vacuum. It is the trade face of the European Green Deal — the EU's comprehensive decarbonization agenda launched in December 2019 — and specifically of the Fit for 55 package (July 2021), which set binding targets of 55% emissions reduction by 2030 versus 1990 levels.

Within the EU, CBAM was the political compromise that made ETS reform acceptable to heavy industry. European steelmakers and cement producers had for years received free ETS allowances — a subsidy that partially compensated for carbon costs and enabled them to compete with unpriced imports. As the EU commission planned to accelerate allowance reductions and phase out free allocations, industry lobbied ferociously: without a border adjustment, phase-out of free allowances would simply drive European manufacturing offshore. CBAM was the quid pro quo. Industry accepted accelerated ETS ambition in exchange for the promise of a level playing field.

The phase-out schedule is mechanically precise: CBAM obligations phase in as free allowances phase out, at a 1:1 ratio. In 2026, the first full CBAM payment year, EU producers still receive approximately 90% of their historical free allowances. Importers pay CBAM on 10% of embedded carbon. Each year through 2034, the free allowance share drops by roughly 10 percentage points. By 2034, free allowances are zero, and CBAM applies to 100% of embedded carbon. The total financial exposure of non-EU exporters roughly decouples annually through the decade.

### The US IRA: Parallel Carbon Competition

CBAM does not exist in isolation. The US Inflation Reduction Act (August 2022) represents a parallel — and in some ways competing — approach to industrial decarbonization. Rather than a carbon price/border mechanism, the IRA uses massive subsidies: tax credits for clean hydrogen production (up to \$3/kg under Section 45V), clean electricity production, and domestic manufacturing of solar panels, wind turbines, and electric vehicle batteries.

This creates an interesting geopolitical dynamic: both the EU and the US are restructuring their industrial bases around carbon, but using opposite instruments. The EU uses the carbon price stick (ETS) and the border enforcement mechanism (CBAM). The US uses the green subsidy carrot (IRA). Both approaches disadvantage foreign producers, but in different ways. Under CBAM, a Turkish steel exporter pays a carbon tariff at the EU border. Under IRA, a Korean steel producer finds it cannot compete with US-subsidized domestic production for American EV supply chains. The net effect — carbon-unpriced imports being systematically disadvantaged in the major developed-economy markets — is structurally similar, even as the legal instruments differ.

The "carbon club" concept — the idea of creating a coalition of nations with equivalent carbon prices that would mutually exempt each other from border taxes while presenting a unified front to non-participating countries — has been discussed at the G7. Germany and France have pushed this concept most actively. A functioning carbon club of EU plus US plus Canada plus UK plus Japan would cover the majority of global manufacturing demand. Non-members would face carbon border taxes at every major import destination. For developing economies, this would not be a trade weapon — it would be a trade siege.

### Turkey and India: The Key Battlegrounds

Turkey occupies a unique position in the CBAM geopolitical map. It is simultaneously a NATO member, an EU accession candidate (technically, though membership talks have been frozen since 2016), a major steel exporter to the EU, and a country with its own nascent carbon market. Turkey's steel sector is its most CBAM-exposed industry: Turkish steel mills exported approximately 3.8 million tonnes to the EU in 2023, much of it from coastal EAF-based producers with relatively lower carbon intensity than their BF/BOF counterparts in China and India.

Turkey's response has been multi-track. Commercially, Turkish mills with high scrap-based EAF production have actually seen a relative competitiveness improvement versus Chinese blast-furnace steel in the EU market — a paradox where CBAM damages high-carbon producers more than low-carbon ones. Diplomatically, Turkey has formally objected to CBAM at the WTO and in bilateral negotiations with the EU, arguing that its long-standing customs union relationship with the EU should have afforded it preferential treatment. Practically, Turkey accelerated development of its own voluntary carbon market and has been in discussions with the EU about formal recognition under CBAM deductibility provisions.

India's response has been sharper. The Indian government filed a formal WTO dispute in 2023, with Trade Minister Piyush Goyal explicitly framing CBAM as "climate colonialism" — the accusation that wealthy nations, having industrialized on cheap carbon for 150 years, are now erecting carbon barriers that prevent developing economies from the same industrial growth path. India's CBAM exposure spans steel, aluminum, and fertilizers — all sectors where Indian producers are cost-competitive on labor and raw materials but carbon-intensive by EU standards.

The "climate colonialism" framing resonates deeply in the Global South. The historical argument is not frivolous: developed economies account for the overwhelming majority of cumulative historical greenhouse gas emissions (the US and EU alone account for roughly 50% of the cumulative stock of CO2 in the atmosphere since 1850). Asking India or Nigeria to decarbonize their steel sectors — at enormous cost and with technology largely developed in the Global North — while facing a trade barrier if they fail to do so fast enough, carries real moral and political weight. CBAM's architects have tried to build in some accommodation through deductibility for domestic carbon prices and through the EU's general CBAM revenue pledge to support climate finance in developing countries, but these provisions are widely seen as inadequate offsets for the trade barrier impact.

## How CBAM Works in Practice: Calculation Mechanics

For importers and their compliance teams, CBAM is simultaneously conceptually simple and operationally complex. The conceptual logic — measure embedded carbon, multiply by ETS price, pay the difference versus carbon already paid — is clear. The operational reality involves verifier accreditation, installation-level data collection, complex averaging rules, and an evolving regulatory framework that has already been revised multiple times since the transitional phase began.

**Step 1: Identify covered goods.** CBAM applies to goods classified under specific Combined Nomenclature codes within the six covered sectors. Steel products include everything from crude steel to complex forms like tubes, pipes, and wire. Aluminum includes primary and secondary aluminum and a growing list of downstream products. Cement includes clinker and calcined clays. Getting the CN code classification right is non-trivial for complex products.

**Step 2: Determine the production installation.** CBAM requires the importer to specify the specific installation (factory, smelter, or kiln) that produced the goods. This is a significant departure from normal customs practice, which typically requires only the country of origin. Identifying the exact installation and obtaining installation-level carbon data requires either direct engagement with the producing facility or reliance on default values.

**Step 3: Calculate embedded emissions.** Using either declared values from the installation (verified by an accredited verifier) or EU default values by country and product category, the importer calculates the total CO2-equivalent embedded in the shipment. For steel, this includes both direct emissions from the production process and indirect emissions from consumed electricity (though the electricity calculation methodology differs from direct emission calculations).

**Step 4: Deduct carbon already paid.** If the exporting facility is covered by a mandatory carbon pricing scheme in its home country, the carbon price paid — properly documented — can be deducted from the CBAM obligation. This deduction is capped at the EU ETS price; if a country's carbon price exceeds the EU price (hypothetically), only the EU ETS price equivalent can be deducted.

**Step 5: Purchase CBAM certificates.** The net certificate obligation (embedded carbon minus deduction, multiplied by the CBAM-year phase-in percentage) is covered by purchasing CBAM certificates at the EU national authority at the weekly average ETS price. Certificates are surrendered at the May 31 annual declaration deadline.

### The Free Allowance Phase-Out: The Compounding Trap

The interaction between CBAM's ramp-up and the EU ETS free allowance phase-out creates a compounding cost pressure for European industry that has important downstream effects. European steel mills currently receive free ETS allowances covering most of their direct emissions (with the free allowance quantity benchmarked against the best-performing 10% of EU installations in the relevant product category). As free allowances decline, European producers face rising explicit carbon costs on their own domestic production — even as CBAM theoretically levels the import playing field.

This means that the period 2026–2034 sees simultaneous cost escalation for both EU domestic producers (rising carbon costs as free allowances decline) and non-EU importers (rising CBAM obligations). The net effect on competitiveness depends critically on who manages the transition to lower-carbon production faster. European mills investing in green hydrogen-based DRI-EAF technology today will emerge from the transition period with dramatically lower carbon intensity — and thus dramatically lower ETS costs — while competitors still running blast furnaces face an ever-rising CBAM bill.

## Worked Examples: The Actual Math Behind the Headlines

#### Worked example:

**The Turkish Rebar Shipment**

A Turkish EAF steel mill in Izmir ships 50,000 tonnes of construction rebar (CN code 7214) to a German distributor. The mill operates with carbon intensity of 1.8 tCO2 per tonne of steel (EAF with Turkish grid electricity, approximately 0.55 tCO2/MWh).

Total embedded carbon = 50,000 tonnes × 1.8 tCO2/tonne = 90,000 tCO2

The shipment arrives in January 2026. In 2026, CBAM applies to 10% of the embedded carbon (since EU free allowances are being phased out at 10% per year starting 2026).

CBAM-liable emissions = 90,000 × 10% = 9,000 tCO2

CBAM certificate cost = 9,000 × EUR 65 (ETS price) = EUR 585,000

Turkey does not yet have a recognized mandatory carbon pricing scheme for steel, so the deduction is zero.

The EUR 585,000 CBAM cost on a 50,000-tonne shipment equals EUR 11.70/tonne in 2026. At the 2034 full phase-in (100%), the same shipment would generate a CBAM bill of 90,000 × EUR 65 = EUR 5.85 million — EUR 117/tonne. At EUR 80/tonne ETS price (a plausible 2034 scenario), it becomes EUR 144/tonne. That is approximately 20–25% of current rebar market prices, making it a decisive competitive variable.

#### Worked example:

**The Chinese Steel Arbitrage Window**

Chinese blast-furnace steel FOB Shanghai: \$520/tonne (rebar equivalent, 2025 pricing)
EU domestic HRC (hot-rolled coil): \$650/tonne ex-works Germany

The current apparent arbitrage: \$130/tonne in favor of Chinese import.

CBAM cost breakdown for Chinese steel (BF/BOF, carbon intensity 2.1 tCO2/tonne):

In 2026 (10% phase-in): CBAM = 2.1 × EUR 65 × 10% = EUR 13.65/tonne
In 2030 (approximately 60% phase-in): CBAM = 2.1 × EUR 65 × 60% = EUR 81.9/tonne
In 2034 (100% phase-in): CBAM = 2.1 × EUR 65 × 100% = EUR 136.5/tonne (approximately \$147/tonne)

At what ETS price does the 2034 full-phase arbitrage completely close?

Price gap \$130/tonne ÷ 2.1 tCO2/tonne = \$61.9/tonne CO2 equivalent.
At 2034 exchange rates (assume EUR/USD 1.05), that is EUR 59/tonne CO2.

Conclusion: if the EU ETS price is above approximately EUR 59/tonne (it has been continuously since 2021), Chinese blast-furnace steel effectively cannot compete in the EU market by 2034 on price alone without either decarbonizing or dramatically cutting production costs elsewhere. The arbitrage window closes.

#### Worked example:

**Carbon Permit Portfolio: ETS Exposure**

A mid-sized EU steel importer regularly buys 200,000 tonnes per year of non-EU steel (carbon intensity 2.0 tCO2/tonne average). In 2026, their CBAM-liable emissions at 10% phase-in = 200,000 × 2.0 × 10% = 40,000 tCO2.

The importer buys EUR 100,000 worth of CBAM certificates at EUR 65/tonne in January 2026, covering approximately 1,538 tCO2.

Scenario A: ETS price rises from EUR 65 to EUR 90 by Q4 2026. The importer now faces a EUR 25/tonne overage on uncovered certificates still to be purchased for the year. For the remaining 38,462 tCO2 needed (at 40,000 tCO2 total - 1,538 already purchased), the additional cost = 38,462 × EUR 25 = approximately EUR 961,538 in unexpected liability.

Scenario B: The importer had hedged by buying ETS futures at EUR 65 when planning the trade year. ETS rises to EUR 90. The hedge gains EUR 25/tonne × 40,000 tCO2 = EUR 1,000,000 P&L.

The lesson: CBAM creates a direct ETS price exposure for non-EU importers — the same financial risk that EU industrial companies have been managing for 20 years via ETS hedging programs. As of 2025, almost no emerging-market steel exporter has this hedging infrastructure in place.

#### Worked example:

**ArcelorMittal Green Steel CBAM Advantage**

ArcelorMittal's Hamburg DRI-EAF plant (Stahl-Werk Becker acquisition basis) operates at approximately 0.8 tCO2 per tonne of steel using natural gas-based DRI. Compare to a Chinese BF/BOF competitor at 2.1 tCO2/tonne.

CBAM advantage at 2034 full phase-in (EUR 65 ETS price):
CBAM cost differential = (2.1 - 0.8) × EUR 65 = 1.3 × EUR 65 = EUR 84.5/tonne

This EUR 84.5/tonne competitive advantage accrues entirely to EU-based or EU-certified low-carbon producers relative to carbon-intensive imports. At EUR 80/tonne ETS (plausible upside): advantage = 1.3 × EUR 80 = EUR 104/tonne.

At EUR 104/tonne competitive differential, EU green steel becomes not merely competitive with Chinese blast-furnace steel in the EU market — it becomes decisively cheaper when CBAM fully applies. This is the structural transformation CBAM is designed to drive.

If ArcelorMittal completes its planned DRI-EAF transition at Bremen (2026) and Dunkirk (2028) on schedule, the company's CBAM-adjusted position by 2030 versus Chinese blast-furnace imports would be equivalent to receiving a EUR 80–100/tonne production subsidy. This explains ArcelorMittal's strong support for CBAM and its aggressive lobbying for full, rapid implementation.

## The Geopolitical Blowback: From WTO Disputes to Carbon Clubs

The diplomatic fallout from CBAM has been swift and substantial. Three distinct blocs of objectors have emerged, each with different legal strategies and economic stakes.

### India's Legal Challenge

India filed a formal WTO dispute request against CBAM in 2023, arguing that the mechanism violates the WTO's fundamental non-discrimination principles. The legal arguments are technically sophisticated:

**Most Favored Nation (MFN) violation**: India argues that CBAM discriminates between imported and domestic goods on the basis of carbon pricing in the country of origin — which is a national policy choice, not a product characteristic. Since WTO rules require that like products from different member countries be treated equally (regardless of domestic policy choices in those countries), CBAM may violate MFN by imposing higher costs on goods from countries without carbon pricing.

**National Treatment violation**: CBAM may also violate the national treatment principle (Article III of GATT) by subjecting imported goods to charges not applied to domestic goods sold in the EU — even though the stated goal is equivalence, the implementation creates de facto discrimination given verification and default value asymmetries.

**GATT Article XX defense**: The EU's primary legal defense is GATT Article XX(b) (measures necessary to protect human, animal, or plant life or health) and Article XX(g) (measures relating to the conservation of exhaustible natural resources). Climate change qualifies under these carve-outs — the question is whether CBAM is designed as a genuine conservation measure or as a disguised restriction on international trade (the "chapeau" test of Article XX).

The WTO dispute is proceeding through its early consultation stage as of 2026. A panel ruling, if it comes, would likely take three to five years. In the meantime, CBAM operates as law.

### China's Domestic Carbon Market Strategy

China's response to CBAM has been characteristically long-term and strategic. Rather than primarily challenging CBAM at the WTO, China has accelerated development of its national ETS — launched in 2021 but initially covering only power generation — with the explicit goal of expanding it to steel, cement, and aluminum and achieving a recognized carbon price high enough to trigger CBAM deductibility credits.

China's ETS carbon price in 2025 is approximately CNY 80–100/tonne (roughly EUR 10–13/tonne) — far below the EU ETS level. But the trajectory matters: China has announced plans to include steel, aluminum, and building materials sectors in the national ETS by 2026–2027. If Chinese steel mills are covered by a recognized mandatory carbon price, even at EUR 10–15/tonne, they can deduct that amount from their CBAM obligation. The net CBAM cost drops from EUR 65 to EUR 50–55/tonne — meaningful but not decisive.

The deeper strategic goal is to use CBAM's deductibility mechanism as an incentive for building domestic carbon market infrastructure. In this sense, CBAM may actually accelerate global carbon pricing adoption more effectively than any international agreement — because countries that develop credible carbon markets gain a concrete commercial benefit (CBAM deductibility), while countries that do not pay the full EU tariff.

### The Developing World Coalition

Beyond India and China, a broader coalition of developing countries has mobilized around CBAM as a focal point for demanding climate finance and technology transfer. The G77+China bloc (now over 130 developing nations) has used CBAM as leverage in climate negotiations, arguing that developed countries owe a "climate debt" to developing nations and that carbon border taxes should be paired with massive transfers of clean technology and green finance.

The specific demands include: full CBAM revenue (estimated at EUR 1–2 billion annually by 2030) being channeled to a green development fund; technology transfer obligations for low-carbon steelmaking, cement, and fertilizer production; graduated CBAM phase-in timelines that give developing economies longer transition periods; and capacity-building support for establishing carbon markets and third-party verification systems.

The EU has made some concessions — CBAM revenues are formally earmarked for climate-related purposes — but has resisted most of the developing-country demands. The political dynamic resembles earlier WTO agricultural disputes, where developed-country trade barriers faced decades of developing-world opposition without resolution.

## Financial Market Transmission: Who Wins, Who Loses

CBAM is not just a regulatory compliance issue — it is a market-moving financial force that creates clear winners and losers across equity markets, commodity markets, and emerging-market credit.

### The Equity Winners

**ArcelorMittal** (MT.AMS): The world's second-largest steelmaker has positioned itself aggressively for the CBAM era. Its European operations are being converted to DRI-EAF technology with explicit green steel branding. In Germany, the Hamburg-based DRI plant uses natural gas now, with a planned hydrogen switchover by 2028–2030. In France and Belgium, Dunkirk and Ghent are slated for BF replacement with green hydrogen EAF. ArcelorMittal has struck commercial agreements for "XCarb" branded green steel at EUR 50–100/tonne premiums over commodity steel — premiums that will look modest once CBAM reaches full phase-in versus Chinese competition.

**Salzgitter AG** (SZG.XETR): Germany's second-largest integrated steelmaker is perhaps the most radical CBAM beneficiary story. Salzgitter's SALCOS program (Salzgitter Low CO2 Steelmaking) is the most advanced green hydrogen steel transition in Europe, with the first hydrogen DRI module operational at the Salzgitter plant in Lower Saxony as of 2025. Salzgitter's carbon intensity is targeted at 0.5 tCO2/tonne by 2033 — versus Chinese blast-furnace's 2.1 — meaning a CBAM-adjusted competitive advantage of 1.6 × EUR 65 = EUR 104/tonne at 2034 full phase-in.

**Green hydrogen producers and electrolyzers**: CBAM implicitly prices the value of switching from gray to green hydrogen across all covered sectors. Every tonne of gray hydrogen used in DRI production that is replaced by green hydrogen eliminates approximately 9–10 tCO2 in embedded carbon — at EUR 65/tonne ETS, that is EUR 585–650 per tonne of green hydrogen in embedded carbon value. Companies enabling this transition — electrolyzer manufacturers like Nel ASA (NEL.OL), Plug Power (PLUG), and ITM Power (ITM.LON) — are structural beneficiaries of CBAM's price signal.

### The Equity Losers

**Tata Steel** (TATASTEEL.NSE): India's largest steelmaker is the clearest CBAM victim among publicly traded companies. Its UK operations (Port Talbot in Wales) were already structurally challenged — Tata announced closure of the Port Talbot blast furnaces in 2024, a decision driven partly by rising ETS costs and partly by competitive pressure from low-cost imports. The irony is that Tata's Indian operations, producing for EU export, now face CBAM on their embedded carbon while Tata's UK operations are being shut partly because of ETS costs on domestic production. Tata's CBAM exposure on Indian steel exports to the EU (approximately 2–3 million tonnes annually) at full phase-in could reach EUR 250–400 million per year.

**Baowu Steel Group** (unlisted parent, exposure via downstream listed entities): China's largest steelmaker produces almost entirely via BF/BOF at 2.0–2.3 tCO2/tonne. While Baowu is state-owned and therefore can absorb CBAM costs through state support, its EU export volumes — approximately 6–8 million tonnes annually in various forms — create a structural disadvantage that market-priced competitors cannot indefinitely absorb. The commercial pressure will ultimately force Chinese steel to redirect away from the EU market (already happening via trade diversion to ASEAN, Africa, and Latin America) or to accelerate very expensive DRI conversion programs.

**Erdemir** (EREGL.IS, Turkey's Eregli Demir Celik): Turkey's largest steelmaker operates a mix of BF/BOF and EAF capacity. Its BF-based operations face full CBAM exposure; its EAF-based operations are relatively advantaged within the Turkish peer group. Erdemir has been actively investing in EAF expansion and scrap procurement to reduce its carbon intensity — a CBAM-driven capex decision that will improve its competitive position but at significant short-term cost.

## Common Misconceptions About CBAM

Six myths that persist in market commentary deserve explicit debunking.

**Myth 1: CBAM is a traditional import tariff.** False. CBAM does not set a fixed percentage duty on import value. It prices embedded carbon at the EU ETS spot price and applies only to the carbon content of goods. A high-value, low-carbon import faces minimal CBAM cost; a low-value, high-carbon import faces a large CBAM cost. The mechanism is carbon-discriminatory, not origin-discriminatory in theory — though in practice it heavily disadvantages countries with carbon-intensive industrial sectors.

**Myth 2: CBAM applies to all imports.** False. CBAM currently covers six sectors only: steel/iron, aluminum, cement, fertilizers, electricity, and hydrogen. The vast majority of EU imports — consumer goods, electronics, textiles, vehicles, chemicals (for now) — are unaffected. The EU's 2026 review is assessing scope expansion, particularly to cover complex manufactured goods, chemicals, and polymers, but full economy-wide application remains a distant prospect.

**Myth 3: Countries with any carbon price are fully exempt.** False. Deductibility applies only to mandatory, legally binding carbon prices in the country of origin that can be verified to have been actually paid at the installation level. Voluntary offsets, aspirational carbon taxes without enforcement, or informal pricing mechanisms do not qualify. China's ETS qualifies in covered sectors once steel and aluminum are formally included and pricing data is verifiable. India's carbon market, as of 2026, does not meet the mandatory-and-verified threshold for most covered sectors.

**Myth 4: CBAM will immediately eliminate cheap steel imports.** False. The phase-in is gradual. In 2026, only 10% of embedded carbon is covered. A Chinese steel importer faces approximately EUR 13–14/tonne in CBAM costs in 2026 — meaningful but not decisive for a product where the price gap to EU domestic production is EUR 100–150/tonne. The decisive shift comes post-2030 as phase-in percentages compound.

**Myth 5: CBAM revenue goes to EU general finances.** The legal situation is complex. CBAM regulation states that revenues shall be credited to EU own resources. There is political commitment, but not legally binding earmarking, to channel CBAM revenues to climate finance. The gap between political commitment and legal earmarking has been a major sticking point in developing-country negotiations.

**Myth 6: Green steel is immediately cost-competitive under CBAM.** Not yet. Green steel produced via green hydrogen DRI currently costs EUR 150–300/tonne more than conventional BF/BOF steel, depending on electrolyzer costs and renewable electricity prices. CBAM at full phase-in in 2034 might eliminate EUR 80–140/tonne of that premium (the CBAM-avoided cost for low-carbon producers). But unless green hydrogen costs fall dramatically — the EU target is EUR 1–2/kg by 2030, versus current EUR 4–6/kg — green steel will remain commercially challenged even with CBAM support.

## Market Signals and the Trading Playbook

For investors and traders, CBAM creates several distinct tradeable signals and structural long/short opportunities.

### The ETS as CBAM Proxy

The most direct CBAM trade is long EU ETS. Every EUR 1 increase in the ETS carbon price raises CBAM certificate costs proportionally for all non-EU importers. The primary ETS instruments are: EU Allowance (EUA) futures on ICE and EEX (European Energy Exchange), with front-month and calendar spread contracts; SPDR MSCI ACWI Climate Paris Aligned ETF and similar products that have indirect ETS sensitivity; and bespoke carbon fund allocations through managers like Andurand Capital or Engie.

ETS prices are driven by European industrial output (more activity = more allowances needed), energy prices (gas-coal substitution in power generation affects short-run ETS demand), policy signals (announcements of accelerated cap reductions or CBAM scope expansion are bullish), and winter weather (cold winters increase heating demand and thus power sector ETS usage).

### The Green Steel Long

The structural long is EU-integrated steelmakers with credible green transition programs versus non-EU blast-furnace producers. The pair trade: long ArcelorMittal or Salzgitter versus short Chinese steel ETF proxies (for European investors, pure-play Chinese steel short is difficult, but positions can be structured through EU-listed steel ETFs that have significant non-EU weighting, or via derivatives on steel price indices that diverge as CBAM bites).

The Tata Steel UK closure in 2024 was a preview of the structural shift. Port Talbot's blast furnaces, among the last in the UK, could no longer compete with ETS costs on their own domestic production plus the competitive pressure of cheap imports not yet fully CBAM-burdened. The future for integrated European steel is DRI-EAF — and the capex cycle for this transition (ArcelorMittal alone has committed EUR 4 billion in Europe through 2030) is a multi-year investment theme.

### Turkish Steel Diversion Trade

Turkey's EAF-heavy coastal mills are experiencing a bifurcated CBAM impact. High-scrap EAF producers (notably those in Iskenderun and Izmir with scrap-based operations) have carbon intensities of 0.6–0.9 tCO2/tonne — dramatically lower than BF/BOF. For these mills, CBAM at full phase-in adds EUR 39–58/tonne. They remain competitive in the EU market. Meanwhile, Turkish inland blast-furnace producers (Erdemir's BF capacity at Eregli) face EUR 130+/tonne at full phase-in.

The trade implication: within Turkey's steel sector, EAF-heavy producers are long-term beneficiaries of CBAM versus BF-heavy competitors, even within the same country. Investors in Turkish steel need to differentiate by process technology, not simply by geography.

### The Carbon Arbitrage Spread

The spread between the EU ETS price and China's domestic ETS price (currently approximately EUR 55/tonne in the EU's favor) is a direct measure of the "deductibility gap" — the amount Chinese steel mills pay extra in CBAM costs per tonne of CO2 versus what they'd pay if carbon prices were equal. Narrowing of this spread (rising China ETS or falling EU ETS) reduces CBAM's competitive bite on Chinese exporters. Widening of the spread increases it.

Watching the spread is a leading indicator of Chinese steel's EU competitiveness: if China's ETS accelerates its coverage to steel and aluminum and prices rise from EUR 12 to EUR 30 by 2028, the effective CBAM cost on Chinese steel drops from EUR 65 to EUR 35 per tonne CO2 — a meaningful relief. If the spread stays wide (EU accelerates its ETS cap reduction and China's ETS remains narrow), the competitive pressure on Chinese exporters intensifies.

## The Contagion: CBAM Is Spreading

Perhaps the most consequential aspect of CBAM for long-term market positioning is not its current form but its spread. The EU mechanism has triggered a cascade of CBAM-equivalent discussions and decisions in other major economies.

**United Kingdom**: The UK announced its own CBAM, scheduled for January 2027, covering the same six sectors as the EU system. UK ETS and EU ETS prices have diverged since Brexit (UK ETS was lower by 10–20% for most of 2022–2025), but the UK CBAM certificate price will track the UK ETS. For exporters shipping to both EU and UK markets, this doubles the CBAM surface area.

**Canada**: Canada's federal carbon pricing system (Output-Based Pricing System for industrial emitters) creates the infrastructure for a CBAM. The Liberal government of 2022–2025 was studying a carbon border adjustment for steel and aluminum imports. The political trajectory as of 2026 is uncertain, but the institutional and legal groundwork exists.

**Australia**: Australia's safeguard mechanism (reformed 2023) creates a domestic carbon cost for large industrial emitters. A Border Carbon Adjustment has been discussed in parliamentary committees. The political environment is more cautious than the EU, but the logic is identical: without border adjustment, Australian ETS costs disadvantage domestic industry versus carbon-unpriced imports.

**United States**: The US has no federal carbon price and therefore no CBAM — yet. However, the Clean Competition Act (introduced multiple times in the Senate) would establish exactly such a mechanism. The bipartisan political argument (protecting US industrial competitiveness and manufacturing jobs from foreign carbon subsidies) is more politically potent than climate framing alone. A future US CBAM covering steel, aluminum, and cement from carbon-unpriced economies would be geopolitically seismic — representing approximately \$50–100 billion in annual trade exposure.

**The Carbon Club Scenario**: If EU, UK, Canada, Australia, and US all implement CBAM-equivalent mechanisms within the next decade, they form a de facto carbon club covering the majority of global manufacturing demand. Non-member economies — primarily China, India, and the broader Global South — would face carbon border adjustments at every major developed-economy market. This would represent the most significant restructuring of global trade incentives since the end of the Cold War, and would fundamentally alter the economics of industrial development for emerging economies.

## The Green Hydrogen Wildcard

No analysis of CBAM is complete without addressing its most transformative potential effect: accelerating the economics of green hydrogen production.

Green hydrogen (H2 produced via electrolysis of water powered by renewable electricity, with zero direct CO2 emissions) is currently the key input for decarbonizing steelmaking via the DRI-EAF route. The problem is cost: green hydrogen currently costs EUR 4–6/kg at the electrolyzer output in Europe, versus EUR 0.8–1.2/kg for gray hydrogen from natural gas reforming. This cost gap — roughly 4x to 5x — makes green DRI steel dramatically more expensive than conventional blast-furnace steel.

CBAM changes the math. If a DRI plant using green hydrogen produces steel at 0.1 tCO2/tonne versus gray-hydrogen DRI at 0.8 tCO2/tonne, the CBAM-avoided cost difference at full phase-in (EUR 65/tonne) is 0.7 × EUR 65 = EUR 45.5/tonne of steel. At EUR 80/tonne ETS: EUR 56/tonne of steel. For a plant producing 2 million tonnes per year, that is EUR 91–112 million in annual CBAM savings on green hydrogen versus gray — a meaningful subsidy to the green hydrogen economics.

The EU's Hydrogen and Decarbonized Gas Market Package, Hydrogen Act, and RFNBO (Renewable Fuels of Non-Biological Origin) certification framework create additional subsidies for green hydrogen production and consumption. Combined with CBAM's implicit carbon pricing support, the EU is creating a layered incentive stack that is genuinely moving green hydrogen economics forward faster than any other jurisdiction.

The market signal: every EUR 1 increase in EU ETS prices raises the green hydrogen incentive value for DRI steelmakers by approximately EUR 0.7–0.8/kg of hydrogen consumed (depending on steel output per tonne of H2 consumed). As ETS prices rise toward EUR 80–100/tonne by the early 2030s (the trajectory implied by the current cap reduction schedule), the economics of green hydrogen for DRI steelmaking approach breakeven versus gray hydrogen — even before the expected cost reductions from electrolyzer scale-up.

## Structural Implications for Emerging-Market Portfolio Construction

For investors with significant emerging-market industrial exposure, CBAM demands a fundamental reassessment of long-term return assumptions for several asset classes.

**EM sovereign credit**: Countries with large export exposure to the EU in CBAM-covered sectors face structural headwinds to trade balance and current account. India (steel, aluminum, fertilizers), Turkey (steel, cement), and to a lesser extent Indonesia (aluminum) and South Africa (steel) face rising CBAM bills that reduce export competitiveness. At the margin, this pressures current accounts and thus sovereign credit quality — though the effect is gradual through the 2034 phase-in and is dwarfed by other macro factors for most sovereigns.

**EM industrial equities**: The sector-level impact is more acute. Portfolio construction for EM industrials should now include explicit carbon intensity scoring for CBAM-exposed sectors. A stock screen that filters for sub-1.0 tCO2/tonne steel producers will outperform a cap-weighted approach in the EU-export-exposed universe as CBAM phase-in progresses.

**FDI location decisions**: CBAM is already influencing greenfield industrial investment decisions. A new aluminum smelter being planned for export to the EU would now face a binary choice: locate in the EU (or a carbon-club member with ETS recognition) and bear no CBAM, or locate outside and bear rising CBAM costs. For investments with 20–30 year return horizons, the CBAM cost trajectory from 2026 to 2034 (by which point it is decisive) is already visible. FDI location analytics for heavy industry must now include CBAM cost projections as a core decision variable.

## Conclusion: The New Grammar of Industrial Trade

CBAM represents a fundamental shift in the grammar of international trade. For most of the post-war trading order, tariffs were the instrument of protectionism and countries were not permitted to use domestic policy choices — including environmental standards — as grounds for trade discrimination. CBAM tests those rules at their limits, arguing that pricing carbon at the border is not protectionism but equivalent treatment.

Whether that legal argument holds at the WTO will be determined over years of dispute proceedings. In the meantime, the commercial reality is unambiguous: carbon intensity is now a competitive variable in global heavy industry. The Turkish rebar mill, the Chinese blast furnace, the Indian cement plant — all must now account for the EU ETS carbon price in their EU market pricing strategy, or exit that market. The carbon price is no longer only a European problem.

The geopolitical implications unfold across a decade. Carbon clubs may form or fracture along energy alliance lines. Developing countries may succeed in carving out transition carve-outs, or they may build domestic carbon markets faster than expected to capture deductibility benefits. The EU ETS price may surge to EUR 100+ as the cap tightens, amplifying CBAM's bite, or it may collapse in a policy reversal that eviscerates the entire framework.

What is not in doubt is the direction of travel. Green steel, green aluminum, and low-carbon cement are no longer niche products for sustainability-minded buyers. They are the products that will be able to enter the EU market — the world's largest single import market — without an increasingly punishing carbon toll. Every year from 2026 to 2034, the toll goes up. Every year, the gap between low-carbon and high-carbon production widens in economic terms.

Carbon Border Adjustment is not just a climate policy. It is a trade weapon, a geopolitical instrument, and the rewriting of the economics of global heavy industry. Investors who understand it now will be positioned for the structural shifts it drives over the next decade. Those who treat it as a compliance footnote will find the paradigm has shifted beneath them.

The age of free carbon riding in international trade is ending. The tariff is carbon. And it is compounding.
