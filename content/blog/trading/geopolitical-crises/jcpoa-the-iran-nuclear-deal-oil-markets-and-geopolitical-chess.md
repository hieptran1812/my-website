> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The JCPOA (July 2015) was not merely a nuclear nonproliferation treaty; it was a supply shock mechanism for global oil markets. When signed, it added roughly 1 million barrels per day back to a market running at approximately 95 mb/d global demand — a 1% supply bump in a commodity where 0.5% swings move price 5–10%. Trump's May 2018 withdrawal reversed that supply addition, ultimately dropping Iranian exports from 2.5 mb/d to 0.4 mb/d and lifting Brent from \$57 to \$86 over six months. The Soleimani killing in January 2020 and the April 2024 Iran-Israel direct exchange each produced geopolitical risk spikes that moved gold, oil, and VIX in predictable directions within 48 hours. Traders who understand the deal architecture — six parties, IAEA verification, SWIFT disconnection, three-layer sanctions design, secondary sanctions extraterritoriality — can front-run the market's binary re-rating of Iranian supply any time the diplomatic temperature changes. The core trade structure: monitor deal probability signals, estimate the 1.0–1.5 mb/d supply delta, then use crude options rather than futures to capture directional moves while limiting tail risk from escalation surprises.

## The moment markets felt it

On the morning of January 16, 2016, the International Atomic Energy Agency certified that Iran had completed its initial nuclear commitments under the Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action. The IAEA's formal statement triggered what diplomats call "Implementation Day" — the precise moment when months of negotiations and a 159-page technical agreement became operational reality. Within hours, the European Union published implementing regulations lifting its oil embargo. The US Treasury's Office of Foreign Assets Control updated its sanctions list, removing Iranian banks and oil entities from the Specially Designated Nationals register.

Oil markets reacted instantly — but not in the direction many expected. Brent crude fell. It had already been sliding, from \$115 in June 2014 to around \$30 by January 2016, as OPEC flooded the market to defend market share against US shale. The JCPOA implementation day added analytical clarity to what traders had already been pricing: Iran's return to global oil markets would add supply to an already-oversupplied market. By mid-2016, Iranian exports had climbed from a sanctions-floor of roughly 1.1 mb/d back toward 2.4 mb/d — a 1.3 mb/d addition to a world already drowning in crude.

That January 2016 Implementation Day crystallizes what makes Iran policy so important for commodity traders: a diplomatic event — lawyers in Vienna agreeing on treaty text — directly controls the physical availability of millions of barrels of oil per day. No other geopolitical event in modern history has produced a more predictable, quantifiable, repeated, and tradeable supply shock mechanism.

## Foundations: Iran's Oil Economy and the Physics of Sanctions

Iran sits on approximately 157 billion barrels of proved crude oil reserves, placing it fourth globally behind Venezuela, Saudi Arabia, and Canada. But the comparison understates Iran's importance. Venezuelan reserves are mostly ultra-heavy Orinoco belt crude requiring upgraders and extensive processing. Canadian reserves are oil sands, extractable only through expensive thermal or mining processes. Iran's reserves are predominantly conventional light and sweet crude — the same quality grade that European refineries were built to process and that Asian petrochemical complexes prefer as feedstock.

Iran's oil geography is also favorable. Its largest fields — Ahvaz, Marun, Gachsaran, and Agha Jari — cluster in Khuzestan province in the southwest, relatively close to major export terminals at Kharg Island and Bandar Imam Khomeini. The Kharg Island terminal alone handled roughly 90% of Iranian crude exports at its peak. Production costs at established Iranian fields are among the lowest globally — estimated at \$5–10/barrel — meaning even severely discounted export prices generate positive cash flow.

Iran reached peak production of approximately 6 mb/d in 1974, before the Islamic Revolution disrupted operations. Post-revolution production settled in the 3.5–4 mb/d range through the 1980s and 1990s, recovering from the damage of the 1980–1988 Iran-Iraq war. Sanctions have constrained investment in secondary recovery techniques (water and gas injection) needed to maintain aging fields, meaning production capacity has gradually eroded from the post-revolution peak.

### Why oil revenues are existentially important

Unlike Gulf monarchies with substantial sovereign wealth funds, Iran depends on oil revenues for government finances in a more immediate and structurally deep way. Oil revenues historically funded roughly 50–70% of the central government's budget. The revolutionary government expanded social programs, fuel subsidies, and employment in state enterprises during the 1990s and 2000s, creating a public spending base that could not easily adjust downward when oil revenues fell.

When sanctions cut Iran's oil exports from 2.5 mb/d in 2011 to 1.1 mb/d by 2013, at roughly \$110/barrel Brent, the revenue impact was catastrophic:

Pre-sanctions annual oil revenue: 2.5 mb/d × 365 × \$110 = approximately \$100 billion
Under-sanctions annual oil revenue: 1.1 mb/d × 365 × \$90 (discounted price) = approximately \$36 billion
Annual revenue loss: approximately \$64 billion — equivalent to roughly 15% of Iran's pre-sanctions GDP

The rial collapsed: from approximately 12,000 rials per dollar in 2011 to over 35,000 by mid-2013, a 65% devaluation. Inflation exceeded 40% annually. Urban middle-class Iranians who had savings in rials saw their purchasing power destroyed within 24 months. Import-dependent industries (medicine, electronics, manufactured goods) faced acute shortages. This is the economic context in which Rouhani won the 2013 presidential election on a platform of diplomatic engagement — the electorate, including significant portions of the religious establishment's economic constituency, demanded relief.

### The nuclear program and why it matters

Iran's nuclear program began in the 1950s under the Shah with US assistance as part of the "Atoms for Peace" initiative. After the 1979 revolution, the program was suspended but later revived, eventually encompassing uranium enrichment facilities at Natanz and Fordow, a heavy water reactor at Arak, and extensive centrifuge development.

From a non-proliferation standpoint, the concern is the dual-use nature of uranium enrichment: the same centrifuge infrastructure used to enrich uranium to 3–5% for reactor fuel can be run further to produce 90% weapons-grade uranium. The difference is time and political decision, not technology. The IAEA uses a technical benchmark called "breakout time" — how long it would take a country to produce enough weapons-grade uranium for one bomb if it decided to race for a weapon. Before JCPOA, Iran's breakout time was estimated at 2–3 months. The JCPOA's centrifuge reductions and monitoring requirements were designed to extend that to at least 12 months.

By 2023–2024, after years of deal collapse and nuclear advances, Iran's breakout time had compressed back to approximately 1–2 weeks — a strategic deterioration representing the failure of the maximum pressure approach to achieve its stated objective of forcing a better deal.

## The political moves

### Why Iran negotiated

By 2013, Iran's economy was in serious distress. The rial had lost roughly 60% of its value since 2011. Inflation exceeded 40%. Oil revenues — which historically funded around 60% of the government budget — had collapsed. The Revolutionary Guards' economic empire, which controls significant productive capacity in oil, construction, and telecommunications, was squeezed. Unemployment, particularly among the educated urban youth who had supported the 2009 Green Movement, was rising. When Hassan Rouhani won the presidential election in June 2013 on an explicitly pro-engagement platform — running against the incumbent hardliner's faction — it signaled that the economic pain had become politically intolerable even within the Iranian establishment.

The deal Iran was willing to accept reflected that desperation. Tehran agreed to:
- Reduce installed centrifuges from approximately 19,000 to 6,104 operational units (all IR-1, the oldest and least efficient design)
- Limit uranium enrichment to 3.67% (far below weapons-grade 90%)
- Reduce its enriched uranium stockpile by 98% (from approximately 10,000 kg to 300 kg)
- Convert the Fordow enrichment facility to a research center with no enrichment
- Modify the Arak heavy water reactor to eliminate its weapons-grade plutonium production potential
- Accept continuous IAEA monitoring including surveillance cameras, daily access to declared sites, and a "snapback" inspection mechanism for undeclared sites

In exchange, Iran received the lifting of nuclear-related UN sanctions under Security Council Resolution 2231, the lifting of EU oil embargo and financial sanctions, unfreezing of approximately \$100 billion in assets held in overseas accounts under sanctions restrictions, and access to the international banking system for non-designated Iranian entities.

The Obama administration assessed this as a favorable nonproliferation outcome: the constraints bought at least 10–15 years of breakout time, during which further diplomatic progress was possible. Critics argued the deal legitimized Iran's nuclear program, provided massive near-term economic relief that would strengthen the revolutionary government, and left Iran's ballistic missile program and regional military activities unaddressed.

### The P5+1 architecture

The negotiating structure was deliberately multilateral. Known as the P5+1 (the five UN Security Council permanent members plus Germany), the format ensured that any deal would have UN Security Council endorsement, preventing Iran from framing it as capitulation to the West alone.

![JCPOA deal architecture with six party actors and IAEA verification role](/imgs/blogs/jcpoa-the-iran-nuclear-deal-oil-markets-and-geopolitical-chess-3.png)

The United States held the most critical lever: OFAC secondary sanctions and SWIFT access. The European Union controlled oil embargo policy and its own SWIFT-based restrictions through Belgian governance of the Society for Worldwide Interbank Financial Telecommunication. Russia provided the diplomatic cover of being a co-signatory rather than a coerced party, crucial for Iran's domestic framing of the deal. China's participation established that the world's largest oil consumer supported the deal's framework. The EU-3 (UK, France, Germany) served as the negotiating channel that could credibly represent European commercial interests alongside security concerns — EU companies had billions in pre-sanctions Iranian business they wanted to restore.

This architecture had a structural weakness baked into it from the beginning: the US could exit unilaterally and reimpose secondary sanctions regardless of what the other parties decided. The EU, Russia, and China could maintain their portions of the deal — lifting their own sanctions, continuing to recognize the UN resolution — but without US dollar clearing, Iranian oil commerce would remain effectively blocked for any firm with significant US market exposure. That asymmetry — the US as the indispensable sanctions authority — would prove decisive in 2018.

### Trump's withdrawal and maximum pressure

On May 8, 2018, President Trump announced US withdrawal from the JCPOA and reimposition of all nuclear-related sanctions. His stated rationale was threefold: the deal did not address Iran's ballistic missile program; the "sunset clauses" allowed key nuclear restrictions to expire after 8–15 years, creating what he called a "countdown to an Iranian nuclear bomb"; and Iran's regional behavior — support for Hezbollah, Hamas, Houthi forces, and Assad in Syria — had not moderated after the deal.

The withdrawal was telegraphed for months. Trump had repeatedly called the JCPOA "the worst deal ever negotiated" during the 2016 campaign. His appointment of Mike Pompeo as CIA Director in January 2017, then as Secretary of State in April 2018, and John Bolton as National Security Advisor in April 2018, created an Iran policy team uniformly opposed to the deal. Markets were pricing significant withdrawal probability — analysts estimated 40–60% in the weeks before the announcement.

Brent crude was trading at approximately \$75/barrel on withdrawal day — already up from \$57 in June 2017, partly on anticipation of this event and partly on strong global demand growth. Markets initially showed a muted reaction to the announcement itself, having already partially priced the withdrawal probability. The real market impact came over the following six months as the reimposed sanctions timeline and waiver decisions became clear.

The administration introduced 90-day and 180-day wind-down periods for existing contracts, then granted temporary waivers to eight importers — China, India, Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, Turkey, Italy, and Greece — to avoid immediate supply disruption. Brent dipped modestly on the waiver news (supply disruption less severe than feared), then climbed as markets realized the waivers were time-limited. Those waivers expired in May 2019. With no renewals, Iranian exports collapsed from roughly 2.5 mb/d in mid-2018 to approximately 0.4–0.5 mb/d by late 2019 — a supply withdrawal of 2 mb/d in roughly 18 months.

#### Worked example: the 2018 withdrawal supply shock

Suppose a portfolio manager runs a commodity macro fund in March 2018, two months before the withdrawal announcement. The market is pricing a 40% probability of withdrawal based on Trump's rhetoric, Pompeo's appointment, and Bolton's hire.

If withdrawal probability rises from 40% to 100%:
- Expected supply loss at 40% probability: 2.0 mb/d × 0.40 = 0.80 mb/d
- Expected supply loss at 100% probability: 2.0 mb/d × 1.00 = 2.00 mb/d
- Marginal supply shock from full re-rating: 1.20 mb/d

Historical oil price elasticity: a 1 mb/d supply shortfall in a 95 mb/d market (roughly 1% of supply) moves Brent approximately \$8–12/barrel in a balanced market. At a 1.20 mb/d incremental shock, the implied price uplift is \$10–14/barrel.

Brent moved from roughly \$68 in March 2018 to \$86 in October 2018 — an \$18 move — somewhat larger than this simple model suggests, because OPEC did not immediately offset the supply loss (Saudi Arabia was targeting budget balance at \$80+ Brent) and markets priced elevated risk premium from Iran's explicit threats to close the Strait of Hormuz (through which approximately 21 mb/d of oil flows, roughly 21% of global seaborne trade).

A trader who entered long Brent futures at \$68 in March and exited at \$86 in October would have earned approximately \$18/barrel on each contract (1,000 barrels per Brent contract = \$18,000 gross profit per contract), against a margin requirement of roughly \$3,500–4,500 at the time — a return on margin of approximately 400–500% in seven months.

## The financial channels

Sanctions affect oil markets through five distinct transmission channels. Understanding each channel matters because different events activate different subsets, producing different market signals and requiring different trading instruments.

### Channel 1: Physical supply reduction

The most direct channel. When Iranian tankers cannot load without insurance, cannot clear payment, and cannot find buyers willing to risk secondary sanctions, exports fall. From 2.5 mb/d pre-2012 to 1.1 mb/d by 2013; from 2.5 mb/d in early 2018 to 0.4 mb/d by late 2019. This is the channel that moves the physical oil balance and fundamentally reprices crude. It operates with a lag of 6–18 months as buyers find alternative sources, OPEC adjusts quotas, and new supply routes develop.

The signal for this channel: Iranian tanker tracking data (now available from commercial providers like Kpler and Vortexa) showing week-by-week export volumes. When exports fall 200,000 barrels per day in a week, the market will price it within 48 hours. Sophisticated commodity desks track this in real time and position before official IEA data confirms the trend.

### Channel 2: SWIFT disconnection and payment clearing

Iran's disconnection from SWIFT in 2012 was the first time the system had been used as a sanctions tool against a sovereign state's central bank. SWIFT is a messaging network, not a clearing system itself — actual dollar clearing happens through Fedwire and correspondent banking — but SWIFT messages are how banks instruct each other about payments. Without SWIFT access, Iranian banks could not send or receive payment instructions from foreign counterparts.

The practical effect: Iran had to resort to barter (oil for food, medicine, manufactured goods), payment in gold bullion physically transported, bilateral accounts denominated in Chinese renminbi, and informal hawala-style transfer networks operated by Iranian diaspora merchants. All of these mechanisms are expensive (2–5% transaction costs vs. fractions of a percent for SWIFT), slow (weeks vs. days), and carry enormous counterparty risk. The haircut Iran accepted on its oil — paying effectively in discounted goods rather than dollars — was a direct tax on its export revenues.

After JCPOA implementation in January 2016, SWIFT reconnected non-sanctioned Iranian banks. But major Western banks remained cautious, fearing that the Obama administration's sanctions relief could be reversed — a rational fear that proved correct. Deutsche Bank, HSBC, BNP Paribas, and others had paid billions in fines for Iran sanctions violations in prior years. They were not rushing back regardless of what SWIFT access permitted.

This banking hesitancy meant that even after Implementation Day, Iranian oil revenues could not easily flow back into the global financial system. Iran accumulated foreign currency reserves in escrow accounts in countries like India and South Korea — funds owed for oil already sold — that it struggled to repatriate. The approximately \$100 billion in "unfrozen assets" that the Obama administration cited was largely these accumulated escrow accounts, not liquid cash freely available.

### Channel 3: Shipping insurance and the Lloyd's mechanism

Lloyd's of London and the EU-based Protection and Indemnity (P&I) Clubs cover approximately 90% of global maritime cargo and third-party liability insurance. These are regulated entities subject to EU law. The EU oil embargo prohibited them from insuring Iranian oil shipments. Without P&I coverage, major port terminals — which require insurance proof for tanker access as protection against oil spill liability — would not accept Iranian tankers. This effectively blocked Iranian oil from major European, American, and many Asian ports regardless of what the purchasing country's government wanted.

The shipping insurance channel is often underappreciated because it sounds administrative. But P&I coverage is not optional for tankers calling at commercial terminals. A tanker operator without P&I coverage cannot access Rotterdam, Singapore, Jebel Ali, or any major terminal. Iran was forced to run what became known as its "ghost fleet" — aging Very Large Crude Carriers (VLCCs) operating without standard insurance, conducting ship-to-ship transfers in international waters to obscure cargo origin, and routing through smaller Iranian-controlled ports like Assaluyeh.

The ghost fleet approach imposed real costs: older vessels cost more to operate, had higher accident rates, could not use standard shipping lanes efficiently, and required payment of insurance-alternative risk premiums to ship owners willing to take on the legal exposure. A 2023 estimate put the additional cost of shadow fleet shipping at approximately \$2–4/barrel compared to standard tanker rates.

### Channel 4: Oil producer equity and sector rotation

The Brent price moves driven by Iran sanctions events translate directly into equity sector rotations. The correlation between Brent crude and integrated oil company (IOC) equity is not 1:1 — integrated majors also earn refining margins that benefit from cheap crude feedstock, and their dividend policies create floor support — but it is strong and exploitable.

![Iran oil exports 2011 to 2024 with key sanctions and deal events](/imgs/blogs/jcpoa-the-iran-nuclear-deal-oil-markets-and-geopolitical-chess-2.png)

For pure-play exploration and production (E&P) companies, the oil price beta is typically 1.2–1.8x. A 10% move in Brent typically moves E&P equities 12–18% in the same direction. Oilfield services companies (Schlumberger, Halliburton, Baker Hughes) move with a lag — they benefit when oil prices incentivize new drilling, but that drilling decision takes 6–12 months to translate into actual services revenue.

Airlines and petrochemical companies move inversely to crude: jet fuel is typically 20–30% of airline operating costs, so a \$10/barrel increase in Brent reduces airline margins by several hundred basis points depending on the airline's hedging program. Refiners sit in the middle — they benefit from the spread between crude input costs and refined product output prices (the "crack spread"), which can actually widen during supply disruptions if product demand holds steady while crude supply falls.

The sector rotation playbook for Iran events:

When Iranian supply is expected to fall (sanctions tightening, deal collapse):
- Long: XLE (oil producers ETF), XOP (E&P focused), crude tanker companies (higher freight rates from tighter supply), defense contractors (elevated regional military spending)
- Short: JETS (airline ETF), LIN (industrial gas/petrochemical feedstock), EM refiners dependent on Iranian crude

When Iranian supply is expected to rise (deal progress, sanctions relaxation):
- Long: JETS, LIN, refiner equities (cheap feedstock), Asian petrochemical companies
- Short: XLE pure E&P names, tanker companies (oversupply depresses freight rates)

### Channel 5: Geopolitical risk premium and safe-haven flows

Beyond the physical supply effect, Iran-related events generate risk premium moves across safe-haven assets. This channel activates quickly (within hours of events) and mean-reverts over days to weeks as the market re-assesses escalation probability.

![Brent crude price around key JCPOA events 2014 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/jcpoa-the-iran-nuclear-deal-oil-markets-and-geopolitical-chess-4.png)

Historical safe-haven response to major Middle East events (average across events above GPR 150):
- Gold: +2.3% over 7 days
- USD index: +1.1%
- Japanese yen vs USD: +1.4% (JPY appreciates as safe haven)
- Swiss franc vs USD: +0.9%
- US 10-year Treasury yield: -18 basis points (price up)
- S&P 500: -2.4%
- Brent crude: +3.1% (oil + safe haven double effect)
- MSCI Emerging Markets: -3.8%

The geopolitical risk channel is distinct from the supply channel: it can move assets even when Iranian oil exports are not immediately affected. The Soleimani killing in January 2020 (GPR 161) moved Brent +4.4% even though Iranian oil exports were already near their floor under maximum pressure sanctions — there was no additional supply loss to price. The market was pricing the risk of Strait of Hormuz closure, direct military confrontation, and contagion across regional energy infrastructure.

## The sanctions chokepoint architecture

The three-layer sanctions design reflects lessons learned from earlier attempts at Iran sanctions that partially failed because they only operated on one or two dimensions.

![Iran sanctions chokepoint architecture three layers from financial to secondary](/imgs/blogs/jcpoa-the-iran-nuclear-deal-oil-markets-and-geopolitical-chess-5.png)

**Layer 1 — Financial core:** SWIFT disconnection for Iranian banks, US dollar clearing prohibition, correspondent banking restrictions, asset freeze for designated entities. This layer prevents Iran from receiving payment in internationally convertible currencies. Without it, Iran can sell oil but cannot efficiently collect proceeds.

**Layer 2 — Oil embargo:** EU import ban on Iranian crude and petroleum products, prohibition on EU shipping and insurance companies servicing Iranian oil cargoes, ban on new investment in Iranian oil and gas sector, arms embargo. This layer restricts the physical movement of Iranian oil to the largest premium-price buyers. Europe historically paid the best prices for Iranian light crude; the embargo forced Iran into the lower-paying Asian market at a discount.

**Layer 3 — Secondary sanctions:** US penalties against any non-US company or individual that engages in specified transactions with Iran, regardless of where they are located or what law governs their home jurisdiction. This is the most extraterritorial and legally aggressive layer. It effectively turns US market access into a hostage — any company choosing to trade with Iran must simultaneously exit the US market, a trade-off that nearly every global company resolves in favor of US market access.

The layered design was deliberate. No single layer was sufficient alone:

Financial sanctions alone could be circumvented by barter, yuan-denominated payments, or gold transactions — and China demonstrated willingness to do all three.

Oil embargo alone would be circumvented by redirecting all exports to Asia, which has no comparable embargo.

Secondary sanctions alone required credible enforcement by OFAC, invited WTO challenges from US trading partners, and created political tensions with allies forced to choose between their companies' global business and their relationships with Iran.

Together, the three layers create a matrix of interlocking risks. A Chinese refiner could pay in yuan (bypassing financial sanctions) and buy Iranian oil (bypassing the embargo that doesn't apply to China) — but if that refiner's parent company or affiliates have US market exposure, the secondary sanctions risk brings the entire framework back into play.

#### Worked example: the South Korean refiner calculation

Consider a South Korean refiner in 2019, after US waivers have expired. Iran offers crude at a \$5/barrel discount to Brent (\$58 vs. prevailing \$63). The refiner processes 200,000 barrels per day.

Annual discount value from Iranian crude: \$5 × 200,000 bbl/day × 365 days = \$365 million

Against that discount, the refiner faces:
- OFAC secondary sanctions designation risk: loss of access to US correspondent banking, estimated impact of \$1.2 billion/year on US-dollar-dependent Korean trade and finance business
- Lloyd's P&I insurance unavailability: additional \$2–3/barrel shadow fleet cost = \$146–219 million/year
- Political cost: US diplomatic pressure on Seoul government, potential impact on US-Korea alliance relations and trade deals
- Legal cost: compliance burden of managing the transaction structure to minimize exposure

Net expected value: \$365M − \$1,200M − \$180M = negative \$1 billion per year

The refiner exits Iranian business. This calculation repeated across every major buyer — including Japanese, Indian, and Korean refiners who had previously been significant Iranian customers — is how secondary sanctions enforcement works. Not through physical interception but through economic rational choice.

China was the exception: Chinese state-owned refiners operated with implicit government backing that buffered them from secondary sanctions designation risk, maintaining some Iranian purchases throughout maximum pressure. But even Chinese demand could not absorb all displaced Iranian volumes at competitive prices — the discount to Brent that Iran had to offer Chinese buyers under maximum pressure reached \$10–15/barrel, effectively a self-imposed tax on its own exports.

## Deep dive: the Soleimani killing and market response

On January 3, 2020, a US Reaper drone strike at Baghdad International Airport killed Qasem Soleimani, commander of Iran's Quds Force. Soleimani was not merely a military official; he was the architect of Iran's regional network of proxy forces in Iraq, Syria, Lebanon, Yemen, and Afghanistan — arguably the second-most powerful figure in Iran after Supreme Leader Khamenei. His killing was the most direct US-Iran military confrontation since the tanker war of the 1980s.

The decision to carry out the strike came after a series of escalating incidents: Iranian-backed militias had attacked US bases in Iraq multiple times in December 2019, culminating in the death of a US contractor on December 27. The US responded with airstrikes against Kataib Hezbollah bases on December 29, which were followed by an attack on the US embassy compound in Baghdad on December 31. The Soleimani killing was ordered as a deterrent strike.

From a market standpoint, the event had two distinctive features. First, it was entirely unanticipated — unlike the JCPOA deal process, which was telegraphed months in advance, the strike came with no market warning. Second, it raised the genuine possibility of direct Iran-US military conflict rather than proxy confrontation, which would qualitatively change the risk calculus for Strait of Hormuz security.

Market response in the 48 hours following:
- Brent crude: +4.4%, from \$67.8 to \$70.8/barrel
- Gold: +2.1%, from \$1,548 to \$1,580/oz
- VIX: +7% over following week
- Defense contractor equities: +3–6%
- Airlines: -3 to -5%
- Israeli shekel: initially stable (Israel not a party)
- Iraqi dinar: -2%

The initial spike was sharp but short-lived. Iran's retaliatory strike on January 8, 2020 — approximately 16 ballistic missiles targeting US military bases at Ain al-Asad and Erbil in Iraq — caused no US casualties (Iran gave advance warning through Iraqi government channels). Markets read this as face-saving retaliation without intent to escalate. By January 9, Brent had given back half the spike, settling around \$69.

The rapid mean-reversion illustrates a consistent pattern in geopolitical event trading: the first 24–48 hours price maximum uncertainty (risk of worst-case outcome), then markets reassess the actual escalation path over the following 72 hours. Traders who exit positions in the immediate spike typically earn poor risk-adjusted returns; those who hold through the reversion can either take profits on the spike or re-enter at better prices for the longer-term fundamental thesis.

#### Worked example: the Soleimani event options trade

A geopolitical macro trader monitoring Iran tensions in late December 2019 observes escalating indicators: US embassy attack December 31, history of Iranian retaliation cycles, and Brent at \$67/barrel with implied volatility around 24% — low by historical standards for the Middle East risk environment.

The trader buys Brent call options:
- Strike: \$73/barrel (approximately 9% out of the money)
- Expiry: February 2020 (45 days)
- Premium: approximately \$1.20/barrel (at 24% implied vol)
- Position: 50 contracts × 1,000 barrels = 50,000 barrels exposure
- Total premium paid: \$1.20 × 50,000 = \$60,000

Scenario analysis:

No escalation, Brent stays \$65-70:
- Options expire worthless
- Loss: \$60,000

Moderate spike to \$74 (controlled escalation):
- Call intrinsic value: \$74 − \$73 = \$1/barrel
- Position value: \$50,000
- Net loss: \$10,000 (better than expected given premium paid)

Significant spike to \$78 (Hormuz threat credible):
- Call intrinsic value: \$78 − \$73 = \$5/barrel
- Position value: \$250,000
- Net profit: \$190,000

Extreme scenario, \$85 (actual Hormuz closure):
- Call intrinsic value: \$85 − \$73 = \$12/barrel
- Position value: \$600,000
- Net profit: \$540,000

The asymmetric structure suits geopolitical event uncertainty perfectly: defined maximum loss (\$60,000), with upside that scales with escalation severity. The actual outcome — Brent reaching \$70.8 on January 3 then retracing — means the \$73 strike was not breached; the position expired worthless. But the risk management rationale remains correct: for an event with genuine tail risk (Hormuz closure), buying convexity at low implied vol is rational portfolio management regardless of outcome.

## The GPR Index and Iran events

The Caldara-Iacoviello Geopolitical Risk Index systematically captures media-reported geopolitical uncertainty, allowing comparison across events and time periods.

![Geopolitical risk index around Iran and JCPOA events](/imgs/blogs/jcpoa-the-iran-nuclear-deal-oil-markets-and-geopolitical-chess-6.png)

The chart reveals a consistent pattern in Iran-related events:

**Deal-signing events reduce GPR.** The July 2015 JCPOA signing was associated with a below-average GPR reading — geopolitical uncertainty fell because a major potential conflict was formally managed. This is the counterintuitive feature of arms control agreements: signing a deal actually reduces tradeable geopolitical risk, creating a window where safe-haven assets that had priced conflict risk deflate.

**Withdrawal and military escalation spike GPR.** Trump's withdrawal (May 2018) generated a GPR reading in the 130 range (elevated but not extreme), while the Soleimani killing (January 2020) produced a reading of 161. The April 2024 direct Iran-Israel missile exchange pushed GPR to 203 — approaching the levels seen during the early weeks of the Russia-Ukraine war.

**GPR spikes above 150 reliably trigger safe-haven flows.** Based on historical data, a GPR reading above 150 is associated with average 7-day returns of gold +2.3%, USD +1.1%, and 10-year Treasuries +12 price ticks (yield -18 bps). The correlation is not 1:1 in every instance, but it is consistent enough to justify a systematic rule: when GPR exceeds 150 on Iran events, add gold longs and trim EM equity exposure.

**GPR mean-reverts in 30–60 days** after acute events that do not lead to sustained conflict. This creates a timing structure: go long safe havens on the spike, exit on the reversion 3–6 weeks later. The exception is events that represent genuine structural shifts — the Russia-Ukraine invasion produced a GPR spike of 345 that persisted for months because the conflict did not resolve quickly.

## How it shows up in real markets

### The Iran-Israel direct exchange (April 2024)

On April 13–14, 2024, Iran launched approximately 170 drones, 30 cruise missiles, and 5 ballistic missiles directly at Israel — the first direct Iranian attack on Israeli territory in history. The strike came in retaliation for an Israeli airstrike on the Iranian consulate in Damascus on April 1, 2024, which killed multiple senior IRGC officers. The attack was partially telegraphed (Iran gave advance signals through intermediaries), and Israeli, US, British, and Jordanian air defenses intercepted 99% of the projectiles.

Market reaction over 72 hours:
- Brent crude: +2.5% to \$90.8/barrel on April 13 (highest since October 2023)
- GPR Index: approximately 203 in the April 2024 reading
- Gold: +1.8% to approximately \$2,390/oz
- VIX: +14% over the following week, peaking around 19
- Israeli shekel: -1.8% vs USD on April 15
- Iranian rial: approximately -3% on the unofficial market

Markets correctly assessed this as controlled escalation rather than regional war. Israel's response — a limited drone strike on Iranian radar installations near Isfahan on April 19, designed as face-saving retaliation that neither side needed to escalate — confirmed the bounded-escalation assessment. By April 25, Brent had retraced to \$87/barrel, and VIX had returned to 16.

The key analytical variable traders needed to assess: did either side want escalation? Iran's answer was no — the strike was designed to establish deterrence (demonstrating ability to hit Israel) without triggering a US military response. Israel's answer was also no in the immediate term, with the hostage situation in Gaza and the ongoing northern front with Hezbollah already consuming military bandwidth. The 72-hour telegraphing was itself a de-escalation signal: a country truly intending to damage Israel would not have announced the strike in advance.

### The Biden JCPOA negotiations (2021–2022)

Biden administration negotiations began in April 2021, conducted through European intermediaries in Vienna. Iran's maximalist opening position was full US sanctions relief before Iran takes any step. The US position was Iranian nuclear compliance demonstrations before lifting sanctions. Neither side could make the first move without appearing to concede ground domestically.

The negotiations produced 11 rounds of talks but no agreement. Oil markets had partially priced a deal — Brent hovered around \$68–77/barrel through late 2021 rather than the \$90+ it would have reached if Iranian supply were fully constrained and OPEC supply were tight. When negotiations effectively stalled by December 2021 and Russia's Ukraine invasion in February 2022 scrambled the diplomatic landscape, oil prices rose sharply — though primarily driven by Russia-Ukraine supply fears rather than Iran.

#### Worked example: deal probability re-pricing in 2021

Suppose in October 2021, the market prices a 50% probability of JCPOA revival over 12 months. Analysts estimate:
- No-deal Brent equilibrium: approximately \$90/barrel (tight market, Iranian supply constrained at 0.8 mb/d)
- Full-deal Brent equilibrium: approximately \$70/barrel (Iran adds 1.5 mb/d over 12 months)
- Current Brent: approximately \$80/barrel — the market average of the two scenarios

Market-implied Brent: (0.50 × \$90) + (0.50 × \$70) = \$80 — consistent with observation.

By December 2021, deal probability falls from 50% to 20% as Iran's new hardline president takes a more demanding negotiating stance:
- New implied price: (0.20 × \$70) + (0.80 × \$90) = \$14 + \$72 = \$86

A portfolio manager who held long Brent futures from \$80 in October, correctly anticipating the deal probability collapse, would have earned \$6/barrel (\$6,000 per contract) by December 2021. More importantly, they would have had conviction to stay long into February 2022 when the Russia-Ukraine war pushed Brent to \$117 — but that required a separate thesis.

## Common misconceptions

### "Iran sanctions are purely about nuclear weapons"

Partly true, but the financial architecture of sanctions — SWIFT, secondary sanctions, oil embargo — was built to maximize economic pain across the entire Iranian economy. The nuclear rationale was the political and legal justification for deploying tools that function as general economic warfare. After JCPOA implementation in 2016, the US explicitly preserved non-nuclear sanctions (terrorism designations, ballistic missiles, human rights) that continued to restrict Iran's banking access and investment climate. Iran's oil exports recovered significantly; its broader economic isolation did not fully lift because non-nuclear sanctions remained in place.

The practical trading implication: even a successful JCPOA 2.0 deal would not produce the full normalization of Iranian economic relations that the 2015 deal implied. Secondary sanctions not covered by the nuclear deal would remain, continuing to deter Western companies from investing in Iranian oil infrastructure and limiting the pace of export recovery.

### "A deal means full Iranian production returns immediately"

False, and the 2015–2016 experience demonstrates exactly why. Iran needed approximately 12–18 months from Implementation Day to approach maximum exports, and not because of physical production limitations but because of financial and logistical infrastructure restoration. Even after sanctions were lifted on paper, major Western banks took 12–18 months to restore Iranian correspondent banking relationships. Lloyd's and P&I clubs took several months to update their underwriting policies. Shipping companies needed to renegotiate contracts and insurance. New long-term supply agreements required negotiation.

A new deal would face the same delays plus additional barriers: Iran's nuclear advances since 2018 (more advanced centrifuges, 60% enrichment) make the technical verification process more complex. US banks and companies, burned by the experience of investing in Iran in 2016–2018 only to exit again when Trump withdrew, would be even more cautious about re-engaging. The market pricing an instantaneous 1.5 mb/d supply addition on any future deal announcement would be wrong by at least 12 months.

### "OPEC can neutralize any Iranian supply addition"

OPEC spare capacity is finite and constrained by individual member interests. In 2015–2016, Saudi Arabia and Gulf producers were already producing near maximum capacity in their market-share battle with US shale — they had neither the willingness nor ability to easily offset Iranian volumes. By 2023–2024, OPEC+ nominally has 5–6 mb/d of spare capacity, but production cuts are politically difficult to implement when member states need revenue.

More importantly, Saudi Arabia's fiscal breakeven oil price is approximately \$80/barrel — the level needed to balance the Saudi government budget. Cutting production to accommodate Iranian exports would be painful domestically. The Saudis' preference has generally been to absorb Iranian supply by allowing prices to fall slightly rather than cut their own output, meaning the price impact of Iranian supply additions falls on all producers roughly proportionally.

### "Secondary sanctions have universal extraterritorial reach"

Legally contested. The EU has consistently maintained that US secondary sanctions applied to non-US companies operating outside US jurisdiction violate international law and WTO non-discrimination rules. The EU passed a "Blocking Statute" in 2018 prohibiting EU companies from complying with US secondary sanctions on Iran. In practice, the blocking statute has been almost entirely ineffective — EU companies chose to exit Iranian business anyway because their US market access far outweighs Iranian trade in scale.

The long-term implication for sanctions as a geopolitical tool: if non-US alternatives to dollar clearing mature (Chinese CIPS system, BRICS payment alternatives, central bank digital currencies), the extraterritorial reach of US secondary sanctions weakens. Russia's experience post-2022 has accelerated development of dollar-clearing alternatives. Iran itself has been a test case — years of exclusion from SWIFT have forced development of alternative payment channels that other sanctioned parties can use. The sanctions toolkit that worked so effectively in 2012–2015 may be less effective in a more multipolar financial system.

## How to trade it / The playbook

![JCPOA positioning playbook from deal monitoring to specific trades](/imgs/blogs/jcpoa-the-iran-nuclear-deal-oil-markets-and-geopolitical-chess-7.png)

Trading JCPOA events requires a systematic framework built around three components: deal probability tracking, supply delta estimation, and instrument selection. Ad hoc reactions to headlines produce poor results because geopolitical events move fast and markets price them within hours.

### Step 1: Map deal probability through leading indicators

Track the following signals in order of reliability:

**IAEA quarterly reports** (highest reliability): The IAEA publishes quarterly technical reports on Iran's nuclear activities — centrifuge counts, enrichment levels, inspector access, safeguards compliance. When Iran violates agreed limits (as it has done repeatedly since 2019), it signals deal deterioration. When Iran cooperates with IAEA requests, it signals potential for engagement. These reports land approximately 60 days after the reporting period and move markets modestly.

**P5+1 diplomatic calendar** (medium reliability): Announced negotiating sessions create binary events — deal progress or breakdown. When talks are scheduled, deal probability rises slightly. When scheduled talks are cancelled or parties issue pre-conditions, deal probability falls. Track European foreign ministry statements; EU-3 collectively is typically the most candid about deal status.

**US Treasury OFAC designations** (medium reliability): New designations of Iranian entities signal escalation. Licenses or waivers signal relaxation. OFAC publishes these in the Federal Register; they are often announced on Fridays (minimizing immediate market impact) and priced into Asian market opens on Monday.

**Iranian domestic politics** (lower reliability, higher impact): Presidential elections, Majlis votes, and Supreme Leader statements represent step-changes in negotiating posture. The pattern has been: pragmatist presidents (Rafsanjani, Khatami, Rouhani) favor engagement; hardliner presidents (Ahmadinejad, Raisi) do not. However, Supreme Leader Khamenei's position matters more than any president's — all major foreign policy decisions require his approval.

**Tanker tracking data** (real-time): Commercial providers (Kpler, Vortexa, TankerTrackers) provide week-by-week Iranian crude export estimates based on satellite and AIS ship-tracking data. This is the purest leading indicator of physical supply changes — it confirms or contradicts diplomatic signals with actual barrels. When tanker trackers show Iranian exports rising despite ongoing sanctions, it signals effective sanctions evasion via China.

### Step 2: Estimate the supply delta

For each deal scenario, construct a supply estimate:

Full sanctions relief (JCPOA restoration):
- Year 1: Iran adds 0.8 mb/d (banking/logistics bottlenecks limit ramp)
- Year 2: Iran adds 1.2–1.5 mb/d (infrastructure restored, buyers re-contracted)
- Peak: Iran returns to approximately 2.5 mb/d exports (close to 2018 pre-withdrawal levels)

Partial waivers (US permits select buyers):
- Immediate: Iran adds 0.3–0.5 mb/d (waiver recipients only)
- Effect: Moderate price relief, not structural shift

Maximum pressure maintained:
- Iran holds at 0.4–0.8 mb/d (China absorbs some regardless of US pressure)
- Price support continues

Apply the price rule of thumb: 1 mb/d supply addition to a 100 mb/d market = approximately 1% supply increase = Brent moves \$7–12/barrel in a normally-supplied market, less if OPEC has significant unused spare capacity, more if markets are already tight.

### Step 3: Choose the instrument for the scenario

Different Iran scenarios call for different instruments:

**Deal breakthrough expected (oil price falls):**
- Buy Brent put options (defined risk, uncapped downside capture if oil falls sharply)
- Short XLE (SPDR Energy ETF) via puts to avoid unlimited loss from short squeeze
- Short Brent crude futures if conviction is high and position size is managed
- Consider airline equity longs as indirect beneficiary of cheaper jet fuel

**Deal collapse expected (oil price rises):**
- Buy Brent call options (asymmetric payoff suits geopolitical binary)
- Long XLE and XOP (oil producers ETF) equity
- Long tanker stocks (STNG, NVGS) if Iranian shadow fleet competes less with standard shipping
- Consider gold longs (dual benefit: oil supply risk premium + safe-haven demand)

**Military escalation risk (uncertainty spike):**
- Long gold (response within hours, faster than oil)
- Long VIX calls (volatility instrument, fastest response to uncertainty)
- Short EM equities (MSCI EM ETF puts)
- Short EM FX basket (particularly oil-importing EM: Turkey, India, Pakistan)

**Strait of Hormuz tail risk hedge:**
- Small position in deep out-of-the-money Brent calls (far-dated, e.g., 6 months, \$100+ strike) as tail risk protection
- These expire worthless in most scenarios but provide massive payoff if Hormuz closes

#### Worked example: sizing a geopolitical tail hedge

A fund with \$200 million AUM wants to protect against Hormuz closure risk (estimated probability: 2–3% over the next 12 months, but catastrophic if realized — oil could spike to \$150+/barrel).

Current Brent: \$77/barrel
Tail risk position: 6-month Brent call, \$110 strike
Option premium: approximately \$1.50/barrel (deep out of the money, low delta)
Position size: 1% of NAV = \$2 million premium budget
Contracts: \$2,000,000 / \$1,500 per contract = approximately 1,333 contracts × 1,000 bbl = 1.333 million barrels exposure

If Hormuz closes and Brent reaches \$140:
- Call intrinsic value: \$140 − \$110 = \$30/barrel
- Position value: \$30 × 1,333,000 = \$39.99 million
- Net profit: \$39.99 million − \$2 million premium = approximately \$38 million
- Portfolio return from hedge: +19% (\$38M / \$200M)

This tail hedge earns nothing in the base case (2% premium cost) but provides meaningful portfolio protection in the low-probability extreme scenario. For a long-risk portfolio (long equities, long EM, short volatility), this tail hedge reduces maximum drawdown from a Hormuz event from potentially -30% to approximately -15%.

### Step 4: Position sizing discipline

Geopolitical event trades carry unique sizing risks:

**Binary outcome risk:** Unlike earnings trades where outcomes cluster around analyst estimates, geopolitical events can produce outcomes far outside any analyst's range. Position sizes should be smaller than for fundamental trades — typically 0.5–1.5% of NAV rather than 3–5%.

**Timing uncertainty:** You can have the right directional thesis and lose money because the event occurs outside your option's expiry window. Use options with sufficient time to expiry (3–6 months minimum for deal-related trades).

**Correlation risk:** Iran events correlate with other Middle East risk events — a spike in Iran tensions often moves Saudi Arabia risk, Strait of Hormuz risk, and Lebanon risk simultaneously. A portfolio long oil, long gold, short airlines, and short EM around Iran events is effectively one concentrated geopolitical bet, not four separate positions.

**Liquidity risk:** In the immediate aftermath of escalation events, bid-ask spreads on oil options widen significantly (sometimes to 10–15% of option premium). Entering options positions after events have already moved markets is expensive. The best entry points are during calm periods with low implied volatility and elevated (but not yet extreme) geopolitical tension.
