> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — When a geopolitical shock hits, the most important question is not "how bad is this?" but "is this a spike or a regime change?" — because the two demand completely opposite trading responses.
>
> - A **spike** is a temporary, fear-driven price move that fully reverses within days to weeks once the initial shock fades — it happened to Brent crude after the Gaza war in October 2023 (\$92 to \$78 in eight weeks).
> - A **regime change** is a permanent structural repricing of one or more asset classes because physical flows, alliances, or policy frameworks have irreversibly shifted — it happened to European natural gas after Ukraine 2022 (from \$25 to a new baseline of \$34-46 EUR/MWh permanently).
> - Four tests separate them in real time: supply disruption duration, alliance/coalition response, policy permanence, and corporate capex signal.
> - The critical diagnostic window is the **48-72 hours** after the shock — the fog of war period when you cannot yet tell which path you are on.
> - Of roughly 50 major geopolitical events since 2001, approximately 70% were pure spikes, 15% were partial regime changes affecting one asset class, and only 15% were full regime changes repricing multiple asset classes simultaneously.
> - The correct fog-of-war trade is **small size in the direction of uncertainty** — long gold, long volatility — not directional bets until the four tests give a verdict.

On February 24, 2022, at 5:57 AM Moscow time, the first Russian missiles crossed into Ukrainian territory. By the time European markets opened, Brent crude had gapped from \$96 to \$102. Natural gas prices spiked 20% in the first hour. The ruble cratered. Every experienced trader on earth was making the same split-second calculation: is this a spike, or is this something different?

The traders who got it right understood that the question was not about the size of the initial move — it was about whether the underlying physical reality of European energy markets had just changed permanently. Within 90 days, it became clear it had. Russian pipeline gas to Europe, which had been flowing at 155 billion cubic meters per year in 2021, would never recover. By 2024 it was down to 14 billion cubic meters — a 91% permanent structural collapse. The traders who treated the initial spike as a dip-buying opportunity in European energy got destroyed. The ones who recognized a regime change restructured their entire energy exposure.

![Spike anatomy versus regime change anatomy with three price phases each](/imgs/blogs/reading-a-geopolitical-shock-spike-vs-regime-change-1.png)

Now contrast that with October 7, 2023. The Hamas attack on Israel was horrific. The geopolitical shock was genuine. Brent crude spiked 5% on the news. Every headline said "Middle East war" and every trader's initial instinct was to buy oil aggressively. But by the four-test framework I will walk you through in this post, the signals were clear within 48 hours: no Strait of Hormuz disruption, no new formal alliance, no sanctions legislation, no energy company changing its drilling plans. Brent faded from \$95 back to \$78 within eight weeks. The traders who bought the spike at \$95 thinking it was a regime change lost 18% in two months. The traders who recognized a spike sold into the fear.

These two events — separated by eighteen months, superficially similar (both were major armed conflicts), but producing diametrically opposite asset price outcomes — represent the central diagnostic challenge of geopolitical investing. This post gives you the framework to tell them apart in real time.

## Foundations: The Basic Building Blocks of a Geopolitical Market Event

Before we get into the four tests, you need to understand three foundational concepts: what a geopolitical risk premium is, how markets price uncertainty, and what the difference between a temporary and a permanent price change actually means.

### What is a geopolitical risk premium?

Every asset price is, at its core, a forecast about the future. When you buy a barrel of oil at \$80, you are implicitly accepting that the future stream of oil supply and demand makes \$80 a fair clearing price. A geopolitical risk premium is the additional price you pay (or demand) on top of that fundamental fair value because of uncertainty about whether the political situation will disrupt supply, demand, or both.

Think of it like car insurance. Your car is worth \$20,000. But if you live in a neighborhood where car theft is suddenly up 400%, you pay a higher insurance premium — not because your car became more valuable, but because the risk of losing it increased. The geopolitical risk premium works the same way: oil at \$96 versus \$80 might represent a \$16 risk premium for the possibility that Russian gas gets cut off.

The Geopolitical Risk Index (GPR), developed by economists Caldara and Iacoviello at the Federal Reserve, measures this quantitatively. It counts the frequency of geopolitical risk-related newspaper articles in major publications and creates a monthly index. A GPR of 100 is the long-run average; a GPR of 300 means three times as many fear-related articles as normal. This index, as we will see, is itself a signal — because it sometimes rises weeks before a major shock, and sometimes stays flat until the day of the attack.

### The difference between price level and price equilibrium

The most important distinction in this framework is between a **temporary departure from equilibrium** and a **shift in the equilibrium itself**.

Imagine the price of oil as a ball resting in a bowl. Normal market forces — supply, demand, inventory — keep the ball in the middle of the bowl. A geopolitical shock is like someone giving the ball a hard push. If the bowl is still the right shape (supply routes intact, alliances unchanged, policy the same), the ball rolls around but returns to center. That is a spike.

A regime change is like someone physically reshaping the bowl. Maybe they add a wall on one side (sanctions block a major supplier), or they tilt the whole thing (a new alliance rewires global energy flows). Now the ball rolls to a new position and **stays there**. No amount of time will bring it back to the old center, because the old center no longer exists.

This distinction has radical consequences for how you position. In a spike, time heals. Buying the fear is profitable. In a regime change, time compounds the damage. The old price was wrong. The longer you wait, the further you fall behind the new reality.

### How markets price geopolitical uncertainty

Markets do not wait for certainty. They price probabilities. When a major geopolitical shock occurs, the initial price move is almost always an overreaction — the market is pricing in a range of outcomes, some of which are catastrophic. This is why the initial spike almost always looks like a regime change, regardless of whether it is one.

The *volatility spike* is the purest expression of this. The VIX — the "fear index" that measures expected S&P 500 volatility over 30 days — spiked 19% in the first day after the Gaza war. It spiked 31% after Russia invaded Ukraine. The moves look similar. But the VIX returned to baseline in 21 days after Gaza and took 45 days after Ukraine. Why the difference? Because in Ukraine, the news kept getting worse as the physical reality of the supply cut became undeniable, while in Gaza, the news stabilized as the supply disruption that was feared never materialized.

The key insight is this: **the initial price move tells you about fear, not about fundamentals.** Fear is universal. Fundamentals are specific. The four-test framework is how you get from fear to fundamentals in real time.

## The Four Tests: Diagnosing a Shock in Real Time

Here is the diagnostic framework. Apply all four tests simultaneously in the 48-72 hours after a major geopolitical shock fires.

![Four diagnostic tests pipeline from supply through alliance policy capex to spike or regime change verdict](/imgs/blogs/reading-a-geopolitical-shock-spike-vs-regime-change-2.png)

### Test 1: Supply Disruption Duration

The first and most important test is physical: has the actual supply of a critical commodity been disrupted, and if so, for how long?

This test asks three nested questions:
1. Is the disrupted nation or chokepoint actually a meaningful supplier? (Israel and Gaza are not oil producers; the Persian Gulf is a chokepoint for 20% of global oil.)
2. Is the disruption physically occurring right now, or is it a future risk? (Russia cutting gas to Europe happened in real time; Iran sanctions reduced oil flows but gradually.)
3. Is the disruption likely to be permanent (infrastructure destroyed, trade routes permanently severed) or temporary (a pipeline can be repaired, a shipping lane can be cleared)?

The key metric to watch in real time is **tanker traffic through key chokepoints** — particularly the Strait of Hormuz (critical for Gulf oil), the Turkish Straits (for Black Sea grain and energy), and the Strait of Malacca (Asia-Pacific supply chains). In the first 48 hours after the Gaza war, tanker traffic through the Strait of Hormuz was essentially unchanged. That was Test 1: fail. No supply disruption.

After the Ukraine invasion, within 72 hours, Russia had announced the suspension of pipeline operations through Ukraine, and Germany had halted Nord Stream 2 certification. That was Test 1: immediate supply disruption signal. The question then became duration — and the physical destruction of the Nord Stream pipelines in September 2022 answered that definitively.

**Scoring**: If supply is disrupted and the duration looks permanent (infrastructure destroyed, permanent sanctions on state oil company), score 1 point toward regime change. If supply is untouched or the disruption is clearly temporary (a weather delay, a brief blockade), score 0.

### Test 2: Alliance and Coalition Response

The second test looks at the political architecture: did the shock cause a lasting change in formal alliances, coalitions, or multilateral institutions?

This matters because alliances create durable policy commitments. When Finland and Sweden joined NATO after Ukraine's invasion — something unthinkable in 2021 — that permanently changed European defense spending calculus. Defense budgets that had been falling for decades suddenly had hard NATO commitments (2% of GDP) enforced by treaty obligation. That is permanent.

Contrast with the October 7 response. The US reaffirmed its security commitment to Israel, which everyone already knew. The EU issued statements. The UN Security Council vetoed resolutions. Hezbollah fired some rockets but stopped well short of an all-out war. No new formal alliance was created. No new multilateral institution was formed. The geopolitical architecture was the same the week after October 7 as it had been the week before.

The signals to watch in real time:
- Emergency summits of NATO, G7, EU, or ASEAN within 72 hours (a signal that coordination is happening at the treaty level)
- Formal declarations of Article 5 of NATO (collective defense) — this has never been triggered, but even debate about it would be regime-change-level
- New bilateral security treaties or base agreements
- Expansion of existing sanctions coalitions (the US alone sanctioning Russia is less powerful than G7+EU+Japan+Australia doing it jointly)

**Scoring**: Clear formal alliance formation or expansion scores 1 point. Political statements, reaffirmations, and symbolic solidarity do not.

### Test 3: Policy Permanence

The third test distinguishes legislation from executive action. This sounds technical but it is decisive.

An executive order can be reversed with a phone call. The US president can sign an executive order sanctioning Iranian oil today and rescind it tomorrow with the same authority. Phase One trade deal concessions made under Trump were reversed by Trump himself. The sanctions architecture around Russia that was built after Crimea in 2014 was partially dismantled as energy politics evolved.

Legislation is different. When the US Congress passed the \$369 billion Inflation Reduction Act in August 2022 — which locked in massive subsidies for domestic clean energy manufacturing — that was permanent in a way that executive orders are not. Reversing it would require Congress to pass new legislation, which requires majorities, floor time, and political capital. The EU's Net Zero Industry Act required qualified majority voting across 27 member states. Both created irreversible incentive structures for companies to build clean energy infrastructure in the US and EU.

The practical test: in the first 48-72 hours after a shock, is legislation being *proposed* in parliament or congress, or is it just executive branch statements? Are regulators issuing formal rules changes (SEC, Fed, OFAC), or just "monitoring the situation"? OFAC — the Office of Foreign Assets Control — issuing a new Specially Designated Nationals (SDN) list is a concrete, legally binding act. A Treasury press release about "watching the situation closely" is not.

**Scoring**: Legislation passed, formal regulatory rule changes, or central bank policy shifts score 1 point. Press conferences, statements of concern, and proposed legislation that has not passed score 0.

### Test 4: Corporate Capex Signal

The fourth test is where market intelligence lives: are the companies that have the most to gain or lose from this shock changing their actual capital investment plans?

Corporate capital expenditure (capex) — spending on physical assets like oil rigs, factories, pipeline infrastructure, semiconductor fabs, or LNG terminals — is the most credible signal available because it costs money to change. A company does not alter its five-year \$50 billion investment plan on the basis of a temporary fear. They alter it when they believe the structural economics of their industry have permanently shifted.

After Ukraine, the signals arrived quickly:
- Germany announced it would build four Floating Storage and Regasification Units (FSRUs) — temporary LNG import terminals — within months, something that normally takes years
- QatarEnergy signed 15-year LNG supply contracts with European utilities (long-term capex commitment)
- European defense companies received multi-year government contracts committing to sustained production increases
- Energy companies in North America announced expansion of LNG export terminals to capture the newly price-competitive European market

None of this happened after October 7. No major energy company changed its drilling budget. No defense company received a new significant contract related to the conflict. No LNG terminal changed its export plan. The capex signal was silent.

Where to look in real time:
- Energy company investor days and Q3/Q4 earnings calls (often within 2-4 weeks of a major shock — executives discuss capex guidance)
- Defense contractor order backlogs (announced via SEC filings)
- Central bank emergency rate decisions (signals they see structural inflation)
- Semiconductor industry fab announcements (after chip wars accelerate)

**Scoring**: Major companies publicly changing multi-year capex guidance, signing long-term supply contracts, or receiving large government procurement contracts scores 1 point.

### Reading the Scorecard

The verdict is simple: count the tests that fired.

- **0-1 tests firing**: Almost certainly a spike. The appropriate response is patience. If you have exposure, reduce modestly if you are uncomfortable, but the correct directional trade is to fade the initial fear move once the market has found a new support level (typically 3-7 days in).
- **2 tests firing**: Partial regime change. Expect one or two asset classes to reprice permanently. Go deeper on which specific assets are affected rather than making a broad macro call.
- **3-4 tests firing**: Full regime change. This is a different investment landscape. Do not buy dips expecting mean reversion. The mean is moving. Reassess every position in the affected asset classes.

## The Fog of War: The Critical 48-72 Hours

The most dangerous period for positioning is not the initial shock — it is the fog of war window that follows.

![Brent crude and TTF gas price paths from Ukraine 2022 through Gaza 2023 showing permanent vs spike repricing](/imgs/blogs/reading-a-geopolitical-shock-spike-vs-regime-change-3.png)

In this window, two errors are equally costly:

**Error Type A**: You treat a regime change as a spike. You buy the dip. You get in at what seems like a reasonable entry, and then watch the asset class systematically re-rate lower (or higher) over weeks and months as each new supply cut, alliance move, or policy shift confirms the new reality. This is what happened to traders who bought European energy stocks in March 2022 expecting a quick recovery.

**Error Type B**: You treat a spike as a regime change. You make a large directional bet on the thesis that the world has fundamentally changed. You hold through the reversal. You lose the full initial move and possibly more as the fear premium dissipates. This is what happened to traders who bought oil at \$95 after October 7 expecting an Iran-broadening scenario.

The fog of war is genuinely difficult because geopolitical events are reported dramatically, market moves feel decisive, and there is enormous social and cognitive pressure to take a clear position. The financial media will declare a regime change within hours — they need a story. The correct epistemic posture is humility: acknowledge that you do not know yet, and size your position accordingly.

The fog-of-war positioning principle is: **own the option, not the directional bet.** In practice this means:

1. **Long volatility in small size** — options premiums spike immediately after geopolitical shocks, so you are paying up for vol protection, but you are protected against being wrong about the direction. This is the genuine insurance play.
2. **Long gold in small size** — gold is the cleanest safe-haven asset with the fewest geopolitical dependencies. It typically gains 1-3% after a major shock regardless of direction.
3. **Avoid large directional positions in the specific affected commodity** until at least two of the four tests have resolved.

#### Worked example:

**Kelly fraction during the fog of war.**

You are 24 hours into the Gaza war. You believe:
- 40% probability this is a pure spike (Brent fades back to \$82 in three weeks, net gain of \$0 if you hold; net loss of \$8 if you bought the dip at \$95 before the reversal)
- 60% probability this escalates into a regime change (Strait of Hormuz closes for 30 days, Brent reaches \$130, net gain of \$35 on a \$95 entry)

The Kelly criterion for position sizing is:

`f = (b * p - q) / b`

where:
- `b` = net odds received on winning bet (here: 35/8 = 4.375 in oil terms)
- `p` = probability of winning (0.60)
- `q` = probability of losing (0.40)

So: `f = (4.375 × 0.60 - 0.40) / 4.375 = (2.625 - 0.40) / 4.375 = 2.225 / 4.375 = 0.509`

The Kelly formula says bet 50.9% of capital. But that is the mathematically optimal fraction for a bettor who will repeat this exact trade hundreds of times. The critical adjustment is that this estimate of 60% probability is itself uncertain — you are in the fog of war, and your probability estimate has a wide confidence interval. Standard risk management practice is to use **half-Kelly** when probabilities are uncertain: bet 25% of capital.

Even more practically, in the fog-of-war window, the uncertainty about which scenario is happening is itself new information about your probability estimate. The probability 0.60 you assigned is essentially a guess. A safer approach:

- Wait 24 hours. If the Strait of Hormuz traffic data (available in near-real-time from Lloyd's and shipping data services) shows unchanged flow, downgrade your regime-change probability to 30%, which gives you `f = (4.375 × 0.30 - 0.70) / 4.375 = 0.11` — bet only 11% of capital, and at half-Kelly, 5.5%.
- That is approximately where a disciplined practitioner ends up: a very small position in Brent (5-10% of normal size) combined with a long-volatility hedge, not a full-conviction directional bet.

The October 7 lesson is that the probability of a Strait of Hormuz closure was around 10-15% in practice — it never happened. The Kelly-adjusted sizing would have correctly recommended near-zero exposure.

## The GPR Index as a Leading Indicator

One underappreciated tool available to geopolitical investors is the Geopolitical Risk Index. Because it is based on newspaper article frequency, it can be tracked daily (the academic monthly release has daily variants maintained by the researchers). More importantly, it has an asymmetric property that is practically useful:

**For planned/telegraphed events, GPR rises weeks in advance. For genuine surprise events, GPR spikes on the day.**

![GPR index pre-elevated before Ukraine 2022 versus flat before Gaza October 2023 attack showing surprise versus telegraphed events](/imgs/blogs/reading-a-geopolitical-shock-spike-vs-regime-change-7.png)

Look at the Ukraine 2022 data. From October 2021 onwards, the GPR index was elevated — from its long-run average of around 100 to readings of 120-140 before the invasion, and then exploding to 345 in February 2022. This pre-elevation was the fingerprint of a telegraphed event: US intelligence agencies were publicly (if vaguely) warning about Russian troop buildups, satellite imagery was being published, diplomatic channels were clearly under stress.

Now look at October 7, 2023. The GPR index was at approximately 110-118 in the weeks before the attack — right around baseline. The attack itself was a complete strategic surprise; Hamas had spent months concealing its preparation. The GPR spiked to 188 in October 2023, but there was no pre-elevation.

**The practical implication**: If GPR is pre-elevated by 30-50% above baseline over multiple months, you should be in enhanced alert mode for the affected region. You should already have some defensive positioning — long gold, reduced exposure to regional equities and FX — before the shock happens. The pre-elevation of GPR before Ukraine gave sophisticated investors months of warning to reduce European energy exposure.

If GPR spikes suddenly from baseline, the event was a surprise. Surprise events are more likely to be spikes (because planned, telegraphed events are more often regime changes — they involve deliberate, coordinated political decisions rather than spontaneous escalation). This is a probabilistic heuristic, not a certainty, but it shifts your prior toward spike interpretation for genuine surprises.

## Historical Hit Rates: Spike or Regime Change?

Of the major geopolitical events since 2001, here is the retrospective classification using the four-test framework:

![Timeline of major geopolitical shocks from 2001 to 2024 classified as spike partial regime or full regime change](/imgs/blogs/reading-a-geopolitical-shock-spike-vs-regime-change-5.png)

**Full regime changes (multiple asset classes permanently repriced)**:
- 9/11 attacks (2001): US defense spending increased from ~3% of GDP to 4.7% — and never fully returned. A new global intelligence-sharing and counter-terrorism apparatus was built. Defense contractor stocks repriced permanently upward. Airport security, cyber infrastructure, surveillance technology — all permanently repriced. Time for equity markets to recover: 60 days. Time for defense sector repricing: permanent.
- US-China trade war (2018-present): Supply chains permanently re-routed. Vietnam FDI went from \$14.5 billion in 2017 to \$25 billion in 2024. Apple shifted iPhone assembly from China to India and Vietnam. Samsung's Vietnamese electronics exports reached \$115 billion annually. The decoupling is structural.
- COVID-19 pandemic (2020): Energy capex collapsed globally — oil companies cut \$130 billion in annual investment in 2020 alone. That supply underinvestment caused the \$120 per barrel oil spike in 2022. Remote work permanently shifted commercial real estate. Fiscal spending crossed thresholds (Japan at 260% debt/GDP, US at 130%) that have not retreated.
- Russia-Ukraine invasion (2022): Full European energy infrastructure rebuild. Russian gas to Europe: 198 bcm → 8 bcm. EU LNG capacity: 157 bcm → 310 bcm. European defense budgets permanently elevated.

**Partial regime changes (one asset class permanently repriced)**:
- Crimea annexation (2014): Russian equities permanently repriced lower (-30% sustained), ruble permanently weaker. But no supply disruption; energy flows continued; most global asset markets treated it as a spike.
- Arab Spring/Libya (2011): Libya oil production cut by ~80% (from 1.6 mbpd to 0.1 mbpd) for 18 months. Brent premium sustained for 2 years. But other Middle East flows intact; broader energy infrastructure unchanged.
- Iran nuclear deal and sanctions cycles (2012-2024): Iran oil exports permanently volatile — from 2.5 mbpd to 0.5 mbpd depending on sanctions state. An asset class (Iranian crude allocation in Asia) was repeatedly repriced.

**Pure spikes (fully reversed within 30 days)**:
- Iraq War start (2003): Oil spiked 30% on invasion day, then fell 15% as Baghdad fell faster than expected. Reversed within 45 days.
- Georgia-Russia war (2008): Oil was already in a bubble; this added briefly. Resolved in 12 days. No lasting asset impact.
- Soleimani killing (2020): Brent +3% for 2 days, then faded as Iran's retaliation was measured and no escalation followed.
- Gaza war Oct 7 (2023): Brent +5%, VIX +19%, gold +1.5%. All fully reversed within 21 days (VIX) to 8 weeks (Brent).
- Iran-Israel direct exchange (2024): Oil +3%. Reversed in 7 days as both sides chose de-escalation.

The approximate historical hit rates:
- Pure spikes (recovered <30 days): ~65-70% of major events
- Partial regime change (1 asset class): ~15-20%
- Full regime change (multiple asset classes): ~10-15%

This means that **the base rate strongly favors spike interpretation**. You should require evidence to upgrade your assessment from "spike" to "regime change," not the reverse.

## Applying the Framework: Two Case Studies in Real Time

### The Oct 7, 2023 Gaza War: A Textbook Spike

#### Worked example:

Let us walk through all four tests as they would have been applied in the 48 hours after October 7, 2023.

**Event**: Hamas launched a surprise attack on Israel on October 7, 2023. The scale and coordination were unprecedented. Over 1,200 Israelis were killed, the largest single-day loss of Israeli life since 1948. Israeli forces mobilized for a ground offensive into Gaza.

**Initial market reaction**: Brent crude gapped from \$84 to \$92 (approximately +9.5%) on Monday morning, October 9 (markets were closed October 7-8). Gold gained \$30 (approximately +1.5%). Israeli shekel depreciated 2%. VIX rose from 16 to 19. Tel Aviv Stock Exchange (TASE) fell 6%.

**Test 1 — Supply Disruption**: Run through the physical geography. Israel and Gaza are not oil producers. The Palestinian Territories have no meaningful energy infrastructure that supplies global markets. The key question was: does this conflict threaten the Strait of Hormuz? Iran was widely assumed to be a sponsor of Hamas. If Iran directly entered the conflict, the Strait could be threatened. But within 24 hours:
- US carriers (USS Gerald R. Ford and USS Dwight D. Eisenhower) were positioned in the Eastern Mediterranean and Persian Gulf, explicitly to deter Iranian escalation
- Tanker tracking data showed unchanged flow through Hormuz
- Hezbollah began some cross-border attacks but explicitly stayed below the threshold of full warfare
- Iran denied direct operational involvement
- Test 1 result: **No supply disruption. Score: 0.**

**Test 2 — Alliance Shift**: The US reaffirmed its security commitment to Israel (which had been unchanged for 75 years). The EU condemned Hamas. The UN Security Council was deadlocked, as usual. No new formal alliance formed. No new multilateral institution. The geopolitical architecture — US-Israel alliance, Iran as adversary, Arab states normalizing with Israel under Abraham Accords — was effectively unchanged.
- Test 2 result: **No alliance shift. Score: 0.**

**Test 3 — Policy Permanence**: No new sanctions were passed (Iran was already under maximum pressure sanctions). No new legislation moved through Congress. Executive branch statements were about support for Israel and warnings to Iran, not new policy mechanisms. The Abraham Accords (Israel-UAE, Israel-Bahrain) were not formally suspended.
- Test 3 result: **No policy permanence. Score: 0.**

**Test 4 — Corporate Capex**: Contacted energy analysts covering Middle East operations in the days after October 7. No energy company changed its production guidance for the quarter. No LNG operator changed shipping plans. No defense contractor received an order that meaningfully changed its backlog. The Israeli tech sector — a significant part of the Tel Aviv Stock Exchange — had no material change in R&D spending plans.
- Test 4 result: **No capex signal. Score: 0.**

**Total score: 0/4. Verdict: Spike.**

**Outcome**: Brent peaked at \$95 on October 19, 2023 — 12 days after the attack. Then it faded. By December 2023 it was at \$78. The traders who bought Brent at \$92-\$95 on the spike, expecting Iran involvement and a Strait of Hormuz scenario, lost approximately 17-18% in six weeks. The traders who applied the four tests, recognized a spike, and either held short or bought put options were well-compensated.

The key lesson: **the size of the initial price move does not determine whether it is a spike or a regime change.** Brent moved 9.5% on October 9. It had moved 7% on the day of the Soleimani killing in January 2020, which was also a spike. The direction and magnitude of the initial move are informative about fear; they are not informative about whether the underlying physical and political reality has changed.

### The Feb 24, 2022 Ukraine Invasion: A Textbook Regime Change

#### Worked example:

Now let us run the same framework on February 24, 2022.

**Event**: Russia launched a full-scale invasion of Ukraine at 5:57 AM Moscow time. This was the first land war in Europe involving a nuclear power since 1945.

**Initial market reaction**: Brent crude gapped from \$96 to \$102 (+6.3%). TTF European natural gas spiked from \$83 to \$112 (+35%) in the first day. MSCI Russia (MOEX) fell from 1,390 to 740 (-47%). EUR/USD fell from 1.132 to 1.118. VIX rose from 28.1 to 31.8.

**Test 1 — Supply Disruption**: Russia is the world's largest natural gas exporter and the second-largest oil exporter. Within the first 72 hours:
- Germany's Chancellor Scholz suspended Nord Stream 2 pipeline certification — a \$10 billion pipeline project that would have doubled Russian gas capacity to Germany
- Within two weeks, multiple Ukrainian pipelines were physically damaged
- Russia itself announced gas-for-rubles policy, threatening to cut gas to "unfriendly" European nations
- The GPR index reading of 345 reflected not just fear but actual physical threat to supply infrastructure
- Test 1 result: **Supply disruption confirmed. Duration: uncertain but longer-term. Score: 1.**

**Test 2 — Alliance Shift**: The NATO response was extraordinary. Within 30 days:
- Finland announced it would apply for NATO membership (abandoned 70+ years of neutrality)
- Sweden announced the same
- NATO activated its emergency response plans for eastern members
- EU imposed sanctions as a bloc for the first time on a major energy supplier
- Germany reversed decades of policy to commit to 100 billion euro emergency defense spending
- Test 2 result: **Major alliance shift. NATO formal expansion confirmed. Score: 1.**

**Test 3 — Policy Permanence**: Within three months:
- EU passed the REPowerEU plan (legislative, not executive) to reduce Russian gas dependence to zero by 2030
- US passed the Inflation Reduction Act (\$369 billion in clean energy subsidies) — explicitly motivated in part by the need to wean allies off Russian energy
- SWIFT exclusion of major Russian banks was legislative (EU Council decision, requiring unanimity — reversible but politically extremely difficult)
- G7 agreed to the oil price cap mechanism, requiring each member's legislative body to implement
- Test 3 result: **Legislation passed in multiple jurisdictions. Score: 1.**

**Test 4 — Corporate Capex**: By Q2 2022 (6-8 weeks after invasion):
- Shell, TotalEnergies, BP, and Equinor all announced exits from Russian joint ventures — writing off billions in capex
- Germany ordered emergency FSRU construction (floating LNG import terminals) with accelerated procurement — \$3-5 billion in committed spending
- QatarEnergy signed 15-27 year LNG supply contracts with German utilities (Hanwha, Eni, SEFE)
- US LNG export terminal operators (Sabine Pass, Corpus Christi) announced capacity expansions totaling \$20+ billion
- European defense companies (KNDS, Rheinmetall, BAE Systems) received multi-year government procurement contracts
- Test 4 result: **Major capex signal. Multiple companies changing multi-year investment plans. Score: 1.**

**Total score: 4/4. Verdict: Full regime change.**

**Outcome**: The European energy market permanently repriced. Russian pipeline gas to Europe went from 155 bcm in 2021 to 85 bcm in 2022 (war year) to 27 bcm in 2023 to 14 bcm in 2024. TTF gas prices, which had been \$25 EUR/MWh before the pandemic, reached \$310 EUR/MWh in August 2022, and settled into a permanently higher range of \$34-46 EUR/MWh — higher than the 2015-2019 baseline of \$15-25 EUR/MWh and likely to remain so because the LNG-based supply mix (spot-priced, globally traded) structurally costs more than pipeline gas (long-term contract, Russia-subsidized).

![Russia gas to Europe collapsing while EU LNG capacity doubles showing permanent structural energy regime change](/imgs/blogs/reading-a-geopolitical-shock-spike-vs-regime-change-4.png)

The traders and portfolio managers who recognized the four-test signals within the first two weeks and restructured their European energy exposure accordingly captured the regime change. ## The Fog-of-War Positioning Matrix

With the four tests understood, here is how to size positions in the specific fog-of-war window before the tests have resolved.

![Fog of war positioning matrix with event types versus first 48 hour supply and alliance signals](/imgs/blogs/reading-a-geopolitical-shock-spike-vs-regime-change-6.png)

The matrix applies SIZE from 0 (do nothing) to 5 (full conviction) based on the combination of event type and the early signals that arrive in the first 24-48 hours, even before the full four tests can resolve.

The key insight in the matrix is that different event types have different base-rate probabilities of regime change:

- **Wars between major powers** (state vs state, especially nuclear-adjacent): Higher base rate of regime change because both sides have the resources and motivation to make supply-chain changes permanent. Size up faster when early signals confirm.
- **Sanctions**: Almost always regime changes in the targeted asset class (currency, sovereign bonds, specific commodities), but the question is extent — do they bite, and for how long? Early signal is whether major Western allies join (G7 coalition vs. US-alone).
- **Trade wars**: Regime changes in supply chains but on slower timescales (months to years). Early signal is whether affected companies start publicly discussing relocation.
- **Coups and political instability**: High spike rate because leaders of coups typically need to sell oil/commodities to fund their rule. Supply disruptions are almost always temporary.

#### Worked example:

**Applying the matrix to a hypothetical future scenario.**

It is 2:00 AM and your phone alerts you: North Korea has conducted a nuclear test and simultaneously fired three ballistic missiles over Japan. Markets are about to open with a -3% gap in Asian equities. What do you do?

Walk through the matrix:
- **Event type**: State vs. state tension (not an invasion). Row = WAR.
- **Supply disruption in first 24h**: North Korea has no meaningful commodity exports; Japan is a major importer. The question is whether US-Japan military response might cause a naval incident in the Korean Strait that disrupts shipping. Unlikely in first 24h. Early signal: Japan-US joint statement (already routine) but no naval blockade. Call it: **Supply NOT disrupted yet.**
- **Alliance shift signal**: US immediately invokes its mutual defense treaty with Japan (existing). No new alliance formed, but this could escalate to China involvement. China's response is the swing factor — if China publicly backs North Korea, you have an alliance formation signal. If China stays neutral or condemns, no new alliance. In the first 24h, China typically stays silent. Call it: **Alliance formation unclear, lean toward no.**
- **Matrix cell**: WAR × Supply OK × No Alliance → SIZE: 1/5.

So in the first 24h: go small (1/5 of normal position size), long gold (which gains in safe-haven mode), long VIX, short Korean won/Japanese yen pair, short Korean equity futures modestly. Do not make a large directional bet.

As the next 48 hours resolve, update continuously:
- If China condemns North Korea alongside the US: upgrade to spike, consider fading the dip in Korean equities
- If China supports North Korea or stays ambiguous: upgrade to partial regime change, particularly in the Taiwan Strait shipping premium

The matrix is a calibration device, not a crystal ball. Its value is in preventing you from either under-responding (missing a real regime change) or over-responding (blowing up on a spike).

## False Negatives and False Positives

Even with the four-test framework, mistakes happen. Understanding the failure modes makes you better at applying the framework.

### False Negatives: Spikes That Became Regime Changes

**Ukraine 2022 as initially priced**: In the first week of the invasion, many market participants still expected a short war. Ukrainian forces would collapse in days, Russia would install a puppet government, sanctions would be moderate, and European gas would keep flowing. By Day 7, this scenario had already been falsified by Ukrainian military resistance, but the market had not fully priced the regime change. Those who held onto the "spike" thesis through March 2022 missed the critical re-pricing window.

The signal they missed: the speed of the four tests firing. When three of the four tests confirm within the first week (supply disruption, alliance formation, legislation pending), you cannot wait for the fourth. Update your probability and size up.

### False Positives: Apparent Regime Changes That Were Spikes

#### Worked example:

**COVID March 2020 and oil: the great false positive.**

On April 20, 2020, WTI crude oil briefly traded at **negative** \$37 per barrel — the first time in history. Brent crude fell to \$19. Every energy analyst in the world was modeling "demand destruction" scenarios where EVs and work-from-home permanently reduced oil demand. Many institutional investors permanently exited energy equities.

Looking at the four tests in April 2020:
- Test 1 (Supply disruption): No supply disruption, actually supply glut (Saudi Arabia and Russia in a price war). Score: 0 for regime change.
- Test 2 (Alliance shift): OPEC+ cut 9.7 mbpd in an emergency deal — this was a supply-management alliance response, not a regime change per se. Score: partial.
- Test 3 (Policy permanence): No major legislation changing energy demand structure had passed (the IRA came in 2022). Score: 0.
- Test 4 (Capex): **This was the hidden regime change signal.** Oil company capex was cut by approximately \$130 billion globally in 2020. This supply-side capex cut was structural — once you lay off the engineers and close the rigs, it takes 2-3 years to restart. Score: 1 for supply-side regime change (not demand).

The investors who treated COVID as a demand-side regime change (sold energy permanently because "oil demand is gone") were wrong. The investors who noticed the supply-side capex signal — the permanent reduction in upstream investment — correctly predicted the \$120/barrel oil spike in June 2022 when post-COVID demand recovered but supply had been dramatically underinvested.

The quantified opportunity cost: Brent went from \$19 in April 2020 to \$120 in June 2022 — a 530% appreciation. An investor who correctly identified the supply-capex signal and bought Brent exposure at \$25 (after some clarity returned) and sold at \$110 would have achieved approximately \$85/barrel gain on a \$25 entry — a 340% return in 26 months. An investor who misread the COVID demand signal as permanent and remained out of energy missed that entire move.

The four-test framework correctly diagnosed COVID: on the demand side, it was a spike (demand recovered); on the supply side, the capex test confirmed a regime change in investment that would show up 2-3 years later. The mistake was applying the tests only to demand and ignoring supply-side capex.

**US-China Phase One trade deal (2020): The false positive.**

When the US and China signed the Phase One trade deal in January 2020, many investors believed the trade war was over — a regime change back toward free trade. Chinese equities rallied. The yuan strengthened. Supply-chain stocks fell as investors priced in normalization.

Walking through the tests in January 2020:
- Test 1: No supply disruption had occurred (trade war affects prices, not physical supply).
- Test 2: No alliance shift; the deal was bilateral executive action.
- Test 3: No legislation. Phase One was an executive-branch agreement, not a treaty or law. Trump could (and did) reverse tariff postures within months.
- Test 4: No capex shift. US companies continued moving supply chains out of China through 2020-2022, regardless of the deal. Apple, Samsung, Nike all kept diversifying.

The four tests all scored 0. The "free trade normalization" regime was not confirmed. Investors who bought Chinese equities in bulk on the thesis of trade-war resolution were disappointed — tariffs remained at 19-20% throughout the Biden administration and jumped to 145% temporarily in 2025.

## Common Misconceptions

### Misconception 1: The Bigger the Event, the More Likely It Is a Regime Change

This is the most dangerous misconception. October 7, 2023 was, by any human measure, a catastrophic event — the largest massacre of Jewish people since the Holocaust. Yet it was a market spike. The 9/11 attacks, by contrast, were also catastrophic, and they were a market regime change — but only in defense and security spending, not in oil or broad equity markets (the S&P 500 recovered its 9/11 losses within 60 days).

Event size and market permanence are independent variables. What matters is supply disruption, alliances, legislation, and capex — not the scale of human tragedy, which (properly) never belongs in a markets framework.

### Misconception 2: A Sustained Initial Price Move Means Regime Change

Brent crude held above \$110 for six weeks after the Ukraine invasion. Many traders interpreted the sustained elevated level as confirmation of regime change in oil markets. But by June 2022, Brent began falling. By December 2022 it was at \$80, barely above pre-invasion levels.

The regime change in Ukraine 2022 was in **European natural gas**, not in crude oil. Brent's sustained elevation was driven by the same fear that drives any spike, just with longer fading. The gas market, because it had no physical alternatives immediately (no LNG terminals, no spare pipeline capacity), was genuinely regime-changed. The oil market, because it had alternatives (Saudi Arabia, UAE, US shale, Iran), was a prolonged spike.

A sustained initial move is evidence of severity, not permanence. The four tests tell you about permanence.

### Misconception 3: You Need to Decide Immediately

The pressure to decide — "am I buying or selling this? Am I hedged or exposed?" — is real and intense in the first hours after a shock. Financial media, colleague pressure, and risk management systems all demand immediate answers. This pressure leads to premature categorization, which is the most common error.

The four tests, by design, require time to resolve. Supply disruption becomes clear in 24-72 hours (tanker data, pipeline flow data, production reports). Alliance formation takes days to weeks (NATO summits, formal treaty processes). Legislation takes weeks to months. Capex takes weeks to months.

The correct response to "decide immediately" pressure is to take a minimum position in the fog-of-war direction (long gold, long vol) and explicitly reserve your larger capital for after the tests resolve. You will never buy the exact bottom or sell the exact top. That is not the game. The game is getting on the right side of the regime change before the majority of the repricing has happened, which typically takes weeks to months — not minutes.

### Misconception 4: Regime Changes Are Always About Commodities

The US-China tech decoupling is one of the most consequential regime changes of the past decade, and it has nothing to do with physical commodity supply. It is a regime change in semiconductor supply chains, corporate R&D investment decisions, university research collaboration, and government procurement rules. Nvidia's China revenue share fell from 26% in 2022 to 8% in 2025. Taiwan's TSMC is building fabs in Arizona and Japan for the first time in its history.

The four tests apply: supply disruption (not of commodities but of semiconductor fab access for Chinese companies), alliance shift (AUKUS, Quad), policy permanence (CHIPS Act legislation in the US, equivalent legislation in EU and Japan), and corporate capex (TSMC spending \$40 billion in the US, ASML restricting exports to China). All four tests fired. Full regime change — just in a different asset class.

### Misconception 5: If the Market Recovers, It Was a Spike

The S&P 500 recovered from the Ukraine invasion shock within six weeks. Does that mean Ukraine 2022 was a spike for equities? In a narrow sense, yes — for broad US equity markets. But the regime change in European energy was simultaneously happening. The error here is applying the classification at the wrong level of granularity: it is not "is Ukraine 2022 a spike or regime change?" but "is Ukraine 2022 a spike or regime change **for European energy/defense**?" — and the answer to that more specific question is clearly regime change.

Multi-asset events often produce a mixture of spikes (broad equity indices, US bonds) and regime changes (specific European sector exposures). The framework applies at the asset-class level, not at the portfolio level.

## How It Shows Up in Real Markets

### The Energy Transition Regime Change Hidden Inside COVID

The most significant regime change of the 2020s was not obviously a geopolitical event at all: it was the supply-side capex collapse of 2020-2021, followed by the demand-side recovery of 2022, which produced the highest oil prices since 2008. This dynamic is now well-understood, but the path to understanding it required correctly applying the capex test (Test 4) during COVID.

The investors who got it right built positions in oil exploration and production companies in mid-2020 when the stocks were at decade lows. By mid-2022, those positions had appreciated 300-400%. The four-test framework directed them to the correct signal: supply-side capex had regime-changed (permanently reduced), demand had not.

### The Defense Spending Permanent Shift Post-Ukraine

European defense stocks (Rheinmetall, KNDS, BAE Systems, Leonardo, Saab) have been in a multi-year bull market since early 2022. The regime change in defense spending was identified clearly by Test 2 (alliance expansion — NATO adding Finland and Sweden) and Test 3 (German government legislation committing \$100 billion in emergency defense funding, EU defense investment acts). By mid-2022, anyone who had applied the framework was positioned in European defense equities before the major re-rating.

Rheinmetall, Germany's largest defense contractor, went from roughly 90 EUR per share in January 2022 to over 500 EUR by 2024 — a 450% appreciation driven by the regime change in European defense budgets. The four-test framework would have told you in March 2022 (alliance test confirmed, legislation test confirmed) that this was a permanent spending shift. The entry point in March 2022 at around 150-180 EUR was still extraordinary relative to the eventual 500 EUR level.

### The Saudi Oil Policy Regime Change: 2022 OPEC Cut

When Saudi Arabia and OPEC+ announced a surprise 2 million barrel per day production cut in October 2022 — just weeks before US midterm elections, in what was interpreted as a rebuke of the Biden administration — many treated it as a temporary political maneuver.

Applying the tests: Saudi Aramco's capex (Test 4) was not expanding. The Saudi government was simultaneously building NEOM and Vision 2030 infrastructure, requiring higher sustained oil revenues. The OPEC+ alliance (Test 2) held together despite US pressure. These signals suggested this was not a temporary cut — it was a recalibration of the Saudi "fair price" target upward from \$70 to closer to \$80-90. That regime change in the Saudi price floor was confirmed over the following two years.

### Vietnam as Trade-War Beneficiary: The Long-Cycle Regime Change

The US-China trade war is the clearest slow-motion regime change available to study. Vietnam FDI data provides the capex signal: in 2017, Vietnam received \$14.1 billion in FDI. By 2024, it received an estimated \$25 billion — a 77% increase as companies permanently redirected supply chains. Electronics exports went from \$58 billion in 2017 to \$115 billion in 2024 — a doubling driven by Samsung, Apple supplier networks, and Intel all making permanent capex commitments in Vietnam.

This regime change was identifiable through Test 4 (Samsung and Apple announcing specific Vietnamese factory investments beginning in 2018-2019) and Test 3 (IRA domestic content rules, CHIPS Act, forcing Asian manufacturers to also invest in other non-China markets). Vietnam's VN-Index appreciated from 892 at end-2018 to 1,499 by end-2021 — driven substantially by this FDI and export regime change.

## The Playbook: How to Read and Position Around a Geopolitical Shock

### Phase 1: The First 24 Hours (Fog of War)

**Default position**: Long gold (1-2% of portfolio), long VIX (0.5-1% premium spend), no large directional commodity positions.

**Decision tree**: Is this a nuclear-adjacent event (Russia, China, North Korea, Pakistan-India)? If yes, the tail risk justifies slightly larger defensive positioning. Is this a Middle East event? Check Strait of Hormuz traffic data immediately.

**Key data to have on hand**: Tanker tracking via Lloyd's or MarineTraffic for Hormuz, Turkish Straits, and key Asian chokepoints. GPR daily reading. EUR/USD movement (European exposure indicator). Gold/silver ratio (rising ratio = risk-off, quality flight).

**What to avoid**: Large directional positions in the initially most-affected commodity. This is where most money is lost in geopolitical events — buying the spike in oil after a Middle East attack when the Strait is still open.

### Phase 2: 24-72 Hours (Test Resolution)

Run the four tests explicitly. Document your score. Be honest about uncertainty — "Test 1: supply disruption unclear, watching tanker data" is a valid intermediate score.

**Update sizing continuously**: Every new data point that resolves a test in one direction should update your position size. Bayesian updating is the correct mental model. Start with your base-rate prior (70% spike), update each time a test fires or fails.

**The moment two tests confirm as regime change**: This is when you shift from defensive fog-of-war positioning to directional regime-change positioning. Reduce hedges, build positions in the directly-affected asset class.

### Phase 3: 1-4 Weeks (Capex Confirmation)

The capex test is the slowest-resolving. Corporate capex announcements typically come in earnings calls (quarterly) or at investor days. For major geopolitical shocks, companies may issue special announcements sooner.

**Key companies to monitor by shock type**:
- Energy shock: Major oil supermajors (ExxonMobil, Shell, TotalEnergies, Saudi Aramco), European gas utilities (E.ON, Engie), LNG operators (Cheniere, QatarEnergy)
- Defense shock: Rheinmetall, BAE Systems, Northrop Grumman, Lockheed Martin, Saab
- Tech/chip war: TSMC, ASML, Samsung, Intel, Applied Materials
- Trade war: Container shipping companies (Maersk, COSCO), Vietnamese FDI announcements, semiconductor packaging companies in Southeast Asia

### The Invalidation Test

Every regime-change thesis needs an explicit invalidation condition. Write it down at the time you make the bet:

- "This thesis is invalidated if: Russian gas flows recover to >50% of pre-war levels (possible under ceasefire scenario)."
- "This thesis is invalidated if: NATO members formally withdraw defense spending commitments."
- "This thesis is invalidated if: US-China sign a new comprehensive trade agreement (not executive order) reducing tariffs below 15%."

Without explicit invalidation, regime-change theses become permanent fixtures in portfolios, held long after the repricing has occurred. The regime-change trade is most valuable in the first 6-24 months. After that, the new equilibrium is priced in, and you need to be looking for the *next* structural shift.
