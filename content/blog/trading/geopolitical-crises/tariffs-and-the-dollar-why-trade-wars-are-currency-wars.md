> **TL;DR — The 5 things you need to know**
>
> 1. **Tariffs and currencies move together.** When the US imposes a 25% tariff on Chinese goods, China can partially offset it by letting the RMB depreciate. A 10% currency move offsets roughly 40-50% of a 25% tariff impact on prices.
> 2. **The dollar paradox is real.** Tariffs are supposed to reduce the trade deficit, but they strengthen the dollar, which makes US exports more expensive and imports from non-targeted countries cheaper — often widening the very deficit they target. The US trade deficit was \$621B in 2018 and \$664B in 2019 despite the tariff war.
> 3. **USD/CNH 7.00 is the trade war signal.** When China allows USD/CNH to cross 7.00, it signals a willingness to absorb pain and escalate. This is the single most important number to watch in any US-China trade confrontation.
> 4. **Japan 2022 was NOT a currency war.** Japan spent \$60B defending the yen from weakness, not engineering weakness. The distinction matters: a country trying to weaken its currency (currency war) vs. a country trying to strengthen it (FX intervention) have opposite geopolitical implications.
> 5. **The Plaza Accord is the endgame template.** In 1985, the US forced Japan and Germany to revalue their currencies up. Japan's yen went from 240 to 120 in 12 months — and the revaluation that followed started the "lost decade." A modern Plaza-style deal with China is the only way this ends bilaterally.

On August 5, 2019, at approximately 9:30 AM Beijing time, the People's Bank of China set the official daily fixing for the renminbi at 6.9225 per US dollar — breaking above 7.00 for the first time since the 2008 financial crisis. Within hours, US President Donald Trump took to Twitter to call China a "currency manipulator." The S&P 500 fell 3% by the close of trading in New York. Yields on the 10-year Treasury dropped 14 basis points. Gold surged. The bond market priced in two additional Fed rate cuts that hadn't been on the table the prior week.

All of this happened because a number — 7.00 — was crossed on a currency chart. Not because a war started. Not because a company missed earnings. Because the exchange rate between the US dollar and the Chinese renminbi crossed an invisible psychological threshold. To anyone not watching the currency markets, it looked inexplicable. To anyone who was, it was the only number that mattered.

Consider the scale of what happened in the eighteen months before that August morning. Starting in January 2018, the US and China had engaged in the largest bilateral tariff escalation since the Smoot-Hawley Act of 1930. The US imposed Section 301 tariffs on the grounds that China was engaged in unfair technology transfer practices. China retaliated dollar-for-dollar (more or less) with its own tariffs on US agricultural goods, aircraft, automobiles, and chemicals. By the summer of 2019, the average US tariff on Chinese goods had risen from approximately 3% to over 21%. Hundreds of billions of dollars in bilateral trade were being taxed in ways they hadn't been since before the WTO era.

The economic damage was real: US soybean exports to China fell from roughly \$22B in 2017 to \$8B in 2018. Chinese imports of US manufactured goods slowed. Factories that had relied on Chinese components faced uncertainty about their supply chains. And yet — and this is the central mystery that this post will explain — the US trade deficit actually widened. The one number that the tariffs were explicitly designed to improve got worse.

This post is about why that number matters, how tariffs and currencies interact, why every major trade conflict in modern history has also been a currency conflict, and what the pattern means for markets in 2025 and beyond. We'll start from zero — no finance background required — and build to the level where you can read a trade war headline and know exactly which currency pair to watch and why.

## Foundations: How Currencies and Tariffs Interact

Before we can understand why trade wars become currency wars, we need to understand the basics of both. Let's start from the ground floor.

### What is an exchange rate?

An exchange rate is simply the price of one currency in terms of another. When we say USD/CNH = 7.18, we mean it costs 7.18 Chinese renminbi (offshore, "CNH") to buy one US dollar. Or equivalently, one US dollar buys 7.18 yuan.

If USD/CNH moves from 6.50 to 7.00, the dollar got more expensive in yuan terms, which means the yuan got cheaper in dollar terms. We say the yuan **depreciated** against the dollar. From the Chinese exporter's perspective, this is actually good news: they earn revenue in yuan and sell goods priced in dollars. When the yuan depreciates, the dollars they receive are worth more yuan when converted — their profit margins go up.

### What is a tariff?

A tariff is a tax imposed by a government on imported goods. If the US places a 25% tariff on Chinese-made bicycles that originally cost \$100, importers now pay \$125 at the border. That extra \$25 either comes out of the exporter's margin (the Chinese factory earns less), gets passed to US consumers as a higher price (\$125 bicycles), or some combination. The theory is that higher prices reduce demand for Chinese bikes, increasing demand for domestically made bikes — improving the US trade balance.

Simple enough in isolation. But here's where it gets interesting.

### The Marshall-Lerner condition

The **Marshall-Lerner condition** is one of economics' most important (and most frequently violated) real-world predictions. It says that a currency depreciation will only improve a country's trade balance if the sum of the price elasticity of exports plus the price elasticity of imports is greater than one.

In English: devaluing your currency makes your exports cheaper for foreigners and makes imports more expensive for your own citizens. Both effects should reduce a trade deficit. But in the short run, two things get in the way:

1. **Contracts are already signed.** A US company that has a six-month supply contract for Chinese components can't immediately switch to a US supplier just because tariffs went up. The quantity demanded doesn't change right away even if the price does.
2. **Exchange rate pass-through is imperfect.** Exporters often absorb part of the currency move in their margins rather than fully passing it through to prices.

The result is the **J-curve**: when a currency depreciates, the trade balance often gets worse in the short run (because import bills are still going out in higher local-currency amounts) before it gets better in the medium run (because export volumes pick up). This is why trade wars drag on — the simple theory that "depreciation fixes the deficit" takes 12-24 months to show up in the data, and by then the political situation has usually changed.

### How a tariff and a currency move interact

Here is the core mechanism that this entire post is built around: a tariff raises the price of imported goods in the importing country. A depreciation of the exporting country's currency lowers the price of those same goods in the importing country's currency. **They partially cancel each other out.**

If the US imposes a 25% tariff on a \$100 Chinese widget, the price in the US becomes \$125. But if China simultaneously allows its currency to depreciate by 10% (from, say, 7.00 to 7.70 yuan per dollar), the Chinese exporter who was earning 700 yuan on a \$100 sale is now earning 770 yuan on the same \$100 sale — so they can afford to cut their dollar price. The \$100 widget becomes \$91, and after the 25% tariff: \$91 × 1.25 = \$113.75. The US consumer is paying \$113.75 instead of \$125 — the depreciation absorbed about 45% of the tariff impact.

This is the currency offset mechanism, and it is the reason every major tariff war since World War II has simultaneously become a currency conflict.

### The pass-through problem

One nuance worth understanding: not all of a currency depreciation passes through to export prices. **Exchange rate pass-through** is the degree to which a currency move translates into a change in the price of traded goods. Economists measure this as a number between 0 (no pass-through — prices don't change at all) and 1 (complete pass-through — prices move by the full amount of the currency change).

In practice, pass-through is typically between 0.3 and 0.7 for manufactured goods, meaning a 10% currency move only translates into a 3-7% change in import prices. The rest is absorbed in the exporter's profit margins. Why? Three reasons:

**Pricing to market:** Chinese exporters selling into the US market know their customers are price-sensitive. If they pass through the full currency benefit as a price cut, they might gain market share but at the cost of brand perception. Many prefer to hold dollar prices stable and pocket the higher yuan margin.

**Invoicing currency:** A significant share of China-US trade is invoiced in US dollars. When you invoice in the buyer's currency, currency moves don't immediately appear as price changes — they appear as margin changes for the exporter. Only on contract renewal does the price adjust.

**Input costs in dollars:** Many Chinese manufacturers use imported components — semiconductors, specialty chemicals, industrial machinery — that are priced in dollars. When the yuan depreciates, these dollar-priced inputs become more expensive in yuan, partially offsetting the competitive benefit of the weaker currency.

The practical implication: the currency offset mechanism is real but imperfect. A 10% yuan depreciation probably translates into a 4-6% reduction in effective tariff impact, not the full 10%. This is still substantial — it means a 25% tariff in practice might only create 15-19% effective price pressure — but it's less than the naive math suggests.

## The Currency Offset Mechanism

Let's make this precise with a full worked example.

#### Worked example:

**Setup:** The US imposes a 25% tariff on \$100B worth of Chinese manufactured goods. China responds by allowing the RMB to depreciate from 6.50 to 7.15 per dollar — a move of about 10%.

**Before tariffs, no currency move:**
- Chinese factory sells widget for 650 RMB
- At 6.50 RMB/USD, that's 650 / 6.50 = \$100.00
- US importer pays \$100.00, no tariff

**After 25% US tariff, no currency move:**
- Chinese factory still sells at 650 RMB = \$100.00
- US importer pays \$100.00 + 25% tariff = \$125.00
- US consumer pays \$125.00 — full 25% tariff impact

**After 25% US tariff AND 10% RMB depreciation (6.50 → 7.15):**
- Chinese factory still earns 650 RMB
- But 650 / 7.15 = \$90.91 per unit in dollar terms
- US importer receives the \$90.91 price + 25% tariff = \$113.64
- US consumer pays \$113.64 vs. \$100.00 original — only a 13.6% effective price rise

**The offset:** The RMB move absorbed (\$125.00 - \$113.64) / \$125.00 = 46% of the tariff impact. The Chinese factory didn't change its price in RMB at all — it benefited from the depreciation. The US consumer felt only 13.6% higher prices instead of 25%.

This is not theoretical. This is exactly what China did during the 2018-2020 trade war. From January 2018 to September 2019, the RMB depreciated approximately 12% against the dollar (from about 6.25 to 7.18). With US tariffs averaging about 25% on targeted goods, the currency move offset roughly 40-50% of the effective price impact — just as the math above suggests.

### Why exporters prefer currency depreciation to price cuts

There's an important asymmetry here. When a country devalues its currency, all exporters benefit simultaneously — not just those facing tariffs. The currency depreciation acts as a broad-based subsidy to the entire export sector. A price cut on specific goods only helps those goods.

Furthermore, a currency depreciation is politically harder to label as "cheating." When the Chinese government explicitly subsidizes a factory, the US can file a WTO complaint and label it as an unfair subsidy. When the central bank allows the exchange rate to move (even intentionally), it's technically just the market. The US labeled China a "currency manipulator" in August 2019, but the label is more political than legally actionable — it triggers consultations, not automatic sanctions.

This asymmetry is why every government facing tariffs reaches for the currency lever first. It's efficient, broad-based, and somewhat defensible.

### The retaliatory cycle and why it escalates

Here's the problem: the currency offset is visible. US tariff architects know about it. So when China lets the yuan depreciate to offset 40-50% of the first round of tariffs, the political pressure to impose more tariffs increases — not decreases. Congress members representing districts with closed factories don't care about currency offset theory. They see the import numbers, they see Chinese products still filling shelves at nearly the same prices, and they demand more tariffs.

This creates the escalation cycle. Tariffs cause currency offset. Currency offset reduces the pain of tariffs. Reduced pain reduces the political cost of imposing more tariffs. More tariffs require more currency offset. The cycle runs until one of four things happens:

1. **Political exhaustion:** One government decides the domestic economic pain (inflation, disrupted supply chains) exceeds the political benefit of continuing the fight.
2. **Third-party disruption:** A recession, pandemic, or other crisis changes the political calculus entirely — trade wars pause when both countries need to focus inward.
3. **Structural change:** Production moves out of both countries to Vietnam, Mexico, or other third-party countries that aren't targeted by tariffs. The bilateral imbalance shrinks not because either country "won" but because supply chains restructured around both of them.
4. **Negotiated deal:** Both governments agree to a framework — typically involving specific purchase commitments, partial tariff rollbacks, and pledges on currency stability — that allows both sides to declare partial victory.

In 2019-2020, the US-China trade war ended primarily via option (2) — COVID disrupted everything and the "Phase One" deal was announced partly to give both sides a reason to pause. Option (3) was clearly underway: Vietnam's electronics exports to the US surged from \$47B in 2018 to \$97B in 2022 as supply chains shifted. The structural reallocation of production is the trade war's most durable legacy.

## The Dollar Paradox in Trade Wars

Here is one of the most counterintuitive dynamics in international economics: tariffs designed to shrink the trade deficit often make it bigger.

The mechanism works in two steps.

**Step 1: Tariffs strengthen the dollar.**

When the US announces major tariffs, several things happen to capital flows:
- Investors anticipate US companies "reshoring" production from China — they buy US stocks and dollar-denominated assets.
- The tariffs are deflationary for some goods (cheaper alternatives may emerge) but inflationary for others — the mixed signal often causes the Fed to hold rates higher for longer, making dollar-denominated bonds more attractive.
- Safe-haven demand for the dollar increases as uncertainty rises globally.
- The US current account deficit narrows (at least nominally, before the full J-curve effects kick in), which also mechanically reduces dollar outflows.

All of these push the DXY (the Dollar Index, which tracks the dollar against a basket of major currencies) higher.

**Step 2: A stronger dollar widens the trade deficit.**

A stronger dollar makes US exports more expensive for foreign buyers. A Japanese company that was buying \$1M of US industrial equipment at 115 JPY/USD (paying 115 million yen) now has to pay 130 million yen if the dollar strengthens to 130. They might buy less, or switch to a European competitor. This reduces US export revenue.

At the same time, a stronger dollar makes imports from non-tariff countries cheaper. If the euro weakens against the dollar, German machinery becomes cheaper for US manufacturers — substituting Chinese imports with European ones without reducing the overall import bill.

The net result: the tariff war hits China specifically, but the stronger dollar hits US exporters broadly and makes non-Chinese imports more attractive. The trade deficit doesn't shrink — it shifts and may even grow.

#### Worked example:

**The 2018-2019 US trade deficit paradox:**

In 2018, the US imposed \$250B in tariffs on Chinese goods, explicitly to reduce the \$621B US trade deficit. The tariff revenue collected was approximately \$50-60B annually — real money that returned to the Treasury.

But simultaneously:
- The DXY (Dollar Index) strengthened approximately 8% in 2018
- US export growth slowed as foreign buyers faced 8% more expensive American goods
- The US trade deficit widened to \$664B in 2019 — up \$43B despite the tariff war

The math: tariff income ≈ \$55B gain; lost export competitiveness ≈ \$60-80B loss in export sales. The tariff made the trade balance worse in aggregate even as it successfully redirected some production away from China.

This is the Triffin dilemma meeting the trade war: the US dollar's role as the world's reserve currency — which requires the US to run persistent current account deficits to supply the world with dollars — fundamentally conflicts with the goal of reducing the trade deficit through tariffs.

## The USD/CNH as the Trade War Signal

If you only watch one number during a US-China trade confrontation, watch USD/CNH — the exchange rate between the US dollar and the offshore Chinese renminbi (traded in Hong Kong).

Why offshore rather than onshore (USD/CNY)? Because the offshore CNH market trades freely 24 hours a day, is open to global capital, and reflects real market pricing without the PBOC's daily fixing constraints. When the market smells weakness or stress in China's currency, it shows up in CNH first.

### The 7.00 line and what it means

For years before 2019, China had informally defended the USD/CNH level at approximately 7.00. This wasn't a hard peg — China has operated a "managed float" since 2005, where the PBOC sets a daily "fixing" and allows the market to trade within a band around it. But 7.00 was the psychological level that Chinese policymakers didn't want crossed.

Why 7.00? Partly arbitrary — it's a round number. But round numbers carry enormous weight in currency markets. They become self-fulfilling lines of defense because every market participant knows every other market participant is watching them. When China defended 7.00, it was signaling: "We will not let this go into disorderly depreciation territory. We are in control."

Breaking 7.00 sent the opposite signal: either China had lost control, or — more ominously — China had chosen to stop defending it. In August 2019, after the US raised tariffs on List 3 goods to 25% in May, China chose the latter.

### Reading USD/CNH as a tariff thermometer

Here is the chronology of the 2018-2020 trade war as told by USD/CNH:

- **January 2018:** USD/CNH ≈ 6.48. China is not under tariff pressure. The RMB is actually appreciating modestly against the dollar.
- **July 2018 (List 1 tariffs, 25% on \$34B):** USD/CNH climbs to ≈ 6.73. The market begins pricing in RMB weakness as a hedge.
- **September 2018 (List 3 at 10% on \$200B):** USD/CNH reaches 6.87. Getting close to the 7.00 line; China is still defending it.
- **May 2019 (List 3 raised to 25%):** USD/CNH spikes to 6.91. The escalation is severe; markets start running scenarios for what happens if 7.00 breaks.
- **August 5, 2019:** The PBOC sets the daily fixing above 7.00 for the first time since 2008. USD/CNH trades to 7.03 intraday. US Treasury immediately labels China a "currency manipulator." The S&P 500 falls 3%. The 10-year Treasury yield drops 14 bps in a day.
- **September 2019 (peak):** USD/CNH reaches 7.18 — the high for the entire tariff war episode.
- **January 2020 (Phase One deal):** USD/CNH retraces to 6.94 as the two sides sign a partial agreement.

![USD/CNH 2018-2024: the trade war currency thermometer with key tariff events annotated](/imgs/blogs/tariffs-and-the-dollar-why-trade-wars-are-currency-wars-2.png)

The pattern is clear: each tariff escalation pushes CNH weaker; each negotiation or agreement pulls it stronger. USD/CNH is not just a side story — it IS the trade war, quantified in real time.

### The 2025 scenario: what happens at 145% tariffs?

In April 2025, President Trump invoked the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) to impose tariffs of 145% on Chinese goods — a level without modern precedent. The immediate question markets asked: where does CNH go?

Historical extrapolation: at 25% tariffs in 2019, CNH went to 7.18 (a 12% depreciation from the pre-war level of 6.25). At 145% tariffs — roughly 6× the tariff intensity — what is the comparable move?

The simple linear extrapolation would suggest 6.25 × (1 + 6 × 0.12) = roughly 10.75. But this is too mechanical. The relationship isn't linear because:

1. **Capital flight risk:** Above ≈ 7.50-8.00, Chinese holders of assets may accelerate capital outflows, creating a dangerous feedback loop. The PBOC would need to spend enormous reserves to prevent this.
2. **Commodity import costs:** China imports most of its oil priced in US dollars. A weaker yuan makes every barrel of crude more expensive in yuan terms — adding domestic inflationary pressure.
3. **Political cost:** The "currency manipulator" label, if it triggers secondary sanctions, is far more damaging than the tariffs themselves.
4. **De-escalation probability:** At 145% tariffs, both sides have strong incentives to negotiate. The US doesn't actually want to destroy trade with China completely; China doesn't want to see its export sector collapse.

In practice, in the 90-day pause negotiated in May 2025, tariffs dropped back to 30%, and CNH stabilized around 7.20-7.30. The 8.00 level remains the "nuclear option" that neither side wants to trigger.

## Japan's 2022 FX Intervention vs Currency War

The most common mistake in reading currency war headlines is conflating two completely opposite phenomena: a country **engineering weakness** in its currency (currency war) versus a country **defending against weakness** (FX intervention). Japan in 2022 is the canonical example of the latter.

### The 2022 yen collapse

From January to October 2022, the Japanese yen depreciated from approximately 115 to 151 per dollar — a 31% move that was the fastest and deepest yen depreciation in decades. The driver: the Federal Reserve was hiking interest rates aggressively (from near-zero to 3.25% by October 2022) while the Bank of Japan maintained its "yield curve control" (YCC) policy, which capped Japanese 10-year bond yields at 0.25%.

When US bonds yield 3%+ and Japanese bonds yield 0.25%, global capital flows relentlessly out of yen and into dollars. The yen fell because of this interest rate differential, not because Japan was trying to gain export advantage.

In September 2022, Japan's Ministry of Finance authorized its first intervention to buy yen since 1998, spending approximately \$20B in reserves. In October, after USD/JPY hit 151.9 — a 32-year low — Japan intervened again, spending approximately \$40B more. In total, Japan spent roughly \$60B in reserves defending the yen.

This is the exact opposite of a currency war. Japan was trying to **strengthen** its currency, not weaken it. The intervention was defensive, aimed at slowing the pace of depreciation and preventing disorderly market conditions — not at gaining a competitive trade advantage.

### Why the distinction matters for markets

| Scenario | Country's goal | Market signal | Implication |
|---|---|---|---|
| Currency war | Weaken currency | Rising USD/XXX | Expect further depreciation; buy dollar |
| FX intervention | Strengthen currency | Falling USD/XXX after spike | Intervention may be temporary; watch reserve depletion |

When Japan intervened in 2022, the signal was clear: "We are fighting the market, not working with it." Markets know that a central bank can run out of reserves but cannot print dollars. The intervention slowed the yen's fall but didn't reverse the underlying dynamic (the interest rate differential). The yen eventually stabilized only when the Fed began signaling rate cuts and the BOJ began signaling rate hikes in 2024.

The 2022 episode produced a crucial data point: even after spending \$60B, Japan couldn't hold a line if the fundamental macro conditions (yield differential) weren't moving in its favor. This is why market participants watch reserve levels and rate differentials alongside spot rates.

## The RMB 7.00 Line in 2025

With 145% tariffs announced on Liberation Day (April 2, 2025), the question of where CNH would go became the central currency market debate of the spring. Let's walk through the decision tree China faces.

### Why China doesn't want to fully offset the tariffs with depreciation

The naive model says: if 145% tariffs raise prices 145%, China should let the RMB depreciate by an equivalent amount to fully offset. At 7.20 CNH/USD starting level, a full offset of a 145% tariff might require a move to approximately 17.00+ — obviously impossible. Even a partial offset of 30-40% of the tariff impact would require a move to approximately 9.50-10.50.

But China has not done this, and will not do this, for four critical reasons:

**1. Capital flight risk above 8.00**

When currencies depreciate sharply, wealthy citizens and corporations accelerate capital outflows. They convert yuan into dollars, euros, or crypto, moving wealth offshore before the currency weakens further. China lost approximately \$1 trillion in reserves between 2015 and 2016 during a much more modest RMB depreciation scare. The PBOC would need to spend trillions to prevent a disorderly move — and it no longer has the reserve cushion it had then (Chinese reserves peaked at \$4.0T in 2014 and were approximately \$3.2T in 2025).

**2. Dollar-priced commodity imports**

China imports approximately \$250B in oil annually, priced in US dollars. If CNH depreciates 20%, every barrel of crude oil becomes 20% more expensive in yuan. This creates domestic inflationary pressure — which the PBOC then has to fight with tighter monetary policy. A currency war that requires tightening your own economy to fight inflation is self-defeating.

**3. The currency manipulator escalation ladder**

If the US labels China a "currency manipulator" (which triggers automatic under IEEPA language), the response can include secondary sanctions — penalties on countries or companies that do business with China using yuan. This is a nuclear option that would severely damage China's ability to conduct international trade.

**4. Credibility and belt-and-road diplomacy**

China has been actively promoting the RMB as an alternative to the dollar in international trade — particularly through its Belt and Road Initiative. Allowing the RMB to depreciate rapidly and disorderly would undermine the currency's credibility as a store of value for Belt and Road partner countries. This conflicts with China's long-term strategic interest.

For all these reasons, China tends to manage CNH depreciation slowly and with intent — as a pressure valve, not a firehose.

## The Dollar as a Trade Weapon

The US dollar is not just a medium of exchange. It is, in the words of former French Finance Minister Valéry Giscard d'Estaing, an "exorbitant privilege" — and in trade wars, it becomes an offensive capability.

### Dollar weaponization: the three channels

**Channel 1: Fed rate hikes as financial pressure**

When the Federal Reserve raises interest rates, it tightens global dollar liquidity. Countries that have borrowed in US dollars — which includes most emerging market sovereigns and corporations — suddenly face higher debt service costs in their local currencies. This forces them to either (a) buy dollars to service debt, further strengthening the dollar, or (b) allow their currencies to depreciate sharply.

In 2022, when the Fed hiked from near-zero to 4.5%, the dollar strengthened approximately 18% against a basket of EM currencies. This was the equivalent of a tariff on all dollar-denominated debt — every EM country effectively paid 18% more in local currency terms on its dollar-denominated debt service. The US didn't impose this cost deliberately, but it was a direct consequence of Fed policy.

**Channel 2: Swap line access as geopolitical reward**

During the March 2020 COVID crisis, the Federal Reserve extended emergency dollar swap lines to 14 central banks — essentially providing unlimited dollar liquidity to prevent a global dollar funding crisis. These swap lines went to the ECB, Bank of Japan, Bank of Canada, Bank of England, Bank of Korea, Reserve Bank of Australia, and others. They did not go to China.

This was not coincidental. Swap lines are discretionary. The Fed can choose whom to extend them to, and the choice carries enormous geopolitical weight. Countries without swap line access — which includes China and most EM economies — face more severe dollar funding crunches during crises. The swap line network is, in effect, the financial infrastructure of US alliances.

**Channel 3: SWIFT and dollar clearing as sanctions tools**

When Russia was excluded from SWIFT in February 2022 and its central bank reserves were frozen, the world saw the most dramatic use of dollar weaponization in history. Approximately \$300B in Russian foreign exchange reserves — held in dollars and euro-denominated assets in Western custody — became inaccessible overnight. This is only possible because the dollar (and dollar-clearing infrastructure) is controlled by a network of US-influenced institutions.

China has watched this closely. The PRC has been building alternative payment infrastructure (CIPS — Cross-Border Interbank Payment System) and pushing for more trade to be settled in yuan. The explicit goal is to reduce vulnerability to dollar weaponization. But as of 2025, the dollar still accounts for approximately 58% of global reserve holdings versus less than 2.2% for the yuan.

## Currency War Endgame — Plaza Accord as Template

Every currency war eventually ends. The mechanisms are: one side capitulates (economic pain exceeds political will), both sides negotiate a managed resolution, or the global system evolves to remove the underlying pressure. The 1985 Plaza Accord is the best modern example of a negotiated resolution — and its consequences are still debated today.

### The 1985 context: Reagan's trade war with Japan

By 1985, the US was running a massive current account deficit — about \$150B, roughly 3.5% of GDP. Japan accounted for a large fraction of this, with a bilateral surplus of approximately \$50B. The Reagan administration faced domestic political pressure from US manufacturers who claimed Japan was engaging in unfair currency manipulation — keeping the yen artificially weak to make Japanese exports cheap.

The dollar had strengthened dramatically from 1980 to 1985 (a 50% appreciation), driven by Reagan's fiscal expansion (deficit spending) combined with Volcker's high interest rates. This wasn't currency manipulation by Japan — it was a dollar problem caused by US fiscal policy. But politically, Japan was the target.

On September 22, 1985, at the Plaza Hotel in New York, finance ministers from the US, Japan, West Germany, France, and the UK signed the Plaza Accord — an agreement to intervene in currency markets to depreciate the dollar. The key target was the yen.

### What happened after Plaza

The results were swift and severe:

- **Pre-Plaza (Sept 1985):** USD/JPY ≈ 240
- **One year later (Sept 1986):** USD/JPY ≈ 152 — a 37% appreciation of the yen

For Japanese exporters, this was catastrophic. A Toyota Camry that sold in the US for \$8,000 required 1.92 million yen at 240 rate. At 152 rate, the same car had to be priced at \$12,630 to maintain the yen revenue — a 58% price increase that made Japanese cars suddenly uncompetitive in the US market.

Japanese export volumes to the US fell approximately 20% in dollar terms. The Nikkei initially rallied as hot money flowed into Japan anticipating domestic stimulus (the Bank of Japan cut rates aggressively to offset export weakness) — the Nikkei went from 13,000 in 1985 to 39,000 in 1989. Then the bubble burst, and Japan's "Lost Decade" began. The Plaza Accord's yen revaluation is widely credited as one of the proximate causes of the Japanese asset bubble and subsequent two-decade stagnation.

#### Worked example:

**A modern Plaza Accord with China — what would it look like?**

Suppose China agreed to a "New Plaza" and allowed the RMB to appreciate from 7.20 to 5.00 per dollar — matching the 37% appreciation Japan accepted in 1985-86.

China's US-bound exports are approximately \$500B per year. At 7.20 RMB/USD, these generate approximately 3.6 trillion RMB in revenue for Chinese exporters. If those exporters want to maintain their RMB revenue, they must raise dollar prices by 37% — from an average of roughly \$10 per item (hypothetically) to \$13.70 per item.

But a 37% price increase would devastate volume. With a price elasticity of demand of approximately -2 (typical for manufactured goods), a 37% price increase would reduce volume by approximately 74%. Applying this:

- Original revenue: \$500B × 1 = \$500B
- After New Plaza: \$500B × 1.37 (price) × 0.26 (volume after -74% decline) = \$178B
- Loss of revenue to Chinese exporters: \$322B per year

This is why China will not accept a Plaza-style deal voluntarily. Japan accepted Plaza because (a) Japan was a US security dependent and (b) Japan believed the US market access was worth the currency pain. China has neither incentive. Any "New Plaza" with China would require either extraordinary US leverage or extraordinary Chinese economic weakness.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "A weaker currency always helps the trade balance."**

Not in the short run, and not always in the long run. The J-curve effect means that trade balances often worsen initially after a depreciation because import bills (contracts already signed) rise faster than export revenues pick up. Countries that rely heavily on commodity imports (China with oil, Japan with LNG) can see inflation surge after depreciation, which requires tighter monetary policy that offsets the trade benefit.

**Misconception 2: "The US benefits if China stops buying US Treasuries."**

This is one of the most persistent myths. If China sells US Treasuries, those bonds have to go somewhere — other investors buy them. In the short run, selling pressure might push yields up slightly, but the global demand for safe-haven dollar assets is enormous. More importantly, if China's Treasury sale caused the dollar to weaken, US exports would become cheaper, reducing the trade deficit — which is the opposite of what tariff hawks want.

**Misconception 3: "Currency manipulation is easy to prove and punish."**

In practice, the distinction between a country "manipulating" its currency and a country conducting ordinary monetary policy is deeply ambiguous. The IMF's framework for identifying currency manipulation requires evidence of "(1) fundamental misalignment, (2) persistent one-sided FX intervention, and (3) external imbalance." China technically fails most of these tests most of the time — the PBOC often intervenes to prevent excessive depreciation, not to cause it.

**Misconception 4: "Trade wars end quickly because they're too painful."**

They rarely do. The 2018-2020 US-China trade war lasted 2+ years and ended with a "Phase One" deal that largely preserved the tariff structure while requiring China to buy specific US goods (which China later failed to meet). The underlying structural issues — tech transfer, subsidies, market access — were not resolved. In 2024-2025, the same conflict escalated again. Trade wars end political cycles, not economic ones.

**Misconception 5: "A strong dollar is always good for the US."**

A strong dollar is good for US consumers (cheaper imports) and for US companies that import components. It's bad for US exporters (their goods become more expensive for foreigners) and for EM countries with dollar-denominated debt. Whether it's "good" depends entirely on which US constituency you're asking.

## How It Shows Up in Real Markets

### 2018-2019: The textbook currency war

From January 2018 to September 2019, USD/CNH rose from 6.25 to 7.18 — a 15% depreciation of the RMB. During this period:

- The DXY strengthened from 90 to 99 (10% dollar appreciation)
- US trade deficit widened from \$621B to \$664B
- S&P 500 fell 20% from September to December 2018 (the "tariff tantrum")
- Gold rose approximately 15% from August 2018 lows to the end of the trade war
- 10-year Treasury yields fell from 3.2% in October 2018 to 1.5% in August 2019 as the trade war spooked growth expectations

The clearest market signal: on August 5, 2019, when USD/CNH crossed 7.00, the S&P fell 3% in a single day. This is the number.

### 2010: The first "currency war" label

In September 2010, Brazilian Finance Minister Guido Mantega publicly declared that the world was in the midst of a "currency war." His target: the US Federal Reserve, which was engaged in quantitative easing (QE). By printing dollars, the Fed was weakening the dollar and causing the currencies of countries like Brazil to appreciate — making Brazilian exports more expensive and flooding Brazilian markets with cheap foreign goods.

Mantega's intervention was significant because it was the first time a senior official explicitly called QE a form of currency manipulation. From 2010 to 2013, the US-Brazil exchange rate moved from approximately 1.67 to 2.40 (a 44% BRL depreciation), and Brazilian exports became more competitive globally. The "currency war" of that era ended when the Fed began tapering in 2013 — the "taper tantrum" reversed the flows.

### 2022: The yen crisis that wasn't a currency war

As described above, JPY went from 115 to 151 in 2022 — but this was not Japan trying to gain competitive advantage. It was a consequence of Fed-BOJ monetary policy divergence. The key market signal: when the BOJ finally pivoted toward rate hikes in 2024 and the Fed began cutting, USD/JPY reversed sharply from 161 (July 2024) to 142 (August 2024) — one of the most violent currency moves in decades, driven by the "yen carry trade" unwinding.

The yen carry trade: borrow yen at near-zero rates, convert to dollars, invest in US Treasuries at 5%. When rates converge, the carry reverses, and everyone rushes for the exit simultaneously. This is not geopolitics — it's pure interest rate mathematics — but it's triggered by geopolitical events (trade wars affecting Fed policy expectations, BOJ signaling, etc.).

#### Worked example:

**The USD/CNH 7.00 line trade (August 2019)**

August 1, 2019: Trump tweets that he will impose 10% tariffs on the remaining \$300B of Chinese imports (List 4). USD/CNH closes at 6.95 — close to but not yet through 7.00.

August 5, 2019: PBOC sets fixing above 7.00. USD/CNH trades to 7.03 intraday, then 7.08. US Treasury officially labels China a "currency manipulator." S&P 500 falls 3%.

Over the next six weeks, USD/CNH reaches 7.18 (September 3, 2019 — the day List 4B tariffs take effect at 15%).

**The trade:**
- Entry: short CNH / long USD at 6.95 (before the 7.00 break, buying the dollar as CNH is expected to weaken)
- Exit: take profit at 7.18 (post-break peak, booking the move)
- Gain: (7.18 - 6.95) / 6.95 = 3.31% on a USD/CNH long

On a \$1M notional position:
- The position is long USD, short CNH
- As CNH weakens from 6.95 to 7.18, each dollar now buys more yuan
- Gain = (\$1,000,000 / 6.95 × 7.18) - \$1,000,000 = \$1,033,094 - \$1,000,000 = \$33,094

Alternatively in FX futures terms: each CME USD/CNH futures contract has notional of 100,000 USD. 10 contracts = \$1M notional. A 0.23 move in USD/CNH × 100,000 × 10 = \$230,000 gain in CNH terms, or approximately \$32,000 in USD terms. This aligned with realized P&L for traders who correctly identified the 7.00 break signal.

**Risk management:** The trade had a natural stop at ≈ 6.85 (if China successfully defended 7.00 and reversed the move). The risk-reward was approximately 3:1 (3.31% potential gain vs. roughly 1.5% risk to the stop).

## How to Trade It — The Playbook

### Signal 1: USD/CNH approaching 7.00

When USD/CNH is within 2-3% of 7.00 during an active tariff escalation, the option market begins pricing in a "tail risk" scenario of a break through 7.00. This shows up as elevated implied volatility in 1-month USD/CNH options (implied vol typically jumps from 5-6% to 10-12%+ as the level approaches).

**Position:** Long USD/CNH (or equivalently, long a call on USD/CNH with a 7.00 strike) when tariff escalation is ongoing and the spot rate is between 6.85-6.99. Stop below 6.80 (if China is successfully defending the level). Target 7.20-7.30.

**Correlated trade:** When USD/CNH breaks 7.00, gold typically rallies 1-2% in the following week (safe-haven demand + de-dollarization narrative). US Treasury 10-year yields typically fall 10-20 bps. These correlated trades can be used to size the overall geopolitical risk position.

### Signal 2: DXY and the tariff announcement spike

Historically, major tariff announcements cause an initial DXY spike of 0.5-1.5% as markets price in the "stronger dollar narrative." But in April 2025, Liberation Day tariffs caused the DXY to actually weaken — from 104.2 on April 2 to under 100 by June 2025. This was unusual and significant.

The reason for the reversal: at extreme tariff levels (145%), markets begin worrying not about dollar strength from reshoring, but about dollar weakness from reduced international trade, capital account deterioration, and loss of US creditworthiness. The dollar smile theory predicts that very high risk events are negative for the dollar because they threaten the underlying growth that supports dollar assets.

**Trading implication:** In a moderate tariff escalation (10-25%), buy the dollar. In an extreme tariff escalation (100%+), the initial dollar spike may reverse — consider using it as a selling opportunity.

### Signal 3: EM currencies as early warning

Emerging market currencies — particularly those with large US dollar-denominated debt — are the first to sell off when dollar tightening begins. The Turkish lira, Argentine peso, South African rand, and Indonesian rupiah all fell sharply during the 2018 tariff war, before the full economic impact was visible in trade data.

Watching DXY vs. EMFX spreads gives you a 3-6 month leading indicator: if EM currencies are weakening while DXY is strong, dollar tightening conditions are building, and export-dependent EM economies will face stress — even if they aren't direct tariff targets.

### Signal 4: The Plaza Accord endgame signal

When does a trade war resolve? Watch for:
1. **Both sides' economic pain becoming politically visible.** In 2019, US farmers faced significant losses from Chinese retaliation tariffs on soybeans (down from \$22B in 2017 to \$8B in 2018). This was the domestic pressure that brought the US to the Phase One table.
2. **CNH stabilizing inside the depreciation band.** If USD/CNH stops rising despite ongoing tariff pressure, it signals China is absorbing the pain without further currency offset — which means they believe a deal is near.
3. **Treasury-Commerce back-channel signals.** When negotiators are getting close, the rhetoric from both sides softens 4-6 weeks before a deal is announced. Watch for fewer Twitter attacks and more "productive conversations" language.

### Position sizing and invalidation

Currency trades involving geopolitical risk have fat-tail distributions — the moves can be much larger and faster than implied volatility suggests. Sizing rules:

- **FX positions:** Keep each geopolitical currency trade to 1-2% of portfolio risk
- **Correlated positions (gold, Treasuries):** Treat them as confirmation trades, not primary positions
- **Invalidation:** If the fundamental driver changes (tariff pause announced, diplomatic meeting scheduled), exit the position regardless of the technical level

## The Vietnam Dong and Emerging Market Currency Spillovers

When the US taxes Chinese goods, production does not vanish — it relocates. The clearest beneficiaries of the 2018-2019 tariff war were Vietnam, Mexico, and India, the three countries best positioned to absorb factory orders fleeing Chinese tariff exposure. And because relocating production means relocating export revenue, these countries saw upward pressure on their currencies in the months that followed. Foreign manufacturers building plants in Vietnam needed to convert dollars into dong to pay for land, labor, and local inputs — a steady bid under the currency that did not exist before the trade war redirected the supply chain.

This creates a tradable structure that desks called the "tariff rerouting" play: long the currencies of the rerouting beneficiaries (VND, MXN, INR) against short CNH, sized up when tariff escalation peaks. The logic is that each round of tariffs simultaneously weakens the yuan (currency offset, capital flight risk) and strengthens the recipients of redirected orders (incoming foreign direct investment, rising export receipts). The trade is cleanest at the moment of maximum escalation, because that is when the reallocation incentive is strongest and the relative-value gap between the targeted currency and the beneficiary currency is widest.

But Vietnam learned that being the beneficiary carries its own risk. VND-denominated exports to the US surged roughly 75% across 2018-2019 as supply chains rerouted through Vietnamese factories — and that surge put Vietnam directly into the crosshairs of the US Treasury's currency surveillance. In 2019, Vietnam was placed on the US currency manipulation watch list, and by December 2020 the Treasury formally labeled it a manipulator, citing a bilateral trade surplus above the threshold, a current account surplus, and one-sided FX intervention to hold the dong down. The lesson is structural: when you absorb another country's export flows, you inherit a slice of that country's trade-surplus politics. The rerouting trade works on the currency, but the secondary tariff risk migrates with the production.

#### Worked example:

**A 1-month VND carry trade vs. the dollar.** Suppose spot USD/VND is 24,000 and you want to go long VND (short USD) for one month using a forward. The forward points are set by the interest rate differential: Vietnamese 1-month rates around 4.5% annualized versus US 1-month rates around 5.5% annualized — a differential of -1.0% in VND's favor of *cost*, since the higher-yielding currency trades at a forward discount.

- Interest rate differential over 1 month: (5.5% - 4.5%) / 12 = 0.0833%
- Forward points: 24,000 × 0.000833 = +20 points
- 1-month forward USD/VND: 24,000 + 20 = 24,020

So to buy VND forward (sell USD forward), you lock in 24,020 rather than 24,000 — you pay 20 points of carry because USD yields more. On a \$1,000,000 notional, that carry cost is 1,000,000 × (24,020 - 24,000) / 24,000 ≈ \$833 for the month. The trade only pays if spot VND appreciates enough to beat that carry: if rerouting FDI inflows push USD/VND down to 23,800 over the month, the spot gain of 1,000,000 × (24,000 - 23,800) / 24,000 ≈ \$8,333 dwarfs the \$833 carry cost — a net gain of roughly \$7,500. The risk is that the SBV intervenes to cap dong strength (to protect its own exporters and stay off the manipulation list), which caps the upside of the very trade the tariffs created.

## The 7.00 Maginot Line and the 1994 Precedent

The 7.00 level in USD/CNY is best understood not as a price but as a fortification — a line China built up the political capital to defend, which is exactly why crossing it carried so much signaling weight. A Maginot Line works only as long as everyone believes it will be held; the moment it is breached, its psychological value inverts and it becomes a marker of how far the defender was willing to retreat. When the PBOC let the fixing print above 7.00 on August 5, 2019, the breach itself — not the size of the move — is what triggered a global EM selloff. Currencies from the Korean won to the South African rand sold off in sympathy, because the market read the broken line as evidence that the largest manager of a managed float had chosen to stop managing.

Behind that line sits a hard constraint that the previous decade had already taught Beijing. China's FX reserves peaked near \$4.0 trillion in 2014 and fell to roughly \$3.0 trillion by the end of 2016 — about a trillion dollars burned in two years defending the yuan against a depreciation scare and the capital flight it triggered. That episode is the reason China manages CNY depreciation as a slow pressure valve rather than a one-shot weapon: defending a peg is expensive, and the reserves spent doing it do not come back. A central bank can sell dollars to support its currency only as long as it has dollars to sell, and the 2015-2016 drawdown showed how fast even the world's largest reserve stockpile can shrink when domestic holders decide to move wealth offshore.

This is why CNY depreciation is more threat than weapon. A full, deliberate devaluation large enough to offset a serious tariff would inflict enormous domestic wealth destruction — Chinese households and firms holding yuan assets would see their global purchasing power collapse, dollar-denominated corporate debt would become crushingly expensive to service, and the capital flight would accelerate precisely when the PBOC could least afford to feed it with reserves. The political economy makes a maximal devaluation close to unusable: it would impoverish the domestic constituency the government most needs to keep stable.

The historical analogy that haunts the region is 1994. In that year, China unified its dual exchange-rate system and effectively devalued the official rate from around 5.8 to 8.7 yuan per dollar — a roughly 33% move in one step. The devaluation dramatically improved Chinese export competitiveness against the rest of Asia, and over the following three years it contributed to the mounting trade and currency pressures that helped set up the 1997 Asian financial crisis, as Southeast Asian economies running dollar pegs found their exports increasingly undercut. Whether 1994 "caused" 1997 is debated, but the sequence is a permanent fixture in regional policy memory: a large Chinese devaluation is read across Asia as the first domino, not an isolated event. That memory is part of why Beijing treats the depreciation lever with such caution — and why the 7.00 line was worth defending for as long as it credibly could be.

## Further Reading

- **James Rickards, "Currency Wars" (2011)** — the book that popularized the term; useful for historical context on the 1930s and post-Bretton Woods era, though some predictions have not aged well
- **Barry Eichengreen, "Exorbitant Privilege: The Rise and Fall of the Dollar"** — the definitive academic treatment of the dollar's reserve status
- **IMF Article IV consultations on China** — the IMF's annual assessment of China's exchange rate policy, available on the IMF website; primary source for the "currency manipulator" determination criteria
- **Federal Reserve H.10 data release** — the Fed publishes daily USD/CNH and DXY data; essential for tracking the trade war thermometer in real time
- **Peterson Institute for International Economics (PIIE) trade tracker** — maintains a real-time database of tariff rates and trade flows during the US-China trade war; essential for calibrating the tariff level at any point in the timeline
- **Bank for International Settlements (BIS) quarterly review** — publishes in-depth analysis of global currency flows, carry trade dynamics, and EM currency stress; freely available at bis.org
- **Chad Bown and Melina Kolb, "Trump's Trade War Timeline"** at PIIE — the best single chronological resource on the 2018-2020 tariff war with precise dates, levels, and market reactions
- On the Plaza Accord specifically: **Yoichi Funabashi, "Managing the Dollar: From the Plaza to the Louvre" (1988)** — written by a journalist with access to the principals; the most detailed account of how the deal was actually negotiated


Trade wars are fundamentally about who bears the cost of rebalancing global economic imbalances. Tariffs are the opening move, but currencies are where the real negotiation happens. The country whose currency depreciates the least — the country that forces others to absorb the adjustment — wins in the short run. In 2018-2019, both the US and China were losers: the US because its trade deficit widened despite the tariffs, and China because its export growth slowed.

The dollar's privilege is that the US can run this game longer than anyone else. Dollar swap lines, SWIFT access, and Treasury market depth are leverage that no other country has. But as Liberation Day 2025 showed, even the dollar has limits: at extreme tariff levels, markets began pricing in the risk that the US itself was degrading the institutional credibility that makes the dollar a safe haven.

The 7.00 line in USD/CNH — that seemingly arbitrary number that triggered a 3% S&P decline in a single day in August 2019 — is a window into this deeper dynamic. It's not just about the exchange rate. It's about who believes the other side will blink first.
