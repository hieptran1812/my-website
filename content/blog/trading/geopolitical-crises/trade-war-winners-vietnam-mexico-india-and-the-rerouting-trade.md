In July 2018, the US Treasury added a 25% tariff to \$34 billion worth of Chinese goods. By the end of that year the list had grown to cover roughly \$250 billion in imports. The conventional wisdom was that American consumers would pay more, China's factories would struggle, and trade volumes would fall. All three happened — but something else happened too, something the conventional wisdom consistently underestimates in every trade conflict: the manufacturing found a new route.

It always does. A tariff does not eliminate comparative advantage. It reprices it. When the cost of shipping a Samsung smartphone from Shenzen to San Francisco suddenly carries a 25% surcharge, Samsung's CFO starts a spreadsheet. That spreadsheet has rows for Vietnam, Mexico, India, Indonesia, and Thailand. The numbers in the Vietnam row looked particularly compelling in 2018 — cheap labor, reasonable ports, an existing semiconductor supply chain seeded by Intel's Da Nang facility, and a government eager to sign MoUs with any Fortune 500 company that called. Within six months of the first tariff wave, Samsung had accelerated its existing Vietnam expansion plans. Within two years, Vietnam was producing more than 50% of Samsung's global smartphone output.

This post is a field guide to that process. It explains why tariffs reroute rather than eliminate trade, which countries won in the US-China trade war, how to identify the next winners before official statistics catch up, and what the Liberation Day tariffs of April 2025 — which simultaneously hit Vietnam at 46% — changed about the calculus. It is grounded in real FDI data, real export figures, and real worked calculations so you can apply the same framework yourself.

## Foundations: Supply Chains, Tariffs, and FDI

Before diving into the country-by-country stories, it is worth building the conceptual scaffolding from scratch. None of this requires a finance degree — it is mostly applied common sense with precise vocabulary.

### What is a tariff?

A **tariff** is a tax imposed by an importing country on goods arriving from a specific exporting country (or all countries). If the US imposes a 25% tariff on Chinese washing machines, an importer who would have paid \$500 per unit now pays \$500 + \$125 = \$625 at the border. The US Treasury collects the \$125. The importer decides whether to absorb the cost, pass it to the consumer, or find a different supplier.

The critical point is that a tariff changes the relative price between suppliers. A 25% surcharge on Chinese washing machines makes South Korean washing machines more competitive — even if South Korean manufacturing is actually more expensive in absolute terms. The tariff alters who wins the cost comparison.

### What is FDI?

**Foreign direct investment (FDI)** is when a company from one country builds or buys a productive asset — a factory, a distribution center, a mine — in another country. It is different from portfolio investment (buying stocks or bonds) because it involves physical control over productive capacity.

When Samsung announces it is building a new smartphone assembly plant in Hanoi, that is FDI. The capital flows from South Korea to Vietnam, a factory gets built, Vietnamese workers get hired, and within 18 months Vietnamese export statistics start showing more smartphones leaving the country.

FDI is the mechanism by which trade rerouting happens. It is also the leading indicator, because the factory has to be built before the exports can flow.

### What is a supply chain?

A modern **supply chain** is rarely a straight line from raw material to consumer. A smartphone contains roughly 1,000 components from dozens of countries. The screen might come from South Korea; the memory chips from Taiwan; the camera sensors from Japan; the rare earth magnets from China; the assembly labor from Vietnam; the software from the US. "Made in Vietnam" on the box means the final assembly step happened in Vietnam — it says nothing about where 90% of the value was actually created.

This is the critical distinction between **rerouting** and **reshoring**, which we will examine in detail. When supply chains move from China to Vietnam, the "China content" often does not disappear — it just arrives in Vietnam as components rather than being shipped to the US as finished goods.

### What is a trade war?

A **trade war** is an escalating cycle of tariffs between countries. Country A raises tariffs on Country B's exports. Country B retaliates. Country A escalates. The key economic insight is that in the short run, tariffs primarily hurt the importing country's consumers (through higher prices) and the exporting country's producers (through lower volumes). In the medium run, they redirect trade flows. In the long run, they may or may not change the underlying comparative advantages that drive trade patterns.

The US-China trade war began in earnest in July 2018 with Section 301 tariffs justified by allegations of intellectual property theft. By 2019, average US tariffs on Chinese goods had risen from 3.1% to more than 21%. By April 2025 — "Liberation Day" — they reached 145% on many categories before a 90-day negotiation pause brought them back to approximately 30%.

### The rerouting thesis in one sentence

When a tariff makes Country X uncompetitive, manufacturing moves to Country Y — and the FDI that enables that move arrives 12 to 18 months before the export data confirms it.

### The comparative advantage foundation

The economic theory behind all of this is **comparative advantage** — the idea, formalized by David Ricardo in 1817, that countries (or companies) specialize in what they produce most efficiently relative to their alternatives, even if they could theoretically produce everything more cheaply than their trading partners.

China's comparative advantage in manufacturing in 2018 was not purely "cheap labor." It was an entire ecosystem: enormous scale that reduced per-unit fixed costs; a domestic supply chain for nearly every input; deep institutional knowledge of factory management; infrastructure (roads, ports, power) built specifically for export manufacturing; and a government that treated factory construction as a strategic priority. All of this took 30 years to build.

A tariff does not erase a comparative advantage. It adds a cost that the market then has to route around. For simple, labor-intensive goods (garments, furniture, AirPod assembly), the comparative advantage can move to Vietnam or Bangladesh within 2-3 years. For complex, capital-intensive goods (semiconductors, advanced automotive), the comparative advantage is embedded in an entire regional ecosystem and moving it takes a decade or more.

This is why Vietnam wins the AirPod production battle but has no path to winning the advanced chip fabrication battle in the current decade. Comparative advantages have different half-lives depending on their source.

### How tariffs interact with global value chains

Modern manufacturing is organized into **global value chains (GVCs)** — the cross-border networks of suppliers, manufacturers, and distributors that collectively produce a product. Apple's iPhone is the canonical example: designed in California, chips fabricated in Taiwan, screens made in South Korea, assembled in China (and increasingly Vietnam and India), sold globally.

When a tariff is imposed on the final assembly step, it changes the economics of that specific step while leaving the rest of the value chain intact. This is why 70-80% of "supply chain diversification" away from China is actually just moving the final assembly step while keeping the upstream value chain — component supply, chip fabrication, materials processing — in China.

The implication for investors: the countries winning the assembly relocation (Vietnam, Mexico, India) capture the employment and GDP impact of manufacturing, but they often do not capture the value that resides upstream. Apple's iPhone generates approximately \$450 of value across the entire supply chain; the assembly step in Vietnam or China captures perhaps \$15-20 of that. The high-value steps — chip design, display development, software — remain in US, Taiwanese, South Korean, and Japanese hands.

## The supply chain rerouting thesis

The rerouting thesis is built on three observations that have held across every major tariff escalation since the 1980s.

**First, tariffs change relative prices but not comparative advantages.** Vietnam has a genuine cost advantage in labor-intensive manufacturing. That advantage existed before the trade war. The tariff did not create it — it made it financially decisive for companies that previously found it cheaper to stay in China despite slightly higher Chinese labor costs.

**Second, FDI announcements precede everything.** Trade data is published with a 2-3 month lag. GDP data arrives quarterly. But FDI announcements — a press release from Samsung, a permitting filing from Intel, a ribbon-cutting at a Foxconn facility — appear in real time. A sophisticated investor watching the FDI wire in August 2017 (Samsung's Vietnam expansion announcement) had a 6-to-12-month head start on the trade data and an 18-to-24-month head start on the equity repricing.

**Third, the host country's equity market reprices last, not first.** This is counterintuitive. You would expect the market to immediately price in "Vietnam is a trade war winner." But markets are uncertain about duration, magnitude, and anti-circumvention risk. The VN-Index actually fell 15% in the six months after tariffs began in 2018 — the market was pricing geopolitical risk, not the FDI story. The FDI story showed up in equity prices with a multi-year lag.

### Why some countries win and others do not

Not every low-wage country becomes a trade war winner. The pattern across multiple historical trade conflicts — the 1980s US-Japan semiconductor trade war, the 2002 US steel tariffs, the 2018 US-China escalation — is consistent: the winners are countries that combine some of the following:

1. **Existing manufacturing infrastructure in the relevant sector.** Vietnam had Samsung and Intel facilities before the trade war. The trade war accelerated investment into an existing supply chain, not a greenfield sector.

2. **Government policy that actively supports FDI.** Vietnam's MPI (Ministry of Planning and Investment) operates a dedicated FDI promotion agency that coordinates permits, SEZ allocations, tax holidays, and infrastructure connections for major foreign investors. Mexico's ProMéxico does the same. Countries without this institutional capacity tend to lose to countries with it.

3. **Geographic or treaty-based tariff advantages.** Mexico's USMCA position means its tariff rate for US-bound goods is 0% regardless of trade war dynamics. Vietnam has signed free trade agreements with the EU (EVFTA), ASEAN, and other major trading partners, giving Vietnamese-made goods preferential access to multiple markets — a diversification buffer.

4. **Sufficient scale of labor supply.** Vietnam has 55 million workers in the 15-64 age bracket. Bangladesh has similar demographics. Countries with smaller populations cannot absorb major manufacturing relocations — Laos or Cambodia can handle small shifts but not Samsung-scale relocations.

5. **Absence of the same cost disease.** Countries that already have high wages relative to output (Eastern European EU members, for instance) lose to countries still on the earlier part of the labor-cost curve.

The countries that consistently fail to capture rerouting FDI are those with one element missing: strong government support but insufficient labor scale (Singapore); abundant labor but weak infrastructure (parts of Sub-Saharan Africa); good infrastructure but high wages (Czech Republic, Hungary).

## Vietnam: the 2018-2024 trade war winner

Vietnam's positioning as a trade war winner was not accidental. It was the product of fifteen years of deliberate industrial policy, seeded by Intel's 2006 decision to build a chip-testing facility in Ho Chi Minh City and accelerated by Samsung's 2008 decision to build its first Vietnam smartphone factory in Bac Ninh province.

By 2018, Vietnam already had a functioning electronics supply chain: engineers, suppliers, customs processes, and SEZs (special economic zones) with preferential tax rates for foreign manufacturers. When the US-China tariff escalation began, Vietnam was not starting from zero — it was accelerating from an existing base.

### The FDI surge in numbers

Pre-trade war, Vietnam was receiving roughly \$14-18 billion per year in FDI. That was already impressive for a country of 97 million people with per-capita GDP around \$2,700.

After the tariff shock, the numbers changed:

| Year | FDI (USD Bn) | Key event |
|------|-------------|-----------|
| 2015 | 14.5 | Baseline |
| 2016 | 15.8 | Steady growth |
| 2017 | 14.1 | Pre-war |
| 2018 | 19.1 | Trade war begins — FDI surge |
| 2019 | 16.1 | COVID preparation delays |
| 2020 | 19.9 | Apple AirPods shift announced |
| 2021 | 15.6 | COVID disruption |
| 2022 | 17.9 | Recovery |
| 2023 | 23.2 | New peak |
| 2024 | 25.0 | Continued acceleration |
| 2025 | 26.5 | Liberation Day uncertainty begins |

The jump from \$14.1B in 2017 to \$19.1B in 2018 was not coincidence — it tracked almost exactly with the tariff announcement calendar. Companies that had been studying Vietnam for years suddenly had a 25-percentage-point financial incentive to move faster.

### The companies that moved

**Samsung** was the most important single actor. By 2023, Samsung's Vietnam operations produced approximately 50% of Samsung's global smartphone output and employed over 220,000 Vietnamese workers. Samsung's Vietnam revenue is roughly equal to 28% of Vietnam's entire GDP — an extraordinary concentration of economic dependency.

**Apple** moved more cautiously but consistently. AirPods assembly shifted to Vietnam through Luxshare and Goertek starting around 2020 — by 2022, approximately 30% of global AirPod supply was manufactured in Vietnam. MacBook assembly followed in 2023. Apple's shift is ongoing; Vietnam now handles assembly for multiple product categories.

**Intel** expanded its existing Da Nang chip-testing facility, which had been operating since 2006. Intel's Vietnam facility tests and packages chips that are fabricated elsewhere — it is a critical node in Intel's global supply chain.

**LG Electronics** moved significant appliance and electronics production, including a \$1.5B facility in Hai Phong province.

**Foxconn, Luxshare, and Pegatron** — the three main Apple contract manufacturers — all expanded Vietnam capacity significantly between 2020 and 2024.

### The export effect

The FDI translated into exports with approximately the expected 12-18 month lag:

![Vietnam exports to US 2015 to 2025 line chart showing the trade war surge](/imgs/blogs/trade-war-winners-vietnam-mexico-india-and-the-rerouting-trade-2.png)

Vietnam's electronics exports to the US followed an almost textbook rerouting curve: gradual growth through 2017, explosive acceleration from 2018 onward, a COVID dip in 2020, then a second surge as Apple's diversification accelerated. From \$41.6B in 2017 to \$109.1B in 2022 and \$111.2B in 2024, the total increase was 167%.

To put that in perspective: Vietnam's total electronics exports to the US grew by \$69.6 billion over seven years. The entire economy of New Zealand produces about \$250 billion per year. Vietnam effectively added a quarter of New Zealand to its export balance sheet from electronics alone.

### The VN-Index story

Here is where the story gets interesting for investors. The VN-Index — Vietnam's benchmark equity index — did not immediately surge when the trade war began. It fell.

From January 2018 (1,120 points) to December 2018 (892 points), the VN-Index dropped 20%. The market was pricing geopolitical risk — the fear that escalating trade wars would damage global growth and hurt Vietnam's manufacturing base. The FDI story was not yet reflected in data, and analysts were uncertain about how durable the shift would be.

The opportunity window opened precisely because the market was wrong about the direction of causality. The trade war was not a risk to Vietnam's manufacturing base — it was a tailwind. But that tailwind showed up in VN-Index prices only from mid-2019 onward, running from 964 (December 2019) to 1,499 (December 2021) — a 55% gain over two years.

#### Worked example:

**The Samsung announcement trade in the VNM ETF**

Samsung announced a \$3.3 billion expansion of its Vietnam operations in August 2017, before the tariffs began. A trader who understood the rerouting thesis would have recognized this as a leading indicator of what was coming.

The VanEck Vietnam ETF (VNM) tracks Vietnamese equities. Let us work through two entry scenarios:

**Scenario A: Buy on the FDI announcement (August 2017)**
VNM price in August 2017: approximately \$18.50 per share (using VN-Index 1,000 as proxy baseline).

**Scenario B: Buy after tariff news is confirmed (July 2018)**
VNM price in July 2018: approximately \$15.00 per share (VN-Index had fallen to ~950).

**Scenario C: Buy at trough (December 2018)**
VNM price in December 2018: approximately \$13.00 per share (VN-Index at 892).

Now trace forward to December 2021 when VN-Index reached 1,499 — approximately 50% above the January 2018 high:
- VNM equivalent in December 2021: approximately \$19.50

Returns:
- Scenario A buyer: \$19.50 / \$18.50 - 1 = +5.4% (small gain, the rally started from below entry)
- Scenario B buyer: \$19.50 / \$15.00 - 1 = +30% (good return)
- Scenario C buyer: \$19.50 / \$13.00 - 1 = +50% (best return, trough buyer)

The counterintuitive lesson: the FDI announcement (Scenario A) did not give you the best entry price. The market's overreaction to geopolitical risk created a better entry six months later. But the FDI announcement was the signal that told you the direction — so you were positioned to add on the dip rather than panic-selling it.

The sophisticated play: buy VNM on the Samsung announcement in August 2017, add to position through the 2018 selloff (as VN-Index fell from 1,120 to 892), and exit in December 2021 at VN-Index 1,499. Average cost of roughly \$14.50, exit at \$19.50 = +34% over approximately 4.5 years, or roughly 7% annualized. Not spectacular, but with a clear fundamental thesis and an identifiable catalyst, in an asset that correlated negatively with US equity risk (since Vietnam was benefiting from, not being hurt by, the trade war).

## Mexico: the nearshoring boom

Mexico's story is structurally different from Vietnam's. Vietnam won by being a low-cost assembly hub for electronics. Mexico is winning by being a geography-optimized, tariff-exempt manufacturing base for complex, heavy-cargo goods that cannot be economically air-freighted from Asia.

The decisive factor is USMCA — the United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement that replaced NAFTA in 2020. Under USMCA, goods manufactured in Mexico with sufficient North American content pay 0% tariff when entering the US. This is not a trade war winner position — it is a structural, treaty-embedded advantage that persists regardless of what tariffs are imposed on China, Vietnam, or anyone else.

### Mexico becomes the top US trading partner

In 2023, Mexico surpassed China as the United States' largest trading partner for the first time since 2002. US-Mexico trade reached approximately \$798 billion, compared to \$575 billion for China. This was not a marginal shift — it was a structural reordering of the world's most important bilateral trade relationship.

The shift was driven by:

1. **Auto manufacturing**: The US auto industry has deep Mexico roots via NAFTA/USMCA. GM, Ford, and Stellantis collectively operate 44 plants in Mexico. As EV production scales, those plants are being retooled for EV components and assembly.

2. **Tesla Gigafactory Monterrey**: Announced in 2023, this is the largest single nearshoring announcement in history — a \$5 billion factory targeting 1 million vehicles per year. (We examine this in a worked example below.)

3. **Aerospace and defense supply chains**: Airbus, Boeing suppliers, and defense contractors have been expanding Mexico capacity for a decade. Chihuahua state has more aerospace plants per capita than any US state.

4. **Electronics**: Foxconn committed \$500 million to a Guadalajara facility for Apple supply chain components. Lenovo and HP both manufacture in Mexico for US distribution.

5. **Nearshoring infrastructure**: Mexico's northern industrial corridor (Monterrey, Saltillo, Juárez, Tijuana) has invested heavily in industrial parks, power infrastructure, and workforce training specifically to capture nearshoring demand.

![US import shares by country 2017 versus 2024 grouped bar chart](/imgs/blogs/trade-war-winners-vietnam-mexico-india-and-the-rerouting-trade-6.png)

### Mexico's structural advantages

Mexico has three advantages that Vietnam cannot replicate:

**Geographic proximity**: A truck from Monterrey to Dallas takes 8-12 hours. A container ship from Ho Chi Minh City to Los Angeles takes 18-22 days. For just-in-time manufacturing, for heavy goods, and for goods with unpredictable demand swings, Mexico's transit time advantage is decisive.

**USMCA treaty protection**: USMCA tariff rates are 0% for qualifying goods. They cannot be changed by executive action — they require legislative renegotiation. This gives investors in Mexico manufacturing much greater certainty than investing in Vietnam, where the tariff rate depends on the current political relationship.

**Existing infrastructure**: Mexico has 20,000 kilometers of highway, 27,000 kilometers of railroad, and 17 major ports. The industrial regions of northern Mexico have reliable power, water treatment, and manufacturing workforce that took decades to build.

#### Worked example:

**Tesla Gigafactory Monterrey: the tariff-savings math**

Tesla announced the Monterrey gigafactory in February 2023. The stated investment: \$5 billion. The target output: 1 million vehicles per year. The key financial question is: what is the tariff-arbitrage value of making those cars in Mexico rather than China?

Let us work through the math.

If Tesla were to manufacture 1 million vehicles per year in China and export them to the US, here is the tariff exposure:

- Average EV price: \$40,000
- US tariff on Chinese-made EVs (as of 2024): 100% (raised from 25% in May 2024 by the Biden administration)
- Tariff cost per vehicle: \$40,000 (the tariff equals the vehicle price at 100%)
- Annual tariff cost: 1,000,000 × \$40,000 = \$40,000,000,000 — **\$40 billion per year**

With USMCA manufacturing in Mexico:
- Qualifying tariff rate: 0% if 75% North American content requirement is met
- Tariff cost per vehicle: \$0
- Annual tariff saving: \$40 billion

Even at the pre-2024 25% tariff rate:
- Tariff per vehicle: \$10,000
- Annual tariff cost: 1,000,000 × \$10,000 = \$10,000,000,000
- Annual saving from Mexico: \$10 billion

The \$5 billion gigafactory investment pays back in **6 months** from tariff savings alone at the 25% rate, and in under 2 months at the 100% rate. The Monterrey investment is not a bet on cheap Mexican labor — it is a bet on the permanence of USMCA combined with the escalation of China tariffs.

This arithmetic explains why every major auto manufacturer, electronics company, and industrial supplier with US sales is currently stress-testing their Mexico capacity.

### The constraints on Mexico

Mexico is not a frictionless winner. Three structural constraints are slowing the nearshoring boom:

**Power grid reliability**: Mexico's CFE (national power company) runs an aging grid. Industrial parks in Nuevo León regularly experience rolling blackouts that would be unacceptable to a semiconductor fab. Several major projects have been delayed pending private power generation solutions.

**Water availability**: Northern Mexico's industrial corridor is in a water-stressed region. Semiconductor manufacturing requires extremely pure water in large volumes. TSMC shelved early discussions about a Mexico fab partly for this reason.

**Skilled labor shortages**: Mexico produces approximately 130,000 engineering graduates per year — impressive in absolute terms but not enough to staff a simultaneous expansion of auto, electronics, aerospace, and semiconductor facilities. Wages for skilled manufacturing workers in Monterrey have risen 40% since 2021, partly eroding the cost advantage.

## India: the emerging challenger

India's position in the supply chain rerouting story is structurally promising but operationally complicated. It is the most important country to watch for the next decade of trade war positioning — but it is 5-7 years behind Vietnam in manufacturing readiness.

### The Apple iPhone case study

Apple's India expansion is the clearest signal of the supply chain shift. Here are the facts:

- **2020**: Apple begins qualifying Wistron's Bengaluru factory for iPhone 12 assembly — the first iPhone assembled outside China
- **2022**: Foxconn's Sriperumbudur factory (Tamil Nadu) begins assembling iPhone 14 — the first time the latest iPhone model was assembled in India simultaneously with China
- **2023**: 7% of global iPhones are assembled in India (up from essentially 0% in 2019)
- **2024**: India target increased to 14%
- **2025 target**: 25% of global iPhone assembly in India

At Apple's current run rate of approximately 200-220 million iPhones per year, 25% India assembly = 50-55 million iPhones per year assembled in India. That requires roughly \$3-4 billion in annual wage payments, \$10+ billion in component supply chain, and roughly 200,000 assembly workers.

The PLI scheme (Production Linked Incentive) is Modi's mechanism for accelerating this. Under PLI, a manufacturer assembling phones in India receives a cash subsidy equal to 4-6% of incremental sales above a baseline. For Apple, this is a \$700 million to \$1.5 billion annual benefit that directly offsets the cost of qualifying Indian factories.

### India's structural advantages

**Demographics**: India will have the world's largest working-age population through at least 2050. At approximately 450-500 million workers in the 15-35 age cohort, labor supply is not the constraint.

**English language**: Unlike Vietnam, China, or Mexico, India's educated workforce communicates in English — reducing friction for US and European companies managing supply chains.

**Geopolitical alignment**: India is an official US strategic partner under the Quad framework. Modi's relationship with successive US administrations has generally been positive. India has been largely exempt from the most aggressive US tariff escalations — the April 2025 Liberation Day tariffs hit India at 26%, significantly less than Vietnam's 46% or China's 145%.

**Software ecosystem**: India's existing dominance in software services creates a ready workforce for embedded systems, firmware, and the "software-defined manufacturing" needs of modern electronics.

### India's structural constraints

**Import substitution culture**: India's industrial policy tradition has historically favored protecting domestic producers over integrating into global supply chains. High tariffs on imported components — the exact inputs that electronics manufacturers need — are a direct headwind to India becoming a low-cost assembly hub. A company assembling iPhones in India imports displays (taxed at 10%), memory (taxed at 10%), camera modules (taxed at 10-15%) — the cumulative tariff burden on components can offset the labor cost advantage.

**Land acquisition**: Building a factory in India requires navigating land acquisition processes that can take 3-7 years even with government support. Vietnam has streamlined this to 12-18 months through SEZ pre-approval.

**Infrastructure gaps**: Power reliability in India's manufacturing hubs (Tamil Nadu, Karnataka) has improved significantly but remains below Vietnam or Mexico in reliability metrics. Logistics — road quality, port congestion, customs clearance times — adds 15-25% to effective manufacturing costs compared to Vietnam benchmarks.

**Scale timeline**: Vietnam had a 15-year head start in building electronics manufacturing infrastructure. India is realistically 5-8 years behind in comparable readiness for complex electronics assembly.

#### Worked example:

**India vs. Vietnam iPhone assembly cost comparison**

A decision-maker at Foxconn choosing between expanding iPhone assembly in Vietnam vs. India would model something like this:

**Assembly labor cost per iPhone unit:**
- Vietnam: \$8-12 per unit (experienced workforce, specialized facilities)
- India: \$10-15 per unit (learning curve premium, higher supervisory overhead)
- China: \$18-22 per unit (higher wages, but more experienced)

**Component import cost per iPhone (display + memory + camera):**
- Vietnam import tariff: 0% to 5% (FTA with many component suppliers)
- India import tariff: 10-15% on key components
- Additional cost in India from tariffs: \$15-25 per iPhone

**Logistics cost per iPhone (to US port):**
- Vietnam to Long Beach: \$4-7 per unit (19-day ocean + handling)
- India to Long Beach: \$6-10 per unit (21-day ocean + longer customs + handling)
- Difference: \$2-4 per unit

**PLI subsidy (India only):**
- PLI credit: approximately \$20-30 per iPhone assembled in India
- This largely offsets the component tariff disadvantage

**Net comparison for a \$600 iPhone:**
- Vietnam: \$10 labor + \$2 logistics = \$12 total incremental cost
- India: \$12 labor + \$20 component tariffs + \$3 logistics - \$25 PLI = \$10 total incremental cost

At current PLI rates, India and Vietnam are roughly cost-competitive for iPhone assembly. The PLI acts as a direct tariff offset. The question is: can India sustain PLI funding at scale (currently capped), and can it solve the land acquisition and infrastructure bottlenecks fast enough to reach the 25% assembly target?

## How to identify winners before the data

The FDI lead indicator framework is the most reliable tool for identifying supply chain rerouting winners before the trade data or GDP statistics confirm the shift. Here is the systematic application.

![FDI lead indicator pipeline from tariff announcement to equity repricing](/imgs/blogs/trade-war-winners-vietnam-mexico-india-and-the-rerouting-trade-7.png)

### Step 1: Monitor FDI announcement wires

The most reliable source is not Bloomberg terminal data — it is government press releases. Vietnam's Ministry of Planning and Investment (MPI) publishes quarterly FDI data with project-level detail. Mexico's ProMéxico and SE (Secretariat of Economy) publish investment announcements in real time. India's DPIIT (Department for Promotion of Industry and Internal Trade) publishes FDI inflows monthly.

The signal: a sharp acceleration in announced FDI projects (not just total dollar volume — individual project announcements matter) from specific sectors (electronics, auto, machinery) is the leading indicator.

### Step 2: Identify the tariff trigger

Not every FDI acceleration is trade-war-driven. A government subsidy program can also cause FDI to surge. The key question is: does this FDI announcement explicitly reference US-China tariffs as a motivation? When Toyota announces a Thailand expansion, is the press release silent about trade policy, or does it mention "supply chain diversification in response to trade uncertainty"?

Company-specific tariff exposure analysis is useful here. A company with 80% China-sourced US sales has enormous incentive to reroute when a 25% tariff hits. A company with 20% China exposure has much less urgency.

### Step 3: Map the timeline lag

Once you have identified a FDI surge in a specific country, estimate the timeline:

- **Month 0**: FDI announcement
- **Month 6-12**: Factory construction begins (visible via satellite imagery — Planet Labs and Maxar publish commercial imagery)
- **Month 12-18**: Employment ramp-up begins (visible in labor market data for SEZs)
- **Month 18-24**: Export statistics begin to reflect new production
- **Month 24-36**: GDP growth accelerates, equity analysts upgrade earnings estimates
- **Month 30-42**: Equity market fully reprices the winner country

The investor's edge window is months 0-18: the period where the FDI signal is clear but the equity market has not yet confirmed it.

### Step 4: Size the anti-circumvention risk

Not all FDI-driven export surges are durable. The US has anti-circumvention provisions that allow the Commerce Department to impose duties on goods that are substantially Chinese in content but assembled in a third country. The risk matrix:

- **Low risk**: Deep supply chain integration (auto components with North American content > 75% per USMCA); genuine value-added transformation in the host country
- **Medium risk**: Electronics with significant Chinese-origin components assembled in Vietnam or Malaysia
- **High risk**: Solar panels, steel, aluminum products assembled with >80% Chinese inputs; textiles with Chinese-origin fiber that is simply cut and sewn in a third country

Vietnam has faced anti-circumvention investigations in solar panels, wood products, and some steel categories. Mexico's USMCA rules-of-origin requirements actually function as an anti-circumvention mechanism built into the treaty — a product assembled in Mexico with <75% North American content does not qualify for USMCA rates.

## The rerouting vs. reshoring distinction

This distinction matters enormously for policy and for investment analysis, yet it is almost never precisely defined in media coverage.

**Rerouting** means the manufacturing moves to a lower-tariff country but retains the same basic structure: Chinese-designed, Chinese-input, third-country-assembled goods sold to US consumers. The value chain is the same; only the final assembly geography has changed.

**Reshoring** means manufacturing returns to the US (or to a strongly allied country with deep domestic supply chains). The US CHIPS Act, the Inflation Reduction Act's domestic content requirements, and TSMC's Arizona fab are examples of genuine reshoring efforts.

The crucial point: almost all the "supply chain diversification" currently being celebrated — Vietnam electronics, Mexico auto, India iPhones — is rerouting, not reshoring. This has several implications:

**For anti-circumvention risk**: Rerouted supply chains are inherently vulnerable to US trade lawyers arguing that the value-add is insufficient to justify different tariff treatment. Vietnam has faced this argument in solar panels; it will face it in electronics if US trade policy becomes more assertive.

**For the winner's durability**: A rerouted supply chain can be rerouted again. If 46% tariffs on Vietnam persist (as happened under Liberation Day), the FDI can shift to Mexico, Indonesia, or Bangladesh. The switching cost is lower than it appears because the underlying supply chain architecture — Chinese component suppliers, Chinese-origin designs — is already built for mobility.

**For investment thesis longevity**: Mexico's USMCA-protected position is more durable than Vietnam's unprotected position, because USMCA's rules-of-origin requirements force genuine value-add rather than merely tariff-rate arbitrage.

#### Worked example:

**Anti-circumvention risk calculation for Vietnamese solar panels**

Vietnam became a major US solar panel supplier after China was hit with anti-circumvention duties on panels assembled with Chinese wafers in Cambodia, Malaysia, and Vietnam. The timeline illustrates the risk:

- **2012**: US imposed anti-dumping and countervailing duties on Chinese solar cells ranging from 24% to 254%
- **2014-2018**: Vietnamese solar exports to US surge as panels are "assembled" in Vietnam with Chinese cells (Vietnamese value-add = minimal cutting and framing)
- **2022**: US Commerce Department launches anti-circumvention investigation into Cambodia, Malaysia, Thailand, and Vietnam
- **2024**: Anti-circumvention duties imposed on Vietnamese solar panels using Chinese-origin cells: up to 200%
- **Result**: Vietnamese solar panel exports to US fell approximately 60% in 2024

The financial exposure: approximately \$8 billion of Vietnam's \$111 billion in US exports (7.2%) are in categories with active anti-circumvention risk as of 2024. This is not fatal to the Vietnam trade story — but it illustrates the floor risk for any rerouting play.

The lesson for investors: when evaluating a rerouting winner, always ask what percentage of exports are "high pass-through" (Chinese input > 70%, minimal transformation) versus "genuine integration" (meaningful local value-add, local supply chain depth). The former is vulnerable; the latter is durable.

## The Liberation Day shock and what it changed

On April 2, 2025, the Trump administration announced sweeping "reciprocal tariffs" under an executive order claiming to balance bilateral trade deficits. The tariff schedule hit every country in the world with rates calibrated to their trade surplus with the US:

| Country | Liberation Day tariff |
|---------|----------------------|
| China | 145% |
| Vietnam | 46% |
| Cambodia | 49% |
| Bangladesh | 37% |
| India | 26% |
| Mexico | 0% (USMCA protected) |
| Canada | 0% (USMCA protected) |
| EU | 20% |
| Japan | 24% |

The shock was that the trade war winners of 2018-2024 — Vietnam, Cambodia, Bangladesh — were hit harder than some of the losers. The logic from the administration: these countries had become "pass-through" nodes for Chinese manufacturing, running large bilateral surpluses with the US that reflected Chinese value embedded in nominally Vietnamese or Cambodian products.

Immediately after the announcement, VN-Index fell approximately 8% in two days. The EWW Mexico ETF (iShares MSCI Mexico) rose 4% — USMCA protection was explicitly confirmed. The INDA India ETF fell modestly (3%), as 26% is manageable.

**The 90-day pause**: On April 9, the administration announced a 90-day pause for most countries (excluding China) to enable negotiations. Vietnam entered direct trade negotiations with the US, offering to reduce tariffs on US goods and to impose stricter anti-circumvention enforcement. By May 2025, negotiations had produced a preliminary framework pointing toward a 10% baseline tariff for Vietnam — the same rate applied to most countries under the pause.

### Two-scenario analysis for Vietnam

**Scenario A: 10% negotiated tariff (most likely as of June 2025)**
- Vietnam exports to US partially restored
- FDI continues to flow, though perhaps more cautiously
- VN-Index recovers from April trough (VN-Index was 1,195 in April 2025, recovered to approximately 1,285 by June)
- Long-term rerouting thesis intact, though with greater headline risk

**Scenario B: 46% tariff sustained**
- Vietnam FDI begins to shift — not reverse, but redirect to Mexico (0%), Indonesia (20%), or Bangladesh (37%)
- VN-Index faces another 10-15% downleg from January 2025 levels
- Samsung begins accelerating capacity shift to India as secondary backup
- Anti-circumvention enforcement intensifies, shrinking the effective export base

The asymmetry favors Scenario A: Vietnam has strong diplomatic and economic incentives to negotiate, and the US has strong consumer-price incentives not to sustain 46% tariffs on the country that makes 30% of global AirPods.

#### Worked example:

**VN-Index tariff sensitivity analysis**

The VN-Index is not a pure manufacturing index. Its composition approximately reflects Vietnam's broader economy:

| Sector | VN-Index Weight | Tariff Exposure |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|
| Financials (banks, insurers) | 30% | Indirect (via export GDP) |
| Real estate | 25% | Indirect (via employment) |
| Manufacturing and export | 20% | Direct |
| Consumer staples/discretionary | 15% | Indirect |
| Other | 10% | Low |

Only the 20% manufacturing/export weight is directly and immediately affected by US tariff changes.

Now model the impact of sustained 46% tariffs cutting Vietnamese electronics exports to the US by 30%:

- Vietnam electronics exports to US: approximately \$111 billion (2024)
- 30% reduction: \$33.3 billion reduction in exports
- As % of Vietnam GDP (approximately \$450 billion): 7.4% of GDP
- Direct hit to the 20% manufacturing sector: full exposure
- But only a fraction reaches VN-Index earnings (many export factories are not listed)

**Earnings impact estimate:**
- Export-sector earnings in VN-Index: approximately 12-15% of aggregate earnings
- A 30% export volume decline with margin compression: earnings impact to this segment of approximately 25-35%
- VN-Index earnings impact from export sector: 12% × 30% = 3.6% aggregate earnings decline
- Applying a 15x P/E multiple: VN-Index fair value impact = -3.6% × 15 = approximately -54 points from a 1,200 base = -4.5%

**But the real impact includes second-order effects:**
- Employment income reduction → consumer spending decline → banks' NPL ratios rise
- Real estate prices soften as factory workers face uncertainty
- FDI pipeline slows → future growth downgraded
- Adding second-order: total VN-Index impact in Scenario B = 10-15% from January 2025 levels

**In Scenario A (10% negotiated tariff):**
- Export reduction: minimal (10% tariff is manageable for most electronics)
- Second-order effects: minimal
- VN-Index: recovers to January 2025 levels (1,310) or above

The asymmetry is clear: Scenario B (-10-15%) vs. Scenario A (+5-10% from trough). This is the kind of asymmetry that makes the VNM ETF worth holding through the negotiation period — as long as you have conviction that the US-Vietnam trade relationship has more incentive to reach resolution than to sustain the 46% rate.

## Common misconceptions

**"Trade wars hurt everyone equally."**

False. They redistribute economic activity. The US consumer pays more (via higher prices). China's export manufacturers lose volume. Vietnam, Mexico, and India gain manufacturing investment. Some US manufacturers gain protection (steel producers benefited from Chinese steel tariffs). Trade wars create losers and winners — the analytical challenge is correctly identifying which is which.

**"Vietnam will keep winning because it is cheap."**

Partially true, but incomplete. Vietnam's labor cost advantage is real but narrowing. Vietnam's monthly manufacturing wage was approximately \$190 in 2018; it is approximately \$310 in 2024. That is still far below China's \$700 average, but the gap is closing. More importantly, Vietnam's competitive advantage in the current cycle is not just cheap labor — it is a functioning electronics supply chain that took 15 years to build, combined with government policy that actively courts foreign manufacturers. Countries with just cheap labor (Bangladesh, Cambodia) have not replicated Vietnam's electronics success.

**"USMCA protects Mexico from all tariffs."**

Partly true. USMCA protects qualifying goods from tariffs — but "qualifying" requires meeting rules-of-origin requirements. For automotive products, 75% of the vehicle's value must originate in North America. For some electronics categories, the rules are more lenient. A company that moves a Chinese factory to Mexico but continues importing 80% of its components from China will not qualify for USMCA rates. The treaty is a tariff shield — but it has a qualifying condition that forces genuine integration into North American supply chains.

**"India cannot compete with Vietnam because its bureaucracy is too slow."**

Historically accurate but changing faster than most investors realize. Modi's government has streamlined a significant number of industrial approvals. Tamil Nadu's "single-window clearance" system for manufacturing investments now processes approvals in 45-60 days for greenfield projects in designated zones — comparable to Vietnam's SEZ approval timelines. The infrastructure gap (power, logistics) is more real than the bureaucracy gap at this point.

**"Supply chain rerouting is permanent once established."**

False, as Liberation Day demonstrated. FDI is sticky over 3-5 year timescales — you do not abandon a \$500 million factory because tariffs change. But new FDI flows are highly sensitive to tariff certainty. If Vietnam's tariff rate is uncertain (ranging from 10% to 46% depending on negotiations), new FDI projects will wait for clarity before breaking ground. This is exactly what happened in Q2 2025: FDI announcement volumes in Vietnam declined approximately 15% quarter-over-quarter as companies waited for the negotiation outcome.

## How it shows up in real markets

### Country ETFs as the primary vehicle

The most liquid and direct expression of the supply chain rerouting trade is through country ETFs:

**VNM (VanEck Vietnam ETF)**
- AUM: approximately \$600 million
- Holdings: 30-40 Vietnamese companies, heavily weighted to financials (banks, insurers) and real estate
- Manufacturing/export weight: approximately 15-20%
- Limitation: the ETF is not a pure play on manufacturing exports — it is a proxy for Vietnamese economic growth broadly

**EWW (iShares MSCI Mexico ETF)**
- AUM: approximately \$1.2 billion
- Holdings: 50-60 Mexican companies across financials, consumer, telecoms, materials
- Nearshoring pure-play component: approximately 25-30% (industrials + materials)
- Better alternative for pure nearshoring exposure: FIBRAMACRO (Mexican industrial REIT, holds Monterrey industrial parks)

**INDA (iShares MSCI India ETF)**
- AUM: approximately \$8 billion
- Holdings: 100+ Indian companies, technology-heavy (Infosys, TCS, Reliance)
- Manufacturing exposure: limited (India's listed market is skewed to services)
- Better alternative for manufacturing exposure: SMIN (iShares MSCI India Small Cap ETF, which has more industrial and manufacturing companies)

### FX signals

Currency movements are often the fastest-moving signal of supply chain position. When FDI flows into a country, demand for its currency rises:

- **Vietnamese dong (VND)**: Managed float by the SBV (State Bank of Vietnam). The SBV intervenes actively, so the dong is not a clean signal. But pressures on SBV's reserve management are visible — a depletion of reserves suggests the SBV is selling USD to defend the dong against depreciation (negative for the trade story); reserve accumulation suggests export strength.

- **Mexican peso (MXN)**: Freely floating and highly liquid. The MXN surged approximately 15% against the USD between 2022 and 2023 as nearshoring news flow accelerated — the market was correctly pricing increased FDI and trade activity. After Liberation Day, MXN fell modestly before recovering — the USMCA protection story quickly dominated.

- **Indian rupee (INR)**: Managed by the RBI but more flexibly than the VND. Generally tracks global risk appetite more than trade-specific FDI flows because India's current account deficit means FDI inflows have to be large to move the currency materially.

### Bond market signals

In Vietnam, the government bond market trades at around 3-4% yield for 10-year bonds. Sovereign credit rating (BB by Fitch as of 2024) reflects both the manufacturing success and the concentrated risk (Samsung's Vietnam operations represent a significant fraction of GDP — if Samsung faced a major problem, it would be a systemic shock).

The credit rating trajectory is the slow-moving but most meaningful signal: Vietnam's credit rating has improved steadily since 2010 and reflects the FDI success story in macro terms.

## How to trade it — the playbook

Putting this all together into a practical decision framework:

### The FDI announcement playbook

**Signal**: Major manufacturer (Fortune 500, major Asian conglomerate) announces expansion in a specific country with explicit reference to US tariff environment.

**Entry**: Country ETF within 30 days of announcement, sized at 2-5% of portfolio.

**Add condition**: If the country ETF sells off 10-15% on geopolitical risk within 6 months of entry while the FDI thesis remains intact (new announcements continue, construction visible in satellite data), add 1-2% more.

**Exit condition A**: FDI announcement flow slows to pre-shock levels. This indicates the structural shift has run its course.

**Exit condition B**: Anti-circumvention duties imposed on the country's export category (e.g., electronics anti-circumvention on Vietnam), reducing the tariff advantage.

**Exit condition C**: Trade deal eliminates the tariff that drove the rerouting in the first place (e.g., US-China deal returns tariffs to 5% — the whole arbitrage disappears).

**Position sizing**: Country ETFs are emerging-market assets with significant currency, political, and liquidity risk. 2-5% per position maximum for most portfolios. The trade works over 3-5 year holding periods, not 3-5 months.

### The sector-specific lens

Not all sectors reroute equally. The hierarchy:

**Fast rerouters** (can shift assembly in 12-18 months):
- Consumer electronics assembly (AirPods, earbuds, simple devices)
- Garments and textiles (cut-and-sew)
- Furniture and household goods

**Medium rerouters** (18-36 months):
- Smartphones and tablets (requires specialized supply chain)
- Industrial machinery (requires precision manufacturing capability)
- Auto components (requires quality management system build-up)

**Slow rerouters** (36-60 months):
- Semiconductors (requires clean-room facilities, water, power reliability)
- Advanced automotive (full vehicle assembly requires full supply chain)
- Aerospace (requires FAA/EASA certification for components)

Vietnam and Bangladesh are winning the fast-rerouter categories. Mexico is winning the medium-rerouter categories. No country outside China and Taiwan is yet competitive in semiconductors.

### Risk management

The key risks in order of likelihood:

1. **Anti-circumvention rulings** (medium probability, medium impact): Have an exit plan if US Commerce Department opens an investigation into your country's exports in your sector.

2. **Currency depreciation** (medium probability, medium impact): Vietnam and India manage their currencies actively. A sharp depreciation (like India's rupee weakening from 83 to 90 in a stress scenario) would partially offset equity gains in USD terms.

3. **Political disruption** (low probability, high impact): Vietnam's one-party system is stable but opaque. Mexico's AMLO/Sheinbaum governments have occasionally made anti-foreign-investment statements. India's regulatory environment can shift with election outcomes.

4. **US-China deal** (low probability, high impact): A comprehensive US-China trade normalization would remove the tariff incentive for rerouting. This is the tail risk that could reverse the whole thesis. Currently assessed as low probability given the depth of strategic competition between the US and China beyond trade.

5. **Liberation Day-style escalation** (medium probability, high impact): As demonstrated in April 2025, the tariff policy can shift rapidly and hit the rerouting winners directly. Position sizing and monitoring of trade negotiation developments are essential.

## Identifying the next beneficiary: a framework for 2025 and beyond

The 2018-2024 cycle produced its winners. The investor's real question is: who is next? The framework that identified Vietnam in 2017 is repeatable, and it reduces to four conditions a country must satisfy before it can absorb rerouting FDI at scale.

**Condition 1 — An existing manufacturing base in the relevant sector.** Greenfield miracles do not happen. Vietnam won because Samsung and Intel were already there; the trade war accelerated an existing trajectory rather than creating one. A country with zero electronics ecosystem cannot become an electronics hub in 18 months, no matter how cheap its labor.

**Condition 2 — Functioning export infrastructure.** Ports, power, roads, and customs that can clear a container in days rather than weeks. This is precisely where India still lags Vietnam and where Sub-Saharan candidates fall out.

**Condition 3 — Tariff-advantaged access to the US market.** Either a low headline tariff or a treaty (USMCA for Mexico, EVFTA for Vietnam's European diversification). After Liberation Day, this condition became the binding one — Vietnam's 46% rate temporarily disqualified it on this axis.

**Condition 4 — A genuine labor-cost advantage.** The wage gap must be wide enough to overcome the friction of moving. Eastern Europe fails here; its wages are too close to the destination market's.

Score the candidates for the next wave against these four:

- **Indonesia** (garments, footwear, furniture): large labor pool (140 million workers), 20% Liberation Day tariff (better than Vietnam's 46%), an established textile and footwear base from Nike and Adidas suppliers. The weakness is infrastructure outside Java.
- **Bangladesh** (textiles): the world's second-largest garment exporter already, with a deep cut-and-sew ecosystem. The 37% tariff and chronic port congestion are the constraints.
- **Morocco** (automotive): the dark horse. It is near-shoring for the *EU*, not the US — Renault and Stellantis run major plants, and the Tangier port sits a ferry-ride from Spain. Morocco illustrates that "rerouting" is not a US-only phenomenon.
- **Mexico** (broad nearshoring): still the highest-scoring candidate, because USMCA satisfies Condition 3 at the treaty level and proximity satisfies Conditions 2 and 4 simultaneously.

India deserves special treatment because its trajectory differs from Vietnam's in a fundamental way. Vietnam's bottleneck was always labor *scale* — and it solved that quickly because the cost advantage was decisive and the government cleared the path. India's bottleneck is *regulatory friction*: land acquisition, component-import tariffs, and inter-state logistics. Labor cost is not India's problem; bureaucracy is. That is a harder constraint to dissolve, because it is structural rather than economic, which is why India will likely take 5-7 years to reach the manufacturing density Vietnam achieved in 3.

The single cleanest signal that diversion is beginning is the **initiation of a bilateral free trade agreement** between the candidate country and a tariff-imposing bloc. An FTA is a multi-year commitment that both governments only undertake when trade flows are already meaningful and expected to grow. Vietnam signed the EVFTA with the EU in 2020, locking in preferential access just as the rerouting surge accelerated. India's FTA with the UK, in advanced negotiation through 2025, is the equivalent tell for the current cycle. When you see FTA talks begin, the FDI has usually already started moving.

#### Worked example:

**Vietnam 2018 vs. Indonesia 2024 — comparing two investment cases at the same point in their cycles**

Place the two countries side by side at the moment each became an investable rerouting candidate.

**Vietnam, entering 2018:**
- GDP growth: 7.1% (one of the fastest in Asia)
- FDI inflow: rising from \$14.1B (2017) to \$19.1B (2018), a +35% jump tracking the tariff calendar
- Equity proxy: the VNM ETF returned approximately +9% in 2018's first half before the geopolitical selloff, while the broad EEM emerging-market ETF fell roughly -16% over the same window — Vietnam was *decoupling* from the EM index because the trade war was a tailwind, not a headwind
- Verdict in hindsight: a clear buy, with the best entry on the late-2018 dip

**Indonesia, entering 2024:**
- GDP growth: approximately 5.0% (solid but below Vietnam's 2018 pace)
- FDI inflow: approximately \$22B, with foreign direct investment in manufacturing rising as garment and footwear orders shift from China and Vietnam
- Tariff position: 20% under Liberation Day — materially better than Vietnam's 46%, which is the new decisive variable
- The catch: infrastructure outside Java and a smaller electronics base than Vietnam had

The parallel is imperfect but instructive. Indonesia in 2024 looks like a *slower-burning* version of Vietnam in 2018 — the FDI signal is present and the relative tariff position is now favorable, but the GDP growth and the depth of the existing manufacturing base are weaker. The framework says: investable, smaller position, longer horizon. Apply the same four conditions, weight the position to the conviction the score produces, and let the FDI wire — not the export data — tell you when to add.
