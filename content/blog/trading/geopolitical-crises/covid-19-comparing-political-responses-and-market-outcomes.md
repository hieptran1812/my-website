> **TL;DR** — COVID-19 was the first true natural experiment in real-time geopolitical risk pricing: four major governments adopted four radically different policy playbooks, and the market verdict was swift and brutal.
>
> - The US deployed approximately \$5 trillion in three legislative waves (CARES Act, CRRSA, ARP) and the S&P 500 not only recovered from its 34% crash in just 126 days — the fastest in modern history — but returned +28% for the full year 2021.
> - The EU's slower but ultimately large response (NextGenEU €750bn approved July 2020, four months after the crash) produced a solid +23% on the STOXX 600 in 2021 but at a noticeably lower altitude than the US.
> - China's zero-COVID policy eliminated the virus repeatedly but at the cost of economic paralysis: the CSI 300 fell 22% in full-year 2022 while the MSCI World gained 20pp — a 26-percentage-point underperformance gap that persisted and widened through 2024.
> - Sweden's voluntary, no-lockdown approach kept its economy open; the OMXS30 gained +37% in 2021, the best single-year return of any developed market in the dataset.
> - The VIX hit 82.7 on March 18, 2020 — its highest reading since the 2008 financial crisis — and the Fed cut rates to 0–0.25% and launched unlimited QE within three days.
> - Speed of fiscal response explains approximately 60% of the variance in equity recovery trajectories in 2020–2021, per cross-country analysis by the IMF and BIS.

On February 19, 2020, the S&P 500 hit an all-time high of 3,386. Thirty-three days later, it had lost 34% of its value — the fastest 30%-plus bear market in US history. The VIX, a real-time measure of how frightened the market is, spiked to 82.7 on March 18, 2020 — a level not seen since the darkest days of 2008. Gold jumped 25% over the year, Brent crude collapsed 73% at its trough, and the US 10-year Treasury yield touched 0.51% — its lowest point ever recorded.

What happened next separated the winners from the losers for the next four years. Governments around the world were forced to make three decisions simultaneously, under enormous uncertainty, with no reliable playbook: how aggressively to suppress the virus itself, how much money to borrow and inject into the economy, and how fast to act. The choices they made were not abstract policy preferences — they were, in effect, bets on the future of their equity markets.

This post dissects those bets. We compare four governments — the United States, the European Union, China, and Sweden — across the full arc of 2020–2024. We track exactly how fiscal speed, monetary coordination, and virus-control philosophy translated into equity returns, bond moves, and currency performance. And we extract the trading logic that, in hindsight, was hiding in plain sight.

## Foundations: Understanding Fiscal Policy, Monetary Policy, and Equity Markets

Before we can compare playbooks, we need to define the tools governments actually have. There are two main levers: fiscal policy and monetary policy. They work differently, move at different speeds, and affect markets through different channels.

### What is fiscal policy?

*Fiscal policy* is what governments do with their own budget — how much they spend and how much they tax. When a government sends \$1,200 stimulus checks to every adult, builds a field hospital, or pays the salaries of workers whose employers had to close, that is fiscal policy. It is enacted by legislatures (Congress, the European Council, the National People's Congress). This matters for timing: getting a law passed takes weeks or months.

The scale is measured in two ways:
1. **Absolute dollar amount** — how many trillions were deployed
2. **As a percentage of GDP** — this normalizes for country size. A \$2T stimulus in the US (GDP ~\$21T in 2020) equals about 10% of GDP. The same \$2T in Sweden (GDP ~\$0.5T) would be 400% of GDP — which is why we compare on a GDP-percentage basis throughout this post.

A *basis point* (abbreviated "bp" or "bps") is one hundredth of a percent — 0.01%. When people say the Fed cut rates by 150 bps, they mean it cut by 1.50 percentage points.

### What is monetary policy?

*Monetary policy* is what central banks do — institutions like the US Federal Reserve ("the Fed"), the European Central Bank (ECB), or the People's Bank of China (PBoC). Central banks cannot pass laws or spend tax money. Their main tools are:

1. **Interest rates** — the rate at which banks can borrow money overnight from the central bank. When this rate falls, borrowing gets cheaper throughout the entire economy: mortgages fall, corporate loans fall, the cost of financing a car or business drops. This encourages spending and investment.

2. **Quantitative easing (QE)** — when the central bank literally creates money electronically and uses it to buy bonds in the open market. This pushes bond prices up, which pushes yields (the interest rate a bond pays) down, and injects cash into the financial system that investors then deploy into stocks, real estate, and other assets.

Central banks can move faster than legislatures. The Fed cut rates to 0–0.25% on Sunday, March 15, 2020 — an emergency meeting, before markets opened Monday. By contrast, the CARES Act took until March 27 to be signed into law.

### How do these tools affect equity prices?

A stock's price, at its most fundamental level, equals the sum of all the company's future profits, discounted back to today. Two things make that sum bigger:
1. **Higher expected future profits** — more spending in the economy means more revenue for companies
2. **Lower discount rate** — when interest rates fall, future dollars are worth more in today's terms, so the present value of any given stream of profits rises

When the Fed cut rates to zero and the government sent \$2.2 trillion into the economy, both happened simultaneously. That is why equity markets can bottom out before the economic data does — the market is pricing in the expected future, not the current quarter.

This is also why *speed* matters so much. The faster a government acts, the sooner the market can anchor to a recovery scenario instead of a catastrophe scenario.

### What is the VIX?

The *VIX* (Volatility Index) is sometimes called "the fear gauge." It measures how much the options market expects the S&P 500 to move over the next 30 days, annualized. When investors are frightened and buying protective options, the VIX rises. A VIX of 20 is roughly "normal." A VIX above 40 signals extreme fear. At 82.7 on March 18, 2020, the market was pricing in movement of roughly 24% over a single month — a catastrophic level of uncertainty.

### What is the risk premium?

*Risk premium* is the extra return investors demand for holding a risky asset (like a stock) instead of a safe one (like a US Treasury bill). When uncertainty spikes — when nobody knows if companies will survive the next quarter — investors demand a much higher premium to hold stocks. This is why stocks fall hard during a crisis: the required return goes up, which means the price you're willing to pay today goes down. When the government signals it will backstop the economy, risk premium collapses, and prices recover sharply.

## The Political Moves: Four Governments, Four Playbooks

Every country started from the same initial conditions: a novel virus, no vaccine, no playbook. What diverged was the political philosophy behind the response.

![Full-year 2021 equity market returns by political response playbook](/imgs/blogs/covid-19-comparing-political-responses-and-market-outcomes-2.png)

### The United States: Maximum Speed, Maximum Size

The US response can be summarized in a single sentence: go big, go fast, and worry about the debt later.

**Monetary first mover:** The Fed moved before Congress did. On March 15, 2020 — a Sunday, while markets were closed — the Federal Open Market Committee cut the federal funds rate from 1.0–1.25% to 0–0.25%. Simultaneously, the Fed announced it would purchase at least \$500bn of Treasury securities and \$200bn of agency mortgage-backed securities. On March 23, as markets were hitting their trough, the Fed escalated to *unlimited* QE — an open-ended commitment with no cap on bond buying.

This was unprecedented. The Fed was effectively saying: we will buy as many bonds as necessary to keep financial markets functioning. That single sentence removed the tail risk of a liquidity crisis — the nightmare scenario where companies can't roll over short-term debt and the whole financial system seizes up.

**CARES Act — \$2.2 trillion, signed March 27, 2020:** Just five weeks after the market peak, Congress passed the Coronavirus Aid, Relief, and Economic Security Act. At \$2.2 trillion — approximately 10% of GDP — it was the largest economic rescue package in US history at that time. Key provisions included:
- \$1,200 direct payments to adults earning under \$75,000 (\$2,400 for couples)
- \$600/week supplemental unemployment insurance (on top of state benefits)
- \$500bn for corporations and large businesses
- \$350bn for small business loans via the Paycheck Protection Program (PPP)
- \$150bn for state and local governments

Five weeks from market peak to a \$2.2T law is extraordinarily fast for democratic legislation. The speed signal mattered as much as the size.

**CRRSA — \$900 billion, December 2020:** In December 2020, Congress passed the Consolidated Appropriations Act (CRRSA), extending unemployment benefits, providing \$600 direct payments, and adding another \$284bn to the PPP.

**American Rescue Plan (ARP) — \$1.9 trillion, March 2021:** The Biden administration's signature pandemic bill added \$1,400 in direct payments per person, extended unemployment benefits through September 2021, \$350bn for state and local governments, and \$130bn for school reopening. Total US fiscal response across the three waves: approximately \$5 trillion — roughly 26% of 2020 GDP.

### The European Union: Slower but Ultimately Coordinated

The EU's political structure made rapid response harder. Fiscal decisions require agreement among 27 member states with wildly different views on debt, solidarity, and economic management.

**ECB moved fast — but it was monetary, not fiscal:** The ECB launched the Pandemic Emergency Purchase Programme (PEPP) in March 2020, with an initial envelope of €750bn later expanded to €1.85 trillion. Like the Fed, the ECB was committing to buy whatever it took in bond markets — a "whatever it takes" continuation of the Draghi doctrine.

**NextGenEU — €750 billion, approved July 2020:** The landmark fiscal response came four months after the crash: the European Commission proposed, and the European Council approved, NextGenEU — the EU's recovery fund. This was historically significant because it involved joint EU borrowing backed by all member states — a form of fiscal solidarity that would have been unthinkable before COVID. Of the €750bn, roughly €390bn was grants and €360bn was loans.

The four-month lag versus five weeks in the US is visible in the equity data: European markets recovered later, slower, and less completely than US markets in 2020, before both converged in 2021.

### China: Zero-COVID Elimination Strategy

China's approach was fundamentally different from the West's: eliminate the virus, not manage it.

From early 2020 through late 2022, China pursued what became known as the *zero-COVID policy* — aggressive mass testing, contact tracing, mandatory quarantine facilities, and city-wide lockdowns whenever any cases were detected. The logic was: a suppressed virus means factories can run, people can work, GDP can grow. And initially it worked — China's GDP grew +2.3% in 2020, the only major economy to post positive growth that year.

But the policy carried enormous structural costs that would become visible in 2022. The most dramatic example: Shanghai, China's financial capital and its largest city by population (approximately 25 million people), was placed under a strict two-month lockdown from April to June 2022 when an Omicron wave breached defenses. Residents were confined to their homes; food delivery systems collapsed; factories across the Yangtze River Delta — including critical semiconductor and EV supply chains — stopped production.

**China's fiscal response was targeted, not sweeping:** Unlike the US, China did not issue direct checks to consumers. Instead it focused on infrastructure investment, tax relief for businesses, and cheap credit through state banks. The total fiscal impulse was approximately 6% of GDP — real, but much smaller than the US or EU relative to GDP, and with less stimulative reach into household consumption.

### Sweden: The Contrarian Bet

Sweden became the most-watched natural experiment in COVID policy. Unlike virtually every other European country, Sweden did not impose a mandatory lockdown in 2020. Schools, restaurants, bars, and most businesses stayed open. The government relied on voluntary social distancing, hygiene recommendations, and trust in citizens to change their behavior.

The epidemiological outcome is debated — Sweden had higher per-capita death rates in spring 2020 than its Nordic neighbors but broadly similar outcomes to the UK and US over the full pandemic. The economic and market outcome is less debated: Swedish GDP contracted less than the EU average in 2020, and the OMXS30 (Sweden's equity benchmark) gained +37% in 2021 — the best return of any major developed market index in the year.

Sweden's fiscal package was approximately 3% of GDP — mostly short-time work schemes (*Kurzarbeit*-style programs allowing employers to reduce hours without layoffs) rather than deficit spending. The economy stayed more open, the government spent less, and the market rewarded both.

## The Financial Channels

Understanding how political decisions flow into market prices requires mapping the transmission mechanisms. There are three main channels:

**Channel 1: The Risk Premium Channel.** When a government commits credibly to backstop the economy — "we will pay workers' salaries, we will keep companies alive" — investors reduce the probability they assign to a catastrophic outcome. Lower catastrophe probability means lower risk premium, which means higher asset prices. This is why the S&P 500 bottomed on March 23, 2020 — the same day the Fed announced unlimited QE, and four days before the CARES Act was signed. The market was pricing the *expectation* of support.

**Channel 2: The Earnings Channel.** Fiscal stimulus directly boosts corporate revenues. PPP loans kept small businesses alive (preserving revenue for suppliers), extended unemployment insurance maintained household spending capacity (preserving retail revenues), and infrastructure spending boosted industrial revenues. Analysts raised their 2021 and 2022 earnings forecasts in direct proportion to stimulus size.

**Channel 3: The Discount Rate Channel.** Near-zero interest rates and massive QE purchases compressed yields across the entire curve. When the US 10-year Treasury yield fell to 0.51% (August 2020), the discount rate used to value all future corporate earnings fell dramatically. A company's stock whose fair value was, say, 18x earnings at a 5% discount rate becomes worth 30x earnings at a 2% discount rate — a 67% uplift from purely mathematical rerating, before any earnings improvement at all.

## US vs EU: Fiscal Speed Determines Recovery

The US-vs-EU comparison is the cleanest natural experiment because both economies had similar starting points (developed market, open economy, democratic governance) but very different fiscal timing.

### The 2020 Recovery Race

The S&P 500 troughed on March 23 at −34% from its February 19 peak. It recovered that peak on August 18, 2020 — just 126 days later. To put this in historical context: after the 2008 crisis, the S&P 500 took over four years to recover its prior peak. After the dot-com crash, it took seven years. The 126-day COVID recovery is the fastest major bear-market recovery in modern market history.

The STOXX 600 (European equity benchmark) also crashed hard — falling approximately 34% from peak to trough. But its recovery was shallower and slower in 2020. The ECB's PEPP provided monetary support quickly, but NextGenEU's approval in July gave the recovery a second leg. By year-end 2020, the S&P 500 was up approximately +16% from its January 2020 level; the STOXX 600 was roughly flat, down ~4%.

### Why Speed Mattered So Dramatically

The gap between "5 weeks" (US) and "4 months" (EU) was not just a bureaucratic lag. It determined two critical things for investors:

**Uncertainty duration:** Every day between crash and credible government commitment is a day where the market has to price in worst-case scenarios. The longer that window stays open, the more deeply the market discounts stocks, and the more selling pressure builds from margin calls, risk-limit breaches, and fund redemptions.

**Earnings forecast revision timing:** Analysts cannot upgrade their earnings forecasts until they know the size of the stimulus package. US analysts knew by late March 2020 that \$2.2T was going in. European analysts were uncertain through June and July. This directly delayed the "earnings upgrade cycle" that drives markets higher.

**The VIX spread:** US equity volatility (measured by VIX) peaked at 82.7 on March 18. It fell below 30 by late April — just five weeks after the CARES Act passed. European volatility (measured by VSTOXX) also peaked in March but remained elevated relative to VIX for several more months, reflecting the higher uncertainty about EU fiscal response.

### The 2021 Divergence

By 2021, with vaccines rolling out and economies reopening, both markets staged strong recoveries:
- S&P 500: +28.4% (full year 2021)
- STOXX 600: +23.1% (full year 2021)

The 5-percentage-point US advantage in 2021 reflected several compounding factors:
1. Faster vaccine rollout in the US (ahead of EU through Q1 2021)
2. Larger fiscal multiplier from the ARP (\$1.9T in March 2021 adding directly to demand)
3. Technology sector overweight in US indices (tech was the primary beneficiary of zero-rate QE-driven rerating)
4. Dollar strength (or euro weakness) that reduced foreign returns in USD terms for global investors benchmarked in dollars

#### Worked example: Measuring the fiscal multiplier effect on earnings

The *fiscal multiplier* is the amount by which GDP grows for every \$1 of government spending. Research from the IMF and academic economists suggests the COVID-era US multiplier was approximately 1.5 — meaning each \$1 of government spending increased GDP by roughly \$1.50.

The US deployed approximately \$5T in fiscal stimulus across 2020–2021. Apply the 1.5x multiplier: the implied GDP boost is \$7.5 trillion above the no-stimulus baseline.

US nominal GDP in 2019 was approximately \$21.4T. Corporate profits are roughly 10–12% of GDP in the US. So \$7.5T in additional GDP implies roughly \$750–900bn in incremental corporate profits over the stimulus period.

The S&P 500's market cap at the March 2020 trough was approximately \$19T. An \$800bn profit increment, valued at the market's forward P/E of ~22x, implies a market cap addition of roughly \$17.6T — close to the observed \$13T recovery in market cap from trough to 2021 year-end.

The math isn't perfect (many other factors matter), but it shows the magnitude of the causal link: large stimulus → large profit increment → large market recovery. The EU deployed 8% vs the US's 26%, which is a 3.25x multiplier difference — and broadly consistent with the 5–7pp market return gap in 2021.

**Intuition:** Government spending does not just "help the economy feel better." It directly flows through to corporate revenue lines, gets amplified by private spending multipliers, and then gets capitalized into stock prices at whatever P/E multiple the market is applying.

## China's Zero-COVID Trap

China's story is the most dramatic in the dataset — a policy that looked like genius in 2020 and 2021, then collapsed spectacularly in 2022.

### The 2020–2021 Phase: Zero-COVID as Competitive Advantage

In 2020, China's GDP grew +2.3% while the US contracted −3.4%, the EU contracted −6.1%, and global GDP contracted −3.1% (IMF data). Chinese factories stayed open when Western factories shut down; supply chains that ran through China kept running when European and US ones stopped. The CSI 300 gained +27% in 2020 — outperforming the S&P 500 (+16%) and crushing the STOXX 600 (−4%).

For investors watching in real-time, China's zero-COVID strategy looked like a form of institutional competence: a government willing and able to make the hard short-term sacrifices (full lockdowns of individual cities) to keep the broader economy running. By 2021, as Western economies were still struggling with Delta variants, China had achieved substantial domestic virus suppression.

The CSI 300's relatively flat 2021 (−5%) versus the S&P 500's +28% was the first warning sign — but most analysts attributed it to China's own regulatory crackdowns on tech platforms and property sector deleveraging rather than zero-COVID per se.

### 2022: The Trap Closes

The Omicron variant changed everything. Omicron's characteristics — extremely high transmissibility, lower severity — made zero-COVID operationally impossible while also reducing the medical justification for it. But China had committed deeply to the policy, both institutionally and politically.

The results:

**Shanghai, April–June 2022:** China's most economically significant city — home to the world's busiest container port and a massive cluster of tech, finance, and manufacturing — was locked down for approximately 60 days. An estimated 25 million residents were confined to their homes or quarantine facilities. Supply chains across the entire Yangtze River Delta region stopped or slowed dramatically.

**GDP impact:** China's Q2 2022 GDP growth rate decelerated to +0.4% year-over-year — essentially zero. Industrial production fell. Retail sales collapsed −11% at the April 2022 peak of the lockdown.

**Market impact:** The CSI 300 fell approximately 22% in full-year 2022. More dramatically, against the backdrop of a global risk-off environment, it underperformed the MSCI World by approximately 26 percentage points. Every point of that gap was zero-COVID premium — the market's estimate of how much economic value the policy was destroying.

![CSI 300 vs MSCI World indexed performance 2022 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/covid-19-comparing-political-responses-and-market-outcomes-3.png)

#### Worked example: Pricing the zero-COVID risk premium

How much of the CSI 300's underperformance was zero-COVID, versus China's property sector crisis and regulatory headwinds?

We can decompose it roughly as follows:

**Baseline:** MSCI Emerging Markets (EM) fell approximately 20% in 2022. This is the "global tightening + risk-off" baseline — what Chinese stocks might have fallen without zero-COVID.

**China actual:** CSI 300 fell approximately 22%. At first glance, that's only 2pp worse than EM.

But China is *overweight* in MSCI EM indices. The more precise comparison: MSCI China (a cleaner China-only cut) fell approximately 23% in 2022, while MSCI EM ex-China fell approximately 15%. That's an 8pp China-specific drag.

Decomposing that 8pp:
- Property sector crisis (Evergrande, Country Garden): approximately 3–4pp
- Zero-COVID operational disruption: approximately 3–4pp
- Regulatory/geopolitical risk premium: approximately 1–2pp

So zero-COVID accounted for perhaps \$300–400bn in market capitalization destruction in CSI-tracked equities alone in 2022 — an enormous but computable number.

**Intuition:** Investors don't just price what's happening today. They price the *option value* of the future. Zero-COVID, by definition, meant investors could not price in any normalization scenario — the government could re-lock any city at any time. That optionality destruction is worth hundreds of basis points of risk premium.

### The Policy Exit: December 2022

On December 7, 2022, China's National Health Commission announced a sweeping reversal of zero-COVID policies: mandatory quarantine requirements were lifted, testing requirements were reduced, and the policy of locking down buildings and neighborhoods was discontinued. The exit was abrupt — not a gradual wind-down but an overnight policy pivot driven by a combination of economic pain, growing social unrest (the "white paper protests" of late November 2022), and the mathematical impossibility of suppressing Omicron.

The market reaction was immediate and fierce:
- Macau casino stocks gained approximately 40% in the 30 days following the announcement
- CSI 300 rallied approximately 20% from December 2022 to January 2023
- Hong Kong's Hang Seng Index (which had already been pricing some policy change) surged

![China zero-COVID policy switch: before and after December 2022](/imgs/blogs/covid-19-comparing-political-responses-and-market-outcomes-7.png)

But the structural drag did not disappear. China's property crisis deepened through 2023 and 2024, consumer confidence remained subdued, and deflation pressures emerged. By January 2024, the CSI 300 (indexed to January 2022 = 100) stood at approximately 70 — while the MSCI World reached approximately 108. The cumulative underperformance from January 2022 to January 2024 was approximately 38 percentage points.

#### Worked example: Calculating the cost of delayed policy normalization

Suppose you manage a \$1 billion emerging market equity portfolio in January 2022 with a 20% China weight. Your China allocation is therefore \$200M.

**Scenario A: You correctly anticipated the zero-COVID extension through 2022.** You reduce China weight from 20% to 10% in January 2022, redeploying the \$100M to MSCI EM ex-China.

- China \$100M at −22% = −\$22M
- EM ex-China \$100M at −15% = −\$15M
- Remaining \$800M at a −20% MSCI World benchmark = −\$160M
- Total portfolio: −\$197M (−19.7% return)

**Scenario B: You stayed benchmark weight (20% China).**

- China \$200M at −22% = −\$44M
- Remaining \$800M at −20% = −\$160M
- Total portfolio: −\$204M (−20.4% return)

**Scenario C: You doubled China (40% weight) expecting zero-COVID exit in 2022.**

- China \$400M at −22% = −\$88M
- Remaining \$600M at −20% = −\$120M
- Total portfolio: −\$208M (−20.8% return)

The active return difference between Scenario A (correctly underweight China) and Scenario C (incorrectly overweight) is 1.1pp in 2022. That sounds small, but: (a) EM managers are measured in basis points, and 110bps of alpha is career-defining; (b) in 2023–2024 as the CSI 300 kept lagging, the Scenario A manager gained another 4–5pp of alpha over the Scenario C manager, for a cumulative 5–6pp difference over three years.

**Intuition:** In geopolitical event investing, the question is rarely "what is happening now" but "how long will this constraint persist?" The market missed that zero-COVID would last through all of 2022 — which created the entry opportunity for those who saw the policy trap closing.

## Sweden's Contrarian Experiment

Sweden's COVID response deserves its own section because it is frequently misunderstood in financial circles. The simplistic narrative is "no lockdown = good for stocks." The reality is more nuanced.

### What Sweden Actually Did

Sweden did not completely ignore COVID. The Swedish government issued recommendations (not mandates) on social distancing, hand hygiene, and mask use. Large gatherings were limited. Nursing homes were closed to visitors. Bars and restaurants operated at reduced capacity under guidelines. Universities shifted online.

What Sweden did NOT do: it did not close schools for ages 1–16, did not close most businesses, did not mandate work-from-home, and did not impose fines or criminal penalties for gathering. The approach relied on the relatively high level of civic trust in Sweden — where government recommendations carry more behavioral weight than in more adversarial political cultures.

### The Economic and Market Outcome

Sweden's GDP contracted −2.8% in 2020 — painful, but significantly less severe than the EU average of −6.1%. This reflected that large parts of the service economy kept running: retail, restaurants, offices, construction.

By 2021:
- OMXS30: +37% (best major developed market)
- Swedish GDP: +5.1% (above EU average)
- Swedish unemployment: peaked at 9.0% in 2020 and returned to 8.5% by end-2021

The OMXS30's sector composition matters here — Sweden's benchmark is overweight industrial and technology exporters (Volvo, Ericsson, Atlas Copco, SKF), which were primary beneficiaries of the global manufacturing and tech boom in 2021. The open economy kept these companies operating through 2020, putting them in a stronger competitive position when demand surged in 2021.

### What Drove the OMXS30's +37%?

Several factors compounded:
1. **Less cyclical damage in 2020:** Companies that stayed open avoided the deep trough that European peers fell into, meaning less "bounce" was required — more of the 2021 gains were real expansion, not recovery.
2. **Sector composition:** Heavy industrials and exporters benefit from the global recovery; Sweden had more of these than France or Germany.
3. **Swedish krona (SEK) depreciation:** The SEK weakened against the USD in 2020, which boosted the foreign-currency revenues of Swedish exporters when translated back to SEK-denominated results.
4. **Monetary support:** The Riksbank (Sweden's central bank) also cut rates and expanded its balance sheet, providing the same monetary tailwind as other central banks.

## Cross-Asset Ripples

The political responses to COVID rippled far beyond equity markets. Fixed income, commodities, currency markets, and cryptocurrency all moved in coherent response to the same policy signals.

![Political response taxonomy across four governments](/imgs/blogs/covid-19-comparing-political-responses-and-market-outcomes-6.png)

### Bond Markets: The QE Compression

When central banks commit to purchasing bonds without limit, bond prices rise and yields fall. The mechanics: a buyer that cannot be outbid (the central bank) buying in the open market forces private sellers to accept lower yields (higher prices) to find buyers. This effect cascades along the yield curve.

The US 10-year Treasury yield fell from approximately 1.9% in January 2020 to a trough of 0.51% in August 2020 — a compression of approximately 140 basis points. In practical terms:
- A 30-year fixed mortgage that would have been 3.8% in January 2020 fell to approximately 2.7% by late 2020
- Corporate investment-grade bond spreads initially blew out (credit risk spike in March) then compressed sharply as the Fed bought corporate bond ETFs for the first time in history
- High-yield ("junk") bond spreads peaked above 1,000 bps in March 2020 then compressed to below 400 bps by year-end

The ECB's PEPP had a similar effect on European sovereign spreads — the Italian 10-year versus German 10-year spread (the "BTP-Bund spread," a key measure of EU fragmentation risk) spiked to approximately 280 basis points in March 2020 and then compressed to below 100 bps as PEPP commitments convinced markets the ECB would not allow a fragmentation crisis.

### Commodities: The Deepest Scar

Brent crude fell approximately 73% from its January 2020 level to its April 2020 trough — hitting briefly negative in WTI (West Texas Intermediate) futures on April 20, 2020, a market phenomenon with no historical precedent. The simultaneous demand collapse (nobody flying, nobody driving) and a supply war between Saudi Arabia and Russia created a physical storage crisis: with nowhere to put oil, futures prices went negative because buyers of the futures were being paid to take delivery.

The commodity complex recovered dramatically when the policy responses took hold. Brent crude reached \$85/bbl by end-2021, and the commodity supercycle that ran through 2021–2022 was driven in large part by the massive fiscal stimulus — US infrastructure spending, EU Green Deal programs, and Chinese construction — that hit an economy with severely constrained supply.

### Gold: The Policy Hedge

Gold gained approximately 25% in 2020 — the best annual performance since 2010. Gold is typically seen as a store of value that benefits from:
1. **Negative real interest rates** — when inflation exceeds nominal yields, the cost of holding gold (which pays no interest) falls relative to holding bonds
2. **Dollar weakness** — gold is priced in USD; a weaker dollar makes it cheaper for non-US buyers
3. **Tail risk / inflation fear** — massive fiscal deficits and unlimited QE raised concerns about long-run inflation and currency debasement

All three conditions materialized in 2020–2021. Real US 10-year yields went deeply negative (nominal yield 0.5% minus expected inflation ~1.5% = real yield −1.0%). The DXY Dollar Index fell approximately 12% from its March 2020 peak through December 2020. And with the US fiscal deficit expanding to approximately \$3.1 trillion (15% of GDP) in 2020, inflation fears — while still modest — created a persistent bid for gold.

#### Worked example: The gold carry trade in 2020

Gold at the start of 2020: approximately \$1,520/oz (COMEX spot, January 2, 2020).
Gold at year-end 2020: approximately \$1,900/oz.
Full-year 2020 return: +25%.

But how does a trader quantify the return in risk-adjusted terms?

**Spot return:** \$1,900 − \$1,520 = +\$380/oz, or +25.0%.

**Alternative cost:** The US 10-year Treasury yielded approximately 0.9% for most of 2020. Holding gold (zero yield) versus a Treasury (0.9% yield) meant giving up approximately \$13.68 per year per \$1,520 invested (0.9% × \$1,520).

**Net risk-adjusted gain:** +25.0% − 0.9% = +24.1%.

By contrast, in a year where the 10-year yielded 4%, the opportunity cost of holding gold would have been much higher — the same +25% price move would net only +21% after opportunity cost, and there would need to be a much larger gold move to compensate for the foregone bond income. This is why gold performs best in zero-rate environments: the opportunity cost of holding it approaches zero, making it a pure bet on inflation/uncertainty.

**Intuition:** Gold is not "a hedge against inflation" in the simplistic sense. It is a hedge against *negative real interest rates* — the condition where the nominal yield on bonds is below inflation. COVID's policy response created exactly that condition.

## Common Misconceptions

### Misconception 1: "Lockdowns caused the market crash"

The market crashed before lockdowns were implemented in most countries. The S&P 500 peaked February 19, 2020 and began its decline when news of the Wuhan outbreak was becoming clear, even though US states did not begin issuing shelter-in-place orders until mid-March. Markets are forward-looking — they priced the *anticipation* of economic disruption. The crash was about uncertainty, not the direct economic impact of lockdowns.

Moreover, Sweden — which never locked down — still saw its equity market fall approximately 30% in the crash (before recovering). The sell-off was global and was driven by universal risk-off, not country-specific policy choices.

### Misconception 2: "The US recovered faster because its economy is bigger"

Size is not the mechanism. The US recovered faster because (a) fiscal response was faster and larger as a percentage of GDP, and (b) the Federal Reserve had deeper institutional experience in unconventional monetary policy from 2008–2015 and deployed it more aggressively.

Japan's economy is large; Japan deployed substantial QE; Japan's TOPIX index underperformed the S&P 500 throughout the period because Japan's fiscal response was more modest and its vaccine rollout was slower. Institutional speed and commitment, not raw economic size, drove recovery pace.

### Misconception 3: "China's zero-COVID strategy was irrational"

In 2020–2021, the strategy was economically rational: the costs of lockdowns (measured in temporary GDP reduction) were lower than the costs of widespread infection in an under-vaccinated population without mRNA vaccine protection. China's excess mortality in 2020–2021 was dramatically lower than the US or UK. The policy's irrationality emerged in 2022 when: (a) Omicron became dominant (high transmissibility made suppression exponentially costly), and (b) mRNA vaccines that provide strong protection were available globally but not adopted by China (which relied on domestically-produced Sinovac, with lower efficacy against Omicron).

The trap was political, not just epidemiological: having publicly committed to zero-COVID as a demonstration of systemic superiority, abandoning it required absorbing a significant reputational cost that delayed the policy exit by perhaps 6–9 months beyond what pure economics would have dictated.

### Misconception 4: "QE will cause hyperinflation"

This was a widely repeated prediction in 2020–2021 and was correct in a moderate form (US CPI peaked at +9.1% in June 2022) but wrong in its catastrophic form. QE did not cause hyperinflation (a 50%+ monthly rate) because the mechanism is different: QE creates bank reserves, which only translate into inflation if banks lend those reserves into the real economy at scale. In practice, QE-created reserves mostly sat in bank reserve accounts at the Fed or flowed into financial assets, not into the goods economy at a velocity that would cause hyperinflation.

The inflation that did occur was primarily driven by the *fiscal* component of the response — direct transfers to households who spent them — combined with COVID-induced supply chain disruptions (goods shortages) and commodity price spikes (energy, food). This is an important distinction: monetary policy (QE) inflated *asset prices*; fiscal policy inflated *goods prices*.

### Misconception 5: "Sweden proved lockdowns were unnecessary"

Sweden's experience is more complex than either side of this debate acknowledges. Sweden had higher per-capita COVID mortality than its Nordic neighbors (Denmark, Norway, Finland) in spring 2020 — a genuine cost. Its better GDP outcome in 2021–2022 reflects sectoral composition (industrial exporters vs. tourism-heavy Southern European economies) as much as the lockdown decision. The appropriate counterfactual is: what would Germany or France have looked like if they had adopted Swedish-style voluntary measures? That counterfactual is genuinely uncertain.

What is clear is that Sweden's market outcome (+37% OMXS30 in 2021) was excellent — but attributing all of that to the no-lockdown policy ignores sector mix, central bank support, and the global industrial cycle that lifted all industrial exporters regardless of COVID policy.

## How It Shows Up in Real Markets

### The "China Re-rating" Trade of 2022–2023

From late October 2022 through January 2023, one of the most-discussed trades in emerging market investing was what became known as the "China re-rating" thesis: if zero-COVID ends, Chinese equities are deeply undervalued (trading at 9–10x forward earnings vs. the S&P 500 at 18–20x), and the pent-up consumer demand release would drive a significant economic and earnings recovery.

The trade worked spectacularly in the short term: Macau gaming stocks gained 40%+ in December 2022. Chinese internet and consumer discretionary names surged. CSI 300 was up approximately 20% from December 2022 to January 2023.

But then it stalled. Property developer defaults (Evergrande, Country Garden) created a structural credit problem. Consumer confidence remained subdued. Youth unemployment hit a record high of approximately 21.3% in June 2023 before the government suspended publication of the data. Deflation (negative CPI in early 2024) signaled a demand shortfall that fiscal stimulus and zero-COVID exit could not fully fix.

The lesson: a geopolitical policy reversal can catalyze a violent initial re-rating, but structural issues that accumulated *during* the policy period do not resolve overnight. Trading a policy exit requires distinguishing the "snap-back" (fast, mechanical) from the "fundamental recovery" (slow, earnings-dependent).

### The VIX Signals Worth Trading Around

The VIX's spike to 82.7 on March 18, 2020 and its subsequent compression offers a tradeable pattern. Historically:
- Every VIX reading above 50 has marked an extraordinary opportunity to buy equities on a 12-month forward basis
- VIX above 40 (a lower threshold) has corresponded to positive 12-month S&P 500 returns approximately 85% of the time

The logic: when VIX is above 50, options are priced for a catastrophe that is usually partially avoided. Selling short-dated options (collecting premium) or buying long equity exposure has historically had very high positive expected value from these levels. The key risk is that the catastrophe partially materializes — the March 2020 case where the initial spike kept going for another week before the bottom — which is why position sizing and staging (buying in tranches as VIX remains elevated rather than all-in) is critical.

#### Worked example: The VIX mean-reversion trade

Suppose you observe VIX at 80 on March 18, 2020. Historical data shows VIX has never remained above 50 for more than 4 months.

You execute the following at-the-close of March 18:
- Buy \$100,000 in SPY (S&P 500 ETF) at approximately \$246/share
- Simultaneously sell covered calls: sell the June 2020 \$270 call for approximately \$12/share (premium)

Your effective entry cost: \$246 − \$12 = \$234/share.
If SPY closes above \$270 at June expiry, your gain is (\$270 − \$234) = \$36/share = +15.4%.
If SPY closes between \$234 and \$270, your net return is positive (you keep the premium, the stock is above cost basis).
Only if SPY closes below \$234 do you lose money.

SPY at June 19 expiry (3 months later): approximately \$315. The stock was called away at \$270 — a +15.4% return in 3 months.

Had you held the stock outright without writing the call: \$315/\$246 − 1 = +28%. You left 12.6pp on the table by selling the call. But: you reduced your maximum risk by \$12/share, and you had a high probability of a positive outcome regardless of whether the market fully recovered by June.

**Intuition:** In high-VIX environments, option premiums are elevated relative to realized volatility. Covered call writing or cash-secured put selling becomes attractive because you are selling insurance at a premium price — collecting the fear premium that panicked investors are willing to pay.

## Currency Markets: The Hidden Scoreboard

While equity markets got the headlines, currency markets were quietly running a parallel election on which policy regime the world trusted most.

### The Dollar's Contradictory 2020 Journey

The US dollar had a strange year: it initially surged (risk-off, dollar-is-safety), then fell sharply, then recovered.

**Phase 1 (February–March 2020): Dollar surge.** As the crisis broke, global investors rushed to dollars — the world's reserve currency and the asset most associated with safety in a genuine shock. The DXY Dollar Index (a measure of the dollar against a basket of major currencies) rose from approximately 99 in early February to approximately 103 at the March peak. This is the standard "risk-off" dollar move — every global crisis initially causes dollar appreciation as investors worldwide sell local currency assets and buy dollars to park in Treasuries.

**Phase 2 (April–December 2020): Dollar collapse.** Once the Fed announced unlimited QE and US fiscal deficits exploded (the US ran a deficit of approximately \$3.1 trillion in FY2020, roughly 15% of GDP), the dollar fell hard. By December 2020, the DXY had fallen to approximately 89.5 — a decline of approximately 14% from the March peak. This reflected:
- Massive increase in the supply of dollars (QE and deficit financing)
- US political uncertainty around the 2020 election
- "Vaccine diversification" — as multiple vaccine candidates progressed globally, non-US assets looked relatively more attractive

**Phase 3 (2021): Dollar partial recovery.** As the US economy rebounded faster than Europe and the Fed began signaling rate hikes sooner than the ECB, the dollar stabilized and then recovered.

### Euro and Swedish Krona Dynamics

The Euro appreciated approximately 9% against the dollar from March 2020 to the end of 2020. This Euro strength, combined with the STOXX 600's stock price gains, created a magnified return for US-dollar-based investors in European equities: a European investor gained +12% in local currency (STOXX 600) in 2020, but a USD-based investor gained approximately +20% accounting for Euro appreciation.

The Swedish krona (SEK) actually *weakened* against both the dollar and euro in 2020 — which had an interesting effect on Swedish corporate earnings. Sweden's major exporters (Volvo, Atlas Copco, Ericsson, SKF) earn revenues globally in dollars and euros but report in SEK. A weaker SEK mechanically boosted their reported earnings in 2020 even before any real volume recovery.

This currency tailwind contributed to the OMXS30's strong absolute performance. A USD-based investor in Sweden in 2020 would have seen those currency gains reversed — the OMXS30's +27% in local currency translated to approximately +19% in USD terms after the SEK's decline. But in 2021, as the SEK recovered alongside global risk appetite, the currency effect flipped back to a tailwind.

### Chinese Yuan: The Controlled Appreciation

China's currency, the renminbi (also called yuan, symbol CNY), is not freely floating. The People's Bank of China manages it within a daily band around a government-set midpoint. In 2020–2021, the CNY actually strengthened: from approximately 7.1 CNY per USD in January 2020 to approximately 6.45 CNY per USD by end-2021 — an appreciation of roughly 9%.

This CNY appreciation was deliberate policy. China wanted to attract foreign capital inflows, demonstrating economic stability. It also helped control import price inflation. For foreign investors in Chinese equities, CNY appreciation added approximately 9% to their USD-denominated returns in 2020–2021 — a meaningful boost on top of the CSI 300's local currency performance.

The irony: the period of CNY strength (2020–2021, zero-COVID appearing to work) coincided with good equity performance. The period of CNY weakness (2022–2023, zero-COVID failing) coincided with equity underperformance. Currency was simultaneously a symptom and a cause of the equity performance divergence.

## Sector Rotation: What Zero Rates Did to Stock Valuations

The most counterintuitive market outcome of COVID was the technology sector boom. Companies that were already richly valued in January 2020 became even more richly valued — not despite the crisis, but partly because of it.

### The Duration Argument for Tech

A *duration* in finance is not just a bond concept. Any asset with a cash flow stream has an implicit duration — the weighted average time until you receive those cash flows. For a mature industrial company (say, Caterpillar), most of the value is in near-term earnings: the company earns steadily today and markets don't assign enormous value to cash flows 20+ years away. Duration is relatively short.

For a high-growth technology company (say, a SaaS company growing at 40% per year), most of the *present value* of the company lies in cash flows 10–20 years in the future. Today's earnings are small; the company is valuable because of what it will earn in 2035. Duration is very long.

When interest rates fall (the discount rate falls), the present value of far-future cash flows increases disproportionately. A dollar in 2040 discounted at 5% has a present value of approximately \$0.23. That same dollar discounted at 1% has a present value of approximately \$0.45 — nearly double. Tech stocks are "long duration" equities: their prices are especially sensitive to changes in discount rates.

This is precisely what happened in 2020–2021. The Fed cut rates to zero and committed to keeping them there. The real yield on 10-year Treasuries went negative (0.5% nominal minus 1.5% expected inflation = −1.0% real). In this environment, the mathematics of discounting *massively* boosted the present value of high-growth, far-future-cash-flow companies. The Nasdaq 100 (technology-heavy index) gained +48.3% in 2020 and a further +27.3% in 2021, dramatically outpacing the S&P 500's +16% and +28.4%.

The reversal was equally dramatic: when the Fed began raising rates from March 2022 (eventually reaching 5.25–5.50% by July 2023), those discount rates reversed, and the same "long-duration" tech stocks fell hardest. The Nasdaq 100 fell approximately 33% in 2022 — the steepest reversal of the COVID-era tech boom.

This rate → tech valuation transmission was the same across US and European markets. The difference was that US indices had dramatically higher tech weights (approximately 28% of S&P 500 in tech at the 2021 peak, versus approximately 8% in STOXX 600). This single compositional difference explains a substantial fraction of the US market's 5pp advantage over Europe in 2021.

### Energy: The Deepest Scar and the Fastest Recovery

Energy was COVID's most extreme sector story. Brent crude, which started 2020 at approximately \$68/barrel, collapsed to approximately \$19/barrel in late April 2020 — a 72% decline. WTI (West Texas Intermediate) futures briefly traded at −\$37.63 per barrel on April 20, 2020 — meaning buyers of the front-month contract were being paid \$37.63/barrel to take delivery. This happened because storage tanks were full and traders could not find physical delivery points; the cost of not taking delivery (paying to store oil somewhere) exceeded the price of the oil itself.

The energy sector was the worst-performing S&P 500 sector in 2020, falling approximately 37%. Energy companies cut dividends, suspended buybacks, wrote down the value of reserves, and in some cases filed for bankruptcy (Chesapeake Energy, Denbury Resources, Whiting Petroleum — all Chapter 11 in 2020).

The recovery from this nadir was equally dramatic. As COVID vaccines enabled economic reopening and OPEC+ (the cartel of oil-producing countries including Saudi Arabia and Russia, which had initially collapsed into a price war in March 2020 before agreeing to historic production cuts) kept supply constrained, Brent crude reached \$86/barrel by October 2021 and \$125/barrel in June 2022 (as Russia's invasion of Ukraine compounded the supply crunch).

The energy sector became the single best-performing S&P 500 sector in 2022 (+65%) — a complete reversal of its 2020 position. Governments that had bet on cheap energy through stimulus spending found their fiscal calculations disrupted by energy inflation; consumers who had grown accustomed to low gas prices found energy costs consuming an outsized share of the stimulus they had received.

## The Inflation Overshoot: The Unintended Consequence

Every government that deployed massive fiscal stimulus in 2020–2021 ran the risk of overshooting on inflation. The US, with its 26%-of-GDP fiscal response, ran this risk most acutely.

### Why Inflation Hit in 2021–2022, Not 2020

In 2020, despite the enormous fiscal stimulus, US inflation was low — approximately 1.2% CPI for the full year. This seems paradoxical: the government injected trillions of dollars into the economy, but prices barely moved.

The explanation: supply and demand were both disrupted simultaneously. Consumers were receiving stimulus checks but had nowhere to spend them (restaurants were closed, travel was impossible, entertainment venues were shut). Demand destruction in services offset demand creation in goods; aggregate price pressure was muted.

By 2021, the calculus changed. Services began reopening (demand surge from pent-up savings), but supply chains remained disrupted (COVID restrictions in Asia constrained manufacturing, semiconductor shortages constrained auto and electronics production, port congestion delayed everything). Demand roared back; supply could not keep up. US CPI accelerated from approximately 1.4% in January 2021 to 7.0% in December 2021 to a peak of 9.1% in June 2022 — the highest US inflation reading since 1981.

The EU followed a similar trajectory, though with a secondary driver: energy prices. Russia's invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 caused European natural gas prices to spike approximately 900% from pre-COVID levels by August 2022. Eurozone CPI peaked at approximately 10.6% in October 2022.

### The Central Bank Response and Its Market Consequences

The Fed responded to the inflation overshoot with the most aggressive rate-hiking cycle since the 1980s. Starting from 0–0.25% in March 2022, the federal funds rate reached 5.25–5.50% by July 2023 — 525 basis points of tightening in 16 months.

For equity markets, this rate-hike cycle created a "quantitative tightening" (QT) effect that precisely reversed the 2020–2021 QE-driven rerating:
- P/E ratios compressed from peak levels (~35x forward for the S&P 500 at the 2021 peak) to approximately 18x by late 2022
- Long-duration tech stocks fell most (Nasdaq −33% in 2022)
- Short-duration value stocks (energy, financials, materials) held up best

This sequence — QE drives a long-duration asset rerating, rate hikes reverse it — is now one of the best-documented policy transmission mechanisms in modern financial history, with COVID as the clearest natural experiment.

#### Worked example: Computing the P/E compression from rate hikes

The *forward P/E ratio* (price-to-earnings) is the stock market's price divided by analysts' expected earnings for the next 12 months. In December 2021, the S&P 500 traded at approximately \$4,766 with forward earnings estimates of approximately \$220/share — giving a forward P/E of approximately 21.7x.

By December 2022, the S&P 500 traded at approximately \$3,840 with forward earnings estimates of approximately \$225/share (earnings estimates actually held up reasonably well) — giving a forward P/E of approximately 17.1x.

The compression in P/E: from 21.7x to 17.1x = −21.2%.

The index return: from \$4,766 to \$3,840 = −19.4%.

Most of the S&P 500's 2022 decline was not about earnings deterioration. It was about P/E compression driven by rising interest rates. Companies' actual profits barely moved; what changed was how much investors were willing to pay for those profits.

The intuition: when you can earn 5.25% "risk-free" (US T-bills by mid-2023), you demand a higher earnings yield from stocks to compensate for the risk. Earning yield = 1 / P/E, so a higher required yield → lower P/E → lower stock price for the same level of earnings.

**The policy link:** The excess fiscal stimulus of 2020–2021 caused the inflation overshoot of 2021–2022, which forced the rate-hike cycle of 2022–2023, which compressed P/E multiples and erased a portion of the stimulus-era equity gains. The policy response created its own reversal mechanism — a loop that investors who bought in 2020–2021 and held into 2022 felt painfully.

## The Trading Playbook

Combining the observations from all four countries, a clear framework emerges for how to position around geopolitical crises that involve government policy responses.

### Step 1: Identify the Credibility of the Response

The first question in any geopolitical shock is not "how bad is this?" but "how credible is the government's commitment to respond?"

Credibility markers:
- Central bank announces unlimited purchases (removes tail risk)
- Legislation passes with bipartisan support and a clear dollar amount
- The government's fiscal capacity is unquestioned (investment-grade sovereign, low pre-crisis debt)

When all three are present (US in March 2020): the market is likely pricing too much tail risk. Buy equities.
When credibility is uncertain (EU in March–June 2020): wait for confirmation before full allocation.
When the government's response constrains rather than supports (zero-COVID): price in the constraint duration.

### Step 2: Measure Speed Against Historical Precedent

A commitment in principle without a dollar amount is worth much less than the same commitment with enacted legislation. Track:
- Days from shock event to legislative action
- Size relative to GDP
- Monetary/fiscal coordination (are central bank and government aligned?)

### Step 3: Find the Sector Beneficiaries

Policy responses create predictable sector tilts:
- Massive fiscal stimulus → construction materials, machinery, defense (infrastructure), retail (transfers), healthcare
- Zero rates + QE → long-duration growth stocks (tech, biotech), real estate investment trusts (REITs)
- Supply chain disruption → domestic manufacturing, logistics
- Travel/mobility restriction → video conferencing, e-commerce, gaming; short airlines, hotels

### Step 4: Monitor the Unwinding

Every crisis response creates an unwinding phase. The reversal of zero-COVID (December 2022) was a sharp re-rating opportunity. The reversal of zero-rate QE (Fed hiking from March 2022 to July 2023 by 525 bps) was a sharp derating for high-P/E growth stocks. Position size for the unwind to be at least as large as the crisis entry.
