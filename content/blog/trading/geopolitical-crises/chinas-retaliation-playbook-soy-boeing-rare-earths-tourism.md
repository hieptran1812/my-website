In August 2018, something strange happened to soybean futures. The price had been drifting lower since the spring, but on August 3rd, the day China officially published its 25% tariff list on US agricultural products, the front-month contract dropped through the floor. By late September, soybeans were trading at \$7.80 per bushel — down from \$10.40 just five months earlier, a collapse of 25%. Farm income in Iowa, a state that had voted for Donald Trump by 9 percentage points in 2016, was evaporating in real time.

This was not a coincidence. The soybean tariff was not selected because China's trade economists believed it was the most welfare-maximizing response to US Section 301 tariffs. It was selected because Iowa grows 12% of US soybeans. Illinois grows another 14%. Indiana and Ohio together add 15% more. These four states alone delivered 53 electoral votes in 2016. China's Ministry of Commerce had run the numbers. The pain would fall exactly where it needed to fall to generate maximum political pressure on the US administration.

That is the core of China's retaliation playbook: not maximization, but calibration. Not brute force, but surgical targeting. And the ability to read that playbook ahead of time — to know which sectors will be hit, in what sequence, and why those sectors — is one of the most useful edges available to a trader or investor operating across US agriculture, aerospace, defense, and technology supply chains.

![China retaliation playbook transmission pipeline from US grievance to negotiated pause](/imgs/blogs/chinas-retaliation-playbook-soy-boeing-rare-earths-tourism-1.png)

The figure above captures the full transmission chain. A US political action (tariff, tech restriction, diplomatic provocation) triggers a target-selection process inside the Chinese system. Specific levers — soy, Boeing, rare earths, tourism, consumer boycotts — are deployed in sequenced waves. The intended endpoint is not economic annihilation. It is a US congressional delegation walking into the White House and telling the president that their constituents cannot take any more.

## Foundations: How China's Economic Leverage Works

To understand the retaliation playbook, you need to start with the structural reality: the United States and China have spent three decades building an economic relationship that is simultaneously the largest bilateral trading partnership in history and an increasingly adversarial strategic competition. That duality is not a contradiction. It is the source of all the leverage on both sides.

In 2017, the year before the trade war began in earnest, total US-China bilateral trade in goods was approximately \$660 billion per year. The US exported around \$130 billion to China and imported around \$500 billion from China, producing a trade deficit that would become the Trump administration's central grievance. But the deficit number, however politically salient, was less strategically interesting than the sectoral composition of US exports.

China was buying from the United States: soybeans and other agricultural commodities (\$20+ billion per year), aircraft and aerospace equipment (\$16 billion, dominated by Boeing), semiconductors and electronic components (\$14 billion), chemicals (\$10 billion), and a range of services including education and tourism (another \$50 billion-plus). When you look at that list through a political economy lens rather than a pure economics lens, the structure becomes clear. Agricultural commodities: rural states, Republican senators, farm lobby. Aircraft: defense-industrial complex, bipartisan congressional protection. Semiconductors: already under Huawei scrutiny, double-edged. Education and tourism: urban, Democratic-leaning, politically weaker.

China's retaliation team — operating through the Ministry of Commerce, the National Development and Reform Commission, and the Communist Party's central foreign policy apparatus — did not need a supercomputer to rank the targets. They needed a map of US electoral geography and a list of corporate lobbying expenditures.

The foundational principle at work is what political scientists call **two-level game theory**: the idea that international negotiations are simultaneously domestic political competitions. A trade war is not just a fight between governments. It is each government using international economic pain to move domestic political coalitions inside the other country. China's retaliation is precisely calibrated to that second level — the domestic US political game, not the bilateral economic ledger.

Three structural factors give China unusual leverage in this game:

**Scale asymmetry in specific commodities.** China consumes 60-65% of global soybean exports. That means no alternative buyer pool can absorb what China stops buying. Brazil can increase production — and did — but it takes multiple growing seasons, and even Brazilian soy requires Chinese infrastructure investment to reach ports. The US soy farmer facing a 25% tariff cannot simply call a different customer.

**Processing concentration in critical materials.** China does not merely produce rare earth elements; it processes them. Separation and refinement of rare earth oxides from raw ore is an extraordinarily capital-intensive, environmentally costly process that China built out over decades. Controlling 85% of global processing capacity is fundamentally different from controlling 85% of global mining output. Even if Australia or Canada mines more ore, that ore still has to go to China for refining. This processing bottleneck is where China's leverage truly lives.

**Corporate lobbying feedback loops.** US multinationals — Boeing, Apple, Caterpillar, Goldman Sachs — all have major China exposure. When their China revenue is threatened, they lobby Congress. When they lobby Congress, they create domestic political pressure that flows back to the White House. China understands that threatening corporate America's China business creates an army of well-funded US lobbyists working, in effect, on China's behalf inside the US political system.

These three structural factors together mean China's retaliation playbook has an inherent efficiency that straightforward tit-for-tat tariffs lack. A 25% tariff on US soybeans costs China nothing in net fiscal terms — it collects the tariff revenue. It costs US farmers \$3 billion per year in lost income. And it generates a political chain reaction in swing states that dwarfs what the raw economic numbers would suggest.

## The Calibrated Retaliation Principle

The single most important thing to understand about China's retaliation strategy is what it is not. It is not a maximizing strategy. China does not deploy all of its leverage simultaneously, because doing so would create maximum blowback, trigger maximum US countermeasures, and destroy the information value of keeping additional levers in reserve.

This is what distinguishes China's playbook from, say, Russia's approach to energy weaponization after the Ukraine invasion. Russia's gas cutoff to Europe in 2022 was largely all-or-nothing. It delivered enormous short-term pain but it also accelerated European diversification at a speed that might never have happened otherwise. By 2023, European gas storage was at record levels, LNG import terminals were being fast-tracked, and Russia had permanently lost its most profitable customer. Maximum retaliation destroyed the weapon.

China's approach is the opposite. In a 2010 confrontation with Japan over the Senkaku Islands — a territorial dispute that had nothing to do with trade economics — China curtailed rare earth exports to Japan by roughly 40%. Japan, which was importing nearly 90% of its rare earths from China and had built a high-tech manufacturing sector that depended on them, panicked. Within months, the two governments had reached a de facto understanding, the export curb was quietly unwound, and the Senkaku dispute continued as a managed, low-level irritant rather than an escalating confrontation. China demonstrated what it could do. It did not follow through to full deployment. That restraint is not weakness — it is the preservation of a bargaining chip for the next round.

The 2018-2025 US-China trade war follows a similar logic. Study the sequencing carefully:

- August 2018: 25% tariff on US soybeans (agricultural pain, swing states)
- December 2018: China airlines begin signaling toward Airbus (aerospace lobby alert)
- 2019: Partial re-engagement, Phase One negotiations begin
- January 2020: Phase One deal signed, soy purchases resume, Boeing orders nominally restored
- July 2023: Germanium and gallium export bans (semiconductor signal)
- August 2023: Graphite restrictions (battery supply chain alert)
- April 2025: 125% retaliatory tariff on US goods in response to "Liberation Day" tariffs
- May 2025: 90-day negotiating pause

Each step is a new data point that reveals capability without fully deploying it. The escalation ladder has many more rungs that China has not yet used — full rare earth weaponization, Apple supply chain disruption, Treasury liquidation signaling (even if actual liquidation is self-defeating) — and their value depends on not using them prematurely. A wise poker player does not show their hand until the pot is right.

## The Soybean Weapon

The soybean is the most elegant weapon in China's arsenal because its targeting logic is almost mathematically perfect. Consider the structure:

US soy exports to China peaked at approximately \$14.2 billion in 2015. By 2017, the eve of the trade war, they had dipped slightly to \$12.4 billion but still represented the single largest agricultural export category in US-China trade. China was buying roughly 30-35 million metric tons of US soybeans per year, the majority of it destined for crushing into soyoil and soymeal to feed China's rapidly growing pig and poultry industries.

The August 2018 tariff was applied at 25% on top of existing duties, bringing the total import cost of US soy to a level that made it uncompetitive versus Brazilian or Argentine origin. Chinese crushers, who were rational commercial actors, immediately switched sourcing. US soy shipments to China for the September-December 2018 period fell by approximately 90% versus the prior year. For the full calendar year 2018, US soy exports to China totaled just \$3.1 billion — a 75% collapse from the 2015 peak.

![US soybean exports to China 2015-2024 showing 2018 tariff collapse and Phase One recovery](/imgs/blogs/chinas-retaliation-playbook-soy-boeing-rare-earths-tourism-2.png)

The chart tells the story precisely. The 2018 line falls off a cliff. Then in 2020, following the Phase One deal — in which China committed to purchasing \$200 billion in additional US goods over two years, with agriculture forming a large component — the line recovers sharply to \$14.7 billion. This recovery illustrates both the power and the reversibility of the weapon. China can turn it off. It did turn it off. The US farm lobby, which had been lobbying furiously for trade deal concessions, got its deal. The mechanism worked exactly as designed.

The political targeting is visible at the county level. Soybean production in the United States is concentrated in what agronomists call the "Corn Belt" — a band running from Ohio westward through Indiana, Illinois, Iowa, Minnesota, and parts of Missouri. This corridor maps almost perfectly onto the states that decided the 2016 election and would again prove competitive in 2020 and 2024. Iowa alone produces roughly 550 million bushels of soybeans per year. A 25% price decline translates directly into farm income losses measured in hundreds of millions of dollars, enough to flip county-level voting patterns in a close election.

The Farm Bureau, the American Soybean Association, and state agriculture departments all immediately recognized the threat and mobilized politically. The Trump administration's response — the \$28 billion Market Facilitation Program, a direct subsidy paid to farmers affected by the tariffs — acknowledged the political reality even as the administration maintained that the tariffs were the right policy. The government was simultaneously imposing the pain and paying to cushion it.

China's position during all of this: zero net fiscal cost. The tariff revenue collected on the small residual volume of US soy that still entered under tariff was pure government income. The political costs fell entirely on the US side. This is what makes agricultural retaliation so attractive from a game-theory perspective — it is close to a dominant strategy for China in the first several rounds of escalation.

#### Worked example:

**Soybean Political Targeting Efficiency**

China imposed a 25% tariff on US soybeans in August 2018. US exports to China fell from \$12.4 billion (2017) to \$3.1 billion (2018), a revenue hit of \$9.3 billion in one year. Assuming a normal soy price of \$10.00/bushel and a 25% tariff-driven drop to \$7.50, US farmer income per bushel declined by \$2.50. On 550 million Iowa-alone bushels per year, that is a \$1.375 billion annual income hit for Iowa alone.

These farmers are distributed across Iowa (6 electoral votes, voted Trump +9 in 2016), Illinois (19 EV, voted Clinton +17 but major soy production), Indiana (11 EV, voted Trump +19), and Ohio (17 EV, voted Trump +8). Total: 53 electoral votes in states that are either swing or needed for a Republican majority.

The Trump administration responded with a \$28 billion Market Facilitation Program over 2018-2019 to compensate affected farmers. Cost per electoral vote targeted: \$28 billion / 53 = **\$528 million per electoral vote.**

China's net fiscal cost for creating this pressure: effectively zero. It collected tariff revenue on the small residual US soy volume and shifted bulk purchases to Brazil at similar prices. In terms of geopolitical leverage efficiency, a \$0 cost that forces the opposing government to spend \$28 billion in compensatory subsidies is one of the most extraordinary returns on a policy tool in modern trade history.

## The Boeing Cancellation

If soybeans represent China's most efficient weapon, Boeing represents its most politically resonant one. The aerospace lobby — Aerospace Industries Association, Boeing's own government relations apparatus, the defense-industrial overlap — is among the most powerful in Washington. And Boeing's China exposure was, pre-trade war, genuinely enormous.

China was Boeing's single largest country market. By volume, Chinese airlines were taking delivery of approximately 25% of all Boeing commercial aircraft produced in a typical year — roughly 100+ aircraft annually. Looking forward to the 2018-2028 decade, Boeing's own market outlook projected China would need 7,400+ new aircraft, worth approximately \$1.1 trillion at list prices. That projection was embedded in Boeing's stock price, in its production planning, in its supplier contracts, and in the political commitments of senators from the 24 states where Boeing or its major suppliers employ significant workforces.

China's signal came in waves, not as a single dramatic announcement. First, Chinese state-owned media began running commentary questioning the value of Boeing's safety record (this would later blend with legitimate 737 MAX concerns). Then Air China, China Eastern, and China Southern — all three major state airlines — began making public statements about diversifying their fleets toward Airbus. In 2018 and 2019, orders for Airbus A320neo and A321neo aircraft from Chinese carriers exceeded 300 planes at list prices exceeding \$37 billion. Meanwhile, new Boeing orders from Chinese carriers effectively dried up.

The 737 MAX grounding, which began in March 2019 following the Ethiopian Airlines crash, gave China a legitimate safety cover to maintain this posture independently of the trade war. China was the first country to ground the MAX, doing so within hours of the crash — faster than the FAA, which grounded it two days later under enormous pressure. Whether that speed reflected genuine safety concern or political calculation is almost impossible to disentangle, which is exactly the kind of ambiguity China prefers.

By end of 2019, Boeing's order backlog from China had declined from 300+ planes to near zero. The revenue implications were severe: in a normal year, 100 China deliveries at an average transaction price of \$120 million per aircraft represents \$12 billion in revenue. At 25 actual deliveries in 2019 versus an expected 100+, the shortfall was roughly \$9 billion for that year alone. Boeing stock, which had traded at \$440 in early March 2019 before the MAX crisis, fell below \$200 by late 2019 and below \$90 in the March 2020 COVID trough.

The political feedback loop worked as intended. Senators from South Carolina (Boeing's 787 plant in North Charleston employs 7,000), Washington state (Renton and Everett facilities, 50,000+ jobs), and Texas (supplier concentration) all found themselves in the uncomfortable position of supporting tariff hawks while watching their constituents' largest employer lose its biggest customer. That discomfort was precisely the signal Beijing wanted to send to the Washington political establishment.

The linkage between Boeing's commercial fortunes and China's political leverage is also apparent in the CAAC's regulatory behavior during the trade war. The Civil Aviation Administration of China refused to recertify the 737 MAX for Chinese airspace until December 2023 — more than two years after the FAA, EASA, Transport Canada, and virtually every other major aviation regulator had cleared the aircraft. The official explanation was that the CAAC was conducting its own independent safety review. The timeline, however, aligned almost exactly with the trade war escalation and de-escalation cycle. As the US-China relationship thawed and bilateral negotiations resumed in 2022-2023, the CAAC's MAX review accelerated. Regulators denied the connection, but no sophisticated market participant believed the timeline was purely technical.

This illustrates a broader principle about how China weaponizes regulatory discretion. Import approvals, safety certifications, customs clearance procedures, environmental compliance reviews — all of these can be slowed or accelerated as informal pressure tools without triggering formal WTO challenges, because they are nominally administrative procedures rather than trade barriers. Qualcomm spent years trying to get Chinese merger approval for its NXP acquisition (ultimately abandoned in 2018 when MOFCOM failed to approve). Intel's proposed acquisition of Tower Semiconductor was abandoned in 2023 after SAMR (China's antitrust regulator) let the 12-month review period expire without ruling. These are not coincidences. They are regulatory leverage deployed at precisely calibrated moments.

#### Worked example:

**Boeing Order Cancellation Value**

Chinese airline fleet orders 2018-2019 that went to Airbus rather than Boeing: approximately 290 aircraft. List price per A320neo: \$110 million. Total list value redirected from Boeing to Airbus: **\$31.9 billion.**

Boeing's actual China deliveries in 2019: approximately 25 aircraft (of which several were pre-MAX-crisis completions). Normal expected run rate: 100+ per year. Shortfall: 75 planes. Average Boeing commercial transaction price (list discount typical at 50%): approximately \$60 million net revenue per plane. Revenue shortfall from China alone in 2019: **75 x \$60M = \$4.5 billion.**

Boeing's total 2019 revenue was \$76 billion, down from \$101 billion in 2018, with the shortfall attributable to both MAX grounding and China exposure. The Chinese message to US senators was arithmetically clear: \$4.5 billion in annual revenue from one country, spread across 24 states, represents about 6,500 direct manufacturing jobs at Boeing's average compensation levels. Those jobs vote. Those jobs call their representatives.

The multiplier effect through Boeing's supply chain amplifies this further. Boeing's direct employment of 150,000 is supported by a supplier base of approximately 1 million additional jobs at 13,000+ suppliers across all 50 states. Spirit AeroSystems in Kansas builds the 737 fuselage. Ducommun in California and Louisiana makes structural components. Woodward in Colorado makes engine control systems. Parker Hannifin in Ohio makes hydraulic systems. Each of these companies has its own congressional relationships. When Boeing loses its China market, the political pressure radiating through the supply chain extends well beyond the immediate Boeing workforce — it reaches the congressional districts of every major aerospace supplier in America.

## The Rare Earth Leverage

Rare earths are where China's leverage transitions from political pain to strategic existential threat. And understanding the rare earth weapon requires understanding the difference between mining and processing.

The term "rare earth elements" covers 17 metallic elements — the 15 lanthanides plus scandium and yttrium — that are not actually rare in the earth's crust. What makes them strategically critical is that they are rarely found in economically concentrated deposits, extracting and refining them is environmentally destructive, and building the processing capacity takes decades and billions of dollars. China spent those decades and those billions. The Mountain Pass mine in California, once the world's largest rare earth operation, shut down in 2002 partly because it could not compete with Chinese pricing. China had built out processing capacity so efficiently — through a combination of state subsidy, lax environmental enforcement, and scale — that the global market essentially shifted to China.

The result by the late 2010s: China controlled approximately 85% of global rare earth processing capacity. The mining share is lower — perhaps 60% — because Australia, Canada, and the United States have significant deposits. But mining raw ore is only the first step. Separating the ore into individual rare earth oxides, then refining those oxides into metal alloys, requires processing technology and infrastructure that exists almost entirely in China. Even ore mined in Australia by Lynas Rare Earths, the largest non-Chinese rare earth producer, was historically shipped to China for processing (Lynas has since built a separation plant in Malaysia partly to avoid this dependency).

![China rare earth processing dominance supply chain from mines to US defense and EV sectors](/imgs/blogs/chinas-retaliation-playbook-soy-boeing-rare-earths-tourism-5.png)

The applications are what make this leverage militarily significant, not just economically painful. Consider the specific materials and their uses:

**Neodymium and praseodymium (NdPr):** Used in permanent magnets for electric motors in EVs, wind turbine generators, and — critically — the guidance systems of precision-guided munitions. An F-35 fighter jet contains approximately 920 pounds of rare earth materials. Tomahawk cruise missiles use neodymium magnets in their guidance actuators.

**Germanium:** Used in infrared optical systems (night-vision equipment, thermal imaging), fiber optic cables, and certain solar cells. The M1A1 Abrams tank's thermal imaging system depends on germanium lenses. Each Abrams requires approximately 1.2 kilograms of germanium in its optics suite.

**Gallium:** Essential substrate for gallium arsenide (GaAs) and gallium nitride (GaN) semiconductors used in radar systems, electronic warfare equipment, 5G infrastructure, and high-power amplifiers for military communications.

**Graphite:** The dominant anode material in lithium-ion batteries. China processes approximately 90% of global natural graphite into battery-grade material. Every EV battery — Tesla, BYD, GM's Ultium packs — contains Chinese-processed graphite in its anode unless a company has specifically sourced from the very small non-Chinese supply available.

In July 2023, China announced export controls on gallium and germanium, requiring export licenses that the Ministry of Commerce would review case-by-case. This was not a ban — but it created immediate uncertainty. Spot prices for germanium jumped from approximately \$1,200 per kilogram to over \$2,100 per kilogram within three months, a 75% increase. Spot gallium rose from roughly \$250 per kilogram to \$350 per kilogram. In August 2023, graphite export restrictions followed, requiring approval for all graphite products going to battery manufacturers.

These were signal shots, not full deployment. China demonstrated it could disrupt the supply chain. It did not actually choke it. The licenses were being granted — slowly and unpredictably — but granted. That unpredictability was itself a form of leverage, forcing US and European battery manufacturers to scramble for alternative sourcing while paying premium prices.

![China critical mineral processing share versus US and rest of world across key strategic materials](/imgs/blogs/chinas-retaliation-playbook-soy-boeing-rare-earths-tourism-6.png)

The above chart shows the brutal reality of processing concentration. For rare earths, graphite, manganese, and gallium, China's share exceeds 80%. The United States, with all its mining potential, processes almost nothing domestically for these materials at scale. Closing that gap requires processing plants that take 5-10 years to permit, build, and ramp to commercial scale. That timeline is China's runway — the window during which its leverage remains unchallenged.

#### Worked example:

**Rare Earth Price Shock on US Defense Procurement**

Germanium spot price before China's July 2023 export control announcement: \$1,200 per kilogram.
Germanium spot price three months later: \$2,100 per kilogram.
Price increase: +\$900 per kilogram, or +75%.

Global germanium production: approximately 130 metric tons per year.
China's share: 60% = 78 metric tons.
US share: roughly 10% = 13 metric tons (primarily from zinc smelter byproducts).

One M1A1 Abrams tank uses approximately 1.2 kg of germanium in its thermal imaging optics.
US Army procurement of Abrams variants (replacement and export): approximately 200 tanks per year.
Annual germanium consumption for this program: 200 x 1.2 = 240 kg.

At pre-restriction price (\$1,200/kg): 240 kg x \$1,200 = **\$288,000 per year** in germanium costs for the tank program.
At post-restriction price (\$2,100/kg): 240 kg x \$2,100 = **\$504,000 per year**.
Additional annual cost: \$216,000 — a minor direct line item, but the broader principle scales.

Apply the same price increase across all 920 pounds (417 kg) of rare earth materials in each F-35:
If the relevant rare earth prices rise 50% on average, and if an F-35 uses \$100,000 worth of rare earths at baseline, a 50% price spike adds \$50,000 per aircraft.
US plans called for 2,443 total F-35s; at \$50,000 per airframe, that is **\$122 million in additional procurement cost** from one rare earth price move — before any supply disruption.

The 2010 Senkaku precedent set the template. Japan caved. The US has begun building domestic rare earth capacity through programs like the Defense Production Act investments and MP Materials' Mountain Pass restart — but at current pace, meaningful processing independence is 5-10 years away.

The matrix reveals a clear preference ordering. Agriculture (soy) and rare earths score highest on political pain per unit of Chinese cost — both inflict acute targeted damage on politically sensitive US constituencies while the cost to China is either zero (tariff revenue) or trivially small (rare earth processing monopoly rents). Consumer boycotts and tourism restrictions are efficient but limited in scale. Boeing cancellations are highly effective but come with a medium-cost penalty since Airbus is slightly more expensive and has capacity constraints. Treasury sales sit at the bottom — extremely costly to China, very limited pain to the US given Federal Reserve offset capacity, and carrying extreme escalation risk. This is why China uses Treasury threat rhetoric but has never meaningfully accelerated its reduction program in a crisis.

## Consumer Boycotts as a Tool

Consumer boycotts are the one weapon in China's arsenal that the government can deploy with partial deniability. State media amplifies grievances; social media users organize boycotts; the government claims it is a spontaneous expression of popular sentiment. The deniability is thin, but it exists.

The 2021 H&M-Xinjiang cotton controversy illustrates the mechanism. H&M had previously issued a statement expressing concern about forced labor allegations in Xinjiang cotton production and noting it would source cotton from elsewhere. When that statement resurfaced on Chinese social media in March 2021, state broadcaster CCTV ran segments amplifying the controversy. Within 48 hours, H&M products had been delisted from major Chinese e-commerce platforms including Tmall and JD.com. H&M's flagship stores in major cities saw sharp foot traffic declines. The company's Greater China revenue fell approximately 25% in the following quarter.

Nike faced similar pressure for the same Xinjiang-related sourcing statements. Apple faced more subtle pressure — the Chinese government imposed procurement restrictions on iPhones for government and state enterprise employees in 2023, a move that was not publicly announced as a ban but spread rapidly through internal directives. Apple's China market share dipped from approximately 17% to 15% in the subsequent quarters, a seemingly small decline that represents hundreds of millions of dollars in lost revenue for a market generating over \$70 billion per year for Apple.

The "guochao" (national trend) movement provides the cultural scaffolding for these boycotts. Domestic brands — Huawei in phones, Li-Ning in sportswear, BYD in vehicles, Moutai in luxury spirits — have been actively repositioned as aspirational choices for younger Chinese consumers. When a foreign company runs afoul of Chinese political sensitivities, the alternative is readily available. This reduces the cost to Chinese consumers of boycotting and increases the cost to foreign companies of defiance.

For traders, consumer boycotts generate direct sector-specific risks: a sudden restriction on Apple products affects Apple's revenue model, its supplier chain (Foxconn, TSMC Taiwan exposure, glass and semiconductor suppliers), and the broader narrative around US tech exposure to China market concentration risk. The boycott risk is particularly acute for consumer discretionary brands where Chinese consumers have genuine domestic alternatives — luxury, sportswear, fast food, electric vehicles.

## The Tourism and Education Weapon

Chinese outbound tourism and education represent a \$50+ billion annual transfer to the US economy that most Americans do not recognize as trade. They should.

Pre-pandemic Chinese tourism to the United States generated approximately \$35 billion per year in visitor spending — the largest single source of inbound tourism revenue. Chinese tourists to the US stayed longer and spent more per trip than visitors from any other country. They were not budget backpackers. They were middle-class families, business travelers, and luxury shoppers who booked high-end hotels, made significant retail purchases, and visited multiple cities. The communities that felt this spending most directly — Las Vegas, New York City luxury retail, Napa Valley wine country — are not the rural swing districts where soy and manufacturing employment dominate. But the hospitality and tourism industries lobby Congress, and their interests matter.

The 370,000+ Chinese students enrolled in US universities in 2019 generated approximately \$15 billion in tuition and living expenses — critical revenue for universities facing declining domestic enrollment and constrained public funding. The American Council on Education estimated that Chinese student tuition represented 5-10% of total revenue at many flagship state universities. For institutions like the University of Illinois, Michigan State, and Purdue, Chinese enrollment was running at 10,000+ students per year, generating hundreds of millions in annual revenue.

When US-China tensions escalated in 2019, the Chinese government issued student visa advisory warnings suggesting Chinese students faced increased scrutiny and potential visa denial at US borders. Simultaneously, US visa processing times for Chinese applicants increased following Trump administration security reviews. The result: Chinese applications to US universities fell approximately 23% in the 2019-2020 cycle versus the prior year. Some of this was noise — the absolute volumes remained large — but the trend line was clear, and university administrators noticed.

The leverage works through the same lobbying feedback loop as agriculture and aerospace. University presidents, whose institutions depend on Chinese tuition revenue, lobby their senators against policies that would further chill US-China educational exchange. The coalition of interests pushing against escalation is broader than many realize — it includes research universities, luxury hotels, Napa wine producers, and tourism boards in cities with significant Chinese visitor traffic.

Post-COVID, Chinese tourism to the US has not recovered to pre-pandemic levels. Chinese student enrollment began declining from its 2019 peak as both US restrictions and Chinese student decisions shifted. Each year this continues, the lobbying coalition that once argued for stable US-China relations atrophies a little more. That erosion is a long-run cost of the trade war that is difficult to price into markets but very real in the political economy.

## The Hostage Logic of Calibrated Retaliation

Given this formidable arsenal — agriculture, aerospace, rare earths, consumer boycotts, tourism and education — the obvious question is why China does not simply deploy all of it at once. The answer is a combination of self-interest and strategic patience that makes China's leverage durable rather than fragile.

**Reason 1: China still needs US technology.** Despite enormous progress in domestic semiconductor design (Huawei's Kirin chips, SMIC's manufacturing advances), China remains critically dependent on US semiconductor technology, EDA software (electronic design automation — Synopsys, Cadence, Mentor Graphics all have de facto monopolies), and advanced manufacturing equipment. Maximizing retaliation would accelerate US technology decoupling, cutting off Chinese companies from the software and hardware they still need to design and manufacture competitive chips. The pain of that decoupling for China's technology sector would exceed any gains from maximum retaliation pressure.

**Reason 2: The Treasury paradox.** China holds approximately \$1 trillion in US Treasury securities, down from a peak of \$1.3 trillion in 2013 but still the second-largest foreign holding after Japan. This position is both a source of leverage (the threat of selling) and a constraint (the cost of selling). If China were to liquidate \$200 billion in Treasuries over a short period, it would push yields higher — perhaps 50-100 basis points at the 10-year tenor — which would reduce the price of its remaining holdings by roughly 5-8%. On a \$1 trillion book, that self-inflicted mark-to-market loss is \$50-80 billion. The "nuclear option" of Treasury dumping is, in game theory terms, not credible precisely because it is mutually assured destruction. China knows this. The market generally knows this. But China can use the threat implicitly to keep the US from pushing too hard.

**Reason 3: Dollar trade finance dependency.** Chinese companies — including state-owned enterprises — rely on dollar-denominated trade finance for a significant portion of their international commerce. Commodities are priced in dollars; shipping contracts are settled in dollars; letter-of-credit markets operate in dollars. Maximizing confrontation with the United States risks Chinese companies' access to correspondent banking relationships with US financial institutions, which underpin the dollar trade finance system. This is why SWIFT exclusion (used against Russia) has never been seriously floated for China — the disruption would be as damaging to Chinese trade as to anyone else.

**Reason 4: Escalation invites maximum response.** The most important self-limiting factor is strategic: going all-in invites the other side's all-in response. If China were to deploy rare earth export controls at their maximum — banning all gallium, germanium, graphite, and rare earth compound exports to the United States — it would trigger exactly the emergency industrial policy response that China does not want to see: a Manhattan Project-scale US domestic rare earth processing program, fast-tracked permitting for Mountain Pass and other deposits, emergency alliance-building with Australia and Canada for processing capacity, and defense procurement set-asides for non-Chinese sources. China's leverage depends on the US not having moved aggressively to substitute. Deploying the weapon fully accelerates the substitution.

These four constraints together explain why China's retaliation announcements are better understood as ceilings than floors. When China announces a new restriction, the market should ask: what is the actual deployment level relative to the theoretical maximum? Almost always, it is well short of maximum. The escalation ladder has more rungs. China is signaling, not deploying.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "China is hurting itself equally."**

This is the most common misreading. When China imposes a 25% tariff on US soybeans, it does not hurt China equally. China's government earns the tariff revenue. Chinese crushing companies pay a higher price for soy but pass the cost to Chinese pork and poultry producers, and ultimately to Chinese consumers through slightly higher food prices. The pass-through is real but diffuse — spread across 1.4 billion people, the impact per household is negligible. The impact on 50,000 Iowa soy farmers is concentrated and politically acute. Concentrated pain on the US side, diffuse inconvenience on the Chinese side: that asymmetry is the point.

**Misconception 2: "Rare earth restrictions will fail because the market will diversify."**

True in the long run. False in the relevant short run. Building a rare earth processing plant — not a mine, a chemical separation and refining facility — takes 5-8 years from groundbreaking to commercial production, even with expedited permitting. The US government passed the Inflation Reduction Act in 2022 with significant critical mineral incentives, but the processing facilities those incentives are meant to build are not operational at scale as of 2026. China's leverage window is the next 5-8 years, during which the US remains structurally dependent. After that window, the leverage will diminish. But that is cold comfort for defense procurement planners who need germanium optics for tanks being ordered now.

**Misconception 3: "China will dump Treasuries."**

This is a perennial market scare that has never materialized, for good reason: the math does not work. As the worked example below demonstrates, selling Treasuries is self-defeating. China's holdings have actually been declining gradually since 2013 — partly as a deliberate diversification into gold, euros, and other reserve assets — but the sell-off has been orderly and slow precisely because rapid selling would destroy the value of the remaining holdings. The Treasury dump threat is a Potemkin weapon: intimidating from a distance, hollow on inspection.

**Misconception 4: "Consumer boycotts are purely grassroots."**

The organic element is real — Chinese consumers do have genuine nationalist sentiments that can be mobilized. But the amplification is not organic. CCTV, People's Daily, and state-directed social media algorithms determine which foreign companies' controversies become national talking points and which fade. A foreign company that runs afoul of Xinjiang cotton concerns is treated very differently from a foreign company that runs afoul of, say, labor practices unrelated to Chinese political sensitivities. The government selects which boycotts to amplify based on diplomatic utility. Traders should monitor Chinese state media for signals of which foreign companies are being targeted, as the amplification itself is a political signal.

## How It Shows Up in Real Markets

The practical market manifestations of China's retaliation playbook are spread across multiple asset classes and sectors. Understanding the transmission helps traders position before the full market reaction.

**Agricultural futures:** Soybean futures (CBOT: ZS) are the most direct instrument. Chicago soy prices are driven by US weather, Brazilian crop cycles, and the US-China trade relationship. When trade tensions rise, soy falls — not because production changes, but because the expected buyer (China) may reduce purchases. Watch the spread between US Gulf soy prices and Brazilian soy prices (FOB Santos). When the US-Brazil basis widens significantly, it signals China is diverting toward Brazil and the US market is losing the China bid. Corn (ZC) and wheat (ZW) are secondary instruments — China occasionally threatens to buy less US corn or wheat, but the leverage is smaller because China has more domestic production capacity.

**Boeing (BA) equity:** Boeing is the most direct equity expression of China aerospace tension. The stock has a documented pattern of underperforming when US-China relations deteriorate. Watch Chinese airline announcements — any Airbus orders that reference fleet replacement of Boeing positions should be read as a political signal as much as a commercial decision. The 737 MAX reapproval process in China (the CAAC did not recluster it until December 2023, four years after the FAA and EASA cleared it) was visibly linked to trade war dynamics.

**Rare earth-exposed equities:** MP Materials (MP), the operator of Mountain Pass, and Lynas Rare Earths (LYC in Australia) are the primary pure-play rare earth equities. Both trade up on Chinese export control news as markets expect demand for non-Chinese supply to increase. Downstream, look at Defense Contractor exposure: Raytheon (RTX), L3Harris (LHX), and Northrop Grumman (NOC) all have materials procurement that includes rare earths. The cost impact at the corporate level is manageable, but sentiment around supply chain security weighs on contracts and program timelines.

**Technology supply chains:** Apple (AAPL) has approximately 18% of revenue from China and a manufacturing supply chain that runs almost entirely through Chinese contractors (Foxconn/Hon Hai, Pegatron). Any escalation that targets consumer electronics — government procurement bans, social media boycott amplification, App Store restrictions — flows directly to Apple's earnings guidance. Watch Chinese state media for Apple-specific mentions. A January 2023 Bloomberg report that Chinese government workers were being told to stop using iPhones on the job was the first public signal of a restriction that Apple had not pre-disclosed.

**Treasury market:** China's gradual reduction of US Treasury holdings since 2013 has not caused the yield spike many predicted, because the global demand for Treasuries from other central banks and institutional investors has more than offset China's selling. But sudden acceleration of Chinese selling — even signaled rather than actual — could cause a short-term yield spike in the 10-30 basis point range. For bond traders, watch TIC data (Treasury International Capital) releases for China's holdings. A month-over-month decline of more than \$20 billion gets attention; a \$50+ billion decline in a single month would be market-moving.

**CNH (offshore renminbi):** China's currency is itself a tool of retaliation signaling. When tensions escalate, the PBOC sometimes allows CNH to depreciate, signaling that China is willing to accept currency weakness as part of a trade war dynamic. A CNH weakening below 7.30 vs. USD historically correlates with escalation phases; a move toward 7.00 correlates with de-escalation or deal signals. The currency move is also self-limiting — excessive CNH depreciation triggers capital outflow pressure from Chinese residents and concerns about financial stability.

![China retaliation escalation timeline from 2018 to 2025 showing calibrated deployment steps](/imgs/blogs/chinas-retaliation-playbook-soy-boeing-rare-earths-tourism-4.png)

The timeline makes the sequencing visible. Each escalation step is discrete, reversible at the right price, and spaced to allow the political impact to register before the next lever is pulled. The Phase One deal in January 2020 illustrates the endpoint design: both sides announced a "deal," China committed to purchase targets it had already been meeting, US tariffs were partly reduced, and both governments declared victory. The retaliation machinery was paused. It was not dismantled.

## How to Trade It — The Playbook

A practical framework for trading around China's retaliation playbook operates on three time horizons: the first-order response (hours to days), the second-order repositioning (weeks to months), and the structural shift (years).

**First-Order Response — Hours to Days**

When China announces a new retaliatory measure, the immediate market move is often overdone in the direction of the announcement. In August 2018, soy futures fell sharply on the tariff news — but then partially recovered as traders absorbed the fact that the Phase One deal was already being discussed. The announcement of germanium and gallium restrictions in July 2023 caused a spike in germanium spot prices, but physical supply did not actually dry up immediately because licenses were still being granted. The rule: sell the announcement, but scale size because the first-day move often overshoots the fundamental impact.

For equities, the inverse applies: Boeing falls on China trade war news, creating an entry opportunity if you believe a deal will eventually be struck. Agricultural names (ADM, Bunge) sell off on soy restriction news, but the US government farm subsidy backstop limits the downside to farm income. The volatility in CBOT soy is the cleanest expression of the trade war in commodity markets — it is liquid, direct, and the China bid is a known fundamental driver.

**Second-Order Repositioning — Weeks to Months**

The more durable trade is identifying supply chain rerouting that benefits third-country companies. The Brazil soy trade in 2018-2019 is the textbook case: while US soy crashed, Brazil's Embrapa (state agricultural research) and Brazilian trading companies (Cargill's Brazil operations, Louis Dreyfus, Amaggi) all benefited. The infrastructure play — Brazilian port expansion, inland grain handling capacity — was visible in commodity company earnings.

For rare earths, the 2023 germanium restriction created a multi-month opportunity in alternative processing companies and recyclers. Umicore (UMICY), the Belgian precious and specialty metals recycler, processes germanium from recycled electronics and is a legitimate non-Chinese source. Indium Corporation and 5N Plus (VNP in Canada) saw increased interest. None of these are large-cap liquid names, but they express the thesis accurately.

**Structural Shift — Years**

The decade-long structural trade created by China's retaliation playbook is supply chain diversification. Every US company with China exposure is, at varying speeds, building a "China plus one" or "China minus one" strategy. This creates multi-year capex cycles in Mexico, Vietnam, India, and Indonesia — all beneficiaries of supply chain relocation. The Mexican peso (MXN), Vietnamese dong (VND), and Indian rupee (INR) have structural tailwinds from near-shoring. EWZ (Brazil ETF), VNM (Vietnam ETF), and INDA (India ETF) all express this structural theme.

For the US domestically, the Inflation Reduction Act and CHIPS Act have seeded a domestic critical minerals and semiconductor manufacturing buildout whose beneficiaries are identifiable: MP Materials, Albemarle (lithium), Livent (now part of Arcadium Lithium), and the domestic semiconductor fabs being built in Arizona (TSMC) and Ohio (Intel). These are 5-10 year capex plays, not quarter-by-quarter trades, but the China retaliation playbook is directly why the political will to fund them exists.

#### Worked example:

**Treasury Dump Threat Calculation**

China holds approximately \$860 billion in US Treasury securities as of late 2025 (declining from the ~\$1.1 trillion peak in 2021).

Scenario: China announces it will sell \$200 billion over 90 days.

Estimated yield impact: Large foreign selling programs have historically moved 10-year yields by roughly 25-50 basis points per \$100 billion in sudden supply. A \$200 billion sell program would likely push 10-year yields up 40-80 basis points.

Call it 60 basis points at the 10-year. Duration of a 10-year bond is approximately 9 years. Price impact = -9 x 0.60% = **-5.4%** on outstanding 10-year Treasuries.

China's remaining \$660 billion in holdings (after the \$200B sold) would lose 5.4% x \$660B = **\$35.6 billion** in mark-to-market value.

But China would also get a bad price on the \$200 billion it sold — if it pushes yields 60bp, it sells at the depressed price. The price concession on the sell itself: roughly 3% x \$200B = \$6 billion lost on execution.

**Total self-harm from Treasury dump: approximately \$42 billion**, plus permanent loss of US Treasury market access as a reserve asset manager, plus retaliatory measures that would likely include freezing Chinese government accounts in US correspondent banks.

For \$42 billion in self-inflicted losses, China would achieve a modest and temporary yield spike that the Fed could easily offset through emergency QE. The "nuclear option" fails its own cost-benefit test. This is why it has never happened despite being threatened repeatedly. The market knows this, which is why US Treasury yields have not durably moved on China-selling news.

## The Before and After: How the Trade War Restructured the Relationship

The trade war that began in 2018 and continues in evolved form into 2025 has permanently shifted the structural parameters of US-China economic relations. It is worth stepping back to see the full before-and-after.

![US-China trade relationship before and after the trade war showing structural shift from interdependence to decoupling](/imgs/blogs/chinas-retaliation-playbook-soy-boeing-rare-earths-tourism-7.png)

The transformation is visible across every dimension. Total bilateral trade declined from roughly \$660 billion in 2017 to around \$575 billion by 2025 — but more importantly, the composition changed. US imports from China declined as companies moved some manufacturing to Vietnam, Mexico, and India. US exports to China became more volatile and politically contingent, no longer a reliable commercial relationship.

Mexico surpassed China as the largest source of US goods imports in 2023 for the first time since China joined the WTO in 2001. That structural shift reflects years of near-shoring decisions made in response to the trade war's unpredictability. Vietnamese exports to the United States roughly doubled between 2018 and 2024. India's electronics exports began growing. The retaliation playbook did not just hurt the targeted sectors — it reorganized global supply chains.

For China, the rebalancing has also involved costs. Its export machine increasingly must find non-US markets. Its technology sector faces a more determined US export control regime. Its manufacturing firms face new tariffs not just from the US but increasingly from the EU and other trading partners worried about Chinese export dumping. The calibrated retaliation strategy succeeded in extracting the Phase One deal and maintaining the basic structure of bilateral trade — but it did not prevent the broader decoupling trend that structural US-China competition was always going to produce.

China's retaliation playbook is not improvised. It is the product of decades of careful study of US political economy, electoral geography, and corporate lobbying structure. The targeting of Iowa soy rather than California tech; the Boeing signal rather than the Caterpillar signal; the germanium restriction rather than the full rare earth weaponization — each choice reflects a deliberate calculation about where the pain-to-cost ratio is highest.

For traders and investors, the value of understanding this playbook lies not in predicting which lever China will pull next — that depends on US actions that are themselves unpredictable. The value lies in mapping the levers in advance, knowing their likely market impact, and positioning to benefit from the signal rather than react in the noise. When China announces an agricultural tariff, the soy complex is already moving. When China signals rare earth restrictions, the processing company equities are already responding. The market that reads the playbook before the announcement is the market that captures the move, not chases it.

The calibrated retaliation principle is the most important insight: China always has more levers in reserve than it has deployed. Every announcement is a ceiling. Every deal is a pause, not a resolution. The trade war is a managed competition with periodic de-escalation phases — and the ability to distinguish an escalation ceiling from a new floor is the edge that the playbook gives you.
