> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The Russia-Ukraine invasion of February 24, 2022 was the most significant geopolitical market shock since 9/11, and the first 72 hours taught us more about how markets process war than any textbook.
>
> - Markets only partially priced the invasion before it happened — equities were down 5-6% from January highs but options implied only a ~30% probability of full invasion, so the shock still landed hard.
> - Three distinct channels transmitted the shock: a commodity supply shock (oil +6%, wheat +13% overnight), a financial sanctions channel (SWIFT exclusion took 6 days to announce), and a risk-off sentiment channel (VIX 28 to 34, gold bid, EUR/USD lower).
> - The first 72 hours were mostly right on direction but badly wrong on duration — markets initially priced a short war and started recovering within days; the real repricing came months later when gas flows were cut and the energy regime changed permanently.
> - Russia had spent years building a "fortress balance sheet" — \$640 billion in reserves, a de-dollarized central bank — that bought time but ultimately could not prevent the ruble from crashing 47% before capital controls forced a bizarre V-shaped recovery.
> - The one trade that was right on both direction and duration: European defense stocks, where Rheinmetall went from \$90 to over \$140 in six weeks as the NATO 2% GDP spending commitment became real.
> - The key lesson: in geopolitical shocks, the commodity channel moves fastest (hours), the sentiment channel is volatile and often reverses, and the sanctions/regime-change channel is the slowest but the most permanent repricing.

On the evening of February 23, 2022, Brent crude oil was trading at \$96.40 per barrel. By the time European markets opened on the morning of February 24, it was above \$102. By March, it would touch \$120. By June, European natural gas — the resource that would define the economic fallout — would reach \$310 per megawatt-hour, a number that seemed almost surreal just months earlier.

The invasion of Ukraine by Russia on February 24, 2022 was the most market-relevant geopolitical event since the September 11 attacks. It was the first large-scale conventional war in Europe in decades. It involved a major energy exporter attacking one of the world's largest grain producers. And it triggered the most sweeping Western sanctions regime ever assembled against a major economy. Every one of those facts moved a different set of markets, at a different speed, and for different durations.

This post is a real-time case study of those first 72 hours — what moved, when, why, and crucially, what markets got right and wrong. The lessons reach well beyond Ukraine: they reveal how markets process geopolitical shocks in general, which channels move fastest, and why the "buy the invasion" trade that worked in the first week completely failed to anticipate what came three months later.

![Brent crude spiked from 96 to 102 overnight on Feb 24 invasion day then climbed to 120 by June 2022](/imgs/blogs/russia-invades-ukraine-the-first-72-hours-in-markets-1.png)

The chart above is the starting point: the commodity channel moving in real time. A price that had spent weeks in the \$85–96 range jumped six percent in a single overnight session before any market opened. From that moment, the repricing cascade was underway.

## Foundations: Understanding Geopolitical Market Shocks

Before we walk through the 72-hour sequence, we need to build the conceptual toolkit. Markets are not a weather system that passively observes events — they are a collective probability-weighting machine, and understanding how geopolitical shocks move them requires understanding what markets were already pricing, what they expected, and what the "surprise" component of the invasion actually was.

### What a market "price" means during a crisis

A stock price or commodity price at any moment reflects the weighted average of every investor's expectation about the future. Before the invasion, Brent crude at \$96 was not saying "nothing bad will happen." It was saying "we have already weighted the probability of various scenarios — including a Russian invasion — and this is the average expected value." When the invasion actually happened, the price did not jump because investors were surprised something could happen. It jumped because the probability distribution shifted: the bad scenario became certain, and investors had not priced it at 100%.

This distinction matters enormously. A market that was "priced for no invasion" would have fallen much further than a market that was "priced for a 30% chance of invasion." The size of the initial move tells you something about pre-invasion pricing.

### The three channels of geopolitical transmission

Geopolitical events reach asset prices through distinct pathways. For the Ukraine invasion, three were active simultaneously:

**The commodity supply shock channel** operates fastest — within hours. When Russia, one of the world's largest oil and gas exporters, attacks Ukraine, a major grain exporter, the immediate question is supply. Oil traders do not wait for sanctions to be announced; they start repricing the probability that Russian barrels get sanctioned, rerouted, or physically disrupted. Wheat traders start discounting Ukrainian planting and harvest disruptions. This channel hits commodity markets first and hardest in the initial hours.

**The financial sanctions channel** operates on a different timescale — days to weeks. SWIFT exclusion (the "nuclear option" of financial warfare), reserve freezing, and cross-border payment restrictions require political coordination across multiple governments. The G7 cannot announce coordinated sanctions in real time; they need emergency sessions, legal frameworks, and diplomatic alignment. So markets spend Days 1-6 pricing the *probability* that SWIFT exclusion will happen, which creates a massive premium on uncertainty.

**The risk-off sentiment channel** activates within the first few hours but is the most volatile and mean-reverting. When geopolitical shock hits, investors rotate toward traditional safe havens: the US dollar, the Swiss franc, the Japanese yen, gold, and US Treasury bonds (which see their yields fall as prices rise). Risky assets — equities, emerging-market currencies, high-yield bonds — get sold. This channel is real but often reverses quickly as markets recalibrate to the actual economic impact.

### The key vocabulary: what is a "safe haven"?

A *safe haven* is an asset that tends to hold its value or appreciate during periods of broad market stress. The logic is that in a crisis, investors want to hold things that are liquid (easy to sell quickly), stable (don't move erratically), and backed by strong institutions. The US dollar is the world's safe-haven reserve currency because it is used in roughly 85% of all foreign exchange transactions — even during global crises, demand for dollars remains high. Gold has been a safe haven for millennia because it is no one's liability and has a fixed physical supply. The Swiss franc is a safe haven because Switzerland has a reputation for political neutrality, a strong current account surplus, and a stable legal system.

### What is VIX and why does it matter?

The *VIX* — formally the CBOE Volatility Index — measures how much the options market expects the S&P 500 to swing over the next 30 days. It is sometimes called the "fear gauge" of markets. When investors are calm, VIX tends to sit between 12 and 20. When something frightening happens, they rush to buy options as insurance, driving up option prices and therefore the VIX. In the weeks before the invasion, VIX was already elevated at 28 (up from a typical 16-18 during calm periods). On invasion day it hit 34, and it sustained above 30 for several weeks.

A VIX of 34 implies the market is pricing roughly a 2.1% daily move in the S&P 500 on average. For context, a "normal" day moves about 0.7%. At 34, markets are telling you to expect volatility roughly three times the normal level.

### What is SWIFT and why is it the "nuclear option"?

*SWIFT* — the Society for Worldwide Interbank Financial Telecommunication — is the messaging network that banks around the world use to send payment instructions to each other. Every major bank is connected to SWIFT. When Bank A in Germany wants to send euros to Bank B in Japan, it sends a SWIFT message with the payment details. Without SWIFT access, you can still have money in an account, but you cannot easily communicate payment instructions to the rest of the world's banking system. It is the plumbing of international finance.

Being cut off from SWIFT is not the same as having your money confiscated, but it is enormously disruptive: it makes it nearly impossible to receive payment for exports, to pay for imports, or to transact in any major currency with non-Russian counterparties. For a commodity exporter like Russia, where oil and gas are sold in dollars and euros, SWIFT exclusion means you cannot receive payment for your main export. That is why it was called the "nuclear option" of financial warfare — it was considered so damaging that Western governments had historically been reluctant to deploy it, fearing a complete severing of the Russian economy from the global financial system.

## Hour 0-6: The Overnight Shock (Feb 24, Pre-Market)

At 3:00 AM Eastern time on February 24, 2022, Russian President Vladimir Putin delivered a televised address announcing the commencement of what he called a "special military operation" in Ukraine. Within minutes, reports began arriving of Russian military movements from multiple directions — from Belarus toward Kyiv in the north, from Crimea toward Kherson in the south, and from the Donbas in the east.

### What markets knew beforehand

This moment was not a complete surprise to markets — but it was more of a surprise than it should have been. In the six weeks leading up to February 24, US intelligence agencies had been leaking details of Russian military planning with unusual specificity, including the date of a potential invasion. In January 2022, the Biden administration publicly warned that Russia had pre-positioned approximately 100,000 troops on Ukraine's borders and was planning an imminent attack.

The *Geopolitical Risk Index* (GPR) — a measure compiled by economists Dario Caldara and Matteo Iacoviello that counts newspaper references to geopolitical risk — had risen from a baseline of about 100 in 2021 to 161 by January 2022, the highest it had been since the Soleimani killing in January 2020. By February, it would reach 345 — its highest reading on record, surpassing even September 11, 2001.

Yet equity markets had only mildly priced the risk. The S&P 500 had fallen about 5-6% from its January 3 high by mid-February, but much of that decline was attributable to rising interest rate expectations, not geopolitical risk. Options traders — who bet specifically on volatility — were pricing roughly a 30% probability of a full-scale invasion based on the implied volatility surfaces in equity and currency options.

This pre-pricing matters enormously for understanding what happened next. If markets had priced zero probability of invasion, the shock on the morning of February 24 would have been catastrophic. Because they priced roughly 30%, the shock was significant but not terminal.

### The first market moves

The Brent crude market moved first. Oil trades around the clock in electronic markets, and the first quotes after Putin's speech showed a contract jumping from \$96.40 to above \$100 within minutes of the announcement — a roughly 6.3% overnight move in a matter of hours. This was the commodity supply shock channel firing.

S&P 500 futures — contracts that allow investors to bet on where the stock market will open — fell about 2.5% during the overnight session. EUR/USD, the exchange rate between the euro and the US dollar, dropped from 1.132 to 1.118, a move of about 1.2%. The euro's weakness made sense: Europe is geographically close to the conflict, is dependent on Russian energy, and has the most direct economic exposure. The dollar strengthened as the safe-haven bid kicked in.

Gold — the traditional crisis safe haven — rose from \$1,898 to \$1,919 per ounce within hours. The Swiss franc (CHF) and Japanese yen (JPY), two other traditional safe havens, both appreciated against the euro and dollar.

### Why the overnight moves were relatively contained

Given the scale of the geopolitical event, the overnight moves were notably contained. S&P futures down 2.5% and oil up 6% for the start of a major land war in Europe seems almost modest. The explanation is threefold:

First, markets had partially pre-priced the event. A 30% pre-priced probability on a -10% hypothetical event implies a -3% expected drag already absorbed.

Second, the initial military objectives were unclear. Was this a limited operation targeting the Donbas? A full invasion aiming at Kyiv? Markets need to know the scope of an event to price it, and in the first hours, that scope was genuinely uncertain.

Third, markets have learned from history to wait for confirmation. Many prior "invasion scares" had been resolved diplomatically at the last minute. The market's default assumption in the first hours was that this might still be resolved before full-scale combat.

## Hour 6-24: European Markets Open and the Cascade Begins

When European equities opened at 9:00 AM Central European Time on February 24, the full picture had become clearer. Russian forces were attacking from multiple directions. Kyiv was under missile attack. The scope of the invasion was unmistakably large. Markets reacted accordingly.

![Multi-asset reaction to Russia-Ukraine invasion from pre-invasion through 3 months for Brent wheat gas EUR gold and VIX](/imgs/blogs/russia-invades-ukraine-the-first-72-hours-in-markets-2.png)

### European equities: geography as a risk factor

European stock markets opened down hard, but with a striking pattern: proximity to the conflict correlated with severity of the selloff.

The Warsaw Stock Exchange in Poland fell approximately 11% on invasion day — the most of any major market. Warsaw's proximity to Ukraine, Poland's role as a NATO frontline state, and the potential for refugee flows and military escalation all amplified the risk premium that Polish equities needed to offer.

Prague's PX index fell around 8%. Budapest fell over 6%. These Eastern European markets were pricing not just economic disruption but the possibility that the war could spread, or that they might face Russian pressure or hybrid warfare. Western European markets were less severe: Frankfurt's DAX fell about 4%, Paris's CAC40 fell 3.5%. The UK's FTSE 100, partly insulated by its oil and commodities exposure (which was rising), fell less than 2%.

The message was clear: European equity markets were not all the same. Investors were applying a spatial discount — the closer you were to the war, the larger the risk premium demanded.

### Defensive assets and the safe-haven bid

While equities were falling, the classic safe-haven trade was running in parallel. Gold rallied from \$1,898 to \$1,919 per ounce in the first day — a modest but clear safe-haven move. The US dollar index (DXY) rose from 96.9 to about 97.5, reflecting the classic "flight to dollar" response.

US Treasury bonds rallied as well. The 10-year US Treasury yield fell from about 1.98% to 1.85% on the day — meaning the price of bonds rose as investors bought them as insurance. A *yield* falling while a *price* rises is not intuitive at first, but remember: a bond is a fixed stream of future payments. If you pay more for that stream today, the rate of return (the yield) is lower. When investors rush to buy bonds for safety, they bid up bond prices and push yields down.

### The Ukrainian hryvnia and Eastern European currencies

The Ukrainian hryvnia (UAH) — Ukraine's national currency — went into virtual freefall. Trading conditions were erratic and the National Bank of Ukraine quickly imposed restrictions on currency exchange. The hryvnia lost roughly 10% against the dollar in the first days, though the true market rate was hard to determine given restricted trading.

Other Eastern European currencies fell against the euro: the Polish zloty weakened, the Czech koruna weakened, the Hungarian forint fell sharply. These are small currencies that lack the global liquidity to absorb large-scale risk-off selling. Emerging-market currencies broadly declined as the global risk appetite dropped.

### The Russian MOEX: the market that stopped

The Moscow Exchange (MOEX) opened on February 24 and immediately fell approximately 45% in the first hour of trading — one of the most catastrophic single-day openings of any major stock market in history. Russian stocks had been falling for weeks as invasion risk mounted, but the confirmation of war triggered panic selling. The Russian central bank quickly intervened, and the MOEX was halted, then eventually closed for roughly a month starting March 1.

This closure created a unique situation: the MSCI Russia index — which tracks Russian stocks held by international investors — essentially went to zero for accounting purposes. International investors who owned Russian equities through index funds or ETFs found those positions frozen, with no ability to sell.

## Day 1-3: The Spike, the Partial Recovery, and What Markets Were Really Pricing

![72-hour sequence of invasion market events from Putin speech to SWIFT exclusion to Brent near 130](/imgs/blogs/russia-invades-ukraine-the-first-72-hours-in-markets-3.png)

### The "buy the invasion" dynamic

One of the most counterintuitive phenomena in crisis markets is what traders call "buy the rumor, sell the news" or, in this case, the reverse: "sell the rumor, buy the fact." Once an event that markets have been pricing actually happens, there is often a partial reversal. The uncertainty resolves; the expected value stops changing direction; and the residual "bad news" premium in prices gets partially unwound.

This is exactly what happened in the first three days after the invasion. The S&P 500 fell roughly 3% on invasion day. By the end of Day 2, it had recovered about half that loss. By Day 3, the S&P was roughly flat relative to its pre-invasion level. US equity investors were effectively saying: "We priced this partially. The event happened. Now let's figure out the actual economic impact."

European markets were more cautious — they stayed negative longer given their greater economic exposure. But even European equities began to stabilize after the first two days.

### Why VIX went to 34 but didn't sustain

The VIX rose from 28 to 34 on invasion day — a meaningful but not extreme move. For comparison, VIX reached 82 during COVID, 48 during the 2008 financial crisis, and 45 after September 11. VIX at 34 was elevated but was pricing roughly "a significant but contained geopolitical shock" rather than a systemic financial crisis.

Why didn't VIX go higher? Three reasons:

First, the US economy was not directly in the conflict. Unlike COVID, which shut down the entire global economy, or 2008, which threatened the banking system, the Ukraine war was geographically contained and did not directly threaten US corporate earnings (other than through energy prices).

Second, the Federal Reserve was already signaling interest rate hikes to fight inflation. Higher rates generally reduce equity valuations, but they also signal a central bank that believes the economy is strong enough to tighten. In a perverse way, the pre-existing monetary tightening path gave markets a stability anchor.

Third, energy companies — a large and growing component of the S&P 500 — were actually rallying strongly on rising oil prices. Exxon, Chevron, ConocoPhillips were all up 3-5% on invasion day, partially offsetting the selling in other sectors.

### The commodity repricing: supply shock arithmetic

The commodity channel was doing something quite different from equities. This was not sentiment-driven — it was supply-shock arithmetic. Ukraine and Russia together account for approximately 29% of global wheat exports, 19% of global corn exports, and roughly 80% of global sunflower oil exports. Ukraine alone supplies about 50% of the world's sunflower oil. When a shooting war starts in the middle of a planting cycle, the math is stark.

CBOT wheat futures — the global benchmark for wheat prices, traded in Chicago — rose from roughly \$790 per bushel to \$895 per bushel in the first day, a gain of \$105 or approximately 13.3%. Corn rose about 6%. Sunflower oil prices jumped approximately 20%. Nickel rose 5% partly because Russia is a major nickel producer and partly because of concerns about sanctions. Aluminum rose 4%.

These were not fear-driven moves that would reverse as calm returned. They reflected a genuine, arithmetic assessment of global supply: if X% of global supply comes from this region, and the region is now in a shooting war, how much of that supply is at risk?

## Russia's Economic Firewall: The "Fortress Balance Sheet"

### How Putin pre-positioned for war

One of the most analytically important aspects of the first 72 hours was the question of how much Russia's pre-positioned defenses would matter. In the years following the 2014 Crimea annexation and the moderate sanctions that followed, Putin had systematically restructured the Russian economy to reduce its vulnerability to Western financial pressure.

The four pillars of Russia's "fortress Russia" strategy:

**Massive foreign exchange reserves.** By February 2022, the Russian central bank held approximately \$640 billion in foreign exchange reserves — the world's fourth-largest reserve stockpile, behind only China, Japan, and Switzerland. This was the largest Russia had ever held. The explicit purpose was to backstop the ruble in a crisis.

**De-dollarization.** Between 2014 and 2022, Russia systematically reduced the dollar share of its reserves from over 40% to approximately 16%, shifting into euros, gold, yuan, and other currencies. The intent was to reduce exposure to US financial sanctions, since the US government can freeze dollar-denominated assets held in US correspondent banks.

**Reduced external debt.** Russia's government and major corporations paid down dollar-denominated external debt aggressively. By 2022, Russia's total external debt had fallen to roughly \$490 billion, compared to peak levels of \$750+ billion. Critically, the government's own external debt was very small.

**Domestic food and energy self-sufficiency.** Russia had become a net food exporter in the years after 2014, reducing its dependence on food imports. Energy prices denominated in rubles actually rose for Russian consumers when oil prices rose globally — an insulation mechanism.

### How much did the firewall actually buy?

In the immediate aftermath of the invasion, the fortress balance sheet bought Russia about three weeks of stability. The ruble fell from 79 per dollar to 91 per dollar in the first 24 hours — a roughly 15% devaluation — and continued deteriorating toward 116 as SWIFT exclusion was confirmed.

But here is the critical catch: roughly \$300 billion of Russia's \$640 billion in reserves were held in G7 jurisdictions — primarily in euros held at the European Central Bank, dollars held at the Federal Reserve and US correspondent banks, and pounds held in the UK. These reserves were frozen within days of the SWIFT announcement. Overnight, nearly half of Russia's financial firewall disappeared from its control.

The remaining reserves — primarily gold held domestically plus yuan held in Chinese accounts — were not frozen but could not easily be deployed to defend the ruble in western markets. Russia found itself with \$300+ billion in reserves it could not spend, and another \$300+ billion it could spend but not convert into the currencies needed to defend the ruble in global FX markets.

## The SWIFT Question: Days 1-6 and the "Nuclear Option"

### Why SWIFT exclusion took 6 days to announce

The decision to exclude Russia from SWIFT — announced on March 2, 2022, six days after the invasion — was not inevitable from Day 1, and markets spent those six days pricing the probability that it would happen. Understanding why it took six days reveals something important about how financial warfare is conducted.

SWIFT is a Belgian cooperative owned by its member banks. It is not a US government entity, and its governance requires consensus among a board that includes European member banks. In 2014, after the Crimea annexation, European governments had considered SWIFT exclusion but rejected it partly because European banks had extensive exposure to Russia. Germany's banks alone had roughly \$20 billion in claims on Russia.

By February 2022, the political calculus had changed. The scale of the invasion — strikes on Kyiv, civilian casualties, missile attacks on major cities — created political pressure that overrode the economic objections. But the announcement still required coordinating the US, EU, UK, Canada, and other G7 members. The six-day gap between invasion and announcement was the time needed to build that consensus.

During those six days, the ruble was essentially in free fall on pure uncertainty. The market was pricing a range: "If SWIFT happens, ruble goes to 120+. If it doesn't happen, ruble recovers toward 85. We're betting on probabilities." The ruble moved to 116 by the first week — reflecting a market pricing roughly 70-80% probability of full SWIFT exclusion.

The CDS market on Russian sovereign debt was even more blunt. A *CDS* — credit default swap — is essentially insurance against a borrower defaulting on its debt. Within 48 hours of the invasion, CDS spreads on Russian sovereign bonds had blown out to levels implying approximately 50-60% probability of default within five years. The bond market was pricing near-default while the Russian government still insisted everything was fine.

![How invasion flowed through three parallel channels commodity supply shock sanctions and risk-off sentiment to asset prices](/imgs/blogs/russia-invades-ukraine-the-first-72-hours-in-markets-5.png)

### What SWIFT exclusion actually meant vs. what was feared

The final SWIFT announcement on March 2 was notable for what it did and did not include. The G7 excluded seven Russian banks from SWIFT — but explicitly carved out energy-related transactions. Gazprombank, the primary conduit for European energy payments to Russia, was not initially sanctioned.

This was a deliberate carve-out: European countries, particularly Germany, were deeply dependent on Russian natural gas and could not afford to block energy payments. The result was a partial SWIFT exclusion — devastating to Russian banks' ability to process trade payments and receive investment flows, but specifically designed to preserve the energy payment pipeline that funded the Russian government.

Markets had been pricing the full-exclusion scenario during the six-day uncertainty period. When the actual announcement came with the energy carve-out, the ruble and Russian bond market partially stabilized from their worst levels — not because things were good, but because the tail risk of "complete financial isolation" had been reduced somewhat. This illustrates a key point about sanctions announcements: the market move happens on the *probability*, and the announcement often causes a partial reversal.

## The Commodity Repricing: Beyond Fear

### Wheat: a worked-out supply shock calculation

The wheat market's 13% one-day move in the first 72 hours was not sentiment. It was math. Let's work through the arithmetic.

**Global wheat production** is approximately 780 million metric tons annually. Russia and Ukraine together export roughly 29% of global wheat — about 80 million metric tons per year. Pre-war CBOT wheat futures were at \$790 per bushel; one bushel is approximately 27.2 kilograms, so global wheat supply is about 28.7 billion bushels annually.

If Russian and Ukrainian exports were cut by 50% — a plausible outcome if Black Sea shipping routes were disrupted — approximately 40 million metric tons of wheat would need to be sourced from alternative suppliers (primarily the US, Canada, Australia, Argentina, and EU). In the short run, those suppliers cannot expand production meaningfully; crop decisions are made months in advance. So the supply deficit would drive importers to bid against each other for the available supply.

A 50% cut in Russian-Ukrainian exports represents approximately 5% of global supply. Economists who studied the 2010-11 food price crisis (when drought in Russia cut exports and food prices rose 30%) found a supply elasticity of roughly 0.3 for wheat: a 1% reduction in supply leads to a 3% increase in price. Applied here: 5% supply reduction times 0.3 elasticity implies a roughly 15% price increase — very close to the 13.3% observed in the first day.

This is supply-shock arithmetic, not fear pricing. And the reason it matters: supply-shock pricing is stickier than sentiment pricing. When fear fades, fear-driven prices revert. When supply actually disappears, prices stay elevated until alternative supply fills the gap.

### The food inflation consequence

The commodity repricing of the first 72 hours planted a seed that would grow for two years. Wheat, corn, sunflower oil, and fertilizer (Russia is the world's largest fertilizer exporter) all rose sharply in the first week. The Black Sea blockade — Russian warships blocking Ukrainian port access — would persist for over a year, keeping Ukrainian grain from reaching global markets.

By mid-2022, global food inflation was running at the highest rates in decades. The World Food Programme estimated that 44 million people were "on the brink of famine" partly as a result of the food supply disruption. This was not priced in the first 72 hours, but its roots were visible in the commodity markets from the first morning.

## The Short-War Pricing Error and Its Consequences

### How the initial equity recovery reflected a dangerous assumption

One of the most important analytical observations about the first 72 hours is that the partial equity recovery after Day 1 reflected a specific implicit bet that markets were making: that the war would be short, that a negotiated settlement was likely within weeks, and that the medium-term economic impact would be limited to a commodity price spike that would fade.

This assumption was wrong in almost every dimension. But it was not an irrational assumption at the time. Historical geopolitical shocks — the 2003 Iraq War, the 2008 Russia-Georgia war, the 2011 Libya conflict — had all resolved relatively quickly in terms of market impact. The pattern of "spike then recovery" was historically well-established.

What was different about Ukraine was the structural change it would create in energy markets. Russia supplied approximately 40-45% of Europe's natural gas — a dependency that could not be replaced quickly. The first 72 hours' TTF gas repricing from \$83/MWh to \$90/MWh was a 9% move. But the real TTF repricing would not happen until weeks and months later as the political decision to actually cut off Russian gas flows became clear. By August 2022, TTF would reach \$310/MWh — a 275% increase from pre-invasion levels.

The before-after above captures the most important analytical point of this post: the same invasion triggered two completely different market narratives six months apart. Markets in the first week said "temporary spike." Markets by June had to reprice the entire European energy regime.

## What Markets Got Right and Wrong: A First-72-Hour Scorecard

![Scorecard of first 72 hour market calls on direction magnitude and duration across oil equities war duration SWIFT gas ruble defense stocks and wheat](/imgs/blogs/russia-invades-ukraine-the-first-72-hours-in-markets-7.png)

The matrix above summarizes the analytical conclusion of the first 72 hours. The pattern is striking and instructive:

**Direction calls were mostly correct.** Markets got the right direction on oil, wheat, risk-off assets, and defense stocks. This is the expected value calculation working: everyone knew the invasion was bad for equities and good for oil. The commodity supply shock direction was obvious.

**Magnitude calls were mixed.** Markets underestimated the eventual oil move (priced a \$110 peak; actual was \$120+). They underestimated the eventual gas move by an enormous margin (\$90 vs. eventual \$310). They correctly sized the equity magnitude at -3% rather than -10%.

**Duration calls were almost uniformly wrong.** This is the most instructive finding. The market priced every negative outcome as temporary. War would be short. SWIFT would be partial or temporary. Gas flows would be maintained. Ruble pain would be brief. Nearly all of these assumptions proved incorrect.

The one durable call: defense stocks. Markets correctly priced that NATO member governments would need to dramatically increase defense spending, and that European defense companies would be the primary beneficiaries. This call was right on direction, magnitude, and duration.

## Worked Examples

#### Worked example:

**The expected-value miss: pre-invasion pricing**

Here is the actual arithmetic of what markets were pricing before the invasion and why the move still landed hard.

Pre-invasion, equity options markets implied roughly a 30% probability that Russia would invade Ukraine in the near term. If a full invasion happened, analysts estimated the S&P 500 would fall approximately 5-8%. Call it 6% as a central estimate.

Expected loss from invasion scenario = 30% probability × 6% loss = 1.8% expected impact already in prices.

Actual: the S&P fell approximately 3% on invasion day and recovered to roughly flat within 10 days.

Was the market wrong? Partially. The 3% first-day drop was consistent with the invasion scenario delivering its expected loss — the market had priced 1.8% in, so a 3% fall represented roughly a 1.2% "surprise component" (reflecting the full certainty and the fact that 30% was an underestimate of the true probability).

The more interesting error: the rapid recovery to flat within 10 days implied the market was saying "now that it's happened, the medium-term impact is already absorbed." That was the wrong framing. The invasion was not a one-time event that resolved uncertainty — it was the beginning of a structural change that would take years to play out. The EV calculation was right but the "event is now over" framing was wrong. The correct framing was "this event has just started."

*Lesson: Expected value correctly measures the initial shock but fails to capture events that are ongoing processes rather than discrete shocks.*

#### Worked example:

**The commodity supply shock calculation**

Ukraine and Russia together export approximately 29 million metric tons of wheat annually (out of roughly 200 million metric tons of globally traded wheat). Pre-invasion CBOT wheat was \$790 per bushel.

If the Black Sea war disrupts 50% of Ukrainian exports — approximately 15 million metric tons — that creates a global shortfall of approximately 7.5% of internationally traded wheat (15 MT / 200 MT).

Using a price-supply elasticity of approximately 0.2 for wheat (a 1% supply reduction causes a ~0.2% price increase): 7.5% shortfall × (1/0.2) implies roughly a 37.5% price increase on a persistent disruption.

Day-one CBOT move was +13% — consistent with markets pricing roughly a 1/3 probability of sustained disruption. The eventual move to \$1,300+ per bushel by March 2022 reflected the market learning that the disruption was real and sustained.

*Lesson: Commodity supply shock arithmetic is knowable in real time. Markets price the probability of disruption, not the full disruption. As the probability resolves to certainty, the price catches up.*

#### Worked example:

**The defense stock re-rating: Rheinmetall's \$50 billion reasoning**

Rheinmetall, Germany's largest defense manufacturer, traded at approximately \$90 per share (roughly \$7.5 billion market capitalization) before the invasion. By end of March, it was near \$140 — a 55% gain in about 5 weeks.

The math that justified this re-rating:

Germany had been spending approximately 1.5% of GDP on defense — below NATO's 2% target, and a longstanding political sore point with the US. Germany's GDP is approximately \$4 trillion. Going from 1.5% to 2.0% means increasing defense spending by 0.5% × \$4 trillion = \$20 billion per year.

Rheinmetall's market share in German defense procurement was roughly 15-20% for army systems, ammunition, and vehicles. If it captured 10% of new German defense spending, that's \$2 billion in additional annual revenue. At typical defense sector margins of 8-10% and a sector price-to-earnings ratio of 20x, that additional revenue implies a market-cap boost of \$2 billion × 9% margin × 20 PE = approximately \$3.6 billion.

Rheinmetall's pre-war market cap was \$7.5 billion. Adding \$3.6 billion implies a post-war market cap of roughly \$11 billion — which at the \$90 pre-war share count implies a fair value around \$130-135. The actual \$140 reached in March was slightly above that re-rated fair value but in the right range.

Rheinmetall eventually reached over \$500 per share by 2024 as the full scale of NATO rearmament became clear — showing that even the "correctly priced" 55% gain in the first 72 hours dramatically underestimated the eventual outcome.

*Lesson: Defense stock re-ratings during major wars are not sentiment — they are present-value calculations on durable government spending commitments. The NATO 2% GDP commitment is a 10-year capital expenditure program, and markets should price it as such.*

#### Worked example:

**The SWIFT announcement trade: ruble path and CDS market**

The ruble's path from invasion to the SWIFT announcement is a textbook lesson in how markets price policy uncertainty.

- **Feb 23 (pre-invasion):** 79.3 rubles per dollar
- **Feb 24 (invasion day):** 91.4 rubles per dollar — a 15% devaluation, pricing rough probability-weighted SWIFT scenario
- **Mar 3 (one week):** 116 rubles per dollar — after SWIFT announcement, pricing near-certainty of financial isolation
- **Peak:** approximately 139 rubles per dollar in mid-March

But here is the trade that actually worked: the CDS market on Russian sovereign debt priced near-default within 48 hours of the invasion — CDS spreads blew out to imply roughly 50-60% cumulative default probability within five years. This was more accurate than the spot ruble market, which still had some support from the central bank's reserve purchases.

The total ruble move from \$79 to \$139 was a 75% depreciation peak-to-trough. \$300 billion in reserves was frozen; the remaining \$340 billion was largely in gold and yuan and could not easily defend the ruble in Western markets. Capital controls announced on February 28 — requiring exporters to convert 80% of foreign-currency revenues to rubles and prohibiting most capital outflows — created an artificial demand for rubles from oil exporters. By May, the ruble had recovered to 67 per dollar — stronger than pre-invasion.

This V-shaped recovery was not a market success story; it was the result of mandatory administrative controls, not fundamental improvement. A foreign investor could not actually buy rubles cheaply at 139 and sell at 67, because capital controls prevented free conversion.

*Lesson: Central bank reserves and capital controls can create apparent stability in a currency under attack, but the administered exchange rate reflects policy, not the underlying economic reality. The CDS market — which prices credit risk without depending on FX liquidity — is often a cleaner signal than the spot FX market in crisis.*

## The "Fog of War" Trades: What Worked and What Failed

### The trades that worked

**Long oil and European energy companies.** The supply shock was real and lasting. Brent went from \$96 to \$120. BP, Shell, TotalEnergies all rallied. Energy sector P&L was the best place to be in the first month.

**Short European equities vs. short US equities (pair trade).** European equities fell more than US equities and stayed down longer. The DAX underperformed the S&P by roughly 8-10 percentage points over the first three months. The proximity and energy-dependency thesis was correct.

**Long European defense stocks.** As discussed in the worked example, Rheinmetall, BAE Systems, Leonardo, and SAAB all rallied significantly and sustained those gains as the NATO rearmament theme developed. This was one of the most durable and analytically justified trades of the crisis.

**Long wheat and agricultural commodities.** The supply shock was persistent. Wheat eventually peaked near \$1,300/bushel in March 2022, a gain of over 60% from pre-invasion levels.

### The trades that failed or reversed

**Short Russian assets immediately.** Russian equities (MOEX) halted within days and were inaccessible for months. Any investor who tried to short Russian stocks through ETFs found themselves locked into frozen positions. The "short Russia" trade was actually very difficult to execute in practice, even though it was obviously correct in direction.

**Short European equities as a long-term position.** European equities sold off hard in February-March 2022, but recovered strongly through 2023. An investor who held a persistent short position on the basis of "Europe is broken" missed the recovery.

**Long VIX after the initial spike.** VIX hit 34 on invasion day and fell steadily thereafter, averaging below 25 by late March. The initial volatility spike attracted vol-buyers who then got ground down as VIX mean-reverted. Geopolitical shocks rarely sustain elevated VIX for long unless they directly threaten the US financial system.

## How the Ruble's V-Recovery Fooled Everyone

![Russian ruble crash from 79 to 116 per dollar after invasion and SWIFT exclusion then V-shaped recovery to 67 by May 2022](/imgs/blogs/russia-invades-ukraine-the-first-72-hours-in-markets-6.png)

The ruble's V-shaped recovery — visible clearly in the chart above — is one of the most misunderstood stories of the entire crisis. By May 2022, the ruble was stronger against the dollar than it had been before the invasion. Financial commentators who predicted ruble collapse were accused of being wrong.

They were not wrong. The ruble's recovery was purely administrative, not fundamental:

**Mandatory export conversion.** The Russian government required major exporters (including Gazprom and Rosneft) to convert 80% of their foreign-currency export revenues into rubles. With oil prices elevated, these companies were earning record revenues in dollars and euros. Forcing conversion of \$200+ billion in annual export receipts created massive ruble demand.

**Capital account closure.** Russians were prohibited from sending money abroad, converting rubles to foreign currency above small limits, or repaying foreign debt in hard currency. This removed the pressure that normally comes from citizens moving savings offshore.

**Counter-sanctions.** Russia began demanding that European gas buyers pay for natural gas in rubles — creating additional demand for rubles as European utilities complied to keep the gas flowing.

None of this reflected economic health. GDP was contracting, inflation was accelerating, imports had collapsed by 40% due to sanctions cutting off supply chains. The ruble's "strength" was the strength of a currency in a closed system — not a free-market signal at all. The market rate for rubles in the black market or for Russians with access to foreign accounts was far weaker.

This matters analytically because the ruble story is a cautionary tale about interpreting prices in markets with large-scale administrative intervention.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception: "Markets predicted the invasion was coming."** Financial commentary often implies that elevated equity volatility and commodity prices before February 24 showed markets foresaw the invasion. This is partially true but mostly false. Options markets priced roughly 30% probability. At 30%, the dominant scenario is still no invasion. An investor who bet on invasion in January 2022 would have been more right than wrong on direction, but would have had significant losses through most of February as diplomatic talks continued.

**Misconception: "SWIFT exclusion killed Russia's economy immediately."** SWIFT exclusion was severe, but the energy carve-out — the explicit decision not to sanction Gazprombank — meant Russia continued receiving European energy payments throughout 2022. Russia's oil and gas export revenues actually increased in 2022 to \$218 billion, up from \$117 billion in 2021, because price rises more than offset volume declines. The economy shrank, but not due to immediate revenue collapse. The economic damage was longer-term and structural.

**Misconception: "The ruble's recovery proved sanctions failed."** As explained above, the ruble's post-May 2022 strength was the result of capital controls and mandatory export conversion, not underlying economic improvement. It is comparable to a dam holding back water — the structural pressure keeps building, even if the current level looks stable. By 2023-24, the ruble had weakened again as import demand recovered and the oil price cap began to bite.

**Misconception: "Defense stocks were obvious buys."** In hindsight, buying Rheinmetall at \$90 looks obvious. In reality, on February 24, 2022, the probability that Germany would actually commit to massive defense spending increases was far from certain. Germany had a decades-long cultural resistance to military spending, rooted in its World War II history. The NATO 2% commitment required political courage that German chancellors had previously avoided. It was not obvious that "war in Europe means Germany rearming." It was a probabilistic bet that turned out correctly.

**Misconception: "Commodity price spikes from wars always reverse quickly."** The 1973 Arab oil embargo caused oil prices to spike and then stay elevated for nearly a decade. The post-Ukraine agricultural and energy price increases were more complex: some reversed (wheat prices fell back by 2023), but European gas markets were permanently restructured as the continent built LNG import infrastructure and diversified away from Russian supply. The "spike and reverse" template badly misfit the energy component of the Ukraine shock.

**Misconception: "Safe haven assets always work in a geopolitical crisis."** Gold rose only 1.1% on invasion day — a modest safe-haven performance. US Treasury bonds rose modestly. The traditional safe-haven playbook was muted because the Federal Reserve had already been signaling aggressive rate hikes, and rate hikes are negative for Treasury bonds (higher rates → lower bond prices). The safe-haven bid was partially offset by the concurrent monetary tightening theme. Safe havens work best when the central bank is in easing mode; they work less reliably when rates are rising.

## How It Shows Up in Real Markets: Case Studies

### The Kyiv trade: what it looked like in real time

Imagine you are a professional portfolio manager at 6:00 AM Eastern time on February 24, 2022. You have just seen Brent crude touch \$102, S&P futures down 2.5%, and the ruble weakening fast. What do you do?

The immediate decision hierarchy: First, assess your existing exposure. If you are long European equities, you are likely to face losses as markets open. You need to decide whether to reduce now in futures markets (which trade before the cash market opens) or hold and wait for the cash open. The cost of action is the spread and potential liquidity discount in a volatile pre-market. The cost of inaction is undefined downside if the news worsens.

Second, you assess convexity. Which positions will get worse non-linearly if the war escalates? Long European natural gas importers (utilities, chemicals) are highly convex to the downside if Russian gas flows are cut. Long European airlines are convex to the downside if oil spikes to \$130. These are positions to reduce quickly.

Third, you consider hedges. Going long oil in this moment is expensive because the price already jumped. But long energy company equities might still be lagging the commodity move — there is often a brief window where oil prices have repriced but oil company stocks have not fully followed.

The actual professional playbook in the first hours of a geopolitical shock: reduce convexity to the downside, add oil exposure through lagging equities, cut emerging-market currency exposure, and reduce European equities relative to US.

### The TTF gas trade: the slow-moving train

The biggest market repricing in the entire Ukraine crisis was not in the first 72 hours — it was in European natural gas between June and August 2022, when TTF went from \$130/MWh to \$310/MWh. But the seeds were visible in the first 72 hours, when TTF moved from \$83 to \$90 — only a 9% move on Day 1.

The reason TTF lagged was structural: European gas storage facilities were roughly 30% full at the start of winter 2022, providing a buffer that gave markets confidence that immediate supply disruption was manageable. What markets did not adequately price was the political decision that Russia would make — to actually cut gas flows to Europe as a retaliatory measure — which did not become clear until the summer of 2022.

The TTF gas trade from \$83 to \$310 was the largest commodity price move of the crisis and was essentially invisible in the first 72 hours. This illustrates the limits of real-time market pricing for delayed structural shocks.

### The bond market as the honest signal

While equity markets were recovering and Russian officials were issuing reassuring statements, the Russian sovereign CDS market was telling a completely different story. Within 48 hours of the invasion, 5-year CDS spreads on Russia had moved from approximately 160 basis points (representing roughly a 7-8% default probability over 5 years) to over 1,000 basis points (roughly a 40-50% cumulative default probability).

This mattered because the CDS market was right. Russia eventually defaulted on its external dollar-denominated debt in June 2022, when coupon payments that it attempted to make were blocked by US sanctions that prevented US clearing houses from processing the payments. Russia's bonds — which had been trading at 60-70 cents on the dollar in the first week of the war — fell to under 10 cents on the dollar over the following months.

The CDS market saw the default risk before the equity market fully processed the broader economic implications. When you see a massive CDS blow-out, pay attention — it is sophisticated credit investors who have done the detailed sovereign balance sheet analysis.

## How to Trade It: The Geopolitical Shock Playbook

For a practitioner reading this, the 72-hour case study suggests a systematic playbook for future geopolitical shocks:

**Hours 0-6: Assess the channels, not the headlines.** The first question is not "is this bad?" but "which channel is this activating?" Commodity supply shock? (Oil exporters rally, food importers fall.) Financial sanctions? (Currency of sanctioned country collapses, its bonds go to CDS for signals.) Military escalation near a major economy? (Regional equities sell, safe havens bid.)

**Hours 6-24: Reduce convexity, not beta.** The worst-case outcomes in geopolitical shocks are non-linear: full SWIFT exclusion, gas flows cut completely, war spreading to NATO territory. Cut the positions that would be worst in the non-linear tail scenarios. Do not simply reduce broad beta (selling everything).

**Days 1-3: Watch for the "buy the invasion" reversal.** Markets often partially recover as uncertainty resolves into certainty. Use the recovery period to assess whether the market is pricing a "discrete shock that resolves" or a "structural regime change." Ask: does the medium-term scenario require the war to end, or does it persist even if the war continues? Energy dependency on Russia persists regardless of ceasefire timing.

**Week 1-2: Follow the sanctions channel.** The financial warfare channel is the slowest but often the most durable. Track what sanctions have actually been announced vs. what the market was pricing in the uncertainty phase. Look for cases where announced sanctions were less severe than feared (partial reversal opportunity) or more severe than anticipated (continuation of the trade).

**Month 1-3: Find the structural regime changes.** The biggest risk-adjusted opportunity in geopolitical shocks is the structural repricing that takes months to fully materialize. In Ukraine: European defense spending, LNG infrastructure buildout, agricultural supply chain restructuring, European energy independence efforts. These themes play out over years, not days.

For the [geopolitical risk premium framework](/blog/trading/geopolitical-crises/geopolitical-risk-premium-what-markets-price-in) that underlies the difference between a spike and a lasting move, see that post for the full analytical framework. The [four diagnostic tests for spike vs. regime change](/blog/trading/geopolitical-crises/reading-a-geopolitical-shock-spike-vs-regime-change) provide the specific criteria to apply when trying to distinguish temporary fear from permanent repricing. ## Signals That the First 72 Hours Would Have Given You

If you were reading the data in real time on February 24-26, 2022, here are the signals that were telling you the war would not be a brief, contained affair:

**The CDS market.** Russian sovereign CDS blowing out to 1,000+ basis points within 48 hours was pricing near-default. This was not noise — sophisticated credit investors were saying the financial isolation was going to be severe.

**The TTF gas forward curve.** Even though spot TTF only moved 9% in the first days, the forward curve — the price for gas deliveries months into the future — showed a steeper backwardation (near-term prices higher than long-term) than could be explained by temporary disruption. The forward curve was saying "something structural is happening to European gas supply."

**The wheat futures open interest.** Large institutional investors — commodity funds, food companies — were building long positions in wheat futures from Day 1. This was not speculative momentum trading; it was physical-market participants hedging against real supply disruption. Their positioning was the smart money telling you the supply shock was real.

**The ruble's failure to recover despite central bank intervention.** When the Russian central bank announced large reserve purchases to support the ruble in the first days, markets watched to see if the purchases would hold the line. They didn't — the ruble kept weakening despite official intervention, signaling that the market was not persuaded by Russia's official reassurances.
