The morning of Saturday, March 20, 2021, Turks woke up to news that central bank governor Naci Ağbal had been fired by presidential decree — effective immediately. The decree arrived at 3:30 a.m. local time, a time stamp that in Turkish political life signals a decision made not to be debated, but to be absorbed as fact. By the time markets in Asia opened a few hours later, the Turkish lira had already begun its move. By Monday's close, it had fallen 15% against the US dollar — from roughly 7.9 TRY per dollar to 9.2. It was the worst single-day currency move Turkey had seen since its catastrophic 2001 banking crisis, and it happened because one man lost his job.

Ağbal had been in the role for exactly 132 days. In that time, he had done something genuinely remarkable for Turkish monetary policy: he had behaved like a textbook central banker. He raised the benchmark interest rate from 10.25% to 19%, directly confronting the inflation that had been steadily rising throughout the pandemic year. Markets had responded well. Foreign investors bought Turkish lira-denominated bonds, attracted by the high nominal yields. The lira had strengthened from a trough near 8.5 to roughly 7.5 per dollar in anticipation of continued orthodoxy. The 10-year Turkish bond yield fell from 14% toward 12% as credibility was priced in. In central banking terms, Ağbal had begun to rebuild the market's trust. Then he was gone.

The reason, broadly understood, was that President Recep Tayyip Erdogan disagreed with him. Erdogan holds a sincere, publicly stated belief that high interest rates cause inflation rather than cure it — a position that contradicts the consensus of global monetary economics. This is not a secret or a surprise: Erdogan had stated this position many times, had fired Ağbal's two predecessors for the same reason, and would, after Ağbal, continue the experiment with a fourth governor who would cut rates from 19% to 8.5% while inflation soared to 85%. This post is about understanding exactly what happened, why it happened the way it did, and what it tells us about how to read and trade emerging-market assets when political risk attacks the most fundamental institutional variable of all: whether the central bank is actually independent.

![The political-monetary-FX doom loop: how each actor reinforces the cycle](/imgs/blogs/turkeys-unorthodox-rate-policy-when-politics-overrides-central-banks-1.png)

The diagram above is the mental model for this entire post. Political pressure on a central bank, if it leads to governor firings and forced rate cuts, does not stop at monetary policy. It starts a feedback loop: rate cuts cause currency depreciation, which causes import prices to rise, which causes inflation, which causes more political pressure. Understanding each link in that chain — and why the chain was so hard to break — is the substance of the analysis that follows.

## Foundations: How Central Banks, Interest Rates, and Currencies Actually Work

Before we can understand why Turkey's experiment was so damaging, we need a solid grounding in the basics. If you already understand what a central bank does, what a policy rate is, and how interest rates connect to currencies, you can skim this section. If any of those terms are unfamiliar, read carefully — the rest of the post depends on this machinery.

### What a central bank actually does

A *central bank* is the government institution responsible for managing a country's money supply and the price of borrowing. Every country with its own currency has one: the United States has the Federal Reserve, the European Union has the European Central Bank, and Turkey has the Central Bank of the Republic of Turkey (CBRT, using the Turkish acronym). The central bank is not a commercial bank — it does not serve ordinary customers. Its clients are other banks, and its main tool is the *policy interest rate* — the interest rate at which commercial banks can borrow from or lend to the central bank overnight.

Why does this rate matter? Because when the central bank raises its rate, commercial banks have to pay more to borrow. They pass this cost on to their own customers: mortgages get more expensive, car loans cost more, business credit becomes tighter. People borrow less, spend less, and the economy slows down. When it slows down, the price pressures that cause inflation ease. This is the standard mechanism: *raise rates → tighten credit → slow spending → lower inflation*.

A *basis point* (bps) is one hundredth of a percentage point — so 25 basis points is 0.25%. When you hear that a central bank "hiked 100 bps", it means the policy rate went up by 1 percentage point. This precision matters in finance because even small rate changes move massive amounts of capital.

### What inflation is and why central banks fight it

*Inflation* is the general rise in the price level over time. A 10% annual inflation rate means that a basket of goods costing 100 Turkish liras in January costs 110 liras by December. For workers, this is painful if their salaries don't keep up. For savers, it means money sitting in a bank account is slowly losing its real purchasing power. For businesses, it introduces planning uncertainty — if you don't know what things will cost next year, you can't price your contracts.

The *real interest rate* is the nominal (stated) interest rate minus the inflation rate. If the policy rate is 8.5% but inflation is 80%, the real rate is approximately -71.5%. This means that in real terms, money is essentially free — lending it out costs you almost nothing after accounting for the fact that the lira you get back next year will be worth much less. Negative real rates are a powerful stimulus: they encourage borrowing and spending, because the cost of credit (in real terms) is negligible or negative. The problem is that when an economy is already running hot with inflation, stimulating it further accelerates the price rise. This is precisely the perverse outcome Turkey engineered between 2021 and 2023.

### How interest rates connect to currencies

This is perhaps the most important mechanical link to understand. Money is mobile. In today's world, an investor in London, New York, or Singapore can, with a few clicks, convert their US dollars into Turkish liras, invest in a Turkish government bond yielding 19%, and convert back to dollars at the end of the year. If the lira holds its value, the investor earns the 19% yield — a huge return compared to what US Treasuries or European bonds offer. This is called a *carry trade*: borrowing in a low-yielding currency and investing in a high-yielding one to earn the "carry" — the yield differential.

But the carry trade has an enormous risk: *currency depreciation*. If the lira falls 20% against the dollar during the year you hold the Turkish bond, your 19% yield is more than wiped out. You earned 19% in lira terms, but you lost 20% on the currency conversion, for a net loss of roughly 1%. The relationship between interest rates and currencies is therefore a two-way street: high rates attract capital (which strengthens the currency), and a stable currency makes the carry trade viable (which attracts more capital). When the central bank cuts rates, both pillars weaken simultaneously: the yield differential shrinks, and the currency depreciates, making the carry trade doubly unattractive.

### What "central bank independence" means and why it matters

*Central bank independence* is the principle that the central bank should make monetary policy decisions based on economic data — inflation, employment, growth — rather than on what politicians want in the short term. This principle exists because politicians have strong incentives to keep interest rates low: low rates stimulate the economy and boost asset prices, which helps incumbents win elections. But low rates also create inflation over time, particularly when used as a chronic stimulus rather than an emergency tool.

An *independent* central bank can raise rates even when this is politically painful — even when it costs the incumbent government votes — because its mandate is price stability, not electoral success. The credibility of this independence is itself economically valuable: when markets believe the central bank will do whatever is necessary to keep inflation near its target, inflation expectations remain anchored. Workers don't demand 50% wage increases because they believe prices will only rise 5%. Businesses don't build massive price cushions into their contracts. The economy is more stable, borrowing is cheaper, and growth is healthier — all because markets trust the institution.

When that independence is destroyed — when it becomes clear that the governor will be fired for raising rates — the entire credibility structure collapses. Markets have to price in the risk that future policy will be determined by political preference rather than economic analysis. This repricing happens across every asset class at once: the currency falls, bond yields rise (to compensate for the risk), and the equity risk premium widens. This is the "institutional risk premium" that Turkey taught the world to measure in real time.

## Erdogan's Heterodox Theory: The Economics of the Interest Rate Reversal

To understand what happened in Turkey, we need to take Erdogan's stated belief seriously as an economic theory — and then explain precisely why it is wrong.

### The claim: high interest rates cause inflation

Erdogan's public statements on monetary policy are unusually explicit for a head of state. He has repeatedly argued, in speeches and interviews, that high interest rates *cause* inflation rather than cure it. The logic, as he has articulated it, is roughly as follows: when businesses have to pay high interest rates on their loans, their cost of production rises, which they pass on to consumers as higher prices. Therefore, cutting rates reduces business costs, reduces prices, and lowers inflation.

This theory has a name in economics: it is sometimes called *cost-push* monetary transmission, and it has a kernel of truth in a narrow sense. In some industries, when interest costs are a significant fraction of total costs, a rate increase does mechanically raise input costs. The steel industry, which carries large inventories, is an example.

There is also an Islamic finance angle to Erdogan's position. In Islamic law, *riba* — roughly translated as "usury" or "interest" — is considered forbidden. Turkey has a large Islamic banking sector, and while Erdogan's stated reasoning was primarily economic rather than theological, his constituency included many voters for whom high interest rates carried a moral taint. This reinforced the political palatability of his position.

### Why the theory is wrong at the macroeconomic level

The mainstream economic consensus — supported by decades of cross-country empirical evidence — is that interest rates primarily control inflation through *demand* rather than through *cost*. The mechanism is:

1. High rates make borrowing expensive, reducing consumer spending and business investment.
2. Reduced demand means firms can't pass on price increases — buyers will simply not purchase if prices rise.
3. With less spending chasing the same goods, price growth slows.

The *Fisher equation* formalizes this: the nominal interest rate equals the real interest rate plus expected inflation. If a central bank holds the nominal rate below the inflation rate — creating negative real rates — it is effectively subsidizing borrowing. Money is cheap in real terms, credit expands, and spending accelerates, which pushes prices higher. Cutting rates does not reduce the price of goods in a demand-driven inflation environment; it accelerates it.

Moreover, the *currency channel* makes Turkey's situation uniquely severe. Turkey imports a large fraction of its energy (approximately 30-40% of primary energy supply), its machinery, and many intermediate goods. When the lira falls — as it does when interest rates are cut and capital flees — the price of every imported good rises in lira terms. This is *import cost-push inflation*, and it is directly caused by the currency weakness that comes from low interest rates. So even the narrow cost-push argument that Erdogan invoked was self-defeating: cutting rates weakened the lira, which raised the lira price of imported inputs, which raised production costs, which raised consumer prices. The mechanism he thought he was avoiding was precisely the one he was accelerating.

The "interest rate lobby" conspiracy theory — which Erdogan invoked to explain why rates were kept high by his predecessors — blamed foreign financial interests for pressuring Turkey to keep rates high in order to extract high returns from Turkish debt. This is a politically resonant narrative in Turkey, which has historically had fraught relationships with the IMF, the World Bank, and Western capital markets. But it misidentifies the mechanism. Foreign investors do buy Turkish bonds partly for the high yields, but the yields are high because Turkey's institutional risk premium is high — the same risk premium that the governor firings were making worse.

## The Political Moves: Three Governors in Two Years

The clearest way to understand the Turkey episode is through the four central bank governors who shaped it. Each appointment and firing tells you something precise about the political-economic feedback loop.

![Governor firing timeline 2019-2024: each firing sent an immediate FX shock](/imgs/blogs/turkeys-unorthodox-rate-policy-when-politics-overrides-central-banks-5.png)

### Murat Çetinkaya: fired July 6, 2019

Çetinkaya was governor from April 2016. He managed Turkey through the failed July 2016 coup attempt — when the lira briefly fell 4.7% in a single day but recovered within two weeks — and through the 2018 lira crisis, when the currency fell from roughly 4.55 to 7.24 per dollar in a matter of months (a 37% depreciation) amid U.S. sanctions over the detention of an American pastor.

In response to the 2018 crisis, Çetinkaya raised the policy rate aggressively, from 8% in April 2018 to 24% by September 2018. This was orthodox policy: the currency was in freefall, inflation was rising (it hit roughly 25% by October 2018), and only a credible rate hike could stem the outflows and begin stabilizing the lira. It worked — the lira recovered to around 5.27 per dollar by early 2019, and inflation began falling.

Then Erdogan began publicly demanding rate cuts. The economy was slowing, the 2019 local elections were approaching, and Erdogan wanted easier conditions. Çetinkaya refused to cut rates as aggressively as Erdogan wanted. On July 6, 2019, he was fired by presidential decree. The lira was at approximately 5.85 per dollar that day. Markets initially read the firing as concerning — the lira slipped modestly — but did not react with the violence of later firings, partly because Çetinkaya's replacement (Murat Uysal) was not immediately seen as a radical change.

### Murat Uysal: fired November 7, 2020

Uysal cut rates steadily after his appointment, bringing the policy rate from 24% down to 8.25% by May 2020 as the COVID pandemic struck and emergency global stimulus made rate cuts the global norm. This timing gave him political cover: the whole world was cutting rates, so Turkey's cuts appeared less heterodox than they were. Markets were relatively calm.

However, the lira had been weakening throughout 2020, from about 5.96 in January to 8.23 in November — a 38% decline. Inflation was rising again. The CBRT's reserves were depleted by currency intervention (using currency swaps with state banks to defend the lira artificially). On November 7, 2020, Uysal was fired and replaced with Naci Ağbal. The timing was significant: it came three days after a sharp market selloff as Turkey's reserves situation became increasingly alarming. Markets interpreted the change as potentially a turn toward orthodoxy — and they were correct, briefly.

### Naci Ağbal: fired March 20, 2021 — the watershed moment

Ağbal's tenure is the most important of the three, because it demonstrated both how quickly orthodox policy can restore credibility and how quickly political intervention destroys it. He was appointed November 7, 2020 and fired March 20, 2021 — exactly 132 days in office.

In those 132 days, Ağbal raised the policy rate from 10.25% to 19% across three meetings: a 475 basis point hike in November 2020, a 200 basis point hike in December 2020, and a 200 basis point hike in March 2021. Inflation was running at approximately 15-16% and rising. The rate hikes were appropriate by any orthodox standard.

The market response was striking. The lira strengthened from roughly 8.5 per dollar at his appointment to approximately 7.5 per dollar by mid-February 2021 — a nearly 12% appreciation in the currency over four months. Foreign investors bought Turkish bonds. The 10-year bond yield, which had been above 14%, fell toward 12%. Analysts who covered Turkey began writing about a genuine return to orthodox policy. JPMorgan, Goldman Sachs, and other major houses upgraded their Turkey assessments.

Then, at 3:30 a.m. on March 20, 2021, came the presidential decree. The official reason given by the government was vague references to Ağbal's "personal dealings." The real reason was the rate hike he had just executed. By Monday's open in Asia, the lira had crashed 15% — from roughly 7.9 to 9.2 per dollar in hours. The 10-year bond yield surged from approximately 12% back toward 18%. It was the largest single-day TRY move since 2001.

Why was this firing worse than the others? Because of what Ağbal had built. Çetinkaya and Uysal operated in a context where markets already priced in significant Turkey political risk. But Ağbal had genuinely begun to rebuild institutional credibility. Foreign investors had returned, not just for yield but because they believed in the direction of policy. When that credibility was destroyed in a 3:30 a.m. decree, the sell-off was amplified by the forced unwinding of positions that had been built on the premise of continued orthodoxy. It was not just a repricing of risk — it was a liquidation of positions that should never have been built had the market properly priced the political risk.

### Şahap Kavcıoğlu: the heterodox experiment in full

Kavcıoğlu was Erdogan's chosen instrument for the rate-cutting experiment. He cut the policy rate from 19% (where Ağbal had left it) in a series of aggressive steps: 100 bps in September 2021, 200 bps in October 2021, 100 bps in November 2021, and 100 bps in December 2021 — bringing the rate to 14% by year-end. He then cut further to 13% and finally to 8.5% by February 2022.

During this period, inflation was accelerating sharply. By January 2022, the rate was 14% and inflation was 48.7%. By April 2022, the rate was 14% and inflation was 61.1%. By October 2022, the policy rate had been cut to 9% and Turkey's CPI inflation peaked at 85.5% according to TurkStat — although some independent economists using alternative methodologies estimated the actual rate was even higher.

The lira, unsurprisingly, collapsed. From roughly 8.5 per dollar in March 2021 (the moment of Ağbal's firing), it hit 18.0 per dollar by December 2021 and stayed near that level through mid-2022. The lira had effectively lost more than half its value in nine months. By mid-2023, just before the U-turn, it had reached approximately 19.7 per dollar.

#### Worked example: the carry trade collapse in real numbers

Let's price what happened to a foreign investor who had bought into the Ağbal orthodoxy and stayed invested.

Suppose you manage an emerging-market bond fund. In November 2020, you invest \$10 million in Turkish 10-year government bonds yielding 14%. To do this, you convert \$10 million into Turkish liras at the prevailing rate of roughly 8.5 TRY/USD, giving you about 85,000,000 TRY. You buy Turkish bonds with a face value of 85 million TRY yielding 14%, so your annual coupon is approximately 11,900,000 TRY.

By March 2021, the lira has strengthened to 7.9 TRY/USD (the Ağbal effect). Your 85 million TRY position is now worth roughly \$10.76 million — a 7.6% currency gain on top of the coupon. You're doing well.

Then Ağbal is fired at 3:30 a.m. on March 20. By Monday's close, the lira is 9.2 TRY/USD. Your 85 million TRY is now worth only \$9.24 million — you've lost \$760,000 from your original \$10 million investment in two days, before accounting for the coupon you'd earned. The Ağbal rally has been wiped out plus more.

You hold on through the year, hoping for a recovery. By December 2021, the lira has collapsed to 18.0 TRY/USD. Your 85 million TRY is now worth only \$4.72 million. You've lost 52% of your original dollar investment, despite having held bonds that nominally paid 14% in lira terms. The lira depreciation of more than 50% overwhelmed the yield completely.

**The lesson**: in an emerging-market carry trade, currency risk dwarfs yield. A governor firing is not just bad news for the central bank — it is an immediate liquidation signal for every position built on the assumption of continued policy orthodoxy.

## The Financial Channels: How Politics Became Economic Pain

The governor firings were the headline events, but the actual economic damage was transmitted through several specific financial channels. Understanding these channels is how you build the analytical framework for evaluating similar situations.

![Turkey TRY per USD 2015-2025: the political event study](/imgs/blogs/turkeys-unorthodox-rate-policy-when-politics-overrides-central-banks-2.png)

### Channel 1: the carry trade reversal

Turkey historically offered some of the highest nominal government bond yields in the emerging market universe. This made it attractive to global carry traders: investors who borrow in low-yield currencies (US dollars, Japanese yen, Swiss francs) and invest in high-yield currencies. At its pre-crisis peak in 2021, Turkey's 2-year bond was yielding around 18-19%, while US 2-year bonds were near 0%. The carry was enormous.

A *carry trade unwind* happens when the conditions that made the trade profitable — high yield differential and a stable or appreciating high-yield currency — deteriorate. When both the yield differential *and* the currency move against you simultaneously, losses compound rapidly. Each governor firing triggered exactly this: foreign holders of Turkish bonds sold their positions (causing the lira to fall as they converted TRY back to USD), which caused the lira to fall further (making remaining holders want to exit too), creating a self-reinforcing selling cascade.

The *duration* of the bonds held matters too. Long-duration bonds — 10-year Turkish government securities — fall in price when yields rise sharply. An investor holding Turkish 10-year bonds at a 12% yield who sees yields spike to 18% (as they did after Ağbal's firing) faces a price decline of roughly 30-40% on the bond itself, before any currency effect. Combined with a 15% currency fall, the losses in dollar terms can exceed 45% in a single trading session. This is why the forced sellers after institutional shocks move so fast.

### Channel 2: import cost-pass-through inflation

Turkey imports a substantial share of its essential commodities. The country has very limited domestic energy production and imports roughly 70-75% of its natural gas and petroleum, primarily from Russia, Iran, and Azerbaijan (sources: Turkish Statistical Institute / EPDK energy regulator, as of 2022). It also imports significant quantities of industrial machinery, pharmaceuticals, and agricultural inputs.

When the lira falls, every one of these imports becomes more expensive in lira terms, directly and immediately. This is *import-cost-pass-through*: the foreign-currency price of the good stays the same, but its lira price rises in direct proportion to the lira's depreciation. A barrel of crude oil that costs \$80 USD costs 672 TRY at 8.4 TRY/USD but costs 1,440 TRY at 18 TRY/USD — a 114% lira price increase on the same physical barrel.

Energy price inflation in Turkey between 2021 and 2022 was extreme. Natural gas tariffs for households were raised multiple times, electricity prices increased sharply, and transport fuel prices tracked global oil prices amplified by the lira's collapse. These energy price rises flowed through to nearly every sector of the economy: food prices rose (because food production, transport, and cold-chain storage are all energy-intensive), manufacturing costs rose, and services inflation climbed as the higher cost of living fed into wage demands.

This is the mechanism that makes Erdogan's theory most precisely wrong in Turkey's specific case. Cutting rates did not reduce production costs — it reduced the lira's value, which raised the lira cost of every imported input, which raised production costs, which raised consumer prices. The mechanism he was trying to prevent was the one he was fueling.

### Channel 3: dollarization and FX reserve depletion

*Dollarization* is the process by which households and businesses in a high-inflation economy shift from holding their savings in the local currency to holding them in US dollars (or another hard currency). It is a rational response to inflation — if you hold liras and inflation is 80%, your savings lose 80% of their real value in a year. If you convert to dollars and the lira falls, you preserve value.

Dollarization in Turkey accelerated sharply from 2021 onwards. The share of foreign-currency deposits in the Turkish banking system rose from roughly 45% in early 2021 to over 60% by 2022 (source: CBRT banking sector data). Turkish citizens were voting with their savings accounts: they did not believe the central bank's rate cuts would solve the inflation problem, because they could see prices rising around them every day.

This dollarization created a self-fulfilling feedback loop. When households buy dollars, they sell liras, which pushes the lira down further, which makes lira savings less attractive, which drives more dollarization, which depresses the lira further. To combat this, the CBRT and government introduced a "currency-protected deposit" scheme (KKM) in December 2021 — a scheme where the government guaranteed depositors against lira depreciation losses. This effectively made the government a buyer of liras at the current market rate and created a contingent liability on the public balance sheet. By 2023, the scheme had amassed deposits worth roughly USD 120 billion (source: CBRT, as of mid-2023), representing a massive potential fiscal cost if the lira continued to fall.

The CBRT also depleted its gross foreign exchange reserves via currency swap arrangements with state banks, effectively lending hard currency to stabilize the lira in the spot market. Net reserve positions (gross reserves minus forward commitments) fell to near zero or even negative at points, meaning the central bank had promised to deliver hard currency it didn't actually hold unencumbered. This reserve depletion further reduced the CBRT's credibility, since a central bank with no net reserves cannot credibly defend the currency against speculative pressure.

### Channel 4: the bond market — yields as a real-time credibility gauge

The Turkish 10-year government bond yield is perhaps the clearest single-number summary of institutional credibility during this period. Its movements tell the story in compressed form.

In March 2021, under Ağbal, the 10-year yield had fallen to approximately 12% — still high by global standards, but dramatically lower than the 16-17% level it had traded at before his appointment. This reflected genuine market confidence: investors were willing to accept less yield because they trusted the policy direction.

After Ağbal's firing, the 10-year yield spiked back to approximately 17-18% within days. Over the next two years, under Kavcıoğlu's heterodox regime, yields did something counterintuitive: they initially *fell* as the policy rate was cut, because the CBRT was simultaneously using regulation to force domestic banks to hold government bonds (reducing their market yields artificially). By May 2023, just before the election, the 10-year yield had been suppressed to approximately 9.1% — an absurd level for a country with 60%+ inflation.

This suppression masked the real cost: it was only possible because domestic banks were ordered to hold the paper. Foreign investors, by contrast, had largely exited Turkish local currency bonds by this point. The artificially suppressed yield was not a market signal — it was a regulatory artifact.

After the June 2023 U-turn, the 10-year yield rapidly normalized. It rose to 25% by June 2023, 30% by September, and peaked at approximately 40% by January 2024. This rapid rise reflected two things simultaneously: genuine monetary tightening (the policy rate was being raised toward the same level), and the unwinding of the distortions that had suppressed yields during the heterodox period. Domestic banks were freed to sell their bond holdings at market rates, and the yield rose to reflect what the market actually required to hold Turkish lira risk.

#### Worked example: the bond price mathematics

Bonds and yields move in opposite directions — when yields rise, bond prices fall, and vice versa. This is mechanical, not subjective. Here is the arithmetic with Turkey's specific numbers.

Suppose you hold a Turkish government bond with a face value of 1,000,000 TRY, paying a fixed coupon of 12% per year (120,000 TRY annually), and maturing in 10 years. At a market yield of 12%, this bond is priced at approximately par — about 1,000,000 TRY (the coupon rate equals the market yield, so the price equals face value).

Ağbal is fired. The market yield on Turkish 10-year bonds shoots from 12% to 18%. What happens to your bond's price?

Using the standard bond pricing formula (present value of coupons plus face value, discounted at the new yield), the price of this bond at 18% is roughly:

\$\$ P = 120,000 \times \frac{1 - (1.18)^{-10}}{0.18} + \frac{1,000,000}{(1.18)^{10}} \approx 671,000 \text{ TRY} \$\$

Your 1,000,000 TRY bond is now worth only 671,000 TRY — a 32.9% price decline in lira terms. Now convert both figures to USD at the pre-event rate of 7.9 TRY/USD and the post-event rate of 9.2 TRY/USD:

- Pre-event value: 1,000,000 / 7.9 = \$126,582 USD
- Post-event value: 671,000 / 9.2 = \$72,935 USD
- Total dollar loss: \$53,647 USD — a 42.4% loss in dollar terms over two trading days.

**The lesson**: long-duration local-currency bonds in an emerging market combine interest-rate risk with currency risk. When both move against you at once — as they do in a governor-firing event — losses are not additive. They are multiplicative. This is why EM fund managers who hold long-dated Turkish bonds in dollar terms face more severe drawdowns than the TRY chart alone suggests.

## The Theoretical Framework: Why the Standard Economics Won

It is worth pausing to establish, precisely, why the orthodox framework predicted what happened in Turkey — and why Erdogan's theory failed.

The *Fisher equation* states: nominal interest rate = real interest rate + expected inflation. Rearranging: real interest rate = nominal rate - expected inflation. When Turkey's nominal rate was 8.5% and expected inflation was 60-80%, the real interest rate was approximately -50% to -72%. What does this mean in practice?

It means that the real cost of borrowing is deeply negative. If you borrow 100 Turkish liras at 8.5% annual interest, you repay 108.5 TRY in a year. But if inflation over that year is 80%, then 108.5 TRY at the end of the year has the purchasing power of only about 60 TRY at the start of the year. You borrowed 100 TRY of purchasing power and repaid only 60 TRY of purchasing power — a windfall to the borrower, paid for by the lender (in this case, ultimately by the holder of Turkish savings).

This means credit is not just cheap — it is subsidized by inflation. Businesses borrow heavily, not because the investment returns justify the cost, but because the real cost of debt is negative and the lira proceeds can be converted to harder assets (real estate, dollars, gold) before the lira further depreciates. This speculative borrowing *adds* to inflationary pressure. It is the opposite of the Erdogan theory's prediction.

The *uncovered interest rate parity* (UIP) condition in international finance predicts that if Turkey's interest rate is higher than the US rate by (say) 15 percentage points, the Turkish lira should depreciate by approximately 15% against the dollar over the same period, leaving the returns equalized for foreign investors. When Turkey cuts rates — reducing the interest differential — the UIP prediction says the lira *should* strengthen (or depreciate less). But the reality in Turkey was the opposite: cutting rates caused the lira to *depreciate faster*, because the rate cut destroyed confidence faster than the mechanical UIP effect could operate. Credibility effects dominate mechanical arbitrage in practice. This is not unusual — UIP is famously poor at predicting short-run exchange rates. But it illustrates that Erdogan's rate cuts were fighting against the fundamental forces that determine currency value.

## The 2023 U-Turn: When Pragmatism Overrode Ideology

The May 2023 Turkish presidential election was supposed to be comfortable for Erdogan. His 21-year grip on power had never been seriously threatened. But the inflation crisis — with consumer prices rising at 85% at its peak, household purchasing power destroyed, and the lira at less than a quarter of its 2018 value — had genuinely eroded support. The main opposition candidate, Kemal Kilicdaroglu, came within a few percentage points of forcing a first-round outright win.

Erdogan survived into a second round and won with 52.2% of the vote. But the narrow margin appears to have been the signal he needed that economic pragmatism was required. Within weeks of the election result, the policy reversal began.

### The Simsek-Erkan-Karahan succession

The key appointments were:

**Mehmet Simsek** was named Finance Minister in June 2023. Simsek is not a career bureaucrat — he had previously served as Finance Minister and Deputy Prime Minister under Erdogan from 2009 to 2018, and after leaving government had worked at Merrill Lynch's European team. His appointment was a clear market signal: Simsek had previously presided over a period of relative macroeconomic stability and had credibility with foreign investors. His return read as a genuine commitment to orthodox policy.

**Hafize Gaye Erkan** was named CBRT governor in June 2023. She had previously been a senior executive at First Republic Bank in the United States and had a conventional market-economy background. She resigned in February 2024 amid what were described as management disputes.

**Fatih Karahan** became governor in February 2024 and is the architect of the final orthodoxy restoration. He holds a PhD in economics from the University of Pennsylvania and had worked at the Federal Reserve Bank of New York before joining the CBRT's research staff. His appointment represented a decisive turn toward technocratic monetary policymaking.

### The rate-hiking sequence

The rate hikes under the new regime were aggressive and sustained:

| Date | Policy Rate | Change |
|---|---|---|
| May 2023 (pre-Simsek) | 8.5% | — |
| June 2023 | 15.0% | +650 bps |
| July 2023 | 17.5% | +250 bps |
| August 2023 | 25.0% | +750 bps |
| September 2023 | 30.0% | +500 bps |
| October 2023 | 35.0% | +500 bps |
| November 2023 | 40.0% | +500 bps |
| January 2024 | 45.0% | +250 bps |
| March 2024 | 50.0% | +500 bps |

Sources: CBRT Monetary Policy Committee press releases, 2023-2024.

The total hiking cycle from the trough of 8.5% to the peak of 50% was 4,150 basis points (41.5 percentage points) — one of the most aggressive tightening cycles in emerging market history over such a short period.

### The lira's response

The lira's response to the U-turn is instructive in what it *did not* do: it did not strengthen. It continued to fall, but in a controlled, predictable, and decelerating manner — which is the crucial distinction.

From roughly 19.7 TRY/USD before the election (May 2023), the lira moved to:
- 25.4 TRY/USD by June 2023 (initial adjustment as managed depreciation replaced artificial stability)
- 27.0 TRY/USD by September 2023
- 29.5 TRY/USD by December 2023
- 32.8 TRY/USD by June 2024
- 34.2 TRY/USD by December 2024

The overall depreciation from pre-U-turn to end-2024 was roughly 74% in cumulative terms. But the *rate* of depreciation slowed dramatically: in the heterodox period, the lira was losing value in sharp, disorderly lurches driven by panic and carry trade unwinds. In the orthodox period, the depreciation was steady, predictable, and consistent with Turkey's inflation differential with trading partners — which is exactly what economic theory would predict.

The 10-year bond yield tells a complementary story. It rose from the artificially suppressed 9.1% of May 2023 to 40% by January 2024, then began falling as disinflation progress became credible — dropping to roughly 26.5% by June 2024 as inflation declined from 65% to around 40-42%. This yield compression — from 40% to 26.5% — is precisely the kind of capital gain that rewards investors who enter at the peak of the rate-hiking cycle.

#### Worked example: the post-U-turn bond trade

This is the trade that sophisticated EM investors executed in mid-to-late 2023 when the Simsek appointment made the orthodox pivot credible.

Suppose you buy 10-year Turkish government bonds in September 2023, when the 10-year yield is approximately 30% and the lira is trading at 27.0 TRY/USD. You invest \$1,000,000. Converted to liras: \$1,000,000 × 27.0 = 27,000,000 TRY. You buy bonds with a face value of 27,000,000 TRY at a yield of 30%.

By June 2024, the 10-year yield has compressed to approximately 26.5% as inflation falls and policy credibility is established. What happened to your bond price?

Using approximate duration math: a 10-year bond at 30% yield has a modified duration of roughly 4.5 years (duration is shorter in high-yield environments because the large coupons return cash faster). A yield decline of 3.5 percentage points (from 30% to 26.5%) generates a price gain of approximately:

\$\$ \text{Price gain} \approx 4.5 \times 3.5\% = 15.75\% \$\$

Your 27,000,000 TRY bond is now worth approximately 27,000,000 × 1.1575 = 31,252,500 TRY.

But the lira has also depreciated from 27.0 to 32.8 TRY/USD over the same period. Converting your TRY proceeds back to dollars:

31,252,500 / 32.8 = \$952,820 USD

You also collected approximately 9 months of coupon (30% × 9/12 = 22.5% of face value):
Coupon in TRY: 27,000,000 × 22.5% = 6,075,000 TRY ≈ \$185,213 at end-period rate

Total return in USD: \$952,820 + \$185,213 - \$1,000,000 = +\$138,033 on a \$1,000,000 investment = +13.8% in USD terms over nine months.

This is a positive return despite significant lira depreciation, because the yield compression and coupon income more than offset the currency loss. The trade worked because the pace of rate hikes significantly exceeded the pace of lira depreciation in real terms — the "quality" of the depreciation improved under the orthodox regime.

**The lesson**: entering an EM bond trade after the institutional credibility event (the orthodox appointment) rather than before the crisis tends to offer an attractive risk-adjusted return, because the most disorderly phase of the crisis is past and the carry — at 30% nominal — provides a large cushion against remaining currency risk.

## Central Bank Independence as the Master Variable

Turkey's experiment provides one of the clearest natural experiments in modern economic history on what central bank independence is worth. Let us be precise about what "worth" means.

### The institutional risk premium

Every emerging-market asset carries what analysts call an *institutional risk premium* — the extra return that investors demand to compensate for the risk that the rules might change: that contracts might not be enforced, that exchange controls might be imposed, that the central bank might be pressured to monetize government debt.

This premium is normally invisible when institutions are functioning well. But it becomes shockingly visible when they break down. In Turkey's case, the institutional shock of March 2021 — when Ağbal was fired at 3:30 a.m. — repriced the institutional risk premium in about 48 hours. Before the firing, Turkish assets were priced as if the central bank's independence was (tentatively) credible. After the firing, they were priced as if it was irrelevant — because it had just been demonstrated to be irrelevant.

Quantifying this premium is approximate, but the market data gives us a framework. Turkish 10-year yields under Ağbal fell to approximately 12% against a US 10-year rate of about 1.5%, implying a Turkey sovereign spread of roughly 10.5 percentage points. After the firing, Turkish 10-year yields rose toward 18%, implying a spread of about 16.5 percentage points — a 6 percentage point widening in the pure institutional risk premium over 48 hours. On a 10-year bond, 6 percentage points of additional yield implies a price decline of roughly 35-40%, which is broadly consistent with the observed market moves.

### Why independence is not just about inflation

The consequences of central bank independence extend well beyond inflation control. Consider the full list of economic outcomes that depend on institutional credibility:

**Long-term borrowing costs**: When markets trust the central bank's inflation commitment, long-term interest rates stay lower because investors don't require a large inflation risk premium to lend for 10 or 20 years. Turkey's government paid dramatically more to borrow during the heterodox period, both from domestic and international creditors.

**Exchange rate stability and trade**: A volatile currency raises the cost of international trade — importers and exporters must either absorb uncertainty or buy expensive hedges. Turkey's export competitiveness, despite benefiting in theory from a weaker lira (cheaper goods in foreign markets), was partially offset by the disruption that extreme volatility caused to supply chains and contract pricing.

**Credit availability for businesses**: When the banking system is under stress — with high non-performing loans from the inflation shock, uncertainty about depositor behavior, and regulatory pressure to hold government bonds at suppressed yields — credit availability to small and medium enterprises contracts. This happened in Turkey: real credit growth to the private sector fell sharply during the heterodox period, despite nominal rates being low.

**Wage-price spirals**: When workers expect high and accelerating inflation, they demand larger nominal wage increases. When businesses grant those increases, they raise prices to cover the higher labor costs. This creates a *wage-price spiral* that is very difficult to break without a recession-inducing rate hike. Turkey experienced exactly this: by 2023, minimum wage increases were running at 50%+ annually, which fed into services inflation and made disinflation slower and more painful.

## Common Misconceptions

### "Low interest rates are always good for growth"

This is the most common confusion, and Erdogan's experiment shows why it fails. Low rates stimulate growth when the economy has spare capacity and inflation is low or below target. In that context, cheaper credit encourages investment and consumption, and the economy expands without generating inflation. But when inflation is already running at 60-80%, the economy does not have spare capacity — it has excess demand. Adding more stimulus via rate cuts does not increase production (that requires capital, labor, and time); it just increases the money competing for the existing goods, pushing prices higher. Low rates in a high-inflation environment do not produce growth; they produce hyperinflationary dynamics.

### "Turkey's inflation was caused by global factors, not domestic policy"

This is a partial truth used to dodge the full picture. Yes, global commodity prices rose sharply in 2021-2022, driven by supply-chain disruptions, the Russia-Ukraine war, and the post-COVID demand surge. This added perhaps 10-15 percentage points to Turkey's inflation compared to what purely domestic factors would have produced. But Turkey's inflation at its peak was 85.5% — far above every comparable EM economy. Egypt's inflation peaked at roughly 40%, Brazil's at roughly 12%, and India's at roughly 7-8% over the same period. The domestic policy component — the rate cuts, the lira collapse, the FX reserve depletion — explains the exceptional difference between Turkey and its peers. The global excuse does not hold when Turkey's own outcome was three to five times worse than its peers facing the same global shocks.

### "The carry trade is essentially free money at high EM yields"

It might look that way in a spreadsheet comparing Turkish 19% yields to US 1% yields. In practice, the carry trade in an institutionally fragile EM is one of the riskiest bets in global finance. The realized return from holding Turkish lira assets from 2019 to 2023 was catastrophic in dollar terms, despite the nominally high yields, because currency depreciation compounded annually against the carry. A straightforward hedged carry position (buying Turkish lira bonds, hedging the currency risk with FX forwards) sounds safer but in practice becomes prohibitively expensive or impossible to execute when the lira's forward curve prices in 30-40% annual depreciation — the hedge cost eats all the yield. The "carry" is only free money if the currency is stable, which is only the case if the central bank is independent and credible.

### "Once inflation reaches 80%, it's impossible to bring down without a depression"

Turkey's own experience refutes this. While the disinflation from 85.5% to 40% took roughly two years of painful high real interest rates, it was achieved without a formal recession (Turkey's GDP growth remained positive, albeit below potential, in 2023-2024). The key was the institutional credibility restoration: once markets believed that the CBRT would hold rates high until inflation fell, inflation expectations began declining, which made the actual disinflation easier. Contrast this with Argentina, which has faced repeated inflation-stabilization attempts without institutional credibility restoration — and has consequently repeatedly failed. The institutional variable is the difference.

### "Political interference only affects the currency, not the real economy"

This underestimates the transmission. Turkey's real GDP per capita, measured in US dollars, fell from roughly \$9,600 in 2013 to approximately \$9,100 in 2023 — a decade of zero progress in dollar terms, driven entirely by the lira's collapse against the dollar. Real purchasing power of Turkish workers declined sharply as lira wage increases failed to keep pace with lira inflation. Small businesses that imported inputs saw their margins compressed by the lira's fall. Fixed-income savers (particularly retirees on lira pensions) saw their real wealth decimated. The political interference affected every Turkish household, not just the FX traders watching the lira chart.

## How It Shows Up in Real Markets

### The "3 a.m. decree" as a trading trigger

Professional EM investors have developed what is informally known as the "3 a.m. decree" protocol for Turkey: when a major policy announcement comes outside business hours (particularly on a Friday night or weekend), it is almost invariably negative for Turkish assets and the correct initial response is to sell the lira and sell Turkish bonds. This is because positive surprises (an unexpectedly hawkish rate decision, a credible new governor) tend to be communicated during business hours with a controlled press release, while panic-driven or politically driven changes tend to arrive as overnight decrees. The time of day of the announcement has genuine information content in the Turkish political context.

The Ağbal firing is the canonical example: 3:30 a.m. local time, presidential decree, no explanation until later in the day. The market reaction was precisely what a rational investor who had internalized this protocol would execute: sell TRY, sell Turkish bonds, buy USD.

### The "Simsek premium" after the 2023 election

In the weeks following Erdogan's May 2023 narrow election victory, markets were uncertain about the policy direction. The lira had been held artificially stable pre-election but was clearly misaligned. When Simsek's appointment was announced, the initial move was counterintuitive: the lira fell sharply (from roughly 20 TRY/USD to 25 TRY/USD) because markets correctly understood that the KKM currency protection scheme would have to be unwound and the artificially suppressed exchange rate would have to be allowed to adjust. But for medium-term investors, the Simsek appointment was a buy signal for TRY-denominated bonds, because it signaled that high nominal yields would be maintained and the carry would be credibly sustained.

Investors who bought 2-year Turkish government bonds in June 2023 at yields of approximately 20-25% (which rose as the rate cycle began) earned positive dollar-adjusted returns through 2024 despite continued lira depreciation, because the coupon income exceeded the currency drag.

### The Turkish lira as an EM institutional indicator

Because Turkey's story is so well-documented and the cause-and-effect relationship is so clear, the TRY/USD rate is now widely used by emerging-market strategists as a proxy for EM institutional risk more broadly. When Turkey's institutional framework is sound (credible independent CB), EM institutional risk is priced lower globally. When it breaks down, EM institutional risk re-prices higher across the asset class. This is not because Turkey is systemically important (its economy is about the size of the Netherlands') but because it provides such a clean real-time signal: the TRY/USD rate and Turkey 10-year yield move within hours of any institutional change, giving EM investors a live indicator.

#### Worked example: the governor-firing signal — position sizing for the event

Suppose you run an EM macro fund with \$500 million in assets. You have some exposure to Turkey — roughly \$25 million (5% of AUM) in a diversified TRY-denominated government bond portfolio. It's March 19, 2021. You know that:

1. Ağbal has been hiking rates against Erdogan's stated preferences.
2. The lira has strengthened from 8.5 to 7.5 per dollar since his appointment.
3. Turkish bonds have rallied significantly.
4. Erdogan has publicly complained about the rate hikes in recent weeks.

Your risk model tells you that the probability of an Ağbal firing in the next 90 days is elevated — call it 25%. The expected lira move on a firing is approximately -12% to -15% based on the Çetinkaya and Uysal precedents (and significantly worse given the credibility Ağbal has built). Your expected dollar loss on your \$25 million position if a firing occurs is approximately:

\$25M × 13% (midpoint currency loss) × 1.0 (holding full position) = -\$3.25M

Probability-weighted expected loss: 25% × \$3.25M = \$812,500

Against your total portfolio of \$500M, this is not catastrophic — 0.16% of AUM. But your actual position risk is concentrated and can generate the full \$3.25M loss on the firing event itself in a 48-hour window. If you are running on a 5% stop-loss per position, the 13% currency move exceeds your stop before you can exit.

The correct pre-event response is to reduce position size. If you cut your Turkey exposure from \$25 million to \$10 million before the event:

Expected loss from a firing: \$10M × 13% = -\$1.3M (probability-weighted: \$325,000)

The cost of the hedge (opportunity cost of holding less Turkey exposure that might not fire): you miss approximately 90 days of carry at 15% annualized = \$15M × 15% × 90/365 = \$554,000.

This is the core trade-off: reducing position size costs you carry income but reduces your tail risk. In institutional fragility situations, where the downside scenario involves a sudden, disorderly 10-15% move with limited ability to exit (liquidity in Turkish bonds evaporates instantly on governor firings), the correct risk management choice is typically to run below full position.

**The lesson**: political tail risks in EM are not symmetric — the downside move on an institutional failure is far larger and faster than any upside from carry accumulation. Size accordingly.

## How to Trade It: The Playbook

Trading Turkey — and more broadly, EM assets where central bank independence is under political stress — requires a framework that integrates institutional monitoring with market timing. Here is the structured approach.

### Signal 1: Monitor political communication about monetary policy

The single most important leading indicator of a Turkish governor firing has consistently been Erdogan's public statements about interest rates. When he says "rates will come down" or attacks "high interest rate lovers" in speeches, the probability of an impending firing rises. Track his speeches and interview transcripts. When the rhetoric intensifies — particularly around political pressure periods like elections, budget cycles, or economic slowdowns — reduce TRY exposure.

This sounds obvious, but many EM fund managers in 2021 were caught by Ağbal's firing despite the extensive public record of Erdogan's views, because Ağbal's 132-day tenure had begun to normalize the notion that things had changed. The base rate of governor firings (three in two years) should have kept risk models on high alert throughout.

### Signal 2: The overnight decree rule

As discussed: major Turkish policy announcements outside market hours are almost always negative. Implement a standing protocol in your risk management system: if a Turkey-related presidential decree arrives outside of 9 a.m. to 5 p.m. Istanbul time (UTC+3), reduce TRY exposure by 50% immediately and evaluate the announcement as it unfolds.

### Signal 3: The credibility restoration signal

When a credible orthodox appointment is made (a recognizable name with a conventional market-economy track record), the playbook reverses:

1. Expect an initial lira adjustment lower as artificially suppressed exchange rates are allowed to normalize.
2. Buy medium-duration TRY bonds (2-5 year maturity) after the initial selloff, when nominal yields are highest.
3. Hold through the rate-hiking cycle, collecting the high carry.
4. Exit as yields compress (price appreciation) and when inflation has fallen sufficiently that the real rate is moving from negative toward positive.

The Simsek/Karahan appointment in June 2023 is the template. The entry point (buying after the initial selloff in June 2023 at 20-25% yields) produced positive dollar-adjusted returns through 2024 despite lira depreciation, because the carry more than compensated.

### Signal 4: The "real rate crossover"

A useful quantitative signal for entry and exit is the real interest rate trajectory. When Turkey's real rate (nominal policy rate minus trailing 12-month CPI) crosses from deeply negative toward zero and eventually positive, the orthodox cycle is working. When the real rate is -70%, the lira is still in crisis mode. When it approaches 0%, the situation is stabilizing. When it turns positive — likely in 2025 as inflation falls toward the 20-30% range against a 40-50% policy rate — the lira should begin appreciating in real terms even as it nominally depreciates.

Investors who time entry using the real rate crossover — entering when real rates turn from negative to neutral — tend to get the best risk-adjusted positioning: the crisis is clearly past, but the yield compression opportunity is still ahead.
