> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The 2018-19 US-China trade war raised average US tariffs on Chinese goods from 3% to 21%, cost US farmers \$4.8 billion per year, and was "resolved" by a Phase One deal that China only 85% fulfilled — yet Biden kept 90% of the tariffs anyway.
>
> - The Shanghai Composite fell 24.6% in 2018 — its worst year in a decade — while the S&P lost 6.2%, illustrating the asymmetry of tariff shock on the initiating vs. target country.
> - China offset approximately 30-40% of the tariff impact by deliberately weakening the RMB 8% against the dollar between January and October 2018.
> - The biggest supply chain winner was Vietnam: FDI surged 35% in 2018 as Apple, Samsung, and Nike accelerated factory relocations away from China.
> - Phase One required China to buy \$200 billion in additional US goods by end of 2021; China bought \$170 billion. No enforcement mechanism existed, and no structural issue was resolved.

On the morning of July 6, 2018, the first shot of the modern trade war was fired. At 12:01 AM Eastern Time, the United States imposed 25% tariffs on \$34 billion worth of Chinese goods — 818 product lines covering industrial machinery, electronics components, and motor vehicles. Within minutes, China's Ministry of Commerce announced it was imposing equivalent retaliatory tariffs on \$34 billion in US goods, targeting soybeans, automobiles, pork, and aquatic products.

By the close of trading that day, the S&P 500 had shrugged — it fell just 0.5%. The market had been pricing in the escalation since the March 2018 investigation announcement. But what came over the next 18 months — six more tariff rounds, an RMB depreciation of 11%, a \$24.6% collapse in the Shanghai Composite, a \$28 billion US farmer bailout, and a "deal" that fixed nothing but stopped the bleeding — produced one of the most consequential market laboratory experiments in modern financial history.

The 2018-19 US-China trade war is the first time a developed-market superpower attempted to use tariffs as a geopolitical weapon against another major economy while markets were deep, liquid, and globally integrated. The pricing signals were extraordinarily informative. Understanding what moved, how much, how fast, and which signals were reliable gives you the analytical toolkit for every trade war that follows.

## Foundations: The Political Backdrop and the Structural Grievances

Every trade war has a political origin story. The 2018-19 US-China confrontation had three overlapping origin stories running simultaneously, each appealing to a different political coalition.

**The Manufacturing Deficit Story** was the populist core. The US goods trade deficit with China reached \$375 billion in 2017 — roughly three times larger than the next biggest bilateral deficit (with the EU at \$151 billion). For Trump's Rust Belt political coalition, this number was simple evidence: China was winning because it was cheating, and the deficit was the proof. The economic nuance — that deficits reflect macroeconomic savings-investment imbalances and not necessarily unfair practices — was politically inert. What mattered was the narrative: China took your factory, and we're going to take it back.

**The IP Theft Story** was the corporate establishment's version of the grievance. The USTR's 2018 Section 301 investigation documented four specific practices: (1) forced technology transfer as a condition of access to Chinese markets, (2) discriminatory and non-market licensing restrictions, (3) state-directed acquisition of US companies to acquire technology, and (4) state-sponsored cyber theft of commercial secrets. The annual cost estimate: \$225-600 billion per year. This grievance had genuine substance — American companies operating in China in joint ventures were systematically required to transfer core IP to their Chinese partners as a market entry condition. Silicon Valley had been complaining about it for 15 years; the Trump administration gave it legal teeth.

**The "Made in China 2025" Alarm** was the national security establishment's version. Released in 2015, Made in China 2025 was a State Council industrial policy plan to achieve 70% domestic content in 10 advanced manufacturing sectors by 2025: next-generation IT, high-end numerical control machinery and robotics, aerospace equipment, maritime equipment and high-tech ships, advanced rail equipment, energy-saving and new-energy vehicles, power equipment, new materials, biopharmaceuticals and high-performance medical devices, and agricultural machinery equipment. The plan was explicit that state subsidies, preferential credit, and government procurement would be used to displace foreign (mostly US) companies in these sectors.

For the national security community, Made in China 2025 was not just an industrial policy document — it was a strategic competition manifesto. If China achieved 70% domestic content in semiconductors, aerospace, and advanced manufacturing, the US would face a peer competitor that was not just militarily powerful but technologically self-sufficient. This is the thread that ran directly from the 2018 trade war to the 2022-2025 chip export controls and the broader technology decoupling that followed.

**China's political backdrop** was equally important. Xi Jinping had consolidated power at the 19th Party Congress in October 2017, eliminating the two-term limit and establishing "Xi Jinping Thought" as Party doctrine. Domestically, this created enormous political pressure to not appear weak in the face of American pressure. Made in China 2025 was a nationally celebrated ambition; any trade deal requiring its rollback would be read domestically as a humiliation. Xi's red lines were clear from the beginning: purchase commitments (politically manageable) could be conceded; structural policy changes could not.

Understanding these political constraints explains why the trade war ended with Phase One rather than a comprehensive deal. The mathematics of political survival — for both sides — produced the precise outcome that markets eventually priced: a temporary truce, not a peace treaty.

## The Seven Tariff Rounds: A Play-by-Play

Let's walk through each of the seven major escalation events in precise detail, because the sequence and the size of each round determined the market pricing at each stage.

**Round 1: July 6, 2018 — List 1, \$34 billion at 25%.**

The first tariff list covered 818 product lines dominated by industrial machinery, electronics components, automobiles, and aerospace parts. The product selection was deliberate — it targeted Chinese exports that embodied US technology (the IP theft grievance) rather than consumer goods, which would immediately visible to US consumers and create political blowback. China retaliated immediately with \$34 billion in tariffs on US goods, targeting soybeans, autos, pork, sorghum, and aquatic products.

The soybean targeting was political genius. US soybean production is overwhelmingly concentrated in Iowa, Illinois, Indiana, and Minnesota — all states with Republican senators. China's 25% tariff on US soybeans immediately created political pressure from Republican agricultural constituencies on the Trump administration to reach a deal. The US average tariff on Chinese goods moved from 3.1% to approximately 6.7% after Round 1.

**Round 2: August 23, 2018 — List 2, \$16 billion at 25%.**

List 2 covered 279 product lines including semiconductors, chemicals, plastics, and motorcycles. The semiconductor inclusion was significant — it targeted inputs to US technology products being assembled in China for re-export to the US. China matched with \$16 billion in retaliatory tariffs on US crude oil, medical equipment, and buses.

**Round 3: September 24, 2018 — List 3, \$200 billion at 10% (later 25%).**

This was the escalation that changed the nature of the trade war. A \$200 billion tariff list dwarfed all previous actions combined. Because China's total exports to the US were approximately \$550 billion, this list — combined with the previous \$50 billion — now covered nearly 46% of all Chinese exports to the US. At 10%, the tariff was deliberately set below 25% to allow room for negotiation while still imposing real costs.

The RMB reacted immediately. USD/CNH moved from 6.70 in July to 6.90 by mid-September and 6.97 by October — an 11.5% depreciation from the January 2018 level of 6.25. This was the deliberate currency adjustment I described earlier: China was simultaneously absorbing tariff pressure in its export revenues and partially offsetting it by letting the currency weaken.

The US average tariff on Chinese goods moved to approximately 12.4% after Round 3.

**Round 4: May 10, 2019 — List 3 raised to 25%.**

The May 2019 escalation was triggered by the collapse of negotiations. In April 2019, the two sides appeared close to a deal — US Treasury Secretary Mnuchin had traveled to Beijing and expressed optimism. Then the US side (apparently after reviewing the draft text) concluded that China had "reneged" on key commitments in the written agreement. Trump announced via tweet on the evening of May 5 that List 3 tariffs would rise from 10% to 25%.

The market reaction to the tweet was immediate and dramatic (see the worked example in the companion post for the precise P&L). The VIX spiked from 15 to 23 in two days. The S&P 500 fell approximately 7% over the following two weeks. The US average tariff moved to approximately 17.5%.

**Round 5: September 1, 2019 — List 4A, \$300 billion at 15%.**

List 4A was the nuclear option in pure dollar volume — it began to impose tariffs on consumer goods (electronics, clothing, footwear, toys) that had been deliberately excluded from earlier lists to avoid direct consumer price impact. The 15% rate was lower than 25%, again preserving negotiating room. The inclusion of smartphones (Apple iPhone production was 90%+ in China) and consumer electronics made the tariff politically salient to a much wider constituency than industrial machinery.

The gold market responded most dramatically to Round 5 — gold rose from \$1,410/oz in early August 2019 to \$1,560/oz by September 4, a 10.6% gain in one month, as the safe-haven bid intensified. The US 10-year yield fell to 1.47% — within spitting distance of the all-time record low — as markets priced a serious growth shock.

**Round 6: December 15, 2019 — List 4A Paused.**

On December 13, 2019, the White House announced that the scheduled escalation of List 4A from 15% to 25% (which would have taken effect December 15) was suspended as both sides had "agreed in principle" to Phase One terms. This was the first genuine de-escalation signal in 18 months. The S&P 500 rallied 2.6% in the following week. The RMB appreciated from 7.03 to 6.93 in one week.

**Round 7: January 15, 2020 — Phase One Signed.**

Phase One was signed at the White House with considerable ceremony. China committed to purchase \$200 billion in additional US goods over 2020-2021 (above the 2017 baseline) broken into four categories: \$52 billion in energy, \$77 billion in manufactured goods, \$32 billion in agriculture, and \$38 billion in services. The US agreed to halve List 4A tariffs from 15% to 7.5% and not impose new tariffs during the commitment period. Lists 1, 2, and 3 (25% tariffs) remained in place.

The S&P 500 gained 1.8% on the signing day. The market's muted reaction to the "deal" (relative to the sharp reactions at each escalation) was itself informative — experienced market participants recognized that Phase One was a ceasefire, not a resolution.

## The Market Scorecard by Asset Class

The 2018-19 trade war was a controlled experiment in how a large-scale tariff shock propagates across asset classes. Here is the full scorecard.

**Equities — Summary.**

The equity scoreboard was dominated by asymmetry. The US stock market (S&P 500) fell 6.2% in 2018 — its worst annual performance since the 2008 financial crisis. But this aggregate masks substantial within-market variation. The Nasdaq, with its higher concentration of China-exposed tech companies (Apple, Qualcomm, Intel, Broadcom) performed slightly better at −3.9% because the AI-driven earnings growth of non-China-exposed hyperscalers partially offset the China headwind.

The Shanghai Composite fell 24.6% in 2018 — its worst annual performance in a decade. China's equity market experienced the full force of the tariff shock compounded by domestic credit tightening (the PBOC's deleveraging campaign from 2017-2018 was actively reducing credit to property developers and shadow banking entities at the same time tariff pressure was hitting export industries). The two headwinds combined to produce a historic drawdown.

Hong Kong's Hang Seng Index fell 13.6% in 2018, reflecting both the China economic slowdown and the early emergence of the political unrest that would explode in full in 2019.

**FX — The Currency Offset.**

The RMB depreciation was the single most important analytical development of the trade war for FX traders. Understanding its mechanics is essential for correctly sizing any future trade-war currency position.

![Average US tariff on Chinese imports bar chart from 3 percent pre-war baseline to 21 percent peak](/imgs/blogs/us-china-trade-war-2018-19-tariff-rounds-and-market-scorecards-2.png)

The PBOC manages the RMB through a "managed float" system — it sets a daily reference rate (the "fix") and allows the currency to trade within a 2% band around the fix. The fix itself is set with reference to the previous day's close, a basket of currencies, and a "counter-cyclical factor" that the PBOC can use to resist market moves it considers excessive.

In 2018, the PBOC let the fix drift systematically weaker. The counter-cyclical factor, which had been used to resist depreciation in 2017 (when the RMB was under downward pressure from US rate hikes), was effectively disabled in 2018, allowing the market's fundamental preference for a weaker RMB to express itself through the fix.

The result: USD/CNH (the offshore RMB rate, which is more freely traded) moved from 6.25 in January 2018 to 6.97 in October 2018. That is an 11.5% RMB depreciation in 9 months — one of the largest managed currency moves by a major economy in modern history.

![USD per CNH exchange rate 2018-2020 showing deliberate RMB depreciation during trade war period](/imgs/blogs/us-china-trade-war-2018-19-tariff-rounds-and-market-scorecards-3.png)

**Bonds — Flight to Safety.**

The US 10-year Treasury yield peaked at 3.23% in October 2018 and fell to 1.47% by September 2019 — a 176 basis point decline in 11 months. This was one of the steepest 11-month declines in US long-term yields since the 2008 financial crisis.

The yield decline had two components: (1) a genuine flight-to-safety bid as equity volatility increased and growth expectations were revised lower, and (2) a Fed policy shift. The Federal Reserve, which had been raising rates in 2018 (four rate hikes: March, June, September, December), reversed course in 2019 — cutting rates three times (July, September, October) in response to the economic uncertainty created by the trade war. The Fed's own communications explicitly cited "trade policy uncertainty" as a factor in their decision to cut rates in a period when the unemployment rate was near historical lows.

This was a significant market signal: the trade war forced the Fed to cut rates despite a near-record-low unemployment rate of 3.5%. That is a measure of the tariff shock's severity — it was sufficient to redirect monetary policy.

**Commodities — The Soybean Weapon and the Gold Bid.**

The soybean story is the most politically revealing part of the 2018-19 trade war and the most instructive example of commodity targeting as geopolitical leverage.

Pre-war: China imported approximately \$12.4 billion in US soybeans per year, representing about 60% of total US soy exports and approximately one-third of China's soybean import needs. The US soybean price was approximately \$10.40 per bushel in early 2018.

China's 25% tariff on US soybeans (Round 1, July 2018) changed the economics of the bilateral soy trade overnight. A US bushel of soybeans at \$10.40 plus 25% tariff = \$13.00 effective cost for Chinese buyers. Brazilian soybeans at approximately \$9.80/bushel (with a small freight premium of \$0.50 for the longer Pacific crossing) = \$10.30 — suddenly \$2.70 cheaper than tariffed US soy. Chinese crushers and animal feed producers shifted their sourcing to Brazil within months.

US soybean prices fell from \$10.40/bushel to a low of approximately \$8.00/bushel by the end of 2018 — a 23% decline. US soybean exports to China collapsed from \$12.4 billion in 2017 to approximately \$3.1 billion in 2018 — a 75% decline.

The scale of the disruption required a massive government response. The Trump administration announced two separate Market Facilitation Programs (MFP): \$12 billion in 2018 and \$16 billion in 2019. Total farm bailout from 2018-2019: \$28 billion. This amount was larger than the entire annual value of US soybean exports to China before the trade war (\$12.4 billion). The US government spent more than double the value of the China soybean trade to compensate farmers for losing it.

Brazil was the direct winner. Brazilian soybean exports to China increased from approximately 53 million metric tons in 2017 to 75 million metric tons in 2018 — a 42% increase in one year. Brazilian agricultural land under soybean cultivation expanded rapidly. By 2020, Brazil had permanently displaced the US as China's primary soybean supplier, a trade position that has not reversed despite Phase One's agricultural purchase commitments.

Gold was the other major commodity winner. Gold was approximately flat in 2018 (in fact slightly negative at −1.9%) as the dollar strengthened. But in 2019, as the trade war uncertainty prolonged and the Fed cut rates, gold rose 18.3% — one of its best years in the post-2008 era. The 2019 gold rally was driven by three simultaneous forces: US real interest rates falling as the Fed cut nominal rates, the dollar weakening as rate differential narrowed, and safe-haven demand intensifying as trade-war-driven economic uncertainty grew.

## The Yuan Depreciation Offset: How China Ran the Currency Cushion

The RMB depreciation deserves deeper examination because it illustrates a fundamental truth about tariff wars: they are simultaneously currency wars, and any analysis that ignores the currency response misunderstands the actual economics of the shock.

A 25% tariff on \$200 billion in Chinese goods imposes a direct cost of approximately \$50 billion per year on Chinese exporters (or more precisely, on the Chinese sellers who must lower their USD prices to maintain competitiveness, or on US importers who pay more, or on a combination of both).

RMB depreciation of 8% against the dollar means that Chinese exporters who earn their revenues in USD receive 8% more RMB for every dollar earned. On \$200 billion of goods, an 8% RMB depreciation = \$16 billion offset in RMB terms. The tariff's effective net cost to China: \$50 billion − \$16 billion = \$34 billion. The currency buffer reduced the tariff's impact by approximately 32%.

![Market scorecard matrix showing winners and losers in equities FX bonds commodities by country](/imgs/blogs/us-china-trade-war-2018-19-tariff-rounds-and-market-scorecards-4.png)

This is why the Trump administration's designation of China as a "currency manipulator" in August 2019 (when USD/CNH crossed 7.00 for the first time since 2008) was not merely rhetoric — it was economically accurate, even though the IMF's formal manipulation criteria were not strictly met. China was deliberately running a currency cushion against the tariff shock.

The political tension this created was significant: if China devalued the RMB enough to fully offset the tariff impact, the US would either have to impose even higher tariffs (tariff-currency spiral) or add a currency manipulation penalty on top of the existing tariffs. Neither option was economically clean, which is why the Phase One deal included explicit commitments on currency policy — China committed to refrain from "competitive devaluation" and to "enhance exchange rate transparency."

#### Worked example:

**The RMB depreciation trade — USD/CNH long position.**

Trader background: You are a macro fund manager watching the US-China trade war escalate. You believe the PBOC will allow the RMB to weaken as a tariff offset. You decide to go long USD/CNH (long the dollar against the offshore RMB) on the first tariff announcement.

Position: Long \$1 million USD/CNH at the entry rate of 6.63 (July 6, 2018, the day List 1 tariffs take effect).

By September 24, 2018 (List 3 announcement day), USD/CNH had moved to 6.90.

Gain on position: (6.90 − 6.63) / 6.63 = 4.07%.

P&L on \$1M position: \$1,000,000 × 0.0407 = **\$40,700**.

The carry cost: CNH deposits earn approximately 3-4% annualized in this period, while USD deposits earn approximately 1.5-2%. The carry cost of being long USD and short CNH is roughly (3.5% − 1.75%) = 1.75% annualized. For a 2.5-month hold (July 6 to September 24): 1.75% × (2.5/12) = 0.36% = \$3,600.

Net P&L: \$40,700 − \$3,600 = **\$37,100** on a \$1M position over 2.5 months (3.7% net return).

The trade continued working through October 2018 (CNH peaked at 6.97). Entry at 6.63 and exit at 6.97 = 5.1% gain on the FX move, net of approximately 0.4% carry for the full 3.5-month hold = approximately **4.7% net return** (\$47,000 on \$1M).

## The Soybean Weapon: Agricultural Targeting as Political Leverage

The soybean example deserves its own section because it illustrates something that pure trade economics misses: agricultural commodity targeting in a trade war is simultaneously an economic weapon and a political weapon, and the political weapon is often more important than the economic weapon.

#### Worked example:

**The soybean tariff pass-through and US farmer revenue loss.**

Pre-tariff baseline: US soybean production approximately 4.5 billion bushels per year. Exports to China: approximately 2 billion bushels at \$10.40/bushel = \$20.8 billion in gross export revenue. (Note: the \$12.4 billion figure cited earlier refers to the *trade data* value which uses a different methodology; the farmgate calculation gives a larger number.)

China's 25% tariff: Effective Chinese import cost of US soy rises from \$10.40 to \$13.00/bushel. Brazilian soy available at approximately \$10.30/bushel (cost + freight). Chinese buyers shift to Brazil.

US soybean price impact: US soy price falls from \$10.40 to \$8.00/bushel as the approximately 2 billion bushels previously destined for China must find alternative buyers in a market without China's demand. A 23% price decline.

US farmer revenue loss: 4.5 billion total US bushels × \$2.40/bu price decline = **\$10.8 billion per year** in total farmer revenue reduction across all US soy production (even the portion not sold to China is priced at the new lower global equilibrium).

That \$10.8 billion annual revenue loss is why the government needed a \$28 billion, two-year farm bailout program. The tariff effectively imposed a \$10.8 billion/year tax on US soybean farmers, and the government spent \$14 billion/year to compensate them.

The political targeting was precise. The states most dependent on soybean exports to China — Iowa, Illinois, Indiana, Minnesota — were all states where Republican Senate incumbents would face competitive re-elections in 2018 and 2020. By threatening their agricultural base, China was sending a direct political signal to Republican senators: pressure your president, or we take your farmers with us.

## Phase One: The Paper Deal That Paused the War

Phase One was signed on January 15, 2020, with considerable fanfare. The economic commitments were specific: China would purchase \$200 billion more in US goods over 2020-2021 compared to the 2017 baseline, divided across:

- **Energy:** \$52.4 billion (LNG, crude oil, coal, refined products)
- **Manufacturing:** \$77.7 billion (semiconductors, aircraft, agricultural equipment, autos)
- **Agriculture:** \$32 billion (soybeans, pork, beef, wheat, corn)
- **Services:** \$37.9 billion (financial services, travel, intellectual property licenses)

Total: \$200 billion over two years = \$100 billion per year in additional purchases, above the 2017 baseline of approximately \$186 billion in total US goods and services exports to China.

**The COVID disruption argument:** China's Phase One defenders note that COVID-19 disrupted global trade in 2020, making the purchase targets impossible to meet. This is partially true — COVID disrupted Chinese economic activity in Q1 2020 and global energy trade through much of the year. However, even accounting for COVID, the shortfall was substantial: China purchased approximately \$75 billion in 2020 against a target of approximately \$100 billion — a \$25 billion shortfall in year one. In 2021 (a strong global recovery year), China purchased approximately \$95 billion against the remaining \$100 billion target — a \$5 billion shortfall. Total two-year delivery: approximately \$170 billion against the \$200 billion commitment, or 85%.

#### Worked example:

**The Phase One purchase gap and market valuation.**

The S&P 500 gained approximately 8.1% in the week following the Phase One signing (from the December 13 announcement through January 17, 2020). The market's pre-Phase One capitalization was approximately \$29 trillion.

Market cap added by Phase One enthusiasm: \$29 trillion × 8.1% = **\$2.35 trillion**.

The actual delivered value of Phase One above the no-deal baseline: The \$170 billion China purchased represents approximately \$30 billion above China's estimated no-deal purchasing level (had the tariff war continued without any deal, China would have continued buying some US goods, perhaps \$140 billion). The Phase One deal's "excess" value to the US economy: roughly \$30 billion over two years = \$15 billion per year.

The market added \$2.35 trillion in equity value for a deal that delivered approximately \$15 billion per year in incremental US exports — a valuation multiple of approximately 157× on the deal's annual economic delivery. This extreme valuation multiple makes sense if you interpret the market as pricing the *option value of not having the trade war continue escalating* rather than the *intrinsic value of the purchase commitments themselves.* The \$2.35 trillion represents the market's estimate of the deadweight loss from continued escalation that Phase One avoided — not the cash flow from the purchase commitments.

This distinction matters because it explains why Phase One was so valuable despite being so limited: the market was not pricing the deal's content, it was pricing the absence of the alternative (continued escalation toward Scenario D — investment bans, financial sanctions, and technology decoupling).

## The Sector Winners and Losers: A Full Map

The trade war did not affect all sectors equally. Some sectors were devastated; others were transformed by the resulting supply chain reconfiguration.

**Biggest Losers: US Agriculture.**

The agricultural sector bore the most concentrated pain. Soybeans, as discussed, saw a 23% price decline. US pork exports to China fell from \$1.1 billion in 2017 to \$0.5 billion in 2018 (−55%) before partially recovering. US sorghum exports to China essentially ceased. The total agricultural impact: US farm income fell approximately \$12-15 billion in 2018 from tariff-related demand destruction. The government's \$28 billion farm support program (USDA Market Facilitation Program + trade adjustment aid) was the largest farm bailout in US history.

**Biggest Losers: Chinese Technology.**

The Chinese tech sector experienced a simultaneous compression from tariff exposure and US technology restrictions. Companies like ZTE (zero-revenue model dependent on US chips) were nearly destroyed — ZTE received a temporary shutdown order in April 2018 after violating Iran sanctions (an unrelated but contemporaneous action). Huawei was placed on the US Entity List in May 2019, cutting it off from US chip suppliers and Android software updates. Chinese consumer tech (Alibaba, Tencent, Meituan) fell 52-74% from peak to trough during the 2018-2019 period, erasing approximately \$1 trillion in market capitalization.

**Biggest Losers: US Multinationals with China Supply Chains.**

Companies with deep China manufacturing exposure (Apple, Caterpillar, Dow Chemical, GM) faced a painful two-year restructuring: either absorb tariff costs (margin compression), raise prices (demand destruction), or accelerate supply chain diversification (capex). Apple's effective tax rate on iPhones assembled in China and sold in the US rose by approximately 3-4 percentage points under full List 4A tariffs. Apple accelerated its supply chain diversification to India and Vietnam as a result.

**Biggest Winners: Vietnam.**

Vietnam was the clearest winner of the US-China trade war, for a simple reason: it was the closest substitute manufacturing hub with existing factory infrastructure, a young and cost-competitive workforce, favorable trade agreements (CPTPP member, EU-Vietnam FTA in force 2020), and political stability. The Vietnam FDI surge in 2018-2019 was extraordinary:

- FDI inflows: \$14.1 billion (2017) → \$19.1 billion (2018) → \$20.4 billion (2019 disbursed). A 37% increase in registered FDI in just one year.
- Electronics exports: \$58 billion (2017) → \$67.5 billion (2018) → \$71.2 billion (2019). Samsung's Vietnam operations alone exported approximately \$60 billion in smartphones annually from 2019 onward.
- Vietnam's bilateral trade surplus with the US surged from \$38.5 billion in 2016 to \$66.6 billion in 2019 — making Vietnam one of the largest bilateral surplus partners with the US, which ironically set it up for its own trade conflict in the 2025 Liberation Day tariffs.

**Biggest Winners: Brazil.**

Brazil gained most immediately from the soybean trade diversion. Brazilian soy exports to China rose from approximately 47 million metric tons in 2017 to approximately 68 million metric tons in 2018 — a 45% increase. The Bovespa (Brazil's main equity index) significantly outperformed other emerging markets in 2018-2019 as agricultural commodity revenues boosted Brazilian corporate earnings.

**Biggest Winners: US LNG Exporters.**

Phase One included a specific commitment for China to purchase \$52 billion in US energy products over 2020-2021. While COVID disrupted actual delivery, the Phase One energy commitments accelerated the commercialization of US LNG export contracts with Chinese buyers. US LNG exports to China, which had been minimal pre-2019, began growing. The long-term beneficiaries were US LNG terminal operators (Cheniere Energy, Sabine Pass) who could now point to Chinese demand commitments in their project financing.

**Biggest Winners: US 10-Year Treasury Bonds.**

Perhaps the clearest and most reliable trade of the 2018-19 period was long US Treasuries. The 10-year yield fell from 3.23% (October 2018 peak) to 1.47% (September 2019 trough) — a 176 basis point decline that produced approximately a 15-17% total return on a 10-year Treasury over 11 months (duration-weighted). This was driven by (1) flight-to-safety demand, (2) Fed rate cuts, and (3) reduced inflation expectations as tariffs were priced as a one-time price level shock rather than a sustained inflationary impulse.

## China's Retaliatory Targeting: The Political Economy of Retaliation

China's retaliation was not economically optimal — it was politically optimal. The distinction matters enormously.

Economically optimal retaliation would target US goods that China can easily substitute (high elasticity of substitution) and that impose maximum cost on US producers. Politically optimal retaliation targets US goods that impose maximum political cost on the political coalition that supports the trade war — regardless of whether the economics are optimal.

China chose politically optimal retaliation.

#### Worked example:

**China's retaliatory targeting efficiency — the electoral-vote analysis.**

China's primary retaliatory targets in 2018-2019: soybeans (\$12B), pork (\$1.1B), sorghum (\$1.1B), aircraft (Boeing jets, \$9B annual order book), automobiles.

The soybean-producing states: Iowa, Illinois, Indiana, Minnesota, Missouri, South Dakota, Nebraska, Kansas.

Iowa: 6 electoral votes, historically competitive, critical Trump state in 2020.
Illinois: 20 electoral votes, Democrat-leaning, but with large rural soy-producing Republican districts.
Indiana: 11 electoral votes, reliably Republican.
Minnesota: 10 electoral votes, the most competitive upper-Midwest state.

Total electoral votes in the primary soy-producing swing states: approximately 47. These same states were exactly the states Trump had flipped from Obama (Iowa, and nearly Minnesota) in 2016 and needed to hold in 2020.

The Boeing targeting was equally precise. Boeing's 737 MAX assembly is in Renton, Washington (reliably Democratic) — less politically potent. But Boeing's supply chain is distributed across congressional districts in South Carolina (reliably Republican) and several other states. China's purchase of 10 Airbus A320s (versus the previously planned Boeing 737s) directly cost Boeing approximately \$1.1 billion per order (at list price of approximately \$110 million per aircraft). The reputational cost — with China implicitly signaling it could replace Boeing's entire China backlog with Airbus — was larger.

The key analytical insight: Chinese retaliation was designed to be politically visible in Republican swing states, not just economically damaging to the US in aggregate. This is why the Trump administration's 2019 farm bailout program was so large — the political cost of leaving swing-state farmers exposed was unacceptable, so the administration spent \$28 billion to absorb the pain that China's retaliation was designed to impose.

## Common Misconceptions: What the Media Got Wrong

**Misconception 1: "The Phase One deal was a major US victory."**

The Phase One deal did not remove tariffs, did not require China to abandon Made in China 2025, did not create an enforceable dispute resolution mechanism, and did not fundamentally alter China's state-directed industrial policy. China delivered 85% of its purchase commitments (inflated by COVID-year shortfalls). Biden retained 90%+ of the Trump tariffs. If Phase One was a "major victory," it is difficult to define what a partial success would look like.

**Misconception 2: "Tariffs protect American jobs."**

A 2019 Federal Reserve study found that the US steel tariffs of 2018 saved approximately 8,700 steel-producing jobs — but cost approximately 75,000 jobs in steel-consuming industries (manufacturing, construction, auto assembly) due to higher input costs. The net employment impact of the tariffs was negative. This does not mean tariffs are never justified — there are national security and strategic industry arguments for protecting specific sectors — but the "saves jobs" framing is almost always an aggregation error that counts protected jobs while ignoring destroyed downstream jobs.

**Misconception 3: "China's stock market crashed because of US tariffs."**

The Shanghai Composite's 24.6% decline in 2018 was partly tariff-driven, but the much larger driver was China's domestic credit crunch. The PBOC's 2017-2018 deleveraging campaign was aggressively reducing leverage in the shadow banking system (trust products, wealth management products, off-balance-sheet bank lending). The corporate bond default rate tripled in 2018. Property developer stress was emerging. The trade war was a significant headwind on top of an already-strained domestic financial system — not the primary cause of the equity decline.

**Misconception 4: "Vietnam "won" the trade war."**

Vietnam gained enormous FDI and export volume during the trade war. But "winning" is relative. Vietnam's bilateral trade surplus with the US surged from \$38B (2016) to \$110B (2022), making Vietnam a target for US trade scrutiny. In 2025, the Liberation Day tariff announcement imposed a 46% tariff on Vietnamese goods — the third-highest tariff among all countries — directly because of the trade surplus that the supply-chain diversion had created. The 2019 "win" became the 2025 vulnerability.

**Misconception 5: "The trade war ended with Phase One."**

Biden retained essentially the entire Trump tariff structure. In 2024, Biden raised tariffs on Chinese EVs to 100%, solar panels to 50%, and steel/aluminum to 25%. In early 2025, Trump imposed Liberation Day tariffs that briefly hit 145% before a 90-day pause reduced them to 30%. The tariff baseline as of mid-2025 is approximately 35% — ten times the pre-trade-war level of 3.1%. The trade war never ended. It changed presidents and added new escalation layers.

## How to Trade It: The 2018-19 Playbook Applied Forward

The 2018-19 trade war produced three reliable trading patterns that have been replicated in every subsequent escalation.

**Pattern 1: Long US Treasuries at each tariff escalation.** Every major tariff announcement produced a flight-to-safety bid in US Treasuries. The 10-year yield fell at every escalation point and rose on every negotiation signal. This pattern is mechanically reliable because tariff escalations simultaneously (a) reduce growth expectations (lowering yields), (b) increase risk aversion (increasing safe-haven demand), and (c) tend to force the Fed toward accommodation (rate cut expectations support Treasuries). The invalidation: a tariff escalation that is simultaneously accompanied by strong fiscal stimulus (which would raise growth expectations and potentially offset the safety bid).

**Pattern 2: Long gold at Rung 4-5, reduce position on negotiation signal.** Gold's 18% gain in 2019 was concentrated in the period of maximum trade war uncertainty (May-September 2019). Gold underperformed when either side sent negotiation signals (gold fell 2-3% on each "progress in talks" announcement). The optimal position: accumulate gold on escalations, reduce on negotiation signals, and not chase the Phase One announcement rally.

**Pattern 3: Long Vietnam/ASEAN manufacturing ETFs on escalation, reduce on resolution.** The VN-Index fell 17% in 2018 on the initial trade war uncertainty, then outperformed significantly through 2019-2021 as the FDI surge and export growth repriced Vietnam's equity market from an emerging market discount to a "trade war winner" premium. The VanEck Vietnam ETF (VNM) and similar vehicles captured this repricing. The risk in 2025: Vietnam's surplus with the US has made it a secondary tariff target, meaning the "trade war winner" trade now carries its own tariff risk.

**Forward application — Liberation Day 2025:** The April 2025 Liberation Day tariffs followed a compressed version of the 2018-19 playbook. IEEPA emergency authority (versus Section 301) made the escalation faster and less predictable. The DXY dollar index fell 5% in two weeks (unusual — normally tariff escalation strengthens the dollar as the US is seen as "winning"). The dollar weakness was a new signal: markets were pricing the tariffs as a structural competitiveness threat to the US itself (higher consumer prices, lower foreign investment in US assets) rather than as leverage on China. The 90-day pause negotiated in May 2025 (reducing tariffs to 30%) produced the same short-squeeze pattern as every prior de-escalation.

## The Equity Market Forensics: Sector-by-Sector Scorecard

The aggregate index numbers — S&P 500 down 6.2%, Shanghai Composite down 24.6% — conceal far more than they reveal. The trade war did not depress equities uniformly; it carved the market into clean winners and losers along a single axis: revenue exposure to the bilateral US-China goods trade. The sectors that sat directly in the tariff crossfire experienced drawdowns two to four times the index average, while domestically-oriented sectors barely registered the shock. Reading the forensics by sector is what separates a usable trade-war playbook from a vague "stocks went down" narrative.

**US agriculture — the deepest, most concentrated wound.** The publicly-traded proxies for the soybean shock were the agricultural processors and equipment makers. Archer-Daniels-Midland (ADM) and Bunge, the two largest grain merchandisers, saw their shares fall 18% and 22% respectively from their 2018 highs as crush margins compressed and the China export channel collapsed. But the drawdown timeline is the instructive part: the agricultural complex did not bottom in 2018 — it ground lower through mid-2019 as each failed negotiation round (the May 2019 collapse most sharply) re-priced the probability that China would ever return as a buyer. Recovery did not begin in earnest until the December 2019 Phase One pre-announcement, and even then the recovery was incomplete: the soybean basis (US cash price relative to the Chicago futures benchmark) remained structurally depressed in the Gulf and Pacific Northwest export channels well into 2021, because the trade had been permanently re-routed to Brazil.

**US industrials — the CapEx deferral channel.** Caterpillar and Deere were the cleanest industrial-sector read on trade-war stress, because their order books are a real-time proxy for global capital-expenditure confidence. Caterpillar fell from a January 2018 high near \$170 to roughly \$112 by December 2018 — a 34% peak-to-trough drawdown, more than five times the S&P decline. The mechanism was not direct tariff cost (though Caterpillar did flag roughly \$100-200 million in incremental tariff expense per quarter); it was the second-order effect of customers deferring equipment purchases amid uncertainty. When a mining company or a construction firm cannot forecast its input costs for the next two years, it postpones the new excavator order. Deere followed the same arc, falling approximately 27% peak-to-trough, with management explicitly citing "trade tensions" as the reason farmers were delaying equipment upgrades — a vicious circle, since the same farmers were the ones losing the soybean revenue that would have funded the purchase.

**US technology — the bifurcation.** Technology was the single most analytically rich sector because it split cleanly into two cohorts. The first cohort — the China-revenue-exposed semiconductor names — was hammered. Qualcomm (QCOM), Micron (MU), and Skyworks Solutions (SWKS) derived 50-65% of their revenue from China (either direct sales or sales to Chinese OEMs assembling for global markets). These three fell 30-45% from their 2018 peaks. Micron, with approximately 57% China revenue exposure, was the textbook casualty. The second cohort — the domestically-monetized platform companies (the FANG complex: Facebook, Amazon, Netflix, Google) — was largely insulated, because their revenue came from advertising and subscriptions in markets China could not tariff. This bifurcation produced the single most reliable relative-value trade of the period.

**China equities — the divergence and the sector map.** The CSI 300 (the large-cap mainland benchmark) fell roughly 25% in 2018 even as the MSCI World index was down only about 8.7%, a divergence of more than 16 percentage points — the widest China-vs-developed-world equity gap since 2008. Within the Shanghai Composite (SHCOMP), the sector breakdown was telling: export-manufacturing and technology-hardware sectors led the decline (down 35-50%), consumer-discretionary names tied to the slowing domestic economy fell 25-35%, while defensive sectors — utilities, consumer staples, and the state-owned banks — held up far better (down only 5-15%), because their cash flows were domestic and policy-supported.

**The relative value trade.** The cleanest expression of the entire trade war thesis in equity space was: long US domestically-monetized tech growth, short China export-manufacturing and China-revenue semiconductors. This single pair captured the central asymmetry of the conflict — that the tariff shock fell hardest on the target (China's export base) and on the most China-dependent US suppliers, while sparing the US growth franchises whose moats were domestic.

#### Worked example:

**Sizing the relative-value pair — SOXX vs. CSI 300, 2018.**

Position thesis: The semiconductor complex (China-revenue-heavy) underperforms Chinese large-caps' eventual policy-supported floor, but more cleanly, you want to express "US tech holds, China exporters break." For a clean illustration, consider the realized 2018 relative move between the iShares Semiconductor ETF (SOXX) and the CSI 300.

In calendar 2018, SOXX fell approximately 9% while the CSI 300 fell approximately 25%. A trader who in January 2018 put on a market-neutral pair — long \$1,000,000 of a basket of domestically-monetized US tech and short \$1,000,000 of CSI 300 exposure (via futures or an A-shares ETF) — would have captured the spread.

Long leg P&L (domestic US tech, roughly flat to +5% for the FANG-weighted basket): assume +2% = +\$20,000.

Short leg P&L (short CSI 300, which fell 25%): +25% × \$1,000,000 = **+\$250,000**.

Combined gross P&L on the \$1M-per-leg, \$2M-gross pair: \$20,000 + \$250,000 = **\$270,000**, a 13.5% return on gross exposure.

Even the cruder version — long SOXX (−9%) against short CSI 300 (−25%) — nets +16% on the spread (−9% minus −25% = +16%), or **\$160,000** on a \$1M-per-leg basis. The relative-value framing matters because it strips out the directional "everything fell" beta and isolates the trade-war signal: the target's export base broke far harder than the initiator's growth franchises.

## The Currency Dimension: PBOC's Rate Management Playbook

The RMB was not a passive casualty of the trade war — it was an actively managed instrument of it, and the People's Bank of China (PBOC) ran a deliberate, legible playbook that an FX trader could read in real time. The earlier section quantified the depreciation offset; this section is about the mechanics of how the PBOC executed it and the specific signals that told you what the central bank was actually doing behind the managed float.

**The CNH-CNY spread as a tension gauge.** China runs two distinct exchange rates for the same currency. The onshore rate (CNY) trades inside mainland China within a tightly controlled 2% band around the daily fix and is subject to capital controls. The offshore rate (CNH), traded freely in Hong Kong and Singapore, has no band and reflects unconstrained market sentiment. The spread between them — CNH minus CNY — is one of the purest real-time gauges of trade-war tension available. When CNH trades weaker (a higher USD/CNH number) than CNY, it means offshore market participants are betting on further depreciation faster than the PBOC's controlled onshore rate will allow. During the sharpest escalation weeks of 2018 and again in August 2019, the CNH-CNY spread blew out to several hundred pips of offshore weakness, signaling that the market wanted the currency lower than Beijing was officially letting it go.

**The managed float and the fixing.** Each morning the PBOC publishes a central parity rate — the fix — and the onshore CNY may then trade up to 2% on either side of it. The fix is nominally set by reference to the prior day's close, a trade-weighted currency basket, and a discretionary "counter-cyclical factor." That counter-cyclical factor is the policy lever. By choosing where to set the fix relative to where the prior close and the basket would imply, the PBOC can lean against or with the market. Through 2018, the PBOC repeatedly set the fix weaker than the prior close implied, walking the currency down in a controlled glide that offset tariff pressure without triggering a disorderly capital-flight panic.

**The 7.00 red line.** For a decade after the 2008 crisis, USD/CNY had never traded above 7.00 — the level had acquired totemic, almost psychological status as a line Beijing would defend. So when the fix was allowed to push CNY through 7.00 on August 5, 2019 — days after Trump threatened the List 4A tariffs — markets read it as a deliberate signal that the PBOC was now willing to weaponize the currency rather than defend the line. Global equities sold off sharply, the US Treasury formally designated China a currency manipulator within hours, and the move confirmed for traders that the 7.00 floor under the dollar was gone. The break was not an accident of market forces overwhelming the PBOC; it was a decision, telegraphed through a weaker-than-expected fix.

**Reading the reserves.** The cleanest confirmation of whether the PBOC is intervening — spending dollars to support the RMB versus letting it slide — comes from the monthly foreign-exchange reserve figure. In the 2015-2016 devaluation scare, reserves fell by roughly a trillion dollars as the PBOC burned through them defending the currency. The tell in 2018-2019 was the opposite: reserves stayed remarkably stable, hovering around \$3.1 trillion. That stability was the signal. It meant the PBOC was not spending reserves to defend the RMB — it was content to let it weaken. A trader watching that reserve series knew the depreciation was sanctioned policy, not a fight the central bank was losing.

## The Phase One Deal: What It Did and Didn't Resolve

The earlier section quantified the purchase shortfall and the market's valuation of the truce. This section is about the political economy — why a deal this hollow got signed at all, what it deliberately left untouched, and why it survived a change of administration nearly intact.

**What China agreed to.** The headline commitment was \$200 billion in additional US goods and services purchases over 2020-2021, above the 2017 baseline, split across energy, manufacturing, agriculture, and services. In exchange, the US halved the List 4A tariff rate from 15% to 7.5% and agreed to refrain from new tariffs during the commitment window. Critically, the 25% tariffs on Lists 1 through 3 — covering roughly \$250 billion of Chinese goods — stayed fully in place.

**What China actually bought.** Against the \$200 billion commitment, China purchased approximately \$170 billion over the two years — about 85% of target, and that 85% is generous accounting that leans on COVID-19 as the excuse for the 2020 shortfall. By the harder measure economists prefer — purchases relative to what China would have bought anyway at pre-war trend — Phase One delivered essentially zero net additional purchasing in some categories. The agricultural numbers came closest to target; the manufactured-goods and services categories fell furthest short.

**What was deliberately not in Phase One.** The deal was silent on every structural issue that started the war. It did not require China to abandon or modify Made in China 2025. It contained no enforceable mechanism on industrial subsidies, no reform of the forced-technology-transfer regime, no enforcement teeth on intellectual-property theft beyond aspirational language, and no rollback of the state-directed investment restrictions that the original Section 301 investigation had catalogued. The deal addressed the symptom Beijing could most easily concede — the trade balance, fixable by buying more soybeans and LNG — while leaving every grievance the national-security establishment actually cared about completely intact.

**The political economy of "good enough."** Both sides needed a deal, and both needed it before their respective political calendars turned hostile. Trump faced re-election in November 2020 and wanted a market-friendly, farmer-friendly win to campaign on; a continued escalation into 2020 risked an equity-market drawdown and farm-belt anger in exactly the swing states he needed. Xi faced a slowing domestic economy, mounting corporate-bond stress, and no desire to concede on the structural issues that touched national pride. A purchase-commitment deal was the unique solution that gave each leader what he needed: Trump got a signing ceremony and a tariff pause, Xi gave up nothing structural. "Good enough" was defined by the politics, not the economics.

**The market reaction on signing day.** On January 15, 2020, the actual signing was an anticlimax precisely because the market had already priced the December pre-announcement. The S&P 500 rose roughly 0.7% on the day, the Shanghai Composite about 0.3%, and soybeans rallied approximately 4.8% in the surrounding window as agricultural buyers priced the purchase commitments. The muted equity move confirmed that the option value of de-escalation had been captured weeks earlier; signing day was the receipt, not the surprise.

**Why Biden kept 90% of the tariffs.** The most telling verdict on Phase One came from the opposing party. The Biden administration, which had campaigned against Trump's trade approach, retained more than 90% of the tariff structure when it took office in 2021 — and in 2024 escalated further, raising tariffs on Chinese EVs to 100% and solar to 50%. The continuity revealed a bipartisan consensus: the China-hawk position had become structural in Washington, detached from any single administration. Tariffs were no longer a Trump policy; they were US policy.

#### Worked example:

**A soybean futures position around the Phase One announcement.**

Setup: You are an agricultural macro trader in early December 2019. The December 13 pre-announcement that a Phase One deal is "agreed in principle" is the catalyst — the deal includes \$32 billion in agricultural purchase commitments, and soybeans are the marquee crop. You go long CBOT soybean futures ahead of the formal signing.

Position: Long 50 CBOT soybean contracts. Each contract is 5,000 bushels. Entry on December 12, 2019, at approximately \$8.95 per bushel.

Total notional: 50 × 5,000 × \$8.95 = **\$2,237,500**.

By the January 15 signing window, soybeans had rallied to approximately \$9.40 per bushel on the purchase-commitment optimism — a move of \$0.45 per bushel.

P&L: 50 contracts × 5,000 bushels × \$0.45 = **\$112,500** gross gain.

Return on notional: \$112,500 / \$2,237,500 = **5.0%** over roughly five weeks.

The cautionary footnote — and the reason this trade was a tactical rent, not a structural position: the purchase commitments were never fully delivered, Brazil retained the structural China soybean franchise, and the post-signing rally faded as the market recognized the gap between commitment and delivery. The lesson generalizes: trade the announcement, not the fundamentals, because in a trade-war deal the announcement and the fundamentals are frequently different things.

## How to Read the Next Round: Signal Framework for 2025

The 2018-19 episode supplied the template, but the 2025 escalation ran a faster, blunter version of it — which makes a forward-looking signal framework more valuable than a backward-looking scorecard.

**The "Liberation Day" 2025 context.** In April 2025, the Trump 2.0 administration used emergency tariff authority (IEEPA) rather than the slower Section 301 process to impose a sweeping tariff regime. On China specifically, the escalation ran from a baseline near 10% up to a peak of 145% within weeks — a velocity of escalation that would have taken eighteen months under the 2018-19 legal machinery. A 90-day pause negotiated in May 2025 then walked the China rate back to roughly 30%. The compressed timeline is the key structural difference: the 2018-19 war gave markets months to price each rung; the 2025 version moved in days, compressing the volatility into sharper spikes.

**The new escalation framework — tariffs as a negotiating chip.** The 2025 playbook treated tariff levels explicitly as bargaining positions rather than fixed policy. The 90-day pause became the signature de-escalation signal — a pause was the market's cue that a deal framework was being negotiated, functionally equivalent to the December 2019 "agreed in principle" moment. The trading implication: in the 2025 regime, the pause announcement (not the deal signing) is the catalyst to fade the safe-haven trade and re-risk, because the pause is when the option value of avoided escalation gets priced.

**Which sectors face the next round.** The escalation has migrated up the technology stack from the 2018-19 industrial-and-consumer-goods focus toward the sectors that define strategic competition: semiconductors (advanced chips and the equipment to make them), electric vehicles, batteries and the critical-minerals supply chain that feeds them, and pharmaceuticals and active pharmaceutical ingredients (a supply-chain-security concern exposed during COVID). These four are where the next escalation rungs are most likely, because they sit at the intersection of the trade-balance grievance and the Made in China 2025 national-security grievance that Phase One never resolved.

**The three signals to watch.** First, the USTR Section 301 review schedule — the statutory four-year review of existing tariffs, and any new Section 301 investigation launches, telegraph where the next tariff lists are being drafted. Second, the Commerce Department Entity List additions — when Chinese firms (particularly in semiconductors, AI, and surveillance) are added, it signals that the conflict is escalating from the trade lane into the technology-denial lane, which historically hits the targeted firms' equity far harder than a tariff does. Third, Treasury and CFIUS actions — new outbound-investment restrictions, inbound-investment screening, and the financial-sanctions toolkit (the Scenario D the market most fears) are the escalation rungs that would turn a trade war into a capital war. Watching these three calendars — USTR, Commerce Entity List, Treasury/CFIUS — gives you the leading edge on the next round before it reaches the tariff headline.
