> **TL;DR** — Europe replaced 40% Russian pipeline gas with LNG in under 18 months, but the price was brutal: TTF peaked at EUR 310/MWh in August 2022 (nearly 4x the pre-crisis price), European industry paid 3x higher gas costs for years, and the pivot required a compressed buildout of five German floating regasification terminals that would normally have taken a decade.
>
> - Russia's invasion in February 2022 triggered the fastest energy supply pivot in recorded history.
> - The TTF-to-Henry Hub spread blew out to over USD 50/MMBtu, creating a generational arbitrage window in LNG shipping, US LNG equities, and European utility repricing.
> - Demand destruction contributed as much as new supply: European industrial gas use fell ~15%, keeping storage from running empty through the 2022-23 winter.
> - The one number to remember: EUR 310/MWh — the August 2022 TTF peak that crystallized Europe's energy dependency risk into a single catastrophic data point.

On March 2, 2022 — six days after Russian tanks crossed the Ukrainian border — the TTF natural gas benchmark closed above EUR 200 per megawatt-hour for the first time in its history. Traders who had spent careers watching European gas trade between EUR 15 and EUR 40 per MWh were staring at a number that felt disconnected from reality. That same week, European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen announced REPowerEU: a plan to eliminate the European Union's dependence on Russian gas — nearly 40% of total supply — by the end of 2022. Market participants who heard the announcement assumed it was political theater. It was not.

By August 2022, TTF had reached EUR 310/MWh — a 273% increase from the pre-invasion level of EUR 83. By December 2022, Germany had received its first LNG tanker at an emergency floating terminal commissioned in under nine months. By mid-2023, TTF had fallen back to EUR 34-40 — not far from pre-crisis levels. The fastest energy supply pivot in recorded history had succeeded, at enormous cost to European industry, consumers, and the global LNG market structure.

This post traces that pivot from the political decision to the physical infrastructure to the market reaction — and most critically, to the trading opportunities it created at every stage.

## Foundations: how natural gas markets work and why pipelines are not like oil

Before understanding what Europe did, you need to understand why replacing pipeline gas is so much harder than replacing oil. Oil is fungible: a tanker of Saudi crude and a tanker of Nigerian crude travel the same oceans, use the same ships, and get processed at broadly similar refineries around the world. Oil prices converge globally because the commodity moves freely. Natural gas is different.

Most natural gas in the world moves through fixed pipelines: giant steel tubes buried in the ground or sunk under shallow seas, carrying gas at high pressure from a producer directly to a consumer. You cannot put pipeline gas in a barrel and ship it somewhere else. The infrastructure is bilateral — it connects exactly one producer to one set of customers. When Europe built the Nord Stream 1 pipeline in 2011 and Nord Stream 2 pipeline in 2021, it was not simply importing Russian gas: it was creating a physical dependency that could only be broken by either building entirely new infrastructure or dramatically reducing gas demand.

Liquefied natural gas, or LNG, is the technology that breaks this dependency. LNG is natural gas that has been super-cooled to -162°C, at which point it liquefies and shrinks to about 1/600th of its gas-state volume. This makes it possible to load onto specially built cryogenic tankers and ship anywhere in the world. At the destination, a regasification terminal warms the LNG back to gas and injects it into the pipeline network. The process involves three capital-intensive steps: liquefaction (at the export terminal), shipping (the tanker), and regasification (at the import terminal). Each step takes years to build and costs hundreds of millions to billions of dollars. In 2022, Europe had very limited regasification capacity compared to its pipeline import capability. That was the structural gap the pivot had to close.

**The global LNG market before 2022.** Before the Russian invasion, global LNG trade was roughly 400 billion cubic meters (bcm) per year. The major exporters were Australia (the world's largest), Qatar, and the United States (whose LNG export capacity had grown rapidly after the shale revolution). The major importers were Japan, South Korea, and China — all countries with no pipeline gas alternatives. Europe imported relatively little LNG compared to its total gas consumption because cheap Russian pipeline gas made LNG economically uncompetitive. That was about to change violently.

**How TTF is priced.** The Title Transfer Facility (TTF) is the main European natural gas benchmark, a virtual trading hub in the Netherlands. Gas traders buy and sell TTF futures on ICE (Intercontinental Exchange) in contracts denominated in euros per megawatt-hour (EUR/MWh). When Russian pipeline flows drop, TTF rises because Europe has fewer cubic meters of gas chasing the same demand. When an LNG tanker docks at Rotterdam and pumps gas into the Dutch grid, TTF falls. The TTF price is essentially Europe's gas supply-demand balance expressed as a single number.

**How Henry Hub relates.** Henry Hub is the US benchmark natural gas price, denominated in US dollars per million British thermal units (USD/MMBtu). Historically, Henry Hub and TTF moved largely independently because there was no physical connection — US gas could not reach Europe without liquefaction and shipping infrastructure. After the US shale boom made American gas extraordinarily cheap (often below USD 3/MMBtu), a structural spread opened: LNG economics require about USD 3.50/MMBtu for liquefaction, USD 2-8/MMBtu for shipping (depending on market conditions), and USD 1/MMBtu for regasification, meaning US LNG delivered to Europe costs roughly USD 7-12/MMBtu above Henry Hub. In normal times that kept LNG volumes modest. In 2022, with TTF at EUR 200-310/MWh (equivalent to roughly USD 60-90/MMBtu), the economics became overwhelming.

## The political moves: decisions made under pressure

The political response to the Russian invasion was not a single decision. It was a cascade of choices, each made under domestic pressure and alliance calculus, from multiple governments simultaneously.

**Germany's reversal.** Germany's decision to kill Nord Stream 2 certification on February 22, 2022 — two days before the invasion — was the most consequential early political move. For years, Germany had resisted pressure from the United States and Eastern European allies to abandon the pipeline. German industry, particularly the chemical sector (BASF), the steel sector (ThyssenKrupp), and fertilizer producers, depended on cheap Russian gas as a feedstock and fuel. The energy company Uniper had built its entire business model around Russian gas import. Killing Nord Stream 2 was not just an economic decision — it was a domestic political earthquake that German Chancellor Olaf Scholz understood would destroy parts of German industry.

The "Zeitenwende" (turning point) speech of February 27, 2022, in which Scholz announced a EUR 100 billion defense fund, was paired with an equally radical shift in energy policy: Germany would build floating LNG terminals as fast as physically possible. The government used emergency procurement powers normally reserved for wartime to charter FSRUs (Floating Storage and Regasification Units) — essentially ships converted into floating LNG terminals — before the physical berths to moor them had even been designed.

**The EU-wide REPowerEU plan.** On May 18, 2022, the European Commission published the REPowerEU plan, which set a target of replacing 101 bcm of Russian gas with alternative supplies by the end of 2022. The plan was politically remarkable because it acknowledged publicly that the EU had a structural vulnerability — 40% of gas from a single hostile supplier — and committed to eliminating it at whatever cost the market required. The plan had three pillars: diversify LNG imports (primarily from the US and Qatar), ramp up renewables (accelerated permitting), and reduce demand through efficiency measures and fuel switching.

The political calculus behind REPowerEU was as important as its mechanics. Russia's gas infrastructure gave it enormous coercive leverage over European politics. Every time Germany hesitated on weapons deliveries to Ukraine, analysts pointed to Gazprom pipeline flows as the explanation. Cutting that dependency was not just an energy policy choice — it was a geopolitical choice to eliminate a tool of Russian foreign policy. In that framing, paying 3x the historical gas price was an insurance premium on European strategic autonomy.

**US LNG politics.** The United States was in a unique position. It had the world's largest LNG export capacity (primarily at Cheniere Energy's Sabine Pass terminal in Louisiana and Corpus Christi terminal in Texas), and it had been pushing for years for European customers to sign long-term contracts for US LNG. European buyers had resisted, preferring the flexibility of spot markets and not wanting to lock in to 20-year deals. After the invasion, US President Biden traveled to Brussels in March 2022 and announced a joint EU-US commitment to increase US LNG deliveries by 15 bcm/year — with a target of 50 bcm/year by 2030. The DOE accelerated export license approvals that had been pending for years. From Washington's perspective, Europe's energy crisis was simultaneously a humanitarian emergency and a strategic opportunity to deepen transatlantic economic ties.

**Qatar's negotiating leverage.** Qatar, the world's largest LNG exporter for many years before Australia overtook it, found itself in an extraordinary position. European governments that had previously complained about Qatar's human rights record and refused to sign long-term contracts were now sending ministers to Doha with urgent requests for multi-decade supply deals. Qatar signed long-term agreements with Germany, Italy, and the Netherlands in 2022-2023 at prices that reflected Qatar's newfound bargaining power. The lesson Qatar drew — and stated explicitly — was that it would only sign long-term contracts, not short-term spot deals, ensuring it captured the structural repricing permanently.

## The financial channels: how political moves became market mechanics

The political decisions above translated into market signals through five distinct financial channels simultaneously.

**Channel 1: TTF spot price.** The most direct channel was the TTF gas price itself. Russian pipeline flows through Nord Stream 1 began declining in June 2022 under the pretext of turbine maintenance (a Siemens turbine caught in Canadian sanctions). By late July, flows had fallen to 20% of capacity. By September, Nord Stream 1 was completely shut down (and then physically destroyed by underwater explosions in September 2022). Each flow reduction pushed TTF higher because Europe had to bid aggressively for LNG cargoes that would otherwise have gone to Asia.

**Channel 2: LNG shipping rates.** The global LNG tanker market has a benchmark index called BLNG1g (Baltic LNG 1 Guinea route). Before 2022, day rates for LNG tankers ran USD 50,000-80,000 per day. By Q4 2022, rates had spiked to USD 400,000-450,000 per day — a 5-6x increase. This reflected two forces: European buyers were chartering vessels for longer periods (removing ship supply from the spot market) and the voyage itself became more expensive because US Gulf Coast to Europe is a longer route than the traditional US Gulf Coast to Asia route, keeping ships at sea longer per cargo.

**Channel 3: European utility repricing.** European electricity generators who buy gas at TTF to generate power had to pass those costs through to electricity customers or absorb margin losses. Utilities with long-term gas supply contracts at pre-crisis prices (primarily with Gazprom via Uniper) suddenly found themselves with contracts worth far below market when Russian supply was cut. Uniper, Germany's largest gas importer, lost EUR 40 billion in 2022 and had to be nationalized by the German government at a cost of EUR 34.5 billion. Utilities without such contracts faced the opposite problem: buying gas at EUR 200-310/MWh to generate electricity, creating electricity prices that made European manufacturing globally uncompetitive.

**Channel 4: US LNG exporter equity repricing.** Cheniere Energy (LNG), New Fortress Energy (NFE), Venture Global LNG, and other US LNG exporters saw their equity values surge as European demand locked in long-term contracts at prices far above prior assumptions. Cheniere's stock price roughly doubled from USD 75 in February 2022 to USD 150 by mid-2022, as the market repriced the value of its long-term contracted volumes at the new prevailing European gas price.

**Channel 5: LNG tanker company equity.** Companies like Flex LNG (FLNG), GOLAR LNG (GLNG), and Höegh LNG suddenly found their fleet values dramatically higher. An LNG tanker that had traded in the secondary market at USD 150-180 million before the crisis was repriced to USD 220-250 million by mid-2022 as the realized day rates made the cash-flow economics far more attractive.

## The infrastructure buildout: terminals in nine months

The physical side of the pivot is where the story gets genuinely remarkable. LNG import terminals are normally the slowest infrastructure to build in the energy sector. A conventional land-based LNG import terminal requires geological surveys, environmental impact assessments, permitting (which in Germany typically takes 3-5 years), financing (USD 500 million to USD 2 billion), engineering, procurement, and construction — typically 5-8 years from concept to first gas. Germany had one LNG terminal (it was still under construction) when the crisis began.

The shortcut was the FSRU: a Floating Storage and Regasification Unit. An FSRU is essentially a modified LNG tanker that, instead of delivering its cargo and sailing away, is permanently moored at a jetty and connected to the onshore pipeline network. The ship performs the regasification function that a land terminal would otherwise perform. FSRUs had been used in emerging markets for decades because they are faster to deploy (12-18 months rather than 5-8 years), can be leased rather than purchased outright, and can be redeployed if needed. Germany decided to use this approach at massive scale.

**The five German FSRUs.** Between June 2022 and December 2023, Germany commissioned five FSRUs at four locations:

1. **Deutsche Energy Terminal (Wilhelmshaven)** — opened December 2022. Capacity 5 bcm/year. First vessel: the Neptune, chartered from TotalEnergies.

2. **Deutsche ReGas (Lubmin / Mukran)** — opened January 2023. Capacity 4.5 bcm/year. Connected to the same pipeline network that had once received Nord Stream gas.

3. **Brunsbüttel** — opened April 2023. Capacity 5 bcm/year.

4. **Elbehafen (Brunsbüttel, second vessel)** — opened late 2023.

5. **Deutsche Energy Terminal (Lubmin II)** — opened 2023.

Total German FSRU capacity by end-2023: approximately 22-24 bcm per year. To put that in perspective: Russia exported roughly 50 bcm to Germany via Nord Stream in 2021. The FSRUs replaced less than half that, with the remainder closed by demand destruction and imports through other European countries.

**Netherlands Gate Terminal.** The Gate LNG import terminal in Rotterdam was Europe's largest existing import terminal before the crisis. It operated at roughly 50-60% utilization before 2022, primarily receiving Qatari LNG. After February 2022, Gate ran at near 100% capacity as European buyers desperately bid for every available cargo. Gate's owners (Royal Dutch Shell, Gasunie, and Vopak) expanded regasification throughput by optimizing loading and unloading procedures.

**Italy's Golar Tundra.** Italy, heavily dependent on Russian gas through the TAG pipeline via Ukraine and Austria, moved with similar speed. The government chartered the Golar Tundra FSRU in mid-2022, moored it at Piombino on the Tuscan coast, and connected it to the Italian grid by early 2023. Despite protests from Piombino residents concerned about industrial zoning, the national energy security need overrode local objections through emergency powers.

## Demand destruction: the hidden half of the pivot

Here is the number that most analyses of the European gas pivot understate: Europe did not replace all the Russian gas with LNG. A substantial fraction of the gap was closed by simply using less gas.

European gas consumption in 2021 was approximately 400 bcm. In 2022, it fell to approximately 360 bcm. In 2023, it fell further to approximately 345 bcm. The 55 bcm reduction from the 2021 baseline — compared to Russian supply that fell from 155 bcm to roughly 25 bcm — means demand destruction closed about 35-40% of the supply gap. Without that, the FSRU buildout alone would have been insufficient to avoid gas rationing in the winter of 2022-23.

The demand destruction came from three sources:

**Industrial shutdowns.** European chemical plants, fertilizer producers, and glass manufacturers are extremely gas-intensive. When TTF hit EUR 200-310/MWh, many of these facilities could not profitably operate. BASF, the world's largest chemical company, curtailed output at its Ludwigshafen, Germany chemical complex — the world's largest integrated chemical site — by roughly 10-15% throughout 2022-2023. Yara, the Norwegian fertilizer giant, shut European production almost entirely in the summer of 2022 when ammonia production became uneconomic at those gas prices. European zinc smelters curtailed output. European aluminum smelters (which use electricity generated partly from gas-fired plants) reduced production. This deindustrialization was economically painful but it directly reduced gas demand.

**Efficiency and fuel switching.** European residential and commercial consumers responded to high prices by turning down thermostats, installing heat pumps (a gas-free heating alternative), and in some cases switching from gas boilers to electric heating. Germany ran a public campaign ("short showers save Germany") that, while mocked online, reflected a genuine behavioral response. The EU estimated that behavioral efficiency measures reduced gas demand by approximately 5-10 bcm across Europe in the winter of 2022-23.

**Mild weather.** The 2022-23 winter in Northern Europe was notably milder than average. Meteorologists estimated that the temperature anomaly reduced heating demand by approximately 5-8 bcm compared to a normal winter. This was pure luck — if the winter had been cold, storage might not have been sufficient. European energy officials admitted privately that mild weather had been critical to avoiding rationing.

The combination of these three demand-side factors — together with the FSRU buildout and the surge in LNG imports from the US, Qatar, and Algeria — was sufficient to keep European storage from running dangerously low. Storage hit a minimum of approximately 53% of capacity in late January 2023 (versus a 5-year average minimum of ~45%), then refilled through the spring. By September 2023, storage was above 90% — the highest in decades.

## The TTF-Henry Hub spread: the core arbitrage

The central financial story of the LNG rerouting is the TTF-to-Henry-Hub spread. Understanding this spread — how it opened, what it meant, and how traders positioned around it — is essential to understanding the trading opportunities the pivot created.

**The mechanics of the spread.** Natural gas economics link the two markets through LNG economics. If you can buy gas at Henry Hub for USD X/MMBtu, liquefy it for approximately USD 3.50/MMBtu, ship it across the Atlantic for approximately USD 2-8/MMBtu (variable), and regasify it for approximately USD 1/MMBtu, you will sell it at TTF only if TTF > HH + 7 to 13 USD/MMBtu (depending on shipping costs and margin). In normal conditions (2018-2021), TTF was roughly at HH + 5-8 USD/MMBtu, making European delivery marginally economic for US LNG.

In August 2022, TTF was at EUR 310/MWh — approximately USD 87/MMBtu at prevailing exchange rates. Henry Hub at the same time was approximately USD 8/MMBtu. The spread was USD 79/MMBtu. After all LNG costs, a US LNG cargo delivered to Europe generated approximately USD 70/MMBtu of profit above cost. Every large US LNG facility that could load a cargo and send it to Europe was doing exactly that.

**Who captured the spread?** The answer depends on how LNG was contracted. US LNG export capacity is primarily contracted through two mechanisms: (a) "tolling agreements" where the buyer pays Cheniere a fixed fee (approximately USD 3.50/MMBtu) and keeps the commodity upside, and (b) "integrated supply agreements" where Cheniere sells LNG at a price indexed to HH + a margin. Buyers with tolling agreements — primarily European utilities like E.ON, Engie, and RWE — captured the full spread. They had purchased Henry Hub gas at spot, paid the tolling fee, and sold at TTF. The profit per cargo on an LNG tanker of approximately 3.4 bcf capacity was approximately USD 70/MMBtu × 3.4 bcf = roughly USD 238 million — on a single cargo.

**The shipping overlay.** Even buyers who captured the commodity spread had to contend with the shipping market. Spot LNG tanker charter rates rose from USD 50,000/day to USD 400,000/day through Q4 2022. A 20-day Atlantic crossing at USD 400,000/day costs USD 8 million — versus USD 1 million at pre-crisis rates. Traders who had locked in long-term time charters at USD 60,000-80,000/day before the crisis had enormous advantages: they could arbitrage the TTF-HH spread without facing the spot shipping cost surge.

![TTF versus Henry Hub spread 2021-2025 showing the arbitrage window opening and closing](/imgs/blogs/lng-rerouting-how-europe-replaced-russian-gas-in-18-months-7.png)

#### Worked example:

**Calculating the August 2022 LNG cargo profit.** A US LNG exporter loads a standard LNG cargo at Sabine Pass, Louisiana in August 2022. The ship holds approximately 170,000 cubic meters of LNG, equivalent to 3.57 billion cubic feet (bcf) of natural gas, or approximately 1,000,000 MMBtu.

- Henry Hub gas cost: \$8.00/MMBtu × 1,000,000 MMBtu = **\$8,000,000**
- Liquefaction tolling fee: \$3.50/MMBtu × 1,000,000 MMBtu = **\$3,500,000**
- LNG tanker charter (spot, 22-day voyage at \$400,000/day): **\$8,800,000**
- Regasification at European FSRU: \$1.00/MMBtu × 1,000,000 MMBtu = **\$1,000,000**
- Total delivery cost: **\$21,300,000** (\$21.30/MMBtu)

Revenue: TTF at EUR 310/MWh. 1 MMBtu = 0.2931 MWh. EUR 310 × 0.2931 = EUR 90.86/MMBtu. At EUR/USD 1.00 (August 2022 exchange rate): **\$90.86/MMBtu**

**Gross profit: \$90.86 - \$21.30 = \$69.56/MMBtu × 1,000,000 MMBtu = \$69,560,000 per cargo.**

That is a \$69.5 million profit on a single ship voyage of 22 days. It explains why every available LNG tanker globally was routed toward Europe.

## Common misconceptions about the LNG pivot

**Misconception 1: "Europe replaced all its Russian gas with LNG."** False. Europe replaced roughly 60-65% of lost Russian supply with LNG and other pipeline sources (Norway, Algeria). The remaining 35-40% was closed by demand destruction — industrial shutdowns, efficiency gains, and mild weather. If European gas demand had remained at 2021 levels, the pivot would have failed, and gas rationing would have occurred in the winter of 2022-23.

**Misconception 2: "Germany's FSRUs replaced Nord Stream 1 capacity."** False. Nord Stream 1 carried approximately 55 bcm/year at full capacity. Germany's five FSRUs together provide approximately 22-24 bcm/year of import capacity. The deficit was made up by importing more gas from Norway (via pipeline), from the Netherlands, and through existing LNG terminals in Belgium and France.

**Misconception 3: "European gas prices normalized quickly."** Partially false. TTF did fall from EUR 310/MWh in August 2022 to EUR 34-46/MWh by mid-2023 — a 90% decline that felt like normalization. But EUR 34-46 is still 50-100% above the pre-2021 baseline of EUR 15-25. European industrial gas consumers (BASF, Yara, glass producers) were paying 2-3x their 2019 gas costs through 2023 and 2024. Several major European chemical plants that were "temporarily curtailed" in 2022 remained permanently closed because the economics of European gas-intensive manufacturing had been structurally disrupted. The normalization in financial market prices masked a genuine structural repricing of European energy-intensive industry.

**Misconception 4: "The US benefited cleanly from Europe's energy crisis."** The US did export far more LNG to Europe and locked in long-term contracts at favorable prices. But US domestic consumers also paid higher energy prices as LNG exports diverted natural gas from the domestic market. Henry Hub averaged USD 6.45/MMBtu in 2022 — the highest since 2008, and roughly 3x the 2020 average. US manufacturing and utilities faced higher costs because marginal gas was now priced at the international LNG parity price.

**Misconception 5: "FSRUs are a temporary solution."** This was the initial framing — German officials repeatedly said the FSRUs were "bridge infrastructure" until renewables replaced gas. In practice, Germany signed 10-year charters for several FSRUs and simultaneously announced permits for land-based LNG terminals (which take 5-7 years to build). The FSRUs locked in European LNG import capacity well into the 2030s, making LNG a structural feature of the European energy mix rather than a temporary fix.

## How it shows up in real markets: case studies

**Case study 1: Uniper's collapse and nationalization.** Uniper was Germany's largest natural gas importer, with long-term supply contracts with Gazprom for approximately 25-30 bcm/year. When Russia cut flows, Uniper was forced to buy replacement gas on the spot market at EUR 200-310/MWh while its downstream contracts with German utilities and industrial customers were priced at EUR 20-40/MWh. The margin loss was catastrophic. Uniper lost EUR 40 billion in 2022 — equivalent to wiping out roughly 10 years of prior profits — and required a EUR 34.5 billion government bailout and nationalization. Uniper's share price fell from approximately EUR 40 at the start of 2022 to under EUR 2.50 by October 2022, a 93% decline, before trading was suspended pending nationalization.

**Case study 2: Cheniere Energy equity repricing.** Cheniere Energy (NYSE: LNG) is the largest US LNG exporter. Before the crisis, Cheniere traded at USD 80-100 per share on expectations of modest long-term LNG demand growth. After February 2022, European buyers were signing multi-decade contracts at prices far above Cheniere's prior modelling assumptions. Cheniere's stock price rose from approximately USD 75 in January 2022 to USD 165 by October 2022. The company raised its distributable cash flow guidance substantially and initiated a major share buyback. Analysts who had modeled LNG at USD 8-10/MMBtu long-term were forced to revise upward to USD 12-15/MMBtu, directly increasing the net present value of Cheniere's contracted cash flows.

**Case study 3: FLEX LNG shipping equity.** Flex LNG (NYSE: FLNG) operates a fleet of 13 modern LNG tankers with a mix of fixed-rate and index-linked charters. When the LNG shipping market seized in Q3-Q4 2022, FLNG shares rose from approximately USD 15 to USD 40 (a 167% gain) as the market recognized that tanker day rates of USD 200,000-400,000/day were far above the fixed-rate charters embedded in FLNG's order book — meaning FLNG was almost certainly going to re-contract expiring vessels at dramatically higher rates. The company raised its dividend substantially through 2022 and 2023.

#### Worked example:

**Calculating the Uniper margin loss.** Uniper contracted to buy 10 bcm/year of Russian gas at approximately EUR 25/MWh (pre-crisis long-term contract). In July 2022, with Russian flows cut, Uniper replaced 5 bcm of missing Russian gas by buying on the spot market at EUR 180/MWh, while selling downstream at EUR 40/MWh (its pre-existing downstream contracts with German utilities).

- Spot gas cost: EUR 180/MWh × (5 bcm × 277.8 GWh/bcm) = EUR 180 × 1,389 TWh... let us simplify:
  5 bcm = approximately 50 TWh/month worth of energy.
- Monthly spot purchase cost: EUR 180 × 50,000,000 MWh = **EUR 9,000,000,000 (EUR 9 billion)**
- Monthly downstream revenue: EUR 40 × 50,000,000 MWh = **EUR 2,000,000,000 (EUR 2 billion)**
- **Monthly margin loss: EUR 7,000,000,000 (EUR 7 billion)**

Across roughly six months of severe curtailment (July-December 2022), this accumulates to roughly EUR 40 billion — matching the actual reported loss. The German government's EUR 34.5 billion bailout was not a policy choice; it was the minimum required to prevent Uniper's bankruptcy from cascading through German utility and industrial customer contracts.

## The LNG value chain and where money was made

![LNG value chain from US shale well to European power plant with costs at each step](/imgs/blogs/lng-rerouting-how-europe-replaced-russian-gas-in-18-months-6.png)

The 2022-2023 LNG boom created winners at every stage of the LNG value chain, but not equally.

**Liquefaction (US exporters):** Cheniere Energy, Sempra Infrastructure, and Venture Global captured massive value. Their terminals ran at 100% capacity. Companies with new terminals under construction (Venture Global's Plaquemines LNG, New Fortress Energy's FLNG 1) were able to pre-sell capacity to European buyers at prices that justified construction costs previously considered marginal.

**Shipping:** LNG tanker owners captured the most visible upside. Spot rates 5-6x above historical levels, combined with multi-year term charters locked in at elevated rates, created a sustained earnings boom for fleet operators. Companies like Golar LNG, Flex LNG, and Höegh LNG generated returns on equity that had not been seen since the early 2000s tanker boom.

**Regasification:** FSRU charterers (initially the German government through Deutsche ReGas and Deutsche Energy Terminal) bore the fixed costs of the charter (approximately USD 200,000-300,000/day per vessel) regardless of utilization. This was a necessary cost of energy security rather than a profitable business in the conventional sense.

**Trading desks:** The LNG trading desks at Shell, TotalEnergies, BP, and commodity trading houses (Vitol, Trafigura, Gunvor) made extraordinary profits from optimizing cargo routing — deciding in real time whether to send a Qatar cargo to Europe or Japan, or whether to bid for a spot US cargo versus waiting for a long-term supply. These desks had information advantages from their global cargo portfolios and used them aggressively.

#### Worked example:

**Calculating the LNG shipping rate impact on Flex LNG.** Flex LNG has 13 tankers. In Q4 2022, 4 vessels had expiring charters that were re-contracted at spot market rates of approximately USD 200,000/day versus prior rates of approximately USD 70,000/day.

- Daily revenue increase per ship: USD 200,000 - USD 70,000 = \$130,000/day
- 4 ships over 90 days (Q4): 4 × \$130,000 × 90 = **\$46,800,000 incremental EBITDA in Q4 alone**

FLNG had previously earned approximately USD 60 million EBITDA per quarter from its entire fleet. The incremental revenue from 4 re-contracted ships nearly doubled quarterly earnings in one quarter. The stock market repriced this within weeks of the rate spike becoming visible.

## European industry: paying the price of the pivot

While traders and LNG companies made extraordinary returns, European industry absorbed the cost. The chemical, steel, fertilizer, glass, and ceramics sectors — all of which use natural gas as a primary input — faced a structural cost shock that reshaped European industrial geography.

**BASF's carbon footprint.** BASF produces more than 200 products that use natural gas as a feedstock at its Ludwigshafen complex. When TTF rose to EUR 200-310/MWh, the economics of ammonia synthesis (the Haber-Bosch process, which uses natural gas and nitrogen to make ammonia for fertilizers) became untenable at European gas prices. BASF curtailed output, began the process of building production capacity in China and the US where gas is cheaper, and announced 2,600 job cuts at Ludwigshafen in 2023. BASF CEO Martin Brudermüller publicly stated in October 2022 that "European chemical production is in a historic crisis" and warned that deindustrialization was a real risk if gas prices remained elevated.

**Fertilizer production collapse.** Yara International, the Norwegian fertilizer company, cut European ammonia output by approximately 70% in August 2022. CF Industries (US) and Nutrien (Canada) benefited because their North American plants used cheap North American gas to produce fertilizer while European plants shut. Global fertilizer prices spiked, contributing to food price inflation worldwide — a secondary channel connecting the Russia-Ukraine war to developing country food security.

**Steel and glass.** Arcelor Mittal curtailed output at several European blast furnaces. European glass manufacturers (Guardian, Saint-Gobain) reduced production at energy-intensive float glass plants. The common theme: any process requiring continuous high-temperature heat faced uneconomic gas costs and chose between curtailment, customer price increases, or both.

The structural damage to European industry is a long-running story. Even as TTF normalized to EUR 34-46/MWh in 2023-2025, this remains 2-3x the historical baseline, and many energy-intensive European plants have permanently relocated production or closed. The pivot succeeded in energy security terms, but the industrial cost was real and ongoing.

## The European energy security stack

![European energy security pivot built on four interdependent layers from political mandate to demand reduction](/imgs/blogs/lng-rerouting-how-europe-replaced-russian-gas-in-18-months-8.png)

The LNG pivot rested on four interdependent layers. Political mandate (REPowerEU) unlocked emergency procurement powers without which FSRU contracts could not have been signed in months rather than years. Long-term LNG contracts with the US, Qatar, and Australia gave European buyers supply certainty that allowed them to reduce spot market bidding. FSRU terminal buildout provided physical import capacity at the speed emergency powers made possible. Demand destruction absorbed the structural gap between available LNG supply and prior Russian pipeline volumes. Remove any layer and the pivot fails.

#### Worked example:

**Calculating the storage buffer needed to avoid rationing.** European gas storage capacity is approximately 100 bcm. In autumn 2022, storage filled to approximately 90 bcm by October 1 — the highest level in years, achieved by aggressive LNG purchasing in the spring and summer. The 2022-23 winter required approximately 55 bcm of gas (typical winter withdrawal from storage). With storage starting at 90 bcm, the theoretical buffer before running empty (at ~10-15 bcm minimum working gas) was approximately 75-80 bcm — higher than the winter draw.

Had storage been at 70% (70 bcm) instead of 90% — which would have been the case if LNG imports had been 15-20 bcm lower — the buffer falls to 55-60 bcm against a 55 bcm winter requirement. The margin for error disappears. Any cold spell above average would trigger emergency gas rationing protocols. The EU Commission estimated that a one-degree-colder-than-average winter would add approximately 8-10 bcm of demand. In that scenario, rationing would have been necessary.

The arithmetic explains why European governments accepted enormous financial costs (FSRU charter rates, premium LNG pricing, Uniper bailout) to push storage above 90%: the insurance value of that additional 15-20 bcm in storage was worth far more than the market price of the gas.

## How to trade it: the playbook

The LNG rerouting created a set of playbook signals that recur whenever geopolitical shocks threaten pipeline gas supplies. The specific 2022-23 window has largely normalized, but the framework applies to any future energy disruption.

**Signal 1: Monitor TTF-Henry Hub spread weekly.** The TTF-HH spread (converted to common units) is the single most important indicator of LNG market tightness. When the spread exceeds USD 12-15/MMBtu, LNG economics are attractive and every available cargo routes to Europe. When the spread is below USD 8-10/MMBtu, US LNG goes to Asia or the Americas. Track this spread to assess whether the LNG arbitrage is open or closed.

**Signal 2: LNG tanker spot rates (BLNG1g).** The Baltic LNG 1 index tracks spot tanker rates for the Atlantic LNG route. When BLNG1g spikes above USD 150,000/day, the shipping bottleneck is tightening and LNG tanker equities (FLNG, GLNG, GMLP) typically rerate higher. When BLNG1g falls below USD 70,000/day, the shipping premium has normalized.

**Signal 3: European storage trajectory.** Track European gas storage as a percentage of capacity (published weekly by GIE — Gas Infrastructure Europe). If storage falls below 65% by October 1 (pre-winter peak), the risk of winter gas shortfalls increases sharply and TTF should trade at a premium. If storage is above 85% by October 1, the near-term supply risk is manageable and TTF is likely to fall.

**The trade in a future Russian-style supply shock:**

1. **LNG shipping:** Buy time-chartered LNG tankers (as equities or physical charter) as early as possible after the shock — before spot rates fully reflect the shortage. FLNG and GLNG are the most liquid equity proxies.

2. **US LNG exporters:** Buy Cheniere Energy (LNG) and New Fortress Energy (NFE) as long-term contracting re-rates their contracted cash flows upward. Model the change in discounted cash flows from re-contracting at new TTF-linked prices.

3. **Short European utilities:** Utilities without gas hedges (RWE, E.ON, Fortum) face margin compression as input costs rise faster than regulated output prices. Short the utility with lowest gas hedge cover and highest gas generation percentage.

4. **Long TTF, short Henry Hub (spread trade):** If you believe the disruption is sustained, long TTF / short HH captures the spread directly. Risk: US gas demand increases (cold winter, LNG demand), narrowing the spread from the HH side.

5. **Invalidation:** The trade inverts if (a) Europe finds alternative supply faster than expected, (b) the disruption proves shorter than feared, (c) European gas demand falls faster than expected (mild winter), or (d) a new pipeline route emerges (unlikely in 12-18 month windows, but possible long-term via Southern Gas Corridor or Balkan routes).

**The 2024-2025 normalized environment.** By 2024-2025, TTF was trading EUR 34-46/MWh, LNG tanker rates had normalized to USD 50,000-80,000/day, and the acute crisis was over. But the structural change remained: Europe was now a major LNG importer, European regasification capacity had quadrupled, and long-term LNG contracts tied European energy to Henry Hub as well as TTF. This means European gas prices will never again be purely a European supply-demand story — they will be linked permanently to US shale production, US LNG export capacity, and Asian demand competition for LNG cargoes. The geopolitical crisis permanently altered the market structure.
