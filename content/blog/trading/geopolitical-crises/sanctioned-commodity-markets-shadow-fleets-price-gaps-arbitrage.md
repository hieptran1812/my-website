> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Sanctions on a commodity almost never stop the commodity from moving. They reroute it, discount it, and hide it. Russia's seaborne crude did not vanish after the December 2022 price cap; it sailed on a dark fleet of 600-plus aging tankers, sold at a Urals discount that peaked near \$30 a barrel, and was frequently refined in India into diesel and jet fuel that re-entered Europe with the origin legally erased. The infrastructure that makes this work — re-flagged ships, parallel insurance, transshipment hubs, and refineries that change a barrel's nationality — is the same infrastructure Iran, Venezuela, and North Korea built before. For a trader, the sanctioned-commodity market is a study in price gaps, and price gaps are arbitrage. For a risk manager, it is a study in enforcement: the gap is wide because the penalty for being caught is real, and it narrows as buyers grow comfortable that they will not be.

When a government sanctions a commodity, it is making a bet about physics and economics that it usually loses in the short run. The bet is that if you cut off enough buyers, enough banks, enough ships, and enough insurers, the barrels will simply stay in the ground and the regime you are punishing will run out of money. The reality is that oil, gas, coal, and metals are fungible, durable, and wanted by someone, somewhere, at a price. The question is never whether the commodity moves. It is *at what discount, through which intermediaries, and with how much friction*.

This post is a practitioner's tour of that market. We will follow a single barrel of Russian crude from a Baltic loading port to a fuel pump in Rotterdam, and we will count the discount it gives up at every handoff. We will look at the shadow fleet — what it is, how big it got, and why an aging tanker registered in Gabon is the load-bearing wall of an entire evasion economy. We will price the Urals-Brent gap and trace it as it widened to \$30 and then collapsed to \$12 as buyers grew bolder. And we will sit in the seat of the people who make money on all of this: the Indian refiner who buys discounted crude and sells undiscounted product, the trader who fronts the cargo, and the compliance officer who has to decide how much enforcement risk that spread is really paying them to take.

The thesis is simple and it generalizes: **a sanctions discount is the market's price of enforcement risk plus the cost of the parallel infrastructure required to move the molecule.** When enforcement is credible and the infrastructure is thin, the discount is wide. When enforcement softens and the infrastructure matures, the discount narrows toward the pure logistics cost. Read that way, the Urals discount is not a political headline. It is a tradable, mean-reverting spread with a regime overlay — and that is exactly how the smartest money in the physical market treats it.

## Foundations: The mechanics of a sanctioned commodity market

Start with the journey, because everything else is a tax levied somewhere along it. A barrel of Russian Urals crude that ends up as European diesel does not teleport. It is loaded, shipped, transferred, refined, relabeled, and re-shipped, and at each of those steps somebody takes a cut or a risk premium.

![Pipeline diagram tracing a barrel of Russian crude from the wellhead through a re-flagged shadow tanker, a Gulf transshipment hub, an Indian refinery, and out as refined product to a European or Asian buyer.](/imgs/blogs/sanctioned-commodity-markets-shadow-fleets-price-gaps-arbitrage-1.png)

The barrel starts at the wellhead and loads at a Russian port — Primorsk or Ust-Luga on the Baltic, or Kozmino on the Pacific for the ESPO grade. So far this is an ordinary crude cargo. The first thing that makes it a *sanctioned* cargo is the ship. After December 2022, a Western-owned, Western-insured tanker carrying Russian crude sold above the price cap is in violation of G7 rules. So the barrel is increasingly loaded onto a different kind of vessel: an older ship, often fifteen to twenty years old, owned through an opaque chain of shell companies, registered under a flag of convenience such as Gabon, Cameroon, the Cook Islands, or Panama, and frequently sailing with its automatic identification system transponder switched off so its movements cannot be tracked in real time.

That ship may sail directly to a buyer, or it may stop at a transshipment hub — anchorages off Fujairah in the UAE, off the coast of Greece near Kalamata, or in Malaysian waters near Singapore — where it conducts a ship-to-ship transfer. The cargo is pumped from one tanker to another, sometimes blended with crude from other origins, and the paperwork is rewritten so that the resulting cargo's stated origin is ambiguous or simply different. This is the laundering step for the *crude* itself: a barrel that left Russia can arrive somewhere with a Malaysian or "various" origin on its bill of lading.

Then comes the most economically important transformation. The crude is delivered to a refinery, very often in India — Reliance's Jamnagar complex or the Nayara refinery at Vadinar — and is refined into products: diesel, jet fuel, gasoline, naphtha. And here is the legal magic that makes the whole trade durable: **a refined product is, under most trade rules, a product of the country where it was refined, not the country where the crude originated.** Russian crude refined in India becomes Indian diesel. That Indian diesel can then be sold to Europe entirely legally, because Europe banned Russian *crude and products*, not products refined elsewhere from Russian crude. The barrel re-enters the very market that sanctioned it, wearing a new passport.

#### Worked example: counting the discount along the chain

Let us put numbers on a single 1-million-barrel cargo, using round figures consistent with the 2023 market.

- **Benchmark.** Brent is at \$80/bbl. A non-sanctioned barrel of comparable quality would fetch roughly that.
- **At the Russian wellhead/port.** Because of the cap and self-sanctioning, the cargo sells at Urals, say \$50/bbl in the deepest part of the discount — a gap of \$30/bbl, or \$30 million on the cargo.
- **Shipping premium.** A shadow-fleet voyage costs more than a normal one: older ship, higher day rates, dedicated insurance. Call the extra freight \$3/bbl versus a normal Baltic-to-India run, or \$3 million.
- **Transshipment and handling.** A ship-to-ship transfer and the associated logistics might add \$1 to \$2/bbl, say \$1.5 million.
- **At the refinery gate.** The Indian refiner has paid roughly \$50 + \$3 + \$1.5 = \$54.5/bbl all-in for crude that competes with \$80 Brent-priced alternatives. Its crude cost advantage is about \$25.5/bbl.
- **Refining cost.** Turning crude into a slate of products costs the refiner roughly \$8 to \$12/bbl in processing, energy, and yield loss. Call it \$10/bbl.
- **Product sale.** The diesel and jet fuel sell at global product benchmarks, which are *not* discounted, because the product is "Indian." 

The refiner's gross capture is roughly the crude discount minus the incremental costs: \$25.5 − \$10 ≈ \$15.5/bbl of margin uplift over what it would have earned buying undiscounted crude, or about \$15.5 million on the cargo. That uplift is split — through negotiation — among the refiner, the trading house that arranged the cargo, and Russia itself, which is willing to give up \$30/bbl at the wellhead precisely because the alternative is selling nothing. **Everyone in the chain is paid out of the discount, and the discount exists because of the sanction.**

## The shadow fleet: the load-bearing wall

None of this works without ships. A "shadow fleet" — sometimes called the dark fleet or grey fleet — is the population of tankers that operate outside the mainstream, Western-regulated, Western-insured shipping system specifically to carry sanctioned or price-capped cargoes.

![Matrix comparing Russian crude, Iranian crude, Venezuelan crude, and North Korean coal across fleet size, primary buyers, discount to benchmark, detection risk, and enforcement action taken.](/imgs/blogs/sanctioned-commodity-markets-shadow-fleets-price-gaps-arbitrage-3.png)

The defining features of a shadow tanker are consistent across the sanctioned commodities. The ship is **old**, because nobody wants to put a young, valuable asset at risk of designation, detention, or being stranded; the average shadow tanker is well past the age at which mainstream charterers would touch it. Its **ownership is opaque**, held through layers of single-ship companies in jurisdictions that do not require beneficial-owner disclosure, so that designating one shell does not capture the vessel. It flies a **flag of convenience** from a registry with light oversight, and it changes flag readily when a registry comes under pressure to clean house. Its **insurance** is not from the Western protection-and-indemnity clubs that cover the vast majority of world shipping; instead it carries cover from Russian insurers or obscure providers whose ability to actually pay out on a major spill is, charitably, untested. And it routinely engages in **deceptive shipping practices**: switching off its AIS transponder, spoofing its location, and conducting ship-to-ship transfers in the open ocean to break the documentary chain between a Russian port and a final buyer.

By the numbers, the Russian-associated shadow fleet grew from essentially nothing before the war to an estimated 600 or so vessels by 2024 — a fleet large enough to move a very large share of Russia's seaborne crude. Estimates vary because the whole point of the fleet is to be hard to count, but analysts consistently put the dark fleet's share of Russian crude exports somewhere around 40 percent and rising over 2023 and 2024. The fleet was built partly by buying up old tankers that mainstream owners were happy to offload at the end of their service lives, which is why a wave of second-hand tanker sales to undisclosed buyers in 2022 and 2023 was itself a tell that the fleet was forming.

This is not a Russian invention. Iran ran a shadow fleet for years to move crude to China's independent "teapot" refiners despite US secondary sanctions, perfecting the AIS-off, ship-to-ship, relabel-the-cargo playbook. Venezuela did the same to move heavy crude to Asia after US sanctions on PDVSA. North Korea ran a smaller, cruder version to move coal and refined products in defiance of UN Security Council resolutions, with the United Nations Panel of Experts documenting transponder spoofing and at-sea transfers in detail. **Russia did not pioneer shadow shipping; it scaled it.** The difference is volume: Iran's evasion moved on the order of one to two million barrels a day at its peak; Russia's seaborne exports are several times that, so the fleet required to move them is correspondingly enormous, and its formation reshaped the entire second-hand tanker market.

#### Worked example: the economics of buying a shadow tanker

Why would anyone spend money assembling a fleet of geriatric ships? Because the cargo discount pays for it many times over.

- An aging Aframax tanker — the workhorse of the Baltic crude trade, carrying roughly 700,000 barrels — might be bought second-hand for \$30 to \$40 million in the heated 2023 market, well above scrap value but far below a modern newbuild.
- That ship can make perhaps six to eight laden voyages a year on a Baltic-to-India route.
- On each voyage it carries 700,000 barrels that are being sold at, say, a \$20/bbl discount to Brent. The *total* discount captured across the chain on one cargo is therefore around \$14 million.
- The operator does not keep all \$14 million — most of it is the buyer's incentive and Russia's revenue sacrifice — but the *freight* premium the shadow operator earns over normal rates might be \$3 to \$5/bbl, or \$2 to \$3.5 million per voyage.
- Across, say, seven voyages a year, that is \$14 to \$24 million of premium freight on a single \$35 million ship.

In other words, a shadow tanker can pay back its entire purchase price in roughly one to two years of operation, after which it is pure profit until it is designated, detained, or sinks. When the asset pays for itself that fast, a fleet of 600 ships is not a mystery; it is an obvious trade. The enforcement risk — the chance the ship gets sanctioned and becomes unusable — is precisely what keeps competitors out and the premium freight high.

There is a subtler point buried in that payback math, and it shapes the whole character of the fleet. Because a shadow tanker recoups its cost so quickly, the operator is economically indifferent to the ship's *residual* value. A mainstream owner protects a modern, valuable vessel; it avoids risky waters, maintains it scrupulously, and would never strand it on a designated route, because the asset is worth far more than a year of earnings. A shadow operator faces the opposite calculus: the ship has already paid for itself, so losing it to a designation or even an accident is a recoverable cost, not a catastrophe. This is why shadow tankers take navigational and maintenance risks that no mainstream owner would countenance, and it is why the safety externality is structural rather than incidental. The fleet is built out of assets whose owners have already been made whole, and an owner who has been made whole does not behave carefully. The market has, in effect, created a population of ships piloted by people with nothing left to lose — which is exactly the population you would least want carrying a million barrels of crude through a crowded strait.

The same logic governs how the fleet *responds* to enforcement. When a tranche of vessels is designated, the operators do not abandon the trade; they rotate. A designated ship is sold, scrapped, or quietly re-shelled under a new name, new owner-of-record, and new flag, and the cargo it would have carried simply shifts to the next available bottom. Designation therefore behaves less like a wall and more like a tax: it raises the cost and churn of the fleet without stopping it, until the designation pace becomes fast enough to outrun the supply of plausible replacement tonnage. That threshold — designations per month versus the rate at which old tankers can be acquired and laundered — is the real enforcement frontier, and it is why analysts who track this market watch second-hand tanker sales and flag-registry changes as closely as they watch the cargo data itself.

## Pricing the gap: the Urals-Brent discount

The discount is the headline number, so it deserves a careful look. Brent is the global seaborne benchmark; Urals is Russia's flagship medium-sour export grade. In normal times Urals trades at a small, stable discount to Brent — a dollar or two — reflecting its slightly lower quality and the freight to deliver it to European refiners who were built to process it.

![Line chart showing Brent and Urals prices from mid-2022 to 2025 with the discount between them shaded, annotated with the December 2022 cap, the peak discount near thirty dollars in March 2023, and the later narrowing as India absorbs the barrels.](/imgs/blogs/sanctioned-commodity-markets-shadow-fleets-price-gaps-arbitrage-2.png)

The war and the sanctions blew that small, quality-driven discount into something far larger and entirely political. Watch the sequence in the chart. In mid-2022, even before the formal cap, the discount was already wide — around \$29/bbl — because Western buyers were *self-sanctioning*, refusing to touch Russian crude ahead of any legal requirement to do so, out of reputational caution and fear of where the rules were heading. That self-sanctioning is a crucial and underappreciated mechanism: much of the early discount was not imposed by law but volunteered by buyers who did not want to be the last European refiner caught buying Russian oil when the political winds were obvious.

The G7 price cap took effect on December 5, 2022, set at \$60/bbl for seaborne Russian crude. Its design was clever: rather than a blunt embargo, it weaponized the West's dominance of maritime *services*. Western insurers, shippers, and financiers were permitted to service Russian oil cargoes only if the crude was sold at or below \$60/bbl. The theory was that this would keep Russian oil flowing — avoiding a global supply shock that would spike prices — while capping the *revenue* Russia earned per barrel. For a while, with the discount sitting around \$30 in early 2023 and Brent in the high \$70s, Urals was indeed trading below \$60, and the cap looked like it was working.

Then the discount narrowed. As the shadow fleet matured and Indian and Chinese buyers grew comfortable with the trade, demand for Russian barrels firmed. The discount compressed from roughly \$30 in early 2023 toward \$13 to \$14 by mid-2023, to around \$12 by early 2024, and toward \$10 to \$11 by 2025. The narrowing is the single most important fact about this market: **the discount is not a constant; it is the market-clearing price of evasion, and as evasion got cheaper and more routine, the discount shrank.** Russia kept more of each barrel's value over time, which is exactly why Western policymakers spent 2023 and 2024 trying to enforce the cap harder — designating specific ships, pressuring registries and insurers — to push the discount back up.

#### Worked example: what the discount narrowing is worth to Russia

The compression looks like a small number per barrel, but it scales brutally.

- Russia exports roughly 3 million barrels a day of seaborne crude. Over a year that is about 1.1 billion barrels.
- A discount of \$30/bbl versus a discount of \$12/bbl is an \$18/bbl difference.
- On 1.1 billion barrels, \$18/bbl is roughly **\$20 billion a year** of revenue swing.

So when the discount narrowed from \$30 to \$12, it handed Russia on the order of \$20 billion a year in additional oil revenue, holding the benchmark constant — money that flows directly to the state budget and, by extension, the war. That single spread, measured in dollars per barrel, is one of the most consequential numbers in the entire conflict's economics, which is why both sides fight over it: Russia by building out the fleet and the buyer relationships that compress it, the West by trying to raise the friction that widens it.

## Before and after: how the market forked

It helps to hold the two market structures side by side, because the sanctioned market is not the old market with a discount bolted on. It is a structurally different animal.

![Before-and-after diagram contrasting the pre-cap oil market with transparent pricing and Western shipping and insurance against the post-cap market with a wide Urals discount, a shadow fleet, parallel insurance, and opaque ownership chains.](/imgs/blogs/sanctioned-commodity-markets-shadow-fleets-price-gaps-arbitrage-4.png)

In the **pre-cap world**, Russia sold the bulk of its crude to Europe at Brent minus a small quality discount. The cargoes moved on mainstream tankers, insured by the London-centered protection-and-indemnity clubs that cover the overwhelming majority of world tonnage, financed through ordinary trade-finance channels at major Western banks, and priced transparently in the daily Platts assessment windows that the whole industry references. Anyone could see who owned the ship, who insured it, and roughly what the cargo cost. It was a boring, legible, low-friction market.

In the **post-cap world**, that legibility is gone by design. The crude sells at a wide and variable discount. It moves on a shadow fleet whose ownership is hidden and whose insurance is a parallel, non-Western system of uncertain solvency. The financing is done in currencies and through banks that sit outside the reach of Western enforcement — rupees, yuan, dirhams routed through banks in friendly jurisdictions. And the pricing is opaque: the headline Urals assessments became increasingly notional as more trade happened in private bilateral deals at undisclosed prices, to the point where price-reporting agencies struggled to know what Urals "really" traded at. The market did not shrink. It went dark.

This forking has a second-order consequence that matters for everyone, not just sanctions wonks. A parallel shipping and insurance system that operates outside mainstream safety oversight, using ancient tankers with questionable cover, is an **environmental and navigational time bomb**. An aging, uninsured shadow tanker that runs aground in the Baltic or the Danish straits could cause a spill that no insurer pays for, and these ships transit some of the most crowded and ecologically sensitive waters on earth. The risk that the West is implicitly accepting — that an accident forces the issue — is one of the live policy debates around the fleet, and it is also, for a trader, a tail risk that could suddenly re-widen the discount if a disaster triggers a crackdown.

## The refining laundromat

The single most powerful node in the whole system is the refinery, because it is where a barrel changes nationality. This deserves its own treatment because it is both the economic engine of the arbitrage and the legal seam that the entire trade depends on.

![Pipeline diagram of the Urals-Brent refining arbitrage showing discounted crude bought by an Indian refiner, the refining cost deducted, refined product sold at the global benchmark, and the net margin captured.](/imgs/blogs/sanctioned-commodity-markets-shadow-fleets-price-gaps-arbitrage-6.png)

India is the textbook case. Before the war, India bought almost no Russian crude — its refiners ran mostly Middle Eastern and West African grades, and the freight from the Baltic to India made Urals uneconomic. After the war, with Urals trading at a \$20-to-\$30 discount, the freight no longer mattered; the crude was so cheap that it paid to ship it halfway around the world. Indian crude imports from Russia went from negligible to roughly 40 percent of India's total crude intake within a year, an astonishing reorientation of one of the world's largest oil-importing economies.

What India's refiners do with that crude is the crux. They refine it into products and sell those products — including a meaningful volume of diesel and jet fuel that flows to Europe, the very market that banned Russian crude. This is the "laundering" or "refining loophole": because the product is legally Indian, its sale to Europe breaks no rule. European policymakers are well aware of this; whether to close the loophole by tracing the crude origin of imported products has been an active and contentious debate, because doing so would penalize a strategically important partner and could disrupt Europe's own diesel supply. The loophole persists because closing it is costly and politically fraught — which is itself a lesson in how sanctions regimes leave the seams they can afford to leave.

The economics are why everyone plays along. The refiner buys crude at a discount and sells product at no discount, capturing the gap minus its processing cost. The trading houses that arrange the cargoes — some mainstream, many newly-formed, opaque outfits that sprang up specifically to handle Russian barrels — take a cut for managing the logistics, the financing, and the compliance gymnastics. And Russia gets a buyer for volume it could not otherwise sell. The arbitrage is *self-funding and self-reinforcing*: the more refiners that build the muscle to handle discounted Russian crude, the more competition there is for those barrels, which narrows the discount, which is the mechanism by which the gap closed over 2023 and 2024.

#### Worked example: the refiner's margin math, in detail

Put yourself in the seat of an Indian refiner deciding whether to take a Russian cargo.

- **Your alternative.** You can buy a comparable Middle Eastern medium-sour grade delivered for, say, \$82/bbl (Brent \$80 plus freight and a small quality premium).
- **The Russian offer.** A cargo of Urals delivered to your terminal at \$60/bbl all-in — that is the discounted crude price plus the shadow-fleet freight and handling, already baked in.
- **Your crude cost advantage.** \$82 − \$60 = \$22/bbl. On a 1-million-barrel cargo, that is \$22 million of cheaper feedstock.
- **Your product yield.** Refining is, to first approximation, conserving: a barrel of crude yields roughly a barrel of products (with some volume gain). The products — diesel, gasoline, jet — sell at the same global benchmarks regardless of which crude you fed in, because nobody can tell the diesel's crude origin once it is in the tank.
- **Your incremental processing cost.** Say \$10/bbl to run the cheaper, perhaps slightly more sour, crude through your units.
- **Net uplift.** \$22 − \$10 = \$12/bbl of extra margin you would not have earned on the Middle Eastern barrel, or about \$12 million per cargo.

Against that \$12 million you weigh the enforcement and reputational risk: the chance that a Western regulator decides your purchasing crosses a line, that a specific tanker you used gets designated mid-voyage, or that a bank refuses to finance your next cargo. For a large refiner with a government that has chosen not to join the sanctions, that risk has been low and the \$12 million is close to free money. That asymmetry — large, certain margin against small, diffuse risk — is precisely why the trade scaled so fast.

## The money side: how a sanctioned cargo actually gets paid for

It is easy to fixate on the ships and forget that every cargo is also a payment, and payment is where sanctions were supposed to bite hardest. Cutting major Russian banks off from SWIFT and freezing the central bank's reserves was meant to make it operationally impossible to settle large oil trades. In practice, the trade rerouted its money exactly as it rerouted its molecules.

The first adaptation was **currency**. A cargo that once would have been invoiced and settled in dollars through a Western correspondent bank is now frequently settled in rupees, yuan, or dirhams. India's purchases of Russian crude have been paid, at various points, in rupees held in special accounts, in dirhams routed through banks in the UAE, and in yuan — each arrangement chosen to keep the dollar, and therefore US jurisdiction, out of the transaction. The moment a payment touches no dollar and no US bank, the most powerful Western enforcement lever — the threat to cut a bank off from dollar clearing — has nothing to grip.

The second adaptation was **the banking channel**. The settlement moves through banks in jurisdictions that have chosen not to enforce the sanctions and that have limited exposure to the US financial system: regional banks in the Gulf, Chinese banks willing to handle the flow, and a constellation of smaller institutions that sprang up or pivoted to service the trade. Russia's own domestic payment-messaging system and China's cross-border yuan settlement infrastructure provide rails that do not depend on Western messaging networks at all. None of these rails is as efficient or as cheap as the dollar-clearing system they replace — settlement is slower, costlier, and clunkier — and that friction is itself part of the discount the seller absorbs. But "slower and clunkier" is not "impossible," and a determined buyer-seller pair with a few billion dollars at stake will absorb a great deal of friction.

The third adaptation, and the most quietly consequential, was **non-convertibility risk**. When India paid for Russian crude in rupees, Russia ended up holding enormous balances of a currency it could not easily spend, because India runs a trade deficit with few things Russia wants to buy in return and the rupee is not freely convertible. For a stretch, Russia had billions of rupees stranded in Indian banks that it could not repatriate or convert at scale. This is a genuine cost of the parallel system: the seller may book the revenue but find it trapped in a currency it cannot use, which is a softer but real form of the sanction biting. It is also why the choice of settlement currency became its own negotiation, with sellers pushing for yuan or dirhams they could actually deploy and buyers leveraging their currency's inconvertibility as a bargaining chip.

#### Worked example: the all-in cost of the parallel payment rail

Consider a \$54.5 million cargo (1 million barrels at the \$54.5/bbl all-in delivered cost from the earlier example) settled outside the dollar system.

- **Currency conversion spread.** Converting the buyer's local currency into something the seller can use, through thinner offshore markets, might cost 1 to 2 percent versus a clean dollar settlement. On \$54.5 million, 1.5 percent is roughly \$0.8 million.
- **Banking friction.** Slower settlement ties up working capital and demands higher fees from the obscure banks willing to handle it; call it another \$0.3 to \$0.5 million per cargo in financing and fees.
- **Non-convertibility haircut.** If a portion of the proceeds lands in a currency the seller cannot fully deploy, the *effective* value of that tranche is impaired. If, say, a quarter of the proceeds is stuck in a soft currency worth 90 cents on the dollar to the seller, that is a \$1.4 million effective haircut.
- **Total payment-side cost.** Roughly \$2.5 to \$3 million on a \$54.5 million cargo, or about \$2.5 to \$3/bbl.

That \$2.5-to-\$3/bbl is part of the discount that never shows up in the ship or the refinery; it is the cost of the *money* moving outside the dollar system. Add it to the freight premium and the transshipment cost and you can account for most of the gap between the wide Urals discount and the pure quality-and-freight discount of the pre-war market. **The discount is not one number; it is a stack of frictions — shipping, handling, payment, and pure risk premium — and decomposing it tells you which ones will fade as the trade matures and which are structural.**

## The sanctions paradox: revenue went up

Here is the fact that most embarrasses the theory behind commodity sanctions: in the first year, Russia's oil revenue *rose*.

![Bar chart of Russian oil and gas export revenue showing 117 billion dollars in 2021, rising to 218 billion in 2022 as prices spiked, then falling to 160 billion in 2023 as cap enforcement bit.](/imgs/blogs/sanctioned-commodity-markets-shadow-fleets-price-gaps-arbitrage-5.png)

Russian oil and gas export revenue was roughly \$117 billion in 2021, before the war. In 2022 — the year of the invasion, the SWIFT cutoffs, the reserve freeze, and the run-up to the price cap — it *jumped* to roughly \$218 billion. Only in 2023, as the cap's enforcement and the discount's persistence took hold, did it fall back to roughly \$160 billion, still well above the pre-war level.

The mechanism is the paradox at the heart of commodity sanctions. Sanctioning a major producer's exports does not just affect that producer; it raises the *global price* of the commodity, because the market fears supply loss. In 2022, the fear of losing Russian barrels drove Brent toward \$120 at points, and that price spike more than offset the per-barrel discount Russia had to swallow. Russia sold its oil at a steep discount to a high global price — and a steep discount to a high price can still be more, in absolute dollars, than no discount to a normal price. The very act of sanctioning a swing producer hands that producer a windfall on the inframarginal barrels it can still sell.

This is the general trap. **You cannot meaningfully sanction a commodity that the world genuinely needs without raising its price, and raising its price partially funds the entity you are trying to starve.** The only ways out are to (a) replace the lost supply from elsewhere so the price does not spike — which is what the price cap, by keeping Russian oil flowing rather than embargoing it, was explicitly designed to do — or (b) accept the price spike and the producer windfall as the cost of the policy. The 2022-to-2023 path shows the cap eventually working *as designed*: it kept the barrels flowing (so the price came back down) while capping the per-barrel revenue (so the discount stuck). Revenue fell from \$218 billion to \$160 billion not because Russia sold less oil, but because the global price normalized while the discount persisted. That is the cap doing its job — slowly, and imperfectly, and far less dramatically than an embargo would have promised on paper.

#### Worked example: decomposing the 2022 revenue jump

It is worth separating the two forces — price and discount — to see why revenue rose despite the sanction.

- **2021 baseline.** Suppose Russia exported oil at an average realized price of \$65/bbl on its export volume, generating \$117 billion.
- **2022, the naive sanction view.** If sanctions had simply forced a \$25/bbl discount with no change in the global price, realized price would have fallen to \$40/bbl and revenue would have *dropped* sharply. That is the outcome policymakers implicitly expected.
- **2022, what actually happened.** The global benchmark spiked — average Brent for 2022 was far above 2021. Even after swallowing a \$20-to-\$30 discount, Russia's *realized* price rose, because the discount came off a much higher base. Combine a higher realized price with still-substantial volumes and you get \$218 billion, not a collapse.
- **2023, the cap bites.** The benchmark normalized back toward the \$80s, removing the windfall, while the discount stayed wide. Realized price fell, and revenue dropped to \$160 billion.

The lesson for anyone modeling commodity sanctions: **always decompose realized revenue into price times volume times (one minus discount).** Sanctions reliably push the discount up and may push volume down, but they also push the global price up, and in the short run the price effect can dominate. Only when supply has rerouted and the price has normalized does the discount effect show up cleanly in the revenue line.

## How the fleet was built, in sequence

The shadow fleet did not appear overnight. It assembled in a recognizable sequence that tracks the escalation of the sanctions, and understanding the sequence helps you anticipate how the *next* sanctioned-commodity market will evolve.

![Timeline of the shadow fleet buildup from the February 2022 invasion through the Western majors' exit, the widening Urals discount, the fleet forming at around one hundred vessels, the December 2022 price cap, the peak of around four hundred vessels and a thirty-dollar discount, and roughly six hundred vessels with a narrowed discount by 2024.](/imgs/blogs/sanctioned-commodity-markets-shadow-fleets-price-gaps-arbitrage-7.png)

The sequence runs like this. The invasion in February 2022 triggered immediate buyer flight. By March, the Western oil majors — Shell, BP, ExxonMobil — were announcing exits from Russian assets and taking large write-downs, and Western trading houses began winding down Russian business. Through the spring, the Urals discount widened as this self-sanctioning bit, well before any law required it. By around August 2022, with it clear that a formal embargo and cap were coming, the first wave of the shadow fleet formed — perhaps a hundred aging tankers bought up by undisclosed owners specifically to carry the barrels the mainstream fleet was abandoning.

The December 2022 price cap and the EU's seaborne crude embargo were the forcing function. They made it legally impossible for mainstream, Western-serviced ships to carry above-cap Russian crude, which turned the shadow fleet from an opportunistic side trade into core infrastructure. Through the first quarter of 2023 the fleet swelled toward 400 vessels and the discount peaked near \$30. Over the rest of 2023 and into 2024 the fleet kept growing — toward 600 vessels — even as the discount *narrowed* to around \$12, because by then the bottleneck was no longer ships; it was buyer comfort, and buyer comfort had grown. The fleet overshot the discount, in a sense: there was enough tonnage to move the barrels easily, so the scarcity premium on shadow shipping fell, and that fed through to a narrower all-in discount for buyers.

This sequence — buyer flight, self-sanctioning, fleet formation, legal forcing function, fleet maturation, discount compression — is the template. It is roughly what happened with Iran over a longer timescale, and it is what you should expect if a major producer is sanctioned in the future. The early discount is wide because the infrastructure is thin and the risk is uncertain; the late discount is narrow because the infrastructure is built and the risk has been priced and found tolerable.

## Iran, Venezuela, and North Korea: the precedents

Russia scaled the playbook, but it did not write it. Looking at the predecessors tells you where the Russian trade is heading and how durable it is likely to be.

**Iran** is the deepest precedent. Four decades of US sanctions, intensified after the 2018 US withdrawal from the nuclear deal, taught Iran to move crude almost entirely outside the Western system. Iranian crude moves on a dark fleet to China's independent "teapot" refiners, which are small, private, and have little exposure to the US financial system, so the threat of US secondary sanctions bites them less. Iranian crude trades at a discount of perhaps \$5 to \$15/bbl to comparable grades — *narrower* than Russia's peak discount, because the Iran trade is mature and the buyer base, while small, is committed and price-sensitive. The Iran case shows the steady state: once a sanctioned trade has run for years, the discount settles into a narrow, stable band that reflects the routine cost of the parallel infrastructure rather than acute enforcement fear.

**Venezuela** shows the same pattern under a different commodity profile. US sanctions on the state oil company PDVSA pushed Venezuelan heavy crude onto a dark fleet bound mostly for China and, intermittently, India, at deep discounts reflecting both the sanction and the difficulty of refining very heavy, sour crude. The Venezuela case also shows the *reversibility* of sanctions: when Washington eased restrictions to court the government or stabilize global supply, Venezuelan barrels partly re-entered the legitimate market and the discount narrowed sharply — a reminder that the sanction discount is a policy variable, and that a trader long the discount is implicitly short a political decision.

**North Korea** is the smallest and most instructive on enforcement. UN sanctions ban North Korean coal exports and cap its refined-product imports, and the United Nations Panel of Experts has documented in granular detail how the regime evades them: ship-to-ship transfers at sea, AIS spoofing, falsified documents, and a network of complicit vessels and front companies. The North Korean trade is tiny, but it proves the floor: even a heavily sanctioned, diplomatically isolated, economically marginal state can sustain a sanctioned-commodity trade if it is willing to operate a dark fleet and finds even one large, tolerant buyer. If North Korea can do it at the margins, a producer the size of Russia can do it at scale.

#### Worked example: why Iran's discount is narrower than Russia's was

A trader might be puzzled that Iran, under harsher and older sanctions, sells at a *smaller* discount than Russia did at the peak. The reason is instructive.

- **Russia, early 2023.** The trade was new. Buyers were few and nervous, the fleet was still forming, and enforcement intent was fresh and uncertain. To clear roughly 3 million barrels a day into a thin, anxious buyer base, the discount had to be large — \$30/bbl — to compensate buyers for taking on novel, uncertain risk and to outbid each barrel's last reluctant taker.
- **Iran, steady state.** The trade is decades old, the fleet is established, and the buyer base (Chinese teapots) is committed and has long since priced the routine risk. The volume to clear is smaller — on the order of 1 to 2 million barrels a day — and it flows to buyers who treat the discount as a normal cost of doing business. So the discount settles at \$5 to \$15/bbl: enough to cover the parallel infrastructure and a modest risk premium, no more.

The general rule: **the sanction discount is widest when the trade is new and the buyer base is thin, and it compresses as the trade matures and buyers grow comfortable.** Russia's discount narrowing from \$30 toward \$12 over two years is simply the Russia trade aging into something closer to the Iran steady state. A trader who understood this in early 2023 — that the \$30 discount was a new-trade premium destined to compress — had one of the cleaner macro views available in the energy complex.

## Trading the sanctioned market

How does a professional actually express a view here? There are a handful of distinct trades, each with a different risk profile, and it is worth being precise about what you are really betting on in each.

**The discount-compression trade.** If you believe a new sanction discount is a new-trade premium that will narrow as infrastructure builds, you want exposure to the spread compressing. In physical markets this is expressed by the refiners and traders who lock in cheap crude and sell product at benchmark, capturing the spread directly. In paper markets it is harder, because Urals is not a deeply liquid futures contract; you may have to proxy it through related grades, freight rates, or refining-margin (crack spread) instruments. The risk is a *re-widening*: an enforcement crackdown, a designated-ship event, or an accident that spooks buyers can blow the discount back out, and you are short that tail.

**The crack-spread trade.** The refining arbitrage is structurally a crack spread — the margin between crude and product. A refiner with access to discounted sanctioned crude has a structurally fatter crack than one without. You can express a view on this by trading refining-margin products or the equities of refiners with the access (and the risk tolerance) to run sanctioned barrels. The risk here is policy: if the refining loophole is closed, that fat crack evaporates overnight.

**The freight trade.** The shadow fleet's formation tightened the market for old tankers and, at times, the broader tanker market, because tonnage tied up in long, indirect sanctioned voyages is tonnage unavailable for normal trade. Tanker rates and tanker-owner equities became, in part, a play on the sanctioned trade. The risk is fleet maturation: as enough shadow tonnage is assembled, the scarcity premium fades.

**The enforcement-risk trade.** The cleanest framing of all of these is that you are pricing enforcement risk. The discount *is* the enforcement risk premium plus infrastructure cost. If you think enforcement will intensify — more ship designations, more pressure on insurers and registries, more secondary-sanction threats — you want to be positioned for the discount to *widen*. If you think enforcement will stay theatrical and the trade will be quietly tolerated, you want to be positioned for it to narrow. Every position in this market is, underneath, a bet on how serious the sanctioning governments really are.

A word on the information edge, because it is where the real money in this market is made and lost. The sanctioned-commodity trade is deliberately opaque, which means the participants with the best *data* — not the best macro view — capture the most value. The price-reporting agencies that assess Urals struggled, as the trade went dark, to know what the grade truly traded at, because more and more deals happened in private bilateral arrangements at undisclosed prices. Into that information vacuum stepped a small industry of vessel-tracking analysts, satellite-imagery firms, and trade-flow specialists who reconstruct the hidden trade from its physical footprint: which tankers loaded where, which went dark and where they reappeared, which conducted ship-to-ship transfers, and where the cargoes ultimately landed. A trader who can see the real flow — who knows that loadings are rising even as official data says otherwise, or that a designation wave has stranded more tonnage than the market realizes — has an edge over one trading off the stale, increasingly fictional headline assessments. In opaque markets, the edge migrates from those who interpret public prices to those who reconstruct private reality, and the sanctioned-commodity market is about as opaque as a multi-trillion-dollar trade gets.

The corollary is a warning. Precisely because the market is opaque, it is also a market where you can be very wrong while feeling very informed. The headline Urals discount you see on a screen may not be the price at which meaningful volume is actually clearing; the fleet-size estimates you build a thesis on are estimates of something designed to evade counting; and the enforcement posture you are implicitly betting on can change by political decision overnight, with no market signal beforehand. The discipline this demands is humility about your own information and explicit, separate sizing for the policy tail. The trader who blew up in this market was rarely the one with the wrong macro view; it was the one who treated an opaque, politically-driven, discontinuous spread as if it were a liquid, transparent, mean-reverting one.

#### Worked example: sizing the discount-compression trade

Suppose it is early 2023. Urals is at a \$30 discount and your thesis is that it compresses toward \$12 over eighteen months as the fleet matures and India scales up, mirroring the Iran path.

- **Your view.** The discount narrows by roughly \$18/bbl over the horizon.
- **Expression.** You are a trading house that can lock in Urals cargoes at the wide discount and sell refined product at benchmark. On a steady program of, say, 5 million barrels a month, an \$18/bbl improvement in your realized crude cost (as you ride the compression) is worth \$90 million a month at the extreme — though in practice you capture a slice of it, shared with the refiner and competed away by other buyers entering.
- **The risk you are short.** A single dramatic enforcement event — a Baltic spill from a designated shadow tanker, a fresh tranche of US secondary sanctions on the insurers, a major buyer pulling back — could snap the discount from \$12 back toward \$25 in weeks. If you are long inventory bought at the narrow discount, that re-widening is a mark-to-market loss.
- **Sizing.** Because the tail is a discrete policy event rather than a smooth distribution, you cannot hedge it with normal volatility instruments; you size the position so that a \$10-to-\$15/bbl adverse re-widening is survivable, not ruinous. The whole trade is really a bet that the policy stays gradualist, and you size it as you would any position with a fat, non-hedgeable political tail: smaller than the expected value alone would justify.

## The enforcement seam and where it can break

The discount is narrow today because enforcement has been, in practice, gradualist. Western governments have preferred to keep Russian oil flowing — to avoid a price spike that would hurt their own consumers — over choking it off, and that preference is the deepest reason the trade works. But the enforcement posture is not static, and a practitioner needs a map of where it could tighten and snap the discount back out.

The first pressure point is **ship designations.** Sanctioning authorities have increasingly named specific vessels, which makes those ships radioactive: ports refuse them, insurers will not touch them, and they can be stranded mid-voyage. Each designation removes a ship from the usable fleet and forces the cargo onto scarcer tonnage, which widens the effective discount. The countermeasure — re-flagging, renaming, and re-shelling the vessel — works but adds cost and friction, and a fast enough designation pace can outrun the fleet's ability to launder its own ships.

The second is **the insurance and services chokehold.** The original cap relied on Western dominance of maritime insurance. As the shadow fleet moved to parallel, non-Western insurance, that lever weakened — but it did not vanish, because shadow insurance is of uncertain quality and many ports, canals, and straits demand credible cover to grant passage. Pressure on registries to deny flags, on ports to demand real insurance, and on the Gulf transshipment hubs to scrutinize cargoes can all raise friction.

The third is **secondary sanctions on buyers and banks**, the Iran-style escalation. If Washington decided to threaten the banks financing Russian crude purchases with loss of dollar access, even large buyers like India's refiners would have to reassess, and the discount would widen to compensate them for the new, sharper risk. This lever has been mostly held in reserve for Russia, precisely because using it would risk the price spike the West has worked to avoid — which tells you the constraint is political, not technical.

The fourth, and the wildest, is **an accident.** A major spill from an aging, uninsured shadow tanker in a sensitive waterway — the Baltic, the Danish straits, the Bosphorus — could force a political reckoning that no policymaker currently wants but none can rule out. Such an event would be the kind of discontinuous shock that snaps a slowly-compressing discount violently wider, and it is the single largest tail risk embedded in any long-discount-compression position.

The unifying point is that **the sanctioned-commodity market is a regime, not a constant.** Its quiet, narrow-discount state is the product of a gradualist enforcement choice that could change. A practitioner who treats the current narrow discount as permanent is mispricing the tail; a practitioner who understands that the discount is the price of an enforcement *posture* — and that postures change — is the one positioned to profit when the regime shifts.

## What generalizes

Step back from oil and the lessons apply to any sanctioned commodity, and to the next crisis as much as this one.

First, **fungible commodities reroute; they do not disappear.** Anything the world genuinely needs — crude, refined products, gas, coal, key metals, fertilizer — will find a buyer at a price, and the sanction's main effect is to set the discount, not to stop the flow. Sanctions on a commodity are a tax on its trade routed to whoever bears the evasion cost and risk, not a switch that turns the trade off.

Second, **the discount is the price of enforcement risk plus parallel-infrastructure cost.** It is wide when the trade is new, enforcement is credible and fresh, and the dark infrastructure is thin; it narrows as the trade matures, buyers grow comfortable, and the fleet and financing rails are built out. This is a mean-reverting spread with a regime overlay, and it is tradable as such by anyone who can map the enforcement posture.

Third, **sanctioning a swing producer can raise the global price and partially fund the target.** In the short run the price effect can swamp the discount effect, which is why a year-one revenue *increase* is the rule rather than the exception. The honest version of a commodity sanction either replaces the lost supply (the price-cap approach, keeping the barrels flowing) or accepts the producer windfall as the cost of the policy.

Fourth, **the laundering node is wherever the commodity changes form or origin** — for oil, the refinery; for metals, the smelter or the re-export hub; for diamonds, the cutting center. That node is the legal seam the whole evasion trade depends on, and whether to close it is always a costly, politically fraught decision that sanctioning governments often decline to make.

Fourth-and-a-half, a point that ties the others together: **the seller's revenue and the buyer's margin are paid out of the same discount, which means the sanctioning government is implicitly subsidizing both the target and the target's customers.** Russia gives up \$20 to \$30 a barrel at the wellhead; that giveaway becomes the Indian refiner's fat crack, the trading house's fee, and the shadow operator's premium freight. The sanction did not destroy value; it redistributed it, away from Russia's per-barrel take and toward a chain of intermediaries who are paid precisely for absorbing the enforcement risk. A policymaker who understands this sees the uncomfortable truth that a leaky sanction is not merely ineffective — it actively enriches a network of evasion specialists, some of whom are based in countries the sanctioning government would rather not strengthen. The choice to tolerate the leak, to keep the barrels flowing and the price low, is a real choice with a real cost, and the discount is the running tally of who collects it.

Fifth, and most important for a trader, **it is a regime, not a constant.** Treat the narrow, quiet, mature-trade discount as the current regime, hold an explicit view on what would flip it — a designation wave, a secondary-sanctions escalation, a tanker disaster — and size your positions for the discontinuous shift rather than the smooth path. The people who lost money in this market were the ones who extrapolated either the wide early discount or the narrow late discount as permanent. The people who made money understood that the discount was the market's running estimate of how serious the sanctioning governments really were — and traded the gap between that estimate and their own read of the politics.

The shadow fleet, the Urals discount, the Indian refining loophole, the parallel insurance system — these are not exotic curiosities. They are the predictable, almost mechanical response of a fungible commodity market to a sanction it can route around, and they will reassemble, in recognizable form, the next time a major producer is cut off. Knowing the shape of that response in advance — the fleet that forms, the discount that swells and then compresses, the refinery that erases the origin, the payment that routes around the dollar — is the entire edge, and it is an edge that travels intact to the next sanctioned commodity and the next crisis.
