> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — OPEC+ is no longer just a revenue-smoothing cartel; since the 2016 Vienna Alliance it has been a Saudi-Russia geopolitical coordination mechanism that uses oil supply as a weapon, a reward, and a price floor.
>
> - The October 2022 2 mb/d cut was not a market-balance decision — it was a deliberate snub of U.S. President Biden three weeks before midterm elections, signaling that Riyadh answers to Riyadh, not Washington.
> - Saudi Arabia's fiscal break-even price (~\$80/bbl) acts as an implicit "OPEC put" floor: when Brent drops below that level, a cut is almost certain.
> - Russia uses OPEC+ membership to legitimize its exports (oil still priced at international benchmarks), evade economic isolation, and maintain geopolitical standing with Gulf monarchies it needs as diplomatic cover.
> - For traders, the critical signal is not JMMC communiques — it is the bilateral Saudi-Russia phone calls that happen 3-6 weeks before any formal meeting.

On the afternoon of 5 October 2022, Joe Biden's National Security Advisor Jake Sullivan was still on the phone with Saudi officials, urging Riyadh to hold the line on production. The next morning, OPEC+ announced a 2 million barrel-per-day production cut — the largest since the COVID crash of 2020. Brent crude jumped from \$88 to \$97 in 48 hours. White House officials called it "shortsighted." Saudi energy minister Prince Abdulaziz bin Salman called it "purely technical." Both sides knew that was not true.

That moment was the clearest demonstration in decades that OPEC+ had evolved into something qualitatively different from the supply-management body it had always claimed to be. It had become a geopolitical instrument, operated primarily by Saudi Arabia and Russia, capable of inflicting or relieving economic pain on Western consumers as a function of diplomatic signaling — not market fundamentals.

![OPEC+ production cut decisions overlaid on Brent crude price from 2020 to 2025](/imgs/blogs/opec-plus-as-a-political-actor-production-cuts-and-market-power-1.png)

This post maps the full anatomy of how that instrument works: how cut decisions are actually made (the Riyadh-Moscow calls that precede every formal meeting), the specific mechanics of the 2022, 2023, and 2024 decisions, the "OPEC put" price floor concept that any oil trader must understand, and — most importantly — how to read and trade a market where supply is a policy variable, not a market outcome.

## Foundations: how oil markets and cartels work

Before OPEC+ can be understood as a geopolitical actor, it needs to be understood as an economic structure. Let's build from the ground up.

**Oil is not like wheat or copper.** Most commodity markets are self-correcting: if the price of copper rises, miners invest in new mines, supply increases, and the price falls. If it drops, marginal mines close, supply falls, and the price recovers. This feedback loop is slow but reliable — it works over years. Oil has those same dynamics, but with one critical difference: a handful of countries hold 70% of the world's proven low-cost reserves, and those countries can choose not to pump.

Saudi Arabia can produce a barrel of oil for roughly \$3-5 at the wellhead. A U.S. shale producer needs \$50-60/bbl to break even on a new well. That means Saudi Arabia has a structural ability to set a floor under prices: it can pump less, let prices recover, and still make more money per barrel than most competitors make at any price.

**The cartel problem — and why OPEC kept failing.** A cartel works by getting all members to restrict output simultaneously. But every member faces the same incentive to cheat: if everyone else cuts, the price rises, and you can sell more barrels at the higher price while everyone else is selling fewer. Iraq famously did this throughout the 1980s and 1990s. Nigeria, Angola, and Venezuela chronically over-produced against their OPEC quotas. The result was a cartel that set quotas nobody fully obeyed — which is why oil prices were volatile and often below the \$60-80/bbl range most Gulf states needed for their national budgets.

**OPEC+ as the structural fix.** In November 2016, Saudi oil minister Khalid al-Falih and Russian energy minister Alexander Novak struck an unprecedented deal: Russia, not an OPEC member, would coordinate production cuts alongside OPEC nations. The resulting "Vienna Alliance" — 13 OPEC members plus 10 non-OPEC states, chiefly Russia — covered roughly 55% of global oil supply. For the first time, Russia's 10-11 million barrels per day of production was inside a coordinated supply management framework.

The addition of Russia changed the game structurally. Russia is too large to be dictated to, and it is not a Gulf monarchy dependent on American security guarantees. That made the alliance something genuinely new: a coalition of producers with divergent political interests who nonetheless share one overriding common interest — an oil price high enough to fund their state budgets and geopolitical ambitions.

**The fiscal break-even price.** Every oil-exporting state has a fiscal break-even — the oil price at which the government's budget balances. This is the single most important number for understanding OPEC+ behavior:

- Saudi Arabia: ~\$78-85/bbl (varies by year depending on Vision 2030 spending)
- Russia: ~\$60-65/bbl (lower, partly because the ruble has depreciated)
- Iraq: ~\$80-85/bbl
- UAE: ~\$65-70/bbl
- Nigeria: ~\$100/bbl (the perennial crisis case)

These numbers explain why Saudi Arabia, not Russia or Iraq, effectively drives cut decisions. Russia has a lower break-even partly because its currency is weaker, so dollar oil revenues go further domestically. But Saudi Arabia's Vision 2030 program — Riyadh's massive economic modernization plan — requires higher oil prices to fund the giant sovereign transfers and mega-projects that underpin MBS's political legitimacy. When Brent drops below \$80, Saudi Arabia faces a structural budget deficit. That is the trigger for a cut.

## The political moves: how Riyadh and Moscow actually decide

The formal process for an OPEC+ decision runs through the Joint Ministerial Monitoring Committee (JMMC) and then the full ministerial meeting. But the formal process is theater. The real decision happens in bilateral communications between Riyadh and Moscow that precede any formal meeting by weeks.

![Anatomy of an OPEC+ cut decision from bilateral call to compliance scorecard](/imgs/blogs/opec-plus-as-a-political-actor-production-cuts-and-market-power-2.png)

**The Saudi-Russia bilateral.** When Saudi Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman (MBS) or Energy Minister Prince Abdulaziz bin Salman decides a cut is needed, the first call is not to OPEC's Vienna secretariat. It is to Alexander Novak, Russia's deputy prime minister for energy (the same man who has managed the Russia-OPEC relationship since 2014). Saudi Aramco's CEO and senior Rosneft officials also maintain back-channel technical communications. By the time the JMMC convenes, Riyadh and Moscow have already agreed on the size and framing of the cut. The remaining 21 OPEC+ members are then brought in to validate a decision already made.

This Saudi-Russia bilateral dynamic is why watching OPEC+ communiques as trading signals is often useless — what you need to watch are geopolitical signals of Saudi-Russian alignment or divergence weeks before any formal meeting.

**The JMMC — technical cover for political decisions.** The Joint Ministerial Monitoring Committee meets monthly. It was created in 2017 ostensibly to monitor compliance with agreed cuts. In practice, it serves as the forum where the Saudi-Russia deal gets packaged into "market-balance" language. The JMMC publishes forecasts of supply-demand balances that invariably support whatever cut the two principals have already decided on. This is not unique to OPEC — central bank meetings work similarly: the real rate decision is made in bilateral Governors' conversations; the formal vote is documentation.

**Why Russia needs OPEC+.** Russia's motivation for staying in OPEC+ has deepened since 2022. Before the Ukraine invasion, Russia needed the higher oil prices OPEC+ coordination produced. After the invasion, Russia needs OPEC+ for three additional reasons:

First, OPEC+ membership gives Russia a diplomatic platform and a forum at which it can present itself as a responsible global actor alongside respected Gulf monarchies — a form of legitimacy and access that Western sanctions seek to deny.

Second, the Gulf states are Russia's primary alternative financial partners. Saudi Arabia and the UAE have both declined to join Western sanctions on Russia. The OPEC+ coordination relationship gives Moscow leverage to prevent Gulf states from being pressured into tightening their Russia policies.

Third, Russian oil exports increasingly flow to India and China — nations that also import Gulf oil. Being inside the OPEC+ framework means Russia is aligned (rather than competing) with Gulf producers in the pricing of non-Western crude benchmarks like Urals and ESPO Blend.

**Saudi Arabia's domestic political logic.** MBS's power rests on his ability to fund the Saudi state — the giant social transfers, infrastructure programs, and military spending that keep the royal family's patronage networks intact. Vision 2030, the economic diversification plan that is the cornerstone of his rule, requires sustained investment that cannot survive oil at \$60/bbl. Every major cut decision by Saudi Arabia should be read through this lens: it is domestic political survival as much as market strategy.

When Biden called King Salman in June 2022 asking for more production to lower U.S. gasoline prices — a politically charged request ahead of midterm elections — the Saudi response was essentially: we have our own political requirements, and lower oil prices are not in them. The subsequent October cut was the formal demonstration of that message.

**The 23-nation vote — and why the other 21 are irrelevant.** On paper, OPEC+ is a body of 23 sovereign nations that vote on production policy. In practice, the core decisions are made by two: Saudi Arabia and Russia. Iraq, which chronically over-produces against its quota, is accommodated rather than disciplined. The UAE occasionally pushes for larger baseline production allocations — it has more spare capacity than its quota reflects — but ultimately accepts Saudi terms. Nigeria and Venezuela produce below their quotas not by choice but because their infrastructure is in decline. The small West African members (Gabon, Congo, Equatorial Guinea) have minimal spare capacity and zero ability to shift the group's decision.

The functional structure is a hegemon (Saudi Arabia), a co-hegemon (Russia), a swing junior partner (UAE), a chronic cheater tolerated for political reasons (Iraq), and a dozen members whose quotas exist on paper.

## The financial channels: how cuts move markets

A production cut by OPEC+ affects markets through four channels that work on different time horizons.

![OPEC+ geopolitical leverage across country and dimension](/imgs/blogs/opec-plus-as-a-political-actor-production-cuts-and-market-power-3.png)

**Channel 1: Physical supply reduction.** When OPEC+ announces a cut, the actual barrels removed from the market typically begin flowing through within 30-60 days. Saudi Arabia, with spare capacity of roughly 2-2.5 mb/d, can implement its share of a cut within weeks. Russia's cuts are more complex — Russian oil production infrastructure cannot easily swing up or down, and Russia has historically been one of the least compliant OPEC+ members (more on this below). The physical supply reduction shows up in OECD crude inventory draws — the key confirming data point that traders watch in the weeks following a cut announcement.

**Channel 2: Futures price repricing.** The immediate market reaction to a cut announcement is almost entirely in futures, not physical barrels. Oil futures are highly liquid (Brent front-month trades roughly 1 million contracts daily), and algorithmic systems respond to OPEC+ announcements in seconds. The typical pattern: announcement → front-month Brent +3-5% within the first trading hour → backwardation strengthens (front-month trades above back months) → physical refiners rush to lock in supply before the cut materializes. This is the channel that makes oil one of the most geopolitically tradeable commodities.

**Channel 3: Petrodollar recycling and asset flows.** Higher oil prices mean larger Saudi, UAE, and Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC) sovereign wealth fund (SWF) inflows. Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund (PIF), Abu Dhabi Investment Authority (ADIA), and Kuwait Investment Authority (KIA) collectively manage over \$2.5 trillion. When oil prices rise sustainably, these funds increase their allocations to international equities, real estate, and private markets — a meaningful positive flow for asset prices globally. Conversely, when oil prices collapse, SWFs become net sellers to cover domestic fiscal deficits. This channel is real but slow — it operates over quarters, not days.

**Channel 4: Inflation and consumer economy impact.** Higher oil prices raise headline CPI in oil-importing economies (the U.S., Europe, Japan, emerging Asia). This is the channel that Biden cared about in 2022: U.S. gasoline prices directly affect consumer sentiment and, by extension, electoral outcomes. A \$10/bbl rise in Brent typically translates to roughly \$0.24-0.28/gallon at the U.S. pump within 2-4 weeks (with considerable variation by region and refinery margin). For central banks, persistent oil-price elevation complicates the inflation picture, potentially requiring tighter monetary policy — which flows through to bond yields and equity valuations.

#### Worked example:

Suppose OPEC+ announces a 1 mb/d production cut starting in 60 days. You want to estimate the fair-value impact on Brent.

Start with a simple supply-demand framework. Global oil demand is approximately 103 mb/d (2024 IEA estimate). Global supply is also approximately 103 mb/d (balanced market). OPEC+ compliance with the announced 1 mb/d cut is historically 70-80% in the first quarter — so the effective supply reduction is 0.7-0.8 mb/d.

The short-run supply elasticity of oil is near zero (refiners cannot easily switch fuels), and the demand price elasticity is roughly -0.05 to -0.10 in the short run (a 10% price rise reduces demand by only 0.5-1%). Using the price = (supply shock / demand elasticity) rule of thumb:

Price impact = 0.75 mb/d / (103 mb/d × 0.07) ≈ 10.4%

If Brent is at \$80/bbl before the cut announcement, the model predicts a \$8.3/bbl price recovery toward \$88/bbl. This is consistent with observed market reactions to similar cuts in 2022-2023, where initial moves of \$5-12/bbl were common. Actual final price depends on compliance, global demand trajectory, and whether U.S. shale adds supply in response.

## The 2022 October cut: the Biden snub in detail

The October 2022 cut is the case study for OPEC+ as a geopolitical instrument because the political dimension was so explicit.

**Background.** In June 2022, President Biden made a controversial trip to Saudi Arabia — fist-bumping Crown Prince MBS, a man he had previously vowed to make a "pariah" over the 2018 killing of journalist Jamal Khashoggi. The stated purpose was to secure Saudi production increases to help lower gasoline prices, which had hit \$5/gallon in the U.S. and were a major drag on Biden's approval ratings ahead of November midterm elections. The Saudis announced a modest 648,000 b/d production increase for July and August — below what Washington had requested — and made clear this was the maximum.

**The cut announcement.** On 5 October 2022, OPEC+ announced a 2 mb/d production cut beginning in November. The timing was chosen with geopolitical precision: three weeks before U.S. midterm elections, when the White House was most vulnerable to energy price pressure, and one month after Biden had signed the Inflation Reduction Act (which contained substantial subsidies for domestic clean energy — a long-term threat to oil demand that Riyadh saw as a declaration of energy-policy war).

The stated justification was "market uncertainty and the adverse impacts of rising interest rates." Brent was trading around \$88-92/bbl at the time — hardly distress levels. The International Monetary Fund calculated that Saudi Arabia needed only \$76-80/bbl to balance its budget in 2022. The cut was not economically necessary.

**The political message.** The Biden administration's response was furious. The White House called it "the result of Russia's war against Ukraine and its manipulation of energy markets." Saudi officials responded that the decision was "purely technical" and questioned why the U.S. "politicizes" OPEC+ decisions. National Security Advisor Jake Sullivan said the U.S. would "reevaluate" its Saudi relationship.

None of those recriminations changed the barrel count. The 2 mb/d cut went ahead. Brent moved from \$88 to \$97 before year-end (though other factors — China COVID lockdowns, weak European demand — limited the full impact). The political signal had been sent: Saudi Arabia would not subordinate its oil policy to American electoral cycles.

## The 2023 cuts: market shock and voluntary architecture

If October 2022 was the political declaration of independence, April 2023 was the demonstration of market power. On 2 April 2023, with almost no prior signal to the market, OPEC+ announced a coordinated voluntary production cut of 1.66 mb/d, on top of the existing 2 mb/d cut from October. The announcement came on a Sunday, when markets were closed — maximizing the gap-up on Monday open.

**Why it shocked traders.** The JMMC had met just weeks before without any signal of a cut. Saudi Energy Minister Prince Abdulaziz had explicitly told reporters he didn't like short sellers in oil markets and enjoyed seeing them "suffer." The April cut was, in effect, a deliberate squeeze: Saudi Arabia had pre-positioned for the rally (via Saudi Aramco's public statements about demand strength) before surprising the market with the production reduction.

Brent jumped roughly \$8/bbl on the Monday open — from \$79 to \$87 — before settling at \$84-85 by week-end. The bounce was real but not sustained because U.S. banking stress (Silicon Valley Bank had just failed) was dampening risk appetite and China's demand recovery was slower than expected.

**The voluntary cut architecture.** The April 2023 cut introduced a structural innovation: "voluntary" cuts by individual members rather than mandatory group quotas. This is now a permanent feature of how OPEC+ operates. Saudi Arabia (500,000 b/d), Iraq (211,000 b/d), UAE (144,000 b/d), Kuwait (128,000 b/d), and others announced individual voluntary reductions. Russia extended its 500,000 b/d cut (already declared in response to the Western price cap on Russian oil).

The voluntary architecture matters politically because:
1. It allows Saudi Arabia to act unilaterally without needing full 23-nation consensus.
2. It gives Saudi Arabia the ability to restore production quickly — voluntarily — without formally tearing up a group agreement.
3. It creates a two-tier structure: baseline OPEC+ quotas (which exist on paper) plus voluntary cuts that the market must track separately.

**July 2023 — the Saudi unilateral.** In July 2023, after a ministerial meeting that extended the April cuts through December, Saudi Arabia additionally announced that it alone would cut an extra 1 mb/d through August, later extended through December 2023. This unilateral move — outside the OPEC+ formal framework — was a signal of Saudi frustration with the oil price failing to sustain above \$90 despite the April cuts. It was also a display of autonomous Saudi power: the kingdom was willing to forego immediate revenue (\$9-10 million per day at \$90 Brent) to test whether the market would respond with a sustained rally.

Brent did rally — from \$74-75 in June to \$93-95 by September 2023 — before the October Gaza war provided an additional geopolitical premium.

#### Worked example:

Saudi Arabia's unilateral 1 mb/d cut in July 2023 — what did it cost, and was it worth it?

At the time of the announcement, Brent was trading around \$76/bbl. Saudi daily production was approximately 9.7 mb/d. The cut reduced this to 8.7 mb/d.

Revenue before cut: 9.7 mb/d × \$76/bbl × 30 days = \$22.1 billion per month
Revenue after cut (at \$76 pre-rally price): 8.7 mb/d × \$76/bbl × 30 days = \$19.8 billion/month
Immediate revenue cost of cut: \$2.3 billion/month

By September 2023, Brent was at \$93-95/bbl. Revenue at 8.7 mb/d:
8.7 × \$94 × 30 = \$24.5 billion/month

Net gain vs. pumping 9.7 mb/d at the old \$76 price: \$24.5B - \$22.1B = \$2.4 billion per month gain

The trade-off worked — but only because the price actually rallied. If the price had stayed at \$76, the unilateral cut would have cost Saudi Arabia \$2.3 billion per month with zero benefit. This is the fundamental risk in every OPEC+ cut: compliance creates the mechanism but demand (or competitor supply) determines whether the price actually moves.

## The OPEC put: defining and trading the price floor

The most important concept for any trader operating in oil markets is the "OPEC put" — the de facto price floor that Saudi Arabia maintains by cutting production whenever Brent drops below the kingdom's fiscal break-even. Understanding this mechanism precisely is what separates oil traders from novices who treat OPEC+ as a supply-management body.

![The OPEC put mechanism from price drop to cut decision to recovery](/imgs/blogs/opec-plus-as-a-political-actor-production-cuts-and-market-power-5.png)

**What the OPEC put is.** A "put" in financial markets is an option that gives you the right to sell an asset at a set price — it is a downside protection instrument. The "OPEC put" is the informal market nickname for Saudi Arabia's willingness to reduce production whenever Brent falls below a threshold that threatens the Saudi fiscal position. It is not an explicit promise — OPEC+ has never published a price target — but it is consistent and observable behavior that has occurred across multiple price drops since 2016.

The rough floor has been: \$50-55 in 2016-2019 (lower Saudi spending then), rising toward \$75-80 by 2021-2022, and effectively \$80-85 by 2023-2024 as Vision 2030 spending has scaled up. The floor moves up with Saudi spending plans — an important fact for long-term oil market positioning.

**What the OPEC put is NOT.** The OPEC put is not a guarantee. It is not a promise. It is a probabilistic statement that Saudi Arabia will likely cut if prices fall below a certain level — because the budget math demands it. But there are scenarios where the put fails:

Scenario A: Non-OPEC supply floods the market faster than OPEC+ can cut. U.S. shale production grew from 5 mb/d in 2014 to 13+ mb/d by 2024. In 2014-2016, Saudi Arabia actually increased production to defend market share rather than cut — deliberately crashing the price to drive out marginal shale producers. The OPEC put was temporarily suspended for strategic reasons.

Scenario B: Compliance collapses. If Russia, Iraq, and Kazakhstan all cheat significantly on their quotas, the effective cut is much smaller than announced, and the price floor fails to hold. This scenario is partially priced in by markets — the discount between announced cuts and expected effective cuts.

Scenario C: Demand shock overwhelms supply management. COVID-19's April 2020 demand collapse (-30 mb/d in a single month) was so large that even the 9.7 mb/d COVID emergency cut could not prevent Brent falling to \$26/bbl. The OPEC put has a demand-shock override.

**Trading the OPEC put.** For a practitioner, the OPEC put creates a relatively well-defined entry opportunity when Brent approaches the \$78-83/bbl zone (the current approximate floor), with the following structure:

- Long Brent crude futures (or call options) when Brent falls into the \$78-83 zone
- Stop-loss below \$72 (signals the put is either failing or not being exercised)
- Take-profit at \$88-93 (the midpoint of Saudi comfort zone above break-even)
- Watch for: Saudi statements about "market management" as confirming signal
- Watch against: U.S. shale rig count increases, weak China PMI data

The asymmetry that makes this attractive: Brent falling from \$82 to \$72 (-12%) requires a demand shock or OPEC+ coordination failure. Brent rising from \$82 to \$93 (+13%) requires only Saudi Arabia following its own stated self-interest. The upside scenario is far more likely in any given 3-month window near the floor level.

## The quota allocation and cheating problem

Every OPEC+ meeting produces a table of quotas — how many barrels per day each member is supposed to produce. These numbers are widely covered in financial media. They are also frequently fictitious.

**How quotas are set.** Quotas are allocated based on "reference production" levels — historical production baselines that each country is supposed to cut from. These baselines are politically negotiated, not independently verified. Iraq insisted on using 2016 production figures as its baseline in 2022 — a year when its infrastructure had been partially rebuilt after ISIS disruption, inflating its apparent quota room. The UAE successfully argued in 2021 for a higher baseline of 3.65 mb/d (vs. the existing 3.17 mb/d) to reflect new Adnoc capacity — giving it more room to produce before being considered in violation.

In other words, countries negotiate their baselines upward during "quiet" periods, which then gives them more production room when cuts are later announced. The practical effect is that official quotas systematically overstate actual production restrictions.

**Compliance rates by country.** The IEA publishes monthly OPEC+ production estimates that can be compared to official quotas. The pattern over 2021-2024:

- Saudi Arabia: Typically 100-110% compliance (often over-delivers its cuts)
- UAE: 84-92% compliance (generally reliable)
- Russia: 79-94% compliance (increasingly unreliable post-invasion, exports difficult to track)
- Iraq: 65-72% compliance (the worst chronic cheater in the group)
- Kazakhstan: Below 70% compliance (Kashagan field technical issues repeatedly cited)
- Others: Variable, mostly below 85%

The weighted average compliance across OPEC+ is typically 75-85% — meaning announced cuts of, say, 2 mb/d deliver roughly 1.5-1.7 mb/d in actual supply reduction. This discount between announced and effective cuts is why markets often respond more modestly than the headline cut size would suggest.

![OPEC+ quota compliance rates by country group 2021-2024](/imgs/blogs/opec-plus-as-a-political-actor-production-cuts-and-market-power-8.png)

**The compensation mechanism.** OPEC+ has a formal procedure requiring members who exceed their quota in one month to compensate with additional cuts in subsequent months. Iraq and Kazakhstan have signed "compensation plans" multiple times. These plans are honored approximately 40-50% of the time — which is why "compensation" is often described by analysts as "the punishment that isn't."

#### Worked example:

Suppose OPEC+ announces a 1.5 mb/d cut. Iraq's share is 211,000 b/d (roughly 14% of the total). Iraq's historical compliance rate over 2022-2024 has been about 70%. What is the expected effective contribution from Iraq?

Expected Iraq contribution = 211,000 b/d × 0.70 = 147,700 b/d

Compare this to Iraq's paper commitment of 211,000 b/d. The gap (63,300 b/d) represents barrels Iraq will produce above its quota that offset Saudi Arabia's over-delivery. If Saudi Arabia delivers 110% of its 500,000 b/d quota (= 550,000 b/d), and Iraq delivers only 70% of its 211,000 b/d quota (= 147,700 b/d), the net Saudi over-delivery is partially offset:

Net effective cut from Saudi + Iraq = 550,000 + 147,700 = 697,700 b/d (paper commitment: 711,000 b/d)

In a 1.5 mb/d announced cut, if overall compliance is 80%, the effective market supply reduction is 1.2 mb/d. At global demand of 103 mb/d and a price elasticity of roughly -0.07 short-term, that drives a price increase of approximately:

\$80 × (1.2/103) / 0.07 ≈ \$13.3/bbl

But this is a theoretical maximum — demand-side weakness, U.S. shale response, and non-OPEC supply growth all attenuate the actual price impact. In practice, observed market moves around 1.5 mb/d cuts have been \$4-10/bbl, with significant variance.

## OPEC+ vs. U.S. energy policy: the structural conflict

The October 2022 cut episode revealed a structural conflict that will define energy geopolitics through the 2030s: the United States wants cheap oil (for domestic political reasons and to fund the energy transition), while Saudi Arabia wants expensive oil (for fiscal survival and Vision 2030).

**The U.S. strategic petroleum reserve (SPR) tool.** After the October 2022 cut, the Biden administration released 180 million barrels from the Strategic Petroleum Reserve over six months — the largest drawdown in SPR history. This successfully capped the oil price rally at around \$97/bbl in October-November 2022. But the SPR drawdown is a one-time weapon: the reserve fell from 641 mb to 372 mb — its lowest level since 1984. Replenishing it requires buying oil at high prices, which partially validates the OPEC+ cut strategy.

**U.S. shale as the structural counterweight.** The more durable U.S. answer to OPEC+ power is domestic production. U.S. crude output went from 5 mb/d in 2014 to 13.2 mb/d by late 2023 — making the U.S. the world's largest oil producer, ahead of Saudi Arabia and Russia. At Brent prices above \$70, U.S. shale economics support new drilling; above \$80, the rig count increases visibly. This creates a natural cap on OPEC+ pricing power: if Saudi Arabia pushes Brent above \$90-95 for an extended period, American shale production eventually floods the market.

This is why the OPEC put has a natural ceiling as well as a floor: the ceiling is roughly the price at which U.S. shale becomes highly attractive to invest in (~\$85-90), triggering a supply response that undermines OPEC+ pricing power within 12-18 months.

**The geopolitical dimension of LNG.** The U.S.-OPEC conflict over oil prices is now layered with a geopolitical dimension over LNG (liquefied natural gas). U.S. LNG exports to Europe — which surged after the Russia-Ukraine war reduced Russian pipeline gas — give Washington economic leverage over European energy security. OPEC+ nations (especially Qatar) are also major LNG exporters. The competition between U.S. LNG, Qatari LNG, and Russian LNG for European and Asian markets adds a second energy dimension to the Saudi-American-Russian strategic triangle.

## OPEC+ and the 2024 framework: gradual restoration

By mid-2024, OPEC+ had accumulated roughly 3.66 mb/d of production cuts vs. its 2022 baseline. The group was losing market share to U.S. shale, Brazil, and Guyana — all of which had expanded production significantly. The internal tensions were building: Iraq wanted to produce more; Kazakhstan pointed to technical reasons for over-production; Russia's actual export flows were murky under the Western price cap.

At the June 2024 ministerial meeting, OPEC+ announced a new framework:

- The mandatory 2 mb/d group cut (from October 2022) would remain through end-2025
- The additional voluntary cuts (April 2023 and Saudi unilateral) would be gradually phased out starting October 2024, in monthly increments of roughly 180,000 b/d
- The phaseout could be "paused or reversed" if market conditions deteriorated

This framework is significant because it signals a shift in Saudi strategy: from pure price maximization to market-share defense. With U.S. production at record highs and demand growth slowing (electric vehicle penetration accelerating, China's economy weaker than expected), holding permanently high prices was becoming less tenable than taking a modest price sacrifice to maintain market share and relevance.

## Common misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "OPEC+ sets the oil price."**

No. OPEC+ influences the supply side of the oil market, which is roughly 55% of global production. The other 45% — U.S. shale, Brazil, Guyana, Canada — responds to price signals and does not coordinate with OPEC+. Demand is entirely outside OPEC+ control. A global recession, as in 2008-2009, crashed Brent from \$147 to \$32 despite OPEC production cuts. COVID-19 crashed it to \$26 despite the largest coordinated cut in history. OPEC+ shapes the supply curve — it does not set the clearing price.

**Misconception 2: "Russia and Saudi Arabia have aligned interests."**

They have one aligned interest: oil prices above their respective fiscal break-evens. Beyond that, their interests diverge significantly. Russia wants to sell as many barrels as possible (since its revenue is volume × price, and its sanctions-constrained discount means it needs volume to compensate for lower realized prices). Saudi Arabia is comfortable with fewer barrels at a higher price — the kingdom has the spare capacity to restrain production without long-term infrastructure damage. Russia is also competing with Saudi Arabia for market share in India and China — the two main buyers of discounted Russian crude. They are allied at the OPEC+ ministerial level and competitors at the tanker level.

**Misconception 3: "Announced cuts equal actual supply reductions."**

The historical compliance rate across OPEC+ has averaged 75-85%. A 2 mb/d announced cut effectively delivers 1.5-1.7 mb/d in practice. Traders who buy the full headline number are systematically overpaying for the market impact. The correct procedure is to apply a 75-80% compliance haircut to any announced cut and use that as the effective supply change input into price models.

**Misconception 4: "The OPEC put is a floor with a fixed price."**

The floor moves. Saudi Arabia's fiscal break-even has risen from roughly \$55 in 2014 to \$78-85 in 2024, driven by Vision 2030 spending commitments. The OPEC put floor is a function of current-year Saudi budget requirements — it is not a fixed price level. Traders who assume the put is still at \$60 because it was at \$60 in 2016 will be surprised to find cuts triggered at much higher prices.

**Misconception 5: "OPEC+ is unified."**

It is not. The group has structural fault lines: Saudi Arabia vs. the UAE on baseline allocation; Saudi Arabia vs. Russia on compliance; Saudi Arabia vs. Iraq on chronic cheating; OPEC core vs. non-OPEC affiliates on the legitimacy of Russia's dominant role. These fault lines have not broken the alliance apart because the shared interest in a price floor is strong enough to override them — but they create real risk of coordination failure, particularly if oil demand peaks sooner than expected and the fight over market share intensifies.

## How it shows up in real markets

**The October 2022 trade.** In the two weeks before the October 5 cut announcement, Saudi energy minister Prince Abdulaziz bin Salman gave a series of signals that the market initially missed: he said oil markets were "schizophrenic," complained about paper traders driving prices, and referenced the "deteriorating economic environment" — all code for "we are about to cut." Brent was at \$88-90. A trader watching these signals (rather than the JMMC calendar) could have entered a long Brent position at \$88 two weeks before the announcement. Brent touched \$97-98 within 10 days of the announcement — a \$9-10/bbl gain or approximately 10-11%.

**The April 2023 surprise.** This one was harder to position for because the signal was the absence of a signal — no JMMC communication, no ministerial statements, a deliberately opaque process. Post-mortems revealed that Saudi officials had been warning oil-market analysts in private conversations about the "excessive" short positioning in the futures market. The lesson: OPEC+ cut signals often come through back-channels first (analyst briefings, off-the-record ministerial comments) before appearing in public statements.

**The 2024 phaseout.** By the second half of 2024, the market had largely priced in the gradual restoration of OPEC+ production, with Brent trading in the \$72-84 range — below the Saudi fiscal break-even. This created a market where Saudi Arabia faced a dilemma: accelerate the phaseout and lose the price floor, or slow the phaseout and lose market share to U.S. shale. The resolution — a slow phaseout with explicit "pause clauses" — kept Brent in the \$72-78 range through end-2024, below the comfort zone but tolerable given the ruble-denominated nature of Russian fiscal math.

#### Worked example:

Pricing an OPEC+ geopolitical risk premium in Brent options.

Suppose Brent is at \$82/bbl. You believe there is a 40% probability of a 1 mb/d cut within 90 days (based on Saudi fiscal needs and Riyadh-Moscow diplomatic signals), and a 60% probability of no cut (demand weakness and compliance concerns dominate). A cut would push Brent to \$90-92; no cut would see Brent drift to \$76-78.

Expected value of Brent in 90 days:
- Cut scenario: 40% × \$91 = \$36.40
- No-cut scenario: 60% × \$77 = \$46.20
- Expected Brent: \$82.60

At current spot of \$82, the market is pricing essentially no geopolitical cut risk. A trader who assigns 40% probability to a cut would buy a 90-day call option struck at \$85 (currently out-of-the-money) and sell a put struck at \$78 (to fund the call partially). In a cut scenario, the \$85 call gains \$6 (intrinsic: \$91-\$85). In a no-cut scenario, the put costs \$1 (intrinsic: \$78-\$77 = \$1, and the call expires worthless).

Expected profit: (0.40 × \$6) - (0.60 × \$1) = \$2.40 - \$0.60 = \$1.80 per barrel net

If the options are priced with implied volatility that assigns only 25% probability to the cut scenario (typical when OPEC+ signals are ambiguous), the \$85 call will be underpriced relative to your 40% probability estimate — making it a positive-expected-value trade.

## How to trade it: the playbook

**Signal 1 — Saudi fiscal stress indicator.** Track Saudi Arabia's monthly budget data, published by the Ministry of Finance. When revenue falls and deficit expands, a cut is more likely. Saudi SAMA (central bank) reserves also matter: when reserves fall below \$400 billion, the pressure to defend oil prices via cuts intensifies.

**Signal 2 — Riyadh-Moscow diplomatic calendar.** OPEC+ cuts are preceded by Saudi-Russia bilateral meetings. Watch for:
- MBS or Prince Abdulaziz calls with Novak or Putin
- Joint statements from Saudi-Russian energy ministers
- Saudi attendance at Russian diplomatic forums (SPIEF, Valdai Club)

These events are public — they appear in official Saudi and Russian state media — but they are rarely synthesized by financial news services as cut predictors. A dedicated tracking system for these bilateral meetings has historically provided 3-6 week advance warning of OPEC+ decisions.

**Signal 3 — Saudi energy minister rhetoric.** Prince Abdulaziz bin Salman has a documented pattern of telegraphing cuts through rhetorical signals: attacking short sellers, warning of "schizophrenic" markets, referencing "deteriorating macro conditions." These statements precede formal cut decisions. Compile a lexicon of his language from past cut cycles and monitor his public speeches systematically.

**Signal 4 — U.S. shale rig count divergence.** Baker Hughes publishes weekly U.S. oil rig counts. A falling rig count while OPEC+ holds cuts signals tightening supply. A rising rig count while OPEC+ struggles with compliance signals that the "OPEC put" may fail as shale fills the gap. The rig count divergence from OPEC+ cut size is the key indicator for whether the price floor holds.

**Signal 5 — OECD crude inventory levels.** OECD commercial crude inventory data (published weekly by the IEA) reflects whether OPEC+ supply reductions are actually flowing through to tighter physical markets. Inventory draws 30-60 days after a cut announcement confirm the supply reduction is real. Inventory builds despite announced cuts signal cheating is outweighing compliance.

**The core trade structure.** Near-floor entries (\$78-83 Brent) when Saudi signals are present:
- Position: Long Brent front-month or 3-month futures; alternatively long Brent call spreads (e.g., \$85/\$95 strike spread)
- Invalidation: Close below \$74 (suggests demand shock has overwhelmed the put, or Saudi strategy has shifted toward market-share defense)
- Profit target: \$88-95 (fiscal comfort zone above Saudi break-even)
- Time horizon: 4-12 weeks (cut decisions take 4-8 weeks to materialize in physical inventories)
- Hedge: Long VIX or long USD/oil-EM crosses to hedge demand shock risk
