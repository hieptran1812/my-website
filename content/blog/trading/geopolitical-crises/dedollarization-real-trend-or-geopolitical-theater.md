> [!important]
> **TL;DR.** De-dollarization is real but slow, and almost nothing like the headlines. The dollar's share of allocated global reserves has fallen from about 71% in 2000 to roughly 58% in 2025 — a genuine, two-decade drift. But the money leaving the dollar has gone mostly into gold and a scattering of smaller currencies, not into a rival. The Chinese yuan, the only currency with the economic weight to challenge the dollar, is stuck near 2% of reserves and has actually slipped since 2021. The reason is structural, not political: a reserve currency needs deep liquid markets, an open capital account, and trusted rule of law, and China chooses to keep its capital account half-closed. BRICS has built useful bilateral plumbing, but there is no shared currency, no shared central bank, and no agreed unit of account. The dollar still sits on one side of about 88% of all foreign-exchange trades. So the right frame is not collapse and not theater. It is slow erosion of a near-monopoly into a still-dominant majority, with the pace set mostly by how recklessly the United States wields financial sanctions and how far China is willing to reform. Trade the drift, not the headline.

There is a particular kind of financial headline that returns every eighteen months or so, dressed in fresh costume, making the same promise it has failed to keep for fifty years: the dollar is about to fall. Sometimes the trigger is a BRICS summit. Sometimes it is a Saudi oil deal rumored to be priced in yuan. Sometimes it is a US sanctions package, or a debt-ceiling standoff, or a new central-bank digital currency. The specifics change. The shape of the claim does not. A rival is rising, the petrodollar is cracking, and the era of dollar dominance is finally ending.

And yet here we are. The dollar buys your oil, denominates your mortgage if you live almost anywhere, sits in roughly three-fifths of the world's official reserves, and appears on one side of nearly nine out of ten currency trades. If de-dollarization is a revolution, it is the slowest revolution in financial history.

This post is an attempt to take the question seriously without either of the two lazy answers. The first lazy answer is the perma-bear's: the dollar is doomed, the debt is unsustainable, the BRICS are coming, sell everything. The second lazy answer is the complacent insider's: nothing ever changes, the dollar is forever, go back to sleep. Both are wrong, and they are wrong in instructive ways. The truth is a measured, undramatic middle: a real downtrend in the dollar's *share*, driven by genuine forces, that nonetheless runs into a structural wall every time it tries to accelerate. Understanding that wall — what it is made of, what could breach it, and what could not — is the whole game.

![Line chart of the US dollar, euro, and Chinese yuan share of allocated global FX reserves from 2000 to 2025, showing the dollar drifting from 71 percent to 58 percent while the yuan stays near 2 percent](/imgs/blogs/dedollarization-real-trend-or-geopolitical-theater-1.png)

Look at that chart before you read another word, because it contains most of the argument. The dollar line slopes down — that part is real, and anyone who tells you it is a myth is not looking. But notice two things. First, the decline is gentle: roughly thirteen percentage points over a quarter century, which is a fraction of a point a year. Second, and more important, notice where the money did *not* go. The euro line is flat to slightly down. The yuan line, despite a decade of Chinese ambition and a place in the IMF's reserve basket since 2016, is a barely-visible scrawl along the bottom, peaking at 2.8% in 2021 and falling since. The dollar is losing share to no single challenger. It is losing share to *diversification itself* — to a long tail of Australian dollars, Canadian dollars, Korean won, and above all to gold. That distinction is not a technicality. It is the difference between a king being overthrown and a king simply ruling a slightly larger court.

## Foundations: what a reserve currency actually is

Before we can argue about whether the dollar is being dethroned, we need to be precise about what throne we are even talking about. "Dominant currency" is a phrase that gets thrown around as if it means one thing. It means at least four different things, and they decline at very different speeds.

The first role is the **reserve asset**: the currency that central banks hold in their official reserves. When the State Bank of Vietnam or the People's Bank of China earns dollars from exports, it has to park them somewhere, and overwhelmingly it parks them in US Treasury securities. This is the role that the famous "71% to 58%" number measures, via the IMF's COFER (Currency Composition of Official Foreign Exchange Reserves) database.

The second role is the **invoicing and settlement currency**: the currency in which international trade is priced and paid. Oil is the canonical example, but it extends to most commodities, much of global manufacturing trade, and nearly all of cross-border finance. A Korean firm buying Brazilian iron ore very likely pays in dollars, even though neither party is American.

The third role is the **funding and borrowing currency**: the currency in which governments, companies, and banks outside the US borrow. There are trillions of dollars of dollar-denominated debt owed by non-American entities. When that debt comes due, the borrowers need dollars, which creates a permanent structural demand for the currency that has nothing to do with America's own economy.

The fourth role is the **vehicle currency** in foreign exchange: the currency you route through to swap two other currencies. If you want to trade Thai baht for Chilean pesos, there is no liquid baht-peso market, so you sell baht for dollars and buy pesos with dollars. The dollar is the hub of the wheel, and that role is measured by the BIS, which finds the dollar on one side of roughly 88% of all FX trades.

> [!note]
> These four roles reinforce one another, which is why the dollar's dominance is so sticky. Oil is priced in dollars, so you need dollars to buy oil, so you hold dollar reserves, so dollar markets stay deep, so the dollar is the cheapest currency to trade, so it is the natural invoicing currency for the next contract. Each role props up the others. To dethrone the dollar you cannot pick off one role; you have to break the whole reinforcing loop at once, and nobody has figured out how to do that.

A real reserve currency, then, is not just the money of a big economy. The eurozone is comparably large to the US, and China is on track to overtake both, yet neither the euro nor the yuan dominates. Economic size is necessary but nowhere near sufficient. What you actually need is a specific bundle of properties.

![Matrix scoring the US dollar, euro, Chinese yuan, and gold against five reserve-currency requirements, showing the dollar passing all five and the yuan failing liquidity, capital openness, and rule of law](/imgs/blogs/dedollarization-real-trend-or-geopolitical-theater-2.png)

You need **deep, liquid markets**, so that a central bank can park a hundred billion dollars and pull it out next week without moving the price. The US Treasury market is the deepest financial market on Earth, with tens of trillions outstanding and enormous daily turnover. You need an **open capital account**, so that money can flow in and out freely — a reserve currency you cannot freely move is not a reserve, it is a hostage. You need **rule of law and predictable institutions**, so that holders trust their claims will be honored, contracts enforced, and property not arbitrarily seized. You need a **large share of trade invoicing**, so there is a real-economy reason to hold the currency beyond pure speculation. And it helps, enormously, to have a **security umbrella** — the simple fact that countries under the American defense umbrella tend to hold American money.

Run the dollar through that list and it passes every test. Run the euro through it and it passes most, failing mainly on the fragmentation of European debt markets (there is no single "euro Treasury" the way there is a US Treasury) and on a weaker security guarantee. Run the yuan through it and it fails the two that matter most: China's capital account is controlled, not open, and its institutions are opaque to foreign holders who have watched the state intervene in markets, in companies, and in capital flows at will. Gold, interestingly, passes the liquidity and neutrality tests beautifully — which is exactly why central banks have been buying it — but fails the invoicing test completely, because you cannot pay for a cargo of crude with a bar of bullion.

That matrix is the foundation for everything that follows. Hold it in your head: the question of de-dollarization is really the question of whether any rival can fill in its missing cells, and at what cost to the country that would have to fill them.

It is worth dwelling on *why* the cells are so hard to fill, because the difficulty is not accidental. Each property in that matrix is expensive to provide, and the price is paid by the issuing country, not by the holders. Take the open capital account. To let foreigners hold your currency as a reserve, you must let them move money in and out at will — which means surrendering control over your exchange rate and your domestic interest rates whenever global capital flows shift. When money floods in, your currency surges and your exporters suffer; when it floods out, your currency collapses and your banks wobble. The United States accepts this volatility as the price of issuing the world's reserve currency, and it has the deep, diversified financial system to absorb it. China has spent thirty years building an export machine that depends on a managed, stable, competitive exchange rate. Opening the capital account would hand control of that exchange rate to foreign investors. From Beijing's point of view, that is not a feature; it is a loss of sovereignty over the single most important price in the economy. The empty cell in the yuan's row is not there because China is incapable. It is there because filling it would cost China something it values more than reserve status.

The rule-of-law cell is even harder, because it cannot be legislated quickly. Trust in a currency's legal foundation is built over decades of honoring contracts, enforcing property rights, and *not* arbitrarily seizing assets — and it can be destroyed in a single high-profile expropriation. The reason foreign reserve managers trust US Treasuries is not a statute; it is two centuries of the United States never having defaulted on or confiscated foreign sovereign holdings (with the politically charged exception of sanctioned adversaries, which we will return to). That kind of trust is a stock, accumulated slowly, and no amount of policy announcement can manufacture it. A country that wants to fill the rule-of-law cell has to *earn* it across a generation of consistent behavior, and any country that has recently intervened heavily in its own markets — freezing prices, halting trading, detaining executives, redirecting capital by decree — has already signaled to foreign holders that their claims are subject to political override. That signal is precisely what an opaque institutional environment communicates, and it is very hard to un-send.

## The numbers, honestly: how much has actually changed

Let me give you the real figures, because the debate is poisoned by people who quote whichever number flatters their thesis.

The dollar's share of *allocated* global reserves — the reserves that central banks report the composition of to the IMF — was about 71% in 2000. It is about 58% in 2025. That is the headline decline, and it is genuine. But three caveats turn it from a scary number into a manageable one.

First, the decline is not steady; it is lumpy and partly mechanical. A big chunk of the apparent fall comes from **valuation effects** rather than active selling. When the dollar weakens against other currencies, the dollar value of a central bank's non-dollar holdings rises, which mechanically shrinks the dollar's *share* even if nobody sold a single Treasury. Studies by the IMF and the Fed have repeatedly found that a substantial portion of the share decline is currency-valuation arithmetic, not flight.

Second, the destination of the diversification is not a rival. Of the roughly thirteen points the dollar lost, the euro captured essentially none on net, the yuan captured barely a point, and the rest scattered into a basket of smaller "nontraditional" reserve currencies — the Australian and Canadian dollars, the Korean won, the Swedish krona — plus a meaningful surge into gold. This is diversification away from concentration, not defection to an alternative bloc.

Third, the yuan's trajectory is the opposite of the triumphant narrative. It climbed from near zero in 2016 to a peak of about 2.8% in 2021, and has *fallen* since, to roughly 2.1% in 2025. The single most-cited candidate to replace the dollar has been losing reserve share for four straight years, during the very period when geopolitical tension with the US was at its highest. If sanctions and rivalry were going to drive a yuan reserve boom, that boom was due — and it did not arrive.

#### Worked example: sizing the "decline" against a portfolio

Suppose a central bank manages \$300 billion in reserves and, in 2000, held the global-average 71% in dollars — that is \$213 billion in dollar assets. By 2025, holding the global-average 58%, it would hold \$174 billion in dollars. On paper that looks like a \$39 billion exit from the dollar.

But watch what valuation does. Say over the period the dollar depreciated 20% against the bank's basket of other reserve currencies. The bank's *non-dollar* holdings — \$87 billion in 2000 terms — would gain roughly 20% in dollar-measured value simply from the exchange-rate move, adding about \$17 billion to the non-dollar side without a single transaction. Mechanically, the dollar's *share* drops even though the manager never sold a Treasury. Of the apparent \$39 billion "decline," a large slice is this arithmetic. The genuinely *active* reallocation — the part that represents a decision to hold less dollar — is materially smaller than the headline share change implies. When you read "the dollar fell 13 points," mentally discount it: a real but quieter active shift sits underneath.

There is a fourth caveat that almost nobody mentions, and it is the most damning of all for the alarmist case. The COFER data covers only *allocated* reserves — the portion central banks voluntarily report the composition of. A large slice of global reserves is *unallocated*, and the unallocated share has shrunk over time precisely because more countries began reporting in detail, including China, which historically disclosed little. When a previously secretive holder starts reporting, its currency mix gets folded into the allocated totals, and that reclassification alone can shift the published shares without any actual buying or selling. Some of the apparent decline in the dollar's allocated share, in other words, is a *measurement* artifact — the data getting more complete — rather than a behavioral one. This does not erase the trend, but it is one more reason to treat the headline thirteen-point figure as an upper bound on the real shift, not a literal account of capital fleeing the dollar.

Put the caveats together and a striking picture emerges. Of the thirteen-point headline decline, a meaningful chunk is valuation arithmetic, another chunk is measurement and reclassification, and the genuinely active reallocation that remains went overwhelmingly into gold and a long tail of small currencies rather than any rival. The "active reallocation into a competitor" — the thing the dollar-collapse thesis actually requires — is close to a rounding error. The yuan, the only candidate with the economic mass to matter, gained about a single percentage point over the entire period and has been giving it back since 2021. If this is a war for monetary supremacy, the challenger has captured roughly one percent of the territory in twenty-five years, and is currently retreating.

So the honest summary of the data is this: there is a real, slow, multi-decade diversification away from extreme dollar concentration, accelerated modestly by valuation effects and flattered somewhat by improved measurement, with no rival currency capturing the outflow. That is a very different animal from "the dollar is collapsing."

## The liquidity trap: why alternatives cannot break out

Here is the deepest reason de-dollarization keeps stalling, and it is worth slowing down for, because it is the single most underappreciated fact in the entire debate. The dollar is protected not mainly by American policy or military power — though those help — but by a self-reinforcing trap that makes switching individually irrational even when it might be collectively desirable.

![Pipeline diagram of the liquidity trap, showing how deep dollar markets lead traders to default to the dollar, which deepens liquidity further, removes any first-mover incentive, and keeps alternative currencies thin and costly](/imgs/blogs/dedollarization-real-trend-or-geopolitical-theater-3.png)

The logic runs in a loop. Dollar markets are the deepest and cheapest in the world to trade, with the tightest bid-ask spreads and the most counterparties. Because they are the cheapest, every trader and corporate treasurer who does not have a specific reason to use another currency defaults to the dollar. That default behavior pours volume into dollar markets, which makes them even deeper and cheaper. Now consider a single country — say, a mid-sized exporter — deciding whether to switch its trade invoicing to the yuan. To switch, it must accept worse liquidity, wider spreads, capital-flow restrictions on the yuan it earns, and the risk that its trading partners do not want yuan either. It bears all the costs of being a pioneer and captures almost none of the benefits, because one country switching does nothing to deepen the alternative market. So no single actor gains by switching first, the alternative currencies stay thin and costly, and the loop closes.

This is a classic coordination problem, structurally identical to why everyone keeps using the same dominant operating system or the same social network even when alternatives exist. The incumbent's advantage is *liquidity*, and liquidity is the one quality that an alternative cannot bootstrap unilaterally. You cannot decree a deep market into existence. It has to be built trade by trade, and every individual trade faces the wall above.

#### Worked example: the cost of going first

Imagine a Vietnamese electronics exporter that ships \$500 million a year and is offered the chance to invoice a Chinese buyer in yuan instead of dollars. Sounds politically tidy. Now price it.

To hedge a year of dollar receivables, the exporter pays a tight forward spread — call it 5 basis points, or \$250,000 on \$500 million, in the deep dollar market. To hedge yuan receivables, especially the offshore yuan that flows through capital controls, the forward market is thinner; assume a 40-basis-point spread, or \$2 million. That is \$1.75 million of extra annual hedging cost, every year, for the privilege of going first. On top of that, the yuan the firm earns cannot be freely repatriated or redeployed the way dollars can, so it carries an implicit liquidity penalty — money you cannot move at will is worth less than money you can. Against all that cost, the benefit to the firm of advancing a multipolar currency order is exactly zero; it captures none of it. A rational treasurer declines. Multiply that decision across thousands of firms and you have the trap, quantified: the system stays on the dollar not because anyone loves it, but because defecting costs real money and buys nothing.

The trap also explains why top-down political pushes keep failing. Governments can sign agreements, launch payment systems, and hold summits, but they cannot order ten thousand private treasurers to each eat a seven-figure cost for a collective benefit they will never personally see. Until an alternative is *already* liquid, switching is irrational; but it cannot become liquid until people switch. That is the wall, and it is made of math, not politics.

## The petrodollar mythology

No de-dollarization discussion survives contact with reality unless it disarms the petrodollar myth, because that myth is the load-bearing wall of nearly every dollar-collapse thesis.

The story goes like this: in the early 1970s the United States struck a secret deal with Saudi Arabia — oil would be priced exclusively in dollars in exchange for American military protection, and this "petrodollar system" is the true, hidden foundation of dollar dominance. Therefore, the story continues, the day Saudi Arabia sells oil in yuan, the dollar's foundation cracks and the whole edifice comes down.

Almost every part of that story is wrong, and the errors matter.

First, the historical deal is far thinner than the legend. There were US-Saudi arrangements in the 1970s involving arms, security, and the recycling of oil revenues into US Treasuries. But there was never a binding, exclusive "all oil must be priced in dollars forever" treaty enforced on the world. Oil ended up priced in dollars for the same boring reason most things ended up priced in dollars: the dollar was already the deepest, most liquid, most widely held currency, so it was the convenient unit. The causation runs *from* dollar dominance *to* dollar oil pricing, not the other way around.

Second, and this is the crucial analytical point, **the currency oil is priced in barely matters.** Pricing is a unit of account; settlement is what actually moves money. Even if Saudi Arabia priced a cargo in yuan tomorrow, the recipient could still settle in dollars, and even if it settled in yuan, the Saudis would face the question of what to do with the yuan — a currency they cannot freely invest at scale because of China's capital controls. They would very likely convert much of it straight back into dollars to buy Treasuries, which is what they do now. Changing the invoice currency does not change where the surplus capital ultimately wants to live, and surplus capital wants to live in deep, open, liquid markets. That is the dollar.

> [!warning]
> The most common analytical error in the entire de-dollarization debate is treating the oil invoice currency as the keystone. It is not the keystone. The keystone is where the world's surplus savings can be safely and liquidly stored, and that is the US Treasury market. As long as that market is the deepest and most open on Earth, oil sellers will keep recycling their proceeds into it regardless of what currency appears on the invoice. Watching the invoice is watching the wrong variable.

Third, the actual Saudi-China oil-in-yuan reality is a pilot, not a revolution. There have been genuine experiments — some Chinese oil purchases settled in yuan, discussions of yuan-denominated contracts on the Shanghai exchange, and gold-convertibility features bolted on to make yuan oil receipts more palatable. These are real and worth tracking. But they remain a sliver of Saudi exports, and they exist alongside, not instead of, the dollar relationship. Saudi Arabia continues to hold enormous dollar reserves and continues to peg its own currency, the riyal, *to the dollar* — a peg it has maintained for decades and shows no sign of abandoning, because the kingdom's own monetary stability depends on it. A country whose currency is pegged to the dollar is not about to torch the dollar.

It is worth pausing on the gold-convertibility wrinkle, because it is the most clever attempt yet to route around the trap and it shows exactly why the trap is so hard to beat. The idea floated for yuan oil contracts is this: let an oil seller who receives yuan convert those yuan into gold on the Shanghai exchange, so that the seller is never stuck holding a currency it cannot freely use. On paper, this solves the "what do I do with the yuan" problem — you turn it into a neutral, freezable-proof asset. But look closer and the solution quietly concedes the whole argument. If the seller's instinct upon receiving yuan is to immediately convert it into gold, then the yuan is functioning as a *transaction rail*, not a *store of value* — a momentary medium that everyone wants to exit as fast as possible. A currency that holders rush to convert the instant they receive it is not becoming a reserve currency; it is demonstrating in real time that it cannot be one. The gold bolt-on is an admission that the yuan alone is not trusted to hold value, dressed up as innovation. And it routes demand into gold, not into the yuan — which is, once again, exactly the diversification-into-gold pattern the reserve data already shows.

#### Worked example: the yuan an oil seller cannot spend

Suppose Saudi Arabia sells China \$10 billion of crude and, in a pilot, accepts payment in yuan — roughly 72 billion yuan at a 7.2 exchange rate. Now the Saudi treasury has 72 billion yuan it must do something with. What are the options?

It could buy Chinese government bonds — but the accessible, foreign-friendly slice of that market is small and subject to capital-flow rules that could change, and the kingdom would be concentrating reserves in the bonds of a strategic rival. It could hold the yuan as cash — but cash earns little and sits exposed to Chinese policy. Or it could convert the yuan back to dollars and buy US Treasuries — deep, liquid, and the asset its own pegged currency is built on. Run the numbers and the third option dominates for all but a small diversification slice. So even a "successful" \$10 billion yuan oil deal results in perhaps \$8–9 billion cycling straight back into dollars. The invoice said yuan; the savings said dollar. That gap — between the currency of the transaction and the currency of the surplus — is why petro-yuan headlines keep failing to move the reserve data.

## The BRICS payment dream versus the BRICS payment ledger

If the petrodollar is the myth that animates the dollar-collapse thesis, BRICS is the institution that is supposed to deliver it. So let us separate the pitch from the ledger.

![Before-and-after diagram contrasting the BRICS payment hype — a new reserve currency, petro-yuan, and an mBridge CBDC replacing the dollar — against the reality of bilateral local-currency deals, a 2 percent yuan reserve share, and the dollar still clearing 88 percent of FX trades](/imgs/blogs/dedollarization-real-trend-or-geopolitical-theater-4.png)

The pitch, recited at every summit and amplified by every perma-bear newsletter, is grand: a new BRICS reserve currency to rival the dollar; oil and commodities priced in a "petro-yuan" or a basket; a central-bank-digital-currency rail (the mBridge project among them) that lets members settle directly, bypassing the dollar and the SWIFT messaging network entirely; and the imminent end of dollar hegemony as the Global South votes with its wallet.

The ledger is more modest. What BRICS has actually built is **bilateral local-currency settlement** — India paying for some Russian oil in rupees and dirhams, China and Russia settling more bilateral trade in yuan and rubles, various pairs of countries agreeing to net out trade in their own currencies to save on dollar conversion costs. This is genuinely useful plumbing, and it does shave dollar usage at the margin. But notice what it is *not*. It is not a shared currency — there is no BRICS dollar, no agreed unit of account, and the members could not agree on one because doing so would require China to give up monetary sovereignty to a bloc, or the bloc to accept Chinese monetary dominance, and neither India nor Russia nor Brazil will accept being a Chinese monetary vassal. It is not a shared central bank, because there is no political union to back one. And it has not moved the macro numbers: the yuan is still near 2% of reserves, and the dollar still sits on one side of roughly 88% of FX trades.

There is also a hard internal contradiction the pitch always glosses over. A reserve currency must run *deficits* with the world to supply that currency abroad — this is the famous Triffin dilemma. The United States supplies dollars to the world precisely by importing more than it exports and running persistent current-account deficits, sending dollars out that foreigners then hold. China, by contrast, is built on running *surpluses*; its entire growth model depends on exporting more than it imports. A surplus country cannot be the world's reserve supplier, because it is structurally pulling money *in*, not pushing money out. For the yuan to become a true reserve currency, China would have to flip its economic model from a surplus exporter to a deficit importer — a transformation so profound it would amount to abandoning the strategy that built modern China. That is not a policy tweak. It is a different country.

> [!note]
> Bilateral local-currency settlement and a global reserve currency are different things, and conflating them is the BRICS sleight of hand. Two countries netting out trade in their own currencies to save conversion fees is sensible bookkeeping. It does not require, create, or imply a currency that the rest of the world wants to *hold* as a store of value. You can have a thriving rupee-ruble trade channel and still have nobody on Earth wanting to keep their savings in rupees or rubles. Settlement plumbing is not a reserve asset.

#### Worked example: the netting that does not become a reserve

Two countries — call them a commodity exporter and a manufacturing importer — run \$50 billion of trade each way per year. Historically each \$50 billion leg was converted into dollars and back, costing, say, 30 basis points round-trip in spreads and fees: about \$150 million a year each, \$300 million combined. They agree to settle bilaterally in local currencies and net the flows, saving most of that \$300 million. Real money, real motive, real adoption.

But now ask the reserve question. At year end, the exporter has a \$5 billion surplus denominated in the importer's currency. Does it *hold* that currency as a reserve asset? Only if that currency is liquid, convertible, and trustworthy enough to store national savings in. If it is not — if it is a controlled or volatile currency — the exporter converts the surplus into, yes, dollars or gold. So the bilateral channel saves transaction costs without creating reserve demand. This is exactly what the data shows: rising local-currency *settlement*, flat-to-falling rival-currency *reserves*. The plumbing grows; the throne does not move.

## The vehicle-currency moat

Step back from reserves and look at the plumbing of the global FX market, because this is where the dollar's dominance is most overwhelming and least appreciated.

![Bar chart of currency shares of global foreign-exchange turnover, with the US dollar at 88 percent towering over the euro at 31, the yen at 17, sterling at 13, and the yuan at 7 percent](/imgs/blogs/dedollarization-real-trend-or-geopolitical-theater-5.png)

The dollar is on one side of about 88% of all foreign-exchange trades, according to the BIS Triennial Survey — the most authoritative count of FX activity in the world. (Shares sum to roughly 200% rather than 100%, because every trade has two currencies and is counted on both sides.) The euro, the second-place currency, is at about 31%. The yuan, despite China being the world's largest trading nation, sits around 7% — and even that figure is flattered by China's own enormous trade volume.

This 88% is the dollar's deepest moat, deeper than reserves, because it reflects the **vehicle-currency** role. When there is no liquid direct market between two currencies — and for most of the thousands of possible currency pairs, there is not — traders route through the dollar. To swap Thai baht for Chilean pesos you sell baht for dollars and buy pesos with dollars, two liquid trades instead of one illiquid one. The dollar is the universal intermediary, the connecting hub that makes the whole network efficient. And this role is even more trapped by the liquidity loop than the others, because the value of being the hub grows with the number of spokes. Every currency that uses the dollar as its bridge makes the dollar a more valuable bridge for every other currency. Displacing it would require not one alternative bridge but a whole alternative network, simultaneously, which is precisely what no coalition has been able to coordinate.

#### Worked example: the cost of routing around the dollar

A frontier-market central bank wants to pay for imports from a non-dollar trading partner without touching the dollar. Direct market between the two currencies: essentially nonexistent, so any trade moves the price violently — call it a 150-basis-point round-trip cost on a thin market. Routing through the dollar instead: two trades, each in a deep market at perhaps 5 basis points, for 10 basis points total round-trip.

On a \$1 billion import program, the direct non-dollar route costs about \$15 million in slippage; the dollar-vehicle route costs about \$1 million. The dollar detour is fifteen times cheaper *even though it involves an extra transaction*, because liquidity dwarfs transaction count. A central bank that insists on avoiding the dollar on principle is choosing to set \$14 million on fire. Most do not, and that is the vehicle-currency moat in one number: the dollar wins not by decree but because going around it is absurdly expensive.

## What sanctions actually did

Now we reach the one force that genuinely could move the needle, and ironically it is a force the United States controls and keeps reaching for: financial sanctions.

When the US and its allies froze roughly \$300 billion of Russian central-bank reserves in 2022, they did something with consequences that ran in two opposite directions at once, and you have to hold both to understand the moment.

In the short run, the freeze was a stunning demonstration of dollar *power*, not dollar weakness. It showed that holding dollar (and euro) reserves means holding assets that the issuer can switch off. In the immediate aftermath, the dollar did not fall — it *rose*, as it always does in a crisis, because frightened money runs *toward* the deepest, safest, most liquid asset, which is the US Treasury. Crisis is the dollar's home field. This is why the de-dollarization-from-sanctions thesis has such a poor short-run track record: every crisis that is supposed to weaken the dollar instead sends capital fleeing into it.

But in the long run, the freeze planted a genuine seed of doubt in the minds of every reserve manager in every country that is not a US ally. The lesson they drew was not "sell dollars now" — that would be irrational given the trap — but "diversify, slowly, at the margin, into assets nobody can freeze." And there is exactly one such asset that is liquid, neutral, and apolitical: **gold**. This is why central-bank gold buying surged after 2022, led by China, Russia, Turkey, India, and a long list of others. They were not building a yuan bloc. They were buying the one reserve asset that has no issuer to offend, no capital controls, and no off switch.

This is the most important nuance in the entire debate, so let me state it plainly. Sanctions are real, and they are the single most powerful de-dollarization force in existence — but they work *slowly and indirectly*, by nudging diversification into gold and nontraditional currencies over years, not by triggering a yuan revolution or a sudden dollar crash. The danger to the dollar is not that any one sanction backfires. It is that the *repeated, casual use* of financial sanctions as a foreign-policy tool gradually teaches the world that dollar assets carry political risk, and political risk has a price. Every sanction is a small tax on dollar trust, paid not today but over a decade.

It is worth understanding the actual machinery of dollar sanctions, because it reveals both their power and their limits. The dollar's enforcement leverage flows through two chokepoints. The first is **correspondent banking**: nearly every cross-border dollar payment ultimately clears through a US bank or a US-supervised clearing system, because dollars are, in the end, claims on the US banking system. Cut a foreign bank off from dollar clearing and it cannot move dollars at all, regardless of how many it nominally holds. The second is **SWIFT**, the messaging network that banks use to instruct one another; expelling a bank from SWIFT does not freeze its money directly but blinds it, severing the communication that makes cross-border payment possible at scale. These two chokepoints are why a sanction announced in Washington can paralyze a bank in a country that has no other quarrel with the United States. The reach is genuinely global, and it is a real demonstration of structural power.

But notice what that machinery also teaches. Every country watching understands that the chokepoints exist, that they have been used, and that they could be used against *them* if the political winds shifted. The rational response is not to dump dollars — the trap makes that costly — but to build redundancy: alternative messaging systems (China's CIPS, Russia's SPFS), bilateral channels that avoid US correspondent banks, and reserves in assets that sit outside the chokepoints entirely. This is the slow, defensive, decade-long hedging that sanctions provoke. It does not show up as a dollar crash. It shows up as a gradually thickening lattice of workarounds, each individually small, that collectively makes the next sanction slightly less effective than the last. The United States is, in effect, slowly spending down the very monopoly that gives its sanctions their bite — and the more it uses the weapon, the faster it depletes the ammunition.

There is a sharp strategic tension here that policymakers do not always seem to internalize. Financial sanctions are powerful *because* the dollar is dominant, and the dollar is dominant *because* it is trusted to be apolitical. Using the sanctions weapon spends down the trust that makes the weapon work. It is a tool that dulls itself with every use. A single decisive sanction against a genuine adversary may be well worth that cost. A habit of reaching for financial sanctions as the default response to every diplomatic dispute is a slow-motion erosion of the asset that makes the sanctions possible in the first place. This is the deep irony at the center of the de-dollarization story: the force most capable of weakening the dollar is wielded by the dollar's own issuer, in the name of its own foreign policy.

> [!warning]
> The dollar's greatest long-run vulnerability is not China, not BRICS, and not the petro-yuan. It is American policy. The more aggressively and frequently the United States weaponizes the financial system — freezing reserves, cutting banks off from dollar clearing, sanctioning entire economies — the stronger the long-run incentive for everyone outside the alliance to build alternatives and stockpile gold. The dollar will not be dethroned by a rival. If it erodes meaningfully, it will be because its issuer taught the world to want an exit. This is the one variable a dollar bear should actually watch.

## The misconceptions, named and buried

Let me collect the recurring errors in one place, because naming them is the fastest path to clear thinking.

**Misconception 1: De-dollarization is a myth.** No. The reserve share has genuinely fallen thirteen points in twenty-five years, central banks are genuinely diversifying, and gold buying is genuinely surging. People who dismiss the whole thing are ignoring real data. The trend is real; it is just slow and non-revolutionary.

**Misconception 2: The dollar is about to collapse.** Also no, and for the structural reasons above: the liquidity trap, the vehicle-currency moat, the Triffin dilemma facing any surplus-country rival, and the simple fact that crisis sends money *into* the dollar. Collapse is not on the table on any horizon a trader cares about.

**Misconception 3: The petrodollar is the keystone.** No. The invoice currency is a sideshow; the keystone is where surplus savings can be stored liquidly and safely, which is the Treasury market. Watching oil-pricing announcements is watching the wrong variable.

**Misconception 4: BRICS is building a rival reserve currency.** No. BRICS is building bilateral settlement plumbing, which saves transaction costs but does not create a currency anyone wants to *hold*. There is no shared unit, no shared central bank, and irreconcilable internal politics preventing one.

**Misconception 5: The yuan is the heir apparent.** Not on current policy. The yuan's reserve share has fallen since 2021, China keeps its capital account controlled by deliberate choice, and a surplus economy structurally cannot supply the world's reserves. The yuan could rise — but only if China makes changes it has so far refused to make.

**Misconception 6: Sanctions are accelerating a sudden dollar exit.** Half right. Sanctions are the strongest de-dollarization force, but they work slowly, through gold and diversification, not through a sudden flight. The short-run effect of any crisis is dollar *strength*.

#### Worked example: pricing the political-risk tax

Try to put a number on the "trust tax" sanctions impose. Suppose a non-aligned central bank decides, after watching reserves get frozen elsewhere, to shift 5% of a \$400 billion reserve book — \$20 billion — out of freezable dollar assets and into gold over five years. That is \$4 billion a year of marginal gold demand from one bank. The world has dozens of such banks. If even fifteen of them make a comparable shift, that is \$60 billion a year of structural gold demand and \$60 billion a year of *reduced* marginal dollar-asset demand — enough to explain both the post-2022 gold surge and a continued drift in the reserve-share line, without anyone selling existing Treasuries in a panic. The tax is real, it is measurable in the gold price, and it compounds quietly. That is what slow de-dollarization actually looks like in the data: not a crash, a drift.

## What would actually move the needle

So if the headlines are wrong and the trend is real but glacial, what would it take to turn the glacier into a river? There are exactly three paths, and each requires years of compounding change, not a summit announcement.

![Directed graph showing three paths to meaningful de-dollarization — US overuse of sanctions driving allies to build alternative rails, China opening its capital account to make the yuan convertible, and oil pricing shifting to several currencies — all converging on a meaningful fall in the dollar's share](/imgs/blogs/dedollarization-real-trend-or-geopolitical-theater-6.png)

**Path A: American self-harm.** If the United States overuses financial sanctions to the point where even its allies — not just rivals — start building sanction-proof rails, the network effects could finally tip. This is the most plausible path precisely because it is the one the US controls and keeps walking toward. It would not happen through a single act but through a steady erosion of the assumption that dollar assets are politically safe. Watch for *allies*, not adversaries, hedging their dollar exposure; that is the tell that this path is live.

**Path B: Chinese reform.** If China opened its capital account, let the yuan float freely, built deep and trustworthy bond markets open to foreigners, and accepted the current-account deficits required to supply the world with yuan, the yuan could become a genuine reserve currency over a decade or two. But each of those steps cedes control the Communist Party has shown no willingness to surrender, and the last one would require abandoning the export-surplus model that built modern China. This path is technically possible and politically improbable.

**Path C: A real shift in commodity invoicing.** If oil and other major commodities genuinely shifted to multiple-currency pricing *and* — crucially — the proceeds found deep non-dollar markets to live in, the petrodollar recycling loop would weaken. But this requires Path B to happen first, because there is nowhere for petro-yuan proceeds to live until China builds open, liquid markets. Path C is downstream of Path B.

Notice the structure: all three paths converge on the same outcome, a meaningful fall in the dollar's share, and at least two of the three depend on choices made in Washington and Beijing rather than on anything the rest of the world can force. The dollar's fate is, to a remarkable degree, in the hands of its issuer and its single plausible challenger, and both have so far chosen the status quo — the US by tolerating its deficits, China by guarding its controls.

It is worth being explicit about what would *not* move the needle, because the false signals are louder than the real ones. A BRICS summit communiqué announcing intent to study a common currency does nothing; intent has been announced for fifteen years. A bilateral deal to settle some trade in local currencies does nothing to reserve status, for the reasons the netting example above made concrete. A new central-bank digital currency pilot does nothing unless it solves the underlying liquidity and trust problems, which technology cannot solve — a CBDC is a faster pipe, not a deeper market, and the dollar's moat is depth, not speed. A single oil cargo priced in yuan does nothing, because the proceeds recycle back to dollars. Even a sizable jump in the gold share of reserves does not dethrone the dollar; it diversifies *around* the dollar without replacing its transactional roles, because you still cannot invoice a shipment or clear a payment in gold. The list of things that look like de-dollarization but are not is long, and learning to recognize them is most of the analytical work.

What *would* move it is unglamorous and slow: a sustained, multi-year pattern of allies hedging dollar exposure; a genuine Chinese decision to open the capital account and accept the loss of exchange-rate control that comes with it; a structural shift in where commodity surpluses are stored, downstream of that Chinese opening. None of these produces a headline on any given Tuesday. All of them would take a decade to register in the data. That mismatch — between the slow reality and the fast news cycle — is exactly why the debate is so consistently distorted.

![Timeline of de-dollarization milestones from 2009 to 2025, contrasting each prediction of dollar decline with an outcome that left the dollar more entrenched](/imgs/blogs/dedollarization-real-trend-or-geopolitical-theater-7.png)

The timeline above is the humbling coda to every dollar-collapse forecast. In 2009 Russia's Medvedev called for a new global reserve currency at the height of post-crisis dollar anxiety; nothing happened. In 2014 Russia's annexation of Crimea and the first sanctions were supposed to start the exodus; instead the ruble collapsed and dollar demand surged. In 2019 Facebook's Libra promised a private global currency that would route around national money; regulators killed it within two years. In 2022 the reserve freeze was the most serious blow yet, and it did accelerate the mBridge CBDC project and bilateral deals — the one milestone with genuine teeth — yet three years on the yuan's reserve share is *lower*, not higher. And in 2025 the perennial BRICS-currency debate returned, and once again produced no agreed unit. Five cycles of confident prediction, five times the dollar ended more entrenched than the forecasters expected. That track record should make anyone humble about the sixth prediction, including the bearish ones — and including this one.

## How to actually trade and think about this

Strip away the ideology and here is how a serious markets participant should hold the de-dollarization question.

First, **trade the drift, not the headline.** The structural signal worth acting on is the steady, multi-year diversification into gold and nontraditional reserve currencies. That has been a real tailwind for gold and a mild structural headwind for the dollar's *share* — though not, note, for the dollar's *exchange rate*, which is driven by interest-rate differentials and growth, not reserve composition. If you want to express the de-dollarization thesis, the cleanest expression has been long gold, not short dollar, and the data backs that up. For the deeper mechanics of how reserve flows and rate differentials drive the currency itself, the FX-focused series on this site is the place to go; this post is about the *plumbing*, not the daily exchange rate.

Second, **fade the collapse trades.** Every time a BRICS summit or a petro-yuan headline sparks a "dollar is over" narrative, the structural trap above tells you the move is almost certainly noise. The track record of dollar-collapse trades is brutal. Crisis is the dollar's home field; betting against it into a crisis has bankrupted more macro funds than almost any other trade.

Third, **watch the right variable, which is American policy.** Do not watch oil invoices or summit communiqués. Watch how aggressively the US weaponizes the financial system, and especially watch whether *allies* — not adversaries — start hedging their dollar exposure. Adversaries hedging is priced in and expected. Allies hedging would be the genuine signal that the network is fraying, and it is the one development that could move the trend from glacial to merely slow.

There is a subtle but important distinction here that trips up even experienced participants: the difference between the dollar's *share* and the dollar's *price*. De-dollarization is a story about share — about what fraction of reserves, trade, and FX flow runs through the dollar. The dollar's exchange rate, by contrast, is driven by something almost entirely separate: the relative path of US interest rates, growth, and risk appetite versus the rest of the world. You can have a decade in which the dollar's reserve share slowly falls *and* the dollar's exchange rate rises, because rate differentials favor the US even as central banks diversify at the margin. Confusing the two leads to one of the most common losing trades in macro: shorting the dollar's exchange rate on a de-dollarization thesis, then getting run over because the Federal Reserve hiked rates and capital poured in. If your thesis is about share, express it through reserve-adjacent assets like gold, not through the spot exchange rate, which marches to a different drummer.

A worked illustration of getting this wrong: a fund reads the reserve-share chart, concludes "the dollar is in structural decline," and puts on a large short against the dollar index expecting depreciation. Over the next two years the dollar's reserve share indeed drifts down another half-point — the thesis was "correct" — but US rates rise faster than peers, the dollar index *appreciates* 12%, and the fund's short bleeds for eight straight quarters before it capitulates. The thesis about share was right; the trade about price was a disaster, because the two variables were never the same thing. The lesson: name your variable before you size your position.

Fourth, **respect the time horizon.** De-dollarization is a decade-or-more theme, not a quarter-or-two trade. If your thesis requires the dollar to lose its crown within your fund's reporting period, you do not have a thesis, you have a hope. The right posture is a small, patient structural tilt — overweight gold, a watchful eye on policy — not a leveraged bet on imminent collapse.

The verdict, then, is the one the title asks for. De-dollarization is *real* — the data is unambiguous that the dollar's share has fallen and that central banks are diversifying. It is *not theater* — there are genuine structural forces, above all the slow erosion of dollar trust through sanctions, pushing it forward. But it is *not imminent collapse*, and it is *not a BRICS revolution*, because the liquidity trap, the vehicle-currency moat, the Triffin dilemma, and the simple absence of any currency that can fill the dollar's missing cells all conspire to keep the pace glacial. The dollar is a king losing a few percentage points of an overwhelming majority each decade, not a king being marched to the guillotine. Trade it that way, and you will be right far more often than the people selling you either fear or complacency. The dollar's century is not ending; it is simply, very slowly, becoming a little less absolute.
