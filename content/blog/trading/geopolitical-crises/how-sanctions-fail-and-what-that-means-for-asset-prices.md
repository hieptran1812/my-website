> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Most comprehensive sanctions regimes fail to achieve their stated policy objectives: only ~34% succeed by the Peterson Institute's count. The five failure modes — third-country bypass, commodity rerouting, domestic adaptation, political hardening, and coalition fracture — are observable in advance. When a regime visibly enters failure, the target's assets reprice upward from forced-selling lows; the trading edge is recognizing failure before the narrative catches up. Indicators: rising export volumes for sanctioned commodities, narrowing asset discounts, third-country import surge.

In March 2022, three weeks after Russian tanks crossed into Ukraine, the consensus among Western policymakers was that the most comprehensive sanctions regime ever assembled against a major economy would crush the Russian state. The ruble had collapsed from 75 to 135 against the dollar. Half of Russia's \$600 billion in central-bank reserves had been frozen overnight. Apple, McDonald's, BP, and a thousand other firms were heading for the exits. The headline in nearly every financial paper amounted to the same sentence: the Russian economy would contract by double digits and the regime would buckle.

It did not happen. By the end of 2022 the ruble had not only recovered but was trading *stronger* than before the invasion, near 60 to the dollar. Russian oil revenues for 2022 came in around \$218 billion, well *above* the \$117 billion of 2021. The economy contracted roughly 2 percent, not the 10 to 15 percent that had been forecast. Two years on, Russia was still selling roughly seven million barrels of crude and products a day, just to different buyers. The sanctions had not failed completely, but they had failed at the one thing they were designed to do, which was to break the Russian war machine's financing. And the assets that the consensus had written to zero, the ruble, the commodity exporters, the discounted Urals barrel, had quietly become some of the best risk-adjusted trades of the cycle for the small number of investors willing to look at the failure honestly.

This post is about that gap, the gap between what sanctions are supposed to do and what they actually do, and about how a disciplined investor reads the difference. The thesis is uncomfortable but it is grounded in six decades of evidence: most comprehensive sanctions regimes fail to force the policy change they target, and when they fail, the target's assets eventually reprice upward from a deeply discounted base. The trade is not "sanctions are bad" or "sanctions are good." The trade is recognizing the specific, observable signals that a sanctions regime has entered failure, and acting on the repricing before the headlines catch up.

![The five failure modes of sanctions shown as a sequential pipeline from third-country bypass through commodity rerouting, domestic adaptation, political hardening, and coalition fragmentation](/imgs/blogs/how-sanctions-fail-and-what-that-means-for-asset-prices-1.png)

Let me be precise about what I mean by "fail." A sanctions regime has an objective, usually stated, sometimes implicit. The objective might be regime change, a reversal of a specific policy (withdraw from a territory, abandon a nuclear program), or simple degradation of the target's capacity to do something. "Failure" here means the regime does not achieve its stated objective even after years of pressure. By that yardstick, the historical record is grim for the sanctioning side and, paradoxically, full of opportunity for the patient investor.

## Foundations: Why sanctions decay and what that means for prices

If you trade macro, commodities, sovereign credit, or emerging-market equity, sanctions are not a side issue. They are one of the largest sources of mispricing in the entire asset universe, precisely because the people setting prices in the early innings, the ones dumping the target's assets, are doing so for reasons that are political and emotional as much as financial. A pension fund sells Russian equity in March 2022 not because it has run a discounted-cash-flow model showing the assets are worth zero, but because its board cannot stomach holding them, its mandate forbids them, and the reputational risk of being seen to own them is intolerable. That is forced selling, and forced selling creates the deepest discounts in markets. The investor's edge is to separate the political question (should these assets be owned, ethically and reputationally) from the financial question (what are these cash flows actually worth as the sanctions decay). Those are different questions, and the market routinely conflates them.

The whole framework rests on one empirical observation that I will defend throughout: sanctions decay through predictable channels. They do not stay at full strength forever. The day a sanctions package is signed is the day of maximum impact; from there, the target adapts, third countries arbitrage, and the squeeze loosens. The exact opposite is true of the *narrative*, which intensifies after the announcement as the press piles on. So you get a structural divergence: real economic impact peaks and then decays, while perceived impact peaks later and stays elevated. The space between those two curves is the trade.

It helps to be clear at the outset about what sanctions actually are, mechanically, because the mechanism is what decays. A sanctions package is a set of legal prohibitions enforced primarily through the financial system and, secondarily, through the threat of secondary measures against third parties. The United States, in particular, does not enforce most sanctions by physically stopping ships or seizing cargo; it enforces them by threatening to cut violators off from the dollar-clearing system and from access to the US market. That is an enormously powerful lever, the dollar is the settlement currency for the majority of global trade, and exclusion from it is close to a commercial death sentence for a global bank, but it is a lever with a specific shape. It works best against entities that need the dollar and the US market. It works poorly against entities that have decided they can live without both, or that operate entirely outside the reach of US enforcement. Every failure mode we will examine is, at bottom, a way that the target or its partners reduce their dependence on the very system the sanctions weaponize. When you understand that sanctions are a tax on dependence, you understand why they decay: the target spends the years after the announcement systematically reducing its dependence, and as dependence falls, the tax falls with it.

There is a second mechanical point worth stating plainly. Sanctions are imposed by governments, but they are enforced, in the first instance, by private firms, banks, insurers, shippers, refiners, who make probabilistic cost-benefit decisions about compliance. A government can write a prohibition, but a Singapore-based trading house decides, every single day, whether the margin on a sanctioned cargo exceeds the probability-weighted cost of getting caught. That decentralized, profit-driven enforcement is the soft underbelly of every regime. As the discount on the sanctioned good widens, the margin for circumvention grows, which means the financial incentive to evade rises exactly when the sanctions are biting hardest. Sanctions thus contain the seed of their own erosion: the more they hurt, the larger the discount, and the larger the discount, the greater the reward for whoever is willing to break them. This is not a flaw that better enforcement can fully fix; it is a structural feature of using price-based pressure against a globally demanded good.

For an investor, the takeaway from the mechanics is that the right mental model is not "the target is cut off" but "the target's cost of doing business has risen by some amount, and that amount declines over time as workarounds mature." Pricing a sanctioned asset, then, is fundamentally a question about the trajectory of that cost, not its initial level. The market, in the panic phase, prices the initial level and extrapolates it forever. The disciplined investor prices the trajectory, and the trajectory almost always bends back toward normalization.

## The five failure modes

Sanctions fail through five distinct channels. They are not mutually exclusive; in a long-running regime all five operate at once, reinforcing each other. But they activate in a rough sequence, and learning to spot each one is the core skill.

### Mode 1: Third-country circumvention

The first and most reliable failure mode is that someone who is not party to the sanctions buys what the target is selling. Sanctions are imposed by a coalition, and the coalition is never the whole world. The moment the United States and Europe stop buying Russian crude, India and China step in. The moment they stop buying Iranian crude, China's independent "teapot" refiners and a fleet of intermediaries fill the gap. The target sells its goods at a discount to a willing buyer outside the coalition, and the buyer pockets the spread.

This is not a loophole; it is the structure. As long as the sanctioned good has global demand, and oil, gas, metals, and grain always do, there will be a buyer who is not in the coalition. The only question is the size of the discount the target must offer to clear the volume, and that discount narrows over time as the logistics of circumvention mature.

The economic gravity here is worth dwelling on. A barrel of oil is fungible. It does not carry a flag once it is refined into diesel or gasoline. When the West stops buying Russian crude, the West does not stop *consuming* the same number of barrels of oil; it simply buys those barrels from someone else, say Saudi Arabia, while India and China buy the Russian barrels the West vacated. The net effect on global supply is close to zero; what changes is the *routing* and the *discount*. This is the reshuffling fallacy that trips up the simplest version of sanctions thinking. Unless the coalition can shrink global *demand* for the good, or persuade every non-coalition buyer to abstain, the barrels find a home. For a globally traded commodity, persuading every non-coalition buyer to abstain is essentially impossible, because the buyers who abstain are simply handing the discount to the buyers who do not. The first defector is rewarded, and reward attracts imitation.

There is also a maturation curve to third-country circumvention that the investor should track explicitly. In the first months, the channel is clumsy: buyers are nervous, intermediaries are few, and the target must offer a steep discount to compensate for the legal and reputational risk its buyers are taking on. As the months pass, the channel professionalizes. Specialized intermediaries emerge. Payment mechanisms in local currencies get built. The legal risk gets priced and, often, found to be smaller than feared. And so the discount the target must offer shrinks, even though the formal sanctions have not changed at all. The investor who watches the discount narrow is watching the failure mode mature in real time, and the narrowing discount is a leading indicator that the target's export revenues, and therefore the value of its export-linked assets, are recovering.

#### Worked example: Iran's customer rotation

Walk through the arithmetic that India's refiners faced in 2019. Iranian crude was selling at a discount of roughly \$10 to \$15 per barrel to comparable Gulf grades because the sanctions had shrunk Iran's customer base. A coastal Indian refiner processing 400,000 barrels a day of crude, if it could substitute Iranian barrels for Saudi barrels at even a \$10 discount, captures \$4 million per day of margin, or roughly \$1.4 billion a year. The penalty for circumvention, the risk of secondary sanctions, was real but probabilistic and negotiable. Faced with \$1.4 billion of certain annual margin against a probabilistic enforcement risk, a large share of global refining capacity will quietly take the trade. That is exactly what happened: after dipping to roughly 0.5 million barrels a day in 2019 under "maximum pressure," Iran's exports climbed back to around 1.8 million barrels a day by 2023, almost entirely on the back of Chinese buyers. The discount narrowed as the channel matured, which is the failure signal compounding on itself.

### Mode 2: Commodity rerouting

The second failure mode is logistical. Even when the buyer exists, the goods have to physically move, and moving sanctioned commodities requires ships, insurance, and payment rails. The sanctioning coalition tries to choke these. The target and its buyers route around them.

The clearest manifestation is the "shadow fleet," a collection of aging tankers, often with opaque ownership, registered under flags of convenience, that carry sanctioned crude without Western insurance. By 2024, estimates put the shadow fleet serving Russia at roughly 600 tankers. Transshipment, where cargo is moved from one vessel to another at sea to obscure its origin, blends sanctioned and non-sanctioned barrels until the paperwork is clean. Payment moves through non-dollar channels, settled in yuan, rupees, or dirhams, or netted through trading houses in jurisdictions that do not enforce the sanctions.

![Comparison of what working sanctions look like versus failing sanctions across policy change, exports, credit spreads, and asset investability](/imgs/blogs/how-sanctions-fail-and-what-that-means-for-asset-prices-4.png)

The investor's signal here is concrete and trackable: the number of shadow-fleet tankers, the volume of ship-to-ship transfers at known transshipment points, the gap between the target's reported exports and the customs imports recorded by its trading partners. When the shadow fleet grows and transshipment volumes rise, the rerouting machinery is winning, and the export floor under the target's economy is firming up.

What makes rerouting such a reliable failure mode is that the infrastructure of circumvention, once built, becomes a permanent feature of the market that does not disappear when one regime ends. The shadow fleet that grew to serve Iran did not vanish; much of that same capacity, and the operational know-how behind it, was available to serve Russia when the moment came. Each sanctions regime, in effect, subsidizes the construction of a parallel logistics system that lowers the cost of the *next* regime's evasion. A tanker bought cheaply, registered in an obscure flag state, insured by a non-Western mutual or by no one at all, and operated by a company with no US exposure, is a durable asset that can carry sanctioned cargo for a decade. The sanctioning coalition, by repeatedly creating demand for this parallel system, has built a structural counterweight to its own future leverage. This is why rerouting tends to get faster and cheaper with each successive regime, and why the export-recovery lead time has compressed over the years.

The insurance angle deserves a specific note because it is where Western leverage is theoretically strongest and practically weakest. Roughly 90 percent of the world's tanker insurance historically ran through the London-centered International Group of P&I Clubs, which gave the coalition a genuine chokepoint: deny insurance, and the cargo cannot move, because no port wants an uninsured supertanker and no responsible operator sails one. But the response was predictable. The target's partners stood up their own insurance, Russian state reinsurance, opaque providers in other jurisdictions, or simply self-insurance where the buyer accepts the spill risk in exchange for the discount. The quality of this cover is dramatically worse than the London market's, which is a genuine environmental and financial risk, but it is sufficient to move the cargo. The chokepoint that looked decisive on paper turned out to be a chokepoint the target could route around within a year, at the cost of degraded safety standards that fall on the global commons rather than on the target. That externalized cost is one reason the rerouting failure mode is so corrosive: the people who pay for it, coastal states facing spill risk, are not the people imposing the sanctions.

#### Worked example: the price-cap arithmetic

The G7 oil price cap of December 2022 was supposed to limit Russian crude to \$60 per barrel by denying Western shipping and insurance to any cargo sold above that price. Here is why it leaked. Suppose Brent trades at \$85 and Russian Urals would normally sell at a \$5 discount, so \$80. Under the cap, a buyer using Western insurance can only pay \$60. But a buyer using a shadow-fleet tanker with non-Western insurance can pay, say, \$70, still a \$10 discount to where Urals would otherwise trade, and a \$15 gift relative to Brent. The Russian seller gets \$70 instead of \$60, a 17 percent uplift. The buyer gets crude \$15 below Brent. The only loser is the cap. With 600 tankers available to carry cap-busting cargoes, the marginal barrel cleared well above \$60 for most of 2023, which is why Russian oil revenues recovered to roughly \$160 billion that year, far above what a binding \$60 cap would have produced. The cap did compress revenue versus the \$218 billion of 2022, but it did not break the floor, and the spread between the cap and the actual clearing price was the rerouting failure mode quantified in dollars.

### Mode 3: Domestic adaptation

The third failure mode is internal. A sanctioned economy reorganizes itself. Import substitution replaces foreign goods with domestic ones, often lower quality but functional. Capital controls stop money from fleeing. The central bank jacks interest rates to defend the currency, then relaxes them once the panic passes. State subsidies cushion the population. The economy becomes poorer and less efficient, but it does not collapse, and the assets that survive the reorganization, the dominant domestic banks, the export champions, the utilities, can be remarkably resilient.

Russia is the textbook case of fast adaptation. Within weeks of the February 2022 sanctions, the central bank under Elvira Nabiullina hiked the policy rate to 20 percent, imposed capital controls that forced exporters to convert foreign-currency revenue into rubles, and restricted outflows. The ruble's collapse to 135 reversed, and by mid-2022 it was trading near 60, stronger than pre-war. This was not a sign of underlying strength so much as a sign of a closed system being forced to balance, but for an investor the distinction matters less than the outcome: the currency did not spiral, and assets priced for a spiral were mispriced.

The currency-control mechanism is worth unpacking because it recurs in every adaptation episode. A currency collapses under sanctions for a simple reason: capital tries to flee, demand for the local currency evaporates, and demand for hard currency spikes. The textbook response, and the one Russia executed, is to break that dynamic by force. Force exporters to sell their dollar and euro earnings for rubles, and you create artificial demand for the local currency. Forbid residents from converting and expatriating, and you stop the flight. Raise rates to 20 percent, and you make holding the local currency attractive enough that the panic abates. None of this fixes the underlying economy, the country is poorer, its capital account is closed, its citizens cannot freely move money, but it absolutely fixes the *price* of the currency, and the price is what the market was extrapolating to disaster. The investor's insight is that a currency defended by controls is not the same instrument as a freely floating currency, and pricing it as though the controls will fail, when the state has both the reserves and the will to enforce them, is a mistake.

Import substitution, the other pillar of adaptation, is slower and messier but equally important. When a country is cut off from foreign machinery, components, software, and consumer goods, it does three things over time: it buys inferior substitutes from non-coalition suppliers, it builds domestic capacity where the economics allow, and it cannibalizes existing stock for parts. The result is a measurable but bounded efficiency loss, the economy produces less, and what it produces costs more, but the catastrophic-collapse scenario rarely materializes because human ingenuity under constraint is formidable. The Soviet economy ran for seventy years largely cut off from Western technology and never collapsed from the cutoff alone; it collapsed from internal contradiction. The lesson for the modern investor is to model adaptation as a haircut to productivity and margins, perhaps a meaningful one, rather than as a terminal event. The companies that survive the substitution era, the dominant domestic banks, the resource champions, the utilities serving captive demand, emerge with weaker growth but intact franchises, and the market that priced them for liquidation was wrong about the level even when it was right about the direction.

A subtle point about adaptation is that it is asymmetric across the asset stack. Equity holders in a sanctioned economy can be wiped out even as the underlying business adapts and survives, because the equity may be expropriated, delisted, or rendered untradeable by the very sanctions that the business is surviving. This is why the cleanest expressions of the adaptation thesis are usually *not* the target's own equities but the adjacent beneficiaries, and why the distressed-debt angle, where legal recovery mechanisms sometimes survive, can be more investable than the equity. The business adapts; whether *your claim on the business* survives the legal and sanctions overlay is a separate question that must be analyzed on its own terms.

#### Worked example: import substitution math

Consider a Russian industrial firm that imported \$100 million a year of Western machinery components before the sanctions. After the cutoff, it faces three options. It can source from China at, say, a 15 percent higher cost and lower quality, raising its component bill to \$115 million. It can develop a domestic substitute at higher unit cost, perhaps \$130 million, but capturing the value domestically. Or it can do without and accept lower output. In practice firms blend all three. The result is a real efficiency loss, the firm is now spending \$115 to \$130 million for what cost \$100 million, a margin hit of 15 to 30 percent on that input line. That is painful, but it is not bankruptcy. If the firm's components were 20 percent of total costs, the blended cost increase is roughly 3 to 6 percent at the company level, survivable for a business with any pricing power. The market in March 2022 priced many such firms as if the cutoff were terminal. The adaptation math said otherwise, and the equity of the survivors compounded as the panic faded.

### Mode 4: Political resistance hardening

The fourth failure mode is the cruelest irony of sanctions: they often strengthen the regime they are meant to weaken. Economic pain inflicted by a foreign power is a gift to any leader skilled at narrative. The story writes itself, we are under siege by hostile foreigners, our suffering is their fault, rally around the flag. Sanctions convert a domestic-policy failure into a foreign-aggression grievance, and grievance is the most durable political fuel there is.

![Cuba sanctions timeline from the 1962 embargo through six decades with no regime change](/imgs/blogs/how-sanctions-fail-and-what-that-means-for-asset-prices-6.png)

Cuba is the purest example. Sixty years of comprehensive US embargo, repeatedly tightened, from the 1962 embargo through the 1992 Cuban Democracy Act and the 1996 Helms-Burton Act, never produced regime change. If anything, the embargo became the central organizing grievance of the Cuban state, the explanation for every shortage, the justification for every restriction on dissent. The 1990s "Special Period," when Soviet subsidies vanished and the economy contracted by a third, was exactly the moment the regime should have fallen under the standard sanctions theory. It did not, because the embargo gave the leadership an external enemy to blame. Cuba's GDP per capita today sits around \$9,000 against roughly \$65,000 in the United States, a measure of how much poorer the embargo helped make the island, and yet the political objective, regime change, remains unmet after six decades.

The political logic of hardening deserves careful treatment because it is the failure mode most investors underweight, precisely because it is psychological rather than economic and therefore does not show up in a spreadsheet. Sanctions are an act of economic warfare by a foreign power, and they are perceived as such by the target's population, including by many who dislike their own government. A regime under sanctions can credibly tell its people that their hardship is the deliberate work of a hostile outside force, and this narrative is extraordinarily potent because it contains a kernel of truth: the sanctions *are* the proximate cause of the empty shelves. The regime converts every shortage into evidence of foreign malice, every queue into a patriotic sacrifice, every dissident into a suspected foreign agent. Authoritarian regimes are usually far better at this narrative work than democracies expect, because controlling the narrative is the core competency that keeps them in power. The sanctioning side, reasoning from its own democratic intuitions, expects economic pain to translate into political pressure on the regime from below. The actual translation, in regime after regime, runs the other way: pain from outside consolidates support behind the leader who promises to resist it.

There is a darker corollary for the investor who is tempted to short the regime's survival. Sanctions tend to strengthen exactly the parts of the state that are most useful for repression and most aligned with the leadership, the security services, the loyal oligarchy, the state-controlled media, while weakening exactly the parts, the entrepreneurial middle class, the internationally connected professionals, that might have pushed for change. By impoverishing the independent private sector and forcing economic activity into state and state-connected channels, sanctions can centralize power rather than diffuse it. The trade implication is to be deeply skeptical of any thesis premised on imminent regime collapse under economic pressure; the base rate for that outcome, across the historical record, is close to zero on any horizon a trader cares about.

#### Worked example: the grievance premium in approval ratings

You can put rough numbers on political hardening. Suppose a leader enters a sanctions episode with a 45 percent approval rating amid an economic slump that, absent foreign pressure, would have pushed approval toward 35 percent as living standards fell. Sanctions hit, the leader reframes the slump as foreign aggression, and approval instead rises to 55 percent in the rally-around-the-flag effect, a swing of roughly 20 percentage points relative to the no-sanctions counterfactual. For a trader, the implication is direct: any asset whose price embeds an expectation of imminent regime change or policy capitulation is mispriced, because the political mechanism is running in reverse. When you see approval ratings rise under sanctions, fade the regime-change trade and lengthen your holding-period assumption for the target's assets, because the squeeze will last longer than the consensus thinks but will also fail more completely.

### Mode 5: Coalition fragmentation

The fifth and final failure mode operates on the sanctioning side. A sanctions coalition is only as strong as its weakest enforcer, and over time the coalition frays. Allies discover that their own economies are bearing costs they did not anticipate. Specific industries lobby for carve-outs. Some members enforce vigorously, others wink. The target learns to route its trade through the laxest jurisdictions, and the effective coverage of the sanctions shrinks even as the formal coverage stays the same.

Europe's experience with Russian gas is the canonical fragmentation story. The political will to cut Russian energy was strong in the abstract, but Germany's industrial base, Hungary's dependence, and a dozen other national interests produced a patchwork of exemptions, timelines, and quiet exceptions. Pipeline gas was treated differently from LNG; some flows continued under long-term contracts; enforcement of secondary measures varied wildly across member states. Each exception was individually defensible and collectively corrosive to the regime's bite.

Fragmentation is the failure mode that distinguishes a coalition regime from a unilateral one, and it is why the breadth of a coalition at announcement is a poor predictor of its durability. A coalition is held together by shared resolve, but resolve is unevenly distributed across members because the *costs* are unevenly distributed. The member whose industry depends on the sanctioned input bears a heavy cost; the member with no such dependence bears almost none. Over time, the high-cost members lobby, quietly or openly, for carve-outs that protect their specific interests, and each carve-out is granted because the alternative, losing that member from the coalition entirely, looks worse. The carve-outs accumulate. Meanwhile, enforcement, which is expensive and politically thankless, gets prioritized differently in each jurisdiction, so the target learns which ports, which banks, and which intermediaries operate in the lax zones and routes its trade accordingly. The formal coalition can look unbroken on paper while its *effective* coverage has eroded to a fraction of the original.

For the investor, coalition fragmentation is the most legible of all the failure modes because it generates a paper trail. Waivers are published. Exemptions are negotiated in public. Enforcement actions, or their conspicuous absence, are a matter of record. When you see the sanctioning bloc start to grant general licenses, extend wind-down periods, or carve out specific sectors, you are watching the coalition's resolve being repriced in real time, and the target's assets should be repricing with it. The Venezuela case is instructive: the periodic US licenses that allowed Chevron to operate, or that temporarily eased oil-sector restrictions, produced immediate, sharp repricing in Venezuelan debt, because each license was a concrete, datable piece of evidence that the coalition's bite was loosening. The license did not need to end the sanctions; it only needed to signal the direction of travel.

#### Worked example: the enforcement-gap discount

Imagine a sanctioned commodity that two jurisdictions can buy through, one enforcing strictly (90 percent chance of penalty for a violation) and one loosely (10 percent chance). A trader assessing the expected cost of moving \$100 million of goods through the loose jurisdiction faces roughly \$10 million of probability-weighted penalty risk, versus \$90 million through the strict one. Naturally, volume migrates to the loose jurisdiction, and as it does, the *effective* discount the target must offer to move its goods shrinks, because the binding constraint, willing buyers, has loosened. The investor watches the geography of the trade: when official waivers multiply and enforcement actions concentrate in one or two venues while others go untouched, coalition fragmentation is advancing, and the floor under the target's export economy is rising.

## The historical scorecard

Pull these threads together across the major regimes of the last sixty years and a pattern emerges that should unsettle anyone who believes comprehensive sanctions reliably change behavior.

![Sanctions failure scorecard across Cuba, Iran, Russia, North Korea, and Venezuela showing failure modes, adaptation, policy change, and market signals](/imgs/blogs/how-sanctions-fail-and-what-that-means-for-asset-prices-2.png)

**Cuba** is sixty years of comprehensive embargo with zero regime change. All five failure modes are entrenched. The grievance narrative (mode 4) is so deeply institutionalized that the embargo functions as a pillar of the regime's legitimacy. From a market standpoint, Cuban assets are essentially frozen, with no tradeable instruments, but the 2015 Obama normalization showed what a policy thaw does: a brief window when Cuban-linked assets and tourism plays became investable, snapped shut again by the 2017 Trump re-tightening. The lesson is that even a sixty-year failure can produce tradeable windows when the sanctioning side, not the target, blinks.

**Iran** is forty years of sanctions with one partial policy success. The 2015 JCPOA nuclear deal was a genuine, if temporary, policy change, achieved through negotiation backed by sanctions pressure. But it was reversed in 2018, and Iran's response, ramping its nuclear program and rebuilding its oil exports through Chinese buyers, demonstrated failure modes 1, 2, 3, and 4 all at once. The oil-export recovery from 0.5 to 1.8 million barrels a day is the cleanest single failure signal in the entire dataset.

![Iran oil exports recovering from the 2019 maximum-pressure trough back toward 1.8 million barrels a day by 2023](/imgs/blogs/how-sanctions-fail-and-what-that-means-for-asset-prices-3.png)

**Russia** is the live case, only two years in, and the verdict is genuinely uncertain. Failure modes 1, 2, and 3 are clearly operating, third-country buyers, the shadow fleet, and rapid domestic adaptation. Mode 5 is emerging as Europe's coalition shows strain. But two years is too short to declare the regime a failure, and there is a real scenario in which cumulative pressure grinds down the war economy over five to ten years. The honest position is that Russia is a "watch the failure signals" case, not yet a confident "buy the failure" case, and the investable thesis is selective, commodity exposure and the narrowing Urals discount, rather than wholesale.

**North Korea** is twenty-plus years of the most comprehensive sanctions on earth, and the regime not only survives but advanced its nuclear program throughout. GDP per capita sits near \$1,800. There are no listed assets to trade, which makes North Korea the limiting case: sanctions can impoverish a country almost without bound and still fail at their political objective, and the absence of any market means there is no investment thesis at all, only a cautionary data point about how far failure can run.

**Venezuela** is five years of sanctions on the Maduro regime. PDVSA's oil output collapsed from a peak near 3 million barrels a day in 2001 to around 0.6 million, a genuine economic devastation, yet the regime survives. For investors, the interesting feature is the distressed-debt angle: PDVSA and Venezuelan sovereign bonds trade at deeply distressed levels, and the periodic US licensing windows (such as the 2023 Chevron license) create episodic repricing in those instruments. Venezuela shows that even amid output collapse, the *financial* assets can offer asymmetric distressed-debt setups when enforcement loosens.

## Reading failure as a market signal

Here is the synthesis, and the part that matters for a portfolio. The five failure modes are not just political phenomena; each one has a market signature, and the signatures lead the price. The job is to detect the failure mode, recognize that it compresses the discount on the target's assets, treat that compression as a forward-looking indicator of eventual policy drift, and convert it into a specific trade.

![Causal chain from a detected failure signal through discount compression and forward indication to a specific asset trade](/imgs/blogs/how-sanctions-fail-and-what-that-means-for-asset-prices-5.png)

The chain runs like this. A failure mode becomes observable, say, Iranian exports rising in the tanker-tracking data, or the Urals discount narrowing in the physical crude market. That observation tells you the target's economy has a firmer floor than the consensus believes, which means the assets linked to that economy are priced for a worse outcome than is now likely. The discount compresses. That compression is itself a forward signal: it tends to precede the eventual, grudging acknowledgment that the sanctions have not worked, which in turn precedes any formal policy shift. You do not need the policy shift to make money; you need only the discount to compress from its panic lows toward something resembling fair value for a sanctioned-but-surviving asset.

The trades that express this are not "buy the sanctioned country's stock market," which is usually impossible or unethical, but the adjacent, investable expressions:

- **Asian refiners and trading houses** that capture the circumvention margin (mode 1). When Iran or Russia must sell at a discount, the buyer pockets the spread, and the buyer is often a listed, investable company.
- **Tanker and freight operators** whose vessels carry the rerouted cargo (mode 2). A growing shadow fleet means tight tanker supply for legitimate trade too, supporting freight rates.
- **Commodity exporters with target-adjacent exposure** that benefit from the supply dislocations sanctions create, even as the target itself adapts.
- **Distressed sovereign and quasi-sovereign debt** of the target, where legal licensing windows (Venezuela, periodically Iran) create episodic, asymmetric repricing.

The reflexivity here is worth naming. The forced sellers in the panic phase are price-insensitive: a fund unwinding a sanctioned position because its mandate now forbids it does not care whether the asset is worth 20 cents or 40 cents on the dollar, it simply has to be out. Price-insensitive selling overshoots, pushing the asset below any reasonable estimate of impaired fair value. The contrarian's edge is to be the price-sensitive buyer on the other side of that flow, demanding a margin of safety so large that even a pessimistic read of the cash flows leaves room for a multiple of the entry price. You are not betting that the sanctions will be lifted; you are betting that the asset is worth more than the panic price even if the sanctions persist at their current, decaying intensity. That distinction is the whole discipline. When it holds, you make money whether the sanctions stay or go, because you bought below the value of a sanctioned-but-surviving cash flow. When it does not hold, when the asset really is impaired toward zero and the discount is justified, you stay away, and the dashboard is what tells you which world you are in.

There is also a timing asymmetry that the patient investor exploits. The failure signals, rising third-country imports, a growing shadow fleet, a stabilizing currency, appear in the data months before the broad market acknowledges that the sanctions have not worked. The acknowledgment, when it comes, is gradual and grudging: an analyst note here, a revised forecast there, a quiet re-rating. By the time the consensus has flipped from "the target is finished" to "the target has adapted," most of the repricing has happened. The money is made in the gap between the data turning and the narrative turning, which is exactly the gap the failure-detection dashboard is built to exploit. This is why the strategy rewards monitoring discipline over conviction: the edge is not in having a stronger opinion than the market, it is in reading the same data the market could read but is not yet reading because it is still anchored on the panic narrative.

### Worked example: sizing the Russia commodity trade

Put concrete numbers on a disciplined version of the Russia thesis as it looked in mid-2022. Suppose you concluded that failure modes 1, 2, and 3 were firmly in place and that Russian oil would keep flowing at a discount. You cannot and should not buy Russian equities. But you can buy the listed Asian refiner that processes discounted Urals. Assume that refiner earns an incremental \$8 per barrel of crack-spread uplift on 300,000 barrels a day of Urals it would not otherwise have access to: that is \$2.4 million a day, roughly \$875 million a year of incremental pre-tax margin. If that refiner trades at, say, a \$10 billion market cap and the incremental margin drops \$500 million to the bottom line after tax, you have added 5 percent to its earnings yield from the sanctions failure alone, on top of its base business. Size the position so that a re-tightening of enforcement, the main risk, costs you no more than 1 to 2 percent of the portfolio, and you have a positively skewed bet on a failure mode that the tanker data lets you monitor in near real time. The point is not the precise figures but the structure: the failure is observable, the beneficiary is investable, and the position can be sized against a monitorable risk.

## The early-warning dashboard

To trade this systematically rather than anecdotally, you need a dashboard, a fixed set of signals you watch for each sanctioned situation, each mapped to a data source and an expected lead time before the target's assets reprice.

Run down the five rows. **Third-country circumvention** shows up as rising imports of the sanctioned good recorded in the customs data of China, India, and Turkey, visible in UN Comtrade and national customs releases, with a lead time of roughly three to six months before the export recovery is widely recognized. **Commodity rerouting** shows up in tanker-tracking AIS data as a growing shadow fleet and rising ship-to-ship transfer counts, a faster signal at two to four months because the ships are physical and trackable. **Domestic adaptation** shows up in central-bank reserve data and stabilizing inflation, a slower signal at six to twelve months because reorganizing an economy takes time. **Political hardening** shows up in approval polling and election results and is the slowest signal at twelve months and beyond, but it is the most important for setting your holding-period assumption. **Coalition fragmentation** shows up in the official record of waivers and the concentration of enforcement actions, with a lead time of three to nine months.

#### Worked example: the discipline that keeps you out of trouble

The contrarian sanctions trade is genuinely dangerous, and the worked example that matters most is the one about risk control, because the failure of this strategy is not subtle, it is a re-tightening that craters your position. Suppose you have allocated to a basket of sanctions-failure trades totaling 8 percent of a \$100 million book, so \$8 million. The dominant tail risk is a sudden enforcement escalation, secondary sanctions on the Chinese buyers, a crackdown on the shadow fleet, that could mark your basket down 40 percent in a week, a \$3.2 million hit, or 3.2 percent of the book. To keep that within a 2 percent maximum-pain budget, you either cut the basket to 5 percent of the book (\$5 million, capping the tail at \$2 million) or you hedge a portion with options on the most liquid leg. The discipline is non-negotiable: this is a trade where you are explicitly betting that a political process fails, and political processes can re-accelerate without warning. Position so that being wrong is survivable, monitor the dashboard so that you exit on the first sign of genuine re-tightening rather than the first scary headline, and never let the contrarian conviction talk you out of the stop.

## The mistakes that destroy the trade

Before turning to when sanctions genuinely work, it is worth cataloguing the analytical errors that most often turn a sound contrarian framework into a loss, because the strategy fails far more often through execution mistakes than through a wrong top-level call.

The first mistake is confusing the day-one impact with the steady state. The market's most common error, and the one that creates the opportunity, is to take the size of the initial shock, a 40 percent currency drop, a halving of exports, and extrapolate it as a permanent condition. The disciplined investor must not make the inverse error: assuming that *all* sanctions decay to nothing. Some do not. The skill is in estimating the decayed steady state, not in assuming the steady state is either the day-one shock or full normalization. The right question is always "what is the new, adapted level of exports, margins, and cash flow once the workarounds mature," and that level is usually well above the panic price and well below the pre-sanction baseline.

The second mistake is ignoring the legal and structural risk to the *instrument* even when the underlying business adapts. A sanctioned company can survive economically while the specific security you hold is expropriated, delisted, frozen, or stripped of its rights by the sanctions overlay. Foreign holders of Russian equities discovered that adaptation of the business did them no good when their shares were trapped in accounts they could not access and dividends they could not receive. The cash flow surviving is necessary but not sufficient; your legal claim on that cash flow must also survive the sanctions architecture, and that is a separate, often harder, analysis.

The third mistake is underestimating the re-tightening tail. Sanctions regimes are not static; they ratchet. A regime that has been decaying for two years can be sharply re-tightened by a single political event, a new atrocity, an election, a secondary-sanctions package aimed at the very intermediaries your thesis depends on. The shadow fleet can be sanctioned vessel by vessel. The Chinese buyers can be threatened with loss of dollar access. When this happens, the discount that had been compressing snaps wide again, and a position sized for the slow grind gets carried out. The error is not holding the position; it is sizing it as though the slow grind were the only scenario.

The fourth mistake is letting the contrarian identity override the data. Once an investor has taken the unfashionable side of a sanctions trade, defending it against social and reputational pressure, there is a powerful temptation to keep defending it as the facts change. The discipline is to hold the position because the dashboard says the failure modes are intact, and to exit the moment the dashboard says they are reversing, regardless of how much intellectual capital has been invested in the contrarian view. The market does not reward you for being a contrarian; it rewards you for being right, and being right means following the data out of the trade as faithfully as you followed it in.

## When sanctions actually work, and how to tell

Intellectual honesty requires the other side of the ledger. Sanctions are not uniformly useless, and the investor who treats every regime as a guaranteed failure will eventually buy an asset that goes to zero because, this time, the sanctions held. So what does success look like, and how do you distinguish it from the failure pattern in time to avoid the value trap?

Working sanctions have a different signature, shown in the left-hand panel of the before-and-after comparison above. The target's policy actually changes, or the regime comes to the negotiating table. Exports fall and *stay* fallen, with no recovery in the customs or tanker data. Credit spreads on the target's debt stay wide rather than compressing. The assets remain genuinely uninvestable because the cash flows behind them really are impaired. The conditions that make sanctions work are reasonably well understood: a near-universal coalition that leaves no major third-country buyer (hard to achieve for any globally traded commodity), a target that is small and trade-dependent rather than large and resource-rich, an objective that is narrow and face-saving for the target rather than existential, and a regime that lacks the narrative skill to convert pain into grievance. South Africa under apartheid sanctions and Libya's 2003 WMD reversal are cases where enough of these conditions held that the pressure contributed to a real policy change.

The practical test for the investor is the recovery signal. If, six to twelve months into a regime, the export data is recovering, the discount is narrowing, and the currency has stabilized, you are looking at failure, and the discount is an opportunity. If, over the same window, exports stay depressed, the discount stays wide, and the currency keeps bleeding, you are looking at a regime that might actually be working, and the cheap asset is a value trap. The dashboard does not just tell you when to buy; it tells you when to stay away, and the difference between those two readings is the difference between the trade that compounds and the trade that goes to zero.

It is worth being concrete about the conditions that tilt a regime toward genuine success, because they are the screen that keeps you out of the value traps. The single most important factor is the universality of the coalition relative to the good in question. Sanctions on a niche good with few global buyers, advanced semiconductors, certain dual-use technologies, specialized aircraft parts, can genuinely bite because there is no deep alternative market to absorb the displaced supply and no easy substitute on the buy side. Sanctions on a fungible, globally demanded commodity like crude oil almost never bite for long, because the reshuffling described earlier finds a buyer at a discount. The second factor is the target's economic structure: a small, open, trade-dependent economy with no commodity export base and no large domestic market is far more vulnerable than a large, resource-rich economy that can feed itself, fuel itself, and sell its commodities to whoever will buy. The third is the nature of the demand: a face-saving, narrowly scoped concession the target can grant without existential cost is achievable, while regime change or surrender on a core security interest essentially never is, because no leadership will trade its survival for sanctions relief. Run a candidate situation through these three filters and you will usually know, before you ever look at a price, whether you are hunting for a failure-driven repricing or staring at a value trap dressed up as one.

## The disciplined contrarian's checklist

Bringing it together, here is how I would operationalize the whole framework as a repeatable process rather than a one-off bet.

First, **separate the ethical question from the financial one explicitly**, on paper. Decide what you will not own for reputational or mandate reasons, and accept that this rules out some of the most direct expressions. Then build your thesis only within the investable, ethical set, the refiners, the tankers, the adjacent commodity exporters, the licensed distressed debt.

Second, **identify which failure modes are active** for the specific situation, and which data source you will watch for each. If you cannot name the signal and the source, you do not have a trade, you have a hunch. The five-row dashboard is the discipline that turns the hunch into a monitorable position.

Third, **demand the recovery signal before sizing up**. The deepest discounts appear at the moment of maximum panic, but so do the genuine value traps. Wait for the export data to firm, the discount to begin compressing, or the shadow fleet to grow, the concrete evidence that adaptation is winning, before committing real size. You will give up some of the absolute bottom in exchange for distinguishing failure from success, and that is a trade worth making. The cost of waiting is small relative to the cost of being early into a regime that turns out to work: a position entered at the panic low into a value trap can lose its entire value, while a position entered after the recovery signal, at perhaps a 15 to 25 percent higher price, still captures the bulk of a repricing that often runs to multiples of the entry. The asymmetry favors patience. The investors who lose money on this strategy are almost never the ones who waited for confirmation; they are the ones who anchored on the cheapness alone and bought before the data told them whether the cheapness was opportunity or warning.

A useful operational rule is to require at least two independent failure signals to confirm before sizing up, rather than acting on any single one. A narrowing discount alone could be noise or a temporary supply shock; a narrowing discount *plus* rising third-country customs imports *plus* a growing shadow fleet together describe a maturing circumvention machine, and three signals pointing the same way are far harder to fake than one. The multi-signal requirement is the same discipline that good investigators use: any single clue is suggestive, but a stack of independent clues all pointing the same direction is what turns a hunch into a high-confidence position you can size with conviction.

Fourth, **size against the re-tightening tail, not the base case**. The base case in a failure trade is a slow grind to fair value. The tail is a sudden enforcement escalation that craters the position. Size so that the tail is survivable, hedge the most liquid leg if you can, and pre-commit to an exit on genuine re-tightening.

Fifth, **set the holding-period assumption from the political read, not the financial one**. Failure modes 1, 2, 3, and 5 can compress the discount in months. But the ultimate repricing, the formal acknowledgment that the sanctions did not work, can take years, and political hardening (mode 4) can stretch it further. This is a patient trade. If your capital or your mandate cannot wait two to five years, the strategy is not for you.

It is worth closing on what this framework is *not*. It is not a license to buy anything cheap because sanctions usually fail. It is not a political argument that sanctions should never be imposed; whether sanctions are good policy is a question of values and objectives that sits entirely outside the investor's mandate. And it is most emphatically not a recommendation to hold assets that your conscience or your mandate forbids. The framework is narrower and more useful than any of those: it is a structured way to recognize that the market systematically misprices sanctioned situations because the marginal seller in the panic phase is reasoning about politics and reputation while the patient buyer can reason about decayed, adapted cash flows. The mispricing is real, it is recurrent, and it is large, but capturing it requires the discipline to watch the data rather than the headlines, to demand confirmation rather than chase cheapness, to size against the re-tightening tail, and to wait years rather than months for the repricing to complete. Most investors will not do those things, which is precisely why the opportunity persists.

The deepest lesson of sixty years of sanctions is that economic pressure and political outcomes are only loosely coupled. A country can be made dramatically poorer without changing its behavior at all, Cuba and North Korea prove that to the point of absurdity. For the investor, that loose coupling is the entire opportunity. The market prices sanctioned assets as though pain equals capitulation, because the people doing the early selling are reasoning politically and emotionally. The disciplined contrarian reasons financially, watches the failure signals in the data, separates the ethics from the economics, and buys the surviving cash flows from the forced sellers at a discount, with the position sized so that being early, or occasionally wrong, never ends the game. The sanctions usually fail. The assets usually reprice. The edge is in knowing which "usually" you are looking at, and having the discipline to wait for the data to tell you.
