September 23, 2022. Kwasi Kwarteng, freshly appointed Chancellor of the Exchequer under new Prime Minister Liz Truss, delivered a "mini-budget" that was anything but mini. In a single statement he announced £45 billion of unfunded tax cuts — the largest in 50 years — with no offsetting spending reductions and no independent fiscal assessment. By the next morning, the British pound had fallen to \$1.035, a record low since decimalization. Within four days, UK 30-year Gilt yields — the price the UK government pays to borrow for three decades — had jumped 150 basis points (1.5 percentage points). A basis point, for context, is one hundredth of a percent; 150 basis points in four days is seismic. UK pension funds, which had borrowed heavily against their bond holdings to match long-term liabilities, received margin calls they could not meet. The Bank of England, which had just weeks earlier begun *selling* bonds to tighten financial conditions, was forced into an emergency *purchase* program of up to £65 billion to prevent systemic collapse.

Forty-four days after taking office, Liz Truss resigned. No elected leader of a G7 country had survived so short a tenure in living memory.

The Truss episode is the sharpest single illustration of the post-COVID political and financial landscape: a world in which fiscal credibility is no longer assumed, in which bond markets can impose discipline on governments within hours, and in which the political consequences of economic mismanagement are immediate and terminal. But the Truss story is only one chapter. To understand it fully, you need to understand what COVID-19 did to the entire global fiscal and political architecture — and why the scars it left will shape markets for decades.

## Foundations: How Fiscal Policy Becomes a Market Variable

Before we can understand why the Truss mini-budget caused a financial crisis, or why the US Federal Reserve hiking rates from near zero to 5.5% matters for the price of a growth stock in Tokyo, we need to build some foundations from scratch.

### What is fiscal policy?

*Fiscal policy* is what a government does with spending and taxes. If the government spends more than it collects in taxes, the difference is the *fiscal deficit* — the gap it has to fill by borrowing. Borrowing means issuing bonds: the government promises to pay you back with interest, and you hand over cash today. The total stock of borrowing accumulated over time is the *national debt* or *sovereign debt*.

For most of the post-World War II era in wealthy countries, fiscal deficits were moderate and bond markets were boring. Interest rates — the price of government borrowing — were set primarily by central banks trying to hit inflation targets, and sovereign bonds from rich countries were seen as essentially risk-free. If the UK government wanted to borrow, it could, at a modest rate. The market absorbed it automatically.

The Truss episode changed that assumption in a dramatic way, and it wasn't just about the UK. It was a signal that bond markets were suddenly paying much closer attention to fiscal math than they had in decades.

### What is a basis point and why does it matter?

A *basis point* (abbreviated bps or bp) is 0.01% — one hundredth of one percentage point. When Gilt yields moved 150 basis points in four days, that means the interest rate on 30-year UK government bonds moved from roughly 3.5% to 5.0%. That may sound small in percentage terms, but for a 30-year bond, a 1% change in yield translates into roughly a 15% change in the bond's price. A 1.5% move means a 22% loss on the face value of existing bonds. For pension funds holding hundreds of billions of pounds of these instruments, that is catastrophic.

### What is a central bank, and what is its "policy rate"?

A *central bank* — the Federal Reserve in the US, the Bank of England in the UK, the European Central Bank for the eurozone — is the institution that controls the supply of money and sets the benchmark short-term interest rate, called the *policy rate* or *base rate*. When a central bank raises rates, borrowing becomes more expensive everywhere: mortgages, car loans, corporate bonds, government bonds. This is supposed to cool inflation by making people spend less. When it cuts rates, borrowing gets cheaper and the economy is stimulated.

### What is inflation?

*Inflation* is the rate at which prices rise. If inflation is 2%, a basket of goods that costs \$100 today will cost \$102 next year. Central banks in most wealthy countries target roughly 2% inflation as the sweet spot — enough to keep the economy out of deflation, not so much that it erodes purchasing power. When inflation persistently runs above target, the central bank raises rates to bring it back down. That is exactly what happened in 2022.

### What is the "neutral rate" (r-star)?

The *neutral rate* — formally called r-star, or r\* — is the real (inflation-adjusted) interest rate at which the economy runs at full potential without either accelerating or decelerating. Think of it as the cruise-control setting. If the actual policy rate is above r-star, monetary policy is contractionary (the economy is slowing). If it's below, policy is stimulative (the economy is heating up).

Before COVID, most estimates put r-star for the US at around 0.5% in real terms. The post-COVID evidence suggests it has risen to around 1-1.5% real (Federal Reserve Bank of New York DSGE model, 2024). That shift — 0.5 to 1 percentage points higher — doesn't sound large, but it means the entire landscape of asset pricing, from stock multiples to real estate cap rates to government borrowing costs, has permanently reset.

### What is duration risk?

*Duration* is a measure of how sensitive a bond's price is to changes in interest rates. A bond with a 10-year maturity might have a duration of 8 years — meaning a 1% rise in interest rates causes roughly an 8% drop in its price. A 30-year bond has even higher duration. This is why the Gilt crisis was so devastating for pension funds: they held enormous quantities of long-duration bonds, and when yields spiked, the value of those holdings collapsed.

With these foundations in place, we can now understand exactly what happened — and why it matters for every investor today.

## Inflation as a Political Crisis: The Biden Approval Collapse

The United States emerged from COVID-19 with the most aggressive fiscal stimulus program in its peacetime history. Between March 2020 and the end of 2021, Congress passed roughly \$5 trillion in emergency spending — the CARES Act (\$2.2 trillion), two further pandemic relief bills, and the American Rescue Plan (\$1.9 trillion) signed by President Biden in March 2021. Total US fiscal stimulus from 2020-2021 amounted to approximately 26% of GDP (IMF Fiscal Monitor, 2021).

At the same time, COVID-19 disrupted global supply chains on an unprecedented scale. Factories in Asia shut down. Container shipping costs rose 500%. The US semiconductor shortage idled auto assembly lines. Demand, turbocharged by stimulus checks, ran into supply that could not keep up. The result was predictable in retrospect: inflation.

But nobody predicted how fast it would arrive or how severe it would get.

### The CPI trajectory

US consumer price inflation (CPI), as measured by the Bureau of Labor Statistics, ran as follows:

- January 2021: 1.4% year-on-year
- June 2021: 5.4% — the first serious alarm bell
- December 2021: 7.0% — a 40-year high not seen since 1981
- June 2022: 9.1% — the peak, the highest reading since November 1981

The Federal Reserve, under Chair Jay Powell, initially characterized the inflation surge as "transitory" — supply chains would heal, stimulus would fade, prices would normalize. That call proved deeply wrong. By late 2021, with inflation still accelerating and labor markets running hot (unemployment fell to 3.5%), the Fed began a dramatic about-face.

### The political transmission

Inflation translates into political pain through the most basic channel in democratic politics: voters feel it in their wallets every time they buy groceries, fill up their car, or pay rent. Unlike abstract economic statistics, a 40% rise in the price of used cars (which happened in 2021-2022) or a 50% jump in electricity bills is visceral and immediate.

President Biden entered office in January 2021 with an approval rating of approximately 54% (FiveThirtyEight aggregate, January 20, 2021). By June 2022 — the month inflation peaked at 9.1% — his approval had fallen to roughly 38-39%, a decline of about 15 percentage points. Multiple academic analyses have estimated that each 1 percentage point rise in CPI was associated with roughly 2 percentage points of approval loss for Biden (though causality is difficult to isolate, as inflation correlated with other economic anxieties).

The figure above shows this relationship graphically. The two curves move in opposite directions: as CPI climbed from 1.4% in early 2021 to 9.1% by mid-2022, approval fell almost perfectly in parallel. The political cost of inflation was direct, large, and front-loaded — it hit fast, because voters update their views on the economy continuously.

The Democrats lost the House of Representatives in the November 2022 midterm elections. Biden's "Bidenomics" framing — emphasizing job creation and manufacturing investment — failed to dent the public's inflation-driven pessimism. By 2024, Biden's approval never recovered above the mid-40s, ultimately contributing to the decision not to seek re-election.

The lesson is structural: inflation is the most politically toxic macroeconomic outcome for incumbents in democratic systems, more so than unemployment, because it is universal (everyone faces it), visible (daily price observation), and attributed to the sitting government regardless of its actual origin.

## The Truss Moment: Fiscal Credibility and Bond Markets

If the Biden approval collapse illustrates inflation as a political crisis, the Truss episode illustrates the other half of the post-COVID fiscal reckoning: the sudden discovery by bond markets that not all sovereign borrowers are equal.

![UK 10-year Gilt yields spiked 150 basis points in days after the Kwarteng mini-budget, forcing a Bank of England emergency intervention](/imgs/blogs/post-pandemic-political-scars-inflation-supply-chains-fiscal-2.png)

### Background: the UK's fiscal situation in 2022

Going into September 2022, the UK faced genuine economic difficulties. Energy prices had surged after Russia's invasion of Ukraine (UK gas prices roughly tripled in 2022). Inflation had risen to over 10%. Real wages were falling. But the UK also entered 2022 with a fiscal deficit around 5-6% of GDP — not catastrophically high by COVID-era standards, but elevated, and with a rising public debt burden.

Liz Truss had campaigned for the Conservative Party leadership on a platform of tax cuts and supply-side growth. Her economic worldview, sometimes called "Trussonomics," held that cutting taxes — particularly the top income tax rate, a reversal of a planned corporate tax increase, and abolishing a bankers' bonus cap — would stimulate investment and grow the economy out of its problems. Her Chancellor, Kwasi Kwarteng, was a committed believer in this framework.

### The mini-budget and market response

On September 23, 2022, Kwarteng delivered what he called a "Growth Plan." It contained:

- £45 billion of net tax cuts (the largest since 1972)
- No independent OBR (Office for Budget Responsibility) economic forecast or fiscal assessment
- No credible funding mechanism — the cuts were unfunded
- Abolition of the 45p top rate of income tax (later reversed)
- Removal of the bankers' bonus cap
- Energy price guarantee (separately expensive but at least seen as having a social rationale)

The absence of an OBR assessment was critical. The OBR is the UK's independent fiscal watchdog, analogous to the US Congressional Budget Office. Bypassing it sent a signal to financial markets that the government was not interested in rigorous scrutiny of its own numbers.

The market reaction was swift and severe:

- GBP/USD fell from roughly \$1.12 to \$1.035 on September 26 — a record low since decimalization and a 7% drop in days
- UK 10-year Gilt yields rose from about 3.1% to 4.5% — a 140 basis point move in roughly five days
- UK 30-year Gilt yields rose even more sharply, hitting nearly 5%
- UK mortgage rates, which are tied to Gilt yields, jumped — threatening a housing market collapse

### The LDI crisis: a hidden systemic risk surfaces

The severity of the BoE's response — a £65 billion emergency bond-buying program announced September 28 — was not just about government borrowing costs. It was about a systemic risk that had built up quietly inside UK pension funds.

*Liability-driven investing* (LDI) is a strategy pension funds use to match their long-term obligations to pensioners with long-term assets. Because pension liabilities stretch decades into the future, funds use long-dated Gilts — and often amplify their exposure through derivatives — to make their assets move in sync with their obligations. By 2022, UK pension funds had approximately £1.5 trillion in LDI strategies (Bank of England Financial Stability Report, December 2022).

When Gilt yields spike suddenly, LDI strategies face margin calls — demands for immediate cash to cover the loss in derivative value. The pension funds did not have enough liquid assets to meet these calls. They began selling Gilts to raise cash, which pushed yields higher, which triggered more margin calls, which triggered more selling — a textbook doom loop. Without the BoE's intervention, several major pension funds faced collapse within days.

The Truss episode thus demonstrated two things simultaneously: first, that markets can punish fiscal profligacy with immediate speed; second, that the financial system had hidden leverage — via LDI — that amplified the shock far beyond what a simple "bond selloff" would normally cause.

Kwarteng was fired on October 14. Truss resigned on October 20, having lasted 44 days. Her successor, Rishi Sunak, reversed the unfunded tax cuts and restored the OBR fiscal framework within days of taking office. UK Gilt yields stabilized and gradually declined.

### What the Truss episode proved — and didn't prove

It is important to be precise about what the Truss episode demonstrated. It did NOT show that governments cannot run large deficits — the US ran a deficit of 6.3% of GDP in 2023 with no such crisis. What it showed is that *credibility* and *institutional framework* matter enormously. A government seen as cavalier about fiscal math — bypassing its own watchdog, producing unfunded plans with no plausible offset — pays a severe premium in borrowing costs.

The distinction between the UK's 2022 Gilt crisis and, say, Japan running a deficit of 5-6% of GDP for decades is largely one of institutional credibility, the currency regime (Japan borrows in its own currency from domestic investors), and investor composition. The UK's Gilt market is significantly held by foreign investors who can sell quickly. That makes fiscal credibility more urgent, not less.

#### Worked example: Duration and the LDI doom loop

Let's make the LDI crisis concrete with simple numbers.

Suppose a UK pension fund holds £100 million of 30-year Gilt positions. A 30-year bond with a coupon of 2% has a duration of approximately 22 years (duration is slightly less than maturity for coupon bonds).

When yields rise by 1.5 percentage points (150 basis points, which is roughly what happened in the Truss crisis):

Price change ≈ − Duration × Δyield = −22 × 1.5% = −33%

So the £100 million position falls to roughly £67 million — a loss of £33 million.

Now suppose this fund has borrowed against its bond position using derivatives (repos and interest rate swaps), with 3:1 leverage — meaning for every £1 of actual equity, it controls £3 of bond exposure. The actual equity supporting the £100 million position is only £33 million.

A 33% loss on the whole position wipes out £33 million — exactly equal to the fund's equity. It is technically insolvent, and the counterparty (usually an investment bank) immediately demands additional collateral. The fund has to sell assets immediately to meet the margin call. The only asset it can sell quickly? More Gilts. And selling Gilts pushes yields higher, which hits other LDI funds with the same math, and the cycle continues.

This is the doom loop. The intervention of the Bank of England — buying Gilts in size — broke the loop by providing a bid when no private buyer would step in. The lesson: leverage amplifies everything, in both directions.

## The Supply-Chain Onshoring Imperative

The Truss and Biden episodes are about the political cost of inflation. But COVID-19 left another political legacy: a profound rethinking of where things are made and where they come from.

![Supply-chain onshoring timeline 2021-2024 — from chip shortage to trillion-dollar industrial policy](/imgs/blogs/post-pandemic-political-scars-inflation-supply-chains-fiscal-4.png)

### The great supply-chain awakening

In 2020 and 2021, the world discovered simultaneously that:

1. **Semiconductors are national infrastructure.** The US imported approximately 92% of its advanced microchips from Asia (primarily TSMC in Taiwan) as of 2021. When COVID disrupted production and shipping, the auto industry — which uses chips throughout — was forced to idle assembly lines. Ford estimated a \$1.5 billion hit to profits from chip shortages in 2021 alone (Ford Q1 2021 earnings call). The idea that the world's dominant military and economic power was entirely dependent on a single chip foundry 100 miles from the People's Republic of China was, in retrospect, a shocking geopolitical vulnerability.

2. **Supply chains across the board were fragile.** "Just in time" manufacturing — the post-1990s model of keeping minimal inventory and relying on reliable global logistics — collapsed under the strain of COVID. PPE shortages, drug supply shortages, and consumer goods shortages all traced back to concentrated production, primarily in China.

3. **Energy dependence is geopolitical exposure.** Europe's dependence on Russian natural gas — 40% of its gas supply in 2021 (IEA data) — turned from an economic choice into a strategic vulnerability when Russia invaded Ukraine in February 2022 and began weaponizing gas supply. European gas prices rose from roughly €25 per megawatt-hour in early 2021 to a peak of over €310 per MWh in August 2022 (TTF benchmark) — a 12-fold increase — before the EU scrambled to build LNG import infrastructure and reduce Russian exposure.

These simultaneous shocks created the political conditions for a fundamental shift in how Western governments think about industrial policy. The phrase "industrial policy" had been almost taboo in mainstream economics for decades — the conventional wisdom held that governments should not pick winners, that markets allocate resources more efficiently, and that subsidies distort trade. COVID changed this calculus overnight.

## Industrial Policy Returns: IRA, CHIPS Act, EU NZIA

The legislative response to supply-chain vulnerability was massive and historically unprecedented in its scale. Three acts stand out.

### The CHIPS and Science Act (August 2022)

Signed by President Biden on August 9, 2022, the CHIPS and Science Act allocated \$52.7 billion for domestic semiconductor manufacturing, including:

- \$39 billion in direct manufacturing subsidies for companies building or expanding chip fabs in the US
- \$13.2 billion for semiconductor research and workforce development
- A 25% investment tax credit for semiconductor manufacturers (roughly \$24 billion over 10 years, per CBO scoring)
- Guardrails preventing recipients from expanding capacity in "countries of concern" (i.e., China) for 10 years

The political logic was bipartisan: national security (don't be dependent on Taiwan for the chips in military hardware), economics (bring high-paying manufacturing jobs home), and technology competition (maintain US leadership against Chinese semiconductor ambitions). The CHIPS Act passed the Senate 64-33 and the House 243-187 — genuinely bipartisan votes.

The real-world effect: TSMC announced it would build a \$12 billion first phase of a fab in Phoenix, Arizona (later expanded to a \$65 billion commitment), Intel committed to massive Ohio and Arizona expansions, and Samsung announced a \$17 billion Texas fab. These investments would likely not have happened — or happened in this quantity, in the US — without the subsidy regime.

### The Inflation Reduction Act (August 2022)

Signed August 16, 2022, the IRA is the largest climate and clean energy legislation in US history. Its headline numbers:

- \$369 billion in climate and clean energy investment over 10 years (Senate Budget Committee scoring; later revised upward by many analysts as uptake exceeded projections)
- Production tax credits for domestic EV manufacturing, solar panels, wind turbines, and battery storage
- Requirements that vehicles and components be manufactured in North America to qualify for consumer credits (\$7,500 per EV)
- Provisions tying subsidy eligibility to domestic content requirements — explicitly designed to build US supply chains

The IRA is nominally about climate. But its structure is explicitly industrial policy: it subsidizes domestic production of clean energy technology, with supply-chain requirements designed to exclude Chinese-made components. The domestic battery requirement, for instance, disqualified most existing EVs with Chinese-sourced battery cells. It was "Buy American" dressed in green clothing.

The IRA has outperformed expectations on investment generation: private investment announcements in clean energy manufacturing topped \$270 billion in the two years following passage (BloombergNEF, 2024), with a significant portion flowing to swing states — Georgia, Michigan, Nevada — in an explicit Bidenomics political calculation.

### The EU Net-Zero Industry Act (March 2024)

The European response to the IRA was initially alarmed. European manufacturers argued that the IRA's domestic content requirements violated WTO rules and would pull investment away from Europe toward the US. After protracted negotiations, the EU produced its own response: the Net-Zero Industry Act (NZIA), proposed in April 2023 and formally adopted in March 2024.

The NZIA:

- Sets a target of 40% of EU clean energy technology demand to be manufactured domestically by 2030 (solar panels, wind turbines, heat pumps, batteries, electrolyzers, carbon capture)
- Establishes strategic net-zero manufacturing projects with fast-track permitting
- Creates a "resilience contribution" mechanism allowing public procurement to favor European suppliers
- Identifies "strategic projects" in critical raw materials processing

The NZIA is more modest than the IRA — it does not match the direct subsidy amounts — but it signals the same underlying shift: European governments accepting that market forces alone will not produce the domestic industrial capacity they deem strategically necessary.

### The structural consequence: higher costs, lower efficiency, more security

The global shift toward onshoring and "friend-shoring" (concentrating supply chains in allied countries) will make many things more expensive. Chips made in Arizona cost roughly 40-50% more than equivalent chips made in Taiwan, according to industry estimates (BCG/SIA, 2021). Domestic solar panels cost more than imported Chinese ones. The efficiency losses are real.

But the political judgment embedded in these policies is that the efficiency cost is worth paying for the security gain. Whether that judgment is correct will only be answerable over decades. What is certain is that it has locked in a new era of state-directed industrial investment, higher input costs for downstream manufacturers, and ongoing trade tensions between the US/EU and China — the country whose supply chains they are deliberately trying to circumvent.

#### Worked example: Calculating the cost of chip re-shoring

Let's make the economics of the CHIPS Act concrete.

A state-of-the-art semiconductor fab (fabrication plant) costs roughly \$20 billion to build and equip. TSMC's Arizona Phase 1 cost approximately \$12 billion for initial construction; subsequent phases are estimated at \$20-30 billion each.

Annual production cost differential (Arizona vs. Taiwan): approximately 40% higher in the US, according to TSMC's own statements (Reuters, 2023).

Suppose a chip that costs TSMC \$1.00 to produce in Taiwan costs \$1.40 to produce in Arizona. For a product like a modern smartphone that uses perhaps \$80 worth of chips, the cost differential is:

\$80 × 40% = \$32 extra per device

Across Apple's approximately 200-225 million iPhones per year (IDC estimates), that is roughly \$6-7 billion in additional chip costs annually — before considering that not all iPhone chips would be made in the US anyway.

The \$52.7 billion CHIPS Act subsidy spreads across 10 years = \$5.27 billion per year. Against industry-wide incremental costs (across not just Apple but the entire US electronics sector), this is partial compensation — a subsidy that cushions but does not fully offset the efficiency loss. The remainder is paid by consumers through higher prices, or absorbed in corporate margins.

The political bargain: American voters and taxpayers pay something like \$5-10 billion per year in higher prices and explicit subsidies to reduce dependence on Taiwan for advanced chips. Whether that is worth it depends on how one values geopolitical security versus economic efficiency — a debate that is fundamentally political, not economic.

## Structural Fiscal Deficits: The Math That Won't Close

Perhaps the most consequential legacy of the pandemic is not the political shock of inflation or the reshaping of industrial policy, but the permanent change to the fiscal arithmetic of G7 governments.

![G7 fiscal deficits as share of GDP — structural gap that never closed back to pre-COVID levels](/imgs/blogs/post-pandemic-political-scars-inflation-supply-chains-fiscal-3.png)

### The COVID fiscal surge

Governments spent on a scale not seen since World War II. The US fiscal deficit was:

- 2019: -4.6% of GDP (CBO)
- 2020: -14.9% of GDP (COVID year) — the largest peacetime deficit in US history
- 2021: -12.4% of GDP — still enormous

Similar patterns in other G7 countries: the UK ran a 2020 deficit of -12.9% of GDP; Germany, normally a fiscal hawk, ran -4.2%; France -9.0%; Italy -9.5%; Canada -11.0%.

The expectation — the hope — was that once the pandemic ended, fiscal positions would normalize back toward pre-COVID baselines. That has not happened.

### Why deficits won't close: the structural ratchet

US fiscal deficits in the post-COVID period:

- 2022: -5.5% of GDP — better than COVID peaks, but already above 2019's -4.6%
- 2023: -6.3% of GDP — worse than 2022, despite a growing economy
- 2024: -6.4% to -6.5% of GDP (CBO January 2025 baseline)

Pre-COVID, the consensus forecast was that US deficits would gradually decline from ~4.5% of GDP as the economy normalized. Instead, they have risen. Why?

**Interest costs have exploded.** The US federal government paid roughly \$659 billion in net interest on its debt in fiscal year 2023, up from \$352 billion in fiscal year 2021 (US Treasury). This is not because the debt grew dramatically in two years — it did grow, but not by a factor of two. It's because interest *rates* doubled. The average interest rate on outstanding US debt was roughly 1.7% in 2021; by 2024 it was approaching 3.2%. When you apply that to a \$27 trillion debt stock, the math is brutal: each 1 percentage point rise in average rate costs approximately \$270 billion per year in additional interest payments.

**Social spending is politically uncuttable.** Social Security, Medicare, and Medicaid together account for roughly 60% of US federal spending. These programs are politically sacred. The demographic pressure of an aging baby-boomer cohort means their costs are rising structurally regardless of the economic cycle. Medicare enrollment is growing by roughly 10,000 people per day as boomers turn 65.

**Defense spending is rising.** The post-Ukraine security environment has pushed NATO members toward the 2% of GDP defense spending target. For the US, defense spending is already higher than that, but political pressure is for more, not less. Europe is rearming. None of this is fiscally contracting.

**Discretionary cuts face gridlock.** The US political system is gridlocked on fiscal matters. Republicans broadly oppose tax increases; Democrats broadly oppose cuts to entitlements. The result is inaction, which means the baseline deficit rolls forward.

### The UK parallel

The UK's fiscal position tells a similar story. The OBR (Office for Budget Responsibility) projects:

- 2023-24 deficit: approximately 4.5% of GDP
- 2024-25 deficit: approximately 4.4% of GDP
- These compare to 2018-19 pre-COVID: approximately 2.1% of GDP

The UK faces its own structural pressures: NHS spending rising with an aging population, defense commitments increasing post-Ukraine, and debt interest costs surging as Gilt yields rise. The OBR's October 2024 Economic and Fiscal Outlook projected that the UK's fiscal position would remain constrained well into the late 2020s.

#### Worked example: The debt service treadmill

Let's trace through the arithmetic of why US deficits are self-reinforcing.

Starting position (end 2024 estimates):
- Total federal debt held by public: approximately \$27.5 trillion (US Treasury)
- Average interest rate on debt: approximately 3.2%
- Annual interest payments: \$27.5T × 3.2% = \$880 billion

Annual primary deficit (before interest): approximately \$350 billion (2024 estimate, CBO)

Total deficit = primary deficit + interest = \$350bn + \$880bn = \$1,230 billion (approximately 4.3% of an \$28T GDP)

But each year the deficit adds to the debt stock. If the 2024 deficit is \$1.23 trillion, the 2025 starting debt is \$28.73 trillion. At the same 3.2% average rate, interest costs in 2025 are approximately \$920 billion — \$40 billion higher than 2024, even with no new spending.

This is the debt service treadmill: even if Congress kept primary spending flat, rising interest costs on a growing debt pile automatically increase the deficit. To stabilize the debt-to-GDP ratio (prevent it from rising), the US would need to run a primary surplus of roughly 1-2% of GDP — meaning cut total non-interest spending by \$300-600 billion per year from current levels. That is politically inconceivable.

The trajectory: CBO's long-term projections (2024 Long-Term Budget Outlook) show the US federal debt rising from roughly 99% of GDP today to approximately 166% of GDP by 2054, under current law — before any new legislation. This is not a crisis timeline; it is a slow-moving pressure that keeps interest rates higher, crowds out private investment, and constrains every future government's policy options.

## The End of the 40-Year Bond Bull Market

Against the backdrop of structural deficits and the post-COVID inflation episode, we can now understand what happened to bonds — and why it matters so much.

![US 10-year real yield 2010-2024 — the reversal of four decades of financial repression](/imgs/blogs/post-pandemic-political-scars-inflation-supply-chains-fiscal-5.png)

### The 40-year bull market in bonds (1981-2020)

In August 1981, the US 10-year Treasury yield hit approximately 15.8% — the peak of the Volcker-era fight against 1970s inflation. Over the next 40 years, yields fell almost continuously. By August 2020, the US 10-year yield reached a record low of approximately 0.52% (FRED H.15 series). That 40-year decline in yields is one of the longest and most powerful bull markets in financial history for any asset class.

Why did it happen? Multiple structural forces:

1. **Disinflation.** After the Volcker shock broke inflation expectations in the early 1980s, inflation trended lower for four decades. Lower inflation expectations mean lower nominal interest rates.

2. **Demographics.** Aging populations in the rich world (US, Europe, Japan) drove massive demand for fixed-income assets as baby boomers saved for retirement. More demand for bonds = higher bond prices = lower yields.

3. **Globalization.** China and other emerging markets flooded global markets with cheap goods, suppressing inflation in the developed world. The "China price" effect kept goods inflation near zero for two decades.

4. **Central bank policy.** Post-2008 financial crisis, central banks in the US, Europe, and Japan pursued near-zero interest rate policy (ZIRP) and quantitative easing (QE) — buying bonds with newly created money. This artificially suppressed yields to historical lows and, by 2020, pushed US real (inflation-adjusted) yields to -1% or below.

The financial consequence of four decades of falling yields: every asset priced by discounting future cash flows became more valuable. Stocks rose (lower discount rates inflate stock multiples). Real estate rose (lower mortgage rates support higher prices). Bonds themselves rose in price (inversely related to yield). This is the central financial story of the post-Reagan era.

### The 2022 reversal: fastest rate hike cycle since 1980

In February 2022, the Federal Reserve's policy rate stood at 0.25% — effectively zero. By July 2023, it had reached 5.5% — a 525 basis point increase in 17 months. This was the fastest tightening cycle since Paul Volcker's fight against inflation in the early 1980s.

The consequences for bonds were severe:

- **Bloomberg Global Aggregate Bond Index 2022**: -16.3% — the worst calendar-year return on record for global investment-grade bonds (Bloomberg data going back to at least 1990, and arguably the worst in decades before that)
- **US Long-Duration Treasuries (TLT ETF)**: -31.2% in 2022
- **UK Gilts**: as described above, -25% or worse on long-dated issues
- **Emerging market USD bonds**: significant losses as dollar strengthened

These are not normal bond losses. A 16% loss in a single year across a supposedly "safe" asset class is a generational event. Pension funds, insurance companies, and millions of individual investors who held bonds for safety found that their "safe" assets had lost more than their equity in many cases.

### The new neutral: why real yields have re-rated

The question now is whether yields will revert to near-zero — as the bond market briefly hoped in late 2023 — or whether the post-COVID world has genuinely re-rated to a higher neutral rate.

The evidence increasingly supports the latter:

**Supply has permanently increased.** Governments are running structural deficits of 4-6% of GDP and must issue more bonds every year to finance them. More supply means lower prices = higher yields.

**Demand has decreased.** China, the world's largest foreign holder of US Treasuries, has been gradually reducing its holdings (from a peak of \$1.317 trillion in November 2013 to approximately \$770 billion by early 2024, per US Treasury TIC data). Japan, the largest single foreign holder, faces pressure to repatriate capital as its own bond yields have finally begun rising. Domestic demand in the US — from pension funds — is increasingly pre-empted by liability matching rather than speculative bond buying.

**Inflation risk premium has returned.** After a decade in which inflation never materially exceeded 2%, bond investors now price in a real risk that inflation could re-emerge. This "term premium" — the extra yield investors demand to hold long-dated bonds as compensation for uncertainty — had collapsed to near zero by 2020 and has partially recovered.

**The neutral rate has risen.** As noted above, Fed NY models estimate r-star has moved from approximately 0.5% to 1.0-1.5% real. This mechanically implies higher equilibrium bond yields: a 1.5% real neutral rate plus a 2% inflation target implies a nominal 10-year yield of perhaps 3.5-4% in steady state, compared to 0.5-1.5% in the ZIRP era.

## Asset Implications of a Higher Neutral Rate World

The shift to a structurally higher neutral rate re-prices virtually every asset class. This is not a temporary feature of a rate-hiking cycle; it is a regime change.

![Asset class performance matrix in a higher neutral rate world](/imgs/blogs/post-pandemic-political-scars-inflation-supply-chains-fiscal-7.png)

### Bonds: structurally disadvantaged

Long-duration government bonds — 10-year, 20-year, 30-year Treasuries or Gilts — face a triple headwind in the new regime:

1. **Price risk.** If yields stay at 4%+, existing bonds issued at 1-2% are worth significantly less. A 30-year Treasury issued at 1.5% coupon trades at roughly 55 cents on the dollar when prevailing yields are 4.5%.

2. **Supply pressure.** Structural deficits mean governments must issue ever-increasing quantities of bonds. More supply, all else equal, means lower prices = higher yields.

3. **Loss of diversification benefit.** For decades, bonds provided portfolio protection during equity selloffs (yields fell as investors fled to safety). In an inflationary environment, bonds and equities can fall together — as 2022 demonstrated dramatically. The diversification benefit may be weaker going forward.

### Equities: bifurcated by duration

Not all equities are equally affected by higher rates, but the impact is significant and varies by sector:

**Growth / technology equities** are inherently long-duration: most of their value lies in cash flows far in the future. When you discount future cash flows at a higher rate, their present value falls. A stock whose value is 70% "terminal value" 20+ years out is extraordinarily sensitive to the discount rate. This is why the Nasdaq fell 33% in 2022, even as the underlying businesses (Alphabet, Meta, Amazon) remained profitable — their future cash flows were worth less in a 4-5% rate environment than in a 1-2% rate environment.

**Value equities** — companies with high current cash flows relative to price, like banks, energy companies, and industrials — benefit from higher rates (banks earn more on deposits and loans) and are less sensitive to discount rate changes (their cash flows are near-term, not distant).

**Dividend-paying equities** become more competitive relative to bonds when bond yields are near zero (a 3% dividend yield was attractive when bonds yielded 0.5%), but less competitive when bond yields reach 4-5%. The calculus shifts.

### Real assets: the structural beneficiary

Real assets — physical property, commodities, infrastructure, farmland — have historically been the best inflation hedge. When inflation runs above central bank targets, real assets tend to outperform. In a world where the neutral rate is higher partly *because* inflation risk is higher, real assets should structurally outperform the ZIRP-era winners (long-duration bonds, high-multiple growth stocks).

Real estate in particular faces a transition: cap rates (the yield on a property relative to its price) were compressed to historic lows in the ZIRP era. As interest rates normalize, cap rates must also normalize — meaning property prices must fall or rents must rise, or both. The commercial real estate sector (offices especially) faces the most acute pressure, with rising vacancies (post-COVID remote work) compounding rising financing costs.

### Emerging market debt: squeezed on both sides

Emerging market governments that borrow in US dollars face a painful squeeze when US rates rise. Higher US rates:

1. Strengthen the dollar (capital flows back to US for higher rates), making dollar debt more expensive to service in local currency terms
2. Raise the absolute borrowing cost — EM sovereigns must pay a spread above US Treasuries, and if the Treasury rate rises, so does their all-in cost
3. Reduce EM growth (export-dependent EMs suffer if US demand cools from rate hikes)

This dynamic — sometimes called the "dollar wrecking ball" — has historically triggered EM crises when the Fed hikes aggressively. The 1980s Latin American debt crisis, the 1997 Asian financial crisis, and the 2013 "taper tantrum" all have this basic structure.

#### Worked example: How the discount rate kills a growth stock's valuation

Let's make the growth-stock duration sensitivity concrete.

Consider a hypothetical growth company: it earns essentially nothing today but is expected to grow to generate \$100 of free cash flow per share in year 10, with continued growth thereafter. A very simplified "terminal value" model values the stock as:

Price = \[Terminal Cash Flow\] ÷ (Discount Rate − Growth Rate)

Suppose the expected long-run growth rate is 3%, and cash flow at year 10 is \$100.

**In the ZIRP world (2021):** Discount rate = 1.5% real + 2% inflation = 3.5% nominal. But add a 1% equity risk premium = 4.5% total.

Price = \$100 ÷ (4.5% − 3%) = \$100 ÷ 1.5% = \$6,667 per share

**In the post-COVID world (2023):** Discount rate = 2% real + 2.5% inflation = 4.5% nominal. Add 1.5% equity risk premium = 6% total.

Price = \$100 ÷ (6% − 3%) = \$100 ÷ 3% = \$3,333 per share

A move in the discount rate from 4.5% to 6% — less than 2 percentage points — cuts the theoretical value of this stock in half. The underlying business has not changed at all. The cash flows are identical. Only the interest rate environment changed.

This is precisely what happened to high-multiple technology stocks in 2022: Peloton fell 90%, Zoom fell 75%, the ARK Innovation ETF fell 75%. The businesses were not catastrophically impaired; their valuations, built on ZIRP-era discount rates, were.

#### Worked example: Fiscal math and the bond market's patience

Here is the math that keeps fiscal policymakers awake at night.

The US debt-to-GDP ratio was approximately 97% at end-2023. Annual GDP growth (nominal, including inflation) is approximately 5% in recent years. To stabilize the debt-to-GDP ratio — prevent it from growing as a percentage of the economy — the government needs to run a primary surplus (before interest) of:

Primary balance needed = (r − g) × Debt/GDP

Where r = interest rate on debt, g = nominal GDP growth rate, Debt/GDP = the ratio.

If r = 3.5% (the average interest rate on all outstanding US debt drifting toward current market rates) and g = 4.5% (nominal growth), then:

Primary balance needed = (3.5% − 4.5%) × 97% = −0.97% of GDP

A negative primary deficit needed means that if the economy grows faster than the interest rate, the debt ratio stabilizes even with a small primary deficit. This is the "r less than g" condition that debt optimists emphasize — and it is genuinely important.

But if r rises to 4.5% and g slows to 3.5% (as might happen in a slowdown):

Primary balance needed = (4.5% − 3.5%) × 97% = +0.97% of GDP

Suddenly you need a *primary surplus* of nearly 1% of GDP. In practice, the US is currently running a primary deficit of roughly 1.5-2% of GDP. The gap between where it is and where it needs to be is 2.5-3% of GDP — roughly \$700-850 billion in annual fiscal adjustment, which would require either massive tax increases, spending cuts, or both.

The historical precedent: outside wartime, it is extraordinarily rare for democracies to achieve fiscal consolidations of this magnitude. The US achieved something like it in the Clinton era (1993-2001), but only due to the tech boom generating windfall tax revenues — not deliberate austerity. Without a comparable boom, the math suggests that US debt-to-GDP will continue rising, which means bond supply will continue rising, which maintains upward pressure on yields.

## Common Misconceptions

### Misconception 1: "Inflation was caused by government spending, so when spending normalizes, inflation returns to 2%"

This is partially true but incomplete. Pandemic spending was a significant demand-side driver of inflation. But supply-chain disruptions, energy price shocks (Ukraine war), and labor market structural shifts also contributed. Some of these supply-side factors have normalized (shipping costs, goods prices), which is why inflation has fallen from 9.1% to the 3-4% range by 2023-2024. But "last-mile" inflation in services — driven by wage growth and housing costs — is stickier. The transition from 4% CPI to 2% CPI is proving much harder than the transition from 9% to 4%, because the remaining inflation is structural, not supply-chain driven.

### Misconception 2: "The US government can't go bankrupt because it prints its own currency"

Technically true for the US, which borrows in dollars and controls dollar creation. But the version of this that causes real-world problems is not default — it's inflation. If a government relies on central bank money creation to finance deficits (called "monetization" or "fiscal dominance"), the result is currency debasement and inflation, not nominal default. This is what happened in the UK and many other countries historically, and it is what bond markets fear when they see large unfunded fiscal expansions. The Truss crisis was not about literal default; it was about the market pricing in inflation risk from an unanchored fiscal stance.

### Misconception 3: "Higher rates always hurt stocks"

Rate rises are bad for high-multiple, long-duration growth stocks. They are often *good* for financial stocks (banks profit from higher net interest margins), energy stocks (commodity prices often rise in inflationary periods), and industrials (onshoring and infrastructure spending are rate-resilient). The 2022 performance dispersion — energy and financials up, growth tech down 30-70% — demonstrates this precisely. Blanket "rates up = sell stocks" is too crude.

### Misconception 4: "Bond losses in 2022 were temporary; bonds always come back"

Individual bonds, held to maturity, return par value (the face amount). But the *price* losses in 2022 are permanent for investors who sold. More importantly, the opportunity cost is real: money locked in a 1.5% coupon bond while prevailing rates are 4-5% earns 2.5-3.5% less than it could. Duration losses "heal" only slowly over years. The notion that bond investors "just wait" ignores the real economic cost of being locked into below-market rates and the possibility that rates stay elevated for longer than expected.

### Misconception 5: "Industrial policy will fail — governments can't pick winners"

The historical record is more nuanced than either side claims. South Korea's industrial policy (POSCO steel, Samsung, Hyundai) and Taiwan's semiconductor promotion (TSMC itself was born from government support) produced world-class companies. The US DARPA effectively invented the internet. But European industrial policy in the 1970s-80s did produce expensive failures. The current wave of IRA/CHIPS/NZIA industrial policy differs from past failures in important ways: it subsidizes private investment rather than building state-owned enterprises, uses competitive procurement, and focuses on sectors where market failure (the externalities of security and decarbonization) is most acute.

### Misconception 6: "The Truss crisis proves that austerity is always correct"

The Truss crisis proved that *unfunded* fiscal expansion — tax cuts with no credible financing — can be punished by markets immediately. It did not prove that all spending is dangerous. The BoE's emergency bond buying (itself a form of fiscal accommodation) was necessary to prevent systemic collapse. The lesson is about credibility and process — having an independent fiscal watchdog (like the OBR or CBO), presenting credible medium-term plans, and not making large permanent fiscal commitments without financing. Not that governments must always run surpluses.

## How It Shows Up in Real Markets

### The mortgage market repricing (2022-2023)

The most direct transmission of the Fed's rate hikes to ordinary Americans was through the mortgage market. The US 30-year fixed mortgage rate rose from approximately 3.1% in January 2022 to 8.0% by October 2023 (Freddie Mac Primary Mortgage Market Survey) — more than doubling in less than two years.

The payment on a \$400,000 mortgage at 3.1%: approximately \$1,710 per month. At 8.0%: approximately \$2,935 per month — 72% higher. Existing homeowners with locked-in low-rate mortgages were reluctant to sell and give up their cheap financing ("the lock-in effect"), reducing housing supply. New buyers were squeezed out by affordability. The result: housing market volumes collapsed even as house prices stayed stubbornly elevated, creating a bizarre market in which prices and activity diverged dramatically.

This is a direct, household-level consequence of the post-pandemic rate regime. Every central bank action to fight inflation had an immediate transmission into the cost of shelter, credit card debt, auto loans, and business investment.

### Japan's yield curve control unwind (2022-2023)

Japan provides a fascinating counter-example and a stress test of the "higher for longer" thesis. The Bank of Japan maintained its *yield curve control* (YCC) policy — targeting a 0% yield on 10-year Japanese government bonds — long after the rest of the world had begun hiking. This meant Japan was an outlier: in a world of 4-5% bond yields, Japanese bonds yielded 0% (or just slightly above).

The market consequences: the yen fell from approximately 115 per dollar at the start of 2022 to 151 in October 2022 (a 32-year low), as capital flowed out of Japan toward higher-yielding dollar assets. Japanese authorities intervened in FX markets spending approximately ¥9 trillion (roughly \$65 billion) to defend the yen.

In December 2022, the BoJ widened its YCC band — a de facto acknowledgment that the 0% target was unsustainable. In July 2023, it widened further and effectively abandoned YCC. In March 2024, the BoJ finally raised its policy rate above zero for the first time since 2016. Each of these steps caused significant ripples in global bond and FX markets, because Japan's \$1 trillion+ of foreign bond holdings (accumulated during the ZIRP era) became subject to potential repatriation.

The lesson: no major central bank can indefinitely hold rates near zero when the rest of the world has normalized to 4-5%. The global rate re-rating eventually pulls every major economy toward higher neutral rates.

### The commercial real estate reckoning (2023-2024)

The intersection of higher rates and post-COVID work-from-home adoption created a slow-motion crisis in commercial real estate, particularly office buildings in major US cities. Office vacancy rates reached 19-22% in markets like San Francisco, Chicago, and New York (CBRE, 2024) — historically unprecedented for Class A space.

Commercial real estate is financed with significant leverage. A building bought in 2019 at a 4% cap rate with debt at 2.5% was a profitable investment. The same building, refinancing in 2023 at a 6% debt rate, with occupancy fallen and the cap rate now at 6%, is worth roughly half what it was — and the debt service is above net operating income. This is the commercial real estate doom loop: rising rates + falling values + maturing debt creates forced sellers and potential credit losses for the regional banks that financed these buildings.

US banks held approximately \$2.7 trillion in commercial real estate loans at end-2023 (Federal Reserve H.8 data), with smaller regional banks disproportionately concentrated in this sector. The stress is slow-moving — loans mature over 3-5 years, not overnight — but the cumulative effect of refinancing at 200-300% higher interest rates will shape bank earnings and credit conditions through the late 2020s.

### Sovereign spread widening in Europe (2023-2024)

Italy provides a microcosm of sovereign fiscal stress in the higher-rate environment. Italy's debt-to-GDP ratio exceeded 140% in 2023 — among the highest in the eurozone. With bond yields rising (Italian 10-year BTP yields moved from approximately 0.7% in 2021 to 4.3% by 2023), the Italian government's interest costs rose sharply. The spread between Italian and German 10-year bonds (the "BTP-Bund spread") — a standard measure of Italian fiscal risk — widened from about 100 basis points in 2021 to over 200 in periods of stress.

The ECB's anti-fragmentation tool (the Transmission Protection Instrument, or TPI) — committed to purchasing bonds of member states facing "unjustified" spread widening — served as a backstop. But the backstop is conditional on the borrowing country meeting fiscal criteria, which creates a political constraint: Italy must maintain fiscal discipline or lose ECB support.

This is the eurozone version of the Truss dynamic: bond market discipline enforced through the spread, with the ECB playing the role of conditional lender of last resort. The higher the neutral rate, the more expensive the entire sovereign debt stock becomes to service, and the narrower the margin for fiscal error.

## How to Trade It: The Playbook

Understanding the structural post-pandemic environment translates into a set of portfolio positions that reflect the new regime rather than the old one. This is educational framing — not individual investment advice.

### Bond positioning: stay short duration

In a world where the direction of rates is uncertain but the long-run equilibrium is higher than the ZIRP era, extending duration (buying 20-30 year bonds) is a position that requires high conviction. Most institutional investors have reduced duration benchmarks and tilted toward shorter-maturity bonds (2-5 year) or floating-rate instruments. Cash itself, earning 4-5% in money market funds, became competitive for the first time since 2007.

The active debate: when does the Fed cut enough that long bonds become attractive again? The market spent much of 2023-2024 pricing in rate cuts that did not materialize, because inflation proved stickier than expected. Duration extension is a bet on disinflation; the post-pandemic regime suggests that bet is harder to win than it was in 2010-2020.

### Industrial policy beneficiaries: real picks, not abstract themes

The IRA and CHIPS Act represent fiscal commitments that are largely locked in regardless of who wins elections — the spending is already flowing and the factories are under construction. The investment follows the money: semiconductor equipment companies (ASML, Applied Materials, KLA Corporation), domestic battery manufacturers, EV charging infrastructure, nuclear power operators (given the IRA's extension of nuclear tax credits), and utility-scale clean energy developers in the US.

The nuance: the IRA has proven more generous than initially scored, because the credits are "direct pay" — uncapped, available to anyone who qualifies, not subject to annual appropriations. This makes the subsidy stream more durable politically than a line-item grant.

### Inflation protection: TIPS, commodities, real estate selectively

In a world where the inflation risk premium is higher, Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS) and commodities deserve a larger structural weight than they had in the ZIRP era. TIPS are US government bonds whose principal adjusts with CPI — if inflation rises, you get paid more. Real yields on TIPS were negative for much of 2020-2021 (investors paying to hold inflation protection); by 2023-2024 they turned positive (2%+ real yields on 10-year TIPS), making them genuinely attractive for the first time in years.

#### Worked example: TIPS vs nominal Treasuries in a higher-inflation world

An investor faces a choice in January 2024:

Option A: 10-year nominal US Treasury at 4.2% yield
Option B: 10-year TIPS at 2.1% real yield

The "breakeven inflation rate" — the inflation rate at which the two bonds deliver the same return — is:

4.2% − 2.1% = 2.1%

If actual average CPI over the next 10 years is above 2.1%, TIPS outperform. If it's below, nominal Treasuries win.

In January 2024, actual CPI was running at approximately 3.1%. If you believe structural forces (fiscal deficits, reshoring costs, energy transition) will keep inflation above 2.1% for the next decade, TIPS are the better bet at these prices. If you believe the Fed will successfully return inflation to 2%, nominal Treasuries are better.

The interesting insight: the breakeven at 2.1% implies markets believe the Fed will hit its 2% target — but the structural post-COVID fiscal and supply-chain arguments suggest the balance of risk is to the upside on inflation, making TIPS relatively attractive.
