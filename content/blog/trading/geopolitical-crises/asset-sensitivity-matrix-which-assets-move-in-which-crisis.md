> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Geopolitical crises do not uniformly hurt all assets; each crisis type activates a specific transmission channel, and the direction of each asset depends entirely on which channel fires.
>
> - Armed conflict is the only crisis type that simultaneously moves gold up, oil up, equities down, and US Treasuries up — because it triggers all three channels at once: risk-off fear, supply-shock repricing, and safe-haven flight.
> - A trade war hits equities and EM currencies but actually strengthens the USD — the opposite of what most beginners expect.
> - Pure risk-off crises send US Treasuries sharply higher; supply-shock events (like Ukraine 2022) flatten or even push bond prices down because inflation fears compete with safety demand.
> - Duration is as important as direction: gold spikes on any fear within days, but oil repricing from a war can persist for years if supply chains are permanently rerouted.
> - Coup or regime change in a small country stays local — Turkish assets tanked in July 2016 but global markets barely noticed; quantifying that local-vs-global differential is one of the most profitable things a geopolitical trader learns.

The matrix above is the reference tool this post will build from the ground up. By the time you finish reading, you will be able to look at a breaking news headline — "conflict escalates near oil chokepoint", "new sanctions package announced", "coup underway in capital city" — and immediately identify which assets are likely to move, in which direction, by roughly how much, and for how long. That is the toolkit of a professional macro trader. Let us build it together.

## Foundations: What Moves Asset Prices and Why

Before we can build the sensitivity matrix, we need to understand the basic plumbing. Why does bad geopolitical news move markets at all? And why does the same headline move different assets in different directions?

### The Four Things That Make Asset Prices Move

An asset's price is, at its core, a compressed estimate of future cash flows discounted for risk and time. When a geopolitical event happens, it can disturb one or more of four things:

1. **Expected cash flows** — Will this company still earn revenue? Will this government still be able to pay its debts?
2. **Discount rates** — Are interest rates about to change? Is the risk premium investors demand going up?
3. **Supply and demand for the underlying commodity** — Is a key shipping lane about to close? Is a major wheat exporter going offline?
4. **Liquidity and capital flows** — Are investors fleeing a region entirely? Is money moving from risky assets to safe ones?

A *risk-off event* is one that primarily disturbs numbers 1, 2, and 4 — it does not touch physical supply, it just scares investors into demanding higher compensation for risk. A *supply-shock event* disturbs number 3 directly. The crucial insight is: **these two types affect bonds in opposite directions.**

### The Seven Asset Classes in Our Matrix

Let us define the assets we track, because each behaves according to a different logic:

**Global equities** — shares of companies worldwide. Their price falls when future earnings look worse, when discount rates rise, or when investors demand higher risk premiums. They are the most *risk-sensitive* asset class: even a small increase in perceived uncertainty hits them hard.

**US Treasuries** — bonds issued by the US government. These are considered the safest asset on Earth. When investors are scared, they *buy* Treasuries, which pushes prices up and yields (the interest rate) down. "US 10-year yield falls 20 basis points" means "people bought so many Treasuries in a panic that the implied interest rate dropped by 0.20%."

**Gold** — the original safe haven. Gold pays no coupon, produces no cash flow, and has limited industrial use. Its price is almost entirely a function of how anxious the world is. When people distrust paper currencies or fear institutional collapse, they buy gold. Gold is sensitive to both real interest rates (the opportunity cost of holding it) and geopolitical fear.

**Oil/commodities** — physical raw materials. Oil is the most geopolitically sensitive commodity: roughly one-third of the world's traded oil passes through a small number of chokepoints (Strait of Hormuz, Suez Canal, Bosporus) that can be disrupted by conflict. Wheat, natural gas, and rare earth metals follow similar logic.

**USD (US dollar)** — the world's reserve currency. In a crisis, the dollar typically strengthens because global trade and debt is denominated in dollars, and scared investors *rush to hold them*. But this is not always true: in trade wars initiated by the US, the dollar can weaken if investors price in long-term US economic damage.

**EM FX (emerging market foreign exchange)** — currencies of developing nations like Brazil, Turkey, South Africa, Indonesia. These are the most crisis-vulnerable currencies. When global risk appetite falls, investors pull capital out of high-yielding EM assets and repatriate to safety, selling EM currencies in the process.

**EM equities (MSCI Emerging Markets)** — stocks in developing countries. They get hit by a double whammy in crises: the businesses themselves may be affected, AND foreign investors simultaneously sell to reduce risk, amplifying the decline.

### The Three Transmission Channels

Every geopolitical event reaches asset markets through one or more of three channels. Understanding which channel fires determines the asset direction.

**Channel 1: Risk-Off Fear** — The event scares investors, who sell risky assets (equities, EM FX, high-yield bonds) and buy safe ones (US Treasuries, gold, USD, Japanese yen, Swiss franc). This channel fires in almost every crisis, but it fires *most purely* in events that have limited physical economic effects — coups in small countries, assassinations, terrorism incidents, pandemic lockdowns.

**Channel 2: Supply-Shock Repricing** — The event physically disrupts the production or transportation of a commodity. A war near oil fields, a blockade of a grain export corridor, or sanctions on a major commodity exporter fires this channel. Oil, natural gas, food prices, and rare earth metals move up. This channel can *complicate* the bond picture because higher commodity prices mean higher inflation, which makes investors demand higher yields from bonds rather than lower ones.

**Channel 3: Safe-Haven Capital Flight** — Money flows to specific jurisdictions or assets perceived as the world's ultimate backstop. The US dollar strengthens. Swiss franc strengthens. Japanese yen strengthens. US Treasuries rally. Gold rallies. This channel often fires alongside Channel 1 but is distinct: it is not just about selling risky assets, it is about buying specific safe ones.

## The Six Crisis Types: Each Fires Different Channels

Now let us define the six crisis types in our matrix and explain which channels each activates.

### 1. Armed Conflict and War

Armed conflict is the most complex geopolitical event for markets because it fires all three channels simultaneously. A major war:

- Creates **risk-off fear** (Channel 1) — investors immediately reprice uncertainty higher, selling equities and EM assets.
- Often creates **supply-shock** (Channel 2) — wars disrupt agricultural land, energy infrastructure, shipping routes, and manufacturing.
- Triggers **safe-haven flight** (Channel 3) — everyone buys US Treasuries, gold, and USD.

This is why Russia-Ukraine 2022 produced the unusual combination of equities down, bonds up, gold up, AND oil up simultaneously. The supply-shock channel overwhelmed the safe-haven bond argument for European bonds (which had their own inflation to worry about), but US Treasuries still held their safe-haven character.

The geographic scope determines magnitude. A war in Eastern Europe hits European assets much harder than US assets. A conflict near the Strait of Hormuz (through which about 21% of global oil flows) is a *global* shock from day one.

### 2. Economic Sanctions

Sanctions are financial weapons: they cut a country off from the global dollar system, freeze assets, restrict trade, and impose costs on whoever complies and whoever does not. Sanctions fire Channels 1 and 3 but have a more *targeted* supply shock effect depending on what the sanctioned country exports.

When major Russian banks were excluded from SWIFT in February 2022, it created immediate FX volatility (the ruble collapsed, then partly recovered), uncertainty in energy markets (Russia is a top-3 oil and gas producer), and capital-flow disruption across EM. But the effect on US equities was more muted than a direct military event — investors quickly assessed "Russia's economy is ~1.7% of world GDP; this is manageable."

Sanctions that target a major commodity exporter (Iran, Russia) have a secondary supply-shock character. Sanctions on a financial system (Venezuela, North Korea, Belarus) primarily hit the local currency and credit markets without major global commodity effects.

The *pace of sanctions escalation* also matters for asset pricing. When sanctions are announced gradually — a few banks added to the list each month, sector restrictions introduced one at a time — markets absorb each step individually. The repricing is continuous rather than sudden. When sanctions are announced as a *comprehensive package* (as happened with Russia in February 2022, when SWIFT exclusion, asset freezes, and export controls were announced nearly simultaneously), the market reaction is a step-function: a large immediate reprice followed by stabilization as the full scope becomes clear.

### 3. Trade Wars and Tariffs

Trade wars are fundamentally different from all other crisis types because they are *bilateral weapons* — each side retaliates, and the damage accumulates through global supply chains. A tariff announcement fires Channel 1 (risk-off for affected industries) but does NOT fire Channel 3 cleanly.

The most important distinguishing feature: **trade wars strengthen the imposing country's currency (USD) while weakening the target currency (CNH, MXN)**. This is the opposite of what you might expect. The logic: tariffs restrict imports, which means *less foreign currency is needed to pay for goods*, which mechanically reduces the current-account deficit and supports the dollar. Meanwhile, the target country's currency weakens as capital flows anticipate economic damage.

Trade wars also hit commodities differently than wars. Tariffs on agricultural goods (soybeans, wheat, pork) depress those commodity prices *for the country being targeted*, because the tariff redirects the supply chain. When the US imposed tariffs on Chinese goods in 2018-2019, US soybean futures *fell* (China stopped buying American soybeans and switched to Brazilian ones) — exactly the opposite of a supply-disruption war event where agricultural prices rise.

### 4. Coup and Regime Change

A coup or sudden regime change is almost entirely a *local shock*. It fires Channel 1 strongly for local assets but barely nudges global markets unless the country is a major commodity producer or has systemic financial ties.

The logic is simple: a coup in Turkey affects Turkish assets (lira, Istanbul stock exchange) dramatically but has minimal effect on German DAX or US S&P 500. Global investors treat it as a local repricing event, check their Turkey exposure, and move on.

The exception is when the country in question controls a critical resource or chokepoint. A coup in Saudi Arabia would immediately fire the global supply-shock channel. A coup in Venezuela (a major oil producer in crisis) creates uncertainty about oil supply. A coup in the Democratic Republic of Congo affects cobalt supply chains.

### 5. Sovereign Debt Crisis

A sovereign debt crisis — when a country cannot pay its debts and must negotiate with creditors — is a highly *contagious* event that spreads through financial linkages rather than physical ones. It fires Channel 1 strongly for EM assets and can fire a reverse-safe-haven effect on the country's own bonds (yields surge, prices collapse).

What makes sovereign debt crises different from the other types: they typically *unfold over months or years*, not days. Greece's yield crisis played out from 2010 to 2015. Argentina defaulted and restructured across 2019-2020. Turkey's lira crisis was a multi-year slow burn. This gives traders time to position, but also means the risk-off signal is "noisy" — there are multiple false recoveries before the final resolution.

Contagion is the key variable: a small country defaulting (Jamaica, Belize) barely registers globally. When a medium-sized economy like Argentina or Greece wobbles, it tests whether larger neighbors are similarly exposed. The 2010-2012 Eurozone crisis started with Greece but quickly raised questions about Portugal, Italy, Ireland, and Spain — hence the "PIIGS" acronym that defined those years.

### 6. Pandemic and Black Swan Events

Pandemics and other sudden systemic shocks (a major financial institution collapse, a catastrophic accident with global reach) are the *purest* risk-off events. They fire Channel 1 and Channel 3 simultaneously but create a *demand-destruction* supply shock rather than a supply-disruption one — the economy shrinks because people stop moving, buying, and consuming.

This is why COVID-19 March 2020 produced oil prices crashing to near zero (demand destruction, not supply disruption) while gold first fell in the liquidity squeeze and then soared as central banks injected trillions. The initial gold sell-off — gold dropped about 5% in the first two weeks of the COVID crash — confused many investors. We will explain that "liquidity paradox" in the worked examples below.

## Reading the Matrix Row by Row

With the six crisis types defined and the three channels understood, let us now walk through the sensitivity matrix systematically.

### Why Equities Fall in Almost Every Crisis

Global equities fall in five of the six crisis types in our matrix. The only real question is *magnitude* and *duration*. The mechanism is simple: uncertainty about future earnings goes up, the risk premium investors demand goes up, and the present value of those future earnings falls.

In a pandemic, equities can fall 30-35% because the earnings shock is enormous and broad-based. In a coup in a small country, global equities might fall less than 1% because the earnings impact on most companies is negligible. The *magnitude* maps directly to the economic exposure of global corporates to the crisis event.

The one partial exception: **trade wars hit specific sectors asymmetrically**. Airlines, logistics, technology hardware, and consumer electronics fall heavily. Defense contractors and domestic-market businesses often *rise* on tariff protection narratives. This makes aggregate equity indices misleading — a -3% move in the S&P 500 hides a -12% move in tech and a +8% move in defense.

### Why US Treasuries Don't Always Rally

This is the most important nuance in the entire matrix. The conventional wisdom is "in a crisis, sell stocks, buy bonds." That works in *pure risk-off* events. It breaks down in *supply-shock* events.

When armed conflict disrupts energy supply, commodity prices spike. Higher commodity prices mean higher consumer inflation. Higher expected inflation means bond investors demand higher yields (lower bond prices) to compensate for the erosion of their fixed payments. The safe-haven demand for Treasuries and the inflation-driven selling of Treasuries fight each other — and the net result depends on whether the war is geographically contained or a global supply-shock event.

In February-March 2022, US 10-year Treasury yields actually *rose* (bond prices fell) during the worst weeks of the Russia-Ukraine escalation, because markets were pricing Brent crude at \$120/barrel and CPI already running hot. The safe-haven bid was overwhelmed by inflation expectations.

Compare that to COVID March 2020 — a pure demand-destruction shock with *no commodity price pressure* — where the 10-year Treasury yield fell to a record low of 0.51%. Classic pure risk-off: equities crashed, bonds soared.

![Bond price behavior flips between pure risk-off crises and supply-shock events](/imgs/blogs/asset-sensitivity-matrix-which-assets-move-in-which-crisis-4.png)

### Gold's Unusual Behavior

Gold rallies in armed conflict, sanctions, and pandemics (eventually), but stays relatively flat in trade wars and sovereign debt crises. The pattern reveals gold's true character: it is a *currency debasement and existential fear* hedge, not a general risk hedge.

In trade wars, no one fears currency debasement — they fear slower growth. Gold does not react much to "slower growth" because gold does not pay anything anyway. Growth slowdown does not make gold relatively more attractive.

In sovereign debt crises, the local currency of the defaulting country collapses, but the dollar and yen stay stable — so the *global* gold price in dollars does not necessarily spike. Local investors in the defaulting country desperately want gold, but that is a small global signal.

The COVID-19 gold pattern is the most educational: gold initially fell in March 2020 (liquidity crunch — everyone was selling everything for cash, including gold), then rallied +25% over the year as central banks printed unprecedented stimulus. That sequence — initial sell-off then multi-month rally — is the "liquidity paradox" that appears in every major panic.

## The Risk-Off vs. Supply-Shock Distinction in Practice

This is the central analytical framework a practitioner uses to classify a new crisis event in real time.

**Pure risk-off checklist:**
- Is this primarily a financial or political fear event with limited physical supply disruption?
- Are commodity prices *falling* or flat (demand destruction)?
- Is the event geographically remote from major commodity chokepoints?
- Is the Fed/central bank likely to *ease* in response (cutting rates, adding liquidity)?

If yes to most of these: buy Treasuries and gold. Sell equities and EM FX. The dollar strengthens but not dramatically.

**Supply-shock checklist:**
- Does the event physically disrupt production, transportation, or export of a major commodity?
- Are commodity prices *rising* sharply?
- Is the affected region a major energy or agricultural exporter?
- Does it raise *inflation expectations*, not just risk premiums?

If yes to most of these: sell equities. Buy oil and commodity-linked assets. Buy gold. Be *cautious* on Treasuries — the inflation vs. safety dynamic makes them unpredictable. The dollar often strengthens on safety but may face inflation pressure long-term.

## Worked Examples: Four Crises Through the Matrix

Now let us apply the matrix to four real historical events with real numbers.

#### Worked example:

**Russia-Ukraine 2022: The Pure Multi-Channel Event**

On February 24, 2022, Russian forces crossed the Ukrainian border at dawn. Within the next three weeks, every major asset class moved — in directions that followed the framework perfectly.

**Brent crude** was trading at \$96.40/barrel on invasion day. Three weeks later it was at \$110.10/barrel — a gain of \$13.70 per barrel, or +14.2%. By the early June peak, it reached \$120/barrel, up +24.5% from invasion day. The supply-shock channel fired immediately: Russia produces roughly 10 million barrels per day (about 10% of global supply), and sanctions created uncertainty about how much would reach market.

**Wheat futures** were at 790 cents per bushel (CBOT) on invasion day. One week later: 1,013 cents — up +28.2%. The reason: Ukraine exports roughly 13% of global wheat and 15% of global corn. Russian forces quickly moved to block Black Sea ports. Global food supply suddenly had a 13% hole, and prices adjusted instantly.

**Gold** moved from \$1,898/oz on invasion day to \$1,988/oz one week later — up +4.7%. Safe-haven channel: gold bought you insurance against the scenario where the war escalated into broader NATO-Russia conflict.

**EUR/USD** moved from 1.1320 to 1.0970 one week after invasion — a drop of 3.1%. The logic: Europe is far more exposed to Russian energy than the US, so European assets repriced lower relative to US dollar assets.

**MSCI Russia** fell from around 1,390 (in USD terms) to approximately 210 within a week — an 84.9% collapse — before the Moscow Exchange halted trading entirely. The ruble fell from 79.3 RUB/USD to 116.0 RUB/USD (a 46% devaluation), then recovered to 83.5 by April as Russia imposed capital controls and demanded ruble payments for gas. Russia's \$300 billion in foreign reserves were frozen by G7 actions within days.

The key insight: **all three channels fired simultaneously** — risk-off (equities and EM FX down), supply-shock (oil and wheat up), and safe-haven (gold and USD up). This is why the matrix for "Armed Conflict" looks the way it does: it is the only crisis type with simultaneously green cells for both gold AND oil, plus red cells for equities AND EM FX.

The math for a practical position: if a portfolio had \$100,000 long Brent crude futures at \$96.40 entry, the three-week gain to \$110.10 = \$13.70/bbl × (lots size in bbls). A single Brent futures contract represents 1,000 barrels. That \$13.70 move = \$13,700 profit per contract. This is how commodity traders made exceptional returns in Q1 2022 — the matrix told them the direction; the magnitude was extraordinarily large.

#### Worked example:

**Trade War 2018-2019: A Different Kind of Crisis**

On May 5, 2019, President Trump announced via Twitter that tariffs on \$200 billion of Chinese goods would rise from 10% to 25% effective May 10. Let us trace the next 48 hours through the matrix.

**CNH (offshore Chinese yuan)** moved from 6.75 to 6.83 CNH per USD within two trading days — a weakening of 1.2%. The logic: tariffs raise the cost of Chinese exports to the US, which means fewer USD will flow into China to pay for goods, weakening the CNY. Markets immediately priced in the trade-flow effect.

**Soybean futures** fell from around \$8.50 per bushel to \$8.20 — a drop of 3.5% within the week. This is the *reverse* of what war does to agricultural prices. Why? China is the world's largest buyer of US soybeans. When tariffs make US soybeans expensive for China, China switches to Brazilian soybeans. US soybean farmers lose their biggest customer. The supply is now redirected, not destroyed — so prices fall for US farmers.

**MSCI EM** fell approximately 4.2% in the five days following the May 5 announcement. The logic: EM countries supply components to Chinese factories that export to the US. A trade war hits the entire EM supply chain.

**DXY (USD index)** rose from about 97.5 to 98.3 — a gain of 0.8%. Counterintuitive to many: the country initiating the trade war saw its currency *strengthen*, not weaken, in the short run. The current-account logic (fewer imports = less dollar outflow = dollar strengthens) dominated.

Now compare this directly to the Russia-Ukraine oil move: Brent went *up* on war (supply shock); US soybeans went *down* on trade war (demand redirection). Same "bad geopolitical news." Completely opposite commodity direction. The matrix distinguishes them correctly.

The full math on USD strengthening: the US imported roughly \$540 billion of goods from China in 2018. A 25% tariff on \$200 billion of that represents potentially \$50 billion less in annual import spending over time. Less import spending = fewer dollars leaving the US economy to pay for Chinese goods = mechanical dollar support. That \$50 billion annual effect works out to roughly \$4 billion per month, a meaningful flow relative to daily FX market turnover.

#### Worked example:

**COVID-19 March 2020: Pure Risk-Off with the Gold Liquidity Paradox**

On March 11, 2020, the WHO declared COVID-19 a global pandemic. By March 23 — just 12 days later — the S&P 500 had fallen from its February 19 peak of 3,386 to an intraday low of 2,192. That is a peak-to-trough decline of **-34.3%** in 33 calendar days — the fastest 34% decline in market history.

Let us trace gold specifically, because its behavior surprised many investors:

- **February 19 (S&P peak)**: Gold at approximately \$1,582/oz
- **March 9 (first sell-off)**: Gold at \$1,673/oz — gold initially RALLIED +5.8% as stocks fell. Safe-haven buying.
- **March 16 (panic selling)**: Gold fell sharply to \$1,477/oz. From its March 9 high, gold fell -11.7% in one week *while stocks were also falling*.
- **March 23 (S&P bottom)**: Gold at approximately \$1,483/oz. Still below its early-March level.
- **August 2020**: Gold hit a record \$2,075/oz — up +40% from the March low. Safe-haven rally fully played out.

Why did gold *fall initially when stocks fell*? This is the **liquidity paradox**, and it matters enormously for crisis positioning.

When a panic is severe enough, institutional investors face **margin calls** — they need *cash*, not gold. A hedge fund that bought gold as protection might actually *sell* gold to raise the cash needed to meet margin calls on losing equity positions. Gold becomes a funding source, not a safe haven, in the first 5-10 days of a liquidity crisis. Only after the Fed emergency actions (March 15: cut rates to zero; March 23: unlimited QE announced) did the *inflation/debasement* narrative kick in, and gold rallied +40% from trough to peak in five months.

The math: if you had bought \$10,000 of gold on March 9 at \$1,673, you watched it fall to \$1,477 by March 16 — a paper loss of \$1,172 (11.7%). If you had sold at the low, you locked in a loss. If you held, your \$10,000 was worth \$14,000 by August (+40%). The lesson: gold's liquidity dip in a crisis is a *buying opportunity* if the central bank will ultimately respond with monetary expansion.

For US Treasuries in COVID: the 10-year Treasury yield fell from 1.59% on February 19 to a record low of 0.51% on March 9, then briefly spiked back to 1.27% on March 18 (the liquidity squeeze in Treasuries too), then fell again to 0.62% by April as Fed buying dominated. This confirmed the pure risk-off character: bonds rallied strongly over the full event despite brief liquidity-driven volatility.

#### Worked example:

**Turkey Coup Attempt July 15-16, 2016: Quantifying Local vs. Global**

Late on Friday July 15, 2016, a faction of the Turkish military attempted to seize power in Ankara and Istanbul. By morning July 16, President Erdogan had returned to Istanbul and loyalist forces were retaking control. By July 17, it was over.

Let us compare Turkish local assets with global markets over the weekend and the following Monday open:

**Turkish lira (TRY/USD)**: moved from 2.88 before the coup attempt to 3.02 in overnight trading — a devaluation of **4.9%** in hours. This is large for a currency over a single night.

**BIST-100 (Istanbul stock exchange)**: opened Monday July 18 down **-7.1%** — the largest single-day drop since the 2008 global financial crisis for Turkish stocks.

**Global markets on Monday July 18?** The S&P 500 *closed up* +0.5%. European indices were flat to slightly down. Emerging markets as a whole fell about -0.4%. The global impact of a coup in Turkey — a country with a ~\$900 billion GDP and NATO membership — was essentially zero on global stock markets.

Why such a small global impact? Because Turkey fails the "global contagion checklist":
- Turkey is not a significant commodity exporter (fails supply-shock test)
- US and China were not directly involved (fails the "major power involvement" test)
- The coup failed quickly, limiting uncertainty duration
- Turkey's financial system was not deeply integrated with global systemic banks (unlike Greece in 2010)

By one month later, the TRY had recovered to 2.94 — almost completely erasing the coup devaluation. The BIST-100 was back near pre-coup levels by September.

The size differential is instructive: **Turkey -5% in a day vs. global markets -0.4%**. The ratio is roughly 12.5:1 for a "local shock." For reference, in the Russia-Ukraine invasion, the gap was much smaller (Russia was -85% but European markets were also -10% to -15%) because Russia's commodity exports were genuinely global. The *local vs. global* multiplier depends on commodity and financial linkage, not on the severity of the political event.

## The Duration Dimension: Spikes vs. Repricing vs. Regime Change

One of the most important upgrades to the basic sensitivity matrix is adding the *time* dimension. The same crisis event can produce completely different return profiles depending on whether you are trading the first-week spike, the two-month repricing, or a multi-year structural shift.

![Asset performance changes dramatically by crisis duration — spike, medium-term, and regime change](/imgs/blogs/asset-sensitivity-matrix-which-assets-move-in-which-crisis-6.png)

### Spike Events: Days to Two Weeks

In the first days of a crisis, the market is pricing *maximum uncertainty*. Investors do not know how bad it will get. Risk premiums spike across the board. Volatility as measured by the VIX jumps. Safe havens are bid up indiscriminately — even assets that do not benefit from the specific crisis logic (like gold in a trade war) get some safe-haven buying from generalist investors who just want "safety."

Spike events are the territory of fast traders: you buy the safest safe haven (gold, 3-month T-bills, JPY) in the first hours, then reassess as more information arrives. The spike typically reverses at least partially within 2-4 weeks once the worst-case scenario does not materialize.

Historical average: the VIX (the "fear index" for the S&P 500) spikes an average of +19% in the week after a major geopolitical event, then recovers about 60% of that spike within 30 days.

### Medium-Term Repricing: One to Three Months

After the initial panic, markets shift to *discounting the actual economic effects* of the crisis. This is the phase where sector-specific and commodity-specific effects dominate. If a war is disrupting oil supply, Brent crude may stay elevated for months — not because of fear, but because physical supply genuinely cannot keep up with demand at the previous price.

During this phase, equities can partially recover as investors assess that the worst-case scenario is not happening, while commodity prices may stay elevated. This creates the situation where gold starts to converge back toward fair value (fear premium unwinding) even as oil stays high (real supply effect persisting).

### Structural Regime Change: Six Months to Years

Some geopolitical events permanently alter the structure of an industry, a trade route, or a financial system. These are the rarest but most profitable to position for correctly.

Russia's energy relationship with Europe is a prime example. Before February 2022, Russia supplied roughly 40% of EU natural gas. By 2023, that had fallen to approximately 8%. This was not a temporary blip — it was a permanent rerouting of energy supply chains that repriced European energy infrastructure for a decade. The investors who correctly identified this as a *structural regime change* (not just a spike) positioned in LNG infrastructure stocks, European renewable energy, and energy efficiency companies — and held for years.

Similarly, the US-China chip war that began in earnest in October 2022 (with export controls on advanced AI chips) created a structural repricing of the semiconductor supply chain. Companies like Nvidia lost 12% on the October 7, 2022 announcement day, but then gained +239% over calendar year 2023 as AI demand overwhelmed the China revenue loss. Investors who recognized the structural shift early — that the chip war would *accelerate* domestic semiconductor investment, not just restrict it — captured extraordinary returns.

## Second-Order Effects: Defense, Cybersecurity, Agriculture, Logistics

The matrix focuses on broad asset classes, but some of the most asymmetric trades in geopolitical crises come from *sector rotation* within equities.

### Defense and Aerospace

Every armed conflict is bullish for defense contractors (Lockheed Martin, Raytheon, BAE Systems, Thales) and aerospace manufacturers. The mechanism: governments immediately announce spending increases, order new weapons systems, and rush to replenish stockpiles. Defense stocks typically outperform the broader market by 10-20% in the first month of a major conflict.

The spending math is substantial. NATO's 28 non-US members have a combined GDP of approximately \$20 trillion. A shift from 1.5% of GDP average defense spending (the pre-Ukraine average) to 2.5% (the post-Ukraine target) represents \$200 billion in incremental annual defense procurement. Spread over a 10-year procurement cycle for weapons systems and platforms, that is potentially \$2 trillion in additional weapons contracts. Defense equity analysts model this as a 15-20 year earnings growth runway for major primes (Lockheed, Northrop Grumman, BAE Systems), which justifies the sector's sustained outperformance long after the initial headline shock.

The asymmetry in defense: these stocks do not *fall* significantly when conflicts de-escalate, because defense budgets, once raised, rarely revert. Governments rarely reduce their military spending meaningfully in peacetime when they have recently experienced a shock. The buy signal is the conflict; the hold signal is permanent. The sell signal (budget reversion) almost never fires.

After Russia invaded Ukraine, NATO members collectively announced plans to raise defense spending from the 2% of GDP target (which most were below) toward 3-4%. That represented hundreds of billions of dollars in incremental weapons procurement over a decade. Defense ETFs (like ITA or XAR in the US) rallied 20-30% over 2022 even as the broader market fell -20%.

### Cybersecurity

Conflict events are consistently positive for cybersecurity stocks (Palo Alto Networks, CrowdStrike, Fortinet). Every geopolitical escalation — war, sanctions, trade tensions — brings with it a surge in state-sponsored cyberattacks against critical infrastructure. After the SolarWinds hack in December 2020, the cybersecurity ETF (HACK) rose +18% over the following six months. After Colonial Pipeline in May 2021, it rose +8% over three months.

The mechanism is straightforward: corporate and government IT security budgets increase when the news is full of hacking incidents. Cybersecurity is one of the few sectors with *positive* geopolitical sensitivity.

### Agriculture and Food

Conflicts near major agricultural regions create complex commodity effects that work differently from energy. Ukraine-Russia together produce roughly 25-30% of global wheat exports and 15% of global corn exports. When the Black Sea export corridor closed in 2022, global food prices surged — wheat futures at one point rose +60% from pre-war levels (before the Black Sea Grain Initiative partially restored flows).

The agricultural sensitivity is not symmetric across crops. A war in the Black Sea region hits wheat, corn, and sunflower oil directly. A conflict in South America would hit soybeans, coffee, and beef. A drought-driven crisis in Asia would hit rice. The sensitivity matrix for agricultural commodities requires region-specific analysis.

### Logistics and Shipping

Wars, sanctions, and blockades redraw shipping routes — sometimes permanently. When Houthi attacks on Red Sea shipping in late 2023 forced container ships to reroute around the Cape of Good Hope, it added roughly two weeks to Asia-Europe transit times and pushed shipping rates 200-300% higher. Container shipping stocks (Maersk, Hapag-Lloyd, Cosco) rallied sharply on the rate increase.

The investment logic: shipping companies benefit in the short run from rate spikes (their fixed cost base means incremental revenue is nearly pure profit). But if the disruption is long-lasting, shippers order more vessels, and the rate advantage erodes. Logistics traders position *early* in the spike and exit as new capacity is committed.

## Regional vs. Global Contagion: The Decision Tree

The single most important tactical judgment a geopolitical trader makes is: **is this a local shock or a global one?** Getting this wrong in either direction is expensive — being short EM equities on a purely local coup means paying carry for nothing; being complacent about a global supply shock means missing a 25% oil move.

![The contagion decision tree from local geopolitical event to global shock classification](/imgs/blogs/asset-sensitivity-matrix-which-assets-move-in-which-crisis-7.png)

The decision tree works from the event characteristics:

**Step 1: Does the event physically impact energy supply?** If yes — oil pipelines, export terminals, shipping chokepoints, OPEC member decision-making — it is at least a regional shock with global commodity implications. Skip ahead to assessing magnitude.

**Step 2: Are the US or China directly involved?** Conflict involving either of the two largest economies immediately becomes a global financial-contagion event, because so much EM trade, debt, and investment flows through the dollar system and Chinese supply chains. A trade war between the US and China in 2018-2019 moved *every* EM currency, not just CNH.

**Step 3: Are major trade routes disrupted?** The Strait of Hormuz, Suez Canal, Panama Canal, Bosporus, and Malacca Strait are the five chokepoints whose closure triggers global shocks. A blockade of Hormuz (through which 21 million barrels/day of oil passes) would be an immediate global commodity crisis regardless of which country initiated it.

**Step 4: What is the financial linkage of the affected country?** Greece failed the energy and trade-route tests but still triggered a global contagion because it was inside the Eurozone — its sovereign debt was on the balance sheets of major German and French banks, and a default risked a euro breakup. The financial linkage was the mechanism.

If an event passes none of these four tests, it is a local shock. **Local shock = small position size.** The position sizing implication is concrete: a practitioner might size a trade on a truly local event at 25% of the size they would use for a confirmed global shock.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Gold always rallies in a crisis."**

Gold rallies in *fear of currency debasement and institutional collapse* crises. It does not reliably rally in trade wars (no debasement fear), sovereign debt crises of small countries (no global debasement), or regime changes. In COVID March 2020, gold *fell* initially despite being in the middle of a global financial panic. The liquidity squeeze was stronger than the safe-haven bid for the first two weeks. A blanket "buy gold in any crisis" rule would have cost you 5-11% on entry timing.

**Misconception 2: "The USD always strengthens in a crisis."**

Usually true, but not in US-initiated trade wars with China, and not when the crisis *originates in the US financial system* (like the 2008 Global Financial Crisis, where the dollar paradoxically strengthened in 2008 on safe-haven demand, then weakened sharply in 2009 as QE diluted it). In Liberation Day April 2025, when the US announced sweeping tariffs, the DXY actually fell from 104 to 99 — because markets priced in long-term US competitiveness damage and potential retaliation against dollar-denominated trade.

**Misconception 3: "Bonds rally in geopolitical crises."**

Only in *pure risk-off* crises without significant commodity price disruption. In supply-shock wars (Ukraine 2022), US 10-year yields actually *rose* during the worst weeks because inflation expectations dominated. In sovereign debt crises, the *affected country's bonds collapse* (Greece 10-year yields went from 5.5% in 2009 to 38% at the peak in 2012). "Bonds rally in crises" is shorthand for "US Treasuries rally in pure fear events" — not a universal rule.

**Misconception 4: "Trade wars hurt all EM countries equally."**

A US-China trade war is *specifically negative* for China-integrated EM supply chains (South Korea, Taiwan, Vietnam's tech sector) but can be *positive* for countries that absorb redirected supply chains (Vietnam's overall FDI, Bangladesh's textiles, Mexico's nearshoring inflows). Vietnam's exports to the US grew from \$47.5 billion in 2018 (pre-trade war) to \$109.1 billion by 2022 — a 130% increase driven partly by manufacturing diverted from China. The matrix shows "EM Equities: Bearish" for a trade war, but smart investors look one level deeper.

**Misconception 5: "The bigger the political shock, the bigger the market move."**

The Russia-Ukraine invasion of 2022 was one of the largest geopolitical shocks in decades — yet the S&P 500 fell only about 5% from pre-invasion levels within the first month, and had recovered by May 2022. The market cared about commodity supply disruption and inflation, not about the political severity of the invasion. Meanwhile, a relatively minor event — the announcement of US tariffs on \$50 billion of Chinese goods in March 2018 — wiped 3% off the S&P 500 in a single day because markets had not priced it in. **Surprise size, not event size, drives the initial move.**

## How It Shows Up in Real Markets: The Playbook

### The Iran Sanctions Playbook: A Slow-Moving Supply Shock

Iran's oil export history provides a clean case study in how sanctions interact with commodity prices. Iran exported approximately 2.5 million barrels per day (mb/d) before the first nuclear-related sanctions tightened around 2012. As sanctions bit, exports fell to 1.1 mb/d by 2013 — effectively removing 1.4 mb/d from global supply. Brent crude was trading around \$100-115/barrel in 2012-2014, partly supported by that supply-reduction premium.

When the JCPOA (Iran nuclear deal) was signed in July 2015 and oil flows began to recover toward 2.5 mb/d again, Brent crude was sitting near \$65/barrel and fell all the way to \$27/barrel by February 2016. Multiple factors contributed to that fall (US shale expansion was the dominant factor), but the +1.4 mb/d supply restoration from Iran was a material contributor.

When the US withdrew from the JCPOA in May 2018 under President Trump and reimposed sanctions, Iranian exports collapsed again — from 2.6 mb/d in early 2018 to 0.5 mb/d by 2019. That removal of roughly 2 million barrels daily was one reason Brent crude traded in the \$60-85/barrel range through 2019 rather than lower.

The math: global oil demand is approximately 100 mb/d. Iran sanctions removed 1.5-2.0 mb/d, which represents about 1.5-2.0% of supply. Historical estimates suggest each 1% of global supply removal raises oil prices by approximately \$7-10/barrel in the medium term, all else equal. So the full Iran sanctions impact should have raised Brent by roughly \$10-20/barrel — consistent with what we observed.

This is the *sanctions sensitivity calculation* that commodity traders run for any sanction on a major producer. The formula is: (supply removed in mb/d) / (global demand in mb/d) × (supply elasticity factor of ~\$7-10/mb-d-%) = expected Brent price impact.

### Reading the Safe-Haven Flow in Real Time

Professional geopolitical traders watch a specific set of markets to classify a new event in real time. These are not exotic data sources — they are freely available:

**Gold spot price (XAU/USD)**: Moving +1% in the first hour of news confirms the event triggered the safe-haven channel. Moving -1% while stocks also fall suggests a liquidity crunch.

**VIX** (the S&P 500 implied volatility index, known as the "fear index"): A jump above 25 from below 20 in a single session is a confirmed risk-off signal. Above 35 historically marks near-term panic bottoms.

**USD/JPY**: The Japanese yen strengthens (USD/JPY falls) in risk-off events because Japan is a massive net creditor nation and investors repatriate Japanese capital in a global panic. A sudden -1% move in USD/JPY (yen strengthens) is a real-time risk-off signal that precedes or accompanies stock declines.

**5-year breakeven inflation rate** (available on the FRED database from the St. Louis Fed): If this rises on the same day as a geopolitical shock, it signals the market is reading the event as inflationary — supply-shock channel is firing. If it stays flat or falls, it is a pure risk-off event.

**Brent crude front-month futures**: The fastest indicator of whether the supply-shock channel has fired. In the first minutes after a major conflict announcement near an oil-producing region, Brent moves before any other asset.

### Using the GPR Index as a Quantitative Timing Tool

The Geopolitical Risk Index (GPR), developed by economists Dario Caldara and Matteo Iacoviello, counts mentions of geopolitical risk in 11 major newspapers worldwide. It is normalized so that 100 equals the average level from 1985 to 2019. Here are the peak readings at major crises:

- **9/11 attacks (September 2001)**: GPR = 326 — the all-time peak in modern data
- **Iraq War invasion (March 2003)**: GPR = 254
- **Russia invades Ukraine (February 2022)**: GPR = 345 — second-highest ever recorded
- **Gaza War October 2023**: GPR = 188
- **Iran-Israel direct exchange (April 2024)**: GPR = 203

Historical analysis shows that when the GPR spikes above 200, the following statistical patterns emerge:

1. Gold outperforms cash by an average of +3.5% over the following 30 days
2. MSCI EM equities underperform MSCI World by an average of -2.8% over 30 days
3. VIX typically jumps 15-25% in the week of the spike and reverts 60% of that within 30 days
4. Brent crude rises an average of +4.1% in the week of the spike

Below GPR 150, geopolitical events are statistical noise — their asset-price impact is indistinguishable from random market fluctuation. This threshold is useful for filtering: if a conflict escalation keeps the GPR below 130, it is not worth building a geopolitical macro trade around it.

The practical use: follow the GPR monthly update (available free at matteoiacoviello.com). When the index spikes above 175 from a sub-150 baseline, that is your signal to review the matrix and size tactical positions. When it falls from above 200 back below 150, the unwind trade is active.

### The Sizing Framework

Knowing direction is necessary but not sufficient. Position sizing must account for the probability that the event escalates vs. de-escalates. A practical framework:

- **Confirmed global shock (hits energy, US/China involved, or financial contagion)**: Full tactical position size. Hedge with options if the volatility surface is cheap relative to realized GPR index spikes.
- **Regional shock with commodity exposure**: Half position. Maintain stop-loss at the point where the commodity price signal reverses.
- **Local shock (coup, small country, no commodity exposure)**: Quarter position or no position. The signal-to-noise ratio is too low.

The GPR Index (Geopolitical Risk Index, Caldara-Iacoviello) peaked at 345 in February 2022 during the Russia-Ukraine invasion. Historically, VIX and gold show the strongest correlation with GPR spikes above 200. Below 150, geopolitical events are background noise for most asset classes.

### Position Checklist: Five Questions Before Placing a Geopolitical Trade

Before executing any trade based on geopolitical analysis, a disciplined practitioner runs through five questions in order:

**1. Which channel is firing?** Risk-off only, supply-shock only, or both? If you cannot answer this, you cannot determine bond direction — and getting bonds wrong ruins a geopolitical macro portfolio.

**2. What is the market pricing that has already happened versus what has not?** If oil spiked +15% on the news and you are reading about it now, the trade may already be done. The question is not "will oil be higher than pre-event?" but "will oil be higher than its current post-event price in 30 days?"

**3. What is the GPR baseline?** If the GPR is already elevated at 180 before today's event, the *marginal* surprise from a further escalation is smaller, and the risk premium for additional escalation is already partly priced. If the GPR is at 110 (calm baseline) and an event shocks it to 250, almost nothing was priced in — that is maximum opportunity.

**4. What is the invalidation scenario?** Every geopolitical trade needs a defined stop-loss trigger. For an energy supply-shock trade long crude oil, the invalidation is: ceasefire announced + OPEC+ increases production to fill the gap. Define that *before* entry, not after the position is losing.

**5. Is this a spike trade or a regime-change trade?** These require entirely different instruments. Spike trades use front-month futures (tight to the spot price, maximum price sensitivity) and options with 30-60 day expiry. Regime-change trades use longer-dated futures, infrastructure equities, and corporate bonds of energy/defense companies — instruments that capture the multi-year repricing, not the one-week panic. Getting this classification wrong is one of the costliest errors in geopolitical investing: holding a spike position too long means watching the risk premium unwind while you are still long; holding a regime-change position on a spike-only event means tying up capital for months in a trade that resolved in days.

### The Unwind Trade

Every geopolitical risk premium eventually unwinds. The Iran nuclear deal signed in July 2015 caused Iran to ramp oil exports from 1.1 mb/d to 2.5 mb/d within two years, adding 1.4 million barrels daily to global supply and helping push Brent crude from \$65 to a low of \$27 by February 2016. The "unwind trade" — selling the geopolitical premium once a crisis de-escalates — is just as important as the initial positioning trade.

The signature of a geopolitical unwind: the GPR index falls, the VIX declines, gold gives back 30-50% of its crisis gain, and EM FX begins to recover. The sequence typically takes 30-90 days after the peak uncertainty. Trying to pick the exact top of the risk premium is less effective than waiting for the *VIX peak* confirmation (when VIX starts falling from a 30+ level, the de-escalation is confirmed) and then positioning for the unwind.
