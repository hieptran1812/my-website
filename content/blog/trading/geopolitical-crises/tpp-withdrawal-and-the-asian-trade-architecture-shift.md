> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — On January 20, 2017, Donald Trump signed an executive memo withdrawing the US from the Trans-Pacific Partnership (TPP) on his first day in office. That single act set off a cascade: Japan's Shinzo Abe scrambled to salvage the pact as CPTPP (11 nations, 13.5% of global GDP); China accelerated RCEP (15 nations, 30% of global GDP, signed 2020), locking in a massive but shallow trade zone; and the Biden administration's replacement, IPEF (2022), offered no tariff reductions at all. For investors, the implications are concrete: US beef exporters pay 38.5% tariffs in Japan while Australians pay zero; Vietnamese manufacturers gained preferential access US competitors cannot match; and supply chains across Southeast Asia have realigned toward RCEP origin rules that explicitly favor Chinese inputs. This post maps the full trade architecture shift — the political logic, the financial channels, the winners and losers, and a practical playbook for positioning around it.

On the morning of January 20, 2017, hours after his inauguration, Donald Trump signed a one-page executive memorandum withdrawing the United States from the Trans-Pacific Partnership. The document was brief. Its consequences were not. Markets had 48 hours to absorb the news over the weekend; when Asian FX reopened Monday morning, January 23, the Australian dollar fell roughly one percent against the greenback, a currency that had been priced partly on TPP optimism and Australia's deep Pacific trade exposure.

That one-percent move was small by headline standards. What it flagged was structural. TPP had taken more than five years and twelve nations to negotiate. It covered tariff schedules on tens of thousands of goods, IP enforcement mechanisms, labor standards, digital trade rules, financial services access, and state-enterprise disciplines — a comprehensive rulebook that would have written US commercial values into Asian trade law for a generation. When the US walked away, it did not just abandon market access. It abandoned the institutional architecture it had spent decades trying to build.

The vacuum that opened was filled — by Japan's Abe rescuing TPP as CPTPP without the US, by China accelerating its own RCEP framework, and ultimately by a Biden-era substitute called IPEF that critics immediately recognized as high on branding and zero on tariff cuts. By 2022, Asia-Pacific trade had reorganized around three overlapping, competing frameworks, none of which included both the United States and China, and only one of which — CPTPP — had genuinely binding commercial standards.

This post explains how that reorganization happened, what it means for tariff flows and FDI patterns across the region, and how an investor should position around the resulting architecture.

## Foundations: Trade Blocs and Market Architecture

Before analyzing the TPP collapse, it helps to be precise about what trade blocs actually do and why market participants care about them at all.

**What a free trade agreement actually changes.** An FTA is a legal contract between governments that modifies the default tariff schedule each country applies to imported goods — the Most Favored Nation (MFN) rate. MFN rates are set under WTO rules: every WTO member must charge every other member the same baseline tariff on a given product category, unless a specific FTA exception applies. An FTA creates that exception for signatories, allowing them to charge each other lower rates — sometimes zero — while continuing to charge MFN rates to everyone else.

The commercial consequence is a preferential cost advantage. If Japan's MFN tariff on beef is 38.5% and Australia has an FTA with Japan granting zero tariffs on beef, an Australian exporter landing \$100 of beef in Japan pays nothing extra. A US exporter landing the same \$100 pays \$38.50 in tariff before any profit calculation. Over a \$1 billion trade flow, that gap is \$385 million — not a rounding error, but a structural profitability wedge that reshapes procurement decisions for decades.

**Rules of origin.** FTAs don't just cut tariffs — they define which goods qualify for those cuts. Rules of origin determine how much of a product must originate from within the FTA zone to receive preferential treatment. This matters enormously in Asia's integrated manufacturing landscape, where a smartphone might contain Korean memory, Japanese displays, Taiwanese chips, and Chinese assembly. Under RCEP, origin stacking allows inputs from any RCEP member to count toward origin thresholds. This makes Chinese supply chains more competitive in Japan and Australia than they would be under bilateral deals.

**Investment and supply chain effects.** Companies don't just optimize for today's tariff schedule — they build factories, sign long-term supplier contracts, and train workforces based on expected future trade costs. When a major FTA shifts, supply chains shift with a lag of two to five years as capex cycles and contracts roll over. Vietnam's FDI surge after 2017 reflects exactly this: manufacturers moving production to a country inside CPTPP to secure preferential access to Japan and Australia that the US could no longer offer from within the agreement.

**Currency and financial market effects.** Trade architecture shifts don't stay in goods markets. They flow through corporate earnings, through FDI-driven capital flows, through portfolio rebalancing by global asset managers repricing regional growth trajectories, and through currency markets where trade-exposed economies like Australia, New Zealand, and Vietnam see their exchange rates partly determined by the health of their export relationships. The AUD's move on January 23, 2017 was a textbook example: the market repriced Australia's trade outlook in real time.

![Before and after comparison of tariff rates under TPP versus the actual no-TPP world](/imgs/blogs/tpp-withdrawal-and-the-asian-trade-architecture-shift-2.png)

**The four pillars of trade architecture value.** When analysts grade a trade agreement, they look at four things. First, tariff depth — how many tariff lines go to zero, and how quickly. Second, standards quality — whether the deal imposes enforceable IP protection, labor standards, environmental rules, and state-enterprise disciplines. Third, coverage breadth — what share of global GDP and trade flows the members represent. Fourth, binding force — whether dispute resolution has teeth. TPP was unusual in scoring highly on all four. Its successors, as we'll see, each sacrifice at least one dimension.

## The Political Moves

To understand the trade architecture shift, you need the political narrative behind it — because the sequencing of decisions, and the personal stakes involved, shaped which alternatives were viable.

**TPP's genesis.** The Trans-Pacific Partnership grew out of a 2005 agreement between Brunei, Chile, New Zealand, and Singapore. The US joined negotiations in 2008, under the Obama administration, and the deal expanded to include 12 nations: the US, Japan, Canada, Mexico, Australia, New Zealand, Vietnam, Malaysia, Singapore, Brunei, Peru, and Chile. Negotiations concluded in October 2015 in Atlanta. The agreement was signed in Auckland, New Zealand on February 4, 2016.

From the start, TPP had a geopolitical rationale as much as a commercial one. The Obama "pivot to Asia" was partly about using trade architecture to anchor US influence in a region where China's economic weight was growing rapidly. TPP explicitly excluded China. The deal's high-standards provisions — on IP, labor, and state enterprises — were designed to be incompatible with China's economic model, creating a quality-standard divide between US-aligned and China-aligned trade systems. Secretary of State Clinton called it "the economic equivalent of a security treaty."

**The ratification failure.** The agreement was signed but never sent to the US Senate for ratification. Both Hillary Clinton (shifting position from her Obama-era support) and Donald Trump opposed it on the campaign trail. By the time Trump was inaugurated, TPP was effectively a dead letter in US domestic politics. His executive memo on January 20, 2017 formalized what markets already suspected: the US was out.

**Japan's response.** No leader had more riding on TPP than Shinzo Abe. He had staked considerable political capital on ratifying TPP in Japan, overcoming fierce domestic resistance from agricultural interests (Japanese rice farmers, beef producers, and dairy cooperatives had long been shielded by tariffs in the 30-700% range). Abe's bet was that TPP would force Japan's agricultural sector to modernize in exchange for massive gains in auto exports and financial services. When the US withdrew, Abe faced a choice: walk away, or rescue the deal without its anchor member.

He chose to rescue it. Working with trade ministers in Canada, Australia, and Vietnam, Abe's government drove the CPTPP negotiation to conclusion, suspending 22 provisions from the original TPP text (mostly IP provisions that the US had demanded and the other 11 members were happy to set aside). CPTPP was signed in Santiago, Chile on March 8, 2018. It entered force for the first six ratifiers — Australia, Canada, Japan, Mexico, New Zealand, and Singapore — on December 30, 2018.

**China's strategic calculation.** China had been conspicuously excluded from TPP. Beijing viewed the deal as a US instrument for containing Chinese economic influence. When the US withdrew, the strategic calculus shifted. China had been running parallel negotiations on RCEP — the Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership — since 2012. RCEP was ASEAN-led and included all 10 ASEAN members plus China, Japan, South Korea, Australia, and New Zealand. It was explicitly not high-standard: no IP provisions, no labor chapters, no state-enterprise disciplines. But it had scale.

With TPP gone, China accelerated RCEP. The negotiations took four more years, complicated by India's participation — India had been a RCEP member but walked out in November 2019, citing concerns that the deal would flood Indian markets with cheap Chinese goods and undercut domestic manufacturing. Without India, RCEP was signed virtually on November 15, 2020, with 15 nations. It entered force on January 1, 2022.

**The Biden non-response.** The Biden administration recognized the strategic problem immediately. The US was outside both CPTPP and RCEP. American exporters faced tariff disadvantages in Japanese and Australian markets where CPTPP members enjoyed preferential access. Supply chains across Southeast Asia were aligning around RCEP origin rules. But the Biden team faced the same domestic political constraint as Obama: no tariff-cutting trade deal could clear Congress.

The solution — or its approximation — was IPEF: the Indo-Pacific Economic Framework, launched in May 2022 at Biden's Tokyo summit. IPEF had 14 members (the US, India, Japan, South Korea, Australia, New Zealand, Fiji, and seven ASEAN nations). It was organized into four "pillars": clean economy, fair economy, connected economy, and trade. The critical detail was buried in the fine print: IPEF offered no tariff reductions. None. It was a framework for discussing supply chain resilience, digital standards, and labor rules — without the commercial incentive of market access that makes countries actually implement painful domestic reforms.

## The Financial Channels

The trade architecture shift shows up in real financial data through five distinct channels: tariff wedges, FDI flows, supply chain realignment, currency movements, and equity sector performance.

**Tariff wedges and corporate margins.** The most direct channel is the simplest. When a tariff changes, the landed cost of a product changes. For commodities traded in large volumes — beef, agricultural products, industrial inputs — a tariff wedge translates almost mechanically into market share shifts. US beef in Japan, as we'll detail in the worked examples, faced an immediate and permanent competitive disadvantage versus Australian and New Zealand competitors once CPTPP entered force. This shows up in Japanese import statistics, in US agricultural export revenue data, and in the earnings of US beef processors versus their antipodean competitors.

**FDI flows as architecture indicators.** Foreign direct investment responds to trade architecture because manufacturers build production capacity where it can access preferential market access. The clearest indicator of architecture value is where global manufacturers choose to put new factories. Vietnam became the primary beneficiary of post-TPP FDI reallocation. Its FDI inflows rose from \$14.1 billion in 2017 to over \$23 billion by 2023, driven by companies — Samsung, Intel, LG, Nike, Foxconn — seeking a production base inside CPTPP that could access Japan, Australia, Canada, and Mexico at preferential tariff rates.

**Supply chain realignment toward RCEP.** A subtler but larger channel is RCEP's rules-of-origin effect on supply chains. Before RCEP, a manufacturer using Chinese inputs to assemble goods in Vietnam for export to Japan would have faced a complex rules-of-origin calculation. Under RCEP's cumulation rules, any inputs from RCEP members — which includes China — count toward origin thresholds. This made Vietnamese manufacturing even more attractive as a hub: you could source cheaply from China (an RCEP member), assemble in Vietnam (an RCEP and CPTPP member), and export to Japan at zero tariff under either CPTPP or RCEP.

**Currency effects.** Trade-sensitive currencies react to architecture shifts. The AUD/USD pair is particularly sensitive to Asia-Pacific trade developments because Australia's exports — LNG, iron ore, beef, wine, dairy — are heavily tilted toward Asian markets. When TPP was alive, Australian exporters anticipated preferential access to Japan and Southeast Asia. When the US withdrew, the AUD softened. When CPTPP was signed and Australia ratified it, some of that access was restored through a different channel. More broadly, currencies of CPTPP members have tended to benefit from the deal's FDI-attracting properties, while currencies of nations outside both CPTPP and RCEP have faced relative headwinds.

![Vietnam FDI inflows bar chart 2015 to 2024 showing surge after trade architecture shifts](/imgs/blogs/tpp-withdrawal-and-the-asian-trade-architecture-shift-5.png)

**Equity sector effects.** Sector performance diverges predictably along trade architecture lines. Exporters of agricultural commodities (Australian beef, New Zealand dairy) benefit from CPTPP's Japan access. Electronic manufacturers in Vietnam and Malaysia benefit from CPTPP's rules-of-origin advantages in consumer markets. Chinese manufacturers benefit from RCEP's origin stacking. US agricultural exporters, machinery producers, and financial services firms that expected TPP-level access to Asian markets have seen that access permanently repriced at a disadvantage.

## CPTPP: High Standards Without the Hegemon

The Comprehensive and Progressive Agreement for Trans-Pacific Partnership is, by the standards of modern trade agreements, a genuinely ambitious document. It preserves most of the TPP text, including chapters on intellectual property protection, labor standards, environmental provisions, digital trade, financial services, government procurement, and state-owned enterprise disciplines. The 22 suspended provisions (mostly the US-demanded IP chapters on pharmaceuticals and data protection) are set aside until a country capable of reactivating them — meaning the US — rejoins.

CPTPP's current members are Australia, Brunei, Canada, Chile, Japan, Malaysia, Mexico, New Zealand, Peru, Singapore, and Vietnam. The UK acceded in 2023. This is a geographically diverse group spanning three continents and covering approximately \$13.5 trillion in combined GDP — roughly 13.5% of global GDP. It is not a small deal. It just lacks the US market, which is the world's largest consumer economy and the ultimate destination for much of Asia's export-oriented manufacturing.

**What CPTPP actually delivers.** The deal eliminates tariffs on roughly 99% of tariff lines between members. Japan's tariff schedule alone — historically one of the most protected in the developed world — sees massive reductions under CPTPP: beef tariffs phased from 38.5% to 9% over 16 years for Australia and New Zealand, wine tariffs to zero, dairy quotas expanded, and hundreds of industrial tariff lines eliminated immediately. For Vietnamese exporters, CPTPP access to Japan, Canada, and Mexico opened markets that previously faced MFN rates.

**The China exclusion matters.** CPTPP explicitly excludes China. The deal's IP and labor chapters are incompatible with China's current economic governance model. For members, this creates a standards club: CPTPP members signal to global business that their trade and investment environments meet a higher governance standard than RCEP-only members. For multinational corporations choosing factory locations, this distinction matters for supply chain risk assessment, IP protection, and regulatory predictability.

**The US re-entry question.** Every CPTPP member would prefer US participation — the US market is simply too large to leave on the table. Japan's export gains from US market access would dwarf what it receives from smaller CPTPP members. But US re-entry requires Senate ratification, which requires overcoming domestic political opposition to trade deals that has only strengthened since 2017. The CPTPP text reserves a slot for the US under Article 5.4, and the suspended provisions could be reactivated. But the political path is unclear under any realistic US political scenario.

#### Worked example:

**Japan beef tariff wedge.** The US exports approximately 200,000 tonnes of beef to Japan annually at an average export price of \$5,000 per tonne, representing roughly \$1 billion in annual trade. Japan's MFN tariff on beef is 38.5%. This means Japanese importers of US beef face a tariff cost of \$38.50 per \$100 of imported beef — or \$385 million across the full \$1 billion trade volume.

Under CPTPP, Australia's beef tariff to Japan is being phased down from 38.5% to 9% over 16 years, with significant reductions already locked in. Australia exports approximately 500,000 tonnes of beef to Japan annually at similar price points — roughly \$2.5 billion. The Australian exporter with a 15% tariff rate pays \$375 million. The Australian exporter with a 9% tariff rate pays \$225 million. The US exporter at 38.5% pays \$385 million on half the volume.

The competitive arithmetic is stark: every kilogram of Australian beef in Japan carries a tariff cost roughly a quarter of what the same kilogram of US beef costs. Japanese supermarket chains, food-service operators, and processors respond to this by substituting Australian and New Zealand beef for US beef in their procurement schedules. US market share in Japanese beef imports has declined while Australia's has grown — the exact inverse of what TPP was designed to produce. The \$385 million tariff differential is permanent until the US rejoins CPTPP or negotiates a bilateral FTA with Japan on comparable terms.

## RCEP: Shallow Coverage, Massive Scale

The Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership is the world's largest free trade agreement by the only metric that unambiguously matters: the combined GDP and population of its members. The 15 RCEP members — the 10 ASEAN nations (Brunei, Cambodia, Indonesia, Laos, Malaysia, Myanmar, Philippines, Singapore, Thailand, Vietnam) plus China, Japan, South Korea, Australia, and New Zealand — represent approximately 30% of global GDP and 30% of global population.

By comparison, CPTPP covers 13.5% of global GDP. The EU single market covers about 15%. NAFTA/USMCA covers about 28%. RCEP, at 30% of global GDP, is genuinely without precedent in scale.

But scale is almost everything RCEP has. The deal is deliberately shallow. There are no provisions on intellectual property enforcement, no labor chapters, no environmental rules, and no meaningful disciplines on state-owned enterprises. On tariffs, RCEP eliminates duties on approximately 90% of goods traded among members over a 20-year timeline — a narrower and slower tariff liberalization schedule than CPTPP's near-universal zero-tariff structure.

The shallowness is not accidental. It reflects the negotiating reality of a deal that includes both Japan (which demanded high-quality rules) and Myanmar (which couldn't meet high-quality standards), both Singapore (already one of the world's most open economies) and Indonesia (which maintains significant protectionist policies across many sectors). RCEP's architecture was designed to reach consensus across an extraordinarily diverse membership by keeping binding commitments minimal.

**RCEP's real value: origin stacking.** The most economically significant feature of RCEP is its rules-of-origin cumulation provision. Under RCEP, inputs from any member country count toward the origin requirements needed to qualify for preferential tariff treatment. This "cumulation" provision means a product assembled in Vietnam from Chinese components and Japanese machinery qualifies as RCEP-origin for tariff purposes when exported to Australia or Japan — even though no single country contributes a majority of the product's value.

This directly benefits China, which is the dominant supplier of intermediate goods across virtually every manufacturing sector in Asia. Under previous bilateral FTAs, Chinese inputs might not count toward origin thresholds. Under RCEP, they do — making China's supply chains the default competitive choice for any RCEP member trying to qualify goods for preferential access.

**What RCEP means for China's strategic position.** RCEP represents China's most significant multilateral trade achievement. Before RCEP, China lacked a free trade agreement with Japan or South Korea — two of its largest trading partners. RCEP changes that, at least in a limited way. More importantly, RCEP gives China a platform for influence-building across ASEAN that the US cannot easily counter through IPEF's tariff-free architecture.

The Belt and Road Initiative operates alongside RCEP, providing infrastructure financing that deepens trade relationships by reducing logistics costs on routes connecting Chinese suppliers to RCEP consumer markets. The combination of RCEP tariff preferences and BRI infrastructure creates a reinforcing gravitational pull toward Chinese supply chains across the region.

#### Worked example:

**RCEP origin stacking.** Consider a \$1,000 finished electronic device assembled in Vietnam for export to Japan. The device contains components worth \$600 manufactured in China (semiconductors, display modules, battery cells) and \$400 in Vietnamese assembly labor, Vietnamese-manufactured casings, and Vietnamese circuit boards.

Before RCEP: To qualify for tariff preferences under Vietnam-Japan bilateral arrangements, the device would need to meet content rules that might not recognize Chinese inputs as Vietnamese origin. The manufacturer might face Vietnam's MFN tariff to Japan, or need to substitute Vietnamese or Japanese components to hit origin thresholds.

After RCEP: Both China and Vietnam are RCEP members. The \$600 in Chinese inputs counts as RCEP-origin. The \$400 Vietnamese value-add counts as RCEP-origin. The combined RCEP-origin content is \$1,000 — the full product value. The device qualifies for RCEP preferential tariff treatment in Japan, potentially zero tariff on a \$1,000 item that previously faced a 5-10% MFN tariff.

The math: on a production run of 100,000 units, that's \$100 million in electronics. A 5% tariff saved under RCEP = \$5 million annually. The origin stacking provision effectively subsidizes Chinese supply chain integration into every other RCEP member's manufacturing sector — at scale, this shapes factory-location decisions, supplier contracts, and long-term industrial geography across the entire region.

## IPEF: The Toothless Framework

The Indo-Pacific Economic Framework for Prosperity was announced by President Biden at the Tokyo Quad summit in May 2022. It brought together 14 members: the US, Australia, Brunei, Fiji, India, Indonesia, Japan, South Korea, Malaysia, New Zealand, Philippines, Singapore, Thailand, and Vietnam.

On paper, IPEF has four pillars: trade (labor, digital rules, supply chains, clean energy), supply chains (resilience, diversification), clean economy (decarbonization, clean energy deployment), and fair economy (anti-corruption, tax). The member list overlaps significantly with CPTPP — Japan, Australia, New Zealand, Vietnam, Malaysia, Singapore, and Brunei are in both.

**The fundamental problem.** IPEF makes no tariff reduction commitments. None. The Biden administration's position was explicit: IPEF was not a traditional FTA and would not be submitted for Congressional approval. Instead of market access — lower tariffs on goods exported from IPEF members to the US market — IPEF offered supply-chain coordination, digital trade standards, and clean-energy cooperation. These are real things. They are not, however, equivalent to what CPTPP or RCEP offer in terms of commercial incentives.

The consequence is predictable. When Asian trade ministers sat down to assess IPEF's value to their countries, the calculation looked like this: CPTPP offers zero tariffs on most goods exported to Japan, Australia, Canada, and Mexico — locked in law and enforceable under binding dispute settlement. RCEP offers tariff reductions on 90% of goods traded with China, South Korea, and 13 other nations. IPEF offers a voluntary pledge to cooperate on supply chain transparency.

For a Vietnamese textile manufacturer trying to decide which supply chain to join, the comparison is straightforward: CPTPP delivers \$270 million in annual tariff savings on Japan trade alone (see worked example below). IPEF delivers a seat at an advisory table. Countries participate in IPEF to manage the bilateral relationship with the US, not because IPEF makes their exporters more competitive.

**India's IPEF participation.** One interesting dimension of IPEF is India's inclusion. India walked out of RCEP in November 2019, partly fearing competitive pressure from Chinese goods and partly objecting to provisions on dairy and agricultural imports. India's IPEF membership signals a desire to align more closely with the US-centered trade network without the tariff-reciprocity commitments of a traditional FTA. India gets supply-chain partnership and digital trade rules without opening its market to competition under enforceable tariff schedules.

## Winners and Losers: Vietnam, Australia, Malaysia

The trade architecture shift has created clear winners and losers. The pattern is not random — it follows the logic of who is inside which agreements and what those agreements actually deliver.

**Vietnam: the clearest winner.** Vietnam is the single country best positioned by the post-TPP architecture. It is a member of both CPTPP and RCEP — giving it the highest-standard preferential access to Japan, Australia, Canada, and Mexico (via CPTPP) while also benefiting from RCEP's origin stacking with Chinese supply chains. No other major manufacturing nation has this dual membership with the combination of low-cost labor, strategic location, and improving infrastructure that Vietnam offers.

The FDI data confirms this. Vietnam's FDI inflows rose from around \$14 billion in 2016-2017 to over \$23 billion in 2023, driven by electronics manufacturers (Samsung accounts for roughly 25% of Vietnam's total exports), apparel producers, footwear companies, and technology firms moving production from China to capture CPTPP access. Samsung's Vietnam factories alone export over \$60 billion annually.

**Australia: agriculture winner, services laggard.** Australia's CPTPP membership has delivered tangible gains for its export-oriented agricultural sector. Beef, wine, dairy, and seafood now enter Japan, Canada, and other CPTPP markets at preferential rates that put Australian producers ahead of US competitors. Australia's beef exports to Japan have grown while US market share has fallen — a direct, measurable consequence of the tariff architecture.

Where Australia has not benefited is in services exports, where the gains from a comprehensive US-Australia relationship (the kind TPP would have facilitated at a multilateral level) remain unrealized. Financial services, legal services, and professional services are where Australia's most productive export capacity lies, and those sectors depend more on bilateral regulatory agreements than on goods tariff schedules.

**Malaysia: electronics and palm oil.** Malaysia is a CPTPP member, giving its electronics sector (which is substantial — Malaysia produces semiconductors and electronic components at significant scale) preferential access to Japan, Canada, and other CPTPP markets. However, Malaysia's palm oil sector has not benefited as much as hoped: some CPTPP members (Canada, Australia) have domestic oilseed industries that lobbied for protections, and palm oil's environmental reputation creates non-tariff barriers that CPTPP's market access provisions don't fully resolve.

**US: structural loser.** The US has no tariff preferences in any major Asia-Pacific market. US exporters to Japan pay MFN rates. US exporters to Australia pay MFN rates. US manufacturers in China pay Chinese MFN rates to access ASEAN markets without RCEP preferences. The cost disadvantage compounds across every sector that competed against CPTPP or RCEP members: agriculture (beef, pork, grain, dairy), industrials, automotive, financial services.

#### Worked example:

**Vietnam textile CPTPP benefit.** Vietnam's textile and apparel sector exports approximately \$3 billion annually to Japan. Under CPTPP, Vietnamese textiles meeting the "yarn forward" rules of origin (a requirement that yarn must be produced in a CPTPP country) face zero tariff in Japan. Non-CPTPP members — including countries like Bangladesh and Cambodia (which is an ASEAN and RCEP member but not CPTPP) — face Japan's MFN tariff of approximately 8-12% on textiles and apparel.

The calculation: on \$3 billion in textile exports, a 9% average tariff savings (midpoint of the 8-12% MFN rate) = \$270 million per year in tariff cost savings for Vietnamese exporters relative to non-CPTPP competitors. That \$270 million represents competitive margin — either passed through as lower prices that win market share, or retained as profit margin that makes Vietnam more attractive for further investment. Either way, it compounds annually and is not available to US textile producers who also lack CPTPP membership.

The "yarn forward" rule has an additional effect: it incentivizes Vietnam to develop domestic yarn and fabric production to meet CPTPP origin requirements, deepening Vietnamese textile manufacturing integration and reducing reliance on Chinese yarn inputs that would not qualify. This is the standards-upgrading effect that sophisticated trade agreements produce — and that RCEP, with its weaker rules, doesn't fully replicate.

#### Worked example:

**IPEF vs RCEP for Thai electronics.** A Thai electronics manufacturer has a \$50 million annual export contract to supply circuit board assemblies to Japanese automotive companies. Thailand is an RCEP member (as part of ASEAN) but not a CPTPP member. Thailand is also an IPEF participant.

Under RCEP: Japan's tariff on electronics circuit boards is in the process of being phased to zero for RCEP members. At a 10% tariff rate still in the phase-down, the manufacturer saves 10% × \$50 million = \$5 million per year compared to MFN rates. This saving is real, legally binding, and enforceable under RCEP's dispute settlement mechanism.

Under IPEF: No tariff reduction. The Thai manufacturer participates in IPEF's supply chain tracking pilot, which involves reporting raw material sources and sustainability data. There is no price discount at the Japanese customs checkpoint.

The Thai firm's sourcing decisions, factory investments, and supplier relationships are structured around the RCEP tariff schedule. IPEF is a compliance reporting exercise. For any manufacturer making multi-year investment decisions, the difference between a legally binding \$5 million annual tariff saving and a voluntary advisory framework is not subtle — it determines where factories are built and which suppliers are contracted.

## Common Misconceptions

**"RCEP is China's TPP."** This misreads both agreements. TPP was a high-standard deal that specifically excluded China and was designed to be incompatible with China's economic model. RCEP is a shallow deal that China participated in but does not dominate — ASEAN was the founding bloc, and Japan and Australia are also members with interests that sometimes conflict with China's. RCEP did not give China control of Asian trade architecture; it gave China a seat at a multilateral table it previously lacked.

**"CPTPP is just TPP without the US."** Yes, technically — but the "just" is doing a lot of work. Twenty-two provisions from the original TPP text are suspended, mostly the US-demanded IP chapters on pharmaceutical data exclusivity, copyright term extension, and technology protection measures. The suspended provisions matter: they were designed to bring Asia-Pacific IP standards closer to US practice, and their suspension represents a significant weakening of the deal's quality on innovation-sector terms. CPTPP is a substantial agreement, but it is genuinely different from what TPP would have been with US participation and full IP provisions in force.

**"The US can just rejoin CPTPP."** In principle, yes — Article 5.4 allows new members. In practice, rejoining would require Senate ratification, which requires 67 votes for a treaty or a congressional-executive agreement at simple majority in both chambers. Given the domestic political dynamics around trade since 2016, this is not imminent. It would also require reopening some of the suspended provisions, negotiating accession terms, and navigating the opposition of domestic agricultural and manufacturing interests that prefer protection. US rejoining is a non-trivial political undertaking even under the most favorable administration.

**"India's RCEP exit was a mistake."** India's calculus was specific: it feared that RCEP's origin rules would allow Chinese goods to flood Indian markets via ASEAN intermediaries, undercutting domestic manufacturing. India also had specific concerns about dairy (New Zealand and Australia wanted dairy market access India wasn't prepared to give) and services (India wanted labor mobility provisions that other members rejected). Whether India made the right call depends heavily on counterfactual assumptions about Chinese competitive impact that remain genuinely uncertain.

**"IPEF is useless."** IPEF is weaker than a tariff-cutting FTA, but it is not literally nothing. Its supply chain resilience pillar has produced some real coordination on semiconductor supply chain transparency. Its clean economy pillar has begun to align standards on clean energy certification. The problem is that these achievements are minor compared to the market-access gap IPEF was supposed to address. Framing IPEF as a geopolitical substitute for market access is like framing a conversation about gym membership as a substitute for actually exercising.

## How It Shows Up in Real Markets

**Agricultural commodity flows.** The most direct real-world indicator of trade architecture effects is in agricultural trade statistics. Japanese beef imports from Australia have risen since CPTPP; from the US they have stagnated or declined. New Zealand dairy exports to Japan have grown. These are not coincidences — they are direct consequences of tariff differentials that compound annually.

**Southeast Asian equity markets.** Vietnamese equities (VN-Index) have outperformed many regional peers partly on the FDI story and partly on export earnings growth driven by CPTPP market access. Malaysian electronics sector equities have similarly benefited from CPTPP positioning. The correlation between trade architecture developments and equity performance is not perfect — other macro factors dominate in any given quarter — but over multi-year periods the architecture signal is visible.

**Manufacturing FDI announcements.** When a major electronics manufacturer announces a new factory, the location choice often reveals the trade architecture logic. Samsung's expansion in Vietnam, Apple's Foxconn-linked Vietnamese production ramp, Nike's Vietnam/Indonesia supply chain build-out — each represents billions of dollars of capital following the CPTPP market access signal. FDI announcements are leading indicators of the tariff architecture's economic weight.

**Currency moves on trade news.** AUD/USD is the most liquid proxy for CPTPP/RCEP trade dynamics: Australia is a CPTPP and RCEP member, heavily exposed to Asian goods trade, and the AUD is deep and liquid. The January 2017 TPP withdrawal shock was visible in AUD. CPTPP signing in March 2018 provided mild support. RCEP signing in November 2020 was less impactful for AUD specifically, since Australia was already a RCEP member by that point. Future CPTPP expansions (UK accession, any US re-entry discussion) would be AUD-positive events.

**US agricultural export earnings.** US beef, pork, and agricultural sector revenue trends in Asia-Pacific markets directly reflect the tariff architecture. USDA data shows Japanese beef imports from the US declining as a share of total Japanese beef imports in the CPTPP era, while Australian market share has grown. This is a policy-driven market share transfer, not a competitiveness story — and it persists until the policy architecture changes.

## How to Trade It: The Playbook

Trade architecture shifts play out over multi-year horizons, not in single trading sessions. The playbook has to match that timeline.

**Long Vietnam exposure.** Vietnam's dual CPTPP/RCEP membership, combined with its FDI-attracting fundamentals, makes it the clearest structural winner. Instruments: VN-Index (Ho Chi Minh Stock Exchange), Vietnam country ETFs, or individual equities in the electronics and manufacturing sectors. The FDI story is partly priced, but the continuing supply chain migration from China to Vietnam — driven by geopolitical risk diversification as much as CPTPP tariff economics — suggests the trend has further to run.

**Long Australian agricultural exporters.** Companies with significant Japan beef, dairy, or wine exposure benefit from CPTPP's phased tariff reductions. The tariff schedule continues to reduce over the 16-year phase-down period, so the competitive advantage versus the US compounds annually. Australian Agricultural Company (AACo), Elders, and similar names are direct plays. The AUD itself is a currency expression of this exposure.

**Short US agricultural exporters with Japan exposure.** US beef processors with significant Japan exposure — Tyson, Cargill (private), Marfrig's US assets — face structural margin compression as their Australian competitors' tariff advantage grows. This is a gradual sector headwind, not an overnight event, but it is persistent and policy-driven.

**ASEAN manufacturing equities.** Companies in Malaysia, Thailand, Vietnam, and Indonesia that are integrating into RCEP supply chains — particularly electronics, automotive parts, and consumer goods — benefit from origin stacking that makes Chinese inputs cheaper and RCEP market access wider. Thailand's electronics sector (Hana Microelectronics, KCE Electronics), Malaysia's semiconductor assemblers, and Indonesia's downstream manufacturing are sector themes here.

**FX: AUD as CPTPP proxy.** Long AUD positions benefit from both CPTPP's goods tariff advantages for Australian exporters and from RCEP's inclusion of Australia alongside China — giving Australia access to preferential rates in the world's largest consumer market while maintaining its high-standard CPTPP positioning. AUD/JPY is an interesting cross: it captures the relative benefit of Australia's CPTPP access to Japan relative to JPY's domestic export economy.

**Watch for re-entry signals.** The most powerful upside scenario for the existing architecture is US re-entry to CPTPP. Any serious political signal of US re-entry — a presidential commitment, a congressional hearing, a trade office consultation — would reprice the entire architecture. AUD, NZD, Vietnam equities, Japan trade-exposed sectors (auto, machinery) would all move. The probability is low but non-zero under any administration that faces a long-term Asia competition problem and the political will to overcome domestic trade skepticism.

**IPEF disappointment as base case.** Position around IPEF's limitations rather than its aspirations. Countries that have forgo RCEP benefits to prioritize IPEF alignment will find themselves with the worst of both worlds: weaker supply chain integration and no tariff advantages. India is the clearest case — outside RCEP and receiving no tariff benefits from IPEF. Indian manufacturing equities face a structural cost disadvantage in ASEAN markets compared to RCEP members.

The TPP withdrawal was not primarily a trade decision. It was a political signal that the US would no longer pay the domestic costs of global institutional leadership. Understanding that signal — and tracking its downstream institutional consequences — is what distinguishes a geopolitically-informed investor from one who is purely reactive to headline events. The architecture built in the seven years after January 20, 2017 will shape Asian trade flows for the next two decades. The tariff wedges are locked in law. The FDI is in concrete. The supply chains are contracted. The investor who understood this in 2017 was positioned for one of the clearest structural trades of the decade.
