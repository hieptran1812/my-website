> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — You cannot predict the future, but you can attach honest probabilities to it, and that is most of the edge in trading geopolitics.
>
> - Geopolitical crises are not coin flips. Each crisis type has a measurable historical base rate: military posturing leads to actual armed conflict about 17% of the time, trade disputes escalate to a full tariff war about 38% of the time, and nuclear standoffs have produced zero uses of a weapon between nuclear-armed states in 78 years.
> - The forecasting process is mechanical: start from a base rate, adjust with structured historical analogies, build a three-scenario tree with explicit asset payoffs, calibrate against prediction markets, then update only on genuinely new and irreversible information.
> - The trade is rarely the forecast itself. The trade is the gap between your calibrated probability and what the market has actually priced. When a prediction market says 70% and oil has barely moved, that gap is the position.
> - The single number to remember: a forecaster who consistently beats the crowd is right about as often as their stated confidence. If you say "70%" ten times, you should be right about seven times. That property is called calibration, and it is the whole game.

In late January 2022, a prediction market called Polymarket showed the probability of Russia invading Ukraine before the end of March climbing past 70%. At that same moment, Brent crude was trading around \$86 a barrel — barely above where it had been in early January, and far below where a serious war on Europe's eastern flank would push it. One market was screaming. The other was whispering. By February 24, the day the tanks crossed the border, Brent was at \$96 and on its way to \$120. The gap between the two markets in late January was, in hindsight, one of the cleanest geopolitical trades of the decade.

That gap did not require a crystal ball. It required something more boring and more learnable: a structured way to turn ambiguous geopolitical signals into a number, and the discipline to compare that number to what assets had already priced. Most investors approach geopolitics the way most people approach the weather — with a vague feeling, a recent headline, and a story. The analysts who consistently do better treat it the way an actuary treats mortality: as a domain with patterns, base rates, and updatable probabilities.

This post is the toolkit. It is the same probabilistic apparatus that intelligence analysts and professional forecasters use, translated for someone who wants to position a portfolio rather than brief a head of state. We will build it from the ground up — what a base rate is, how to pick the right historical analogy, how to draw a scenario tree that maps to asset prices, how to use prediction markets as a check on your own bias, and how to update a probability without fooling yourself. By the end, you will be able to point this machinery at any live situation in the news and produce a defensible, tradeable view.

## Foundations: Why Geopolitical Forecasting Is Possible (But Different)

Start with the myth, because it is the thing you have to clear away before anything else works. The myth says geopolitics is fundamentally unpredictable — a chaos of human decisions, accidents, and black swans that no framework can tame. People who believe this myth do one of two things: they either refuse to forecast at all (and so get blindsided), or they forecast purely on narrative and emotion (and so get blindsided differently).

The myth is half right. You genuinely cannot know whether China will move on Taiwan in a specific year, or whether a specific coup will succeed. What you *can* know is the rate at which situations *like* this one have resolved one way or the other across history. That distinction — between predicting a specific outcome and estimating a probability over a class of outcomes — is the entire foundation. A weather forecaster does not know if it will rain on your wedding. They know that on days that look like this one, with this pressure system and this humidity, it rained 40% of the time. That is a forecast, and it is useful, and it is honest.

### The skill versus luck question

For decades the conventional wisdom, popularized by the political scientist Philip Tetlock's early research, was that expert political forecasters were no better than "a dart-throwing chimpanzee." Pundits who were confident and famous were, on average, *worse* than simple statistical baselines. This was demoralizing but incomplete. Tetlock's later work — the Good Judgment Project, a multi-year forecasting tournament run for the U.S. intelligence community — found something more hopeful. A small fraction of ordinary people, with no special access to classified information, consistently and significantly outperformed everyone else, including intelligence analysts with security clearances. He called them *superforecasters*.

The superforecasters were not smarter in any dramatic way, and they were not domain experts. What separated them was *method*. They thought in probabilities rather than certainties. They started from the outside view (the base rate) before considering the specifics of the case. They updated their forecasts frequently and in small increments as news arrived. They were comfortable saying "63%" rather than "likely," and they kept score so they could learn from being wrong. The good news for an investor is that none of this is innate talent. It is a discipline you can copy.

### Why geopolitics is different from economic forecasting

It is worth being honest about how this differs from the kind of forecasting you may already know. Economic forecasting has *models with error bars*. If you want to forecast next quarter's inflation, you have decades of data, well-understood mechanical relationships, and a literature full of equations that produce a central estimate and a confidence interval. You can be wrong, but you are wrong *inside a structure*.

Geopolitics has almost none of that. There is no equation that takes "Chinese troop movements" as an input and outputs "probability of invasion." The events are rare, the actors are strategic (they hide their intentions and bluff), and each situation is partly unique. This is why geopolitical forecasting leans so heavily on *reference classes* — collections of past events similar enough to the current one to serve as a statistical anchor — rather than on causal models. You are not running a simulation of the future. You are asking: of the situations that rhymed with this one, how often did each thing happen? That is a weaker tool than an economic model, but used carefully it is dramatically better than a gut feeling, and it has the enormous virtue of being *checkable*.

There is one more structural feature worth naming. Many geopolitical crises have a precursor window. Wars between states are rarely launched from a standing start; they are preceded by weeks or months of mobilization, rhetoric, alliance signaling, and economic positioning that leave a detectable trail. The companion post on reading precursor signals covers this in depth, but the relevant point here is that the window exists. It is what makes probabilistic forecasting actionable rather than academic — you usually get time to update before the event resolves.

### What "good" actually means: calibration and the scoring rule

Before going further, it is worth pinning down what it even means for a forecast to be good, because the intuitive answer is wrong. The intuitive answer is "a good forecaster is right a lot." But "right" is meaningless for a probabilistic forecast. If you say there is a 30% chance of war and war happens, were you wrong? No — you said it was *possible*, just less likely than not. You cannot judge a single probabilistic forecast at all. You can only judge a *track record* of them.

The property that matters is *calibration*. A forecaster is calibrated if, across all the times they said "70%," the thing happened about 70% of the time; across all the times they said "20%," it happened about 20% of the time. Calibration is checkable, it is learnable, and it is the single most important quality in a forecaster. A calibrated forecaster who says "60%" is handing you a genuinely useful number, because you can size a position against it and trust that, over many such situations, the probabilities will play out. An uncalibrated forecaster who is always 90% confident is worse than useless, because their confidence carries no information.

The standard tool for measuring this is the *Brier score*, and it is simple enough to compute by hand. For each forecast, you take the probability you assigned to the outcome that actually happened, subtract it from 1 if the event occurred (or use the probability directly if it did not), square the difference, and average across all your forecasts. A lower Brier score is better; a perfect forecaster scores 0, and a forecaster who always says 50% on a coin flip scores 0.25. The reason this matters for an investor is that the Brier score *rewards calibration and punishes overconfidence simultaneously* — you cannot game it by always sounding confident. Keeping a running Brier score on your own geopolitical calls is the discipline that converts forecasting from a feeling into a skill, because it gives you the feedback loop without which no skill improves. The superforecasters were, above all, people who kept score and learned from it.

## The political moves: How Forecasters Actually Think

Before the tools, the mindset, because the tools fail without it. The defining move of a good forecaster is what Tetlock calls taking the *outside view first*. When a vivid situation lands — troops massing, a missile test, a tariff threat — the instinct is to dive immediately into the specifics: this leader's psychology, this week's headlines, this particular grievance. That is the *inside view*, and it is where almost all forecasting errors are born, because the specifics feel so compelling that they crowd out the base rate entirely.

The outside view forces a different opening question: *what reference class is this, and what usually happens in that class?* Only after you have anchored on that number do you let the specifics of the case push you up or down from it. A forecaster who starts at the base rate of 17% for "military posturing leading to armed conflict" and then adjusts is far harder to fool than one who starts from a blank slate and lets a frightening headline set the number at 80%.

The second move is *frequent, small updates*. Bad forecasters set a view and defend it. Good forecasters treat every forecast as provisional and nudge it as evidence arrives — up two points here, down five there. The third move is *calibrated confidence*: matching the strength of your claim to the strength of your evidence, and being willing to live at 55% or 45% rather than collapsing every question into a confident yes or no. The fourth is *keeping score*, because a probability you never check against reality is just a feeling with a decimal point.

The contrast is stark once you see it laid out. On the left is the way most market commentary works: a vivid story drives the view, it collapses into a single confident point estimate, there is no anchor to compare it against, and it is rarely revisited until the event forces a reckoning. On the right is the structured process: it begins from a base rate, adjusts by analogy, calibrates against an external market, and updates as new data arrives. The left feels like insight. The right *is* insight, and the difference shows up in the track record.

## The financial channels: Base Rates as the Starting Point

Now the first concrete tool. A base rate is the historical frequency of an outcome within a reference class. It is the answer to "how often does *this kind of thing* happen?" and it is the single highest-leverage input in the entire process, because it is the anchor that everything else adjusts from.

Here are real, defensible base rates for the crisis types that matter most to markets. These are compiled from the standard quantitative international-relations datasets — the Correlates of War project, its Militarized Interstate Disputes data, the sanctions literature, and the scholarship on coups — and they are the numbers a careful analyst would start from.

![Horizontal bar chart of historical base rates for crisis escalation ranging from 42 percent for emerging market debt default down to 1 percent for nuclear use](/imgs/blogs/forecasting-geopolitical-events-frameworks-base-rates-and-scenarios-5.png)

Read each bar as a starting probability, not a verdict. Military posturing — troop movements, exercises, mobilization rhetoric — escalates into actual armed conflict roughly 17% of the time. Most saber-rattling is exactly that: a signal sent for domestic or diplomatic purposes that resolves without a shot. Trade disputes escalate into a full tariff war about 38% of the time; most are negotiated down, but a meaningful minority spiral. Sanctions achieve their stated political objective — actually changing the target's behavior — only about 25–30% of the time, and even that figure is generous because it counts partial successes. Coup attempts succeed about half the time, but only about 15% produce a stable government two years later. And nuclear standoffs have, in 78 years of nuclear-armed states confronting each other, produced exactly zero uses of a nuclear weapon in anger — a base rate of effectively 0% that nonetheless carries a tail risk no rational forecaster ignores.

### The cry-wolf discount

Base rates are not static. They shift as a situation repeats, and the most important shift is what we can call the *cry-wolf discount*. When an actor threatens something repeatedly and repeatedly does not follow through, the market learns to discount the threat, and the effective base rate for that specific actor falls below the generic one.

North Korea is the textbook case. Pyongyang has conducted missile tests, nuclear tests, and bellicose statements on a near-annual cadence for two decades. Early in that history, each test moved Korean and Japanese markets meaningfully. By now, the market has internalized the base rate: these are signaling events, not preludes to war, and the price reaction has shrunk to near zero. The companion post on North Korea escalations covers this dynamic in detail. The forecasting lesson is double-edged. The cry-wolf discount is usually correct — the boy who cries wolf is usually lying. But the original fable ends with a real wolf, and the discount is exactly what makes the eventual real event so violently mispriced. A disciplined forecaster applies the discount while keeping a small, explicit probability reserved for the day the wolf is real.

#### Worked example:

Suppose you want a base-rate-anchored probability for Chinese military action against Taiwan in a given calendar year as of 2024. Begin with the outside view. The reference class is "a major power with a revisionist territorial claim, facing a smaller entity under the security umbrella of a rival superpower." Across the post-1945 period, the rate at which such standoffs converted into a full invasion *in any given year* is low — these situations persist for decades far more often than they resolve violently. A reasonable reference-class base rate for invasion in a specific year is on the order of 3–5%.

Now adjust. China has been escalating military exercises and rhetoric — that pushes you up. But the cry-wolf discount applies: incursions into Taiwan's air defense identification zone have become routine, hundreds per year, with no escalation, which means the market has learned to treat them as signaling. The cost of an invasion to China — the near-certain destruction of its semiconductor supply, the freezing of its dollar reserves, and a likely U.S. military response — is enormous and well understood by Beijing, which pushes you down. Netting these, a defensible 2024 estimate for a full invasion *in that specific year* lands around 3–4%, with the bulk of the remaining probability mass sitting in "continued grey-zone pressure" rather than "war." The single most common error here is to let the vividness of the worst case drag the number to 20% or 30%. The base rate is your defense against that.

The discipline is this: write the base rate down *before* you read today's news. If you find your final number is wildly different from the base rate, you should be able to articulate exactly which case-specific factor justifies the gap. If you cannot, the gap is your bias, not your insight.

### Constructing the reference class is the hard part

The base-rate chart makes it look as though the reference class is handed to you. It is not. The hardest and most consequential judgment in the entire process is deciding *which* historical events belong in the reference class for the situation in front of you, because the same event can be filed under several classes with very different base rates.

Consider the 2025 tariff escalation. You could file it under "trade dispute," with a 38% base rate of escalating to a full tariff war. Or under "U.S. policy action with a 90-day negotiating window built in," where the base rate of permanent escalation is far lower because the structure of the threat signals an intent to negotiate. Or under "great-power economic confrontation between integrated economies," where the deep mutual dependence creates a strong incentive to settle and the base rate of a permanent rupture is lower still. Three defensible reference classes, three different anchors. The skill is to build the class narrowly enough that the members genuinely resemble the case, but broadly enough that you have enough members to compute a stable rate. Too narrow ("U.S.-China semiconductor tariff disputes during an AI race") and your sample size is one, which is no base rate at all. Too broad ("international disagreements") and the rate is meaningless. The superforecaster's habit is to compute the base rate under *several* plausible reference classes and see how much the answer moves. If all three classes give roughly the same number, you can lean on it. If they diverge wildly, that divergence is itself information: it tells you the outcome hinges on which features of the situation dominate, and that is exactly where you should focus your case-specific analysis.

## Structured Analogy: Finding the Right Historical Comparison

The base rate gives you a generic anchor. The next tool sharpens it by asking a more specific question: which past events most resemble *this* one, and how did they turn out? This is structured analogy, and it is both the most powerful and the most dangerous tool in the kit, because choosing the wrong analogy is the single most common forecasting error in all of geopolitics.

The danger is that analogies are persuasive precisely when they are misleading. The 2022 invasion of Ukraine was widely under-forecast in part because many analysts anchored on the wrong precedent. They compared it to Crimea 2014 — a fast, nearly bloodless, limited annexation that the West largely accepted — and concluded that any further Russian action would be similarly limited and similarly tolerated. The better analogies were darker: Georgia 2008 (a full conventional invasion of a neighbor) or even the Winter War of 1939 (a great power launching a full-scale assault on a smaller neighbor it expected to fold quickly). The analysts who reached for Crimea got the magnitude wrong by an order of magnitude.

### The methodology

Structured analogy fixes the cherry-picking problem by forcing rigor. The process has five steps. First, list several candidate analogies — not one, several, including ones that point in different directions. Second, define the dimensions along which similarity actually matters for the outcome. Third, score each analogy on each dimension. Fourth, weight the analogies by their overall similarity. Fifth, take the similarity-weighted average of the outcomes as your estimate. The point is not that the arithmetic is sacred. The point is that the *process* drags your hidden assumptions into the open where you can argue about them.

#### Worked example:

Let us build a structured analogy for the probability that a Taiwan standoff converts into a full Chinese invasion (as opposed to a blockade, a grey-zone campaign, or continued stalemate). We choose five historical analogies and four similarity dimensions.

The dimensions are: power differential between attacker and defender, economic interdependence between the two sides, domestic political pressure on the would-be aggressor to act, and the degree of international isolation the aggressor would face. We score each analogy from 1 to 5 on how *similar* it is to the Taiwan case on that dimension (5 = very similar), and we record whether that historical episode actually produced a full invasion.

| Analogy | Power diff | Econ interdep | Domestic pressure | Intl isolation | Similarity (avg) | Invasion? |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Korea 1950 | 3 | 1 | 4 | 4 | 3.0 | Yes |
| Falklands 1982 | 4 | 2 | 5 | 3 | 3.5 | Yes |
| Kuwait 1990 | 5 | 2 | 4 | 3 | 3.5 | Yes |
| Crimea 2014 | 4 | 3 | 4 | 2 | 3.25 | Partial |
| Sino-Indian 1962 | 4 | 1 | 3 | 3 | 2.75 | No (border, limited) |

Now the arithmetic. Three of the five analogies produced a full invasion, one a partial annexation, one a limited border war. If we score "Yes" as 1.0, "Partial" as 0.5, and "No" as 0.0, and weight by the similarity score, we compute a weighted average. The weighted sum of outcomes is (3.0×1.0) + (3.5×1.0) + (3.5×1.0) + (3.25×0.5) + (2.75×0.0) = 3.0 + 3.5 + 3.5 + 1.625 + 0 = 11.625. The sum of weights is 3.0 + 3.5 + 3.5 + 3.25 + 2.75 = 16.0. The similarity-weighted outcome is 11.625 / 16.0 = 0.727.

Read carefully, because this number is a trap if you take it at face value. It says that *conditional on a standoff resolving into action at all*, the historical pattern leans heavily toward full invasion rather than a limited move — roughly 73%. It does *not* say there is a 73% chance of an invasion. The analogies are all cases where something already happened; they tell you about the *character* of the action given that the dam breaks, not the *probability* that it breaks. You combine this with the base rate from the previous section. If the annual probability of *any* major action is 4%, and the conditional character of that action skews 73% toward "full invasion," then the annual probability of a full invasion specifically is roughly 0.04 × 0.73 ≈ 3%, with the remainder split across blockade and limited action. The structured analogy refined the *shape* of the risk without inflating its *size*. That is exactly what it is supposed to do.

The most valuable habit this builds is the deliberate inclusion of analogies that disagree. Sino-Indian 1962 — a great power that chose a limited, bloody-nose border war over a full invasion and then withdrew — is the analogy a bullish-on-stability analyst would reach for, and including it keeps the estimate honest. If every analogy on your list points the same way, you have not done structured analogy; you have done motivated reasoning with extra steps.

## Scenario Trees: The Three-Scenario Framework

Base rates and analogies give you point estimates. But a market does not move to a point estimate — it moves across a distribution of possible futures. The scenario tree is the tool that turns a probability into a portfolio decision by spelling out the discrete futures, their probabilities, and what each one does to asset prices.

The standard structure is three branches: a *baseline* that captures the most likely continuation of the status quo, an *escalation* branch for the situation getting materially worse, and a *de-escalation* branch for it getting materially better. A common starting weight, before any case-specific adjustment, is something like 60% baseline, 25% escalation, 15% de-escalation — but those numbers should be earned by base rates and analogies, never assumed. The discipline of three branches is deliberate: two branches collapse into a binary and lose the texture of the middle case, while five or six branches create false precision you cannot actually justify.

The crucial step that separates a useful scenario tree from a creative-writing exercise is defining, in advance, the *specific observable events* that would confirm each branch. A scenario you cannot attach observable triggers to is not a forecast — it is a mood. For an escalation branch, the triggers are the red flags: a named threshold the situation must cross. For de-escalation, the green flags. Writing these down before the event lets you update mechanically rather than emotionally when news breaks.

### Mapping scenarios to assets

Each branch then gets mapped to asset prices. For a Middle East energy crisis, the relevant assets are oil, gold, equities (especially emerging-market equities, which are most exposed), safe-haven currencies, and the dollar. For each scenario you estimate the return on each asset, multiply by the scenario's probability, and sum to get the *expected value* — the probability-weighted return that tells you how to size a position. The companion post on the asset-sensitivity matrix covers which assets move in which crisis type; here the focus is on the arithmetic that turns those sensitivities into a number.

![Iran nuclear scenario tree chart with three branches diplomacy holds at 45 percent with oil 70 to 80 dollars enrichment at 35 percent with oil 90 to 110 dollars and strike at 20 percent with oil 130 to 160 dollars](/imgs/blogs/forecasting-geopolitical-events-frameworks-base-rates-and-scenarios-6.png)

#### Worked example:

Build the full scenario tree for the 2025 Iran nuclear situation, focused on oil, and use it to size a position. The three branches and their case-adjusted probabilities are: *diplomacy holds* (a deal or stable standoff keeps enrichment capped) at 45%, *Iran enriches to weapons-grade* (90% enrichment, no immediate strike) at 35%, and *a U.S. or Israeli military strike* on Iranian facilities at 20%.

Now the oil price under each. Brent is trading near \$75 as the base case. If diplomacy holds, the risk premium bleeds out and oil settles in the \$70–80 range; call the midpoint \$75. If Iran enriches to weapons-grade, the risk premium rises sharply on the prospect of confrontation and oil runs \$90–110; midpoint \$100. If there is an actual strike — with the attendant risk to the Strait of Hormuz, through which roughly a fifth of seaborne oil passes — oil spikes to \$130–160; midpoint \$145.

The probability-weighted expected oil price is: (0.45 × \$75) + (0.35 × \$100) + (0.20 × \$145) = \$33.75 + \$35.00 + \$29.00 = \$97.75, call it \$98. Against a spot price of \$75, the scenario tree implies oil is *undervalued by roughly 30%* if your probabilities are right and the market is pricing closer to the diplomacy case alone.

That gap is the trade, but it must be sized for the distribution, not the average. The expected value is \$98, but the *path* matters: 45% of the time you lose money (oil drifts to \$75 or below), 55% of the time you make it, and 20% of the time you make a fortune. This is a positively skewed, asymmetric bet — exactly the shape you want to express through options rather than a linear position, because a call option on oil caps your loss at the premium while keeping the violent upside of the strike scenario. If you put \$100,000 of premium into out-of-the-money oil calls and the strike scenario hits, the position can return several multiples; if diplomacy holds, you lose the premium and no more. The scenario tree did not tell you oil will go up. It told you the *shape* of the bet and therefore the right *instrument* to express it. That is the payoff of the whole exercise.

The red-flag and green-flag list for this tree writes itself once the branches are defined. Red flags that shift weight toward the strike branch: an IAEA report that Iran has crossed a specific enrichment threshold, the withdrawal of IAEA inspectors, an Israeli mobilization of long-range strike assets, or the repositioning of U.S. carrier groups into the region. Green flags that shift weight toward diplomacy: a resumption of formal negotiations, a verified pause in enrichment, or a sanctions-relief gesture. You do not have to guess when to update. You wrote the triggers down in advance, and when one fires, you move the probability.

## Prediction Markets as Calibration

You now have a probability built from base rates, sharpened by analogy, and structured into a scenario tree. The natural next question is: how do you know if your number is any good *before* the event resolves? The answer is to compare it against a market of other forecasters putting money or reputation behind their own numbers. That is what prediction markets are for.

A prediction market is an exchange where contracts pay out based on whether a specified event occurs. A contract that pays \$1 if "Russia invades Ukraine before March 2022" and \$0 otherwise will trade at a price that, in an efficient market, equals the crowd's probability estimate — a price of \$0.70 implies a 70% probability. Several venues exist. Polymarket is a large crypto-based exchange with real money and deep liquidity on major geopolitical questions. Metaculus is a reputation-based platform favored by serious forecasters, with a strong track record on well-specified questions. Manifold Markets uses play money but attracts a sharp crowd. Good Judgment Open is the public arm of the superforecasting research and is especially strong on geopolitical questions.

### How to actually use them

The mistake is to treat the prediction market as the answer. It is not. It is a *calibration check* — a mirror you hold up to your own estimate to find out whether a divergence is information or bias. The procedure is simple and humbling. Build your own probability first, independently, so you are not anchored. Then look at the market. If you said 40% and the market says 20%, stop and interrogate the gap. Do you possess information the crowd lacks — a better analogy, a signal others are ignoring? Or are you simply more frightened than the evidence warrants, anchored on a vivid story the crowd has already discounted? Most of the time, honest interrogation reveals that the crowd is right and you are biased. Occasionally it reveals a genuine edge. Either way, you learn something.

Prediction markets are not omniscient. They are most accurate on frequent, binary, well-specified questions with deep liquidity — elections, scheduled decisions, clear yes/no outcomes. They fail on exactly the events that matter most for tail-risk hedging: rare, catastrophic, hard-to-specify events with thin liquidity, where a handful of participants can move the price and the crowd has no base rate to anchor on. A Polymarket contract on "will a nuclear weapon be used in 2026" is nearly useless as a probability estimate because the market is thin, the event is unprecedented, and the few traders are mostly expressing fear rather than analysis. Know which regime you are in.

### The trade hiding in the gap

The most valuable use of prediction markets for an investor is not calibrating against other forecasters — it is calibrating against the *actual asset markets*. When a prediction market has moved but the relevant asset has not, you have found a divergence that is directly tradeable, because one of the two markets is lagging.

#### Worked example:

Return to late January 2022. The aggregated prediction markets for "Russia invades Ukraine before end of March" had climbed to about 70%. Brent crude was trading around \$86 — up modestly from \$77 in December, but nowhere near pricing a full European land war. The historical base rate for what a major-power invasion does to oil, drawn from the curated event data, is a move on the order of \$15–25 a barrel within weeks as the risk premium gets repriced.

![Dual axis time series showing prediction market invasion probability rising from 10 percent in October 2021 to 100 percent in February 2022 while Brent crude lagged](/imgs/blogs/forecasting-geopolitical-events-frameworks-base-rates-and-scenarios-7.png)

The expected-value calculation is direct. The prediction market says 70% probability of invasion. The conditional oil move given invasion is roughly +\$20 (a blend of the immediate spike and the sustained premium). The expected oil move attributable to this risk is therefore 0.70 × \$20 ≈ \$14. If oil has only moved up \$9 from its December low and the risk-adjusted fair move is \$14-plus on top of the pre-crisis baseline, the asset market is *underpricing* the risk the prediction market is flagging. That is a long-oil setup with a clean catalyst and a defined invalidation: if the prediction market probability falls (de-escalation), you exit. As the chart shows, the prediction market led Brent by roughly 30 days — the probability hit 70% in late January while oil did not fully reprice until mid-to-late February. That 30-day lead was the window.

The general principle: prediction markets aggregate information faster than commodity and equity markets reprice geopolitical risk, because the prediction market's *entire job* is to price the event, while the oil market is busy pricing a hundred other things. When the two diverge, the asset market is usually the laggard, and the lag is your entry.

## Probability Updating: How to Change Your Mind Correctly

Forecasting is not a one-time act. A forecast made in October and never revised is worthless by February. The skill that separates superforecasters from everyone else, more than any other, is *updating* — changing the probability correctly as new evidence arrives. Done well it is a quiet superpower. Done badly it is how you get whipsawed by every headline.

The formal name for correct updating is Bayesian reasoning, and in plain language it is this: you have a *prior* probability (your current best estimate), new evidence arrives, and you produce a *posterior* probability that blends the two in proportion to how strong the evidence is. You do not throw away your prior and start fresh every time a headline drops — that is overreaction. You do not ignore the evidence and cling to your prior — that is anchoring. You nudge, in proportion to how much the new evidence actually changes the picture.

### The cardinal rule: update on new information, not repetition

The most important and most violated rule of updating is this: *update on genuinely new information, not on the repetition of old information.* Markets and forecasters constantly re-react to the same fact dressed in a new headline. If "China conducts military exercises near Taiwan" was already in your model, then the fourth round of exercises this year is not news and should move your probability by almost nothing. But the human brain treats vividness as information, so each fresh headline *feels* like an update even when it carries zero new content. The discipline is to ask of every piece of news: does this tell me something I did not already know, or is it a re-skin of a fact I already priced?

### The reversibility check

A second discipline guards against the opposite error — overreacting to dramatic but cheap signals. Before you move a probability sharply upward on an escalation signal, ask: *could this signal be reversed in thirty days at low cost?* Rhetoric is fully reversible — a leader can rage on Monday and negotiate on Friday, so bluster should barely move your number. Troop movements are partly reversible — expensive to mount, but they can stand down. But certain signals are *commitments*: actions so costly or irreversible that taking them only makes sense if the actor intends to follow through. Field hospitals and stockpiled blood supplies near a border are a commitment signal — you do not build them as a bluff. Recognizing a breakaway region as independent is a commitment — it burns the diplomatic bridge. The reversibility check is how you weight a signal: reversible signals get small updates, commitment signals get large ones.

#### Worked example:

Trace the Ukraine probability through the winter of 2021–2022 the way a disciplined forecaster would have, and watch how the reversibility check governs each step. Start in October 2021 with a prior of 10% — the base rate for a major invasion in a given window, lightly elevated by the early buildup. In December, intelligence reports put 100,000 troops massed at the border. Troops are partly reversible (Russia had massed and stood down before, in spring 2021), so this is a meaningful but not decisive update: move to about 25%.

In January, reports arrive of field hospitals being established and blood supplies stockpiled near the border. This is a *commitment* signal — you do not provision for casualties as a bluff — so it warrants a large update: jump to about 40%. Through early February, Western governments begin evacuating their embassies from Kyiv, a costly and reputationally awkward move they would not make on a whim: update to about 60%. On February 21, Russia formally recognizes the Donetsk and Luhansk breakaway regions as independent states — an act that incinerates the diplomatic off-ramp and only makes sense as a prelude to action: update to about 80%. On February 24, the invasion begins: the probability resolves to 100%.

Notice what made this sequence correct rather than a panicked ramp. Each update was triggered by a *new* and increasingly *irreversible* signal, not by the tenth restatement of "tensions are high." A forecaster who had instead jumped to 70% in December on the strength of dramatic headlines, then second-guessed back down to 30% when a diplomatic meeting was announced, then up again, would have been whipsawed into terrible position management. The smooth, evidence-gated climb — 10, 25, 40, 60, 80, 100 — is what calibrated updating looks like, and it is what let the disciplined forecaster be positioned long oil and long defense before the crowd, while the cry-wolf skeptics were still discounting it as more of the same.

### Scoring yourself: the feedback loop that builds the skill

Updating well is only half the discipline. The other half is checking, after the fact, whether your probabilities were honest — and that is where the Brier score from the Foundations section earns its keep. Without scoring, you will remember your good calls and forget your bad ones, and your sense of your own accuracy will drift steadily upward regardless of reality. The score is the antidote.

#### Worked example:

Suppose over a year you make four geopolitical forecasts and you record the probability you assigned to the event happening, then check what actually occurred. Forecast 1: 80% chance of a Ukraine invasion — it happened, so the outcome is 1. Forecast 2: 12% chance of a Chinese invasion of Taiwan that year — it did not happen, outcome 0. Forecast 3: 70% chance of further US-China tech-war escalation — it happened, outcome 1. Forecast 4: 45% chance of an Iran deal — it did not happen, outcome 0.

The Brier score is the average squared error between your probability and the outcome. For each: forecast 1 is (0.80 − 1)² = 0.04; forecast 2 is (0.12 − 0)² = 0.0144; forecast 3 is (0.70 − 1)² = 0.09; forecast 4 is (0.45 − 0)² = 0.2025. The average is (0.04 + 0.0144 + 0.09 + 0.2025) / 4 = 0.3469 / 4 = 0.087. A Brier score of 0.087 is excellent — for reference, always guessing 50% on every question scores 0.25, and a perfectly calibrated and informed forecaster approaches 0. The score tells you something a win-loss tally never could: forecast 4 (the Iran deal at 45%) was your worst single call, not because 45% was an unreasonable estimate, but because the outcome went the other way and you can now ask whether you were anchored on a diplomatic narrative the evidence did not support. Run this exercise across a year of calls and the pattern of your errors — too confident on conflicts you follow closely, too timid on regions you ignore — becomes visible and fixable. That visibility is the entire point. A forecast you never score is a forecast you never learn from.

## Common misconceptions

Five myths cost investors real money in geopolitical forecasting. Each is corrected with a number or a case.

**Myth 1: "If it would be shocking, it must be unlikely."** Investors routinely confuse the *emotional magnitude* of an event with its *probability*. The 2008 collapse of Lehman Brothers and the cascade that followed felt shocking, and many treated "shocking" as a synonym for "won't happen." But a 38% base rate for trade-war escalation or a 17% base rate for posturing-to-conflict are *not small numbers* — a one-in-six chance is roughly the odds of rolling a specific number on a die, which happens constantly. The correction: decouple how dramatic an outcome feels from how often its reference class actually occurs. Shocking and likely are independent axes.

**Myth 2: "This time the base rate doesn't apply."** Every forecaster who ignored base rates and got burned told themselves the current situation was special. Brexit was widely treated as near-impossible by markets and bookmakers right up until the result — GBP/USD fell about 7% in hours on June 24, 2016, from roughly 1.48 to 1.36, because the market had priced "remain" as near-certain and had to violently reprice a result whose base rate (referendums producing surprise outcomes) was never that low. The correction: the burden of proof is on the claim that the base rate *doesn't* apply, and "it feels different" is not proof.

**Myth 3: "I should focus on the regions I know."** Investors overweight the conflicts they follow closely — the Middle East, Ukraine, Taiwan — and systematically underweight risks in regions they do not, like the Sahel, the Horn of Africa, or Central Asia. This *availability bias* means portfolios are often hedged against the famous risks and naked to the obscure ones, even when the obscure ones carry higher base rates of instability. The correction: scan the full map of reference-class risk, not just the part that is in the news.

**Myth 4: "The last outcome predicts the next one."** After Russia's nearly costless annexation of Crimea in 2014, many analysts over-assigned probability to further cheap, limited Russian successes — which contributed directly to mispricing the cost and scale of the 2022 invasion. This is *anchoring on the most recent event*, and it is especially dangerous because recency feels like wisdom. The correction: the previous outcome is one data point in the reference class, not a trend; weight it accordingly.

**Myth 5: "A compelling story beats a boring statistic."** When a geopolitical narrative is vivid and coherent — a charismatic strongman, a clear grievance, an obvious flashpoint — investors abandon statistical thinking and forecast the story. But narrative coherence is *negatively* correlated with predictive accuracy: the most compelling stories are often the ones that have been over-fitted to recent events. The correction: when you notice a forecast feels like a great story, that is precisely the moment to return to the base rate and ask whether the numbers support the narrative or merely decorate it.

## How it shows up in real markets

The framework is not academic. Every major geopolitical repricing of the past decade is, in hindsight, a story of base rates ignored or respected, scenarios drawn or skipped, prediction-market signals heeded or dismissed.

The Ukraine invasion, traced throughout this post, is the cleanest positive case: the prediction markets and the commitment signals (field hospitals, embassy evacuations, Donbas recognition) gave a disciplined forecaster a 30-day lead over the oil and wheat markets. Brent ran from \$96 on invasion day to \$120 within months; wheat futures jumped from roughly \$790 to over \$1,000 a bushel as the Black Sea export corridor closed. A forecaster who had updated correctly was positioned before the crowd.

The 2025 tariff shock is the cautionary counterpoint. When the United States announced sweeping tariffs in April 2025, the average U.S. tariff on Chinese goods spiked toward 145% before a 90-day pause negotiated it back toward 30%. Markets that had anchored on the 38% base rate for "trade dispute escalates to full tariff war" — and on the structured-analogy lesson that the 2018–19 trade war ultimately settled rather than spiraled — were less surprised by both the spike and the rapid de-escalation than those forecasting purely on the alarming headlines. The Vietnamese stock market, which fell to around 1,195 on the tariff announcement and recovered to 1,250 within weeks on the pause, rewarded forecasters who held the de-escalation branch at a meaningful weight rather than collapsing to a single catastrophic scenario.

The Iran-Israel exchanges of 2024 showed the cry-wolf discount in fast motion. The April 2024 direct exchange spiked Brent to about \$91 and the VIX by roughly 14% — but both recovered within about a week, because the market correctly read the exchange as a calibrated, telegraphed signal rather than the start of open war. Forecasters who had drawn the scenario tree knew the "limited, face-saving exchange" branch carried far more weight than the "regional war" branch, and they faded the spike rather than chasing it.

## How to trade it: The playbook

Here is the whole framework compressed into an operating procedure, and then applied live.

The procedure is the workflow from the opening figure. Identify the situation and define the precise question (a vague question produces a useless forecast). Pull the base rate for that reference class. Build a structured analogy with at least three competing analogies to refine the shape of the risk. Draw a three-scenario tree with explicit, observable red-flag and green-flag triggers and an asset payoff for each branch. Compute the expected value and, crucially, note the *skew* — a positively skewed bet wants options, a symmetric bet wants linear exposure. Calibrate the whole thing against prediction markets and, more importantly, against where the asset has actually moved; the gap between the two is the trade. Then update on new, irreversible information only, using the reversibility check to size each update. The invalidation is built in: when a green flag fires or the prediction-market probability falls below your entry threshold, you exit.

![Matrix of five live geopolitical situations with baseline escalation and de-escalation probabilities for Taiwan Iran Russia-NATO US-China tech and OPEC cohesion](/imgs/blogs/forecasting-geopolitical-events-frameworks-base-rates-and-scenarios-2.png)

Now five live situations to monitor as of 2026, each run through the framework. The matrix above is the summary; the reasoning follows.

**Taiwan Strait.** Baseline (continued grey-zone pressure) ~70%, escalation (blockade or invasion within the year) ~12%, de-escalation (formal tension reduction) ~18%. The base rate for invasion in a given year is low (3–5%); the structured analogy showed the *character* of any action skews toward "full" rather than "limited," but the cry-wolf discount on routine ADIZ incursions and the catastrophic cost to China keep the size small. Calibrate against Polymarket's Taiwan contracts, which have hovered in the low single digits annually. The trade is not betting on invasion; it is owning cheap tail hedges (semiconductor-supply-chain shorts, long volatility) when the prediction-market probability is at its lows and complacency is highest.

**Iran nuclear program.** Baseline (capped standoff) ~45%, escalation (enrichment to weapons-grade or a strike) ~20% strike plus 35% enrichment, de-escalation (deal) on the diplomacy weight. Three red flags to watch: an IAEA report of a crossed enrichment threshold, withdrawal of inspectors, and repositioning of U.S. or Israeli strike assets. The oil scenario tree from earlier gives a probability-weighted Brent near \$98 against spot, with the positively skewed payoff that argues for expressing the view through oil calls rather than futures.

**Russia-Europe escalation beyond Ukraine.** Baseline (conflict stays contained to Ukraine) ~82%, escalation (a NATO member is drawn in) ~6%, de-escalation (ceasefire) ~12%. The escalation branch has a hard probability *ceiling* imposed by NATO's Article 5 mutual-defense commitment: a direct attack on a NATO member triggers a collective response that Russia has every incentive to avoid, which is why this branch stays in single digits despite the alarming proximity of the war. The trade is recognizing that the ceiling exists — fading the periodic spikes in European defense and energy names that price a NATO-wide war the base rate says is very unlikely.

**US-China tech war escalation.** This is the one situation where the *escalation* branch is the baseline: ~70% probability of further escalation in semiconductor and AI-chip export controls within 24 months, ~25% steady state, ~5% genuine de-escalation. The reasoning is structural, not emotional: the data shows each round of controls (Nvidia's China revenue share falling from 26% to single digits as successive rules bit) has been followed by another, the bipartisan U.S. political consensus on tech containment is durable, and China's retaliation through critical-minerals export controls creates its own escalatory ratchet. The trade is positioning for continued bifurcation of the semiconductor supply chain rather than betting on a thaw.

**OPEC+ political cohesion.** Baseline (the coalition holds and manages supply) ~60%, escalation (a price war or fracture) ~25%, de-escalation here meaning even tighter cohesion ~15%. The base rate question is: how often do coalitions of this size fracture? Historically, large cartels fracture under one specific trigger — a member cheating on quotas while prices are falling and fiscal pressure is rising. The 2020 Saudi-Russia price war is the analogy. The signal to watch is the combination of falling oil prices *and* visible quota non-compliance; absent both, the cohesion base rate is high. The trade is reading those two signals together rather than panicking at every OPEC+ meeting headline.

The thread running through all five is the same discipline. Start from the base rate so you are not blown around by headlines. Refine with competing analogies so you are not fooled by the first comparison that comes to mind. Structure the futures into a scenario tree with observable triggers so you can update mechanically. Calibrate against the crowd and against where assets have actually moved so you can find the gap. And update only on new, irreversible information so you are neither anchored nor whipsawed. You will still be wrong often — that is the nature of forecasting rare events. But you will be wrong in a calibrated, sized, learnable way, and over enough situations that is what an edge looks like.

## Common Calibration Benchmarks for Geopolitical Forecasters

Before turning to cross-links, a practical note on calibration benchmarks. The most reliable external reference points for geopolitical probability estimates are the Good Judgment Open (GJOpen) superforecaster community, Metaculus for longer-horizon questions, and Polymarket for near-term binary outcomes. As a rough calibration target: if your probability estimate differs from the GJOpen consensus by more than 15 percentage points, examine your assumptions before acting. A large divergence is sometimes justified — you have information the crowd lacks, or you have applied a structural constraint (like the NATO Article 5 ceiling on Russia-Europe escalation) that tilts the base rate significantly. But more often, a large divergence signals anchoring, recency bias, or selection of an unrepresentative analogy. Building the habit of comparing your estimate to a crowd-sourced benchmark, before committing a position, is the single cheapest improvement available to a geopolitical forecaster. The crowd is often wrong in degree — it under-reacts to tail risk — but it is rarely wrong in direction, and you need a good reason to bet against it.
