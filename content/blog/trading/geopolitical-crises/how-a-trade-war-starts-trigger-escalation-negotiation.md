On the morning of March 22, 2018, the S&P 500 opened down 2.9%. The Nasdaq fell 3.8%. In a single session, American equity markets shed approximately \$725 billion in market capitalization. No earnings had missed. No central bank had raised rates. No bank had failed. The cause was a White House memorandum ordering the United States Trade Representative to prepare a list of tariffs on up to \$60 billion in Chinese goods.

No tariffs had actually been imposed yet. The investigation had just been announced. Yet the market priced the shock immediately. That reaction tells you everything you need to know about how trade wars work: they are fundamentally political events that markets price as regime changes, not temporary disruptions. The question for any investor watching a trade dispute escalate is not "will the tariffs hurt trade?" — they always will — but "where are we on the escalation ladder, and what does the endgame scenario look like from here?"

## Foundations: Why Trade Wars Exist and How the Legal Architecture Works

To understand a trade war, you need to first understand the political economy that produces it. Trade wars are almost never started by economists or trade ministers. They are started by presidents or prime ministers responding to a domestic political crisis — a region losing jobs, an industry losing market share, or a national security establishment alarmed about technological dependence.

**The domestic political calculation** is the starting point for everything. When Donald Trump campaigned in 2016, the core trade promise was simple: the United States ran a \$375 billion goods trade deficit with China, and that deficit was destroying Rust Belt manufacturing jobs. This was not a nuanced argument about comparative advantage — it was a political coalition-building exercise. The voters who cared most about trade deficits were concentrated in Pennsylvania, Ohio, Michigan, and Wisconsin: the exact swing states that had flipped to Trump and needed to stay Republican in 2020.

This political geography matters enormously for how far a trade war goes. A president willing to impose tariffs that hurt consumers everywhere but help workers in a handful of swing states has a very clear incentive structure. The tariff is, in essence, a transfer from consumers (who pay higher prices) to politically important workers (who get protected jobs). The economics are almost always negative-sum in aggregate; the politics can be positive-sum for the coalition that matters.

The IP grievance adds a second dimension. The USTR's 2018 Section 301 investigation found that China was engaged in "unreasonable and discriminatory" trade practices including: (1) forced technology transfer as a condition of market access, (2) licensing restrictions that benefit Chinese companies, (3) state-directed acquisition of US firms and assets for advanced technology, and (4) outright cyber theft of commercial secrets. The USTR's estimate of the annual cost to the US economy: between \$225 billion and \$600 billion per year. That is not a rounding error — it is the entire GDP of countries like Thailand or Egypt. Even if the estimate is overstated by a factor of three, the grievance is real and the political energy it generates is genuine.

### The Legal Instruments: Section 301, Section 232, and IEEPA

Understanding the legal instruments matters because each one has a different timeline, different WTO standing, and different political risk profile. Three instruments dominated the 2018-2025 trade war era.

**Section 301 of the Trade Act of 1974** is the primary IP and unfair-trade tool. The process works as follows: the president directs the USTR to investigate. The USTR opens a public comment period (typically 60 days), investigates, makes findings, proposes a tariff list, accepts further public comment, then imposes tariffs. Total elapsed time: typically 12-18 months. The tariff list is subject to WTO dispute settlement — the affected country files a WTO Panel request, which takes 12-18 months to produce a preliminary ruling and 3-7 years to reach a full decision. The catch: even if the WTO rules against the tariffs (as it did against US steel tariffs in 2022), there is no enforcement mechanism. The US simply continues imposing tariffs and does not pay the equivalent damages.

**Section 232 of the Trade Expansion Act of 1962** is the national security tool. It allows the president to impose tariffs on any import that threatens national security. The process requires a Department of Commerce investigation (typically 6-12 months), but the president has broad authority to act on the findings. The 2018 steel and aluminum tariffs (25% on steel, 10% on aluminum) were imposed under Section 232. They affected not just China but the entire world — including US allies like the EU, Canada, and South Korea. This created an immediate diplomatic crisis with allies who were then asked to join a trade coalition against China.

**IEEPA — the International Emergency Economic Powers Act of 1977** — is the emergency tool and the most powerful weapon in the arsenal. Under IEEPA, the president declares a national emergency, identifies an "unusual and extraordinary threat" from a foreign country, and can immediately impose tariffs, asset freezes, or transaction bans. There is no mandatory investigation period. There is no WTO consultation requirement. Tariffs can take effect the same day.

This is precisely what happened on April 2, 2025, when the Trump administration's "Liberation Day" executive order imposed tariffs of 10-50% on virtually all trading partners simultaneously under IEEPA emergency authority. Markets could not price this in gradually — the tariffs were announced at 4 PM and took effect within 24 hours. The DXY dollar index fell 5% in two weeks as the market processed the extraordinary scope of the action. The speed of the IEEPA instrument is why the market reaction is sharper and more disorderly than Section 301 or Section 232 actions, which are telegraphed months in advance.

### Why These Instruments Lay Dormant From 1995 to 2018

The creation of the World Trade Organization in 1995 created a multilateral trade adjudication system that gave US presidents a reason not to use unilateral trade instruments. The WTO dispute settlement body provided a legitimate, internationally respected mechanism for resolving trade grievances — and using it was seen as the "responsible" approach that maintained the rules-based international order.

Two things changed. First, WTO dispute settlement became increasingly slow, contested, and subject to blocking tactics (most notably, the United States blocking appointments to the WTO Appellate Body starting in 2017, which effectively paralyzed the appeals system). Second, the nature of the trade grievance with China — technology transfer, industrial policy subsidies, state-owned enterprise advantages — did not fit neatly into WTO rules that were designed for a world of tariff disputes between market economies, not disputes between a market economy and a state-directed economy with explicit industrial ambitions.

The Section 301/232/IEEPA toolkit came back because the WTO tool no longer seemed capable of addressing the actual problem.

## The Political Moves: How Each Side Chooses Its Weapons

The political move at the beginning of a trade war is as important as the tariff itself. Understanding why a government chooses a specific retaliation target tells you exactly which domestic constituencies they are protecting — and which foreign constituencies they are trying to hurt.

When China retaliated against the first round of US tariffs in July 2018, the initial \$34 billion Chinese tariff list was not random. It targeted US agriculture (soybeans, corn, wheat, pork, sorghum), commercial aircraft (Boeing 737s), and automobiles. Each category was chosen with surgical precision. US soybean production is concentrated in Iowa, Illinois, Indiana, and Ohio — all swing states with Republican senators and congressional delegations. Boeing assembly is concentrated in South Carolina and Washington — a red state and a purple state respectively. The agricultural targeting was a direct attempt to impose political costs on the constituencies that had elected Trump.

The Chinese political calculus in 2018 involved its own domestic pressures. Xi Jinping had consolidated power at the 19th Party Congress in October 2017, eliminating the two-term limit. "Made in China 2025" — the state industrial policy plan to dominate 10 advanced manufacturing sectors by 2025 — had been publicly launched in 2015 and was generating enormous political capital domestically. Any trade deal that required China to abandon or substantially modify its industrial policy would be seen as a humiliation and would undermine Xi's domestic narrative of China's unstoppable technological rise.

This structural asymmetry — Trump needing to show "wins" for Rust Belt workers, Xi needing to protect industrial policy from rollback — made the trade war both inevitable and extremely difficult to resolve. The two sides were not simply disagreeing about tariff levels; they were disagreeing about whether China had the right to operate a state-directed economy at all.

## The Escalation Ladder: Five Rungs to Peak Tariff

The five-rung model of trade war escalation (expanded to seven steps in the figure above) describes how trade disputes move from grievance to peak tension. Understanding which rung you're on tells you which market signals to watch.

**Rung 1: Investigation/Announcement.** This is the opening shot. The key feature is uncertainty about scope and timeline. Markets price the investigation as a "maybe" — the sector most exposed sells off, but the broad market often absorbs the move. In March 2018, the announcement of the Section 301 investigation cost the S&P 500 2.9% in a single day. But note: this was for an *investigation announcement*, not actual tariffs. The market was pricing in the probability distribution of outcomes, not the tariffs themselves.

**Rung 2: First Tariff Tranche.** Tariffs are imposed. The currency of the target country typically weakens (lowering export costs to offset some of the tariff impact). The affected sectors reprice sharply. Safe-haven assets (gold, US Treasuries, JPY) begin their bid. In July 2018, when List 1 tariffs hit (\$34 billion at 25%), the RMB began its depreciation from 6.25 to a peak of 6.97 by October 2018 — an 11.5% move.

**Rung 3: Retaliation.** The target country responds in kind. This is when markets price the trade war as bilateral rather than unilateral. The VIX upticks. Companies with supply chains exposed to both countries see multiple compression. Earnings guidance becomes problematic because input costs and export revenues are both uncertain.

**Rung 4: Counter-Retaliation.** The initiating country responds to the retaliation with more tariffs. This is the peak uncertainty moment. Safe-haven bids intensify. Corporate capital expenditure freezes — companies cannot make investment decisions when they do not know what tariff regime their supply chains will operate under in 12 months. The US-China trade war reached Rung 4 by September 2018, when List 3 (\$200 billion at 10%) was imposed, and again in May 2019, when List 3 was raised to 25%.

**Rung 5: Sector-Specific Weapons.** Both sides deploy their most targeted leverage: rare earth export controls, agricultural boycotts, aviation bans. This is the last rung before negotiation or escalation beyond trade. It is also the rung where the "who blinks first" calculation becomes concrete — which country has the leverage that the other side cannot easily substitute?

## The Five Forces That Decide Who Blinks First

The "who blinks first" question is the most important analytical question in any trade war. It determines whether you position for a deal (buy equities, sell safe-havens) or for a prolonged standoff (hold gold, stay underweight the bilateral exposed sectors). Five forces determine the answer.

**Force 1: Current Account Exposure.** Which country needs the trade more in absolute terms? The US-China bilateral trade deficit was \$419 billion in 2018 in goods. China exported far more to the United States than the United States exported to China, meaning US tariffs hit China harder in absolute dollar terms than Chinese tariffs hit the US. However, China's exports to the US were a smaller share of China's GDP than US exports to China were of US GDP — so the relative exposure was more balanced than the headline number suggests.

**Force 2: Domestic Political Timeline.** Which leader faces an election soonest? This is often the decisive factor. Trump needed to show "wins" before the 2020 presidential election. A prolonged trade war without a "deal" was a political liability; a "Phase One deal" — even one with limited substance — could be framed as a victory. Xi had no comparable electoral pressure — the Party Congress cycle is five years and highly managed. This gave China a longer time horizon for endurance.

**Force 3: Currency Flexibility.** Can the target country offset the tariff impact by devaluing its currency? A 25% tariff on Chinese goods costs US importers 25% more. If the RMB simultaneously depreciates 8% against the dollar, Chinese exporters receive 8% more RMB for every dollar of goods sold — partially offsetting the tariff. In 2018, China's central bank allowed the RMB to depreciate approximately 8% against the dollar, offsetting an estimated 30-40% of the List 3 tariff impact on the \$200 billion list.

This is why trade wars and currency wars are the same war viewed from different angles. Every tariff creates an incentive for the target country to devalue; every devaluation creates pressure for the initiating country to impose more tariffs. The 2019 Trump tweet labeling China a "currency manipulator" was precisely this feedback loop going through its formal diplomatic expression.

**Force 4: Strategic Commodity Leverage.** Which country controls an input the other cannot easily source elsewhere? China's most powerful card was (and remains) its dominance of rare earth processing: 85% of global rare earth processing capacity, 90% of graphite, 65% of lithium refining. Rare earths are critical inputs for US defense systems (F-35 fighter jets require approximately 920 pounds of rare earth materials), electric vehicles, and semiconductors. The United States had no short-run substitute.

The US's most powerful agricultural card was soybeans: China imported 95% of its soy from abroad, and the United States supplied 60% of global soy exports. China's 25% tariff on US soy was devastating to US farmers — but it was also painful for China, which needed protein for its livestock industry. Brazil was able to increase supply, but Brazilian soy was more expensive to ship and required years of additional capacity expansion.

**Force 5: Alliance Support.** Is the initiating country backed by its major allies, or does it stand alone? The United States in 2018 had complicated alliance dynamics. The Trump administration simultaneously applied Section 232 steel and aluminum tariffs to US allies (EU, Canada, Japan, Australia), which meant those allies were simultaneously negotiating their own exemptions rather than presenting a unified front against China. By alienating allies on steel and aluminum, the US weakened the multilateral pressure that could have been brought to bear on China's structural practices.

#### Worked example:

**Section 301 announcement market cap destruction.**

On March 22, 2018, the White House memorandum on the Section 301 investigation sent the S&P 500 down 2.9% in a single session. Let's quantify what that meant in absolute dollar terms.

The S&P 500 market capitalization at the start of 2018 was approximately \$25 trillion. A 2.9% one-day decline means:

\$25,000,000,000,000 × 0.029 = **\$725,000,000,000** in market capitalization destroyed in one day.

For context: the total US goods trade deficit with China in all of 2018 was \$419 billion. In other words, the one-day market reaction to the announcement of a *possible* tariff investigation was 73% larger in dollar terms than the entire annual trade imbalance the investigation was supposed to address.

This disparity is not irrational. The market was not pricing just the tariff revenue impact. It was pricing: (1) supply chain disruption costs for US companies sourcing from China, (2) Chinese retaliatory costs for US exporters, (3) the probability of broader escalation across more sectors, (4) the second-order effects of investment uncertainty on US corporate capital expenditure, and (5) the systemic risk of a prolonged deglobalization regime.

The Nasdaq fell 3.8% on the same day, versus the S&P's 2.9% — a difference of 0.9 percentage points explained by the Nasdaq's higher concentration of technology companies with significant China revenue exposure (Apple, Intel, Qualcomm) versus the more diversified S&P.

## How Markets Read the Escalation Ladder

Markets are not just reactive to trade war announcements; they develop a pricing vocabulary specific to the rung of the ladder. Understanding this vocabulary is how you extract a trading signal from a news headline.

**At Rung 1 (investigation announcement):** The signal is sector-specific. The industries targeted by the proposed tariffs sell off sharply; their supply-chain partners adjust more moderately. The broad equity market dips but not severely. The currency of the target country begins to weaken modestly. Safe-haven assets (gold, JPY, US Treasuries) bid slightly higher. VIX upticks 5-10%.

**At Rung 2 (first tariff imposition):** The currency move accelerates. The target country's equity market sells off more sharply than the initiating country's market. Sectors with bilateral exposure (auto, semiconductor, agriculture) see compression across both countries' equity markets. US bond yields fall as the tariff is priced as a deflationary shock to the economy (tariffs raise consumer prices but reduce investment and growth, causing the Fed to price in a slower rate cycle).

**At Rung 3-4 (retaliation/counter-retaliation):** VIX spikes into the 20-30 range. Gold breaks out. US 10-year Treasury yields fall as flight-to-quality intensifies. Corporate credit spreads widen modestly (trade wars reduce free cash flow predictability). Companies with supply chains exposed to both countries see multiple compression beyond just earnings guidance reductions.

**At Rung 5 (sector weapons):** Market reactions become commodity-specific. A rare earth export restriction sends semiconductor and defense stocks down. An agricultural boycott sends specific commodity futures down sharply. These are the most actionable signals because they are highly specific and often create extreme dislocations in commodity markets before they fully recover.

**At the Negotiation Signal:** This is when the "buy the dip" trade activates. A tweet, a statement, a diplomatic photo — any signal of negotiating intent causes a violent short-squeeze in the most beaten-up exposed sectors. In May 2019, when Trump tweeted that he was waiting for a "great deal" from China, the S&P futures reversed a 1.5% overnight loss to close essentially flat within four hours.

![S&P 500 and USD per CNH during the trade war showing co-movement with tariff escalation events](/imgs/blogs/how-a-trade-war-starts-trigger-escalation-negotiation-4.png)

#### Worked example:

**The "tariff tweet" intraday pattern.**

On the evening of May 5, 2019, President Trump tweeted: "For 10 months, China has been paying Tariffs to the USA of 25% on 50 Billion Dollars of High Tech, and 10% on 250 Billion Dollars of other goods. These payments are partially responsible for our great economic results. The 10% will go up to 25% on Friday."

The market reaction:

- S&P 500 futures: fell 1.5% before the US open the next morning (May 6).
- The S&P opened Monday, May 6 down 450 points (-1.7%).
- The intraday low on May 6: S&P 500 at 2,859, down approximately 2.6% from Friday's close of 2,945.
- USD/CNH: moved from 6.73 (Friday close) to 6.80 (Monday close), a 1.0% RMB depreciation in 24 hours.

Now calculate the P&L on a short S&P 500 futures position opened at the tweet (Friday after-hours at approximately 2,945) and closed at the Monday intraday low (2,859):

One S&P 500 E-mini futures contract = \$50 per index point.

Move = 2,945 − 2,859 = 86 points.

P&L per contract = 86 × \$50 = **\$4,300 per contract** (short position).

For a trader holding 10 contracts (a \$1.47M notional position), that is a \$43,000 gain on a one-day trade.

The risk, of course, is that negotiation tweets can reverse the move equally fast. On May 9, 2019, when China's Vice Premier Liu He arrived in Washington for talks, the S&P reversed nearly half of the Monday loss in a single session.

## The Four Endgame Scenarios

Every trade war eventually reaches one of four endgame scenarios. Knowing which scenario is playing out tells you whether the market re-rating is permanent or temporary.

![Trade war endgame scenarios branching from dispute to full deal phase deal standoff or escalation](/imgs/blogs/how-a-trade-war-starts-trigger-escalation-negotiation-5.png)

**Scenario A: Full Deal.** Tariffs are removed in exchange for structural reforms. This is the rarest outcome and has essentially never occurred in modern trade war history. It requires the target country to fundamentally restructure domestic policy — which means politically costly concessions. Markets would reprice sharply higher (equity gains of 8-12% would be typical). The FX of the target country would appreciate. Bond yields would rise as the safe-haven bid unwinds.

**Scenario B: Phase Deal.** A partial rollback of tariffs in exchange for purchase commitments and promises of future negotiations. This is the most common outcome (the US-China Phase One agreement of January 2020 is the canonical example). Markets reprice 3-5% higher initially. The tariff baseline remains elevated compared to pre-war levels. Supply chains remain in adjustment. The structural issues that caused the trade war are not resolved; they are deferred.

**Scenario C: Permanent Standoff.** No deal is reached, but both sides exhaust their appetite for further escalation. Tariffs become a fixture of the trade relationship. Markets eventually reprice supply chains to reflect the new cost structure — some production moves, some tariff costs are absorbed, some are passed to consumers. The equity market eventually normalizes but at a permanently lower multiple for sectors most exposed to the bilateral trade relationship.

**Scenario D: Escalation Beyond Trade.** The trade dispute becomes the template for a broader geopolitical competition involving investment bans, technology sanctions, financial system exclusions, and potentially military posturing. This is the scenario that markets treat most severely. Capital flight from the bilateral relationship accelerates. Companies restructure supply chains on a war-game basis rather than a cost-optimization basis. The discount rate applied to bilateral operations rises sharply and may never normalize.

The US-China relationship has been hovering between Scenarios B and C since 2020, with periodic escalation signals suggesting a trajectory toward Scenario D in the technology sector (chip export controls, investment screening, TikTok bans, Huawei restrictions).

#### Worked example:

**Scoring the US-China "who blinks first" forces.**

Let's apply the five-force scorecard to the 2018-2019 US-China standoff to evaluate who had more staying power.

**Force 1 — Current Account Exposure:** The US bilateral goods deficit with China was \$419 billion in 2018. China had far more to lose from tariffs in absolute dollar terms. US advantage.

**Force 2 — Election Timeline:** Trump needed a "deal narrative" before November 2020. China had no comparable electoral clock — the NPC cycle is predictable and managed. China advantage.

**Force 3 — Currency Flexibility:** China allowed the RMB to depreciate ~8% between January and October 2018. A 25% tariff on \$200 billion of goods = \$50 billion annual cost. RMB -8% = Chinese exporters receive ~8% more RMB per dollar of exports = \$16 billion offset per year on the \$200 billion list. Roughly 30-40% of the tariff cost is offset by currency. China advantage (currency as a buffer).

**Force 4 — Strategic Commodity Leverage:** China controls 85% of global rare earth processing. US controls 60% of global soy exports. Rare earths matter more for US national security; soybeans matter more for Chinese food security. Rough parity, slight US disadvantage given the strategic importance of rare earths for defense supply chains.

**Force 5 — Alliance Support:** The US had G7 backing in principle but had alienated EU, Canada, and Japan with Section 232 steel/aluminum tariffs. China had SCO and BRI relationships. US had marginally better formal alliance support but had actively complicated it. Marginal US advantage.

Scorecard: US advantage on 2 forces (current account, alliance support), China advantage on 2 forces (election timeline, currency), rough parity on 1 (strategic commodities).

**Net assessment: US had marginally more staying power in the bilateral standoff.** This was the correct read. China signed Phase One in January 2020 — before the US election, giving Trump his "deal narrative." The deal was structured to give Trump a political win (purchase commitments, a signing ceremony) without requiring China to abandon Made in China 2025 or modify its fundamental industrial policy posture.

## The Negotiation Endgame: How Trade Wars Actually End

Trade wars rarely end cleanly. The negotiating dynamics are shaped by the same political pressures that started the war — which means the endgame comes when the political calculation shifts, not when the economic calculation is resolved.

**The "deal is done when the election timeline forces it" pattern** is the most reliable predictor of trade war resolution timing. Trump's Phase One deal was signed January 15, 2020 — exactly 10 months before the presidential election. This gave Trump a "win" to campaign on while leaving the structural tariff regime in place. The deal itself required China to purchase \$200 billion in additional US goods over two years. The purchase commitments were the "win"; the failure to address structural IP, technology transfer, and industrial subsidy issues was the detail that did not make the headline.

**WTO dispute settlement** is the slow-motion alternative. WTO panels take 12-18 months to produce an initial ruling; the Appellate Body (now paralyzed by US blocking of appointments) would add 2-3 years. Even when the WTO rules against tariffs — as it has ruled against US steel/aluminum tariffs multiple times — there is no enforcement. Countries pay the tariffs as a political choice, not a legal obligation. This means WTO dispute settlement can produce vindication but not resolution.

**Unilateral back-down** is politically catastrophic for the initiating country. No president has ever removed tariffs voluntarily without a deal that can be framed as a win. This limits the endgame options significantly — the minimum acceptable outcome for the initiating country is something that can be called a "deal," even if the deal is largely symbolic.

**Third-party mediation** is essentially non-existent in trade disputes. Unlike military conflicts, trade disputes have no neutral mediator with the legitimacy to broker a solution. The WTO serves this function in theory but is too slow and too weakened to play it effectively.

#### Worked example:

**Escalation speed comparison: US-China vs. US-EU.**

The US-China escalation was extraordinarily fast by historical standards:

- March 2018: Section 301 investigation announced.
- July 6, 2018: List 1 (\$34B at 25%) imposed.
- August 23, 2018: List 2 (\$16B at 25%) imposed.
- September 24, 2018: List 3 (\$200B at 10%) imposed.
- May 10, 2019: List 3 raised to 25%.
- Total elapsed from investigation to peak tariff: **14 months.**

Compare the US-EU steel/aluminum dispute:

- March 8, 2018: Section 232 tariffs announced (25% steel, 10% aluminum) — affecting all countries.
- March-May 2018: EU, Canada, Mexico exempted temporarily.
- June 1, 2018: Exemptions expire — tariffs hit EU.
- July 2018: EU threatens auto tariffs; USTR Lighthizer and EU Trade Commissioner Malmstrom begin negotiations.
- July 25, 2018: Trump-Juncker meeting produces "framework" — tariff pause while negotiations proceed.
- October 2021: Tariff suspension negotiated (not full removal — "tariff-rate quota" system).
- Total elapsed from announcement to partial resolution: **42 months.**

Why did the EU dispute take three times as long to reach even partial resolution? Four factors:

1. **Coalition complexity:** The EU requires consensus among 27 member states — every bilateral deal moves at the speed of the most reluctant member.
2. **Retaliatory targeting:** EU retaliation targeted \$3.3 billion in politically symbolic US goods (Harley-Davidson motorcycles, bourbon whiskey, Levi's jeans) — each targeting a Republican congressional district. This took months to negotiate at the technical level.
3. **Sector specificity:** The EU's nuclear option was auto tariffs (25% on US cars), which Trump specifically threatened to avoid because of its impact on German auto stocks and thus EU economic stability.
4. **US election cycles:** The Trump-Juncker framework in July 2018 was partly driven by Trump's need to remove EU tariffs before the November 2018 midterms.

The lesson: multilateral or alliance-impacting trade disputes are slower to escalate and slower to resolve than bilateral great-power confrontations.

## How to Trade It: The Playbook by Rung

The trade war playbook requires knowing your rung. Here is the signal map and the position for each stage.

**Investigation announcement (Rung 1):**
- Signal: Presidential directive, USTR notice in Federal Register, White House memorandum.
- Position: Short the sector most exposed to the tariff list. Long volatility (VIX calls or put options on exposed ETFs). Long gold modestly.
- Invalidation: Rapid negotiation signal within 30 days of announcement (rare but happens — USTR often opens a 60-day comment period with negotiating back-channels active).

**First tariff imposition (Rung 2):**
- Signal: USTR publishes tariff list effective date. Federal Register notice.
- Position: Long USD vs. target-country currency (the target always depreciates). Short target-country equity ETF. Long US 10-year Treasuries (tariff = deflationary + risk-off). Long gold.
- Invalidation: Target country offers immediate negotiating framework; US exempts key sectors.

**Retaliation/counter-retaliation (Rungs 3-4):**
- Signal: Target-country announces retaliatory tariff list. Second USTR tariff round.
- Position: Add to safe-haven positions (gold, JPY, CHF). Reduce exposure to multinationals with bilateral revenue exposure. Consider long VIX if below 20. Short commodity futures in the retaliatory target (soybeans during China retaliation).
- Invalidation: High-level bilateral meeting announced.

**Sector weapons (Rung 5):**
- Signal: Rare earth export restriction notice, agricultural boycott communique, investment ban executive order.
- Position: This is the maximum fear moment and often the best buying opportunity for a Phase Deal resolution. Establish a small long position in the most beaten-up exposed equities as a "Phase Deal" option position.
- Invalidation: Escalation continues — no bilateral meeting scheduled.

**Negotiation signal:**
- Signal: Tweet, statement, "constructive talks" press release, diplomatic dinner photo.
- Position: Buy the beaten-up equities. Cover gold short. Sell USD vs. target-country currency. The short-squeeze is violent and fast — position early.
- Invalidation: Details of negotiations emerge and reveal gap is too large for near-term resolution.

## Common Misconceptions About Trade Wars

**Misconception 1: "Trade deficits prove the other country is cheating."**

Trade deficits reflect the savings-investment balance of an economy, not "unfair trade." The US runs a persistent goods trade deficit because it has low national savings and high domestic consumption — a structural feature of the US economy. The US ran a goods trade deficit with every major trading partner in 2018, not just China. If China were cheating, you would expect the US to have a surplus with everyone else — it doesn't. The trade deficit is a macroeconomic phenomenon; the IP theft grievance is a legitimate policy complaint; they are separate issues.

**Misconception 2: "Higher tariffs protect domestic industries permanently."**

The canonical counter-example: the 1930 Smoot-Hawley Tariff raised US tariffs on over 20,000 imported goods. US exports fell by 61% between 1929 and 1933 as trading partners retaliated. US manufacturing employment fell, not rose. Tariffs protect specific industries at the cost of creating higher input prices for downstream industries that use the protected inputs. US steel tariffs in 2018 helped US steel producers and hurt US auto manufacturers and construction firms that buy steel.

**Misconception 3: "A trade deal resolves the underlying grievance."**

Phase One of the US-China deal required China to purchase \$200 billion in additional US goods over two years. China purchased approximately \$170 billion. Neither the IP theft issues nor the industrial policy subsidies (Made in China 2025) nor the technology transfer requirements were addressed. Biden kept 90%+ of Trump's tariffs. The deal resolved nothing structural — it bought political time.

**Misconception 4: "Tariffs are paid by foreign exporters."**

This is the most persistent misconception. Tariffs are paid by the importer — in the US case, by US companies importing Chinese goods. They either absorb the cost (lower margins) or pass it to consumers (higher prices). Federal Reserve research from 2019 found that the cost of US tariffs on Chinese goods was almost entirely borne by US consumers and firms rather than Chinese exporters. The 2018 US tariff on Chinese washing machines, for example, raised the average washer price in the US by \$86 — with no measurable change in Chinese export prices.

**Misconception 5: "Trade wars are temporary."**

The pre-trade-war baseline tariff of 3.1% on Chinese imports has never returned. Under Biden (2021-2024), the average tariff remained above 19%. In 2024, Biden raised tariffs on Chinese EVs to 100% and on solar panels to 50%. Trump's 2025 "Liberation Day" tariffs pushed the headline number to 145% before the 90-day pause negotiated it back to 30%. The structural shift toward elevated tariffs on Chinese goods appears permanent across party lines.

## How It Shows Up in Real Markets: The 2018 Case Study

To ground all of this in real market outcomes, here is what the 2018 US-China escalation looked like in each major asset class.

**Equities:** The S&P 500 ended 2018 at −6.2%, its worst year since 2008. This is deceptive in two ways: first, the trade war was only one of several headwinds (Fed rate hikes, Q4 earnings deceleration). Second, the drawdown within the year was much larger — the market fell over 20% from its September 2018 peak to the December 24, 2018 intraday low (the "Powell Put" moment). The Shanghai Composite fell 24.6% in 2018, its worst year in a decade.

**FX:** The RMB depreciated from 6.25 to 6.97 by October 2018 — an 11.5% move. This was not a market-driven collapse; the PBOC managed the rate down deliberately to offset tariff costs. By contrast, the DXY dollar index strengthened from 89 to 97 in 2018 — a nearly 9% appreciation — as the tariffs were priced as relatively more damaging to China than to the US.

**Fixed Income:** The US 10-year Treasury yield peaked at 3.23% in October 2018 and fell to 2.51% by year-end — a 72 basis point decline in just 2.5 months. The trade war was deflationary (lower growth expectations) and increased safe-haven demand — a textbook negative-supply-shock / risk-off combination.

**Commodities:** US soybeans fell from \$10.40/bushel to \$8.00/bushel (−23%) between early 2018 and late 2018. Gold initially sold off with risk assets in early 2018, then rallied 18% in 2019 as the trade war uncertainty persisted. Oil was volatile — higher from Iran sanctions / OPEC cuts, lower from global growth concerns.

**Sectors:** The clearest sectoral story was agriculture (big loser), Chinese tech (big loser, down 35%), and Vietnam/ASEAN manufacturers (big winner, with Vietnam FDI rising 35% in 2018 as companies accelerated supply chain diversification away from China).
