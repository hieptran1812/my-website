> [!important]
> **TL;DR.** Within 72 hours of Russia invading Ukraine, the G7 froze roughly \$300 billion of Russian central-bank reserves, cut seven banks off SWIFT, and began an oil embargo that culminated in a \$60-per-barrel price cap. The reserve freeze and advanced-chip export controls genuinely bit — they isolated half of Russia's war chest and cut high-end chip imports by about 70%. But the oil weapon mostly *rerouted* rather than destroyed: Russian crude that once flowed to Europe simply turned east to India and China, moved on a ~600-vessel shadow fleet, and Russia still earned roughly \$160 billion from oil in 2023. The lesson for any market participant is that a sanction is only as strong as the chokepoint it controls — and oil had no single chokepoint the West could hold.

On the morning of February 24, 2022, Russian columns crossed into Ukraine from the north, east, and south. By the time European markets opened, the Moscow Exchange had been halted, the ruble had gapped through 90 to the dollar, and every trader with Russian exposure was staring at a screen that would not let them sell. Over the following weekend — February 26 and 27 — the United States, the European Union, the United Kingdom, Canada, and Japan did something that had never been done to a G20 economy: they froze the foreign reserves of its central bank and began severing its banks from the global payment system.

What followed was not one sanction but a layered campaign that unfolded over the next year, each layer aimed at a different artery of the Russian economy: its money, its payments, its oil, and its access to advanced technology. This post takes that campaign apart piece by piece, using the real numbers, and asks the only question that matters for a market participant: which pieces actually changed Russia's behavior or balance sheet, and which ones the market routed around within months?

## Foundations: what a "sanction" actually is, and why most of them fail

Before we touch a single Russian number, it helps to be precise about what a sanction *is* as a financial instrument. A sanction is not a law of physics. It is a rule that some set of governments imposes on the people and companies they control, forbidding them from doing business with a target. That last clause is the entire game. A sanction can only reach activity that touches a jurisdiction the sanctioning country governs — its banks, its currency, its companies, its insurers, its ports.

This gives every sanction a built-in limit: it is strong exactly where the sanctioning bloc controls a **chokepoint** that the target cannot route around, and weak everywhere else. The dollar is a chokepoint because almost all cross-border trade is invoiced and cleared in dollars, and dollar clearing runs through US-supervised banks. SWIFT is a chokepoint because it is the messaging layer most banks use to instruct payments. Marine insurance is a chokepoint because most of the world's tanker insurance is written in London. Advanced semiconductor tools are a chokepoint because three or four companies on Earth can make them.

Russia is unusually hard to sanction precisely because its single largest export — oil — has *no* chokepoint the West fully controls. Anyone with a tanker and a buyer can move crude. So the central drama of the Russia sanctions, which we will trace all the way through, is the West repeatedly trying to manufacture an artificial chokepoint around oil (first an embargo, then an insurance-based price cap) and Russia repeatedly building a parallel system to escape it.

Keep three questions in your head for every measure below:

1. **What artery does it target?** Money, payments, oil, or technology.
2. **Does the sanctioning bloc control a chokepoint on that artery?** If not, expect leakage.
3. **What is the cheapest adaptation available to the target?** The market will find it, and the price of that adaptation is the real cost of the sanction.

With that frame, let's open the package.

### A short history of why 2022 was different

Sanctions are old. The US has sanctioned Cuba since 1960, Iran in waves since 1979, and added Crimea-related measures on Russia after 2014. What made February 2022 a genuine break was not the *idea* of sanctions but the *target's size and integration*. Cuba and Iran were small, semi-isolated economies. Russia in 2022 was the eleventh-largest economy in the world, the dominant supplier of energy to a continent, a top-three crude exporter, the largest wheat exporter, and a critical source of palladium, nickel, and enriched uranium. Cutting it off was not like quarantining a small clinic; it was like trying to amputate a major organ from the global economy without the patient — everyone else — bleeding out.

That integration cut both ways. It gave the West enormous leverage, because Russia depended on Western banks, insurers, technology, and consumer markets. But it also gave Russia enormous *counter*-leverage, because Europe depended on Russian gas, the world depended on Russian oil and grain, and global supply chains depended on Russian metals. Every sanction the West designed had to be weighed against the self-harm it would inflict. This is why the package was layered and phased rather than dropped all at once: the coalition was probing for measures that hurt Russia *more* than they hurt the coalition itself. The reserve freeze passed that test easily — it cost the West almost nothing. A total, immediate oil embargo did not, which is the entire reason the convoluted price-cap mechanism had to be invented.

There is also a 2014 prologue that matters enormously. After annexing Crimea, Russia was hit with a first, milder round of sanctions. Crucially, the Kremlin treated those sanctions as a *dress rehearsal*. Between 2014 and 2022 it built SPFS as a SWIFT alternative, dramatically increased its gold and yuan reserves relative to dollar and euro holdings, paid down external debt, built up a sovereign wealth buffer, and pushed "import substitution" in agriculture and some industries. So when the 2022 hammer fell, Russia was the most sanctions-prepared major economy on Earth. Any analyst who modeled 2022 as a surprise to an unprepared target got the magnitude of the damage badly wrong. The target had spent eight years building shock absorbers.

> [!note]
> The single most common analytical error in early 2022 was treating Russia as if it were 2014 Russia. The Kremlin had spent the intervening years explicitly de-risking from exactly the sanctions that arrived. Adaptation that looked miraculously fast was in fact pre-built.

## Layer 1: The reserve freeze — the genuinely unprecedented blow

### What happened

A central bank holds foreign reserves the way a household holds a savings account in a bank it does not own: the money exists as an entry on someone else's books. Russia's central bank, by early 2022, held roughly \$630 billion of reserves. But "holding dollars" or "holding euros" does not mean stacks of cash in a Moscow vault — it means deposits and bonds parked at the Federal Reserve, the European Central Bank, the Bank of Japan, and in Western commercial banks and custodians.

On February 26, 2022, the G7 simply *froze* the entries. Around **\$300 billion** — close to half of the total — was immobilized overnight. The Bank of Russia could still see the balances on a screen, but it could not spend them, move them, or use them to defend the ruble. The only reserves it retained full use of were its physical gold (held domestically) and its yuan holdings at Chinese institutions.

This was the part of the package that genuinely had no precedent at this scale. Sanctioning a small economy's reserves had been done; freezing the war chest of a nuclear-armed G20 commodity giant had not.

### Why it hurt

A central bank's reserves are its ammunition for defending the currency. When the ruble began collapsing, the standard playbook would have been for the Bank of Russia to sell dollars and buy rubles, propping up the exchange rate. With half its reserves frozen, that ammunition was cut in half — and the half it retained (gold and yuan) was far harder to deploy into a USD/RUB market.

#### Worked example: why the frozen half mattered more than the number suggests

Suppose the Bank of Russia wants to defend the ruble at 80 to the dollar after the invasion. To do that it must stand in the market and buy rubles — selling its dollars and euros — whenever the rate threatens to weaken past 80.

- Pre-freeze, it had roughly \$630B of reserves, of which perhaps \$430B was in freely usable USD, EUR, GBP, and JPY assets.
- Post-freeze, the usable Western-currency portion fell to near \$0. What remained was about \$130B in gold and roughly \$80B in yuan.

Here is the trap. To defend USD/RUB, you need *dollars* — or at least a currency deep and convertible enough to be swapped into dollars instantly. Gold must first be sold (into a market the sanctions were chilling) and yuan must be converted through Chinese intermediaries who themselves feared secondary sanctions. So the *effective* defensive firepower in the currency that mattered did not fall from \$630B to \$300B; it fell from roughly \$430B to something close to zero in the first, critical week.

That is why the ruble gapped so violently before capital controls took over: the central bank's normal tool — selling reserves — had been confiscated mid-fight.

### The ruble's crash, and the misleading "recovery"

![The ruble fell 47% to a March low near 116 per dollar, was forced back by capital controls, then resumed a slow decline](/imgs/blogs/russia-sanctions-anatomy-swift-reserves-oil-price-cap-2.png)

The ruble fell about **47%** at its worst, touching roughly 116 to the dollar in early March 2022 from a pre-invasion level near 79. Headlines screamed collapse. Then, within weeks, the ruble not only recovered but *strengthened* past its pre-war level, trading near 57 by June. Russian officials declared the sanctions a failure.

This recovery was almost entirely artificial, and understanding why is essential to reading any sanctioned currency:

- **Capital controls.** Russians were forbidden from converting rubles to foreign currency or moving money abroad. Demand for dollars was administratively switched off.
- **A 20% emergency policy rate.** The Bank of Russia more than doubled rates overnight, making it brutally expensive to short the ruble or hold cash that might flee.
- **Forced conversion of export revenue.** Exporters — flush with oil and gas cash at sky-high 2022 prices — were ordered to convert 80% of their foreign earnings into rubles, creating a wall of forced ruble buying.
- **Collapsed imports.** With foreign goods cut off, Russians weren't buying euros to import cars and electronics. Less import demand meant less currency outflow.

In other words, the "strong ruble" of mid-2022 was a currency in a sealed box: you could not get money out, you were forced to bring export money in, and there was little to spend it on. It was a price quoted in a market that had been administratively gutted. By 2025, once the export windfall faded and reserves bled, the ruble had drifted back toward 95 — the slow, honest decline the controls had masked.

> [!note]
> A recovering exchange rate under heavy capital controls is not a sign that sanctions failed. It is a sign that the price has stopped being a real price. The honest read-through is the *parallel* rate and the *behavior* of reserves and imports, not the official screen.

### The legal afterlife: frozen versus seized

A subtle but consequential point sits inside the word "freeze." The \$300B was *immobilized*, not *confiscated*. Legally, the assets still belong to the Bank of Russia; the West has merely forbidden anyone in its jurisdiction from helping Russia use them. The difference matters because *seizing* the assets outright — handing them to Ukraine for reconstruction, as many proposed — would cross a far brighter line in international law and would terrify every other sovereign reserve holder. Most of the frozen reserves sit at **Euroclear**, the Belgian securities depository, where they continue to generate interest. By 2024, the coalition settled on a middle path: leave the principal frozen but channel the *windfall profits* (the interest the frozen assets earn) toward Ukraine. That compromise tells you how carefully the West is treading around the precedent — it wants Russia's money to fund Ukraine without establishing that any country's reserves can simply be taken.

For a markets analyst, the frozen-versus-seized distinction is the crux of the de-dollarization debate. A *freeze* is reversible and conditional — it can be lifted in a peace deal, so it reads as a sanction. *Seizure* is permanent and unconditional — it reads as confiscation, and confiscation is what would genuinely accelerate other central banks fleeing dollar and euro reserves. The market has, so far, priced a freeze, not a seizure. If that line is ever crossed, the repricing of "safe" reserve assets would be a far larger event than anything that happened to the ruble.

## Layer 2: SWIFT — the payment cutoff that was less than it sounded

### What happened

On February 27, 2022, the EU and partners announced that selected Russian banks would be removed from SWIFT — the Society for Worldwide Interbank Financial Telecommunication. Seven banks were named initially, with more added later. Notably, **Sberbank** (Russia's largest) and **Gazprombank** were initially *exempted*, because Europe still needed those banks to pay for the gas it was still importing.

SWIFT itself is widely misunderstood. It is not a bank, it does not hold money, and it does not move money. It is a secure messaging network — the email system that banks use to instruct one another: "pay \$10 million from my account at your bank to this beneficiary." Cutting a bank off SWIFT does not freeze its money. It removes the standardized, fast, trusted channel for *instructing* cross-border payments.

### Why it hurt less than the headlines implied

Removing a bank from SWIFT raises the friction of cross-border payments; it does not eliminate them. Banks can fall back to telex, email, phone, or bilateral messaging systems. It is slower, more error-prone, and more expensive — but money still moves.

More importantly, Russia had been building an alternative since 2014, when the first Crimea sanctions scared it. By 2022 it had:

- **SPFS** (System for Transfer of Financial Messages) — its domestic SWIFT-equivalent, used heavily inside Russia and with friendly banks abroad.
- Growing use of **China's CIPS** (Cross-Border Interbank Payment System) for yuan-denominated trade.
- A pivot to invoicing trade with India, China, and Turkey in rubles, yuan, rupees, and dirhams instead of dollars and euros.

#### Worked example: the cost of a payment after a SWIFT cutoff

Imagine an Indian refiner buying a \$50 million cargo of Russian crude after Sberbank-type banks are SWIFT-restricted.

- **Before:** A single SWIFT MT103 message routes the \$50M dollar payment through correspondent banks in hours. Cost: a few hundred dollars in fees, near-zero friction.
- **After:** The dollar channel is closed. The refiner instead pays in **rupees** into a Russian bank's rupee account in India (a "vostro" account), or routes yuan through CIPS. The payment clears — but now Russia is sitting on rupees it struggles to spend, because India runs a trade *surplus* with Russia in this flow. Russia's effective haircut is not the SWIFT fee; it is the cost of holding billions in a currency it cannot easily convert or repatriate.

By 2023, Russia reportedly had tens of billions of rupees stranded in Indian banks that it could not efficiently move out. *That* — the currency mismatch — was the real cost of the payment sanctions, far more than the SWIFT messaging friction itself. The lesson: when you cut the dollar rail, trade does not stop; it shifts to a worse currency for the seller, and the seller eats that cost.

### Secondary sanctions: the real teeth in the payment system

The genuinely powerful payment weapon was never SWIFT itself. It was the threat of **secondary sanctions** — the US warning that any bank *anywhere in the world* that helped a sanctioned Russian entity could itself lose access to the dollar system. A Chinese, Turkish, or Emirati bank does not care much about a SWIFT rule it can route around. It cares enormously about being cut off from dollar clearing, because that would end its ability to do international business at all. This is the dollar chokepoint reasserting itself one layer up: even when Russia escapes the direct rail, its *counterparties' banks* remain hostages to the dollar system.

The effect through 2023 and 2024 was visible and behavioral. Banks in friendly third countries became skittish about Russian transactions — not because a law forced them to refuse, but because their compliance departments calculated that the risk of US enforcement outweighed the fees from Russian business. Payments to Russia slowed, got rejected, or required ever more convoluted routing. This produced a recurring pattern: a new Russian trade channel opens, processes billions, draws a US warning, and the foreign banks quietly back away, forcing Russia to find the *next* channel. The cumulative friction is real, even though no single transaction is impossible.

#### Worked example: why a Turkish bank refuses good business

A Turkish bank is offered \$200 million of Russian-related payment flow at an attractive 0.5% fee — \$1 million of clean revenue.

- The upside is bounded: \$1 million in fees.
- The downside is catastrophic and unbounded: if the US designates the bank for sanctions evasion, it loses dollar-clearing access. For a bank that intermediates billions in normal dollar trade, that is an existential loss — call it the entire franchise.
- Expected-value math for the compliance officer: even a 2% perceived chance of designation implies an expected loss far larger than the \$1M fee once you weight it against the value of the whole dollar-clearing relationship.
- Rational choice: **refuse the business**, or accept it only at a fee high enough to compensate — which itself becomes another cost Russia must absorb.

This is why secondary sanctions, not SWIFT removal, are the payment system's actual chokepoint. They convert the universal dependence on dollar clearing into a self-policing network where every foreign bank does the West's enforcement for it.

## Layer 3: The oil weapon — embargo, then the price cap

This is the heart of the package and the part the West agonized over most, because oil is simultaneously Russia's economic lifeline and a globally fungible commodity that no one bloc can fence in.

### The dilemma

In 2021, oil and gas were roughly 45% of Russia's federal budget revenue. Cut off Russian oil and you starve the war machine. But Russia was the world's second- or third-largest crude exporter, supplying around 7–8 million barrels per day to global markets. Yank that off the market suddenly and the global oil price spikes, hammering every Western consumer and potentially earning Russia *more* per barrel on the volumes it still sold. That is exactly what happened in early 2022: prices rose so much that despite Western disengagement, **Russian oil revenue actually rose from about \$117 billion in 2021 to roughly \$218 billion in 2022.**

The sanctioning coalition faced a genuine policy paradox: hurt Russia's revenue without spiking the price for everyone else. Their answer was a two-stage design.

### Stage one: the embargo

The US banned Russian oil imports outright on March 8, 2022 — easy, because the US barely bought any. The harder move was the EU, which by late 2022 phased in a ban on **seaborne** Russian crude (December 2022) and refined products (February 2023), while pipeline crude to a few landlocked members got temporary carve-outs. This forced Russia to find new buyers for the millions of barrels Europe stopped taking.

### Stage two: the \$60 price cap

An embargo by the buyers alone would just send Russian oil elsewhere at the going world price. So the G7 added a cleverer instrument: a **price cap**, set at **\$60 per barrel** for seaborne Russian crude, effective December 5, 2022.

![How the sixty-dollar oil price cap uses Western insurance and shipping as the enforcement chokepoint](/imgs/blogs/russia-sanctions-anatomy-swift-reserves-oil-price-cap-6.png)

The mechanism is the most elegant idea in the whole package, so it's worth slowing down. The G7 could not stop a tanker from carrying Russian oil. But it *could* control the **services** that make seaborne oil trade possible — and crucially, marine insurance, much of which is written through London and the International Group of P&I Clubs. The rule became:

> A shipper may use Western insurance, financing, brokering, and shipping services to carry Russian crude **only if** that crude was bought at or below \$60 per barrel. Sell it above \$60, and you forfeit access to all of those services.

The theory was beautiful: let Russian oil keep flowing (so the world price doesn't spike) but force the sale price down (so Russia's per-barrel revenue is capped). Western consumers stay supplied; the Kremlin's margin gets squeezed.

#### Worked example: how the cap was supposed to squeeze a single cargo

Take one Aframax tanker carrying 730,000 barrels of Urals crude.

- **World benchmark (Brent):** \$85/bbl.
- **Cap:** \$60/bbl.
- If the buyer wants London insurance and a Greek-owned tanker (the cheap, reliable option), Russia must sell at **\$60** or less.
  - Cargo value at cap: 730,000 × \$60 = **\$43.8 million.**
  - Cargo value if it had sold at Brent: 730,000 × \$85 = \$62.05 million.
  - Forced discount per cargo: **\$18.25 million** — money the cap, in theory, takes straight off Russia's top line.
- Across millions of barrels per day, the intended drain runs into the tens of billions per year.

That was the design. Now the reality.

### What actually happened to the discount

![Urals traded at a deep discount to Brent right after the cap, then the gap narrowed as the shadow fleet matured](/imgs/blogs/russia-sanctions-anatomy-swift-reserves-oil-price-cap-3.png)

For a while, it worked. Urals — Russia's main export grade — had always traded a few dollars below Brent. After the cap, the discount blew out to as much as **\$29–30 per barrel** by March 2023. Russia really was selling oil far below the world price, exactly as intended, and 2023 oil revenue fell to roughly **\$160 billion** from 2022's \$218 billion.

But look at the right side of that chart. The discount narrowed steadily — to about \$12 by 2024 and roughly \$10 by 2025. Russia learned to escape the insurance chokepoint by building a fleet that did not need Western services at all. The cap's bite faded as the workaround matured.

#### Worked example: how the shadow fleet erases the cap's discount

Return to the same Aframax, now in 2024, with the cap still nominally \$60 and Brent at \$80.

- Russia routes the cargo on a **shadow-fleet** tanker — an aging vessel with opaque ownership, often reflagged, insured by a Russian or non-Western provider rather than London.
- Because no Western service is used, the \$60 cap **does not apply**. Russia can sell near the prevailing Urals price — say \$68 (an \$12 discount to Brent reflecting quality and logistics, not the cap).
  - Cargo value: 730,000 × \$68 = **\$49.6 million.**
  - Compared with the capped \$60 sale (\$43.8M), Russia captures an extra **\$5.8 million per cargo.**
- The cost of escaping the cap is the price of the shadow fleet itself — buying old tankers, self-insuring, accepting some buyers who demand a small extra discount for the legal risk. Russia happily paid it, because the per-cargo recapture dwarfed the per-cargo cost.

This is the chokepoint principle in action. The cap held only as long as Russia needed London's insurers. The moment it could self-insure on its own fleet, the artificial chokepoint dissolved.

### The gas weapon Russia turned on Europe — and why it backfired

Oil was the West's weapon against Russia. **Gas** was Russia's weapon against the West, and its failure is one of the most instructive episodes in the whole conflict. In 2021, Russia supplied roughly 40% of the EU's gas via pipeline — an apparently devastating dependence. Through 2022, Russia progressively throttled those flows: cutting deliveries to "unfriendly" countries, demanding payment in rubles, and ultimately, after the Nord Stream pipelines were sabotaged in September 2022, sending pipeline gas to Europe to a fraction of its former level.

The price reaction was violent. European benchmark gas (TTF) rocketed from around €75/MWh before the war to an extraordinary peak near **€310/MWh** in August 2022 — roughly ten times normal levels. For a winter, Europe genuinely feared rationing, factory shutdowns, and frozen homes. This looked like Russia's trump card.

It did not work, and the reason is again the chokepoint principle — applied in reverse. Russia controlled the *pipelines*, but it did not control the *gas market*. Europe responded with a crash program: it leased floating LNG import terminals (FSRUs), signed LNG cargoes from the US, Qatar, and elsewhere, filled storage aggressively, subsidized demand reduction, and rode an unusually mild winter. EU LNG import capacity jumped from about 163 bcm in 2022 toward 280 bcm by 2024. By the winter of 2022–2023, TTF had collapsed back toward €40/MWh, and by 2023 Russian pipeline gas to Europe had fallen near 27 bcm from over 150 bcm pre-war — and Europe was, remarkably, fine.

The strategic lesson is brutal for Russia. By weaponizing gas, it did not merely fail to coerce Europe; it *permanently destroyed its single largest, highest-value, captive market*. Pipeline gas to Europe is sticky and lucrative; you cannot redirect a pipeline to China overnight (the pipelines do not exist, and building them takes a decade). So unlike oil, which Russia successfully rerouted on ships, gas revenue from Europe was lost more or less for good. Russia spent its most powerful coercive lever and got coerced markets that learned to live without it.

#### Worked example: the asymmetry of the oil and gas weapons

Compare the two energy weapons on the dimension that matters — *reroutability*.

- **Oil (Russia's resource, West's weapon):** moved by ship, fungible, sellable to anyone. When Europe stopped buying, India and China bought. Volume preserved, revenue dented but durable (~\$160B in 2023). The weapon mostly failed because oil is reroutable.
- **Gas (Russia's weapon, on Europe):** moved by fixed pipeline, not fungible at short notice, sellable only where infrastructure exists. When Russia cut Europe, Europe replaced it with LNG within ~18 months, and Russia could *not* redirect those volumes to Asia for lack of pipelines. Russia lost roughly €100B+/year of high-value sales it cannot recover.

Same continent, same war, opposite outcomes — and the entire difference is whether the commodity could be rerouted around the chokepoint. That single variable explains more about energy-sanctions outcomes than any political analysis.

## Layer 4: Export controls — the quietly devastating one

While the oil drama played out in public, the measure that may have done the most durable damage got far less attention: **export controls on advanced technology**, especially semiconductors and the tools, software, and components that go into weapons.

The US and allies barred the sale to Russia of high-end chips, chipmaking equipment, avionics, optics, and a long list of dual-use components. Here the West *did* control a real chokepoint: the most advanced semiconductors and the machines that make them come from a tiny number of firms in the US, the Netherlands, Japan, Taiwan, and South Korea — all aligned with the sanctions.

The effect: Russia's imports of advanced chips fell by roughly **70%**. The consequences rippled into the war directly. Russia was documented cannibalizing chips from dishwashers and consumer electronics for military hardware, struggling to produce precision-guided munitions at the rate it expended them, and grounding or stripping aircraft for spare parts it could no longer legally buy from Boeing or Airbus.

#### Worked example: the cost of losing the chip chokepoint

Consider a precision-guided missile that needs, say, 30 imported high-grade chips per unit.

- **Pre-war:** chips sourced openly from Western distributors at, say, \$50 each → \$1,500 of chip content, available in unlimited quantity.
- **Post-controls:** the same chips must be smuggled through third countries (Turkey, the UAE, Central Asia, Hong Kong shell firms) at a 3–5× markup and unreliable supply. Effective cost jumps to perhaps \$200 each → \$6,000 per unit, *if* you can source them at all.
- The binding constraint is not the \$4,500 extra cost — Russia can afford that. It is **availability and reliability**: production rates fall when supply is intermittent, and quality drops when substitute chips are used. That degradation of *output*, not the price, is the sanction's real payload.

This is the part of the package that aged best for the sanctioners, precisely because there was a genuine, hard-to-route-around chokepoint. Smuggling raised cost and lowered reliability; it never restored the open pipeline.

### Why chips are the one chokepoint nobody can build around

It is worth dwelling on *why* advanced semiconductors are such a uniquely durable chokepoint, because it is the exception that proves the whole framework. To make a leading-edge chip you need extreme-ultraviolet lithography machines, which are made by exactly **one** company on Earth (ASML, in the Netherlands), using light sources, optics, and components from a handful of suppliers in Germany, the US, and Japan. To design the chip you need software from three US-headquartered firms. To fabricate it you need a few fabs in Taiwan and South Korea. Every link in that chain sits in a country aligned with the sanctions.

This is the opposite of oil. Oil comes out of the ground in dozens of countries and moves on thousands of interchangeable ships. Advanced chips come from a chain so concentrated that no coalition of sanctioned states — even China, which is pouring hundreds of billions into the attempt — has been able to replicate it at the leading edge within a decade. Russia, with a tiny domestic semiconductor industry, had no hope of substituting at all. So when the controls hit, Russia was forced into smuggling *old* and *consumer-grade* chips through third countries, which is exactly why we saw the dishwasher-cannibalization stories. They were not propaganda; they were the physical signature of an economy that genuinely could not buy what it needed.

The same logic, incidentally, is the engine of the separate US-China chip war: the US is using identical export-control tooling against China's access to advanced GPUs and lithography, betting on the same uncopyable chokepoint. The Russia case is the proof-of-concept that this lever, unlike the oil lever, actually holds.

### Transshipment: the leak the controls couldn't fully plug

No chokepoint is perfectly tight. Russia found partial relief through **transshipment** — buying restricted goods through intermediary countries that had not signed onto the controls. Trade data through 2022–2024 showed suspicious surges: exports of laptops, chips, and dual-use electronics from the EU and US to Armenia, Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, Georgia, Turkey, and the UAE spiked far beyond those countries' plausible domestic consumption, with the excess almost certainly flowing onward to Russia.

The sanctioners chased this leak with a second wave of enforcement — pressuring transit countries, sanctioning specific intermediary firms, and leaning on Western exporters to screen end-users. The result was a cat-and-mouse equilibrium: transshipment kept *some* chips flowing, but at higher cost, lower volume, and constant disruption as routes were exposed and shut. The 70% decline in *advanced* chip imports persisted even as lower-grade components leaked through. The chokepoint bent; it did not break.

## Putting the scorecard together: what worked, what didn't

![A side-by-side of the sanctions that genuinely bit versus the oil flows that simply rerouted east](/imgs/blogs/russia-sanctions-anatomy-swift-reserves-oil-price-cap-4.png)

Step back and the pattern is stark, and it maps exactly onto the chokepoint principle from the Foundations section.

**What worked — where the West held a real chokepoint:**

- **The reserve freeze.** Russia's reserves lived on Western books; the West simply locked the door. Roughly \$300B isolated, and Russia's ability to defend its currency the normal way was gone. There was no parallel system to route around custody of dollar and euro assets.
- **Advanced-chip export controls.** The high end of the semiconductor supply chain is controlled by a handful of aligned firms. Smuggling raised cost and cut reliability; advanced imports fell ~70% and weapons production degraded.

**What didn't — where no chokepoint existed:**

- **Oil flows.** Crude is fungible and movable by anyone with a ship. The barrels Europe refused were absorbed by India and China within a year.
- **The shadow fleet.** Roughly 600 tankers, opaquely owned and non-Western-insured, neutered the price cap's enforcement mechanism.
- **Oil revenue.** Despite the entire apparatus, Russia still earned around \$160 billion from oil in 2023 — down from the windfall peak, but more than enough to fund the war.

## Where the oil actually went: the map redrawn

The single clearest illustration of "sanctions reroute rather than destroy" is the geography of Russian crude.

![Russian crude shifted from a Europe-dominated buyer base to India, China, and Turkey within about a year](/imgs/blogs/russia-sanctions-anatomy-swift-reserves-oil-price-cap-7.png)

Before the war, Europe was the dominant customer for Russian seaborne crude, taking roughly half. Within about a year of the embargo and cap:

- **India** went from buying almost no Russian crude to taking **40%+** of Russia's seaborne exports — refineries bought cheap Urals, refined it into diesel and other products, and in some cases sold those products *back* into Western markets (legal, because refined product from India is Indian, not Russian).
- **China** absorbed **20%+**, much of it via its independent "teapot" refiners.
- **Turkey** took roughly **10%**, also becoming a refining-and-re-export hub.
- The rest moved through an opaque mix of shadow-fleet routes and smaller buyers.

The total volume Russia exported barely fell. What changed was *who bought it, at what discount, and on whose ships*. The West succeeded in making Russia sell at a discount for a while, and in forcing an expensive parallel logistics system into existence. It did not succeed in taking the barrels off the market.

There is a delicious irony buried in this map. Indian refineries bought cheap Russian Urals, refined it into diesel, jet fuel, and gasoline, and then exported a meaningful share of those products *to Europe* — entirely legally, because once crude is refined in India the resulting product is, by rule of origin, Indian. So Europe, having banned Russian crude, ended up buying refined fuel made *from* Russian crude, at a markup, with India pocketing the refining spread. The molecules the sanctions were meant to keep out walked back in wearing a different passport. This is the fungibility of commodities defeating the geography of sanctions in its purest form.

### The metals and grain arteries the West chose not to cut

It is as revealing to note what the package *spared* as what it hit. Russia is a major supplier of **palladium** (critical for catalytic converters and electronics), **nickel** (batteries and steel), **enriched uranium** (fueling a large share of Western nuclear reactors), and **wheat and fertilizer** (feeding much of the developing world). The West largely left these alone.

Why? Because the chokepoint analysis ran the *wrong way* on these goods — the West needed them more than it could afford to punish Russia by refusing them. Sanctioning Russian uranium would have idled Western reactors; sanctioning fertilizer and wheat would have driven food prices up across Africa and the Middle East, risking famine and political instability. Russian palladium and nickel are deeply woven into Western industrial supply chains. In each case, the self-harm exceeded the harm to Russia, so these arteries stayed open. This is the coalition explicitly choosing not to fire weapons that would ricochet — and it is why blanket "maximum pressure" rhetoric rarely survives contact with a globally integrated economy.

#### Worked example: why the West didn't sanction Russian uranium

Suppose a Western utility runs a nuclear fleet that buys 30% of its enriched uranium and conversion services from Russia.

- If that supply is sanctioned overnight, the utility cannot instantly source elsewhere: Western enrichment capacity is limited and takes years to expand.
- Worst case, reactors must derate or shut — removing gigawatts of baseload power from a grid already stressed by the gas crisis.
- The cost to the West (power shortages, electricity-price spikes) plausibly *exceeds* the few billion dollars of revenue denied to Russia.
- Rational coalition choice: leave uranium unsanctioned, or phase it out over a multi-year window while domestic enrichment scales — which is exactly the cautious path that was taken.

The arteries the West left open are not oversights. They are the places where the chokepoint pointed back at the sanctioner.

## Common misconceptions, corrected

> [!warning]
> **Misconception 1: "The strong ruble proved sanctions failed."**
> The ruble's mid-2022 strength was the product of capital controls, a doubled policy rate, forced export conversion, and collapsed imports. It was a quoted price in a sealed market, not a free-market verdict. By 2025 the ruble had drifted back toward 95 as the props were removed and reserves bled.

> [!warning]
> **Misconception 2: "Cutting Russia off SWIFT froze its money."**
> SWIFT is a messaging network, not a vault. The cutoff raised payment friction and pushed trade onto worse currencies (rupees, yuan) and slower rails (SPFS, CIPS). The real cost to Russia was stranded, hard-to-convert currency balances — not frozen cash.

> [!warning]
> **Misconception 3: "The \$60 price cap capped what Russia earns per barrel."**
> The cap only applied to cargoes using Western insurance and shipping. Once Russia built a ~600-ship shadow fleet with non-Western insurance, the cap stopped binding on those cargoes. The Urals discount that hit ~\$30 in early 2023 narrowed to ~\$10 by 2025.

> [!warning]
> **Misconception 4: "Sanctions are an on/off switch."**
> Sanctions are a friction tax that the target pays to keep doing what it was doing. Whether that tax is crippling or trivial depends entirely on whether the sanctioner controls a chokepoint with no substitute. Reserves and advanced chips had chokepoints; oil did not.

## How markets actually traded the package

For a market participant, the Russia sanctions offered a clean set of lessons in how to read a sanctions shock — and how *not* to.

**The first move is rarely the trade.** On invasion day, Brent spiked, the ruble cratered, wheat and gas soared, and the VIX jumped. But the durable trades came from the *second-order* adaptation: long Indian refiners that bought discounted Urals, long tanker rates as the fleet reshuffled, long European LNG infrastructure as the continent scrambled to replace pipeline gas, and a structural bid under gold as a reserve asset that cannot be frozen.

**Watch the chokepoint, not the headline.** The headline "G7 caps Russian oil at \$60" sounds devastating. The tradeable question was always: *can the West actually enforce it?* Once the shadow fleet was visibly scaling and the Urals discount started narrowing through 2023, the market correctly priced a fading cap — and Russian revenue stabilized.

**Reserve confiscation repriced something larger than Russia.** The most consequential market signal in the whole episode had nothing to do with Russia's economy. The moment the West froze a G20 central bank's reserves, every other non-aligned central bank — China, Saudi Arabia, others — quietly updated its view that dollar and euro reserves carry a political risk. That is part of the structural bid under gold and the slow, decade-long drift in reserve composition. Sanctions on one country can reprice the safe-haven status of the sanctioner's own assets.

**Discounts mean-revert when enforcement is leaky.** The Urals-Brent spread is the cleanest tradeable proxy for "is the cap working." It widened on enforcement and narrowed on evasion. A participant who understood the shadow-fleet buildout had a roadmap for that spread well before consensus.

#### Worked example: sizing the Urals-Brent normalization trade

Suppose in early 2023 you observe the Urals discount at \$29/bbl and you believe the shadow fleet will erode enforcement over 18 months, normalizing the discount toward \$12.

- Your thesis: the discount compresses by ~\$17/bbl as evasion scales.
- A refiner or trader long Urals (buying Russian crude) and short Brent futures as a hedge captures that compression: on a 1-million-barrel position, a \$17/bbl move is **\$17 million** of spread gain, independent of the absolute oil price.
- The risk is timing and enforcement crackdowns (new vessel sanctions can re-widen the spread temporarily). You size it as a *trend* trade with stops on enforcement-driven re-widening, not a single-date bet.

The point is not the precise P&L — it is that the most reliable sanctions trades come from modeling the *adaptation curve*, not from reacting to the announcement.

### The first 72 hours, hour by hour, as a market template

Because this was the cleanest large-scale sanctions shock in modern memory, it serves as a template for how risk repriced in real time. Walking the sequence:

- **Invasion morning (Feb 24).** Risk-off detonation. Brent jumped from the mid-90s toward 100+, wheat limit-up, European gas surging, the VIX spiking into the low 30s, and gold catching a bid toward 1,920. Equities sold off globally. This was the *reflexive* phase — everything correlated, everyone de-risking at once. Almost none of it was a durable trade; much of it round-tripped within weeks.
- **The weekend (Feb 26–27).** The reserve freeze and SWIFT announcement landed when markets were closed — a classic feature of major sanctions, which are deliberately timed for weekends to let the system absorb them before trading. The Monday open then gapped to price the *new information*, not the invasion itself: the ruble was untradeable offshore, Russian ADRs collapsed toward zero, and European banks with Russian exposure gapped down.
- **The first weeks.** The market began separating *announced* from *enforceable*. Commodities Russia genuinely dominated and could not be quickly replaced (gas, palladium, wheat) held their gains; commodities that were globally fungible (oil) gave much back as traders realized the barrels would reroute rather than vanish.
- **The following months.** The durable trades emerged from the adaptation: European LNG infrastructure, tanker day-rates, Indian refining margins, and the structural gold bid from reserve managers. These were quieter, slower, and far more profitable than the day-one panic.

The template generalizes. A sanctions shock has a reflexive phase (everything correlated, mostly noise), an information phase (the weekend package repricing), and an adaptation phase (where the real money is). Confusing the reflexive phase for the durable trade is the single most expensive mistake in event-driven macro.

### Reading the tape for "is it working"

A disciplined analyst wants live indicators of sanctions efficacy, not headlines. The Russia case offered four clean ones, each mapping to a layer of the package:

- **The Urals–Brent discount** proxies oil-cap enforcement. Widening = cap biting; narrowing = evasion winning. It widened to ~\$30 then compressed to ~\$10.
- **Russian advanced-chip import proxies** (third-country trade-data surges, teardown reports of weapons) proxy the export-control chokepoint. Persistently constrained = working.
- **The ruble's parallel/offshore rate and reserve drawdown** proxy the financial squeeze, cutting through the managed official rate.
- **Russian pipeline-gas volumes to Europe** proxy the mutual energy decoupling — and they collapsed toward zero, marking a permanent loss of Russia's premier market.

Each indicator is a price or volume you can actually watch, and each tells you about a *specific* chokepoint rather than the political mood. That is the difference between trading the sanction and trading the headline.

## A practitioner's playbook for reading any sanctions package

Distilling the Russia experience into a repeatable checklist:

1. **Identify the artery and the chokepoint.** For each measure, name the artery (money, payments, oil, tech) and ask whether the sanctioning bloc controls a substitute-free chokepoint on it. Reserves: yes. Advanced chips: yes. Oil: no. Payments: partly. That single judgment predicts most of the outcome.

2. **Price the adaptation, not the announcement.** Every sanction has a cheapest workaround. Estimate its cost and lead time. The shadow fleet took roughly a year to scale; that timeline *was* the trade in the Urals spread.

3. **Distinguish stock from flow damage.** The reserve freeze was a one-time *stock* hit — \$300B locked, permanently. The oil measures were a *flow* tax that Russia steadily reduced. Stock damage is durable; flow taxes erode as adaptations mature.

4. **Treat capital-controlled prices as unreliable.** A managed exchange rate, a halted equity market, or an administratively-set discount tells you about policy, not about fundamentals. Triangulate with reserves, imports, and parallel-market prices.

5. **Watch the second-order beneficiaries.** Sanctions create winners: Indian and Turkish refiners, LNG infrastructure, tanker owners, gold, and the operators of alternative payment rails. These are often cleaner, more durable trades than shorting the target directly.

6. **Remember the boomerang.** Freezing a major central bank's reserves teaches every other reserve manager a lesson about the political risk embedded in the sanctioner's own currency. The largest, slowest market effect of a sanction can land on the sanctioner.

7. **Map the arteries the sanctioner *won't* cut.** The goods left untouched — Russian uranium, palladium, nickel, wheat, fertilizer — tell you where the chokepoint points back at the West. Those arteries are where the target retains leverage, and where supply-side trades (long the commodities the West dare not sanction) carry an implicit policy floor under them.

8. **Separate stock shocks from flow shocks in your time horizon.** A one-time confiscation (reserves) reprices instantly and permanently; a flow tax (the oil cap) decays on the adaptation curve. Trade the first as a level shift, the second as a fading spread, and never confuse the two.

9. **Time your entries around the weekend-package cadence.** Major sanctions land on weekends by design, so the tradeable repricing happens at the Monday gap, not on the invasion-day panic. Position for the information you can anticipate (a package is coming) rather than chasing the reflexive first move.

### A note on the limits of sanctions as a coercive tool

It is worth ending the playbook with a sober point that the Russia case makes unavoidable. Sanctions are excellent at imposing *cost* and poor at compelling *behavior*. The package degraded Russia's technological base, locked half its reserves, and forced its oil to sell at a discount — yet it did not stop the war, which was the stated political objective. Cost and coercion are different currencies. Markets, mercifully, only need to price the first: an analyst's job is to estimate the economic damage and its persistence, not to predict whether the pain will change a leader's mind. Conflating the two — assuming that because a sanction *hurts* it will therefore *work* politically — is a category error that has misled forecasters from Cuba to Iran to Russia. Price the cost, model the adaptation, and leave the geopolitics to resolve on its own clock.

## The largest consequence was the one nobody sanctioned

If you zoom all the way out, the most important effect of the Russia sanctions may have nothing to do with Russia. It is what every *other* government learned by watching.

For seventy years, the implicit deal underpinning the dollar's reserve status was that dollar (and euro) reserves are the safest assets on Earth — politically neutral, always usable, immune to seizure. The reserve freeze put an asterisk on "politically neutral." It demonstrated, in public, that if you fall far enough out of favor with the issuing bloc, your reserves can be locked in a weekend. That is a *non-zero* political risk that, before 2022, most reserve managers had priced at essentially zero.

The response has been slow, rational, and visible in the data. The dollar's share of global FX reserves has continued its gradual multi-decade drift lower, sitting under 58% by the mid-2020s versus over 70% at the turn of the century. Central banks — led by China, but joined by many emerging markets — have been steady, heavy buyers of **gold**, an asset that sits in your own vault and cannot be frozen by anyone. This is not a dramatic "death of the dollar"; the dollar remains overwhelmingly dominant because no alternative offers comparable depth, liquidity, and rule of law. But at the margin, the freeze nudged every non-aligned reserve manager toward diversification, and that marginal, structural bid is one of the most durable trades to come out of the entire episode.

#### Worked example: how a central bank reprices reserve risk after the freeze

Imagine a non-aligned central bank holding \$400 billion in reserves, 65% in dollar and euro assets, after watching Russia's freeze.

- It now assigns, say, a 1-in-50 chance over a decade that it could fall out of favor enough to face similar treatment. That is a small probability — but applied to roughly \$260B of Western-currency reserves, the *expected* frozen amount is over \$5B, plus the un-hedgeable tail risk of losing access in a crisis precisely when reserves are needed most.
- The cheapest insurance is to shift some allocation into gold and, more slowly, into other currencies and assets held outside Western custody.
- Even a modest re-weighting — moving 5% of reserves, \$20B, toward gold over several years — is, multiplied across dozens of central banks, a structural, price-insensitive source of demand for gold and a structural, slow drag on Western-currency reserve share.

No one *sanctioned* the dollar. But by demonstrating that reserves are confiscatable, the West taught the world to hold a little less of them. The boomerang is real, and it is the largest, slowest market consequence of the whole package.

## The bottom line

The Russia sanctions package was the most comprehensive economic-warfare campaign ever mounted against a major economy, and it delivered a split verdict that follows directly from first principles. Where the West controlled a chokepoint with no substitute — custody of \$300 billion in reserves, and the top end of the semiconductor supply chain — the sanctions bit hard and durably. Where it did not — the globally fungible, ship-anywhere oil market — Russia absorbed a temporary discount, built a parallel logistics and insurance system, redirected its barrels to Asia, and kept the revenue flowing at roughly \$160 billion a year.

For anyone trading or analyzing the next sanctions shock — and there will be a next one — the framework is the whole lesson. Do not ask "how big is the sanction?" Ask "what artery does it target, does the sanctioner hold a chokepoint on it, and what does the cheapest workaround cost?" Answer those three questions and you will be ahead of the headlines, every time.

And keep the boomerang in view. The most enduring market consequence of the most aggressive sanctions package in history was not the ruble's crash or the Urals discount — both of which faded. It was the quiet, permanent lesson every other reserve manager absorbed about the political risk of holding the sanctioner's currency, and the slow structural bid that lesson put under gold. Sanctions are a tool of statecraft, but they are also a market event with first-, second-, and third-order effects that play out over different time horizons. The reflexive first move is loud and usually wrong. The adaptation is where the money is. And the boomerang is where the history is written. Read all three, in that order, and the next package — whoever imposes it on whomever — will look less like a shock and more like a script you have already studied.
