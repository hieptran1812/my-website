On the night of July 15, 2016, Turkish military jets screamed over Ankara and tanks rolled across the Bosphorus Bridge. By 3 a.m., the coup attempt against President Recep Tayyip Erdogan had failed — but not before the Turkish lira had crashed nearly 5% in a matter of hours and trading desks across London, New York, and Hong Kong scrambled to reprice every Turkish asset they held. Within the next trading session, the BIST100 equity index fell 7.1%. Ordinarily, a failed coup that ends with the elected president still in power should be quickly repriced as a minor event. Instead, it marked the beginning of a multi-year sequence of political decisions that would strip the lira of 80% of its value.

That is the central paradox of EM currency crises triggered by geopolitics: it is not the violence or the drama that destroys currency value. It is the sequence of political decisions that follows — who gets to set monetary policy, whether the rule of law holds for foreign investors, and whether the government will borrow in a way the market can sustain. The shot that starts the race is geopolitical. The race itself is financial.

## Foundations: How Geopolitical Risk Flows Into Currency Markets

To understand how a political event in Ankara or Johannesburg ends up moving the exchange rate in real time, you first need a map of the transmission mechanism. Think of it in three interlocking layers: the political layer, the capital layer, and the monetary layer.

**The political layer** is where the shock originates. A coup attempt, a sanctions announcement, a surprise election result, a charismatic leader overruling the central bank — each of these is a signal to investors about the *future path of policy*. Investors do not react to the event itself so much as to what the event implies about the government's next moves. When the Turkish coup failed and Erdogan consolidated power in 2016, sophisticated investors immediately began asking: will Erdogan now move to control the central bank more directly? That question, not the coup itself, drove the sustained lira weakness.

**The capital layer** is where political signals become market prices. Emerging market assets are disproportionately held by foreign institutional investors — pension funds, sovereign wealth funds, hedge funds, and bond mutual funds based in New York, London, and Tokyo. These investors made their initial allocation based on a certain set of assumptions: a credible central bank, a rule-of-law environment protecting their property rights, a current account that generates the dollars needed to service foreign debt. When geopolitics calls any of those assumptions into question, capital leaves. It leaves fast. An EM economy might have absorbed foreign capital inflows equivalent to 5–7% of GDP for years. When geopolitics triggers an exit, that capital can reverse direction in days, not months.

**The monetary layer** is where the central bank is forced to respond. When capital leaves, it sells the local currency to buy dollars (or euros, or yen). This supply of local currency overwhelms demand and pushes the exchange rate toward weakness. The central bank has two choices: (1) sell its foreign exchange reserves to buy the local currency back, defending the rate — this buys time but burns through the reserve cushion; or (2) raise interest rates sharply, making local currency deposits more attractive to foreign holders — this defends the currency but raises borrowing costs domestically, slowing growth and triggering a credit crunch. Neither option is pain-free. In most EM currency crises, the central bank first tries (1), exhausts its reserves, then is forced into (2) at a worse starting point.

### Why EM Currencies Are Structurally Vulnerable

A useful way to frame EM currency vulnerability is the **"original sin" doctrine** first articulated by economists Barry Eichengreen and Ricardo Hausmann in the late 1990s. The core insight: most emerging market economies cannot borrow in their own currency in international markets. They must issue bonds in dollars or euros. This means a currency depreciation simultaneously increases the local-currency cost of their foreign-currency debts, creating a doom loop. The more the currency falls, the bigger the debt burden. The bigger the debt burden, the more capital flees. The more capital flees, the more the currency falls.

This structural vulnerability means that EM currencies react more severely to geopolitical shocks than developed-market currencies, even when the shock itself is of similar magnitude. A geopolitical shock that would move the British pound by 2% might move the Turkish lira by 15–25% — not because Turkey is 10 times more exposed to the geopolitical risk, but because the structure of Turkey's capital account and debt profile amplifies the transmission.

### The Current Account as a Shock Absorber (or Amplifier)

A country's **current account balance** is the net of what it earns from the world (exports, tourism, remittances) versus what it sends to the world (imports, interest on foreign debt, profits repatriated). A current account surplus means the country is, on net, a creditor to the rest of the world — it generates the foreign currency it needs from trade. A current account deficit means it must attract foreign capital inflows to fund the gap.

When geopolitics triggers capital flight, a country with a current account surplus can weather the storm far better than one with a deficit. The current account surplus provides a natural demand for the local currency (foreigners must buy TRY or ZAR to pay for Turkish or South African exports). A country with a large current account deficit — like Turkey in 2018, running a deficit near 6% of GDP — has no such buffer. When capital leaves, there is no offsetting demand from trade, and the currency falls harder.

Vietnam, as we will see, is the exception that proves the rule: its current account surplus provided a crucial buffer during periods of EM-wide turbulence.

## The Political Moves: Case Study 1 — Turkey (TRY)

The Turkish lira's story from 2016 to 2023 is one of the most complete case studies in how political decisions about monetary policy translate directly into currency destruction. To understand it, you need to understand one man's theory of economics — and the market's eventual, brutal verdict on that theory.

### The 2016 Coup Attempt: The Initial Shock

On July 15, 2016, a faction of the Turkish military attempted to seize power. Within hours, the attempt was crushed. Erdogan rallied supporters, appeared on CNN Turk by FaceTime, and by morning had retaken control. The immediate market reaction was swift but recoverable: TRY fell from roughly 2.88 to 3.02 per dollar overnight, a depreciation of about 4.7%. The BIST100 opened 7.1% lower. Within two weeks, most of the equity and currency losses had been recovered.

But the coup's aftermath set in motion a chain of political decisions that would prove far more costly. Erdogan used the coup attempt as justification for a sweeping purge of the military, judiciary, and civil service. Over 150,000 people were arrested or dismissed. NATO relationships frayed. Investor confidence in the rule-of-law environment — the foundation of any cross-border capital allocation — began to erode. Foreign direct investment flows, which had been recovering after 2013, stalled.

### The Erdogan Doctrine: Interest Rates Cause Inflation

By 2018, Erdogan had moved from political consolidation to active intervention in monetary policy. His stated belief — expressed repeatedly in public statements and interviews — was the inverse of standard economic theory: he claimed that high interest rates *cause* inflation rather than reduce it. His reasoning was heterodox: high rates raise the cost of production for businesses, which then pass those costs on to consumers as higher prices. While this argument has a grain of theoretical validity in specific contexts, it is empirically wrong in Turkey's situation, where the primary driver of inflation was currency weakness and import price pass-through.

The market knew this. Every time Erdogan signaled displeasure with central bank rate hikes, or fired a central bank governor who had hiked rates, the lira fell further. Between 2019 and 2021, Turkey went through three central bank governors in rapid succession. The market interpreted each firing as a confirmation that monetary policy was subordinated to political preference — and responded by selling TRY.

![Turkey lira collapse 2015 to 2025 with key political events](/imgs/blogs/emerging-market-currency-crises-triggered-by-geopolitics-2.png)

The sequence of events tells the story with brutal clarity:

- **August 2018**: The first major lira crisis. The immediate trigger was a diplomatic row with the United States over the detention of American pastor Andrew Brunson. The US imposed tariffs on Turkish steel and aluminum. But the underlying vulnerability — Turkey's 6% current account deficit, its \$220 billion in foreign-currency corporate debt, and Erdogan's public pressure on the central bank — had been building for years. TRY fell from 4.5 to 7.24 per dollar in a matter of weeks, a loss of nearly 40%.

- **2021–2022**: The policy-driven self-destruction. With inflation running at 20–25% and rising, Erdogan instructed the central bank to *cut* rates. Between September and December 2021, the policy rate was cut from 19% to 14%. TRY responded by collapsing from 8 per dollar to 18 per dollar in the space of three months — a 55% loss in a single quarter. By October 2022, Turkish inflation had hit 85%, the highest in 24 years.

- **May–June 2023**: The U-turn. After winning re-election in a contested result, Erdogan made a striking personnel choice: he appointed Mehmet Simsek, a former Merrill Lynch economist and former finance minister with strong market credibility, as Finance Minister. Simsek promptly appointed Hafize Gaye Erkan (and later Fatih Karahan) as central bank governor and began a rapid-fire rate hiking cycle. The policy rate was raised from 8.5% to 50% within about 12 months. TRY stabilized, not recovered — the damage to the real exchange rate was too severe for a reversal — but the pace of depreciation slowed dramatically.

#### Worked example:

**Sizing the TRY carry trade loss in 2021.** A hedge fund allocates \$10 million to a TRY carry trade in January 2021, when TRY/USD is at 7.44. They borrow dollars at 1.5% (the USD LIBOR equivalent), convert to TRY, and invest in Turkish treasury bills yielding 16%. The gross carry is 14.5% per year, which looks extraordinary.

By December 2021, TRY has moved from 7.44 to 18.0 per dollar. The fund converts its TRY principal back to dollars. They have 10,000,000 × 7.44 = 74,400,000 TRY at the start. After 12 months of 16% TRY yield, they have 74,400,000 × 1.16 = 86,304,000 TRY. At the December rate of 18.0 TRY/USD, that converts to 86,304,000 / 18.0 = \$4,794,667.

The fund started with \$10 million and ended with \$4.79 million — a loss of \$5.21 million, or -52.1%, despite earning a 16% carry yield in local currency terms. The FX loss of -58.7% overwhelmed the carry income of +16%. This is the core risk of EM carry trades: when geopolitics triggers a currency collapse, the carry becomes irrelevant.

### The 2023 U-Turn: Credibility as a Market Commodity

The Turkish experience after June 2023 demonstrates something important: market credibility, once lost, can be rebuilt, but it takes time and it is expensive. Simsek's appointment triggered an immediate short-covering rally in TRY. NDF markets — where offshore investors bet on TRY moves — had been holding massive short positions (bets that TRY would fall further). When the policy reversal became credible, those shorts were covered, creating demand for TRY and a brief period of relative stability.

The lesson is not that U-turns are free. The Turkish economy paid a severe price in 2023–2024: real interest rates were deeply negative even after the 50% nominal rate, inflation remained in double digits, and the real exchange rate was still far below its pre-crisis level. But the *rate of depreciation* slowed. For a carry trader, stabilization at 32–37 per dollar with a 40–50% yield is actually a viable trade — as long as the political commitment holds.

## The Financial Channels: How Political Decisions Become Market Moves

Understanding *why* political decisions translate into FX moves requires mapping the specific financial channels through which political risk transmits to asset prices.

### Channel 1: The Risk Premium Channel

Every EM bond and equity is priced with a risk premium on top of the "risk-free" US Treasury rate. This premium — sometimes called the EMBI spread (Emerging Market Bond Index spread) — compensates investors for the additional risks of holding EM assets: currency risk, political risk, default risk. When geopolitics raises the probability of adverse outcomes, the risk premium must rise to compensate investors. A higher risk premium means lower bond prices, higher bond yields, and lower equity valuations.

During Turkey's 2018 crisis, Turkish 5-year credit default swap (CDS) spreads — a direct market measure of the perceived probability of default — spiked from around 170 basis points in early 2018 to over 550 basis points by August 2018. Each basis point of CDS spread represents 0.01% annual cost of protection against default. A move from 170 to 550 basis points represents a market pricing of roughly a 3–4 times increase in the implied probability of a credit event.

### Channel 2: The Capital Flow Channel

Foreign institutional investors typically hold significant positions in EM local-currency bonds and equities. When geopolitics triggers a risk repricing, these investors face both an analytical signal (the country risk has risen) and a mechanical trigger: many institutional mandates specify maximum country risk ratings or maximum CDS spreads. When Turkey crossed certain threshold CDS levels in 2018, some global EM bond funds were mechanically required to reduce their positions — regardless of whether the fund manager personally thought the sell was right.

This mechanistic element is crucial for understanding the speed and severity of EM currency crises. The selling is not purely discretionary and analytically driven. It includes forced sellers responding to risk-management rules, margin calls on leveraged carry positions, and index rebalancing as country weights shift due to price changes.

### Channel 3: The Confidence-Multiplier Channel

There is a second-order effect that is harder to quantify but arguably more important: the confidence-multiplier. Domestic residents — Turkish citizens, South African businesses — watch what foreign investors do. When foreign capital begins leaving, domestic agents accelerate their own dollar-buying, as a hedge against further depreciation. This creates a self-fulfilling dynamic: capital flight by foreigners triggers defensive dollar purchases by residents, which adds further pressure to the exchange rate, which validates the original decision to buy dollars.

Turkey in 2021 saw a textbook version of this. As TRY approached 14 per dollar, Turkish households and businesses rapidly converted their savings into gold and foreign currency (the "dolarization" phenomenon). By late 2022, more than 60% of Turkish household savings were held in foreign currency or gold — a structural shift that made the central bank's job of stabilizing TRY dramatically harder.

## Case Study 2: South Africa (ZAR) — The Rand as a Geopolitical Barometer

South Africa's rand (ZAR) occupies a peculiar position in the global FX landscape. It is the most liquid African currency by far, and it is actively traded on global exchanges rather than merely quoted. This liquidity, which is a genuine advantage for South Africa's access to capital markets, comes with a cost: ZAR is highly sensitive to global risk sentiment and acts as a real-time barometer of emerging market mood.

But what makes ZAR particularly interesting for geopolitical analysis is that it has a *domestic* geopolitical risk that is as significant as any external shock: the political trajectory of the African National Congress, South Africa's dominant party since the end of apartheid in 1994.

### The ANC and Market Credibility

The ANC's market credibility has followed a specific arc. Under President Thabo Mbeki (1999–2008), and even under Jacob Zuma's early years, South Africa maintained relatively orthodox macroeconomic management. The South African Reserve Bank (SARB) had a strong tradition of independence and inflation targeting. This credibility allowed ZAR to benefit from the global EM carry trade: South Africa offered attractive yields relative to the US, with a reasonably stable exchange rate.

The inflection point came with the "Nene-gate" scandal of December 2015. President Zuma abruptly fired respected Finance Minister Nhlanhla Nene, replacing him with a little-known backbencher, Des van Rooyen. ZAR crashed 9% in a single day — one of the most severe single-day moves in ZAR history outside of the 2020 COVID shock. Zuma reversed the decision within four days and appointed Pravin Gordhan, but the damage to institutional credibility had been done.

#### Worked example:

**Quantifying the Nene-gate shock to a ZAR carry trade position.** A London-based EM fund holds a \$5 million long-ZAR position established when ZAR/USD was 13.5 (13.5 rand per dollar). On December 9, 2015, ZAR opens at 15.9 per dollar — a 17.8% depreciation from the position entry. The fund has \$5 million × 13.5 = ZAR 67,500,000. After the shock, that ZAR position is worth 67,500,000 / 15.9 = \$4,245,283.

The fund has lost \$754,717 — 15.1% of the position — in a single day. If the position was leveraged 3:1 (common for EM carry), the fund had \$15 million notional exposure against \$5 million equity. The mark-to-market loss on the full notional position is \$15 million × 17.8% = \$2.67 million — against equity of \$5 million — a 53.4% loss of capital on the leveraged position, in one day.

This is why geopolitical shocks destroy leveraged EM carry positions so catastrophically. The leverage that amplifies the carry return also amplifies the mark-to-market losses when political risk reprices.

### Load-Shedding and the Energy-Geopolitics Link

South Africa's most persistent market headwind from 2019 to 2024 was not a conventional geopolitical crisis — it was chronic electricity shortages ("load-shedding") caused by the collapse of state utility Eskom. Eskom's dysfunction was not a natural disaster; it was the consequence of a decade of political patronage appointments, infrastructure underinvestment, and corruption under the Zuma administration.

Load-shedding at Stage 6 (rolling 6-hour blackouts) caused GDP growth to fall by roughly 1.5–2 percentage points annually. Manufacturing capacity utilization dropped. Foreign investors reduced South Africa's weight in EM portfolios on the grounds that the electricity crisis was not fixable in the near term. ZAR weakened steadily, from around 15 per dollar in 2021 to 19 per dollar by mid-2023.

The market read ZAR not just as a currency but as a vote on whether South Africa's political class would solve the load-shedding crisis. Every time ANC ministers announced a new energy plan, ZAR rallied. Every time Eskom announced a Stage 6 extension, ZAR sold off. The currency became a real-time prediction market on South African governance quality.

![South Africa rand ZAR/USD 2015 to 2025 with political events](/imgs/blogs/emerging-market-currency-crises-triggered-by-geopolitics-5.png)

### The Zuma Corruption Saga and Its FX Fingerprint

Jacob Zuma's corruption trials — which began in earnest in 2018 and continued through 2023 — created a specific type of geopolitical risk that markets struggle to price: *institutional-degradation risk*. Investors were not primarily worried about Zuma himself. They were worried about what his prosecution (or non-prosecution) signaled about the independence of South African institutions.

When Zuma violated his parole terms in July 2021 and was threatened with re-imprisonment, mass riots broke out in KwaZulu-Natal province, resulting in over 300 deaths and the destruction of logistics infrastructure worth an estimated \$3.4 billion. ZAR fell sharply during the riots, not merely because of the economic damage but because the riots signaled that South Africa's political risk was structural, not episodic.

The Constitutional Court's eventual handling of the Zuma matter, and the 2022 appointment of independent-leaning officials to key Eskom and National Prosecuting Authority positions, contributed to a partial ZAR recovery in late 2022 and 2023. By mid-2024, with load-shedding materially reduced for the first time in years and a Government of National Unity formed after the ANC lost its outright majority in May 2024 elections, ZAR had regained some of its geopolitical discount.

#### Worked example:

**Pricing the ZAR geopolitical risk premium.** The South African Reserve Bank's policy rate in mid-2023 was 8.25%. US Fed Funds was 5.25%. The raw interest rate differential is 3%. But ZAR/USD was trading at around 19.0, significantly weaker than its purchasing power parity (PPP) implied value of roughly 12.5 (based on comparative price levels). The deviation from PPP represents a political and governance discount of approximately (19.0 - 12.5) / 12.5 = 52%.

Investors demanding 3% carry yield to hold ZAR were being compensated for currency risk — but that carry yield was only 3% annually, while the potential downside from another political shock (a 10–15% ZAR depreciation in a week) was orders of magnitude larger. This asymmetry explains why sophisticated EM investors use options (buying ZAR puts for downside protection) rather than naked spot carry when geopolitical risk is elevated.

## Case Study 3: Vietnam (VND) — The Managed Float as Relative Safe Harbor

Vietnam's dong (VND) occupies the opposite end of the EM currency spectrum from TRY and ZAR. It is not freely floating — the State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) manages VND within a band against the US dollar and actively intervenes to prevent large moves in either direction. This managed float regime sacrifices some flexibility but provides a crucial benefit: it removes the violent volatility that comes from free-floating EM currencies facing geopolitical shocks.

### The Mechanics of Vietnam's Managed Float

The SBV announces a central reference rate each morning and allows VND to trade within a band (expanded from ±1% to ±5% from 2016) around that rate. When capital flows create pressure to move outside the band, the SBV intervenes — selling dollars when VND weakens too much, buying dollars when VND strengthens too much. The SBV accumulated substantial foreign exchange reserves through periods of export surplus — reaching approximately \$110 billion by 2021 — which gave it the firepower to defend the managed float.

Vietnam's current account surplus (driven by electronics exports, which reached \$115 billion in 2024 from Samsung, Apple suppliers, and Foxconn factories that relocated from China during the trade war) means the country naturally generates dollar inflows from trade. This structural dollar demand for VND makes the SBV's job fundamentally easier than Turkey's or South Africa's: Vietnam does not need foreign portfolio capital to fund its exchange rate. It earns foreign currency from selling goods.

### Vietnam as a Trade War Safe Harbor

The US-China trade war of 2018–2020 demonstrated how Vietnam can turn a geopolitical event that devastated other EM currencies into a relative advantage. When the US imposed 25% tariffs on Chinese goods in 2019, global supply chains began shifting production to Vietnam. FDI inflows surged to \$23.2 billion in 2023, a record high. Vietnam's exports to the US went from \$33.5 billion in 2015 to \$109.1 billion in 2022 — a 3.3x increase driven substantially by trade war diversion.

![VN-Index 2018 to 2025 Vietnam as relative EM safe harbor during currency turmoil](/imgs/blogs/emerging-market-currency-crises-triggered-by-geopolitics-7.png)

During the 2018–2019 period when TRY was losing 40% and ZAR was under sustained pressure, the VND moved in a narrow band, depreciating only about 3–4% against the dollar from 2018 to 2020. Global EM investors noticed: Vietnam began appearing in "relative safe harbor" narratives despite being an emerging market.

### The Liberation Day Test: April 2025

Vietnam's relative stability was tested sharply on April 2, 2025, when the Trump administration announced "Liberation Day" reciprocal tariffs — and Vietnam was hit with a proposed 46% tariff rate, among the highest in the announcement. The VN-Index fell from 1,310 in January 2025 to 1,195 in April — an 8.8% correction. VND came under modest pressure, depreciating from about 24,200 to 25,100 per dollar (approximately -3.7%).

Critically, this was a dramatically smaller reaction than Vietnam's EM peers. Countries with floating currencies and current account deficits saw far larger FX moves in the same period. Vietnam's managed float, combined with its trade diversification and the SBV's reserve buffer, contained the damage. When the 90-day tariff pause was announced in May 2025, the VN-Index recovered to 1,250 within weeks.

#### Worked example:

**Comparing VND and TRY response to a \$1 billion capital outflow shock.** Suppose a geopolitical shock causes \$1 billion of capital to leave both Turkey and Vietnam simultaneously.

For Turkey (pre-2023 crisis, reserves approximately \$88 billion net, current account deficit 6% of GDP = approximately \$10 billion annual): the \$1 billion outflow represents approximately 11.4% of one year's current account deficit. Daily FX turnover in TRY is approximately \$15–25 billion. The shock is significant relative to the structural deficit, and the reserves-to-import cover was already stressed. Probability of >5% TRY depreciation: high.

For Vietnam (reserves approximately \$90 billion, current account surplus approximately \$12 billion, managed float): the \$1 billion outflow is less than 10 days of current account surplus inflows. The SBV can absorb the outflow using reserves without market-visible stress. Daily FX turnover in VND is far lower (a less liquid market), but the SBV controls the fixing rate directly. Probability of >2% VND depreciation: low, unless the outflow is sustained for weeks.

The structural difference — surplus vs. deficit, managed float vs. free float — makes Vietnam approximately 3–5 times more resilient to a given dollar-size capital outflow than a deficit EM like Turkey.

## Common Misconceptions About EM Currency Crises

### Misconception 1: Currency crises are caused by trade deficits

Trade deficits (*current account* deficits) are a vulnerability, not a cause. Many countries have run large trade deficits for years without experiencing a currency crisis. The crisis trigger is typically a geopolitical shock or a policy credibility loss that causes *capital account* reversal — foreign investors withdrawing their portfolio capital. The trade deficit determines how vulnerable the country is to that withdrawal, not whether the crisis occurs. Malaysia ran a current account deficit in the 1990s and still survived the 1997 Asian financial crisis better than Thailand and Indonesia because Prime Minister Mahathir imposed capital controls.

### Misconception 2: A strong currency means a safe country

A strong currency reflects current conditions, not future resilience. Turkey's TRY was at 2.35 per dollar in January 2015 — a relatively "strong" level by recent standards. An investor who bought TRY in January 2015 citing its "strength" has lost approximately 94% of their dollar value by 2025. Currency strength is backward-looking; what matters is the forward trajectory of the factors driving the exchange rate: current account dynamics, reserve adequacy, central bank credibility, and political stability.

### Misconception 3: Central bank rate hikes always stabilize a currency in crisis

Rate hikes work to stabilize currencies when the crisis is caused by inflation differentials and carry trade unwinding. They can fail — or even backfire — when the crisis is caused by a fundamental loss of credibility. If markets believe the central bank will be forced to cut rates again due to political pressure (as in Turkey 2021), the rate hike is read as temporary and does not attract capital back. Worse, very high rates can crush growth so severely that the probability of fiscal crisis rises, further undermining the currency. The distinction is between a *liquidity crisis* (solvable with rate hikes) and a *solvency or credibility crisis* (where rate hikes are insufficient).

### Misconception 4: EM currency crises are primarily about corruption or bad governance

Governance matters, but timing matters more. South Africa has had governance challenges since 2009, yet ZAR was relatively stable until specific trigger events (Nene-gate, the July 2021 riots). What causes the market to price a known governance problem is usually a catalyst that makes the problem *acute* — a specific political decision that forces a repricing of previously tolerated risk. Markets are not always vigilant. They can ignore known problems for years, then reprice all at once.

### Misconception 5: The NDF market predicts where the onshore rate will go

Non-deliverable forwards (NDFs) for restricted currencies reflect the offshore market's expectation, not the guaranteed future rate. For currencies under capital controls (like VND, or historically like Indian rupee or Chinese yuan), the NDF rate can diverge significantly from the onshore spot rate, reflecting the offshore supply and demand balance rather than the fundamental value. Large NDF-spot divergences are a signal of pressure or stress — but the onshore rate ultimately depends on the central bank's willingness and ability to defend its target.

## How It Shows Up in Real Markets

### The NDF Market: Offshore Access to Restricted Currencies

For most EM currencies, the onshore FX market is either restricted or illiquid for foreign investors. The solution developed over the past 30 years is the **non-deliverable forward (NDF)**: a contract traded offshore in which two counterparties agree on an exchange rate at a future date, with the difference settled in dollars rather than in the restricted currency.

![NDF market how offshore investors trade EM currency risk without holding the currency](/imgs/blogs/emerging-market-currency-crises-triggered-by-geopolitics-8.png)

NDF markets exist for most major EM currencies: TRY, ZAR, INR, BRL, CNY, KRW, VND, NGN, and many others. The market is primarily over-the-counter, traded between large banks in London, Singapore, and New York. Bid-ask spreads are wider than for developed-market FX, and liquidity can deteriorate rapidly during geopolitical stress — precisely when hedging demand is highest. This liquidity dynamic is a critical risk for investors who rely on NDF hedges: the hedge may become costly or difficult to roll at the moment it is needed most.

#### Worked example:

**Constructing an NDF hedge on TRY exposure.** A UK-based company has an outstanding receivable of TRY 50,000,000 from a Turkish distributor, expected to be paid in 90 days. Current TRY/USD spot rate is 32.0. The 90-day NDF rate is quoted at 33.5 (reflecting the interest rate differential and TRY risk premium). The company sells TRY 50,000,000 forward at 33.5 (equivalently, agrees to deliver TRY at 33.5 per dollar in 90 days, to receive \$1,492,537).

Scenario A: TRY depreciates to 36.0 in 90 days. The company receives its TRY 50 million, which at spot is worth 50,000,000 / 36.0 = \$1,388,889. However, the NDF settles: the company receives 50,000,000 / 33.5 - 50,000,000 / 36.0 = \$1,492,537 - \$1,388,889 = \$103,648 in cash from the NDF counterparty. Total receipt: \$1,492,537 — exactly the hedged value.

Scenario B: TRY appreciates to 30.0. The company receives TRY 50 million, worth 50,000,000 / 30.0 = \$1,666,667 at spot. But the NDF settles against them: they pay 50,000,000 / 30.0 - 50,000,000 / 33.5 = \$1,666,667 - \$1,492,537 = \$174,130. Net receipt: \$1,492,537. The hedge locks in the rate in both directions.

The cost of this certainty is the bid-ask spread (typically 40–80 pips wider than spot for TRY NDF in normal conditions, several hundred pips wider during stress) and the loss of upside if TRY appreciates.

### Early Warning Signals: Building a Crisis Dashboard

Practitioners who trade EM currencies around geopolitical events use a set of early-warning signals that can be organized into a monitoring framework.

![EM currency crisis signal matrix early warning indicators](/imgs/blogs/emerging-market-currency-crises-triggered-by-geopolitics-6.png)

**Signal 1: CDS spread velocity.** A country's 5-year CDS spread is the most direct market measure of default risk. What matters is not the level but the *velocity*: a spread that moves from 200 to 300 basis points in a week is more alarming than one that has been at 350 basis points for a year. Velocity suggests the market is repricing a specific new risk, not just reflecting a known background level.

**Signal 2: NDF-spot basis.** When the NDF rate for a restricted currency (like VND or INR) diverges significantly from the onshore spot rate, it signals that offshore investors are trying to hedge or speculate in size — and the onshore market cannot absorb the pressure. A persistently wide NDF-spot basis is an early warning of capital account stress.

**Signal 3: FX reserve trajectory.** Monthly data on central bank reserves is typically released with a 30–45 day lag, but the trajectory of reserve changes over 3–6 months is a powerful signal. Turkey's reserves fell from approximately \$105 billion gross to \$73 billion net in 2020 — a drain that, in retrospect, was a clear signal of the impending TRY crisis.

**Signal 4: Political credibility events.** The appointment or dismissal of the finance minister or central bank governor is one of the highest-signal events in EM investing. Nene-gate in South Africa moved ZAR 9% in a day. Erdogan's repeated firing of central bank governors was the clearest possible signal of policy subordination. Simsek's appointment was the clearest possible signal of the U-turn. These are not subtle events — they are binary credibility changes.

**Signal 5: Dolarization rate.** When domestic residents shift their savings from local currency to dollars (or gold), it reflects a loss of confidence that precedes official market action. Turkey's dolarization rate rose from 45% to over 65% between 2019 and 2022 — a two-year warning that the market eventually confirmed.

## How to Trade It: The Playbook

### Strategy 1: The Geopolitical Short via NDF

When a geopolitical shock hits an EM currency — a coup attempt, a sanctions announcement, a surprise political result — the initial spike in volatility is often followed by a temporary stabilization as markets assess the damage. This "technical bounce" can be used as an entry point for a medium-term NDF short position (bet on further depreciation), using the following framework:

**Entry conditions**: Political credibility has been impaired (central bank governor fired, major sanction imposed, coup attempt followed by democratic backsliding), the current account is in deficit (greater than 3% of GDP), and FX reserves are below 6 months of import cover.

**Position sizing**: No more than 2–3% of portfolio notional in any single EM currency NDF position. Currency crises can resolve faster than expected if an IMF program materializes or credible leadership is appointed.

**Stop-loss**: If a credible orthodox policymaker is appointed (equivalent of Simsek appointment), cover the short immediately. Central bank credibility events are binary.

**Target**: 3–6 month NDF positions, targeting a 20–40% depreciation from entry for a fully-confirmed crisis, or closing at a 10–15% stop-loss if political resolution occurs.

### Strategy 2: The Carry-Adjusted Long After the U-Turn

The Turkish experience after June 2023 demonstrates that the highest-risk-adjusted returns in EM currency trading often come *after* the crisis, not before. When the policy U-turn is credible, carry yields of 30–50% can be earned while the currency stabilizes. The risk is that the U-turn proves temporary.

**Entry conditions**: Orthodox policymaker appointed with track record; policy rate raised to positive real territory (nominal rate minus inflation, even if still negative, is improving); CDS spreads declining from peak; IMF program in place or being negotiated.

**Position sizing**: Larger than in the crisis short — 4–6% of portfolio — because the downside is bounded by the policy rate (the new government will not let the currency fall as fast as the old one), but position must be sized for potential reversal.

**Exit signal**: Political pressure on the central bank resumes, or the policymaker who drove the U-turn resigns or is dismissed.

### Strategy 3: The Vietnam Relative Value Trade

Vietnam offers a different trade: not a directional bet on VND, but a relative value expression in Vietnamese equities. When global EM stress rises — particularly around US-China trade tensions — Vietnam historically outperforms other EM markets because its export-oriented economy benefits from supply chain diversion. The playbook: buy VN-Index exposure (via ETFs or futures) when US-China tensions escalate, and reduce exposure when US-China tensions ease, because the "trade war beneficiary" premium in Vietnamese equities partially unwinds when the trade war de-escalates.

**Signal to buy**: New round of US tariffs on China announced; FDI announcements of major manufacturers shifting from China to Vietnam; VN-Index dips sharply on EM risk-off without fundamental VND reason.

**Signal to reduce**: US-China trade negotiations progressing; VND under specific pressure (separate from EM-wide risk-off); Liberation Day-type tariff risk specifically targeting Vietnam.

**The invalidation**: Vietnam gets directly targeted with tariffs at a level (above 25–30%) that materially damages its export competitiveness. This happened in April 2025 with the initial 46% proposed tariff — but the 90-day pause demonstrated that Vietnam has diplomatic cards to play (it ran a large bilateral surplus with the US and could offer to buy more American goods).

### Sizing and Risk Management

A complete EM geopolitical trade framework requires three discipline rules:

**Rule 1: Asymmetric sizing.** EM currency crises are fat-tailed events. The upside on a confirmed short is large (20–80%) but the probability of getting the timing wrong is also high. Size positions so that a 15–20% adverse move (stop-loss trigger) costs no more than 0.5–1.0% of total portfolio.

**Rule 2: Event risk hedging.** For any significant EM currency position, buy cheap out-of-the-money options (if available) against the tail risk. For TRY in 2022, a 30-day put option on TRY struck 15% out of the money cost approximately 3–4% of notional — expensive, but it paid 10–15x in October 2021 when the currency fell 30% in a month.

**Rule 3: Correlation monitoring.** EM currencies are not independent. A geopolitical shock to one major EM currency (TRY or ZAR) creates contagion risk in other EM currencies as global EM funds reduce overall EM exposure. Monitor the DXY and US 10-year yield simultaneously — a rising dollar and rising US rates create headwinds for *all* EM currencies, independent of the specific geopolitical trigger.
