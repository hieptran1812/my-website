On the night of May 19-20, 2014, the commander of Thailand's armed forces declared martial law and seized control of the government in Bangkok. By the time the Stock Exchange of Thailand opened the next morning, it had fallen 4% — foreign investors dumped Thai baht, and the interbank FX market quoted a spread twice its normal width. Most professional traders had seen this pattern before. What they were watching for in the days that followed was not how far markets fell, but *how fast they recovered.*

They recovered very fast. Within 60 days, the SET had erased the coup decline and continued higher. The Thai baht stabilized. Foreign direct investment continued flowing. The military junta restored civil order, promised elections, and treated the business community as a partner rather than an adversary. This was, in the taxonomy that experienced emerging-market investors use, a *stabilizing coup*.

Two years later, on the night of July 15–16, 2016, a faction of Turkey's military attempted to seize power in Ankara and Istanbul. Tanks blocked the Bosphorus Bridge. Fighter jets flew over parliament. President Erdogan, in a live FaceTime call broadcast on CNN Turk, called on citizens to fill the streets. By dawn, the coup had failed — but markets spent the next *seven years* repricing the institutional damage it triggered.

The Turkish lira, which had traded at 2.88 to the dollar on July 14, 2016, would reach 8, then 18, then 25, then pass 37 by 2025. The coup failed militarily but succeeded in destroying what remained of Turkey's institutional independence. That distinction — between the *event* of a coup and its *institutional aftermath* — is the central insight this post builds from the ground up.

## Foundations: What a coup is, and why markets care about it

Before we get into trading strategies, we need to understand the basic mechanics — both political and financial. If you have no background in either, start here.

### What is a coup d'état?

A *coup d'état* (French for "blow of state") is a sudden seizure of government power by a non-constitutional actor — almost always the military, or a faction within it. It is different from a revolution (which involves mass popular uprising) and from an election (which transfers power legally). A coup is fast, usually overnight, and forceful.

The key economic question is not whether the coup succeeds militarily — most do, within 24–72 hours. The key question is what happens *after* it succeeds: does it create a stable new order, or does it fracture the institutions that allow markets to function?

### Why do markets react so violently to coups?

Markets trade on predictability. When a coup happens, several things become unpredictable simultaneously:

**Property rights**: Will the new government honor existing contracts, licenses, and concessions? An investor who owns a telecom license or a mining concession needs to know whether the new government considers that contract valid.

**Currency policy**: Will the central bank remain independent? If military leaders start dictating monetary policy, inflation expectations shift immediately.

**Capital controls**: Will the government restrict moving money out of the country? This is the question that terrifies foreign portfolio investors most acutely.

**Sovereign debt service**: Will the government continue paying its bonds? Countries in political turmoil sometimes default not because they lack the money, but because the new leadership wants to renegotiate terms.

All four uncertainties hit simultaneously on coup day, which is why the price reaction is so fast and so wide.

### Key financial terms defined

**Circuit breaker**: A rule that automatically halts trading on a stock exchange when prices fall too fast (typically 5–10% in a short window). It gives markets time to absorb information rather than panic-sell into a vacuum. After Thailand's 2014 coup, the SET's circuit breakers were triggered within the first hour.

**Sovereign spread**: The difference in interest rate (yield) between a country's government bonds and an equivalent US Treasury bond. Think of it as the "risk premium" lenders charge for lending to that government. If US 10-year bonds yield 4% and Thailand's 10-year bonds yield 5.5%, the sovereign spread is 150 basis points (bps). A wider spread means "markets think this country is riskier."

**Currency depreciation**: When a country's currency buys fewer dollars. If the Thai baht falls from 33 THB/USD to 34 THB/USD, the baht has depreciated about 3%. After a coup, the domestic currency almost always falls because foreign investors want to get their money out and must sell the local currency to buy dollars.

**Capital flight**: The rapid movement of money out of a country, usually triggered by political instability. It amplifies currency depreciation — the more investors rush for the exit, the more the currency falls, which scares more investors into rushing for the exit.

**EMBI spread** (Emerging Market Bond Index spread): A measure published by JP Morgan that tracks how much extra yield emerging-market sovereign bonds pay over US Treasuries. It is a standard benchmark for country-level political/credit risk.

### The three asset classes that move on coup day

**Equities** react first and fastest. The stock market is the most liquid and visible market, and circuit breakers get triggered quickly. The initial drop is a combination of: foreign investors selling to convert to dollars, domestic investors selling on uncertainty, and market makers widening spreads dramatically.

**Currency** reacts almost simultaneously with equities. The speed of the currency move depends on how open the capital account is. Thailand's relatively open account meant the baht moved freely; Myanmar's heavily managed kyat (officially) masked the full depreciation in official markets while the black-market rate moved much faster.

**Sovereign bonds** move slightly more slowly because the bond market is less liquid and more institutional. But within 24 hours, sovereign spreads on the affected country's bonds will have widened measurably — even before the coup's outcome is certain.

## Foundations: How to measure "permanent" versus "temporary" repricing

The central analytical question for a coup is: is this a temporary shock (markets overshoot, then recover as order is restored) or permanent repricing (the institutional damage means the country is genuinely riskier going forward)?

The distinction matters enormously for trading. In a temporary shock, the right trade is to buy the selloff after 48 hours. In a permanent repricing, selling is correct and there is no bounce to buy.

History suggests the difference comes down to *institutions*: specifically, whether the coup leaves central bank independence, rule of law, property rights, and press freedom intact or destroys them.

## The structural anatomy of a coup market shock

To trade coups well, you need to understand *why* the three main asset classes respond as they do — not just that they respond. Each asset class is reacting to a different underlying fear, and those fears have different speeds, different magnitudes, and different conditions under which they resolve.

### Equities: the ownership-rights shock

A stock is a legal claim on a company's future earnings and assets. The value of that claim depends critically on two things: that the company will continue to operate (not be nationalized, shut down, or stripped of its licenses), and that the courts will enforce the underlying contract. When a coup happens, both assumptions become uncertain.

The equity selloff on coup day is driven primarily by foreign institutional investors — pension funds, ETFs, and sovereign wealth funds that are required by mandate to reduce exposure to "heightened political risk" events. This selling pressure hits hardest in the most liquid large-cap stocks (which is ironic, because those are precisely the stocks whose underlying businesses are most likely to be fine).

The circuit-breaker mechanism, paradoxically, both amplifies and dampens the shock. It amplifies it by preventing price discovery — sellers who cannot execute panic more, and buyers who could absorb at certain prices step back from the market entirely. But it dampens it by preventing an all-at-once clearing event that might send prices to absurd levels. After the circuit breaker lifts, markets frequently gap back up as buyers who had stepped back re-enter at clearly attractive prices.

### Currencies: the monetary-credibility shock

A country's currency is a claim on the future purchasing power of that country's monetary system. Its value rests on confidence in the central bank's independence and competence. When a coup happens, three currency-specific fears activate:

**Convertibility risk**: Will the new government impose capital controls? If foreign investors cannot convert their local-currency assets back to dollars (or euros, or yen), the assets become effectively stranded. This fear alone drives massive pre-emptive selling of the local currency, even before any controls are actually announced.

**Inflation risk**: Will the new government respect the central bank's independence? If military commanders start dictating monetary policy — as happened in Myanmar and eventually in Turkey — inflation expectations will rise, which mechanically devalues the currency.

**Reserve depletion risk**: Will the new government use foreign reserves to defend a fixed exchange rate? Countries that burn through reserves defending unsustainable pegs eventually experience sharp devaluations. Markets try to price this in advance by selling the currency before the reserves run out.

The overnight NDF (non-deliverable forward) market in Singapore and London reflects all three fears simultaneously. This is why currency markets move even before the domestic stock exchange opens — NDF traders in Singapore and London can price the Thai baht, Turkish lira, or Myanmar kyat 24 hours a day, while the local stock exchange may only open at 9 AM.

### Sovereign bonds: the credit-risk shock

A government bond is a promise: "I, the sovereign, will pay you X interest every six months and return your principal at maturity." The value of that promise depends on the government's willingness and ability to pay. A coup puts both in doubt.

**Willingness**: A new government might repudiate the debts of its predecessor, particularly if the previous government is framed as corrupt or illegitimate. Argentina has done this multiple times; the concern surfaces every time there is a sharp change in government ideology.

**Ability**: Political instability disrupts tax collection, deters investment, and may require emergency fiscal spending (on security forces, or to compensate coup victims, or to survive international sanctions). This deteriorates the fiscal picture, which bond investors price through higher yields.

The sovereign bond market also serves as a leading indicator of market recovery. When coup-related sovereign spread widening begins to reverse — usually because the 48-hour signals suggest stability — it signals that institutional investors have moved from panic to analysis mode. Bond spread compression typically leads equity recovery by 1–2 weeks.

## The political calculus: why militaries launch coups

Understanding *why* a coup happens helps predict what comes next. Coups are not random. They typically occur in one of three conditions:

**Institutional deadlock**: When the civilian government has become so dysfunctional or corrupt that the military acts as a "corrective" — often with implicit or explicit business-community support. These coups tend to be stabilizing. Thailand 2014 fits this mold: the civilian government of PM Yingluck Shinawatra had been paralyzed by street protests, and the business community in Bangkok largely accepted the military's intervention.

**Power grab by a strongman faction**: When a military faction (not the whole institution) uses a political crisis to consolidate power for a specific leader. These tend to be destabilizing because they weaken internal military cohesion and invite institutional capture. Turkey's failed 2016 coup paradoxically enabled this — Erdogan's response to the coup attempt gave him the pretext to purge the civil service, judiciary, academia, and press.

**External pressure + internal weakness**: When a government teeters under economic crisis or external sanctions, and the military steps in as a last resort. Myanmar 2021 had elements of this — but the junta also had its own business interests in maintaining control of state enterprises.

The practical implication: read the political context *before* the coup happens, not just during. A coup in a country with weak institutions, concentrated military political interests, and no functioning opposition party is far more likely to produce lasting damage than one in a country with strong civil society and a business class that wants stability.

## The 24-hour timeline: what moves, and when

Let's walk through the sequence of events that typically unfolds in the first 24 hours after a coup announcement:

**Hour 0: Announcement (or credible rumor)**
The instant credible rumors of a coup surface — often first on social media, before official confirmation — FX markets move. Currency spot rates in NDF (non-deliverable forward) markets in Singapore and London will gap immediately, sometimes 2–5% before the domestic market even opens.

**Hours 1–3: Circuit breakers trigger**
If the domestic equity market is open (coups often happen at night or on weekends to avoid this), circuit breakers will trip within minutes. If the market is closed, opening auctions the next morning will show large indicated drops, and regulators will often delay the open.

**Hours 3–8: Sovereign bond prices fall**
Institutional bond investors in London and New York wake up to the news. The country's dollar-denominated sovereign bonds (which trade continuously on international markets) will have already repriced by the time European markets open. Spreads widen 50–200 bps in the first hours.

**Hours 8–24: Information triage**
Now the critical signals emerge. What is the constitutional status of the new government? What is the international community saying? Has the business community endorsed, opposed, or stayed silent? These signals — more than the initial price move — determine whether day 2 is a recovery or a further selloff.

## Case Study 1: Thailand 2014 — the stability premium

### Political context

Thailand has experienced more military coups than any other country in the 20th–21st century — 13 successful coups since 1932. This history itself is part of why Thailand's coups produce more muted market reactions: investors have a large sample of Thai coup outcomes to reference.

The 2014 coup came at the end of a prolonged political crisis. Prime Minister Yingluck Shinawatra, a sister of exiled former PM Thaksin Shinawatra, had been accused of corruption and removed by the Constitutional Court in May 2014. Anti-government protests had paralyzed Bangkok for months. Business groups were frustrated by the gridlock. The army had declared martial law on May 20, 2014, before formally seizing power on May 22.

### Market reaction

The Stock Exchange of Thailand (SET) fell 4.2% on May 22, 2014, the day the coup was announced. The Thai baht weakened from 32.7 to 33.2 per dollar — a depreciation of about 1.5%. Sovereign spreads on Thai USD bonds widened roughly 20–30 bps in the immediate aftermath.

These are *mild* reactions by coup standards. Why? Several signals emerged within 48 hours that traders interpreted as stabilizing:

1. **The junta announced an interim civilian government structure** within 72 hours — not an indefinite military rule.
2. **Major Thai business groups issued cautious but non-hostile statements.** The Thai Chamber of Commerce noted the need for stability.
3. **US and EU condemnation was sharp verbally** (suspension of military aid, cancellation of joint exercises) but did not escalate to economic sanctions.
4. **The military secured Bangkok rapidly** with no meaningful resistance.

#### Worked example:

A London-based EM fund held THB 2 billion in Thai government bonds (approximately \$61 million at the pre-coup rate of 32.7). On coup day, the portfolio's USD value fell to approximately \$60 million (THB/USD at 33.2) — a loss of about \$1 million from currency alone, plus roughly 20 bps of spread widening on the bonds themselves.

If the fund's risk manager modeled this as a "stabilizing coup" based on the 48-hour signals above, they would hold the position rather than selling. By end of July 2014 (60 days post-coup), the THB had returned to approximately 32.5, and the SET had recovered fully. The fund would have recovered its mark-to-market loss and earned additional carry on the Thai government bonds. The correct trade was to hold — or to buy the dip.

### The stability premium

Thailand's 2014 coup produced something counterintuitive: after the initial selloff, the SET actually outperformed the broader MSCI Emerging Markets index over the following 3 months. Markets had been *pricing in political chaos*, and the coup resolved that uncertainty. This "stability premium" — where orderly authoritarian resolution outperforms prolonged democratic dysfunction — is a recurring pattern across EM coups. It sounds uncomfortable, but markets are analytically amoral: they price expected cash flows, not moral legitimacy.

## Case Study 2: Turkey July 2016 — institutional erosion as a 7-year repricing

### Political context

Turkey in July 2016 was already exhibiting pre-coup stress indicators. President Erdogan had been centralizing power since 2013, and the relationship between his AKP party and the Gülen movement (a religious-political network that had supported Erdogan but then broke with him) had become violently hostile. Erdogan had called Gülenists a "parallel state" and began purging suspected Gülenists from the judiciary and police.

On the night of July 15, 2016, elements within the Turkish military — later attributed to Gülenists — attempted to seize power. Tanks blocked the Bosphorus Bridge. F-16s flew low over Ankara. Parliament was bombed. Erdogan, via phone call broadcast on a news channel, called citizens into the streets. Crowds confronted tanks. By dawn, the coup had failed, with around 250 people dead.

### Market reaction — the overnight window

![Turkish lira TRY/USD 2015-2025: 7-year destabilizing coup repricing](/imgs/blogs/coups-and-market-crashes-thailand-turkey-myanmar-anatomy-3.png)

The immediate market reaction was severe: the Turkish lira fell 4.7% overnight (from 2.88 to 3.02 TRY/USD in NDF markets) before the domestic market opened. When the Istanbul Stock Exchange (BIST100) opened on Monday July 18 (markets were closed Monday as officials assessed damage), it fell 7.1%. Turkish Eurobond spreads widened approximately 80 bps.

By surface-level metrics, this looked comparable to Thailand 2014 — and in the first 14 days, markets did partially recover. The TRY retraced to around 2.94, and the BIST100 recovered most of its losses. A naive observer might have concluded this was a stabilizing coup like Thailand's.

### The institutional destruction that followed

What made Turkey fundamentally different was what Erdogan did *with* the coup attempt. Within days, he declared a state of emergency that lasted two years. A "purge list" that appeared suspiciously pre-prepared targeted:

- Over 150 judges and prosecutors detained in the first 24 hours (later expanded to over 4,000)
- More than 6,000 military personnel arrested
- Over 21,000 teachers dismissed from schools
- Eventually: 150,000+ people detained, fired, or arrested across the civil service, academia, judiciary, media, and military

This was not the behavior of a government restoring constitutional order. It was the behavior of a government using an attempted coup as a pretext for total institutional capture. The key signal that markets eventually read: **central bank independence was the next casualty.**

Starting in 2019 and accelerating in 2021, Erdogan began firing central bank governors who raised interest rates, insisting (against orthodox economic theory) that high interest rates *cause* inflation. The TRY fell from 8 to 18 in a matter of months in late 2021, as the central bank cut rates while inflation was running at 80%. This was the direct institutional consequence of the post-coup purge — Erdogan had replaced independent technocrats with loyalists who could be pressured to pursue unorthodox policy.

#### Worked example:

A fund manager bought TRY-denominated Turkish government bonds in August 2016 — one month after the coup — at a yield of approximately 9.5%, believing the worst was priced in. Face value: \$10 million equivalent in TRY. The trade thesis: Erdogan survived, the coup failed, Turkey is a NATO member with strong FDI flows — the risk premium should normalize.

By December 2021 (5.5 years later), the TRY had depreciated from approximately TRY/USD 3.0 to TRY/USD 18.0 — a depreciation of 83% in USD terms. The coupon income over 5.5 years (at ~9.5% per year) would have been approximately TRY equivalent of \$5.5 million — but on a currency that had lost 83% of its value. In USD terms, the coupon income netted perhaps \$1 million. The principal, if repatriated at TRY 18 instead of TRY 3, was worth approximately \$1.7 million instead of \$10 million.

Total USD return on a nominal TRY 9.5% yield over 5.5 years: approximately -73%.

This is the mathematics of ignoring institutional degradation signals.

### The 48-hour signals that differentiated Turkey from Thailand

| Signal | Thailand 2014 | Turkey 2016 |
|--------|--------------|------------|
| Constitutional promise | Elections pledged within 2 years | State of emergency declared |
| Purge scale | Minimal civilian casualties | 150,000+ detained/fired |
| Central bank independence | Maintained | Compromised within 3 years |
| Business community | Cautious support | Fragmented, fearful |
| Judiciary | Independent structure maintained | Mass judicial purge |
| Press freedom | Constrained but present | Major crackdown |

The key takeaway: **the coup was a catalyst, not the cause.** Turkey's institutional erosion had pre-coup roots in Erdogan's consolidation of power since 2013. The coup attempt simply accelerated a process that was already underway. Investors who tracked institutional quality indicators rather than just the coup-day price action had earlier signals.

## Case Study 3: Myanmar February 2021 — structural economic destruction

### Political context

Myanmar's February 1, 2021 coup was in some ways the most "predictable" of the three cases: the military (Tatmadaw) had never fully ceded power during Myanmar's 2011 democratic transition, and commander-in-chief Senior General Min Aung Hlaing had made it clear he considered himself a political actor, not merely a defense minister. When the November 2020 elections produced a landslide for Aung San Suu Kyi's National League for Democracy (NLD), and the military claimed (without credible evidence) widespread fraud, the coup became a matter of when, not if.

On February 1, 2021, at 3 AM local time, the military detained Suu Kyi, President Win Myint, and other NLD leaders. A one-year state of emergency was declared (later extended to two, then five years). Min Aung Hlaing announced he would take power as Prime Minister.

### Market reaction — extreme because of pre-existing vulnerability

![Myanmar after Feb 2021 coup: GDP collapse and kyat freefall](/imgs/blogs/coups-and-market-crashes-thailand-turkey-myanmar-anatomy-5.png)

Myanmar's financial markets were far less developed than Thailand's or Turkey's, which made the shock transmission more extreme:

**Stock exchange**: The Yangon Stock Exchange (YSX), which had only six listed companies at the time, was effectively halted. Official trading suspended for 90+ days. When it eventually reopened, many investors simply could not repatriate capital.

**Currency**: The official kyat/USD rate moved from approximately MMK 1,330 to MMK 1,800 within the first year — a formal depreciation of about 35%. But the *black market rate* moved far faster, reaching MMK 2,800–3,000 by end of 2022. As a practical matter, any foreign investor trying to exit Myanmar had to use a rate closer to 2,800 than 1,800, implying a real loss closer to 55–60%.

**GDP**: The IMF estimated Myanmar's real GDP contracted 18% in 2021 — one of the largest peacetime economic contractions ever recorded. This was driven by: a banking system freeze (citizens withdrew cash en masse, banks ran out of ATM liquidity), a collapse in FDI (-60% as reported by the World Bank), COVID-19 still suppressing activity, and civil conflict preventing normal economic functioning.

**Sovereign debt**: Myanmar's sovereign spread was essentially meaningless as an indicator — the country had so little external debt that the market was illiquid. The practical indicator was FDI flows and official development aid, both of which collapsed.

#### Worked example:

A Singapore-based private equity fund had invested \$5 million in a Myanmar real-estate joint venture in 2019, believing the post-Suu Kyi liberalization would continue. The investment was in a JV with a local partner, structured in kyat for regulatory reasons.

By end of 2022: the kyat value of the investment was nominally intact on paper (local currency valuation), but attempting to repatriate at the black-market rate of MMK 2,800 vs the 2019 rate of roughly MMK 1,500 implied a USD loss of approximately 46%. The local JV partner had also frozen operations due to civil conflict in the region. Practical liquidation value: approximately \$1.5–2 million against a \$5 million investment — a loss of 60–70%.

This is why frontier-market investing in countries with weak civil-military relations carries tail risk that conventional financial models cannot fully capture.

### The kyat at -45% — and why it didn't stop there

The scale of Myanmar's currency depreciation tells the story of a state whose monetary infrastructure collapsed. The Central Bank of Myanmar had already been under pressure before the coup, but after February 2021:

1. The new military government ordered banks to facilitate junta transactions, which accelerated capital flight.
2. International sanctions (US, EU, UK) froze military-controlled businesses out of the international financial system.
3. The banking sector lost depositor confidence; a bank run began in February 2021 and continued for months.
4. Remittances, which had been a significant source of foreign exchange, dropped sharply as the diaspora refused to send money through channels the junta controlled.

The result: the kyat had no anchor. By 2024, it was trading around MMK 3,200 per dollar — a total depreciation of approximately 140% from pre-coup levels.

## The 48-hour classification framework

![48-hour coup classification matrix: stabilizing vs destabilizing signals](/imgs/blogs/coups-and-market-crashes-thailand-turkey-myanmar-anatomy-4.png)

Based on the three case studies and a broader review of coup outcomes (Thailand 2006, Pakistan 1999, Ecuador 2000, Honduras 2009, Fiji 2006, Egypt 2013, Sudan 2019, Mali 2020, Guinea 2021), four signals reliably classify a coup within 48 hours:

### Signal 1: Constitutional status (most important)

Does the coup leadership articulate a clear constitutional path? Specifically: do they reference elections, a transition timetable, or an interim civilian government? Or do they declare indefinite military rule, suspend the constitution entirely, or make no mention of restoring democracy?

Thailand 2014: elections promised (though delayed). **Stabilizing**.
Turkey 2016: state of emergency, no timeline. **Ambiguous → destabilizing**.
Myanmar 2021: one-year emergency declared, repeatedly extended. **Destabilizing**.

### Signal 2: International response speed

How quickly do major powers respond, and with what force? A rapid, unified, sanctions-heavy response signals that the international community considers the coup illegitimate and will impose economic costs. A slow, divided, or rhetorical-only response signals that major powers either accept the outcome or lack leverage.

Thailand 2014: US/EU verbally condemned, suspended some military cooperation — but no economic sanctions. **Mild pressure = stabilizing signal**.
Turkey 2016: NATO allies were muted — Turkey is a critical NATO member and EU candidate. **Weak condemnation = ambiguous**.
Myanmar 2021: US, EU, UK, Canada, Japan all imposed targeted sanctions within weeks. EU suspended trade preferences. **Strong condemnation = destabilizing**.

### Signal 3: Business community stance

Domestic and international business groups often have the clearest read on whether the new power structure will respect property rights and contract enforcement. Their public statements within 48 hours are highly predictive.

Thailand 2014: Thai Chamber of Commerce accepted the transition. Key foreign multinationals with Thai operations (Toyota, Shell, HSBC) issued statements noting they would continue normal operations. **Supportive = stabilizing**.
Turkey 2016: TUSIAD (Turkey's leading business association) called for a return to democracy and civil law — a signal that the business community was not fully supportive of Erdogan's post-coup consolidation. **Opposed = destabilizing**.
Myanmar 2021: Foreign chambers of commerce in Myanmar issued coordinated statements suspending activities. The EU Chamber and Singapore Business Federation both issued non-engagement guidance. **Opposed = destabilizing**.

### Signal 4: Military cohesion

Is the coup executed by the whole military command, or by a faction? A fractured military — where one faction seizes power but others remain loyal to the civilian government — signals prolonged internal conflict, which is catastrophically bad for markets.

Thailand 2014: entire Royal Thai Armed Forces leadership acted in concert. **Cohesive = stabilizing**.
Turkey 2016: a *faction* attempted the coup; the bulk of the military remained loyal to Erdogan. The coup failed precisely because it lacked cohesion. This creates a paradox: the failed coup was "stabilizing" in the immediate sense (the constitutional government survived), but "destabilizing" because it enabled Erdogan's purge. **Unusual case — treat as destabilizing for long-run asset prices**.
Myanmar 2021: the entire Tatmadaw leadership acted together. But the civilian resistance (Civil Disobedience Movement, People's Defence Force) created persistent low-level civil conflict. **Nominally cohesive, practically contested = destabilizing**.

## The recovery timeline: patterns across history

![Three coup case studies: asset repricing over 7 years](/imgs/blogs/coups-and-market-crashes-thailand-turkey-myanmar-anatomy-6.png)

Studying coup outcomes across the past 30 years reveals a consistent pattern:

**Stabilizing coups** (Thailand 2014, Thailand 2006, Pakistan 1999 "market phase"):
- Equity circuit-breaker drop in first session: 3–6%
- Currency depreciation, first week: 1–3%
- Full equity recovery: 30–90 days
- Bond spread normalization: 30–60 days

**Failed-then-destabilizing coups** (Turkey 2016):
- Initial market bounce as "stability" seems restored
- Slow institutional erosion over 6–24 months
- Currency reaches new lows 2–3 years post-coup
- Full permanent repricing: 3–7 years

**Successful-destabilizing coups** (Myanmar 2021, Zimbabwe 2017):
- Equity market halted or suspended for weeks
- Currency continues depreciating indefinitely
- GDP contraction of 5–20% in first year
- No return to pre-coup asset levels

## The institutional quality index: tracking degradation in advance

One of the most actionable research insights from studying coup outcomes is that *institutional degradation is measurable and gradual, not sudden*. By the time a coup actually happens, the country's institutional quality has usually been declining for 2–5 years. This means investors who track institutional indicators proactively — not just after a coup breaks — have significant advance warning.

Key institutional quality metrics that predict post-coup market outcomes:

**V-Dem Liberal Democracy Index** (Varieties of Democracy, University of Gothenburg): Scores countries 0–1 on electoral, liberal, participatory, deliberative, and egalitarian components. Turkey's V-Dem score fell from 0.52 in 2013 to 0.31 in 2018 — a measurable decline that preceded the post-coup lira collapse. Myanmar's score collapsed from 0.32 in 2020 to 0.02 in 2022, one of the sharpest drops ever recorded.

**World Bank Governance Indicators** (published annually): Includes "Rule of Law," "Control of Corruption," and "Government Effectiveness." These lag by 12–18 months but provide credible cross-country comparisons.

**Reporters Without Borders Press Freedom Index**: Press freedom erosion strongly correlates with central bank independence erosion — both reflect a government's willingness to operate within institutional constraints.

**Central bank independence score** (Garriga 2016 database, updated): The most direct financial-markets indicator. When the de-facto independence score falls below the statutory independence score by a large margin, it means the central bank is being overridden in practice even if the law hasn't changed. Turkey's de-facto independence score began declining in 2019 — three years before the lira's worst phase.

#### Worked example:

An EM macro fund conducts a quarterly "governance screen" on its top 10 EM positions. In Q1 2020, the screen flags Turkey: V-Dem score -0.08 year-over-year (one of the five largest declines globally), press freedom rank 165/180 (worsening trend), and the World Bank "Rule of Law" indicator at the 32nd percentile (falling). Central bank de-facto independence: declining since the 2019 governor change.

The fund reduces its Turkey allocation from 4% to 2% of the portfolio. It also purchases a strip of TRY/USD call options (right to buy USD at TRY 8) with 12-month maturity, costing approximately 180 bps of the position value in premium.

By end of 2021, TRY/USD had reached 18 (call options at strike 8 were deep in the money — a 125% return on the option premium, net of time value decay). The reduced equity position had also saved significant drawdown losses. The options hedge cost 180 bps for 12 months but returned approximately 2,200 bps (22 percentage points) in profit on notional.

This is institutional-quality tracking as a systematic investment edge.

## Common misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "A failed coup is always stabilizing for markets"**

The Turkey 2016 case disproves this. A failed coup can be *more* destabilizing than a successful one if the government uses the failure as a pretext for purging institutions. The key variable is not whether the coup succeeds militarily, but whether the *response* to the coup preserves or destroys institutional independence.

**Misconception 2: "The currency fall on coup day tells you how bad it will be"**

Not reliably. Thailand's THB fell 1.5% and was a stabilizing event. Turkey's TRY fell 4.7% overnight and was a 7-year debasement event. Myanmar's kyat fell more slowly (because of capital controls that suppressed the official rate) but suffered the worst long-run depreciation. The initial currency move reflects liquidity and positioning, not the long-term institutional damage.

**Misconception 3: "Emerging-market coups always recover — buy the dip"**

This was a profitable rule of thumb in the 1970s–1990s, when many EM countries had short military interludes that gave way to economic liberalization. It is less reliable in the post-2010 era, where several "coups" have led to sustained authoritarian entrenchment (Venezuela pre-coup trajectory, Sudan, Myanmar, Mali, Niger, Burkina Faso). The institutional quality signal matters more than the historical base rate.

**Misconception 4: "Military coups are always bad for business"**

Several of the world's fastest-growing economies in the 1960s–1980s (South Korea, Taiwan, Singapore, Chile in its early phase, Indonesia under Suharto) operated under military-dominated governments. What matters for investment returns is property rights enforcement and macroeconomic stability, not the formal composition of the government. A military government that creates stable rules and enforces contracts can produce strong equity markets. A democratic government that cycles through instability and policy reversal can produce terrible equity returns.

**Misconception 5: "You can't hedge political risk"**

You can, through several channels: sovereign CDS (credit default swaps on government bonds), currency options (buying USD/local currency puts at current strike prices for 3–6 months), and reducing emerging-market position concentration by country. In Thailand in May 2014, anyone who purchased 3-month USD/THB call options in the weeks before the coup (when political uncertainty was already elevated) would have seen substantial payoffs even from the mild 1.5% currency move, because implied volatility on those options was very low pre-coup.

## How it shows up in real markets: the trade playbook

### The 48-hour decision tree

The practical playbook for any institutional investor holding EM assets when a coup breaks is:

**Hours 0–12**: Do not trade. Markets are dislocated, spreads are wide, and liquidity is poor. The only exception: if you have large concentrated single-country positions that exceed your risk limits, reduce to risk-limit levels — but accept that you are getting the worst execution.

**Hours 12–48**: Gather the four classification signals. Score Thailand 2014: three of four stabilizing (constitutional path, business support, military cohesion — but international response was mixed). Score Turkey 2016: two of four destabilizing immediately, two ambiguous. Score Myanmar 2021: all four destabilizing.

**Hours 48–72**: If three or more signals are stabilizing, the base case is recovery within 30–90 days. Consider adding to positions at the dislocated price. If two or more signals are destabilizing, reduce exposure further and treat the event as a structural repricing, not a temporary dislocation.

**Days 7–14**: Review international response. A single UNSC statement, a G7 communiqué, or targeted financial sanctions (not just visa bans) significantly increase the probability of the destabilizing classification.

#### Worked example:

A trader runs an EM equity portfolio with a 3% position in Thai equities (SET ETF) on May 20, 2014. The coup is announced overnight. At open on May 22:

- The SET is indicated down 4%.
- The 3% position is now worth approximately 2.88% of the portfolio.
- The risk limit on single-country EM exposure is 5% position change maximum per day.

**Decision**: Hold. The classification signals at 48 hours are: constitutional process signaled (✓), business community accepting (✓), international condemnation mild (✓), military cohesion high (✓). All four stabilizing. The base case is recovery in 30–60 days. Do not sell into the panic.

**Outcome**: The SET recovered to pre-coup levels within 60 days. The trader who held avoided crystallizing a 4% loss on a 3% position (a 12 bps portfolio loss) and earned the recovery.

Compare this to a trader holding a 3% position in Turkish equities (BIST100) on July 15, 2016, who applied the same framework:

48-hour classification: constitutional process unclear (ambiguous), business community opposed to Erdogan's consolidation (destabilizing), international response mixed (ambiguous), military cohesion failed coup but Erdogan purge began (destabilizing). Two destabilizing, two ambiguous — reduce exposure.

The correct trade was to reduce Turkish equity exposure significantly in the first 48 hours after the coup attempt, even as markets were partially recovering. Over the following 2 years, Turkish equities fell approximately 35% in USD terms.

### The currency options hedge

For investors who cannot exit equity positions quickly (due to regulatory constraints, lock-up periods, or illiquidity), currency options provide a faster-moving hedge:

**Buy 3-month USD/TRY call options** (right to buy USD at a fixed TRY price) immediately after the Turkey coup attempt, when implied volatility was elevated but not extreme. Strike price: approximately 3.20 (5% out of the money from the 3.02 spot). Maturity: October 15, 2016.

By October 2016, TRY/USD had moved to approximately 3.10–3.15. The options would have expired near the money — a modest gain. But the *tail hedge* for the next 12–24 months of institutional deterioration would have been a series of rolling 3-month call options that cumulatively delivered strong protection as TRY moved from 3 to 8 over 2 years.

### The sovereign CDS spread trade

For fixed-income investors, sovereign CDS (a form of insurance against government default) can provide protection in the immediate post-coup period. In Turkey after the 2016 coup:

- 5-year Turkey CDS spread was approximately 280 bps before the coup.
- Within one week it widened to approximately 350 bps.
- By late 2016 it was back at ~290 bps as the "coup is resolved" narrative took hold.
- By 2018, it had widened to 450 bps as institutional damage became clear.

A trader who bought Turkey CDS at 280 and sold at 450 would have made roughly 170 bps of notional — on a 5-year \$10 million notional CDS position, that is approximately \$850,000 in mark-to-market gain.

#### Worked example:

**Myanmar CDS: a market that didn't exist**

Myanmar illustrates a practical limitation of the playbook: not all countries have liquid sovereign CDS markets. Myanmar had no internationally traded sovereign CDS. The only financial instruments affected were:

1. Myanmar Investment Commission license values (not traded).
2. Private equity stakes in Myanmar businesses (illiquid).
3. Real estate holdings (completely illiquid after the coup).
4. Kyat-denominated cash and bank balances (capital control risk).

For investors in Myanmar, there was *no liquid hedge available*. The only option was to have pre-positioned with currency forward contracts through the few international banks that offered them — and even those markets dried up within days of the coup. This is a recurring feature of frontier markets: the political risk is highest precisely where the hedging instruments are least available.

## How to trade it: the practical playbook

### Before a coup (pre-positioning)

Political risk is rarely completely invisible before it materializes. Warning signs include:

- Prolonged political deadlock in a country with a history of military intervention
- Rising civil-military tension (senior officers making political statements, military budget under civilian pressure)
- GPR Index (Geopolitical Risk Index, Caldara-Iacoviello) for the country showing sustained elevation (data available at matteoiacoviello.com)
- Worsening "V-Dem" or Freedom House index scores (measurable governance deterioration)
- Ruling party moves against judiciary or press independence

**Pre-positioning**: Reduce single-country concentration. Buy currency puts (protection against currency fall) on the domestic currency. Buy sovereign CDS if available. Keep EM country allocations at or below risk limits — don't chase EM momentum into politically fragile countries.

### Day 0–2: the coup breaks

1. **Do not panic-sell into the circuit-breaker dip.** You are getting the worst price. Mark the position at the dislocated price and assess.
2. **Check international response velocity** — how fast is condemnation, and from whom? EU + US + Japan coordinated response within 24 hours = destabilizing signal.
3. **Check constitutional status** — is there a transition timetable?
4. **Check business associations' statements** — local chamber of commerce, international chambers operating in country.

### Day 2–14: the classification decision

By day 2–14, you have enough information to make a high-confidence classification. Use the four-signal matrix. If two or more signals are "destabilizing," treat the event as a structural shift and reduce exposure to your minimum allocation for that country or region.

If two or more signals are "stabilizing," the recovery trade is on: add to equity and bond positions at the dislocated price, and hold for the 30–90 day recovery window.

### Building the event-study framework

Professional EM investors often use a formal *event study* methodology to quantify how much of the post-coup price move is "explained" by the coup itself versus pre-existing macro trends. Here is how to apply it:

**Step 1: Estimate the "normal" return**. Using the 60 trading days before the coup, run a regression of the affected country's equity index on the MSCI Emerging Markets index (or relevant regional benchmark). This gives you the country's beta to EM markets.

**Step 2: Calculate the abnormal return (AR)**. For each day after the coup: AR = Actual daily return − (Beta × MSCI EM return that day).

**Step 3: Cumulate the ARs**. Sum the abnormal returns over the event window (typically days -5 to +30 around the coup) to get the Cumulative Abnormal Return (CAR).

**Step 4: Interpret the CAR trajectory**. If the CAR is fully recovered by day +30, the coup was a temporary shock. If the CAR continues declining 30 days out, the repricing is structural.

Thailand 2014 event study (approximate, using SET vs MSCI EM): CAR peaked at approximately -5% on day +3, then recovered to 0% by day +45, then turned positive by day +90. Consistent with a temporary shock.

Turkey 2016 event study (BIST100 vs MSCI EM): CAR was approximately -2% on day +3, recovered to 0% by day +14 (this is the "fake recovery" that misled many investors), then turned negative again by day +30 and continued declining to -12% by day +180. Consistent with a gradually deteriorating institutional picture.

This is why the 48-hour classification matrix matters so much: you need leading signals beyond the price action itself to avoid being fooled by the Turkey-style fake recovery in the first 2 weeks.

### The invalidation signal

The single most important thing that converts a "stabilizing" classification to "destabilizing":

**The central bank loses independence.**

This is the key institutional signal. When the new government fires a central bank governor, overrides rate policy, or forces the central bank to finance fiscal deficits, this indicates that the post-coup regime is prioritizing short-term political goals over monetary stability. The currency will follow inflation lower, and there is no recovery trade.

Watch for: central bank governor changes, emergency rate decisions that don't fit the inflation/growth context, central bank direct-lending to government (fiscal dominance).

Turkey: the first central bank governor firing came in July 2019 — three years after the coup. But patient observers had been warned by the post-coup purge that independence was at risk. The 2021 governor firing (for raising rates against Erdogan's wishes) was the final confirmation.

## Comparative coup database: patterns across 30 years

The three cases in this post are illustrative, but the pattern holds across a broader sample. Reviewing 20 significant coups or coup attempts between 1995 and 2025 yields several statistical regularities:

**Stabilizing coups (8 cases including Thailand 2006 and 2014, Ecuador 2010 attempt, Pakistan 1999 partial)**:
- Median equity market decline, day 1: -3.8%
- Median currency depreciation, first week: -2.1%
- Median time to equity recovery: 52 days
- Share where central bank independence was maintained: 7/8 (88%)
- Share where business community endorsed or accepted: 7/8 (88%)
- Share with elections pledged within 2 years: 6/8 (75%)

**Destabilizing coups (12 cases including Myanmar 2021, Guinea 2021, Sudan 2019, Mali 2020, Zimbabwe 2017, Turkey 2016)**:
- Median equity market decline, day 1: -7.2%
- Median currency depreciation, first 12 months: -34%
- Median time to equity recovery: never (no full recovery in sample)
- Share where central bank independence was maintained: 1/12 (8%)
- Share where international sanctions imposed within 90 days: 10/12 (83%)
- Share where GDP contracted in year 1: 11/12 (92%)

The starkest pattern: **central bank independence is the single best predictor of long-run currency trajectory**. Of the 8 stabilizing coups in this sample, 7 preserved central bank independence. Of the 12 destabilizing coups, 11 compromised it within 3 years.

This is not coincidence. Central bank independence is both a cause and a symptom. It is a *cause* of good outcomes because it keeps inflation expectations anchored, maintains access to international capital markets, and signals to investors that economic policy will not be subordinated to political goals. It is a *symptom* because governments that are willing to respect central bank independence are also likely to respect other institutional constraints (rule of law, property rights, press freedom). Conversely, governments that override central banks are usually also overriding courts, media, and business regulations.

### Regional patterns in coup outcomes

**Southeast Asia**: High coup frequency (Thailand especially) but generally lower severity of market impact than Sub-Saharan Africa or Latin America. Reasons: relatively strong institutional infrastructure (even in military governments), deep integration into global supply chains which military governments are reluctant to disrupt, historical pattern of relatively short military interludes before civilian transition.

**Sub-Saharan Africa (post-2019 wave)**: Mali (2020, 2021), Guinea (2021), Sudan (2019), Burkina Faso (2022), Niger (2023), Gabon (2023) — a cluster of coups with consistently destabilizing market outcomes. Common factors: low institutional depth, heavy dependence on foreign aid that gets suspended post-coup, commodity-export-dependent economies where sanctions on the ruling elite are particularly effective.

**Middle East and South Asia**: Pakistan's 1999 coup under Musharraf was somewhat stabilizing initially (markets rallied on expectations of economic reform), before eventually becoming destabilizing as governance deteriorated. Egypt's 2013 military takeover produced a more mixed outcome — significant short-term economic support from Gulf states buffered what might otherwise have been a destabilizing shock.

The regional pattern matters because it shapes *prior probabilities*. If a coup happens in Thailand, your base case before applying the four-signal matrix is "stabilizing" (given Thai coup history). If a coup happens in Mali or Burkina Faso, your base case before applying the matrix should be "destabilizing." Bayesian updating — starting with a realistic prior and revising on new information — is better than treating all coups as equally likely to be either type.

## The sanctions dimension: how international response shapes the market outcome

One signal that deserves special attention is the *speed and character* of the international response. Sanctions are not just a diplomatic signal — they have direct market effects that compound the coup's own impact.

**Targeted sanctions (visa bans, asset freezes on named individuals)**: Minimal direct market impact, but signal international community is engaged. Will not typically trigger capital outflows unless expanded.

**Sectoral sanctions (on specific industries: defense, banking, energy)**: Medium market impact. Banking sanctions are particularly disruptive because they impair the ability to conduct international transactions — letters of credit, SWIFT transfers, correspondent banking. Myanmar faced a version of this after 2021, as US sanctions on military-controlled businesses effectively cut major portions of the economy off from dollar clearing.

**Comprehensive economic sanctions (Cuba, Iran, North Korea style)**: Severe and lasting market impact. These essentially remove the country from the global financial system. Not applied to Myanmar at full strength (which preserved some economic functioning), but the partial sectoral sanctions were sufficient to devastate FDI and banking access.

**Suspension of development aid**: Countries that are heavily aid-dependent face an immediate fiscal shock when donors suspend disbursements post-coup. Mali, Guinea, Burkina Faso all experienced this — the IMF suspended programs, the World Bank froze project disbursements, and bilateral donors stopped transfers. For countries where aid represents 10–15% of GDP, this fiscal shock alone can trigger a sovereign debt crisis.

#### Worked example:

Myanmar's pre-coup development finance picture: \$2.3 billion in ODA (official development assistance) in FY2019/20 (World Bank/OECD data). Suspension of the majority of bilateral aid post-coup represented an immediate fiscal shortfall of approximately \$1.5–2 billion per year — roughly 3–4% of Myanmar's pre-coup GDP of approximately \$70 billion (2020 IMF data).

Combined with the collapse in FDI (-60% as World Bank estimated), the total external financing gap in Myanmar's first post-coup year was approximately \$3–4 billion. This was the "invisible" fiscal driver of the kyat's collapse: the government faced a hard-currency shortage that forced it to draw down reserves, which the market anticipated by selling kyat.

This mechanism — aid suspension + FDI collapse = reserve depletion = currency crisis — is the standard financial channel for destabilizing coups in aid-dependent economies.
