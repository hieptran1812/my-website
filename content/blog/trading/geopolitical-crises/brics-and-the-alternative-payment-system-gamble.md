> **TL;DR** — The BRICS de-dollarization project is real, slow, and structurally incomplete: the dollar's share of global reserves has fallen from 71% in 2001 to 58% in 2025, but CNY has only risen to 2.2%, and the liquidity gap that keeps the dollar dominant will take at least another decade to close even under an optimistic scenario.
>
> - mBridge (China-HK-UAE-Saudi CBDC corridor) is the most credible near-term threat to dollar settlement, but it handles fractions of global trade volume and faces US secondary-sanction pressure.
> - India-Russia local-currency oil trade (\$10B+ in 2022-23) exposed the fatal flaw: India accumulated rupees Russia cannot spend, creating a de-dollarization trap more costly than just using dollars.
> - A genuine reserve-currency shift would send gold up 30-50%, push US 10-year Treasury yields up 80-150 basis points, and drag DXY down 15-25% — but the probability of that scenario materializing within five years is low.
> - The operative trading signal is not "will it happen" but "is the market pricing it in" — and current gold positioning suggests partial pricing already.

In August 2023, the BRICS summit in Johannesburg admitted six new members — Saudi Arabia, Iran, Ethiopia, Egypt, Argentina, and the UAE — taking the bloc from five countries controlling roughly 23% of global GDP to a claimed coalition of eleven nations accounting for about 37% of world output. The South African rand fell 1.4% on the day after the summit as traders parsed what the expansion actually meant. Gold gained \$18 in 48 hours. Neither move was large. But the directional logic was interesting: something structurally significant had changed, yet markets couldn't decide whether to price it as geopolitical theater or as the early signal of a genuine monetary regime shift.

This is the central tension in the de-dollarization story. The political intent is real. The financial plumbing is not yet there. Knowing the distance between those two points — and what would have to happen to close it — is what lets a practitioner position correctly.

## Foundations: how reserve currencies work and why they're so hard to displace

A reserve currency is a currency that other countries' central banks hold in their vaults as a store of value and as the medium they use to settle international trade. When Vietnam buys crude oil from the Middle East, it typically pays in US dollars even though neither country is America. When South Korea insures a container ship, the insurance contract is denominated in dollars. When Brazilian coffee is traded on the CME, the price clears in dollars.

This is not a natural law. It is a historical equilibrium that was established at Bretton Woods in 1944, reinforced by the petrodollar agreements of the 1970s, and cemented by three decades of US financial-market openness after 1980. But equilibria can shift. The question for investors is: what does a shift look like in motion, and what does it do to prices?

The mechanism has five interlocking parts.

**Part 1: The transactions motive.** Countries hold dollars because that is the currency in which they invoice trade. Roughly 88% of all foreign exchange transactions globally involve one leg in US dollars, according to BIS Triennial Survey data. That statistic has barely moved in fifteen years despite the dollar's declining reserve share. The transactions motive is stickier than the reserve motive because it reflects actual commercial plumbing — invoicing, letters of credit, clearing houses.

**Part 2: The reserve motive.** Central banks hold foreign exchange reserves as a buffer against external shocks — a country that runs out of hard currency cannot pay its import bills or service its foreign debt. The IMF's COFER database shows the composition of reported reserves. The dollar's share peaked at about 71% in 2001, declined to 58% by 2025. That 13-percentage-point drop sounds large. In absolute terms, though, dollar reserves kept growing because total reserves grew; emerging-market central banks were simply diversifying at the margin into euro, renminbi, and gold, not selling dollars.

**Part 3: The safe-asset motive.** The US Treasury market is the largest, most liquid government bond market on earth — about \$27 trillion outstanding as of 2025. When global investors face stress, they buy Treasuries because there is no other asset they can buy in size without moving the price. US 10-year yields fell to 0.51% in March 2020 during COVID peak panic even as the US was running a fiscal deficit in the trillions. That is not a sign of weakness — it is the paradox of the reserve currency: the US can borrow cheaply in a crisis it partially caused.

**Part 4: The petrodollar covenant.** After Nixon ended dollar-gold convertibility in 1971, the US negotiated with Saudi Arabia a deal: Saudi Arabia would price oil in dollars and recycle those dollars back into US Treasuries; the US would provide security guarantees. That recycling mechanism created structural demand for Treasuries from oil exporters. It is one reason the de-dollarization concern intensified when Saudi Arabia opened the door to accepting yuan for Chinese oil purchases in 2022-23.

**Part 5: Network effects and switching costs.** The dollar payment network includes thousands of correspondent banking relationships, SWIFT messaging, clearing houses (CHIPS processes \$1.8 trillion per day), and legal infrastructure. Switching costs are enormous. A country that invoices oil in yuan still needs a liquid yuan FX market, a yuan hedging instrument, and a counterparty willing to hold yuan. Each of those costs is real.

## The political moves — what BRICS-plus is actually attempting

### The Johannesburg expansion and what it signals

The 2023 Johannesburg summit expansion was a deliberate geopolitical signal. Adding Saudi Arabia and UAE to BRICS put two of the world's largest oil exporters inside a bloc that China and Russia explicitly frame as an alternative to the "Western-led" international financial order. The political message was: the countries that set the oil price need not be tied to dollar settlement.

But the expansion also revealed deep internal contradictions. Argentina, freshly admitted, immediately entered a currency crisis and then elected Javier Milei, who proposed dollarizing the Argentine economy — the exact opposite of the BRICS agenda. Ethiopia was in civil war. Iran's inclusion was geopolitically charged (the US views Iranian membership as bloc normalization of sanctions evasion). India, already a BRICS member since the bloc's founding, publicly pushed back against any BRICS currency, with Finance Minister Nirmala Sitharaman saying India had no interest in a common currency.

The expansion was better understood as a political negotiating move than a monetary integration project. China used the expansion to demonstrate to the Global South that there is an alternative coalition to align with. That has value in trade negotiations, commodity pricing discussions, and in pressuring Western countries on issues like IMF quota reform. But negotiating coalitions are not monetary unions.

### Russia's forced experiment in de-dollarization

Russia's de-dollarization was not a choice — it was a consequence of the most aggressive financial sanctions campaign in history. After the February 2022 invasion of Ukraine, the G7 froze approximately \$300 billion in Russian foreign exchange reserves held in Western accounts, cut major Russian banks from SWIFT, and applied a \$60/barrel price cap on Russian oil.

Russia had to find dollar alternatives or collapse. It pivoted aggressively: oil was redirected to India and China, priced in rupees and yuan. Trade with China shifted to yuan settlement. Russia built its own payment messaging system (SPFS) as an internal SWIFT substitute. The ruble, which fell to 116 per dollar in March 2022, recovered to below 80 by May 2022 as Russia mandated exporters sell 80% of their foreign currency earnings.

But Russia's experiment exposed a structural flaw: accumulating local currency from trading partners creates a pile of money you cannot easily deploy. Russian exporters accumulated rupee balances in Indian banks. India accumulated oil without paying dollars. But India's exports to Russia were tiny — Russia had no goods India wanted to buy with those rupees. The rupee pile sat, depreciating in real terms, earning minimal returns, while Russia still needed hard currency for technology imports it could only access through intermediaries. De-dollarization through necessity is not the same as de-dollarization through strength.

### India-Russia oil — the rupee accumulation trap in detail

The India-Russia oil trade grew from near zero to over \$10 billion per year in 2022-2023. India, a large oil importer, happily took discounted Urals crude (at a \$20-30/bbl discount to Brent in peak discount periods). Russia was glad to have a buyer not subject to Western pressure.

The problem: rupee-denominated trade worked in one direction. Russia needed to spend those rupees on something. India's exports to Russia are modest — some pharmaceuticals, machinery, agricultural products. The bilateral trade deficit meant Russia was accumulating rupees it could not recycle. By mid-2023, Russian banks were reportedly holding tens of billions in rupee balances they could not easily convert. India's central bank (RBI) was reluctant to provide a convertibility mechanism that would effectively make the rupee a proxy for dollar-sanctioned transactions.

This is not a failure of will — it is a fundamental liquidity problem. A reserve currency requires holders to be confident they can convert it back, and that requires a deep market of willing buyers. The dollar has that depth. The rupee does not. The yuan partially does — but only because China maintains a managed exchange rate and capital controls, which themselves limit yuan utility as a reserve asset.

## The mBridge CBDC corridor — the most credible near-term threat

The most technically serious component of the de-dollarization architecture is mBridge (Multi-CBDC Bridge), a project launched by the BIS Innovation Hub with Hong Kong, Thailand, UAE, and China's PBOC. Saudi Arabia joined as a full participant in 2024.

![mBridge CBDC settlement corridor showing how central banks bypass correspondent banking](/imgs/blogs/brics-and-the-alternative-payment-system-gamble-3.png)

### How mBridge works

Traditional cross-border payment works like this: a Chinese exporter gets paid by a UAE importer. The money flows from a UAE bank to a correspondent bank in a third country (often a US bank) that holds accounts in both currencies, then on to the Chinese bank. Each hop takes time and costs fees. SWIFT messages coordinate the chain. The whole system depends on access to correspondent banking infrastructure that US sanctions can cut.

mBridge replaces the correspondent bank chain with a shared distributed ledger operated by participating central banks. When China's PBOC and the UAE's central bank are both on the bridge, a Chinese exporter can receive e-CNY instantly while the UAE importer's e-AED is debited in the same atomic transaction. No US correspondent bank. No SWIFT message. Settlement in seconds rather than days.

In the pilot tests conducted in 2022 and 2023, mBridge processed over \$22 million in real-value transactions across 20 commercial banks. The technology works. The question is scale and political sustainability.

### Why mBridge is not yet a SWIFT replacement

mBridge handles cross-border settlement between central banks that choose to join. That's a small universe. SWIFT connects over 11,000 financial institutions in more than 200 countries. mBridge has four founding members and is adding participants cautiously.

More importantly, mBridge creates a direct regulatory challenge for the US. If mBridge becomes a vehicle for sanctions evasion — and its current membership (China, Russia's trade partners, Iran-adjacent states) creates exactly that concern — the US can apply secondary sanctions against participating banks. Any bank that clears through mBridge and also wants access to the US financial system faces an existential choice. European banks, Japanese banks, Korean banks — the vast majority of global financial intermediaries — will not take that risk.

In late 2024, the BIS withdrew from the mBridge governance structure, reportedly due to concerns about the project being used to circumvent sanctions. BIS exit is a significant signal: the multilateral legitimacy shield that the BIS provided is gone. mBridge continues under China's leadership, but it now looks more like a regional payment system for sanctioned economies than a global dollar alternative.

The practical ceiling for mBridge in the next five years is probably somewhere between \$50-100 billion per year in transaction volume — meaningful for bilateral Russia-China or China-Iran trade, but dwarfed by the \$6 trillion per day processed by the global dollar system.

## The financial channels — how the political moves translate to markets

### Channel 1: The reserve diversification channel

When central banks diversify out of dollars at the margin, they sell Treasuries and buy alternative assets — gold, euro-denominated bonds, renminbi bonds (CGBs), and other currencies. The dollar's reserve share declining from 65% to 58% over fifteen years represents about \$1.5-2 trillion in cumulative alternative allocation. That is real demand that did not go to Treasuries.

This creates a mild but persistent headwind for Treasury prices (upward pressure on yields) and a mild tailwind for alternative assets. Gold's secular bull market from \$1,200 in 2018 to over \$2,000 in 2024 is partly attributable to central bank gold buying — particularly by China, India, Turkey, and Poland — which went from about 400 tonnes per year pre-2022 to over 1,000 tonnes per year in 2022-2023.

### Channel 2: The oil pricing channel

Saudi Arabia's willingness to accept yuan for Chinese oil purchases, announced in early 2023, would have been considered impossible in 2015. The petrodollar covenant — oil priced in dollars, proceeds recycled into Treasuries — was considered the structural bedrock of dollar hegemony. If Riyadh begins pricing oil in yuan even for a subset of purchases, it creates marginal demand for yuan and marginal reduction in dollar demand.

The catch: Saudi Arabia still keeps its reserves predominantly in dollars. The Saudi Monetary Authority (SAMA) holds about \$440 billion in reserves, overwhelmingly dollar-denominated. Pricing some oil in yuan does not mean Riyadh wants to hold yuan; it means China demanded it as a condition of its purchasing relationship. Saudi Arabia recycles yuan back to dollar assets through currency conversion, keeping the net dollar demand broadly intact.

### Channel 3: The sanctions evasion feedback loop

Every time the US uses SWIFT/dollar exclusion as a sanctions tool, it incentivizes non-US actors to build alternatives. The freezing of Russia's reserves in 2022 sent a clear signal to every major emerging market: dollar reserves held in Western institutions can be confiscated. China's PBOC reportedly accelerated gold purchases and yuan internationalization after watching Russia's \$300 billion freeze. Even ostensibly US-allied countries like Saudi Arabia began quietly diversifying.

This is the perverse dynamic of weaponizing the reserve currency: every deployment of the dollar as a sanctions tool makes the sanction less painful to the target over time and makes holding dollars less attractive to bystanders.

## Why a SWIFT alternative is 5-10 years away — the liquidity trap

The fundamental barrier to dollar displacement is the liquidity trap, and it deserves careful explanation because it is often misunderstood.

Liquidity, in financial markets, means the ability to buy or sell a large amount of an asset without moving the price. The US Treasury market processes \$700-900 billion in daily trading volume. The yuan-denominated government bond (CGB) market is much smaller and has capital controls limiting foreign participation. The euro bond market is deep but fragmented across seventeen sovereign issuers.

When a Malaysian oil company receives \$100 million in payment for petroleum sold to China, and China wants to pay in yuan, the Malaysian company faces a practical problem: what do they do with \$100 million worth of yuan? They need to:

1. Find a liquid yuan/MYR FX market (thin outside of Hong Kong and Singapore)
2. Hedge the yuan/MYR exchange rate (limited instruments available)
3. Or convert yuan to dollars — which brings them back to the dollar system

The Malaysian company takes on additional FX risk and transaction cost for every yuan-denominated deal compared to a dollar deal. At scale, this makes yuan settlement more expensive than dollar settlement. That cost differential — call it the liquidity premium — is the moat around the dollar.

#### Worked example:

Suppose a Thai palm oil exporter ships \$5 million worth of product to China. China offers to pay in yuan (CNY). The current USD/CNY rate is 7.25. The Thai exporter receives 36.25 million yuan.

The Thai exporter needs Thai baht to pay wages and local suppliers. They need to convert CNY to THB. The USD/THB rate is 35, and the CNY/THB implied rate is 35/7.25 = 4.83 THB per CNY.

But the Thai exporter cannot easily trade CNY/THB directly — the market is thin. They would go through a bank that does CNY/USD and USD/THB. The bank charges a spread of 0.3% on each leg versus 0.15% for a straight USD/THB trade. The double-conversion cost on \$5 million is approximately \$15,000 more than a dollar-settled deal.

Multiply this across all global trade: if 10% of the roughly \$32 trillion in annual global trade shifted to yuan settlement with a cost premium of even 0.1%, the global extra transaction cost would be \$3.2 billion per year. That is real money — and it comes directly out of profit margins for the exporters and importers doing the deals. Until CNY liquidity deepens enough to eliminate the premium, dollar invoicing remains the economically rational choice for most parties.

## How long the transition would take — a data-driven timeline

![How long until the dollar loses reserve dominance — projected timeline](/imgs/blogs/brics-and-the-alternative-payment-system-gamble-5.png)

At the current rate of decline — approximately 0.5 percentage points per year in dollar reserve share — the dollar would fall below 50% reserve share sometime around 2040-2045. That is not the same as losing dominance: a currency can remain the largest reserve currency at 40-45% share (the euro held 25-27% for most of its existence without threatening the dollar). Losing "dominant reserve currency" status would probably require falling below 40%.

The rate of decline could accelerate under two scenarios:

**Scenario A: Confidence crisis.** A major geopolitical event causes a sudden reappraisal of the safety of dollar reserves. The closest historical analogy is what happened to sterling after Suez in 1956: Britain's retreat from military adventure triggered capital outflows from sterling balances that accelerated reserve diversification. A comparable dollar event might be a US debt ceiling breach that triggers a technical default, a US military defeat that calls security guarantees into question, or a direct conflict over Taiwan that triggers unprecedented financial retaliation in both directions.

**Scenario B: CNY breakthrough.** China liberalizes its capital account, makes the yuan fully convertible, and allows the Chinese bond market to open to unrestricted foreign investment. This would increase CNY liquidity dramatically and reduce the cost premium of yuan settlement. The political obstacle is that capital account liberalization would mean China loses control of its exchange rate and monetary policy — an enormous sacrifice the Communist Party has historically refused.

Neither scenario is likely within five years. The base case is continued slow erosion.

## The asset price impact — what a genuine shift does to gold, Treasuries, and DXY

![Asset price impact in gradual versus shock de-dollarization scenarios](/imgs/blogs/brics-and-the-alternative-payment-system-gamble-6.png)

### Gold: the clearest beneficiary

Gold is the only reserve asset that exists outside any national jurisdiction. It cannot be frozen, sanctioned, or confiscated (if physically held). After watching Russia's \$300 billion reserve freeze, central banks globally began buying gold at rates not seen since the 1970s. World Gold Council data shows central banks purchased over 1,000 tonnes per year in 2022 and 2023, more than doubling the 2015-2021 average.

#### Worked example:

Global central bank foreign exchange reserves total approximately \$12 trillion. Gold currently represents about 15% of that, or \$1.8 trillion at \$2,400/oz (that is about 750 million oz, or 23,350 tonnes).

If de-dollarization concern causes central banks to raise gold allocation from 15% to 20% of reserves over ten years — an increase of just 5 percentage points — that represents \$600 billion in additional gold demand. The existing aboveground gold stock is about 205,000 tonnes, worth \$15.8 trillion at \$2,400. The new demand represents 3.8% of the existing stock.

But gold's annual mine production is only about 3,500 tonnes per year. Sustained demand of an additional \$60 billion per year (that \$600 billion spread over ten years) relative to annual mine supply of about \$268 billion creates a meaningful demand-supply imbalance. Working the math: \$60 billion extra annual demand / \$268 billion annual mine supply = a 22% incremental demand shock on mine supply each year. That implies significant upward price pressure — consistent with the +30-50% long-run scenario estimate.

### US Treasuries: upward pressure on yields

If central banks reduce their Treasury holdings as part of reserve diversification, they are net sellers of Treasuries. The US Treasury market needs buyers: foreign central banks historically absorbed 30-40% of Treasury issuance. Declining official demand means the US government pays higher interest rates to attract private buyers.

#### Worked example:

The US ran a budget deficit of approximately \$1.8 trillion in fiscal year 2024. It needs to issue that much in new Treasuries plus refinance maturing debt. Total annual gross issuance is roughly \$25-30 trillion.

If foreign official demand falls from 30% to 25% of that market — a 5-percentage-point shift — the private sector needs to absorb roughly \$1.25-1.5 trillion more in Treasuries annually. At current rates, attracting that extra demand requires a higher yield. A rough rule of thumb from academic literature (Warnock and Warnock, 2009): a \$100 billion reduction in annual foreign demand raises 10-year yields by about 40-60 basis points.

A \$1.25 trillion increase in required private absorption (10x \$125 billion) would thus push yields up by roughly 400-600 basis points in a shock scenario — which is clearly excessive for a gradual transition. In a gradual scenario spread over ten years, the annual increment is \$125 billion, implying a 40-60 bps yield increase per year — far too much to be absorbed without the Fed intervening. The realistic impact in a well-managed gradual transition is probably 80-150 bps on 10-year yields, which is what the matrix above reflects.

### DXY: the headline number

DXY is a trade-weighted index of the dollar against six major currencies — 57.6% euro, 13.6% yen, 11.9% sterling, 9.1% Canadian dollar, 4.2% Swedish krona, 3.6% Swiss franc. Notably, it has no yuan or emerging market currency component.

A genuine de-dollarization event would reduce the structural demand for dollars (fewer transactions, fewer reserves needed), weakening it against most currencies. The historical analogy is the DXY bear market of 2001-2008, during which it fell from about 120 to 72 — a 40% decline driven by current account deficits, declining interest rate differentials with Europe, and marginal reserve diversification. That was not a collapse of the dollar system; it was a correction.

A de-dollarization-driven DXY decline of 15-25% from current levels (around 99-100 as of mid-2025) would imply DXY falling to the 74-85 range. That would be the lowest since the early 2010s. It would be painful for USD-denominated asset holders but manageable for the global economy.

## Common misconceptions about de-dollarization

### Misconception 1: BRICS will create a common currency

The BRICS common currency idea surfaces at almost every summit. It requires an honest assessment: it is not going to happen in any near-term timeframe. The eurozone, with far more economic integration, political alignment, legal harmonization, and institutional infrastructure, took forty years to build and still faces existential crises (Greece 2015, COVID 2020) that a BRICS union would lack the tools to manage.

BRICS members have wildly different inflation rates, interest rate levels, exchange rate regimes, and capital account openness. Brazil runs 10%+ overnight interest rates. Russia has 16%+ rates. China has managed near-zero inflation. A common monetary policy would be incoherent for this group. India explicitly rejected the idea. The "BRICS currency" is a political talking point, not a monetary plan.

### Misconception 2: The dollar's reserve share falling to 58% means it's collapsing

The 13-percentage-point decline from 71% in 2001 to 58% in 2025 looks dramatic. Context: the dollar's reserve share was roughly 55-60% through most of the 1980s. It surged in the 1990s as EM crises (Mexico, Asia, Russia) caused countries to stockpile dollars as a buffer. The current decline is largely a mean-reversion from an anomalously high Cold War-era peak, combined with genuine diversification at the margin.

The euro was introduced in 1999 and peaked at about 27% of reserves in 2009. It has since fallen back to 20%. A currency can be important without being dominant. The dollar can fall to 50% and still be far and away the most important reserve currency.

### Misconception 3: Russia's de-dollarization worked

Russia in 2025 uses far fewer dollars in bilateral trade than in 2021. That is true. But Russian oil revenues fell from \$218 billion in the war-boom year of 2022 to around \$160 billion in 2023 as the price cap bit and buyers demanded discounts. The Urals discount to Brent narrowed from a peak of \$30/bbl to about \$10-12/bbl by 2024 as India and China normalized it into their purchase terms — but the discount is still there, costing Russia roughly \$10-15 billion per year in foregone revenue. And Russia faces acute technology import problems: advanced semiconductor imports fell 70% after export controls, and no yuan-based substitute supply chain has emerged because China itself faces NVIDIA-derived chip shortages and is not willing to re-export controlled chips.

### Misconception 4: China wants to replace the dollar now

China has every incentive to move slowly. A premature yuan internationalization that triggers a capital flight (as Chinese savers move money offshore into dollars) would be economically destabilizing. China's capital controls are not just a political tool — they are a macroprudential valve. Removing them would expose the banking system to runs and potentially require a painful currency adjustment. Beijing will internationalize the yuan at a pace it controls, not at the pace BRICS rhetoric suggests.

### Misconception 5: Holding dollar reserves is safe

After Russia's \$300 billion freeze, this illusion is gone. But the alternative is not obviously safer. Holding yuan means accepting a managed, controlled exchange rate and limited convertibility. Holding gold means accepting storage costs, no yield, and high volatility. Holding euros means accepting eurozone political risk. Every alternative has its own flavor of counterparty or political risk. The dollar's unique advantage is that its risk is systemic rather than political — the US cannot choose to "freeze" foreign dollar holdings in private US banks the way it could freeze Russian central bank reserves held at the Fed or custodied at Belgian clearing houses.

## How it shows up in real markets

### The 2022 Russia freeze and gold buying acceleration

After the G7 froze Russian reserves in February 2022, central bank gold purchases hit 1,136 tonnes in 2022 — the highest since 1967. The countries buying were overwhelmingly the same countries worried about being targeted by US sanctions: Turkey bought 148 tonnes, China's PBOC added to its reserves (though reporting is opaque), India bought 33 tonnes, Qatar bought 15 tonnes.

Gold moved from \$1,898 on February 24, 2022 to \$1,988 over the following week — a 4.7% gain even as equities fell and the ruble collapsed. The gold reaction was not about Russia-Ukraine per se; it was about what the reserve freeze signaled for the future of dollar reserves globally.

#### Worked example:

Central bank gold purchases at 1,000 tonnes/year at \$2,000/oz = \$64 billion/year of demand. Annual mine supply is about 3,500 tonnes = \$224 billion. So central banks alone are absorbing 1,000/3,500 = 28.6% of mine supply. If investment and jewelry demand is roughly constant, this structural buying creates a persistent floor under the gold price.

At \$2,400/oz in mid-2025, if the de-dollarization concern fades and central bank buying returns to 400 tonnes/year (2015-2019 average), the demand shock reversal would reduce demand by 600 tonnes/year = 600 * \$77,000/kg = \$46 billion per year less demand. That is a meaningful negative for gold. So the gold price in 2024-2025 contains a real geopolitical risk premium.

### mBridge pilot and the Saudi pricing pivot

In October 2022, the BIS-led mBridge pilot settled \$22 million in real transactions across 20 commercial banks from China, Thailand, Hong Kong, and UAE. The transactions cleared in seconds. The technical proof-of-concept was successful.

In March 2023, the Wall Street Journal reported that Saudi Arabia was in active discussions with China about pricing some oil sales in yuan. Brent crude moved \$2/bbl on the report before recovering as analysts noted the limited scale. DXY fell 0.4% on the day, then recovered.

The market's measured reaction to both events reflects the correct calibration: the technology works and the political intent is real, but the scale is too small to matter yet. The trade is to watch for when the numbers get large enough to matter.

## The playbook — how to position for de-dollarization scenarios

### The signals to watch

**Signal 1: IMF COFER reserve data.** Published quarterly with a one-quarter lag. Watch for CNY share crossing 3% (currently 2.2%) or dollar share falling below 55%. Either would represent an acceleration beyond the current trend.

**Signal 2: Central bank gold purchase data.** World Gold Council publishes quarterly. If 1,000+ tonnes/year is sustained for multiple years, the structural bid under gold is real. A fall below 600 tonnes/year would signal the post-Russia-freeze panic is fading.

**Signal 3: mBridge transaction volume.** Any pilot reports showing transaction volumes above \$100 billion/year would signal that the plumbing is scaling. Watch BIS quarterly reports and People's Bank of China announcements.

**Signal 4: Saudi Arabia's reserve composition.** SAMA's annual report shows reserve allocation. A meaningful shift from US Treasuries toward yuan bonds, gold, or other assets would be the most significant data point. Saudi Arabia recycling petrodollars is the heart of the covenant.

**Signal 5: US-China financial decoupling escalation.** Each US export control expansion (chips, software, biotech) increases China's incentive to build a parallel financial infrastructure. Track the scope of each new Executive Order on technology controls.

### The position structure

**Base case (no near-term acceleration):**
- Overweight gold relative to benchmark — the structural central bank buying bid is real and persistent. Entry on pullbacks to the 200-day moving average.
- Mild underweight US Treasuries — the headwind from reserve diversification is real but slow, insufficient to create a crisis on its own. Underweight duration, not credit.
- Neutral DXY — the dollar is not collapsing; erosion is gradual. Tactical positions against specific EM currencies where de-dollarization creates real flow (INR, BRL on bilateral trade corridors).

**Scenario A position (confidence crisis — 10-15% probability):**
- Large overweight gold, gold miners, and Swiss franc (the safe haven outside both dollar and yuan systems).
- Short long-duration Treasuries (TLT or equivalent) — yield spike is the core mechanism.
- Long EM local-currency bonds in countries with strong fiscal positions (India, Brazil, Indonesia) — they benefit from dollar weakness.
- Short DXY via options — the downside scenario is a 15-25% move.

**Scenario B position (CNY breakthrough — 5-10% probability):**
- Long yuan — a capital account opening would cause immediate appreciation as inflows surge.
- Long Chinese government bonds — foreign demand floods in.
- Short dollar-invoiced commodity proxies — if oil is priced in yuan, the dollar oil-demand channel weakens.

**The invalidation conditions:**
- US fiscal consolidation that reduces Treasury issuance and improves current account — takes pressure off the dollar.
- Chinese political instability that deters reserve accumulation in yuan.
- mBridge secondary sanctions — US designates mBridge participants, killing the corridor.
- India-China border conflict — breaks the BRICS coalition's two largest members.

#### Worked example:

A portfolio manager has \$100 million in a multi-asset fund. The current allocation is 60% global equities, 35% bonds (mostly US Treasuries), 5% alternatives.

In the base case de-dollarization scenario, the manager wants to:
1. Reduce US Treasury duration: sell \$5M in 10-year Treasuries, buy \$5M in 2-year (reducing duration by ~8 * \$5M = \$40M of DV01 equivalent)
2. Add gold: allocate \$5M from cash/short-term to gold ETF (GLD)
3. Add EM local bonds: \$3M from US equities into a diversified EM local currency bond fund

Cost of hedges: GLD has 0.4% annual fee, EM bond fund 0.5%. Net cost ~\$35,000/year for the de-dollarization hedge overlay.

Expected payoff in base case (gradual slow erosion): essentially flat — gold slowly grinds up, Treasuries slowly underperform, EM local outperforms. The overlay costs roughly \$35k/year and generates maybe \$100-200k/year in incremental return if the gradual scenario plays out.

Expected payoff in Scenario A (confidence crisis): gold +40% = +\$2M on \$5M position; Treasury short avoids -15% loss on \$5M = +\$750k; EM bonds +25% = +\$750k. Total crisis upside: +\$3.5M on a \$35k/year hedge. The asymmetry is compelling.

## The Timetable Problem: Why Speed Matters More Than Intent

The most important and underappreciated aspect of de-dollarization is not the geopolitical intent behind it — that is well-documented and broadly understood — but rather the time constraint. Every alternative payment system faces a compounding infrastructure deficit that makes the stated timelines unrealistic.

SWIFT currently processes approximately 45 million messages per day across 11,500 member institutions in 200+ countries. CIPS, China's domestic interbank payment system, processes roughly 1,500 transactions per day. mBridge, as of 2025, had completed fewer than 100 test transactions. The gap between the existing infrastructure and what a credible SWIFT alternative would require is not one order of magnitude — it is three to four orders of magnitude.

The optimistic BRICS scenario is that mBridge scales from test transactions to significant commercial volume by 2030. This would require every participating central bank to: build API integration with the mBridge protocol; onboard domestic commercial banks; establish legal frameworks for cross-border finality and dispute resolution; create FX liquidity pools deep enough to handle large transaction flows; and coordinate real-time settlement across time zones. The fastest comparable infrastructure buildout in financial history — the Euro real-time gross settlement system (TARGET) — took eight years from design to full operation with unlimited ECB institutional support. mBridge has none of those advantages.

For the practitioner, this timetable analysis has a concrete implication: the risk scenario to hedge is not a sudden 2027 dollar collapse. It is a slow-motion 15-year erosion of dollar share from 59% of reserves to perhaps 45–50%, with the pace of erosion determined by geopolitical events (especially US sanctions usage) rather than by BRICS summits. Size accordingly.
