On June 1, 2017, President Donald Trump stood in the White House Rose Garden and announced that the United States would withdraw from the Paris Agreement. Within hours, shares of the iShares Global Clean Energy ETF (ICLN) dropped more than 2%, coal stocks popped, and the headlines screamed that the energy transition had been dealt a fatal blow. By January 20, 2021, newly inaugurated President Joe Biden signed an executive order rejoining the accord before the day was out. ICLN was up more than 120% from its 2017 lows. The energy sector was repriced again.

What actually happened in between — and why the market's first instinct was repeatedly wrong — is one of the cleanest case studies in geopolitical risk trading available today. The Paris Agreement episode shows exactly how to distinguish a real policy shock from a theatrical one, how to size the trade, and when the macro override (technology cost curves) swamps the diplomatic signal.

This post maps every channel from the diplomatic event to asset prices, walks through four worked examples with explicit dollar math, and ends with a trading playbook you can apply the next time a US administration changes its position on climate.

## Foundations: What the Paris Agreement Actually Is (and Is Not)

Before we can trade the policy shock, we need to understand what the Paris Agreement actually does — because most of the confused market reaction in June 2017 came from traders who overestimated its legal teeth.

### The agreement is a framework of promises, not a law

The Paris Agreement was adopted on December 12, 2015, at the COP21 climate conference in Paris. It entered into force on November 4, 2016, after ratification by countries representing more than 55% of global greenhouse gas emissions. As of 2024, 195 parties had ratified it, making it one of the most broadly signed international environmental agreements in history (UNFCCC, 2024).

Here is the key thing to understand: **the Paris Agreement does not set legally binding emissions targets**. What it requires is that each country submit a "Nationally Determined Contribution" (NDC) — a self-set pledge describing how it plans to reduce emissions — and that it report progress transparently. The NDC itself is not enforceable under international law. Countries are legally required to *have* an NDC and to *update* it over time, but there is no penalty for missing the target in that NDC.

Think of it like a public commitment device. If you announce to your entire neighborhood that you will run a marathon in under 4 hours, there is social pressure to follow through. Nobody can take your house if you don't. The Paris Agreement works on exactly that logic — but scaled to sovereign governments and backed by capital markets that use the signals to allocate trillions of dollars.

### What an NDC is

An NDC (Nationally Determined Contribution) is each country's climate action plan filed with the UNFCCC (United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change). NDCs include:

- A headline emissions reduction target (e.g., "reduce emissions 50% below 2005 levels by 2030")
- The sectors covered (energy, industry, transport, agriculture)
- Whether the target is conditional on international finance
- Adaptation plans for managing climate risks

The US NDC under Obama: reduce greenhouse gas emissions 26–28% below 2005 levels by 2025.
The US NDC under Biden (refiled 2021): reduce emissions 50–52% below 2005 levels by 2030.
The EU NDC (Fit for 55): reduce net emissions at least 55% below 1990 levels by 2030.
China's NDC: peak CO2 emissions before 2030; achieve carbon neutrality by 2060.

None of these are domestic law by themselves. Each requires separate legislation or regulation to implement — the EU's Emissions Trading System, the US Clean Power Plan (or its successors), China's national carbon market. The Paris Agreement is the diplomatic umbrella; the domestic policy instruments are where the real market action happens.

### The emissions trading system (EU ETS): how carbon gets a price

The European Union Emissions Trading System (EU ETS) is the world's largest carbon market by value. It caps the total amount of greenhouse gases that certain sectors can emit, then issues a corresponding number of emission allowances. Companies that need to emit more buy allowances from those that emit less. The price of one allowance (one tonne of CO2) becomes the "carbon price."

When the carbon price is high, it is more expensive to burn fossil fuels, so utilities and industrial companies switch to cleaner alternatives or invest in efficiency. When the carbon price is low (as it was in the 2013–2017 period — as low as \$5/tonne at points), the incentive to decarbonize is weak.

The EU ETS operates independently of the Paris Agreement. The US can be in or out of Paris; the EU ETS still runs. But US diplomatic signals affect *sentiment* in the carbon market because they affect global expectations about future supply and demand for allowances.

### Clean energy ETFs: the asset-price translation layer

For most retail and institutional investors, the easiest way to express a view on climate policy is through exchange-traded funds:

- **ICLN** (iShares Global Clean Energy ETF): holds solar, wind, hydroelectric, and related companies globally
- **QCLN** (First Trust NASDAQ Clean Edge Green Energy ETF): US-focused, heavier in EV supply chain
- **XLE** (Energy Select Sector SPDR Fund): US integrated oil & gas majors (ExxonMobil, Chevron, ConocoPhillips)
- **XOP** (SPDR S&P Oil & Gas Exploration & Production ETF): US E&P companies, more volatile than XLE

These four ETFs are the most liquid proxies for the clean-vs-fossil policy trade. They can be long/short in combination to express a relative-value view on climate policy direction.

## The Political Moves

Understanding the market reaction requires a precise timeline. The dates matter because the market often discounts weeks or months before the formal event.

### The Obama years: building the architecture (2015–2016)

The Obama administration was the primary architect of the Paris Agreement framework. The United States under Obama pledged the 26–28% reduction target and helped construct the "ratchet mechanism" — the requirement that countries update their NDCs every five years with increased ambition.

The Clean Power Plan (CPP), issued by the EPA in August 2015, was the domestic instrument designed to deliver the Obama NDC. It set state-by-state emissions standards for power plants. Immediately upon publication, 27 states filed lawsuits, and in February 2016 the Supreme Court issued a stay blocking implementation pending litigation. This is crucial: **the CPP was never actually in effect**, which means the US was already making its Paris pledge based on a domestic regulation that had been legally blocked.

Markets priced the CPP as a future probability, not a certainty. The uncertainty around implementation was already embedded in fossil fuel valuations.

### Trump announces withdrawal: June 1, 2017

Trump's withdrawal announcement came after several months of internal White House debate, with the business community (including ExxonMobil and many coal companies) actually arguing against withdrawal on the grounds that remaining "at the table" was more useful than exiting.

The announcement said the US would seek "to negotiate a new deal that's fair to the United States." Under Paris Agreement rules, a country cannot formally submit notice of withdrawal until three years after the agreement entered into force for that country. That date for the US was November 4, 2019. The formal withdrawal notice was submitted on November 4, 2019, and took effect one year later on November 4, 2020 — the day after the 2020 presidential election.

So the timeline looked like this:

- June 1, 2017: Trump *announces intent* to withdraw (no legal effect yet)
- November 4, 2019: US formally submits withdrawal notice to the UN
- November 4, 2020: US withdrawal legally takes effect

For markets, the June 2017 announcement was the informational shock. The formal exit in November 2020 was already fully priced.

### Biden rejoins: January 20, 2021

On his first day in office, President Biden signed an executive order directing the US to rejoin the Paris Agreement. Because the US had submitted the relevant rejoining instrument (acceptance of the agreement) to the UN, the US was formally a party again on February 19, 2021 — 30 days after the executive order.

No congressional approval was needed. The Paris Agreement was treated as an executive agreement (not a treaty requiring two-thirds Senate ratification), a legal classification established under the Obama administration. This meant a single executive order could both exit and re-enter — and explains why market participants always priced in a high probability of Biden-era re-entry from the moment Biden became the likely Democratic nominee in mid-2020.

### The Inflation Reduction Act: August 16, 2022

The IRA was the legislative capstone of US climate re-engagement. Signed by President Biden on August 16, 2022, it allocated roughly \$369 billion (Congressional Budget Office estimate) to climate and clean energy over a decade, making it the largest climate investment in US history. The IRA used tax credits (the Investment Tax Credit for solar, the Production Tax Credit for wind, new credits for green hydrogen and battery storage) rather than regulations, which made it more durable against future reversal.

Markets reacted immediately: ICLN rallied approximately 22% in the month following the IRA signing. Offshore wind developers, battery manufacturers, and EV supply chain companies saw sharp re-ratings.

## The Financial Channels

The Paris Agreement and its associated political moves affect asset prices through five distinct channels. Understanding which channel dominates in each regime is the key to sizing the trade correctly.

### Channel 1: Carbon price signal

When the US is "in," global carbon market participants expect eventual US carbon pricing — either a federal carbon tax or a national cap-and-trade system. This raises the implied "cost of carbon" embedded in US industrial asset valuations. Conversely, when the US exits, the probability of near-term US carbon pricing falls, which removes the carbon cost premium from US fossil fuel stocks.

But — and this is critical — the EU ETS carbon price does not mechanically respond to US diplomatic moves. The EU ETS is governed by EU caps and MSR (Market Stability Reserve) rules. US withdrawal from Paris may *marginally* affect EU carbon market sentiment, but the primary driver is EU domestic supply/demand of allowances.

This is why traders who shorted EU carbon credits on the June 2017 withdrawal announcement lost money. EU ETS had its own reform cycle underway (the MSR reform that took effect in January 2019), and EU policy was moving in the opposite direction — tightening caps.

### Channel 2: Capital allocation / investment flows

Asset managers and institutional investors increasingly apply ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance) screens to their portfolios. When the US is in Paris, the signal to global capital is that the world's largest economy is committed to the transition — making it safer to make long-duration bets on clean energy infrastructure.

US withdrawal in 2017 created what climate economists called a "policy uncertainty shock." A paper by Baker, Bloom, and Davis (2016) showed that policy uncertainty suppresses investment in capital-intensive industries. The uncertainty did suppress some utility-scale renewable procurement decisions in 2017–2018, particularly those dependent on the (now-uncertain) regulatory pathway.

But the counterpoint is that state-level action filled much of the gap. California, New York, and 27 other states that maintained Renewable Portfolio Standards (RPS) — requirements that utilities source a specified percentage of electricity from renewables — continued to drive procurement regardless of federal policy. This is why US renewable capacity additions actually increased during the non-participation years.

### Channel 3: Stranded asset risk

Fossil fuel assets have defined lifespans. A coal plant built in 2020 expects to operate until 2050+. A gas field developed today might produce for 30 years. Under an aggressive Paris-compatible decarbonization scenario, many of those assets would be shut down before end of their economic life — becoming "stranded assets."

When the US re-enters Paris and signals stronger climate ambition, the implied probability of a US carbon price in the 2030s increases. This raises stranded asset risk for long-lived fossil fuel investments, which should theoretically discount coal, oil sands, and other high-carbon assets.

In practice, this channel operates slowly — equity markets discount terminal value effects over 20-30 year horizons, and near-term cash flows dominate current valuation. The stranded asset channel is most relevant for infrastructure bonds and project finance (longer duration, more exposed to terminal-period regulatory risk) than for traded equities.

### Channel 4: Technology cost curves (the market override)

This is the most important channel that policy traders systematically underweight. Solar photovoltaic (PV) costs fell approximately 90% from 2010 to 2020. Wind (onshore) costs fell approximately 70% over the same period. These cost declines were driven by Chinese manufacturing scale, learning-curve effects, and supply chain optimization — forces entirely independent of US federal climate policy.

By 2017, utility-scale solar was cheaper than new coal in most US markets on a levelized cost basis. By 2020, solar was cheaper than *existing* coal in a growing number of markets. By 2022, new wind and solar capacity additions were economically compelling without any subsidy in many geographies.

This means the energy transition was happening regardless of the diplomatic dance. US exit from Paris delayed some corporate ESG commitments and created short-term policy uncertainty, but it could not stop the fundamental economics from playing out. This is why the trade that "shorts clean energy because the US exited Paris" had a short shelf life: you were fighting an economic force, not just a policy trend.

### Channel 5: FX and dollar liquidity

Clean energy investment is global, but much of the equipment (solar panels, wind turbines, battery storage) is priced in USD. When the US re-enters climate accords and signals green infrastructure spending, it tends to be associated with broader USD fiscal expansion (the IRA, for example, was funded in part through deficit spending). A slightly weaker dollar makes USD-priced equipment cheaper for non-US buyers, supporting demand for US-manufactured clean energy equipment.

Conversely, a US policy pivot toward fossil fuels is often associated with energy export expansion (LNG, crude oil), which supports USD via the petrodollar mechanism. These are second-order effects, but worth tracking in longer-duration trades.

## EU ETS Carbon Price: The Independent Signal

The EU ETS deserves its own section because it is the most liquid, directly tradeable climate-linked asset class, and its behavior since 2017 is a masterclass in how domestic policy reform can dominate international diplomatic signals.

![EU ETS carbon credit price 2017-2023 showing the 12-fold rise independent of US participation](/imgs/blogs/paris-agreement-us-in-us-out-us-in-climate-and-energy-markets-4.png)

In early 2017, EU ETS carbon allowances (called EUAs, or European Union Allowances) were trading at roughly \$5–8 per tonne of CO2. The market had been depressed for years because the 2008-09 financial crisis and subsequent slow European recovery had suppressed industrial production and thus demand for allowances, while the supply cap was set based on pre-crisis projections. The result was a massive surplus of allowances.

The Market Stability Reserve (MSR) reform was approved in 2018 and took effect in January 2019. The MSR automatically reduces the supply of allowances when the cumulative surplus exceeds a threshold. This structural reform was the proximate cause of the ETS price recovery — not US re-entry into Paris.

From January 2019 to December 2021, ETS prices rose from roughly \$18–25/tonne to over \$80/tonne. The energy crisis that hit Europe in late 2021 (driven by tight gas supplies, low wind output, and the post-COVID demand recovery) caused a further spike: gas-to-coal switching by utilities created extra demand for carbon allowances, pushing prices to an all-time high near \$98/tonne in February 2023 (source: ICE EUA futures, February 2023).

Prices then corrected as the energy crisis eased and some industrial production softened, falling back to roughly \$60–70/tonne by late 2023.

The trading implication: **EU ETS price is driven by EU domestic supply/demand, not by US Paris participation**. Traders who bought EUAs in 2019 based on EU structural reform made 4–5x returns. Traders who shorted EUAs in 2017 because "the US left Paris" lost money. The relevant research to do before expressing a carbon credit view is EU industrial production forecasts, MSR surplus calculations, and EU energy mix data — not US diplomatic news.

## The Investment Flow Inflection

Alongside carbon prices, the most important financial variable to track is global clean energy investment relative to fossil fuel investment. This is because the investment flow determines which assets are being built for the next 30 years — it is the structural bet.

![Global clean energy investment vs fossil fuel investment 2015-2022](/imgs/blogs/paris-agreement-us-in-us-out-us-in-climate-and-energy-markets-6.png)

The IEA's World Energy Investment reports provide the authoritative annual numbers. Key data points (all IEA sources):

- 2015: Clean energy investment ~\$500 billion; fossil fuel supply investment ~\$900 billion
- 2017: Clean ~\$560 billion; fossil ~\$820 billion (US exit announcement had no immediate effect on global flows)
- 2019: Clean ~\$630 billion; fossil ~\$790 billion (gap narrowing)
- 2021: Clean ~\$755 billion; fossil ~\$790 billion (near parity)
- 2022: Clean ~\$1.1 trillion; fossil ~\$0.9 trillion (clean overtook fossil for the first time)

The 2022 crossover is structurally significant. Once clean energy investment is larger than fossil fuel investment on a sustained basis, the capital stock being built locks in a trajectory — new clean capacity displaces fossil capacity not through regulation but through economic competition for the next unit of generation.

The IRA played a role in the 2022 jump, but so did the energy crisis (which made European governments accelerate offshore wind and solar), rising fossil fuel costs (which made clean alternatives more competitive), and continued technology cost declines.

#### Worked example: Sizing the IRA catalyst trade

You are trading on August 1, 2022 — two weeks before the IRA is signed. The IRA has passed the Senate on August 7 (in final form) and you are confident Biden will sign it. ICLN is at \$22.50. XLE is at \$78.00.

You want to express a long clean energy / short fossil view with \$100,000 of capital.

**Step 1: Compute implied leverage through the ratio spread**

You buy \$60,000 of ICLN at \$22.50 = 2,667 shares.
You short \$40,000 of XLE at \$78.00 = 513 shares.

Net market exposure: long \$60,000 clean, short \$40,000 fossil = net long \$20,000 in the energy complex.

**Step 2: Estimate the policy-driven return**

Historical analysis of the Biden re-entry (Jan 2021) showed ICLN +18% in the 30 days after announcement. The IRA is a larger and more durable policy catalyst because it is legislation (harder to reverse). You estimate ICLN +20% in 60 days; XLE roughly flat to -5% (higher carbon costs, potential marginal oil demand destruction).

**Step 3: Compute P&L at target**

ICLN leg: 2,667 × \$22.50 × 20% = \$12,003 gain
XLE leg: 513 × \$78.00 × 5% = \$1,999 gain (short benefits from a -5% move)
Total P&L: ~\$14,002 on \$100,000 deployed = 14% return over 60 days.

**Step 4: Define the stop**

If the IRA is significantly weakened in conference or faces legal challenge, ICLN might give back 10% before any rally. Stop-loss: if ICLN falls below \$20.25 (10% below entry) and XLE does not move correspondingly, close the position. Risk = \$60,000 × 10% = \$6,000 on the long leg.

The risk/reward ratio is approximately 14:6 = 2.3:1, which clears the hurdle for a catalyst-driven trade.

The actual result: ICLN rose approximately 22% in the month following the IRA signing (August 16 to September 16, 2022). XLE fell approximately 8% over the same period as oil prices weakened. The realized P&L on the spread would have been roughly 20-22% on the long leg plus 3-4% on the short, for a combined ~24-26% return.

## Before and After: How the Energy Sector Split

The withdrawal years (2017–2021) created an unusual energy market dynamic: US federal policy pointing one way, markets and state policy pointing another. This two-speed condition is important for traders because it created sector-level dispersion.

![US energy sector federal vs state split during non-participation 2017-2021](/imgs/blogs/paris-agreement-us-in-us-out-us-in-climate-and-energy-markets-5.png)

At the federal level under Trump:
- The Clean Power Plan was replaced by the Affordable Clean Energy Rule (a much weaker regulation)
- The coal lease moratorium on federal lands was lifted
- Offshore oil and gas leasing was expanded
- EPA reversed numerous methane emission regulations

At the state and market level:
- 29 states maintained or strengthened Renewable Portfolio Standards
- Corporate power purchase agreements (PPAs) from tech companies (Google, Amazon, Microsoft) accelerated — these are long-term contracts to buy renewable energy directly from generators, bypassing federal policy entirely
- The Investment Tax Credit (ITC) for solar and Production Tax Credit (PTC) for wind survived — these were bipartisan tax provisions that Republicans in Congress did not repeal, largely because they benefited manufacturing states

The net result: US renewable energy capacity grew faster during the non-participation years than before them. Total US renewable electricity capacity additions from 2017 to 2020 were larger than the four years prior. The federal policy shift created headwinds for *new* regulatory mandates but could not stop the economic math of cheap solar and wind.

For traders, this meant the "short clean energy on Trump withdrawal" trade had a clear expiry: it worked for the first 3-6 months while uncertainty was highest, but then the technology cost curve re-established the trend and the trade became a loser.

#### Worked example: The short-lived withdrawal trade

June 1, 2017. Trump announces withdrawal. ICLN closes at \$10.20 on that day. You short \$50,000 worth of ICLN at \$10.20 = 4,902 shares.

**The winning path** (short-term):
By September 1, 2017 — three months later — ICLN has fallen to \$8.90, down about 12.7%.
Gain: 4,902 × (\$10.20 - \$8.90) = \$6,373. A 12.7% return in 3 months.

**Where it goes wrong** (if you hold):
By January 20, 2021 — Biden's Day 1 — ICLN is at approximately \$22.80.
If you had held your short from June 2017 to January 2021 (3.5 years), the mark-to-market loss would be:
4,902 × (\$22.80 - \$10.20) = \$61,765 loss on a \$50,000 notional position — more than 100% of your capital.

The lesson: policy shocks create short-term dislocations. Long-running technology cost curves determine the multi-year direction. The trade that worked was capturing the 3-6 month dislocation and then reversing it. Holding the "policy short" against the long-run trend is how you get blown up.

## Sector-Level Anatomy of the Policy Trade

To trade the Paris Agreement cycle with conviction, you need to go deeper than ETF-level analysis. The sector-specific transmission differs materially between subsectors of clean energy and between types of fossil fuel companies.

### Solar power: the most resilient subsector

Solar photovoltaic is the subsector most resistant to US federal policy shifts because of three structural factors.

First, the economics are purely local. A solar farm in Texas or Arizona is installed at a specific cost, sells power at a specific contracted price (often a long-term PPA), and earns tax credits determined at the time of installation. If the ITC is 30% today (as it is under the IRA), a developer can lock in that credit for projects placed in service over the next decade. The Paris Agreement status does not change this math.

Second, state-level mandates dwarf federal ones. California's mandate for 100% clean electricity by 2045, New York's offshore wind build-out, and Texas's competitive wholesale market (which rewards the lowest-cost generators — now dominated by wind and solar) all operate independently of federal climate policy. These state drivers collectively represent more renewable procurement than federal mandates ever required.

Third, Chinese manufacturing scale is a structural deflationary force. China produces over 80% of the world's solar panels (modules). The cost of a utility-scale solar module fell from over \$2.00 per watt in 2010 to under \$0.25 per watt by 2022. This price decline is driven by supply competition, not US policy. US tariffs on Chinese panels (Section 201 and Section 301 tariffs) created cost headwinds for US solar, but the underlying global cost curve continued down regardless.

The result: US solar capacity additions grew from approximately 14.6 GW in 2016 to 29 GW in 2020 to 33 GW in 2023, with every single one of those years occurring under different federal policy configurations.

### Wind power: more sensitive to regulation, but still durable

Onshore wind faces more regulatory dependencies than solar because wind projects require federal permits for projects on federal land, and offshore wind in the US requires federal Bureau of Ocean Energy Management (BOEM) approval. The Trump administration's energy policies created delays for some offshore wind projects, but onshore wind on private land and in state jurisdiction continued to expand.

Offshore wind was a different story. The large-scale US offshore wind build-out was a Biden-era initiative, with BOEM fast-tracking lease sales and approvals from 2021 onward. Companies like Ørsted, Equinor, and Dominion Energy's offshore wind subsidiary saw their US project pipelines expand significantly under Biden. The IRA added 10-year production tax credits for offshore wind, creating a financing backdrop for utility-scale development.

The investment implication: offshore wind companies are *more* policy-sensitive than onshore solar companies. A Ørsted or Vineyard Wind project depends on BOEM approvals, state contract awards, and federal tax credits simultaneously. These stocks react more violently to US policy shifts than does First Solar (which can sell globally and benefits from US domestic content bonuses but is not exclusively dependent on US policy).

### Natural gas: the pivot point

Natural gas sits at a unique intersection in the energy transition trade. From a climate perspective, gas is a fossil fuel (methane, the primary component, is a potent greenhouse gas). From an energy market perspective, gas is the "transition fuel" that has replaced coal in power generation, cutting US power sector CO2 emissions significantly since 2005.

When the US re-enters Paris, the signal is not simply "bad for all fossil fuels." Aggressive climate policy accelerates coal retirement (unambiguously bearish for coal) and creates new demand for gas as a backup to intermittent renewables (at least until battery storage scales). Gas therefore has a more complex relationship with climate policy — it can be bullish for gas producers in the 5-10 year horizon even under strong climate policies, while being bearish in the 15-25 year horizon as renewables plus storage approach full displacement.

This means the simple "long clean, short fossil" trade is an approximation. A more precise trade would be: long solar + wind operators, short coal, neutral to long gas (on a 1-5 year view), short gas (on a 10+ year view, as the storage buildout accelerates).

### Coal: the clearest directional signal

Coal occupies the opposite end of the spectrum from solar. It is the most carbon-intensive electricity source (approximately twice the CO2 per MWh of natural gas), faces the strongest regulatory pressure under any credible climate policy, and has been steadily uneconomic against gas and renewables for over a decade.

When the US announced withdrawal from Paris in 2017, coal stocks spiked: the Market Vectors Coal ETF (KOL) rose approximately 8% in the week following the announcement, and ARCH Resources (then Arch Coal) spiked even more. The thesis: if federal climate regulations are rolled back, coal plants get extended lifetimes, coal demand increases.

The thesis was wrong for one simple reason: coal plants were not retiring because of federal regulation. They were retiring because they were losing money competing against cheap natural gas. The "Trump coal rescue" never materialized in production terms because the economics didn't support it. By 2020, coal's share of US electricity generation had fallen to approximately 19% from 45% in 2010, and this happened almost entirely for market reasons.

This is the sharpest worked example of the "policy versus economics" distinction. The policy signal was bullish coal; the economic reality was relentlessly bearish. Traders who bought coal on the withdrawal announcement and held for more than 6-9 months gave back all gains and more.

## The Inflation Reduction Act in Detail: Why It Changed the Analysis

The IRA deserves more depth than a passing mention, because it represents a qualitative shift in how US climate policy is structured — and that structure has direct implications for durability of the investment thesis.

### Tax credits vs. regulations: the political economy distinction

Prior US climate policy relied primarily on EPA regulations: the Clean Power Plan, methane rules, fuel economy standards. Regulations can be issued and reversed by executive order. They are also subject to court challenge — and the Clean Power Plan was blocked by the Supreme Court's West Virginia v. EPA ruling (June 2022, which held that the EPA needs explicit congressional authorization to make major rules with large economic consequences).

The IRA used tax credits, which are statutory — they are embedded in the US tax code by an act of Congress. Reversing them requires another act of Congress, with a 60-vote threshold in the Senate (or the elimination of the filibuster). Because solar and wind manufacturing has now been built in Republican congressional districts, there is bipartisan political resistance to repealing IRA credits even from legislators who otherwise oppose climate policy.

This makes the IRA-era US climate policy substantially more durable than the Obama-era regulatory approach. Markets have correctly priced this: the re-rating of clean energy stocks in August 2022 was larger and stickier than the re-rating from Biden's re-entry into Paris in January 2021, because Paris re-entry was a fragile executive action while IRA credits are a statutory entitlement.

### The domestic content premium

The IRA includes bonus tax credits (an additional 10% on top of the base 30% ITC) for projects using domestically manufactured components — solar panels made in the US, wind turbines with US-manufactured blades and towers, battery cells made in US factories. This provision was designed to rebuild US clean energy manufacturing capacity and reduce dependence on Chinese imports.

For equity traders, this creates a bifurcation within clean energy: companies with US manufacturing capacity (First Solar for solar modules, Nextracker for trackers, several domestic battery manufacturers) benefit disproportionately from the IRA vs. companies that source from China and cannot claim the bonus credit.

This manufacturing bifurcation is an alpha source independent of the macro Paris Agreement cycle. Even in a policy-tightening regime, First Solar (with its US thin-film manufacturing) should outperform a Chinese-module-dependent installer.

### Duration risk in green bonds

The IRA also accelerated the US green bond market. When a utility issues a "green bond" to fund a solar farm or EV charging network, it is making a long-duration financing commitment (typically 10-30 year bonds). The credit risk on that bond includes policy risk: if the relevant tax credits are eventually repealed or the regulatory environment reverses, the project economics may deteriorate.

Prior to the IRA, US green bonds carried a meaningful "policy durability" risk premium — higher yield spreads than comparable EU green bonds, reflecting the regulatory reversibility risk. Post-IRA, that spread narrowed as the market reassigned the revenue base from "EPA regulation" (reversible by executive order) to "ITC tax credit" (reversible only by Congress). This spread compression was itself a trade: long US green bonds vs. short comparable conventional utility bonds in 2022-2023 captured a 20-40 basis point spread narrowing.

## The NDC Ratchet and Long-Duration Asset Pricing

One of the most underappreciated financial implications of the Paris Agreement is the NDC ratchet mechanism: every five years, countries are required to submit new, more ambitious NDCs. This creates a predictable schedule of policy escalation signals, which should show up in long-duration asset prices.

The NDC update cycle runs as follows:
- 2020: First NDC update due (US filed new NDC under Biden in April 2021)
- 2025: Next NDC update due (under Paris rules, all parties must submit by February 2025)
- 2030: Next major milestone year (most current NDCs target 2030 reductions)

For project finance and infrastructure equity investors, the 2025 NDC update is the next catalyst on the horizon. A more ambitious US NDC (committing to, say, 60-65% below 2005 by 2030) would imply additional domestic regulatory action and increased long-term carbon cost expectations, re-rating stranded asset risk for fossil fuel projects with post-2030 cash flows.

For sovereign bond investors, countries with credible NDC trajectories backed by domestic legislation (Germany's carbon pricing schedule, UK's net-zero legislation, California's energy mandates) have lower long-term fiscal risk from energy transition costs than those without such frameworks. ESG sovereign bond screeners increasingly weight NDC ambition, creating a spread differential between "climate credible" and "climate laggard" sovereigns.

## Common Misconceptions

### "The US exit killed the energy transition"

This is the most common misconception, and the data flatly contradicts it. US clean energy capacity additions were larger in 2018, 2019, and 2020 than in 2016. Global renewable energy investment continued to grow every year through the non-participation period. Solar costs fell another 40% from 2017 to 2020. The energy transition slowed in some respects (corporate ESG commitments were less ambitious, some regulatory proposals were shelved) but it did not reverse.

The confusion comes from conflating federal regulatory intent with market outcomes. A US President can signal climate ambition or disinterest, but cannot reverse the global supply chain economics that have made solar the cheapest electricity source in history.

### "US re-entry is a buy signal for all clean energy"

Also false. US re-entry into Paris is a buy signal for the near-term sentiment trade. But the durable catalyst requires domestic legislation — specifically, tax credits or direct spending — because that is what changes the after-tax economics of project development. The executive order rejoining Paris on January 20, 2021 was bullish for ICLN, but the real re-rating came 18 months later with the IRA.

Traders who chased the Biden re-entry without waiting for the legislative anchor had a frustrating 2021 as ICLN gave back much of its initial rally before the IRA renewed the trend.

### "Carbon credits go up when the US is in Paris"

The EU ETS — the most liquid carbon market — is driven by EU domestic supply/demand, not US diplomatic positioning. US re-entry into Paris does not add a single tonne of demand for EU allowances. What matters for ETS prices are: EU GDP and industrial production, the gas-coal spread in European power markets, the MSR surplus calculation, and EU regulatory calendar (Fit for 55 proposals, airline emissions rule changes).

US-linked carbon trading would require either a federal US carbon price (which has been proposed but never enacted) or offsets-based markets (like California's cap-and-trade and its linkage with Quebec), which are more obscure and less liquid than ETS.

### "Fossil fuel companies are the simple losers from Paris re-entry"

Partially true, but nuanced. Large integrated oil majors (ExxonMobil, Chevron, Shell, BP) have significant renewable energy divisions and carbon capture investments. For these companies, aggressive climate policy is not purely negative — it creates a competitive moat for companies with existing low-carbon assets and punishes pure-play fossil operators more harshly. The "long XLE on US withdrawal" trade actually overstates the impact because XLE's largest holdings are diversified majors, not pure coal or oil sands operators. A cleaner bear climate trade is long coal companies (ARCH, CONSOL) short, or long offshore oil sands operators short.

### "State-level policy cannot compensate for federal retreat"

Wrong. The 29 states with RPS mandates in 2017 represented approximately 60% of US electricity demand. California's policy alone drove enough procurement to make California the world's fifth-largest solar market. New York's offshore wind program (the Climate Leadership and Community Protection Act, signed 2019) committed to 70% renewable electricity by 2030 — a legally binding state mandate. These state actions are not impacted by federal climate diplomacy.

## How It Shows Up in Real Markets

### The January 2021 re-entry surge

When Biden's election became clear in November 2020, the market began pricing in re-entry. From November 3, 2020 (election day) to January 20, 2021 (inauguration), ICLN rose approximately 75%. This is a pre-announcement rally — the market did not wait for the executive order.

From January 20 to the end of January, ICLN added another 5-8% as the order was formally executed and the market confirmed there were no surprises in the language. Then ICLN pulled back roughly 15-20% through March 2021 as traders took profits and the "buy the rumor, sell the news" dynamic played out.

The cleaner trade was to buy the sector in September–October 2020, when Biden's polling lead was widening but clean energy equities had not yet fully priced re-entry. At that point, ICLN was trading near \$17-18, approximately where it was before the COVID crash. The reward was 75%+ over 10 weeks before any executive order was signed.

#### Worked example: The pre-announcement re-entry trade

October 1, 2020. Biden leads Trump by 8-10 points in national polls. Prediction markets show approximately 65% probability of Biden victory. ICLN trades at \$17.40.

You estimate:
- If Biden wins: ICLN re-rates to reflect re-entry + likely climate legislation = \$25-30 target (based on DCF re-rating of clean energy projects at lower regulatory risk premium)
- If Trump wins: ICLN falls back toward \$12-14 (re-tests pre-COVID levels as Trump second term locks in no-Paris path)

Expected value:
65% × (\$27.50 - \$17.40) + 35% × (\$13.00 - \$17.40)
= 65% × \$10.10 + 35% × (-\$4.40)
= \$6.565 + (-\$1.54)
= \$5.025 expected gain per share

At \$17.40 per share, that is a 28.9% expected return. You buy \$50,000 notional at \$17.40 = 2,874 shares.

**Actual outcome**: ICLN reaches approximately \$22.80 by January 20, 2021, and peaks near \$34 in late January before pulling back.
- At \$22.80 by Jan 20: gain = 2,874 × (\$22.80 - \$17.40) = \$15,520, or 31.0% in 3.5 months.
- At the January peak of \$34: gain = 2,874 × (\$34 - \$17.40) = \$47,724, or 95.4%.

Most systematic traders would have taken profits somewhere in the \$25-\$28 range (50-60% gain) once the event risk (actual inauguration) was past. The high-water mark chasers who held through the ARK/clean energy bubble of late January got a painful 40% drawdown by March.

### The IRA signing: August 2022 inflection

Between Biden's re-entry in January 2021 and the IRA signing in August 2022, clean energy equities were volatile and ultimately flat to down. Interest rates were rising (bad for long-duration assets like solar projects), supply chain disruptions raised equipment costs, and the legislative path of the Build Back Better climate provisions was uncertain for most of 2021-22.

The IRA broke the impasse. By switching from regulatory mandates (which courts can block) to tax credits (which are much harder to unwind), it provided durable economic certainty to project developers. The \$369 billion envelope, the 10-year duration of the key credits, and the domestic content bonuses for US-manufactured equipment all served to re-anchor the investment thesis.

The market's reaction was immediate: ICLN rose from approximately \$20 to approximately \$25 in the 30 days following the signing — a 25% move. The broader clean energy build-out accelerated: 2023 set a new US record for solar installations at approximately 33 GW.

#### Worked example: IRA event-driven sizing with options

The IRA had unusual advance notice as a catalyst: the bill text was released to the public on July 27, 2022 (a week before Senate passage on August 7). This created an 8-day window where the fundamental catalyst was visible but the market had not fully priced it.

You buy ICLN September 30, 2022 call options struck at \$22.50 (at-the-money) on August 1, 2022, when ICLN is at \$22.30. The calls cost approximately \$1.80 per share (implied volatility around 35%).

You buy 30 contracts = 3,000 options on 3,000 shares, paying \$1.80 × 3,000 = \$5,400 total premium.

**Scenario A (IRA signed, ICLN rallies to \$26)**:
Option payoff: (\$26 - \$22.50) × 3,000 = \$10,500 intrinsic value
Minus premium paid: \$10,500 - \$5,400 = \$5,100 profit = 94% return on premium.

**Scenario B (IRA delayed, ICLN stays at \$22.30)**:
Options expire near worthless (slight time value). Loss = \$5,400 (the full premium).

**Scenario C (IRA fails, ICLN falls to \$18)**:
Options expire worthless. Loss = \$5,400. ICLN equity holders would have lost (\$22.30 - \$18) × shares — the options have defined maximum loss (the premium), which makes them more capital-efficient here.

The IRA was signed August 16. ICLN reached \$27 by August 26. Intrinsic payoff at \$27: (\$27 - \$22.50) × 3,000 = \$13,500. Net profit: \$13,500 - \$5,400 = \$8,100 = 150% return on premium in 25 days.

The options approach was superior here because it capped the downside to the premium while preserving full upside participation in the catalyst move.

## How to Trade It: The Playbook

The climate policy cycle driven by US Paris Agreement participation creates two distinct market regimes, each with characteristic trade expressions.

![Climate policy shift trading playbook matrix](/imgs/blogs/paris-agreement-us-in-us-out-us-in-climate-and-energy-markets-7.png)

### Regime 1: Policy tightening (US re-entry, climate legislation)

**Indicators**: new US administration with stated climate agenda, Paris Agreement re-entry, legislative progress on climate bill, rising carbon prices in California/Quebec cap-and-trade, new EPA regulations proposed

**Short-term trades (0–6 months)**:
- Long ICLN, QCLN, or equivalent clean energy ETF
- Short XLE or XOP (the pair trade)
- Long EU ETS futures (via EUA-listed contracts on ICE) if EU domestic reform is also positive
- Long ENPH, FSLR (US solar manufacturers with domestic content advantage under IRA)

**Long-term trades (1–3 years)**:
- Long infrastructure bonds of renewable energy project developers (higher yield, longer duration, benefits from locked-in tax credit certainty)
- Long green hydrogen companies (most speculative, depends on long-term carbon price signal)
- Long battery storage pure-plays (FLUX, etc.)

**Risk factors**: rising interest rates (long-duration renewable project finance is rate-sensitive), supply chain inflation in solar panels or steel for wind, regulatory delays on permitting

### Regime 2: Policy loosening (US withdrawal, regulatory rollback)

**Indicators**: executive orders reversing climate rules, EPA rolling back regulations, withdrawal from international accords, budget proposals eliminating clean energy tax credits

**Short-term trades (0–6 months)**:
- Short ICLN or QCLN
- Long XLE/XOP
- Short California carbon futures (CCAs on the ICE) if state policy is also uncertain
- Long coal operators (ARCH Resources, CONSOL Energy) if coal phase-out timeline extends

**The crucial caveat**: These trades have a 3-6 month shelf life. After that, the technology cost curve re-establishes the trend. A sustained "short clean energy" position across 2017–2021 destroyed capital even though the federal policy was unambiguously negative for the sector. Run the policy trade for the uncertainty premium, then exit.

**Fade indicator**: When LCOE (Levelized Cost of Energy) data for new solar/wind continues to show cost declines, the fundamental wind is at your back for clean energy regardless of policy. Bloomberg NEF's annual LCOE survey (published every year) is the key data point.

### Sizing guidance

Policy event trades are event-driven, so they follow event-risk sizing rules:

- Maximum position: 3-5% of portfolio per trade
- Time horizon: 30-90 days for the catalyst trade; extend only if legislative catalyst arrives
- Stop-loss: 10-15% on the clean energy ETF leg, or a specific policy reversal trigger (e.g., if the IRA is declared unconstitutional by a circuit court)
- Pairs, not outright: running ICLN long / XLE short cuts the correlation to broader market moves and isolates the sector-specific policy premium

### The carbon credit overlay

For traders with access to futures markets, the EU ETS (EUA futures on ICE) offers a more direct expression of carbon price views than equity ETFs. Key data to monitor:

1. **Surplus/deficit in allowances**: the MSR calculation, published quarterly by the EU. When cumulative surplus exceeds 833 million allowances, the MSR absorbs supply, tightening the market.
2. **Gas-coal spread in European power**: when gas is expensive, utilities switch to coal, buying more allowances. This amplifies ETS price moves.
3. **EU industrial production index**: a leading indicator of allowance demand.
4. **Legislative calendar**: upcoming EU climate legislation (Fit for 55 measures, revised ETS scope to maritime/aviation) creates demand expansions that take time to price in.

US re-entry into Paris has historically caused a 3-7% rally in EUA futures in the weeks following the announcement — not because of fundamental change in EU supply/demand, but because of sentiment around global climate coordination. This is a tradeable, but relatively small, signal-to-noise move.

### The technology cost floor trade

This is the highest-conviction long-run trade: solar and wind costs have declined on a learning curve for 40 years, and every decade of continued deployment is projected to drive further 20-30% cost reductions. This trade does not depend on any specific policy — it is a pure economics argument.

The expression: long a diversified clean energy technology basket (First Solar for solar, Vestas/Siemens Gamesa for wind, ENPH for rooftop solar/storage) held over 3-5 year periods, rebalanced annually. This portfolio captured 200-400% returns from 2017 to 2022 even through the non-participation years, outperforming both ICLN (which suffered from its concentration in speculative names) and individual sector bets.

## Further Reading & Cross-Links

The Paris Agreement episode connects directly to several broader themes in geopolitical crisis trading:

The mechanics of how diplomatic events transmit to energy commodities follow the same framework as the [Russia-Ukraine war's impact on natural gas markets](/blog/trading/geopolitical-crises/russia-ukraine-war-energy-and-commodity-markets) — a foreign policy event changing global supply expectations. The key difference is that the Paris Agreement moves capital allocation (long-run) while war moves physical supply (immediate).

The EU ETS carbon price dynamics connect to [sovereign debt and fiscal policy contagion](/blog/trading/geopolitical-crises/eurozone-sovereign-debt-crisis-market-mechanics) — in both cases, a structural EU policy mechanism creates a market outcome that is largely independent of US positioning. Understanding that EU carbon policy is EU-owned, not US-influenced, is essential before putting on any carbon credit trade.

The IRA tax credit structure, and why it is more durable than regulation, mirrors the analysis in [central bank independence and credibility](/blog/trading/geopolitical-crises/central-bank-independence-and-market-credibility) — the long-duration credibility of a policy instrument depends on its institutional backing (statute vs. executive order vs. regulation), and markets price that duration premium or discount accordingly.

For the technology cost curve argument specifically, the decline in solar and wind LCOE over the 2010-2022 period tracks almost exactly with the manufacturing learning curves documented in the semiconductor and battery industries — a "Wright's Law" dynamic where every doubling of cumulative production produces a consistent percentage cost reduction.
