> **TL;DR** — Currency wars are cycles of competitive devaluation where each country's move forces rivals to respond, destroying value for everyone while delivering only temporary trade gains.
>
> - Three distinct mechanisms: unilateral surprise (China 2015 -1.9% overnight), direct FX intervention (Japan 2022 ~\$60 billion to defend 145), and rate-driven depreciation (Turkey 2021-23 lost 80% of its value)
> - Detection signals: falling reserves, real effective exchange rate divergence from peers, rate cuts while peers hike, and widening spot-to-NDF spreads

> - The trade: long USD versus the devaluing currency, long gold, short EM equities — each captures a different channel of currency war risk

On August 11, 2015, traders in Asia opened their screens to a number that made no sense. The People's Bank of China had set its daily yuan reference rate 1.9% weaker than the previous session — the largest single-day devaluation in two decades. By 10 a.m. Hong Kong time, the ripple was global: the South African rand, the Malaysian ringgit, the Chilean peso, the Indonesian rupiah were all down. The Bloomberg EM FX index lost nearly 1% before New York even opened. The S&P 500 dropped 1.5% that day. Nobody was sure whether China was panic-selling its way into a hard landing, quietly abandoning its exchange rate peg, or deliberately weaponizing its currency to steal export market share from South Korea, Japan, and Germany.

That ambiguity is the defining feature of currency wars. They do not come with declarations, declarations of war, or clear start dates. They arrive in the data — in a central bank balance sheet, a reserve drawdown, a rate cut that contradicts the central bank's stated mandate — and they demand that every market participant form a view before the full picture is clear. This post gives you the framework to form that view faster.

## Foundations: How Exchange Rates Work as Economic Weapons

Before we can understand currency wars, we need to understand why exchange rates matter to trade and politics at all.

An exchange rate is simply the price at which one currency trades for another. When the US dollar buys 100 Japanese yen, an American importing a \$10 product from Japan pays roughly \$0.10 per unit. When the yen weakens to 150 per dollar, that same product suddenly costs the American only about \$0.067 — a 33% reduction in cost. The Japanese exporter still receives 10 yen, but the American price has fallen. Japan's products become more competitive in the American market without the Japanese factory doing anything differently.

This basic arithmetic is the entire logic of competitive devaluation. If your currency falls relative to your trading partners' currencies, your exports become cheaper for foreign buyers. Your GDP, which is partly driven by net exports (exports minus imports), receives a boost. Employment in export-sector industries rises. Politicians and central bank governors who are accountable for growth and employment find this very appealing.

The catch is that the mechanism works only if your trading partners don't respond. If every country devalues simultaneously, prices all adjust together, no one gains market share, and what you're left with is higher inflation (because imported goods now cost more in every currency), lower trust in central banks, and capital flight from the most vulnerable economies to the largest reserve currency — historically the US dollar.

This is the "race to the bottom" in the title. The term was popularized by economists in the 1930s describing how the Smoot-Hawley tariff and subsequent rounds of competitive currency devaluation turned a recession into the Great Depression. Each nation, acting rationally in its own short-term interest, produced an outcome that was irrational for the group. Modern economists call this a prisoner's dilemma — a game-theory situation where individual rationality produces collective irrationality.

### The Three Mechanisms of Devaluation

Currency wars are not a single phenomenon. They operate through three distinct mechanisms, each with different speed, durability, and market signals:

**Unilateral surprise devaluation** is the most dramatic. A government or central bank makes a deliberate policy decision to reset the exchange rate in a single move, often communicating it through the morning reference rate or a formal announcement. China's 2015 move is the textbook example. The PBOC shifted its methodology for the daily "fix" — the reference rate around which the yuan is allowed to trade — producing a 1.9% gap overnight. The move was sudden, concentrated in time, and difficult to hedge against in advance. The signal that appears in market data is a large discontinuity in the spot rate, often accompanied by a spike in implied volatility in FX options markets as traders price in the possibility of further moves.

**Direct FX intervention** is more gradual but potentially enormous in scale. In this mechanism, the central bank buys or sells its own currency in the open market to push the rate in the desired direction. To defend a weak currency (resisting further devaluation), the central bank sells dollars and buys its own currency — drawing down its foreign exchange reserves. To accelerate depreciation, it does the reverse: buys dollars, accumulating reserves. Japan's 2022 intervention is the canonical recent example of defensive intervention. The Bank of Japan spent an estimated \$60 billion in the second half of 2022 buying yen in the open market to defend the 145-per-dollar level that officials had publicly signaled as their pain threshold. The reserves drawdown is observable in monthly data published by the Ministry of Finance — a detective's trail of footprints left in official statistics.

**Rate-driven depreciation** is the slowest and deepest mechanism. Rather than acting directly in the FX market, a government applies pressure on the central bank to cut interest rates while peers are hiking. Lower rates make the currency less attractive to foreign investors (who can earn more in dollars or euros), so capital flows out and the currency weakens organically. Turkey's 2021-2023 experience is the extreme version of this story. President Erdogan, operating on the heterodox view that high interest rates cause inflation rather than cure it, pressured the central bank to cut rates to 8.5% when inflation was running at 85%. The result was that the lira went from roughly 8 per dollar in early 2021 to 26 per dollar by June 2023 — an 80% loss of value over 30 months.

### Beggar Thy Neighbor: The Political Logic

The phrase "beggar thy neighbor" was coined in the 18th century to describe policies that benefit one country at the direct expense of another. Competitive devaluation is the quintessential beggar-thy-neighbor policy. The nation that devalues first exports its unemployment problem to its trading partners. Their factories become relatively more expensive, their export workers face reduced orders, their governments face political pressure to respond.

The logic runs deeper than trade competition. For highly indebted nations, devaluation also reduces the real burden of foreign-currency debt — if you owe dollars but earn domestic currency, a weaker domestic currency makes you poorer. Conversely, foreign-currency debt becomes more crushing when your currency collapses, which is why EM currency crises often become debt crises simultaneously. Turkey's corporate sector, which had borrowed heavily in euros and dollars during the years of cheap global money, found itself in exactly this trap during the 2021-23 lira collapse.

### The Gold Standard Era and the 1930s Template

The most complete historical template for a currency war is the period from 1931 to 1936, when country after country abandoned the gold standard. The gold standard fixed each currency to a specific quantity of gold, which meant exchange rates were effectively locked. When the Great Depression hit and deflationary pressures mounted, the gold standard became a straitjacket — countries could not run expansionary monetary policy without risking a gold drain. Britain left the gold standard in September 1931, which freed the pound to depreciate. Other countries followed in waves. Each departure gave a temporary competitive advantage to the departing nation, creating an incentive for the next nation in line to do the same.

By 1936, almost every major economy had devalued relative to gold. The Tripartite Agreement of 1936 — between the US, UK, and France — was an attempt to stabilize the situation, but it was too late and too informal to prevent the broader economic damage. This experience is why, when the Bretton Woods system was designed in 1944, John Maynard Keynes and Harry Dexter White explicitly built in mechanisms to prevent competitive devaluation: the International Monetary Fund was given oversight authority, and countries were required to maintain fixed exchange rate parities except in cases of "fundamental disequilibrium."

## The Political Moves: Who Does What and Why

The three cases in this post — China 2015, Japan 2022, and Turkey 2021-23 — illustrate how different the political calculus looks behind each devaluation episode.

### China 2015: The Export Machine Sputters

By mid-2015, China's economy was under stress. GDP growth, which had averaged 10% per year through the 2000s, was slowing toward 7%. The stock market had crashed from its June peak, wiping out trillions in paper wealth. Export growth had turned negative. The PBOC was simultaneously managing a currency peg at roughly 6.20 yuan per dollar, maintaining a band that required constant intervention to sustain.

The political decision in August 2015 was framed as a reform — moving to a more market-oriented reference rate mechanism. But the direction of the move (weaker yuan) was not accidental. China's export sector, employing tens of millions of workers, needed relief. The PBOC chose to let the fix drop 1.9% in one day, then another 1.6% the next, for a cumulative two-day move of about 3.5%.

The domestic calculus: with the stock market already in freefall, the Communist Party needed a policy lever to show economic management competence. A modest currency adjustment was plausibly deniable as a reform measure rather than a panic move. The external calculus: the US and European economies were growing; a slightly weaker yuan would tilt export competitiveness toward Chinese factories at a moment when demand from China's own consumers was weak.

The consequence they did not fully anticipate was the signal it sent to global capital. If the PBOC would allow 1.9% in a day, what was the floor? Within weeks, capital outflows from China accelerated dramatically — eventually reaching an estimated \$1 trillion over the following 12 months. China burned through hundreds of billions of dollars in reserves trying to prevent a disorderly rout in the opposite direction: the very capital flight that a sudden surprise devaluation can trigger. The PBOC eventually had to tighten capital controls sharply in 2016 to stop the bleeding.

### Japan 2022: The Reluctant Defender

Japan's situation in 2022 was structurally different. Japan was not trying to gain competitive advantage through a weaker yen — the yen was weakening organically because the Bank of Japan was the last major central bank maintaining near-zero interest rates while the Federal Reserve was hiking aggressively. With US rates rising from 0% to 4.5% and Japanese rates stuck at zero, the interest rate differential made it rational for capital to flow out of yen and into dollars. The yen went from around 115 per dollar in January 2022 to 151 by late October — a 31% depreciation in less than a year.

This created a specific political problem for the Japanese government. Japan is a large net energy importer. When the yen weakens, oil and gas prices — which are denominated in dollars — become more expensive in yen terms. Japan's import cost surge was feeding inflation into an economy where the public and policymakers had spent 30 years fighting deflation. The political pressure was not to use the weak yen for export gains — it was to stop the yen from weakening further and driving up household energy bills.

The Ministry of Finance's response — spending approximately \$60 billion in direct yen-buying intervention — was essentially a holding action while waiting for the Fed to eventually peak in its hiking cycle and for the BOJ's yield curve control policy to become untenable. It succeeded in buying time. The yen stabilized briefly after each intervention, but the structural interest rate differential remained until the BOJ began its own rate normalization in 2024.

### Turkey 2021-23: The Ideological Experiment

Turkey's lira collapse is in a different category altogether. It was not driven by export strategy or a defense against external pressure. It was driven by domestic politics and ideological conviction that overrode standard monetary policy frameworks.

Erdogan's theory — that high interest rates cause inflation rather than reduce it — is at odds with every major school of macroeconomic thought, including both Keynesian and monetarist frameworks. When central bank governors disagreed and raised rates in early 2021, they were fired. Between July and December 2021 alone, the CBRT cut rates five times — from 19% to 14% — while inflation was accelerating from 19% toward 80%. The lira fell from 8.5 per dollar to 18 per dollar in that six-month period, a 52% collapse.

The political logic was electoral: Erdogan faced elections in 2023. Lower rates, even if they destroyed the lira, were intended to boost credit growth and short-term economic activity. Cheap mortgages and consumer loans would show up in GDP data before the election; the inflation consequences would arrive after. The strategy worked electorally — Erdogan won re-election in May 2023 — but the economic cost was severe. Millions of Turkish households watched their savings evaporate in real terms. The corporate sector, leveraged in hard currency, faced insolvency crises. By June 2023, the government pivoted to orthodox policy under new Finance Minister Mehmet Simsek and CBRT Governor Hafize Erkan, raising rates sharply to restore credibility.

## The Financial Channels: How Devaluation Moves Asset Prices

A government policy decision to devalue creates multiple simultaneous waves through financial markets. Understanding which wave hits which asset class — and in what sequence — is what separates a prepared trader from a surprised one.

### The Export Channel and Trade Data

The most obvious channel is the direct trade effect. A weaker domestic currency makes exports cheaper and imports more expensive. This shows up in trade data, typically with a lag of 3-6 months (the "J-curve" effect — the trade balance often deteriorates briefly before improving, because existing import contracts are still invoiced at old prices while export volumes take time to rebuild). For market participants, the signal to watch is not the current trade balance but forward-looking indicators: export orders in PMI surveys, shipping data, and factory utilization rates in the devaluing country.

### The Capital Flow Channel and Reserve Data

The capital flow channel is faster and more directly visible to markets. When a currency falls — or is suspected of being about to fall — foreign investors who hold assets in that country face a dual loss: the asset may fall in value AND the currency in which it is denominated falls. The rational response is to exit. This capital flight amplifies the original currency move and can become self-fulfilling: the more the currency falls, the more foreign investors exit, which pushes the currency lower still.

For countries defending their currencies through intervention, the capital flow channel shows up most directly in reserve data. The IMF publishes monthly data on foreign exchange reserves for most countries through its COFER database. A sustained, month-over-month decline in reserves — particularly when paired with statements from officials about exchange rate stability — is a key detection signal that a currency defense is underway and may be failing.

### The Inflation Channel and Monetary Policy Response

A weaker currency raises the price of all imported goods. For a country that imports significant quantities of food, energy, or intermediate manufacturing inputs, this imported inflation pressure can be severe. Central banks in these countries face a dilemma: raise rates to defend the currency and fight inflation, but risk crushing growth; or keep rates low to support the economy, but allow inflation and currency weakness to persist.

Turkey's central bank faced exactly this dilemma between 2021 and 2023. Under political pressure to keep rates low, it allowed inflation to reach 85% and the lira to lose 80% of its value before the policy U-turn. The eventual rate hikes — from 8.5% in May 2023 to 40% by January 2024 — showed just how severe the monetary tightening required to restore credibility after a devaluation episode can be. Turkish 10-year yields peaked above 40% in early 2024, from below 10% in early 2023.

### The Contagion Channel and EM Spillovers

Currency crises are contagious. When one emerging market currency falls sharply, investors reassess their positions across the entire EM universe. The mechanism is partly rational — a devaluation in Country A may signal that Country A's trading partners will lose export market share, so their currencies also deserve scrutiny. But a significant portion of EM contagion is technical: portfolio managers who have exposure to multiple EM markets and need to reduce risk overall will sell across the board, not just in the directly affected country.

China's 2015 devaluation triggered this contagion effect dramatically. The Indonesian rupiah fell 2.5% in the week following China's announcement. The Malaysian ringgit, which had been under pressure from falling commodity prices, dropped another 3%. The South African rand, the Brazilian real, and the Korean won all came under selling pressure. These are countries with very different fundamentals and trade profiles, but they were all swept into the same risk-off wave.

The MSCI EM FX index — which tracks a basket of emerging market currencies — tends to move -1.9% on average in the 7 days following a major geopolitical event, based on historical data across the top geopolitical events since 2001. During dedicated currency war episodes, that move is typically larger and more sustained.

## How to Detect a Currency War in the Data

The challenge for practitioners is distinguishing deliberate competitive devaluation from organic currency weakness driven by macro fundamentals. A currency can fall for many legitimate reasons: a deteriorating current account deficit, a commodity price collapse that reduces export revenues, a global risk-off episode driven by factors external to the country. The question is whether there is a policy fingerprint — evidence that the government or central bank is intentionally facilitating or accelerating the weakness.

![Five detection signals that distinguish competitive devaluation from organic FX weakness](/imgs/blogs/competitive-devaluation-when-countries-race-to-the-bottom-6.png)

### Signal 1: Reserve Movements

Foreign exchange reserves are the most direct forensic evidence of central bank activity in currency markets. When a central bank is selling dollars to support its currency, reserves fall. When it is buying dollars to accumulate reserves (which weakens its own currency), reserves rise. Both types of intervention leave a monthly data trail in IMF COFER and national central bank publications.

The key pattern to watch is a sustained, directional change in reserves that does not correspond to current account flows. A country running a large trade surplus should be accumulating reserves organically. If reserves are falling despite a trade surplus, it suggests the central bank is actively defending the currency — and that pressure exists to weaken it. Conversely, a country running a persistent trade deficit but showing rising reserves is almost certainly intervening to prevent currency strengthening.

### Signal 2: Real Effective Exchange Rate Divergence

The nominal exchange rate (say, yuan per dollar) is influenced by many factors including global inflation trends. The real effective exchange rate (REER) adjusts the nominal rate for inflation differentials and trade-weighted baskets — it measures whether the currency is genuinely cheap or expensive in terms of purchasing power. The IMF and BIS both publish REER data.

A country engaged in competitive devaluation typically shows a REER that is trending lower over time relative to its trading partners' REERs. The key comparison is not the level — a currency can be undervalued for fundamental reasons — but the rate of change. A REER that is falling faster than peers, over multiple quarters, while the central bank is cutting rates, is a meaningful signal.

### Signal 3: The Rate Differential

One of the most powerful and tradable signals of currency war conditions is the interest rate differential. In a world where central banks in the US, Europe, and major EM economies are hiking rates to control inflation, a country that is cutting rates — or maintaining rates far below the global neutral rate — is implicitly tolerating currency weakness. The carry trade (borrowing in low-rate currencies and investing in high-rate currencies) amplifies this: capital flows toward higher yields, further strengthening those currencies and weakening the low-rate currency.

Turkey's rate cuts in 2021, China's rate reductions in 2022 while the Fed hiked, and Japan's yield curve control in 2022-23 all created significant rate differentials that drove currency weakness. A practitioner monitoring these differentials had advance warning of the currency moves that followed.

### Signal 4: Official Communication

Governments that want their currencies to weaken rarely say so explicitly, because overt currency manipulation invites trading partner retaliation and potential IMF censure. But they often give signals through what they don't say. A finance minister who previously praised currency stability and suddenly stops commenting on the exchange rate, a central bank governor who emphasizes growth risks rather than inflation risks in public communications, officials who cite "excessive volatility" rather than "excessive weakness" when asked about currency moves — these linguistic shifts are meaningful.

The reverse also applies: when Japan's officials explicitly named 145 yen per dollar as a level requiring a "response," they were signaling a floor for intervention, which gave the market a clear reference point around which to price options and size positions.

### Signal 5: Spot-to-NDF Spread

For currencies with offshore non-deliverable forward (NDF) markets — common for EM currencies with capital controls — the spread between the onshore spot rate and the offshore NDF rate is a sensitive measure of market pressure. A wide spread, with the NDF pricing the currency weaker than the official onshore rate, indicates that offshore markets see more depreciation pressure than the official rate reflects. This is often an early warning signal that intervention is needed to maintain the onshore peg and that reserves are likely to be drawn down.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: A weaker currency always helps a country's economy.**

This is true under narrow conditions that often don't hold. For the export boost to materialize, the country needs to have a supply capacity to actually increase exports — idle workers and factories that can rapidly scale up production. For many EM countries in 2015, this supply slack was limited; the dominant effect of currency weakness was higher import costs for food and energy that hit the poorest households hardest. Vietnam, which does have significant export manufacturing capacity, genuinely benefited from a more competitive dong. Countries like Brazil, which is a commodity exporter priced in dollars, saw limited export benefit because their commodity revenues are dollar-denominated regardless of the exchange rate.

**Misconception 2: FX intervention always fails.**

The textbook view is that a central bank with finite reserves cannot defeat a currency market with infinite capital. This is true in the extreme — if a currency is fundamentally overvalued, no amount of intervention will sustain it indefinitely. But intervention can be highly effective at buying time, smoothing volatility, and establishing credibility around specific levels. Japan's 2022 intervention stabilized the yen for weeks at a time. Switzerland's SNB maintained a EUR/CHF floor of 1.20 for three years (January 2012 to January 2015) before abandoning it. The key variable is whether the fundamental pressure is temporary (in which case intervention can succeed) or structural (in which case it eventually fails and you've spent your reserves in the process).

**Misconception 3: The G20 "no competitive devaluation" pledge prevents currency wars.**

This is the most consequential misconception in currency policy. G20 communiques since 2013 have consistently included language to the effect that member countries will "refrain from competitive devaluations" and "not target exchange rates for competitive purposes." The problem is structural: there is no enforcement mechanism, no tribunal, no sanctions regime for violation, and no agreed-upon definition of what constitutes "competitive" devaluation versus legitimate monetary policy. China in 2015 maintained that its devaluation was a reform of the reference rate methodology; Turkey maintained that its rate cuts reflected an alternative economic theory. Both claims could be challenged, but neither the IMF nor any G20 body had the authority to compel a policy change. The pledge is a statement of intent among sovereigns, not a legally binding commitment.

**Misconception 4: Currency wars are a modern phenomenon.**

The language of "currency war" was popularized by Brazilian Finance Minister Guido Mantega in 2010, but the phenomenon is at least as old as the gold standard. The competitive devaluations of the 1930s, the sterling crises of the 1960s and 1970s, the Asian financial crisis of 1997-98 (which featured sharp devaluations by Thailand, Indonesia, South Korea, and others in rapid succession), and the episodes following the 2008 financial crisis (when quantitative easing by the Fed and ECB caused capital flows into EM that some countries tried to offset through intervention) all fit the broad pattern.

**Misconception 5: The dollar always strengthens during a currency war.**

The dollar tends to strengthen when EM currencies weaken, because dollars represent the safe haven destination for capital fleeing devaluing currencies. This is the historical pattern: during the 2015 China devaluation episode, the DXY rose as EM currencies fell. But the causality can invert in unusual circumstances. During the 2025 "Liberation Day" tariff episode, the dollar weakened despite US trade policy aggressiveness, because markets were pricing in the economic damage to the US itself from high tariffs. The DXY fell from 109 in January 2025 to below 100 by mid-2025, even as other currencies were under stress. Currency war dynamics can produce counterintuitive dollar behavior when the US is itself perceived as the source of instability.

## How It Shows Up in Real Markets: Three Case Studies

### Case Study 1: China's August 2015 Devaluation and the EM Selloff

The sequence of events in August 2015 illustrates how quickly a unilateral devaluation transmits across asset classes.

August 11, 2015: The PBOC sets the daily yuan reference rate 1.9% weaker, the biggest one-day move since 1994. Within hours, Asian equity markets are selling off. The MSCI EM FX index drops 1.0% on the day. Commodity prices, which are priced in dollars but consumed heavily by China, fall on fears that Chinese demand is weakening — Brent crude falls 2.5%.

August 12: PBOC devalues by another 1.6%. The VIX rises. EM currency declines broaden. The Indonesian rupiah, Malaysian ringgit, and South African rand each fall more than 1.5%.

August 24: Global equity markets experience their sharpest single-day drop since 2011 — the "Black Monday" mini-crash, with the S&P 500 falling 3.9% at the open. Shanghai Composite falls 8.5%. The episode is partly attributed to the uncertainty generated by China's August 11-12 moves, combined with worries about Fed rate hike timing.

The professional lessons from August 2015: surprise devaluations require an immediate response; the first wave (EM FX) hits within hours; the equity market impact can be delayed by days or weeks but is real; commodity markets linked to the devaluing country's demand profile get hit simultaneously.

#### Worked example:

A portfolio manager holds \$10 million in a diversified EM equity fund on August 10, 2015. The MSCI EM index is at 900. After the China devaluation:

- MSCI EM drops approximately 15% over the following two weeks (from 900 to 765).
- Portfolio value: \$10,000,000 × (1 - 0.15) = \$8,500,000. Loss of \$1,500,000.
- An EM hedge via put options on EEM (iShares MSCI EM ETF) costing 1.5% of portfolio value (\$150,000) in premium would have limited the downside to approximately 5% on the hedged portion, saving roughly \$1,000,000 net of option cost.
- The unhedged manager loses \$1,500,000; the hedged manager loses approximately \$500,000. The asymmetry underscores why currency war detection signals — had they been read correctly in the weeks prior — would have justified the hedge cost.

### Case Study 2: Japan 2022 — Defending 145

The Japan 2022 intervention episode is a masterclass in FX defense mechanics. The Bank of Japan was caught in a structural bind: its yield curve control (YCC) policy capped 10-year JGB yields at 0.25%, which made Japanese bonds unattractive when US Treasuries were yielding 4%+. Capital flowed out of yen into dollars organically.

![Japanese yen path 2020 to 2025 with BOJ intervention and carry trade unwind](/imgs/blogs/competitive-devaluation-when-countries-race-to-the-bottom-2.png)

The Ministry of Finance's response was to spend approximately \$60 billion in three interventions — September 22, October 21, and October 24, 2022 — each timed for maximum effect in thin market hours (late New York time, early Tokyo time) to get maximum yen per dollar spent.

#### Worked example:

Japan's Ministry of Finance spends \$20 billion in the September 22 intervention. The yen is at 145.9 per dollar at the moment of intervention.

Step 1: MOF instructs BOJ to sell \$20,000,000,000 and buy yen.
Step 2: At 145.9 yen per dollar, this purchases approximately 2,918,000,000,000 yen (roughly 2.9 trillion yen).
Step 3: This sudden surge in yen demand moves the spot rate. The yen appreciates roughly 3-4% immediately — from 145.9 to approximately 140.3 per dollar.
Step 4: For a dollar-based investor who had a short yen position of \$100 million notional (i.e., expected yen to weaken): the position was worth roughly +\$3 million at 145.9; after the move to 140.3, it is worth roughly -\$4 million — a \$7 million swing on a \$100 million position in under an hour. This is why intervention is so painful for carry traders and why the BOJ's ammunition, while finite, is formidable in short bursts.

The aftermath: the yen bounced from a low of 151.9 (October 2022) to around 130 by early 2023, as the Fed signaled a slower pace of future hikes. The \$60 billion intervention bought approximately 4 months of stability.

### Case Study 3: Turkey 2021-23 — When Interest Rate Theory Goes Wrong

Turkey's currency crisis is the most extreme devaluation episode among major economies in the modern era. The lira lost 80% of its purchasing power against the dollar between early 2021 and mid-2023.

![Turkish lira depreciation 2015 to 2025 with key policy decisions annotated](/imgs/blogs/competitive-devaluation-when-countries-race-to-the-bottom-1.png)

The core mechanism was straightforward but devastating. With domestic interest rates at 8.5% and inflation at 85%, the real interest rate in Turkey was approximately -76%. Saving in lira meant watching your purchasing power evaporate at roughly 6% per month. The rational response for Turkish citizens and businesses was to convert lira savings into dollars, gold, or real estate — all of which are stores of value in hard currency terms. This structural demand for dollars from domestic actors, combined with foreign portfolio outflows, drove persistent depreciation.

#### Worked example:

A Turkish construction company borrows 10 million euros from a European bank in January 2021, at 3% annual interest, when the exchange rate is 9 TRY per euro. The company's revenues are in lira (it sells apartments to Turkish buyers).

At the time of borrowing:
- Euro debt: 10,000,000 EUR = 90,000,000 TRY at 9 TRY/EUR
- Annual interest payment: 300,000 EUR = 2,700,000 TRY

By June 2023, the rate is approximately 28 TRY per euro:
- Same debt: 10,000,000 EUR = 280,000,000 TRY (the debt tripled in lira terms)
- Annual interest payment: 300,000 EUR = 8,400,000 TRY (interest payment tripled in lira terms)
- The company's apartments, priced in lira, are not worth three times more — perhaps 1.8× in nominal terms due to inflation
- The company is effectively insolvent. Its hard-currency debt is 280 million TRY; its assets are worth perhaps 160 million TRY at current lira values.

This worked example is replicated across Turkey's corporate sector. The Bank for International Settlements estimated that Turkish non-financial corporates had approximately \$165 billion in foreign currency debt exposure by 2022. The currency collapse effectively wiped out equity in a significant portion of this sector.

### Case Study 4: The Safe Haven Response — Gold and USD in Currency Wars

During currency war episodes, two assets have historically been the clearest beneficiaries: the US dollar and gold. The logic differs for each.

The dollar rises because it is the primary reserve currency and the destination for capital fleeing devaluing currencies. In the 2015 EM selloff triggered by China's devaluation, the DXY rose approximately 2% in August. In the broader 2022 episode when the yen and the pound (during the Truss mini-budget crisis) were weakening simultaneously, the DXY hit a 20-year high of 114.8 in September 2022.

![USD index DXY during major geopolitical and financial crises 2020 to 2025](/imgs/blogs/competitive-devaluation-when-countries-race-to-the-bottom-3.png)

Gold's behavior during currency wars is subtler. Gold is priced in dollars, so a stronger dollar would normally depress gold prices. But gold also serves as a hedge against fiat currency debasement more broadly — when multiple currencies are in distress simultaneously, gold often rises even as the dollar rises. Historical data across the top 10 major geopolitical events since 2001 shows gold averaging a +2.3% gain in the 7 days following a major shock, while the USD averages +1.1%. The mechanisms are different: dollar gains from safe-haven capital flows; gold gains from debasement fears and central bank reserve diversification.

Central banks themselves have become significant gold buyers since the 2022 Russian asset freeze demonstrated that dollar reserves can be politically weaponized. Emerging market central banks — China, India, Turkey, Poland — dramatically accelerated gold purchases from 2022 onward, viewing gold as the one major reserve asset that cannot be sanctioned. This structural demand adds a floor to gold prices during currency war episodes.

#### Worked example:

A \$1 million portfolio at the start of Turkey's lira crisis (January 2021):

Portfolio A — no hedges:
- \$700,000 in MSCI EM equities
- \$300,000 in TRY-denominated Turkish bonds

By June 2023:
- MSCI EM equities: down approximately 18% in USD terms (China drag, global tightening) → \$574,000
- Turkish bonds: face value preserved in TRY, but TRY fell 70% vs USD → \$300,000 × 0.30 = \$90,000
- Total: \$664,000. Loss: \$336,000 (-33.6%)

Portfolio B — currency war hedge added in January 2021:
- \$600,000 in MSCI EM equities
- \$100,000 in Turkish bonds (reduced)
- \$150,000 in gold (XAU)
- \$150,000 in USD cash

By June 2023:
- EM equities: \$492,000 (-18%)
- Turkish bonds: \$30,000 (-70% FX)
- Gold: rose approximately 15% from Jan 2021 to Jun 2023 (volatile path, net positive) → \$172,500
- USD cash: \$150,000 (held value)
- Total: \$844,500. Loss vs initial: \$155,500 (-15.6%)

The hedge portfolio outperforms by \$180,500 — approximately 18 percentage points of outperformance — simply from reducing EM fixed income exposure, adding gold, and holding USD cash.

## The G20 Pledge and Why It Is Structurally Toothless

Every G20 communique since the Seoul Summit of 2010 has included a commitment along the lines of: "We will refrain from competitive devaluations. We will not target our exchange rates for competitive purposes." The language sounds firm. The reality is that this pledge has essentially no enforcement mechanism.

The structural problems are multiple:

**No definition of "competitive."** What separates a competitive devaluation from a monetary policy response to domestic conditions? When the Federal Reserve conducts quantitative easing and the dollar weakens, is that competitive devaluation? When China cuts rates in response to slowing domestic growth and the yuan falls, is that competitive? There is no agreed international legal standard that distinguishes legitimate domestic monetary policy from beggar-thy-neighbor currency manipulation.

**No tribunal.** The IMF has a mandate under Article IV of its Articles of Agreement to "oversee the compliance" of member countries with exchange rate obligations. But in practice, the IMF lacks the authority to compel policy changes or impose sanctions on members who do not require IMF assistance. Countries that are not dependent on IMF lending have little reason to comply with IMF objections to their exchange rate policies.

**No sanctions regime.** G20 communiques are political statements, not legal agreements. They are not ratified by legislatures and carry no treaty force. A country that "violates" the no-competitive-devaluation pledge faces no automatic consequence other than diplomatic criticism. China was criticized diplomatically for the 2015 move; Turkey received IMF expressions of concern during the lira crisis; neither faced material consequences from G20 institutions.

**Geopolitical calculation.** Even where the will to enforce existed, the political complexity of the G20 membership — which includes both the US and China, both Russia (until suspension) and Western allies — makes coordinated action against any member nearly impossible. The G20 works by consensus, and consensus is extremely difficult when the member in question is a major economic power with significant geopolitical leverage.

The bottom line: the G20 pledge on competitive devaluation serves a signaling function — it establishes a normative expectation that can be cited when criticizing a country's policy — but it does not prevent currency wars. A practitioner who is waiting for G20 enforcement to constrain a devaluation is waiting for something that will not come.

## How to Trade Currency Wars: The Playbook

Currency wars create specific, time-bounded trading opportunities in FX, gold, and equities. The key is having a framework ready before the signal fires, so that position sizing and entry can be executed quickly.

![Currency war trading playbook with positions, catalysts, sizing, and invalidation signals](/imgs/blogs/competitive-devaluation-when-countries-race-to-the-bottom-7.png)

### Position 1: Long USD vs the Devaluing Currency

The most direct trade is simply to be long dollars and short the currency that is being devalued or is under competitive pressure. The challenge is identifying the devaluation before or shortly after it begins, rather than chasing a move that is already largely priced in.

The entry signals to watch:
- Reserve data showing month-over-month decline of more than 3% in three consecutive months
- Rate cuts by the target central bank while the Fed and peers are holding or hiking
- Political pressure on the central bank (governor replacements, government statements about exchange rate levels)
- Spot-to-NDF spread widening to more than 2%

Position sizing should be modest — currency positions carry overnight roll costs, and central bank interventions can produce sharp short-covering rallies. A reasonable initial allocation is 0.5-1.0% of AUM per active signal, with scaling rules as the thesis confirms.

Invalidation: a credible IMF standby program (which comes with conditionality that stabilizes reserves), a pivot to orthodox monetary policy (rate hikes), or a reserve injection from a strategic partner (as Saudi Arabia and Gulf states have provided to Pakistan and Egypt in various crisis episodes).

### Position 2: Long Gold

Gold is the meta-hedge when the currency war becomes broad-based — when multiple currencies are weakening simultaneously and the debasement trade becomes global rather than country-specific. Gold also benefits from the structural central bank buying trend that has accelerated since 2022.

The gold trade is most cleanly expressed through spot gold or gold ETFs. The entry trigger is a currency war that is spreading beyond one country: when a second major EM currency comes under pressure in the same quarter, the likelihood of gold outperformance increases significantly. Sizing at 1-2% of AUM in a volatile episode, adding on weakness, is a reasonable approach.

The gold trade can be invalidated by a sharp Fed rate hike cycle that strengthens the dollar broadly — as happened in 2022, when gold fell despite the broader EM currency stress, because the dollar was strengthening dramatically. In that scenario, gold's dollar-price depression from USD strength outweighs the debasement bid. Watch the real US 10-year yield (nominal yield minus inflation expectations): when real yields are rising toward 2%+, gold struggles.

### Position 3: Short EM Equities

When currency wars are in full flight, EM equities face a triple headwind: the weaker currency reduces the USD value of returns for foreign investors; import cost inflation squeezes corporate margins; and capital flight raises the cost of capital in EM markets, reducing equity valuations.

The most efficient expression is through EM equity ETFs (EEM, VWO) or index futures where available. The short should be sized against the specific EM exposure — a country-specific thesis (e.g., Turkey equities) is expressed through country ETFs or individual stocks; a broader EM contagion thesis uses the MSCI EM index.

Timing matters: EM equity shorts often have the best risk-reward early in a currency crisis, before the central bank pivot or IMF program that can trigger sharp rebounds. Once reserves stabilization is confirmed or a credible new monetary framework is in place — as Turkey showed with its 2023 orthodox pivot — EM equity reversals can be violent and rapid.

### Cross-Asset Execution Framework

For a trader running a discretionary macro book, a currency war detection would trigger a specific sequence:

Day 0 (signal fires): Size a 0.5% USD long / target FX short in the FX market. Add a 0.5% gold long (XAU/USD). Flag EM equities for increased monitoring.

Day 3-5 (contagion signals): If a second EM currency comes under pressure, add 0.5% gold and initiate a 0.5% EM equity short (EEM puts or futures).

Day 10-20 (full escalation): If reserves are falling, officials are denying devaluation intent, and the IMF has not yet engaged, add a further 0.5% USD long and hold EM equity shorts until an intervention or policy pivot signal appears.

Exit triggers: IMF program announcement, central bank governor credibly committed to rate hikes, reserves stabilization for two consecutive months, G7 coordination signal on currency stability.
