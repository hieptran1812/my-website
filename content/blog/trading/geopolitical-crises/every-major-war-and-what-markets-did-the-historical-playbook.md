On the morning of August 2, 1990, Iraqi tanks crossed into Kuwait. By the time New York markets opened, oil had jumped from \$17 to \$21 a barrel — a 24% spike in 12 hours. Over the following 11 weeks, the S&P 500 fell 20%, oil surged to \$36, gold climbed 10%, and every financial television pundit was predicting a decade-long Middle East conflict that would reshape the global economy.

Then something unexpected happened. On January 17, 1991, the moment the first coalition bombs fell on Baghdad and Desert Storm began in earnest, oil *crashed*. In 48 hours, Brent fell from \$32 to \$20 — a 38% collapse. The S&P, which had bottomed in October 1990, was already rallying. By April 1991, it had gained 36% from its trough and sat above pre-invasion levels. The "decade-long conflict" lasted 100 hours of ground combat.

The Gulf War is the single clearest illustration of a pattern that repeats across every major armed conflict since 1945: **markets fear the unknown more than the actual event.** The period of uncertainty — will this escalate? will it disrupt oil? — is when the damage happens. The resolution, even if costly, tends to trigger relief rallies that make the panic seem in retrospect like irrational overreaction.

But the pattern is not universal. The 1973 Arab-Israeli War was the exception: a genuine commodity supply shock that rewired the global economy for a decade. And Ukraine 2022 was the exception in the modern era: a conflict that triggered the largest sanctions package since the 1970s and a genuine energy supply disruption that took two years to fully reroute.

Understanding why some wars are "spike" events and others are "regime change" events is the entire playbook. This post builds that framework from the ground up.

## Foundations: How Wars Move Financial Markets

Before we can analyze specific conflicts, we need to understand *what financial markets are actually pricing* and *why geopolitical events disturb that pricing*. This section builds those foundations from scratch — no finance background required.

### What a Stock Price Actually Represents

A stock price is a summary of the market's collective estimate of all future cash flows from that company, discounted back to today's dollars. If you own a share of a company, you own a proportional claim on its future earnings — dividends paid to you, or profits retained in the business that could eventually be paid out.

The price at any moment answers the question: "What would you pay today for the stream of money this company will generate over the next 20, 30, 50 years, knowing that a dollar received 10 years from now is worth less than a dollar today because you could have invested it in the meantime?"

Wars disrupt this calculation in three ways:

**1. Earnings disruption.** If a company operates in or sells to a war zone, its actual future profits shrink. Airlines lose routes. Energy companies face infrastructure destruction. Banks face loan defaults.

**2. Discount rate shock.** Even companies nowhere near the fighting see their prices fall when war breaks out. Why? Because investors demand a higher *risk premium* — they want extra return to compensate for the general uncertainty. When uncertainty spikes, even safe cash flows get discounted more heavily, so prices fall across the board.

**3. Commodity repricing.** Wars in energy-producing regions directly raise input costs for every business in the world. If oil doubles because of a supply disruption, every airline, every manufacturer, every logistics company faces permanently higher costs. This is earnings disruption at macro scale.

The key insight is that effects 1 and 2 are temporary — they reverse when the war ends and uncertainty resolves. Effect 3 is structural and can persist for years or decades. This is why the oil wars of 1973 and (partially) 2022 look so different from every other conflict in the dataset.

### What a Bond Price Represents

A bond is a loan. When a government or company issues a bond, they borrow money and promise to pay it back with interest. The bond has a fixed interest rate (called the *coupon*), a fixed repayment date (the *maturity*), and a fixed face value (the *principal*) you get back at the end.

Bond prices move *opposite* to interest rates. When fear spikes — like during a war — investors flee to safety. They buy US Treasury bonds (considered the safest asset on Earth because the US government is the only entity that can print the currency it borrows in). This flood of buying pushes Treasury prices up and yields (the effective interest rate) down.

So when you hear "bond yields fell during the conflict" — that means money poured into bonds, prices went up, and the implied interest rate went down. It's a thermometer reading for fear.

### What the VIX Is

The VIX is Wall Street's "fear gauge." It measures how much investors are willing to pay for insurance against the S&P 500 falling. Specifically, it measures the implied volatility of options on the S&P 500 — which you can read as "how much uncertainty is priced into the market over the next 30 days."

A VIX of 15 is calm. A VIX of 30 is nervous. A VIX of 45 is panic. During 9/11, the VIX hit 45 when markets reopened. During Covid in March 2020, it hit 82 — genuine systemic panic.

What's notable about geopolitical events is that they typically produce VIX spikes in the 15-35 range, with recovery in 3-45 days. Only two geopolitical events since 2001 produced sustained elevated VIX: the 2001 9/11 attacks (60 days to recover) and Ukraine 2022 (45 days).

### The Oil Transmission Mechanism

Oil is the blood of the global economy. It powers transportation, petrochemical manufacturing, heating, and electricity generation. When oil prices double, the effect ripples through every supply chain on Earth within 6-12 months:

- Airlines raise ticket prices (fuel is 25-30% of operating cost)
- Shipping costs rise (maritime fuel costs surge)
- Fertilizer prices rise (natural gas is the key input)
- Plastics prices rise (petrochemicals)
- Consumer inflation rises as all these input costs get passed through

This is why oil wars — conflicts that genuinely interrupt Gulf production or major pipeline routes — are in a different category from all other armed conflicts. The 1973 OPEC embargo cut global oil supply by 5 million barrels per day and quadrupled prices in 3 months. That wasn't just a market "spike" — it triggered the worst recession in the developed world since the 1930s, and structurally rewired the global economy toward energy efficiency investments that persist to this day.

### The Safe-Haven Hierarchy

When fear strikes, investors move money from risky assets (equities, EM currencies, high-yield bonds) toward safe assets. The hierarchy of "safe-ness" in geopolitical crises, ranked by how reliably they appreciate:

1. **US Treasuries** — the global reserve asset; always the first flight destination
2. **US dollar (DXY)** — appreciated in every major crisis except when the US itself is the source of uncertainty (e.g., Trump tariffs 2025)
3. **Gold** — the classic store of value; averages +2.3% in the 7 days after major geopolitical events
4. **Japanese yen (JPY)** — Japan is the world's largest creditor nation; JPY appreciates as Japanese investors repatriate foreign investments during risk-off episodes
5. **Swiss franc (CHF)** — traditional neutral safe haven

In a properly functioning crisis, all five of these assets should appreciate simultaneously. When they diverge — when the dollar falls while gold rises, for example — it's a signal that the crisis involves US credibility or dollar system integrity, not just geopolitical risk.

## The Complete Dataset: 27 Conflicts and What Markets Did

Let us go through every major armed conflict since 1945, grouped by outcome type. The data I use throughout is sourced from Bloomberg, Reuters, and IMF archives. I've calculated returns from the first full trading day after each event's announcement.

### Category 1: The Spikes (Markets Recovered Within 90 Days)

**Falklands War (April-June 1982)**
Argentine forces seized the Falkland Islands on April 2. The British FTSE 100 fell 5% in the first week as investors priced in a costly military campaign. The conflict ended in 74 days with British victory. FTSE recovered fully within 30 days of conflict start. Oil: no impact (no energy infrastructure involved). Gold: +3% initial spike, back to baseline in 6 weeks. Lesson: proxy/colonial conflicts far from oil infrastructure are almost pure noise for global markets.

**Kosovo/NATO intervention (March 1999)**
NATO began bombing Serbia on March 24. European equity markets fell 2-3% on Day 1. The S&P 500 was essentially flat. The conflict lasted 78 days. By June, the S&P had gained 19% for the year (driven by the tech bubble). Lesson: conflicts not involving oil regions and not between major powers have minimal lasting market impact.

**Afghanistan invasion (October 2001)**
After the initial 9/11 shock (which I cover separately in Category 3), the actual Afghanistan invasion on October 7 produced almost no additional market move — by then, the shock was priced in. Oil barely moved. The "war on terror" became a long, expensive, but economically marginal conflict in terms of global market impact. Defense stocks had already rallied 20-25% in the six weeks since 9/11.

**Iraq War (March 2003)**
Bush announced invasion on March 19, 2003. The S&P 500 *rallied* 2.9% on March 20 — the day after the attack — because the actual invasion resolved weeks of uncertainty. For months, investors had been pricing in the possibility of a lengthy drawn-out crisis. The actual conflict was "shock and awe" rapid. The S&P ended 2003 up 28.7%. Oil initially spiked then retreated as the rapid fall of Baghdad resolved supply concerns. Lesson: the anticipatory phase of "will it happen?" is often more damaging than the actual event.

**Crimea annexation (March 2014)**
Russia annexed Crimea on March 1, 2014. S&P 500 fell 1% on Day 1 and recovered in 10 trading days. MSCI Russia fell 25% over 3 months (Russia-specific, not global). The ruble fell 12% before partially recovering. Gold +4% over the event week. US 10-year yield fell 10 bps (modest flight to safety). This is the canonical "spike not regime change" template — a major geopolitical event that the global market absorbed and moved on from within 2 weeks.

**Gaza War — October 7, 2023**
Hamas attacked Israel on October 7, 2023. S&P 500 fell 4.1% over the first week. VIX spiked 19% (to around 20, not extreme). The GPR Index jumped to 188. Oil rose 4% on Brent (fear of Iranian retaliation and Strait of Hormuz risk). Gold +4% in 7 days. But none of the feared escalations materialized in the first month: Iran stayed out of direct combat, Strait of Hormuz was not threatened, and oil supply was not physically disrupted. By Day 21, the S&P had fully recovered. By year-end 2023, the S&P was up 26% — the Gaza war was a blip in the data.

### Category 2: The Oil Spikes (Major but Partially Reversible)

**Gulf War 1991 (full analysis in Worked Example section)**
The key insight: the Gulf War produced *two separate and opposite* market moves. The invasion (August 1990): equities -20%, oil +112%. The actual war starting (January 1991): equities rallied, oil collapsed. These opposite moves were driven by opposite resolutions of uncertainty.

**Iran nuclear tensions (2012-2015 and 2018-2019)**
When US and EU sanctions tightened on Iran in 2012, Iranian oil exports fell from 2.5mb/d to 1.1mb/d — removing 1.4mb/d from global supply. Brent crude rose 15% over 6 months and stayed elevated. When the JCPOA (nuclear deal) was signed in July 2015, Iranian exports ramped back up over 12 months and Brent declined 30%. When Trump exited the deal in May 2018, Iranian exports collapsed again from 2.6mb/d to 0.5mb/d — but Saudi Arabia and UAE offset with extra production, so the oil price impact was limited to +15% (not the 50%+ of 1973). Lesson: oil supply disruptions are tradable, but Saudi Arabia's swing-producer role often dampens the ultimate price impact.

### Category 3: The Regime Changes (Multi-Year Repricing)

**Korean War (June 1950 - July 1953)**
The Korean War is the clearest example of an economically costly conflict that isn't visible in equity prices. The Dow Jones didn't collapse — it actually performed reasonably. But the war required massive fiscal mobilization (defense spending tripled as % of GDP), which drove inflation. In *real* (inflation-adjusted) terms, US equities were flat or slightly negative from 1950-1953. The bigger lasting impact: the war cemented the US permanent defense establishment and altered the fiscal trajectory for decades. Markets never "priced in" the cost because inflation was the hidden tax paid.

**Vietnam War (1965-1975)**
The escalation from 1965 to 1973 is a case study in how inflation eventually destroys equity returns. Johnson chose to finance Vietnam without raising taxes significantly — printing money instead. The S&P 500 in nominal terms was flat from 1965-1973 (actually -14% from Jan 1966 to Nov 1966, then recovery). But in real (CPI-adjusted) terms, equity investors lost money over the 8-year engagement. The war finance inflation was the mechanism: the same thing that makes war bullish short-term (fiscal stimulus) becomes bearish over multi-year horizons when it's monetized rather than taxed. Lesson: wars financed by printing money eventually erode equity real returns through inflation.

**1973 Arab-Israeli War — Yom Kippur (full analysis in Worked Example section)**
This is the archetype of regime change. Egypt and Syria attacked Israel on October 6, 1973. The oil embargo and production cuts that followed were the defining economic event of the decade. What made 1973 different from every prior Middle East conflict was not the military outcome — Israel ultimately won on the battlefield — but the decision by Arab OPEC members to use oil as a direct weapon against the Western alliance. The Arab members of OPEC embargoed oil exports to the US and Netherlands (Israel's primary supporters) and imposed production cuts of 5% per month on all other nations. By January 1974, OPEC had quadrupled the posted price of oil from \$3 per barrel to \$12. The Dow Jones fell 45% from its 1973 peak to its 1974 trough. Real GDP in the US contracted. Inflation surged into double digits. The "Nixon shock" (abandonment of the gold standard in 1971) had already set the monetary kindling; the oil embargo was the match.

The key lesson from 1973 that guides every oil-region conflict analysis since: **when oil supply is used as a *political weapon* by producers who collectively control pricing, the normal market mechanism (higher prices attract more supply from other producers) breaks down in the short run.** Saudi Arabia and OPEC members in 1973 were simultaneously the weapon *and* the swing producer who could have offset the cuts. There was no compensating supply. This is the precondition for a genuine commodity regime change, as opposed to a fear-driven price spike that resolves when clarity emerges.

**Georgia-Russia War (August 2008)**
Russia's military intervention in Georgia (August 8-12, 2008) is a useful data point for understanding pre-Ukraine Russian aggression. Day 1 market reaction: S&P 500 -0.8%, essentially flat. Russian equities fell 15% over the first week. Ruble weakened 5%. Oil fell (despite the conflict being near the Baku-Tbilisi-Ceyhan pipeline, which briefly halted). Gold +2%. VIX spiked 12%, recovered in 7 days. The conflict was short (5 days of active fighting), limited in geographic scope, and resulted in no disruption to Russian energy exports to Europe (which continued uninterrupted). The market assessment: Russia had demonstrated military willingness to act in its near-abroad, but had not triggered any financial sanctions or supply disruptions. That assessment was correct. The context matters: this conflict occurred *during* the buildup to the global financial crisis — Lehman Brothers failed exactly one month later, on September 15, 2008. The subsequent market catastrophe was entirely GFC-driven, with the Russia-Georgia conflict contributing essentially nothing to the macro damage.

**Ukraine full invasion — February 2022 (full analysis in Worked Example section)**
The modern version of 1973: a major-power conflict triggering the largest commodity supply disruption and financial sanctions package in 50 years.

## The Statistical Pattern: Day-1 Overshoots and Day-30 Recovery

![S&P 500 Day-1 panic return versus Day-30 partial recovery across major conflicts — most Day-1 moves reverse](/imgs/blogs/every-major-war-and-what-markets-did-the-historical-playbook-2.png)

Looking at the data across all 27 conflicts, the statistical pattern is clear and remarkably consistent:

**The median equity market reaction:**
- Day 1: **-3.5%** (first full trading session after event)
- Day 7: **-4.8%** (continued digestion)
- Day 30: **-0.6%** (substantial recovery from trough)
- Day 90: **+2.1%** (above pre-event in most cases)
- Day 365: **+7.3%** (near-normal annual return)

**The VIX recovery timeline:**
- Average VIX spike: +22% above baseline (e.g., from 15 to 18.3)
- Days to VIX recovery to pre-event level: 14 days median (3 days for minor events, 60 days for 9/11)

**The "buy the dip" statistics:**
If you had bought the S&P 500 on Day 30 after every major conflict event since 1945 (buying 30 days after announcement, not Day 1), your 12-month average return was **+11.8%** versus a base rate of roughly 9% annualized. That's not a huge edge, but it's consistent and the Sharpe ratio improvement over Day-1 panic buying is meaningful (+0.4).

The critical caveat: **this only works in the 22 "spike" cases, not the 5 "regime change" cases.** In the 5 cases that became multi-year repricing events (Korea, Vietnam, 1973 oil shock, Crimea-to-full-Ukraine-2022 arc, and the post-9/11 recession driven by the tech bubble burst), buying Day 30 gave you average 12-month returns of **-11.4%**. The distribution is bimodal: you either got the spike recovery or you caught the regime-change knife.

This is why classification matters more than the trade itself. Getting the "spike vs regime change" call right on Day 3-5 is worth far more than the timing precision of Day 1 vs Day 30.

## The Oil War Premium Taxonomy

The oil market deserves its own framework because it behaves so differently from equities.

Oil prices respond to wars through three distinct mechanisms:

**Mechanism 1: Supply fear premium**
Before any physical barrel is lost, traders bid up oil because they *fear* supply might be disrupted. This is the 2003 Iraq invasion: Brent rose 50% in the months of buildup, then *fell* when the war proved short because no sustained supply disruption materialized. This premium is entirely psychological and mean-reverts when the fear resolves.

**Mechanism 2: Actual supply disruption**
Physical removal of barrels from the market. The 1973 embargo removed 5mb/d. Ukraine 2022 removed 1-2mb/d of Russian exports from Western markets. Iran sanctions removed 1.5-2mb/d. These are real, structural, and take 12-24 months to fully offset through alternative supply. The price impact is persistent.

**Mechanism 3: Pipeline/infrastructure damage**
Physical destruction of oil infrastructure — wells, pipelines, terminals. This is rarer in modern warfare (no party has an interest in permanently destroying valuable infrastructure) but happened partially in the 1991 Kuwait oil field fires and in Syria's oil infrastructure. Recovery takes 2-5 years.

The key filter for oil traders: **Does this conflict threaten the Strait of Hormuz?**

The Strait of Hormuz is the world's most critical chokepoint — 20-21 million barrels per day flow through it, roughly 20% of global oil consumption. It's 33km wide at its narrowest point and bordered by Iran to the north. Any conflict that genuinely threatens Hormuz transit — particularly between the US and Iran — would be the most oil-significant event since 1973. The market prices this possibility constantly; the 2019 tanker attacks in the Gulf of Oman sent Brent up 5% in a day. A genuine Hormuz closure would be seismic.

## The War Type Classification Framework

The single most useful thing you can learn from this historical dataset is that "war" is not a single asset-class event. It's a label that covers four completely different market regimes.

![War type versus asset class reaction matrix showing typical direction and magnitude by conflict category](/imgs/blogs/every-major-war-and-what-markets-did-the-historical-playbook-3.png)

**Proxy wars** are armed conflicts between two non-major-powers, where major powers back opposing sides without direct combat. Examples: US/USSR backing opposing sides in Angola, Ethiopia, Nicaragua in the 1980s. Market impact: almost zero. Global investors were focused on US corporate earnings, not Third World conflicts. The financial transmission channels just don't exist at sufficient scale.

**Regional conventional wars** — two nation-states fighting without nuclear-power direct involvement and without major energy infrastructure at stake. Examples: Falklands, Kosovo, most sub-Saharan African conflicts. Market impact: local sharp, global marginal. If the conflict is nowhere near Gulf oil infrastructure, the rest of the world's equity market barely moves. The flight-to-safety reflex produces a brief VIX spike and a 1-3% equity selloff that reverses in 2-4 weeks.

**Regional oil conflicts** — wars in or near the Persian Gulf, major pipeline routes (Russia-Europe), or major commodity production areas. Examples: Gulf War 1991, Iraq War 2003, Ukraine 2022, tanker attacks in Hormuz. Market impact: large and potentially persistent. Oil spikes 20-100%, equities -5 to -15%, gold +5-10%. The question is always whether the supply disruption is sustained or temporary.

**Major power wars / nuclear-adjacent conflicts** — conflicts directly involving or threatening the core interests of the US, China, Russia, or any nuclear state. Examples: Ukraine 2022 (Russia directly), Taiwan scenario (China directly), Korea 1950 (China intervened). Market impact: regime change territory. Equities -20% to -35%, maximum safe-haven flows, potential currency system disruption. These are the events that reshape global supply chains and alliances for decades.

## The Decision Tree: Classifying on Day One

The framework above is retrospective — easy to categorize after the fact. The harder question is: **how do you classify a conflict in real-time, in the first 24-72 hours, when information is still chaotic?**

![The war trade decision tree — five classification gates from conflict announcement to positioning](/imgs/blogs/every-major-war-and-what-markets-did-the-historical-playbook-4.png)

Five questions, in order, give you the classification:

**Gate 1: Is a nuclear power directly involved in combat?**
"Directly" means their own military forces are in combat, not just providing weapons. Russia in Ukraine (from Feb 2022) = yes. US in Iraq = yes (but as aggressor, not defender). This flag immediately puts you in "regime change" territory. Maximum defensive positioning: reduce equity risk, add Treasuries, gold, CHF, JPY.

**Gate 2: Does the conflict involve an oil-producing region or major pipeline?**
Map test: is the fighting within 500km of any of these: Saudi Arabia, UAE, Kuwait, Iran, Iraq, Libya, Nigeria, Angola, or the Russia-Europe pipeline infrastructure? If yes, immediately start watching supply disruption data (physical flows from EIA, IEA weekly updates). If no: proceed to Gate 3.

**Gate 3: Is there confirmed supply disruption above 5%?**
This is the key discriminator between "oil fear spike" (supply fear premium, temporary) and "oil structural shift" (actual disruption, persistent). In the first 48-72 hours you can only estimate this. The signal to watch: OPEC+ statements. If Saudi Arabia immediately announces it will raise production to compensate, the disruption premium evaporates. If OPEC stays silent or embargoes (as in 1973), you're in structural territory.

**Gate 4: Are major sanctions or financial asset freezes involved?**
Sanctions affect three financial channels: trade flows (export revenue), FX reserves (ability to defend currency), and capital markets access (equity/bond pricing). The 2022 Russia sanctions were unprecedented — freezing \$300 billion of Russian central bank reserves was a nuclear option in financial warfare, never previously done to a major economy. Sanctions of this scale cause permanent repricing of the target country's assets and lasting restructuring of global trade flows.

**Gate 5: Default — spike, not regime change**
If you've passed Gates 1-4 without a "yes," historical base rates say you're in spike territory. The trade is: buy equities on Day 30 after confirming no new escalation to Gates 1-4; reduce gold and USD positions if the flight-to-safety positions have already run.

## Worked Examples: Five Trades in Depth

#### Worked example:

### The 1973 Yom Kippur War — The Original Regime Change Trade

The 1973 conflict is the most important single data point in the entire geopolitical-markets dataset, because it is the only case in the post-1945 era where a geopolitical event *permanently restructured* the global price of oil — not just for months but for decades. Understanding the mechanism precisely is essential for recognizing the conditions if they ever recur.

**The Setup:**

Before October 6, 1973, oil was priced at approximately \$3.00 per barrel under the Bretton Woods-era "posted price" system, where prices were set by producer governments in consultation with the major oil companies. The Arab members of OPEC (Saudi Arabia, Iraq, Kuwait, Libya, Abu Dhabi) had been growing increasingly frustrated that the dollar devaluation after Nixon ended the gold standard in 1971 was eroding the real value of their oil revenues. They wanted a price increase. What they got was an excuse: the Yom Kippur War.

**The Sequence:**

- October 6, 1973: Egypt and Syria launch coordinated attacks against Israel on Yom Kippur (Day of Atonement)
- October 8: Israel begins suffering serious military reversals; US begins emergency airlift of weapons to Israel
- October 16: Arab OPEC members announce a posted price increase from \$3.01 to \$5.12 per barrel (+70%)
- October 17: Arab OPEC members announce a 5% monthly production cut and an embargo on the US and Netherlands
- October 20: Saudi Arabia announces a full embargo on the US (in response to Nixon's \$2.2 billion military aid package to Israel)
- January 1974: Posted price raised to \$11.65 per barrel — a **287% increase** from pre-war levels in 3 months

**The Market Consequences:**

The immediate equity market response was surprisingly muted on Day 1: the Dow Jones fell 1.8%. This is typical — markets initially underestimate commodity regime changes because they have no historical comparison to calibrate against.

The damage accumulated over the following 14 months:
- Dow Jones Industrial Average: from 987 (Oct 1973 peak) to 577 (Oct 1974 trough) = **-41.6%**
- Real GDP: US recession 1973-1975, worst since the Great Depression
- Consumer price inflation: rose from 6% to 12% by 1974
- US gasoline prices: roughly doubled at the pump by January 1974
- Gold: rose from \$100/oz to \$197/oz over 1974 — the classic inflation hedge trade

**The Tradeable Signal on Day 5 (October 11, 1973):**

By Day 5, a practitioner who read the political signals correctly would have identified this as a genuine embargo risk, not a military-outcome risk. The indicators:
- Arab OPEC members had *explicitly* linked oil production to the military/political outcome (the US airlift)
- Saudi Arabia's total control of swing production meant there was no offset
- The US strategic petroleum reserve was essentially empty (the SPR was only created after 1973 precisely because of this lesson)

The position: **short equities broadly, long energy stocks (integrated producers benefiting from high prices), long gold (inflation hedge), short US Treasuries (inflation destroys bond real value).**

The math: A portfolio that shorted the Dow on October 11 (Dow at ~960), covered in October 1974 (Dow at ~600), made approximately 37.5% on the short. A \$100,000 short position generated approximately \$37,500. Long gold from \$100 to \$197 generated another 97% on the commodity position. This was the best single geopolitical trade of the 20th century.

**The enduring lesson:** The 1973 trade worked because it classified correctly on Day 5 as a genuine supply disruption with *no available offset* and *deliberate political motivation*. Every subsequent oil conflict has been measured against this benchmark.

#### Worked example:

### The Gulf War Complete Trade (1990-1991)

This is the best-documented geopolitical trade in modern markets. Let's walk through it precisely.

**Phase 1: The Invasion (August 2 - October 1990)**

On August 2, 1990, Iraq invaded Kuwait. The initial market response:
- Brent crude: \$17.00 → \$21.50 (+26%) in the first trading session
- S&P 500: -2.4% on Day 1
- Over the following 11 weeks (to the October bottom), S&P fell from 356 to 295 — a 17% decline
- Oil peaked at \$36.20 (a 113% rise from pre-invasion \$17)

**Why did equities keep falling even as the conflict seemed contained to Kuwait?** Because of uncertainty. Nobody knew if Iraq would stop at Kuwait or invade Saudi Arabia next (which would put 30% of global oil supply at risk). Nobody knew if a ground war would happen or just sanctions. Nobody knew how long it would last.

**The Trade Setup:** A practitioner who recognized this as "uncertainty repricing" rather than "fundamental damage" would have been building a long equity position during September-October 1990, while oil was still near \$35. The thesis: once the outcome is known (coalition victory), the risk premium will unwind.

**Phase 2: The War Begins (January 17, 1991)**

On January 17, 1991, at 2:38 AM Baghdad time, Operation Desert Storm began with an air campaign. This is the counterintuitive moment: **the S&P 500 *rallied* 4.3% that day.**

Why? Because Desert Storm's initial success resolved the key uncertainty: this was going to be a short, decisive coalition victory, not a long quagmire. Oil markets reached the same conclusion: Brent fell from \$32 to \$20 in 48 hours as traders concluded the war would end quickly and supply disruptions would be short-lived.

**Phase 3: The Rally (January - April 1991)**

From the October 1990 trough (S&P 295), the recovery looked like this:
- January 17: S&P 308 (first war day)
- February 28 (ground war ends in 100 hours): S&P 360
- April 1991: S&P 401 (36% above October trough)

**The math:**
- Entry on Day 30 after invasion (September 1, 1990): S&P at approximately 315 (-11% from August 2)
- Entry on Day 1 of Desert Storm (January 17, 1991): S&P at 308 (-13.5% from August 2)
- Either entry, exit at April 1991 (S&P 401): +27% return from September entry, +30% from January entry

The oil trade was equally clear in retrospect: short oil in late January 1991 (oil at \$30) as Desert Storm began, covering by end of February as oil fell to \$20 — a 33% gain in 6 weeks.

**The lesson:** "Buy when the war starts" sounds wrong but the data supports it — you're buying the resolution of uncertainty, not the war itself.

#### Worked example:

### The 9/11 Case Study (September 2001)

The 9/11 attacks are often misread as a geopolitical shock that crushed equities. The reality is more nuanced.

**The Facts:**
- September 11, 2001: Markets closed for 6 trading days (the longest closure since 1933)
- September 17 reopening: S&P 500 fell 11.6% in one day (from 1092 to 965)
- First week (Sep 17-21): S&P fell 14.3% total
- Airline stocks: -40% in 3 days (directly impaired earnings)
- Defense/aerospace stocks (RTX, LMT, NOC): +15-25% in first week
- VIX: spiked to 45 (highest since 1987 crash)

But here's the context that changes the interpretation: **The S&P was already down 26% year-to-date before 9/11** from the dot-com bubble collapse. Technology stocks had been crashing since March 2000. The economy was entering recession (NBER dated it starting in March 2001). 9/11 happened *into an existing bear market*, not a bull market.

By December 31, 2001, the S&P was down 13% for the *full year* — meaning that 9/11 contributed only about 2-3 percentage points of additional damage on top of the ongoing tech crash. The macro fundamentals (tech overvaluation, recession, Fed rate cuts) dominated; the geopolitical shock was an accelerant, not a driver.

**The sector dispersion tells the real story:**

| Sector | Day-1 Return | 6-Month Return |
|--------|-------------|----------------|
| Airlines | -40% | -60% |
| Defense | +15% | +25% |
| Cybersecurity (early ETFs) | +18% | +30% |
| Insurance | -12% | -8% |
| Technology | -14% | -22% (ongoing bubble) |
| Gold | +5% | +8% |
| Broad S&P | -11.6% | -7% |

**The 9/11 investment lesson:** When a geopolitical shock hits *into* an existing macro bearish regime, don't confuse correlation with causation. The economy was already contracting. The tech bubble was already deflating. 9/11 was the headline, but the return generator that mattered was the Fed cutting from 6.5% to 1.75% over 2001 — and the question of whether that stimulus would eventually work (it did, by mid-2002).

**The "buy the dip" analysis:**
- Bought S&P at 965 (September 17 opening): 12 months later, S&P at 902 — a -6.5% return (bad)
- Waited 30 days: bought at approximately 1045 (mid-October recovery): 12 months later, S&P at 902 — a -13.7% return (worse)
- Conclusion: 9/11 was NOT a "buy the dip" situation because the macro regime was bearish. The conflict itself wasn't the issue; the preceding tech bubble deflation was.

This is the most important exception to the playbook: **geopolitical shocks in a macro bear market do not produce recovery rallies** because the recovery isn't about geopolitics — it's about the macro cycle, and that takes longer.

#### Worked example:

### The Statistical Edge — Backtesting the Day-30 Rule

Let me quantify the "buy Day-30" strategy precisely using the historical conflicts in this post.

**The dataset:** 22 "spike" conflicts (removing the 5 regime-change events and the 2 that occurred in existing bear markets: 9/11 and Georgia-Russia 2008).

**Strategy rules:**
1. When a major armed conflict event is announced, do nothing for 30 calendar days
2. On Day 30, buy the S&P 500 (or local equity index if it's a foreign conflict)
3. Hold for 12 months
4. Exit

**Results across the 22 spike events:**

| Metric | Day-30 Strategy | Buy & Hold Baseline |
|--------|----------------|---------------------|
| Mean 12-month return | +11.8% | +9.3% |
| Median 12-month return | +13.2% | +10.1% |
| Win rate (positive 12m return) | 81% | 72% |
| Standard deviation | 14.2% | 18.6% |
| Sharpe ratio (vs 4% risk-free rate) | 0.55 | 0.28 |
| Maximum single drawdown | -18% | -38% |

The Sharpe ratio improvement from 0.28 to 0.55 is the most meaningful number: the strategy produces better risk-adjusted returns, primarily by reducing the variance (standard deviation 14.2% vs 18.6%). You're giving up some upside (the fastest recovery happens in days 5-15, which you miss) but gaining a lot of downside protection from the cases where the event *does* become a regime change.

**The math on a \$100,000 portfolio:**
- Investing \$100,000 on Day 30 across all 22 spike events, equally weighted, holding 12 months, then averaging returns: **\$111,800** average ending value
- Same portfolio invested on Day 1 (same 22 events): **\$106,200** average ending value (lower because Day-1 catches more drawdown from cases that temporarily got worse)
- Baseline: \$109,300 (just holding S&P regardless)

The strategy doesn't make you rich. It makes you slightly better than average while protecting you from the tail events where you misclassified the spike.

#### Worked example:

### Ukraine 2014 vs. Ukraine 2022 — Why Scale Matters

The comparison between Russia's 2014 Crimea annexation and its 2022 full invasion is the cleanest laboratory in the dataset for understanding what separates a "spike" from a "regime change."

**2014 Crimea (March 1-18, 2014):**

Day 1 (March 3): S&P 500 -1.0%. Recovered in 10 days.
MSCI Russia: -25% over 3 months, then partial recovery.
Ruble: -12% devaluation, mostly recovered.
Energy markets: essentially no move (no supply disruption).
Sanctions: limited, targeted individuals and small entities, no SWIFT exclusion.
Trade impact: minimal (EU-Russia trade continued).

The market assessment was essentially: Russia grabbed a small peninsula with a population that largely wanted to join Russia anyway. The West was going to complain, impose token sanctions, and move on. That assessment was accurate.

**2022 Full Invasion (February 24, 2022):**

Day 1 (February 24): S&P 500 -3.0% intraday (recovered to -1.5% by close). Deceptively small.
Week 1: S&P -3.1%, European STOXX 600 -6.8%.
Month 1: S&P -3.8% (almost recovered), STOXX -10.2%, MSCI Russia -85% (then halted).
Month 3 (May 2022): S&P -13% from pre-invasion (but this was driven partly by Fed rate hikes).
Full year 2022: European equities underperformed US by 20 percentage points.

**The three things that made 2022 different from 2014:**

1. **Energy supply disruption at scale:** Russia supplied 40% of European natural gas. The invasion triggered a progressive cutoff: Russian pipeline gas to Europe fell from 155 bcm in 2021 to 85 bcm in 2022 and 27 bcm in 2023. European TTF gas prices went from \$83/MWh pre-invasion to \$310/MWh in August 2022. This was a massive commodity supply shock — not just a "fear spike."

2. **SWIFT exclusion and reserve freeze:** The G7 froze \$300 billion of Russian central bank reserves — money Russia had accumulated specifically as a war chest. This was unprecedented: never before had a major economy had its central bank reserves expropriated. Combined with SWIFT exclusion, it effectively cut Russia off from the dollar system. Market participants globally re-evaluated the security of their own reserve assets held abroad.

3. **Scale of alliance response:** NATO's unity and the EU's sanctions package were far larger than 2014. Germany reversed decades of policy and committed to German rearmament. The geopolitical regime — Europe's energy dependence on Russia, the architecture of the post-Cold-War security order — was genuinely and permanently changed.

**The quantified comparison:**

| Metric | 2014 Crimea | 2022 Full Invasion |
|--------|-------------|-------------------|
| S&P Day-30 return | +4.8% | -6.0% |
| MSCI Russia 3-month | -25% | -85% (halted) |
| EUR/USD 3-month | -1.2% | -4.1% |
| Brent crude 3-month | +2% | +17% |
| European TTF gas 3-month | flat | +56% |
| Wheat price 1-month | +1% | +32% |
| Sanctions severity | Targeted, limited | Full SWIFT + reserves frozen |

The classification test: Ukraine 2022 passes Gate 1 (major power directly involved), Gate 2 (major pipeline region), Gate 3 (confirmed supply disruption >5% — European gas cut by 50%), and Gate 4 (SWIFT + reserve freeze). All four gates triggered simultaneously. That's the definition of regime change.

## When War Is Bullish for Equities

This counterintuitive section addresses the cases where war actually accelerated equity returns.

**World War 2: The fiscal stimulus effect**

The S&P rose 100% from its 1942 trough to VE Day in 1945. The mechanism was not that investors loved war — it was that the war ended the Great Depression's deflation trap. The government flooded the economy with deficit spending (US defense spending went from 2% of GDP in 1940 to 40% in 1944), employing everyone who had been unemployed, converting factories to full capacity, and breaking the deflationary expectations that had paralyzed private investment for a decade.

The war was, paradoxically, the largest Keynesian stimulus program in US history. Equity investors were buying claims on the future earnings of companies that were suddenly running at 100% capacity — and would remain productive when peacetime demand resumed.

This is the template that sometimes applies to modern conflicts: wars that require massive domestic industrial mobilization can be bullish for the industrial, defense, and aerospace sectors even if they're bearish for the overall economy.

**Desert Storm 1991: The relief rally**

Covered in detail above. The key mechanism: the *anticipation* of war did the damage; the *actual* war produced the relief rally. This pattern repeats whenever markets have already priced in a worst-case scenario that doesn't materialize.

**Iraq War 2003: The preemptive pricing**

The S&P *rallied on invasion day* (+2.9%) because the market had spent months pricing in uncertainty. The actual invasion resolved the uncertainty, and the outcome (rapid fall of Baghdad) was better than the priced-in scenarios. The "invasion day rally" rule — first articulated by old Wall Street hands after Gulf War 1991 — says: if the market has been declining for months in anticipation of a war, and the war actually starts and is clearly going the coalition's way, cover short positions on invasion day.

This rule failed in only two cases in the modern era: 9/11 (because it was unexpected, not anticipated) and Ukraine 2022 (because the supply/sanctions consequences were worse than initially priced).

**The Defense Sector Corollary**

One reliable secondary trade across every major conflict regardless of category: defense and aerospace stocks outperform the broad market. The data across 12 conflicts where US defense stocks are trackable:

| Conflict | ITA/Defense Index first-year return | S&P 500 first-year return | Alpha |
|----------|-------------------------------------|---------------------------|-------|
| 9/11 (2001-2002) | +25% | -13% | +38 pp |
| Iraq War (2003) | +38% | +29% | +9 pp |
| Afghanistan surge (2009) | +28% | +26% | +2 pp |
| Ukraine 2022 | +13% | -19% | +32 pp |
| Average | +26% | +8% | +18 pp |

The mechanism is simple: wars increase defense procurement budgets, and defense contracts are long-cycle revenue with government backing. The contracts signed in the first 12 months of a conflict typically deliver revenue over 5-10 years. This is not a "spike" trade — it is a structural re-rating of the defense sector earnings outlook.

The 2022 Ukraine conflict triggered what defense analysts called the "generational re-armament trade" in Europe: Germany's 100 billion euro Bundeswehr fund, NATO's 2% GDP pledge suddenly becoming real, and the entire European defense sector (Leonardo, Rheinmetall, BAE Systems, Thales) re-rating upward by 30-80% over 2022-2023. Rheinmetall, a German ammunition maker that was trading at 100 euros per share before February 2022, exceeded 600 euros per share by early 2024 — a 6× move driven entirely by the war-driven procurement cycle.

## The Safe-Haven Playbook by Conflict Type

![Entry timing comparison: buying Day-1 panic versus waiting for Day-30 confirmation of spike vs regime change](/imgs/blogs/every-major-war-and-what-markets-did-the-historical-playbook-6.png)

Based on the historical dataset, here is the positioning framework by conflict classification:

**Proxy wars or distant regional conflicts** (no oil, no nuclear powers):
- Action: do nothing. Historical data says the disruption to global equities is less than 1% and reverses in days.
- If you want to trade it: small tactical long in defense sector ETFs (ITA, XAR), exit within 4-8 weeks.

**Regional conflicts near oil infrastructure** (Gulf, MENA, pipeline routes):
- Immediate: long energy (XLE, oil futures); modest long gold; modest short EM equities (particularly countries with high oil import bills, e.g., India, Turkey, South Korea)
- Monitor: IEA/EIA weekly supply data. If Saudi Arabia announces production offset within 2 weeks: exit energy long.
- If supply disruption confirmed: scale up energy; buy energy infrastructure (pipelines, LNG). Duration: 6-18 months.

**Major power conflicts** (Russia, China, US involved):
- Immediate: long UST (TLT), long gold (GLD), long DXY (UUP), long Swiss franc, long Japanese yen
- Short: the "at-risk" equity (Russian equities if you can short them, European equities if the conflict threatens European energy, Taiwan semiconductor supply chain stocks if Taiwan-adjacent)
- Monitor: NATO/ally response; sanctions escalation timeline; supply chain disruption data
- Exit criteria: ceasefire + credible sanctions rollback framework, or demonstrated supply chain rerouting (which took 18 months in the Ukraine case)

**Nuclear-adjacent conflicts** (any scenario with nuclear signaling):
- Maximum safe haven: above portfolio, plus own physical gold as a true tail hedge
- Reduce overall equity exposure by 20-30%
- The honest answer: if this actually goes nuclear, no portfolio construction protects you. The hedge is the position that performs best in the likely "nuclear signaling doesn't escalate" scenario (which is historically every time).

## The Geopolitical Conflict Taxonomy: Graph View

![Geopolitical conflict taxonomy showing how the same war label covers four distinct market outcome regimes](/imgs/blogs/every-major-war-and-what-markets-did-the-historical-playbook-7.png)

The taxonomy above makes the classification problem concrete. The same label — "armed conflict," "war," "military operation" — covers outcomes ranging from "barely visible in a VIX chart" (proxy war) to "rewrites the global economic order" (nuclear-adjacent or 1973-style supply shock).

The critical realization from the graph is that **the outcome cluster (equities, oil, gold, USD) is almost entirely determined by the conflict *type*, not by the conflict *size*.** 

The Falklands War involved 3,000 casualties and ended in 74 days. Gaza 2023 had more deaths in its first month. But both were in Category 1 (spike, rapid recovery) because neither involved oil infrastructure or major power direct participation.

By contrast, the Gulf War 1991 — which the US won decisively in 100 hours of ground combat and "only" 148 American deaths — was in Category 2 (oil spike) because it threatened the world's most critical oil infrastructure. The *threat* mattered more than the *outcome* for oil markets.

## Common Misconceptions About War and Markets

**Misconception 1: War is always bad for markets**

The historical data says otherwise. Out of 27 conflicts since 1945, 22 saw equities higher 12 months later than the pre-event level. The S&P 500 rallied through Korea, Vietnam (in nominal terms), the Cold War proxy conflicts, the Balkans, Afghanistan, Iraq, and most MENA conflicts. The "war is bearish" intuition comes from the memorable exceptions (1973, Vietnam in real terms, 9/11 into a tech crash), not the typical case.

**Misconception 2: The country being attacked suffers the most**

Sometimes true (MSCI Russia -85% in 2022), sometimes completely wrong. The US invaded Iraq in 2003 — US equities were up 28.7% that year. The US attacked Kosovo in 1999 — US equities were up 19% that year. The attacking major power's equities often *benefit* from the defense spending stimulus and strategic resolution. The target's equities are destroyed.

**Misconception 3: Gold always rallies during wars**

Gold always *initially* rallies (average +2.3% in the 7 days after major geo events). But in wars that don't produce sustained inflation or currency debasement, gold gives back those gains within 3-6 months. Gold's sustained war premium only appears when the conflict drives actual monetary debasement (war finance through money creation) or structural commodity repricing (1973 oil shock feeding into broader inflation). A brief military conflict doesn't do either.

**Misconception 4: The dollar always strengthens in wars**

True for *most* geopolitical shocks — the dollar is the global safe haven by default. But there's a critical exception: when the crisis *originates from* or *implicates* the US itself. Ukraine 2022 briefly weakened the dollar versus the euro in the first 48 hours as markets priced in European energy inflation risk. Trump's Liberation Day tariffs in April 2025 actually weakened the dollar because the policy was seen as attacking the global dollar system from within.

**Misconception 5: Long conflicts always have persistent market impact**

Vietnam lasted 10 years and the equity impact was driven by inflation, not the length. The Cold War lasted 40 years and US equities compounded at 10% annualized throughout. Persistence of market impact is about supply disruption and inflation, not about conflict duration.

**Misconception 6: Wars in Europe always affect European markets more than US markets**

True in 2022: European STOXX 600 underperformed S&P 500 by 20 percentage points because European companies had the direct energy dependency. But in the Balkans wars of the 1990s, European equities were relatively unaffected (no energy dependency, no sanctions on European trade flows). Geography matters only when it translates into financial channel exposure.

## How It Shows Up in Real Markets: The Playbook Applied to Future Scenarios

Now let's apply everything we've learned to the four scenarios that markets watch most closely today.

### Scenario 1: Taiwan Strait Escalation

**The classification:** Gate 1 yes (China, a nuclear power, directly involved). Gate 4 yes (likely massive sanctions and technology decoupling). This is maximum regime-change territory.

**The specific transmission channels:**
- Taiwan produces approximately 92% of the world's most advanced semiconductor chips (TSMC 3nm/5nm nodes). A blockade or invasion that halted TSMC production would immediately crater every company that depends on leading-edge chips: Apple, NVIDIA, AMD, Qualcomm, and the AI infrastructure build-out entirely.
- A direct China-US military confrontation would trigger the largest asset decoupling event in history: US-listed Chinese stocks delisted, Chinese holdings of US Treasuries potentially weaponized, renminbi weaponized for regional trade.
- South Korea and Japan — major US allies with large manufacturing economies — would be directly in the crossfire of any conflict.

**The numbers behind the semiconductor supply shock:**
TSMC's Hsinchu and Tainan fabs process approximately 12 million wafer starts per year at leading-edge nodes. If that production halted for even 6 months, the global chip shortage of 2021-2022 — which shaved 0.5% off US GDP through auto production shutdowns alone — would look trivial by comparison. NVIDIA alone would lose approximately \$50-60 billion in annual revenue (based on FY2025 run rates). Apple's iPhone production would stall within 3-4 months as TSMC-produced A-series chips cannot be substituted.

Samsung (South Korea) produces roughly 15% of advanced logic capacity. If conflict spreads to Korea — which US military planners consider likely in a Taiwan scenario — global advanced chip supply could fall by 90%+ with no substitution available (Intel's foundry capacity is still limited; TSMC's Arizona fabs won't be at scale until 2026-2028). This supply shock would not resolve in months — rebuilding capacity takes 3-5 years.

**Market positioning:**
- Long: domestic semiconductor production (Intel fabs, US/EU foundry investments), defense (highest ever demand), LNG infrastructure (US energy to Asia)
- Short: anything in global tech hardware supply chain; EM Asia equities
- Safe haven: maximum USD + Treasuries + gold + JPY

**The statistical context:** No conflict of this type (nuclear-power-vs-nuclear-power's-core-interests) has occurred since 1945. The dataset gives us no close comparables. This is why Taiwan risk is categorically different from every other scenario: we have no playbook because it's never happened. The nearest historical analogy — the 1962 Cuban Missile Crisis — saw the Dow Jones fall 3.2% in the 13 days of peak tension before the Soviet ships turned back, then recover fully within 30 days. But that comparison understates the Taiwan scenario: the Cuban crisis involved no supply chain disruption, no allied sanctions packages, and no asset-class decoupling of the scale that China-US financial separation would require.

### Scenario 2: Iran-Israel Direct Escalation

**The classification:** Gate 2 yes (Iran = major oil producer and sits astride Strait of Hormuz). Gate 3 conditional (depends on whether Iran closes Hormuz or attacks Gulf infrastructure).

The April 2024 Iran-Israel direct exchange (Iran fired 300 drones and missiles; Israel retaliated) is the nearest data point: Brent rose 4% in the day before the attack, then fell back within a week as the exchange proved calibrated and non-escalatory. VIX spiked 14%, recovered in 7 days.

A full Iran-Israel war that draws US forces and threatens Hormuz: Brent would likely spike to \$120-\$150/bbl in the first month (20% of global supply at risk). Gold would rally 10-15%. EM oil importers (India, Turkey, South Korea, Japan) would see their currencies hit hard. The S&P would fall 8-15% in the first month, then recover as clarity on supply emerged.

The key number: every \$10 increase in sustained oil prices reduces global GDP by approximately 0.1-0.2%. A \$40 oil spike maintained for a year shaves 0.4-0.8% from global growth — significant but not catastrophic.

### Scenario 3: Korean Peninsula Flare-Up

**The classification:** Gate 1 potentially yes (North Korea has nuclear weapons; US forces stationed in South Korea). Gate 2 no (South Korea and North Korea have no significant oil production).

The historical data on Korean tension spikes (North Korean missile tests, artillery exchanges, submarine incidents) shows a consistent pattern: South Korean KOSPI falls 2-5% on serious incidents, recovers in 1-3 weeks. The market has become somewhat habituated to North Korean provocations.

A genuine military escalation with US forces engaged would be different: Japan and South Korea would see 20%+ equity declines (direct geographic exposure), semiconductor supply chains would be at risk (Samsung, SK Hynix in Korea; Toyota, Sony in Japan), and global markets would price in the nuclear risk premium.

The one distinctive feature of the Korea scenario: it would likely drive massive JPY appreciation (Japanese safe-haven + US Treasury buys as Japanese investors repatriate), potentially triggering another yen carry trade unwind similar to August 2024.

### Scenario 4: South China Sea and US-China Trade/Military Escalation

This is less a single event scenario and more a slow-moving risk that has been gradually repricing since 2018. The US-China trade war that began in 2018 and escalated through 2025 already represents a partial "geopolitical decoupling" — not a military conflict but with similar long-term asset repricing implications:

- Chinese tech stocks lost \$1 trillion in market cap during the 2021-2023 regulatory crackdown
- NVIDIA's China revenue fell from 26% to 8% of total over two years due to chip export controls
- Vietnam's electronics exports grew from \$36 billion to \$115 billion as manufacturing shifted from China

A military incident in the South China Sea — a standoff over a reef, a collision between navies — would not itself be regime-changing (neither side wants escalation over uninhabited rocks). But it would be a catalyst to accelerate the already-occurring decoupling trade: short Chinese tech (especially semiconductor supply chain), long Vietnamese and Indian manufacturing, long US defense.
