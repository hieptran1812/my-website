> **TL;DR**
>
> Lebanon pegged its currency (Lebanese pound, LBP) at 1,507 per USD from 1997 to 2019 — a 22-year fixed rate that became the central pillar of the country's financial identity. To defend the peg against chronic current account deficits, the central bank (Banque du Liban, BdL) ran a scheme economists now call a Ponzi: offering Lebanese commercial banks interest rates of 14–20% in USD to attract foreign deposits, recycling those dollars into government budget support, and booking fictitious "profits" to make the balance sheet look solvent. When dollar inflows reversed in late 2019 — triggered by a regional oil shock, declining remittances, and a spreading sense that the music was stopping — the scheme unraveled with extraordinary speed. Banks imposed a unilateral deposit freeze in October 2019. Lebanon defaulted on its Eurobonds in March 2020, the first sovereign default in Lebanese history. The LBP plunged from 1,507 to over 89,500 per USD by 2023 — a 98% devaluation. On August 4, 2020, an explosion at the port of Beirut killed 218 people and caused damage estimated at \$15 billion in a country whose GDP was only \$18 billion. And for 30 months — from the May 2022 elections through November 2023 — Lebanon had no president, no functioning government capable of passing reforms, and no IMF deal. The result: an entire middle class effectively expropriated. Bank deposits that existed in 2019 lost 80–95% of their real purchasing power. This post explains exactly how each piece — the peg, the Ponzi, the political system, the explosion, the vacuum — connected and why the disaster was visible in advance if you knew where to look.

October 17, 2019 was supposed to be an ordinary Thursday in Beirut. The Lebanese government had announced a tax on WhatsApp calls — a small levy on internet telephony — as part of a package to plug a budget gap that had festered for years. By nightfall, hundreds of thousands of Lebanese were in the streets in every major city, waving flags, blocking highways, and demanding the resignation of the entire political class. The WhatsApp tax was withdrawn within 24 hours. But the protests did not stop. They ran for weeks. The slogan was "Killun ya'ni killun" — "All of them means all of them."

By the first week of November 2019, Lebanese banks were quietly instructing branch managers to limit dollar withdrawals, refusing to process international wire transfers, and blocking customers who tried to move money abroad. There was no law authorizing these controls — no parliamentary vote, no cabinet decree. The banks simply stopped honoring their obligations unilaterally. Overnight, the life savings of hundreds of thousands of families were effectively frozen inside institutions that could not pay them back. The Lebanese pound on the parallel (black) market began to move away from the 22-year official peg of 1,507 per dollar — first to 2,000, then accelerating.

What followed over the next four years was one of the most severe economic collapses in modern peacetime history. The World Bank, in a 2021 report, classified Lebanon's crisis as one of the worst economic depressions globally since the mid-19th century. This post reconstructs the mechanics of that collapse: the financial engineering that made it inevitable, the political architecture that made reform impossible, and the cascading shocks — including a catastrophic port explosion — that turned a financial crisis into a civilizational rupture.

## Foundations: Currency Pegs, Ponzi Finance, and Political Debt

### What a currency peg actually is

A currency peg is a government's commitment to exchange its domestic currency for a foreign reference currency at a fixed rate. Lebanon pegged the Lebanese pound (LBP) to the US dollar at 1,507.5 LBP per USD in 1997, ending a period of hyperinflation that followed the 1975–1990 civil war. The peg was maintained by the Banque du Liban (BdL), which stood ready to buy or sell dollars at the fixed rate using its stock of foreign exchange (FX) reserves.

Pegs work as long as the central bank has enough FX reserves to honor the commitment. If the market suspects the peg will break — if private actors believe the central bank is running out of dollars — they rush to convert domestic currency before the devaluation happens, which accelerates the very reserve drain they fear. This self-fulfilling dynamic is called a currency attack or speculative attack, and it is why pegs either hold completely or collapse dramatically.

The structural problem with Lebanon's peg was that the country ran chronic current account deficits — it imported far more than it exported. A current account deficit means the country must attract foreign capital to finance the gap. For Lebanon, that capital came almost entirely from the Lebanese diaspora: Lebanese emigrants living in the Gulf, West Africa, the Americas, and Europe who sent remittances home and deposited dollar savings in Lebanese banks attracted by high interest rates.

### Ponzi finance: the Minsky framework

Economist Hyman Minsky categorized borrowers into three types: hedge borrowers (can repay principal and interest from cash flows), speculative borrowers (can pay interest but must roll over principal), and Ponzi borrowers (cannot pay either from cash flows — they depend on rising asset prices or new borrowing to service old debt). Lebanon's BdL had become a Ponzi institution in Minsky's framework by the mid-2010s.

The mechanics: BdL offered Lebanese commercial banks interest rates of 14–20% on dollar deposits placed at the central bank. This was far above global market rates of 1–3%. To fund these payments, BdL received dollar transfers from commercial banks, lent dollars to the government at rates of ~10%, and booked the difference as a profit — while using new deposit inflows to pay interest on old deposits. The scheme generated positive cash flow only as long as fresh dollar inflows exceeded outflows. When remittances and new deposits decelerated, the math turned against BdL with no mechanism to recover.

Crucially, BdL's balance sheet showed these transactions in ways that obscured the underlying insolvency. The central bank recorded the interest payments it owed to commercial banks as assets rather than liabilities in some accounting treatments — a practice the IMF later described as "significant weaknesses in the BdL's accounting framework." When analysts unwound the actual net reserve position (subtracting mandatory reserve requirements that banks held at BdL and were legally untouchable), the true usable reserve buffer was far smaller than headline gross reserve figures suggested.

### Political debt: a concept specific to Lebanon

Political debt differs from ordinary sovereign debt in one crucial way: ordinary sovereign debt can theoretically be restructured through negotiation, because the debtor country is a unified legal entity with a government capable of making binding commitments. Political debt is the accumulation of obligations that cannot be restructured because the political system that incurred the debt is itself insolvent — because the entities that would need to agree on reform each benefit from the status quo and each have veto power over change.

Lebanon's Ta'if Agreement (1989), which ended the civil war, institutionalized power-sharing across 18 recognized religious sects. Every major state institution was assigned to a specific sect — the President is Maronite Christian, the Prime Minister is Sunni Muslim, the Speaker of Parliament is Shia Muslim. Every ministry, every senior civil service position, every military command position follows sectarian allocation rules. This system, called confessionalism, created 18 competing patronage networks. Each sect's political elite controlled access to state resources — jobs, contracts, licenses, subsidies — as the basis of their political power. Reforming the state meant threatening those resources, which meant threatening the political survival of every major political actor simultaneously. The result was that Lebanon accumulated fiscal deficits, central bank losses, and banking system liabilities for decades without any reform mechanism capable of resolving them.

## The Political Moves

### Hezbollah and the veto over the state

Hezbollah — the Shia political and military organization designated as a terrorist organization by the United States, European Union, and Arab League — is not a peripheral actor in Lebanese politics. It is the dominant force in the country's largest sectarian community and commands an armed wing with military capabilities exceeding those of the Lebanese Armed Forces in certain dimensions. Hezbollah holds seats in parliament, participates in government, and controls — directly or through its ally Amal — the Shia parliamentary bloc of approximately 27 seats.

Hezbollah's economic interests cut directly against the reforms Lebanon needed. A capital controls law would impose transparency on financial flows, including the parallel economic networks Hezbollah operates. An IMF program would require fiscal consolidation, which would expose the patronage spending that Hezbollah directs to its constituencies in the south and the Bekaa Valley. An independent judiciary — a standard IMF governance condition — would threaten Hezbollah members and allies who operate outside normal legal constraints. Every time an IMF deal got close to completion, Hezbollah and its allies found reasons to stall, change the terms, or trigger a political crisis that dissolved the negotiating government.

### The 2022 elections and presidential vacuum

Lebanon held parliamentary elections in May 2022. The elections produced a fragmented result: Hezbollah and Amal lost their parliamentary majority for the first time in years, "change" candidates affiliated with the 2019 protest movement won 13 seats, and traditional parties — the Free Patriotic Movement (FPM), the Lebanese Forces, Future Movement — held reduced blocs. No single group commanded a majority.

Electing a president in Lebanon requires a two-thirds quorum in the 128-seat parliament to begin the session, and an absolute majority (65 votes) for the first round, dropping to a simple majority in subsequent rounds. Any bloc that refused to attend the session could prevent the vote. Over the following 26 months, parliament met 12 times to elect a president. Each session failed — sometimes because Hezbollah and FPM stayed away, sometimes because the Christian blocs could not agree on a candidate, sometimes because external actors (Saudi Arabia, Iran, France) had competing preferences. Lebanon was without a president from October 31, 2022 until Joseph Aoun was elected in January 2025, a 26-month vacuum that left the country without anyone legally empowered to sign executive decrees, appoint ministers, or ratify international agreements.

### IMF stalemate: why the deal never closed

Lebanon began formal IMF negotiations in May 2020. By April 2022, Lebanon and the IMF reached a staff-level agreement — a preliminary framework for a program worth approximately \$3 billion. But the agreement required prior actions: passing a capital controls law, completing a bank resolution framework, unifying the exchange rate, and auditing BdL's accounts (BdL had not undergone an external audit in years). None of these prior actions were ever completed.

The capital controls law was drafted and re-drafted multiple times but blocked by the banking lobby, which feared that any formal law would also require formalizing the deposit losses — converting implicit expropriation into legally explicit haircuts that depositors could contest in court. The bank resolution framework required deciding how to allocate losses among shareholders, depositors, and the state — a decision that implicated the political class because many senior politicians had significant bank deposits and some owned stakes in banks. The BdL audit, led by external firm Alvarez & Marsal, was terminated in 2020 when BdL refused to provide full access to its records. By 2023, Lebanon still had no formal IMF program. Without a program, it could not access multilateral financing or unlock the \$11 billion in international reconstruction pledges made after the port explosion.

## The Financial Channels

### How BdL engineered the Ponzi in practice

The Banque du Liban's "financial engineering" operations — a term BdL Governor Riad Salameh used with considerable pride — worked through a series of transactions designed to attract dollars into the Lebanese banking system and park them at BdL. The mechanics evolved through several phases.

**Phase 1 (1997–2008): Standard peg defense.** BdL maintained the peg through conventional reserve management. Lebanon ran current account deficits but diaspora remittances, tourism, and some real estate investment covered most of the gap. Lebanese banks offered moderately high dollar rates (5–8%) that were above global rates but financially sustainable.

**Phase 2 (2009–2016): Engineering acceleration.** As regional instability (Syrian war, declining Gulf investment) reduced organic dollar inflows, BdL began offering structured operations to commercial banks. A typical operation: BdL would issue certificates of deposit (CDs) to commercial banks in Lebanese pounds, pay Lebanese pound interest on those CDs at 10–12%, and simultaneously accept dollar deposits from the same banks at 16–20% USD rates. The bank's dollar deposit at BdL counted as a liquid, high-quality asset on the bank's balance sheet. BdL counted the dollar inflow as gross FX reserves. The net effect was a circular flow that increased both BdL's gross reserves and the banking system's nominal assets without creating any productive economic activity or increasing Lebanon's capacity to service future obligations.

**Phase 3 (2016–2019): Reserve deception.** By 2016, BdL's net reserve position — gross reserves minus obligations to commercial banks — had turned negative or marginally positive depending on which liabilities were counted. To avoid triggering market concern, BdL continued to report gross reserves prominently while not prominently disclosing the offsetting liabilities. The IMF's 2019 Article IV consultation warned explicitly that "large gross FX reserves conceal a substantially lower net FX position" and that BdL's operations "constitute quasi-fiscal activities that generate large financial risks." These warnings were public. They did not trigger market action because Lebanon's Eurobonds still traded at relatively tight spreads and the banking system was paying depositors on time.

**The collapse trigger:** In 2019, multiple factors converged. The regional economy softened, depressing Gulf-based Lebanese remittances. The political risk premium on Lebanese assets widened following credit rating downgrades by Moody's (October 2018) and S&P (January 2019). Confidence-sensitive deposit inflows decelerated. BdL's gross reserves began declining from their \$45 billion peak. Commercial banks, sensing growing risk, began quietly reducing their exposure to BdL certificates of deposit — the exact opposite of the inflow BdL needed. Once the financial engineering machine required new deposits to pay old ones and new deposits were slowing, the arithmetic of collapse was locked in.

### Lollars: the trapped money concept

The bank freeze created a monetary phenomenon with no precise historical precedent: the "lollar" (Lebanese dollar). A lollar is a dollar-denominated deposit in a Lebanese bank that cannot be withdrawn in cash dollars and cannot be transferred abroad at the dollar's face value. In theory, your bank statement shows \$50,000. In practice, you cannot touch \$50,000. You can withdraw a small amount per month in physical dollars (each bank sets its own cap, typically \$200–400/month). You can receive LBP at the official rate (1,507 LBP per lollar) — which was worth far less than the parallel market rate for real dollars. Or you can use your lollar balance to pay bills within Lebanon at the official rate.

The lollar discount — the gap between the face value of bank deposits and the real dollar value you could access — widened as the crisis deepened. By 2022, a lollar was worth perhaps 5–10 cents on the dollar in terms of real purchasing power. The entire Lebanese banking sector had become insolvent: its dollar liabilities (deposits) exceeded its real dollar assets. But because no law required marking these liabilities to market and no resolution framework existed, the insolvency was acknowledged by everyone and formally recognized by no one.

### Deposit haircuts and the legal void

In a normal banking crisis, a resolution framework — like the FDIC process in the US, or the bail-in mechanism in EU bank resolution — determines how losses are allocated among creditors in a legally binding way. Depositors may receive a government guarantee up to a threshold (\$250,000 per depositor in the US), and losses above that threshold are shared with other creditors according to a defined hierarchy. Lebanon has none of this. The Lebanese deposit insurance scheme, Deposit Guarantee Corporation (DGC), was funded to cover only approximately \$75 million total — adequate for normal bank failures, laughably insufficient for a system with \$100+ billion in deposits.

Lebanon's banks imposed the freeze unilaterally, relying on force majeure and emergency terms buried in account agreements. Depositors who sued — and many did — won court cases ordering their banks to return deposits. The banks simply did not comply, and the Lebanese judicial system had no enforcement mechanism capable of compelling them. By 2023, there were over 20,000 pending cases of depositors suing their banks in Lebanese courts, and a growing number of Ponzi-style confrontations where depositors — sometimes armed — physically entered bank branches and demanded their money, in some cases succeeding in withdrawing funds by threatening staff. These scenes, captured on video and widely circulated internationally, illustrated the complete breakdown of legal financial order.

#### Worked example: BdL Ponzi mechanics

Suppose BdL offers a Lebanese commercial bank a 16% annual USD return on a 1-year deposit of \$100 million. The bank accepts — it's an attractive return compared to 1–2% on US Treasuries. BdL now has \$100 million more in gross FX reserves. BdL simultaneously lends the Lebanese government \$100 million (or buys Lebanese Eurobonds) at 10% annual interest. At year end:

- BdL owes the bank: \$100M × 16% = \$16 million interest
- BdL receives from government: \$100M × 10% = \$10 million interest
- Net cash loss for BdL: \$6 million

How does BdL fund the \$6 million gap? It attracts another dollar deposit — say, \$106 million from a new round of financial engineering. The gross reserves increase by \$6 million even though BdL has become \$6 million poorer in net terms. This works indefinitely as long as new deposits keep arriving. But if new deposits slow — as happened in 2019 — BdL must pay \$16 million from existing reserves while receiving only \$10 million from the government, drawing down the reserve buffer by \$6 million per \$100 million of such operations. Across a portfolio of \$60–70 billion in such operations (the estimated scale by 2019), the annual reserve drain at this rate would be \$3.6–4.2 billion per year — consistent with the observed reserve decline from \$45 billion in 2018 to \$38 billion by mid-2019.

#### Worked example: What happened to a depositor's USD 50,000?

A Lebanese professional — a doctor, an engineer, a small business owner — had accumulated \$50,000 in a bank savings account by September 2019. This was a realistic sum for a middle-class household that had saved diligently for 10–15 years. Here is what happened to that money, step by step:

**October 2019:** Bank imposes informal withdrawal caps. You can take out \$200/week in physical dollars. At that rate, it would take 48 years to withdraw the full \$50,000 — clearly not a viable plan.

**November 2019:** You try to wire \$5,000 to a relative abroad. The bank informs you that international transfers require "prior approval" that is effectively never granted for capital transfers.

**March 2020:** Lebanon defaults on its Eurobonds. Your bank begins offering "fresh dollar" conversions: you can receive your deposit in LBP at the official rate of 1,507 LBP per dollar. At that rate, \$50,000 becomes 75,350,000 LBP. The parallel market rate is now 3,000 LBP per dollar, so those 75.35 million LBP are worth approximately \$25,000 in real purchasing power — a 50% haircut on day one.

**June 2021:** The parallel market rate reaches 15,000 LBP per dollar. If you had accepted LBP at the official rate in March 2020, your 75.35 million LBP are now worth \$5,023 — a 90% loss from the original \$50,000. If you kept the account in "lollar" form without converting, the lollar discount means you still cannot access real dollars, and if you could sell the deposit obligation at market prices, it would fetch approximately 5–10 cents on the dollar.

**2023:** The Lebanese government and BdL formally unify the exchange rate at approximately 89,500 LBP per dollar. Banks begin allowing some conversions at this rate. If you now receive your \$50,000 deposit in LBP at the unified rate, you receive 4,475,000,000 LBP (4.475 billion LBP). At the market rate of 89,500, this is back to nominally \$50,000 — but this is circular. The rate was set to equal the parallel market rate, so it's not a recovery; it's a formal acknowledgment that your deposit is worth what the parallel market said it was worth (approximately \$50,000 face value but with enormous uncertainty about whether you can actually spend it on goods and services). Your real purchasing power, relative to the pre-crisis international dollar, has still been devastated by inflation within Lebanon that occurred at the parallel rate for years.

The realistic outcome for most depositors: losses of 75–95% of real pre-crisis purchasing power, with the exact amount depending on when they accepted LBP conversions, whether they held physical dollars outside the banking system, and whether they had access to foreign accounts.

## Deposit Confiscation Mechanics: The Legal Architecture of Theft

### How the freeze was legally constructed

Lebanese bank account agreements — like those in most countries — contain force majeure clauses allowing banks to suspend operations in extraordinary circumstances. Lebanese banks in late 2019 relied on these clauses, plus communications from the central bank and banking associations suggesting that capital controls were necessary for "systemic stability." The trick was that the freeze was never formalized in law. A formal capital controls law would have required specifying terms — allowed withdrawal amounts, duration, priority rules — that would also create legal rights for depositors to enforce. By operating informally, banks preserved maximum flexibility but also maximum legal ambiguity.

The Banking Control Commission of Lebanon (BCCL), the regulator, did not formally order the freeze either. The central bank issued a series of circulars (memoranda with regulatory force) governing certain FX transactions, but these were always framed as prudential measures rather than deposit controls. The resulting legal gray zone was intentional: it allowed all parties to maintain the fiction that deposits were accessible in principle while being inaccessible in practice.

### Multiple exchange rate tiers: a taxonomy

By 2021, Lebanon had at least four distinct "dollar" prices operating simultaneously:

1. **Official rate (1,507 LBP/USD):** Used for BdL debt servicing calculations, some legacy contracts, and government accounting. Disconnected from reality.

2. **BdL Sayrafa platform rate (8,000–25,000 LBP/USD over 2021–2022):** A BdL-operated platform that provided a "managed" rate for certain transactions, subsidized by burning FX reserves. Used for some import subsidies (fuel, wheat, medicine).

3. **Parallel (black) market rate (10,000–90,000 LBP/USD over 2020–2023):** The actual price for physical dollar exchange on the street. This was the price that mattered for daily commerce and wages.

4. **Lollar rate:** The effective dollar value of a bank deposit dollar — approximately 5–15 cents on the dollar in real terms during 2021–2022.

This four-tier monetary system created enormous arbitrage opportunities for those with political connections who could access subsidized rates and sell at parallel market rates, and devastating losses for ordinary citizens who received wages in LBP at the parallel rate while facing LBP prices that tracked the parallel rate.

#### Worked example: Purchasing power destruction of LBP savings

Suppose you held 100,000,000 LBP in cash (not in a bank) in September 2019. At the pegged rate of 1,507 LBP per dollar, this was worth \$66,357. This was a substantial sum — the equivalent of multiple years of a middle-class salary for many Lebanese.

By June 2023, with the parallel/market rate at approximately 89,500 LBP per dollar, those same 100,000,000 LBP are worth: 100,000,000 ÷ 89,500 = **\$1,117**.

That is a loss of \$65,240 — or **98.3% of the original dollar value** — in four years, holding physical cash in the national currency. No bank run, no fraud, no investment loss: purely the consequence of holding the domestic currency through a peg collapse and hyperinflation.

For context, Lebanon's annual inflation rate reached 211% in 2022 according to the IMF. Workers receiving LBP-denominated salaries saw their real wages collapse by 90%+ even as nominal wages remained nominally the same. The minimum wage in Lebanon was set at 675,000 LBP per month — worth approximately \$450 at the pre-crisis rate, but only \$7.50 at the 2022 parallel market rate.

## The Port Explosion: A Civilizational Shock

### What happened on August 4, 2020

At 6:07 PM local time on August 4, 2020, 2,750 tonnes of ammonium nitrate — impounded from an abandoned cargo ship in 2013 and stored in Hangar 12 of the port of Beirut for six years with no adequate safety measures — detonated in one of the largest non-nuclear explosions in recorded history. The explosion was detected on seismographs across the region, registering as a 3.3-magnitude earthquake. The pressure wave shattered windows up to 25 kilometers away and was heard in Cyprus, 240 kilometers distant.

The immediate human toll: 218 people killed, approximately 6,500 injured, and 300,000 people displaced from their homes. The port of Beirut — through which approximately 60% of the country's imports and 85% of its grain imports were processed — was destroyed. The adjacent neighborhood of Mar Mikhael, a cultural and commercial center, was leveled.

The World Bank's rapid damage assessment, published in August 2020, estimated total losses at \$3.8–4.6 billion in direct physical damage, with the broader economic impact including business disruption, healthcare costs, and reconstruction needs bringing the total to approximately \$15 billion. Lebanon's GDP in 2020 was approximately \$18.1 billion. The explosion had inflicted damage equivalent to **83% of annual GDP** in a single afternoon.

### Fiscal and political transmission

The explosion did not just kill people and destroy infrastructure. It detonated the fragile political equilibrium that had allowed Lebanon to kick the reform can down the road.

Prime Minister Hassan Diab's government — itself a technocratic caretaker installed after the 2019 protests — resigned on August 10, 2020, six days after the blast. Designating a new prime minister required sectarian consultations. Former Prime Minister Saad Hariri was designated in October 2020, then withdrew in July 2021 after failing to form a government. Najib Mikati eventually formed a government in September 2021 — 13 months after Diab resigned.

The political vacuum during those 13 months meant Lebanon had no government capable of signing the IMF framework agreement. The \$11 billion in international reconstruction aid pledged at the CEDRE conference in 2018 and reaffirmed after the explosion was conditioned on governance reforms Lebanon never passed. The explosion thus had a compounding fiscal effect: it destroyed \$15 billion of economic capacity, eliminated any short-term path to reconstruction finance, and consumed whatever residual political energy might have gone toward an IMF deal.

#### Worked example: Port explosion fiscal shock ratio

Lebanon's GDP in 2020, before the explosion: approximately \$18.1 billion (World Bank). The explosion occurred before the GDP figures for that year were fully compiled, so we use the pre-explosion estimate.

World Bank total economic loss estimate: \$15 billion.

Shock ratio = Economic loss ÷ GDP = \$15B ÷ \$18.1B = **0.83, or 83% of annual GDP**.

For comparison:
- Hurricane Katrina (2005): approximately \$125 billion damage on a US GDP of \$13 trillion = **0.96% of GDP**
- 2011 Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami: approximately \$235 billion damage on Japan GDP of \$5.9 trillion = **3.9% of GDP**
- 1994 Northridge earthquake: approximately \$50 billion on US GDP of \$7 trillion = **0.71% of GDP**

Lebanon's port explosion, relative to GDP, was approximately **21 times more severe** than Hurricane Katrina's impact on the US economy and approximately **21 times more severe** than the Northridge earthquake's impact. The only comparable modern events in terms of GDP ratio are wartime destructions — which is fitting, given that the explosion was caused by state negligence of arms-related material.

Furthermore: Lebanon's remaining BdL reserves at the time of the explosion were approximately \$30 billion in gross terms. The reconstruction need of \$15 billion therefore represented half of gross reserves — but net usable reserves (excluding mandatory reserve requirements and liabilities to commercial banks) were already far smaller. Lebanon had no realistic domestic financing capacity for reconstruction. The only viable path was international aid, which was conditional on reforms that the political system was constitutionally incapable of delivering.

### The ammonium nitrate: state negligence documented

The 2,750 tonnes of ammonium nitrate had been sitting in Hangar 12 since 2013, when Lebanese customs authorities impounded it from the MV Rhosus, a Moldovan-flagged vessel that had declared bankruptcy in Beirut port. At least ten letters were sent by port and customs officials to Lebanese judges requesting authorization to re-export or dispose of the material, warning of its danger. The requests were ignored. No judge acted. No minister ordered disposal. The material sat in a port shed, improperly stored, for six years, because the Lebanese political system had no mechanism by which a routine safety problem — one that required a decision rather than patronage allocation — could be resolved.

This is the essence of what political debt means in practice. The state had incurred a physical obligation (safe disposal of hazardous material) that should have been easy to fulfill, but could not fulfill it because the sectarian patronage logic governing every Lebanese institution meant that no actor had both the authority and the incentive to act. The result was 218 deaths and a \$15 billion destruction event.

## The Presidential Vacuum: 30 Months Without a Head of State

Lebanon's president is elected by the 128-member parliament. The Constitution requires that the parliament hold an election session within one month before the expiration of the presidential term. If no candidate receives a two-thirds majority in the first round, subsequent rounds require only an absolute majority (65 votes). However, holding the session itself requires a two-thirds quorum — 86 members must be present. Any bloc can prevent the session by staying away.

President Michel Aoun's term expired on October 31, 2022. Lebanon subsequently held 12 failed parliamentary sessions to elect a president between November 2022 and late 2023, a period that stretched to 26 months before Joseph Aoun (head of the Lebanese Armed Forces, no relation to Michel Aoun) was elected in January 2025. During this period:

- No president existed to countersign government decisions or ratify international agreements
- The caretaker government of PM Mikati had limited legal authority — caretaker governments are constitutionally barred from major policy decisions
- The IMF staff-level agreement, reached in April 2022, required a functioning government to complete prior actions; no such government existed
- Lebanon could not formally apply for World Bank or other multilateral reconstruction financing without a government authorized to sign binding agreements
- The Lebanese Central Bank governor position also turned over during this period: Riad Salameh, who had governed BdL since 1993 (30 years), was not reappointed when his term ended in July 2023, facing money laundering investigations in multiple European jurisdictions

The presidential vacuum was not an anomaly or an accident. It was a predictable outcome of Lebanon's confessional political structure applied to a situation where the stakes of the presidency — control over the resolution of the banking crisis, IMF negotiations, post-Hezbollah political realignment — were so high that no major bloc would accept a candidate chosen by another.

## Early Warning Signals: What You Could Have Seen

### BdL reserve data as the primary indicator

The key signal was in BdL's published gross foreign reserve data, combined with knowledge of the offsetting liabilities. BdL published monthly balance sheets that showed gross FX reserves prominently. What required more work was estimating the liabilities against those reserves — specifically, the dollar-denominated obligations BdL had incurred through financial engineering operations.

Analysts at the Institute of International Finance (IIF), Capital Economics, and several independent Lebanese economists (notably Jad Chaaban at the American University of Beirut) had published calculations showing that by 2018–2019, BdL's net FX position — gross reserves minus dollar obligations to commercial banks — was either marginally positive or negative, depending on assumptions about the classification of mandatory reserve requirements. The IMF's July 2019 Article IV report (published with unusual directness for an Article IV) stated explicitly: "staff assess that BdL's current net FX position is substantially lower than gross FX reserves suggest."

The reserve depletion rate was the second signal. From their \$45 billion peak in 2018, gross reserves fell to \$42 billion in early 2019 and \$38 billion by mid-2019 — a \$7 billion decline in 18 months, representing an accelerating drain. At that rate, gross reserves would fall below \$20 billion by 2021 — a level at which the peg would become untenable even if the financial engineering was still running.

### Eurobond spread as a contemporaneous indicator

Lebanon's dollar-denominated Eurobonds are traded on international markets and their yield spreads over US Treasuries reflect market assessments of default risk. The sequence of spread movements tells the crisis story in real time:

- January 2019: Spreads widen sharply following S&P downgrade to CCC+
- April 2019: 5-year Eurobond yield reaches ~12% (equivalent to ~900 basis points spread over US Treasuries)
- September 2019: Spreads exceed 1,500 basis points — deep distressed territory
- January 2020: Trading at 30–40 cents on the dollar, pricing near-certain default
- March 2020: Default

An investor watching Eurobond spreads had approximately 12 months between the S&P downgrade and the formal default to exit Lebanese sovereign exposure. The parallel market premium on the LBP (the gap between the official 1,507 rate and parallel market rates) provided additional early warning from October 2019 onward.

## The Deposit Haircut in Institutional Context

### How other countries handled similar crises differently

Lebanon's deposit freeze is often compared to Cyprus's 2012–2013 bank bail-in, the Argentine 2001 "corralito" account freeze, and Iceland's 2008 banking collapse. The comparison is instructive because it reveals what Lebanon did differently — and worse.

**Cyprus (2012–2013):** Cyprus's banking sector had also grown to approximately 550% of GDP through large inflows from Russian and other offshore depositors attracted by high interest rates. When Cypriot banks' exposure to Greek sovereign debt triggered a crisis, the EU and IMF negotiated a resolution. The agreement imposed a one-time haircut of approximately 47.5% on deposits above EUR 100,000 (deposits below that threshold were protected). The haircut was formally legislated, applied transparently, and implemented over a defined timeline. The Cypriot banking system reopened (with capital controls) within three weeks. By 2016, the capital controls were fully lifted. The damage was severe — depositors above the threshold lost nearly half their money — but the legal structure created certainty, allowing economic activity to restart.

**Argentina (2001–2002):** The Argentine "corralito" (little fence) froze bank deposits in December 2001 as the country's currency board peg (1:1 peso to dollar) collapsed. Argentina initially imposed a formal bank freeze through legislative decree, then converted dollar deposits to pesos at the old 1:1 rate before devaluing the peso, effectively taking approximately 30% of dollar depositors' wealth immediately. The process was chaotic and legally contested, but Argentina had a functioning government capable of making decisions, passing laws, and eventually completing a debt restructuring with private creditors by 2005. The economy recovered to pre-crisis growth levels within four years.

**Lebanon (2019–present):** No formal haircut. No legal structure. No government decision. No defined timeline. A banking system that has been zombie-operational for five years — technically open, processing transactions, but unable to honor its core obligation of returning deposits on demand. This is the worst possible resolution path: the losses are real and enormous, but because they are never formally acknowledged, the legal system cannot resolve claims, the banking system cannot restart, and new lending and investment cannot occur because no one trusts that deposits placed today will be accessible tomorrow.

The lesson for policymakers and investors: informal deposit freezes without legal resolution frameworks impose costs far greater than the immediate financial losses. They destroy the institutional credibility of the entire banking system for a generation.

### The role of dollarization in Lebanon's fragility

Lebanon's economy was heavily dollarized well before the crisis — meaning that large portions of private contracts, wages, and commercial transactions were denominated in USD rather than in LBP. Approximately 70–80% of bank deposits were in foreign currency (primarily USD) rather than LBP. This dollarization was a rational response to Lebanon's inflationary history and reflected depositors' distrust of the domestic currency.

The irony: dollarization, which existed to protect depositors from LBP devaluation risk, ultimately concentrated the banking system's vulnerability. If deposits had been in LBP, a devaluation would have reduced the real value of bank liabilities automatically, potentially recapitalizing banks through inflation. Because deposits were in USD, devaluation of the LBP did not automatically reduce bank liabilities — it only made the USD liabilities larger relative to the LBP-denominated revenues the banks could collect from domestic borrowers. The banking system was squeezed from both sides: USD liabilities they could not honor, and LBP-denominated local assets worth progressively less in USD terms.

This dollarization trap is relevant for any analysis of other heavily dollarized emerging market banking systems — Ecuador, El Salvador, Panama (fully dollarized), or partially dollarized systems like Cambodia or Zimbabwe. Full dollarization (no domestic currency at all) eliminates devaluation risk but also eliminates the inflation escape valve. Lebanon had the worst of both worlds: a domestic currency that was pegged to the dollar (providing no automatic stabilizer) while the banking system was almost entirely dollarized (concentrating all currency risk in bank balance sheets rather than spreading it across the economy through gradual inflation).

#### Worked example: Lollar arbitrage and the two-tier market

By mid-2021, with the parallel market at approximately 15,000 LBP per dollar and the official BdL Sayrafa platform rate at approximately 12,000 LBP per dollar (and the still-official-on-paper rate at 1,507), a significant arbitrage existed for anyone with access to both systems.

Suppose a Lebanese business owner had \$10,000 in a "lollar" bank deposit (face value \$10,000 but effectively inaccessible as real dollars). Their bank allowed them to buy fuel subsidies using their lollar balance at the official rate of 1,507 LBP per dollar. They spent \$1,000 of lollars to buy fuel worth 1,507,000 LBP at official prices. They then sold that fuel on the open market at the parallel market price equivalent — say, 15,000 LBP per liter when official price implied 1,507 LBP per liter. Effective gain: approximately 10x per unit of lollar spent on subsidized fuel.

This arbitrage — converting frozen lollar deposits into subsidized goods and reselling at parallel prices — became a significant industry. BdL was essentially paying for this arbitrage with its dwindling FX reserves, as it used real dollars to import subsidized goods priced in artificial official-rate LBP. By the time BdL ended fuel subsidies in September 2021, the central bank had burned through approximately \$4–5 billion in reserves subsidizing fuel, wheat, and medicine imports at the official exchange rate while parallel market prices were 5–15x higher. This subsidy program transferred enormous wealth to those with political connections and access to subsidized goods, while the cost was borne by BdL's reserve depletion — meaning by all future Lebanese citizens who would receive a more depleted reserve base in any eventual stabilization program.

## Common Misconceptions

### "The civil war caused this"

Lebanon's 1975–1990 civil war was devastating and imposed enormous long-run costs. But the specific financial collapse of 2019–2023 is analytically distinct from civil war legacy. The financial crisis was built on decisions made specifically in the 1990s and 2000s: the choice to maintain a fixed peg requiring large dollar inflows, the decision to finance government deficits through BdL rather than structural reform, the acceleration of financial engineering operations in 2016–2019. Countries with similar civil war histories have not generated comparable banking crises. The civil war is not a sufficient explanation.

### "It was Hezbollah's fault"

Hezbollah is one veto player in a system of multiple veto players. The banking sector's lobby — representing Maronite and Sunni business elites — was equally committed to preserving the existing system because bank shareholders had extracted enormous profits from the Ponzi and would face losses in any resolution. The Free Patriotic Movement, a Maronite Christian party aligned with Hezbollah on some issues, protected BdL Governor Salameh for years. Allocating the blame to a single actor understates the systemic nature of the failure. The Ta'if confessional structure gave every major actor a veto over change, and every major actor used it.

### "Deposits are gone forever"

Deposits have not literally disappeared — the legal obligations exist, and Lebanese courts continue to recognize them. What has happened is that the real purchasing power of those deposits has been largely destroyed through devaluation and inflation. Some recovery scenarios exist: if Lebanon completes an IMF program, receives reconstruction financing, and implements a banking sector resolution, depositors could receive some recovery value — potentially 15–30 cents on the dollar according to various estimates. This is not a recovery of losses but it is not zero. The trajectory as of 2025 remained uncertain given continued political fragility.

### "The port explosion was the cause of the crisis"

The explosion was a massive accelerant, not the origin. By August 2020, the peg had already broken (parallel rate at \$4,000), Lebanon had already defaulted (March 2020), and the bank freeze had already been running for nine months. The financial crisis was fully underway before August 4. What the explosion did was eliminate the emergency political will that sometimes appears after a crisis, destroyed physical infrastructure needed for recovery, and made international rescue financing conditional on political reforms Lebanon remained incapable of enacting.

## How It Shows Up in Real Markets

### Eurobond distressed trading

Lebanon's Eurobonds were restructuring candidates from early 2020. By mid-2020, they were trading at 12–18 cents on the dollar — pricing in a severe haircut. Distressed debt investors who specialize in sovereign restructurings analyzed the situation and largely avoided Lebanon because the restructuring timeline was unknown (no functioning government to negotiate with), the IMF program was stalled, and the legal framework for a restructuring agreement was unclear given the constitutional vacuum.

For those who did hold Lebanese Eurobonds into the 2020s, the investment became effectively a bet on political resolution — specifically, on whether a functioning government would eventually emerge, complete an IMF deal, and implement a bank restructuring that included Eurobond holders in the recovery value. As of 2025, this resolution had not occurred, and bonds were still trading well below par.

### Currency and remittance impacts

The LBP's collapse affected regional remittance flows. Lebanese diaspora members, who had previously sent money home in dollars to take advantage of the high interest rates, reversed the flow. Some diaspora members began receiving requests for support from family members whose savings were frozen. The net remittance direction for some families shifted from Lebanon-to-diaspora to diaspora-to-Lebanon — the opposite of the flow that had financed the Ponzi.

For traders: the parallel market LBP rate became a leading indicator of political sentiment. Periods when political negotiations seemed to progress (e.g., following the April 2022 IMF staff-level agreement) saw some LBP stabilization at 25,000–30,000. Periods of political breakdown saw the rate push toward 90,000+. The rate's volatility reflected the market's assessment of whether Lebanon would ever complete a reform program.

### Regional financial contagion: limited but present

Lebanon's banking sector had cross-border exposures, particularly to other Levantine markets and the Gulf. Some Gulf-based Lebanese banks or Lebanese bank subsidiaries faced reputational and liquidity pressure. However, Lebanon's financial collapse did not generate significant regional contagion in the sense of triggering bank runs in neighboring countries — the Lebanese banking system, while large relative to Lebanon's GDP (bank assets were approximately 400% of GDP), was small in absolute terms relative to regional financial markets.

The more significant regional effect was geopolitical: the collapse of a functioning Lebanese state created a power vacuum that Hezbollah — backed by Iran — partly filled, altering the regional balance in ways that affected Israeli security calculations and ultimately contributed to the 2023–2024 Israeli-Hezbollah military confrontations in southern Lebanon.

## How to Trade It: The Playbook

### Entry frameworks for distressed sovereign situations

Lebanon illustrates the key framework for trading distressed sovereign debt: **the resolution timeline is the dominant variable**. Eurobond prices in a default situation reflect the market's estimate of (recovery value × probability of resolution) discounted by the time until resolution. When resolution is uncertain — as in Lebanon, where a 26-month presidential vacuum followed by continued political fragility meant no clear reform timeline — discounting the recovery value heavily is correct.

The practical implication: distressed sovereign bonds in political-paralysis-driven collapses trade at steeper discounts than those in pure fiscal crises with functioning governments. Lebanon's Eurobonds at 12–18 cents in 2020–2022 were not necessarily cheap, given that the recovery timeline was indefinite. Argentina's bonds in similar distress situations (2001, 2020) restructured within 2–3 years because Argentina had functioning political institutions capable of reaching agreements. Lebanon lacked this capacity.

**Rule 1: Political resolution capacity is a prerequisite for sovereign distressed recovery.** If the political system cannot make binding commitments — as in Lebanon's confessional paralysis — do not anchor on face-value recovery expectations.

**Rule 2: BdL reserve data is the leading indicator for currency peg sustainability.** Monitor gross reserves, estimate net reserves (subtract mandatory reserve requirements and financial engineering liabilities from public filings), and compute the reserve depletion rate. When net reserves approach zero and the depletion rate is accelerating, peg collapse is a matter of when, not if.

**Rule 3: Parallel market premiums are the market signal.** When the official exchange rate diverges significantly from the parallel market rate, the parallel market is pricing the true equilibrium. A 20%+ parallel premium in a country with capital controls is a strong peg-collapse signal. Lebanon's parallel premium went from zero to 30% within weeks of the October 2019 protests.

**Rule 4: Multi-party veto systems produce longer crises.** In Lebanon, 18 sects each with patronage networks and some with armed capacity means that any solution must satisfy more veto players than in a two-party democratic system. Factor this into recovery timeline assumptions: add years, not months, to expected restructuring completion.

### The scorecard for identifying the next Lebanon

Experienced sovereign credit analysts who reviewed Lebanon in retrospect identified a checklist of simultaneous conditions that, when present together, signal very high probability of currency peg collapse and banking crisis. These conditions were all present in Lebanon by 2018–2019:

1. **Chronic current account deficit financed by capital flows (not productivity).** Lebanon's current account deficit was approximately 20–25% of GDP annually by 2018. This is sustainable only as long as capital inflows continue. When inflows are dominated by diaspora deposits rather than foreign direct investment or export revenues, they are confidence-sensitive — they can reverse quickly.

2. **Net FX reserves declining while gross reserves are still reported positively.** The gap between BdL's gross reserves and its net usable reserves (after offsetting liabilities) was not published prominently, but could be calculated from BdL's own balance sheet data. This gap was the critical signal that the headline reserve figure was misleading.

3. **Interest rate differential that cannot be explained by fundamentals.** When a country's banks are offering 14–20% on dollar deposits while the global risk-free rate is 1–2%, the differential is either compensation for very high default risk (which should be pricing into Eurobond spreads) or compensation for eventual devaluation (which means the peg is expected to break). Either interpretation is a warning.

4. **Political system incapable of fiscal adjustment.** Lebanon's primary fiscal deficit (excluding interest payments) was large and persistent. Multiple governments had promised reform programs; none had been implemented. The constitutional structure explained why: every reform measure threatened a different sect's patronage, creating an effective veto coalition against change.

5. **Eurobond spread acceleration.** Credit default swap (CDS) spreads and Eurobond yield premiums are market summaries of all the above. Lebanon's spreads began accelerating in early 2019, providing a market-level confirmation of the fundamental analysis.

When all five conditions are present simultaneously, the question is not whether a crisis will occur but when — and the "when" typically depends on confidence-sensitive capital flow dynamics that are inherently hard to time precisely.

### Practical trade structures

For investors who encounter the next Lebanon-type situation:

**Eurobond shorts (if accessible):** Before a peg breaks, sovereign Eurobonds in countries with net reserve depletion and fiscal deficits are candidates for credit default swap (CDS) purchases if CDS is liquid. For Lebanon specifically, CDS markets were thin and the timing was difficult, but the directional signal was clear 12 months before default.

**Parallel currency exposure:** If you can access parallel market currency in a distressed-peg country, holding physical USD or offshore USD accounts is the straightforward hedge. Lebanese residents who kept savings offshore or held physical dollars outside the banking system preserved their wealth; those who trusted the banking system did not.

**Avoid local currency fixed income in a Ponzi peg:** Lebanese pound Treasury bills offered nominally high yields in LBP during the peg era — sometimes 10–12%. Any investor holding LBP T-bills through the devaluation faced currency losses of 98%+ that entirely overwhelmed the yield advantage. This is the "yield illusion" in high-interest-rate pegged environments: the high yield is compensation for devaluation risk that is dramatically underpriced by the official peg.

Lebanon's collapse contains the complete anatomy of a political-financial crisis: a structural imbalance (chronic current account deficit) addressed by a fragile fix (a hard peg supported by a Ponzi deposit scheme) embedded in a political system congenitally incapable of reform (18-sect confessional paralysis) and then devastated by a physical catastrophe (the port explosion) that simultaneously wiped out economic infrastructure and any remaining political momentum for change. Each element reinforced every other. The peg required the Ponzi; the Ponzi required political protection; the political protection required the sectarian system; the sectarian system required paralysis; and the paralysis ensured that when the explosion came, no one was positioned to catch the country before it hit the bottom.

The tragedy of Lebanon is that virtually every element of this collapse was visible in advance to anyone who read the IMF reports, tracked the BdL reserve data, understood the political arithmetic, and asked the basic question: what happens when the inflows stop? The answer, it turned out, was everything.
