> **TL;DR** — Once a geopolitical crisis starts, the most likely next moves follow a predictable escalation ladder. Actors have constrained toolkits, tit-for-tat dynamics are empirical, and the investor who maps the current rung to probable next rungs can position *before* the announcement, not after.
>
> - Every major confrontation climbs a seven-rung ladder from diplomatic protest to strategic military action — skipping rungs is costly and rare.
> - The US, China, Russia, and OPEC each have a specific toolkit and a predictable sequence: knowing the actor plus the current rung identifies the most probable next move.
> - Roughly 70% of trade-dispute retaliations land within 30 days and match the opponent's scale within ±20% — this is exploitable timing.
> - Red-line identification is the key skill: specific observable triggers (ADIZ intrusion vs. blockade, enrichment to 60% vs. 90%) shift scenario probabilities and asset impacts non-linearly.
> - De-escalation follows its own five-stage sequence with measurable signals — each stage is an early buy signal for the crisis-affected asset.
> - The investor's framework: locate current rung → enumerate next three to five rungs with probabilities → compute probability-weighted expected move → size position → define invalidation events.

The morning of February 23, 2022, anyone who had been watching the right signals for the preceding six weeks already knew that something catastrophic was coming. Russian troop deployments near the Ukrainian border had swelled to 190,000. Senior diplomat Sergei Lavrov had shifted his language from "we have no plans" to "the situation is critical." The ruble had weakened 4% over two weeks without a news trigger. Three Russian state banks had quietly restructured their hard-currency balances. Satellite imagery showed field hospitals erected along supply routes. And Russia's domestic media framing had shifted from describing Ukraine as a neighbor to describing it as a "threat to Russian security."

Six separate signals. All pointing at the same rung on the escalation ladder — the rung just below armed conflict.

Yet when the invasion actually launched on February 24, European equity futures still opened down 3%. Oil spiked from \$96 to over \$102 in hours. The VIX jumped from 28 to nearly 32. Even a partly-anticipated shock moved markets because most participants had been watching the event, not the ladder.

This article is about changing that. It is about moving from reactive positioning — "what just happened and what do I buy now?" — to anticipatory positioning: "we are on rung four, the next most probable move is a SWIFT restriction or reserve freeze, here is what that does to EUR/USD and sovereign spreads, and here is how I size before the announcement."

## Foundations: What an escalation ladder is and why nations use it

To understand escalation ladders, start with a simple neighborhood dispute. Two households disagree about a fence on the property line. The first move is polite: a note under the door. If that is ignored, the next move is a face-to-face conversation. Then a certified letter. Then a call to the local council. Then a formal complaint with the housing authority. Then a lawyer's letter. Then court. Nobody jumps straight from the polite note to court — the social and financial costs of each escalation act as brakes, and each rung must be tried before the next can be justified to a third party.

Nations operate on exactly the same logic, just with higher stakes. The "polite note" is a UN speech. The certified letter is an ambassador recall. The housing authority complaint is a targeted sanction. Court is armed conflict.

Herman Kahn formalized this as the "escalation ladder" in his 1965 book *On Escalation: Metaphors and Scenarios*. He described 44 rungs of potential nuclear conflict. For modern conventional geopolitical crises, the ladder compresses to seven practical rungs that cover the full range of tools any major power actually has available.

**Why actors rarely skip rungs.** Three forces keep nations on the ladder rather than jumping past multiple steps:

*Cost and credibility.* Each rung is costlier than the last — in economic terms (sanctions hurt the sender too), in political capital (domestic constituencies resist military action without exhausted alternatives), and in international legitimacy (allies require that lower-rung options be tried before committing to higher ones). A country that jumps straight to military action loses the ability to claim justification.

*Reversibility.* Lower rungs are largely reversible. A tariff can be lifted in a trade deal. An ambassador can be sent back. A sanction can be suspended. Military action cannot be undone. The irreversibility cost rises steeply at Rung 5 and above, which is why actors resist crossing that threshold without exhausting prior rungs.

*The domestic audience.* In every democracy and every autocracy, leaders face domestic constituencies. Moving up the ladder gives those audiences time to adjust narratives. A war that started with a UN protest, then sanctions, then proxy support is far easier to justify domestically than one launched cold. Leaders use the ladder as a legitimacy-building sequence as much as a coercive tool.

**What a "rung" is technically.** A rung is a discrete, observable, and largely reversible action that signals willingness to escalate further if demands are not met. Each rung has three characteristics: (1) a specific actor who takes it, (2) a specific mechanism through which it operates (legal, financial, military), and (3) a specific set of assets that respond when it is activated. Those three characteristics are what make the ladder useful to an investor — they allow you to pre-map the asset impact of each likely next step.

## The seven rungs: from diplomatic protest to strategic escalation

Let us walk each rung precisely, with the real mechanisms and the historical asset moves attached.

**Rung 1: Diplomatic protest.** A government issues a formal statement of displeasure — a UN speech, a foreign ministry press conference with strong language, a summoning of the opposing country's ambassador for a formal rebuke. The threshold for Rung 1 is low. It costs almost nothing and carries almost no commitment. Market impact is minimal: typically less than 0.5% moves in the directly affected country's equities and currency.

Historical example: After North Korea's 2019 missile tests, the US issued Rung 1 protests through the UN Security Council. Korean won moved 0.3% on the day. No material asset response.

**Rung 2: Economic pressure.** This covers tariffs, investment screening (CFIUS in the US), sector-specific export restrictions, and travel bans. The key feature of Rung 2 is that it is bilateral and economic rather than financial. It targets the flow of goods, services, and capital through legal instruments. Asset impact is moderate: equities of affected sectors move 2-5%, FX moves 1-3% in exposed currencies.

Historical example: President Trump's March 2018 announcement of steel and aluminum tariffs under Section 232 was a Rung 2 move. European equity markets fell 1.8% the following day; the euro weakened 0.7%.

**Rung 3: Targeted sanctions.** OFAC SDN list additions, sector-specific bans (blocking a single bank, barring an individual oligarch, restricting a specific industry from dollar clearing). These target entities, not the full economy. Sovereign credit spreads of the targeted country typically widen 30-100 basis points. Currency weakens 5-15%.

Historical example: After Russia annexed Crimea in March 2014, the first Western response was targeted sanctions against specific individuals and businesses. Russian sovereign spreads widened by about 80 basis points over the following month. The ruble weakened 9%.

**Rung 4: Comprehensive sanctions and asset freezes.** SWIFT exclusions, reserve asset freezes via IEEPA, oil price caps with shipping and insurance bans, full sector prohibitions. These target the entire economy rather than specific entities. The asset impact is severe and often partially irreversible: currency depreciates 20-50% (or more in the shock phase), sovereign spreads blow out 500-2,000 basis points, equities circuit-break.

Historical example: The February 24, 2022 package against Russia froze \$300 billion in sovereign reserves and excluded seven Russian banks from SWIFT. The ruble crashed to 116 per dollar within days — a 47% devaluation from its pre-invasion level. Moscow stock exchange halted trading for weeks.

**Rung 5: Proxy conflict and covert operations.** Arming third parties fighting on one side, providing intelligence to proxies, running cyber campaigns that constitute acts of coercion, funding opposition movements. The distinguishing feature is that Rung 5 involves violence, but the principal actors maintain plausible deniability. Asset impact: oil typically spikes 5-15% on Rung 5 events (geopolitical risk premium re-prices), gold adds 3-6%, VIX rises 15-20%.

Historical example: US arms transfers to Ukraine escalated through 2022 from MANPADS to HIMARS to Abrams tanks — each step a Rung 5 ratchet. Each arms escalation announcement correlated with a 2-4% oil spike in the following week.

**Rung 6: Limited direct military action.** Airstrikes, naval blockades, freedom of navigation operations in contested waters, direct exchanges of fire. The actors are now officially in conflict, even if neither declares war. Asset impact is sharp: gold typically moves +7-10% in the week following a Rung 6 event, VIX surges 25-35%, EM equities fall 8-12%.

Historical example: The April 13-14, 2024 direct Iranian drone and missile strike on Israel was a Rung 6 event — the first direct state-on-state exchange between Iran and Israel. Brent crude rose from \$90 to \$92 in the immediate aftermath. Gold hit a new all-time high above \$2,400.

**Rung 7: Strategic escalation.** Full conventional conflict, nuclear signaling, or destruction of critical infrastructure at scale. The asset impact is a regime change, not a spike: markets reprice the entire risk premium environment for months or years. The Russia-Ukraine invasion caused a structural repricing of European energy (TTF gas went from \$83/MWh in February 2022 to \$310/MWh by August 2022), a permanent shift in European defense spending, and a restructuring of global food supply chains.

## Each actor's toolkit: what the US, China, Russia, and OPEC have in their playbook

![Actor toolkit matrix showing what each major power does at each escalation rung](/imgs/blogs/anticipating-the-next-move-escalation-ladders-in-geopolitical-crises-2.webp)

Each major actor has a constrained toolkit. The constraint comes from three sources: available instruments (you cannot implement OFAC sanctions if you are not the US), credibility capital (overusing a tool degrades its deterrent effect), and domestic political feasibility (a tool that destroys domestic jobs is politically expensive).

**The US toolkit, in order.** The US has the world's most powerful financial toolkit because the dollar's reserve status gives it access to OFAC, SWIFT pressure, and IEEPA asset freezes. The typical US escalation sequence is: (1) UN speech / allied briefings → (2) Section 232/301 tariffs or CFIUS investment blocks → (3) OFAC SDN additions + secondary sanctions warnings → (4) SWIFT pressure + IEEPA reserve freeze → (5) arms to proxies / intelligence sharing → (6) carrier group deployment / limited strikes → (7) full military engagement.

The critical tool in the US kit that other actors cannot replicate is secondary sanctions — the ability to threaten any third-country entity that deals with the sanctioned party. This extraterritorial reach is why US sanctions are uniquely powerful. When the US adds Russia's Sberbank to the SDN list with secondary sanctions, a Vietnamese steel company buying Russian iron ore faces a choice: deal with the US financial system or deal with Russia. Most choose the US financial system.

**China's toolkit, in order.** China's toolkit is predominantly economic and gray-zone rather than financial or military. The typical sequence is: (1) diplomatic statement / trip cancellation → (2) consumer boycott / tourism ban / coal or agricultural tariffs → (3) rare-earth and critical mineral export controls → (4) Boeing cancellation / state-enterprise procurement switch → (5) gray-zone maritime harassment (Taiwan Strait / South China Sea ADIZ intrusions) → (6) cyber campaigns (attributed to state-aligned actors) → (7) PLAN military exercises / Taiwan Strait blockade signal.

China's key leverage tool is rare-earth and critical mineral controls. China processes 85% of the world's rare earths, 90% of graphite, 80% of gallium, and 65% of lithium. When China imposed germanium and gallium export controls in July 2023, semiconductor input prices spiked 40-60% for those materials within weeks. This tool is costly to use because it accelerates the diversification efforts China wants to prevent — but it produces immediate market impact.

**Russia's toolkit, in order.** Russia's toolkit is built around energy leverage, nuclear signaling, and information warfare rather than financial tools. The typical sequence is: (1) information warfare / state media escalation → (2) energy flow restriction (pipeline throttling, ruble payment demands) → (3) grain and food leverage (Black Sea export corridor threats) → (4) proxy forces in third countries → (5) nuclear signaling (raised alert levels, Sarmat test demonstrations) → (6) hypersonic or unconventional weapons demonstration → (7) direct strategic action.

Russia's energy tool was uniquely powerful until 2023, when Europe diversified away from Russian pipeline gas. Russian pipeline gas to Europe fell from 155 billion cubic meters in 2021 to just 27 bcm in 2023. That means Russia's most powerful Rung 2-3 tool is now largely spent against European targets — a permanent degradation of its toolkit, which matters for forward-looking escalation analysis.

**OPEC+/Saudi Arabia's toolkit, in order.** OPEC+'s toolkit is built around oil supply management. The sequence is: (1) OPEC+ consultation signals / bilateral diplomacy → (2) surprise production cut or hike (±1-2 mb/d decisions affecting global balance) → (3) petrodollar alternatives (CNY-denominated oil contracts, mBridge payment corridor) → (4) Hormuz leverage via coordinated signals with Iran → (5) strategic reserve drawdown or refusal to fill emergency drawdowns.

The October 2022 OPEC+ production cut — an explicit 2 mb/d reduction announced just weeks before US midterm elections — was a Rung 2 geopolitical move as much as an oil market management decision. It was widely interpreted as a snub of President Biden, who had visited Riyadh in July seeking production increases. Brent crude rose 3% on the day of the announcement.

## The tit-for-tat constraint: why retaliation is empirically predictable

![Geopolitical retaliation timing: most retaliations fall within 30 days, trade disputes faster than sanctions](/imgs/blogs/anticipating-the-next-move-escalation-ladders-in-geopolitical-crises-3.webp)

One of the most useful empirical facts about geopolitical escalation is the tit-for-tat constraint: retaliatory moves tend to match the opponent's move in scale and arrive quickly. The chart above shows the distribution across historical trade and sanctions disputes. In trade disputes, roughly 70% of retaliatory moves land within 30 days. In sanctions disputes, the response is somewhat slower (bureaucratic preparation required), but still converges to 50% within 30 days.

**Why scale-matching happens.** A retaliation that exceeds the original move risks re-escalating beyond what domestic audiences and allies will support. A retaliation far below the original move signals weakness and invites further pressure. The equilibrium is proportionality — matching the scale of the opponent's move to signal resolve without triggering a further ratchet. This is why China's response to US tariffs on \$34 billion of goods in July 2018 was tariffs on exactly \$34 billion of US goods, targeting agricultural products (soybeans, pork, dairy) for maximum political pain in Trump-voting states.

**The domestic political constraint on timing.** The 30-day window reflects real bureaucratic and political dynamics. Legal preparation for sanctions responses takes 2-4 weeks. Trade tariff implementation requires notice periods. Domestic coalition-building takes time. An autocracy like China or Russia can move faster (Putin signed the ruble payment decree 10 days after the SWIFT exclusion) but still faces logistics constraints. A democracy like the EU typically moves slower (the first comprehensive Russia sanctions package took 3 weeks to clear internal EU disagreements).

**When tit-for-tat breaks down.** Three conditions break the proportionality pattern: (1) asymmetric dependence (if China depends 40% on US semiconductor equipment and the US depends 2% on China for the same, China cannot retaliate symmetrically in that domain); (2) domestic crisis (if the retaliating country is in political or financial stress, it may over-retaliate to distract or under-retaliate due to constrained resources); (3) third-party mediation (if a backstage negotiation is occurring, the public response may be deliberately de-escalatory while talks proceed).

#### Worked example:

**US-China tariff tit-for-tat, 2018.** On July 6, 2018, the US imposed 25% tariffs on \$34 billion of Chinese goods under Section 301. China's retaliation landed July 6 — the same day — with tariffs on \$34 billion of US exports: soybeans, pork, automobiles, and seafood. Scale match: exact. Timing: 0 days (pre-prepared after the March announcement of the investigation).

On September 24, 2018, the US escalated to 10% tariffs on \$200 billion of Chinese goods (List 3). China responded on September 24 with 5-10% tariffs on \$60 billion of US goods. The asymmetry (China's retaliation was smaller) reflected asymmetric dependence — China's US imports totaled only \$130 billion versus its exports of \$540 billion, limiting proportional matching in dollar terms.

Market implication: After the July 6 exchange, the CSI 300 fell 3.8% in three days and the yuan weakened 1.2%. US agricultural exporters (ADM, Bunge) fell 4-7%. The pattern was: announcement → 3-day equity selloff in exposed sectors → partial recovery as markets priced in negotiation probability.

The investor who had mapped the escalation ladder knew in March 2018 — when the Section 301 investigation was launched — that a Rung 2 tariff exchange was the most probable next step. The time between the March investigation announcement and the July 6 first tariffs was four months of lead time to build short exposure to Chinese consumer equities and long exposure to Mexican and Vietnamese manufacturing equities (the supply-chain rerouting beneficiaries).

## Red-line identification: what specific triggers unlock the next rung

Identifying the current rung is only half the work. The higher-value insight is identifying the specific observable events that would shift a situation from one rung to the next — the "red lines" that each actor has defined for themselves, explicitly or implicitly.

Red-line identification matters because asset repricing is non-linear around thresholds. A situation at Rung 4 may have oil pricing in a \$3-5 risk premium; the same situation crossing to Rung 5 (proxy conflict) can add \$10-15 to oil in days. If you can identify in advance that a specific observable event (a naval blockade of a major strait) would trigger the Rung 5 transition, you can position *before* that event rather than after.

**The Taiwan red-line matrix.** Taiwan is the most consequential red-line scenario in global markets. The key distinction is between a series of sub-threshold moves (each individually manageable) and a genuine threshold-crossing event. The escalation sequence runs:

*Below Rung 5 (current state, 2026):* ADIZ intrusions, large-scale military exercises (like August 2022 post-Pelosi), gray-zone maritime operations, economic pressure on Taiwan's trading partners. Asset response: TSMC falls 5-10% on sharp escalation episodes, then recovers. VIX spikes 8-12%. USD/TWD weakens 2-4%.

*Rung 5 threshold event:* A kinetic incident — a Chinese vessel firing on a Taiwanese coast guard ship, a shoot-down of a military aircraft in disputed airspace, a cyberattack on Taiwan's financial or power infrastructure. This would trigger an asset regime shift: TSMC could fall 20-30%, semiconductor ETFs drop 15-20%, Brent crude rises 8-12% (Strait of Taiwan shipping risk), gold adds 6-8%.

*Rung 6 threshold event:* A naval blockade — Chinese warships establishing a defined perimeter around Taiwan that prevents commercial shipping. This is the scenario that reprices the entire global semiconductor supply chain. TSMC manufactures approximately 90% of the world's most advanced chips (sub-5nm). A sustained blockade would trigger supply chain restructuring spending worth \$200-400 billion, US CHIPS Act emergency activation, and a sustained global equity risk-off premium.

**The Iran nuclear red-line matrix.** Iran's nuclear status creates an asymmetric red-line structure: the oil market is pricing approximately a \$5/bbl risk premium for "Iran could go nuclear." But the actual threshold events that would trigger physical conflict (and a \$15-25/bbl oil spike) are specific and observable.

*Current state:* Iran enriching uranium to 60% purity (weapons grade requires 90%+), approximately 9,000 advanced centrifuges operating, IAEA monitoring partially restored. The oil market's baseline risk premium is roughly \$5/bbl.

*Next rung triggers:* (1) Enrichment to 90% purity, detected by IAEA. This is the "breakout" threshold — the step that directly precedes weaponization. Historical precedent: Brent crude rose approximately \$4-6/bbl on each IAEA report showing Iranian enrichment acceleration in 2021-22. (2) Expulsion of IAEA inspectors, removing monitoring capacity. (3) Iranian announcement of nuclear device assembly.

![US-China chip war escalation scenarios showing three probability-weighted paths from current FDPR state](/imgs/blogs/anticipating-the-next-move-escalation-ladders-in-geopolitical-crises-7.webp)

#### Worked example:

**Taiwan strait: probability-weighted expected oil move.** Suppose you are assessing the Taiwan strait situation in mid-2026. Based on the observable signals (PLA exercises, CCP party congress messaging, US carrier group positioning, Taiwan election outcomes), you assign the following 12-month probabilities:

- Scenario A — Full naval blockade (Rung 6): 8% probability. Expected oil impact: +\$18/bbl (Strait of Taiwan carries approximately 20% of global LNG and significant crude flows).
- Scenario B — Kinetic incident short of blockade (Rung 5): 20% probability. Expected oil impact: +\$10/bbl.
- Scenario C — Continued sub-threshold pressure / status quo (Rung 4): 60% probability. Expected oil impact: +\$1/bbl (sustained risk premium already priced).
- Scenario D — De-escalation / reduced tension: 12% probability. Expected oil impact: -\$3/bbl (risk premium fades).

Probability-weighted expected oil move: (0.08 × \$18) + (0.20 × \$10) + (0.60 × \$1) + (0.12 × -\$3) = \$1.44 + \$2.00 + \$0.60 - \$0.36 = **\$3.68/bbl expected oil upside.**

Current Brent at \$67.50. A \$3.68 expected upside is approximately 5.5%. That is meaningful enough to justify a modest long crude or long gold position as a hedge — not a full position (the base case is status quo), but enough to compensate for the tail scenarios.

If any observable signal shifts Scenario A from 8% to 20% (say, a naval exercise involving a blockade rehearsal), the expected move jumps to: (0.20 × \$18) + (0.20 × \$10) + (0.50 × \$1) + (0.10 × -\$3) = \$3.60 + \$2.00 + \$0.50 - \$0.30 = **\$5.80/bbl.** That 58% increase in expected upside would justify adding significantly to the position.

## Spike or regime change? The most expensive distinction in geopolitical trading

![Spike versus regime change: two fundamentally different market responses to geopolitical shocks](/imgs/blogs/anticipating-the-next-move-escalation-ladders-in-geopolitical-crises-4.webp)

Every geopolitical shock produces one of two market outcomes: a spike or a regime change. Getting this distinction wrong is the single most costly mistake in geopolitical trading. A spike looks terrifying in real-time but reverses in days to weeks. A regime change feels like a spike initially but never fully recovers — the asset permanently reprices to a new equilibrium.

Approximately 80% of geopolitical events produce spikes. Approximately 20% produce regime changes. The two look identical in the first 24-72 hours. The signals that distinguish them only emerge over 5-10 days.

**The three questions that separate a spike from a regime change:**

1. *Does the shock physically disrupt supply chains or supply flows?* The 2020 Soleimani killing was a spike because it did not disrupt Iranian oil exports (Iran was already under maximum sanctions). The 2022 Ukraine invasion was a regime change because it physically removed Russian gas from European supply and Ukrainian wheat from global markets. Physical disruption is the most reliable predictor of regime change.

2. *Does the shock trigger a permanent realignment of alliances or trade relationships?* The 2019 US-China trade war tariffs were a semi-permanent regime change for US-China trade (most tariffs survived multiple subsequent administrations). The 2014 Crimea annexation was not a full regime change — it triggered targeted sanctions but not the comprehensive economic divorce that came in 2022.

3. *Can the central banks and fiscal authorities offset it?* The COVID shock to markets (March 2020, -34% on the S&P in five weeks) looked like a regime change but proved to be a spike because the Fed deployed unlimited QE and Congress passed the largest fiscal stimulus in US history within weeks. The 2022 Russia sanctions were a regime change for European energy because there was no policy response that could rapidly replace 155 bcm of Russian gas.

#### Worked example:

**Soleimani killing (spike) vs. Ukraine invasion (regime change).** On January 3, 2020, the US killed Iranian General Qasem Soleimani in a drone strike at Baghdad airport. Brent crude rose 4.4% in 24 hours. The S&P futures fell 1.5% overnight. Gold rose \$29/oz. Within 72 hours, all three moves had fully reversed. Why? The killing did not physically disrupt oil supply (Iran was already sanctioned to minimal exports), did not trigger a new alliance realignment, and the Fed was already in easing mode.

By contrast, the February 24, 2022 Ukraine invasion. On invasion day, Brent crude rose from \$96 to \$102 (+6.2%). That was the spike. What followed was the regime change: by March, Brent hit \$117. European TTF gas went from \$83/MWh to \$310/MWh by August 2022 — a 373% increase. European power bills doubled. German industrial competitiveness permanently declined. None of these moves reversed. The ruble, after its initial 47% crash, recovered to approximately pre-invasion levels by May 2022 (due to capital controls and mandatory FX conversion requirements) — but the structural damage to the Russian economy was not reversed. Russian advanced chip imports fell 70%, permanently limiting defense production capacity.

The investor's diagnostic at the time of the shock: (1) Does this physically disrupt a major supply flow? For the Soleimani killing — no, Iranian exports were already at minimum. For Ukraine — yes, 17% of European gas supply was at risk immediately. (2) Has a financial channel (sanctions, reserve freeze, SWIFT) been activated that cannot be easily reversed? For Soleimani — no new sanctions, maximum pressure already in place. For Ukraine — \$300 billion in reserves frozen, which cannot be unfrozen without political conditions not met.

## De-escalation: recognizing the exit ramp before the crowd

De-escalation is underappreciated as a trading signal. Most investors watch for escalation events because they produce sharp, visible moves. But de-escalation events — which are often ambiguous and spread over weeks — produce sustained, tradeable reversals in the affected assets.

The key insight is that de-escalation follows its own predictable sequence.

![The five-stage de-escalation signal sequence from back-channel contacts to public concession](/imgs/blogs/anticipating-the-next-move-escalation-ladders-in-geopolitical-crises-5.webp)

**Stage 1: Back-channel contacts.** Before any public statement changes, diplomatic contacts activate through intermediaries — typically Switzerland, Qatar, Oman, Turkey, or the Vatican, depending on the parties. These are not publicly announced. The signal is usually indirect: a third-country foreign minister schedules a meeting with both parties in the same week, or a news agency in the neutral country reports "ongoing contacts." By the time Stage 1 is publicly visible, the process is usually already well advanced.

**Stage 2: Ambiguous language.** A senior official replaces "unacceptable" with "we are open to a framework that addresses our concerns." A press spokesperson says "dialogue is always possible" after months of "there is nothing to discuss." These language shifts are measurable if you track the exact wording of official statements over time. They precede formal talks by 3-6 weeks on average.

**Stage 3: Third-party mediator emergence.** A neutral country or international organization publicly offers to host talks. The parties do not immediately accept, but neither immediately rejects. This is a public Schelling point — both sides can signal openness to de-escalation without making an explicit concession.

**Stage 4: Talks announced.** An official negotiation framework is established. Asset response: typically 60-80% of the escalation-phase move reverses in the week following the announcement, often overshooting (markets price the resolution, not just the talks).

**Stage 5: First substantive concession.** One party makes a verifiable, observable concession that the other side can present as a win. This is the signal that the ladder is being descended in earnest. The remaining crisis premium in the asset fully unwinds.

**Why de-escalation is structurally slower than escalation.** Escalation is easy because each step increases pressure — the incentive for the escalating party is clear. De-escalation requires each party to give up leverage (the threat of the next rung) in exchange for uncertain concessions from the other side. This is the classic prisoner's dilemma structure: even if both parties would prefer de-escalation, each fears being the first to step back. Third-party mediators solve this by providing a face-saving mechanism — both parties can claim they responded to a neutral invitation, not that they backed down.

#### Worked example:

**Iran JCPOA oil price signal, 2015.** The July 14, 2015 announcement of the JCPOA — the Iran nuclear deal — triggered one of the cleanest de-escalation trades in recent history. Let us map the signal sequence:

*Stage 1 (March 2015):* US and Iranian negotiators began meeting in Lausanne in more intensive format. Swiss Federal Councillor reported both parties making "concrete progress."

*Stage 2 (April 2015):* The Lausanne framework announcement. US Secretary Kerry described Iran's uranium enrichment reduction as "significant." Iranian Foreign Minister Zarif used the word "agreement" in public for the first time.

*Stage 3 (May 2015):* Extended nuclear talks in Vienna with EU, P5+1 all present. All parties confirmed their attendance at a July deadline meeting.

*Stage 4 (July 14, 2015):* JCPOA formally announced. Iran to reduce enriched uranium stockpile by 98% and reduce centrifuges from 19,000 to 6,104.

*Stage 5 (Implementation day, January 2016):* Iran completed the required reductions; IAEA certified compliance; US and EU lifted nuclear-related sanctions.

Oil market response: Brent fell approximately \$7/bbl (from \$58 to \$51) in the two weeks following the July announcement, as markets priced the return of ~2.5 million barrels per day of Iranian oil. The investor who had identified Stage 2 (the April language shift) and taken a short position in crude futures had a three-month lead on the announced de-escalation, capturing most of the \$7/bbl move before the announcement.

The reverse trade — buying Iranian-exposed assets at the Stage 1-2 transition — would have returned roughly 12% in crude futures from April to August 2015.

## The investor's anticipation framework: five steps from ladder to position

With the conceptual tools in place, we can describe the full investor workflow.

![Expected 7-day asset price change per escalation rung for gold, oil, USD, and equities](/imgs/blogs/anticipating-the-next-move-escalation-ladders-in-geopolitical-crises-6.webp)

The chart above shows the expected 7-day asset price changes by escalation rung, derived from the series' curated event-study data (Caldara-Iacoviello GPR, IMF Working Paper on geopolitical risk and asset prices). Three observations worth internalizing:

First, the divergence between safe-haven longs (gold, oil, USD) and risk shorts (EM equities, S&P 500) is relatively modest at Rungs 1-2, meaningful at Rungs 3-4, and substantial at Rungs 5-7. This tells you when it is worth building the hedge versus when it is not.

Second, oil responds more sharply than gold at higher rungs. This reflects oil's supply sensitivity to military conflict and energy geopolitics, versus gold's more sentiment-driven safe-haven role.

Third, the EM equities and S&P 500 drawdowns at Rung 7 (-15% and -13% respectively) are estimates based on historical Rung 6-7 events — these are not tail scenarios, they are the average outcome of the eight historical events in this range.

**Step 1: Locate the current rung.** Map the active observable events against the rung definitions. Use the actor toolkit matrix (Fig 2) to identify which specific instrument is being used. If you see OFAC SDN additions without SWIFT exclusion, you are at Rung 3. If you see SWIFT exclusion plus reserve freeze, you are at Rung 4. Be rigorous — the rung designation should be defensible from specific, named observable events.

**Step 2: Enumerate next 3-5 rungs with probabilities.** Do not assume escalation is the only direction. De-escalation is always a scenario. For each of the 3-5 plausible next states (escalate to next rung, hold at current rung, de-escalate one rung, negotiated resolution), assign a probability. The probabilities should sum to 100%. They should reflect signal counts: if four of your six early-warning signals are active, weight escalation more heavily.

**Step 3: Map each next rung to primary asset impacts.** Use the expected-move data from the chart above, adjusted for the specific actors and supply chains involved. A Rung 5 event in the Taiwan Strait (semiconductor supply chain) has different asset impacts than a Rung 5 event in West Africa (less systemically connected).

**Step 4: Compute the probability-weighted expected move.** Sum the probability-weighted asset impacts. If the expected move is less than the bid-ask spread plus the cost of carry on your hedge, stay flat — the expected value does not justify the position.

**Step 5: Size position and define invalidation.** Position size should be proportional to conviction probability times expected magnitude. Define in advance what observable event would invalidate your thesis — a specific observable de-escalation signal (ambassador return, talks announcement) that would cause you to exit.

![Geopolitical position sizing matrix showing recommended allocation based on probability and expected move magnitude](/imgs/blogs/anticipating-the-next-move-escalation-ladders-in-geopolitical-crises-8.webp)

The matrix above provides a practical reference. The key takeaway: stay flat when the probability is low AND the expected move is small. Buy optionality (OTM calls or puts) when the probability is medium — you want asymmetric exposure without delta risk. Commit to a full position only when your signal count supports high probability AND the expected move is large enough to justify the carry cost of the hedge.

#### Worked example:

**US-China chip war: applying the full five-step framework.** As of mid-2026, the US-China semiconductor confrontation is at approximately Rung 3-4: comprehensive export controls (BIS October 2022, October 2023 rules) have been implemented, but no full TSMC supply cut has occurred. Nvidia's China revenue has fallen from 26% to approximately 8% of total revenue.

*Step 1 (Current rung):* Rung 3-4. FDPR enforcement (extraterritorial controls forcing TSMC and others to cut advanced chip supply to Chinese entities), OFAC-like entity list additions (Huawei, SMIC), and sector-specific export bans. Not yet Rung 4 comprehensive: China still receives older-generation chips, and TSMC still ships some non-advanced products to Chinese customers.

*Step 2 (Next rungs with probabilities, 12-month):*
- Scenario A: Full TSMC ban (Rung 4.5) — US forces TSMC to cease all chip shipments to China, including legacy nodes. Probability: 15%. Signal requirement: a specific BIS rulemaking targeting all TSMC-China sales, likely triggered by a Chinese military breakthrough traced to commercial chips.
- Scenario B: Status quo held (Rung 3-4 maintained) — Probabilities: 55%. Signal: continued diplomatic engagement without further rulemaking; trade deal framework being discussed.
- Scenario C: Chinese rare-earth retaliation (Rung 3, lateral escalation) — China implements comprehensive gallium, germanium, and graphite export controls affecting US semiconductor production. Probability: 30%. Signal: export permit system extended to additional minerals, Chinese Ministry of Commerce statements on "reciprocal measures."

*Step 3 (Asset impacts):*
- Scenario A: Nvidia falls 20-25% (China revenue loss + structural valuation hit). TSMC falls 20-30% (demand loss + retaliation risk). US semiconductor ETF (SOXX) falls 15-20%.
- Scenario B: Nvidia flat to +5% (AI demand supports). Semiconductor sector broadly stable.
- Scenario C: US semiconductor input costs rise 40-60% for affected materials. MP Materials (rare-earth mining/processing) rises 30-40%. ASX (Australia semiconductor materials) rises 20-25%. Nvidia initially falls 8-10% (supply chain uncertainty), then recovers as Defense Production Act sourcing is activated.

*Step 4 (Expected moves):*
- NVDA: (0.15 × -22%) + (0.55 × +3%) + (0.30 × -9%) = -3.30% + 1.65% - 2.70% = **-4.35% expected**
- MP Materials: (0.15 × +10%) + (0.55 × +2%) + (0.30 × +35%) = 1.50% + 1.10% + 10.50% = **+13.1% expected**

*Step 5 (Position sizing):* The expected value for long MP Materials is strongly positive with asymmetric upside in the highest-probability tail scenario. This warrants a half to full position. The expected value for short NVDA is modestly negative (-4.35%) but with a high-probability base case of flat-to-up — meaning the short has negative expected value and high carry risk. More appropriate: buy NVDA puts as defined-risk short.

## Common misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "The biggest escalation event produces the biggest market move."** False. The October 2017 Catalan independence referendum was the most dramatic political event in Western Europe that year, but produced only a 1.8% IBEX decline and a 28-basis-point widening in Spanish sovereign spreads. The reason: Spain is deeply integrated into the eurozone financial system, the referendum had no legal standing under Spanish law, and markets correctly priced a low probability of actual independence. The rung analysis tells you why: Catalonia was at Rung 1 (legal/political dispute) throughout, never escalating to economic disruption.

**Misconception 2: "All sanctions produce the same market response."** False by a factor of three to four. Iran's first round of sanctions in 2012 cut oil exports by 800,000 barrels per day and produced a genuine structural move in oil. Russia's February 2022 comprehensive sanctions produced a 47% ruble depreciation and a 373% European gas price spike. The 2014 Crimea-era targeted sanctions produced a modest 80-basis-point widening in Russian spreads. The difference is rung depth: Rung 3 targeted sanctions, Rung 4 comprehensive sanctions, and the presence or absence of physical supply disruption.

**Misconception 3: "Once a country is sanctioned, the pressure stays constant."** False. Sanctioned countries adapt. Iran developed a shadow tanker fleet, built barter relationships with China and India, and restructured its currency management to reduce dollar dependence. Russia rerouted oil through India, China, and Turkey within six months of the 2022 sanctions, capturing 80-85% of the pre-war oil revenue level by 2023 (\$160 billion versus \$218 billion in the first war year). Sanctions are not a one-time shock — they are the opening move in an adaptation game. The market impact of sanctions typically peaks in the first 6-12 months, then partially reverses as the target adapts.

**Misconception 4: "Nuclear signaling means nuclear use."** False, and the confusion is expensive. Russia mentioned nuclear weapons over 200 times in official statements between February and December 2022. Each mention produced a brief VIX spike of 2-4% in US futures that fully reversed within 48-72 hours. Nuclear signaling is Russia's Rung 6-7 deterrence communication, not an operational preparation. The actual observable signals of operational preparation (nuclear force movements, command authority changes, DEFCON equivalent alert shifts) would be qualitatively different from verbal signaling and would produce structural rather than temporary market moves.

**Misconception 5: "De-escalation is the reversal of the escalation move."** False. De-escalation rarely fully undoes the damage. After the US-China Phase One deal in January 2020, the US maintained tariffs at 19.3% — far above the pre-trade-war baseline of 3.1%. Chinese soybean purchases to the US resumed but never fully offset the supply chain rerouting that had already occurred. The Russian ruble partially recovered from its 2022 crash, but Russian banks remained on the SWIFT exclusion list, Russian assets remained largely uninvestable for Western capital, and the energy relationship with Europe did not restore. De-escalation moves assets from "severe crisis discount" to "managed tension discount" — rarely back to the pre-crisis equilibrium.

## How it shows up in real markets

**Case study 1: The US-China trade war 2018-2019.** The full escalation sequence ran from March 2018 (Section 301 investigation launch, Rung 2 pre-announcement) to January 2020 (Phase One deal, partial de-escalation). Seven tariff rounds in 21 months. Each round followed a clear pattern: US announces next tranche → markets sell 1-3% in China-exposed sectors → China retaliates within 30 days, usually on agriculture → agriculture stocks fall, logistics and Vietnam-beneficiary stocks rise.

The investors who had mapped the ladder in March 2018 — when the investigation was announced and Rung 2 was clearly next — had four months to build exposure to Vietnam FDI beneficiaries (Vietnam FDI inflows jumped from \$14.1 billion in 2017 to \$19.1 billion in 2018, and Vietnamese electronics exports rose 16% year-over-year). The VN-Index peaked in early 2018 at 1,120 and fell back with initial trade war concern, but investors who understood Vietnam as the supply-chain winner held through the dip and were rewarded with a rally to 986 by mid-2019 as the FDI flows confirmed the thesis.

**Case study 2: The Iran-Israel shadow war, April 2024.** The April 13-14, 2024 event — Iran's first direct conventional strike on Israel using approximately 300 drones, cruise missiles, and ballistic missiles — was a Rung 6 event. It was also a nearly complete escalation ladder walk-up in plain sight: the Israeli airstrike on the Iranian consulate in Damascus on April 1 was a Rung 5-6 provocation. Iran's response followed within 13 days (inside the 30-day tit-for-tat window). Brent crude rose from \$89 to \$91.50 (2.8%). Gold hit a new all-time high of \$2,431/oz. But the response was calibrated: Iran announced the attack in advance to regional partners, minimizing casualties, and explicitly stated the action was "concluded" after Israel's missile defense intercepted most projectiles. Markets reversed within 48 hours because the calibration signaled both parties wanted to stop at Rung 6 rather than escalate to Rung 7.

**Case study 3: The October 2022 OPEC+ production cut.** The OPEC+ announcement on October 5, 2022 of a 2 mb/d production reduction was the clearest example of an economic toolkit being used as a geopolitical instrument. The US had visited Saudi Arabia in July 2022 seeking increased production to reduce gasoline prices ahead of midterm elections. OPEC+ rejected the request. The October cut, announced just 33 days before US midterms, was Rung 2 Saudi geopolitical signaling: demonstrating that the US could not dictate OPEC+ policy. Brent crude rose 3.4% on the announcement. The Biden administration's response was equally Rung 2: a threat to release more from the Strategic Petroleum Reserve and a review of US-Saudi relations. Neither side escalated further.

## How to trade it: the geopolitical escalation playbook

The framework above distills into a three-phase practical playbook.

**Phase 1: Pre-positioning (signals visible, event not yet announced).** This is the highest-alpha phase and the most underutilized by most investors. Use the six-category signal framework from post A6 of this series to count active signals. When four or more of the six categories show concurrent activation for the same actor-target pair, begin building a position — half-sized, using the expected-move calculation to size. The instruments of choice in this phase are: long gold (clean safe-haven, liquid), long near-dated oil calls (positive convexity if the event hits), and long low-cost volatility via VIX call spreads. Avoid building a full position before confirmation, because the false-positive rate at four-of-six signals is approximately 30% (based on the A7 base-rate analysis).

**Phase 2: During the shock (48 hours post-announcement).** Scale the position differently depending on whether this is a spike or a regime change diagnosis. In the first 24 hours, treat every event as a potential spike — keep position moderate. By 48-72 hours, the three diagnostic questions (supply disruption, alliance realignment, policy offset capacity) should give you a preliminary answer. If the signals point to regime change, add to the position on the first partial-recovery rally. If the signals point to a spike, begin fading the position — selling into the continued panic.

**Phase 3: Post-shock (regime change confirmed).** A confirmed regime change requires a portfolio regime shift, not just a tactical hedge. The 2022 Ukraine invasion regime change meant: (1) structurally long European energy alternatives (LNG, nuclear, Norwegian gas equities) for 18-24 months; (2) long European defense for 3+ years (German rearmament, Rheinmetall, BAE Systems); (3) short Russian-exposed EM equities as a structural position; (4) long frontier market food exporters (Argentina, Ukraine's competitors in wheat: Australia, Canada) until Black Sea corridor fully substituted.

**The invalidation framework.** Every geopolitical position needs a specific invalidation event. "The situation getting better" is not specific enough. Invalidations must be: observable (a specific report, announcement, or data point), timely (you will know within a defined window), and material (if it occurs, the thesis is clearly wrong, not just delayed). For a long crude position built on Taiwan Strait escalation probability, the invalidation event might be: "Both the US and China send senior diplomats for direct bilateral talks with a specific agenda item on Taiwan" — observable (public announcement), timely (you would know within days), and material (it definitively shifts the de-escalation probability upward).
