## Foundations: The Master Mental Model in One Framework

The five-step chain above is the spine of the entire series. Every post, regardless of the specific crisis type, traces the same causal path. Understanding the chain is more valuable than memorizing any individual case study, because the chain generalizes across all events.

**Step 1: The Trigger**

Every geopolitical event has a triggering event — a date, a decision, an action. The trigger is the moment of maximum uncertainty: the first minutes and hours when information is fragmentary, analysis is thin, and markets are moving on headline reaction rather than fundamental assessment.

The key question at the trigger: *What kind of event is this?* The taxonomy matters immediately because it determines which channels activate and which assets move. An assassination is not a war; a sanctions announcement is not a coup; a central bank independence attack is not an energy supply shock. Getting the category right in the first hour determines whether your initial positioning is in the right direction.

**Step 2: The Political Moves**

This is the most important step, and the one most often skipped by traders focused on the asset price reaction. Political actors don't move randomly — they move based on:
- Domestic political incentives (elections, approval ratings, factional pressures)
- Alliance calculus (what do allies expect? what do adversaries fear?)
- Economic constraints (what can the country actually afford?)
- Historical precedent (what did the same actor do in previous similar situations?)

Every post in this series has dissected the political moves of each actor in each crisis. The insight that emerges: political moves are more constrained and more predictable than markets assume in the first 24 hours of a crisis. Leaders facing geopolitical decisions are not free agents — they are heavily constrained by domestic politics, alliance obligations, and economic reality. These constraints make the political response more predictable, which makes the financial impact more predictable.

**Step 3: The Financial Channels**

Political moves don't directly move asset prices — they activate financial transmission channels. The six primary channels:

1. **Trade channels:** Tariffs, export restrictions, import bans — move supply chains, commodity prices, corporate revenues
2. **Sanctions channels:** Financial sanctions (SWIFT), asset freezes, investment restrictions — move FX, sovereign spreads, equity access
3. **Energy channels:** Production cuts, embargoes, transit disruption — move oil, gas, petrochemicals
4. **Credit channels:** Sovereign default risk, banking system stress — move bond yields, credit default swaps
5. **Capital flow channels:** Foreign investment restrictions, capital controls, reserve adequacy — move FX, equity flows
6. **Expectations channels:** Forward guidance, policy uncertainty — move volatility, option prices, longer-duration assets

Understanding which channels are activated by which political moves is the core analytical skill this series develops.

**Step 4: The Asset Impact**

Each channel activation creates specific, directional price moves in specific assets. The timing varies: trade channels take months to materialize (supply chains adjust slowly); sanctions channels move within days (financial markets are faster than physical trade); energy channels move within hours (commodity futures are highly liquid).

The magnitude and duration are determined by: how fundamental the change is (structural vs. cyclical), how much was already priced in, and whether the change creates secondary effects (contagion, feedback loops, policy responses).

**Step 5: The Trade**

The actionable conclusion from every analysis. For every event, the practitioner asks:
- What is the primary directional trade? (which asset, which direction)
- What signals confirm the thesis? (leading indicators, confirmation data)
- What is the invalidation condition? (when does the thesis break)
- What is the position size? (sized for uncertainty at day 1, sized for conviction at day 7)
- What is the exit timeline? (spike-and-revert vs. structural repricing)

## The 6×5 Asset Sensitivity Matrix

![6x5 asset sensitivity matrix crisis types sanctions war coup debt crisis trade war pandemic versus assets equities credit USD oil gold](/imgs/blogs/geopolitical-crises-and-markets-the-complete-playbook-capstone-2.png)

This matrix synthesizes the quantitative findings from all 86 posts into a single reference framework. The magnitudes are drawn from the event studies, historical data, and the curated dataset in the series.

**Reading the matrix:**

The six crisis types represent the fundamental categories of geopolitical shock, ordered roughly from most to least persistent:

1. **Sanctions (Structural):** The longest-lasting market repricing. Energy sanctions (Russia-Ukraine) can create oil price moves of +5 to +40% that persist for 6-18 months. Financial sanctions create sovereign spread widening of +100-500bp that may never fully reverse (Russian MOEX remains suspended). EM bonds in sanctioned countries experience persistent widening that requires years to normalize.

2. **War (Kinetic):** Depends heavily on whether the war involves major commodity producers. The Russia-Ukraine war is the outlier — involving the world's #1 fertilizer exporter, #2 oil exporter, and #3 wheat exporter simultaneously. A comparable war not involving major commodity producers (e.g., the Nagorno-Karabakh conflict) has minimal commodity impact. Equities in affected regions fall 10-35% and take 1-6 months to recover.

3. **Coup (Country-Specific):** The most confined crisis type. Market impact is concentrated in the target country (equities -10 to -50%, currency -5 to -30%), with minimal spillover unless the country is a critical commodity producer (Saudi Arabia) or financial hub. Recovery depends almost entirely on whether the coup establishes a credible government quickly or triggers prolonged political instability.

4. **Debt Crisis (Contagion):** The most dangerous for EM investors because of contagion risk — one country's default can trigger capital flight from all EM countries with similar characteristics. The 1997 Asian financial crisis and the 2008 global financial crisis demonstrate the contagion mechanism. EM credit spreads widen most severely (+200-2000bp for the specific country, +20-80bp for the peer group).

5. **Trade War (Slow Burn):** The most insidious because effects accumulate slowly and are easily dismissed until they become permanent. Equities in targeted countries fall 2-8% (with much of the impact through corporate earnings rather than valuation compression). Supply chains restructure over 2-5 years. The US-China trade war (2018-present) is the defining case study.

6. **Pandemic (Systemic):** The most severe and most idiosyncratic. COVID-19 created a -34% S&P drawdown — comparable to the Great Depression — followed by the fastest equity recovery in history. Oil demand collapse (-73% Brent trough) was unprecedented. The pandemic category has no reliable analogy to other crisis types and requires its own framework (the COVID special post covers this).

**Key matrix insights:**

- **Gold and USD are positive in almost every crisis type.** The exceptions: pandemics create USD ambiguity (both safe-haven and risk-off dynamics compete), and oil pandemics create oil demand collapse that overrides geopolitical safe-haven bids.

- **EM equities are negative in almost every crisis type.** The 3-5% EM equity drawdown in "minor" geopolitical events is structural, not accidental — EM markets are disproportionately exposed to energy costs, commodity price volatility, and capital flow sensitivity.

- **Oil is the most directionally uncertain.** War and sanctions involving oil producers drive oil UP; pandemics drive oil DOWN; trade wars can go either way. The oil direction in any event is primarily determined by whether supply or demand is affected.

## The Political Move Decoder

![Political move decoder table showing each action type and which assets move which direction with magnitudes](/imgs/blogs/geopolitical-crises-and-markets-the-complete-playbook-capstone-4.png)

This decoder table is the fastest-access reference for practitioners. When a political move is announced, look up the row and read the expected directional signals across all five asset classes.

**The decoder's most useful rows:**

**Energy Sanctions** (Russia-Ukraine, Iran nuclear, Venezuela): Oil up strongly (+10-40%), gold up (+2-5%), USD up, EM bonds widen, target equities down severely. This is the most market-impactful single political move in the modern era.

**IMF Program Approval**: The only political move that creates immediate positive signals across bonds AND equities. The "IMF stamp of approval" — 100-150bp spread tightening, 5-10% equity rally — is the most reliable short-term EM long signal. Every other political move creates mixed or negative signals for EM assets.

**Democratic Recovery Election Win**: The second-best EM equity signal in the decoder. When a reform-oriented opposition wins a credible election in a backsliding country, equities rally 15-30% in the following month (Poland 2023 is the case study). Unlike IMF programs, this rally is sustained because it reflects fundamental improvement in institutional quality.

**Central Bank Capture**: The most severe long-term currency and bond destroyer. Turkey is the case study — once an executive captures the central bank and forces interest rates below inflation, the currency enters a multi-year destruction spiral that no defensive position can fully protect against.

## The Geopolitical Risk Hierarchy

![Geopolitical risk hierarchy five tiers from existential nuclear energy to slow-burn political backsliding](/imgs/blogs/geopolitical-crises-and-markets-the-complete-playbook-capstone-6.png)

The hierarchy orders geopolitical risks by their potential market impact, severity, and duration. Understanding where any specific event falls in the hierarchy determines the correct portfolio response.

**Tier 1: Existential (Nuclear + Energy Supply Cutoff)**

Nuclear risk is the binary event that has no precedent in the modern financial system. The post-1945 global financial architecture has never experienced nuclear weapons use; existing models (VaR, stress tests, standard scenario analysis) provide no reliable framework. The only actionable guidance: if credible nuclear posture changes occur, the risk management response is to reduce all risk assets to minimum positions and hold maximum safe havens (gold, short-dated Treasuries, CHF, JPY), regardless of valuation.

Energy supply cutoffs at a global scale (>20% of global supply) sit alongside nuclear risk because the economic consequences are comparable in magnitude to World War-level disruption. The 1973 OPEC embargo caused a US recession and 400% oil price increase; a comparable modern cutoff from major Middle Eastern producers would be similarly catastrophic.

**Tier 2: Structural (Major War + Financial Contagion)**

Wars between major economies with significant commodity production create structural repricing lasting years. The Russia-Ukraine war restructured European energy markets in 2022-2024; those structural changes are still playing out in energy policy, defense spending, and geopolitical alignment.

Financial contagion — when sovereign stress in one country spreads to multiple countries with similar characteristics — creates the most challenging investment environment. The 2010-2012 European sovereign debt crisis affected 19 eurozone members; the 1997 Asian financial crisis swept from Thailand to Korea to Indonesia to Malaysia within months.

**Tier 3: Significant (Limited War + Single Sovereign + Trade War)**

These events create real but containable market impacts. Single-country sovereign crises (Argentina, Greece, Sri Lanka) can be managed through IMF programs and creditor restructuring. Limited conventional conflicts (Nagorno-Karabakh, various African conflicts) have minimal global market impact unless they involve critical commodity producers.

Trade wars create slow-moving, cumulative damage that is easily underestimated by markets initially. The US-China trade war was consistently underpriced in 2018-2019 by investors who expected rapid resolution. Its structural impact — supply chain rewiring, tech decoupling, Vietnam/Mexico beneficiary thesis — continues to play out.

**Tier 4: Tactical (Assassination + Election Surprise + Small Economy Coup)**

The largest category of geopolitical events. As the Soleimani post demonstrates, these create spike-and-revert patterns in 90%+ of cases. The 72-hour framework applies; the mean-reversion trade is the base case.

**Tier 5: Slow-Burn (Democratic Backsliding + Cyber + Policy Creep)**

The most easily dismissed and most persistently underpriced category. Democratic backsliding in Hungary has created a 185bp structural risk premium that markets price permanently. Cyber attacks are increasing in frequency and sophistication; their economic impact is growing but still below other Tier categories. Policy creep (regulatory capture, rule-of-law erosion, property rights deterioration) creates multi-year compounding discounts.

## The Complete Practitioner Event Checklist

When any geopolitical event occurs, run through this 15-question checklist in the first 24 hours:

**Categorization (Questions 1-4):**

1. *What is the crisis type?* (Sanctions / War / Coup / Debt Crisis / Trade War / Pandemic) — determines which row of the 6×5 matrix applies
2. *Where does this sit in the risk hierarchy?* (Tier 1-5) — determines position sizing limits and appropriate urgency
3. *Which major commodity-producing countries are involved?* (oil producers, agricultural exporters, industrial metal miners) — determines energy and commodity price direction
4. *Does this event have a precedent?* (use the series case studies as analogies)

**Political Analysis (Questions 5-8):**

5. *What are the domestic political constraints on each major actor?* (elections, approval ratings, coalition pressures) — determines how constrained the response is
6. *What alliance obligations exist?* (NATO Article 5, ANZUS, bilateral defense treaties) — determines cascade probability
7. *What are the economic leverage points between actors?* (energy dependency, trade volumes, financial exposure) — determines sanction effectiveness
8. *What political move is most likely from each actor?* (apply the decoder table)

**Market Signal Assessment (Questions 9-12):**

9. *Was this event priced in advance?* (check pre-event CDS, options skew, currency levels vs. 90-day average) — determines the remaining premium
10. *Have any of the five cascade signals fired?* (nuclear posture, alliance cascade, economic decoupling, domestic political change, prior pricing) — determines tail vs. mean-reversion
11. *What is the geopolitical risk index (GPR) level?* (from Caldara-Iacoviello database) — above 200 = maximum defensive positioning
12. *What are the safe haven asset readings right now?* (gold, JPY, USD, 10yr Treasury yield, VIX) — confirms the initial market assessment

**Positioning Decisions (Questions 13-15):**

13. *What is the primary Phase 2 trade?* (directional: which asset, which direction, what size — use 30-50% of target on day 1)
14. *What is the invalidation signal?* (what would make the trade wrong — be specific)
15. *What is the exit timeline and trigger?* (spike-and-revert: exit by day 5-7; structural: hold 3-12 months with quarterly review)

## Case Study Synthesis: Russia-Ukraine as the Complete Framework

The Russia-Ukraine invasion is the most complete case study in modern geopolitical investing because it spans all five categories of the master framework, tests all the cascade signals, and demonstrates the difference between initial spike reaction and structural repricing.

![Russia Ukraine invasion asset returns by time horizon 1 week 1 month 3 months from pre-invasion baseline](/imgs/blogs/geopolitical-crises-and-markets-the-complete-playbook-capstone-5.png)

The multi-horizon return data shows the critical insight: different assets normalized at different speeds. Wheat (the most supply-disrupted commodity) stayed elevated longest. Gold, which peaked at +5.3% in week 1, had already given back most of its gains by month 3 as equity markets stabilized. Oil (+15% at week 1, +16% at month 1) remained elevated for six months due to actual supply disruption.

The pattern: safe haven assets (gold, JPY) spike then mean-revert as the immediate crisis uncertainty dissipates; commodity assets (oil, wheat) remain elevated as long as actual supply disruption persists; financial assets (EM credit, EUR/USD) normalize over months as the economic transmission channels clarify.

## Common Misconceptions — The Series-Level Correctives

After 86 posts of analysis, certain misconceptions recur across multiple crisis types. Here are the series-level correctives:

**The fundamental asymmetry:** Markets consistently underprice geopolitical tail risk in calm periods and overprice it during crisis peaks. The asymmetry is structural: during calm periods, investors extrapolate calm; during crises, they extrapolate catastrophe. Both extrapolations are wrong. The systematic investor captures the premium from providing risk transfer in both directions — selling volatility when it's elevated, buying it when it's cheap.

**Geography is not destiny in commodity markets.** The 2019 Saudi Aramco Abqaiq attack disrupted 5% of global oil supply temporarily — and oil recovered within two weeks because the strategic petroleum reserve and OPEC+ spare capacity provided a buffer. Location of the disruption matters much less than the amount of slack in the global supply system. When slack is low (2022), even modest supply disruptions create large, persistent price moves.

**Small countries create outsized financial effects.** Sri Lanka's debt crisis (\$80bn GDP) had disproportionate market impact because it tested the contagion mechanism for all frontier market debt. Ghana's default affected the template for African sovereign restructuring across 30+ countries. The size of the economy is not the primary determinant of market impact — the precedent-setting power of the event is.

**Information availability is not the bottleneck.** The Russia-Ukraine invasion was the most publicly telegraphed major geopolitical event in recent history — US intelligence declassified invasion planning weeks in advance; satellite imagery was publicly available. The bottleneck was synthesis and willingness to act. Pre-positioning requires not just information but institutional willingness to hold positions that may look wrong for weeks before the event materializes.

**The "geopolitical discount" never fully closes.** Even after a specific geopolitical event resolves, the premium demanded for exposure to affected regions and assets does not return to pre-crisis levels immediately. Russia's MOEX, Iran's financial markets, and Myanmar's stock exchange are all trading at permanent discounts relative to pre-crisis valuations that reflect the enduring governance and access risk. Practitioners who time the "discount compression" trade must recognize that full normalization may take years, not months.

## The Complete Investor Framework

The series concludes with a synthesis into a single operational framework that any investor can apply to any geopolitical event:

**1. First, classify the event** (use the crisis taxonomy: Sanctions/War/Coup/Debt/Trade/Pandemic). Each category has a default asset sensitivity profile.

**2. Second, identify the actors and their constraints** (domestic political calendar, alliance obligations, economic capacity). Constrained actors make predictable moves.

**3. Third, activate the transmission channels** (which of the six channels does this event operate through?). Each channel has a specific asset impact signature.

**4. Fourth, apply the five-signal cascade test** (nuclear risk, alliance activation, economic decoupling, domestic political change, prior pricing). 0-1 signals = mean reversion trade; 3+ signals = structural repricing.

**5. Fifth, size with modified Kelly** (quarter-Kelly, 10% portfolio cap, phase into position over 3 days).

**6. Sixth, monitor the quarterly review calendar** (for ongoing situations: IMF reviews, sanctions review dates, trade negotiation deadlines). Each review is a tradeable event.

**7. Seventh, update the framework as new information arrives** (the five-signal check runs continuously, not just on day 3). The structural vs. tactical classification can change as events evolve.

#### Worked example:

**Applying the Complete Framework: A Future Taiwan Strait Incident**

This is a synthetic worked example using the framework to pre-think a potential future scenario.

*Crisis type:* Military confrontation or blockade attempt in the Taiwan Strait.

*Actors and constraints:* China (PLA military pressure is a tool of domestic political legitimacy for Xi Jinping; economic costs of full blockade are severe for China itself, which imports 80% of its semiconductors); Taiwan (no direct military equivalent to deter China; depends on US deterrence); US (mutual defense interpretation of the Taiwan Relations Act is strategic ambiguity; presidential authorization required for military response; 2024 election cycle affects political risk appetite).

*Transmission channels activated:*
- Trade channels: Taiwan produces ~90% of advanced semiconductors (TSMC); disruption would be catastrophic for global electronics supply chains
- Sanctions channels: US would likely impose immediate comprehensive sanctions on China; China would retaliate with rare earth export controls
- Capital flow channels: Foreign capital would exit China immediately; China would likely impose capital controls preemptively
- Energy channels: China imports ~75% of its oil; a Pacific naval confrontation would affect energy supply routes

*Five-signal cascade check:*
- Nuclear: China has ~400 nuclear warheads; posture change would be a binary signal — watch PLA Second Artillery Corps movements
- Alliance: US would be obligated (under strategic ambiguity) to respond; Japan-US alliance (Treaty of Mutual Cooperation) explicitly covers Senkakus and potentially Taiwan support; Signal fires at moderate probability
- Economic decoupling: Complete by design — a Taiwan action is inherently a decoupling event
- Domestic political change: Would it affect Xi's position? Domestically, a successful "reunification" would strengthen Xi; a failed attempt would be catastrophic. This creates pressure for success, not moderation
- Prior pricing: Taiwan/China assets are somewhat priced (TSMC trades at 15x vs. 25x for non-geopolitical peers); but the full invasion scenario is not priced

Score: 3+ cascade signals. **This is a structural tail risk, not a spike-and-revert trade.**

*Asset positioning framework:*
- Reduce all Taiwan exposure (TSMC, Foxconn, Taiwan ETF) to minimum
- Reduce China equity exposure by 60-80% (CNY, MSCI China, A-shares)
- Long: NVIDIA competitors (AMD, Intel — with domestic capacity), semiconductor equipment companies with non-China exposure, defense stocks (Raytheon, Lockheed, LMT, Northrop)
- Long: Oil (supply route uncertainty), Gold (structural shock safe haven)
- Long: Japanese equities (manufacturing relocation beneficiary), Indian equities (supply chain alternative)
- Short: EUR/USD (European economy is deeply exposed to China/Taiwan semiconductor supply)

*Position sizing:* Quarter-Kelly on each leg, capped at 10% portfolio for China short and 5% for each commodity position. Total geopolitical overlay: 25-30% of portfolio.

*Invalidation:* China backs down without incident (Xi faces domestic political cost but manages it through information control); US formally signals military deterrence that proves effective; Taiwan agrees to preliminary "reunification framework" talks that reduce immediate military pressure.

## The One Framework to Remember

If the practitioner has absorbed only one thing from 86 posts, it should be this:

**Geopolitical events are almost always mean-reverting spikes — unless three cascade signals fire simultaneously: (1) Nuclear risk posture change, (2) Major alliance activation with mobilization orders, and (3) Structural economic decoupling from major trading partner.**

In 95%+ of geopolitical events since 1945, these three conditions have not simultaneously fired. The Soleimani killing: no nuclear, no NATO Article 5, no economic decoupling. The Khashoggi murder: no nuclear, no alliance, no decoupling. The 2014 Crimea annexation: no nuclear use, no NATO activation (Ukraine not in NATO), limited economic decoupling (sanctions but not full decoupling).

The exception that makes the rule: the Russia-Ukraine full-scale invasion in February 2022 came closest to firing all three — nuclear signaling occurred (Putin's nuclear threat on day 3), NATO mobilized (not Article 5 but significant positioning), and economic decoupling was explicit and structural. The 2022 Ukraine event was not a spike-and-revert; it was the rare case where the structural repricing framework applied.

Knowing when you are in the 95% (mean reversion) vs. the 5% (structural repricing) is the primary skill this series develops.

The geopolitical world will continue generating events — territorial disputes, sanctions, coups, debt crises, elections, and shocks we haven't seen before. What remains constant is the five-step chain from trigger to trade, the systematic reading of political moves, the transmission channel analysis, and the disciplined application of the five-signal framework. These tools work in every crisis because they are grounded not in specific geopolitical knowledge but in the universal logic of how political decisions under pressure create financial market signals.
