> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Major geopolitical actions are almost never bolts from the blue; states broadcast their intentions for weeks or months across six observable signal categories, and an investor who scores those signals can reprice risk days before the market does.
>
> - The six categories are diplomatic language, military positioning, legislative and regulatory telegraphing, economic pre-positioning, leadership reshuffles, and state media framing — each readable from public sources.
> - Score each category 0 to 2 and sum to a 0-to-10 threat index; below 4 is noise, 4 to 6 warrants a hedge, 7 and above warrants a real position change.
> - Russia gave roughly 90 days of visible signals before invading Ukraine on February 24, 2022 — satellite imagery, a written ultimatum to NATO, embassy drawdowns, and a state media enemy-image campaign all fired before the first missile.
> - The US-China trade war was telegraphed for seven months: the Section 301 investigation opened in August 2017, and the first tariff list did not land until March 2018.
> - The single most reliable signal is the gap between what a government says and what its press release claims — the wider that gap, the closer the action.

On November 1, 2021, a commercial satellite operator published images that any analyst with an internet connection could see: rows of Russian tanks, self-propelled artillery, and troop tents assembling near Yelnya, several hundred kilometers from the Ukrainian border. There was no announcement. No declaration of war. Just hardware, sitting in a field, photographed from orbit by a company that sells pictures to anyone who pays. Markets barely moved. Brent crude was around \$84 a barrel. European natural gas was elevated but not panicked. The S&P 500 was making new highs.

One hundred and fifteen days later, on February 24, 2022, Russia invaded. Brent spiked toward \$110, wheat futures jumped roughly 30 percent in two weeks, European gas screamed higher, and the ruble eventually collapsed toward 116 to the dollar. The market repriced a geopolitical catastrophe in 72 hours — a catastrophe that had been assembling, in plain sight, for nearly four months.

This post is about closing that gap. It teaches the discipline an intelligence analyst uses to read a country's intentions *before* it acts, translated into a framework an investor can actually run: six signal categories, a scoring system, and a threshold above which you change your portfolio. The figure below is the entire method in one picture.

Most investors react to headlines. The headline is the *last* signal, not the first — by the time a tank crosses a border or a tariff list is published, the move has already happened and the easy money is gone. The skill that separates a geopolitical analyst from a headline-reader is the ability to watch the *inputs* on the left side of that diagram, score them, and act while the rest of the market is still calling it noise.

## Foundations: The Intelligence Analyst's Approach to Political Signals

Before we get to the six categories, let us build the mental equipment from zero. If you have never thought about how analysts forecast a country's behavior, a few concepts will make everything that follows click into place. None of this requires a finance background or a security clearance — every technique here uses information that is freely, publicly available.

### What a "signal" actually is

A *signal* is any observable piece of information that shifts your estimate of what a country is likely to do next. The key word is *observable*. We are not guessing at a leader's secret thoughts. We are watching the things a state cannot easily hide because doing them requires moving physical objects, passing laws, spending money, or speaking in public.

Here is the crucial insight that makes the whole framework work: **major state actions have long lead times and visible preparations.** A country cannot invade a neighbor without first moving an army, which takes weeks and is visible from space. It cannot impose sweeping tariffs without a legal process, which is published. It cannot freeze a rival's assets without drafting the executive authority, which leaves a paper trail. It cannot rally its population behind a confrontation without first preparing them through state media, which is broadcast. The preparation *is* the signal. The action is just the moment the preparation becomes irreversible.

Contrast this with *noise* — information that feels meaningful but does not shift the real probability of action. A fiery speech with no policy behind it. A routine military exercise that happens every year. A diplomatic protest that every country files reflexively. Distinguishing signal from noise is the entire game, and it is why we score categories rather than react to any single dramatic event.

### Base rates: the discipline that keeps you honest

A *base rate* is the historical frequency of an event before you look at any current evidence. Wars are rare. Most border tensions do not become invasions. Most tariff threats end in a negotiated climbdown. Most fiery UN speeches lead to nothing. If you forget this, you will see war in every troop movement and sell your entire portfolio every time a foreign minister raises his voice — and you will lose money to a hundred false alarms for every real event you catch.

The professional discipline is to start from the base rate (most tensions fizzle) and update *only* as signals accumulate across multiple independent categories. One signal is almost always noise. Three or four signals firing together, across categories that do not usually correlate, is where the base rate stops protecting you and the probability of action becomes genuinely elevated. This is why our framework sums six categories rather than trusting any one.

### Convergence: why six categories beat one

The reason we watch six independent categories is *convergence*. Any single category can fire for innocent reasons. A military exercise might be routine. A hawkish speech might be domestic theater. A new export-control law might be ordinary bureaucratic housekeeping. But when diplomatic language hardens *and* troops mass *and* emergency legal authorities get drafted *and* state media starts constructing an enemy *and* the central bank starts hedging *and* hawks replace moderates in the cabinet — all at once, all pointing at the same target — the probability that this is coincidence collapses. Six independent witnesses telling the same story is a confession.

### What you can read, and where

Everything in this framework comes from open sources. *Open-source intelligence*, or OSINT, is information collected from publicly available material — and in the modern era it is astonishingly rich:

- **Satellite imagery** from commercial providers like Planet Labs and the European Union's Sentinel-2 program, much of it free or cheap, lets anyone watch troop movements, port activity, and stockpiling.
- **Official transcripts** — UN General Assembly speeches, foreign-ministry press conferences, the US State Department daily briefing, the Kremlin's published readouts — are posted online within hours.
- **Legislative trackers** — the US Federal Register, congressional bill databases, the EU's official journal, China's State Council announcements — publish draft and final regulations.
- **Economic data** — central-bank reserve reports, customs and trade data, currency-forward positioning — is released on regular schedules.
- **State media** — outlets like RT, TASS, China's People's Daily and Global Times, and Iran's Press TV — broadcast the narrative a government wants its population to absorb.

The information is not the bottleneck. The *framework for weighting it* is. That is what the rest of this post provides.

## Signal Category 1: Diplomatic Language Shifts

The first and earliest signal is usually verbal. Long before a country acts, it talks — and the *words it chooses* move along a predictable ladder of escalation. Reading that ladder is the most accessible analytical skill in this entire framework, because the transcripts are free and the pattern is remarkably consistent across cultures and decades.

Diplomatic language is deliberately coded. Every phrase carries a precise, conventional weight that diplomats and analysts understand even when the public does not. The progression typically runs:

1. **"We are concerned"** / "We are monitoring the situation" — the lowest rung. This is routine. Countries express concern about almost everything. Base rate of action after this phrase: near zero.
2. **"This is unacceptable"** / "We condemn in the strongest terms" — a step up. The country is now publicly committed to a position, which raises the cost of backing down.
3. **"There will be consequences"** / "All options are on the table" — a real escalation. The phrase "all options" is diplomatic code that explicitly includes military force. When a foreign ministry says this, it is signaling that it has decided to act and is now preparing its audience.
4. **"We reserve the right to..."** / "We have warned repeatedly and our patience is not unlimited" — the final verbal rung before action. This is a country building its public justification for what it has already decided to do.

The single most powerful diplomatic signal is the **written demand**. Verbal complaints are cheap and deniable. A formal, written list of demands published for the world to see is a country putting its credibility on the line. In December 2021, Russia did exactly this: it published two draft treaties demanding that NATO roll back its presence in Eastern Europe and rule out Ukrainian membership — demands the alliance could not possibly accept. Analysts who understood the language recognized this immediately as a *pretext-builder*: an offer engineered to be refused, so that the refusal could later justify action.

There are three further verbal tells worth learning, because they recur across decades and cultures. The first is the **recall of an ambassador**. Withdrawing a country's top diplomat "for consultations" is a graduated escalation: a temporary recall is a warning shot, while a permanent recall or the expulsion of the *other* country's ambassador signals that normal channels are closing. China recalled diplomats and downgraded contact with Australia in 2020 before the trade measures landed — a textbook sequence. The second tell is the **shift from bilateral to unilateral language**: when a government stops saying "we are working with our partners on a solution" and starts saying "we will defend our interests by whatever means necessary," it has decided it no longer needs the cover of allies, which usually means it has decided to act alone. The third is the **deadline**. Diplomats almost never set hard public deadlines unless they intend to enforce them, because a missed deadline destroys credibility. A published deadline attached to a demand is among the strongest verbal signals in the entire framework.

A subtle but important refinement: read the *audience*, not just the words. The same hardline statement carries different weight depending on whom it is aimed at. A leader speaking to a domestic rally may be performing toughness for internal political reasons with no intention to act externally — this is cheap theater. The same words delivered in a formal diplomatic note, at the UN, or in a written communiqué to the target country are far more committing, because they bind the speaker in front of an international audience that will hold them to it. An analyst learns to discount domestic-audience bluster and to weight international-audience commitments heavily.

#### Worked example: Scoring diplomatic language

Suppose you are tracking Country A's rhetoric toward Country B over eight weeks. You read every foreign-ministry transcript and assign each statement a rung from 1 (concern) to 4 (final warning). Here is the path you observe:

- Weeks 1-2: "We are watching developments closely." Rung 1.
- Weeks 3-4: "These actions are unacceptable and provocative." Rung 2.
- Weeks 5-6: "All options remain on the table." Rung 3.
- Weeks 7-8: A formal written demand is published, with a stated deadline. Rung 4.

To convert this into a 0-to-2 category score for our threat index, we map the *peak rung reached plus the speed of escalation*. Reaching rung 4 in eight weeks is fast and complete, so this category scores the maximum **2 out of 2**. Had the rhetoric stalled at rung 2 ("unacceptable") and stayed there, the score would be **1**. Had it never left rung 1, the score would be **0**. The eight-week climb from "watching" to a written ultimatum with a deadline is, on its own, worth a hard look — and it is exactly the path Russia walked in late 2021.

The intuition to remember: **words are the cheapest signal, so a country only climbs the verbal ladder when it intends to use the credibility it is spending.** A government that escalates its language and then has to back down loses enormous face. So when the language ladder is climbed deliberately and publicly, the country has usually already decided to act — it is preparing its audience, not bluffing.

## Signal Category 2: Military Positioning and Kinetic Indicators

Words can be theater. Moving an army cannot. Military positioning is the most expensive and least deniable signal a country can send, which makes it the most reliable — and thanks to commercial satellites, it is now visible to anyone.

The physical indicators an analyst watches:

- **Troop concentrations near a flashpoint.** Armies are enormous logistical organisms. You cannot assemble 100,000 soldiers, their armor, fuel, and ammunition near a border in secret. Commercial imagery from Planet Labs or the free Sentinel-2 feed reveals tent cities, vehicle parks, and supply depots that were not there a month ago.
- **Field hospitals and blood supplies.** This is the signal analysts treat as nearly decisive. Routine exercises do not require field hospitals stocked with blood. When imagery and reporting show medical units and blood-supply staging near a buildup, the preparation is for casualties, not maneuvers. Reporting on Russian field-hospital deployments in early 2022 was, for many analysts, the moment the probability tipped from "likely coercion" to "likely invasion."
- **Naval movements toward a flashpoint.** Carrier groups, amphibious assault ships, and submarine deployments toward a contested zone signal intent and reposition the escalation ladder.
- **Surprise or out-of-cycle exercises.** Militaries exercise on predictable schedules. An exercise announced suddenly, at an unusual time, near a sensitive border, is often a cover story for a buildup — "we are just exercising" is the standard pretext for assembling forces.
- **Reserve call-ups and mobilization.** Calling reservists to active duty is economically and politically costly. A government only does it when it expects to need the manpower.
- **Logistics tells.** Removal of unit identification markings, the staging of fuel and pontoon-bridging equipment, and the forward-positioning of electronic-warfare assets are all preparations specific to offensive action, not defense.

The discipline here is to weight *capability plus configuration*, not just headcount. Troops dug into defensive positions are a different signal than troops staged in offensive formations with bridging equipment and field hospitals. A defensive posture can hold for years; an offensive posture has a short shelf life because armies cannot sit fueled-and-loaded indefinitely. This last point is one of the most useful timing tools in the framework: once an offensive posture is fully assembled — troops loaded, fuel staged, medical units forward — there is a *window* during which action is likely, because the force degrades and the cost of standing down rises with every passing week. An army held at full offensive readiness is expensive, exhausting for the troops, and politically awkward to disband without using. The configuration therefore tells you not just *whether* but *roughly when*.

There is also a maritime dimension that energy and shipping investors must watch. Movements toward chokepoints — the Strait of Hormuz, the Bab-el-Mandeb, the Taiwan Strait, the Black Sea — matter disproportionately because a fraction of world trade or oil passes through narrow waters. A naval buildup near Hormuz is a direct input to the oil risk premium regardless of whether shots are ever fired, because the *option* on disruption alone reprices crude and tanker rates. Insurance markets often move first here: a spike in war-risk insurance premiums for vessels transiting a contested strait is itself a tradeable signal that the professionals closest to the risk are pricing a higher probability of disruption.

One caution specific to this category: imagery requires interpretation, and interpretation is where analysts go wrong. A tent city could be an exercise. Vehicles in a field could be in storage. The discipline is to look for the *combination* of indicators that defense alone cannot explain — offensive formations *and* bridging equipment *and* field hospitals *and* removed unit markings — rather than reading invasion into any single photograph. A buildup that lacks the logistics of an offensive (no fuel staging, no medical units, no bridging) is more likely coercion or genuine defense, and should be scored lower.

#### Worked example: Sizing a position from a military signal

Suppose satellite imagery confirms a large offensive buildup near a major oil-transit chokepoint, and your diplomatic-language tracker is also at rung 3. You judge a supply disruption is now meaningfully more likely. You want to express this without betting the farm, because the base rate says most buildups do not become disruptions.

Say your portfolio is \$1,000,000, currently with no energy hedge. Brent is at \$80. You estimate a 25 percent probability of a disruption that would spike Brent to \$110 (a \$30, or 37.5 percent, move) within 60 days, and a 75 percent probability of no disruption, in which case a long-dated call option you buy expires worthless.

You allocate 1.5 percent of the portfolio — \$15,000 — to two-month Brent call options struck near \$85. The expected value is: 0.25 × (payoff if it spikes) − 0.75 × (\$15,000 premium lost). If the spike to \$110 makes your \$15,000 of calls worth roughly \$75,000, expected value is 0.25 × \$75,000 − 0.75 × \$15,000 = \$18,750 − \$11,250 = **+\$7,500**, a positive-expectancy hedge that also protects your equity book if energy-driven inflation hits stocks. The military signal did not tell you to go all-in; it told you the probability had shifted enough to make a small, asymmetric bet worth its premium. That is the correct response to a single strong signal: a hedge, not a lurch.

## Signal Category 3: Legislative and Regulatory Telegraphing

The third category is the most overlooked by investors and the most beloved by analysts, because it is the most legible: countries write down their intentions in the form of laws, executive orders, and regulatory frameworks, and they publish them.

Before a state can wield an economic weapon, it usually needs legal authority. Building that authority leaves a public trail:

- **Emergency economic powers.** In the United States, the *International Emergency Economic Powers Act* (IEEPA) lets a president declare a national emergency and then regulate or freeze foreign assets and transactions. The *declaration of a national emergency* with respect to a country is a flashing signal — it is the legal precondition for sanctions, asset freezes, and import bans. The declaration itself is published in the Federal Register before the punitive action follows.
- **Trade-investigation openings.** A *Section 301 investigation* (into unfair foreign trade practices) or a *Section 232 investigation* (into imports that threaten national security) is the legal runway for tariffs. Opening one is a months-ahead telegraph that tariffs are coming, because the law requires the investigation to conclude before duties can be imposed.
- **Export-control framework updates.** Additions to entity lists, new licensing requirements, and amendments to export-administration regulations signal that a technology-denial campaign is being built. The October 2022 US semiconductor export controls were preceded by months of rule-drafting and entity-list expansion.
- **Asset-seizure enabling laws.** Legislation that authorizes the confiscation (not just freezing) of foreign state assets is a strong signal of intent to escalate economic warfare.

The monitoring tool is concrete and free: the **US Federal Register** publishes proposed and final rules, executive orders, and emergency declarations daily and is fully searchable. Other jurisdictions have equivalents — the EU's *Official Journal*, the UK's legislation portal, China's State Council and Ministry of Commerce announcements. An analyst sets up alerts on these and treats a new emergency declaration or investigation opening as a category-3 signal firing.

The critical analytical point: **legislation is slow, which is exactly what makes it valuable as an early warning.** A law cannot be passed overnight. The deliberateness that frustrates a government in a hurry is a gift to the analyst, because it stretches the action's preparation across a window you can see and act within.

A few refinements sharpen this category. First, distinguish *enabling* from *triggering* legislation. An enabling law or emergency declaration creates the *authority* to act but does not commit the government to using it — it is a loaded weapon, not a fired one. A triggering action (the actual sanctions designation, the published tariff list, the entity-list addition) is the weapon firing. The gap between the two is the analyst's window: the United States declared the legal emergency and built the IEEPA authority well before specific designations followed in many sanctions programs. Watching the enabling step lets you position before the triggering step.

Second, watch for *consultation and comment periods*. Many regulatory actions require a public comment window before they take effect. A proposed rule with a 60-day comment period is a published countdown — the action is coming, and you know roughly when. Trade associations and law firms publish detailed analyses of these proposed rules, which an investor can read for free.

Third, pay attention to *appropriations and budget lines*. Funding a new sanctions-enforcement office, expanding an export-control bureau's headcount, or adding an emergency defense-spending supplement are budgetary signals that the machinery for an action is being built. Governments fund what they intend to do. A sudden defense-budget supplement or a surge in funding for an enforcement body is a quiet but reliable telegraph.

#### Worked example: Pricing a tariff pass-through cost

Legislative signals are not just early warnings of *whether* something happens — they let you estimate the *cost* once it does. Suppose a Section 301 investigation has concluded and a 25 percent tariff is announced on a category of imports. You hold a stock — call it RetailCo — that imports \$2 billion of goods annually from the targeted country, and these imports are 40 percent of its cost of goods sold.

The tariff adds 25 percent to the landed cost of those imports: 0.25 × \$2,000,000,000 = **\$500 million** in new annual cost. RetailCo's pre-tariff operating profit is \$1.2 billion. If it can pass through half the tariff to consumers via price increases, it absorbs \$250 million, cutting operating profit to \$950 million — a 21 percent hit to operating profit. If competitive pressure forces it to absorb the whole \$500 million, profit falls to \$700 million, a 42 percent hit. With the stock trading at 15 times earnings, a 21-to-42 percent profit cut implies a structural repricing of roughly 20 to 40 percent in the share — *if* the market has not already priced it.

The signal that gave you this lead time was the Section 301 investigation opening *months* before the tariff. An analyst tracking the Federal Register saw it coming, modeled the pass-through, and was positioned before the announcement. The lesson: a legislative signal is both a timing alarm and a quantitative input.

## Signal Category 4: Economic Pre-Positioning

The fourth category is the subtlest and, for an investor, often the most lucrative, because economic pre-positioning is a government betting its own money on its own intentions. When a state expects to do something disruptive, it quietly protects itself against the consequences first — and those defensive moves are visible in economic data.

What to watch:

- **FX reserve restructuring.** A country planning to absorb sanctions will shift its reserves out of vulnerable forms. Russia spent the years before 2022 cutting the dollar share of its reserves and building gold and yuan holdings — a multi-year tell that it was preparing for financial confrontation, even if the exact timing was unknown.
- **Strategic stockpiling.** A country expecting a trade disruption or its own retaliation buys ahead. China's large grain and soybean purchases ahead of agricultural-tariff escalations, and any nation's accelerated stockpiling of energy, food, or critical inputs, signal that it expects supply to tighten.
- **State-actor currency hedging.** When state enterprises or a central bank suddenly increase currency-forward hedging, they are insuring against a move they expect.
- **Out-of-cycle policy-rate changes.** A central bank hiking or cutting rates ahead of a known event, or building a liquidity buffer, can signal preparation for a shock.
- **State-enterprise restructuring.** Reorganizing strategic companies — moving assets out of reach, consolidating control — often precedes a confrontation that the government expects to invite retaliation.

The chart below shows how, in the Russia-Ukraine case, the broad geopolitical-risk gauge and the count of active signals climbed in lockstep through the buildup — economic pre-positioning being one of the signals quietly firing alongside the visible military ones.

![Line chart of the geopolitical risk index rising alongside the count of active signals from October 2021 to February 2022](/imgs/blogs/how-to-read-a-countrys-political-signals-before-it-acts-5.webp)

The analytical edge here is that economic pre-positioning is *self-interested and therefore honest*. A government can lie in a speech for free. Restructuring reserves, stockpiling commodities, and hedging currency exposure all cost real money and impose real opportunity costs. A state only pays those costs when it genuinely expects to need the protection. That makes economic pre-positioning one of the highest-quality signals in the framework — though also one of the slowest, since these moves unfold over months.

#### Worked example: Reserve drawdown to currency move to position size

Suppose you monitor Country C's central bank and observe its FX reserves drawing down sharply — from \$3.2 trillion to \$3.0 trillion over three months, a \$200 billion drop — at the same time its diplomatic language has reached rung 3 and state media has turned hostile toward a trading partner. A reserve drawdown of this speed often signals the central bank is defending its currency against capital flight ahead of an expected confrontation, or pre-positioning for one.

You judge that if the confrontation escalates, the currency (call it the CNX) could depreciate 8 percent against the dollar over the following quarter as capital exits. You want to express a short-CNX view sized to the conviction. Your conviction is moderate — three signals firing, threat score around 6 — so you risk 2 percent of a \$1,000,000 portfolio, \$20,000, on the trade.

Using a currency forward with 10-to-1 effective leverage, \$20,000 of margin controls \$200,000 of notional CNX exposure. An 8 percent depreciation on \$200,000 notional yields 0.08 × \$200,000 = **\$16,000** profit, an 80 percent return on the \$20,000 risked. If the confrontation fizzles and the CNX is flat to up 2 percent, you lose roughly \$4,000 plus carry. The reserve-drawdown signal sized the bet: a moderate threat score justified a 2 percent risk allocation, not a 10 percent one. Pre-positioning by the state told you to pre-position yourself.

#### Worked example: Reading a stockpiling signal into a commodity trade

Stockpiling is one of the cleanest economic signals because it directly tells you what the state expects to become scarce. Suppose you observe that Country E — a major agricultural importer — has accelerated its grain purchases far beyond seasonal norms, buying an extra 10 million tonnes over two months ahead of an escalating trade dispute with a key supplier. The state is insuring itself against a supply cutoff it expects to trigger or suffer.

You reason that if the dispute escalates into agricultural tariffs or an export ban, grain prices will rise. Wheat is trading at \$6.00 a bushel. You estimate a 30 percent probability of a disruption that takes wheat to \$8.00 (a 33 percent move) within three months, and a 70 percent probability of no disruption.

You buy three-month wheat call options struck at \$6.50, allocating \$10,000 of premium on a \$1,000,000 portfolio (1 percent). If wheat reaches \$8.00, the in-the-money calls could be worth roughly \$45,000; if no disruption, the premium expires worthless. Expected value: 0.30 × (\$45,000 − \$10,000) + 0.70 × (−\$10,000) = 0.30 × \$35,000 − \$7,000 = \$10,500 − \$7,000 = **+\$3,500**. The state's stockpiling told you both the *direction* (grain scarcity) and gave you the *lead time* to buy options while they were still cheap, before the disruption was priced. When a government buys ahead, the analyst buys the option on the same scarcity.

## Signal Category 5: Leadership Reshuffles

The fifth category is about *people*. A country's intentions are encoded in who it puts in charge. When hawkish figures replace moderates in the ministries that matter — defense, foreign affairs, finance, intelligence — the country is staffing for the action it intends to take.

The personnel signals an analyst watches:

- **Hawks replacing moderates in key portfolios.** A new defense or foreign minister with a documented hardline record is a signal that policy is about to harden. Governments choose ministers who will execute the policy they have already decided on.
- **Centralization of decision-making.** When a leader sidelines the cabinet and concentrates decisions in a small inner circle of loyalists, the off-ramps that institutions normally provide disappear, and the probability of a dramatic unilateral action rises.
- **Purges and loyalty tests.** Firing officials who counsel restraint, or promoting those who counsel confrontation, removes the internal brakes on escalation.
- **Central-bank leadership changes.** In the economic sphere, replacing an orthodox central banker with one willing to subordinate monetary policy to political goals signals a willingness to absorb economic pain for political ends — Turkey's repeated central-bank governor firings are the canonical example, each one preceding a lira crisis.
- **Military-command reshuffles.** Promoting commanders associated with offensive doctrine, or reorganizing the chain of command toward a specific theater, signals operational intent.

The data here is public: cabinet appointments, ministerial changes, and senior military promotions are announced through official government records and reported widely. The analyst's job is to *read the record of the people being appointed* — a defense minister's published views, a central banker's track record, an intelligence chief's institutional loyalties — and infer the policy they were chosen to execute.

The intuition: **personnel is policy.** A leader does not appoint a hawk to a key ministry and then pursue a dovish policy. The appointment *is* the decision, made visible. By the time a hardliner is installed in defense or foreign affairs, the policy direction is set; the analyst who reads the appointment correctly has weeks of lead time before that policy produces a market-moving action.

#### Worked example: Quantifying a reshuffle signal into a threat-score increment

Reshuffle signals are qualitative, so the discipline is to convert them into a defensible 0-to-2 increment with explicit criteria. Score 0 if appointments are routine or moderate; 1 if one key portfolio (defense, foreign affairs, finance, or intelligence) goes to a documented hardliner; 2 if two or more key portfolios go to hardliners *or* decision-making visibly centralizes around a confrontation-minded inner circle.

Suppose over six weeks Country D appoints a hawkish defense minister (known for advocating a forward military posture) and simultaneously fires its orthodox finance minister, replacing him with a loyalist who has signaled willingness to use the economy as a weapon. Two key portfolios, both to hardliners: this category scores **2 of 2**. Combined with a diplomatic-language score of 2 and an economic-pre-positioning score of 1, the running threat index is already at 5 of 10 from just three categories — enough to begin hedging before the military or legislative signals have even fired. The reshuffle, scored rigorously, was an early contributor to a threat index that crossed the action threshold ahead of the headlines.

## Signal Category 6: State Media Framing

The sixth and final category is the one that ties the others together: state media. Before a government acts against another country, it almost always prepares its own population first — because a confrontation requires public support, or at least public acquiescence. That preparation is broadcast, and it follows a recognizable arc.

![Pipeline of state media framing escalating from neutral coverage through concerns criticism enemy image to justified action](/imgs/blogs/how-to-read-a-countrys-political-signals-before-it-acts-4.webp)

The escalation arc, which analysts call *enemy-image construction*, runs through stages:

1. **Neutral coverage.** The target country is reported on factually, as a normal counterpart.
2. **Concerns raised.** State outlets begin highlighting the target's "provocations," "threats," or "interference." The framing shifts from neutral to wary.
3. **Official criticism.** Coverage becomes openly hostile. The target is blamed for problems, its motives are portrayed as malign, and officials are quoted condemning it.
4. **Enemy image.** The target is dehumanized or cast as an existential threat — historical grievances are revived, the target's people or leadership are demonized, and the confrontation is framed as defensive and necessary.
5. **Action justified.** The narrative is now primed so that when the action comes, the domestic audience receives it as a justified response rather than an aggression.

This arc takes weeks to months, and it is fully observable. Monitoring outlets like RT and TASS (Russia), People's Daily and Global Times (China), and Press TV (Iran) lets an analyst watch the domestic narrative shift in real time. The transition from stage 2 to stage 4 is the high-value window: by the time state media is constructing a full enemy image, the action it justifies is usually close.

A second, related signal is **domestic political pressure forcing external action.** When a government faces internal trouble — economic crisis, protests, a legitimacy problem — state media sometimes manufactures an external enemy to rally the population and redirect anger. An analyst watches for the coincidence of domestic difficulty and a sudden hostile media turn against a foreign target; this *diversionary* pattern has preceded many confrontations.

#### Worked example: Quantifying a media sentiment shift

State-media signals can be made quantitative by tracking the share of coverage that is hostile toward the target over time. Suppose you sample 100 articles per week from a state outlet and code each as neutral, critical, or hostile (enemy-image). Over six weeks you observe:

- Week 1: 85 neutral, 12 critical, 3 hostile. Hostile share = 3 percent.
- Week 3: 50 neutral, 35 critical, 15 hostile. Hostile share = 15 percent.
- Week 5: 20 neutral, 40 critical, 40 hostile. Hostile share = 40 percent.

The hostile share rose from 3 percent to 40 percent in five weeks — a 13-fold increase — and the outlet has clearly moved from stage 1 to stage 4 on the enemy-image arc. To score this for the threat index: a hostile share that crosses roughly 30 percent, having climbed steeply from a low base, scores the maximum **2 of 2**. A modest rise that stalls in the "critical" band scores **1**. Stable, neutral coverage scores **0**. The 13-fold jump is a category-6 maximum, and it is the kind of shift that, combined with the other categories, pushes a threat index decisively into action territory. Crucially, this is measurable from public broadcasts — you do not need a source inside the government, only a disciplined content count.

## Synthesizing Signals: Building Your Political Threat Dashboard

We now have six categories, each scored 0 to 2. Summing them produces a **threat index from 0 to 10**. This is the dashboard that turns scattered observations into a single, actionable number — and the matrix below shows how different real events lit up different combinations of categories.

![Matrix of six signal categories against four geopolitical events showing which signals fired strong weak or absent for each](/imgs/blogs/how-to-read-a-countrys-political-signals-before-it-acts-3.webp)

The scoring rubric, made explicit so it is repeatable:

- **0** — the category shows nothing unusual; baseline behavior.
- **1** — the category shows elevated but ambiguous activity; could be noise.
- **2** — the category shows a strong, unambiguous signal consistent with imminent action.

Sum the six for the threat index, then map it to action:

- **0 to 3 (low).** Noise. The base rate dominates. Take no portfolio action; keep monitoring.
- **4 to 6 (elevated).** Real but uncertain. Begin *hedging* — small, asymmetric, cheap protection (out-of-the-money options, a modest safe-haven tilt). You are insuring, not betting.
- **7 to 10 (high).** Action is likely. Make a genuine *position change* — meaningful exposure to the assets that will move (energy, gold, volatility, currency), sized to conviction, with a clear invalidation level.

The matrix above reveals a vital subtlety: **the categories that fire differ by event type.** Russia's invasion lit up all six. The US-China trade war lit up legislative, economic, and media strongly but left the military category dark — wars and trade wars have different signatures. China's pressure on Australia fired diplomatic, legislative, and media signals without any military buildup. An analyst must therefore read the *pattern*, not just the sum: a high score driven by military and field-hospital signals points to kinetic conflict and an energy/defense trade; a high score driven by legislative and economic signals points to trade or financial warfare and a supply-chain/currency trade.

#### Worked example: Scoring Russia, February 2022, to a maximum threat index

Let us score the Russia situation as it stood in mid-February 2022, applying the rubric category by category:

- **Diplomatic language: 2.** A written ultimatum to NATO with implicit deadlines — rung 4 reached. Maximum.
- **Military positioning: 2.** Over 100,000 troops in offensive configuration, field hospitals and blood supplies staged, naval assets repositioned. Maximum.
- **Legislative/regulatory: 1.** Recognition of the breakaway regions provided a legal pretext, though the buildup was driven more by other categories. Elevated.
- **Economic pre-positioning: 2.** Years of de-dollarizing reserves, gold accumulation, and a war chest built specifically to absorb sanctions. Maximum.
- **Leadership/decision-making: 2.** Decision visibly centralized in a small inner circle; restraining voices absent. Maximum.
- **State media framing: 2.** A months-long enemy-image campaign casting Ukraine as a threat requiring "denazification." Maximum.

Sum: 2 + 2 + 1 + 2 + 2 + 2 = **11**, capped at the maximum of **10**. This is the highest possible reading. An analyst running this dashboard in mid-February 2022 would have been at the top of the action band — not hedging, but holding a real position in energy, wheat, gold, and volatility, with safe-haven currency exposure, *before* February 24. The market, anchored on the base rate that buildups usually do not become invasions, was still at \$84 Brent. The dashboard said otherwise.

### False positives and how to discount them

The honest analyst must address the framework's weakness: it will sometimes flash high and nothing will happen. Coercive diplomacy frequently *uses* these signals deliberately — a country masses troops, hardens its language, and runs a media campaign specifically to extract concessions *without* intending to act. The 2021 buildup itself was read by many as exactly this kind of bluff, right up until it was not.

Three discounts keep you honest:

1. **Reversibility check.** Signals that are cheap to reverse (rhetoric, a brief exercise) deserve a discount. Signals that are expensive and hard to reverse (field hospitals, reserve restructuring, reservist call-ups) deserve full weight. Coercion relies on reversible signals; commitment requires irreversible ones.
2. **Domestic-cost check.** Ask what the government loses if it backs down now. The more public credibility it has spent, the less likely the signals are a bluff, because climbing down becomes politically devastating.
3. **Position for the false positive.** Because false positives happen, the *elevated* band (4 to 6) calls for cheap, asymmetric hedges that lose little if nothing happens. You only commit real capital at 7-plus, where the irreversible signals dominate and the cost of being wrong is justified by the probability of being right.

## Case Study: Russia's 2022 Invasion — 90 Days of Visible Signals

Let us walk the full timeline as an analyst would have experienced it, to see the framework in motion.

**November 2021 (military fires).** Commercial satellite imagery shows large troop concentrations assembling far from the border — Yelnya and other staging areas. This is the first hard signal. Category score: military jumps to 1, then 2 as the buildup grows. Threat index: ~3. An analyst notes it but the base rate (buildups as coercion) still dominates. Brent ~\$84.

**December 2021 (diplomatic and media fire).** Russia publishes draft treaties demanding NATO rollback — a written ultimatum engineered to be refused. State media intensifies the "Ukraine as threat" framing. Diplomatic language hits rung 3-4; media moves to stage 3-4. Threat index climbs toward 6. Now an analyst begins hedging: cheap energy calls, a modest gold tilt.

**January 2022 (economic and personnel fire).** Embassy staff drawdowns by Western nations (themselves a signal that *their* analysts had scored the threat high), reports of field-hospital and blood-supply staging, and the visible centralization of decision-making in a small circle. Economic pre-positioning — years of de-dollarized reserves — is now contextually obvious. Threat index toward 8-9. The hedges become positions.

**February 15-23, 2022 (the tell).** Russia announces a partial pullback, but commercial imagery shows the buildup *continuing and intensifying* — the gap between the statement and the satellite is itself a maximum-strength signal. On February 21, Russia recognizes the breakaway regions, providing the legal pretext. Threat index: 10. The dashboard is screaming. Brent is still only around \$96.

**February 24, 2022 (the action).** Invasion at dawn. Brent toward \$110, wheat +30 percent in two weeks, European gas to multiples of pre-war levels, ruble eventually to 116. The market repriced in 72 hours what the dashboard had been flagging for 90 days.

The lead-time point is the whole lesson. The chart below ranks ten real events by how many days passed between the *first* detectable signal and the market-moving action.

The longer the legal and logistical runway an action requires, the more lead time the analyst gets. Wars and tariffs — the events with the longest preparation — are exactly the ones the framework catches earliest.

## Case Study: US-China Trade War — 7 Months of Regulatory Telegraph

The trade war shows the framework working on a non-military event, where the legislative category does the heavy lifting.

**August 2017 (legislative fires).** The US Trade Representative opens a *Section 301 investigation* into China's intellectual-property and technology-transfer practices. By law, tariffs cannot be imposed until the investigation concludes — so this single, public action is a months-ahead telegraph. An analyst tracking trade policy scores the legislative category 2 immediately. Diplomatic language is already hostile (rung 2-3); media framing on both sides is turning. Threat index: ~5. Begin hedging exposure to China-import-dependent companies and to Chinese equities.

**Late 2017 to early 2018 (escalation).** The investigation proceeds publicly. Rhetoric hardens further. China begins economic pre-positioning — notably, buying agricultural goods ahead of the retaliation it anticipates. Both sides' state media construct the trade conflict as the other's fault. Threat index climbs toward 7.

**March 2018 (the action).** The first tariff list is announced, and the trade war is formally underway. Markets that had been complacent reprice trade-exposed sectors. But the seven-month telegraph meant an analyst running the dashboard was positioned long before — short the most import-dependent margins, underweight the most exposed Chinese exporters, and watching for the supply-chain rerouting trade (Vietnam, Mexico) that would follow.

The contrast with Russia is instructive. The trade war's military category never fired — there was no army to watch. The signal came almost entirely from the legislative category (the 301 investigation), supported by diplomatic, economic, and media signals. This is why the framework sums *six* categories: different events fire different subsets, and an analyst who only watched for tanks would have missed the entire trade war.

## Case Study: China and Australia — Sanctions by Sequence

The third case shows the framework working on an economic-coercion campaign with no military component at all, and it illustrates the *sequencing* tell that precedes targeted trade measures.

Through 2020, relations between China and Australia deteriorated after Australia called for an independent inquiry into the origins of the COVID-19 pandemic. An analyst running the dashboard would have watched a clear progression. **Diplomatic signals fired first:** China downgraded ministerial contact, recalled and reduced diplomatic engagement, and its embassy circulated a list of grievances to journalists — a written set of complaints functioning as a quasi-ultimatum. **State media fired hard:** outlets ran sustained hostile coverage casting Australia as a hostile, US-aligned actor, moving rapidly up the enemy-image arc. **Legislative and regulatory machinery engaged:** China's customs and commerce authorities opened anti-dumping and biosecurity reviews — the bureaucratic runway for trade measures, exactly analogous to a Section 301 investigation.

Then the measures landed *in sequence*, not all at once: barley tariffs, then restrictions on beef, wine, coal, cotton, lobster, and timber rolled out over months. The sequencing itself is the signal — a government testing the target's response and the international reaction at each step, escalating where it meets little resistance. An analyst who saw the diplomatic recall, the media campaign, and the regulatory reviews converge had a window of roughly four months (the lead time shown earlier for the barley tariff) to position: short the most China-dependent Australian exporters in the targeted categories, and watch for the *rerouting* trade as Australian commodities found new buyers and other suppliers filled the Chinese gap.

The threat-index pattern here was distinctive: diplomatic, legislative, and media categories scored high while military scored zero. That pattern *itself* told the analyst this was economic coercion, not conflict — pointing to a commodity and equity trade, not an energy-and-volatility trade. The framework does not just say "something is coming"; the firing pattern tells you *what kind* of something, and therefore which assets to trade.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Geopolitical events are unpredictable black swans."** This is the most expensive myth in markets. True surprises — events with no detectable preparation — are rare. The events that move markets most (wars, sanctions regimes, trade wars) require the most preparation and therefore leave the longest signal trails. Russia's invasion was visible for 90 days; the trade war for 7 months. They felt like surprises only to those watching the headlines instead of the signals. What is genuinely unpredictable is the *exact timing* — but the elevated probability is readable well in advance, and that is enough to position.

**Misconception 2: "If the signals were really there, the market would already price them."** Markets are efficient at processing *published, quantitative* information and surprisingly poor at processing *slow-moving, qualitative, multi-source* signals like a media-framing shift or a legislative runway. The market anchored on \$84 Brent through Russia's entire buildup precisely because the base rate ("buildups are usually coercion") is a powerful, lazy prior. The edge in geopolitical analysis comes from doing the synthesis work the market is structurally reluctant to do until forced by a headline.

**Misconception 3: "You need a security clearance or insider sources."** Everything in this framework is open-source: commercial satellite imagery, published transcripts, the Federal Register, central-bank reserve reports, state-media broadcasts. The 2022 buildup was tracked in real time by journalists and independent analysts using free Sentinel-2 imagery and Planet Labs photos. The bottleneck is not access to secrets; it is the discipline to monitor and score public information systematically.

**Misconception 4: "A single dramatic signal means it's time to act."** No. A single signal — even a dramatic one like a troop buildup — is usually coercion or noise, and the base rate says most single signals lead nowhere. The framework's power is *convergence*: only when multiple independent categories fire together does the probability genuinely shift. Acting on one signal is how you accumulate a hundred false-alarm losses.

**Misconception 5: "The framework tells you what will happen."** It does not. It tells you the *probability has shifted* and *by how much*, which is all a probabilistic trade ever needs. You will be wrong sometimes — coercion will succeed, bluffs will be called off. That is why position sizing scales with the threat index and why the elevated band uses cheap, asymmetric hedges. The framework manages probability; it does not deliver certainty.

## How It Shows Up in Real Markets

The framework's whole purpose is the trade. Here is how a high threat index expresses itself across asset classes, with the real reactions from the cases above as evidence.

**Energy.** Military signals pointing at an oil-producing region or transit chokepoint mean a Brent risk premium. Russia's invasion took Brent from ~\$96 to ~\$110; the Iran-Israel direct exchange in April 2024 put a risk premium into crude even without a supply loss. A high military-category score near oil infrastructure is a long-energy signal.

**Safe havens.** Across major geopolitical shocks, gold has historically risen on average in the days after the event, the dollar and yen have strengthened as flight-to-quality bids, and government-bond yields have fallen as money seeks safety. A high threat index is a signal to tilt toward gold, the dollar, the yen, and duration. The relationships are covered in depth in [safe havens and the flight to quality](/blog/trading/geopolitical-crises/safe-havens-flight-to-quality-and-the-dollar-in-a-crisis).

**Volatility.** The VIX spiked roughly 31 percent around the Russia invasion and 19 percent around the October 2023 Gaza shock. A rising threat index is a signal to get long volatility cheaply *before* the spike, when implied volatility is still calm.

**Equities and emerging markets.** Risk-off repricing hits equities broadly and emerging-market assets hardest. A high threat index argues for trimming the most exposed equity and EM-FX positions and for anticipating the *rerouting trade* — the beneficiaries (Vietnam, Mexico, India in the trade-war case) of supply chains fleeing the conflict zone.

The scatter below makes the core relationship concrete: the more signal categories that fired before an event, the larger the eventual market move tended to be.

The relationship is not perfect — markets are noisy and sometimes a well-telegraphed event is already priced — but the tendency is clear and exploitable: events that lit up five or six categories (Russia's invasion, the Iran-Israel exchange) produced the largest moves, while events that lit up only two (a sudden strike with little warning) produced briefer, smaller dislocations. The threat index is, in effect, a forecast of *both* probability and magnitude.

## How to Trade It: The Playbook

Here is the operational discipline, start to finish.

**1. Build the monitoring stack.** Set up free or cheap feeds for each category: a satellite source (Sentinel-2, Planet Labs) for flashpoints you care about; transcript alerts for the relevant foreign ministries and the UN; a Federal Register alert (and EU/UK/China equivalents) for legislative signals; a calendar for central-bank reserve and trade-data releases; and a regular sampling routine for the relevant state-media outlets. The stack is the unglamorous foundation — without systematic monitoring, you are back to reacting to headlines.

**2. Score weekly.** Once a week, score each of the six categories 0 to 2 for each situation you track, and write the threat index down with the date. The written record matters: it stops you from rationalizing after the fact and lets you see the *trend* — a threat index rising from 3 to 5 to 7 over three weeks is itself the signal.

**3. Map the score to action.** Below 4, do nothing but watch. From 4 to 6, place cheap asymmetric hedges sized to ~1 to 2 percent of the portfolio — out-of-the-money options on energy and volatility, a modest gold tilt. At 7-plus, convert hedges into real positions sized to conviction, concentrated in the assets the *pattern* (not just the sum) points to: energy and volatility for kinetic signals, currency and supply-chain plays for economic/legislative signals.

**4. Read the pattern, not just the number.** A 7 driven by military and field-hospital signals is a different trade than a 7 driven by a Section 301 investigation. Let the firing categories choose the asset.

**5. Define invalidation.** Before you put on a position, write down what would prove you wrong: a genuine, imagery-confirmed pullback; a negotiated settlement; a reversal of the legislative process; a softening of the media campaign. If the irreversible signals reverse, the thesis is dead — exit. The discipline of a pre-defined invalidation is what separates a probabilistic trade from a stubborn conviction.

**6. Respect the base rate.** Most situations you score will stay below 4 and resolve into nothing. That is correct and expected. The framework's value is not in flagging every event but in catching the rare, high-conviction setups where multiple irreversible signals converge — and in sizing your response so the many false alarms cost little while the few real events pay for everything.

The deeper point, threaded through every case: **a country's most important decisions are made before they are announced, and the preparation is visible.** The market trades the announcement. The analyst trades the preparation. The gap between the two — 90 days for Russia, 7 months for the trade war — is the entire opportunity, and this framework is how you occupy it.
