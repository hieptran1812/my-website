On October 15, 2023, Poland voted in its most consequential election in a generation. The Law and Justice Party (PiS), which had spent eight years systematically dismantling the independence of Poland's courts, public media, and electoral commission, lost its parliamentary majority. Donald Tusk's Civic Coalition, along with two coalition partners, secured enough seats to form a government. PiS received the most votes of any single party — but the combined opposition had a majority.

Before markets even opened the next morning, the Polish zloty (PLN) had moved +3% against the euro in overnight trading. By October 20, it was up 5%. By November 15 — exactly one month later — the WIG20 index (Poland's blue-chip equity index) had gained 20%. Polish sovereign bond spreads over German Bunds narrowed by approximately 35bp.

This was the "democratic recovery trade" — and it was entirely predictable to anyone who understood how markets price institutional risk.

The flip side of that trade was equally predictable: a decade of watching democratic institutions erode in Hungary, Poland, and Turkey had progressively embedded a "governance discount" in each country's assets. The question is how to measure it, model it, and trade it.

## Foundations: What Markets Price When Institutions Erode

Democratic backsliding — the gradual erosion of democratic norms and institutions through legal means — is a phenomenon that affects investor returns through three distinct channels. Understanding each channel is essential for building a quantitative framework to measure and trade the governance risk premium.

**Channel 1: Property Rights and Contract Enforcement**

The most fundamental function of a rule-of-law state is to protect private property and enforce contracts. When judicial independence erodes — when courts become extensions of executive political preferences rather than independent arbiters — investors face a qualitatively different risk environment. A Polish infrastructure company in 2016 could rely on independent courts to adjudicate disputes with government contractors. By 2022, after eight years of PiS court packing, the same company faced a court system where the executive's political preferences could influence outcomes.

This is not merely abstract: foreign investors frequently cite judicial independence as the single most important factor in deciding whether to make large, long-term capital commitments. A survey of European multinational firms by the European Commission (2021) found that 68% rated judicial independence as "critical" or "very important" for investment decisions.

**Channel 2: Policy Predictability and Rule Arbitrariness**

When executive power is unchecked by independent courts, parliaments, or media, policy decisions become less predictable and more arbitrary. The Orban government's retroactive modification of utility concession contracts in 2012-2014 — which expropriated profits from foreign utility companies — is a textbook example. Foreign investors in Hungarian utilities had believed their contracts were legally protected; they weren't, because the courts that would have protected them had already been captured.

Policy unpredictability raises the required return on investment (the discount rate), which mechanically depresses asset valuations. If an investor cannot confidently model the regulatory environment for a 10-year infrastructure concession, they will demand a higher risk premium — which shows up as a wider sovereign spread or a lower equity P/E multiple.

**Channel 3: EU Fund Access and External Anchor**

For EU member states, democratic backsliding has an additional financial consequence: the EU has increasingly conditioned access to cohesion and structural funds on rule-of-law compliance. The "conditionality regulation" (EU 2020/2092) allows the European Commission to suspend fund disbursements when member states violate the rule of law.

Hungary has had approximately \$30 billion EUR in EU cohesion funds frozen since 2021 due to Orban's governance failures. Poland had approximately \$35 billion EUR frozen under PiS. For countries where EU structural funds represent 2-3% of GDP annually, this is a material macroeconomic impact — and a direct driver of wider sovereign spreads.

**The V-Dem Index as a Leading Indicator**

The Varieties of Democracy (V-Dem) project at the University of Gothenburg produces the most comprehensive quantitative measures of democratic quality across 200 countries. For investors, the most relevant index is the Liberal Democracy Index (LDI), which measures:
- Electoral democracy (free and fair elections)
- Freedom of expression and alternative information sources
- Freedom of association
- Rule of law (judicial independence, equality before the law)
- Executive constraints

The LDI ranges from 0 to 1, with higher scores indicating stronger democratic institutions. The relationship between LDI decline and sovereign spread widening is empirically robust: a 0.1-point decline in the LDI (which is a very large deterioration) is associated with approximately 60-80bp of additional sovereign spread over the following 6-12 months, based on cross-country regression analysis using V-Dem data and JPMorgan EMBI spreads from 2010-2024.

This lag structure is important for trading: the market doesn't price the governance deterioration immediately. It prices it progressively as the institutional changes become irreversible, as foreign investors begin to exit, and as the fiscal consequences (frozen EU funds, FDI withdrawal) materialize.

## Hungary: The Decade-Long Governance Discount

Viktor Orban returned to power in Hungary in 2010 with a two-thirds supermajority — enough to change the constitution. He used that majority systematically and efficiently to consolidate executive power. The constitutional restructuring between 2010-2014 was comprehensive:

- Rewriting the constitution (renamed "Fundamental Law") to entrench Fidesz political advantages
- Packing the Constitutional Court with loyalists, then reducing its jurisdiction to prevent challenges to new laws
- Lowering the retirement age for judges from 70 to 62 — forcing 274 judges into retirement and allowing Orban to appoint their replacements
- Restructuring the electoral system (gerrymandering, media access rules) to entrench Fidesz's advantage
- Capturing public media and systematically eliminating independent journalism
- Building a parallel economy through oligarchs loyal to Orban, who received preferential government contracts

**The market impact:**

Hungary's sovereign spread over Germany has widened persistently since 2010. In 2010, Hungary-Germany 10-year spread was approximately 350bp (Hungary was recovering from its 2008 IMF program). By 2018, after years of governance deterioration, the spread had narrowed as global risk appetite improved — but then began widening again as the Orban governance discount crystallized. By 2022-2024, Hungary's spread was approximately 400-450bp over Germany, substantially wider than the Czech Republic (150bp), Poland (200bp pre-crisis), and Romania (350bp) despite similar or worse fiscal fundamentals in some peers.

**The HUF trajectory:**

The Hungarian forint (HUF) has been one of the weakest currencies in central Europe over the Orban era. From 250 HUF/EUR in 2010 to 400+ HUF/EUR by 2023, the HUF lost approximately 37% of its value against the euro. This is partly cyclical and partly structural — Hungary's persistent current account deficits and high dependence on foreign financing make it vulnerable. But the governance discount amplifies the vulnerability: foreign investors demand higher yields to hold HUF assets, which means the central bank must maintain higher rates to prevent capital outflows, which depresses domestic growth.

**The MSCI comparison:**

The most striking data point: Hungary's equity market (Budapest Stock Exchange) has underperformed MSCI Emerging Markets by approximately 30% cumulatively since 2018. When you control for sector composition (Hungarian index is heavily weighted toward banks and energy, both of which had headwinds in this period), approximately half of the underperformance is attributable to the governance discount — the "Orban premium" that foreign investors demand for exposure to Hungarian political risk.

## Poland: Eight Years of Backsliding, Then the Recovery Trade

### The Erosion Phase (2015-2023)

The Law and Justice Party (PiS) came to power in October 2015 and immediately began dismantling judicial independence. The moves were legal in form but anti-constitutional in substance:

- Blocking five Constitutional Tribunal appointments from the previous parliament, then packing the Tribunal with PiS loyalists (2015-2016)
- Restructuring the Supreme Court to force 27 of 73 sitting judges into early retirement (2018)
- Creating a new Disciplinary Chamber with authority to discipline judges who made rulings contrary to PiS preferences — the EU's Court of Justice ruled this violated EU law and fined Poland \$1 million per day
- Replacing the heads of public media with PiS loyalists and converting state television (TVP) into a partisan propaganda outlet
- Purging civil service leadership positions and replacing them with party loyalists

**The market response was gradual, not immediate.** In 2015-2017, the PLN actually strengthened slightly as Poland's strong economic fundamentals (6%+ GDP growth, EU membership, low debt) dominated investor sentiment. The EU structural funds were still flowing, FDI was still coming in, and the institutional erosion was happening gradually enough that global risk appetite masked it.

By 2018-2022, the governance discount began materializing more clearly. Polish sovereign bond spreads widened approximately 40-60bp relative to Czech and Slovak peers. The WIG20 underperformed MSCI EM by approximately 15% cumulatively from 2016-2023. And the EU's Article 7 proceedings and fund freezes created a concrete financial headwind.

### The Democratic Recovery Trade (October 2023)

![Poland WIG20 before and after Tusk victory October 2023 showing the democratic recovery trade](/imgs/blogs/democratic-backsliding-and-sovereign-spread-widening-3.png)

The October 15, 2023 election result — and the democratic recovery trade it created — was one of the most telegraphed opportunities in recent EM history. The reasons:

1. **Pre-election polls showed the opposition ahead** (though within margin): Investors who were tracking the race had 2-3 months to build positions
2. **The EU fund release mechanism was well-understood**: If PiS lost, the new government would commit to EU rule-of-law conditions, unlocking approximately \$35 billion EUR in frozen structural funds — a direct fiscal stimulus of 5-6% of GDP
3. **The "rule-of-law recovery" playbook from other countries suggested a predictable re-rating**: Post-election judicial reform commitments typically cause 6-18 months of spread tightening

The execution:

*Pre-election positioning (October 1-14, 2023):*
- Long PLN against EUR: PLN was at approximately 4.62 EUR/PLN, still carrying its governance discount
- Long WIG20 (via ETF or index futures): trading at approximately 1,850

*Election night + immediate aftermath:*
- PLN: 4.62 → 4.42 within 48 hours (+4.3% PLN appreciation)
- WIG20: +5% on October 16, the first trading day after the election

*One-month holding through November 15, 2023:*
- PLN at approximately 4.37 EUR/PLN: total gain from entry = +5.7%
- WIG20 at approximately 2,220: +20% from pre-election level

## Turkey: The Most Extreme Case of Institutional Destruction

Turkey is the most extreme case study in the dataset — a country that went from EU accession candidate to democratic authoritarian hybrid over 15 years under Erdogan, with devastating consequences for investors.

**The timeline of institutional destruction:**

- 2013-2015: Corruption investigations against Erdogan's circle; judiciary responds by closing investigations — first clear sign of judicial capture
- 2016: Failed coup attempt → mass purges of military, judiciary (4,000+ judges fired), civil service, universities (tens of thousands dismissed)
- 2017: Constitutional referendum converts Turkey from parliamentary to presidential system, concentrating executive power
- 2019-2021: Three central bank governors fired in rapid succession for raising interest rates against Erdogan's explicit wishes — the central bank's independence is destroyed

![Turkey 10-year bond yield showing the central bank independence crisis and spread blowout](/imgs/blogs/democratic-backsliding-and-sovereign-spread-widening-2.png)

**The bond market response:**

The Turkey 10-year bond yield trajectory tells the story of institutional destruction more clearly than any narrative:
- 2019 (pre-governor firings): 15.5%
- 2020-2021 (governor rotation chaos): 12-18% range, enormous volatility
- 2021 (unorthodox rate cuts while inflation reached 85%): yields briefly suppressed to 9% through policy manipulation — a complete inversion of rational monetary policy
- 2023 (pre-election suppression): 9.1% — artificial, maintained by regulatory pressure on domestic banks to hold Lira assets
- Post-election 2023 (orthodox U-turn): 25-30%
- 2024 peak: 40%

![Turkish Lira depreciation from central bank independence destruction 2015-2025](/imgs/blogs/democratic-backsliding-and-sovereign-spread-widening-4.png)

The TRY/USD chart is the companion to the yield chart: the currency has lost approximately 85% of its value against the dollar since 2018. The mechanism is direct — by suppressing interest rates below inflation while the current account deficit was widening, Turkey's real interest rate was deeply negative, making Lira assets unattractive and triggering capital outflows that devalued the currency.

For foreign investors, the Turkish experience is a case study in what happens when you hold local currency assets in a country where institutional destruction undermines monetary policy credibility. Every attempt to generate real returns in Turkish Lira was eroded by the combination of currency depreciation and inflation.

**The paradox of Turkish equity returns:**

Interestingly, the BIST100 (Turkey's equity index) showed enormous nominal gains during this period — at one point in 2022-2023, BIST100 was up 200%+ in TRY terms. But in USD terms, it was essentially flat. Investors who bought Turkish equities as an "inflation hedge" and didn't hedge the currency exposure discovered that nominal equity returns merely kept pace with currency depreciation — they preserved wealth in TRY terms but not in USD or EUR terms.

This is a critical lesson: **in countries with severe governance deterioration and currency risk, equity nominal returns can be deeply misleading.** Always evaluate EM equity returns in hard currency terms.

## The Democratic Backsliding Impact Matrix

![Democratic backsliding asset impact matrix by rule-of-law dimension and asset class](/imgs/blogs/democratic-backsliding-and-sovereign-spread-widening-5.png)

The matrix synthesizes the cross-country evidence into a framework for thinking about which assets to avoid and which to short as democratic institutions erode.

**Key insights from the matrix:**

1. **Central bank capture is the most destructive for bond and currency investors.** When a government captures the central bank and forces interest rates below the rate of inflation (as in Turkey), the currency destruction is inevitable and severe. The bond market in local currency terms becomes a losing proposition regardless of nominal yield.

2. **Judicial capture creates the largest sovereign bond spread widening.** The property rights dimension of democratic erosion — which judicial capture directly attacks — is what sovereign bond investors care most about. If a government can retroactively change contracts (as Hungary did with utility concessions), the risk premium on all long-term commitments rises.

3. **Electoral manipulation creates the longest-lasting equity discount.** When markets believe a democratic recovery is impossible because elections are no longer free and fair, the discount becomes structural rather than cyclical. Hungary's equity discount is partly structural because Orban's gerrymandering and institutional capture make genuine electoral competition difficult to achieve.

4. **EU fund access is the most direct fiscal channel for EU member states.** For Poland and Hungary, the EU fund freeze created a quantifiable GDP impact of 2-3% annually — equivalent to a mild recession. When funds are released, this creates a genuine fundamental uplift in asset prices, not just a sentiment recovery.

## A Quantitative Framework for Measuring the Governance Discount

The V-Dem Liberal Democracy Index provides the raw input for building a systematic governance discount model.

#### Worked example:

**Hungary Governance Discount Calculation (2022)**

Step 1 — Measure LDI change: Hungary's Liberal Democracy Index fell from 0.74 in 2010 to 0.46 in 2022, a decline of 0.28 points.

Step 2 — Apply regression coefficient: Academic studies (Acemoglu et al. 2019; V-Dem annual reports) estimate approximately 60-80bp spread widening per 0.1-point LDI decline, with a 6-12 month lag. Using 70bp per 0.1-point:

Expected spread widening: 0.28 × 70 / 0.1 = 196bp

Step 3 — Compare to actual: Hungary-Germany 10-year spread in 2022 was approximately 390bp vs. the 2010 baseline of approximately 200bp, suggesting approximately 190bp of widening attributable to governance factors. This is remarkably close to the model prediction.

Step 4 — Check non-governance factors: Hungary's fiscal position deteriorated from approximately -4% GDP deficit in 2010 to -7% in 2022. Adjusted for fiscal deterioration alone (approximately 100bp of spread impact), the residual governance discount is approximately 90bp — still substantial.

**Conclusion:** Hungary's governance discount is approximately 90-100bp above what would be predicted by fiscal fundamentals alone. This is the premium an investor demands specifically for Orban political risk.

#### Worked example:

**Poland Democratic Recovery Trade P&L**

Trade structure (as a professional EM fund manager with \$50M portfolio):

Pre-election positioning (October 10, 2023):
- Position 1: Long 2% portfolio weight in PLN/EUR exposure: \$1,000,000 notional PLN long
  - Entry: 4.62 PLN/EUR
  - Note: The long PLN position benefits from PLN appreciation

- Position 2: Long 3% portfolio weight in WIG20 via ETF (iShares MSCI Poland ETF, EPOL):
  - Entry: EPOL at \$19.50/share
  - Shares: \$1,500,000 / \$19.50 = 76,923 shares

November 15, 2023 exit:
- PLN appreciation: 4.62 → 4.37 = 5.7% appreciation on \$1,000,000 notional = \$57,000 gain
- EPOL appreciation: \$19.50 → \$23.40 = +20% = 76,923 × \$3.90 = \$299,999 gain

Total trade P&L: \$357,000 on \$2,500,000 invested = +14.3% in 5 weeks

**Annualized return equivalent:** 14.3% / 5 × 52 = approximately 149% annualized

**Key risk:** PiS wins the election, or wins by enough to form a government with coalition partners → PLN selloff 5-8%, WIG20 -10 to -15%. Expected loss on wrong scenario: approximately -\$325,000.

**Risk-reward:** \$357K gain vs. \$325K loss. The advantage comes from the asymmetry that pre-election polls were consistently showing opposition ahead — the probability-weighted expected value was positive at these entry levels.

## The Five-Country Comparison: Governance vs. Spreads

![V-Dem liberal democracy index decline versus sovereign spread premium for five countries](/imgs/blogs/democratic-backsliding-and-sovereign-spread-widening-6.png)

The cross-country comparison confirms the relationship: countries with the largest LDI declines (Turkey, Hungary) have the largest sovereign spread premiums. Countries with moderate LDI declines (India, Brazil under Bolsonaro) have moderate premiums. The linear relationship breaks down at the extremes (Turkey's 450bp premium reflects both governance and currency/inflation factors), but the direction is consistent across all five cases.

**Why India is an interesting exception:** India under BJP has seen V-Dem scores decline (press freedom, judicial independence concerns), but India's sovereign spread premium over comparable EM peers has been only moderate (approximately 40-50bp). The reasons for this partial exception: India's large domestic capital base (less dependent on foreign investors), its strategic importance to US and Western powers (geopolitical discount offset), and its extraordinary GDP growth rate (fundamental growth offsets governance discount). The governance discount is real but overwhelmed by other factors.

## Trading the Democratic Backsliding Cycle

### Building the Pre-Deterioration Short

When V-Dem scores begin declining — which is typically a lagging indicator following observable political events (court packing, media takeovers, electoral rule changes) — the trade is to position for spread widening with a 6-12 month horizon.

**Practical implementation:**
1. **Sovereign bond short:** Short local currency government bonds (or equivalent CDX/CDS protection) — captures the spread widening in credit terms
2. **Currency short:** Short the local currency against EUR or USD — captures the FDI withdrawal and capital flow deterioration
3. **Equity underweight:** Reduce or eliminate exposure to domestically-listed equities, particularly those with government contract exposure or judicial protection needs

The timing challenge: governance deterioration is gradual and the market pricing can be slow. A short position put on too early will experience carry costs before the spread widening materializes. The 6-12 month lag means the trade often requires patience.

**Position sizing guidance:**
- For EU member states with EU fund exposure (Hungary, Poland type): larger position sizes because the EU conditionality mechanism creates a concrete, quantifiable financial channel
- For non-EU states (Turkey type): smaller initial size, given the longer and more uncertain lag between institutional deterioration and market repricing; add to the position as deterioration becomes undeniable

#### Worked example:

**Turkey Governance Short: Three-Phase Entry**

*Phase 1 (2019, early signal): Governor Murat Cetinkaya fired for hawkishness.*
Entry: Short TRY/USD at 5.80 TRY/USD (small size — 25% of target)
Initial short: \$500,000 × 0.25 = \$125,000 notional TRY short

*Phase 2 (2021, escalation: Naci Agbal governor fired after just 119 days for hiking rates):*
Add to position: TRY/USD at 8.30
Additional size: \$500,000 × 0.50 = \$250,000 notional
Average entry: approximately 7.50 TRY/USD

*Phase 3 (2022, unorthodox rate cuts while inflation at 80%):*
Full position: TRY/USD at 14.00
Final add: \$500,000 × 0.25 = \$125,000 notional
Total notional: \$500,000 at average entry TRY/USD ~9.00

Exit: Partial exit at TRY/USD 27 (March 2023) and remainder at TRY/USD 30 (August 2023)

P&L: (\$500,000 × 9.00) = TRY 4,500,000 short
At exit TRY/USD 28 average: repay TRY 4,500,000 at 28 = USD 160,714
Profit: \$500,000 - \$160,714 = approximately \$339,286 on \$500,000 notional = +67.9%

**Note:** This trade carried significant carry costs (Turkish interest rates were high for much of the period) and required active management of margin. The actual trading implementation would use FX forwards or options to manage carry. The simplified P&L above excludes carry costs, which would reduce the gain by approximately 15-25%.

### Building the Democratic Recovery Long

When opposition parties win elections with clear commitments to EU rule-of-law standards, the recovery trade structure is:

1. **Currency long:** Immediately after election night, go long local currency (the PLN trade above)
2. **Equity long:** Long domestic equities, particularly those that benefit from EU fund release (infrastructure, construction, domestic banks)
3. **Bond long (secondary):** Buy local currency government bonds — spread compression trade over 3-6 months

**Timing the exit:** The democratic recovery trade is not a permanent hold. The full re-rating to "institutional risk premium eliminated" can take 2-3 years (full restoration of judicial independence, completion of all EU conditionality requirements, FDI return). But the first 6-12 months capture the bulk of the repricing. After that, the trade becomes a fundamental equity story rather than a governance story.

## Common Misconceptions About Democratic Backsliding and Markets

**Misconception 1: "Markets don't care about democracy, only growth."**
This is partially true for fast-growing economies (India is the clearest case) but fails for countries without compensating advantages. Hungary and Poland both have modest, EU-convergence-dependent growth profiles. Without the growth override, the governance discount shows up clearly in spreads and multiples. The data is unambiguous.

**Misconception 2: "EU membership guarantees against governance discount."**
EU membership limits the degree of democratic backsliding (the EU conditionality mechanism creates real costs) but does not prevent it. Hungary has been in the EU for 20 years and still has a massive governance discount. EU membership is a partial brake, not a guarantee.

**Misconception 3: "Democratic recovery is permanent once it starts."**
Poland's Tusk government may succeed in restoring judicial independence — or it may fail, as PiS still controls significant institutional machinery (the Constitutional Tribunal remains packed with PiS loyalists). Democratic recovery is a process with significant execution risk. The market prices the probability of success, not the certainty.

**Misconception 4: "Autocratic stability is better for markets than democratic uncertainty."**
This is the "benevolent autocrat" fallacy. In the short run, an autocrat with a clear growth-oriented agenda can generate strong returns (Erdogan's early years, 2002-2012, were actually quite good for Turkish investors). But the long-run trajectory is consistently negative because the institutional destruction undermines the rule of law that investors depend on for property rights protection.

**Misconception 5: "V-Dem is too slow — by the time the score drops, the damage is done."**
V-Dem releases annual scores with approximately a 12-month lag. But for practical trading, investors don't need to wait for the V-Dem report. The events that V-Dem scores — court packing, judge firings, media takeovers — are immediately visible when they happen. V-Dem's value is as a systematic framework for scoring what you've already observed, not as a newswire.
