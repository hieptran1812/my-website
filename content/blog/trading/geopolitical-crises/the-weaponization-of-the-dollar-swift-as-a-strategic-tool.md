> [!important]
> **The one-paragraph version.** The United States can inflict catastrophic economic damage on almost any country without firing a shot, because the dollar sits at the center of global finance and Washington controls access to it. The popular shorthand for this is "kicking a country off SWIFT," but SWIFT is only the messaging layer — a financial telex. The real weapon is **correspondent banking**: to move dollars, a foreign bank needs an account at a US-touching bank, and the US Treasury can order those accounts closed. Lose your dollar correspondents and you can no longer settle dollar trades, no matter how many SWIFT messages you send.
>
> - **The dollar is a network, not a currency.** Roughly **\$6.6 trillion** changes hands in FX markets every day, and the dollar is on one side of about **88%** of those trades. Half of all cross-border bank claims and trade invoices are dollar-denominated. That ubiquity is the source of the power.
> - **The architecture has five layers** — the Fed/Treasury at the top, the big USD correspondent banks below, then SWIFT messaging, then the world's regional banks, then end users. Control of the top layer cascades down through all the rest.
> - **The weapon comes in four flavors:** freezing a country's reserves (Russia, ~\$300B in 2022), cutting banks out of dollar clearing (Iran, Russia), secondary sanctions that punish *third parties* for dealing with a target, and export controls on technology.
> - **The paradox:** every time the dollar is used as a weapon, the targets — and the bystanders watching — build alternatives. Use it too much and you teach the world to live without it. De-dollarization demand spiked after the 2022 Russia freeze precisely because every central bank suddenly asked, "Could that be me?"
> - **The shelf life:** the dollar's share of global reserves has fallen from **71% in 2000 to ~58% in 2025** — a slow structural decline, not a collapse. There is no single replacement; the diversification is into gold, the euro, the renminbi, and a long tail of currencies.
> - **For markets:** sanctions are *fast* (front-run the freeze) but de-dollarization is *slow* (a decade-plus trend, not a trade). The two timescales must never be confused.

When people say a country has been "cut off from SWIFT," they picture a single dramatic switch being flipped in Belgium — and then the target's economy going dark. The mental image is wrong in almost every detail, and the gap between the image and the reality is exactly where the real power lives.

SWIFT — the Society for Worldwide Interbank Financial Telecommunication — is a messaging cooperative headquartered near Brussels. It does not hold anyone's money. It does not move anyone's money. It carries *instructions*: "Bank A, please pay Bank B \$10 million on behalf of customer X." It is, in the most literal sense, a very secure, very standardized email system for banks. You could shut SWIFT down entirely tomorrow and not a single dollar would change ownership; banks would simply fall back to telex, fax, and phone calls, the way they did before SWIFT existed in the 1970s.

So why does "kicking a country off SWIFT" sound like a death sentence? Because it is usually paired with the thing that actually kills: cutting the target off from the **correspondent banks** that clear dollars. That is the move that strands an economy. And understanding the difference between the messaging layer and the settlement layer is the single most important idea in the entire subject of financial coercion. This post is the keystone of the sanctions sub-series, and everything in it builds on that one distinction.

## Foundations: why the dollar is a weapon at all

Before we can talk about how the weapon is *fired*, we have to understand why it exists. A currency becomes a strategic tool only when the rest of the world has no practical choice but to use it. The dollar reached that status through a combination of accident, design, and sheer inertia, and the result is a structure where one government sits at a chokepoint that nearly every cross-border transaction must pass through.

### The dollar does three jobs, and ubiquity in each reinforces the others

Economists describe an international currency by three functions, and the dollar dominates all three:

1. **Medium of exchange (invoicing and settlement).** When a Korean shipyard sells a tanker to a Greek owner, the contract is almost certainly priced in dollars — not because either party is American, but because everyone *else* prices in dollars, so dollars are the path of least resistance. Roughly half of all global trade outside the eurozone is invoiced in dollars, and in commodities the share is far higher.
2. **Unit of account (the price tag).** Oil, copper, wheat, gold — the world quotes them in dollars. A Vietnamese refiner buying crude thinks in dollars per barrel even though it pays salaries in dong. This is the "petrodollar" legacy we will return to.
3. **Store of value (reserves and savings).** Central banks hold their rainy-day funds overwhelmingly in dollar assets — mostly US Treasuries — because Treasuries are deep, liquid, and (until recently) considered politically untouchable. As of 2025 the dollar is still about **58%** of disclosed global reserves.

The crucial point is that these three functions are **self-reinforcing**. Because trade is invoiced in dollars, banks need dollar liquidity, so they hold dollar reserves, which deepens the dollar money market, which makes dollars the cheapest currency to transact in, which makes invoicing in dollars even more attractive. Economists call this "strategic complementarity" — your incentive to use the dollar rises with everyone else's use of it. It is the same network effect that locks in a dominant social network or operating system, except the stakes are the plumbing of the entire world economy.

That network effect is the deep reason the dollar can be a weapon. A weapon needs leverage, and leverage requires that the target *cannot easily route around you*. If a sanctioned country could simply switch to invoicing in euros or yuan overnight, dollar sanctions would be a minor inconvenience. They are not, because the network is sticky.

To feel just how sticky, consider the scale. The dollar is on one side of roughly **88%** of all foreign-exchange transactions — a figure that has barely budged in decades despite the dollar being only about a quarter of world GDP. About **half** of all cross-border bank lending and international debt securities are denominated in dollars. When two countries that are *neither* American trade with each other, they still overwhelmingly settle in dollars: a Brazilian exporter selling to an Indonesian buyer will, more often than not, invoice in dollars, because both sides find it easier to hold and convert the world's most liquid currency than to manage the bilateral real–rupiah exchange rate directly. This is the phenomenon of the "vehicle currency" — the dollar is the connecting hub that lets every currency reach every other currency through one deep, cheap market rather than through hundreds of thin, expensive bilateral ones.

The hub structure is what makes the network impossible to abandon piecemeal. A single country deciding to ditch the dollar faces the full cost of thin bilateral markets while getting none of the benefit — it is like being the first person to leave a dominant messaging app: you bear all the inconvenience and reach no one. Coordinated abandonment is theoretically possible but practically glacial, because someone has to build and fund the deep alternative markets *before* the switch makes sense, and no one wants to pay that bill first. The dollar's incumbency is defended not by law or by force but by the brutal economics of network effects — which is also why, when the erosion finally comes, it comes slowly and from the edges inward rather than all at once.

#### Worked example: the cost of being "dollar-clean" vs. "dollar-cut"

Imagine a mid-sized manufacturing firm in a country that has just been sanctioned. Before sanctions, it imports \$200 million a year of components, priced in dollars, settled through its bank's dollar correspondent in New York. Its all-in cost of moving money — wire fees, FX spread, financing — runs about **0.4%**, or \$800,000 a year.

After it loses dollar access, it must route the same trade through a patchwork of local-currency deals, barter arrangements, and intermediaries in third countries who charge a premium for the sanctions risk they absorb. Suppose the effective cost rises to **6%** — a figure consistent with what sanctioned entities have actually paid for "workaround" trade. On \$200 million that is **\$12 million a year**, a fifteen-fold increase, before counting the trades it simply cannot do at all because no counterparty will touch it. The firm has not been bombed. It has been *taxed into uncompetitiveness* purely by losing its place in the dollar network. Multiply across an entire economy and you see why the freeze bites.

### The Triffin tension sitting underneath it all

There is an old contradiction baked into any global reserve currency, first articulated by the economist Robert Triffin in the 1960s. For the world to have enough dollars to use as reserves and settlement, the United States must supply them — which it does by running persistent current-account deficits, sending dollars abroad in exchange for goods. But the more dollars pile up overseas relative to America's own economy, the more people quietly wonder whether all those claims can ultimately be honored. The reserve issuer enjoys an "exorbitant privilege" (cheap borrowing, the power we are describing) but carries a long-run credibility cost.

Weaponizing the dollar adds a *political* layer to this economic tension. Triffin worried about whether the dollars were *backed*; the modern worry is whether they are *safe to hold* — not financially, but politically. A central bank that fears its dollar reserves could be frozen has a brand-new reason to hold fewer of them, independent of any concern about US fiscal health. We will see that this political Triffin problem is the engine of the central paradox of this whole essay.

It is worth pausing on why this political dimension is so corrosive in a way the original Triffin dilemma was not. A purely economic doubt about the dollar's backing can be answered with economic policy — fiscal discipline, credible monetary management, deep liquid markets. The United States can, in principle, fix an economic credibility problem. But a *political* doubt — "will my reserves be frozen if my government does something Washington dislikes?" — cannot be answered with economics at all. It can only be answered with a binding promise *never to use the weapon*, and that is precisely the promise the US will not make, because the weapon is too valuable. So the political Triffin problem is, in a deep sense, *unsolvable* by the issuer: the very thing that would reassure reserve holders (a credible no-weaponization pledge) is the thing the issuer is least willing to give up. This is what makes the modern erosion qualitatively different from past worries about the dollar — it stems not from doubts the US can fix, but from a power the US refuses to relinquish. Every reserve manager now prices a small, permanent "political seizure" premium into dollar holdings, and that premium does not go away no matter how sound US finances become.

## How the weapon actually works: SWIFT vs. correspondent banking

Now to the mechanism. The reason commentators conflate "SWIFT" with "dollar access" is that the two are usually cut at the same time, so it looks like one action. But they are different layers doing different jobs, and the distinction determines exactly how much damage a sanction does.

![How a SWIFT exclusion freezes a bank: the message routes to a correspondent clearer, which debits a nostro account and settles in dollars — but if OFAC cuts the correspondent, no nostro means no settlement and the target is frozen.](/imgs/blogs/the-weaponization-of-the-dollar-swift-as-a-strategic-tool-2.png)

### Following a single dollar payment

Trace what happens when a bank in, say, Kazakhstan pays a supplier in Germany in dollars. The Kazakh bank does not have a vault full of physical dollars in Almaty. What it has is a **nostro account** — "our account with you" — at a large bank that operates inside the US dollar clearing system, perhaps Citibank or JPMorgan in New York. That New York bank is the **correspondent**. The dollars "in Kazakhstan" are really a balance sitting in New York.

To make the payment, the Kazakh bank sends a SWIFT message: *debit our nostro account, credit the German bank's correspondent.* The actual money moves between accounts inside the US clearing system (ultimately across the Federal Reserve's settlement rails). SWIFT carried the *instruction*; the correspondent network moved the *value*.

Now consider the two ways to attack this:

- **Cut SWIFT only.** The Kazakh bank loses the convenient standardized messaging channel. Annoying, slower, more error-prone — but it can still phone, fax, or use an alternative messaging system to instruct its correspondent. The dollars can still move. This is a *speed bump*, not a wall.
- **Cut the correspondent.** OFAC (the US Treasury's Office of Foreign Assets Control) names the Kazakh bank, and every US-touching correspondent must, on pain of crippling penalties, close its nostro account and refuse to clear its dollars. Now there is *nowhere for the dollars to settle*. The bank can send all the messages it likes; no correspondent will act on them. This is the wall.

This is why the careful phrasing matters. "Russia was cut off from SWIFT" in 2022 is shorthand; what actually froze Russian trade was the combination of SWIFT removal for select banks *and*, far more importantly, the designation of major Russian banks so that no Western correspondent would clear their dollars or euros. SWIFT removal without correspondent designation is largely symbolic; correspondent designation without SWIFT removal still strangles the target.

#### Worked example: why \$50 million gets stuck

Suppose the Kazakh bank above holds a nostro balance of **\$50 million** at its New York correspondent and has \$30 million of payments queued. The day it is designated by OFAC, that \$50 million does not vanish — it is *blocked*. The correspondent is legally forbidden from acting on any instruction to move it. The \$30 million of payments fail. Suppliers abroad never get paid; some seize goods, some sue, all stop shipping.

Meanwhile the bank still owes its own depositors and counterparties. It has \$50 million of "money" it cannot touch and \$30 million of obligations it cannot meet. Within days it faces a liquidity crisis that has nothing to do with whether it is solvent on paper. The \$50 million was never really *in Kazakhstan*; it was always a permission to use New York's plumbing, and that permission has been revoked. This is the difference between *having dollars* and *having access to the dollar system* — and the weapon attacks the second, not the first.

### Why other countries can't just ignore an OFAC designation

The deepest part of the mechanism is **secondary sanctions**, and they are what turn a US measure into a global one. A *primary* sanction forbids US persons from dealing with a target. A *secondary* sanction threatens to cut off *anyone* — a French bank, a Chinese trading house, an Indian refiner — from the US financial system if *they* deal with the target.

A bank in Singapore has no legal obligation to obey US foreign policy. But it has dollar correspondents, dollar customers, and dollar-denominated assets it cannot afford to lose access to. Faced with a choice between continuing to bank a small sanctioned client and risking its own access to the entire dollar system, it will drop the client every time. The US never has to coerce the Singapore bank directly; it just has to make the cost of non-compliance existential. This "deputizing" of the world's banks is why a US sanction can shut a target out of markets in countries that never signed on to the policy.

The mechanism is so reliable that it is largely *self-enforcing*. OFAC does not have an army of investigators monitoring every Singaporean bank. It does not need one. It maintains a public list (the Specially Designated Nationals list), publishes the rules, and lets the banks' own compliance departments do the enforcing — because the banks have far more to lose than OFAC could ever extract. Over the past two decades the global banking system has built a vast, expensive **compliance apparatus** — sanctions-screening software, know-your-customer checks, transaction monitoring — whose entire purpose is to keep the bank on the right side of US rules. A large international bank may spend *hundreds of millions of dollars a year* on this machinery. The cost is enormous, and it is borne entirely by the private sector, which is precisely why the weapon is so cheap for the US to wield: Washington sets the rules; the world's banks pay to enforce them on themselves.

There is a subtler consequence called **de-risking**. Because the penalty for getting a sanctions call wrong is existential, banks do not merely avoid sanctioned entities — they avoid entire *categories* of clients and even whole *countries* that look risky, regardless of whether any specific transaction is illegal. A small bank in a poor country with weak documentation can find that no Western correspondent will deal with it at all, not because it has done anything wrong, but because the upside of banking it is small and the tail risk of an inadvertent violation is unbounded. Whole regions have been partially cut off from the dollar system this way — collateral damage from a weapon that was never even fired at them. De-risking is the dollar weapon's shadow: the chilling effect that does damage by deterrence alone.

#### Worked example: the secondary-sanction calculus

A European bank earns **\$2 million a year** in fees from a sanctioned client. Continuing the relationship risks a US enforcement action. Historically, OFAC settlements for sanctions violations have run into the *hundreds of millions to billions* of dollars — one large European bank paid nearly **\$9 billion** in 2014 for sanctions breaches. Even setting the fine aside, the existential risk is losing dollar-clearing access, which for a bank with hundreds of billions in dollar flows is not a number you can write down — it is the end of the franchise.

So the bank's expected-value math is brutally simple: **\$2 million of upside** against a tail risk measured in *billions plus business death*. No rational compliance officer takes that bet. The client is dropped within hours of the designation. The weapon works not because everyone agrees with it, but because the asymmetry of the payoff makes refusal irrational.

## The four weapons in the arsenal

"Dollar weaponization" is not one tool but a family of them, each hitting a different target with a different reach and a different escape hatch. Keeping them straight matters, because markets react differently to each and because each is breeding its own counter-measure.

![The dollar weapons inventory: reserve freezes, SWIFT exclusion, secondary sanctions, and export controls, each with its mechanism, target, limits, and emerging counter-measure.](/imgs/blogs/the-weaponization-of-the-dollar-swift-as-a-strategic-tool-5.png)

### Weapon 1: Reserve freezes

The most dramatic. A central bank parks its reserves in safe foreign assets — US Treasuries held in custody at the New York Fed or at commercial custodians, euro bonds held in Europe, deposits at the Bank for International Settlements. A freeze *immobilizes* those holdings: the assets still exist, but the owner cannot sell, spend, or repatriate them.

The defining case is the 2022 immobilization of roughly **\$300 billion** of Russian central-bank reserves by the G7 — over half of Russia's total. Overnight, a nuclear-armed state with the world's eleventh-largest economy discovered that the war chest it had built precisely to weather a crisis was inaccessible at the moment it most needed it. The shock to *every other central bank* was profound, and it is the single event that most accelerated de-dollarization thinking.

The limit of the reserve weapon is its **reach**: it only touches assets held within the freezing jurisdiction's grasp. Gold sitting in a country's own vaults is untouchable. Reserves held onshore, or in a jurisdiction that won't cooperate, are safe. This is exactly why post-2022 you see central banks repatriating gold and shifting reserves toward assets the US cannot reach.

There is also a vital distinction inside this weapon that markets obsess over: **freeze versus confiscation**. A freeze immobilizes the assets but leaves legal title with the owner — the dollars are still Russia's, they just cannot be moved. Confiscation transfers ownership outright, typically to fund the sanctioning side's objectives (in the Russia case, the proposal to use the reserves to rebuild Ukraine). The two look similar from the target's chair — in both cases you can't touch the money — but they are worlds apart for *every other reserve holder*. A freeze is a reversible, norm-respecting act of coercion: behave, and you might get your money back. Confiscation breaks the underlying promise that sovereign reserves are property rather than hostages, and it would tell every central bank on earth that dollar reserves can be permanently expropriated by political decision. That is why Western governments have been so cautious about crossing the line from freeze to seizure: the freeze is a weapon, but confiscation would be closer to detonating the foundation the weapon stands on. The legal debates over the "immobilized assets" and the income they generate are, at root, arguments about how far you can push the weapon before the dollar's reputation as a safe store of value takes structural damage.

### Weapon 2: SWIFT exclusion and correspondent cutoff

Covered in detail above. The targeted, surgical version designates specific banks; the blunt version removes a whole country's banks from SWIFT and from Western clearing. Iran is the long-running test case — repeatedly cut off, repeatedly forced into elaborate workarounds (oil-for-gold schemes, front companies, barter), each of which raised the cost of its trade without fully stopping it.

The limit here is **substitutability over time**. The first time a target is cut off, it scrambles. But over years it builds parallel channels — non-dollar trade with willing partners, its own messaging system, networks of small banks with no US exposure to lose. The weapon is devastating on day one and slowly leakier thereafter.

It is worth being precise about what the "alternative rails" actually are, because they are routinely overhyped. **CIPS** (China's Cross-Border Interbank Payment System) is often described as a "SWIFT alternative," but this conflates the two layers again: CIPS is partly a *clearing* system for renminbi, which is a genuine alternative to dollar correspondent clearing — for renminbi. It does nothing for dollar trade and it is a fraction of the dollar system's size, handling a tiny share of global payments. Russia's **SPFS** is a domestic financial-messaging system — a SWIFT substitute at the *messaging* layer — but messaging was never the binding constraint, so SPFS solves the easy problem and not the hard one. **mBridge**, a project linking several central banks' digital currencies for direct settlement, is the most genuinely threatening to the correspondent model because it would let banks settle cross-border *without* a US-touching intermediary at all — but it remains experimental and small. The honest summary: the alternatives that exist mostly address the *messaging* layer (which was never the real weapon) or work only for *non-dollar* currencies (which limits their reach). A true peer to the dollar's correspondent-clearing depth does not yet exist. Which is exactly why the weapon still works — and exactly why its builders keep building.

### Weapon 3: Secondary sanctions

The force multiplier. By threatening third parties, secondary sanctions extend a US measure to the whole world. They are why a sanctioned entity finds that *even non-American, non-allied* banks won't touch it.

Their limit is the most interesting of all: secondary sanctions work **only as long as access to the dollar system is worth more than the sanctioned business**. The day a bank can do its dollar-equivalent business through a non-US channel, the threat loses its teeth. Russia–China trade has migrated heavily to renminbi precisely to put it beyond the reach of secondary sanctions. The weapon's power is *exactly* proportional to the dollar's indispensability — which is why overuse is self-defeating.

### Weapon 4: Export controls

Not strictly financial, but increasingly fused with the financial weapons. By controlling access to advanced technology — high-end semiconductors, chipmaking equipment, design software — and by leveraging the fact that this technology runs through US-touching supply chains, Washington can deny capabilities, not just money. The "foreign direct product rule" extends US jurisdiction to goods made *anywhere* if they use US technology, mirroring the secondary-sanctions logic.

The limit is **indigenization**: deny a country chips long enough and it pours national resources into building its own, eventually eroding the chokepoint. The weapon buys time; it does not buy permanent advantage.

#### Worked example: stacking the weapons on one target

Consider a hypothetical mid-sized economy hit with the full stack. Its **\$80 billion** of foreign reserves: roughly \$45 billion sits in Western jurisdictions and is frozen, while \$20 billion in gold at home and \$15 billion in a friendly third country survive — so about **56%** of the war chest is neutralized instantly. Its three largest banks are designated, cutting them from dollar and euro clearing; secondary sanctions then scare off the Singaporean and Gulf banks that might have helped, so even neutral counterparties retreat. Export controls choke the imported components its export industries depend on.

The combined effect is not additive but *multiplicative*: the reserve freeze removes the cushion, the clearing cutoff removes the ability to transact, the secondary sanctions remove the workaround, and the export controls remove the ability to substitute production. The economy doesn't shrink by the sum of four shocks — it seizes, because each weapon disables the escape route from the others.

## The reserve-currency decline: a slow tide, not a wave

If the dollar weapon is so powerful, why worry about its future? Because the data show the foundation is eroding — slowly, but unmistakably — and the weaponization is part of the cause.

![USD share of global FX reserves fell from 71.1% in 2000 to 57.9% in 2025, while the CNY share rose only to about 2.15% — a slow structural decline into many currencies, not a single replacement.](/imgs/blogs/the-weaponization-of-the-dollar-swift-as-a-strategic-tool-3.png)

The chart tells a measured story. The dollar's share of disclosed global reserves has slid from **71% in 2000 to about 58% in 2025** — a decline of roughly thirteen percentage points over a quarter century. That is real, but it is *gradual*: under half a percentage point a year on average. There is no cliff.

Two features deserve emphasis. First, the **2022 inflection**. The freeze of Russian reserves did not cause a dollar crash; the share barely moved in the year after. What it did was change the *conversation* in every central bank reserve-management committee on earth. The question shifted from "what maximizes return?" to "what is politically safe to hold?" — and that question, once asked, does not get un-asked.

Second, and crucially, **the renminbi is not the replacement**. The CNY share peaked under 3% and has since *fallen* to about 2.15%, partly because China's capital controls and opaque legal system make the renminbi an uncomfortable place to park reserves. The diversification away from the dollar is not flowing into one rival; it is fragmenting into gold, the euro, the Australian and Canadian dollars, and a long tail of smaller currencies. The world is not switching reserve currencies. It is *spreading its bets*.

Where exactly is the thirteen lost points going? Careful work by central-bank researchers gives a textured answer. Roughly a quarter of the dollar's lost share has flowed into the renminbi (despite its recent retreat), and the remaining three-quarters into a scattering of **non-traditional reserve currencies** — the Australian dollar, the Canadian dollar, the Swedish krona, the Korean won, the Singapore dollar — plus a structural, accelerating bid for **gold**. Notably, the euro has *not* gained; its share has been flat-to-down, hobbled by the eurozone's lack of a unified safe asset (there is no pan-European Treasury, only a patchwork of national bonds of varying credit quality) and by recurring existential crises. The yen has drifted lower too. So the story is emphatically not "dollar down, one rival up." It is "dollar down, *fragmentation* up" — reserve managers diversifying into a long tail of smaller, liquid, *politically neutral* currencies and into the one asset that answers to no government at all.

This fragmentation matters enormously for the weapon's future. A world that had simply swapped the dollar for the renminbi would just have a *new* chokepoint — Beijing's instead of Washington's. But a *fragmented* reserve world has no chokepoint at all. If reserves are spread across a dozen currencies and gold, then no single government can freeze a meaningful fraction of any country's war chest. Fragmentation is not merely the dollar losing share; it is the *disappearance of the very chokepoint structure that makes financial weaponization possible*. The slow tide in the chart is, at a deeper level, the slow dissolution of the conditions under which the weapon exists.

#### Worked example: what a 13-point shift is actually worth

Global disclosed reserves run on the order of **\$12 trillion**. A thirteen-percentage-point fall in the dollar's share over 25 years means roughly **\$1.5 trillion** of reserves that, in a 2000-style world, would have been held in dollars are now held elsewhere. That is a vast sum in isolation — but spread across 25 years and a \$28-trillion-plus US Treasury market, it is a gentle headwind, not a stampede. The lesson for anyone tempted to trade "the death of the dollar" is that the trend is real and the *pace* is glacial. Confusing the two is how traders lose money being right too early.

## A short history: how the dollar got its throne

To judge the dollar's shelf life you have to know how it won the crown, because reserve currencies turn over on the scale of human lifetimes, not news cycles.

![Eight inflection points in the dollar's reserve-currency history, from the 1944 Bretton Woods anchor to the 2024 de-dollarization debate, with the USD reserve share noted at each milestone.](/imgs/blogs/the-weaponization-of-the-dollar-swift-as-a-strategic-tool-7.png)

- **1944, Bretton Woods.** With Europe in ruins and the US holding most of the world's gold, the dollar was fixed to gold at \$35 an ounce and every other currency was fixed to the dollar. The dollar became the world's anchor by being the last currency standing that was still convertible to gold.
- **1971, the Nixon shock.** Facing a drain on its gold, the US "closed the gold window," ending dollar–gold convertibility. Remarkably, the dollar did *not* lose its reserve status. The world kept using it because the network was already built — the first great demonstration that inertia, not gold backing, sustains a reserve currency.
- **1973, the petrodollar.** As oil prices quadrupled, an informal understanding emerged that oil would be priced and sold in dollars, and that oil exporters would recycle their surplus dollars into US assets. This bolted the dollar to the most strategically vital commodity on earth and gave every oil-importing nation a structural need to hold dollars.
- **2001, the euro.** The launch of a unified European currency created, for the first time since the dollar's ascent, a *credible* rival with a large, deep capital market behind it. The euro took share — it is now the clear number two — but never threatened the throne, hobbled by the eurozone's fragmented bond markets and periodic existential crises.
- **2009, China's SDR proposal.** In the wake of the financial crisis, China's central bank governor floated replacing the dollar as the anchor with the IMF's Special Drawing Rights — a "super-sovereign" reserve asset. It went nowhere practically but signaled, loudly, that the largest creditor nation was thinking about the day after the dollar.
- **2015, the renminbi enters the SDR.** The IMF added the CNY to the SDR basket, a symbolic blessing of China's currency as a reserve-grade asset. Symbolism outran reality; the renminbi's actual reserve share stayed tiny.
- **2022, the Russia freeze.** The moment the weapon's cost became visible to its own users. (See above.)
- **2024 onward, the de-dollarization debate.** BRICS-bloc discussions of alternative settlement systems, local-currency trade, and even a notional common unit moved from fringe to mainstream — more aspiration than architecture so far, but a clear statement of intent.

The throughline: reserve currencies are dethroned over **decades**, usually only when a clearly superior network exists to defect to. Sterling's handover to the dollar took roughly half a century and two world wars. Nothing in today's data suggests the dollar is on the verge of an abrupt fall — only that the long, slow erosion is real and that weaponization is greasing the slope.

The sterling analogy deserves a closer look, because it is the only modern precedent for a reserve-currency transition and it is sobering for the "dollar collapse" crowd. The pound was the unquestioned global currency in 1900. The United States overtook Britain economically around the turn of the century, ran enormous trade surpluses, and emerged from the First World War as the world's creditor while Britain became a debtor. By every fundamental measure the dollar should have replaced sterling by 1920. Yet sterling clung to its reserve role into the *1950s* — three to four decades after the fundamentals had turned — sustained purely by network inertia, the depth of London's markets, and the absence of a fully formed alternative. It took two world wars, the dismantling of the British Empire, the collapse of the gold standard, and an explicit American policy of building dollar institutions (Bretton Woods, the IMF, the World Bank) to finally complete the handover. The lesson is not that reserve currencies are eternal; it is that they are *astonishingly persistent*, outliving the fundamentals that justify them by entire generations. Anyone forecasting the dollar's imminent demise is implicitly betting that this time the transition will run ten times faster than the only historical example we have — and is doing so with no superior network yet built to defect to.

There is a second lesson hiding in the petrodollar story that bears on the weapon directly. The 1973 arrangement worked because oil exporters had nowhere better to put their surplus dollars than US assets, and because oil buyers had to acquire dollars to pay for energy. That created a *circular* demand for dollars bolted to the most essential commodity on earth. The modern threat to this circle is not that oil stops being priced in dollars overnight — it won't — but that *incremental* volumes of energy trade begin settling in other currencies. Russia selling oil to China and India outside the dollar, Gulf states accepting renminbi for some shipments to Asia: none of these is decisive on its own, but each is a small puncture in the petrodollar circle that took half a century to inflate. The weapon, by pushing energy exporters toward non-dollar buyers, is quietly helping to deflate it.

## The central paradox: use it and lose it

We now arrive at the idea that ties the whole subject together. The dollar's power as a weapon depends entirely on the world's *dependence* on the dollar. But every use of the weapon gives the world a reason to reduce that dependence. The weapon, fired, corrodes the very condition that makes it lethal.

![The 'use it too much and lose it' paradox: weaponizing the dollar drives targets and bystanders to seek alternatives, building de-dollarization demand, which lowers the dollar's reserve share, which shrinks the weapon's reach and deterrent value.](/imgs/blogs/the-weaponization-of-the-dollar-swift-as-a-strategic-tool-6.png)

Walk the loop. The dollar is weaponized against a target. The target — and, more importantly, every *bystander* who realizes it could be next — starts seeking alternatives: non-dollar trade channels, gold reserves, parallel messaging and clearing systems. That demand for alternatives, aggregated across many nations and many years, slowly lowers the dollar's share of trade and reserves. As the dollar's share falls, there are fewer transactions the US can intercept and fewer reserves it can freeze — so each future use of the weapon reaches less of the target's economy. The deterrent value drops. The weapon still works, but on a shrinking fraction of the world.

This is not a hypothesis; it is observable. The clearest evidence is the migration of Russia–China trade into renminbi and the surge of central-bank *gold* buying after 2022 — central banks bought over **1,000 tonnes** of gold annually in 2022, 2023, and 2024, roughly double the prior decade's pace. Gold is the ultimate de-weaponization asset: it sits in your own vault, settles with no correspondent, and answers to no foreign treasury. A central bank buying gold instead of Treasuries is, quite literally, buying immunity from the dollar weapon.

The strategic dilemma for US policymakers is genuine and unresolved. Each individual use of the weapon is tempting because it is *cheap and effective right now*. The cost — incremental erosion of the dollar's indispensability — is *diffuse and deferred*. That is the textbook structure of a resource that gets overexploited: private benefit now, shared cost later. The dollar's reserve status is a commons that weaponization quietly grazes down.

The paradox also has a *political-economy* dimension that makes it almost impossible to manage. The benefits of a sanction accrue to whichever administration is in office, in the news cycle of the moment — a visible, claimable foreign-policy win. The costs accrue to *future* administrations, in the form of a marginally less dominant dollar a decade hence, and are nearly impossible to attribute to any single decision. No politician was ever voted out for the slow erosion of the dollar's reserve share; many have been rewarded for a tough sanctions stance. The incentives point relentlessly toward *more* use, not less, even by leaders who fully understand the long-run cost. This is why the paradox is so durable: it is not that policymakers are unaware of it, but that the structure of accountability makes the self-defeating choice the rational one for each individual decision-maker. The commons gets grazed not out of ignorance but out of the ordinary logic of who-bears-the-cost-and-when.

There is one more twist worth naming. The paradox is *non-linear*. For a long stretch, weaponization erodes the dollar's dominance only at the margins — a country here builds a workaround, a central bank there buys gold — and the weapon stays overwhelmingly effective. But network effects can flip: if enough alternative infrastructure gets built, the cost of *using* the alternatives falls below some threshold, and adoption can accelerate suddenly as the network effect that once protected the dollar begins to work *for* the alternative instead. No one knows where that threshold is, and we are almost certainly nowhere near it today. But the existence of a possible tipping point is why prudent policymakers worry about *cumulative* use rather than any single sanction: the danger is not that one more designation breaks the dollar, but that the slow accumulation of workarounds eventually builds an alternative network deep enough to defect to — at which point the slow tide could become, for the first time since sterling, a genuine current.

#### Worked example: the discounting trap

Suppose a sanction delivers **\$10 billion** of coercive value today — leverage in a negotiation, damage to an adversary. Suppose it also raises the probability that, over the next decade, an extra **\$200 billion** of reserves and trade migrate out of the dollar system, costing the US some fraction of its borrowing advantage and its future coercive reach — call the present value of that future loss **\$3 billion**.

A policymaker facing a crisis *today* heavily discounts the \$3 billion: it is uncertain, far off, and someone else's problem by the time it bites. So the sanction looks like a \$10 billion gain against a barely-felt cost, and it gets fired. Repeat across dozens of decisions over a decade, and the deferred costs compound into the thirteen-point reserve-share decline we measured earlier. No single decision was irrational; the *aggregate* is the slow draining of the weapon's power. That is the paradox in arithmetic form.

### Two worlds, side by side

It helps to make the dependence concrete by laying the present world next to a plausible future one. The picture is not a forecast of collapse; it is a map of which levers survive a weaker dollar and which ones quietly stop working.

![Two worlds side by side: a world of dollar dominance where the US can freeze reserves, cut SWIFT and correspondent access, and enforce secondary sanctions across \$6.6T of daily FX volume, versus a world where the dollar weakens and CIPS/mBridge rails bypass US clearers, secondary sanctions lose bite, reserves diversify into gold and CNY, and coercion becomes a one-time shock rather than a standing threat.](/imgs/blogs/the-weaponization-of-the-dollar-swift-as-a-strategic-tool-4.png)

Read the left column as a list of things that are true *only because the dollar is unavoidable*. The US can freeze reserves — but only the reserves held in dollars within its reach. It can strand a target by cutting SWIFT and correspondents — but only because there is no comparably deep alternative rail. It can force third-country compliance through secondary sanctions — but only because losing dollar access is a corporate death sentence. Every single lever rests on the same foundation: there is nowhere else to go.

Now read the right column. None of these changes is dramatic on its own. CIPS — China's cross-border interbank payment system — and mBridge — a multi-central-bank digital-currency settlement experiment — are small and clunky today. Local-currency trade between, say, Russia and China or India and the Gulf is friction-heavy. Gold is illiquid and yields nothing. But notice what happens to the *left* column as the right column grows: each alternative does not need to *replace* the dollar to *blunt a specific weapon*. A working non-dollar rail blunts the correspondent cutoff. Gold reserves blunt the freeze. Local-currency trade blunts secondary sanctions. The world does not need to abandon the dollar to make the weapon less lethal; it only needs to build *partial exits*, weapon by weapon. That is precisely why the erosion is gradual and the weapon's decline is incremental rather than sudden — each partial exit chips at one lever without toppling the whole structure.

The deepest implication is that the two columns are not a binary the world chooses between; they are a slider that has been moving leftward-to-rightward, click by click, since 2022. The dollar weapon does not have an on/off future. It has a *dimmer*, and weaponization itself is the hand turning it down.

## Common misconceptions

**"Kicking a country off SWIFT cuts off its money."** No. SWIFT is messaging. The money is cut off by correspondent-bank designations. A country removed from SWIFT but with intact correspondents can still transact (clumsily); a country with intact SWIFT but designated banks cannot. Always ask which layer was actually hit.

**"The dollar is about to collapse / be dethroned."** The data say otherwise. The decline is real but glacial — roughly half a point of reserve share per year — and there is no superior network to defect to. The renminbi's share is *falling*. Betting on imminent collapse has bankrupted a long line of traders who were directionally right and fatally early.

**"De-dollarization means everyone switches to the yuan."** No. The renminbi's reserve share peaked under 3% and has since declined; China's capital controls make it unattractive as a reserve asset. The real de-dollarization is *fragmentation* — into gold, the euro, and a long tail of currencies — not a handover to a single rival.

**"Sanctions don't work because targets find workarounds."** Both halves are true and that's the point. Sanctions impose large, real costs (the 6%-vs-0.4% tax in our earlier example) without being airtight. They are a *tax and a friction*, not a hermetic seal. Judging them by whether the target survives misses that they can still be strategically decisive by raising costs.

**"Freezing reserves is the same as seizing them."** Legally and practically distinct. A *freeze* immobilizes assets but leaves ownership intact; a *seizure* (or confiscation) transfers ownership. The 2022 Russian reserves were *frozen*; the far more radical step of *confiscating* them to fund Ukraine has been fiercely debated precisely because outright seizure would shatter the legal norm that has made sovereigns comfortable holding dollars — and would accelerate de-dollarization far faster than a reversible freeze.

**"Gold buying by central banks is just an inflation hedge."** Partly, but the post-2022 surge is heavily a *sanctions hedge*. Gold is the one major reserve asset with no counterparty and no foreign treasury controlling access. The timing — the buying spike coincides exactly with the Russia freeze — gives the game away.

## How it shows up in real markets

The dollar weapon is not theoretical for traders; it moves prices, and on two very different clocks.

**The fast clock: sanction events.** When a designation lands, the affected assets gap *immediately*. A sanctioned country's bonds and currency collapse within hours; counterparties dump exposure; the cost of any remaining trade with the target spikes. The 2022 ruble initially crashed (before capital controls and energy revenue engineered a recovery), Russian sovereign and corporate bonds went to distressed pricing, and anyone holding the wrong paper at the wrong moment was frozen alongside it. The tradeable insight is that sanctions risk is a *jump* risk — it does not arrive gradually, so it cannot be hedged after the fact. You are either positioned before the headline or you are trapped.

**The slow clock: de-dollarization.** The structural decline shows up in glacial trends — the secular bid for **gold** (central-bank buying is a multi-year support under the gold price), the gradual rise in **non-dollar trade settlement**, the slow diversification of reserve portfolios. These are decade-scale trends. They reward patience and ruin impatience. The gold bull case rooted in de-dollarization is sound as a *strategic allocation* and treacherous as a *tactical trade*.

**The cross-asset signature.** When weaponization fears spike, you often see a distinctive pattern: gold bid, the dollar paradoxically *also* bid in the immediate crisis (because a crisis is when everyone scrambles for dollars to settle obligations — the "dollar smile"), and the targeted region's assets in freefall. The long-run de-dollarization story and the short-run flight-to-dollars are *not* contradictory; they operate on different clocks, and conflating them is a classic error. In a panic, even people who want to escape the dollar need dollars *first* to do the escaping.

**The volatility of the rules themselves.** A subtler market effect is that weaponization makes the *rules* of cross-border finance less predictable, and uncertainty has a price. When sanctions can be imposed quickly and unpredictably, every internationally exposed business must hold a larger buffer against the chance that a counterparty, a currency, or a settlement channel is suddenly cut off. That shows up as wider risk premia on emerging-market assets, higher financing costs for trade with politically sensitive regions, and a structural bid for assets perceived as politically neutral. The weapon does not have to be fired at *you* to cost you money; the mere fact that it *could* be fired raises the cost of operating across borders for everyone. This is the financial equivalent of a standing army: expensive to maintain in latent risk even in peacetime.

**Second-order targets.** Markets also reprice the *bystanders*. Banks heavily exposed to a newly sanctioned jurisdiction sell off as investors price the compliance and write-down risk. Commodities the target dominates (Russian energy and grain, for instance) carry a sanctions premium. And the currencies and assets of countries seen as *future* candidates for the weapon trade with a persistent risk discount — a quiet, ongoing cost the weapon imposes far beyond its immediate targets.

#### Worked example: positioning around a designation

Say you manage a portfolio with **\$5 million** of exposure to a country flashing on the sanctions radar — wide CDS spreads, diplomatic breakdown, the works. The asymmetry is stark. If sanctions *don't* come, holding earns you, say, a **9%** yield, or roughly \$450,000 over a year. If they *do* come, the position can gap to **40 cents on the dollar or worse** in a day — an instant **\$3 million** loss, with the remainder *frozen* and unsellable for years.

The expected-value math is dominated by the tail: even at a modest 15% annual probability of designation, the expected loss (0.15 × \$3 million = \$450,000) exactly eats the entire yield, and that ignores the catastrophic illiquidity of the frozen remainder. The rational move is to cut the exposure *before* the headline, accepting the lower yield as the price of not being trapped. Sanctions risk is the rare case where "I'll get out when it happens" is impossible — the door is welded shut the instant the news hits.

## How to trade it

A practical framework for turning all of this into positioning, with the two clocks kept rigorously separate.

1. **Treat sanctions as jump risk, and de-risk pre-emptively.** You cannot hedge a designation after it lands — liquidity vanishes and the assets freeze. For any exposure to a country on the geopolitical watch list, size the position assuming you may *never get out*. If that assumption makes the position too big, it already is.

2. **Own gold as the structural de-dollarization trade — and size it for years, not weeks.** Central-bank gold buying is a durable, multi-year bid born directly of sanctions fear. That makes gold a sound *strategic* allocation against the slow erosion of the dollar order. But it is a terrible *tactical* trade for impatient capital; the thesis can be right for a decade while the price chops sideways for stretches of it.

3. **Never short the dollar *because of* de-dollarization on a short horizon.** The trend is real and slow; the dollar's crisis behavior is to *rally* (the dollar smile). Short-horizon dollar bears repeatedly get run over by the very crises they thought would vindicate them. If you want to express de-dollarization, do it through gold and reserve-diversification beneficiaries, not through a tactical dollar short.

4. **Watch the bystanders, not just the targets.** The richest, least-crowded trades are often in the *second-order* exposures — banks and corporates with sanctioned-jurisdiction risk, commodities the target dominates, and the persistent risk discount on "next candidate" sovereigns. The market prices the headline target instantly; it prices the ripple more slowly.

5. **Distinguish a freeze from a confiscation in your tail scenarios.** A freeze is reversible and norm-preserving; outright confiscation of sovereign reserves would be a regime change in the rules of holding dollars and would accelerate de-dollarization dramatically. Any move toward seizing (not just freezing) reserves is a signal to *increase* the structural gold and diversification allocation, because it raises the long-run cost of the dollar order itself.

6. **Respect the two clocks above all else.** The single most expensive mistake in this entire domain is trading the slow story on the fast clock — being "right about the death of the dollar" and getting margin-called before the thesis matures. Sanctions are events; de-dollarization is a tide. Trade events with stops and pre-positioning; trade the tide with patience and strategic allocation. Confuse them and the market will teach you the difference at your expense.
