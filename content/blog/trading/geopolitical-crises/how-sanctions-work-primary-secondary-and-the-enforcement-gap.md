> [!important]
> **TL;DR.** A sanction is not one act — it is a chain of delegated legal authority that ends at a bank's compliance screen. *Primary* sanctions bind US persons and dollar flows. *Secondary* sanctions reach foreign firms with no US presence, using access to the dollar as leverage. *Sectoral* sanctions target whole industries. The chain leaks: shadow fleets, third-country routing, barter, and crypto let targets adapt within months. Empirically, sanctions achieve their stated policy goal only about **30–40%** of the time, and far less when unilateral and long-running. For traders, the signal is rarely the headline designation — it is the *gap* between announced reach and actual enforcement, which is where mispriced risk premia live.

On 26 February 2022, two days after Russian tanks crossed into Ukraine, the G7 announced it would freeze roughly \$300 billion of the Russian central bank's foreign reserves and cut major Russian banks out of SWIFT. The ruble fell from about 80 to the dollar to 135 within ten trading days — a peak depreciation near **47%**. Headlines called it the financial equivalent of a precision strike. Yet by May the ruble had clawed back most of that loss, Russian oil revenue in 2022 came in *higher* than 2021 (\$218 billion versus \$117 billion, as crude prices spiked), and a fleet of some 600 ageing tankers quietly kept the barrels moving to India and China.

That single episode contains the whole story of how sanctions work — and how they don't. This post takes the legal machinery apart piece by piece, then shows you where the gap between paper and practice opens, and how to trade the difference.

## Foundations: a sanction is a delegated legal instruction

The first mental correction most people need: a sanction is not a soldier and it is not a tariff. It is a *legal instruction* that travels down a chain of delegated authority and ends as a line of code in a bank's transaction-screening system. Understanding the chain tells you exactly where it can break.

In the United States — still the center of gravity for global sanctions because the dollar clears through it — the chain starts with statutory authority. Two laws matter most. The **International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA, 1977)** lets the President declare a national emergency over an "unusual and extraordinary threat" originating abroad and then block transactions and freeze assets tied to it. The **Trading with the Enemy Act (TWEA, 1917)** is the older cousin, now used mainly for Cuba. Congress also passes target-specific statutes — CAATSA (2017) for Russia/Iran/North Korea, the various Iran sanctions acts — that *mandate* certain designations rather than leave them to executive discretion.

The President signs an Executive Order invoking IEEPA. That EO delegates day-to-day operation to the Treasury Department, specifically the **Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC)**. OFAC is the engine room. It decides *who* gets named, *what* is prohibited, and *what licenses* (exceptions) exist. When OFAC designates a person or entity, it adds them to the **Specially Designated Nationals and Blocked Persons List** — the SDN list — which as of 2024 holds well over 12,000 entries.

Here is the critical part for markets. OFAC does not knock on doors. It publishes a name. The *enforcement* is done by tens of thousands of banks, insurers, brokers, shippers, and corporates who screen every counterparty against the SDN list because the penalty for getting it wrong is catastrophic. That outsourced, automated, fear-driven compliance is both the strength of the system (massive reach at near-zero government cost) and its weakness (it only catches what the screen can see). Hold onto that last clause — *it only catches what the screen can see* — because it is the single sentence that explains every leak, every workaround, and every faded sanctions trade in the rest of this post. The government writes the rule; tens of thousands of private compliance officers enforce it out of fear of ruinous fines; and the gap between the rule and the enforcement is exactly the set of transactions that have found a way to stay invisible to the screen.

#### Worked example: tracing a single blocked wire

Suppose a German auto-parts maker, Müller GmbH, ships \$2.4 million of components and the buyer wires payment through its bank in a third country. The wire is in dollars, so it must clear through a US correspondent bank. The correspondent's screening engine compares the beneficiary, the originator, and every intermediary against the SDN list. If the beneficiary is "Promsvyazbank" — added to the SDN list in February 2022 — the engine flags a match. The \$2.4 million is not sent; it is *blocked*, parked in a segregated account, and reported to OFAC within 10 business days. Müller never sees the cash. No US official touched the transaction. The SDN entry plus the dollar's plumbing did all the work. That is primary enforcement operating exactly as designed.

The reason this is worth slowing down on: every leak we will examine later is, mechanically, a way to keep that wire *out of the screen's view* — by changing the currency, the routing, the named beneficiary, or the settlement method.

### Why the dollar is the load-bearing wall

It is worth dwelling on *why* a German parts maker's payment ends up under US jurisdiction at all, because the entire architecture of modern sanctions rests on this single structural fact. When two non-US parties trade in dollars, neither of them holds physical dollars — dollars exist almost entirely as ledger entries in bank accounts, and the master ledger for the dollar lives in the United States. A dollar payment from a bank in Frankfurt to a bank in Mumbai does not teleport; it moves as a series of debits and credits between *correspondent accounts*, and the chain almost always passes through a US bank that holds the ultimate dollar balances. That US bank is a US person. The moment the payment touches it, US jurisdiction attaches.

This is sometimes called the "dollar choke point," and it is the reason a country with a 25% share of global GDP can project financial power vastly out of proportion to its economic weight. Roughly 60% of global central-bank reserves, around half of cross-border loans and international debt securities, and the dominant share of trade invoicing are in dollars. Oil — the single most sanctioned commodity — has historically been priced and settled almost entirely in dollars (the "petrodollar" arrangement). To sell oil is, by default, to handle dollars; to handle dollars is to enter US jurisdiction. Every sanctions program you will ever analyze is built on top of this plumbing. When you read about "de-dollarization" later, understand that what is actually being attempted is the slow construction of *alternative plumbing* — payment rails that do not pass through a US correspondent — precisely to drain this choke point of its power.

### The cast of enforcers beyond OFAC

OFAC is the most visible actor, but a real sanctions program mobilizes a wider cast, and knowing who does what helps you read which lever is being pulled. The **Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS)** inside the Commerce Department runs *export controls* — the rules on what technology and goods (semiconductors, machine tools, aircraft parts) may be shipped to a target. The 2022 controls cutting Russia off from advanced chips, and the escalating restrictions on selling cutting-edge AI chips to China, are BIS actions, not OFAC freezes. The **State Department** drives the diplomacy that turns a unilateral US measure into a multilateral one — and recall the data: multilateral sanctions are roughly twice as effective. The **Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN)** sets the anti-money-laundering rules that give banks the surveillance infrastructure sanctions enforcement rides on. And outside the US, the **EU, the UK's OFSI, and the UN Security Council** run their own lists; a UN designation is the gold standard because it is, in principle, globally binding, but the Security Council veto means the most geopolitically charged targets (Russia is a permanent member) can never be sanctioned by the UN at all. That veto gap is exactly why the Russia program had to be a US-and-allies coalition rather than a UN measure.

## The three kinds of reach

People say "sanctions" as if it were one thing. There are three meaningfully different kinds, and they differ in *whom they legally bind*. Confuse them and you will misread every news story.

![Matrix comparing primary, secondary, and sectoral sanctions by who they bind and their asset impact](/imgs/blogs/how-sanctions-work-primary-secondary-and-the-enforcement-gap-2.png)

### Primary sanctions

A **primary sanction** prohibits *US persons* — US citizens, US companies, anyone physically in the US, and any dollar transaction clearing through the US — from dealing with the target. This is the bedrock. When OFAC blocks an entity, US persons must freeze any of that entity's assets in their control and stop transacting with it. The legal hook is jurisdiction: the US can bind people and money under its own authority.

Primary sanctions are powerful precisely because the dollar is the world's reserve currency. Roughly 88% of all foreign-exchange transactions have the dollar on one side, and the overwhelming majority of cross-border trade is invoiced or settled in dollars. To touch a dollar is to touch the US banking system, which means touching US jurisdiction. So even a wholly foreign trade, if priced in dollars, runs through a US correspondent and into the reach of a primary sanction.

### Secondary sanctions

A **secondary sanction** is the controversial one. It reaches a *foreign* person or firm that has no US presence at all, by threatening to cut *them* off from the US market if they deal with the primary target. The US is not claiming jurisdiction over the foreign firm's conduct directly; it is offering a choice — "trade with Iran, or trade with us, but not both."

This is extraterritorial leverage, and allies hate it. When the US reimposed Iran sanctions in 2018, European firms like Total, Siemens, and Maersk pulled out of Iran not because European law required it but because the alternative was losing access to the US financial system and the dollar. The EU even passed a "blocking statute" forbidding European companies from complying with US secondary sanctions — and companies complied with the US anyway, because the dollar mattered more than a Brussels regulation. That single fact tells you where real power sits.

#### Worked example: why a Chinese bank obeys a US law it is not subject to

Take the real case of Bank of Kunlun, a Chinese bank that processed oil payments — say \$500 million worth for an SDN-listed Iranian entity. Bank of Kunlun has no branches in the US and is not a "US person." Strictly, US primary sanctions don't bind it. But the US can designate Bank of Kunlun under secondary authority, which would block it from the US correspondent network. The bank's *total* dollar-clearing business — say \$40 billion a year of legitimate trade finance for ordinary Chinese exporters — is worth far more than the \$500 million Iranian deal. A rational treasurer compares \$500 million of revenue against the risk of losing \$40 billion of franchise value and walks away from Iran. The sanction worked without a single US court asserting jurisdiction. (Bank of Kunlun was in fact penalized in 2012 and became a cautionary tale precisely because it was one of the few that *didn't* walk away.)

### Sectoral sanctions

A **sectoral sanction** does not freeze an entity outright; it restricts *specific activities* across a whole industry. The 2014 US response to Russia's annexation of Crimea is the textbook case. Rather than fully blocking Russia's largest banks and energy firms — which would have shocked global markets — the US issued "Sectoral Sanctions Identifications" that barred US persons from providing new long-term financing (over 30 days, later 14) to named Russian banks, and from supplying technology for Arctic, deepwater, and shale oil projects. The firms could still do ordinary business; they just couldn't raise long-term dollar debt or buy advanced drilling kit.

The logic is surgical: choke the financing and the technology that the target needs *for the future* without detonating its present economy. It is the scalpel to the primary block's hammer.

#### Worked example: the slow squeeze of a sectoral financing ban

Here is how a sectoral financing restriction actually bites, because it is the least obvious of the three. Take a Russian energy major that, under the 2014 rules, can still pump and sell oil but cannot raise new dollar debt with a maturity beyond 30 days. Say the firm has \$15 billion of dollar bonds maturing over the next three years and had planned to *roll* them — repay each one by issuing a new bond. Suddenly it cannot. It must now repay that \$15 billion out of cash flow, or refinance domestically in rubles at far higher rates, or shrink its investment program. If it diverts \$5 billion a year of operating cash flow to debt repayment instead of new drilling, its production five years out is materially lower than it would have been. The sanction did not touch a single barrel *today* — it quietly removed the financing that funds *tomorrow's* barrels. This is why sectoral sanctions show up in markets as a slow grind in forward production estimates and credit spreads, not a dramatic same-day price gap. A trader who expects a Russia-style headline reaction from a sectoral measure will be confused when nothing dramatic happens on day one; the impact accrues over quarters.

### The escalation logic: why governments climb the ladder

These three instruments form an escalation ladder, and policymakers climb it deliberately. The sequence usually runs: targeted designations of individuals and a few entities (low market impact, high signaling value) → sectoral restrictions on financing and technology (slow squeeze, contained market impact) → broad primary blocks of major banks (sharp impact) → and at the extreme, central-bank reserve immobilization and SWIFT exclusion (maximum impact, used only against Russia so far). Each rung is reversible and calibrated, which is the appeal: a government can signal resolve, leave room to escalate further if the target doesn't yield, and avoid the irreversibility of military action. For a trader, knowing *which rung* a program sits on — and which rungs remain above it — is half the work of pricing both the current impact and the tail risk of further escalation.

### How a target moves up the ladder over time

It is rare for a target to be hit with the full arsenal at once. Iran's history shows the typical layering: targeted designations of individuals and the Revolutionary Guard, then sectoral pressure on its energy and banking sectors, then the comprehensive 2012 squeeze cutting it from SWIFT, then relief under the JCPOA, then snapback. Russia followed the same staircase compressed into a decade — individual designations and sectoral financing limits in 2014, then the comprehensive 2022 package. The reason the layering matters to a trader is that *the market learns the staircase*. After 2014, every Russia-related headline was read against the question "is this another rung, and how many rungs remain?" By 2022 the market had a mental model of what "maximum pressure" looked like, which is partly why the initial shock, though violent, faded — participants could see that the central-bank freeze and SWIFT cut were near the top of the ladder, with little credible escalation left that wouldn't hurt the sanctioners more.

## The SDN list: the operational core

Everything routes through the SDN list, so it is worth knowing precisely what it is. It is a published, machine-readable file — names, aliases, passport numbers, vessel IMO numbers, crypto wallet addresses, dates of birth — maintained by OFAC and updated, sometimes, several times a week. Banks ingest it into screening software (vendors like Fircosoft, LexisNexis, and Bridger feed thousands of institutions) that fuzzy-matches every name in every transaction against it.

Two technical points have outsized market consequences.

**The 50% rule.** OFAC's guidance says any entity owned **50% or more**, directly or indirectly, by one or more blocked persons is *itself* blocked, even if it is not named on the list. So a sanctioned oligarch's holding company is blocked, and so are the subsidiaries that company majority-owns. This is why sanctions ripple outward unpredictably: a single oligarch designation can freeze dozens of unnamed companies, and compliance teams must trace ownership webs to figure out who is captured. It is also a leak point — drop ownership to 49% and the subsidiary is, on paper, clean.

**False positives and de-risking.** Screening engines are tuned to over-flag, because a missed match means a nine- or ten-figure fine. The result is enormous collateral damage: ordinary firms with names similar to SDN entries get their wires held; banks "de-risk" by simply dropping entire countries or customer classes rather than do the diligence. Whole regions — Somalia, parts of the Caribbean — lost correspondent banking access not because they were sanctioned but because banks decided the screening risk wasn't worth the small revenue. That de-risking is itself a market force.

#### Worked example: sizing the penalty that drives compliance

Why do banks comply so zealously? Look at the numbers. In 2014, BNP Paribas pleaded guilty to processing roughly \$8.8 billion of transactions for Sudanese, Iranian, and Cuban entities over several years, and paid an **\$8.9 billion** penalty — at the time the largest sanctions fine in history. In 2019, Standard Chartered paid \$1.1 billion; in 2012, HSBC paid \$1.9 billion in a related money-laundering and sanctions case. Set that against the economics: a bank might earn a \$20,000 fee on a \$2 million Iranian oil wire. Risking a multi-billion-dollar fine and possible loss of its US dollar-clearing license to earn \$20,000 is, for any sane treasurer, never worth it. The fine asymmetry — billions of downside against thousands of upside — is the real enforcement engine. OFAC sets the price of getting caught so high that banks police themselves.

## What enforcement actually looks like — and where it leaks

Now we can put the chain together and see the failure modes. Authority flows Treasury → OFAC → SDN → correspondent bank → local bank → target. At each handoff, a transaction either passes a compliance check or finds a way around it.

![Graph of the enforcement chain showing a compliant path where the wire is blocked and an evasion path through shells and third countries](/imgs/blogs/how-sanctions-work-primary-secondary-and-the-enforcement-gap-5.png)

The evasion paths are not exotic. They are well-worn, and they explain why a sanctioned economy rarely collapses the way the announcement implies.

**Third-country routing and transshipment.** Goods and money are routed through a non-sanctioning country that adds a layer of obscurity. Russian oil is sold to a trader in the UAE or a refiner in India; the refined product is then perfectly legal to sell anywhere, "Latvian gasoline" rather than "Russian crude." Microchips banned for export to Russia show up first in Armenia, Kazakhstan, or Turkey, whose imports of US semiconductors from neighboring suppliers spiked implausibly after 2022 before being re-exported.

**Shadow fleets.** To move oil under a price cap or embargo, you need ships and insurance that don't answer to Western authorities. A "dark fleet" of roughly 600 ageing tankers — old, opaquely owned, often switching off their transponders (AIS) at sea and re-flagging through registries like Gabon or the Cook Islands — emerged to carry Russian and Iranian crude outside the Western insurance and shipping system. The cap was meant to bite by denying Western services; the shadow fleet routes around the services entirely.

**Barter and non-dollar settlement.** If the problem is the dollar's plumbing, settle in something else. India bought Russian oil partly in rupees and dirhams; Iran and China have run oil-for-goods barter. Russia and China expanded settlement in rubles and yuan. None of this is frictionless — rupee balances piled up that Russia struggled to spend, and yuan is not freely convertible — but it keeps trade flowing outside the screen.

**Crypto and informal value transfer.** For smaller flows, cryptocurrency and old hawala-style networks move value without a correspondent bank in the loop. North Korea's Lazarus Group has stolen and laundered billions in crypto to fund the state. Crypto is over-hyped as a sanctions buster — chain analysis is good and most laundering eventually touches a regulated exchange — but for marginal, smaller transfers it is a real gap.

**The 50% workaround and renamed entities.** Sanctioned firms spin up new subsidiaries below the 50% ownership threshold, rename, re-domicile, and re-emerge with clean paperwork. Compliance is always a step behind the corporate restructuring.

![Before and after comparison of tight enforcement with frozen reserves versus leaky enforcement with shadow fleets and third-country routing](/imgs/blogs/how-sanctions-work-primary-secondary-and-the-enforcement-gap-4.png)

The lesson is structural, not moral. A sanction's *announced* reach is its theoretical maximum. Its *realized* reach is whatever survives after every counterparty with an incentive to evade has had a few months to reorganize. The gap between the two is the enforcement gap, and it is large.

### Anatomy of the price cap: a sanction designed around its own leak

The Russian oil price cap, introduced in December 2022, deserves a close look because it was the first major sanction explicitly *engineered* around the enforcement gap — an admission, in policy form, that you cannot simply stop the oil. The Western coalition did not try to embargo Russian crude outright, because removing roughly five million barrels a day from the market would have sent global prices to levels that hurt the sanctioners more than the target. Instead the cap said: Russia may keep selling oil, but Western shipping, insurance, and financing services may only be used if the cargo sold for *under* a set price (initially \$60 per barrel). Since Western insurers (the London-centered "P&I clubs") covered the overwhelming majority of global tanker tonnage, the theory was that Russia would be forced to sell cheap to keep access to insurance.

The leak was foreseeable and it came fast. Russia and its buyers built the shadow fleet — buying up old tankers and arranging non-Western insurance, often of dubious quality — specifically to carry cargoes that *didn't* use Western services and therefore weren't bound by the cap at all. Within a year, a large share of Russian seaborne crude was moving on this dark fleet, outside the cap's reach. The market read this precisely through the Urals discount: it gapped wide (near \$30/bbl below Brent) when the cap was new and Russia scrambled, then narrowed toward \$12/bbl through 2024 as the evasion infrastructure matured and more buyers grew comfortable. The cap did extract a real cost — that persistent discount is billions of dollars of foregone Russian revenue, exactly its design intent — but it did not keep the oil off the market, and Russian oil revenue stayed robust. The cap is the clearest case study you have of the central thesis: a well-designed sanction imposes a *toll*, priced live as a discount, rather than a *blockade*.

#### Worked example: who actually pays the price cap

Trace the dollars. Before the cap, Russia sold Urals at roughly Brent minus \$2 — call it \$83 when Brent is \$85. After the cap and the scramble for shipping, Urals traded around Brent minus \$30 — about \$55. On exports of, say, 3 million barrels a day of crude, the extra \$28 discount is \$28 × 3,000,000 = **\$84 million a day**, or roughly \$30 billion a year transferred away from Russia. But follow where it went: a chunk became margin for the Indian and Chinese refiners buying cheap, a chunk became freight and (often inflated) insurance fees for the shadow-fleet operators and Gulf intermediaries, and only a portion was pure lost rent. As the discount narrowed to \$12/bbl by 2024, the annual toll fell to roughly \$13 billion. The shrinking number *is* the enforcement gap closing in real time — and any trader watching the Urals-Brent spread had a cleaner read on the sanction's bite than anyone reading the political headlines.

## The Iran case study: sanctions that bite, then bleed off

No regime illustrates the cycle better than Iran's oil, because we can watch the export volumes respond in near-real-time to the tightening and loosening of enforcement.

![Line chart of Iran crude oil exports in million barrels per day from 2011 to 2024 showing the impact of sanctions and the JCPOA](/imgs/blogs/how-sanctions-work-primary-secondary-and-the-enforcement-gap-3.png)

Trace the curve. In 2011, Iran exported around 2.5 million barrels per day. The 2012 escalation — the US and EU coordinated to cut Iran off from SWIFT and threatened secondary sanctions on any bank buying Iranian oil — slashed exports to roughly 1.1–1.5 mb/d within a year. That is what *multilateral, financially-enforced* sanctions look like: a fast, deep cut, because Europe and Asia's big buyers cooperated and the threat to banks was credible.

Then the JCPOA nuclear deal was signed in July 2015. Sanctions relief let exports ramp back toward 2.5 mb/d by 2017–early 2018. In May 2018 the US unilaterally withdrew and "snapped back" sanctions. Exports collapsed again toward 0.5 mb/d by 2019. But here is the tell: the second collapse, though sharp, did not hold. By 2022–2023 Iran was exporting 1.2–1.8 mb/d again, almost all to China, sold at steep discounts through a shadow fleet and intermediary traders. The *same* legal regime that cut exports to 0.5 mb/d in 2019 leaked back up to 1.8 mb/d by 2023 — not because the law changed, but because enforcement of the secondary threat against Chinese buyers was never seriously prosecuted, and the evasion infrastructure matured.

#### Worked example: the discount as the price of the enforcement gap

Iran's discount is the cleanest financial reading of the enforcement gap. Suppose benchmark Brent trades at \$85 per barrel and Iran must sell its crude at a \$15 discount to find a willing, sanctions-tolerant buyer. On 1.6 million barrels per day, that discount costs Iran \$15 × 1,600,000 = **\$24 million per day**, or roughly \$8.8 billion a year of foregone revenue. The sanctions did not stop the oil; they imposed a *toll*. The size of that toll — the discount — is a live, market-priced estimate of how leaky the regime is. A widening discount says enforcement is tightening (fewer willing buyers, more risk premium demanded); a narrowing discount says the evasion machine is winning. Russia's Urals discount told the identical story: a peak near \$30/bbl when the cap was fresh, narrowing to about \$12/bbl by 2024 as India and China built out the buying-and-shipping infrastructure to absorb the barrels.

### The chip story: where sanctions worked better than the oil story

Iran's oil is the case where sanctions leaked the most, but Russia's *technology* is the case where they bit hardest, and the contrast is instructive. Oil is a fungible commodity with many willing buyers, so it routes around restrictions easily. Advanced semiconductors are the opposite: they are made by a tiny number of firms (TSMC, Samsung, Intel) using equipment from an even tinier number (ASML's extreme-ultraviolet lithography machines are a near-monopoly), and there is no shadow fleet for a 3-nanometer chip. So when the 2022 export controls hit, Russia's imports of advanced chips fell by an estimated **70%**. Russia could still smuggle consumer-grade chips out of washing machines and route older parts through Armenia and Kazakhstan, but the cutting-edge components that go into precision weapons and modern data centers became genuinely scarce and expensive.

The lesson generalizes into a single rule for estimating any enforcement gap: *the gap is a function of how substitutable and fungible the sanctioned thing is.* Fungible commodities with many suppliers and buyers (oil, wheat, base metals) leak heavily. Concentrated, non-substitutable, hard-to-make goods (advanced chips, certain aircraft parts, EUV lithography) leak slowly and the sanction holds. When you assess a new program, your first question should be: is the target item more like oil or more like a 3-nanometer chip? The answer tells you most of what you need to know about whether the sanction will bite or bleed.

## A short history: how the toolkit grew teeth

Sanctions are old, but the *modern, dollar-enforced, secondary-reaching* version is recent. The arc matters because it explains why the tool keeps getting reached for despite a modest success record.

![Timeline of US sanctions milestones from the 1962 Cuba embargo through the 2022 Russia SWIFT cut](/imgs/blogs/how-sanctions-work-primary-secondary-and-the-enforcement-gap-6.png)

The 1962 Cuba embargo was a blunt trade ban — no Cuban goods, no US trade — and it is still in place six decades later, having achieved essentially none of its regime-change goal. The 1979 freeze of Iranian assets after the embassy hostage crisis pioneered the *asset-freeze* as a fast financial weapon. The 1990 Iraq embargo, multilateral through the UN, was among the most comprehensive ever and devastated Iraq's economy — and is the source of the grim debate about civilian harm that shadows sanctions policy to this day. The 1990s Balkans sanctions refined the targeting of individuals and regimes rather than whole populations.

The Iran cycle of 1979 onward deserves a second look because it shows the asset-freeze maturing into a strategic weapon. When the US froze roughly \$12 billion of Iranian assets during the 1979–1981 hostage crisis, it discovered that immobilizing a state's offshore holdings was fast, reversible, and did not require firing a shot — the freeze became a bargaining chip resolved in the 1981 Algiers Accords. That template, refined over the following decades, is a direct ancestor of the 2022 Russian reserve freeze; the difference is only one of scale and coordination. The 1990 Iraq embargo, by contrast, is the cautionary tale on the other side: a near-total UN trade embargo that crippled the economy, contributed to severe civilian hardship through the decade, and yet did not dislodge Saddam Hussein — only an invasion did. That episode hardened the policy consensus toward "smart" or "targeted" sanctions on individuals and sectors rather than whole-population embargoes, a shift that defines the modern toolkit.

The genuine inflection was post-9/11. Treasury, hunting terrorist financing, built the modern financial-intelligence and correspondent-banking enforcement apparatus — the machinery that makes secondary sanctions credible. By the time the 2014 Russia sectoral sanctions and the 2015–2018 Iran cycle came around, the US could weaponize the dollar's plumbing with a precision unimaginable in 1962. The 2022 Russia package — central-bank reserve freeze plus SWIFT exclusion — was the apex of that capability. Notice the trajectory: from banning *goods* (Cuba) to freezing *assets* (Iran 1979) to controlling *dollar clearing* (post-9/11) to immobilizing a G20 central bank's *reserves* (Russia 2022). Each step extended reach. None of them solved the leak problem.

## The 40% number: what the evidence actually says

The single most useful fact to carry into any sanctions trade is that they usually do not achieve their *stated* political objective. The foundational empirical work — Hufbauer, Schott, Elliott, and Oegg at the Peterson Institute, who built a database of 200-plus 20th-century sanctions episodes — found an overall "success" rate of roughly **34%**, where success is defined generously as the target making at least a partial policy concession at least partly attributable to the sanctions. Subsequent scholars, applying stricter criteria (notably Robert Pape), argued the real rate of *decisive* policy change is closer to 5%. Even the optimistic reading lands around one in three.

Drill into the conditions and the picture sharpens:

- **Financial sanctions beat trade sanctions** by roughly 2–3×. Cutting off banking access (which is hard to replace) hurts more than blocking goods (which can be sourced elsewhere).
- **Multilateral beats unilateral** by about 2×. The 2012 multilateral Iran squeeze worked; the 2018 unilateral US version leaked because buyers had a willing alternative in China.
- **Short beats long.** Episodes resolved within five years succeed maybe half the time; those grinding past a decade fall toward 20%. Time lets the target build the evasion infrastructure — the shadow fleet, the routing, the barter channels — and adapt its economy.
- **Modest goals beat regime change.** Asking a target to release a prisoner or tweak a policy works far more often than demanding a government fall. Cuba, Iran, North Korea, and now Russia are all cases where the *maximal* goal — regime or fundamental policy change — was not achieved despite real economic pain.

![Grid scorecard rating Cuba, Iran, Russia, and North Korea on compliance, economic impact, and policy change achieved](/imgs/blogs/how-sanctions-work-primary-secondary-and-the-enforcement-gap-7.png)

The scorecard makes the central paradox visible: economic *impact* is often high — Iran's exports were genuinely gutted, North Korea is genuinely poor, Russia's chip imports fell — while policy *change* stays low. Pain and persuasion are different variables. Sanctions are reliably good at imposing cost and unreliably bad at changing behavior. For a trader, that distinction is the whole game: the *cost* (discounts, frozen flows, supply disruption) is what moves prices, and it is real even when the political goal fails.

### Why economic pain so rarely buys political change

Why does the link between pain and capitulation break so reliably? Several mechanisms compound. First, the **rally-round-the-flag effect**: external economic pressure often *strengthens* a regime's domestic support by letting it blame foreigners for hardship — the leadership reframes the sanction as a siege to be heroically endured. Second, **elite insulation**: sanctions hit ordinary citizens hardest while the politically connected elite, who control the import licenses and the smuggling routes, often *profit* from scarcity and rising black-market prices. The very people a sanction needs to pressure are frequently the ones it enriches. Third, **adaptation**: given a few years, an economy reorganizes — import substitution, new trading partners, new settlement rails — and the marginal pain of the sanction fades even as it remains formally in place. Cuba has had six decades to adapt; the embargo is now part of the economic furniture, not an acute shock.

This is the deep reason the effectiveness rate clusters around one in three and falls with duration. It also explains the policy paradox that sanctions keep getting used despite the record: they are cheaper and more domestically popular than war, they signal resolve, and the *cost* they impose is real and visible even when the *change* never comes. A government can point to a collapsed ruble or gutted exports as evidence it "did something," and that political accounting is what keeps the tool in constant rotation.

### Sanctions are not free for the sanctioner

A trader also has to price the *blowback*, because sanctions impose costs on the country imposing them and those costs constrain how far a program will go. Western firms wrote off billions exiting Russia (Shell, BP, Renault, and dozens more took large impairments). Energy sanctions raise prices for the sanctioners' own consumers — Europe's gas crisis of 2022 was partly self-inflicted. Lost export markets hurt the sanctioner's manufacturers (US farmers lost Chinese soy sales in the trade war; German machine-tool makers lost Russia). And the deepest cost is the structural one already noted: each aggressive use erodes the dollar's unquestioned centrality by giving every potential future target a reason to build off-ramps. The practical implication is that the *threat* of escalation has limits — a sanctioner will not impose a measure whose blowback exceeds the pain it inflicts, which is exactly why a full embargo of Russian oil was never attempted and why secondary sanctions against major Chinese banks have been threatened far more often than used.

#### Worked example: converting "40% effective" into a trade expectation

You read that the US is about to impose fresh secondary sanctions on a target's oil sector. How should you price it? Start from base rates. Unilateral + financial + ambitious goal + likely to run for years → call it well under the 34% headline, maybe a 15–20% chance of achieving the *political* objective. But the *economic* impact — supply disruption, a discount, some frozen flows — is near-certain in the short run regardless. So the trade is not "will the sanction succeed?" (probably not) but "how big and how durable is the supply/flow disruption before the evasion machine catches up?" If you expect, say, 0.8 mb/d of crude knocked offline for six to twelve months before third-country routing absorbs it, you can size an oil-price impact and a fade. The 40% number tells you to bet on *cost*, fade the *capitulation*, and put your time horizon on the *enforcement-gap closure* — typically a few quarters, not years.

## Common misconceptions

A few beliefs about sanctions are so widespread that they actively mislead traders. Each one, examined, points back to the same enforcement-gap logic.

**"Sanctions are an alternative to war."** They are sometimes a *prelude* to war or a substitute for doing nothing, but framing them as the peaceful option that works obscures the 60–70% failure rate. Policymakers reach for them because they are visible, fast, and domestically popular — not because they reliably work. The honest framing is that sanctions are a tool for imposing *cost* and *signaling resolve*, and only occasionally a tool for *changing behavior*; conflating the three is the root of most bad sanctions analysis.

**"Freezing reserves bankrupts the target."** Freezing \$300 billion of Russian reserves was dramatic, but a commodity exporter running a current-account surplus doesn't need its reserves to fund imports — it earns fresh hard currency every day from selling oil and gas. Reserves matter for defending a currency in a crisis; they matter far less for an economy still earning export dollars. That is why the ruble recovered: the income kept coming.

**"The dollar makes US sanctions permanent."** The dollar's centrality is the source of US sanctions power, but heavy use accelerates *de-dollarization* efforts — BRICS settlement schemes, China's CIPS as a SWIFT alternative, central-bank gold buying. Each aggressive use teaches counterparties to build off-ramps. The power is real but it is being slowly taxed by its own overuse, which is a genuine long-horizon macro theme.

**"If a country is sanctioned, you can't touch anything connected to it."** Licenses, exemptions, and wind-down periods riddle every program. Humanitarian goods, certain energy payments (the US repeatedly carved out channels for European gas payments to Russia), and general licenses for specific activities exist. The legal reality is far more porous than the headline.

**"Secondary sanctions mean the US controls the world."** They give the US enormous leverage *as long as the dollar is indispensable and the target's economy is small relative to the foreign firm's US business*. Against China — whose own market and dollar-clearing volume are too large to casually sacrifice — secondary threats are far less credible, which is exactly why Chinese buyers of Iranian and Russian oil have largely gone unpunished.

**"Sanctions are a precise, surgical tool."** The 50% rule and aggressive over-screening make sanctions far blunter than the "targeted" branding suggests. A single oligarch designation cascades to dozens of unnamed majority-owned subsidiaries; de-risking banks drop entire countries; humanitarian goods get caught despite carve-outs because banks won't risk processing *anything* touching a sanctioned jurisdiction. The collateral damage to ordinary citizens and to legitimate third-party trade is a feature of how the enforcement is outsourced, not a bug that better targeting can fully fix. For a trader, this bluntness is why second-order victims (suppliers, neighboring economies, correspondent-banking-starved regions) often move more than the headline target.

**"A frozen asset is a seized asset."** Freezing and seizing are different, and the difference is a live legal and market question. The \$300 billion of Russian reserves is *frozen* — immobilized, earning interest the West has begun redirecting to Ukraine — but outright *confiscation* raises thorny questions of sovereign immunity and sets a precedent that alarms every reserve manager. The unresolved status matters because full confiscation would be the single most powerful accelerant of de-dollarization yet, and the market treats the prospect as a tail risk rather than a base case.

### What a designation does to a tradeable security

Bring this down to the instrument level, because that is where sanctions become a concrete trade. When an entity with listed securities is added to the SDN list, the immediate consequences are mechanical: US persons may no longer buy, hold, or in some cases even sell the security without a specific license; index providers (MSCI, FTSE) drop it from their indices, forcing passive funds to dump it regardless of price; exchanges may suspend or delist it; and clearing and custody for US investors freezes. The result is a near-instant collapse in the security's accessible price and a liquidity vacuum — there are sellers forced out and few permitted buyers. The 2022 designations saw Russian sovereign and corporate bonds, and the depositary receipts of major Russian firms, gap toward distressed or untradeable levels within days. The forced, price-insensitive selling by passive index funds is itself a recurring feature worth understanding: it creates dislocations that are not about fundamentals at all, only about the mechanics of who is *allowed* to hold the paper.

#### Worked example: the forced-seller dislocation

Suppose an emerging-market bond fund holds \$80 million of a soon-to-be-sanctioned issuer's dollar bonds, marked at 95 cents on the dollar. The designation lands. The fund's mandate and US law require it to exit, but the buyer universe just shrank to a handful of distressed-debt specialists who *are* permitted to hold the paper under a wind-down license. With forced sellers and almost no bids, the bonds gap to, say, 20 cents — a \$60 million mark-to-market loss driven not by any change in the issuer's ability to pay but purely by the collapse of the *permitted-holder* base. A distressed specialist who can legally warehouse the paper through the wind-down period might buy at 20 and recover 40–50 as the dust settles. The dislocation — the gap between fundamental recovery value and the forced-seller clearing price — is the trade, and it exists *because* of the enforcement mechanics, not despite them.

## How it shows up in real markets

Sanctions are a recurring, tradeable macro catalyst, and once you have the legal anatomy in hand the market patterns become surprisingly repeatable. The single most important behavioral regularity is the *announce-then-fade* arc: the market prices something close to the theoretical maximum disruption on the headline, then grinds back toward the realized impact as the enforcement gap reveals itself over the following weeks and quarters. Almost every sanctions episode rhymes with this structure, and most of the recurring trades below are variations on correctly estimating where the realized impact will settle relative to the panicked opening print.

**Oil and the risk premium.** Energy is where sanctions hit hardest because the targets are so often petrostates. The market prices a *supply-disruption premium* on the announcement, then *fades it* as the enforcement gap reveals itself. The 2022 Brent spike toward \$120-plus on the Russia package faded back into the \$70s–\$80s once it became clear the barrels were still flowing through the shadow fleet. The tradeable edge is in correctly estimating how much supply *actually* leaves the market versus how much just gets rerouted at a discount.

**Currencies.** A targeted economy's currency gaps lower on a comprehensive financial sanction (ruble −47%, Iranian rial in chronic collapse), but the durability depends on the current account. Export-surplus targets see currency recoveries that catch shorts offside. Import-dependent ones (the rial) stay broken. The ruble's 2022 round-trip is the canonical trap: shorts piled in on the −47% move, then the combination of continued energy export income, aggressive capital controls (forcing exporters to convert hard currency, banning outflows), and a sharp rate hike to 20% reversed most of the depreciation within weeks. The lesson is to separate the *flow* picture (is the country still earning hard currency?) from the *stock* picture (are its reserves frozen?). A frozen-reserve, export-surplus economy can defend its currency with controls and fresh income far longer than the dramatic opening move suggests, while an import-dependent economy with no export cushion sees its currency stay broken regardless of headlines.

**Commodities beyond oil.** Russia is a top producer of nickel, palladium, wheat, and fertilizer; sanctions and self-sanctioning by buyers spike those markets. Palladium and nickel both had violent 2022 moves. China's dominance of rare earths makes *its* potential retaliatory export controls a mirror-image risk. The map of "what is this target a chokepoint for?" is the map of where the price shocks land.

**Specific equities and credit.** Designated firms' bonds and any listed equity gap to near-zero or get delisted; their *suppliers and customers* — often un-sanctioned but exposed — sell off in sympathy. European firms with Russia revenue (Renault, Société Générale, Raiffeisen) took large hits in 2022 from write-offs, not from being sanctioned themselves.

**The de-risking and compliance trade.** Sanctions are a structural tailwind for compliance-technology firms, trade-finance specialists who can navigate the rules, and — perversely — the intermediaries and shippers who profit from the enforcement gap (Greek shipowners and Gulf traders had banner years moving discounted crude).

**Gold and non-dollar reserve assets.** This is the slow-burn beneficiary of heavy sanctions use. When the G7 froze Russia's dollar and euro reserves in 2022, every central bank in the non-aligned world absorbed a lesson: dollar reserves can be switched off. The response has been visible in the data — central-bank gold buying ran at multi-decade highs in 2022–2024, led by China, Turkey, India, and others diversifying away from seizable paper assets toward a reserve asset no one can freeze. This is not a headline trade; it is a structural bid under gold that compounds with each new aggressive sanctions program. It is the clearest market expression of the de-dollarization theme, and unlike the over-hyped "the dollar is collapsing tomorrow" narrative, the gold bid is real, measurable, and persistent.

**Volatility and the fade pattern.** Sanctions headlines reliably spike implied volatility in the affected commodity and currency, then bleed it out as the enforcement gap reveals itself. The repeatable structure — sharp announcement spike, multi-week fade as evasion infrastructure absorbs the shock — is itself tradeable for those who position in options around the pattern rather than chasing the spot move.

### A note on the second-order winners

One of the least-appreciated market consequences of sanctions is the redistribution they create. A sanction does not destroy the value of the sanctioned trade; it *relocates* the rent. When Russian crude must sell at a \$20 discount, that \$20 does not vanish — it is split between the discounted buyer (Indian refiners enjoyed record margins in 2022–2023, in part by buying cheap Russian crude and exporting refined products to Europe at full price), the intermediaries who arrange the now-complicated logistics, and the shippers and insurers who command a premium for tolerating the risk. The map of "who profits from the enforcement gap" is as tradeable as the map of "who is hurt by the sanction," and it is far less crowded because most analysts stop at the obvious victim. Indian refining margins, Greek and Gulf shipping economics, and the fortunes of obscure trading houses in Dubai and Geneva are all downstream of how leaky a given sanction turns out to be.

#### Worked example: pricing a sanctions headline in real time

A headline crosses: "US to sanction [target]'s largest oil exporter under secondary authority." Walk the chain in your head. First, primary vs. secondary — secondary means the bite depends on whether *foreign buyers* comply, so ask who buys this oil. If it's mostly China, discount the bite heavily (low compliance, as the scorecard shows). Second, multilateral or unilateral — is the EU in? If not, halve your impact estimate. Third, the flow size — how many barrels per day, and is there a shadow-fleet/third-country route already built (as for Iran and Russia) or is this a fresh target with no evasion infrastructure yet? A fresh target with no escape routes is a *much* bigger short-term supply shock than adding another layer to an already-evading target. Put numbers on it: if you judge 1.0 mb/d genuinely at risk for two quarters before routing absorbs it, that's a meaningful but fadeable Brent catalyst. The mechanical discipline — primary/secondary, multilateral/unilateral, flow size, evasion maturity — turns a scary headline into a sized, time-bounded trade.

## How to trade it

Pulling the analysis into an actionable checklist.

**1. Classify the sanction before you classify the trade.** Read the actual designation. Is it primary (binds US persons — the target is cut from the dollar directly), secondary (binds foreigners — bite depends on their compliance), or sectoral (restricts activities — slower, surgical)? A full SDN block is a step-change; a sectoral financing restriction is a slow squeeze. Different instruments, different price paths.

**2. Estimate the enforcement gap, not the announcement.** The headline gives you the theoretical maximum impact. Your job is to estimate realized impact: who has an incentive and a route to evade, how mature is the evasion infrastructure, and how credible is the secondary threat against the key buyers? The gap is widest when the target is a commodity exporter with willing large buyers (China/India) and time to adapt.

**3. Trade the cost, fade the capitulation.** Sanctions reliably impose economic *cost* (discounts, disruption, write-offs) and unreliably produce political *capitulation*. Position for the near-term supply/flow disruption; fade the "this will force them to fold" narrative, because the base rate says it usually won't.

**4. Watch the discount as your live gauge.** For sanctioned commodities, the discount to the benchmark (Urals to Brent, Iranian crude to Dubai) is a real-time, market-priced read on how leaky enforcement is. Widening discount = tightening enforcement = bullish the benchmark and the disruption trade. Narrowing discount = the evasion machine is winning = time to fade.

**5. Map the chokepoints, not just the headline target.** Ask what the target is a global chokepoint for — Russia for palladium/nickel/wheat, China for rare earths, a Gulf state for shipping lanes. The second-order commodity and supplier exposures often offer better risk/reward than the obvious oil trade, because they are less crowded.

**6. Respect the licenses and wind-downs.** Programs carve out exceptions, grant general licenses, and set wind-down periods. A "total ban" with a 180-day wind-down and an energy-payment carve-out is not a total ban for two quarters. Read the OFAC FAQs, not just the press release.

**7. Hold the long-horizon de-dollarization theme lightly but seriously.** Each aggressive sanction nudges counterparties toward alternative settlement (CIPS, yuan, gold, BRICS schemes). This is glacial and over-hyped on any given day, but it is a real structural tax on US sanctions power and a slow tailwind for gold and non-dollar reserve assets. Don't trade it on a headline; do weight it in a multi-year reserve-asset thesis.

**8. Distinguish the forced-seller dislocation from the fundamental move.** When a security is designated, the early price collapse is often driven by mechanical forced selling (index ejection, mandate-driven exits) into a shrunken permitted-buyer base, not by fundamentals. That dislocation can overshoot recovery value badly. If you have the legal capacity to hold sanctioned-or-distressed paper through a wind-down, the gap between the forced-seller clearing price and the eventual recovery is one of the cleaner risk-adjusted opportunities sanctions throw off — precisely because most participants are *forbidden* from competing for it.

The throughline of all eight: a sanction is a legal instruction that ends at a compliance screen, and every dollar of mispricing lives in the gap between what the screen is *supposed* to catch and what it *actually* does. The announcement gives you the theoretical maximum; the discount, the routing, the substitutability of the sanctioned good, and the credibility of the secondary threat give you the realized impact. Size the cost, fade the capitulation, watch the discount as your live gauge, and put your time horizon on the closing of the enforcement gap — usually a few quarters. Trade the gap, not the announcement.
