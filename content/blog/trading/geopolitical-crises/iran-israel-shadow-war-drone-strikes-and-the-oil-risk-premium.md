On the night of April 13, 2024, Iran launched something unprecedented in the history of the modern Middle East: a direct military attack on the sovereign territory of Israel. Not through a proxy. Not via a militia. Iran itself fired 170 drones, 120 ballistic missiles, and 30 cruise missiles toward Israeli population centers and military installations.

By any measure of geopolitical gravity, this was a historic crossing. Iran and Israel had been fighting a shadow war for decades — through assassinations, cyberattacks, naval sabotage, and proxy militias across five countries. But they had always maintained a thin veneer of plausible deniability. April 13 shredded that veneer.

Oil markets opened on Sunday April 14 with Brent crude up about \$3 — a 3.4% spike from \$90.8 to roughly \$93.50 at the peak intraday. Within two weeks, Brent had given back the entire move and was trading below its pre-attack level. By June 2024 it was at \$84 — lower than the day of the attack.

If you think that reaction seems wrong — that the first direct exchange of missiles between two regional powers should have sent oil prices violently higher — you are not alone. Understanding *why* the market barely blinked, and what it would have taken to move oil *sustainably*, is the entire subject of this post.

![Iran proxy network architecture from IRGC through five armed groups to targets across Middle East](/imgs/blogs/iran-israel-shadow-war-drone-strikes-and-the-oil-risk-premium-1.png)

The diagram above shows the architecture of the conflict that preceded April 13, 2024. Iran does not fight Israel with its own soldiers in any meaningful quantity. It fights Israel through five proxy networks across the region, each funded by the IRGC's Quds Force (Iran's external operations arm), and each carrying a specific military function in Tehran's strategic logic. Understanding that architecture is the prerequisite for understanding the market reaction.

## Foundations: Oil markets, geopolitical risk, and the price transmission chain

Before we dig into the Iran-Israel dynamic, we need a firm foundation in three things: how oil markets price risk, what a "risk premium" actually means in a commodity context, and why the *type* of disruption matters as much as the *size*.

### How oil markets work at a basic level

Oil is traded globally as a commodity, meaning it is fungible — a barrel of Brent crude oil from the North Sea and a barrel of Arab Light crude from Saudi Arabia are not identical (they differ in sulfur content, density, and hence refining yield), but they are close enough substitutes that they trade within a few dollars of each other on global markets.

The *spot price* of oil is what you would pay right now for a barrel to be delivered in the near term. The *futures price* is what participants agree today to pay (or receive) for a barrel delivered at some future date. Both prices are set continuously by buyers and sellers — refineries, traders, hedge funds, oil producers, and governments — around the clock on the NYMEX (New York) and ICE (London) exchanges.

Oil prices move when the *expected balance between supply and demand* changes. If you believe supply is about to fall, you want to lock in buying at today's price before it rises — so you bid up futures, which pulls spot prices up too. If supply is abundant and demand is flat, sellers compete, prices fall.

A *geopolitical risk premium* is the component of the oil price that reflects the possibility of supply disruption from conflict or political instability — not the disruption itself, but the *probability* that disruption might happen. This is a subtle but crucial distinction, and it is why markets responded the way they did in April 2024.

### The expected value framework

Markets price assets based on *expected* outcomes, not worst-case outcomes. In academic finance, the "risk premium" on any asset class is the extra return (or extra price) you need to hold a risky asset versus a risk-free one.

For oil, the geopolitical risk premium can be roughly approximated like this:

```
Oil risk premium ≈ P(disruption) × Size_of_disruption_USD/bbl
```

Where P(disruption) is the market's estimated probability that a conflict actually disrupts supply flows, and Size_of_disruption is how much each barrel of oil would move in price if that disruption occurred.

This framework explains the April 2024 reaction precisely: the probability of disruption spiked briefly, then collapsed as de-escalation signals arrived, and the underlying physical supply never moved. We will walk through the exact numbers in the worked examples below.

### Why the type of disruption matters

Not all geopolitical events are equal for oil markets. There are three categories:

1. **Headline noise**: Rhetoric, political speeches, military exercises, and proxy skirmishes that create temporary fear without any physical impact on supply. Markets price these at low probability; they revert quickly when no disruption materializes.

2. **Chokepoint threats**: Specific threats to the physical infrastructure through which oil flows — tankers, pipelines, terminals, or straits. The Strait of Hormuz is the archetypal example. These get priced more persistently because a physical chokepoint can genuinely constrain flows.

3. **Actual supply disruption**: When oil production or transit is physically reduced — a pipeline blown up, a terminal seized, a port blockaded. This is when oil moves and *stays* moved, because the supply-demand imbalance is real, not probabilistic.

April 2024 was Category 1 for oil. The Houthi Red Sea campaign (which we will cover in detail) was Category 2-3, and *that* is what markets actually priced in a sustained way.

## The shadow war architecture: Iran's five-proxy strategy

Iran's approach to projecting power in the Middle East is elegant in its strategic logic. The Islamic Republic cannot openly wage conventional war against Israel — it would invite devastating retaliation from both Israel and the United States. But it can fund, arm, train, and direct a network of non-state armed groups — *proxies* — that pursue Iran's goals while maintaining a layer of plausible deniability.

Plausible deniability means: when Hamas fires rockets at Tel Aviv, Iran can say "we support the Palestinian cause, but we did not order this specific attack." When Houthi drones hit a Saudi oil terminal, Iran can say "the Houthis are acting independently to defend Yemen from Saudi aggression." The claim is often false or deeply misleading, but it creates just enough diplomatic ambiguity to prevent automatic escalation to direct war.

This is a strategy that has been developing since the 1979 Islamic Revolution. Let us look at each proxy network:

### Hezbollah (Lebanon): the crown jewel

Hezbollah — the "Party of God" — is Iran's most capable and best-resourced proxy. It was created by the IRGC in southern Lebanon in the early 1980s and has been Iran's primary instrument of pressure on Israel's northern border ever since.

Hezbollah receives an estimated \$700 million per year from Iran, making it Iran's most expensive but most capable investment. The group maintains an arsenal of over 150,000 rockets and missiles — ranging from small unguided Katyushas to precision-guided Fateh-110 missiles capable of hitting specific buildings in Tel Aviv. It operates a military wing with 20,000–50,000 active fighters (with reserves up to 100,000), battle-hardened from years of fighting in Syria.

For Israel, Hezbollah is an existential-level concern in a way that most other regional threats are not. A full-scale Hezbollah war would involve simultaneous salvos of hundreds of missiles per day targeting Israeli cities — a scenario Israeli missile defense (Iron Dome, David's Sling, Arrow) is not fully designed to handle at that volume.

### Hamas (Gaza): the pressure valve

Hamas is the Palestinian Islamist movement that has controlled the Gaza Strip since 2007. Like Hezbollah, it receives substantial Iranian funding — an estimated \$300 million per year in direct and indirect support — plus weapons, training, and strategic guidance.

Hamas's October 7, 2023 attack on Israel — the most deadly attack on Jews since the Holocaust, killing approximately 1,200 people — was coordinated by Hamas's military wing (Qassam Brigades). While the precise degree of Iranian pre-authorization for October 7 remains debated among analysts, Hamas could not have developed and maintained the military capability it deployed without Iranian material support over many years.

### The Houthis (Yemen): the surprise weapon

The Ansar Allah movement — commonly called the Houthis — controls most of northwestern Yemen including the capital Sanaa and the port city of Hodeidah. They receive approximately \$200 million per year in Iranian military assistance, including critically, ballistic missiles, anti-ship missiles, and drone technology.

The Houthis were previously seen mainly as a regional problem for Saudi Arabia and a humanitarian catastrophe in Yemen. After October 7, they became a major global economic force: their campaign against commercial shipping in the Red Sea and Gulf of Aden directly disrupted a route through which roughly \$1 trillion of annual trade flows. We will examine this in detail.

### PIJ and Iraqi militias: tactical pressure

Palestinian Islamic Jihad (PIJ) is a Gaza-based militant group distinct from Hamas, more directly controlled by Iran, and receiving approximately \$70 million per year. It is used as a pressure tool — Iran can direct PIJ to launch attacks when Hamas has strategic reasons to maintain some quiet.

The Iraqi Shia militias — groups like Kata'ib Hezbollah, Harakat al-Nujaba, and others operating under the umbrella of the Popular Mobilization Forces — receive approximately \$100 million per year in Iranian support and have carried out over 170 attacks on US military bases in Iraq and Syria between October 2023 and April 2024.

### The strategic logic

The entire proxy network costs Iran approximately \$1.2–1.4 billion per year in direct military support. Against Iran's 2023 oil revenues of approximately \$35 billion, this represents roughly 3.4–4% of oil income. For that relatively small investment, Iran:

- Maintains constant pressure on Israel's four frontiers (north, south, east, naval)
- Keeps Israel's military tied down and defense expenditures high
- Threatens US bases across the region, complicating American military planning
- Can escalate or de-escalate pressure based on diplomatic conditions
- Maintains regional influence and deterrence against regime-change operations

This is geopolitics on the cheap. The proxy strategy is rational precisely because it is so cost-effective relative to its strategic outcomes.

## The April 2024 direct exchange: crossing the threshold

For all the decades of shadow war, one line had never been crossed: Iran had never directly and openly attacked Israeli territory with its own military forces. April 2024 changed that.

### The triggering event

On April 1, 2024, Israeli aircraft struck the Iranian consulate building in Damascus, Syria. The strike killed seven IRGC officers, including two senior generals: Brigadier General Mohammad Reza Zahedi (who commanded IRGC Quds Force operations in Syria and Lebanon) and his deputy Mohammad Hadi Haji Rahimi.

Attacking a diplomatic facility is itself a significant escalation — consulates are technically sovereign territory under international law, and Iran treated the strike as an attack on Iranian soil. Iranian Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei publicly pledged retaliation, and Iran spent 12 days doing something unusual: it announced its retaliation in advance, giving Israel, the United States, and regional partners time to prepare.

### The April 13 attack

On the night of April 13-14, 2024, Iran launched its first-ever direct military assault on Israel from Iranian soil:

- **170 Shahed-136 drones**: slow-moving kamikaze drones (speed ~185 km/h) launched in waves
- **120 Shahab-3 and Emad ballistic missiles**: medium-range ballistic missiles capable of carrying warheads of 600-1,000 kg
- **30 cruise missiles**: terrain-hugging precision weapons

The combined salvo was designed to overwhelm Israeli air defense systems through numbers — drones to deplete interceptors, then faster missiles through the gaps. It did not work. Approximately 99% of the weapons were intercepted by the combined air defense network of Israel (Iron Dome, David's Sling, Arrow-3), the United States (USS Carney and USS Arleigh Burke in the Red Sea, F-15/F-16s from Jordan), the United Kingdom (RAF Typhoons from Cyprus), France, and Jordan.

Only a handful of ballistic missiles reached Israeli territory. Minor damage was done to an air base in the Negev. There were no Israeli military or civilian fatalities from the attack itself (one young Bedouin girl was seriously injured by falling debris).

### The April 19 Israeli response

Israel's cabinet debated its response for six days. The decision was calibrated: on April 19, Israel struck a radar installation near Isfahan — a city in central Iran that also houses major nuclear facilities. The message was deliberate: "we can strike near your nuclear program." The scale was deliberately limited: Israel did not want to trigger a wider war.

Iran's response to the Israeli response was to publicly downplay the strike — a sign of mutual de-escalation. Both sides signaled they had made their points and neither wanted escalation to full war.

### The market reaction

![Brent crude oil price 2020 to 2025 annotated with April 2024 Iran-Israel missile exchange events](/imgs/blogs/iran-israel-shadow-war-drone-strikes-and-the-oil-risk-premium-2.png)

The chart tells the story plainly. Brent was at approximately \$90.8/bbl on April 13, 2024. It spiked to roughly \$92-93 in the immediate aftermath — a move of about \$2-3/bbl, or 2-3%. Over the next two weeks, it gave back that gain. By June 2024, Brent was trading in the mid-\$80s.

Why? Because oil markets are not pricing political symbolism or historical precedent. They are pricing physical supply. Iran's attack was intercepted. Israel's response was limited and targeted. Most critically: not a single barrel of oil stopped flowing as a result of the exchange.

## Why oil markets underpriced the April 2024 attack

#### Worked example:

**The risk premium calculation — what the April 2024 move actually implied**

Before the attack (April 12, 2024): Brent = \$89.0/bbl

Peak after the attack (April 14, 2024): Brent = \$92.0/bbl

Price move = \$3.0/bbl = +3.4%

Now let's work backwards using the expected value framework to see what probability of disruption that move implies.

The key variable is: what would a *full Hormuz closure* do to oil prices? Economists and energy analysts estimate that a complete closure of the Strait of Hormuz — cutting 21 mb/d from global supply — would spike Brent by \$30-40/bbl in the short term (within days to weeks). Let us use \$30 as a conservative estimate.

Before the attack, what was the market's implicit probability of Hormuz closure? Given the long-running shadow war, let us say the market was already pricing a 2% baseline probability of serious Hormuz disruption. That embedded baseline premium = 2% × \$30 = \$0.60/bbl.

The April 13 attack raised that probability temporarily. The actual move was \$3.0/bbl. That additional \$3.0 implied an increase in Hormuz disruption probability of:

\$3.0 / \$30 = 10% additional Hormuz closure probability

So the market briefly priced a ~12% total probability of serious supply disruption (2% baseline + 10% attack-triggered). As de-escalation signals arrived over the following days, that probability fell back toward the baseline. The Brent price followed.

**The insight**: the market was not wrong to move \$3. It was correctly updating the probability of disruption. And it was not wrong to reverse the move — that was the market correctly incorporating the de-escalation signals. The size and speed of the reversal tell you the market never believed there was a more-than-~10-15% chance of actual supply disruption.

### The deterrence premium theory

There is a concept that traders call the "deterrence premium" — the idea that *demonstrated direct escalation with de-escalation* can actually reduce the perceived risk premium, because it proves that both sides have an off-ramp and will use it.

Before April 2024, one of the risks oil markets had to price was the unknown: what happens if Iran and Israel ever directly exchange fire? Does it spiral immediately to full war? Do the Gulf states become battlegrounds? Does Hormuz get threatened?

After April 2024, that unknown is partially resolved: both sides exchanged fire, both sides de-escalated, life continued. The event was *historically significant* but *not supply-relevant*. Markets now have a data point suggesting that even direct Iranian-Israeli military exchanges do not automatically lead to Hormuz closure or sustained supply disruption.

This is why, paradoxically, the April 2024 event may have slightly reduced the long-run oil risk premium compared to what it would have been without the event, even though the event itself caused a short-term spike.

## The Strait of Hormuz: the threat oil markets actually fear

If proxy war and even direct missile exchanges do not move oil sustainably, what does the market actually price? The answer is: physical chokepoint risk. And there is no chokepoint more important to the global oil market than the Strait of Hormuz.

![Strait of Hormuz oil chokepoint 21 mb per day from six Gulf exporters to global market](/imgs/blogs/iran-israel-shadow-war-drone-strikes-and-the-oil-risk-premium-3.png)

### What Hormuz is and why it matters

The Strait of Hormuz is a 39-kilometer-wide (24-mile) channel of water between the Sultanate of Oman to the south and Iran to the north. It connects the Persian Gulf — where Saudi Arabia, Iraq, Iran, Kuwait, the UAE, and Qatar all produce oil — to the Gulf of Oman and then the Arabian Sea, through which all that oil reaches global markets.

The numbers are staggering. Approximately 21 million barrels per day of crude oil and petroleum products transit Hormuz. That is roughly 20% of global oil consumption. On top of that, Hormuz handles roughly 25-30% of global liquefied natural gas (LNG) trade, primarily Qatar's massive LNG exports.

There is no alternative route for most of this oil. Saudi Arabia has a pipeline (Petro Rabigh) that can carry about 5 mb/d overland to the Red Sea coast, bypassing Hormuz. The UAE has an Abu Dhabi-to-Fujairah pipeline with capacity of about 1.5 mb/d. Together, that is perhaps 6-7 mb/d that could bypass Hormuz — still leaving 14-15 mb/d stranded in the Gulf if the strait were closed.

### What Hormuz closure would actually look like

Iran has openly threatened to close the Strait of Hormuz multiple times over the past four decades. Let us think through what this would actually involve militarily:

**Mine-laying**: The fastest way to disrupt Hormuz is to seed the shipping channels with naval mines. Iran has a substantial mine inventory. Clearing mines takes time — days to weeks even with advanced minesweeping equipment — creating an effective disruption while maintaining some deniability.

**Fast-boat harassment**: Iran's Revolutionary Guard Navy (IRGCN) operates hundreds of fast patrol boats and has practiced "swarm" tactics designed to overwhelm the defenses of larger warships. A sustained harassment campaign would raise insurance costs for tankers and deter some shipping.

**Anti-ship missiles**: Iran has extensive anti-ship missile batteries on the Iranian coastline overlooking Hormuz. A volley of Noor or Qader anti-ship missiles against tankers could sink or damage vessels, creating physical wrecks in the shipping lane.

**Combined arms**: The realistic scenario would use all three simultaneously — mines in the channels, fast boats to deter minesweepers, missiles against tankers trying to push through.

### Why Iran has NOT closed Hormuz

Here is the key paradox: Iran has threatened Hormuz closure repeatedly but never executed it. The reason is straightforward and economic: Iran itself exports oil through Hormuz.

Iran's 1.6 mb/d of oil exports — its primary source of government revenue — transit Hormuz. If Iran closes the strait, it cuts its own income. For a government already under severe US sanctions, voluntarily destroying its primary remaining revenue stream would be economically suicidal.

Additionally, Iran's key oil customers are China, India, and other Asian buyers who would suffer directly from a Hormuz closure. Iran has spent enormous political capital cultivating those relationships; destroying them in an oil disruption would cost Iran far more in the long run than any military benefit.

This is why analysts describe the Hormuz threat as "a gun Iran keeps pointing at its own foot." It is real as a threat — the physical infrastructure exists to execute it — but the economic self-harm makes it far less likely than Iranian rhetoric suggests.

The market understands this, which is why Hormuz-threat rhetoric produces short spikes (measured in hours or days) that quickly revert, while actual physical events — ships seized, mines found — produce more sustained moves.

## The Houthi Red Sea disruption: the shock that markets actually priced

While the April 2024 Iran-Israel exchange left oil largely unmoved, another Iran-backed actor was causing a genuine and sustained market disruption: the Houthis in Yemen.

![Red Sea shipping route before Houthi attacks via Suez Canal compared to post-Houthi reroute via Cape of Good Hope](/imgs/blogs/iran-israel-shadow-war-drone-strikes-and-the-oil-risk-premium-7.png)

### The campaign

Following Hamas's October 7, 2023 attack on Israel and Israel's subsequent military response in Gaza, the Houthis declared they would attack any commercial vessel linked to Israel or heading to Israeli ports, as a show of solidarity with Palestinians. In practice, the targeting became indiscriminate — Houthi missiles and drones hit dozens of vessels with no Israeli connection.

By early 2024:
- Over 100 commercial vessels had been attacked or threatened
- Bab el-Mandeb traffic — the southern entry point to the Red Sea — fell by approximately 50%
- Major shipping lines (Maersk, Hapag-Lloyd, CMA CGM, MSC) diverted their vessels around the Cape of Good Hope
- Container shipping rates from Asia to Europe tripled from pre-crisis levels
- War risk insurance premiums for Red Sea transit rose by 10× within weeks

The US and UK launched Operation Prosperity Guardian — airstrikes on Houthi missile and drone storage sites — but failed to meaningfully suppress Houthi capabilities. The disruption persisted throughout 2024.

#### Worked example:

**The Houthi shipping cost calculation — what rerouting actually costs a shipping company**

Consider a typical large container ship operating the Asia-to-Europe route:

**Pre-Houthi route (via Suez Canal):**
- Distance: ~11,000 nautical miles
- Transit time: ~23 days
- Fuel consumption: ~80 metric tons (MT) per day of very-low-sulfur fuel oil (VLSFO)
- Total fuel: 80 MT/day × 23 days = 1,840 MT
- Fuel cost at \$600/MT: 1,840 × \$600 = \$1,104,000
- Suez Canal toll: ~\$700,000
- War risk insurance: ~\$100,000
- Total voyage cost: ~\$1,904,000

**Post-Houthi route (via Cape of Good Hope):**
- Distance: ~14,500 nautical miles (adds 3,500+ nm around South Africa)
- Transit time: ~37 days (+14 days)
- Additional fuel: 80 MT/day × 14 extra days = 1,120 extra MT
- Additional fuel cost: 1,120 × \$600 = **\$672,000**
- No Suez Canal toll: saves ~\$700,000
- War risk insurance now applied to Cape route: ~\$200,000 (lower than Red Sea, but non-zero)
- Additional crew costs and time charter extension: ~\$300,000
- Total additional cost: \$672,000 - \$700,000 + \$200,000 + \$300,000 + \$1,000,000 (new insurance premium) = approximately **\$1,472,000 extra per voyage**

*Note: Some industry analyses, including Drewry, estimated the all-in extra cost including time charter at \$1.5-2M per voyage; we use \$1.67M as a round mid-estimate used by Lloyd's Market Association.*

Now scale that across an industry:

A mid-size shipping company operating 20 vessels each doing 2 voyages per month on the Asia-Europe route:
- Extra cost per month: 20 vessels × 2 voyages × \$1.67M = **\$66.8 million per month**
- Annualized: **\$801 million per year**

That is a real, sustained cost shock that gets passed through to consumer prices for manufactured goods (especially electronics, furniture, automotive parts) shipped from Asia to Europe. This is fundamentally different from the oil market's non-reaction: it is a genuine supply-chain disruption that cannot be priced away by de-escalation rhetoric.

### Why oil moved less than shipping from the Houthi campaign

The Houthi campaign affected *oil* much less than it affected *container shipping*, and the reason is structural:

Oil tankers and LNG carriers taking the Asia-to-Europe route mostly do not go through the Red Sea — they already use the Cape of Good Hope route to load Persian Gulf crude and deliver it to Europe. The Suez Canal is primarily a container ship and smaller tanker route.

More importantly, the Houthi attacks created a *supply chain* shock (slower delivery of manufactured goods) rather than a *commodity supply* shock (less oil in the global market). The total amount of crude oil being produced and consumed globally barely changed; it just cost more and took longer to transport some of it.

This distinction matters enormously for commodity pricing: oil prices are primarily driven by the global supply-demand balance for oil itself, not by the cost of transporting it over one particular route.

## The Iran nuclear escalation tail risk

The single largest tail risk in the Iran-Israel conflict — and the one that would most sustainably move oil markets — is Iran's nuclear program.

### Where Iran stands today

As of mid-2025, Iran has enriched uranium to approximately 84% purity. Weapons-grade uranium requires 90% enrichment. The difference between 84% and 90% is technically small and can be crossed in weeks once the infrastructure and feedstock are in place.

The International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) estimates Iran has accumulated sufficient enriched uranium to potentially produce enough weapons-grade material for several nuclear devices relatively quickly if it decided to do so — the precise timeline depends on technical factors and is estimated at weeks to months for the first device, longer for a deliverable weapon.

Iran has not yet built a weapon, for multiple reasons: fear of Israeli/US preemptive strikes, international sanctions pressure, and some degree of strategic ambiguity (the ability to threaten without the political costs of actually arming).

### The Israeli red line

Israel has consistently stated that a nuclear-armed Iran is an existential threat it will not accept. Multiple Israeli governments have drawn the "red line" at Iran crossing the nuclear threshold. If Iran moves toward weapons-grade enrichment and actual weaponization, the probability of an Israeli military strike rises sharply.

What would an Israeli strike on Iranian nuclear facilities look like? The attack would require Israeli F-35s and potentially F-15Is flying long-range strike missions, likely with aerial refueling over Saudi airspace (which Israeli-Saudi back-channel cooperation makes increasingly plausible), hitting multiple hardened underground facilities. Natanz, Fordow, and Isfahan are the key sites.

### The market implications of a nuclear escalation scenario

#### Worked example:

**The nuclear breakout options trade — positioning for the tail risk**

Suppose you believe there is a meaningful probability (say 15%) that Iran crosses the nuclear threshold within 12 months, triggering an Israeli military response. Here is how a practitioner might position for this tail risk.

**The thesis**: If Israel strikes Iran's nuclear facilities, the response would include Iranian attacks on Gulf oil infrastructure and an attempt to threaten Hormuz. Markets would price a sustained \$15-25/bbl oil risk premium. Let us use \$20 as our estimate.

**Current Brent**: \$70/bbl (as of mid-2025)

**Target Brent on strike scenario**: \$90/bbl

**Instrument**: 12-month Brent call options with \$85 strike (15 USD/bbl out-of-the-money)

**Cost**: Call option premium for \$85 strike 12-month Brent approximately \$2.50-3.50/bbl (based on ~35% implied volatility for 12-month Brent). Use \$3.00/bbl.

**Position size**: 100 contracts × 1,000 bbl/contract = 100,000 bbl exposure. Premium paid: 100,000 × \$3.00 = **\$300,000 total cost**.

**Payoff if scenario occurs (Brent at \$90):**
- Option intrinsic value at \$90: \$90 - \$85 = \$5.00/bbl
- Profit per barrel: \$5.00 - \$3.00 = \$2.00
- Total profit on position: 100,000 × \$2.00 = **\$200,000**

**Payoff if Brent stays at \$70-80 (scenario does not occur):**
- Option expires worthless
- Total loss: \$300,000 (the premium paid)

**Expected value calculation:**
- P(scenario) × Payoff - P(not scenario) × Premium
- 15% × \$200,000 - 85% × \$300,000
- = \$30,000 - \$255,000 = -\$225,000

At 15% probability, this specific trade has negative expected value in isolation — it is a tail hedge, not a directional bet. The *purpose* of the trade is not to maximize expected value; it is to provide *insurance* against a scenario where your other portfolio positions (long equities, short volatility) suffer catastrophic losses. The insurance premium is \$300,000.

The numbers change dramatically if you buy the options when the implied probability is lower (option prices are cheaper) — say when IAEA announces renewed full inspection access, suggesting lower nuclear risk. The same options at a \$2.00 premium with 15% probability of the scenario:

Expected value = 15% × \$300,000 - 85% × \$200,000 = \$45,000 - \$170,000 = -\$125,000. Still negative on expected value, but significantly cheaper insurance.

**The insight**: Tail risk options are not about positive expected value — they are about portfolio construction and the correlation between the tail event and your other positions. If your portfolio is long anything that benefits from global growth and oil stability (equities, credit, emerging markets), the nuclear escalation tail is a hedge, not a speculative trade.

## Iran oil exports: sanctions, recovery, and the China connection

To understand Iran's strategic position — and the market implications of Iranian behavior — you need to understand how Iran's oil exports have fluctuated with the political environment.

![Iran oil export volumes in mb/d from 2011 to 2024 by sanctions regime](/imgs/blogs/iran-israel-shadow-war-drone-strikes-and-the-oil-risk-premium-6.png)

### The sanctions history

**2011-2015 (Obama-era sanctions)**: The EU and US imposed coordinated sanctions on Iranian oil, causing exports to fall from ~2.5 mb/d in 2011 to below 1.1 mb/d in 2013-2015. Iran lost roughly \$60-80 billion in oil revenues over this period — a devastating economic blow.

**2016-2018 (JCPOA recovery)**: The Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action, signed in July 2015 and implemented in January 2016, suspended most sanctions in exchange for Iranian nuclear program constraints. Exports quickly recovered to ~2.5 mb/d.

**2018-2021 (Trump maximum pressure)**: President Trump withdrew from the JCPOA in May 2018 and reimposed — then expanded — US sanctions, including threatening secondary sanctions on any country buying Iranian oil. Exports collapsed from 2.5 mb/d to below 0.5 mb/d by mid-2019. For Iran, this was catastrophic: GDP contracted sharply, inflation exceeded 40%, and the Iranian rial lost 60% of its value.

**2022-2024 (China absorption)**: China — which buys roughly 14 mb/d of oil globally and is Iran's largest customer — began absorbing Iranian oil at steep discounts (reportedly \$10-15/bbl below Brent) despite US sanctions. Chinese independent refineries (teapot refineries) and state-linked entities bought Iranian crude through intermediaries, allowing Iran to partially recover exports to 1.6-1.8 mb/d.

### What this means for geopolitics

The China factor fundamentally changes Iran's strategic calculus. Because China is buying Iranian oil despite sanctions — and because the US is reluctant to directly sanction Chinese entities importing from Iran for fear of broader trade war — Iran's oil revenues have partially recovered.

This gives Tehran more strategic latitude than the pure sanctions picture suggests. Iran is not the desperate, economically isolated regime of 2019. It has revenue, and that revenue funds the proxy network we described at the start of this post.

For traders, the Iran oil export volume is a signal about Iran's strategic confidence and its willingness to take risks. When Iran's exports are high (as in 2024), Tehran has more revenue and more flexibility. When exports are low (as in 2019), Tehran is under pressure and may take more extreme action — but also may be more willing to negotiate.

## The escalation ladder: a scenario framework

To tie together everything we have covered, here is a structured way to think about different Iran-Israel conflict scenarios and their market implications.

![Iran Israel escalation scenario matrix showing oil shipping gold and Israeli equities impact across five scenarios](/imgs/blogs/iran-israel-shadow-war-drone-strikes-and-the-oil-risk-premium-5.png)

### Scenario 1: Status quo shadow war (base case, ~60% probability)

The conflict continues as it has since 2020: Israeli strikes on Iranian-linked targets in Syria, Hezbollah rocket fire from Lebanon, occasional Houthi attacks, IAEA inspections continuing with some friction. Oil markets embed a small (~\$1-2/bbl) shadow war premium that barely registers in price action. Gold benefits modestly as a safe haven. Israeli equities carry a 2-4% discount versus their fundamentals.

### Scenario 2: Hormuz harassment escalation (~15% probability)

Iran uses its IRGCN fast boats and mines to harass tanker traffic without fully closing the strait. Insurance rates spike 5-10×, some tankers divert or wait, and Brent rises \$5-10/bbl in the short term. The US repositions naval assets, Iran tests escalation limits, markets stay elevated for 2-4 weeks before a diplomatic resolution or US deterrence reassertion brings prices back.

### Scenario 3: Houthi escalation or resumed Red Sea disruption (~20% probability)

The Houthi campaign against commercial shipping resumes at full intensity, combined with expanded targeting including oil tankers from Gulf states. This scenario is the most likely "activated" scenario and produces sustained shipping cost increases but only moderate oil price moves (\$2-5/bbl) unless Iranian oil exports are directly targeted.

### Scenario 4: Iranian nuclear breakthrough (~8% probability)

Iran enriches to weapons grade and announces or demonstrates a nuclear device. This triggers an Israeli preemptive strike within 6-18 months, an Iranian counter-response targeting Gulf oil infrastructure, and a sustained \$10-20/bbl oil premium. This is the tail scenario that drives the options trade we described.

### Scenario 5: Direct comprehensive war (~3-5% probability)

Full-scale war between Israel and Iran involving Israeli strikes on Iranian territory, Iranian full-scale missile campaign against Israel, and attempts to close Hormuz. This is the scenario where Brent could spike \$20-40/bbl in days, gold rallies 15%+, and Israeli equities fall 30%+. The most severe scenario but also the least likely given the mutual deterrence mechanisms both sides have developed.

## The escalation timeline: five years that led to April 2024

![Iran-Israel escalation timeline 2019 to 2024 from Abqaiq attack through April 2024 direct exchange](/imgs/blogs/iran-israel-shadow-war-drone-strikes-and-the-oil-risk-premium-4.png)

The April 2024 direct exchange did not emerge from nowhere. It was the culmination of five years of escalating provocations that had gradually pushed both sides closer to direct conflict:

**September 2019 (Abqaiq)**: Drones and cruise missiles — attributed to Iran but launched through intermediaries — struck Saudi Arabia's Abqaiq oil processing facility, the largest crude stabilization facility in the world. The attack temporarily knocked out 5.7 mb/d of Saudi processing capacity (about 5% of global supply). Oil spiked 14-15% in 24 hours, then partially reversed. This was the largest single supply disruption since the Gulf War.

**January 2020 (Soleimani)**: US President Trump ordered a drone strike at Baghdad airport that killed IRGC Quds Force commander Qasem Soleimani, Iran's most important military strategist and the architect of the proxy network. Iran's military response — ballistic missiles against US bases in Iraq — caused no American fatalities (through warning or Iranian intentional misaiming). Oil briefly spiked then reversed as markets priced in Iran's careful calibration.

**2021-2022 (Tanker war)**: Iran seized or harassed multiple commercial vessels in the Gulf of Oman and Persian Gulf, including seizing the South Korean-flagged MT Hankuk Chemi. Insurance rates rose, tanker operators started avoiding Iranian waters. This was below the threshold that would move oil sustainably but demonstrated Iran's capability and willingness.

**October 2023 (Gaza)**: Hamas's October 7 attack on Israel dramatically raised the stakes. Israel's military response in Gaza, combined with Hezbollah rocket fire from Lebanon and the Houthi Red Sea campaign, stretched Israeli military capacity and raised the risk of miscalculation.

**April 1, 2024 (Damascus)**: Israel's strike on the Iranian consulate in Damascus, killing two IRGC generals, crossed a line Tehran said it could not leave unanswered. This set the stage for the April 13 direct exchange.

## The proxy war cost accounting: why this continues indefinitely

#### Worked example:

**Iran's proxy war economics — why this is geopolitics at bargain prices**

Let us run the numbers on what Iran's proxy strategy actually costs and generates:

**Annual proxy network costs (direct military support):**
- Hezbollah (Lebanon): \$700M/year
- Hamas military wing: \$300M/year
- Houthis (Yemen): \$200M/year
- PIJ + Iraqi militias: \$170M/year
- **Total: ~\$1,370M/year (\$1.37 billion)**

**Iran's annual oil revenues (2023):** ~\$35 billion (at 1.8 mb/d × \$53/bbl average realized price × 365 days)

**Proxy cost as share of oil revenues:** \$1.37B / \$35B = **3.9% of oil revenues**

**What Iran gets for that 3.9%:**
- Hezbollah: 150,000+ rockets pointed at Israel, credible deterrent against Israeli ground invasion of Lebanon, political influence in Lebanese government
- Hamas: control of Gaza, ability to activate conflict on Israel's southern border on strategic timetable
- Houthis: disruption capability against Saudi Arabia, UAE, Red Sea shipping, and indirect pressure on Israel's economy
- PIJ: direct Iranian-controlled strike capability in Palestinian territories
- Iraqi militias: sustained pressure on US forces, early warning network, influence in Baghdad

**Comparison**: The US spends approximately \$50 billion/year on military aid to Israel and \$750 billion/year total on defense. Iran achieves comparable strategic pressure on the US and Israel with \$1.37 billion/year through proxy networks — roughly 0.18% of what the US spends.

This is why the proxy strategy persists: it is extraordinarily cost-effective. Iran cannot match Israeli or American military technology directly. But it can fund, arm, and direct non-state actors that impose real costs on its adversaries at minimal direct financial cost.

**The investment return on Iran's proxy spending:**

If Iran's proxy spending forces Israel to maintain 30,000 active military personnel on its northern border perpetually (to deter Hezbollah), and Israeli military personnel costs average \$50,000/year, that is \$1.5 billion/year in defensive costs imposed on Israel. Iran spends \$700M on Hezbollah and imposes \$1.5B in direct defense costs on Israel — a 2.1:1 cost imposition ratio, before accounting for economic disruption, civilian security costs, infrastructure hardening, etc.

**The insight**: Iran's proxy strategy is a rational investment in asymmetric pressure, not ideological recklessness. It persists because the strategic return on investment is strongly positive — and will persist as long as Iran's oil revenues allow the funding.

What changes the calculation is a sustained collapse in Iranian oil revenues — either through tighter sanctions enforcement (blocking China's purchases) or a structural fall in global oil prices below Iran's fiscal breakeven (estimated at ~\$80-100/bbl). At \$60/bbl Brent with 1.6 mb/d of exports, Iran's oil revenues fall to roughly \$21 billion per year. At that level, funding the proxy network at \$1.37B starts to represent a much more painful 6.5% of revenues, and domestic budget pressures intensify. This is why oil prices, sanctions enforcement, and Iran's domestic economic situation are all leading indicators for Iran's proxy aggression level — not just Iran-specific news.

## Common misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Iran-Israel conflict always spikes oil."**

Not true. The April 2024 direct exchange is the most dramatic disproof: two regional powers exchanged fire for the first time in history, and Brent moved \$3 and then reversed. Oil moves when supply is disrupted — it does not move on headlines, military exchanges, or even deaths. The September 2019 Abqaiq attack was actually the rare case where a conflict-linked event did spike oil meaningfully (+14%) because it removed 5.7 mb/d from processing capacity temporarily.

**Misconception 2: "Iran will close Hormuz if pushed."**

Possible but unlikely, for the reason we explained: Iran exports its own oil through Hormuz. A closure would hurt Iran more than anyone in the short term. The Hormuz threat is a deterrent tool, not a practical weapon. Iran has exercised *pressure* tactics — seizing tankers, mining waters, fast-boat harassment — but has never attempted full closure. This does not mean it is impossible in a maximum escalation scenario, but the cost-benefit is deeply unfavorable to Tehran.

**Misconception 3: "Sanctions do not work on Iran."**

Partially wrong. The 2012-2015 sanctions did work: Iranian oil exports fell from 2.5 mb/d to below 1.1 mb/d, GDP contracted significantly, and Iran eventually came to the JCPOA negotiating table. What partially undermines sanctions today is China's willingness to buy Iranian oil in defiance of US secondary sanctions. The sanctions *reduced* Iranian oil revenues substantially even if they did not eliminate them. Without China's absorption role, Iran's exports would be far lower.

**Misconception 4: "The Houthis are just a regional nuisance."**

The Houthi Red Sea campaign proved otherwise. A non-state armed group using relatively cheap drones and anti-ship missiles disrupted trade worth hundreds of billions of dollars annually, diverted the global shipping fleet around Africa, tripled container rates, and created inflationary pressure in Europe. The Houthis demonstrated that the era of cheap maritime shipping through the Red Sea-Suez corridor can be disrupted by an Iran-funded militia at relatively low cost to Iran.

**Misconception 5: "An Israeli strike on Iranian nuclear facilities would be clean and surgical."**

Unlikely. Iran's nuclear facilities are hardened, dispersed across multiple sites, and include underground installations at Fordow that may be partially beyond conventional strike capabilities. An Israeli strike would require multiple follow-on strikes and would almost certainly trigger a significant Iranian military response targeting Gulf oil infrastructure, Israeli civilian areas (via Hezbollah), and potentially US assets in the region. "Surgical" underestimates the probability of sustained escalation.

## How it shows up in real markets

### September 2019: Abqaiq — the model of a real supply shock

The September 14, 2019 attack on Saudi Arabia's Abqaiq facility is the gold standard of geopolitical oil disruption because it was a genuine supply shock, not just a threat. Production was disrupted for about two weeks. Brent spiked from \$60 to \$71 (+18%) within 24 hours — the largest single-day percentage move since the Gulf War. It then gradually reversed as Saudi production was restored.

The lesson: oil moves on *actual* supply disruption, not on blame or geopolitical symbolism. Analysts immediately started calculating how long production would be offline, what the inventory drawdown would be, whether alternative supply could fill the gap. When Saudi Arabia signaled rapid restoration, prices reversed.

### January 2020: Soleimani killing — headline spike, quick reversal

The killing of IRGC General Soleimani produced an immediate oil spike of about 4% (from \$68 to \$71) as markets feared Iranian retaliation might target oil infrastructure. When Iran's response — ballistic missiles against US bases in Iraq with no fatalities — proved limited, oil reversed to pre-event levels within three days. The market correctly assessed: no supply disruption, no sustained premium.

### 2023-2024: Houthi campaign — the shipping shock that lasted

Unlike the Soleimani killing and the April 2024 direct exchange, the Houthi Red Sea campaign produced a sustained market impact — but primarily in shipping, not oil. Container shipping rates from Asia to Europe tripled from pre-crisis levels of ~\$1,500/40ft container to above \$6,000 at peak. Container shipping companies (Maersk, Hapag-Lloyd) reported significantly higher revenue in H1 2024 compared to the prior year. Marine insurance premiums for Red Sea transit remained elevated throughout the crisis.

Oil markets were more muted: Brent moved only modestly (\$2-4/bbl) as a Houthi premium, because most of the oil directly affected by the Red Sea route was already priced with other route options.

### 2024-2025: The "new normal" — elevated but range-bound

By mid-2025, the Iran-Israel conflict has settled into an elevated but range-bound equilibrium: the shadow war continues (Israeli strikes on Iranian-linked targets in Syria, Hezbollah frontier tensions, Houthi harassment operations), but there has been no repeat of the April 2024 direct exchange. Oil markets have returned to pricing based primarily on OPEC+ production policy, US shale output, and demand from China and India — the geopolitical risk premium has compressed back toward baseline (~\$1-3/bbl above where fundamentals would put it).

## How to trade it: the Iran-Israel playbook

The central lesson of this post is that Iran-Israel conflict creates *headline risk* but rarely creates *supply risk* unless specific chokepoints are physically disrupted. Here is how a practitioner translates this into a trading framework:

### The signals that actually matter

**Signal 1: AIS (Automatic Identification System) data for Hormuz tanker traffic**

Commercial satellite companies and Bloomberg track Automatic Identification System transponder data for every commercial vessel in the Strait of Hormuz. If tanker traffic through Hormuz actually slows — vessels anchoring, diverting, or going dark — that is a real signal of supply disruption. This is the single most important data point for oil risk premium. It costs tens of thousands per month to subscribe to real-time AIS data, but it is the ground truth beneath the headlines.

**Signal 2: Houthi attack frequency and targeting**

The Houthi attack rate in the Red Sea is publicly tracked by naval agencies and shipping intelligence firms. When the attack frequency rises and the targeting expands to oil tankers (rather than primarily container ships), the supply disruption risk rises. When attacks slow, the premium erodes.

**Signal 3: Iran oil export volumes (tanker tracking)**

Bloomberg, Kpler, and Vortexa track Iranian oil exports via satellite-based tanker tracking. A sudden drop in Iranian oil exports could signal either a new sanctions push or Iran voluntarily reducing exports as a negotiating tactic. Either way, it affects global supply.

**Signal 4: IAEA inspection access**

When the IAEA announces restricted access to Iranian nuclear facilities, the probability of nuclear escalation rises. When Iran restores inspection access, it falls. IAEA reports are public and provide real-time insight into the nuclear escalation risk.

### The specific trades

**The Hormuz premium trade (tactical, short-duration):**

When Iranian rhetoric spikes around Hormuz — a military exercise announced, a tanker seized, a threatening speech from the Supreme Leader — buy near-term (1-3 month) Brent call options. The implied volatility on Brent options typically rises with rhetoric; options become more expensive. Fade the position quickly if no physical disruption materializes within 1-2 weeks. The half-life of a Hormuz rhetoric spike is typically days to weeks.

**The Houthi shipping trade (structural, medium-duration):**

When Houthi attack frequency rises and Red Sea traffic falls, shipping stocks (Maersk, Hapag-Lloyd, Zim Integrated) and container rates tend to outperform. This is a more sustained trade than the oil trade because the rerouting disruption plays out over weeks and months, not days.

**The nuclear tail hedge (strategic, long-duration):**

For portfolios with substantial Middle East exposure (Gulf sovereign wealth fund investments, Israeli technology exposure, emerging market equity), maintaining a small position in long-dated Brent call options (\$85-100 strike, 6-18 month duration) provides insurance against the nuclear escalation tail. The cost is the option premium; the benefit is portfolio protection against the worst-case scenario.

**The oil risk premium fade (contrarian, after spikes):**

Historically, oil spikes caused by Iran-Israel events — the Abqaiq attack being the key exception — tend to reverse within 1-3 weeks if no physical supply disruption materializes. A contrarian strategy of *fading* oil spikes triggered by Iran-Israel headlines (shorting Brent or selling call options) has historically performed well, at the cost of being badly wrong on the rare event where actual disruption occurs.

### Risk management: what invalidates these trades

Every trading framework needs clear invalidation criteria — the signals that tell you your thesis is wrong and you need to exit.

**Invalidates the Hormuz premium fade:** If you are short Brent oil after a Hormuz rhetoric spike and any of the following materialize, exit immediately: (a) a commercial tanker is actually struck and sinks in Hormuz waters — this is a physical deterrent event; (b) IRGCN fast boats conduct live-fire exercises in the shipping lanes rather than the usual harassment; (c) AIS data shows a sudden 20%+ drop in tanker transits over 48 hours, suggesting operators are pulling ships proactively. These signals mean the market's fears are materializing physically.

**Invalidates the Houthi shipping trade:** Exit Maersk/Hapag long positions if the Houthis announce a ceasefire or significantly extend the scope of their exclusions (for example, exempting Asian-flagged vessels), which has happened episodically. The ceasefire risk is real — Yemen peace negotiations have resumed multiple times — and a genuine ceasefire could cut shipping premiums overnight.

**Invalidates the nuclear tail hedge:** Close the Brent call options and book losses if (a) Iran agrees to a new nuclear verification framework with snap inspections, (b) US-Iran diplomatic back-channel talks are confirmed with meaningful concessions, or (c) Iran's enrichment is verifiably rolled back below 60%. These signals reduce the probability of nuclear breakout meaningfully enough that the insurance premium is no longer worth paying.

### Position sizing discipline

The single biggest mistake in geopolitical trading is over-sizing. Because geopolitical events feel dramatic and the potential moves feel large, traders often risk too much capital. The correct approach:

For a geopolitical tail hedge like the Brent calls, limit the premium cost to **no more than 1-2% of portfolio net asset value**. At that size, even a total loss on the options (the most common outcome, since most tail events do not materialize) does not materially harm the portfolio. But when the event does occur and Brent spikes \$20/bbl, the options return 5-10× on the position — which at 1-2% of NAV translates to a 5-20% portfolio contribution. That is meaningful insurance, not reckless speculation.
