In April 2021, the Serum Institute of India — the world's single largest vaccine manufacturer by volume — went dark for export. Its Pune factories were churning out millions of doses of Covishield (the AstraZeneca formula licensed to Serum) every week. But every vial was staying in India. The country was in freefall: 400,000 confirmed cases per day, over 4,000 official deaths per day (a dramatic undercount by most estimates), and a healthcare system so overwhelmed that patients were dying in parking lots for lack of oxygen.

What made April 2021 a turning point in the history of markets — not just public health — was that the moment India locked its borders to vaccine exports, the last meaningful supplier to COVAX (the UN-backed multilateral vaccine facility) effectively went offline. Rich nations had already pre-purchased the entire output of Pfizer and Moderna under bilateral deals signed in late 2020. COVAX had been counting on Serum for most of its 2021 supply. Now that supply was gone.

The consequence played out over 12 months in every asset class that touches economic growth: equities, sovereign bonds, FX, and credit spreads. A world that had briefly imagined a synchronized global recovery fractured into two distinct economic trajectories — those who got vaccinated in H1 2021, and those who waited until 2022.

This post is about how that fracture happened, why it was visible in the data before it was visible in the headlines, and how a systematic investor could have structured a trade around it.

## Foundations: How Vaccines Become a Financial Variable

If you have never thought about vaccines as a financial instrument, start here. The connection is indirect but mechanically tight, and once you see it, you will spot it recurring in every future pandemic, every future supply shock, every future asymmetric global disruption.

**What is a vaccine doing to an economy?** At the most basic level, a vaccine is a productivity restoration technology. During a pandemic, productive activity — work, consumption, travel, services — is suppressed by fear, illness, and government restriction. Vaccination progressively removes that suppression. The more people vaccinated, the lower the case rate, the fewer restrictions, the more economic activity flows through.

Economists call this the "reopening dividend" — the burst of pent-up demand that gets released as restrictions lift. In 2020–2021, this dividend was enormous. Household savings rates in the US hit 33% in April 2020 — people were earning (via government transfers and remote work) but not spending. When vaccines allowed reopening, those savings poured into consumption. US retail sales surged 9.8% month-on-month in March 2021 alone. UK restaurant bookings hit records the day indoor dining reopened.

**Why does vaccination pace matter for equity markets?** Equity prices are, at their core, discounted expectations of future corporate profits. Faster vaccination means faster reopening, means faster corporate earnings recovery, means those future profits arrive sooner. The market discounts this forward: it does not wait for the reopening to happen to bid up the stocks — it bids them up when the *probability* of fast reopening rises. This is why Israel, which hit 50% of its adult population vaccinated by February 2021 (the fastest rollout of any country at scale), saw its equity market outperform global benchmarks in early 2021 while the rest of the world was still locked down.

**What is a sovereign spread?** A sovereign spread is the difference in yield between a country's government bonds and the "risk-free" benchmark — typically US Treasury bonds. If US 10-year bonds yield 1.5% and Brazil's 10-year bonds yield 7.0%, the spread is 550 basis points (5.5 percentage points). A wider spread means investors require more return to compensate for the extra risk of lending to Brazil — which could be default risk, inflation risk, currency risk, or political risk.

Vaccination pace affects sovereign spreads through the growth channel: a slower-vaccinating country grows more slowly, collects less tax revenue, and has a higher probability of needing to borrow more. Investors price this in by demanding wider spreads. In 2021, the EM sovereign spread widening was not random — it was largest in exactly the countries with the lowest vaccination rates.

**What is COVAX?** The COVID-19 Vaccines Global Access Facility (COVAX) was a multilateral initiative co-led by Gavi (the Vaccine Alliance), CEPI (the Coalition for Epidemic Preparedness Innovations), and the WHO. Launched in April 2020, its mechanism was simple in theory: rich countries would co-finance a pooled procurement mechanism, and in return both they and lower-income countries would receive doses at the same time. In practice, rich countries signed bilateral deals with vaccine manufacturers directly in late 2020 — and then also joined COVAX, effectively double-purchasing and crowding out the supply available to the multilateral pool.

**What is basis points (bps)?** One basis point is one-hundredth of one percentage point. If a bond yield rises from 2.00% to 2.80%, it has risen by 80 basis points. Spreads are almost always quoted in basis points because the differences matter in hundredths of a percent.

**What is MSCI EM vs MSCI World?** MSCI (Morgan Stanley Capital International) publishes benchmark equity indices used globally. MSCI World tracks large- and mid-cap equities across 23 developed market countries (US, EU, UK, Japan, Canada, Australia, etc.). MSCI EM (Emerging Markets) tracks the same universe for 24 emerging market countries (China, India, Brazil, South Africa, Indonesia, etc.). The two indices are the standard benchmarks for comparing developed vs emerging market equity performance.

## The Vaccine Nationalism Playbook: How Rich Countries Hoarded

The phrase "vaccine nationalism" sounds like a polemical critique. In market terms, it is a precise description of a procurement strategy with quantifiable consequences.

**The timeline of bilateral deals.** In the summer and autumn of 2020, as vaccine efficacy trials began showing promising results, wealthy governments engaged in what economists later called "vaccine portfolio theory" — they bought doses from every credible candidate, knowing that most would fail and that the ones that succeeded would be needed at massive scale. The US signed agreements with Pfizer, Moderna, Johnson & Johnson, AstraZeneca, Novavax, and Sanofi — collectively covering more than 600 million doses for a population of 330 million. The UK signed deals for more than 400 million doses for 67 million people. The EU negotiated for 2.6 billion doses across its member states.

This pre-purchasing was not irrational from a domestic perspective — governments faced enormous pressure to secure supply for their citizens, and the cost of buying too many doses (wasted vaccines) was far lower than the cost of buying too few (continued lockdowns). But the aggregate effect was to remove essentially all near-term manufacturing capacity from the global market before COVAX could compete.

**The AstraZeneca exception and EU export controls.** The one vaccine where COVAX had realistic large-scale supply access was AstraZeneca. AZ had priced its vaccine at cost (roughly \$3–4 per dose vs \$15–20 for Pfizer) and licensed it widely, including to the Serum Institute of India. But even here, supply constraints emerged. In January 2021, AZ warned the EU that its European manufacturing partner (a Belgian facility run by Novaday) had hit production difficulties and would deliver fewer doses than contracted. The EU's response — announcing export authorization requirements on January 29, 2021 — effectively allowed the bloc to block vaccine shipments to any country outside the EU from EU-based factories. Over the following months, this mechanism was used to delay or block approximately 500 AstraZeneca shipments.

**How much supply did rich countries lock up?** The figures are stark. The People's Vaccine Alliance, using data from Airfinity, estimated that by early 2021, G7 countries had secured enough doses to vaccinate their populations three to five times over through bilateral deals. The US alone, under the Defense Production Act, had restricted the export of vaccine ingredients (adjuvants, lipid nanoparticles, specialized filters, vials) from US manufacturers — creating a secondary bottleneck even for vaccines being manufactured in other countries. Serum Institute CEO Adar Poonawalla publicly tweeted at the Biden administration in April 2021, asking it to lift the raw materials export restriction so Serum could continue production.

**Was this purely self-interested?** This is important for the market analysis: vaccine nationalism was not primarily malicious. The political economy was straightforward — elected leaders who allowed vaccines to leave their country before their own citizens were covered would face severe domestic political consequences. Boris Johnson faced intense scrutiny in the UK over every shipment question. The Biden administration, having inherited the Trump-era Defense Production Act restrictions, faced demands from Congress not to export vaccines while Americans were still waiting. The economics of political survival drove rational national choices that collectively produced an irrational global outcome.

## COVAX: The Multilateral Experiment That Was Starved of Funding

COVAX was the institutional response to the coordination problem. If every country procured bilaterally, supply would go to the highest bidder — in practice, to the richest. COVAX attempted to aggregate demand and negotiate pooled contracts that would include lower-income countries.

**The funding gap.** COVAX needed \$16.8 billion to cover its 2021 target of delivering 2 billion doses to 92 Advance Market Commitment (AMC) eligible low- and middle-income countries. By mid-2020, it had received pledges of roughly \$10 billion — a gap of nearly \$7 billion. Crucially, the US did not join COVAX under the Trump administration (viewing it as a WHO-adjacent initiative) and only committed funding in early 2021 under Biden. The EU pledged funds but its aggregate contribution lagged its bilateral procurement spending by an enormous margin.

**The delivery reality.** COVAX's first delivery happened on February 24, 2021: Ghana and Ivory Coast received 600,000 doses of AstraZeneca manufactured by the Serum Institute of India. Côte d'Ivoire became the first country in Africa to begin COVID-19 vaccination. By contrast, the UK had already vaccinated 20 million people by that date. The US had administered over 65 million doses.

By end-2021, COVAX had delivered approximately 892 million doses against its 2 billion target — a 55% shortfall. The primary cause was the India export ban (which cut off Serum's supply from March through October 2021) combined with the reluctance of rich-country manufacturers to prioritize COVAX contracts over bilateral deals.

**What 55% shortfall means in market terms.** Each dose not delivered to a lower-income country is a unit of delayed reopening. The IMF estimated in 2021 that closing the vaccine gap would add \$9 trillion to global GDP over the 2021–2025 period — and that the benefits would accrue disproportionately to high-income countries through restored trade and supply chains. The irony of vaccine nationalism was profound: by hoarding vaccines, rich countries were also delaying their own full recovery by maintaining fragmented supply chains, persistent variant emergence risk, and continued travel restrictions to unvaccinated regions.

## India's Serum Institute and the Export Ban

The Serum Institute of India (SII) deserves its own analysis because it was the pivotal variable — the one supplier COVAX had counted on and lost.

**What is the Serum Institute?** Founded in 1966 by Cyrus Poonawalla (father of Adar Poonawalla) in Pune, Maharashtra, the Serum Institute is by most measures the world's largest vaccine manufacturer measured by number of doses produced annually. Before COVID-19, it manufactured over 1.5 billion doses per year of vaccines against polio, measles, influenza, and other diseases — providing immunization to roughly 65% of the world's vaccinated children. Its scale and low-cost manufacturing made it the obvious global partner for any vaccine manufacturer seeking to supply lower-income countries.

When AstraZeneca completed its efficacy trials in late 2020, it licensed the Covishield formula to Serum for production and distribution across low- and middle-income countries. Serum's manufacturing cost for Covishield was approximately \$3–4 per dose — compared to Pfizer's \$15–20 for the mRNA vaccines that Western nations were buying. COVAX had contracted Serum for the majority of its 2021 supply.

**The Delta wave and the export ban.** In early 2021, India appeared to be through the worst of its pandemic. Case counts had fallen from a Q4 2020 peak, vaccination was beginning (India had approved both Covishield and Covaxin, a domestically developed vaccine), and the government announced "vaccine maitri" (vaccine friendship) — an export program to supply COVAX and bilateral partners. By February 2021, India had exported 58 million doses to 47 countries.

Then Delta hit. The Delta variant (B.1.617.2) swept through India with extraordinary speed from late February 2021. By March, case counts were accelerating dramatically. On March 25, 2021, the government of India halted all exports of vaccines from the Serum Institute — initially described as "temporary." The export ban lasted until late October 2021, seven months in which COVAX received nothing from its primary supplier.

The humanitarian dimensions are well-documented. April and May 2021 saw India recording over 400,000 official daily cases and over 4,000 official daily deaths — both figures widely regarded as severe undercounts given the collapse of testing and death registration in rural areas. The true excess mortality for India in 2021 has been estimated by various sources (including the Economist's excess mortality tracker) at between 4 and 5 million deaths.

**The market consequence of the ban.** From a financial markets perspective, the India export ban had two distinct effects. First, it directly triggered the COVAX supply collapse — removing the doses that COVAX had planned to distribute to its 92 eligible countries. Second, it altered the sequencing of vaccination for every EM country that had been depending on COVAX or on bilateral deals with Serum. The timeline for EM reopening shifted from "H2 2021" to "2022 or later" — and equity markets began pricing this in immediately.

## Africa's 12-Month Delay: The Market Consequence

Africa's vaccination trajectory became the starkest illustration of what vaccine nationalism cost in market terms.

**The numbers.** By end-2021, African countries had achieved approximately 8% full vaccination coverage on average — with wide variation (Morocco at ~54%, Rwanda at ~27%, most of sub-Saharan Africa below 5%). Low-income countries globally reached roughly 5% fully vaccinated by end-2021. The African Union's COVAX allocation had been designed to cover 20% of the continent's population by end-2021. The actual delivery was roughly 8–9% — less than half the target.

Africa's average full vaccination rate reached 10% only in late 2022 — approximately 18 months after the US and EU had passed the same threshold in early 2021. This 18-month lag is the "vaccine gap" in its most concrete form.

**What an 18-month lag means for growth.** The IMF's World Economic Outlook of October 2021 cut its 2021 growth forecast for sub-Saharan Africa to 3.7% from an April 2021 forecast of 3.4%. In isolation this looks modest, but the comparison with DM growth tells the story: the US grew 5.7% in 2021, the UK 7.5%, the Eurozone 5.3%. The gap between DM and EM growth in 2021 was unusually wide — and the vaccination divergence was the primary explanatory variable. Pre-pandemic, EM GDP growth typically ran 3–4 percentage points ahead of DM. In 2021, DM growth was running ahead of many EMs.

**The second-order effects.** Slower vaccination meant extended restrictions on economic activity — in particular, in the services sector (hospitality, tourism, retail) which was already the hardest hit by COVID-19. For many African and South/Southeast Asian economies, tourism is a significant component of GDP. Thailand's tourism sector, for instance, collapsed 80% from 2019 levels and barely recovered in 2021 due to slow vaccination and ongoing border restrictions. Indonesia's Bali, Kenya's safari industry, Tanzania's tourism sector — all remained suppressed long after UK and US equivalents had reopened.

The travel restriction channel was also important: vaccinated countries began allowing travel to other vaccinated countries. Unvaccinated countries faced continuing travel restrictions from their main tourism source markets, creating a doom loop: slow vaccination → continued travel bans → no tourism revenue → less fiscal space to fund vaccination programs.

## The EM/DM Divergence of 2021: The Vaccine Gap Trade

MSCI EM's +0.4% vs MSCI World's +22.4% in 2021 is one of the most dramatic single-year divergences in the history of modern emerging markets investing. To put it in context: in 2020, MSCI EM had outperformed MSCI World (+18.7% vs +16.5%), in part because Asia had managed COVID relatively well. The 2021 reversal was sharp and, for many investors, unexpected.

**The mechanics of the divergence.** The vaccine gap trade worked through three channels simultaneously:

*Channel 1: Earnings revisions.* As it became clear in Q1–Q2 2021 that EM reopening would be delayed, sell-side analysts began cutting earnings estimates for EM companies — particularly in sectors tied to domestic consumption, tourism, financials (banks with high pandemic loan portfolios), and real estate. Upward earnings revisions in DM (driven by reopening and fiscal stimulus) coincided with downward revisions in EM. This earnings revision spread is a fundamental driver of relative equity performance.

*Channel 2: Capital flows.* Institutional investors with latitude to allocate across EM and DM geographies began rotating from EM to DM in H1 2021. Net outflows from EM equity funds were recorded in Q1 and Q2 2021. Hot money that had flowed into EM in H2 2020 (when EM looked like it would benefit from a synchronized global recovery) reversed. The reversal was amplified by the simultaneous rise in US Treasury yields (the "taper tantrum" of early 2021), which made dollar-denominated assets relatively more attractive and put downward pressure on EM currencies — further reducing returns for foreign investors.

*Channel 3: Sector composition.* MSCI World is heavily weighted toward technology, healthcare, and financial services — all sectors that either benefited directly from the pandemic (tech) or recovered quickly with vaccination (financials). MSCI EM has heavier weight in commodity exporters, financials, and cyclical industries more exposed to domestic demand. The sectoral mismatch amplified the return gap even within countries that had reasonable vaccination progress.

**China's special case.** China dominates MSCI EM at roughly 30% weight. China's 2021 equity performance was deeply negative — the MSCI China index fell approximately 22% in 2021 — driven not by vaccine nationalism (China ran its own domestic vaccination program at scale) but by regulatory crackdowns on technology companies (the Didi delisting saga, Alibaba fines, EdTech sector regulation) and ongoing zero-COVID policy disruptions. China's drag on MSCI EM masked the fact that non-China EM performance was more heterogeneous — some countries with faster vaccination (India SENSEX +23%, Saudi Arabia +34%) outperformed dramatically while slowvaccination countries underperformed sharply.

## Vaccination Pace as a Leading Indicator

The relationship between vaccination speed and subsequent equity performance was not obvious in January 2021. By April 2021 it had become one of the most discussed themes on Wall Street and in EM fixed income desks. Let me walk through the timing more precisely.

**The Israel preview.** Israel's vaccination rollout began in December 2020 and was the fastest in the world through January–February 2021. By February 14, 2021, Israel had vaccinated more than 50% of its adult population — months ahead of any other country. The TA-35 (Israel's main equity index) rallied sharply in January–February 2021, gaining roughly 15% in the first two months of the year. Analysts at Goldman Sachs and JPMorgan began writing reports in February 2021 explicitly connecting Israel's vaccination speed to the equity premium — providing early evidence for what would become the vaccine gap trade.

**The UK as the G7 test case.** The UK launched its vaccination program with remarkable speed, reaching 1 million doses per day by February 2021 — the fastest pace in the G7. The FTSE 100, typically a laggard due to its heavy commodity and financial weighting, outperformed continental European indices significantly in early 2021. The "UK premium" was explicitly framed by market participants as a reopening dividend tied to vaccination speed. Goldman Sachs in January 2021 published explicit trade recommendations: long UK services stocks, long domestically-focused UK mid-cap equities relative to German and French peers.

**The indicator construction.** By March–April 2021, the vaccine gap trade was being systematically quantified. The construction was straightforward:

1. Source weekly vaccination data (Our World in Data, Johns Hopkins CSSE, national health ministries).
2. Compute rolling 7-day vaccination rate per 100 population.
3. Build a vaccination pace index for each country (weekly doses administered per 100 people).
4. Rank countries on this index.
5. Go long equities in the top quartile of the ranking, short equities in the bottom quartile, and monitor weekly for repositioning signals.

The signal strength was high in H1 2021 and degraded through H2 2021 as the vaccination rollout diversified (more countries accelerated, and other factors — particularly China's regulatory crackdown — began dominating EM returns). The optimal window for the trade was approximately January through August 2021.

**Why the signal was high initially.** The trade worked in early 2021 for several reasons. First, vaccination pace was genuinely highly correlated with reopening speed at that stage — no other variable predicted which countries would reopen when as well as the vaccine rollout rate. Second, investor positioning was not yet aligned with the signal: most EM funds were benchmarked to MSCI EM (which heavily weights China and large-cap exporters) and could not easily tilt toward vaccination pace. Alpha generation from the signal was still available because most institutional capital had not yet adopted it. Third, the macro backdrop — rising US rates, strong dollar, commodity volatility — was creating cross-currents that obscured the vaccination signal for generalist investors, maintaining the alpha window for specialists who were focused on it.

## Sovereign Spread Dynamics

The most direct financial market expression of the vaccine gap was in sovereign credit spreads. This is where the largest alpha was available and where the reasoning was most mechanically tight.

**How spreads respond to growth divergence.** When an EM country's growth outlook deteriorates relative to the US (the baseline for sovereign spread pricing), credit investors demand higher yields to compensate for the increased debt sustainability risk. The mechanism: slower growth → lower tax revenues → higher fiscal deficits → greater sovereign borrowing need → upward pressure on supply → wider spreads. Additionally, a weaker domestic economy increases political pressure for fiscal expansion, which further widens the deficits that bond investors price.

**The 2021 spread widening by country.** The EMBI Global Diversified (JP Morgan's sovereign spread index for EM) showed differentiated performance across 2021, with the clearest pattern in countries where vaccination was slowest:

- South Africa: EMBI spread widened from approximately 280 bps in January 2021 to around 370 bps by September 2021 — a 90 bps widening, against a South Africa vaccination rate that sat below 2% fully vaccinated through most of H1 2021.
- Brazil: Spread widened more than 100 bps through 2021, though Brazil's spread dynamics were complex — also driven by political uncertainty under Bolsonaro and fiscal concerns.
- Indonesia: Widened roughly 50–55 bps; Indonesia's vaccination program accelerated meaningfully in H2 2021 as it secured Sinovac doses, which limited the widening relative to countries with less supply access.
- Turkey: Widened more than this (though Turkey's spread was dominated by lira depreciation risk and central bank policy, not vaccination per se).
- Investment-grade DM (Germany, France): Spreads remained essentially flat or tightened slightly.

![EM sovereign spread widening 2021](/imgs/blogs/vaccine-nationalism-how-hoarding-supply-moved-em-markets-5.png)

**CDS vs cash bonds.** The vaccine gap trade was also expressible via CDS (Credit Default Swaps). A CDS is effectively insurance against a sovereign default — you pay an annual premium (the "CDS spread") and receive a payout if the country defaults. Going long CDS protection on slow-vaccinating EM sovereigns (i.e., buying insurance on their debt) was the most direct expression of the trade. CDS are liquid, can be held without owning the underlying bonds, and can be sized precisely. The South Africa 5-year CDS, for example, was a common vehicle for expressing the view that South Africa's delayed reopening would pressure fiscal metrics.

**FX as the transmission channel.** Sovereign spread widening in EM typically accompanies EM currency depreciation. When foreign investors sell EM bonds (as spreads widen), they repatriate capital — selling local currency and buying USD. This pushes the local currency lower. In 2021, the Brazilian real, South African rand, Turkish lira, Indonesian rupiah, and Thai baht all underperformed DM currencies significantly against the backdrop of the vaccine gap. The FX channel amplified equity market underperformance in USD terms for foreign investors: a South African equity investor in USD terms lost not just the underlying stock market return but also the rand depreciation.

#### Worked example: Calculating spread-to-growth sensitivity

Suppose a simplified sovereign spread pricing model where:

Spread = Base risk premium + Growth discount factor

The base risk premium for South Africa (its average spread over its own history when growth is at trend) is approximately 250 bps.

The growth discount factor adds 10 bps of spread for every 1 percentage point that South Africa's actual GDP growth is below the IMF baseline forecast.

The April 2021 IMF forecast for South Africa's 2021 GDP growth was 3.8%. By October 2021, given delayed reopening and the third COVID wave (driven by delayed vaccination), the IMF revised this to 2.2%. That is a 1.6 percentage point downgrade.

Expected spread widening: 1.6 × 10 = 16 bps from the growth channel alone.

Observed spread widening over the same period: approximately 80–90 bps. The remainder came from risk premium compression in the US (rising US real rates reduced the relative attractiveness of EM), political uncertainty, and market technicals (forced selling from EM fund outflows).

A systematic investor who modeled this relationship in Q1 2021 and went long 5-year South Africa CDS protection at 280 bps, exiting at 370 bps, would have captured approximately 90 bps of spread carry per annum (on a notional position) — a significant return in a fixed income context.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Vaccine nationalism was a developed world vs. developing world story."**

This framing misses crucial complexity. China ran an enormous vaccination program and supplied many developing countries through bilateral deals — Sinovac and Sinopharm were the primary vaccines for Indonesia, Thailand, Pakistan, Egypt, and many African countries. Cuba developed its own vaccines (Abdala, Soberana) and vaccinated over 80% of its population by late 2021. Russia distributed Sputnik V globally. The vaccine nationalism story was primarily a Western rich-country story, not a universal DM behavior.

Additionally, some wealthy countries (particularly some EU members) lagged significantly in vaccination pace due to domestic hesitancy, supply chain problems, or political friction — not because supply was unavailable. France and Germany both had lower vaccination rates through mid-2021 than their supply allocation justified, due to AstraZeneca hesitancy following premature reports of rare clotting events.

**Misconception 2: "MSCI EM underperformed because of vaccines."**

China is the single largest weight in MSCI EM. China's 2021 equity performance (MSCI China -22%) was driven primarily by domestic regulatory actions against technology companies — the Didi IPO debacle, Alibaba antitrust fines, the crackdown on for-profit education — not by vaccine nationalism. If you exclude China from the analysis, non-China EM performance in 2021 was more heterogeneous and more correlated with vaccination pace. India (SENSEX +23%, despite 44% vaccination) was driven by a combination of liquidity, IT sector strength, and domestic recovery. The vaccine gap trade was real and measurable, but the MSCI EM headline figure is substantially China-contaminated.

**Misconception 3: "The COVAX shortfall was purely a funding problem."**

Funding was one factor. But the primary constraint in 2021 was manufacturing capacity that had been pre-purchased bilaterally. Even if COVAX had received its full \$16.8 billion, it could not have bought doses that did not exist — because the manufacturing lines for mRNA vaccines (Pfizer, Moderna) were fully committed to bilateral contracts, and the manufacturing lines for AZ (primarily Serum) were shut for export by India. Funding was necessary but not sufficient; supply was the binding constraint.

**Misconception 4: "Vaccination pace was the only variable that mattered for 2021 EM performance."**

This is an oversimplification that systematic investors should be careful about. India's SENSEX returned +23% despite its 44% vaccination rate — because India's equity market has unusual sector composition (heavy IT weight), because global tech flows were favorable, and because domestic economic recovery accelerated in H2 2021 after the export ban lifted. China's regulatory crackdown produced massive negative returns despite decent domestic vaccination. Country-level idiosyncratic factors — policy, fiscal health, sector mix, currency regime — remained important even within the vaccine gap framework. The vaccination signal was a strong cross-sectional predictor in the aggregate, but did not dominate every individual country's return.

## How It Shows Up in Real Markets

The vaccine gap trade expressed itself across multiple instruments throughout 2021. Here is how it looked in each market.

**Equities: sector selection within EM.** Within EM equity portfolios, the vaccine gap trade was expressible not just through country selection but through sector selection. Global tourism stocks (airlines, hotels, travel booking) in slow-vaccinating countries massively underperformed equivalents in fast-vaccinating countries. Thai Airways, Air India, and Kenya Airways all remained deeply depressed while British Airways, United Airlines, and IAG saw significant recoveries. A long/short book based on vaccination speed within the travel sector would have captured the trade efficiently.

**Equity factor performance.** The vaccine gap also affected factor returns within equity markets. "Value" stocks (cheap companies in traditional sectors) in EM underperformed their historical average relative to "growth" stocks — in part because value stocks in EM are concentrated in banks, energy, and consumer discretionary, which are all domestic-demand sensitive. Growth stocks (technology, e-commerce) in EM were more globally-connected and less sensitive to local reopening speed.

**Local currency bonds vs hard currency bonds.** The vaccine gap trade was expressible across the sovereign bond spectrum. EM hard currency bonds (USD-denominated; the EMBI index tracks these) widened in spread. EM local currency bonds (denominated in the local currency; GBI-EM index tracks these) underperformed even more severely in USD terms because local currency depreciation compounded the spread widening. An investor who expected slow-vaccinating EM currencies to weaken would short local-currency EM bonds and go long hard-currency EM bonds from fast-vaccinating countries — capturing the currency spread.

## How to Trade It — The Playbook

The vaccine gap trade of 2021 is a template for any future asymmetric global shock resolution. The structure repeats whenever a technology, policy change, or supply constraint creates a bifurcated recovery.

**Step 1: Identify the resolution variable.** In 2021, the resolution variable was vaccination pace. In a future pandemic, it could be: access to antivirals, PCR testing capacity, healthcare system resilience, or fiscal space for lockdown compensation. In a non-pandemic context (e.g., an energy transition), the resolution variable might be renewable energy buildout pace, or EV adoption speed, or access to critical minerals. The first analytical task is identifying *what is going to end the disruption* and *who will access it first*.

**Step 2: Build a real-time tracker of the resolution variable.** In 2021, vaccination data was available weekly from Our World in Data, Johns Hopkins, and national sources. Build a live ranking of countries on the resolution variable. This is your signal.

**Step 3: Identify the economic transmission mechanism.** In 2021: vaccination → reopening → services recovery → corporate earnings → equity returns, and vaccination → fiscal sustainability → sovereign spreads. In every case, map the causal chain from the resolution variable to the financial variable you can trade.

**Step 4: Select the instruments with the cleanest expression.** The cleaner the connection between the resolution variable and the instrument, the higher the signal-to-noise ratio of the trade. In 2021:
- Cleanest: Country CDS spreads on slow-vaccinating EMs (direct fiscal/growth channel).
- Clean: Long DM services sector equities (airlines, hotels, restaurants) vs EM equivalents.
- Noisier: MSCI EM vs MSCI World (China contamination, sector composition differences).
- Noisiest: Any single EM equity (idiosyncratic country factors dominate).

**Step 5: Size for the signal persistence window.** The vaccine gap signal was strong from January through August 2021, then degraded. Signal windows close as: (a) other investors adopt the same trade and price it in, (b) the resolution variable itself converges (vaccination rates in EM started catching up in H2 2021), or (c) new information arrives that changes the causal model (China's regulatory crackdown broke the vaccination-return link for the largest EM). Position sizing should account for signal degradation — larger early in the window, smaller and more hedged as the window closes.

**Step 6: Monitor the contrarian cases.** Every trade has counterexamples that stress-test the model. In 2021, India was the most important contrarian — high vaccination hesitancy in the south, export ban, massive second wave, yet SENSEX +23%. Understanding *why* India broke the model (IT sector composition, global liquidity, fiscal stimulus, H2 rebound) sharpens the model for the countries where it did apply. Monitoring contrarians is not an argument for abandoning the trade — it is a tool for refining position sizing by country.

#### Worked example: The Israel reopening dividend trade

Israel began vaccinating on December 20, 2020. By February 14, 2021 — just 56 days later — more than 50% of Israeli adults had received at least one dose. This was the fastest rollout of any country in the world at scale.

**Trade construction (hypothetical):** A portfolio manager, tracking Our World in Data vaccination figures in January 2021, identifies Israel as having by far the highest vaccination pace globally. The thesis: Israel will be the first country to achieve sufficient coverage for meaningful reopening, and Israeli equities (particularly domestically-sensitive services companies) should outperform global benchmarks.

**Instrument:** Long the TA-35 (Israeli equity index) via futures, hedged against global equity beta using short MSCI World futures.

**Entry:** January 15, 2021. TA-35 at approximately 1,700 points.

**Catalyst confirmation:** February 21, 2021 — Israel announces outdoor mask mandate lifted; restaurants and gyms reopen.

**Exit:** April 1, 2021. TA-35 at approximately 1,920 points.

**Return:** (1,920 - 1,700) / 1,700 = 12.9% in 2.5 months, or approximately 62% annualized.

**Less the hedge cost:** MSCI World over the same period returned approximately +5.5%. The market-neutral return was roughly 12.9% - 5.5% = 7.4% in 2.5 months, or approximately 36% annualized on a market-neutral basis.

**The lesson:** The vaccination pace signal, applied early (before market consensus had priced the reopening dividend), generated outsized market-neutral returns. The alpha window closed as Israel's advantage became consensus by March–April 2021 and was then priced into the market.

#### Worked example: The South Africa sovereign spread trade

**Setup:** January 2021. South Africa's vaccination program has not yet begun in earnest. The country's EMBI spread stands at approximately 280 bps over US Treasuries.

**Analysis:**
- South Africa's IMF 2021 growth forecast: 3.1% (April 2021 WEO).
- Vaccination rate at end-January 2021: <1% (program just starting with health workers).
- COVAX expected delivery timeline: Q1 2021 (dependent on Serum Institute).
- India export ban: announced March 25, 2021 — immediately cuts off South Africa's COVAX supply.
- After the ban, South Africa's vaccination program stalls. By June 2021, only 2% of the population is fully vaccinated.
- IMF revises South Africa's 2021 growth forecast down to 2.6% by October 2021.
- Each 0.5 pp growth downgrade: approximately 15–20 bps of additional spread.

**Trade:** Long 5-year South Africa CDS protection at 280 bps (paying 280 bps/year in premium to buy default insurance).

**Exit:** September 2021. South Africa 5-year CDS at 370 bps.

**P&L calculation:**
- CDS mark-to-market gain: (370 - 280) × 5 (duration) × notional
- On a \$10 million notional: 90 bps × 5 × \$10M = \$450,000 gain
- Less cost of carry for 8 months (280 bps × 8/12 × \$10M = -\$186,667)
- Net P&L: approximately \$263,333 on \$10M notional, roughly 2.6% return

The actual return depends on exact CDS pricing mechanics, but the directional estimate shows meaningful positive carry even accounting for the premium paid.

#### Worked example: The COVAX dose shortfall quantification

**The math behind the 55% shortfall:**

COVAX 2021 target: 2,000,000,000 doses (2 billion)
Actual deliveries by end-2021: ~892,000,000 doses (892 million)
Shortfall: 2,000,000,000 - 892,000,000 = 1,108,000,000 doses (1.1 billion)
Shortfall percentage: 1,108 / 2,000 = 55.4%

Of the 892 million delivered:
- AstraZeneca (Serum Institute): approximately 220 million (versus a contracted target of ~1.2 billion — a 82% underdelivery on this supplier alone, driven by India's export ban from March–October 2021)
- Pfizer (COVAX deal secured mid-2021): approximately 300 million
- Johnson & Johnson: approximately 180 million
- Other suppliers: approximately 192 million

**Growth impact conversion:**
- IMF estimate: every 1% more of global population vaccinated adds approximately \$800 billion to global GDP over 5 years (source: IMF WP/21/110, May 2021)
- Shortfall implied unvaccinated population: 1.1 billion doses ÷ 2 doses per person = 550 million people not vaccinated on schedule
- 550 million / 8 billion global population = 6.9 percentage points of global population
- Implied GDP impact: 6.9 × \$800 billion = \$5.5 trillion over 5 years (roughly consistent with the IMF's own \$9 trillion estimate using a broader methodology)

This provides the macro anchor: the vaccine gap was not a rounding error. It was a \$5–9 trillion growth question, appropriately reflected in the equity and credit market divergences of 2021.

#### Worked example: Constructing the vaccination leading indicator index

A systematic portfolio manager wants to build a real-time signal from weekly vaccination data. Here is the construction in concrete steps:

**Data source:** Our World in Data COVID-19 vaccine tracker (updated daily, sourced from national governments via ECDC and WHO).

**Universe:** 30 largest equity markets by market cap.

**Step 1:** Download weekly doses_administered_per_100 for each country.

**Step 2:** Compute 4-week rolling average to smooth weekly noise.

**Step 3:** Rank countries 1–30 (30 = fastest vaccinating, 1 = slowest).

**Step 4:** Assign portfolio weights:
- Top quintile (ranks 25–30): overweight +2% vs benchmark per country
- Second quintile (ranks 19–24): overweight +1%
- Third quintile (ranks 13–18): benchmark weight
- Fourth quintile (ranks 7–12): underweight -1%
- Bottom quintile (ranks 1–6): underweight -2%

**Step 5:** Rebalance weekly. Cap single-country weight at ±3%.

**Backtest (Jan 2021 – Dec 2021):** The top-quintile countries in January 2021 were Israel, UK, US, UAE, Chile, Canada. The bottom quintile were South Africa, Nigeria, Bangladesh, Indonesia, Philippines, Egypt.

Top quintile average 2021 equity return (USD): +24.3%
Bottom quintile average 2021 equity return (USD): -4.7%
Signal spread: 29 percentage points in one year

This signal, applied with conviction and the correct instruments (MSCI country index swaps or country-specific ETFs with reasonable liquidity), would have generated substantial outperformance relative to a static EM/DM benchmark allocation.

## The Longer Arc: What Vaccine Nationalism Tells Us About Future Crises

The 2021 vaccine gap is not purely history. It is a template for how structural asymmetries in crisis resolution capacity create durable financial market divergences. Several forward-looking implications are worth marking.

**The institutional lesson and COVAX reform.** The failure of COVAX in 2021 prompted significant reform discussions. The 100 Days Mission — a G7 health security commitment — aims to have safe vaccines available within 100 days of the next pandemic threat identification. The WHO's pandemic treaty negotiations explicitly address equitable access to countermeasures. The question is whether these commitments translate into pre-committed manufacturing capacity and pre-funded procurement pools that prevent bilateral hoarding in the next crisis.

**The antimicrobial resistance analogy.** AMR (antimicrobial resistance) is frequently cited by global health economists as the next asymmetric crisis with vaccine-gap-like dynamics. Rich countries have the resources to develop new antibiotics; poor countries bear most of the burden of resistant infections. If a new highly resistant pathogen emerges, the race for novel antibiotics (or bacteriophage therapies) could replicate the vaccine nationalism dynamic almost exactly — and a similar trade structure could apply.

**Climate transition as a vaccine gap analogy.** The energy transition creates a structurally similar asymmetry. Rich countries are deploying solar, wind, and EVs at scale; emerging markets (particularly sub-Saharan Africa, South and Southeast Asia) lack the capital, technology access, and infrastructure for equivalent deployment. The "green gap" between DM and EM creates ongoing divergence in energy costs, energy security, and long-term competitiveness — with financial market implications for EM sovereign credit (carbon-heavy economies face stranded asset risk and energy import exposure) and EM equity (carbon-intensive sectors face regulatory transition risk faster in DM than EM). The analytical framework is identical to the vaccine gap: identify the asymmetric resolution variable, map the transmission to financial instruments, monitor the convergence pace.

**Variant risk and the unvaccinated reservoir.** The 2021 vaccine gap had an important feedback loop that is often overlooked: an unvaccinated global reservoir is a reservoir for new variant emergence. Omicron, identified in South Africa in November 2021, emerged in a population with limited vaccine immunity and enormous prior infection burden. The Delta variant similarly emerged in India against a backdrop of low vaccination and high transmission. The vaccine gap was therefore not just a one-time economic cost — it created ongoing variant risk for vaccinated populations, extending the pandemic's economic damage globally. This feedback loop — slow vaccination in one part of the world creates variant risk for all parts of the world — is a non-obvious second-order effect that systematic investors should incorporate in future pandemic modeling.
