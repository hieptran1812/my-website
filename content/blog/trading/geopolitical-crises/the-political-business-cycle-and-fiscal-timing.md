On October 19, 2022, Liz Truss resigned as UK Prime Minister after just 45 days — the shortest-serving PM in British history. The proximate cause was not a vote of no confidence, not a scandal, not a foreign crisis. It was the bond market. Six weeks earlier, on September 23, her Chancellor Kwasi Kwarteng had unveiled a "mini-budget" containing \$45 billion GBP in unfunded tax cuts. Within 48 hours, the UK 10-year Gilt yield had spiked from 3.5% to 4.5%. Within a week it hit 4.99% — a 150bp move. The pound fell to 1.035 against the dollar, the weakest level in modern history. Pension funds using Liability-Driven Investment strategies faced margin calls that threatened a doom loop; the Bank of England was forced to launch an emergency \$65 billion GBP bond-buying program to prevent a fire sale cascade. The IMF issued a rare public rebuke of a G7 government's fiscal policy.

This was not an accident. It was the Nordhaus political business cycle, in reverse, in one catastrophic week.

Understanding this cycle — and its limits — is essential for any macro trader or long-term investor. The political business cycle is perhaps the only true seasonality in asset markets: it is structural, repeating, and rooted in the incentives of democratic systems. Governments that understand it use it. Markets that understand it profit from it. And when governments violate it — as Truss did — the punishment is swift and severe.

## Foundations: The Nordhaus Model and Why Politicians Can't Help Themselves

The political business cycle was formally described by economist William Nordhaus in a landmark 1975 paper in the *Review of Economic Studies*, though the intuition had been recognized by politicians and economists for decades before. Nordhaus's insight was deceptively simple: if voters judge incumbents primarily by the state of the economy at election time, and if incumbents control fiscal levers, rational incumbents will manipulate those levers to create favorable conditions precisely at the moment voters go to the polls.

The model works through a simple mechanism. Imagine you are an elected leader with a four-year term. In years one and two, you have political capital to spend on painful reforms or fiscal consolidation — the election is far away and voters have short memories. In year three, you begin to loosen fiscal policy: more spending, targeted tax cuts, perhaps subsidies to politically important constituencies. The goal is to have the economy in its maximum growth phase by the time year four arrives and election day approaches. After the election — regardless of who wins — fiscal tightening resumes to address the deficit created by the stimulus.

This creates a predictable cycle that economists measure through what they call the "fiscal impulse" — the year-over-year change in the cyclically adjusted government budget balance. A positive fiscal impulse means the government is actively pumping money into the economy (expansionary). A negative impulse means tightening. The pattern is remarkably consistent across democracies with clear electoral timetables.

The Nordhaus model makes three testable predictions: (1) government spending and deficits should be systematically higher in pre-election years, (2) economic growth should be systematically higher in election years relative to the long-run average, and (3) post-election years should show higher inflation, higher interest rates, and fiscal consolidation. All three predictions hold, to varying degrees, in the data.

**The mechanism is rational, not conspiratorial.** Voters consistently punish incumbents for high unemployment and reward them for low unemployment and strong growth. The American Political Science Association's retrospective voting research shows that every 1 percentage point reduction in unemployment in the final year of a presidential term is worth approximately 3-4 percentage points in vote share. With this payoff function, it would be irrational for a president *not* to stimulate the economy before an election.

**Markets understand this.** The Stock Trader's Almanac, maintained by Jeffrey and Yale Hirsch since 1967, has documented the presidential cycle's impact on equity returns for over five decades. The pattern is striking in its consistency.

![S&P 500 average returns by presidential cycle year from 1950 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/the-political-business-cycle-and-fiscal-timing-2.png)

The numbers from the Almanac data, covering 1950-2024:
- **Year 1 (post-election):** average S&P 500 return of approximately +8.1%. This is the year of political capital spending on reform, fiscal tightening, and sometimes rate hikes to address inflation created by the previous cycle.
- **Year 2 (midterm year):** approximately +11.2% average. Midterm elections create their own dynamic — the president's party typically loses seats, creating gridlock, which paradoxically tends to be positive for markets (less policy uncertainty).
- **Year 3 (pre-pre-election):** approximately +15.3% — the strongest year. This is when pre-election fiscal loosening begins in earnest, economic growth is accelerating, and animal spirits build in anticipation of the coming election campaign spending.
- **Year 4 (election year):** approximately +12.4%. Still above average, but the uncertainty of the election result creates some volatility, particularly in H1.

The 7-percentage-point gap between Year 3 and Year 1 is not statistical noise. Over 18 complete presidential cycles since 1950, Year 3 has been positive 94% of the time (17 of 18), while Year 1 has been negative in several cycles (notably 2001, 2009, and 2022). This asymmetry creates a genuine tactical trading opportunity.

## Foundations: The Fiscal Channel — How Stimulus Reaches Asset Prices

Before trading the cycle, understand precisely how government fiscal decisions translate into equity and bond prices. There are five distinct channels:

**Channel 1: Aggregate Demand (direct).** Government spending directly adds to GDP. A \$1 of government spending generates more than \$1 of GDP through the fiscal multiplier effect — the initial spending recipient earns income, spends some of it, and the next recipient does the same. Estimates of the multiplier range from 0.8 to 1.5 depending on the state of the economy (higher in recessions, lower near full employment). More GDP = higher corporate revenues = higher earnings = higher stock prices.

**Channel 2: Expectations (forward-looking).** Bond and equity markets are forward-looking. If markets anticipate a fiscal expansion, they begin pricing it in before the spending actually hits the economy. This is why Year 3 of the presidential cycle often sees equity outperformance even before the stimulus actually flows.

**Channel 3: Deficit and borrowing (bond market).** Fiscal expansion requires financing. The Treasury must issue more bonds to fund the deficit. More bond supply means lower prices and higher yields — this is the primary reason why pre-election fiscal expansions tend to hurt long-duration bonds. The "term premium" — the extra yield demanded by investors to hold long-dated bonds — rises as deficits widen.

**Channel 4: Inflation.** Fiscal stimulus that hits a near-full-employment economy generates inflation rather than real growth. This further pressures bonds (higher nominal yields required) and eventually forces central banks to raise rates, which is the mechanism that kills the cycle.

**Channel 5: FX.** Higher deficits, higher inflation, and the prospect of tighter monetary policy create complex FX dynamics. In the short run, higher rates tend to attract capital and strengthen the currency. In the longer run, unsustainable deficits erode confidence in the fiscal trajectory and weaken the currency — the Truss mini-budget was a pure Channel 5 event.

## The Political Moves: Pre-Election Stimulus in Action

### Biden's \$1.9 Trillion Moment (2021)

On March 11, 2021, President Biden signed the American Rescue Plan (ARP) into law. The \$1.9 trillion package included \$1,400 direct stimulus checks to most Americans, \$350 billion in state and local government aid, extended unemployment benefits, and \$122 billion for K-12 schools. This was one of the largest peacetime fiscal interventions in US history, coming on top of the \$2.2 trillion CARES Act (March 2020) and the \$900 billion December 2020 package — total fiscal support of approximately \$5 trillion, or about 25% of GDP, over 18 months.

The market impact was immediate and powerful. The S&P 500 continued its recovery from COVID lows, ultimately gaining 26.9% in 2021. But the bond market began to revolt: the 10-year Treasury yield rose from 0.93% at start of 2021 to 1.74% by late March — a 81bp spike driven precisely by markets pricing in the inflationary consequences of massive fiscal stimulus into a recovering economy.

In August 2022, the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) added another \$369 billion in climate and energy investments, \$64 billion to extend Affordable Care Act subsidies, and provisions allowing Medicare to negotiate drug prices. The political calculation was explicit: with midterms approaching in November 2022, the administration was demonstrating legislative achievement.

### UK's Truss Disaster: When Fiscal Limits Bite

Not all pre-election fiscal moves succeed. The UK Conservative Party's experience in September 2022 represents the most instructive case study of fiscal limits in a developed economy since the 1976 UK IMF bailout.

Liz Truss was elected Conservative leader on September 5, 2022, promising radical supply-side reform. Her Chancellor, Kwasi Kwarteng, unveiled the "Growth Plan" (universally called the "mini-budget") on September 23. The package included abolition of the 45p top income tax rate, reversal of the planned corporation tax rise from 19% to 25%, cuts to stamp duty, and extension of the energy price cap — total unfunded cost of approximately \$45 billion GBP per year.

The critical flaw: this was entirely unfunded. No spending cuts, no tax increases elsewhere, and — crucially — no Office for Budget Responsibility (OBR) scoring or fiscal forecasts. The OBR scoring, standard practice for UK budget statements, would have provided an independent assessment of the deficit impact. Its absence signaled to markets that the government was deliberately bypassing independent scrutiny.

The market reaction was ferocious. Within 24 hours, Gilt yields had spiked 50bp. Within 48 hours, they were up 100bp. Within a week, the 10-year Gilt had moved 150bp and the pound had fallen to historic lows.

![GBP/USD 2020-2024 showing the Truss mini-budget flash crash in September 2022 context](/imgs/blogs/the-political-business-cycle-and-fiscal-timing-6.png)

The deeper problem was structural: the UK's Liability-Driven Investment (LDI) pension fund strategy had created a hidden systemic risk. Defined benefit pension funds had been using interest rate swaps and leveraged Gilt positions to "immunize" their portfolios — matching long-dated liability cash flows with long-dated assets. When Gilt yields spiked, these positions generated margin calls. Funds sold Gilts to meet margin calls, which drove yields higher, which generated more margin calls — a textbook doom loop. The Bank of England's emergency intervention halted the cascade. Governor Andrew Bailey gave funds "until October 14" to sort out positions — a moment of remarkable monetary theatre.

The Truss mini-budget violated the most basic rule of the political business cycle: **fiscal credibility is the floor that must never be breached.** Stimulus works when markets believe the expansion is temporary and the underlying fiscal trajectory is sustainable. When they don't, the term premium blowout can offset the entire benefit of the stimulus.

## The Financial Channels: Measuring the Fiscal Impact

### The US Deficit Trajectory

The structural US fiscal position shows the Nordhaus pattern clearly when you adjust for the economic cycle.

![US federal deficit as percent GDP by presidential cycle year and fiscal impulse pattern](/imgs/blogs/the-political-business-cycle-and-fiscal-timing-4.png)

The Congressional Budget Office tracks the "structural" or "cyclically adjusted" budget balance — what the deficit would be at full employment, stripping out the automatic stabilizers (higher unemployment benefits in recessions, lower tax revenues). This is the cleanest measure of deliberate fiscal policy stance.

The data from 1980-2024 shows:
- **Average structural deficit in Year 4 (election year):** -4.1% of GDP
- **Average structural deficit in Year 1 (post-election):** -3.3% of GDP
- **Difference:** approximately -0.8pp, meaning election years run about \$180 billion more in structural deficit (at current GDP levels)

This gap has widened in recent cycles as political dysfunction has made fiscal consolidation harder. The 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act, for example, was passed in Year 1 of the Trump presidency — a notable exception to the pattern, driven by ideological commitment rather than electoral timing. But its \$1.5 trillion in 10-year costs created the fiscal conditions that necessitated the COVID stimulus, which itself fed into the 2020-2022 inflationary surge.

### The Bond Market's Reaction Function

When governments spend more, bond markets move in predictable ways. The relationship between deficit-to-GDP and 10-year Treasury yields, while noisy in the short run, is positive over multi-year periods. A 1 percentage point increase in the deficit-to-GDP ratio is associated with approximately 20-30bp of additional yield over a 2-3 year horizon (academic estimates range from 15bp to 50bp depending on methodology).

For long-duration bonds (20-30 year Treasuries), the sensitivity is much higher. The duration effect amplifies yield moves: a 50bp yield rise on a 30-year Treasury with duration of 20 years produces approximately a 10% price loss. This is why "short Treasuries" is the natural trade in pre-election fiscal expansions — but timing matters critically.

## Worked Examples and Historical Deep Dives

#### Worked example:

**The Biden Stimulus Yield Impact (March-April 2021)**

On March 10, 2021, the 10-year Treasury yield was 1.55%. The ARP was signed the next day.

Step 1 — Calculate the fiscal impulse: The CBO scored the ARP at \$1.9 trillion over 10 years, approximately \$380 billion per year at peak, or about 1.8% of 2021 GDP. On top of \$900 billion passed in December 2020 (about \$230 billion annualized stimulus), the total fiscal impulse was approximately 2.8% of GDP in 2021 — the largest single-year expansion since World War II outside of COVID's 2020 stimulus.

Step 2 — Estimate yield impact using the academic multiplier: 2.8pp fiscal impulse × 25bp per pp = 70bp expected yield rise.

Step 3 — Actual outcome: By March 31, 2021, the 10-year yield was 1.74%. By April 30, it was 1.63%. By end-Q1 2022, it was 2.34%. The total rise from the ARP signing to end of 2021 was approximately 80bp — consistent with the model's 70bp prediction.

Step 4 — Trade outcomes: A trader who bought the ProShares UltraShort 20+ Year Treasury ETF (TBT) on ARP signing day saw the fund rise approximately 35% over the next 12 months. Conversely, the iShares 20+ Year Treasury Bond ETF (TLT) fell approximately 17% over the same period.

**Net trade P&L on \$1,000,000 notional short TLT position:** +\$170,000 gross before costs and financing.

#### Worked example:

**The Truss 45-Day Loss Calculation**

A trader who recognized that Kwarteng's mini-budget was structurally unfunded and opened short positions in GBP/USD and long positions in Gilt yields on September 23, 2022:

*Short GBP/USD:*
- Entry: 1.1200 on September 23
- Exit: 1.0350 on September 26 (GBP low) 
- P&L on \$1,000,000 GBP position: (1.1200 - 1.0350) × \$1,000,000 = \$85,000 profit in 3 trading days

*Long UK Gilt Yields (via futures short or inverse ETF equivalent):*
- 10-year Gilt yield moved from 3.47% on Sept 22 to 4.57% on Sept 27 = 110bp move
- On a Gilt futures position with DV01 of \$10,000 per bp, this is: 110 × \$10,000 = \$1,100,000 profit on one futures contract
- A position sized at 10% of \$10M book: notional exposure worth 10 × \$10,000 DV01 per contract → \$110,000 gain

*Combined trade P&L over 5 trading days: approximately +\$195,000 on \$10M book = +1.95% in one week.*

The key signal that made this trade high-conviction: the absence of OBR scoring. Without independent fiscal validation, the market had to price in maximum uncertainty.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Pre-election fiscal expansion always boosts equities."**
This is only true when fiscal credibility is maintained. The Truss mini-budget is the clearest counterexample in recent history: unfunded stimulus that breaks market confidence in fiscal sustainability drives bond yields so high that the discount rate effect crushes equity valuations. Italy in 2018 (spread widening from 130bp to 330bp over Germany after Lega-M5S budget proposals) and Argentina repeatedly demonstrate this failure mode.

**Misconception 2: "Year 3 is always a buy."**
Year 3 averages +15.3%, but is not guaranteed. 2011 (Year 3 of Obama's first term) saw the S&P 500 return just +2.1% amid the European sovereign debt crisis, the US debt ceiling crisis, and S&P's US downgrade. Structural global shocks can overwhelm the domestic political cycle. In 2011, the GPR Index spiked to 148 — elevated geopolitical risk partially offset the domestic fiscal tailwind.

**Misconception 3: "The cycle is purely American."**
The Nordhaus effect appears in UK, German, French, Japanese, and most established parliamentary democracies, though its timing and magnitude differ based on electoral systems. Parliamentary systems with snap elections make the cycle less predictable. But the underlying incentive — boost growth before votes — is universal in democratic systems.

**Misconception 4: "Central banks offset political business cycles."**
The US Federal Reserve has statutory independence, but in practice, the Fed has been reluctant to tighten aggressively in the 12 months before a presidential election. The 1972 Burns Fed (Arthur Burns, Nixon ally) was the most egregious case — the Fed kept rates too low, enabling Nixon's landslide re-election, which fed into the 1973-74 inflationary shock. Modern Fed independence is stronger, but the 2021-22 cycle — where the Fed delayed tightening until March 2022 despite clear inflation signals — is consistent with some degree of political sensitivity.

**Misconception 5: "Fiscal expansion always widens deficits."**
Sometimes fiscal expansion is partly self-financing. The Biden CHIPS Act (2022) and IRA were designed with deficit offsets (Medicare drug pricing, minimum corporate tax) that the CBO scored as partially deficit-reducing. But markets focus on the net flow of Treasury issuance, not the CBO scoring — and in 2022, gross issuance was still rising, which kept upward pressure on yields.

## How the Presidential Cycle Shows Up in Real Markets

### Sector Rotation Through the Cycle

The presidential cycle doesn't just affect market-wide returns — it drives sector rotation as fiscal priorities shift.

**Year 1 (post-election, austerity phase):**
- Outperform: Healthcare (defensive), Consumer Staples (defensive), Utilities (defensive, but rate-sensitive caveat)
- Underperform: Consumer Discretionary (discretionary spending contracts with rising unemployment), Small Caps (higher borrowing costs bite harder)

**Year 2 (midterm year, gridlock premium):**
- Historically, the midterm year produces a trough in September-October followed by a strong Q4 rally. The average Q4 return in midterm years is +7% for the S&P 500. The "gridlock" outcome (divided government) tends to be market-positive because it reduces the risk of large policy changes.

**Year 3 (pre-pre-election, fiscal acceleration):**
- Outperform: Consumer Discretionary, Industrials, Materials — sectors that benefit from infrastructure spending and consumption growth
- The Russell 2000 (small caps) tends to outperform the S&P 500 in Year 3 because smaller domestic companies benefit more from domestic fiscal expansion

**Year 4 (election year, pre-election peak):**
- Outperform: Defense (geopolitical posturing increases), Energy (commodity pricing elevated near elections)
- H2 tends to be strong regardless of which party wins — the "certainty premium" of knowing the election result boosts risk appetite

### The Biden IRA and Infrastructure Playbook

The Inflation Reduction Act (August 2022) and Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (November 2021) were studied examples of politically targeted fiscal expansion. The IRA's \$369 billion in climate-related spending was designed to flow primarily to manufacturing states (Michigan, Ohio, Pennsylvania) and Sun Belt states (Georgia, Nevada) — the exact swing-state geography of 2022-2024 electoral maps.

This created specific sector tailwinds:
- **Clean energy:** First Solar (FSLR) gained 70% in the 12 months following IRA passage
- **Battery manufacturers:** Panasonic, LG Energy (as suppliers to US EV factories built under IRA incentives)
- **Domestic industrials:** Companies building US manufacturing facilities — Foxconn, TSMC, Samsung — all received direct incentives

The trade was straightforward: long domestic industrials and clean energy from August 2022, with the thesis that \$369 billion in committed spending would flow through these sectors over 10 years. The sector-specific ETF QCLN gained approximately 25% in the 12 months post-IRA.

#### Worked example:

**Presidential Cycle Year 3 Sector Rotation Trade (2019)**

2019 was Year 3 of the Trump presidency. The period saw significant fiscal loosening relative to the baseline (the TCJA still flowing through), relatively accommodative monetary policy (Fed cut rates 3 times in 2019), and pre-election narrative building.

Entry: January 2, 2019, long XLY (Consumer Discretionary ETF) / short XLU (Utilities ETF) — a classic Year 3 cyclical vs. defensive trade
- XLY January 2019 price: \$98.50
- XLU January 2019 price: \$50.10
- Position: long \$500,000 XLY, short \$500,000 XLU

Exit: December 31, 2019
- XLY December 2019 price: \$127.00 → gain of \$145,685 on long
- XLU December 2019 price: \$67.90 → loss of \$178,842 on short (utilities rallied more than expected on rate cuts)

Result: Net loss of \$33,157 — the trade was directionally correct (XLY outperformed XLU by 29% vs 35%) but rate cuts unexpectedly boosted utilities more than the differential. This illustrates the risk of the cycle overlay when monetary policy pivots: in 2019, the Fed's unexpected rate cuts distorted the usual Year 3 pattern by also boosting rate-sensitive defensives.

**Key lesson:** The presidential cycle works best when monetary policy is on a predictable path. When the Fed surprises with unexpected cuts or hikes, the sector rotation logic can be partially overridden.

## The Trading Playbook: How to Position Around the Political Business Cycle

### Pre-Election Fiscal Expansion Playbook (Years 3-4)

**Signal 1: Budget resolution and appropriations posture.** Watch what Congress passes in September-October of Year 2. Continuing resolutions (kicking the can) signal gridlock; actual budget agreements with spending increases signal Year 3 stimulus is coming. The House Ways and Means Committee hearing schedule provides advance notice of tax legislation.

**Signal 2: CBO baseline revision.** The Congressional Budget Office updates its 10-year baseline twice per year. An upward revision to the deficit (larger deficit than previously projected) is the purest signal of fiscal loosening. The January CBO update (released in February/March) covers the budget year just starting — a widening deficit projection is a bond-negative, cyclical-positive signal.

**Signal 3: Infrastructure spending pace.** Monthly Treasury Statement data (released 8 business days after month-end by the Bureau of the Fiscal Service) shows actual federal outlays by category. An acceleration in defense, infrastructure, or transfer payments outlays is real-time confirmation of fiscal stimulus.

**Position for fiscal expansion:**
1. Long domestic cyclicals: XLY, IWM (Russell 2000), XLI (Industrials)
2. Short long-duration Treasuries: TBT, TMV, or outright futures short
3. Steepener trade: long 2-year Treasuries / short 30-year Treasuries
4. Long commodity producers if fiscal stimulus includes infrastructure (materials sector)
5. Short USD if deficit concerns are primary narrative

**Invalidation signals:**
- CBO re-scores the fiscal package as deficit-neutral or better
- Fiscal pivot: Congress forces spending offsets (pay-fors) in reconciliation
- Fed tightens aggressively enough to offset fiscal impulse (requires real rate > neutral)
- Global risk-off (geopolitical shock) overwhelms domestic cycle

### Post-Election Tightening Playbook (Year 1)

The post-election period (January of Year 1) is historically the most dangerous for risk assets because:
1. Political capital is now deployed on reform rather than stimulus
2. Deficits from the pre-election expansion must be addressed
3. The Fed often begins tightening to address inflation generated by the previous stimulus cycle

**Position for post-election tightening:**
1. Long-duration Treasuries (paradoxically — if the economy slows, rates fall)
2. Defensives: XLV (Healthcare), XLP (Consumer Staples)
3. Value over growth (lower duration stocks are less affected by rate uncertainty)
4. Long USD if Fed is tightening
5. Reduce small-cap exposure (credit-sensitive, more affected by higher rates)

**Exception:** If Year 1 coincides with a severe recession (2001, 2009), the Fed cuts aggressively and fiscal stimulus resumes, making the "post-election tightening" trade counterproductive. Always check the NBER business cycle dating before applying the political cycle overlay.

#### Worked example:

**Sizing the Presidential Cycle Trade with Kelly Criterion**

A trader has identified that Year 3 has produced positive S&P 500 returns 94% of the time since 1950. How large should the position be?

Kelly fraction formula: f* = (p × b - q) / b
Where: p = probability of win (0.94), q = 1-p = 0.06, b = average win / average loss ratio

From historical data:
- Average Year 3 gain: +15.3%
- Average loss in negative Year 3 years: -5.1% (only 1 notable negative: 2011 at -3.1%, rounding some periods)
- b = 15.3 / 5.1 = 3.0

Kelly fraction: f* = (0.94 × 3.0 - 0.06) / 3.0 = (2.82 - 0.06) / 3.0 = 2.76 / 3.0 = 0.92

Full Kelly says bet 92% of the portfolio — clearly impractical and overstated (Kelly assumes perfect knowledge of probabilities). Using half-Kelly (0.46) or quarter-Kelly (0.23) as practitioner norms:

Quarter-Kelly: allocate 23% of portfolio to Year 3 risk-on tilt (vs. neutral 0% tilt)
- On a \$10M portfolio: \$2.3M additional exposure to cyclicals above benchmark weight
- Expected return contribution: \$2.3M × 15.3% excess return expectation = \$351,900 incremental return per Year 3

This is a portfolio construction exercise, not a trading signal. The presidential cycle is an overlay on top of the fundamental investment process, not a replacement for it.
