April 2022. You are watching a live satellite feed of the Yangshan Deep-Water Port, 32 kilometres south of Shanghai. The cranes are still. Hundreds of container ships circle in holding patterns offshore. Inside the city, 25 million people are confined to their homes or, if they test positive, to government quarantine facilities that range from converted exhibition halls to repurposed schools. The Shanghai lockdown — announced as a short rolling closure on March 28 — is now entering its third week with no visible end date.

For a portfolio manager with exposure to Chinese equities, the question is not "will this end soon?" The question is: "what is the *political* cost-benefit function of the man who controls this decision, and where is he on that curve right now?" The epidemiology is irrelevant. The virus cannot be eliminated with Omicron in circulation. Every public-health scientist in the world knows this. What matters is whether Xi Jinping knows it — or more precisely, whether he has been given the political room to act on that knowledge.

This post is about learning to read that decision framework, pricing the tail outcomes, and understanding why two countries took radically different bets on pandemic policy — and why those bets produced radically different market outcomes.

## Foundations: How Lockdown Policies Work as Market Variables

Before we get into the mechanics of CSI 300 underperformance or Macau gaming revenue, we need to build the right mental model. Most investors make a critical error: they treat pandemic policy as a public-health variable and try to forecast market outcomes from epidemiological inputs. That is the wrong level of analysis.

**What a lockdown actually is, economically.** A lockdown is a forced reduction in the velocity of economic activity. Money still exists. People still have wages (often government-supported). But the transactions that normally happen — restaurant meals, retail purchases, travel, entertainment, discretionary services — are either prohibited or so constrained that they collapse. The economic damage is not from the disease itself but from the deliberate suppression of human movement and commerce.

Think of it like this: imagine an economy as water flowing through a pipe. A lockdown is someone partially blocking that pipe. The water (money) is still there, the pressure (demand) builds up behind the block, and when the block is removed, you get a surge — the "reopening trade" — as pent-up demand floods through. But while the block is in place, the sectors downstream from that constraint (restaurants, casinos, airlines, hotels) generate nothing, while sectors upstream (logistics, manufacturing for export, digital services) may continue or even benefit.

**What makes China's zero-COVID different from Sweden's open model.** The difference is not just the *degree* of restriction but the *mechanism* of control. Zero-COVID was a political doctrine, not a sliding-scale public health response. It meant that any detected outbreak — regardless of scale, location, or variant severity — triggered a mandatory suppression response: mass testing, mandatory isolation of positives (in government facilities, not at home), quarantine of close contacts, and geographic lockdowns. The system was designed to achieve a literal zero-transmission target, which was achievable against the original Wuhan strain and Delta, but became mathematically impossible against Omicron's reproductive number of 8–15.

Sweden, conversely, chose a social-trust model: voluntary distancing recommendations, no mandatory closures of schools or outdoor spaces, no national lockdown order. Sweden's constitution (Regeringsformen) made mandatory lockdowns legally difficult — the government lacks the executive power to force citizens to stay home — but the policy choice also reflected a deliberate judgment by the Public Health Agency of Sweden that focused protection of the vulnerable was more achievable than blanket suppression.

**Why these are political variables, not health variables.** Here is the crucial insight for a trader: once you understand that these policies are driven by political logic (regime legitimacy in China's case, constitutional structure in Sweden's case, public opinion in most European cases), you can treat the *policy regime* as your primary variable, and model the economic outcome as a function of regime continuity or regime change.

When China abandoned zero-COVID in December 2022, no new epidemiological data had emerged. Omicron had been circulating and obviously uncontrollable for months. What changed was the *political* cost-benefit equation: the Urumqi apartment fire on November 24, 2022, killed at least 10 people, with widely-shared accounts suggesting rescue was hampered by COVID barriers and sealed doors. The white paper protests that followed — dozens of cities, blank sheets of paper held aloft as a symbol of the unsayable — represented an unprecedented challenge to CCP social control. The political cost of zero-COVID suddenly exceeded the political cost of abandoning it.

That is a regime-change signal, and it is unambiguously in the domain of political risk analysis, not public health forecasting.

## China's Zero-COVID Doctrine: Political Logic and Economic Cost

To understand how zero-COVID became the most market-moving policy of 2022, you need to understand why Xi Jinping adopted it in the first place and why it became so difficult to exit.

**The original political logic.** In early 2020, China's response to the Wuhan outbreak was the most dramatic public-health intervention in human history: a hard lockdown of a city of 11 million people, followed by mass testing, mandatory quarantine, and a contact-tracing system that was aggressive by any standard. It worked, by the metric that mattered to the CCP: transmission fell to near zero by March 2020, and China reopened its economy while the United States and Europe were still reeling from their first waves.

This created a powerful political narrative: the CCP had *defeated* COVID, demonstrating the superiority of a system that could impose the necessary sacrifices to protect the collective. Western democracies, whose political systems made sustained mandatory restrictions difficult, could not achieve the same result. Zero-COVID was not merely a health policy — it was a proof of concept for CCP governance.

**The vaccine problem.** Here is a crucial structural factor that most Western coverage underweighted: China's domestically developed vaccines — Sinovac and Sinopharm — had efficacy rates of approximately 50–60% against symptomatic infection, compared to 90%+ for mRNA vaccines (Pfizer-BioNTech, Moderna). China had vaccinated most of its population with these vaccines, but the protection they provided against Omicron infection was substantially lower, particularly against severe disease in elderly populations who had lower vaccination uptake.

Allowing Omicron to circulate freely with a partially-vaccinated elderly population and limited ICU capacity (China has roughly 3.6 ICU beds per 100,000 population versus 25–35 in the US or Germany) risked a catastrophic healthcare system overload. This was a real constraint — not merely a political excuse — but it meant that exit from zero-COVID required a prior investment in healthcare capacity and mRNA-equivalent boosters that had not been made.

**The economic cost accumulates.** Through 2020 and 2021, the economic cost of zero-COVID was manageable because outbreaks were contained quickly. A city might lock down for 2–3 weeks and then reopen; supply chains adjusted; consumers adapted. The policy looked sustainable.

Omicron changed this calculus completely. Its high transmissibility meant that outbreaks could not be contained before spreading across multiple districts. The zero-transmission target was no longer achievable except by maintaining essentially continuous, city-wide lockdowns whenever any community transmission was detected. This is what happened in Shanghai: what began as a rolling district-by-district closure escalated to a full citywide lockdown because the outbreak kept escaping containment.

![China sector performance Q2 2022 peak lockdown: retail -11%, property -30%, GDP only 0.4% growth](/imgs/blogs/lockdown-politics-zero-covid-china-vs-open-sweden-4.png)

By Q2 2022, the macroeconomic damage was undeniable. GDP growth came in at just 0.4% year-on-year — the worst quarterly performance since the immediate outbreak quarter of Q1 2020. Retail sales fell 11.1% year-on-year in April 2022. The property sector, already under pressure from the Evergrande crisis, saw sales plunge 30% year-on-year. Shanghai's port throughput — a bellwether for global supply chains — fell approximately 40% during the lockdown, creating backlogs that rippled through electronics and automotive supply chains worldwide.

The fiscal math was also deteriorating. Zero-COVID required enormous government expenditure: mass testing operations (at peak, China was conducting millions of PCR tests per day), construction and operation of quarantine facilities (makeshift hospitals, converted venues), and community-level enforcement infrastructure. Estimates suggest testing alone cost hundreds of billions of renminbi over the policy's lifespan.

## The Shanghai Lockdown: March 28 to June 1, 2022

The Shanghai lockdown is the single most important event in understanding the CSI 300's 2022 underperformance, and it deserves a detailed treatment because it illustrates how zero-COVID's costs metastasized from manageable to catastrophic.

**The sequence.** Shanghai's outbreak began in early March 2022, driven by a sub-variant of Omicron. City authorities initially attempted a phased approach: lock down one half of the city (Pudong) for four days, then the other half (Puxi), then reopen. This "two-phase rolling" strategy failed within days because the virus had already spread across district boundaries. On April 5, authorities acknowledged the plan was not working and imposed a full citywide lockdown — more than a week after the initial phased closure began. The lockdown officially lasted until June 1, 2022 — approximately 65 days of full citywide restriction, preceded and followed by extended periods of partial restrictions.

**The humanitarian and economic machinery.** The lockdown was enforced through a combination of physical barriers (steel fences around residential compounds), QR code health passes, and a massive deployment of community workers and police. Residents who tested positive were removed from their homes and taken to government quarantine facilities, which were centralized (often in large exhibition halls or temporary structures) rather than home-based. This was one of the most contested elements of the policy: families were separated, pets were left behind, and the conditions in some mass quarantine facilities were documented as inadequate.

The economic damage was concentrated in several vectors. First, the direct loss of consumption: Shanghai accounts for roughly 3.8% of China's GDP and is the country's largest financial and trade hub. Estimates of the direct GDP loss from the lockdown ranged from tens of billions of US dollars per week at its peak. Second, supply chain disruption: Shanghai's port is the world's largest container port by volume. The slowdown created ripple effects through automotive, electronics, and chemical supply chains globally, adding to the inflationary pressures already building from the Russia-Ukraine conflict. Third, business confidence: multinational companies reconsidered their China exposure; foreign direct investment sentiment deteriorated sharply through the year.

## CSI 300 vs MSCI World: A 30-Point Divergence

With that economic backdrop, let us trace exactly what happened to the CSI 300 and why the divergence from global equities was so severe.

**The baseline.** The CSI 300 index tracks the 300 largest companies listed on the Shanghai and Shenzhen Stock Exchanges. It includes technology giants, financial institutions (Ping An Insurance, China Merchants Bank), industrials, property developers, and consumer staples. It is the primary benchmark for Chinese mainland (A-share) equities.

At the start of 2022, both the CSI 300 and the MSCI World were indexed to 100. By April 2022 — as the Shanghai lockdown began — the CSI 300 had fallen to roughly 78 on that index, while the MSCI World sat at approximately 91. The divergence was already 13 points, driven by the combination of zero-COVID uncertainty, property sector stress (the Evergrande crisis), and a regulatory crackdown on technology companies that had been running since 2021.

By October 2022, the CSI 300 had fallen to approximately 74, while the MSCI World was at roughly 80. The CSI 300 fell approximately 28.5% from its January 2022 level to its October trough, while the MSCI World fell around 20% over the same period. The net underperformance at the trough was approximately 8–10 percentage points in contemporaneous terms, but the *China-specific* underperformance — stripping out the global rate-hike factor — was estimated at 15–20 percentage points.

In absolute terms: the CSI 300 started 2022 at approximately 4,940 and bottomed in late October at approximately 3,527 — a peak-to-trough decline of 28.5%. This against a MSCI World decline of roughly 20% over the same period. The gap of 8–10pp in realized returns represents the pricing of zero-COVID as a structural economic drag, layered on top of the global risk-off from Fed tightening.

**The sector decomposition.** Not all CSI 300 sectors suffered equally. Consumer discretionary, consumer staples, real estate, and financials (exposed to property developer debt) bore the brunt. Technology — which might have been expected to benefit from a lockdown (people at home using more apps) — was simultaneously hammered by the regulatory crackdown on private tutoring, gaming, and platform businesses. Only select export-oriented industrials and certain SOE (state-owned enterprise) sectors held up reasonably.

#### Worked example: Calculating the cost of holding CSI 300 through the lockdown

Suppose you are a global emerging markets portfolio manager. On January 1, 2022, you hold a position equivalent to \$10 million in CSI 300 exposure. Your benchmark is 30% China in the EM index, and your actual weight matches benchmark.

- **January 2022**: CSI 300 at notional 100. Position value: \$10,000,000.
- **April 2022**: CSI 300 at 78. Position value: \$7,800,000. Loss: \$2,200,000.
- **October 2022** (trough): CSI 300 at 74. Position value: \$7,400,000. Total loss from start: \$2,600,000 (-26%).
- **MSCI World** fell to 80 by October. A comparable \$10M in MSCI World is now worth \$8,000,000. Loss: \$2,000,000 (-20%).
- **Underperformance cost**: \$7,400,000 vs \$8,000,000 = \$600,000 of additional loss simply from the China-specific zero-COVID drag.

Now consider an active manager who, in late March 2022, identified the Shanghai lockdown as a regime-risk event rather than a normal drawdown, and reduced China exposure from benchmark (30%) to 15% of their EM allocation. Their China position falls from \$10M to \$5M. The October trough value of the China position is \$3,700,000 (vs \$5M held). The manager avoided \$300,000 of additional drawdown on the China sleeve — a meaningful contribution to relative performance in a difficult year.

The political-risk reduction trade was not primarily about predicting the *magnitude* of the lockdown. It was about recognizing that the policy regime was one where the downside was unbounded (the government could always extend restrictions) and the upside was capped (the market had already priced significant recovery from zero-COVID exit, which was blocked as long as the policy held).

## The Overnight Reversal: December 2022

The most remarkable feature of China's zero-COVID exit was its speed. After nearly three years of the policy being treated as a non-negotiable pillar of CCP governance — after Xi Jinping won his historic third term at the Party Congress in October 2022 partly on a platform of zero-COVID success — the entire apparatus was dismantled in a single overnight press release.

**The trigger sequence.** On November 24, 2022, a fire broke out in a high-rise apartment building in Urumqi, Xinjiang. At least 10 people were killed. Social media accounts (many subsequently deleted) described residents unable to evacuate because doors were sealed or barricaded as part of COVID quarantine enforcement, and fire trucks unable to access certain floors due to COVID barriers. The accounts were not fully verified, but they resonated with a population exhausted by three years of disruptions.

The protests that followed were extraordinary by any standard. On November 25–27, people gathered in dozens of cities — Shanghai, Beijing, Wuhan, Chengdu — holding blank sheets of white paper. The empty sheet was a symbol of everything that could not be said: the unspoken criticism of zero-COVID, of autocracy, of censorship itself. For anyone who studies political risk, blank paper protests in an authoritarian state are a high-signal event. They indicate that the informal social contract between the government and the governed is under stress in a way that normal opinion polling cannot capture.

The government's response was calibrated. Rather than a crackdown (which carried its own political costs and would have been internationally visible), authorities began quietly signaling a change. State media ran unusually critical coverage of zero-COVID's economic costs. A series of expert statements appeared endorsing the lesser severity of Omicron versus earlier variants. And on December 7, 2022, the National Health Commission released the "10 measures" — a document that, in a single press release, abolished mandatory mass testing, quarantine camps, the requirement to test negative before entering public venues, and virtually every other pillar of the zero-COVID enforcement architecture.

![China zero-COVID pipeline of key events from Jan 2020 Wuhan lockdown to Dec 7 2022 abandonment](/imgs/blogs/lockdown-politics-zero-covid-china-vs-open-sweden-3.png)

There was no transition period. No 30-day wind-down. No regional pilot. The policy that had locked 25 million people in their homes in April 2022 simply ceased to exist on December 7, 2022. The political reversal was total and immediate.

**The immediate market reaction.** The CSI 300 had already begun pricing in the possibility of a zero-COVID exit in November 2022 (as protests signaled political stress), recovering from 74 to approximately 82 on our indexed scale. From December 2022 through January 2023, it continued to recover toward 89–95, and for a brief period in early 2023 reached near parity with MSCI World.

But the most dramatic moves were in the reopening-sensitive sectors — particularly Macau casino stocks, which had been among the most direct victims of zero-COVID.

## The Reopening Trade Playbook

The China reopening trade of December 2022 to January 2023 is a masterclass in how to think about policy-regime reversals in equity markets. It also illustrates some important cautionary lessons about the difference between the first-order trade (obvious sectors rally) and the second-order reality (structural problems remain).

**The first-order trade: sectors most impacted by zero-COVID.** The sectors that had been most directly suppressed by zero-COVID were the first and fastest to rally:

1. **Macau casino operators**: Sands China, Galaxy Entertainment, MGM China, Wynn Macau, Melco Resorts. These companies had seen gross gaming revenue (GGR) fall to 91% below their 2019 peak levels during zero-COVID's operation. The December 7 announcement triggered immediate buying. Sands China and Galaxy Entertainment both rallied 40–60% within six weeks of the announcement.

2. **Chinese airlines**: Air China, China Southern Airlines, China Eastern Airlines. International route capacity had been reduced to approximately 5% of 2019 levels during zero-COVID. The reopening of borders in January 2023 triggered strong forward booking data and airline stock recoveries of 30–35%.

3. **Hotels, travel, and domestic tourism**: Trip.com and domestic hotel chains saw booking surges as pent-up travel demand began to release.

4. **Consumer discretionary**: Companies selling goods and experiences that had been restricted during lockdown — restaurants, entertainment, luxury goods — all benefited from the initial reopening thesis.

![China sectors before and after zero-COVID abandonment Dec 2022, casinos from GGR minus 91 percent to plus 40-60 stock rally](/imgs/blogs/lockdown-politics-zero-covid-china-vs-open-sweden-5.png)

**The second-order complication: what the reopening trade got wrong.** The initial rally was followed by a significant sobering. By mid-2023, the CSI 300 had fallen back to around 86 on our index — below the MSCI World's 105, and dramatically below where the reopening thesis had projected. Several structural problems that zero-COVID had masked or accelerated came back into view:

- **Property sector crisis**: The Evergrande-linked property developer debt crisis did not go away with COVID's end. China's property sector — which in aggregate represents roughly 25–30% of GDP when construction, related industries, and land finance are included — remained under severe stress.

- **Youth unemployment**: China's youth unemployment rate hit 21.3% in June 2023 before the government suspended publication of the statistic. This reflected both the economic damage from zero-COVID and the structural overcapacity of certain graduate-heavy sectors.

- **Western decoupling pressure**: Semiconductor export controls, electric vehicle tariffs, and tech investment restrictions continued to weigh on the foreign direct investment climate and the export outlook for high-value-added manufacturing.

By January 2024, the CSI 300 had fallen to around 70 on our index — 38 percentage points below MSCI World at 108, a wider gap than at the October 2022 trough.

#### Worked example: The Macau casino reopening trade in numbers

Sands China (1928.HK) provides the cleanest worked example because its revenue was almost entirely dependent on the Macau gaming market with essentially no alternative revenue streams during zero-COVID.

**Pre-COVID baseline**: Sands China's 2019 annual gross gaming revenue contribution was in line with the broader Macau market running at approximately MOP 25–30 billion per month aggregate. The stock traded at roughly HK\$40–45 per share in early 2020, before COVID struck.

**Zero-COVID trough**: By November 2022, Macau's gross gaming revenue was running at roughly MOP 2–3 billion per month — approximately 90% below the 2019 monthly average. Sands China's stock traded at approximately HK\$14–16 per share, a decline of roughly 65% from pre-COVID levels.

**The announcement (December 7, 2022)**: Within two weeks of the 10-point measures announcement, Sands China had risen to approximately HK\$22–24 per share. The move was approximately +50% from the November trough.

**The fundamental justification**: If you believed that Macau GGR would recover to 70% of 2019 levels within 12 months — a conservative assumption given pent-up demand from mainland Chinese visitors — the implied revenue run-rate was substantial. At pre-COVID enterprise-value-to-revenue multiples, the implied equity value was significantly above the HK\$14–16 trough.

**The actual recovery**: By Q3 2023, Macau GGR had recovered to approximately 85% of 2019 levels — faster than the conservative model assumed. Monthly GGR was running at MOP 18–20 billion. The casino stocks held most of their gains through this period.

**The lesson**: The trade worked because (1) the zero-COVID suppression was a policy variable that could change overnight, (2) the underlying demand was real and pent-up (not structurally destroyed), and (3) the sector had no alternative revenue streams to cushion the downside — which meant the upside on reopening was symmetric and large.

## Sweden's Contrarian Experiment

While China was executing the world's most aggressive suppression strategy, Sweden was running its opposite: a voluntary, non-mandatory approach to pandemic management that made it the outlier in Europe and, for a period, made Swedish officials international pariahs in the public health community.

**What Sweden actually did.** Sweden did not impose a national lockdown. Primary and middle schools remained open throughout the pandemic. Restaurants, bars, and shops stayed open (with distancing guidance). Outdoor gatherings were limited but indoor gatherings only faced caps, not bans. The Public Health Agency of Sweden, under State Epidemiologist Anders Tegnell, pursued a strategy of focused protection of the vulnerable (particularly nursing home residents) combined with voluntary behavioral adaptation.

This was not simply ideological libertarianism. Sweden's constitution (Regeringsformen) made emergency executive powers legally difficult to invoke — the government could not simply issue a lockdown decree the way a prime minister in a Westminster system might. The policy also reflected a judgment that sustained voluntary compliance was more achievable than mandatory compliance in a high-trust society, and that the economic and social costs of school closures in particular would be severe and long-lasting.

**The Swedish mortality reality.** Sweden's approach carried real costs. Sweden's excess deaths in 2020 were approximately 9,400, corresponding to roughly 93 per 100,000 population — higher than Norway (roughly 20 per 100,000 in 2020) and Denmark (roughly 30 per 100,000 in 2020), which imposed stricter restrictions. However, Sweden's excess mortality was comparable to the United Kingdom (approximately 92 per 100,000 in 2020) despite the UK having national lockdown policies, and significantly lower than the United States (approximately 180 per 100,000 in 2020).

For a finance perspective, the mortality question matters primarily insofar as it contextualizes the political sustainability of the model and its eventual market outcome. The Swedish approach was sustainable in the sense that it was not dramatically more lethal than alternatives, and it produced radically different economic outcomes.

![OMXS30 vs STOXX 600 indexed Jan 2020, Sweden gained 12.9% in 2020 while Europe fell 4%, cumulative 37pp gap by end 2021](/imgs/blogs/lockdown-politics-zero-covid-china-vs-open-sweden-2.png)

**The market outcome: OMXS30 vs European STOXX 600.** The OMXS30 index of Sweden's 30 largest listed companies is dominated by large industrial exporters (Volvo, Atlas Copco, Ericsson), financial institutions (Swedbank, SEB), and pharmaceuticals. It is not a pure play on Swedish domestic consumption. Nevertheless, the contrast with European peers through 2020–2021 is striking:

- **2020**: OMXS30 gained 12.9% over the calendar year. The STOXX 600 (pan-European) fell approximately 4%. The gap was 16.9 percentage points.
- **2021**: OMXS30 gained 37.4%. The STOXX 600 gained approximately 23%. The gap was 14.4 percentage points.
- **Cumulative 2020–2021**: OMXS30 compounded to approximately +55% from the January 2020 baseline. STOXX 600 compounded to approximately +18%. The cumulative outperformance was approximately 37 percentage points.

**Disentangling the drivers.** The OMXS30's composition offers a partial explanation: Sweden's largest companies are globally oriented industrials that benefit from global trade recovery (2021 was a strong year for global manufacturing), which would have helped regardless of domestic COVID policy. STOXX 600 includes a heavier weight of financials, energy, and domestically-oriented companies.

But a sector-adjusted analysis still shows meaningful domestic outperformance attributable to the no-lockdown model. Swedish domestic services — hotels, restaurants, retail — continued to operate through 2020 with only voluntary restrictions, while equivalent German or French operators were closed for extended periods. This showed up in Swedish employment data (Sweden's unemployment peaked at 9.0% in 2020), in retail sales data, and in consumer confidence surveys.

The no-lockdown model also meant that Swedish companies did not receive the same level of government fiscal support as many European counterparts — they did not need it as much, because they stayed open. This meant less fiscal debt accumulation and, for equity investors, less concern about the sustainability of government support programs.

#### Worked example: The OMXS30 outperformance in portfolio terms

Consider a European equity portfolio manager who, in February 2020, allocated \$50 million to European equities with a benchmark of approximately 8% Sweden (OMXS30) and 92% broader STOXX 600. Their initial allocation is therefore \$4 million to OMXS30 and \$46 million to STOXX 600.

**By end of 2020 (benchmark):**
- STOXX 600: \$46,000,000 × (1 - 0.04) = \$44,160,000
- OMXS30: \$4,000,000 × (1 + 0.129) = \$4,516,000
- Total benchmark: \$48,676,000

**Tilted manager** who overweighted OMXS30 to 25% of the European allocation (\$12.5M OMXS30, \$37.5M STOXX 600) based on the Sweden-no-lockdown thesis:
- STOXX 600: \$37,500,000 × 0.96 = \$36,000,000
- OMXS30: \$12,500,000 × 1.129 = \$14,112,500
- Total tilted: \$50,112,500 vs benchmark \$48,676,000
- **Excess return in 2020**: +\$1,436,500, approximately +2.9pp over benchmark

By end of 2021, the OMXS30 compounding advantage widens further. A manager who maintained the OMXS30 overweight through both years would have generated approximately 5–6pp of cumulative excess return from the single call that Sweden's domestic economy would avoid the worst lockdown drag.

The trade was uncomfortable in 2020 — recommending overweight Sweden in the middle of a global pandemic, when Swedish epidemiologists were being criticized internationally. That discomfort is precisely the alpha source. Uncomfortable, well-reasoned, politically-analyzed trades are where excess returns live.

## PM Löfven and the Political Cost of Being Right

Sweden's no-lockdown approach was controversial, and it had genuine political costs. Prime Minister Stefan Löfven's story is a useful case study in how political leadership and market-relevant policy interact.

**The June 2021 no-confidence vote.** Stefan Löfven became the first Swedish Prime Minister in history to lose a no-confidence vote, on June 21, 2021. The vote passed by 181-109. He initially chose to call a snap election rather than resign, but ultimately chose to step down and allow a new government formation process. He resigned as PM in November 2021.

Critically, the primary cause of the no-confidence vote was not COVID policy. The proximate trigger was a dispute over rental regulations: the Left Party (Vänsterpartiet, which supported Löfven's minority government from outside the coalition) demanded that the government withdraw a planned deregulation of the rental housing market, specifically reforms that would allow newly constructed apartments to be rented at market rates rather than the heavily regulated "use value" (bruksvärde) system that governs Swedish rents. When Löfven refused, the Left Party voted with the right-wing opposition to bring down the government.

So the PM who oversaw Sweden's pandemic response was brought down by a housing policy dispute, not a COVID failure. This is a useful reminder that political risk rarely arrives on schedule or through the channel you expect.

**The public opinion dimension.** Swedish public opinion on the pandemic strategy was more complex than international coverage suggested. In early 2020, polls showed majority support for stricter restrictions. By 2021, as Swedish mortality outcomes proved to be not dramatically worse than locked-down neighbors, and as economic damage was significantly lower, public opinion had shifted. The Public Health Agency consistently maintained high credibility ratings.

The international reputational cost was significant: Tegnell and Sweden's health authorities were featured negatively in international media through most of 2020. For a small, export-oriented economy deeply integrated into global supply chains and reliant on international reputation, this had some indirect costs. But the *direct* market effect was the opposite of what you might expect from negative headlines: investors voted with capital toward Sweden's equities rather than away from them.

**Why being right about the economics did not eliminate the political turbulence.** The Swedish government's policy produced better economic outcomes than most European alternatives. But the political class still faced turbulence (the no-confidence vote), public criticism (especially the nursing home deaths), and international reputational costs. Being economically right did not insulate the government from political vulnerability through other channels.

For market participants, the takeaway is: you can be right about the *economic* effects of a policy decision and still be caught by the *political* instability generated around the margin of that decision. Sweden's equity market outperformed; Swedish politics remained messy.

## Cross-Asset Implications

Both the China and Sweden stories generated cross-asset effects beyond their domestic equity markets.

**China's zero-COVID and global supply chains.** The Shanghai lockdown's effect on global supply chains was not symmetric across sectors. Semiconductor supply chains — already stressed by the US-China tech war and post-COVID chip shortages — took additional hits when factories in Shanghai's industrial suburbs faced logistics disruptions. Automotive manufacturers dependent on components from Shanghai-area suppliers saw production shutdowns ripple through their global operations.

This created a cross-asset effect in industrial metals. Copper, a sensitive indicator of Chinese industrial activity, fell from approximately US\$4.90/lb in February 2022 to approximately \$3.20/lb by July 2022 — a decline partly attributable to the Shanghai lockdown's demand destruction signal. Investors tracking copper as a "Chinese growth proxy" were receiving an accurate signal from the lockdown's economic damage.

**Renminbi impact.** China's currency weakened substantially through 2022. The CNY/USD rate moved from approximately 6.35 at the start of 2022 to 7.25 at the October trough — a depreciation of approximately 14%. This reflected both the economic damage from zero-COVID and the divergence in monetary policy (the Fed was hiking aggressively while China was easing to offset lockdown damage). For global investors with unhedged Chinese equity exposure, the currency movement compounded the equity return loss.

**Swedish krona and interest rate dynamics.** Sweden's no-lockdown model allowed the Riksbank (Sweden's central bank) to maintain relatively accommodative monetary policy through 2020 without the same fiscal stimulus requirements as countries running extended lockdowns. The Swedish krona weakened against the dollar in early 2020 but recovered faster than many European currencies as the economic damage proved more contained.

## Common Misconceptions

Several misconceptions about the China and Sweden pandemic trades are worth addressing directly, because they represent analytical errors that can be costly in real portfolio management.

**Misconception 1: "Zero-COVID worked until Omicron."** This framing misunderstands the policy's nature. Zero-COVID worked in the narrow sense of achieving near-zero domestic transmission through 2020–2021. But the *cost* was a permanent suppression of the domestic services economy and a permanent need for massive testing and enforcement infrastructure. The policy was not free during its operational phase — it was subsidized by fiscal expenditure, by household savings depletion, and by the delayed demand of a population that could not spend normally. Omicron did not "break" zero-COVID; it made the cost of maintaining it politically unsustainable by requiring continuous full-city lockdowns rather than occasional district-level ones.

**Misconception 2: "Sweden's success proves lockdowns don't work."** Sweden's outcome does not prove this. Sweden benefited from specific structural advantages: low population density (outside Stockholm), high baseline trust in government allowing voluntary compliance, strong fiscal capacity, and a labor market with high rates of remote-eligible work. These factors are not replicable everywhere. The Swedish model also produced clearly worse outcomes than Norway and Denmark — suggesting that some level of restriction beyond Sweden's baseline was beneficial, even in the Nordic context.

**Misconception 3: "The China reopening trade was straightforward."** The first phase was straightforward in direction (go long sectors crushed by zero-COVID). The sizing and the exit were not. The property market did not recover. Youth unemployment remained high. The structural growth trajectory of the Chinese economy post-COVID has been one of deceleration, not re-acceleration. Traders who sized up large on the reopening thesis and held into 2023–2024 gave back significant gains as the CSI 300 fell to new multi-year lows relative to MSCI World.

**Misconception 4: "Political protests are reliable exit signals."** The Urumqi fire and white paper protests *were* a reliable signal in this case — but that is partly because they came on top of an already-unsustainable policy trajectory. Protests in authoritarian states do not always produce rapid policy capitulation. The reason the December 2022 protests succeeded was the combination of: (1) economic stress that had undermined government credibility, (2) Omicron's demonstrated biological uncontainability, (3) a post-Party Congress political window where Xi had just secured his third term and could afford to pivot without it looking like weakness, and (4) the high healthcare infrastructure cost of allowing an Omicron wave that was coming regardless.

## How It Shows Up in Real Markets

For practical investment application, pandemic policy as a market variable has several observable manifestations that can be tracked in real time.

**Air traffic data as a leading indicator.** Before official economic data confirms the damage (or recovery), air traffic data — airport throughput, flight bookings, airline load factors — moves. During the Shanghai lockdown, Pudong International Airport was essentially closed to international commercial traffic. This was observable in real time through CAAC (Civil Aviation Administration of China) data and through FlightAware/FlightRadar24 traffic monitoring. Investors who tracked this had the demand destruction signal several weeks before Q2 GDP data confirmed it.

**Port congestion data.** Yangshan Port's container throughput is tracked by multiple shipping data providers (Alphaliner, Container xChange). The April 2022 throughput collapse to approximately 40% of normal was visible before economic data was published. This created a tradeable signal in container shipping equities and in global industrial supply chain-exposed stocks.

**Gaming revenue data (Macau).** Macau publishes monthly gaming revenue data (GGR) within days of each month ending. This data became the most accurate real-time indicator of zero-COVID's severity: when GGR was near zero, zero-COVID was tight; when GGR recovered, the policy had genuinely relaxed. Investors who tracked GGR monthly — rather than waiting for company earnings — had a 4–6 week information advantage over those reading quarterly reports.

![Macau gross gaming revenue 2022-2023: collapsed to MOP 1-3 billion under zero-COVID, recovered to MOP 18-20 billion by mid-2023](/imgs/blogs/lockdown-politics-zero-covid-china-vs-open-sweden-7.png)

**Property transaction data.** Chinese real estate transaction data — sales volumes in major cities — is published weekly in some markets and monthly at the national level. The collapse during zero-COVID (and the subsequent slow recovery) provided a real-time read on the demand side of the property crisis that was overlapping with zero-COVID damage.

**Social media signals (with caveats).** The white paper protests of November 2022 were visible on Chinese social media for approximately 12–24 hours before censorship was applied. International social media amplified them. For political risk traders with a China desk, this was a high-signal event requiring immediate analytical response: what does an unprecedented multi-city protest mean for policy trajectory? The answer — faster exit from zero-COVID than previously priced — was available to analysts who were watching on the nights of November 25–27, 2022.

## How to Trade It: The Policy-Regime Playbook

Drawing together the China and Sweden lessons, here is a structured framework for trading pandemic (and more broadly, government-policy) regimes in equity and cross-asset markets.

**Step 1: Identify whether the policy is a variable or a constant.** Zero-COVID was, for most of its lifespan, treated by markets as a near-constant — a structural feature of the Chinese economy to be priced in as a persistent drag. This underpriced the tail risk of regime change (the sharp rally when it ended). Sweden's no-lockdown model was also treated as a constant, but one priced somewhat correctly by OMXS30 outperformance in 2020.

The question is: under what conditions could the regime change, and what is the probability of each? For zero-COVID, the relevant conditions were: (1) Omicron's arrival, (2) political consolidation (Party Congress providing a window to pivot), (3) social protest, and (4) healthcare system capacity. Track these conditions explicitly.

**Step 2: Identify the sectors with the highest policy sensitivity and the lowest alternative revenue.** Casino operators in Macau had zero alternative revenue when cross-border travel was blocked. Airlines had minimal revenue with 5% of normal capacity. These sectors had the largest reopening upside *and* the most transparent indicators (GGR data, airport throughput) to track in real time.

**Step 3: Separate the first-order trade from the second-order reality.** The first-order trade (go long suppressed sectors on reopening) is often correct in direction. The second-order question — will the structural economy recover to pre-policy levels? — is often much more complicated. China's structural challenges (property, debt, demographic headwinds, geopolitical decoupling) meant that the reopening thesis had a sharp time limit.

**Step 4: Size appropriately for tail events.** A regime change like December 7, 2022 is a tail event: low probability on any given day, high magnitude when it occurs. Options (long calls on reopening-sensitive stocks) can provide levered exposure with defined downside if the regime change is delayed.

#### Worked example: Sizing the Macau reopening options trade

In November 2022, with zero-COVID still in force, implied volatility on Macau casino stocks was elevated. Suppose a trader wanted exposure to the reopening scenario for Sands China (1928.HK), which was trading at approximately HK\$16.

**Option 1 — direct equity position**: Buy 100,000 shares at HK\$16 per share. Total outlay: HK\$1,600,000. If the stock rallies 50% on reopening to HK\$24: profit = HK\$800,000 (+50%). If zero-COVID continues and the stock falls 20% to HK\$12.80: loss = HK\$320,000 (-20%). Maximum loss is 100% of position if the company has existential problems.

**Option 2 — defined-risk call options**: Buy 3-month at-the-money call options on Sands China with strike at HK\$16, paying approximately HK\$1.20 per share in premium (reflecting elevated implied volatility). For the same HK\$1,600,000 outlay, the trader controls options on 1,333,333 shares. If the stock rallies to HK\$24: intrinsic value per option = HK\$8.00, total value = HK\$10,667,000, profit = HK\$9,067,000 (+567%). If zero-COVID continues and the stock falls to HK\$12.80: all options expire worthless, maximum loss = HK\$1,600,000 (-100% of premium, but that is the defined risk).

The levered option position has a dramatically asymmetric profile: the upside is 567% vs the delta-1's 50%, while the downside is capped at the premium (the same in dollar terms as the equity position's outlay). The trade-off is that the options expire — if zero-COVID persists beyond the 3-month window, the trader must roll or exit.

For a tail-event trade where the timing is unknown, the option structure allows a trader to be *directionally right* without betting their portfolio on *when* the regime change occurs. The long-dated equity position survives a delayed reversal; the option requires active management of expiry risk.
