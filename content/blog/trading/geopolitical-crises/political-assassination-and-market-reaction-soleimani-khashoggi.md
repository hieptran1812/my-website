> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Political assassinations almost universally produce short-term spikes in oil (+3-6%), gold (+1-2%), and VIX (+5-10%) that fully reverse within 48-72 hours, because the target's elimination rarely triggers the retaliatory escalation that markets initially fear.
>
> - The Soleimani killing (Jan 3, 2020): oil +4% overnight, S&P futures -1.5%, gold +1.6% — full reversal by Jan 8 as Iran's missile strike deliberately caused zero US casualties.
> - The Khashoggi murder (Oct 2018): Saudi TASI fell -7%, recovered within 3 weeks as MBS retained power and oil markets normalized.
> - The 72-hour rule: what the target's allies do in the first 72 hours determines whether you have a spike or a cascade. Allies mobilizing forces = potential cascade; allies condemning verbally = spike to fade.
> - The Archduke Ferdinand exception (1914) shows when the cascade does happen: alliance rigidity plus automated mobilization schedules plus no off-ramp. Identify these three conditions before assuming mean reversion.

The timestamp read 1:00 AM in Washington, DC, and oil futures markets were exploding. On January 3, 2020, a US MQ-9 Reaper drone had just killed Major General Qasem Soleimani — commander of Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps Quds Force, the most important military figure in Iran, and the architect of Iranian proxy power across the Middle East — at Baghdad International Airport. Oil opened the Asian session and immediately spiked 4%. Gold jumped 1.6%. US equity futures fell 1.5%.

Traders who had lived through the 2003 Iraq War, the 2011 Arab Spring, the 2019 Saudi oil facility attack, were asking the same question: is this the beginning of a regional war, or is it a spike to fade?

By January 8, five days later, markets had their answer. Iran had launched 16 ballistic missiles at two US bases in Iraq — a dramatic display of retaliatory capability. And deliberately caused zero American casualties. Every missile was telegraphed. Every warning was given. Iran wanted to retaliate publicly enough to satisfy its domestic audience without triggering the full-scale US military response that would end the Islamic Republic. The escalation ladder had a rung, and Iran stepped on it, then stopped.

By January 10, Brent crude was below its pre-Soleimani level. The S&P 500 was at new all-time highs. The spike had been sold, and the sellers were right.

This is the pattern. Understanding why it recurs — and when it doesn't — is the assassin's market handbook.

## Foundations: Why Assassination Almost Never Triggers War

The intuition that killing a country's most important military or political figure should trigger war is understandable but empirically wrong. History is full of assassinations that had minimal lasting geopolitical impact.

**The substitutability problem.** States are institutional, not personal. When a military commander is killed, he is replaced — typically with someone equally capable, equally ideologically committed, and now personally motivated by revenge. The Quds Force did not dissolve when Soleimani died; his replacement, Ismail Qaani, continued the same strategy. The Iranian proxy network Soleimani built — Hezbollah, Hamas, the Houthis, Iraqi Shia militias — did not collapse. It may have become more decentralized and somewhat less coherent, but the strategic architecture survived.

**The retaliation calculus.** The target country (in this case, Iran) must weigh the domestic political benefit of retaliation against the military risk of escalation. For Iran's leadership, the calculus in January 2020 was transparent: retaliate with enough force to demonstrate capability and will, but calibrate carefully to avoid triggering US military response that could be existential for the regime. The "zero casualties" missile strike was the perfect solution — politically, it looked like retaliation; strategically, it was de-escalation.

**The markets understand this.** After a 20-year track record of post-assassination spikes that reversed, sophisticated traders know the pattern. The initial spike is driven by algorithmic systems, retail panic, and the genuine uncertainty of the first hours. But as the 72-hour framework plays out and the cascade signals don't fire, the smart money reverses the initial trade.

The key question is never "will there be retaliation?" (there almost always is some form). The question is "will retaliation escalate toward regime change, territorial war, or alliance mobilization?" The answer is almost always no — because each side understands the other's red lines.

## The Soleimani Killing: A Perfect Case Study

### The Event (January 3, 2020)

Qasem Soleimani was traveling through Baghdad when a US drone killed him along with Abu Mahdi al-Muhandis, the leader of the Kataib Hezbollah militia. The US cited "imminent threat" as the legal justification, though details remained disputed. Iran declared three days of national mourning. Supreme Leader Khamenei pledged "severe revenge."

**Why Soleimani mattered:** Unlike most military commanders, Soleimani was genuinely irreplaceable in terms of relationships and strategic influence. He had personally cultivated every major Iranian proxy leader for 20 years. He was in Baghdad for diplomatic purposes when he was killed — reinforcing to regional actors that the US was willing to act outside established rules of engagement. This was not a battlefield assassination; it was a strategic assassination of a near-head-of-state figure.

**The market's overnight response (January 3-4, 2020):**
- Brent crude: \$68.00 → \$71.75 overnight (+5.5%), opened Jan 4 at \$70.50
- WTI crude: \$63.00 → \$65.56 overnight (+4%)
- Gold: \$1,523 → \$1,548 (+1.6%)
- S&P 500 futures: -1.5%
- USD/JPY: fell (yen safe-haven bid)
- VIX: rose approximately 7% to 13.4

**The 72-hour watch:**
The critical 72-hour window ran from January 3 to January 6. The questions the market was asking:
1. Would Iran activate Hezbollah against Israel? (No — Hezbollah stayed quiet)
2. Would Iran mine the Strait of Hormuz? (No — shipping continued)
3. Would Iraq expel US forces? (Parliament voted to request US withdrawal, but the operative word is "request")
4. Would Russia or China signal support for escalation? (No — both condemned the assassination diplomatically, neither provided military backing)

By January 5, none of the cascade signals had fired. Oil began its retreat from the spike peak.

**January 8, 2020 — Iran's missile strike:**
Iran launched 16 Qiam-1 and Fateh-313 missiles at Al-Asad air base and Erbil air base in Iraq. No US or coalition casualties. Iranian state media showed the launches; the missiles hit empty hangars. Every US military asset had been pre-positioned away from impact zones. Iran had notified Iraq in advance, who may have notified the US.

This was deliberate. It was the most sophisticated piece of strategic communication in recent Middle Eastern history: Iran demonstrated that it could and would retaliate, while simultaneously ensuring the retaliation could not be the basis for US military escalation. The domestic Iranian audience got its revenge; the US got its off-ramp.

**Market resolution:**
By January 10, Brent crude was back to \$65 — below the pre-assassination level. The S&P 500 hit new all-time highs on January 17. Gold gave back most of its gains.

The trade: buy the volatility spike on day 1 (sell vol via short VIX, sell Brent puts), fade the crisis by day 3.

## The Khashoggi Murder: The Saudi Exception

### The Event (October 2, 2018)

Jamal Khashoggi, a Saudi journalist and Washington Post columnist, was killed inside the Saudi consulate in Istanbul by Saudi operatives acting on orders from (according to US intelligence assessments) Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman (MBS). The murder was exposed through Turkish intelligence, creating an extraordinary diplomatic crisis.

**Why this was different:** This was not a geopolitical assassination in the traditional sense — Khashoggi was a dissident journalist, not a military figure. The market impact operated through a different channel: it raised the question of whether MBS would survive politically, which was directly relevant to Saudi economic policy (Vision 2030, oil production decisions, NEOM, Saudi Aramco's planned IPO).

**The market response:**
- Saudi Tadawul All Share Index (TASI): fell approximately 7% in the two weeks following the murder
- Saudi sovereign bonds: spread widened approximately 25bp
- Oil: actually recovered — Brent fell from \$85/bbl to \$75/bbl over October-November 2018, but primarily due to President Trump's tweets demanding Saudi oil production increases and global demand slowdown fears, not the Khashoggi murder specifically

**The MBS survival factor:**

The critical market question was: does Khashoggi's murder threaten MBS's position? If yes, Saudi economic policy was at risk. If no, the murder was a brutal crime that had no fundamental asset impact.

The answer was clear within 3-4 weeks: MBS retained power. G20 leaders attended the Riyadh summit in November 2018 despite some boycotts. President Trump declined to invoke the Magnitsky Act. Western governments, with the exception of Canada and some European parliaments, maintained relationships with the Kingdom. Saudi Aramco's IPO was delayed but not canceled.

Once MBS survival was confirmed, the TASI recovered. Within 3 weeks of the murder, the index had recovered approximately 60% of its decline. By Q1 2019, it had fully recovered.

**Investor lesson:** The Khashoggi case illustrates that assassinations/murders of non-state actors matter to markets primarily through the political survival of the decision-makers at the top. Markets weren't pricing Khashoggi's death; they were pricing the probability that MBS would fall, and thus whether Saudi economic strategy would change.

## The 72-Hour Framework: What to Watch

![VIX spikes and recovery times for major political and assassination events](/imgs/blogs/political-assassination-and-market-reaction-soleimani-khashoggi-2.png)

The data across multiple events confirms a consistent pattern: VIX spikes from assassination/political events are systematically smaller than from structural crises (COVID = +82% VIX spike, Russia-Ukraine = +31%), and they recover faster (Soleimani: 4 days; Archduke Ferdinand level events are the only exception).

The 72-hour framework operates on five signals:

**Signal 1: Allied military mobilization**
Has any country that the target's nation is allied with begun mobilizing military forces? In the Soleimani case, Russia and China condemned but did not mobilize. This was the clearest signal that Iran was isolated in any potential escalation.

**Signal 2: Economic decoupling actions**
Has the target's country taken economic decoupling actions (asset freezes, trade restrictions, energy cutoffs)? Iran sanctioning the US would be meaningless; but if a major oil exporter targeted a major oil consumer with an energy cutoff, the market impact would be structural rather than spike-and-revert.

**Signal 3: Nuclear posture changes**
Any signal that nuclear-capable states are altering their nuclear posture — raising alert levels, moving weapons, changing command authorities — is the ultimate cascade signal. Iran does not have nuclear weapons (though it is near weapons-grade enrichment capacity). This signal was not triggered in 2020.

**Signal 4: Domestic political destabilization in the attacker country**
Did the assassination create a political crisis for the country that conducted it? In the US case, there was intense Congressional criticism of the Trump administration's decision to kill Soleimani without Congressional authorization — but this did not approach political destabilization.

**Signal 5: Strait/corridor closure threat**
The Strait of Hormuz is the critical chokepoint: 20% of global oil passes through it. Any credible threat to close it would create oil spikes far beyond the Soleimani level. Iran has the capability and, in extremis, the willingness to try. The fact that Iran's January 2020 retaliation did not involve Hormuz was the single most important market signal.

If none of the five signals fires in 72 hours: fade the spike.

## The Archduke Ferdinand Exception: When Cascade Happens

![Archduke Ferdinand assassination 1914 alliance cascade creating World War I with no diplomatic off-ramp](/imgs/blogs/political-assassination-and-market-reaction-soleimani-khashoggi-6.png)

The Archduke Franz Ferdinand assassination on June 28, 1914, is the exception that proves the rule — the one case in modern history where an assassination triggered exactly the cascade that markets now routinely discount. Understanding why 1914 was different is critical to knowing when the 72-hour framework might fail.

**What was different in 1914:**

1. **Alliance rigidity:** The great powers had assembled two rigid alliance blocs — the Triple Entente (France, Russia, UK) and the Triple Alliance (Germany, Austria-Hungary, Italy) — with mutual defense obligations that were automatic, not discretionary. When Austria-Hungary declared war on Serbia, Russia was obligated to mobilize. When Russia mobilized, Germany was obligated to mobilize. There was no ASEAN or G7 equivalent to allow diplomatic off-ramps.

2. **Mobilization schedules:** Germany's Schlieffen Plan required attacking France through Belgium within two weeks of mobilization orders. This was not a political decision — it was a logistical timetable. Once mobilization began, the military timetable overrode political decision-making. Kaiser Wilhelm II reportedly tried to reverse the mobilization order on July 31 and was told by his generals it was operationally impossible.

3. **No off-ramp institution:** In 2020, the UN Security Council, direct diplomatic channels between Iran and the US (through Switzerland), and the Iraqi government all provided off-ramp options. In 1914, the Concert of Europe had collapsed after the 1871 Franco-Prussian War, and there was no standing multilateral institution capable of halting mobilization cascades.

4. **The Serbia-Austria dynamic:** Austria-Hungary's ultimatum to Serbia (July 23, 1914) was deliberately designed to be unacceptable — it required Serbia to allow Austrian officials to conduct investigations on Serbian soil, which would have violated Serbian sovereignty. It was a pretext for war, not a genuine diplomatic solution.

**What it means for traders today:**

For the Ferdinand exception to apply to a modern assassination, you need ALL THREE: alliance cascade architecture (rigid mutual defense obligations), automated escalation mechanisms (military mobilization schedules without political override), and no functioning diplomatic off-ramp institution. Modern international architecture makes all three simultaneously extremely unlikely — but not impossible. The NATO Article 5 architecture is the closest modern equivalent to the 1914 alliance system; a deliberate attack on a NATO member by a nuclear-armed state would create exactly the Ferdinand dynamic.

The check: before assuming spike-and-revert, ask whether the event could trigger Article 5 (NATO), Article 4 (ANZUS), or a comparable mandatory multilateral response. If yes, the Ferdinand exception may apply and the position size must account for tail risk that does not mean-revert.

## The Assassination Asset Response Matrix

![Assassination impact matrix by event type and asset class showing oil gold USD EM equities target country and VIX](/imgs/blogs/political-assassination-and-market-reaction-soleimani-khashoggi-5.png)

The matrix crystallizes the differential impact across event types. Several asymmetries stand out:

**Oil is the most reliable spike asset across all assassination types.** Even in the Khashoggi case, where the direct oil market connection was minimal, the broader Middle Eastern uncertainty bid drove some oil premium. Oil's spike-and-revert is sharper and faster than VIX because the supply disruption premium dissipates rapidly once markets confirm no physical supply is threatened.

**Target-country equities almost always fall more than EM peers.** Whether it's the Saudi TASI after Khashoggi, the Egyptian EGX after the Mubarak transition, or the Pakistan PSX after political crises, domestic equity markets of the affected country show sharper and more persistent drawdowns than global EM peers. The country-specific risk premium persists longer because succession uncertainty takes weeks to resolve.

**Alliance leader assassinations (the Ferdinand type) create the most persistent impacts.** The VIX impact may not revert if the cascade signals fire. For these events, the option-buying strategy (buying volatility on day 1) should use longer-dated options — not 2-week expiry but 3-month expiry — because the risk timeline extends.

## Brent Crude Around Political Events: Spike vs. Structural

![Brent crude around political events distinguishing spike-and-revert assassinations from persistent war shocks](/imgs/blogs/political-assassination-and-market-reaction-soleimani-khashoggi-4.png)

The long-term Brent chart distinguishes two types of oil market shocks:

**Spike-and-revert (assassination/political):** The 2020 Soleimani killing, the 2019 Abqaiq attack (Saudi oil facility), the 2014 Mosul fall (ISIS) — all created 3-10% spikes that reverted within 2-4 weeks. In each case, no actual supply was disrupted for more than a few days, and the spike premium dissipated.

**Structural (war/supply):** The 2022 Russia-Ukraine invasion created a persistent +40% oil price increase that lasted 6+ months because actual supply was disrupted (Russia's 10M bbl/day production partially came off the market, Urals crude trading at steep discount). The 1973 OPEC embargo, the 1979 Iranian Revolution, the 1990 Iraq invasion of Kuwait — all structural, not spike-and-revert.

**The discriminating question:** Is any physical supply actually threatened? If yes, size the trade for persistence. If no (Soleimani case), size for reversion.

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "Assassinating a key leader will destabilize the adversary."**
This is a foreign policy assumption that has repeatedly proven false, and markets are right to not price it in. State institutions outlast individuals. The IRGC Quds Force, the Hamas military wing, the Hezbollah command structure — all have survived the deaths of senior leaders. Markets price succession risk (who comes next?) not leadership elimination per se.

**Misconception 2: "The bigger the figure killed, the bigger the market impact."**
Soleimani was arguably more important to Iran's regional strategy than almost any other individual. Yet the market impact was smaller than many analysts predicted. What matters is not the figure's importance but the retaliatory options available to the target state. A North Korean leader's death would have enormous market impact — not because of who they are, but because North Korea has nuclear weapons and unpredictable succession dynamics.

**Misconception 3: "Oil always spikes after Middle East assassinations."**
Oil spiked after Soleimani but barely moved after Khashoggi. The difference: Soleimani was an Iranian military commander in a country that neighbors major oil infrastructure and controls the Strait of Hormuz. Khashoggi was a dissident journalist. Oil's response is specifically about supply disruption risk, not about the geopolitical significance of the target.

**Misconception 4: "The Archduke Ferdinand analogy is always relevant."**
Ferdinand is the exception, cited precisely because it was so different from the hundreds of other assassinations that didn't trigger wars. Modern analysts constantly invoke Ferdinand as a warning when they're actually describing Soleimani or Khashoggi — events with no alliance cascade architecture. The Ferdinand analogy is only valid when you can identify all three conditions: alliance rigidity, mobilization automation, and no diplomatic off-ramp.

**Misconception 5: "Volatility is a good trade on day 1."**
VIX call buying on day 1 of an assassination event is a crowded trade — everyone knows the spike playbook. By the time VIX options markets have reacted, the premium is already elevated. The better entry is:
- **Day 0 (night of event):** Buy crude oil calls (1-2 week expiry), buy gold spot
- **Day 2-3 (if 72-hour signals don't fire):** Sell those calls (capture time value decay), buy S&P exposure
- **Day 5+:** Fade any residual VIX premium by selling options (collecting premium as events normalize)

## The Trading Playbook

#### Worked example:

**Soleimani Trade: Full Construction**

*Day 0 (Jan 3, 2020, pre-Asia open):*
- Buy Brent crude futures at \$68.50 (March 2020 contract): 5 lots × \$10/bbl DV01 equivalent
- At \$70/bbl target: P&L = \$1,500 × 5 = \$7,500 per dollar of move
- Actual overnight high: \$71.75 → P&L on 5 lots: (71.75-68.50) × 1,000 × 5 = \$16,250

*Day 3 (Jan 7, 2020): 72-hour signals checked — all negative:*
- No ally mobilization
- No Hormuz threat
- No economic decoupling
- Iran's rhetoric declining from "severe revenge" to "proportionate response"
- Exit crude long: \$68.80 → lock \$16,250 from peak, exit at approximately \$15,000 gain from entry

*Day 8 (Jan 8, post-Iran missile strike): De-escalation confirmed:*
- Buy S&P 500 futures: 2 contracts at 3,215 (S&P still 0.5% below pre-event level)
- Exit January 17: S&P at 3,330 → gain of 115 points × 2 contracts × \$50/pt = \$11,500

*Total trade P&L:* Approximately \$26,500 on \$500,000 risk capital = +5.3% over 17 days from one geopolitical event.

**Key risk management:** Stop-loss on the crude long at \$73.00 (if oil continued rallying past the initial spike, suggesting structural supply disruption). Stop-loss on S&P long if S&P broke below the initial panic low (suggesting cascade scenario was materializing).

#### Worked example:

**The Cascade Check: Pre-Trade Checklist**

Before any assassination trade, run this checklist (answers for Soleimani in brackets):

1. Does the target's country have nuclear weapons? [NO — Iran has no deployed weapons]
2. Is the target's country party to a mutual defense alliance that is NOW activated? [NO — Iran has no formal mutual defense treaties]
3. Have mobilization orders been issued by any major power? [NO — Russia/China condemned verbally only]
4. Is there a geographic chokepoint (strait, canal, pipeline) that the target's country controls and has threatened? [POSSIBLE — Hormuz, but no threat materialized]
5. Is the attacker country in a position of domestic political instability that makes de-escalation politically difficult? [NO — Trump had political incentive to claim victory, not escalate further]

Score: 0/5 cascade signals → Fade the spike aggressively.

If score is 3/5 or higher: reduce position size by 50%, extend option expiry dates to 3 months, consider that mean-reversion may not occur.

#### Worked example:

**Khashoggi Trade: The Political Survival Framework**

The Khashoggi trade was not an oil trade or a VIX trade — it was a political survival trade. The question: what is the market-implied probability that MBS loses power?

On October 5, 2018 (three days post-murder disclosure), the Saudi TASI had fallen 7% from its pre-murder level.

If MBS survives: TASI returns to pre-crisis level = +7.5% recovery
If MBS falls (political collapse): TASI falls additional 15-25% (Vision 2030 disruption, oil policy uncertainty, capital flight)

Market-implied probability of MBS survival: 
Using EV calculation: Expected return = P(survive) × 7.5% + P(fall) × (-20%)
Setting expected return = 0 (risk-neutral):
0 = P × 7.5% + (1-P) × (-20%)
0 = 7.5P - 20 + 20P
20 = 27.5P
P = 72.7%

Market was pricing a 27% probability of MBS political collapse. The actual probability, given Western governments' economic interests in Saudi stability, was much lower — perhaps 5-10%. This gap between implied probability (27%) and fundamental probability (5-10%) was the trade.

Entry: Long TASI exposure (via GCC equity ETF as proxy) at -7% from pre-crisis level
3-week exit: TASI recovered 4.5% → P&L: +4.5% on notional

The risk: MBS did fall. Position size should reflect this tail risk — not a full position but a 30-40% of normal size, given the genuine uncertainty in the first week.

## How to Build the Assassination Trading Framework Into a Portfolio

**Step 1: Pre-event positioning**

Before major geopolitical events (summit meetings, elections in volatile regions, military operations with assassination potential), build a small tactical "event hedge":
- 0.5-1% of portfolio in Brent crude calls (1-2 month expiry, 5% OTM)
- 0.5-1% of portfolio in gold calls (1 month, 2% OTM)
- These are cheap insurance policies; their collective cost is manageable

**Step 2: Day-0 response**

When an assassination occurs:
- If pre-event hedge is in place, let it run for 24-48 hours
- Add to crude and gold positions if the overnight move has been moderate (<3%) — more spike may come as Asian and European markets open
- Note the opening levels carefully for your fade trade setup

**Step 3: 72-hour evaluation**

Run the cascade checklist. If score is 0-1/5:
- Begin unwinding crude and gold longs (harvest the spike premium)
- Initiate tactical long in S&P 500 or the adversely affected equity market (the target country's adversary's market)
- Sell VIX options (collect premium as it decays)

**Step 4: Day 5-10 confirmation**

If the political landscape has confirmed de-escalation (no mobilization, no economic decoupling, target's state has given its "symbolic" retaliation), the trade is complete. The premium should have dissipated and the equity recovery trade can be closed for profit.

The entire cycle — entry on day 0, exit on day 5-10 — produces asymmetric risk-reward because the downside (assassination triggers actual war) is a genuine tail risk while the base case (spike and revert) is the 95%+ probability outcome over 70+ years of data.
