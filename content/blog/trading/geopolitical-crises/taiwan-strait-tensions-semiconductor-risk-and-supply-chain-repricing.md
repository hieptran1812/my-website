On August 2, 2022, then-Speaker Nancy Pelosi's plane touched down in Taipei. Within 24 hours, the People's Liberation Army launched the largest military exercises around Taiwan in 25 years — live-fire drills, naval encirclement simulations, and ballistic missile tests that flew over the island itself. The Taiwan Stock Exchange (TAIEX) fell 4% in two days. TSMC's American Depositary Receipt dropped from \$85 to roughly \$82. The Philadelphia Semiconductor Index (SOX), a basket of the world's biggest chip companies, fell 3%. And the S&P 500? Down about 0.5% — barely a ripple.

The gap between those two numbers — the 3.5% TSMC move versus the 0.5% S&P move — tells you almost everything about how global markets currently price Taiwan risk. They are pricing it as a spike, not a regime change. A dramatic event with a fast recovery, not the permanent repricing of supply-chain risk that a serious escalation would demand.

Whether that pricing is right — or whether it is a collective blind spot driven by wishful deterrence theory — is the central question of this post.

## Foundations: Semiconductors, Foundries, and Why Geography Matters

Before we can talk about geopolitical risk premiums, we need to understand why a single island creates such an extreme concentration point. The semiconductor supply chain is one of the most complex and geographically specific industrial systems ever built. Let us start from scratch.

### What a semiconductor is

A semiconductor is a material — almost always silicon — that conducts electricity under some conditions but not others. By etching billions of microscopic transistors onto silicon wafers, engineers create *chips* (also called *integrated circuits*) that can process information. Every smartphone, laptop, car, data center server, missile guidance system, and MRI machine runs on chips.

The most important distinction in chips is the *process node* — the size of the transistors measured in nanometers (billionths of a meter). Smaller transistors mean you can fit more of them on a chip, which means more computing power per watt of electricity. The leading-edge nodes today are 3nm and 2nm. For reference, a human hair is about 80,000nm wide. We are talking about structures built at atomic scales.

### What a foundry is

Here is the crucial business model that explains the Taiwan story. Most chip *designers* — companies like Nvidia, Apple, AMD, and Qualcomm — do not own factories. They design the blueprint for a chip, then send that blueprint to a *foundry* — a company that specializes in actually manufacturing chips. This model is called *fabless* (design only) versus *fab* (owns manufacturing).

The foundry business is extraordinarily capital-intensive. A single leading-edge fabrication facility (*fab*) costs \$10-20 billion to build and requires tens of thousands of specialized workers, ultra-pure environments cleaner than an operating room, and equipment so precise it can place atoms. There are only a handful of companies in the world capable of operating leading-edge fabs.

The dominant one — by a margin that has no parallel in any other industry — is Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company, known as TSMC.

### TSMC's market position

TSMC was founded in 1987 by Morris Chang, with backing from the Taiwanese government. The insight: separate chip design from chip manufacturing, let specialists do each. For 37 years, TSMC has accumulated process advantages, workforce expertise, supplier ecosystems, and economies of scale that no competitor has been able to replicate.

The numbers as of 2024:
- TSMC produces 92% of all chips at 5nm or smaller (the most advanced commercial nodes)
- TSMC produces approximately 60% of all advanced logic chips globally across all nodes
- TSMC's revenue in 2024 was approximately \$90 billion
- Apple's A-series and M-series chips: 100% made by TSMC
- Nvidia's H100/H200 AI accelerators: 100% made by TSMC
- AMD's Ryzen and EPYC processors: 100% made by TSMC
- Qualcomm's Snapdragon mobile chips: effectively 100% TSMC
- Broadcom's networking chips: approximately 90% TSMC

This is not just market concentration — it is a *single point of failure* for the global technology supply chain. A six-month outage of TSMC's Taiwan facilities would not just hurt tech stocks. It would halt production of every Apple device, every Nvidia AI system, every AMD server processor, and every Qualcomm-powered smartphone globally. The downstream impact would cascade through automotive, medical equipment, defense systems, and industrial automation.

### Why Taiwan?

TSMC is in Taiwan because Taiwan made a deliberate national industrial policy decision in the 1970s-1980s to build a semiconductor industry, backed by the government's Industrial Technology Research Institute, subsidized land, trained engineers from US graduate schools, and decades of protected investment. TSMC's 55,000 engineers in Hsinchu Science Park, and the supplier ecosystem around them, cannot be replicated in three years. They took three decades to build.

The geographic concentration risk is obvious in hindsight. Taiwan sits roughly 160km (100 miles) off the coast of mainland China. The People's Republic of China has never recognized Taiwan's government as legitimate and has formally committed to eventual "reunification" — by peaceful means if possible, by force if necessary. The Chinese Communist Party has stated repeatedly that the "Taiwan question" must be resolved, and has linked it explicitly to the 100th anniversary of the founding of the People's Republic in 2049.

### The cross-strait balance of power: a primer

The *Taiwan Strait* is the 180km-wide body of water separating mainland China and Taiwan. For decades after the Chinese Civil War (1949), both sides claimed to be the legitimate government of all of China. Today's practical situation: Taiwan operates as a self-governing democracy with its own military, currency, constitution, and international relationships, while China treats it as a renegade province.

The United States has formal commitments to Taiwan's defense under the *Taiwan Relations Act* (1979), which obligates the US to provide Taiwan with arms for self-defense and maintains ambiguity — deliberately — about whether the US would directly intervene militarily if China attacked. This deliberate ambiguity is called *strategic ambiguity*, and it is a feature, not a bug: it deters Taiwan from declaring formal independence (which China says would trigger war) while deterring China from attacking (since it cannot be certain the US would stay out).

Japan, with US military bases on Okinawa fewer than 700km from Taiwan, has its own vital interest: the sea lanes through the Taiwan Strait carry much of Japan's trade. A Chinese blockade of Taiwan would be an economic blockade of Japan by extension.

## The Political Moves: China's Pressure Toolkit

China has a sophisticated toolkit for pressuring Taiwan short of military invasion. Understanding this toolkit is how you read each geopolitical signal for its market implications.

### The military dimension: ADIZ incursions

The most visible escalation metric is China's incursions into Taiwan's Air Defense Identification Zone (ADIZ) — the airspace around Taiwan where Taiwan requires foreign aircraft to identify themselves. China does not recognize Taiwan's ADIZ, but flying military aircraft into it is a deliberate signal.

The trend is stark:
- 2020: approximately 380 ADIZ incursions
- 2021: approximately 960 incursions
- 2022: approximately 1,700+ incursions (more than one year-on-year doubling)
- 2024: the incursions have continued at elevated levels with new tactics including coordinated multi-aircraft formations

Beyond ADIZ flights, China has escalated to live-fire naval exercises in the Taiwan Strait, gray-zone maritime activities (coast guard and fishing vessels asserting Chinese territorial claims), and deliberate rehearsals of a blockade — surrounding Taiwan from multiple directions with naval assets.

The August 2022 exercises following Speaker Pelosi's visit were the largest since 1995-1996. China fired ballistic missiles that flew over Taiwan itself — unprecedented. It deployed naval assets to essentially encircle the island. The exercises were explicitly described as a rehearsal for a blockade.

### The economic pressure channels

Military signaling gets headlines, but China's economic tools are often more consistent:

**Trade restrictions**: China has imposed restrictions on Taiwanese agricultural exports (pineapples, fish, grouper), citing phytosanitary concerns but timed precisely with political statements. These are low-cost signals — small enough that Taiwan absorbs them, but unmistakable in their political messaging.

**Tourism**: Before COVID, approximately 2.7 million mainland Chinese tourists visited Taiwan annually. China has restricted this flow at times of political tension, hurting Taiwan's service economy.

**Investment linkages**: Despite political tensions, Taiwan and China have deep business ties. Taiwan's cumulative investment in mainland China exceeds \$200 billion. Taiwanese manufacturers — especially in electronics — built much of the factory base that produces China's exports. These linkages create both leverage (China can threaten Taiwanese businesses) and deterrence (hurting Taiwanese business also hurts Chinese growth).

**Semiconductor chip imports**: Here is the most powerful economic check on any Chinese action: China itself imports enormous quantities of semiconductors. China imported roughly \$415 billion in semiconductor devices in 2023 — its single largest import category. China's smartphone manufacturers (Huawei, Xiaomi, Oppo), data center operators, and automotive manufacturers all depend on chips, many of which come from Taiwan or from fabs that depend on TSMC-manufactured components.

If China were to attack Taiwan and TSMC's fabs were destroyed or offline, China's own technology industry would be devastated. This creates what analysts call the *silicon shield* — the argument that Taiwan's semiconductor dominance makes it too economically valuable to destroy.

### The diplomatic isolation campaign

China has relentlessly pursued Taiwan's diplomatic isolation. Taiwan now has formal diplomatic relations with only about 12 countries (mostly small Pacific island nations and some Central American states). China has induced dozens of countries to switch recognition from Taiwan to Beijing, most recently Honduras in 2023. It has blocked Taiwan's participation in most UN-affiliated organizations, including the World Health Organization during COVID.

This isolation campaign matters to markets because it affects Taiwan's ability to secure international support in a crisis and signals China's long-term resolve.

## The Financial Channels: How Political Moves Become Market Moves

When a geopolitical move occurs in the Taiwan Strait, it does not affect markets randomly. There are specific transmission mechanisms that connect political signals to asset prices.

### The semiconductor premium channel

The most direct channel is through semiconductor company valuations. Every news event that raises the perceived probability of a Taiwan conflict reduces the expected future cash flows of TSMC, Nvidia, Apple, AMD, and other TSMC-dependent companies. Markets discount this through lower price-to-earnings multiples.

This creates the *Taiwan discount* — a structural reduction in TSMC's valuation multiple relative to comparable US-headquartered chipmakers. As of late 2024, TSMC trades at roughly 20-22x forward earnings. Intel and domestic US equivalents (where applicable) have historically traded at premiums. The discount reflects the geopolitical risk premium embedded in Taiwan-located production.

### The supply-chain repricing channel

A second channel operates through supply-chain stocks and their customers. When Taiwan tensions rise, investors reduce positions in:
- Fabless chip designers (Nvidia, AMD, Qualcomm, Broadcom) — they lose their foundry
- Consumer electronics manufacturers (Apple, Dell, HP) — they lose chip supply
- Hyperscalers buying AI chips (Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Meta) — their AI buildout stalls
- Automotive OEMs — modern cars contain 1,000-3,000 chips

Sectors that benefit in tension spikes include:
- Defense stocks (Lockheed Martin, Raytheon, Northrop Grumman)
- Domestic US chip producers (Intel, though its position is weak)
- CHIPS Act beneficiaries (GlobalFoundries, Micron for domestic capacity)

### The risk-off safe-haven channel

Taiwan tensions, like most geopolitical events, trigger a flight to quality. Gold typically rises 2-3% in the first week. The US dollar and Japanese yen strengthen. US Treasury yields fall (prices rise). Emerging market equities and currencies weaken — the Taiwan Strait is in Asia, and an Asian military conflict raises risk premiums across the region.

The [safe havens and flight-to-quality](/blog/trading/geopolitical-crises/safe-havens-flight-to-quality-and-the-dollar-in-a-crisis) pattern is consistent: gold averages +2.3% over seven days after major geopolitical events, the USD +1.1%, and EM equities -3.8%.

There is a specific Japan dimension worth understanding. Japan's yen (JPY) is traditionally the most reliable safe-haven currency in an Asian geopolitical crisis. Japan has \$1.1 trillion in external assets — the world's largest net international investment position — meaning yen tends to repatriate in a risk-off event. Additionally, Japan's geography makes it directly implicated in any Taiwan scenario: the sea lanes through the Taiwan Strait are Japan's primary trade routes for Middle Eastern oil. A Chinese blockade of Taiwan is, functionally, a blockade of Japanese energy imports too. For this reason, some defense analysts argue Japan would be compelled to respond militarily to any Taiwan blockade, even without explicit US involvement.

South Korea presents a different dynamic. Samsung, the only meaningful competitor to TSMC at advanced process nodes, would theoretically benefit from a Taiwan crisis — customers fleeing TSMC would need alternative foundry capacity. But Korea is simultaneously exposed: Korean exports transit the same sea lanes, Korean companies use TSMC chips, and Korea sits within range of Chinese military retaliation. Korean assets (KOSPI, Korean won) tend to sell off in Taiwan tension events, not rally, because the geographic risk overwhelms the competitive-advantage narrative.

Vietnam, positioned as a key beneficiary of supply-chain diversification away from China since 2018, faces a complex exposure in a Taiwan scenario. Vietnam has benefited enormously from electronics FDI — Samsung, LG, Intel, and dozens of others have built large facilities there. But a Taiwan crisis would disrupt the upstream chip supply that Vietnamese electronics assembly depends on, and would likely cause a broad emerging-market risk-off that hurts Vietnamese equities and currency. The VN-Index would likely fall 10-15% in a blockade scenario, despite Vietnam's longer-term supply-chain positioning story.

## Market Reactions to Taiwan Signals: The Evidence

Let us look at what actually happened in markets during the major Taiwan tension events. The data is instructive about both the size of the reaction and the speed of the recovery.

### The Pelosi visit event study (August 2022)

On August 2, 2022, Speaker Nancy Pelosi became the highest-ranking US official to visit Taiwan in 25 years. The visit was telegraphed in advance, which allowed some pre-positioning. Markets fell modestly on August 2 as she landed. China's military response came August 4-7 with the largest exercises since 1996.

Asset price reactions (approximate, from contemporaneous reporting):
- TAIEX (Taiwan stock market): -4% over two trading days
- TSMC ADR: fell from approximately \$85 to \$82 (-3.5%)
- Philadelphia Semiconductor Index (SOX): -3%
- MSCI Emerging Markets: -2%
- S&P 500: -0.5%
- Gold: +1.8%
- USD: +0.4%

By the end of August 2022 — four weeks later — TSMC ADR was back near \$85. The SOX had recovered. The Taiwan TAIEX was recovering. Investors who bought the Pelosi dip in TSMC made roughly 3.5% in four weeks.

This event study teaches three things. First, the Taiwan geopolitical risk premium is real and quantifiable — it was approximately 3-4% on the most directly exposed asset. Second, it dissipated quickly because investors assigned low probability to escalation beyond exercises. Third, the S&P 500 barely moved, reflecting that US investors see Taiwan primarily as a tail risk, not a permanent discount on US equities.

### The broader TSMC ADR pattern (2021-2024)

The chart below shows TSMC's ADR price from 2021 through 2024. TSMC peaked near \$140 in early 2022, fell dramatically through the year (driven partly by the broad tech selloff and semiconductor inventory correction, not just geopolitics), and then recovered strongly into 2024 as the AI chip demand surge drove TSMC's earnings higher.

![TSMC ADR price 2021-2024 annotated with key geopolitical events and recovery pattern](/imgs/blogs/taiwan-strait-tensions-semiconductor-risk-and-supply-chain-repricing-7.webp)

The key observation: each geopolitical tension spike (Pelosi visit, exercises, US chip export controls) created a dip that was subsequently recovered. Investors who treated Taiwan tension events as buying opportunities in TSMC rather than permanent impairment signals were rewarded.

This recovery pattern is the market's revealed opinion: the probability of a catastrophic Taiwan outcome is low enough that the temporary fear spike is a discount opportunity, not a signal to permanently exit.

Whether that assessment is correct is a different question.

### How does TSMC's valuation compare to US chipmakers?

One of the clearest expressions of the Taiwan risk premium is the gap between TSMC's price-to-earnings (P/E) multiple and comparable US-based chip companies.

A P/E multiple tells you how much investors are willing to pay per dollar of earnings. A higher multiple means investors expect either faster growth or lower risk (or both). When two companies grow at similar rates but one has a geographic risk that the other does not, the riskier one tends to trade at a lower multiple — the difference being the risk premium.

As of late 2024, approximate forward P/E multiples:
- TSMC: approximately 22x forward earnings
- Intel: approximately 25x (though Intel has operational challenges that complicate the comparison)
- GlobalFoundries (US-based, mature nodes): approximately 18x (lower growth, mature nodes)
- Nvidia: approximately 35-40x (AI premium plus TSMC dependency risk — interestingly, Nvidia is priced for growth rather than discounted for Taiwan dependency)

The most striking comparison is the divergence: TSMC — the dominant, highest-quality chipmaker by almost any operational metric — trades at a significant discount to US companies with far worse market positions. The discount reflects the market's ongoing pricing of Taiwan risk into TSMC's multiple. If Taiwan were located in Wisconsin, TSMC's multiple would arguably be 30-35x, suggesting an implied stock price 35-50% higher than current levels.

For an investor who believes deterrence will hold through the relevant investment horizon, this multiple gap is the core investment thesis for owning TSMC over domestic US alternatives.

## The Four Scenarios: What Taiwan Conflict Actually Means for Markets

Geopolitical analysis requires thinking in scenarios, not point forecasts. Taiwan has four distinct risk scenarios, each with different market implications.

### Scenario 1: Status quo / extended pressure campaign

The baseline scenario, and the most likely: China continues its pressure toolkit — elevated ADIZ incursions, periodic exercises, trade restrictions, diplomatic isolation — without crossing into armed conflict. Taiwan's economy continues, TSMC continues operating, and the market prices a structural but modest Taiwan discount into semiconductor stocks.

Market impact: Limited. Occasional spikes of 3-5% in TSMC and SOX that recover within weeks. The Taiwan discount persists as a structural 15-20% valuation discount on TSMC vs. comparable US fabs.

Probability: Most defense analysts assign this 60-75% probability through 2030.

### Scenario 2: Naval blockade

China encircles Taiwan with naval assets and enforces a blockade — stopping ships from delivering goods to or removing goods from Taiwan. This is considered China's most likely aggressive action if it decides to escalate: it avoids the enormous risks of an amphibious invasion, it puts economic pressure on Taiwan and its trading partners, and it is not technically a "war" in the traditional sense.

A blockade would likely last 6-12 months. TSMC's fabs would continue operating — the 55,000 engineers and the equipment are on the island. But finished chips could not be shipped to Apple, Nvidia, or any other customer. Taiwan depends on imports for energy and food — a long blockade would also create humanitarian pressure.

Market impact: Severe. Global advanced chip supply falls 30-40% as TSMC output cannot reach customers. Smartphone, PC, server, automotive, and industrial production crunch. Estimated GDP impact: \$1 trillion globally. S&P 500 falls 15-25%. Nvidia falls 30-50% as AI buildout stalls. Defense stocks surge 20-40%. Gold, USD, treasuries surge. MSCI Asia ex-Japan falls sharply.

Probability: Defense analysts assign this 10-20% probability through 2030.

### Scenario 3: Limited military strike

China strikes specific military targets in Taiwan — radar installations, naval bases, airfields — without a full invasion. This is a high-risk scenario for China because it could trigger US and Japanese military response without achieving reunification. It is more likely as a "coercive" signal before a blockade than as a standalone scenario.

Market impact: 5-15% S&P fall, sharp spike in VIX to 40+, semiconductor stocks fall 15-25%. The key variable is US response — if the US responds militarily, the scenario rapidly evolves.

### Scenario 4: Full invasion

China launches an amphibious invasion to capture Taiwan. This is the tail scenario — the one that would reshape global markets for years. It has not happened in 75 years of cross-strait tension because the military, economic, and diplomatic costs are enormous.

An amphibious invasion requires crossing 160km of ocean against a well-armed defender, facing potential US and Japanese military intervention, enduring global sanctions comparable to or exceeding those on Russia, and then trying to operate TSMC fabs — facilities that require continuous power, ultra-pure water, rare chemicals, and international supply chains — while under international embargo.

TSMC reportedly has protocols to disable its facilities if an invasion occurs — what some analysts call a "silicon scorched earth" option. In reality, TSMC's facilities are so complex that they would likely be degraded even if intact, because the international supplier ecosystem that keeps them running would be disrupted by sanctions.

Market impact: Catastrophic. Global advanced chip supply falls 60-90% for 2-3 years. Global recession. S&P falls 30-50%. The technology sector is effectively halved. Every AI buildout stalls. Automotive production collapses. Defense stocks surge 50-100% but are hard to hold during a global market crash. Gold surges, USD surges as a safe haven, then potentially weakens if US is involved in the conflict and its deficit position is questioned.

Probability: Most defense analysts assign full invasion 5-10% through 2030, rising to 15-20% if the China-Taiwan status quo breaks down significantly.

## Worked Example: The TSMC Outage Scenario NPV

#### Worked example:

Let us compute the full economic footprint of a TSMC six-month outage. This exercise reveals why the Taiwan risk is not priced like a normal corporate risk — the numbers are extraordinarily large relative to TSMC's market capitalization.

**Step 1: Direct TSMC revenue impact**

TSMC's 2024 revenue is approximately \$90 billion per year, or \$7.5 billion per month. A six-month outage means \$45 billion in direct revenue lost to TSMC. TSMC would still incur most of its fixed costs (55,000 employees, facility maintenance, debt service), so the operating income impact would be larger in percentage terms.

**Step 2: Downstream multiplier effect**

Here is where the numbers get startling. For every \$1 of chips that TSMC produces, what is the downstream value of finished goods that depend on those chips?

A heuristic: TSMC's chips go into devices and systems where the chip represents roughly 10-20% of the total product value. A smartphone contains TSMC chips worth roughly \$30-50 (the application processor alone), but the phone sells for \$800-1,200. Servers contain TSMC chips worth \$1,000-5,000 but sell for \$20,000-100,000. AI accelerator systems with TSMC chips worth \$10,000 sit in systems sold for \$100,000+.

Using a conservative multiplier of 5x: \$45B in lost chip revenue becomes \$225B in lost finished goods production.
Using a more realistic multiplier of 8x: \$360B in lost production.
Some estimates go to 10x for the most complex systems: \$450B.

**Step 3: Secondary economic effects**

The lost production triggers layoffs, reduced investment, and confidence effects beyond the direct supply chain. McKinsey Global Institute and several academic papers estimate a 1.5-2x secondary multiplier on direct production losses for severe supply disruptions.

At 1.5x secondary multiplier on \$250B (midpoint direct): secondary impact of \$375B.
Total estimated GDP impact: \$225B direct + \$375B secondary = \$600B minimum.
Upper range: \$450B direct + \$675B secondary = \$1.1 trillion.

**The intuition**: TSMC's market cap is roughly \$700-800 billion (as of 2024). A six-month outage would cause economic damage roughly equal to TSMC's entire market capitalization — spread across the global economy, not just Taiwan. This is why the Taiwan risk premium should arguably be much larger than markets currently price it.

## Worked Example: The Silicon Shield Economic Calculation

#### Worked example:

The "silicon shield" theory argues Taiwan is safe because it is too economically integrated — attacking it hurts China too much. Let us stress-test this with numbers.

**China's economic stake in Taiwan:**
- China's annual imports from Taiwan: approximately \$80B
- The majority are semiconductors and semiconductor components
- China's electronics exports depend heavily on Taiwanese chips and components in the supply chain

**The cost of invasion to China:**

*Immediate semiconductor shock*: China imports \$415B in chips annually. Roughly \$80B comes directly from Taiwan. But the indirect effect is larger — many chips from TSMC go into components that China's assembly factories buy from third parties. A Taiwan-triggered global chip shortage would devastate China's electronics manufacturing sector, which generates roughly \$1.4T in annual export revenue.

*Global sanctions*: If China invaded Taiwan, the G7 response would almost certainly include sanctions analogous to or exceeding the Russia package. Russia's GDP fell roughly 2-3% in 2022 despite high oil revenues. China is far more integrated into the global trading system — its exports are \$3T+ versus Russia's \$500B. Economists estimate China would lose 5-15% of GDP annually under severe sanctions, or \$700B-2T per year.

*Military cost*: An amphibious operation at scale, potentially contested by US and Japanese forces, would cost hundreds of billions and potentially be militarily catastrophic.

**The benefit to China:**
Taiwan's GDP is approximately \$750B (2024). Annexing Taiwan would give China control of TSMC's fabs (though whether they could operate them under sanctions is dubious) and eliminate a long-running political embarrassment.

**NPV calculation:**

Assume a 5% discount rate. The annual "benefit" of controlling Taiwan (in terms of economic output): approximately \$750B at current size, but degraded under sanctions — call it \$300B in sustainable benefit.

NPV of Taiwan (at 5% discount, in perpetuity): \$300B / 0.05 = \$6T.

Annual cost: at minimum \$700B in sanctions GDP impact + \$100B in military costs + \$80B in lost semiconductor imports + cascading domestic manufacturing losses. Call it conservatively \$1T per year.

NPV of cost (at 5%, in perpetuity): \$1T / 0.05 = \$20T.

The economic NPV calculation says invasion destroys \$14T of Chinese wealth to gain \$6T — a deeply negative expected value, even before accounting for military risk. This is the strongest version of the silicon shield argument: not just that Taiwan matters, but that attacking it would be an economic self-immolation for China.

The counterargument: Beijing may not be making a pure economic calculation. If the CCP leadership views Taiwan's integration into the democratic world as an existential threat to its legitimacy — and some analysts argue this is the real driver — then economics become secondary.

## The CHIPS Act and Supply-Chain Insurance

In August 2022 — the same month as the Pelosi visit — President Biden signed the CHIPS and Science Act into law. The timing was not coincidental. The \$52B in subsidies was explicitly framed as reducing America's vulnerability to a Taiwan disruption.

The global response:
- **US CHIPS Act**: \$52B in subsidies; TSMC Arizona (\$40B investment, two fabs), Intel Ohio and Arizona (\$20B each), Samsung Texas (\$25B), Micron Idaho and New York (\$40B+)
- **EU Chips Act**: €43B; TSMC Dresden fab (planned), Intel Germany (planned), STMicroelectronics expansion
- **Japan support**: Approximately ¥2 trillion (\$14B) in subsidies; TSMC Kumamoto fab (\$8.6B, Phase 1 opened 2024), Samsung Japan fab (planned), Micron Hiroshima expansion
- **India**: Approximately \$10B in incentives; Micron assembly and test plant, Tata Electronics fab (planned)

The TSMC Arizona facility is the most watched. Phase 1 (4nm process, 20,000 wafer starts per month) produced its first chips in early 2024. Phase 2 (2nm or 3nm, same capacity) is planned for 2026-2028. Phase 3 (advanced packaging, additional capacity) is also planned.

The political narrative is "onshoring" — bringing chip manufacturing back to the US. The economic reality is more nuanced. These fabs are extraordinarily expensive to build and operate in the US context.

## Worked Example: The CHIPS Act Insurance Premium

#### Worked example:

Is the CHIPS Act economically rational? Let us calculate the "insurance premium" — the extra cost of onshoring versus buying chips from Taiwan at market rates.

**TSMC Arizona Phase 1 economics:**

- Capital cost: approximately \$20B for Phase 1 (the full complex with Phase 2 is \$40B)
- Capacity: 20,000 wafer starts per month at 4nm
- Wafer cost at TSMC Taiwan (4nm): approximately \$17,000-20,000 per wafer (from analyst estimates)
- Target wafer cost at TSMC Arizona: reportedly 30-40% higher, or approximately \$23,000-28,000 per wafer

The premium: approximately \$5,000-8,000 per wafer above Taiwan pricing.

At 20,000 wafers per month: \$100-160M per month extra cost.
Per year: \$1.2-1.9B in higher costs versus Taiwan production.

**Amortizing the capital subsidy:**

The CHIPS Act provides approximately \$5-7B in direct subsidies to TSMC's Arizona fabs. At a 15-year straight-line amortization: approximately \$400-470M per year in subsidy value.

Net insurance premium after subsidy: approximately \$750M-1.5B per year.

**What is this insurance buying?**

In a Taiwan crisis scenario, 20,000 wafers per month of domestic 4nm production would supply roughly:
- Approximately 200-400 million mobile processors per year (if simplified to one per wafer)
- Or approximately 50-100 million laptop-class chips per year
- Or approximately 10-20 million high-end server chips per year

Against total TSMC Taiwan production of approximately 400,000 wafers per month (of which perhaps 40,000-60,000 are at leading nodes), the Arizona capacity is roughly 4-5% of critical supply. It is not "onshoring" in any meaningful supply-chain security sense. It is more accurately described as a proof-of-concept that advanced fabs can be built in the US — and a geopolitical signal to allies and adversaries alike.

**The intuition**: The US is paying roughly \$1B/year in insurance premiums to demonstrate to TSMC, to allies, and to China that it is committed to building domestic chip capacity — and to slightly reduce the catastrophic impact of a Taiwan blockade. Evaluated as pure economic investment, the returns are negative. Evaluated as geopolitical insurance, the cost looks reasonable given what a Taiwan disruption would cost.

## The Semiconductor Supply Chain: Before and After

The diversification push is real, but its timeline and scope are often overstated in political rhetoric. Here is an honest assessment of where the supply chain was in 2020 and where it will realistically be in 2027.

![Semiconductor supply chain comparison 2020 Taiwan-dominant versus 2027 post-CHIPS diversified](/imgs/blogs/taiwan-strait-tensions-semiconductor-risk-and-supply-chain-repricing-6.webp)

The critical constraint is process leadership. Even with massive investment, no US or European fab will produce chips at 3nm or below before approximately 2027-2028 at the earliest (Intel's 18A node, which is competitive with TSMC's 3nm, has faced repeated delays). TSMC's next-generation 2nm and 1.4nm nodes will remain Taiwan-only through at least 2028.

This means that for the next four to six years, any scenario that disrupts TSMC's Taiwan fabs disrupts the supply of the most critical chips in the world — AI accelerators, high-end smartphone processors, and advanced CPUs. The diversification being built now will reduce but not eliminate this vulnerability.

There is also a workforce problem that gets little attention in policy discussions. Advanced chip manufacturing requires an extraordinary concentration of specialized human capital — engineers trained in process chemistry, lithography physics, metrology, and yield optimization. TSMC has 55,000 such engineers in Taiwan, built over decades, supported by Taiwan's university system, technical institutes, and a culture of manufacturing engineering that is deeply embedded. Building a new fab in Arizona or Germany requires transplanting not just equipment but knowledge — the tacit understanding of how to troubleshoot a 3nm process node that only comes from years of running one.

TSMC has publicly acknowledged this: the TSMC Arizona fab initially faced challenges recruiting and retaining engineers with the necessary skills, and some production delays were attributed to workforce issues. TSMC responded by bringing engineers from Taiwan to Arizona for extended training periods — an effective solution, but one that highlights the extent to which US diversification is currently parasitic on Taiwan's human capital base. True independence from Taiwan risk requires building domestic engineering talent pipelines that do not yet exist at scale.

## The Investor Positioning Dilemma

You cannot "hedge" Taiwan invasion risk cleanly. This is one of the reasons markets underweight it.

Consider the positions available to an investor who genuinely believes there is a 15% chance of a Taiwan blockade within five years:

**Position 1: Short TSMC, Nvidia, Apple**

These would fall 30-70% in a blockade. But holding short positions for years through ongoing AI chip demand surges is brutal. Nvidia rose 239% in 2023, primarily on AI demand. A 15% probability of catastrophe is not a reason to be short through a 239% rally.

**Position 2: Long defense stocks**

Lockheed Martin, Raytheon, Northrop Grumman, and L3 Harris would rise sharply in a Taiwan conflict. But defense stocks are priced with some defense premium already, and they are not a pure Taiwan play. They rise in any geopolitical escalation anywhere.

**Position 3: Long gold and Treasury bonds**

Classic safe havens. They work well in the spike scenario (the first week of a conflict). But in a prolonged invasion scenario, inflation from supply chain disruption and US fiscal expansion might erode real returns on treasuries.

**Position 4: Long domestic chip capacity**

Intel, GlobalFoundries, and US-based Micron would benefit from any scenario that reduces TSMC Taiwan dependence. But these companies have their own operational challenges and execution risk.

The honest answer: There is no clean hedge for a catastrophic Taiwan scenario. If it happens, global equities fall 30-50%, commodity markets spike, and almost everything in a standard diversified portfolio falls. The best defense is broad diversification, awareness of the risk, and avoiding concentration in companies that are 100% dependent on TSMC Taiwan with no short-term alternative.

The structural trade for long-term investors: TSMC's valuation discount is real and persistent. If you believe deterrence holds (the 70-75% base case), TSMC is structurally cheap versus comparable US chipmakers. You are being compensated for bearing geopolitical tail risk.

## Worked Example: The Pelosi Visit Trade — Buying the Dip

#### Worked example:

Let us be specific about the "buy the Taiwan dip" trade that history has repeatedly rewarded.

**Setup**: TSMC ADR opens August 2, 2022 at approximately \$85. Over the next two trading days, it falls to approximately \$82 as Pelosi lands and China announces its exercises.

**Entry**: You buy 100 shares of TSMC ADR at \$82.00. Capital deployed: \$8,200.

**Thesis**: The Pelosi visit is a geopolitical spike, not a regime change. TSMC's fabs are still running. Nvidia and Apple are still ordering chips. The most likely outcome is that exercises conclude without escalation, and TSMC resumes its prior price within 4-6 weeks.

**Exit**: TSMC ADR recovers to approximately \$85 by August 31, 2022 (end of month). You sell at \$85.00. Proceeds: \$8,500.

**Return calculation**:
- Gain: \$8,500 - \$8,200 = \$300
- Return: \$300 / \$8,200 = 3.66% in approximately four weeks
- Annualized: 3.66% × (52/4) = approximately 47.6% annualized

**Risk**: If the exercises had escalated into a blockade, TSMC at \$82 could have fallen to \$55-60 (a further -25 to -30% from your entry) as supply-chain fears emerged. Maximum loss scenario: -30% on \$8,200 = -\$2,460.

**Expected value calculation**: If we assign:
- 85% probability of recovery to \$85: gain of \$300
- 10% probability of moderate escalation, price falls to \$70: loss of -\$1,200
- 5% probability of severe escalation, price falls to \$55: loss of -\$2,700

Expected value: (0.85 × \$300) + (0.10 × -\$1,200) + (0.05 × -\$2,700)
= \$255 - \$120 - \$135 = \$0

At these market-implied probabilities, the expected value is approximately zero — which is correct for an efficient market. The trade works if you believe the market is overpricing the risk of escalation in the immediate post-visit period. Historically, it has been.

**The intuition**: The market assigns approximately 15% probability to bad outcomes on tension spikes. If your read of the geopolitical situation gives you a lower probability — say 5% — you have a positive expected value trade. Political reading, not just price-watching, drives the edge.

## The Geopolitical Balance of Deterrence

Why has China not invaded? This is the most important question for understanding the risk premium.

### The military calculus

An amphibious invasion of Taiwan is one of the most complex military operations conceivable. Taiwan is separated from mainland China by 160km of open water — the Taiwan Strait — with significant weather windows limiting operation. Taiwan has spent decades building layered defenses specifically designed to make an amphibious assault costly.

The US military's position matters enormously. The Seventh Fleet, including multiple carrier battle groups, operates in the Western Pacific. Japan hosts approximately 55,000 US troops. Okinawa, home to US Marine Corps and Air Force assets, is approximately 640km from Taiwan. A Chinese invasion would likely trigger US military involvement — either directly or through arms supply and intelligence support — with enormous risk of military defeat or stalemate.

The RAND Corporation and other defense research institutions have published analyses of Taiwan conflict scenarios. The consensus: a Chinese invasion of Taiwan in the 2025-2030 timeframe would be extremely difficult and costly even for China, with real risk of failure. China's window of advantage — if it exists — depends on completing military modernization that is still in progress.

Some analysts cite 2027 (the 100th anniversary of the People's Liberation Army) as a potential deadline. Others cite 2035 as when Chinese military capabilities reach the level needed for a high-confidence invasion. The uncertainty itself is part of the deterrence structure.

### The economic deterrence

As computed in our silicon shield example above, China's economic stake in cross-strait stability is enormous. China imports hundreds of billions in semiconductors; its own electronics manufacturing base depends on chips that flow through Taiwan. The risk of invasion triggering a global sanctions regime analogous to or exceeding Russia's would devastate China's trade-dependent economy.

China's leadership is aware of this. The Russia-Ukraine war was an object lesson: even Russia, with massive commodity revenues, saw its economy significantly damaged by Western sanctions. China's economy is far more integrated into the global trading system and would face correspondingly larger damage.

### The stability-instability paradox

Here is the key strategic tension: the factors that make Taiwan stable in normal times may make escalation more likely over the long run.

The silicon shield creates stability today — China cannot destroy TSMC without destroying its own tech sector. But as CHIPS Act investments gradually reduce the cost of a Taiwan disruption to the US and its allies, the shield weakens. If by 2030 there is meaningful US and Japanese domestic advanced chip capacity, a Taiwan disruption becomes less catastrophic — and China's window to use the silicon shield as protection may close.

This creates a potentially perverse dynamic: the onshoring investments intended to reduce Taiwan risk may reduce the silicon shield's deterrent effect before they provide meaningful supply-chain security. China could perceive a closing window and act before US onshoring completes.

Markets have not priced this dynamic. The working assumption is that onshoring reduces risk. The strategic reality may be more complicated.

## Common Misconceptions

### Misconception 1: "The CHIPS Act will solve the Taiwan chip concentration problem."

The CHIPS Act investments are significant but will not solve the problem within the relevant geopolitical risk window. TSMC Arizona Phase 1 (4nm, 20,000 wafer starts/month) came online in 2024. This is approximately 5% of TSMC's leading-edge Taiwan capacity. TSMC's 3nm and 2nm nodes will remain Taiwan-only through at least 2027-2028. Even by 2030 with full buildout, the US will have perhaps 15-20% of leading-edge capacity, not majority capacity. The concentration risk is reduced, not eliminated, within the relevant strategic timeframe.

### Misconception 2: "TSMC would be fine even if China invaded because the fabs are too valuable to destroy."

This misunderstands how TSMC fabs operate. They are not just factories — they are ecosystems of inputs (ultra-pure chemicals, photomasks, spare parts for ASML machines) that come from around the world via just-in-time supply chains. An invasion would trigger global sanctions that cut off these inputs within weeks, degrading fab performance even if the physical structures were intact. TSMC also reportedly has protocols to disable key fab equipment if an invasion occurs. The fabs are not a prize that would be functional under Chinese control.

### Misconception 3: "China is bluffing — the economic cost is too high for any attack."

This reasoning assumes Chinese leadership makes purely economic calculations. The CCP has signaled repeatedly that Taiwan is a core political interest that supersedes economic cost. Xi Jinping has said reunification is "a historical inevitability." Domestic political legitimacy within China is increasingly tied to Taiwan posture. A leadership that felt its political survival required action on Taiwan might accept significant economic cost. The economic deterrence is real but not absolute.

### Misconception 4: "US strategic ambiguity will deter China."

Strategic ambiguity works as long as China believes the probability of US intervention is high enough. If Chinese military development reaches the point where leaders believe they can win before US intervention is decisive, the ambiguity calculation changes. The US has not formally committed to defend Taiwan with military force — deliberate policy ambiguity means China cannot be certain, but neither can Taiwan. Multiple US presidents have made off-script comments implying a stronger commitment; Chinese analysts track this carefully.

### Misconception 5: "Taiwan tensions are priced in TSMC's discount."

The Taiwan discount on TSMC's P/E multiple is real — roughly 20-25% versus comparable domestic US fabs. But this discount reflects the market's current low probability assignment for severe scenarios. A blockade that cuts global chip supply 30-40% would not cause TSMC to trade at a 25% discount — it would cause TSMC to be uninvestable at almost any price, because the company's entire output cannot reach customers. The current discount prices the "tension spike" scenario, not the "blockade" scenario.

## How It Shows Up in Real Markets: Case Studies

### Case Study 1: The 1995-1996 Taiwan Strait Crisis

The closest historical analogue to a major Taiwan military escalation. In July 1995 and March 1996, China fired ballistic missiles into the waters around Taiwan, conducted large-scale military exercises, and maneuvered naval forces aggressively in the strait — attempting to intimidate Taiwanese voters ahead of the island's first direct presidential election.

The US response was decisive: President Clinton ordered two aircraft carrier battle groups into the region, the largest US naval deployment in Asia since the Vietnam War. China backed down. Taiwan held its election. Lee Teng-hui won in a landslide — the intimidation had backfired, boosting support for the candidate China opposed.

Market reaction: The Taiwan TAIEX fell approximately 15% during the crisis period. US markets were relatively unaffected. The semiconductor industry was far less Taiwan-concentrated in 1996 than it is today — TSMC was still building its dominance. The financial stakes of a modern equivalent crisis would be dramatically higher.

The lesson: US military signaling was decisive in 1996. Whether equivalent signaling would be as effective today — when China's military is dramatically more capable — is the key uncertainty for modern deterrence calculations.

### Case Study 2: The Nvidia China revenue cliff (2022-2023)

In October 2022, the Biden administration announced sweeping restrictions on the export of advanced AI chips to China — specifically targeting Nvidia's A100 and H100 chips. Nvidia's stock fell approximately 12% on the announcement. China represented about 26% of Nvidia's revenue at the time.

Within 18 months, Nvidia's stock had risen 239% — the largest one-year gain for a major technology company in recent history. The China revenue clipped from 26% to approximately 8-9% of total, but AI demand in the rest of the world — from US hyperscalers, European cloud providers, and Asian markets outside China — more than compensated.

The lesson: Tech nationalism (restricting chip exports to China) and Taiwan risk are related but distinct risks. Nvidia proved that being cut off from China was survivable when AI demand outside China was explosive. But this logic breaks down in the Taiwan scenario: in a blockade, it is not Nvidia losing access to China — it is Nvidia losing access to TSMC, meaning Nvidia cannot produce chips for anyone.

### Case Study 3: ASML and the equipment chokepoint

There is one company almost never mentioned in Taiwan risk discussions that deserves more attention: ASML, a Dutch company that produces extreme ultraviolet (EUV) lithography machines — the equipment that enables TSMC to produce chips at 5nm and below. ASML has a monopoly on EUV machines. Without ASML, TSMC cannot produce its most advanced chips.

ASML is already restricted from selling its most advanced machines to China (under Dutch, US, and allied export controls). ASML's stock trades at a significant premium to broader tech — partly because it sits in the Netherlands (low Taiwan geographic risk) and partly because its machines are essential to everyone's fab plans. In any Taiwan scenario, ASML's equipment (which is typically shipped from the Netherlands) would still be able to serve TSMC Arizona or TSMC Japan fabs.

This creates a second chokepoint in the supply chain that markets rarely discuss: even if diversified fabs are built, they all require ASML EUV machines. ASML's monopoly actually reduces some of the concentration risk — the machine supplier is in a politically stable NATO member country.

### Case Study 4: The Huawei chip breakthrough (2023)

In August 2023, Huawei released the Mate 60 Pro smartphone containing a 7nm chip — the most advanced chip produced domestically in China. The chip was made by SMIC (Semiconductor Manufacturing International Corporation), China's leading foundry. Despite years of US export controls intended to prevent China from accessing advanced chipmaking equipment, China had apparently made more progress than US intelligence estimated.

This event was significant for Taiwan risk analysis. It suggested:
1. China's domestic chip capability was advancing faster than officially acknowledged
2. China was willing to accept lower yields and higher costs to develop indigenous capacity
3. US export controls were leaking through indirect channels (second-hand equipment, component reverse-engineering)

For Taiwan, the implication is double-edged. If China develops meaningful domestic chip capacity over 10-15 years, it reduces both the economic deterrent (China needs TSMC less) and its potential economic losses from an invasion. This gradual de-linking of Chinese tech from Taiwan could shift the strategic calculus over time.

## How to Trade It: The Playbook

This section describes how practitioners position around Taiwan tension events, not a recommendation to do so. Markets involve risk; geopolitical events involve uncertainty that models cannot fully capture.

### The tension spike playbook

When a Taiwan tension event occurs (military exercise, political visit, aggressive ADIZ incursion pattern):

**Short-term (1-7 days)**:
- Buy: Gold, USD (DXY), Japanese yen, US Treasuries, defense ETF (ITA, XAR)
- Sell/underweight: TSMC ADR (TSM), SOX ETF (SOXX), technology hardware names, MSCI Asia Pacific ETF

**Medium-term (1-4 weeks), if no escalation**:
- Reverse the above: Sell safe havens as the spike dissipates, buy TSMC and semiconductor names as the market returns to pre-tension levels
- Historical pattern: TSMC recovers within 4-6 weeks of non-escalatory tension events

**Signals that the spike is becoming a regime change** (abandon the reversal):
- China announces sustained naval blockade operations (not just exercises)
- China fires missiles that hit Taiwan territory
- US aircraft carrier groups are ordered to the Taiwan Strait
- Taiwan declares martial law or the government moves to undisclosed locations

### The structural positioning playbook

For longer-horizon investors who want to manage Taiwan risk structurally:

**The diversification trade**: Own TSMC (cheap relative to US fabs) for the base case (deterrence holds), but hedge with Intel and GlobalFoundries exposure for the onshoring scenario. Own ASML for the equipment bottleneck premium regardless of scenario.

**The insurance trade**: Long defense stocks (Lockheed, Raytheon, Northrop) as a structural hedge. These appreciate on any geopolitical tension, not just Taiwan specifically, so they are a broad hedge rather than a pure Taiwan play.

**What to watch**: The most important leading indicators for Taiwan risk escalation are:
- PLA military exercises that include live missile tests (higher signal-to-noise than ADIZ incursions)
- US-Taiwan arms sale announcements and China's response
- Congressional Taiwan policy votes and their passage margin
- China's domestic political dynamics — leadership consolidation increases assertiveness
- TSMC capex guidance — if TSMC significantly increases investment outside Taiwan (AZ, Japan), it signals their own assessment of risk

### What invalidates the deterrence thesis

The base case (deterrence holds) is invalidated by:
1. Major shifts in Chinese military capability assessments by US intelligence (public signals through Congressionally mandated reports)
2. A significant change in US Taiwan policy toward formal security guarantees (would provoke China) or toward withdrawal (would embolden China)
3. Taiwan domestic politics moves toward formal independence declaration (triggers Chinese red line)
4. Major US military setbacks elsewhere that reduce credibility of intervention threats
5. Economic collapse or severe political instability within China that changes the cost-benefit calculus for leadership
