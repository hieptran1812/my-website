> [!important]
> **TL;DR — The Liberation Day Tariff Shock**
>
> On April 2, 2025, President Trump invoked the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) to impose a 10% baseline tariff on all US imports, plus country-specific "reciprocal" tariffs — 46% on Vietnam, 20% on the EU, 24% on Japan, and an escalating series that reached 145% on China by April 11. The S&P 500 fell 10.5% in two sessions — the worst two-day drop since March 2020. US Treasury yields spiked from 4.0% to 4.5% in three days, creating an unusual "sell everything including Treasuries" dynamic that alarmed the White House. On April 9, Trump announced a 90-day pause on all country-specific tariffs except China, triggering a 7.9% single-day rally in equities. The core trade-off: tariffs raise import prices for US consumers and businesses, but Trump is betting the threat forces trading partners to negotiate lower barriers. The bond market — not the stock market — is the real circuit breaker. When yields spike, the \$1.8 trillion annual US deficit becomes materially more expensive to finance, creating political pressure that stock market declines alone do not.

## The Morning Everything Changed

At 4:00 PM on April 2, 2025, in the White House Rose Garden, President Trump stood before a placard reading "Liberation Day" and announced what he called the most significant restructuring of US trade policy since the Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act of 1930. The number that stopped traders cold: a 10% baseline tariff on all imports from every country on earth, effective April 5, plus a second layer of country-specific "reciprocal" tariffs calibrated, in theory, to match what each country charges on US goods.

The immediate reaction was muted — markets had been expecting some tariff action. The S&P 500 closed down 0.9% on April 2. Then traders started running the actual numbers on individual companies, calculating how much their supply chains would cost under the new regime, and the selling began. By the close on April 3, the S&P was down 4.8%. By April 4 it had fallen another 5.5%, for a cumulative two-day decline of 10.5%.

That two-day decline wiped out roughly \$5 trillion in US market capitalization. Global markets followed: European indices fell 6%, Japanese stocks fell 8%, and MSCI Emerging Markets dropped 7%. But what really caught the White House's attention was what happened in the US Treasury market — the supposedly safest asset in the world — where yields *rose* sharply instead of falling, as would normally happen when investors rush to safety during an equity selloff. That anomalous bond market signal is what ultimately forced the April 9 pause.

## Foundations: Trade, Tariffs, and How They Move Markets

Before diving into the mechanics of what happened, it helps to build the conceptual foundation from the ground up. Many of these terms get thrown around as if everyone already knows them — they do not.

### What Is a Tariff?

A tariff is a tax on imported goods, paid at the border by the US importer (the American company buying the foreign product), not by the foreign exporter. This is a crucial distinction that gets mangled in political discourse. When the US imposes a 25% tariff on Chinese steel, a US steel manufacturer importing Chinese steel pays the US Treasury 25% of the declared value at customs. The Chinese steelmaker does not pay anything directly.

What happens next depends on who has more bargaining power. If the US importer cannot easily source the steel elsewhere, the Chinese exporter may be able to keep their price unchanged — the US importer absorbs the tariff cost, which then gets passed to US consumers as higher prices. If there are many alternative suppliers, the Chinese exporter may have to lower their price to keep the business, effectively absorbing some of the tariff themselves. In practice, tariff costs are typically split between the two parties, with the exact split depending on market structure.

### What Is IEEPA?

The International Emergency Economic Powers Act of 1977 gives the US President sweeping authority to regulate international commerce during a "national emergency." Trump invoked IEEPA to justify the Liberation Day tariffs, declaring the US trade deficit itself to be a national emergency. This legal maneuver was significant because it bypassed the normal Congressional process for trade legislation, allowing tariffs to take effect within days rather than years.

IEEPA had been used before for targeted sanctions — freezing individual entities' assets, restricting specific transactions — but using it to impose across-the-board tariffs on all imports from all countries was legally novel. Several legal challenges were filed immediately, but courts have historically given presidents broad deference on matters of national security and foreign commerce.

### What Is the Trade Deficit?

The US trade deficit is the gap between what the US imports (buys from foreigners) and what it exports (sells to foreigners). In 2024, the US imported roughly \$3.3 trillion in goods and exported about \$2.1 trillion — a goods trade deficit of approximately \$1.2 trillion. This sounds alarming, but economists debate whether it is actually a problem.

The mainstream economic view: a trade deficit is not inherently bad. Americans run a trade deficit partly because the US dollar is the world's reserve currency — foreigners want to hold dollars, so they sell us goods and take our currency in return. The capital inflow (foreigners investing in US assets) exactly matches the trade deficit by accounting identity. Running a trade deficit means the US is consuming more than it produces, financed by borrowing — which is sustainable as long as foreigners want to hold dollar-denominated assets.

Trump's view: the trade deficit represents American jobs being sent abroad, manufacturing capacity being hollowed out, and strategic dependencies being created. Even if economists argue the aggregate welfare effects are ambiguous, the distributional effects are real — specific communities that lost manufacturing plants experienced genuine hardship.

### What Is the Supply Chain?

The supply chain for any manufactured product is the sequence of steps that converts raw materials into a finished product in the hands of a consumer. For a smartphone assembled in China, the supply chain might include: lithium mined in Chile → refined in China → battery cell manufactured in South Korea → assembled with screens (South Korea), memory chips (South Korea/Taiwan), camera modules (Japan) → final assembly in Shenzhen → shipped to US distributor → retail store.

Tariffs do not just hit the final assembly step. They hit the declared customs value, which typically reflects the cost of all components and labor. A 145% tariff on a \$550 China-assembled phone means paying an additional \$797 in tax — making the product unviable at normal retail prices unless manufacturers reprice dramatically or relocate production.

### How Tariffs Move Markets

Tariffs affect equity markets through three channels:

**Earnings channel:** Tariffs raise input costs for US companies that import components or finished goods. This directly compresses profit margins unless companies can raise prices to offset. For importers like retailers and tech assemblers, this hits the bottom line immediately.

**Macro channel:** Across the economy, tariffs act like a tax on consumption. If import prices rise by \$300 billion across the board, that is \$300 billion less in consumer spending power elsewhere. This slows GDP growth, which reduces corporate revenue broadly.

**Uncertainty channel:** Even before tariffs take effect, companies delay investment decisions because they do not know what the trade environment will look like six months from now. Capital expenditure freezes, hiring slows, and confidence surveys crater — all of which feed back into slower growth.

Bond markets respond to tariffs through the **inflation channel**: higher import prices show up directly in the Consumer Price Index, especially in goods categories. If the Fed reads this as demand-pull inflation that needs to be fought with rate hikes, bond yields rise. If it reads tariff inflation as a one-time price level adjustment that should be looked through, yields might not react much. The April 2025 reaction — yields rising sharply — suggested bond markets were not willing to look through a 145% tariff escalation.

### Key Terms Reference

- **Basis point (bp):** One-hundredth of a percentage point. A yield rising from 4.0% to 4.5% is a move of 50 basis points.
- **DXY:** The US Dollar Index, which measures the dollar against a basket of six major currencies (EUR, JPY, GBP, CAD, SEK, CHF). A rising DXY means a stronger dollar.
- **MSCI EM:** The MSCI Emerging Markets Index, a benchmark tracking stocks in 24 emerging market countries.
- **10Y yield:** The yield on the 10-year US Treasury bond, the benchmark for long-term borrowing costs globally.
- **VIX:** The CBOE Volatility Index, derived from S&P 500 options prices. Often called the "fear index." Readings above 30 indicate significant market stress.

## The Liberation Day Architecture

The Liberation Day tariff structure had two layers, both announced simultaneously but taking effect at different dates.

**Layer 1: The Universal Baseline.** A flat 10% tariff on imports from every country, effective April 5, 2025. No country was exempt except Canada and Mexico, which remained under the United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement (USMCA) framework that governs North American trade. This was the broadest tariff in US history in terms of geographic coverage.

**Layer 2: Country-Specific Reciprocal Tariffs.** On top of the 10% baseline, each country received an additional "reciprocal" tariff calculated by a formula that ostensibly matched the tariff and non-tariff barriers that country imposes on US exports. In practice, the formula was:

```
Reciprocal Rate = (Trade Deficit with Country) / (Total Imports from Country) / 2
```

The division by two was framed as a "generous" concession. The problem: this formula does not actually measure the barriers foreign countries impose on US goods. It measures the bilateral trade deficit, which is driven by many factors unrelated to trade policy — including the composition of what each country produces, the dollar's reserve currency status, and domestic savings rates.

![Country-by-country Liberation Day tariff rates — China at 145 percent total, Mexico exempt via USMCA](/imgs/blogs/the-2025-trump-tariff-shock-repricing-the-global-supply-chain-2.png)

### China: The Highest Escalation

China's path to 145% was a layered accumulation:
- Pre-2025 baseline: roughly 19-22% (Biden-era tariffs maintaining Trump-era Section 301 rates)
- February 2025: Trump added 10% IEEPA fentanyl-related tariffs → ~29% total
- April 2, Liberation Day: Added 34% "reciprocal" → ~63% total (a 34% add on top of existing rates)
- April 4: After China retaliated with 34% tariffs on US goods, US raised to 104% total
- April 11: After China raised further to 84%, US raised to 125% "reciprocal" + existing fentanyl base = **145% total**

At 145%, the effective tariff exceeds the value of many goods. A widget that costs \$100 to make in China requires the US importer to pay \$145 in tariff on top of the \$100 purchase price. The total delivered cost of \$245 destroys any cost advantage that China's manufacturing had. At these rates, US-China trade in most goods categories becomes economically unviable except for goods with no alternative supply source.

### Vietnam: The Unfortunate Collateral Target

Vietnam received a 46% "reciprocal" tariff — one of the highest of any country — despite being a US ally and having actively positioned itself as an alternative to China-based manufacturing. The formula gave Vietnam a high rate because its bilateral trade surplus with the US (\$123 billion in 2024) is large relative to US exports to Vietnam. Vietnamese officials argued this was structurally unfair: Vietnam runs a surplus because it manufactures electronics and garments for global supply chains, not because it imposes barriers on US goods. Vietnamese tariffs on US goods average roughly 10%, not 46%.

The irony: Vietnam had been a primary beneficiary of the 2018-2020 US-China trade war, receiving massive FDI from companies relocating production from China. Samsung, Intel, LG, and dozens of Apple suppliers had built factories in Vietnam specifically to serve the US market without Chinese supply chain exposure. The 46% Liberation Day tariff threatened to undo this entire diversification playbook overnight.

### The Theoretical Justification

The White House's stated theory was that the threat of sustained high tariffs would bring countries to the negotiating table to reduce their own barriers to US goods. In this framework, the tariffs are a negotiating tool, not a permanent policy. This theory has historical precedent: USMCA itself was negotiated under the threat of tariffs, and the Phase One US-China trade deal of 2020 came after 18 months of escalating tariff pain. Whether the Liberation Day tariffs are a credible negotiating tool depends on whether trading partners believe the US will actually sustain them — which the April 9 pause arguably undermined.

## The Immediate Market Reaction

The scale and speed of the market reaction to Liberation Day was, by most measures, the worst two-day equity decline since the COVID-19 pandemic crash of March 2020.

### Equity Markets

The S&P 500 fell 4.8% on Thursday April 3 (the first full trading day after the April 2 announcement) and another 5.5% on Friday April 4, for a cumulative decline of approximately 10.5% over 36 hours of trading. From its pre-Liberation Day level of roughly 5,633, the index hit an intraday low near 4,982 on April 4 — a drawdown of about \$5 trillion in market capitalization.

![S&P 500 April 2025 — Liberation Day shock, two-day 10.5 percent decline, and April 9 pause rally](/imgs/blogs/the-2025-trump-tariff-shock-repricing-the-global-supply-chain-3.png)

The Nasdaq Composite fell 11% over the same period, weighted down by tech companies with heavy China supply chain exposure (Apple, Qualcomm, Broadcom) and those with China revenue exposure (Nvidia's data center chips, though export controls had already reduced this).

International markets fell in sympathy and sometimes more steeply. The CAC 40 (France) and DAX (Germany) each fell roughly 6%. The Nikkei 225 fell 8% over the same period, hit by both the market shock and the yen's unusual appreciation (normally yen weakens when risk is off, but safe-haven demand was different this time). MSCI Emerging Markets fell 7%, with Vietnam's VN-Index declining nearly 10% from 1,310 in January 2025 to around 1,195 in April — a direct reflection of the 46% tariff announcement targeting the country's exports.

### The Bond Market Anomaly

The most important and least understood market reaction happened in the US Treasury market. In a typical risk-off episode — a geopolitical shock, a recession scare, a financial crisis — investors sell equities and *buy* Treasuries, which drives Treasury prices up and yields down. The 10-year Treasury yield fell on March 11, 2020 (COVID pandemic declared) and fell on September 11, 2001, and fell during the 2008 financial crisis.

After Liberation Day, the opposite happened. The 10-year yield *rose* from approximately 4.0% before the announcement to 4.5% by April 7, a move of 50 basis points in three trading days. This is extraordinary. Bond market volatility (measured by the MOVE index, the bond equivalent of the VIX) hit its highest levels since the March 2020 COVID crisis.

Why did yields rise when they normally fall?

**Inflation fear:** A 10%+ across-the-board tariff is a direct goods price shock. If core PCE inflation, which the Fed targets, rises by 1-2 percentage points due to tariff pass-through, the Fed may need to hike rates rather than cut them. Investors priced in a higher terminal Fed funds rate, pushing all yields up.

**Foreign selling:** Several reports and subsequent market commentary suggested that some foreign holders of US Treasuries — potentially including sovereign wealth funds or central banks — were selling US bonds as a form of financial retaliation or portfolio repositioning. China holds roughly \$760 billion in US Treasuries. Even a modest reduction in holdings can move the market.

**Fiscal deterioration:** Tariffs as a revenue source are projected to raise perhaps \$300-400 billion per year at the Liberation Day rates. But the US deficit is \$1.8 trillion. The bond market may have been pricing in the possibility that tariff-driven economic slowdown reduces tax revenues more than tariff revenues compensate, leaving the fiscal position worse, not better.

### The Dollar Paradox

In a standard risk-off event, the US dollar *strengthens* because global investors rush to dollar-denominated safe assets. After Liberation Day, the dollar *weakened* — the DXY (Dollar Index) fell from approximately 104.2 on April 2 to around 99.5 by mid-May. This was the second anomaly in the market reaction.

The dollar's weakness had several potential explanations. First, if tariffs succeed in reducing the US trade deficit, they reduce the supply of dollars that foreigners earn through trade surpluses, which over time would strengthen the dollar — but in the short run, uncertainty about US economic growth was weighing more. Second, the tariff shock raised fears of US stagflation (slow growth plus inflation), which is historically bad for the dollar because the Fed cannot respond to stagflation with a clean policy prescription. Third, some investors interpreted the tariffs as a signal that the US was retreating from the rules-based trading order that had made dollar assets the global default safe haven — a longer-term structural dollar-bearish view.

### What the Market Was Actually Pricing

Putting it together: equity markets were pricing in a recession. Bond markets were pricing in stagflation risk (inflation preventing the Fed from cutting even as growth slows). The dollar was pricing in a potential erosion of US institutional credibility. None of these are certain outcomes, but the collective market movement was sending a clear signal: the Liberation Day tariffs, if sustained, posed a severe macroeconomic risk that the US had not faced from trade policy since the 1930s.

## The 90-Day Pause and the Tariff Put

On April 9, 2025, at approximately 1:18 PM Eastern Time, Trump posted to Truth Social: "Based on the fact that more than 75 Countries have called Representatives of the United States, including the Departments of Commerce, Treasury, and the USTR, to negotiate a solution to the subjects being discussed relative to Trade, Trade Barriers, Tariffs, Currency Manipulation, and Non Monetary Tariffs, and that these Countries have not, at my strong suggestion, retaliated in any way, shape, or form against the United States, I have authorized a 90 day PAUSE..."

The S&P 500 rose 7.9% in the remaining 90 minutes of trading that day — one of the largest single-afternoon moves in US market history.

### The Terms of the Pause

The 90-day pause applied to all country-specific reciprocal tariffs for all countries *except China*. The 10% universal baseline remained in effect for all countries. China's tariffs were simultaneously raised further, eventually reaching 145%. The pause gave 70+ countries time to negotiate bilateral trade agreements with the US, with the implicit promise that successful negotiators would see their reciprocal tariff reduced or eliminated.

The asymmetry between China (tariff escalation) and everyone else (pause plus negotiations) was deliberate. It framed the conflict as specifically about China, not a general US retreat from global trade. This allowed countries like Japan, the EU, South Korea, and Vietnam to simultaneously negotiate with the US while distancing themselves from China's position.

### The "Tariff Put" Thesis

A "put" in options trading is a contract that gives the holder the right to sell at a specified price — it puts a floor under losses. Market strategists quickly coined the term "tariff put" to describe the observable pattern: Trump relieves tariff pressure when markets fall sharply enough, effectively putting a floor under equity market declines.

The thesis is based on several data points. In September 2019, Trump paused tariff escalation against China after the S&P fell about 7%. In December 2018, after the market fell 20%, trade tensions eased. After Liberation Day, the pause came when the S&P was down about 10.5% from recent highs. The implied "put strike" — the drawdown level that triggers relief — appears to be roughly 8-10%.

![The tariff put circuit — yield spike forces pause, markets rally, next cycle begins at higher floor](/imgs/blogs/the-2025-trump-tariff-shock-repricing-the-global-supply-chain-5.png)

### The Bond Market as the Real Trigger

Market participants debating the tariff put often focus on equity market levels, but the April 9 evidence suggests the bond market is the more direct trigger. The pause came after Treasury yields had risen 50 basis points in three days, not simply because the S&P was down 10%.

The reason: US government finances are more directly sensitive to bond yields than to equity prices. The US carries a \$1.8 trillion annual deficit, requiring it to issue enormous quantities of new debt each year. When 10-year yields rise 50 basis points, the interest cost on that new debt rises materially. For the administration's agenda of extending the 2017 tax cuts, a bond market revolt that drove yields to 5.5% or higher would be fiscally catastrophic — making the tax cuts more expensive to finance at the exact moment they were supposed to be politically delivered.

Trump has historically demonstrated sensitivity to the bond market in a way he has not always shown for equities. His first-term trade war pauses often came with bond market moves cited by insiders as key inputs. The Liberation Day pause reinforced this pattern.

### Limits of the Tariff Put

The tariff put thesis has important limits. First, it is not guaranteed: if Trump were willing to accept a recession in pursuit of his trade policy goals, the put would not be exercised. Second, the put does not prevent gradual escalation — each cycle resets at a higher tariff floor (the 10% baseline stayed, China tariffs remained at 145%). Third, the put is self-defeating if markets price it in: if investors believe every 10% decline triggers relief, they buy the dip rather than selling, which means smaller declines trigger less panic, and Trump has less pressure to relieve. Fourth, if tariff-driven inflation forces the Fed to hike rather than cut, the Fed itself becomes a headwind to equity markets that the tariff put cannot offset.

## China's Escalation — 125% and Counting

China's response to Liberation Day was calibrated to be painful but not immediately catastrophic — a measured counter-escalation designed to demonstrate resolve while keeping the door open for negotiation.

### The Escalation Ladder

On April 4, the day the S&P 500 fell 5.5%, China announced 34% tariffs on all US goods — matching the "reciprocal" tariff rate Trump had assigned to China. This was effective immediately. Chinese authorities also placed 28 US companies on export control lists, restricting their ability to import certain Chinese goods, and added 11 US companies to an "unreliable entity list" that could restrict their operations in China.

On April 11, after the US raised China tariffs to 125% (on top of existing fentanyl tariffs, reaching 145%), China raised its tariffs on US goods to 125% — the matching number that preserved symbolic parity at a level where both sides had effectively cut off normal trade.

### Beyond Tariffs: China's Non-Tariff Weapons

China deployed several non-tariff measures that carried significant strategic weight:

**Rare earth export controls.** China announced expanded export licensing requirements for rare earth elements and processed rare earth products. China controls approximately 85% of global rare earth processing capacity. Rare earths are essential for EV motors, defense systems, wind turbines, and consumer electronics. Even a partial restriction on rare earth exports would create severe supply shortages for US and European manufacturers.

**Boeing delivery suspensions.** China instructed its airlines to halt taking delivery of Boeing aircraft ordered and partially paid for — a direct hit to one of the US's largest exporters. Boeing has significant China exposure; the suspension threatened to push aircraft deliveries to Airbus and Chinese-built COMAC aircraft instead.

**Tourism and investment signal.** China issued travel advisories warning Chinese citizens about safety in the US, and Chinese regulatory agencies began scrutinizing pending approvals for US companies operating in China — a signal that the business environment for US firms in China was deteriorating.

### The Trade Math at 145%/125%

At 145% US tariffs on Chinese goods and 125% Chinese tariffs on US goods, bilateral trade in most product categories becomes economically unviable. Here is what happens to a sample supply chain:

A Chinese factory makes a product for \$100. It would normally sell to a US importer for \$130 (30% markup). With a 145% tariff, the importer pays \$130 + \$188.50 tariff = \$318.50 total cost. Unless the product has no substitute anywhere on earth, the importer will find an alternative supplier in Vietnam, Mexico, Taiwan, or elsewhere — even at a higher factory price — because the tariff-inclusive cost from China is prohibitive.

This is what "near-embargo" means in practice. It is not a legal ban on trade; it is an effective economic ban, because the prices make trade unworkable. The effect is similar to the US and Soviet Union's near-complete trade isolation during the Cold War.

## Country Negotiations — Who Got What

The 90-day pause opened parallel negotiations with 70+ countries. The framework: each country needed to demonstrate it was reducing its own barriers to US goods in exchange for a reduction or elimination of its reciprocal tariff.

### United Kingdom: Fastest to a Framework

The UK moved fastest, leveraging the "special relationship" and the fact that the US runs a goods trade surplus with the UK (the UK buys more from the US than it sells). A framework agreement was announced within weeks of the pause, keeping US tariffs on UK goods near the 10% baseline level while the UK agreed to expand market access for US agricultural products, reduce non-tariff barriers to US services, and cooperate on AI and semiconductor supply chain security.

### Japan: Leverage Through Auto Dependency

Japan's 24% reciprocal tariff threatened some of the world's most profitable automobile supply chains. Japanese automakers — Toyota, Honda, Nissan, Mazda — have large US production facilities and employ hundreds of thousands of American workers, giving them political cover. Japan's negotiators focused on reducing the automobile tariff (the 25% "section 232" auto tariff that had been separate from Liberation Day) in exchange for expanded US agricultural access (Japan agreed to accept more US beef, pork, and corn) and a commitment on currency policy.

### European Union: Hardball and Digital Services

The EU's 20% reciprocal tariff threatened major European exporters: German cars, French wines and spirits, Italian fashion, pharmaceutical products. The EU's negotiating position was complicated by internal divisions — Germany wanted to protect auto exports, France wanted to protect agriculture, smaller members worried about spillover effects. The EU also threatened counter-tariffs and a digital services tax targeting US tech platforms (Google, Meta, Amazon) if talks broke down — a credible threat given Europe's large consumer market.

### Vietnam: The Urgent Case

Vietnam had the most to lose in percentage-of-GDP terms. Vietnam exports approximately \$127 billion per year to the United States (2024 data), representing nearly 30% of Vietnam's GDP. A sustained 46% tariff on these exports would be devastating to the country's economic model, which is built on export-led manufacturing growth.

Vietnamese officials flew to Washington within days of the Liberation Day announcement. Their negotiating offer: agree to reduce Vietnam's own tariffs on US goods (Vietnam's average tariff is roughly 10%, but some categories are higher), increase purchases of US agricultural products, and cooperate on preventing "tariff evasion" — Chinese goods rerouted through Vietnam with minimal processing to gain Vietnam's lower tariff rate. This last point was the key concession the US actually wanted: the White House suspected that Vietnamese trade surplus figures were inflated by Chinese companies using Vietnam as a pass-through.

### India: The Long Game

India received a 26% reciprocal tariff, which was high but below Vietnam and some Southeast Asian peers. India's negotiations moved slowly because India has historically been reluctant to open its markets quickly and because the Modi government has its own domestic manufacturing ambitions (Make in India) that it did not want to compromise. The strategic overlay: India is a key US partner in the Indo-Pacific security architecture vis-a-vis China, which gave India more negotiating leverage than pure trade economics would suggest.

## The Supply Chain Repricing Playbook

Liberation Day set in motion a fundamental repricing of global manufacturing geography. When a 145% tariff makes China-based production unviable for the US market, companies face a forced choice: absorb the cost, pass it to consumers, or restructure their supply chains. Supply chain restructuring takes years and billions of dollars of capital investment — which means the pain is front-loaded and the benefits (if any) come later.

![Global supply chain shift — China-centric before Liberation Day, diversified after](/imgs/blogs/the-2025-trump-tariff-shock-repricing-the-global-supply-chain-6.png)

### Winners

**Vietnam (conditional):** If Vietnam successfully negotiates its 46% tariff down to a lower rate (ideally the 10% baseline), it becomes the primary beneficiary of US-China trade decoupling. Samsung, Intel, LG, and Apple suppliers already have factories there. FDI to Vietnam had been rising steadily since the first US-China trade war in 2018, reaching \$25 billion in 2024. A favorable negotiated outcome could see that jump further as companies look to build out Vietnam manufacturing as a China alternative.

**Mexico (strong):** Mexico is exempt from Liberation Day tariffs under USMCA. This gives Mexican manufacturing a massive cost advantage over Chinese manufacturing for the US market — not just the tariff delta but the entire chain of customs, compliance, and delivery speed advantages of a neighboring country. Near-shoring to Mexico was already happening; Liberation Day accelerated it dramatically. Auto parts, electronics assembly, and appliance manufacturing are the primary sectors.

**US domestic manufacturing (policy tailwind, slow to materialize):** The tariff structure is explicitly designed to make imported goods more expensive relative to US-made alternatives. This creates an economic incentive for domestic manufacturing — but building factories takes 2-5 years, and the manufacturing labor force needed to staff them takes years to develop. The short-run beneficiaries of tariff protection are existing US steel, aluminum, and discrete manufacturing sectors. The long-run picture depends on whether the tariffs are sustained long enough to justify the investment.

**Defense and aerospace (indirect):** US defense contractors are largely insulated from import tariffs (they use domestic supply chains for most critical components) and benefit from increased defense spending that typically accompanies periods of geopolitical tension.

### Losers

**US retailers:** Walmart, Target, Amazon, and specialty retailers import heavily from China. Their entire cost structure and consumer price proposition depends on the efficiency gains of global supply chains. At 145% tariffs, they face either margin compression (absorbing costs) or price increases (passing to consumers) or supply disruptions (while sourcing alternatives). All three outcomes are bad for their earnings. Walmart warned shortly after Liberation Day that price increases on some categories were inevitable.

**Technology supply chains:** Apple, Qualcomm, Broadcom, and scores of other technology companies have deep China manufacturing dependencies that cannot be restructured quickly. Apple assembles most of its iPhones in China (approximately 90% of production capacity) through Foxconn and Pegatron. Even if Apple wanted to move production overnight, there is no alternative ecosystem with the same scale and capability.

**US agricultural exporters:** China was the largest or second-largest market for many US agricultural exports: soybeans, pork, sorghum, cotton. At 125% tariffs on US agricultural goods entering China, Chinese buyers will simply source elsewhere — Brazilian soybeans, European pork. US farmers lose a market they spent decades developing.

**Global shipping:** The uncertainty created by tariff escalation caused companies to front-load imports (buy before tariffs hit) and then stop ordering, creating a boom-bust pattern in freight volumes that was highly destructive for shipping companies' revenue visibility.

### The US Trade Deficit by Country

Understanding who gets targeted requires understanding the bilateral trade deficits that drove the Liberation Day formula.

![US trade deficit by country in 2024 — China largest at 295 billion, Mexico third despite USMCA exemption](/imgs/blogs/the-2025-trump-tariff-shock-repricing-the-global-supply-chain-4.png)

China's \$295 billion deficit is by far the largest. The EU's \$208 billion and Mexico's \$171 billion are next. Vietnam's \$123 billion deficit is enormous relative to the size of its economy — which is why it received such a high tariff rate despite being a small country. Notably, Mexico has a large deficit but is exempt due to USMCA, illustrating that the tariff formula was not purely mechanically applied — political and treaty relationships matter.

## Common Misconceptions

### "Tariffs are paid by China / the exporting country"

No. Tariffs are paid by the US importer at the US border. The question of who ultimately bears the cost (importer vs. exporter through price negotiation) depends on market structure. Studies of the 2018-2020 US-China trade war found that Chinese exporters reduced their prices modestly but the majority of the tariff cost was borne by US importers and consumers.

### "The trade deficit proves the US is being ripped off"

A bilateral trade deficit does not mean unfair treatment. The US runs deficits with countries that have low barriers (like Canada) and with countries that have high barriers (like China). The deficit is primarily driven by savings rates, the dollar's reserve currency role, and industrial structure — not just tariff rates. Germany runs surpluses with virtually everyone because Germans save more than they invest domestically and therefore export capital; that is a structural fact, not evidence of cheating.

### "Manufacturing will return to the US quickly"

Factory construction takes years. Equipment lead times can be 18 months or more. The manufacturing workforce for complex electronics assembly does not exist at scale in the US. Apple's CEO Tim Cook noted in 2017 that Foxconn in Zhengzhou employed 350,000 people on a single campus — building an equivalent workforce capability in the US is a multi-decade project, not a multi-year one. Short-run "tariff protection" benefits existing domestic producers; long-run domestic capacity expansion is a separate and much harder challenge.

### "The 90-day pause means the tariffs are going away"

The 10% baseline tariff on all imports stayed in effect even after the pause. China's 145% tariff stayed in effect. The pause merely froze the country-specific reciprocal tariffs while negotiations proceeded. The floor of the tariff regime — 10% on everything — is meaningfully higher than the pre-Liberation Day average tariff on most goods.

### "Bond yields rising after the tariff shock means no recession"

Rising yields in this context reflect inflation fears and foreign selling, not optimism about growth. In a stagflationary scenario (high inflation + slow growth), yields can rise even as the economy deteriorates. The 1970s stagflation saw Treasury yields hitting double digits while economic growth was negative. The Liberation Day yield spike was not a green light; it was a warning signal of a particularly difficult macroeconomic scenario.

### "Tariffs will raise enough revenue to close the deficit"

At Liberation Day rates — a 10% baseline on all imports plus higher country-specific rates — the theoretical annual tariff revenue would be roughly \$400-\$500 billion per year, based on the roughly \$3.3 trillion in annual US imports. This sounds significant, but it misses two critical dynamics. First, higher tariffs reduce import volumes (that is partly the point), which directly reduces the revenue base. At a 50% tariff, US imports might fall 25-30%, meaning actual revenue is closer to \$300-\$350 billion. Second, tariff-driven economic slowdown reduces income tax revenues, capital gains revenues, and corporate tax revenues — potentially by more than the tariff revenue raised. The Congressional Budget Office's preliminary assessments of sustained Liberation Day tariffs estimated a net fiscal deterioration, not improvement, over a 10-year window.

### "A weaker dollar helps US exporters enough to offset tariff damage"

The dollar weakened after Liberation Day (DXY falling from 104.2 to ~99.5), which theoretically makes US exports more competitive by reducing their price in foreign currencies. This is true, but the math is dwarfed by the tariff impact. A 5% dollar depreciation makes US goods 5% cheaper for foreign buyers — helpful, but modest. A 125% Chinese retaliatory tariff on US agricultural goods makes those goods 125% more expensive for Chinese buyers — catastrophic for the specific US exporters affected. The currency effect is a rounding error relative to the tariff effect in the most exposed sectors.

### "Supply chains will diversify quickly and the tariff pain is temporary"

The most seductive misconception. Corporate executives naturally discuss supply chain diversification as soon as tariffs are announced, which creates a narrative of rapid adaptation. The reality: building a new factory requires identifying a location, negotiating with local government, constructing the building (12-18 months minimum), ordering specialized equipment (lead times of 12-24 months for precision manufacturing equipment), hiring and training workers (6-12 months to build a competent assembly workforce), and qualifying the new facility with customers (3-6 months of testing and audit). End-to-end: 3-5 years minimum for a greenfield facility, and that assumes no regulatory or permitting delays. During those years, the tariff cost is real and ongoing. The adaptation arrives years after the pain.

## How It Shows Up in Real Markets

### Case Study 1: Apple's Extraordinary Exposure

Apple is the most visible case study of Liberation Day's supply chain impact. As of April 2025, approximately 90% of Apple's iPhone production capacity was located in China (through Foxconn's Zhengzhou facility) and a secondary share in India (which was being ramped as a hedge). Apple's China-based manufacturing serves both its own product needs and reflects the deeply integrated manufacturing ecosystem — the tooling, the skilled labor, the component suppliers — that has been built in China over 20 years.

The immediate question investors asked on April 3-4: if 145% tariffs are sustained, what happens to Apple's earnings? Apple's stock fell over 20% in the two weeks after Liberation Day before partially recovering on the pause.

#### Worked example:
**The iPhone Tariff Math**

An iPhone 16 Pro has a retail price of \$1,199 and a bill of materials (manufacturing cost) estimated at approximately \$550, of which roughly \$400 represents components and assembly work performed in China (the remaining \$150 represents components sourced globally: TSMC chips from Taiwan, OLED screens from South Korea, etc.).

Under a 145% tariff, US Customs assesses the tariff on the declared import value (approximately \$550 total manufacturing cost as declared at the border):

\$550 declared value × 145% tariff rate = \$797.50 tariff

New total landed cost: \$550 + \$797.50 = \$1,347.50 (versus \$550 before Liberation Day)

**Scenario A — Apple passes through 50% of incremental cost:**
Incremental tariff cost to pass through = \$797.50 × 50% = \$398.75
New retail price: \$1,199 + \$398.75 ≈ \$1,598 (a 33% retail price increase)

**Scenario B — Apple absorbs the full tariff:**
New gross margin = (\$1,199 revenue - \$1,347.50 cost) / \$1,199 = **-12.4%**
Versus pre-tariff gross margin of approximately (\$1,199 - \$550) / \$1,199 = **54.1%**

Neither scenario is viable. Apple's total China-related revenue exposure (products sold in China) is approximately 19% of total revenue, or roughly \$73 billion per year. But the manufacturing exposure is larger — it affects the cost structure of all iPhones sold globally, not just those sold in China. If Apple's blended gross margin on hardware falls from 36% to 15%, the earnings impact is approximately \$35-40 billion per year — a catastrophic outcome for a company that earned about \$94 billion in net income in FY2024.

This is why Apple stock fell so dramatically and why Apple almost immediately began briefing analysts on its plans to accelerate India manufacturing — where a 26% tariff applies, a fraction of China's 145%.

### Case Study 2: The Bond Market Circuit Breaker

#### Worked example:
**Why 50 Basis Points in Three Days Changes the Political Calculation**

The US government deficit for fiscal year 2025 is approximately \$1.8 trillion. To finance this, the Treasury must issue new debt — selling Treasury bills, notes, and bonds to investors globally. In a given year, the Treasury issues over \$2 trillion in new debt (some of this is refinancing maturing old debt, not just covering the current year deficit).

When the 10-year Treasury yield rose 50 basis points (from 4.0% to 4.5%) in three days after Liberation Day, what does that mean in dollar terms for the US government?

**Annual interest cost calculation:**

Additional interest cost on \$2T of new debt = \$2,000,000,000,000 × 0.50% = \$10,000,000,000

That is \$10 billion per year in additional interest, on just the new debt issued in a single year. Over 10 years, if the rate stays 50bps higher and the debt rolls over:

Cumulative additional interest cost over 10 years ≈ **\$100 billion**

For context: \$100 billion is roughly equal to the entire US State Department budget for three years, or the entire SNAP (food stamp) program for a year.

But this is just one year's issuance. The US has \$36+ trillion in total outstanding debt, of which roughly \$8-9 trillion matures and needs to be refinanced each year. If 50bps becomes the new structural yield level across the entire refinancing:

Annual refinancing exposure ≈ \$8.5T × 0.50% = **\$42.5 billion per year more in interest**

This is why bond market moves are not theoretical — they translate directly into federal budget constraints in dollar terms. A 50bps rise in yields makes the 2017 tax cut extension materially more expensive to sustain. The White House understands this math. The April 9 pause came after three days of rising yields, not three days of falling stocks.

### Case Study 3: Estimating the Tariff Put Strike Price

#### Worked example:
**Quantifying the "Tariff Put" and its Risk-Adjusted Return**

Based on the observable pattern across Trump trade war episodes, the implied "put strike" — the equity drawdown that triggers tariff relief — appears to be approximately 8-10% from recent highs.

Evidence:
- September 2019 tariff pause: S&P was down approximately 7% from its July highs when Trump announced a delay in the next round of China tariffs.
- Liberation Day April 9, 2025 pause: S&P was down approximately 10.5% from its pre-Liberation Day level.
- Average: roughly 8-9% drawdown triggers a pause signal.

**A simple tariff-put trade:**

Hypothetical trade: buy S&P 500 futures when the index is down 8% from recent highs; target exit at the announcement of a tariff pause (typically +5-7% on the announcement day) or at break-even if no pause within 30 days.

Entry: S&P at 8% drawdown from 5,633 = approximately 5,183
Target exit: S&P +7% from entry on pause announcement = approximately 5,546 (+7%)
Stop-loss: S&P at -5% further from entry (if no pause signal and decline continues) = approximately 4,924

Expected value calculation (rough, illustrative):
- Probability of pause within 30 days: ~60% (based on 3 historical episodes, 2 of 3 resolved quickly)
- Return if pause: +7% = +\$7 per \$100 at risk
- Return if no pause, stop hit: -5% = -\$5 per \$100 at risk
- Probability-weighted EV: (0.60 × \$7) + (0.40 × -\$5) = \$4.20 - \$2.00 = **+\$2.20 per \$100 at risk**

Positive expected value does not mean it is risk-free. The key risk: a tariff pause that does not materialize because the administration believes markets are pricing in inevitable relief (making it less painful to hold the line). The second risk: the pause is temporary and the next escalation creates an immediate new drawdown before your position can be fully exited.

### Case Study 4: Vietnam's GDP at Risk

#### Worked example:
**Vietnam's 46% Tariff Exposure — Quantifying the GDP Impact**

Vietnam's exports to the United States totaled approximately \$127 billion in 2024 (annualized), making the US Vietnam's largest single export destination. Vietnamese GDP in 2024 was approximately \$430 billion, meaning US-bound exports represent roughly 29.5% of the entire economy.

Electronics and electronic components are the dominant category: approximately 38% of US-bound exports, or roughly \$48 billion, consists of Samsung phones, LG appliances, Intel CPUs (assembled in Vietnam), and Apple iPhone components.

**Tariff cost impact on US importers:**

US importers of Vietnamese goods currently pay approximately 10% in existing tariffs. A jump to 46% means:

Additional tariff rate = 46% - 10% = 36 percentage points higher
Additional tariff cost on \$127B of imports = \$127B × 36% = **\$45.7 billion more per year**

This is the additional cost borne by US importers. The pass-through question: how much of this do Vietnamese exporters absorb (price reductions) versus US consumers (price increases)?

Assume a 60% pass-through to US consumers and 40% price reduction by Vietnamese exporters.

Vietnamese exporter price reduction = \$45.7B × 40% = \$18.3B
Revenue loss to Vietnamese exporters = \$18.3B per year

But it gets worse. At 46% tariffs, some importers switch to alternative suppliers (Mexico, Indonesia, Bangladesh), meaning volumes fall, not just prices.

Assume 25% volume reduction in Vietnamese exports to US:
Lost export volume = \$127B × 25% = **\$31.75 billion per year**

Combined effect: price compression (\$18.3B) + volume loss (\$31.75B) = roughly \$50B in Vietnamese export revenue erosion out of a \$430B economy.

**Impact on GDP growth:**
Vietnam's export multiplier (manufacturing supply chains employ many workers who spend locally) is approximately 1.4. Export revenue loss of \$50B × 1.4 multiplier effect / \$430B GDP ≈ **16% of GDP shock.**

This does not mean Vietnam's GDP falls 16% — exports do not disappear overnight, companies keep factories running while seeking alternatives, and the 90-day pause provided some breathing room. But the potential downside if tariffs are sustained is GDP growth falling from 7% (2024 trajectory) to perhaps 3-4%, a severe blow to an economy that had been one of the world's fastest-growing.

This is why the VN-Index fell nearly 10% on Liberation Day and why Vietnamese trade negotiators were in Washington within 48 hours.

## How to Trade It — The Playbook

Trading a tariff shock regime requires a framework that accounts for the escalation-pause cycle, the asymmetry between winners and losers, and the multiple macro tail risks.

### The Core Framework: Escalation-Pause Cycle Positioning

**Signal 1 — Escalation incoming:** Watch for White House rhetoric around trade partner "unfairness," Congressional hearings on trade deficits, or section 301/IEEPA investigations being opened. These precede tariff announcements by 2-8 weeks. Pre-position by reducing exposure to China supply chain stocks (Apple, Qualcomm, Broadcom) and US retailers, increasing cash or defensive allocation.

**Signal 2 — Tariff put activation:** When the S&P has fallen 8-10% from recent highs AND 10-year Treasury yields have risen 30+ basis points in less than two weeks, the historical pattern suggests tariff relief is becoming more likely. This is the moment to consider adding risk — equity calls, Treasury puts, relief-rally positioning.

**Signal 3 — Pause announced:** The announcement day rally (+5-10%) happens fast. Most of the return is captured in the first two hours. Positioning needs to be in place before the announcement. After the initial rally, wait for the 2-3 day giveback before reassessing whether the relief is structural or temporary.

**Position invalidation:** If the S&P declines past 15% without a policy response, and bond yields continue rising, the tariff put thesis is wrong for this cycle. Exit risk positions and reassess — either the administration has higher pain tolerance than previous episodes, or the bond market/fiscal dynamics have shifted in a way that makes the calculus different.

### Sector Playbook

**Overweight in a tariff shock regime:**
- US steel and aluminum (direct protection from import competition)
- Defense and aerospace (government contracts, domestic supply chains)
- Mexico exposure (near-shoring beneficiaries — Mexican manufacturing ETFs)
- Domestic consumer staples (less import exposure, pricing power)
- Energy (tariffs reduce global demand growth for oil, but domestic producers benefit from reduced import competition for refined products)

**Underweight:**
- China supply chain dependent tech (Apple, Qualcomm, Broadcom, Nvidia indirectly)
- Mass-market US retailers (Walmart, Target, dollar stores)
- Global shipping (volume disruption, route confusion)
- Companies with significant China revenue exposure (not just China manufacturing — luxury brands, casinos, software companies operating in China)

**Special situation — Vietnam conditional long:**
Vietnamese equities (VN-Index) are a directional bet on the negotiation outcome. If Vietnam successfully negotiates its tariff down to 10-15%, Vietnamese manufacturing equities surge. If tariffs stay at 46%, Vietnamese export-dependent companies are in serious trouble. This is a binary-ish trade where the negotiation outcome is the key variable. Entry at the Liberation Day dip (VN-Index around 1,195 in April 2025) offered a compelling risk-reward if you had conviction in the negotiation outcome.

### The Macro Overlay

Tariffs in 2025 interact with several pre-existing macro conditions:

**Fed policy:** The Fed had been in a gradual cutting cycle entering 2025. Tariff-driven inflation forces the Fed to pause cuts, which removes a key equity market tailwind. If tariff inflation is severe enough, the Fed may need to hike — a dramatically negative scenario for equities. Watch CPI goods components (the tariff impact channel) and the Fed's forward guidance language carefully.

**US deficit:** At \$1.8 trillion annually, any yield spike has immediate fiscal consequences. This creates a natural governor on how far the administration can push tariff escalation before the bond market forced response. The higher the starting deficit, the lower the "pain threshold" yield level that triggers political reaction.

**Dollar trajectory:** A weakening dollar (DXY falling from 109 in January 2025 to 99 by mid-2025) makes imports more expensive in dollar terms — amplifying tariff effects. It also signals that capital flows are shifting away from the US, which is a structural headwind if sustained.

### Further Signals to Watch

1. **US-China trade volume data (monthly):** A sustained decline in US imports from China (showing up in Census Bureau monthly trade data with a 30-45 day lag) confirms tariffs are biting. A decline of 30%+ within 3 months of sustained tariffs would indicate near-embargo conditions.

2. **Vietnam FDI announcements:** Major announcements of factory construction in Vietnam by Apple suppliers, Samsung, or US tech firms signal the market believes Vietnam's tariff will be negotiated down. Each announcement is a directional bet on the outcome.

3. **10-year Treasury yield vs. 2-year:** The yield curve shape tells you whether the market believes the Fed will fight tariff inflation (flat/inverted curve) or look through it (steepening curve). A sharply steepening curve (long yields rise more than short yields) signals inflation risk is the dominant concern.

4. **Chinese rare earth export data:** Any movement toward actual rare earth export restrictions from China would be a severe supply shock for Western manufacturing. Monthly Chinese customs data on rare earth exports is the leading indicator.

5. **US CPI goods components (monthly):** The "core goods" component of CPI is the most direct tariff impact channel. Watch for month-over-month jumps in apparel, furniture, electronics, and appliances — these categories have the highest China import intensity and will show tariff pass-through first, typically with a 2-4 month lag after tariffs take effect.

6. **Corporate earnings guidance revisions:** Each quarterly earnings season, executives from import-heavy companies will either raise or lower earnings guidance based on tariff cost assumptions. A wave of guidance cuts (Apple, Walmart, Best Buy, Nike, Gap, etc.) is a leading indicator of broader earnings deterioration that has not yet shown up in consensus estimates.

7. **Freight and logistics data (Freightos Baltic Index, US containerized freight rates):** Tariff announcements cause immediate shifts in freight patterns. A spike in rates on Vietnam-to-US routes signals importers switching destinations. A collapse in China-to-US rates signals order cancellations. Freight data leads economic data by 4-8 weeks.

### Managing Risk in the Tariff Shock Regime

The tariff shock regime introduces several sources of risk that do not exist in normal market conditions:

**Policy reversal risk:** Positions built on tariff escalation can be unwound in minutes by a tweet or announcement. Keeping position sizes moderate and maintaining stop-losses is essential — the April 9 rally of 7.9% in 90 minutes can be completely ruinous if you are short into it.

**Sequencing risk:** The escalation-pause cycle means the macro backdrop is constantly shifting. A position that is correct for the escalation phase (short China supply chain stocks) can lose money in the pause phase (relief rally) even if your long-run thesis is right. Managing through the cycle requires conviction on timing, not just direction.

**Tail risk — unexpected escalation:** The April 2 Liberation Day announcement itself was a tail risk that most scenario analyses had not adequately priced. A single announcement moved markets 10% in 48 hours. Maintaining some optionality (through options rather than pure delta-one positions, or through reduced position sizing) protects against the next unexpected escalation.

**Inflation-rate feedback loop risk:** If tariff-driven inflation forces the Fed to hike rather than cut in 2025-2026, the entire equity valuation framework shifts. A 25bp Fed hike that the market is not expecting can undo weeks of tariff-driven rally. This is the scenario where both the tariff put and the Fed put are simultaneously off the table — the most dangerous outcome for equity longs.
