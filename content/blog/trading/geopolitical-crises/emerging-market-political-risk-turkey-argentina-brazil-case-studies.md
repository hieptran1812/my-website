On August 12, 2019, Argentine stocks crashed 38% in a single trading session — the second-largest single-day collapse in any major stock market in history. The trigger was not a hurricane, a war, or a bank run. It was a primary election result: Alberto Fernandez, a left-wing Peronist, beat incumbent reformer Mauricio Macri by 16 percentage points in a preliminary vote nobody thought would be that decisive. Investors processed the result and decided it meant Argentina would default on its sovereign debt — again. They were right. Nine months later, Argentina formally restructured \$65 billion in bonds, its ninth sovereign default on record.

That moment crystallizes everything you need to understand about emerging market political risk. In developed markets, elections produce policy tweaks. In emerging markets, they can produce debt defaults, currency collapses, and central bank purges. The financial stakes of political outcomes are orders of magnitude higher because institutions are weaker, foreign debt loads are larger, and central bank independence is a political choice rather than a constitutional guarantee. A single cabinet reshuffle in Ankara can wipe out a third of the currency's value. A spreadsheet error in Buenos Aires fiscal targets can push sovereign spreads past 4,000 basis points — a level where the market is pricing in certain default.

This post builds three case study templates from Turkey, Argentina, and Brazil. Each teaches a different facet of EM political risk. Turkey shows what happens when a leader decides that orthodox monetary policy is ideologically wrong and fires whoever disagrees. Argentina shows the arc of perpetual political instability — from reform hope to populist spending to default to shock therapy — and how each transition reprices assets. Brazil shows a relatively more institutionalized EM where elections still move markets 10–20% because fiscal credibility is contested. Together they give you a complete toolkit: what to watch, how to measure it, and how to position around it.

## Foundations: What Political Risk Actually Is in Emerging Markets

Before diving into specific crises, let us build the conceptual foundation. "Political risk" is a catchall that finance professionals use to describe any situation where government decisions — rather than pure economic forces — drive asset prices. But that definition covers too much. To be useful for trading and portfolio management, we need to split it into three distinct types.

**Type 1: Central bank independence risk.** This is what Turkey exhibits in its most extreme form. A central bank is supposed to set interest rates based on economic conditions — primarily inflation and growth. When a political leader can fire the central bank governor for raising rates (because higher rates slow growth and make the leader unpopular), monetary policy becomes a political instrument rather than an economic one. The market immediately prices this in: if the central bank cannot fight inflation credibly, investors demand a higher return to hold the country's currency and bonds. That demand manifests as currency weakness and higher bond yields.

**Type 2: Fiscal sustainability risk.** Argentina is the canonical example. A government can spend beyond its means for years if foreign investors keep lending. Eventually, the math stops working: the debt load becomes too large relative to export revenues, the currency becomes overvalued, and the moment investors decide to stop rolling over debt, a crisis is inevitable. Political risk here is about whether the next government will run primary surpluses (revenue minus non-interest spending) large enough to make debt service credible. That's why EMBI+ spreads — the extra yield investors demand to hold Argentine bonds vs US Treasuries — track Argentine politics so closely.

**Type 3: Electoral credibility risk.** Brazil is a middle case. Its institutions are stronger than Turkey's or Argentina's: the central bank has had formal independence since 2021, and Brazil has not defaulted on foreign debt since the 1980s Brady bond restructuring. But elections still move Brazilian assets 10–20% because investors disagree strongly about which party will manage fiscal policy more credibly. The Bolsonaro vs. Lula dynamic in 2022 was essentially a referendum on whether Brazil would maintain a spending cap framework (Teto dos Gastos) that had imposed fiscal discipline since 2016.

The three types share a common transmission mechanism: **credibility**. Emerging market assets — bonds, equities, and especially currencies — trade at a premium or discount to developed market equivalents based on how much credibility the market assigns to the institutions managing them. Political events that raise credibility (Simsek's appointment in Turkey, Milei's primary surplus in Argentina) compress spreads and strengthen currencies. Events that destroy credibility (Erdogan firing governors, Fernandez's spending surge) widen spreads and weaken currencies.

Think of a country's institutional credibility as a credit score. In developed markets, the credit score barely changes from year to year — the Fed chair can be replaced and markets barely notice because the institution is bigger than the individual. In emerging markets, the "credit score" is fragile. A single political decision can cut it in half, and rebuilding it takes years of demonstrated discipline. That fragility is what makes EM political risk simultaneously dangerous and tradeable.

**The vocabulary you need.** Before going further, let us define the key terms:

*EMBI+* (Emerging Markets Bond Index Plus): A JPMorgan index measuring the spread — the extra yield — that sovereign bonds from developing countries offer above equivalent US Treasury bonds. When EMBI+ spreads for a country are 500 bps (5 percentage points), the market requires 5% extra annual return to hold that country's debt vs risk-free Treasuries. Higher spread = more perceived credit risk = more stress.

*Currency peg vs. float*: A currency peg ties the exchange rate to another currency (usually the dollar) at a fixed rate. A float lets the rate move based on supply and demand. Argentina has oscillated between pegs and floats. Pegs create a political temptation: they can suppress inflation optics short-term but build up enormous pressure that eventually snaps.

*Parallel exchange rate / "blue dollar"*: When a country has strict capital controls — limits on how much foreign exchange citizens can buy — a black market rate emerges. In Argentina for years, the "official" peso/dollar rate was the government-controlled rate, while the "blue dollar" (parallel market rate) reflected what people actually paid to convert pesos to dollars. The gap between official and blue rate is a real-time measure of market distrust of the official rate. At its peak under Fernandez, the blue dollar traded at twice the official rate.

*Primary surplus / fiscal balance*: The primary surplus is government revenue minus government spending, excluding interest payments on existing debt. It is the number that tells you whether a government can service its debt from its own tax collection (primary surplus) or needs to keep borrowing to pay interest (primary deficit). Markets watch this obsessively in high-debt EMs.

## Turkey: Three Governors, Three Crashes, One U-Turn

The Turkish lira story from 2018 to 2023 is the cleanest available case study in what happens when executive power overrides monetary policy. To understand it, start with Erdogan's stated economic philosophy: he genuinely believes that high interest rates cause inflation, rather than cure it. This is the opposite of conventional economic theory and the opposite of how every major central bank operates. For Erdogan, lowering interest rates would increase investment and production, lowering prices. When his central bank governors disagreed and raised rates to fight inflation, he fired them.

Between 2019 and 2021, Erdogan went through three central bank governors in less than two years. Each firing had the same structure: the governor raised interest rates to fight rising inflation, the lira briefly stabilized or rallied, and then Erdogan fired them and appointed a more compliant successor who cut rates. The market repeated the same response each time: sell the lira, sell Turkish bonds, buy safety.

**The 2018 crisis: the opening act.** Turkey's lira was already under pressure in early 2018 from rising US interest rates (a stronger dollar always pressures EM currencies that have dollar-denominated debt) and a large current account deficit. The lira had moved from around 3.50/USD to 4.55/USD by mid-June 2018. Then in August 2018, a combination of US sanctions (related to a detained American pastor) and a dollar rally triggered a full panic: the lira collapsed from 4.55 to 7.24 in weeks — a 37% drop in eight weeks. The Erdogan government responded by raising rates sharply and eventually appointing his son-in-law Berat Albayrak as Finance Minister, which briefly calmed markets.

**The 2019-2020 cycle.** Murat Cetinkaya, the central bank governor who oversaw the 2018 emergency rate hike, held rates high through 2019. The lira recovered to around 5.30/USD. But Erdogan wanted rate cuts to stimulate the economy before local elections. In July 2019, he fired Cetinkaya — who had refused to cut — and appointed Murat Uysal. Uysal began cutting rates. Inflation remained above 10% while the central bank cut. The lira drifted down.

In November 2020, Uysal surprised markets with an unexpected rate hike to 15% to stop another lira selloff. Erdogan fired him days later and appointed Naci Agbal. Agbal hiked rates aggressively, taking the benchmark rate to 19% by March 2021. The lira rallied sharply. Markets cheered: here was a credible governor willing to fight inflation with orthodox tools.

Then on March 20, 2021 — a Saturday night, with Turkish markets closed — Erdogan fired Agbal too, citing his "wrong path" of rate hikes. Markets opened Monday. The lira crashed 15% in minutes. It was the single sharpest daily lira move since 2001. Agbal had lasted exactly 133 days.

**The December 2021 catastrophe.** The replacement governor, Sahap Kavcioglu, did exactly what Erdogan wanted: cut rates even as inflation accelerated past 20%, then 50%, then toward 85% in 2022. The result was an unprecedented currency crisis for a country that was not in formal default. Between November and December 2021, the lira fell from 10/USD to 18/USD — halving its value in six weeks. The government introduced a currency-protected deposit scheme (KKM) that guaranteed lira deposits against devaluation loss at taxpayer expense, which provided temporary stability but stored enormous fiscal risk.

By the eve of the May 2023 elections, the lira had drifted to around 19–20/USD, artificially suppressed by KKM and interventions that burned through foreign exchange reserves. Inflation was officially 85% (and estimated even higher by independent measures). Turkey had essentially run an economy where the central bank's interest rate was 8.5% while inflation was 85% — a real interest rate of negative 76%. For comparison, no other economy of significant size had maintained a real rate that deeply negative for that duration in modern history.

#### Worked example: The cost of holding lira through the Agbal firing

Imagine you are a foreign fund manager holding Turkish 10-year government bonds in March 2021, just before the Agbal firing. The bond yields 17%. That sounds attractive — Turkish bonds pay 17% annually. But here is what the firing did to your position in two trading days:

Before firing (March 19, 2021): lira at 7.20/USD, bond yield 17%, bond price at par (100).

After firing (March 22, 2021): lira at 8.30/USD — a 13% drop in the currency. Bond yields spike to 20% as markets price in higher inflation risk — meaning bond prices fall from 100 to roughly 88 (a 10-year bond loses approximately 7 points of price for every 1% yield increase, so 3% yield increase = roughly 21 points of price loss on a 10-year, but with Turkish duration slightly shorter given high yield, call it 12 points of price decline).

Your position: you earned zero days of coupon (the weekend). You lost 13% on currency and roughly 12% on bond price. In two trading days, a 17%-yield bond cost you 25% of your principal. The 17% annual yield you sought would take more than 18 months to recover that loss — assuming the lira stabilized (it did not).

This is why EM bond yields must be analyzed in total return (coupon plus currency plus price) rather than just the headline yield number. A 17% yield is negative total return if the currency is collapsing.

![Turkey 10-year bond yield showing heterodox period and orthodox recovery](/imgs/blogs/emerging-market-political-risk-turkey-argentina-brazil-case-studies-2.png)

**The 2023 U-turn: what orthodoxy costs and buys.** Erdogan won re-election in May 2023 (narrowly, requiring a runoff) and then executed one of the sharpest policy reversals in EM history. He appointed Mehmet Simsek — a former Merrill Lynch economist and ex-finance minister with market credibility — as Finance Minister, and Hafize Gaye Erkan (later replaced by Fatih Karahan) as central bank governor. Simsek announced a return to "rational policies" and the central bank began hiking aggressively: the benchmark rate went from 8.5% to 40% over several months in 2023.

The lira continued to weaken in the short term — from 20 to 27/USD, then to 32/USD — as rate normalization meant the old KKM subsidies were unwound and the real exchange rate was allowed to adjust. But by 2024, Turkish 10-year bond yields peaked at 40% and began falling as international investors returned, inflation started responding to tight money, and the current account stabilized. The EMBI+ spread for Turkey compressed meaningfully. The lira stabilized, floating around 32–37/USD instead of collapsing weekly.

The policy lesson is clear: *credibility has a price.* Restoring it after Erdogan's heterodox years required accepting a controlled lira weakening and a period of very high real interest rates. The market's reward was spread compression and equity inflows. The political economy challenge remains: Erdogan's electoral base includes many small businesses and consumers who are hurt by high interest rates. The U-turn is durable only as long as Erdogan tolerates it.

#### Worked example: The Simsek credibility trade

A practitioner who positioned for the Turkey orthodox U-turn in June 2023 could have bought Turkish dollar-denominated bonds (Eurobonds, which are not affected by lira depreciation). A Turkish 5-year dollar Eurobond in June 2023 yielded around 9–10%. As spreads compressed through 2024 — from roughly 400 bps to 200 bps above equivalent US Treasuries — the bond's price appreciated by approximately 8–10 points (2% spread compression on a 5-year duration means roughly 10 points of price gain). Adding 10% coupon income over the period, total return was approximately 18–20% in under 18 months in hard currency. The signal to enter was Simsek's appointment combined with the central bank's first oversized hike — classic credibility restoration markers.

## Argentina: The Perpetual Cycle of Hope, Crisis, and Default

Argentina is the world's most studied case of EM political risk because it has defaulted on sovereign debt nine times and has oscillated between market-friendly and populist governments so regularly that each transition produces a textbook-quality repricing event. Unlike Turkey, where the story is about one leader's idiosyncratic theory of monetary policy, Argentina's story is about structural political economy: a wealthy country with a chronically dysfunctional fiscal relationship between the state and society that makes sustained reform nearly impossible.

To understand Argentina's market behavior, you need to grasp one structural fact: Argentina has had chronically high inflation for decades — often 30–80% annually — because successive governments financed spending by printing pesos. This is not stupidity; it reflects deep political economy. The constituency for spending (unions, pensioners, subsidized industries, provincial governments) is large and organized. The constituency for fiscal discipline (export industries, bond creditors, upper-middle-class savers) is smaller and less politically decisive. The result is a cycle: market-friendly governments promise reform and attract investment; populist governments spend freely, print money, and eventually face a crisis; the crisis leads to default or devaluation; the next market-friendly government inherits chaos and tries to reform again.

**The Kirchner-to-Macri transition (2015): reform euphoria.** Cristina Fernandez de Kirchner governed from 2007 to 2015 with a mix of capital controls, export taxes, nationalized industries, and confrontational battles with bond creditors (she refused to pay holdout creditors from the 2001 default, calling them "vulture funds"). By late 2015, Argentina was in a quiet financial siege: no access to international capital markets, a large official-vs-parallel peso gap, official reserves nearly exhausted.

Mauricio Macri won the November 2015 election on a platform of ending capital controls, settling with holdout creditors, and returning Argentina to international capital markets. The market reaction was immediate: the Merval in USD terms rallied from roughly 680 to 950 within months of his election. EMBI+ spreads fell from above 600 to the high 300s as Argentina successfully raised \$16 billion in new sovereign bonds in April 2016 — the first international bond issuance since the 2001 default.

The Macri experiment worked for two years. By October 2017 midterm elections (which Macri's party won), the Merval had reached 1,400 in USD terms, spreads were below 400, and Argentina seemed on a sustainable reform trajectory.

**The 2018 crisis: IMF deal and the first crack.** In early 2018, rising US interest rates caused global investors to reduce EM exposure. Argentina, with a large current account deficit and significant short-term dollar debt, was particularly vulnerable. The peso came under pressure. By mid-2018, Argentina was in a full currency crisis: the peso fell from 20 to 38 per dollar in a matter of months. The government negotiated a \$57 billion IMF deal — the largest in IMF history at the time — and implemented severe austerity. The Merval crashed in USD terms. EMBI+ spreads climbed back above 700.

**The August 2019 PASO shock: the defining EM political risk event of the decade.** Argentina's "PASO" system holds mandatory primary elections before the main vote. On August 11, 2019, the PASO results came in: Fernandez beat Macri 47–32%, a margin far larger than any poll had suggested. The next morning, August 12, 2019:

- The Merval in USD terms fell 38% in a single session — from roughly 1,000 to 620.
- Argentine dollar-denominated bonds fell 20–35 points in price.
- EMBI+ spreads went from approximately 800 bps to 2,300 bps in one trading day.
- The peso fell from 45 to 60 per dollar — a 25% devaluation in hours.

To put this in context: the 2008 financial crisis, the worst global financial event in 80 years, caused the S&P 500 to fall 38% over six months. Argentina lost that much in one day because investors re-evaluated the probability of default from "possible" to "almost certain" based on one election result.

The reasoning was simple: Macri was implementing IMF austerity. Fernandez (whose vice presidential running mate was former president Kirchner) had campaigned on reversing it. If Fernandez won, Argentina would almost certainly not be able to maintain the primary surplus the IMF required. Without those surpluses, debt service becomes impossible on a \$65 billion debt load. The market priced in expected losses immediately.

#### Worked example: Sizing the PASO loss on Argentine bonds

Suppose a hedge fund held \$10 million in Argentine 2028 dollar bonds yielding 12% on August 11, 2019 (the day before PASO results). Bond price at close: approximately 92 cents on the dollar. Position market value: \$9.2 million.

August 12 open: bonds trading at approximately 60 cents on the dollar (roughly 35% price decline on the day, in line with published market data from Bloomberg). Position market value: \$6.0 million.

One-day mark-to-market loss: \$3.2 million on a \$10 million notional position — a 32% loss in a single session. At no point during the holding period had default actually occurred. The loss came purely from repricing of political probability. The lesson: in EM, the repricing of political risk happens before the event — often in a single session — not gradually over months.

**The Fernandez era: default, restructuring, and the blue dollar gap.** Fernandez won the October 2019 election and took office in December. By May 2020 — coinciding with COVID lockdowns that had crushed the economy — Argentina defaulted for the ninth time, this time on its IMF obligations and then on its international bonds. EMBI+ spreads hit 4,400 bps — a level where bonds trade as deeply distressed (cents on the dollar) rather than as interest-rate instruments.

A restructuring agreement was eventually reached in September 2020, extending maturities and reducing coupons. Spreads fell from 4,400 to roughly 1,350 bps post-restructuring. But the underlying fiscal path did not improve: the government funded large transfers through money printing, keeping official rates negative in real terms and maintaining capital controls. The result was a widening gap between the official peso rate and the "blue dollar" parallel rate. At its widest in 2022–2023, the blue dollar was trading at roughly double the official rate — meaning if you held pesos, you were losing roughly 50% of your real value relative to what the uncontrolled market said your pesos were worth.

**Milei's shock therapy: the 2023 election and what followed.** Javier Milei, a libertarian economist turned politician, won the November 2023 runoff election with 56% of the vote on a platform of dollarizing the economy, eliminating multiple government ministries, and achieving a zero-deficit primary balance immediately. Markets responded before the election: EMBI+ spreads began falling from the October 2023 level of around 2,100 bps as polls shifted toward Milei. On election night, spreads fell further to around 1,560 bps. The Merval in USD jumped approximately 40% in the days following Milei's victory.

On his first day in office (December 10, 2023), Economy Minister Luis Caputo announced: a 50% devaluation of the official peso (from 400 to 800 per dollar), elimination of most public subsidies, cancellation of government contracts and public works projects, and a commitment to an immediate primary surplus. The short-term pain was intense: monthly inflation hit 25% in December 2023 and January 2024 as suppressed prices were unlocked. Real wages fell sharply. The economy contracted.

But the financial response was remarkable. By mid-2024, monthly inflation had fallen from 25% to below 5%. The EMBI+ spread compressed from 1,560 to below 900 bps. The Merval in USD rose from approximately 820 at election to 1,150 six months later — a 40% gain — and continued rising to 2,100 by year-end 2024, roughly +75% from election day over six months, matching the brief in this post's topic. Argentina signed a new \$14 billion IMF extended fund facility in January 2024.

![Argentina Merval USD index across political transitions](/imgs/blogs/emerging-market-political-risk-turkey-argentina-brazil-case-studies-4.png)

**The critical question: is Milei's adjustment durable?** This is the key risk for any investor holding Argentine assets. Milei's fiscal adjustment — the deepest in Argentine peacetime history — has produced real results in terms of spreads and equity performance. But the political economy constraints that caused Argentina's previous nine defaults have not vanished. The primary surplus is achieved partly through spending cuts that have reduced real incomes for the poorest Argentines. The parallel rate gap has narrowed but capital controls remain partially in place. Full dollarization (Milei's original platform) has not been implemented because Argentina lacks sufficient dollar reserves to back a full conversion at a viable rate. If a political backlash produces a reversal — either under Milei himself (if he softens to win midterms) or under a future administration — the cycle begins again.

This is the structural EM political risk trap: reforms are painful enough in the short term that they generate political opposition, which eventually produces a reversal. Investors who understand this cycle know to size positions for the next inflection point — watching primary surplus data monthly, reserve accumulation rates, and political coalition stability as the leading indicators.

## Brazil: Elections as Real-Time Fiscal Credibility Referendums

Brazil occupies a middle position in the EM political risk spectrum. It is far more institutionally developed than Argentina and has not experienced Turkey's central bank capture. Its central bank gained formal operational independence in 2021. Its currency — the Brazilian real (BRL) — has floated freely since 1999. Its sovereign debt is predominantly domestic-currency-denominated, which means Brazil cannot "run out of reais" the way Argentina can run out of dollars.

Yet Brazil's elections move assets 10–20% on result day, and spread widening of 150–200 bps in the 60 days before elections is common. Why? Because Brazil's fiscal credibility is genuinely contested between its two main political blocs.

**The structural fiscal fault line.** Since Brazil's mid-1990s economic stabilization (the Plano Real), the country has managed relatively orthodox monetary policy but has struggled with a public sector that consumes roughly 40% of GDP. The Teto dos Gastos (expenditure ceiling), introduced in 2016 under Temer, was the critical institutional tool: it capped real government spending growth, forcing future governments to make tradeoffs rather than always spending more. When Bolsonaro respected (mostly) the spending cap and when the Lula government proposed to replace it, markets repriced the risk that Brazil's debt trajectory would deteriorate.

This is not ideological. Markets care about numbers: primary surplus or deficit, debt/GDP trajectory, real interest rates. Bolsonaro's government, despite being associated with the political right, spent heavily during COVID (Auxilio Emergencial cash transfers were massive) and pushed fiscal spending ahead of the 2022 election. Lula's government, despite association with the left, initially maintained a fiscal framework. The market's concern was not which party won but what their revealed fiscal behavior would be relative to expectations.

**The 2018 election: Bolsonaro and the reform rally.** The October 2018 election pitted Bolsonaro (right-wing, pro-market on fiscal matters, backed by Chicago-trained economist Paulo Guedes as finance minister designate) against Fernando Haddad of the Workers' Party (PT). Throughout the campaign, as polls shifted toward Bolsonaro, Brazilian assets rallied. The BOVESPA rose from roughly 76,000 in early September to 90,000 by election day — approximately 18% in six weeks driven largely by political repricing. On election night (October 28), after Bolsonaro won the runoff with 55% of the vote, the BOVESPA opened the next Monday up another 3–4%. The BRL strengthened from 4.00 to 3.72 per dollar over the post-election period.

What did the market see in Bolsonaro's win? Primarily: pension reform. Brazil's pension system was the single largest driver of its deficit — an extremely generous system by international standards. The market expected a right-wing government to push pension reform through Congress. It did: the Reforma da Previdência passed in late 2019, raising retirement ages and reducing projected future pension spending by an estimated R\$800 billion over ten years. The BOVESPA hit all-time highs in early 2020 (before COVID) in recognition.

**The 2022 election: Lula and the spending concern.** The 2022 election featured a different context. The Bolsonaro government had become fiscally less disciplined as the election approached: it bypassed the spending cap to fund fuel subsidies and cash transfers ahead of the vote. Lula campaigned on reversing the cap entirely and implementing a new "fiscal framework" that would allow more social spending.

Throughout September-October 2022, as polls fluctuated — Brazil's election was extremely close — the BOVESPA and BRL moved with the polls. When polls showed Bolsonaro closing the gap, assets rallied. When Lula's lead widened, assets fell. On October 30, 2022, Lula won the runoff by the narrowest margin in Brazil's democratic history: 50.9% vs 49.1%. The next trading day, November 1, BOVESPA fell approximately 2–3% (a muted reaction because the margin was so close that markets had already partially priced it in during the final campaign days). The BRL weakened from roughly 5.18 to 5.40 per dollar over the following week.

The larger response came in November when Lula's transition team announced it would seek a constitutional amendment to exclude R\$175 billion in social spending from the spending cap, permanently. This was not just fiscal loosening — it was structural dismantling of the constraint that had kept Brazilian fiscal policy anchored since 2016. The BOVESPA fell another 4% on that announcement. The BRL touched 5.50 per dollar. Brazilian CDS (credit default swap) spreads widened.

The subsequent negotiation — which produced the "Arcabouço Fiscal" (new fiscal framework) in 2023 — was less damaging than feared but still represented a loosening of the original Teto. Brazilian assets partially recovered but never returned to the euphoric levels of the 2018 Bolsonaro period during Lula's first year in office.

#### Worked example: The Brazilian election options trade

A practitioner who wanted to express the view that the 2022 Lula win would weaken the BRL without knowing the margin of victory could use a simple BRL put option (the right to sell BRL / buy USD at a fixed rate). On September 15, 2022 (six weeks before the runoff), USDBRL was at approximately 5.18. A one-month at-the-money BRL put option with a 5.20 strike would have cost roughly 2.5% premium (an estimate based on typical BRL implied volatility of 15–18% annualized).

If USDBRL moved to 5.45 post-Lula announcement (a 5% move), the put's intrinsic value on expiry would be approximately 5.45 minus 5.20 = 0.25 per dollar, or roughly 4.8% of notional. Premium paid: 2.5%. Net gain: approximately 2.3% of notional on a position with defined maximum loss of 2.5%. If Bolsonaro had won and the BRL strengthened to 4.90, the option expires worthless — maximum loss is the 2.5% premium paid, which was the cost of political uncertainty insurance.

This illustrates the canonical EM election trade structure: use options to define your risk while still participating in a directional political outcome, because you cannot be certain of the result or its magnitude.

## The Generic EM Election Signal: 60 Days of Spread Widening

The three country cases above reveal a pattern that appears across dozens of EM elections. Researchers at JPMorgan, the IMF, and academic institutions have documented it: in the 60 days before an EM election where fiscal credibility is genuinely contested, local bond spreads (and EMBI+ for dollar-bond issuers) systematically widen. The average widening is approximately 60 basis points above the T-90 baseline for typical elections; it reaches 150–300 bps for high-stress elections (close race, populist candidate viable); and it can exceed 1,000 bps in Argentina-style extreme cases.

The mechanism is straightforward. Investors holding EM bonds do not want to be caught holding during a potential regime change that might produce default, devaluation, or central bank capture. So they reduce position size in the 60 days before the vote. This selling pressure widens spreads. The degree of widening is essentially a prediction market on election outcome and its consequences: the wider the spread, the more the market fears a loss of fiscal discipline.

The signal works in reverse too: if a market-friendly candidate wins, the 60-day pre-election spread widening reverses sharply post-result. This is the "catch-up" trade — buying EM bonds in the two weeks after a reform-friendly election outcome, when the pre-election risk premium is being unwound.

![EM election bond spread widening 60 days before vote across countries](/imgs/blogs/emerging-market-political-risk-turkey-argentina-brazil-case-studies-5.png)

**Measuring the signal in practice.** The most reliable indicators to watch 60–90 days before an EM election:

1. **EMBI+ spread velocity**: How fast spreads are widening, not just the level. A 10 bps/week widening rate signals rising concern; above 50 bps/week signals acute stress.

2. **CDS spreads**: Credit default swap spreads on sovereign debt are more liquid than bonds and reflect pure credit risk pricing without the noise of supply-demand dynamics in the bond market.

3. **Implied FX volatility**: Options markets price in uncertainty before events. Rising implied volatility on the local currency in the 30-day tenor tells you markets expect a large move around the election.

4. **Official vs. parallel FX rate gap** (where capital controls exist): A widening gap signals rising capital flight pressure — citizens trying to exit the local currency faster than the official system permits.

5. **Central bank reserve trajectory**: Falling reserves mean the central bank is selling dollars to support the currency — a signal of stress. Accelerating reserve drawdown in the pre-election period suggests unsustainable intervention.

## The Trade: Positioning Around EM Political Risk

The case studies above reveal three canonical trade structures that professionals use around EM political risk events.

**Trade 1: Spread compression post-orthodoxy.** When an EM country replaces heterodox policy leadership with a credible orthodox figure (as Turkey did in June 2023 with Simsek, or Argentina did implicitly with Milei), EMBI+ spreads compress rapidly. The entry signal is the appointment announcement, not the first rate decision. The instrument is typically dollar-denominated sovereign bonds (Eurobonds) or CDS selling (collecting premium as spread compresses). Target: 150–300 bps of spread compression over 6–18 months. Exit trigger: political backpressure that threatens the orthodox appointment, or failure to meet fiscal targets.

**Trade 2: Pre-election defensive positioning.** Sixty days before a high-stakes EM election, reduce local currency bond exposure (local bonds suffer from both FX risk and spread risk). Add put options on the local currency with 45–60 day expiry. This is insurance, not a directional bet — you pay the premium to avoid being on the wrong side of a 30% currency devaluation. Define the cost upfront and accept it as a portfolio management fee. After the result, assess whether to maintain or unwind based on the winner's revealed policy direction.

**Trade 3: Day-two catch-up.** After a market-friendly election result in an EM country where spreads had widened 200–400 bps pre-election, the reversal often happens over 5–15 trading sessions as the risk premium unwinds. This is a high-conviction short-duration trade: buy bonds or local currency on the day after results, with a 30-day time horizon. The risk is that initial market optimism proves premature (as it did after Macri's 2018 IMF deal failed to stabilize Argentina). Use a 3% price stop-loss and size accordingly.

#### Worked example: Sizing a Turkey Eurobond post-Simsek trade

An EM macro fund allocates \$5 million to Turkey 2028 Eurobonds on June 8, 2023 (the day after Simsek's appointment was confirmed). Entry price: 75 cents on the dollar (yield approximately 11.5%). Spread above comparable US Treasury: approximately 580 bps.

Thesis: over 12 months, the market re-rates Turkey as a credible orthodox emerging market, compressing spreads from 580 to 350 bps (a return toward where Turkey traded in 2017–2019 under earlier orthodox periods).

Scenario A (base case — spread compresses 200 bps to 380 bps): Bond price rises from 75 to approximately 87 cents (10-year modified duration roughly 6, 200 bps tightening = +12 points of price). Coupon income: 11.5% for 12 months = approximately \$575,000 on \$5 million position. Total return: approximately \$600,000 (price) + \$575,000 (coupon) = \$1.175 million, or 23.5%.

Scenario B (bear case — Erdogan reverses Simsek, spreads widen back to 650 bps): Bond price falls from 75 to 68 cents. Loss: \$350,000. Partial offset from 6 months coupon: approximately \$287,000. Net: -\$63,000, a loss of 1.3%.

The asymmetry — 23.5% upside vs 1.3% downside — reflects the nature of post-crisis recovery trades where the floor is a bond already trading at distress levels and where orthodox policy has a long track record of compressing EM spreads.

**What invalidates the EM political risk trade.** Three categories of invalidation:

First, *re-politicization*: if the market-friendly finance minister or central bank governor is replaced (especially mid-term), exit immediately. The turkey-governor firing playbook is the canonical cautionary tale. Do not wait for the official statement — exit on credible rumors of a change.

Second, *IMF deal breakdown*: in both Argentina and Turkey, IMF program compliance was a key credibility anchor. If a country misses IMF targets — especially fiscal deficit targets — the program goes on hold, signaling that political pressure is winning over fiscal discipline.

Third, *external shock amplification*: EM political risk trades work in a benign global backdrop (stable US rates, moderate dollar, decent global growth). A sharp US rate hike cycle, a strong dollar, or a global recession can overwhelm the domestic political improvement. Always hedge EM political risk trades with some component of the global EM risk environment — specifically, watch the DXY dollar index and US 10-year yields as external anchors.

## Synthesis: What the Three Templates Teach

Turkey, Argentina, and Brazil are not just three interesting stories. They are three archetypes that cover the universe of EM political risk scenarios you will encounter across dozens of emerging markets.

**The Turkey archetype — central bank capture.** Applies to any EM country where the executive directly controls monetary policy appointments without legal independence protections. Lookups: Is there a law protecting central bank independence, and has it ever been enforced against a sitting president? What is the central bank's real interest rate (policy rate minus inflation)? Countries with deeply negative real rates combined with political executive control of the central bank are in a Turkey-like vulnerability zone. Current candidates to watch: Pakistan, Egypt, Venezuela (extreme case), parts of sub-Saharan Africa.

**The Argentina archetype — fiscal sustainability cycle.** Applies to any EM with high foreign-currency-denominated debt, chronically high inflation, and a political system that cycles between reformers and populists. The warning sign is structural: a primary deficit that is financed by borrowing, combined with low foreign exchange reserves and a political opposition that promises fiscal expansion if it wins. The EMBI+ spread is your real-time thermometer. Current candidates to watch include Ecuador, Tunisia, Sri Lanka (post-2022 crisis), and Kenya.

**The Brazil archetype — institutionalized EM with election volatility.** Applies to EM countries with formal institutions (floating exchange rates, formal central bank independence) but contested fiscal policy frameworks. The elections produce 10–20% moves but not 40–80% collapses. Colombia, South Africa, Mexico, Indonesia, and India all fit this category to varying degrees. The EM election signal (pre-election spread widening, post-election compression) is most reliable for this archetype.

Understanding which archetype a country fits helps you calibrate both the magnitude of expected moves and the instruments to use. Turkey-archetype events call for FX hedges and hard-currency bonds rather than local currency exposure. Argentina-archetype events call for extreme position sizing discipline and CDS rather than bond positions. Brazil-archetype events call for options-based hedging that caps your worst-case loss while keeping upside.

**The common thread: credibility is the commodity.** Across all three archetypes, what moves asset prices is the market's assessment of institutional credibility — specifically, whether the entity controlling monetary and fiscal policy can be trusted to prioritize economic stability over short-term political expediency. That credibility can be built quickly (Simsek in Turkey, Milei's primary surplus in Argentina) or destroyed instantly (Agbal's Saturday-night firing). It cannot be manufactured through communication alone — it requires demonstrated policy choices that impose real political costs on the leader enforcing them.

For the practitioner, this means the most valuable information is not macroeconomic forecasts or sophisticated models. It is political intelligence: who is in the room, what does the leader actually believe about inflation and interest rates, what is the election calendar, and what constraints does the leader face from coalition partners or legal institutions. The technicals (spread levels, currency valuations, reserve adequacy) tell you how much risk is priced in; the political intelligence tells you what is about to change.

That combination — political intelligence plus financial measurement — is what separates effective EM investing from a random walk through high-yielding but lethal assets.
