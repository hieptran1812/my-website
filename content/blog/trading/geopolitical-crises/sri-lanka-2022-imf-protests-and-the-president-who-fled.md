> **TL;DR** — Sri Lanka's 2022 crisis was a textbook perfect storm: COVID wiped out tourism revenue (\$3.5B/year), a reckless fertilizer ban collapsed rice harvests, and a decade of debt-funded infrastructure left \$7.3B due in 2022 with almost no foreign reserves left to pay it.

> - **The rupee lost 44%** against the dollar in 2022 alone, collapsing from 200 to 360 LKR/USD when the central bank finally abandoned its peg in March 2022.
> - **Dollar-denominated sovereign bonds (SLSD)** crashed from near-par (96 cents) to a trough of ~10 cents on the dollar by July 2022, before recovering to ~45 cents after the November 2023 restructuring deal — a 350% return for distressed investors who bought at the bottom.
> - **The Hambantota port** — built with a \$1.1B Chinese loan — became a 99-year lease to China after Sri Lanka defaulted on the debt in 2017, the defining "debt trap diplomacy" case study.
> - **The IMF's \$2.9B Extended Fund Facility** (agreed September 2022) required tax hikes, currency flexibility, and debt restructuring. China was the key holdout creditor for 14 months, refusing the "common framework" until November 2023.
> - **The early warning signals** — external debt at 390% of exports, import cover below 3 months, and a government running twin deficits — were all visible years before the crisis.

On July 9, 2022, over 100,000 Sri Lankans stormed the Presidential Palace in Colombo. They swam in the president's pool, cooked in his kitchen, and lounged in his beds. By the next day, President Gotabaya Rajapaksa had fled to the Maldives on a military aircraft. Days later, still in exile, he emailed his resignation to parliament. The country of 22 million people — an island nation of extraordinary natural beauty that had hosted 2.3 million tourists just three years earlier — had run completely out of foreign exchange. There was no fuel to pump, no medicine on pharmacy shelves, and the national airline could barely keep planes flying.

This wasn't a war. It wasn't a natural disaster. It was a sovereign debt crisis — the most acute in South Asia in living memory, and one of the most dramatic political implosions of the 21st century. The story of how Sri Lanka got there, and how it got out, is a masterclass in what happens when a small open economy runs too many structural risks simultaneously, borrows in foreign currency without a credible repayment plan, and then gets hit by a global shock it has no buffer to absorb.

For investors, the Sri Lanka crisis matters because it contains every mechanism of emerging market blow-ups in concentrated form: currency collapse, import compression, social unrest, sovereign default, geopolitical creditor complexity, and ultimately, IMF-mediated restructuring. Understanding the sequence, the signals, and the market pricing at each stage teaches you how to read the next one.

## Foundations: What Every Term Means Before We Go Further

Before we trace the crisis mechanics, let's build the vocabulary. These concepts sound technical but they're all built on ideas you already understand.

### Foreign Exchange Reserves: The National Savings Account

Every country that trades internationally needs to hold some savings in foreign currency — mostly US dollars — to pay for imports (oil, food, medicine, machinery) and to service foreign-denominated debt. These savings are called **foreign exchange (FX) reserves**, held by the central bank.

Think of it like a household's emergency fund. If your monthly expenses are \$5,000 and you have \$500 in savings, you have exactly 0.1 months of cover — you're one missed paycheck from disaster. Economists measure FX reserves in **months of import cover**: how many months could the country keep importing at its normal rate if all foreign income stopped? The conventional danger threshold is below 3 months. Sri Lanka in April 2022 had 0.9 months.

### The Current Account and Trade Balance

A country's **current account** is the net flow of money into and out of it from trade and services. If Sri Lanka exports tea, garments, and services (like tourism) worth \$13B per year but imports oil, food, and machinery worth \$17B per year, the current account deficit is \$4B. That \$4B shortfall must be financed somehow — either by borrowing in foreign currency, drawing down reserves, or attracting foreign investment.

When a country runs persistent current account deficits, it accumulates foreign debt. The debt eventually must be repaid, in foreign currency. If the country's exports don't grow fast enough to generate the foreign currency needed to service that debt, you get a **balance of payments crisis**: the country literally cannot pay its external bills.

### Sovereign Bonds: The Government's Promissory Note

When a government borrows money internationally, it issues **sovereign bonds** — promissory notes promising to pay a fixed interest rate (the coupon) and return the principal at maturity. Sri Lanka's dollar-denominated bonds are called **SLSD** (Sri Lanka Sovereign Debt). Like any bond, their price moves inversely to perceived risk: if markets think Sri Lanka might not pay, the price falls and the yield rises.

When a sovereign bond trades at "10 cents on the dollar," it means the market price has dropped from face value (\$1,000 per bond) to \$100 — a 90% loss from par. Only distressed debt specialists and hedge funds willing to accept that risk are buying at those levels. They're making a bet that the country will eventually restructure the debt and they'll get more than 10 cents back.

### The IMF Extended Fund Facility: A Structural Bail-Out

The **International Monetary Fund (IMF)** is a multilateral lender of last resort for countries facing balance of payments crises. It lends in dollars (technically in Special Drawing Rights, a basket currency) in exchange for **conditionality** — policy changes the country must implement to restore fiscal sustainability.

The **Extended Fund Facility (EFF)** is the IMF's longest program (3-4 years) designed for countries with deep structural problems, not just short-term liquidity gaps. It requires the most extensive reforms: tax policy changes, spending cuts, currency flexibility, state-owned enterprise reform, and typically a requirement that the country first reach agreement with its private creditors (bondholders) to restructure the debt before the IMF money flows.

### Debt Restructuring: The Haircut Negotiation

When a country cannot service its sovereign bonds, it may reach a **restructuring agreement** with bondholders — essentially a renegotiation of the terms. The two main tools are:

1. **Principal reduction (haircut)**: Bondholders accept less than face value. If you hold \$1,000 face value and accept \$450, you've taken a 55% haircut on principal.
2. **Maturity extension**: The same principal is owed, but the payment dates are pushed out 5-10 years, reducing the immediate cash demand.

Most restructurings use a combination. The key metric is **NPV (net present value) reduction** — how much has the present value of all future payments been reduced, relative to the original terms? For Sri Lanka's November 2023 deal, the NPV reduction for SLSD holders was roughly 27-36% depending on assumptions, with bonds settling at approximately 45 cents on the dollar.

### The Common Framework: The G20's Debt Restructuring Club

After COVID-19 left many low-income countries over-indebted, the G20 created the **Common Framework** in November 2020 — a process for coordinating debt relief. The problem is that modern sovereign creditors include not just the Paris Club (Western governments) and private bondholders, but also **China**, which has become the world's largest bilateral creditor and which has historically refused to operate within Western-led restructuring frameworks.

China's participation in the Common Framework for Sri Lanka was the single biggest uncertainty in the restructuring process and the key reason the deal took until November 2023 to close — 14 months after the IMF program was announced.

## The Political Moves

To understand how the crisis happened, you need to understand the politics that drove it. The Sri Lanka story isn't just about macroeconomics — it's about a family dynasty that made a series of catastrophically bad bets on ideology, development policy, and political survival.

### The Rajapaksa Dynasty and the Debt-for-Development Strategy

Mahinda Rajapaksa became president in 2005 and ended the decades-long civil war in 2009 — making him a genuine national hero in the Sinhalese Buddhist majority. With that political capital, his government pursued an ambitious development agenda financed by foreign borrowing. The showcase projects included the Hambantota Port (in Mahinda's home district), the Mattala Rajapaksa International Airport (nicknamed the "world's emptiest airport"), the Port City Colombo, and dozens of highways and stadiums.

The financing came primarily from two sources:
- **International Sovereign Bonds (ISBs)** sold to global fixed-income investors at yields of 6-8%, raising billions in the early 2010s when global interest rates were near zero and investor appetite for emerging market yield was insatiable.
- **Bilateral loans from China**, mostly through China Development Bank and Exim Bank of China at commercial interest rates (not the soft concessional terms China uses for the poorest countries), for specific projects under the Belt and Road Initiative.

By 2019, Sri Lanka's total external debt had reached approximately \$51B — an eye-watering 390% of annual export earnings. The debt service alone consumed over 30% of government revenue. But as long as the economy grew and tourism revenue provided foreign currency inflows, the system could keep rolling over.

### The 2019 Easter Bombings: The First Shock

On Easter Sunday, April 21, 2019, coordinated Islamist terrorist bombings struck luxury hotels and churches in Sri Lanka, killing 269 people including many foreigners. In a single day, international tourism confidence collapsed. Booking cancellations wiped out the remainder of the 2019 tourism season, and the industry — which generated \$3.6B annually and employed hundreds of thousands — went into survival mode.

Gotabaya Rajapaksa, Mahinda's brother and former defense secretary, ran for president later that year partly on a security platform. He won decisively in November 2019. Weeks later, he announced sweeping tax cuts: the VAT rate was slashed from 15% to 8%, income tax was cut, and other levies were abolished. The stated goal was to stimulate the economy. The actual effect was to blow a massive hole in government revenue — fiscal revenue fell from 12.6% of GDP in 2019 to 8.7% in 2020 — at the exact moment the economy needed fiscal buffers.

### COVID: The Existential Shock

When COVID-19 hit in early 2020, Sri Lanka was already wobbling. Tourism — which had recovered somewhat from the Easter bombings — collapsed to near-zero. The 2.3 million tourists of 2019 became roughly 0.5 million in 2021, a decline of roughly 78%. The \$3.5B per year in tourism foreign exchange receipts essentially vanished.

Remittances from Sri Lankans working abroad, another major FX source, also declined. The current account deficit blew out. And without significant foreign currency inflows, the central bank began burning through its reserves to defend the rupee's exchange rate — an act of political theater that protected importers in the short run but destroyed the reserve buffer that would be needed to service the upcoming debt wave.

### The Organic Farming Disaster: A Self-Inflicted Wound

In April 2021, with the country already under pressure, Gotabaya Rajapaksa made one of the most catastrophic unilateral agricultural policy decisions in modern history. He banned all synthetic fertilizers and pesticides overnight, declaring Sri Lanka would become the world's first 100% organic agricultural nation. The stated rationale was to save foreign exchange spent on imported fertilizers (~\$400M/year), reduce farmer debt, and promote environmental sustainability.

What followed was predictable to every agronomist but apparently not to the presidential advisors who championed the policy. Rice yields fell by an estimated 20-30% almost immediately. Tea production — Sri Lanka's largest export crop and a critical source of foreign currency — fell sharply. Farmers who had relied on synthetic inputs for decades could not simply switch overnight to organic methods. Food prices rose, food insecurity spread, and the country ironically had to spend additional foreign exchange importing rice and other staples to cover the domestic production shortfall.

By November 2021, facing protests from farmers and devastating production numbers, Gotabaya reversed the ban. But six months of agricultural disruption had done real damage — not just to crops, but to the credibility of government policy-making in the eyes of international creditors and the IMF.

### The Debt Wall Hits: 2022

Throughout 2021, Sri Lanka's government was making a catastrophic mistake in its debt management: it was using its dwindling foreign reserves to repay sovereign bonds as they matured, prioritizing **bondholders over economic stability**. By early 2022, official reserves had fallen to around \$2.3B — against debt service obligations of roughly \$7.3B due that year.

In February 2022, Russia invaded Ukraine. Global commodity prices spiked. Oil, which Sri Lanka imports entirely, became 40% more expensive. Food commodity prices — wheat, cooking oil — surged. The import bill exploded at the exact moment the country could least afford it.

On March 7, 2022, the government finally abandoned the rupee's official exchange rate peg that had kept the currency artificially strong. The rupee immediately crashed. Within weeks it had fallen from around 200 to 300 LKR per USD, and would reach 360 by July. The official acknowledgment of the peg abandonment triggered a cascade: import letters of credit couldn't be opened, fuel shipments stopped arriving, power plants ran out of fuel for generation.

Fuel queues stretched for kilometers. People waited days for cooking gas. Hospitals began postponing non-emergency surgeries due to medicine shortages. Rolling power cuts of 12-13 hours per day became routine. The Sri Lanka economy was in free fall.

### July 9, 2022: The Palace Falls

By late June, the public's patience was exhausted. Inflation had reached 54% (it would later peak at 70% in September 2022). Fuel station queues were measured in days, not hours. Protesters had been camping outside the Presidential Secretariat for months in a movement called "GotaGoGama" (Gota Go Village).

On July 9, 2022, the dam broke. Estimates suggest 100,000 to 500,000 people descended on Colombo. Crowds overwhelmed security forces at the Presidential Palace — a colonial-era building of considerable grandeur — and streamed in. Videos circulated worldwide of protesters swimming in the pool, filming themselves in the ornate bedrooms, and cooking in the kitchens with food found in the refrigerators.

Gotabaya had already quietly left the palace in the pre-dawn hours. On July 13, he boarded a Sri Lanka Air Force plane to the Maldives (one of the few countries that hadn't yet ended its visa-on-arrival agreement with Sri Lanka). From the Maldives, he flew to Singapore. On July 14, he emailed his resignation to the Speaker of Parliament.

It was the most direct expression of mass popular fury at an economic crisis anywhere in the world since Argentina's 2001 collapse. And as in Argentina, the immediate political chaos gave way — slowly and painfully — to a new government capable of dealing with the IMF.

## The Financial Channels

The political story is dramatic, but the financial mechanics are what you need to understand to trade around similar situations.

### The Rupee Collapse: How Currency Pegs Become Currency Crises

![Sri Lanka rupee depreciated from 200 to 360 per USD in 2022 before partially recovering to 300 by 2024](/imgs/blogs/sri-lanka-2022-imf-protests-and-the-president-who-fled-2.webp)

Sri Lanka's central bank maintained an official exchange rate throughout 2021 and into early 2022, keeping the rupee at around 200-202 LKR/USD through direct intervention — selling dollars from reserves to support the peg. This intervention was unsustainable: every dollar sold to defend the peg was a dollar less in reserves to service external debt.

The mechanics of a currency peg breaking are always the same: the central bank runs out of reserves before the speculators run out of bets. When the peg was abandoned in March 2022, the rupee found its market-clearing level almost instantly. The market was effectively saying: "At 200 LKR/USD, everyone wants dollars. At 360 LKR/USD, sellers and buyers find each other."

The currency collapse itself then became a secondary driver of the crisis:
- All imports (denominated in dollars) became 44% more expensive overnight in local currency terms.
- Foreign debt (denominated in dollars) became 44% more expensive to service in local currency terms.
- Inflation imported from the currency collapse pushed domestic prices up, reducing real wages and household purchasing power.
- Businesses that had dollar-denominated obligations (airlines, importers) faced immediate liquidity crises.

**So how do you read this?** The "LOOP" model: pegged currency → reserve burn → peg breaks → inflation → social unrest → political crisis → default → IMF → restructuring → partial recovery. Every step is visible in the data before the next one materializes. The signal to watch is the rate of reserve burn relative to upcoming debt maturities. When burn rate exceeds 6 months of import cover equivalent per year, peg defense is almost certainly unsustainable.

### The FX Reserve Drain: The Slow Bleed and the Fast Fall

![Sri Lanka FX reserves fell from 7.5 billion USD in 2019 to 1.6 billion in April 2022](/imgs/blogs/sri-lanka-2022-imf-protests-and-the-president-who-fled-3.webp)

FX reserves are the most important real-time indicator in an emerging market sovereign crisis. Let's trace Sri Lanka's trajectory with precision.

**2019:** \$7.5B in reserves — roughly 4 months of import cover. Not comfortable for a country with Sri Lanka's debt profile, but manageable.

**2020:** COVID-19 hits. Tourism revenue collapses by ~\$2.5B. Reserves fall to \$5.5-6.0B. The central bank begins cutting interest rates to stimulate the economy, making rupee assets less attractive and accelerating capital outflows.

**2021:** Organic farming ban causes agricultural disruption. Central bank prints money to cover fiscal deficits. Reserves continue draining. By end of 2021, they're around \$3.1B. The central bank is simultaneously trying to defend the exchange rate peg (selling dollars) and cover fiscal financing gaps (printing rupees). Both actions contradict each other.

**Early 2022:** Accelerating drain. By April 2022, usable reserves are down to \$1.6B — with import payments running at roughly \$1.8B/month. The country has less than one month of import cover. The peg must break.

**Post-IMF (2023):** With the EFF program in place, reserves begin rebuilding. Emergency swap lines from India (about \$1.5B) provided a bridge. By end-2023, reserves had recovered to around \$4.4B — still not comfortable, but no longer existential.

#### Worked example: Import Coverage Collapse

Sri Lanka's April 2022 situation in numbers:

- Usable FX reserves: \$1.6B (source: Central Bank of Sri Lanka, April 2022 release)
- Monthly import bill: approximately \$1.8B (annual import bill ~\$21.5B ÷ 12)
- Import cover months: \$1.6B ÷ \$1.8B = **0.89 months**
- IMF minimum threshold: 3 months (conventional)
- Gap vs. threshold: country needed ~\$5.4B to be at the IMF floor, had \$1.6B

A country with 0.89 months of import cover cannot guarantee it will receive the next oil shipment. Fuel suppliers demand payment upfront or in advance for exactly this reason. This is why the fuel queues happened: the country's creditworthiness with oil suppliers collapsed simultaneously with its reserves.

Contrast this with a country like Thailand in 2022, which held approximately \$200B in reserves against much lower import bills — decades of current account surpluses created by its manufacturing export machine.

#### Worked example: The FX Reserve Burn Rate Signal

Sri Lanka's reserves fell from approximately \$7.5B in mid-2019 to \$1.6B in April 2022 — a drop of about \$5.9B over 33 months.

- Monthly burn rate: \$5.9B ÷ 33 months = **\$179M/month average**
- But burn accelerated: the last 12 months (Apr 2021–Apr 2022) saw a drop from ~\$4.0B to \$1.6B = \$2.4B in 12 months = **\$200M/month**
- And the final 6 months accelerated further to roughly \$340M/month as peg defense intensified

This burn trajectory was visible in public Central Bank of Sri Lanka monthly data throughout 2021. Any analyst watching this could see by mid-2021 that reserves would breach the danger threshold within 12-18 months absent a major policy reversal or external financing.

**So how do you read this?** Monthly FX reserve data from central banks is public. Build a simple burn rate model: take the 3-month moving average of reserve changes and project forward. When the projected depletion date (reserves hitting 3 months import cover) is within 18 months, sovereign stress is imminent. The yield on the 5-year dollar bond should be pricing this risk. If it isn't, there may be a short opportunity in the sovereign.

### The Debt Trap: How Hambantota Became a 99-Year Lease

![China debt trap: from BRI loan to 99-year lease on Hambantota Port through debt-for-equity swap](/imgs/blogs/sri-lanka-2022-imf-protests-and-the-president-who-fled-5.webp)

The Hambantota Port is the canonical example of what critics call "debt trap diplomacy" — a pattern where Chinese loans for infrastructure projects in developing countries result in those countries ceding strategic assets when they can't repay.

The mechanics:

1. **2007-2012:** China's Merchants Port Holdings provided approximately \$1.1B in loans at commercial interest rates (around 6.3%) to build the Hambantota Port in southern Sri Lanka. The port was in Mahinda Rajapaksa's home electoral district, making it politically valuable regardless of economic merit.

2. **The utilization problem:** Hambantota was a small town with no natural commercial traffic. The port was built not where trade routes passed, but where votes could be won. By 2014, the port was operating at under 5% of capacity. Revenue was nowhere near sufficient to service the \$1.1B loan.

3. **The debt-for-equity swap (December 2017):** Unable to service the debt, Sri Lanka and China Merchants Port Holdings agreed to a deal: 70% equity stake in the port, plus a 99-year lease on the surrounding land, in exchange for the debt. China effectively received the port as collateral enforcement.

4. **The geopolitical reaction:** India, the United States, and regional security analysts immediately highlighted that a 99-year Chinese-operated port just 200 miles from the Indian coast — and directly in the Indian Ocean shipping lanes — was a significant strategic asset. China now had a foothold in waters where it had previously had no presence.

The debate about whether Hambantota was "deliberate" debt trap diplomacy (China purposely lending unsustainably to get the asset) or simply a commercial transaction that went wrong is genuinely contested among academics and policy experts. The *effect*, however, is the same: Sri Lanka lost control of a strategic port for 99 years because it borrowed in foreign currency without a credible repayment plan.

#### Worked example: The True Cost of the Hambantota Loan

Let's price the Hambantota loan properly with the information we now have:

- Principal: \$1.1B
- Interest rate: approximately 6.3% per year (commercial, not concessional)
- Annual interest cost: \$69.3M/year
- Port revenue in 2014 (peak year before handover): approximately \$12M
- Annual shortfall: \$57.3M/year — the port was servicing only 17 cents on every dollar of interest owed

At 6.3% compounded over a decade with inadequate revenue, the debt grew faster than any realistic port revenue. The math of the debt trap: a project funded by commercial debt *must* generate commercial returns. A port serving under 5% of capacity cannot generate commercial returns. The only question was how long before the creditor enforced the collateral.

**So how do you read this?** When evaluating BRI-funded projects in emerging markets, the question isn't "will it be built?" — it's "will it generate enough revenue to service the debt at the contracted interest rate?" Apply a simple debt service coverage ratio: projected annual project revenue / annual debt service. Below 1.2x, the project is a debt trap risk. Below 0.5x, as Hambantota was, it's a certainty.

### The Sovereign Bond Collapse: From Near-Par to Distressed

![SLSD sovereign bond price from 96 cents on dollar in 2020 to 10 cents in July 2022 then 45 cents after restructuring](/imgs/blogs/sri-lanka-2022-imf-protests-and-the-president-who-fled-6.webp)

Sri Lanka's dollar-denominated sovereign bonds tell the entire story of the crisis in price form:

**2019-early 2020:** Bonds trading near par — around 95-96 cents on the dollar. The market was watching the Easter bombing shock but still believed Sri Lanka's credit was broadly intact.

**2020-2021:** COVID erodes the credit thesis. Bonds drift from 92 to 65 cents on the dollar as FX reserves fall and the fiscal deficit expands. But no default yet — the government is still servicing bonds.

**Early 2022:** Acceleration. By January 2022, when it became clear that reserves were insufficient to cover 2022 maturities and no credible IMF deal was in sight, bonds fell through 45 cents. By March (when the peg broke), they were at 18 cents. By the July palace storming, they touched a trough of approximately 10 cents — meaning the market was pricing in a roughly 90% recovery of nothing, or 10 cents of recovery in a restructuring.

**September 2022:** IMF announces the \$2.9B EFF program. Bonds recover from ~10 to ~12 cents. The IMF announcement signals the country is on a restructuring path, which is modestly constructive — it means there will be *some* recovery, the country is not being abandoned.

**2023:** Long negotiation. Bonds trade in the 12-30 cent range as the market watches China and private creditor negotiations. Uncertainty about China's participation in the common framework kept a discount on recovery values.

**November 2023:** Restructuring deal announced. SLSD bonds reached approximately 45 cents on the dollar — the price of the new restructured instruments. Holders who bought at 10 cents and received 45 cents in the restructuring recorded a **350% return on notional principal** (though denominated in dollars, and timed over 16 months).

#### Worked example: The Distressed Debt Trade

Suppose a hedge fund bought \$10M face value of SLSD bonds at 10 cents on the dollar in August 2022, immediately after the palace storming.

**Entry cost:** \$10M × 10% = **\$1M invested**

The fund's thesis: Sri Lanka had strategic importance (it sits astride major Indian Ocean shipping lanes), the IMF was engaged, the country had never before defaulted on sovereign bonds (a track record of payment), and the chaos of July 2022 had pushed prices to levels pricing in near-total loss which was unlikely given IMF engagement.

**November 2023 outcome:** \$10M face value restructured at approximately 45 cents = **\$4.5M received** (in the form of new instruments, either cash or new bonds at the restructured terms)

**Return:** (\$4.5M - \$1M) / \$1M = **350% gross return** on invested capital over approximately 16 months

The risks the fund was taking: (1) China might refuse to participate in common framework, forcing the deal to collapse or be substantially less generous to private creditors; (2) political instability might derail the IMF program; (3) the 16-month waiting period with no liquidity.

Sophisticated distressed sovereign funds (Gramercy, Autonomy, Greylock) were active in Sri Lanka bonds during this period. The trade is relatively well understood in the distressed debt community.

**So how do you read this?** When a sovereign bond trades below 20 cents with an IMF program active or imminent, the risk/reward on the long side often becomes favorable. The key variable is the IMF's assessment of debt sustainability — the program's DSA (debt sustainability analysis) gives you the upper bound on recovery values, because the IMF requires that debt become "sustainable" as a condition of the program.

## The China Creditor Problem: Why the Deal Took 14 Months

The IMF announced its \$2.9B EFF program in September 2022. The restructuring deal with private bondholders wasn't finalized until November 2023. The 14-month gap was almost entirely due to China.

China is now the world's largest bilateral creditor, holding approximately \$843B in overseas loans as of 2021 (source: AidData, William & Mary). China's loans have grown enormously through the Belt and Road Initiative, but China has consistently resisted joining the Paris Club — the longstanding multilateral framework through which Western government creditors coordinate debt restructuring.

The G20 Common Framework, created in November 2020, was designed partly to bring China into a multilateral restructuring process. But in Sri Lanka, as in Zambia and Ethiopia (the other 2022 Common Framework cases), China moved slowly and on its own terms.

China's position on Sri Lanka debt had several components:

1. **China did not accept a nominal principal haircut** on its bilateral loans. Instead, it agreed to extend maturities, allow grace periods on principal payments, and accept below-market interest rates. From China's perspective, this is equivalent to an NPV reduction; from the West's perspective, the lack of a headline haircut is problematic because it means private bondholders are taking deeper haircuts while the state lender is protected.

2. **China insisted on comparable treatment** with other creditors — it would not move before seeing what the Paris Club and private bondholders were accepting. This created a standoff where everyone was waiting for everyone else.

3. **The Hambantota port was a complicating factor**: China had a strategic interest in Sri Lanka beyond pure bond recovery — it wanted to preserve its position at Hambantota. Being seen as a cooperative restructuring partner was not purely about the economics.

The breakthrough came in November 2023, partly through bilateral US-China diplomacy at the sidelines of international summits, and partly because Zambia's restructuring closing (also November 2023) created a template. Once China could point to what it had accepted in Zambia as "comparable," it became easier to reach Sri Lanka agreement.

**So how do you read this?** In any sovereign restructuring where China is a major creditor, add 12-18 months to your expected restructuring timeline compared to a Paris Club-only creditor base. This delays recovery for bondholders and creates uncertainty that justifies a discount on expected recovery values.

## Common Misconceptions

### "Sri Lanka's crisis was caused by Chinese debt"

This is a dangerous oversimplification. Chinese bilateral debt was approximately \$3.5B of Sri Lanka's total \$51B external debt — about 7%. The much larger source of stress was International Sovereign Bonds (ISBs) — \$12.6B — issued to global private investors at high interest rates during the post-2008 easy money era.

China's debt gets disproportionate attention for two reasons: (a) the Hambantota port is a vivid, concrete story; and (b) it serves the geopolitical narrative of Western governments seeking to highlight the risks of BRI lending. Both are real issues. But blaming China for Sri Lanka's crisis while ignoring the far larger private bond market problem is analytically misleading.

The root cause was a structural deficit between what Sri Lanka earned in foreign currency and what it owed to all creditors combined — regardless of nationality.

### "The IMF made the crisis worse with austerity"

The IMF's EFF program did require significant fiscal consolidation: the VAT was raised back toward 15%, income taxes were expanded, energy subsidies were cut, and fiscal targets were imposed. These measures were painful, particularly for the poor.

However, the counterfactual is not "no austerity" — it's "continued economic collapse." By September 2022, Sri Lanka was already experiencing 70% inflation, had no fuel, and was essentially cut off from international credit markets. The IMF program, harsh as its conditions were, was what enabled the country to access emergency financing, stabilize the currency, and begin rebuilding reserves. By 2024, inflation had fallen from 70% to approximately 4%, the rupee had stabilized, and the economy was growing again (modestly).

The real critique of IMF conditionality in Sri Lanka is more nuanced: the fund should arguably have engaged earlier (the government first approached the IMF in early 2022, but formal negotiations only began in mid-year) and might have prioritized social safety nets more explicitly as a counterweight to the fiscal contraction.

### "Organic farming caused the crisis"

The fertilizer ban was a catastrophic own-goal and contributed meaningfully to the crisis. But it was one of several compounding factors, not the primary cause. Even without the agricultural disruption, Sri Lanka faced an acute debt crisis in 2022 because:

1. COVID had wiped out \$3.5B/year in tourism revenues
2. \$7.3B in debt service was due in 2022 against \$2.3B in reserves
3. The 2019 tax cuts had blown a permanent hole in fiscal revenue

The fertilizer ban made an existing crisis much worse and provided the most visible policy failure for the public narrative — but a crisis was coming regardless.

### "The restructuring wiped out bondholders"

SLSD bondholders who held from par to trough did suffer enormous losses — an 85-90% price decline at the trough. But the restructuring settlement at approximately 45 cents represented meaningful recovery relative to the trough of 10 cents. Holders who bought at par and received 45 cents in restructuring took a 55% loss, which is painful but not total wipeout.

More importantly, the restructuring gave Sri Lanka the debt relief needed to restore market access over time. Countries that complete IMF-supervised restructurings typically regain the ability to issue new international bonds within 3-7 years. Sri Lanka's trajectory suggests potential market return by 2026-2027.

## How It Shows Up in Real Markets

The Sri Lanka crisis created several distinct tradeable opportunities and important early warning signals that are now embedded in the toolkit for emerging market practitioners.

### The IMF Program Types and Market Reactions

![IMF program types compared by duration conditions and sovereign spread impact](/imgs/blogs/sri-lanka-2022-imf-protests-and-the-president-who-fled-7.webp)

The announcement of an IMF program is one of the most reliable positive signals in emerging market sovereign debt. But the *type* of program matters enormously for the size and durability of the spread compression that follows.

For Sri Lanka, the September 2022 EFF announcement caused bonds to move from approximately 10 cents to 12 cents — a modest initial move, because the program required debt restructuring before the first disbursement. The real move came as restructuring milestones were hit through 2023.

The pattern: IMF SBA (Stand-By Arrangement) → spread tightens 100-200bps quickly, fades if reforms lag. IMF EFF (Extended Fund Facility, like Sri Lanka) → initial modest move, then gradual tightening as structural milestones are met, culminating in bonds trading at restructured recovery value.

### The Rupee as an Inflation Transmission Channel

Sri Lanka's rupee collapse illustrates how currency depreciation transmits into inflation with a time lag. The rupee fell from 200 to 360 by July 2022 — a 44% decline. Inflation peaked at 70% in September 2022, two to three months after the currency trough.

The mechanism: most of Sri Lanka's consumption goods (oil, medicine, food commodities) are imported and priced in dollars. When the rupee falls 44%, the *rupee price* of all these goods rises 44% almost immediately. That import price inflation then spreads through the economy as sellers reprice, workers demand higher wages, and the central bank faces a dilemma between fighting inflation (raising rates, which suppresses growth) and supporting growth (cutting rates, which weakens currency further).

For traders: when a pegged EM currency is forced to free-float after a long peg defense, the inflation peak typically lags the currency trough by 2-4 months. This creates a predictable window: the currency has already troughed, but rate expectations (which drive local bond markets) haven't yet adjusted to the coming inflation. Local government bond yields typically spike 300-500bps in the 3-6 months following the currency break.

### The External Debt/Exports Ratio as Early Warning

The most powerful early warning signal in Sri Lanka's data — and in most sovereign debt crises — is the **external debt to export earnings ratio**.

Sri Lanka's ratio in 2022: approximately \$51B external debt / \$13B annual export earnings = **392%**

This means it would take Sri Lanka nearly 4 years of its entire export earnings — with nothing spent on imports, domestic consumption, or other obligations — to repay its external debt. In practice, countries with this ratio are chronically dependent on rolling over debt and on foreign exchange inflows beyond exports (tourism, remittances, FDI). When any of those inflows stop, the math breaks.

Empirical research (most notably studies by Carmen Reinhart and Kenneth Rogoff in "This Time Is Different") suggests sovereign distress becomes highly likely when external debt exceeds 200% of exports and near-certain at 300%+ in combination with weak institutional quality. Sri Lanka was well past both thresholds by 2021.

#### Worked example: Building the Early Warning Dashboard

Here's the dashboard an EM macro trader would have built on Sri Lanka through 2021:

| Indicator | Q3 2020 | Q1 2021 | Q3 2021 | Threshold | Signal |
|-----------|---------|---------|---------|-----------|--------|
| FX reserves (months import cover) | 3.4 | 2.5 | 2.0 | < 3 months | RED |
| External debt / exports (%) | 310% | 355% | 390% | > 200% | RED |
| Fiscal deficit / GDP (%) | -11.1% | -9.8% | -8.5% | > -5% | RED |
| Rupee real effective exchange rate | Overvalued | Overvalued | Overvalued | vs. BEER model | AMBER |

By Q3 2021, every indicator was flashing red or amber. The SLSD 5-year bond was still trading in the 50s (cents on dollar), meaning the market was pricing about a 50-50 chance of default — already distressed, but not yet at the extreme pessimism that would emerge in 2022. A trader who shorted SLSD bonds in Q3 2021 when they were at 55-65 cents and covered at 10 cents in July 2022 would have made approximately 85% on the short in under 12 months.

### Sovereign Spread Compression: The IMF Trade

The inverse of the short-the-deteriorating-sovereign trade is the **long-the-restructuring-recovery** trade. After an IMF program is announced and a country begins meeting milestones, spread compression follows a predictable pattern:

1. **Program announcement:** Initial 50-100bps compression as the IMF seal removes the immediate default tail risk.
2. **First disbursement:** Another 100-200bps as program credibility is established.
3. **Debt restructuring completion:** Large move — bonds jump to the restructured recovery level.
4. **Return to market access:** Gradual re-rating as the country demonstrates sustained primary fiscal surpluses.

For Sri Lanka, the bond price trajectory from 10 cents (trough) to 45 cents (restructuring completion) captures steps 1-3. Most of the gain came in stage 3. The challenge for retail or smaller investors is that distressed sovereign bonds are illiquid, traded in large minimum sizes (\$100,000-\$200,000 face value), and typically only accessible through institutional accounts or EM-specialist funds.

## How to Trade It: The Playbook

This section synthesizes everything into an actionable framework. These are not recommendations for specific securities — they're the mechanics and signals a practitioner uses.

### Signal 1: Import Coverage Below 3 Months → Sovereign Stress Imminent

Source: central bank monthly reserve data (freely available on central bank websites or IMF International Financial Statistics).

Calculation: gross FX reserves ÷ average monthly imports (= annual imports ÷ 12).

Action: when import cover falls below 3 months AND external debt service in the next 12 months exceeds 50% of current reserves, a currency break is likely within 6-18 months. Short the currency (via NDF or options if accessible), short sovereign bonds (via CDS if available), or avoid the currency in long EM portfolios.

### Signal 2: The "IMF Arrival" Trade

When an IMF team visits a country in crisis and negotiations begin (publicly acknowledged), sovereign bonds typically:
- Fall 5-10 cents in the preceding weeks (market fears the program will require creditor haircuts)
- Stabilize at announcement of program framework
- Begin recovering as disbursement milestones are met

The **entry point with best risk/reward** is typically 4-8 weeks after the initial IMF announcement, once the initial "restructuring required" selloff has occurred but before the first disbursement. In Sri Lanka, that would have been October-November 2022 when bonds were at 10-13 cents.

### Signal 3: Watch China's Position for Restructuring Timeline

In any EM sovereign crisis where China holds 5%+ of external debt:
- Add 12-18 months to expected restructuring timeline vs. Paris Club-only cases
- Apply a 5-15 cent discount to expected recovery values to compensate for deal uncertainty
- Watch for G20 summit communiqués and bilateral US-China meetings as restructuring catalysts

When China reaches an in-principle agreement (as it did for Zambia and Sri Lanka in late 2023), bonds typically pop 5-10 cents in a single day on the announcement.

### Signal 4: External Debt / Exports Ratio as Portfolio Screen

For broad EM sovereign portfolios, screen all holdings quarterly for countries where external debt exceeds 200% of annual exports AND:
- Current account deficit is widening
- FX reserves fell more than 20% in the past 12 months

Any country hitting all three flags deserves deep-dive analysis and position sizing reduction. Build the screen from IMF World Economic Outlook data, World Bank Debt Statistics, and individual country central bank reports.

#### Worked example: External Debt / Exports Cross-Country Comparison (2022)

| Country | External Debt (USD bn) | Exports (USD bn) | Ratio | Status (2022) |
|---------|----------------------|-----------------|-------|---------------|
| Sri Lanka | ~\$51B | ~\$13B | 392% | **Defaulted** |
| Pakistan | ~\$130B | ~\$31B | 419% | IMF program |
| Egypt | ~\$155B | ~\$53B | 292% | IMF program |
| Argentina | ~\$280B | ~\$75B | 373% | Chronic stress |
| Thailand | ~\$180B | ~\$290B | 62% | No stress |

The contrast with Thailand is instructive: Thailand has large external debt in absolute terms, but its massive export base makes the ratio perfectly comfortable. The ratio captures *sustainability*, not absolute size.

### The Trade Structure for Distressed Sovereign Debt

For institutional investors who can access distressed sovereign bonds directly:

**Entry conditions (all three required):**
1. Bond price below 20 cents on the dollar
2. IMF program announced or near announcement
3. Country has strategic importance (either geopolitically or to major creditors) that makes total loss politically unacceptable

**Position sizing:** Distressed sovereign bonds can go to zero. Use 1-3% of portfolio maximum, not concentrated positions. The 350% return on notional for SLSD sounds attractive until you remember 10% of notional is 90% loss from par — position sizing must account for the par-to-trough path, not just the trough-to-recovery path.

**Exit:** Target the restructured recovery level (published in the restructuring term sheet). Do not try to hold for "beyond restructuring" appreciation on short maturities — the new instruments typically have limited upside as the country will still be in fiscal adjustment mode.

### A Note on the External Debt / Export Ratio as a Signal System

The most important quantitative takeaway from Sri Lanka is the external debt-to-exports ratio. Here's the math one more time, cleanly:

**External debt / annual exports** = how many years of total export earnings would be needed to repay all foreign debt (with no spending on imports or anything else)

- Below 100%: comfortable (country earns more in exports annually than it owes in foreign debt)
- 100-200%: elevated, watch closely
- 200-300%: stressed, active monitoring required
- Above 300%: crisis territory unless there are very large non-export FX inflows (remittances, tourism, FDI)

Sri Lanka at 392% was in unambiguous crisis territory. The signal was visible in 2020, two years before the crisis peaked.

**The companion metric** is the debt service coverage ratio: annual debt service (interest + principal due) / annual current account receipts (exports + services + transfers). When this exceeds 30-35%, debt service is consuming such a large share of foreign income that any shock — a commodity price move, a tourism disruption — is enough to tip the country into crisis. Sri Lanka's ratio exceeded 40% by 2021.
