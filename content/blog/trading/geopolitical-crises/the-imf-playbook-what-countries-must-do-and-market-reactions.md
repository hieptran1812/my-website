> **TL;DR** — IMF program approval is one of the most reliable sovereign bond buy signals in emerging markets, producing 100-150bp of spread tightening on announcement day and 5-10% equity rally in the first month — but 30% of programs go off-track within 12 months, creating a second tradeable event around quarterly compliance reviews.
>
> - IMF toolkit: Stand-By Arrangement (SBA) for emergency liquidity; Extended Fund Facility (EFF) for structural reform; both require fiscal consolidation, rate hikes, FX devaluation, and subsidy removal.
> - 2022-24 crisis wave: Pakistan (\$3B SBA, 9th program), Egypt (\$8B EFF + 40% EGP devaluation), Ghana (first SSA default since 2008, then \$3B program), Sri Lanka (\$2.9B EFF with creditor haircuts).
> - The political cost: IMF austerity caused government collapses in Sri Lanka (Rajapaksa resignation), mass protests in Egypt and Pakistan, and political crises in Ecuador and Argentina.
> - Trading playbook: buy EM sovereign bonds on IMF approval day, hold through first tranche disbursement, watch quarterly compliance review dates, exit when program goes off-track.

On June 29, 2023, Pakistan's economy was hours from complete collapse. Foreign exchange reserves had fallen to \$3.5 billion — barely three weeks of import cover. The rupee had lost 40% of its value in 2023 alone. International bond markets were effectively closed; Pakistan's 10-year Eurobond was yielding over 30%. A default on \$1 billion in Eurobond payments was days away.

At 11:47 PM Islamabad time, the International Monetary Fund announced that its Executive Board had approved a \$3 billion Stand-By Arrangement for Pakistan. The nine-month package was small — barely enough to cover immediate obligations — but it was the signal the market had been waiting for.

The next morning, Pakistani government bonds rallied. The rupee stabilized. International banks that had refused to transact with Pakistani counterparties began cautiously re-engaging. Pakistan's stock market (PSX) opened up 3.1% on the announcement day and continued rallying for the following three weeks.

This is the IMF stamp of approval effect — and it is one of the most reliable short-term trading signals in emerging markets.

But here's the uncomfortable truth that the stamp-of-approval narrative omits: Pakistan was completing its **ninth** IMF program. The country has been in IMF programs for 22 of the past 30 years. The pattern — crisis, IMF rescue, brief stabilization, structural problems persist, next crisis, next program — is not unique to Pakistan. It describes Argentina, Egypt, Ghana, and dozens of other chronic IMF clients.

Understanding why IMF programs trigger market rallies, why so many ultimately fail, and how to trade the quarterly compliance cycle is essential for anyone investing in frontier and emerging market debt.

## Foundations: What the IMF Actually Does

The International Monetary Fund is a 190-member institution created at the 1944 Bretton Woods conference to prevent the beggar-thy-neighbor exchange rate manipulation and competitive devaluations of the 1930s. Its core mandate is to promote international monetary cooperation and provide balance of payments support to countries in crisis.

**What the IMF is not:** It is not a development bank (that's the World Bank). It is not a charity. It is not a lender of last resort in the central bank sense. It is a credit cooperative whose members contribute quota-based capital, from which distressed members can borrow — at below-market but above-concessional rates.

**How IMF programs work:**

When a country approaches the IMF for support, the process follows a consistent structure:

1. **Article IV Consultation:** The IMF conducts an annual assessment of each member's economy. When crisis signals appear, IMF staff engage more intensively.

2. **Negotiations:** IMF staff negotiate "conditionality" — the policy measures the country must implement in exchange for the loan. These negotiations can take weeks to months, involve the Finance Minister and central bank governor, and produce a detailed "Letter of Intent" and "Memorandum of Economic and Financial Policies" (MEFP).

3. **Board Approval:** The IMF's 24-member Executive Board (weighted by quota contributions; the US has 17% of votes — enough to veto) votes on the program. Board approval triggers the "stamp of approval" market response.

4. **Disbursement:** Funds are disbursed in tranches tied to quarterly reviews. The first tranche is released immediately on program approval; subsequent tranches require passing each quarterly review.

5. **Quarterly Reviews:** IMF staff assess compliance with conditionality targets — fiscal deficit, primary surplus, FX reserves, monetary aggregates. Failure to meet targets can result in "off-track" status, which freezes further disbursements until the country gets back on track (usually requiring new negotiations).

**The two main program types:**

**Stand-By Arrangement (SBA):** The classic emergency program. Duration: 12-24 months. Focus: immediate balance of payments stabilization, often requiring rapid fiscal adjustment and currency devaluation. Pakistan's June 2023 program was an SBA — designed to bridge the country to a more comprehensive program or to address a specific liquidity crunch.

**Extended Fund Facility (EFF):** The structural reform program. Duration: 3-4 years. Focus: deep structural reforms — pension reform, SOE privatization, energy subsidy removal, financial sector reform. Sri Lanka's \$2.9 billion program (March 2023) was an EFF because the country's problems were structural (persistent fiscal deficits funded by money printing) rather than merely cyclical.

The difference matters for markets: SBAs are about buying time; EFFs are about changing the economic model. EFFs take longer to implement, face stronger political resistance, and consequently have higher off-track rates.

## The IMF Conditionality Menu

![IMF conditionality stack from fiscal consolidation through structural reform to social protection floor](/imgs/blogs/the-imf-playbook-what-countries-must-do-and-market-reactions-7.png)

The conditionality stack shows the layered structure of modern IMF programs. What the stack reveals:

**The fiscal consolidation layer is non-negotiable.** Every IMF program since 1945 has required fiscal deficit reduction. The magnitude varies: an SBA for a country in acute crisis might require 3-4% of GDP in deficit reduction in year one; an EFF might spread 6% of GDP in deficit reduction over three years. The mechanism is always the same — revenue increases (tax reform, compliance improvement), spending cuts (capital expenditure, subsidies, public sector wages), or both.

**The monetary stabilization layer is the political flashpoint.** Rate hikes to achieve positive real interest rates (when inflation is 40-80%, as in Egypt and Pakistan in 2022-23, this means rates must be extremely high) immediately increase the cost of household and business borrowing. FX liberalization — allowing the currency to devalue to its market-clearing level — is the single most politically visible conditionality measure because it instantly increases the cost of imported goods.

**The structural reform layer is where programs fail.** Pension reform, SOE privatization, labor market liberalization — these require legislation, which requires political coalition building, which takes time and faces powerful vested interests. The IMF's quarterly reviews cannot easily verify structural reform progress the way they can verify fiscal accounts. This is why programs go off-track: structural reforms slip while fiscal measures (which are more easily measured) may be technically on target.

**The social protection floor is the IMF's evolution.** After the catastrophic social consequences of 1990s "Washington Consensus" programs — which caused enormous hardship through rapid austerity without social safety nets — the IMF has progressively strengthened social protection requirements. Since 2015's Integrated Strategy for Revenue and Social Protection (ISRP), IMF programs formally require minimum spending on health, education, and social transfers. The practical effect is limited (the floor is usually inadequate to offset the impact of subsidy removal), but it reflects the IMF's recognition that politically unsustainable programs ultimately fail.

## The 2022-2024 Crisis Wave: Four Case Studies

### Pakistan: The Perpetual Program

Pakistan's relationship with the IMF is the most instructive in the world. Nine programs over 30 years, each providing temporary stabilization before the structural problems — chronic fiscal deficits financed by external borrowing, energy subsidies that drain the fiscal position, a narrow tax base, and political cycles that undo consolidation between programs — reassert themselves.

The June 2023 SBA was different from previous programs in its explicit acknowledgment of the pattern. The IMF's staff report was unusually direct: Pakistan's fiscal consolidation gains were repeatedly undone by post-election stimulus packages (see the Political Business Cycle post in this series), energy sector circular debt had reached 2.5 trillion PKR (\$8.9bn), and structural reforms had "lagged considerably" across all previous programs.

**Market response:**
- Pakistan Eurobond spreads: approximately 2,800bp pre-announcement → 2,300bp day of announcement = 500bp tightening (unusually large because the market had priced near-certain default)
- PKR/USD: 285 pre-program → 285-295 range post-program (stabilized but not recovered — \$3bn is very small relative to Pakistan's debt service needs)
- PSX 100 Index: +3.1% on announcement day, +8% over first month

**The conditionality:** Pakistan committed to ending all energy subsidies (estimated at 1.5 trillion PKR annually), raising electricity and gas tariffs to cost-recovery levels, allowing the PKR to float freely (which meant a further 10-15% devaluation), and implementing a "Super Tax" on large corporations to boost revenue.

**Political cost:** Within months of the program, electricity and gas bills doubled for Pakistani households. The resulting political backlash contributed to instability in the government of Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif. The pattern of IMF programs requiring measures that undermine the political party that signs them is a recurring feature of Pakistan's crisis cycle.

### Egypt: \$8 Billion EFF and the Mandatory 40% Devaluation

Egypt's December 2022 EFF program is a case study in the IMF's FX devaluation requirement and its consequences.

The context: Egypt had pegged the Egyptian Pound (EGP) at approximately 18-19 per USD for most of 2022, despite needing to import approximately \$80 billion annually and having increasingly limited USD reserves. The peg was unsustainable — a black market had developed where the actual exchange rate was 25-27 EGP/USD. Hot money flows that had come in during the COVID period (attracted by high EGP yields) were reversing as the Fed hiked rates.

The IMF's conditions for the \$8 billion EFF included:
- Immediate float of the EGP (which means accepting market-clearing devaluation)
- Reduction of energy subsidies (Egypt spends approximately 3% of GDP on fuel subsidies)
- SOE privatization (selling stakes in state-owned entities to private investors)
- Fiscal consolidation equivalent to 3% of GDP over the program period

**The devaluation math:**

The IMF program was announced in November-December 2022. The EGP moved from 19.5 to approximately 25.5 in December 2022 — a 31% devaluation. By early 2024, after further pressure, EGP had reached 30.9 to the USD, a further move of 21%.

Total cumulative devaluation from program announcement to stabilization: approximately 58%. For Egyptian households, this meant that the cost of imported food, medicine, and consumer goods increased by approximately 40-60% in two years — despite fuel subsidies partially offsetting energy cost increases.

**Market response:**
- Egyptian sovereign Eurobond spreads: approximately 900bp pre-announcement → 750bp post-announcement = 150bp tightening
- EGX30 (Egyptian equities): +7% in first month as markets re-rated Egyptian stocks on reduced currency overvaluation risk

**The political cost:** Egypt experienced significant social pressure from rising prices. The Sisi government managed this through tight media control and security apparatus, but economic discontent is visibly elevated. This illustrates how authoritarian governments can implement IMF conditionality more completely (no political opposition with voice) but at the cost of suppressed social feedback that may materialize later.

### Ghana: First Sub-Saharan African Default Since 2008

Ghana's December 2022 default on its external debt (approximately \$13 billion in Eurobonds and bilateral debt) was the first Sub-Saharan African sovereign default since Seychelles in 2008. The combination of COVID revenue loss, commodity price shocks (cocoa and gold are Ghana's major exports), rising US interest rates (which made dollar-denominated debt refinancing impossibly expensive), and persistent fiscal deficits created an unmanageable debt trajectory.

The \$3 billion IMF EFF program, approved in May 2023, came with Ghana's "Domestic Debt Exchange Programme" (DDEP) — a restructuring of approximately GHS 137 billion in domestic government bonds that imposed significant haircuts on local banks and pension funds. International creditors faced their own restructuring under the G20 Common Framework.

**Market response:**
- Ghana's Eurobond (2032 maturity): recovered from approximately 38 cents on the dollar pre-program to approximately 55 cents post-program = approximately +45%
- Note: This is a distressed debt recovery trade, not a normal spread-tightening trade

**The creditor restructuring dynamic:** Ghana's default created a complex multi-stakeholder negotiation that is still ongoing. The "G20 Common Framework" — designed to coordinate between Paris Club creditors, China (a major bilateral lender to Ghana), and commercial creditors — has been criticized for its slow pace. The IMF program proceeds but external debt service is suspended pending restructuring completion.

**Trading Ghana:** Buying Ghanaian Eurobonds at 35-38 cents (distressed prices in late 2022/early 2023) and holding through program approval produced approximately 40-50% gains on the bonds. This is not a spread trade — it's a distressed debt recovery trade with specific legal and restructuring risks.

### Sri Lanka: Political Collapse and EFF Recovery

Sri Lanka's 2022 crisis was perhaps the most dramatic in the 2022-24 wave: a complete collapse of foreign exchange reserves (briefly below \$1 billion), fuel and medicine shortages, massive public protests that forced President Gotabaya Rajapaksa to flee the country in July 2022, and the island's first sovereign default since independence.

The \$2.9 billion 4-year EFF (March 2023) came with:
- Fiscal consolidation target of 8.5% of GDP primary surplus by 2025 (one of the most aggressive in recent IMF history)
- Comprehensive debt restructuring including both bilateral (China, India, Japan) and commercial creditors
- Energy pricing reform (electricity prices increased 30% immediately)
- Revenue measures including VAT rate increases

**Political consequences:** The government that signed the IMF program — Ranil Wickremesinghe, who became president after Rajapaksa fled — was implementing measures so politically toxic that his party lost the September 2024 presidential election decisively. The new president (Anura Kumara Dissanayake from a left-wing alliance) initially questioned IMF program terms but ultimately maintained the program after recognizing the limited alternatives.

**Market response:**
- Sri Lanka Eurobond recovery: from approximately 35-40 cents (pre-program) to 60+ cents post-program
- Sri Lanka was effectively excluded from international capital markets until the debt restructuring completes

## The IMF Stamp of Approval Effect: Quantified

![IMF program types market impact: spread tightening equity rally and off-track compliance risk](/imgs/blogs/the-imf-playbook-what-countries-must-do-and-market-reactions-6.png)

The data across 35 IMF programs from 2000-2024 shows the stamp-of-approval effect is largest for SBAs (which address acute crises) and moderate for EFFs (which are more anticipated). Flexible Credit Lines (FCLs) for pre-qualified countries produce smaller immediate effects because markets already knew the backstop was available.

The off-track rate is the critical risk. Approximately 30% of programs miss at least one quarterly benchmark within the first 12 months. The off-track rate varies by:
- **Fiscal targets:** Lower off-track rate — governments can usually hit fiscal targets through emergency measures
- **Structural benchmarks:** Higher off-track rate — legislative reforms, SOE privatizations, and pension changes face political resistance that delays implementation
- **FX targets:** Variable — depends on reserve accumulation (which depends on export performance and capital flows)

#### Worked example:

**The Pakistan SBA Trade (June 2023)**

Entry signal: IMF Executive Board approval on June 29, 2023

Step 1 — Assess the opportunity:
- Pakistan 2032 Eurobond yield: approximately 38% (price approximately 52 cents on dollar)
- Pakistan CDS 5-year: approximately 4,500bp
- Post-program target yield: if Pakistan avoids default and completes the program, fair value yield is approximately 12-15% = price 70-80 cents

Expected value calculation:
- Probability of program success (Pakistan reaches IMF compliance through 9 months): 65% based on historical SBA completion rates for repeat program countries
- Expected gain if success: 52 cents → 70 cents = +34.6%
- Probability of default/off-track requiring restructuring: 35%
- Expected loss if default: 52 cents → 35 cents (restructuring price) = -32.7%

Expected value: 0.65 × 34.6% + 0.35 × (-32.7%) = 22.5% - 11.4% = **+11.1% expected return**

Step 2 — Position sizing (using fractional Kelly):
Full Kelly suggests approximately 30% of portfolio (EV/variance ratio). Use half-Kelly = 15%.

On a \$20M EM debt portfolio: \$3,000,000 in Pakistan 2032 bonds at 52 cents
= approximately \$5.77M face value of bonds

Step 3 — Outcome:
By September 2023 (3 months later), Pakistan 2032 bond had recovered to approximately 62 cents as:
- First quarterly review passed
- IMF disbursed second tranche
- PKR stabilized

P&L: \$3,000,000 × (62-52)/52 = \$576,923 gain = +19.2% on investment in 3 months

Step 4 — Exit trigger: Watch the December 2023 second quarterly review. If Pakistan misses fiscal targets (which was a real risk given election-year spending pressures), exit immediately.

**Actual outcome:** Pakistan went to elections in February 2024 (delayed from November 2023). The IMF program went through several tense negotiations but ultimately remained on track. The bond recovered to 65 cents by Q2 2024.

#### Worked example:

**Greece's Three-Bailout Trade (2010-2018)**

Greece's sovereign debt crisis is the most extensively documented in modern financial history. The IMF-EU-ECB "Troika" provided three successive programs totaling approximately \$360 billion EUR.

The Greek 10-year yield trajectory tells the story of IMF program market dynamics:
- 2010: Pre-program yield 12.1% → first bailout → yield peaks 24.5% in 2011 (market doesn't believe the program will work) 
- 2012: Second bailout + PSI haircuts (private sector involvement — first major EU sovereign debt restructuring) → yield spikes to 38% before restructuring closes
- September 2012: Draghi "whatever it takes" speech — transformative event that was not an IMF action but supported the IMF program's viability
- 2015: Tsipras-Troika standoff → capital controls → yield briefly back to 18%
- 2018: ESM program exit — the first time Greece had clear market access since 2010; yield 4%

![Greece 10-year bond yield through the three IMF-EU bailout programs and recovery](/imgs/blogs/the-imf-playbook-what-countries-must-do-and-market-reactions-2.png)

The Greek trade was brutal for anyone who bought Greek bonds at the 2010 bailout on the "stamp of approval" thesis:
- 2010 bailout buyers (at yield ~12%, price ~88): saw bonds fall to price 30-40 cents during the 2011-2012 crisis
- 2012 PSI participants: took 53.5% haircut on face value

The lesson: IMF programs in countries with **debt sustainability concerns** (where even with adjustment, debt/GDP remains unsustainable) require creditor involvement that can wipe out bondholders. The "stamp of approval" trade only works cleanly when the IMF assessment says debt is sustainable *with the program*. Greece in 2010 was almost certainly not sustainable even with the program — a fact the IMF itself later acknowledged in its 2013 "mea culpa" on Greece.

## Argentina: The Perpetual Case Study

![Argentina EMBI sovereign spread showing the default cycle and IMF program impact](/imgs/blogs/the-imf-playbook-what-countries-must-do-and-market-reactions-4.png)

Argentina's nine sovereign defaults and perpetual IMF relationship is the most complex case in the dataset. The 2020 default (ninth) was followed by a debt restructuring that gave bondholders approximately 55 cents on the dollar. The November 2023 election of Javier Milei — who promised dollarization, chainsaw-style government spending cuts, and IMF compliance — produced an extraordinary 40%+ rally in Argentine assets:
- MERVAL (equity index): +40% in USD terms in one month
- Argentine Eurobonds: from 25-30 cents to 45-50 cents in H2 2023
- Argentina EMBI spread: 2,100bp → 1,500bp

By June 2024, with Milei's fiscal shock therapy showing early results (\$6.4bn peso fiscal surplus in 5 months, vs. -2.9bn a year earlier), the spread had fallen to 900bp.

The Milei trade is the purest expression of the IMF-stamp-of-approval dynamic: markets priced in the IMF credibility anchor before the formal program was even negotiated, because Milei's explicit commitment to IMF compliance served as a credibility substitute.

## The IMF Approval Trade: The Before and After

![IMF stamp of approval effect on sovereign spreads and equities before and after program announcement](/imgs/blogs/the-imf-playbook-what-countries-must-do-and-market-reactions-5.png)

The before-after framework illustrates the mechanics of the market reaction. The key insight: the IMF doesn't fix the fundamental problem (the country still has a balance of payments deficit, a fiscal deficit, and structural weaknesses). What it provides is:
1. **Liquidity bridge:** Direct USD disbursement that extends the runway before the next crisis
2. **Credibility signal:** The IMF's stamp tells other creditors "the IMF team has assessed this country and believes the program is viable" — which unlocks bilateral and multilateral lending
3. **Coordination mechanism:** Once the IMF program is in place, the G7 Paris Club and other bilateral creditors coordinate relief (this is why the G20 Common Framework exists)
4. **Policy anchor:** IMF conditionality provides political cover for governments to implement painful measures ("we have to do this for the IMF") that might otherwise be impossible domestically

None of these factors address the fundamental structural problems. That's why programs recur.

## Common Misconceptions About IMF Programs

**Misconception 1: "The IMF imposes austerity on unwilling countries."**
This framing, common in left-wing political analysis, misunderstands the basic mechanics. Countries approach the IMF voluntarily when they have run out of other options. The IMF doesn't force anyone to sign a program. The conditionality reflects the IMF's assessment of what's needed for fiscal sustainability — which often aligns with what the country's own technocrats believe but couldn't implement without external political cover.

**Misconception 2: "IMF programs always work."**
30% of programs go off-track within 12 months. Among programs for countries with serious structural problems (repeat clients like Pakistan, Argentina, Egypt), the failure rate is higher. IMF programs provide a liquidity bridge; they cannot fix political economy problems, vested interests, or structural competitiveness issues.

**Misconception 3: "The IMF stamp of approval trade always works."**
It works reliably when debt sustainability is assured *with* the program. It fails (sometimes catastrophically) when the debt burden is too large to be sustainable even with adjustment — as in Greece (2010), Sri Lanka, and Zambia. In those cases, the stamp-of-approval rally is a trap because the eventual restructuring will wipe out bondholders.

**Misconception 4: "IMF austerity hurts growth permanently."**
The empirical evidence is mixed. Short-run GDP contraction is nearly universal (fiscal consolidation removes stimulus, rate hikes compress credit). But the long-run growth effects of IMF programs are debated. South Korea (1997 IMF program), which implemented conditionality rapidly and completely, subsequently achieved extraordinary growth. Argentina (multiple programs, repeated partial compliance) has not. The growth outcome depends primarily on whether the structural reforms are implemented fully and whether the political system can maintain the reform trajectory.

**Misconception 5: "China's lending is an alternative to the IMF."**
China has become a major bilateral lender to developing countries through the Belt and Road Initiative. Chinese lending terms are less transparent, don't require the same conditionality, and initially seemed to offer a "no strings attached" alternative. But the Zambia and Sri Lanka cases show that Chinese bilateral debt creates its own restructuring complications (China has been slow to participate in the G20 Common Framework), and no Chinese-led institution provides the IMF's "stamp of approval" signal. Countries need IMF credibility even when they have Chinese financing.

## The Quarterly Compliance Trade

The most sophisticated IMF-related trade is not the stamp-of-approval entry — it's the quarterly compliance cycle trade.

**The framework:**
Every IMF program has quarterly reviews (or semi-annual for some smaller programs). Reviews assess whether the country has met:
- Quantitative performance criteria (QPCs): Fiscal deficit, primary balance, net international reserves, net domestic assets — hard targets with specific numbers
- Structural benchmarks (SBs): Legislative actions, regulatory changes, institutional reforms — softer targets with more interpretive flexibility

**The trading calendar:**
If a country has a program approved in January, the quarterly reviews typically fall in:
- April (Q1 review): First real test of compliance
- July (Q2 review): Midpoint assessment
- October (Q3 review): Often the hardest review (pre-election spending pressures in many countries)
- January (Q4 review): Year-end assessment, sets up for next year's disbursement

**The trade before each review:**
- If recent data (monthly fiscal balance reports, central bank reserve data) shows on-track compliance: Buy sovereign bonds 2-3 weeks before the review, hold through the approval announcement
- If data shows off-track risk: Sell bonds 2-3 weeks before the review, or buy CDS protection

**The informational advantage:** IMF Article IV Consultation documents, program documents, and quarterly review letters are public. Monthly fiscal data is published by finance ministries. Central bank reserve data is published by the IMF's International Reserves and Foreign Currency Liquidity template. A trader who systematically tracks these data sources can anticipate quarterly review outcomes weeks before the IMF press release.

#### Worked example:

**Egypt Quarterly Review Trade (2023)**

Egypt's EFF program was approved in December 2022. The Q1 2023 review was scheduled for April 2023.

Data tracking through Q1 2023:
- Egypt's primary fiscal balance: On track (revenue collection improving, spending controls in place)
- EGP exchange rate: Stable at 30.7 after the December 2022 devaluation — no further pressure
- Net international reserves: Rising from \$34bn (Dec 2022) to \$34.8bn (March 2023) — on track

Entry: March 20, 2023 (3 weeks before expected review)
- Bought Egypt 2025 Eurobond at yield 12.5% (price approximately 90.5 cents)

IMF confirms Q1 review completion and second tranche disbursement: April 14, 2023

Exit: April 21, 2023 (one week after confirmation)
- Egypt 2025 Eurobond: yield 11.2% (price approximately 93 cents)

P&L: (93 - 90.5) / 90.5 = +2.8% in 4 weeks = approximately 36% annualized

**Key insight:** This is a "harvesting the compliance premium" trade — systematic alpha from monitoring observable compliance indicators ahead of formal IMF reviews.

#### Worked example:

**Sri Lanka Distressed Debt Recovery (2022-2023)**

Sri Lanka defaulted in April 2022. The EFF was approved in March 2023.

**Pre-default assessment (November 2022):**

Sri Lanka 2030 Eurobond: trading at 42 cents on the dollar (yield approximately 18%)

Step 1 — Assess sovereign restructuring scenarios:
- Optimistic (50% recovery, IMF program succeeds, restructuring completes by 2024): Final bond value ~55-60 cents
- Base case (40% recovery, typical frontier sovereign restructuring): ~45-50 cents
- Pessimistic (30% recovery, prolonged political instability, debt restructuring drags 3+ years): ~30-35 cents

Step 2 — Probability weight:
- P(optimistic) = 35%, P(base) = 45%, P(pessimistic) = 20%

Expected value: 0.35×57 + 0.45×47 + 0.20×32 = 19.95 + 21.15 + 6.40 = 47.5 cents

Buy at 42 cents: Expected gain = 47.5 - 42 = +5.5 cents = +13.1% expected return on distressed purchase

Step 3 — Position limit: Cap at 3% of portfolio (distressed sovereign debt is illiquid and has binary default risk)

Actual outcome: By June 2024, Sri Lanka bonds were trading at 57-60 cents as:
- IMF quarterly reviews passed
- Bilateral restructuring with India and Japan completed
- China restructuring agreement in principle reached

P&L: 42 cents → 58 cents = +38% in 18 months
