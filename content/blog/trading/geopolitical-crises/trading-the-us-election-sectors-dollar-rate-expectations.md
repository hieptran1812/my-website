Election night in November 2024 ran late. By 2 a.m. Eastern, the Associated Press called Pennsylvania for Donald Trump, and within twenty minutes the futures market told the whole story of what four years of policy expectations looked like in compressed form. S&P 500 futures surged above their prior close. Tesla (TSLA) — flagged in prediction markets for weeks as the ultimate Trump adjacency play via Elon Musk's campaign involvement — opened the next morning up 14.7%. The iShares Global Clean Energy ETF (ICLN) opened down 5.1%, a foretaste of what the following seven weeks would deliver. The Mexican peso (MXN) lost 3% against the dollar in the first twelve hours, and within six weeks had given up more than 8%.

None of this was random. Traders who understood the structural mechanics of US election-driven market moves — the sector rotation playbook, the dollar reaction function, the VIX collapse post-result — had been building positions for 45 to 60 days. They were not guessing who would win. They were constructing exposure that paid regardless of outcome if correctly sized, or they were expressing a directional lean if they trusted the polling and prediction market consensus enough. Either way, they were working from a framework, not a hunch.

This post builds that framework from scratch. We start with the foundations — why elections move markets at all — then work through the specific mechanics of sector rotation, dollar dynamics, and rate repricing. We examine the presidential cycle in equity markets across seven decades. We end with a concrete positioning playbook and four detailed worked examples with real numbers. The 2024 election case study appears throughout as the living evidence.

## Foundations: Understanding US Election Market Dynamics

Before we can trade around elections, we need to understand precisely why and how elections move markets. This is not self-evident. Markets are forward-looking, and in theory they should price in expected policy as information about the likely winner accumulates through polling and prediction markets. In practice, three structural forces create persistent mispricings and tradeable dislocations.

**The uncertainty premium.** Markets price risk as a function of the variance of outcomes, not just the expected outcome. In the 60-to-90-day window before an election, uncertainty about policy is genuinely elevated even when one candidate leads in the polls. A 70% probability of a Trump win still means a 30% probability of a Harris win — and those two outcomes imply very different policy environments for energy, healthcare, and financial regulation. That residual variance gets priced through the options market as a higher implied volatility, which shows up in an elevated VIX. The moment the election resolves, variance collapses. Even if the "expected" candidate wins, uncertainty about the *exact* policy path falls dramatically, and the VIX drops. This is the core mechanic.

**Sector-specific policy sensitivity.** Different sectors of the economy face dramatically different regulatory, tax, and subsidy environments depending on which party controls the White House and Congress. A pharmaceutical company's earnings are materially affected by whether the Inflation Reduction Act's drug price negotiation provisions are expanded or repealed. A defense contractor's backlog is directly linked to the size of the Pentagon budget, which a Republican administration historically increases more aggressively than a Democratic one. An oil and gas driller's capital expenditure plans depend on whether new federal leases are available. These are not marginal effects — they drive 20–30% return spreads between winning and losing sectors across a four-year term.

**The dollar's reaction function.** The US dollar is simultaneously a risk-off safe haven, a carry trade vehicle, and a policy barometer. Election outcomes move the dollar through three channels at once: the expected fiscal stance of the incoming administration (larger deficits are modestly dollar-weakening long term but dollar-strengthening short term via higher rate expectations), the expected trade policy (tariffs are initially dollar-strengthening because they reduce import demand), and the global risk appetite signal (a US political shock that raises global uncertainty is dollar-strengthening because investors flee to dollar assets). These channels can reinforce or partially offset each other, which is why the dollar's post-election move varies more than equity sectors' moves.

**Rate repricing.** Federal Reserve policy is officially independent of electoral politics, but the Fed's mandate is responsive to inflation and employment, and the party in power shapes fiscal policy that feeds into both. A Republican administration that enacts large tax cuts and tariffs sends an inflationary signal that pushes Fed Funds futures toward higher-for-longer expectations. A Democratic administration that expands Medicaid and green energy subsidies while raising corporate taxes sends a different signal — potentially lower growth but also potentially lower inflation. The bond market reprices this immediately after an election result.

These four forces — uncertainty premium, sector sensitivity, dollar reaction, and rate repricing — are not independent. They interact through capital flows, position unwinding, and momentum. Understanding the sequence (Figure 1 below) is the first step to building a coherent playbook.

## The Political Moves: Policy Agendas by Party and Candidate Type

Markets do not respond to party labels in the abstract. They respond to specific policy promises and the probability those promises become law. That probability is a function of which chamber of Congress the president's party controls, the size of any legislative majority, and the history of that administration's relationship with Congress.

That said, there are durable, historically consistent policy differences between the parties that recur across administrations. These are the ones worth encoding in your trading framework.

### The Republican Policy Package for Markets

The modern Republican policy package — consistent across the Reagan, Bush (both), and Trump administrations — centers on four market-relevant pillars.

**Tax policy.** Republican administrations cut corporate and individual income taxes. The 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act under Trump's first term reduced the corporate tax rate from 35% to 21%, producing an immediate 10–15% uplift in S&P 500 earnings estimates. The 2025 Trump administration's "One Big Beautiful Bill" extended those cuts and proposed further reductions. For equity investors, lower corporate taxes directly increase after-tax earnings, supporting higher valuations. This is a broad market tailwind, but it particularly benefits large-cap domestically focused companies that pay near the statutory rate.

**Deregulation.** Republican administrations systematically reduce regulatory burden across banking (Basel III rollback, reduced stress-test frequency), energy (environmental permitting, NEPA reform, approval of LNG terminals and pipelines), and healthcare (FDA approval acceleration, telehealth deregulation). For the financial sector, reduced regulatory overhead is an earnings tailwind. For energy, it accelerates development.

**Defense spending.** Republican administrations historically propose higher Pentagon baseline budgets. The Reagan buildup, the post-9/11 defense expansion under Bush, and Trump's first-term \$750 billion request followed by an \$886 billion ask in 2025 all reflect this pattern. Defense contractors — Lockheed Martin (LMT), Raytheon/RTX (RTX), Northrop Grumman (NOC), General Dynamics (GD), L3Harris (LHX) — see their revenue backlog expand directly when defense appropriations rise.

**Trade protectionism.** Both Trump terms imposed broad tariffs — Section 232 on steel and aluminum, Section 301 on China, and the sweeping "Liberation Day" tariffs of April 2025. Tariffs are complicated for equity markets: they can be inflationary (raising input costs), supportive of domestic producers in protected industries, and damaging to export-oriented sectors that face retaliation. They are also immediately USD-positive because they reduce import demand and therefore reduce the supply of dollars flowing abroad.

### The Democratic Policy Package for Markets

The modern Democratic policy package centers on different pillars with different market implications.

**Clean energy and climate.** The Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) of 2022 — the signature legislative achievement of the Biden administration — allocated \$369 billion to clean energy subsidies, tax credits for electric vehicles, and manufacturing incentives for solar panels, wind turbines, and battery storage. This directly benefited ETFs like ICLN (iShares Global Clean Energy), QCLN (First Trust NASDAQ Clean Edge), SEDG (SolarEdge), ENPH (Enphase), and NEE (NextEra Energy). Trump's 2025 administration began targeting IRA provisions for repeal, which explains the immediate post-election selloff in clean energy names.

**Healthcare spending expansion.** Democratic administrations generally seek to expand Medicaid, increase ACA subsidies, and broaden prescription drug coverage. This is a complex signal for healthcare stocks: expanded coverage increases volume for hospitals (HCA, THC) and managed care organizations (UNH, CVS), but drug price negotiation provisions (as enacted in the IRA) suppress pharmaceutical margins. The net effect varies by sub-sector.

**Infrastructure investment.** The Bipartisan Infrastructure Law (2021) and the CHIPS Act (2022) represented a Democratic administration's investment in physical and digital infrastructure. ETFs like PAVE (Global X US Infrastructure), IDRV (iShares Self-Driving EV), and individual names like Caterpillar (CAT), Vulcan Materials (VMC), and Martin Marietta (MLM) benefited from the long pipeline of infrastructure spending.

**Regulation and antitrust.** Democratic administrations have been more aggressive on antitrust enforcement (FTC under Lina Khan, DOJ under Merrick Garland), financial regulation (CFPB under Rohit Chopra), and environmental enforcement. For large technology platforms and financial institutions, this creates regulatory cost and acquisition constraint headwinds.

### Why "Gridlock" Is Also a Trade

Students of congressional dynamics know that the most market-favorable outcome in many election cycles is divided government — a president of one party, a Congress controlled by the other. When neither party can pass major legislation, tax rates stay stable, no new regulation is imposed, and the existing economic expansion can continue uninterrupted. The "gridlock is good" thesis has substantial historical support: the best S&P 500 returns since 1950 have occurred under divided government.

This creates a secondary election trade: identify the scenarios where gridlock is likely (Republican president, Democratic Senate, for example) and overweight the stability-benefiting sectors (consumer staples, utilities) relative to the high-policy-sensitivity sectors that need legislative action to fully realize their thesis.

## The Financial Channels: How Policy Reaches Market Mechanics

Policy promises are just words until translated into capital flows, earnings estimates, and rate expectations. The transmission mechanism from policy announcement to price move runs through five specific financial channels.

**Earnings estimate revision.** Equity analysts update their earnings models in response to expected tax, regulatory, and revenue changes. A corporate tax rate cut from 21% to 15% (as proposed in some 2025 Republican plans) would increase S&P 500 EPS by approximately \$20–25 (roughly 8–10% of forward earnings), all else equal. This drives immediate multiple re-rating. The fastest-moving analysts update within hours of a result; consensus estimate revisions follow over the next two to four weeks.

**Multiple expansion and contraction.** Beyond earnings, the price-to-earnings (P/E) multiple itself changes as a function of discount rate and risk perception. When policy certainty rises post-election, the equity risk premium falls, supporting multiple expansion even if earnings stay flat. When a result produces policy uncertainty (for example, a contested election), the risk premium rises, compressing multiples.

**Debt market repricing.** Corporate debt is directly sensitive to rate expectations, spread levels, and regulatory environment. Bank stocks (XLF) rally post-Republican-win partly because deregulation expands lending capacity and net interest margins — but also because Treasury yields often rise in "reflationary" Republican victory scenarios, benefiting banks' balance sheet income. Conversely, higher long-end rates hurt leveraged sectors like utilities and REITs.

**Currency translation effects.** For multinational companies reporting in USD, a stronger dollar reduces the USD value of overseas revenues. Post-2024 election, the dollar strengthened 3–4% against a broad basket within six weeks. For S&P 500 companies with significant overseas revenue (technology, consumer discretionary multinationals), this was a headwind to consensus estimates that took analysts several weeks to fully revise.

**Commodity price effects.** Energy policy directly moves oil and gas prices, which cascade through input costs for industrials, airlines, chemicals, and fertilizer companies. Trump-era "energy dominance" policies that accelerate US oil production are bearish for oil prices medium-term (more supply), but near-term uncertainty can cause oil to spike. The net effect on XLE (oil majors) is positive because tax and regulatory changes increase E&P profitability even as crude prices moderate.

## Sector-by-Sector Analysis: The ETF Playbook

Let us go sector by sector through the eight largest S&P 500 sectors, identify the key ETFs, and map historical election-driven return patterns.

### Defense and Aerospace (ITA, XAR)

**ETFs:** iShares US Aerospace and Defense (ITA), SPDR S&P Aerospace and Defense (XAR), Invesco Aerospace and Defense (PPA). Individual names: LMT (Lockheed Martin), RTX (Raytheon Technologies), NOC (Northrop Grumman), GD (General Dynamics), LHX (L3Harris Technologies), HII (Huntington Ingalls).

**Historical pattern:** Defense contractors are the most consistently Republican-win-positive sector in the market. The logic is mechanical — Pentagon budget proposals under Republican administrations average 4–6% real annual growth, versus 1–3% under Democratic administrations. Contractors with long-cycle programs (F-35, B-21, Columbia-class submarines, hypersonic missiles) build multi-year backlog that is largely immune to short-term volatility.

Post-2024 election, ITA gained +12% in the seven weeks from November 6 to December 31. LMT added +7%, RTX +9%, NOC +11%. The move was not just about defense budgets — the Trump administration's signaling on NATO "burden sharing" and potential Middle East escalation both supported demand for US weapons exports.

**The Democrat nuance:** Defense is not a pure partisan trade. Democratic administrations that face a deteriorating geopolitical environment (Obama after ISIS expansion, Biden after the Ukraine invasion) also increase defense spending significantly. The IRA itself included \$858 billion in national security spending. The better read: Republican wins produce larger *baseline* defense budgets and faster procurement approvals; Democrat wins can still produce defense tailwinds if the security environment demands it.

**Trading vehicle:** For a clean, liquid expression, ITA (AUM ~\$7bn, average daily volume ~\$300m) is preferred over XAR due to higher liquidity and tighter spreads. Options liquidity is reasonable — 30-day IV typically 20–25%.

### Energy and Oil and Gas (XLE, XOP)

**ETFs:** Energy Select Sector SPDR (XLE), SPDR S&P Oil and Gas Exploration and Production (XOP), iShares US Oil and Gas Exploration and Production (IEO). Individual names: XOM (Exxon), CVX (Chevron), COP (ConocoPhillips), SLB (Schlumberger/SLB), EOG (EOG Resources).

**Historical pattern:** Energy is the most directly policy-sensitive sector. Republican administrations approve more federal leases (offshore, ANWR, public land), reduce methane regulations, and accelerate pipeline permitting. XLE rose +8.4% in the seven weeks post-Trump-2024, though the sector underperformed the broader market partly because crude oil prices actually declined slightly on the "energy dominance" supply-expansion narrative.

The key distinction is between upstream (E&P: XOP, IEO) and integrated majors (XLE). Majors benefit from deregulation and tax incentives for capital investment. E&P companies benefit from lease availability. Midstream (Kinder Morgan, Williams Companies, MPLX) benefit from pipeline approvals.

**Post-Biden IRA rollback risk for clean energy:** The IRA did not primarily benefit XLE — it benefited ICLN, QCLN, SEDG, ENPH, NEE, and FSLR. The 2025 Republican attempt to unwind IRA clean energy tax credits created a windfall for conventional energy by removing a competitor subsidy. This is a structural trade worth monitoring across the entire Trump second term.

### Financials (XLF, KBE, KRE)

**ETFs:** Financial Select Sector SPDR (XLF), SPDR S&P Bank (KBE), SPDR S&P Regional Banking (KRE). Individual names: JPM (JPMorgan Chase), BAC (Bank of America), GS (Goldman Sachs), MS (Morgan Stanley), WFC (Wells Fargo), BRK.B (Berkshire Hathaway).

**Historical pattern:** Financials are perhaps the most reliably Republican-positive sector. The deregulation narrative — reduced stress-test frequency, relaxed Basel III capital requirements, neutered CFPB — directly boosts earnings through lower compliance costs and higher capital deployment flexibility. The post-2024 election XLF gain of +15.2% in seven weeks was the largest among major sector ETFs.

Higher rate expectations (Republican fiscal expansion is inflationary, pushing rates up) also help banks' net interest income. When the 10-year Treasury yield rises, bank lending margins expand, boosting earnings for rate-sensitive institutions like regional banks (KRE).

**The banking sub-sector play:** Large-cap banks (JPM, BAC, GS) benefit from reduced regulatory oversight. Regional banks (KRE) benefit additionally from relaxed Community Reinvestment Act requirements and reduced capital requirements for smaller institutions. A post-Republican-election entry into KRE over XLF captures both the deregulation story and the rate sensitivity in a tighter-focused vehicle.

### Healthcare (XLV, IBB, XBI)

**ETFs:** Health Care Select Sector SPDR (XLV), iShares Nasdaq Biotechnology (IBB), SPDR S&P Biotech (XBI). Individual names: UNH (UnitedHealth), JNJ (Johnson and Johnson), LLY (Eli Lilly), PFE (Pfizer), ABBV (AbbVie), AMGN (Amgen).

**Historical pattern:** Healthcare is the most ambiguous sector in the election playbook because both parties introduce risk and opportunity but in different sub-sectors.

Republican wins create uncertainty for Medicaid-expansion states (potentially negative for managed care organizations that have large Medicaid revenue exposure) but reduce drug pricing pressure (positive for large pharma). Democratic wins expand Medicaid enrollment (positive for MCOs) but impose drug price negotiation (negative for pharma).

Post-2024 election, XLV declined -2.1% in seven weeks — a mild underperformance rather than a significant selloff. The market was pricing in the risk to the ACA (which primarily hurts MCOs) against the relief on IRA drug price provisions (which was pharma-positive). These forces roughly offset at the sector level.

**The biotech differentiation:** IBB and XBI are more sensitive to FDA approval rates than to the ACA/drug pricing debate. Republican administrations historically have been faster on FDA approval timelines. XBI (small-cap biotech) is particularly sensitive to risk appetite and interest rate movements — higher rates compress the DCF-heavy valuations of early-stage biotech.

### Clean Energy (ICLN, QCLN, FAN, ACES)

**ETFs:** iShares Global Clean Energy (ICLN), First Trust NASDAQ Clean Edge Green Energy (QCLN), First Trust Global Wind Energy (FAN), ALPS Clean Energy (ACES). Individual names: NEE (NextEra Energy), ENPH (Enphase), SEDG (SolarEdge), FSLR (First Solar), PLUG (Plug Power), BE (Bloom Energy).

**Historical pattern:** Clean energy is the mirror image of fossil fuel energy in the election playbook. It benefits maximally from Democratic wins (IRA subsidies, regulatory tailwind, clean electricity standards) and faces maximum headwind from Republican wins (IRA rollback, removal of EV mandates, re-permitting of competing fossil fuel infrastructure).

Post-2024 Trump election: ICLN -15.3% in seven weeks, QCLN -12.8%, FSLR -20.1%, ENPH -18.4%, SEDG -22.0%. These were not panic moves without fundamental basis — they reflected genuine recalculation of the regulatory and subsidy environment. The Trump administration's 2025 "energy dominance" executive orders targeted solar panel tariff exemptions, EV tax credits, and offshore wind permitting.

**The IRA partial-rollback nuance:** Not all IRA clean energy provisions are equally vulnerable. Domestic manufacturing incentives (CHIPS Act-adjacent, supporting American solar panel factories) have bipartisan political support because they create manufacturing jobs in Republican districts. Pure consumer EV credits and offshore wind subsidies are more vulnerable. A sophisticated clean energy short in a Republican-win scenario would overweight FSLR (most exposed to ITC/tariff dynamics) and underweight domestic manufacturers with bipartisan political cover.

### Industrials and Infrastructure (XLI, PAVE, GII)

**ETFs:** Industrial Select Sector SPDR (XLI), Global X US Infrastructure Development (PAVE), SPDR S&P Global Infrastructure (GII). Individual names: CAT (Caterpillar), GE (GE Vernova), HON (Honeywell), MMM (3M), DE (Deere), ETN (Eaton).

**Historical pattern:** Industrials benefit from both parties' policy agendas, but through different channels. Republican tariffs on imported steel and aluminum directly support domestic steel producers (NUE, STLD) and heavy equipment manufacturers that compete with Chinese imports. Democratic infrastructure spending creates direct contract revenue for engineering and construction companies.

Post-2024 election, XLI gained +10.3% in seven weeks. The primary drivers were: (1) tariff protection for domestic manufacturers, (2) expectations of continued infrastructure contract pipeline from the already-enacted Bipartisan Infrastructure Law that was too advanced to be meaningfully scaled back, and (3) deregulation of manufacturing facilities.

**The tariff nuance for XLI:** Tariffs are not uniformly positive for industrials. Companies that use steel and aluminum as inputs (machinery makers, vehicle manufacturers, appliance producers) face higher input costs. The net effect depends on whether the company is a net beneficiary of tariff protection on its outputs or a net payer of tariffs on its inputs. Caterpillar's mining equipment exports, for example, face retaliatory tariffs from China — a headwind that partially offsets the domestic deregulation tailwind.

## Dollar Dynamics: The DXY Reaction Function Around Elections

The US dollar index (DXY) moves around elections through three overlapping channels: trade policy expectations, fiscal/monetary policy expectations, and risk sentiment. Understanding which channel dominates in a given election determines the dollar's direction.

![DXY dollar index reactions around the 2016, 2020, and 2024 US presidential elections](/imgs/blogs/trading-the-us-election-sectors-dollar-rate-expectations-7.png)

**The 2016 Trump election (November 8, 2016):** DXY moved from approximately 98.5 pre-election to 100.4 the day after the result, and then continued climbing to 103.2 by year-end. The market priced in: (1) large fiscal stimulus (tax cuts + infrastructure promises) → higher rate expectations → higher dollar; (2) tariff threat → reduced import demand → lower dollar supply. Channel 1 dominated. The 10-year Treasury yield surged from 1.83% pre-election to 2.60% by year-end, pulling capital into dollar assets.

**The 2020 Biden election (November 3, 2020):** DXY fell from approximately 94.0 pre-election to 92.5 the day after the result was called. Biden's expected agenda — larger fiscal stimulus (ultimately the \$1.9 trillion ARP) but also higher corporate taxes and regulatory costs — was net dollar-neutral to mildly negative because markets anticipated a less aggressive rate path than a Trump continuation. The ongoing COVID-era QE environment was the dominant dollar headwind throughout 2020.

**The 2024 Trump election (November 5, 2024):** DXY moved from approximately 103.8 pre-election to 105.1 the day after. By January 2025, DXY had climbed to 109.3. The primary driver was the tariff narrative — massive tariff threats imply dramatically reduced imports, which is USD-positive through the current account mechanism, and also imply higher US inflation, which implies higher Fed Funds for longer. The peso's 8% decline against the dollar over the subsequent seven weeks was the clearest expression of this tariff channel.

**The DXY pattern: what to watch for.** Three variables determine whether the dollar rallies or falls post-election:

1. **Tariff stance:** Aggressive tariff threats are USD-positive (reduces imports → reduces dollar outflows). This was the 2016 and 2024 pattern.

2. **Fiscal stance:** Large unfunded tax cuts increase debt supply → potentially USD-negative long term, but near-term USD-positive if they force the Fed to keep rates elevated. The net effect is typically short-term USD-positive, medium-term USD-negative.

3. **Risk sentiment:** A chaotic or contested election result is USD-positive (safe haven demand). A clean, decisive result reduces safe-haven demand, which can be mildly USD-negative regardless of who wins.

**EM currency exposure:** The most direct trade expression of a Republican-tariff win is short emerging market currencies versus USD. The peso (MXN) is the canonical trade because Mexico is the largest US trading partner and most directly exposed to USMCA renegotiation threats. The yuan (CNH) is also directly exposed but has central bank management that dampens volatility. The Canadian dollar (CAD) is exposed through USMCA. The Korean won (KRW), with large tech export exposure, is sensitive to semiconductor-specific trade policy.

## Rate Expectations: How Elections Reprice the Fed Path

The Federal Reserve does not change its benchmark rate on election night, but futures markets absolutely do reprice their expectation of the Fed's path over the next 12–24 months.

Post-Trump 2024 election, the December 2025 Fed Funds futures contract moved from implying approximately 3.5% to implying approximately 4.2% over the following four weeks. Markets were pricing in: (1) tariff-driven inflation that would require the Fed to maintain elevated rates; (2) fiscal expansion (tax cut extension + increased military spending) that would keep the economy running hot; and (3) potential Fed independence pressure that markets initially interpreted as making rate cuts less likely under political pressure.

This rate repricing has cascading effects:

**Duration: sell the long end.** When rate expectations rise, long-duration bonds fall. The iShares 20+ Year Treasury Bond ETF (TLT) declined approximately 5% in the seven weeks post-election. Leveraged and highly indebted sectors suffer — utilities (XLU), REITs (VNQ), and long-duration growth stocks see multiple compression as the discount rate rises.

**Financial sector uplift.** As discussed above, bank net interest income benefits from higher rates and a steeper yield curve. The post-election steepening of the yield curve (2s-10s spread widening) was a significant tailwind for KRE (regional banks), which are more rate-sensitive per dollar of assets than the large-cap money center banks in XLF.

**Growth vs. value rotation.** Higher rates compress the present value of future earnings, which disproportionately hurts growth stocks that derive most of their value from earnings far in the future. The election-driven rate spike in late 2024 produced a brief rotation from growth (QQQ) to value (VTV, IVE) that lasted approximately six weeks before tech earnings results re-anchored growth sentiment.

**Mortgage rate spike and housing.** Higher Treasury yields immediately translate into higher 30-year fixed mortgage rates. Post-2024 election, the 30-year fixed rate rose from approximately 6.7% to 7.1% by December. This hurt homebuilders (ITB, XHB) and mortgage REITs (AGNC, NLY), partially offsetting the broader financial sector rally.

## Presidential Cycle: The Four-Year Pattern in Equity Markets

The presidential cycle is one of the most documented and contested patterns in market history. Here is the evidence, the likely mechanism, and the appropriate trading application.

![S&P 500 average annual returns by presidential cycle year from 1950 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/trading-the-us-election-sectors-dollar-rate-expectations-2.png)

The data is clear across seven decades: Year 3 of the presidential cycle (the year before the election) produces the strongest average returns. Year 1 (the first year after an election) and Year 2 (midterm election year) produce the weakest. Year 4 (election year itself) is solid but below Year 3.

The average returns by cycle year, covering the 1950–2024 period:
- **Year 1** (post-election): approximately +8.1%
- **Year 2** (midterm year): approximately +7.2%
- **Year 3** (pre-election): approximately +15.3%
- **Year 4** (election year): approximately +11.2%

**Why Year 3 is strongest.** The most widely accepted explanation is that incumbent administrations — regardless of party — have a structural incentive to engineer economic expansion during the 12 months before the election. This means: stimulative fiscal policy (tax cuts, spending increases, infrastructure spending announcements), pressure on the Fed to maintain accommodative monetary policy, and avoidance of major trade disruptions or geopolitical escalations that would hurt consumer confidence. The economy enters the election year on a pre-manufactured positive trajectory.

The second-order effect is that markets front-run the Year 3 policy stimulus. Investors who know that administrations tend to turn the fiscal dials up in Year 3 position early, pulling returns into Year 2's second half and amplifying Year 3 momentum.

**Year 2 (midterm year) underperformance.** Year 2 underperformance reflects a different dynamic: midterm elections introduce uncertainty about the president's legislative agenda. In 18 of the last 19 midterm elections, the president's party has lost House seats. This means that whatever ambitious legislative agenda the president entered office with — tax reforms, healthcare overhaul, climate legislation — typically becomes impossible to pass after the midterm. Markets spend much of Year 2 repricing the legislative premium they had assigned in Year 1.

**Year 4 (election year) resilience.** Despite the noise of an election, Year 4 produces solid average returns because the administration's incentives are fully aligned with market performance. Incumbent presidents want to show a strong economy; sitting Fed chairs want to demonstrate institutional independence but also stability. The combination produces a policy environment that, historically, has been supportive of equity returns even as political uncertainty peaks.

**The 2025–2026 application.** With the 2024 election producing a Trump second term, the presidential cycle framework places 2026 (Year 2, midterm year) as a historically weak period and 2027 (Year 3) as the setup for the strongest year of the cycle. A practitioner building a long-term position framework should increase equity allocation in late 2026 in anticipation of the Year 3 tailwind, regardless of the political news flow.

## VIX and the Uncertainty Premium: 60 Days Out to Resolution

The VIX — the CBOE Volatility Index, measuring 30-day implied volatility on the S&P 500 — has a remarkably consistent relationship with the electoral calendar.

![VIX uncertainty premium rising 60 to 90 days before election day and collapsing post-result](/imgs/blogs/trading-the-us-election-sectors-dollar-rate-expectations-5.png)

Across the six presidential elections from 2000 to 2020, the average VIX level follows a consistent arc:

- **120 days before election:** VIX ~16.2 (close to long-run average)
- **90 days before election:** VIX ~17.1
- **60 days before election:** VIX ~19.8 (meaningful premium developing)
- **30 days before election:** VIX ~21.4 (peak uncertainty premium)
- **Election day:** VIX ~20.1 (still elevated but starting to fall)
- **30 days after election:** VIX ~16.8 (sharp resolution collapse)
- **60 days after election:** VIX ~15.3 (back to background)

This pattern is not mechanical — the 2008 election occurred in the middle of the financial crisis, and the 2020 election occurred during COVID, making both years extreme outliers. But the pattern holds across the more "normal" elections of 2004, 2012, 2016, and 2024. The core mechanism: as the vote approaches, the binary nature of the outcome creates peak variance in expected policy. The moment the result is known, that variance collapses — regardless of who won.

**The tradeable implication.** Options on equity indices and individual sectors are overpriced during the pre-election period relative to post-election realized volatility. A trader who sells index-level volatility 30–45 days before the election and covers immediately post-result has historically captured the volatility premium while managing the risk of a surprise result (which would temporarily spike realized volatility above implied). The cleanest expression is selling VIX call spreads or selling SPY straddles 30 days before the election.

**The sector skew.** While the VIX measures broad equity volatility, the implied volatility of individual sector ETFs and single names responds even more sharply. During the 2024 pre-election period, ICLN options were pricing 40% implied volatility (versus a historical realized of ~25%), XLF was pricing 22% (versus historical realized ~18%), and LMT was pricing 28% (versus historical realized ~20%). Selling this volatility premium on the specific sectors with the clearest policy sensitivity is more capital-efficient than a broad VIX position.

#### Worked example:

**Sizing the uncertainty premium on SPY puts.**

Assumptions: It is September 5, 2024. Election day is November 5, 2024 — sixty days away. The S&P 500 is trading at 5,600. SPY is at \$559. You are managing a portfolio of \$100,000 in long equity positions.

Your portfolio has a beta of 1.1 to the S&P 500. A 10% S&P decline would produce approximately an \$11,000 loss (\$100,000 × 1.1 × 10%).

**Hedge option 1: Buy SPY puts.**
- Strike: 530 (5.2% out of the money, near the 1-standard-deviation level for 60 days)
- Expiry: November 15, 2024 (ten days post-election, captures both the pre-election uncertainty and the brief post-result period)
- Market price for SPY 530 put with November 15 expiry: approximately \$4.20 per share, or \$420 per contract (100 shares)
- To hedge a \$100,000 portfolio at 1.1 beta: you need approximately \$100,000 × 1.1 / (559 × 100) = 1.97, round to 2 contracts
- Total hedge cost: 2 × \$420 = \$840
- As a percentage of portfolio: 0.84%

This \$840 cost buys you protection if SPY falls below \$530 by expiration. If a contested election sent SPY to \$500 (10.5% decline), your put position would pay approximately 2 × (530 − 500) × 100 = \$6,000 against a portfolio loss of approximately \$11,550 (\$100,000 × 1.1 × 10.5%). Net loss after hedge: approximately \$5,550 versus \$11,550 without the hedge.

**Hedge option 2: Sell covered calls.** If you own SPY directly, you can sell November 15 calls at the 580 strike (approximately 3.7% OTM) for \$3.80 per share, or \$380 per contract. Selling 2 contracts generates \$760 in premium income that partially offsets a potential loss. This reduces upside above \$580 but funds your hedge.

The "risk-free" portion of the hedge (covered call premium minus put cost) is approximately \$760 − \$840 = −\$80. Net cost of the collar: \$80 for a \$100,000 portfolio. That is an extremely cheap election hedge.

## The 60-to-90 Day Pre-Election Window: A Positioning Framework

The 60-to-90 day window before an election is the primary deployment period for election-driven positioning. By 60 days out, prediction markets have typically converged to a stable probability estimate. By 30 days out, the volatility premium is at its peak and the asymmetry of opportunity has shifted.

![Pre-election positioning timeline from poll monitoring through sector rotation to post-election exit](/imgs/blogs/trading-the-us-election-sectors-dollar-rate-expectations-6.png)

Here is the structured sequence:

**Day -90 to -60: Reconnaissance.** Monitor prediction markets (Polymarket, PredictIt), not just polling averages. Prediction markets aggregate the wisdom of actual money-at-risk and tend to be better calibrated than polls in presidential races. Build a scenario matrix: Republican-win sectors, Democrat-win sectors, gridlock outcome. Begin sizing the sector ETF positions you would deploy if the leading candidate's probability exceeds 65%.

**Day -60 to -45: Begin sector rotation.** Start building positions in the scenario-1 (leading candidate wins) sector ETFs. Use a phased entry: one-third of target position at day -60, one-third at day -45, reserve one-third for day -30 confirmation. The phased entry manages the risk that the prediction market consensus is wrong — you're never fully deployed on a single scenario.

**Day -45 to -30: Hedge layer.** Add the protection structure described in the worked example above. Sell covered calls on existing longs to fund put protection. If you're running the sector rotation trade, add a small VIX call spread (buy the 20-strike call, sell the 25-strike call for net debit of approximately \$1.50 per spread) as insurance against a surprise result.

**Day -30 to election day: Monitor, trim, maintain.** Track whether prediction market probabilities are stable or shifting. If a major revelation (October surprise) shifts probabilities materially, rebalance your sector weights. Reduce gross exposure to 80-85% of normal levels around election week itself — this is not because you expect a crash but because the binary outcome means your positions will either work immediately or need rapid unwinding.

**Election day to day +5: Execute the post-result rotation.** When the result is called, exit your VIX hedges immediately (the VIX premium collapses within the first trading session post-result). Sell puts that you bought for protection — they will lose value rapidly as implied vol drops. Increase gross exposure back to target. Rotate into the confirmed-scenario sector ETFs if you were holding reduced exposure.

**Day +5 to +30: Policy read-through.** As the transition unfolds, the administration's actual policy intentions crystallize. Key appointments (Treasury Secretary, CEA chair, USTR) signal the economic agenda more precisely than campaign rhetoric. The market's initial post-election sector rotation is directionally correct but often overshoots in the first week. The +5 to +30 day period tends to see partial mean-reversion in the most extreme movers, which creates re-entry opportunities.

**Day +30 to inauguration: Final rotation.** By January of the inauguration year, the market has digested the personnel and policy signals and has built a base expectation for the legislative agenda. This is when the policy-thesis-driven positions should be at full size — you've had 60+ days of information beyond election night to refine your sector allocation.

#### Worked example:

**Sector ETF rotation trade around the 2024 election.**

Assumptions: You are a US equity investor with \$50,000 to deploy in a pre-election sector rotation trade. Based on prediction market consensus (Trump at 65%+ probability on Polymarket from mid-October onward), you are building a Republican-win scenario.

**Entry (October 1, 2024):**
- \$20,000 into XLF at \$43.80 per share = 456.6 shares → round to 456 shares at a cost of \$19,973
- \$15,000 into ITA at \$142.50 per share = 105.3 shares → round to 105 shares at a cost of \$14,963
- \$10,000 into XLE at \$89.20 per share = 112.1 shares → round to 112 shares at a cost of \$9,990
- \$5,000 cash reserve held for hedges and confirmation

**Hedge (October 15, 2024):**
- Sell 2× SPY 570-strike covered calls (November 15 expiry) at \$3.40 each = \$680 premium received
- Buy 3× SPY 520-strike puts (November 15 expiry) at \$2.10 each = \$630 paid
- Net hedge cost: \$630 − \$680 = −\$50 (a \$50 credit, effectively free insurance)

**Election day (November 5, 2024):** Trump wins. S&P 500 futures surge +2.2%.

**Exit hedges (November 6 pre-market):**
- Buy back the 2× SPY 570 calls at \$0.80 = \$160 paid (saved \$680 − \$160 = \$520 from original short)
- Sell the 3× SPY 520 puts at \$0.40 = \$120 received (lost \$630 − \$120 = \$510 on puts)
- Net hedge result: \$520 + \$120 − \$630 − \$160 = −\$150 (hedge cost \$150 net, plus original −\$50 credit = \$100 total net hedge cost)

**Exit positions (December 31, 2024):**
- Sell 456 shares XLF at \$50.45 = \$23,005 → gain of \$3,032 (15.2%)
- Sell 105 shares ITA at \$161.97 = \$17,007 → gain of \$2,044 (13.7%)
- Sell 112 shares XLE at \$96.98 = \$10,862 → gain of \$872 (8.7%)

**Total trade P&L:**
- XLF gain: \$3,032
- ITA gain: \$2,044
- XLE gain: \$872
- Hedge cost: −\$100
- **Net profit: \$5,848 on \$44,926 deployed = +13.0% in 91 days**

Annualized, this is approximately +52%. It required a correct directional call on the election outcome, which was available at better-than-even odds from prediction markets, but not a certainty.

## Common Misconceptions

Understanding what the election playbook does *not* say is as important as understanding what it does.

**Misconception 1: The stock market "prefers" Republican presidents.** The aggregate S&P 500 return during Democratic presidencies has historically exceeded that during Republican ones — approximately +10.8% annualized versus +8.4%. This is not because Democratic policy is "better for markets" — it reflects that Democratic presidencies happened to coincide with periods of economic recovery (Clinton in the 1990s, Obama after 2008, Biden during the post-COVID reopening). The stock market responds to economic conditions, not party labels. What the election playbook captures is the *relative* performance of sectors within each administration, not absolute market direction.

**Misconception 2: You need to predict the winner to trade this.** You do not. The uncertainty premium trade (selling pre-election volatility premium, buying back post-result) pays regardless of who wins, as long as the result comes in cleanly and the VIX collapses. A portfolio with balanced sector exposure — long defense and long clean energy simultaneously — captures sector winners regardless of outcome, with each leg hedging the other.

**Misconception 3: The first week's move is the right move.** The post-election first week often overshoots. ICLN fell 5% on election night 2024 and another 10% over the following week, before partially recovering 3–4% by year-end. Traders who shorted ICLN on election night captured the bulk of the move; traders who chased the short on day 3 or 4 were buying into momentum that was already partially exhausted.

**Misconception 4: The president controls the economy.** The Federal Reserve, external shocks (pandemics, wars, oil embargoes), and the lagged effects of the *prior* administration's policies all have as much or more influence on the economy than the current president's policies. George W. Bush inherited a dot-com bubble; Obama inherited the financial crisis; Trump's first term coincided with the COVID collapse; Biden's term saw post-COVID reopening inflation. The presidential cycle's policy effects are real but they are always operating on top of a larger macro backdrop.

**Misconception 5: The dollar always rallies when Republicans win.** Not true. Under the 2008 Bush financial crisis, the dollar was extremely volatile. Under Reagan's early term, the dollar rallied then collapsed. The tariff-and-rate-expectations channel has dominated in the Trump elections (2016 and 2024), but this is a specific combination of policy factors, not a universal law.

**Misconception 6: Clean energy is a permanent loser under Republicans.** The IRA's domestic manufacturing credits have survived aggressive Republican legislative challenges because they create jobs in Republican congressional districts. The residential EV tax credits and offshore wind subsidies are more vulnerable. Within the clean energy complex, FSLR (First Solar, domestic manufacturer) is less exposed to IRA rollback than ICLN (global clean energy ETF with heavy exposure to non-domestic solar).

## How It Shows Up in Real Markets: The 2024 Election Case Study

The 2024 US presidential election is the most detailed and data-rich case study available for the complete election playbook in action. Here is the full sequence.

### Pre-Election Period (September 1 – November 5, 2024)

By September 2024, prediction markets had Trump at approximately 52–55% probability. The VIX was at 16–18, near its long-run average, suggesting the market had not yet built in a meaningful uncertainty premium. This was the entry window for positioning.

The key sector ETF moves during the September 1 to November 5 pre-election period (relative to the S&P 500 flat baseline):

- **XLF:** +3.2% relative (financials building in deregulation expectation)
- **ITA:** +4.1% relative (defense building in budget expansion)
- **ICLN:** −6.8% relative (clean energy pricing IRA rollback risk)
- **XLE:** +2.1% relative (energy pricing deregulation + tariff support)

By late October, as Trump's prediction market probability rose above 65%, sector divergence had already begun. The VIX, meanwhile, had climbed to 21 by October 31 — the peak uncertainty premium, right on schedule 30 days before election day.

### Election Night and Week 1 (November 5–12, 2024)

AP called Pennsylvania for Trump at approximately 1:30 a.m. November 6. By the S&P 500 open:

- **S&P 500 (SPY):** +2.5% on the open, +2.7% on the day
- **Tesla (TSLA):** +14.7% — the largest election-night single-stock move among mega-caps
- **XLF:** +4.8% on the day
- **ITA:** +5.1% on the day
- **ICLN:** −5.1% on the day
- **MXN/USD:** −3.1% intraday (peso collapsed on tariff threat)
- **VIX:** fell from 21 to 16 intraday — the classic uncertainty collapse

The S&P 500's +2.5% on election day was the second-largest single-day post-election gain on record, after the 1932 Roosevelt election.

### Weeks 2 Through 7 (November 13 – December 31, 2024)

As the transition unfolded and cabinet appointments were announced, sector moves continued to extend:

**Key cabinet appointments and their market implications:**
- Scott Bessent (Treasury Secretary): financial-markets-savvy, fiscal-conservative signaling → extended XLF rally
- Doug Burgum (Interior Secretary): accelerated oil and gas leasing → XLE +3% post-announcement
- Chris Wright (Energy Secretary): former Liberty Oilfield Services CEO → IEO +4% post-announcement
- Pete Hegseth (Defense Secretary): strong defense-spending advocate → ITA +2% post-announcement
- RFK Jr. (HHS Secretary): vaccine skeptic → healthcare ambiguity, XLV flat

By December 31, 2024, the complete seven-week scorecard showed in Figure 3.

This is the central fact of election-driven sector rotation: the spread is large, it occurs rapidly, and it is directionally predictable from the policy agenda even if the exact magnitude is not.

## How to Trade It: The Playbook

Assembling the full playbook from the preceding analysis:

### Core Framework: Four Scenarios, Two Strategies

**Strategy A: Directional sector rotation.** Choose the leading candidate based on prediction market consensus above 65%. Build the sector ETFs aligned with that candidate's policy agenda. Exit hedges immediately post-result. Extend winners into inauguration.

**Strategy B: Scenario-neutral volatility capture.** Sell the pre-election volatility premium (covered calls, short straddles) with strikes sufficiently OTM to survive a surprise result. Exit immediately post-result when IV collapses. No directional sector bet.

Most sophisticated practitioners combine both: a core directional sector view using perhaps 60–70% of their election-period capital, plus a volatility premium capture using the remaining 30–40%.

### Sector ETF Position Sizing

For a \$100,000 election-period allocation in a Republican-win scenario:

| ETF | Allocation | Rationale |
|-----|------------|-----------|
| XLF | \$30,000 | Deregulation + rate tailwind |
| ITA | \$20,000 | Defense budget expansion |
| XLE | \$15,000 | Energy dominance + deregulation |
| XLI | \$15,000 | Tariff protection for domestics |
| Cash/hedges | \$20,000 | VIX call spreads, SPY puts |

**Short positions (for advanced accounts):** Short ICLN (\$10,000) and short TLT (\$10,000) funded from the cash allocation. These express the IRA-rollback thesis and the higher-rate-expectations thesis respectively.

### The Dollar Overlay

For currency-capable accounts, the dollar overlay adds a separate tactical layer:

- **Post-Republican-win:** Short MXN/USD (buy USD, sell MXN) — 45 to 90 day hold
- **Post-Democrat-win:** Short USD/JPY (buy JPY) — captures the rate-differential-narrowing thesis
- **Either outcome:** Long USD/CNH — both tariff scenarios (Republican aggressive, Democrat defensive) are net USD-positive versus China

Position sizing: 5–10% of total portfolio in FX overlays, with stops at 2% adverse moves.

#### Worked example:

**Dollar positioning around the 2024 election — MXN trade.**

On November 1, 2024, four days before election day, the MXN/USD rate was 18.00 (18 pesos per dollar). Prediction markets had Trump at 67%.

**Trade setup:** Long USD/MXN (buy dollars, sell pesos) via FX forward or through the currency ETF MXN (note: this is the ProShares UltraShort Mexican Peso ETF or through a brokerage FX account).

For a \$20,000 allocation in a FX brokerage account at 10:1 leverage (standard for major pairs): you control \$200,000 notional of USD/MXN.

**Entry:** November 1, 2024 at 18.00
**Stop-loss:** 17.70 (1.67% adverse move = \$200,000 × 1.67% = \$3,340 loss)
**Target:** 19.50 based on prediction market consensus premium for tariff risk (8.3% favorable move)

**Outcome:** By December 20, 2024, USD/MXN had moved to 20.25 — a 12.5% move from entry.

- P&L on \$200,000 notional at 12.5% move: \$200,000 × 12.5% = \$25,000 gain
- As a return on the \$20,000 deployed capital: \$25,000 / \$20,000 = 125% return

The actual result exceeded the 19.50 target because the tariff threats intensified through December. A prudent exit at the initial 19.50 target would have yielded:
- 8.3% move on \$200,000 notional = \$16,600 gain = 83% return on \$20,000 capital

This is the leverage effect in FX — even a moderate rate move produces outsized returns on deployed margin. The risk management discipline (pre-defined stops) is essential because the same leverage amplifies losses.

#### Worked example:

**Presidential cycle timing — entering in Year 3.**

Assumptions: It is December 31, 2026 (end of Year 2 of the Trump second term, the midterm year). The S&P 500 stands at 5,200, having underperformed its historical average during the midterm year as expected from the presidential cycle framework. You have \$50,000 to allocate.

You are implementing the Year 3 presidential cycle trade: full allocation to S&P 500 (via SPY) on the basis that historical Year 3 average returns are +15.3%, versus +7.2% in Year 2.

**Entry:** January 2, 2027 at SPY \$519 (implied if S&P is 5,200). Buy 96 shares at a cost of \$49,824.

**Historical base case outcome (Year 3 average):**
- +15.3% return on \$49,824 = \$7,623 gain
- Exit December 31, 2027 at SPY ~\$598
- Total position value: \$57,408

**Comparison: If you entered at the beginning of Year 2 (January 2, 2026):**
- Year 2 average return: +7.2% → gain of \$3,588 on \$49,824
- Exit December 31, 2026 at SPY ~\$557
- Total position value: \$53,412

**Two-year compound (entering Year 2, holding through Year 3):**
- Year 2 gain: \$3,588; Year 3 gain (on now \$53,412): \$8,172
- Total two-year gain: \$11,760 (23.6% two-year return)

**Year-3-only entry (skipping Year 2, entering at start of Year 3):**
- Year 3 gain on \$49,824: \$7,623 (15.3% in one year)

The Year-3-only entry returns 15.3% in one year versus the Year-2-entry's effective annual rate of approximately 11.3% over two years. The Year 3 timing produces superior single-year returns — but at the cost of sitting in cash during Year 2's +7.2%, which is still a respectable return.

The optimal implementation: maintain a 70% equity allocation in Year 2 (capturing most of the +7.2%) and increase to 100%+ (using modest leverage or adding small-cap cyclicals via IWM) at the start of Year 3 to capture the full Year 3 premium.

### Key Takeaways for the Next Election Cycle

The playbook built in this post will need calibration for the specific candidate and context of each future election. But the structural mechanics — the uncertainty premium, the sector rotation matrix, the dollar's tariff-sensitivity, the presidential cycle's Year 3 effect — are durable patterns that have held across twelve presidential elections since 1950. The magnitude of each channel shifts election to election; the direction is more predictable than almost any other form of macro trade.

Three principles should govern how you apply this in practice. First: prediction markets, not polls. Polls measure stated opinion; prediction markets measure money-at-risk consensus. The prediction market aggregate has been the more reliable election probability indicator in the post-2000 data. Second: size to scenario probability, not to conviction. If you think there is a 70% chance of a Republican win, your Republican-scenario allocation should reflect that probability — not 100% of your capital. Hold 70% in the Republican-scenario sectors, 30% in Democrat-scenario sectors (or cash), and rebalance post-result. This way you profit even if your conviction was slightly wrong, and you avoid catastrophic loss if you were significantly wrong. Third: the post-result rotation window is short. The first trading day's move captures 40–50% of the seven-week total return in winning sectors. Being positioned before the result is what makes this trade work; chasing it after is expensive.

Elections are among the most data-rich, structurally predictable events in macro markets. Treat them as a disciplined quantitative exercise, not a political expression, and the framework will serve you across every presidential cycle going forward.
