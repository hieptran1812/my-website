> **TL;DR** — Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine did not just disrupt oil markets — it triggered the worst global food supply crisis since the 1970s, threatening famine across the Middle East and Africa.
>
> - Ukraine and Russia together supply 29% of global wheat exports and 19% of corn; their combined share of some fertilizer markets exceeds 40%
> - The Black Sea blockade cut Ukraine's grain exports by 29% in 2022, sending CBOT wheat from \$790 to a peak of \$1,076/bushel (+36%) by May 2022
> - Countries like Egypt, Lebanon, and Tunisia — which import 50–60% of their wheat from Russia and Ukraine — faced existential import bill crises
> - The fertilizer shock was arguably worse: urea nitrogen rose +352% from pre-war levels to peak; potash +263%; marginal farms across the Global South became uneconomic

> - The deepest lesson: food dependency is a geopolitical weapon, and the Global South's refusal to sanction Russia was not principled neutrality — it was rational self-interest

The morning of February 24, 2022, Ukrainian farmers woke to the sound of artillery. Within hours, Russia's ground invasion had closed major highways, disrupted fuel supply lines, and made spring planting in the eastern oblasts — including some of Ukraine's richest black-soil farmland — effectively impossible. By May, CBOT wheat futures were trading at \$1,076 per bushel, their highest level since 2008. In Cairo, flour prices had risen 40%. In Beirut, where 60% of wheat imports came from the Black Sea region, a country already in economic collapse faced bread shortages. In Sri Lanka, a government that had eliminated synthetic fertilizer imports "to go organic" was collapsing under the weight of simultaneous food price inflation and a foreign exchange crisis.

![Ukraine grain export collapse and CBOT wheat price surge 2019-2024](/imgs/blogs/russia-ukraine-war-food-wheat-fertilizer-and-the-global-south-1.png)

This is the diagram above: grain export volumes collapsed 29% in 2022 (top panel) while wheat prices spiked 36% to \$1,076/bushel at peak (bottom panel). When the war closed the Black Sea, it sent a single shock through both the supply side and the price signal simultaneously — exactly the worst combination for importing countries.

This post traces that chain from military action to food price inflation in a Tunisian market to fertilizer scarcity on a Nigerian farm. It is a story about how geographic concentration in agricultural supply chains creates weapons — and how nations with full stomachs often do not realize those weapons exist until someone else picks them up.

## Foundations: how global wheat markets work

Before we can understand what broke in 2022, we need to understand how the global food system was built — specifically, why so many countries ended up dependent on a narrow slice of the Black Sea coastline.

### What is a commodity market, and why do prices move so fast?

A *commodity market* is a global marketplace for undifferentiated physical goods — wheat, corn, oil, natural gas, fertilizer. Unlike a brand-name product, a ton of hard red winter wheat from Ukraine is essentially interchangeable with a ton from Kansas. This means there is one global price (with small regional premiums or discounts), set by the intersection of global supply and global demand.

The key feature of commodity markets relevant to food crises is *price elasticity*. Elasticity describes how much demand changes when price changes. If bread is 20% more expensive, most families do not eat 20% less bread — they cut other spending. Economists say food demand is *inelastic*: a 10% supply reduction might cause a 30–40% price spike, not a 10% one, because buyers panic-buy at the first sign of shortage, and sellers demand a premium to ration scarce supply. This inelasticity is why commodity markets are so volatile during supply shocks.

### What is CBOT wheat, and who sets the global price?

*CBOT* stands for the Chicago Board of Trade, the world's largest and most liquid grain futures exchange. When grain traders, importers, and speculators around the world want to hedge or express a view on wheat prices, they transact in CBOT wheat futures — standardized contracts for delivery of 5,000 bushels of soft red winter wheat at a specified future date.

A *futures contract* is an agreement to buy or sell a commodity at a fixed price on a future date. If an Egyptian flour mill knows it will need to import 100,000 MT of wheat in six months, it can *lock in* the current price today by buying futures contracts. If prices rise before delivery, the mill has protected itself. If prices fall, it will have paid above-market — that's the cost of the insurance.

Because CBOT futures are liquid (thousands of buyers and sellers transact every day), they function as the global reference price for wheat. When geopolitical events disrupt supply, CBOT futures react immediately — often before a single ship has been rerouted or a single silo emptied.

### Ukraine's agricultural footprint: the breadbasket of Europe

Ukraine is a country slightly smaller than Texas, but its agricultural land — particularly the *chernozem* (black soil) belt running across its central and southern regions — is some of the most fertile on earth. Centuries of accumulated organic matter make Ukrainian soil naturally productive for cereals and oilseeds.

Pre-war figures tell the story:

| Crop | Ukraine's global export share | Russia's global export share | Combined |
|---|---|---|---|
| Wheat | 12% | 17% | 29% |
| Corn (maize) | 15% | 2% | 17% |
| Sunflower oil | 50% | 27% | 77% |
| Barley | 16% | 14% | 30% |

Ukraine and Russia together — the "Black Sea breadbasket" — supply nearly a third of global wheat trade and more than three-quarters of global sunflower oil. No other region of comparable size controls such a large share of multiple agricultural commodity markets simultaneously.

This concentration was not accidental. Post-Soviet agricultural transformation (particularly in Ukraine) converted collective farms into large agro-industrial enterprises that could scale rapidly and compete on global markets. Ukraine's Black Sea ports — Odessa, Mykolaiv, Pivdennyi — gave direct access to the Mediterranean and beyond. Shipping a ton of grain from Odessa to Alexandria, Egypt, takes roughly 3–4 days and costs far less than overland alternatives.

### Russia as the world's fertilizer cabinet

Russia's role in the 2022 food crisis extended beyond grain. Russia and its ally Belarus are the world's largest or second-largest producers of multiple *fertilizer* inputs — the chemical compounds that enable modern high-yield agriculture.

*Fertilizers* are nutrients applied to soil to support plant growth. The three main macronutrients are nitrogen (N), phosphorus (P), and potassium (K) — often referred to as N-P-K. Without adequate fertilizer inputs, crop yields fall dramatically. Modern agriculture, which feeds 8 billion people, depends on synthetic fertilizers derived from fossil fuel feedstocks and mined minerals.

| Fertilizer type | Russia/Belarus share | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Ammonium nitrate (N) | Russia: 23% of global exports | Direct nitrogen for grain crops |
| Urea (N) | Russia: 15% of global exports | Most widely used nitrogen fertilizer |
| Potash / MOP (K) | Russia+Belarus: 40% combined | Essential for root development, quality |
| Phosphate (P) | Russia: 14% of global exports | Supports cell structure, energy transfer |

When Russia invaded Ukraine and Western sanctions were imposed, the global fertilizer market faced a double shock: production disruptions on the supply side and shipping and insurance restrictions creating de facto shortages even for technically unsanctioned product. The result was a fertilizer price explosion that would not be fully felt in crop yields until the 2023 and 2024 growing seasons.

### Who imports from the Black Sea: the vulnerability map

The countries most exposed to a Black Sea supply disruption were not wealthy Western nations — they were middle-income and lower-income importers in the Middle East, North Africa, and Asia.

The geography of wheat dependency in 2021 (pre-invasion):

| Country | Wheat imports from Russia+Ukraine | Total wheat imports (MT/year) | Population dependent |
|---|---|---|---|
| Egypt | 50–55% | 12 MT | 100 million |
| Lebanon | 60% | 1.5 MT | 5 million |
| Tunisia | 45% | 2 MT | 12 million |
| Libya | 43% | 0.8 MT | 7 million |
| Turkey | 60% | 8 MT | 84 million |
| Indonesia | 25% | 11 MT | 273 million |
| Bangladesh | 30% | 7 MT | 170 million |
| Pakistan | 35% | 4 MT | 220 million |

Note that these are not small or obscure nations. Egypt is the largest Arab country by population and the world's largest wheat importer. Turkey is a NATO member and a major regional power. Indonesia and Bangladesh together have more people than the European Union. A disruption to Black Sea grain was not a regional inconvenience — it was a shock to the food security of hundreds of millions of people.

## The Black Sea blockade: how it worked and why it mattered

On February 24, 2022, Russia launched its invasion from multiple axes — east from Russia, north from Belarus, and south from Crimea. The southern axis was particularly important for the food crisis: Russian naval forces in the Black Sea moved to assert control over the sea lanes connecting Ukrainian ports to the world.

![From war to food crisis: the complete causal chain](/imgs/blogs/russia-ukraine-war-food-wheat-fertilizer-and-the-global-south-2.png)

The causal chain above tracks how military action in one country became food inflation in another. Each link in the chain is a specific, documentable mechanism — not a metaphor.

### Naval control and port mining

The Black Sea is a semi-enclosed body of water with only one exit: the Turkish Straits (Bosphorus and Dardanelles). Russia has maintained a naval presence in the Black Sea since the annexation of Crimea in 2014, with its Black Sea Fleet headquartered at Sevastopol.

As the invasion began, Ukrainian forces mined the approaches to Odessa — the largest Ukrainian port, handling approximately 65% of Ukraine's grain exports pre-war — to deter a Russian amphibious assault. This decision was militarily rational but economically catastrophic: maritime insurance for commercial vessels navigating the Black Sea rose by factors of 10 to 20, and the international maritime shipping community — particularly risk-averse shippers operating under Lloyd's of London insurance frameworks — largely withdrew.

By March 2022, cargo vessels that had been loading grain in Odessa when the war began were stranded. New ships would not enter. An estimated 20+ vessels were trapped in Ukrainian ports for weeks. The effective grain export capacity of Ukraine dropped to near zero.

### The planting season disruption

The blockade affected not just the movement of grain already in silos — it disrupted the entire spring planting cycle of 2022.

Modern Ukrainian agriculture depends on inputs that arrive via the same port infrastructure used for exports: diesel fuel for farm machinery, spare parts, herbicides, pesticides, and — critically — seeds. The eastern oblasts of Zaporizhzhia, Kherson, and Kharkiv, which are among Ukraine's most productive agricultural regions, were directly in or near the war zone. Farmers in these regions faced active military operations, fuel shortages, and an inability to safely access fields. In Kherson Oblast, Russian forces occupied the land entirely.

The result was a compound supply shock: not only could Ukraine not export its existing grain stocks efficiently, it could not plant its 2022 crop at normal levels. The combination of reduced planting area (estimated 15–20% decline) and physical disruption to export corridors drove the 29% export volume collapse documented in the data above.

It is worth noting that Ukrainian farmers showed remarkable resilience. Many continued to farm under extraordinary conditions — planting in regions adjacent to active front lines, transporting harvests on routes that passed military checkpoints, and accepting significant personal risk to maintain national agricultural output. The Ukrainian government's decision to prioritize grain transport infrastructure in its emergency logistics plans (including fuel allocation and convoy protection) partially offset what would otherwise have been an even more severe production collapse. The 39 MT actually exported in 2022, while down 29% from the 55 MT peak, represented an extraordinary logistical and human achievement under wartime conditions.

### Why alternative routes were inadequate in the short run

Ukraine had land borders with five EU member states (Poland, Slovakia, Hungary, Romania, and Bulgaria) and Moldova. In theory, grain could have been rerouted overland immediately.

In practice, three structural bottlenecks blocked this:

**Rail gauge incompatibility**: Ukrainian railways use a 1,520mm "Russian gauge" track, wider than the 1,435mm European standard gauge used in Poland and other neighboring countries. Every shipment crossing the border required physical transfer of grain from one set of railcars to another — a labor-intensive process that capped throughput.

**Infrastructure capacity**: Ukraine's border crossing points were designed for normal trade flows, not emergency bulk commodity exports. The Danube ports (Ismail, Reni, Ust-Dunaisk) were small and lacked sufficient barge and truck infrastructure for handling tens of millions of metric tons annually.

**Logistics ecosystem**: The Black Sea port system had decades of accumulated infrastructure — grain elevators, terminals, fumigation facilities, customs clearance — that could not be replicated overnight in Constanta (Romania) or Gdansk (Poland).

These bottlenecks meant that in the critical spring and summer of 2022, when global food markets were most stressed and the price signals were most urgent, the alternative routes could handle only a fraction of normal Black Sea throughput. The infrastructure gap would take 12–18 months to close.

## The commodity price spike: mechanics and timeline

![Wheat import dependency: who relied on Russia and Ukraine](/imgs/blogs/russia-ukraine-war-food-wheat-fertilizer-and-the-global-south-3.png)

The dependency map above shows that the supply shock hit a set of countries with high political sensitivity and limited fiscal buffers. Understanding the price mechanics requires understanding who was buying, how fast, and why the market overshot fundamental supply-demand balance.

### The wheat price spike: why it overshot

CBOT wheat futures opened at approximately \$790/bushel in the week before the invasion. By March 7, 2022 — less than two weeks into the war — they had reached \$1,007. By early May, they peaked at approximately \$1,076/bushel, a gain of 36% from pre-invasion levels.

This price move had several distinct components:

**Fundamental supply shock**: The loss of Ukrainian export capacity represented approximately 24–30 MT of annual supply reduction. Against global annual wheat consumption of approximately 780 million MT, this is a 3–4% supply shock. With demand inelastic at roughly -0.3 (meaning a 1% price rise reduces demand by 0.3%), the fundamental model implies a price rise of roughly 3%/0.3 = 10%. Actual prices rose 36%. The gap represents two additional factors.

**Speculative and precautionary premium**: When a major commodity supply route goes offline suddenly and no one knows how long the disruption will last, buyers do not wait for prices to rise to the market-clearing level — they buy ahead of need. Major Middle Eastern sovereign wheat buyers (particularly the Egyptian General Authority for Supply Commodities, GASC) began issuing emergency tenders at above-market prices, communicating urgency to the entire market. Speculators in futures markets saw the strategic buying, increased their long positions, and drove prices further.

**Second-source depletion**: When Black Sea grain went offline, buyers switched to alternative origins — Australia, Canada, US, France. This additional demand on non-Black-Sea suppliers drove up their prices too, creating a cascading price effect across the entire global wheat market, not just the Black Sea segment.

### The fertilizer spike: more severe and more persistent

![Black Sea food crisis: key events timeline 2022-2024](/imgs/blogs/russia-ukraine-war-food-wheat-fertilizer-and-the-global-south-4.png)

The timeline above shows how fertilizer markets — unlike wheat futures — did not recover quickly. The key reason is that fertilizer supply chains are slow-moving industrial systems with multi-year investment cycles. You cannot rapidly commission a new potash mine; you can more quickly divert a grain shipment.

The fertilizer price spike had started before the invasion. The 2021 European energy crisis drove up natural gas prices, and natural gas is the primary input for nitrogen fertilizer (urea) production via the Haber-Bosch process. Urea prices had already roughly doubled from \$250/MT to \$500/MT by late 2021. Then the Ukraine invasion compounded the shock:

- Russia imposed a temporary export ban on ammonium nitrate and other nitrogen fertilizers in early 2022.
- Western financial sanctions and shipping restrictions created "de facto" embargoes even on technically unsanctioned fertilizer: banks and shipping companies refused to handle Russian-origin goods regardless of whether they were legally restricted.
- Belarus potash exports — which had already been partially sanctioned after the 2021 Lukashenko political crisis — collapsed further.

The combined result: urea nitrogen reached \$1,400/MT at peak (from a pre-crisis \$310/MT baseline — a rise of 352%). Potash reached \$890/MT (from \$245/MT — a rise of 263%).

## The global food inflation: from commodity markets to kitchen tables

### The transmission mechanism

Understanding how a commodity price spike becomes food inflation requires understanding the production chain between raw wheat and bread.

Wheat futures prices are wholesale, bulk, undifferentiated commodity prices. Before wheat becomes bread, it passes through:
1. **Milling**: Converting wheat grain to flour (adds milling margin, ~10–15% of wheat cost)
2. **Transportation**: Moving flour to bakeries (significant for landlocked countries)
3. **Baking**: Converting flour to bread product (labor, energy, packaging)
4. **Retail distribution**: Wholesale to retail markup

At each stage, there are both pass-through effects (higher input costs) and *stickiness* (prices do not move immediately due to long-term supply contracts, government price subsidies, or retailer absorption). The degree to which wheat price increases transmit to consumer bread prices varies significantly by country:

- In **high-income countries** with diversified supply and government buffers, bread prices rose 10–15% in 2022 — the EU average was approximately +15%.
- In **middle-income import-dependent countries** like Egypt, flour prices rose approximately 40% by mid-2022.
- In **fragile states** with limited government fiscal capacity and high food import bills relative to GDP, the impact was catastrophic.

### Country-level impacts: from statistics to political crises

![2022 food and fertilizer commodity price shock](/imgs/blogs/russia-ukraine-war-food-wheat-fertilizer-and-the-global-south-5.png)

The price data above tells part of the story. The other part is what those prices meant for specific countries.

**Egypt** is the world's largest wheat importer and the most exposed large economy to the Black Sea supply disruption. Egypt imports approximately 12 MT of wheat per year, with roughly 50–55% historically sourced from Russia and Ukraine. The Egyptian government subsidizes bread through the *baladi* program — a decades-old system of subsidized flat bread available to low-income Egyptians. When wheat prices doubled, the Egyptian government had two choices: pass the price increase to consumers (risking political unrest in a country already politically sensitive following the Arab Spring), or absorb the cost and run a fiscal deficit. It chose the latter, exhausting reserves and requiring a \$3 billion IMF emergency financing program by December 2022.

**Lebanon** was already in economic collapse before the war — its banking system had frozen depositors' funds, the lira had lost more than 90% of its value since 2019, and political paralysis had left the country without basic services. It imported roughly 60% of its wheat from the Black Sea. Lebanon's Silos (grain storage) in Beirut had been destroyed in the August 2020 port explosion. By mid-2022, Lebanon had less than two months of wheat reserves. International food aid organizations scrambled to source alternative grain from other markets at above-market prices.

**Tunisia** faced a convergence of food price inflation, a political crisis following President Saied's 2021 consolidation of power, and a widening fiscal deficit. Wheat constituted 45% of its imports from Russia and Ukraine. The doubling of the import bill created a balance-of-payments crisis that eventually required an IMF Extended Fund Facility negotiation.

**Sri Lanka** became the most internationally visible crisis of 2022. While its immediate collapse was driven by a broader foreign exchange crisis, food import costs — including both grain and fertilizer (the government had banned synthetic fertilizer imports in 2021, a decision that devastated crop yields and made it even more dependent on imports) — were a significant accelerant. Protests in Colombo in July 2022 led to the storming of the presidential palace and President Gotabaya Rajapaksa's resignation.

## The fertilizer crisis: a slow-motion food problem

### Why fertilizer matters more than immediate food prices

Fertilizer is an input to food production, not food itself. This means the fertilizer crisis of 2022 would not show up in food prices immediately — it would show up in *reduced harvests* in 2023 and 2024, as farmers who couldn't afford fertilizer at \$1,400/MT either planted less, applied lower rates, or substituted cheaper-but-less-effective alternatives.

The mechanism: when fertilizer prices triple, marginal farms in lower-income countries reduce applications. Nitrogen fertilizer under-application typically reduces wheat yields by 15–25% (depending on soil quality and rainfall). Applied across millions of smallholder farmers in sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, and MENA, the aggregate effect is a multi-year yield reduction that extends the food crisis well beyond the period of acute price spikes.

This is the "slow-motion food crisis" thesis: the acute phase of the 2022 food crisis — the headline commodity price spikes — was largely resolved within 12–18 months as Black Sea grain began moving again and prices normalized. But the fertilizer under-investment of 2022 would continue to suppress yields through 2023 and 2024, creating a persistent food insecurity tail risk even after commodity prices normalized.

The World Food Programme estimated that more than 200 million people faced acute food insecurity in 2022-2023, a significant increase from the pre-crisis baseline.

Understanding why this timing lag matters is critical for interpreting the data correctly. A commodity trader who saw wheat futures fall back from \$1,076 to \$650 by December 2022 might conclude: "crisis over." A food systems analyst watching fertilizer application rates across East Africa and South Asia would have reached the opposite conclusion: the food crisis was just entering its second, slower-moving phase. The acute commodity shock was the visible part; the fertilizer-yield feedback loop was the structural damage operating below the surface of market prices.

The production chain from fertilizer decision to harvest outcome takes approximately 12–18 months. Farmers decide on input application in the months before planting (typically November–January for winter wheat, March–May for spring crops in the Northern Hemisphere). The harvests resulting from under-fertilized 2022 and 2023 planting seasons would show up in global supply data in 2023 and 2024. This delayed transmission is why food security analysts and commodity traders often look at different indicators and reach different conclusions about the same crisis.

### The India-Russia fertilizer deal: how some navigated it

India provides the clearest example of a country that managed the fertilizer crisis strategically rather than being victimized by it.

India is the world's second-largest fertilizer consumer (after China). It imports approximately 40-45% of its potash needs and a significant share of its nitrogen requirements. When Western countries and major shipping companies moved to avoid Russian goods, India did the opposite: it increased fertilizer imports from Russia, negotiating discounts of 20–30% below market prices as Russia needed payment outlets that bypassed Western financial networks.

The Indian government's position was explicitly transactional: it maintained diplomatic relations with Russia, refrained from joining Western sanctions coalitions, and secured preferential commodity pricing in return. India also mediated in the Black Sea Grain Initiative discussions, positioning itself as a neutral facilitator with economic interests on both sides.

This behavior was widely criticized by Western governments as enabling Russian aggression. From India's perspective — and from the perspective of its 1.4 billion people who depend on affordable food prices — the calculus was straightforward.

### Brazil: the accidental beneficiary

Brazil illustrates a different dynamic: the accidental winner.

Brazil is one of the world's largest agricultural exporters (soybeans, corn, cotton, sugar, beef). It is also one of the world's largest fertilizer *importers*, buying approximately 85% of its fertilizer needs from abroad — with Russia and Belarus among the top suppliers.

The 2022 fertilizer crisis should have been devastating for Brazilian agriculture. Instead, Brazilian corn and soybean exports boomed because:

1. **Competitor supply fell**: Brazilian soybeans directly compete with Ukrainian sunflower oil in edible oil markets. With sunflower oil at \$2,000/MT (vs. \$1,200 pre-war), soybean oil became more competitive at equivalent prices, driving up Brazilian export values.

2. **Brazil partially substituted**: Brazil sourced additional fertilizer from the Middle East (Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Morocco) and negotiated direct deals with Russia that bypassed sanctions channels, leveraging its non-NATO, non-EU status.

3. **Brazil's domestic reserves**: Brazil had accumulated significant soybean stocks from prior years; its actual 2022 crop was planted at near-normal rates despite elevated fertilizer costs.

The net result was that Brazilian agricultural export revenues rose sharply in 2022 even as input costs rose — a windfall for Brazilian agribusiness that helped sustain Brazil's current account position during a period of global financial volatility.

## The Black Sea Grain Initiative: diplomacy as logistics

On July 22, 2022, five months into the war, a UN and Turkish-brokered agreement called the Black Sea Grain Initiative (BGIA) was signed in Istanbul. The agreement created a maritime safe corridor allowing Ukrainian grain to leave Odessa, Chornomorsk, and Yuzhne ports under a monitored protocol.

The mechanics were straightforward but politically remarkable: a Joint Coordination Centre (JCC) staffed by representatives of Ukraine, Russia, the UN, and Turkey was established in Istanbul to inspect ships, verify cargo manifests, and authorize transits. Russian naval forces agreed not to target ships transiting through the designated corridor.

### What the initiative achieved

Over its 12-month operational period (August 2022 – July 2023), the BGIA facilitated the export of approximately 33 million metric tons of grain and foodstuffs. At average market prices, this was worth roughly \$10–12 billion in export value — food that reached markets in Africa, the Middle East, and Asia.

The price impact was significant: global wheat prices fell from their May 2022 peak of \$1,076/bushel to approximately \$800/bushel by August 2022 (the month BGIA ships began moving), and continued declining toward \$650 by year-end. The initiative did not eliminate the food crisis — prices remained above pre-war levels, and fertilizer costs remained elevated — but it prevented the worst-case scenario of sustained Black Sea closure.

### Russia's leverage: using grain as a diplomatic tool

Russia repeatedly demonstrated that it viewed the BGIA not as a humanitarian obligation but as a diplomatic asset to be used or withheld based on geopolitical calculations.

In October 2022, following Ukrainian drone attacks on the Russian Black Sea Fleet in Sevastopol, Russia suspended participation in the BGIA for four days before resuming on October 31. In November 2022, Russia sought to negotiate the extension of the deal in exchange for concessions on SWIFT reconnection for the Russian Agricultural Bank — a bank not covered by the original deal.

Most significantly, on July 17, 2023, Russia formally withdrew from the BGIA, citing its claims that an analogous agreement facilitating Russian fertilizer and grain exports had not been honored. The withdrawal came precisely as the deal was due for its regular 120-day renewal negotiation.

The timing was notable: Russia made the withdrawal announcement one day after a drone strike on a bridge connecting Crimea to mainland Russia, suggesting the action was at least partly retaliatory. The message to the international community was clear: Black Sea grain access was a lever, and Russia would pull it when it chose to.

Within 24 hours of the BGIA termination, CBOT wheat futures rose 8.5% in a single session.

## Worked examples: the numbers behind the crisis

#### Worked example:

**The food price transmission math — why the 36% spike was bigger than the supply shock implied**

Ukraine and Russia together represent approximately 29% of global wheat exports, or about 80 million metric tons per year in normal conditions.

Suppose the war reduces their combined exports by 30% — roughly 24 million MT reduction. Annual global wheat consumption is approximately 780 million MT.

Supply shock = 24/780 = 3.1%.

Now apply economic price elasticity. The price elasticity of demand for wheat is approximately -0.3 (meaning a 1% price rise reduces demand by 0.3%). To clear a 3.1% supply shortfall through price alone:

Required price rise = supply shock / elasticity = 3.1% / 0.3 = **10.3%**

But CBOT wheat actually rose 36% at its peak — more than three times the fundamental model's prediction. Why?

Three amplifiers explain the gap:

1. **Speculative premium**: Uncertainty about the duration of the disruption caused buyers to price in worst-case scenarios rather than base cases. If there was a 30% probability that Black Sea grain was disrupted for multiple years, rational buyers would pay above the simple one-period clearing price.

2. **Strategic reserve-building by MENA buyers**: Egypt, Turkey, and other large importers accelerated purchasing beyond their immediate needs, building strategic buffers. This is rational individual behavior but collectively amplifying — the more everyone stockpiles, the larger the apparent demand, and the higher the price signal.

3. **Contagion to other exporters**: Buyers who couldn't source from Ukraine/Russia switched to US, Australian, and French grain, driving up prices in those markets too. The global wheat market repriced as a whole, not just the Black Sea segment.

*The intuition*: A 3% supply shock causes a 10% fundamental price rise; panic buying and geopolitical uncertainty triple that to 36%. Market prices price uncertainty, not just fundamentals.

#### Worked example:

**Egypt's import bill calculation — why the food crisis forced a sovereign loan**

Egypt imports approximately 12 million metric tons of wheat per year. Pre-war, roughly 60% of this came from Russia and Ukraine (approximately 7.2 MT).

Pre-war cost calculation:
- Pre-war wheat price: \$400/MT (FOB Black Sea)
- Annual import bill from Russia/Ukraine: \$400 × 7.2 MT = **\$2.88 billion**
- Total wheat import bill (all sources): approximately \$4.8 billion

Peak-price calculation (May 2022):
- Peak wheat price: approximately \$900/MT (delivered to Egypt — Black Sea FOB \$1,076 plus freight adjustments)
- Same 7.2 MT at peak prices: \$900 × 7.2 MT = **\$6.48 billion**
- Total annual wheat import bill at peak: approximately \$10.8 billion

The increase: \$6.48B - \$2.88B = **\$3.6 billion** more per year just for the Black Sea share of imports.

Egypt's total foreign exchange reserves in early 2022 were approximately \$40 billion — comfortable by most metrics, but heavily allocated to servicing prior debt, managing the Egyptian pound peg, and maintaining fuel subsidies. The additional \$3.6 billion in annual food costs was not in any budget. Egypt devalued its currency in March 2022 (moving from a managed peg of approximately 15.7 EGP/USD to around 18.5 EGP/USD), which further increased the local-currency cost of imported wheat, amplifying domestic inflation.

By December 2022, Egypt had agreed to a \$3 billion IMF loan program — partly driven by food import costs, partly by debt service pressures. The IMF program required currency flexibility, subsidy reform, and fiscal consolidation — policies that the Egyptian government found politically painful precisely because food costs were already elevated.

*The intuition*: A country that imports more than a billion dollars a month of food from a war zone is not buffered by foreign exchange reserves — it's racing against them.

#### Worked example:

**The fertilizer compound effect — how input cost inflation makes farms uneconomic**

A typical wheat farm in a middle-income country (Ukraine, Egypt, Pakistan) uses approximately 200 kg per hectare of nitrogen fertilizer (as urea) for a reasonable yield.

Pre-crisis urea price: \$310/MT (per metric ton = per 1,000 kg)
Cost per hectare: (200/1,000) × \$310 = **\$62/ha**

Peak 2022 urea price: \$1,400/MT
Cost per hectare: (200/1,000) × \$1,400 = **\$280/ha**

Increase in fertilizer cost: \$280 - \$62 = **\$218/ha**

Now contextualize against farm economics:
- Wheat yield at good input application: approximately 5 MT/ha
- Wheat price at \$300/MT (bulk domestic price): revenue = \$300 × 5 = **\$1,500/ha**
- Pre-crisis fertilizer as share of revenue: \$62/\$1,500 = 4.1%
- Peak-crisis fertilizer as share of revenue: \$280/\$1,500 = 18.7%

At the same wheat price, fertilizer cost had risen from 4% to nearly 19% of revenue — a 4.5x increase as a share of farm economics. For marginal farms — those with lower yields, smaller holdings, higher financing costs — fertilizer alone may have consumed 30–40% of expected revenue.

The rational farmer response: apply less fertilizer, or plant less land. Across millions of smallholders in South Asia and sub-Saharan Africa, this aggregate response reduced 2023 and 2024 crop yields, extending the food insecurity problem past the period of acute commodity price spikes.

*The intuition*: High commodity food prices and high fertilizer prices hit farmers from both sides — input costs rise, but the domestic price of their crop often doesn't fully compensate because of government price controls or simply because subsistence farmers aren't selling at world-market prices.

---

#### Worked example:

**The Black Sea Grain Initiative: calculating Russia's diplomatic value per ton of grain**

Over its 12-month operation, the BGIA moved approximately 33 million metric tons of grain. At approximately \$300/MT average realization (well below peak prices, reflecting mix of wheat, corn, and other grains), the total market value was roughly:

33 MT × \$300/MT = **\$9.9 billion** in food exports

Now consider what Russia received in exchange. The explicit concession Russia sought — and largely didn't receive — was reconnection of the Russian Agricultural Bank to SWIFT and removal of restrictions on shipping and insurance for Russian fertilizer exports. Russia valued these at roughly \$2–3 billion per year in lost revenue.

But Russia received something arguably more valuable than cash: diplomatic cover with the 42 African nations (representing more than 1.4 billion people) that would have been most impacted by continued Black Sea closure. These nations had been neutral or sympathetic in UN votes on Ukraine-related resolutions. Their continued non-participation in Western sanction regimes — maintaining oil, fertilizer, and food trade with Russia — was worth far more to Russia than any SWIFT access.

An approximate diplomatic-value calculation:
- Russia's oil revenues at discounted prices (2022): approximately \$218 billion
- Revenue protection from maintaining non-Western buyer relationships: estimated \$40–60 billion per year
- Diplomatic cost of BGIA (opportunity cost of not blockading): approximately \$9.9 billion in Ukrainian exports

Russia spent \$9.9 billion in forgone Ukrainian export suppression to protect \$40–60 billion in its own commodity revenue relationships. The ratio is approximately 4–6:1 in Russia's favor — making the BGIA, counterintuitively, a strategically rational choice for Russia even from a purely economic standpoint.

*The intuition*: Grain diplomacy is asymmetric. The political cost to Russia of maintaining the corridor was modest; the political cost of withdrawing it (in July 2023) was equally modest. Russia extracted maximum value by threatening withdrawal repeatedly, extracting concessions, and ultimately terminating when the diplomatic calculus shifted.

## Geopolitical dimensions: the food-sanctions nexus

### Why the Global South didn't sanction Russia

When the US, EU, UK, Canada, Japan, Australia, and their allies imposed sweeping sanctions on Russia following the February 2022 invasion, a striking pattern emerged: the vast majority of the world's nations — representing more than half its population — did not join.

India (1.4 billion people), China (1.4 billion), Indonesia (273 million), Brazil (215 million), South Africa (60 million), Bangladesh (170 million) all maintained normal or increased trade relations with Russia in 2022 and beyond. This was not ideological solidarity with Russia. It was, in many cases, food and fertilizer dependency.

- India bought Russian fertilizers and crude oil at steep discounts, lowering inflation and protecting its current account.
- Bangladesh imported Russian wheat at below-market prices through bilateral government-to-government deals.
- Indonesia, one of the world's largest palm oil producers, received Russian fertilizer inputs on favorable terms.

Western commentary often framed this as "sitting on the fence" or "not taking a principled stand." From the perspective of a government responsible for feeding a billion people at affordable prices, the calculation was simpler: Russian grain and fertilizer were 20–30% cheaper than Western alternatives, and their political costs were lower because Western countries were not threatening food access.

This dynamic gave Russia a durable and largely unremarked form of economic protection throughout the sanctions regime — one that grew larger as the price discounts on Russian commodities widened.

![Global food supply chain: 2021 vs 2024 before-after](/imgs/blogs/russia-ukraine-war-food-wheat-fertilizer-and-the-global-south-6.png)

The supply chain shift above was real but took 18 months to materialize. In the critical 2022 window, the "after" infrastructure simply did not exist. The before-after comparison understates the pain of the transition: every ton that moved through Danube ports in 2022-2023 moved at higher cost, with more logistics complexity, and with less certainty than a Black Sea shipment.

### The "weaponization of food" thesis

Academic and policy communities have debated whether Russia deliberately "weaponized" food supply — using grain access as a geopolitical tool in the way energy had been weaponized through Nord Stream.

The evidence for intentional weaponization is strong but not definitive:

**In favor**: Russia's timing of BGIA suspensions and terminations was consistently correlated with political events (Ukrainian drone attacks, Western arms shipments, UN votes). Russia's stated demands during BGIA negotiations repeatedly included concessions (SWIFT access, sanctions rollbacks) that had nothing to do with food logistics. The BGIA termination announcement in July 2023 came precisely as Russia sought leverage in post-dam-break negotiations.

**Against**: Russia also had independent economic incentives to resume Black Sea grain flows (BGIA created shipping lanes that could potentially carry Russian goods). Russia's primary economic interest in 2022 was to maximize its own commodity export revenues, not to create famine — starvation does not benefit Russian markets.

The most defensible synthesis: Russia used food access as a *pressure tool* when convenient, but did not construct a deliberate long-term food-denial strategy. The difference matters for future policy: deterrence against deliberate weaponization requires different tools than managing incidental supply disruptions.

### The slow discovery of food as strategic leverage

Perhaps the most durable geopolitical lesson of the 2022 food crisis is how surprised Western policymakers were by the food-sanctions nexus.

Western sanctions were designed around financial channels (SWIFT), energy supply (Brent, Nord Stream), and advanced technology (chips, semiconductors). Food and fertilizer were explicitly *excluded* from Western sanctions regimes — humanitarian exemptions were written into virtually every sanctions package. This exclusion was both morally necessary and strategically naive: it assumed that the absence of formal sanctions would be sufficient to maintain commercial food and fertilizer flows, without accounting for the de facto restrictions created by shipping companies, banks, and insurers taking maximum risk-aversion positions.

The result was a sanctions design with unintended civilian harm concentrated not in Russia but in third-country food importers — precisely the countries whose political support for Western positions was most needed and most uncertain.

## Country vulnerability matrix: who was exposed and why

![Country vulnerability food crisis scorecard 2022](/imgs/blogs/russia-ukraine-war-food-wheat-fertilizer-and-the-global-south-7.png)

The matrix above codifies the vulnerability framework across six key countries. The color coding captures a crucial insight: crisis severity was not determined by wheat import dependency alone. Lebanon had the highest single-source dependency (60% from Russia+Ukraine) but its food crisis was compounded by near-zero foreign exchange reserves and the absence of an active IMF program — factors that would have been manageable separately but were devastating in combination.

Three variables predicted crisis severity better than import dependency alone:

**1. FX reserve buffers**: Countries with multiple months of import cover (Egypt: 9 months, Indonesia: 6 months) had time to secure alternative sources, negotiate IMF programs, and absorb price increases through subsidies. Countries with 1–2 months of cover (Lebanon, Pakistan at certain points) had no such buffer.

**2. IMF program status**: Having an active IMF program in 2022 was a crisis-management advantage, not just a credibility signal. The IMF provided emergency funding, but more importantly, it provided a *coordination mechanism* with other official creditors and commercial banks that helped countries roll over maturing debt even as import bills were rising.

**3. Domestic production and subsidy capacity**: Countries with significant domestic wheat production (Turkey: 20 MT/year domestic, half its consumption) or the fiscal capacity to maintain price subsidies (Saudi Arabia, UAE) were effectively insulated even when import prices rose. The vulnerability concentrated in countries that were both 100% import-dependent *and* had limited fiscal room.

## Common misconceptions

**"The food crisis ended when wheat prices fell back to pre-war levels."**
This is wrong in two ways. First, wheat prices did not fall back to pre-war levels: CBOT wheat was \$790 pre-invasion and was still approximately \$650–700 in 2023 — down from \$1,076 peak but 15–20% above baseline. Second, the fertilizer crisis created a second-wave yield reduction problem: under-invested 2022-2023 growing seasons produced lower yields in 2023-2024, sustaining food insecurity even as commodity market prices normalized.

**"Western sanctions caused the food crisis by restricting Russian fertilizer exports."**
This confuses the mechanism. Western sanctions explicitly *excluded* food and fertilizer from restriction. The de facto shortfall was caused by risk-aversion in private shipping and banking — companies voluntarily avoiding Russia-related transactions to minimize compliance uncertainty and reputational risk. This is an important distinction for sanctions policy design: the civilian harm came not from government decisions but from private-sector overcaution. Targeted communication by governments to financial institutions about the scope of exemptions could have reduced this effect.

**"The Black Sea Grain Initiative saved the day."**
The BGIA was important but not transformative. Its 33 MT over 12 months represented less than half of Ukraine's pre-war annual export capacity. The initiative helped stabilize prices and prevented the worst-case scenarios, but it did not restore normal supply flows — Ukraine's 2022 and 2023 exports (39 MT and 46 MT respectively) remained well below the pre-war 55 MT peak, even with BGIA in operation.

**"Russia was the primary beneficiary of the food crisis."**
Russia's agricultural sector benefited from higher prices for its own grain exports (Russian wheat at \$800+ vs. \$400 pre-war). But Russia's fertilizer export revenues were partially offset by shipping and payment channel restrictions. The largest beneficiaries were non-Black-Sea grain exporters: Brazil (soybeans, corn), Australia (wheat), Canada (wheat, canola), and India (certain grain categories) all saw significant export revenue gains as buyers diversified away from the Black Sea.

**"Food security is a developing-country concern, not a market problem."**
The 2022 crisis demonstrated that food security is both a humanitarian concern *and* a hard-nosed geopolitical and financial markets issue. The fact that African nations did not sanction Russia was not about ideology — it was about import dependency. Any investor or policymaker who ignored this mechanism in 2021 found themselves surprised by both the price dynamics and the political consequences in 2022. Food security belongs in every macro framework and every portfolio risk discussion; it is not a welfare issue siloed from asset markets.

## How it shows up in real markets

### The Egypt-IMF program: food crisis as sovereign credit trigger

Egypt's December 2022 \$3 billion IMF Extended Fund Facility was directly traceable to the food import cost escalation. Egypt's prior IMF program (2016–2019) had restored macroeconomic stability, but the combination of the COVID-19 pandemic, the 2021 energy price rise, and the 2022 food shock had depleted the fiscal buffer.

For fixed-income investors, the signal was the Egyptian sovereign CDS spread: it widened from approximately 450 basis points in January 2022 to over 1,200 basis points by October 2022 — a tripling in the market's assessment of Egypt's default probability. The IMF program announcement in December 2022 brought CDS spreads back down to the 700–800 basis point range, but Egyptian sovereign bonds continued trading at significant discount to par through 2023.

The lesson for credit markets: sovereign credit risk in food-import-dependent countries is a function not just of fiscal fundamentals but of commodity import costs. A country with a "manageable" fiscal deficit can tip toward crisis if it imports 10–15% of GDP in food at a price that doubles in six months.

### Tunisia's political crisis: food as a governance accelerant

Tunisia's 2021 presidential coup (when President Saied suspended parliament and assumed emergency powers) preceded the food crisis but was compounded by it. Saied had come to power on a wave of popular discontent with economic mismanagement; when food prices rose 40–50% in 2022, the political pressure intensified.

Tunisia's IMF program negotiations in 2022–2023 became politically toxic: the austerity measures required by the Fund included reductions in food subsidies — precisely the subsidies that were insulating Tunisian consumers from global wheat prices. The IMF agreement was repeatedly delayed and almost collapsed over this issue. For Tunisia's ordinary citizens, the choice between "IMF money + subsidy cuts" and "no IMF money + maintained subsidies but eventual default" was not purely financial — it was about whether families could afford bread.

The Tunisia case illustrates a general mechanism: food price shocks do not just create economic crises. They create *legitimacy crises* for governments, which then feed back into sovereign credit risk and political instability in ways that financial models typically underestimate.

### The Danube logistics boom: infrastructure creation under crisis

One positive externary of the food crisis was the rapid development of Danube River grain export infrastructure. Prior to 2022, the Danube ports of Reni, Ismail, and Galati in Ukraine/Romania were minor players in global grain logistics. By mid-2023, they had been upgraded with new grain elevators, extended barge fleets, and improved customs facilities.

The investment was driven partly by EU emergency funds, partly by private logistics companies who saw the profitable opportunity, and partly by Ukrainian government subsidies to incentivize infrastructure development. By 2024, the Danube route was handling approximately 7 million MT per year — a fraction of pre-war Black Sea capacity, but a genuine infrastructure asset that increased Ukraine's long-run export resilience.

For investors in agricultural infrastructure and commodity logistics, the crisis created a template: concentrated supply chains create tail risks that, when they materialize, force rapid competing investment in alternative routes. The crisis-induced investment in Danube infrastructure may prove durable even when/if Black Sea routes fully normalize.

### The fertilizer sector: structural shifts in procurement

The 2022 fertilizer crisis accelerated a structural shift in fertilizer procurement that will take years to fully work through.

Countries that relied heavily on Russian and Belarusian potash — including Brazil (85% import dependent) and the entire ASEAN agricultural system — began diversifying. Canada's Nutrien, the world's largest potash producer, saw record demand and announced production expansion programs. Morocco's OCP Group (one of the world's largest phosphate producers) signed long-term supply agreements with multiple countries seeking to reduce Russian exposure. Saudi Arabia's Ma'aden expanded nitrogen fertilizer exports to Southeast Asia.

These structural shifts took 2–3 years to materialize as meaningful supply diversification. In the interim (2022–2023), the disruption was real and the price spike was painful. By 2025, the fertilizer supply chain was significantly more diversified — a structural improvement that reduced Russia and Belarus's leverage going forward, but one that was achieved at significant cost to farmers and food-importing nations.

## How to trade it: the playbook for food-geopolitics crises

Food security crises driven by geopolitical supply disruptions have a characteristic pattern that creates tradeable dynamics — if you can identify the signal early.

### The signal framework

**Signal 1: Concentration metrics**. Before a crisis, map the geographic concentration of key agricultural commodity supply. The 2022 crisis was entirely predictable from two numbers publicly available in any FAO database: Russia+Ukraine = 29% of wheat exports. If a geopolitical event threatens a cluster with >20% of any critical commodity market, the price risk is non-trivial. The practical trade: build a commodity-linked positions (long CBOT wheat futures or agriculture-linked ETFs) when geopolitical tension in a concentrated supply region rises above a threshold.

**Signal 2: MENA sovereign credit widening**. Food-import-dependent sovereigns in MENA react to commodity price rises faster than commodity importers in other regions because food constitutes a larger share of household budgets (25–40% vs. 10–15% in OECD countries), subsidies create fiscal exposure, and political tolerance for food inflation is lower. Widening CDS spreads in Egypt, Tunisia, or Jordan during commodity spikes are a leading indicator of sovereign stress — and potentially a trading opportunity (long CDS protection on these names when grain prices spike).

**Signal 3: IMF program watch**. Countries that approach the IMF for emergency financing during food crises tend to do so under duress — which means IMF conditionality (currency devaluation, subsidy reduction) is being imposed at the worst political moment. Sovereign bond prices in these countries typically bottom around IMF announcement and recover over 6–12 months as the adjustment program stabilizes the external position.

**Signal 4: Fertilizer futures curve**. Urea futures and potash spot prices are less liquid than grain futures but respond to the same geopolitical triggers. Buying fertilizer-linked equities (Nutrien, Mosaic, OCP Group, Yara) during geopolitical disruptions to Russian/Belarusian fertilizer supply has historically provided both commodity exposure and operating leverage to price spikes.

### Invalidation conditions

Any food-security geopolitical trade has four key invalidation conditions:

1. **Diplomatic resolution**: A new grain corridor deal or direct ceasefire reopens Black Sea shipping faster than expected → commodity prices fall faster than modeled
2. **Alternative supply surge**: A record harvest in Australia, US, or Canada in the same season offsets the disruption → the fundamental supply shock is smaller than feared
3. **Demand destruction**: Food price inflation causes genuine demand reduction (buying less food, substituting crops) → prices correct without a supply-side resolution
4. **IMF backstop**: Rapid IMF mobilization for exposed sovereigns (faster than March–December 2022 Egypt case) reduces sovereign credit widening → the sovereign credit trade doesn't materialize

The practical lesson: food-geopolitics trades are typically 3–6 month duration, not multi-year. The acute phase resolves faster than the chronic phase, and the market is efficient at pricing the acute phase once the shock is known. The edge lies in understanding *before the event* which countries are exposed — the dependency mapping that most market participants ignored until February 24, 2022.

The 2022 food crisis was not a natural disaster. It was a geopolitical decision — taken in Moscow, with military means — that traveled through physical commodity markets, grain futures exchanges, fertilizer supply chains, sovereign foreign exchange reserves, and IMF negotiating rooms before it landed in the food budgets of some of the world's most vulnerable people. The weapon was not invented in 2022 — historians of the 1970s OPEC embargo and the 1974 Soviet grain embargo will recognize the pattern. What was new was the scale, the speed, and the extent to which the decision-makers wielding the weapon did not bear most of its costs. Understanding that asymmetry is not just an academic exercise. It is the starting point for any serious analysis of how nations will manage food security in the decades ahead.
