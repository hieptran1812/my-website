> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Rice is the most policy-driven major staple on Earth because only about 8-9 percent of what the world grows ever crosses a border, so a single big exporter's decision to ban or free exports swings the whole world price.
>
> - Vietnam is one of the top three rice exporters, and its supply comes almost entirely from the Mekong Delta running three crops a year (Winter-Spring, Summer-Autumn, Autumn-Winter).
> - The benchmark the world quotes is the **5-percent-broken** export price in dollars per tonne; the premium product is fragrant rice, above all the ST25 variety crowned "world's best rice."
> - When India (roughly 40 percent of world rice trade) banned non-basmati white-rice exports in July 2023, the world price spiked, Vietnam's 5-percent benchmark jumped from around \$540 to about \$650 per tonne, and Vietnamese exports hit a record near 9 million tonnes in 2024.
> - The one number to remember: **\$650 per tonne** — the Vietnamese 5-percent benchmark at the peak of the 2023 India-ban shock.

In the third week of July 2023, a single notice from the government of India changed the price of dinner for half the planet. India, the world's largest rice exporter by a wide margin, announced it would ban the export of non-basmati white rice with near-immediate effect. Within days, importers from the Philippines to West Africa scrambled to lock up whatever rice was still for sale, and the price of the grain that feeds more people than any other began to climb hard.

The country that caught most of that displaced demand was Vietnam. Its export benchmark — the price of 5-percent-broken white rice loaded onto a ship in the Mekong Delta — vaulted from roughly \$540 per tonne in the early summer to about \$650 by August, the highest in more than a decade. Vietnamese farmers saw their paddy fetch prices they had not seen in years, the country booked a record export haul, and a quiet truth about the world rice market was suddenly visible to everyone: this is the most fragile, most policy-driven staple market there is, and Asia's exporters hold the lever.

This post is about how that lever works. We will build the rice market from the ground up — the geography of the Mekong, the triple-crop calendar, the grades that trade, the thinness that makes it explosive — and then trace exactly how an Indian policy move travels through the system to land on a Vietnamese farmgate price and a Filipino importer's invoice. By the end you should be able to read the Asian rice market the way a trader does: watch India's policy, watch the Mekong harvest, and never forget how little of this grain actually trades.

There is a reason this market rewards understanding over information. The headlines during a rice spike are full of the wrong frame — "global rice shortage," "crops fail," "food crisis" — when the truth is almost always that there is plenty of rice in the world and a shortage only of rice *for sale*, manufactured by a policy decision and amplified by fear. A reader who knows that the relevant supply is the thin traded sliver, not the giant production pool, and who knows that the trigger is usually a government office rather than the weather, sees the 2023 episode for what it was: a predictable, mean-reverting, policy-and-panic spike. That clarity is worth more than any single data point, and building it is the goal of this post.

## Foundations: how the world rice market actually works

Start with the thing that makes rice different from every other commodity we cover in this series. Oil, copper, and gas are global pools: a barrel pumped in Texas competes directly with a barrel pumped in Saudi Arabia, and roughly half of all the oil produced gets shipped across a border to someone who wants it. Rice is the opposite. The world grows somewhere around 520 million tonnes of milled rice a year, and the overwhelming majority of it is eaten in the very country that grew it. Only about 8-9 percent — call it 50-some million tonnes — is ever sold internationally.

That single fact is the master key to everything that follows, so let us be precise about why it is true. Rice is the staple food for more than half of humanity, concentrated in Asia. The two biggest producers, China and India, together grow roughly half the world's rice, and each is essentially self-sufficient: they grow it to feed their own enormous populations, not to sell. Most governments in rice-eating countries treat domestic rice supply as a matter of national security, and they feed their own people first. What is left over for export is a thin surplus skimmed off the top of a very large harvest.

Now hold two numbers next to each other. World production: about 520 million tonnes. World trade: about 52 million tonnes. The traded market is roughly one-tenth the size of the produced market. This is the structural reason rice prices are so volatile and so sensitive to policy — and it is worth seeing it laid out before we go further.

The demand side compounds the fragility. Rice demand is highly **inelastic** — a fancy word for "people keep buying it even when the price moves." In rice-dependent countries, rice is not one option among many; it is the meal. When the price doubles, families do not switch to something else, because for the poorest buyers there is nothing cheaper to switch to. They cut back at the margin and absorb the rest. This matters for price behavior in a precise way: when supply falls in a market with inelastic demand, the price must rise *a lot* to balance the books, because buyers will not back away on their own. Inelastic demand is the reason a small supply cut produces a large price move rather than a small one — the price, not the quantity demanded, does almost all the adjusting. Put the thin traded market and the inelastic demand together and you have the recipe for the most explosive staple price on Earth: a tiny pool of supply that can shrink on a policy whim, meeting a wall of demand that refuses to bend.

A few definitions you will need throughout, defined from zero:

- **Paddy** is rice as it comes off the field — the unmilled grain still in its husk, usually sold wet. Vietnamese farmers sell paddy in **VND per kilogram** at the canal-side to traders and mills.
- **Milled rice** is paddy after the husk and bran are removed. Milling a tonne of paddy yields roughly 0.65 tonnes of milled rice; the rest is husk, bran, and breakage.
- **Broken rice** refers to kernels that snapped during milling. Grades are quoted by the percentage of broken kernels: **5-percent-broken** means at most 5 percent of the kernels are fragments. Lower broken percentage equals higher grade equals higher price.
- **FOB** ("free on board") is the price of the rice loaded onto the ship at the export port, quoted in **US dollars per tonne**. This is the number the world watches. The Vietnamese 5-percent-broken FOB benchmark is *the* reference price for ordinary Asian white rice.
- **Fragrant rice** (jasmine-type) is aromatic, premium rice. Vietnam's ST25 variety won the "World's Best Rice" award and sells at a large premium over ordinary white.

The chain runs from a flooded field in the Mekong Delta to a price quoted in dollars on a Reuters screen, and the policy lever sits right at the end of it.

A second structural fact deserves its own line, because it shapes how the market trades: there is no liquid, centralized rice *futures* market that dominates the way the CME's WTI contract dominates oil or CBOT's contract dominates wheat. There is a CBOT rough-rice future, but it is thin and references US long-grain rice, not the Asian white-rice grades that feed most importers. The real Asian rice market trades **physically and over the counter** — exporters and importers negotiate cargoes directly, often government-to-government, and the "price" everyone quotes is an assessed FOB benchmark published by trade bodies like the Vietnam Food Association and Thai exporters, not a screen-traded settlement. This matters enormously for how the market behaves. Because there is no deep paper market to absorb shocks, a supply scare cannot be smoothed by speculators taking the other side at scale the way it can in oil. The physical buyers simply have to find physical rice, and when they cannot, the assessed price gaps. The absence of a deep paper layer is part of why rice prices move in steps rather than smooth curves.

That also means the four-players framework from the rest of this series — producers, consumers, hedgers, speculators — looks lopsided in rice. There are producers (farmers and exporting nations) and consumers (importing nations and their populations), but the hedger-and-speculator layer that financializes oil and grains is shallow. Rice is closer to a pure physical commodity than almost anything else we cover, which is exactly why government policy, rather than financial flows, is the dominant price driver. When we say rice is the most policy-driven staple, the thin paper market is half of the reason and the thin physical trade is the other half.

#### Worked example: milling math, from paddy to a tonne of exportable rice

A Mekong trader buys 100 tonnes of wet Winter-Spring paddy from farmers at a farmgate price of 8,000 VND per kilogram. That is 8,000,000 VND per tonne, so the paddy outlay is 800,000,000 VND for the lot.

Milling that 100 tonnes of paddy yields about 65 tonnes of milled white rice (a 65 percent milling rate is a reasonable round number for good-quality paddy). To turn the VND cost into the dollar world the export market speaks, take an exchange rate of about 25,000 VND per US dollar. The 800,000,000 VND paddy cost equals roughly \$32,000. Spread across 65 tonnes of milled rice, that is about **\$492 of paddy cost per tonne of milled rice** before milling, bagging, transport, and margin.

So when the FOB 5-percent benchmark is quoted at \$540 per tonne, the milled-rice-equivalent paddy cost of about \$492 leaves a thin sliver — a few tens of dollars per tonne — to cover everything else and pay the exporter. The takeaway: the farmgate VND price and the FOB dollar price are tied together tightly by the milling ratio and the exchange rate, and a \$100 move in the FOB price is an enormous move relative to that thin sliver of margin.

## The Mekong Delta: the rice bowl and its triple-crop clock

Almost all of Vietnam's exportable rice comes from one place: the Mekong Delta, the flat, water-laced fan of land in the country's far south where the Mekong River splits into nine branches and pours into the sea. The Vietnamese call it the rice bowl, and the name is literal. The delta is one of the most productive rice landscapes on the planet, and what makes it special is not just the soil and the water — it is the calendar.

In most of the world, rice is a once-a-year crop: plant in spring, harvest in autumn, then the land rests. The Mekong, sitting in the tropics with year-round warmth and a controllable flood, runs **three crops a year off the same fields**. Each crop has a name and a character, and a trader who follows Vietnamese rice learns to read them like seasons.

![The Mekong Delta triple-crop calendar from Winter-Spring to Autumn-Winter](/imgs/blogs/vietnamese-rice-and-the-export-ban-dynamics-of-asia-6.png)

- **Winter-Spring** (Dong Xuan) is planted around November-December after the annual flood recedes and harvested roughly February to April. It is the biggest and best-quality crop — cool, dry harvest weather means fewer broken kernels and higher grades. Winter-Spring alone is close to half of the year's output and is the crop that sets the tone for export prices.
- **Summer-Autumn** (He Thu) is planted right behind it, around April-May, and harvested July to September. It is large but lower-grade: the rainy harvest season produces more breakage and more moisture, so more of it ends up as 15-percent or 25-percent-broken rather than the premium 5-percent.
- **Autumn-Winter** (Thu Dong) is the third, smallest crop, planted on the higher ground that escapes the flood and harvested toward the end of the year, closing the cycle before the flood returns.

Why does the calendar matter to a price? Because the world's importers buy continuously, but Vietnam's supply arrives in three distinct pulses. When the Winter-Spring harvest comes in heavy and high-quality around March, there is abundant good rice for sale and the FOB price softens. In the gap before a harvest — say, late in the year when the small Autumn-Winter crop is all that is left — domestic stocks tighten and the price firms. A trader who knows the Vietnamese harvest clock knows when the country is flush and when it is scraping, and that timing interacts violently with any policy shock from elsewhere in Asia.

The delta's productivity rests on one thing above all: water, and specifically *fresh* water arriving at the right time. The Mekong flood is the delta's heartbeat — it deposits nutrient-rich silt, flushes salts out of the soil, and recharges the system before the dry-season crops go in. Anything that disrupts that flow is a direct threat to the rice supply, and there are two big ones a trader should keep on the radar. The first is **upstream dams**. The Mekong runs through six countries before it reaches Vietnam, and the cascade of dams built upstream over the past two decades changes how much water and silt arrive in the delta and when. Less silt means farmers must buy more fertilizer; altered flow timing complicates the harvest clock. The second is **saltwater intrusion**. The Mekong Delta sits barely above sea level, so when the dry-season river flow is weak, seawater pushes inland up the river branches and turns freshwater channels brackish. A bad intrusion year — 2016 and 2020 were severe — can damage standing crops across hundreds of thousands of hectares, especially the dry-season Winter-Spring crop that is most exposed.

These are slow-burning supply risks rather than sudden shocks, but they set the backdrop. A delta that is structurally a little more fragile each decade is a supply base that the world's thin rice trade leans on more heavily every time another exporter stumbles. When you read that Vietnam shipped a record 9 million tonnes in 2024, hold in mind that this came off a delta facing rising salinity and upstream water competition — the cushion is real, but it is not infinite, and that is part of why the long-run story for Asian rice prices has a gentle upward tilt even in calm years.

#### Worked example: a Mekong farmer's revenue per hectare

Consider a smallholder in An Giang province, in the heart of the delta, farming the Winter-Spring crop. Mekong rice yields are high: a good Winter-Spring crop returns about 7 tonnes of paddy per hectare.

Suppose the farmgate paddy price is 7,500 VND per kilogram (a typical level), which is 7,500,000 VND per tonne. Revenue per hectare is therefore 7 tonnes times 7,500,000 VND, or **52,500,000 VND per hectare** for the crop. At 25,000 VND per dollar, that is about **\$2,100 per hectare** of gross revenue from one harvest.

Now watch what the 2023 shock did. When the world price spiked and exporters competed to buy paddy, the farmgate price climbed toward 9,000 VND per kilogram. At that price the same 7-tonne hectare grosses 63,000,000 VND, about \$2,520 — an extra \$420 per hectare, a roughly 20 percent jump, flowing straight from an Indian policy decision to an An Giang farmer's pocket. The takeaway: the FOB dollar price is not an abstraction for the delta; it is transmitted through the farmgate VND price into real household income, one crop at a time.

## The grades: why "5-percent-broken" is the benchmark, and where ST25 sits

When commodity traders say "the rice price," they almost always mean one specific thing: the FOB price of 5-percent-broken white rice from Thailand or Vietnam, quoted in dollars per tonne. It is the benchmark for the same reason WTI is the benchmark for crude — it is a standardized, liquid, widely-traded grade that everyone can reference. Most of the ordinary white rice that feeds importing nations in Africa and Southeast Asia trades at or around this grade.

The grade ladder runs by broken percentage. Five-percent-broken is the standard export grade. Below it sit 15-percent and 25-percent-broken, cheaper rice that goes to more price-sensitive buyers and to food processing. At the very bottom is 100-percent-broken — pure fragments — used for brewing, animal feed, and the cheapest food markets. The lower the breakage, the higher the grade and the price, which is why the dry Winter-Spring harvest (less breakage) is worth more than the rainy Summer-Autumn crop.

Above ordinary white rice sits the fragrant tier. Jasmine-type aromatic rice commands a premium, and Vietnam has pushed hard up this quality ladder. The country's ST25 variety, bred in Soc Trang province, won the World's Best Rice award and now anchors Vietnam's premium export story. Fragrant and specialty rice can sell at hundreds of dollars per tonne above the 5-percent white benchmark, and the strategic point is this: as Thailand and Vietnam compete with cheaper exporters like India and Pakistan on ordinary white rice, moving up into fragrant and specialty grades is how they defend margin. Vietnam's average export price has drifted higher over the past decade in part because the mix has shifted toward fragrant rice.

This grade structure matters for reading the market because a headline "rice price spike" is really a spike in one grade. When India banned *non-basmati white* rice in 2023 but kept exporting basmati and parboiled, it was a surgical cut aimed at the exact grade that competes with Vietnamese and Thai white rice. That is why the 5-percent benchmark moved so violently while premium basmati barely flinched.

There is also a geography to the grades that maps onto who buys what. The price-sensitive importers — many West African nations, for instance — are big buyers of broken and parboiled rice, the cheaper end of the ladder, because they are buying calories at the lowest cost per tonne. The Philippines and much of Southeast Asia buy ordinary 5-percent and 15-percent white. The premium fragrant tier sells into wealthier urban markets and the diaspora trade — Vietnamese and Thai jasmine on supermarket shelves in richer countries. So a policy shock does not hit every importer equally: India's broken-rice and white-rice restrictions hit the poorest, most price-sensitive importers hardest, which is why these episodes carry such political weight and why they so often trigger copycat protective moves down the chain.

#### Worked example: the fragrant-rice premium and why Vietnam climbs the ladder

Suppose an exporter can sell ordinary 5-percent white rice at an FOB price of \$540 per tonne, or fragrant jasmine-grade rice at \$680 per tonne — a premium of \$140, about 26 percent. The fragrant rice costs a bit more to grow and mill, say \$40 per tonne extra in seed, lower yield, and careful handling. The net margin uplift from selling fragrant rather than ordinary white is therefore about **\$100 per tonne**.

Now scale that across volume. If Vietnam shifts 2 million tonnes of its annual export mix from ordinary white to fragrant, the extra margin is 2,000,000 times \$100, or **\$200 million** of additional export earnings in a year, with no extra tonnage shipped — pure mix improvement. The takeaway: in a market where Vietnam cannot control its biggest competitor's policy or the world price, the product mix is the one lever it fully owns, and moving up the grade ladder into fragrant rice like ST25 is a multi-hundred-million-dollar national strategy, not a marketing flourish.

## Why rice is the most policy-driven staple: the thin-market multiplier

We have said it twice; now let us prove it with arithmetic, because this is the single most important mechanism in the entire market. The reason a rice price reacts to policy the way gunpowder reacts to a spark is the gap between how much rice exists and how much rice is *for sale*.

![Why rice is fragile, a giant harvest versus a tiny traded sliver](/imgs/blogs/vietnamese-rice-and-the-export-ban-dynamics-of-asia-5.png)

Picture the two pools side by side. The production pool holds about 520 million tonnes — vast, and mostly spoken for, eaten where it is grown. The trade pool holds only about 52 million tonnes — the thin surplus that crosses borders. Every importer in the world that cannot feed itself — much of Africa, the Philippines, parts of the Middle East — must buy from that small second pool. And one country, India, normally supplies a huge share of it.

Here is the multiplier. India's *production* is around 135 million tonnes. India's *exports* in a strong year are around 17-20 million tonnes. So India's exports are only about 13-15 percent of its own crop — but they are something like 35-40 percent of *all the rice the world trades*. That asymmetry is the whole game. A change that is small relative to India's harvest is enormous relative to the global market.

The bar chart of who actually exports makes the dominance concrete.

![Who exports the world's rice, India dominant and Vietnam in the top three](/imgs/blogs/vietnamese-rice-and-the-export-ban-dynamics-of-asia-4.png)

India towers over everyone, at roughly twice the volume of the next exporter. Vietnam and Thailand trade places for the number-two and number-three slots from year to year, each shipping somewhere around 8-9 million tonnes. Pakistan and the United States fill out the next tier. The instant you see this shape, the policy fragility is obvious: the market leans on one supplier, and that supplier's government can turn the tap.

It is worth a moment on how Vietnam climbed into that top tier, because it was not always there. For much of the twentieth century Vietnam was a rice *importer*, its delta scarred by war and held back by collectivized farming that strangled output. The turning point was Doi Moi, the late-1980s market reforms that handed farmers control of their own land and output. Production exploded almost overnight, and by the early 1990s Vietnam had flipped from importer to one of the world's largest exporters — one of the great agricultural turnarounds of the modern era. That history is why Vietnam's place in the top three is structural rather than accidental: the Mekong's natural productivity, unlocked by reform and a triple-crop calendar, made the country a permanent fixture of the thin world market. When India steps back, Vietnam is one of only two or three suppliers on Earth with the spare exportable surplus to step forward, and that scarcity of substitutes is exactly what gives the whole market its policy-driven, gap-prone character.

#### Worked example: the trade-thinness multiplier

Suppose India decides to hold back 20 percent of its normal export volume — not a catastrophe at home, just a precautionary cut to keep domestic prices calm before an election. India normally exports about 18 million tonnes, so a 20 percent cut removes 3.6 million tonnes from the world market.

Now measure that against the right denominator. Against India's own 135-million-tonne crop, 3.6 million tonnes is a rounding error — about 2.7 percent. But against the world *trade* pool of roughly 52 million tonnes, that same 3.6 million tonnes is about **7 percent of everything the world can buy**, vanishing overnight.

And in 2023 India went much further than a 20 percent trim — it banned the entire non-basmati white-rice category, pulling something like 9-10 million tonnes off the table. Against the trade pool, that is close to a fifth of world trade gone in a single announcement. The takeaway: because the denominator that matters is tiny, an exporter's policy move that looks modest at home detonates in the world market — this is the thin-market multiplier, and it is why rice prices gap rather than drift.

## The 2023 India export ban: anatomy of a price shock

Now we can run the real event start to finish, because every piece is in place. By mid-2023 India faced a domestic problem familiar to every rice government: an erratic monsoon threatened the crop, food inflation was politically sensitive ahead of state elections, and the government wanted domestic rice cheap and plentiful. On 20 July 2023, it banned the export of non-basmati white rice with immediate effect. Earlier it had already restricted broken-rice exports and slapped a 20 percent duty on parboiled rice; the July move was the big one.

The reaction was textbook export-ban dynamics. The world's importers, suddenly facing the disappearance of their cheapest and largest supplier, did the rational individual thing that is collectively destabilizing: they panic-bought from whoever was left. Whoever was left was mainly Vietnam and Thailand. Buyers rushed to lock up Vietnamese 5-percent-broken rice, exporters bid hard for Mekong paddy to fill the orders, and the FOB benchmark took off.

It is worth slowing down on the *mechanism* of the spike, because it is the same self-reinforcing loop that powers every export-ban episode and it is the reason the price overshoots the fundamentals. Step one: a supply scare (the monsoon worry) makes the biggest exporter restrict exports to protect itself. Step two: that restriction removes a large slice of the thin world market overnight, so genuine scarcity appears in the *traded* pool even though there is plenty of rice in the world. Step three — and this is the accelerant — importers do not just replace the lost supply, they *over-order*. A buyer who is suddenly unsure whether rice will be available next month rationally builds a buffer, ordering more than current needs. That precautionary demand arrives at exactly the moment supply has shrunk, so demand jumps as supply falls. Step four: the price has to rise far enough to choke off the panic and ration the remaining surplus, which it does by gapping higher. Step five: copycat behavior — other exporters, seeing their own domestic prices rising as world buyers descend on them, are tempted to impose their own curbs, which would shrink the pool further. The loop feeds on itself until the price is high enough to break the panic or the original restriction is lifted.

This is why the 2023 spike was much larger than the underlying supply change justified. India did not lose its crop; it chose to withhold its exports. The "shortage" was manufactured by policy and then amplified by fear and over-ordering into a price move of more than 20 percent in the benchmark grade in a matter of weeks. Understanding that the spike is mostly a *behavioral* amplification of a *policy* choice — not a physical shortage — is what lets a clear-eyed trader avoid chasing the top: spikes built on panic and policy mean-revert hard the moment the policy reverses, exactly as the 5-percent benchmark did when India eased in late 2024.

![The 5-percent-broken benchmark and the 2023 India-ban spike in US dollars per tonne](/imgs/blogs/vietnamese-rice-and-the-export-ban-dynamics-of-asia-3.png)

The chart tells the story cleanly. Through 2021 and 2022 the Vietnamese 5-percent benchmark sat in a sleepy range around \$470-490 per tonne. As India tightened through the first half of 2023 the price firmed toward \$540. Then the July ban hit, and by August the benchmark had jumped to about \$650 — and it stayed elevated, touching its highest levels in over fifteen years into early 2024 as the ban persisted. Only when India finally began easing the restrictions in late 2024 did the price mean-revert back toward \$500 and then lower.

Vietnam was the prime beneficiary on volume. Freed of its largest competitor in the ordinary-white-rice segment, and sitting on solid Mekong harvests, Vietnam shipped more rice than ever before.

![Vietnam rice exports break to a record after India steps back, in million tonnes](/imgs/blogs/vietnamese-rice-and-the-export-ban-dynamics-of-asia-2.png)

Vietnamese export volume, which had hovered around 6.2-6.4 million tonnes a year through 2019-2021, climbed to about 7.1 million tonnes in 2022, then 8.1 million in 2023, and broke to a record of roughly **9.0 million tonnes in 2024**. Higher volume at a much higher price meant export *value* surged even faster than the tonnage — a windfall for the Vietnamese trade balance and for the delta.

The structure of that windfall is worth naming precisely, because it is what makes an export-ban shock so lucrative for the gap-filler. In an ordinary year, an exporter who ships more rice usually does so by accepting a lower price — you move extra volume by undercutting. The 2023-24 shock broke that trade-off. Vietnam shipped *more* volume *and* received a *higher* price, simultaneously, because the extra demand was not Vietnam discounting to win share — it was the world's buyers, stripped of their largest supplier, bidding up whatever Vietnam had. Volume and price moved together instead of against each other, which is the rare and profitable signature of a thin-market supply shock landing on a country that happens to have the surplus to sell. That is why these episodes are transformative for an exporter's annual rice earnings and why a gap-filler like Vietnam has every incentive to keep its taps open when a rival shuts down.

#### Worked example: the 2023 spike P&L for a Filipino importer

The Philippines is the world's largest rice importer, buying most of it from Vietnam. Take a state buyer that needs to contract 500,000 tonnes of 5-percent-broken rice for the year.

Before the shock, at a benchmark of \$540 per tonne, that contract costs 500,000 times \$540, which is **\$270 million**. After the India ban, with the benchmark at \$650 per tonne, the identical 500,000 tonnes costs 500,000 times \$650, or **\$325 million**. The same rice, the same buyer, the same ships — and an extra **\$55 million** on the bill, a 20 percent increase, purely because of a policy decision made in New Delhi.

For an importing nation, that \$55 million is real budget pain and real food inflation passed to households. The takeaway: in a thin market, the importer at the end of the chain absorbs the full force of the exporter's policy move, which is exactly why rice-importing governments hold strategic reserves and sign long-term supply deals — they are trying to insulate themselves from a lever they do not control.

#### Worked example: Vietnam's export-value windfall, volume times price

The cleanest way to see why 2023-24 was a windfall for Vietnam is to decompose the change in export value into a volume piece and a price piece.

Take a stylized "before" of 7.1 million tonnes at an average realized FOB price of \$520 per tonne. That is 7,100,000 times \$520, which is about **\$3.69 billion** of export revenue. Now take an "after" of 9.0 million tonnes at an average realized \$620 per tonne (the benchmark ran above this at the peak, but the year-average realization is lower). That is 9,000,000 times \$620, or about **\$5.58 billion**.

The total gain is roughly **\$1.89 billion**. Decompose it: holding price at \$520, the extra 1.9 million tonnes adds about \$0.99 billion (the volume effect); holding volume at the new 9.0 million tonnes, the extra \$100 per tonne adds about \$0.90 billion (the price effect). The takeaway: Vietnam won on *both* levers at once — it sold more rice *and* sold it dearer — and that double win is precisely the export-FX prize that pulls an exporting government toward keeping the taps open during a global shortage.

## Vietnam's own balancing act: food security at home versus export dollars

Here is where Vietnam's position gets genuinely interesting, because Vietnam is not just a bystander to India's policy — it is itself an exporter that must make the same choice India made, only in the opposite direction. When the world price spikes, every rice-exporting government faces a brutal trade-off, and how Vietnam handles it tells you a lot about how the market clears.

![Vietnam's balancing act, domestic supply versus export dollars](/imgs/blogs/vietnamese-rice-and-the-export-ban-dynamics-of-asia-7.png)

On one side sits domestic food security. Rice is the staple of the Vietnamese diet and a politically sensitive good. If exporters bid paddy prices up to chase the high world price, domestic rice gets more expensive for Vietnamese consumers, and the government — like every rice government — feels pressure to protect its own people. The lever it can pull is to throttle exports: register and limit export volumes, prioritize filling the national reserve, and keep enough rice at home to hold domestic prices down. Vietnam has done exactly this before; in 2008, during a previous global rice panic, Vietnam temporarily suspended new export contracts to protect domestic supply, which (in the thin-market way) only added to the world spike.

On the other side sits the export-FX prize. A high world price is a once-in-a-decade chance to earn scarce foreign exchange, reward farmers, and run a record trade surplus in rice. Letting exports run captures all of that — but at the cost of dearer domestic rice and the risk of being caught short if the harvest disappoints.

In 2023, Vietnam threaded the needle. Rather than slam the door the way India did, it leaned toward exporting — capturing the record volumes and the high price — while watching domestic stocks and the national reserve closely, and signaling it would intervene if domestic supply got tight. That choice is why Vietnam, not India, was the headline winner of 2023-24: it stayed in the market and took the business its largest competitor had abandoned. The deeper point is that the world rice price is the *sum* of these national choices. India chose protection; Vietnam chose participation; and the gap between those two decisions is a big part of why the price went where it did.

Vietnam has the institutional machinery to throttle if it wants to. Rice exports run through licensed exporters; the Vietnam Food Association coordinates and publishes the benchmark assessments; the government maintains a national food reserve and can direct exporters to prioritize domestic supply. In past panics this machinery has been used as a brake. The clearest contrast is Vietnam's own behavior in 2008. That year, during the global rice crisis, Vietnam temporarily suspended signing new export contracts to keep rice at home and tame domestic inflation. The move protected Vietnamese consumers in the short run, but — in the thin-market way — it pulled supply off the world market and added fuel to the global spike that drove the Thai benchmark over \$1,000 per tonne. It also briefly dented Vietnam's reputation as a dependable supplier, the very asset that wins long-term contracts.

The lesson Vietnam appears to have absorbed from 2008 is that being a *reliable* exporter is a strategic asset worth protecting, and that lesson shaped the 2023 response. By staying in the market in 2023 while India withdrew, Vietnam both earned the windfall and burnished its credibility as the supplier that does not abandon its buyers in a crisis. For an importing nation deciding where to sign its next multi-year supply deal, that track record is worth real money. So Vietnam's 2023 choice was not just opportunistic — it was a deliberate bet that reputation compounds, and that a country which keeps its taps open during a global scare will be rewarded with the next decade of contracts. The contrast with India's stop-start policy, which leaves buyers permanently unsure, is the strategic core of Vietnam's rice story.

#### Worked example: the cost of throttling, what Vietnam gave up in 2008 versus gained in 2023

Put the two strategies side by side in rough dollar terms. Imagine a year in which Vietnam *could* export 8 million tonnes into a spiked market at an average realized \$600 per tonne — a potential **\$4.8 billion** of rice export revenue.

If Vietnam throttles, suspending new contracts to protect domestic supply and shipping only 6 million tonnes, it earns 6,000,000 times \$600, or **\$3.6 billion**, leaving **\$1.2 billion** of export revenue on the table — the direct cost of the 2008-style choice (before counting the harder-to-measure reputational damage). If Vietnam participates and ships the full 8 million tonnes, it banks the whole \$4.8 billion *and* keeps its reliable-supplier credibility intact. The takeaway: throttling has a large, quantifiable opportunity cost in a spike year, which is exactly why Vietnam leaned toward exporting in 2023 — the FX prize and the reputational prize pointed the same way, and only a genuine domestic shortage would have justified pulling the brake.

## Common misconceptions

**"There's a global rice shortage when the price spikes."** Usually false. The 2023 spike happened in a year of near-record *world* rice production. There was no global shortage of rice — there was a shortage of rice *for sale* on the world market, created by a policy decision. The thin-market multiplier means the traded price can scream while warehouses in producing countries stay full. Always separate "how much rice exists" from "how much rice is for export."

**"Vietnam and Thailand can simply replace India."** Only partly, and only with a lag. India's exportable surplus is so large that when it leaves, Vietnam and Thailand can fill *some* of the hole by selling more and pulling down their own stocks, but they cannot conjure another 9 million tonnes overnight. That is why the price had to rise: the remaining exporters ration their limited surplus by price, not by magically expanding supply. Vietnam's record 9 million tonnes in 2024 was a stretch, not a substitute.

**"Rice prices follow oil and the broad commodity index."** Much weaker than you would think. Rice is driven first by Asian monsoon, harvest, and export policy — idiosyncratic, regional, political factors. It can spike while energy and metals are falling, because its supply shock comes from a government office, not from global demand. This is the agriculture/softs character: each crop dances to its own weather and policy, which is exactly why the broad-basket allocator treats softs as a diversifier rather than a single bet (see the cross-asset softs note in the further reading).

**"The farmgate VND price and the FOB dollar price are loosely related."** They are tightly linked, not loosely. The milling ratio and the exchange rate chain them together, as the worked examples showed: a move in the FOB dollar benchmark transmits within weeks into the VND paddy price the An Giang farmer receives. When you see the FOB benchmark jump \$100, expect the Mekong farmgate to follow.

**"ST25 winning 'World's Best Rice' is just marketing."** It is marketing, but it is also strategy with real margin behind it. Pushing up the quality ladder into fragrant and specialty grades is how Vietnam defends its average export price against cheaper white-rice competitors. The premium tier is a genuine, durable lever on national export revenue, not a trophy. As the worked example showed, a \$100-per-tonne mix improvement across a couple of million tonnes is a couple hundred million dollars a year — a number worth building a national agricultural strategy around.

**"Rice has a deep futures market like oil and wheat, so the price discovers smoothly."** False, and it changes how the market behaves. The dominant Asian white-rice grades trade physically and over the counter, with the benchmark *assessed* by trade bodies rather than settled on a deep exchange. There is no large speculator pool standing ready to absorb a supply scare. That thin paper layer is precisely why rice prices gap in steps during a shock instead of drifting smoothly — there is no financial cushion, only physical buyers who must find physical rice. Treat rice price action as a series of policy-driven step-changes, not a continuously discovered curve.

**"A weak harvest is what spikes the rice price."** Usually it is the *fear* of a weak harvest, filtered through a government's policy reaction, that does the damage. In 2023 the Indian crop turned out manageable; it was the El-Nino-driven *worry* that triggered the export ban, and the ban that triggered the spike. The forecast moved the policy and the policy moved the price — the realized weather was almost a footnote. This is why a rice trader watches forecasts and policy signals, not just final crop figures.

## How it shows up in real markets

**The 2008 rice crisis — the template.** Before 2023 there was 2008, the canonical rice panic. A cascade of export restrictions — India and Vietnam among others curbing or suspending exports to protect domestic supply — collided with import panic, and the Thai 5-percent benchmark, the world reference, rocketed from around \$350 per tonne to over \$1,000 in a few months before collapsing back. No global shortage; a policy-and-panic spike. Every feature of 2023 was present in 2008, only larger. The 2008 episode is the reason importing nations like the Philippines now hold bigger reserves and sign government-to-government deals — they learned that the lever they cannot control can move the price by a multiple.

It is worth dwelling on the 2008 sequence because it is the clearest demonstration of the export-ban loop running to its limit. The trigger was modest — high global food and fuel prices and a few production worries, nothing like a famine. India restricted non-basmati exports in late 2007 and through 2008. Vietnam suspended new export contracts. The Philippines, the world's largest importer, held a series of high-profile import tenders and, getting nervous about supply, bid aggressively — which the market read as proof of scarcity, sending other importers scrambling too. Each defensive move by one actor looked rational in isolation and was collectively catastrophic: the price nearly tripled in months. Then, just as fast, it broke. The harvests came in fine (there had never been a real shortage), the panic exhausted itself, exporters reopened, and the benchmark collapsed back toward \$500 within a year. The 2008 round trip is the purest illustration in the historical record that a rice spike is a *policy-and-psychology* event riding on a thin market, not a story about the world running out of rice.

**The Philippines as the perennial importer — the demand mirror.** The flip side of Vietnam the exporter is the Philippines the importer, and the two are joined at the hip. The Philippines is chronically rice-deficit — a growing population, limited arable land, typhoon-exposed harvests — and is the world's largest rice importer, sourcing the bulk of it from Vietnam under long-term arrangements. When the 2023 shock hit, the Philippines felt it directly as food inflation, and Manila responded with price caps and tariff cuts to shield consumers. The relationship is the demand-side bookend of everything in this post: Vietnam's record export year was, viewed from Manila, a record import *bill*. A trader watching Vietnamese FOB prices should watch Philippine import policy and tenders as the leading demand signal, the same way they watch Indian policy as the leading supply signal — the two largest single names on opposite sides of the thin market.

**The 2023-24 India ban and Vietnam's record.** Covered above in detail: India's July 2023 non-basmati ban, the Vietnamese 5-percent benchmark to \$650, exports to a record ~9 million tonnes in 2024, and the late-2024 mean-reversion as India eased. The clean lesson is the sequence — policy shock, importer panic, price spike concentrated in the affected grade, gap-filling by the remaining exporters, and reversion once the policy reverses.

**The El Nino overlay.** The 2023-24 episode had a weather amplifier. El Nino conditions threatened Asian monsoons and rice crops across the region, which is part of *why* India moved to protect its domestic supply in the first place. Weather and policy are not separate stories — a feared bad harvest is the trigger that makes a government reach for the export lever. A rice trader watches the monsoon and the policy office together, because the first drives the second. The chain of causation in 2023 ran weather worry to policy reaction to price spike: El Nino raised the odds of a weak Indian monsoon, the weak-monsoon fear pushed New Delhi to protect domestic supply with an export ban, and the ban — not the actual crop loss, which turned out manageable — drove the price. This ordering is the single most useful thing to internalize, because it tells you that the *forecast* of bad weather, by triggering a policy response in a thin market, can move the price more than the *realized* weather ever would on its own.

**The fragrant-rice margin defense.** Through all of this, Vietnam's structural move up into fragrant and specialty rice (the ST25 story) kept lifting its average realized export price even in calmer years. It is the slow-motion version of the same theme: in a market where you cannot control the policy of your biggest competitor, you control your product mix. Over the past decade Vietnam's average rice export price has trended higher even when the white-rice benchmark was flat, precisely because a growing share of its shipments is fragrant rice that sells at a premium. This is the constructive, long-run story underneath the dramatic spikes: a country quietly trading up its product, defending margin against cheaper competitors, and turning "Vietnamese rice" from a generic-commodity label into something closer to a quality brand.

**The farmgate transmission, in real time.** A vivid feature of the 2023-24 episode was how fast the FOB dollar price reached the An Giang and Dong Thap farmgate. As exporters scrambled to fill orders, they competed for paddy, and farmgate prices in the Mekong climbed to multi-year highs within weeks of the world spike. Farmers who happened to hold the right Winter-Spring crop at the right moment saw a genuine income windfall. This is the mechanism that makes the whole subject matter to ordinary people: the chain from a New Delhi policy notice to an An Giang household's income is short, direct, and fast, mediated only by the milling ratio and the exchange rate. When a trader models the rice market, the farmgate VND price and the FOB USD price are not two separate variables — they are the same variable seen at two ends of a very short pipe.

## The playbook: how a trader reads the Asian rice market

Tie it back to the spine of this whole series. A commodity price is a physical thing forced through a financial contract, and the contract here is the FOB dollar benchmark for 5-percent-broken rice. Rice is a consumption staple, not a monetary asset — nobody hoards rice as a store of value the way they hoard gold — but it is the *most political* consumption staple, because governments treat domestic rice supply as national security. That politics is the dominant price variable. Here is how to read it.

**Watch India's policy above all else.** India is roughly 40 percent of world rice trade. Any signal that India is tightening (export bans, duties, minimum export prices) or loosening is the single highest-information event in the market. A tightening move concentrates demand onto Vietnam and Thailand and lifts the 5-percent benchmark; an easing move (as in late 2024) sends it back down. Treat Indian export policy the way an oil trader treats an OPEC+ meeting — it is the supply lever for the whole market.

**Watch the Mekong harvest clock.** Vietnam's supply arrives in three pulses. The Winter-Spring harvest around February-April is the big, high-quality one that loosens the market; the gaps between harvests tighten it. Map the calendar onto the policy backdrop: a policy shock that lands *before* a big harvest, when stocks are thin, hits hardest; one that lands just after Winter-Spring, when the delta is flush, is cushioned.

**Never forget the thin-market multiplier.** Whenever you read a rice headline, immediately separate production from trade. The price reacts to the ~52-million-tonne trade pool, not the ~520-million-tonne production pool. A move that is trivial against world production can be huge against world trade. If you internalize one thing from this post, make it that the relevant denominator is one-tenth the size of the obvious one.

**Read the grade, not just "the price."** A spike is usually a spike in one grade. The 2023 shock hit ordinary non-basmati white rice — the 5-percent benchmark — while basmati and parboiled were spared. Know which grade a policy targets and which exporters and importers it actually touches.

**Track the farmgate-to-FOB chain in both directions.** The VND paddy price and the USD FOB price are tightly coupled through milling and the exchange rate. A jump in the FOB benchmark flows to the An Giang farmgate within weeks; a domestic policy that caps the farmgate caps Vietnam's competitiveness on the FOB. The chain runs both ways, and the exchange rate is the hinge.

**Watch the demand-side name too: the Philippines.** India is the supply lever, but the Philippines — the world's largest importer — is the clearest demand signal. Aggressive Philippine import tenders and stockpiling, as in 2008 and again in 2023-24, broadcast scarcity fear and pull the benchmark up; Philippine tariff cuts and price caps tell you how much import-cost pain is being absorbed at the consumer end. The two biggest single names sit on opposite sides of the thin market, and reading both is how you triangulate where the FOB price is heading.

#### Worked example: sizing a policy shock before it hits the screen

Suppose you read a credible report that India is about to extend its white-rice export restrictions for another full year, and you want a back-of-envelope sense of the price impact before the benchmark moves. Start with the pool that matters: world rice *trade* is about 52 million tonnes. India's restricted white-rice category is on the order of 9-10 million tonnes — call it roughly 18 percent of world trade staying off the market.

In a market with inelastic demand, removing nearly a fifth of traded supply does not require the price to move a fifth — it can require much more, because buyers will not voluntarily cut consumption and instead bid against each other for the remaining surplus. The 2023 episode gives the empirical anchor: a comparable shock moved the Vietnamese 5-percent benchmark from about \$540 to \$650, roughly a 20 percent jump, and held it elevated while the policy persisted. So a reasonable planning assumption for an extension is a benchmark sustained in the \$620-660 range rather than reverting to the \$500s — and the trade is positioned around the *policy* timeline (when India might ease), not around the harvest, because in this market the policy is the dominant variable. The takeaway: you can size a rice policy shock with two numbers — the share of *world trade* removed and the empirical 2023 elasticity anchor — long before the assessed benchmark catches up.

There is also a discipline a good rice trader keeps that separates them from the panic: knowing that policy-and-panic spikes mean-revert. The 2008 spike round-tripped within a year. The 2023 spike faded the moment India eased in late 2024. Because the underlying production was never short, the high prices were always temporary, sustained only as long as the policy and the fear lasted. The mistake the headlines invite is to extrapolate the spike into a permanent new normal; the discipline is to remember that in a market where the shortage is manufactured, the recovery is too. When the policy that caused the spike reverses, the price falls back, and the trader who understood the mechanism is positioned for the reversion while the crowd is still chasing the top.

The unifying insight: rice is the commodity where a single government's pen moves the world price more reliably than weather does, because so little of the harvest is ever truly for sale. The Mekong Delta is the physical engine, the 5-percent-broken FOB benchmark is the financial contract, and Asia's export policy — India's first, Vietnam's second — is the lever. Read the lever, and you read the market. And remember the one number that captures the whole story: \$650 per tonne, the Vietnamese 5-percent benchmark at the peak of a shock that began not with a failed harvest but with a single line in an Indian government notice.
