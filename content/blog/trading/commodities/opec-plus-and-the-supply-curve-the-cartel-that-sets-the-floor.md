> **TL;DR** — OPEC+ is a club of oil-producing governments that tries to set a *floor* under the oil price by collectively holding barrels off the market — and the whole arrangement lives or dies on one number, **spare capacity**, the volume the cartel could pump tomorrow but chooses not to.
>
> - A **cartel** is a group of sellers that coordinates output to lift price above what competition would give. OPEC is the original; **OPEC+** added Russia and nine allies from late 2016, expanding the club's share of world oil to roughly half.
> - **Spare capacity** — barrels that can be brought on within ~30-90 days and held for months, almost all Saudi and UAE — is the market's shock absorber. When it is fat, a supply scare fizzles; when it is thin, every outage becomes a price spike.

On the morning of **12 April 2020**, oil ministers from two dozen countries — exhausted after the longest video-conference negotiation in the cartel's sixty-year history — agreed to do something no group of producers had ever done. They would take **9.7 million barrels a day** off the market in a single coordinated cut: roughly a tenth of all the oil the planet was producing, gone by decree. Saudi Arabia and Russia, who three weeks earlier had been at each other's throats in a price war, signed the same page. The United States, which is not even a member, leaned on the talks because American shale producers were about to go bankrupt by the hundreds.

It did not work fast enough. Eight days later, on 20 April 2020, the front-month WTI contract settled at **negative \$37.63** a barrel — the first time in history oil traded below zero, because the world had simply run out of tanks to put it in. The largest supply cut ever attempted was swamped by the largest demand collapse ever recorded, as a planet in lockdown stopped driving and flying overnight. The cartel had reached for its biggest lever and, for a few terrible days, it barely moved the needle.

That episode contains the whole story of this post. OPEC+ is the most powerful supply-side force in the oil market — and also a deeply *limited* one. It can lean hard on price by adjusting how many barrels it withholds, and most of the time it gets its way. But it is a club of rival governments, each tempted to cheat; it sits on a shrinking share of a market increasingly set by American shale it cannot control; and it can only ever push price, never command it. This post is about how that lever actually works: what OPEC and OPEC+ are, why **spare capacity** is the single most-watched number in oil, the cheating problem that has plagued the cartel since 1960, and the central idea that ties it all together — **OPEC sets the floor, but shale caps the ceiling.**

## Foundations: the supply curve, the marginal barrel, and what a cartel does

Start from zero, because the whole argument rests on one idea from first-year economics that turns out to govern the world's most important commodity.

A **supply curve** is a ranking. Take every barrel of oil the world can produce and line them up from cheapest-to-lift to most-expensive-to-lift. At the far left sit the Gulf barrels — Saudi Arabian, Iraqi, Kuwaiti crude that gushes out of giant fields for a handful of dollars a barrel in operating cost. Move right and the cost climbs: other OPEC+ barrels, then US shale that needs the price somewhere in the \$45-55 range to be worth drilling, then at the expensive far right the deepwater wells and Canadian oil sands that only pencil out when oil is dear. This ordering — cheapest barrel first, most expensive last — is called the **merit order**, and it is exactly what the cover figure above shows: a stack of tiers from the cheap Gulf floor up to the costly ceiling.

Now drop a **demand line** onto that stack. The world wants to consume some quantity of oil — call it about **103 million barrels a day** in a normal recent year. Demand calls the cheapest barrels first and keeps going up the stack until it has gathered every barrel it needs. The *last* barrel it reaches — the most expensive one that still has to be produced to meet demand — is the **marginal barrel**, and here is the crucial, almost magical fact:

> **The price of oil is set by the cost of the marginal barrel — and every other barrel, cheap or dear, sells at that same price.**

The Saudi barrel that costs \$8 to lift and the deepwater barrel that costs \$65 to lift both sell for whatever the marginal barrel costs. The cheap producer simply pockets a fatter margin. This is why the *position of the marginal barrel* — how far up the cost stack demand reaches — is everything. Push demand higher up the stack and the price rises; everyone wins, but the high-cost producers win most because they finally clear breakeven. Pull demand back down the stack and price falls; the high-cost barrels go dark first.

A **cartel** enters this story as a group of low-cost producers who notice something. They sit at the cheap left end of the stack. If they *voluntarily withhold* some of their own cheap barrels — refuse to pump them — they force demand to climb higher up the stack to find its barrels, which lifts the marginal cost and therefore the price *of every barrel they do sell*. They give up some volume to get a higher price on the rest. Whether that trade is worth it depends on arithmetic we will do carefully below. But the mechanism is simple: **a cartel raises price by manufacturing scarcity at the cheap end of the supply curve.**

This is the entire economic basis for OPEC. The members are not magicians and they do not "set" the price by announcement. They move one variable — how much of their cheap supply they choose to leave in the ground — and let the supply curve do the rest. The phrase you will hear traders use is *"the call on OPEC"*: total world demand, minus everything produced by everyone who is **not** in the cartel, equals the quantity the market needs OPEC to supply. OPEC's job is to decide how much of that call to actually meet, and how much to leave unmet to support price.

#### Worked example: the marginal barrel sets the price for everyone

Suppose world demand is **103.0 mb/d**. Non-OPEC producers — the US, Canada, Brazil, Norway and the rest — together pump **70.0 mb/d** no matter what (they are price-takers, drilling as fast as their economics allow). The "call on OPEC" is therefore 103.0 − 70.0 = **33.0 mb/d**. If OPEC supplies all 33.0, demand only has to climb the stack as far as a barrel that costs, say, \$60 to produce, and oil prices around \$60. Now OPEC decides to supply only **31.0 mb/d** — it withholds 2 mb/d. Demand still needs 103.0, so it must call on 2 mb/d of *more expensive* barrels further up the stack — deepwater and stretched shale at, say, \$80 marginal cost. The price of oil rises to roughly **\$80** — and OPEC earns that \$80 on every one of the 31 barrels it *did* sell, not just the marginal one. **By withholding 2 barrels, the cartel lifted the price it receives on the other 31 — that is the whole trick of a cartel, compressed into one line.**

### Why withholding pays: the elasticity of demand

The trick above only works because of a property of oil demand that economists call **inelasticity.** Demand elasticity measures how much the quantity people consume changes when the price changes. If a 10% price rise causes consumption to fall only 1%, demand is *inelastic* (elasticity ≈ −0.1); if a 10% price rise cuts consumption 10%, demand is *elastic* (elasticity ≈ −1.0). Oil demand, especially in the short run, is famously inelastic: when petrol gets more expensive, you still have to drive to work, the airline still has to fly its booked routes, and the factory still needs to run. People grumble and pay. Consumption barely budges for months.

This is exactly the condition under which withholding barrels *raises* a producer's total revenue. The arithmetic is intuitive once you see it. A cartel that cuts output loses revenue on the barrels it no longer sells (a volume effect) but gains revenue on the barrels it still sells because their price went up (a price effect). When demand is inelastic, a small cut in volume produces a *large* rise in price, so the price effect dominates and total revenue goes *up*. When demand is elastic, the opposite holds and cutting is self-defeating. The whole viability of OPEC rests on oil demand being inelastic in the short run — and it reliably is.

#### Worked example: does cutting output raise total revenue?

Start with OPEC selling **33.0 mb/d** at **\$60** a barrel: daily gross revenue is 33.0 × \$60 = **\$1,980 million**. Now cut output by 2 mb/d to **31.0 mb/d**. Because short-run oil demand is inelastic, that ~6% supply cut drives the price up by roughly a third, to **\$80**. New revenue: 31.0 × \$80 = **\$2,480 million a day** — an extra **\$500 million a day** despite selling *fewer* barrels. The cartel produced less oil and made more money. Now run the counterfactual where demand is *elastic* — say the same 2 mb/d cut only lifts price to \$64 because consumers cut back hard: revenue is 31.0 × \$64 = **\$1,984 million**, essentially flat, and the cut wasn't worth the lost volume. **Withholding barrels raises a cartel's revenue precisely because short-run oil demand is inelastic — which is why OPEC's entire strategy is built on the assumption that people cannot quickly stop using oil.**

There is a long-run sting in the tail, though, and OPEC knows it. While demand is inelastic over months, it is far more elastic over *years*: sustained high prices push consumers toward efficiency, electric cars, and alternatives, and push rival producers (shale, deepwater) to drill more. So a cartel that squeezes too hard for too long erodes its own future market — it kills demand and breeds competing supply. This long-run elasticity is the deep reason OPEC+ plays for a moderate, sustainable band rather than the maximum price it could extract today. A barrel left in the ground keeps its value; a customer who buys an electric car is gone forever.

## What OPEC is: the original cartel

**OPEC** — the Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries — was founded in **Baghdad in September 1960** by five governments: Saudi Arabia, Iran, Iraq, Kuwait, and Venezuela. Its purpose was political before it was financial. In the 1950s the global oil trade was run by a handful of Western majors — the "Seven Sisters" — who set the prices that producing countries received for their own crude. OPEC was the producing states banding together to take that pricing power back. Over the decades membership grew and shifted; today it is around a dozen members, dominated by Saudi Arabia (the heavyweight), with the UAE, Iraq, Kuwait, Iran, Nigeria, Libya, Algeria, and others.

The thing to understand is *why these particular countries can form a cartel when most attempts at cartels fail.* Cartels are notoriously unstable — agree to keep prices high, and every member is tempted to quietly undercut. What lets OPEC persist is a specific economic geography: its core members sit at the **very cheap end of the supply curve** and control a **large enough share** of world output that withholding their barrels genuinely moves the price. A cartel of high-cost producers would be pointless (they are the marginal barrel already, with no power to withhold profitably). A cartel controlling 5% of supply would be ignored. OPEC's power has always come from the combination: cheap to produce, and big enough to matter.

How big? At its 1970s peak OPEC controlled over half of world production. That share eroded as non-OPEC supply (the North Sea, Alaska, later shale) grew, and by the mid-2010s OPEC alone was down toward **30-33 mb/d out of ~100 mb/d** — roughly a third. A third of the market is enough to influence price but not enough to dictate it, especially against a rising tide of producers who don't take orders. That erosion is exactly what set up the move that created OPEC+.

A second foundational point: OPEC manages output through **quotas**. Periodically the members meet (historically in Vienna), assess the "call on OPEC," and assign each member a production *ceiling* — a quota. The sum of the quotas is the cartel's collective target. Compliance is then measured against those quotas: a member at 100% compliance is producing exactly its quota; a member at 80% compliance is **over-producing** by a fifth — cheating, in plain language. We will return to why cheating is the cartel's permanent affliction, because it is the single deepest tension in the whole system.

### OPEC+: adding Russia and the allies

By 2016 OPEC had a problem. The US shale boom had flooded the market with new barrels (the full story is in [The Shale Revolution: How the US Became the Swing Producer](/blog/trading/commodities/the-shale-revolution-how-the-us-became-the-swing-producer)), oil had crashed from over \$100 to under \$30, and OPEC's ~33% share was no longer big enough to defend price on its own. A cut by OPEC alone would just hand market share to everyone outside it, including a resurgent Russia, the world's second- or third-largest producer and a non-member pumping flat-out.

The solution, struck in **December 2016**, was to enlarge the club. OPEC's dozen members plus **ten non-OPEC producers** — led overwhelmingly by **Russia**, and including Kazakhstan, Mexico, Oman, Azerbaijan and others — agreed to coordinate cuts together. This bigger alliance is **OPEC+** (sometimes "the Declaration of Cooperation"). Adding Russia's ~10-11 mb/d and the allies pushed the coordinating group's share of world output back up toward **half**, restoring real pricing power.

The Saudi-Russia axis is the load-bearing relationship of OPEC+. Together they are the two largest exporters in the group, and when they agree, the cartel moves; when they fall out, the cartel breaks. We will see both — the March 2020 fallout that triggered a price war, and the post-2022 alignment that defended the floor. But the structural point is this: **OPEC+ exists because the original cartel was no longer big enough to set a floor by itself.** It is OPEC's answer to losing share to shale.

### The Big Three and the shrinking share

To see why the club had to grow, look at who actually produces the world's oil. Three countries each pump roughly 10-13 million barrels a day — the **United States, Russia, and Saudi Arabia** — and they tower over everyone else. The chart below ranks the top producers as of 2024.

![Horizontal bar chart of top crude oil producers in 2024 with the US Russia and Saudi Arabia largest](/imgs/blogs/opec-plus-and-the-supply-curve-the-cartel-that-sets-the-floor-5.png)

Three features of this chart explain the whole modern structure of the oil market. First, **the United States is now the single largest producer** at about 13.2 mb/d — and it is *not in OPEC+*. American shale is a swarm of thousands of independent, profit-seeking companies; there is no US oil ministry, no quota, no one who can order them to cut. That blue bar at the top is the reason OPEC+ can no longer set price by fiat: the biggest producer on Earth answers only to price, not to a cartel. Second, **Russia (amber) is a coordinating ally, not an original OPEC member** — its ~10.8 mb/d is exactly the volume OPEC needed to bring inside the tent in 2016 to rebuild a controlling share. Third, **Saudi Arabia at ~9.6 mb/d is the swing producer** — not the biggest, but the one with the spare capacity and the discipline to actually move the balance.

Add up the OPEC+ members (the amber bars plus the rest of the dozen smaller OPEC states) and the group controls roughly half of world supply — enough to defend a floor. But notice what the chart makes vivid: a huge slice of production, led by that top blue bar, sits *outside* the cartel's control entirely. **OPEC+ is the largest single bloc, but it is a coalition of about half the market trying to manage a balance the other half ignores — which is precisely why its lever sets a floor but not a ceiling.**

#### Worked example: how much of the market does the cartel actually steer?

World crude production runs about **102 mb/d**. From the chart, sum the OPEC+ core: Saudi 9.6 + Russia 10.8 + Iraq 4.3 + UAE 4.0 + Iran 3.3 + Kuwait 2.7 = **34.7 mb/d** from just six members, and adding the smaller OPEC states (Nigeria, Libya, Algeria, etc.) and remaining allies brings the bloc to roughly **48-50 mb/d**, or about **half**. The non-cartel half — the US at 13.2, Canada at 5.0, China at 4.2, Brazil at 3.6, plus Norway, Guyana and others — pumps flat-out regardless of any quota. So when OPEC+ withholds 2 mb/d, it is moving about 4% of total supply, which is plenty to shift the marginal barrel and the price. But it is moving *its own half* while the other half keeps growing. **Controlling half the market is enough to defend a floor by cutting; it is not enough to hold a ceiling against a rival half that adds barrels every time price rises.**

## Spare capacity: the single most important number in oil

If you remember one concept from this post, make it this one. **Spare capacity** is the volume of oil a producer can bring online within roughly **30 to 90 days** and *sustain for months*, but is choosing not to produce right now. It is not "oil in the ground" (that is reserves) and it is not "oil in a tank" (that is inventory). It is *idle, ready production capacity* — wells drilled, infrastructure built, a turn of the valve away from flowing.

Almost all of the world's usable spare capacity sits in **Saudi Arabia**, with a meaningful chunk in the **UAE** and smaller amounts elsewhere. This is not an accident: Saudi Arabia deliberately maintains a buffer of idle capacity — historically 1.5 to 2 mb/d, sometimes much more — precisely so it can act as the world's shock absorber. When a war or a hurricane or a sanction knocks barrels offline somewhere, Saudi can open the taps and replace them, keeping the price from spiking. That role — quietly holding the buffer that stabilizes the whole market — is why people call Saudi Arabia **"the central bank of oil."** Just as a central bank holds reserves it can deploy to stabilize a currency, Saudi holds spare barrels it can deploy to stabilize the price.

Here is why spare capacity is *the* number. The oil market's vulnerability to a price spike depends almost entirely on how much cushion exists. Picture two worlds — and the chart below makes the difference concrete.

![OPEC spare capacity in million barrels per day from 2019 to 2024 with the thin 2022 buffer marked](/imgs/blogs/opec-plus-and-the-supply-curve-the-cartel-that-sets-the-floor-3.png)

In the chart, spare capacity ballooned to about **8 mb/d in 2020** — when everyone had slashed output during the demand collapse, the world was awash in idle capacity, and no supply scare could possibly move price. Then, as demand recovered through 2021 and OPEC+ slowly restored production, the cushion drained. By **2022**, with the recovery in full swing and Russian barrels disrupted by sanctions, OPEC's usable spare capacity had fallen to roughly **3 mb/d** — and almost all of it was Saudi and Emirati. That is a dangerously thin buffer: a single large outage could exceed it, leaving no one able to replace the lost barrels. That thinness is precisely why oil could spike toward \$120 in early-to-mid 2022. The market knew the shock absorber was nearly empty.

The relationship is worth stating sharply: **fat spare capacity = a calm, shock-proof market; thin spare capacity = a fragile, spike-prone one.** A trader who wants to know how dangerous the next geopolitical headline will be looks first at spare capacity. With 6 mb/d of cushion, a 2 mb/d outage is a yawn. With 2 mb/d of cushion, the same outage is a crisis.

#### Worked example: can the cushion absorb a 2 mb/d outage?

Suppose a conflict knocks **2.0 mb/d** of a producer's exports offline overnight. Case A: OPEC's usable spare capacity is **6.0 mb/d** (as in 2020-21). Saudi and the UAE simply raise output by 2.0 mb/d to replace the lost barrels; total supply is unchanged, and price barely moves — maybe a brief \$3-5 risk-premium blip that fades. Case B: spare capacity is **2.5 mb/d** (as in 2022). The cartel *can* just barely cover the 2.0 mb/d loss — but doing so drains the cushion to **0.5 mb/d**, leaving the market with essentially no protection against the *next* shock. Traders price that fragility immediately: even though physical barrels are replaced, the risk premium can add \$15-25 to the price because everyone knows the buffer is nearly gone. **The same 2 mb/d outage is a non-event with a fat cushion and a price crisis with a thin one — which is why spare capacity, not the outage itself, is the number that matters.**

## The supply curve is a political object, not just geology

Here is the deepest idea in the post, and the one that separates oil from almost every other market. In a textbook commodity, the supply curve is pure geology and engineering: it costs what it costs to pull the stuff out of the ground, full stop. In oil, a large slice of the supply curve is **a policy choice.**

When Saudi Arabia holds 2 mb/d of capacity idle, that 2 mb/d is *not* missing from the supply curve because it is too expensive to produce — it is some of the cheapest oil on Earth, costing well under \$15 a barrel to lift. It is missing because a *government decided* to withhold it. The cartel can shift its own portion of the supply curve left (withhold, raise price) or right (open the taps, lower price) by political decision, on a timescale of weeks. No drilling, no exploration, no geology — just a meeting and a press release.

This is why a chart of "the oil supply curve" is partly a chart of OPEC+ intentions. The cheap left end of the curve is governed by ministers, not just by reservoir engineers. And it is why oil prices respond so violently to OPEC meetings: the market is trying to read, in real time, how far the cartel intends to shift the cheap end of the curve. A surprise cut shifts it left and prices jump; a surprise of *no cut* (when the market expected one) effectively shifts it right and prices fall.

The **swing producer** is the entity that performs this shifting. A swing producer is whoever holds enough idle, flexible capacity to move the global balance by raising or cutting output at will — the producer of *last resort* who fills the gap or creates it. For most of OPEC's history, Saudi Arabia was the swing producer: it sat on the spare capacity and made the marginal decision. (As we will see, US shale has partly taken over this role on the *upside*, which is the central plot twist of modern oil. For the full treatment of shale's swing role, see [The Shale Revolution: How the US Became the Swing Producer](/blog/trading/commodities/the-shale-revolution-how-the-us-became-the-swing-producer).)

Being the swing producer is a burden as much as a privilege. The swing producer gives up volume to defend price — it is the one who actually cuts. The free-riders keep pumping and enjoy the higher price the swing producer creates, without sacrificing any of their own barrels. That asymmetry — the swing producer pays, the free-riders gain — is the seed of the cartel's central conflict.

There is a second reason the supply curve is political: each member's *national budget* is funded by oil revenue, and the price each one needs to balance its books — its **fiscal breakeven** — differs wildly. This is the price of oil at which a government's spending equals its income. A country with a small population and huge reserves can balance its budget at a modest oil price; a country with a large population, expensive social programs, or the costs of war needs a much higher price. Saudi Arabia's fiscal breakeven has been estimated in the \$80-90 range in recent years (it funds an ambitious economic-transformation program); some Gulf states balance below \$50; sanctioned or war-strained members may "need" well over \$100 that they have no hope of getting. These divergent budget needs are why members fight over quotas. The low-breakeven members can tolerate cheaper oil and resist cuts; the high-breakeven members are desperate for a higher price and push hardest to cut — but are also the most tempted to cheat, because they need every barrel of revenue. The floor OPEC+ chooses to defend is, in part, a political compromise between members with very different prices to pay their bills. **Where the cartel sets the floor is not an engineering number; it is a negotiated answer to the question "what price do our governments need to survive?"**

## Quota discipline and the cheating problem

A cartel is a promise, and the trouble with promises is that everyone is tempted to break them quietly. This is the cartel's eternal problem, and oil economists describe it with the most famous setup in game theory: the **prisoner's dilemma.**

Consider one OPEC member deciding whether to honor its quota or cheat — pump a bit more than agreed. Look at the payoff matrix above. There are two things the others might do (comply or cheat) and two things our member might do (comply or cheat), giving four outcomes:

- **Everyone complies** (top-left): price stays high, our member earns its fair share of the high-price pie. Good for the group.
- **Our member complies, others cheat** (top-right): our member cuts its barrels, but because everyone else is over-producing the price falls anyway. Our member gave up volume *and* got a low price — the "sucker's payoff," the worst individual outcome.
- **Our member cheats, others comply** (bottom-left): the others' discipline holds the price high, and our member quietly grabs extra volume on top. Best individual outcome — free-riding on everyone else's restraint.
- **Everyone cheats** (bottom-right): the market floods, price collapses, a full-blown price war. Worst outcome for everyone collectively.

Now do the logic each member does. *If the others comply,* I do better by cheating (free-rider win beats fair share). *If the others cheat,* I also do better by cheating (at least I'm not the sucker). So **whatever the others do, cheating is the individually-rational choice** — cheating *dominates*. And since every member reasons identically, the "logical" outcome is the bottom-right cell: everyone cheats, the price collapses, and everyone ends up worse off than if they had all cooperated. That is the prisoner's dilemma, and it is why naive cartels fall apart. (For the formal treatment of dominant strategies, the Cournot game, and why cooperation is hard, see [Cartels, Collusion and the Cournot Game: From OPEC to Algorithms](/blog/trading/game-theory/cartels-collusion-and-the-cournot-game-from-opec-to-algorithms).)

So how does OPEC survive at all? Three things hold the cartel together against the pull toward cheating:

1. **Repetition.** This is not a one-shot game; the members meet again and again, year after year. A member who cheats today can be punished tomorrow — most brutally, Saudi Arabia can flood the market and crush the price, hurting the cheater far more than the cheating gained. The shadow of future retaliation makes cooperation rational *if* members value the future enough.
2. **A dominant enforcer.** Saudi Arabia is large enough that its threat to flood is credible. It is the cartel's policeman precisely because it has the spare capacity to make a price war genuinely painful. A cartel with no enforcer collapses; OPEC has one.
3. **Monitoring.** Tanker-tracking, satellite imagery of storage tanks, and secondary-source production estimates mean cheating is increasingly visible. A cheat that everyone can see is easier to punish, which raises the cost of cheating.

How is cheating actually caught? OPEC has no police force, so it relies on a layered surveillance system that has grown remarkably sophisticated. The cartel itself reports two sets of numbers each month in its Monthly Oil Market Report: each country's *self-reported* production (the "direct communication" figures, which cheaters naturally understate the cheating in) and a set of *secondary-source* estimates compiled by independent agencies, tanker-trackers, and consultancies. The gap between a member's quota and its secondary-source output is the market's read on compliance. Independent satellite firms count the floating-roof storage tanks at export terminals (a tank's lid sits lower when it is fuller, visible from orbit), and tanker-tracking services use ship transponders to watch every loaded cargo leave port. A member that loads more tankers than its quota allows is caught within days — which is why blatant, large-scale cheating is harder than it used to be. The cheating that survives is the quiet, deniable kind: a member that "can't quite" hit its target because of "technical issues," a few hundred thousand barrels here and there.

The other release traders watch obsessively is the **OPEC+ compliance rate**, usually expressed as a percentage of the agreed cut actually delivered. A 120% compliance reading means the group cut *more* than promised (often because some members, like Saudi Arabia, over-deliver to compensate for others' cheating); an 80% reading means a fifth of the promised cut never materialized. When the headline says "OPEC+ cuts 2 mb/d" but compliance is running at 75%, the *real* withdrawal is only about 1.5 mb/d — and a savvy trader prices the 1.5, not the 2.

But the discipline is never perfect, and here is the cruel irony every oil trader internalizes: **compliance is worst exactly when prices are high.** When oil is expensive, every extra barrel a member sneaks out is worth a lot of money, and the temptation to free-ride is at its maximum. So high prices sow the seeds of their own undoing — members cheat, supply leaks higher, and price softens. Compliance is *best* when prices are low and there is little to gain from cheating and a shared terror of making things worse. This is why OPEC's actual compliance rate is a number traders watch like hawks: it tells you how much of the announced cut is real and how much is theater.

#### Worked example: is it worth cheating on a quota?

A member has a quota of **3.0 mb/d** while oil sells at **\$80**. It earns 3.0 × \$80 = **\$240 million a day** (gross). Now it cheats by pumping an extra **0.3 mb/d** — a 10% over-produce that it hopes nobody notices. If the price holds at \$80 (because the cartel's cut is mostly intact), the member now earns 3.3 × \$80 = **\$264 million a day** — an extra **\$24 million a day** for free. That is the free-rider win, and the math is why the temptation is irresistible. But suppose *every* member reasons this way and the whole cartel over-produces by 10%. The extra supply pushes the price down to, say, **\$62**. Now our member earns 3.3 × \$62 = **\$204.6 million a day** — *less* than the \$240 million it earned while complying. **Cheating pays handsomely if you are the only one doing it and disastrously if everyone does — which is exactly the trap that makes a cartel inherently fragile.**

## The episodes: how OPEC actually behaves under fire

Theory is clean; the real cartel is messy. Three episodes since 2014 show OPEC+ reaching for its lever under very different conditions — and they are the case studies every oil trader carries in their head.

![WTI crude annual average price annotated through the 2014 price war the 2020 cut and the 2022 spike](/imgs/blogs/opec-plus-and-the-supply-curve-the-cartel-that-sets-the-floor-6.png)

### 2014-16: the price war meant to kill shale

By 2014, US shale had added millions of barrels a day and oil was oversupplied. The textbook OPEC move would have been to cut — withhold barrels, defend the price. At the **November 2014 meeting**, OPEC, led by Saudi Arabia, did the opposite: it **refused to cut** and kept pumping flat-out. The reasoning was strategic and brutal. Shale was the new high-cost barrel at the right end of the supply curve. If Saudi cut to defend \$100 oil, it would just be subsidizing American drillers — handing them market share while sacrificing its own. Instead, Saudi decided to let the price *crash* and stay low, hoping to drive the high-cost shale producers out of business: a deliberate march down the supply curve to make the marginal barrel unprofitable.

The price fell from over \$90 in 2014 to **\$43 average in 2016** (you can see the cliff in the chart). It was a genuine war of attrition. Shale companies bled, hundreds of smaller ones went bankrupt, and US production did dip in 2016. But shale proved far more resilient than Saudi expected: surviving firms slashed their breakeven costs through better technology, and when prices recovered, drilling roared back. The price war hurt OPEC's own members badly — Saudi burned through foreign reserves, and Venezuela's economy collapsed. By late 2016, Saudi gave up the strategy and pivoted to the opposite approach: enlarge the cartel (OPEC+) and cut to support price. **The price war is the episode that taught OPEC it could not simply crush shale — which is exactly why shale ended up the ceiling-setter.**

### April 2020: the collapse and the historic cut

The 2014-16 war had a sequel. In **early March 2020**, as COVID-19 began to crater oil demand, OPEC+ proposed deeper cuts to defend the price. **Russia refused.** The Saudi-Russia axis broke, and Saudi responded the way the cartel's enforcer always responds to a defector — it announced it would *flood* the market, slashing its official selling prices and threatening to pump at maximum. For three weeks in March 2020, Saudi and Russia waged an open price war into the teeth of the worst demand collapse in history. It was the prisoner's dilemma's bottom-right cell, live: both defected, and the price cratered.

The result was the chaos of April 2020: storage filled, the front-month WTI contract went **negative \$37.63**, and the entire industry faced ruin. That terror is what forced the deal. On **12 April 2020**, OPEC+ agreed the largest coordinated cut ever — **9.7 mb/d**, about 10% of world supply — with even the US informally party to the talks. The cut was real and historic, but the chart shows the limit of the lever: 2020's annual average WTI was still just **\$39**, because no supply cut, however large, can offset a demand collapse of that scale. Supply management defends a price against *supply* shocks and modest demand swings; it is helpless against a once-in-a-century *demand* shock. **April 2020 is the clearest demonstration that OPEC+ pulls the supply lever, not the demand lever — and demand is the bigger force.** The full anatomy of the negative print is its own story; see [Crude Oil: WTI vs Brent, the World's Two Benchmark Barrels](/blog/trading/commodities/crude-oil-wti-vs-brent-the-worlds-two-benchmark-barrels) for why it was WTI specifically that went sub-zero.

### 2022-23: defending the floor

The 2020 cut, then the staged unwinding of it through 2021, worked as intended: as demand recovered, OPEC+ slowly restored barrels and price climbed back from \$39 (2020) to \$68 (2021) to \$95 average in 2022. Then in **February 2022 Russia invaded Ukraine**, Western sanctions disrupted Russian flows, and — with spare capacity already thin (that 3 mb/d buffer from the chart earlier) — oil spiked toward \$120. (How a geopolitical shock transmits to price when the physical barrel cannot flow is the subject of [Geopolitics and Supply Shocks: When the Physical Cannot Flow](/blog/trading/commodities/geopolitics-and-supply-shocks-when-the-physical-cannot-flow).)

What is striking is what OPEC+ did *next*. As the war premium faded and recession fears grew in late 2022, prices began sliding back toward \$75. Rather than let them fall, OPEC+ — now firmly Saudi-Russia aligned — announced a series of **production cuts** through late 2022 and 2023, including a headline 2 mb/d cut in October 2022 and further voluntary Saudi cuts in 2023. The explicit goal was to **defend a floor** somewhere in the \$75-85 range. You can see it in the production chart from earlier: OPEC+ output drifting down from 43 mb/d in 2022 toward 41 mb/d in 2024, a deliberate withholding. **The 2022-23 cuts are the textbook case of the cartel doing exactly what its economics say it should: shifting the cheap end of the supply curve left to keep the marginal barrel — and the price — from falling through the floor.**

![OPEC+ crude production from 2019 to 2024 with the 2020 record cut and 2022 to 2023 defence cuts marked](/imgs/blogs/opec-plus-and-the-supply-curve-the-cartel-that-sets-the-floor-2.png)

## OPEC sets the floor, shale caps the ceiling

Now we arrive at the synthesis — the single most important thing to understand about the modern oil price, and the reason the whole supply curve has changed shape since 2010.

OPEC+ is very good at defending a **floor**. When prices fall, it has a powerful, fast lever: cut quotas, withhold the cheap barrels, force the marginal barrel up the stack, support price. The 2022-23 cuts are this in action. The floor is a *political* decision — the cartel chooses where to defend, and as long as discipline mostly holds, it can prevent a sustained collapse. The floor moves around (it was effectively \$0 in the 2020 chaos and ~\$75 by 2023), but the *mechanism* of defending one is reliable.

What OPEC+ cannot do is defend a **ceiling**. When prices rise high enough, a different producer wakes up: **US shale.** Shale wells are unique on the supply curve — they are short-cycle. A conventional deepwater project takes 5-10 years from decision to first oil; a shale well can go from drilling decision to flowing barrels in a matter of *months*. So when oil rises into shale's profitable range (roughly \$55-70+), American producers add rigs fast, drill fast, and flood new barrels into the market within a year. That surge of supply pushes the marginal barrel back *down* the stack and caps the rally. Shale is the price-responsive barrel that shows up on the *upside* — the de facto ceiling-setter.

This is the great asymmetry of the modern oil market, and the figure below lays out both halves of the clamp.

![Before and after diagram of OPEC defending the floor by cutting and shale capping the ceiling by ramping](/imgs/blogs/opec-plus-and-the-supply-curve-the-cartel-that-sets-the-floor-7.png)

On the downside (left), price falls, OPEC+ meets, cuts a quota, parks spare capacity, and stabilizes price at the floor. On the upside (right), price spikes, shale economics turn green, rigs are added within months, new barrels flood in, and the rally is capped. **Two different actors, two different mechanisms, clamping oil into a band:** the floor is set politically by a cartel withholding cheap barrels; the ceiling is set geologically-and-economically by thousands of independent shale drillers responding to price. Neither is in charge alone. This is why oil has spent much of the post-2016 era oscillating in a band rather than trending to extremes — there is a defender below and a flood above.

The asymmetry also explains a subtle behavioral point. OPEC+ *knows* shale caps the ceiling, so it has stopped trying to push prices to the moon — there is no point, because a \$120 price just summons a shale flood that crashes the price six months later (as 2018 showed). Instead the cartel plays for a *sustainable* band: high enough to fund its members' budgets, low enough not to wake shale up fully or destroy demand. The cartel learned its lesson in the 2014-16 war: it cannot kill shale, so it manages *around* it.

#### Worked example: the floor-and-ceiling band in numbers

Suppose OPEC+ wants to defend a floor of **\$70** and knows shale's "wake-up" price is around **\$75-80** (below that, shale barely grows; above it, rigs pour in). Start with oil at \$95 after a supply shock. Shale responds: over the next year, US producers add enough rigs to bring **1.2 mb/d** of new supply online. That extra 1.2 mb/d pushes the marginal barrel down the stack, and price falls toward **\$78**. Now OPEC+ faces a choice. If it does nothing, shale growth and softening demand could push price below its \$70 floor. So it announces a **1.0 mb/d cut**, withholding cheap barrels to offset shale's flood. The two forces roughly cancel: price stabilizes around **\$75-80** — inside the band. **The price band is not a coincidence; it is the equilibrium of a cartel pushing up from below and a shale industry pushing down from above, each reacting to the other's barrels.**

## Common misconceptions

**"OPEC sets the oil price."** No — OPEC sets a *target* and adjusts its own *supply* to chase it, but the price is set by the global balance of supply and demand at the marginal barrel. In April 2020, OPEC+ made the largest cut in history and oil still averaged \$39 and briefly went negative, because demand had collapsed. The cartel pulls one lever (its share of supply); it does not control demand, non-OPEC supply, inventories, or the dollar. It influences price; it does not dictate it.

**"OPEC is a monolith that acts in unison."** It is a fractious club of rival governments with conflicting budgets, politics, and geology — Saudi Arabia (deep pockets, big spare capacity, plays the long game) versus Iran and Venezuela (sanctioned, desperate for revenue, prone to cheating) versus Russia (huge, non-OPEC, with its own agenda). The 2020 Saudi-Russia price war was members openly fighting each other. Cheating on quotas is endemic. Treating OPEC as a single rational actor will mislead you; it is a coalition that holds together only because Saudi Arabia enforces it.

**"Spare capacity is just oil reserves."** No — these are completely different. **Reserves** are oil in the ground that *could* be produced over years or decades. **Spare capacity** is fully-developed production that could flow within ~30-90 days but is being deliberately held back. Saudi Arabia has both vast reserves and large spare capacity; a country like the US has vast production but essentially *zero* spare capacity (shale runs flat-out). Only spare capacity can respond to a shock in weeks, which is why it, not reserves, is the number that governs short-term price.

**"Higher prices make OPEC discipline stronger."** Backwards. High prices make cheating *more* tempting — every extra barrel snuck out is worth more money — so compliance tends to *fray* when prices are high and *tighten* when they are low and everyone is scared. The cartel is most united in a crisis (2020) and most leaky in a boom.

**"US shale killed OPEC's power."** Overstated. Shale capped OPEC's ability to push prices *high* (the ceiling), but it did nothing to remove OPEC's ability to defend prices *low* (the floor) — in fact shale, with zero spare capacity and a focus on short-cycle returns, *needs* someone to defend the floor, and that someone is still OPEC+. Shale and OPEC+ are not pure rivals; they are the ceiling and the floor of the same band.

## How it shows up in real markets: reading OPEC like a trader

So how does a curious investor actually *use* all of this? OPEC is not a distant idea you admire from afar; it is a set of readable signals. Here is the trader's checklist.

**1. Watch spare capacity first.** Before any OPEC meeting, before any geopolitical headline, the first question is: how much cushion is there? The IEA and EIA publish estimates. Fat spare capacity (5-6+ mb/d) means the market can shrug off shocks and OPEC has room to add barrels — a bearish setup for price. Thin spare capacity (under 3 mb/d) means the market is fragile, every outage is amplified, and the risk premium is structurally high — bullish, and dangerous. In early 2022, the thin buffer was the tell that the war shock would spike violently.

**2. Read the meeting against expectations, not in absolute terms.** OPEC+ meets and the question is never "did they cut?" but "did they cut *more or less than the market expected*?" A 1 mb/d cut when the market priced a 1.5 mb/d cut is *bearish* — it is effectively a disappointment, and price can fall on a cut. Conversely, holding output steady when the market feared an *increase* can rally price. Oil reacts to the surprise relative to the consensus, exactly the way any asset reacts to news. (This "priced-in vs surprise" logic is general; see [Cross-Asset Transmission: How One Print Hits Every Market](/blog/trading/event-trading/cross-asset-transmission-how-one-print-hits-every-market).)

**3. Track compliance — the gap between announced and actual cuts.** A cut is only as real as the members' willingness to honor it. Tanker-tracking firms and secondary-source production estimates reveal whether OPEC+ is actually delivering the barrels it promised to withhold. A headline 2 mb/d cut that turns into a 1.2 mb/d *actual* reduction (because members cheat) is a much weaker price support than the headline implies. When you see prices fail to rally on a "big" cut, the explanation is usually that the market already knows the cut won't be fully delivered.

**4. Locate the floor and the ceiling.** Ask: where is OPEC+ defending, and where does shale wake up? In the 2023-25 era, the de facto floor sat around \$70-75 (Saudi's budget needs and announced cuts), and the shale ceiling sat around \$80-90 (where US rig counts ramp hard). Oil oscillating inside that band is the *normal* state; a sustained break *below* the floor signals OPEC discipline is failing (cheating, or a Saudi-Russia split), and a sustained break *above* the ceiling signals a genuine shortage that even a shale flood cannot fill fast enough — a real crisis.

**5. Remember which shock you are facing.** OPEC+ can defend against a *supply* shock or a modest demand wobble — that is what the spare-capacity lever is for. It is nearly helpless against a *large demand* shock (2008, 2020). When demand is the problem, watch the macro (recession risk, China growth, the dollar), not the cartel. The cartel's lever is supply; if the disease is demand, the cartel's medicine is the wrong one.

**6. Read Saudi Arabia's official selling prices for the cartel's private view.** Each month Saudi Aramco sets the **OSP** — the premium or discount at which it sells its crude to refiners in Asia, Europe and the US, quoted against the regional benchmarks. The OSP is a price the cartel's enforcer sets *with information you don't have* about real physical demand. When Saudi *raises* its OSP to Asian buyers, it is signaling confidence that demand is strong enough to bear higher prices — a quietly bullish tell. When it *cuts* the OSP, it is fighting for market share or bracing for weak demand — bearish. The OSP, the quota decisions, and the spare-capacity number together are the cartel's three observable fingerprints, and a trader who reads all three has a far better picture of the oil balance than one who only watches the flat price scroll across a screen.

#### Worked example: trading an OPEC meeting outcome

Oil is trading at **\$82**. Going into a Sunday OPEC+ meeting, the consensus expects a **1.0 mb/d** extension of existing cuts — that is "priced in." Three scenarios:

- **They cut 1.0 mb/d (as expected):** the news is already in the price. Oil barely moves, maybe a \$0.50 wiggle. *Buying the rumor and selling the news* — a trader who bought ahead expecting the cut takes profit, and price can even dip.
- **They cut 1.5 mb/d (hawkish surprise):** an extra 0.5 mb/d withheld beyond expectations. Oil jumps to perhaps **\$86-88** as the market re-prices a tighter balance — a \$4-6 move on the surprise.
- **They roll over with no new cut (dovish surprise):** the market feared a cut and got none, so it re-prices a looser balance. Oil falls to perhaps **\$77-79.**

**The tradable event is never the cut itself — it is the gap between the cut and what the market already expected, which is why two identical-sized cuts can move price in opposite directions.**

## The takeaway: a powerful lever with hard limits

Pull the threads together and OPEC+ comes into focus not as an all-powerful price-setter, nor as a spent force, but as exactly what the supply curve makes it: **the manager of the cheap, marginal barrels at the left end of the stack, with the power to set a floor and the wisdom (learned the hard way) not to chase a ceiling it cannot defend.**

The mechanics reduce to a few durable truths. The price of oil is set by the marginal barrel, and OPEC+ moves that marginal barrel by choosing how much cheap supply to withhold. Withholding pays only because short-run oil demand is inelastic — people cannot stop driving overnight — and that same inelasticity is the long-run trap, because squeezing too hard for too long breeds the alternatives and the rival supply that erode the cartel's future. Its power is real because its core members are the cheapest producers and big enough to matter; its power is limited because it is a fractious coalition forever tempted to cheat, sitting on a shrinking share of a market increasingly set by American shale it cannot command. Where it chooses to defend the floor is a political compromise between members whose national budgets need very different prices to balance. Spare capacity is the number that governs everything short-term — fat means calm, thin means fragile — and Saudi Arabia, the holder of that buffer, is the central bank of oil. And the whole modern system has settled into a band: OPEC+ defending the floor from below by cutting, shale capping the ceiling from above by flooding.

Connect this back to the spine of the series. A commodity price is a physical thing forced through a financial contract, and the *shape* of the forward curve — contango or backwardation — is the market's running readout of scarcity. OPEC+ is one of the two hands on that scarcity dial (demand is the other). When the cartel withholds barrels and spare capacity is thin, the physical market tightens, the front of the curve can flip into backwardation, and the convenience yield of holding real barrels rises. When OPEC opens the taps and the world is glutted, the curve sags into contango. The cartel does not just move the price level; it shapes the curve that decides who profits from the roll. To read OPEC is, in the end, to read the most political input into the most physical of markets — and to never forget that the floor is a choice while the ceiling is a fact.
