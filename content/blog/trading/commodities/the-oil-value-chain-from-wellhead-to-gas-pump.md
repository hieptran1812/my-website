> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A barrel of crude oil is not a single business. It is a relay race through three very different businesses — **upstream** (find it and lift it), **midstream** (move it and store it), and **downstream** (refine it and sell it) — and each one earns a completely different kind of money.
>
> - **Upstream is price-levered.** Its profit is the oil price minus the cost to lift the barrel, so when oil booms it mints money and when oil crashes it bleeds. This is the boom-bust end of the chain.
> - **Midstream is a toll road.** Pipelines, storage tanks, and terminals charge a fee *per barrel moved*, often locked in by long contracts, so their cash flow barely cares what oil costs. This is the steady end.
> - **Downstream is a margin business.** A refinery earns the *crack spread* — the value of the gasoline and diesel it produces minus the crude it buys — so it can actually do *well* when crude is cheap.
> - The one number to remember: of the roughly **\$3.50** you pay for a gallon of gasoline, the crude itself is usually about **half**; the rest is refining, transport, marketing, and tax. The oil price is the biggest single piece, but it is never the whole story.

In the winter of 2014, the price of crude oil fell off a cliff. WTI went from around \$100 a barrel in the summer to under \$50 by December, and then to the mid-\$20s by early 2016. For the companies that *pump* oil out of the ground, it was carnage — dozens of American shale drillers went bankrupt, rigs were stacked, and entire towns in North Dakota emptied out.

And yet, in that very same stretch, a different set of oil companies were having a *great* time. Refiners — the businesses that buy crude and turn it into gasoline, diesel, and jet fuel — saw their margins fatten. Gas-station chains were busy. Pipeline operators kept collecting the same fees they always had, because the oil was still flowing; only its *price* had collapsed, and they got paid by the *barrel*, not by the dollar.

How can a single commodity going down by two-thirds be a catastrophe for one set of oil companies and a windfall for another? The answer is that "the oil business" is a myth. There is no such thing. There is a *chain* of businesses, strung along the path a barrel takes from a hole in the ground to the nozzle at your local station, and each link in that chain earns money in a fundamentally different way. Understanding that chain is the single most useful map you can carry into the entire energy sector — it tells you *where* in the cycle each kind of company wins, and *which* part of the chain is actually exposed to the thing everyone watches on TV: the oil price.

## Foundations: the three segments of the oil chain

Before any economics, get the geography of the journey straight. A barrel of oil passes through three big stages, and the industry has names for them that you will hear constantly.

**Upstream** is everything to do with *getting the oil out of the ground*. The formal term is **exploration and production**, almost always shortened to **E&P**. Upstream companies hunt for oil (exploration — seismic surveys, drilling test wells), and then, where they find it, they build wells and *produce* the crude — "lifting" it to the surface. Upstream owns the reservoir, the drilling rig, the wellhead, and the pumping equipment. Its product is raw crude oil sitting at the surface, ready to be moved. When you hear "ExxonMobil's production" or "shale drillers" or "the rig count," that is upstream.

**Midstream** is everything that *moves and stores* the crude (and later the refined products). The moment oil comes out of the ground, it has to get somewhere — to a refinery, to an export terminal, to a tank for later. Midstream is the network that does that: **gathering systems** (small pipelines that collect oil from many wells in a field), **trunk pipelines** (the big interstate arteries), **storage tanks** (the giant tank farms, most famously at Cushing, Oklahoma), **tankers and rail**, and **terminals** (where oil is loaded, unloaded, blended, and handed off). Midstream is the toll road of the oil world.

**Downstream** is everything that *turns crude into usable products and sells them*. The heart of downstream is the **refinery** — an enormous chemical plant that boils crude apart into gasoline, diesel, jet fuel, heating oil, and a long list of other products. Downstream also includes **marketing and retail**: the wholesale distribution of those products and, at the very end, the branded gas station where a driver fills up. When you hear "refining margins" or "Shell stations," that is downstream.

So the molecule's journey runs: a well lifts crude (upstream) → a gathering line and pipeline carry it to storage and on to a plant (midstream) → a refinery splits it into products (downstream) → a terminal and tanker truck deliver those products to a station (midstream and downstream) → you pump it into your car (the very end of downstream).

Here is the crucial point that the rest of this post unpacks: **each of those three segments is a different kind of business that earns money in a different way and behaves differently when the oil price moves.** That is the whole game.

### A quick word on units and scale

A **barrel** (abbreviated *bbl*) is 42 US gallons — about 159 litres. It is the universal unit of the oil trade, a leftover from the wooden barrels used in 1860s Pennsylvania, and it has stuck for a century and a half. One barrel of crude, run through a typical refinery, yields very roughly 19–20 gallons of gasoline, 11–12 gallons of diesel and heating oil, 4 gallons of jet fuel, and the rest a mix of other products — and because of how the chemistry expands, a barrel of crude actually produces *slightly more* than 42 gallons of finished products (refiners call this "processing gain"). The world consumes a little over **100 million barrels per day**. That is the scale of the relay race we are describing — a hundred million barrels, every single day, each one running the full length of the chain.

This series is built on one spine: a commodity price is a physical thing forced through a financial contract, and the gap between the physical and the paper is where the action is. The value chain is the *physical* side of that story — the literal path the molecule takes — and it is what gives the paper its meaning. A futures contract on WTI is, at the bottom, a claim on a barrel sitting in a tank in Cushing, which exists only because the chain put it there.

### Why a chain exists at all: the value added at each link

It is worth pausing on a question most people never ask: *why* is oil a chain of separate businesses rather than one company doing everything? The answer is that crude oil at the wellhead is nearly useless to almost everyone. You cannot put raw crude in a car, fly a plane on it, or heat a house with it. It is a thick, dirty, variable mixture of hundreds of different hydrocarbons, contaminated with sulphur, water, and dissolved gas, sitting in the middle of nowhere. Every link in the chain exists to add a specific kind of *value* that turns that useless mixture into a useful product in your hand at the moment you want it.

Upstream adds the value of **extraction** — turning oil that is locked in rock thousands of feet down into oil at the surface. That is a geological and engineering problem, and it is enormously capital-intensive and risky (you can spend hundreds of millions drilling a well that finds nothing). Midstream adds the value of **place and time** — moving the barrel from where it is (a remote field) to where it is needed (a refinery, an export dock) and storing it until the moment it is wanted. Crude in West Texas and crude on the Gulf Coast are *different* economic goods even though they are chemically identical, because one is next to a refinery and the other is 500 miles away; midstream closes that gap, and gets paid the difference. Downstream adds the value of **transformation** — splitting the useless mixture into the specific, clean, specified products (87-octane gasoline, ultra-low-sulphur diesel, jet-A) that engines actually run on, and then distributing them to the corner where you need them.

Each link adds a distinct value, so each link can be a distinct business with its own economics, its own competitors, and its own cycle. A useful way to keep this straight is the question *"what is this link being paid for?"* Upstream is paid for taking geological and price risk. Midstream is paid for bridging distance and time. Downstream is paid for chemical transformation and last-mile convenience. The pump price you pay is the sum of all those value-adds plus the tax stacked on top — which is exactly the structure the final worked example will lay out gallon by gallon.

## Upstream: the price-levered boom-bust business

Upstream is the part of the chain everyone instinctively pictures when they think "oil company" — derricks, gushers, drilling rigs. And it is the part most directly exposed to the oil price, because of one simple arithmetic fact: **an upstream company's profit per barrel is the oil price minus the cost of producing that barrel.** The oil price is in the numerator. When it moves, profits move with brutal leverage.

To see this clearly you have to separate two different cost numbers that get confused all the time.

The **lifting cost** (also called the *operating cost* or *cash cost*) is what it costs to *operate an existing well and pump a barrel out* — the electricity for the pumps, the labour, the maintenance, the chemicals. It does *not* include the money already spent finding the oil and drilling the well. Lifting costs are often startlingly low: for the cheapest Middle Eastern fields, a few dollars a barrel; for mature US wells, maybe \$10–20.

The **full-cycle cost** (or *breakeven*) is the *total* cost of the barrel including everything: the exploration, the drilling, the equipment, the financing, the taxes and royalties paid to the resource owner, plus the lifting cost — spread across all the barrels the well will ever produce. This is the number that decides whether it makes sense to drill a *new* well in the first place. Full-cycle breakevens range from the teens for the best onshore fields up to \$60–80 for the hardest sources.

The gap between these two matters enormously during a price crash. If oil falls below a field's *full-cycle* cost, companies stop drilling *new* wells (no point spending money you can't recoup) but keep producing the *existing* ones as long as the price covers the *lifting* cost. That is why production does not collapse the instant prices fall — the sunk cost of an already-drilled well is gone, so a producer pumps as long as each barrel covers its cash cost. Only when prices fall below lifting cost do wells actually get shut in.

![A cost ladder of crude oil sources from low cost Middle East onshore through US shale to high cost deepwater and oil sands](/imgs/blogs/the-oil-value-chain-from-wellhead-to-gas-pump-4.png)

This cost ladder is why the *source* of a barrel matters so much. A Saudi barrel that lifts for a few dollars is profitable at almost any price. A US shale barrel with a \$45 breakeven is a money machine at \$90 and a money pit at \$40. A deepwater or oil-sands barrel needs persistently high prices just to justify the enormous upfront capital. So when oil falls, the chain doesn't break evenly — the high-cost end of the ladder goes dark first. This is also why we say upstream is the *swing*: the marginal barrel — the most expensive one the world still needs — sets the price, and that marginal barrel has migrated over the decades from Texas to the Gulf to the North Sea and, since the 2010s, back to US shale. (That story gets its own post; see [The Shale Revolution: How the US Became the Swing Producer](/blog/trading/commodities/the-shale-revolution-how-the-us-became-the-swing-producer).)

#### Worked example: upstream profit at \$40 versus \$90 oil

Take an E&P company whose **full-cycle breakeven is \$35 a barrel** — a decent shale operator. Watch what the oil price does to its profit per barrel.

- **At \$90 oil:** profit per barrel = \$90 − \$35 = **\$55**. On 100,000 barrels a day, that is \$5.5 million a day of operating profit, or roughly \$2 billion a year. The company is swimming in cash, paying dividends, buying back stock.
- **At \$40 oil:** profit per barrel = \$40 − \$35 = **\$5**. On the same 100,000 barrels a day, that is \$500,000 a day, or about \$180 million a year — *one-eleventh* of the high-price figure.

The oil price fell by 56% (from \$90 to \$40). The company's per-barrel profit fell by **91%**. That asymmetry — a moderate move in the price causing a violent move in the profit — *is* operating leverage, and it is the defining feature of upstream. **An E&P company is, financially, a leveraged bet on the oil price minus a roughly fixed cost, which is why upstream earnings swing far harder than crude itself.**

And this leverage is exactly why the WTI chart below is, first and foremost, an *upstream* chart. Every spike is an upstream boom; every crash is an upstream bust. The same line that is merely a cost input to a refiner is the entire fortune of a driller.

![WTI crude oil annual average price from 2000 to 2025 showing the 2008 spike the 2015 crash and the 2020 collapse](/imgs/blogs/the-oil-value-chain-from-wellhead-to-gas-pump-3.png)

### Who owns the oil? Resource owners versus the operators

There is a second layer to upstream that beginners often miss: the company doing the drilling frequently does not *own* the oil in the ground. In most of the world, the *state* owns the subsurface minerals. An international oil company that wants to produce there signs a contract with the government — a concession, a production-sharing agreement, or a service contract — that splits the barrels (or the cash) between the operator and the resource owner. The national oil companies (Saudi Aramco, Iran's NIOC, Mexico's Pemex, Brazil's Petrobras) sit on the largest reserves on earth and are state-controlled.

The United States is the great exception: there, private landowners typically own the minerals under their property, which is a big part of *why* the shale revolution happened in America and nowhere else — a farmer in Texas could lease his mineral rights to a driller and collect a royalty, aligning thousands of small landowners with the industry. (The Vietnam case is different again — the state owns the resource and works it through the national champion; see [Oil & Gas Sector Vietnam: The PVN Chain](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/oil-gas-sector-vietnam-the-pvn-chain).)

The practical upshot for reading the chain: a chunk of every high-price windfall does not go to the listed E&P company at all — it goes to the resource owner via royalties and taxes. When you hear that a government's budget "needs \$80 oil to balance," that is the resource owner's breakeven, a political number layered on top of the geological one.

This split also explains a chronic confusion in headlines. When oil jumps and you read that "oil companies are making record profits," the profit is being shared across a *long list* of claimants on each barrel: the operator, the state via royalties and taxes, the service companies that drilled the well, the landowner, and the bondholders. The listed E&P's slice — the one a shareholder owns — is the *residual* after all the senior claims are paid, which is precisely why it is the most leveraged. A high oil price lifts the whole stack, but it lifts the thin residual slice at the bottom by the largest *percentage*, because the costs above it are roughly fixed. That residual-claimant arithmetic is the same operating-leverage point as the \$40-versus-\$90 example, seen from the ownership side rather than the cost side.

### The volume story underneath the price

Upstream earns price *times volume*, and while the price is the volatile part, volume is the slow, structural story. The single biggest volume shift of the century was the US shale boom: American crude production roughly *tripled* from about 5 million barrels a day in 2008 to a record above 13 million by 2024. That extra ~8 million barrels a day reshaped the entire global map — it is why the US flipped from the world's largest importer toward energy self-sufficiency, and a large part of why the 2014–16 price crash happened at all (the world simply had more oil than it knew what to do with).

![US crude oil production from 2005 to 2024 rising from about five to over thirteen million barrels per day](/imgs/blogs/the-oil-value-chain-from-wellhead-to-gas-pump-5.png)

For an upstream investor, this is the second dial to watch alongside price: is the company *growing* its barrels, holding them flat, or in decline? A driller can have a falling oil price but rising volume (the shale playbook of the 2010s) and partly offset the pain. The combination of the two — price and volume — is the upstream P&L in a single sentence.

### The decline curve and the treadmill of drilling

There is a feature of upstream that has no parallel in most businesses and that explains its restlessness: **oil wells deplete.** The day a well is drilled is the day of its peak output, and from then on the flow falls along a *decline curve*. For a conventional well the decline is gentle — a few percent a year. For a shale well it is brutal: a typical tight-oil well can produce half of its first-year output and then fall **60–70% in the first year** and keep dropping fast, so most of a shale well's lifetime oil comes out in the first two or three years.

That single fact reshapes the entire upstream business into a treadmill. To merely *hold production flat*, a shale-heavy producer has to keep drilling new wells continuously, just to replace the steep decline of the ones it already has. Stop drilling and output falls within months. This is why the **rig count** is watched so closely as a forward signal: rigs drilling today are the barrels of six to twelve months from now. It is also why US shale is called *short-cycle* — capital goes in and oil comes out within a year, unlike a deepwater megaproject that takes five years to first oil. Short-cycle economics make shale the fastest-responding source on earth, which is precisely why it became the swing producer: it can ramp up and throttle down in response to price faster than any cartel can.

The treadmill has a profound investment consequence. An upstream company's reported earnings can look wonderful in a boom while the company is quietly *liquidating itself* — pumping barrels faster than it replaces them with new discoveries or drilling. The number that captures this is the **reserve replacement ratio**: how many barrels of new reserves the company books for each barrel it produces. Below 100% and the company is shrinking its future, however good this year's profit looks. A serious upstream investor reads the reserve report, not just the income statement.

### Royalties, taxes, and the capital-expenditure cycle

Layer on the cost structure and you get the boom-bust rhythm that defines the segment. In good times, high prices and high cash flow tempt every producer to drill aggressively — capital expenditure surges, rig counts climb, costs of rigs and crews and sand inflate, and the new supply eventually floods the market and *crashes the price the producers themselves caused*. In bad times, capex is slashed, drilling stops, decline curves quietly eat away at supply, and eventually the shortfall pushes prices back up. The industry is famous for this self-inflicted cycle: capital discipline collapses at the top and over-corrects at the bottom. An investor who understands the decline-curve treadmill can read where in this capex cycle the industry sits — and the most dangerous time to own an upstream pure-play is at the euphoric top, when everyone is drilling and the next glut is being manufactured.

## Midstream: the toll road that barely cares about the oil price

Now walk one link down the chain. The crude has been lifted; it is sitting at the wellhead. It has to *get somewhere* — and that is the midstream business, the least glamorous and, for many investors, the most attractive segment, because of one structural feature: **midstream gets paid to move and store barrels, not to own them.**

A pipeline operator does not buy the oil at one end and sell it at the other, taking the price risk. It *rents out capacity* in its pipe. A producer pays a fixed fee — say, \$2 a barrel — to ship its crude from the field to a hub. The pipeline collects that fee whether oil is \$40 or \$120. Its revenue is *volume × tariff*, and the tariff is set by long-term contracts and, for interstate pipelines in the US, regulated. The oil price barely enters the equation.

This is the toll-road analogy, and it is exact. A highway toll booth charges you the same dollar to cross the bridge whether your car is a cheap hatchback or a luxury sedan; it is selling *passage*, not the car. A pipeline sells passage for barrels. Storage works the same way — a tank farm rents you a tank by the month, and collects rent whether the oil inside is rising or falling in value.

The contracts that make this so stable are worth naming. The classic midstream deal is **take-or-pay**: the shipper commits to pay for a minimum volume of capacity *whether or not it actually ships it*. If a producer reserves space for 100,000 barrels a day and only ships 60,000, it still pays for 100,000. From the pipeline's point of view, this converts volatile usage into a near-guaranteed revenue stream — closer to a bond coupon than to an oil bet. It is why midstream companies (often structured as master limited partnerships, or MLPs, in the US) have historically been prized for their fat, steady dividends.

#### Worked example: a midstream toll calculation

Suppose a pipeline runs 300 miles from a shale basin to a coastal export terminal. Its tariff is **\$2.20 per barrel** for the full route. It has signed take-or-pay contracts for **400,000 barrels per day** of committed capacity.

- **Contracted revenue floor:** 400,000 bbl/day × \$2.20 = **\$880,000 per day**, or about **\$321 million a year** — locked in regardless of the oil price, because the shippers pay for the capacity whether they use it or not.
- **Now crash the oil price from \$90 to \$45.** The pipeline's revenue change: essentially **zero**. The barrels still flow (producers keep pumping existing wells down to lifting cost), and even barrels that *don't* flow are still paid for under take-or-pay. The toll is \$2.20 either way.

Compare that to the upstream company from earlier, whose profit fell 91% on the same price move. **The same \$45 crash that gutted the driller left the pipeline's cash flow almost untouched — that is the difference between owning the barrel and merely renting it passage.** This is why, in a downturn, capital flees upstream and crowds into midstream: investors are paying up for a cash flow that does not care about the headline they are afraid of.

### Why Cushing matters

There is one piece of midstream geography you have to know, because it sits at the centre of the most-quoted oil price on earth. **Cushing, Oklahoma** is a small town that happens to be the largest crude-oil storage and pipeline hub in the United States — dozens of pipelines converge there, feeding into tens of millions of barrels of tank capacity. Cushing is the *physical delivery point* for the WTI futures contract. When the NYMEX WTI contract expires, the seller is obligated to deliver real barrels into tanks at Cushing, and the buyer is obligated to take them.

That single fact is why the WTI price can do strange things when Cushing fills up. The infamous day in April 2020 when WTI futures went *negative* happened precisely because Cushing's tanks were nearly full — the holders of expiring contracts faced taking delivery of barrels with literally nowhere to put them, and paid others to take the obligation off their hands. Storage is the shock absorber of the entire chain, and when the absorber is full, the physical market and the paper market collide. (The mechanics of that collision — spot versus futures, contango, and the cost of carry — are the heart of [Spot vs Futures: The Two Prices of the Same Barrel](/blog/trading/commodities/spot-vs-futures-the-two-prices-of-the-same-barrel) and the forward-curve posts.)

The investment lesson from Cushing is more general: **storage economics flip the midstream business from boring to lucrative at exactly the moment the rest of the chain is in pain.** When the market is oversupplied and the forward curve is in steep contango — future prices well above today's — anyone with an empty tank can buy cheap oil now, sell it forward at a higher price, and pocket the difference for the cost of storage. In a glut, the tank itself becomes the most valuable asset in the chain. This is the link straight back to the series spine: the *cost of carry* and the *contango* that make the storage trade work are the financial gears turning on top of a physical tank, and whoever owns the tank profits from the shape of the curve. (See [Contango vs Backwardation: What the Shape of the Curve Means](/blog/trading/commodities/contango-vs-backwardation-what-the-shape-of-the-curve-means) for the curve mechanics.)

### The basis differential: why location is a price

There is a subtler way midstream shapes the oil market, and it is one professional traders live by: the **basis differential** — the price gap between crude at two different *locations*. Crude trapped in a landlocked basin with not enough pipeline capacity to carry it out trades at a *discount* to crude sitting at a coastal hub with easy access to refineries and export docks. The discount is, in effect, the price the market puts on the missing pipeline. When the Permian Basin in West Texas outran its pipeline capacity around 2018, Midland crude traded as much as \$10–20 a barrel *below* the same crude at the Gulf Coast — a punishing penalty for being stuck. The day a new pipeline opened, that discount collapsed, because the barrels could finally get out.

For a producer, this is brutal: you can be pumping at a healthy headline oil price and still receive far less because your barrels are stranded. For a midstream company, it is the source of value: building the pipeline that *closes* the differential is how it earns its toll, and the size of the differential before the pipe is built is a direct read on how much that pipe is worth. **The oil price you see on the screen is the price at one location; the price a producer actually receives is that benchmark minus a basis differential that midstream capacity sets — distance is literally a number on the invoice.** This is the same WTI-versus-Brent logic at a finer grain (a landlocked benchmark versus a seaborne one), explored in [Crude Oil: WTI vs Brent](/blog/trading/commodities/crude-oil-wti-vs-brent-the-worlds-two-benchmark-barrels).

### How midstream is owned: the MLP structure

A quick note on a quirk that explains why midstream attracts a particular kind of investor. In the United States, many pipeline and storage companies are organised as **master limited partnerships (MLPs)** rather than ordinary corporations. An MLP pays no corporate income tax; instead it passes its cash flow straight through to its unit-holders, who are taxed personally. Combined with the steady, fee-based, take-or-pay cash flows of the toll-road business, this structure historically produced large, tax-advantaged distributions — which is why income-seeking investors (and retirees) have long flocked to midstream MLPs as a kind of "oil bond." The flip side is that an MLP that over-distributes leaves itself little cushion when a customer goes bankrupt or a project needs funding, and several stumbled in the 2014–16 crash for exactly that reason. The structure does not change the underlying economics — a toll road is a toll road — but it changes who owns it and why.

## Downstream: the margin business that loves cheap crude

The last link is the one that touches your daily life: the refinery and the gas station. And it is the segment with the most counterintuitive economics of all, because **downstream often does *best* when crude oil is *cheap*.**

To understand why, you have to stop thinking of crude oil as the product and start thinking of it as the *raw material*. A refiner does not sell crude. It *buys* crude and sells gasoline, diesel, jet fuel, and the rest. Its profit is the difference between what it pays for the input and what it gets for the outputs. The industry has a name for that difference: the **crack spread** (so called because the refinery "cracks" the long hydrocarbon molecules in crude into shorter, more valuable ones).

The most quoted version is the **3-2-1 crack spread**: it models a refiner taking *3 barrels of crude* and producing *2 barrels of gasoline and 1 barrel of distillate* (diesel/heating oil). The spread is the dollar value of those 2+1 product barrels minus the cost of the 3 crude barrels, divided by 3 to express it per crude barrel. When that number is high, refining is hugely profitable; when it is low, refineries can actually lose money and shut down for "maintenance."

The crucial insight: the crack spread depends on the *gap* between crude and product prices, not on the *level* of crude. So a refiner can thrive when crude is cheap — if product prices (set by what drivers and airlines will pay) hold up while the crude input falls, the gap *widens*. That is exactly what happened in 2015 and again, dramatically, in 2022. It is also why an integrated company with a big refining arm can partly hedge its own upstream pain: when oil crashes and the drilling division bleeds, the refining division's margins can fatten.

![The 3-2-1 crack spread the downstream refining margin in dollars per barrel from 2018 to 2024 spiking in 2022](/imgs/blogs/the-oil-value-chain-from-wellhead-to-gas-pump-7.png)

Look at that spike in 2022. Crude was high that year — but products were *higher still*, because of a global shortage of refining capacity (refineries had closed during the pandemic) running into a surge of post-lockdown demand. Refiners that year earned the fattest margins in a generation. The crack spread is its own market, with its own supply-and-demand for refining *capacity*, layered on top of the crude market. (The full mechanics of cracks, gasoline-vs-diesel, and the seasonal product cycle get a dedicated post: [Refining and Crack Spreads: Turning Crude Into Products](/blog/trading/commodities/refining-and-crack-spreads-turning-crude-into-products).)

#### Worked example: a barrel's economics, all the way to the pump

This is the example that ties the whole chain together. Let us trace the money in a single gallon of gasoline at the pump, at a moment when crude is around \$84 a barrel (so \$2.00 per gallon of crude, since 84 ÷ 42 = \$2.00) and the pump price is **\$3.50 a gallon**. (These proportions are illustrative but close to typical US conditions.)

| Stage | Who earns it | Per gallon |
|---|---|---|
| Crude oil (the barrel itself) | Upstream + resource owner | \$2.00 |
| Pipeline, storage, transport | Midstream | \$0.15 |
| Refining (the crack margin) | Downstream refiner | \$0.45 |
| Distribution and retail marketing | Downstream marketer + station | \$0.30 |
| Taxes (federal + state, US average) | Government | \$0.60 |
| **Pump price** | **You pay** | **\$3.50** |

Read down the column and the structure of the whole industry jumps out. The crude — the thing on every news ticker — is the biggest single slice at \$2.00, about **57%** of the pump price. But it is *not* the whole price. Of the remaining \$1.50, the government takes \$0.60 in tax, the refiner takes \$0.45 as its crack margin, the marketer and station split \$0.30, and the midstream toll is a modest \$0.15. **The oil price dominates the pump price but never fully explains it — which is exactly why gasoline at the pump falls more slowly than crude when crude drops, and why a refiner can be fattening its \$0.45 slice on the very day the upstream driller's \$2.00 slice is collapsing.** Each stage owns a different piece of your \$3.50, and each piece responds to a different force.

### Why the pump price is "sticky"

That table also explains a thing every driver notices and resents: when crude oil crashes, the pump price falls, but *slowly and incompletely* — "rockets up, feathers down." Part of the reason is structural. Roughly 43% of your \$3.50 (the tax, the refining margin, the retail margin, the transport) does not move at all when crude moves — those are fixed or slow-moving slices. So even a 30% crash in crude only mechanically pushes about 57% of the price, and only the crude portion of it. The pump is anchored by the half of the price that has nothing to do with the wellhead. Knowing the table turns a vague grievance into a precise expectation.

### The product slate: not all gallons are equal

The refiner's economics get richer once you see that crude does not become *one* product — it becomes a *slate* of them, each with its own price and its own demand cycle. From light to heavy, the main cuts are: light gases (propane, butane), gasoline, jet fuel and kerosene, diesel and heating oil (the "middle distillates"), and at the bottom the heavy residual fuel oil and asphalt. The *value* of these products differs enormously: gasoline and diesel are the high-value heart of the slate; heavy residual fuel oil is often worth *less* than the crude it came from. So a refiner's profitability depends not just on the crack spread in aggregate but on its **product yield** — how much of the valuable light products it can squeeze out of each barrel.

This is where refinery *complexity* becomes an asset. A simple refinery just boils crude apart and sells whatever falls out, including a lot of low-value heavy stuff. A complex refinery has expensive extra units (a "cracker," a "coker," a "hydrotreater") that take the cheap heavy bottoms and chemically *upgrade* them into more gasoline and diesel. Complexity costs billions to build, but it lets a refiner buy *cheaper, heavier, dirtier* crude and still produce a high-value light slate — capturing a wider margin. So two refiners facing the same crude and product prices can earn very different cracks depending on what they were built to do.

### Seasonality: the calendar is a price input

Downstream margins also breathe with the calendar in a way the upstream barrel does not. Gasoline demand peaks in the **summer driving season** in the northern hemisphere, so gasoline cracks tend to fatten in spring as refiners build inventory ahead of the rush, and the United States even switches to a more expensive *summer-blend* gasoline (less prone to evaporating in heat) that adds a few cents and a supply bottleneck every May. Diesel and heating oil run the opposite pattern, firming into the **winter** as heating demand climbs in cold climates. A refiner can tilt its slate toward gasoline in spring and distillate in autumn to chase whichever crack is fattest — a flexibility the rigid upstream barrel simply does not have. For an investor, the practical signal is that refining-margin numbers should always be read against the season: a "weak" gasoline crack in January is normal, not a warning.

## Integrated majors versus pure-plays

Now zoom out from the chain to the *companies* that operate on it, because there are two fundamentally different ways to own a piece of oil.

A **pure-play** company operates in *one* segment. A pure-play E&P (like a focused shale driller — Pioneer, Diamondback in their heydays) does only upstream. A pure-play refiner (Valero, Phillips 66's core) does only downstream. A pure-play midstream firm (Enterprise Products, Kinder Morgan) does only pipelines and storage. A pure-play is a *concentrated bet* on its segment's economics — maximum leverage to its piece of the chain, and nothing to cushion it.

An **integrated major** — the supermajors: ExxonMobil, Shell, Chevron, BP, TotalEnergies — owns *all three* segments at once. It drills the oil, ships it through its own pipelines, refines it in its own plants, and sells it at its own branded stations. Its barrel can travel the entire chain inside one company.

The investment consequence is the heart of figure 6: **integration smooths the cycle, while a pure-play amplifies it.** When crude is high, the integrated major's upstream arm prints money — but its downstream refining margins get squeezed (expensive crude input). When crude crashes, upstream bleeds — but downstream margins fatten on cheap input. The two arms partly offset, so the integrated company's *total* earnings swing less violently than either segment alone. It is a natural internal hedge.

#### Worked example: integrated versus pure-play through a price swing

Take a stylised year where oil falls from \$90 to \$45.

- **Pure-play E&P** (breakeven \$35, from earlier): per-barrel profit goes from \$55 to \$10 — an **82% collapse** in upstream profit. Its stock, which is essentially a leveraged oil-price proxy, gets crushed.
- **Pure-play refiner:** its crack spread might *widen* from \$15 to \$22 a barrel as crude (the input) falls faster than products. Its profit *rises* perhaps 40%. Its stock does well in the same crash.
- **Integrated major** owning both: the upstream collapse is partly offset by the downstream gain. Say upstream is 60% of earnings and downstream 40%. A rough blend: 0.6 × (−82%) + 0.4 × (+40%) ≈ **−33%**. Painful, but a third of the pure-play E&P's drop.

The numbers are illustrative, but the *shape* is real and it is the whole point. **The pure-play hands you the full, undiluted swing of its segment — glorious in a boom, brutal in a bust — while the integrated major trades away some of the upside in exchange for a far gentler ride down.** Which one you want depends entirely on where you think you are in the cycle and how much volatility you can stomach. (For how oil fits a whole portfolio, see [Energy: Oil & Gas, the Inflation Engine](/blog/trading/cross-asset/energy-oil-gas-the-inflation-engine).)

### The case against integration, and why it persists

For all its appeal, integration is not a free lunch, and the market has long argued about whether the supermajors should even stay integrated. The bear case is the **conglomerate discount**: a sprawling company that owns wells, pipes, plants, and stations is hard for any single analyst to value cleanly, and investors who wanted pure upstream exposure could just buy a pure-play E&P and pair it with a pure-play refiner themselves — getting the same blend without paying a manager to bundle it for them. By that logic the market should value an integrated major at *less* than the sum of its parts, and activists periodically push the majors to break up and "unlock" that hidden value, as several refiners and chemical arms have indeed been spun out over the years.

The counter-argument — why integration survives — is partly the internal hedge we just quantified, partly *operational*: an integrated company has guaranteed outlets for its own crude (its refineries), guaranteed feedstock for its refineries (its own crude), a trading arm that profits from the price and location differentials *between* its own links, and the balance-sheet heft to fund a five-year deepwater project that no pure-play could carry through a downturn. In a crisis, that diversification is exactly what let the majors defend dividends that pure-plays slashed. The debate never fully resolves, which itself is informative: integration is a trade-off between *resilience* and *focus*, and which one the market rewards swings with the cycle — focus is prized at the euphoric top, resilience at the terrified bottom.

## Common misconceptions

**"When oil goes up, all oil stocks go up."** No. Upstream and downstream often move in *opposite* directions on the same day, because high crude is good for the driller's selling price and *bad* for the refiner's input cost. In 2022, US refiners had a record year for crack margins; in 2020, refiners suffered while integrated majors' downstream arms were the only thing keeping them afloat. "Oil stocks" is not one trade — it is at least three.

**"Pipelines are an oil-price bet."** Mostly false. A pipeline's cash flow comes from per-barrel tariffs under long-term, often take-or-pay contracts, so it tracks *volume*, not price. In the 2014–16 crash, well-run midstream operators kept paying their dividends because the barrels kept flowing. The real risks to midstream are *volume* risks — a basin drying up, a customer going bankrupt and walking away from its contract, or a regulator cutting the tariff — not the flat oil price.

**"The gas-station owner is getting rich when prices spike."** Backwards. The retail station's margin is a thin, roughly fixed few cents a gallon, and it gets *squeezed* when prices spike fast (wholesale costs jump before the station can pass them through) and *eased* when prices fall (it can hold the pump price up a little longer). The big swings in the pump price are crude and tax, not station profit. The station makes more money on the coffee and the snacks than on the fuel.

**"Cheap oil is bad for the whole oil industry."** Only for the upstream half. Cheap crude is genuinely good for refiners (cheaper input), good for the airlines and truckers and plastics makers who *consume* products, and roughly neutral for pipelines. "The oil industry" contains both producers and *consumers of crude* — and a low oil price simply transfers money from the first group to the second, down the chain.

**"A barrel is worth the oil price."** A barrel of *crude* is worth roughly the crude price, yes — but the *products* refined from it are usually worth more, which is the entire reason refineries exist. And the gallon of those products you actually buy carries a stack of transport, refining, marketing, and tax on top. The "oil price" is the value at one specific point — the wellhead-ish, Cushing-delivered point — not the value of the molecule everywhere along the chain.

**"All barrels are the same."** Not even close. Crude varies by *density* (light vs heavy) and *sulphur content* (sweet vs sour), and those differences carry real money. Light, sweet crude (like WTI) is easy to refine into high-value gasoline and diesel and commands a premium; heavy, sour crude needs a complex refinery to process and trades at a discount. A barrel of Canadian oil-sands crude and a barrel of Saudi light crude are *different products* with different buyers, different refineries, and different prices, even though both show up in the news as simply "oil." The benchmark prices you see quoted (WTI, Brent) are specific grades at specific points; every other barrel trades at a differential to one of them, set by its quality and its location. The single word "oil" hides a whole catalogue of distinct goods moving through distinct parts of the chain.

## How it shows up in real markets

**The 2014–16 crash: the chain splits apart.** This is the cleanest case study in the book. WTI fell from ~\$100 (mid-2014) to the mid-\$20s (early 2016) as shale flooded the market. Upstream: dozens of US E&P bankruptcies, the rig count collapsing from ~1,600 to ~400, capital expenditure slashed industry-wide. Midstream: largely intact — barrels kept flowing, take-or-pay contracts held, though some operators with too much exposure to bankrupt shippers took hits. Downstream: refiners enjoyed healthy margins on cheap crude, and the integrated majors leaned on their downstream and trading arms to defend dividends none of them cut. One commodity, three completely different outcomes, exactly as the chain predicts.

**April 2020: storage breaks the chain.** When COVID lockdowns vaporised demand, the world kept producing oil it could not use. Cushing filled toward capacity, and on 20 April 2020 the expiring WTI contract settled at **−\$37.63** — holders paid to escape a delivery obligation into tanks that were full. This was a *midstream* event masquerading as a price event: the binding constraint was *storage*, not the value of crude itself. Spot crude in the cash market was low but positive; the negative number was a paper artefact of the physical chain hitting a wall. It is the most vivid demonstration ever of why the physical link — storage at Cushing — sets the meaning of the paper price.

**2022: the downstream's turn.** Russia's invasion of Ukraine spiked crude, but the bigger story for the *chain* was refining. Years of refinery closures (pandemic-era shutdowns, energy-transition retirements) had quietly shrunk global refining capacity just as travel roared back. Product prices outran even the high crude, and the 3-2-1 crack spread blew out to roughly \$38 a barrel — the fattest refining margins in a generation. Pure-play refiners had a banner year; the integrated majors posted record *total* profits as upstream *and* downstream fired at once, a rare alignment. (For how these shocks ripple across every asset, see [Geopolitics, Elections, and Unscheduled Shocks](/blog/trading/event-trading/geopolitics-elections-and-unscheduled-shocks).)

**The chain as an inflation transmission line.** Step back and the value chain is also the route by which an oil shock reaches the rest of the economy — and the route reveals *why* the shock is so pervasive. A jump in crude at the wellhead does not just raise the price you pay at the pump; it flows down the chain into the cost of *everything that moves or is made*. Diesel sets the cost of trucking and shipping, so a crude spike lifts the delivered price of groceries and goods. Jet fuel lifts airfares. Petrochemicals from the refinery feed into plastics, fertiliser, and packaging. This is why economists watch oil as a leading inflation input: a barrel that gets more expensive at the well becomes, weeks later, a more expensive loaf of bread on a shelf 1,000 miles away, because the chain carried the cost the whole distance. The pump-price table earlier is the consumer-facing tip of a much larger pass-through that runs through every product the chain produces. (How that single input ripples into bonds, equities, and currencies is the subject of [Commodities as Macro Signals: Oil, Copper, Gold](/blog/trading/macro-trading/commodities-as-macro-signals-oil-copper-gold) and [Oil, Inflation, and Equities: The Energy Linkage](/blog/trading/cross-asset/oil-inflation-and-equities-the-energy-linkage).)

**The trading houses: the chain's connective tissue.** One more player threads the whole chain without owning much of it: the commodity trading houses (Vitol, Glencore, Trafigura). They buy a cargo of crude at the wellhead end of one country, charter the tanker, store it, ship it, and sell it into a refinery on the other side of the world — profiting on the *geographic and time spreads* the physical chain creates. They are the arbitrageurs who make sure a barrel flows from where it is cheap to where it is dear, and they live precisely in the gaps between the chain's links. When the curve goes into contango they fill tankers and tanks; when a region runs short they redirect cargoes mid-ocean toward the highest bid. They monetise, in real time, every differential the chain throws off — by location, by grade, by time. (See [Commodity Trading Houses: Glencore, Vitol, Trafigura](/blog/trading/finance/commodity-trading-houses-glencore-vitol-trafigura).)

## The takeaway: how to read the chain as an investor

The value chain is not trivia. It is a working map, and here is how to use it.

**Locate the company on the chain before you form a view.** The first question about any oil-related investment is: *which segment?* An E&P pure-play is a leveraged bet on the oil price minus a breakeven you can look up. A midstream firm is a toll-collector whose risk is volume and contracts, not price. A refiner is a bet on the *crack spread* — the gap, not the level. An integrated major is a smoothed blend of all three. Putting them in the same "oil stocks" bucket is the single most common analytical error in the sector.

**Match the segment to where you think the cycle is.** If you believe oil is heading higher and you want maximum exposure, the upstream pure-play with the lowest breakeven gives you the most leverage. If you think oil is going to *fall* but demand for products will hold, a refiner can profit from the widening crack. If you have no view on price but want a steady income, the midstream toll road is the segment whose cash flow cares least about the number you can't predict. If you want oil exposure but cannot stomach the swings, the integrated major's internal hedge buys you a gentler ride. The chain tells you which tool fits which view.

**Watch the right number for each segment.** For upstream: the oil price *relative to the company's breakeven*, and its production volume. For midstream: throughput volume, contract coverage (how much is take-or-pay), and any regulatory tariff risk. For downstream: the crack spread and refining utilisation. Reading an upstream company off the crack spread, or a refiner off the flat oil price, gets you the wrong answer.

**Read the chain as a relay, not a single race.** The deepest habit to build is to stop asking "what is oil doing?" and start asking "what is *this barrel* doing at *this stage*?" When crude spikes, run the chain in your head: upstream wins, downstream's input cost rises (watch whether product prices keep up — if they don't, cracks compress), midstream is indifferent, the consumer at the pump pays more, and inflation gets a nudge weeks later as diesel feeds through to everything that moves. When crude crashes, run it the other way: upstream bleeds (and the high-breakeven sources go dark first), refiners may feast on the widening crack, midstream keeps collecting tolls, the storage trade lights up if the curve tips into contango, and the pump price falls slowly because half of it never depended on crude. The same shock produces opposite outcomes at different links, and naming the link tells you the sign. That is the entire reason to carry the map: it converts a single, scary headline number into a precise, segment-by-segment set of expectations.

**Remember the spine.** A barrel is a physical thing forced through a financial contract, and the chain is the physical journey that gives the contract its meaning. The futures price at Cushing exists only because the midstream put a barrel in a tank there; the crack spread exists only because the downstream can turn that barrel into something worth more; the whole structure of contango and storage economics (the gears of this whole series) is the chain's *time* dimension, the cost of holding a barrel between two links. When you can see the molecule's journey from wellhead to pump — and see a different business earning a different margin in a different way at each stop — the energy sector stops being one confusing blob and becomes a set of distinct, readable bets. That is the edge the value chain gives you.
