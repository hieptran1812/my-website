Roughly **80%** of palladium and a large share of platinum go into autocatalysts, which is why a market many people file under "precious metals" actually trades like an auto-parts supply chain with a geopolitics problem bolted on.
>
> - The two metals split by **engine type**: a gasoline engine's converter uses **palladium** (plus a sliver of fabulously expensive **rhodium**); a diesel engine's converter historically used **platinum**. That single chemical fact drives everything.
> - When palladium ran far above platinum — the 2018-2022 squeeze that peaked near **\$3,440/oz** intraday in March 2022 — automakers paid engineers to redesign gasoline catalysts and swap cheaper platinum back in. That **substitution** is the trade that defines the complex, and it is why the palladium price that looked unstoppable then collapsed by half.

A thin, concentrated supply plus a swinging demand picture equals very high variance.
> - The one fact to remember: the electric-vehicle transition threatens **both** metals — a battery car has no tailpipe, so it needs **no autocatalyst at all** — while the hydrogen **fuel cell**, which needs platinum, is the complex's one big demand hope. PGMs are a bet on which of those futures arrives, and how fast.

On the morning of 7 March 2022, palladium did something a precious metal is not supposed to do: it went nearly vertical. Russia had invaded Ukraine eleven days earlier, the West was assembling sanctions, and traders suddenly realized that roughly two ounces in five of the world's palladium came out of a single Russian company's mines above the Arctic Circle. The metal, which had already spent four years grinding higher, leapt toward **\$3,440 an ounce** in intraday trading — more than gold, more than platinum, more than it had ever been worth. For a few days palladium was the most expensive of the major precious metals, a metal most people have never knowingly held, trading at a price that implied the world's car factories might not be able to get enough of it to keep building gasoline engines.

And then, over the next two years, it fell by **half**. By 2024 palladium averaged around **\$1,000 an ounce**, back below platinum, the squeeze fully unwound. No new mine flooded the market. What killed the palladium price was not supply — it was **demand re-engineering**. Faced with a metal that had become ruinously expensive, the world's automakers had quietly spent those years redesigning their gasoline catalytic converters to use less palladium and more platinum, which was cheaper. The substitution they set in motion at the top of the squeeze took years to bite, but when it did, it broke the trade.

That round trip — a four-year squeeze to a record high, then a halving — is the single best lesson in how the platinum-group metals actually work. These are not monetary metals you buy as insurance against the dollar (that is gold's job, and we link out to it below). They are **catalysts**: rare, dense, chemically magical metals whose entire economic reason for existing is to make a chemical reaction happen faster, and whose dominant use is to sit in your car's exhaust system turning poison into harmless gas. To understand their price, you have to understand a catalytic converter, an emissions regulation, an engineer's bill of materials, and the map of where on Earth these metals come out of the ground. This post walks through all of it.

## Foundations: what the platinum-group metals are and why a converter needs them

Start from zero, because almost nobody outside the trade knows what these metals do.

The **platinum-group metals** — PGMs for short — are a cluster of six chemically similar elements that sit together in the periodic table: **platinum (Pt)**, **palladium (Pd)**, **rhodium (Rh)**, plus the lesser-traded ruthenium, iridium, and osmium. For commodities purposes, three matter: platinum, palladium, and rhodium. They are *precious* in the literal sense — rare and expensive, priced per **troy ounce** like gold and silver, around 31.1 grams to the ounce. But they are precious for a completely different reason than gold is. Gold is valued because it is inert, scarce, and has been money for five thousand years. The PGMs are valued because they are the best **catalysts** humanity has found for a set of reactions that the modern world cannot do without.

A **catalyst** is a substance that speeds up a chemical reaction without being consumed by it. It provides a surface on which reactant molecules can meet and rearrange far more easily than they would on their own, and then it walks away unchanged, ready to do it again millions of times. Platinum, palladium, and rhodium happen to be superb catalysts for the specific reactions that turn the toxic gases coming out of an engine into harmless ones. That is the whole game. Their density, their resistance to corrosion, their stability at the searing temperatures inside an exhaust pipe — all of it makes them nearly irreplaceable for this one job.

### What a catalytic converter actually does

When you burn gasoline or diesel in an engine, you do not get clean combustion. You get a hot stream of exhaust laced with three regulated poisons: **carbon monoxide (CO)**, a colorless lethal gas; **unburned hydrocarbons (HC)**, the raw fuel that did not combust; and **nitrogen oxides (NOx)**, which cause smog and acid rain. Left alone, every car would pump these straight into the air, and cities would be unbreathable — as some were, before the 1970s.

The **catalytic converter** is the device that fixes this. It is a metal canister in your exhaust line, packed with a honeycomb ceramic substrate that has been coated with a "washcoat" carrying a tiny but precious amount of PGM. As the hot exhaust flows through the honeycomb, the PGM surface drives two kinds of reaction at once: it **oxidizes** the CO and hydrocarbons into harmless carbon dioxide and water, and it **reduces** the NOx back into ordinary nitrogen and oxygen. A converter that does all three is a **three-way catalyst**, and it is one of the great unsung engineering achievements of the twentieth century. It is also the reason the air in big cities got dramatically cleaner from the 1980s on, even as the number of cars exploded.

Here is the crucial point for a trader: that "tiny but precious amount of PGM" is, multiplied across the roughly **90 million** new vehicles the world builds every year plus the existing fleet, the **dominant source of demand** for these metals. Autocatalysts consume on the order of **80% of all palladium**, a large share — historically over **40%** — of all platinum, and almost all rhodium. Jewelry, industrial chemistry, electronics, and investment make up the rest. When you trade platinum or palladium, you are, first and foremost, trading the **car industry's appetite for converters** and the **regulations that force them to exist**.

### The full demand breakdown — and why platinum has more legs than palladium

It is worth pinning down where every ounce goes, because the *diversity* of demand is itself a trading variable. Palladium is the more concentrated bet: roughly **80-85%** of palladium demand is autocatalysts, with the small remainder split across electronics (palladium is used in multilayer ceramic capacitors and some plating), dentistry, and a modest investment slice. That concentration is a double-edged sword — it makes palladium a clean, high-beta play on gasoline-car production, but it also means palladium has almost nowhere to hide when autocatalyst demand turns down. There is no large second market to absorb the metal if cars stop needing it.

Platinum's demand is more diversified, which is exactly why it has the more interesting long-run case. Autocatalysts are the biggest single use (historically around **40%**), but platinum also has a substantial **jewelry** market (especially in China, where platinum jewelry rivals gold in some years), a real **industrial-chemical** market (platinum catalysts are used to make nitric acid, silicones, and petroleum products), a growing **hydrogen** use (fuel cells and electrolyzers), and a meaningful **investment** market in bars, coins, and exchange-traded funds. So platinum has four or five demand legs to palladium's one-and-a-half. When you model the two metals, you are modeling two very different demand *structures*, not two flavors of the same thing.

This is also why the "investment" framing is a trap for these metals. Yes, you can buy platinum and palladium coins and ETFs, and a small slice of demand is investment-driven. But the investment tail does not wag the industrial dog. Unlike gold — where the dominant marginal buyer is an investor or central bank reacting to real interest rates — the marginal PGM buyer is a **catalyst manufacturer** filling an order from an automaker who is building cars to meet an emissions standard. Price the metal off *that* buyer, not off the coin shop.

### Why platinum and palladium are not the same trade

The single most important distinction in the whole complex is which metal goes in which kind of engine, because the two metals are partial **substitutes** but not perfect ones, and the substitution is the trade.

- **Gasoline engines** run a three-way catalyst built primarily around **palladium**, with a small but vital amount of **rhodium** (rhodium is extraordinarily good at the NOx-reduction step and has traded as high as **\$20,000+ an ounce**, dwarfing the other two). Gasoline is the world's dominant fuel, so palladium demand is enormous.
- **Diesel engines** historically used **platinum**-rich catalysts, because platinum tolerates the cooler, oxygen-rich diesel exhaust better. Diesel is big in Europe and in trucks, so platinum's autocatalyst demand was historically tied to **diesel's** share of the car market.

That split is why these two metals, chemically cousins, can trade like entirely different assets. A regulation or a fuel-mix shift that helps gasoline hurts platinum and helps palladium; the reverse helps platinum. And because engineers *can* — with effort and cost — redesign a gasoline catalyst to use some platinum in place of palladium, the **price spread** between the two metals is not a passive readout. It is a force. When palladium gets dear enough relative to platinum, it pays to re-engineer, and the engineering flows demand from one metal to the other. Hold that idea; it is the spine of this post.

## The squeeze: how palladium ran to a record and why

The figure below is the central data picture of the complex: palladium's four-year climb to a March-2022 intraday peak near **\$3,440 an ounce**, then its collapse back below **\$1,100** by 2024. Every feature of that curve has a cause worth understanding.

![Palladium annual average price with the 2018 to 2022 squeeze to 3,440 dollars and the 2023 to 2024 collapse](/imgs/blogs/platinum-and-palladium-the-autocatalyst-metals-2.png)

For most of the 2000s and early 2010s, palladium was the cheaper, less glamorous PGM — it traded *below* platinum, often well below. In 2015 palladium averaged around **\$690 an ounce** while platinum averaged over **\$1,050**. The "precious" PGM in the public mind was platinum, the metal of luxury watches and wedding bands. Palladium was the industrial afterthought.

Then four forces lined up, all pushing palladium demand up and palladium supply tight, all at once:

**1. Dieselgate killed diesel — which gutted platinum demand and pumped up gasoline.** In September 2015, regulators caught Volkswagen using software to cheat diesel emissions tests. The "Dieselgate" scandal shattered consumer and regulatory trust in diesel across Europe, the one region where diesel had majority share of passenger cars. Diesel's market share went into a multi-year decline; buyers switched to gasoline. Because diesel converters use **platinum** and gasoline converters use **palladium**, this was a direct demand transfer: platinum's biggest demand engine sputtered while palladium's roared.

**2. Tightening emissions rules raised the PGM *loading* per car.** Standards like Euro 6 and China 6, plus tighter US rules, demanded that converters scrub *more* pollutant per car. Cleaner exhaust means more catalyst surface, which means **more grams of PGM packed into each converter**. So even before any change in the number of cars, the metal *per car* was rising. For gasoline cars, that meant more palladium and rhodium.

**3. Supply could not respond.** Unlike oil, you cannot drill a new palladium well in a year. PGM mines are deep, capital-intensive, and concentrated in two countries (more on this below). Palladium is also mostly a **byproduct** — it comes out of the ground alongside platinum and nickel, so you cannot simply mine "more palladium" without mining more of everything else, regardless of which metal's price is screaming. Supply was structurally flat against rising demand.

**4. Then the Russia risk premium.** Russia, through Norilsk Nickel, supplies roughly **40%** of the world's palladium. As tensions rose and then war broke out in February 2022, the market priced the risk that a chunk of that supply could be sanctioned or cut off. That fear is what produced the violent final spike toward **\$3,440** in early March 2022 — a war premium stacked on top of an already-tight market.

The **byproduct problem** in force 3 deserves a closer look, because it is what makes PGM supply so uniquely unresponsive to price — far more so than oil or even copper. When palladium's price triples, the textbook response is "produce more palladium." But you cannot. The economics of a South African PGM mine or Norilsk's operation are set by the *basket* of metals in the ore — platinum, palladium, rhodium, plus nickel and copper at Norilsk — not by any single metal. The geology fixes the ratios: a given orebody yields, say, two parts platinum to one part palladium in South Africa, and you cannot tell the rock to hand you a different mix because palladium got expensive. So a palladium-specific demand surge cannot pull out palladium-specific supply. The only ways to add palladium are to (a) mine *more of the whole basket* (slow, capital-intensive, and only worthwhile if the *whole* basket pays), or (b) recycle more old converters. Neither responds in a quarter. This is why a palladium squeeze can run for *years* — the supply curve is nearly vertical in the short run, so all the adjustment has to come from the **demand** side, through the slow substitution loop described in the next section.

There is also a deeper reason this matters for how you trade the complex. In a "primary" commodity like oil, a high price pulls out more supply, which caps the price — a self-correcting system on the supply side. In a byproduct metal like palladium, that supply governor is largely missing; the *only* self-correcting force is demand re-engineering, which is slow and lagged. A market whose sole equilibrating mechanism is a multi-year demand response is a market that **overshoots**, hard, in both directions. That is the structural reason PGMs are high-variance, separate from the geopolitics — the byproduct nature removes the fast supply brake that tames most commodity squeezes.

#### Worked example: a catalyst-loading calculation — how many dollars of metal are in one car

Make the demand concrete. A modern gasoline car's three-way catalyst contains, very roughly, **5 grams** of palladium, plus a small amount of rhodium and sometimes a little platinum. (Loadings vary widely by engine size and emissions standard; treat this as a representative midsize gasoline car.) An ounce is 31.1 grams, so 5 grams is about **0.161 troy ounces** of palladium.

At palladium's quiet 2015 price of **\$690/oz**, that 5 grams cost the automaker 0.161 × \$690 ≈ **\$111** per car. At the March-2022 squeeze price near **\$3,000/oz** (using a round trading level, below the intraday spike), the *same 5 grams* cost 0.161 × \$3,000 ≈ **\$483** per car. Now multiply by the rhodium content — even a fraction of a gram of rhodium at \$15,000-\$20,000 an ounce can add another \$100-\$200 — and you can see how the PGM bill in a single gasoline car swung from roughly **\$150** to well over **\$600** in a few years.

For an automaker building 10 million cars a year, a \$450 swing in the per-car metal bill is **\$4.5 billion** of annual cost. **That is the dollar pressure that funds an engineering team to redesign the converter — and it is exactly why the squeeze sowed the seeds of its own reversal.**

## The substitution trade: why the squeeze ate itself

This is the most important mechanism in the whole complex, so it gets its own figure. When palladium ran far above platinum, the cost saving from swapping platinum back into a gasoline catalyst became large enough to justify the considerable R&D expense of re-engineering. That re-engineering, plus ordinary "thrifting" (using fewer total grams through better catalyst design), slowly drained palladium demand — and the price that looked unstoppable rolled over.

![The substitution loop: palladium dear, then automakers swap platinum back in, then palladium demand eases and the spread converges](/imgs/blogs/platinum-and-palladium-the-autocatalyst-metals-4.png)

Read the loop left to right. A palladium price far above platinum creates a large per-car saving available to any automaker who can use platinum instead. That saving funds catalyst-chemistry research; engineers find washcoat formulations and catalyst architectures that tolerate platinum in the high-temperature gasoline environment that previously favored palladium. At the same time, every emissions cycle pushes engineers to **thrift** — to hit the standard with fewer total grams of precious metal, because the cheapest gram is the one you do not use. Both forces reduce the palladium drawn into each new converter. Demand eases — slowly, because car platforms take years to redesign and the existing fleet is already built — and the spread compresses back toward parity. By 2024, palladium was back below platinum, and the substitution that began at the top of the squeeze was the reason.

The key feature is the **time lag**. Substitution does not happen the day the price spikes. Catalyst formulations must be developed, validated against emissions standards over thousands of test miles, and rolled into car platforms on multi-year design cycles. So the demand response to a 2019-2021 price shock shows up in 2023-2025. This lag is what lets a squeeze run far and long before it breaks — and it is why the *reversal*, when it comes, can be just as violent as the spike. A trader who understood the substitution loop in 2021 had a thesis for why palladium would eventually fall even while it was still rising.

#### Worked example: the platinum-for-palladium substitution arbitrage

Put numbers on the swap. Suppose a gasoline catalyst uses **5 grams** of palladium. The metals trade at **palladium \$2,400/oz** and **platinum \$1,000/oz** — a spread of \$1,400 an ounce, palladium dear. In grams, that is (\$2,400 − \$1,000) ÷ 31.1 ≈ **\$45 per gram** of spread.

If an automaker can re-engineer the catalyst to replace, say, **3 of the 5 grams** of palladium with platinum (the swap is rarely one-for-one and never total, but partial substitution is very real), the gross metal saving is 3 grams × \$45/gram ≈ **\$135 per car**. Across 10 million cars that is **\$1.35 billion a year**. Against that, the automaker must weigh the R&D, validation, and re-tooling cost — call it a one-time \$200-\$400 million program — which the annual saving repays in well under a year at this spread.

So at a \$1,400 spread the swap is a screaming go. At a \$200 spread (the metals near parity), the per-gram saving is ~\$6.4, the 3-gram saving is ~\$19 per car, ~\$190 million a year across the fleet — barely above the program cost, and not worth the engineering risk. **The substitution incentive is a direct function of the spread: wide spread, the swap pays and palladium demand will erode; narrow spread, the swap stalls and the two metals trade on their own supply-and-demand again.**

## The great flip: platinum and palladium swap places

The next chart overlays the two metals on one axis, and it tells the whole multi-decade story in one picture: platinum traded *above* palladium for a generation, then palladium ran *far above* platinum through the squeeze years, then the two converged back toward parity as substitution and the EV scare did their work.

![Platinum versus palladium annual average prices showing the flip where palladium rose far above platinum then reconverged](/imgs/blogs/platinum-and-palladium-the-autocatalyst-metals-3.png)

Trace the blue platinum line and the red palladium line. In 2015, platinum (\$1,053) sits comfortably above palladium (\$690) — the old normal, where platinum was the senior, more valuable PGM. The lines cross around 2017-2018. From there palladium soars away — \$2,192 in 2020, \$2,398 in 2021 — while platinum stagnates in the \$880-\$1,090 range, weighed down by collapsing diesel demand. The shaded region marks the **inversion era**, the years palladium traded above platinum. Then both retreat and reconverge: by 2024 palladium (\$1,000) has fallen back to roughly platinum's level (\$950), the flip undone.

Why did platinum stay so weak even as palladium screamed? Because platinum's biggest demand engine was **diesel**, and diesel was dying in exactly the years palladium was booming. The same Dieselgate shock that boosted palladium (by pushing buyers to gasoline) **hurt** platinum (by killing diesel). Platinum had a partial offset from jewelry and a small one from early hydrogen demand, but nothing that could match the loss of its diesel base. So the flip was not one metal getting strong in a vacuum; it was a **demand transfer** between two substitute metals, driven by a single regulatory scandal and an emissions-rule cycle.

The convergence back toward parity by 2024 is the substitution loop closing. As automakers swapped platinum back into gasoline catalysts, platinum gained a new demand source just as palladium lost one — and the spread that had blown out to over **\$1,300** in palladium's favor compressed back to roughly zero. The flip, and the un-flip, are the same trade viewed from two ends.

#### Worked example: a supply-disruption price impact — what a South African power cut does

PGM supply is tight and concentrated, so a supply hit moves the price hard. Suppose South Africa, which produces about **75%** of the world's platinum, suffers a severe bout of **load-shedding** (rolling power cuts — a chronic problem for the South African grid) that forces smelters and deep mines to throttle back, knocking out **10%** of the country's platinum output for a quarter.

10% of South Africa's 75% global share is **7.5% of world platinum supply** removed for the quarter. In a market where annual demand is roughly in balance with supply and above-ground stocks are modest, a 7.5% supply loss is enormous — there is no spare capacity to fill it, because you cannot ramp a deep PGM mine quickly. With demand close to inelastic in the short run (a car factory cannot stop needing converters), the price has to rise enough to ration the shortfall and pull metal out of inventories. Historically, supply scares of this size have moved platinum **15-30%** in weeks. If platinum is at \$1,000/oz, a mid-range 20% move is **+\$200/oz** — a \$200 swing from a power problem on another continent. **In a thin, concentrated market with no shock absorber, a regional disruption translates almost one-for-one into a global price spike, which is exactly why PGMs are a high-variance bet.**

## Supply: two countries, one chronic risk each

We have said "concentrated" several times. Here is what that means. The chart below shows where the world's platinum and palladium actually come out of the ground — and it is the single scariest feature of the complex from a supply-security standpoint.

![Illustrative mine supply shares for platinum and palladium showing South Africa and Russia dominate both metals](/imgs/blogs/platinum-and-palladium-the-autocatalyst-metals-6.png)

(These shares are rounded to the well-known public figures from USGS and Johnson Matthey; treat them as illustrative of the structure, not to the decimal.)

Two countries dominate, and each carries a distinct, chronic risk:

- **South Africa** mines roughly **three-quarters** of the world's platinum and a large share of its palladium and rhodium, almost all of it from the **Bushveld Complex**, a vast layered geological formation. South Africa's PGM mines are among the **deepest and most labor-intensive** in the world, which means the supply is hostage to two chronic problems: an **unreliable electricity grid** (the state utility Eskom has imposed years of rolling load-shedding, which throttles smelters and hoists) and **labor relations** (the sector has a history of long, bitter strikes — the 2014 platinum strike lasted five months and removed a huge volume of supply). Every South African power crisis or wage standoff is a live supply risk for the whole complex.

- **Russia** supplies roughly **40%** of the world's palladium, almost all of it from **Norilsk Nickel**, whose mines sit above the Arctic Circle and produce palladium as a byproduct of nickel and copper. Russian palladium is exposed to **sanctions and geopolitical risk** — the fear that war, sanctions, or a transport disruption could remove a large slice of palladium supply overnight. That fear is precisely what drove the March-2022 spike.

Put the two together and you get the headline fact: **South Africa and Russia between them supply roughly three-quarters of *both* PGMs.** There is no Saudi Arabia of platinum holding spare capacity to open the taps in a crisis — no shock absorber. When one of these two suppliers wobbles, the price has nowhere to hide. This is the opposite of the oil market, where OPEC spare capacity and the US shale "swing producer" can dampen shocks (we link out to those mechanisms below). PGMs are a market where a regional power cut or a sanctions headline is a global price event.

There is one structural cushion worth naming: **recycling**. A meaningful and growing share of PGM supply — on the order of a quarter to a third for some of these metals — comes from **autocatalyst recycling**, recovering platinum, palladium, and rhodium from the converters of scrapped cars. Recycling is more responsive to price than mining (high prices pull more scrapped converters into the recovery stream) and is geographically diversified, so it slightly softens the concentration problem. But it is not enough to offset a major primary-supply disruption, and the scrapped cars being recycled today were built with the loadings of a decade ago.

Recycling also has a strange, slow rhythm that matters for the long-term picture. The converters being scrapped *today* came out of cars built **10-15 years ago** — so the recycled-supply stream is a delayed echo of the loadings and fuel mix of the past, not the present. As the gasoline fleet built during the high-loading 2010s reaches end of life over the 2020s and 2030s, the recycling stream will actually *grow* — even as new-car demand for PGM falls because of electrification. There is a real scenario where, a decade out, recycled supply from the old combustion fleet is climbing into a market where new-car demand is shrinking, producing a double squeeze on the price. The "urban mine" of scrapped converters becomes a more important supplier precisely as the primary demand it once served fades. This is one more reason the long-run PGM picture is a *demand-loss* story more than a supply-scarcity story — the scarcity narrative that justified the squeeze is gradually being replaced by an abundance-of-scrap narrative on a long lag.

A second under-appreciated supply nuance is the **lead time** on new mine capacity. Even when prices justify expansion, sinking a new deep shaft in the Bushveld or developing a greenfield PGM project takes the better part of a decade from decision to first metal. So the supply response to the 2018-2022 squeeze — to the extent miners even believed the high prices would last — is only arriving now, into a market whose demand outlook has already darkened because of EVs. Miners who committed capital at \$2,400 palladium are bringing it on at \$1,000 palladium. This whipsaw between a decade-long supply lead time and a faster-moving demand picture is endemic to the complex and is a large part of why PGM equities (the miners) are even more volatile than the metals themselves.

## The EV cliff and the hydrogen hope: the demand future

Now the part that makes PGMs a genuinely high-variance, almost binary, long-term bet. Everything above is about *internal-combustion* cars — cars with a tailpipe. The catalytic converter exists to clean *exhaust*. A **battery electric vehicle (BEV)** has no engine, no combustion, and **no exhaust**. It therefore needs **no catalytic converter and no PGM at all**. Every BEV that replaces a gasoline or diesel car removes an autocatalyst's worth of platinum or palladium demand from the world — permanently.

![Drivetrain demand comparison showing internal combustion cars need a converter while battery cars need none and fuel cell cars need platinum](/imgs/blogs/platinum-and-palladium-the-autocatalyst-metals-5.png)

The figure lays out the three drivetrains side by side. On the left, the world as it has been: the internal-combustion car has a tailpipe, needs a converter, and carries 2-7 grams of PGM per vehicle. That fleet is the entire demand base — about 80% of palladium demand and the bulk of platinum's. On the right, the electric future. The **BEV** removes the converter entirely: zero PGM, a demand cliff where every electric car sold replaces an autocatalyst. The one offset is the **fuel-cell electric vehicle (FCEV)**, which generates electricity onboard from hydrogen — and a hydrogen fuel cell uses a **platinum** catalyst at its core. FCEVs are the complex's single biggest demand *hope*.

This is the asymmetry that defines the long-term trade:

- **For palladium, the EV transition is almost pure threat.** Palladium's demand is overwhelmingly gasoline autocatalysts. There is no large electric use case for palladium on the horizon. As BEVs take gasoline share, palladium demand structurally erodes. The 2023-2024 price collapse was partly the substitution loop and partly the market beginning to price this demand cliff.

- **For platinum, the EV transition is threat *and* hope.** Platinum loses its diesel and (some of its) gasoline autocatalyst demand as the fleet electrifies — but it is the catalyst in a **hydrogen fuel cell** and in the **electrolyzers** that produce green hydrogen. If a hydrogen economy takes off — fuel-cell trucks, buses, and stationary power, plus green-hydrogen production — platinum gains a major new demand source precisely as its old one fades. That is why platinum, the weaker metal through the squeeze years, is the one with the more interesting long-term bull case.

The catch is that the hydrogen hope is exactly that — a hope, with timing measured in decades and policy, not a certainty. Fuel-cell vehicles remain a tiny fraction of the market; green-hydrogen economics are still hard. So platinum is a bet that one uncertain future (hydrogen) arrives fast enough to offset another (BEV-driven autocatalyst decline). Palladium has fewer escape routes. The complex as a whole is therefore a high-variance position: a concentrated, disruption-prone supply on one side, and a demand picture caught between a cliff and a hope on the other.

It is worth being precise about *where* platinum sits in the hydrogen value chain, because there are two distinct demand sources and they have different sizes and timelines. The first is the **fuel cell** itself — the device in a fuel-cell vehicle (or a stationary backup-power unit) that combines hydrogen and oxygen to make electricity, with water as the only exhaust. A proton-exchange-membrane (PEM) fuel cell uses platinum as the catalyst on both electrodes; a fuel-cell car uses on the order of tens of grams of platinum, considerably *more* per vehicle than a gasoline car's converter uses palladium. So each FCEV is a much bigger platinum buyer than each gasoline car is a palladium buyer — which is why even a modest FCEV fleet could move the platinum balance.

The second source is the **electrolyzer** — the device that *produces* green hydrogen by splitting water with renewable electricity. PEM electrolyzers, one of the leading technologies for green hydrogen, also use PGM catalysts (platinum and iridium). If the world builds out large-scale green-hydrogen production to decarbonize steel, shipping, and heavy industry, the electrolyzer build-out is a second, separate platinum (and iridium) demand stream stacked on top of the fuel-cell stream. The bullish platinum case is that *both* arrive together over the 2030s-2040s. The bearish counter is that battery-electric drivetrains keep winning the light-vehicle market on cost, hydrogen stays confined to a few hard-to-electrify niches, and the platinum demand never materializes at scale. Nobody knows which wins — which is exactly why platinum is a long-dated, high-variance call option on the hydrogen economy, priced today off a fading autocatalyst business.

#### Worked example: the EV-penetration demand hit

Quantify the cliff. Suppose the world builds **90 million** new light vehicles in a year, and today **15%** are pure battery electric (a rough recent figure), so **85%** — about **76.5 million** — still have a tailpipe and a converter. Take an average autocatalyst loading of **4 grams** of PGM (palladium-heavy on the gasoline ones, platinum on the diesels) per combustion vehicle. That is 76.5 million × 4 g = **306 million grams**, or about **9.8 million troy ounces** of PGM pulled into new converters that year.

Now run the BEV share to **40%** (a plausible figure for the early 2030s in an aggressive-electrification scenario). Combustion vehicles fall to 60% × 90 million = **54 million**. At the same 4-gram loading, that is 54 million × 4 g ≈ **216 million grams**, or about **6.9 million troy ounces** — a drop of roughly **2.9 million ounces a year**, nearly **30%** of new-converter PGM demand, gone. In a market that already struggles to clear an extra few hundred thousand ounces of disruption, a multi-million-ounce structural demand loss is a different order of problem. **Unless hydrogen demand or thrifting-in-reverse fills the gap, the autocatalyst demand base shrinks with every point of EV penetration — and that, not any single squeeze, is the dominant force over the next decade.**

#### Worked example: can hydrogen save platinum? Sizing the offset

Now ask the platinum-specific question: could fuel-cell demand fill the hole the EV cliff blows in platinum's autocatalyst demand? Platinum's autocatalyst use is roughly **3 million ounces a year**. Suppose electrification halves that over a decade — a loss of about **1.5 million ounces a year** of platinum demand.

A fuel-cell vehicle uses on the order of **30 grams** of platinum (roughly **0.96 troy ounces**) — several times a gasoline car's PGM content. To replace 1.5 million ounces of lost autocatalyst platinum, the world would need to build about 1,500,000 ÷ 0.96 ≈ **1.56 million FCEVs a year**. For scale, recent global FCEV sales have been in the *tens of thousands* per year — so the fuel-cell fleet would have to grow by roughly **two orders of magnitude** to offset the autocatalyst loss on vehicles alone. Add electrolyzer demand for green hydrogen and the required FCEV count drops, but the conclusion holds: the hydrogen offset is *real and large per unit*, but it needs an enormous build-out to move the platinum balance, and that build-out is a multi-decade, policy-dependent bet. **Platinum's bull case is not that hydrogen is certain — it is that platinum is cheap relative to a future in which hydrogen even partly arrives, which is a very different, optionality-driven way to own a metal.**

## Common misconceptions

**"Platinum and palladium are precious metals, so they trade like gold."** No. This is the single most expensive mistake in the complex. Gold is a **monetary** metal — its price is driven by real interest rates, the dollar, and demand for a store of value, and roughly half of all the gold ever mined sits in vaults and jewelry boxes as a *stock*. (We link out to the gold series for that whole machine.) PGMs are **industrial** metals whose price is driven by **car production, emissions rules, and the substitution spread**. In a recession, gold can *rise* as a safe haven while palladium *falls* because car sales drop. They are nearly opposite trades wearing the same "precious metal" label. The correct mental model for palladium is "a leveraged bet on global gasoline-car production," not "a smaller gold."

**"Palladium will keep rising because supply is so tight."** This was the consensus near the 2021-2022 top, and it was wrong — not because supply loosened, but because **demand re-engineered**. A tight supply story ignores the substitution loop and the EV cliff. The lesson of the 2023-2024 halving is that in a market with a real substitute (platinum) and a structural demand threat (BEVs), "supply is tight" is necessary but nowhere near sufficient for a sustained bull case. Always ask: at this price, what is the *demand* doing?

**"The metals are interchangeable, so the spread doesn't matter."** Backwards. The metals are *partial* substitutes, and that is exactly **why the spread is the most important variable**. If they were perfectly interchangeable, they would trade at a fixed ratio and there would be no trade. If they were not substitutes at all, the spread would carry no information. Because substitution is real but **costly and slow**, the spread can blow out (incentivizing the swap), and then the swap slowly compresses it — a tradable cycle. The spread is the signal; ignoring it is ignoring the whole mechanism.

**"The EV transition means PGMs go to zero."** Too strong. BEVs remove autocatalyst demand, yes, but the internal-combustion fleet does not vanish overnight — hundreds of millions of combustion cars will be built and sold through the 2030s, especially hybrids (which still have an engine and a converter), and the existing fleet needs replacement converters for years. Meanwhile platinum has the hydrogen offset. The transition is a multi-decade **grind down** in autocatalyst demand, not an instant collapse — which means PGMs stay a tradable, high-variance market for a long time, not a dead one.

**"Rhodium is a rounding error."** Far from it. Rhodium is the smallest PGM market by volume but has repeatedly been the **most expensive metal on Earth**, spiking toward and above **\$20,000-\$29,000 an ounce** in 2021. It is essential for the NOx-reduction step in gasoline three-way catalysts, has an even thinner and more concentrated supply than palladium, and almost no substitute. A few hundredths of a gram of rhodium can add hundreds of dollars to a converter's metal bill. When you model autocatalyst cost, rhodium is not a rounding error — it is sometimes the swing factor.

## How it shows up in real markets

**The 2014 South African platinum strike.** From January to June 2014, the three biggest South African platinum producers were hit by a five-month strike — the longest and costliest in the country's mining history. It removed roughly **40% of global platinum supply** for the duration and cost the companies and workers billions. Yet the platinum price rose only modestly, because the market was working through large above-ground stocks and diesel demand was already softening. The lesson: even a massive supply shock does not guarantee a price spike if there is inventory to draw down and demand is weak. Supply concentration makes PGMs *fragile*, but the price reaction always depends on where stocks and demand sit at the time.

**The 2018-2022 palladium squeeze.** Walked through above: Dieselgate (2015) transferred demand from platinum to palladium; tightening emissions rules raised per-car loadings; byproduct supply stayed flat; and the Russia war premium of early 2022 produced the final spike toward **\$3,440**. At the top, palladium was worth more than gold — a metal most people had never heard of, briefly the most valuable of the precious complex. The squeeze is the textbook case of a demand-driven, supply-inelastic commodity market overshooting.

**The 2020 COVID auto-production shock — then the 2021 chip-shortage twist.** When the pandemic shut car factories in spring 2020, autocatalyst demand collapsed almost overnight, and palladium dropped sharply from its early-2020 highs. But the more instructive episode came *after*: as factories restarted, a global **semiconductor shortage** capped how many cars could actually be built through 2021. Fewer cars built should have meant less PGM demand — and yet palladium and especially rhodium stayed extraordinarily firm, because the *supply* side (South African smelter outages, Russian risk) was tightening at the same time and inventories were thin. The lesson is that PGM prices are set by the *balance* of two volatile forces, and a demand drop and a supply drop can arrive together, leaving the price high even as the underlying car market shrinks. You cannot read PGMs off car-sales numbers alone; you have to net them against the supply side.

**The 2023-2024 collapse.** Palladium fell from ~\$2,400 (2021 average) to ~\$1,000 (2024 average), a halving, with **no new supply**. The drivers: the **substitution** automakers set in motion at the top finally bit demand; **thrifting** cut grams per converter; and the market began pricing the **BEV demand cliff** for a metal with no electric use case. The reconvergence with platinum (the un-flip) happened in the same window. This is the cleanest real-world demonstration that in a substitutable, demand-threatened commodity, a record-high price is a *cause* of its own reversal, on a multi-year lag.

**The hybrid surprise of 2024-2025.** A subtle twist deserves a mention because it complicates the simple "EVs kill PGMs" story. As pure-BEV sales growth wobbled in some markets and consumers gravitated toward **hybrids** (which pair a battery with an internal-combustion engine — and therefore *still* have a tailpipe and a catalytic converter), the near-term autocatalyst demand picture turned out less dire than the most aggressive EV-cliff forecasts had assumed. A hybrid is, for PGM-demand purposes, still a combustion car: it needs a converter, often with loadings comparable to a conventional car. So the demand cliff is real over the long run but **lumpy and non-linear** in the medium run, and the path of hybrid-versus-pure-EV adoption is now one of the most important demand variables for the complex. A trader who assumed a smooth, fast collapse in autocatalyst demand would have been wrong-footed by the hybrid resilience.

**The 2021 rhodium melt-up.** Rhodium ran from a few thousand dollars an ounce in 2019 to nearly **\$30,000** an ounce in early 2021 — a roughly tenfold move — on the same tightening-emissions and tight-supply forces as palladium, but amplified by rhodium's even thinner market and near-total lack of substitutes. It then fell hard as auto production normalized and substitution/thrifting reduced rhodium loadings. Rhodium is the most violent corner of an already-volatile complex, and a reminder that the thinner the market, the bigger the overshoot in both directions.

#### Worked example: reading the spread as a position, start to finish

Suppose it is late 2021. You observe: palladium ~\$2,400/oz, platinum ~\$1,050/oz — a spread of about **\$1,350**, palladium dear, near the widest it has ever been. You know the substitution loop: at a \$1,350 spread, the per-car saving from swapping platinum into gasoline catalysts is roughly (\$1,350 ÷ 31.1) ≈ **\$43/gram**, and a 3-gram swap saves ~\$130 per car, ~\$1.3 billion a year across a 10-million-car fleet — far above the R&D cost. So you reason: automakers *are* re-engineering right now, the demand response will arrive on a 2-3 year lag, and on top of that the EV cliff is a structural headwind for palladium specifically.

Your thesis: the spread should **compress** — palladium underperforming platinum — over the next few years, regardless of the short-term war premium. You could express it as a **long-platinum / short-palladium spread trade** (betting on convergence) rather than an outright direction bet, which neutralizes the "are commodities going up or down" question and isolates the substitution mechanism. By 2024, the spread had collapsed to roughly zero (palladium ~\$1,000, platinum ~\$950). **The substitution loop gave you a falsifiable, mechanism-based thesis with a clear signal — the spread — and a clear catalyst — the slow, lagged demand response — which is exactly how a trader should frame a market driven by a known industrial process rather than by sentiment.**

## The playbook: how to think about the autocatalyst metals

Pull it together into a way of reading the complex. The figure below is a regime map: the platinum-palladium spread is not just a number, it is a signal about which force is in charge.

![A regime grid reading the platinum-palladium spread as a substitution trade signal](/imgs/blogs/platinum-and-palladium-the-autocatalyst-metals-7.png)

Here is the trader's frame, point by point:

- **The spread is the master variable.** When **palladium trades far above platinum**, the substitution clock is running — automakers are being paid, in saved metal cost, to swap platinum back in, and that swap will erode palladium demand on a multi-year lag. A wide premium is therefore *fragile*; it carries the seed of its own compression. When the metals trade **near parity**, the easy substitution gain is exhausted, and the next move is set by supply shocks and the structural EV path rather than by the spread itself. The pre-2017 world, where **platinum traded above palladium**, was the diesel era; if diesel or hydrogen demand ever revives platinum's lead, that is a different regime again.

- **Supply concentration means tail risk is always live.** Roughly **75% of both metals** comes from just South Africa and Russia, each with a chronic, recurring risk — South African load-shedding and strikes, Russian sanctions and geopolitics. There is no spare-capacity shock absorber. So PGMs carry a permanent **upside-tail risk** from supply: any headline out of Eskom or about Russian sanctions can spike the price in days. Size positions for that — a thin, concentrated market gaps, it does not glide.

- **Demand is the slow, dominant force.** Over a quarter, supply headlines move the price. Over a decade, **demand structure** wins. The autocatalyst demand base shrinks with every point of EV penetration, hurting palladium most and platinum less (because of hydrogen). The honest long-term view: palladium is a fading franchise with periodic supply spikes; platinum is a fading franchise *with a hydrogen call option* attached. Neither is a buy-and-forget "precious metal."

- **Do not confuse these with gold.** Gold is monetary; PGMs are industrial. In a recession gold can rally on safe-haven demand while palladium falls on collapsing car sales. If you want the precious complex contrast in full, follow the cross-links to the gold series below — and notice how *differently* an industrial catalyst behaves from a monetary metal even though both are "precious."

- **Watch the fuel mix, not just the car count.** Because the metals split by engine type, the variable that matters is not "how many cars" but "how many *of each kind* of car." A swing toward gasoline (and away from diesel) is bullish palladium and bearish platinum; a swing toward hybrids keeps autocatalyst demand alive longer than the pure-BEV forecasts assume; a swing toward pure BEVs is the cliff. The single most useful demand dashboard for the complex is the breakdown of new-vehicle sales into diesel / gasoline / hybrid / BEV / fuel-cell — that mix, region by region, is what actually sets the autocatalyst demand for each metal.

- **The miners amplify everything.** If you want leverage to the PGM view, the mining equities (the South African producers, Norilsk, and the North American miners) move more than the metals — they carry operating leverage on top of the metal price, plus the country risk of where they dig. That cuts both ways: at \$2,400 palladium the miners minted money; at \$1,000 palladium some of them ran at a loss and shut high-cost shafts. The decade-long mine lead time means their capital decisions are chronically out of phase with the price. Treat PGM equities as a *levered, lagged* expression of the same supply-and-demand picture, not as a separate asset.

- **The whole complex is a high-variance bet, by construction.** A concentrated, disruption-prone supply (sharp upside tails) plus a demand picture caught between an EV **cliff** and a hydrogen **hope** (a wide, uncertain long-term range) is the definition of high variance. That is not a flaw to be smoothed away — it is the *character* of the asset. You trade PGMs precisely because they move violently when the autocatalyst story, the substitution spread, or a supply headline shifts. The skill is knowing which of those three is driving on any given day, and the spread chart plus the supply map tell you most of it.

The deepest lesson connects back to this series' spine. A commodity price is a physical thing forced through a financial contract, and for the PGMs the "physical thing" is astonishingly specific: a few grams of catalyst, in a canister, in a car's exhaust, in a fleet shaped by emissions law and a slow electrification. There is no convenience yield to speak of, no great forward curve to roll — the PGM trade is not about contango and backwardation the way oil and gas are. It is about a **demand engine you can name and count** (converters per car, cars per year, grams per converter, the fuel mix) and a **supply you can map to two countries**. Master those two things and the squeeze, the flip, the collapse, and the EV cliff all stop being surprises and start being the readable consequences of an industrial process. That is what it means to read the autocatalyst metals like a trader rather than a jeweler.
