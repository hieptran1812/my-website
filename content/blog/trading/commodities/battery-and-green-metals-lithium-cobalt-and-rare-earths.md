> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Green metals are real industrial commodities forced through immature, opaque, often China-priced markets, so geology, factory demand, and government policy collide inside a brutal price cycle that buy-and-hold investors keep getting wrong.
>
> - **Lithium** is the boom-and-bust poster child: it moonshot to roughly \$80,000/tonne in late 2022, then collapsed toward \$13,000 by 2024 as a wave of new mines and brine projects all arrived at once — a textbook capex-cycle whipsaw.
> - **Cobalt** is the ethics problem: about 70% comes from the Democratic Republic of Congo with artisanal-mining and child-labour concerns, which is why carmakers are racing to design it out by switching from NMC to LFP battery chemistry.
> - **Rare earths** are not actually rare — the chokepoint is *separation and refining*, where one country holds roughly 85-90% of capacity, so "just mine it elsewhere" does not break the monopoly.
> - The one fact to remember: a green metal is a tiny, violent, policy-driven market where the supply response always arrives years late — you trade the cycle, you do not marry the metal.

In November 2022, a tonne of battery-grade lithium carbonate in China changed hands for around \$80,000. Two years earlier the same tonne had cost \$6,000. The number on the screen had multiplied more than thirteenfold, and every mining executive on Earth was scrambling to dig faster. Then the cycle turned. By 2024 the price had collapsed to roughly \$13,000 — an 84% crash from the peak — and the same executives were quietly mothballing the projects they had broken ground on at the top. Fortunes were made and unmade not on a discovery or a war, but on the oldest mechanism in commodities: a price that screamed "build" right before the building flooded the market.

That single round trip tells you almost everything about the metals of the energy transition. We have decided, as a civilization, to electrify transport and rebuild the grid, and that decision pulls enormous new demand onto a handful of materials — lithium, nickel, cobalt, graphite, and the rare earths. But the rocks do not care about our timelines. A new mine takes five to ten years to bring online. A refinery takes nearly as long and almost nobody wants one in their backyard. So demand can lurch in a year while supply crawls over a decade, and the gap between them is paid in violent price swings.

This post is about those swings and why they happen. We will build up from what these metals actually are and where they come from, walk through the capex cycle that whipsaws every one of them, confront cobalt's uncomfortable supply chain, and then unpack the rare-earth story that confuses almost everyone — because the bottleneck is not the ground, it is the chemistry plant. The thread running through all of it: **green metals are where geology meets industrial policy meets a brutal price cycle.** A commodity is always a physical thing forced through a financial contract, and for these metals the contract is young, thin, and easy to push around.

![Battery and EV materials map showing lithium nickel cobalt graphite and rare earths feeding a cell and motor](/imgs/blogs/battery-and-green-metals-lithium-cobalt-and-rare-earths-1.png)

Look at the figure for a second before we go on. An electric car is not a bet on one metal. It is a bet on a *basket* — five very different materials, mined in different places, refined by different chemistries, each with its own supply story, all of which have to show up for the car to roll off the line. That is why the energy transition behaves less like a single commodity trade and more like a portfolio of fragile supply chains, any one of which can be the binding constraint in a given year.

## Foundations: what a green metal is and why its market is so young

Before we can talk about why these prices swing, we need to define the materials and, more importantly, the *kind of market* they trade in. This is where green metals differ most from the oil and copper you may already know.

**A "green metal" (or "battery metal" or "critical mineral") is just an industrial commodity whose demand is being reshaped by decarbonization.** There is nothing exotic about the elements themselves — lithium is the third element on the periodic table, cobalt and nickel are ordinary transition metals, and the rare earths are seventeen elements that sit together at the bottom of the table. What makes them "green" is the destination: most of the marginal new demand goes into batteries, electric motors, and grid equipment rather than into the steel and wiring that have absorbed metals for a century.

Let me define the cast quickly, because the rest of the post leans on these:

- **Lithium** is the lightest metal and the one that makes a battery a *lithium*-ion battery. It carries charge between the two ends of the cell. It is sold mostly as **lithium carbonate** or **lithium hydroxide** — white powders, not shiny metal — and quantities are often quoted in **LCE**, "lithium carbonate equivalent", so different chemical forms can be compared on one scale.
- **Cobalt** is a hard, silvery metal used in the *cathode* (the positive end) of one major battery family to keep it stable and energy-dense. It is mostly a *byproduct* of copper and nickel mining, which matters enormously for how its supply behaves.
- **Nickel** is a workhorse industrial metal — most of it still goes into stainless steel — but the high-purity "Class 1" nickel used in batteries is a smaller, tighter sub-market.
- **Graphite** is carbon, and it forms the *anode* (the negative end) of almost every lithium-ion cell. Natural graphite is mined; synthetic graphite is baked from petroleum coke.
- **Rare earths** are seventeen elements (the fifteen lanthanides plus scandium and yttrium). The ones that matter most for the transition — neodymium, praseodymium, dysprosium, terbium — make the powerful permanent magnets inside electric-motor and wind-turbine generators.

So far, so much chemistry. Here is the part that actually drives the price action. **These are immature, opaque markets.** Compare them to crude oil. Oil has two deep, transparent global benchmarks (WTI and Brent), a century of futures trading, public inventory data released weekly, and thousands of participants. A barrel's price is discovered in plain sight. (If those mechanics are new to you, the sibling post on [crude oil, WTI vs Brent](/blog/trading/commodities/crude-oil-wti-vs-brent-the-worlds-two-benchmark-barrels) lays them out.)

Lithium, by contrast, had essentially no liquid futures market until very recently. For years the "price" was an assessment — a number that a price-reporting agency such as Benchmark Mineral Intelligence or Fastmarkets *estimated* by surveying buyers and sellers, much like a property appraisal rather than a stock quote. A great deal of physical lithium, cobalt, and rare-earth material trades on long-term contracts negotiated privately between a producer and a battery maker, with the China spot market acting as the visible reference. That opacity is not a footnote; it is the mechanism. When nobody can see real-time supply and demand clearly, prices overshoot in both directions because everyone is trading on the same lagged, partial information.

Two more structural facts before we move on, because they recur throughout:

1. **The supply response is slow and lumpy.** You cannot turn a mine up like a tap. Permitting, financing, construction, and ramp-up take the better part of a decade, and the projects tend to arrive in clusters — all the ones greenlit during the last price spike open at roughly the same time. So supply does not glide up to meet demand; it lurches.
2. **Much of the *refining* is concentrated in China.** Mining is somewhat spread around the world, but the step that turns ore or brine into battery-grade chemical is heavily concentrated in Chinese plants. That concentration is the single most important fact about rare earths, and a big one for lithium and graphite too.

Put those together and you have the recipe for a market that booms and busts harder than almost anything else in commodities. Now let us watch it happen.

### The demand side: why electrification pulls so violently

It is worth being precise about *why* the demand on these metals lurches, because "EVs are growing" is too vague to be useful. The violence comes from three multiplying factors stacked on top of each other.

First, **the per-unit intensity is large.** A combustion car uses a few kilograms of copper and essentially no lithium, cobalt, or rare-earth magnets in its drivetrain. An electric car uses roughly four times the copper, around 8 kg of lithium, a kilogram or two of rare-earth magnet material, and tens of kilograms of nickel and graphite. So electrifying a single vehicle does not nudge demand for these metals — it multiplies it. The transition is not a marginal new buyer; it is a step-change in how metal-hungry each unit of transport is.

Second, **the volume is enormous and growing off a small base.** When EVs were 1% of car sales, the metal demand they created was a rounding error. When they cross into the tens of percent of a 90-million-unit-a-year global car market, the same per-car intensity suddenly translates into millions of tonnes of new annual metal demand. Growth that compounds off a small base looks gentle for years and then, abruptly, dominates the market — and the supply chain, which planned around the old small base, is caught flat.

Third, **grid storage and renewables stack demand on top.** Batteries do not only go into cars. Grid-scale storage — giant battery installations that soak up solar and wind output and release it later — is a second, fast-growing call on the same lithium and (increasingly) the same LFP chemistry. Wind turbines need rare-earth magnets in their generators. Solar and grid build-outs need vast amounts of copper and aluminum. So the same handful of metals is being pulled by cars, by storage, and by the grid all at once, and these demands are correlated — they all surge when policy and economics align, and they all soften together when they do not.

The result is a demand curve that can shift far and fast. And because (as we established) the supply curve crawls, the gap between a fast-moving demand line and a slow-moving supply line is what gets paid out in price. Hold that geometry in your head — a demand line that can jump in a year against a supply line that takes a decade to bend — and every boom-bust in this post becomes predictable rather than surprising.

## The lithium whipsaw: a thirteenfold round trip in three years

Lithium is the cleanest illustration of the whole pattern, so we will spend real time on it. Start with the price.

![Lithium carbonate China battery grade price 2019 to 2025 showing 2022 peak and collapse](/imgs/blogs/battery-and-green-metals-lithium-cobalt-and-rare-earths-2.png)

The story the chart tells: through 2019 and 2020, lithium was a sleepy \$6,000-to-\$9,000/tonne backwater, depressed after an earlier mini-boom had faded. Then electric-vehicle sales went vertical. China's EV market alone roughly doubled, Europe's incentives kicked in, and battery factories ("gigafactories") were being announced faster than anyone could supply them. Demand outran the existing mines, and because the market is thin and opaque, the price did not rise politely — it gapped. By late 2022 battery-grade carbonate touched roughly \$80,000/tonne in China, more than thirteen times its 2020 level.

That price was a scream of "BUILD." And the industry heard it. Australian hard-rock mines expanded, African projects were fast-tracked, Chinese converters added capacity, and even high-cost lepidolite (a low-grade lithium ore that only makes sense at sky-high prices) came online. The problem is that all of this takes time, and it all landed in 2023 and 2024 at once — right as EV sales growth cooled from "vertical" to merely "strong." Supply flooded in, demand growth normalized, and the price did the same thing in reverse: it collapsed, sliding below \$15,000 and bottoming near \$11,000-\$13,000, a level at which a chunk of the world's marginal lithium supply loses money.

Before we generalize, let us pin a number to the demand side, because the whole boom rests on it.

#### Worked example: how much lithium is in one EV battery pack — and what the price swing did to its cost

A useful rule of thumb is that a lithium-ion battery needs roughly **0.13 kg of LCE (lithium carbonate equivalent) per kWh** of capacity. (It varies by chemistry; this is a reasonable middle estimate.) Take a typical 60 kWh pack — about what a mainstream electric crossover carries.

- Lithium content: 60 kWh × 0.13 kg LCE/kWh ≈ **7.8 kg LCE**, call it ~8 kg.

Now price that lithium at the two extremes of the cycle:

- At the 2020 trough (\$6,000/tonne = \$6/kg): 7.8 kg × \$6 ≈ **\$47** of lithium per car.
- At the late-2022 peak (\$80,000/tonne = \$80/kg): 7.8 kg × \$80 ≈ **\$624** of lithium per car.
- At the 2024 bust (\$13,000/tonne = \$13/kg): 7.8 kg × \$13 ≈ **\$101** of lithium per car.

So the lithium in a single mainstream EV swung from about \$47 to \$624 and back to \$101 in roughly three years. Across a market of more than ten million electric vehicles a year, a \$500-per-car swing in just *one* of five key materials moves billions of dollars and rewrites the economics of every battery plant on the planet — which is exactly why the price had to overshoot to clear the imbalance.

(Notice the asymmetry worth holding onto: even at the insane \$80k peak, the lithium was only a few hundred dollars of a battery pack that costs several thousand. The metal price terrorizes the *miners*, but it is a smaller line item for the *carmaker* than the headlines suggest — a point we will return to in the misconceptions.)

### Where the lithium actually comes from: brine versus rock

The supply side has its own split personality, and it matters for the cycle. There are two main ways to produce lithium:

**Brine.** In the high deserts of the "Lithium Triangle" — Chile's Atacama, Argentina, and parts of Bolivia — lithium is dissolved in underground salt water. Producers pump the brine into vast evaporation ponds and let the sun concentrate it over 12 to 18 months before processing. Brine is cheap to operate once it is running (the sun does the work) but slow to scale and slow to start. Chile's production is dominated by two firms operating under state concessions; this is a low-cost, geographically concentrated, slow-moving source.

**Hard rock (spodumene).** In Australia — by far the largest single producer — lithium comes from a mineral called spodumene, mined conventionally from open pits, crushed into a concentrate, and shipped (mostly to China) for chemical conversion. Hard rock is faster to bring on and faster to turn off than brine, and it sits higher on the cost curve. When the price spiked, it was largely spodumene and the marginal Chinese lepidolite that ramped — and when it crashed, those high-cost tonnes were the first to bleed.

This brine-versus-rock divide is why the lithium supply curve is so awkward. The cheap, stable tonnes (brine) cannot scale quickly, so the *marginal* tonne that sets the price during a boom is the expensive, fast-to-build rock. That marginal-cost dynamic — the price is set by the most expensive barrel or tonne the market needs — is the same mechanism that governs oil, which you can see worked through in the post on [OPEC and the supply curve](/blog/trading/commodities/opec-plus-and-the-supply-curve-the-cartel-that-sets-the-floor). When demand surges, you have to call on the expensive rock, and the price has to rise enough to make that rock profitable. When demand softens, the expensive rock is the first to be priced out, and the price falls toward the cost of the next-cheapest tonne.

#### Worked example: where the lithium price finds a floor on the cost curve

Lay out a stylized lithium cost curve from cheapest to most expensive — the supply stack the market climbs as demand rises:

- **Chilean / Argentine brine:** all-in cost ≈ **\$5,000/tonne.** Cheap (the sun does the concentration) but slow to expand.
- **Established Australian spodumene:** all-in cost ≈ **\$8,000/tonne.** The reliable workhorse.
- **Marginal new spodumene (high strip ratio, ramping mines):** ≈ **\$12,000/tonne.**
- **Chinese lepidolite (low-grade ore):** ≈ **\$15,000/tonne.** Only profitable when the price is high.

Now read prices off this stack. At the \$80,000 peak, *every* source is wildly profitable, which is exactly why everyone built. As the glut hit and the price fell to ~\$13,000, the price dropped *below* the lepidolite cost (\$15,000) and barely above marginal spodumene (\$12,000) — so the lepidolite tonnes started losing money and shutting in, and the marginal spodumene was on the edge. That shut-in is the market mechanism that eventually puts a floor under the price: supply leaves until the price stops falling. The lesson: a green metal does not fall forever — it falls to roughly the cash cost of its marginal producer, and that cost is where you start looking for the bottom of the cycle.

## The capex cycle: the engine behind every green-metal whipsaw

Lithium's round trip was not bad luck. It is the inevitable output of a feedback loop that governs every commodity with slow supply — and green metals have the slowest supply of all. Let us make the mechanism explicit, because once you can see it, you can spot it forming in cobalt, nickel, and any future "critical mineral" the headlines latch onto.

![Capex cycle pipeline high price builds oversupply crash underinvestment next squeeze](/imgs/blogs/battery-and-green-metals-lithium-cobalt-and-rare-earths-3.png)

Walk the stages in the figure:

1. **Price spikes.** Demand grows faster than today's mines can supply. Because the market is thin, the price does not nudge — it leaps.
2. **Everyone builds.** A high price is a signal to invest. Miners raise capital, sign offtake deals, and break ground on new projects. Crucially, *every* producer responds to the *same* signal at the *same* time.
3. **Long lead time.** Nothing happens for years. A greenfield mine takes five to ten years from discovery to first production once you add permitting, financing, and construction. The price can stay high — even rise further — during this dead period, which only encourages *more* projects.
4. **Supply floods.** All those projects, greenlit during the spike, finally open within a year or two of each other. Supply does not arrive smoothly; it dumps.
5. **Price crashes.** The flood meets demand that has, by now, often cooled. Oversupply sends the price below the marginal cost of production. Now the *high-cost* tonnes lose money.
6. **Underinvestment.** Burned investors flee. New projects are cancelled, expansions shelved, exploration budgets slashed. The seeds of the *next* shortage are planted in the wreckage of this one — because in five to ten years, when demand has grown again, there will be no new supply ready.

This is a *pipeline*, not a circle, but it rhymes: stage 6 quietly sets up the next stage 1 years later. That lag is the whole problem. The investment decision and its consequence are separated by half a decade, so the system perpetually over- and under-shoots. Economists call this the **cobweb cycle** or **capex cycle**, and it is most violent precisely where supply is slowest — which is to say, in mining.

#### Worked example: a green-metal miner's profit and loss through the whipsaw

Make the cycle concrete with a stylized lithium miner. Suppose this producer can mine and convert lithium at an **all-in cost of \$9,000/tonne** and sells **20,000 tonnes/year** of LCE. Walk it through three years of the cycle:

- **Boom year (2022), sells at \$60,000/tonne (an annual-average price, below the spot peak):** revenue = 20,000 × \$60,000 = \$1.2 billion. Cost = 20,000 × \$9,000 = \$180 million. Operating profit ≈ **\$1.02 billion.** The stock triples, the CEO announces three expansions, and the board approves a new mine.
- **Transition year (2023), sells at \$25,000/tonne:** revenue = \$500 million, cost still ~\$180 million, profit ≈ **\$320 million.** Still very profitable — which is the trap, because it looks like the good times are merely cooling, not ending. The expansions stay funded.
- **Bust year (2024), sells at \$13,000/tonne:** revenue = \$260 million, cost ~\$180 million (and the new expansion has *raised* fixed costs), profit shrinks to ~**\$80 million** before the new project's depreciation — and a *higher*-cost competitor selling at \$13,000 against a \$15,000 cost is now losing money outright and starts to shut in.

The lesson in the numbers: the miner that looked like a money-printing machine at \$60,000 is barely breathing at \$13,000, and the marginal producer is underwater. That is the precise moment supply starts to leave — which, years later, seeds the next squeeze.

Notice what the cycle does to an *investor*. The temptation is to buy the miner when it is printing billion-dollar profits — exactly at the top, when the price is screaming "build" and everyone else is buying too. The discipline is to recognize that a green-metal price far above the marginal cost of production is, by definition, temporary, because it is summoning the supply that will destroy it. We will come back to how to act on that.

## Cobalt: the ethics problem and the chemistry that designs it out

Lithium's story is about *price*. Cobalt's story is about *where it comes from* — and the industry's scramble to depend on it less.

### The concentration problem

Roughly **70% of the world's mined cobalt comes from a single country**: the Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC). And cobalt is mostly a *byproduct* — it comes up alongside copper and nickel rather than being mined for its own sake — which means its supply is governed by decisions made for other metals entirely. (The same copper that anchors the global-growth signal, explored in the cross-asset note on [metals, copper and silver](/blog/trading/cross-asset/metals-copper-silver-the-economys-pulse), is what drags most cobalt out of the ground.)

A large share of that DRC output comes from big industrial mines, but a meaningful slice comes from **artisanal and small-scale mining (ASM)** — informal operations, often hand-dug, sometimes in dangerous conditions, and associated with documented child-labour and human-rights concerns. This is the uncomfortable core of the cobalt story. A metal that goes into a product marketed as clean and progressive has, at the bottom of its supply chain, one of the most ethically fraught extraction stories in the commodity world. That tension — clean destination, dirty origin — is a recurring theme of the entire transition, and it is most acute in cobalt.

The concentration also creates ordinary supply risk on top of the ethics. When 70% of a metal comes from one country, any political instability, export-policy change, or infrastructure failure there can swing the global price. This is the same geographic-chokepoint vulnerability that makes commodities the most geopolitically exposed asset class — embargoes, export bans, and single-country dependence are recurring themes across the complex.

### Designing cobalt out: NMC versus LFP

Here is where cobalt's story takes a turn the lithium story does not: the industry has a *technical* escape route. Battery chemistry is not fixed, and carmakers have been actively engineering cobalt out of their cells.

![Before and after comparison of NMC battery chemistry versus LFP designing out cobalt](/imgs/blogs/battery-and-green-metals-lithium-cobalt-and-rare-earths-4.png)

The two families in the figure:

- **NMC** (nickel-manganese-cobalt) is the high-energy-density chemistry that powered the first generation of long-range EVs. It uses cobalt to keep the cathode stable, plus a lot of nickel for energy density. NMC packs more range into less weight — but it is expensive and it carries the cobalt exposure.
- **LFP** (lithium iron phosphate) uses iron and phosphate — both cheap and abundant — and **no cobalt and no nickel at all.** Its energy density is lower (so the car gets less range for the same weight), but it is cheaper, safer (less prone to thermal runaway), and longer-lasting in charge cycles. Over the last several years LFP has gone from a niche to a *majority* of new battery installations globally, led by Chinese makers, and it has marched up-market as engineers squeezed more range out of it.

There is also a parallel effort *within* NMC to cut the cobalt fraction — the industry moved from a roughly 1:1:1 nickel-manganese-cobalt ratio toward high-nickel formulas like "NMC 811" (eight parts nickel, one manganese, one cobalt), slashing the cobalt per cell. Between LFP's rise and high-nickel NMC, the cobalt intensity of the average battery has fallen sharply.

This is the second-order effect that makes cobalt fundamentally different from lithium as an investment. Lithium is, for now, *irreplaceable* — every lithium-ion chemistry needs lithium, by definition. Cobalt is *substitutable*, and the substitution is actively happening. A material the market can engineer around has a structurally weaker demand outlook than one it cannot.

#### Worked example: the cost saving from designing cobalt out

Take a 60 kWh pack and compare an older cobalt-heavy chemistry to a modern low- or no-cobalt one. Suppose:

- An older NMC cell uses roughly **0.13 kg of cobalt per kWh**, and cobalt costs about **\$33,000/tonne = \$33/kg**.
- Cobalt content: 60 kWh × 0.13 kg/kWh ≈ 7.8 kg. Cost ≈ 7.8 × \$33 ≈ **\$257 of cobalt per pack.**

Now switch to LFP, which uses **zero** cobalt:

- Cobalt cost per pack: **\$0.** Direct saving ≈ **\$257 per vehicle** on cobalt alone — before counting the larger saving from also eliminating the expensive nickel.

Across ten million vehicles a year, designing cobalt out of the chemistry removes on the order of \$2-3 billion of annual cobalt demand. The lesson: when a metal can be substituted away this cheaply, every dollar its price rises only accelerates its own replacement — a built-in ceiling that lithium does not have.

That self-limiting demand is exactly why cobalt, after a 2018 boomlet, has spent recent years drifting rather than spiking even as EV volumes climbed. The cars multiplied; the cobalt per car shrank faster.

### The recycling overhang

There is a third force bearing down on cobalt and the other battery metals over the longer run: **recycling.** A battery is not consumed the way a barrel of oil is burned — the metals are still there at the end of the pack's life, and they can be recovered. As the first big waves of EVs reach retirement and as gigafactory scrap accumulates, a growing stream of "urban-mined" lithium, cobalt, and nickel comes back into the supply chain without anyone digging a new hole. This matters most for the metals that are expensive and concentrated — cobalt above all, because recovering it both removes the ethics exposure and adds supply the DRC did not provide. For an investor, recycling is a slow-building structural headwind on the *primary* (mined) demand for these metals: it does not bite for years, but over a decade it shaves the top off the demand curve that the bulls extrapolate forever. The transition's metal demand is real, but a meaningful slice of the steady-state requirement will eventually be met by yesterday's batteries rather than by new mines — another reason to treat "demand can only grow" as a half-truth.

## Nickel and graphite: the two metals everyone forgets

Lithium gets the headlines and cobalt gets the ethics debate, but two other materials sit quietly in the basket and deserve a paragraph each, because they complete the supply-chain story and add their own twists.

**Nickel** is the energy-density metal. In NMC cells, more nickel means more range, which is why carmakers pushed toward high-nickel formulas even as they cut cobalt. But nickel is a confusing market because not all nickel is equal. Most of the world's nickel is "Class 2" — the lower-purity form that goes into stainless steel — while batteries need high-purity "Class 1" nickel or chemically processed equivalents. Indonesia transformed this market over the last decade: it banned raw-ore exports to force processing onshore, attracted enormous Chinese investment in new smelters and high-pressure acid-leach plants, and flooded the world with nickel. The consequence was a glut that crushed the price from its 2022 squeeze levels and pushed high-cost producers elsewhere (notably in Australia) to shut down. So nickel ran its own capex cycle — driven this time by a single country's industrial policy rather than by a price spike — and the 2022 LME short squeeze, which we cover below, sits on top of all of that. Nickel's lesson is that *processing policy*, not just mining, can remake a metal's supply almost overnight.

**Graphite** is the anode in essentially every lithium-ion cell, and it is the most China-dominated material in the entire battery. China mines a large share of natural graphite and refines the overwhelming majority of the battery-grade product, and it also dominates *synthetic* graphite (baked from petroleum coke in energy-intensive furnaces). Graphite rarely makes headlines because it has not had a spectacular price spike, but it is arguably the quietest single point of failure in the supply chain — and, like rare earths, it has featured in export-control measures, precisely because the dependence is so concentrated. If you only watch lithium, you can miss that the anode side of the battery is even more concentrated than the cathode side.

The reason these two matter for an investor is the same reason the cover figure shows five materials, not one: **the binding constraint rotates.** In 2022 it was lithium. In a future year it could be high-purity nickel, or battery-grade graphite, or a single rare-earth oxide. A car needs all of them, so whichever is tightest in a given year sets the marginal pain — and the metal that was the hot story last cycle is rarely the one that bites next. That rotation is why a basket lens beats a single-metal bet.

## Rare earths: not rare, but a refining chokepoint

Now the metal class that confuses almost everyone, including policymakers who should know better. The phrase "rare earths" is one of the most misleading names in all of commodities, and the misunderstanding leads straight to bad strategy.

### The first myth: they are not actually rare

The seventeen rare-earth elements are *not* geologically scarce. Cerium is more abundant in the Earth's crust than copper. Neodymium is more common than lead. They are called "rare" for a historical reason — they were discovered late and found mixed together in awkward minerals — not because there is little of them. Deposits exist on every continent. The United States has them, Australia has them, Brazil and India and Vietnam have them. If the problem were finding the ore, there would be no problem.

The difficulty is what comes *after* the ore. The seventeen elements have nearly identical chemistry — that is the whole reason they were hard to tell apart for a century — so separating them from one another and from the surrounding rock is fiendishly hard. **Separation requires hundreds of stages of solvent extraction**, is chemically dirty (it generates radioactive and acidic waste because rare-earth ores often carry thorium and uranium), is capital-intensive, and is technically demanding to run well. *That* is the bottleneck. Not the mine. The refinery.

It helps to know what these elements actually *do*, because it explains why a handful of them are worth fighting over while most are not. The transition cares mostly about four "magnet" rare earths — neodymium and praseodymium (often sold together as "NdPr"), plus dysprosium and terbium in smaller doses. Combined with iron and boron, they make **NdFeB magnets**, the strongest permanent magnets known, and those magnets are what let a compact electric motor or a wind-turbine generator produce so much torque for their size. Dysprosium and terbium are added so the magnet keeps its strength when it gets hot — exactly the condition inside a hard-working motor. The other thirteen rare earths have uses (polishing powders, catalysts, phosphors) but nothing like the strategic pull of the magnet four. So when you read about a "rare-earth crisis," it is almost always really a *magnet*-rare-earth crisis — a tiny subset of a small market, which makes the chokepoint even narrower and the supply shock even sharper than the headline suggests.

![Illustrative bar chart showing China holding the dominant share of rare earth separation capacity](/imgs/blogs/battery-and-green-metals-lithium-cobalt-and-rare-earths-5.png)

The bar is illustrative — the dataset behind this series does not carry a rare-earth array, so treat the exact figures as round, widely-cited estimates rather than precise data — but the *shape* is the point and it is not in dispute: **one country controls on the order of 85-90% of the world's rare-earth separation and refining capacity.** China spent decades building that capability while the rest of the world, deterred by the environmental cost and thin margins, let its own refining wither. (A US mine at Mountain Pass in California, for years, dug ore and then *shipped it to China* to be separated, because there was no domestic plant to do the chemistry.)

### Why "mine it elsewhere" does not solve it

This is the strategic error that follows directly from the "rare" misnomer. When a country worried about dependence announces it will "secure its rare-earth supply" by opening a domestic mine, it has usually solved the wrong problem.

![Before and after showing the rare earth chokepoint is refining not the mine](/imgs/blogs/battery-and-green-metals-lithium-cobalt-and-rare-earths-6.png)

Trace the figure. The mistaken fix (left) reasons: the ore is common, so let us dig our own. You build a mine in the US or Australia, you pull up perfectly good rare-earth ore — and then you discover there is no local separation plant capable of turning that mixed concentrate into the individual high-purity oxides a magnet maker needs. So the concentrate gets shipped to China anyway, to the same refineries you were trying to escape. You have built a mine and changed nothing about the dependence.

The real bottleneck (right) is the separation step itself: seventeen near-identical elements, a dirty and capital-heavy multi-stage process, and one country holding the overwhelming majority of the capacity. **The monopoly is on refining, and you only break a refining monopoly by building refineries** — which takes years, billions of dollars, an appetite for the environmental burden, and the metallurgical know-how that has accumulated in one place for decades. Mining elsewhere is necessary but nowhere near sufficient.

### The strategic-weapon angle

Because the chokepoint is so concentrated, rare earths are a geopolitical lever, not just a commodity. The textbook episode came in **2010**, when a maritime dispute between China and Japan escalated and rare-earth shipments to Japan were reported to have been curtailed. Prices of some rare-earth oxides spiked many-fold over the following months. The episode was a wake-up call: it showed every advanced economy that a critical input to magnets, electronics, and defense systems could be turned off at the source. More recently, rare earths and the magnets made from them have featured directly in trade and export-control disputes, used as a bargaining chip precisely because the dependence is so hard to unwind quickly.

This is the "strategic" in "strategic mineral." A commodity whose supply is concentrated in one jurisdiction, that is hard to substitute, and that feeds essential industries is not merely a thing you buy — it is a pressure point. That is why governments treat rare earths as a national-security matter and pour subsidies into the slow, painful work of standing up non-Chinese refining capacity.

### The "green inflation" tension

Step back and notice the macro problem hiding in all of this. We have committed to a fast energy transition, which means a fast, durable increase in demand for green metals. But we have also established that the supply response is slow, lumpy, concentrated, and politically fraught. A demand curve that shifts out quickly against a supply curve that bends slowly does not just produce one boom — it can produce a *sustained* upward pressure on a whole class of input costs over the years it takes supply to catch up. Some economists call this **"green inflation" (or "greenflation")**: the idea that decarbonizing puts persistent upward pressure on metals, and therefore on the cost of the machines we are building to decarbonize.

The tension is real but it is not destiny, and the cycle is exactly why. The lithium round trip showed that a price spike, however dramatic, summons the supply that eventually crushes it — \$80,000 lithium did not last because \$80,000 was precisely the signal that funded the \$13,000 glut. So "green inflation" is better understood as a series of violent overshoots around a slowly rising trend than as a smooth, permanent ramp. The trend can be up — more metal is genuinely needed — while the *path* is a sawtooth of booms and busts. For a macro investor that distinction matters: the durable signal is the trend (more copper, more lithium, more grid metal needed for a decade), while the tradable signal is the cycle around it. The macro framing of how commodity costs feed inflation sits in the [commodities-as-macro-signals](/blog/trading/macro-trading/commodities-as-macro-signals-oil-copper-gold) note; the point here is that green metals are the corner of the complex where the demand story is most certain and the price path is least.

#### Worked example: a rare-earth supply shock and the price impact

Rare-earth markets are small and inelastic, so a modest supply disruption produces an outsized price move. Stylize it. Suppose the world consumes **60,000 tonnes/year** of a key magnet rare-earth oxide, and the short-run **price elasticity of demand is roughly −0.2** (meaning a 1% price rise cuts quantity demanded by only 0.2%, because there is no quick substitute for a magnet).

Now suppose a disruption removes **10% of supply** (6,000 tonnes). For the market to clear with such inelastic demand, the price must rise enough to destroy 10% of demand:

- Required price change ≈ (supply shortfall %) ÷ (elasticity) = 10% ÷ 0.2 = **roughly a 50% price increase.**

If the oxide started at \$60/kg, the shock pushes it toward \$90/kg — a 50% jump from losing one-tenth of supply. And that is a *gentle* case; the historical 2010 episode saw some oxides multiply several-fold because elasticity is even lower in the very short run and because buyers panic-stockpiled. The lesson: in a tiny, inelastic, single-source market, small physical disruptions become enormous price events — which is exactly what makes them effective as a strategic threat.

## Common misconceptions

A handful of beliefs cause investors to misread green metals. Each one is wrong, and the correction comes with a number.

**Myth 1: "Rare earths are running out / are geologically scarce."** No. Rare-earth elements are common in the crust — cerium is more abundant than copper. The scarcity is in *separation capacity*, where one country holds roughly 85-90% of the world total. You do not solve a refining bottleneck by finding more ore; the ore was never the constraint.

**Myth 2: "Surging EV demand means lithium can only go up."** The price went from ~\$6,000 to ~\$80,000 and back to ~\$13,000 *while EV sales rose every single year.* Demand growth and price are not the same thing, because supply responds — violently and on a lag. A rising-demand commodity with a slow, lumpy supply response is precisely the kind that boom-and-busts hardest. Strong demand is the *setup* for the cycle, not an exemption from it.

**Myth 3: "These are huge markets where a few percent doesn't matter."** They are tiny. The entire lithium market at the peak was worth a fraction of a single large oil major's revenue; the global rare-earth oxide market is a few billion dollars a year — smaller than a mid-cap company. Small markets move violently because a single new mine or a single export decision can shift the supply-demand balance by double digits, and a 10% supply shock in an inelastic market can move price ~50% (see the worked example above).

**Myth 4: "Cobalt is a one-way bet because every battery needs it."** Every battery needs *lithium*; not every battery needs *cobalt*. LFP chemistry uses none and is now the majority of new installations, while high-nickel NMC has cut cobalt content sharply. Designing cobalt out of a pack saves on the order of \$250 per vehicle, which is exactly the incentive that keeps shrinking cobalt demand per car even as car volumes rise. Substitutability is a structural cap on price.

**Myth 5: "The metal price is what's killing EV affordability."** Even at the absurd 2022 peak, the lithium in a mainstream pack was a few hundred dollars of a battery costing several thousand, and the cell is one part of a car costing tens of thousands. The metal price terrorizes the *miners*' equity, but it is a modest line item for the *carmaker*. Confusing the two leads to bad trades in both directions.

**Myth 6: "We'll just build our way out of the China dependence in a few years."** The lead times defeat this. A greenfield mine is five to ten years; a separation refinery is years on top of that, plus permitting fights over the radioactive waste, plus the metallurgical know-how that one country has spent four decades accumulating. The 2010 rare-earth scare prompted exactly this "build it elsewhere" rush, and a decade and a half later the refining concentration had barely moved — several of the projects went bankrupt when the panic-price faded. Diversifying a refining monopoly is a ten-to-twenty-year, capital-and-politics-intensive project, not a quick fix, and any thesis that assumes a fast unwind is mispricing the timeline. The dependence is sticky precisely because the hard part is the chemistry plant nobody wants to build or live next to.

## How it shows up in real markets

Three real episodes show the mechanics in action, with dates and numbers.

### The 2021-2022 lithium moonshot and the 2023-2024 collapse

We have already walked the price path, but watch how it played out across the market. As carbonate ran from the teens of thousands toward \$80,000 in 2022, lithium-miner equities went parabolic — Australian spodumene producers and global lithium names multiplied several times over, and capital flooded into exploration and expansion. The narrative was total: lithium was the "new oil," supply could never catch up, the price would stay high for a decade. Then 2023 arrived. New Australian and African supply ramped, Chinese lepidolite and converter capacity came on, EV growth in China decelerated from breakneck to merely fast, and the destocking of an over-built inventory chain accelerated the slide. By 2024 the price was near \$13,000, miner equities had given back most of their gains, and the same projects that were rushed at the top were being delayed or shelved. The round trip was a textbook of the capex cycle — and a brutal lesson for anyone who bought the "new oil" story at the peak.

### The 2010 China-Japan rare-earth episode

In September 2010, amid a territorial dispute, China's rare-earth exports to Japan were reported to have been disrupted. Because Japan's electronics and auto industries depended on Chinese rare earths and there was no alternative source at scale, the prices of several rare-earth oxides spiked sharply — some many-fold — over the following months, and a wave of "rare-earth crisis" headlines followed. The episode triggered a brief global investment boom in non-Chinese rare-earth projects (including the restart of Mountain Pass in the US and the build-out of Lynas in Australia and Malaysia). But here is the cycle again: as prices crashed back over 2012-2015 once the panic faded and a little new supply appeared, several of those projects went bankrupt — and the *refining* dependence on China barely budged, because almost nobody built the separation plants. A supply scare produced a price spike, the spike summoned investment, the investment overshot, the price collapsed, and the structural problem (refining concentration) remained. It is the same machine as lithium, just driven by geopolitics rather than EV demand.

### The 2022 nickel short squeeze on the LME

Green metals can also break the *market plumbing*. On 8 March 2022, with the Russian invasion of Ukraine threatening Russian nickel supply, the price of LME nickel — used in NMC batteries and stainless steel — spiked from around \$25,000 to over \$100,000/tonne intraday as a massive short position (held by a large producer) was caught in a squeeze. The London Metal Exchange took the extraordinary step of *cancelling* trades and suspending the market for days. For our purposes the lesson is not the squeeze mechanics but the fragility: these are markets thin and concentrated enough that a single large position, in a moment of supply fear, can detach the price from any fundamental anchor entirely. (The episode is one reason battery makers accelerated the shift toward nickel-free LFP.)

Across all three episodes the pattern holds: small, opaque, concentrated markets, slow supply, and a price that overshoots in both directions — sometimes driven by demand, sometimes by geopolitics, sometimes by a blown-up trade, but always amplified by the immaturity of the market itself.

## The playbook: how an investor should think about green metals

So you understand the mechanics. What do you *do* with them? The honest answer for most people is "be very careful," and here is the discipline behind that.

**Treat green metals as small, volatile, policy-driven trades — not buy-and-hold positions.** Everything we have covered points the same way. The markets are tiny and thin, so they move violently. The supply response is slow and lumpy, so prices overshoot. The demand is being reshaped by technology (LFP designing out cobalt and nickel) and by government (subsidies, export controls, domestic-content rules), so a thesis can be invalidated by a chemistry change or a policy announcement rather than by fundamentals. None of that describes an asset you tuck away for twenty years and forget.

A few concrete principles:

1. **The price tells you where you are in the cycle — read it against marginal cost.** A green-metal price far *above* the marginal cost of production (lithium at \$60,000+ against a ~\$9,000 cost) is not a bull signal; it is a *warning* that the price is summoning the supply that will destroy it. A price *below* marginal cost (lithium at \$11,000-13,000, where high-cost tonnes lose money) is where the seeds of the next squeeze are being planted, because supply is leaving. Buy-and-hold gets this exactly backwards — it buys the euphoria and sells the despair. The cycle rewards the opposite.

2. **Distinguish irreplaceable from substitutable metals.** Lithium is, for now, structurally irreplaceable in lithium-ion batteries — there is no lithium-ion battery without lithium. Cobalt and nickel are being actively designed out. A demand thesis on an irreplaceable metal is sturdier than one on a substitutable metal, where every price rise accelerates its own obsolescence. Weight your conviction accordingly.

3. **For rare earths, follow the refineries, not the mines.** A new non-Chinese *mine* changes little; a new non-Chinese *separation plant* changes the strategic picture. When you read "country X secures rare-earth supply," ask whether they built refining capacity or just dug a hole. Almost always it is the hole, and almost always it changes nothing.

4. **Respect the policy layer.** These are the most policy-driven commodities in the world. Subsidies (the demand floor), export controls (the supply weapon), and domestic-content rules (the demand redirection) can swamp fundamentals for years. A green-metal position is partly a bet on government decisions, which means it carries a kind of risk that no supply-demand model captures. Size it like the option-on-policy that it is.

5. **Size for the volatility.** A metal that can fall 84% from peak to trough (lithium) or get its market suspended after a 4x intraday spike (nickel) is not a position you size like a diversified equity index. If you take exposure at all — via a miner, a diversified critical-minerals fund, or a futures position where one exists — size it small enough that the inevitable whipsaw cannot force you out at the worst moment. (The cross-asset framing for fitting a volatile commodity sleeve into a portfolio sits in the [macro-signals from oil, copper and gold](/blog/trading/macro-trading/commodities-as-macro-signals-oil-copper-gold) note.)

6. **Choose your vehicle deliberately — there are three, and they behave differently.** The first is a *pure-play miner* (a lithium or rare-earth producer): maximum leverage to the metal, but you also take on operational risk, balance-sheet risk, and the timing risk of the capex cycle landing on the company at the wrong moment. The second is a *diversified critical-minerals fund or basket*: it spreads the binding-constraint risk across the five metals so you are not betting on which one is tightest this year, at the cost of diluted upside in any single boom. The third is a *direct futures or physical position*, which exists cleanly only for the maturer metals (copper, nickel, increasingly lithium) and not at all for most rare earths — so for the most concentrated, most strategic metals, there is often no clean way to take direct exposure, and you are forced into equities. Match the vehicle to the metal: a futures contract for nickel, a basket for the transition broadly, a single miner only when you have a specific, time-bounded cycle view and the stomach for the drawdown.

7. **Watch the policy calendar as closely as the price.** Because these are the most policy-sensitive commodities, the events that move them are often not supply-demand prints but government announcements: an export-control measure, a domestic-content rule in an EV subsidy, a new strategic-stockpile program, a permitting decision on a flagship refinery. A green-metal investor who only watches inventories and prices is reading half the board. The other half is the legislative and trade-policy calendar of the major producing and consuming nations — and a single line in a trade deal about magnets or graphite can matter more than a quarter of demand data.

Step back to the series' spine. A commodity is a physical thing forced through a financial contract, and the *character* of that contract shapes the price. For oil, the contract is deep, liquid, and centuries-old, so the price is discovered cleanly. For green metals, the contract is young, thin, and opaque — assessed prices, private offtakes, a single dominant refiner — so the price is discovered *badly*, with enormous overshoot in both directions. Layer on a supply response that crawls over a decade while demand lurches in a year, and add a government at every node treating these as strategic assets, and you get the most violent corner of the commodity world. Green metals are where geology meets industrial policy meets a brutal price cycle. The electrification of everything is almost certainly real and durable. The path of any single green-metal price along the way is almost certainly not a straight line — so you trade the cycle, you do not marry the metal.

![Lithium versus copper indexed to 2019 showing the green metal cycle is far wilder than a mature metal](/imgs/blogs/battery-and-green-metals-lithium-cobalt-and-rare-earths-7.png)

The closing figure makes the whole argument in one picture. Copper — a deep, mature, century-old market and the subject of the cross-asset [copper and silver](/blog/trading/cross-asset/metals-copper-silver-the-economys-pulse) note — barely leaves a band of roughly 100 to 155 over six years, even through a pandemic and a supply scare. Lithium over the same window tripled, then peaked far off the top of the chart, then gave it all back and kept falling. Same demand driver (the energy transition), wildly different price behavior — and the difference is almost entirely the *maturity of the market*, not the metal. That gap, between a mature commodity and an immature one, is the single most useful thing to hold in your head when a "critical mineral" is the hottest story on the screen.
