> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Vietnam's economy grew faster than its ability to power itself, so a country that used to *export* energy now *imports* rising volumes of thermal coal priced in US dollars — while the state utility, EVN, is forced to sell that power at a tariff capped in Vietnamese dong.
>
> - The power mix is still roughly **half coal** (~47%), with hydro (~28%), gas, and a fast-grown solar/wind slice (~13%). Demand grows ~8-10% a year, faster than the economy.
> - Domestic coal can't keep up, so Vietnam imports **50+ million tonnes of thermal coal a year**, paid in USD off the global (Newcastle) benchmark. That is the FX bill underneath the lights.
> - EVN's costs are in dollars at world prices; its retail price is set by the state in dong for social and inflation reasons. When coal spiked in 2022, that mismatch handed EVN a multi-billion-dollar loss — the textbook regulated-utility squeeze.
> - The **one fact to remember:** in mid-2023, a drought, a heatwave, and strained coal logistics hit at once and the North suffered **rolling blackouts that idled foreign-owned factories** — turning "power reliability" into a front-line FDI risk.

In late May and early June of 2023, something happened in northern Vietnam that is not supposed to happen in a fast-rising manufacturing hub: the power went out. Not for a few flickering seconds, but in rolling, scheduled cuts that rolled across provinces — Bac Ninh, Bac Giang, Thai Nguyen, the industrial belt north of Hanoi where the world assembles a large share of its phones and electronics. Factories owned by some of the biggest names in global manufacturing were told to cut load or go dark for hours at a time. The grid operator was openly rationing electricity in the middle of a heatwave, in the middle of a workday, in the middle of the country's biggest export engine.

For a country that spent the 2010s telling investors "come build here, the power is cheap and reliable," it was a deeply uncomfortable week. And it was not a freak accident. It was the visible surface of a deeper structural story — one about a power mix that is still mostly coal, a domestic coal industry that stopped being able to feed its own power plants, an import bill denominated in dollars, and a retail electricity price frozen in dong by a government that treats cheap power as a social contract.

This post is about that whole machine and the squeeze running through the middle of it. It is the closing post of our Vietnam track, and it ties the series' spine — *a physical commodity forced through a financial contract* — to the most consequential commodity story in any developing economy: who pays for the electrons, and in which currency.

![Vietnam energy squeeze chain from power demand to imported coal in USD to capped VND tariff to EVN margin squeeze](/imgs/blogs/electricity-coal-and-vietnams-import-dependency-1.png)

Read that chain left to right and you have the entire post in one line. Power demand grows almost twice as fast as the economy. Domestic coal can't keep up, so the gap gets filled with imported coal bought in US dollars. EVN burns it to make electricity, then sells that electricity at a retail tariff the state caps in dong. The two currencies, and the two price-setting mechanisms, don't move together — and when global coal jumps, the gap between them becomes a loss. Everything below is an unpacking of those six boxes.

## Foundations: how a country powers itself, and what "import dependency" means

Let's build this from zero, because almost every term here is doing real work.

**Electricity is not a commodity you can store at scale.** Unlike a barrel of oil or a tonne of wheat, electricity must be generated at virtually the same instant it is consumed. Supply and demand have to match second by second across the whole grid, or the system's frequency drifts and protective equipment trips plants offline to avoid damage. That is why a grid operator can be forced into *rolling blackouts*: when it physically cannot make enough power to meet demand, the only release valve is to cut some customers so the rest of the system survives. There is no warehouse of spare electrons to draw down.

**The "power mix" is the recipe of fuels a country burns or harnesses to make that electricity.** Coal, natural gas, hydropower (water spinning turbines behind dams), nuclear, solar, wind. Each has a different cost, a different reliability profile, and — crucially for our story — a different *currency exposure*. Hydro is essentially free to run once the dam is built, but it depends on rainfall. Coal and gas have to be bought continuously, and if you import them, you buy them in dollars at world prices. Solar and wind are cheap to run but intermittent: they produce when the sun shines or the wind blows, not when you ask.

**A baseload plant** is one that runs nearly all the time to cover the steady, always-on demand — coal and large hydro play this role in Vietnam. **A peaker** runs only when demand spikes — gas turbines, typically. The grid has to be planned so that baseload covers the floor and flexible plants cover the peaks, with transmission lines strong enough to carry power from where it's made to where it's used. Hold onto that last clause; the transmission grid becomes the villain of the renewable section.

**Now, "import dependency."** A country is energy-*independent* if it produces enough fuel at home to meet its own needs, and energy-*dependent* if it must buy a meaningful share from abroad. Vietnam used to be a net energy *exporter* — it shipped crude oil from its offshore fields and exported anthracite coal from the mines of Quang Ninh province in the North. As recently as the late 2000s, coal was an export earner. But two things happened: domestic output of cheap, accessible coal plateaued (the easy seams get mined out, and deeper mining costs more), while electricity demand exploded as the economy industrialised. The lines crossed. Vietnam became a net energy importer, and the single fastest-growing piece of that import bill is *thermal coal* — the lower-grade coal you burn in a power station, as distinct from the premium coking coal used in steelmaking.

**Thermal coal is priced globally in US dollars per tonne**, benchmarked most famously off the **Newcastle** index — the price of coal loaded at the Australian port of Newcastle, the reference point for the Asia-Pacific seaborne market. When a Vietnamese power company buys imported coal, it pays a price tied to that dollar benchmark, plus freight. So the fuel going into a "domestic" Vietnamese power plant is, in financial terms, a dollar-denominated commodity. That is the paper side of the physical thing — exactly the series' spine. The lump of coal is physical; the price it clears at is a financial contract written in dollars.

A few more terms before we move, because they recur:

- **Thermal coal vs coking coal.** *Thermal* (or "steam") coal is burned to boil water and spin a steam turbine — it's the power-station fuel and the subject of this post. *Coking* (or "metallurgical") coal is a premium grade baked into coke for blast-furnace steelmaking — it's the steel-mill fuel, and it belongs to the [steel and HPG story](/blog/trading/commodities/steel-hpg-and-the-vietnam-construction-cycle), not here. The two trade at different prices off different benchmarks; conflating them is one of the most common errors in energy commentary.
- **Capacity vs generation.** *Capacity* (measured in megawatts, MW) is the maximum a plant *could* produce at any instant — the size of the engine. *Generation* (measured in megawatt-hours, MWh, over time) is what it *actually* produced. A 1,000 MW solar farm has lots of capacity but generates only when the sun is up, so its annual generation is far less than a 1,000 MW coal plant that runs day and night. The gap between the two — the *capacity factor* — is the whole reason a power mix measured by capacity looks very different from one measured by generation.
- **Capacity factor.** The fraction of a plant's theoretical maximum it actually achieves over a year. Coal baseload runs at a high capacity factor (60-80%); solar in a sunny tropical zone runs at maybe 16-20% (it's dark half the time and hazy or cloudy some of the rest). This number is why "we installed X gigawatts of solar" translates into far fewer kilowatt-hours than the headline suggests, and why solar cannot simply *replace* coal megawatt-for-megawatt.
- **Reserve margin.** The spare generating capacity available above the expected peak demand, as a percentage. A healthy system keeps a comfortable cushion (15-20%+) so that a plant tripping offline, a heatwave, or a drought doesn't cause a shortfall. A thin reserve margin is a grid running without a safety net — which is precisely the condition that turned 2023's bad weather into blackouts.

#### Worked example: turning a power bill into tonnes of coal

Let's make "import dependency" concrete with the most basic arithmetic in the whole post.

A modern coal power plant needs roughly **0.4-0.5 tonnes of thermal coal to generate one megawatt-hour (MWh)** of electricity, depending on coal quality and plant efficiency. Vietnam generates on the order of **280 terawatt-hours (TWh)** of electricity a year, and coal is about **47%** of that — call it **130 TWh** from coal, or 130,000,000 MWh.

At 0.45 tonnes of coal per MWh, that is:

- 130,000,000 MWh × 0.45 t/MWh ≈ **58.5 million tonnes of thermal coal burned per year** just for power.

Domestic mines supply a chunk of that, but the shortfall — and the highest-quality coal for the newest plants — is imported. Vietnam's customs data shows imports of **50-55 million tonnes a year** by 2023-2024. At an imported price of, say, \$120 per tonne, the import bill alone runs to **50,000,000 × \$120 = \$6 billion a year** in foreign currency.

The intuition here is simple and sobering: every kilowatt-hour a northern factory uses is, at the margin, a few cents of US dollars leaving the country to buy a tonne of Australian or Indonesian coal.

## The power mix: still half coal, with a fast-grown green slice

Here is what Vietnam actually burns and harnesses to make its electricity.

![Vietnam electricity generation mix bar chart coal 47 percent hydro 28 gas 9 solar 9 wind 4 other 3](/imgs/blogs/electricity-coal-and-vietnams-import-dependency-2.png)

Coal is the spine at about 47% of generation. Hydropower is the second pillar at roughly 28% — Vietnam is geographically blessed with big rivers and has built large dams, especially in the North (the Da River cascade) and the Central Highlands. Gas is around 9%, fed historically by domestic offshore fields whose output is now declining, which is its own looming import story (Vietnam is building LNG import terminals to replace falling domestic gas — a topic we cover in the [LNG and the globalization of gas](/blog/trading/commodities/lng-and-the-globalization-of-gas-shipping-the-uncontainable) post). And then the new arrival: solar at roughly 9% and wind at about 4%, a combined ~13% renewable slice that barely existed in 2018.

Two features of this mix decide everything that follows.

**First, coal and hydro together are ~75% of the system, and they have opposite weaknesses.** Coal's weakness is *cost and currency* — you have to keep buying the fuel, increasingly in dollars. Hydro's weakness is *weather* — a dam with no water behind it is a very expensive pile of concrete. Critically, these two are not evenly distributed. The North leans heavily on coal and hydro; the South has more gas and the bulk of the new solar. So a drought that drains northern reservoirs and a heatwave that spikes northern demand can hit one half of the country at once, while the South — with different fuels and a transmission bottleneck in between — can't always send help. That regional split is exactly why 2023's blackouts were a *northern* event.

**Second, the green slice arrived in a rush, not a plan.** Vietnam's solar capacity went from almost nothing to one of the largest in Southeast Asia in roughly two years (2019-2020), pulled in by a generous fixed price for solar power. That speed is a triumph of investment — and, as we'll see, a headache for a grid that couldn't lay transmission lines fast enough to carry it.

One more subtlety worth naming: this mix is measured by *generation share*, not by *installed capacity*, and the two tell different stories. By installed capacity, solar's slice looks much larger — Vietnam has a lot of solar nameplate megawatts — but because solar only runs at a ~16-20% capacity factor while coal runs at 60-80%, solar's share of actual electricity *generated* is far smaller than its share of capacity. This is the trap in every "look how much renewable capacity we built" headline: capacity is the engine size, generation is the distance travelled, and a grid keeps the lights on with generation. It's also why a country can simultaneously have "a huge solar fleet" and "a coal-dependent grid" — both statements are true because they measure different things. When you read any power-mix number, the first question to ask is whether it's capacity or generation; the answer changes the whole picture.

#### Worked example: how fast must capacity grow at 8-10% demand?

Power demand growing at **8-10% a year** sounds abstract until you compound it.

Start with demand of 100 units today. At 9% annual growth:

- Year 1: 109
- Year 3: ~119 × 1.09 ≈ **129**
- Year 5: ≈ **154**
- Year 8: ≈ **199** — demand roughly **doubles in 8 years**.

A doubling every 8 years means the country must build the equivalent of *its entire existing power system again* within a decade just to stand still — new plants, new mines or import contracts, new transmission. To put a dollar figure on it: a single 1,200 MW coal plant costs on the order of **\$2 billion** to build. Doubling a ~80 GW system implies tens of such plants, on the order of **\$10-15 billion of capital per year** across all fuels. The pressure to add the *cheapest, fastest* capacity — which historically meant coal — is enormous, and it collides head-on with climate commitments.

The takeaway: 8-10% demand growth is not a number you can grow into gradually; it is a forced march that has to be financed, fuelled, and wired every single year.

## From exporter to importer: the rising coal-import bill

This is the structural pivot of the whole story. Watch the trend.

![Vietnam thermal coal imports bar chart by year in million tonnes with a rising trend line](/imgs/blogs/electricity-coal-and-vietnams-import-dependency-3.png)

The bars bounce around year to year — imports dipped in 2021-2022 when global coal was expensive and EVN tried to lean on cheaper domestic supply, then surged back in 2023 as demand recovered and plants needed to refill stockpiles. But the trend line points firmly up: from the low-40s million tonnes around 2019 toward 55 million tonnes by 2024. The wobble around the trend is itself informative — it's the system flexing between "burn what we can mine at home" and "import the rest," with the swing factor being the global price and the urgency of demand.

Why can't domestic coal just scale up to match? Three reasons, each worth understanding because they generalise to many resource economies.

**The geology got harder.** Vietnam's accessible anthracite is concentrated in Quang Ninh province, and decades of mining have pushed operations deeper and into thinner, faulted seams. Deeper mines mean higher extraction costs, more water to pump, more safety risk, and slower output growth. The cheap coal near the surface is largely gone.

**The quality is wrong for the newest plants.** Vietnam's domestic anthracite is high-grade but limited in volume; many of the newest coal plants were actually designed to burn imported *sub-bituminous* coal (lower energy, but cheaper and abundant from Indonesia and Australia). So even when domestic coal exists, it may not be the right feedstock for the specific boilers that have been built. The plant's appetite was set at the design stage to depend on imports.

**Demand simply outran supply.** Even flat domestic output looks like shrinking *share* when total coal demand for power is rising 8-10% a year. The gap between what the mines deliver and what the plants need can only be closed one way: ships from Newcastle and Kalimantan.

It's worth pausing on the historical arc, because it reframes how you read Vietnam's energy politics. Through the 1990s and 2000s, Vietnam was a confident energy *exporter*. Crude oil from the Bach Ho ("White Tiger") field and others, developed with Soviet and then international partners, was for years the country's single largest export earner — a petrostate-lite story. Quang Ninh's anthracite, mined since the French colonial era, was shipped to Japan, China, and Korea. Energy was something Vietnam *sold to the world*. Within a single generation that flipped: crude exports faded as fields matured and as a domestic refining-and-petrochemical complex (Dung Quat, then Nghi Son) absorbed more of the barrels at home; coal turned inward to feed a power fleet that kept multiplying; and offshore gas, the cleanest of the fossil options, began its own decline. By the late 2010s the country was a net energy importer across the board. The psychological shift matters: a state that grew up thinking of itself as resource-rich has had to internalise that it is now resource-*short* relative to its own appetite — and that reframing is what drives the urgency (and occasional panic) in its energy planning.

The gas piece deserves a sentence of its own because it's the next coal-style problem in slow motion. Vietnam's domestic gas, piped from offshore basins to feed power plants and fertiliser, is in structural decline as the mature fields deplete and new discoveries (like the large but politically and technically complex Block B and Ca Voi Xanh / "Blue Whale" fields) face long, costly development timelines. The plan to bridge the gap is imported **LNG** — liquefied natural gas shipped in, regasified at new coastal terminals, and burned in new gas plants. But LNG, like coal, is bought in dollars at a volatile global price (the JKM Asian benchmark), so substituting imported LNG for declining domestic gas swaps one domestic fuel for another dollar-priced import. Vietnam isn't escaping import dependency by going to gas; it's adding a second dollar-denominated fuel line to the same bill.

#### Worked example: the dollar coal-import bill and its FX bite

Let's size the foreign-currency cost precisely, because this is where the macro story bites.

Take **52 million tonnes** of imported thermal coal (a representative recent year) at a delivered price of **\$110 per tonne**:

- 52,000,000 t × \$110/t = **\$5.72 billion** of imports, paid in US dollars.

Now compare that to the currency it's recovered in. EVN sells the resulting power for **Vietnamese dong**. Suppose the dong weakens 5% against the dollar over the year (a routine move for a managed-but-drifting currency). The *same* coal contract, settled in dollars, now costs 5% more in dong terms even if the dollar price never changed:

- \$5.72 billion × 1.05 (in dong terms) ≈ the equivalent of **\$6.0 billion** worth of dong — an extra **~\$290 million** of cost purely from the exchange rate, with zero change in the physical coal.

That is the hidden second squeeze. EVN isn't just exposed to the dollar *price* of coal; it's exposed to the dollar *itself*. A weaker dong inflates the cost of every imported tonne, and EVN cannot pass that through instantly because its selling price is set by the state. The lesson: an import-dependent utility carries two FX risks at once — the commodity price and the currency — and both are denominated in a money it does not earn.

#### Worked example: the energy-import bill as a macro drag

Zoom out from EVN to the whole country, because the coal bill is also a national-accounts story.

Add up the dollar-denominated energy imports Vietnam now carries: thermal coal (~\$6 billion), refined petroleum products and crude for the refineries (well over \$10 billion in many years), and a growing LNG bill as gas terminals come online. The combined fossil-energy import line runs into the tens of billions of dollars a year — call it a conservative **\$20 billion** of energy imports.

Set that against the trade balance. Vietnam runs a goods trade surplus (its export factories sell more than the country imports overall), but that surplus is *thin and hard-won* — often in the \$20-30 billion range. So the energy-import bill alone is roughly the same order of magnitude as the entire trade surplus the export economy fights to generate. Put differently: a sizeable chunk of what Vietnam earns assembling phones and stitching garments for the world goes straight back out the door to pay for the fuel that powers those factories.

Now stress it. If global energy prices rise 30% in a bad year, that \$20 billion bill becomes **\$26 billion** — an extra **\$6 billion** of dollars leaving the country, enough to swing the trade balance materially and put pressure on the dong (which, recall, then makes the next coal cargo even more expensive in local terms). The intuition: Vietnam's energy import dependency isn't just EVN's problem — it's a structural transmission line from global commodity prices straight into the country's currency and external accounts.

## The EVN squeeze: dollar costs in, dong price out

Now we reach the financial heart of the post: why the state utility loses money, and why that's a feature of the system rather than a one-off mistake.

**EVN — Vietnam Electricity — is the state-owned utility that dominates the sector.** It owns or controls most of the transmission grid, the dispatch (deciding which plants run when), and a large share of generation, and it is the single buyer that purchases power from independent producers and sells it to households and businesses. Critically, **EVN does not set its own retail price.** The average retail electricity tariff is approved by the government, which treats cheap, stable electricity as a pillar of social policy and a weapon against inflation. Raising the power tariff is politically fraught — it feeds straight into the cost of living and into factory costs — so the government tends to hold it down and approve increases slowly, in small steps, often well after costs have already risen.

So picture the asymmetry that sets up. EVN's *costs* are largely market-driven and partly dollar-denominated: imported coal off the Newcastle benchmark, imported or domestic gas, and power bought from private generators whose own fuel costs float. EVN's *revenue* is administratively fixed in dong and slow to adjust. In a calm year, the approved tariff is set to roughly cover average costs, and EVN runs near break-even. But the moment global fuel prices jump, costs leap while the tariff sits frozen — and the gap is a loss EVN simply has to absorb.

![EVN price control squeeze before and after coal spike showing USD fuel cost up and VND tariff frozen turning to a loss](/imgs/blogs/electricity-coal-and-vietnams-import-dependency-4.png)

The before-and-after captures the mechanism. In a normal year with coal near \$60 a tonne, EVN's dollar fuel cost per kilowatt-hour is modest, the dong tariff covers it, and the utility runs near break-even. When coal spikes toward \$400 a tonne — as it did in 2022 — the fuel cost per kilowatt-hour triples, the tariff stays frozen for inflation reasons, and EVN posts a record loss. The structure didn't change; only the coal price did, and the rigid tariff turned a price shock into a balance-sheet hole.

This is the **classic regulated-utility margin squeeze**, and it's worth naming because it shows up worldwide — any utility with market-priced fuel and a state-capped consumer price faces it. (It's the same dynamic that crushed European utilities in 2022, and a cousin of the inflation-passthrough problem we discuss in the cross-asset [energy as the inflation engine](/blog/trading/cross-asset/energy-oil-gas-the-inflation-engine) post.) What makes Vietnam's version sharper is the *currency* layer: the cost is in dollars, the cap is in dong, and the dong tends to weaken in exactly the global risk-off episodes that also spike commodity prices.

![Dual axis chart of Vietnam coal import volume bars and illustrative Newcastle coal price line showing the 2022 spike](/imgs/blogs/electricity-coal-and-vietnams-import-dependency-5.png)

Overlay the global coal price (the red line — an *illustrative* Newcastle benchmark, anchored to roughly \$60 a tonne pre-2021, the ~\$400 spike in 2022 after Russia's invasion of Ukraine scrambled the global energy market, and the slide back toward \$130 by 2024) on Vietnam's import volumes, and you can read EVN's pain directly. In 2022 the price line goes vertical while EVN is contractually committed to keep generating. It cut import volumes where it could and leaned on domestic coal and hydro, but the coal it *did* buy cost a fortune, and the frozen tariff meant none of that cost reached the customer. The result: EVN reported a loss measured in the tens of trillions of dong — on the order of **\$1 billion or more** — for 2022, and the strain carried into 2023.

#### Worked example: EVN's fuel-cost squeeze, in US cents per kWh

This is the USD-anchored worked example, and it is the single most important calculation in the post.

A coal plant burning **0.45 tonnes of coal per MWh** (= 1,000 kWh) means each kWh carries **0.45 / 1000 = 0.00045 tonnes** of coal.

**Normal year, coal at \$60/tonne:**
- Fuel cost per kWh = 0.00045 t × \$60 = **\$0.027**, i.e. **2.7 US cents per kWh**.

**Spike year, coal at \$360/tonne (the 2022 shock):**
- Fuel cost per kWh = 0.00045 t × \$360 = **\$0.162**, i.e. **16.2 US cents per kWh** — *six times* the normal fuel cost.

Now the selling side. Vietnam's average retail electricity tariff is roughly **2,100 VND per kWh**. At an exchange rate near **24,500 VND per USD**, that's:
- 2,100 / 24,500 = **\$0.0857**, i.e. about **8.6 US cents per kWh**.

So in a normal year, an 8.6-cent tariff comfortably covers a 2.7-cent fuel cost (the rest pays for the grid, the non-coal plants, debt, and operations). But in the spike year, the **coal fuel cost alone (16.2 cents) is nearly double the entire retail tariff (8.6 cents)** — before you've paid for a single transmission tower, a single employee, or a single dong of interest. Every coal-fired kilowatt-hour sold in 2022 lost money on fuel alone.

The intuition: EVN was selling electricity for less than the coal it took to make it. That is not a management failure — it is exactly what a dollar-priced input and a dong-capped output do to you when the global price triples.

### Why the tariff can't just track costs

A natural reaction is: why not simply let the tariff float with fuel costs, the way a competitive market would? Several forces lock it down, and understanding them is understanding the politics of energy in any developing economy.

**Electricity is an inflation input, not just a household bill.** Power feeds into the cost of nearly everything — every factory, every cold-storage warehouse, every office. A tariff hike doesn't just raise household bills; it propagates through the whole price level. A government fighting to keep headline inflation near target (and to protect a managed currency) is deeply reluctant to push up a price that touches every other price. So the tariff becomes a macro-stabilisation tool, held down precisely when fuel costs — and thus inflationary pressure — are highest. The cruelty is in the timing: the tariff is frozen hardest exactly when EVN most needs it to rise.

**Cheap power is part of the FDI sales pitch.** Vietnam competed for decades on cheap, reliable inputs — cheap labour, cheap land, cheap power. Raising the industrial tariff risks undercutting the very promise that drew the factories. There's a tension here: the same investors who flee unreliable power also flee expensive power, and the state is trying to deliver both cheapness and reliability from a system that, when coal spikes, can't afford both.

**It's politically visible.** Unlike a tax buried in a budget, the electricity tariff is a number every citizen sees on a monthly bill. Raising it is conspicuous and unpopular, so it gets approved cautiously, in small increments, after public consultation and high-level sign-off — a slow process by design. The 2023 hikes (3% in May, ~4.5% in November) were the first in *four years*, and even those modest moves came only after EVN's losses became impossible to ignore.

The net effect is a tariff that adjusts in slow, lagged steps while fuel costs move in fast, violent leaps. EVN sits in the gap, absorbing the shock on its balance sheet — which is, in effect, a transfer from the state utility (and ultimately the state) to consumers and industry. The "subsidy" isn't a line in the budget; it's a loss on EVN's income statement.

#### Worked example: how a small tariff hike fails to close a fuel gap

Watch how feeble a normal tariff increase is against a fuel shock, because this is why EVN's losses can't be fixed with a modest hike.

Recall the spike-year fuel math: coal-fired fuel cost jumped from **2.7 to 16.2 US cents per kWh** — a rise of **13.5 cents per kWh** on the coal fleet. Against a retail tariff of **8.6 cents/kWh**, a "big" tariff increase of 5% adds:

- 8.6 cents × 5% = **0.43 cents per kWh** of extra revenue.

That 0.43-cent increase recovers about **3%** of the 13.5-cent fuel-cost jump on the coal fleet. You would need a tariff increase of roughly **+150%** on the coal-attributable portion to fully cover a coal-price tripling — politically unthinkable. So the tariff tool is structurally too weak and too slow to neutralise a global fuel shock; the only fast release valves left are to burn cheaper fuel (hydro, domestic coal), cut imports, or eat the loss. In 2022, EVN did all three — and still lost roughly \$1 billion.

The lesson: a capped, slow-moving tariff isn't a small inconvenience during a fuel spike — it is mathematically incapable of keeping pace, which is why the squeeze shows up as a loss rather than a price increase.

### The PDP8 plan and the coal-vs-climate bind

Layered on top of the price squeeze is a planning bind. Vietnam's **Power Development Plan 8 (PDP8)**, approved in 2023, is the master blueprint for the system to 2030. It tries to thread an almost impossible needle: meet ~9% demand growth, honour a pledge (made at COP26) to reach net-zero by 2050 and to stop building *new* coal, and keep the lights on and the FDI flowing — all at once.

The plan leans hard into renewables and gas (including imported LNG) for new capacity, freezes most new coal, and bets heavily on offshore wind in the 2030s. But the existing coal fleet — much of it young, financed, and contracted for decades — doesn't disappear; it keeps running and keeps needing imported fuel through the 2030s. So the import-dependency story doesn't end with PDP8; it *persists* through the transition, even as the marginal new megawatt goes green. The bind is structural: you can stop building coal plants overnight, but you can't stop *feeding* the ones you already have, and those are the plants that set the dollar fuel bill for the next fifteen years.

## The 2023 northern blackouts: when three pressures hit at once

The financial squeeze is chronic. The blackouts were acute — a physical failure to make enough power, concentrated in the North in May-June 2023. And they happened not because of one cause but because three independent pressures peaked together.

![Northern blackout chain drought and heatwave and coal logistics converging into a northern supply gap and rolling blackouts](/imgs/blogs/electricity-coal-and-vietnams-import-dependency-6.png)

Trace the three streams converging on the right.

**Stream one: an El Nino drought drained the reservoirs.** The North leans heavily on hydropower, and hydro depends entirely on water behind the dams. An El Nino year brought below-average rainfall; by late May, several major northern reservoirs had fallen to or near their "dead water" level — the point below which the turbines can no longer run. Hydro output, normally a big slab of northern supply, collapsed at exactly the wrong moment. This is hydro's structural weakness made vivid: zero fuel cost, but total dependence on the weather.

**Stream two: a record heatwave spiked demand.** The same weather system brought brutal heat — temperatures pushing past 40°C across the North. Air conditioning is the single most weather-sensitive electrical load there is, and demand surged to records just as supply was shrinking. Demand and supply moved in opposite directions simultaneously.

**Stream three: coal logistics strained.** With hydro gone, the system leaned harder on coal plants, but several units were down for maintenance or running short of fuel, and the logistics of getting enough coal — domestic and imported — to the right plants on time buckled under the surge. A grid that depends on coal to backstop failed hydro discovers that "we have coal plants" and "those plants have fuel in the bunker today" are two different statements.

When three streams like that converge, the grid operator runs out of options and the only release valve is rationing. The result was rolling blackouts across the northern industrial provinces.

#### Worked example: the blackout cost to one FDI factory

Why does a blackout in Bac Ninh make global headlines? Because of what's plugged into that grid.

Consider a large electronics-assembly plant — the kind of FDI giant that anchors Vietnam's export economy. Suppose it produces output worth **\$8 million per day** when running at full tilt. A power cut that idles the lines for, say, **8 hours of a 24-hour day** costs roughly:

- \$8,000,000 × (8 / 24) ≈ **\$2.67 million of lost output for that day**, at that one factory.

Multiply across dozens of factories in the affected provinces over several days, and the direct hit runs into the hundreds of millions of dollars — before you count spoiled work-in-progress, missed shipment deadlines, contractual penalties, and the diesel burned running emergency generators (at a fuel cost several times higher than grid power). Vietnam's own industry estimates put the 2023 blackout damage in the **\$1.4 billion** range for affected manufacturers.

The deeper cost is reputational and doesn't show up in any single day's arithmetic: an FDI investor choosing between Vietnam and a regional rival now has to price in "power reliability" as a real risk. The lesson — power reliability is no longer a back-office utility concern in Vietnam; it is a line item in the investment case for the entire export model.

### Why the South couldn't rescue the North

A reasonable question is: Vietnam wasn't short of power *nationally* in 2023 — the South had gas plants and a glut of solar. Why couldn't the surplus simply flow north?

The answer is the transmission grid — specifically, the long, thin spine that connects a country shaped like a 1,650-kilometre-long S. Vietnam's main North-South high-voltage backbone is a 500kV line that runs the length of the country, and it has a finite carrying capacity. Pushing large amounts of power from the southern surplus to the northern deficit means shoving it down that single corridor, and the corridor was already near its limit. When the North needed gigawatts of help, the wire could only deliver a trickle. The country had power; it just couldn't move it to where the shortage was.

This is the single most underappreciated fact about electricity systems, and it generalises far beyond Vietnam: **a grid is only as strong as its weakest interconnection.** Two regions joined by a thin wire are, in a crisis, almost two separate grids. The policy response was swift and telling — Vietnam fast-tracked a new 500kV "circuit 3" line to roughly double North-South transfer capacity, treating the wire itself as critical energy-security infrastructure on par with the power plants. The blackouts taught the lesson the expensive way: in an electricity system, transmission is not plumbing you can add later; it is the thing that decides whether your generation is one national resource or a set of stranded regional islands.

### Reserve margin: the cushion that wasn't there

Step back from the weather and the wire, and the root enabling condition was a *thin reserve margin* — too little spare capacity above peak demand to absorb a bad day. A system that grows demand at ~9% a year but adds capacity more slowly steadily erodes its cushion. Each year the peak creeps closer to the ceiling, until a single bad summer pushes demand through it.

Reserve margin is the number a grid analyst watches above almost all others, because it converts every other risk into a probability. With a fat 25% cushion, a drought-plus-heatwave is survivable: you fire up reserve plants and ride it out. With a thin 5-8% cushion, the same weather is a blackout, because there's nothing left to call on. Vietnam's northern reserve margin had been ground down by years of demand outrunning new capacity — so when hydro collapsed and demand spiked, there was no slack to deploy. The weather was the trigger; the missing cushion was the loaded gun.

## The renewable boom and the grid it outran

The optimistic counter-story is the solar rush — and it carries its own hard lesson about why building generation is the easy half of an energy transition.

Between 2019 and 2020, Vietnam offered a generous **feed-in tariff (FIT)** — a fixed, above-market price guaranteed for 20 years to anyone who built solar and connected it. Investors responded the way investors always do to a guaranteed return: they piled in. In barely 18 months, Vietnam went from negligible solar to one of the largest solar fleets in Southeast Asia, the vast majority of it clustered in the sunny, dry south-central provinces (Ninh Thuan, Binh Thuan) where the irradiation is best and the land is cheap.

The problem is geography and wire. The sun is brightest where the *people and factories aren't*. To use all that southern solar, you need transmission lines to carry the power to the demand centres — and high-voltage lines take years to permit, route, and build, far longer than a solar farm that can be erected in months. So the generation arrived in a rush and the wires didn't.

![Solar rush versus grid before and after showing feed in tariff boom then transmission lag and curtailment](/imgs/blogs/electricity-coal-and-vietnams-import-dependency-7.png)

The before-and-after tells the story. The boom side: a feed-in tariff attracts investors, solar farms cluster in the sunny South, and gigawatts get added in roughly 18 months. The strain side: transmission lines lag behind, those lines overload at the midday solar peak, and the grid operator is forced to *curtail* — to order solar farms to switch off output it cannot carry. Curtailment is the bitter irony of a fast transition: clean power, already paid for, deliberately wasted because the wire to move it doesn't exist.

There's a second-order grid problem too, which any high-renewables system eventually hits. Solar produces a giant slug of power in the middle of the day and nothing after sunset — just as the evening demand peak arrives. So you get a "duck curve": the net demand the rest of the fleet must cover dips deep at midday (when solar floods in) and then ramps up violently in the early evening (when solar drops to zero and people get home and turn on the air conditioning). Managing that ramp needs flexible plants — gas peakers, hydro, storage — and a grid that can shuffle power around fast. Vietnam's grid, built for steady coal-and-hydro baseload, was not designed for it.

The duck curve creates a brutal operational dilemma. The fleet has to ramp from its midday trough up to the evening peak in a couple of hours — a steep climb that coal plants, which prefer to run flat, handle poorly. Coal units don't like being cycled hard: ramping them up and down quickly wears the boilers, burns fuel inefficiently, and takes time the evening ramp doesn't allow. So a system with lots of solar but little flexible capacity ends up keeping coal plants running *through the midday solar peak* anyway — at low, inefficient output — just so they're warm and ready for the evening, which is part of why solar gets curtailed instead of coal: you can't switch the coal off and back on fast enough, so you switch the solar off instead. The clean megawatt-hour loses to the dirty one not on cost but on *flexibility*.

The structural fix is **storage** — batteries (and pumped hydro, where geography allows) that soak up the cheap midday solar surplus and release it into the evening ramp. Storage is what lets a high-solar grid actually *use* its midday flood instead of curtailing it, and it's the piece Vietnam (like most fast-transitioning systems) is only beginning to build at scale. Until the batteries and the transmission catch up, the solar fleet's real-world contribution is capped not by how much sun falls on the panels but by how much of that energy the grid can carry and time-shift. The panel was the cheap, fast part; the storage and the wires are the slow, expensive part — and they are the part that actually decides how green the system becomes.

#### Worked example: the wasted value of curtailed solar

Curtailment isn't just inefficient — it's money set on fire. Let's price it.

Suppose a region has **2,000 MW** of solar capacity and, at the midday peak on a sunny day, the grid can only accept 80% of it, curtailing **20% = 400 MW** for the ~4 peak hours:

- 400 MW × 4 hours = **1,600 MWh** of clean electricity wasted that day.

Value it at the retail tariff (~8.6 US cents/kWh = \$86/MWh):

- 1,600 MWh × \$86 = **\$137,600 of power wasted in a single afternoon**, in one region.

Over a year of sunny days, that compounds into tens of millions of dollars of clean energy thrown away — energy that, if it had a wire to travel on, could have displaced imported coal and shrunk that dollar import bill. The lesson is the one every energy transition learns the hard way: **megawatts of generation are useless without the megawatts of transmission to carry them**, and the grid, not the panel, is usually the binding constraint.

## Common misconceptions

**"Vietnam is an energy exporter — it has oil and coal."** It *was*, and it still produces both, but it crossed into net energy *importer* status years ago. Domestic crude output from aging offshore fields is declining, domestic gas is falling (driving new LNG import terminals), and thermal-coal imports run **50+ million tonnes a year**. The export-era framing is a decade out of date.

**"Cheap electricity means cheap to produce."** No — it means cheaply *priced*. Vietnam's retail tariff (~8.6 US cents/kWh) is held low by state policy, but the *cost* of the coal-fired marginal kilowatt-hour can exceed that price when global coal spikes. The cheapness is a subsidy borne by EVN's balance sheet, not a reflection of low production cost.

**"The 2023 blackouts were just a freak heatwave."** The heatwave was the trigger, not the cause. The cause was structural: a coal-and-hydro-heavy northern grid with thin reserve margins, a regional transmission bottleneck that limits help from the South, and a chronic under-investment in capacity relative to ~9% demand growth. Remove the structure and the heatwave is survivable; the heatwave merely exposed it.

**"Adding lots of solar fixed the supply problem."** Solar helped on sunny middays in the South — but it produced nothing during the *evening* peak, did little for the *North* (where the blackouts were), and added so fast that transmission couldn't carry it, forcing curtailment. New generation that the grid can't deliver where and when it's needed doesn't end blackouts. Wires and timing matter as much as gigawatts.

**"EVN loses money because it's badly run."** EVN has real inefficiencies, like any sprawling state monopoly. But its 2022 loss was overwhelmingly a *fuel-cost-versus-frozen-tariff* problem — dollar-priced coal tripling while a dong retail price stayed capped for inflation reasons. Any utility on earth with that input/output mismatch would have lost money. It's a structural squeeze, not a management scandal.

## How it shows up in real markets

**The 2022 fuel shock and EVN's record loss.** When Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine scrambled global energy, thermal coal (Newcastle) spiked from the \$100s toward ~\$400 a tonne, while EVN's retail tariff was frozen. The result was a reported loss on the order of \$1 billion-plus for 2022 — a clean, real-world demonstration of the dollar-cost/dong-price squeeze. The eventual policy response was a pair of tariff increases in 2023 (a 3% rise in May, another ~4.5% in November), the first hikes in four years — telling, because they came *after* the loss, confirming how slow and politically gated the passthrough is.

**The May-June 2023 northern blackouts.** The convergence of El Nino drought (hydro at dead-water levels), a 40°C+ heatwave (record AC demand), and strained coal logistics produced rolling cuts across the northern industrial belt. The economic damage to manufacturers was estimated around \$1.4 billion, and it directly shaped Vietnam's subsequent rush to approve new transmission (notably the 500kV "circuit 3" line to move power north) and to firm up coal-import contracts. It was the moment "power reliability" became an explicit FDI risk in boardroom conversations.

**The post-FIT solar curtailment.** After the 2019-2020 solar boom, southern solant farms faced repeated curtailment orders as transmission lagged — a live example of generation outpacing the grid. The policy whiplash that followed (the expiry of the generous FIT, disputes over connection, a freeze on new approvals while the grid caught up) is the cautionary tale every fast-transitioning economy studies: subsidise generation without simultaneously building transmission, and you create stranded clean power.

**The structural import-bill trend.** Year after year, the coal-import line grinds higher (toward 55 million tonnes), embedding a multi-billion-dollar dollar-denominated bill into Vietnam's trade and FX accounts. It's a slow-moving but relentless macro pressure — a developing-economy energy bill that rises with every percentage point of GDP growth, denominated in a currency the country must earn through exports. This is why energy security and the trade balance are now discussed in the same breath in Hanoi.

**The hydro-drought swing factor.** Hydro's ~28% share is the system's free fuel — and its biggest year-to-year wildcard. In a wet year, full reservoirs let hydro run hard, displace expensive coal, and shrink the import bill; in a dry year (an El Nino), hydro collapses and the system must burn more coal to compensate, *raising* imports exactly when global coal may also be expensive. So Vietnam's coal-import bill isn't a smooth line — it's hydro's mirror image, jumping in drought years and easing in flood years. The 2023 surge in coal imports back toward 51 million tonnes was partly this: the drought that drained the dams forced more coal through the boilers.

#### Worked example: how a dry year inflates the coal bill

Quantify the hydro-coal seesaw, because it's the swing that makes the import bill jump.

Suppose a drought cuts hydro generation by **20 TWh** versus a normal year (a plausible northern shortfall). That power has to come from somewhere, and at the margin it comes from coal. Converting 20 TWh = 20,000,000 MWh of replacement coal generation at 0.45 tonnes of coal per MWh:

- 20,000,000 MWh × 0.45 t/MWh = **9 million extra tonnes of coal** needed.

If much of that is imported at **\$120 a tonne**:

- 9,000,000 t × \$120 = **\$1.08 billion of extra coal imports** — caused purely by a dry rainy season, before any change in the coal price itself.

And the cruelty compounds: droughts (El Nino years) often coincide across the region, lifting Asian power demand and coal prices simultaneously, so the dry year tends to be the *expensive* coal year too. The lesson: in a hydro-and-coal grid, the weather isn't just a reliability risk — it's a billion-dollar swing in the dollar import bill, and it swings the wrong way at the worst time.

## The takeaway: reading Vietnam's energy story

Here is how to think about all of this as an investor or an analyst watching Vietnam.

**Power reliability is now a first-order macro and FDI variable, not a utility footnote.** The single most important shift since 2023 is that the reliability of the grid is priced into the investment case for the entire export-manufacturing model. Watch reserve margins (how much spare capacity sits above peak demand), reservoir levels heading into the dry season, and the pace of transmission build-out — especially the North-South interconnection. A thin reserve margin plus a forecast El Nino is a flashing warning light for the following summer.

**The EVN tariff is the political pressure valve — watch it.** Because retail power is a social-and-inflation lever, tariff increases are slow, gated, and politically costly, which means EVN's balance sheet absorbs fuel shocks first. When you see global coal spike, expect an EVN loss *before* you see a tariff hike, and expect the hike to lag by quarters. For anyone analysing Vietnam's listed power and utility names, that regulated-margin dynamic is the core risk — and it's the through-line of the [Vietnam utilities sector](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/utilities-power-water-gas-sector-vietnam-defensive-cash) story.

**The coal-import bill is a dollar liability bolted onto a dong economy.** Every point of demand growth, absent a faster transition, adds dollar-priced coal to the import bill — and that bill swells further whenever the dong weakens. It is a structural drain on the trade balance that links Vietnam's growth directly to global coal prices and the dollar. That's the same dollar-commodity mechanism we unpack in [why a strong dollar weighs on commodity prices](/blog/trading/commodities/the-dollar-and-commodities-why-a-strong-dollar-weighs-on-prices) — here it lands on a national power bill.

**The transition is real but grid-bound.** Solar and wind grew explosively and will keep growing, but the binding constraint has shifted from *generation* to *transmission and flexibility*. The next decade of Vietnam's energy story is less about how many panels get installed and more about whether the wires, the storage, and the flexible plants get built to use them — and whether gas (increasingly imported LNG) can bridge the gap as domestic fields decline.

**What to actually watch, as a checklist.** If you're tracking Vietnam's energy and macro story, four indicators carry most of the signal. *Reservoir levels* heading into the March-June dry season tell you whether hydro will hold or collapse — low reservoirs plus an El Nino forecast is a blackout-risk warning for the coming summer. *The coal-import run-rate* (monthly customs tonnage) tells you how fast the dollar bill is growing and how hard the system is leaning on imports. *EVN's reported results and any tariff-hike announcements* tell you where the squeeze stands and whether the state is finally letting price catch up to cost — watch for hikes *lagging* fuel spikes by quarters. And *transmission project milestones* (the 500kV circuit-3 line, North-South capacity) tell you whether the grid is becoming one national resource or staying a set of regional islands. Those four — water, tonnage, tariff, wire — are the dashboard for the entire story.

Step back to the series' spine and the picture is clean. A commodity price is a physical thing forced through a financial contract — and in Vietnam, the physical thing is a tonne of coal off a ship from Newcastle, the financial contract is a dollar price EVN must pay, and the twist is that the electricity it makes is sold back through a *second* contract: a dong tariff the state controls. The whole drama of Vietnamese energy lives in the space between those two contracts, in two different currencies, set by two different forces. Keep the lights on, keep the factories running, keep inflation down, keep EVN solvent — you cannot do all four at once when global coal triples. Something has to give, and what gives tells you exactly how a fast-growing economy is choosing to bear the cost of its own success.
