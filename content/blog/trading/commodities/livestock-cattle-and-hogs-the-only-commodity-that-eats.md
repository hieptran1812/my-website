> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Livestock is the only commodity that eats another commodity to exist, that you cannot put in a warehouse, and that is governed by a multi-year biological clock instead of the cost of carry. A trader who reads cattle and hogs as a *feed-cost margin* plus a *herd cycle* understands them; a trader who reads them like oil does not.
>
> - A feedlot is a **converter**: it buys a young feeder calf plus corn and soybean meal, feeds it to slaughter weight, and sells a fat animal. Its profit is the **feeding margin** — fat-cattle value minus the cost of the feeder and the feed. This is the cattle version of the soybean crush.
> - Livestock is **unstorable on the hoof**: a finished 1,400 lb steer must be sold on a biological deadline. You cannot warehouse beef on the hoof the way you warehouse copper or crude, so the forward curve is driven by biology and seasonality, not storage cost.
> - The **cattle cycle runs about ten years**. High prices make ranchers *retain heifers* to breed, which paradoxically tightens supply first, then floods it years later, crashing prices and triggering liquidation. Hogs run a faster, roughly four-year version.
> - The one fact to remember: a **drought first floods the beef market, then starves it**. Forced liquidation dumps cattle now (prices down), then leaves too few breeding cows to make calves later (prices up two to three years out). Read the herd, not the headline.

In the autumn of 2014, the price of a finished steer in the United States hit a record that no rancher had ever seen. Live cattle futures on the CME traded near \$1.70 per hundredweight-equivalent levels that, on a per-animal basis, meant a 1,400-pound steer was worth well over two thousand dollars at the feedlot gate. Ranchers who had spent years losing money were suddenly, briefly, rich. And here is the part that confused everyone outside the business: the record-high price was caused not by booming demand but by a *shortage that a drought had created two and three years earlier*. The 2011-2012 drought across Texas and the southern Plains had burned the pastures to dust, and ranchers had been forced to sell off their breeding cows because they could not feed them. That sell-off flooded the beef market in 2011 and 2012 and pushed prices down at the time. But it also meant that, three years later, there were simply not enough cattle left alive to slaughter. The herd had shrunk to its smallest size since 1951. The shortage that drove the 2014 record had been *planted* by the drought of 2011.

That single fact — that a flood of supply today guarantees a famine of supply in three years — is the thing that makes livestock the strangest market in the entire commodity complex. Every other commodity you can trade is, at bottom, a storage-and-carry story: you can put the barrel in a tank, the copper in a warehouse, the corn in a silo, and the shape of the forward curve falls out of what it costs to store the thing until later. (If that sentence is unfamiliar, the [forward curve post](/blog/trading/commodities/the-forward-curve-the-most-important-chart-in-commodities) and the [contango-vs-backwardation post](/blog/trading/commodities/contango-vs-backwardation-what-the-shape-of-the-curve-means) build it from zero.) Livestock breaks that model in two ways at once. First, you cannot store a finished animal: a steer at slaughter weight must be sold within days, or it costs you money to keep feeding it and it eventually dies. Second — and this is the part that has no parallel anywhere else — a steer *eats another commodity to exist*. It is a corn-and-soybean-meal machine wearing a hide. Its entire economic life is a bet that the value of the meat it becomes will exceed the cost of the feed it ate.

This post explains that strange market from zero. By the end you will understand the three live contracts traders actually use — live cattle, feeder cattle, and lean hogs — and how they fit together; you will be able to compute a feedlot's margin per head like an operator; you will know why the cattle cycle takes a decade and why high prices reliably precede a glut; you will understand why a drought is a two-stage price event; and you will be able to read livestock the way a livestock trader reads it: as a feed-cost margin sitting on top of a slow biological clock.

## Foundations: what livestock actually is as a market

Start with the physical reality, because every financial quirk in this market is downstream of biology.

A beef animal's life runs through three stages, and each stage has its own market. A calf is born on a **cow-calf operation** — a ranch with breeding cows that produce one calf each per year. The calf nurses and grazes on grass for six to ten months until it weighs roughly 500 to 800 pounds. At that point it is called a **feeder** — an animal ready to be "fed up" to slaughter weight but not yet there. The feeder is sold (this is the first market) to a **feedlot**, a large operation that does nothing but feed cattle a high-energy ration of corn and protein for about 150 days, packing on weight fast until the animal hits roughly 1,300 to 1,500 pounds. At that point it is a **finished** or **fat** animal, ready for slaughter, and it is sold (this is the second market) to a **packer** — a meatpacking plant that slaughters it and breaks it into boxed beef. So the chain is: cow-calf → feeder → feedlot → fat cattle → packer → beef. Three physical animals, three prices.

The futures market gives you contracts on two points in that chain, plus hogs:

- **Live cattle** (CME symbol LE) is the *finished* animal — the fat steer ready for slaughter. One contract is 40,000 pounds of live animal, quoted in **US cents per pound**, which the trade states as dollars per **hundredweight** (cwt), where one hundredweight is 100 pounds. A live cattle price of "185" means \$185 per cwt, or \$1.85 per pound of live animal. A 1,400 lb steer at \$185/cwt is worth 14 × \$185 = \$2,590.
- **Feeder cattle** (CME symbol GF) is the *young* animal going *into* the feedlot — the 700 to 800 lb calf. One contract is 50,000 pounds, also quoted in dollars per cwt. Feeders trade at a *higher* price per pound than fat cattle, because a pound of young animal with all its growth ahead of it is worth more than a pound of finished animal. This price relationship is the heart of the feedlot's margin, and we will return to it.
- **Lean hogs** (CME symbol HE) is the finished hog, quoted in dollars per cwt of *carcass* (lean) weight. One contract is 40,000 pounds. Hogs run a faster biological cycle than cattle, which gives them a different and shorter price rhythm.

Two definitions you must hold before anything else makes sense.

**Hundredweight (cwt).** This is just the livestock world's pricing unit: 100 pounds. Prices are quoted per cwt because animals are big and a per-pound price would be a cluttered decimal. \$185/cwt = \$1.85/lb. To get the value of a whole animal, multiply (animal weight in lb ÷ 100) × price per cwt. A 1,400 lb steer at \$185/cwt → 14 cwt × \$185 = \$2,590.

**The feed conversion ratio.** This is the most important biological number in the whole market: how many pounds of feed an animal must eat to put on one pound of body weight. For cattle in a feedlot, it is roughly **6 pounds of feed per pound of gain**. For hogs, it is much more efficient — roughly **3 pounds of feed per pound of gain** — which is one reason pork is cheaper than beef and why the hog cycle is faster. This single ratio is what makes livestock "the commodity that eats": the animal is literally a machine that converts cheap feed (corn, soybean meal) into expensive protein (beef, pork), and the conversion ratio tells you how much feed each pound of output costs.

### Why a young animal costs more per pound than a finished one

One feature of livestock pricing trips up every newcomer, so it is worth nailing down before the margin math: a **feeder** (the young 750 lb animal) trades at a *higher* price per pound than a **fat** animal (the finished 1,400 lb steer), even though the fat animal is the one ready to be eaten. The 2014 numbers make it concrete — feeders above \$240/cwt while fat cattle sat near \$170/cwt. That looks backwards until you see what you are actually buying. When you buy a feeder, you are buying a pound of animal *with all of its growth still ahead of it*. Every one of those 750 pounds is going to be carried up to 1,400, and the cheap, fast weight gain in the feedlot is where the value is created. When you buy a fat animal, you are buying a pound that has already done its growing; there is no cheap gain left in it. So the market pays a *premium per pound for youth* and a discount per pound for finish. The feeder price embeds the option to put cheap pounds on a cheap frame.

This per-pound inversion is precisely why the feeding margin is a spread and not a level. The feedlot pays the high feeder price for 750 lb, adds 650 lb of the cheapest pounds it can (corn-fed weight gain), and sells the blended 1,400 lb at the lower fat-cattle price. It is *arbitraging the difference between the price of a young pound and the price of a finished pound*, financed by feed. When feed is cheap, those added pounds are cheap and the arbitrage is fat; when corn spikes, the added pounds cost more than the fat-cattle price pays for them, and the arbitrage inverts into a loss — exactly the squeeze the worked examples below trace. The relationship between the feeder price, the corn price, and the fat-cattle price *is* the feedlot's profit-and-loss, and it is why feeder cattle, live cattle, and corn are quoted and traded as a linked set.

There is also a **basis** wrinkle worth flagging, because it is where a lot of real livestock money is made or lost. Basis is the difference between the local cash price a feedlot actually receives for its animals and the nearby futures price. For an unstorable commodity, basis is not a storage-cost story (as it is for grain or oil); it is a *delivery-window and local-supply* story — how many cattle are finishing in your region in the exact week you need to sell, and how hungry the nearby packing plant is for them. A feedlot that hedges by selling live-cattle futures still bears basis risk: it locked the futures price, but the cash price it gets can run a few dollars per cwt above or below futures depending on local conditions that week. Because the animal cannot wait, the feedlot cannot "hold for a better basis" the way a grain elevator holds corn for a better basis — it must sell into whatever local market exists when the animal finishes. Unstorability turns basis from a manageable carry into a hard, time-boxed risk.

### The feedlot is a converter, not a speculator

Hold one commercial picture before the numbers: a feedlot is the livestock world's version of a refinery or a flour mill. (The [refining and crack-spreads post](/blog/trading/commodities/refining-and-crack-spreads-turning-crude-into-products) makes exactly this point about oil.) A refinery buys cheap crude and sells more-valuable products; its profit is the *crack spread*. A soybean processor buys beans and sells meal and oil; its profit is the *crush* — see the within-series [crush post](/blog/trading/commodities/the-crush-and-the-spread-soybeans-into-meal-and-oil). A feedlot buys cheap feeders plus cheap feed and sells more-valuable fat cattle; its profit is the **feeding margin**, sometimes called the **cattle crush**. In every case the business is a *spread*, not a *level*. The feedlot does not get rich because cattle prices go up; it gets rich because the gap between what it sells the fat animal for and what it paid for the feeder and the feed is wide.

This is exactly what the cover figure above shows: two inputs (the feeder animal, and the feed) flow into the feedlot, and one more-valuable output (the fat animal) flows out, with the margin as the difference. Keep that machine in your head — every section of this post is a different view of that one machine.

The feedlot's two big costs are therefore the **feeder** (the animal it buys) and the **feed** (the corn and soybean meal it pours into that animal for 150 days). Feed is the input the rest of the commodity complex actually cares about, because it links livestock straight back to the grain market. So let us look at what feed actually costs.

![Corn and soybean annual average prices from 2005 to 2025 showing the 2012 and 2022 feed cost spikes](/imgs/blogs/livestock-cattle-and-hogs-the-only-commodity-that-eats-2.png)

The chart above shows the two feed inputs over two decades. Corn (the energy ration, on the left axis) and soybeans (which the feedlot buys as soybean *meal*, the protein ration, tracked on the right axis) move together because they compete for the same Midwest acres and respond to the same droughts. Notice the two violent spikes: the 2012 Midwest drought drove corn to nearly \$7 a bushel, and the 2021-2022 post-pandemic and Ukraine-war period did it again. Each of those spikes was a body blow to feedlot margins, because feed is the single largest variable cost of producing a pound of beef. When you see corn spike on that chart, you should immediately think: *feedlot margins just got squeezed, and the herd is about to start shrinking.* That causal chain — feed cost up → feeding margin down → fewer cattle placed → tighter beef supply later → cattle prices up — is the spine of the entire market. The grain market and the cattle market are joined at the feed bunk. (The growing-season mechanics behind those corn spikes live in the within-series [grains post](/blog/trading/commodities/grains-corn-wheat-and-soybeans-the-calories-that-trade).)

#### Worked example: a feedlot's margin per head

Let us run an actual feedlot's economics, because the margin is the whole game and you should be able to compute it.

The feedlot buys a **750 lb feeder** at a feeder-cattle price of **\$250/cwt**. Cost of the animal: 7.5 cwt × \$250 = **\$1,875**.

It feeds that animal for about 150 days to a finished weight of **1,400 lb**. That is 1,400 − 750 = **650 lb of gain**. At a feed conversion ratio of roughly 6 lb of feed per lb of gain, the animal eats about 650 × 6 = **3,900 lb of feed**, the bulk of it corn. Roughly 50 to 55 bushels of corn (a bushel of corn weighs 56 lb) plus soybean meal and roughage. Suppose the total feed bill works out to **\$1.00 per pound of gain** in a normal-corn year, so 650 lb × \$1.00 = **\$650 of feed**. Add about **\$150** of yardage, vet, interest, and death loss. Total cost to produce the finished animal: \$1,875 feeder + \$650 feed + \$150 other = **\$2,675**.

Now it sells the **1,400 lb fat steer** at a live-cattle price of **\$190/cwt**: 14 cwt × \$190 = **\$2,660**.

Margin per head: \$2,660 − \$2,675 = **−\$15**. The feedlot *lost fifteen dollars on that animal.*

That negative number is not a mistake — it is the normal state of the feeding business. Feedlots routinely operate at a loss per head and make their money on volume, basis, and timing, the same way a refinery survives on a thin per-barrel crack. The entire point of the cattle crush is that this margin is *small and often negative*, which is why feed costs and feeder prices matter so enormously: a \$0.20 move in corn or a \$10 move in the feeder price is the difference between a \$50 profit and a \$50 loss per head. **A feedlot does not bet on the price of cattle; it bets on the spread between fat cattle and the feeders-plus-feed it took to make them — and that spread is usually razor-thin.**

### How a feedlot locks the margin: the cattle crush

Because the margin is thin and floats with three different prices (feeder, feed, fat), a feedlot can hedge it the way a refiner buys the crack or a soybean processor puts on the crush. The feedlot is naturally *long fat cattle* (it will sell them) and *short feeders and corn* (it will buy them). To lock the margin the day it places the cattle, it does the reverse in futures: **buy feeder-cattle and corn futures** (locking the input cost) and **sell live-cattle futures** (locking the output price). Now the *spread* — the feeding margin — is fixed regardless of where flat prices go. If cattle rally, the gain on its physical animals is offset by a loss on its short live-cattle futures; if cattle crash, the loss on the animals is offset by the futures gain. This is the **cattle crush** as a trade, and it is the direct analogue of the soybean crush and the refining crack. The feedlot has converted an unstorable, biological, feed-eating asset into a locked processing margin.

#### Worked example: the feed-cost squeeze when corn jumps

Take the same feedlot, but now a drought spikes corn from a normal level to a 2012-style level mid-feed. Feed cost per pound of gain rises from \$1.00 to **\$1.40**. The feed bill on 650 lb of gain goes from \$650 to 650 × \$1.40 = **\$910** — an extra **\$260 per head** out of nowhere. With the feeder cost (\$1,875) and other costs (\$150) unchanged, total cost rises to \$1,875 + \$910 + \$150 = **\$2,935**. Selling the fat steer at the same \$190/cwt (\$2,660), the margin is now \$2,660 − \$2,935 = **−\$275 per head.**

A feedlot running 50,000 head a year just saw its annual margin fall by \$260 × 50,000 = **\$13 million** from the corn move alone. Its response is rational and immediate: it bids *less* for the next round of feeders (it cannot pay up for the input when the feed bill is crushing it), so the feeder price falls, and it *places fewer cattle*. Fewer cattle placed today means fewer fat cattle in five to six months, which tightens beef supply and eventually pushes fat-cattle prices up. **A corn spike does not raise beef prices immediately — it lowers feeder prices now and raises fat-cattle prices half a year later, by shrinking the number of animals on feed.** This lag is why livestock is a timing market.

### The packer's margin and the beef cutout

There is one more processing margin in the chain, and it matters because it is where the live animal turns into the meat you actually buy — and because it dislocates spectacularly in a crisis. The **packer** buys the fat animal from the feedlot and slaughters it, selling the result as **boxed beef**: the primal and sub-primal cuts (ribeye, chuck, round, brisket) packed in boxes and shipped to retailers and restaurants. The wholesale value of all the cuts from one animal, expressed per hundredweight of carcass, is the **beef cutout** value. The packer's margin — the **gross packer margin** or "spread" — is the cutout value minus the cost of the live animal minus the cost of slaughtering and fabricating it. The packer is long boxed beef and short live cattle, exactly as the feedlot is long fat cattle and short feeders-and-feed. The chain is a series of nested processing margins, each one a long-output/short-input spread.

The packer's margin is normally thin and stable, but it is the link in the chain with the *least* slack, because slaughter capacity is fixed and hard to add — there are only so many plants, and they run near capacity. That rigidity is exactly what blew up in 2020. When packing plants shut on COVID outbreaks, the bottleneck was not demand and not the herd; it was the *throat* of the chain. Fat cattle backed up on farms with nowhere to be slaughtered, so live-cattle prices *fell*. At the same time, almost no boxed beef was reaching grocery stores, so the cutout value — and retail beef prices — *spiked*. The packer spread, normally a modest figure, exploded to historic widths because the same animals were worth little alive (nowhere to process them) and a fortune as boxed beef (none available). That dislocation between the live animal and the meat is impossible in a storable commodity: you cannot have cheap copper at the mine and expensive copper at the warehouse for long, because you can move and store copper. You cannot move or store a live animal through a closed plant, so the price of the same beef at two points in the chain came completely unglued. The packer margin is the chain's pressure gauge, and in 2020 it pinned.

#### Worked example: the packer cutout spread

Take a 1,400 lb fat steer bought by the packer at **\$190/cwt**, so 14 cwt × \$190 = **\$2,660** for the live animal. After slaughter it yields a carcass of roughly **63%** of live weight, or about **880 lb of carcass**. Suppose the boxed-beef cutout is valued at **\$310/cwt of carcass**: 8.8 cwt × \$310 = **\$2,728** of boxed beef, plus another **\$220** in drop credits (hide, offal, tallow — the parts sold separately), for **\$2,948** of total revenue. Subtract the \$2,660 live-animal cost and about **\$230** of slaughter-and-fabrication cost, and the packer margin is \$2,948 − \$2,660 − \$230 = **+\$58 per head** — a normal, thin spread. Now run the 2020 dislocation: the plant is closed, so the live-animal cost the packer *could* pay collapses to \$150/cwt (\$2,100) for the few it processes, while the cutout, with stores empty, jumps to \$450/cwt (8.8 × \$450 = \$3,960). The spread balloons to \$3,960 + \$220 − \$2,100 − \$230 = **+\$1,850 per head**. **In a normal market the packer earns a thin, stable spread; in a processing-bottleneck crisis that spread can widen thirty-fold, because an unstorable animal cannot route around a closed plant.**

## The cattle cycle: a ten-year biological clock

Now the single most important idea in livestock, and the one that has no analogue in oil, copper, or even grains: the **cattle cycle**. Beef supply does not respond to price the way a factory responds to demand. It responds on a *biological delay measured in years*, and that delay creates a slow, self-reinforcing boom-bust cycle that has run with remarkable regularity for over a century — roughly a **ten-year** period from peak to peak.

Here is the mechanism, and it is genuinely counterintuitive, so go slowly.

![Six-phase strip of the cattle cycle from high prices through retain heifers herd builds expansion glut to liquidation](/imgs/blogs/livestock-cattle-and-hogs-the-only-commodity-that-eats-3.png)

The phase strip above lays out the full loop. Start at **high prices** (phase 1). Cattle are scarce, margins are fat, and every rancher wants to expand the herd to cash in. To expand a herd you need more breeding cows. Where do breeding cows come from? They come from **heifers** — young females. And here is the twist: to grow the herd, a rancher must *keep* his heifers to breed them instead of *selling* them for slaughter. This is called **heifer retention** (phase 2). But every heifer kept back to breed is a heifer *not* sent to the packer. So the very act of expanding the herd *removes animals from the slaughter supply in the near term*. The herd-building decision, made because prices are high, makes the near-term shortage *worse*, which drives prices *higher still*. This is the paradox at the heart of the cattle cycle: **the response to high prices tightens supply before it loosens it.**

A retained heifer takes about two years to produce her first calf, and that calf takes another year and a half to reach slaughter weight. So from the decision to expand to the first extra beef hitting the market is a **three-to-four-year lag** (phases 3 and 4, herd builds and expansion). During those years the herd is growing on paper, but the *beef supply* is still tight or even tighter, because cows and heifers are being held back to breed rather than slaughtered. Prices stay high, which encourages *even more* retention. The system overshoots.

Eventually — three or four years in — all those retained heifers have calved, and their calves have calved, and the now-much-larger herd starts sending a flood of finished cattle to the packers. Beef supply finally surges. Prices fall (phase 5, **glut**). They fall below the cost of production, and now feeding cattle loses money. Ranchers respond to low prices the mirror-image way: they stop retaining heifers and start **liquidating** — culling cows, selling everything, getting out (phase 6). Liquidation dumps even *more* beef onto an already-glutted market in the near term, driving prices *lower still* — the same paradox running in reverse. But by selling off the breeding cows, ranchers are destroying next year's calf crop. Three years later the herd is too small, beef is scarce, prices spike, and the whole ten-year cycle begins again.

The cattle cycle is, at root, a control system with a multi-year delay in the feedback loop — and any control engineer will tell you that a system with a long lag between action and effect *oscillates*. That is exactly what cattle prices do. The phase strip above captures the whole loop: the oscillation is the claim, and the long lags between each phase are why it is slow.

#### Worked example: the herd-cycle timing trade

Suppose you are a trader in year 1 of a cycle. Cattle prices are at a record high — say live cattle at **\$190/cwt** — because the herd is at a multi-decade low after a drought-driven liquidation. The financial press is euphoric: "beef demand soaring, cattle prices to keep climbing." A naive trader buys live-cattle futures expecting the trend to continue.

You read the *herd*, not the headline. You know that high prices are right now triggering massive heifer retention. You pull the USDA's semi-annual **Cattle inventory report** and confirm it: the number of beef heifers being held for breeding has jumped 5% year-over-year. You reason: that retention is removing animals from slaughter today (so prices may stay high or rise for another year or two — the paradox), but it is building a herd that will flood the market in roughly three years. So you do *not* chase the front-month rally. Instead you look at the **deferred** live-cattle contracts two to three years out, which the curve is pricing only modestly below spot, and you *sell* them — betting that by the time those contracts expire, the retained heifers will have flooded the market and prices will be far lower.

Three years later the herd has expanded, beef supply surges, and live cattle falls to **\$150/cwt**. Your short deferred contract, sold near \$185, is now worth \$150 — a **\$35/cwt** gain, or 35 × 400 (a contract is 400 cwt) = **\$14,000 per contract**. **The high price did not predict more high prices; it predicted the glut that ends them, because high prices set off the biology that creates the glut.** Reading the cattle cycle is reading that lag.

## Why a drought floods, then starves, the beef supply

The drought story I opened with is worth its own section, because it is the cleanest illustration of how livestock's unstorability and biology combine to make it a two-stage price event — and because droughts are the single most important supply shock in this market.

![Before and after showing a drought floods beef now by forced liquidation then starves it later with a smaller herd](/imgs/blogs/livestock-cattle-and-hogs-the-only-commodity-that-eats-4.png)

The before-and-after above tells the whole story. Cattle, unlike grain, mostly live on grass for the first part of their lives. Grass needs rain. When a drought hits the Plains, the pasture dries up and there is simply nothing for the cows to eat. A rancher facing a pasture with no grass and no cheap hay has an ugly choice: buy expensive feed to keep the cows alive at a loss, or sell the cows now. Most sell. This is **forced liquidation** (the left panel). Crucially, they sell not just the animals headed for slaughter anyway, but the *breeding cows* — the productive heart of the operation — and even the young heifers they would otherwise have kept. All those animals hit the market at once.

The near-term effect is a **flood**. Slaughter volumes spike, beef supply jumps, and prices *fall* — counterintuitively, a drought makes beef *cheaper* in the first year. This is the opposite of what most people expect from a "supply shock," and it traps traders who reason "drought = shortage = buy." In the drought year, drought means *glut*.

But look at what the liquidation did: it killed the breeding cows. Fewer breeding cows means fewer calves born next year and the year after. The pipeline that produces beef has been gutted (the right panel). So two to three years later, when those missing calves *would* have been reaching slaughter weight, they simply do not exist. Beef supply collapses to a multi-decade low, and prices spike to records — exactly what happened in 2014, three years after the 2011-2012 drought. And because rebuilding a herd requires *retaining* heifers (which removes them from slaughter), the recovery is slow and the high prices persist for years. The biological clock sets the recovery speed, and you cannot rush it. **A drought is a price event you trade twice: short the flood now, then position for the famine in two to three years.** No storable commodity behaves this way, because no storable commodity destroys its own future production capacity when it is dumped on the market.

## Lean hogs: the same logic, a faster clock

Hogs run the same feed-margin-plus-biology playbook as cattle, but the biology is faster, so the cycle is shorter and sharper. Understanding the difference sharpens your grasp of both.

A sow (a breeding female pig) has a gestation of under four months and produces *litters* of ten or more piglets twice a year, versus a cow's single calf per year after a nine-month gestation. A hog reaches slaughter weight (around 280 lb) in about six months from birth, versus 18-plus months for cattle. And the feed conversion ratio is far better — roughly 3 lb of feed per pound of gain versus 6 for cattle. Put those together and the hog supply can respond to price *years faster* than the cattle supply. The result is the **hog cycle** (historically called the "hog-corn cycle" or "pig cycle"), a roughly **three-to-four-year** boom-bust, against cattle's ten.

![Illustrative live cattle versus lean hog price paths showing the long cattle cycle and the short hog cycle](/imgs/blogs/livestock-cattle-and-hogs-the-only-commodity-that-eats-7.png)

The illustrative chart above shows the two clocks side by side (the shapes are illustrative — there is no livestock price series in this kit, so these paths are drawn to show *cycle length*, not exact prices). The cattle line traces one long, slow hump over a decade; the hog line oscillates two or three times in the same span. That difference in *frequency* is the whole point. A hog producer who sees high prices and expands can have extra pork on the market in a year, not three — so the hog glut arrives fast, and the bust arrives fast behind it. Hog traders live on a quarterly clock (the USDA **Hogs and Pigs report**, which counts the breeding herd and the pig crop); cattle traders live on a multi-year clock.

The hog market is also more tightly bound to corn than cattle, because hogs eat grain for *all* of their lives (they are not grass animals for a first phase the way cattle are). The old farmer's rule was the **hog-corn ratio**: how many bushels of corn one hundredweight of hog will buy. When the ratio is high (hogs expensive relative to corn), feeding hogs is profitable and producers expand; when it is low, they liquidate. It is the hog version of the feeding margin, expressed as a single ratio.

#### Worked example: a hog feeding margin

A hog producer buys a feeder pig at **40 lb** and feeds it to a market weight of **280 lb** — that is **240 lb of gain**. At a 3:1 feed conversion ratio, the pig eats 240 × 3 = **720 lb of feed**, almost all corn and soybean meal. In a normal-corn year, suppose feed costs **\$0.32 per pound of gain**, so feed cost = 240 × \$0.32 = **\$76.80**. Add the feeder-pig cost of about **\$45** and other costs of about **\$25**, for a total cost of roughly **\$147** per hog.

The producer sells the 280 lb hog at a lean-hog price of **\$90/cwt**. On a carcass-yield basis a 280 lb live hog yields roughly 210 lb of carcass weight, so 2.1 cwt × \$90 = **\$189**. Margin per hog: \$189 − \$147 = **+\$42**.

Now spike corn so feed cost per pound of gain jumps to **\$0.45**: feed cost = 240 × \$0.45 = **\$108**, total cost = \$45 + \$108 + \$25 = **\$178**, and the margin collapses to \$189 − \$178 = **+\$11**. A further corn spike, or a soft hog price, tips it negative and the producer starts liquidating sows. Because the hog can be brought to market in six months, that liquidation feeds through to the bacon counter within a year — far faster than cattle. **The hog is the most feed-sensitive, fastest-cycling animal on the board, which is why pork prices are the quickest livestock read on the corn market.**

## Why livestock barely correlates with the rest of the commodity complex

Here is a fact that surprises people building a commodity portfolio: live cattle and lean hogs have almost no correlation with crude oil, copper, or gold, and only a loose, lagged link even to the grains they eat. A broad commodity index like the S&P GSCI carries livestock at only about 7% weight, and a diversified one like Bloomberg's BCOM at around 5% — partly *because* livestock zigs when the rest of the complex zags, which makes it a genuine diversifier. Why is livestock so decoupled?

![Matrix comparing metals energy grains and livestock on storability and feed dependence](/imgs/blogs/livestock-cattle-and-hogs-the-only-commodity-that-eats-6.png)

The matrix above isolates the two axes that set livestock apart. The rest of the commodity complex moves on a shared set of macro drivers: the dollar, global growth, real interest rates, the cost of storage and carry. Copper and oil are *industrial* assets whose demand rises and falls with the world economy (the [copper post](/blog/trading/commodities/copper-doctor-copper-and-the-pulse-of-the-global-economy) and the cross-asset [energy post](/blog/trading/cross-asset/energy-oil-gas-the-inflation-engine) develop that). Gold is a *monetary* asset driven by real yields. All of them are storable, so their forward curves and their behavior are governed by the cost of carry — the storage-and-convenience-yield machinery this series is built around (see [convenience yield and the cost of carry](/blog/trading/commodities/convenience-yield-and-the-cost-of-carry-why-the-curve-has-a-shape)).

Livestock obeys none of that. Its price is set by two things the macro cycle barely touches: **the feed-cost margin** and **the herd's position in its biological cycle**. A recession that crushes copper demand has only a modest effect on how much beef Americans eat — meat demand is far more stable than industrial-metal demand. And the supply side is driven by where the herd is in its ten-year clock, which is set by *weather and biology three years ago*, not by today's macro. So you get years when oil and copper are crashing on a growth scare while cattle are ripping to records because the herd happens to be at a cyclical low. The bottom row of the matrix is the reason: livestock is the only commodity that *cannot be stored* and that *consumes another commodity to exist*, and those two properties together sever it from the carry-and-macro logic that ties the rest of the complex together. It marches to feed costs and biology.

#### Worked example: the diversification value of an uncorrelated leg

Suppose you run a commodity book that is long a basket of energy and metals, and in a given year a global growth scare sends crude down 25% and copper down 20%. Your book is bleeding. Now suppose that same year the cattle herd is at a cyclical low from a prior drought, and live cattle rallies 18% while the rest of the world worries about recession — because Americans keep eating roughly the same amount of beef regardless of the manufacturing PMI, and the supply is simply short. A 10% allocation to that livestock leg adds +1.8% to the book while the 90% in energy-and-metals subtracts heavily. The livestock leg did not "hedge" the macro in any designed sense; it was simply *marching to a different clock*. **In a commodity portfolio, livestock earns its place not by out-returning oil but by being uncorrelated to it — its returns come from a biological cycle and a feed margin that the global macro barely touches.** That is also why allocators treat the softs-and-livestock corner as its own bucket; the cross-asset [agriculture and softs post](/blog/trading/cross-asset/agriculture-softs-the-food-and-fiber-markets) places it in the broader food-and-fiber complex.

### What the forward curve means when you cannot store

It is worth pausing on what this unstorability does to the *shape* of the livestock forward curve, because it is genuinely different from everything else in the complex. For a storable commodity, the futures curve is anchored by arbitrage: if a distant contract trades far above spot, someone buys the physical, stores it, and sells the future, capturing the spread minus storage — and that arbitrage flattens the curve toward the cost of carry. (That carry mechanism is the whole subject of the [convenience-yield post](/blog/trading/commodities/convenience-yield-and-the-cost-of-carry-why-the-curve-has-a-shape).) For live cattle, that arbitrage *does not exist*. You cannot buy a fat steer today and "store" it to deliver against a contract six months out — it would be overweight, over-fat, and dead long before then. So the cattle curve is not a carry curve at all; it is the market's *forecast of the supply that biology will deliver* in each future month. A contract for next spring prices the cattle the market believes will be finishing next spring, given the placements happening in feedlots right now. The curve is a biological supply schedule wearing the clothes of a futures curve, and there is no storage arbitrage to discipline it — only the slow, physical ripening of animals already on feed. That is why a livestock trader studies *placement* and *inventory* data, not storage levels and carry: the only thing that connects today's price to next year's is how many animals are physically in the pipeline, and how fast they are growing.

### The global herd: a deeper layer of decoupling

There is also a geographic layer to livestock's decoupling that is easy to miss. The big beef exporters — Brazil, Australia, the United States, Argentina — each run their *own* herd cycle on their *own* weather, and those cycles are not synchronized. Australia's herd swings violently on its drought-and-flood cycle (a multi-year drought forces liquidation, then years of rain trigger a rebuild that withholds slaughter cattle), and that rebuild can flood the world market with cheap beef just as a US drought is starving it, or vice versa. Brazil, now the world's largest beef exporter, adds its own herd dynamics and the swing factor of Chinese import demand. So global beef prices are a *superposition* of several out-of-phase ten-year cycles plus trade-policy shocks (an export ban, a disease-driven import halt). This is a second reason livestock sits apart from the macro-driven commodities: even its own global supply is a patchwork of uncorrelated biological clocks, none of which is tied to the dollar or the global growth cycle the way an oil field or a copper mine is. For a trader, it means the relevant data is a stack of national herd reports, not a single global balance.

## Seasonality: the grilling-season rhythm

On top of the multi-year cattle cycle sits a within-year **seasonal** rhythm, driven by both demand and biology, and a trader has to separate the two clocks.

On the demand side, beef demand in the United States peaks in the **summer grilling season** — Memorial Day through Labor Day — when Americans fire up barbecues and demand for steaks and ground beef rises. Retailers and packers buy ahead of it, so wholesale beef and live-cattle prices tend to firm into late spring and early summer. There is a secondary bump around the winter holidays for prime cuts. Pork has its own seasonal demand quirks — ham demand around the holidays, bacon demand fairly steady — but the cleaner pork seasonal is on the *supply* side.

On the supply side, biology imposes a calendar. Calves are mostly born in spring (cows are bred so they calve when grass is greening), which means the supply of feeders coming off grass peaks in the **fall** — the autumn "run" of feeder cattle, when calves are weaned and sold into feedlots. That fall surge of feeder supply tends to pressure feeder prices seasonally. Hogs have a pronounced seasonal too: hog weights and slaughter numbers rise into the fall and winter (pigs grow better in cool weather and the spring pig crop reaches market weight then), so lean-hog prices are often seasonally weakest in the late fall — the "fall low" that hog traders watch for.

The practical point is that a livestock trader is always reading *two* clocks at once: the slow ten-year herd cycle that sets the *level*, and the within-year seasonal that sets the *timing of the swings around that level*. A price that looks high might be a cyclical high (sell it) or just a seasonal grilling-season bump on a cyclical low (a dip to buy). Confusing the two is one of the classic ways to lose money in this market.

## Common misconceptions

**"A drought means a beef shortage, so I should buy cattle."** Wrong on the timing, which is everything. In the *drought year*, forced liquidation *floods* the market and pushes prices *down*. The shortage and the price spike come two to three years *later*, after the gutted breeding herd fails to produce calves. In 2011-2012 the drought pushed cattle down; the record high came in 2014. Trade the flood first, then the famine. Get the lag wrong and you are short at the bottom and flat at the top.

**"High cattle prices mean cattle are a good thing to be long."** Backwards as a forward-looking signal. High prices *trigger* heifer retention, which builds the herd that floods the market and crashes prices two to three years out. In the cattle cycle, a record high is closer to a sell signal for deferred contracts than a buy signal, because the high price is *causing* the future glut. The herd inventory report tells you where you are in the cycle; the spot price alone misleads you.

**"Livestock prices follow grain prices one-for-one, since animals eat grain."** Only loosely, and with a lag. A corn spike does raise the *cost* of producing beef, but it does not raise beef prices immediately — it first *lowers feeder prices* (feedlots bid less) and *shrinks placements*, and only six months to two years later does the smaller cattle supply push fat-cattle prices up. And the cattle cycle can easily swamp the corn signal: in a year when the herd is at a cyclical low, cattle can ride to records even with cheap corn. Feed cost sets the *margin*; biology sets the *supply*.

**"Beef demand is cyclical like industrial demand, so cattle crash in recessions."** Far less than metals. Meat consumption is one of the stickiest items in a household budget — people trade down from steak to ground beef, but total meat demand barely moves with the business cycle, certainly nothing like copper or oil demand. That demand stability, plus a supply driven by a multi-year biological clock, is exactly why livestock is so weakly correlated with the macro-sensitive commodities. It is a feed-and-biology market, not a growth market.

**"You can store meat, so livestock isn't really unstorable."** You can store *frozen beef in a freezer*, but you cannot store *beef on the hoof*. A finished 1,400 lb steer is on a biological deadline: every extra day past its finish weight it eats feed at a loss, gets fatter and lower-grading, and eventually its value falls. There is no warehouse for a live animal at peak value. That is the deep difference: copper in a warehouse this year is identical to copper next year, but a steer cannot wait, so the storage-and-carry logic that shapes every other forward curve simply does not apply.

## How it shows up in real markets

**The 2014 record and the 2011-2012 drought (the two-stage event).** The 2011-2012 drought across Texas, Oklahoma, and the southern Plains forced one of the largest cattle liquidations in modern history; the US beef cow herd fell to its smallest since 1951 by the start of 2014. The near-term effect in 2011-2012 was extra slaughter and soft prices; the lagged effect was that by 2014 there were too few cattle to slaughter, and live-cattle futures ran to all-time records (live cattle traded above \$170/cwt and feeder cattle above \$240/cwt in late 2014). A trader who understood the two-stage drought mechanism was positioned for the 2014 squeeze in 2012, while the herd was still being liquidated and the press was bearish. This is the cleanest real-world case of "drought floods, then starves."

![Illustrative cattle-crush feeding margin per head against real corn feed cost showing the margin goes negative when corn spikes](/imgs/blogs/livestock-cattle-and-hogs-the-only-commodity-that-eats-5.png)

**The 2021-2022 feed-cost squeeze.** The chart above pairs the *real* corn feed cost (the amber line, from USDA/CME data) with an *illustrative* cattle-crush feeding margin per head (the bars — there is no livestock price series in this kit, so the margin magnitudes are illustrative, but the *shape* is the point). As corn surged toward \$7 in 2021-2022 on post-pandemic demand and the Ukraine war, feeding margins were crushed and went negative for stretches. Feedlots responded exactly as the model predicts: they bid less for feeders and pulled back placements, setting up tighter cattle supplies and the strong fat-cattle prices that followed in 2023-2024 as feed costs eased and the herd, still shrinking from earlier drought, ran short. The figure makes the central claim visible: when the amber feed-cost line spikes, the margin bars go red, and the supply response follows on a lag.

**The 2014-2015 PEDv hog shock.** A different kind of supply shock: in 2013-2014 the Porcine Epidemic Diarrhea virus (PEDv) killed millions of piglets across US hog operations, abruptly cutting the pig crop. Lean-hog prices spiked to records in mid-2014 as the missing pigs failed to reach market — then collapsed in 2015 once the herd recovered and the fast hog cycle flooded the market with the rebound crop. The whole boom-bust played out in barely 18 months, a vivid demonstration of how much faster the hog clock runs than the cattle clock: a disease shock that would have taken cattle three years to work through ran its full course in hogs in under two.

**Livestock's macro decoupling in 2020.** When COVID hit in March 2020, oil famously went *negative* and copper crashed on the demand collapse (the cross-asset [energy](/blog/trading/cross-asset/energy-oil-gas-the-inflation-engine) and [metals](/blog/trading/cross-asset/metals-copper-silver-the-economys-pulse) posts cover those moves). Livestock had a completely different and bizarre crisis: not a *demand* collapse but a *processing* collapse, as packing plants shut on COVID outbreaks. Live-cattle prices fell (animals backed up on farms with nowhere to be slaughtered) at the same time that *retail beef prices spiked* (no boxed beef reaching stores) — a violent dislocation between the live animal and the meat, driven entirely by a bottleneck in the unstorable supply chain, and totally unrelated to the oil-and-copper demand story. It was a textbook reminder that livestock marches to its own, very physical, drummer.

## The playbook: how a trader reads cattle and hogs

Pull it together into what you actually watch.

**Read the feed-cost margin first.** Livestock is a processing business, so start with the spread, not the price level. Watch corn and soybean-meal prices (the [grains post](/blog/trading/commodities/grains-corn-wheat-and-soybeans-the-calories-that-trade) covers their drivers) against feeder and fat-cattle prices. When corn spikes, feeding margins get crushed, feedlots cut placements, and the cattle supply tightens six months to two years out. The cattle crush — long fat cattle, short feeders and corn — is the position that isolates the margin. A widening crush invites expansion and a future glut; a collapsing crush forces contraction and a future shortage.

**Locate yourself in the herd cycle.** The single most valuable habit is to track the USDA semi-annual **Cattle inventory** report (and the quarterly **Hogs and Pigs** report) and ask one question: is the breeding herd *expanding* or *liquidating*? Heifer retention rising means the herd is building — near-term supply tightens (bullish now) but a glut is being planted two to three years out (bearish deferred). Liquidation means the opposite: a flood now, a famine later. The spot price will mislead you; the inventory tells you where you are on the ten-year clock. The way to express a cycle view is in the *deferred* contracts, not the front month — sell the back end into a retention-driven high, buy it into a liquidation-driven low.

**Trade a drought twice.** When a drought hits the Plains, do not reflexively buy cattle. Recognize the two stages: forced liquidation *floods* the market now (the near-term move is *down*), then guts the breeding herd and *starves* supply two to three years later (the lagged move is *up*). The sophisticated expression is to fade the initial liquidation flood and accumulate deferred long exposure for the famine that the liquidation guarantees. Watch drought monitors and pasture-condition reports as a *supply* signal with a multi-year fuse.

**Use hogs as the fast read on corn.** Because the hog cycle runs in three to four years and hogs eat grain for their whole lives, lean hogs are the quickest livestock response to the corn market. A corn spike shows up in hog liquidation and then pork prices within a year, versus two-plus for cattle. The hog-corn ratio is the single number that tells you whether hog producers are about to expand or contract.

**Separate the two clocks.** Always distinguish the slow herd cycle (which sets the *level* over years) from the within-year seasonal (grilling-season demand into summer, the fall feeder run, the autumn hog low). A high price is only a sell signal if it is a *cyclical* high; a seasonal bump on a cyclical low is a dip to buy. Reading livestock badly almost always traces back to confusing a seasonal wiggle for a cyclical turn, or vice versa.

**Respect the decoupling — and use it.** Do not model cattle off the dollar or global growth the way you would copper or oil; meat demand is sticky and the supply runs on biology, so livestock is one of the few genuinely uncorrelated legs in a commodity book. That uncorrelation is its value to an allocator: it earns its weight in the index not by beating oil but by zigging when oil zags. (The cross-asset [agriculture and softs post](/blog/trading/cross-asset/agriculture-softs-the-food-and-fiber-markets) places it in the wider food-and-fiber bucket.)

The deepest takeaway returns to where we started. This series' spine is that a commodity price is a physical thing forced through a financial contract, and that the forward curve — set by storage, carry, and convenience yield — decides who profits. Livestock is the exception that proves the rule. It is the one commodity you *cannot* store, so its curve is not a carry curve at all; it is a *biological supply schedule*. And it is the one commodity that *eats* another commodity, so its margin is not a storage spread but a *feed spread*. Strip away the carry machinery that governs oil and copper and gold, and you are left with two gears: what it costs to feed the animal, and where the herd sits on its ten-year clock. Master those two gears and you have mastered the only commodity that eats.
