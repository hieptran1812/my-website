> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Commodities hedge inflation because they ARE inflation: energy and food prices are much of what the CPI measures, so when a supply shock lifts the raw input, the inflation print and the commodity price rise together. But the *spot* price is what has that high inflation beta, and you cannot hold spot — you hold a rolling futures position whose roll cost can quietly eat the gain.
>
> - **Spot commodities have a genuinely positive inflation beta** and are one of the few assets that rise during a *supply-shock* inflation, exactly when stocks AND bonds are both falling (the 1970s, 2021-22).
> - **The asterisk is the roll.** You hold futures, not barrels, and in contango each monthly roll bleeds 10-15% a year. A long-only index can therefore *lag* inflation for a decade — and did, from 2011 to 2020.
> - **The hedge is regime-dependent.** Commodities hedge *unexpected, supply-driven* inflation best; *demand-driven* inflation less reliably; and in disinflation they fall outright. They are not a constant hedge — they are a conditional one.
> - **The number to remember:** in 2022, a year of 8% US inflation, the BCOM commodity index returned **+16%** while a 60/40 portfolio fell **−16%**. That is the case for commodities. The 2011-2020 decade of negative real returns is the case against buying and holding them.

In October 1973, the Organization of Arab Petroleum Exporting Countries declared an embargo. Within five months the price of a barrel of oil roughly quadrupled, from around \$3 to nearly \$12. Americans who had never thought about a commodity in their lives suddenly sat in mile-long gas lines, and the consumer price index — the official measure of the cost of living — went on a tear that would define the decade. By 1980, US inflation had touched 14%. Over those years almost everything an investor could own lost real value: the S&P 500's real return for the 1970s was roughly flat-to-negative, and long bonds were a catastrophe as yields marched higher. One thing went up. Commodities — oil, gold, grains, metals — soared. An investor who had owned a basket of raw materials through that decade came out the other side with their purchasing power not just intact but enhanced.

That decade is the entire reason "commodities hedge inflation" is the single most repeated sentence in every brochure for every commodity fund ever sold. And the remarkable thing is that it is *true* — more deeply true than most of the people repeating it understand. Commodities do not merely correlate with inflation by some statistical accident. They are, to a large degree, *what inflation is made of.* When the price of energy and food rises, that rise does not predict inflation; it largely *is* inflation, because energy and food are a big slice of the basket the CPI measures.

But here is the part the brochure does not print. Between 2011 and 2020, US inflation ran at a low but persistently positive pace — the price level rose by about a quarter over the decade — and a long-only commodity investor was *destroyed*. A broad commodity index lost more than half its value in nominal terms over those years, and far more in real terms. The asset class sold to you as "the" inflation hedge delivered one of the worst real returns of any major asset over an entire decade *while inflation was positive the whole time.* Both of these things are true, and the gap between them — between the spot commodity price that genuinely tracks inflation and the rolling futures position you can actually buy — is what this post is about.

The figure above is the whole argument in one frame. On the left is why the spot price tracks inflation: commodities are the inputs whose rising prices *are* much of the CPI, so they have a real positive inflation beta and rise in the one regime — a supply shock — where stocks and bonds both fall. On the right is the asterisk: you cannot hold spot, you hold a futures position that must be rolled, and the roll cost can erase the spot gain. We are going to build both halves from zero, put real numbers on each, and finish with how a thoughtful investor should actually use commodities — as a *partial, regime-aware* inflation hedge, not a buy-and-hold promise.

## Foundations: what "hedging inflation" even means

Before we can ask whether commodities hedge inflation, we have to be precise about three words that are usually thrown around loosely: *inflation*, *hedge*, and *real return*. Get these right and most of the confusion in the brochures dissolves.

**Inflation** is the rate at which the general price level rises — equivalently, the rate at which the purchasing power of a unit of money falls. In the United States it is measured most commonly by the Consumer Price Index, the CPI, which tracks the cost of a fixed basket of goods and services a typical household buys. When the news says "inflation was 8%," it means that same basket cost 8% more than it did a year earlier. The single most important fact for our purposes is *what is in that basket.* Roughly a third of the CPI is housing, but a very large and very *volatile* chunk is energy (gasoline, heating, electricity) and food. Those two categories are where the big swings come from. Housing is sticky and slow; energy and food can move 30% in a year. So when inflation *surprises* — when it jumps unexpectedly — it is overwhelmingly because energy and food prices jumped. Hold that thought; it is the engine of everything that follows.

It helps to know roughly how the basket breaks down, because the weights tell you exactly how much of inflation is a commodity story. In the US CPI, energy is on the order of 7-8% of the basket directly (gasoline alone is several percent), and food is roughly 13-14%. So a *fifth* of the headline index is energy and food — categories that are essentially commodities plus a thin margin. That sounds modest until you remember that these are also the *most volatile* fifth: in a calm year housing inches up 3-4%, but gasoline can swing 40% and grains 30%. Because the *variance* of a sum is dominated by its most volatile components, energy and food contribute *far more than a fifth* of the *movement* in inflation even though they are only a fifth of the *level*. This is the precise sense in which the analysts say "inflation is a commodity story": not that commodities are most of the price level, but that they are most of the *change* in it — and the change is the whole game for a hedge. It is also why economists separate "headline" CPI (everything) from "core" CPI (everything *except* food and energy): they strip out exactly the commodity-driven components to see the slow-moving trend underneath. When headline runs far above core, you are looking at a commodity shock — and a commodity shock is the regime where the commodity hedge is built to win.

**A hedge** is an asset you own *because* it goes up when something you fear goes up — it offsets a risk rather than simply adding return. A fire-insurance policy is a hedge against your house burning down: you do not buy it expecting it to make you rich, you buy it so that the one scenario that would ruin you is covered. An *inflation hedge* is an asset whose price rises when inflation rises, so that as the cost of living eats into your other assets, this one asset gains ground and protects your overall purchasing power. The key word is *when.* A real hedge has to deliver in the specific bad scenario you are insuring against — not on average across all scenarios, but precisely in the regime that hurts you.

**Real return** is the return *after* subtracting inflation — your gain measured in purchasing power, not in dollars. If your portfolio rose 5% in a year when inflation was 8%, your *nominal* return was +5% but your *real* return was about −3%: you have more dollars but they buy less than your old dollars did. This is the only return that matters for whether you got richer in any way that counts. And it reframes the inflation-hedge question with brutal clarity. We are not asking "does this asset go up in nominal terms during inflation" — almost everything eventually does, because inflation lifts all nominal prices. We are asking the much harder question: **does this asset's price rise by *more* than inflation, delivering a positive real return, precisely in the years when inflation is high?**

#### Worked example: nominal versus real, and why it changes the verdict

Suppose over a year the US CPI rises 8%. You hold two assets. Asset A, a broad stock fund, rises 2% in price. Asset B, a basket of commodities, rises 25%. In nominal terms both went *up* — a naive observer might say both "kept up with inflation" because neither lost dollars. But run the real-return math. Asset A's real return is roughly \$1.02 of new value divided by \$1.08 of new prices, minus one — about **−5.6%**: you lost purchasing power. Asset B's real return is \$1.25 divided by \$1.08, minus one — about **+15.7%**: you *gained* purchasing power, by a wide margin. Only Asset B did the job a hedge is supposed to do.

The lesson is that "it went up in dollars" is not the test. An inflation hedge has to beat inflation in the bad years, and that is a far stricter bar than simply rising — it is the bar that separates a true hedge from an asset that merely floats up with the tide of nominal prices.

### Why commodities are a candidate at all

Most assets fail the strict test in a high-inflation year, and they fail it for a structural reason worth understanding. A **stock** is a claim on a company's future profits, and those profits are discounted back to today at an interest rate. When inflation surges, central banks raise interest rates to fight it (we explain that whole reaction in [Real vs nominal: inflation and real yields](/blog/trading/macro-trading/real-vs-nominal-inflation-real-yields-master-signal)). A higher discount rate lowers the present value of distant profits, so stock prices — especially of growth companies whose profits are far in the future — tend to *fall* in the early innings of an inflation shock. A **bond** is even worse: it pays a *fixed* stream of dollars, and inflation directly erodes the purchasing power of every one of those fixed payments, while the rate hikes that fight inflation push bond prices down mechanically. So the two assets that make up almost every ordinary portfolio — stocks and bonds, the classic 60/40 — both tend to *lose* real value in exactly the inflation scenario you most want to be protected against. This is the hole an inflation hedge is meant to fill.

Commodities are different in kind, and the difference is not subtle. A barrel of oil, a bushel of wheat, a tonne of copper — these are not claims on future dollars. They are the *physical inputs* whose prices rising *is* the inflation. When the cost of living jumps because energy got expensive, the thing that got expensive is the very thing the commodity investor owns. There is no discounting, no erosion of a fixed coupon — the asset and the threat are, in large part, the same object. That is the seed of a real inflation beta, and it is why commodities, almost alone among liquid assets, have a structural claim to hedge inflation. The rest of this post is about how strong that claim really is, and the three asterisks that weaken it in practice.

## Why spot commodities genuinely track inflation

Let us make the central mechanism precise, because it is the strongest part of the case and the part most people hand-wave. The claim is not "commodities tend to go up when inflation goes up, for reasons." The claim is **causal and definitional**: a large part of measured inflation simply *is* the change in commodity prices, passed through into the basket the CPI tracks.

Walk it through. Gasoline is a direct CPI component, and its price is essentially crude oil plus refining and taxes; when crude rises, the gasoline line of the CPI rises within weeks. Electricity and home heating are CPI components driven by natural gas and coal. The food-at-home line of the CPI is built on grains, oilseeds, sugar, coffee, and meat — all exchange-traded commodities, or one input-step away from them. Even the prices of manufactured goods carry embedded metals and energy. So when the World Bank or the BLS decomposes a big inflation move, an enormous share of it traces directly back to the commodity complex. The commodity price is not a *leading indicator* of inflation in those categories — it is the *ingredient*. This is why the correlation is so much more robust than a typical macro relationship: it is closer to an accounting identity than a statistical regularity.

![WTI crude vs US CPI inflation move together year by year](/imgs/blogs/commodities-as-an-inflation-hedge-and-when-they-are-not-2.png)

The chart above overlays the annual-average crude oil price with the annual-average CPI inflation rate over the period our macro dataset covers (the BLS CPI series here begins in 2020). Watch 2021 into 2022: crude oil more than doubled off its 2020 lows, and CPI inflation climbed from near 1% to nearly 8% in lockstep. The oil line and the inflation line are not two independent things that happened to move together — the oil move is *inside* the inflation move. That is the inflation beta you are buying when you buy commodities, and it is real.

There is a second, slower pass-through channel worth understanding, because it explains why the commodity-inflation link extends well beyond the gasoline pump. Commodities are *inputs to nearly everything else*, so a rise in raw-material prices works its way through the whole production chain with a lag. Higher oil raises shipping and trucking costs, which raises the delivered price of every physical good. Higher natural gas raises the cost of fertilizer (gas is the feedstock), which raises the cost of growing food next season. Higher copper and steel raise the cost of appliances, cars, and construction. So a commodity shock hits the CPI twice: immediately through the direct energy-and-food lines, and then again over the following six-to-eighteen months as the input-cost increase ripples into the prices of manufactured goods and services. This lagged second wave is part of why central banks watch commodities so nervously — a spike today is a down payment on broader inflation a year out. For the commodity investor, it means the inflation beta is not a one-month coincidence but a *persistent* relationship: the asset you hold is upstream of a large share of the price level, both now and with a lag.

Crucially, this beta shows up most powerfully in the one regime that is most dangerous for everything else: a **supply shock**. When the cause of inflation is a sudden constraint on the supply of a raw input — an oil embargo, a war that takes a major exporter's grain off the market, a pipeline that blows up — the price of that input spikes *first*, before it works through into the broader price level. The commodity is at the front of the queue. And because a supply shock simultaneously *hurts* the economy (higher input costs squeeze corporate margins and consumer wallets) while *forcing* central banks to hike (to fight the resulting inflation), it is the textbook scenario where stocks fall AND bonds fall together. The diversifier you reach for in a normal recession — bonds — is broken in a supply-shock inflation, because the central bank is tightening into weakness rather than easing. Commodities are the one liquid asset built to rise in that exact corner of the world.

#### Worked example: putting a number on the spot inflation beta

Take 2021 to 2022 from our data. The annual-average WTI crude price went from \$68.13 in 2021 to \$94.90 in 2022 — a rise of about **+39%**. Annual-average CPI inflation over those same years ran around 4.4% then 7.9%. Now think about it as a *beta* — how much does the commodity move for each point of inflation? A crude move of +39% against an inflation environment running near 8% implies a spot inflation beta of roughly **4 to 5×**: the commodity moves several times as much as the inflation rate itself. That is enormous leverage to the inflation theme — far more than gold's roughly 1×-ish long-run beta, and the opposite sign to stocks and bonds in that year.

The takeaway is that spot commodities are not a *gentle* inflation hedge — they are a *high-octane* one, with a large positive beta concentrated in supply-shock years, which is exactly when you need it and exactly when nothing else works.

### The 1970s, the proof of concept

The cleanest historical demonstration is the one we opened with. Through the 1970s, two oil shocks (1973 and 1979) drove a decade of high inflation, and the relative performance of asset classes was a perfect illustration of the theory. Stocks delivered roughly flat real returns across the decade — nominal gains entirely eaten by inflation. Long bonds were a slaughter, losing real value year after year as yields climbed. And commodities — oil above all, but the whole complex — soared, handing real investors who owned them a substantial real gain. The 1970s are why pension funds and endowments added a "real assets" sleeve in the first place, and the case study is laid out in detail over in the cross-asset series at [Case study: 1970s stagflation, commodities win](/blog/trading/cross-asset/case-study-1970s-stagflation-commodities-win). For our purposes, hold the shape of it: high, supply-driven inflation, stocks and bonds both failing in real terms, commodities the lone winner. That is the regime in which the commodity inflation hedge does exactly what it says on the tin.

### Expected versus unexpected inflation — the distinction that decides everything

There is a subtlety hiding inside the word "inflation" that, once you see it, reorganizes the entire debate. Not all inflation is created equal as far as a hedge is concerned, because markets are forward-looking. If everyone *already knows* inflation will be 4% next year, that expectation is *already in the prices* of stocks, bonds, and commodities today. A bond's yield already embeds the expected 4%; a stock's valuation already discounts it; even the commodity forward curve already prices the expected drift. **Expected inflation is not a risk you need to hedge — it is already paid for.** The thing that actually hurts a portfolio is *unexpected* inflation: the surprise, the print that comes in hotter than the market had assumed, the shock nobody had in their models.

This matters enormously for commodities, because their inflation-hedging power is concentrated in the *unexpected* component. When inflation surprises to the upside, it is almost always because a commodity surprised to the upside — an embargo, a war, a drought, a freeze that nobody had priced. The supply shock that drives the surprise *is* a commodity-price event, so commodities capture the unexpected part with a high beta. Bonds and stocks, by contrast, get *hurt* precisely by the surprise: the bond that priced 2% inflation gets crushed when 9% arrives; the stock that priced cheap money gets repriced when rates jump to fight the surprise. So the cleaner statement of the whole thesis is this: **commodities hedge *unexpected* inflation, and they hedge it because unexpected inflation is usually a supply-shock — that is, a commodity event in disguise.** This is also why academic studies that regress commodity returns on *realized* inflation find a modest relationship, but studies that isolate the *unexpected* component find a strong one. The signal lives in the surprise.

The corollary is a warning. If inflation rises *as expected* — a slow, telegraphed grind that the market saw coming — commodities offer far less unique protection, because the move was already priced and there was no supply-shock surprise to capture. The hedge is sharpest when inflation *ambushes* the market, and dullest when inflation merely *confirms* what everyone already feared. That asymmetry is the bridge to the asterisks: in a world of telegraphed, demand-driven, well-supplied inflation, the spot beta is muted and — as we are about to see — the instrument you can actually buy can bleed away even that.

#### Worked example: hedging the surprise, not the level

Suppose the market enters a year expecting 3% inflation, and that expectation is fully priced. Two scenarios unfold. In Scenario A, inflation lands exactly at 3% — no surprise. Bonds return roughly their yield, stocks perform near their priced path, and commodities, with no supply shock to ride, drift with their own supply-demand and roll. The "hedge" did little, but you also did not need it — nothing was hurt by a surprise that did not happen. In Scenario B, a supply shock hits and inflation lands at 8% — a 5-point *surprise*. Bonds priced for 3% get crushed (a 5-point inflation surprise can knock a long bond down double digits); stocks repriced lower on the rate response; and the commodity that *caused* the surprise has spiked, say +40% on spot. The hedge earned its keep entirely in Scenario B — the 5 points of *unexpected* inflation — and did essentially nothing in Scenario A.

The takeaway is that you are not buying commodities to hedge the *level* of inflation, which is already in the price; you are buying them to hedge the *surprise*, and the surprise is almost always a supply shock wearing an inflation costume.

## The first asterisk: you cannot hold spot, you hold the roll

Now we turn to the part the brochure omits, and it is not a minor caveat — it is the difference between the asset that hedges inflation and the asset you can actually own.

Everything we just said is about the **spot price** — the price for a barrel of oil delivered *today*. The spot price is what has the high inflation beta, because the spot price is what feeds into the CPI. But you, an investor, **cannot hold spot.** You cannot store a thousand barrels of crude in your brokerage account; it would have to live in a steel tank at Cushing, insured, guarded, and financed. Natural gas you cannot store at all without a refrigerated terminal. So a commodity fund — and any index you can buy — does the only practical thing: it holds **futures contracts**, standardized paper promises to buy the commodity at a future date, and it must **roll** them. As each contract nears expiry, the fund sells it and buys the next month's contract, because it cannot take delivery of physical barrels. That swap happens every single month, forever. We built this mechanism in full in [Roll yield and why long-only commodity ETFs bleed](/blog/trading/commodities/roll-yield-and-why-long-only-commodity-etfs-bleed); here we only need its consequence for the inflation hedge.

The consequence is brutal. The shape of the forward curve decides whether that monthly roll makes or loses money. When the curve is in **contango** — later-dated contracts more expensive than nearer ones, the *normal* state for oil and especially natural gas — each roll means selling the cheaper expiring contract and buying the dearer next one: sell low, buy high, every month. That is a **negative roll yield**, a carry cost that bleeds the position even if the spot price never moves. (The opposite shape, **backwardation**, pays you; we cover that as a return source in [Backwardation as a structural return source](/blog/trading/commodities/backwardation-as-a-structural-return-source-the-carry-of-commodities).) So the total return of a long-only commodity position is not the spot return. It is:

```
total return  =  spot return  +  roll yield  +  collateral yield
```

The spot return is the piece with the high inflation beta. The roll yield, in a contango market, is a steady drag that can run **−10% to −15% a year**. The collateral yield is the T-bill interest the fund earns on the cash backing its futures — which, helpfully, *also* rises with inflation, but is a small offset. The investor's realized inflation hedge is the *sum*, and in a contango regime the roll can quietly cancel a large part of the spot inflation gain.

![A long-only index lags spot because the roll cost compounds](/imgs/blogs/commodities-as-an-inflation-hedge-and-when-they-are-not-3.png)

The chart above shows the gap with stylized index data. The blue line is the *spot* price index — the thing the news shows you and the thing with the inflation beta. The red line is the *long-only total return* an investor actually earns, dragged down month after month by the contango roll. Over the period, the spot index ends roughly flat-to-up while the investable position has bled away a third of its value. The shaded wedge between them is the roll cost — the gap between the inflation hedge in theory and the inflation hedge in your account. *This wedge is why the asset that "hedges inflation" lost money for a decade while inflation was positive.*

#### Worked example: the roll drag eats the spot inflation gain

Suppose it is an inflationary year. The spot price of oil rises **+20%**, a strong move that, on its own, would make oil a beautiful inflation hedge for that year. You did not hold spot, though — you held a long-only fund tracking the front-month contract, and the curve sat in steady contango the whole year. Each monthly roll cost you about 1.5% of carry; over twelve rolls that compounds to roughly **−16%** of roll drag (about 1.5% a month, compounded). Set the collateral yield aside as a small plus. Your realized return is approximately the spot gain *plus* the roll drag: \$1.20 × (1 − 0.16) ≈ **\$1.008** — a return of about **+1%**, against a +20% spot move.

So in the very year that spot oil delivered a textbook +20% inflation hedge, your investable position returned about *one percent.* You were right about oil, right about inflation, and the roll quietly took almost the entire gain. That single arithmetic is why "commodities hedge inflation" is a statement about spot, and a much weaker statement about the thing you can buy.

### Why this matters so much for the inflation case specifically

It is worth dwelling on *why* the roll is so corrosive to the inflation-hedge argument in particular, because it is not a coincidence — it is almost a built-in contradiction.

The forward curve tends toward contango when the market is *well supplied* — when there is plenty of oil today, holders demand a premium to deliver it later, and storage is being paid for. The curve tends toward backwardation when the market is *tight* — when buyers will pay up for a barrel *today* because they need it now. Now ask: when is inflation a supply-shock story? When the market is *tight*, when supply is constrained and buyers are scrambling for the physical. So the very supply-shock inflation that gives commodities their high spot beta also tends to *flip the curve into backwardation*, which means the roll yield turns *positive* and works *for* you. That is the good news for the inflation case: in a genuine supply-shock inflation, you often get the spot gain *and* a positive roll. 2022 was exactly this — backwardated oil curves handed long holders a positive carry on top of the price spike.

The bad news is the slow, grinding, *low-inflation* years in between. From 2011 to 2020, oil and the broad complex were generally well supplied (the US shale revolution flooded the market — see [The shale revolution](/blog/trading/commodities/the-shale-revolution-how-the-us-became-the-swing-producer)), curves sat in persistent contango, and the roll bled relentlessly. Inflation was positive but low, so the spot inflation beta gave you little, and the contango took even that. The hedge that shines in the dramatic supply-shock year quietly bankrupts you in the long boring stretches — and most years are boring stretches. This asymmetry, more than anything, is what separates the spot story from the realized story.

## The second and third asterisks: volatility and long real drawdowns

The roll is the subtlest asterisk. The other two are blunter but just as important.

**The second asterisk is volatility.** Commodities are extraordinarily volatile — single commodities routinely swing 25-40% in a year, and even diversified baskets run at equity-like-or-higher volatility. Set that against the thing you are hedging: inflation moves a few percentage points a year. So the commodity's price swings *dwarf* the inflation signal you are trying to capture. In any given year, whether your commodity hedge "worked" is dominated by idiosyncratic supply-and-demand noise — a warm winter, an OPEC meeting, a Chinese growth scare — and only weakly by the inflation environment. The inflation beta is real *on average over time*, but in any single year it is buried under a mountain of commodity-specific volatility. A hedge you can only rely on "on average over a long horizon, if you can stomach 30% drawdowns along the way" is a far weaker promise than the brochure implies.

It is worth making the volatility asterisk concrete with the numbers from our own data, because the magnitude is genuinely startling. Look back at how crude oil moved year to year: +74% in 2021, +39% in 2022, then −18% in 2023 and −10% in 2025. Inflation over those same years ran in a band of roughly 3% to 8%. So the *commodity* moved by 40 to 70 percentage points while the *inflation signal* it was supposedly tracking moved by a handful of points. The ratio of noise to signal is on the order of ten to one in a typical year. This means that if you hold commodities for any single year and ask "did my inflation hedge work," the honest answer is "it depends almost entirely on commodity-specific events that have nothing to do with inflation" — a Saudi production decision, a mild winter, a Chinese stimulus rumor. The inflation beta is a faint, reliable current beneath a violent surface chop. You can only *see* it by averaging over many years, and you can only *survive* to do that averaging if you have sized the position small enough to ride out the chop. A hedge whose signal is buried under ten times its own volatility is a real hedge — but only for an investor with the position size and the patience to let the signal emerge.

#### Worked example: why the volatility forces the position size down

Suppose you want your commodity sleeve to contribute, in a bad inflation year, about the same *dollar* protection as a 5% loss on your stocks would cost. Your stocks are \$600,000, so a 5% loss is \$30,000 you want to offset. In a supply-shock year a commodity sleeve might gain, say, 20%. To generate \$30,000 of offset at a 20% gain you would need a sleeve of \$150,000 — 15% of a \$1,000,000 portfolio. But now flip to a *bad* year for commodities: that same \$150,000 sleeve, in a −30% commodity year (entirely possible given the volatility), *loses* \$45,000. So sizing the hedge big enough to meaningfully offset a stock drawdown in the good year exposes you to a \$45,000 drag in the bad year — and bad commodity years vastly outnumber supply-shock years. The volatility forces a trade-off: size it for the rescue and you bleed in the lost decade; size it for survival and it cannot fully rescue you.

The takeaway is that commodity volatility is not just a comfort issue — it directly caps how large the sleeve can prudently be, which directly caps how much inflation protection it can deliver. The asterisk is structural, not psychological.

**The third asterisk is the long real drawdown.** This is the one that actually empties accounts. Because commodities have no yield of their own — no dividend, no coupon; an ounce of copper pays you nothing for holding it — their long-run return depends entirely on price appreciation plus (or minus) roll. And there have been *brutally* long stretches where price did not appreciate and roll bled. The decade from 2011 to 2020 is the canonical example: a broad commodity index lost well over half its value in nominal terms across those years, a real-terms catastrophe, *while inflation was positive the entire time.* An investor who bought "the inflation hedge" in 2011 because they were worried about money-printing after the financial crisis spent a decade watching it fall as the price level rose — the precise opposite of what they were promised. No equity or bond bear market in that era was remotely as punishing for as long.

![When commodities hedge inflation well and when they do not](/imgs/blogs/commodities-as-an-inflation-hedge-and-when-they-are-not-4.png)

The grid above organizes when the hedge works. In a **supply-shock** inflation (top row), spot surges because the shock *is* the commodity price, stocks and bonds both fall, and commodities are a strong hedge — the 1970s and 2022. In a **demand-pull** inflation (middle row) — a hot economy bidding up everything — commodities rise but so does almost everything else, stocks often hold up, and the *unique* protection commodities offer is weaker. And in **disinflation** (bottom row), when prices are cooling because growth is slowing, commodities *fall* — bonds rally, stocks recover, and commodities are precisely the *wrong* asset to hold. The hedge is not a constant. It is a regime-conditional instrument, and the regime that makes it shine is a narrow one.

#### Worked example: the 1970s versus the 2011-2020 decade

Put the two regimes side by side in real-return terms. Through the 1970s, with inflation averaging around 7-8% a year and oil quadrupling twice, a commodity basket delivered a *strongly positive real* return — call it on the order of several percent a year above inflation, the decade that built the entire "real assets" thesis. Now the 2011-2020 decade: inflation averaged a low ~1.7% a year, but a broad commodity index *fell* — the Bloomberg complex lost more than half its nominal value, which against positive inflation means a deeply negative real return, on the order of **−7% to −10% a year** in real terms for a long stretch. Same asset class, same "inflation hedge" label, two diametrically opposite outcomes — separated entirely by *which inflation regime* you were in and *which way the roll was running.*

The takeaway is that "commodities hedge inflation" is true of one decade and catastrophically false of the next, and the difference is not luck — it is the regime, the curve shape, and the difference between spot and roll.

### Demand-pull inflation: the unreliable middle

The regime grid flagged a middle case worth its own paragraph, because it is the one most likely to fool you. **Demand-pull** inflation is when prices rise because aggregate demand is hot — a booming economy, strong wages, plenty of credit — rather than because supply was cut. In a demand-pull inflation, commodities *do* rise (more demand for everything includes more demand for raw inputs), so superficially the hedge "works." But the protection is far less *unique*, because in a hot-demand world stocks often do *fine* too — earnings are growing, the economy is strong, and the central bank may be only gently leaning against the wind. You did not *need* a special hedge, because your stocks were not falling. The commodity inflation hedge earns its keep in the *supply-shock* regime precisely because that is the regime where your other assets betray you; in a demand-pull regime, where your stocks are holding up, the commodities are a nice-to-have, not a rescue. Knowing which inflation you are in is therefore not academic — it determines whether the commodity sleeve is doing the job you bought it for.

There is also a timing trap inside the demand-pull case. A demand-pull inflation is, by definition, the *late* stage of an economic expansion — the economy running hot, capacity strained. And the late stage of an expansion is exactly when the central bank is most determined to tighten, which means the demand-pull inflation often *ends* in a recession the tightening engineers. When that recession arrives, demand for raw inputs collapses, and commodities — having risen during the late-cycle heat — fall hard into the downturn. So a commodity position bought as a demand-pull inflation hedge can hand you the *worst* of both worlds: you buy it as inflation peaks, it works for a few months, and then it craters as the very tightening you were hedging tips the economy into a slump. This is part of why 2008 was so brutal for commodities — oil hit \$147 in July as the inflation scare peaked, then collapsed to \$30 by December as the financial crisis destroyed demand. The hedge that looked perfect in the summer was a wealth-destruction machine by Christmas. Demand-pull inflation is not just a weaker regime for commodities; it is a *treacherous* one, because the inflation peak and the demand collapse are often only months apart.

## Common misconceptions

**"Commodities always go up with inflation."** No — *spot* commodities have a positive inflation beta *on average and especially in supply shocks,* but in any given year volatility dominates, and across the 2011-2020 decade a broad commodity index *fell by more than half* while inflation was positive throughout. The relationship is a tendency concentrated in specific regimes, not a guarantee.

**"If I buy a commodity ETF, I get the inflation hedge."** This is the most expensive misconception. The ETF holds futures and rolls them; in contango the roll bleeds 10-15% a year, and a long-only fund can therefore *lag* spot — and lag inflation — for years. The spot price has the inflation beta; the rollable instrument you bought has the spot beta *minus the roll.* In 2009, oil's spot price roughly doubled and the famous USO oil ETF captured almost none of it. Read the curve before you read the brochure — the mechanics are laid out in [Roll yield and why long-only commodity ETFs bleed](/blog/trading/commodities/roll-yield-and-why-long-only-commodity-etfs-bleed).

**"Gold and commodities are the same inflation trade."** They are not, and conflating them is a classic error. Gold is a *monetary* asset — its inflation link runs through real interest rates and currency debasement, and it can rise when *real* commodity demand is weak. Commodities are *consumption and industrial* assets — their inflation link runs through actual supply and demand for the physical input. In a disinflationary growth slump, gold can rally on falling real rates while industrial commodities collapse on weak demand. They are different trades for different reasons; the monetary case for gold lives in its own series at [Real interest rates, the master variable behind the gold price](/blog/trading/gold/real-interest-rates-the-master-variable-behind-the-gold-price).

**"A strong dollar doesn't matter to the inflation hedge."** It matters a great deal. Commodities are priced in dollars worldwide, and tightening cycles that fight inflation also tend to *strengthen* the dollar — which mechanically weighs on dollar commodity prices, partially offsetting the inflation tailwind. The two forces can fight each other; the dollar channel is explained in [The dollar and commodities](/blog/trading/commodities/the-dollar-and-commodities-why-a-strong-dollar-weighs-on-prices).

**"Commodities are a low-risk way to protect against inflation."** They are a *high-risk* way. The asset class runs at equity-like-or-higher volatility, has no yield to cushion the ride, and has delivered decade-long real drawdowns. It can absolutely protect purchasing power in the right regime — but it is a volatile, drawdown-prone instrument, not a safe one, and sizing it as if it were safe is how investors get hurt.

## How it shows up in real markets

### 2021-2022: the case for, in full color

The pandemic recovery and the 2022 invasion of Ukraine produced the cleanest supply-shock inflation in forty years, and commodities did exactly what the theory promises. Crude oil's annual average ran from \$39 in 2020 to \$68 in 2021 to \$95 in 2022; natural gas, wheat, and a host of inputs spiked alongside. US CPI inflation climbed to a 40-year peak of **9.06%** in June 2022.

![The 2021 to 2022 inflation spike rode the oil spike](/imgs/blogs/commodities-as-an-inflation-hedge-and-when-they-are-not-5.png)

The chart above plots monthly CPI inflation against the annual crude price through the spike, with the June-2022 peak marked. The inflation surge and the oil surge are the same event viewed two ways. And the payoff for asset allocators was stark: in calendar 2022, the Bloomberg Commodity Index returned roughly **+16%** while the S&P 500 fell about 18%, long Treasuries fell about 31%, and a standard 60/40 portfolio fell about 16%. This is the rare year where the *only* major thing that worked was commodities — because it was a supply-shock inflation, the regime in which bonds (the usual diversifier) fail and commodities are the lone rescue. Better still, oil curves were *backwardated* through much of 2022, so long holders earned a positive roll *on top* of the price gain. Spot beta plus positive roll: the inflation hedge firing on all cylinders.

#### Worked example: sizing a commodity inflation hedge in a 60/40

Here is the practical question an allocator actually faces. You run a \$1,000,000 portfolio, traditionally 60% stocks (\$600,000) and 40% bonds (\$400,000). You are worried about a repeat of a 2022-style supply-shock inflation, in which *both* sleeves fall together. You decide to carve out a commodity sleeve as insurance. How big?

Suppose you size it at **10%** — \$100,000 — funded by trimming \$60,000 from stocks and \$40,000 from bonds, leaving 54% stocks / 36% bonds / 10% commodities. Now stress it with 2022's returns: stocks −18%, bonds −31%, commodities +16%. The old 60/40 would have lost about 0.6×(−18%) + 0.4×(−31%) = **−18.2%**, or \$182,000. The new mix loses 0.54×(−18%) + 0.36×(−31%) + 0.10×(+16%) = −9.7% − 11.2% + 1.6% = **−19.3%**... wait — re-run it carefully: 0.54×(−18) = −9.72; 0.36×(−31) = −11.16; 0.10×(+16) = +1.6; sum = **−19.28%**? That is *worse*, because in this particular stress trimming bonds hurt. Re-fund the sleeve from *stocks only*: 50% stocks / 40% bonds / 10% commodities gives 0.50×(−18) + 0.40×(−31) + 0.10×(+16) = −9 − 12.4 + 1.6 = **−19.8%** — still dominated by the bond loss. The honest lesson: a 10% commodity sleeve *softens* a supply-shock year but does not rescue a portfolio whose *bonds* are the bleeding wound; the hedge offsets a slice, not the whole.

The takeaway is that commodity sizing is about *partial* offset and *correlation* benefit, not salvation — and that the funding decision (what you sell to buy it) matters as much as the sleeve size itself.

### 2011-2020: the case against, in full color

Now the decade that the brochures never mention. After the financial crisis, with central banks printing money, a generation of investors piled into commodities as inflation insurance against the debasement they were sure was coming. The debasement inflation never came — inflation stayed stubbornly *low* — and the commodity complex entered a long bear market. The US shale revolution flooded the oil market, China's investment boom matured and its metals demand cooled, and curves sat in grinding contango that bled long holders month after month. A broad commodity index lost more than half its value over the decade. Inflation was positive the whole time, averaging around 1.7% a year, so in *real* terms the loss was even worse. The asset bought as an inflation hedge delivered one of the worst real returns of any major asset class *during years of positive inflation.* That is not a hedge failing at the margin — it is a hedge inverting completely, and it is the single most important counterweight to the 1970s story.

![Commodity basket runs hot when inflation does but overshoots both ways](/imgs/blogs/commodities-as-an-inflation-hedge-and-when-they-are-not-6.png)

The grouped bars above set the annual price change of an oil-and-copper basket beside the CPI inflation level for the recent years our data covers. Two things jump out. First, the basket *does* run hot when inflation runs hot — 2021 and 2022 show big positive commodity moves alongside high CPI. Second, and just as important, the commodity bars are *enormous* relative to the inflation bars and they *overshoot in both directions* — a +74% oil move in 2021, then negative moves in 2023 and 2025 even as inflation stayed positive. That overshoot in both directions is the volatility asterisk made visible: the inflation signal is there, but it is riding on a wave of commodity-specific noise many times its size.

### April 2020: the roll at its most extreme

The most vivid illustration of the spot-versus-roll gap came in April 2020, when the front-month WTI contract briefly settled at *negative* \$37 a barrel — traders paid to get rid of oil they could not store. The curve went into "super-contango," with prompt oil near \$20 but a year out still around \$36. A long-only fund rolling the front month was forced to sell its near-worthless front contracts and buy far dearer deferred ones — a ruinous roll. Anyone who, seeing crude "at \$20," bought a front-month oil ETF expecting to ride the rebound captured a small fraction of the recovery, because the super-contango roll shredded the position. This is the spot-versus-roll gap at its most violent, and it is a permanent reminder that the price on the screen and the return in your account are two different numbers.

## The playbook: how to use commodities as a partial inflation hedge

Pull the threads together into something you can act on. Commodities *are* an inflation hedge — but a specific, conditional, instrument-sensitive one, and using them well means respecting all three asterisks.

![From a high spot beta to a weaker realized hedge](/imgs/blogs/commodities-as-an-inflation-hedge-and-when-they-are-not-7.png)

The figure above is the summary you should carry away. On the left is the brochure promise: a high spot inflation beta, the unique ability to rise when stocks and bonds both fall, a genuine diversifier. On the right is what you actually keep after the deductions: minus the roll cost (10-15% a year in contango), minus the volatility that dwarfs the signal, minus the risk of multi-year real drawdowns. The realized hedge is real but materially weaker than the spot beta — which leads to a handful of concrete rules.

**Size it as a sleeve, not a core holding.** Because of the volatility and the drawdown risk, commodities belong as a *minority* allocation — commonly 5-15% in a portfolio that wants the hedge — not as a large core position. The point is to add a *positively-inflation-correlated, negatively-stock-and-bond-correlated* slice that softens the worst regime, not to bet the portfolio on raw materials. Size it as insurance: enough to matter in the bad year, small enough to survive the lost decade. The allocation framing lives in the cross-asset series — see [Energy, oil & gas, the inflation engine](/blog/trading/cross-asset/energy-oil-gas-the-inflation-engine).

**Be curve-aware, always.** The single most important habit: before you buy any commodity vehicle, look at whether the curve is in contango or backwardation. A backwardated curve hands you a positive roll on top of the spot beta — the hedge at its best. A steeply contango curve (natural gas is the worst offender) will bleed you 10-15% a year regardless of what spot does. The same inflation hedge can have wildly different realized returns depending only on the curve shape at the time. Buy-and-hold through a contango regime is how investors lose money owning "the inflation hedge."

**Prefer diversified, roll-smart vehicles over a single front-month ETF.** A single-commodity front-month fund concentrates both the volatility and the roll risk. A diversified index that holds many commodities and uses an *optimized* roll schedule (buying further out on the curve to minimize contango drag, or weighting toward backwardated commodities) captures more of the spot beta and less of the roll cost. The index trade-offs — energy-heavy GSCI versus diversified BCOM — are covered in [Commodity index investing: GSCI, BCOM, and owning the basket](/blog/trading/commodities/commodity-index-investing-gsci-bcom-and-owning-the-basket).

**Know which inflation you are hedging.** Commodities are best — and most unique — as a hedge against *unexpected, supply-driven* inflation: the embargo, the war, the chokepoint. That is the regime where bonds fail and you most need them. They are a weaker, less-distinctive hedge against *demand-pull* inflation (where your stocks are probably fine anyway), and they are an outright *loser* in disinflation. If your inflation thesis is "a supply shock could hit," commodities are the trade. If it is "a slow grind of demand-driven price rises," they are far less reliable.

**Mind the dollar.** The rate hikes that fight inflation tend to strengthen the dollar, which weighs on dollar-priced commodities and can blunt the inflation tailwind. In a tightening cycle, the inflation beta and the dollar headwind partly cancel — a reason the hedge can disappoint even in a positive-inflation year. The mechanism is in [The dollar and commodities](/blog/trading/commodities/the-dollar-and-commodities-why-a-strong-dollar-weighs-on-prices).

**The signals to watch, in order.** Before and during any commodity inflation-hedge position, run down a short checklist. *One — the curve shape.* Is the front of the curve in backwardation (the roll pays you) or contango (it bleeds you)? This single read changes your expected return by 10-15% a year and is the first thing to check. *Two — the inflation regime.* Is the inflation story a *supply shock* (commodities shine, bonds fail, you most need the hedge) or a *demand-pull / disinflation* story (commodities are weak-to-negative and you may not need them)? The spread between headline and core CPI is a quick tell: a wide gap means a commodity-driven shock. *Three — the dollar.* Is the dollar strengthening (a headwind that blunts the inflation tailwind) or weakening (a tailwind on top of it)? *Four — real yields.* Rising real yields signal a central bank tightening into the inflation, which strengthens the dollar and pressures the demand side of commodities; falling real yields are friendlier. *Five — your sizing.* Is the sleeve small enough that a −30% commodity year cannot derail your plan, and is it funded from the part of the portfolio you actually want to diversify? Tick those five and you are using commodities the way they reward — as a curve-aware, regime-aware, appropriately-sized insurance slice, not a buy-and-forget promise.

#### Worked example: the full decision, end to end

Bring it together. It is early 2022. You read the checklist. The curve: front-month oil is in *backwardation* — the roll will pay you, not bleed you. The regime: a supply shock from war and reopening, with headline CPI running far above core — the exact regime where bonds fail and commodities are the unique rescue. The dollar: strengthening, a partial headwind, but not enough to offset a war-driven oil spike. Real yields: rising, a caution, but the supply shock dominates. Sizing: you hold a 10% sleeve, fundable from stocks, small enough to survive a reversal. Every light is green for the *good* regime. You hold the sleeve through 2022, and it returns roughly +16% (BCOM) — plus a positive roll — while your stocks fall 18% and your bonds fall 31%. The sleeve did not save the portfolio, but it was the one green number in a sea of red, and it softened the worst synchronized stock-and-bond year in a generation. Now run the *same* checklist in 2015, mid-decade: contango curve (the roll bleeds), no supply shock (a glut, if anything), strengthening dollar — every light red. The discipline that told you to *hold* in 2022 tells you to *stay small or stay out* in 2015. That is the entire skill.

The takeaway is that using commodities as an inflation hedge is not a buy-and-hold decision but a *repeated, conditional* one, and the checklist is what turns a blunt brochure promise into an instrument you can actually steer.

The deepest point to leave with is the one the spine of this series keeps returning to: **a commodity price is a physical thing forced through a financial contract.** The physical thing — the spot barrel — is what genuinely tracks inflation. The financial contract — the rolling future you can actually own — is what you get, and the gap between them, set by the forward curve and the cost of storage, is exactly where the inflation hedge is won or lost. Commodities are not a magic inflation shield you can buy and forget. They are a high-octane, regime-dependent, curve-sensitive partial hedge — devastatingly effective in a 1970s or a 2022, quietly ruinous across a 2011-2020. Own them for the supply-shock years, size them so the lost decades cannot break you, and never confuse the price on the screen with the return in your account.
