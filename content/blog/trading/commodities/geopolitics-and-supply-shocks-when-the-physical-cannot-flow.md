> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Commodities are the one asset class made of physical things that must be mined or grown in specific places and shipped through specific narrow chokepoints, so geopolitics can sever the flow in a way it can never sever a stock certificate.
>
> - A handful of maritime chokepoints — Hormuz (~20% of world oil), Suez / Bab-el-Mandeb, Malacca, the Turkish Straits, Panama — carry most of the world's energy and grain. Block any one and the world re-routes, freight spikes, and the price jumps before a single barrel is lost.
> - Embargoes and sanctions mostly **re-route** supply, they do not destroy it: a sanctioned barrel finds a new buyer at a discount (the shadow fleet, the price cap), so the trade map redraws while world supply barely changes.
> - Because demand for energy and food is **inelastic** in the short run, a tiny actual shortfall produces an outsized price move — which is why the *fear* (the risk premium) usually moves the price more than the barrels actually lost.
> - The one number to remember: roughly **20% of the world's seaborne oil** transits the Strait of Hormuz, a waterway barely 21 miles wide at its narrowest. That single fact is why oil rises on bad news while almost every other asset falls.

On the morning of 24 February 2022, traders woke to the news that Russian tanks had crossed into Ukraine. Within hours, Brent crude — the world's benchmark barrel — was climbing, and over the following ten days it touched roughly \$139, a price not seen since the 2008 super-spike. European natural gas, priced at the Dutch TTF hub, went vertical: a market that had traded near 9 euros per megawatt-hour in 2020 printed close to **339 euros** by the following August. Wheat, much of it grown in the black soils of Ukraine and southern Russia, jumped to a record near \$12.94 a bushel.

Here is the strange part. On that February morning, not one barrel of oil had actually gone missing. Russia was still pumping. The Strait of Hormuz, half a world away, was open. The wheat already in silos had not rotted. And yet every one of these markets had repriced violently *upward*, while the S&P 500 fell and bond yields whipsawed. Commodities did the one thing that makes them unique among assets: they **rose on bad news**.

This post is about why. Stocks and bonds are paper claims — promises whose value lives in a database and can be transferred at the speed of a keystroke. A barrel of crude is a physical thing that has to be lifted out of the ground in one specific country and carried by ship through one specific narrow waterway to reach the refinery that needs it. Every link in that chain sits on a map, and every point on that map can be cut by a war, an embargo, a blockade, or a drought. That physicality is the source of both the danger and the opportunity. Let us trace the whole mechanism, from the chokepoints on the map to the risk premium in the price.

## Foundations: why a physical asset is a geopolitical asset

Start with the spine of this whole series: **a commodity price is a physical thing forced through a financial contract.** When you buy a December crude future, you are not buying an idea of oil — you are buying a claim on a thousand barrels that, if you held it to delivery, would show up as actual liquid at a tank farm in Cushing, Oklahoma. The contract is paper; the thing underneath it is matter. And matter has a geography.

Three properties of that geography make commodities uniquely exposed to politics. Define each one carefully, because the rest of the post builds on them.

**Property one: production is concentrated in specific places.** You cannot grow oil in a server farm. Roughly a third of the world's crude comes from a single region — the Persian Gulf — and another large slice from Russia. Half the world's high-grade copper comes from Chile and Peru. The lithium that powers electric-vehicle batteries is concentrated in a handful of salt flats and Australian hard-rock mines. When production is geographically concentrated, a single government's decision — to nationalize a mine, to ban an export, to go to war — can swing a meaningful share of world supply. A "commodity" is, in this sense, a hostage to the politics of the ground it sits on.

**Property two: the physical thing has to move, and it moves through narrow places.** A barrel pumped in Saudi Arabia is useless to a German refiner until it has crossed thousands of miles of ocean. Ships are the cheapest way to move bulk matter, and ships follow sea lanes, and sea lanes funnel through a few narrow straits and man-made canals. These pinch points are called **chokepoints**: a chokepoint is a stretch of waterway so narrow, or so critical to the shortest route, that a large fraction of world trade has no practical alternative but to pass through it. A chokepoint is a single point of failure for a global supply chain.

**Property three: short-run demand is inelastic.** *Elasticity* measures how much the quantity people buy changes when the price changes. Demand for a luxury handbag is elastic — double the price and sales collapse, because nobody *needs* a handbag. Demand for the diesel that runs the trucks delivering food to your city is **inelastic**: if diesel doubles in price next week, the trucks still have to run, the hospitals still need power, and the harvest still has to move. People keep buying almost the same quantity even at a much higher price, because the alternative — not eating, not heating, not moving — is unthinkable in the short run. Inelastic demand is the amplifier in this whole story. It is the reason a 2% shortfall in supply does not produce a 2% price rise but something far larger.

Put the three together and you have the core thesis. Commodities are *physical* (so geography matters), *concentrated and channelled* (so a few political actors and a few straits control the flow), and *inelastic* (so any disruption to that flow is magnified into a violent price move). No other asset class checks all three boxes. A stock is a paper claim on a diversified, location-independent enterprise; a bond is a promise to pay. Neither has to pass through the Strait of Hormuz.

#### Worked example: how inelastic demand turns a small shortfall into a big spike

Suppose the world consumes 100 million barrels of oil a day, and a conflict removes 3 million barrels a day from the market — a 3% supply cut. How much does the price rise?

If demand were elastic, the answer would be "about 3%." But energy demand is inelastic in the short run. Economists estimate the short-run price elasticity of oil demand at roughly −0.05 to −0.10, meaning a 1% rise in price reduces the quantity demanded by only 0.05% to 0.10%. To clear a 3% supply shortfall through demand alone, the price has to rise until people *voluntarily* give up 3% of consumption. With an elasticity of −0.05, that requires a price increase of about 3% ÷ 0.05 = **60%**.

So a 3% supply cut maps to roughly a 60% price spike. Start at \$70 a barrel and you land near \$112. That is the arithmetic behind every oil-shock headline: the price move is the supply shortfall divided by the (tiny) elasticity, and dividing by a small number is how a modest physical disruption becomes a dramatic price event. **The smaller the elasticity, the bigger the spike from the same lost barrels.**

You can see this amplifier at work across two decades of crude. The chart below plots WTI's annual average alongside the geopolitical-shock years, with the intraday peaks marked. The line is the *annual average*, which already smooths away the sharpest moves — and even so, every named conflict or supply scare leaves a visible bump. The dashed reference line at the top marks the Mar-2022 Brent intraday peak near \$139, far above any annual average, because the worst of a shock happens in days, not years.

![WTI crude annual average and the geopolitical-shock years from 2000 to 2025](/imgs/blogs/geopolitics-and-supply-shocks-when-the-physical-cannot-flow-2.png)

Three features of this chart carry the whole story. First, the 2008 super-spike to \$147 intraday — driven by surging demand against tight Gulf supply and a thin spare-capacity cushion — shows how high the price can run when the world has no slack. Second, the 2020 collapse, including the infamous −\$37.63 negative settlement, shows the mirror image: when demand vanishes (the pandemic lockdowns) and storage fills up, the price can fall *below zero* because holding the physical barrel suddenly costs money. Third, the 2022 war spike, where Brent touched ~\$139, shows geopolitics doing in a fortnight what fundamentals took years to do in 2008. The price of a physical, inelastic, choke-pointed commodity is *jumpy* in a way no diversified equity index ever is, and the jumps cluster around the years when the physical flow was threatened.

## The chokepoints: a few narrow waterways that carry the world

If you wanted to design the most geopolitically fragile distribution network imaginable, you would route most of the world's energy and food through a half-dozen straits and canals, each only a few miles wide, several of them bordered by unstable or hostile states. That is exactly the network the world has. Walk through the main chokepoints, because they reappear in every supply-shock story.

**The Strait of Hormuz** is the single most important. It connects the Persian Gulf — Saudi Arabia, Iraq, the UAE, Kuwait, Qatar, Iran — to the open ocean. Roughly **20% of the world's seaborne oil** and a large share of its liquefied natural gas (LNG, gas chilled to a liquid for shipping, the subject of [its own post in this series](/blog/trading/commodities/lng-and-the-globalization-of-gas-shipping-the-uncontainable)) pass through a waterway that narrows to about 21 miles, with the shipping lanes themselves only two miles wide in each direction. Iran sits on its northern shore. Every few years a tanker is seized or a drone strikes a ship there, and oil jumps on the fear that the strait could close. There is almost no way around it: the Gulf's only significant bypass pipelines can carry a fraction of the volume that normally sails through.

**The Suez Canal and the Bab-el-Mandeb** together form the Asia-to-Europe artery. The Bab-el-Mandeb is the strait at the southern mouth of the Red Sea; Suez is the canal at its northern end that cuts through Egypt to the Mediterranean. A tanker from the Gulf bound for Rotterdam either takes this Red Sea short-cut or sails all the way around the southern tip of Africa — an extra ~3,500 nautical miles and ten to fourteen days. In 2021 a single grounded container ship, the *Ever Given*, wedged itself across the Suez Canal and halted roughly 12% of global trade for six days. In 2023–24, missile attacks on Red Sea shipping forced much of the world's container and tanker traffic onto the long Africa detour, and freight rates multiplied.

**The Strait of Malacca** is the Gulf's road to Asia. Between Indonesia, Malaysia, and Singapore, it carries something like 30% of all seaborne trade — most of the oil that fuels China, Japan, and Korea passes through it. It is long, busy, and historically a haven for piracy.

**The Turkish Straits** — the Bosphorus and the Dardanelles — are the only way out of the Black Sea. Russian crude loaded at Novorossiysk and Ukrainian and Russian wheat loaded at the Black Sea ports must thread this narrow passage through the middle of Istanbul to reach the Mediterranean and the world.

**The Panama Canal** carries grain, liquefied petroleum gas, and containers between the Atlantic and Pacific. Its locks are fed by fresh water from a rainfall-dependent lake, so a **drought** can throttle it — in 2023–24, low water forced the canal to cut the number of daily transits, an ecological chokepoint rather than a military one. This is the chokepoint nobody expected, and it is a reminder that "supply shock" is not only about war.

A crucial property unites all of them: **the alternatives are worse, slower, or smaller.** This is what makes a chokepoint a chokepoint rather than just a busy route. Hormuz has only thin bypass pipelines that carry a fraction of its volume. The Suez alternative is a two-week detour around Africa. Malacca's bypasses (the Sunda and Lombok straits) are deeper-draft but longer and add days. The Turkish Straits have no alternative at all — there is simply no other way out of the Black Sea by sea. A route is only a chokepoint when the next-best option imposes a real penalty in time, cost, or capacity, and that penalty is exactly what shows up as a freight and price spike when the primary route closes. The map is not a list of waterways; it is a list of *places where the world has no good Plan B.*

#### Worked example: the freight-cost spike from a Suez re-route

A war or blockade forces tankers off the Suez / Red Sea route and around the Cape of Good Hope. What does that do to the cost of delivering oil, even if not one barrel is lost?

Take a Gulf-to-Europe voyage. Via Suez it is roughly 6,400 nautical miles; around Africa it is roughly 11,400 — an extra 5,000 nautical miles. At a typical laden tanker speed of ~13 knots (about 312 nautical miles a day), that is an extra **16 days each way**, call it ~12 extra days when you blend laden and ballast legs.

Now the economics. A Very Large Crude Carrier (VLCC) might earn a day-rate of \$40,000 in a calm market. Twelve extra days adds 12 × \$40,000 = **\$480,000** to the voyage cost. Spread over a 2-million-barrel cargo, that is about **\$0.24 a barrel** of extra freight — small. But here is the second-order effect that makes freight explode: the longer voyage ties each ship up for longer, so the same fleet completes fewer voyages per year. Effective shipping capacity falls, and with charterers scrambling for the fewer available ships, day-rates do not stay at \$40,000 — they spike to \$150,000 or \$200,000. At \$200,000 a day, the same detour adds 12 × \$200,000 = \$2.4 million per voyage, or about **\$1.20 a barrel**, and the scramble can push it far higher. **A blocked strait raises the price not by destroying oil but by making the ships that carry it suddenly scarce.**

## The chokepoint mechanism: how a blocked strait becomes a price spike

It is worth being precise about the causal chain, because a common beginner's error is to picture a blocked strait as oil simply *vanishing*. It does not. The mechanism is subtler and more interesting, and the figure below lays it out as a sequence.

![Pipeline showing a blocked strait re-routing cargoes into longer voyages, higher freight, delayed supply, and a price spike](/imgs/blogs/geopolitics-and-supply-shocks-when-the-physical-cannot-flow-3.png)

Step one: the strait is **blocked** — by a war, a naval blockade, a swarm of missiles making the route too dangerous to insure, a drought that throttles a canal, or a single grounded ship. Step two: cargoes **re-route**. The oil still exists, the buyer still wants it, so the tanker simply takes the long way around. Step three: those longer voyages **consume ship capacity** — every vessel is at sea for more days, so the same fleet delivers fewer cargoes per month. Step four: with effective capacity down, **freight rates spike** as charterers bid for the scarce remaining ships. Step five: delivered supply is **delayed** — the barrels are real, but they arrive weeks late, which to a refiner running low on inventory is indistinguishable from a shortage *right now*. Step six: the **price spikes**, lifted by both the higher landed cost and the risk premium that the blockage might get worse.

Notice what this chain teaches. A chokepoint event is, at first, a *timing* shock and a *freight* shock, not a *reserves* shock. The world has not lost any oil; it has lost the ability to deliver oil on schedule. But because demand is inelastic and refiners hold only weeks of inventory, a timing shock behaves like a real shortage. The price reacts to the barrel that is *late*, not just the barrel that is *gone*.

This is also why chokepoint scares tend to fade if the disruption proves temporary. When the *Ever Given* was refloated after six days, oil and freight gave back most of their spike within weeks — the delayed cargoes arrived, inventories rebuilt, and the timing shock resolved. The lesson for a trader: distinguish a *timing* disruption (which mean-reverts) from a *structural* one (which does not). A canal that reopens in a week is a fade; a war that closes a strait for a year is a regime change.

## Embargoes and sanctions: the world re-routes, it does not stop

The second great mechanism of commodity geopolitics is the **embargo** or **sanction** — a government decision to forbid trade with a particular country's commodities. The mental model most people carry is that a sanction removes the sanctioned country's exports from the world. That model is almost always wrong, and understanding *why* is one of the most valuable insights in this whole subject.

Start with history. In October 1973, the Arab members of the OPEC oil cartel declared an embargo against the United States and other nations that had supported Israel in the Yom Kippur War. They cut production and refused to ship to the targeted countries. The result was the first great oil shock: the price of crude roughly quadrupled, from around \$3 to nearly \$12 a barrel, gas-station lines snaked around blocks, and the West learned for the first time that energy could be used as a weapon. The 1973 embargo is the archetype, and it worked partly because the world had little spare production and few alternative routes — the cartel genuinely could take barrels off the market. (For how that cartel sets the supply floor and why it cannot always hold, see [OPEC+ and the supply curve](/blog/trading/commodities/opec-plus-and-the-supply-curve-the-cartel-that-sets-the-floor).)

Now contrast 1973 with 2022. After Russia invaded Ukraine, the West imposed sweeping sanctions on Russian oil — and the textbook said Russian exports should collapse, removing ~7–8 million barrels a day and sending prices to the moon. That is not what happened. Instead, the barrels **re-routed**. Crude that had flowed to Europe turned east and sailed to India and China, who were delighted to buy discounted oil. A so-called **shadow fleet** — older tankers with opaque ownership and insurance, operating outside Western jurisdiction — sprang up to carry it. The G7 imposed a **price cap** (a rule allowing Western shipping and insurance services only if the cargo sold below a set price, designed to keep the oil flowing while squeezing Russia's revenue), and the result was exactly that: the oil kept flowing, just at a discount of roughly \$15 to \$30 a barrel to the global benchmark.

The figure below contrasts the theory with the reality.

![Before and after diagram showing sanctions intended to strand barrels but actually re-routing them to new buyers at a discount](/imgs/blogs/geopolitics-and-supply-shocks-when-the-physical-cannot-flow-5.png)

Why the difference between 1973 and 2022? Two reasons. First, the modern oil market is global and liquid — a barrel is fungible, and once one buyer refuses it, another with looser scruples and a need for cheap energy steps in. Second, a sanction's bite depends on whether the *physical* alternative exists. In 1973 there were few alternative suppliers with spare capacity; in 2022 the world had ample tanker capacity and willing eastern buyers, so the barrels simply changed direction. **A sanction does not destroy supply unless it can also destroy the routes around it** — and in a world of ships that can sail anywhere, those routes are hard to close.

This re-routing has a profound trading implication. The *world* price barely moved on the Russian sanctions once the re-routing was understood, because global supply was roughly preserved. What changed was the **map**: a two-tier market emerged, with a sanctioned discount barrel and an unsanctioned premium barrel. The trade was not "buy oil because it's going up"; it was "trade the discount" — the spread between Russian Urals crude and Brent. Geopolitics did not move the level so much as it dislocated the *relationships*, and the dislocations are where the edge lives.

#### Worked example: the sanctions-discount and the re-routing economics

After the 2022 sanctions, Russian Urals crude traded at a discount of, say, \$25 a barrel to Brent. Brent is at \$90, so Urals is at \$65. Who captures that \$25, and why does the barrel still flow?

An Indian refiner buys Urals at \$65 instead of \$90 Brent-grade crude — a saving of \$25 a barrel. On a 2-million-barrel cargo, that is **\$50 million** of saved input cost. Out of that \$25, some is eaten by the higher costs of the re-route: a shadow-fleet tanker charges more, insurance is dearer, and the longer voyage to India costs extra freight — say \$8 a barrel of extra friction in total. The refiner still nets ~\$17 a barrel of advantage versus buying Brent-grade crude. Russia, meanwhile, receives \$65 instead of the \$90 it once got from Europe — a real \$25 hit to its per-barrel revenue, but still well above its lifting cost, so it keeps pumping.

Add it up: the barrel flows because *everyone in the new chain is better off than not trading* — the refiner saves money, the shadow fleet earns a premium, and Russia still profits versus shutting in the well. **Sanctions redistribute the value of a barrel; they rarely delete the barrel.** This is why the world price held even as the trade map redrew. The merchants who specialize in arbitraging exactly these dislocations — Glencore, Vitol, Trafigura — are profiled in [commodity trading houses](/blog/trading/finance/commodity-trading-houses-glencore-vitol-trafigura).

## Wars at the source: when the ground itself is the front line

The third mechanism is the most direct: a war or upheaval *in a producing or exporting region*. Here the supply disruption can be real, not merely a re-routing, because the wells, fields, mines, or ports themselves are in the line of fire or cut off from the world.

The Russia–Ukraine war is the textbook case because it hit *two* commodity complexes at once. Russia is one of the three largest oil producers and was Europe's dominant supplier of pipeline natural gas. Ukraine, together with Russia, is one of the world's great breadbaskets — the two together normally supply something like a quarter of world wheat exports and large shares of corn, barley, and sunflower oil. The war threatened all of it: oil and gas from the sanctions side, grain from the blockade of Ukraine's Black Sea ports, where ships could not safely load and mines drifted in the shipping lanes. Wheat spiked toward \$13 a bushel; corn jumped; the price of bread rose in dozens of food-importing countries from Egypt to Indonesia.

The European gas shock deserves its own chart, because it was the purest "war at the source" event of the decade. Europe had built its energy system on a foundation of cheap Russian pipeline gas. When that gas was throttled — partly by sanctions, partly by Russia weaponizing the flows, culminating in the sabotage of the Nord Stream pipelines — there was no quick re-route, because pipeline gas cannot simply be loaded onto a ship the way oil can. Europe had to replace pipeline gas with far more expensive seaborne LNG, bid away from Asia, and the price reflected the scramble.

![Bar chart of European TTF gas price showing the 2022 spike to about 339 euros per megawatt-hour](/imgs/blogs/geopolitics-and-supply-shocks-when-the-physical-cannot-flow-4.png)

Look at the magnitude. A market that traded near 9 euros per megawatt-hour in 2020 spiked to roughly **339** in August 2022 — a thirty-seven-fold move. That is what a real, hard-to-re-route supply cut looks like when it hits an inelastic, infrastructure-locked market. Gas is much harder to re-route than oil: oil sails anywhere on a tanker, but pipeline gas is captive to its pipe, and the LNG terminals and ships needed to substitute take years to build. The lesson generalizes: **the harder a commodity is to re-route, the more a source disruption moves its price.** Oil, the most fungible and most shippable, often re-routes and only spikes briefly; pipeline gas, captive to fixed infrastructure, spikes hardest and longest.

The Middle East offers the oil-side version of the same lesson. Conflicts there — the Iran–Iraq war of the 1980s, the 1990 Iraqi invasion of Kuwait, the periodic flare-ups around Iran — threaten both production at the source and transit through Hormuz. The 1990 Gulf War sent oil from the mid-\$teens to over \$40 within months as Iraq's and Kuwait's combined ~4 million barrels a day were knocked off the market and the world feared for the rest of the Gulf. Each of these events is a variation on one theme: when the fighting is *where the commodity comes from* or *where it must pass*, the supply loss can be genuine and the price response is correspondingly fierce.

#### Worked example: a Hormuz-closure scenario

Suppose tensions escalate to the point where the Strait of Hormuz is closed for a month, halting the ~20 million barrels a day that normally transit it. Not all of that is permanently lost — some can crawl out through limited bypass pipelines, perhaps 4 million barrels a day — so the *actual* shortfall to the world might be ~16 million barrels a day out of 100 million consumed, a 16% supply cut.

Apply the inelastic-demand arithmetic. With a short-run demand elasticity of about −0.05, clearing a 16% shortfall through demand alone requires a price rise of 16% ÷ 0.05 = **320%**. Start at \$80 and that maps to a price north of \$330 a barrel. In practice it would never get that far in a straight line — governments would release strategic reserves, OPEC's spare capacity outside the Gulf would surge, demand would be rationed, and the market would price the *probability* of a quick reopening — but the arithmetic shows why even a *partial, temporary* Hormuz closure is the single scariest scenario in the oil market. **A 16% cut to an inelastic market is not a 16% problem; it is potentially a tripling.** This is also why the mere *threat* to Hormuz, with no closure at all, can add \$5–\$15 to the price as a risk premium — the subject of the next section.

## Why the fear moves the price more than the lost barrels

Here is the most counterintuitive and most important idea in commodity geopolitics: **most of the time, the price moves on the fear, not on the fact.** A tanker is seized in the Gulf and oil jumps \$4, even though that one tanker carried a rounding error of the world's daily consumption. A drone hits a Saudi facility and oil spikes 10% in a day, then gives most of it back within a week once repairs are underway. The actual barrels lost were trivial; the price move was not. What is going on?

The answer is the **risk premium** — the extra amount the market is willing to pay *now* to hold the physical commodity, as insurance against a worse disruption *later*. A refiner who needs a steady supply of crude to keep its plant running cannot afford to be caught short if Hormuz closes next month. So when the probability of a closure rises, that refiner — and thousands like it — bid up the price today to lock in barrels and build a buffer. The price reflects not the supply situation that *is*, but a probability-weighted average of all the supply situations that *might be*.

Think of it as the market pricing **probability × impact.** A 10% chance of a disruption that would remove 16% of supply has an *expected* impact of 1.6% of supply — and through the inelasticity amplifier, even that expected loss justifies a meaningful price rise today. The figure below lays out the four cases.

![Two by two matrix of barrels at risk versus escalation probability showing how the risk premium grows](/imgs/blogs/geopolitics-and-supply-shocks-when-the-physical-cannot-flow-6.png)

Read the matrix. Down one axis is how many barrels are actually at stake; across the other is how likely the feared escalation is. The bottom-right cell — many barrels at risk, high probability — is the panic premium, the regime where oil rises tens of dollars. But notice the *bottom-left* cell: even a **low** probability of a **huge** disruption commands a real premium, because the impact is so catastrophic that the market pays up even against long odds. This is the "fat tail" — a small chance of a Hormuz closure is worth paying to insure against precisely because the outcome is so extreme. The risk premium is the market's insurance bill, and inelastic demand makes that insurance expensive.

This reframes how a trader should listen to news. The question is never "how many barrels were lost?" in isolation; it is "how did this event change the probability distribution of *future* losses, and was that change already in the price?" A tanker seizure that the market shrugs off was already discounted; a tanker seizure that adds \$5 told you the market had *not* been pricing that escalation path. The premium is the difference between the calm-baseline price and the fear-adjusted price, and the figure earlier in this post made that gap concrete: each shock print sits above a calm-market baseline, and the gap *is* the geopolitical premium.

There is a subtle but important asymmetry in how the premium behaves. It tends to inflate *fast* and deflate *slow*. When an escalation hits, the premium jumps in minutes, because nobody wants to be caught short physical barrels and the bidding is urgent. When tensions cool, the premium leaks out gradually, because the market stays wary of a relapse and is reluctant to give back its insurance all at once. That asymmetry — sharp up, grinding down — is itself tradeable: the violent up-move often overshoots and presents a fade, while the slow bleed-down rewards patience over a quick reversal bet. It also means the premium is rarely zero in a tense region; there is a persistent baseline of fear priced into oil from the Gulf at almost all times, and what moves the price is the *change* in that fear, not its level. Reading geopolitics as a trader is, in the end, the discipline of reading a probability distribution that the rest of the world reads as a headline.

![Horizontal bar chart of oil shock prices versus a calm baseline showing the geopolitical premium](/imgs/blogs/geopolitics-and-supply-shocks-when-the-physical-cannot-flow-7.png)

#### Worked example: the risk-premium math

The market is calm and oil sits at a \$70 fair value based on actual supply and demand. Now tensions rise around Hormuz. Traders collectively assess a **15% probability** that the strait closes in the next three months, and they estimate that *if* it closed, the price would spike to \$160 — a \$90 jump above fair value. How much risk premium should the price carry *today*, before anything has happened?

A first-pass estimate is the probability-weighted impact: 15% × \$90 = **\$13.50**. So the price might trade around \$70 + \$13.50 ≈ **\$83.50** purely on fear, with zero barrels actually lost. That \$13.50 is the geopolitical risk premium — the market's expected-value insurance bill for a disruption that has not happened and probably will not.

Now watch what happens when the news evolves. If diplomacy cools tensions and the perceived probability falls from 15% to 5%, the premium drops to 5% × \$90 = \$4.50, and the price *falls* \$9 — even though, again, nothing physical changed. The premium breathes with the probability. **The single most underappreciated fact in oil trading is that a large share of the price, much of the time, is paying for disruptions that never occur** — and the premium can be made or lost on diplomacy alone.

## Resource nationalism: when governments hoard their own ground

There is a slower, structural cousin of the acute supply shock: **resource nationalism** — the tendency of governments to assert control over the commodities produced within their borders, especially when prices are high. It comes in several flavors, and a trader should know the playbook because it reshapes supply over years, not days.

The bluntest form is the **export ban**. When domestic food or fuel prices spike, governments frequently ban or tax exports to protect their own consumers — keeping the commodity *inside* the country at the expense of the world price. In 2023, India, the world's largest rice exporter, banned exports of most non-basmati rice to tame domestic food inflation. The world rice price jumped, and exporters like Vietnam and Thailand saw their benchmark prices surge as buyers scrambled for the remaining supply. Indonesia has periodically banned palm-oil and nickel-ore exports; Russia has used grain export quotas; dozens of countries imposed food export restrictions in the 2008 and 2022 price spikes. An export ban is a chokepoint a government builds at its own border. (The full mechanics of food export bans and government hoarding get their own treatment in [food security, export bans, and when governments hoard](/blog/trading/commodities/food-security-export-bans-and-when-governments-hoard).)

The deeper form is **nationalization** — a government seizing privately or foreign-owned production assets, from the great oil nationalizations of the mid-twentieth century (Mexico in 1938, the creation of national oil companies across the Gulf in the 1970s) to more recent expropriations of mines and oil fields in Latin America. Nationalization tends to *reduce* long-run supply, because state-run producers often underinvest and foreign capital flees the risk, so the fields and mines slowly decline. The price effect is gradual and structural rather than a spike, but it is real: a barrel that never gets developed because investors fear expropriation is a barrel permanently removed from the future supply curve.

A third flavor is the **windfall tax or contract renegotiation** — a government that, rather than seizing the asset outright, raises royalties, taxes, or its share of production when prices are high. The effect is gentler than nationalization but points the same way: it raises the cost and risk of developing the resource, so over time fewer projects get the green light and the future supply curve shifts left. A barrel that a company decides not to drill because the host government keeps changing the deal is just as absent from the future market as a barrel sitting under a sanctioned border.

Resource nationalism is the political economy version of the convenience-yield idea that runs through this series: a country, like a refiner, derives a *convenience yield* from physically holding its own commodity in a tight market. When the world is short and prices are high, the value of having the physical thing in your own hands — feeding your own people, fueling your own industry — rises above the value of selling it abroad. So governments hoard, exactly as a merchant hoards inventory, and the world supply available to *everyone else* shrinks. The acute supply shock and the slow squeeze of resource nationalism are two expressions of the same underlying fact: when something physical and essential gets scarce, whoever holds it gains power.

For a trader, resource nationalism matters because it is *predictable in direction even if not in timing.* It clusters at the tops of price cycles — high prices are exactly when the political temptation to hoard, tax, or seize is greatest — so a long-running commodity boom tends to breed its own supply-side response not just from new mines but from governments tightening their grip. The structural effect is to make the supply curve *stickier on the way down*: once a field is nationalized or a project is cancelled, it does not come back quickly when prices fall, which is part of why commodity supercycles end in gluts that take years to clear. The acute and the structural mechanisms are linked: the acute spikes raise prices, the high prices trigger the nationalism, and the nationalism shapes the supply available for the *next* shock.

## The shock absorbers: why every spike eventually finds a ceiling

A naive reading of the inelastic-demand arithmetic would predict that any serious disruption sends prices to the moon and keeps them there. In practice, spikes almost always find a ceiling and most of them partly reverse, because the market has *shock absorbers* — physical and policy buffers that the simple elasticity model ignores. Knowing the buffers is essential, because the entire question of "fade the spike or chase it?" comes down to whether the absorbers can plausibly cover the loss.

**Spare capacity** is the first and most important. A few producers — chiefly Saudi Arabia and the UAE — deliberately keep some wells idle so they can raise output quickly when the world is short. This **spare capacity** is the oil market's central shock absorber: when a disruption hits, the question every trader asks first is "how much spare capacity is there to cover it?" In a year with 5 million barrels a day of spare capacity sitting in the Gulf, a 2-million-barrel disruption is annoying but coverable, and the price spike is modest and brief. In a year with almost no spare capacity — as in 2008, or 2022 — the same disruption is terrifying, because there is no cushion, and the price runs. The thinner the spare-capacity cushion, the more violent and durable the spike from the same lost barrels. This is why the same physical event produces a \$5 move in one year and a \$40 move in another: the buffer was different.

**Strategic reserves** are the second absorber. Major importing nations hold **Strategic Petroleum Reserves** — government-owned crude stockpiles, hundreds of millions of barrels, that can be released into the market to blunt a shock. The United States released tens of millions of barrels from its reserve in 2022 to cap the post-invasion spike, and the coordinated International Energy Agency releases added more. A strategic reserve is a chokepoint in reverse: where a blocked strait *subtracts* supply, a reserve release *adds* it, on government command. The catch is that reserves are finite and have to be refilled later, so they buy time rather than solving the problem — but buying time is often exactly what is needed to let a *timing* shock resolve.

**Demand rationing and substitution** are the slower absorbers. At a high enough price, even inelastic demand bends: factories switch from gas to oil, drivers cut discretionary trips, and over months, behavior adjusts. The 2022 European gas crisis is the clearest case — sky-high prices forced genuine demand destruction, as energy-intensive industry idled and households turned down thermostats, and that demand cut, alongside a frantic build-out of LNG import capacity, eventually pulled TTF back from ~339 to the 30s. The lesson: the same inelasticity that makes the *initial* spike so violent also guarantees that, given enough price and enough time, demand *does* eventually respond — which is why even the worst spikes have a half-life.

Put the absorbers together and you get a sharper trading rule than "commodities spike on bad news." The rule is: **the size and durability of a spike are inversely proportional to the available buffers.** Before you judge any geopolitical move, take the inventory — how much spare capacity, how much strategic reserve, how much room to ration — and weigh it against the barrels at risk. A large disruption against a fat cushion fades; a small disruption against an empty cushion runs. The 2019 Abqaiq spike faded fast partly because the Saudis had inventory and repair capacity to cover it; the 2022 oil and gas shocks ran because the cushions were thin and, for pipeline gas, almost nonexistent.

#### Worked example: spare capacity decides whether a disruption is a crisis

Two identical disruptions, two different years. In each, a conflict removes 2 million barrels a day from a 100-million-barrel market — a 2% cut.

**Year A** has 5 million barrels a day of spare capacity in the Gulf. Saudi Arabia and the UAE simply turn on idle wells and replace the lost 2 million within weeks. The net shortfall to the world, after the cushion responds, is roughly zero. The price might jump \$5 on the initial fear and then give most of it back as the spare capacity comes online. The *realized* supply impact was nil.

**Year B** has only 1 million barrels a day of spare capacity, because demand has grown and producers are already running flat out. Now the cushion covers only half the loss, leaving a net 1-million-barrel shortfall — a 1% cut to an inelastic market. Through the elasticity amplifier (1% ÷ 0.05), that maps to a ~20% price rise, and because there is no cushion left to reassure the market, the *risk premium* on top is large too. The price runs and stays elevated.

Same disruption, completely different outcome. **The barrels lost tell you almost nothing; the barrels available to cover them tell you almost everything.** This is the single most important number a geopolitical oil trader watches, and it is why "how much spare capacity?" is the first question on every crisis morning.

## Common misconceptions

**"A sanction removes the country's exports from the world market."** Usually false. The 2022 Russian oil sanctions removed almost nothing from *global* supply — the barrels re-routed to India and China at a discount via the shadow fleet, and the world price barely moved. Sanctions redistribute and discount barrels far more often than they delete them. The exception is when no physical alternative route exists (1973, or pipeline gas), where the bite is real.

**"The price spikes because barrels are actually lost."** Mostly false, at least at the moment of the spike. The initial move is dominated by the *risk premium* — the market pricing the probability of future losses — and by *freight and timing* shocks, not by realized lost reserves. When a tanker seizure adds \$4, essentially zero barrels have gone missing; the market has repriced the probability distribution of what *might* happen. The realized supply loss, if any, arrives later.

**"If I want exposure to a geopolitical spike, I should just buy a commodity ETF and hold it."** Dangerously incomplete. A long-only commodity ETF that holds futures suffers from **roll yield** — the slow bleed of rolling expiring contracts forward in a contango-shaped curve — which can erode your position for months while you wait for a shock that may never come. (See [roll yield and why long-only commodity ETFs bleed](/blog/trading/commodities/roll-yield-and-why-long-only-commodity-etfs-bleed).) Geopolitical risk is episodic and hard to time; paying carry every month to wait for it is often a losing trade. The professionals express geopolitical views through spreads, options, and the *shape* of the curve, not naive length.

**"Higher oil prices are always bad for the economy and therefore bad for everything."** Too simple. A geopolitical oil spike is bad for oil *consumers* (importing nations, airlines, drivers) but good for oil *producers* (exporting nations, energy companies). Commodities are the natural hedge precisely because they rise when the geopolitical news is bad — which is the whole reason allocators hold them. (See [oil, inflation, and equities: the energy linkage](/blog/trading/cross-asset/oil-inflation-and-equities-the-energy-linkage) for how the transmission works across asset classes.)

**"The biggest geopolitical risk is always a war."** Not necessarily. The Panama Canal was throttled in 2023–24 by a *drought*, not a war. A chokepoint can be closed by climate, by accident (the *Ever Given*), by piracy, or by a government's own export ban, as readily as by a military blockade. "Geopolitical" is shorthand for "anything that severs the physical flow," and nature and policy do that as surely as armies do.

## How it shows up in real markets

Pull the threads together with the events that actually moved prices, because the mechanisms only matter if you can see them in the tape.

**2022, the dual shock.** The Russia–Ukraine war is the richest single case study because it activated every mechanism at once. Oil: a *sanctions* event that turned out to be a *re-routing*, so Brent spiked to ~\$139 on the initial fear and then settled back into the \$90s as the discounted barrels found eastern buyers — the level barely changed, but the Urals–Brent *spread* blew out to \$25–\$30, and *that* was the trade. Gas: a *war-at-the-source* event with no easy re-route, so TTF went from single digits to ~339 euros and stayed elevated for over a year. Wheat: a *blockade* of Black Sea ports plus the *Turkish Straits* chokepoint, spiking the price to a record before a negotiated grain corridor partly reopened the flow. One war, three completely different price signatures — and the difference between them was entirely about *how re-routable each commodity was.*

**2019, the Abqaiq strike.** In September 2019, drones struck Saudi Arabia's Abqaiq processing facility, instantly knocking out ~5% of global oil supply — a genuinely large, real loss. Oil spiked ~15% on the Monday open, the largest single-day jump in decades. And then? Within weeks it had given almost all of it back, because the Saudis repaired the facility faster than feared and drew on inventories to keep shipments flowing. The episode is the perfect illustration of *timing versus structural*: a huge headline loss that proved temporary mean-reverted, exactly as a chokepoint timing shock does. The traders who faded the spike, betting on a quick repair, were right.

**2023–24, the Red Sea and the Panama drought, simultaneously.** Two of the world's chokepoints constricted at the same time for completely different reasons — missiles in the Bab-el-Mandeb forcing the Africa detour, and low water in Panama cutting transits. Crude itself stayed relatively contained because oil re-routes well and the world was well-supplied, but **freight rates and the price of *getting* things, rather than the things themselves, soared.** Container rates multiplied; LNG and product tankers re-routed; the cost of distance went up across the board. It was a reminder that a chokepoint shock often shows up first and most violently in *freight*, the price of moving matter, before it ever reaches the price of the matter itself.

**The recurring Hormuz premium.** Across the entire period, every flare-up around Iran and the Gulf added a few dollars of risk premium to oil and then bled it off as tensions cooled — a tanker seizure here, a threat to close the strait there. None of these closed Hormuz. None removed meaningful barrels. But each one breathed the risk premium in and out, and a trader watching the Gulf could see, in real time, the market pricing probability × impact on the most important chokepoint on earth.

#### Worked example: reading a geopolitical headline as a trader

A headline crosses the wire: "Tanker seized in the Strait of Hormuz; oil jumps \$3." How should a trader process this in the first sixty seconds?

First, separate *barrels* from *premium*. One tanker carries ~2 million barrels — about 0.02 days of world consumption, a rounding error. So essentially none of the \$3 is realized supply loss; nearly all of it is the *premium* — the market raising its estimated probability of a broader closure. Second, ask whether the \$3 looks *cheap or rich* versus the probability shift the headline implies. If a single seizure is a routine event that has historically blown over, \$3 of fresh premium may be an *overreaction* to fade. If it signals a genuine escalation — say, a state actor rather than a one-off — the premium may be *too small* and have room to grow. Third, decide the *expression*: an outright long pays carry and is blunt; a call option or a calendar spread isolates the spike risk while limiting the bleed if nothing happens.

The discipline is always the same: a geopolitical move is a *repricing of a probability distribution*, and your job is to judge whether the new price over- or under-states the true change in odds. **You are not trading the news; you are trading the gap between the premium the market just put on and the premium the situation deserves.** For how a single print or shock transmits across every market at once, see [cross-asset transmission](/blog/trading/event-trading/cross-asset-transmission-how-one-print-hits-every-market) and [geopolitics, elections, and unscheduled shocks](/blog/trading/event-trading/geopolitics-elections-and-unscheduled-shocks).

## The takeaway: how to price geopolitical risk

Step back and assemble the playbook, because the value of all this is in how it changes the way you read the world.

**Hold a chokepoint map in your head.** The single most useful habit is to know, for any commodity you trade, *where it comes from* and *what narrow places it must pass through* to reach its buyers. Oil: the Gulf, Hormuz, Malacca, Suez. Russian crude and Black Sea grain: the Turkish Straits. Asia-Europe everything: Suez and Bab-el-Mandeb. LPG and grain between oceans: Panama. When news breaks, your first question is "does this touch a chokepoint or a source?" — because that determines whether it is a real flow disruption or just noise.

**Distinguish re-routable from captive.** The deepest single lesson of 2022 is that the price response to a disruption scales with how *hard the commodity is to re-route.* Oil, fungible and shippable, mostly re-routes and spikes only briefly — so its disruptions are often *spread* trades (the discount barrel versus the premium barrel) rather than *level* trades. Pipeline gas, captive to its infrastructure, spikes hardest and longest, so its disruptions are level trades. Grain sits in between. Before you size a geopolitical trade, ask: can this barrel find another road? If yes, fade the level and trade the spread; if no, respect the spike.

**Separate timing shocks from structural ones.** A canal that reopens, a facility that gets repaired, a tanker that gets released — these are *timing* shocks, and they mean-revert. The Abqaiq spike, the *Ever Given*, most tanker seizures: all faded. A war that closes a strait for a year, a sanctions regime that permanently redraws the map, a nationalization that starves a field of investment — these are *structural*, and they do not mean-revert. Most of the alpha in geopolitical trading is in correctly sorting one from the other in the first hours, when the market often treats a timing shock as if it were structural (an overreaction to fade) or, more rarely, a structural shift as if it were temporary (an underreaction to chase).

**Respect the premium, and remember it breathes both ways.** A large share of the commodity price, much of the time, is paying for disruptions that never happen. That premium is built on probabilities, and probabilities move on diplomacy as much as on missiles. The premium that inflates on an escalation deflates on a de-escalation, and a trader who is long *only* the premium — with no real supply story behind it — is short an option on peace. Know whether you are trading the barrels or the fear, because they behave completely differently.

**And finally, the deepest point — this is why commodities exist in a portfolio at all.** Stocks and bonds are claims on a peaceful, functioning, well-supplied world; they fall when that world is threatened. Commodities are the physical things themselves, and they *rise* when the world is threatened — when a strait closes, when a war cuts a source, when fear floods the market. That negative correlation to bad geopolitical news is not a quirk; it is the entire reason a portfolio holds the physical asset alongside the paper ones. The barrel that becomes terrifyingly valuable when the strait closes is the one asset doing its job precisely on the day everything else is falling apart. A commodity is a physical thing forced through a financial contract — and geopolitics is the force that reminds you, violently and without warning, that the physical thing was always the point.
