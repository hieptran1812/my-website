> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — In 1973 the Arab members of OPEC used oil as a weapon: an embargo plus production cuts removed only a small share of the world's barrels, but because the economy could not stop burning oil overnight, the price quadrupled in months and created stagflation — the first time high inflation and a slump arrived together.
>
> - The setup: a postwar world of cheap ~\$2-3 oil, the United States flipping from exporter to importer, and pricing power passing from the Texas Railroad Commission to OPEC. The trigger: the October 1973 Yom Kippur War and the Arab oil embargo on the US and the Netherlands.
> - The consequence: oil ran from ~\$3 to ~\$12 a barrel, gas lines and rationing followed, and the cost shock raised prices while it cut output — the combination that broke the old economics. A second shock in 1979 (the Iranian Revolution) took oil to ~\$35.
> - The legacy is still with us: the Strategic Petroleum Reserve, the International Energy Agency, CAFE fuel-economy standards, the petrodollar system, and the permanent treatment of energy as a strategic asset, not just a commodity.
> - The one lesson to remember: a commodity the economy **cannot quickly substitute** is a geopolitical weapon — and supply shocks make commodities the one asset class that *gains* real value when inflation is supply-driven and stocks and bonds both lose.

In the autumn of 1973, the price of the single most important physical substance in the industrial world changed by a factor of four in a matter of months — and almost nobody saw it coming. For a generation before that autumn, a barrel of crude oil had cost something like two or three American dollars. It was so cheap that an American family thought no more about the cost of filling a car than about the cost of the air in its tyres. Petrol was a rounding error. Energy was free, or near enough, and the entire postwar economic miracle — the suburbs, the interstate highways, the petrochemical and plastics industries, cheap food grown with oil-derived fertiliser and hauled by diesel truck — had been quietly built on the assumption that it would stay that way forever.

Then, in October 1973, the Arab members of the Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries announced an embargo. They would stop selling oil to the United States and the Netherlands, the countries they judged most supportive of Israel in the war that had just broken out, and they would cut their total production month by month until their political demands were met. The actual volume withheld from the world was modest — a few million barrels a day out of a global market of around fifty. And yet the price did not rise by the few percent that the lost barrels represented. It quadrupled, from roughly \$3 to roughly \$12, and at the petrol pump in America it brought something the country had not seen since the war: lines of cars stretching around the block, stations rationing fuel or simply running dry, a national 55-mile-per-hour speed limit imposed to save gasoline, and a creeping, frightening new word that economists had not even had a name for — **stagflation**.

This is the story of how that happened, why a small cut in barrels produced an enormous move in price, why the result was a kind of economic sickness the textbooks said was impossible, and what the world built in response that still governs energy today. It is the founding crisis of the modern energy world, and it is the clearest case study in the entire commodity universe of a single, durable truth: a physical thing that the economy cannot quickly do without is not merely a commodity — it is a weapon, and the one asset that wins when the inflation is coming from a supply shock.

## Foundations: how energy became something that could be weaponised

Start with the spine of this whole series: **a commodity price is a physical thing forced through a financial contract.** Oil is matter. It has to be pumped out of specific places in the ground, carried by pipeline and tanker to specific refineries, turned into fuel, and burned by engines that cannot run on anything else without being rebuilt. Every link in that chain sits on a map, and a barrel is useless until it has physically arrived where it is needed. That physicality is the root of everything that follows, because it means the flow of oil can be *cut* — and a paper claim on a barrel that never arrives is worth nothing.

To understand why 1973 was possible, you have to understand three things that were true about the world by the early 1970s, none of which had been true a generation earlier. Define each one carefully, because the whole crisis is the collision of these three facts.

**Fact one: the United States had run out of spare capacity at home.** For most of the twentieth century, the world's swing producer — the body that decided the marginal barrel and therefore the price — was not in the Middle East at all. It was in Texas. A state agency called the **Texas Railroad Commission**, which despite its name regulated oil production, set monthly quotas (called *allowables*) for how much each Texas well could pump. When the world needed more oil, Texas opened the taps; when it needed less, Texas throttled back. As long as the United States had idle wells it could turn on, it controlled the global price by controlling the marginal barrel, and oil stayed cheap and stable. But American oil production peaked in 1970. After that, there were no idle wells left to open. The Texas Railroad Commission set its allowable to 100% — pump everything you can — and for the first time the United States had no spare capacity to put a lid on the price. The swing-producer role was now vacant, and it would not stay vacant for long.

**Fact two: the United States had become a net importer.** As domestic production peaked and demand kept climbing, the gap was filled by imports — increasingly from the Persian Gulf. A country that imports a commodity it cannot live without has handed a lever to whoever sells it. The United States in 1973 was no longer self-sufficient in the one substance its economy ran on, and the countries that now supplied the marginal barrel were concentrated in a politically volatile region with grievances of its own.

**Fact three: OPEC had grown up.** The Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries — OPEC — was founded in 1960 by a handful of producing nations who were tired of having the price of their one export set by the big Western oil companies (the "Seven Sisters") that dominated production. For its first decade OPEC was weak; the companies still held the contracts and the price. But as the 1970s opened, the producing governments were nationalising their oil, taking control of their own output, and discovering — for the first time — that with the United States out of spare capacity and the world dependent on their barrels, *they* now held the swing-producer lever that Texas had just dropped. The cartel that the previous post in this series describes as [the body that sets the floor under oil](/blog/trading/commodities/opec-plus-and-the-supply-curve-the-cartel-that-sets-the-floor) had just been handed the power to set the ceiling, too.

Put the three together. The world's largest economy could no longer regulate its own oil price, depended on imports for the marginal barrel, and those imports came from a group of countries that had just realised they collectively controlled the one substance the industrial world could not function without. The gun was loaded. All that was missing was a reason to fire it. That reason arrived in October 1973.

It is worth pausing on *why* the price had been so stable and so cheap for so long, because the contrast is what makes the shock comprehensible. From the 1930s to 1970, the marginal barrel — the last barrel needed to balance supply and demand, which is always the barrel that sets the price — lived in Texas, and Texas managed it deliberately to keep the price low and steady. A producing region that holds genuine spare capacity acts as a shock absorber: when demand jumps or a foreign supply is disrupted, it simply pumps more, and the price barely moves. The whole postwar system of cheap, reliable energy rested on the existence of that idle capacity. The instant it vanished — the instant American wells were already pumping flat-out and the spare barrels lived only in the Persian Gulf — the shock absorber moved offshore, into the hands of governments with their own agenda. Nothing about the *chemistry* of oil changed in 1970. What changed was *who held the spare capacity*, and therefore who held the price. That is the structural pivot the rest of the decade turned on, and it is the single most important thing to understand about why a weapon that had been unavailable for forty years suddenly came to hand.

#### Worked example: why the embargo did not need to remove much oil

The single most counter-intuitive fact about 1973 is that the price quadrupled even though the embargo removed only a small fraction of world supply. Here is the arithmetic that makes that possible, and it is the most important number in this whole post.

The world consumed roughly 50 million barrels a day in 1973. The Arab production cuts and the embargo withheld, at the deepest point, something on the order of 4 to 5 million barrels a day from the global market — call it a **5% to 9% supply cut**, and far less than that once non-Arab producers raised output to fill some of the gap; the *net* shortfall the world actually felt was smaller still, perhaps 4% to 5%.

Now bring in the concept that does all the work: **price elasticity of demand**. Elasticity measures how much the quantity people buy falls when the price rises. For a luxury good it is large — double the price of a handbag and sales collapse, because nobody needs a handbag. For oil in the short run it is tiny. Economists estimate the short-run price elasticity of oil demand at roughly −0.05, meaning a 1% rise in price reduces the quantity demanded by only about 0.05%. People still have to drive to work, heat their homes, and run the trucks that deliver food; they cannot stop overnight.

To clear a supply shortfall, the price has to rise until buyers *voluntarily* give up exactly that much consumption. With an elasticity of −0.05, eliminating a 5% shortfall through demand alone requires a price rise of about 5% ÷ 0.05 = **100%** — a doubling — for that shortfall alone, and the fear of a deeper cut, plus producers seizing the moment to reprice their oil, did the rest, taking the move to roughly 4x. So a single-digit-percentage cut in barrels mapped to a multi-hundred-percent move in price. **The smaller the elasticity, the bigger the spike from the same lost barrels — and oil's elasticity is about as small as it gets.** That is the entire mechanical heart of every oil shock in history, and it is why a commodity the economy cannot quickly substitute is a weapon rather than just a good.

## The trigger: October 1973 and the embargo

On 6 October 1973, on the Jewish holy day of Yom Kippur, a coalition of Arab states launched a surprise attack on Israel. The war itself lasted under three weeks. But the economic weapon it triggered would reshape the world for a decade. On 16 October, the Gulf members of OPEC unilaterally raised the posted price of oil by 70% — the first time the producing governments, rather than the Western companies, had set the price by fiat. The next day, the Arab members announced **production cuts** of 5% per month until their political objectives were met, and shortly after, a full **embargo** — a ban on oil exports — targeting the United States and the Netherlands specifically.

The distinction between the cut and the embargo matters, because beginners often conflate them. The **production cut** reduced the total volume of oil the world had to share — a genuine reduction in global supply. The **embargo** was a refusal to sell to two named buyers; on its own, an embargo mostly *re-routes* oil rather than destroying it, because the embargoed buyer simply purchases the same molecules from a different seller while the embargoing producer sells to a different buyer, and the trade map redraws without much net loss. (This is exactly the dynamic the [geopolitics and supply-shocks post](/blog/trading/commodities/geopolitics-and-supply-shocks-when-the-physical-cannot-flow) traces in detail for modern sanctions.) What made 1973 so violent was the *combination*: a real production cut that shrank the global pie, plus an embargo that created panic, hoarding, and a scramble in which every buyer tried to secure barrels at once. Panic buying on top of a real shortfall is what turns a 5% cut into a 4x price move.

The mechanics of the staged cut deserve a closer look, because they show a cartel learning to use a weapon in real time. The Arab producers did not announce a single, fixed reduction. They announced a *schedule* — output would fall by 5% each month, and keep falling, until their conditions were met. A schedule of escalating cuts is far more powerful than a one-off reduction, because it attacks the market through *uncertainty* as much as through physical shortage. A buyer facing a known, fixed shortfall can plan around it; a buyer facing an open-ended commitment to keep cutting has no idea how bad it will get, and so buys aggressively *now*, before the next cut lands. The schedule manufactured exactly the precautionary scramble that drove the price. It is a tactic every supply-side actor since has studied: the threat of escalation, credibly maintained, often moves the price more than any single action, because markets price the worst plausible outcome, not just the current one. The producers had discovered that *managing expectations* of future supply was a lever as powerful as managing the supply itself.

There was also a financial accelerant that beginners rarely appreciate. In 1973 there was no deep, liquid oil-futures market of the kind that exists today (NYMEX crude futures did not launch until 1983). Prices were set in long-term contracts and a thin spot market, so when panic hit, there was no transparent forward curve to anchor expectations and no easy way for the price signal to summon a hedging-and-arbitrage response that might have damped the move. The market was, in the language of this series, almost all physical and very little paper — and a market that is all physical, with no developed paper layer to absorb and redistribute risk, is far more prone to violent, illiquid lurches. The build-out of the modern oil-futures complex in the 1980s was, in part, a response to exactly this fragility: a financial layer that lets producers, consumers, and speculators trade the risk continuously, so that the price discovers itself smoothly rather than in terrifying jumps.

There is a second, subtler mechanism layered on top of the production cut, and it is the one that turned a serious shortage into a four-fold repricing. Once the producing governments saw the market panic — buyers bidding frantically against one another for scarce cargoes, spot prices at auction running far above the official posted price — they realised they had been *underpricing* their oil for years relative to what the market would now bear. The embargo did not merely subtract barrels; it shattered the old pricing convention in which Western companies set a low posted price, and replaced it with one in which the producing governments set a high one and the market validated it. A large part of the 1973 move, in other words, was not the physical shortfall at all but a permanent *re-rating* of the price level — the producers capturing value they had always been entitled to but had never had the power to take. This is why oil never returned to \$3: the shortage was temporary, but the transfer of pricing power was permanent.

The price did exactly what the elasticity arithmetic predicted, amplified by that re-rating. From around \$3 a barrel before the war, oil reached roughly \$12 by early 1974 — a near-quadrupling in about five months. The chart below traces the whole arc, with the pre-2000 era reconstructed from well-known annual averages (the data this series uses begins in 2000) and spliced to the real modern series so you can see both the 1973 jump and how it compares to everything that came after.

![Oil price from 1970 to 2025 on a log scale, marking the 1973 and 1979 shocks and the 1986 crash](/imgs/blogs/the-1973-oil-shock-when-energy-became-a-weapon-2.png)

Read the chart on a logarithmic scale, where equal vertical distances mean equal *percentage* moves, because that is how a price that ranges from \$3 to \$140 must be read. Three features carry the story. First, the dashed early segment shows the 1973 step — a near-vertical move from the cheap-oil plateau to a new, permanently higher level. Oil never went back to \$3; the world had been repriced. Second, the 1979 spike to the mid-\$30s, which we will come to, sits even higher. Third — and this is the feature people forget — the long slide into the 1986 crash, when the price the cartel had pushed up finally broke. The shocks were real and the prices were high, but they were not permanent, and the mechanism that eventually broke them is as important as the mechanism that created them.

For the American on the ground, the arithmetic of barrels and elasticities arrived as something concrete and frightening: the petrol station. Because price controls were in place — the US government had capped what stations could charge — the market could not clear through price the way the textbook says. When a market cannot ration scarce supply by letting the price rise, it rations by *queue*. Cars lined up for blocks. Stations sold fuel only on alternate days depending on whether your licence-plate number was odd or even. Some ran dry by mid-morning. A 55-mile-per-hour national speed limit was imposed to stretch the gasoline that existed. The psychological shock — that a rich, powerful country could suddenly not get enough of the fuel it ran on — was, in many ways, larger than the economic one.

#### Worked example: the household energy-cost shock

Translate the quadrupling into a family budget, because that is where a macro number becomes a felt experience. Take a 1973 American household that drove 12,000 miles a year in a car getting about 12 miles per gallon — typical of the heavy, un-aerodynamic cars of the era. That is 12,000 ÷ 12 = **1,000 gallons of gasoline a year**.

Before the shock, gasoline cost roughly \$0.36 a gallon, so the household's annual fuel bill was about 1,000 × \$0.36 = **\$360**. After the shock, with crude quadrupled and pump prices up sharply (to around \$0.55 to \$0.60 a gallon, restrained somewhat by price controls but still up well over 50%), the same 1,000 gallons cost roughly 1,000 × \$0.57 = **\$570**.

That is an extra \$210 a year — and in 1973, when median household income was around \$10,500, \$210 was about 2% of a family's entire pre-tax income, appearing suddenly and on a purchase they could not avoid. And gasoline was only the visible part: heating oil, electricity from oil-fired plants, the diesel in every delivered good, and the oil embedded in fertiliser and plastics all rose too, so the true hit to a household's real spending power was several times the petrol line alone. **A commodity you cannot substitute does not just cost you more at the pump — it raises the price of nearly everything you buy, which is why an oil shock is an inflation shock in disguise.**

## The consequence: the birth of stagflation

Here is where 1973 stopped being an energy story and became an economic revolution. To see why, you need to understand the world that the shock destroyed.

For the quarter-century after the Second World War, Western economists believed they had essentially solved the business cycle. The governing framework was a relationship called the **Phillips curve**, which described a stable trade-off between inflation and unemployment: when the economy ran hot, prices rose but jobs were plentiful; when it cooled, prices were stable but unemployment rose. The two moved in *opposite* directions. This gave policymakers a dial. If unemployment was too high, you stimulated demand — cut taxes, spent more, eased money — and accepted a little more inflation as the price of more jobs. If inflation got uncomfortable, you cooled demand and accepted a little more unemployment. The two problems were never supposed to arrive together, because they were two ends of the same seesaw.

The oil shock broke the seesaw. After 1973, the United States and most of the developed world experienced something the Phillips curve said was impossible: **high inflation and high unemployment at the same time**. Prices were galloping — US inflation, which had been running around 3%, surged into double digits — *and* growth stalled and unemployment climbed. The combination needed a name, and it got an ugly one: **stagflation**, a portmanteau of stagnation and inflation. It was the central economic disease of the 1970s, and it could not be cured by the demand dial, because the demand dial only works on demand problems. This was a *supply* problem.

The figure below shows precisely why an oil shock breaks the old logic, by contrasting the two kinds of downturn side by side.

![Before and after comparison of a demand slump versus an oil supply shock and why stagflation results](/imgs/blogs/the-1973-oil-shock-when-energy-became-a-weapon-3.png)

On the left is the world the Phillips curve described — a *demand* slump. When households and firms cut spending, two things fall together: prices ease (because nobody is bidding for goods) and output drops (because nobody is buying them). The cure is obvious and it works: push demand back up with looser policy, and both prices and jobs recover. On the right is what an oil shock does instead. Energy is an input to nearly everything — it is in the cost of making, moving, and selling every good. When the price of that input quadruples, the cost of producing everything rises, so firms charge more (inflation) *and* produce less because they and their customers can afford less at the new prices (recession). Prices go up while output goes down — at the *same time*. And now the demand dial is worse than useless: if you stimulate demand to fight the recession, you only add fuel to the inflation; if you crush demand to fight the inflation, you deepen the recession. There is no setting of the one dial that fixes both, which is exactly why stagflation was so politically and economically traumatic.

#### Worked example: the stagflation mechanism in one firm

Make the macro concrete with a single factory. A firm makes a product that sells for \$100. Its costs are \$40 of labour, \$30 of energy (the fuel to run the machines and the energy embedded in its raw materials), and \$20 of other inputs, leaving \$10 of profit.

Now the oil shock hits and the energy component quadruples — not all the way, since not every input is pure oil, but say the firm's energy cost roughly doubles, from \$30 to \$60. Its total cost jumps from \$90 to \$120. At the old \$100 price the firm now *loses* \$20 on every unit. It has two choices, and both are contractionary. It can raise its price toward \$120 to stay solvent — that is the **inflation**, and now its customers can afford fewer units. Or it can hold the price and cut production, laying off workers and idling capacity to stem the losses — that is the **recession**. In practice firms do some of both: they raise prices *and* cut output. Multiply that across an entire economy and you get exactly the impossible combination — the price level rising while production and employment fall. **An oil shock raises prices and cuts output through the very same channel, which is why it produces inflation and recession together rather than one or the other.**

This is the deepest lesson of 1973 for anyone trying to read markets, and it explains the strange behaviour of commodities that the rest of this post builds toward. *Where the inflation comes from determines which assets win.* The economics profession spent the rest of the decade rebuilding its theories around supply shocks and expectations, and it took the brutal interest-rate medicine of the early 1980s — deliberately engineering a deep recession to break inflation's back — to finally end the era. But the markets had already learned the lesson the hard way, and it is the lesson a commodity investor still trades on today.

There is one more transmission channel worth tracing, because it explains why the inflation proved so stubborn and why ending it was so painful. An oil shock does not just raise the price level once; it can raise inflation *expectations*, and expectations are self-fulfilling. When workers see prices rising and expect them to keep rising, they demand higher wages to protect their purchasing power. When firms grant those wages, their costs rise again, so they raise prices again, which confirms the workers' expectations and triggers the next round of wage demands. This is the **wage-price spiral**, and the two oil shocks lit it. The initial energy cost shock was a one-time event, but it set off a feedback loop in which inflation kept generating more inflation long after the original spike in the oil price had passed. By the late 1970s, double-digit US inflation had become *embedded* — built into every wage contract and price decision — and an embedded inflation does not fade on its own.

Breaking it required deliberately shattering those expectations, which is what the US Federal Reserve under Paul Volcker did at the turn of the decade. The Fed pushed short-term interest rates above 19% — an almost unthinkable level — choking off demand, throwing the economy into a deep recession, and pushing unemployment toward 11%. The point of the pain was to convince workers and firms that the central bank would tolerate a recession rather than accommodate inflation, breaking the expectation that prices would keep rising and therefore breaking the spiral. It worked: inflation fell from its double-digit peak to around 4% by the mid-1980s. But the cost was the worst recession since the 1930s, and the episode taught a generation of central bankers a lesson they have never forgotten — that the cheapest way to fight inflation is to never let it become embedded in the first place. The aggressive, front-loaded hiking cycles of the modern era, including the [2021-2023 inflation fight](/blog/trading/macro-trading/2021-2023-inflation-and-the-fastest-hiking-cycle), are the direct descendants of the trauma the oil shocks inflicted.

## The second shock: 1979 and the Iranian Revolution

Just as the world was digesting the first shock, a second one arrived. In 1978-79, the Iranian Revolution overthrew the Shah and convulsed one of the world's largest oil exporters. Iranian production collapsed during the upheaval, removing several million barrels a day from the market. As in 1973, the actual volume lost was a single-digit percentage of world supply — and again, the price response was wildly disproportionate, because the elasticity arithmetic is the same regardless of *why* the barrels go missing. Panic buying, precautionary stockpiling by governments and companies still scarred by 1973, and the fear of a wider Middle Eastern conflagration (the Iran-Iraq War broke out in 1980) drove oil from the low teens to roughly \$35 a barrel by 1980 — at the time, an almost unimaginable price.

The figure below puts the two shocks side by side so you can compare the magnitude of each jump.

![Bar chart comparing the price before and after the 1973 and 1979 oil shocks](/imgs/blogs/the-1973-oil-shock-when-energy-became-a-weapon-4.png)

The two shocks were different in character even as they were identical in mechanism. The 1973 shock was a *deliberate political weapon* — a cartel consciously withholding supply to achieve a foreign-policy aim. The 1979 shock was an *accidental* supply loss from a revolution, with no one at the wheel. But to the market, the distinction was invisible. Both were supply shocks; both hit an inelastic demand curve; both produced a price spike out of all proportion to the barrels lost; and both deepened the stagflation. The second shock, arriving on top of the first, embedded high oil prices and high inflation so deeply into expectations that breaking them required the most aggressive monetary tightening in modern American history.

The 1979 shock also revealed something important about how panic, not just physics, drives these events. The actual Iranian supply loss was partly offset within months as Saudi Arabia and others raised output, and the *net* global shortfall was small — on some estimates under 4% at its worst, and briefly. Yet the price more than doubled. The reason was that everyone who had lived through 1973 now reacted pre-emptively: governments topped up strategic stocks, refiners built inventory buffers, and traders bought ahead of a feared shortage. That precautionary demand — buying barrels not to burn but to *hold* against an uncertain future — added to the real demand and amplified the spike. A shock is never only the barrels lost; it is the barrels lost *plus* the barrels that frightened buyers rush to hoard, and the second number can dwarf the first.

#### Worked example: how precautionary hoarding amplifies a small shortfall

Quantify the hoarding channel, because it is the part beginners miss. Suppose a revolution removes 3 million barrels a day from a 65-million-barrel-a-day world — a real shortfall of about 4.6%. On the elasticity arithmetic alone (a −0.05 elasticity), that maps to a price rise of roughly 4.6% ÷ 0.05 ≈ **92%**, a near-doubling.

Now add the hoarding. Scarred by the last shock, refiners and governments decide to build their precautionary inventories by, say, 30 days of cover. If world consumption is 65 million b/d, building 30 days of extra cover across the system means buying an *additional* slug of oil — and even if that buying is spread over a few months, it adds several million barrels a day of *extra demand* on top of the existing demand, at the very moment supply is short. The demand curve does not just stay put while supply falls; it shifts *outward* as everyone scrambles for a buffer. That double hit — supply down and demand up at once — is why the 1979 price ran well past what the physical shortfall alone could justify. **The feared shortage pulls forward demand into a panic of hoarding, and that hoarding is often a bigger price driver than the original lost barrels — which is exactly why strategic reserves, held by calm public hands, are designed to break the panic.**

#### Worked example: a 1970s portfolio and the case for real assets

Now connect the macro story to the one thing this series ultimately cares about — how to read and position for it. Put yourself in the shoes of an investor holding the conventional "balanced" portfolio of the early 1970s: a mix of stocks and bonds. How did each piece fare through the decade of oil shocks and stagflation?

Take \$100 invested at the start of the 1970s. **Bonds** were a disaster. A bond is a promise to pay a *fixed* number of dollars in the future; when inflation runs into double digits, those fixed future dollars are worth far less, and as the central bank eventually jacks interest rates up to fight the inflation, the price of existing low-rate bonds collapses. Through the 1970s, long bonds lost a large share of their real (inflation-adjusted) value. **Stocks** were almost as bad in real terms. Although nominal share prices roughly went sideways, US inflation cumulatively roughly doubled the price level over the decade, so an investor in the broad market lost something like 40-50% of their *real* purchasing power by the end of the 1970s — one of the worst real-return decades for equities on record. The cost shock crushed profit margins, and the high rates needed to fight inflation crushed valuations.

Now the punchline. **Commodities and gold soared.** Oil itself went up more than tenfold across the decade. Gold, freed from its fixed \$35 peg in the early 1970s, ran to over \$800 by January 1980. A small allocation to real assets — the physical things whose rising prices *were* the inflation — did not just protect the portfolio; it was the only thing that made money in real terms. **When inflation is supply-driven, the commodity is not a victim of the inflation; it is the source of it, so owning the commodity is owning the one thing guaranteed to keep pace.** This is the single most important takeaway for an allocator, and the [cross-asset case study of the 1970s](/blog/trading/cross-asset/case-study-1970s-stagflation-commodities-win) is named for exactly this conclusion.

## The legacy: what the shocks built

The 1970s did not just reprice oil. They rewired the institutions, laws, and habits of the entire industrial world around a new premise: **energy is a strategic, national-security asset, not merely a commodity to be left to the market.** The policy architecture that came out of the shocks is still standing, and most of it dates from the few years right after 1973. The timeline below lays out the response.

![Timeline of the policy response: the IEA, the Strategic Petroleum Reserve, CAFE standards, and the non-OPEC supply surge](/imgs/blogs/the-1973-oil-shock-when-energy-became-a-weapon-5.png)

Walk through the pillars, because each one is a deliberate countermeasure to a specific vulnerability the shock exposed.

**The Strategic Petroleum Reserve (SPR).** The United States created, in 1975, a vast government stockpile of crude oil stored in salt caverns along the Gulf Coast — at its peak more than 700 million barrels, enough to replace months of imports. The logic is pure insurance against the inelasticity problem: if a future embargo or war cuts off supply, the government can release oil from the reserve to soften the shortfall and damp the price spike, buying time for the economy to adjust. The SPR is a national shock absorber, built precisely because 1973 proved how violently the price reacts to even a small physical shortfall.

**The International Energy Agency (IEA).** Founded in 1974 by the oil-importing nations, the IEA is a club of consumers built to do collectively what no single country could do alone: hold coordinated emergency stockpiles (members pledge to keep at least 90 days of net imports in reserve), share oil among members in a crisis, and produce the data and forecasts that make the market more transparent. If OPEC was a cartel of producers, the IEA was, in part, the importers' answer — an attempt to rebalance the power that 1973 had handed to the sellers.

**CAFE standards.** In 1975 the US enacted **Corporate Average Fuel Economy** standards — legal minimums for the average miles-per-gallon of the cars each manufacturer sold. The idea was to attack the *demand* side: if the inelasticity of oil demand is the amplifier that makes shocks so violent, then making the economy use less oil per unit of activity shrinks the amplifier. Over the following decades, fuel economy roughly doubled, and the amount of oil needed to produce a dollar of US economic output fell dramatically. This is demand destruction made permanent through engineering and law.

**The petrodollar system.** As oil prices quadrupled, the oil-exporting nations earned enormous dollar surpluses (oil is priced and paid for in US dollars worldwide). Those "petrodollars" were recycled back into the Western financial system — deposited in Western banks, invested in US Treasury bonds, and lent onward to developing nations. This cemented the dollar's role as the currency of global energy and, by extension, of global trade, a system the [petrodollar and dollar-dominance post](/blog/trading/finance/petrodollar-and-dollar-dominance) traces in full. The 1970s did not just reprice oil; they wired the world's most important commodity to the world's reserve currency.

**The dash for non-OPEC oil.** Most importantly for the market, the high prices set off a global hunt for oil *outside* OPEC's control. Fields that had been uneconomic at \$3 a barrel were highly profitable at \$30. The North Sea (between Britain and Norway) and Alaska's North Slope — both expensive, technically difficult provinces — were developed at enormous cost precisely because the price now justified it. New supply takes years to come online, which is why it did not break the price immediately. But it was coming, and when it arrived, it changed everything.

Notice the deeper logic that ties these pillars together: every one of them is an attempt to *re-create the shock absorber* that the United States lost when its production peaked in 1970. The SPR is a physical shock absorber — idle barrels the government can release. The IEA is a *collective* shock absorber — pooled stocks across many importing nations. CAFE and conservation are a *demand-side* shock absorber — a smaller amplifier, so the same physical disruption produces a smaller spike. And the dash for non-OPEC oil is a *supply-side* shock absorber — new spare capacity outside the cartel's hands. The whole edifice of post-1973 energy policy is the importing world deliberately rebuilding the slack that Texas used to provide, because the lesson of the shock was that a system with no slack is a system at the mercy of whoever holds the marginal barrel. When you read about a modern SPR release, an IEA coordinated action, or a new fuel-economy rule, you are watching a forty-year-old reflex: the consumer nations topping up the shock absorber so they are never again as defenceless as they were in the autumn of 1973.

## How it shows up in real markets: how the shock eventually broke

A commodity series that only told you how prices go *up* would be dangerously incomplete, because the most important lesson of the 1970s for a trader is the one that played out in the 1980s: **high prices are their own cure.** The same elasticity that makes the upside violent eventually works in reverse, and the cartel that had seemed all-powerful in 1973 was, within a decade, fighting a losing battle against its own success.

The figure below shows the two forces that did the breaking.

![Dual-axis chart of the falling oil price and the rising non-OPEC supply index from 1979 to 1986](/imgs/blogs/the-1973-oil-shock-when-energy-became-a-weapon-7.png)

Two slow-acting responses, both set in motion by the high prices themselves, converged on OPEC in the 1980s.

**Demand destruction.** Inelastic demand is a short-run phenomenon. In the *long* run, people respond to a permanently higher price by changing the very machines and habits that consume the commodity. Through the late 1970s and early 1980s, Americans bought smaller, more efficient cars (helped by CAFE); utilities switched power plants from oil to coal, gas, and nuclear; factories invested in efficiency; and homes were insulated. The result was a structural, permanent fall in the amount of oil the economy needed. US oil demand actually *fell* in the early 1980s even as the economy grew — something that had never happened in the cheap-oil era. The amplifier had been turned down.

**The non-OPEC supply surge.** Meanwhile, the North Sea, Alaska, Mexico, and other non-OPEC provinces — all greenlit when prices spiked — finally came on stream in the early 1980s, flooding the market with barrels OPEC did not control. OPEC's response was to cut its own production again and again to defend the high price. But every cut it made simply ceded more market share to the new producers, and there is a limit to how much a cartel will shrink itself to prop up a price that benefits its competitors. Saudi Arabia, which had absorbed most of the cuts, finally gave up in 1985-86, opened its taps, and let the price find its own level. It crashed — from around \$30 to under \$15 in 1986. The weapon had run out of ammunition, undone by the demand destruction and rival supply that its own high prices had summoned.

The pattern that played out from 1973 to 1986 is not a museum piece — it is a template the energy market keeps re-running. The clearest modern echo came in 2022, when Russia's invasion of Ukraine and the sanctions that followed threatened to cut a large exporter's barrels and molecules off from Western buyers. Brent crude spiked toward \$139 and European natural gas went vertical, and the move came — exactly as in 1973 — not from the world physically running out but from inelastic demand meeting a feared supply loss, layered with the same precautionary hoarding. And the response rhymed too: the United States released a record volume from the Strategic Petroleum Reserve (the very institution 1973 created), the IEA coordinated emergency stock releases among its members, demand destruction kicked in as high prices forced conservation, and a scramble for non-Russian supply re-drew the global trade map. The price spiked, then — as the demand and supply responses matured — fell back. If you understood 1973, you understood 2022 before it happened: same inelastic commodity, same weaponisation of supply, same self-limiting cure. The decade-by-decade specifics change; the mechanism does not.

The other lasting market lesson is about *who got hurt and who got paid* on the way down. The 1986 crash was as violent as the 1973 spike, and it ruined the high-cost producers and the oil-patch economies (Texas, the North Sea, the Soviet Union, whose oil-export revenues collapsed) that had over-invested at the top. A commodity price that has been pushed to an extreme by a supply shock is not a stable plateau to build a business on; it is a peak from which the fall, when demand and supply finally respond, can be just as dramatic as the climb. The producer who hedged forward at \$30 in 1981 — locking in the high price — survived; the one who assumed \$30 was the new normal and borrowed against it did not. This is exactly why the [producer-versus-consumer hedging post](/blog/trading/commodities/hedging-for-producers-vs-consumers-the-two-sides-of-the-trade) insists that a hedge is a risk-management choice and not a market view: the 1980s are full of graves dug by producers who treated a shock-inflated price as permanent.

#### Worked example: the demand-destruction response in numbers

Quantify the long-run cure so you can see why it broke the price. Suppose, after the shocks, the world faces a permanently higher oil price and responds over a decade. Three things happen to the supply-demand balance.

First, **demand falls** through efficiency. Say improved fuel economy and fuel-switching cut the oil intensity of the economy by about 20% over the decade — so where the world would have consumed, say, 60 million barrels a day at the old efficiency, it now consumes closer to 50. That is a 10-million-barrel-a-day chunk of demand simply engineered away.

Second, **non-OPEC supply rises**. Call it a 30% increase from the new high-cost provinces, adding several million barrels a day of supply that OPEC does not control.

Now do OPEC's arithmetic. To hold the price up, OPEC must keep total supply in line with the shrunken demand. With demand down 10 million b/d *and* rivals adding several million b/d, OPEC has to cut its *own* output massively — by a third or more — just to defend the price. At some point the cut is not worth it: OPEC is shrinking its revenue and handing customers to competitors to prop up a price that mostly benefits those competitors. So it stops cutting, the supply that demand can no longer absorb hits the market, and the price collapses. **High prices summon both less demand and more supply, and given enough time those two forces always overwhelm a cartel — which is why no commodity squeeze, however dramatic, is permanent.** The 1986 crash is the lesson that the most reliable bear case for any high commodity price is the high price itself.

## Common misconceptions

**Misconception 1: "The embargo caused the price spike by physically running the world out of oil."** No. The world was never close to running out. The Arab cut, net of other producers raising output, removed only a low-single-digit percentage of global supply. The spike came not from the barrels lost but from the *inelasticity* of demand — a 5% shortfall against a demand curve with an elasticity near −0.05 mechanically implies a price move of roughly 100% before you even add panic and repricing. The lesson is that for an inelastic commodity, the price move is the shortfall divided by a tiny number, so a small disruption produces a large move.

**Misconception 2: "Stagflation proved the economists simply didn't understand inflation."** The opposite. Stagflation was a *new kind* of inflation — supply-driven, not demand-driven — and the existing toolkit was built for demand problems. The economists' models genuinely could not produce inflation and recession together because they had been built in an era where all the shocks were demand shocks. 1973 forced a revolution in macroeconomics (the modern emphasis on supply shocks and inflation expectations) precisely because the old models were incomplete, not because the economists were fools.

**Misconception 3: "OPEC has had unlimited power over the oil price ever since 1973."** The 1986 crash is the refutation. A cartel can withhold supply and lift the price in the short run, when demand is inelastic and rivals cannot respond. But the high price it creates destroys demand and summons rival supply over the following years, eroding the cartel's market share until it can no longer defend the price. OPEC's power is real but cyclical and self-limiting — it is strongest right after a shock and weakest a few years later, when the demand and supply responses have matured.

**Misconception 4: "Commodities always protect you from inflation."** Only when the inflation is supply-driven, as in the 1970s. The [post on commodities as an inflation hedge](/blog/trading/commodities/commodities-as-an-inflation-hedge-and-when-they-are-not) makes the full case, but the short version is that commodities track inflation beautifully when *they* are the cause of it (a supply shock) and can lag badly when inflation is demand-driven and a strong central bank is raising real rates against a backdrop of ample commodity supply. The 1970s were the perfect storm for commodities; not every inflation is.

**Misconception 5: "Price controls and rationing protected American consumers."** They protected the *posted price* but not the consumer. By capping what stations could charge, US price controls prevented the market from clearing through price, so it cleared through queues, shortages, and the alternate-day rationing instead. The controls also blunted the demand-destruction and new-supply signals that a free price would have sent, arguably prolonging the shortage. A capped price on an inelastic good does not make the good cheaper to obtain; it just changes the currency of rationing from dollars to time spent in line.

## The takeaway: the playbook the shock wrote

The 1973 oil shock is the founding case study of the commodity world, and it leaves a playbook that an investor or analyst can still use today. Here is how to carry it forward.

**Watch the elasticity, not just the headline.** When a supply disruption hits a commodity, the size of the price move is governed by how easily the economy can do without the thing in the short run. Oil, natural gas, and food — things with tiny short-run elasticities — produce violent spikes from small disruptions. A commodity the economy can substitute quickly produces a muted one. Before you react to a supply-shock headline, ask: how inelastic is demand for this, and therefore how large is the multiplier on the lost barrels? That single question separates a market that will spike 4x from one that will barely move.

**Diagnose the *source* of inflation before you position.** This is the deepest lesson of the decade. When inflation is **supply-driven** — an oil shock, a war, a chokepoint closure, a crop failure — it raises prices while cutting output, stocks and bonds both lose real value, and the commodity at the centre of the shock is the one asset that gains, because its rising price *is* the inflation. When inflation is **demand-driven** — a hot economy, loose money — the dynamics differ, central banks can fight it with rates, and the commodity edge is weaker. The figure below is the whole positioning lesson in one grid.

![Matrix showing why commodities win in supply-driven inflation while stocks and bonds lose](/imgs/blogs/the-1973-oil-shock-when-energy-became-a-weapon-6.png)

The bottom-right cell is the entire trade: in supply-driven inflation, the commodity is not collateral damage, it is the cause, so the real asset wins while the paper claims on future cash flows — stocks and bonds — both lose. A small allocation to commodities or energy is, in this frame, not a return-chasing bet but an *insurance policy* against the one macro scenario that hurts a conventional portfolio most. That is why allocators since the 1970s have kept a commodity sleeve: it is the hedge that pays off precisely when everything else is failing.

**Respect the self-correcting cure.** The flip side of "high inelasticity means violent spikes" is "high prices mean eventual collapse." Every commodity bull market plants the seeds of its own end: the high price destroys demand (efficiency, substitution, conservation) and summons new supply (high-cost fields, new technology), and over a few years those two forces overwhelm even a cartel. When you see a commodity at an extreme high, the most reliable bear case is the high price itself. The 1986 crash is the eternal reminder that the cure for high prices is high prices — and the [supercycle post](/blog/trading/commodities/the-commodity-supercycle-decades-long-booms-and-busts) traces how this capex-and-depletion cycle repeats across decades.

**Treat energy as a strategic asset, because the world does.** The lasting institutional legacy — the SPR, the IEA, CAFE, the energy-security apparatus of every major government — exists because 1973 proved that a country dependent on imported, inelastic energy has handed a lever to its suppliers. Energy policy is national-security policy. When you analyse an energy market, you are never analysing a pure commodity; you are analysing a politically managed strategic resource, where governments hold reserves, write efficiency laws, and will intervene in a crisis. That overlay of state action is part of the price, and it is why a [supply shock](/blog/trading/commodities/geopolitics-and-supply-shocks-when-the-physical-cannot-flow) in energy moves the whole macro picture in a way no other commodity does.

The deepest truth of 1973, the one that ties this back to the spine of the whole series, is this. A commodity is a physical thing forced through a financial contract — and the more essential and the less substitutable that physical thing is, the more the contract on it behaves like a claim on the economy's survival rather than a claim on a luxury. Oil in 1973 was the most essential, least substitutable thing in the industrial world, and so the moment a cartel realised it could withhold the marginal barrel, oil stopped being a commodity and became a weapon. The price quadrupled, the old economics broke, and the world rebuilt itself around the lesson. For the investor, the lesson distilled to a single, durable rule: **when inflation comes from a supply shock in something the economy cannot quickly do without, the commodity is the one asset that wins — because the commodity is the shock.**
