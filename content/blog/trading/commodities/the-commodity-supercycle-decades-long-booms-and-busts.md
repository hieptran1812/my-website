That lag is the entire engine: scarcity drives a decade-long boom, the high price finally triggers a capex wave, the new supply floods in years too late, and a decade-long bust follows.

In the second week of May 2024, copper touched an all-time record of roughly **\$11,104 a tonne**. In March 2022, Brent crude spiked above **\$139 a barrel** as tanks rolled into Ukraine. In April 2024, cocoa hit an absurd **\$12,931 a tonne**, more than five times its long-run normal. Read the headlines on any one of those days and you would think each was its own freak event — a war, a drought, a squeeze. And in the short run, each was. But step back far enough and a different pattern comes into focus, one that operates on a timescale most investors never train their eyes to see: commodities, as a group, tend to swing through enormous, multi-decade arcs of boom and bust. Not the two-or-three-year wiggles of the business cycle, but generational moves that can carry a metal or a barrel up for a full decade and then grind it back down for another.

These long arcs have a name: **supercycles**. They are the reason copper spent the entire 2000s climbing and then the entire 2010s sagging. They are why a generation of mining executives got rich from 2003 to 2011 and then spent the next decade apologizing to shareholders for the projects they sanctioned at the top. They are why the same word — *commodities* — can describe both the best-performing asset class of one decade and the worst of the next. And they are not mysterious. A supercycle is the predictable result of one stubborn physical fact colliding with one stubborn financial fact: **demand for raw materials can surge in a single quarter, but the supply of raw materials takes the better part of a decade to respond.** That mismatch — the **capex and depletion lag** — is the engine of the whole thing.

This post is about that engine: how it works, why it produces decade-long booms and busts rather than gentle oscillations, what the great historical supercycles actually looked like in the data, and how a patient investor might think about positioning for one — including the genuine debate over whether the energy transition is seeding a new supercycle right now, or whether "this time" is the same false dawn it usually is.

The figure above is the whole post in one frame. A structural demand surge hits a supply that cannot move for years; the resulting scarcity drives a decade-long boom; the high price finally sanctions a wave of new mines and wells; that new supply arrives late and floods the market; the glut drives a decade-long bust; and the bust seeds the underinvestment that becomes the next cycle's setup. ## Foundations: what a supercycle is and why commodities have them

Start from zero. A **commodity** is a standardized raw material — a barrel of crude oil, a tonne of copper, a bushel of wheat — interchangeable enough that it trades on an exchange at a single public price rather than being negotiated cargo by cargo. (If you want the full treatment of how a physical good gets forced through a financial contract, that is the foundation of this whole series; see the within-series links at the end.) Commodities are **consumption and industrial assets**: they are bought to be *used up* — burned, smelted, eaten, built into things — not held in a vault to feel rich. That single fact is why their prices are so tightly tied to the physical pace of real-world activity, and it is the root of everything that follows.

Why does the "consumption asset" point matter so much for cycles? Because it ties the price of a commodity to a *flow* — the rate at which the world is using the stuff up — rather than to a *stock* that can be held indefinitely. A stock of gold sitting in a vault does not get consumed; its price is driven by what people are willing to pay to *hold* it, which is a monetary and psychological question. A barrel of oil or a tonne of copper, by contrast, is bought to be destroyed in use, so its price is driven by the balance between how fast the world consumes it and how fast the world can produce it. That balance is a *physical* race between consumption and production, and when one side of that race can sprint (consumption) while the other can only crawl (production, because of the build lag), you get the violent, sustained imbalances that are the raw material of a supercycle. The whole reason gold gets its own series in this body of work and we contrast against it is that gold, as a monetary asset, does not have this consumption-versus-production race at its core — and so it does not have supercycles in the same mechanical sense.

Now the key distinction this whole post hangs on. There are two very different kinds of "cycle" in a commodity price, and confusing them is the single most expensive mistake a long-horizon investor can make.

A **business cycle** is the ordinary, garden-variety oscillation that the whole economy runs through every few years: expansion, peak, recession, recovery, repeat. It typically lasts **2 to 7 years**. When the economy is expanding, factories run hot and demand for raw materials rises, so commodity prices firm; when a recession hits, demand falls and prices soften. Crucially, a business-cycle move in a commodity price **mean-reverts** — it undoes itself within a few years — because over that horizon, supply *can* respond. Producers have spare capacity they can switch on, inventories they can draw down or rebuild, and the slack absorbs the swing. The price wiggles, then comes home.

A **supercycle** is something else entirely. It is a structural move in the *level* of commodity prices that lasts **10 to 20 years** — and the business cycles ride on top of it like ripples on a swell. During a supercycle's boom phase, prices trend higher for a decade with the normal recessions only denting the climb; during the bust phase, they grind lower for a decade with the normal recoveries only briefly interrupting the slide. The defining feature is that a supercycle is driven by a **structural shift in demand that supply genuinely cannot answer for years** — not a passing wiggle that spare capacity absorbs, but a sustained, generational change in how much of a raw material the world needs.

![Business cycle versus supercycle: a short two to seven year wiggle that mean-reverts beside a structural decade-long move in the level](/imgs/blogs/the-commodity-supercycle-decades-long-booms-and-busts-6.png)

The figure above lays the two side by side. On the left, the business cycle: driven by the demand wiggle, absorbable by spare capacity, mean-reverting within a few years — read it as noise, useful for timing an entry but not for forming a decade view. On the right, the supercycle: driven by a structural shift, *unanswerable* by supply because of the capex lag, trending for a decade and then reversing for another — read it as the level, the thing a long-horizon investor positions for.

### Why "supply cannot answer" is the crux

Everything turns on that phrase. In most markets — most goods, most services — if demand rises and price goes up, supply responds quickly: a factory adds a shift, a software firm hires, a restaurant opens a second location. Price signals get answered in months, so prices stay close to the cost of production and don't run away. Commodities are the great exception, and the reason is brutally physical.

To produce more copper, you cannot simply add a shift. You have to **find** a new copper deposit (years of exploration), **prove** it is economic (years of drilling and study), **permit** it (years of environmental review, water rights, and local negotiation), **finance** it (billions of dollars committed against a price you cannot know), and **build** it — the shafts, the mill, the roads, the power lines, often an entire town in a remote place. From the moment a board sanctions a major new mine to the moment the first metal ships is, on average, **about a decade**. For a deepwater oilfield it can be 5 to 10 years; for a giant LNG terminal, similar. The supply of a major commodity is, in the language of economists, **inelastic in the short run**: it does not respond to price for years, because the response is a physical construction project measured in geological time.

That single asymmetry — fast demand, slow supply — is the seed of the supercycle. It means that when a structural demand surge arrives, the price cannot be quickly relieved by new production. Instead, the price itself has to do all the work of rationing the scarce supply among buyers, and it has to keep doing that work for as long as it takes new supply to arrive — which is *years*. So the price doesn't wiggle up and come home; it gallops up and *stays* up, for a decade, until the wave of new capacity finally lands.

#### Worked example: why a price doubling buys you a mine in ten years, not next year

Suppose copper demand surges and the price doubles from \$5,000 to \$10,000 a tonne. Every producer on Earth now wants to mine more. So a major miner finds a promising deposit and, after several years of drilling to prove it up, its board **sanctions** a new mine at, say, a cost of \$5 billion. Here is the timeline that follows. Years 1 to 2: permitting and financing. Years 2 to 5: more permitting, environmental review, and early construction. Years 5 to 9: building the shafts, the concentrator, the power and water and road infrastructure. Years 9 to 12: first metal, then a slow ramp to full output. So the supply you "ordered" the day the price doubled does not reach the market for roughly **ten years**. By then — and this is the cruel part — the high price has called forth not just *your* mine but dozens of others sanctioned in the same euphoria, and they all arrive within a few years of each other. The market that was screaming for copper in year 0 is drowning in it by year 11. **The capex lag means supply always answers the last cycle's prices, not the current one's, which is exactly why the answer arrives as a glut and turns the boom into a bust.**

### Two flavors: demand-driven and supply-driven supercycles

Not every supercycle starts on the demand side. There are really two engines, and they produce the same violent, sustained price move from opposite directions.

A **demand-driven supercycle** is the one we have been describing: a structural surge in *how much* the world wants — a giant economy industrializing — slamming into a supply that cannot grow fast enough. The 2000s China cycle is the archetype. These tend to lift the *broad* industrial complex together, because a single force (one economy's buildout) is pulling on copper, oil, iron ore, and coal all at once.

A **supply-driven supercycle** runs the other way: demand is roughly normal, but supply is suddenly and durably *constrained* — by a cartel withholding output, a war, a wave of resource nationalism, or a structural collapse in investment. The 1970s oil shocks are the archetype. These can be narrower (concentrated in the constrained commodity) but every bit as violent, because the same lag applies in reverse: once supply is cut, it takes years to bring new supply online to relieve the shortage, so the price has to ration demand the whole time.

In practice the two often blur together — the most powerful supercycles have *both* a demand surge and a starved supply base, which is precisely the combination the green-metals thesis claims for the present. The reason the distinction matters for an investor is that it tells you where to watch: in a demand-driven cycle you track the demand engine (a country's PMI, its credit growth, its construction); in a supply-driven cycle you track the supply constraint (OPEC discipline, capex trends, geopolitical risk). Misread which engine is running and you watch the wrong gauge.

## The capex and depletion lag: the heart of the machine

Let us slow down and look closely at the lag itself, because it is the part most investors underweight, and it is genuinely the whole story. There are two distinct slow forces at work, and they reinforce each other.

The first is the **capex lag**: the long delay between deciding to build new supply and that supply actually producing. We have already walked the mine timeline. But notice the second-order effect that makes it worse: because the build is so long and so expensive, and because the price you will sell into a decade from now is unknowable, producers are *reluctant to commit*. They wait for the price to prove itself, to stay high long enough to convince a board that this is a real structural shift and not a passing spike. That hesitation adds *more* years to the front of the lag. By the time the industry as a whole is convinced and the capex wave finally breaks, the boom may already be half over — and the supply that wave produces will land squarely into the bust.

The second is the **depletion lag**: existing supply is constantly *running down* underneath you. Every oil well's output declines a few percent a year as the reservoir empties; every mine's ore grade falls as the richest rock gets dug first; the best, easiest, cheapest deposits were found and exploited generations ago, so each new tonne tends to come from deeper, lower-grade, harder places. Depletion means that even just to *stand still* — to keep total supply flat — the industry has to keep investing heavily, year after year. So when a bust causes the industry to slash capital spending, it isn't merely "not adding" supply; it is allowing the existing base to quietly erode. That erosion is invisible for years and then, suddenly, the world is short — which is precisely how a bust sows the seed of the next boom.

![Capex and depletion lag: demand jumps in a quarter while a new mine takes about a decade from sanction to first metal, then output ramps slowly](/imgs/blogs/the-commodity-supercycle-decades-long-booms-and-busts-4.png)

The timeline above contrasts the two speeds. The price signal lands at year 0. The mine is sanctioned in year 1 to 2, fights through permits and finance to year 5, is built through year 9, and only reaches full output past year 12 — by which point the cycle that demanded it has long since turned. And underneath the whole thing, depletion keeps biting: old mines fade and ore grades keep falling, so even maintaining today's supply takes constant investment.

### Why the lag turns a smooth demand curve into a violent price cycle

Here is the part that is genuinely counterintuitive, and worth dwelling on. The *demand* surge that drives a supercycle is usually quite smooth — a country urbanizes over twenty years, its steel and copper and oil consumption rising steadily. There is nothing violent about it. Yet the *price* that results is extraordinarily violent, swinging from generational lows to records and back. Where does the violence come from, if demand is smooth?

It comes from the lag interacting with the smooth demand. Because supply cannot adjust in real time, the price has to clear the market all by itself in the short run. When demand creeps above what current supply can deliver, there is no quick relief valve, so the price doesn't rise a little — it rises *a lot*, because it has to choke off enough marginal demand to balance a market that has no other way to balance. Then, years later, the capex wave finally delivers, all at once, a slug of new supply that was sized for the boom. Now supply creeps *above* demand, and again there is no quick relief — the price doesn't fall a little, it *collapses*, because it has to incentivize enough producers to idle capacity (which they resist, because their capital is already sunk). Smooth demand, lagged supply, and a price that has to do all the adjusting alone: that is the recipe for a swing that looks far more dramatic than the underlying demand story would ever suggest. The supercycle is, in a real sense, *manufactured by the lag itself.*

#### Worked example: how the lag amplifies a modest demand surprise

Suppose the world consumes 25 million tonnes of copper a year and supply is essentially fixed in the short run. Now a structural shift lifts demand by just **3% a year above trend** for several years running — a modest-sounding number. In year 1, the world wants 0.75 million tonnes more than it can get. With no quick supply response, inventories get drawn down and the price has to rise enough to push that 0.75 Mt of demand back out of the market. Copper demand is fairly price-insensitive in the short run (you can't half-wire a building), so it might take a price rise of **30% or more** to ration away just a 3% shortfall. Repeat that for three or four years as the demand shift compounds, and the price can easily **double or triple** off a demand surprise that never exceeded a few percent a year. **A small, persistent demand surprise becomes an enormous price move purely because supply cannot answer it — the lag is an amplifier, turning a 3% demand wedge into a price that doubles.**

### The four phases of a supercycle

It helps to break the long arc into four recognizable phases, because each one feels different, and an investor's job is mostly to know which phase they are standing in.

**Phase 1 — Starvation (the bottom).** Prices have been low for years. The previous bust crushed capital spending; producers have stopped sanctioning new projects and are returning cash to shareholders instead. Depletion is quietly eroding the existing supply base. Valuations are washed out, the asset class is unloved, and the consensus is that commodities are structurally dead. *Nothing in the price tells you a turn is coming* — which is exactly the point. This phase is the setup, and it is invisible while you are in it. The supply that would relieve the next demand surge is, by definition, not being built.

**Phase 2 — Ignition (the early boom).** A structural demand surge arrives and meets the starved supply base. Because no new supply can come online for years, the price doesn't drift up — it accelerates. Early in this phase the move is met with disbelief ("a dead asset class is bouncing, it won't last") and is repeatedly faded by traders anchored to the old low price. They are run over. The producers, scarred by the last bust, refuse to believe the price is real and hold off on new investment, which only tightens the market further. This is the phase that rewards the patient investor who bought into the starvation.

**Phase 3 — Conviction and the capex wave (the late boom).** The high price has now persisted long enough that even the scarred producers are convinced it is structural. Boards finally sanction the wave of new mines, wells, and terminals. Capital spending climbs toward records. Generalist investors, seeing a decade of gains, pile in. "This time is different" narratives flourish to justify the high price. The market feels euphoric and safe — and it is the single most dangerous phase, because the capex being sanctioned right now is the supply that will eventually break the cycle. The seeds of the bust are planted at the moment of maximum optimism.

**Phase 4 — Glut and bust (the decade down).** The capex wave finally delivers — late, and all at once, because the projects sanctioned during the conviction phase reach completion within a few years of each other. Supply now overshoots demand, especially if the demand surge has also matured. With no quick way to take supply offline (capital is sunk; producers pump to recover it), the price collapses and stays depressed. Capital spending gets slashed in response. And so the cycle hands off to the next Starvation phase — the bust is sowing the underinvestment that will become the next boom's setup. The wheel turns, on a clock measured in decades.

The reason this framing is useful is that it reframes the investor's question from the unanswerable "what is the right price?" to the far more tractable "which phase are we in?" — and the tell for each phase is the *state of capital spending and sentiment*, not the price level.

## The historical supercycles: three big ones

Supercycles are not a theory; they have happened repeatedly, and the data is unambiguous. Economic historians generally count a handful of them since the industrial revolution, but three are worth knowing in detail because they map cleanly onto the engine we just built.

### Post-WW2 reconstruction (roughly late 1940s to early 1970s)

After the Second World War, the United States, Western Europe, and Japan rebuilt shattered economies and then boomed for a quarter-century. Cities were reconstructed, highway systems and suburbs were built from scratch, car ownership exploded, and a vast new middle class consumed steel, copper, oil, and concrete on a scale the world had never seen. That was a structural, generational demand surge — exactly the kind supply cannot quickly answer — and it drove a long commodities upcycle through the postwar decades. It is the cleanest "industrialization of a giant economic bloc" example before China, and it set the template: rebuild and urbanize a major economy, and you light a multi-decade fuse under raw materials.

What is worth noticing is how *long* this phase ran without producing the kind of headline price spikes we associate with the 1970s or 2000s. For much of the postwar boom, supply roughly kept pace — new oilfields in the Middle East, new mines opened across the developing world — so prices were firm but not explosive. The explosive phase came at the *end*, in the 1970s, when the easy supply expansion ran out and the constraints bit. That sequencing is itself a lesson: a demand-driven supercycle can simmer for years before the supply lag fully asserts itself, and the most violent prices often come late in the cycle, not at its start. An investor who had been waiting for a dramatic price spike to confirm the boom would have missed two decades of it.

### The 1970s (the inflationary, supply-shock supercycle)

The 1970s were a different flavor — driven less by a clean demand surge and more by **supply shocks** layered onto an already-tight, inflationary world. The 1973 OPEC oil embargo and the 1979 Iranian revolution quadrupled and then re-doubled oil prices; broad commodity prices soared alongside, and the era became synonymous with *stagflation* — high inflation with stagnant growth. The 1970s matter for our purposes because they show the supply side of the engine in its starkest form: when supply is suddenly *constrained* (by a cartel, a war, a geopolitical rupture) against demand that cannot quickly shrink, the same violent, sustained price move results. Commodities were the asset class that *won* the 1970s while stocks and bonds were crushed by inflation — a pattern worth filing away, and one the cross-asset series treats in depth (linked below).

The 1970s also illustrate the bust mechanism with unusual clarity, because the supply lag eventually cut the other way. The decade's sky-high oil prices did two things over the following years: they pulled forward an enormous wave of new supply from outside OPEC (the North Sea, Alaska, Mexico) that took most of a decade to develop, and they crushed demand by forcing efficiency — smaller cars, better insulation, a switch away from oil in power generation. Both responses arrived with the usual delay, and when they finally landed in the early-to-mid 1980s, oil collapsed and entered a long bust that ran into the late 1990s, bottoming near \$10 a barrel. That two-decade arc — a supply-shock boom in the 1970s, a glut-and-demand-destruction bust through the 1980s and 1990s — is a complete supercycle, and it set up the depleted, underinvested supply base that the China demand surge then ignited in the 2000s. One cycle's bust really is the next cycle's setup.

### The 2000s China supercycle (the big one)

This is the supercycle most living investors actually traded through, and it is the textbook illustration of the demand-surge mechanism. In December 2001, China joined the World Trade Organization. What followed was the fastest, largest industrialization and urbanization in human history: hundreds of millions of people moving from countryside to city, a construction boom of almost unimaginable scale, China becoming the workshop of the world. China's appetite for raw materials went vertical. By the peak of the cycle, **China was consuming roughly half of the world's copper, iron ore, and other industrial metals** — far more than its share of global GDP — and a huge and growing share of the world's oil.

That structural demand surge slammed into exactly the supply lag we have described, and the prices did exactly what the engine predicts. Let us look at the data.

![LME copper annual average 2000 to 2025 showing the 2003 to 2011 China boom, the 2011 to 2020 bust, and the 2024 record](/imgs/blogs/the-commodity-supercycle-decades-long-booms-and-busts-2.png)

The copper chart above is the supercycle in one picture. Copper averaged about **\$1,800 a tonne in 2003**, at the very start of China's WTO era. Over the next eight years it rose more than fourfold, to nearly **\$8,800 a tonne by 2011** — a relentless, decade-long climb interrupted only briefly by the 2008-09 financial crisis, which dented but did not break the trend. That is the boom phase: a structural China demand surge that supply could not answer for years, so the price had to do all the rationing, and it galloped. Then comes the bust. From 2011 to about 2020, copper sagged from \$8,800 down to the \$5,000-6,000 range, as the mines sanctioned during the boom finally delivered their supply *and* China's growth matured and slowed. Two decades of one structural story — up for a decade, down for a decade — exactly as the lag predicts. (The 2024 record near \$11,104 is a separate, newer story we will come to.)

Oil tells the same story on the same clock.

![WTI crude oil annual average 2000 to 2025 showing the 2003 to 2008 boom, the 2014 to 2020 bust, and the 2022 war spike](/imgs/blogs/the-commodity-supercycle-decades-long-booms-and-busts-3.png)

The oil chart traces the identical arc. WTI crude averaged about **\$30 a barrel in 2003** and rose to nearly **\$100 in 2008** — the China-driven demand boom, peaking with an intraday spike above \$147 in July 2008. The financial crisis crashed it briefly, but oil reflated and spent 2011 to 2014 stuck around \$90-100 as China kept buying. Then came oil's particular bust: the **shale revolution** (covered in its own post in this series) flooded the market with American supply just as the boom's demand was maturing, and from 2014 to 2020 oil collapsed — bottoming with the surreal *negative* settlement of April 2020, when the May contract closed at **-\$37.63** during the pandemic demand crash. Boom for a decade, bust for the next: the same machine, a different commodity.

There is a subtle but important difference in *why* each commodity's bust arrived when it did, and it teaches the lag's mechanics. Copper's bust was driven mainly by the demand side maturing — China's construction-led growth slowing — combined with the long-delayed arrival of mines sanctioned during the boom. Oil's bust was driven mainly by the *supply* side: the US shale revolution added millions of barrels a day of new, short-cycle American production, a supply response that arrived faster than a conventional one would have because shale wells take weeks, not years, to drill. In other words, oil partly escaped the slow conventional lag because a new, faster supply technology appeared — and that faster supply is precisely why oil's bust was sharper and earlier than copper's. The lesson is that a supercycle's bust phase is timed by *whichever side moves first* — maturing demand or arriving supply — and that anything which shortens the supply lag (like shale) brings the bust forward.

#### Worked example: how shale shortened oil's supply lag and brought the bust forward

Compare two supply responses to the same high oil price. A conventional deepwater project sanctioned in 2008 at \$100 oil might not deliver first barrels until 2015 or later — the classic 7-year-plus lag. A shale well, by contrast, is drilled and fracked in **weeks** for a few million dollars, and US producers can add rigs within months of a price rise. So when oil sat near \$100 from 2011 to 2014, the shale response didn't take a decade; US crude production roughly *doubled* from about 5.5 million barrels a day in 2010 to over 9 million by 2015 — a flood of new supply in just a few years. That faster supply is why oil's bust hit in 2014, years before copper's deeper bottom. **Shale compressed oil's supply lag from years to months, and a shorter lag means a faster, earlier bust — the clearest proof that the lag, not the price level, sets the timing of a supercycle's turn.**

The two together make the point that a supercycle is a *cross-commodity* phenomenon, not a quirk of one market.

![Copper and oil together 2000 to 2025 as a supercycle gauge showing the shared 2000s boom on a dual axis](/imgs/blogs/the-commodity-supercycle-decades-long-booms-and-busts-5.png)

Plotting copper (left axis, dollars per tonne) and WTI oil (right axis, dollars per barrel) on the same timeline shows them moving together through the 2000s boom — the shared shaded region. When a single structural force (China's industrialization) is driving demand across *all* industrial raw materials at once, the individual commodity stories rhyme, and the whole complex rises and falls as one. That co-movement is itself the signature of a supercycle: a business-cycle wiggle hits commodities unevenly, but a supercycle lifts the entire basket on the same multi-decade tide. (The cross-asset and macro series, linked below, build on exactly this co-movement to read commodities as a macro signal.)

#### Worked example: China's demand share and the price impact

Walk the 2000s China copper story in numbers. Around 2000, China consumed perhaps **13% of the world's refined copper**. By the cycle's peak it was consuming **roughly 50%**. Over that span, world copper demand grew from about 15 million tonnes to over 20 million tonnes a year — and essentially *all* of that net growth came from China. So China didn't just add demand; it became the *entire marginal buyer*, the source of every incremental tonne the world's mines had to find. Now apply the lag. The world's copper mines, which had been starved of investment through the cheap-copper 1990s (copper sat under \$2,000 a tonne for years), could not possibly find and build enough new capacity in time. So the price had to ration China's surge against a near-fixed supply, and copper went from \$1,800 to \$8,800 — a **4.9x move** — over eight years. **When a single giant economy goes from a seventh of world demand to a half, and supply needs a decade to respond, the price has no choice but to multiply — that is the China supercycle in one sentence.**

## Common misconceptions

The supercycle idea is widely misunderstood, and the misunderstandings are exactly where investors lose money. Here are the most common, each corrected with a number or a fact.

**Misconception 1: "A big price move means we're in a supercycle."** No. Most big commodity moves are business-cycle wiggles or one-off supply shocks, not supercycles. Cocoa's run to \$12,931 a tonne in 2024 was a West African disease-and-weather shock, not a structural decade-long shift in chocolate demand — and it is already partly reversing. The test of a supercycle is not the *size* of the move but its *structural driver*: is a generational, sustained shift in demand (or a durable supply constraint) at work, one that supply genuinely cannot answer for years? If the move can be relieved by spare capacity or a normal harvest, it is a wiggle, not a cycle.

**Misconception 2: "High prices fix high prices quickly."** They do — but on a *decade* lag, not a quarterly one. The old trader's saying "the cure for high prices is high prices" is true, but the cure takes 7 to 15 years to arrive because that is how long new supply takes to build. People who shorted copper in 2005 because "\$3,700 is obviously too high and supply will respond" were right about the eventual response and catastrophically wrong about the timing — copper kept climbing to \$8,800 over the next six years before the supply finally landed.

**Misconception 3: "The bust is the end of the story."** The bust is the *seed* of the next boom. A decade of low prices crushes capital spending; depletion quietly erodes the existing supply base; nobody sanctions new mines because the price doesn't justify it. So when the next structural demand surge arrives, it lands on a supply base that has been starved for a decade — and the lag means it will take *another* decade to catch up. The bust manufactures the scarcity that powers the recovery. This is why the most important number in the whole supercycle isn't the peak price; it's the years of underinvestment that preceded the bottom.

**Misconception 4: "Supercycles are predictable and easy to ride."** They are *explicable* but not easy. The boom phase contains gut-wrenching business-cycle drawdowns (copper fell over 60% in the 2008 crisis *inside* the 2000s boom), and the turning points are only obvious in hindsight. The hard part isn't understanding the engine; it's holding a position through the wiggles while it plays out over a decade — and not mistaking the late-cycle euphoria for the early-cycle setup. A useful discipline is to write down, in advance, what would tell you the cycle has turned (a sustained capex boom and broad euphoria for a top; years of slashed investment and universal despair for a bottom) so that you are reacting to the *state of the cycle* rather than to the last few months of price, which is exactly the data that whips investors in and out at the worst moments.

**Misconception 5: "Every commodity moves together in a supercycle."** Mostly, but not perfectly. A demand-driven supercycle (like the 2000s) lifts the broad industrial complex together because one force drives them all. But the timing and depth differ by commodity because each has its own supply lag and its own supply shock: oil's bust was deepened and brought forward by shale; copper's bust was shallower because its supply lag is even longer. The co-movement is the signature, but the dispersion within it is where the relative-value opportunity lives.

## How it shows up in real markets

Let us connect the engine to how an investor actually experiences and uses it, with real dates and numbers.

### Reading where you are in the cycle

Before you can position for a supercycle, you have to locate yourself within one, and the honest truth is that this is hard *in real time* and easy only in hindsight. The price chart that looks like an obvious decade-long boom on a finished chart felt, while it was happening, like a series of confusing rallies and scary drawdowns. So an investor needs a diagnostic that does not depend on knowing the future shape of the curve. That diagnostic exists, and it is not the price.

The single most useful diagnostic is not the price level but the **state of supply investment**. At a supercycle *bottom*, you will see: years of falling or flat capital spending across the industry; mining and energy companies trading at low valuations and being punished by shareholders for any new project; a widespread narrative that "commodities are dead" or "structurally in decline." That was the world of 2015-2020 — oil majors slashing capex after the price crash, miners writing off the projects they overpaid for at the 2011 top, and an entire generation of investors who had decided commodities were uninvestable. *That* is what a setup looks like, and it never feels like one, because the price is depressing and the news is bleak.

At a supercycle *top*, you will see the mirror image: capital spending at record highs; producers sanctioning ever-more-marginal projects; "this time is different" narratives explaining why the high price is permanent; and a flood of generalist investors piling into the asset class after a decade of gains. That was 2011 — peak mining capex, peak optimism, peak China extrapolation — right before the decade-long bust. The euphoria that makes the top feel safe is precisely the thing that makes it dangerous.

#### Worked example: early-cycle versus late-cycle entry, in P&L

Take two investors in copper-related assets. **Investor A** buys near the supercycle bottom — say copper at \$5,000 a tonne in 2016, when the news is grim, valuations are washed out, and capex has been falling for years. **Investor B** buys near the top — copper at \$8,800 in 2011, at peak euphoria, when "China will buy everything forever" is the consensus. Fast-forward. Investor A holds through the bottoming process and into the next leg; by 2024 copper is near \$9,150 and touched \$11,104 — a gain of roughly **+83%** on the metal alone, before the leverage that mining equities add. Investor B, who bought at \$8,800 in 2011, watched copper fall to under \$5,000 by 2016 — a drawdown of roughly **-43%** — and had to wait until 2021, a *full decade*, just to get back to breakeven on the metal. Same asset, same engine; the only difference is *where in the cycle* each one bought. **The supercycle rewards buying the capex-starvation bottom and punishes buying the euphoric top, and the gap between the two outcomes is measured not in percent but in lost decades.**

### The 2024 copper record: a new cycle, or an echo?

Look again at the copper chart and the 2024 record near \$11,104 stands apart from the 2000s arc. Is it the start of a *new* supercycle? This is exactly the kind of "this time" debate that surrounds every potential cycle, and it is worth taking seriously rather than dismissing. The bull case: the energy transition is a genuine new structural demand source (an electric car uses roughly 4x the copper of a petrol car, and the grid buildout needs millions more tonnes), arriving on top of a supply base that endured a decade of underinvestment after the 2011-2020 bust — the classic setup. The bear case: China's old construction-driven demand engine is sputtering as its property bubble deflates, so green demand has to replace a fading old source rather than adding to it, and "green demand" forecasts have a habit of disappointing. The honest answer is that we will only know in hindsight — which is *always* true at the start of a supercycle, and is exactly why the early innings feel so uncertain.

### How a supercycle view actually gets expressed

Knowing a supercycle is coming is one thing; capturing it is another, and the instrument you choose matters enormously because of one quirk we cover at length elsewhere in this series: long-only commodity exposure *bleeds*. If you simply buy and roll front-month futures, you pay a roll cost every time the curve is in **contango** (the shape where later-dated contracts are more expensive than nearby ones), and over a decade that drag can eat much of the spot gain. So a patient supercycle investor rarely expresses the view through naive long-only futures alone. The common expressions, each with a different risk profile:

- **Producer equities** — shares in the miners and energy companies. These give *leveraged* exposure to the commodity (a company with high fixed costs sees its profits swing far more than the underlying price) and avoid the roll bleed, but they add company-specific risk: bad management, a flooded mine, a debt blowup. In a supercycle's early innings, washed-out producer equities bought at the starvation bottom are often the highest-return expression — and the most volatile.
- **Physical and physical-backed holdings** — owning the metal, or a fund that holds it. No roll bleed, no company risk, but storage costs and no income.
- **Futures with curve awareness** — holding the commodity through futures while paying attention to the curve shape, rolling into backwardation (the favorable shape) where possible.
- **Royalty and streaming companies** — firms that finance miners in exchange for a cut of future production, giving commodity-price exposure with less operating risk.

The deeper point is that *the same supercycle can make you rich or poor depending on the wrapper*, because a decade-long holding period is exactly the horizon over which roll costs, leverage, and company risk compound. A correct macro call expressed through the wrong instrument is a common way to be right and still lose. (The roll-yield and curve mechanics that govern this are covered in their own posts in this series.)

### Why underinvestment is the quiet setup

The most important real-market dynamic, and the one easiest to miss, is the slow build-up of underinvestment during a bust. Through the 2014-2020 oil bust and the 2011-2020 mining bust, the industry did exactly what depressed prices tell it to do: it cut capital spending hard, returned cash to shareholders, and stopped sanctioning long-lead projects. Oil majors' upstream capex fell sharply after 2014; few major new copper mines were sanctioned in the 2010s. None of this showed up in prices at the time — that is the point. Underinvestment is invisible while it happens and only reveals itself years later, when the depleting old supply base can no longer meet a recovering demand and the world suddenly discovers it is short. The 2021-2022 commodity surge across energy and metals was, in significant part, that bill coming due. **The seed of every boom is planted in the indifference of the preceding bust.**

#### Worked example: the underinvestment setup in a single mine's arithmetic

Consider the copper industry's choice during the 2015-2020 bust, in round numbers. To keep global copper supply merely *flat*, the industry needs to bring on roughly 1 million tonnes of *new* mine capacity every year, just to offset depletion and grade decline at existing mines — that is, replace what naturally runs down. A single large new mine might add 300,000 tonnes a year and cost \$5 billion and take a decade to build. During the bust, with copper at \$5,000 and shareholders demanding buybacks not capex, the industry sanctioned far fewer of these than the 3-or-so per year needed just to stand still. Run that shortfall for five years and the industry has under-built by well over a million tonnes of annual capacity that simply does not exist — and cannot be conjured for another decade. So when demand recovers, the cupboard is bare, and the price has to ration a structural shortage. **A decade of skipped mines is a decade of supply that will not exist when the next demand surge arrives — which is precisely why the bust is the setup, and the depressing bottom is the opportunity.**

## Where the next supercycle might come from

The question every long-horizon investor wants answered: are we at the start of a new supercycle right now? The most-discussed thesis combines two of the forces we have built.

![Next supercycle thesis: electrification adds a metals demand wedge while oil gas and mining underinvestment starve supply, converging on a tight market](/imgs/blogs/the-commodity-supercycle-decades-long-booms-and-busts-7.png)

The diagram above maps the thesis. On the demand side, the **energy transition** is a potential new structural surge: electric vehicles (each using several times the copper of a petrol car), the buildout of wind, solar, and the electrical grid to carry it all, and the metals — copper, lithium, nickel — that decarbonization requires in enormous tonnage. On the supply side, a decade of **chronic underinvestment**: mining capex starved through the 2010s bust, and oil and gas upstream spending cut hard after 2014 and never fully restored. Both forces converge on a structurally tight market — green metals demand surging into a supply that, by the same 7-to-15-year lag, cannot answer quickly. If that holds, it is the setup for a new decade-long upcycle. (The dedicated post on the energy transition and the green-metals demand shock, linked below, drills into this thesis in detail.)

But the figure deliberately ends on a fork, not a conclusion, because **"this time" is always debated** — and usually wrong. There is a real tension in the green-supercycle thesis. The demand is genuine but *uncertain in timing*: EV adoption curves keep getting revised, grid buildouts move at the speed of permitting, and a recession can defer green capex for years. And there is a darker possibility the thesis itself raises: if the metals shortage drives the cost of the transition up, you get **"green inflation"** — decarbonization made more expensive by the very scarcity it creates — which could slow demand and short-circuit the cycle. The intellectually honest position is that the *ingredients* of a supercycle (a new structural demand source plus a starved supply base) are visibly present, but whether they combine into a genuine decade-long boom or fizzle into another false dawn will only be clear in hindsight. That uncertainty is not a flaw in the analysis; it is the permanent condition of standing at the start of a possible supercycle.

## The takeaway: how a long-horizon investor positions for a supercycle

Pull it all together into something you can actually use. The supercycle is the longest, slowest, and most powerful force in commodity prices, and it operates on a timescale most investors never train themselves to see. Here is how to think about it.

**Watch supply investment, not price.** The price tells you where you have *been*; the state of capital spending tells you where you are *going*. Years of falling capex, washed-out valuations, and "commodities are dead" narratives mark the setup — the capex-starvation bottom — even though (especially though) it feels like the worst possible time to buy. Record capex, marginal projects getting sanctioned, and "this time is different" euphoria mark the danger zone near the top. The cycle's most reliable tell is that the bottom feels hopeless and the top feels safe.

**Position early, into the starvation, not late, into the euphoria.** The entire asymmetry of the supercycle, as the worked P&L showed, is that buying the depressed bottom and buying the euphoric top are the same asset with opposite outcomes — one compounds for a decade, the other spends a decade getting back to breakeven. The discipline a supercycle demands is the willingness to buy what nobody wants, when the news is grim and the chart has been falling for years, *because* that is when supply has been starved and the next surge has nowhere to go but price.

**Respect the lag in both directions.** Do not short a structural boom because the price "looks too high" — the supply that will eventually relieve it is a decade away, and the price can multiply while you wait. And do not extrapolate a boom forever — the capex wave you can already see being sanctioned *will* land, late and all at once, and turn the boom into a bust. The lag is the clock; learn to read it.

**Hold through the wiggles.** A supercycle plays out over 10 to 20 years and contains brutal business-cycle drawdowns inside it (copper fell 60% in 2008 *during* the 2000s boom). The engine works on a decade horizon; the patience to hold a structural position through a cyclical drawdown is the price of admission. Most investors who understand supercycles intellectually still fail to capture them, because they get shaken out by a wiggle they mistook for the end of the cycle.

**Stay humble about "this time."** Every potential new supercycle is debated, and most debates resolve against the bulls — false dawns are more common than real cycles. The green-metals thesis has the right *shape* (new structural demand, starved supply), but its timing and magnitude are genuinely uncertain, and the honest investor sizes the position to survive being early and wrong about the timing, not to bet the house on a forecast.

**Mind the wrapper as much as the call.** A correct decade-long view can still lose money if it is expressed through an instrument that bleeds — naive long-only futures rolling through years of contango, or a single over-levered producer that blows up before the thesis plays out. The supercycle is a holding-period bet measured in years, and over that horizon roll costs, leverage, and company-specific risk compound just as relentlessly as the spot price does. Match the wrapper to the holding period: a structural multi-year view usually wants washed-out producer equities or curve-aware exposure bought at the starvation bottom, not a front-month future held mechanically for a decade.

The deepest insight the supercycle offers is this: commodity prices are governed by a clock that runs on geological and engineering time — the **decade it takes to build a mine or an oilfield** — colliding with a demand that can shift in a quarter. That single mismatch manufactures booms that run too long and busts that run too deep, and it means the seeds of every recovery are sown in the indifference of the preceding collapse. A patient investor who internalizes that — who learns to buy the starvation and fear the euphoria, and who respects the decade-long lag in both directions — is reading the one cycle that the rest of the market is too short-sighted to see.

And that short-sightedness is the durable edge here. Most market participants are structurally unable to act on a decade horizon: traders are judged quarterly, funds face annual redemptions, and corporate boards are rewarded for buying back stock in the bust and sanctioning megaprojects in the boom — the exact opposite of what the cycle rewards. The supercycle is not a secret; the data is public and the mechanism is simple. What is genuinely scarce is the *temperament* to buy a depressed, unloved asset because supply has been starved, and to hold it through years of doubt while the slow physics of mine-building grinds the imbalance into a price. The engine is mechanical; the discipline to ride it is not — and that gap between knowing and doing is precisely where the long-horizon return lives.
