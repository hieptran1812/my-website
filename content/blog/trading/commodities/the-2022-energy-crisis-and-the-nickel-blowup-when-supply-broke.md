> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — 2022 is the year the physical supply chain broke and commodity markets stopped functioning normally, because a single war severed one of the world's top exporters of energy, metals, and grain from the West all at once.
>
> - **Energy:** European gas at the Dutch TTF hub spiked from about 9 euros/MWh in 2020 to roughly **339 euros/MWh** in August 2022 — about ten times a normal level — as Russian pipeline gas was cut and Europe had to outbid Asia for every scarce LNG cargo.
> - **Metals:** the LME nickel price ran past **\$100,000 a tonne** intraday on 8 March 2022; the exchange halted trading and **cancelled about \$3.9 billion** of trades — a major venue voided a day of deals to keep its members solvent.
> - **Grain:** wheat hit a record near **\$12.94 a bushel** because Ukraine and Russia together are a breadbasket, and the Black Sea ports that ship their grain were blockaded.
> - The one lesson to remember: commodities are the asset class most exposed to a real war, because a physical supply chain has no redundancy — and in a true supply break the curve slams backwardated, correlations break, and the only hedge is owning the physical-linked asset *before* the break.

On the morning of 8 March 2022, traders watching the London Metal Exchange saw something that is not supposed to happen. The price of nickel, a grey industrial metal that goes into stainless steel and electric-car batteries and normally trades around \$25,000 a tonne, was not just rising — it was vertical. It had already doubled the day before. Now it blew past \$50,000, past \$80,000, and touched **\$101,365** a tonne in a matter of hours. Margin calls running into the billions of dollars were going out to firms that could not possibly pay them. And then the exchange did the unthinkable: it stopped. The LME halted nickel trading, and a few hours later it announced that every trade executed that morning would be **cancelled** — roughly \$3.9 billion of deals erased as if they had never occurred.

This was not an isolated glitch. It was one symptom of a much larger illness that gripped the entire commodity complex in 2022. Two weeks earlier, on 24 February, Russian tanks had crossed into Ukraine, and the Western sanctions response began to sever Russia — one of the planet's top exporters of crude oil, natural gas, nickel, aluminum, palladium, and wheat — from the financial and shipping infrastructure of the West. Within days, European natural gas had gone parabolic, wheat had hit a record, and oil had spiked toward \$139. The markets that price the physical world were all screaming the same thing at once: the stuff is not where it needs to be, and it cannot get there.

This post is about that year — the year the physical supply chain broke. We will walk through all three breaks, energy and metals and grain, because they are the same story told in three commodities. And we will end on the only thing that matters for a curious investor: the playbook for what a real supply break does to a market, and what — if anything — protects you when it happens.

## Foundations: why a war breaks commodities and nothing else

Start from the spine that runs through this whole series: **a commodity price is a physical thing forced through a financial contract.** When you buy a futures contract on natural gas or nickel or wheat, you are not buying an idea — you are buying a paper claim on a real quantity of matter that, somewhere, has to be pumped, mined, or grown, and then physically carried to whoever needs it. The contract lives in a database; the thing underneath it is molecules. And molecules have a geography, a supply chain, and a hard physical limit on how fast they can move.

That single fact is why commodities are the asset class most exposed to a real war. Walk through the reasons carefully, because the rest of the post is just these principles playing out three times.

**Reason one: production is concentrated, and one country can be a top exporter of many things at once.** Russia is the standout. Before 2022 it supplied roughly 40% of Europe's natural gas, about 10% of the world's crude oil, close to 10% of the world's nickel and a meaningful share of high-purity *class-1* nickel (the pure metal that batteries and the LME contract require, as opposed to the cheaper nickel-in-iron used for stainless steel), a large slice of the world's palladium, and — together with Ukraine — close to a quarter of all internationally traded wheat. When one country is simultaneously a top exporter of energy, a top exporter of metals, and half of a grain breadbasket, a single conflict can break several unrelated-looking markets at the same time. They were never really unrelated; they shared a supplier.

**Reason two: a physical supply chain has no redundancy.** A bank can route a payment through ten different networks. A physical molecule of methane that was supposed to travel from a Siberian gas field through a pipeline to a German furnace has exactly one path, and when that pipe is shut, there is no second pipe waiting. The alternative — liquefying gas, loading it onto a specialised tanker, sailing it across an ocean, and re-gasifying it at a terminal that may not even exist yet — takes years to build and is far more expensive. There is no spare capacity sitting idle, because spare capacity costs money and nobody builds it for a war that might never come. The chain is optimised for normal times, which makes it brittle in abnormal ones.

**Reason three: short-run demand is inelastic.** *Elasticity* measures how much the quantity people buy falls when the price rises. For a physical necessity — the gas that heats a home, the diesel that moves food, the wheat that becomes bread — short-run demand barely budges when the price doubles, because people cannot simply stop heating, eating, or moving. Inelastic demand is the amplifier: when a small slice of supply disappears, the price has to rise *a lot* to force just enough people to consume less, because almost nobody wants to. A 5% supply cut does not produce a 5% price rise; it produces a 50% one.

**Reason four: the buffers are thin, and they were already drained.** Every physical market carries shock absorbers — oil has OPEC spare capacity and strategic reserves, gas has storage, metals have warehouse stocks, grain has carryover from the last harvest. These buffers are what let a market absorb a small disruption without a price spike. But buffers cost money to hold, so they are kept lean, and crucially they were already *thin* going into 2022 after the pandemic distortions. Oil spare capacity was low, European gas storage was unusually depleted heading into the winter, and metal inventories on the LME were near multi-year lows. When the buffer is full, a supply break is absorbed; when the buffer is empty, the same break goes straight to the price. 2022 hit lean buffers, which is part of why the moves were so violent. A market's fragility is not constant — it depends on how full the tanks happen to be when the shock arrives.

Put these together and you get the core of 2022. A single adversary supplier (concentration) was cut from the West (no redundancy), the things it supplied were necessities (inelastic demand), and the shock-absorbing buffers were nearly empty (thin inventory). No diversified stock index has to pass through a pipeline, sit in a warehouse, or wait for a harvest. That is why, while the S&P 500 fell through 2022, commodities did the one thing that makes them unique: many of them **rose on the bad news**.

#### Worked example: why a 6% gas cut produces a 60% price spike

Suppose Europe's gas demand is roughly 400 billion cubic metres a year, and Russia supplies about 150 of that through pipelines. Now suppose pipeline flows are cut by a quarter — call it a loss of roughly 38 bcm, which is about **6% of total supply** taken off the table in the short run.

If demand were elastic, the price would rise about 6% and the market would clear. But gas demand in the short run is brutally inelastic — homes still need heat, and a chemical plant cannot run at 94% to save a little gas without shutting whole production lines. Estimates of the short-run price elasticity of European gas demand cluster around −0.1, meaning a 1% rise in price cuts the quantity demanded by only about 0.1%. To clear a 6% shortfall through demand alone, the price has to rise until people *voluntarily* give up 6% of consumption: roughly 6% ÷ 0.1 = **60%**.

So a 6% physical cut maps to something like a 60% price spike — and in the real 2022, where the cut was deeper and the fear was worse, the move was far larger than that. **The price move is the supply shortfall divided by the (tiny) elasticity, which is how a modest physical disruption becomes a dramatic price event.**

## Break one: European gas and the energy-bill crisis

Of the three breaks, the European gas crisis was the deepest and the most consequential, because gas is not just a commodity — it is the input to electricity, heating, fertiliser, and a large chunk of heavy industry. When gas breaks, everything downstream of gas breaks with it.

Here is the setup. For two decades Europe had wired itself to cheap Russian pipeline gas. It was a deliberate bet: pipeline gas is cheaper than shipped gas, and the relationship was assumed to be stable because both sides depended on it — Russia needed the revenue, Europe needed the heat. The benchmark price for this gas is the **Dutch TTF** (Title Transfer Facility), a virtual trading hub in the Netherlands that functions as the reference price for European gas the way Henry Hub does for the United States. In 2020, TTF traded around 9 euros per megawatt-hour (MWh) — gas was cheap and plentiful.

Then, through the second half of 2021 and accelerating brutally after the February 2022 invasion, Russian flows fell — first through reduced deliveries, then sabotage of the Nord Stream pipelines in September 2022, then near-total cutoff. Europe was suddenly short a quarter of its gas with winter coming and almost no way to physically replace it on short notice. The price did what an inelastic market with a severed supply does: it went vertical. TTF printed close to **339 euros/MWh** in August 2022 — roughly thirty-six times the 2020 level and about ten times any normal price. The chart below lays out the path.

![European gas Dutch TTF spikes to 339 EUR/MWh in 2022](/imgs/blogs/the-2022-energy-crisis-and-the-nickel-blowup-when-supply-broke-2.png)

Two features of this chart carry the story. First, the *scale*: the August 2022 spike towers so far over the 2020 bar that you can barely see the baseline. That is what a supply break looks like on a price chart — not a 20% wiggle but a multiple. Second, the *re-normalisation*: by 2024 the price had fallen back toward 34 euros, still well above the 2020 level but a fraction of the panic peak. This matters because it tells you something about supply breaks generally — the worst of the price is paid in the months when the market does not yet know whether the supply will come back, and it unwinds once the physical chain adapts (here, by Europe building LNG terminals, refilling storage, and slashing demand). The fear is more expensive than the eventual reality, almost always.

What did this do in the real economy? Electricity prices follow gas in Europe because of how power markets are priced: the wholesale electricity price is usually set by the *marginal* generator — the most expensive plant needed to meet demand in a given hour — and at the margin that plant is very often a gas turbine. So when gas goes up ten-fold, the gas-fired power plants that set the clearing price drag the entire electricity price up with them, even the power generated by cheap nuclear or wind. Households across Europe opened energy bills that had tripled or worse. Governments scrambled to cap prices and subsidise consumers. And energy-intensive industry — fertiliser, aluminum smelting, glass, ceramics, chemicals — simply began to shut down, because at 339-euro gas the cost of the input exceeded the value of the output.

#### Worked example: a European factory's gas-cost shock

Take a mid-sized glass or fertiliser plant that burns, say, 200,000 MWh of natural gas a year as its main energy input.

At the calm 2020 price of about **€30/MWh** (a slightly elevated but normal level), its annual gas bill is 200,000 × 30 = **€6 million**. That is a meaningful but manageable line in a plant doing tens of millions in revenue.

Now mark the same plant at the **€340/MWh** crisis peak. The gas bill becomes 200,000 × 340 = **€68 million** — more than an eleven-fold increase, a swing of **€62 million** on a single input. For most such plants, that number is larger than the entire margin of the business. There is no price at which they can sell glass or fertiliser and recover a €68 million energy bill, so the rational move is to **stop**: idle the furnace, lay off the shift, and import the finished product from somewhere with cheaper energy instead.

This is **demand destruction** — the market clearing not by people choosing to buy less, but by factories being forced out of existence. **An energy-bill multiple this large does not get absorbed; it deindustrialises, and that is exactly what began happening across Europe's heavy industry in 2022.**

### The transmission chain: from gas to fertiliser to food

The gas spike did not stay in the gas market. Natural gas is the feedstock for **ammonia**, and ammonia is the basis of nitrogen fertiliser — roughly 60–80% of the cost of making a tonne of ammonia is the gas burned to make it. So when European gas went to ten times normal, European ammonia plants did the same arithmetic as the glass plant in the worked example and shut down: at crisis-level gas, you cannot make fertiliser at a price a farmer will pay. Roughly half of Europe's ammonia capacity went idle through 2022. That fed straight into the food story — more expensive and scarcer fertiliser means a more expensive and smaller harvest the following season, layering a second-order food shock on top of the wheat blockade. One severed gas pipe propagated, link by physical link, into the price of bread.

The same logic hit electricity-intensive metals. Aluminum and zinc smelters in Europe are essentially "congealed electricity" — the power cost is so dominant that a smelter is really an electricity-arbitrage business wearing a metals hat. When the power price tracked gas up ten-fold, European smelters could not run profitably and a large share of the continent's aluminum and zinc capacity was curtailed or shut. This is why the 2022 aluminum price held up even as global demand softened: not because demand was strong, but because the *supply* of European metal was being switched off by the energy bill. Energy is the master input, and a sufficiently large energy shock reprices everything energy touches, which is almost everything physical.

### The policy response: caps, subsidies, and rationing

Governments could not rebuild a pipeline in a winter, so they reached for the only levers they had: price caps, consumer subsidies, and demand cuts. Across Europe, governments spent on the order of hundreds of billions of euros shielding households and firms from the full energy bill — effectively moving the cost from the consumer's gas bill onto the public balance sheet. The European Union agreed targets to cut gas demand by 15% and refilled its gas storage to near-full ahead of the winter, paying up to do it. Some countries rationed industrial gas or paid factories to idle voluntarily. Most importantly, Europe embarked on a crash programme to build floating LNG import terminals (FSRUs — floating storage and regasification units, ships that turn liquid LNG back into pipeline gas) so it could physically receive the seaborne cargoes it was now outbidding the world for.

All of this is the policy face of an inelastic supply break: when you cannot increase supply quickly and you cannot let the price fully ration demand without political collapse, you spend public money to bridge the gap and you try to engineer demand down by decree. The mild winter of 2022–23 helped enormously — a cold one would have forced genuine rationing. Europe got through, but the lesson it drew was structural, not seasonal: never again wire your economy to a single adversary's pipeline. That conclusion is still reshaping global energy flows today.

## The scramble for LNG: Europe outbids Asia

Europe could not magic up a new pipeline, but gas *can* travel another way: as **LNG** — liquefied natural gas, methane chilled to about minus 162°C until it becomes a liquid roughly 1/600th of its gaseous volume, loaded onto specialised tankers, shipped across oceans, and re-gasified at the destination. (The full mechanics live in [the LNG post in this series](/blog/trading/commodities/lng-and-the-globalization-of-gas-shipping-the-uncontainable).) Before 2022, LNG let three regional gas markets — the US, Europe, and Asia — partly connect, with traders arbitraging the price differences by sending cargoes wherever they fetched the most.

In 2022, Europe needed an enormous volume of LNG and needed it *now*, and the only way to get cargoes to sail to Rotterdam instead of Tokyo was to pay more than Asia would. So Europe bid up the price of every available cargo, and the result is visible in the way the three regional gas prices, normally loosely linked, tore violently apart and then re-converged.

![Three regional gas prices tore apart in 2022 then re-converged](/imgs/blogs/the-2022-energy-crisis-and-the-nickel-blowup-when-supply-broke-4.png)

Read the figure this way: in 2021 the three prices were elevated but recognisably in the same world. In 2022 the European TTF and the Asian JKM (the Platts Japan-Korea Marker, the benchmark for LNG delivered into Asia) both exploded to the mid-30s in dollars per MMBtu while US **Henry Hub** stayed around \$6 — because the United States had abundant shale gas and was now exporting it, but its export capacity was physically capped by the number of liquefaction terminals it had built. The US price could not rise to meet the European one, because the gas was stuck behind a bottleneck of too few export trains. That gap — cheap US gas, expensive European and Asian gas — is the LNG arbitrage, and in 2022 it was the widest it had ever been.

#### Worked example: the LNG-reroute economics

Take a single LNG cargo of roughly 3.5 billion cubic feet of gas — about 3.6 million MMBtu — sitting on a tanker that can sail either to Asia or to Europe.

In early 2022, Asian buyers (JKM) were paying around **\$34/MMBtu** and European buyers (TTF) were paying around **\$37/MMBtu** in equivalent terms. For the cargo owner, the choice is pure arithmetic: selling into Europe instead of Asia earns an extra \$3/MMBtu × 3.6 million MMBtu = **about \$10.8 million more** on that single cargo, before adjusting for the difference in shipping distance.

So Europe did not *ask* for the gas — it **outbid** for it, cargo by cargo, paying whatever premium over Asia was needed to redirect the tankers mid-voyage. Spot-market cargoes that had been heading east turned around and sailed west. **The reason Europe avoided physically running out of gas in the winter of 2022–23 is that it was rich enough to outbid the rest of the world for every spare molecule of LNG on the water — a luxury a poorer importer would not have had.** That is the brutal logic of a supply break: the scarce thing goes to whoever pays the most, and the bidding is global.

This is also why the crisis rippled far beyond Europe. By pulling LNG cargoes toward itself, Europe made gas more expensive and scarcer for the developing-world buyers — Pakistan, Bangladesh, parts of South Asia — who could not match European prices and suffered blackouts as a result. A supply break in one place is exported to everywhere connected to the same physical market.

## Break two: the LME nickel squeeze

Now to the nickel blowup — the single most dramatic market event of 2022, and the one that taught a lesson the energy crisis did not: that even an **exchange**, the supposedly neutral plumbing on which markets run, can break.

First the setup. Nickel was already nervous because Russia is a major producer of high-purity class-1 nickel, the LME-deliverable grade, and the war raised the fear that Russian supply would be sanctioned or cut off. Into that fear walked a structural fault: a very large *short* position. A short is a bet that the price will fall — you sell a contract today, planning to buy it back cheaper later. A Chinese stainless-steel and nickel giant, Tsingshan, held an enormous short position on LME nickel, reportedly well over 100,000 tonnes. For a producer this can be a legitimate hedge: you sell forward to lock in a price for the metal you are going to make. But it leaves you catastrophically exposed if the price *rises* instead of falls, because a short loses money without limit as the price climbs — there is no ceiling on how high a price can go.

When the war fear sent nickel up, the short started losing money, and that triggered the deadliest dynamic in markets: a **short squeeze**. As the price rises, a short position generates margin calls — demands from the broker to post more cash to cover the growing paper loss. To stop the bleeding, the short has to *buy* the contract back. But the short here was so enormous that the buying needed to close it would itself drive the price even higher, generating even bigger losses and even bigger margin calls, forcing even more buying. The squeeze feeds on itself: shorts buying to cut their losses become the very buying pressure that deepens those losses. On 7 March nickel rose about 70%. On the morning of 8 March it doubled again, blowing past \$100,000 a tonne. The chart below shows just how far off any normal level that was.

![LME nickel and the March 2022 squeeze peak from 2016 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/the-2022-energy-crisis-and-the-nickel-blowup-when-supply-broke-3.png)

Look at where that red star sits. The blue line is the orderly annual-average market, drifting between roughly \$9,000 and \$26,000 a tonne across the decade. The star is the 8 March intraday peak of **\$101,365** — nearly four times the highest annual average on the chart, reached in a single morning. That is not a market discovering a new price. That is a market mechanism failing: the price was no longer being set by what nickel is worth to a steelmaker, but by the forced, panicked buying of a trapped short and the predatory buying of those who saw the trap.

#### Worked example: the nickel short-squeeze loss

Take a stylised producer who is short **100,000 tonnes** of nickel, entered near the 2022 average of about **\$25,000 a tonne**. (This is illustrative, but the magnitudes are close to the real Tsingshan position.)

A short loses (current price − entry price) on every tonne. So as the price runs:

- Marked at **\$50,000**: loss = (50,000 − 25,000) × 100,000 = \$2.5 billion underwater.
- Marked at **\$80,000**: loss = (80,000 − 25,000) × 100,000 = \$5.5 billion underwater.
- Marked at the **\$101,365** peak: loss = (101,365 − 25,000) × 100,000 ≈ **\$7.6 billion** underwater.

Every \$1,000 the price climbs is another \$100 million of paper loss on this position. And here is the killer: those losses arrive as **margin calls** — the broker demands the cash *now*, intraday, in real money. No producer holds \$7.6 billion of spare cash to post against a hedge. **A short squeeze does not bankrupt you slowly; it bankrupts you in hours, because the loss is unbounded and the margin is due immediately.**

Here is where the exchange itself broke. Those billions in margin were owed not just by Tsingshan but flowed up through the **clearing chain** — the brokers and clearing members who stand between a trader and the exchange and *guarantee* each other's trades. If Tsingshan could not pay its margin, its broker was on the hook; if the broker could not pay, a clearing member could fail; and the failure of a clearing member is the thing that can collapse an exchange, because the whole system rests on the promise that the cleared trade always settles. The LME was staring at the possibility that one of its own members would default and take others down with it.

So it made a choice that is still litigated today: it **halted** nickel trading and then **cancelled** every trade executed that morning, rolling the price back to the previous day's close — roughly **\$3.9 billion** of deals voided. The decision tree below lays out what that choice actually was.

![Why the LME halted and voided a day of trades](/imgs/blogs/the-2022-energy-crisis-and-the-nickel-blowup-when-supply-broke-5.png)

Trace the two branches. If the exchange let the trades stand and enforced the margin calls, the shorts and their brokers would default and a clearing member might fail — but the longs (the funds and traders who had legitimately *bought* nickel during the spike and were sitting on huge real gains) would keep those gains, and the contract would remain sacred: a deal is a deal. If instead the exchange voided the day, it rescued the shorts and the clearing chain and kept itself solvent — but it stripped the longs of billions in booked, realised gains and shattered the most basic promise an exchange makes, which is that a trade you execute *is final*. The LME chose the second branch. It protected its members' solvency over its traders' property rights.

This is the single most important lesson of the nickel episode, and it has a name: **venue risk**. You can be completely right about a market, execute a perfect trade at a real price on a real exchange, book a genuine profit — and have the exchange take it back to save itself. The "neutral" plumbing is not neutral when its own survival is at stake. (For the deeper anatomy of the nickel chain and the two grades of the metal, see [the aluminum-and-nickel post in this series](/blog/trading/commodities/aluminum-nickel-and-the-2022-nickel-squeeze-when-the-market-broke).) For a curious investor the takeaway is humbling: the rules of the game can be rewritten mid-game, by the referee, in the referee's favour.

### Why the exchange could not see the position

A reasonable question: how did a short position large enough to break the market build up without anyone stopping it? The answer is a structural blind spot. A large part of Tsingshan's short was held not as cleared LME futures but as **over-the-counter (OTC)** positions — private contracts arranged directly with banks, off the central exchange. OTC positions are bilateral and opaque: the exchange does not see them, the regulator does not aggregate them, and so the *total* size of the short was invisible until the squeeze was already underway. The visible, on-exchange position was only part of a much larger whole.

This matters for the general lesson. A market can look orderly on the exchange screen while a system-threatening position accumulates in the dark, off-exchange, where no risk manager is adding it all up. When that hidden position finally has to be unwound — through forced buying in a squeeze — it hits the visible market all at once, and the visible market has no idea how big the wave is until it arrives. Opacity plus leverage plus an inelastic underlying is the recipe for a market that breaks without warning. The 2008 financial crisis was the same shape in a different asset (opaque derivatives on housing); the nickel squeeze was a commodity-market echo of it.

### The aftermath: lawsuits, lost trust, and a market that shrank

The cancellation did not end the story; it started a new one. The funds and traders whose realised gains were voided sued the LME, arguing it had no right to confiscate legitimate profits — that a trade executed at a real price on a real exchange must be honoured. The legal fights ran for years. Whatever the courts ultimately decided, the *reputational* damage was instant and lasting: a meaningful share of the speculative and fund liquidity that makes a futures market liquid simply left LME nickel, because traders concluded the venue could not be trusted to honour its own trades under stress. A market that loses its speculators loses its liquidity, and a less-liquid market is *more* prone to violent moves — so the cancellation, intended to restore stability, arguably made the nickel contract more fragile for years afterward. Trust, once broken, is expensive to rebuild, and an exchange runs entirely on trust.

### Nickel was not alone: the wider metals fear

Nickel made the headlines because its exchange broke, but it was the loudest note in a wider chord. Russia is a major producer across the metals complex, and each metal it dominated carried its own 2022 fear premium. **Palladium** — used in the catalytic converters that scrub car exhaust, and roughly 40% of it mined in Russia — spiked toward \$3,440 an ounce intraday in March 2022 on the fear of lost Russian supply. **Aluminum** carried a double premium: Russia is a top producer, *and* European smelters were being switched off by the power-cost crisis described earlier, so the supply fear came from both ends at once. Even where the metal kept flowing, buyers began self-sanctioning — refusing Russian metal on reputational grounds — which fractured the market into "Russian" and "non-Russian" units of the same physical commodity trading at different prices. That fracturing is itself a symptom of a broken market: in a healthy commodity market a tonne of nickel is a tonne of nickel, fungible regardless of origin. When the origin starts to matter to the price, the market has stopped functioning as a single pool.

## Break three: wheat and the Black Sea grain drama

The third break was the quietest in the financial press but the one with the highest human stakes: wheat, and the food it becomes.

Ukraine is not called the breadbasket of Europe by accident. Its black-soil plains, together with southern Russia, supplied close to a quarter of the wheat that crosses borders in world trade, plus a large share of corn, barley, and sunflower oil. That grain reaches the world almost entirely by ship, loaded at a handful of ports on the Black Sea and threaded out through the Bosphorus. When the war began, those ports were blockaded and mined, and a substantial slice of the world's exportable grain was suddenly trapped behind a naval cordon. Wheat did what a necessity with a severed supply does: it spiked to a record.

![CBOT wheat and the 2022 war spike to a 12.94 record](/imgs/blogs/the-2022-energy-crisis-and-the-nickel-blowup-when-supply-broke-6.png)

The chart marks the move: CBOT wheat, which had traded around \$5–7 a bushel for most of the prior decade, jumped to an intraday record near **\$12.94 a bushel** in March 2022. The annual average for 2022 settled around \$9, which still represented the most expensive wheat in well over a decade. For a wheat-importing country — Egypt, much of North Africa and the Middle East, parts of Asia — this was not a trading curiosity on a screen; it was the price of bread, and bread prices have toppled governments before.

The drama that followed is a textbook case of how the world tries to un-break a physical supply chain. Through 2022 the United Nations and Turkey brokered the **Black Sea Grain Initiative**, a corridor agreement that let grain ships sail out of Ukrainian ports under inspection and safe passage. For about a year it worked, moving tens of millions of tonnes of grain to market and pulling the wheat price back down — visible in the chart as the 2023 annual average falling back toward \$6.50. Then Russia withdrew from the deal in mid-2023, and the risk premium crept back into the price. The corridor was a patch on a broken chain, and patches are fragile. (The deeper mechanics of how export restrictions and government hoarding cascade into food-price spikes live in [the food-security post in this series](/blog/trading/commodities/food-security-export-bans-and-when-governments-hoard).)

There is a second-order twist worth naming. The grain shock and the fertiliser shock compounded each other. The same gas spike that idled European ammonia plants made nitrogen fertiliser scarce and expensive worldwide, which threatened to shrink the *next* harvest everywhere — not just in the war zone. A farmer who cannot afford fertiliser plants the same field but harvests less from it, so a fertiliser shortage today is a grain shortage next year. The 2022 food crisis was therefore not one event but two stacked on top of each other: an immediate supply cut (Ukrainian grain trapped behind a blockade) and a slower-burning input cut (gas-driven fertiliser scarcity reducing future yields). This is the cascading, compounding nature of a real supply break — one severed pipe does not break one market, it propagates through every physical chain connected to it.

### Oil: the spike that came back

Crude oil deserves its own note, because it behaved differently from gas, nickel, and wheat — and the difference is instructive. Russia is one of the world's three largest oil producers, so the invasion sent Brent spiking toward **\$139 a barrel** — the highest since the 2008 super-spike — in the first days. But unlike European pipeline gas, oil is a *globally fungible, seaborne* market: a barrel can sail anywhere. So while sanctions and self-sanctioning (buyers refusing Russian cargoes) initially spiked the price, the molecules quickly re-routed rather than disappearing. Russian crude found new homes in India and China at a discount to Brent, carried by a "shadow fleet" of older tankers operating outside Western insurance and the G7 price cap. World oil supply barely fell; the *map* of buyers redrew. And so oil, after its initial spike, drifted back down through 2022 even as the war ground on — the 2022 annual average for WTI settled near \$95, elevated but far below the panic peak. The contrast is the whole point: a commodity that can physically re-route (oil) recovers; a commodity locked to one pipeline (European pipeline gas) does not, until new physical infrastructure is built. The depth and duration of a supply break depends on how substitutable the physical delivery path is.

#### Worked example: the wheat-importer hit

Take a country that imports **5 million tonnes** of wheat a year. There are roughly 36.7 bushels in a tonne of wheat, so that is about 183 million bushels.

At a calm pre-war price of about **\$6 a bushel**, the annual import bill is 183 million × 6 ≈ **\$1.1 billion**.

At the **\$12.94 record**, the same physical quantity of wheat costs 183 million × 12.94 ≈ **\$2.37 billion** — more than double, a swing of over **\$1.2 billion** on the food bill of a single nation, for the exact same number of loaves of bread.

For a wealthy importer this is a painful budget line. For a poor one that subsidises bread to keep the peace, it is a fiscal and political emergency — the government either eats the cost (blowing a hole in the budget) or passes it on (risking unrest). **A grain supply break does not stay in the commodity market; it lands directly on the price of staying fed, which is why food is the supply break with the highest stakes of all.**

## Common misconceptions

Several intuitions about 2022 are wrong in ways that matter, and correcting them with numbers is how you actually learn the lesson.

**Misconception 1: "The price spiked because the supply physically ran out."** Mostly false. In every one of the three breaks, the *fear* moved the price far more than the realised loss of physical volume. On the day Brent touched \$139, not one barrel had actually gone missing — Russia was still pumping. The 6%-cut-to-60%-spike arithmetic from the Foundations section shows how a small actual shortfall, divided by a tiny elasticity, produces an enormous price move. The shortage that mattered was a *feared* shortage, priced in advance. When the feared catastrophe did not fully materialise — Europe found LNG, the grain corridor opened — prices unwound hard.

**Misconception 2: "An exchange is neutral plumbing; a trade on a major exchange is final."** False, as roughly \$3.9 billion of cancelled nickel trades proved. When the survival of the exchange's own members is at stake, the exchange can and will rewrite the outcome — halt trading, void deals, change margin rules intraday. Venue risk is real, and it is asymmetric: it tends to protect the large clearing members and the institutions, not the individual long who was right.

**Misconception 3: "Diversification protected investors in 2022."** Largely false, and this is the cruelest one. In a normal year, a portfolio of stocks and bonds diversifies because the two tend to move in opposite directions. But the 2022 shock was an *inflation and energy* shock, and an energy-cost spike hits the value of stocks (higher input costs, slower growth) and bonds (higher inflation, higher rates) *at the same time*. Both fell together. The classic 60/40 portfolio had one of its worst years in modern history. The only major asset class that rose was the one that *caused* the pain: commodities. Diversification across paper assets does not protect you from a shock to the physical economy itself.

**Misconception 4: "Sanctions destroyed Russian commodity supply."** Mostly false — they *re-routed* it. A sanctioned barrel of oil or tonne of nickel does not evaporate; it finds a new buyer at a discount. Russian crude largely redirected to India and China at a discount to Brent, carried by a "shadow fleet" of older tankers. World supply barely changed; the *map* of who-buys-from-whom redrew. This is why the oil price, after its initial spike, came back down through 2022 even as the war continued — the molecules kept flowing, just to different addresses.

**Misconception 5: "This was a one-off; it can't happen again."** False, and dangerously complacent. The deep cause — the West's dependence on a single adversary supplier for critical energy and materials — was a structural vulnerability that existed for decades before anyone priced it. The same vulnerability exists today in other forms: China's dominance of rare earths and battery-metal refining, the concentration of high-end chips, the chokepoints that carry the world's oil. 2022 was not a freak event; it was a stress test that the just-in-time, single-source global supply chain failed.

**Misconception 6: "Producers who hedge are always safer than those who don't."** Not necessarily — the nickel squeeze proved that a hedge can become the danger. A *short* hedge (selling forward the metal you will produce) protects you if the price falls, which is the normal worry for a producer. But if the price spikes instead, that same hedge generates unbounded mark-to-market losses and intraday margin calls *before* you have produced and sold the physical metal that would settle it. The Tsingshan position was, on paper, a producer hedge; it nearly bankrupted the company because the timing mismatch between the paper loss (due now, in cash) and the physical gain (realised later, as metal) is itself a risk. A hedge transfers price risk but introduces *liquidity* risk — the risk of being forced to fund a paper loss before the offsetting physical gain arrives. In a violent enough move, the liquidity risk is the one that kills you. The lesson is not "don't hedge"; it is "a hedge you cannot fund through a spike is not a hedge, it is a different bet."

## How it shows up in real markets

Pull the three breaks together and a single pattern emerges — the signature of a real supply break, as opposed to an ordinary cyclical move. Learn to recognise it, because it is the thing that actually distinguishes a supply break from a normal sell-off or rally.

**The curve slams into backwardation.** In normal times a commodity forward curve is often in mild *contango* — futures priced above spot, reflecting the cost of storing the physical thing over time. In a supply break the curve flips violently the other way, into steep *backwardation*: spot priced far above every future. This is the market screaming "I need the physical thing *now*, and I will pay almost anything to have it today rather than in six months." In 2022, oil, gas, and several metals all moved to steep backwardation. The shape of the curve, not its level, is the tell. (If contango and backwardation are unfamiliar, [the natural-gas post](/blog/trading/commodities/natural-gas-henry-hub-ttf-jkm-and-the-most-seasonal-market) and the series' curve posts build them from scratch.)

**Correlations break.** The relationships you relied on stop holding. Commodities, which usually fall when stocks fall (both are pro-cyclical), instead *rose* while stocks and bonds fell. The dollar, which usually rises in a crisis as a haven, rose *and* commodities rose, breaking the normal inverse dollar-commodity link — and that broken link mattered doubly, because most commodities are priced in dollars, so a country whose currency was weakening against the dollar paid *even more* for the same physical barrel or tonne than the headline dollar price suggested. A Japanese or European importer in 2022 faced a double hit: the dollar price of energy spiked, and their own currency fell against the dollar at the same time, so the cost in yen or euros rose by more than the dollar chart alone showed. When the shock originates in the physical economy rather than the financial one, the statistical relationships estimated from financial history simply do not apply. (For how a single shock transmits across every asset class, see the [event-trading cross-asset post](/blog/trading/event-trading/geopolitics-elections-and-unscheduled-shocks).)

**Volatility explodes and liquidity vanishes.** A market in a supply break is a market where nobody knows the right price, so bid-ask spreads blow out, position limits tighten, and exchanges raise margin requirements — which forces more selling and buying at exactly the worst moment. The nickel halt was the extreme version: liquidity did not just dry up, the market closed entirely for over a week. Thin liquidity in a panic is its own danger, separate from the direction of the price.

**The strategic response: re-shoring and friend-shoring.** The longest-lasting consequence of 2022 was not a price; it was a policy. The West concluded that depending on a single adversary supplier for critical inputs was a strategic error, and began the slow, expensive work of unwinding it: building LNG import terminals, re-shoring (bringing production home) and friend-shoring (sourcing from allies) critical metals and components, subsidising domestic battery and chip supply chains. This is a multi-year, multi-trillion-dollar shift, and it has a price consequence — sourcing from allies instead of from the cheapest producer is structurally more expensive, a quiet upward pressure on the cost of physical goods that will outlast the war that triggered it. (For the broader macro lens on how this fed the inflation of the early 2020s, the cross-asset series covers [energy as the inflation engine](/blog/trading/cross-asset/energy-oil-gas-the-inflation-engine).)

#### Worked example: reading the three breaks as one signal

Suppose you woke on 24 February 2022 with a diversified paper portfolio and wanted to know, in real time, whether this was a normal geopolitical wobble or a genuine supply break. The tell was the *pattern across markets*, not any single price.

Within ten days: Brent was up toward \$139 (about a 35% jump from its ~\$94 2022-average context), European gas had gone parabolic on its way to a 339-euro peak (a multiple, not a percentage), nickel had blown past \$100,000 (a four-times move in a morning), and wheat had hit \$12.94 (more than double its calm level). Four unrelated-looking commodities, all severed from the same supplier, all spiking at once, while the S&P 500 *fell* about 10% over the same window and the supposedly safe 60/40 portfolio bled from both legs.

That joint pattern — multiple physical commodities spiking together while paper assets fell together — is the unmistakable signature of a supply break. **No single price tells you it is happening; the broken correlation across the whole complex does. When the things you eat, heat with, and build with all spike at once and your diversified portfolio falls anyway, you are not in a normal sell-off — the physical supply chain has broken, and the only thing rising is the physical itself.**

## The playbook: what protects you when supply breaks

Bring it home to the only question that matters for a curious investor. When a real supply break hits — and another one will, eventually, somewhere — what actually protects a portfolio, and what is the playbook?

![The supply-break playbook — curve, correlations, and the physical hedge](/imgs/blogs/the-2022-energy-crisis-and-the-nickel-blowup-when-supply-broke-7.png)

Start with the figure's hard truth, read left to right. In a **normal market** (the blue, left side) the curve is calm, correlations hold, diversification works, and the paper price tracks the physical thing — so the standard toolkit (a diversified stock-bond portfolio, rebalancing, hedging the odds with cheap options) does its job. In a **real supply break** (the red-and-green right side) all four of those break: the curve slams backwardated, correlations invert, diversification across paper assets fails because the shock hits every paper asset at once, and the *only* thing that protects you is owning the physical-linked asset itself. That asymmetry is the whole playbook.

Concretely, here is how to read and act on a supply break.

**One: own the physical-linked asset *before* the break, not during it.** The single brutal lesson of 2022 is that the protection has to be in place beforehand. Once the break is visible, the price has already moved — gas was already at ten times normal, nickel had already quadrupled. You cannot buy insurance after the fire starts. A small, permanent allocation to real assets — broad commodity exposure, energy, or the producers of scarce materials — is what turns a supply break from a catastrophe into a hedge. The cost of carrying that allocation in the calm years (the roll cost in contango, the years commodities lag) is the *premium* you pay for protection in the break. (The cross-asset and commodity-index posts in this series cover how to actually hold that exposure and what it costs.)

**Two: watch the *shape* of the curve, not just the level.** A move from contango to steep backwardation across the energy complex is the earliest, clearest signal that the market is pricing physical scarcity rather than financial sentiment. The level can rise for many reasons; a violent flip into backwardation is specifically the market saying it needs the physical thing *now*. That is the signal to trust the supply-break thesis over the "it's just a wobble" one.

**Three: respect venue risk.** The nickel cancellation proved that being right is not enough — you also have to survive the plumbing. In a true panic, exchanges raise margins, tighten limits, and in the extreme void trades. Size positions so that an intraday margin spike or a forced liquidation does not destroy you, and never assume a cleared trade is truly final when the exchange's own survival is on the line.

**Four: expect the correlation breakdown and do not fight it.** When commodities rise while stocks and bonds fall together, the models built on financial-market history are temporarily useless. Do not rebalance mechanically into the falling paper assets expecting the old relationships to reassert quickly; a physical-economy shock can run for months. The discipline is to recognise the regime change early — the joint commodity spike is the tell — and let the physical-linked sleeve do the work it was held for.

**Five: understand that the structural shift outlasts the spike.** The price of gas came back down; the strategic re-wiring of the world's supply chains did not. Re-shoring and friend-shoring are a slow, durable upward pressure on the cost of physical goods, because sourcing from allies rather than from the cheapest producer is simply more expensive. The 2022 spike was the acute event; the de-globalisation of critical supply is the chronic condition it left behind, and it is mildly bullish for real assets over the long run.

**Six: separate the spike from the substitution.** Not every supply break is permanent, and the smart read distinguishes the breaks that re-route from the breaks that require new infrastructure. Oil re-routed (shadow fleet, new buyers) and recovered in months; European pipeline gas required physically building LNG terminals and recovered over years; a metal locked behind a single dominant refiner (rare earths, some battery metals) might not have a quick substitute at all. The duration and depth of the trade is set by how substitutable the physical delivery path is — so when a break hits, the first question is not "how high" but "how replaceable is the lost supply, and how fast." That single question separates a brief spike to fade from a structural re-rating to hold.

#### Worked example: sizing a real-asset hedge against a supply break

Suppose a curious investor holds a \$500,000 portfolio, mostly stocks and bonds, and wants insurance against the next 2022-style supply break. How much real-asset exposure does the job?

In 2022 a broad commodity index rose on the order of 15–25% while a 60/40 stock-bond portfolio fell roughly 15%. Say the investor carries a permanent **10% allocation** — \$50,000 — in broad commodity exposure. In a break where commodities rise 20% and the rest of the portfolio falls 15%, the commodity sleeve gains 0.10 × 20% = +2.0% at the portfolio level, while the remaining 90% loses 0.90 × 15% = −13.5%. The sleeve does not make the year positive — it offsets only a fraction of the drawdown.

The point is not that 10% in commodities turns a crisis into a win; it does not. The point is the *trade-off*: that 10% sleeve quietly costs the investor a little in the calm years (the roll bleed in contango, the years commodities lag) in exchange for being the one thing that rises when everything else breaks together. **The hedge is a small, permanent premium paid in good years to own the asset that pays out in the one year diversification fails — and it only works if it is already in place when supply breaks, because by the time the break is visible the price has already moved.**

Here is the synthesis, and the place to end. Every other asset class is a paper claim that can be transferred, hedged, and diversified at the speed of a keystroke. A commodity is matter — it has to be mined or grown in one place and physically carried to another, through a chain with no redundancy, to satisfy a demand that cannot wait. In normal times that physicality is an inconvenience (storage costs, roll costs, the bleed of contango). In a supply break it becomes the entire point: when the chain snaps, paper claims on a healthy economy all fall together, and the only thing that rises is a claim on the scarce physical thing itself. 2022 was the year the world relearned that lesson the hard way — that commodities are not just another sleeve in a portfolio, but the asset class that pays you precisely when everything else is breaking, *if* you owned the physical before the physical broke.
