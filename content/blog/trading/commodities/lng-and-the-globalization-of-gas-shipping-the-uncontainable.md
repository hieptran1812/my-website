> **TL;DR** — Natural gas is the commodity that hates to travel; liquefying it to −162°C shrinks it ~600× so it can ride a ship, and that single trick is slowly fusing three isolated regional gas markets into one contested global one.
>
> - The physical chain — cool to liquid, load a cryogenic tanker, ship, regasify, inject into the grid — is brutally capital-intensive: a liquefaction plant costs tens of billions and takes years, so LNG supply cannot respond fast and a shortage can stay expensive for years.
> - The trade is an arbitrage on the gap between three prices — Henry Hub (US), TTF (Europe), JKM (Asia) — capped by the cost to ship a cargo between them; a flexible cargo sails to whichever basin pays the best netback.
> - In 2022 that machine flipped on in full view: when Russian pipeline gas was cut, Europe's price (TTF) blew out and pulled US cargoes that would have gone to Asia, and US LNG exports ran from roughly zero in 2016 to the world's largest by 2024.
> - The one number to remember: a cargo is ~3.5 TBtu, and the inter-basin spread net of freight is the single figure that decides where it goes.

On the morning of 26 August 2022, the front-month European gas contract — the Dutch TTF — settled at around 339 EUR per megawatt-hour. Translate that into the unit Americans price gas in, and it is roughly 100 US dollars per million British thermal units (MMBtu). On that same day, in Louisiana, the US benchmark — Henry Hub — was trading at a little over 9 dollars per MMBtu. The exact same molecule of methane, the exact same fuel, was worth more than ten times as much on one side of the Atlantic as the other.

For a commodity, that is an almost absurd dislocation. Oil never does this. A barrel of crude in Texas and a barrel in Rotterdam trade within a few dollars of each other, because a barrel is cheap to put on a tanker and sail anywhere. Gas, historically, could not be shipped at all — it could only flow down a pipe to wherever that pipe happened to end. So gas did not have one world price; it had three, and they could diverge by 10× and stay there. The thing that was, slowly and at enormous cost, closing that gap in 2022 was a fleet of refrigerated ships carrying liquefied natural gas. This post is about how that fleet works, why it costs so much, and why the spread it arbitrages is the most important number in the global gas trade.

This is the post where the series' spine — *a physical thing forced through a financial contract* — gets its most extreme test. Gas is the hardest major commodity to move, so the physical constraints (the −162°C, the tanker, the regas terminal) dominate everything. Get the physics right and the economics, the contracts, and the trade fall straight out of it.

## Foundations: why gas is the commodity that hates to travel

Start with the problem LNG exists to solve. Natural gas — mostly methane, CH₄ — is a gas at ordinary temperatures and pressures. That single fact is the source of all its trouble. A gas is mostly empty space. A cubic meter of methane at room temperature weighs less than a kilogram, while a cubic meter of crude oil weighs about 850 kilograms. To move useful amounts of energy as gas, you either have to compress it hard or move staggering volumes — and either way, the only practical container is a pipe.

A pipeline is a magnificent machine, but it has one property that defines the entire gas market: it is a *fixed link between a fixed pair of points*. Once you have laid steel from a field in Siberia to a city in Germany, that gas can go to Germany and nowhere else. The buyer and the seller are married for the thirty-year life of the pipe. There is no spot market in the way oil has one, because there is no way to redirect the molecules. If Germany has a glut and Japan has a shortage, the pipe cannot care.

The consequence is that for most of the twentieth century, the world did not have *a* gas market. It had three regional markets that barely spoke to each other:

- **North America**, anchored on the Henry Hub pipeline junction in Louisiana, priced in US dollars per MMBtu. A continent-sized pipe network with its own supply and demand.
- **Europe**, historically fed by Russian, Norwegian, and North African pipelines, now priced off virtual trading hubs — the Dutch **TTF** is the benchmark — in euros per megawatt-hour.
- **Asia**, with almost no domestic pipeline gas of its own, dependent on imports, priced off the **JKM** (Japan–Korea Marker) in US dollars per MMBtu.

Three prices, three units, three worlds. A surplus in one could sit next to a crisis in another and nothing equalized them, because gas could not cross the ocean. That is the world LNG breaks open.

A quick word on those units, because they trip up everyone and they matter for every calculation in this post. The US prices gas in **dollars per MMBtu** — million British thermal units, a unit of *energy*. Europe prices in **euros per megawatt-hour (MWh)** — also energy, but a different scale and a different currency. Asia's JKM is back in **dollars per MMBtu**. To compare them you have to convert to one common ruler. The handy rule of thumb: **1 MWh ≈ 3.412 MMBtu**, so to turn a TTF price in EUR/MWh into USD/MMBtu you divide by ~3.412 and multiply by the EUR/USD exchange rate. That is why the August 2022 TTF print of ~339 EUR/MWh maps to roughly 100 USD/MMBtu (339 ÷ 3.412 ≈ 99 EUR/MMBtu, times ~1.0 EUR/USD at the time). Throughout this post the charts already do that conversion so you can compare all three on one axis — but in the wild, the unit mismatch is a real source of confusion and, occasionally, of mispriced trades.

Why does each region have the price it does? Because each is, fundamentally, a *closed pipeline pool* with its own supply and demand until LNG bridges it. North America has its own vast production and consumption, so Henry Hub clears on US shale output versus US heating and power demand. Europe historically balanced Norwegian, Russian, and Algerian pipeline gas against its own demand. Asia — Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, and increasingly China — has almost no domestic gas and almost no pipeline imports, so it has *always* relied on LNG and has *always* paid a premium for it. That structural premium is why Asia's JKM usually sits at the top of the three: it is the buyer of last resort with the fewest alternatives, so it pays up. Hold that in mind — it is why, in normal years, the world's marginal LNG cargo points east.

### What LNG actually is

LNG — liquefied natural gas — is the trick that lets gas ride a ship. Cool methane down far enough and it stops being a gas and condenses into a clear, cold liquid, exactly as steam condenses into water. The temperature you need is brutal: **−162°C** (−260°F), close to the coldest natural conditions anywhere on Earth's surface. But the payoff is enormous. As it liquefies, the methane collapses to about **1/600th** of its gaseous volume. Six hundred cubic meters of gas become one cubic meter of liquid.

That 600× shrinkage is the whole game. Now the energy is dense enough that a single ship can carry a meaningful cargo. An LNG tanker is, in effect, a giant thermos: a hull wrapped around a few enormous insulated tanks, holding the liquid at atmospheric pressure and that frigid temperature for a three-week voyage. A standard modern cargo holds roughly **3.5 trillion BTU (TBtu)** of energy — enough to heat a few hundred thousand homes for a winter, packed into one ship.

It is worth pausing on *why* the cold and not high pressure. There are two ways to densify a gas: squeeze it (compressed natural gas, CNG) or chill it (LNG). CNG, used in some vehicles, only compresses gas a couple of hundred times and needs heavy pressure vessels — fine for a bus, hopeless for an ocean crossing. Cooling to a liquid gives you the full 600× *at atmospheric pressure*, so the tanks do not have to be pressure bombs; they just have to be extraordinarily well insulated. That trade — accept the −162°C, avoid the high pressure — is what makes a giant, thin-walled, atmospheric tank feasible at the scale a ship needs. The cost of the bargain is the refrigeration plant on the export end and the re-warming plant on the import end, which is exactly where the money and the years go.

One more piece of physics that shapes the economics: LNG is never perfectly contained. Heat leaks through even the best insulation, so a little of the liquid is constantly boiling back into gas. That **boil-off** — around 0.1% of the cargo per day — is not waste in a modern ship; it is captured and burned to power the vessel, so the cargo partly fuels its own voyage. But it is a real loss of saleable gas, and on a long Asia-bound trip it eats a couple of percent of the cargo. Every link in this chain leaks a little value, and those leaks are why the all-in cost of moving gas is so much higher than moving oil.

#### Worked example: how much energy is in one LNG cargo

A typical large LNG carrier holds about 170,000 cubic meters of liquid. Each cubic meter of LNG re-expands to roughly 600 cubic meters of gas, so the cargo is about 170,000 × 600 ≈ 102 million cubic meters of gas. Natural gas carries roughly 1,030 BTU per standard cubic foot, and one cubic meter is about 35.3 cubic feet, so each cubic meter of gas is about 35.3 × 1,030 ≈ 36,360 BTU. Multiply: 102,000,000 × 36,360 ≈ 3.7 trillion BTU. Round to the industry shorthand: **a cargo is about 3.5 TBtu**. At a US feed-gas price of \$3.00/MMBtu, the gas inside that ship is worth roughly 3,500,000 MMBtu × \$3.00 ≈ \$10.5 million as raw feedstock — but, as we will see, it is worth several times that once it lands in Asia. The takeaway: one ship moves the gas equivalent of a small power-plant's annual fuel, which is exactly why a single cargo is a tradeable unit worth tens of millions of dollars.

## The physical chain: five links, each one expensive

LNG turns gas into a shippable commodity through a chain of five physical steps, and the defining feature of that chain is that every link is capital-intensive and slow to build. Walk it end to end.

**1. The gas field.** It begins as ordinary wellhead gas — methane plus impurities (water, CO₂, heavier hydrocarbons, sometimes sulfur). The gas is cleaned and dried, because at −162°C any water freezes into ice that would plug the plant. In the US, this feed gas is cheap: it trades at the Henry Hub price, which has spent most of the last decade between \$2 and \$4 per MMBtu thanks to the shale glut.

**2. Liquefaction.** This is the heart and the cost of the whole system. A *liquefaction train* — the industry's name for one production line — is a refrigeration plant on a colossal scale, chilling gas through stages of compression and expansion until it condenses. The energy to run that cooling is itself paid for in gas: roughly **8–12% of the feed is burned or consumed in the process**. Add the capital recovery on a plant that cost tens of billions, and the all-in *liquefaction fee* charged to a cargo is commonly quoted as around **115% of the Henry Hub feed price** plus a fixed tolling charge — the "115%" captures the fuel the plant eats, and the fixed charge recovers the steel. A single train can cost \$5–15 billion and takes years to build. This is the bottleneck of the entire global gas market.

**3. The LNG tanker.** A purpose-built cryogenic ship loads the liquid and carries it across the ocean. These ships are not cheap either — a newbuild runs over \$200 million — and they are not perfectly insulated: a small fraction of the cargo, around **0.1% per day**, boils off and is typically burned to power the ship. A three-week Gulf-Coast-to-Asia voyage therefore loses a couple of percent of its cargo as fuel, which is a real cost folded into the shipping leg.

**4. Regasification.** At the destination, an *import terminal* does liquefaction in reverse: it re-warms the liquid back into a gas (often using seawater or, less efficiently, by burning some of the gas) and pressurizes it for the pipeline. Regas is far cheaper than liquefaction — a small fee per unit — but it is still a multi-billion-dollar terminal that must exist before a single cargo can land.

**5. The import grid.** Finally, the regasified gas is injected into the buyer's domestic pipeline network and sells at the local hub price — TTF in Europe, JKM in Asia. That local price is what the whole chain was chasing.

The crucial structural fact: links 2 and 4 are *fixed infrastructure that takes the better part of a decade to build*. You cannot conjure a liquefaction train in response to a price spike. That lag is the reason gas shocks last for years rather than months — a point we will return to, because it is the single most important thing a trader needs to know about this market.

### Two kinds of cost: the fixed toll and the variable spread

There is a structure hidden in those five links that matters enormously for how the market behaves, and it is worth pulling out explicitly. The costs split into two categories with completely different economics.

The **fixed costs** are the steel: the liquefaction plant, the terminals, the ships. These are sunk capital, paid as a *tolling fee* whether or not a given cargo moves. A buyer who signed a 20-year liquefaction toll pays it every month regardless — it is a reservation fee for capacity, like a gym membership you owe even when you skip a workout. Because it is sunk, it does *not* enter the decision of whether to ship the next cargo. Once you have paid the toll, the train will liquefy your gas at near-zero extra cost, so you will run it as long as the cargo clears its *variable* costs.

The **variable costs** are the molecules and the voyage: the feed gas you buy at Henry Hub, the fuel the plant burns, the shipping, the regas. These are the costs that scale with each cargo and that decide, at the margin, whether to load and where to sail. This is why, in the per-cargo P&L worked example later, a cargo can keep sailing even when the price has fallen below the *full* (fixed + variable) cost stack: the holder who has already paid the sunk toll only needs the price to beat the *variable* leg — feed plus shipping plus regas, maybe \$5–6/MMBtu — to be better off shipping than not. That distinction between the full-cost economics that decide whether to *build* a plant and the variable-cost economics that decide whether to *run* it is the same logic that governs every capital-intensive commodity in this series, and it is why LNG supply, once built, keeps flowing through low prices and only the *building* of new supply stops.

### Why the long lead time makes prices spike for years

In a commodity with fast supply response — say, US shale oil, where a rig can be drilling within months — a price spike is self-limiting: high prices call forth new barrels and the price comes back down. Gas has no such fast valve internationally. When Europe lost Russian pipeline gas in 2022, the only way to replace it was with LNG cargoes, and the global liquefaction fleet was already running near flat-out. There was no spare capacity to bring on, and new capacity was three-to-five years away. So the price could not be solved by new supply on any useful horizon. It had to be solved the only other way a market can solve a shortage: by **destroying demand** — pricing gas high enough that European factories shut down and households turned thermostats down — until consumption fell to meet the available supply. That is why the 2021–2023 European gas crisis was measured in years, not weeks.

![Timeline of the multi-year liquefaction build from final investment decision to first cargo](/imgs/blogs/lng-and-the-globalization-of-gas-shipping-the-uncontainable-7.png)

The timeline above is the reason the spike persists. A liquefaction project does not begin construction the day a price spikes; it begins years earlier, when a developer signs enough long-term offtake to convince lenders, and reaches a *final investment decision* (FID). After FID come permitting and financing — environmental reviews and the raising of tens of billions in project debt — which alone can run a year or two. Then the physical build: pouring the foundations, fabricating cryogenic modules in shipyards, laying the export jetty, hooking up the feed-gas pipelines. Then a commissioning phase where the train is slowly cooled to operating temperature and the first cargo is loaded. Add it up and the gap between "we decide to build" and "first cargo sails" is commonly **four to seven years** — and that is *after* the developer was confident enough to commit billions. From the moment a shortage appears to the moment new supply can plausibly answer it, you are looking at half a decade. No price, however high, compresses concrete-curing and module-fabrication into the months a shortage demands. The market's only fast lever is demand, and it pulls that lever with price.

This asymmetry — fast demand destruction, glacial supply addition — is the defining dynamic of the entire LNG market and the single most important thing to carry forward from this post. It explains why gas is more volatile than oil, why crises last years, and why the *next* wave of capacity coming online is the structural force a long-horizon trader cares about more than any single day's price.

## The US story: from zero to the world's largest exporter in under a decade

The most dramatic single arc in modern energy is the United States going from a net gas *importer* to the world's largest LNG *exporter* in less than ten years. As recently as the mid-2000s, the US was building LNG *import* terminals, convinced it was running short of domestic gas. Then the shale revolution hit. Hydraulic fracturing unlocked so much cheap gas that the Henry Hub price collapsed from roughly \$8–9/MMBtu in 2005–2008 to around \$2–3 for most of the 2010s. The US suddenly had a glut of the cheapest gas in the world and nowhere to sell it — domestic prices were so low that producers were losing money.

The obvious move was to ship the surplus to the regions where gas was three-to-five times more expensive. But that required reversing those half-built import terminals into export terminals and adding liquefaction trains — the multi-billion-dollar, multi-year investment described above. The first such terminal, Sabine Pass in Louisiana, shipped its first cargo in **February 2016**. From that standing start, US LNG exports ran up the staircase shown in the figure above: roughly 0.5 billion cubic feet per day (bcf/d) in 2016, ~5 by 2019, ~10 by 2021, and around **12 bcf/d by 2024**, making the US the single largest LNG exporter on Earth — rivaled only by Qatar and Australia.

Notice the *shape* of that climb in the chart: it is a staircase, not a smooth ramp. Each step up is a new liquefaction train or a new terminal reaching full output — Sabine Pass trains 1 through 6, then Corpus Christi, Cameron, Freeport, Calcasieu Pass, and the rest. Capacity arrives in lumpy, multi-billion-dollar increments, each one decided years earlier, which is the supply-lag dynamic made visible. The US did not gently respond to high foreign prices; it committed enormous capital in discrete bets and the gas showed up in chunks, years after the decision.

There is a second, more profound consequence of the US becoming an exporter: **it connected the US domestic gas price to the rest of the world for the first time.** Before 2016, Henry Hub was an island — US gas was trapped on the continent, so its price was set purely by domestic supply and demand and could sit at \$2 while Asia paid \$10 with no link between them. Once export terminals existed, a high enough foreign price could reach back across the ocean and *bid up* US gas, because every molecule a producer sold to an exporter was a molecule not sold domestically. This is why, in 2022, Henry Hub itself spiked to over \$6 even though the US had a glut: the world was pulling US gas out through the export terminals, tightening the domestic market. Exporting your surplus does not just earn you the foreign price; it slowly drags your home price toward the world price. American consumers and politicians discovered this the hard way, which is why US LNG export permitting became a live policy fight — exports are a transfer of cheap domestic energy into the global pool, and that pool can bite back.

#### Worked example: the landed-cost stack of a US cargo into Asia

This is the calculation that decides whether a US cargo is worth shipping at all. Build the cost stack one layer at a time, all in US dollars per MMBtu.

- **Feed gas** at Henry Hub: assume **\$3.00/MMBtu**.
- **Liquefaction fee**, quoted as ~115% of feed plus a fixed tolling charge. The 15% covers the fuel the plant burns: 1.15 × \$3.00 = \$3.45. Add a fixed liquefaction toll of ~**\$2.50/MMBtu** to recover the plant's capital. Running total: \$3.45 + \$2.50 = **\$5.95/MMBtu**.
- **Shipping** Gulf Coast → Northeast Asia, including boil-off, charter, and the Panama/Suez routing: in a normal market, roughly **\$2.00/MMBtu**. Running total: \$5.95 + \$2.00 = **\$7.95/MMBtu**.
- **Regasification** at the import terminal: ~**\$0.50/MMBtu**. All-in landed cost: ≈ **\$8.45/MMBtu**.

Now compare to the destination price. If JKM (Asia) is at **\$11.50/MMBtu** (the 2024 level), the gross margin is \$11.50 − \$8.45 ≈ **\$3.05/MMBtu**. On a 3.5 TBtu cargo that is 3,500,000 MMBtu × \$3.05 ≈ **\$10.7 million of gross margin on a single ship**. The takeaway: a US cargo only sails when the destination price clears the entire \$8-ish cost stack, and the margin above that stack is the prize the whole industry is built to capture.

## The economics: it is all a spread, capped by freight

Step back from the cost stack and the whole LNG trade resolves into one idea: it is an **arbitrage on the spread between three regional prices**, and the spread is capped by the cost of shipping a cargo from the cheap basin to the expensive one.

Think of the three benchmarks — Henry Hub, TTF, JKM — as three pools at different heights, connected by a pump (the LNG chain) that has a fixed running cost. As long as the height difference between two pools is *greater* than the cost of pumping, cargoes flow and the flow narrows the gap. The instant the gap shrinks to *equal* the pumping cost, the flow stops — there is no profit left in moving the next cargo. So the regional prices cannot diverge by more than the all-in cost to ship LNG between them. That ceiling — roughly the \$8-ish landed-cost stack from the worked example — is what couples the three markets together. They are not *one* price, but they are no longer *independent* either; they are tethered, with the tether length set by freight.

![Three regional gas prices in a common unit and the LNG arbitrage spread, 2021 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/lng-and-the-globalization-of-gas-shipping-the-uncontainable-3.png)

The figure makes the spread visible. Henry Hub (the US source) sits low — \$2 to \$6 across these years. TTF and JKM (the European and Asian destinations) sit far above, and in 2022 they exploded to the mid-30s while Henry Hub stayed near \$6. That gigantic gap — a destination price five-to-six times the US source price — is the arbitrage. It is also why every spare US cargo in 2022 was worth a fortune to whoever owned it: the spread was many times the cost to ship.

Contrast this with oil, the commodity that anchors so much of this series, and the difference jumps out. Oil's tether is short: a barrel costs only a dollar or two per barrel-equivalent to ship across an ocean on a cheap tanker, so WTI in Texas and Brent in the North Sea rarely diverge by more than a few dollars — and when they do (the shale-glut era pushed Brent to a sustained premium over WTI), it is measured in single digits. Gas's tether is long *and* it can snap. In normal times the ~\$8 landed-cost stack holds the regional prices within roughly that band of each other. But the tether only binds while there is a spare cargo to move; once the global fleet and the destination terminals are maxed out, *no* amount of price difference can pull another ship across, and the spread is free to blow out to the \$30+ gap of 2022. Oil's arbitrage almost never breaks because spare tankers are plentiful; gas's arbitrage breaks precisely when you need it most, because the physical chain is so much larger, slower, and more specialized. That is the deepest reason gas is structurally more volatile than oil.

#### Worked example: why Asia usually wins the marginal cargo

This is the calculation behind the claim that, in normal years, the world's marginal LNG cargo points east. Take a calm 2024-style market: TTF (Europe) ≈ \$10.50/MMBtu, JKM (Asia) ≈ \$11.50/MMBtu. From the US Gulf, shipping to Europe costs ~\$1.20/MMBtu and to Asia ~\$2.50/MMBtu.

- **Netback to Europe** = \$10.50 − \$1.20 = **\$9.30/MMBtu**.
- **Netback to Asia** = \$11.50 − \$2.50 = **\$9.00/MMBtu**.

Here Europe edges it by \$0.30 — a near tie, which is the normal state: the market arbitrages the two netbacks until they almost equalize. Now suppose a cold Asian winter lifts JKM to \$13.50 while TTF holds at \$10.50. Netback to Asia becomes \$13.50 − \$2.50 = \$11.00, beating Europe's \$9.30 by \$1.70/MMBtu, and cargoes swing east. The takeaway: Asia's structural premium (no domestic gas, fewest alternatives) usually keeps its *price* above Europe's by enough to overcome its longer freight — so in a normal year cargoes drift toward Asia, and it takes an abnormal event like a European supply cut to flip them west, which is exactly what 2022 was.

### Destination flexibility: the revolution that made gas a traded commodity

There is a second, subtler revolution buried in this, and it is contractual rather than physical. For decades, LNG was sold on rigid, **destination-locked** contracts: a twenty-year deal between one liquefaction plant and one buyer, with the cargo legally tied to a single delivery port and the price set by a formula pegged to oil (more on that below). Under those terms there was no arbitrage to do — the cargo went where the contract said, full stop.

The modern market broke that lock. A growing share of LNG, especially US volumes, now ships on **destination-flexible** terms: the cargo's owner can divert the ship mid-voyage to whichever basin pays the most. That flexibility is what turned LNG from a bilateral utility arrangement into a *traded commodity* with a spot market, a forward curve, and an active class of traders whose entire job is to point ships at the highest price.

![Decision tree showing an LNG cargo sailing to whichever basin pays the higher netback after shipping](/imgs/blogs/lng-and-the-globalization-of-gas-shipping-the-uncontainable-4.png)

The decision a flexible cargo's owner makes is the one in the figure: compute the **netback** for each destination — the price you would receive there *minus* the cost to ship the cargo there — and send the ship to whichever netback is higher. Europe is a short, cheap haul from the US Gulf; Asia is a long, expensive one. So Europe wins on freight, but Asia often wins on price. The cargo goes wherever the price advantage beats the freight disadvantage.

#### Worked example: the 2022 reroute — Asia vs Europe netback

This is the calculation that physically rerouted the world's gas in 2022. Take a US cargo loaded at the Gulf Coast and price its netback to each basin, in US dollars per MMBtu. Use the 2022 crisis levels: TTF (Europe) ≈ \$37, JKM (Asia) ≈ \$34, with shipping of ~\$1.20 to Europe (short haul) and ~\$2.50 to Asia (long haul).

- **Netback to Europe** = \$37 − \$1.20 freight = **\$35.80/MMBtu**.
- **Netback to Asia** = \$34 − \$2.50 freight = **\$31.50/MMBtu**.

Europe wins by \$35.80 − \$31.50 = **\$4.30/MMBtu**. On a 3.5 TBtu cargo that is 3,500,000 × \$4.30 ≈ **\$15 million more** to deliver the same ship to Rotterdam than to Tokyo. So the rational owner pointed the ship at Europe — and so did essentially every other flexible cargo. In normal years Asia, the premium market, attracts most US LNG; in 2022 the European price overshot Asia's *and* sat at the short end of the freight, so the netback math flipped and the Atlantic basin pulled the cargoes that Asia had expected. The takeaway: the destination of the world's marginal gas cargo is decided by a one-line netback comparison, and in a crisis that one line redraws the energy map.

That reroute is exactly what the data shows. When Russian pipeline gas to Europe was cut after the February 2022 invasion of Ukraine, Europe had to replace tens of billions of cubic meters with seaborne LNG essentially overnight. It did so by *outbidding Asia* for every available cargo — pushing TTF to the levels that made the European netback win. But before we look at that price, there is one more physical constraint to understand, because it is what kept the dislocation so violent and so long-lived.

### The hidden constraint: ships and terminals, not just gas

It is tempting to treat the LNG arbitrage as a pure price calculation, but there is a physical layer underneath that bites in exactly the moments you most need it. To execute the 2022 reroute, three things all had to exist at once: spare *liquefaction* capacity to make the gas, spare *ships* to carry it, and spare *regasification* capacity at the destination to land it. In a calm market all three have slack. In a crisis they bind simultaneously.

The ship constraint is the subtle one. The global LNG tanker fleet is finite — a few hundred specialized vessels — and a newbuild takes a couple of years to deliver. When 2022 redirected huge volumes from short Atlantic hauls to... well, Europe is the short haul, so the *good* news for Europe was that pulling cargoes from the Atlantic basin actually shortened average voyages. But the deeper crunch was on the receiving end: Europe did not have enough regasification terminals to land all the LNG it suddenly needed. The continent had been built around pipelines, not import jetties. Germany, which had *zero* LNG import terminals before the crisis, scrambled to deploy **floating storage and regasification units (FSRUs)** — ships that double as floating import terminals, moored offshore — to add regas capacity in months rather than years. That FSRU dash is why Europe could physically absorb the cargoes at all. The lesson for a trader: the inter-basin spread can scream "ship it here" while the destination physically cannot receive another cargo, in which case the spread stays blown out until terminal capacity catches up. Price is the signal; steel is the constraint.

![TTF European gas price spiking in 2022 with the August monthly peak marked](/imgs/blogs/lng-and-the-globalization-of-gas-shipping-the-uncontainable-5.png)

TTF ran from around 9 EUR/MWh in 2020 — a sleepy, cheap, pipeline-fed market — to a monthly peak near 339 in August 2022, before settling back to the 34–41 range in 2023–24, still far above its pre-crisis level. That spike was not Europe paying more for the same pipeline gas; it was Europe *competing in the global LNG auction for the first time at full force*, and winning by paying whatever it took to pull ships away from Asia. The price had to be high enough to (a) win the netback war against Asia and (b) destroy enough European demand to balance the books, because no new supply could arrive for years.

## How LNG is priced: oil-indexed legacy vs hub-indexed spot

To finish the economics, you have to understand the contracts, because LNG carries two completely different pricing DNA side by side, and the shift from one to the other is the story of the market growing up.

![Before and after comparison of oil-indexed legacy LNG contracts versus hub-indexed spot pricing](/imgs/blogs/lng-and-the-globalization-of-gas-shipping-the-uncontainable-6.png)

**The legacy model: oil-indexed, destination-locked, twenty-year.** When the LNG industry was young, there was no liquid gas-price benchmark to write a contract against — so the industry borrowed oil's. A classic Asian LNG contract priced the cargo as a *slope* times the oil price: roughly **11–14% of the price of crude**, with a lag of a few months. The logic was historical (gas competed with oil in power generation) and practical (oil had a deep, trusted price). These contracts were 20-year **take-or-pay** deals — the buyer paid even if it could not physically take the gas — and the cargo was **destination-locked** to one port. The whole structure existed to make a \$10-billion liquefaction plant financeable: the bank lending the money wanted a guaranteed 20-year buyer at a formula price, not exposure to a spot market that did not yet exist.

**The modern model: hub-indexed, flexible, traded.** As Europe and then a global spot market developed real gas benchmarks — TTF, JKM, Henry Hub — contracts began pricing off *the gas market itself* rather than oil. A modern cargo might be priced as "Henry Hub × 115% + a fee" (for US export volumes) or simply indexed to TTF or JKM. Tenors shrank from 20 years to a few years or a single spot cargo, and destination clauses loosened. This is the model that makes the arbitrage above possible — because only a hub-indexed, destination-flexible cargo can chase the netback.

#### Worked example: oil-indexed vs hub-indexed, same day

Take a single delivery date and price the same cargo both ways, in US dollars per MMBtu, to see how far apart the two regimes can sit. Suppose Brent crude is \$80/barrel and the gas hubs are at their 2024 levels (JKM ≈ \$11.50).

- **Oil-indexed price**: a 12% slope on Brent. There are about 5.8 MMBtu of energy in a barrel of oil, so \$80/bbl ÷ 5.8 ≈ \$13.80/MMBtu of oil-equivalent energy. At a 12% slope the formula gives 0.12 × \$80 ≈ \$9.60/MMBtu (slopes are quoted directly on the dollar oil price, plus a small constant; call it **\$9.60–10.50/MMBtu** with the constant).
- **Hub-indexed price**: simply JKM, ≈ **\$11.50/MMBtu**.

In this snapshot the hub price sits *above* the oil-indexed price — so a buyer locked into the old oil formula is paying less than spot, and a seller on the oil formula is leaving money on the table. Flip the inputs — oil at \$110 and a gas glut pushing JKM to \$8 — and the oil-indexed buyer is badly overpaying. The takeaway: oil-indexation prices gas off a fuel it no longer competes with, so the oil-indexed and hub-indexed prices wander apart with no force pulling them together — which is exactly why the market migrated to hub indexation, where the price tracks real gas scarcity.

#### Worked example: a full per-cargo P&L

Put it all together into the trade a real LNG desk books. You own a flexible 3.5 TBtu cargo of US gas. Your costs are the landed-cost stack from earlier — feed at Henry Hub \$3.00, liquefaction 1.15 × \$3.00 + \$2.50 toll = \$5.95, shipping to Asia \$2.00, regas \$0.50 — for an all-in delivered cost of **\$8.45/MMBtu**. Asia (JKM) is bid at **\$11.50**.

- **Revenue**: 3,500,000 MMBtu × \$11.50 = **\$40.25 million**.
- **Cost**: 3,500,000 MMBtu × \$8.45 = **\$29.58 million**.
- **Gross margin**: \$40.25m − \$29.58m = **\$10.67 million per cargo**.

Now stress it. If a warm winter pushes JKM down to \$9.00 while your costs are fixed, revenue falls to 3,500,000 × \$9.00 = \$31.5m and margin collapses to \$31.5m − \$29.58m ≈ **\$1.9 million** — still positive, so the cargo still sails, but the prize is gone. Push JKM to \$8.00 and the margin turns slightly negative against the *full* cost stack, though if your feed and liquefaction are already sunk (you signed a take-or-pay toll), you ship anyway as long as the price beats the *marginal* shipping + regas of ~\$2.50. The takeaway: the cargo-level P&L is a thin spread on a huge notional, which is why LNG traders live and die by the inter-basin spread and freight, not by the absolute level of gas.

## Common misconceptions

**"LNG means gas has one world price now, like oil."** No — it has three prices that are *tethered*, not fused. The tether length is the all-in cost to ship LNG between basins, roughly \$8/MMBtu of landed cost in the worked example. As long as building liquefaction is slow and shipping is costly, the regional prices can still diverge by several dollars — and in a crisis like 2022, by *tens* of dollars, because the physical fleet hits its limit and the tether stops binding. Oil's tether is a couple of dollars; gas's is many times that.

**"The 2022 spike was about Europe paying more for Russian gas."** No — it was about Europe *replacing* Russian pipeline gas with seaborne LNG and bidding against Asia for it. TTF went to ~339 EUR/MWh not because pipeline gas got expensive but because Europe entered the global LNG auction at full force for the first time and had to win every available cargo. The price was the cost of pulling ships across the Atlantic that would otherwise have gone east.

**"Liquefaction is just cooling — it can't be that expensive."** The fuel alone eats 8–12% of the feed gas, and the plant costs \$5–15 billion per train and takes years. That capital and that lead time are the entire reason LNG supply is slow and gas shocks last for years. The ~115%-of-Henry-Hub liquefaction fee in the worked examples is not a markup for profit; most of it is fuel burned and capital recovered.

**"A US cargo always goes to the highest-priced market."** It goes to the highest *netback* — price minus the freight to get there. Asia usually posts the higher price but sits at the long, expensive end of the freight, so Europe (short haul) frequently wins the cargo even at a lower headline price. The 2022 reroute worked example shows Europe winning by \$4.30/MMBtu of netback despite Asia and Europe being within a few dollars on the screen.

**"Oil-indexed LNG contracts are obsolete."** A large slice of long-term Asian LNG is still oil-indexed, because lenders financing a \$10-billion plant still want a 20-year formula buyer. The market is *migrating* to hub indexation, not done migrating. The two regimes coexist, and the gap between an oil-indexed price and a hub price (the worked example showed them several dollars apart) is itself a source of trades and disputes.

**"The arbitrage is risk-free profit."** It is not. A flexible cargo's owner is exposed to the spread *moving against them mid-voyage*: a three-week trip to Asia is three weeks during which JKM can fall, Europe can rally, or freight rates can spike, turning a profitable netback into a loss by the time the ship arrives. Traders hedge this with paper — selling JKM or TTF futures against the physical cargo — which is the series' physical-meets-paper spine in its purest form: a steel ship full of cold liquid, with its price risk laid off in a financial contract. The "arbitrage" is really a spread *bet* with execution risk, shipping risk, and basis risk, not a guaranteed gain.

## How it shows up in real markets

**The 2016 inflection — Sabine Pass.** Before February 2016, the US shipped zero LNG and Henry Hub was a purely domestic price, decoupled from the rest of the world. The first Sabine Pass cargo physically connected US shale gas to the global market for the first time. From that moment, Henry Hub stopped being an island: a high enough TTF or JKM could now reach back across the ocean and bid up US gas. The arbitrage in this post did not exist as a meaningful force until that ship sailed. Worth dwelling on the irony: Sabine Pass was originally built, at great expense, as an *import* terminal in the mid-2000s, when the US feared it was running out of gas. Shale arrived, the thesis inverted, and the owners spent billions more to bolt liquefaction trains onto an import terminal and run it backwards. That single reversal — from "America needs to import gas" to "America is the world's largest gas exporter" — happened in barely a decade and is one of the fastest, largest swings in the history of any commodity market. It is the cleanest possible illustration that even the most capital-intensive, slow-to-build physical chain will eventually bend to a large enough price gap.

**The 2021–2023 European crisis.** This is the case study that proved the whole thesis. Russian pipeline gas to Europe fell through 2021 and was largely cut in 2022. Europe had no fast substitute — pipelines take years, and the only flexible supply was seaborne LNG running near global capacity. So Europe did the only thing it could: it bid TTF up to ~339 EUR/MWh to (a) outbid Asia in the netback war and (b) destroy its own demand. US flexible cargoes that would normally have gone to Asia turned around and sailed to Europe instead — visible in the export and TTF figures above. The crisis lasted *years*, exactly as the slow-supply argument predicts, and only eased once a mild winter, demand destruction, and a wave of new liquefaction capacity finally caught up. It is the cleanest real-world demonstration that LNG links the three markets and that the link can be overwhelmed.

Trace the mechanism step by step, because every link in this post lights up. The shock was a supply cut, not a demand surge — Europe needed to *replace* roughly 150 billion cubic meters a year of Russian pipeline gas. The only replacement was LNG, and the global LNG fleet was already near flat-out feeding Asia. So Europe could only get cargoes by paying enough to out-netback Asia (the reroute worked example), which meant TTF had to rise *above* JKM net of the freight difference — and it did. But winning the auction was only half the problem: Europe also had to physically *receive* the gas, which is why the FSRU dash mattered, and it had to *use less*, which is why TTF had to stay painfully high for over a year to grind down industrial and household demand. Notice what was absent the whole time: any meaningful new supply, because none could be built on the crisis's timescale. The price did all the work — rationing demand and redirecting ships — precisely because the physical chain could not flex. That is the slow-supply thesis playing out in real time, and it is why the episode reads like a controlled experiment for everything in this post. There is a quiet sequel, too: a chunk of the Asian buyers who lost their cargoes to Europe in 2022 (notably in price-sensitive South and Southeast Asia) simply *could not afford* gas at those levels and switched back to coal or went without — a reminder that the "demand destruction" the price achieves is not abstract, it is real factories idled and real grids burning dirtier fuel.

**Qatar and Australia, the other giants.** The US is the largest exporter, but it is not alone, and the other two giants have very different DNA. Qatar sits on the world's cheapest, largest gas field — the North Field, the offshore extension of Iran's South Pars — and has been a top-two LNG exporter for two decades. Its gas is so cheap to produce that Qatar can undercut almost anyone on the liquefaction-fee leg of the cost stack, and its North Field expansion is designed precisely to defend that low-cost crown as new US supply floods in. Qatar also still sells a large share of its LNG on the old oil-indexed, long-term, destination-locked model — it is a national champion financing 20-year offtake, not a fleet of traders chasing spot netbacks — which is part of why a big chunk of the global market is still oil-indexed. Australia, by contrast, built a huge LNG industry on the back of expensive offshore and coal-seam gas to feed nearby Asia; its advantage is geography (the shortest haul to Japan and Korea) rather than cheap molecules, and several of its projects struggle with high costs and aging fields.

The three together — the US, Qatar, and Australia — supply the bulk of the world's LNG, and the competitive contest between them is the supply story of the 2020s. The US sells flexible, hub-indexed, mostly Henry-Hub-plus-fee cargoes that go wherever the netback points. Qatar sells cheap, long-term, often oil-indexed volumes locked to specific buyers. Australia sells geography. A trader reading the market has to know which kind of supply is at the margin, because only the flexible US-style cargoes actually arbitrage the spread day to day; the locked Qatari and Australian volumes flow on their contracts regardless.

The question that hangs over all of it is a supply race. A wave of new US and Qatari trains is due to start up across the mid-decade, and when it lands it will *lengthen the tether* — adding so much liquefaction capacity that the regional spreads should compress back toward the cost of freight and away from the crisis blowouts of 2022. If that wave arrives on schedule and demand does not keep pace, the world could swing from the 2022 shortage to a glut, with spreads narrow and the cheapest producers (Qatar, the best US projects) winning the volume war. If demand — especially Chinese and broader Asian — grows fast enough to soak it up, the tether stays tight and spreads stay wide. That balance, between a known supply wave and uncertain demand, is the real long-horizon bet a gas trader is making, and it is far more about the build timeline in the figure earlier than about any single day's price.

#### Worked example: the spread signal vs the shipping cost

Here is how a trader turns all of this into a daily decision. The rule is simple: **compare the inter-basin spread to the cost of shipping a cargo between those basins.** Suppose on a given day TTF is \$12/MMBtu and JKM is \$15/MMBtu, and the cost to ship a flexible Atlantic cargo to Asia rather than Europe is ~\$1.30/MMBtu of *extra* freight.

- Asia–Europe spread = \$15 − \$12 = **\$3.00/MMBtu**.
- Extra freight to choose Asia over Europe = **\$1.30/MMBtu**.

Since the spread (\$3.00) exceeds the extra freight (\$1.30), the cargo should sail east: Asia's netback beats Europe's by \$3.00 − \$1.30 = **\$1.70/MMBtu**, or about \$6 million on a 3.5 TBtu cargo. If instead the spread were only \$1.00 — below the \$1.30 freight — the cargo stays in the Atlantic. The takeaway: the signal that matters is *spread minus freight*, not the absolute price of gas anywhere; when that difference is positive and widening, cargoes physically move toward it, and a trader can position ahead of the flow.

## The takeaway: what to actually watch

LNG is the answer to a physical impossibility — moving a gas across an ocean — and every feature of the market falls out of how expensive and slow that answer is. Hold these in your head and you can read the global gas market:

- **Watch the inter-basin spread, net of freight — not the absolute price.** The single number that moves cargoes is *destination price minus the cost to ship there*. When TTF − Henry Hub or JKM − Henry Hub blows out past the ~\$8 landed-cost stack, US export economics are wide open and ships sail. When the spread compresses toward freight cost, the arbitrage closes and flows slow. This is the live read on where the world's marginal cargo is going.
- **Respect the lead time.** Liquefaction takes years and tens of billions. That is why gas shocks are measured in years, and why a tight market clears by destroying demand rather than by fast new supply. When you see a spike, ask: is new liquefaction capacity due soon, or is the fleet maxed out? The answer tells you whether the spike fades in quarters or persists for years.
- **Know which contract regime a cargo is on.** An oil-indexed, destination-locked legacy cargo cannot arbitrage; a hub-indexed, destination-flexible cargo can and will. The migration from the first to the second is what turned LNG into a traded commodity, and the share of flexible volume is what determines how fast the regional prices converge.
- **Treat the supply wave as the macro bet.** The mid-decade flood of new US and Qatari trains is the structural force that should compress spreads back toward freight cost. Whether it arrives on time, and whether demand (especially Asian) grows to absorb it, is the real long-horizon question.
- **Distinguish the run decision from the build decision.** Once a plant is built, its gas keeps flowing through low prices because only variable costs gate the next cargo — so a glut clears slowly and painfully. New supply, by contrast, only appears when prices are high enough, *years earlier*, to justify a fresh final investment decision. This asymmetry — supply that is sticky on the way down and glacial on the way up — is why the market overshoots in both directions.
- **Know which terminals and ships are free.** The spread tells you where a cargo *wants* to go; regasification and shipping capacity tell you whether it physically *can*. A blown-out spread that cannot be arbitraged because the destination has no spare import terminal will stay blown out until steel is added — as Europe's FSRU scramble showed in 2022–23.

Put those together and you can read almost any gas headline. A cold snap in Asia? Watch JKM lift and the marginal cargo swing east. A pipeline cut in Europe? Watch TTF blow past Asia and pull Atlantic cargoes west, with the spike's *duration* set by how soon new liquefaction and regas can answer. A new US train starting up? Watch the inter-basin spreads compress a notch toward freight cost. None of it is mysterious once you hold the physical chain and the two-cost structure in your head.

Tie it back to the series' spine. A commodity price is a physical thing forced through a financial contract — and gas is the most physical of them all. The −162°C, the 600× shrinkage, the cryogenic ship, the multi-year plant: those constraints *are* the market. They set the tether between the three regional prices, they make supply slow, and they hand the trade to whoever can read the spread against the freight and point a ship the right way. Gas spent a century as three separate prices because it could not travel. LNG is the cold, expensive, slow machine that is finally — partially, contestably — making it one.
