In the middle of 2024, the price of robusta coffee on the ICE exchange in London did something it had never done in the contract's history: it traded above the price of arabica. To anyone who does not follow coffee, that sentence is meaningless. To anyone who does, it was an earthquake. For decades the rule had been simple and unbroken — **arabica is the premium bean and robusta is the cheap one**. Arabica goes into the bag of whole beans you grind at home; robusta goes into the tin of instant and into the cheap filler of supermarket espresso. Arabica always cost more, often two or three times more, per pound. That gap, the **arabica premium**, was one of the most stable relationships in all of agricultural commodities. And in 2024 it nearly vanished.

The reason sat almost entirely in one place: the **Central Highlands of Vietnam**, a plateau of red volcanic soil around the city of Buon Ma Thuot, where smallholders on a hectare or two of land grow close to **40% of the world's robusta**. That season the rains failed, a long drought baked the region during the critical flowering and bean-filling months, the coffee trees were getting old, and — quietly, over several years — a lot of farmers had been digging up their coffee to plant durian and other fruit that paid better. The crop came in short. And because Vietnam is not just *a* robusta producer but *the* robusta producer, there was no other origin big enough to fill the hole. The world price simply had to rise until enough demand was rationed away. Robusta roughly doubled, from around **\$2,000 a tonne** to over **\$4,000**, and the strangest thing happened to Vietnam's national accounts: the country exported *less* coffee by weight than the year before and earned *more* money than ever before.

This post is about that whole machine — how Vietnam, a country that grew almost no coffee a hundred years ago, became the world's number-two coffee exporter and the king of robusta; what robusta actually is and why it is its own market, not a discount version of arabica; how the price of a bean in a London clearinghouse becomes a price in Vietnamese dong paid at a farm gate in Dak Lak; and what 2024 taught everyone about how fragile a concentrated origin really is. It is a story about the spine of this whole series — **a physical thing (a coffee cherry) forced through a financial contract (the ICE London future)** — told from the side of the world that actually grows the stuff.

## Foundations: what robusta is, and how Vietnam came to own it

Start from zero, because most coffee drinkers have never heard the word robusta and have certainly never tasted it knowingly.

A **commodity** is a standardized physical good — one tonne of a given grade is interchangeable with another tonne of the same grade — which is exactly what lets it trade at a single public price on an exchange instead of being haggled over sack by sack. Coffee is one of the oldest and most-traded soft commodities on earth, drunk by something like two-thirds of the adult population of the planet every day. But "coffee" is not one thing. It trades as **two completely separate species, on two separate exchanges, in two separate units**, and the difference between them is the key that unlocks Vietnam's whole role in the market.

- **Arabica** (*Coffea arabica*) is the higher-altitude, cooler-climate, more delicate species. It has more acidity, more aromatic complexity, less caffeine, and a softer, less bitter cup. It is what specialty cafés and home grinders mostly use. It trades as the **ICE "C" contract in New York, quoted in US cents per pound**. Brazil and Colombia dominate its supply.
- **Robusta** (*Coffea canephora*) is the lower-altitude, hot-lowland, hardier species. It carries roughly **twice the caffeine** of arabica, a heavier body, a harsher and more bitter taste, and — crucially for a farmer — it is far more resistant to heat, disease, and pests, and it yields more beans per tree. It trades as the **ICE Robusta contract in London, quoted in US dollars per tonne**. Vietnam dominates its supply.

That last fact is the one to hold onto. **Arabica and robusta are priced in different cities, in different currencies, in different units.** Arabica in cents per pound in New York; robusta in dollars per tonne in London. When you read that "coffee" went up, you have to ask *which* coffee, because the two can and do move apart — and in 2024 they moved toward each other in a way that had never happened before.

Now, why robusta tastes "worse" but matters enormously. Robusta's heavier body and higher caffeine make it the **backbone of instant coffee** — the freeze-dried and spray-dried product that is still a huge share of global consumption, especially across Asia and the developing world — and a standard component of **espresso blends**, where a slug of robusta adds body, a thicker crema, and a caffeine kick that pure arabica cannot give. So robusta is not a niche. It is roughly **40% of all the coffee the world drinks by volume**, and it is the workhorse bean of the mass market. It just rarely gets its name on the bag.

### How Vietnam came to grow 40% of it

A century ago, Vietnam was a marginal coffee origin. The French planted some during the colonial period, but the crop stayed small. The transformation came after the country's *Doi Moi* economic reforms began in 1986, when the government and farmers turned the **Central Highlands** — a cool, high plateau of deep red **basaltic (volcanic) soil**, ideal for robusta — into a coffee frontier. Over roughly two decades, from the late 1980s through the 2000s, Vietnam went from almost nothing to the world's number-two coffee producer overall and the undisputed number-one robusta producer. The growth was so fast that it crashed the world robusta price in the early 2000s by flooding the market — the first lesson that this single origin is now big enough to move the global price by itself.

The geography is tight and specific. The heart of it is four provinces:

- **Dak Lak** — the single largest coffee province, home to **Buon Ma Thuot**, universally called the coffee capital of Vietnam (it hosts a biennial coffee festival and is the spiritual center of the trade).
- **Lam Dong** — to the south, the only province that also grows a meaningful amount of arabica (in the cooler highlands around Da Lat), but still mostly robusta.
- **Gia Lai** and **Dak Nong** — the other two big robusta provinces of the plateau.

Across these provinces the production model is overwhelmingly **smallholder**: hundreds of thousands of farming households, most working **one to three hectares** each, drying their own beans on tarps and patios, and selling to a web of local **collectors** (middlemen) who aggregate the crop and pass it up to exporters. There are very few giant plantations. This matters enormously for how the price behaves and how the chain works, and we will come back to it.

That smallholder structure has two consequences worth naming up front. First, it makes the supply **resilient in normal years but brittle in a shock**: with so many independent farmers, no single estate failing can dent the crop, but a region-wide stress like drought hits everyone at once, and there is no large, professionally-managed operator with the irrigation and capital to ride it out. Second, it makes the **information flow slow and the cash market opaque**: there is no single price board the way a grain elevator posts a number. The farmgate price is discovered farm by farm, district by district, through thousands of small cash transactions with collectors, which is why the *basis* — the gap between that farmgate cash price and the world futures price — is where so much of the local trade's skill and profit lives.

### How the bean is processed: why Vietnam's robusta is "natural"

One more foundation matters for understanding the product Vietnam actually sells. After picking, a coffee cherry has to be turned into a dried, stable green bean that can be bagged and shipped, and there are two main ways to do it. In the **washed (wet) process**, the fruit is pulped off and the beans are fermented and washed before drying — this is common for high-end arabica and gives a cleaner, brighter cup. In the **natural (dry) process**, the whole cherry is simply laid out in the sun to dry and the dried fruit is later hulled off. Vietnamese robusta is overwhelmingly **natural-processed**: farmers spread the cherries on patios, tarps, and roadsides to dry in the post-harvest sun, then sell the dried beans to collectors who hull and grade them.

This is not a quality footnote — it is part of *why* the supply is concentrated and weather-exposed. Sun-drying depends on dry weather right after harvest; unseasonal rain at harvest can cause beans to mold or ferment unevenly, knocking down quality and the price the farmer receives. So Vietnamese robusta's price and quality are exposed to weather *twice*: once during the growing season (drought cuts the crop) and again at harvest (rain ruins the drying). A trader watching the Central Highlands therefore watches the rainfall calendar with two different worries at two different times of year.

![Vietnam coffee exports volume flat to down while export value surged in 2024](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-robusta-coffee-the-worlds-number-two-exporter-2.png)

The chart above is the single most important picture of the modern Vietnamese coffee story. The blue bars are export **volume** in million tonnes; they are flat-to-falling, sitting around 1.5–1.8 million tonnes for years and then *dropping* to roughly 1.35 million tonnes in 2024. The green line is export **value** in US dollars, and it does the opposite — it climbs steadily and then leaps in 2024 to a record near **\$5.5 billion**, and higher still into 2025. Volume down, value up. Everything else in this article is, in one way or another, an explanation of why those two lines diverge — and what it tells a reader about how a concentrated commodity origin actually behaves under stress.

### The harvest cycle and why timing matters

Robusta in Vietnam has **one main harvest a year**, running roughly from **October to January**, with the bulk of the picking in November and December. This single-harvest, single-origin concentration is structurally important: it means the world's robusta supply for an entire year is largely set in a three-month window in one country. If the weather during the prior flowering and bean-fill period (around the dry-season months) was bad, the world finds out all at once at harvest, and there is no second crop and no southern-hemisphere origin big enough to smooth it out. (Brazil grows a robusta variety it calls *conilon*, and it is the world's number-two robusta source, but it is not large enough to offset a Vietnamese shortfall — and Brazil's own arabica was struggling in 2024 too.)

The harvest timing also drives a yearly rhythm in the cash market: prices and farmer selling are heaviest right after harvest (fresh supply hits), and the **basis** — the gap between the local farmgate price and the world futures price — tightens or widens through the year as stocks are drawn down. Traders who follow Vietnamese robusta watch the harvest weather, the pace of farmer selling, and the size of carry-over stocks the way an oil trader watches OPEC.

## Robusta is its own market: the arabica–robusta spread

Here is the single most useful framing in the entire coffee complex, and the one that made 2024 historic. Because arabica and robusta are different species serving partly different uses, there is normally a **price gap** between them — the **arabica premium** — and that gap is itself a tradeable, watchable quantity that tells you something real about the world.

Most of the time, arabica trades well above robusta. The premium exists because arabica is harder to grow (it needs altitude and cool temperatures, is more disease-prone, yields less per tree), tastes better, and is what the higher-margin specialty and home market wants. When you see a big arabica premium, it means the two beans are being treated as genuinely different products for different buyers.

But the premium is not fixed. It **widens** when arabica is scarce or robusta is plentiful, and it **narrows** when robusta is scarce or arabica is plentiful. And here is the behavioral hinge: when the premium narrows enough, **roasters substitute**. A blender who was using, say, 70% arabica and 30% robusta will, if robusta gets relatively cheap, push more robusta into the blend to cut costs; and conversely, if robusta gets expensive relative to arabica — which is exactly what happened in 2024 — the cost advantage of using robusta evaporates, and some blenders shift back toward arabica or toward cheaper robusta origins. The spread is a real economic signal that ripples into what is actually in your cup.

![The arabica premium over robusta narrowed sharply as robusta nearly caught arabica](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-robusta-coffee-the-worlds-number-two-exporter-4.png)

To compare the two on one chart you have to put them in the same unit, because arabica is quoted in US cents per pound and robusta in US dollars per tonne. One tonne is about 2,205 pounds, so a price of *c* cents per pound converts to roughly *c* × 22 dollars per tonne. Do that, and the chart above shows both as USD per tonne. The lavender line is arabica, the amber line is robusta, and the pink band between them is the arabica premium. Watch it collapse: in 2021 arabica sat roughly **\$1,800 a tonne above robusta**; by 2024 the gap had shrunk to roughly **\$1,200** and on a daily basis at points in mid-2024 it briefly **inverted** — robusta actually traded *above* arabica for the first time, something the annual averages smooth away but which genuinely happened on the screen. That inversion is the headline event: it meant the "cheap" bean had become, momentarily, the expensive one.

#### Worked example: a roaster's blend decision when the spread narrows

A mid-sized roaster makes a supermarket espresso blend that is 60% arabica, 40% robusta. Suppose arabica costs **\$4.50/kg** and robusta costs **\$2.00/kg** in a normal year. The blend's bean cost is `0.60 × $4.50 + 0.40 × $2.00 = $2.70 + $0.80 = $3.50/kg`. Robusta's cheapness is what makes the blend affordable — every point of robusta saves money versus pure arabica.

Now jump to the 2024 squeeze: robusta has doubled to **\$4.00/kg** while arabica has risen more modestly to **\$5.20/kg**. The same 60/40 blend now costs `0.60 × $5.20 + 0.40 × $4.00 = $3.12 + $1.60 = $4.72/kg` — a **35% jump** in raw bean cost, and notice that **robusta is now contributing almost as much per kilo as arabica**. The whole economic logic of putting robusta in the blend — that it is cheaper — has nearly broken. The roaster's options are ugly: raise the shelf price, shrink the pack, accept a thinner margin, or reformulate toward whatever bean is now relatively cheapest. **When the arabica–robusta spread collapses, the robusta blender loses the cost edge that justified the recipe in the first place — that is the substitution pressure the spread measures.**

## Grades, instant coffee, and moving up the value chain

Before the 2024 drama, it helps to understand *what* Vietnam actually ships and where it sits in the value chain, because that shapes how much of the world price the country captures.

Robusta is sold by **grade**, and the grade is mostly about defects and screen size. The export specifications you will see on a Vietnamese contract are things like **Grade 2, 5% black and broken** — meaning beans graded with up to 5% black, broken, or defective beans by count — which is a standard bulk robusta grade. Higher grades (fewer defects, larger screen size) fetch a premium; lower grades a discount. The grade discount is one of the components of the **basis** we discussed: a farmer whose sun-drying went badly and whose beans have more defects gets a lower farmgate price because the exporter will only be able to sell those beans against a lower grade. This is why the natural-drying weather at harvest feeds directly into the price the farmer receives, bean lot by bean lot.

The overwhelming bulk of Vietnam's exports has historically been **green (unroasted) beans**, sold to international traders and roasters who do the high-margin work — roasting, blending, branding, and turning beans into the **instant coffee** and espresso blends that consumers actually buy. This is the classic commodity-exporter position: you sell the raw material cheap and someone else captures the margin downstream. A tonne of green robusta might leave Vietnam at a few thousand dollars; by the time it is roasted, branded instant coffee on a supermarket shelf in another country, the value has multiplied several times over, and almost none of that multiplication accrues to Vietnam.

Vietnam has been trying, slowly, to climb that chain — building domestic **soluble (instant) coffee** processing capacity (companies like Trung Nguyen and a growing roasting sector) so that more of the value is captured at home rather than exported as raw beans. This is the same value-chain logic that runs through every commodity-exporting economy: the raw producer wants to move downstream toward the branded, processed product where the real margins are, while the importing roasters want to keep the value-add (and the brand) on their side. Robusta's role as the **instant-coffee backbone** is what makes this prize so large — instant coffee is a massive, branded, high-margin global business built largely on a bean that Vietnam grows and mostly sells raw.

#### Worked example: the value Vietnam exports versus the value it captures

Take **one tonne of green robusta** leaving Vietnam in a normal year at an FOB price of about **\$2,000/tonne**. That is what the farmer's beans, minus the basis, are worth at the dock.

Now follow that tonne downstream. Roasting loses some weight, but roughly speaking a tonne of green beans becomes on the order of **830 kg of roasted coffee** (roasting drives off moisture, a "roast loss" of about 15–18%). If that roasted coffee is processed into instant and sold at retail — even at a conservative wholesale-equivalent of, say, **\$10/kg** for finished branded product — the downstream value is on the order of `830 kg × $10 = $8,300`, before you even count the retail markup, which can push the shelf value far higher. **So a tonne of beans that earned Vietnam about \$2,000 at the dock can carry several times that value by the time it is a branded jar on a shelf abroad — which is exactly why a commodity-exporting country fights to move downstream, and why "we grow 40% of the world's robusta" understates and overstates Vietnam's power at the same time: dominant in the raw bean, minor in the finished product.**

## The 2024 supply scare: the year volume fell and value soared

Now the centerpiece. Why did robusta double in 2024, and why did Vietnam's export value rise even as its export volume fell? Three forces converged on the Central Highlands at once, and a fourth force — the price-volume math itself — turned the supply shortfall into a revenue boom.

**Force one: drought.** An El Niño-driven dry spell hit the Central Highlands hard during the 2023–24 dry season, exactly when the robusta trees needed water for flowering and bean development. Reservoirs ran low, irrigation was rationed in places, and the bean-fill suffered. A robusta tree under water stress sets fewer and smaller beans, and that damage is locked in months before harvest — you cannot fix it later.

**Force two: aging trees.** A large share of Vietnam's robusta was planted in the boom decades of the 1990s and 2000s, which means a lot of the trees are now **20-plus years old** and past their peak yield. Coffee trees decline with age; replanting is expensive and takes three to four years to come back into bearing, during which the farmer earns nothing from that plot. So a slow, structural yield drag was already in the system before the drought arrived.

**Force three: farmers switching crops.** This is the quietly decisive one. For several years, **durian** — the spiky, pungent fruit that China cannot get enough of — became dramatically more profitable per hectare than coffee, and Vietnamese farmers, who are rational businesspeople, began ripping out coffee trees and planting durian and other fruit (avocado, passionfruit, pepper) or interplanting them between the coffee rows. Every hectare converted is a hectare permanently out of the world robusta pool. This is the supply equivalent of compound interest working against you: it is gradual, it is driven by relative prices, and it does not reverse quickly even when coffee prices spike, because a durian orchard you just planted is not something you tear up next season.

The economics behind that switch are worth spelling out, because they explain why the acreage loss is so sticky. A well-managed hectare of durian for the China export market could, in the boom years, earn a farmer several times what a hectare of robusta earned — durian sold for export commands a price per kilo many multiples of green coffee's, and a mature orchard yields heavily. Faced with that gap, a farmer with one or two hectares and a family to support does the obvious thing. The catch is the lag and the irreversibility: a durian tree takes around five years to bear well, so the farmer who converted in, say, 2020 spent years earning little from that plot before the fruit came — and once the orchard is mature, they will not bulldoze it to replant coffee just because robusta had one good year. So the coffee acreage that left during the durian rush is gone for the better part of a decade regardless of what the coffee price does next. This is the structural, slow-moving force that re-rated robusta's price floor upward, distinct from the one-off weather hit — and it is exactly the kind of supply response (or non-response) that makes perennial-crop commodities behave so differently from annually-planted grains.

Put the three together — a bad-weather year on top of aging trees on top of a shrinking planted area — and the 2024 crop came in materially short. And because Vietnam *is* the robusta market, that shortfall could not be sourced elsewhere. The price had to rise until demand fell enough to balance the smaller supply. It roughly doubled.

![Robusta coffee ICE London annual average price roughly doubled in 2024](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-robusta-coffee-the-worlds-number-two-exporter-3.png)

The chart above is the robusta price on ICE London, annual averages in USD per tonne. For years it lived in a band around **\$1,300 to \$2,000**. Then it climbs to **\$2,450 in 2023** and explodes to roughly **\$4,100 in 2024** — and keeps going into 2025. That near-doubling from 2023 to 2024 is the single move that drives Vietnam's revenue surge, the spread collapse, and the default drama we will get to. One physical thing — a short crop in one province — expressed through one financial contract, repriced the entire global robusta market.

#### Worked example: the value-up, volume-down math of 2024

This is the arithmetic that surprises everyone, so let us do it cleanly. Vietnam's coffee export **volume** fell from about **1.62 million tonnes in 2023 to about 1.35 million tonnes in 2024** — a drop of roughly `(1.35 − 1.62) / 1.62 ≈ −17%`. At the same time the average **price** Vietnam realized on its exports rose enormously: robusta's annual average roughly **+67%** (from ~\$2,450 to ~\$4,100), and because exporters sell at the higher prevailing prices through the year, the realized export unit value rose by a similar large factor.

Export value is just `volume × price`. If volume is down 17% but the price is up, say, **+70%**, then value changes by `(1 − 0.17) × (1 + 0.70) − 1 = 0.83 × 1.70 − 1 = 1.41 − 1 = +41%`. And indeed Vietnam's coffee export value jumped from about **\$4.2 billion in 2023 to roughly \$5.5 billion in 2024** — about **+31%** in the official figures, and higher still into 2025 as prices kept climbing. **The lesson: for a price-inelastic commodity from a concentrated origin, a supply shortfall is not a revenue disaster for the exporting country — it is a windfall, because the price rises proportionally more than the volume falls.** The country sells less and earns more.

That last point deserves emphasis because it is counterintuitive and it generalizes. For a commodity where world demand does not fall much when the price rises (people keep drinking coffee), a supply shock transfers wealth *to* the producing region, not away from it — as long as your own farm still has a crop to sell. The farmer whose trees survived the drought and who held some beans back had the best year of their life. The farmer whose crop failed got the high price on a tiny quantity. And the exporter who had **pre-sold** beans at the old low price before the spike — that exporter, and the farmers behind those contracts, got crushed. Which brings us to the drama.

## The farmgate, the basis, and the default drama of 2024

To understand the 2024 default story you first have to understand how a Vietnamese smallholder actually gets paid, because it is the mechanism that makes the whole chain tick — and the mechanism that broke.

The farmer does not sell to ICE London. The farmer sells dried beans to a **local collector** (a middleman who drives around the district buying from farms), often for cash, at a **farmgate price quoted in Vietnamese dong per kilogram** (VND/kg). The collector aggregates and sells up to a **provincial trader or directly to an exporter**. The exporter grades, blends to a contract spec, bags, and ships, selling at an **FOB price in USD per tonne** that is tied directly to the ICE London futures price. So the chain runs: **farmer (VND/kg) → collector → exporter (USD/tonne, FOB) → ICE London (USD/tonne, the world reference) → global roaster.**

The crucial concept that links the bottom of that chain to the top is the **basis**: the difference between the local cash price a farmer receives and the world futures price. Working down from the screen, the farmgate price in VND/kg is, roughly, the ICE London price in USD/tonne, **converted to dong at the USD/VND exchange rate, then reduced** by ocean freight, a quality/grade discount, the exporter's margin, and the collector's margin. That stack of deductions *is* the basis. When robusta is scarce and exporters are scrambling to fill contracts, the basis tightens (the farmer gets a bigger slice of the world price); when beans are plentiful right after harvest, it widens.

![How the world price in USD becomes a farmgate price in VND minus the basis](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-robusta-coffee-the-worlds-number-two-exporter-6.png)

The figure above walks that translation: start from the ICE London price in dollars per tonne, convert at the exchange rate, strip out freight and the quality discount and the middleman margins, and you arrive at the farmgate price in dong per kilo. The gap is the basis, and it widens when the market is loose and tightens when it is tight. For everyone in the chain, the basis is where the money is made or lost — the world price is set in London and nobody local controls it, so the whole local game is about capturing a better slice of it.

![Dak Lak farmgate VND price tracks the ICE London world price over time](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-robusta-coffee-the-worlds-number-two-exporter-7.png)

The chart above makes the link concrete and visible over time. The amber line is the real ICE London world price in USD per tonne; the dashed green line is an illustrative Dak Lak farmgate price in thousand VND per kilo, derived from that world price by stripping out a basis and converting at the exchange rate (it is a teaching translation, not a reported series — the caption says so). The point is the shape: **the two move together**. When London doubles, the farmgate roughly doubles. The Vietnamese farmer, sitting on a hillside in Dak Lak with no Bloomberg terminal, is nonetheless paid a price that is chained, link by link, to a futures contract clearing in London. That is the spine of this whole series — a physical bean, priced through a financial contract — made personal.

#### Worked example: a Central Highlands farmer's revenue per hectare (VND, then USD)

Take a representative smallholder in Dak Lak with **2 hectares** of mature robusta yielding about **3 tonnes of green beans per hectare** in a normal year — so **6 tonnes (6,000 kg)** of sellable green coffee.

In a normal pre-spike year, the farmgate price might be about **45,000 VND/kg**. Gross revenue is `6,000 kg × 45,000 VND = 270,000,000 VND`, or about **270 million dong**. At an exchange rate around **24,500 VND per US dollar**, that is `270,000,000 / 24,500 ≈ $11,000` for the whole 2-hectare farm's annual crop — a modest living once you subtract fertilizer, irrigation, hired picking labor, and the cost of the trees aging out.

Now the 2024 spike. The farmgate price roughly **doubles to about 90,000–100,000 VND/kg** (it briefly went even higher). The same 6 tonnes now grosses about `6,000 kg × 95,000 VND ≈ 570,000,000 VND`, or roughly **\$23,000** at the same exchange rate — *if* the farmer still has 6 tonnes to sell. The cruelty of the supply shock is right there: the farmer whose drought-hit trees only yielded 4 tonnes grosses `4,000 × 95,000 ≈ 380,000,000 VND ≈ $15,500` — still a great year, but less than a full-crop farmer captured. **Anchored in dollars, a 2-hectare Dak Lak smallholding swung from roughly \$11,000 to over \$20,000 in annual crop revenue in a single year, purely because a futures contract in London doubled — that is the leverage a concentrated origin gives the farmer who still has a crop.**

### Why farmers defaulted

Now the part that made international headlines. In the Vietnamese coffee trade, it is common for farmers and local traders to **pre-sell** part of their crop forward — to agree, before or early in the harvest, to deliver a quantity of beans at a fixed price, taking cash up front. In a normal year this is sensible risk management: it locks in a price and provides working capital. In 2024 it was a disaster, because many farmers and small traders had pre-sold at the *old*, low prices — and then the spot price doubled.

A farmer who had agreed in mid-2023 to deliver beans at the equivalent of, say, **\$2,400 a tonne** suddenly faced a spot market where those same beans were worth **\$4,000+**. Delivering on the contract meant handing over beans worth \$4,000 and receiving only \$2,400 — leaving \$1,600 per tonne on the table. Many simply **refused to deliver**, or demanded to renegotiate, or sold to whoever would pay spot instead. Exporters who had sold those beans onward to international roasters at fixed prices were caught in the middle: they owed physical coffee they could no longer source at the price they had planned, and some faced **washouts** (paying cash to cancel) or defaults of their own up the chain. The default wave rippled from farm to collector to exporter to international buyer, and it shook trust in Vietnamese forward contracts for a season.

This is a textbook case of why the **four players** in any commodity market — producers, consumers, hedgers, and speculators — exist and how their incentives collide under stress; the player who sold forward (the hedger) gave up the upside to the player who held physical, and when the move is violent enough, the contract itself becomes the battleground. (The full anatomy of those four players is its own post in this series, linked at the end.)

It is worth being precise about *who* bore the loss and who captured the gain, because it is not the simple "farmers won" story the headlines told. A forward sale is a transfer of price risk: the farmer or trader who pre-sold at \$2,400 had, in effect, *sold the upside* to the buyer on the other side of that contract — usually an exporter or international trader who then locked in a matching sale downstream. When the price doubled, that upside was worth a fortune, and it belonged to whoever held the contract, not to whoever held the beans. The default was the bean-holder trying to claw the upside back by refusing to honor the transfer. So the 2024 episode was not really "farmers got rich"; it was a violent fight over *who owned the price move*, fought through the enforceability of forward contracts — and the farmers who came out best were specifically the ones who had **not** pre-sold and who still had physical beans to sell into the spike. The ones who had locked in early, exactly as prudent risk management told them to, watched the windfall accrue to their counterparty and faced the agonizing choice between honoring a ruinous contract and defaulting. That asymmetry — prudence punished, the gambler who held physical rewarded — is the cruelest and most instructive part of the whole episode, and it is why forward markets in physically-settled commodities live or die on contract enforcement and counterparty trust.

#### Worked example: the pre-sale default P&L that triggered the walk-aways

A local trader pre-sold **100 tonnes** of robusta in mid-2023 at a fixed **\$2,400/tonne**, taking a deposit and planning to buy the physical beans from farmers at harvest to fulfill it. The contract value is `100 × $2,400 = $240,000`.

By the time delivery came due in early 2024, the spot price to buy those beans from farmers had risen to about **\$4,100/tonne**. To honor the contract, the trader must now **buy** 100 tonnes at `100 × $4,100 = $410,000` and **deliver** them for the agreed `$240,000`. The loss on performing is `$240,000 − $410,000 = −$170,000` — the trader would lose **\$1,700 on every tonne**, \$170,000 in total, on a deal that was supposed to be a safe lock-in.

Faced with that, the trader's incentive to **default** — to walk away, forfeit the deposit, and sell the beans at spot instead — is overwhelming, because the deposit is far smaller than the \$170,000 loss. **A forward sale converts a price you like into an obligation you may come to hate: when the market moves enough against the short, the contract's penalty for breaking is smaller than the loss for keeping it, and rational actors default — which is exactly the chain reaction that hit Vietnamese coffee in 2024.** This is why robust contract enforcement and creditworthy counterparties matter so much in physical commodity trading, and why the 2024 episode made international roasters far more careful about *who* in Vietnam they sign with.

## Common misconceptions

A few beliefs about Vietnamese coffee and robusta are widespread and wrong, and correcting them with numbers sharpens the whole picture.

**"Robusta is just cheap, low-quality arabica."** No — it is a *different species* with a different chemistry, not a grade of arabica. It has about **twice the caffeine**, a heavier body, and a more bitter cup, and it is the deliberate, preferred ingredient for instant coffee and for adding body and crema to espresso blends. It trades on its own exchange (ICE London) in its own unit (USD/tonne). Treating it as discount arabica misses that it is roughly **40% of all the coffee the world drinks** and serves uses arabica cannot. In 2024 it even traded *above* arabica for a stretch — the "cheap bean" was briefly the expensive one.

**"Vietnam grows coffee everywhere."** No — it is astonishingly concentrated. The bulk comes from **four Central Highlands provinces** (Dak Lak, Lam Dong, Gia Lai, Dak Nong), with Dak Lak and the city of Buon Ma Thuot at the center. That concentration is exactly why one region's drought becomes a global price event: there is no geographic diversification to absorb a local shock, and unlike grains there is no second hemisphere with an offsetting harvest.

**"A bad harvest is bad for Vietnam's coffee economy."** Often the opposite, in aggregate. Because robusta demand is price-inelastic, a supply shortfall can send the price up *more* in percentage terms than the volume falls — so national export **value rises even as volume falls**, as 2024 proved (≈1.35 Mt shipped, ≈\$5.5 bn earned, a record). The pain is concentrated on the specific farmers whose own crop failed and on traders caught short on forward sales, not on the export earnings line.

**"The farmer gets the world price."** No — the farmer gets the world price **minus the basis**: freight, quality discount, and the collector and exporter margins. The whole local trade is a fight over the size of that basis. When the market is tight, the basis compresses and farmers capture more of the London price; when beans are plentiful, the middlemen capture more.

**"Futures speculators caused the 2024 spike."** The spike was overwhelmingly **physical** — drought, old trees, durian conversion, a genuinely short crop in the dominant origin. Speculative flows can amplify and accelerate a move, but they cannot make robusta double for a year against an abundant crop. The cause was beans that did not exist, not paper that did.

**"Robusta and arabica are interchangeable, so blenders can just swap freely."** Only partly, and the limit is the point. A roaster *can* shift a blend's robusta-to-arabica ratio within a range, and that substitution is what eventually caps a spread move — but the swap is constrained by recipe, taste, caffeine, crema, and the machinery and brand a roaster has built around a given profile. Instant-coffee makers in particular are deeply committed to robusta's body and yield. So substitution is a real but *slow and bounded* release valve, not a frictionless arbitrage; it dampens extreme spreads over months, which is precisely why the 2024 inversion could happen and persist for a stretch before demand adjustment pulled things back.

## How it shows up in real markets: reading the robusta price

So you are a curious observer — maybe an investor, maybe just someone who wants to understand why their coffee got expensive. What actually moves the robusta price, and how do you read it? Here is the practitioner's checklist, in rough order of importance.

**1. Central Highlands weather, especially the dry season.** Because Vietnam is the swing producer of robusta and has one harvest a year, the rainfall during the flowering and bean-fill months (roughly the dry season before the October–January harvest) is the single biggest fundamental. A drought year — like 2023–24 — sets up a short crop that the market will pay up for. Watch El Niño/La Niña forecasts; El Niño tends to mean dry and hot for the Central Highlands.

**2. The pace of farmer selling and carry-over stocks.** Even with a given crop size, the *timing* of when farmers release beans moves the cash market and the basis. When prices are spiking, farmers (rationally) hold back, hoping for more — which tightens the spot market further and feeds the rally. When prices fall, they dump, which accelerates the decline. The behavioral feedback is real and it is why robusta rallies can be so sharp.

**3. The arabica–robusta spread.** When the spread narrows toward zero (or inverts, as in 2024), it signals robusta-specific tightness and creates substitution pressure: roasters start shifting blends, which eventually caps the robusta rally by destroying some demand. A persistently *wide* spread, by contrast, says robusta is the cheap workhorse doing its normal job.

**4. The durian/crop-switching trend and the age of the trees.** These are the slow structural variables. The more hectares that convert out of coffee, and the older the surviving trees get, the lower the *sustainable* level of Vietnamese robusta supply — which raises the floor under the price over the medium term. This is a multi-year story, not a trading signal, but it is why many analysts think robusta's long-run price has structurally re-rated higher.

**5. The exchange rate and freight.** Because the farmer is paid in VND and the world price is in USD, the **USD/VND rate** sits inside the basis: a weaker dong raises the VND value of a given dollar price (good for farmers), and shipping costs and Red Sea / Suez disruptions move freight, which is another basis component. These are second-order but they matter at the margin for who captures what.

**6. Inventory and certified stocks.** Robusta that has been graded and warehoused against the ICE London contract sits in **certified (exchange) stocks**, and the level of those stocks is a visible thermometer of physical tightness. When certified stocks drain toward multi-year lows — as they did through the 2023–24 squeeze — it tells the market there is little buffer between a short crop and an empty warehouse, which is precisely the condition for a violent rally and even a temporary **backwardation** (nearby prices above deferred ones, the curve shape that says "I want it now, not later"). Watching the trend in certified stocks alongside the harvest is how a physical trader confirms whether a price move is a real shortage or just noise.

### A short history: Vietnam has crashed this market before

It is worth remembering that the 2024 supply scare was the *up* version of a cycle whose *down* version Vietnam also caused. In the late 1990s and early 2000s, Vietnam's explosive new plantings flooded the world with robusta faster than demand grew, and the resulting glut sent world coffee prices — robusta especially — to catastrophic lows. The early-2000s coffee crisis devastated grower incomes across the producing world; that crash, too, had "made in the Central Highlands" stamped on it. The same concentration that amplifies a shortage into a price spike also amplifies a surplus into a price collapse, because Vietnam's planting and harvest decisions swing the global balance both ways.

That history carries a warning that balances the bullish 2024 narrative: the durian-driven acreage loss and aging trees raise the *floor* under robusta over the medium term, but a few good monsoon seasons, a wave of replanting with higher-yield clones, and the demand destruction from a sustained high price could just as easily swing the market back toward surplus and pull the price down hard. Concentration is symmetric. The reader who only remembers 2024 will be tempted to think robusta only goes up; the reader who also remembers the early 2000s knows the swing producer swings both ways.

![Vietnam grows about 40 percent of the world robusta output a share waffle diagram](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-robusta-coffee-the-worlds-number-two-exporter-5.png)

The waffle above is the whole risk picture in one frame: each square is 1% of world robusta output, and Vietnam's amber block — about **40%** — dominates it, with Brazil's *conilon*, Indonesia, India, and a long tail of smaller origins filling the rest. This is *why* the robusta price is a Vietnam-weather story first and everything else second. No other origin is large enough to be the swing producer. When you read about a coffee rally, your first question should always be: *what is happening in the Central Highlands?*

#### Worked example: sizing the world-price impact of a Vietnamese shortfall

Suppose the world robusta crop is about **5 million tonnes** and Vietnam is **40%** of it, so **2 million tonnes** from Vietnam. A drought cuts Vietnam's crop by **15%**, removing `0.15 × 2,000,000 = 300,000 tonnes` from the world supply — that is `300,000 / 5,000,000 = 6%` of global robusta, gone, with no other origin able to replace it quickly.

Now bring in **price-inelasticity**. Coffee demand barely responds to price in the short run — a reasonable price elasticity of demand for robusta is around **−0.2** (a 1% price rise cuts quantity demanded by only 0.2%). To ration a 6% supply loss through such inelastic demand, the price must rise by roughly `6% / 0.2 = 30%` *at least* — and in practice far more, because stockholders hoard and the move feeds on itself, which is how a 15% national crop loss turned into a near-**doubling** of the world price in 2024. **A small percentage loss in a dominant, inelastic-demand origin produces a large percentage move in the world price — the concentration of supply is a price-amplifier, and Vietnam is the amplifier for robusta.**

## What robusta does to the Central Highlands — and how to think about it

Step back from the screen and look at what this commodity *is* on the ground. For the Central Highlands, robusta coffee is not a line in a portfolio; it is the economic base of an entire region. Hundreds of thousands of households, several million people, depend on the crop directly, and a web of collectors, processors, transporters, fertilizer dealers, and exporters depend on it indirectly. When robusta doubled in 2024, Dak Lak felt a genuine boom — new motorbikes, paid-off debts, money flowing into Buon Ma Thuot — for the farmers who had a crop to sell. The region's fortunes are chained, just like the farmgate price, to a futures contract clearing on the other side of the world.

That chaining is the deep point of this whole series, and Vietnamese robusta is one of its cleanest illustrations. A coffee cherry is about as physical as a commodity gets — it grows on a tree, it rots if you do not dry it, it has to be picked by hand in a three-month window in a specific place. And yet its price is set in dollars per tonne in a London clearinghouse, by the collision of a real crop against real demand, expressed through a financial contract that a farmer in Dak Lak will never see but is nonetheless paid by. **The physical and the paper are the same price viewed from two ends of a chain — and the basis is the length of the chain.**

So how should a curious investor or observer actually *use* all this? A few durable takeaways:

- **When you read "coffee prices soared," ask which coffee.** Arabica (NY, cents/lb) and robusta (London, USD/tonne) are different markets with different drivers. A robusta-specific spike is usually a Vietnam-supply story; an arabica-specific one is usually a Brazil-weather story.
- **Watch the spread, not just the level.** The arabica–robusta spread narrowing or inverting is a louder signal than either price alone — it tells you robusta is the one that is tight, and it warns of roaster substitution that will eventually cap the move.
- **Respect the concentration.** Roughly 40% of world robusta from four provinces means the price is one drought away from a violent move. That is a risk for a roaster and an opportunity for anyone positioned for the supply story — but it cuts both ways, because a bumper crop and continued conversion-driven re-rating can swing it hard the other direction.
- **Remember the value-volume divergence.** For a concentrated, inelastic commodity, a producing country can ship *less* and earn *more*. Export volume and export value are different stories, and 2024 is the canonical case: ~1.35 million tonnes, ~\$5.5 billion, both records of a kind — one a low, one a high.

There is also a longer climate story sitting underneath all of this, and it deserves a sentence in any honest forward view. Robusta tolerates heat better than arabica, which is part of why a warming world has pushed some of the global coffee map toward robusta in the first place — but "more heat-tolerant" is not "heat-proof." The 2023–24 drought was a preview of the kind of stress a hotter, more variable climate can put on the Central Highlands, where most farms rely on groundwater and reservoir irrigation that a long dry season can exhaust. If extreme dry years become more frequent, the *volatility* of the Vietnamese robusta crop rises, and a more volatile dominant origin means a more volatile world price — more 2024-style spikes, and the defaults and substitution scrambles that come with them. At the same time, a new regulatory layer is arriving: the European Union's deforestation rules (EUDR) will require exporters to prove their coffee was not grown on recently deforested land, which adds traceability cost and pressure all the way down to the smallholder. Both forces — climate and regulation — point the same way: the cost and risk of producing a tonne of Vietnamese robusta are structurally rising, which is one more reason the bean that the market still calls "cheap" may not stay that way.

The market's name for what Vietnam owns is "robusta," and the word makes it sound like the rough understudy to arabica's lead. But 2024 showed the truth: the workhorse bean, grown on smallholdings in a single plateau in one country, is large enough and concentrated enough to set its own world price, to invert a relationship that had held for decades, and to turn a bad harvest into a national windfall. The next time the robusta price moves, you will know exactly where to look first — and you will know that the farmer in Dak Lak, the collector, the exporter, and the screen in London are all reading the same number from four different ends of the same chain.
