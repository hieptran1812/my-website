> **TL;DR** — Commodities are not an investment that compounds. They are a regime tool and a supply-shock hedge, and the roll is the whole game.
>
> - Own them for one honest reason: a small, regime-dependent **inflation and supply-shock hedge** that diversifies a stock-and-bond portfolio in the one scenario where both fall together. Not for yield, not for compounding — they have neither.

In March 2022, a curious thing happened in three different brokerage accounts on the same week. An investor who had bought a broad commodity index fund the previous summer was sitting on a gain of more than 40% — oil, wheat, nickel, and natural gas had all rocketed as Russia invaded Ukraine, and a diversified basket caught all of it. A second investor, who had been equally bullish on oil and expressed it through the most famous oil ETF in the world, had made far less than the headline crude move suggested, robbed quietly by the roll. And a third, who had reached for a leveraged play on the energy boom by buying shares in oil producers, had done beautifully on the way up — and would, a few months later, watch those same shares fall hard as recession fears hit, even though oil itself was still elevated. Three people, three correct macro calls, three completely different outcomes. The difference was not the view. It was everything that came after the view.

## Foundations: what a commodity allocation actually is

Before we build a playbook, we have to be honest about what we are building it for, because the word "allocation" smuggles in an assumption that does not survive contact with the asset class.

When you allocate to **stocks**, you are buying a claim on the future profits of real businesses. Those businesses retain earnings, reinvest them, grow, and pay you a slice — so a stock index compounds over time. A dollar in the S&P 500 in 1990 is worth many dollars today not because of speculation but because the underlying companies earned money and grew. Stocks are a *compounding* asset: time, on average, is your friend.

When you allocate to **bonds**, you are lending money in exchange for a coupon. The bond pays you interest along the way and returns your principal at maturity. There is a positive expected return baked into the contract — the yield. Bonds, too, pay you to wait.

A **commodity** is structurally different, and the difference is the foundation of everything else. A barrel of oil is not a business; it does not earn profits, retain earnings, or pay a dividend. A bushel of wheat does not accrue interest. A tonne of copper sitting in a warehouse does not grow into two tonnes. Commodities are **consumption and industrial goods** — things you burn, eat, smelt, or wear out. They have a price that goes up and down, but they have **no internal source of return**. There is no coupon, no dividend, no compounding engine inside the asset. We made this argument in depth across [what a commodity is](/blog/trading/commodities/what-is-a-commodity-the-physical-asset-that-trades-on-paper) and [spot vs futures](/blog/trading/commodities/spot-vs-futures-the-two-prices-of-the-same-barrel); here it becomes the load-bearing fact of the whole playbook.

So when we say "allocate to commodities," we cannot mean what we mean for stocks and bonds. We are not buying a stream of growing cash flows. We are buying **price exposure to a physical thing** — and we are doing it through a contract that has its own costs and its own behavior. The honest framing is this: a commodity allocation is a **bet on a regime**, paid for with a small slice of the portfolio, that earns its keep in the specific environments where it protects you and costs you a little in all the others. It is closer to **insurance** than to investment. Understanding that one reframing is most of the battle.

### The four moving parts of any commodity position

Every commodity position you can hold breaks into the same four pieces, and the entire series has been a tour of them:

- **Spot return** — the change in the physical price itself, the number on the news. This is the piece everyone watches and the piece that matters least to your realized return over time.
- **Roll yield** — the gain or loss from rolling expiring futures into the next contract. In [contango](/blog/trading/commodities/contango-vs-backwardation-what-the-shape-of-the-curve-means) it is negative (the bleed); in [backwardation](/blog/trading/commodities/backwardation-as-a-structural-return-source-the-carry-of-commodities) it is positive (the carry). This is the piece people have never heard of and the piece that does most of the damage or most of the good.
- **Collateral yield** — the interest earned on the cash a futures position does not tie up, parked in Treasury bills. This is a quiet tailwind that grows with interest rates.
- **The vehicle's own quirks** — fees, the basket's composition, whether it is an index, a single contract, an equity, or physical metal. The wrapper changes the payoff.

Hold those four in your head. The rest of this post is about deciding each one deliberately instead of by accident.

### Why a price that goes up is not a return that compounds

It is worth dwelling on the difference between a *price* and a *return*, because conflating them is the most expensive mistake in the asset class. A stock's total return is its price change *plus* the dividends it threw off along the way, and the underlying business reinvested its retained earnings, so the price itself is the score of a compounding machine. A commodity's price has no such machine behind it. When oil goes from \$50 to \$100, no economic value was *created* — a barrel of oil is still a barrel of oil, with the same energy content it always had. The price doubled because the balance of supply and demand shifted, and that same balance can shift it straight back to \$50, as it did between 2008 and 2009 and again into 2020. There is no floor of accumulated earnings under a commodity price the way there is under a stock index.

This is why a long-run chart of real (inflation-adjusted) commodity prices is roughly *flat to declining* over a century, punctuated by violent spikes, while a real chart of equities marches up and to the right. Commodities, adjusted for inflation, tend to fall over the very long run as technology makes extraction cheaper and substitutes emerge — the shale revolution and the energy transition are the latest chapters of that story, covered in [the shale revolution](/blog/trading/commodities/the-shale-revolution-how-the-us-became-the-swing-producer) and [the energy transition and green-metals demand shock](/blog/trading/commodities/the-energy-transition-and-the-green-metals-demand-shock). What this means for the allocator is stark: your entire expected return from a commodity sleeve has to come from the *roll* (when curves backwardate), the *collateral* (T-bill yield on the idle cash), and the *rebalancing* of a volatile, mean-reverting price — never from the spot price compounding, because it does not. Internalize that and the whole playbook follows: small size, regime timing, curve-awareness, insurance framing.

## Decision one: why own commodities at all

Start with the question most allocators skip, because it is the only one that can save you from owning the asset class for the wrong reason. **Why hold commodities in the first place?** There is a real case and an honest case against, and a thoughtful investor needs both before sizing a single dollar.

### The real case: a partial, regime-dependent hedge

The genuine argument for commodities rests on one structural fact: **commodities are the inputs whose rising prices ARE much of inflation.** When the consumer price index jumps because energy and food got expensive, the thing that got expensive is, definitionally, the commodity. So commodity spot prices have a real, positive **inflation beta** — they tend to rise precisely when inflation rises, and especially when inflation comes from the supply side (a war, an embargo, a drought). We built this carefully in [commodities as an inflation hedge](/blog/trading/commodities/commodities-as-an-inflation-hedge-and-when-they-are-not).

Why does that matter for a portfolio? Because of the one scenario that terrifies every stock-and-bond investor: **supply-shock inflation**, where stocks fall (higher costs, higher discount rates) and bonds fall (inflation erodes fixed coupons) *at the same time*. A traditional 60/40 portfolio, which relies on bonds rallying when stocks fall, has no defense in that regime — both legs drop together. This is exactly what happened in 2022, when a 60/40 had its worst year in decades. Commodities are one of the very few assets that *win* in that environment, because the shock that hurts stocks and bonds **is** the rising commodity price. That is the real diversification case, and it is not imaginary: in the stagflationary 1970s, commodities were among the only assets that protected purchasing power, as the cross-asset series documents in its [1970s stagflation case study](/blog/trading/cross-asset/case-study-1970s-stagflation-commodities-win).

So the real case is narrow but genuine: commodities are a **partial, regime-dependent hedge** against supply-shock inflation, and a diversifier in the one regime where the standard portfolio is naked. Not a return engine. A tail hedge.

### The honest case against

Now the other side, stated as plainly. Commodities have **no yield and no compounding** — we established that in the Foundations. They have **long, brutal real drawdowns**: a broad commodity index lagged inflation for most of the decade from 2011 to 2020, losing real value year after year while stocks tripled. They carry **enormous volatility** — 25-30% annualized swings that dwarf the inflation signal you are trying to capture. And they suffer the **roll drag** that we will make the centerpiece of decision two: a long-only holder in a contango market pays rent every month just to keep the position.

Put those together and you get the honest verdict, which the figure below makes precise. The spot price has a real inflation beta on the brochure. But three deductions — the roll cost, the volatility, and the multi-year drawdowns — shrink what an investor actually keeps, turning a strong spot hedge into a weak realized one. The asset class is a hedge you pay to carry, not a holding that pays you to wait.

![The roll is the whole game with spot flat while the long-only fund falls](/imgs/blogs/building-a-commodity-allocation-the-commodities-playbook-capstone-2.png)

That chart is the single most important picture in the series. The blue line — the spot price — ends roughly where it began over five years. The red line — what a long-only fund actually delivered, holding the same commodity through a contango curve — ends about 30% lower. Same commodity. Same period. The investor was *right about spot* and lost a third of their money to the roll. This is not a glitch; it is the asset class working as designed.

#### Worked example: sizing a commodity sleeve in a 60/40

Make the "why" concrete with money. Suppose you have a \$1,000,000 portfolio in a classic 60/40: \$600,000 in stocks, \$400,000 in bonds. You decide to carve out a **5% commodity sleeve**, funded by selling \$50,000 of each leg pro-rata, leaving roughly \$570,000 stocks, \$380,000 bonds, \$50,000 commodities.

Now run two scenarios over a year.

```
Scenario A - supply-shock inflation (the regime you bought it for):
   stocks       -18%  ->  570,000 x 0.82 = 467,400   (down 102,600)
   bonds        -12%  ->  380,000 x 0.88 = 334,400   (down  45,600)
   commodities  +35%  ->   50,000 x 1.35 =  67,500   (up    17,500)
   total                                  =  869,300  (-13.1%)
   versus a pure 60/40 in the same year:
   stocks 600k x 0.82 + bonds 400k x 0.88 = 844,000  (-15.6%)
   the sleeve cushioned the loss by about 2.5 percentage points
```

```
Scenario B - calm, disinflationary year (the regime that costs you):
   stocks       +12%  ->  570,000 x 1.12 = 638,400   (up   68,400)
   bonds        + 5%  ->  380,000 x 1.05 = 399,000   (up   19,000)
   commodities  - 8%  ->   50,000 x 0.92 =  46,000   (down  4,000)
   total                                  = 1,083,400 (+8.3%)
   versus a pure 60/40 in the same year:
   stocks 600k x 1.12 + bonds 400k x 1.05 = 1,092,000 (+9.2%)
   the sleeve cost about 0.9 percentage points of drag
```

The sleeve cost you 0.9 points in the calm year and saved you 2.5 points in the bad year. That asymmetry — a small, recurring premium paid in good times for a large payoff in the one bad regime — is **exactly the shape of an insurance policy**. The intuition: you do not own a commodity sleeve to make money on average; you own it so the one year that wrecks a 60/40 wrecks *you* a little less.

## Decision two: the roll is the master variable

If decision one is "should I be here at all," decision two is the one that separates people who understand commodities from people who got burned by them. **Spot is not what you earn.** What you earn is spot *plus the roll plus collateral yield*, and the roll term is the one that quietly governs your fate. This is the heart of the series — we built it from zero in [roll yield and why long-only ETFs bleed](/blog/trading/commodities/roll-yield-and-why-long-only-commodity-etfs-bleed) and turned it into a return source in [backwardation as a structural carry](/blog/trading/commodities/backwardation-as-a-structural-return-source-the-carry-of-commodities) — so here we make it the master decision of the playbook.

The mechanism, in one breath: you cannot store a barrel of oil in your brokerage account, so any fund or position holds **futures**, and futures expire, so the position must be **rolled** — sell the expiring contract, buy the next one — every month, forever. Whether that monthly swap makes or loses money depends entirely on the **shape of the forward curve**:

- In **contango** (later months dearer), you sell the cheap front month and buy the dearer next month — sell low, buy high, every roll. Negative carry. The bleed.
- In **backwardation** (front month dearest), you sell the dear front month and buy the cheaper next month — sell high, buy low, every roll. Positive carry. The pay.

The figure below puts numbers on it. Roll yield is not a footnote that shaves a few basis points; it spans a range that can **dominate** your return.

![Roll yield by curve shape from steep contango bleed to steep backwardation carry](/imgs/blogs/building-a-commodity-allocation-the-commodities-playbook-capstone-6.png)

Read the magnitudes. A steeply contangoed market — natural gas is the classic example — can cost a long-only holder on the order of **12% a year** in roll alone. A steeply backwardated market — tight oil in a supply crunch — can *pay* a similar amount. The flat case is a wash. This is why the same "bet on oil" can be a slow-motion disaster or a quiet money machine depending on nothing but the curve's slope. The price never has to move for the roll to determine your year.

The practical consequence is the central rule of the entire playbook: **read the curve before you read the brochure.** Before you put a dollar into any commodity vehicle, look at the shape of its forward curve. If it is in steep contango, a long-only holding is a melting ice cube; you want a curve-aware product, or you want to wait, or you want to express the view another way. If it is in backwardation, the structure is paying you to hold, and even a flat spot price earns a carry. We pulled this apart in [contango vs backwardation](/blog/trading/commodities/contango-vs-backwardation-what-the-shape-of-the-curve-means) and [convenience yield and the cost of carry](/blog/trading/commodities/convenience-yield-and-the-cost-of-carry-why-the-curve-has-a-shape); the takeaway for the allocator is that the curve is not background detail — it is the single most important input to whether you should hold the position at all.

#### Worked example: the roll decision, naive long-only vs backwardation-tilted

Two investors both want oil exposure for a year. The spot price of oil rises a healthy **10%** over the year. Both are "right." But they hold different structures.

**Investor 1 — naive long-only** holds a front-month fund through a curve that averages **mild contango**, costing roughly −6% in roll over the year. The collateral (T-bills) yields +5%.

```
Investor 1 total = spot + roll + collateral
                 = (+10%) + (-6%) + (+5%)
                 = +9%
```

**Investor 2 — backwardation-tilted** holds the same spot exposure but through a structure positioned in the backwardated part of the curve (or selecting commodities currently in backwardation), earning roughly +4% in roll. Same +5% collateral.

```
Investor 2 total = spot + roll + collateral
                 = (+10%) + (+4%) + (+5%)
                 = +19%
```

Identical view, identical spot move, identical collateral — and a **ten-percentage-point gap** in realized return, created entirely by which side of the roll they sat on. The intuition: in commodities, *how* you hold the view routinely matters more than *whether* the view was right, because the roll can add or subtract more than the spot move itself.

### Why some markets are structurally cursed and others structurally blessed

The roll is not random — it is set by the physics of storage, which we built up across [convenience yield and the cost of carry](/blog/trading/commodities/convenience-yield-and-the-cost-of-carry-why-the-curve-has-a-shape) and [cash-and-carry storage arbitrage](/blog/trading/commodities/cash-and-carry-and-storage-arbitrage-locking-in-the-curve). The harder and more expensive something is to store, the more its curve tends to sit in **contango**, because holders demand compensation for the cost of warehousing future supply. Natural gas is the extreme: you cannot store it without enormous pressurized or refrigerated facilities, and it has a violent seasonal demand pattern, so its curve is chronically and steeply contangoed — a long-only natural-gas fund is one of the most reliable wealth-destruction machines retail investors have ever found, bleeding well into the double digits per year even when gas prices are flat. Crude oil is milder but still usually contangoed in a well-supplied market. At the other end, a commodity in acute physical shortage — tight oil during a supply shock, a metal where inventories have drained — slips into **backwardation**, because the people who need the physical good *now* will pay a premium over future delivery (the convenience yield). That backwardation is the structural carry, and it is why [backwardation as a return source](/blog/trading/commodities/backwardation-as-a-structural-return-source-the-carry-of-commodities) is the one durable way commodities pay you to hold them.

For the allocator this hardens decision two into a screen: before you size any single-commodity position, look at where its curve sits. A persistently contangoed market (gas, often crude) demands a curve-aware structure or a tactical-only stance; a backwardated one is offering to pay you. The roll is not the same across the complex, and treating "commodities" as one homogeneous bleed-or-pay block is the error — each curve has its own physics, and the playbook means reading them one at a time.

### The collateral yield: the quiet third leg

There is one piece of the return that works *for* you and that almost no retail brochure mentions: the **collateral yield**. Because a futures position ties up only a small margin deposit — often under 10% of the contract's notional value — a commodity fund with, say, \$1 billion in assets and \$1 billion in oil exposure might have \$900 million sitting idle in cash. That cash is parked in U.S. Treasury bills, and the interest those bills pay is real return added on top of spot and roll. When T-bills yield near zero, as they did for most of the 2010s, this leg vanishes and a contangoed commodity fund is pure bleed. When they yield 5%, as in 2023-2024, the collateral leg can offset most of a mild contango drag all by itself. This is why the *same* commodity sleeve can feel completely different in a zero-rate world versus a 5%-rate world — and why the high-rate years of the early 1980s were, paradoxically, decent years for commodity index investors despite mediocre spot performance. The allocator's takeaway: in a high-rate regime the carry math is gentler and a broad sleeve is cheaper to hold; in a zero-rate regime the contango bleed bites in full and you lean harder on curve-awareness and regime timing to justify the position.

## Decision three: the vehicle is a choice with consequences

Once you know why you are here and that the roll governs your return, you have to pick a wrapper — and this is where most retail money goes to die, because the vehicles look interchangeable and are not. The same bullish view on a commodity pays out completely differently depending on the tool. The figure below is the vehicle ladder: what each one delivers, its characteristic trap, and the job it is actually good for.

![The vehicle ladder of futures index single ETF miners and physical](/imgs/blogs/building-a-commodity-allocation-the-commodities-playbook-capstone-3.png)

Walk the rungs.

**Futures and broad index funds** give you direct exposure plus the roll plus the T-bill collateral yield — the cleanest, most honest version of "owning commodities." A broad index spreads the bet across energy, metals, agriculture, and more, so no single contract dominates and the roll is diversified across many curves. The trap is the energy-heaviness of some indices and the contango bleed in long-only baskets, which we tackle head-on in decision four. For a rebalanced portfolio sleeve, this is usually the right rung.

**Single-commodity ETFs** — the famous oil fund USO and its kin — hold one commodity through front-month futures, rolled monthly. They are the most intuitive product and the most dangerous. Because they concentrate in one curve, they suffer the **worst roll drag** when that curve is in contango. USO investors in 2009 were right that oil would double and captured almost none of it; in April 2020, super-contango nearly destroyed the fund, as we recounted in [the negative-oil post](/blog/trading/commodities/april-2020-negative-wti-the-day-oil-traded-below-zero). These products are tactical instruments for short, sharp directional bets — never buy-and-hold vehicles.

**Commodity equities and miners** — oil producers, copper miners, agricultural processors — look like a clever way to get commodity exposure with a dividend attached. And they do offer **operating leverage**: a miner with fixed costs sees its profits swing far more than the metal price, so in a boom the equity outruns the commodity. But two traps lurk. First, miners are **equities** — they carry equity-market beta, management risk, balance-sheet risk, and country risk, so they fall in a general market crash even if the commodity holds up. Second, that equity correlation means they can fail you in the exact regime you bought them for: a recession that spikes oil on supply fears can still crush oil-producer *stocks* as the broad market sells off. Miners are a **growth tilt**, not a portfolio crash hedge. The Vietnam-market posts on the [steel sector](/blog/trading/commodities/steel-hpg-and-the-vietnam-construction-cycle) and the equity-flavored [oil and gas chain](/blog/trading/vietnam-stocks/oil-gas-sector-vietnam-the-pvn-chain) show this levered-equity behavior in the wild.

**Physical metal** — holding the actual bar — works for exactly one corner of the complex: **gold**, the monetary metal. Gold is cheap to store, does not spoil, and pays no one to hold the surplus, so a gold ETF can simply keep bars in a vault with no roll and no bleed. That is why gold behaves like a currency rather than a barrel and gets its own series; the framing that decides everything is in [is gold money, a commodity, or a currency](/blog/trading/gold/is-gold-money-a-commodity-or-a-currency-the-framing-that-decides-everything). For oil, gas, grains, or industrial metals, physical holding is absurd — you cannot store a year of oil demand in your garage, and the storage cost is exactly what creates the contango that bleeds the paper holder. Physical is gold's job, not oil's.

#### Worked example: the same bullish oil view through three vehicles

It is the start of a year. You are convinced oil is going up, and you are right: **spot oil rises 20%** over the next twelve months. Watch how three vehicles deliver that identical, correct view. The curve is in **mild-to-moderate contango** (roughly −10% roll for a front-month holder); T-bills yield +5%.

```
Vehicle 1 - single-commodity oil ETF (USO-style), front-month roll:
   spot +20%  +  roll -10%  +  collateral +5%   =  +15%
   you were right and kept three-quarters of the move

Vehicle 2 - curve-aware product (holds longer-dated / backwardated tenors):
   spot +20%  +  roll  -2%  +  collateral +5%   =  +23%
   smarter roll placement saved eight points of bleed

Vehicle 3 - an oil producer (E&P) stock, ~2x operating leverage to oil,
            but caught in a -8% general equity drawdown mid-year:
   commodity-driven gain ~+40% (2x the move)  minus  equity beta drag -8%
   net ~ +32% in a calm market...
   ...BUT in a year where the oil spike coincides with a recession scare,
   the same stock can finish DOWN even with oil up, as equity beta dominates.
```

Three correct views on oil; three very different realized returns — and the E&P case is the most dangerous precisely because it can *invert* in the wrong regime, turning a right call on oil into a loss. The intuition: in commodities the wrapper is not a formality, it is half the trade — choose the vehicle to match the job, never the headline.

## Decision four: what "owning the index" actually buys

If you choose the index rung — and for a long-term sleeve you usually should — you face one more decision that quietly determines what you own: **which** index. The two giants behave like different asset classes, and most investors never check. The figure below lays their sector weights side by side.

![GSCI energy-heavy versus BCOM diversified sector weights](/imgs/blogs/building-a-commodity-allocation-the-commodities-playbook-capstone-4.png)

The **S&P GSCI** is roughly **62% energy**. Buying it is not buying "commodities" — it is buying a leveraged bet on oil and gas with some metals and agriculture sprinkled on top. When energy rips, GSCI rips; when energy bleeds in contango, GSCI bleeds. The **Bloomberg BCOM** caps any single sector and spreads weight across energy (about 30%), agriculture, industrial metals, and a hefty precious-metals slug, so it behaves like a genuinely diversified basket with a much gentler roll profile (because it is not dominated by the contango-prone energy curves). We dug into this in [commodity index investing](/blog/trading/commodities/commodity-index-investing-gsci-bcom-and-owning-the-basket); for the allocator the lesson is blunt: **the ticker on the brochure hides a sector bet.** If you want an oil proxy, GSCI is fine and you should know that is what you bought. If you want a diversified inflation sleeve that will not live or die on the energy curve, BCOM-style breadth is the more honest choice.

This connects straight back to decision two. The energy curves — crude and especially natural gas — are the ones most prone to **steep contango**, because energy is the hardest and most expensive thing to store, as [natural gas, the most seasonal market](/blog/trading/commodities/natural-gas-henry-hub-ttf-jkm-and-the-most-seasonal-market) and the [LNG post](/blog/trading/commodities/lng-and-the-globalization-of-gas-shipping-the-uncontainable) explained. So an energy-heavy index is *also* a roll-heavy index. The two decisions are not separate: the more energy your basket holds, the more your fate rides on the energy curve's shape, and the more carefully you have to read decision two before you buy.

#### Worked example: GSCI vs BCOM in a metals-led, energy-soft year

Suppose it is a year where China stimulates, industrial metals and precious metals run hard (+25%), but oil is flat-to-soft (−5% spot) and its curve is in steep contango (−12% roll). You hold \$100,000 in a commodity sleeve. Compare the two indices, using their sector weights as rough exposure proxies.

```
GSCI (62% energy, 11% industrial metals, 5% precious, 15% ag, 7% livestock):
   energy:      62% x (-5% spot -12% roll) = 62% x -17% = -10.5%
   ind metals:  11% x +25%                  =             +2.8%
   precious:     5% x +25%                  =             +1.3%
   ag+livestock:22% x  0%   (assume flat)   =              0.0%
   index return  ~ -6.4%  ->  100,000 -> ~93,600

BCOM (30% energy, 16% ind metals, 19% precious, 24% ag, 6% softs, 5% livestock):
   energy:      30% x -17%                  =             -5.1%
   ind metals:  16% x +25%                  =             +4.0%
   precious:    19% x +25%                  =             +4.8%
   ag+softs+lvs:35% x  0%                    =              0.0%
   index return  ~ +3.7%  ->  100,000 -> ~103,700
```

Same year, same commodities, same \$100,000 — and a **ten-percentage-point swing** between the two indices, driven entirely by how much each one bet on the contango-bleeding energy curve versus the rallying metals. The intuition: "I own a commodity index" tells you almost nothing; *which* index encodes a sector bet and a roll profile that can flip your year from green to red.

### The hidden tax inside a long-only index: front-running the roll

There is a subtler cost buried in the index choice that compounds the contango problem. The big front-month index funds do not roll all at once on the last day — that would expose them to a single bad print — so they roll over a *published* window, selling a fixed fraction of the expiring contract each day across several days. That sounds prudent, and it is, but it has a perverse consequence: the roll schedule is **public**, and other traders know exactly when a giant fund must sell the expiring contract and buy the next. They position ahead of it, buying the next month before the fund does and selling it back a few cents dearer. That front-running is a small, recurring tax layered on top of the curve-shape cost — for the largest, most mechanical index funds it has been estimated to subtract a further fraction of a percent per roll, which annualizes into real money. This is why some "enhanced" or curve-aware index products deliberately roll on unpredictable schedules or hold deferred contracts: they are trying to dodge the predictable-elephant tax. For the allocator, it is one more argument that the *cheapest-looking* long-only index is not always the *cheapest to own* once the roll mechanics are priced in, and that the index decision deserves the same curve-awareness as decision two.

This is also where the [financialization of commodities](/blog/trading/commodities/the-financialization-of-commodities-when-wall-street-arrived) story comes home to roost. When index investing exploded in the 2000s, hundreds of billions of dollars flowed into the same handful of front-month contracts on the same predictable schedules, and that wall of mechanical, price-insensitive money arguably steepened the very contango it then paid to roll through. An individual allocator cannot fix that, but understanding it sharpens the vehicle choice: you are not the only one in the long-only index, and the crowd you are standing in has a known, exploitable footprint.

## Decision five: sizing it like insurance, not a core holding

Now the question of how much. Given everything above — no compounding, real drawdowns, the roll drag — the answer is firm and unglamorous: **a small sleeve, rebalanced.** The right mental model is not "what return will this add" but "how much insurance do I want, and what premium am I willing to pay for it."

A typical institutional answer is **0-10%** of the portfolio, often landing around 5%. Why so small? Because of the asymmetry from decision one. A sleeve large enough to matter in the good regime (supply-shock inflation) is, in every other year, a drag on a portfolio that compounds in stocks and bonds. The volatility is so high that even a 5% sleeve contributes meaningful risk; double it to 10% and you are letting a non-compounding, high-vol asset push around a portfolio whose long-run engine is elsewhere. The sleeve is there to be **switched on by the regime**, not to be a permanent core.

Two operational rules make the small sleeve work:

**Rebalance it.** Commodities are extraordinarily volatile, which means a fixed-weight sleeve naturally **sells high and buys low** if you rebalance to target. After a +40% commodity year, your 5% sleeve has grown to 7% of the portfolio; trimming it back to 5% banks the gain and funds cheaper stocks or bonds. After a −30% year, your sleeve has shrunk to 3.5%; topping it back up buys the asset cheap. This disciplined rebalancing harvests the volatility itself as a small return source — one of the few ways a non-compounding asset can contribute to long-run results. The [all-weather and risk-parity post](/blog/trading/cross-asset/all-weather-and-risk-parity-owning-every-regime) builds this idea out: a fixed slice of every regime, rebalanced, so each environment has something that works.

**Treat the carry as the cost of the insurance.** In contango years the sleeve bleeds a little through the roll. Do not panic and sell — that bleed *is the premium* on the inflation-tail policy. You are paying a small, recurring cost in calm regimes to own protection for the regime that wrecks a 60/40. Cancel the policy because it cost you something in a quiet year and you will be unhedged when the supply shock arrives.

#### Worked example: the rebalancing bonus on a volatile sleeve

Watch how rebalancing turns volatility into a small return on a sleeve whose price ends *exactly* where it started. You hold a 5% commodity sleeve in a \$1,000,000 portfolio — so \$50,000 — and you rebalance to the 5% target at the end of each year. Assume, to isolate the effect, that stocks-and-bonds are flat all three years, so the only moving part is the commodity sleeve, and the commodity round-trips: +40%, then −30%, then +19% (which compounds back to roughly the starting price).

```
Buy-and-hold (no rebalancing), 50,000 left alone:
   year 1:  50,000 x 1.40 = 70,000
   year 2:  70,000 x 0.70 = 49,000
   year 3:  49,000 x 1.19 = 58,310     ->  +8,310 vs start

Rebalanced to 5% (~50,000) each year-end, harvesting the swings:
   year 1:  50,000 x 1.40 = 70,000  -> trim 20,000 back into stocks/bonds
   year 2:  50,000 x 0.70 = 35,000  -> top up 15,000 from stocks/bonds (buy cheap)
   year 3:  50,000 x 1.19 = 59,500  -> trim 9,500 back
   banked along the way: +20,000 (yr1 trim) -15,000 (yr2 top-up) +9,500 (yr3 trim)
   net realized from the sleeve: about +14,500 on the round-trip
```

Same commodity, same start-and-end price — and the rebalanced version banked roughly \$14,500 versus \$8,310 for buy-and-hold, because trimming after the +40% and buying after the −30% mechanically sold high and bought low. The intuition: on a volatile, mean-reverting, non-compounding asset, the *discipline of rebalancing* is itself a return source — which is exactly why a small commodity sleeve earns more held in a rebalanced framework than held as a static lump you forget about.

There is one important interaction with **gold**. Gold deserves to be considered separately, not lumped into the commodity sleeve, because it is monetary rather than industrial — it has no roll problem, behaves like a currency, and protects against a different thing (monetary debasement and real-rate moves) than oil and copper do (supply shocks and the industrial cycle). Many thoughtful allocations hold a small gold position **alongside** a small commodity sleeve, treating them as two different insurances. The framing that decides which job each one does is, again, [is gold money, a commodity, or a currency](/blog/trading/gold/is-gold-money-a-commodity-or-a-currency-the-framing-that-decides-everything).

## Decision six: the regime that turns it on or off

The final piece ties the playbook to the calendar. Commodities are not a constant — their inflation hedge, their roll, and their return all depend on **which regime you are in**. The map below is the allocator's compass: two axes — growth direction and the dollar-and-inflation direction — carving out where commodities win and where they lose.

![The regime map of when commodities win and lose across growth and dollar](/imgs/blogs/building-a-commodity-allocation-the-commodities-playbook-capstone-5.png)

Read the quadrants.

**Commodities win** when growth and inflation are rising together with a weak dollar — the **late-cycle, supply-shock, China-up** regime. This is the top-left of the map and the best environment the asset class ever sees: demand is hot, supply is tight or shocked, the curves backwardate (so the roll pays), and a weak dollar lifts the price of globally-traded commodities (because they are priced in dollars, so a cheaper dollar means a higher dollar price for the same physical good). We traced this dollar mechanism in [the dollar and commodities](/blog/trading/commodities/the-dollar-and-commodities-why-a-strong-dollar-weighs-on-prices) and the demand side in [the supercycle](/blog/trading/commodities/the-commodity-supercycle-decades-long-booms-and-busts). The bottom-left — **stagflation**, falling growth but a supply shock keeping inflation hot — is rare, but it is the *unique* win: the one regime where stocks and bonds both fall and commodities are nearly the only thing standing, exactly the 1970s and partly 2022.

**Commodities lose** when growth is falling into recession with a strong dollar and cooling inflation — the bottom-right, the **worst regime**. Demand collapses, the curves slide into contango (the roll bleeds), and a strong, safe-haven dollar caps any price rally. A recession is the classic commodity killer; the [2008 spike-and-crash post](/blog/trading/commodities/the-2008-commodity-spike-and-crash-oil-at-147-then-32) is the textbook case — oil at \$147 in July, then \$32 by December as the financial crisis destroyed demand. The top-right — rising growth but a strong dollar and disinflation — is mixed: the rally is real but the strong dollar caps it, so you trim rather than cut.

The allocator's move is to let the sleeve **breathe** with the regime, within the small band from decision five. Late-cycle, supply-shock, weak-dollar signals argue for holding the sleeve at the top of its range (say 8-10%). Early-recession, strong-dollar, disinflation signals argue for cutting toward the floor (2-3%) — you keep a token position as cheap insurance against being wrong, but you do not fight a falling tide. The macro signals that tell you which regime you are in — the dollar, real rates, the shape of the curve itself — are the subject of the [macro-as-signals post](/blog/trading/macro-trading/commodities-as-macro-signals-oil-copper-gold) and [the energy-inflation linkage](/blog/trading/cross-asset/energy-oil-gas-the-inflation-engine).

#### Worked example: a regime-timed allocation through one cycle

Trace a \$1,000,000 portfolio with a flexible 2-10% commodity sleeve across four phases of a cycle, adjusting the sleeve to the regime while the rest stays in a 60/40.

```
Phase 1 - LATE CYCLE / supply shock building (weak dollar, curve backwardating):
   sleeve dialed UP to 10% = 100,000.  Regime is the BEST case.
   commodities +30%, roll +5% -> sleeve ~ +35% -> 135,000 (gain 35,000)
   trim back to target, banking the gain.

Phase 2 - PEAK / overheating (strong dollar starting, rally capped):
   sleeve trimmed to 6% = 60,000.  Mixed regime.
   commodities +5%, roll +2% -> sleeve +7% -> 64,200 (gain 4,200)

Phase 3 - RECESSION HITS (strong dollar, demand collapse, deep contango):
   sleeve cut to the 2% floor = 20,000 BEFORE the drop.  WORST regime.
   commodities -35%, roll -10% -> sleeve -45% -> 11,000 (loss 9,000)
   but only 2% was exposed, so the portfolio damage is tiny.

Phase 4 - RECOVERY (China restimulates, dollar softens):
   sleeve rebuilt to 8% = ~75,000 buying the asset CHEAP after the crash.
   commodities +25%, roll +6% -> sleeve +31% -> ~98,000 (gain ~23,000)
```

The same asset class was a 10% conviction holding in the regime that rewards it and a 2% token in the regime that punishes it — and the act of cutting to the floor *before* the recession meant the brutal −45% sleeve year barely scratched the portfolio. The intuition: you do not earn your commodity returns by holding a constant slice; you earn them by holding the *right size for the regime* and rebalancing the volatility, which turns a non-compounding asset into a useful, accountable tool.

## Common misconceptions

**"Commodities are a long-term buy-and-hold inflation hedge."** Partly true on spot, badly wrong on realized return. The [2011-2020 decade](/blog/trading/commodities/commodities-as-an-inflation-hedge-and-when-they-are-not) saw a broad commodity index *lose real value* even as inflation ran positive, because the roll bled and spot drifted. Commodities hedge **unexpected supply-side** inflation in bursts; they are a poor hedge against slow, demand-pull inflation and an actively *bad* holding in disinflation. The hedge is a regime tool, not a set-and-forget allocation. The honest takeaway: own them for the supply shock, not the decade.

**"My commodity ETF tracks the commodity."** It tracks the *futures*, net of the roll — which can diverge from spot by 10-15% a year. The famous case is USO rising a fraction of oil's 2009 doubling. Anyone surprised that "oil went up 20% but my oil ETF went down" has met the roll. Read the curve before the brochure; the ETF's price path is spot **minus the contango bleed plus the collateral yield**, never spot alone.

**"Miners are just a leveraged way to own the metal."** They are leveraged to the metal *and* to the stock market, management, costs, debt, and the country. In a market crash they fall with equities even when the commodity holds, and in a recession-driven oil spike the producer stock can finish down while oil is up. Miners are a growth tilt with operating leverage, not a clean commodity exposure and not a portfolio crash hedge.

**"GSCI and BCOM are basically the same — they're both 'commodities.'"** GSCI is ~62% energy; BCOM caps energy near 30% and adds a big precious-metals slug. In an energy-soft, metals-strong year they can diverge by ten points or more, as the worked example showed. The index choice is a sector bet and a roll-profile choice hiding behind a single label.

**"Gold and commodities are the same trade."** Gold is monetary — cheap to store, no roll, behaves like a currency, hedges debasement and real rates. Oil and copper are industrial — expensive to store, roll-cursed, hedge supply shocks and the cycle. They protect against different things and belong in **separate** sleeves. Lumping them together is how an investor ends up over-hedged against one risk and naked against the other.

**"More commodity exposure means more diversification."** Up to a point, then the opposite. Because the asset class is so volatile (25-30% annualized), a sleeve large enough to matter — say above 10-15% — starts to *dominate* the portfolio's risk rather than diversify it, even though it is a small share of the *capital*. A 15% sleeve at 30% volatility can contribute as much portfolio risk as the entire 60% equity allocation. So past a small band the marginal commodity dollar adds concentration, not protection. The diversification benefit is real but it saturates fast, which is the deepest reason the sizing answer is "small."

**"Commodities have a positive long-run expected return like stocks."** No. As the Foundations established, there is no compounding engine inside a commodity. The long-run real price is roughly flat-to-down. Whatever positive expected return a commodity sleeve has comes from the roll (in backwardated markets), the collateral yield, and the rebalancing bonus — never from the price grinding upward over decades the way an equity index does. An investor who expects "stock-like returns with commodity diversification" has misunderstood the asset at the root.

## How it shows up in real markets

**The 1970s — the case for, in its purest form.** Twin oil shocks (the [1973 embargo](/blog/trading/commodities/geopolitics-and-supply-shocks-when-the-physical-cannot-flow) and the 1979 Iranian revolution) drove a stagflation that crushed both stocks and bonds in real terms for a decade — and commodities were among the only assets that protected purchasing power. This is the bottom-left of the regime map made real, and the strongest historical evidence that a commodity sleeve earns its keep in exactly the regime a 60/40 cannot defend. The cross-asset series covers it in the [1970s stagflation study](/blog/trading/cross-asset/case-study-1970s-stagflation-commodities-win).

**The 2000s China supercycle — the demand-led boom.** From 2002 to 2008, Chinese industrialization drove a multi-year commodity bull, with copper — [Dr. Copper](/blog/trading/commodities/copper-doctor-copper-and-the-pulse-of-the-global-economy) — and oil leading. Curves backwardated, the roll *paid*, and a broad sleeve compounded handsomely for half a decade. This is the top-left "best regime" quadrant, and a reminder that when growth and a weak dollar align, commodities can run for years. The full arc is in the [supercycle post](/blog/trading/commodities/the-commodity-supercycle-decades-long-booms-and-busts) and the cross-asset [China case study](/blog/trading/cross-asset/case-study-2000s-china-commodity-supercycle).

**2008 — the recession killer.** Oil hit \$147 in July 2008 and crashed to \$32 by December as the financial crisis destroyed demand and the dollar bid as a safe haven. This is the bottom-right worst-regime quadrant in its rawest form: a demand collapse plus a strong dollar, turning a parabolic commodity into a falling knife in months. Any allocator who had not cut the sleeve going into the recession learned why the regime decision matters. The full story is in [oil at 147 then 32](/blog/trading/commodities/the-2008-commodity-spike-and-crash-oil-at-147-then-32).

**April 2020 — the roll, weaponized.** As the pandemic killed oil demand and Cushing storage filled, the front-month WTI contract settled at **−\$37.63** — physically negative, because holders of the expiring contract would have had to take delivery of oil they could not store. The super-contango that resulted (the next months trading far above the collapsed front) inflicted catastrophic roll losses on long-only funds; USO had to restructure to survive. This single episode is the most violent possible demonstration of decisions two and three together — the curve and the vehicle deciding everything while spot was a footnote. We told it in full in [negative WTI](/blog/trading/commodities/april-2020-negative-wti-the-day-oil-traded-below-zero).

**2021-2022 — the modern stagflation rhyme.** Post-pandemic demand, then the Russian invasion, drove a broad supply-shock inflation. A 60/40 had its worst year in decades as stocks and bonds fell together — and a diversified commodity sleeve had a banner year, with energy, grains, and metals all surging. This is the cleanest recent proof of the real case from decision one: in the regime that breaks the standard portfolio, the commodity sleeve was the one thing that paid. The macro backdrop is in [2021-2023 inflation and the fastest hiking cycle](/blog/trading/macro-trading/2021-2023-inflation-and-the-fastest-hiking-cycle).

**The 2011-2020 lost decade — the case against.** After the supercycle peaked, a broad commodity index spent most of a decade grinding lower in real terms — the shale revolution flooded oil, China's growth slowed, the dollar strengthened, and curves sat in contango so the roll bled year after year. An investor who had bought "commodities as a long-term inflation hedge" in 2011 lost real money for nine years. This is the honest counterweight to the 1970s: outside the supply-shock regime, the asset class is a drag, which is exactly why it is sized like insurance and timed to the regime rather than held as a constant core. The [shale revolution post](/blog/trading/commodities/the-shale-revolution-how-the-us-became-the-swing-producer) explains the supply side of that decade.

**The 2024 cocoa and coffee moonshots — single-name supply shocks.** Not every commodity move is macro. In 2024, West-African disease and weather sent cocoa from around \$3,000 to a peak near \$12,900 a tonne, while Brazilian drought and Vietnamese robusta scarcity roughly doubled coffee — moves that owed nothing to the dollar, recession, or the Fed and everything to the physical crop. We covered these in [softs](/blog/trading/commodities/softs-coffee-sugar-cocoa-and-cotton-the-tropical-markets) and the Vietnam-track [robusta post](/blog/trading/commodities/vietnam-robusta-coffee-the-worlds-number-two-exporter). For the allocator they are a reminder that a *diversified* sleeve catches idiosyncratic supply shocks a single-commodity bet would miss, and that the agriculture and softs weight in BCOM is doing real work the energy-heavy GSCI under-captures. Breadth is not just a risk control; it is exposure to the next surprise nobody is positioned for.

**The 2022 nickel squeeze — when the vehicle itself can break.** In March 2022 nickel spiked from around \$25,000 toward \$100,000 a tonne in two days on a short squeeze, and the London Metal Exchange took the extraordinary step of *cancelling trades* — meaning even traders who were right and had the gains saw them voided. We told this story in [the nickel squeeze](/blog/trading/commodities/aluminum-nickel-and-the-2022-nickel-squeeze-when-the-market-broke). The lesson for the playbook sits underneath all six decisions: the paper market is a human institution that can suspend, halt, or unwind in a true dislocation, which is one more reason a thoughtful allocation stays small, diversified, and never bets the portfolio on a single contract's behaving normally under stress.

## The takeaway: the playbook in one model

We can now restate the entire series as a single mental model, which the final figure draws. A commodity price is a **physical thing forced through a financial contract.** The cost of **storage** and the **convenience yield** of having the physical good now set the shape of the **forward curve** — and that curve, not the spot price, decides who profits from the roll. Storage cost pushes the curve into contango and the long-only roll bleeds; tightness pushes it into backwardation and the roll pays. And underneath it all sits the fact that organizes the whole asset class: commodities are **consumption and industrial assets, not monetary ones** — which is precisely why they do not compound, why gold (the monetary metal) gets its own treatment, and why a commodity allocation behaves like insurance rather than investment.

![The series spine of physical to paper with storage curve and consumption not monetary](/imgs/blogs/building-a-commodity-allocation-the-commodities-playbook-capstone-7.png)

So here is the playbook, assembled from the six decisions, the way a thoughtful investor should actually run it:

- **Why:** own commodities for one honest reason — a small, regime-dependent hedge against supply-shock inflation and a diversifier in the one regime that breaks a 60/40. Not for yield, not for compounding; they have neither.
- **Roll:** the master variable. Spot is not what you earn. Read the curve before the brochure: steep contango is a melting ice cube, backwardation is a paid carry, and the roll can add or subtract more than the spot move itself.
- **Vehicle:** match the tool to the job. Broad index for a rebalanced sleeve; single-commodity ETFs only for short tactical bets; miners as a levered, equity-correlated growth tilt that can fail in a crash; physical for gold alone.
- **Index:** if you buy the basket, know whether you bought an oil bet (GSCI, ~62% energy) or a diversified basket (BCOM). The ticker hides a sector and roll-profile choice.
- **Size:** small (0-10%, often ~5%), rebalanced to harvest the volatility, with the contango bleed understood as the premium on the insurance — never cancelled in a quiet year.
- **Regime:** let the sleeve breathe. Dial up in late-cycle, supply-shock, weak-dollar regimes; cut toward the floor in recession, strong-dollar, disinflation regimes; keep gold as a separate, monetary insurance alongside.

### What this looks like in practice

If you wanted to translate all of this into an actual allocation tomorrow, the concrete version is short. Decide first whether you even need the sleeve — a younger investor decades from spending, with no near-term liability that an inflation spike would wreck, can rationally hold *zero* commodities and accept the supply-shock risk, because their long horizon lets stocks compound through it. The sleeve earns its keep most for investors who are spending soon or who cannot stomach the one year a 60/40 falls 20% in real terms. If you decide you want it, set a small target — somewhere in the 3-7% band for most people — and choose a *diversified, curve-aware* vehicle rather than a single-commodity front-month ETF, so you are not betting the whole sleeve on one contango-prone curve. Rebalance on a fixed schedule (annually is plenty) to harvest the volatility and to force the sell-high-buy-low discipline. Hold any gold position *separately*, sized to its own monetary job. And let the band breathe with the regime only if you have an honest, repeatable read on the dollar, real rates, and the cycle — and if you do not, a static small sleeve, rebalanced, is a perfectly defensible default that still captures most of the supply-shock protection. The point is never to maximize the commodity return in isolation; it is to make the *whole portfolio* more robust across regimes for the smallest premium.

And the single honest takeaway, the one this whole series has been building toward: **commodities are not an investment that compounds. They are a regime tool and a supply-shock hedge, and the roll is the whole game.** Hold them small, hold them deliberately, read the curve, time the regime, and treat the bleed as the price of the protection. Do that, and a commodity sleeve becomes what it should be — not a get-rich engine, but a quiet, accountable hedge that earns its keep in the one year that matters most. Get any of the six decisions wrong, and you will join the long line of investors who were right about the price and lost money anyway. That is the series, and that is the playbook: a physical thing forced through a paper contract, a curve that decides who profits from the roll, and an asset you must respect for what it is — a consumption good and an insurance policy, never a store of value, because that job already belongs to gold.
