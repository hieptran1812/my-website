> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A commodity is a standardized, fungible physical good, but almost nobody trades the good itself; they trade a paper contract that promises the good, and the entire asset class lives in the gap between the two.
>
> - A commodity becomes *tradable* only when it is **standardized** (a defined grade and quantity), **fungible** (one unit is as good as any other), and **deliverable** (there is a real place and time it changes hands).
> - There are two prices for the same thing at all times: the **spot price** (the barrel for delivery now) and the **futures price** (the barrel for delivery later). The relationship between them — the forward curve — encodes the cost of storage and the value of having the good on hand.
> - Commodities are **consumption and industrial assets**: oil is burned, copper is built into wiring, wheat is eaten. That is the deep reason they behave nothing like gold, which is mostly held — and why gold gets its own series and we keep contrasting against it.
> - The one number to anchor on: on 20 April 2020, the front-month WTI crude oil contract settled at **−\$37.63 a barrel** — a *negative* price — because the paper claim came due and there was physically nowhere to put the oil. That single print is this entire series in miniature.

On 20 April 2020, something happened in the oil market that, by the textbook, was supposed to be impossible. The price of a barrel of West Texas Intermediate crude — the benchmark grade of American oil — fell below zero. Not to a few dollars. Below *zero*. The May futures contract settled the day at minus \$37.63 a barrel. For a few surreal hours, the market was saying that if you owned oil, you would have to pay someone almost forty dollars to take each barrel off your hands.

How can a real, useful, energy-dense physical substance — the stuff that moves ships and planes and makes plastic — be worth *less than nothing*? The answer is the thesis of this entire series, and it is hiding in one word in the previous paragraph: **contract**. The thing that went negative was not oil. It was a *futures contract* — a piece of paper (well, an electronic entry) promising delivery of 1,000 barrels of physical crude into a specific tank farm in Cushing, Oklahoma, in May. The contract was about to expire. Whoever held it when the music stopped would be legally obligated to take delivery of actual, sloshing oil. But the world was in the first weeks of the COVID lockdowns, demand had evaporated, and every storage tank, pipeline, and supertanker within reach was already full. There was physically nowhere to put the oil. So the holders of those paper claims did the only thing they could: they paid people to take the obligation away.

That is the whole game in a single day. A commodity is a physical thing, but it trades as a paper claim — and when the paper claim collides with the physical reality of storage and delivery, you discover that the two were never quite the same price. This series is about that gap. Everything in it — the forward curve, the cost of carry, who profits when contracts roll, why an airline hedges and a hedge fund speculates, why oil and copper and wheat each have their own personality — flows from the simple fact that a commodity lives two lives at once.

![A commodity exists as a physical barrel on one side and a standardized paper contract on the other](/imgs/blogs/what-is-a-commodity-the-physical-asset-that-trades-on-paper-1.png)

Let me promise you what you will be able to do by the end of this series. You will be able to look at any commodity — crude, natural gas, copper, gold, corn, coffee — and read three things off it almost instantly: whether the market is tight or glutted (the *shape* of the forward curve), who is being paid to do what (the four players), and whether a price move is a real supply-and-demand signal or a paper-flow artifact. You will understand why a long-only oil fund can lose money for years while the oil price goes nowhere, why a copper price tells you more about Chinese construction than any government statistic, and why "gold is a commodity" is true on the surface and deeply misleading underneath. This first post lays the foundation for all of it. Let's build it from zero.

## Foundations: What actually makes something a commodity

Start with the everyday meaning of the word and then sharpen it until it becomes the trading definition.

In ordinary speech, "commodity" means something basic, plentiful, and interchangeable — the opposite of special. We say a business has been "commoditized" when its product becomes a generic thing customers buy purely on price, like printer paper or USB cables. That instinct is exactly right, and it is the seed of the financial definition. A commodity is a good so standardized that *one unit is indistinguishable from another*, so the only thing left to compete on is price.

But not every basic good is a tradable commodity in the sense this series cares about. Sand is basic and plentiful, but you don't see a sand futures market on your screen. To become a *tradable* commodity — something that can have a single, public, continuously-quoted price that the whole world references — a good has to clear three specific bars. Memorize these three; they are the gate every commodity passes through, and they explain which goods get a market and which don't.

**1. Standardization.** There must be a precise, agreed-upon specification of *what* is being traded. Not "oil" but "West Texas Intermediate crude, with a sulfur content no greater than 0.42% and an API gravity between 37 and 42 degrees, delivered to Cushing, Oklahoma." Not "copper" but "Grade A copper cathode meeting LME specification, in lots of 25 tonnes." Not "wheat" but "No. 2 Soft Red Winter wheat, deliverable to approved Chicago elevators." This specification is written and enforced by an exchange. Without it, a price is meaningless, because "the price of oil" would depend on *which* oil, *where*, *how clean*.

**2. Fungibility.** Each unit of the standardized grade must be interchangeable with every other unit. Your barrel of WTI and my barrel of WTI are, by construction, the same barrel for trading purposes — we can swap them and nothing changes. (The word *fungible* comes from the Latin for "to perform" or "to substitute": a fungible good performs the same function no matter which specific unit you get.) This is why money is the ultimate fungible good — one ten-dollar bill is as good as any other — and why a painting is the opposite of fungible. Fungibility is what lets a market quote a *single* price instead of negotiating every lot individually.

**3. Deliverability.** There must be a real, physical way for the good to change hands at a defined place and time. This is the bar people forget, and it is the one that made oil go negative. A commodity contract isn't an abstraction floating in the cloud; it is anchored to physical settlement. Even though more than 95% of futures contracts are closed out before delivery (the holder sells the contract back rather than taking the barrels), the *threat* of delivery is what keeps the paper price tethered to the physical reality. The moment a contract approaches expiry, the paper and the physical have to converge — because the contract is, at bottom, a legally enforceable promise to hand over real stuff. Delivery is the rope that ties the balloon to the ground.

When a good clears all three bars, you can build a market around it. And here is the move that defines this asset class: once a good is standardized, fungible, and deliverable, you no longer need to touch it to trade it. You can trade a *promise* about it. A farmer in Iowa, a refiner in Texas, an airline in Singapore, and a hedge fund in London can all buy and sell the same standardized barrel of oil without anyone ever moving a drop. The physical good becomes a financial instrument. That is the leap.

#### Worked example: Why a diamond is not a commodity but copper is

Suppose you and I each own something valuable and want to know whether it can trade like a commodity. I own a 25-tonne lot of LME Grade A copper cathode; you own a flawless 2-carat diamond appraised at \$30,000.

Run the three tests. My copper is **standardized** — it meets a written LME spec down to the purity (99.9935% copper) and the physical form (cathode sheets). It is **fungible** — any other lot of Grade A cathode is an exact substitute; a smelter does not care which lot it gets. It is **deliverable** — there are LME-approved warehouses in Rotterdam, Singapore, and elsewhere where it can physically change hands. All three bars cleared. Result: there is a single global copper price, quoted continuously, that we can both reference; my 25 tonnes are worth (at, say, \$9,400/tonne) about **\$235,000** and we both know it to the dollar without negotiating.

Your diamond fails the very first test. Two 2-carat diamonds are *not* interchangeable — one might be flawless and colorless, another slightly included and faintly yellow, and the price difference between them can be 5x. There is no single grade; there are thousands of grade combinations (the "4 Cs": carat, cut, clarity, color). So there can be no single public diamond price, and there is no diamond futures contract you can pull up on a screen. Your diamond is worth "around \$30,000" only after an appraisal of *that specific stone*. The intuition: a thing can be precious without being a commodity — commodity-ness is about sameness, not value.

This is also why "commoditization" is a real business fear. When your product becomes standardized and interchangeable, you lose pricing power and the market sets your price for you. The same property that makes a market possible is the property that makes the seller a price-taker.

## The six sectors: a map of the whole asset class

"Commodity" is an umbrella over a surprisingly diverse set of goods, and the way the market carves them up is not arbitrary. The sectors are defined by *how the physical good is produced and stored*, because that physical reality is what drives each one's price behavior. There are six families. Learn them once and you have a mental filing cabinet for everything else.

![The commodity world splits into six sectors defined by how each good is produced](/imgs/blogs/what-is-a-commodity-the-physical-asset-that-trades-on-paper-3.png)

**Energy.** Crude oil, natural gas, refined products (gasoline, diesel, heating oil), coal. These are *pumped or extracted and then burned*. Energy is the largest commodity sector by trading volume and economic importance — it is the literal fuel of the economy — and it is where the physical-vs-paper drama is most intense, because energy is expensive and dangerous to store. Crude oil alone is the single most important commodity on Earth; its benchmarks (WTI in the US, Brent for the rest of the world) are the reference price for the global economy's energy bill.

**Industrial metals.** Copper, aluminum, nickel, zinc, lead, tin, plus the battery metals (lithium, cobalt). These are *mined, refined, and built into physical things* — wiring, cars, buildings, phones, batteries. Their demand is a direct read on industrial activity, especially construction and manufacturing. Copper is so reliable a barometer of the global economy that traders call it "Dr. Copper" — the metal with a PhD in economics. We'll come back to it.

**Precious metals.** Gold, silver, platinum, palladium. These are *mined* like industrial metals but carry a second life as a store of value. This sector is where our "consumption vs monetary" thesis bites hardest. Silver, platinum, and palladium are *partly industrial* — silver in solar panels and electronics, platinum and palladium in catalytic converters — so they straddle the line. Gold is *mostly monetary*: it is held, not consumed, and its price is driven by interest rates and fear rather than industrial demand. That difference is so fundamental that gold gets [its own dedicated series](/blog/trading/gold/is-gold-money-a-commodity-or-a-currency-the-framing-that-decides-everything), and throughout this one we will keep using gold as the contrast case that proves the rule.

**Grains.** Corn, wheat, soybeans, rice, oats. These are *grown*, on an annual cycle, and that single fact — one harvest a year in each hemisphere — shapes everything about them. Grain prices have a seasonal heartbeat (planting, weather, harvest) and are exquisitely sensitive to droughts, floods, and the size of the carryover stockpile from last year. A grain market is, at heart, a bet on the weather.

**Softs.** Coffee, sugar, cocoa, cotton, orange juice. These are *grown in tropical or specialized regions*, often concentrated in a handful of countries — coffee in Brazil and Vietnam, cocoa in West Africa. That geographic concentration makes them volatile: a frost in Brazil or a disease in Ivory Coast can move the global price 50% because there is no easy substitute supply. (Vietnam is a giant here — the world's largest robusta-coffee exporter — which is why this series carries a dedicated Vietnam track.)

**Livestock.** Live cattle, feeder cattle, lean hogs. These are *raised*, and they are the one sector that breaks the storage rules: you cannot easily store a live animal the way you store oil in a tank, and the animal keeps eating and aging. This gives livestock a unique price structure where the forward curve reflects the biology of growing an animal to slaughter weight, not the cost of warehousing.

Notice the through-line: the sector tells you the *physics*, and the physics tells you the *price behavior*. Energy is hard to store, so its forward curve swings violently between glut and scarcity. Grains have one harvest a year, so they trade the weather and the stockpile. Softs are geographically concentrated, so they trade single-country shocks. Livestock can't be stored at all, so its curve tracks biology. When you meet a new commodity, your first question is always: *how is this thing produced and stored?* The answer predicts how it will trade.

## The physical and the paper: the central idea of this series

Now we arrive at the heart of it. A commodity is a physical good, but the market that prices it trades almost entirely in *paper claims* on that good. There are, at every moment, two distinct prices for the same standardized barrel, and the relationship between them is the source of nearly everything else.

The **spot price** is the price for the physical good *right now* — pay cash today, take delivery this week. It reflects the supply and demand for the actual stuff at this moment: how much oil is coming out of the ground versus how much refineries want to run today.

The **futures price** is the price, agreed *today*, for delivery of the same good at a *specified date in the future* — say, three months out. You don't pay the full price now; you post a small good-faith deposit called *margin* (typically 5–15% of the contract's value) and agree to settle later. Critically, you can close the position by selling the contract back before delivery ever happens, which is what the overwhelming majority of traders do. The futures market is where price discovery actually occurs, because it is deep, liquid, and electronic — far more so than the clunky physical spot market where barrels and bushels actually move.

![Spot and futures are two prices for the same good, one for delivery now and one for later](/imgs/blogs/what-is-a-commodity-the-physical-asset-that-trades-on-paper-5.png)

Why does the futures price differ from the spot price at all? Because owning the physical good for three months *costs money*, and the futures price has to account for that. This is the **cost of carry**, and it has three pieces:

- **Storage.** A tank, a warehouse, a silo — physical space costs rent. Oil storage, grain elevators, and metal warehouses all charge by the day.
- **Insurance and spoilage.** Stuff can leak, rot, or be stolen. Grain degrades; oil tanks can fail.
- **Financing.** If you buy a barrel today and hold it three months, you've tied up cash that could have earned interest. That foregone interest is a real cost.

Against those costs sits one benefit of holding the physical good rather than a paper claim: the **convenience yield**. This is the value of having the actual stuff *on hand* — the ability to keep a refinery running, fulfill a contract, or profit from a sudden shortage — that a paper claim cannot give you. When the market is tight and everyone is scrambling for physical supply, the convenience yield is high; when there's a glut and nobody needs the stuff urgently, it's near zero.

Put those together and you get the master relationship of this entire asset class:

> **Futures price ≈ Spot price + Storage + Financing − Convenience yield**

When storage and financing dominate (a glutted, oversupplied market), futures trade *above* spot, and prices rise as you go further out the curve. The market is in **contango**. When the convenience yield dominates (a tight, scarce market where everyone wants the physical good now), futures trade *below* spot, and prices *fall* as you go further out. The market is in **backwardation**. The shape of that curve — contango or backwardation — is, as we'll argue in [the forward-curve post](/blog/trading/commodities/the-forward-curve-the-most-important-chart-in-commodities), the single most important chart in commodities, because it tells you instantly whether the physical market is loose or tight and who is getting paid.

#### Worked example: Pricing a futures contract from carry

Let's price a three-month oil futures contract from scratch, using the carry relationship, so the formula stops being abstract.

Suppose physical WTI crude trades at a spot price of **\$72.00** a barrel. You're a trader deciding whether to buy a barrel now and store it, or buy a three-month futures contract. Here are the carry costs over three months:

- **Storage:** commercial tank storage runs about \$0.40 per barrel per month → \$1.20 over three months.
- **Financing:** short-term interest is 5% per year. Tying up \$72 for a quarter costs \$72 × 5% × (3/12) = **\$0.90**.
- **Convenience yield:** the market is well-supplied, so the value of holding physical is low — call it \$0.30 over the quarter.

Plug into the relationship: Futures ≈ \$72.00 + \$1.20 + \$0.90 − \$0.30 = **\$73.80**.

So the fair three-month futures price is about **\$73.80**, a touch above spot. The market is in mild contango — the curve slopes up — because storage and financing slightly outweigh the (low) convenience yield. Now flip the scenario: imagine a supply scare where refiners desperately need crude *now* and the convenience yield jumps to \$5.00. The futures price becomes \$72.00 + \$1.20 + \$0.90 − \$5.00 = **\$69.10**, *below* spot. The curve has flipped into backwardation. The intuition: the forward curve is not a forecast of where prices are *going* — it is a thermometer reading the temperature of the physical market *right now*, with a high convenience yield (scarcity) pulling the far end of the curve down below spot.

This is why I keep saying the gap between physical and paper is where everything happens. The gap *is* the cost of carry minus the convenience yield, and reading it is reading the market's pulse.

## How a physical good became a financial asset class

It helps to know where futures markets came from, because the history explains why the physical-vs-paper split exists at all — it was *engineered* to solve a real problem, not invented by speculators to gamble.

Go back to the American Midwest in the mid-1800s. Chicago sat at the meeting point of the great grain-growing prairies and the rail and water routes east. Every autumn, farmers harvested and hauled their grain to the city all at once, and every autumn the same disaster repeated: a flood of grain hit the market simultaneously, prices crashed, and grain that couldn't be sold or stored rotted in the streets and was dumped in Lake Michigan. Then, by spring, with the glut consumed, prices spiked and millers couldn't afford flour. The physical reality — grain arrives in a lump once a year but is eaten all year round — was generating ruinous price swings that hurt farmers and millers alike.

The solution, formalized when the Chicago Board of Trade introduced standardized futures contracts in 1865, was to let a farmer and a miller agree on a price *before the harvest*. The farmer could lock in a sale price in spring and plant with confidence; the miller could lock in a purchase price and budget for the year. To make those agreements tradable, the exchange had to do exactly the three things our Foundations section described: define a standard grade (No. 2 yellow corn, say), make each contract fungible (every September corn contract identical), and guarantee deliverability (into approved Chicago elevators). Out of that necessity, the entire machinery of this asset class was born. Futures were not a financial toy bolted onto the grain trade; they were the grain trade's *survival mechanism*. Every commodity market that followed — oil, metals, coffee, livestock — copied the template.

For its first century the commodity market stayed close to its roots: dominated by the physical players, the hedgers, with speculators as a relatively small (if essential) counterparty. Then, in the 2000s, something changed that you need to understand because it's still shaping prices today. Investors discovered commodities as an *asset class* — something to allocate to in a portfolio, alongside stocks and bonds, for diversification and inflation protection. Wall Street built commodity *index funds* that bought a basket of futures and rolled them forward forever, and pension funds poured tens of billions of dollars into them. This is called the **financialization** of commodities, and it permanently changed the market: suddenly a huge, price-insensitive pool of money was holding long futures positions not because it needed oil or copper, but because a portfolio model said "hold 5% commodities."

Two big consequences flow from financialization, and both matter for how you read prices today. First, the *index weights* started to matter, because index money flows where the index tells it to. The two dominant benchmarks weight the sectors very differently: the S&P GSCI is roughly **62% energy** — it is essentially a bet on oil — while the Bloomberg Commodity Index (BCOM) caps any one sector and spreads across energy, agriculture, metals, and precious metals much more evenly (energy around 30%). A pension fund tracking the GSCI is far more exposed to an oil move than one tracking BCOM, even though both are "commodity allocations." Second, financialization made the *roll* a market-moving force in its own right. When billions of index dollars all roll their front-month contracts to the next month on the same few days (the "Goldman roll"), that mechanical selling-and-buying can push the curve around regardless of physical fundamentals — a pure paper-flow effect with no barrel behind it.

#### Worked example: Why the index you pick changes your oil exposure

Suppose you allocate \$100,000 to "commodities" and you have to choose a benchmark. With the GSCI (about 62% energy), roughly **\$62,000** of your money is effectively riding on energy prices — call it \$50,000 on crude oil and refined products alone. With the BCOM (about 30% energy), only about **\$30,000** sits in energy, with the rest spread across agriculture (~24%), industrial metals (~16%), and precious metals (~19%).

Now run a 20% oil rally through both. The GSCI sleeve's energy exposure (\$62,000) gains roughly 20% × \$62,000 = **\$12,400** from energy alone. The BCOM sleeve's energy (\$30,000) gains about 20% × \$30,000 = **\$6,000**, but its larger metals and ags weights might be flat or down, muting the total. Same headline ("I own commodities"), wildly different result. The intuition: "commodities" is not one thing — the *weights* decide what you actually own, and an uninformed index choice is a giant unhedged bet on oil hiding inside a portfolio that thinks it's diversified. This is the kind of nuance the [cross-asset series on the energy complex](/blog/trading/cross-asset/energy-oil-gas-the-inflation-engine) develops for allocators.

The history and the financialization both reinforce the series' spine. The physical-vs-paper split was built deliberately to let real businesses transfer real risk; the paper layer then grew so large and so financial that it took on a life of its own, with index flows and the roll moving prices in ways disconnected from any tank or silo. Reading commodities well means holding both truths at once: the physical reality is the anchor, but the paper flows are the weather, and on any given day the weather can dominate.

## Why oil goes from \$147 to negative: the physical asset's wild personality

Stocks and bonds are, at bottom, claims on a stream of future cash flows, and they move within a certain civility. Commodities are claims on a *physical thing that must be produced, transported, stored, and consumed in the real world* — and the real world is messy, lumpy, and prone to violent imbalances. That is why commodity prices have a personality you rarely see in financial assets: long sleepy ranges punctuated by terrifying spikes and crashes.

Oil is the poster child. Look at how the price of WTI crude has behaved over a single generation.

![WTI crude oil annual average price from 2000 to 2025 with major boom and bust years marked](/imgs/blogs/what-is-a-commodity-the-physical-asset-that-trades-on-paper-2.png)

Read that chart as a story about *physical* supply and demand colliding, because that's what every wiggle is. The long climb through the 2000s was the China supercycle — a billion people industrializing at once, pulling oil demand up faster than supply could follow, until WTI touched roughly \$147 a barrel intraday in July 2008. Then the global financial crisis hit, demand cratered as the world economy seized up, and the price collapsed to the low \$30s within months — a more than 70% crash driven purely by demand destruction. The 2010s recovery was undone by a *supply* shock from the opposite direction: the American shale revolution flooded the market with new crude, and the price crashed again in 2014–2016. Then 2020's COVID demand collapse produced the negative print we opened with. Then Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine spiked it back toward \$100 on supply fear.

Every one of those moves was the physical world — a war, a recession, a new drilling technology, a pandemic — slamming into a market that *cannot store its way out of an imbalance fast enough*. You can't just build an oil tank overnight when supply outruns demand, and you can't conjure a new oil field overnight when it doesn't. The lag between physical supply and physical demand is what makes the price swing. This is the deep reason commodities are *consumption and industrial assets*, not monetary ones: their price is governed by the physics of making and using stuff, not by interest rates and faith.

#### Worked example: The leverage that turns a 10% move into a 100% gain (or wipeout)

Commodity futures are traded on *margin*, and margin is leverage, and leverage is why these markets make and destroy fortunes fast. Let's make it concrete.

You think oil is too cheap at \$72 and want exposure to 1,000 barrels (one standard WTI contract). The full notional value is 1,000 × \$72 = **\$72,000** worth of oil. But to open the futures position, the exchange doesn't ask for \$72,000 — it asks for an *initial margin* deposit of, say, \$7,200 (10% of notional). You control \$72,000 of oil exposure with \$7,200 of cash. That's **10-to-1 leverage**.

Now oil rises 10%, to \$79.20. Your gain is 1,000 × \$7.20 = **\$7,200**. On your \$7,200 of posted margin, that is a **100% return** — a 10% move in the underlying became a doubling of your money. Wonderful. But the leverage cuts exactly as hard the other way: if oil instead *falls* 10% to \$64.80, you lose \$7,200 — your entire margin. You'd get a *margin call* (a demand to post more cash) well before that, and if you didn't meet it, your broker would liquidate the position. The intuition: futures let a small amount of cash command a large amount of physical exposure, which is why hedgers can protect huge inventories cheaply and why speculators can be wiped out by a move that, in the physical world, was almost a non-event. Leverage is the amplifier that makes the physical-paper gap dangerous.

This leverage, plus the violent physical swings, is why commodities are simultaneously one of the most useful and one of the most punishing asset classes. They are useful because the leverage lets a real-world business hedge its exposure for pennies on the dollar; punishing because the same leverage, in speculative hands, turns a normal price move into ruin.

## Dr. Copper: a commodity as an economic instrument

Here is one of the most beautiful ideas in the whole asset class, and it shows why a commodity is more than a thing to bet on — it can be an *instrument for reading the world*. Copper is used in almost everything that gets *built*: electrical wiring, plumbing, motors, transformers, electronics, and now, enormously, electric vehicles and the power grids that charge them. Because copper demand is so broad and so tied to construction and manufacturing, the copper price moves *with the global industrial cycle*. When the world is building, copper demand rises and the price climbs; when the world slows, copper sags. It is so reliable that traders nicknamed it "Dr. Copper" — the only metal with a PhD in economics.

![LME copper annual average price from 2000 to 2025 tracking the global industrial cycle](/imgs/blogs/what-is-a-commodity-the-physical-asset-that-trades-on-paper-4.png)

That chart is a chart of the world economy wearing a copper costume. The surge from under \$2,000 a tonne in 2000 to nearly \$9,000 by 2011 *is* the China supercycle — the same force that drove oil, now visible in metal. The crash in 2009 is the financial crisis. The 2024 record high near \$11,000 intraday is the market pricing in the electrification of everything: every EV needs roughly four times the copper of a gasoline car, and every solar farm and grid upgrade is copper-hungry. When you see copper breaking out, you are watching the market vote on whether the world is about to build more or build less. As [the macro series puts it](/blog/trading/macro-trading/commodities-as-macro-signals-oil-copper-gold), oil, copper, and gold together form a three-instrument dashboard for the global economy: oil for energy demand, copper for industrial growth, and gold for fear and real interest rates.

And this is exactly where the consumption-vs-monetary contrast becomes sharp. Copper's price is *consumption-driven*: it rises when more copper is being *used up* in physical construction. Gold's price is *not* — most gold ever mined still exists, sitting in vaults and jewelry, and very little is "consumed." Gold rises when real interest rates fall and people want a store of value, as [the gold series explains in detail](/blog/trading/gold/real-interest-rates-the-master-variable-behind-the-gold-price). Two metals, both mined, both shiny — but one is an industrial commodity reading the world's construction activity, and the other is a monetary asset reading the world's fear. Confusing the two is the single most common mistake beginners make in this asset class. Keep them in separate boxes.

#### Worked example: Reading the economy off the copper-gold ratio

Because copper reads growth and gold reads fear, the *ratio* between them is a clean signal of risk appetite — and you can compute it from the data in this series.

Take two snapshots. In 2011, near the peak of the China boom, copper averaged about \$8,828 a tonne and gold was around \$1,570 an ounce. Convert copper to a per-ounce-comparable basis isn't necessary; the simplest version traders use is just copper price ÷ gold price in consistent units, or even copper-tonne ÷ gold-ounce as a relative index. Let's use a clean relative move instead. In 2020, during the COVID fear shock, copper averaged about \$6,181 a tonne while gold *surged* toward record highs as real rates went deeply negative. So copper *fell* relative to gold — the ratio dropped. By 2024, copper had climbed back to roughly \$9,150 a tonne on the electrification boom while gold also rose, but the copper rebound signaled returning industrial confidence.

The read: when the copper-to-gold ratio is *rising*, the market is voting for growth over fear — risk-on, good for stocks, often rising bond yields. When it's *falling*, fear is winning — risk-off, flight to safety. In the 2020 COVID crash, the ratio collapsed (gold up, copper down): a textbook fear signal. The intuition: you don't need an economist's forecast when two commodities are already taking the vote in dollars and cents — one priced by what's being built, the other by what's being feared.

## The four players: who is on the other side?

A price is a handshake between a buyer and a seller, so to understand commodity prices you have to understand *who* is doing the buying and selling and *why*. There are four broad roles, and every print in every commodity market is some combination of them meeting. We'll devote [a full post to the four players](/blog/trading/commodities/the-four-players-producers-consumers-hedgers-and-speculators), but you need the map now.

![The four players in a commodity market are producers, consumers, hedgers, and speculators](/imgs/blogs/what-is-a-commodity-the-physical-asset-that-trades-on-paper-7.png)

**Producers** make the physical good — the oil company, the farm, the copper mine. Their problem is that they will have output to sell *in the future*, and they don't know what the price will be then. A wheat farmer who plants in spring won't harvest until fall, and a price crash in between could bankrupt her. So producers *sell futures* — they lock in a price today for output they'll deliver later. This is **hedging**: trading away price uncertainty for certainty.

**Consumers** need the physical good as an input — the airline buying jet fuel, the bakery buying wheat, the smelter buying copper ore. Their problem is the mirror image: they'll need to *buy* the good in the future and a price spike could wreck their costs. So consumers *buy futures* — they lock in their input cost today. Also hedging, in the opposite direction.

Producers and consumers are the **hedgers**: real-world businesses using the paper market to transfer the price risk of their physical exposure. They are the reason futures markets exist at all. The futures market was *invented* so that a farmer and a miller could agree on a price before the harvest and both sleep at night.

But notice a problem: the hedgers' needs don't perfectly cancel. At any moment there might be more producers wanting to sell forward than consumers wanting to buy forward, or vice versa. Someone has to take the other side of the leftover risk. That someone is the **speculator** — the fund, the trading firm, the individual trader — who has no use for the physical good and is purely trying to profit from price moves. Speculators are not villains; they are the market's shock absorbers. By taking the other side of hedgers' trades, they provide *liquidity* and they *absorb risk* the hedgers want to shed. In a market in backwardation, speculators who go long are effectively being *paid* (via the upward-drifting price as contracts roll) to carry the risk that producers wanted to offload — a risk premium for a service. Rounding out the cast are the **arbitrageurs and market makers**, who keep the spot price, the futures price, and the cost of storage all consistent with each other; if the gap between them drifts away from the true cost of carry, arbitrageurs pounce and snap it back.

#### Worked example: A farmer and a baker meet a speculator

Let's watch the four players interact in one clean trade so the abstractions become people.

A wheat farmer expects to harvest 5,000 bushels in September. Wheat futures for September delivery trade at **\$6.00** a bushel today. She's terrified the price will fall by harvest, so she *sells* one futures contract (5,000 bushels) at \$6.00, locking in **\$30,000** of revenue regardless of where the price ends up. She has hedged.

Across the country, a baker knows he'll need 5,000 bushels of wheat in September to make flour. He's terrified the price will *rise*, so he wants to *buy* September wheat at \$6.00 to lock his cost. If he steps in, he takes the other side of the farmer's trade directly and both are hedged — perfect. But suppose this week no baker is buying; the hedging demand is one-sided. Enter the **speculator**, a fund that has studied the weather and thinks wheat is heading higher. The fund *buys* the farmer's contract at \$6.00. Now: if September wheat falls to \$5.00, the farmer is thrilled — she locked in \$6.00 and avoided a \$5,000 loss; the speculator eats that \$5,000 loss. If wheat rises to \$7.00, the speculator gains \$5,000 and the farmer "lost" the upside (but she was never trying to bet on price — she was protecting her business).

The intuition: the farmer paid for *certainty* by giving up the upside, and the speculator was paid for *taking on uncertainty* — when the hedgers don't net out, the speculator is the counterparty who makes the farmer's insurance possible. Every commodity price is this negotiation, scaled to millions of contracts.

## The scale of it: a commodity market is a real-world flow

It's easy to treat a price chart as an abstraction, so anchor yourself in the sheer physical scale these markets coordinate. Take crude oil. The world produces and consumes well over 100 million barrels *every single day*. That oil comes from a concentrated set of giant producers, and knowing who they are tells you who has the power to move the price by opening or closing a tap.

![Top crude oil producers in 2024 measured in millions of barrels per day](/imgs/blogs/what-is-a-commodity-the-physical-asset-that-trades-on-paper-6.png)

Three countries — the United States (about 13.2 million barrels a day after the shale revolution), Russia (about 10.8), and Saudi Arabia (about 9.6) — dominate. Together they pump roughly a third of the world's crude. That concentration is why a single decision by Saudi Arabia (and the OPEC+ cartel it leads) to cut production a few million barrels a day can swing the global price, and why a war involving Russia jolts the entire market. The physical supply is lumpy and political, and the paper price reacts instantly to the *expectation* of physical changes. This is also where commodities meet geopolitics and macro policy in a way no other asset class does — the same forces covered in [the macro series on the dollar system](/blog/trading/macro-trading/dollar-system-why-usd-rules-markets-dxy) and the [event-trading work on geopolitical shocks](/blog/trading/event-trading/geopolitics-elections-and-unscheduled-shocks).

Now compare that physical flow to the paper market on top of it. The *physical* oil market moves about 100 million barrels a day. The *paper* market — the futures and options traded on those barrels — turns over many multiples of that every day, because the same barrel of paper oil can be bought and sold dozens of times before it's ever delivered (if it's delivered at all). The paper market is far bigger than the physical one. That's not a flaw; it's the feature. The deep, liquid paper market is what lets a price form continuously and lets everyone — from a Saudi oil ministry to a retail trader — express a view. But it also means the paper price can, in the short run, be pushed around by financial flows that have nothing to do with whether the physical world is short or long a barrel. Separating the two — physical signal from paper noise — is a skill this series will build relentlessly.

## Common misconceptions

A few beliefs about commodities are so common, and so wrong, that clearing them up now will save you a lot of confusion later. Each comes with a number.

**Misconception 1: "If I think oil is going up, I just buy an oil fund and hold it."** This is the most expensive mistake in the asset class, and it's a direct consequence of the physical-vs-paper gap. A long-only commodity fund doesn't own oil in a tank; it owns *futures contracts* that must be "rolled" — sold before expiry and replaced with later-dated ones — over and over. In a contango market (futures above spot), you're perpetually selling low (the cheap expiring contract) and buying high (the more expensive next one). That bleed is called negative *roll yield*, and it can be brutal: an illustrative long-only energy index can lose 30% over five years *while the spot price goes nowhere*, purely from the roll. The number to remember: in steep contango (as in natural gas), the roll can cost on the order of **−12% a year**. The fund tracks the spot price terribly. We'll dissect this in the spot-vs-futures and forward-curve posts; for now, internalize that *holding a commodity via paper is not the same as owning the physical thing*, and the difference compounds against you.

**Misconception 2: "Gold is a commodity, so it trades like oil."** No. Gold is mined, yes, and it has a futures market, yes — but its price is driven by *monetary* forces (real interest rates, currency debasement fears, central-bank buying), not industrial consumption. Almost all the gold ever mined still exists; it isn't burned or built into things and used up. Oil's price reacts to refinery runs and OPEC; gold's price reacts to the Federal Reserve and fear. In the 2020 COVID panic, oil *crashed* (demand vanished) while gold *soared* (real rates went negative and investors fled to safety) — the two moved in opposite directions on the same day. They are different animals, and treating gold as "just another commodity" will get your mental model exactly backwards. (This is precisely why gold has [its own series](/blog/trading/gold/gold-futures-comex-contango-backwardation-and-paper-vs-physical).)

**Misconception 3: "Speculators cause high prices and should be banned."** This one is politically popular and economically confused. Speculators don't set the long-run price — the physical balance of supply and demand does. What speculators *do* is provide liquidity and absorb the risk that hedgers want to shed. Studies of markets that *restricted* speculation generally find *higher* volatility and *wider* bid-ask spreads, not lower prices, because removing the shock absorbers makes the price jumpier. Speculators can amplify short-term moves, but the idea that they manufacture sustained high prices out of thin air doesn't survive contact with the data: a barrel of paper oil eventually has to converge with the physical barrel at delivery, and no amount of speculation can make 100 million barrels a day of real demand disappear.

**Misconception 4: "The futures price is the market's forecast of the future spot price."** Tempting, but mostly false. As the carry worked example showed, the futures price is *spot plus cost of carry minus convenience yield* — it's an arbitrage relationship anchored to today's spot price and storage economics, not a crystal-ball forecast. A market in backwardation (futures below spot) is *not* predicting that prices will fall; it's telling you the physical market is tight *right now* and the convenience yield is high. Confusing the curve's slope with a price forecast leads to terrible trades. The curve is a thermometer, not a forecast.

**Misconception 5: "Commodity prices always rise with inflation, so commodities are a perfect inflation hedge."** Partly true, importantly incomplete. Commodities — especially energy — are a *component* of inflation and tend to rise when inflation is driven by real-economy demand or supply shocks, which is why the [1970s stagflation episode](/blog/trading/cross-asset/case-study-1970s-stagflation-commodities-win) was a golden era for the asset class. But the roll yield (Misconception 1) can eat the inflation protection if you hold the wrong way, and in a demand-collapse recession commodities can fall *despite* high prior inflation. They're a powerful but conditional hedge, not an automatic one — a nuance the [cross-asset work on the energy-inflation linkage](/blog/trading/cross-asset/oil-inflation-and-equities-the-energy-linkage) develops fully.

## How it shows up in real markets

Let's ground all of this in three real episodes where the physical-vs-paper gap wrote the headlines, with real dates and numbers.

**April 2020: when paper oil went negative.** We opened with it; now read it through the framework. On 20 April 2020, the May WTI futures contract settled at **−\$37.63**. The cause was a perfect collision of all three of our foundational properties. *Deliverability*: WTI is physically deliverable to Cushing, Oklahoma. *Storage*: Cushing's tanks were essentially full because COVID lockdowns had collapsed demand. *Standardization and the roll*: the May contract was about to expire, meaning holders would have to take physical delivery into tanks that didn't exist. The paper claim and the physical reality were forced to converge, and there was no physical room — so the paper price went *below zero* to reflect the cost of being stuck with un-storable oil. The June contract, with a month more breathing room, still traded above \$20 the same day. That spread — negative front month, \$20+ second month — is *super-contango*, the steepest possible upward curve, and it is the market screaming "we are physically out of storage." Anyone who understood the physical-paper gap saw it coming; anyone who thought "oil is cheap, I'll just buy it" got destroyed.

**2021–2022: the European gas crisis.** Natural gas is even harder to ship than oil — historically it had to flow through fixed pipelines, which meant regional prices could diverge wildly because the gas physically couldn't move between regions. When Russia throttled pipeline gas to Europe after the 2022 invasion of Ukraine, the European benchmark (Dutch TTF) spiked to around **€340 per megawatt-hour** in August 2022 — roughly *ten times* its normal level — while US natural gas (Henry Hub) stayed near \$6–8 per MMBtu because America had its own plentiful shale gas. Same molecule, radically different price, because the *physical* good couldn't easily cross the ocean. The fix was physical too: a fleet of ships carrying liquefied natural gas (LNG) gradually arbitraged the gap, and US LNG exports — essentially zero in 2015 — made America the world's largest LNG exporter by 2023. The lesson: when a commodity is hard to transport, the physical reality of *moving it* can dominate the price, and geography becomes destiny.

**2024: cocoa's moonshot and the limits of paper.** In 2024, cocoa — the bean behind chocolate — did something almost unprecedented: the price roughly *tripled* in a year, peaking near **\$12,931 a tonne** intraday, after disease and bad weather devastated the crop in Ivory Coast and Ghana, which together grow about 60% of the world's cocoa. Here the physical scarcity was so severe and so concentrated that no amount of paper trading could conjure beans that didn't exist on the trees. Speculators who were *short* cocoa (betting on a fall) got crushed as the physical shortage forced the paper price relentlessly higher. The episode is a clean demonstration of Misconception 3's flip side: paper markets can't override a genuine physical shortage — when the trees don't produce, the price has to ration the scarce beans, and it does so by going vertical.

Notice the common thread across all three: in each case the *physical* reality — full tanks, frozen pipelines, dead cocoa trees — asserted itself and dragged the paper price to where the physical situation demanded. The paper market is faster and more liquid, but at the moment of truth, the physical wins. That is the spine of this series stated as a pattern: *the paper price can wander, but delivery is the rope, and the rope eventually pulls.*

It is worth pausing on why these three episodes are *teachable* rather than just dramatic. Each one isolates a different one of the three foundational properties failing under stress. April 2020 was a **deliverability** crisis — the contract demanded physical delivery into a place with no room. The European gas crisis was a **transport** crisis — the standardized molecule existed in abundance, just on the wrong continent, and couldn't move. The 2024 cocoa squeeze was a **supply** crisis — the standardized grade simply wasn't being grown in sufficient quantity, and no financial cleverness could substitute for missing beans. When you study a commodity blow-up in this series, your first diagnostic question is always *which physical property broke?* — because the answer tells you whether the shock will mean-revert quickly (a transport bottleneck can be relieved by ships, as Europe's was) or persist (a multi-year supply deficit, as cocoa's drought-and-disease shock threatened to be). The same framework that explained the negative-oil headline will let you triage the next crisis the day it hits the tape, instead of a year later in a postmortem.

## The takeaway: how a curious investor should think about commodities

You've now got the foundation. Let me compress it into the handful of mental moves that will serve you through every post in this series and every commodity you ever look at.

**First, always separate the physical from the paper.** When you see a commodity price, ask: am I looking at the spot price (physical, now) or a futures price (paper, later)? And what is the *shape* of the curve between them — contango (upward, glutted/loose) or backwardation (downward, tight/scarce)? The curve is your single best read on the physical market's health, and we'll make reading it second nature in [the forward-curve post](/blog/trading/commodities/the-forward-curve-the-most-important-chart-in-commodities). Never assume that "buying the commodity" through a fund gives you the spot return — the roll can quietly bleed you, especially in contango.

**Second, ask how the physical good is produced and stored.** That physics predicts the price behavior. Hard to store (energy)? Expect violent glut-and-scarcity swings. One harvest a year (grains)? Trade the weather and the carryover stock. Geographically concentrated (softs, some metals)? Watch for single-country shocks that paper can't fix. Can't be stored at all (livestock)? The curve follows biology. The sector is the first clue to the personality.

**Third, keep consumption and monetary assets in separate boxes.** Oil, copper, wheat, coffee are *consumed* — their prices read real-world supply and demand for stuff being used up. Gold is *held* — its price reads real interest rates and fear. This is why gold gets [its own series](/blog/trading/gold/is-gold-money-a-commodity-or-a-currency-the-framing-that-decides-everything) and why we keep contrasting against it: the same chart-reading instincts that work for copper will mislead you on gold. Silver, platinum, and palladium straddle the line — part industrial, part monetary — which is exactly why they're more volatile than either pure case. As an asset-class allocator decides ([as the cross-asset series frames it](/blog/trading/cross-asset/metals-copper-silver-the-economys-pulse)), the question "is this thing consumed or hoarded?" determines almost everything about how it fits a portfolio.

**Fourth, identify who's on the other side.** Behind every price is a hedger transferring risk and a speculator absorbing it. When you trade a commodity, you're almost always taking the other side of a real-world business's hedge — and in a backwardated market, you may be getting *paid* a risk premium to do it. Understanding the four players ([the dedicated post](/blog/trading/commodities/the-four-players-producers-consumers-hedgers-and-speculators)) turns a confusing price into a legible negotiation.

**Fifth and last, use commodities as instruments, not just bets.** Copper reads global construction. Oil reads energy demand and geopolitics. The copper-gold ratio reads risk appetite. These markets are a real-time dashboard of the physical world economy, more honest in some ways than official statistics because real money is voting in real time, as [the macro series argues](/blog/trading/macro-trading/commodities-as-macro-signals-oil-copper-gold). Even if you never trade a single futures contract, learning to read commodities makes you a sharper reader of the entire economy.

That is the spine of "Commodities & Energy: The Physical and the Paper." A commodity is a standardized physical good forced through a financial contract; the forward curve, the cost of storage, and the convenience yield are the gears that decide who profits; and because commodities are consumption and industrial assets rather than monetary ones, they behave in ways that reward anyone who keeps the physical and the paper firmly distinct in their head. The negative-oil day we opened with wasn't a glitch — it was the asset class telling the truth about itself, loudly, for one day. Everything else in this series is learning to hear that truth on the quiet days too.

The next stop is the most important relationship in the whole class: [spot versus futures, the two prices of the same barrel](/blog/trading/commodities/spot-vs-futures-the-two-prices-of-the-same-barrel). Once you can read those two prices against each other, the forward curve — and everything it tells you about who's winning — comes into focus.
