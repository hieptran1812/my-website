On a humid morning in late 2022, the sales desk at Vietnam's biggest steelmaker had a problem that no spreadsheet had warned them about. The furnaces at Dung Quat were running hot, the ore was paid for, and the rebar was piling up — but the phones had gone quiet. The country's property developers, the buyers who took most of that steel, had simultaneously stopped breaking ground. A corporate-bond market that had financed apartment towers from Hanoi to the Mekong had frozen almost overnight, and with it the orders for the reinforcing bar that goes into every concrete column. Hoa Phat, a company that had spent four years and billions of dollars building one of Southeast Asia's largest integrated steel complexes, watched its quarterly profit collapse from a record to a loss in the space of two quarters. It was the first net loss the company had reported in over a decade.

That collapse, and the recovery that followed, is the whole story of this post in miniature. Steel is the most cyclical major commodity there is, because it is consumed almost entirely by construction and heavy industry — the two things an economy buys most when it is booming and least when it is scared. And in Vietnam, the company that sits in the middle of that cycle, owning more of the supply chain than anyone else, is Hoa Phat Group, ticker HPG on the Ho Chi Minh exchange. If you want to take the temperature of Vietnamese industry without reading a single government statistic, you can do worse than watching what HPG's furnaces are doing and what its share price is saying.

This post builds the steel business from the rock up. We will follow a tonne of iron ore from a ship at Dung Quat through the blast furnace to a length of rebar on a construction site, and at every step we will ask the question this series always asks: a commodity is a physical thing forced through a financial contract — so who profits, who is exposed, and how do you read it? The answer for steel turns out to be unusually clean, because Hoa Phat has deliberately bought up almost the entire chain, which makes its margin a textbook spread between a global dollar-priced input and a local dong-priced output.

![Steel value chain from iron ore and coking coal through furnace to rebar with Hoa Phat integrated across it](/imgs/blogs/steel-hpg-and-the-vietnam-construction-cycle-1.png)

Look at the chain above before we go further, because it is the map for everything that follows. Ore and coal come in at the left, priced in dollars and set by a benchmark in China. They pass through a port, a furnace, and a rolling mill — all of which Hoa Phat owns — and come out at the right as rebar and coil, priced in dong and sold into a construction market that has its own boom-bust rhythm. The genius and the risk of the business both live in that picture: Hoa Phat captures the margin at every internal handoff, but it is also exposed to the price of two things at the start of the chain and the demand for two things at the end of it, and it controls none of those four prices.

## Foundations: how steel is actually made

Before we can read Hoa Phat, we need to understand what a steel mill physically does, because the cost structure of the business falls directly out of the chemistry. We will define every term as we go.

**Steel is iron with the carbon tuned.** Iron ore is rock — typically hematite or magnetite — that is roughly 60% iron by weight, bound up with oxygen and impurities. To turn that rock into structural steel you have to do two things: strip the oxygen off the iron (a process called *reduction*), and then control how much carbon ends up in the final metal. Too much carbon and the steel is brittle like cast iron; too little and it is soft. Construction-grade rebar sits at a precise, low carbon content that makes it strong but still bendable.

There are two dominant routes to get there, and the difference between them drives almost everything about a steelmaker's cost and carbon footprint.

**Route one: the blast furnace plus basic oxygen furnace (BF-BOF).** This is the old, capital-heavy, integrated route, and it is the one Hoa Phat is built on. You take iron ore, you take *coking coal* — a specific high-grade coal that is baked in ovens into a hard, porous carbon called *coke* — and you load them with limestone into a blast furnace, a tower-sized vessel where superheated air burns the coke. The coke does double duty: it provides the heat, and its carbon chemically rips the oxygen off the iron ore. Out the bottom comes molten *pig iron*, which is iron with too much carbon. That pig iron then goes into a *basic oxygen furnace*, where pure oxygen is blown through it to burn off the excess carbon down to the target. The result is *crude steel* — liquid steel ready to be cast and rolled. The whole route runs on two imported rocks: iron ore and coking coal.

**Route two: scrap plus electric arc furnace (EAF).** Here you skip the ore entirely. You take steel *scrap* — old cars, demolished buildings, factory offcuts — and melt it with giant electrodes that strike an arc, like an industrial welding torch scaled up to a building. The EAF route is cheaper to build, lower-carbon, and flexible, but it depends on a steady supply of scrap and a lot of cheap electricity, and in a country like Vietnam that does not yet have decades of accumulated steel to demolish, scrap is comparatively scarce and expensive. So Vietnam leans heavily on the ore-based BF-BOF route, which is exactly why imported iron ore and coking coal matter so much here.

The crude steel coming out of either route is then *rolled* — squeezed between heavy rollers — into the products people actually buy. The two that matter for our story are:

- **Rebar** (reinforcing bar): the ribbed steel rod that goes inside reinforced concrete. This is *the* construction staple — every column, beam, and foundation in a concrete building is full of it. Rebar demand is a direct read on how much building is happening.
- **Hot-rolled coil (HRC):** flat steel wound into giant coils, the feedstock for everything from car bodies and appliances to the pipe and structural sections used in industry. HRC is a higher-value, more technically demanding product, and Vietnam historically had to import most of it — which is precisely the gap Hoa Phat's Dung Quat complex was built to fill.

It is worth dwelling on *why* Vietnam leans on the ore route rather than the scrap route, because it explains the country's import dependency. The EAF-scrap route is, in many places, the cheaper and greener way to make steel — the United States makes most of its steel from scrap this way. But scrap is, in effect, *recycled* steel, and you can only recycle steel that already exists. A young, fast-growing economy is still in the phase of *accumulating* its steel stock — pouring it into new buildings, bridges, and machines — rather than demolishing old steel to feed back into furnaces. So the domestic scrap pool is thin and expensive, and the country must import either the scrap itself or, as Hoa Phat chose, the iron ore to make virgin steel from scratch. The deep reason a Vietnamese steelmaker is exposed to the global ore benchmark is therefore demographic and developmental: the country has not been industrialized long enough to have built up the scrap that would let it skip the ore. As Vietnam matures over the coming decades, the scrap pool will grow and the EAF route will take more share — but for now, the blast furnace and imported ore rule.

The coke step inside the blast-furnace route is its own small economic universe, and it is one more place integration pays off. *Coke* is not coal; it is coal that has been *baked* in sealed ovens at high temperature to drive off volatile compounds, leaving a hard, porous, almost-pure carbon that can bear the weight of the ore burden in the furnace and react cleanly with it. Coking coal that can make good coke is a premium, geologically scarce grade — most coal in the world is *thermal* coal, fit only for burning in power plants, not for making coke. Australia and a handful of other regions dominate the seaborne premium coking-coal trade, and the price of that grade is set globally and quoted in dollars. A mill that owns its own coke ovens, as Hoa Phat does, captures the conversion margin between raw coking coal and finished coke instead of paying it to a third party — yet another internal handoff turned into captured margin.

So the foundational picture is this: a Vietnamese integrated steelmaker buys two imported rocks priced in dollars, burns enormous amounts of energy to turn them into liquid steel, and rolls that into rebar and coil sold domestically in dong. Hold onto that dollar-in, dong-out structure — it is the single most important thing about the financial shape of the business.

#### Worked example: what's actually inside a tonne of pig iron

Roughly speaking, to make one tonne of crude steel by the blast-furnace route you need on the order of **1.5 tonnes of iron ore** and **0.6 tonnes of coking coal** (plus coke losses, limestone, and a lot of energy). Put dollar prices on those inputs using rough global benchmarks: iron ore around \$110 per tonne and premium coking coal around \$220 per tonne.

- Ore: 1.5 t × \$110 = **\$165**
- Coking coal: 0.6 t × \$220 = **\$132**
- Raw materials alone: about **\$297 per tonne of steel**

Add coke-making losses, energy, refractories, labour, and depreciation, and the all-in cash cost of a tonne of integrated steel lands somewhere around \$450 to \$520. The intuition to keep: well over half the cost of a tonne of Vietnamese steel walked off a ship as dollar-priced ore and coal — the steelmaker's profit is whatever is left after those imported rocks are paid for.

## Hoa Phat: the company that bought the whole chain

Hoa Phat did not start as a steel giant. It began in 1992 trading construction machinery, moved into making steel pipe and furniture, and only became a serious primary steelmaker in the 2000s. The decision that made it the company it is today was the bet on *vertical integration* — owning every stage of the chain rather than buying inputs and selling outputs on the open market.

The clearest expression of that bet is the **Dung Quat Integrated Steel Complex** in Quang Ngai province, on the central coast. Built in two phases starting in 2017, Dung Quat is an enormous integrated site: it has its own coke ovens, its own blast furnaces and oxygen converters, its own rolling mills, and — critically — its own deep-water port. That port is the detail that quietly does the most work. Iron ore is a low-value, high-bulk commodity; the cost of unloading it and trucking it inland can be a meaningful slice of the input cost. By owning a deep-water berth where the largest ore carriers can dock and discharge directly into the plant, Hoa Phat strips out a layer of port fees, traders, and inland freight that a non-integrated mill has to pay.

The output speaks for itself. Watch what happened to Hoa Phat's crude steel production as Dung Quat came online.

![Hoa Phat crude steel output 2019 to 2024 in million tonnes per year](/imgs/blogs/steel-hpg-and-the-vietnam-construction-cycle-2.png)

In 2019, before Dung Quat phase one was fully ramped, Hoa Phat made about 2.8 million tonnes of crude steel. By 2021, with the complex running, it was making 8.0 million tonnes — nearly a tripling in two years. That is the signature of a company that did not grow by buying market share but by physically building more furnace capacity than anyone else in the country. Then came the 2022-23 dip, where output fell back to 6.7 million tonnes as the property freeze hit demand, followed by a recovery to a fresh record of about 8.5 million tonnes in 2024. The shape of that line — a steep ramp, a cyclical dip, a recovery — is the shape of the whole story.

Why does owning the chain matter so much? Because steel is a *commodity* product. A tonne of construction rebar from Hoa Phat is chemically identical to a tonne from a rival; buyers choose almost entirely on price. In a business where you cannot charge a premium for your product, the only durable competitive advantage is being the lowest-cost producer. And the way you get to be the lowest-cost producer in steel is by integrating, so that you capture the margin a non-integrated rival has to pay away at every handoff.

![Vertical integration grid comparing Hoa Phat integrated to a non-integrated rival across the chain](/imgs/blogs/steel-hpg-and-the-vietnam-construction-cycle-5.png)

The grid above lays out the comparison stage by stage. When ore and coal arrive, Hoa Phat unloads them at its own port; a non-integrated mill pays a third-party port and often a trader's cut. When it is time to make coke and iron, Hoa Phat runs its own ovens and blast furnaces; a non-integrated rival often has to buy semi-finished billet or even finished coil at the market price, which already bakes in someone else's profit. Stack those advantages and Hoa Phat ends up at the bottom of the domestic cost curve. That position is not just a nice-to-have — it is what determines who survives a downturn. When steel prices fall in a slump, the high-cost mills hit the point where every tonne loses money and they idle their furnaces; the low-cost producer keeps running at a thin but positive margin, takes share from the wounded, and comes out of the cycle bigger. Hoa Phat has done exactly that, twice.

There is a subtle cost to integration that deserves naming, because it explains the violence of Hoa Phat's 2022 earnings swing. An integrated mill is *capital-intensive* — building blast furnaces, coke ovens, ports, and rolling lines costs billions, and that money is usually borrowed. So an integrated producer carries two kinds of leverage at once. The first is *operating leverage*: a high proportion of its costs are fixed (depreciation on those furnaces, the minimum energy to keep them lit), so profit per tonne is very sensitive to volume. The second is *financial leverage*: the debt taken on to build the plant means interest must be paid in good years and bad. Stack operating leverage on top of financial leverage and you get a company whose earnings can triple in an up-cycle and turn negative in a down-cycle — which is exactly the Hoa Phat earnings record. Integration buys the lowest cost per tonne, but it pays for that with the highest sensitivity to the cycle. There is no free lunch: the same furnaces that make Hoa Phat the survivor in a bust are the fixed costs that make the bust hurt.

This is also why the *timing* of a capacity build matters so much for a steelmaker's fortunes. Hoa Phat finished the bulk of its Dung Quat phase-one build right before the 2022 demand wall. It walked into the downturn carrying fresh debt, fresh depreciation, and inventory bought at the 2021-2022 input-price peak — every form of leverage loaded and pointed the wrong way. A company that had finished building two years earlier, or two years later, would have hit the same downturn with a very different balance sheet. The lesson for reading any heavy-industry cyclical: track where the company is in its *own* capex cycle, not just where the commodity is in its price cycle. A producer mid-build is far more fragile than the same producer with the plant paid down and the debt retired.

#### Worked example: what the port saves per tonne

Suppose unloading imported iron ore through a third-party port and trader adds, conservatively, \$8 per tonne of ore versus discharging it at your own berth. With 1.5 tonnes of ore per tonne of steel, that is **1.5 × \$8 = \$12 per tonne of steel** saved on ore handling alone, before counting the same logic on coal and the margin saved by not buying semi-finished billet.

Twelve dollars a tonne sounds trivial until you multiply by volume: across 8.5 million tonnes of output, \$12 per tonne is **roughly \$100 million a year** of cost advantage from one piece of integration. The intuition: in a commodity business where margins are measured in tens of dollars per tonne, owning the port is not a logistics footnote — it is a material chunk of the profit.

## The margin is a spread, not a price

Here is the single most important financial idea in this whole post, and it is worth slowing down on. Most beginners think a steelmaker "makes money when steel prices go up." That is half-true and dangerously incomplete. What a steelmaker actually earns is the *spread* between its output price and its input cost — and because the two are priced in different currencies and set in different markets, that spread can get squeezed even when the steel price itself is rising.

![Input cost squeeze with USD ore and coal in and VND steel out and the margin spread](/imgs/blogs/steel-hpg-and-the-vietnam-construction-cycle-4.png)

The figure above is the financial heart of the business. On the left are the costs, all priced in dollars: iron ore off the global benchmark, coking coal off the Australian benchmark, plus conversion and energy. On the right is the revenue, priced in dong: rebar and coil sold to Vietnamese builders. The gross margin is the gap between the two stacks, and it is exposed to a squeeze from either side. Squeeze one: ore and coal prices rise while the steel price is sticky, and the margin compresses from the cost side. Squeeze two: construction demand falls, the steel price drops, and the margin compresses from the revenue side. In a bad year, both happen at once — and that is precisely what 2022 did to Hoa Phat.

The currency split deserves a moment of its own. Iron ore and coking coal trade on global markets quoted in US dollars, and the reference price the whole world watches is the iron-ore benchmark delivered into China — because China makes about half the world's steel and therefore buys most of the world's seaborne ore. So Hoa Phat's input cost is effectively set in Qingdao, not in Quang Ngai. Its revenue, meanwhile, is collected in dong from domestic builders. When the dong weakens against the dollar, the dollar cost of ore stays the same in dollar terms but rises in dong terms, squeezing the margin even if nothing else moves. A steelmaker in this position is therefore running an unhedged bet on three things at once: the dollar price of its inputs, the dong price of its output, and the exchange rate between them.

This is why steel is a *spread* business, and why it behaves so much more violently than the underlying steel price. If steel sells for \$600 and costs \$500, the margin is \$100. A 10% rise in input cost to \$550 — with the steel price unchanged — cuts the margin in half, to \$50. The margin has the same leverage to input costs that an option has to its underlying: a small move in a big number swings a large move in the small number that is left over. That leverage is wonderful on the way up and brutal on the way down, and it is why steelmaker earnings are some of the most volatile in any equity market.

The currency exposure deserves to be treated as a third risk on its own, alongside the input price and the demand cycle, because it is the one investors most often forget. Hoa Phat's costs are effectively dollar-denominated — the ore and coal are priced in USD, and even the imported equipment and some financing carry dollar exposure — while its revenue is collected in dong. That means a depreciating dong is a headwind to the margin all by itself, mechanically, even if global ore prices and domestic steel prices both hold perfectly steady. When the State Bank of Vietnam lets the dong slide to defend its reserves or manage capital flows, a Vietnamese steelmaker's dollar-priced cost base rises in dong terms and the margin compresses. So an investor reading HPG is implicitly taking a view on the dong as well as on steel, ore, demand, and China. Few single-stock theses carry that many uncorrelated macro exposures stacked on top of an operating business — which is precisely why the stock is so volatile and why it is best understood as a leveraged bet on a whole constellation of cyclical forces, not as a simple "buy steel" trade.

#### Worked example: Hoa Phat's steel margin, tonne by tonne (USD-anchored)

Let's build a single tonne's economics in dollars so the spread is concrete. Assume Hoa Phat sells construction rebar at the dong equivalent of **\$600 per tonne** and faces these costs:

- Iron ore: 1.5 t × \$110 = \$165
- Coking coal: 0.6 t × \$220 = \$132
- Conversion (energy, coke loss, labour, depreciation): about \$200
- **Total cash cost ≈ \$497 per tonne**

Gross margin per tonne = \$600 − \$497 = **about \$103 per tonne**, or roughly a 17% gross margin. Across 8.5 million tonnes that is on the order of \$875 million of gross profit in a good year. The intuition to carry: the margin is a thin slice sitting on top of a big cost base, so anything that moves the big number — ore, coal, the exchange rate — swings the thin slice hard.

## The demand side: property and public works

We have built the cost side from the rock up. Now the demand side, because a steelmaker's fortunes are set as much by who is buying as by what it costs to produce. In Vietnam, steel demand runs on two engines, and the relationship between them is the key to reading the cycle.

**Engine one: the private property cycle.** Most steel in a developing economy goes into buildings, and in Vietnam that has meant a long property boom — apartment towers, commercial developments, and the speculative land market that financed them. Crucially, that boom was funded heavily by *corporate bonds*: property developers issued bonds to raise the cash to buy land and build. When that bond market was flowing, construction roared and rebar demand with it. When it froze, the whole engine stalled.

**Engine two: public infrastructure investment.** The government's own building program — highways, bridges, airports, urban metro lines, the long-discussed north-south expressway and high-speed rail — is the other big buyer of steel. This is *public investment*, funded by the state budget rather than by private credit, and it runs on a different clock. When the private property engine stalls, the government can deliberately accelerate public investment to keep construction activity — and steel demand, and employment — from collapsing. That is exactly the policy lever Hanoi reached for in 2023.

Before we look at how the two engines interact, hold the actual demand numbers in view, because they ground everything that follows. National steel consumption is the total tonnage of steel the whole country swallows in a year, and its swings *are* the construction cycle made visible.

![Vietnam steel consumption 2019 to 2024 in million tonnes per year](/imgs/blogs/steel-hpg-and-the-vietnam-construction-cycle-3.png)

The step chart above reads the national appetite for steel year by year. Consumption sat around 23 to 24 million tonnes through 2021 as the property boom ran hot, then stepped down to about 21 million tonnes through the 2022-23 freeze, and recovered toward 23.5 million tonnes in 2024 as the public-investment push and a property thaw pulled it back up. Notice how *shallow* the dip looks in absolute terms — a fall of only two or three million tonnes, perhaps 12% of the market. That modest-looking demand wobble is the same downturn that flipped Hoa Phat from a record profit to a loss, which is the single best illustration of operating leverage you will find: a small move in volume, a savage move in profit. Keep that contrast in mind — the country's demand barely flinched, yet the producer's earnings cratered, because the margin is a thin slice on a big, fixed cost base.

A second thing worth reading off this chart is the *floor*. Even at the bottom of the worst property freeze in a decade, Vietnam still consumed around 21 million tonnes of steel. That floor is the structural demand of an economy that is still urbanizing, still industrializing, and still building out its road and power network — the kind of baseline a mature, slow-growing economy would not have. It is why a long-horizon investor can hold a Vietnamese steel thesis through a brutal cyclical dip: the secular trend of a developing economy building its physical capital stock keeps the floor rising over time, even as the cycle oscillates around it.

![Two demand engines property and public infrastructure and how they offset](/imgs/blogs/steel-hpg-and-the-vietnam-construction-cycle-7.png)

The diagram above shows how the two engines split and then recombine. Steel demand branches into private property and public infrastructure. Property can be in a boom (credit flowing) or a freeze (the 2022-23 bond crisis). When it freezes, the public-investment push is the offset that keeps total demand from cratering. The whole point of the figure is that *total* demand is steadier than either source on its own, because the two are partly counter-cyclical: the government leans into public works precisely when the private cycle is weak. This is what kept Hoa Phat's furnaces from going fully cold in 2023 and set up the 2024 recovery.

That said, do not overstate the offset. Public investment in Vietnam is large but it is not large *enough* to fully replace a private property boom at its peak; government infrastructure spending notoriously moves slowly through planning and disbursement; and the property sector is simply the bigger buyer in absolute tonnes. So the offset softens the cycle, it does not cancel it. The 2022-23 downturn was real and painful even with public works ramping — Hoa Phat still posted losses. The lesson is that the public-investment engine puts a *floor* under the cycle, not a ceiling on the downside.

#### Worked example: the property-freeze demand hit

Walk through what the 2022-23 freeze did to the margin, using the consumption data. National steel consumption fell from about 24 million tonnes in 2021 to roughly 21 million tonnes in 2023 — call it a **12.5% drop** in the size of the whole market. For a producer, a demand drop like that hits the margin through two channels at once.

- **Channel one, utilization.** A blast furnace has enormous fixed costs and cannot be cheaply switched off and on, so when orders fall the plant runs below capacity. Spreading the same fixed cost over fewer tonnes raises the cost *per tonne*. If fixed costs are \$150 of a \$497 tonne and output falls 15%, the fixed cost per tonne rises to about \$176 — adding roughly \$26 to unit cost.
- **Channel two, price.** With every mill chasing fewer orders, the steel price itself falls. Say rebar drops from \$600 to \$540, a \$60 hit to revenue per tonne.

Combine them: the \$103 margin in the good-year example collapses toward \$540 − (\$497 + \$26) = **about \$17 per tonne**, an 80%-plus compression in profitability from a 12.5% drop in demand. The intuition: in a high-fixed-cost commodity business, a modest fall in volume produces a savage fall in profit — operating leverage cuts both ways.

## How input shocks ripple through

We have seen demand squeeze the margin from the revenue side. Now watch the input side, because for an ore-based steelmaker the cost of iron ore and coking coal is where the most sudden shocks come from. These are globally traded commodities, and they move on Chinese demand, on weather (coking coal especially — Australian mines flood), and on geopolitics, none of which a Vietnamese steelmaker can influence.

The transmission is direct and fast. Hoa Phat carries some weeks of ore and coal inventory, so a price shock does not hit instantly, but within a quarter or two a jump in the benchmark ore price flows straight into the cost of every tonne of steel. And because, as we saw, the margin is a thin slice on a big cost base, a moderate move in the input price is a large move in the margin.

#### Worked example: an iron-ore input-cost shock

Start from the good-year tonne: \$600 revenue, \$497 cost, \$103 margin. Now suppose the global iron-ore benchmark rises by **\$30 per tonne** — a move of the size that has happened repeatedly when Chinese steel mills restock. With 1.5 tonnes of ore per tonne of steel, the cost increase is:

- 1.5 t × \$30 = **\$45 added to the cost of every tonne of steel**

If the steel price does not rise to match — and it often cannot, because Vietnamese rebar prices are set by competitive domestic demand, not by the producer's costs — then the margin falls from \$103 to \$103 − \$45 = **\$58 per tonne**, a 44% margin hit from a single input move the company had no control over. Across 8.5 million tonnes that \$45 swing is roughly \$380 million of gross profit. The intuition: the input benchmark set in China can wipe out nearly half a Vietnamese steelmaker's margin without the steel price moving at all — the producer is short ore and coal whether it likes it or not.

Now flip it to show the offset working. In 2023, even as the property engine was weak, the government's public-investment push provided demand that helped stabilize steel prices, and falling input costs (ore and coal came off their 2022 war-spike highs) helped the margin recover from the cost side. By 2024 both tailwinds — recovering demand plus normalized input costs — were pushing the same direction, and Hoa Phat printed record output. That is the cyclical recovery in action: the margin spread is squeezed by the cycle, and it expands again when the cycle turns, with the public-investment engine putting a floor under the bottom.

#### Worked example: the public-investment offset, in tonnes

How much steel does the public-works engine actually move? Take a single large project as a yardstick. A long-span expressway and its bridges can consume on the order of **hundreds of thousands of tonnes** of rebar and structural steel; a major airport terminal, similar. If the government accelerates a slate of such projects that collectively add, say, **2 million tonnes** of annual steel demand during a private-sector slump, that is enough to offset a meaningful chunk of a property-driven decline.

Against national consumption of roughly 22 million tonnes, a 2-million-tonne public-investment swing is about **9% of the entire market** — material enough to be the difference between mills running at a profit and mills idling at a loss. The intuition: public infrastructure is not a sideshow for a steelmaker; in a down-cycle it can be the marginal buyer that keeps the lowest-cost producer's furnaces lit and profitable.

## HPG as the proxy for the whole construction cycle

Step back and look at the company against its market. Hoa Phat's output and Vietnam's total steel consumption move on the same cycle but at very different scales, and the relationship between them tells you something important about both the company and the country.

![Hoa Phat output versus Vietnam steel consumption on a dual axis](/imgs/blogs/steel-hpg-and-the-vietnam-construction-cycle-6.png)

The chart plots Hoa Phat's output on the left axis against total national consumption on the right. Two things jump out. First, they rise and fall together — the 2022-23 dip is visible in both lines — which confirms that Hoa Phat is riding the national construction cycle, not some company-specific story. Second, and more interesting, Hoa Phat's share of its home market roughly tripled across the period. In 2019 its 2.8 million tonnes was around 12% of national consumption; by 2024 its 8.5 million tonnes was something like a third of everything Vietnam used. The company grew through the cycle by adding low-cost capacity exactly when weaker rivals were retreating.

That share gain is why HPG works as a *proxy* for the construction cycle. When a single company supplies a third of a country's steel, and steel is the material backbone of construction, that company's volumes and margins effectively summarize whether the country is building. An equity investor who wants exposure to "Vietnam is building more" does not need to assemble a basket of cement makers, contractors, and developers; HPG captures most of that theme in one liquid, large-cap stock. The flip side is that HPG is *only* that — it is a leveraged play on the construction cycle, with all the volatility that implies, and it will not protect you when the cycle turns.

There is one more dimension to the Hoa Phat story that is genuinely forward-looking: the move into higher-value flat steel. Vietnam has long imported most of its HRC, the flat coil that feeds manufacturing. Hoa Phat's second giant complex, Dung Quat phase two, is aimed squarely at that import-substitution gap — making domestically the HRC that car, appliance, and pipe makers used to buy from abroad. If that bet pays off, it shifts part of Hoa Phat's revenue away from pure construction rebar (the most cyclical product) toward manufacturing-linked flat steel (still cyclical, but tied to a broader and more durable industrial base). It is the company's attempt to be less of a pure construction-cycle proxy over time.

The product mix matters more than it first appears, and it is one of the more sophisticated things to track in the name. Rebar is the most commoditized, most construction-tied, most cyclical product a steelmaker can sell — its demand is a pure function of how many buildings are going up. HRC sits one rung up the value ladder: it is harder to make to specification, it commands a better margin, and its demand is spread across the whole manufacturing economy — autos, appliances, electronics enclosures, pipe — rather than concentrated in construction. A steelmaker that shifts its mix from rebar toward HRC is doing two things at once: lifting its average margin per tonne, and diversifying its demand base away from the single most volatile end-market. For an investor, a rising HRC share is a quiet de-risking of the business model, even though the headline "steel company" label does not change. The catch is that HRC also puts Hoa Phat into more direct competition with the Chinese, Korean, and Japanese flat-steel exporters who have made it for decades — so the import-substitution prize comes with stiffer competition and the constant threat of dumping. The flat-steel bet is the company's most important strategic question, and it will decide whether HPG slowly becomes a broader industrial play or stays a leveraged construction proxy.

## China sets the marginal price

We cannot finish the foundations of steel without confronting the elephant in the room: China. China makes roughly **half of all the steel in the world** and consumes a similar share. That dominance means China sets the marginal price for both the inputs and the outputs of the entire global steel industry, and a Vietnamese producer lives inside that price whether it trades with China directly or not.

On the input side, China is the dominant buyer of seaborne iron ore, so the global ore benchmark is effectively the China-delivered price. When Chinese mills restock, ore rises and Hoa Phat's costs rise with it; when Chinese property weakens and mills cut output, ore falls and Hoa Phat's costs ease. On the output side, China is a massive *exporter* of finished steel, especially when its own domestic demand is weak. Cheap Chinese steel exports can flood into Southeast Asia and undercut local producers' prices — which is why Vietnam, like many countries, periodically imposes anti-dumping duties on imported Chinese steel to protect domestic mills. So China influences Hoa Phat from both ends: it sets the cost of the ore coming in and it caps the price of the steel going out.

There is a structural asymmetry here that is easy to miss. China's steel industry is so large that even a *small percentage* shift in its domestic balance is an enormous *absolute* tonnage on the world market. When Chinese property weakens, Chinese mills do not immediately cut output to match — output is sticky for the same operating-leverage reasons we saw with Hoa Phat — so the surplus steel gets exported. A 5% drop in Chinese domestic steel demand can throw tens of millions of tonnes onto the export market, more than the entire annual consumption of Vietnam. That wall of cheap exported steel is what local producers across Southeast Asia fear, and it is why the steel trade is one of the most heavily protected in the world, dense with anti-dumping cases and safeguard duties. For Hoa Phat, the practical read is that a *weak* China is a double-edged thing: it lowers ore costs (good for the cost side) but it also unleashes export dumping that caps domestic steel prices (bad for the revenue side). A *strong, stimulating* China is the mirror image: ore costs rise, but the export pressure eases and regional prices firm. The net effect depends on which channel dominates in a given episode, which is why you cannot read "China is slowing" as simply bullish or bearish for a Vietnamese steelmaker — you have to trace it through both the cost and the price channel.

This is the deepest reason steel is a *China-influenced* business, and it connects steel back to the broader commodity complex. The same Chinese construction and industrial cycle that drives the iron-ore price also drives copper, the price that the broader market reads as the pulse of global growth. If you want to understand why Vietnamese steel margins swing the way they do, you have to watch China — its property starts, its stimulus, its steel-export quotas — as closely as you watch Vietnam's own construction sites. Iron ore, coking coal, and steel are not isolated markets; they are the heavy-industry corner of a single global commodity complex whose center of gravity is Chinese demand. A Vietnamese steel investor is, whether they realize it or not, taking a position on the Chinese industrial cycle dressed up as a domestic construction play.

## Common misconceptions

**"Hoa Phat makes more money when steel prices rise."** Only sometimes. Hoa Phat makes money on the *spread* between steel and its inputs. In a global commodity boom, iron ore and coking coal often rise *faster* than steel, and the spread compresses even as the headline steel price climbs. In 2021-2022, ore and coal spiked on the post-COVID restock and the energy crisis; Hoa Phat's revenue per tonne rose, but its cost per tonne rose more, and the margin got squeezed. Watch the spread, not the steel price.

**"A bigger steelmaker is automatically a safer one."** Size in a high-fixed-cost, cyclical business is a double-edged sword. Hoa Phat's huge furnaces give it scale economies in a boom, but those same furnaces are expensive to run below capacity and cannot be cheaply switched off in a bust. The 2022 loss came partly *because* the company had just finished a massive capacity build and then ran into a demand wall. Scale lowers cost per tonne when volumes are high and raises it when volumes fall — it amplifies the cycle rather than dampening it.

**"Public investment will smooth out the cycle."** It softens it, but the offset is partial and slow. Government infrastructure spending in Vietnam is real and growing, but it moves through a planning-and-disbursement pipeline that takes quarters to years to ramp, and even at full tilt it does not match a private property boom in tonnage. The 2022-23 downturn happened *despite* a public-investment push. Treat public works as a floor under the down-cycle, not as a cancellation of it.

**"HPG is a defensive way to play Vietnam's growth."** The opposite. HPG is one of the most *cyclical* large stocks on the exchange — a leveraged bet on the construction cycle, with operating leverage on volumes, financial leverage from the debt that built Dung Quat, and an unhedged spread between dollar costs and dong revenue. It can compound beautifully across a multi-year building boom and lose half its value in a freeze. It is a high-beta cyclical, not a defensive holding.

**"Vietnamese steel is insulated from China because it is sold domestically."** No. Even steel made and sold entirely inside Vietnam is priced against the cost of imported Chinese steel and the global, China-set ore benchmark. Chinese export dumping caps domestic prices; the China-delivered ore price sets domestic costs. There is no hiding from China in a steel business anywhere in Asia.

## How it shows up in real markets

The cleanest case study is the one we opened with: the **2022-23 property and bond freeze**. Through 2020 and 2021, Vietnamese property developers had financed a building boom with a wave of corporate-bond issuance. In 2022, a combination of a regulatory crackdown on bond-market abuses, rising interest rates, and a few high-profile developer defaults froze that market almost completely. Developers could not roll their debt, projects stalled, and the orders for rebar dried up. Hoa Phat — which had *just* completed the enormous Dung Quat capacity build and was carrying the debt to show for it — ran straight into the wall. National steel consumption fell from roughly 24 million tonnes in 2021 toward 21 million by 2023, the company's output dropped from 8.0 to 6.7 million tonnes, and it posted its first quarterly losses in over a decade. The stock fell more than 70% from its 2021 peak. Every part of the squeeze we built up earlier — falling demand, falling prices, high fixed costs, expensive inventory bought at the war-spike top — landed at once.

Then the **2023-24 recovery** showed the offset working in real time. The government accelerated public investment, pushing on highways, airports, and the long-planned infrastructure backlog, putting a floor under steel demand even while property stayed weak. Input costs eased as ore and coking coal came off their 2022 highs. And the property market itself slowly began to thaw as policy support arrived. By 2024 Hoa Phat was running its furnaces at a fresh record of about 8.5 million tonnes, its margins had recovered, and the stock had retraced much of its loss. The recovery validated the bull case for an integrated low-cost producer: it survived the bottom that pushed weaker mills out, and it came through the cycle with more share than it had going in.

The 2024 recovery also exposed a detail that often confuses newcomers: the stock and the earnings did not move in lockstep, and the stock moved *first*. Equity markets are forward-looking machines; they try to price next year's earnings, not this quarter's. So HPG began to recover well before the reported profit did, anticipating the demand thaw and the margin normalization. An investor waiting for the earnings to confirm the turn would have bought the recovery late, after the stock had already re-rated. This is the eternal frustration and opportunity of deep cyclicals: the best entry is when current results are still ugly but the forward setup is improving, and the best exit is when current results are glorious but the forward setup is deteriorating. The data lags; the price leads. Reading a steelmaker well means reading the *forward* spread and the *forward* demand engines, not the trailing income statement.

The third, longer-arc case study is the **import-substitution build itself**. Hoa Phat's whole strategic history is a bet that Vietnam would keep industrializing and would want to make its own steel rather than import it. The first Dung Quat complex substituted for imported construction long steel; the second phase aims at imported HRC. This is the same supercycle logic that drove the 2000s commodity boom — a large, fast-industrializing Asian economy building out its physical capital stock and pulling in the raw materials to do it. Reading Hoa Phat over a decade is, in a real sense, reading Vietnam's industrialization in a single ticker. The risk in that thesis is symmetrical to its appeal: if Vietnamese growth slows structurally, or if the country urbanizes far enough that its steel intensity peaks the way China's did, the secular floor under demand stops rising and the business becomes a pure cyclical with no growth tailwind. For now, with the country still building roads, ports, power lines, and apartments at a developing-economy clip, that secular tailwind is real — but it is a thesis to revisit, not a permanent law.

## The playbook: how to read HPG and steel

So how should a curious investor actually read this? Steel and Hoa Phat reward a checklist, because the moving parts are knowable and the cycle is legible if you watch the right gauges. Here is the frame.

**1. Read the spread, not the steel price.** The number that drives Hoa Phat's earnings is the gap between the steel price and the cost of ore plus coking coal. Track all three. A rising steel price with faster-rising ore is *bearish* for margins even though the headline looks bullish. The healthiest setup for the stock is steel prices firm while ore and coal are soft — the spread widening from both sides.

**2. Watch the two demand engines separately.** Property: is the corporate-bond market flowing, are developers breaking ground, are apartment pre-sales recovering? Public investment: is budget disbursement accelerating, are the flagship infrastructure projects actually moving dirt? When property is weak, the public-investment pace is the swing factor that decides whether the down-cycle has a floor.

**3. Respect the operating and financial leverage.** Hoa Phat has high fixed costs (the furnaces) and meaningful debt (from building them). That means small swings in volume produce large swings in profit, and a demand air-pocket can flip the company from record profit to loss in two quarters — as it did in 2022. This is a high-beta cyclical; size your conviction and your position to that reality.

**4. Watch China at both ends.** China sets the ore benchmark that is Hoa Phat's cost and exports the cheap steel that caps Hoa Phat's price. Chinese property starts, Chinese stimulus, and Chinese steel-export volumes are leading indicators for a Vietnamese steelmaker's margins. When China stimulates and restocks, ore rises (cost headwind) but regional demand firms (price tailwind); when China dumps exports, prices get capped. Hold both effects in mind at once.

**5. Mind the inventory cycle, which amplifies everything.** Steel mills, traders, and construction sites all hold inventory, and that inventory is itself pro-cyclical in a way that exaggerates the swings. When prices are rising, everyone in the chain wants to hold *more* steel — builders pre-buy, traders stockpile — which adds a layer of restocking demand on top of real construction demand, accelerating the up-move. When prices turn down, the same players dump inventory to avoid holding a falling asset, which adds destocking *supply* on top of falling real demand, accelerating the down-move. So the inventory cycle makes the steel price overshoot in both directions relative to underlying construction. For Hoa Phat, this matters twice: once through the steel price it sells at, and once through the value of the ore and coal inventory sitting on its own books, which gets written down when input prices fall. Watching whether the chain is restocking or destocking is one of the better short-term reads on where steel prices are headed next.

#### Worked example: the inventory-value write-down

Suppose Hoa Phat carries the equivalent of two months of ore and coal inventory, and input prices fall 20% over a quarter as a demand scare unwinds the 2022-style restock. If that inventory was bought at a cost equivalent to \$300 per tonne of finished steel and is now worth \$240, the company faces a write-down of about **\$60 per tonne** on the inventory it is working through — a non-cash but very real hit to reported margin, layered on top of the lower selling price.

Across, say, 1.5 million tonnes of inventory-affected output in the quarter, that is roughly \$90 million of write-down. The intuition: a steelmaker does not just earn the *current* spread, it also wears a mark-to-market on the rocks already in its yard — falling input prices help next quarter's margin but punish this quarter's reported profit through inventory losses, which is why the stock can fall on "good" news of cheaper ore.

**6. Use HPG as the construction-cycle proxy — and know what that means.** Because Hoa Phat supplies roughly a third of Vietnam's steel, its volumes and margins are a clean read on whether the country is building. That makes HPG a powerful single-stock expression of "Vietnam is industrializing and constructing." But it also means HPG is a *pure-play on a cyclical theme* — it will lead the construction cycle up and lead it down, and it offers no shelter when the cycle rolls over. The disciplined way to own a name like this is to buy it when the cycle is hated and the furnaces are running cold, and to trim it when the headlines are euphoric and the margin spread is at its widest — the opposite of what feels comfortable, because deep-cyclical stocks are cheapest on trailing earnings exactly when those earnings are about to recover, and most expensive on trailing earnings exactly when they are about to roll over.

The through-line of this whole series applies cleanly here. A commodity price is a physical thing forced through a financial contract; for steel, the physical thing is two imported rocks and an enormous amount of energy, and the financial contract is a dollar-priced cost stack sold for dong-priced revenue into a domestic construction cycle. Hoa Phat's profit is the spread that survives that journey, leveraged by fixed costs and debt, floored by public investment, and ultimately governed by the price China sets at both ends of the chain. Read those four things — the spread, the two demand engines, the leverage, and China — and you are reading not just a stock but the industrial pulse of a country.
