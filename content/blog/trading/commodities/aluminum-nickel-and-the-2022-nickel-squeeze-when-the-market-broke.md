> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Aluminum is a bet on cheap electricity and nickel is a bet on who controls the supply chain, and in March 2022 the nickel market broke so badly that the London Metal Exchange cancelled a day of trades — proving that "the exchange can suspend you" is a real, priceable risk.
>
> - **Aluminum is congealed electricity.** Smelting one tonne eats roughly 14 megawatt-hours of power, so electricity is about 35% of the cash cost. When European power tripled in 2022, EU smelters switched off and the metal jumped to about \$2,705/tonne.
> - **Nickel comes in two grades.** Class-1 is the pure metal the battery world and the LME contract demand; class-2 is cheap nickel-in-iron for stainless steel. Indonesia banned raw-ore exports to force refiners onshore and now mines and refines over half the world's nickel.
> - **The squeeze.** A giant short (the producer Tsingshan) plus a forced wave of buying sent LME nickel from about \$25,000 to an intraday **\$101,365** on 8 March 2022. Margin calls ran to billions the brokers could not collect.
> - **The one fact to remember:** the LME halted nickel and **voided roughly \$3.9 billion of trades**, retroactively erasing the longs' realized gains to keep the short-and-broker chain solvent. The market can be suspended — price that.

On the morning of 8 March 2022, a trader who was long nickel watched his screen print a number that made no physical sense. Nickel — the dull silvery metal in your kitchen sink and the cathode of your car battery — had traded around \$25,000 a tonne for years. That morning it touched **\$101,365**. The price had more than doubled in a few hours, on top of a 70% jump the day before. Anyone who had bought even a small long position the previous week was, on paper, spectacularly rich.

And then it was taken away. The London Metal Exchange — the 145-year-old institution that sets the global reference price for nickel, copper, and aluminum — stopped trading and announced that every nickel trade executed that day would be **cancelled**, as if it had never happened. Prices were reset to the prior session's close near \$48,000. Roughly \$3.9 billion of transactions were unwound. The longs who had booked huge gains got nothing. The market stayed closed for eight days. Lawsuits followed for years.

This post is about the two metals at the center of that story — energy-hungry **aluminum** and supply-chain-contested **nickel** — and about the lesson the squeeze taught everyone who trades on a venue: the contract you hold is only as firm as the institution standing behind it. We will build both metals from the ground up, then walk the squeeze hour by hour, then end on how a trader prices the risk that the market itself can be switched off.

Why pair these two metals in one post? Because between them they teach the two great forces that move every industrial metal, and they teach them in their purest forms. Aluminum is the clearest example of a metal that is really a *cost* story — its price is dominated by one input, electricity, to a degree no other metal matches. Nickel is the clearest example of a metal that is really a *supply-chain-control* story — who mines it, who refines it, what grade is deliverable, and which government writes the export rules. Cost and control: master how those two forces work in aluminum and nickel, and you have a framework for reading copper, zinc, lead, tin, and the rest. And the 2022 squeeze is the rare event that put both forces, plus the deepest structural risk of all — that the venue can stop the game — into a single dramatic week. That is why it is worth studying in detail rather than as a curiosity.

![Timeline of the March 2022 LME nickel squeeze from a giant short to the exchange voiding trades](/imgs/blogs/aluminum-nickel-and-the-2022-nickel-squeeze-when-the-market-broke-1.png)

The spine of this whole series runs straight through this episode: **a commodity price is a physical thing forced through a financial contract.** Nickel the metal did nothing strange that week. Nickel the *contract* — a paper promise to deliver six tonnes at a future date, marked to market and margined daily by a clearing house — is what broke. The gears of the forward market (margin, settlement, the obligation to deliver) seized, and the exchange chose to grind them to a halt rather than let the chain default. Hold that distinction. It is the difference between the warehouse and the warrant, between owning metal and owning a promise.

## Foundations: the building blocks of two industrial metals

Before any drama, the basics. Both aluminum and nickel are **industrial metals** — bought to be consumed in manufacturing, not held as a store of value. That single fact separates them from gold (which gets [its own series](/blog/trading/gold/is-gold-money-a-commodity-or-a-currency-the-framing-that-decides-everything) because the framing — commodity or currency — decides everything about how it trades). Industrial metals rise and fall with factories, construction, and cars. They are, as the cliché goes, a bet on the world building things.

Both trade on the **London Metal Exchange (LME)**, the venue that prices most of the world's base metals. If you have not yet read how the LME's warehouse-and-warrant system works — the physical backbone that lets a paper contract settle in real metal — start with the sibling post on [industrial metals and the LME](/blog/trading/commodities/industrial-metals-and-the-lme-the-warehouse-that-prices-the-world). The short version you need here: the LME runs a 3-month-forward market, contracts can settle by physical delivery of warranted metal sitting in approved warehouses, and a central **clearing house** stands between buyers and sellers, collecting **margin** (a cash deposit against losses) every day. Those three features — physical delivery, daily margin, a clearing house — are exactly the gears that jammed in 2022.

Why does the LME quote a *3-month* forward as its core price rather than a simple spot price? History and physics. The exchange was founded in 1877, in the age of sail and early steam, when it took about three months for copper and tin to arrive in London from Chile and Malaya. A merchant who agreed today to deliver metal in three months wanted to lock in a price today, so the three-month forward became the natural quoting convention — and it stuck. To this day the headline LME price you see quoted is usually the 3-month forward, not spot, which is a quirk that confuses newcomers from the oil world (where the front-month future is the headline). For our purposes the key point is that an LME position is a *promise about the future*, marked to market and margined every single day until it settles or rolls. That daily mark-to-market is the mechanism that turns a paper loss into a same-day cash demand — and it is the gear that turned Tsingshan's losing hedge into an existential liquidity crisis within 48 hours.

One more structural fact worth internalizing before the drama: the LME is unusual in that it allows large positions to live partly **off-exchange**, in over-the-counter (OTC) deals struck bilaterally with banks. These OTC positions are not visible on the exchange's order book. A trader can therefore build a short far larger than anything the on-screen open interest would suggest, distributed across a handful of banks who each hold a slice and hedge it back onto the LME. This invisibility is not a footnote — it is central to why the 2022 squeeze caught the exchange by surprise. You cannot manage a risk you cannot see, and for weeks the LME could not see the true size of the short building under its own contract.

Let me define the terms a beginner will trip on:

- **Tonne** — a metric ton, 1,000 kilograms. Base-metal prices are quoted in US dollars per tonne.
- **Long / short** — *long* means you own the metal (or a contract to receive it) and profit when the price rises; *short* means you have sold metal you do not yet own (or a contract to deliver it) and profit when the price falls. A producer who has not yet mined its metal but sells futures to lock in a price is short the futures even though it is long the physical mine.
- **Hedge** — a position taken to offset a risk, not to speculate. A nickel producer that sells futures is *hedging*: if the price falls, the futures gain offsets the cheaper metal it will eventually sell.
- **Margin call** — when your position loses money, the clearing house demands more cash to cover the loss. If you cannot pay, you are in default.
- **Short squeeze** — when the price of something heavily shorted rises sharply, shorts are forced to buy to close their positions, and that buying pushes the price up further, forcing yet more shorts to buy. A feedback loop that feeds on itself.
- **Open interest** — the total number of live contracts outstanding on the exchange at any moment. It is the on-screen measure of how much positioning exists, but it does *not* include OTC positions struck off-exchange — a gap that mattered enormously in 2022.
- **Deliverable float** — the amount of metal that actually qualifies to settle the contract (LME-warranted class-1 nickel in approved warehouses). A small deliverable float relative to outstanding short positions is the kindling for a squeeze: there is not enough deliverable metal for the shorts to source, so they must buy back instead, and that buying is what runs the price.

Keep "short squeeze" in mind — it is the engine of the whole episode. Now to the metals.

### Aluminum: the most electricity-hungry metal there is

Aluminum is the second-most-used metal on Earth after iron, and it is everywhere: cans, window frames, car bodies, aircraft, power lines, foil. It is light, doesn't rust, and conducts heat and electricity well. It is also, by the standards of any other commodity, absurdly energy-intensive to make.

The chain runs in three stages, and the everyday version is worth holding in your head before the formula:

![Aluminum value chain showing bauxite to alumina to smelter to metal with power as the dominant cost](/imgs/blogs/aluminum-nickel-and-the-2022-nickel-squeeze-when-the-market-broke-4.png)

1. **Bauxite** — a reddish ore dug out of the ground, mostly in Australia, Guinea, China, and Brazil. Cheap and abundant.
2. **Alumina** — bauxite is refined into a white powder (aluminum oxide) via the Bayer process. About two tonnes of alumina make one tonne of metal.
3. **Aluminum metal** — alumina is fed into a **smelter**, where a massive electric current splits the oxygen off the aluminum (the Hall-Héroult electrolysis process). This step is where the energy goes.

Here is the number that defines aluminum economics: producing one tonne of primary aluminum consumes roughly **14 megawatt-hours (MWh)** of electricity. For scale, an average American home uses about 10–11 MWh in an entire *year*. A single tonne of aluminum eats more power than a household burns in fourteen months. A typical smelter produces hundreds of thousands of tonnes a year, so it draws power like a small city and runs flat out, 24/7, because shutting down and restarting an electrolytic cell can wreck it.

Because power is roughly 35% of the cash cost of making aluminum (more, when electricity is expensive), the price of aluminum is, to a first approximation, **a bet on the price of electricity**. This is why aluminum smelters cluster next to cheap, reliable power: Norwegian and Canadian hydro dams, Icelandic geothermal, Gulf-state subsidized gas, Chinese coal. A smelter without a cheap power contract is a money-loser the day it opens. Aluminum is, in the trader's shorthand, **congealed electricity** — a tonne of the metal is effectively a battery storing the cheap kilowatt-hours that went into making it.

There is a second, subtler reason power dominates aluminum: you cannot easily turn a smelter off. The Hall-Héroult cells are pots of molten cryolite and alumina kept at roughly 950 degrees Celsius by the electric current itself. If you cut the power for more than a few hours, the molten bath **freezes solid**, ruining the pots and forcing a months-long, multi-million-dollar reline. So a smelter cannot nimbly throttle production up and down with the power price the way a gas-fired power plant can. It runs flat out or it shuts for good. This all-or-nothing quality is why aluminum supply is *sticky on the way down* — smelters limp along at a loss for a surprisingly long time, hoping power prices fall before they have to take the irreversible step of switching off. And it is why, when European power costs finally became unbearable in 2022, the supply that came offline did not trickle back the moment prices eased: restarting a frozen pot line takes a year and a fortune, so the lost capacity stayed lost, keeping the metal tight well into 2023.

This stickiness has a market consequence worth holding onto: aluminum inventories and the forward curve carry a lot of information about *energy expectations*, not just current demand. When the market believes power will stay expensive, it expects smelter supply to stay constrained, and it prices that into the curve and into the willingness to hold metal in warehouses. The physical thing — a silvery ingot — is being forced to express a forecast about electricity prices a year out. That is the physical-meets-paper bridge in its purest single-metal form, and it is exactly the gear-logic the series spine keeps returning to.

#### Worked example: a smelter's power-cost math and its breakeven

Take a smelter that consumes 14 MWh of electricity per tonne of aluminum. Suppose its long-term power contract is at \$40 per MWh — a typical price for a hydro-backed smelter.

- Power cost per tonne = 14 MWh × \$40/MWh = **\$560/tonne**.
- Add alumina (about \$700/tonne of metal at roughly \$350/tonne alumina × 2), labor, carbon anodes, and overhead — call it another \$1,200/tonne.
- Total cash cost ≈ \$560 + \$1,200 = **\$1,760/tonne**.

At an aluminum price of \$2,400/tonne, this smelter earns about \$640/tonne — healthy. Now suppose a power crisis drives its marginal electricity cost to \$300/MWh (Europe in 2022 saw spot power far above this):

- Power cost per tonne = 14 MWh × \$300/MWh = **\$4,200/tonne** — alone.
- Total cash cost ≈ \$4,200 + \$1,200 = **\$5,400/tonne**.

The smelter would now lose roughly \$3,000 on every tonne it makes, even with aluminum at \$2,400. The rational move is to switch off — which is exactly what dozens of European smelters did in 2022. **The intuition: for an aluminum smelter the power bill is the business, so when electricity becomes unaffordable the smelter shutters and the lost supply props up the metal price.**

That is precisely what the data shows. When the 2022 European energy crisis hit — natural gas and power prices spiked after Russia invaded Ukraine and Europe's gas supply was choked — EU smelters representing roughly half of European capacity curtailed or shut. With supply cut, the metal price rose.

![LME aluminum annual average with the 2022 European power-crisis price spike](/imgs/blogs/aluminum-nickel-and-the-2022-nickel-squeeze-when-the-market-broke-3.png)

Aluminum averaged about \$1,604/tonne in 2016, when global power was cheap and metal abundant; it spiked to roughly \$2,705 in 2022 as the power crisis bled into the metal, then eased back toward \$2,250–\$2,420 as energy normalized. The price moved with the cost of the one input that dominates it. This is the cleanest example in all of commodities of a metal whose price is a derivative of an energy price — a linkage we explore more broadly in the cross-asset post on [metals as the economy's pulse](/blog/trading/cross-asset/metals-copper-silver-the-economys-pulse).

### Nickel: two metals wearing the same name

Nickel is where the story gets stranger, because "nickel" is really two different products that happen to share a chemical symbol, and the gap between them is the key to the 2022 break.

- **Class-1 nickel** is the pure stuff — at least 99.8% nickel, produced as briquettes, cathodes, or powder. It is what electric-vehicle battery makers need (in the nickel-rich cathodes that give EVs their range), what high-grade alloys need, and — critically — it is the only grade **deliverable against the LME nickel contract**. It is the scarce, expensive half of the market.
- **Class-2 nickel** is the cheap, abundant half: **nickel pig iron (NPI)** and **ferronickel**, which are nickel-in-iron alloys made directly from low-grade ore. Class-2 goes into **stainless steel**, which absorbs roughly two-thirds of all nickel demand. It is not LME-deliverable; it trades as a physical product against discounts to the LME price.

This split matters because for years the world built enormous class-2 capacity (cheap stainless feedstock) while the *tradable, deliverable* class-1 supply stayed tight. The LME price tracks class-1 scarcity, but the bulk of physical nickel flowing around the world is class-2. A short hedger producing class-2 NPI could be sitting on a mountain of physical nickel and still get crushed by a class-1 squeeze on the exchange — because what he holds is not what the contract demands he deliver.

It helps to see where the demand actually comes from, because it explains why the two grades pull in different directions. Roughly two-thirds of all nickel goes into **stainless steel**, where the nickel arrives as cheap class-2 NPI or ferronickel — this is the bulk, steady, China-and-Indonesia-driven demand. The fast-growing slice, and the one that captures headlines, is **electric-vehicle batteries**, which want nickel-rich cathode chemistries. The two main EV cathode families are NMC (nickel-manganese-cobalt) and NCA (nickel-cobalt-aluminum); both pack a lot of nickel because nickel raises a battery's energy density, which is what gives an EV more range per kilogram. A competing chemistry, LFP (lithium-iron-phosphate), uses *no* nickel at all and has been gaining share, especially in China and in cheaper cars — which is one reason the breathless "nickel will run out because of EVs" thesis kept disappointing. Battery demand needs class-1 (or class-1-equivalent), so a surge in EV sales tightens precisely the scarce, deliverable grade, while the abundant class-2 keeps stainless fed. The two grades are, increasingly, two markets.

There is a real-world subtlety the squeeze later exposed. Because the LME contract demands class-1 and the world's marginal *new* supply is class-2 (now sometimes converted to class-1 onshore in Indonesia), the **deliverable** market can be tight even when total nickel is in glut. That structural gap — abundant nickel, scarce LME-deliverable nickel — is the permanent fault line that makes nickel periodically squeeze-prone. Every commodity future has a version of this problem (the cheapest-to-deliver grade diverging from the bulk of production), but nickel's is unusually wide, and in March 2022 it cracked open.

![Class-1 versus class-2 nickel and how Indonesia onshored the value chain](/imgs/blogs/aluminum-nickel-and-the-2022-nickel-squeeze-when-the-market-broke-5.png)

Layered on top of the two-grades problem is a geopolitical one: **Indonesia**. Indonesia sits on the world's largest nickel reserves, mostly as low-grade laterite ore good for class-2. In 2014, and definitively from 2020, Indonesia **banned the export of raw nickel ore**. The logic was industrial nationalism: if you want our ore, build your smelter here. Foreign companies — overwhelmingly Chinese — poured tens of billions of dollars into Indonesian smelting and processing parks. New high-pressure acid-leach (HPAL) technology even began converting cheap class-2 feedstock into battery-grade class-1 onshore. The result: Indonesia went from a bit player to mining and refining **over half of the world's nickel supply** in under a decade. The export ban worked exactly as intended — it captured the value chain. This onshoring is the same energy-transition-metal dynamic covered in the sibling post on [battery and green metals](/blog/trading/commodities/battery-and-green-metals-lithium-cobalt-and-rare-earths), where lithium and rare earths face their own chokepoints.

#### Worked example: why a class-2 producer can be both long metal and short the contract

Take a producer — call it a stainless-steel-and-nickel giant — that mines laterite ore in Indonesia and turns it into class-2 NPI. (Figure 5 above shows the two-grade split this example rests on.) It is *physically long* nickel: it owns and produces enormous quantities of the metal-in-iron. But to lock in revenue, it sells LME nickel futures as a hedge — it is *short the LME contract*.

- Suppose it produces the equivalent of 150,000 tonnes of nickel content a year and sells, say, 100,000 tonnes of LME futures short at \$25,000/tonne to hedge.
- Its bet: it doesn't matter which way the price moves, because losses on the short futures are offset by the higher value of its physical metal, and vice versa.

The flaw: its physical metal is **class-2 NPI**, not LME-deliverable **class-1**. If the LME class-1 price spikes for a reason specific to deliverable supply — a scramble for the scarce pure metal — the producer's short futures bleed catastrophically, while its mountain of class-2 NPI cannot be delivered to close the position. **The intuition: a hedge only works if what you hold is what the contract demands; when the two diverge, the "hedge" becomes a naked, uncoverable short.** This is exactly the trap that closed on Tsingshan in March 2022.

## The squeeze, hour by hour

Now we can walk the actual break. The protagonist was **Tsingshan Holding Group**, a privately held Chinese company that is the world's largest nickel and stainless-steel producer — and the pioneer of cheap Indonesian class-2 nickel. Tsingshan held an enormous short position in LME nickel futures, reportedly on the order of 150,000+ tonnes across the LME and over-the-counter (OTC) positions with banks. The short was, in Tsingshan's mind, a hedge against its vast physical production.

The trigger came from outside the metal. On 24 February 2022, Russia invaded Ukraine. Russia is a major class-1 nickel producer (Norilsk Nickel), and the market feared sanctions would cut off Russian supply of exactly the deliverable grade the LME contract needs. Nickel — already drifting higher on EV-battery demand — started to climb.

As the price rose, Tsingshan's short lost money. Every \$1,000/tonne move against a 150,000-tonne short is \$150 million of mark-to-market loss. The clearing house and Tsingshan's brokers issued **margin calls** — demands for cash to cover the growing loss. To stop the bleeding, the natural move for a trapped short is to **buy back** the futures. But buying to cover *pushes the price up*, which deepens everyone else's short losses, which forces more buying. The squeeze had ignited.

It is worth being precise about the doom loop, because it is the same engine in every short squeeze from Volkswagen in 2008 to the meme stocks of 2021. A short who is losing money faces a margin call. To make the call stop, he buys back his short. His buying lifts the price. The higher price triggers a *larger* margin call on every other short still in the trade — and on himself, on whatever he hasn't yet covered. Those shorts now must buy too. Their buying lifts the price again. Each turn of the loop is self-reinforcing, and crucially, it has nothing to do with the metal's fundamentals. The price is no longer discovering the value of nickel; it is discovering the size of the trapped short and the depth of the panic. The loop only stops when either the shorts are all out, or the venue intervenes. In 2022 the venue intervened.

There was a second, aggravating wrinkle: Tsingshan's short was spread across roughly **ten banks** as OTC positions, plus its on-exchange position. Each bank that had sold Tsingshan a forward had hedged itself by going short the LME outright. So as the price ran, *the banks* were also receiving brutal margin calls from the LME clearing house, and they in turn were calling Tsingshan for the cash to cover. The risk was now systemic in the literal sense: not one trader's problem but a web of major banks all exposed to the same losing position through the same counterparty. If Tsingshan could not pay its banks, the banks could not pay the clearing house, and a clearing member default loomed. This is why an event that looks, from the outside, like one Chinese company's bad hedge was actually a threat to the plumbing of the entire metals market.

On Monday 7 March, nickel rose about 70% to over \$50,000. Brokers worked through the night trying to arrange the cash. On the morning of Tuesday 8 March, the forced buying went vertical: nickel doubled in a few hours, touching **\$101,365/tonne** intraday — a price with no relationship to the cost of producing or using the metal. It was pure short-covering and, almost certainly, opportunistic longs piling in to ride the squeeze. In the space of about an hour, the world's reference price for nickel ceased to mean anything about nickel.

![LME nickel annual average with the March 2022 intraday squeeze peak of 101,365 dollars](/imgs/blogs/aluminum-nickel-and-the-2022-nickel-squeeze-when-the-market-broke-2.png)

The chart says it all: against a market that averaged about \$25,800 in 2022, the intraday print of \$101,365 sits like a flare. Note the scale — the annual-average line barely lifts off the floor while the squeeze peak is near the top of the frame. That is what a market dislocation looks like: a price utterly detached from the physical reality below it.

#### Worked example: the short-squeeze loss as the price runs

Let me size the damage on a stylized 100,000-tonne short entered near the 2022 average of about \$25,834/tonne. The loss is linear in the price, but the magnitudes are staggering.

- **Marked at \$50,000:** loss = (\$50,000 − \$25,834) × 100,000 = **\$2.42 billion**.
- **Marked at \$80,000:** loss = (\$80,000 − \$25,834) × 100,000 = **\$5.42 billion**.
- **Marked at the \$101,365 peak:** loss = (\$101,365 − \$25,834) × 100,000 = **\$7.55 billion**.

Every \$1,000/tonne the price climbs adds \$100 million to the hole. The clearing house does not wait — it issues a margin call for the loss *that day*, in cash. A producer with a real business and real assets simply does not keep \$7.5 billion of spare cash lying around to post overnight. **The intuition: a short's loss is unbounded as the price runs, and because margin is collected daily in cash, the squeeze kills you through your bank account long before the trade itself is settled.**

![Mark-to-market loss on a 100,000-tonne nickel short as the price runs from 25,000 to 100,000](/imgs/blogs/aluminum-nickel-and-the-2022-nickel-squeeze-when-the-market-broke-6.png)

That curve — illustrative, but anchored to the real entry and the real \$101,365 peak — is the heart of why the squeeze became a *systemic* event rather than just one company's bad day. The margin calls were so large that Tsingshan's brokers (and through them, the LME's clearing house) faced the prospect that the short could not pay. If a clearing member can't collect from its client and can't cover the gap itself, the default cascades up into the clearing house and threatens every other participant. The LME was staring at the failure of the clearing chain.

### The cancellation, and the controversy

At about 08:15 London time on 8 March, the LME suspended nickel trading. Hours later it made the decision that would define the episode: it **cancelled all nickel trades executed that day**, reverting the official price to the 7 March close of roughly \$48,000, and kept the market closed. Trading did not resume until 16 March, with daily price limits bolted on. Roughly **\$3.9 billion** of trades were voided.

![Decision tree of the LME nickel cancellation showing who the void protected and who it cost](/imgs/blogs/aluminum-nickel-and-the-2022-nickel-squeeze-when-the-market-broke-7.png)

The decision split the market into winners and losers, and the controversy is entirely about *who* the cancellation protected. Walk the two branches:

- **If the LME had let the trades stand**, Tsingshan and its brokers would have owed the full mark-to-market loss. Some brokers might have defaulted; a clearing member could conceivably have failed, threatening the clearing house itself. But the contract would have remained sacred — a deal is a deal — and the longs who bought during the spike would have kept their (very real, very large) gains.
- **By voiding the day**, the LME rescued the shorts and the broker chain from default and kept the clearing house solvent. But it retroactively *erased the longs' realized gains*, and — more damaging long-term — it told every participant that an LME contract is **not firm**: if a powerful enough party is about to lose, the exchange might just press undo.

#### Worked example: the LME-cancellation counterfactual — what a long lost

Suppose a hedge fund bought 1,000 tonnes of LME nickel at \$40,000/tonne early on 8 March and watched it run to the \$101,365 peak, where it sold. On the screen, before the cancellation:

- Gain = (\$101,365 − \$40,000) × 1,000 = **\$61.4 million**, realized and booked.

After the LME voided the day, both the buy and the sell were erased as if they never happened. The fund's gain went from \$61.4 million to **\$0**. Worse, a fund that had been *short* into the spike and had bought to cover at \$80,000 (locking a painful but survivable loss) had that cover cancelled too — and was put back into the short at the old price, then forced to live through the post-reopening volatility again. **The intuition: the cancellation did not merely freeze the market; it reached into accounts and reversed trades that had already settled, turning realized profit into nothing — which is why "the venue can undo your trade" became, that week, a quantifiable risk rather than a theoretical one.**

The optics were brutal. The LME is owned by Hong Kong Exchanges and Clearing; the squeezed short was a major Chinese producer; the longs who lost their gains were largely Western funds and traders. Several of them — most prominently the hedge fund Elliott Management and the market-maker Jane Street — sued the LME for billions, arguing the cancellation was an unlawful taking of their property. (UK courts ultimately sided with the LME's authority to act in a disorderly market, but the reputational damage was done and reforms followed.) Whatever the legal merits, the market's takeaway was unambiguous: the rules can change mid-game.

### The aftermath: a market that took years to trust again

The damage did not end when trading resumed on 16 March. The reopening was a fiasco in its own right: the LME bolted on daily price limits to cap how far nickel could move in a session, but in the first days the price kept hitting the limit immediately and trading would halt again, because the limit was set too tight and there were no real buyers and sellers willing to meet. A market that cannot find a clearing price is not really open. Liquidity in LME nickel — the ease of getting in and out without moving the price — collapsed and stayed thin for a long time. Open interest, the total number of live contracts, fell sharply as participants who once used the contract to hedge simply stopped, unwilling to rely on a price that an exchange had shown it would override.

That loss of trust is the real, lasting cost, and it is worth dwelling on because it is the invisible price of the cancellation. A benchmark is valuable precisely because everyone agrees to be bound by it — physical metal deals worldwide are priced as "LME plus or minus a premium," and that only works if the LME number is firm and trustworthy. When the exchange voided a day of trades, it did not just hurt the specific longs; it injected doubt into every future contract. Producers, consumers, banks, and funds all had to reprice the possibility that, in the next crisis, their position might be unwound. Some quietly shifted hedging to other venues or to OTC arrangements; some simply hedged less. A benchmark that loses credibility loses liquidity, and a market with thin liquidity is *more* prone to the next squeeze, not less — a quietly vicious feedback loop the cancellation set in motion.

The regulators noticed. The UK's Financial Conduct Authority and the Bank of England launched reviews, and an independent report (commissioned by the LME from the consultancy Oliver Wyman) catalogued the failures: the exchange had been blind to the scale of the OTC short, its risk systems had not flagged the building danger, and its crisis governance was muddled in the critical hours. The reforms that followed were a direct answer to each gap: **mandatory OTC position reporting** so the exchange can see large bilateral positions; **daily price limits** as a circuit breaker (set more sensibly than the first botched attempt); and tighter rules on accountability and large-position scrutiny. In short, the LME spent the following years trying to rebuild the firmness it had spent in a single morning.

## Common misconceptions

**"The squeeze meant nickel was genuinely scarce at \$100,000."** No. The physical nickel market was tight but functioning; class-2 supply from Indonesia was abundant. The \$101,365 print reflected *forced buying by trapped shorts and opportunistic longs*, not the cost of metal. Within a year nickel was back near \$21,500 average and falling toward \$17,000 in 2024 as Indonesian supply flooded in. The squeeze price was a market-structure artifact, not a supply signal.

**"Tsingshan was a reckless speculator who got what it deserved."** Tsingshan was *hedging* its enormous physical production — a normal, prudent activity. Its error was a grade mismatch (shorting LME class-1 against physical class-2 it could not deliver) and sheer size, not gambling. The line between a hedge and a naked short is exactly the line we drew earlier: it depends on whether what you hold can settle what you owe.

**"Aluminum and nickel move together because they're both base metals."** They are driven by different forces. Aluminum is a power-cost story — it tracks electricity prices and energy crises. Nickel is a supply-chain-and-grade story — it tracks Indonesian policy, EV-battery demand, and class-1 availability. In 2022 both spiked, but for unrelated reasons (aluminum on EU power, nickel on the squeeze). Treating them as one trade is a mistake.

**"A short squeeze can't happen on a regulated exchange with margin."** The 2022 nickel squeeze happened on *the* benchmark metals exchange, with a clearing house and daily margin. Margin is what *transmits* the squeeze: it forces the trapped short to find cash daily, accelerating the forced buying. Exchange structure does not prevent squeezes — and, as nickel showed, the exchange's own response is itself a source of risk.

**"Cancelling the trades fixed the problem."** It stopped the immediate default cascade but created a worse, slower problem: a loss of trust. Volume and open interest in LME nickel collapsed and took years to recover, because no one wants to trade a contract that might be voided. The cancellation protected the clearing chain at the cost of the contract's credibility — a trade-off, not a fix.

**"Nickel is a one-way bet on the EV boom."** This thesis — buy nickel because every electric car needs it — looked obvious in 2021 and then lost money for years. Two forces undercut it. First, the no-nickel LFP battery chemistry took large market share, especially in China, blunting the per-car nickel demand. Second, and more decisively, Indonesia's onshoring flooded the world with new supply, including class-1-equivalent metal made from cheap laterite ore. Nickel fell from a 2022 average near \$25,800 toward roughly \$17,000 in 2024 even as EV sales kept rising. Demand growth means nothing if supply grows faster — a lesson that applies to nearly every "future-of-everything" commodity story.

## How it shows up in real markets

The aluminum-as-electricity and nickel-as-supply-chain stories are not historical curiosities. They are live, recurring market dynamics. Here is how each shows up.

### Aluminum tracks the energy map

Whenever and wherever power gets expensive, aluminum smelters are the canary. The 2022 European crisis is the textbook case — roughly half of EU smelting capacity curtailed, and the metal price climbed even as a recession loomed. But the pattern recurs: Chinese aluminum production is throttled in dry years when hydro power in Yunnan province falls short, and during winter pollution-control campaigns that cut coal power. A trader watching aluminum watches *power* first and metal demand second. When you see a regional energy crisis, the aluminum smelters in that region are the supply that disappears, and the global price feels it. This is the energy-to-metals transmission that the cross-asset framework calls the [energy linkage](/blog/trading/cross-asset/metals-copper-silver-the-economys-pulse) — aluminum is its purest single-metal expression.

China makes this concrete because it produces well over half the world's aluminum, and a large slice of its smelting sits in Yunnan, which leans on hydroelectric dams. In a drought year the reservoirs run low, the province rations power to its smelters, and Chinese aluminum output drops — tightening the *global* balance because China is so dominant. A trader who watches Yunnan rainfall and reservoir levels is, in effect, getting an early read on world aluminum supply months before it shows in the price. The same logic runs in reverse: a wet year refills the dams, the smelters run flat out, and the extra metal weighs on the price. The point that generalizes is that for an energy-intensive metal, the supply signal often lives in *weather and power-grid data*, not in metal-market data at all — which is why the sharpest aluminum analysts spend as much time on electricity markets and hydrology as on the metal itself. It is the cleanest case in commodities of a metal whose fundamentals you read by looking somewhere other than the metal.

The deeper point for the series spine: aluminum's forward curve and inventory behavior are dominated by **energy expectations**. If the market expects power to stay expensive, it prices in tighter future supply. The metal is a physical thing, but the contract is forced to express a *power-price forecast*. That is the physical-meets-paper bridge in one metal.

Aluminum has also given the LME its *own* slow-burning structural drama, separate from nickel but rhyming with it. After Russia invaded Ukraine, a large share of the aluminum sitting in LME-approved warehouses was Russian metal (produced by Rusal, one of the world's biggest smelters, which runs on cheap Siberian hydro power). Many Western buyers refused to take Russian metal on principle, but it remained LME-deliverable — so a trader who bought an LME aluminum contract risked being delivered metal his own customers would not touch. This split the physical market into "Russian" and "non-Russian" aluminum trading at different real-world premiums even though the LME quoted a single price. It is the same lesson the nickel grade-split taught, in a geopolitical key: the contract says you will receive *aluminum*, but what you can actually *use* depends on provenance the contract does not specify. The LME eventually restricted new Russian metal inflows in 2024, but the episode reinforced how a single benchmark price can paper over a fractured physical reality underneath.

### Nickel and the onshoring of the supply chain

Indonesia's ore ban is the template every resource-rich nation now studies. By forcing refining onshore, Indonesia turned itself from an ore exporter into the gatekeeper of global nickel. For a trader this means the single most important nickel variable is **Indonesian policy**: an export-quota change, a royalty hike, a permitting slowdown, or a new ban can move the global price more than any demand shock. The supply curve for nickel is, increasingly, a political document drafted in Jakarta.

The strategy is worth understanding because it is now being copied across commodities, from Indonesian nickel to Chinese rare earths to Zimbabwean lithium. The logic is simple and brutal: raw ore is a cheap, low-margin export, but refined metal and finished products capture far more value and create jobs and industry at home. So a country sitting on a dominant share of an ore says: you may not export the ore; you must build the refinery, the smelter, and ideally the battery plant inside our borders, and we will tax you and employ our people at every step. Indonesia executed this with unusual force — it weathered a World Trade Organization challenge from the European Union (which lost ore access) and kept the ban anyway, betting correctly that the world's appetite for nickel would force the smelters to come. They came, overwhelmingly Chinese-financed, and built an entire processing industry on Sulawesi and Halmahera islands. The result reshaped the global supply map in under a decade: Indonesia now controls a majority of mined *and* refined nickel, which means it controls the swing supply that sets the world price. When one country holds that much of a market, the commodity stops being a pure supply-demand instrument and becomes a tool of statecraft.

The grade split keeps biting, too. Because the LME contract is class-1 but the world's marginal supply is class-2-converted-to-class-1, the *deliverable* market can stay tight even as total nickel floods the market. That structural tension — abundant nickel, scarce deliverable nickel — is what makes nickel periodically squeeze-prone. It is the same scarcity-of-the-deliverable-grade problem that any commodity futures contract faces when the cheapest-to-deliver supply diverges from the bulk of production. And it cuts both ways: the very Indonesian flood that crushed the nickel price after 2022 also slowly *eroded* the class-1 tightness that made the squeeze possible, because the new HPAL plants can now turn cheap ore into deliverable metal. The market that broke in 2022 because deliverable nickel was scarce had, by 2024, the opposite problem — a glut so deep that high-cost producers elsewhere (in Australia, New Caledonia, and beyond) were shutting mines. The structural fault line did not disappear; it moved.

### The squeeze rewired how traders think about venues

After Nickelgate, "venue risk" stopped being an afterthought. Sophisticated desks now explicitly ask, before sizing a position on any exchange: *What happens if the venue intervenes?* Can it impose price limits? Halt trading? Cancel trades? Change margin requirements overnight? These were always theoretical powers in every exchange rulebook; nickel made them concrete. The LME's own reforms — daily price limits, position-reporting requirements, scrutiny of large OTC positions — were a direct response, and other exchanges quietly reviewed their own crisis playbooks.

The episode also reminded everyone that **OTC positions are invisible to the exchange**. A huge slice of Tsingshan's short lived in bilateral deals with banks, off the LME's screens. The exchange could not see the true size of the short until it was too late. This is why post-2022 reforms pushed for OTC position reporting — you cannot manage a squeeze you cannot see. For traders, the lesson is that the visible open interest on a screen may be a fraction of the real positioning, and the hidden part is exactly what can blow up.

The commodity-trading houses that finance and move physical metal — the [Glencores, Vitols, and Trafiguras](/blog/trading/finance/commodity-trading-houses-glencore-vitol-trafigura) — watched nickel closely too, because they run their own large hedges on the LME and depend utterly on the contract being firm. When the benchmark wobbles, everyone who uses it to price physical deals is exposed.

### Spotting a squeeze before it detonates

Nickel was not a bolt from the blue to everyone — a handful of sharp observers saw the setup forming. The warning signs of a building squeeze are recognizable, and they are worth committing to memory because they recur across markets:

- **A large, concentrated short in a thin contract.** The deliverable supply of LME class-1 nickel was small relative to the size of the positions stacked against it. When the float of deliverable metal is tiny and one party is short a multiple of it, the contract is a tinderbox. Ask: how big is the position relative to what could actually be delivered?
- **Backwardation steepening and warehouse stocks falling.** When the front of the curve trades at a growing premium to the back and the metal in approved warehouses is draining, the market is telling you prompt supply is scarce — exactly the condition that lets a squeeze run. (The curve-reading toolkit is in the [contango versus backwardation](/blog/trading/commodities/contango-vs-backwardation-what-the-shape-of-the-curve-means) post.)
- **A fundamental headline that scares the deliverable grade specifically.** The Russia-sanctions fear in 2022 threatened class-1 supply, not class-2. A shock that hits the *exact* grade the contract demands is far more dangerous than a general supply scare.
- **A position too big to exit quietly.** The cruelest feature of a squeeze is that the trapped party's only escape — buying back — is the very act that deepens the trap. If a short is so large that covering it would itself move the price violently, the position is, in a real sense, already a loss waiting to be realized.

None of these is a precise timing signal, and trying to *be* the predator who squeezes a known short is dangerous (you can be wrong about the size, or the venue can change the rules on you, as the nickel longs learned). But as a *defensive* checklist — am I about to take the other side of one of these setups? — it is invaluable. The point is not to play the squeeze; it is to avoid being the one squeezed, and to know that the venue's response is part of the risk you are taking on.

### This was not the first squeeze, and it will not be the last

Nickel sits in a long line of corners and squeezes, and the family resemblance is instructive. The Hunt brothers tried to corner the *silver* market in 1979–80, driving the price from about \$6 to nearly \$50 an ounce before the exchange (COMEX) changed the rules — limiting positions and demanding more margin — and the price collapsed, ruining them. In 2008, a short squeeze in *Volkswagen* shares briefly made it the most valuable company in the world when Porsche revealed it controlled far more stock than the market realized, trapping the shorts. In 2021, retail traders squeezed *GameStop* and other heavily shorted stocks, and the brokerage Robinhood controversially restricted buying — its own version of a venue intervening mid-crisis. The pattern is always the same: a heavily shorted asset, a trigger, a forced-buying loop, and ultimately a venue or regulator stepping in to change the rules — protecting one side at the expense of the other. Nickel's distinction was the scale, the systemic threat to the banks, and the bluntness of the remedy: not a margin change or a position limit, but the wholesale *erasure* of a day's trades. The lesson generalizes far beyond metals: any market with concentrated short positioning and a thin deliverable float can squeeze, and when it does, the institution running the market becomes a participant with its own survival to protect.

## The takeaway: how to think about venue and structure risk

So how should a curious investor or trader actually use all this? Three concrete lenses.

**First, read aluminum as an energy derivative.** Before you form a view on aluminum, form a view on power in the regions that smelt it — Europe, China, the Gulf, the hydro belts of Canada and Norway. A power crisis is an aluminum supply cut waiting to happen; cheap, abundant power is an aluminum glut. The cleanest signal is the energy bill, not the demand forecast. When you see European gas spike or Chinese hydro falter, mark aluminum supply down before the metal price has moved.

**Second, watch the deliverable grade, not the headline tonnage, for nickel.** Total nickel supply can be growing (Indonesia is relentless) while *LME-deliverable class-1* stays tight — and it is the deliverable grade that the contract and any squeeze key off. Track Indonesian policy as the master supply variable, and treat persistent LME backwardation (front prices above back prices — a signal of prompt scarcity, explained in the sibling post on [contango versus backwardation](/blog/trading/commodities/contango-vs-backwardation-what-the-shape-of-the-curve-means)) plus low LME warehouse stocks as the squeeze warning lights. The 2022 setup — a tight deliverable market, a giant concealed short, and a supply-shock headline — is the pattern to recognize before, not after.

**Third, and most important, price the venue itself.** The deepest lesson of Nickelgate is that the contract you hold is a promise from an institution, and institutions can break promises when their own survival is at stake. Before you size any leveraged position on any exchange, ask the questions the squeeze made unavoidable:

- Can the venue impose price limits or halt trading, trapping me in a position I cannot exit?
- Can it raise margin overnight, forcing me to find cash or be liquidated?
- Can it *cancel* trades, erasing gains I thought were realized?
- How large might the hidden OTC positioning be that I cannot see on the screen?

If the honest answer to any of these is "yes" — and for almost every venue it is — then your real risk is larger than your position size suggests, and your worst case is not a price move but an *intervention*. The practitioners who hedge tail risk with options ([protective puts, collars](/blog/trading/options-volatility/hedging-a-portfolio-with-options-protective-puts-collars-and-tail-risk)) do so partly because an option's loss is capped at the premium regardless of how the venue behaves — a structurally different exposure than a margined futures short that a squeeze and a cancellation can compound into ruin. Understanding what actually [sets an option's price](/blog/trading/options-volatility/what-sets-an-options-price-the-five-inputs-and-the-intuition) helps you weigh that capped-loss structure against the open-ended risk of the underlying.

What does pricing the venue look like in practice, concretely? It changes how you size. A trader who learned the right lesson from nickel does three things differently. First, on any contract with a thin deliverable float and a known concentrated short, he assumes his stop-loss may not fill — because a limit-up market simply does not trade, so the "I'll cut at a 15% loss" plan evaporates the moment it matters most. He therefore sizes the position as if he could lose far more than his intended stop, because in a squeeze he can. Second, he prefers structures with *defined* worst cases — a long option, a spread, a position on a venue with hard daily limits he understands — over a naked margined short whose loss is open-ended both in the price and in the venue's discretion. Third, he treats *gains* in an extreme dislocation as provisional until they are not just settled but safely behind him, because the nickel longs learned that "realized and booked" is not the same as "kept." None of this is exotic. It is simply taking seriously that the institution behind the contract is a player with its own balance sheet to defend, and adjusting size so that the venue's worst plausible action is survivable rather than fatal.

There is a final, more philosophical takeaway that the nickel episode forces on anyone who thinks hard about it. We tend to treat market prices as objective facts — *the* price of nickel was \$101,365 at 6 a.m. on 8 March. But the LME then declared, by fiat, that the price had *not* been \$101,365, that those trades never happened, that the real price was \$48,000. Both statements were "true" in the only sense that matters in a centralized market: the venue said so. A price is not a fact discovered in nature; it is a *consensus enforced by an institution*, and the institution can revise the consensus. For most traders, most of the time, this is invisible — the consensus holds, the price is firm, and you can build a whole career without noticing the institution behind the curtain. The value of a break like nickel is that it makes the curtain visible for a moment, and once you have seen it, you size accordingly forever after.

Here is the closing thought, the one that ties the metals to the spine. A commodity price is a physical thing forced through a financial contract — and the contract is only as solid as the venue enforcing it. Aluminum showed us that the metal's price is really an energy price wearing a metal's clothes. Nickel showed us that two grades sharing a name can hide a fatal mismatch between what you own and what you owe. And the squeeze showed us the deepest gear of all: that the market can be **switched off**, that trades can be **erased**, and that when the clearing chain is threatened, the institution will protect its own solvency before it protects your booked profit. That is not a reason never to trade. It is a reason to size as if the rules can change — because in March 2022, on the most established metals exchange in the world, they did.
