> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Grains are the one commodity complex where supply is a *flow set once a year*, not a tap you can open: a crop's size is fixed by spring acreage and summer weather, demand for food and feed never stops, so the price spends all year auctioning a fixed amount of calories against steady appetite.
>
> - The **big three** are **corn** (animal feed + ethanol + food), **wheat** (the global bread grain, the most politically sensitive), and **soybeans** (crushed into protein meal for animal feed + vegetable oil). Together they are the world's tradable calories.
> - A grain crop is **acreage × yield**. Acreage is decided at planting (April–May in the US); yield is decided by the weather, and most of all by the heat and rain during **pollination** in July–August. That summer window is the **weather market** — the weeks when the price is most violent.
> - Because demand is **inelastic** (people and animals must eat), a small supply shock moves price a lot. A ~15% yield loss in a US drought sent corn from the \$5–6 range to an intraday **\$8.31** in August 2012; the 2022 Russia–Ukraine war, which threatened a quarter of world wheat exports, spiked CBOT wheat to **\$12.94**.
> - The world has **two harvests**: the Northern Hemisphere (US, Black Sea) plants in spring and harvests in autumn; the Southern Hemisphere (Brazil, Argentina) plants in our autumn and harvests in our late winter. So the calendar is never far from *somebody's* weather market.
> - The one number to remember: a standard grain futures contract is **5,000 bushels**. At \$5 corn that is a \$25,000 notional bet on the size of a harvest that does not yet exist.

In the summer of 2012, the American Midwest stopped raining. By late July the corn belt — Iowa, Illinois, Indiana, Nebraska — was in the grip of the worst drought since the 1930s. The crop was in the ground; the acres had been planted back in April when the year looked normal. But corn does its single most important act of the year in mid-summer: it **pollinates**, and pollination in 35°C heat with no soil moisture simply fails. Ears that should have filled with 800 kernels filled with 400, or fewer. There was nothing any farmer could do but watch.

The futures market did the only thing it can do when supply shrinks and demand does not: it raised the price until something gave. Corn, which had traded near \$5–6 a bushel that spring, climbed through July and on **21 August 2012** touched **\$8.31** a bushel — a record. The market was not predicting the future; it was *rationing the present*. At \$8 corn, the marginal ethanol plant idles, the marginal feedlot trims its herd, the marginal export order goes to Brazil instead. The price rose until enough buyers walked away to match the smaller crop. That is what a grain price *is*: a live auction that allocates a fixed pile of calories among everyone who wants to eat, feed an animal, or burn a gallon of fuel.

This post is about that auction and the three grains that dominate it. We will define the big three and what each is actually *for*; we will build the one idea that makes grains unlike oil or copper — that supply is an annual flow you cannot speed up, so the price is a running referendum on the weather; we will walk the Northern and Southern growing calendars that give the world two harvests a year; and we will turn real shocks (2012's drought, 2022's war) into the arithmetic of why a regional weather event becomes a global price event. By the end you will read the grain calendar, a yield scare, a basis quote, and a contract ticket the way a grain trader does.

## Foundations: what a grain is, and the big three

Start from zero. A **grain** is the edible seed of a cereal or oilseed plant, grown in bulk and storable for months in a dry bin. That last property — *storable* — is what lets grain be a traded commodity at all: a perishable tomato cannot be a futures market, but a bushel of corn can sit in a silo from one autumn to the next. Grain is fungible (one bushel of US No. 2 yellow corn is interchangeable with any other), gradeable (there are official quality standards), and bulk-shippable (by truck, rail, barge, and ocean vessel). Those traits — fungible, gradeable, storable, shippable — are exactly what make any physical thing tradeable on paper, the theme of this whole series. (For the full definition, see the opener, [what is a commodity](/blog/trading/commodities/what-is-a-commodity-the-physical-asset-that-trades-on-paper).)

Three grains dominate the money and the headlines. Define each by what it *does*, because the use determines the demand:

**Corn (maize).** The workhorse. Most of the world's corn is not eaten directly by people — it is **animal feed**. A kernel of corn is mostly starch, which is dense calories, and that is exactly what a chicken, pig, or steer needs to put on weight. In the United States, a second giant buyer is the **ethanol** industry: roughly a third of the US corn crop is fermented into fuel ethanol that gets blended into gasoline. A smaller slice becomes food and industrial products (corn syrup, starch, oil). So corn demand has three legs — feed, fuel, food — and the feed and fuel legs make corn a bet on *livestock economics and energy policy* as much as on dinner plates. The US is the largest corn producer and exporter; corn futures trade on the Chicago Board of Trade (CBOT), now part of CME.

**Wheat.** The bread grain. Unlike corn, most wheat *is* eaten by humans, directly, as flour: bread, noodles, pasta, flatbread. That single fact makes wheat the most **politically sensitive** of all grains. When the price of wheat doubles, the price of bread doubles, and bread is the staple that governments watch most nervously — bread shortages have toppled regimes. Wheat is also the most *geographically spread* grain: it grows in temperate zones across the US, Canada, the European Union, Russia, Ukraine, Australia, and Argentina, and there are many distinct classes (hard red winter, soft red winter, spring wheat, durum) with different protein contents and uses. The benchmark US contract is CBOT soft red winter (SRW) wheat, but the global wheat price is really set at the margin by the cheapest large exporter — for the last decade, that has often been Russia and the wider **Black Sea** region.

**Soybeans.** Not really a grain at all — botanically it is a legume, an **oilseed** — but it trades in the same complex and on the same calendar, so the market lumps it in. A soybean is valuable for two products you get by crushing it: about 80% of the bean's weight becomes **soybean meal**, a high-protein animal feed, and about 18–19% becomes **soybean oil**, the world's second-most-consumed vegetable oil. So soybeans are fundamentally a bet on **protein demand** (meal feeds the world's chickens, pigs, and farmed fish) and **vegetable-oil demand** (cooking oil, and increasingly biodiesel). China is the dominant importer — it buys soybeans, crushes them at home, and feeds the meal to the world's largest pig herd. The split of the bean's value between meal and oil is its own tradeable spread, the **crush**, which gets a dedicated post: [the crush and the spread](/blog/trading/commodities/the-crush-and-the-spread-soybeans-into-meal-and-oil).

Put the three together and you have the **grain complex** — corn, wheat, soybeans — the calories and protein that feed the planet, all trading on the same Chicago exchange, all priced in **US cents or dollars per bushel**, all moving partly together (they compete for the same Midwest acres and the same weather) and partly apart (different uses, different geographies, different shocks).

It is worth dwelling on *why* these three, and not, say, rice or barley, dominate the financial market. Rice is the world's other great staple — more people get their calories from rice than from wheat — but most rice is grown and eaten within the same countries, very little crosses borders, and there is no deep, liquid global rice futures market on the scale of Chicago corn. **Tradeability requires a thick traded margin**, and corn, wheat, and soybeans have it: huge volumes move internationally, the US and a handful of other exporters supply a global buyer base, and the CBOT contracts have a century of liquidity behind them. So the "calories that trade" are specifically these three, because they are the ones the world ships in bulk and prices on a screen.

There is a hierarchy of substitution among them that a trader must hold in mind. Corn and soybeans are **direct competitors for the same acres** in the US Midwest — a farmer with a field in Iowa chooses, each spring, whether to plant corn or soybeans based on the relative price (the **corn–soybean ratio**, often around 2.2–2.5 bushels of corn per bushel of soybeans at planting parity). They are also partial substitutes in animal feed (both supply energy and, via soy meal, protein), so their prices move together. Wheat competes for acres with corn in some regions and is a feed substitute when it gets cheap enough relative to corn (livestock can eat feed wheat), which puts a soft ceiling on how far corn can rise above wheat. These cross-links mean a shock to one grain ripples into the other two, which is why the spikes in the chart later in this post line up across all three.

#### Worked example: what a bushel is, and what a contract is worth

A **bushel** is an old unit of dry volume, but in grain trading it is standardized by weight per crop: a bushel of corn or soybeans is **56 pounds**, a bushel of wheat is **60 pounds**. Prices are quoted in dollars (or cents) per bushel. A standard CBOT grain futures contract is **5,000 bushels**.

So the notional value of one contract is just `5,000 × price`:

- One corn contract at \$4.20/bu = `5,000 × \$4.20 =` **\$21,000**.
- One corn contract at \$7.00/bu = `5,000 × \$7.00 =` **\$35,000**.
- One soybean contract at \$13.00/bu = `5,000 × \$13.00 =` **\$65,000**.
- One CBOT wheat contract at \$6.00/bu = `5,000 × \$6.00 =` **\$30,000**.

The smallest price tick is a quarter-cent (\$0.0025) per bushel, which on 5,000 bushels is `5,000 × \$0.0025 =` **\$12.50** per tick. So a one-cent move in corn (four ticks) is `5,000 × \$0.01 =` **\$50** per contract. The lesson: a grain contract is a 5,000-bushel claim on a harvest, and at \$4 vs \$7 corn the very same contract is a \$21,000 or a \$35,000 bet — the leverage on the size of a crop that does not exist yet is enormous.

## The one idea: supply is an annual flow, demand never stops

Here is the single fact that makes grains behave unlike every other commodity in this series. Re-read the spine of the series — a commodity price is a physical thing forced through a financial contract — and then notice what is different about grains: for oil or copper, when the price rises, producers can *make more of the physical thing* on a timescale of weeks to months. A shale well can be drilled and fracked in weeks (see [the shale revolution](/blog/trading/commodities/the-shale-revolution-how-the-us-became-the-swing-producer)); a copper mine can run an extra shift. Supply is, to a useful approximation, a **tap** that responds to price.

A grain crop is not a tap. It is a **once-a-year flow**, and its size is fixed by two numbers locked in months before anyone knows what the price will be:

> **Crop size = acreage × yield.**

**Acreage** — how many acres get planted with this crop — is decided at planting time. In the US corn belt that is April and May. Once the seed is in the ground, the acreage is set; you cannot plant more corn in July because the price went up. **Yield** — how many bushels each acre produces — is decided by the weather over the growing season, and overwhelmingly by the weather during the few weeks of **pollination** (for corn, when the plant sets its kernels; mid-July to early August in the US). A hot, dry pollination window permanently caps the yield no matter how much it rains in September.

So by Labor Day, the size of the US corn crop is essentially *known and fixed*. Nothing anyone does between September and next April can make it bigger. Meanwhile, **demand never stops**: every day of the year, animals eat, ethanol plants run, mills grind flour, families buy bread. And that demand is **inelastic** — a fancy word for a simple truth: people and animals must eat roughly the same amount whether grain is cheap or expensive. You do not buy twice as much bread because wheat got cheap, and you do not stop feeding your pigs because corn got dear; you grumble and pay.

The combination — a fixed annual supply meeting steady, inelastic demand — is what turns the grain price into a **running auction**. Across the whole year, the market's job is to ration a fixed pile of calories among all the hungry buyers, and the only tool it has is price. The figure below contrasts the two worlds: the responsive supply tap of oil and metal against the fixed, weather-set flow of grain.

![Before and after comparison contrasting oil and metal supply that can respond to price against a fixed annual grain crop facing steady demand](/imgs/blogs/grains-corn-wheat-and-soybeans-the-calories-that-trade-7.png)

This is why **inelasticity is the amplifier**. When supply can respond, a 10% shortfall pulls in a bit more supply and the price barely moves. When supply *cannot* respond until next year and demand barely budges, the price has to do *all* the work of clearing the market — so a small physical shortfall becomes a large price move. The grain market is a machine for converting a few percent of missing bushels into a doubling of price, precisely because nobody can grow more until next harvest and nobody can easily eat less.

There is one release valve, and it is worth naming because it is the only thing standing between a small crop and a famine-grade price: **carryover stocks**. At any moment, the world is not living hand-to-mouth on this year's crop alone — there is grain in storage left over from prior years, the **carryover**. When a harvest comes up short, the market does not have to ration this year's smaller crop against a full year's demand; it can dip into the carryover to soften the blow. That is why the single most important number on the USDA's balance sheet is **ending stocks** — how much grain will be left in the bin when the next harvest arrives — usually expressed relative to annual use as the **stocks-to-use ratio**. A high stocks-to-use ratio (a fat carryover buffer) means a bad harvest can be absorbed with a shrug; a low one means there is no cushion, and a bad harvest detonates. The 2012 spike was so violent partly because stocks were already thin going in; there was no buffer to soak up the drought.

Notice what this does to the demand side. Demand is *not* perfectly fixed — it has a small, grudging elasticity, and the channels matter. When corn gets expensive, the *first* demand to give way is the most price-sensitive use: discretionary feeding and the marginal ethanol plant (which idles when corn is dear and ethanol margins go negative). Exports are next — a high US price sends buyers shopping in Brazil or Argentina, so US export demand falls as the price rises. The *last* demand to give way is human food and the protein people will not give up. So the market rations from the bottom of the priority stack upward: fuel and discretionary feed get cut first, exports redirect, and only an extreme price reaches into the food on the table. Watching *which* demand is being destroyed tells a trader how high up the rationing ladder the price has climbed.

#### Worked example: a drought yield shock with inelastic demand

Suppose a normal US corn year produces about **15 billion bushels**, and a hot, dry pollination window cuts the national yield by **15%**. The crop falls to `15 × (1 − 0.15) =` **12.75 billion bushels** — a loss of 2.25 billion bushels, about 15% of supply.

Now bring in **demand elasticity**. Economists estimate the short-run price elasticity of corn demand is roughly **−0.2** — meaning a 1% rise in price reduces the quantity demanded by only about 0.2%. To clear the market, the quantity demanded has to fall by the full 15% (since that is all the supply there is). Using the elasticity, the required price change is approximately:

`price change ≈ quantity change ÷ elasticity = (−15%) ÷ (−0.2) =` **+75%**.

So a 15% yield loss implies roughly a **75% price rise** — corn from \$5 toward \$8–9. That is almost exactly what happened in 2012: a roughly 13–15% yield miss drove the price from the \$5–6 range to an intraday \$8.31. The intuition to carry away: with inelastic demand, divide the percentage shortfall by the (small) elasticity, and you see why a "modest" 15% crop loss detonates into a price spike — the price must rise far enough to *destroy* exactly as much demand as the weather destroyed supply.

## The growing-season calendar and the weather market

If supply is fixed once a year, then the grain market lives or dies on a **calendar**, and a grain trader's whole year is organized around it. Walk the Northern Hemisphere corn cycle, because every other grain rhymes with it.

**Pre-season (winter–early spring).** Old-crop grain from last autumn's harvest is still in storage, being fed, milled, and exported. The market trades the *known* supply against steady demand, plus the first guesses about how many acres farmers will plant. The USDA's late-March **Prospective Plantings** report is the first hard read on acreage intentions and can move the market sharply.

**Planting (April–May).** Farmers put the seed in the ground. The market watches the *pace* of planting: a cold, wet spring that delays planting raises the risk that some acres go in late (and yield less) or get switched to a different crop. Acreage is being locked in. This is when the crop's *potential* size is set.

**The weather market (June–August).** This is the dangerous part. The corn plant grows, then **pollinates** (tassels and silks, mid-July to early August), then fills its kernels. Pollination is the single most weather-sensitive moment of the year: a stretch of extreme heat and drought during the pollination window can permanently cap yield. Because the crop's fate is being decided *right now* and is *irreversible*, the market trades every weather forecast, every rain map, every heat ridge. A two-week dry spell in July can move the price more than anything else all year. Traders call this stretch the **weather market**, and it is when grain volatility peaks. The cover figure marks exactly this: price risk through the year is low at planting (acreage is just a guess), rises into summer, **peaks at pollination**, and falls away once the crop is made.

**Harvest (September–November).** The combines roll. The crop's size becomes *known* — and a curious thing happens: once the harvest is in, the biggest uncertainty of the year is resolved, so volatility usually *falls*. The market shifts from trading the *weather* to trading **demand and exports** — how fast the known pile gets used up. The price often makes its seasonal low around harvest, when supply is most abundant and bins are overflowing (the so-called "harvest low").

**Storage and rationing (December–next spring).** The crop is in the bin. From here until next harvest, the market's job is to ration that fixed pile. If the crop was big, the price sags and the market encourages use (more exports, more feeding). If the crop was small, the price stays high to slow demand down so the supply lasts until the next harvest. The forward curve and storage economics — covered in [the forward curve](/blog/trading/commodities/the-forward-curve-the-most-important-chart-in-commodities) and [convenience yield and the cost of carry](/blog/trading/commodities/convenience-yield-and-the-cost-of-carry-why-the-curve-has-a-shape) — govern who profits from holding grain through the year.

The key insight for a trader: **price risk is not spread evenly through the year — it is concentrated in the pollination window.** A grain options trader will pay up for July volatility and sell cheap January volatility, because that is where the *information* arrives. The size of next year's crop is a sequence of coin flips that all land in about six weeks of summer.

The mechanics of *why* pollination is the hinge are worth knowing, because they explain the asymmetry of the price response. A corn plant sets its yield potential in two steps. First, around the time the plant tassels, it determines the *number of kernels per ear* — and this is fixed during a roughly two-week window. Extreme heat (above ~35°C) and dry soil during this window cause the silks to dry out and the pollen to die, so kernels never form. Once an ear has fewer kernel sites, *no amount of later rain can add them back* — the loss is permanent. Second, during the weeks after pollination, the plant fills those kernels with starch (the "grain fill" period), and good late-summer weather can make each kernel heavier. So a drought *before and during* pollination is catastrophic and irreversible; a drought *after* is merely bad. This is why the market hangs on the late-July forecast above all others, and why the price response to weather is asymmetric: a heat ridge during pollination can spike the price violently, but a timely rain *after* the kernels are already lost cannot bring the crop back, so the price does not symmetrically collapse. Yield damage is a ratchet.

Wheat and soybeans have their own critical windows. Winter wheat (planted in autumn, harvested early summer) is most vulnerable to a hard freeze without snow cover in late winter and to drought during its spring growth; spring wheat rhymes with corn. Soybeans set their yield later than corn — their critical window is **August**, during pod-fill — which is why a hot, dry August can hurt soybeans even after the corn crop is largely made. A grain trader therefore watches a *staggered* sequence of weather windows through the summer, not a single date.

#### Worked example: a farmer's revenue per acre at \$4 vs \$7 corn

Put yourself in the farmer's boots, because the farmer's economics drive the acreage decision that sets next year's supply. Suppose an Iowa farmer expects a yield of **200 bushels per acre** — a good modern corn yield. The farmer's revenue per acre is simply `yield × price`:

- At **\$4.00/bu** corn: `200 × \$4.00 =` **\$800 per acre**.
- At **\$7.00/bu** corn: `200 × \$7.00 =` **\$1,400 per acre**.

Now suppose the all-in cost to grow an acre of corn — seed, fertilizer, fuel, chemicals, machinery, and cash rent on the land — is about **\$900 per acre**. At \$4 corn the farmer *loses* \$100 an acre; at \$7 corn the farmer makes \$500 an acre. That gap is the engine of the whole supply cycle: high prices make farmers want to plant every spare acre with corn (and bid up rent and switch acres away from soybeans), which sets up a bigger crop next year — which, if the weather cooperates, pushes the price back down. The takeaway: a grain price is also a *planting signal*, and the farmer's revenue-per-acre math is the mechanism that turns this year's high price into next year's glut.

## Two hemispheres, two harvests

Everything above describes the *Northern* Hemisphere — the US, Canada, the EU, the Black Sea, China — where spring is April–May and harvest is September–November. But grain grows on both halves of the planet, and the Southern Hemisphere's seasons are flipped six months. **Brazil and Argentina** — now giants in soybeans and corn — plant in *our* autumn (October–December) and harvest in *our* late winter and early spring (February–May).

So the world has **two harvests a year**, half a year apart, and the global grain calendar is never far from *somebody's* weather market. When the US crop is being decided in a July heat ridge, the South American crop is safely in the bin; when the South American crop is sweating through a January drought, the US bins are full. The figure traces both clocks.

![Two-hemisphere harvest clock showing the US growing season offset six months from the Brazil and Argentina season](/imgs/blogs/grains-corn-wheat-and-soybeans-the-calories-that-trade-4.png)

This two-harvest structure has three big consequences a trader must internalize.

**First, the world has a partial buffer.** Because supply arrives in two pulses six months apart, a bad harvest in one hemisphere can be partly offset by a good one in the other. A US drought is less catastrophic for *global* supply if Brazil just had a record soybean crop. The two hemispheres are imperfect substitutes (different crops dominate in each, and logistics differ), but they smooth the global flow.

**Second, the "weather market" never fully sleeps.** A dedicated grain trader watches the US summer (June–August) *and* the South American summer (December–February). The South American weather market — especially Argentine and southern-Brazilian dryness in January — can move Chicago prices just as hard as a Midwest drought, because Brazil is now the world's largest soybean exporter and a top corn exporter. The 2023–24 season saw exactly this: weather scares in Brazil's *safrinha* (second-crop) corn moved the global price.

**Third, the calendar creates seasonal patterns in volatility and in the curve.** The market tends to build a weather-risk premium into the summer contract months of *whichever* hemisphere is growing, and that premium bleeds out once the crop is safely made. This is why grain futures have a pronounced seasonal shape: the new-crop contract (the first one harvested from the crop now being grown) trades differently from the old-crop contract still drawing down last year's supply.

There is a fourth consequence that has reshaped the market over the past two decades: **Brazil's rise has roughly synchronized two crops in one year on the same land**. Brazilian farmers plant soybeans in October–November, harvest them in February–March, and then immediately plant a *second* corn crop — the **safrinha** ("little harvest") — on the same fields, harvesting it in June–July. So Brazil now produces a soybean crop *and* a corn crop on an offset calendar, and the safrinha corn has grown so large that it competes directly with US corn exports in the second half of the year. A January–February drought in central Brazil now threatens *both* the soybean harvest being gathered and the safrinha corn being planted, which is why South American weather in our winter can move Chicago as hard as a US summer drought. The world's two-harvest structure used to mean "US in autumn, South America in spring"; it now means a near-continuous stream of crop-weather risk somewhere on the planet, which is why a serious grain desk is staffed and watching forecasts essentially year-round.

For wheat, add a third growing pole: the **Black Sea** (Russia, Ukraine, Kazakhstan), which became the swing exporter of the world wheat market over the 2010s. Russia's wheat harvest in July–August and its export pace through autumn now set the floor for the global wheat price — which is exactly why a war in that region became a global food shock. Australia and Argentina add Southern-Hemisphere wheat harvests in our late spring (November–December), so even wheat, the temperate grain, has a staggered global supply calendar that a trader must track origin by origin.

## The big three on a chart, and the two great shocks

Look at the three grains together over the last fifteen years and the structure of the market jumps out: long stretches of low, range-bound prices punctuated by violent spikes, and the spikes line up across all three grains because they share acres, weather, and macro forces.

![Line chart of corn, wheat, and soybean annual average prices from 2008 to 2024 with the 2012 drought and 2022 war marked](/imgs/blogs/grains-corn-wheat-and-soybeans-the-calories-that-trade-2.png)

Two episodes stand out, and both are the same lesson told twice: a regional supply shock, meeting inelastic global demand, becomes a global price event.

**2012 — the Midwest drought (a yield shock).** As described in the opening, a once-in-a-generation US drought hit during pollination. The corn crop's yield fell about 13–15% below trend. Because the US was then the dominant corn exporter and demand is inelastic, the shortfall could not be sourced elsewhere fast enough, and the price had to ration the smaller pile. Corn touched an intraday **\$8.31** in August 2012; soybeans, hit by the same drought, made their own record near \$17–18 intraday. The figure below isolates corn and marks both the cheap pre-ethanol era (corn near \$2 in 2005) and the 2012 drought peak.

![Corn annual average price from 2005 to 2025 with the August 2012 drought intraday peak marked](/imgs/blogs/grains-corn-wheat-and-soybeans-the-calories-that-trade-3.png)

**2022 — the Black Sea war (an export shock).** On 24 February 2022, Russia invaded Ukraine. Together, Russia and Ukraine accounted for roughly **a quarter of world wheat exports** and a large share of corn and sunflower oil. Overnight, the market had to price the possibility that Ukrainian ports would be blockaded (they largely were) and that Russian exports would be sanctioned or disrupted. This was not a *yield* shock — the wheat was growing fine — it was an *export* shock: the physical grain existed but might not be able to reach the buyers who needed it. CBOT wheat spiked to an intraday **\$12.94** in March 2022, and corn and soybeans rallied alongside it. The figure below isolates wheat and marks the spike against the cheap-glut years of the mid-2010s.

![Wheat annual average price from 2008 to 2024 with the March 2022 Black Sea war intraday peak marked](/imgs/blogs/grains-corn-wheat-and-soybeans-the-calories-that-trade-5.png)

The contrast between the two shocks is itself the lesson. 2012 was a **production** shock (the grain was never grown); 2022 was a **logistics/political** shock (the grain existed but could not move). Both produced near-identical price action, because the market does not care *why* the calories are unavailable — it only cares that they are, and it rations with price either way. A grain trader watches *both* channels: the weather (does the crop get grown?) and the politics of export (can the crop get to market?). The export channel — export bans, government hoarding, blockades — gets its own post: [food security, export bans, and when governments hoard](/blog/trading/commodities/food-security-export-bans-and-when-governments-hoard). For how an unscheduled geopolitical shock transmits across markets in real time, see [geopolitics, elections, and unscheduled shocks](/blog/trading/event-trading/geopolitics-elections-and-unscheduled-shocks).

#### Worked example: why a 25% export disruption moves the price more than 25%

In 2022, the threat was to roughly **25% of world wheat exports**. Naively you might expect a 25% price move. But trade math compounds it. World wheat *production* is large, but only about 25% of it crosses borders — most wheat is eaten in the country that grows it. The traded, exportable pool is the thin margin that the world price is actually set on.

Suppose global wheat exports are about **200 million tonnes**, and the war puts **50 million tonnes** (25%) at risk. The importers who depend on Black Sea wheat — Egypt, Turkey, North Africa, parts of Asia — *must* buy from somewhere, and the only somewhere is the other exporters (US, EU, Australia, Argentina), who have limited spare supply. The price of the *remaining* exportable wheat has to rise enough to (a) ration the scramble of importers and (b) coax a little more wheat out of every other origin and out of domestic-use piles. With the exportable pool inelastic in the short run, removing a quarter of it can lift the marginal price 50% or more — which is why CBOT wheat nearly doubled from its pre-war level intraday. The intuition: price moves are set on the *thin traded margin*, not the big production total, so a shock to a quarter of *trade* hits far harder than a shock to a quarter of *production*.

## Bushels, contracts, and basis: how the price actually reaches a farmer

So far we have talked about *the* corn price as if there were one number. There are really two prices for every farmer, and the gap between them is one of the most practical concepts in grain trading: **basis**.

The **Chicago futures price** is the global, standardized reference — the price for a generic 5,000-bushel contract of No. 2 yellow corn, deliverable at official delivery points, at a future month. But a farmer in western Iowa is not selling a futures contract; she is selling *actual corn* at *her local elevator* (the grain-buying facility down the road), *today*. The price the local elevator pays her is the **cash price**, and it differs from the Chicago futures price by a local adjustment called the **basis**:

> **Cash price = Futures price + Basis.**

Basis captures everything local that the standardized futures price cannot: the cost of transporting grain from her farm to where it is ultimately needed (a barge terminal, an export port, a feedlot), the local supply-and-demand balance (is there an ethanol plant nearby bidding hard for corn?), local storage availability, and grain quality. Basis is usually *negative* in the farm belt (the local price is below Chicago, because the grain still has to be shipped from the interior to the consumer), and *positive* near a consumption point like an export terminal or a livestock-heavy region.

Basis matters enormously in practice because it is the part of the price that the futures hedge does *not* cover. A farmer who sells futures to lock in a price has locked the *Chicago* component but is still exposed to her *local basis* moving. A grain merchandiser's entire business is, in large part, trading basis — buying grain at a wide (cheap) basis in the country and selling it at a narrow (rich) basis at the destination, capturing the spread for moving and storing it.

#### Worked example: a basis calculation

It is harvest, and an Iowa farmer wants to know what her local elevator will pay. She checks the screen: December CBOT corn futures are trading at **\$4.50** per bushel. Her local elevator quotes a basis of **"−35 cents"** (that is, 35 cents *under* Chicago). The cash price she receives is:

`Cash = Futures + Basis = \$4.50 + (−\$0.35) =` **\$4.15 per bushel**.

If she has 50,000 bushels to sell, her gross revenue is `50,000 × \$4.15 =` **\$207,500**.

Now suppose she instead *stores* the corn and sells in March, when she expects the basis to *narrow* (improve) to **−15 cents** as harvest pressure fades and the local elevator needs grain, while futures are flat at \$4.50. Her March cash price would be `\$4.50 + (−\$0.15) =` **\$4.35** — 20 cents better, or `50,000 × \$0.20 =` **\$10,000** more, *before* subtracting the cost of storing the grain for the extra months. The lesson: a grain price quote is two numbers, futures plus basis, and a large part of a farmer's and a merchandiser's skill is timing the basis, not just the flat price.

#### Worked example: the storage carry — does the curve pay you to hold grain?

Grain is storable, so the **shape of the forward curve** tells you whether the market is paying you to hold it. Suppose at harvest the December corn futures contract is \$4.50 and the July contract (seven months later) is \$4.80. The market is offering you **30 cents per bushel** to carry grain from December to July. Is it worth it?

Your cost of carrying grain is mostly **storage** (roughly 3–5 cents per bushel per month in a commercial elevator) plus **the interest** on the money tied up in the grain. At, say, 4 cents/bushel/month storage over seven months that is `7 × \$0.04 =` **\$0.28**, plus interest on \$4.50 at 5% for seven months ≈ `\$4.50 × 0.05 × (7÷12) =` **\$0.13**, for a total carry cost of about **\$0.41**. The curve is paying you 30 cents to incur 41 cents of cost — so a *pure* storage trade loses about 11 cents.

When the curve pays *less* than full carry like this, it is signalling that grain is relatively tight — holders are being penalized for storing, which nudges grain out of the bin and into use. When the curve pays *more* than full carry (a steep **contango**), it is signalling a glut, and storage is profitable. This is the exact storage-and-convenience-yield machinery of the whole series, applied to grain — see [the forward curve](/blog/trading/commodities/the-forward-curve-the-most-important-chart-in-commodities) and [convenience yield and the cost of carry](/blog/trading/commodities/convenience-yield-and-the-cost-of-carry-why-the-curve-has-a-shape). The takeaway: the grain curve is not random — it is the market's running offer to pay (or penalize) the people who hold the world's calories between harvests.

## The acreage-price feedback: high prices sow the next glut

We have met every piece of the puzzle; now connect them into the cycle that governs grain prices over the *years*, not just within a year. It is a self-correcting feedback loop with a one-year lag, and understanding it is what separates a grain trader from a tourist.

![Acyclic pipeline showing high price leading to more acreage then a bigger crop then more supply then a lower price](/imgs/blogs/grains-corn-wheat-and-soybeans-the-calories-that-trade-6.png)

Trace the loop. A **high price** this year (say, after a drought) sends a loud signal to every farmer in the world: *plant this crop next spring*. Farmers respond — they shift acres from a cheaper crop (soybeans, say) into the expensive one (corn), they plant marginal land that was idle, and globally, farmers in Brazil and Ukraine and Australia respond to the same price signal. **Acreage rises.** If the *next* season's weather is merely normal, the bigger acreage times a normal yield produces a **bigger crop**. The extra supply floods into storage, ending stocks rebuild, and the **price falls back** — often overshooting to a glut, because everyone planted at once. Then low prices send the opposite signal (plant *less*), acreage falls, and the cycle eventually swings back.

This is why grains spend most of their time *cheap and range-bound* and only occasionally spike: the spikes are weather or war shocks, and the system's response — more acres next year — is built to push the price back down. The 2012 spike was followed by a record-large 2013–2014 crop and a multi-year price slump into 2016, exactly as the loop predicts. The 2022 spike was likewise followed by a sharp retreat as the Black Sea kept exporting (through a grain corridor) and high prices pulled in more global acreage.

The trader's lesson: a grain price spike is *self-limiting* on a one-year horizon, because it plants the seeds (literally) of its own reversal. The dangerous regime is **back-to-back bad years** — two consecutive droughts, or a drought followed by a war — because then the acreage response never gets a chance to rebuild stocks, and the price stays high for longer. The state of **ending stocks** (how much grain is left in the bin when the next harvest arrives), often expressed as the **stocks-to-use ratio**, is the single best gauge of how much buffer the system has. Low stocks-to-use means a small shock has nowhere to hide and the price will be jumpy; high stocks-to-use means the market can absorb a bad harvest with a shrug.

#### Worked example: how a price spike pays for extra acreage

Recall the farmer's economics: at \$4 corn she lost \$100/acre, at \$7 corn she made \$500/acre. Now scale that to the whole country. Suppose US corn acreage is normally about **90 million acres**. A spike to \$7 corn makes corn far more profitable than soybeans, so farmers shift, say, **4 million acres** from soybeans into corn, lifting corn acreage to **94 million**.

At a normal yield of **180 bu/acre**, those 4 million extra acres add `4,000,000 × 180 =` **720 million bushels** to the crop — roughly a 5% increase in supply. Plug that into our elasticity rule of thumb (a 5% supply *increase* against demand elasticity ≈ −0.2 implies a price *fall* of about `5% ÷ 0.2 =` **25%**). So the very price spike that made corn profitable triggers an acreage response that, with normal weather, knocks the price back down by roughly a quarter the following year. The intuition: the grain market's high prices contain their own antidote — they bid acres away from competing crops, and the resulting supply bulge is what ends the rally.

## Common misconceptions

**"Grain prices reflect how much grain exists in the world."** Mostly wrong, in the short run. Grain prices are set on the **thin traded margin** and on **expectations of the upcoming crop**, not on the total stockpile. The world can be awash in wheat and still see the price spike if 25% of *exports* (a much smaller number than production) is threatened, as in 2022. Watch the **exportable surplus** and **ending stocks**, not the gross production headline.

**"A bumper US harvest means low prices, period."** Not necessarily. Price is global. A record US corn crop can coincide with high prices if South America had a drought, if a competing crop failed, or if demand (ethanol mandates, Chinese hog rebuilding) surged. The US crop is one input to a global balance sheet. In 2021, US harvests were fine, yet prices rallied hard on global demand and tight stocks.

**"The annual average price tells you what happened."** Dangerously incomplete. The annual *average* corn price in 2012 was about \$6.89 — but the intraday *peak* was \$8.31, and the *path* (a calm spring, a terrifying July, a slow grind down) is where all the trading risk and opportunity lived. Grains are a market where the *seasonal path* matters more than the annual average; the averages in this post's charts smooth out exactly the volatility a trader cares about. Always look at the contract-month and intra-year detail, not just the yearly mean.

**"Soybeans are a grain."** Botanically false (it is an oilseed/legume), and the distinction matters: soybeans are valued for **protein meal and oil**, not starch calories, so soybean demand tracks *animal protein and vegetable-oil* consumption (and China's hog herd) rather than the feed-and-fuel story that drives corn. They share a calendar and an exchange, but a different demand engine.

**"Ethanol is a small footnote in corn."** No — in the US it is roughly a *third* of the entire corn crop. That makes US corn prices partly a function of **gasoline demand and biofuel policy**, not just food and feed. A change in the ethanol blending mandate or a collapse in driving (as in 2020) is a corn-demand event. Corn is, in part, an energy commodity.

## How it shows up in real markets

**The annual rhythm of a grain desk.** A grain trader's year is a fixed liturgy of USDA reports and weather windows. The cycle starts in late winter with the **Outlook Forum** and acreage-intentions guesses; the late-March **Prospective Plantings** report sets the acreage baseline; the spring is about planting progress (watched in the weekly **Crop Progress** reports); June 30 brings the **Acreage** report (actual planted acres); then comes the white-knuckle **July–August weather market**; the autumn brings harvest and the big monthly **WASDE** yield revisions; and winter shifts attention to the Southern Hemisphere crop and export demand. The monthly **WASDE** (World Agricultural Supply and Demand Estimates) is the market's official scorecard — the USDA's balance sheet of supply, demand, and ending stocks for every major crop — and a surprising number on it can move grains limit-up or limit-down in a single tick. The WASDE and the broader report-trading playbook get a dedicated post: [weather, the WASDE, and the supply shock](/blog/trading/commodities/weather-the-wasde-and-the-supply-shock-trading-the-report).

**2012: a textbook yield shock.** The drought was a regional US weather event that became a global food-price event. World food-price indices spiked, import bills jumped for food-deficit nations, and the episode fed into the broader 2010–2012 stretch of high agricultural prices that contributed to social unrest in several countries. The mechanism was exactly the inelastic-demand amplifier: a ~15% US yield loss in the world's dominant corn exporter could not be sourced elsewhere fast enough, so the price did the rationing.

**2022: a textbook export shock.** The Black Sea war showed that grain is a *strategic* commodity, not just a financial one. Beyond the price spike, the episode triggered export restrictions elsewhere (India banned wheat exports in May 2022 to protect domestic supply), a UN-brokered grain corridor to let Ukrainian grain out, and a scramble by import-dependent nations to secure supply. It is the clearest modern illustration of why wheat is the most politically sensitive grain — and why food security sits at the intersection of commodity markets and national policy. The cross-asset view of the agricultural complex, including how grains sit alongside softs and fibers in an allocator's portfolio, is in [agriculture and softs, the food and fiber markets](/blog/trading/cross-asset/agriculture-softs-the-food-and-fiber-markets).

**2007–08: the first modern food crisis.** Before 2012 and 2022, the 2007–08 episode rewrote how the world thinks about grain. A confluence of forces — a weak US dollar, surging oil prices that pulled corn into ethanol and raised the cost of fertilizer and freight, droughts in Australia, and a wave of **export restrictions** as country after country banned exports to protect its own people — drove wheat and rice to records. Rice, normally the least financialized grain, roughly tripled as exporters like India and Vietnam restricted shipments and importers panic-bought. The episode is the canonical case of the *export-ban feedback loop*: each country's rational decision to hoard made the global shortage worse, turning a moderate supply problem into a price crisis. It is the reason food security is treated as a national-security issue, and it is covered in depth in [food security, export bans, and when governments hoard](/blog/trading/commodities/food-security-export-bans-and-when-governments-hoard).

**The China–soybean axis.** No single relationship matters more to the soybean market than China. China imports well over half of all soybeans that cross borders on earth, crushes them domestically, and feeds the meal to the world's largest pig herd. That makes the soybean price hostage to two Chinese variables: the size and health of the hog herd (an outbreak of African swine fever that culls pigs collapses feed demand, as in 2018–19) and the politics of US–China trade. During the 2018–19 trade war, China shifted soybean purchases away from the US toward Brazil, US soybean prices fell, and US farmers were left with unsold beans — a vivid demonstration that for soybeans, *demand geography* is as important as supply weather. A soybean trader watches Chinese crush margins and hog inventories the way a corn trader watches Iowa rain.

**Grains as a macro signal and an inflation input.** Food is a large share of the consumer price index, especially in emerging markets, so a grain spike is an inflation event that central banks watch. The 2010–2012 and 2021–2022 grain rallies both fed into broad inflation and into the political pressure that follows. For the connection between commodity prices and the macro picture, see [commodities as macro signals](/blog/trading/macro-trading/commodities-as-macro-signals-oil-copper-gold) and the broader energy-and-food inflation linkage in [oil, inflation, and equities](/blog/trading/cross-asset/oil-inflation-and-equities-the-energy-linkage). For how a single print radiates across every market at once, see [cross-asset transmission](/blog/trading/event-trading/cross-asset-transmission-how-one-print-hits-every-market).

## The playbook: how to read the grain market

Pull it together into the way a grain trader actually thinks. The price is a running auction on a fixed annual crop, so your job is to track three things, in order.

**1. The calendar tells you where the risk is.** Price risk is not uniform — it is concentrated in the **pollination window** (US July–August, South America December–February). In the off-season the market trades the known crop against demand; in the weather market it trades every forecast. Know which phase you are in, and size your risk accordingly. Volatility is a seasonal animal: it sleeps in winter and bites in summer.

**2. The weather tells you the yield.** During the weather market, the crop's size is being decided in real time, and the variable you cannot hedge is rain and heat during pollination. Watch soil moisture, the medium-range forecasts, and the heat ridges over the corn belt and the South American growing regions. A two-week dry spell in July is the single biggest source of upside price risk in the calendar.

**3. The WASDE tells you the balance sheet.** Once the crop is made, the game shifts to the supply-and-demand ledger: acreage (locked at planting), yield (locked by weather), demand (feed, fuel, food, exports), and the residual — **ending stocks** and the **stocks-to-use ratio**. Low stocks-to-use means the market has no buffer and any shock is amplified; high stocks-to-use means it can absorb a bad harvest. The monthly WASDE is the official update to that ledger and the most reliable scheduled mover of grain prices.

How a trader actually *reads* a WASDE is worth spelling out, because it is the discipline that turns weather-watching into a price view. The report is a balance sheet: beginning stocks, plus production (acreage × yield), plus imports, equals total supply; against that sits total use (feed, food, fuel, exports); and the difference is **ending stocks**. The whole report collapses, for trading purposes, into one line: did ending stocks come in *above* or *below* what the market expected? A yield revision of even one or two bushels per acre, multiplied across ~90 million acres, swings production by tens of millions of bushels and can flip the stocks number from comfortable to tight. Because the market prices the *expectation* in advance, the trade is in the *surprise*: a smaller-than-expected ending-stocks number is bullish (the buffer is thinner than priced), a larger one is bearish. This is the same surprise-versus-expectation logic that governs every scheduled data release, and it is why a grain desk treats the noon WASDE release like an FOMC decision. The full report-trading playbook — how to position into a WASDE, how to read the number against the whisper, and how the same machinery handles a weather supply shock — is the subject of [weather, the WASDE, and the supply shock](/blog/trading/commodities/weather-the-wasde-and-the-supply-shock-trading-the-report).

Layer on the structural truths. Remember the **two harvests** — the world is never far from somebody's weather market, so a US trader who ignores Brazil and Argentina is half-blind. Remember the **acreage-price feedback** — a price spike is self-limiting on a one-year horizon, because high prices buy more acres and (with normal weather) a bigger crop, so fading a weather spike that pulls in a global acreage response is a recognized trade, while the dangerous regime is back-to-back bad years that never let stocks rebuild. Remember that the price reaches the farmer as **futures plus basis**, so the local basis is its own tradeable variable. And remember that each grain has a *different demand engine* — corn is feed-fuel-food (and partly an energy bet through ethanol), wheat is the political bread grain set at the margin by the Black Sea, soybeans are a protein-and-oil bet hostage to China and the crush.

The deepest point, and the one to end on: of all the commodities in this series, grains are where the **physical and the financial are most violently linked through the weather**. An oil trader can model OPEC's decisions; a copper trader can model China's construction. But a grain trader is, in the end, betting on the rain — on whether a few weeks of summer over Iowa and Mato Grosso deliver the water that fills the kernels. The market exists to convert that meteorological coin flip into a price, every day, all year, so that a fixed pile of calories gets rationed among a hungry world. When you watch corn climb in a July heat wave, you are watching the most fundamental commodity market there is do its only job: putting a price on tomorrow's food before it has even been grown.
