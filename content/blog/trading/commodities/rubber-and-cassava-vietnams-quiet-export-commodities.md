> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Natural rubber and cassava are Vietnam's two quiet export commodities: lower-profile than coffee or rice, but each ties Vietnam directly to Asian industrial demand and, above all, to **China**. They share one DNA — both are China-demand-levered, both trade on **thin physical markets with no deep futures**, so their price is set by trade flows, not by a screen full of speculators.
>
> - **Rubber has two masters, not one.** About 70% of natural rubber goes into **tires**, so the price tracks the global auto cycle. But natural rubber competes with **synthetic rubber made from oil**, so when crude rises the synthetic substitute gets dearer and natural-rubber demand firms. That makes rubber the rare farm crop whose price is levered to the **oil price** as well as the car market.
> - **Cassava is an agro-industrial crop priced at the China border.** Vietnam ships dried chips and tapioca starch mostly to China for food, ethanol, and animal feed. The two things that move it are **Chinese border-trade policy** (official versus informal flows) and **mosaic-virus disease**, which cuts yield at the source.
> - **Thin markets mean China is the price.** Neither has a liquid global futures contract the way oil or copper does, so there is no deep paper market to absorb a shock. When the giant buyer pulls back, the physical price falls with almost nothing to cushion it.
> - The one number to remember: Vietnam is roughly the world's **#3 natural-rubber exporter**, shipping more than **2 million tonnes** in 2022-2023 at an export price in the rough band of **\$1,500 to \$2,000 a tonne** — a multi-billion-dollar trade that almost no one outside the industry ever talks about.

In late 2014, two prices that should have nothing to do with each other fell off a cliff together. One was crude oil, which collapsed from around \$100 a barrel toward \$45 over the back half of the year as American shale flooded the market. The other was natural rubber — the milky latex tapped from a tropical tree, half a world away from any oil well — which slid from a 2011 boom near \$4,800 a tonne down into a long, grinding band around \$1,500. A Vietnamese rubber smallholder in Binh Phuoc, who had never read an oil report in her life, watched the price the local trader offered her per kilogram of cup lump quietly shrink, season after season, for reasons that traced all the way back to a drilling boom in Texas.

That link is not a coincidence, and it is the single most surprising thing about rubber. Natural rubber and oil are bound together because rubber has a twin: **synthetic rubber**, made from petroleum. Tire factories — which swallow about 70% of all the rubber in the world — can dial the blend between the two. When oil is cheap, synthetic is cheap, and natural rubber has to compete down to meet it. When oil is dear, the synthetic substitute gets expensive and natural rubber firms up. So the price a farmer gets for a tropical tree crop is tied, through the tire, to the price of a barrel of crude.

This post is about Vietnam's two **quiet** export commodities — natural rubber and cassava — the ones that never make the headlines the way coffee, rice, or the stock market do, but that together earn Vietnam billions of dollars a year and tie its rural economy tightly to one customer above all others: China. They are quiet for a reason. Both trade on **thin physical markets** with no deep, liquid futures contract, so there is no glamorous screen of speculators, no CNBC ticker, just cargoes crossing a border and a price set by who needs the physical stuff. Understanding them is understanding how a real export economy is wired to its biggest buyer — and what happens when that buyer changes its mind.

![Two China-levered export chains, rubber from plantation to tire and cassava from farm to starch, both pulled by Chinese industrial demand](/imgs/blogs/rubber-and-cassava-vietnams-quiet-export-commodities-1.png)

The figure above is the whole post in one frame. Two different crops, two different chains — rubber tapped from trees and processed into SVR blocks and latex; cassava dug from the ground and milled into starch — both flowing to the same giant buyer, and both priced by physical trade flows rather than a deep futures market, with China leading. The rest of this article walks each chain, shows why rubber answers to both the car and the barrel, and ends on how these two quiet commodities fit the Vietnamese export economy and what actually moves them.

## Foundations: what these two commodities are and why "quiet" matters

Start from zero, because rubber and cassava are corners of the commodity world that even seasoned investors rarely look at. A **commodity** is a standardized physical good — one tonne of a given grade is interchangeable with another tonne of the same grade — which is what lets it trade at a single public price rather than being haggled over cargo by cargo. (The full treatment of how a physical good gets forced through a financial contract is the foundation of this entire series, linked at the end.) Vietnam is a commodity-export powerhouse far beyond its famous coffee and rice: it is also one of the world's largest exporters of both **natural rubber** and **cassava**, and those two are the focus here.

**Natural rubber** is the elastic material made from **latex**, the milky white fluid that the rubber tree (botanical name *Hevea brasiliensis*) produces when its bark is cut. The tree is native to the Amazon but is now grown across the tropical belt of Southeast Asia — Thailand, Indonesia, Vietnam, Malaysia — which together dominate world supply. A worker **taps** the tree by shaving a thin diagonal cut in the bark; the latex bleeds out and is collected in a cup. That raw latex is then processed into standardized, tradeable grades: solid blocks of **technically specified rubber** (in Vietnam, graded as **SVR** — Standard Vietnamese Rubber, with sub-grades like SVR 3L and SVR 10), sheets of **ribbed smoked sheet** (**RSS**), or kept liquid as **concentrated latex** for products like gloves and condoms. Roughly 70% of all that rubber ends up in **tires**; the rest goes into hoses, belts, seals, footwear, and medical goods.

**Cassava** (also called manioc, yuca, or in its processed form **tapioca**) is a starchy root crop — a woody shrub whose swollen tuberous roots are dug out of the ground, much like a potato but larger and more fibrous. Cassava is one of the most important calorie crops in the tropics because it grows on poor soil with little water. Vietnam grows a great deal of it, but mostly not to eat directly: the roots are processed into two main export products — **dried chips** (sliced and sun-dried roots, used as a feedstock for animal feed and **ethanol**) and **tapioca starch** (a fine white powder extracted by washing and settling the root pulp, used in food, paper, textiles, and industry). This is why cassava is best described as an **agro-industrial** commodity: it is farmed like an agricultural product but consumed largely as an industrial input.

### Why "quiet" is a structural fact, not an accident

Both crops are "quiet" in a precise, structural sense that matters enormously for how their prices behave: **neither has a deep, liquid global futures market**. Crude oil trades on NYMEX and ICE in millions of contracts; copper has the LME; corn and wheat have the CBOT. These deep paper markets do something important — they let producers, consumers, and speculators all express a view, and they create a continuous, transparent, forward-looking price that absorbs shocks. (For the mechanics of how a futures market turns a physical good into a continuously priced paper asset, see the spine posts linked at the end.)

Natural rubber does have futures — notably on the Singapore Exchange (SGX, where the SICOM/TSR20 contract lives), the Tokyo (TOCOM/OSE) and Shanghai (SHFE) exchanges — but these are **far thinner and more regional** than oil or copper, dominated by physical-industry hedgers and a relatively small set of Asian participants. Cassava has essentially **no global futures market at all**; its price is a physically negotiated number between exporters and Chinese buyers, reported by trade associations and customs, not screamed across a trading floor.

Why does this matter? Because a thin market has **no shock absorber**. In a deep market, when a buyer pulls back, speculators and arbitrageurs step in at some price, and the forward curve spreads the adjustment across time. In a thin physical market, when the dominant buyer pulls back, there is often **no one else to sell to at anything like the same price**, and the spot price simply falls until it clears. That is the central behavioral fact about both rubber and cassava, and it is why the identity of the buyer matters so much.

### The buyer that matters: China

For both commodities, that buyer is overwhelmingly **China**. China is the world's largest auto and tire manufacturer, so it is the largest consumer of natural rubber on Earth — it buys the tropical world's latex to make tires for cars sold in China and exported everywhere. And China is the largest importer of cassava products, buying Vietnamese (and Thai, and Cambodian) chips and starch for its food industry, its **ethanol** plants, and its animal feed. So both of Vietnam's quiet exports are levered to the same thing: **Chinese industrial demand**. When China's economy is booming and its factories are humming, both prices firm; when China slows, both soften, often together, and with little to cushion the fall.

This is the common thread the whole post hangs on. Two physically different crops — one tapped from a tree, one dug from the ground — converge on the same customer and the same kind of price behavior: **China-demand-levered, thinly traded, set by physical flows.** Now let us take them one at a time, starting with rubber, which has the more surprising price story.

### Why Vietnam grows these two, and why they matter to the rural economy

Before the mechanics, it is worth understanding *why* these two crops anchor so much of rural Vietnam, because that is what makes their price swings a genuine macro story rather than a niche curiosity. Both crops suit Vietnam's land and labor. Rubber thrives on the **red basalt soils** of the southeast and the Central Highlands — the same regions that grow coffee and pepper — and the colonial-era French plantations established a deep base of plantation know-how, processing capacity, and the state-owned Vietnam Rubber Group that still anchors the industry. Cassava grows almost anywhere, including the **poor, sloping, marginal land** that will not support more demanding crops, which makes it a cash crop of last resort for some of the country's poorest farmers — a hardy plant that turns bad soil into a saleable carbohydrate.

That is exactly why the price matters so much. These are not crops grown by a handful of large agribusinesses that can hedge and diversify; they are grown by **hundreds of thousands of smallholders** for whom the farmgate price is household income. Rubber and cassava between them support a large rural workforce and earn Vietnam a multi-billion-dollar slice of its export revenue every year. When the China-levered price falls, it is not a number on a screen — it is a real cut to real rural incomes across Binh Phuoc, Tay Ninh, Dong Nai, and the Central Highlands. The thin-market fragility we keep returning to lands, ultimately, on the smallholder. That human weight is why a quiet, headline-free commodity deserves a careful look.

## Natural rubber: the farm crop priced by the oil market

Rubber's defining peculiarity, the thing that sets it apart from every other agricultural commodity, is that it has a near-perfect **industrial substitute made from oil**. Coffee has no substitute; you cannot synthesize a cup of coffee from petroleum. Cocoa has no substitute; chocolate is chocolate. But rubber does: **synthetic rubber**, principally styrene-butadiene rubber (SBR) and polybutadiene rubber (BR), is manufactured from petrochemical building blocks — butadiene and styrene — that are themselves derived from crude oil and natural gas. Roughly **45% of the world's rubber consumption is natural and 55% is synthetic**, and in many applications, above all tires, the two are partly interchangeable.

This single fact rewires how you think about the rubber price. A tire is a blend of natural and synthetic rubber, and a tire maker's procurement desk is constantly comparing the two on price and performance. Natural rubber has properties synthetic cannot fully match — heat resistance, tear strength, low heat build-up — which is why truck and aircraft tires use a high natural-rubber share. But at the margin, in passenger-car tires and in many industrial goods, the blend can shift. So natural rubber does not get to price itself in a vacuum; it has to stay **competitive with synthetic**, and synthetic's cost is set by the oil-derived feedstock.

![Why natural rubber tracks both tire demand and the oil price through the synthetic substitute](/imgs/blogs/rubber-and-cassava-vietnams-quiet-export-commodities-3.png)

The figure lays out the two forces. On the left, what pulls rubber demand up: more cars and trucks means more tires (the 70% demand sink), and a higher oil price makes the synthetic rival dearer, so tire makers tilt toward natural rubber at the margin and its price firms. On the right, the mirror image: when auto sales slow — especially in China — and oil falls, synthetic gets cheaper and the blend tilts away from natural rubber, and the price softens. Rubber lives at the intersection of the **car cycle** and the **barrel**.

### The first master: tire demand and the auto cycle

Because about 70% of natural rubber goes into tires, the dominant driver of rubber demand is the **production of vehicles and the replacement of worn tires**. This splits into two streams. **Original-equipment (OE) demand** is tires fitted to new vehicles, which tracks new-car and new-truck production and is therefore highly cyclical — it surges in an economic boom and slumps in a recession. **Replacement demand** is tires bought to replace worn ones on the existing fleet, which is steadier (people still need to replace bald tires in a downturn) but still sensitive to how many miles people drive.

Put together, rubber demand is firmly tied to the **global industrial and auto cycle**, and within that, to **China** above all — China produces and sells more vehicles than any other country and is the world's tire-manufacturing hub, exporting tires globally. So a Vietnamese rubber producer's fortunes rise and fall with Chinese auto sales, Chinese tire-factory utilization, and Chinese tire exports. When you read that Chinese car sales disappointed in a given quarter, that is, two steps removed, bearish news for a rubber farmer in the Central Highlands.

### The second master: oil, through the synthetic substitute

Here is where rubber gets genuinely unusual. Layer the oil link on top of the auto cycle. When crude oil rises, the petrochemical feedstocks for synthetic rubber — butadiene and styrene — get more expensive, so **synthetic rubber's production cost rises**. That does two things at once: it pushes the synthetic price up directly, and it makes tire makers more willing to substitute toward **natural rubber**, lifting natural-rubber demand. Both effects firm the natural-rubber price. When oil falls, the chain runs in reverse: synthetic gets cheap, substitution tilts away from natural rubber, and natural prices soften.

This is why natural rubber, alone among the big tropical farm crops, has a meaningful **positive correlation with the oil price** over the medium term. It is not a tight, mechanical link — there are years when rubber-specific supply factors (weather, disease, planting cycles) dominate — but the oil floor is always there in the background, set by the cost of the synthetic substitute. A rubber analyst watches the oil price the way a coffee analyst watches Brazilian frost.

The takeaway is the relationship, not the precise number: rubber has an oil-shaped floor under it.

#### Worked example: the natural-versus-synthetic cross-price

Walk the substitution through with round numbers. Suppose a tire maker can use either natural rubber at **\$1,500 a tonne** or synthetic rubber at **\$1,450 a tonne**, and at those prices it runs a blend of, say, 45% natural and 55% synthetic. The two are close enough that the blend is set mostly by the technical properties the tire needs.

Now crude oil rises sharply — say WTI goes from \$50 to \$80 a barrel — and the petrochemical feedstock cost pushes synthetic rubber's price up to **\$1,750 a tonne**. Suddenly synthetic is \$250 a tonne *more expensive* than natural rubber's \$1,500. The procurement desk responds: wherever the tire spec allows, it tilts the blend toward the now-cheaper natural rubber, raising natural-rubber demand. That extra demand bids natural rubber up — perhaps to **\$1,700 a tonne** — narrowing the gap until the two are competitive again. **The natural-rubber price rose not because more cars were sold, but because oil rose and dragged its synthetic substitute up with it — the cross-price link is the oil floor under rubber.** A farmer who understands this watches the crude screen, not just the auto reports.

### The supply side: the Hevea tree and the tapping cycle

Now the other half of the price equation — supply — which has its own slow, biological rhythm. The rubber tree is a **perennial** that takes about **six to seven years** from planting before it can be tapped, and then it yields for roughly **20 to 30 years** before it is felled and replanted (the old wood becomes furniture timber). This long lag means rubber, like the tree-crop softs, has a built-in **boom-bust cycle**: when prices boom — as they did spectacularly in 2010-2011 — farmers across Southeast Asia plant new trees, but the extra latex does not arrive for the better part of a decade, by which time it often floods in all at once and crashes the price. The long, grinding rubber bear market of 2012-2020 was in large part the hangover from the 2011 planting boom.

Tapping itself is a daily, labor-intensive craft. A tapper scores the bark in the pre-dawn cool (latex flows best at low temperature), the tree bleeds latex for a few hours into a cup, and the worker collects it before it coagulates. The rhythm is seasonal too: most rubber regions have a **wintering** period (in Vietnam, roughly February to April) when the trees shed leaves and latex flow drops sharply, tightening supply, and a peak-flow season in the wet months. So rubber supply has both a multi-year planting cycle and an annual wintering cycle layered on top of the demand drivers.

### Vietnam's place: roughly the world's #3 exporter

Vietnam is one of the giants of this market. It is roughly the **world's third-largest natural-rubber producer and exporter**, behind Thailand and Indonesia, and a major force in the SVR (technically specified) grades that China's tire industry buys. The crop is concentrated in the **southeast** — Binh Phuoc, Binh Duong, Tay Ninh, Dong Nai — historically the heart of the old colonial plantations, and increasingly in the **Central Highlands** (Gia Lai, Kon Tum) and the north. Production is a mix of large state and corporate plantations (the Vietnam Rubber Group, VRG, is a major listed entity) and a large number of smallholders who sell cup lump to local processors.

![Vietnam natural-rubber exports in million tonnes per year from 2019 to 2024](/imgs/blogs/rubber-and-cassava-vietnams-quiet-export-commodities-2.png)

The chart shows Vietnam's rubber-export volume climbing past **2 million tonnes** as plantations matured, reaching a plateau around **2.14 million tonnes** in 2022-2023, then easing to about **2.00 million tonnes** in 2024 as Chinese demand cooled. That is a very large physical trade — millions of tonnes of latex products crossing borders every year — and it is almost entirely invisible in the financial press because it has no glamorous futures market attached. The volume is steady; it is the **price** that swings with the oil-and-auto cycle, which is why a stable export tonnage can still mean a wildly variable export *value* from year to year.

#### Worked example: a rubber smallholder's revenue per hectare, in VND and then USD

Put real numbers on a Vietnamese smallholder's economics. Say a farmer in Binh Phuoc has a mature plantation yielding about **1,800 kilograms of dry rubber per hectare per year** (a reasonable Vietnamese smallholder yield). The local processor pays a farmgate price of, say, **35,000 VND per kilogram** of dry-rubber content in a firm market. The gross revenue per hectare is:

1,800 kg × 35,000 VND/kg = **63,000,000 VND per hectare** per year (about **\$2,500** at roughly 25,000 VND to the dollar).

Now anchor it to the export market in dollars. That dry rubber, once processed into SVR blocks, exports at roughly **\$1,700 a tonne** in a firm market. The farmer's 1,800 kg is 1.8 tonnes, worth 1.8 × \$1,700 = **\$3,060** at the export gate — the difference between the farmer's \$2,500 and the \$3,060 export value being the processor's and exporter's margin and costs. Now suppose oil crashes and Chinese auto demand slumps together, and the export price falls to **\$1,300 a tonne**. The export value of that same 1.8 tonnes drops to **\$2,340**, and the farmgate price the processor can offer falls in step — perhaps to **27,000 VND/kg**, cutting the farmer's revenue to about **48,600,000 VND** (roughly **\$1,950**) per hectare. **A 24% fall in the dollar export price flowed almost straight through to a 23% cut in the farmer's income, because the thin physical market gave the price almost nothing to lean on — that is what China-levered, thinly-traded means in a farmer's bank account.** Hold that pass-through; it is the heart of the rubber story.

### The value chain: from a cut in the bark to a tire in China

Trace the physical journey, because each step is a different business earning a different margin, and the whole thing is priced off the tire-demand signal at the end. The chain runs from the tapped tree, through field collection of latex or cup lump, to a processing plant that coagulates and dries it, to a graded product (SVR, RSS, latex), to export and shipment, and finally to a tire factory — mostly in China.

![The natural rubber value chain from tapped tree through processing to graded SVR and the tire factory](/imgs/blogs/rubber-and-cassava-vietnams-quiet-export-commodities-5.png)

The figure walks the stages. At the **tapping** stage, the smallholder or plantation worker earns the farmgate price for the dry-rubber content of the latex or cup lump. At **field collection**, traders aggregate small lots. At the **processing plant**, the latex is coagulated, washed, dried, and pressed into standardized bales — this is where the raw material becomes a tradeable, gradeable commodity (SVR 10, SVR 3L, RSS sheets, or concentrated latex). At **export**, the graded product is shipped FOB from Vietnamese ports, mostly to China but also India, the EU, Korea, and Japan. And at the **tire factory**, the rubber meets its demand: about 70% of it becomes tires. The price signal flows backward down this chain — when Chinese tire makers cut orders, the export price falls, the processor pays less, and the farmgate price drops, all the way back to the cut in the bark.

### The grades, and why grade decides the price you actually get

A subtlety that matters for anyone reading the rubber market: there is no single "rubber price." Like every physical commodity, rubber trades as a set of **grades**, each with its own price and its own buyer, and the spread between grades is itself a piece of market information. Vietnam's exports are dominated by **technically specified rubber (TSR)**, graded under the SVR system by the impurity and physical properties of the block. **SVR 3L** is a high-quality, light-colored grade made from fresh field latex, used where appearance and consistency matter; **SVR 10** and **SVR 20** are lower, made from cup lump and field coagulum, used in tires where color does not matter but cost does. **RSS** (ribbed smoked sheet) is a traditional sheet grade graded visually by cleanliness (RSS 1 is the cleanest). **Concentrated latex** is the liquid grade for dipped goods — gloves, condoms, balloons — and trades on its own dynamics (it boomed during the pandemic glove shortage).

Why does the grade mix matter? Because Vietnam's historical specialty has been **SVR 3L**, a latex-based grade, while the dominant *tire-industry* demand is for the cup-lump grades **SVR 10 and SVR 20** — which Thailand and Indonesia produce in volume. So Vietnam has at times found itself producing more of the grade the tire industry wants *less* of, and selling it at a discount or being forced to reprocess. The grade premium — the price gap between, say, SVR 10 and SVR 3L — moves with the relative scarcity of each, and a Vietnamese exporter's realized price depends as much on having the *right grade* as on the headline level. This is the rubber version of the **basis**: the standardized regional futures price is one thing; the price for the *specific grade you actually have, delivered where the buyer wants it,* is another, and the gap between them is a live, separate risk.

### Vietnam's competitive position: a price-taker in a three-country oligopoly

Zoom out to the global supply map. Natural rubber is one of the most **geographically concentrated** commodities on Earth on the supply side: **Thailand, Indonesia, and Vietnam together produce well over half** of the world's natural rubber, with the rest spread across China, India, Malaysia, Ivory Coast (a fast-growing African producer), and others. Vietnam sits firmly in this top tier — roughly the world's #3 — but the concentration cuts the opposite way from cocoa. In cocoa, supply concentration gave the producing countries *pricing leverage* during a shortage. In rubber, the supply is concentrated but the **demand is even more concentrated in China**, and the producers compete fiercely against each other *and* against the oil-priced synthetic substitute. The result is that no single producer, Vietnam included, has pricing power. Vietnam is a **price-taker**: it can choose how much to plant and tap, but the price it receives is set by Chinese tire demand and the oil-floored synthetic alternative, divided up among three big competing origins. This is exactly why a Chinese slowdown or an oil crash passes straight through to the Vietnamese farmgate — there is no producer cartel and no deep paper market to defend the price.

## Cassava: the agro-industrial root priced at the China border

Now the second quiet export, which has a completely different chain but the same China dependency and the same thin-market behavior. Cassava is, on the surface, just a humble starchy root — a tropical staple that feeds hundreds of millions of poor people directly. But Vietnam's cassava is mostly **not** eaten at home; it is processed and exported as an **industrial feedstock**, and that turns it into one of Vietnam's largest agricultural export earners — frequently a billion-dollar-plus trade — that almost no one outside the industry notices.

### What cassava becomes: chips and starch

The cassava root is bulky, fibrous, and perishable — it starts to deteriorate within a couple of days of being dug — so it cannot be shipped far in raw form. It must be processed near where it is grown, into one of two main export products. **Dried chips** are roots that are sliced and dried (in the sun or in driers); they are a cheap, storable carbohydrate feedstock used for **animal feed** and, crucially, as a raw material for **ethanol** distilleries (cassava starch ferments readily into alcohol). **Tapioca starch** (and its refined cousin, modified starch) is the fine white powder extracted by grinding the root, washing out the starch, and settling and drying it; it is a versatile industrial and food ingredient — a thickener and binder in food, a sizing agent in paper and textiles, a base for sweeteners and biochemicals.

These two products have different buyers and different price logic. Chips compete with corn and other feed grains as a carbohydrate source, so the chip price has one eye on the **corn price** and the **ethanol economics** in China. Starch is a higher-value, more specialized product whose price reflects food-and-industrial demand. Both, for Vietnam, are sold overwhelmingly to **China**.

### Why China is even more dominant for cassava than for rubber

If China is the dominant buyer of Vietnamese rubber, it is the *overwhelming* buyer of Vietnamese cassava. China takes the large majority of Vietnam's chips and starch exports — by most trade accounts, a share well above 80% and often near 90% — because China's vast food industry, its animal-feed sector, and especially its **fuel-ethanol** program create enormous demand for cassava feedstock, and Vietnam (along with Thailand) sits right next door across a land border.

![Where Vietnam's rubber and cassava go showing China dominates both export destinations](/imgs/blogs/rubber-and-cassava-vietnams-quiet-export-commodities-7.png)

The chart compares the destination concentration of the two crops (the exact split is not in our dataset, so the shares shown are an illustrative approximation of widely reported figures). For rubber, China is the largest single destination but a meaningful slice also goes to India, the EU, Korea, and Japan. For cassava, China is far more dominant — the overwhelming majority of Vietnam's chips and starch heads north across the border. That extreme concentration is exactly what makes cassava so sensitive to **Chinese demand and Chinese border policy**: there is almost no alternative market to absorb the volume if China steps back.

### The border-trade dynamic: official versus informal flows

Here is a wrinkle that makes cassava genuinely different from a normal seaborne commodity: a large share of the Vietnam-to-China cassava trade moves **overland across the northern land border**, through both official border gates and, historically, substantial **informal (unofficial) channels** — small-scale cross-border trade that flexes with the relative price, the exchange rate, and how tightly Chinese customs is policing the frontier at any given moment.

This **border-trade dynamic** is a price driver you simply do not find in oil or copper. When China tightens border controls — for tax reasons, for quality or phytosanitary inspection, or as a broader policy lever — the informal channel narrows, exporters are funneled through official quotas and inspections, and the effective price Vietnamese sellers receive can swing sharply even if underlying Chinese demand is unchanged. A change in a Chinese import procedure at Lang Son or Lao Cai can move the farmgate price for a cassava grower in Tay Ninh more than any global supply-and-demand balance. The market is priced at the border, by the border.

### The supply risk: mosaic virus

On the supply side, cassava's signature risk is **disease**, and one in particular: **cassava mosaic disease**, caused by a mosaic virus spread by whiteflies and by infected planting material (cassava is propagated from stem cuttings, so a diseased plant's cuttings spread the virus). The disease stunts the plant and shrivels the root, and it can slash yields dramatically — outbreaks have cut yields by large fractions across affected provinces in Southeast Asia. Because cassava is propagated vegetatively rather than from seed, once the virus is in the planting stock it is hard to root out, and it spreads geographically as farmers share cuttings.

Mosaic disease is to cassava what frost is to coffee or swollen-shoot is to cocoa: a biological supply shock that can tighten the market sharply and is largely outside human control in the short run. A bad mosaic outbreak in the Vietnamese cassava belt cuts the exportable crop, tightens chip and starch supply to China, and — in this thin physical market — can push prices up fast, because there is no deep inventory or futures market to cushion the shortfall.

### The ethanol-and-corn link: cassava's third lever

There is a third driver of the cassava price that is easy to miss and that ties it, surprisingly, into the global grain complex: **ethanol economics and the corn price**. A large slice of Chinese cassava-chip demand goes into **fuel ethanol** distilleries and into **animal feed**, and in both uses cassava chips are a **carbohydrate feedstock that competes directly with corn**. An ethanol plant or a feed mill in southern China will buy whichever carbohydrate is cheaper per unit of starch or energy — domestic corn or imported cassava chips. So the cassava-chip price has a ceiling set by the corn price: if cassava chips get too expensive relative to corn, Chinese buyers switch to corn, and chip demand collapses.

This means a Vietnamese cassava farmer is, at one remove, exposed to the **Chinese corn balance** and to **Chinese biofuel policy**. A bumper corn harvest in China, or a policy decision to dial back ethanol blending, can soften cassava-chip demand even if nothing changes in Vietnam. Conversely, a Chinese corn shortfall or an expansion of the ethanol program firms chip demand. Starch follows a different logic — it is a higher-value, more specialized product tied to food and industrial demand rather than to the corn-ethanol economics — which is why Vietnam has gradually pushed up the chain from chips toward starch and modified starch, to escape the brutal corn-substitution ceiling and capture more value. The corn link is the cassava market's quiet third lever, alongside Chinese demand and the border.

![The cassava chain from root to dried chips and tapioca starch to China with mosaic disease as a yield risk](/imgs/blogs/rubber-and-cassava-vietnams-quiet-export-commodities-6.png)

The figure maps the chain and its two pressure points. The cassava root splits into two product streams — dried chips (feed and ethanol) and tapioca starch (food and industrial) — both of which flow through the **China border trade** to Chinese end demand. Hanging over the source is the **mosaic-virus risk**, which cuts yield before the root is even dug. So the cassava price is squeezed between a supply risk at the farm (disease, weather) and a demand-and-policy risk at the border (Chinese demand, border controls), with very little global market in between to smooth it.

#### Worked example: a cassava-starch margin from root to China FOB

Trace the margin through the chain with round numbers. A starch mill in Tay Ninh buys fresh cassava roots from farmers at, say, **2,800 VND per kilogram** of root. It takes roughly **4 kilograms of fresh root to make 1 kilogram of tapioca starch** (cassava root is mostly water and fiber; the starch is a fraction of the wet weight). So the raw-material cost of root in one kilogram of starch is:

4 kg root × 2,800 VND/kg = **11,200 VND of root per kilogram of starch**, or **11,200,000 VND per tonne** of starch (about **\$450** per tonne at 25,000 VND to the dollar).

Add processing cost — energy, labor, washing, drying, wastewater treatment, packaging — of perhaps **\$80 a tonne**, giving an all-in cost around **\$530 a tonne**. The mill sells the starch FOB to China at, say, **\$600 a tonne**, leaving a gross margin of about **\$70 a tonne**. Now suppose Chinese demand softens and the China FOB price falls to **\$520 a tonne**. The mill's margin is wiped out — it is now selling below its all-in cost — so it must push the root buying price down to survive. If it cuts the root price it pays farmers from 2,800 to **2,300 VND/kg**, the root cost in a tonne of starch falls to about **\$370**, restoring a thin margin — but the **farmer just absorbed the entire China price shock**, taking an 18% cut in the price of their roots. **In a thinly-traded crop sold to one dominant buyer, a fall in the China FOB price flows straight back down the chain to the farmgate, because there is no futures market and no alternative buyer to share the pain — the grower is the shock absorber.** That is the cassava story in one calculation.

## The common thread: China-levered, thin-market commodities

Step back and see what rubber and cassava share, because the parallel is the real lesson. Both are **China-demand-levered**: the dominant end buyer for both is Chinese industry — tire factories for rubber, food, feed, and ethanol plants for cassava. Both are **thinly traded**: rubber has only regional Asian futures, cassava has essentially none, so neither has a deep global paper market to absorb shocks or generate a transparent forward curve. And both are therefore **priced by physical trade flows** rather than by financial speculation: the price is whatever the cargoes clear at, set by who needs the physical stuff this month.

This combination produces a specific behavior: when the giant buyer pulls back, the price falls with very little to cushion it, and the pain flows straight down the chain to the farmer — as both worked examples above showed. There is no deep speculative community to step in and buy the dip on a thesis; there is no liquid forward curve to spread the adjustment over time. The thin physical market means the spot price does all the work, fast, and the most exposed person — the smallholder at the bottom of the chain — absorbs most of the shock. (This contrasts sharply with a financialized commodity like oil or copper, where a deep futures market and a forward curve change who bears the risk and how the price adjusts — the spine of this series, linked at the end.)

#### Worked example: a China-demand shock hits both crops at once

Model a single Chinese slowdown and watch it ripple through both commodities together. Suppose Chinese GDP growth disappoints and auto sales fall 8% while the food-and-feed sector tightens its belt. On the **rubber** side, fewer cars means lower OE tire demand, Chinese tire factories cut utilization, and rubber import orders drop; say the rubber export price falls from **\$1,700 to \$1,400 a tonne** — an 18% drop. On Vietnam's roughly 2 million tonnes of annual rubber exports, that is a fall in export value of about 2,000,000 × \$300 = **\$600 million** of lost revenue, spread across the whole rubber chain back to the farmgate.

On the **cassava** side, weaker Chinese feed and ethanol demand softens chip and starch orders, and a tightening at the border narrows the informal channel; say the cassava-product export price falls 15%. Both of Vietnam's quiet exports take a hit **in the same quarter from the same cause** — and because both are thinly traded, neither has a deep paper market to soften the blow, so both spot prices fall almost immediately and the farmgate prices follow. **A single shift in Chinese industrial demand simultaneously dented two unrelated-looking Vietnamese crops, which is the whole point: they are not really two independent commodities but two expressions of the same China-demand bet.** That correlation — invisible until China sneezes — is the key risk for anyone exposed to Vietnam's rural export economy.

## Common misconceptions

A handful of myths cloud these two markets. Each is worth correcting with a number or a mechanism.

**Myth 1: "Rubber is just an agricultural commodity, so it tracks the weather and harvests like coffee."** Half true and badly incomplete. Rubber supply does have a planting cycle and a seasonal wintering, but rubber's *demand* — and therefore much of its price — is driven by the **industrial auto cycle and the oil price**, not by food demand. Rubber is the one big tropical crop whose price is meaningfully correlated with **crude oil**, because oil-based synthetic rubber is its substitute. A coffee model that ignores oil is fine; a rubber model that ignores oil is broken.

**Myth 2: "There's no link between oil and a tree crop."** As the cross-price worked example showed, the link runs straight through the tire: oil up → synthetic-rubber feedstock dearer → tire makers tilt toward natural rubber → natural-rubber demand and price firm. It is not a tight day-to-day link, but over the medium term the oil price puts a floor under natural rubber. The 2014-2016 oil crash dragging rubber down with it was this mechanism in plain sight.

**Myth 3: "Cassava is a food crop, so its price is about feeding people."** For Vietnam's *exports*, no. Most exported cassava is processed into **industrial feedstock** — chips for ethanol and feed, starch for food and industry — and sold to **China** as an industrial input. So the price is driven by Chinese ethanol economics, feed demand, the corn price (chips compete with corn as feed), and border policy — not by direct human-food demand. The humble root is an agro-industrial commodity in disguise.

**Myth 4: "Because Vietnam is a top-three exporter, it sets the rubber price."** No. Being a large *exporter* gives Vietnam volume, not pricing power, because the price is set by **demand** — overwhelmingly Chinese tire demand — and by competition with Thailand, Indonesia, and the oil-priced synthetic substitute. Vietnam is a price-*taker*, which is exactly why a Chinese slowdown or an oil crash flows straight through to the Vietnamese farmgate. Volume leadership is not the same as price leadership.

**Myth 5: "Thin markets are calmer because there are no speculators."** Backwards. Thin physical markets are *more* fragile to a demand shock, not less, precisely *because* there is no deep speculative community and no liquid forward curve to absorb it. When the dominant buyer pulls back, there is no one else to sell to at the same price, so the spot price falls fast and far. The absence of a paper market removes the shock absorber; it does not add stability.

## How it shows up in real markets

Look at how these dynamics have actually played out, with real episodes.

**The 2011 boom and the long bust (2012-2020).** Natural rubber boomed spectacularly into 2011, with prices reaching around \$4,800 a tonne (in the illustrative levels of our chart) on the back of the post-crisis Chinese stimulus, a strong auto cycle, and high oil prices. Farmers across Southeast Asia, including Vietnam, planted heavily. But the rubber tree's six-to-seven-year lag meant the new supply did not arrive until mid-decade — right as Chinese growth was decelerating and the 2014-2016 oil crash was pulling the synthetic substitute down. The result was a long, grinding bear market that kept rubber stuck in a roughly \$1,400-1,800 band for most of 2015-2020. This is the perennial-lag boom-bust, with the oil link making it worse: high prices and high oil both encouraged planting, then both reversed together. Vietnam's rubber-export *volume* kept rising through this period as trees matured, even as the *price* sagged — a stable tonnage masking a battered value.

**The 2020-2022 recovery.** Rubber firmed off the 2020 pandemic low as the oil price recovered, Chinese auto demand rebounded, and — a quirk specific to that period — a surge in demand for **medical gloves** (a latex product) during the pandemic tightened the latex market. This showed the multi-driver nature of rubber: the auto-tire demand, the oil floor, and an occasional latex-specific shock can all move it. By 2022-2023 Vietnam's export volume plateaued around 2.14 million tonnes, then eased in 2024 as Chinese demand cooled — the China lever in action.

The glove episode is worth dwelling on because it demonstrates how the **grade** can decouple from the headline. Concentrated latex — the liquid grade used for dipped goods — spiked hard in 2020-2021 as the world scrambled for medical gloves, even while the tire-grade block rubber was still recovering more slowly with the auto cycle. A Vietnamese producer who happened to be geared toward latex caught a windfall the block-rubber producers did not. It is a reminder that "the rubber price" is really a family of grade prices, each answering to a slightly different demand, and that the spread between them carries information about which end-use is tight. The same logic runs through cassava: a tight starch market (food-and-industrial demand) and a soft chip market (corn-substituted feed demand) can coexist in the same season, and a mill geared toward the right product captures the difference.

**A note on weather and the wintering season.** Layered on top of all the demand drivers is rubber's annual supply rhythm. During the **wintering** period — roughly February to April in Vietnam — the trees shed leaves and latex flow drops sharply, tightening supply seasonally. A trader watching the Vietnamese rubber market reads the calendar as well as the China data: a demand pickup that coincides with the low-flow wintering season can firm the price more than the same demand arriving in the peak-flow wet months. It is a smaller effect than the China-and-oil drivers, but in a thin market even a seasonal supply dip can move the price noticeably, because there is no deep inventory or paper market to smooth it.

**Cassava and the China border.** Vietnam's cassava trade has repeatedly demonstrated the border-policy sensitivity. In years when China tightened import controls, shifted ethanol policy, or adjusted phytosanitary requirements at the northern border, Vietnamese cassava prices have swung sharply even with stable underlying demand, as the informal channel narrowed and exporters were squeezed through official gates. The crop is also periodically hit by **mosaic disease** outbreaks that have spread through the Southeast Asian cassava belt since the late 2010s, cutting yields and tightening starch and chip supply. These two forces — border policy on the demand side, mosaic on the supply side — are the recurring stories of the cassava market, and neither shows up in any global price screen, because there isn't one.

**The China lever, pulled once, moving both.** The deepest lesson is the correlation. When Chinese industrial demand softens, rubber and cassava — physically unrelated, sold through completely different chains — tend to weaken *together*, because both are bets on the same buyer. A diversified-looking exposure to "Vietnamese agricultural exports" can turn out to be a concentrated bet on Chinese demand. That hidden correlation, masked in good times, is the single biggest risk in Vietnam's quiet-export complex.

**Who actually intermediates the trade.** It is worth naming the players, because they shape how the price flows. At the bottom are the **smallholders and plantations** producing latex and roots. Above them sit **local collectors and processors** — the rubber-processing factories and the cassava starch mills — who buy the raw crop, transform it into a gradeable export product, and bear the working-capital risk of holding inventory. Above them are **exporters and trading firms** who aggregate cargoes, manage the relationships with Chinese buyers, and handle the border logistics and FOB sales. And on the other side of the border sit the **Chinese importers, tire makers, feed mills, and ethanol plants** — the demand. In a deep-futures commodity like oil, this physical chain coexists with a parallel financial market where the trade houses hedge their inventory. In rubber and cassava, the hedging tools are thin to nonexistent, so the processors and exporters carry **unhedged price risk** on their inventory — which is precisely why, when the China price drops, they cannot lay the risk off in a paper market and instead push it back down to the farmgate. The absence of a deep futures market does not make the risk disappear; it just decides *who* is forced to hold it, and the answer is the smallholder.

**The 2024 cooldown.** Vietnam's rubber-export volume easing from the 2.14-million-tonne plateau to about 2.00 million tonnes in 2024 is a clean, recent illustration of the China lever. As Chinese property and broader industrial demand softened and the auto cycle matured, tire-factory orders eased and Vietnamese rubber shipments pulled back — a volume signal of a demand-led, not supply-led, slowdown. Cassava saw parallel pressure as Chinese feed and ethanol demand and tighter border procedures squeezed flows. Two crops, one cause, the same year: the correlation in plain sight.

## The takeaway: how to read Vietnam's quiet exports

Pull it together into a way of thinking about these two commodities and what they teach about commodity markets generally.

**Watch China first, for both.** The dominant driver of both rubber and cassava is **Chinese industrial demand** — tire and auto production for rubber, food, feed, and especially ethanol for cassava. Before you look at anything else, look at the health of the Chinese economy and the specific demand channel: Chinese auto sales and tire-factory utilization for rubber; Chinese feed demand, ethanol-blending policy, and the corn price for cassava chips. When China is strong, both firm; when China slows, both soften, often together.

**For rubber, watch oil too — it's the second master.** Rubber is unique among tropical crops in having an oil-priced substitute. Track the **crude-oil price** as the floor under natural rubber: when oil rises, the synthetic substitute gets dearer and natural rubber firms; when oil crashes, expect rubber to follow it down regardless of the auto cycle. Also keep the **planting cycle** in mind — a boom today plants the seeds of a bust six-to-seven years out, when the new trees mature.

**For cassava, watch the border and the disease.** The two specific levers are **Chinese border-trade policy** (official versus informal flows, customs tightening, ethanol policy) on the demand side and **mosaic virus** on the supply side. Neither is visible in any futures price; you read this market through trade-association reports, customs data, and on-the-ground disease and border news. The corn price matters too, since chips compete with corn as a feed carbohydrate.

**Respect the thin-market fragility.** The defining structural fact of both commodities is the **absence of a deep global futures market**. That means no shock absorber: when the giant buyer steps back, the price falls fast and the pain flows straight to the farmgate, because there is no liquid forward curve and no deep speculative community to spread the adjustment. This is the opposite of a financialized commodity like oil or copper, where the paper market changes who bears the risk. The thin physical market is why the smallholder, not the speculator, is the shock absorber.

**See the hidden correlation.** Rubber and cassava look like two independent crops, but they are two expressions of the same **China-demand bet**, and they tend to move together when China's industrial cycle turns. Anyone exposed to Vietnam's rural export economy — through equities, through the currency, through the macro picture — should see that a "diversified" basket of Vietnamese exports can be a concentrated bet on one customer.

And that is how these two quiet commodities fit the Vietnamese export economy: they are billions of dollars of physical trade, tying millions of rural livelihoods directly to the health of Chinese industry, priced not on a glamorous screen but at a border and a port, by the buyer who needs the physical stuff. The spine of this whole series is that a commodity price is a physical thing forced through a financial contract — but rubber and cassava show the limiting case, where the financial contract is thin or absent, and the physical flow itself *is* the price. When there is no deep paper market to lean on, the commodity is exactly as strong, or as fragile, as its biggest buyer's appetite. For Vietnam's quiet exports, that buyer wears one name, and it is worth never forgetting it.
