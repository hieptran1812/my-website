> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The softs are tropical cash crops — coffee, sugar, cocoa, and cotton — and they behave more violently than grains for two structural reasons: their production is **concentrated in a handful of countries**, so one nation's weather or politics can move the world price, and most of them grow on **trees and cane with multi-year supply lags**, so a high price cannot quickly summon new supply.
>
> - **Concentration is the master fact.** About 70% of the world's cocoa comes from just two countries, Ivory Coast and Ghana; Vietnam alone is the dominant robusta-coffee origin; Brazil is the swing producer of both arabica coffee and cane sugar. When a single origin sneezes, the whole world's price catches a cold.
> - **The crops are perennial.** A coffee or cocoa tree takes three to five years from planting to first meaningful harvest, so when prices spike, farmers plant — but the new supply does not arrive for years, by which time it often arrives all at once and crashes the price. This is the boom-bust engine of softs.
> - **Each soft has its own quirk.** Sugar is tied at the hip to Brazilian ethanol (mills swing cane between sugar and fuel with the price); cotton is a fibre that competes with polyester (so its ceiling is set by the synthetic substitute, not by food demand); cocoa has almost no substitute, which is why the 2024 crop failure sent it to the moon.
> - The one number to remember: cocoa, which had traded around **\$2,400 a tonne for years**, spiked to an intraday record near **\$12,931 a tonne in April 2024** — a more-than-fivefold move driven almost entirely by crop failure in two West African countries. That is what concentrated supply plus a perennial lag plus a weather shock can do.

In the spring of 2024, the price of cocoa — the bean that becomes chocolate — did something that genuinely stunned the people who trade it for a living. It had spent years as one of the sleepiest contracts on the board, drifting in a band around **\$2,300 to \$2,450 a tonne**, the kind of market a portfolio manager could ignore for a decade. Then, over a few months, it tripled, quadrupled, and finally touched an intraday record near **\$12,931 a tonne** in April 2024 on the ICE exchange. A commodity that had moved like a sloth for a generation suddenly moved like a meme stock. Chocolate makers watched their single most important raw material cost more than five times what it had a year earlier, with no way to hedge their way fully out of it and no quick way for the world to grow more beans.

Nothing about that move was financial in origin. There was no squeeze on a derivatives exchange, no central-bank surprise, no war on a shipping lane. The cause was almost embarrassingly physical: the cocoa trees of West Africa, which between them produce roughly 70% of the world's beans, had a catastrophic season. Aging trees, a viral disease called swollen shoot, black-pod fungus thriving in heavy rain, and then a stretch of harsh dry weather all converged on the same two countries — Ivory Coast and Ghana — in the same season. The crop failed. And because there is no other big region that grows cocoa, there was nowhere to buy the missing beans. The world price simply had to rise until enough demand was destroyed to balance the shrunken supply.

This is the defining behavior of the **softs** — the market's name for the tropical and semi-tropical cash crops: coffee, sugar, cocoa, cotton, and a few smaller cousins like orange juice and rubber. They are "soft" in the old pit-trading sense, as opposed to the "hard" mined commodities like metals, but the label that matters for a trader is not soft versus hard. It is that the softs are **concentrated and perennial** in a way grains are not, and those two facts together make them the most weather-driven, most violent, and arguably most fascinating markets in the whole commodity complex. This post is about why that is true, crop by crop, and how a softs trader actually frames the three things that drive every move: where the supply is concentrated, what the weather is doing to it, and how long the supply lag is before a price signal can be answered.

## Foundations: what a "soft" is and why concentration changes everything

Start from zero, because the softs are the corner of the commodity world that beginners know least. A **commodity** is a standardized physical good — one tonne of a given grade is interchangeable with another tonne of the same grade — which is what lets it trade at a single public price on an exchange rather than being haggled over cargo by cargo. (If you want the full treatment of how a physical good gets forced through a financial contract, that is the foundation of this entire series, linked at the end.) The **softs** are the agricultural commodities that are grown as cash crops in warm climates, harvested, and shipped around the world to be consumed as food, drink, or fibre. The four big ones, and the ones this post covers, are:

- **Coffee** — the roasted seed of a tropical shrub, drunk by roughly two-thirds of the planet. It trades as two distinct contracts: **arabica** (the higher-quality, higher-altitude bean) and **robusta** (the hardier, more caffeinated, lower-altitude bean).
- **Sugar** — sucrose, extracted mostly from **sugarcane** (a tropical grass) and secondarily from **sugar beet** (a temperate root crop). It is a food sweetener but also, crucially, a feedstock for ethanol fuel.
- **Cocoa** — the fermented, dried seed of the cacao tree, the raw material of all chocolate. It has essentially no substitute.
- **Cotton** — the fluffy fibre that grows around the seeds of the cotton plant, the dominant natural fibre for clothing, competing against synthetic **polyester**.

Now the single most important idea in the whole post, the one that separates softs from grains. **Grains are grown almost everywhere.** Corn, wheat, and soybeans are planted across vast temperate zones — the American Midwest, the South American pampas, the Black Sea steppe, the European plain, the Australian outback, the plains of China and India. If one big growing region has a bad year, others can often partly compensate, and because the major exporters sit in **both hemispheres**, the world gets two harvests a year — a northern one and a southern one — that smooth the supply. (The grain complex and its growing-season calendar get their own post in this series, linked at the end.)

The softs are the opposite. They are **tropical perennials** that will only grow in specific bands of latitude, altitude, rainfall, and temperature, which means their production is **geographically concentrated in a handful of countries**. Cocoa needs a hot, humid, equatorial climate and has settled overwhelmingly in West Africa. Arabica coffee needs cool tropical highlands and is dominated by Brazil and Colombia; robusta tolerates hotter lowlands and is dominated by Vietnam. Cane sugar needs tropical heat and is led by Brazil, India, and Thailand. This concentration is the structural fact that makes the softs behave the way they do, and it is worth being precise about why.

### Why concentration multiplies weather risk

When supply is spread across many countries and both hemispheres, a local weather shock is diluted. A drought in one place is partly offset by a good year somewhere else, and the global price barely flinches. But when 70% of a crop comes from two adjacent countries with the same climate, those two countries are correlated — a bad rainy season tends to hit both at once — and there is no large alternative origin to fill the gap. The shock passes straight through to the world price, undiluted.

There is a second multiplier on top of geography: these are **perennial tree and cane crops, not annual row crops**. You plant corn in the spring and harvest it the same autumn; if the price is high this year, farmers plant more next year and supply responds within a single season. A coffee or cocoa tree, by contrast, takes **three to five years** from planting to its first real harvest and then produces for decades. So when a soft's price spikes, the supply response is delayed by years, and during those years the price has to do all the work of balancing the market by destroying demand rather than summoning supply. We will return to this **perennial-lag trap** at the end, because it is the engine of the multi-year boom-bust cycles that make softs so distinctive.

Put the two facts together and you get the softs' signature: a market where a single country's weather or politics is a global price event, and where the price can stay dislocated for years because supply physically cannot catch up. That is why a softs desk obsesses over things an oil trader never thinks about — the rainfall in Dak Lak province in Vietnam, the harmattan wind blowing dust off the Sahara onto Ivorian cocoa pods, a frost warning in the highlands of Minas Gerais in Brazil.

#### Worked example: why concentration turns a 10% crop loss into a price crisis

Suppose a crop has just two big producers, Country A and Country B, who between them make 70% of the world's supply, and the rest of the world makes the other 30%. Say global production is **5.0 million tonnes** a year and demand is steady at 5.0 million tonnes. Now Country A and Country B share a bad rainy season and each loses 15% of its crop. Their combined 70% share — 3.5 million tonnes — falls by 15%, a loss of 3.5 × 0.15 = **0.525 million tonnes**. The rest of the world cannot ramp up (their trees are already producing flat out), so world supply drops to 5.0 − 0.525 = **4.475 million tonnes** against 5.0 million of demand — a **10.5% shortfall**.

Here is the violent part. Demand for a thing like chocolate or coffee is **inelastic** — people do not stop buying chocolate because it got 10% dearer; they grumble and pay. To choke off 10.5% of demand when buyers barely respond to price, the price may have to rise not 10% but 50%, 100%, or more. **Concentration plus inelastic demand is the recipe for a price that moves several times more than the underlying crop loss — which is exactly how a 15% harvest miss in two countries became a fivefold cocoa move.** Hold that asymmetry; it is the whole reason softs are violent.

### The harvest calendar: why softs are seasonal in a way oil is not

Oil flows out of the ground every day of the year at a roughly constant rate; a soft is **harvested once or twice a year** and then stored and consumed over the following months. That single difference puts a **calendar** at the center of every softs market, and a trader who does not know the calendar is trading blind.

Each crop has a planting window, a growing season during which the developing crop is exposed to weather, and a harvest window when the supply for the whole year arrives at once. Brazilian arabica is harvested roughly May through September, and the trees there go through their vulnerable, frost-exposed winter in June and July — which is why coffee gets jumpy every Southern Hemisphere winter. The West African main cocoa crop is harvested October through March, with a smaller mid-crop in spring, so the market spends the wet season (which feeds black-pod disease) and the dry harmattan season (which stresses the pods) on edge. Vietnam's robusta comes in from October through January, centred on the Central Highlands. Cane sugar is harvested in long campaigns — Brazil's centre-south crush runs roughly April to November, India's and Thailand's over the Northern Hemisphere winter. Cotton is picked in the autumn across the Northern Hemisphere producers.

The consequence is that a soft's price has a **seasonal rhythm** layered on top of its weather risk. Supply is most abundant right after harvest, when the new crop floods in, and tightest in the months before the next harvest, when the previous year's supply is running down. This is also why the **forward curve** — the relationship between the price for delivery now and the price for delivery in future months — carries so much information in softs: a market that is tight before a harvest will often show a steep premium for nearby delivery (backwardation) that collapses once the new crop arrives. The mechanics of that curve shape, and who profits from rolling a contract through it, are the spine of this whole series and get their own dedicated posts.

### Two prices for the same bean: the physical and the paper

Like every commodity in this series, a soft has **two prices that must be held apart**: the **physical** price of actual beans of a specific quality at a specific place, and the **paper** price of the standardized futures contract on the exchange. The gap between them is called the **basis**, and in softs the basis is unusually large and unusually important, because the crops are quality-differentiated and perishable in a way that standardized crude oil is not.

The ICE futures contract specifies a standard grade delivered to a standard set of licensed warehouses. But a real coffee roaster does not want generic exchange-grade beans; it wants a particular origin, a particular cup profile, a particular processing method, delivered to its particular roastery. So the roaster pays the futures price *plus a differential* — a premium or discount for the specific physical coffee it actually buys. The futures market lets the roaster hedge the *flat price* (the overall level of coffee), but the differential — the basis — moves on its own, driven by the relative scarcity of that specific quality. In a crisis year like 2024, the flat price soared, but the basis for *physically available* beans of the right grade blew out even further, because what was scarce was not coffee-in-the-abstract but the specific deliverable beans. This is why a hedge that fixes the futures price can still leave a physical buyer scrambling for actual supply, a subtlety we will return to in the misconceptions section.

#### Worked example: the basis a coffee roaster actually pays

A roaster needs to buy a particular Colombian arabica and uses the ICE 'C' futures to hedge. Suppose the futures (the flat price) is **240 cents per pound**, and the roaster's specific Colombian lot trades at a differential of **+35 cents per pound** over the futures — a premium for its quality and origin. The roaster's all-in physical cost is 240 + 35 = **275 cents per pound**. The roaster hedges the 240-cent flat price by buying futures, so if coffee-wide prices rise, the gain on the futures offsets the higher flat cost of its beans.

But now imagine a supply scare makes that specific Colombian grade scarce, and the differential widens from +35 to **+70 cents per pound** even though the futures barely move. The roaster's physical cost jumps to 240 + 70 = **310 cents per pound** — an extra 35 cents per pound — and the futures hedge does *nothing* to offset it, because the futures price (the flat price) did not change; only the basis did. On 37,500 pounds (one contract's worth), that unhedged basis move is 37,500 × \$0.35 = **\$13,125** of extra cost the hedge never touched. **In softs the basis is a live, separate risk from the flat price, which is why a futures hedge protects the level of the market but not the scramble for the specific physical bean you actually need.** Knowing the difference is the line between a hedger and a gambler.

## Cocoa: the 2024 moonshot and the market with no substitute

Begin with cocoa, because it is the cleanest, most extreme example of everything the softs are about. Cocoa is the most geographically concentrated major commodity on Earth: **Ivory Coast and Ghana together grow roughly 70%** of the world's beans, with the rest scattered across Nigeria, Cameroon, Ecuador, and Indonesia. There is no Brazil-sized backup producer. And cocoa is the rare commodity with **almost no substitute** — you cannot make chocolate out of anything else, and chocolate demand is famously sticky. Concentrated supply, no substitute, inelastic demand: cocoa is the softs turned up to maximum.

![ICE cocoa annual average price and the 2024 intraday record of about 12931 dollars a tonne](/imgs/blogs/softs-coffee-sugar-cocoa-and-cotton-the-tropical-markets-2.png)

The chart tells the story at a glance. For years, cocoa sat in a quiet band: about **\$2,290 in 2018, \$2,370 in 2020, \$2,430 in 2021, \$2,450 in 2022.** It was a market you could fall asleep watching. Then 2023 lifted it to around **\$3,290** as a poor West African season began to bite, and 2024 detonated: the annual average leapt to roughly **\$8,500**, and the intraday peak in April 2024 touched the record near **\$12,931 a tonne**. A more-than-fivefold move from the old base, in a commodity that had been inert for a generation.

What caused it was a stack of physical problems landing on the same two countries at once. The cocoa trees of West Africa are **old** — many were planted decades ago and yield less as they age. They are riddled with **cocoa swollen shoot virus disease**, which kills trees and has no cure other than cutting them down. **Black-pod disease**, a fungus, thrives in the heavy rains that came in late 2023 and rotted pods on the tree. Then the weather flipped to a harsh, dry **harmattan** season that further stressed the crop. On top of all that, structural under-investment — farmers in Ivory Coast and Ghana are paid a government-fixed farmgate price that for years lagged behind world prices, leaving little money to replant or treat disease — meant the trees were in no shape to withstand a bad season. Three consecutive deficit years drained global stockpiles, and with no alternative origin to draw on, the price had to ration the remaining beans by force.

The lesson cocoa teaches is the purest version of the softs thesis: **when supply is concentrated in one region and there is no substitute, the price has no shock absorber.** Oil has OPEC spare capacity and strategic reserves; grains have two hemispheres and many exporters; cocoa has neither. So a regional crop failure becomes a global price crisis with nothing to dampen it.

#### Worked example: the cocoa squeeze on a chocolate maker's input cost

Put yourself in the shoes of a mid-sized chocolate manufacturer that buys **4,000 tonnes of cocoa beans a year.** In 2023, when cocoa averaged roughly **\$3,290 a tonne**, that bean bill was 4,000 × \$3,290 = **\$13.16 million**. In 2024, with the annual average around **\$8,500 a tonne**, the same 4,000 tonnes cost 4,000 × \$8,500 = **\$34.0 million** — an increase of **\$20.84 million**, or about **+158%**, in a single year. If a chocolate maker had to buy spot at the April 2024 intraday peak near \$12,931, that 4,000 tonnes would have momentarily cost 4,000 × \$12,931 = **\$51.7 million**, nearly four times the prior year.

Cocoa is typically 40% to 50% of the raw-material cost of a chocolate bar, so an input move of that size cannot be absorbed by trimming margins — it forces price increases on the shelf, smaller bars (the practice retailers call shrinkflation), or recipe changes toward cheaper fillers. **A concentrated, substitute-free soft transmits a regional crop failure directly onto a global industry's cost base, which is why cocoa buyers spent 2024 in a genuine scramble.** That is the difference between a commodity with a shock absorber and one without.

### The farmgate trap: why the boom did not reach the farmer

There is a cruel twist in the cocoa story that is essential to understanding why the 2024 spike was so severe and why it may not quickly cure itself. In both Ivory Coast and Ghana, the government sets a **fixed farmgate price** at the start of each season — the price the farmer is guaranteed for the year's beans — through a state cocoa board. The board sells the country's crop forward on the world market and pays farmers the fixed price regardless of where the spot price ends up.

In a normal year this protects farmers from price crashes. But in the 2023-24 boom it worked the other way. Because the boards had **sold much of the crop forward at low prices** before the spike, the farmers were locked into a farmgate price set far below the \$10,000-plus world price. The people who actually grow the beans saw little of the windfall, while the world price screamed. That matters for supply, because a farmer who is not paid the boom price has **no extra money to replant aging trees, treat swollen-shoot disease, or expand acreage** — which is exactly the investment the crop desperately needed. The boards did raise farmgate prices substantially for the following season, but the damage was done: years of under-payment had left an aging, diseased tree stock that a single good price could not quickly repair.

This is the perennial-lag trap with a political accelerant. Even when the price finally signals "plant more, invest, save the crop," the money does not reach the grower fast enough, and even if it did, the trees take years to respond. **A concentrated soft can stay expensive for an extended stretch precisely because the supply response is throttled twice over — once by tree biology and once by the pricing institutions between the world market and the farmer.** Concentration is not only geographic; in cocoa it is also institutional.

## Coffee: two beans, two contracts, two units

Coffee is the world's most-traded soft and one of the most-traded commodities of any kind, and it is unusual in that it is really **two markets**, because there are two commercially distinct beans grown in two different places under two different conditions.

**Arabica** is the premium bean — smoother, more aromatic, lower in caffeine — and it grows in cool **tropical highlands**, above roughly 1,000 metres. It is finicky: it needs altitude, mild temperatures, and reliable rainfall, and it is vulnerable to **frost** when grown at the edge of its range. The dominant arabica producers are **Brazil and Colombia**, with Brazil the swing producer that the whole market watches. Arabica trades as the **ICE 'C' contract** in New York, quoted in **US cents per pound**, with one contract covering **37,500 pounds** of beans.

**Robusta** is the workhorse bean — harsher in flavour, higher in caffeine, used heavily in instant coffee and espresso blends — and it is hardier, tolerating hotter, lower-altitude conditions and resisting disease better. The dominant robusta producer, by a wide margin, is **Vietnam**, the world's number-one robusta exporter and number-two coffee producer overall; Brazil and Indonesia also grow large robusta crops. Robusta trades on **ICE in London**, quoted in **US dollars per tonne**. (Vietnam's robusta dominance — the Dak Lak harvest cycle, farmgate-versus-ICE pricing, and the 2024 supply scare — gets a dedicated post in this series, linked at the end.)

![Coffee arabica in cents per pound versus robusta in dollars per tonne with the 2024 surge marked](/imgs/blogs/softs-coffee-sugar-cocoa-and-cotton-the-tropical-markets-3.png)

The chart plots both beans, and you have to read it carefully because the **two contracts trade in different units** — arabica on the left axis in cents per pound, robusta on the right axis in dollars per tonne. (To compare like with like: one US cent per pound is about \$22 a tonne, since a tonne is roughly 2,205 pounds, so arabica at 240 cents per pound is around \$5,290 a tonne, in the same ballpark as robusta in 2025.) The story both axes tell is the same: a powerful **2024-25 surge**. Arabica climbed from around **107 cents per pound in 2020** to roughly **240 cents in 2024** and a multi-decade high near **330 cents in 2025** (with early-2025 intraday prints around **430 cents**). Robusta did something even more dramatic in percentage terms, more than **doubling** from around **\$2,450 a tonne in 2023 to \$4,100 in 2024** and onward toward **\$5,000 in 2025** — robusta, the cheap bean, briefly rivalled arabica.

Two concentrated origins drove it. **Brazil**, the arabica giant, suffered drought and excessive heat across its 2024 growing season, damaging the crop and draining stocks. **Vietnam**, the robusta giant, had its own problems: drought in the Central Highlands, plus farmers who in earlier low-price years had pulled up coffee trees to plant more lucrative durian and pepper, shrinking the productive area just as global demand for cheap robusta (for instant coffee and as an arabica substitute when arabica got expensive) was rising. With both beans' dominant producers hit at once and global stocks low, the entire coffee complex repriced upward. This is the concentration thesis again: two countries, two weather problems, one global price shock.

#### Worked example: a coffee position P&L on one ICE 'C' contract

The arabica contract is a clean way to see the leverage in a coffee position. One ICE 'C' contract covers **37,500 pounds** of coffee, and the price is quoted in **US cents per pound**. Suppose you are long one contract and the price moves **50 cents per pound** in your favour — say from 240 to 290 cents, the kind of move arabica made in a few weeks during the 2024-25 surge.

The dollar value of that move is 37,500 lb × \$0.50/lb = **\$18,750** of profit on a single contract. Now consider the leverage: the full notional value of that contract at 290 cents is 37,500 × \$2.90 = **\$108,750**, but a futures exchange only requires you to post a **margin** — a good-faith deposit — that might be \$10,000 to \$15,000 per contract. So a 50-cent move that is about a 20% change in price can be a 100%-plus return on the margin posted, in either direction.

That is the double-edged sword of softs: because they are violent, a frost or a drought can hand a leveraged long a fortune in weeks — and hand a leveraged short a margin call just as fast. **The same concentration and weather sensitivity that make softs fascinating make them brutal to trade with leverage, because the move that proves your thesis right can arrive faster than your account can survive the move that comes first.** A 50-cent coffee swing is a rounding error to a chart and a life-changing event to an over-leveraged trader.

### The arabica-robusta spread

Because coffee is two beans, traders watch the **spread** between them — the price difference between arabica and robusta — as a market in its own right. Normally arabica trades at a healthy premium to robusta, because it is the higher-quality bean. When that premium gets very wide, roasters substitute cheaper robusta into their blends, which lifts robusta demand and compresses the spread; when the premium gets very narrow or even inverts (as it nearly did in 2024-25, when robusta's supply scare drove it up toward arabica), it signals an unusual robusta shortage and tends to revert. The spread is a relative-value trade: you are not betting on the direction of coffee, but on the *relationship* between the two beans normalizing.

#### Worked example: a robusta-arabica spread trade

Suppose in early 2024 you observe that the arabica premium over robusta has collapsed to historically thin levels — robusta's Vietnam supply scare has pushed it up so fast that the usual quality premium for arabica has nearly vanished. You judge this is unsustainable: over time, the premium should re-widen as robusta supply recovers or as roasters switch back to arabica. So you put on a **spread**: long arabica, short robusta, in roughly balanced dollar amounts, betting the gap between them widens back toward normal.

Say you do this when the arabica-minus-robusta gap is an unusually thin **30 cents per pound** equivalent, and over the following months it re-widens to a more normal **90 cents per pound** equivalent. The spread moved **60 cents per pound** in your favour. On a position sized to one arabica contract's worth of exposure (37,500 lb), that 60-cent widening is 37,500 × \$0.60 = **\$22,500** of gross profit on the spread — and crucially, much of the *coffee-wide* price risk is hedged out, because if all coffee rallies or falls together, your long and short legs largely offset. **A spread trade isolates the bet you actually have a view on — the relationship between the two beans — and strips out the directional risk you do not, which is why relative-value is the softs specialist's favorite tool.** You are trading the structure of the market, not guessing the weather.

## Single-country risk: why one origin's weather is the whole world's price

We have now seen it twice — cocoa's West Africa failure, coffee's twin Brazil-and-Vietnam shocks — so let us make the mechanism explicit, because it is the heart of how softs trade.

![Why a local shock in one growing country becomes a global price event for a concentrated soft](/imgs/blogs/softs-coffee-sugar-cocoa-and-cotton-the-tropical-markets-4.png)

The figure walks the chain from a local event to a global price. On the left is the shock: a **frost in Brazil's arabica belt**, a **swollen-shoot outbreak in Ivory Coast**, a **drought in Vietnam's robusta highlands**. Any one of these damages part of one origin's harvest. On the right is the consequence: because there is **no big spare origin** to make up the loss, because **trees and cane cannot regrow in a single season**, and because buyers worldwide must compete for a smaller pool of supply, the world price jumps for everyone. The arrow from a provincial weather report to a global price tag is short and direct, with nothing in between to soften it.

This is why the calendar matters so much in softs. Each crop has a **vulnerability window** — a stretch of weeks when the developing crop is exposed to a specific weather risk — and the market trades nervously through it every year. For Brazilian arabica, the dreaded event is a **frost** during the Southern Hemisphere winter (roughly June and July): a hard frost in the highlands of Minas Gerais or Paraná can kill or damage trees, and because the trees take years to recover or replace, a single severe frost can lift coffee prices for two or three years. The 1975 "Black Frost" in Brazil and the frosts of 1994 are legendary in the coffee market precisely because their effects lingered so long. Sugar's window is the monsoon in India and the rainy season in Brazil; cocoa's is the West African wet and dry seasons. A softs trader lives by these calendars the way a sailor lives by the tides.

The deeper point is that **concentration converts idiosyncratic, local risk into systematic, global risk**. In a diversified crop spread over many countries, one farm's frost is noise that averages out. In a concentrated crop, one *region's* frost is the whole market, because that region *is* a large fraction of world supply. There is no diversification to hide behind. This is the exact opposite of how most financial risk works, where spreading bets reduces risk — in concentrated softs, the world's supply is already an undiversified bet on a few patches of tropical land, and the price reflects that fragility every day.

## Sugar: the crop that is also a fuel

Sugar adds a twist the other softs do not have, and it is the single most important thing to understand about the sugar market: **sugar is also a fuel.** Sugarcane can be processed into either **sugar** (the sweetener) or **ethanol** (the alcohol blended into gasoline), and in Brazil — the world's largest sugar and cane-ethanol producer — the mills can **switch** between the two products depending on which is more profitable at the moment. This makes sugar's price uniquely tethered to the oil and ethanol markets, in a way coffee and cocoa are not.

![ICE sugar number 11 annual average price and the Brazilian sugar versus ethanol switch](/imgs/blogs/softs-coffee-sugar-cocoa-and-cotton-the-tropical-markets-5.png)

Sugar #11 (the world raw-sugar benchmark, traded on ICE in **US cents per pound**) has swung between roughly **12 cents in 2018** — a glut-and-cheap-sugar low — and **24 cents in 2023**, a multi-year high. The bars show the cycle, and the annotations point to the switch logic. When sugar is **cheap** (the 2018 low near 12.3 cents), Brazilian mills tilt their cane toward **ethanol**, because at low sugar prices fuel pays better; that diversion of cane away from sugar tightens sugar supply and helps put a **floor** under the sugar price. When sugar is **dear** (the 2023 high near 24 cents), mills pivot cane back toward **sugar**, adding sugar supply and putting a **ceiling** on the price. The ethanol option therefore acts as a built-in stabilizer that the other softs lack — sugar has a partial shock absorber, courtesy of the fuel market, that cocoa can only dream of.

There is a second swing factor in sugar: **India's export policy.** India is one of the largest sugar producers and a swing exporter, and its government periodically **restricts or bans sugar exports** to keep domestic food prices down (just as it does with rice). When India pulls its sugar off the world market, global supply tightens and the price jumps; when it allows exports to resume, the price eases. So sugar is driven by three forces at once — cane weather in Brazil and Thailand, the **sugar-versus-ethanol** economics tied to oil, and **Indian export politics** — which makes it one of the more analytically rich softs.

The number that ties these forces together is the **stocks-to-use ratio** — the amount of sugar sitting in storage worldwide divided by annual consumption, expressed as a fraction of a year's demand. It is the single most important supply-demand gauge in every agricultural market. A *high* stocks-to-use ratio means the world is sitting on a comfortable buffer, and the price stays low and calm; a *low* ratio means the cushion is thin, and any shock — a bad monsoon, an export ban, a switch of cane to ethanol — sends the price flying because there is nothing in storage to absorb it. The reason sugar (and every soft) can swing so violently is that the stocks-to-use ratio is often low: storage of an agricultural good is costly and the crop is perishable, so the world does not carry the kind of multi-year inventory cushion that, say, the oil market keeps. When you hear that a market is "tight," that is the stocks-to-use ratio talking. The mechanics of how a crop report and a stocks number move a market in seconds get their own dedicated treatment in this series, linked at the end.

#### Worked example: the Brazil sugar-versus-ethanol switch economics

Here is the calculation a Brazilian mill makes, simplified to the essence. The mill can turn one tonne of cane into either a certain amount of sugar or a certain amount of ethanol, and it compares the revenue from each. Suppose at current prices, processing a tonne of cane into **sugar** yields revenue equivalent to **\$0.18 per pound of sugar**, while processing the same tonne into **ethanol** yields revenue equivalent to **\$0.16 per pound of sugar** (converting the ethanol price into its sugar-equivalent so the two are comparable). The mill makes sugar, because sugar pays 2 cents per pound more.

Now let the oil price rise, dragging ethanol up with it, so the ethanol route now yields **\$0.20 per pound** of sugar-equivalent while sugar is still **\$0.18**. The mill flips: it diverts cane to ethanol, because fuel now pays 2 cents more. That decision, multiplied across hundreds of mills, **removes sugar from the world market** even though the cane crop was unchanged — and the missing sugar pushes the sugar price up until the two routes pay roughly the same again. **Sugar's price is therefore anchored not only to its own crop but to the oil-and-ethanol economics, so a sugar trader has to watch the crude market and Brazilian fuel policy as closely as the cane weather.** Sugar is the one soft whose floor is set in the oil market, not the field.

## Cotton: a fibre, not a food, capped by polyester

Cotton is the odd one out among the softs: it is the only one that is not a food or a drink. It is a **fibre**, grown for the fluffy boll around its seeds, and its demand comes not from grocery shelves but from **textile mills and the apparel industry**. That single fact changes everything about how its price behaves, because cotton has a competitor that food crops do not: a **synthetic substitute** called **polyester**.

![ICE cotton number 2 annual average price with the 2022 spike and the polyester ceiling](/imgs/blogs/softs-coffee-sugar-cocoa-and-cotton-the-tropical-markets-6.png)

Cotton #2 (the ICE benchmark, in **US cents per pound**) shows the pattern: a low near **64 cents in 2020** during the COVID demand collapse, a sharp **spike to about 108 cents in 2022** as post-pandemic apparel demand roared back against tight supply, and then a fade back to the **80s** as demand normalized. That spike-and-fade shape is the signature of a fibre that competes with a substitute.

Here is the key dynamic. Cotton and polyester are both used to make cloth, and apparel makers can adjust the **blend** — more cotton or more polyester — depending on the relative price. When cotton runs too far above polyester, mills shift the blend toward the cheaper synthetic, which **caps cotton's upside**: the polyester price acts as a ceiling that food softs like coffee and cocoa do not have. Cotton's demand is also tightly tied to the **consumer apparel cycle** — when the economy is strong and people buy clothes, cotton demand rises; when a recession hits and apparel spending falls, cotton demand sags. So cotton trades more like a **consumer-cyclical** commodity than a weather-driven food, even though weather in the big producing countries (China, India, the United States, Brazil, Pakistan) still drives the supply side.

The **cotton-polyester spread** is the relationship a cotton specialist watches the way a coffee trader watches the arabica-robusta spread. When cotton is cheap relative to polyester, mills favour cotton and its demand firms; when cotton is expensive relative to polyester, demand erodes as blends shift synthetic. Polyester itself is made from petroleum derivatives, so its price moves with oil and petrochemical markets — which means cotton, like sugar, has a quiet link back to energy through its substitute. The fibre that clothes the world is priced at the intersection of three things: its own weather-driven supply, the consumer apparel cycle, and the price of the synthetic that can replace it.

There is a geography to cotton that is worth knowing, because it differs from the food softs. Cotton is grown in many countries — China, India, the United States, Brazil, and Pakistan are the big producers — so its *supply* is less concentrated than cocoa's or robusta's. But the **trade flows** are concentrated on the demand side: the spinning mills that turn raw cotton into yarn are heavily clustered in Asia, with **China** the dominant importer and processor for decades, so a shift in Chinese mill demand or Chinese import policy can move the world price even when supply is steady. The United States is the largest *exporter*, and the weekly US export-sales report is to cotton roughly what a crop report is to grains — a scheduled data release that can jolt the market. So cotton is less about a single growing country's weather and more about the **apparel demand cycle plus the China trade flow**, which is why it trades like a consumer-cyclical with an agricultural supply side bolted on.

That demand-cyclical character makes cotton an unusually good read on the consumer. When clothing sales are strong, mills run hot and pull cotton; when a recession hits and shoppers stop buying clothes, mill demand collapses and cotton sags — often before the official retail data confirm the slowdown. A cotton trader is, in part, watching the global apparel consumer, with the polyester price as the ceiling and the weather in five producing countries as the floor.

## Common misconceptions

**"Softs move like grains."** They do not. Grains are grown in both hemispheres across many large exporters, so they get two harvests a year and a measure of geographic diversification; the softs are tropical perennials concentrated in a few countries. That is why cocoa could go up fivefold in 2024 while no grain did anything remotely as dramatic. The number that proves it: roughly **70% of cocoa from two countries** versus corn and wheat from dozens. Concentration is not a footnote; it is the entire difference in behavior.

**"A high price will quickly fix the shortage."** For an annual crop, yes — high corn prices this year mean more corn planted and harvested within twelve months. For a perennial soft, no. A coffee or cocoa tree planted in response to today's high price will not bear a real crop for **three to five years**, and full maturity takes longer still. So a soft can stay expensive for years after the shock that caused it, because the supply response is physically delayed. The high price does its work by destroying demand, not by quickly adding supply.

**"Sugar is just a food commodity."** Sugar is half a fuel. In Brazil, the world's largest producer, mills swing cane between sugar and **ethanol** with the price, so sugar's floor is partly set by the oil market. A sugar trader who ignores crude and Brazilian fuel policy is missing one of the three legs that price the commodity. The other two legs — cane weather and **Indian export policy** — are equally non-obvious to a newcomer.

**"Cotton is priced by cotton supply and demand."** Cotton's ceiling is set by **polyester**, its synthetic competitor, not by cotton fundamentals alone. When cotton gets too dear, apparel makers shift the blend toward poly, capping the upside. Because polyester is made from petroleum, cotton has a hidden link to oil through its substitute. Cotton is a fibre that competes with a chemical, which makes it behave differently from every food soft.

**"You can hedge softs the way you hedge oil."** Partly, but the basis risk is brutal. A chocolate maker can buy cocoa futures, but the futures are for a standard grade delivered to a standard location, while the maker needs a specific bean quality at a specific factory — and in a year like 2024, when the spot scramble was for *physical* beans of the right type, the futures hedge protected the price but not necessarily the *supply*. Softs are physical, perishable, and quality-differentiated in ways that make hedging messier than hedging a barrel of standardized crude.

## How it shows up in real markets

The softs deliver some of the most spectacular price events in all of commodities, and a few real episodes make the abstractions concrete.

**The 2024 cocoa moonshot.** Already described, but worth restating as the canonical case: a concentrated, substitute-free soft (70% from Ivory Coast and Ghana) suffered three straight deficit years from aging trees, swollen-shoot disease, black-pod fungus, and adverse weather, with no alternative origin to draw on. Cocoa went from about **\$2,400 a tonne** to an intraday **\$12,931** — and because chocolate demand is inelastic, the price had to rise that far to balance the market. It is the textbook demonstration of what happens when a soft has no shock absorber.

**The 2024-25 coffee surge.** Brazil's arabica drought and Vietnam's robusta scarcity hit simultaneously, doubling robusta from around \$2,450 to \$4,100 a tonne and lifting arabica to multi-decade highs near 330 cents per pound (with intraday prints toward 430). The unusual feature was robusta, the cheap bean, surging so hard it nearly closed its discount to arabica — a sign of how concentrated and tight the robusta market had become when its dominant origin, Vietnam, faltered.

**The 1970s sugar spike and the 2010 squeeze.** Sugar has a long history of violent moves. In 1974 it spiked toward 65 cents a pound (in then-current cents) on a supply panic, then collapsed. In 2010-2011 it ran above 30 cents on Indian and Brazilian supply problems. Each time, the cure for high prices was high prices: extreme levels eventually pulled forward planting and demand destruction, and the price crashed — the boom-bust rhythm of a concentrated crop.

**The 1994 and 1975 Brazil coffee frosts.** Twice in living memory, a hard winter frost in Brazil's coffee highlands damaged the trees so badly that arabica prices spiked and *stayed* elevated for two to three years, because the damaged trees took that long to recover or be replaced. These frosts are the clearest historical proof of the perennial-lag trap: the supply could not respond quickly, so the price had to stay high for years.

**The cotton spike of 2010-2011.** Cotton briefly soared above 200 cents a pound — a record — on a collision of flooding in Pakistan, an export ban in India, and strong Chinese demand. But the very height of the price triggered the polyester switch and a demand collapse, and cotton crashed back. The episode showed both the upside violence of a fibre supply shock and the ceiling that the synthetic substitute eventually imposes.

The common thread across every one of these is the spine of the softs: **concentrated supply meets a weather or policy shock, and a perennial lag prevents a quick supply response, so the price overshoots violently and then, eventually, mean-reverts when demand destruction and the slow supply response finally catch up.** That overshoot-and-revert rhythm is the softs' personality.

## The perennial-lag trap and how a softs trader frames a market

Everything above converges on one structural feature that deserves its own picture, because it is the deepest reason softs behave the way they do.

![The perennial lag trap timeline showing a price spike leading to planting then a delayed supply glut and crash](/imgs/blogs/softs-coffee-sugar-cocoa-and-cotton-the-tropical-markets-7.png)

The timeline walks the trap. In **Year 0**, a shock sends the price soaring, and farmers — finally rewarded for planting — put new trees in the ground. For **Years 1 to 3**, those young trees grow but bear nothing; supply is unchanged, so the high price persists. By **Years 3 to 5**, the price is often *still* high, because the planted supply has not arrived and the market is still short. Then, in **Years 5 to 7**, the new trees finally reach full yield — and because so many farmers planted at once during the boom, the new supply arrives in a flood. The result, in **Year 7 and beyond**, is a **glut**: too much supply hits a market whose demand has by now adjusted downward, and the price **crashes** and stays low for years, until the cycle resets.

This is why softs do not trend gently like a well-supplied commodity. They lurch through long **boom-bust cycles** measured in years, not months, because the supply response is delayed by the biology of the tree. The high price cannot summon quick supply, so it overshoots; then the delayed supply arrives all at once and overshoots the other way. A trader who understands this does not get fooled by either extreme — a screaming-high price is not a reason to chase it forever (the glut is coming, just slowly), and a crushed-low price is not permanent (the next shock will find a market with no buffer).

One more force amplifies the swings: the **flow of speculative and index money**. The softs are small markets by the standards of oil or rates — the entire annual cocoa crop is worth a tiny fraction of the daily volume in crude — so a relatively modest inflow of financial capital can move them hard. When a soft starts trending, trend-following funds pile in, and the broad commodity index funds (which hold a basket of commodities including coffee, sugar, and cotton) add to the position as the weight rises, which can extend a move well past what the fundamentals alone justify. In thin softs, the line between a fundamental move and a positioning-driven overshoot is blurry, and the sharpest reversals often come when crowded speculative longs are forced to liquidate. This is the physical-versus-paper tension at the heart of this whole series: the price on the screen is set by both the beans in the warehouse and the contracts in the funds, and in a small market the paper can briefly dominate the physical. A disciplined softs trader watches the **commitments-of-traders** positioning data the way a copper trader watches warehouse stocks — to know whether a move is the crop talking or the crowd.

So how does a softs trader actually frame a market? It comes down to the three questions this post has been circling, asked in order:

- **First, concentration: who grows it, and how concentrated is the supply?** Map the production to its few origin countries. Cocoa is two countries; robusta is essentially Vietnam; arabica is Brazil-plus-Colombia; cane sugar is Brazil-plus-India-plus-Thailand. The more concentrated, the more violent the market and the more a single country's fate drives the price. Know whose weather and whose politics matter.
- **Second, weather and policy: what is happening in those origins right now?** Watch the **vulnerability windows** — Brazil's frost season, India's monsoon, West Africa's harmattan — and the policy levers, especially export bans (sugar, and rice in the grain world) and government farmgate prices. A softs desk is, in large part, a weather desk for a dozen specific patches of tropical land.
- **Third, the supply lag: how fast can supply respond?** For a perennial (coffee, cocoa), the answer is *years*, which means a shock can keep the price elevated far longer than intuition suggests, and a glut can keep it depressed just as long. For sugar, the cane is faster but the ethanol switch and Indian policy add their own dynamics. For cotton, the polyester ceiling caps the upside. Knowing the lag tells you how *durable* a price move is likely to be.

Then a fourth question sits on top, the one that turns analysis into a trade: **what is the substitute or the switch?** Cocoa has none, which is why it can run to the moon. Coffee has the arabica-robusta substitution, which links the two beans. Sugar has the ethanol switch, which links it to oil. Cotton has polyester, which caps it and links it to petrochemicals. The substitute or switch is what sets the ceiling and where the relative-value trades live.

Connect this back to the spine of the whole series. A soft-commodity price is a physical thing — a bean, a cane stalk, a fibre boll grown on a specific patch of tropical land — forced through a financial contract on ICE in New York or London. The forward curve, the cost of carry, and the convenience yield decide who profits from rolling that contract, exactly as in oil and metals (those mechanics get their own posts in this series). And the softs are pure **consumption** commodities — bought to be eaten, drunk, or worn, never hoarded as money — which is why they are driven by harvests and weather rather than by interest rates and fear, and why they sit at the opposite end of the spectrum from gold, the monetary metal that gets its own series. The softs are the commodity world at its most physical, most fragile, and most human: a few patches of warm earth, a handful of countries, the weather, and the slow patience of a tree. Read those three things — concentration, weather, and the lag — and you understand why the tropical markets move the way they do.
