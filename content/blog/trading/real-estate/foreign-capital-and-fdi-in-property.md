> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Foreign money reaches property through two very different channels, and lumping them together is the most common mistake people make about it. The first channel is **direct purchases**: foreign buyers bidding for homes, which adds demand without adding supply and pushes prices up — which is why governments tax it (Singapore's ~60% buyer stamp duty) or cap it (Vietnam lets foreigners own at most ~30% of the units in any one building, on a 50-year lease, never the land). The second channel is **foreign direct investment (FDI)** into *development* and especially *industrial and logistics* property — Vietnam's structural bright spot, riding the manufacturing wave as supply chains shift out of China.
>
> - **Direct buyers add demand, not supply** — they compete for a fixed stock of homes, so the price pressure is real and governments cap or tax it.
> - **FDI into development and industrial property adds capacity** — factories, warehouses, worker housing — and is far stickier than a portfolio flow.
> - **Foreign capital is powerful but fickle.** "Hot money" floods in near tops and flees in busts, so it *amplifies* the cycle in both directions. Sticky FDI does the opposite — it cushions.
> - **The number to remember:** Vietnam caps foreign individuals at **~30% of the units in a single building** on a **50-year leasehold**, while Singapore stacks a roughly **60% Additional Buyer's Stamp Duty** on a foreigner's home purchase. Same problem, two very different tools.

In late 2021, a property agent in Singapore could still sell a luxury condo to a Chinese or Indonesian buyer with a stamp-duty bill that, while not trivial, was survivable. By April 2023 the government had doubled the foreigner surcharge to a flat 60% of the purchase price. On a S\$5 million apartment that is a S\$3 million tax — paid up front, on top of the price — purely for the crime of being foreign. Demand at the top of that market did not slow. It stopped.

Two thousand kilometres north, in the industrial belt around Hanoi and the port province of Hải Phòng, a very different kind of foreign money was arriving — and being courted, not taxed. A Korean electronics giant signs a fifty-year land lease in an industrial park, pours a billion dollars of concrete and machinery, and hires forty thousand workers. The provincial government does not levy a 60% surcharge on that. It builds the road to the factory gate. One country, two channels of foreign capital, two opposite policy reflexes — punish the first, chase the second.

That contrast *is* the post. Foreign money does not touch property through one pipe; it comes through two, and the two do almost opposite things. The figure above is the mental model for everything that follows: on the left, the direct-purchase channel — a foreign buyer bidding for an existing home, adding demand to a fixed stock, which is why it gets capped and taxed; on the right, the FDI channel — capital that funds new development and new industrial floorspace, which adds supply and productive capacity, which is why governments compete for it. Get those two channels straight and most of the confusion about "foreign buyers" and "hot money" and "the FDI boom" dissolves.

We will build every term from zero, anchor each idea in a worked example with real numbers, and keep Vietnam as the running case while keeping the universal model honest against Singapore, Australia, Sydney, Vancouver, and the great capital-flight busts of the past. Three characters recur: a **foreign buyer** (call him Mr. Kang, a Korean professional eyeing a Saigon apartment), an **industrial-park developer** (call her the developer, leasing factory land to manufacturers), and **Minh**, our local — a Hanoi office worker who keeps getting outbid and wants to understand who, exactly, he is bidding against.

## Foundations: the two channels, and the words you need

Before anything else, we need a small vocabulary. None of it is hard; the trouble is that the words get used loosely, and the looseness hides the single most important distinction in the whole subject.

### Foreign capital vs FDI

**Foreign capital** is the broad bucket: any money that crosses a border to be put to work in another country. It includes someone buying a flat in a city they will never live in, a pension fund buying shares on the local stock exchange, a bank parking money in local bonds, and a manufacturer building a factory. All of it is "foreign capital." The bucket is too big to be useful on its own — that is exactly why people get confused.

**Foreign direct investment (FDI)** is a specific *subset*: foreign capital that takes a lasting, controlling stake in a real productive asset or business in the host country. The textbook threshold is a 10% or larger ownership stake, but the spirit of it is what matters: FDI is money that builds or buys something *real and operated* — a factory, a logistics park, a hotel, a stake in a development company. The investor is not just renting exposure to a price; they are putting roots down. A Samsung plant in Bắc Ninh is FDI. A Japanese developer taking a 30% stake in a Vietnamese residential project is FDI. These investors mean to stay for years.

The mirror image of FDI is **portfolio investment**: foreign money that buys a *financial claim* — a listed share, a bond, a deposit — without taking operational control. The portfolio investor owns a slice of paper that they can sell with one click. They are renting exposure to a price, not building anything.

> Hold onto that contrast: **FDI builds and stays; portfolio investment buys paper and can leave in an instant.** Nearly everything else in this post is a consequence of that one difference.

### Portfolio (hot) money vs sticky FDI

Because portfolio money can leave instantly, it earns a nickname: **hot money**. Hot money is capital that chases the highest short-term return and is ready to bolt the moment sentiment sours — a fire that flares up and gutters out fast. It is *fickle* by design: liquid claims, no sunk cost, easy exit.

FDI is the opposite. It is **sticky**: a built factory cannot be wired abroad overnight. The investor has sunk capital into concrete, machines, hiring, and contracts — abandoning it means writing all of that off. So FDI tends to ride out a downturn rather than flee it. When people say "Vietnam's foreign capital is high quality," they usually mean the *mix* is tilted toward sticky FDI rather than fickle hot money — though, as we will see, Vietnam has both.

This stickiness distinction is the single most useful filter you can apply to any headline about foreign money and property. The smart question is never just "is foreign capital coming in?" It is **"which channel, how sticky, and what happens when it reverses?"**

### Foreign-buyer rules and ownership caps

When foreigners buy *homes* — the first channel — governments often intervene, because direct buying adds demand to a fixed local housing stock without adding supply (more on that mechanism below). The interventions come in two broad flavours:

- A **tax or surcharge** on the purchase. The buyer can buy, but pays an extra slab of stamp duty. Singapore's *Additional Buyer's Stamp Duty (ABSD)* for foreigners — a punitive **~60%** of the price as of 2023 — is the headline example. A *stamp duty* is simply a tax on a transaction document; the government collects a percentage of the price when ownership transfers.
- An **ownership cap or restriction** on *what* and *how much* a foreigner can own. Vietnam's rule is the cleanest example: a foreign individual may own at most **~30% of the apartment units in any single building** (and tighter limits in landed-house zones), and only ever on a **50-year leasehold**, never on the land itself.

These two tools — tax vs cap — are different levers pulling on the same problem. A tax says "you may buy, but it will cost you dearly." A cap says "only this much foreign ownership is allowed, full stop, no matter how much you are willing to pay."

### Freehold vs leasehold, and why Vietnam grants only the latter to foreigners

A quick but essential definition. **Freehold** ownership means you own the property — and usually the land under it — outright and forever (subject to tax and law). **Leasehold** means you hold the right to use the property for a fixed term — say 50 or 99 years — after which the right expires (or must be renewed) and reverts to the underlying landowner.

In Vietnam this matters enormously, because **all land is owned by the State** — there is no private freehold of *land* for anyone, foreign or local. Vietnamese citizens get an effectively indefinite *land-use right* that functions much like ownership; foreigners get a strictly time-boxed one. A foreign individual buying a Vietnamese apartment gets a **50-year lease** on the home (extendable in principle), not the land beneath it. So even setting aside the 30% cap, a foreign buyer in Vietnam is buying a wasting, time-limited right — a structurally weaker claim than a local's.

### Capital controls and repatriation

A **capital control** is any rule a country uses to restrict money crossing its border — limits on how much foreign currency you can take out, taxes on outflows, approvals required to repatriate profits, or ceilings on foreign ownership of certain assets. **Repatriation** is the act of taking your money — and your profits — back home, converting it out of the local currency into your own. A foreign investor's return is only real once it has been *repatriated*: a paper gain trapped behind capital controls, or eaten by a currency that has collapsed, is not a gain you can spend.

Countries use capital controls partly to tame hot money. If money can flood in and out freely, the cycle whipsaws; if outflows are slowed, a panic is dampened. The cost is that controls also deter the *good* capital — the investor who fears they cannot get their money out later thinks twice about putting it in now.

### The industrial / logistics property segment

Most people, when they hear "real estate," picture homes and offices. But there is a whole segment built for *making and moving things*: **industrial property** (factories, plants, serviced industrial land in special zones) and **logistics property** (warehouses, distribution centres, cold storage near ports and highways). These are not glamorous, but they are where the manufacturing economy physically lives — and where, in Vietnam, foreign capital is most concentrated and most consequential.

We will give this segment its own section, because it is the heart of the FDI story and Vietnam's structural bright spot.

### The manufacturing-FDI → industrial-real-estate link

Here is the chain that makes industrial property the bright spot. When a foreign manufacturer decides to build a plant in a country — manufacturing FDI — it does not just need a building. It needs *industrial land*, then a *factory*, then *warehouses and logistics* to move parts in and goods out, then *housing* for thousands of workers, then *shops and services* around the workers. One factory-investment decision pulls several kinds of real estate up with it. This is the multiplier that links the manufacturing-FDI wave to a sustained property tailwind, and we will trace it step by step.

### Currency risk for foreign investors

A foreign investor earns and ultimately spends in their *home* currency, but the property pays them in the *local* one. If the local currency weakens against the home currency between buying and selling, the investor loses purchasing power even if the local price rose. This is **currency risk** (or *FX risk*) — the risk that exchange-rate moves erode (or amplify) a return that looked fine in local terms. For an emerging-market property buyer, currency risk is often *larger* than the property-price risk itself.

### Why hot money amplifies cycles

Put the pieces together and you get the central dynamic. Hot money chases returns, so it floods *in* when a market is already hot — late in a boom, when prices are rising and everyone is greedy. That extra demand pushes prices *past* fair value: an **overshoot** to the upside. Then sentiment turns. Hot money, being liquid and unanchored, *flees* — and because everyone is selling at once into a market with few buyers, prices overshoot to the *downside*. So foreign hot money does not smooth the cycle; it widens it, deepening both the mania and the bust. Sticky FDI does the reverse — it stays through the dip and cushions it.

Those are the foundations. Now the mechanics, channel by channel.

## Direct foreign buyers — and why they get capped and taxed

Start with the simplest, most visible channel: a foreigner buying a home to own. Why does this raise prices, and why do governments react so hard?

The mechanism is pure supply and demand, but the key word is *fixed*. In the short run, a city's housing stock is roughly fixed — you cannot conjure ten thousand finished apartments overnight (this is *supply inelasticity*, covered in depth in [supply elasticity and why some cities boom and bust](/blog/trading/real-estate/supply-elasticity-why-some-cities-boom-and-bust)). When a foreign buyer steps into that fixed market and bids for a flat, they add a unit of *demand* without adding a unit of *supply*. The marginal buyer who would have set the price is now outbid; the clearing price ratchets up. No new home was created — a foreign name simply went on the deed of an existing one, at a higher number.

Crucially, foreign buyers are often *price-insensitive* in ways locals are not. A buyer parking wealth offshore — fleeing capital controls at home, hedging political risk, or just diversifying — may not care whether the rental yield makes sense or whether the price is "fair" relative to local incomes. They are buying a *store of value* in a stable jurisdiction, not an income stream. So they bid harder than a local who must actually service a mortgage out of a local salary. In hot cities, that gap between a global buyer's wallet and a local's paycheque is exactly what makes foreign demand politically explosive.

This is why governments reach for the cap or the tax. They are not (usually) being xenophobic for its own sake; they are responding to a real complaint from voters — that locals are being priced out of their own city by buyers who will leave the lights off. The policy choice is between **taxing** the demand (let it in, but make it pay) and **capping** it (limit how much can come in at all). Different countries pick different tools, and the figure below lines up four of them — Singapore, Australia, Vietnam, and the United States — against the tool they use and the effect it has.

![A comparison matrix of four markets and how they treat foreign home buyers, Singapore with a sixty percent stamp duty surcharge, Australia with approval gates and surcharges steering money to new supply, Vietnam with a thirty percent ownership cap and leasehold, and the United States largely open](/imgs/blogs/foreign-capital-and-fdi-in-property-3.png)

Read across that matrix and the spread of philosophies is striking. Singapore builds a near-total *tax* firewall (the ~60% ABSD), Australia *gates and steers* foreign money toward new construction, Vietnam *caps the quantity* and grants only a wasting leasehold, and the United States leaves residential buying almost entirely *open* — no federal ban, only a tax (FIRPTA, the Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act) collected when the property is eventually sold. The same foreign buyer, walking into each of these four markets, meets a completely different welcome. We will work through the two most extreme tools — Singapore's tax and Vietnam's cap — in numbers below.

#### Worked example: a foreign buyer adds demand to a fixed stock

Imagine a Hanoi building with 100 apartments and 100 local buyers, each able to pay up to ₫7.0 billion (≈ \$270,000). The market clears at ₫7.0 billion — the highest price the marginal buyer will pay. Now 30 wealthy foreign buyers arrive, each happy to pay ₫9.0 billion (≈ \$347,000) because to them this is cheap, stable, offshore parking.

Supply is still 100 units. Demand is now 130 bidders. The 30 highest bids — the foreigners at ₫9.0 billion — win the top 30 units, and the marginal *winning* local now has to bid above the 70th-ranked local to get one of the remaining units. The clearing price for the whole building drifts up toward ₫9.0 billion. Thirty locals who could pay ₫7.0 billion are now shut out entirely. No new apartment was built; 30 homes simply changed which wallet they answer to, at a 29% higher price.

*Foreign buying does not enlarge the housing supply — it enlarges the demand bidding for a fixed supply, and in a tight market that lands almost entirely in the price.*

The worked example is deliberately stylised — real markets have many buildings, new construction, and substitution. But the core is exactly right: **direct foreign buying adds demand to a fixed stock, and in supply-constrained cities that shows up as price.** This is the empirical reason Sydney, Vancouver, Auckland, Singapore, and Hong Kong all eventually slapped surcharges or bans on foreign buyers. We will see those real episodes later.

#### Worked example: Singapore's 60% ABSD on a foreign buyer

Let us put real numbers on the tax tool. Mr. Kang, our Korean professional, finds a Singapore condo he likes for S\$3,000,000 (≈ \$2.22 million). As a foreigner, he pays the **Additional Buyer's Stamp Duty of 60%** of the price — *on top of* the standard Buyer's Stamp Duty (which scales up to ~6% on the top slice) and on top of the price itself.

- Price: S\$3,000,000
- ABSD at 60%: S\$3,000,000 × 0.60 = **S\$1,800,000**
- Standard BSD (roughly ~6% on the upper portion, ≈ S\$164,000 here): **≈ S\$164,000**
- Total upfront tax: ≈ **S\$1,964,000**, about **65% of the price**

So Mr. Kang must write a cheque of roughly S\$4.96 million to take ownership of a S\$3 million flat. For the deal to break even, the property must appreciate ~65% *just to recover the tax* before he sees a cent of real gain — and Singapore property does not casually rise 65%. The tax does not forbid the purchase; it makes the economics absurd for all but the most committed buyer. That is the point: a 60% surcharge is a near-total firewall against foreign residential demand at the top of the market.

*A purchase tax does not ban the foreign buyer — it prices them out by turning a 65% tax bill into a hurdle the property has to clear before the buyer makes a dollar.*

The contrast with the cap is instructive. A tax says "pay this and you may proceed"; a cap says "you may not proceed past this share, at any price." The tax raises revenue and lets the rich foreigner in if they truly want; the cap holds the *quantity* of foreign ownership down regardless of willingness to pay. Vietnam, as we will now see, chose the cap.

## Vietnam's foreign-ownership rules: the 30% cap and the 50-year lease

Vietnam's approach to channel one is a *quantity cap plus a weaker form of title*, and it is worth understanding precisely because it is so different from the tax model.

The legal backbone is the **Law on Housing** (the 2014 framework, carried forward and refined in the 2023 Housing Law effective from 2024) and the **Land Law 2024** (effective August 2024), which together set out what foreigners may and may not own. The core rules a foreign individual faces:

- **They may buy and own apartments** (and a limited number of landed houses in permitted projects) in *commercial housing developments* — but **not** land-use rights directly, and not housing in areas restricted for security or defence reasons.
- **A 30% cap per building.** Foreign individuals and organisations together may own at most **~30% of the total apartment units in any one apartment building**. For landed houses in a given ward-level area, the cap is tighter (a fixed number of units, often cited around 250 houses or 10% of a project's houses, whichever applies).
- **A 50-year leasehold, not freehold.** A foreign individual's ownership runs for a **50-year term** from the date on the certificate, extendable in principle but never the indefinite right a citizen enjoys, and never including the land itself.
- **Payment and repatriation rules.** Purchases must generally be funded through the Vietnamese banking system, and there are foreign-exchange rules governing how sale proceeds can later be converted and repatriated.

Notice what this combination does. The **30% cap** mechanically limits how much of any building's price can be set by foreign demand — at most 30% of the units can be in foreign hands, so the other 70% always clears at *local* willingness to pay. The **50-year lease** makes the asset structurally less attractive to a foreigner than to a local, because it is a wasting right — every year that passes, there is one fewer year left on the clock. Together they keep the foreign channel small, contained, and tilted toward locals — without raising a single dollar of punitive tax.

This is a deliberately *light-touch* version of the firewall. Singapore's 60% tax is a sledgehammer aimed at killing foreign demand outright. Vietnam's 30% cap is more of a fence — foreigners are welcome up to a point, then the gate closes. The difference reflects priorities: Singapore, a tiny rich city-state, treats foreign buying as a pure threat to local affordability; Vietnam, a fast-growing emerging market, *wants* some foreign residential demand (it brings money and signals confidence) but refuses to let it dominate.

#### Worked example: the 30% cap binding on a building's units

Take a new HCMC tower with **200 apartments**. The 30% cap means foreigners may own at most **0.30 × 200 = 60 units**. Suppose foreign demand is strong — 90 foreign buyers want in, each willing to pay ₫6.0 billion (≈ \$232,000), while locals will pay up to ₫5.0 billion (≈ \$193,000).

Without the cap, the 90 highest bids would be the foreigners, and the building's clearing price would be pinned near ₫6.0 billion — locals largely shut out. *With* the cap, only **60** foreigners can be allotted units. The remaining 30 foreign buyers are turned away regardless of their willingness to pay; the other 140 units must clear at *local* demand, near ₫5.0 billion. So the cap holds 70% of the building at the local price and limits the foreign price-pull to a contained slice.

Run the developer's math: 60 units sold to foreigners at ₫6.0 billion = ₫360 billion; 140 units to locals at ₫5.0 billion = ₫700 billion; total ₫1,060 billion (≈ \$40.9 million). Had the cap not existed and all 200 cleared near the foreign price of ₫6.0 billion, the developer would have grossed ₫1,200 billion (≈ \$46.3 million). The cap costs the developer ~₫140 billion of gross revenue — which is precisely why developers lobby against tight caps, and precisely why the State keeps them: the cap transfers value from "highest global bidder" back toward "local buyer."

*A 30% ownership cap is a quantity firewall — it lets foreign money set the price on at most three units in ten, and forces the other seven to clear at what locals can actually pay.*

There is a second-order effect worth flagging. Because the foreign 30% is the scarce, premium slice, developers often *price it separately* — selling the foreign-eligible units first, at a premium, to lock in hard-currency-adjacent demand, then releasing the rest to locals. So the cap does not eliminate the foreign price-pull; it concentrates it into a defined, marketable tranche. The cap contains the foreign channel; it does not make it disappear.

## FDI into development: foreign money that builds

Now cross to the second channel. Instead of a foreigner buying a *finished* home, picture foreign capital funding the *creation* of new property — taking a stake in a development project, a joint venture with a local developer, or building a project outright. This is **FDI into real-estate development**, and it behaves very differently from direct buying.

The first difference: **it adds supply.** A foreign-funded residential or commercial project produces *new* floorspace — apartments, offices, malls that did not exist before. Where direct buying competes for a fixed stock and lifts prices, development FDI *expands* the stock. In a supply-starved city, that is, if anything, *helpful* for affordability over time, not harmful. This is exactly why Australia's foreign-buyer rules steer foreign money toward *new* dwellings: a foreigner generally may buy a brand-new apartment (because it adds to supply) but is barred from buying an established home (which would just compete for existing stock). The policy is engineered to convert foreign demand into new construction rather than price pressure.

The second difference: **it is far stickier than a portfolio flow.** A development takes years — land, permits, construction, sales. A foreign developer who has sunk capital into a half-built tower cannot wire it home when sentiment wobbles; they are committed. So development FDI tends to be patient capital, less prone to the hot-money whipsaw — though it carries its own risk, which is that a foreign developer who *over*-commits near a top can be caught with unsold inventory when the cycle turns (the leverage and presale mechanics of which are covered in [credit and developer financing](/blog/trading/real-estate/credit-and-developer-financing)).

The third difference: **it transfers skills and standards.** Foreign developers, hotel operators, and logistics specialists bring construction techniques, project-management discipline, and quality benchmarks that ripple through the local industry. A Japanese or Singaporean developer's joint venture in HCMC raises the bar for everyone — this is part of why governments court development FDI rather than fearing it.

That said, development FDI is not an unalloyed good. A flood of foreign-funded luxury supply can be misallocated — building high-end towers a local market cannot absorb, leaving "see-through buildings" of empty units (a hallmark of late-cycle excess, vividly on display in parts of China's property bust). And foreign capital, even when sunk, can stop *flowing*: the spigot of *new* commitments can shut overnight when global risk appetite turns, starving a development pipeline mid-stream. So development FDI is stickier than portfolio money but not immune to the cycle.

Still, of the two channels, development FDI is the one governments want — and within development FDI, one segment stands above the rest for Vietnam.

## The industrial and logistics boom: Vietnam's structural bright spot

If you remember one thing about foreign capital and Vietnamese property, make it this: **the most important foreign-capital story is not foreigners buying apartments — it is foreigners building factories.** Industrial and logistics property, powered by the manufacturing-FDI wave, is Vietnam's structural bright spot, and it runs on a tailwind with a name: **China plus one**.

![A bar-and-line chart showing Vietnam registered foreign direct investment rising from about twenty-one billion dollars in 2020 toward roughly thirty-eight billion in 2024 while industrial land rent rises about sixty percent over the same window, with a shaded band marking the China plus one acceleration](/imgs/blogs/foreign-capital-and-fdi-in-property-2.png)

### What "China plus one" means

For two decades, the world's factory was China. But rising Chinese wages, US–China trade tensions and tariffs, and the supply-chain shocks of the pandemic taught multinationals a hard lesson: depending on a *single* country for manufacturing is fragile. The response is **"China plus one"** — keep a China base, but add *one more* manufacturing location to diversify the risk. Vietnam, with its long coastline, young workforce, low wages, political stability, and a web of free-trade agreements, became the prime "plus one."

The result is a sustained wave of **manufacturing FDI** into Vietnam — electronics (Samsung, LG, Foxconn assembling for Apple), footwear and apparel, furniture, and increasingly higher-value components. Registered FDI rose from the low-\$20-billions a year in 2020 toward the high-\$30-billions by 2024 (illustrative order of magnitude; the chart above flags it as such), with *manufacturing and processing* the dominant share. That is the engine. The industrial-property boom is its exhaust.

### Why factory FDI pulls property up

Here is the chain — the manufacturing-FDI → industrial-real-estate link, made concrete. When a manufacturer commits to a Vietnamese plant, it sets off a cascade of property demand, each link feeding the next.

![A six-step pipeline showing how a factory FDI decision creates demand for industrial land, then a built factory, then logistics and warehousing, then worker housing, then retail and services](/imgs/blogs/foreign-capital-and-fdi-in-property-5.png)

1. **Industrial land demand.** The manufacturer needs a serviced plot — flat, powered, water-connected, road-linked — usually inside an industrial park or special economic zone. They lease it (often a 50-year lease, the same horizon as foreign residential title) from an industrial-park developer.
2. **Factory built or leased.** Many manufacturers do not build; they rent a **ready-built factory (RBF)** from a developer who specialises in putting up standard factory shells on spec. The developer earns the rent; the manufacturer gets to start production in months, not years.
3. **Logistics and warehousing.** A factory imports parts and exports finished goods, so it needs warehouses, distribution centres, and cold storage near the port and the highway. This is the *logistics* segment, and it has boomed alongside the factories.
4. **Worker housing.** A plant employing tens of thousands needs those workers housed nearby. That lifts demand for affordable residential and dormitory housing in the surrounding province — a separate, often under-supplied segment.
5. **Retail and services.** Workers spend money. Shops, clinics, schools, and services spring up around the park, and a town grows where there was farmland. The industrial-park developer often captures this upside too, through mixed-use master plans.

One factory-investment decision, five distinct kinds of property demand. *This is the multiplier that makes industrial FDI Vietnam's structural bright spot:* it is not a single building, it is an ecosystem, and the foreign capital that triggers it is *sticky* — a manufacturer with a billion dollars of sunk plant is not fleeing on a bad headline.

#### Worked example: an industrial-park developer leasing to a manufacturer

The developer owns 100 hectares of serviced industrial land in a northern Vietnamese park. A foreign electronics maker — call them the manufacturer — leases **10 hectares (100,000 m²)** for a 50-year term to build an assembly plant.

Suppose the industrial land lease is priced at **\$130 per m² for the full term** (an illustrative figure consistent with northern-Vietnam asking rents in the chart above). The developer collects:

- Land lease: 100,000 m² × \$130/m² = **\$13,000,000** for the 50-year right.

But the manufacturer also wants a ready-built factory shell on part of the plot, which they will *rent* monthly. Say a 40,000 m² factory at **\$5.00 per m² per month**:

- Factory rent: 40,000 m² × \$5.00 = **\$200,000 per month** = **\$2,400,000 per year**.

So from one tenant, the developer books \$13 million up front for the land right *plus* a \$2.4-million-a-year rental stream for the building — a stream that, capitalised at, say, a 9% yield, is worth roughly \$2.4m ÷ 0.09 ≈ **\$26.7 million** of asset value. The FDI decision by the manufacturer did not just "create a factory"; it created a multi-million-dollar, multi-decade income stream for a property developer, plus the logistics and housing demand that radiates outward.

*The link from manufacturing FDI to real estate is not abstract — it is a rent cheque: every factory a foreigner builds is a tenant a property developer collects from for decades.*

### Why this segment is more resilient

Industrial and logistics property is *structurally* more attractive than the residential foreign-buyer channel for three reasons. First, the demand is driven by **real economic activity** (making and moving goods), not by speculative store-of-value buying — so it is anchored to the manufacturing economy, not to sentiment. Second, the leases are **long and the tenants are sticky** — a manufacturer that has tooled a plant for a specific product line does not relocate on a whim. Third, it rides a **multi-year structural tailwind** (China plus one) rather than a cyclical sugar rush. That combination is why "industrial is boring and safe" is half right and half wrong, as we will see in the misconceptions section — boring, yes; *risk-free*, no.

For how this segment fits a broader portfolio — the income, leverage, and rate sensitivity of property as an asset — see [real estate, REITs, income, leverage and rates](/blog/trading/cross-asset/real-estate-reits-income-leverage-and-rates).

## Hot money: how foreign capital amplifies the cycle

Now the dark side of the first channel — and of portfolio flows generally. Foreign capital, especially the fast, liquid kind, does not behave like a patient local owner. It chases returns, and that chasing makes property cycles wider and more violent.

![A branching diagram showing foreign sentiment as a switch, with greed driving capital to flood in and overshoot prices up, and fear driving capital to flee and overshoot prices down](/imgs/blogs/foreign-capital-and-fdi-in-property-4.png)

The dynamic runs as a feedback loop, sketched above. Start near the top of a boom. Prices have been rising; the story is everywhere; global yield-chasers and momentum buyers — the hot money — pile in late, because that is what hot money does, it follows the trend. Their buying is the *last and biggest* wave of demand, and it pushes prices *past* what local incomes and rents could ever justify: an **overshoot to the upside**. Locals, watching foreigners bid up their city, conclude that prices "can only go up" — the classic top-of-cycle psychology covered in [the anatomy of a property bubble and crash](/blog/trading/real-estate/anatomy-of-a-property-bubble-and-crash).

Then something cracks — a rate hike, a policy change, a global risk-off shock. Sentiment turns. And here is the asymmetry: the hot money that took months to flood in can *flee in days*. Foreign portfolio holders sell their listed property shares, dump their bonds, pull their deposits, and wire the money home. Foreign buyers vanish from the auction rooms. With the marginal buyer gone and everyone trying to exit through the same narrow door, prices do not just fall to fair value — they **overshoot to the downside**, below what fundamentals justify, because there is no one left to bid. The same capital that amplified the boom now amplifies the bust.

The deep reason is **stickiness, or the lack of it**. Hot money has no sunk cost anchoring it, so it leaves the instant the expected return turns negative. And because *everyone's* hot money does the math at the same time, the exit is a stampede. This is why economists distinguish so sharply between FDI (welcome, stabilising) and short-term portfolio inflows (useful but destabilising) — and why emerging markets, Vietnam included, watch the *composition* of their foreign capital, not just the headline total.

#### Worked example: hot money inflating then fleeing a market

Take a market where fair value — what local incomes, rents, and a normal cost of capital justify — is **₫5.0 billion (≈ \$193,000)** for a typical unit. In the boom, foreign hot money floods in and bids prices to **₫8.0 billion (≈ \$309,000)** — a 60% overshoot above fair value. Minh, our local, looks at this and despairs; the math no longer makes any sense, but prices keep rising, so he assumes he is the one who is wrong.

Then rates rise abroad and risk appetite collapses. The hot money flees. Foreign buyers disappear and foreign holders sell. With the marginal buyer gone, the price does not settle gently back to ₫5.0 billion — it overshoots *down* to **₫3.5 billion (≈ \$135,000)**, 30% *below* fair value, because in a panic there are sellers and no buyers.

Tally the swing. A foreigner who bought near the top at ₫8.0 billion and was forced to sell in the panic at ₫3.5 billion lost **₫4.5 billion, about 56% of their money** — before any currency loss. Minh, who never bought because the math never worked, can now buy the *same* unit at ₫3.5 billion — *below* its fair value of ₫5.0 billion. The hot money manufactured both the unaffordable top *and*, by fleeing, the bargain bottom.

*Hot money does not just push prices around fair value — it overshoots both ways, so the same flow that makes a market unaffordable at the top hands the patient local a bargain at the bottom.*

The contrast with sticky FDI is the whole point, and it deserves its own picture.

![A two-panel diagram contrasting fickle hot money that exits in days and amplifies a bust against sticky foreign direct investment in a built factory that is locked in and cushions the same shock](/imgs/blogs/foreign-capital-and-fdi-in-property-6.png)

When sentiment turns, the two channels do opposite things. The **portfolio investor** holds a liquid claim — a listed share, a deposit — with no sunk cost, so they sell with one click, wire the money home in days, and their selling *amplifies* the bust. The **direct investor** holds a built factory: poured concrete, installed machines, a fifty-year lease, signed supply contracts. They *cannot* wire a factory abroad. Exiting would mean abandoning a billion dollars of sunk capital, so they stay, keep producing, and keep paying rent — *cushioning* the shock rather than deepening it. Same country, same downturn, opposite behaviour, and the difference is entirely about stickiness.

This is why Vietnam's tilt toward sticky industrial FDI is genuinely reassuring, while its exposure to fast portfolio flows (foreign holdings of listed property developers and corporate bonds) is the part that can whipsaw. We saw exactly this whipsaw in the 2022–23 bond crunch — discussed in the real-markets section.

## Currency and repatriation risk: the return you can actually keep

A foreign investor's headline return is a lie until two more things are accounted for: **what the currency did**, and **whether they can get the money out**. Both can turn a paper gain into a real loss.

### Currency risk

A foreign investor earns in the local currency but lives in their home one. If the local currency weakens between buying and selling, the foreigner loses purchasing power even if the local *price* rose. For emerging markets like Vietnam, this risk is real and persistent: the Vietnamese đồng has a long-run tendency to depreciate gently against the US dollar (the USD/VND rate has drifted from the low-23,000s a few years ago toward roughly 25,900 by 2025–26), reflecting inflation differentials and policy. A few percent a year of depreciation, compounded, quietly eats a foreign investor's return.

#### Worked example: currency risk eroding a foreign buyer's return

Mr. Kang, our Korean buyer, buys a Saigon apartment for **₫6.0 billion** when the rate is **₫24,000 per USD**. In dollars, he put in 6,000,000,000 ÷ 24,000 = **\$250,000**.

Five years later, the apartment has risen a healthy **25%** in local terms, to **₫7.5 billion**. In *đồng*, Mr. Kang looks like a winner. But the đồng has weakened to **₫27,000 per USD** over those five years (a ~12.5% depreciation against the dollar). When he sells and repatriates:

- Sale proceeds: ₫7.5 billion ÷ 27,000 = **\$277,778**.
- Dollar gain: \$277,778 − \$250,000 = **\$27,778**, or about **+11.1%** over five years (≈ 2.1% a year).

So a 25% *local* gain became an 11% *dollar* gain — the currency quietly ate more than half of it. Now flip the scenario: had the đồng instead weakened to ₫30,000 per USD (a sharper ~25% depreciation), the same ₫7.5 billion would convert to just \$250,000 — Mr. Kang's *entire* 25% local gain wiped out, leaving him exactly flat in dollars despite a "winning" property.

*For a foreign buyer, the property is only half the bet — the other half is the currency, and in an emerging market the currency can erase a perfectly good local gain.*

This cuts both ways, of course: a foreigner whose *home* currency weakens, or who buys into a strengthening local currency, gets a tailwind. But for emerging-market property bought by hard-currency investors, the base case is a headwind, and a sophisticated investor either hedges it (at a cost) or demands a higher local return to compensate.

### Repatriation risk

Currency risk assumes you *can* convert and send the money home. Repatriation risk is the chance that you *cannot* — or not on the terms you expected. Capital controls, foreign-exchange rationing in a crisis, new taxes on outflows, or simply a banking system that runs short of dollars can trap a foreign investor's proceeds in the local currency. In a genuine balance-of-payments crisis, a government may *freeze* repatriation entirely to defend its reserves — exactly when a foreign investor most wants out.

Vietnam's rules are relatively orderly — sale proceeds can generally be converted and repatriated through the banking system, subject to documentation and tax — but the *risk* of tightening always exists, and it is precisely this risk that makes foreign capital demand a premium for emerging-market property. The investor is pricing in not just the property and the currency, but the small-but-real chance the exit door is harder to open than the entrance was.

Repatriation risk is also the deep reason hot money is *hot*. Sophisticated portfolio investors, knowing the exit can slam shut in a crisis, sell *early* and *fast* at the first sign of trouble — better to leave at a loss while you still can than to be trapped. That pre-emptive flight is itself a cause of the downside overshoot. The fear of not being able to leave makes everyone leave sooner.

## Common misconceptions

A handful of beliefs about foreign capital are so common, and so wrong, that correcting them is most of the value of this post.

**"Foreign buyers cause all the price rises."** No. Foreign *direct buyers* add demand to a fixed stock and can push prices up *at the margin* — real, but bounded, especially where a cap like Vietnam's 30% holds most of the market at local prices. The dominant drivers of property prices are local: interest rates and mortgage availability ([the master variable](/blog/trading/real-estate/interest-rates-and-mortgages-the-master-variable)), local incomes and credit, supply constraints, and the credit cycle ([the credit cycle and property prices](/blog/trading/real-estate/the-credit-cycle-and-property-prices)). Foreign buyers are a convenient political scapegoat precisely because they are visible and can't vote; blaming them lets politicians avoid the harder local fixes (build more, ease zoning, manage credit). In most markets, foreign residential buying is a *contributor* near the top, not the *cause*.

**"FDI and hot money are the same thing — it's all just 'foreign investment.'"** This is the costliest error, and the whole post is built to correct it. FDI is *sticky* — sunk into factories, parks, and developments that cannot flee overnight, and that *cushion* a downturn. Hot money is *fickle* — liquid claims with no sunk cost that *amplify* the cycle and stampede for the exit in a panic. A country with a high ratio of sticky FDI to hot money is far more stable than one with the reverse, even at the same headline level of "foreign capital." When you read that "foreign investment is pouring in," the only question that matters is *which kind*.

**"Industrial property is boring and therefore safe."** Half true. Industrial and logistics property is *less* speculative than the residential foreign-buyer game — it is anchored to real production and long leases, and Vietnam's China-plus-one tailwind is structural. But it is not risk-free. It is *concentrated* (a handful of anchor tenants, a few key export markets), *cyclical* (a global manufacturing slowdown cuts factory demand), and *exposed to the same China-plus-one logic that lifted it* — if a "Vietnam plus one" diversification wave moves the next factory to India or Indonesia, or if automation shrinks the workforce a plant needs, the demand can soften. "Boring" describes the day-to-day; it does not mean the cycle has been repealed.

**"Capping or taxing foreign buyers will fix affordability."** It helps at the margin and is politically popular, but it is not a cure. If a city is unaffordable mainly because of low rates, abundant local credit, and a planning system that won't allow new supply, then removing foreign buyers shifts the price down a little and the underlying squeeze remains. Singapore's 60% ABSD cooled the *foreign* top end sharply, but Singapore housing is still expensive because of local demand and scarce land. The foreign-buyer lever is real but small relative to the local fundamentals.

**"A weak local currency is always bad for foreign property investors."** It depends entirely on *which side* of the trade you are on and *when*. A foreigner who already *owns* property in a country whose currency then weakens loses on repatriation — bad. But a foreigner looking to *buy* into a country whose currency has *already* weakened gets the asset cheaper in their home currency — a tailwind. Currency is a two-sided risk, not a one-way curse, and timing relative to the FX move is everything.

**"Foreign capital flooding in is unambiguously good for a country."** Inflows feel great — rising prices, busy construction, a confident market. But hot inflows sow the bust they later cause: they inflate the boom, build the overhang, and then flee, leaving a deeper crash than the fundamentals warranted. The *quality* and *stickiness* of the capital matter more than the quantity. A torrent of hot money is a liability dressed as a blessing; a steady stream of sticky FDI is the real prize.

## How it shows up in real markets

Abstractions are cheap. Here is the mechanism in real, named, datable episodes — Vietnam first, then the global canon.

### Vietnam's industrial-FDI boom (2018–2026)

This is the textbook case of the *good* channel. As US–China tensions and pandemic shocks drove the China-plus-one shift, manufacturing FDI poured into Vietnam — Samsung alone has invested well over \$20 billion cumulatively and at times accounted for a fifth of Vietnam's exports; Foxconn, LG, Pegatron, and others followed, assembling electronics for Apple and the world. Registered FDI climbed from the low-\$20-billions a year toward the high-\$30-billions by the mid-2020s (order-of-magnitude figures), with manufacturing the dominant share. The property echo was a sustained industrial and logistics boom: industrial-park occupancy in the key northern and southern clusters ran high, and industrial land asking rents rose materially — illustratively on the order of ~60% over the early-2020s window (the chart earlier in this post sketches that shape). Listed industrial-park developers became some of the most sought-after property plays precisely because their demand was anchored to *factories*, not to speculative home-buying. This is sticky FDI doing what it is supposed to do: building capacity, employing people, and generating decades-long rent streams.

### Vietnam's 2022–23 hot-money whipsaw

The flip side showed up almost simultaneously, in the *portfolio* channel. Vietnam's corporate-bond market — heavily used by property developers — had attracted yield-chasing capital during the easy-money years. When the Tân Hoàng Minh arrests (April 2022) and the Vạn Thịnh Phát / SCB scandal (October 2022) hit, confidence cracked and the bond market *froze*: new issuance collapsed, investors rushed to redeem, and developers who had relied on rolling over bonds were suddenly starved of funding. This was hot-money dynamics in the financing layer — capital that had flooded in chasing yield now fled or refused to roll, amplifying the property freeze. The State Bank had to cut policy rates four times in 2023 and Decree 08 was issued to give bond-restructuring relief. The contrast with the industrial-FDI story is the lesson in one country: the sticky factory capital kept humming through the same months that the fickle bond capital seized up.

### Sydney and the foreign-buyer wave (2010s → 2025 ban)

Australia is the textbook of the *tax-and-steer* model on channel one. Through the 2010s, a wave of foreign (heavily Chinese) buyers poured into Sydney and Melbourne, and prices roared. The policy response layered up: the Foreign Investment Review Board (FIRB) approval requirement and fees; state-level *foreign-buyer stamp-duty surcharges* (8% in New South Wales, rising elsewhere); and the long-standing rule that foreigners may buy *new* dwellings (adding supply) but not *established* homes. By 2025, with affordability still raw, Australia went further and imposed a temporary **ban on foreign buyers purchasing established homes (2025–2027)**. The arc is instructive: a country that started by taxing and steering foreign demand toward new supply ended up banning it from existing stock — the cap logic and the tax logic converging.

### Vancouver and Canada's foreign-buyer ban

Vancouver is the cautionary tale of *unchecked* channel one. Through the early 2010s, foreign capital — much of it parking offshore wealth, often leaving units empty — drove Vancouver prices to among the least affordable on Earth relative to local incomes. The policy escalation followed the now-familiar ladder: a **15% foreign-buyer tax (2016)**, later raised to 20%; an **empty-homes tax** to punish units left dark; and ultimately a **national two-year ban on foreign buyers (effective 2023, later extended to 2027)**. The "leave the lights off" complaint — foreigners buying as a store of value, not to live — is exactly the price-insensitive demand we described in the foundations, and Vancouver is what it looks like when a city lets it run before reaching for the firewall.

### Singapore's ABSD firewall (2011 → 60% in 2023)

Singapore built the most aggressive *tax* firewall in the world, in escalating steps. The ABSD began modestly in 2011, then ratcheted: foreigners faced 15%, then 20%, then 30%, and finally — in April 2023 — a flat **60%** surcharge on any residential purchase. As our worked example showed, a 60% tax plus standard duty pushes the total upfront tax toward two-thirds of the price, which is a near-total wall against foreign residential demand at the top. The effect was exactly as intended: foreign buying at the luxury end fell sharply. But — proving the misconception above — Singapore housing remained expensive, because local demand and scarce land, not foreigners, are the dominant drivers. The tax killed the *foreign* contribution; it did not kill the price.

### The 1997 Asian financial crisis: hot money's masterclass in amplification

The defining historical lesson on hot money is the **1997–98 Asian financial crisis**. Through the mid-1990s, short-term foreign capital flooded into Thailand, Indonesia, Malaysia, and South Korea, much of it funding property and construction booms. It was *hot* — short-term, dollar-denominated, unanchored. When confidence cracked in mid-1997, it fled all at once: currencies collapsed (the Thai baht and Indonesian rupiah lost more than half their value), property markets crashed, and the repatriation door slammed as reserves ran dry. The capital that had inflated the boom *manufactured* the bust by fleeing — and the currency collapse meant foreign investors who *couldn't* get out fast lost on the asset *and* the đồng-equivalent at once. It is the clearest historical proof that the *composition* of foreign capital — sticky FDI vs hot portfolio money — can be the difference between a soft landing and a catastrophe, and it is why every emerging market since, Vietnam included, watches its short-term inflows with a wary eye.

### China's developer-FDI reversal (2021→)

A final, more recent case bridges both channels. China's property sector had attracted enormous foreign capital — into both developer bonds (a portfolio/hot-money channel) and development. When Beijing's "three red lines" deleveraging campaign hit in 2020–21 and Evergrande began to default in 2021, foreign capital in the *bond* channel fled hard: offshore developer bonds that had traded near par collapsed toward cents on the dollar, and the spigot of new foreign funding to Chinese developers shut almost completely. The sticky, sunk development capital was trapped in half-built towers; the fickle bond capital fled. China's bust is, among many other things, a demonstration that even a giant economy is not immune to the hot-money exit dynamic when the underlying credit was built on capital that could leave.

## When this matters / Further reading

This matters the moment you stop reading "foreign capital" as one thing. The next time a headline announces that foreign investment is "flooding into" — or "fleeing from" — a property market, you now have the one question that cuts through it: *which channel?* Direct buyers competing for finished homes, who add demand and get capped and taxed? Or FDI into development and industrial property, which adds supply and capacity and tends to stay? And the follow-on: *how sticky is it, and what happens when it reverses?* Sticky FDI cushions a downturn; fickle hot money deepens it.

For Vietnam specifically, the practical reading is hopeful but precise. The country's *best* foreign-capital story is the industrial and logistics boom riding the China-plus-one wave — sticky, structural, anchored to real factories, and generating decades of rent. The *managed* story is residential foreign buying, deliberately kept small by the 30% cap and the 50-year lease so that locals keep the majority of any building. And the *risky* story is the portfolio channel — foreign holdings of listed developers and corporate bonds — which can whipsaw, as 2022–23 proved. Three kinds of money, three behaviours; never lump them together.

![A grid showing Vietnam's three foreign-capital channels as separate cells, industrial FDI that is sticky and structural, residential buyers that are capped and managed, and portfolio flows that are fickle and fast](/imgs/blogs/foreign-capital-and-fdi-in-property-7.png)

That grid is the picture to carry away: in Vietnam's foreign-capital landscape, industrial FDI is the sticky structural bright spot, residential foreign buying is a small contained channel, and portfolio flows are the fickle hot money that can leave overnight. If you are a local like Minh, the comfort is that the cap and the leasehold genuinely hold most of the residential market at local prices — and that the part of foreign capital that matters most for Vietnam's future is the part that builds factories, not the part that bids for your apartment.

None of this is financial advice; it is a mechanism.
