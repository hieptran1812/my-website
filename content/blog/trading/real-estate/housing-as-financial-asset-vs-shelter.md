> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A home is two things at war with each other: **shelter** (a place to live, a consumption good you use up) and a **financial asset** (an investment you want to appreciate). Those two roles want opposite things, and which one a society favors decides almost everything about its housing market.
>
> - As shelter, you want housing **cheap and plentiful**; as an asset, you want it **scarce and rising**. The same object, opposite incentives.
> - When a society tilts toward housing-as-asset, **prices detach from incomes**, the young are locked out, family formation slows, and wealth concentrates with people who already own.
> - Because owners are usually the **voting majority**, policy tends to protect prices — which is why affordability rarely improves by policy alone, even when everyone agrees it is a problem.
> - The one number to hold onto: a home in **Ho Chi Minh City costs roughly 32 years of the median income** (some surveys say 34) — one of the most asset-tilted markets on Earth, versus an affordable benchmark of about 3 to 5 years.

Minh is 29, works in software in Ho Chi Minh City, and has done everything the older generation told him to do. He finished his degree, found a stable job, saved a chunk of his salary every month. And he still cannot buy the modest 70-square-metre apartment he and his partner want to start a family in. The asking price is around ₫7 billion (≈ \$270,000). His household earns maybe ₫450 million a year (≈ \$17,000). Do the division and the apartment costs him **more than 15 years of his entire pre-tax income** — and that is for a couple with two good salaries. For the city's median household, the same flat is closer to **32 years of income**. Minh is not bad with money. He is standing on the wrong side of a structural fact about what a home has become.

A few kilometres away, Minh's aunt bought a similar apartment in 2014 for about ₫1.5 billion. She borrowed most of it, lived in it the whole time, and the price is now somewhere north of ₫5 billion. She did not work harder than Minh, time the market, or have special insight. She simply owned a home while the country's housing turned, over a decade, from a thing you live in into a thing you store wealth in. Her apartment did her job for her.

This is the deepest theme under everything else in this series — under [cap rates](/blog/trading/real-estate/cap-rate-noi-and-the-income-approach), under [leverage](/blog/trading/real-estate/leverage-and-the-mortgage-how-debt-amplifies-property), under [cycles](/blog/trading/real-estate/anatomy-of-a-real-estate-cycle-four-phases), under [tax](/blog/trading/real-estate/how-real-estate-is-taxed-the-full-map). A home is simultaneously **shelter** — something you consume by living in it — and a **financial asset** — something you hold and want to grow. Those two roles are not just different; they are in direct conflict. The diagram above is the mental model for the whole piece: one house, two opposite jobs, pulling the market in opposite directions.

![One house framed two ways: as shelter where you want it cheap and plentiful, and as a financial asset where you want it scarce and rising](/imgs/blogs/housing-as-financial-asset-vs-shelter-1.png)

This is not a moral story about greedy owners and virtuous renters. Minh's aunt did nothing wrong. The tension is built into the object itself, and almost every adult sits on both sides of it at different points in life — a renter who desperately wants prices to fall, who then becomes an owner who quietly hopes they never do. The goal of this post is to make that tension legible: to define the two roles from zero, show exactly where they collide, and trace what happens to a society when the asset role wins. By the end you will have a single lens — *is this market treating homes mainly as shelter or mainly as an asset?* — that explains affordability crises, birth-rate worries, wealth gaps, and the strange political stickiness of high house prices, in Vietnam and almost everywhere else.

## Foundations: the two roles of a home

Before we can talk about the conflict, we have to define both roles cleanly. A home wears two hats, and each hat comes with its own vocabulary from economics. Let us build them from the ground up.

### A consumption good: shelter you use up

A **consumption good** is something you buy in order to use it up — food, electricity, a haircut, a night in a hotel. You pay, you consume the benefit, and the thing is (partly or wholly) gone. The defining feature is that the *using* is the point.

Housing, viewed as shelter, is exactly this. You need a roof, walls, a place to sleep, somewhere to keep your family warm and dry and safe. Living in a home consumes its services month by month, the same way driving consumes a car or eating consumes a meal. Economists call the stream of benefit you get from living in a dwelling its **housing services** — the warmth, the security, the location near your job and your kids' school. When you rent, you pay cash for that stream directly: rent is the price of one month of housing services. When you own, you still consume the same stream — you just do not write a rent cheque for it.

The crucial property of a consumption good is its **use value**: how much the using is worth to you. A home's use value is high and steady — everyone needs shelter, every month, for their whole life. It does not depend on whether prices are rising or falling. A house worth ₫3 billion and a house worth ₫9 billion can deliver the *same* shelter. Use value is about the living, not the price tag.

### A financial asset: a store of value you want to grow

A **financial asset** is something you hold not to use up but to *store and grow wealth*. A share of stock, a bond, a gold bar, a savings account — you buy them expecting to get back more later, or at least to preserve what you put in. The defining feature is the opposite of a consumption good: the *holding and appreciating* is the point, and ideally you never "use it up" at all.

Housing, viewed as an asset, is one of the largest stores of wealth on the planet. (Globally, residential real estate is worth on the order of **\$300 trillion**, more than all stocks and bonds combined — the single biggest asset class there is, as we covered in [what real estate is as an asset class](/blog/trading/real-estate/what-is-real-estate-as-an-asset-class).) When you own a home as an asset you care about its **exchange value**: what you could sell or borrow against it for. You want that number to rise. You watch comparable sales the way a stockholder watches the share price. You feel richer when prices climb and poorer when they fall — even though the house you live in is physically identical either way.

Three asset roles stack on top of each other here, and it is worth naming each:

- **Appreciation.** The price goes up over time, so holding the home is itself a return. If you bought at ₫1.5 billion and it is now ₫5 billion, you have made ₫3.5 billion by doing nothing but holding.
- **Store of value.** Housing tends to hold its real (inflation-adjusted) worth over long periods, which is why people across cultures park savings in property rather than watch cash erode. In a country with a history of currency instability, "put it in land" is folk wisdom precisely because land is a store of value.
- **Leverage vehicle.** Because you can borrow most of the purchase price with a mortgage, housing lets you control a large asset with a small slice of your own money — which magnifies the appreciation enormously. We will quantify this below; it is the engine that makes housing-as-asset so powerful and so seductive.

### Imputed rent: the hidden bridge between the two roles

There is one concept that lives exactly on the seam between shelter and asset, and most people have never heard its name: **imputed rent**.

Imputed rent is the rent an owner-occupier *implicitly* pays to themselves. Here is the idea. If you own your home outright and live in it, you are getting housing services for free in cash terms — no rent cheque leaves your account. But economically, you *are* paying: you are forgoing the rent you could have collected by letting the place out, and you are tying up capital in the home instead of earning a return elsewhere. The market value of the shelter you consume each month is your imputed rent. It is the use value, measured in money.

Why does this matter? Because it is the cleanest way to see the two roles separately. The **imputed rent is the shelter return** — the consumption value, what you would have paid to rent the same place. The **price change is the asset return** — the appreciation, the investment value. A homeowner earns *both at once*, and we will stack them up in a figure later. Imputed rent is also why national statisticians add a line to GDP for owner-occupied housing: people are "consuming" shelter even when no rent changes hands, and the economy has to count it.

### Price-to-income: the affordability gauge, recapped

We built this metric in detail in [income, affordability, and the price-to-income ratio](/blog/trading/real-estate/income-affordability-and-the-price-to-income-ratio), but it is the single most important number in this entire post, so let us recap it from zero.

The **price-to-income ratio** is simply the price of a representative home divided by a representative annual household income. It answers one brutally simple question: *how many years of your whole income would it take to buy a home?* If a home costs ₫6 billion and a household earns ₫600 million a year, the ratio is 10 — ten years of every cent you earn, before food, before tax, before anything.

The ratio is the thermometer for the shelter-vs-asset tension. When homes are treated mainly as shelter, prices stay tethered to what people earn, because shelter is bought out of income — so the ratio sits low, roughly **3 to 5** in genuinely affordable places. When homes are treated mainly as assets, prices float free of local incomes (buyers are bidding with savings, leverage, and the expectation of appreciation, not just their salaries), and the ratio climbs. Ho Chi Minh City sits around **32**; one widely-cited survey put it at **34 years of income, the 5th-highest in the world**. Hanoi is close behind at about 31. Hong Kong, the textbook asset-tilted market, hits **44**. The global median is roughly **15**.

Hold those numbers. A price-to-income of 5 versus 32 is not a small difference in degree. It is the difference between a market where shelter wins and a market where the asset role has taken over.

### The two demands, side by side

Here is the heart of the foundations. Housing faces **two distinct demands at the same time**: a *consumption demand* (people who want to live somewhere) and an *investment demand* (people who want to store and grow wealth). The same apartment can be bid on by a young couple who need a home and by an investor who wants a fourth property to park money in. Both push the price up — but they want completely different things from the market afterward.

![A matrix comparing what the shelter role and the asset role each want from price, supply, policy, and time horizon, showing they pull in opposite directions on every lever](/imgs/blogs/housing-as-financial-asset-vs-shelter-2.png)

Read the matrix one column at a time and the conflict becomes unavoidable. On **price**, shelter wants it low (near incomes, so people can afford to live); the asset wants it high (rising every year, so wealth grows). On **supply**, shelter wants more homes built (abundance makes housing cheap); the asset wants supply tight (scarcity keeps your home valuable). On **policy**, shelter wants loose zoning and renter support; the asset wants prices protected and holding taxes low. On **time horizon**, shelter is about *now* — you need a place to live today; the asset is about *later* — you hold for years and sell at a gain. Every single lever points in opposite directions depending on which hat the home is wearing. That is the tension, and everything that follows is a consequence of it.

## Use value vs exchange value: where the two roles collide

Economists have a pair of old terms for exactly this split, going back centuries to Adam Smith: **use value** and **exchange value**. Use value is how useful a thing is to you — how much you'd value actually using it. Exchange value is what you can trade it for in the market. For most goods the two roughly track each other. Housing is the great exception, and the gap between them is where all the drama lives.

Smith illustrated the split with his famous diamond-water paradox: water has enormous use value (you die without it) but almost no exchange value (it is nearly free), while a diamond has trivial use value but immense exchange value. Housing is strange because it carries *both* a high use value (everyone needs it, always) *and* a high, unbounded exchange value (everyone wants to store wealth in it). That double-high status is precisely what makes a home a contested object: it is one of the very few things that is at once a daily necessity and a premier investment. Bread is a necessity but a poor store of wealth; gold is a store of wealth but no one needs to eat it. A home is both, which is why the fight over it never ends — and why a society's housing policy is really a long argument about which of the two values gets to dominate the price.

The use value of a home is bounded. There is only so much shelter a family can consume. A 70 m² apartment shelters Minh's future family about as well whether the market prices it at ₫3 billion or ₫9 billion. Doubling the price does not give you twice the warmth, twice the security, twice the proximity to work. **Use value is anchored to human needs, which do not inflate.** A family in 2010 and a family in 2026 need roughly the same square metres, the same number of bedrooms.

The exchange value of a home, by contrast, is unbounded in principle. It is set by what buyers — *including investors* — are willing to pay, which depends on expected appreciation, available credit, the alternatives for parking savings, and the simple belief that prices will keep rising. Exchange value can detach from use value and keep climbing as long as money keeps flowing in expecting a return.

Here is the collision stated as plainly as I can. **When exchange value runs far ahead of use value, housing has become more asset than shelter.** The price-to-income ratio is precisely the gap between the two measured against income. A ratio of 5 means exchange value sits close to the use value an ordinary income can support. A ratio of 32 means exchange value has sprinted ahead — the price reflects the home's role as a store of wealth far more than its role as a place to live.

And the two roles do not just diverge; they actively undermine each other. Every dollar of investment demand that bids the price up makes the home a *worse* deal as shelter, because shelter is bought out of income and incomes did not rise. Every owner who hopes their home keeps appreciating is, whether they think of it this way or not, hoping that shelter stays expensive for the next person. The asset's wish — scarcity and rising prices — is the shelter-seeker's nightmare. You cannot maximise both at once. A society chooses, mostly without ever voting on it explicitly, which role wins.

#### Worked example: imputed rent vs the financial return on the same home

Let us pull the two returns apart on a single home so the tension is concrete. Take Minh's aunt's apartment, worth about ₫5 billion (≈ \$193,000) today.

**The shelter return (use value).** A comparable apartment in her building rents for roughly ₫10 million a month, or **₫120 million a year** (≈ \$4,600). That is her *imputed rent* — the value of the shelter she consumes by living there. As a percentage of the ₫5 billion value, the shelter yields about **2.4% a year**. This return shows up as something real but invisible: she does not pay rent to anyone, so she keeps that ₫120 million of housing services "for free" relative to a renter.

**The financial return (exchange value).** Over the past year, prices in her district rose roughly 8%, lifting her home's value by about **₫400 million** (≈ \$15,400). That is her appreciation — the asset return — and it is *more than three times larger* than the entire year's shelter value, even before we account for the leverage she used.

Notice what just happened. The thing she lives in returned **2.4% as shelter** and **8% as an asset** in the same year. The asset role dwarfed the shelter role. For Minh's aunt that feels wonderful. But that ₫400 million of appreciation is exactly what put the apartment ₫400 million further out of Minh's reach. *The price rise that made her richer as an owner made the same shelter more expensive for the next family — one number, two opposite meanings.*

## What high price-to-income does to a society

It is tempting to treat a high price-to-income ratio as just a number that makes buying annoying. It is much more than that. A persistently high ratio reshapes who can form a household, where wealth ends up, and even how many children get born. The mechanism is not mysterious; it is a chain of consequences that follows directly from shelter becoming unaffordable on an ordinary income.

![A branching diagram showing prices far above incomes lead to the young being locked out and owners getting richer, which in turn delays family formation and widens the wealth gap](/imgs/blogs/housing-as-financial-asset-vs-shelter-3.png)

Trace the figure. When **prices run far ahead of incomes**, two things happen at once. On one branch, the **young are locked out**: a deposit that used to take a few years to save now takes a decade or more, so people who do not already own simply cannot get in. On the other branch, **existing owners get richer**, because the asset they happen to hold keeps appreciating regardless of their effort. Follow each branch one step further and you get the two defining social effects of an asset-tilted housing market: **delayed family formation and lower birth rates** flowing from the lock-out, and a **widening wealth gap** flowing from the owner windfall. Let us take them in turn.

### Lock-out: the deposit that recedes faster than you can save

The first casualty of a high ratio is the first-time buyer. To buy, you typically need a deposit — a slice of the price you pay upfront, with the bank lending the rest (see [leverage and the mortgage](/blog/trading/real-estate/leverage-and-the-mortgage-how-debt-amplifies-property)). The cruel arithmetic is that when prices rise faster than you can save, the deposit you are chasing *grows faster than your savings*. You are running toward a finish line that is moving away from you.

There is a second, quieter cruelty in the lock-out: the renter who cannot buy is not standing still while they wait — they are paying rent the whole time, which eats the very savings they need for a deposit. In an asset-tilted market rents are high too (the asset has to earn its keep somehow), so the would-be buyer hands a large slice of income to a landlord every month, leaving less to save, while the deposit target climbs away. The owner, by contrast, is building equity with every mortgage payment and consuming shelter at the same time. The renter pays for shelter and gets nothing to keep; the owner pays for shelter and keeps an appreciating asset. That asymmetry, compounding monthly for years, is why the gap between an owner and a lifelong renter (which we quantify below) grows so wide so fast.

#### Worked example: how a price-to-income of 32 vs 5 changes years-to-buy

Let us make the lock-out brutally concrete with two imaginary cities, identical except for their price-to-income ratio.

Take a household earning **₫450 million a year** (≈ \$17,400) — roughly Minh's two-salary household. Assume, generously, that they can save **20% of income**, or ₫90 million a year, and that they need a **30% deposit** to buy.

**Affordable city (price-to-income = 5).** A home costs 5 × ₫450 million = **₫2.25 billion**. A 30% deposit is ₫675 million. At ₫90 million saved per year, that deposit takes **7.5 years** — long, but reachable. They buy in their mid-thirties and get on with life.

**Asset-tilted city (price-to-income = 32).** The same home, in HCMC terms, costs 32 × ₫450 million = **₫14.4 billion**. A 30% deposit is ₫4.32 billion. At ₫90 million saved per year, that deposit takes **48 years** — longer than a working life — *and that is before prices move at all*. If prices then rise 8% a year while incomes rise 5%, the gap widens every year and the deposit is never caught. The household never buys. They rent for life.

The honest version uses Minh's actual two-good-salary situation, where the flat is "only" ~15× their income rather than 32× the *median*. Even there, the deposit math stretches to 20-plus years of disciplined saving. *A price-to-income ratio is not an abstraction; multiplied through a savings rate it is the number of years of your life the market demands before it lets you in — and past a certain level, that number exceeds a lifetime.*

### Delayed family formation and the birth-rate channel

This is where housing stops being a finance topic and becomes a demographic one. Across many high-cost societies, researchers find that unaffordable housing is associated with **later marriage, later first births, and fewer children overall**. The logic is direct: people delay starting families until they have stable, large-enough housing, and when that housing keeps receding, they delay — and delay shrinks total fertility, because a birth postponed is often a birth foregone.

Vietnam is living this in real time. The total fertility rate has fallen below replacement (roughly 2.1 children per woman), and in Ho Chi Minh City — the most expensive, most asset-tilted market in the country — it has dropped to among the **lowest in the nation**, well under 1.5. Housing is not the only cause (urban life, careers, and the cost of raising children all weigh in), but a 70 m² flat that costs 32 years of income is a powerful reason for a young couple to wait, and to stop at one child instead of two. We connected this demand wave to the cycle in [demographics and urbanization](/blog/trading/real-estate/demographics-and-urbanization-the-demand-wave); here the arrow runs the other way — housing cost feeding *back* onto demographics, suppressing the very household formation that drives long-run demand.

### Inequality: wealth concentrates with people who already own

The third consequence is the quietest and the most consequential over a generation. When housing tilts toward asset, the people who already own ride the appreciation, and the people who do not are left behind — and because owning a home is itself the main way ordinary households build wealth, the gap compounds.

This is the engine behind one of the most important facts in modern wealth statistics: a large share of the difference in net worth between the middle-aged and the young, between the propertied and the renter, is **housing equity**. The owner's home appreciates; the renter's rent is consumed and gone. Over decades the two diverge into different wealth classes, and the divide is increasingly *inherited* — which brings us to the next idea.

### Intergenerational wealth transfer: the bank of mum and dad

In an asset-tilted market, the deposit becomes too large to save from a normal salary in a normal time. So how do young people buy at all? Increasingly, they do not buy *from their income* — they buy *from their parents' housing wealth*. A gift toward the deposit, an inheritance, the family home borrowed against. This is **intergenerational wealth transfer**, and it is the safety valve that keeps asset-tilted markets functioning at all.

But notice what it does. It converts housing from something you earn into something you inherit. The young person who can buy is increasingly the one whose family already owned property — so the existing distribution of housing wealth gets *copied forward* to the next generation, and the renter family's children start the race even further behind. Affordable, shelter-tilted housing lets you buy a home with a good job. Asset-tilted housing increasingly requires you to have been *born to the right family*. That is a profound change in how a society works, and it follows directly from letting the asset role win.

#### Worked example: a society's asset tilt, measured by price-to-income

You can read a society's choice about housing straight off its price-to-income ratio. Walk up the scale:

- **Ratio 3 to 5** — shelter wins. Homes are bought out of income; a normal worker can buy with a normal job in under a decade of saving. Many US Midwest and Texas metros, much of Germany.
- **Ratio ~10 to 15** — mixed. Buyable, but a serious multi-decade financial undertaking; investment demand is clearly present. New York (~10), London (~12), Tokyo (~15).
- **Ratio ~25 to 35** — asset dominant. Shelter is now mostly out of reach on a median income; the market is driven by leverage, savings, expectation of appreciation, and family transfers. Seoul (~26), Mumbai (~28), **Hanoi (~31)**, **HCMC (~32)**, Beijing (~35).
- **Ratio 40+** — extreme asset tilt. Even high earners cannot buy without family help; the home is a store of wealth first and shelter a distant second. Hong Kong (~44).

*The price-to-income ratio is not just an affordability statistic — it is the single number that tells you which of housing's two roles a society has chosen to let win, and how completely.*

## The homeowner political economy: why prices rarely fall by policy

Here is a puzzle. Everyone — politicians, economists, the public — agrees that unaffordable housing is a problem. Affordability is on every party's manifesto. And yet, decade after decade, in market after market, prices keep rising relative to incomes and almost nothing makes them durably fall. Why? The answer is not stupidity or a lack of good ideas. It is **political economy** — the way the structure of who-benefits shapes what policy actually does.

The key concept has a memorable name from the economist William Fischel: the **homevoter**. The idea is that a homeowner's house is usually their single largest asset — often the majority of their net worth — and so, when they vote, when they show up to a planning meeting, when they support or oppose a candidate, they vote partly to **protect their home's value**. They will fight new development next door (it might lower their price or change their neighbourhood), oppose higher property taxes (it costs them directly), and support anything that props prices up. Not because they are villains — because their largest asset is on the line, and people protect their largest asset.

![A before-and-after comparison of an owner and a renter, showing the owner benefits from rising prices and votes to protect them while the renter and young want prices down but are outvoted](/imgs/blogs/housing-as-financial-asset-vs-shelter-4.png)

The figure lays out the divide. The **owner** holds the asset, treats a price rise as a win, wants prices up or at least stable, and votes to protect home values and keep holding taxes low. The **renter and the young** — Minh — own nothing, watch a price rise as a wall getting taller, want prices down toward incomes or more supply built, and are *outvoted*, because in most established cities owners are the majority. The arithmetic of democracy then does the rest: policy follows the majority, and the majority owns.

This is why the policies that would actually lower prices — building a lot more housing, taxing property holdings, taxing speculative gains — are so politically hard. Each of them lowers the value of the largest asset of the people who vote most reliably. And it explains the strange asymmetry in how governments treat house prices: when prices *fall*, it is a "crisis" and the state intervenes to support the market; when prices *rise* far beyond incomes, it is a "boom" and policy mostly cheers. The system has a floor under prices (the owner majority will not tolerate a crash) but no ceiling (rising prices make the majority feel rich). That asymmetry, repeated over decades, is most of why price-to-income ratios ratchet up and rarely down.

### The self-reinforcing loop

The political economy is not a one-time fact; it is a loop that feeds on itself, getting tighter with every turn.

![A six-stage loop showing owners as the voting majority leading to price-protecting policy, tight supply, worsening affordability, a larger share of net worth tied to housing, and more pressure to prop prices, which feeds back to the start](/imgs/blogs/housing-as-financial-asset-vs-shelter-6.png)

Follow the pipeline (the final arrow loops back to the start, as the caption notes). **Owners are the majority of voters**, so **policy protects home prices**. That protection shows up as **little new supply and low holding taxes**, which means **affordability worsens further**. As prices climb, **housing becomes a bigger and bigger share of owners' net worth** — for many households their home is now most of what they have — which raises the stakes enormously, generating **even more pressure to prop prices up**. And that pressure flows straight back to the start, reinforcing the owner majority's determination to protect prices. Each loop makes the next one stronger, because each loop makes housing a larger fraction of household wealth, which makes a price fall more catastrophic, which makes the political resistance to anything that might lower prices more fierce.

#### Worked example: an owner's political incentive against falling prices

Put a number on the homevoter's incentive so it is not just a story. Suppose an owner — Minh's aunt — has a home worth ₫5 billion (≈ \$193,000) against which she still owes a ₫1 billion mortgage. Her **home equity** (value minus debt) is ₫4 billion, and let's say that is **80% of her ₫5 billion total net worth**.

Now imagine a policy that would make housing meaningfully more affordable for Minh — say, a wave of new supply plus a holding tax that, together, push prices down 20%. Her home falls from ₫5 billion to ₫4 billion. But her ₫1 billion mortgage does not shrink — debt is fixed in nominal terms. So her equity drops from ₫4 billion to ₫3 billion: a **25% hit to her net worth from a 20% price fall** (leverage cuts both ways). For a household whose home is most of their wealth, a "modest" price correction is a major personal loss.

So when Minh's aunt votes, even if she sympathises with Minh, she is being asked to support a policy that would wipe out a quarter of her life savings. *Most people, asked to vote for a 25% cut to their own net worth so that strangers' children can afford homes, will quietly vote no — and that, repeated across an owner majority, is why prices so rarely fall by design.*

## Housing's dual return: you live in it AND it appreciates

We have seen the conflict and its social fallout. Now let us look at what makes housing-as-asset so uniquely powerful — and so hard to give up. Most assets do one thing: a stock appreciates but you cannot live in it; a home you rent shelters you but builds no equity. Housing as an owner-occupier does **both at once**, and it amplifies the second with borrowed money. That combination is genuinely special, and understanding it is the key to why "just treat housing as shelter" is such a hard sell.

![A vertical stack showing housing's total return building up from imputed rent as the shelter return, plus price appreciation, amplified by leverage, summing to the total return an owner earns](/imgs/blogs/housing-as-financial-asset-vs-shelter-5.png)

The stack shows the full return to an owner-occupier, layer by layer.

- **Use value: imputed rent.** The shelter you consume, worth what you would have paid to rent the same place — say ₫120 million a year on Minh's aunt's flat. You "earn" this just by living there instead of renting. It is the consumption return, and a renter gets shelter too but pays cash for it out of pocket.
- **Financial return: appreciation.** On top of the shelter, the home's price rises. In a Vietnamese boom that can be 5% to 10% a year. This is the asset return, and a renter gets *none* of it.
- **Leverage amplifies it.** Because you bought with a mortgage — putting down maybe 30% and borrowing 70% — the appreciation lands on your small slice of equity, multiplying the percentage return. We will compute this next.
- **Total return to the owner.** Add the shelter you consume to the leveraged appreciation and you get a combined return that is very hard for any other ordinary investment to beat. This is *why housing feels unbeatable* — and why owners are so reluctant to see it treated as mere shelter.

#### Worked example: the wealth gap between an owner and a lifelong renter over 20 years

This is the example that explains everything about the owner-renter divide. Let us race two people for 20 years, starting from the same place, differing only in that one buys and one rents.

Both start with **₫600 million** (≈ \$23,000) in savings. The home costs **₫2 billion** (≈ \$77,000). Assume home prices grow **6% a year**, the owner takes a 70% mortgage at an effective cost roughly offset by the rent they no longer pay (we will keep it simple and net those against each other), and the renter invests their ₫600 million in a diversified portfolio returning **6% a year** as well — a fair fight on the raw return.

**The owner.** They put ₫600 million down on the ₫2 billion home (a 30% deposit) and borrow ₫1.4 billion. After 20 years at 6% appreciation, the home is worth ₫2bn × 1.06²⁰ ≈ **₫6.4 billion**. They have been paying down the mortgage, but even if we conservatively assume they still owe ₫700 million at year 20, their equity is roughly ₫6.4bn − ₫0.7bn = **₫5.7 billion**. Their ₫600 million deposit, applied to the *whole* ₫2 billion asset, captured the appreciation on all of it — that is leverage. And the whole time, they lived in the home, consuming shelter.

**The renter.** Their ₫600 million invested at 6% for 20 years grows to ₫600m × 1.06²⁰ ≈ **₫1.9 billion**. That is a perfectly good return on their money — it nearly tripled. But it grew only their *own* ₫600 million, because a renter cannot easily borrow ₫1.4 billion to invest in stocks the way they can borrow it to buy a home. And the whole time, they paid rent that vanished.

The owner ends with ~₫5.7 billion of housing equity; the renter ends with ~₫1.9 billion of financial wealth. The gap — about **₫3.8 billion** — opened up not because the owner was smarter or earned more, but because **housing let them apply leverage to a 6% return while living inside the asset**. *Twenty years of the same 6% return produced three times the wealth for the person who could borrow to buy it and live in it — which is exactly why housing-as-asset is so coveted, and why being shut out of it is so costly.*

There is an honest counterweight worth stating, which we work through fully in [rent vs buy, the real math](/blog/trading/real-estate/rent-vs-buy-the-real-math): the renter's outcome depends heavily on whether they actually invest the money they saved by not buying, and the owner's outcome depends on transaction costs, maintenance, and what prices actually do. Leverage that magnifies a 6% gain magnifies a 6% *loss* just as hard, as the [bubble and crash post](/blog/trading/real-estate/anatomy-of-a-property-bubble-and-crash) shows in painful detail. The dual return is powerful, not free.

## The financialization of housing

So far we have mostly talked about owner-occupiers — people living in the homes they own. But the asset role does not stop there. Over the past few decades, in market after market, housing has gone through what economists call **financialization**: the process by which homes become, more and more, financial instruments to be traded, leveraged, and parked in, rather than places to be lived in. This is the asset role taken to its logical end, and it is the strongest force pushing markets toward the asset side of the tension.

Financialization shows up in several concrete forms:

- **Investor and second-home buying.** A growing share of purchases are by people who already have somewhere to live and are buying for investment — a second flat to rent out or flip, a third one bought off-plan to resell before completion. In Vietnam's boom districts, a large fraction of new apartments are bought by investors, not occupiers; whole towers can sit dark at night, owned but empty, held purely for appreciation. Every such purchase is investment demand crowding shelter demand.
- **Leverage and easy credit.** Financialization runs on borrowed money. When credit is cheap and plentiful (see [the credit cycle and property prices](/blog/trading/real-estate/the-credit-cycle-and-property-prices) and [interest rates and mortgages](/blog/trading/real-estate/interest-rates-and-mortgages-the-master-variable)), investors can buy more property with less of their own cash, bidding prices up faster than incomes ever could. Vietnam's 2021–22 boom rode a wave of developer bond financing and loose credit; when that froze in 2022–23, the asset side seized up.
- **Land banking and hoarding.** Developers and speculators hold land off the market waiting for it to appreciate, deliberately keeping supply tight — the asset role's wish for scarcity made into a business strategy. We dug into this in [how tax shapes behavior](/blog/trading/real-estate/how-tax-shapes-behavior-speculation-and-hoarding); the absence of a holding tax in Vietnam makes hoarding nearly free, which is why it is so common.
- **Securitization and global capital.** At the deepest end, housing debt gets packaged into securities and sold to global investors, plugging local home prices into world capital flows. This is what turned American mortgages into the fuse for the 2008 global crisis. Foreign capital flowing into a local property market (see [foreign capital and FDI in property](/blog/trading/real-estate/foreign-capital-and-fdi-in-property)) is the same phenomenon: money from anywhere on Earth bidding on homes that locals need to live in.

The common thread is that financialization adds buyers who do not care about use value at all. An investor in another country buying a HCMC tower off-plan is bidding purely on exchange value — expected appreciation — and Minh, who needs the shelter, is bidding against them with a salary. It is not a fair fight, and it pushes the price-to-income ratio relentlessly upward. Financialization is, in one sentence, *the asset role escaping the boundary of people who actually live in the homes*.

## The spectrum of housing systems

If the tension is universal, why are some places far more livable than others? Because societies make different choices — sometimes deliberately, sometimes by accident — about which role to favour, and those choices are visible in how their housing systems are built. It helps to lay the main models on a spectrum, from systems engineered to deliver shelter to systems that let the asset role run.

![A grid comparing three housing systems — Singapore's engineered ownership, Germany and Vienna's rental-heavy model, and speculative markets like Hong Kong and HCMC — with how each works and its trade-off](/imgs/blogs/housing-as-financial-asset-vs-shelter-7.png)

The grid lays out three reference points; almost every real market sits somewhere among them.

**Singapore: engineered ownership for all.** Singapore decided early that owning a home should be near-universal, and built a state machinery to deliver it. The Housing & Development Board (HDB) builds flats and sells them, on long (99-year) leaseholds, at controlled prices to citizens, with subsidies and mandatory savings (the Central Provident Fund) channelled into the purchase. The result: roughly **78% of Singaporeans live in HDB flats**, the vast majority owning them. The trade-off is that the state controls supply and pricing heavily, and resale of public flats is rule-bound. Singapore did not abolish the asset role — HDB flats appreciate — but it deliberately made the shelter role reachable for almost everyone first. It is the clearest example of a society *choosing* not to let the asset role win.

**Germany and Vienna: the rental model.** Germany and the city of Vienna run housing largely as shelter by making *renting* a stable, dignified, long-term way to live rather than a waiting room before ownership. Strong tenant protections, rent regulation, and a large stock of social and municipally-owned housing (in Vienna, a huge share of residents live in city-built or city-supported apartments) mean a family can rent the same flat for decades with security. Home-ownership rates are correspondingly low — around half of Germans rent — and, crucially, German house prices historically stayed far flatter relative to incomes than in asset-tilted markets. The trade-off is that fewer households build wealth *through* their home; the system delivers affordable shelter but routes wealth-building elsewhere. It is the other deliberate choice: keep housing cheap to live in, and accept that it is a weaker wealth engine.

**Speculative markets: Hong Kong and HCMC.** At the far end sit the markets where the asset role dominates almost completely: tight supply (Hong Kong's geography and land policy; HCMC's slow approvals and land hoarding), low or no holding taxes, heavy investor and second-home demand, and a culture of property-as-savings. Prices float free of incomes — price-to-income of **44 in Hong Kong, ~32 in HCMC** — and the young are priced out unless family wealth helps. The trade-off is the mirror image of Germany's: owners enjoy enormous gains, but each generation of non-owners falls further behind, and the social costs (lock-out, low fertility, inequality) mount. These markets did not necessarily *choose* this; often it is the default that happens when no one deliberately protects the shelter role.

The point of the spectrum is not that one system is simply "best." Each makes a real trade-off. Singapore's model requires a powerful, trusted state and heavy intervention. Germany's accepts weaker household wealth-building. Speculative markets reward existing owners spectacularly while shutting out the young. What the spectrum proves is the thing this whole post is about: **the outcome is a choice, not a law of nature.** A society that ends up at the speculative end did not have to; it let the asset role win by default. Vietnam, with the [Land Law 2024](/blog/trading/real-estate/vietnam-land-law-2024-and-the-coming-property-tax) and a long-debated property tax, is standing exactly at this fork.

## Common misconceptions

The shelter-vs-asset tension breeds a set of beliefs that feel obviously true and are mostly wrong. Each one quietly assumes the asset role should win.

**"A home is always a great investment."** A home is *sometimes* a great investment and sometimes a mediocre or terrible one, and you cannot know which in advance. The dual return is real, but so is the downside: leverage magnifies losses as readily as gains (a 20% price fall can wipe out far more than 20% of your equity, as the homevoter example showed), transaction costs are large (Vietnam's [2% transfer tax plus fees](/blog/trading/real-estate/transfer-taxes-and-capital-gains-on-property) alone eat into any gain), maintenance is a constant drain, and prices *do* fall — US homes lost about **27% nationally from 2006 to 2012**, and many buyers at the top spent a decade underwater. "Always" is doing enormous work in this sentence, and it is false. What is *reliably* true is that a home delivers shelter; its investment performance is uncertain.

**"Rising house prices make a country richer."** This is the most seductive and most wrong of all. When house prices rise faster than incomes, the country has not produced more shelter, more goods, or more income — the same houses simply trade at higher numbers. The "wealth" created is a transfer, not a creation: it enriches existing owners *at the expense of* future buyers, who must now pay more for the same shelter. A nation where homes cost 32 years of income is not richer than one where they cost 5; it is one where shelter has become a tollbooth that the propertied collect from the young. Rising prices redistribute wealth toward owners; they do not grow the pie.

**"The market will fix affordability."** Left alone, the market does not generally fix housing affordability, because of everything in the political-economy section: the people who benefit from high prices are the majority, supply is slow and politically blocked, and the asset role keeps attracting investment demand that bids prices above what shelter-seekers can pay. Markets fix shortages of things that can be quickly produced and that no powerful group wants kept scarce. Housing is the opposite: slow to build, with a large, organised constituency that *wants* it scarce. Affordability that improves does so because of deliberate choices — more supply, taxes on holding and speculation, public building — not because the market drifted there on its own.

**"Renting is throwing money away."** This belief, examined fully in [rent vs buy](/blog/trading/real-estate/rent-vs-buy-the-real-math), confuses the two roles. Rent buys shelter — the use value — which is not "thrown away" any more than buying food is. What renting forgoes is the *asset* return (appreciation and leverage), not the shelter. In a market where the asset role is wildly expensive (price-to-income of 32), the appreciation you forgo may be more than offset by not overpaying 32× income for the privilege. Whether renting "wastes" money depends entirely on prices, rents, and what you do with the money you did not sink into a deposit — it is a calculation, not a slogan.

**"High prices just mean the city is desirable."** Desirability raises prices, yes — but a price-to-income of 32 is not pure desirability; it is desirability *times* the asset role, leverage, scarce supply, and investment demand. Tokyo is intensely desirable and sits at a ratio of ~15; HCMC at ~32 is not twice as desirable as Tokyo. The extra is the asset tilt — tight supply, no holding tax, heavy speculative demand — not extra desirability. Conflating the two lets a market excuse an affordability crisis as a compliment.

## How it shows up in real markets

Abstractions earn their keep when they explain the world. Here is the shelter-vs-asset tension playing out in named, datable markets.

**Ho Chi Minh City and Hanoi: lock-out in real time.** This is the case the whole post orbits. Primary apartment prices in HCMC reached roughly **111 million VND/m² in 2025** (≈ \$4,222/m²), with a price-to-income ratio around **32** (one survey: 34 years, 5th-highest globally); Hanoi sits close behind at ~31 after prices jumped roughly 40% in a single year. On a median income, shelter is simply out of reach — the asset role has won. The visible symptoms are exactly the ones the theory predicts: a generation of Minhs renting indefinitely or leaning on family wealth, dark investor-owned towers, and HCMC's fertility rate among the lowest in Vietnam. Vietnam reached this point partly by *omission* — no annual property tax, light constraints on holding land idle, and a cultural conviction that property is the safest store of savings, all of which favour the asset over shelter.

**Singapore: a deliberate counter-example.** Singapore faced the same pressures — a dense, desirable, capital-rich city-state — and chose differently. By building HDB flats at scale, selling them on subsidised long leaseholds, and channelling forced savings into ownership, it engineered a roughly **78% public-housing-ownership** outcome. Private property in Singapore is still wildly asset-tilted (ratio ~17 and the luxury market is global-money territory), but the *majority* of residents live in homes where the shelter role was deliberately protected. Singapore is the proof that the speculative outcome is not inevitable — but also that escaping it takes an enormous, sustained, state-led effort that few governments attempt.

**Vienna and Germany: shelter through renting.** Vienna built and maintained a vast stock of municipal housing such that a large share of the city rents secure, affordable, high-quality apartments for life, and Germany's strong tenant law and modest historical price appreciation made long-term renting normal and dignified. The result is famously affordable, stable housing — and, as the trade-off, lower home-ownership and less household wealth tied up in property. When German house prices finally surged in the 2010s (as ultra-low rates pushed investors in), the country got a taste of the asset role and the affordability strain that comes with it, a reminder that no system is permanently immune.

**The United States, 2006–2012: the asset role crashing.** America let housing financialize hard in the 2000s — easy credit, securitised mortgages, speculative buying — pushing the asset role to an extreme. The national price index ran from 100 (in 2000) up to a peak of **184.6 in mid-2006**, then collapsed about **27% to a trough of 134 in early 2012**. Leverage that had magnified the gains magnified the losses; millions owed more than their homes were worth. It is the clearest demonstration that the dual return is not a free lunch — when the asset role detaches far enough from use value and runs on borrowed money, it can break, and the shelter is worth the same the whole way down.

**Japan after 1990: the slow normalization.** Japan's late-1980s bubble took the asset role to a mania — Tokyo land was said to be worth more than all of California — and then deflated for two decades. The lesson runs the other way: Japanese house prices spent thirty years drifting back toward incomes, and Tokyo today sits at a relatively sane price-to-income of ~15 with stable or even falling prices in much of the country. Combined with Japan's near-absence of land-value appreciation, housing there has drifted *back* toward being shelter — affordable to buy, not a get-rich asset — partly by policy and demographics, partly by the long hangover of letting the asset role run too hot. It is a glimpse of what the other side of an asset-tilt can look like.

## When this matters / Further reading

This tension matters most at exactly the moments your own life intersects with housing. When you are deciding whether to [rent or buy](/blog/trading/real-estate/rent-vs-buy-the-real-math), you are really deciding how much you value the asset role (appreciation, leverage, forced saving) versus simply paying for shelter — and in a market with a price-to-income of 32, that math leans differently than in one at 5. When you read a headline cheering that "house prices hit a record high," you now know to ask *for whom* — a windfall for owners is a wall for the Minhs. When a government debates a property tax, looser zoning, or more public building (as Vietnam is, under the [Land Law 2024](/blog/trading/real-estate/vietnam-land-law-2024-and-the-coming-property-tax)), you can see past the slogans to the real question underneath: *which of housing's two roles will this policy favour?*

The single most useful habit this post can leave you with is a question to carry into any property market, anywhere: **is this market treating homes mainly as shelter or mainly as an asset?** Look at the price-to-income ratio (3 to 5 means shelter, 30-plus means asset), at the holding tax (none means hoarding is free), at the share of buyers who are investors, at whether the young can buy from their incomes or only from their parents'. Those signals tell you, faster than any forecast, what kind of market you are standing in and what it will do to the people living in it.

None of this is a verdict that the asset role is evil or that owners are villains — Minh's aunt did nothing wrong, and most of us hope to be on the owner's side of the trade someday. It is a recognition that a home is genuinely two things at once, that those two things want opposite outcomes, and that the balance a society strikes between them is one of the most consequential choices it makes — shaping who can form a family, where wealth ends up, and which generation gets to belong. The tension never fully resolves. But seeing it clearly is the first step to deciding, deliberately rather than by default, which side you want to win.
