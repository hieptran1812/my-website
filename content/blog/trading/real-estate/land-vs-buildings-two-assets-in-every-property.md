> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Every property is two completely different assets fused into one price: the **land** underneath (scarce, immovable, tends to *appreciate* because good locations are finite) and the **structure** on top (reproducible, wears out, *depreciates* like a car). Separating them is the master key to real estate.
>
> - The structure decays toward zero over its useful life; the land underneath does the compounding. A 30-year-old building on prime land is worth more than a brand-new one in the sticks because you are mostly buying land in the first case and mostly buying a building in the second.
> - "Location, location, location" is just shorthand for "you are paying for land, and land is priced by location." Central HCMC land can cost fifty times the fringe per square metre.
> - The land is worth its *best legal use*, not its current one — which is why a parking lot on a downtown corner gets torn down for a tower, and why an old house on prime land can have a *negative*-value building you pay to demolish.
> - The one number to remember: in central District 1, roughly **80% of a home's price is land**; on the rural fringe it can be **20%**. The more central the location, the more you are really buying dirt.

In 2025, a friend of mine — call him **Minh** — paid ₫7.0 billion (≈ \$270,000) for a 70-square-metre apartment in central Ho Chi Minh City. A colleague of his bought a much bigger house, nearly three times the floor area, out in a satellite district for the *same* ₫7.0 billion. Minh's flat is small, a bit dated, on the fourth floor of a building that went up in the 2010s. His colleague's house is new, spacious, and gleaming. On every visible measure — size, age, finish — the colleague got the better deal.

And yet five years from now, almost everyone who watches Vietnamese property would bet Minh's small flat will have gained *more* value than the big new house. Not because Minh is luckier, but because he bought a different mix of two assets. Minh bought mostly **land**. His colleague bought mostly **building**. And land and building are not the same kind of thing at all — one tends to go up forever, the other wears out and falls toward zero.

This is the single most useful idea in all of real estate, and almost nobody is taught it cleanly. Once you see that every property is two assets glued together, the confusing parts of the market stop being confusing. Why is "location, location, location" the only rule everyone repeats? Why do people knock down perfectly good houses? Why is hoarding empty land — doing *nothing* with it — sometimes the smartest move on the board? Why did Japanese land become, for a moment in 1990, theoretically worth more than all the land in the United States? Every one of those answers is the same answer, seen from a different angle.

![A property price equals land value plus structure value, shown as two stacked columns for a central HCMC flat with eighty percent land and a suburban house with forty percent land](/imgs/blogs/land-vs-buildings-two-assets-in-every-property-1.png)

The figure above is the mental model for this entire post. A property is never one price — it is **land value + structure value**, and the *mix* changes dramatically with location. We are going to take that split apart screw by screw: define both assets from zero, show exactly why one appreciates while the other depreciates, watch the land share of a home's value rise over time and across a city, and then use the model to explain teardowns, land hoarding, and the famous bubbles. By the end, you will look at any building — your own, a listing online, a half-finished tower — and instinctively ask the only question that matters: *how much of this is land, and how much is just a wasting structure sitting on top of it?*

This is educational, not financial advice. The numbers Minh and our recurring US investor "Dana" use are illustrative and rounded so you can do them in your head; the mechanisms are real.

## Foundations: the two assets, ground rent, and depreciation

Before we can split a property in two, we need to be precise about what each half *is*. Let's build the vocabulary from the ground up — literally.

### Land vs improvements

When a surveyor, an appraiser, or a tax office looks at a property, they almost never see "a house." They see two line items:

- **Land** (also called the *site* or, in legal-speak, *the realty* / *đất* in Vietnamese). This is the physical parcel — a defined patch of the Earth's surface at a specific location, plus the legal right to use it. Crucially, land is *immovable* (you cannot pick it up and carry it to a cheaper city) and, for any given location, *unique* — there is exactly one parcel at that corner of District 1, and they are not making another one.
- **Improvements** (the *structure*, the *building*, *công trình xây dựng*). This is everything humans added on top: the foundation, walls, roof, plumbing, the swimming pool, the driveway, the landscaping. An *improvement* is anything of value that was *built*, as opposed to the raw site it sits on.

The total price you pay is the sum: **property value = land value + improvement value**. This split is not an accounting trick; tax authorities, lenders, and insurers all use it because the two pieces behave so differently. Your fire insurance, for example, only covers the *structure* — if your house burns down, the land is still there, unscorched, often worth as much as before. That alone tells you the two are separate assets.

A quick test that makes the distinction concrete: imagine the building vanished overnight — a fire, a flood, a wrecking ball. What is left? A *vacant lot*, and that vacant lot still has a price. That price is the **land value**. Whatever the property was worth *with* the building, minus the land value, is roughly the **structure value**. Hold onto that subtraction; we will use it constantly.

### Site value, location, and what land actually "is"

Here is the subtle part. The land's value has almost nothing to do with the *dirt itself*. A cubic metre of soil in District 1 is chemically identical to a cubic metre of soil in Cu Chi, 40 kilometres away. Yet the District 1 parcel can be worth fifty times more per square metre. The difference is not the soil — it is the **location**: the bundle of advantages that come from *where* the parcel sits. Proximity to jobs, schools, transit, customers, the river, the airport; the prestige of the address; the density of people willing to pay to be near each other.

Appraisers call this **site value** or **location value**, and the key insight is that it is *created by everyone around the parcel, not by the owner*. When the city builds a metro line, when a new shopping district opens, when the neighbourhood gets safer and busier — the land under your feet becomes more valuable, and you did nothing. This "unearned" quality of land value is one of the oldest observations in economics, and it is the reason land behaves so differently from a building you have to maintain.

### A Vietnamese wrinkle: you own the structure, you hold the land

One thing trips up international readers and matters enormously for the two-asset split in Vietnam: in Vietnam, *all land is constitutionally owned by the people, administered by the State*. You never get freehold title to land the way you would in the US or UK. What you get is a **land-use right** (*quyền sử dụng đất*) — a long, transferable, mortgageable, inheritable right to use a specific parcel, evidenced by the famous "red book" or "pink book" certificate (*sổ đỏ / sổ hồng*). In everyday practice this right trades, prices, and behaves almost exactly like ownership — but legally it is a *use right*, and that distinction has teeth.

For **residential land**, the use right is typically *stable and long-term* (effectively indefinite for housing), which is why Vietnamese buyers treat residential land like a freehold asset that appreciates forever. For **apartment projects and many commercial uses**, the underlying land-use right often runs for a defined term — commonly **50 years**, sometimes up to 70 — after which renewal is a live question. This is one reason apartments are so often modelled with a 50-year life: *both* the structure's useful life and the project's land-use term can expire around the same horizon, so the asset is sometimes treated as "fully wasting" at year 50 — land right *and* building together. For a landed house on residential land, by contrast, the land right keeps appreciating long after the structure has worn out and been rebuilt.

So when we say "land appreciates," in Vietnam we precisely mean *the land-use right* appreciates — and the security and duration of that right is itself part of the land's value. A parcel with clean, long-term residential title is worth more than an otherwise-identical parcel with a murky or short-dated use right, because the buyer is purchasing more *years of location*. The mechanism is universal; the legal wrapper is local.

### Ground rent and Ricardo's rent theory

The cleanest way to understand *why* central land costs more is **ground rent** — the income you could earn purely from controlling a location, separate from any building on it.

Imagine two identical bakeries, run identically, selling identical bread. One sits on a busy District 1 corner; one sits on a quiet rural road. The District 1 bakery sells far more bread at higher prices simply because of foot traffic — because of its location. The *extra* profit it earns, over and above what the rural bakery earns, is not due to better baking. It is due to the *site*. That extra is the **ground rent** of the District 1 location — the economic payment the location itself commands.

This is **David Ricardo's theory of rent**, first written down in 1817 and still the bedrock of land economics. Ricardo studied farmland: the most fertile fields produced more grain for the same labour and seed, so they commanded higher rents; the rent of any plot equalled its advantage over the *worst* land still in use. Swap "fertility" for "location" and the logic is identical for cities. The rent a location commands equals its locational advantage over the marginal (fringe) site. **Land value, then, is just the capitalised ground rent** — the lump sum you would pay today to own that stream of locational advantage forever.

> A *basis point* is one hundredth of a percent (0.01%); we will use it later. For now, the term to lock in is **ground rent**: the income a location earns purely for being where it is.

#### Worked example: splitting Minh's ₫7bn flat into land and structure

Let's make the split real with Minh's apartment. He paid ₫7.0 billion (≈ \$270,000). How much of that is land, and how much is the concrete-and-tile structure?

For an apartment, you can't point at "his patch of dirt" — he owns a share of the land under the whole building. But the split still exists, and appraisers estimate it. In central HCMC, land is so scarce relative to the floor space stacked on it that the *land component* of an apartment's value typically runs very high — call it **80%** for a prime central building.

- Land component: 80% × ₫7.0bn = **₫5.6 billion** (≈ \$216,000)
- Structure component: 20% × ₫7.0bn = **₫1.4 billion** (≈ \$54,000)

Now his colleague's suburban house, same ₫7.0bn total but a big new building on cheaper land. Out there the land is plentiful and the building is large and new, so the split flips — say **40% land / 60% structure**:

- Land component: 40% × ₫7.0bn = **₫2.8 billion** (≈ \$108,000)
- Structure component: 60% × ₫7.0bn = **₫4.2 billion** (≈ \$162,000)

Same ₫7.0bn price tag, completely different machines under the hood. Minh owns ₫5.6bn of appreciating land and ₫1.4bn of wasting structure. His colleague owns ₫2.8bn of land and ₫4.2bn of structure that is already starting to age.

*Two properties at the same price can be opposite bets — one is mostly land, the other is mostly building, and those two assets move in opposite directions.*

### Depreciation: three flavours, and why it's like a car

The reason a building is a "wasting asset" is **depreciation** — the loss of value as a structure ages and the world moves on. Appraisers break it into three distinct causes, and you should know all three because each behaves differently:

1. **Physical depreciation** — plain wear and tear. The roof leaks, the pipes corrode, the paint fades, the lift breaks. Time and weather grind every building down. This is the most obvious flavour and the one maintenance can slow but never stop.
2. **Functional obsolescence** — the building no longer matches how people want to live or work, even if it's structurally sound. A 1990s office with no fibre wiring, low ceilings, and a single elevator; a flat with a tiny "servant's kitchen" nobody wants anymore; a house with no parking in a car-owning suburb. The bones are fine; the *floor plan* is outdated.
3. **Economic (external) obsolescence** — value lost to forces *outside* the property line. A new highway roars past the bedroom window; the factory next door starts polluting; the neighbourhood's main employer shuts down. Nothing about the building changed, but its surroundings did, and the structure (and sometimes the land) is worth less.

Put them together and a building is genuinely like a car: it rolls off the lot, and from day one its *physical* value heads downhill. A car loses value every year until it is worth only scrap; a building loses value every year until it is worth only the cost of clearing it away. The technical term for "how long before the structure is worn out" is its **useful life** — typically 40 to 60 years for a concrete residential building, sometimes less for cheaper construction. Vietnamese apartment buildings are often assumed to have a **50-year** useful life, which is also how long many land-use rights for apartment projects run.

The flip side of depreciation is **replacement cost**: what it would cost *today* to rebuild the structure from scratch with current materials and labour. The structure component of value can never sustainably exceed its replacement cost — if it did, someone would just build a new one. As a building ages, its value falls from "replacement cost when new" toward "near zero," which is exactly the path that makes it the opposite of land.

The simplest way to model this fall is **straight-line depreciation**: spread the loss evenly over the useful life. A structure worth ₫2.0 billion new, with a 50-year life, loses ₫2.0bn ÷ 50 = ₫40 million of value *every year*, until at year 50 the structure is worth essentially nothing and the land underneath is all that remains. We will run that exact number shortly.

So here are the two assets, side by side, defined from zero:

| | **Land** | **Structure / building** |
|---|---|---|
| What it is | a unique location | a reproducible improvement |
| Supply | fixed — they aren't making more | elastic — you can always build another |
| Over time | tends to **appreciate** | **depreciates** toward zero |
| Driven by | location, ground rent, scarcity | physical wear, obsolescence, replacement cost |
| Insurable? | no (it can't be destroyed) | yes (it can burn down) |
| Analogy | a finite, compounding deposit | a car, losing value every year |

Everything else in this post is an application of this table.

## Why land appreciates and buildings depreciate

We've asserted that land goes up and structures go down. Let's prove it, because the *reason* is the whole game — and the reason is a clash between **scarcity** and **reproducibility**.

### Land: fixed supply meets rising demand

Land is the textbook example of a good in *perfectly inelastic supply*: no matter how high the price climbs, the quantity does not increase. You cannot manufacture another District 1. (Yes, Singapore and Hong Kong reclaim land from the sea, and HCMC expands its administrative boundaries — but these are marginal, expensive, and don't add *central* land where demand concentrates.) The supply curve for "land at this exact location" is a vertical line.

Now stack rising demand on top of that fixed supply. Cities grow. Vietnam's urban population keeps climbing; incomes rise; more people and more businesses compete for the same finite central locations. When supply can't move and demand keeps pushing, **price is the only thing that can give** — so land prices rise, often faster than incomes. This is not a quirk of Vietnam; it is the structural reason urban land appreciates almost everywhere over the long run. Land is the asset that captures a growing economy's demand to be *in the good spots*, and the good spots can't multiply.

There's a deeper point hiding here that connects to the wider economy. Because land is fixed and captures the unearned gains of growth and public investment, it tends to absorb a rising share of a society's wealth as that society develops. We'll see this drives the famous property cycle in a later post; for the business-cycle context of why land and credit boom and bust together, see [the business cycle for traders](/blog/trading/macro-trading/the-business-cycle-four-phases-for-traders).

### Buildings: reproducible, so they can't outrun their own replacement cost

A structure is the opposite. Anyone with capital and a permit can build one. Because buildings are *reproducible*, their value is anchored to **replacement cost** — and replacement cost, for an *existing* building, is always being eaten by depreciation.

Think about it from a buyer's point of view. Why would you pay ₫3 billion for a 25-year-old structure when, for ₫3 billion, you could build a brand-new one with modern wiring, a better layout, and a fresh 50-year life? You wouldn't. So an old structure must sell at a *discount* to the new-build cost, and that discount widens every year as the building ages. The structure is racing against two clocks at once: physical wear pulling its value down, and the option to "just build new" capping it from above. Land faces neither clock — you cannot "build new land," so its value is set purely by location and demand, with no replacement cost to drag it back.

![Land value compounding at eight percent a year while the building depreciates straight-line over fifty years, with the total dipping then rising as land comes to dominate](/imgs/blogs/land-vs-buildings-two-assets-in-every-property-2.png)

The chart above tracks both assets for a single ₫7.0bn property over 20 years. The land line (blue) compounds; the structure line (red) slopes steadily down; the total (green dashed) barely dips at first, then turns up and accelerates as land takes over. By around year 7, **the land alone is worth more than the entire original purchase price**, and the structure has become a rounding error. This is the master picture of the whole post: *the structure decays toward zero; the land underneath does the compounding.*

#### Worked example: depreciating Minh's ₫2bn structure over 50 years

Let's nail the structure's decline with a clean number. Take a structure worth **₫2.0 billion** when new (a slightly larger build than Minh's flat; the math is identical at any size), with a **50-year useful life** and straight-line depreciation.

- Annual depreciation = ₫2.0bn ÷ 50 = **₫40 million per year** (that's −2% of the *original* cost each year)
- After 10 years: structure value = ₫2.0bn − (10 × ₫40m) = ₫2.0bn − ₫400m = **₫1.6 billion**
- After **20 years** (the *book value* we care about): ₫2.0bn − (20 × ₫40m) = ₫2.0bn − ₫800m = **₫1.2 billion**
- After 40 years: ₫2.0bn − ₫1.6bn = ₫0.4 billion
- After 50 years: ₫2.0bn − ₫2.0bn = **₫0** — the structure is, on paper, worthless

Meanwhile, suppose the land under that structure was worth ₫5.0bn at the start and compounds at a calm 8% a year. After 20 years, land = ₫5.0bn × (1.08)²⁰ ≈ ₫5.0bn × 4.66 = **₫23.3 billion**. So at year 20, the property that started as ₫5.0bn land + ₫2.0bn structure = ₫7.0bn has become ₫23.3bn land + ₫1.2bn structure ≈ **₫24.5 billion** — and the structure, which was 29% of the value at purchase, is now under 5% of it.

*A building you bought for ₫2 billion is worth ₫1.2 billion after 20 years and ₫0 after 50 — the structure is the part of your property that is quietly evaporating while the land does all the work.*

### So why doesn't an old house become worthless?

Because a house is *both* assets, and the appreciating half usually swamps the depreciating half. The structure loses ₫40m a year, but the land can gain hundreds of millions a year in a hot market. The owner *feels* like "my house went up," but precisely speaking, the **land went up faster than the structure went down**. Strip the building away and you'd see the truth: an empty lot in a great location appreciates steadily; an old building on it is just a slowly-deflating bonus sitting on top.

This is why the old appraiser's joke — *"the house is free; you're paying for the land"* — is closer to literally true the more central and the older the property gets.

## Ground rent and the rent gradient: why central land costs 50× the fringe

We said land is priced by location. Let's make "location" quantitative, because the pattern is astonishingly regular. Plot land price against distance from a city's centre and you get a curve that falls steeply at first, then flattens — the **rent gradient** (sometimes the *bid-rent curve*). Land is most expensive at the centre, where the most ground rent can be earned, and gets cheaper as you move out toward the fringe, where the marginal land barely commands a premium over farmland.

![A network showing land value falling with distance from the central business district, from four hundred million per square metre at the District 1 core down to eight million at the fringe](/imgs/blogs/land-vs-buildings-two-assets-in-every-property-3.png)

The figure above sketches the gradient for HCMC with illustrative per-square-metre land prices. The District 1 core commands the peak ground rent — it is where the most businesses and people most want to be — so its land carries the highest price. Move out one ring to Districts 3, 4, or Binh Thanh and the price drops sharply; move out to Districts 9 and 12 and it drops again; reach the Cu Chi or Nha Be fringe and central land's premium has all but evaporated. The fringe parcel is the *marginal* land in Ricardo's sense — the worst location still worth developing — and everything closer to the centre is priced by *how much better* than the fringe it is.

Why does the curve fall so fast? Two forces. First, **proximity has steeply diminishing returns**: being one kilometre from the centre is worth far more than being twenty-one kilometres versus twenty. Second, **transport cost**: the further out you live, the more time and money you spend commuting, so you'll only move out if the land is cheap enough to compensate. People and businesses bid up central land precisely to *save* on transport and to be near each other — and that bidding war is what carves the gradient.

This single curve explains a mountain of real-estate behaviour. It's why the same flat costs three times as much one metro stop closer to the centre. It's why a new bridge or metro line *re-prices* land along its whole route — by shrinking effective distance to the centre, it lifts the gradient over there. And it's why "đất nền" (raw land plots) speculation in HCMC's satellite provinces is really a bet on the gradient *shifting outward* as the city sprawls and infrastructure arrives.

#### Worked example: the rent gradient — centre vs ring

Let's price two identical 50-square-metre parcels, one central and one on the ring, using round gradient numbers.

- **Central parcel (District 1 core):** land ≈ ₫400 million per m². For 50 m²: 50 × ₫400m = **₫20.0 billion** (≈ \$772,000).
- **Ring parcel (Districts 9/12 area):** land ≈ ₫45 million per m². For 50 m²: 50 × ₫45m = **₫2.25 billion** (≈ \$87,000).

Same 50 square metres of the *same planet*, identical in every physical respect — and the central one is worth **nearly nine times** the ring one. Push to the true fringe (₫8 million/m²) and the central land is **fifty times** the fringe per square metre. Nobody added better dirt; the difference is pure location — pure ground rent.

And notice what this does to the land-vs-structure split. If you build the *same* ₫1.5bn house on each parcel:

- Central property = ₫20.0bn land + ₫1.5bn house = ₫21.5bn → land is **93%** of value.
- Fringe property = ₫0.4bn land + ₫1.5bn house = ₫1.9bn → land is **21%** of value.

*The rent gradient doesn't just set land prices — it decides whether you are buying land with a house on it, or a house with a little land under it.*

## Highest-and-best-use: the land is worth its best legal use, not its current one

Now a twist that trips up almost every beginner. We've been talking about "land value" as if a parcel has one obvious price. But land value depends on **what you're allowed to do with the parcel** — and specifically, on the *most valuable* legal, feasible thing you could do, whether or not anyone is doing it today. Appraisers call this **highest-and-best-use** (HBU), and it is the rule that turns parking lots into towers.

The principle: a parcel's land value equals the value it would have under its **highest and best use**, defined as the use that is (1) **legally permitted** (zoning, land-use planning, the land-use-right purpose), (2) **physically possible** on the site, (3) **financially feasible**, and (4) **maximally productive** — i.e., produces the highest land value of all the candidates that pass tests 1–3. The land is worth what its *best* allowed use would make it worth, even if the current owner is doing something far less valuable.

This is why a dilapidated noodle shop can sit on land worth ₫40 billion: the land isn't priced as "a noodle shop's worth of dirt," it's priced as "what a developer would pay to put a tower here." The current use is almost irrelevant to the *land* value; it only tells you how much the owner is *under-using* their land.

![A matrix comparing four uses of the same central plot — parking lot, townhouse, apartment tower, and office — across the land value unlocked and whether each is legal and feasible, with the tower winning](/imgs/blogs/land-vs-buildings-two-assets-in-every-property-4.png)

The matrix above runs the test for one central plot. A bare **parking lot** "unlocks" only ₫8bn — the land is idling, earning a sliver of its potential. A **3-floor townhouse** does better at ₫14bn. An **apartment tower** unlocks the most, ₫40bn, and it's both legally permitted (the plot is zoned for high-rise) and feasible — so *the tower is the highest-and-best-use, and ₫40bn is what the land is worth*. An **office block** might unlock ₫35bn, but if the plot is zoned residential, an office is **illegal** here — so it's struck out, no matter how much money it would theoretically make. Legality is a hard gate: an option you can't legally build adds nothing to land value.

This is the single biggest reason zoning and land-use law are so financially explosive in Vietnam. The **Land Law 2024**, effective August 2024, shifted the country from a fixed 5-year land-price framework to *annual* land-price tables — bringing official valuations closer to market, which directly changes the "land value unlocked" line for every parcel. When a plot gets *rezoned* from low-rise to high-rise, its highest-and-best-use jumps, and the land value can multiply overnight — without a single brick being laid. Rezoning is, quite literally, the act of legally unlocking land value.

#### Worked example: highest-and-best-use uplift, parking lot to tower

Suppose Minh's developer cousin owns a downtown corner currently used as a **parking lot**. As a parking lot, the land generates modest rent and appraises at **₫8.0 billion** under that use. But the plot is zoned for high-rise residential, and an apartment tower built there would, after construction costs, leave the land worth **₫40.0 billion**.

- Land value under current use (parking lot): **₫8.0bn**
- Land value under highest-and-best-use (tower): **₫40.0bn**
- HBU uplift = ₫40.0bn − ₫8.0bn = **₫32.0 billion** of value the land *already has* but the parking lot isn't capturing.

The land is worth ₫40bn *right now*, today, even while it's still a parking lot — because a buyer would pay ₫40bn knowing they can build the tower. The cousin running a parking lot on ₫40bn of land is sitting on ₫32bn of *unrealised* HBU. That gap is exactly why downtown surface parking lots are temporary: someone eventually pays the land's true (HBU) value and builds the tower.

*A parcel is worth its best legal use, not its lazy current one — which is why land "wants" to be developed up to its zoning limit, and why a rezoning can mint billions without moving a shovel.*

## Teardown economics: when the building is worth negative

We can now confront the strangest consequence of the two-asset model: sometimes the structure is worth **less than nothing**. Not zero — *negative*. The building actively *reduces* the value of the property, and a buyer will pay *more* for the bare land than for the land-with-building.

How can that be? Walk through the logic. If a parcel's highest-and-best-use is "build a new tower," then any buyer's plan is: acquire the site, **demolish** whatever is on it, build new. The existing structure isn't an asset to that buyer — it's an *obstacle* that costs money to remove. Demolition isn't free: you pay for the wrecking crew, the debris hauling, the permits, maybe asbestos handling. So the old structure's contribution to value is *negative the cost of getting rid of it*.

When does this happen? When the land has appreciated so much, and the structure depreciated so far, that the building's remaining usefulness is worth less than the demolition cost. On prime, rapidly-appreciating land, this is common — it's the entire premise of the **teardown** market that you see in expensive cities worldwide, from Tokyo (where wooden houses are routinely assumed to have *zero* structure value after ~30 years) to coastal California to central Hanoi.

![A teardown comparison showing an old structure listed at two billion on prime land worth eight billion, where the buyer actually pays nine billion for the land alone and spends three hundred million to demolish the structure that has negative value](/imgs/blogs/land-vs-buildings-two-assets-in-every-property-6.png)

The before-after above shows the trap. On the left, the **seller** lists the property as ₫8.0bn of prime land *plus* ₫2.0bn of "structure" — they're hoping to get paid for the old building. On the right is the **buyer's** reality: the land has actually risen to ₫9.0bn (land alone keeps appreciating), and the structure isn't worth ₫2.0bn or even ₫0 — it's worth **−₫300 million**, because that's what demolition will cost. The buyer is paying for land and *subtracting* the cleanup bill. The structure has become a liability the buyer must pay to make disappear.

This is why you'll see seemingly bizarre listings: "older home, sold as-is, land value" — code for "the building is worth nothing, possibly negative, you're buying the dirt." It's why renovation sometimes makes no sense: pouring ₫1bn into renovating a structure on land headed for a teardown is ₫1bn lit on fire, because the next buyer will demolish it anyway.

#### Worked example: the teardown where the structure is worth negative

Let's run the numbers cleanly. A corner property in a fast-gentrifying central district:

- **Bare land value** (highest-and-best-use, ready to build): **₫9.0 billion** (≈ \$347,000)
- **Existing old structure**: functionally obsolete, would cost **₫300 million** (≈ \$11,600) to demolish and clear
- Therefore **structure contribution to value** = −₫300m
- **Total property value** = land + structure = ₫9.0bn + (−₫0.3bn) = **₫8.7 billion**

So the property *with* its building is worth **₫8.7bn** — *less* than the ₫9.0bn the bare land would fetch. A buyer who paid ₫9.0bn (the seller's "land plus a free building" pitch) overpaid by ₫300m, because they still have to spend ₫300m tearing the building down. The rational buyer offers **₫8.7bn**, demolishes for ₫300m, and ends up holding ₫9.0bn of clean, build-ready land — exactly break-even on the dirt.

*When land races ahead and a structure ages out, the building flips from asset to liability — you're not paying for it, you're paying to get rid of it.*

This negative-structure result is the sharpest possible demonstration of the post's whole thesis. The two assets aren't just *different* — they can have *opposite signs*. Land: +₫9.0bn and rising. Structure: −₫0.3bn and falling. Glue them together and call it "a house," and the two-asset reality is hidden until a buyer with a wrecking ball reveals it.

## Land as a share of home value — and why it keeps rising

We've now seen the land share at a single moment (80% central, 40% suburban). The deeper pattern is that **the land share of a property's value rises over time** and **rises as you move toward the centre** — and both for the same reason: land appreciates while the structure depreciates, so the appreciating half keeps eating a bigger slice of the pie.

### Over time: the structure's slice shrinks every year

Return to the depreciation timeline. Even if land grew at the same rate the structure shrank, the land's *share* would still climb, because the structure is heading to zero while the land is heading up. Add real appreciation on the land and the effect is dramatic.

![A timeline of a building's depreciation from new to fifteen, thirty, and fifty years, with the structure value falling from two billion toward zero while the land rises from five billion to twenty-three billion](/imgs/blogs/land-vs-buildings-two-assets-in-every-property-5.png)

The timeline above walks one property through its life. At **year 0** it's ₫2.0bn structure + ₫5.0bn land (structure = 29% of value). By **year 15** the structure has worn to ₫1.4bn while land has grown to ₫8.0bn (structure = 15%). By **year 30** the structure is ₫0.8bn against ₫12.5bn of land (structure = 6%). By **year 50** the structure is near zero and obsolete — a teardown candidate — while the land has compounded to ₫23bn. The structure's *share* of value collapsed from 29% to nearly nothing, purely from the two assets moving in opposite directions. **A property, held long enough, becomes almost entirely a land holding** with a worn-out building sitting on it as a teardown option.

#### Worked example: the land share climbing over a 20-year hold

Let's prove the share shift with arithmetic the reader can check. Take a property bought as **₫4.2bn land + ₫2.8bn structure = ₫7.0bn** (the same 60/40 mix as our earlier chart). Land compounds at 8% a year; the structure depreciates straight-line over 50 years, losing 2% of its ₫2.8bn original cost — ₫56 million — every year.

At **purchase (year 0)**:

- Land = ₫4.2bn, structure = ₫2.8bn, total = ₫7.0bn
- Land share = ₫4.2bn ÷ ₫7.0bn = **60%**

At **year 10**:

- Land = ₫4.2bn × (1.08)¹⁰ = ₫4.2bn × 2.159 = **₫9.07bn**
- Structure = ₫2.8bn − (10 × ₫56m) = ₫2.8bn − ₫560m = **₫2.24bn**
- Total = ₫11.31bn → land share = ₫9.07bn ÷ ₫11.31bn = **80%**

At **year 20**:

- Land = ₫4.2bn × (1.08)²⁰ = ₫4.2bn × 4.661 = **₫19.58bn**
- Structure = ₫2.8bn − (20 × ₫56m) = ₫2.8bn − ₫1.12bn = **₫1.68bn**
- Total = ₫21.26bn → land share = ₫19.58bn ÷ ₫21.26bn = **92%**

In twenty years the land share went from 60% to 92% without the owner lifting a finger — the appreciating asset ate the wasting one's slice. Note the structure number, ₫1.68bn at year 20, is exactly the endpoint labelled on the value-over-time chart earlier, and the ₫19.58bn land matches its blue line. Same model, two views.

*Hold any property long enough and it converts itself into a land holding: the building's share of value drifts toward zero while the land quietly becomes almost the whole thing.*

### Across the map: the land share rises toward the centre

The other axis is location, and we've already seen it: central land is so expensive that it dominates the value of anything built on it, while cheap fringe land is dwarfed by the cost of the building.

![A stacked bar chart of land share versus structure share of property value across central, midtown, suburb, and rural locations, falling from eighty percent land downtown to twenty percent at the fringe](/imgs/blogs/land-vs-buildings-two-assets-in-every-property-7.png)

The bars above make the gradient of *ownership* explicit. In the **central** core, ~80% of what you pay is land — you are, in effect, buying a location and getting a building thrown in. In the **midtown** ring it's ~65%. In the **suburb** it's ~45% — now you're buying land and building roughly evenly. On the **rural fringe** it's ~20% — you're buying a building, with the cheap land underneath almost an afterthought. The thesis in one picture: *the more central the location, the more you are really buying land.*

This is the precise, quantitative meaning of "**location, location, location**." It's not mysticism. It's that the central buyer's money goes almost entirely into the one asset that appreciates (land), while the fringe buyer's money goes mostly into the one that depreciates (structure). Over a long hold, the central buyer's portfolio is dominated by the compounding asset and the fringe buyer's by the wasting one. Same word — "house" — completely different financial machine.

## Common misconceptions

The two-asset model corrects a whole catalogue of beginner beliefs. Here are the ones worth naming and killing.

**"My house is appreciating."** Usually false as stated. Your *land* is appreciating; your *house* (the structure) is depreciating. What you observe — a higher total price — is the land's gain outrunning the building's loss. This matters the moment the gap narrows: in a flat market, or for a new build on cheap land, the depreciating structure can drag the *total* down even as you faithfully repaint and repair. People who don't separate the two assets are baffled when a "house" loses value; people who do, expected it.

**"A newer house is always a better investment."** Often the reverse. A new house is mostly *structure* — the asset that depreciates — and that structure starts losing value the day it's finished, just like a new car. An older house on better land is mostly *land* — the asset that appreciates. Minh's small, dated, central flat is a better *land* bet than his colleague's big new suburban house precisely because Minh bought more of the appreciating asset. "New" buys you comfort and lower maintenance, not necessarily appreciation.

**"Land always wins / land never goes down."** Dangerously overstated. Land *tends* to appreciate over the long run because of fixed supply and rising demand, but it is not magic. Land prices *can* and *do* crash — spectacularly. Japanese urban land fell roughly 70–80% from its 1990 peak and stayed down for two decades. US land values collapsed in 2008. Vietnamese đất nền in satellite provinces has had violent boom-bust swings (the 2018 fever, the 2022–23 freeze). Land's edge is the *long-run trend*; over any given decade it can hand you a brutal drawdown. "Land always wins" is how people get wiped out at the top of a cycle.

**"The building is most of what I'm buying, so I should focus on it."** Only true on cheap land. The more central and expensive the location, the more your money is land and the *less* the building matters to your financial outcome. Obsessing over granite countertops on a ₫20bn central plot is rearranging deck chairs; the granite is 2% of your value. On the fringe, where the building is 80% of value, build quality genuinely matters. Match your attention to where your money actually is.

**"Renovating always adds value."** Not when the land's highest-and-best-use is a teardown. Money sunk into a structure that the next buyer will demolish is simply destroyed. Renovation adds value only when the structure's improved condition will *survive* into the buyer's plan — i.e., when the building isn't on the chopping block. On prime, redevelopment-ready land, the rational move is often to spend *nothing* on the building and sell the land.

**"Empty land sitting idle is a waste."** From the owner's financial view, often the opposite. Because land appreciates while producing no depreciation and little carrying cost (Vietnam has *no broad annual property tax* — non-agricultural land-use tax is roughly 0.03–0.15% of the state price, trivially small), **holding raw land can be perfectly rational** even while it "does nothing." The owner is letting the appreciating asset compound, tax-light, while waiting for the highest-and-best-use to ripen (a rezoning, a new road). This is *land banking*, and it's a feature of the model, not a bug. It's also exactly why a meaningful holding tax — floated by Vietnam's Ministry of Finance and debated 2023–2026 but not enacted — would change owner behaviour so much: it would make idle land *expensive to hold*, forcing it toward development.

## How it shows up in real markets

The two-asset model isn't a classroom abstraction; it's the engine under the biggest property stories of the last half-century. Here are concrete cases — Vietnamese and global — where land-vs-structure explains what happened.

### Central HCMC land vs the suburb: the same money, two different assets

Go back to Minh and his colleague, but zoom out to the whole market. HCMC primary apartment prices ran roughly ₫91 million/m² in 2024 and ₫111 million/m² (≈ \$4,222/m²) in 2025 — up about a third in a year (CBRE/Ministry of Construction data via VnExpress). That ferocious rise is almost entirely a *land* phenomenon: the structures didn't get a third more valuable; the locations did, as a growing city bid up its finite central sites. Out in the satellite districts, where land is plentiful, primary prices rose far less. The investor who bought central — more land share — captured the move; the investor who bought a big house on cheap fringe land mostly owns a depreciating building and watched it appreciate slowly if at all. **The price-to-income strain — HCMC near 32 years of income, fifth-highest globally (Numbeo/VnExpress, mid-2026) — is fundamentally a story about central *land* scarcity**, not the cost of pouring concrete.

### The Japanese land bubble: when land "always wins" stopped winning

By 1990, Japanese urban land had appreciated so violently that the land under the Imperial Palace in Tokyo was, by one famous back-of-envelope calculation, worth more than all the land in California — and all the land in Japan supposedly exceeded the value of all land in the United States, a country 25 times larger. This was the two-asset model run to its manic extreme: the *land* component had detached from any sane ground-rent it could ever earn. Then it broke. From the 1990 peak, Japanese urban land prices fell roughly 70–80% and *did not recover* for two decades — Japan's "Lost Decades." The lesson cuts directly against "land always wins": land is the appreciating asset *on trend*, but when its price races miles ahead of the ground rent it can actually produce, it can crash as hard as anything. We'll dissect this bubble in its own post; for now it's the cautionary twin of everything above.

### Tokyo today: structures with a 30-year zero

Modern Japan also illustrates the *structure* side at its purest. In much of Japan, wooden houses are conventionally assumed to depreciate to **zero structure value in about 20–30 years** — buyers price a used house as land *minus* the cost to demolish the building. Roughly a sixth of home purchases are followed by a teardown. This is the negative-structure result as a default cultural assumption: the building is a wasting consumable, the land is the asset. It's the cleanest real-world proof that "house" and "land" are separate, and that the structure's natural endpoint is zero (or below).

### US coastal vs heartland: the land share gap

In the United States, economists have measured the land share of home value directly, and it tracks the rent gradient beautifully. In expensive coastal metros — San Francisco, Los Angeles, the New York area — land is often **60–80%+** of a home's value: you're buying location, the house is incidental. In cheaper heartland markets — much of the Midwest and South — land can be **20–30%**: the structure is most of what you own, because buildable land is abundant. This is the same central-vs-fringe split as HCMC, scaled to a continent. It also explains why coastal US home prices are so much more volatile: when most of your value is *land*, and land is the volatile asset, the whole "house" swings with it. The Case-Shiller national index falling ~27% from its 2006 peak to its 2012 trough was overwhelmingly a *land*-price collapse; the structures didn't lose a quarter of their bricks.

### The downtown parking lot: highest-and-best-use, waiting

Every major city has them: a surface parking lot or a low-slung old building occupying a block that is obviously "worth a tower." These are highest-and-best-use gaps held open — sometimes deliberately (the owner is land-banking the appreciating asset, paying trivial holding costs while waiting for the right moment or a rezoning), sometimes stuck on legal or assembly hurdles (you often need to buy several adjacent plots to build big). The parking lot earns a little cash and, more importantly, lets the land compound until the HBU uplift is too large to ignore. When it finally redevelops, the "land value created" was mostly *already there* — it just took a buyer willing to pay the land's true HBU price and demolish whatever sat on it.

### Vietnam's 2022–23 freeze: land, leverage, and the cycle

Vietnam's recent property shock shows the land asset interacting with credit. The 2022–23 corporate-bond crunch — triggered by the Tân Hoàng Minh (April 2022) and Vạn Thịnh Phát/SCB (October 2022) arrests — froze developer funding and stalled projects across the country. Land values in speculative đất nền areas, which had been bid up on the assumption that the gradient would keep marching outward, fell hard and fast when the credit that fuelled the speculation vanished. The State Bank cut policy rates four times in 2023 and Decree 08 gave bond-restructuring relief to thaw the freeze. The episode is a reminder that *land is the appreciating asset, but its price is amplified by leverage* — and leverage cuts both ways. The long-run trend is up; the path is a cycle, and the cycle is where fortunes are made and lost.

### Vietnam's twist: you own the building, but only a *right* to the land

There is a wrinkle that makes the two-asset model even sharper in Vietnam than in most countries, and every local buyer should understand it. Under Vietnamese law, **all land is owned by the people and administered by the state** — you do not buy land outright. What you actually purchase and hold is a **land-use right** (LUR, *quyền sử dụng đất*), recorded on the famous "pink book" (*sổ hồng*). For residential land allocated to households and individuals, that right is **long-term and stable** (effectively indefinite and inheritable in practice), so for a landed house the land asset still behaves much like ownership. But the legal form matters: you own a *right* to the appreciating asset, not the dirt itself, and the state retains powers — to reclaim land for public projects with compensation, and to set the land-price tables that drive your taxes and fees (now updated annually under the Land Law 2024).

The split gets genuinely strange for **apartments**. When you buy a condo unit, you own your unit (structure) plus a *shared* land-use right to the plot under the building — but the building itself has a **defined useful life** stamped on its design (commonly 50–70 years). When the structure reaches the end of that life and is condemned, the residents' claim is to the *land*, not a perpetual right to a unit in the sky. Who pays to rebuild, how the shared land value is divided, and what happens to owners on the 40th floor of a worn-out tower are questions Vietnam is still writing the rulebook for. The two-asset model predicts the tension exactly: the **structure depreciates toward its legal expiry while the land-use right underneath is the thing with lasting value** — which is why a 40-year-old apartment in a prime district trades on its land-share story and its redevelopment odds, not on the tired concrete itself.

#### Worked example: why the central old flat outlives the suburban new one

Minh's dated central flat and his colleague's new suburban house are both "50-year buildings." But Minh's unit sits on land worth ₫16bn for the plot's share; the colleague's sits on land worth ₫1.5bn. When both structures hit year 45 and are condemned, Minh's land-use right is the prize that makes redevelopment obvious and lucrative — a developer will happily fund a new tower to unlock that central land. The colleague's worn house, on cheap land, may simply not be worth rebuilding: the land can't pay for the concrete. *The structure's expiry date is the same for both; what differs is whether there's enough land value underneath to justify a second life — and that, once again, is location.*

## When this matters / Further reading

This isn't abstract. The land-vs-structure split is the lens for almost every real property decision you will ever make.

**When you buy a home**, ask what you're actually buying. A central, slightly older flat is a land bet; a big new suburban house is a structure bet. Neither is "right" — but know which one you're making, because they will behave completely differently over the next twenty years. If appreciation is your goal, you want land share; if comfort and low maintenance are your goal, you're paying for structure, and that's fine, as long as you don't mistake it for an investment.

**When you value a property**, decompose it: estimate the bare-land value (what would the empty lot fetch at its highest-and-best-use?) and add the structure's depreciated, replacement-cost-anchored value — and don't be surprised if, on prime land, the structure number is small or even negative. The total is land + improvements, always.

**When you read the market**, translate the headlines. "House prices up 30%" almost always means "central land up a lot, structures unchanged." "Land always goes up" means "on trend, with cycle-sized drawdowns that can wipe you out at the peak." "Location, location, location" means "you're buying land, and land is priced by location."

When you look at any property — a listing, a tower, your own front door — split it in two. *How much of this is land, and how much is just a wasting building sitting on top of it?* Get that one answer, and most of real estate stops being mysterious.
