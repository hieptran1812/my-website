Learn the local pattern and every global cycle theory suddenly pays off here too.
>
> - **2007–08:** a post-WTO flood of foreign capital, a stock mania (the VN-Index touched ~1,170 in March 2007), and a land frenzy — then a crash when the 2008 crisis pulled the capital back out.
> - **2011–13:** inflation near **18%** in 2011 forced brutally tight money; the market froze, prices fell, and the bad debts were parked at a new state vehicle, **VAMC**, in 2013.
> - **2018:** an *đất nền* (raw land-plot) fever in HCMC's satellite provinces, fed by rumors of new highways and special economic zones that mostly never arrived.
> - **2022–23:** a *corporate-bond* crunch — the Tân Hoàng Minh (Apr-2022) and Vạn Thịnh Phát/SCB (Oct-2022) arrests froze the market that funded developers — stalled projects nationwide, until the SBV cut policy rates four times in 2023 and **Decree 08** let developers restructure their bonds.

In early 2007, a young professional in Ho Chi Minh City — call him **Minh** — watched friends double their money in a year, not by building a business, but simply by buying an apartment off-plan and selling the contract before the building was finished. Vietnam had just joined the World Trade Organization. Foreign money was pouring in. The stock index was climbing almost vertically. The whole country felt, briefly, like it had discovered a machine that turned patience into wealth. Minh, sensibly cautious until then, finally bought near the top of 2007. Within eighteen months the apartment was worth a third less, the developer had stopped building, and the friends who had "discovered the machine" were quietly underwater.

We will follow Minh across four cycles in this post, because his story *is* the story of Vietnamese property — a person of ordinary good sense, repeatedly tempted by a market that looks, each time, like it has finally escaped gravity, and repeatedly reminded that it has not. The four episodes look superficially different. One was a foreign-capital mania. One was an inflation shock. One was a rumor-fed land craze in the provinces. One was a financial-engineering accident in the bond market. But underneath, they are the same machine — the universal property cycle of credit, speculation, and a slow supply response — running on Vietnamese rails.

The figure below is the whole sweep in one picture: two decades, four episodes, and the slow thaw that followed the last freeze. Notice how *violent* the turns are. This is not the gentle, decade-long undulation of a mature market. These are sharp peaks and hard crashes — and by the end of this post you will understand exactly why Vietnam's version of the cycle hits harder, and how to recognize each phase while you are standing in it.

## Foundations: the Vietnamese version of the machine

Before we can tell the four stories, we need the local vocabulary. The universal cycle has a handful of moving parts — credit, leverage, speculation, supply lag, a central bank — and Vietnam implements each one with its own institutions. If you only know the American or Chinese version, half of what follows will be opaque. So let us build the Vietnamese machine from zero, one part at a time. A practitioner can skim; a newcomer cannot proceed without this section.

### The SBV: Vietnam's central bank and credit gatekeeper

The **State Bank of Vietnam (SBV)** is the country's central bank — the equivalent of the US Federal Reserve. But it does its job differently, and the difference *is* the cycle. A Western central bank moves one lever, the policy interest rate, and lets the price of money ripple through the economy. The SBV moves that lever too — its refinancing and discount rates — but it also reaches directly into the banking system and tells individual banks **how much they may lend this year**. That second tool is the *credit-growth cap* or *credit quota* (in Vietnamese, *room tín dụng*), and it has no real analogue in the United States.

Here is why it matters for property. When the SBV grants the banking system, say, 14% credit growth for the year and a bank's quota is nearly used up by October, that bank simply stops writing new mortgages and developer loans until January — regardless of how good the borrower is. Credit does not gently tighten as a price; it *switches off* as a quantity. And because Vietnamese property runs almost entirely on bank credit and, later, bonds, when the tap closes the market does not cool — it freezes. The credit quota is the single most important reason Vietnam's cycles are sharper than developed markets': the supply of money to property is administered, and administered supply can go to zero overnight.

A *basis point* — a term we will use a few times — is one hundredth of a percentage point, so 0.01%. When you read "the SBV cut 100 bps," that means it lowered a rate by one full percentage point, for example from 6% to 5%.

### The VN-Index: the country's sentiment thermometer

The **VN-Index** is the benchmark stock-market index for the Ho Chi Minh City Stock Exchange — Vietnam's equivalent of the S&P 500, started at a base of 100 in July 2000. It matters to a *property* post because in Vietnam, stocks and real estate are tightly linked through a shared driver: liquidity and confidence. When foreign and domestic money floods in, it lifts both at once; when it drains, both fall together. The VN-Index near **1,170 in March 2007** is the single cleanest marker of the first mania — it was the visible tip of a wave of capital that was simultaneously bidding up land. Many of the largest companies on the index are themselves property developers, so the index partly *is* a property index. Watch the VN-Index and you are watching the mood that prices both apartments and shares.

### The corporate-bond channel and the presale model

This is the part that makes the 2022 crisis intelligible, so spend a minute here. A **corporate bond** is a loan in tradable form: a developer wanting ₫1,000 billion to build a project sells investors a piece of paper promising to pay, say, 11% a year and return the principal in three years. The investor — often an ordinary Vietnamese saver, sold the bond across a bank counter — is the lender. The developer gets cash *without* a bank examining the project as closely as a loan officer would.

Through the late 2010s and into 2021, Vietnamese developers leaned heavily on this channel because bank lending to real estate was being squeezed by the credit quotas described above. Bonds were the workaround. By 2021 the real-estate sector was one of the largest issuers of corporate bonds in the country, and a great deal of that paper was sold to retail buyers who treated it like a high-yield savings deposit — without grasping that it was unsecured developer debt.

Layer on the **presale model**, and you see the fragility. In Vietnam, as across Asia, developers sell apartments *before* they are built — collecting deposits and staged payments from buyers, then using that cash to fund construction. The buyer is, in effect, an unsecured lender to the developer too. So a Vietnamese developer is funded by three fragile sources at once: bank loans (rationed by quota), corporate bonds (sold to trusting retail savers), and presale deposits (from buyers betting the building gets finished). All three depend on *confidence*. Shake confidence in any one, and the developer's funding can collapse — which is precisely what happened in 2022.

### Đất nền: raw land plots, the national obsession

**Đất nền** translates literally as "foundation land" — raw or lightly serviced land plots, usually on a city's fringe or in the provinces, sold by the square meter with the implication that you will (someday) build on them. For a huge share of Vietnamese savers, đất nền is *the* asset class. It is tangible, it has no maintenance, it sits on a long cultural belief that land always appreciates, and — crucially — it can be flipped purely on a story: a rumored ring road, a planned bridge, a hoped-for special economic zone. A plot does not need tenants or income to be "worth more"; it needs only a buyer who believes the story.

That is exactly what makes đất nền the most cyclical asset in the country. A rental apartment has a floor — its rent — that catches it when speculation fades. Raw land has *no income at all*. When the story fades and credit vanishes, a đất nền plot has nothing to catch it: no rent, no liquidity, often no buyer at any price. Đất nền is the purest expression of the land-speculation leg of the universal machine, and it is the leg that breaks hardest. We have a whole figure on this later.

### VAMC: where bad debt goes to wait

The **Vietnam Asset Management Company (VAMC)** is a state vehicle created in 2013 to deal with the wreckage of the 2011–13 freeze. When the market crashed, banks were left holding loans against property that was now worth far less than the loan — *non-performing loans*, in the jargon, meaning the borrower has stopped paying. A bank full of non-performing loans cannot lend, which deepens the freeze. VAMC's job was to *buy* (or warehouse) those bad loans off the banks' books — often paying with special bonds rather than cash — so the banks could show cleaner balance sheets and start lending again. Critics noted it mostly *parked* the bad debt rather than truly resolving it, but as a cycle marker it is perfect: **VAMC's creation in 2013 dates the bottom of the second cycle**, the moment the state formally acknowledged the freeze and built the plumbing to thaw it.

### Decree 08: the 2023 bond-restructuring relief valve

**Decree 08** (Nghị định 08), issued in March 2023, is the policy equivalent of VAMC for the bond crisis. When developers could not repay the bonds coming due in 2022–23 because the market had frozen and they could not refinance, the government issued Decree 08 to give them legal room to **restructure**: extend bond maturities by up to two years with bondholder consent, and — strikingly — *repay bondholders in assets such as apartments* instead of cash. It also temporarily relaxed some investor-suitability rules. Decree 08 did not make the losses vanish; it stretched them over time so that a wall of maturities did not force a wave of developer bankruptcies all at once. It is the second half of the 2022–23 story and a clear marker of the policy turn toward thaw.

### Credit-growth caps, again — the lever that overshoots

We met the credit quota above, but it deserves one more pass because it appears in *every* Vietnamese cycle as the accelerator and the brake. The SBV expands credit growth generously in good years (the gas pedal) and slams it shut when inflation or speculation alarms it (the brake). Because property is the most credit-dependent asset, it feels both extremes most violently. Easy credit + the cultural belief that land only rises + a thin, illiquid market = a mania. Then the brake comes down hard, and the same thinness that amplified the boom amplifies the bust. The quota is why Vietnam does not get gentle cycles; it gets whiplash.

### Why Vietnam's cycles are sharper than developed markets'

Pulling it together, four structural features make Vietnamese property cycles more violent than, say, American or Singaporean ones:

1. **Thin, immature credit.** Mortgage finance is young, often short-term, and frequently structured as a *teaser-then-reset* loan — a low promotional rate for 12–24 months, then a floating reset to 12–14%. Borrowers buy on the teaser and choke on the reset. (We unpack the mechanics of this trap in [leverage and the mortgage](/blog/trading/real-estate/leverage-and-the-mortgage-how-debt-amplifies-property).)
2. **Extreme speculation.** A large fraction of buyers are not end-users but flippers, and đất nền lets them speculate on pure land with no income floor.
3. **Opaque, channel-concentrated funding.** When developers depend on one fragile channel — bonds in 2021 — a single shock to that channel freezes the whole industry. There is little diversification of funding.
4. **Weak data and high informality.** Prices, transaction volumes, and inventory are poorly measured, so the market is driven by rumor and sentiment more than by fundamentals — which makes it overshoot in both directions.

With that machine assembled, let us watch it run four times.

## 2007–08: the post-WTO mania and bust

Vietnam joined the WTO in January 2007, and the event acted like opening a valve. Global investors, newly able to access a fast-growing frontier economy, poured capital in. Foreign direct investment surged; portfolio money chased the stock market; and a great deal of it found its way, directly or indirectly, into land. The VN-Index, which had been around 750 at the start of 2007, rocketed to roughly **1,170 by March 2007** — a level it would not durably reclaim for *fourteen years*. That spike is the cleanest single marker of the mania, because the same flood of liquidity that lifted shares was simultaneously bidding up apartments and land.

In the property market, the symptoms were textbook bubble. Off-plan apartments in HCMC and Hanoi were sold and resold *before construction*, sometimes changing hands several times in months, each flip at a higher price. The buyer was not pricing the future rent of an apartment; they were pricing the *next buyer's belief*. Prices in prime HCMC districts roughly doubled in a year in the hottest segments. Easy bank credit fed the frenzy — banks, flush and competitive, lent freely against property that was rising so fast the loan looked safe by the time the ink dried.

Then two things broke it. First, domestically, inflation began surging in late 2007 and the SBV slammed on the brakes in 2008 — raising rates and tightening credit hard to defend the currency and tame prices. Second, and decisively, the **global financial crisis** hit in 2008. The foreign capital that had flooded in on the WTO story reversed: portfolio investors pulled money out of frontier markets worldwide, and Vietnam was no exception. The VN-Index collapsed from ~1,170 to around 235 by early 2009 — a fall of roughly **80%**. Property, lifted by the same wave, fell with it. Off-plan prices in the hot segments dropped 30–50%; the developers who had been flipping land found no buyers; projects stalled.

This is the universal machine in its first Vietnamese run: a credit-and-capital flood (the fuel), pure speculation on extrapolated prices (the mania), and then a sudden withdrawal of the fuel (the bust), amplified by a thin, leveraged, sentiment-driven market. Note the distinctively *Vietnamese* element: the trigger was an external capital flood tied to a single political event (WTO entry), and the reversal was an external shock (the 2008 crisis) compounded by the SBV's blunt credit brake. A frontier market lives and dies by the tide of foreign capital in a way a mature market does not.

There is a second-order Vietnamese feature worth naming, because it recurs. Much of the bank lending behind the 2007 boom flowed through *state-owned* and newly-private commercial banks that were expanding aggressively, often with thin risk controls and cozy ties to the developers they financed. Credit did not just expand in aggregate; it expanded *into property specifically*, because property was the collateral everyone trusted and the asset everyone was chasing. That concentration of bank exposure to a single rising asset is precisely what turns a price correction into a banking problem — and it is why the *next* cycle's bust, in 2011–13, left the banks so badly impaired that the state had to build VAMC to clean them up. The 2007 mania planted the bad-debt seeds that the 2011–13 freeze would harvest. Cycles are not independent events; each one's excess becomes the next one's vulnerability.

One more detail makes 2008 distinctively local: the *currency*. As foreign capital fled in 2008, it sold đồng to buy dollars, putting the currency under pressure and forcing the SBV to defend it with even tighter money than the inflation fight alone would have required. In a frontier economy, a property bust, a stock crash, a credit squeeze, and a currency defense tend to arrive *together*, each amplifying the others — a reinforcing knot that a large, deep economy with a reserve currency simply does not face. This currency-and-capital fragility is a thread through 2008, 2011, and the late-2022 đồng defense alike.

#### Worked example: Minh's 2007 round-trip

Minh buys an off-plan apartment in District 7, HCMC, in mid-2007 for **₫2.0 billion (≈ \$120,000** at the ~2007 rate of roughly ₫16,500 per dollar**)**. He pays a 30% deposit of ₫600 million from savings and finances the rest with staged payments, expecting — as everyone does — to sell the contract before completion at a tidy markup. For a few months it works on paper: a neighbor's identical unit trades hands at ₫2.5 billion, so Minh's paper gain looks like ₫500 million.

Then 2008 arrives. Credit tightens, the crisis hits, and the off-plan market goes bidless. By early 2009 the most recent comparable transaction in his project is **₫1.3 billion** — a 35% drop from his ₫2.0 billion purchase. Minh is not wiped out, because he is not fully leveraged, but his ₫600 million deposit is now worth, against a ₫1.3 billion unit with ₫1.4 billion of staged payments still owed, deeply negative: he owes more than the apartment is worth. He cannot sell without crystallizing a loss of roughly **₫700 million (≈ \$42,000)** — more than his entire down payment. He hangs on, makes payments through gritted teeth, and only claws back to break-even years later.

*The lesson Minh learns first: in a mania you are not buying an apartment, you are buying the next person's confidence — and confidence, unlike rent, can go to zero overnight.*

## 2011–13: inflation, tight money, the freeze, and VAMC

The recovery from 2009 was fast but unstable. A large fiscal-and-credit stimulus in 2009–10 reflated the economy and the property market — but it also lit the fuse on inflation. By 2011, Vietnam's consumer price inflation reached roughly **18% year-on-year**, one of the highest rates in Asia. An 18% inflation rate is an emergency: it erodes savings, destabilizes the currency, and forces a central bank to choose between the economy and the price level. The SBV chose the price level.

What followed was the tightest money Vietnam had seen in years. The SBV pushed policy rates sharply higher and — using its quota tool — throttled credit growth hard, with property singled out as a "non-priority" sector to be starved first. Lending rates to property developers and buyers spiked; some effective borrowing costs exceeded 20%. For a market that runs on credit, this was oxygen deprivation. Transactions collapsed. Prices, which had recovered after 2009, rolled over and fell — 20–30% in many segments, more in the speculative fringe — over 2011–2013. The market did not crash in a single spectacular day the way the stock index had in 2008; it *froze*. Inventory piled up. Developers who had built into the 2010 recovery now sat on unsold towers with loans they could not service.

The figure below shows the squeeze that did it: inflation spiking toward 18% in 2011, the policy response choking the market, and the 2013 creation of VAMC marking the bottom.

![A line chart of Vietnam from 2010 to 2014 showing consumer price inflation rising to about 18 percent in 2011 alongside a sharply higher policy interest rate, with a marker for the creation of VAMC in 2013](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-property-cycles-2007-2013-2022-4.png)

The 2011–13 freeze is the universal machine running in *reverse*, and the lever that ran it was money. (If you want the general theory of how the price of money drives every asset, the macro companion is [the liquidity cycle and asset prices](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-liquidity-cycle-and-asset-prices).) The distinctively Vietnamese feature was the *quota brake*: the freeze was not just a higher price of credit, it was an administered *cutoff* of credit to property, which is far more abrupt than a rate hike alone.

By 2013 the wreckage was systemic. Banks were stuffed with non-performing loans against frozen property. To break the deadlock, the government created **VAMC** in 2013 to warehouse the bad debt off the banks' books, and it rolled out a ₫30 trillion support package for affordable housing to put a floor under the bottom. These actions did not instantly revive the market, but they dated the bottom — and from 2014 onward, with inflation tamed and credit loosening again, the market slowly recovered. The cycle had completed: stimulus → reflation → inflation → tight money → freeze → state cleanup → slow thaw.

#### Worked example: the 2011 squeeze on a leveraged buyer

In 2010, during the post-stimulus reflation, a buyer — Minh's cousin **Lan** — buys a ₫3.0 billion (≈ \$150,000) apartment in Hanoi with a ₫2.1 billion loan (70% loan-to-value) at a promotional rate of **12%**, planning to refinance or sell within two years. Her monthly payment at 12% on a 20-year amortizing loan is roughly **₫23 million (≈ \$1,150)**.

Then the 2011 squeeze hits. Her teaser period ends and the loan floats to a crisis-era rate of **20%**. Her monthly payment jumps to roughly **₫35 million (≈ \$1,750)** — a 52% increase — at the exact moment her household's real income is being eroded by 18% inflation. Worse, she cannot sell to escape: the market is frozen, and the most recent comparable for her flat has fallen to **₫2.3 billion**, below her ₫2.1 billion loan after costs. She is trapped between a payment she can barely make and a sale that would not clear her debt. She survives only by cutting every other expense to the bone and waiting two years for rates to fall.

*The lesson: tight money does not just lower prices — it raises your payment and freezes your exit at the same time, which is why a leveraged buyer fears the squeeze far more than the headline price drop.*

## 2018: the đất nền fever

The 2014–2019 recovery was, for most of its length, healthier than the manias around it — real end-user demand from a young, urbanizing population and a growing middle class. But every long expansion grows a speculative head, and Vietnam's appeared in 2017–2018 in its most characteristic form: an **đất nền fever** in the provinces and satellite zones around the big cities.

The story-fuel was specific. The government floated plans for three **special economic zones (SEZs)** — Vân Đồn in the north, Bắc Vân Phong in the center, and Phú Quốc island in the south — with the promise of special incentives and a flood of development. The mere *prospect* of an SEZ law set off a land rush: speculators poured into these areas and bid raw land up several-fold in months on nothing but the expectation that the zones would be created and land would become scarce. Simultaneously, plots in HCMC's satellite provinces — Long An, Đồng Nai, Bình Dương, Bà Rịa–Vũng Tàu — were bid up on rumors of new ring roads, airports (Long Thành), and industrial parks. In the hottest spots, đất nền prices doubled or tripled in 2017–2018.

This is the **land-speculation leg** of the machine in its purest form. Recall the key property of đất nền: it has *no income*. A flipper buying a fringe plot is making a single bet — that a greater fool will pay more, soon — with no rent to fall back on if the story fades. And the stories were thin. In mid-2018 the National Assembly **postponed** the SEZ law indefinitely amid public protests. The single biggest pillar under the đất nền fever was kicked away. Local authorities in several provinces moved to *freeze* land-plot subdivision and transactions to halt the speculation. The fever broke in the hottest SEZ-adjacent areas almost overnight; prices in some Vân Đồn and Phú Quốc plots fell sharply, and many speculators were left holding land they could not sell.

The 2018 episode is important precisely because it was *not* a full national crash — the broader market kept rising into 2019. It was a *localized speculative spike* in the riskiest corner of the market, the raw-land fringe, and it shows the machine's land-speculation leg overshooting on its own even while the credit cycle was still benign. It also rehearsed the lesson that 2022 would teach at national scale: the assets with no income floor — raw land, off-plan paper — are the first to freeze and the last to thaw.

The đất nền fever also exposes a peculiarly Vietnamese information problem. In a mature market, you can look up the price-per-square-meter history of a neighborhood, the rental yields, the inventory, the months-of-supply — and a speculative spike shows up against that baseline as obviously anomalous. In the 2018 đất nền market there *was* no reliable baseline. Prices were whatever the last roadside land-brokerage banner claimed, transactions were often informal, and "the new airport is coming" was the entire valuation model. When the market is priced on rumor because there is no data to price it on fundamentals, it can detach from reality further and faster than a well-measured market ever could — and snap back just as violently when the rumor dies. The thinness of Vietnamese property data is not a footnote; it is a structural amplifier of the cycle, and it is at its worst in exactly the raw-land segment where speculation is most concentrated.

There is also a recurrence worth flagging: đất nền fevers are not a one-off. A milder land-plot rush flared again in 2021–early 2022 in provinces near planned infrastructure, riding the same psychology, before the bond crunch ended it. The pattern — infrastructure rumor, leverage, a plot-flipping frenzy, then a freeze — is a *standing feature* of the Vietnamese market that reappears whenever credit is easy and a plausible story is available. Recognizing the template is more useful than memorizing any single episode.

#### Worked example: a đất nền plot bought 2018, frozen 2022

Minh, older and (he thinks) wiser, avoids the apartment trap this time and buys what "everyone knows is safe": a **500 m² đất nền plot** in a satellite district near a rumored ring-road interchange, in early 2018, for **₫2.5 billion (≈ \$110,000** at ~₫22,800/USD**)**. He finances 60% with a bank loan of ₫1.5 billion. Within 18 months a neighboring plot trades at **₫4.5 billion** — a paper gain of ₫2.0 billion. Minh feels vindicated: land always wins.

He does not sell, because why would you sell something that only goes up? Then the ring-road timeline slips, the broader credit climate tightens, and by the 2022 bond freeze the satellite-province land market is bidless. The last comparable plot transacts at **₫3.0 billion** — and even that is a fire-sale; most plots simply do not trade at all. Mark Minh's plot to that ₫3.0 billion. His paper "gain" of ₫2.0 billion has evaporated to ₫0.5 billion, but the real problem is liquidity: he *cannot realize even the ₫3.0 billion* because there is no buyer. Meanwhile his loan's teaser has reset to **14%**, costing him roughly **₫17.5 million (≈ \$700) a month** in interest on a plot that produces **zero income** to offset it. The plot bleeds him every month while he waits, helpless, for a buyer.

*The lesson: raw land feels safer than an apartment because it "can't fall apart" — but an apartment at least has rent to catch it, and đất nền has nothing; when credit vanishes, a plot with no income and no buyer is the most dangerous thing you can own.*

## 2022–23: the bond crunch and the freeze

The fourth cycle is the most instructive because its trigger was not inflation, not foreign capital, not a land rumor, but the **funding plumbing itself**. Through 2019–2021, with bank credit to property rationed by quota, developers had increasingly funded themselves by issuing **corporate bonds** — much of it sold to retail savers as a high-yield alternative to bank deposits. By 2021, real estate was one of the largest corporate-bond issuers in Vietnam, and a wall of those bonds was set to mature in 2022–2024. The whole structure depended on developers being able to *roll over* maturing bonds by issuing new ones — refinancing old debt with new debt. That works only as long as buyers keep showing up.

In **April 2022**, the chairman of **Tân Hoàng Minh**, a prominent developer, was arrested and roughly ₫10 trillion of the group's bonds were cancelled by regulators over fraudulent issuance. The message to every retail bond buyer in the country was sudden and clear: this paper you thought was as safe as a deposit can be voided, and you can lose everything. Demand for new developer bonds evaporated. Then in **October 2022** came the far larger shock: the arrest of the chair of **Vạn Thịnh Phát**, a sprawling property conglomerate, in connection with a massive fraud at **Saigon Commercial Bank (SCB)** — later revealed as one of the largest financial frauds in the country's history. The news triggered a **run on SCB**, which the SBV had to step in to backstop. Confidence in developer bonds did not just weaken; it collapsed.

The figure below traces the chain — how two arrests propagated, step by step, into a nationwide freeze, and how policy eventually arrested the fall.

![A pipeline diagram of the 2022 bond freeze chain running from the Tan Hoang Minh and Van Thinh Phat arrests through the bond market freezing, developer funding stopping, and projects stalling, to the SBV cutting rates four times and Decree 08 relief beginning a slow thaw](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-property-cycles-2007-2013-2022-2.png)

Trace the cascade. With the bond market frozen, developers could not issue new bonds to repay the maturing ones — their primary refinancing channel was shut. At the same time, the SBV, fighting its *own* battle to defend the đồng against a surging US dollar in late 2022, raised rates and kept credit tight, so the *bank* channel was no relief either. And presale cash dried up because buyers, watching projects stall and developers wobble, stopped putting deposits down. All three funding sources — bonds, bank loans, presales — choked at once. Developers ran out of cash to finish projects *or* repay debt. Construction sites across the country went quiet; some large developers teetered on default; secondary-market apartment and land prices fell 20–30% in many segments. The market froze as hard as it had in 2011–13, but this time the cause was a *funding-channel* collapse, not an inflation shock.

The policy response, through 2023, was a deliberate thaw. **Decree 08** (March 2023) gave developers legal cover to restructure their bonds — extending maturities up to two years and even repaying bondholders in apartments — so that the wall of maturities did not force a wave of bankruptcies. And as global inflation pressure eased and the đồng stabilized, the **SBV cut its policy rates four times over 2023**, lowering its refinancing rate from 6.0% toward 4.5% and pushing lending rates down with it. Cheaper credit plus bond relief began, slowly, to unfreeze the market. It was not a V-shaped recovery — many stalled projects stayed stalled, and the worst frauds played out in court for years — but the free-fall stopped and a halting recovery began in 2024–2025.

#### Worked example: a developer caught in the 2022 bond freeze

Consider an illustrative mid-sized HCMC developer, **Nhà Việt JSC** (a stand-in, not a real company), which by 2021 has funded a large township project with **₫2,000 billion (≈ \$87 million)** of three-year corporate bonds paying **11%**, sold mostly to retail investors through a bank. The plan, like everyone's, is to roll the bonds at maturity by issuing new ones, using fresh bond cash plus presale deposits to keep building and to repay the old holders.

In 2022, ₫800 billion of those bonds come due. In a normal year, Nhà Việt issues ₫800 billion of new bonds and uses the proceeds to repay the maturing ones — a clean rollover. But after the April and October arrests, *no one will buy a new developer bond at any yield*. The rollover fails. Nhà Việt must find ₫800 billion in cash it does not have: bank credit is shut by quota and rate hikes, and presale deposits have collapsed because buyers see the project stalling. It cannot pay. It defaults on the ₫800 billion — retail savers who thought they held a safe deposit now hold a defaulted bond — and construction halts, which further kills presales, which deepens the cash hole. Only **Decree 08** offers a way out: with bondholder consent, Nhà Việt extends the maturities two years and offers some holders apartments in lieu of cash, buying time for the SBV's rate cuts to revive demand.

*The lesson: a developer that funds long-dated buildings with short-dated bonds it must constantly roll over is solvent only as long as the bond market stays open — and a single fraud scandal can slam that window shut for everyone at once.*

## The 2023–2026 thaw and where we are now

The thaw that began in 2023 has been real but uneven, and understanding its shape matters because *we are still living in it*. Three forces drove the recovery. First, the **SBV's four rate cuts** in 2023 lowered the cost of credit across the board, easing the squeeze on both developers and buyers. Second, **Decree 08** stretched the bond maturity wall over additional years, converting an acute crisis into a chronic-but-survivable one. Third, **Land Law 2024** (effective August 2024) overhauled the legal framework — most importantly replacing the old, rigid five-year framework land-price table with *annual* land-price tables meant to track market reality more closely, which reduces a chronic source of dispute and stalled projects (land valuation for compensation and fees).

The recovery's character is worth naming precisely. It has been **bifurcated**: prime, completed apartments in central Hanoi and HCMC by reputable developers recovered first and fastest — by 2024–2025, primary apartment prices in the two cities were rising sharply again (Hanoi primary averages climbing toward ~100 million VND/m² and HCMC toward ~111 million VND/m², roughly **\$4,200/m²** at ~₫25,900/USD). Meanwhile the speculative fringe — distant đất nền, stalled projects from weak developers, half-finished townships tied up in legal disputes — stayed frozen far longer, and much of it has not recovered at all. This is the cycle's signature again: income-producing, completed, prime assets recover first; income-less, speculative, fringe assets recover last or never.

#### Worked example: the 2023 SBV rate cut on a buyer's payment

Return to Minh, now a chastened end-user (not a flipper) in 2023, finally buying a real apartment to live in. He takes a **₫3.5 billion (≈ \$135,000)** mortgage. In early 2023, before the cuts, his floating rate is **13.9%** — the post-teaser ceiling that SOE banks were citing — giving a monthly payment on a 20-year loan of roughly **₫43 million (≈ \$1,660)**.

Over 2023 the SBV cuts four times, and by year-end Minh's floating rate has reset down to about **11%**. On the same ₫3.5 billion, 20-year loan, his monthly payment falls to roughly **₫36 million (≈ \$1,390)** — a saving of about **₫7 million (≈ \$270) every month**, or ₫84 million a year. That is a 16% cut in his payment for doing nothing but holding the same loan through a rate-cut cycle. Multiply that relief across millions of borrowers and you see *why* a central-bank easing cycle thaws a frozen property market: it does not just lower the price of new credit, it lowers the *payment on existing debt*, freeing household cash flow and reviving demand.

*The lesson: the same policy lever that froze the market in 2011 and 2022 — the price and quantity of credit — is the lever that thaws it; watch the SBV, and you are watching the thermostat for the whole market.*

### Where the cycle sits as of mid-2026

It is fair to ask: are we early or late in the current Vietnamese cycle? The honest answer is that the post-2023 recovery has the shape of an *early-to-mid expansion* in the prime segment and a still-frozen *recession* in the fringe — the two phases coexisting, which is itself a sign of how uneven this thaw has been. Prime Hanoi and HCMC apartment prices have not just recovered but pushed to new highs, with primary averages up sharply in 2024–25; that is expansion behavior. But the bond maturity wall stretched out by Decree 08 still looms, many fringe and weak-developer projects remain stalled in legal limbo, and affordability has deteriorated to the point where HCMC's price-to-income ratio of ~32 years is among the most stretched on Earth. A market where prime prices make new highs while affordability hits record lows and a chunk of the supply is still frozen is not a clean, healthy expansion; it is a recovery balanced on cheap credit and pent-up demand, with the next vulnerability — over-stretched valuations and a still-fragile funding system — already visible. Whether the next turn is a gentle plateau or another sharp freeze will, as always, be decided by the same two levers: the SBV's rate and its credit quota.

## What's the same and what's distinctively Vietnamese

Step back from the four stories and the structure becomes clear. The figure below splits the machine into the part that is *universal* — portable to any market you will ever analyze — and the part that is *distinctively Vietnamese*.

![A grid comparing what is universal in property cycles versus what is distinctively Vietnamese across funding, speculation, the policy lever, and the backstop](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-property-cycles-2007-2013-2022-7.png)

**What is universal** — the same in Phoenix, Tokyo, Shanghai, and Saigon:

- **A credit cycle drives the boom and the bust.** Property is the most credit-dependent asset on Earth, so when credit expands, prices rise faster than incomes; when it contracts, they fall. Every one of Vietnam's four cycles turned on the availability of credit. This is the engine we built in [the credit cycle and property prices](/blog/trading/real-estate/the-credit-cycle-and-property-prices).
- **Land is bid up on extrapolation.** People price land off the recent past — "it doubled last year, so it will double again" — which is exactly the psychology that makes manias and crashes. The đất nền fever is this universal trait in a local costume.
- **A supply lag makes the market overshoot.** Buildings take years to finish, so the supply responding to a boom arrives into the bust. This [construction lag](/blog/trading/real-estate/anatomy-of-a-real-estate-cycle-four-phases) is why property cycles exist everywhere.

**What is distinctively Vietnamese:**

- **The corporate-bond funding channel.** Developers' heavy, concentrated reliance on retail-sold bonds (because bank credit was quota-rationed) created a single point of failure that froze the entire industry in 2022 — a uniquely Vietnamese fragility.
- **Đất nền as a mass-market speculative asset.** The cultural centrality of raw land plots, traded on pure story with no income floor, makes the speculation leg unusually large and unusually fragile.
- **Credit quotas.** The SBV's administered cap on bank lending growth turns credit tightening from a gentle price signal into an abrupt quantity cutoff — the single biggest reason Vietnam's cycles are sharper than developed markets'.
- **A state that tends to rescue.** When the freeze gets systemic, the state intervenes structurally — VAMC in 2013, Decree 08 and four rate cuts in 2023 — putting a floor under the market in a way that shapes expectations (and feeds the "the state will always save it" belief we tackle below).

## Common misconceptions

Vietnamese property is wrapped in beliefs that feel like common sense and are, on the evidence of these four cycles, dangerous. Here are the ones that cost people the most.

**"Vietnamese property only goes up."** This is the belief at the root of every mania, and it is false. It is *true* that over very long horizons, urban land in a fast-growing, urbanizing economy has appreciated enormously — that is the grain of truth that makes the myth sticky. But the path is not a smooth upward line; it is the violent saw-tooth in this post's cover figure. Buyers who bought near the 2007 top, the 2010 reflation peak, or the 2021–22 top all sat underwater for *years*. "Up over the long run" and "down hard at the wrong entry point" are both true, and confusing the first for the second is how people get hurt. The price-to-income ratio in HCMC is now around **32 years of income** (one of the highest in the world — for context the global median is ~15), which tells you how much *future* appreciation is already priced in, and how little margin of safety remains at today's prices.

**"Đất nền is safe because it's real land."** This post's clearest lesson is the opposite. Raw land is the *most* dangerous property asset in a downturn, precisely because it has no income. An apartment that falls 30% still produces rent; a đất nền plot that falls 30% produces nothing while you pay 14% interest on the loan against it, and worse, it may not trade *at all* — illiquidity, not just price, is the killer. Tangibility is not safety. A plot you can stand on but cannot sell is not "safe"; it is a frozen liability with a property-tax-free veneer of solidity. (The 2018-into-2022 worked example above is exactly this trap.)

**"The state will always rescue the market."** The state *has* intervened repeatedly — VAMC in 2013, Decree 08 and rate cuts in 2023 — which makes this belief feel earned. But rescue is not the same as making you whole. The 2023 rescue stopped the free-fall; it did not refund the retail savers whose Tân Hoàng Minh and SCB-linked bonds were cancelled or defaulted, and it did not un-freeze the stalled fringe projects. State intervention puts a floor under *systemic* collapse to protect the banking system and social stability; it does not protect the individual speculator from the consequences of buying the wrong asset at the wrong price. Counting on a bailout to your specific plot is a category error.

**"A high rental yield, or a low one, tells you the market is cheap or expensive."** Vietnamese rental yields on apartments are famously low — often 3–5% gross, sometimes lower in prime areas — because so much of the price is speculative land value and expected appreciation, not income. A foreign investor used to a 6–8% cap-rate market reads that as "expensive" and a local reads it as "normal, you make money on appreciation." Both are partly right: a low yield *does* mean you are paying up for expected growth and have less income cushion if growth fails. The yield is information about *where the return is supposed to come from*, not a simple cheap/expensive verdict. (We unpack yields and cap rates in [cap rate, NOI, and the income approach](/blog/trading/real-estate/cap-rate-noi-and-the-income-approach).)

**"The 2022 freeze was caused by the property market overbuilding."** Tempting, but mostly wrong about the *trigger*. There was over-leverage and some over-supply in the fringe, yes — but the 2022 freeze was triggered by a **funding-channel** shock, not a glut. The arrests froze the bond market; the bond market freezing is what stopped developer cash; *that* stalled projects. A market with the same physical supply but a healthy funding channel would not have frozen the way it did. Mistaking a financing accident for a supply glut leads you to the wrong lesson about what to watch — watch the funding, not just the cranes.

**"Foreigners drive Vietnamese property prices."** Foreign capital matters at the margin and dominated the *2007 mania specifically* (the WTO flood), but foreign ownership of residential property is legally capped and a small share of the total market. The dominant forces are domestic: local credit, domestic speculators, and domestic savers' obsession with land and bonds. The 2022 crisis was an almost entirely *domestic* affair — Vietnamese developers, Vietnamese bonds, Vietnamese savers. Overweighting "foreign money" in your model of the market will leave you blind to the domestic credit and funding dynamics that actually drive it.

## How it shows up in real markets

Here the four cycles become concrete — specific names, provinces, dates, and tickers — with anything reconstructed or approximate flagged as illustrative.

**The 2007 VN-Index spike and the off-plan flip culture (2007–08).** The VN-Index reaching ~1,170 in March 2007 is the documented high-water mark; it then fell roughly 80% into early 2009 as the WTO capital flood reversed in the global crisis. In the property market, the most visible symptom was *off-plan contract flipping* in HCMC's District 2 and District 7 and Hanoi's western developments — apartments resold several times before completion. Precise transaction-level price changes from that era are poorly documented (a recurring problem with Vietnamese data), but contemporaneous reporting put prime-segment off-plan declines at 30–50% from the 2007–08 peak. The mechanism is the universal one: a capital flood inflates prices via pure speculation, then withdraws.

**The 2011 macro emergency and the property freeze (2011–13).** CPI inflation near 18% in 2011 is the documented driver; the SBV's response — sharply higher rates and quota tightening, with real estate named a non-priority sector — is the policy fact. The result was a multi-year freeze and 20–30%+ price declines across many segments (the deeper fringe falls are illustrative ranges, as comprehensive indices barely existed). The *institutional* marker is precise: VAMC was established in 2013 to warehouse the resulting bank bad debt, and a ₫30 trillion affordable-housing support package was launched to put a floor under the bottom. This cycle is the cleanest example of the credit-quota brake turning a rate hike into an outright freeze.

**The đất nền and SEZ fever (2017–18).** The three proposed special economic zones — Vân Đồn (Quảng Ninh), Bắc Vân Phong (Khánh Hòa), and Phú Quốc (Kiên Giang) — set off a documented land rush; the National Assembly's *postponement* of the SEZ law in mid-2018 (amid public protests) is the documented event that broke the speculation, and several provincial authorities moved to halt land-plot transactions to cool the frenzy. Reported price moves — doubling or tripling in months in the hottest plots, then sharp falls — are widely cited but, again, poorly indexed, so treat the exact multiples as illustrative. The lesson is exact even if the numbers are fuzzy: pure raw-land speculation on a thin story is the most fragile leg of the market.

**The Tân Hoàng Minh and Vạn Thịnh Phát/SCB shocks (2022).** These are the documented triggers of the fourth cycle: the April 2022 arrest of Tân Hoàng Minh's chairman and the cancellation of ~₫10 trillion of its bonds, then the October 2022 arrest of the Vạn Thịnh Phát chair tied to the SCB fraud — later described as among the largest financial frauds in Vietnam's history — which triggered a run on SCB that the SBV had to backstop. The corporate-bond market's new issuance collapsed in the months that followed. These are not illustrative; they are the named events that froze developer funding nationwide.

**Novaland and the stalled-project wave (2022–23).** Novaland (HoSE ticker **NVL**), one of Vietnam's largest listed developers, became the public face of the developer cash crunch: its share price fell sharply in late 2022 as the market feared it could not service its debt, and several of its large projects (including the vast Aqua City and NovaWorld developments) stalled pending restructuring. The specifics of any single firm's restructuring are detailed and evolving, so treat firm-level figures as illustrative — but the *pattern* (a listed developer whose bond-and-presale funding model seized up, whose stock cratered, whose projects stalled, and which then leaned on Decree 08 restructuring) is the representative case of the 2022–23 freeze.

**The 2023 policy rescue and the bifurcated thaw (2023–26).** Documented: the SBV cut its policy rates four times through 2023 (the refinancing rate easing from 6.0% toward 4.5%); Decree 08 (March 2023) enabled bond restructuring and asset-for-debt swaps; Land Law 2024 (effective August 2024) replaced the framework land-price table with annual tables. The *bifurcation* of the recovery — prime completed Hanoi/HCMC apartments rising again into 2024–25 (primary averages climbing toward ~100M VND/m² in Hanoi and ~111M VND/m² in HCMC) while fringe đất nền and stalled projects lagged — is the documented shape of where Vietnam sits now. It is the income-vs-no-income lesson, recovering in real time.

**The global rhyme.** None of this is uniquely Vietnamese in *kind*. The bond-funded developer that cannot roll its debt is China's Evergrande in miniature; the credit-flood mania and reversal is the US in 2004–08; the inflation-and-tight-money freeze is what many emerging markets suffered in 2022. The portfolio-level intuition — why property zigs and zags with credit and rates relative to other assets — is laid out in [the cross-asset view of real estate](/blog/trading/cross-asset/real-estate-reits-income-leverage-and-rates). Vietnam runs the universal machine; it just runs it faster and rougher.

## When this matters / Further reading

This post matters the next time you stand inside a Vietnamese property boom and it feels, again, like the machine has escaped gravity. It has not. The four cycles in this post are not four different worlds; they are one machine running four times, and every time the warning signs were the same: credit expanding faster than incomes, land bid up on story rather than income, a single funding channel doing too much of the work, and a chorus insisting that *this time* property only goes up. If you can name the phase you are standing in — recovery, expansion, hypersupply, or freeze — and you can see which funding channel is carrying the boom, you can act on the only thing anyone can actually act on: not where prices will be, but what the market is about to do next.

For a Vietnamese reader, the practical payoff is concrete. Before you buy, ask: *What is funding this market right now — bank credit on a generous quota, retail-sold developer bonds, presale deposits — and what happens to my asset if that channel freezes?* Ask whether the thing you are buying has an income floor (an apartment with rent) or none (a đất nền plot, an off-plan contract). And watch the two levers that have started and stopped all four cycles: the **SBV's policy rate** and its **credit quota**. They are the thermostat for the entire market.
