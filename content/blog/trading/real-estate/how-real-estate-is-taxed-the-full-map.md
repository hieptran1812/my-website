> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Property is not taxed once. It is taxed at **four distinct moments**: when you **acquire** it (transfer tax plus a registration fee), while you **hold** it (an annual property or land tax), when it **earns** (rental-income tax), and when you **sell** it (capital-gains tax). Most buyers think about only one of the four, and that is exactly how the other three quietly eat their return.
>
> - The four moments are **Acquire → Hold → Earn → Sell**, and every country taxes all four — just in wildly different proportions.
> - **Vietnam taxes the transaction**: a 2% transfer tax plus a 0.5% registration fee when you buy, and 2% of the whole sale price when you sell — but almost nothing to hold.
> - **The US is the mirror image**: a light transfer cost, but a heavy **~1.1% per year** property tax for as long as you own, offset by a generous capital-gains exclusion on your home (\$250k single / \$500k married).
> - The number to remember: your **after-tax return** is what you actually keep, and you cannot compute it until you have mapped **all four moments** — not just the one your agent mentions.

A friend of mine bought an apartment in Ho Chi Minh City in 2021. She is sharp — she negotiated hard on the price, she read the contract, she compared mortgage rates at three banks. When I asked her, a year later, what the property *cost* her in tax, she said: "Tax? There's no property tax in Vietnam. That's the whole point." She was wrong, but understandably so. She had paid roughly **₫245 million (≈ \$9,500)** in tax the day she signed — a 2% transfer tax and a 0.5% registration fee — and she had simply folded it into "closing costs" and forgotten it. She will pay 2% of the sale price again when she sells. And she had genuinely never been told that the *reason* her annual costs are near zero is a deliberate policy choice that other countries make in reverse.

Across the Pacific, a US investor I will call Dana made the opposite mistake. Dana bought a \$300,000 house and was thrilled that "there's basically no tax when you buy a home here" — true, her transfer costs were a rounding error. What she under-weighted was the **property tax**: roughly **1.1% of the home's value, every single year**, which on her house is about **\$3,300 a year**, or **\$33,000 over a decade** — many times what my friend in HCMC will ever pay to hold. Dana taxes the holding; my friend taxes the deal. Neither of them had the full map.

That full map — the four moments where property meets the tax authority — is the subject of this post. The figure below is the mental model we will build, piece by piece: four taxable moments in sequence, each with a Vietnam note and a US note, and the loop back to the top when the next buyer acquires and starts the cycle again.

![A flow diagram of the four taxable moments of property ownership, Acquire, Hold, Earn, and Sell, each annotated with how Vietnam and the United States tax it, with a loop back to the next buyer](/imgs/blogs/how-real-estate-is-taxed-the-full-map-1.png)

Why does this matter so much? Because property is the single largest asset most people will ever own, and tax is the single largest *non-obvious* cost of owning it. A buyer who optimizes the price by 3% and ignores a tax structure that takes 5–13% of value over the life of the asset has optimized the wrong thing. The headline return on real estate — the number in the brochure, the "property always goes up" story — is a **pre-tax** number. The number that actually compounds in your net worth is the **after-tax** return, and you cannot get there without all four moments on the table. Let's put them there.

## Foundations: the four taxable moments and the words you need

Before we go deep on any single tax, we need a shared vocabulary. Finance and tax law are full of terms that sound technical but describe simple ideas. Here is every term this post uses, defined from zero. A practitioner can skim; if you are new, read it slowly — the rest of the post assumes these.

### The four taxable moments

Think of a property's life with you as having a beginning, a middle, and an end — and the middle has two flavors. Tax can attach at any of these:

1. **Acquire** — the moment you *buy*. Money changes hands, ownership transfers, and the state takes a cut for recording that transfer and recognizing your new title.
2. **Hold** — every period (usually every year) that you *own* the property. The state may levy a recurring tax simply for the fact of ownership, independent of whether you do anything with the property.
3. **Earn** — when the property *produces income*, almost always **rent**. The rent is income, and income is taxed.
4. **Sell** — the moment you *exit*. If the property is worth more than you paid, you have a **gain**, and the gain may be taxed.

That's the whole map. Four moments. Every property-tax system on Earth is some combination of how hard it taxes each of these four. The art of this post is showing you that **countries make opposite choices**, and that the *combination* — not any single rate — determines your after-tax outcome.

### Transfer tax

A **transfer tax** is a tax charged *when ownership of the property moves from one party to another*. It is usually a percentage of the transaction value (the price). It is a one-time cost, paid at the moment of the deal. Different jurisdictions call it different things — "stamp duty" (UK, Singapore, Hong Kong), "transfer tax" or "deed tax" (US states), "personal income tax on transfer" (Vietnam, which treats the seller's proceeds as taxable income). The economic effect is the same: a slice of the deal goes to the state. In Vietnam, this is **2% of the declared transfer price**, and it is technically a personal income tax (PIT) on the seller — but in practice it is negotiated as part of the deal and frequently borne by the buyer.

### Registration fee

A **registration fee** (in Vietnam, *lệ phí trước bạ*, literally "before-ledger fee") is the charge for the state to **officially record you as the new owner** in the land/property registry. It is what converts a private contract into a legally recognized title. In Vietnam it is **0.5% of the property value** for real estate. Conceptually it is distinct from the transfer tax — one taxes the *transaction*, the other pays for the *recording* — but for a buyer they arrive together at closing and both are real cash out the door. Throughout this post, when I say "the acquisition tax in Vietnam is 2.5%," I mean the 2% transfer plus the 0.5% registration.

### Property tax / land tax (the *ad valorem* tax)

A **property tax** (also called a **land tax** when it falls only on the land, or an **annual property tax** to emphasize that it recurs) is a tax you pay *every year for as long as you own the property*, calculated as a percentage of the property's value. Economists call this an **ad valorem** tax — Latin for "according to value" — because the bill scales with what the property is worth. This is the defining tax of the **Hold** moment. The US property tax is the canonical example: roughly **1.1% of assessed value per year**, paid to your local county to fund schools, roads, fire departments, and police. Vietnam, crucially, has **no broad annual property tax** — only a tiny non-agricultural land-use tax (around 0.03%–0.15% of the state-set land price), which on most homes amounts to a trivial sum.

### Rental-income tax

When a property earns **rent**, that rent is **income**, and income is taxed. **Rental-income tax** is the tax on the money a property produces while you hold it. There are two very different ways to do it, and the difference is enormous:

- **Tax the gross rent.** Take the rent received, apply a flat rate, done. Vietnam does this: if your rental income exceeds **₫100 million per year (≈ \$3,900)**, you owe roughly **10% of the gross rent** (5% VAT-style plus 5% PIT), with no deduction for your costs.
- **Tax the net rent.** Subtract your expenses — mortgage interest, repairs, management fees, insurance, and a large non-cash deduction called **depreciation** — from the rent, and tax only what's left. The US does this. Because depreciation is generous (you deduct a slice of the building's value every year *as if it were wearing out*, even when its market value is rising), a US landlord's *taxable* rental income is frequently **near zero or negative** even while collecting real cash rent.

"Tax the gross" versus "tax the net" is one of the most consequential distinctions in this entire post, and we will return to it.

### Capital-gains tax

A **capital gain** is the profit you make when you sell an asset for more than you paid. If you buy a property for ₫7 billion and sell it for ₫10 billion, your capital gain is ₫3 billion. **Capital-gains tax** is the tax on that profit, levied at the **Sell** moment. Again, two designs:

- **Tax the gain.** Compute proceeds minus cost (your *basis*), tax the difference. This is the US approach — and US homeowners get a huge break: the first **\$250,000 of gain** on a primary residence (\$500,000 for a married couple) is **completely excluded** from tax.
- **Tax the whole price.** Skip the gain calculation entirely and just take a percentage of the *full sale price*. Vietnam does this: **2% of the whole transfer value**, gain or no gain. This is blunt — you owe it even if you sold at a loss — but it is administratively simple and hard to evade.

### Assessed (cadastral) value vs market value

Almost every property tax is charged not on the *price you actually paid* but on an official number the state maintains. The **market value** is what the property would actually sell for between a willing buyer and seller. The **assessed value** (or **cadastral value**, from the *cadastre* — the official land registry) is the value the government assigns for tax purposes. These two numbers are often *not equal*, and the gap matters enormously.

In the US, counties try to assess close to market value, though there's usually a lag and sometimes legal caps on how fast the assessment can rise. In Vietnam, the official **state land-price table** has historically sat *far below* market — sometimes 30–70% below — which is precisely why declared transfer prices (and therefore taxes) are often understated. The **Land Law 2024** (effective August 2024) replaced the old five-year framework price with **annual land-price tables** designed to track the market more closely — a reform aimed squarely at closing this gap. We will see why this is such a big deal.

### Marginal vs effective tax rate

When you hear a tax **rate**, you have to ask: marginal or effective? The **marginal rate** is the rate applied to the *next* dollar (or đồng) of the tax base — the headline number. The **effective rate** is the *total tax you actually pay* divided by the *total value or income*. They are usually different, because exclusions, deductions, brackets, and a tax base smaller than market value all drag the effective rate below the marginal one. When a US homeowner hears "capital gains are taxed at 15–20%," the *effective* rate on a home sale is frequently **0%**, because the exclusion wipes out the entire gain. Never confuse the headline rate with what you pay.

### Tax base vs tax rate

This is the most underrated idea in all of taxation. Your tax bill is:

$$\text{Tax} = \text{Tax base} \times \text{Tax rate}$$

The **tax rate** is the percentage. The **tax base** is *the thing the percentage is applied to*. A government can raise or lower your tax by changing *either*. Vietnam's transfer tax has a clean 2% rate but a base — the *declared price* — that is often understated, so the effective tax is lower than 2% of market. A US property tax has a ~1.1% rate on an *assessed* base that's close to market, so the effective tax is close to 1.1% of what the house is really worth. **Whenever you analyze a property tax, you must look at both the rate and the base.** A 2% rate on a base that's half of market value is an effective 1% tax. Hold this thought; it is the secret to understanding why two countries with similar-sounding rates can tax property completely differently.

### After-tax return

Finally, the number it all rolls up to. Your **pre-tax return** is the gain (price appreciation plus rent) divided by what you invested, ignoring tax. Your **after-tax return** is the same calculation *after subtracting every one of the four taxes*. The gap between them is the **tax drag**. The entire purpose of mapping all four moments is to compute the after-tax return honestly — because that, not the brochure number, is what compounds your wealth.

With the vocabulary in place, let's walk the four moments in order, each with Minh (our HCMC buyer, in ₫) and Dana (our US investor, in \$) carrying the running examples.

## Moment 1 — ACQUIRE: the tax you pay to buy

The first time the tax authority touches your property purchase is at the **closing** — the moment money and title change hands. Two charges typically arrive together: the **transfer tax** and the **registration fee**.

In Vietnam, this moment is *expensive*. The transfer tax is a **2% personal income tax** on the declared transaction value, and the registration fee (*lệ phí trước bạ*) is **0.5%**. Together, **2.5% of the price** leaves your pocket the day you sign. On a ₫7 billion apartment — a realistic 2025 price for a modest 70 m² flat in Hanoi or HCMC — that is real money.

#### Worked example: Minh buys a ₫7 billion flat

Minh buys a 70 m² apartment in District 7, HCMC, for **₫7,000,000,000 (≈ \$270,000** at ₫25,900 to the dollar). At closing he owes:

- **Transfer tax (PIT): 2% × ₫7bn = ₫140,000,000 (≈ \$5,400)**
- **Registration fee: 0.5% × ₫7bn = ₫35,000,000 (≈ \$1,350)**
- **Total acquisition tax: ₫175,000,000 (≈ \$6,750)**, or **2.5% of the price**.

Add notary fees, brokerage, and a few small administrative charges, and Minh's all-in "closing cost" rounds to roughly ₫245 million — but the *tax* portion alone is ₫175 million. He pays this once, up front, and it is **not** added to his cost basis in any way that reduces a future tax (Vietnam taxes the whole sale price, not the gain, so basis is irrelevant — more on that at Moment 4).

*The intuition: in Vietnam, the most expensive day of your ownership, tax-wise, is often the day you buy.*

Now Dana. In the US, the **Acquire** moment is comparatively cheap. There is no national transfer tax; instead, individual states and counties levy a **transfer or deed-recording tax** that ranges from **\$0 in a handful of states to around 1–4%** in high-cost jurisdictions, with most buyers paying well under 1%. There is no broad federal "you bought a house" tax at all.

#### Worked example: Dana buys a \$300,000 house

Dana buys a single-family home for **\$300,000**. Say she's in a state with a 0.5% combined transfer-and-recording cost (a typical middle-of-the-road figure):

- **Transfer/recording tax: 0.5% × \$300,000 = \$1,500.**

That's it for the Acquire moment. Compared to the ~\$6,750 (₫175M) Minh paid on a comparable purchase, Dana's acquisition tax is a quarter of the size *as a percentage of price*, and far less in absolute terms relative to the asset.

*The intuition: the US barely taxes the act of buying a home — which is exactly why Americans transact freely, and why "closing costs" there are dominated by lender fees, not government taxes.*

Already you can see the divergence. On the very first moment, Vietnam charges **2.5%** and the US charges roughly **0.5%**. If the story ended here, Vietnam would look like the high-tax country. But it doesn't end here — and the next moment flips the whole picture.

### Who actually pays, and why the rate is negotiable

A subtle but important point: the *legal* incidence of a tax (who the law says owes it) and the *economic* incidence (who actually bears the cost) can differ. In Vietnam, the 2% transfer PIT is *legally* the seller's tax — it is income tax on the seller's proceeds. But in a seller's market, the seller simply prices it in, and the buyer effectively pays. The 0.5% registration fee is the buyer's by law. In the US, who pays the transfer tax varies by state and by negotiation — sometimes the seller, sometimes the buyer, sometimes split. When you read "the rate is 2%," always ask *on what base* and *paid by whom in practice* — both move the real number.

## Moment 2 — HOLD: the tax you pay just to own

Here is where the two systems diverge most violently. The **Hold** moment is the recurring tax you pay *every year you own the property*, regardless of whether you live in it, rent it, or leave it empty.

In the US, this is the **property tax**, and it is the dominant tax of property ownership. The national average is roughly **1.1% of the home's value per year**, though it ranges widely — under 0.5% in some low-tax states to over 2% in others. It is assessed locally and funds local services. Crucially, it **recurs forever** and it **compounds against your return**: a tax you pay every year is far heavier, over a long hold, than a one-time tax of the same rate.

#### Worked example: Dana's 10-year property-tax bill

Dana's house is worth about \$300,000 on average over her 10-year hold (it appreciates, so call it \$300k as a round midpoint). At a 1.1% annual property tax:

- **Annual property tax: 1.1% × \$300,000 = \$3,300 per year.**
- **Over 10 years: \$3,300 × 10 = \$33,000.**

That \$33,000 is **more than 20 times** the \$1,500 she paid to acquire the house. The Hold moment, not the Acquire moment, is where the American property owner's tax bill actually lives.

*The intuition: in the US, owning a home is like renting it from the government — you pay ~1.1% of its value every year for as long as you hold it, and over a decade that recurring drip dwarfs the one-time cost of buying.*

Now Minh. Vietnam has **no broad annual property tax**. What it does have is a **non-agricultural land-use tax**, levied on the *land* (not the building) at a rate of roughly **0.03% to 0.15%** of the state-set land price — and the state land price is itself well below market. For most urban apartments, where the land component is small and the state price is low, this works out to a trivial sum — often a few hundred thousand đồng a year, or even less.

#### Worked example: Minh's 10-year holding tax

Minh's apartment sits on a small slice of land (it's one unit in a tower, so his land share is tiny), valued in the state table at, say, ₫500 million for his share, taxed at the residential band of ~0.03%:

- **Annual land-use tax: 0.03% × ₫500,000,000 ≈ ₫150,000 per year (≈ \$6).**
- **Over 10 years: ≈ ₫1,500,000 (≈ \$58).**

Round it to **near zero**. Compared to Dana's \$33,000, Minh's holding tax over the same decade is essentially a rounding error.

*The intuition: in Vietnam, holding property is nearly free of tax — which is a large part of why Vietnamese households park so much wealth in land and empty apartments, and why a holding tax has been debated for years as a tool to discourage speculation.*

This is the heart of the post's thesis. **Vietnam barely taxes the Hold; the US taxes it heavily.** And because the Hold tax recurs every year while the Acquire tax is one-time, the *long-run* burden flips depending on how long you hold. A Vietnamese owner who holds for 20 years pays almost nothing extra for the extra decade; a US owner who holds for 20 years pays *another* \$33,000+.

### The policy logic behind the choice

Why would a country choose to tax the holding so heavily? Because an annual ad valorem tax is, in the eyes of public-finance economists, one of the *most efficient and progressive* taxes available. It is hard to evade (you can't hide a house), it falls on a relatively immobile base (land doesn't flee to a tax haven), it funds local services where the property is, and — by making it costly to leave property idle — it discourages speculative hoarding and encourages productive use. Vietnam's Ministry of Finance has **floated a property/second-home holding tax repeatedly** between 2023 and 2026, precisely to cool speculation and broaden the tax base. As of mid-2026 it has not been enacted, but the direction of travel is clear, and we'll discuss what its arrival would do to the map.

## Moment 3 — EARN: the tax on rental income

Not every owner rents out their property — Minh might live in his flat, Dana might live in her house. But when a property *earns* rent, that income is taxed, and the **method** of taxation differs more than the rate.

Vietnam taxes **gross rent**. If your annual rental income exceeds the **₫100 million (≈ \$3,900)** threshold, you owe approximately **10% of the gross** — split as 5% value-added tax and 5% personal income tax — with **no deduction for your costs**. Your mortgage interest, repairs, management fees: none of it reduces the bill. The 10% comes off the top of every đồng of rent.

#### Worked example: Minh rents out his flat

Suppose Minh rents his ₫7bn apartment for **₫25,000,000 per month (≈ \$965)**, or **₫300,000,000 per year (≈ \$11,580)**. He's well over the ₫100M threshold, so:

- **Rental-income tax: 10% × ₫300,000,000 = ₫30,000,000 per year (≈ \$1,160).**

He pays this on the *gross* rent. If his actual costs (building management fees, occasional repairs, a few months of vacancy) run ₫40 million a year, his *economic* profit is ₫260M but he's taxed as if it were ₫300M. There's no relief for the costs.

*The intuition: Vietnam's rental tax is simple and blunt — a flat 10% slice of the gross rent — which means the more cost-heavy your property, the worse the deal, because you're taxed on revenue you never really kept.*

Now Dana. The US taxes **net rental income** — rent *minus* expenses — at the owner's ordinary income tax rate. And the list of deductible expenses is long: mortgage interest, property tax, insurance, repairs, management fees, and the big one, **depreciation**. Depreciation lets a landlord deduct a portion of the *building's* value (not the land) every year — residential rental property is depreciated over 27.5 years — as a non-cash expense, on the theory that the structure wears out. The result is striking.

#### Worked example: Dana rents out her house

Dana rents her \$300,000 house (say \$240,000 of it is the building, \$60,000 the land) for **\$2,000 per month = \$24,000 per year**. Her cash expenses — mortgage interest, property tax, insurance, repairs, management — run about **\$14,000**. Now the depreciation deduction:

- **Depreciation: \$240,000 ÷ 27.5 years ≈ \$8,730 per year** (a non-cash deduction — no money actually leaves her pocket).

Her *taxable* rental income is:

- **\$24,000 rent − \$14,000 cash costs − \$8,730 depreciation = \$1,270.**

At, say, a 22% marginal rate, her rental-income tax is **0.22 × \$1,270 ≈ \$280** for the year — even though she collected \$24,000 in rent and pocketed roughly \$10,000 in cash after real expenses. The depreciation deduction shielded almost all of it.

*The intuition: the US taxes the net, and depreciation is so generous that a profitable rental can show near-zero taxable income for years — the tax is deferred until you sell, when depreciation is "recaptured."*

So on the Earn moment, the *rate* (10% vs ~22%) is not the real story — the *base* is. Vietnam taxes a big base (gross rent) at a small rate; the US taxes a tiny base (net rent after depreciation) at a bigger rate, and the tiny base usually wins. A cost-heavy rental is taxed more kindly in the US; a clean, cash-cow rental with few costs is taxed more simply (and often more lightly in absolute đồng) in Vietnam. The lesson, again: **rate and base must be read together.**

## Moment 4 — SELL: the tax on the exit

The final moment is the **exit**. You sell, and if you've made money, the gain may be taxed. Here, for once, the *rate* looks similar (both countries are in the low-double-digits at the headline level), but the *base* and the *exemptions* diverge completely.

Vietnam taxes the **whole sale price** at **2%** — the same 2% PIT we met at the Acquire moment, now on the seller's side. It does **not** compute your gain. Whether you sell at a profit, at break-even, or at a loss, you owe 2% of the price the deal closed at.

#### Worked example: Minh sells after 10 years

Minh's ₫7bn flat appreciates to **₫10,000,000,000 (≈ \$386,000)** over a decade. He sells. His capital-gains tax:

- **2% × ₫10,000,000,000 = ₫200,000,000 (≈ \$7,720).**

Note what this is *not*: it is **not** 2% of his ₫3bn gain (which would be ₫60M). It is 2% of the **whole price**. The system ignores his basis entirely. If, in a downturn, he had sold the same flat for ₫6.5bn — a loss — he would *still* owe 2% × ₫6.5bn = ₫130M. The tax is on the transaction, not the profit.

*The intuition: Vietnam's exit tax is brutally simple — 2% of whatever you sell for — which means it's predictable and unavoidable, but it can tax you even when you've lost money.*

Now Dana. The US taxes the **gain**, not the price — and then hands homeowners an enormous exemption. The **primary-residence exclusion** lets a single filer exclude the first **\$250,000 of gain** (a married couple, **\$500,000**) when they sell a home they've lived in for at least two of the last five years.

#### Worked example: Dana sells after 10 years

Dana's \$300,000 house appreciates to **\$420,000** over the decade — a **\$120,000 gain**. She sells. Her capital-gains tax:

- Gain = \$420,000 − \$300,000 = **\$120,000.**
- The primary-residence exclusion covers the first \$250,000 of gain for a single filer.
- **\$120,000 < \$250,000 → entire gain excluded → capital-gains tax = \$0.**

Dana pays **nothing** on her exit. Had she instead bought it as an investment property (no primary-residence exclusion), she'd owe long-term capital-gains tax — roughly 15% of the \$120,000 gain, or \$18,000, plus depreciation recapture on the deductions she took while renting. But as a homeowner under the exclusion, her Sell tax is zero.

*The intuition: the US lets ordinary homeowners walk away from a sale tax-free up to a huge threshold — which is why "your home is your best tax shelter" is a genuine truth in the US, and not in Vietnam.*

So on the Sell moment: Vietnam's blunt 2%-of-price catches everyone, gain or no gain; the US's tax-the-gain-with-a-big-exclusion lets most homeowners exit free but taxes investors on real profits. Once again, opposite philosophies — and once again, the headline rate ("2%" vs "15%") tells you almost nothing until you know the base and the exemption.

## How the four stack into the after-tax return

Now let's assemble the four moments into the only number that matters: the **after-tax return**. The figure below is the bird's-eye comparison — Vietnam and the US side by side across all four moments, showing *what* is taxed, the *typical rate*, and *who pays*.

![A comparison matrix of Vietnam versus the United States across the four taxable moments, showing what is taxed, the typical rate, and who pays at each moment](/imgs/blogs/how-real-estate-is-taxed-the-full-map-2.png)

Read the matrix and a pattern jumps out. Vietnam's two **danger-red** cells — the heavy taxes — are at the *ends*: Acquire and Sell, the two transaction moments. Its Hold cell is **green** (near-free). The US is the mirror: its red cell is **Hold**, and its Acquire, Earn, and Sell cells are all green (light, sheltered, or excluded). **Vietnam taxes the transaction; the US taxes the holding.** That single sentence is the post's thesis, and the matrix proves it.

But a matrix shows the *structure*; it doesn't show the *timing*. Two taxes of the same total size feel very different depending on *when* they hit. The next figure plots where the tax actually falls across a 10-year hold.

![A timeline showing where property tax falls over a ten-year hold, with Vietnam clustering tax at the buy and sell years and the United States spreading it across every year of the hold](/imgs/blogs/how-real-estate-is-taxed-the-full-map-3.png)

In Vietnam, the tax is **clustered at the two ends** — a big bite at Year 0 (buy) and another at Year 10 (sell), with a flat near-zero line in between. In the US, the tax is a **steady annual drip** across all ten years (the property tax), with light ends. The *shape* of the burden is completely different, and that shape has real consequences — it changes how long people hold, how often they transact, and how much liquidity the market has, as we'll see.

#### Worked example: total lifetime tax, Minh vs Dana

Let's total every tax each of them pays over the decade, assuming **owner-occupation** (so no rental tax — we'll add the rental case separately), and express it as a share of the purchase price.

**Minh (₫7bn flat, sells at ₫10bn):**

- Acquire: ₫140M transfer + ₫35M registration = **₫175M**
- Hold: ~₫1.5M over 10 years ≈ **₫2M** (round)
- Earn: **₫0** (owner-occupied)
- Sell: 2% × ₫10bn = **₫200M**
- **Total ≈ ₫377M**, or **~5.4% of the ₫7bn purchase price** — and almost all of it is at the two transaction ends.

**Dana (\$300k house, sells at \$420k):**

- Acquire: 0.5% × \$300k = **\$1,500**
- Hold: 1.1% × \$300k × 10 = **\$33,000**
- Earn: **\$0** (owner-occupied)
- Sell: **\$0** (gain under the \$250k exclusion)
- **Total ≈ \$34,500**, or **~11.5% of the \$300k purchase price** — and almost all of it is the annual holding tax.

So over a 10-year owner-occupied hold, **Dana's lifetime tax (~11.5% of value) is roughly double Minh's (~5.4%)** — but Minh's is front-and-back-loaded at the transactions, while Dana's is a steady annual drip. (Note: these are tax-only figures and exclude mortgage interest, maintenance, and other carrying costs, which are large in their own right but not the subject of this post.)

*The intuition: count all four moments and the "low-tax" country can turn out to be the higher-tax one over a long hold — the headline you remember (2% transfer in Vietnam) is exactly the one that misleads you.*

Two caveats keep this honest. First, the comparison flips with **holding period**: hold for only 3 years and Vietnam's transaction-heavy structure looks far worse (you pay the buy-and-sell taxes but barely any holding tax); hold for 30 years and the US's annual drip dominates. Second, it flips with **what you do with the property**: a rented Vietnamese flat adds a 10%-of-gross rental tax every year, which over a decade can exceed the transaction taxes. The point is not that one country is cheaper — it's that you *cannot know* without mapping all four moments and your own holding period and use.

### The same comparison, but now they rent it out

The owner-occupied case above turned off Moment 3 entirely. Switch it on — both Minh and Dana now rent — and the picture shifts again, because the **Earn** tax recurs annually just like the Hold tax, and it compounds the divergence between the two systems.

#### Worked example: lifetime tax when both rent the property

Take the same 10-year hold, but now both rent out the property the whole time.

**Minh (rents at ₫300M/year, sells at ₫10bn):**

- Acquire: **₫175M** (unchanged)
- Hold: ~**₫2M** (unchanged)
- Earn: 10% × ₫300M × 10 years = **₫300M**
- Sell: 2% × ₫10bn = **₫200M**
- **Total ≈ ₫677M**, or **~9.7% of the ₫7bn price.** The rental tax alone (₫300M) now *exceeds* the two transaction taxes combined (₫375M is close, and over a longer hold the rent tax overtakes it).

**Dana (rents at \$24k/year, sells at \$420k as an investment property):**

- Acquire: **\$1,500** (unchanged)
- Hold: **\$33,000** (unchanged)
- Earn: ~\$280 × 10 ≈ **\$2,800** (depreciation shelters almost all of it)
- Sell: now an investment property, so no \$250k exclusion → 15% × \$120k gain = \$18,000, *plus* depreciation recapture of ~25% × (\$8,730 × 10) ≈ \$21,800 → roughly **\$39,800** at exit
- **Total ≈ \$77,100**, or **~25.7% of the \$300k price.**

Renting flips Dana's Sell tax from \$0 to nearly \$40,000 — the lost primary-residence exclusion plus **depreciation recapture**, the tax you pay back on the depreciation deductions you took along the way (the IRS lets you defer, not avoid). Minh's rental tax, by contrast, is a clean, predictable ₫300M with no recapture to settle at the end.

*The intuition: renting changes the answer dramatically — the US shelters the annual rent with depreciation but claws it back at the sale, while Vietnam takes a steady, simple 10% of gross every year and asks no questions at exit.*

This is the deepest lesson of the four-moments map: the moments **interact**. The US's generous rental depreciation isn't a gift — it's a *loan* the tax authority calls in at the Sell moment through recapture. The exclusion that makes a US home sale tax-free vanishes the moment the property becomes an investment. You cannot evaluate any single moment in isolation, because a break at one moment is often paid for at another.

## The two philosophies: tax-the-transaction vs tax-the-holding

Step back from the numbers and you see two coherent, opposite design philosophies. They are not random — each follows from a different theory of what a property tax is *for*.

### Vietnam: tax the transaction

Vietnam's system taxes the *moments of change* — buying and selling — and leaves the *state of ownership* almost untaxed. Why?

- **Administrative simplicity.** A transaction is a discrete, observable event with a clear price and a notary in the room. Taxing it is easy: take a percentage at closing. An annual ad valorem tax, by contrast, requires the state to *value every property every year* — a massive administrative undertaking that needs accurate, current assessments. For a country that until 2024 used a five-year framework land price far below market, building a credible annual-valuation system is genuinely hard.
- **Hard to evade.** You can understate a declared price (and people do — which is why the effective transfer tax is below 2% of market), but you cannot make a sale invisible; it must be notarized and registered to be legally valid.
- **Revenue timing.** Transaction taxes deliver lumpy revenue tied to market activity — great in a boom, thin in a freeze (as in 2022–23, when the transaction market seized up and these revenues collapsed). That volatility is a known weakness of the transaction-heavy model.

The cost of this philosophy: it **discourages transactions** (every sale is taxed, so people hold longer and the market is less liquid) and it **fails to discourage hoarding** (since holding is free, parking wealth in idle land or empty apartments carries no annual penalty — a major driver of Vietnam's speculative land culture).

### The US: tax the holding

The US system taxes the *state of ownership* — every year, ad valorem — and barely taxes the *moments of change*. Why?

- **Stable, local revenue.** The property tax is the bedrock of US *local* government finance. It funds schools, roads, and emergency services in the very community where the property sits, and it delivers a steady, predictable annual stream regardless of whether the market is hot or frozen.
- **Efficiency and fairness.** Public-finance economists broadly favor recurring land/property taxes: they fall on an immobile base, are hard to dodge, are roughly progressive (the wealthy own more valuable property), and — by making idle property costly to hold — push land toward productive use.
- **It discourages hoarding, not transactions.** Because *holding* is what's taxed, owners are nudged to use property productively or sell it; transactions, lightly taxed, flow freely, giving the US a far more liquid housing market.

The cost: a heavy annual tax is a real and visible burden on every homeowner, it can force long-time owners out of appreciating neighborhoods (the "house-rich, cash-poor" problem), and it requires the politically fraught machinery of annual assessments and appeals.

Neither philosophy is "right." They are different answers to the question *when should the state take its cut?* — at the moment of change, or for the duration of ownership. Knowing which philosophy your country follows tells you, before you even buy, where your tax burden will concentrate and how it will shape your strategy (hold long in Vietnam to amortize the transaction taxes; weigh the annual carrying cost heavily in the US).

### Why the tax base — assessed vs market value — quietly decides everything

Underneath both philosophies sits the **tax base**, and in Vietnam the gap between assessed and market value has historically been the system's biggest lever. The figure below contrasts the two.

![A comparison of market value versus assessed or cadastral value, showing how the official value the state taxes can sit well below the real market price in Vietnam](/imgs/blogs/how-real-estate-is-taxed-the-full-map-5.png)

#### Worked example: under-assessment shrinks the tax

Minh's flat genuinely sells for **₫7bn**, but suppose the state land-price table (or a deliberately understated declared price) values the transaction at only **₫4bn** for tax purposes. The 2% transfer tax is charged on the *declared* base, not the market price:

- On the true market value: 2% × ₫7bn = **₫140M.**
- On the understated base: 2% × ₫4bn = **₫80M.**

The same 2% *rate* yields **₫60M less** tax purely because the *base* is smaller. The effective rate on market value drops from 2% to about **1.1%**.

*The intuition: a tax rate is only half the equation — a low base can make a high headline rate gentle, which is exactly how Vietnam's "2%" has long collected far less than 2% of real property values.*

This under-assessment is precisely what the **Land Law 2024** targets. By replacing the stale five-year framework price with **annual land-price tables** that track the market, the reform widens the tax *base* — meaning the same 2% rate will collect more, and any future annual property tax would bite harder. A reform that "only changes how land is valued" is, in tax terms, a tax increase delivered through the base rather than the rate. Watch the base, not just the rate.

### From pre-tax to after-tax: the drag, visualized

Finally, the reason all of this matters to your wallet. The headline return everyone quotes is pre-tax; the four taxes drag it down to the after-tax return you actually keep.

![A before and after comparison showing how the headline pre-tax return on a property is reduced by the four taxes into a lower after-tax return](/imgs/blogs/how-real-estate-is-taxed-the-full-map-6.png)

#### Worked example: pre-tax vs after-tax return

Minh buys at ₫7bn, sells at ₫10bn after 10 years, owner-occupied (no rent). His **pre-tax** outcome:

- Gain = ₫10bn − ₫7bn = **₫3bn**, a **+43% total return**, or roughly **+3.6% per year** (compound) before tax.

Now subtract the four taxes (₫175M acquire + ~₫2M hold + ₫0 rent + ₫200M sell = **₫377M**):

- After-tax gain = ₫3bn − ₫377M = **₫2.623bn.**
- After-tax total return = ₫2.623bn ÷ ₫7bn = **+37.5%**, or roughly **+3.2% per year.**

The four taxes shaved his annual return from **3.6% to 3.2%** — a drag of about **0.4 percentage points per year**. That sounds small until you compound it over decades: 0.4 points a year, over 30 years, is a meaningful chunk of final wealth. And this is the *low-tax* country, owner-occupied. Rent the flat out and add ₫30M/year of rental tax, and the drag roughly doubles.

*The intuition: the brochure quotes you 3.6%; you earn 3.2%; the four taxes live in that gap — and over a lifetime of holding, that gap is real money.*

## Common misconceptions

Property tax is a field thick with confident half-truths. Here are the ones that cost people the most.

### "Property has no tax in Vietnam."

This is the single most common — and most expensive — myth, and it's the one my friend believed. It is **false**, but it contains a kernel of truth that makes it sticky. Vietnam has almost no *annual holding* tax, so the *feeling* of owning is tax-free. But the *transactions* are heavily taxed: 2.5% to buy, 2% to sell. A buy-hold-sell over a decade carries roughly **5% of value** in tax. What people mean by "no property tax" is "no *annual* property tax" — and even that may change if the Ministry of Finance enacts the holding tax it has floated since 2023. The accurate statement is: *Vietnam taxes the transaction heavily and the holding lightly* — not "no tax."

### "The headline rate is what you pay."

Almost never. The **effective** rate — what you actually pay divided by what the asset is really worth — is dragged below the headline by a smaller tax **base** (assessed value below market), by **exclusions** (the US \$250k home-sale exclusion can make a 15% capital-gains rate an *effective* 0%), and by **deductions** (US depreciation can make a 22% rental rate an effective near-0%). Conversely, Vietnam's "2% capital-gains tax" sounds gentle until you realize it's 2% of the *whole price*, not the gain — so on a small gain, the effective rate on the *profit* can exceed 50%. Headline rate and base are two different numbers; always compute the effective rate.

### "Only the sale is taxed."

Many first-time buyers picture tax as a one-time event at the end — "I'll worry about capital gains when I sell." This ignores three of the four moments. You're taxed when you **buy** (transfer + registration), potentially every year you **hold** (property/land tax), and every year it **earns** rent (rental-income tax) — all before you ever get to the sale. In the US, the **Hold** tax alone typically dwarfs the sale tax over a long hold. Treating the sale as "the tax moment" is how people under-budget for ownership by tens of thousands.

### "Capital-gains tax is always a percentage of my profit."

Only in tax-the-gain systems like the US. In Vietnam, the "capital-gains" tax is **2% of the full sale price**, computed *without reference to your gain at all*. You can sell at a loss and still owe it. Assuming every country taxes "the profit" leads buyers to wrongly believe they'll owe nothing if they sell at break-even — in Vietnam, they'll owe 2% of the price regardless.

### "An annual property tax is just a small percentage, so it doesn't matter."

A 1.1% annual tax *sounds* trivial next to a 2% transaction tax. But "annual" is doing enormous work. A 1.1% tax paid *every year for 10 years* is a 10x-larger cumulative bite than a one-time 1.1%, and it compounds against your return the entire time. Over a 30-year hold, a 1.1% annual property tax can cumulatively exceed **a third of the home's value**. Recurring taxes and one-time taxes of the same headline rate are not remotely comparable — the recurring one is far heavier over any long horizon.

### "Renting out my property is taxed the same everywhere."

The *method* differs so much that the rate is almost a distraction. Vietnam taxes **gross** rent at ~10% with no deductions; the US taxes **net** rent (after every expense plus depreciation) at your income rate, which frequently nets to near-zero taxable income. A cost-heavy, mortgaged rental is taxed far more gently in the US; a clean, low-cost rental may be simpler and lighter in Vietnam. "Rental income is taxed at X%" is meaningless without knowing whether X% is applied to gross or net.

## How it shows up in real markets

Abstract structure becomes concrete in how these systems actually shape behavior. Here are real-world patterns, Vietnamese and global.

### The Vietnamese "park-it-in-land" culture

Because **holding is nearly tax-free**, Vietnamese households have a strong incentive to store wealth in property — including idle land plots (*đất nền*) and empty, never-rented apartments. There is no annual penalty for letting a flat sit dark. Combined with limited trust in other savings vehicles and a long history of land as the ultimate store of value, this produces the well-documented phenomenon of high-vacancy "ghost" developments and speculative land-banking in HCMC's satellite provinces (the 2018 *đất nền* fever in areas like Long Thành is a textbook case). A meaningful **annual holding tax** — the kind the Ministry of Finance has floated repeatedly since 2023 — is the standard policy tool economists prescribe to break this, precisely because it makes idle holding costly.

#### Worked example: what a holding tax would do to a land-banker

Suppose a speculator holds **₫20 billion (≈ \$772,000)** of idle land plots, never rented, waiting to flip. Today, the annual tax is negligible — call it a few hundred thousand đồng. Now imagine Vietnam enacts even a modest **0.5% annual property tax** on the market-tracking Land Law 2024 base:

- **0.5% × ₫20bn = ₫100,000,000 per year (≈ \$3,860).**

Suddenly, holding idle land costs ₫100M a year — ₫1bn over a decade — whether or not the plots ever appreciate. That recurring carrying cost is precisely the friction that forces speculators to either *use* the land productively, *rent* it, or *sell* it to someone who will. The same tax that funds local services in the US is, in Vietnam's debate, framed primarily as a speculation brake.

*The intuition: a holding tax turns idle land from a free option into a metered cost — which is exactly why a country with a speculation problem reaches for one, and exactly why the people holding idle land resist it.*

Whether and when Vietnam enacts such a tax is one of the most consequential open questions for the market, and it would move the country measurably along the transaction-vs-holding spectrum.

### The 2022–23 transaction freeze and the revenue cliff

A transaction-heavy tax system has a hidden fragility: when transactions stop, the revenue stops. In late 2022, Vietnam's corporate-bond market seized up after the Tân Hoàng Minh (April 2022) and Vạn Thịnh Phát / SCB (October 2022) episodes, developer financing froze, and property transactions collapsed. Because so much property tax revenue in Vietnam is tied to *transactions* (the 2% transfer PIT and 0.5% registration), government revenue from real estate fell sharply alongside the volume. A country that taxed the *holding* instead would have kept collecting through the freeze — its base (existing property) didn't disappear, only the deal-flow did. This is the transaction-heavy model's structural weakness, on full display.

### The American "house-rich, cash-poor" squeeze

The flip side — the holding-heavy model's characteristic failure — shows up in long-time US homeowners in fast-appreciating areas. A retiree who bought a California or Texas home decades ago for \$80,000 may now own a \$900,000 house. If the property tax tracked that value at ~1.1%, the annual bill would be ~\$10,000 — potentially unaffordable on a fixed retirement income, even though the owner is "wealthy" on paper. This tension is so politically charged that several US states have passed assessment caps (California's Proposition 13 being the famous example) that limit how fast the *taxable* base can rise — a base-side intervention that, again, shows the base matters as much as the rate. The annual tax that funds local schools so reliably is the same tax that can force a long-time owner out of an appreciating neighborhood.

### The US homeowner's tax shelter

The \$250k / \$500k primary-residence exclusion is one of the most valuable tax breaks in the US code, and it shapes behavior visibly. It's a major reason Americans treat their home as a savings vehicle: a couple who buys at \$400k and sells at \$880k pockets the entire \$480k gain tax-free. It also creates a "lock-in" of sorts at the top end — gains *above* the exclusion are taxable, so very-long-held, highly-appreciated homes can carry a real exit tax that discourages downsizing. The exclusion is why "your home is your best tax shelter" is genuinely true in the US in a way it simply isn't in Vietnam, where the 2%-of-price exit tax applies to everyone regardless of gain.

### Singapore: a deliberate hybrid that taxes both ends

Singapore is instructive because it taxes *both* moments heavily and on purpose. It layers a buyer's stamp duty plus an **Additional Buyer's Stamp Duty (ABSD)** — which can reach 60% for foreign buyers — onto the transaction *and* levies an annual property tax based on the property's "annual value" (estimated rental value), with higher rates on non-owner-occupied and luxury homes. The transaction taxes are a deliberate speculation brake (especially on foreign and multiple-property buyers); the annual tax is the steady local-revenue workhorse. Singapore shows that the "transaction vs holding" choice isn't binary — a government can dial both, and use the *structure* of each (higher ABSD on foreigners, higher annual rates on empty homes) as a precise policy instrument.

### The global spectrum, in one frame

Put the world on a line and you get a spectrum from transaction-heavy to holding-heavy, with a balanced middle. The figure below places several markets.

![A grid placing countries on a spectrum from transaction-heavy taxation like Vietnam to holding-heavy taxation like the United States, with a balanced middle and the behavioral effects of each](/imgs/blogs/how-real-estate-is-taxed-the-full-map-7.png)

On the **transaction-heavy** end sit Vietnam, Hong Kong, and India — high stamp/transfer duties front-load the cost, people hold long and transact rarely, and market liquidity is low. On the **holding-heavy** end sit the US and UK — annual property/council taxes mean the cost is felt every year and funds local services, while light transaction costs let the market turn over freely. In the **balanced** middle sit Singapore, France, and Germany, which tax both moments moderately and spread the burden across the asset's life. Where a country sits is not an accident — it reflects a deliberate choice about administrative capacity, revenue stability, and what behavior the government wants to encourage or discourage. And it directly shapes how *you*, the owner, should think about holding period, liquidity, and after-tax return in that market.

## When this matters / Further reading

The four-moments map matters most at exactly the moment people ignore it: **before you buy**. The price you negotiate is visible and one-time; the tax structure is invisible and lifelong, and it determines a larger share of your after-tax return than almost any price haggling will. Three practical takeaways:

- **Map all four moments for your specific market and use, before you buy.** Owner-occupier or landlord? Short hold or 20-year hold? The answer determines whether the transaction taxes or the holding tax dominates *your* bill. In Vietnam, hold long to amortize the heavy transaction taxes across more years; in the US, weigh the ~1.1%-per-year carrying cost as heavily as the purchase price itself.
- **Read the base, not just the rate.** A 2% rate on an under-assessed base is a different tax from a 2% rate on full market value. Vietnam's Land Law 2024 reform — widening the assessed-value base toward market — is, economically, a tax increase delivered through the base. The same logic explains why US assessment caps (Prop 13) are such a big deal: they freeze the base, not the rate.
- **Watch the policy direction.** Vietnam's repeatedly-floated annual holding tax would, if enacted, move it meaningfully along the spectrum toward the holding-heavy model — penalizing the idle land and empty apartments that currently sit tax-free, and reshaping the speculative incentives that define the market today.

None of this is individualized advice — tax law is detailed, jurisdiction-specific, and changes; consult a qualified professional for your actual situation. The goal here is the *map*, so you ask the right questions and aren't blindsided by three of the four moments.
