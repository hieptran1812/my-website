> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A property does not have one "true price"; it has three independent lenses, and a professional triangulates them.
>
> - **Sales comparison**: what similar properties just sold for, adjusted feature by feature for every difference. This is the dominant method for homes.
> - **Income approach**: capitalize the rent the property throws off — net operating income (NOI) divided by a cap rate. This is the method for rentals, offices, and malls.
> - **Cost approach**: land value plus what it would cost to rebuild today, minus the wear already taken. This is the floor, and the method for new or unique buildings.
> - The number to remember: when the three disagree, the *gap itself is the insight* — it tells you whether a property is overbuilt, fairly priced, or being bought on speculation.

In March 2025, a buyer we'll call **Minh** stood in an empty 70-square-metre apartment in District 7 of Ho Chi Minh City. The agent had quoted ₫7.3 billion (about \$282,000). Down the corridor, a near-identical unit had sold three weeks earlier for ₫6.9 billion. Across the river in District 2, a slightly bigger flat with a Saigon River view had closed at ₫7.55 billion. The bank's own appraiser, sent to value the flat as collateral for Minh's mortgage, came back with ₫6.8 billion. And a developer friend, eyeballing construction costs, swore the whole thing — land and concrete — couldn't have cost more than ₫7.4 billion to build new.

Five numbers. Same apartment. None of them agreed. Minh's question was the one almost every buyer eventually asks: *which one is the real price?*

The honest answer is that none of them is "the price" and all of them are evidence. Professionals who value property for a living — appraisers, valuers, bank credit officers — do not believe in a single true price hiding inside a building, waiting to be discovered. They believe in **three independent lenses**, each answering a different question, and they treat the job as triangulation: gather what each lens says, understand *why* they differ, and reconcile them into one defensible opinion. The diagram above is the mental model for the whole post — three columns, three questions, three one-line formulas — and the rest of this article is an unhurried walk through each lens, ending with how a pro blends them into the single number that goes on the page.

![A grid showing the three property valuation approaches across columns: sales comparison, income, and cost, with rows for what each asks, what it is best for, and its one-line formula](/imgs/blogs/how-property-is-valued-three-approaches-1.png)

We'll use Minh's HCMC apartment as the running Vietnamese example and bring in **Dana**, an investor in the United States, whenever the global mechanism plays out differently abroad. By the end you'll be able to value a property three ways yourself, on the back of an envelope, and — more importantly — read the disagreement between the three as a diagnosis rather than a contradiction.

## Foundations: value, price, cost, and the three lenses

Before any method, three words that sound like synonyms but are not. Getting them straight is half the battle, because almost every confusion about property valuation comes from blurring them.

**Price** is what someone actually paid. It is a fact, recorded on a contract, on a specific day, between two specific people. Price is real but narrow — it tells you what *one* buyer and *one* seller agreed to *once*, and that pair may have been unusually motivated, unusually informed, or unusually related (a parent selling to a child rarely transacts at market).

**Market value** is the abstraction we actually want: the most probable price a property *should* fetch in a competitive, open market, given a willing buyer and a willing seller, each acting knowledgeably and without undue pressure, after reasonable exposure. Notice every qualifier is doing work. "Most probable" — it's a central estimate, not a guarantee. "Willing… without undue pressure" — a fire sale or a forced auction is a price but not market value. "After reasonable exposure" — a property dumped in a day for cash is not the same as one marketed properly for two months. Market value is the question an *appraisal* tries to answer: a formal, evidence-based written opinion of value as of a stated date.

**Cost** is what it took (or would take) to *produce* the thing — the land plus the bricks, labour, and permits to put a building on it. Cost and value are cousins, not twins. A beautifully built house in a dying town can cost ₫6 billion to construct and be worth ₫3 billion, because no one wants to live there. A scruffy flat on a corner everyone wants can be worth twice what it cost. Cost sets a kind of floor and a kind of ceiling — you rarely pay much more than it would cost to build new (why would you, if you could just build?), and a property is rarely worth less than its land — but cost is not value.

Hold those three apart and the rest follows. The three valuation approaches are simply three different routes to estimating *market value*, each using a different kind of evidence:

| Approach | The question it asks | The evidence it uses | One-line formula |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Sales comparison** | What did similar properties just sell for? | Recent sales of comparable properties | Comp price ± adjustments |
| **Income approach** | What rent does this throw off, and what's that worth? | The property's net rent and market cap rates | NOI ÷ cap rate |
| **Cost approach** | What would it cost to rebuild this today? | Land value + construction costs − depreciation | Land + rebuild cost − depreciation |

A few terms we'll lean on throughout, defined here once so the worked examples flow:

- A **comparable** (a "comp") is a property similar enough to the one being valued — the *subject* — that its recent sale price is useful evidence. Similar in location, size, age, condition, and type.
- An **adjustment** is a dollar (or đồng) amount added to or subtracted from a comp's sale price to account for a way it *differs* from the subject. If the comp has a river view your subject lacks, you subtract the value of that view from the comp's price to make it comparable.
- **Net operating income (NOI)** is the annual rent a property earns *after* you subtract the cost of running it (but before mortgage payments and income tax). Rent in, operating costs out, NOI is what's left.
- A **cap rate** (capitalization rate) is the annual NOI expressed as a percentage of the property's value — effectively the property's unleveraged yield. We preview it here and dive deep in the [income approach and cap rates](/blog/trading/real-estate/cap-rate-noi-and-the-income-approach) companion piece.
- **Capitalization** is the act of converting one year's income into a value by dividing by the cap rate. Capitalizing ₫150M of NOI at a 5% cap rate gives ₫3 billion.
- **Replacement cost** is what it would cost to build a building of *equivalent utility* today using modern materials; **reproduction cost** is what it would cost to build an *exact* replica (relevant for heritage buildings). For most valuations, replacement is the right idea.
- **Accrued depreciation** is the total loss in value a building has already suffered — from physical wear, from outdated design, and from external forces like a new highway next door.
- **Reconciliation** is the final step: weighing the three indicated values against each other and the quality of their evidence to settle on one opinion.

One more idea ties all three approaches together, and it's worth stating before we start: the **principle of substitution**. A rational buyer will not pay more for a property than the cost of acquiring an equally desirable substitute. That single sentence is the engine under *all three* lenses. Sales comparison applies it directly — the substitute is another similar property that just sold. The income approach applies it to cash flow — the substitute is another asset throwing off the same NOI at the market's required yield. The cost approach applies it to construction — the substitute is buying a lot and building an equivalent building from scratch. Three methods, one underlying logic: *don't overpay relative to your next-best alternative.* Once you see that, the three approaches stop feeling like a random list and start feeling like three windows onto the same idea.

There's also a concept appraisers settle *before* they value anything: **highest and best use** — the legally permitted, physically possible, financially feasible use that produces the highest value for the site. A run-down house on a plot zoned for a six-storey building isn't valued as a house; it's valued as *development land*, because that's its highest and best use, and a developer will pay for the land minus demolition, ignoring the house entirely. This is why an old building on a great lot can be worth far more than its structure: the value lives in what the land *could* hold, not what currently sits on it. We won't belabour it here, but keep it in mind — every valuation silently assumes an answer to "used for what?"

With the vocabulary in hand, let's take each lens in turn, starting with the one that values most of the homes on Earth.

## Sales comparison: what the neighbours got

The sales comparison approach rests on a single, almost childishly simple idea: **a buyer will not pay much more for a property than it would cost to acquire an equally desirable substitute.** If the flat next door — same building, same size, same view — sold last month for ₫6.9 billion, then yours is worth roughly ₫6.9 billion, because a rational buyer would simply buy the substitute instead. Economists call this the *principle of substitution*, and it is the engine under every home valuation, every Zillow "Zestimate", every bank's quick desktop check, every agent's listing price.

The catch — and it is the whole craft — is that no two properties are ever truly identical. One comp is bigger, one is on a higher floor, one faces a river, one was renovated last year, one sold to a buyer in a hurry. So you can't just average the comps' raw prices. You have to **adjust** each comp toward the subject, feature by feature, until you've stripped out every difference. What's left is each comp's price *as if it were your property* — and those adjusted prices should cluster tightly, telling you the subject's value.

![A sales comparison adjustment grid showing three comparable sales adjusted for size, floor, view, and age toward the subject property, yielding clustered adjusted prices](/imgs/blogs/how-property-is-valued-three-approaches-2.png)

The direction of an adjustment trips up beginners, so fix it firmly: **you always adjust the comp, never the subject, and you adjust the comp toward the subject.** If the comp is *superior* to the subject (it has a view yours lacks), you *subtract* — the comp sold for more partly because of that view, and your view-less subject wouldn't command it. If the comp is *inferior* (it's smaller than yours), you *add* — your bigger subject is worth more than what the small comp fetched. The mantra appraisers chant: **CBS — Comp Better, Subtract.** Comp worse, add.

#### Worked example: valuing Minh's flat by sales comparison

Minh's subject is a 70 m² flat, 8th floor, no view, finished 2 years ago. We have three comps from the last three months:

- **Comp A** sold for **₫7.30bn**. It's 75 m² (bigger), 5th floor (lower), same no-view, and brand new.
- **Comp B** sold for **₫6.90bn**. It's 65 m² (smaller), 8th floor (same), no view, and 5 years old.
- **Comp C** sold for **₫7.55bn**. It's 70 m² (same), 12th floor (higher), has a river view, same age.

Now we adjust each toward the subject. Suppose the local market tells us a square metre is worth about ₫30M, each floor of height about ₫40M, a river view about ₫200M, and a year of age about ₫40M.

**Comp A** (₫7.30bn): it's 5 m² bigger → subtract 5 × ₫30M = **−₫150M**. It's on the 5th not 8th floor (3 floors lower, inferior) → add 3 × ₫40M = **+₫120M**. Same view → ₫0. It's brand new vs the subject's 2 years → it's superior on age, subtract 2 × ₫40M = **−₫80M**. Net adjustment: −150 + 120 − 80 = **−₫110M**. Adjusted price: 7.30bn − 0.11bn = **₫7.19bn**.

**Comp B** (₫6.90bn): 5 m² smaller (inferior) → add 5 × ₫30M = **+₫150M**. Same floor → ₫0. No view, same → ₫0. It's 5 years vs 2 years (older, inferior) → add 3 × ₫40M = **+₫100M**, and there's an extra +₫200M because it sold a touch low in a slower week — call the size-and-age and condition net **+₫450M**. Adjusted price: 6.90bn + 0.45bn = **₫7.35bn**.

**Comp C** (₫7.55bn): same size → ₫0. It's on the 12th floor (4 floors higher, superior) → subtract 4 × ₫40M ≈ −₫160M, but the premium for high floors flattens out, so call it **−₫100M**. It has a river view the subject lacks (superior) → subtract **−₫200M**. Same age → ₫0. Net: −100 − 200 = **−₫300M**. Adjusted price: 7.55bn − 0.30bn = **₫7.25bn**.

The three adjusted prices: **₫7.19bn, ₫7.35bn, ₫7.25bn.** They cluster within ₫160M of each other — a tight, reassuring spread. The sales-comparison indicated value lands around **₫7.2bn (≈ \$280,000)**. Notice the raw prices ranged from ₫6.9bn to ₫7.55bn — a ₫650M spread — but *after adjustment* the cloud collapsed to ₫160M. That collapse is the method working.

*The art is not the arithmetic; it's knowing what each feature is worth, and that knowledge comes only from watching a real, active market trade.*

### Which adjustments matter, and the order to make them

Appraisers adjust in a rough hierarchy, biggest-impact first. **Transactional** adjustments come before physical ones: the financing terms (did the seller offer a sweetheart loan?), the conditions of sale (was it a distressed or related-party deal?), and the market date (prices may have moved since the comp sold — in HCMC's 2024–25 run-up, a comp from eight months ago might need a +5% time adjustment just for the market rising). Only after those do you touch the **physical** differences — location within the neighbourhood, size, age, condition, layout, parking, view.

A practical rule keeps this honest: the comp that needs the *fewest and smallest* adjustments is the most reliable, because every adjustment is itself an estimate that can be wrong. A comp that needs a 25% gross adjustment to reach the subject isn't really comparable; you're no longer comparing, you're guessing. Good appraisers prefer three clean comps over ten distant ones.

### Where the adjustment amounts come from: paired-sales analysis

The single most common question about sales comparison is: *how do you know a river view is worth ₫200M and not ₫100M or ₫400M?* The honest answer is that you don't pull it from the air — you *extract* it from the market using **paired-sales analysis** (sometimes called matched-pairs). The idea is elegant: find two sales that are identical in every way *except one feature*, and the price difference between them *is* the market's price for that feature.

#### Worked example: extracting the view adjustment by paired sales

Suppose in Minh's building two flats sold last quarter that are genuinely twins — same 70 m², same 8th floor, same age, same condition — except one faces the river and one faces the car park. The river flat sold for **₫7.55bn**; the car-park flat sold for **₫7.35bn**. The difference, **₫200M**, is the market's revealed price for "river view" in this building, *with no estimation required* — the market made the comparison for us. Now repeat for floor height: find two otherwise-identical flats four floors apart, and the price gap divided by four is the per-floor adjustment. Stack up enough paired sales and you've built the whole adjustment grid from observed evidence rather than opinion.

In practice you rarely find perfect pairs, so appraisers use *near*-pairs and a dose of judgment, and they triangulate from several methods: paired sales where they exist, statistical regression on a larger sample where there's enough data, and cost-based reasoning as a backstop (a covered parking space "costs" roughly what it costs to build one). The point is that good adjustments are *derived from the market*, not asserted. When you see an adjustment grid where every number is a suspiciously round figure with no paired-sales support, be skeptical — someone may be reverse-engineering the grid to hit a price they'd already decided on.

*An adjustment grid is only as honest as the paired sales behind it; a number with no market evidence is an opinion wearing a spreadsheet's clothes.*

## Income approach: a property is worth the rent it earns

The sales-comparison lens treats a property as a *thing* — a box you compare to other boxes. The income approach treats it as a *machine that produces cash*. To an investor, a rental flat, an office tower, or a shopping mall is not a pile of concrete; it's a stream of future rent, and a stream of cash is worth the lump sum that would produce the same income. This is the same logic that prices a bond or a dividend stock — which is exactly why it bridges so cleanly to the [stock-as-a-claim-on-a-business mindset](/blog/trading/equity-research/stock-as-claim-on-a-business-investor-mindset) — and it is the dominant method for any property bought primarily to earn income rather than to live in.

The mechanism has two steps. First, turn the rent into **NOI** by stripping out the costs of running the building. Second, **capitalize** the NOI — divide it by a cap rate — to get value.

![A pipeline showing the income approach flow from gross rent through vacancy and operating expense deductions to net operating income, then dividing by the cap rate to reach value](/imgs/blogs/how-property-is-valued-three-approaches-3.png)

Let's build NOI carefully, because every term matters:

- Start with **gross potential rent** — the rent if the property were fully let all year.
- Subtract a **vacancy and credit allowance** — no building is 100% occupied forever; tenants leave, units sit empty between leases, some rent goes uncollected. A 5% allowance is a common starting point.
- That gives **effective gross income** — the rent you actually expect to collect.
- Subtract **operating expenses** — the costs of keeping the building running and rentable: management fees, maintenance and repairs, insurance, property taxes, common-area costs. Crucially, you do **not** subtract the mortgage payment or income tax here — NOI is deliberately *unlevered* and pre-tax, so it describes the building itself, independent of how any particular owner financed it.
- What remains is **NOI**.

Then capitalize: **Value = NOI ÷ cap rate.** The cap rate is the yield the market currently demands on properties like this one — set by interest rates, by how risky and illiquid the asset is, and by how much rent growth buyers expect. A low cap rate means buyers will accept a low yield (they're optimistic, or rates are low, so they pay up); a high cap rate means they demand more yield (they're cautious, or rates are high, so they pay less). Because value is NOI *divided* by the cap rate, a lower cap rate produces a *higher* value for the same income — and that inverse relationship is the same seesaw that links a bond's price and its yield, which we unpack in the [bond price-and-yield seesaw](/blog/trading/fixed-income/price-and-yield-the-seesaw-at-the-heart-of-bonds).

#### Worked example: valuing Minh's flat by the income approach

Suppose Minh could rent his District 7 flat for **₫15M per month**, or **₫180M per year** in gross potential rent.

- **Vacancy and credit allowance, 5%**: −₫9M. Effective gross income = ₫180M − ₫9M = **₫171M**.
- **Operating expenses**: building management and sinking-fund fees ₫9M, maintenance and repairs ₫7M, insurance and the tiny non-agricultural land-use tax ₫2M, miscellaneous ₫3M → total **−₫21M**.
- **NOI** = ₫171M − ₫21M = **₫150M per year.**

Now capitalize. For prime HCMC residential, market cap rates sit low — investors accept thin yields because they expect price appreciation. Say the market cap rate is **5.0%**:

$$\text{Value} = \frac{\text{NOI}}{\text{cap rate}} = \frac{₫150\text{M}}{0.05} = ₫3.0\text{bn}.$$

That is a jolt. The sales-comparison lens said ₫7.2bn; the income lens, on the *same flat*, says ₫3.0bn — less than half. Far from a mistake, that gap is one of the most important facts about Vietnamese residential property, and we'll return to it shortly. For now, the mechanics: at a 5% cap rate, every ₫1 of annual NOI is worth ₫20 of value (because 1 ÷ 0.05 = 20), so the rent has to work hard to justify the price.

*The income approach prices a property as a cash machine; when that valuation is far below the market price, the market is buying something other than the rent — usually the hope of appreciation.*

To make the same point in a market where rents pay their way, meet Dana.

#### Worked example: Dana's US duplex by the income approach

Dana, our US investor, is looking at a small duplex in a mid-sized American city listed at **\$300,000**. Each side rents for \$1,500/month, so gross potential rent is 2 × \$1,500 × 12 = **\$36,000/year**.

- **Vacancy 5%**: −\$1,800 → effective gross income **\$34,200**.
- **Operating expenses** (taxes \$3,600, insurance \$1,400, maintenance \$2,000, management \$2,700, misc \$700): −\$10,400. (Note: US property taxes are a real annual line; in Vietnam there's no broad annual property tax yet, which is partly why VN cap-rate maths looks so different.)
- **NOI** = \$34,200 − \$10,400 = **\$23,800/year.**

US small-rental cap rates run far higher than HCMC's — say **7.5%** in this market:

$$\text{Value} = \frac{\$23{,}800}{0.075} = \$317{,}000.$$

Here the income approach lands *above* the \$300,000 asking price — the rent comfortably supports, even exceeds, the price. Dana's duplex is a genuine income asset; Minh's flat, valued the same way, is not. Same arithmetic, opposite conclusion, because the rent-to-price relationship is structurally different in the two markets.

*When the income value sits at or above the asking price, you're buying cash flow; when it sits far below, you're buying a bet on the future.*

### Direct capitalization versus discounted cash flow

What we just did — divide *one* stabilized year's NOI by a single cap rate — is called **direct capitalization**. It's the workhorse, and it's perfect when a property's income is stable and predictable: a fully-let apartment block, a single-tenant building on a long lease. But it quietly assumes the NOI is roughly level forever, which falls apart for properties with *lumpy* futures — a building that's half-empty today but filling up, a property with a major lease expiring in three years, a development that won't produce income until it's finished.

For those, professionals use **discounted cash flow (DCF)**: project the NOI year by year for, say, ten years, project a sale (a "reversion") at the end, then discount every future cash flow back to today at a *discount rate* that reflects the risk. It's the same machinery that values a stock or a bond by its future cash flows — the property is just a longer, lumpier stream. DCF is more honest about lumpy income but also more fragile, because it stacks assumption on assumption (every year's rent, the exit cap rate, the discount rate), and small changes in those compound. The craft is matching the tool to the property: direct capitalization for stable income, DCF for income that changes shape over time. For most valuations in this post, direct capitalization is the right and sufficient lens.

A subtlety that trips up first-time investors: the **gross yield** versus the **net yield**. Minh's flat rents for ₫180M on a ₫7.0bn price — a *gross* yield of 180 ÷ 7,000 ≈ **2.6%**. But after vacancy and operating costs, the NOI is ₫150M, a *net* yield (the true cap rate) of 150 ÷ 7,000 ≈ **2.1%**. Always check which one a quoted "yield" is — landlords advertise gross, the income approach runs on net, and the gap between them (here, costs ate about a fifth of the rent) is exactly the operating cost the gross number hides.

## Cost approach: the floor under everything

The third lens steps back from both sales and rent and asks a builder's question: **what would it cost to recreate this property from nothing today?** Buy the land, pour the foundation, raise the structure, fit it out — total it up, then knock off the value the existing building has already lost to age and obsolescence. The logic, again, is substitution: a rational buyer won't pay much more for an existing building than the cost of buying a lot and building an equivalent one. Cost therefore acts as a ceiling on price in normal markets and, because land is always worth *something*, as a floor under value.

![A stacked breakdown of the cost approach showing land value plus replacement construction cost, minus accrued depreciation, equalling the indicated value](/imgs/blogs/how-property-is-valued-three-approaches-4.png)

The cost approach has three ingredients:

1. **Land value** — what the bare site is worth, almost always estimated by sales comparison of *vacant land* sales. Land is valued separately because it doesn't depreciate the way buildings do; a structure wears out, a location does not.
2. **Replacement cost new** — what it would cost, in today's prices, to build a building of equivalent utility. You can estimate this per square metre (the *comparative-unit* method) or by pricing each component (the *quantity-survey* method that contractors use).
3. **Accrued depreciation** — the total value the *existing* building has lost, in three flavours:
   - **Physical deterioration**: ordinary wear and tear — a 12-year-old roof, dated plumbing, scuffed floors.
   - **Functional obsolescence**: design that's out of step with what the market now wants — a windowless interior room, a ceiling too low, a layout no one builds anymore.
   - **External (economic) obsolescence**: value lost to forces *outside* the property — a new elevated expressway built beside it, a factory that opened next door, a neighbourhood in decline. This is the only kind the owner can't fix by spending money.

Value = land + replacement cost new − accrued depreciation.

#### Worked example: valuing Minh's building share by the cost approach

We have to be careful: Minh owns a flat in a tower, not a standalone house, so "rebuild cost" means his pro-rata share of the land and his unit's slice of the structure. Treat it as a self-contained box on its own notional 60 m² of allocated land for the illustration:

- **Land value**: his allocated land share is worth roughly ₫40M/m² × 60 m² = **₫2.4bn**. (Allocated urban land for an apartment is valued well below street-front land — this is illustrative.)
- **Replacement cost new**: rebuilding an equivalent finished unit today — structure, fit-out, common-area share — comes to about **₫5.0bn**.
- **Gross cost** before depreciation = 2.4 + 5.0 = **₫7.4bn**.
- **Accrued depreciation**: the building is 12 years old (older than our sales-comp example assumed for the unit — the tower predates the recent fit-out). Say it has lost about ₫0.6bn to physical wear and slightly dated common areas — roughly 12% of the structure's cost. Subtract **−₫0.6bn**.
- **Indicated value** = 7.4 − 0.6 = **₫6.8bn (≈ \$263,000).**

So the cost lens says ₫6.8bn — close to the sales-comparison ₫7.2bn, and far above the income approach's ₫3.0bn.

*The cost approach reminds you that a big share of an apartment's value is the land underneath it — a point we develop in the [land-versus-buildings piece](/blog/trading/real-estate/land-vs-buildings-two-assets-in-every-property) — and that the structure itself is a wasting asset that loses value as it ages.*

### Pulling depreciation apart: the three kinds, with numbers

The softest, most error-prone number in the cost approach is depreciation, so it pays to break it into its three components rather than guess a single percentage. Let's redo the depreciation on Minh's unit explicitly.

#### Worked example: the ₫0.6bn depreciation, decomposed

The structure's replacement cost new is ₫5.0bn. Of that, accrued depreciation breaks into:

- **Physical deterioration**: the building is 12 years old with, say, a 50-year economic life. Straight-line, that's roughly 12 ÷ 50 = 24% of the *curable and short-lived* portion worn out — but most of the structure (concrete frame, foundations) barely ages, so the *effective* physical loss is smaller. Call it about **₫0.45bn**: worn finishes, an aging lift, dated bathrooms.
- **Functional obsolescence**: the unit has a slightly awkward original layout the market has moved past, and no fibre-optic conduit. A modern equivalent wouldn't have these flaws. Cost to cure, or the value the market discounts for them: about **₫0.15bn**.
- **External (economic) obsolescence**: nothing material here — the neighbourhood is improving, not declining — so **₫0**. (If a new elevated road had been built beside the tower, this number could be large and *incurable*, since the owner can't move the road.)

Total accrued depreciation = 0.45 + 0.15 + 0 = **₫0.6bn**, exactly the figure we subtracted. Decomposing it this way does two things: it forces you to justify each piece against something real, and it separates the *curable* depreciation (worn finishes you could renovate away) from the *incurable* (a bad location, an unfixable layout), which matters enormously when deciding whether a renovation is worth it.

*Depreciation isn't one number; it's physical wear plus dated design plus outside forces — and only the first two can be fixed with money.*

### When the cost approach is the one you trust

For an ordinary flat with plenty of comps and a rental market, the cost approach is usually the *weakest* of the three — too many soft estimates (what's the land really worth? how much depreciation, exactly?). But it comes into its own in three situations:

- **Brand-new construction**, where there's barely any depreciation to estimate and the cost is fresh and known.
- **Unique or special-use buildings** — a church, a school, a power substation, a custom factory — that almost never sell and produce no market rent, so there are neither comps nor income to work with. Cost may be the *only* approach that applies.
- **Insurance valuation**, where you specifically want the cost to rebuild after a fire, not the market value (insurers cover the structure, not the land).

## Why the three diverge — and what the gap is telling you

We now have three numbers for Minh's flat: sales comparison ₫7.2bn, cost ₫6.8bn, income ₫3.0bn. A beginner sees a contradiction and asks which is "right". A professional sees a *signal* and asks what the spread means. **Divergence is data.** Each lens measures a different thing, so when they disagree, the disagreement diagnoses the property and the market.

![Two side-by-side scenarios showing how the valuation approaches diverge: a new special-use building where cost exceeds income value, and a hot home market where comparable sale prices exceed income value](/imgs/blogs/how-property-is-valued-three-approaches-5.png)

Three common patterns, each with a meaning:

**Sales and cost agree, but income is far below (Minh's case).** This is the signature of a market where buyers are paying for *expected appreciation*, not current rent. HCMC residential is the textbook example: at a 5% gross yield (and a *net* yield closer to 4% after costs), the rent alone can't justify the price — buyers are betting the price keeps rising, as it did roughly +33% in 2024 and another +40% into 2025 by the Ministry of Construction's figures. The income approach is *correctly* telling you the rent doesn't pay; it just isn't telling you the whole story, because the whole story includes a wager on the future. The gap between the price and the income value is, quite literally, the price of that wager.

**Cost is far above both sales and income.** This is the signature of an *overbuilt* property — someone spent more to construct it than the market will ever pay or the rent will ever support. A lavish custom mansion in a modest neighbourhood, a gold-plated office tower in a city with too much office space. The cost approach says ₫9bn; the market and the rent say ₫6.5bn; the ₫2.5bn gap is *over-improvement* — money sunk into a building the market doesn't reward. Developers who ignore this signal during a boom are the ones holding empty towers in the bust.

**Sales is far above both cost and income.** This is the signature of a *speculative bubble* or a genuine land-scarcity premium. If flats are trading at ₫7.2bn when they cost ₫6.8bn to build *and* rent at an income value of ₫3.0bn, the market price is being driven by something other than fundamentals — land scarcity, capital flight into property, or pure momentum. Sometimes that premium is durable (Singapore, Hong Kong, central Tokyo have permanently scarce land); sometimes it evaporates (Vietnam 2011–13, the US 2006–2012, where the Case-Shiller national index fell about 27% peak-to-trough as prices that had detached from incomes and rents reconnected).

The lesson is not "find the right number". It's "read the spread". A property where all three lenses cluster is a property priced on fundamentals. A property where they fan out is a property where someone is paying for a story — and your job is to decide whether you believe the story.

### Two famous times the spread screamed and people ignored it

**Japan, 1990.** At the peak of Japan's asset bubble, the land under the Imperial Palace in Tokyo was reckoned to be worth more than all the land in California. Capitalized at the rent commercial property actually earned, prices implied cap rates so low — yields of barely 1–2% — that the income approach was shouting that prices had detached entirely from the cash the buildings produced. Buyers reconciled to the sales-comparison number anyway ("but it just traded at this price!") and treated the income lens as broken. It wasn't broken; it was *right*. When the bubble burst, Japanese land prices fell for the better part of two decades. The income lens had been the truth-teller all along.

**China, 2021 (Evergrande).** China's developers built vast inventories of apartments valued, on their books, near *cost* — land plus construction. But many of those flats sat in cities with more housing than people who wanted to live there, so the rent they could earn (income approach) and the price at which they'd actually clear (sales comparison) were far below cost. The three lenses had fanned wide, with cost stranded above a collapsing market and income. When Evergrande defaulted in 2021 and the property machine seized up, the gap closed the painful way: cost-based book values were written down toward what the market and the rent would actually bear. Once again, the lens that diverged from cost was the one telling the truth.

Both episodes carry the same lesson Vietnam's own cycles teach. When sales and cost soar above income for years, the comfortable story is "this time the rent doesn't matter, it's all appreciation". Sometimes — for genuinely scarce land — that story holds. Far more often, income is the lens quietly keeping score, and the spread eventually closes by the price falling to meet the rent, not the rent rising to meet the price.

## Reconciliation: weighting three numbers into one

Eventually the appraiser has to write *one* number on the page. Reconciliation is how. It is emphatically **not** an average — averaging ₫7.2bn, ₫3.0bn, and ₫6.8bn to get ₫5.7bn would be a professional embarrassment, because it pretends all three lenses are equally trustworthy for this property when they plainly aren't. Reconciliation is a *weighting* judgment: give the most weight to the approach whose evidence is strongest for *this* property type and *this* market, and the least to the approach that's straining.

![A weighted graph showing the three approaches feeding into a final opinion of value, with the sales comparison weighted 60 percent, income 30 percent, and cost 10 percent](/imgs/blogs/how-property-is-valued-three-approaches-7.png)

The weights follow the property type, not a formula:

- **An owner-occupied home in an active market**: lean hard on **sales comparison** (it's what buyers actually use), give the **income** approach a small voice (most homes aren't bought for rent), and treat **cost** as a sanity-check floor. Roughly 60% / 30% / 10% — or even 80% / 10% / 10% in a deep, liquid market with great comps.
- **An income property** (apartment block, office, retail): lean hard on the **income** approach (it's what investors actually use), with sales comparison as a cross-check and cost as a floor. Maybe 70% income / 20% sales / 10% cost.
- **A new or special-use building**: lean on **cost**, because comps and income may barely exist.

#### Worked example: reconciling Minh's three values

For Minh's flat — an owner-occupied home in HCMC's hot but well-traded District 7 — the appraiser weights the home-style way and, importantly, *trims* the income value rather than letting it drag the answer toward nonsense. The three indicated values, lightly rounded for reconciliation, are sales ₫7.2bn, income ₫6.4bn (the appraiser uses a forward-looking, appreciation-adjusted income figure here, not the raw ₫3.0bn current-rent capitalization, because for a *residential* valuation the rent-only number understates what owner-occupiers will pay), and cost ₫6.8bn.

$$\text{Value} = 0.60 \times 7.2 + 0.30 \times 6.4 + 0.10 \times 6.8 = 4.32 + 1.92 + 0.68 = ₫6.92\text{bn}.$$

Round to a defensible **₫7.0bn (≈ \$270,000)** as the final opinion of value. Notice it lands *inside* the spread of the three lenses but *closest to* sales comparison — exactly where a home valuation should sit.

![A bar chart comparing the three approaches' value estimates for one HCMC apartment converging on a reconciled opinion of value around seven billion dong](/imgs/blogs/how-property-is-valued-three-approaches-6.png)

*Reconciliation is the appraiser's judgment made explicit: it says which evidence to trust and by how much, and produces a number you can defend line by line.*

A word on why the appraiser used ₫6.4bn for income in the reconciliation rather than the raw ₫3.0bn from the strict current-rent capitalization. For *investment* property you'd use the strict number and let it dominate. For *residential* owner-occupied property, the income approach is acknowledged to undervalue, because homeowners derive value from living there that no rent captures — so the income lens is either down-weighted heavily or replaced with an appreciation-adjusted figure. Either way the discipline is the same: be explicit about why each lens gets the weight it does. An appraisal that just asserts a number without reconciling is not an appraisal; it's a guess with a letterhead.

## Common misconceptions

**"An appraisal is THE price."** No — an appraisal is one professional's *opinion* of the most probable price, as of a specific date, supported by evidence. Two competent appraisers can land 5–10% apart on the same property and both be defensible, because the adjustments and weights involve judgment. The market price is whatever a buyer and seller actually agree on, which may be above or below any appraisal. The appraisal is evidence, not a verdict.

**"Online estimates (Zestimate, the bank's automated value) are appraisals."** They're *automated valuation models* — algorithms that run a statistical sales-comparison on whatever data they have. They're fast, free, and genuinely useful as a first pass, but they can't see inside the property, can't judge condition, can't make the fine adjustments a human makes, and are weakest exactly where it matters most: unique properties, thin markets, and rapidly moving prices. In Vietnam, where transaction prices are routinely *under-declared* on contracts to dodge the 2% transfer tax, automated models built on registry data are systematically biased low. Treat them as a starting hypothesis, never a conclusion.

**"Comps don't lie."** Comps lie all the time — in a *thin* market. The sales-comparison approach needs a steady flow of genuinely similar, arm's-length, recent sales. In a market where almost nothing has traded (a luxury segment, a rural area, a frozen market like Vietnam in 2011–13 or 2022–23), the "comps" you're forced to use are stale, dissimilar, or distressed, and every large adjustment compounds the error. A confident-looking grid built on three bad comps produces a confident-looking wrong answer. Thin comps are *the* central problem in Vietnamese valuation, and we return to it below.

**"Value equals cost."** Tempting and wrong. Cost is what you spent; value is what someone will pay. They diverge whenever a building is over-improved for its location, functionally obsolete, or sitting in a market that's moved. The cost approach is a *check*, not the answer, for ordinary property — its biggest use is to catch over-improvement (cost far above market) and to set a land-based floor.

**"Land always appreciates, buildings always depreciate, so newer is always better value."** Half-true, dangerously stated. Land *usually* holds or gains value because it's scarce and doesn't wear out; buildings *do* lose value to wear and obsolescence. But a new building in the wrong location is still a bad value, and an older building on a brilliant lot can be worth far more than its depreciated structure suggests — because you're really buying the land. "Newer" is not a value strategy; *location plus the right structure at the right price* is.

**"A higher cap rate means a better property."** Backwards. A higher cap rate means the market demands *more yield*, which it does for *riskier, less desirable* property — a worse building, a worse location, a shakier tenant. The trophy assets in the best locations trade at the *lowest* cap rates because buyers will accept a low yield for safety and growth. A 9% cap rate isn't a bargain; it's the market pricing in problems you should go find.

**"An appraisal is objective and scientific, so there's a right answer."** An appraisal is *disciplined*, not *objective*. Every step involves judgment: which comps to pick, how big each adjustment is, what cap rate the market really demands, how much depreciation has accrued, and — the biggest lever of all — how to weight the three approaches in reconciliation. Change any of those defensibly and the number moves. That's why two honest, competent appraisers regularly differ by several percent, and why appraisal is regulated as a *professional opinion* rather than a measurement. The scientific part is the *method* (transparent, evidence-based, reproducible logic); the answer is still a judgment. The danger isn't subjectivity — it's *hidden* subjectivity, an appraiser who buries the judgment calls and presents a single number as if it fell out of a machine.

## How it shows up in real markets

**Vietnam's thin-comp problem.** The sales-comparison approach assumes a flow of clean, recent, arm's-length sales. Vietnam fights this assumption on three fronts. First, transaction prices are widely *under-declared* on contracts — buyers and sellers agree a "low" official price to cut the 2% personal-income-tax-on-transfer and the 0.5% registration fee, with the balance paid off the books, so the registry price isn't the real price. Second, large slices of the market periodically *freeze*: in 2011–13 (when CPI ran near 18% in 2011 and VAMC was set up to clean up bank balance sheets) and again in 2022–23 (after the Tân Hoàng Minh and Vạn Thịnh Phát/SCB arrests froze the corporate-bond market), transaction volumes collapsed and there were simply no fresh comps. Third, the Land Law 2024 (effective August 2024) replaced the old five-year framework land-price table with *annual* land-price tables that aim to track the market more closely — a direct attempt to give valuers and tax authorities a better reference point in a market where private comps are unreliable. When sales evidence is this compromised, valuers lean harder on the income and cost approaches and on official land tables — which is precisely the reconciliation discipline this post is about.

**How banks value collateral.** When Minh applies for a mortgage, the bank doesn't care about the most optimistic price; it cares about what it could *recover* if it had to foreclose and sell quickly. So bank appraisers are systematically *conservative*: they favour the lower of the approaches, apply a haircut, and then lend a fraction of that — the *loan-to-value* ratio, typically 70% in Vietnam, often less in a stressed market. That's why the bank's ₫6.8bn collateral value came in below the ₫7.3bn asking price: the bank is valuing the *downside*, not the dream. The same dynamic plays out globally — in 2008, US lenders learned the hard way that the appraisals supporting their loans had ridden the bubble up, so the "value" backing trillions in mortgages evaporated when prices reconnected to fundamentals.

#### Worked example: the appraisal that sizes Minh's loan

Minh agrees to buy the flat for **₫7.3bn** and asks the bank for a mortgage. The bank's appraiser ignores the asking price and reconciles the three approaches to a conservative collateral value of **₫6.8bn** — it leans on the income approach and the official land table, not the seller's optimism. The bank then lends against *that* number at a 70% loan-to-value ratio:

$$\text{Max loan} = 70\% \times ₫6.8\text{bn} = ₫4.76\text{bn}.$$

Minh planned on borrowing 70% of ₫7.3bn (₫5.11bn) and putting down ₫2.19bn. Instead the loan is capped at ₫4.76bn, so his required down payment jumps to ₫7.3bn − ₫4.76bn = **₫2.54bn** — ₫350m more cash than he budgeted, purely because the *appraised* value, not the price, sizes the loan. *The bank's valuation isn't an opinion about what the flat is worth to Minh; it's an opinion about what the bank could recover without him — and that gap lands squarely in his down payment.*

**A US appraisal in a refinance.** Dana wants to refinance her duplex to pull out equity. The lender orders a full appraisal. The appraiser walks the property, pulls three to six comps, builds an adjustment grid like the one above, runs the income approach (it's a rental, so income carries real weight here — perhaps 50% income, 40% sales, 10% cost), and reconciles to one number. If the appraisal comes in *below* what Dana hoped, her refinance shrinks or dies — the loan is sized off the *appraised* value, not the value in her head. This is the single most common place an ordinary person collides with formal valuation: the refinance or purchase appraisal that comes in "low" and reshapes the whole deal. It came in low not out of malice but because the comps, adjusted honestly, said so.

**The special-use building with no comps and no rent.** A municipality needs to value a 40-year-old water-treatment plant for an insurance policy. There are no comparable sales (nobody buys water-treatment plants on the open market) and no rent (it earns nothing). Sales comparison is impossible; income is impossible. Only the **cost approach** applies: estimate the land, estimate the replacement cost of an equivalent modern plant, subtract the depreciation for 40 years of wear and outdated technology. This is the clearest case where two of the three lenses simply don't exist, and the third is the entire valuation.

**The hot-market over-improvement.** During HCMC's 2018 đất nền (land-plot) fever in the satellite provinces, some developers and individuals built lavish villas on speculative plots far from any real demand. Valued by cost, those villas looked worth ₫10bn; valued by the rent they could earn (near zero, in an empty district) or by comparable resales (few, and falling once the fever broke), they were worth a fraction. The three approaches fanned wide apart, and the gap *was the warning*. The buyers who reconciled honestly walked away; the buyers who trusted the cost number alone — "but it cost ₫10bn to build!" — were left holding it.

#### Worked example: a thin market where only the income approach is reliable

Dana is asked to value a small office building in a town where exactly two offices have sold in the last three years — one a distressed bank sale, one a related-party transfer between two family companies. Both "comps" are useless: one is a forced sale (not market value), one isn't arm's-length. Sales comparison is dead on arrival. The cost approach is shaky too — local construction-cost data is thin and the land value itself rests on the same missing comps.

But the building is **fully leased to a national pharmacy chain** on a 10-year lease at **\$60,000/year**, with the tenant paying its own operating costs (a *triple-net* lease, so NOI ≈ the rent). Investors in this region buy stabilized single-tenant retail at a **8.0% cap rate**:

$$\text{Value} = \frac{\$60{,}000}{0.08} = \$750{,}000.$$

Here the income approach isn't just *a* lens — it's the *only* trustworthy one, because it rests on a real contract (the lease) and a real market signal (the cap rate investors actually pay), neither of which depends on the missing comps. Dana reconciles to essentially the income number, **\$750,000**, and notes in her report that sales comparison and cost were considered and rejected for lack of reliable data.

*In a thin market, the income approach can stand alone because a signed lease is a harder fact than a stale comp — value the contract, not the absent neighbours.*

## When this matters to you / further reading

You collide with property valuation more often than you'd think, and now you can read each collision correctly. When you buy a home, the agent's price is a sales-comparison estimate — ask to see the comps and check whether the adjustments are honest. When you apply for a mortgage, the bank's appraisal is a deliberately conservative blend weighted toward what it could recover, which is why it can land below the asking price without anyone being wrong. When you consider a rental as an investment, run the income approach yourself — NOI ÷ cap rate — and if that value sits far below the price, you've found, precisely, the size of the appreciation bet you're being asked to make. And when the three lenses fan apart, don't reach for the comfortable number; sit with the gap, because the gap is the most informative thing in the whole exercise.

The deepest practical skill here is humility about precision. There is no single true price, only a defensible *range* and an honest opinion about where inside it the property most probably sits. A valuation that hides its uncertainty behind a confident single figure is less trustworthy than one that shows its three lenses and reconciles them out loud. That, in the end, is what separates a number with a letterhead from an appraisal.
