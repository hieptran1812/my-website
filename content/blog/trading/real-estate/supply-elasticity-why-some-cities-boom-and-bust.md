> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — The *same* surge in housing demand produces wildly different outcomes depending on one thing: how easily a city can build. Where supply is **elastic** (flat land, light zoning — Houston, Phoenix, the satellite fringe of Ho Chi Minh City), a demand surge mostly turns into *more houses at roughly stable prices*, and then — because building takes years — into a *glut and a price bust*. Where supply is **inelastic** (boxed in by geography or red tape — San Francisco, central Hanoi and HCMC), the same surge turns into *pure price*: violent booms, brutal unaffordability, and almost no gluts.
>
> - **Elasticity sets the amplitude of the cycle and decides where the pain lands.** Elastic cities pay for booms with overbuilding and price crashes; inelastic cities pay with permanent unaffordability.
> - A demand shock always splits into two channels — *more quantity* and *higher price*. Elastic supply routes it into quantity; inelastic supply routes it into price. That single split explains most of what looks mysterious about housing.
> - The **construction lag** (2–4 years from "break ground" to "keys in hand") is why elastic markets overshoot into gluts: by the time the homes arrive, the demand that justified them has already cooled.
> - The one number to remember: hand the same **+20% demand** to two cities, and the elastic one might see prices rise ~10% (then dip), while the inelastic one sees them spike **~50%** — and stay there.

In 2006, two American cities were both being flooded with the same thing: cheap mortgage money and a national mania for buying houses. One was Las Vegas, sprawling across the flat Nevada desert with almost nothing to stop a builder. The other was coastal California — San Diego, the Bay Area — wedged between an ocean and a mountain range and wrapped in decades of land-use rules. Same demand shock. Same era of easy credit. Yet the two markets behaved like different species of animal. Las Vegas threw up subdivisions at a furious pace, prices ran up, and then — when the music stopped in 2008 — it crashed harder than almost anywhere in the country, because all those new homes had nobody to live in them. Coastal California also boomed and also fell, but it never built its way into a true glut, and within a decade its prices weren't just back — they were higher than ever, and *less* affordable than before.

The story everyone tells about 2008 is about demand: subprime loans, speculation, a bubble. That's half the picture. The other half — the half that explains why the *same* bubble produced a building binge in Phoenix and a pure price explosion in San Francisco — is **supply**. Specifically, how *elastic* supply was: how easily each city could respond to all that demand by simply building more.

This is one of the most powerful and least taught ideas in all of real estate. Once you have it, you can look at any city in the world — Houston or Hanoi, Tokyo or Tampa, the rice-paddy fringe of HCMC or its locked-down central core — and make a real prediction about how its housing will behave. Will a boom turn into homes or into prices? Will the cycle end in a glut or in an affordability crisis? Elasticity tells you. The diagram below is the mental model for the whole post: take one identical demand shock, send it into an elastic market and an inelastic one, and watch it come out the other side as two completely different things.

![Two panels showing the same rightward demand shift hitting an elastic flat supply curve to become mostly more quantity at stable price versus an inelastic steep supply curve to become mostly higher price](/imgs/blogs/supply-elasticity-why-some-cities-boom-and-bust-1.png)

We'll meet two recurring characters along the way. **Minh** is a young professional in HCMC weighing whether to buy a central apartment or a land plot out on the fringe. **Dana** is a US investor trying to understand why her Phoenix rental and her Bay Area rental — bought the same year with the same thesis — have had completely opposite decades. Their two markets are the elastic and inelastic poles of the same idea, and watching their experiences will keep the abstraction honest.

## Foundations: elasticity in one supply-and-demand picture

Before we can talk about elastic versus inelastic cities, we need three building blocks, all defined from zero: the **demand curve**, the **supply curve**, and the **elasticity** that describes how steep the supply curve is. If you've never seen a supply-and-demand diagram, don't worry — we'll build it from a single street corner.

### Demand: how much people want to buy at each price

A **demand curve** is just a list, drawn as a line, that answers one question: *at each possible price, how many homes do people want to buy?* The list always slopes the same way. At a very high price, few people can afford to buy, so demand is low. Drop the price, and more buyers appear. So the demand curve slopes *downward* as you move from left (low quantity) to right (high quantity): lower price, more buyers willing.

What shifts the *whole* demand curve to the right — meaning more buyers at *every* price — is what we'll call a **demand shock**: a wave of new buyers or new buying power. Falling interest rates make mortgages cheaper, so the same family can now afford a bigger loan. A booming local economy brings in workers who need homes. A national speculative mania convinces everyone that prices only go up, so investors pile in. A new metro line makes a far-flung district suddenly commutable. Any of these pushes the demand curve right: at any given price, more people now want to buy.

The crucial fact for this whole post: a demand shock is the *same* in both an elastic and an inelastic city. The wave of money hitting Las Vegas in 2006 and the wave hitting San Francisco were broadly the same wave. What differed was not the demand — it was the *supply response*.

### Supply: how many homes builders will provide at each price

A **supply curve** answers the mirror question: *at each price, how many homes will get built and offered for sale?* This one slopes *upward*: the higher the price homes fetch, the more profit in building them, so the more builders want to build. Cheap homes, few builders bother; expensive homes, everyone wants in.

But here's where cities diverge, and it's the heart of everything. *How much* extra building you get for a given rise in price depends on how hard it is to build. In some cities, a small rise in prices unleashes a torrent of new construction — there's flat empty land everywhere, permits are quick, and builders pounce. In other cities, prices can double and barely any new homes appear — there's no land left, the zoning forbids density, and getting a permit takes half a decade. The *responsiveness* of supply to price is what we call **elasticity**.

### Price elasticity of supply, defined plainly

The **price elasticity of supply** is a single number that measures how much the *quantity* of new homes responds when the *price* changes. The textbook definition is the percent change in quantity supplied divided by the percent change in price:

$$ E_s = \frac{\%\ \Delta\ \text{quantity supplied}}{\%\ \Delta\ \text{price}} $$

Here $E_s$ is the elasticity, $\Delta$ means "change in", and the two percentages are exactly what they say. You don't need to compute this to use it — the intuition is what matters:

- **Elastic supply** means $E_s$ is large (greater than 1). A 10% rise in price calls forth *more* than a 10% rise in new homes. On the diagram, this is a **flat** (nearly horizontal) supply curve. Houston is the poster child: a small price rise, a big building response.
- **Inelastic supply** means $E_s$ is small (less than 1, often near zero). A 10% rise in price barely budges the number of new homes. On the diagram, this is a **steep** (nearly vertical) supply curve. San Francisco is the poster child: prices can soar and the housing stock hardly grows.

There's a famous set of estimates by the economist Albert Saiz that put real numbers on this for US metros. Wide-open Sun Belt cities came out with supply elasticities above 2 or 3 — extremely responsive. Hemmed-in coastal cities like San Francisco, Los Angeles, and Miami came out near or below 1 — barely responsive. The gap isn't subtle; it's the difference between a market that answers demand with bulldozers and one that answers with bidding wars.

### Why the slope decides everything

Now combine the two curves. A market sits where supply meets demand — the **equilibrium**, the price and quantity at which the homes builders will provide exactly equals the homes buyers want. A demand shock shoves the demand curve to the right. The new equilibrium slides *up along the supply curve*. And the shape of that supply curve decides how the shock splits between two outcomes:

- If supply is **flat** (elastic), the new equilibrium slides mostly *sideways* — lots more quantity, only a little more price. The shock becomes **houses**.
- If supply is **steep** (inelastic), the new equilibrium slides mostly *upward* — barely more quantity, a lot more price. The shock becomes **price**.

That is the entire engine of this post in one sentence: **a demand shock always splits into more-quantity and higher-price, and the elasticity of supply decides the split.** Everything else — gluts, busts, affordability crises, the difference between Phoenix and the Bay — falls out of this one fact.

#### Worked example: splitting the same +20% demand two ways

Let's make the split concrete with Dana's two markets. Suppose a city has **100,000** homes selling at an average of **\$300,000**. A demand shock arrives — call it the 2020–2021 era of record-low mortgage rates plus remote-work migration — that, at the *old* price, would have **20%** more buyers wanting in. The question is how that +20% demand resolves.

In Dana's **elastic** Phoenix-type market (say $E_s = 3$, very responsive), builders rush in. Most of the demand becomes new homes. The arithmetic of an elastic split: the +20% demand might resolve as roughly **+15% quantity** and only **+5% price**. So the city ends up with about **115,000** homes at around **\$315,000**. Dana's existing rental rose a modest \$15,000 in value, but the city got 15,000 new homes.

In Dana's **inelastic** Bay-Area-type market (say $E_s = 0.3$, barely responsive), builders *can't* respond — no land, no permits. The same +20% demand has almost nowhere to go but price. The split flips: roughly **+3% quantity** and **+17% price**. The city ends up with about **103,000** homes at around **\$351,000**. Only 3,000 new homes, but every existing home jumped \$51,000.

Same demand. Same +20%. One city got 15,000 homes and a \$15,000 price bump; the other got 3,000 homes and a \$51,000 price bump. *Elasticity didn't change how much people wanted to buy — it changed whether their wanting turned into houses or into prices.*

## What makes supply inelastic

If elasticity is the master variable, the obvious next question is: *what makes a city elastic or inelastic in the first place?* Inelasticity isn't random. It's the sum of a handful of specific, nameable frictions, each of which makes it harder to add homes. Some are imposed by nature; most are imposed by people. The figure below stacks them up and shows them feeding into the same outcome — a supply curve bent toward vertical.

![A graph showing geography zoning slow approvals neighbour vetoes and land assembly each feeding into few new homes built which bends the supply curve steep so demand becomes price](/imgs/blogs/supply-elasticity-why-some-cities-boom-and-bust-2.png)

### Geographic constraints: when you physically can't build out

The first and most ancient constraint is the land itself. A city ringed by ocean, mountains, wetlands, or a steep river valley simply runs out of flat, buildable ground. San Francisco sits on the tip of a peninsula with water on three sides; Saiz's research found that a huge share of its potential building land is literally underwater or on unbuildable slopes. Hong Kong is mountains and harbor. Mumbai is a narrow tongue of land. When you can't build *outward*, your only option is to build *upward* — and that runs straight into the next constraint, zoning.

Geography is the one constraint policy can't fully repeal. But it's worth seeing how *little* land cities actually run out of in practice: most "we're out of land" cities have plenty of land — they've just made it illegal to use it densely. Tokyo, which is enormous and dense, keeps building and stays comparatively affordable. Geography sets a floor on inelasticity; *rules* do most of the rest.

### Zoning and land-use regulation: making density illegal

**Zoning** is the body of law that dictates what can be built where: how tall, how dense, what use (homes, shops, factories), how big the lot, how much parking. **Land-use regulation** is the broader bundle including environmental review, historic preservation, design rules, and growth boundaries. Together they are the single biggest reason supply is inelastic in expensive cities — and unlike geography, they're entirely a choice.

The clearest case is the American "single-family zone." In huge swaths of US cities, it is *illegal* to build anything but one detached house per lot. You could have demand for a six-unit building screaming at you, with buyers lined up, and the law forbids it. That law turns a potentially elastic plot (which could absorb six families) into an inelastic one (which can absorb exactly one). Multiply across a whole city and you've manufactured inelasticity by statute. Minimum lot sizes, height caps, floor-area limits, and parking mandates (forcing each apartment to come with one or two car spaces, which eats land and money) all do the same thing: they cap how many homes a given piece of land may legally hold.

### Slow approvals: the permitting tax on time

Even where dense building is legal, *getting permission* can take years. A project may need environmental impact reports, traffic studies, multiple public hearings, design review, and the chance for opponents to sue and appeal at each step. Every month of delay is money — interest on the land loan, holding costs, inflation in construction prices — and it adds risk, because the market that justified the project might vanish before the permit clears. A two-to-five-year approval gauntlet makes builders demand a much higher expected price before they'll start, which is the same as saying supply is less elastic: it takes a *bigger* price rise to get the *same* building response.

### NIMBY opposition: the incentive to block

**NIMBY** stands for "Not In My Back Yard" — the well-documented tendency of existing homeowners to oppose new housing near them. It's not irrational. A homeowner's largest asset is their house, and its value is *higher* when supply is scarce. New towers nearby can mean traffic, shade, change, and — crucially — competition that softens their home's price. So incumbents show up to hearings, organize, and sue to block new building. In a system where local discretion governs approvals, a motivated minority of neighbors can stop a project that a silent majority of future residents would benefit from. NIMBYism is the political engine that *keeps* zoning restrictive: the people who'd gain from more housing (renters, young people, future arrivals) aren't in the room; the people who'd lose are.

### Land assembly: the holdout problem

To build something big — a tower, a master-planned block — a developer often needs to combine many small adjacent parcels into one site. This is **land assembly**, and it has a nasty failure mode: the **holdout**. If you need ten lots and nine owners agree to sell, the tenth, knowing you can't build without them, can demand an outrageous price or simply refuse. The math of assembly means a single stubborn owner can kill a project, which makes large dense developments slow, expensive, and risky to even attempt. In fragmented-ownership cities — much of central Hanoi and HCMC, with their dense patchwork of narrow "tube houses," each individually owned — assembly is brutal, and it's a major reason the central cores can't easily redevelop to higher density.

### Infrastructure: the supply you can't see

One more, subtler constraint: even buildable, legal, un-opposed land is useless for housing if you can't *get to it* or *service it*. A flat field 40 km from downtown with no road, no water main, no sewer, and no transit is not really "supply" — nobody will live there. Infrastructure (roads, metro lines, water, power) is what *converts* raw land into usable housing supply. This cuts both ways: the *absence* of infrastructure makes a city inelastic even where land is plentiful, and *building* infrastructure (a new metro line) is one of the most powerful ways to make supply more elastic — a lever we'll return to in the policy section.

#### Worked example: the regulatory tax baked into each unit

Economists call the gap between what a home *costs to build* and what it *sells for*, in a tightly regulated city, the **regulatory tax** on housing — the price premium created purely by the difficulty of building. Let's estimate it for one of Minh's options.

Suppose, on the elastic HCMC fringe, the all-in cost to deliver a basic home is **₫25 million per m²** (≈ \$965/m²) — land, materials, labor, and a normal builder's profit — and homes there actually sell for about **₫28 million/m²** (≈ \$1,080/m²). The gap is small: roughly **₫3 million/m²** (≈ \$116/m²), a healthy but ordinary margin. Supply is elastic, so price hugs cost.

Now central District 1, inelastic. The *physical* cost to build the structure is similar — say **₫35 million/m²** (≈ \$1,350/m², a bit higher for a tower). But finished apartments sell for around **₫111 million/m²** (≈ \$4,290/m²) per 2025 HCMC figures. The gap is **₫76 million/m²** (≈ \$2,930/m²) — and most of that is *not* the cost of bricks. It's the cost of *land made artificially scarce by the impossibility of adding more of it*: no buildable parcels, fragmented ownership, height limits, slow approvals. For a 70 m² central apartment, that regulatory-and-scarcity wedge is about **₫5.3 billion** (≈ \$205,000) of the price — value that exists purely because the city can't build more. *The "expensive" part of an expensive city is mostly the price of the homes that were never allowed to exist.*

## Elastic markets: more building, milder price swings, glut risk

Now let's live inside an elastic market and watch the whole cycle play out. Elastic cities — Houston, Phoenix, Dallas, Atlanta, much of the US Sun Belt, and HCMC's flat satellite fringe — share a profile: cheap flat land, fast or light permitting, weak NIMBY power, and a culture of building. When demand surges, they answer with construction. The figure below traces the elastic path on the left and previews the inelastic one we'll cover next.

The good news first. In an elastic market, a demand surge mostly becomes *homes*, not *prices*. That's wonderful for affordability: workers can move to Houston, and Houston builds the houses to hold them, so prices stay reachable even as the population booms. This is why fast-growing Sun Belt metros, despite enormous in-migration, kept price-to-income ratios far below the coastal cities. Supply elasticity is the quiet hero of affordability.

There's a wider economic payoff hiding in that, too. When a city can build, it can *absorb* people who want to move there for better jobs — and that mobility is one of the great engines of a growing economy. Economists have argued that the inability of inelastic superstar cities (San Francisco, New York) to build housing has cost national economies a meaningful slice of growth, because workers who could have been far more productive in those cities simply couldn't afford to move there. Elastic cities let labor flow to where it's most valuable; inelastic ones wall it out with price. So elasticity isn't only about your individual home's price path — it's about whether a city can grow at all, or whether it ossifies into an expensive enclave for those who already own. Houston's willingness to build is, in this sense, an economic asset as much as a housing one.

But elastic supply has a dark side, and it has a name: the **glut**. Because builders respond eagerly to high prices — and because building takes *years* (more on this lag in its own section) — elastic markets have a powerful tendency to *overshoot*. The price signal says "build!", thousands of builders break ground at once, and two to four years later a flood of new homes lands on the market — often *after* the demand that justified them has already cooled. Now supply *exceeds* demand. Prices, which barely rose on the way up (because supply was elastic), can fall *outright* on the way down (because there are too many homes). The elastic market's signature outcome is **boom, then build, then glut, then bust**.

This is exactly what happened to the Sun Belt in 2008. Las Vegas, Phoenix, and the Florida and California exurbs built furiously into the mid-2000s mania. When demand collapsed, they were left with vast inventories of empty homes, and their prices fell *more* than the inelastic coastal cores — Las Vegas home prices fell roughly 60% peak-to-trough, against a national average near 27%. Elasticity that had kept prices tame on the way up turned into a glut that crushed them on the way down.

#### Worked example: the đất nền glut math — units delivered vs absorption

Let's quantify a glut with Minh's fringe option, the **đất nền** (literally "foundation land" — raw or lightly serviced land plots sold for future building, the speculative workhorse of Vietnamese property). Say a fast-growing satellite district near HCMC has genuine demand for about **2,000** new building plots per year — that's the **absorption rate**, the number of plots real end-users actually buy and use annually.

A demand surge hits in Year 0: prices jump, speculation ignites. Developers, seeing fat margins on cheap rezoned farmland, rush to subdivide. Over the next two years they bring **9,000** new plots to market — far more than the 2,000-per-year the area can absorb. Do the math: at 2,000 plots absorbed per year, 9,000 plots is **4.5 years** of demand delivered in two. The market is now drowning in supply.

The result is a **glut of 5,000 unabsorbed plots** (9,000 delivered minus ~4,000 absorbed over the two years). With thousands of empty lots and speculators needing to sell — especially once credit tightens, as it did in Vietnam's 2022–2023 bond crunch — prices fall hard. Fringe đất nền in several HCMC satellite provinces fell on the order of **30–50%** from their 2021–2022 peaks. The plots that traded at, say, **₫15 million/m²** (≈ \$580/m²) at the top changed hands at **₫8–10 million/m²** (≈ \$310–390/m²) in the bust, when they traded at all. *Elastic supply plus a multi-year building lag is a glut machine: the homes arrive on a schedule set years ago, not by the demand that exists when they land.*

## Inelastic markets: price-only response, affordability crisis, fewer gluts

Now cross to the other pole. Inelastic cities — San Francisco, Los Angeles, New York, London, Hong Kong, and the locked-down central cores of Hanoi and HCMC — answer a demand surge in the opposite way: with **price**, almost entirely. The supply curve is so steep that the new equilibrium slides straight up. Few new homes appear; existing homes simply cost more.

The defining feature of an inelastic boom is its *violence*. Because price is doing 100% of the adjustment that an elastic city would split between price and quantity, the price moves are far larger. Coastal California's home prices roughly tripled from the late 1990s to 2006, fell in the crash, and then more than recovered — central San Francisco homes are among the most expensive on Earth, with median prices that put a starter home out of reach of a household earning several times the median income. The boom isn't just bigger; it *ratchets*. Inelastic supply that prevents overbuilding also prevents the glut that would otherwise pull prices back down. So instead of a crash, you get a *permanent step up* in the price level — what the data chart later in this post shows as a line that spikes and then plateaus rather than spiking and crashing.

That permanent step is the **affordability crisis**. When supply can't grow, every additional dollar of demand becomes a higher price, and prices march away from local incomes year after year. The honest way to measure this is the **price-to-income ratio** — the number of years of the median household's *entire* income it would take to buy the median home. A ratio of 3–5 is considered affordable; above 10 is severely unaffordable. By this measure, the inelastic cities are off the charts: Hong Kong around **44**, Beijing around **35**, HCMC around **32** (one global survey put it at "34 years' income," fifth-highest in the world), Hanoi around **31** — against a global median near 15 and US cities like New York around 10. These aren't poor cities; they're *inelastic* ones. Their residents earn good money and still can't buy, because the homes that would have made buying possible were never built.

There is one mercy in inelasticity: **fewer gluts and shallower busts**. Because the inelastic city never overbuilds, it rarely faces a wall of empty new units. When the 2008 demand collapse came, coastal cores fell — but they fell *less* than the overbuilt Sun Belt, and they recovered faster and higher. The inelastic market trades the elastic market's crash risk for a slow, relentless grind upward in prices. Neither is "better" in the abstract; they're different distributions of pain. The elastic city risks your equity in a bust; the inelastic city risks your ability to ever buy at all.

#### Worked example: affordability as a function of elasticity

Let's tie affordability directly to elasticity with Minh. Suppose the median HCMC household earns **₫360 million per year** (≈ \$13,900) — round numbers for clarity. And suppose a standard 70 m² apartment is the target home.

On the **elastic fringe**, that apartment costs about **₫28 million/m² × 70 = ₫1.96 billion** (≈ \$76,000). Price-to-income: ₫1.96bn ÷ ₫360m ≈ **5.4 years**. Stretchy, but reachable on a long mortgage. Supply elasticity kept the fringe within reach.

In the **inelastic center**, the same 70 m² costs about **₫111 million/m² × 70 = ₫7.77 billion** (≈ \$300,000). Price-to-income: ₫7.77bn ÷ ₫360m ≈ **21.6 years** for that specific flat — and the citywide ratio, blending central and fringe and counting larger homes, lands around the ~32 figure surveys report. Same household, same city, same year: a fringe home costs about 5 years of income; a central home costs four times that. *The difference isn't that central buyers earn less — it's that central supply can't grow, so its price ran away from income while the fringe's didn't.*

## The same shock, two cities: the core comparison

Everything so far converges on one comparison, so let's make it head-to-head. Before we run the demand shock through time, it's worth laying the two market types side by side on the four attributes that actually matter to a buyer or an investor — price volatility, glut risk, affordability, and the typical end outcome. The figure below is that scorecard, with real exemplar cities in each row: Houston, Phoenix, and the HCMC fringe on the elastic side; San Francisco and central Hanoi/HCMC on the inelastic side.

![A comparison matrix of elastic cities versus inelastic cities across price volatility glut risk affordability and typical outcome showing elastic markets have lower volatility but higher glut risk while inelastic markets have high volatility and collapsing affordability](/imgs/blogs/supply-elasticity-why-some-cities-boom-and-bust-3.png)

The scorecard captures the whole trade in four columns. **Price volatility** is low-to-medium in elastic cities (prices grind up gently, with small swings) and high in inelastic ones (violent, jumpy booms). **Glut risk** flips entirely: elastic cities overbuild and face real glut-and-bust danger, while inelastic cities rarely overbuild and so rarely glut. **Affordability** stays within reach where supply keeps pace and collapses where it can't — the price-to-income ratio is modest in Houston and brutal in San Francisco and central HCMC. And the **typical outcome** is the punchline: elastic markets boom and then bust on the glut; inelastic markets grind up forever into permanent unaffordability. Notice that *neither column is uniformly "good."* The elastic city hands you affordability but threatens your equity in a bust; the inelastic city protects you from a crash but prices out the next generation. That tension — affordability versus stability — is the deep trade that elasticity governs, and no city gets to escape it entirely.

With the static scorecard in hand, now run the *identical* demand shock — the same +20% surge in people wanting to buy, driven by the same falling rates and the same optimism — and send it into an elastic city and an inelastic one. Track what happens to price over the next eight years. The illustrative chart below does exactly this.

![A line chart of a home price index over eight years showing the inelastic market spiking about fifty percent and staying high while the elastic market rises about ten percent then dips below the start as a glut arrives](/imgs/blogs/supply-elasticity-why-some-cities-boom-and-bust-4.png)

Read the two lines carefully, because they are the thesis of the post drawn out over time. Both start at an index of 100 in Year 0. In Year 1, the same +20% demand shock hits both (the amber line).

The **inelastic** market (red) does what inelastic markets do: with almost no new supply possible, price absorbs the entire shock. It climbs steeply — to roughly +35% by Year 2, +50% by Year 4 — and then it *stays there*. There's no glut to pull it back, because the city never overbuilt. The new high price level is permanent. This is the coastal-US, central-HCMC pattern: a violent boom that ratchets into a new, higher, more unaffordable plateau.

The **elastic** market (blue) does the opposite. Price rises only modestly at first — to about +10% — because most of the demand is becoming *new homes* rather than higher prices. But then the construction lag bites. The building that high prices triggered in Years 1–2 finishes in Years 3–5, and a *flood* of new supply lands just as the original demand surge has faded. Now there are too many homes. Price doesn't just give back its gains — it falls *below* the starting point, to roughly −7% in Year 6, before slowly recovering toward 100 as the glut gets absorbed. This is the Sun Belt, đất nền-fringe pattern: a mild boom, a building binge, and then a genuine bust.

Stand back and the comparison is almost unfair in how clean it is. Same demand. Same eight years. One city ends up 50% more expensive and permanently unaffordable; the other ends up roughly where it started, having ridden a boom-build-glut-bust round trip. The *only* thing that differed was the slope of the supply curve.

#### Worked example: Dana's two rentals, a decade apart

Dana bought two rental houses in 2014, each for **\$300,000**, with the same thesis: growing cities, rising rents. One was in Phoenix (elastic); one was in the Bay Area (inelastic). A decade later, the demand shock of 2015–2022 (tech wages, then pandemic rates and migration) had hit both.

Her **Phoenix** house rode the elastic path. Prices rose through the late 2010s and spiked in 2021 — Phoenix was one of the hottest pandemic markets — to about **\$480,000**. But Phoenix also *built*: tens of thousands of new homes came online, and as rates rose and migration cooled in 2022–2023, the market softened and her house slipped back to about **\$430,000**. Solid gain, but with a visible give-back, and the rent stopped rising as new apartments competed.

Her **Bay Area** house rode the inelastic path. It couldn't build neighbors — the city wouldn't allow it and the geography wouldn't permit it — so the same waves of demand became pure price. It climbed to about **\$560,000** by 2018, dipped briefly, then pushed to about **\$620,000** and largely *held* there, because no glut ever arrived to knock it down. Her rent ratcheted up the whole way, since no new supply ever competed.

Same purchase price, same year, same investor thesis. The Phoenix house gained ~43% with a bumpy give-back; the Bay Area house gained ~107% and held. *Dana didn't pick better in the Bay — she picked a market where the city's inability to build did the work that her own judgment couldn't.*

## Vietnam: elastic đất nền fringe vs inelastic central core

Vietnam is a perfect natural laboratory for elasticity, because a single metro like HCMC contains *both* poles inside its own borders. Understanding this resolves a lot of confusion about Vietnamese property — why the fringe crashes while the center grinds, why some land "always goes up" and other land burns speculators. The figure below puts the two Vietnamese markets side by side.

![Two panels contrasting the elastic đất nền fringe which overbuilds into a glut and price bust against inelastic central Ho Chi Minh City which responds with price only and no glut](/imgs/blogs/supply-elasticity-why-some-cities-boom-and-bust-5.png)

### The elastic fringe: đất nền and the satellite provinces

Out past the city's edge — in Long An, Đồng Nai, Bình Dương, the rural districts of HCMC itself — land is flat, plentiful, and (until recently) cheaply convertible from agriculture. This is **đất nền** country, and it's about as elastic as real estate gets. When a demand surge hits — a new highway announced, a ring road, a wave of speculative money — developers and individual speculators subdivide farmland into plots and sell them *fast*. Supply explodes. And then, on cue, supply *overshoots*, exactly as the elastic model predicts.

Vietnam has lived through this repeatedly. The 2018 đất nền fever sent satellite-province plot prices up multiples in a year; the 2022–2023 freeze — triggered by the corporate-bond crunch after the Tân Hoàng Minh and Vạn Thịnh Phát arrests, which choked developer credit — turned the boom into a textbook glut-and-bust. Plots in many fringe districts fell 30–50%, and lots that had changed hands three times in a year suddenly had no buyers at all. This is the elastic signature: easy to build, easy to overbuild, brutal on the way down.

### The inelastic core: District 1 and the central wards

Now go to the center — District 1, District 3, the Old Quarter of Hanoi. Here, land is *finished*. There are no empty parcels to subdivide. Ownership is fragmented into thousands of individually held tube-houses, making assembly nearly impossible. Approvals for tall central towers are slow and political. Geography and history have made central supply almost perfectly inelastic. So when the same waves of money that flood the fringe reach the center, they have nowhere to go but *price*. Central HCMC primary apartment prices climbed from roughly ₫91 million/m² in 2024 to about ₫111 million/m² (≈ \$4,290/m²) in 2025 — and through every fringe bust, the center barely flinched. It doesn't glut, because it can't overbuild. It just grinds up, and the city-wide price-to-income ratio sits near 32 — among the highest on Earth — as a direct result.

#### Worked example: a metro line making fringe supply elastic — and capping prices

Here's where the two halves connect, and where policy enters. The single most important thing that *converts* useless far land into elastic housing supply is **infrastructure** — above all, a metro line. HCMC's Metro Line 1 (Bến Thành–Suối Tiên), which opened in late 2024 after years of delay, is the live example.

Before the metro, land 15 km east of central HCMC was cheap precisely because it was inaccessible — a 90-minute crawl through traffic. Call it **₫20 million/m²** (≈ \$770/m²). It was *land*, but it wasn't really *housing supply*, because nobody wanted to commute from it. The metro changes the equation: now that land is a 30-minute train ride from the center. Two things happen at once. First, demand for it jumps — it's suddenly livable — which by itself would push prices up. Land near the new stations did indeed re-rate sharply, some doubling in anticipation.

But here's the elasticity twist that's easy to miss: the metro doesn't just raise demand for that land, it *adds an enormous amount of newly usable supply*. Thousands of hectares that were economically dead become buildable for commuters all at once. If the city *also* allows dense building around the stations (transit-oriented development), that new elastic supply *caps* how high prices can run — the demand surge becomes apartments along the line rather than pure price spikes downtown. The metro is thus a double-edged elasticity lever: it can let prices spike at the stations (a one-time re-rating) *and*, if paired with upzoning, flatten the longer-run supply curve so the whole eastern corridor stays more affordable than the locked central core. *Infrastructure is how a city manufactures elasticity where geography didn't provide it — but only if the zoning lets the new supply actually get built.*

## The policy lever: making supply more elastic

Here is the most important and most hopeful idea in this entire subject: **elasticity is largely a policy choice.** Geography you can't repeal. But zoning, approvals, NIMBY veto power, minimum lot sizes, parking mandates, land-assembly rules, and infrastructure are all things a city *decides*. An inelastic city is, for the most part, a city that has *chosen* to make building hard. Which means it can choose otherwise. The figure below lays out the toolkit.

![A grid of policy levers including upzoning by-right permits metro and infrastructure cutting minimum lot and parking rules and land assembly reform all pointing at the goal of flattening the supply curve](/imgs/blogs/supply-elasticity-why-some-cities-boom-and-bust-7.png)

The levers, each aimed at the same target — letting more homes get built faster on the same land:

- **Upzone.** Legalize density. Allow apartments, townhouses, and mid-rises where only single houses were permitted. This is the single most powerful lever, because it converts inelastic single-family plots into elastic multi-unit ones at the stroke of a pen. When New Zealand's cities and several US states (California, Oregon) ended single-family-only zoning, the immediate effect was a jump in permitted units on the same land.
- **By-right (as-of-right) permitting.** If a proposed building meets the written code, approve it *automatically* — no discretionary hearings, no case-by-case veto. This strips out the years of delay and the NIMBY ambush that make supply inelastic. Tokyo's relatively by-right, nationally-set zoning is a big reason it keeps building and stays affordable despite being one of the largest cities on Earth.
- **Build infrastructure and transit.** As HCMC's Metro Line 1 showed, rail and roads convert dead far-land into usable, elastic supply. Pair the line with dense zoning around stations and you've created elasticity where there was none.
- **Cut minimums.** Shrink minimum lot sizes, eliminate or reduce parking mandates, relax setbacks and floor-area limits. Each of these silently caps how many homes a plot can hold; relaxing them lets cheaper, smaller, denser homes legally exist.
- **Reform land assembly.** Create clear, fair rules to combine fragmented parcels so a single holdout can't kill a project — while guarding against the abuse of forcing people off their land. This is the hardest and most fraught lever, especially in Vietnam where land rights are sensitive, but fragmented ownership is a real brake on central redevelopment.

The unifying logic: every one of these flattens the supply curve, so the *next* demand surge becomes more homes instead of higher prices. A city that does these things doesn't stop demand shocks — it changes what they turn into. That's the deepest practical takeaway of the whole topic: you cannot easily control how much people want to live in your city, but you *can* control whether their wanting becomes housing or becomes an affordability crisis.

## The construction lag: why elastic markets overshoot

We've referenced the construction lag repeatedly; it deserves its own treatment, because it's the mechanism that turns an elastic market's *virtue* (it builds) into its *vice* (it gluts). The figure below traces the lag across a single cycle.

![A timeline showing a demand surge at year zero triggering permits and groundbreaking at year one construction through years two and three a flood of completions at years three to four and a price bust at year five](/imgs/blogs/supply-elasticity-why-some-cities-boom-and-bust-6.png)

The **construction lag** is the gap between the moment builders *decide* to build and the moment the finished homes actually hit the market. For a single house it might be a year; for a large apartment tower or a master-planned subdivision, two to four years is typical — land acquisition, permitting, financing, then the build itself. This lag is the source of housing's most stubborn instability.

Walk the timeline. In Year 0, demand surges and prices spike — the "go" signal. Builders, responding rationally to high prices, break ground in Year 1. But the homes aren't finished. Through Years 2–3, construction grinds on while demand, quietly, *cools* — the original buyers got satisfied, rates rose, the migration wave passed. Then, in Years 3–4, all those projects that started together *finish* together, and a flood of completions lands on a market that no longer needs them. Supply exceeds demand. Prices, in Year 5, fall on the glut. The very elasticity that let the market respond is what let it *overrespond*.

This is why elastic markets are *cyclical* in a way inelastic ones aren't. An inelastic city can't overbuild, so it can't glut, so its prices don't crash — they grind. An elastic city builds enthusiastically on a lag, so it perpetually risks delivering yesterday's demand into tomorrow's slump. The lag is also why housing busts cluster: when *every* builder reads the same price signal and breaks ground at the same time, they all deliver at the same time, and the glut is synchronized. Vietnam's 2018 đất nền projects delivering into the 2022–2023 credit freeze is exactly this pattern; the US Sun Belt's mid-2000s projects delivering into the 2008 collapse is the same pattern at national scale.

#### Worked example: how the lag converts a boom into a bust

Let's quantify the lag's damage. Suppose Minh's fringe district has steady demand for **2,000** homes a year and starts in balance at **₫1.5 billion** per home (≈ \$58,000). A demand shock in Year 0 lifts the *desired* purchases to **3,000** a year and pushes prices up ~20% to **₫1.8 billion** (≈ \$70,000). Builders, seeing the higher price, plan for the new demand and start enough projects to deliver **3,000+ homes a year**.

But the projects take three years. By the time they deliver in Year 3, two things have changed. First, demand has reverted toward its old **2,000/year** (the shock faded). Second, the building decisions made in Years 0–1 are still arriving — and because lots of builders piled in, completions overshoot to **3,500/year**. So the market now faces **3,500 homes delivered into 2,000 of demand** — a surplus of 1,500 homes a year piling up. Prices don't hold at ₫1.8bn; they fall *through* the original ₫1.5bn to around **₫1.2 billion** (≈ \$46,000) as the glut clears. A buyer who paid ₫1.8bn at the Year-0 peak is down a third. *The lag means builders are always answering a question the market asked years ago — and in an elastic market, that delayed answer is a glut.*

## Common misconceptions

Elasticity is counterintuitive, and the confusion shows up as a handful of durable myths. Each is wrong in an instructive way.

**"Building never lowers prices — new homes are always luxury and expensive."** This is the most common and most damaging myth. It confuses the *price of the new units* with the *effect on the overall market*. New homes are often built at the top of the market (that's where the margins are), but adding them still loosens the whole market through **filtering** — the process by which households move *up* into the new homes, vacating their old ones, which are then taken by households a rung down, and so on down the ladder. Every new luxury unit ultimately frees a cheaper unit somewhere. The hard evidence is unambiguous: across many studies, metros that build more have *lower* real price growth, and even the arrival of market-rate buildings has been shown to *reduce* rents in the surrounding blocks. The myth survives because the causal chain is invisible — you see the shiny expensive new tower, you don't see the older apartment it freed up across town.

**"We're out of land — that's why prices are high."** Almost always false, and it's the alibi that inelasticity hides behind. Genuinely land-constrained cities (Hong Kong, Singapore, the tip of San Francisco) exist, but the *overwhelming* cause of high prices in expensive cities is regulatory inelasticity, not physical scarcity. Tokyo is enormous, dense, and comparatively affordable because it *builds*. Most "out of land" cities have abundant land they've made it illegal to use densely — single-family zones, height caps, growth boundaries. "Out of land" usually means "out of *permitted* land," which is a choice, not a fact of geography.

**"Demand alone sets prices."** The whole post is the refutation. Demand sets *how much pressure* there is; elasticity decides whether that pressure becomes quantity or price. The same demand made Houston build and made San Francisco unaffordable. If you analyze a market by demand alone — "lots of people want to live here, so prices will rise" — you'll be right about the inelastic city and badly wrong about the elastic one, where that same demand becomes homes and even gluts.

**"Property in big cities never goes down."** True of *inelastic* cores, dangerously false on the *elastic* fringe — and people who learned the rule on one apply it to the other. Central District 1 land grinding ever upward teaches Vietnamese investors that "land always wins." Then they buy fringe đất nền with the same conviction and get wiped out 40% when the glut hits, because the fringe is elastic and the center is not. "Never goes down" is a statement about elasticity, not about real estate in general.

**"Rent control or buyer subsidies will fix affordability."** Both treat the symptom (high prices) while ignoring the cause (inelastic supply). A buyer subsidy in an inelastic market is *demand* — it shifts the demand curve right, and against a steep supply curve that becomes higher prices, partly capitalizing the subsidy into the seller's pocket. The only durable fix for an affordability crisis caused by inelastic supply is to make supply more elastic. Demand-side fixes in inelastic markets tend to feed the very prices they're meant to relieve.

**"A glut means the market was irrational."** Gluts aren't irrationality — they're the *rational* response of many builders to a real price signal, colliding with the construction lag. Each builder who broke ground in the boom was making a sensible individual bet on the prices they saw. The glut is **emergent**: it comes from everyone responding correctly at the same time and all delivering at the same time. Understanding this keeps you from the lazy "they were all crazy" story and points you at the real culprit — elastic supply plus a multi-year lag.

## How it shows up in real markets

Enough theory — here is elasticity at work in real, datable episodes. Each is the same model in different clothing.

**US Sun Belt vs coastal cores in 2008.** The cleanest natural experiment in modern housing history. The same national demand shock — subprime credit, the everyone-can-buy mania — hit elastic and inelastic metros alike. The elastic Sun Belt (Las Vegas, Phoenix, the Florida and Inland Empire exurbs) built furiously, then gutted on the glut: Las Vegas fell roughly 60% peak-to-trough, Phoenix and parts of Florida 50%+, against a national Case-Shiller decline near 27% (peak 184.6 in mid-2006 to a trough of 134 in early 2012). The inelastic coastal cores (San Francisco, coastal LA, New York) fell *less* and recovered *higher* — the national index has since more than doubled to ~330 by early 2026, but the inelastic metros lead that, because they never overbuilt and their scarcity reasserted itself. Same shock, opposite cycles, all explained by elasticity.

**HCMC fringe đất nền bust vs the central grind, 2018–2024.** Vietnam's own version, inside one metro. The 2018 đất nền fever and the 2020–2021 cheap-credit surge pushed satellite-province plot prices up multiples. When the 2022–2023 corporate-bond crunch (Tân Hoàng Minh, April 2022; Vạn Thịnh Phát/SCB, October 2022) froze developer credit and SBV policy tightened before its four 2023 cuts, the elastic fringe gutted — plots down 30–50%, projects stalled, speculators trapped. Meanwhile central HCMC apartment prices barely paused and then resumed their grind, from ~₫91 million/m² (2024) toward ~₫111 million/m² (2025). The fringe is elastic and busted; the center is inelastic and ground up. A Vietnamese investor who watched both and *only* learned "the fringe is dangerous" missed the real lesson: it's not the fringe, it's the *elasticity*.

**Houston's permanent affordability through a population boom.** Houston is the developed world's most famous elastic city — flat Texas land, famously *no zoning code* at all (it relies on private deed restrictions and by-right permitting). It absorbed enormous population growth over decades and stayed strikingly affordable, with price-to-income ratios a fraction of coastal California's, precisely because it builds whatever demand asks for. Houston pays for this with sprawl and the occasional localized oversupply, but its residents can buy homes — the elastic bargain in its purest form.

**Tokyo: a huge city that stayed affordable by building.** Tokyo metro houses ~37 million people, yet its real home prices have been roughly flat for decades and its price-to-income ratio (~15) sits below most global megacities. The reason is institutional elasticity: zoning is set nationally and is largely by-right, density is permitted, and the city builds hundreds of thousands of units a year. Tokyo is the standing refutation of "big global cities must be unaffordable" — they must only if they choose to be inelastic.

**Singapore: inelastic geography, solved by the state.** Singapore is genuinely land-constrained — a small island — which should make it San-Francisco-unaffordable. Yet its price-to-income (~17) is far below Hong Kong's (~44) despite both being islands. The difference is that the Singaporean state acted as an enormous elastic supplier: the Housing Development Board built and built, housing the great majority of the population in public flats. Singapore manufactured elasticity through state construction where the market and geography wouldn't provide it — a different lever, same principle.

**Hong Kong: the inelastic extreme.** The other island, the opposite policy. Mountainous geography, a tight government land-release policy that deliberately drip-feeds buildable land, and a result that is the world's least affordable major housing market — price-to-income around 44, "nano-flats" of a few square meters, and a permanent affordability crisis. Hong Kong is the inelastic model pushed to its limit: a city that answers every demand surge with price because it has chosen, and been forced by geography, to never let supply respond.

## When this matters / Further reading

Supply elasticity is the variable that decides what *kind* of real-estate market you're actually in — and it touches real decisions. If you're **Minh**, choosing between a central HCMC apartment and a fringe land plot, you're really choosing between an inelastic asset that will grind up and keep getting less affordable, and an elastic one that can boom but can also glut and crash 40%. Knowing which is which is the difference between "land always wins" and a wipeout. If you're **Dana**, comparing two cities for a rental, elasticity tells you whether your downside is a price crash (elastic) or your upside is a permanent ratchet (inelastic) — and whether new supply will compete away your rent. If you're a *citizen* of an expensive city, elasticity tells you that your affordability crisis is mostly a policy choice your city made, and could unmake.

The deeper point is that elasticity sets the *amplitude and shape* of the entire cycle. It decides whether a boom ends in a glut-driven bust or a permanent unaffordable plateau. It decides whether building "works" to lower prices (it does, through filtering) or whether you're stuck with demand-side band-aids that feed the fire. And it sits underneath almost every other real-estate phenomenon — the cycle, the land-versus-building split, the rent-versus-buy math — as the hidden parameter that tunes them all.
