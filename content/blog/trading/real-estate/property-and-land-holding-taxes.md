> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — An annual tax on the *value of land* is, to economists, about as close to a perfect tax as exists. Land can't flee a high-tax country, its supply is fixed (so taxing it doesn't make less of it exist — no waste), and it captures the unearned windfall that public investment, like a new metro line, hands to whoever happens to own nearby. The pure version is the **land-value tax (LVT)**; the common, slightly worse cousin is the ordinary **property tax** on land *and* building.
>
> - A *land-value tax* falls only on the scarce site; a *property tax* also falls on the building, which mildly penalises improving and developing. The land tax is the ideal; the property tax is the workable compromise.
> - Because the supply of land is perfectly fixed, a tax on it causes **no deadweight loss** — it doesn't shrink how much land exists, unlike a tax on almost anything else. That single fact is why economists from Adam Smith to Milton Friedman called it "the least bad tax".
> - A holding tax **capitalises** into a *lower purchase price*: announce a permanent 1% annual tax and a buyer pays roughly the present value of that stream less for the property. The seller who owns it on announcement day eats the one-time hit.
> - The one number to remember: the US funds towns and schools with a property tax averaging **~1.1% of market value every year**; Vietnam's annual holding tax is **~0.03–0.15% of a deliberately low state price** — effectively zero. That gap explains a great deal about land-banking and speculation in each country.

In 2025 our recurring HCMC buyer, **Minh**, paid ₫16 billion (≈ \$618,000) for a narrow but central plot in Ho Chi Minh City — land he had no immediate plan to build on. He simply intended to *hold* it. Maybe he would build a townhouse in five years; maybe he would flip it; maybe he would just let it sit behind a sheet-metal fence and watch the price climb. Here is the striking part: from the day he signed to the day he eventually sells, the Vietnamese state will ask Minh for almost *nothing* for the privilege of owning that prime urban land. The annual non-agricultural land-use tax on it runs to a tiny fraction of a deliberately low official price — a few hundred thousand đồng a year, the cost of a couple of lunches. The land could double in value while contributing essentially zero to the city that made it valuable.

Now meet **Dana**, a US investor who buys a \$300,000 house in a typical American suburb. Every single year, like clockwork, her county sends a bill for roughly **\$3,300** — about 1.1% of the home's value — whether she lives in it, rents it out, or leaves it empty. Over ten years that is \$33,000, more than 10% of what she paid, handed to the local government to run the schools, the fire department, and the roads. If Dana tried Minh's strategy — buying land and sitting on it, doing nothing — that annual bill would bleed her every year she failed to put it to use.

Same asset class. Same human impulse to own land and watch it appreciate. Wildly different annual cost of *holding*. And that difference — the **holding tax** — is one of the most powerful and least understood forces in all of real estate. It is the tax that economists across two centuries and every ideological camp have praised as nearly perfect, and the tax that politicians, almost everywhere, are terrified to impose. This post is about why both of those things are true at once.

![Two stacked columns comparing a property tax that taxes both land and the building against a land-value tax that taxes only the land underneath](/imgs/blogs/property-and-land-holding-taxes-1.png)

The figure above is the mental model for the whole post. There are two flavours of holding tax. The ordinary **property tax** falls on the *land plus the structure* sitting on it. The purer **land-value tax** falls on the *land alone* and leaves your building untaxed — so improving your property never raises your tax bill. We are going to build both from zero: define what an annual holding tax even is, show with a supply-and-demand picture why taxing land is uniquely harmless, watch a new tax *capitalise* into a lower price, see how a metro line hands landowners a windfall the tax can claw back, and finally confront why a tax economists adore is one almost no politician will touch — with Minh and Dana carrying the numbers the whole way.

This is educational, not financial advice. The figures Minh and Dana use are rounded so you can do them in your head; the mechanisms and the policy facts are real and dated.

## Foundations: ad valorem taxes, property tax vs land tax, and the words you need

Before we can argue about whether a land tax is "perfect", we need a precise vocabulary. Real estate taxation is drowning in jargon, almost all of which is simpler than it sounds. Let's define every term from the ground up.

### Transaction taxes vs holding taxes — the crucial split

There are two completely different moments at which the state can tax property, and confusing them is the single most common mistake beginners make.

- A **transaction tax** is charged *once, when the property changes hands*. Examples: the **transfer tax** or **personal income tax on a sale** (in Vietnam, 2% of the declared sale price), the **registration fee** (*lệ phí trước bạ*, 0.5% in Vietnam), stamp duty in the UK, the closing costs in the US. You pay it at the moment of buying or selling, and then you are done.
- A **holding tax** (also called a *recurrent* or *annual* property tax) is charged *every year, simply for owning the asset* — regardless of whether you ever sell it. This is the one Dana pays \$3,300 of each year. It is a *flow*: a cost that recurs as long as you hold.

This post is about **holding taxes**. The distinction matters enormously because the two do completely different jobs. A transaction tax discourages *trading* (and, perversely, can lock people into homes they would otherwise sell). A holding tax discourages *idle ownership* — it makes sitting on an unused asset expensive year after year. If you want to understand the full landscape of every tax that touches a property, the companion piece [how real estate is taxed: the full map](/blog/trading/real-estate/how-real-estate-is-taxed-the-full-map) lays out all of them; here we zoom into the annual holding tax specifically, because it is the one with the deepest economics behind it.

### Ad valorem: a tax on value

Almost every holding tax is an **ad valorem** tax. The Latin means "according to value" — the tax is a *percentage of the asset's value*, not a flat fee. A 1% ad valorem property tax on a \$300,000 home is \$3,000; on a \$600,000 home it is \$6,000. The bill scales with what you own.

The opposite would be a *specific* or *unit* tax — a fixed amount per square metre, say, regardless of how valuable that square metre is. Ad valorem is far more common for property because it tracks ability to pay: a more valuable home implies a wealthier owner and a more valuable location. The catch, which we will return to, is that ad valorem taxes require somebody to *estimate the value* of every property every so often — and that estimation is where most of the political and practical pain lives.

### Property tax vs land-value tax

Here is the distinction the cover figure draws, and the spine of this entire post. Recall from [land vs buildings](/blog/trading/real-estate/land-vs-buildings-two-assets-in-every-property) that every property is really *two* assets fused into one price: the **land** (the scarce, immovable site) and the **improvements** (the reproducible building on top). A holding tax can be levied on either or both:

- A **property tax** taxes the *combined* value — land **plus** building. This is what almost every country that has a holding tax actually uses, including the US. Build a bigger, better house and your assessed value rises, so your tax rises. Tax base = land value + structure value.
- A **land-value tax (LVT)** taxes *only the land value*, and ignores the building entirely. Build a mansion or leave the lot empty — your LVT bill is identical, because it depends only on the value of the dirt and its location, not on what you put on it. Tax base = land value alone.

That one difference — does the building get taxed or not — drives almost everything that follows. The LVT is the version economists call nearly perfect; the property tax is the workable, slightly-worse compromise that real governments actually run. We will spend a section on each.

### Assessed value, cadastral value, and the assessment ratio

To charge an ad valorem tax you need a *number* — an official estimate of each property's value. This is the **assessed value** (US) or **cadastral value** (the term used across much of Europe and, loosely, in the Vietnamese *bảng giá đất*, the official land-price table). It is produced by a government **assessor** who estimates what the property is worth, usually from recent comparable sales.

Crucially, the assessed value is often *not* the full market value. Many systems apply an **assessment ratio** — a legal fraction of market value that is actually taxed. If a home's market value is \$300,000 and the assessment ratio is 80%, the assessed value is \$240,000, and the tax rate applies to *that*. In the US, assessment ratios vary by state but are often near 100% for residential property. In **Vietnam**, the official land-price table has historically sat *far below* true market value — sometimes 30–50% of it or less — which is one of the two reasons the Vietnamese holding tax is so trivial. (The other is the rock-bottom rate, coming up.) The **Land Law 2024**, effective August 2024, replaced the old five-year fixed framework price with *annual* land-price tables meant to track the market more closely — a quiet but important reform we will return to.

### Millage and the effective rate

The tax rate on property is often quoted in an old unit called **mills**. One **mill** is one-thousandth — so a rate of "10 mills" means \$10 of tax per \$1,000 of assessed value, i.e. 1.0%. American property-tax bills are full of millage rates set separately by the county, the city, the school district, and special districts, all stacked on top of each other.

What matters for comparison is the **effective rate**: the actual annual tax divided by the property's *market* value. This folds together the assessment ratio and the millage into one honest number. The US national average effective property-tax rate is about **1.1%** of market value, ranging from roughly **0.3%** in low-tax states like Hawaii and Alabama to about **2.2%** in high-tax states like New Jersey and Illinois (Tax Foundation / Census ACS, 2024–25). Vietnam's effective holding tax — the non-agricultural land-use tax measured against true market value — is on the order of **0.05%**, more than twenty times lower.

### Deadweight loss — the cost of a tax beyond the revenue

This is the most important economics word in the post, so let's build it carefully. When a government taxes something, it collects revenue — money moves from the taxpayer to the state. That transfer by itself is not a "loss" to society; it is just a reshuffle. The *loss* comes from something subtler: most taxes **change behaviour** in a way that destroys value that nobody captures.

Tax cigarettes and people smoke less; tax labour and some people work less; tax sodas and fewer get sold. The mutually-beneficial trades that *would have happened* but now don't — because the tax made them not worth it — are pure lost value. Nobody gets that value: not the buyer, not the seller, not the government. Economists call this evaporated surplus the **deadweight loss** of a tax. It is the efficiency cost, the "waste" a tax creates over and above the revenue it raises. The whole reason a land tax is special, as we are about to see, is that it has *no* deadweight loss.

### Tax incidence and capitalisation — who really pays

Two more terms, both about *who actually bears* a tax, which is rarely who writes the cheque.

- **Tax incidence** is the question of who *truly* pays a tax once prices adjust — the *economic* burden, not the *legal* one. A landlord may be the one legally liable for a property tax, but if they can raise the rent to cover it, the *tenant* bears part of the incidence. Working out incidence is about whether the cost can be passed on, which depends on supply and demand elasticities.
- **Capitalisation** is the specific way a *holding* tax shows up in an asset price. Because the tax recurs forever, a buyer treats it as a permanent reduction in the property's net income and **subtracts the present value of the whole future tax stream from the price they will pay**. So a new annual tax doesn't just cost future owners year by year — it instantly *lowers the price* of the asset, and the person holding it at announcement takes that hit once, up front. We will work this out with numbers; it is the most counter-intuitive and important mechanism in the post.

With the vocabulary in place, we can now make the big claim: a tax on land is almost uniquely harmless. Here is exactly why.

## Why a land tax is "almost perfect": the vertical supply curve

Ask an economist to name the least-bad tax and a startling number — across the entire political spectrum, from Adam Smith to Milton Friedman to Joseph Stiglitz — will say a tax on the *value of land*. The reason is a single picture: the supply curve for land is **vertical**.

![Two supply and demand panels showing that taxing land with a fixed vertical supply causes no deadweight loss while taxing an ordinary good cuts the quantity produced and creates a deadweight loss triangle](/imgs/blogs/property-and-land-holding-taxes-2.png)

The figure above is the entire argument in two panels. Let's read it from first principles.

### What a supply curve is, and why land's is vertical

A **supply curve** answers: at each possible price, how much of a thing will be produced and offered for sale? For almost every good, the curve **slopes upward** — a higher price calls forth more supply, because producers will make more shirts, grow more wheat, or pump more oil when the price is high enough to be worth it. That responsiveness is the good's **supply elasticity**: how much the quantity supplied changes when the price changes.

Now consider *land* — specifically, the raw quantity of land at a given location. No matter how high the price climbs, **no one can manufacture more of it**. The amount of land in central HCMC, or in Manhattan, is fixed by geography. Price it at \$1 or \$1 billion per square metre and the quantity in existence does not budge. The supply curve is a **vertical line**: supply is *perfectly inelastic*. (Subtleties — reclamation, upzoning that adds *usable floor area* — we'll honour shortly, but for the *quantity of land at a location*, the vertical line holds.) This is the same fixed-supply fact that drives the long, violent [real-estate cycle](/blog/trading/real-estate/why-real-estate-cycles-are-long-the-18-year-cycle); here we use it for tax.

### Why a vertical supply curve means no deadweight loss

Look at the *right* panel of the figure first, the ordinary good. The blue supply curve slopes up. Put a tax on it and the effective supply curve shifts up (the amber dashed line) — producers now need a higher price to cover the tax. The new equilibrium where supply-plus-tax meets demand sits at a **lower quantity** (5.4 units drops to 4.4). Those ~1 unit of trades that used to happen now don't, because the tax made them not worth doing. The red triangle is the **deadweight loss** — value that simply vanishes. Every normal tax does this.

Now look at the *left* panel, land. The supply curve is vertical at a fixed quantity (5 units). Slap a tax on the landowner and... the quantity of land *cannot change*. There are still exactly 5 units. No trades disappear because the supply was never going to move in the first place. The owner simply earns less net rent — the tax comes straight out of the land's return — but the *use* of the land, and the quantity of it, is completely unaffected. **There is no triangle. There is no deadweight loss.** The tax is a pure transfer from landowner to state with zero efficiency cost.

That is the whole magic. A tax distorts behaviour only when it changes the quantity of something. Land's quantity *can't* change, so a land tax distorts nothing. It is, in the technical sense economists mean, the closest thing to a free lunch in public finance: revenue with no waste.

#### Worked example: the land tax that changes nothing

Suppose a central plot earns its owner **₫100 million a year** in ground rent (the pure locational income, separate from any building) and the relevant return investors demand is **5%**. The land is worth its capitalised rent: ₫100M ÷ 0.05 = **₫2.0 billion**.

Now the government imposes an annual land tax equal to **₫40 million** — 40% of the rent, or 2% of the ₫2.0bn value. What happens to how the land is *used*? **Nothing.** The owner still wants to extract the maximum ₫100M of rent from the site; charging less would just mean less money to pay the tax with. The land is still there, still in its best use. The owner's *net* income falls from ₫100M to ₫60M, and at a 5% required return the land is now worth ₫60M ÷ 0.05 = **₫1.2 billion** — the tax burden got capitalised into the price. But the *amount of land*, the *use of land*, the *bread baked on it* — all unchanged.

*A land tax takes a slice of the landowner's rent without making a single useful thing harder to do — which is exactly what no other tax can claim.*

### The "single tax": Henry George's big idea

This insight is old. In 1879 the American economist **Henry George** published *Progress and Poverty*, one of the best-selling economics books of the nineteenth century, and built an entire movement around one proposal: abolish *all* other taxes and fund government from a **single tax** on the value of land. His argument was both efficiency (no deadweight loss, as above) and *justice*: land value, George insisted, is created by the *community* — by everyone choosing to live and work near each other, by public roads and schools — and not by the individual owner. So letting owners pocket rising land values while taxing workers' wages was, to George, backwards. Tax the unearned thing (land), free the earned things (labour, capital, buildings).

You do not have to buy the full Georgist program — almost no jurisdiction taxes land *only* — to accept the narrow point that nearly every economist accepts: **a tax on land value is unusually efficient, because the thing it taxes can't run away or shrink.** That is the bedrock the rest of the post stands on.

### The honest caveats

Three things keep the "perfect tax" from being literally perfect, and intellectual honesty demands stating them.

- **Land value is hard to measure separately from the building.** In the real world you observe the *combined* property price; splitting out the pure land value requires assessment skill and good comparable-sales data. Bad assessments mean unfair bills. This is a practical problem, not a theoretical one — but it is real.
- **At the margin, land *can* be created.** Land reclamation (Singapore, the Dutch polders, HCMC's Thu Thiem), and upzoning that allows taller buildings, both add *usable* land or floor area. The supply isn't *perfectly* vertical at every scale. But for the existing stock at a location, it is close enough that the no-deadweight-loss result holds in practice.
- **It can be a cash-flow squeeze for the asset-rich, cash-poor.** A retiree who owns a now-valuable home but has little income can struggle to pay a tax based on the land's market value. This is the single most politically explosive objection, and we will give it a full hearing in the politics section.

None of these overturns the core result. They shape *how* you implement a land or property tax well — not *whether* the economics favour it.

## Land-value capture: the windfall public investment hands to owners

Here is the second pillar of the case for holding taxes, and the one that connects most directly to Vietnam's frantic urban growth. It is the idea of **land-value capture**.

![Two before and after panels showing a publicly funded metro line lifting nearby land values, with no holding tax leaving the whole windfall private and a land tax letting the public recapture part of the gain](/imgs/blogs/property-and-land-holding-taxes-6.png)

The figure above tells the story. Recall the central fact about land from [land vs buildings](/blog/trading/real-estate/land-vs-buildings-two-assets-in-every-property): a parcel's value comes almost entirely from its *location*, and location value is created by *everyone around it* — not by the owner. When the city builds a metro station, opens a new park, improves the schools, or rezones a district for high-rises, the land nearby becomes more valuable, and the owner did *nothing* to earn it. Economists call this an **unearned increment** — a windfall handed to whoever happens to hold the land.

### The metro line and the windfall

Picture a new metro line in HCMC — say the long-awaited Line 1 to the eastern districts. Before the line, a plot 300 metres from a future station is ordinary suburban land. The moment the line opens (often the moment it is *credibly announced*), that plot's value jumps — 20%, 30%, sometimes far more — because being a short walk from rapid transit is worth a great deal. The plot's owner just got richer by tens of percent, purely because the *public* spent billions of public money on a railway.

Without a holding tax, that entire windfall stays **private** (the left panel). The public paid for the metro; the landowner pockets the gain. With even a modest **land tax**, the right panel happens: as the land's assessed value rises with the metro, the annual tax on it rises too, and the public **recaptures part of the windfall** it created — money that can help pay for the very metro line that generated the gain. This is **land-value capture**: using the tax system to claw back, for the community, some of the land value the community itself created.

#### Worked example: the metro windfall and the tax that captures it

Dana, scouting overseas, buys a \$200,000 plot near a planned light-rail station in a US suburb. The line opens; comparable plots within walking distance jump **30%**, so her plot is now worth **\$260,000** — a **\$60,000** windfall she did nothing to earn.

Her local property tax is **1.1%** of value. Before the line, her annual bill was \$200,000 × 1.1% = **\$2,200**. After the reassessment, it is \$260,000 × 1.1% = **\$2,860** — an extra **\$660 a year**. Over the next 30 years, that incremental tax (ignoring further appreciation and discounting, to keep it simple) sums to about **\$19,800** flowing back to the public purse — roughly a third of the \$60,000 windfall recaptured over a generation. Dana still keeps the lion's share of her gain, but the town that built the rail line gets a steady cut.

Now run the same plot in Vietnam. The land doubles near the metro; the *market* value soars. But because the holding tax is ~0.05% of a *low official* price that updates slowly, the incremental public recapture is **near zero**. The entire windfall stays with Minh.

*Land-value capture is how a city gets a return on the infrastructure it builds; a country with no real holding tax simply gifts that return to landowners.*

### Why this matters more in a fast-urbanising country

This mechanism is most powerful precisely where land values are moving fastest — which describes Vietnam exactly. HCMC and Hanoi primary apartment prices rose **30–40% in a single year** into 2025 (CBRE/MoC via VnExpress); land near new infrastructure can move even more. Every percentage point of that increase that flows untaxed to landowners is a percentage point the public *didn't* recapture to fund the next road, the next school, the next metro. A country building infrastructure as fast as Vietnam, while taxing land holding at near-zero, is in effect running a giant, continuous transfer from the public that builds to the private owners who happen to sit nearby. Land-value capture is the tool that would change that — and Vietnam barely uses it.

## How a holding tax capitalises into a lower price

We now come to the single most counter-intuitive fact about holding taxes, and the one most people — including many homeowners and even some policymakers — get wrong. A *new, permanent* holding tax does not simply make ownership more expensive going forward. It **lowers the price of the asset itself, immediately**, and the burden of that one-time drop falls on whoever owns the asset on the day the tax is announced.

![A timeline showing a property priced at 100, then a 1 percent annual tax announced, then the future tax stream discounted to a present value of about 20, then a new lower purchase price of about 80, with the one-time hit borne by the seller](/imgs/blogs/property-and-land-holding-taxes-4.png)

The figure above walks the mechanism step by step. Let's build the logic.

### Why a recurring cost lowers the price

Think about what a buyer is really purchasing: a stream of future benefits from the property (rent, or the imputed value of living there, plus eventual resale). A rational buyer prices the asset at the **present value** of that future stream — the lump sum today that is worth all those future benefits, discounted back at some required return. (If "present value" is new, the intuition is just that ₫1 next year is worth slightly less than ₫1 today, so a stream of future money is worth a finite sum now; we use the same machinery to value rent in [cap rate, NOI, and the income approach](/blog/trading/real-estate/cap-rate-noi-and-the-income-approach).)

Now impose a permanent annual tax. That tax is a permanent *reduction* in the property's net future income — a stream of outflows that goes on forever. A buyer, pricing the asset, simply **subtracts the present value of the entire future tax stream** from what they would otherwise pay. The price falls by that present value. The tax got *capitalised* into a lower price.

### The arithmetic of capitalisation

The present value of a constant payment of \$T per year, forever, discounted at rate r, is the famous perpetuity formula: PV = T ÷ r. (Define: a *perpetuity* is a stream that pays the same amount every year with no end date.) If the annual tax equals a fraction t of the property's value V, then T = t × V, and the price drops by roughly (t × V) ÷ r.

#### Worked example: how a 1% tax lowers the price a buyer pays

A plot is worth **₫1.0 billion** with no holding tax. The government announces a permanent **1% annual** land tax, so the tax is ₫10 million per year. The market discounts future cash flows at **5%**.

The present value of "₫10 million a year, forever" is ₫10M ÷ 0.05 = **₫200 million**. A rational buyer now refuses to pay ₫1.0bn — because on top of the purchase price they would owe ₫10M every year forever, worth ₫200M today. So they offer roughly **₫800 million** instead. The ₫200M tax burden has been **capitalised** straight into the price: the price fell by the present value of the tax.

Who actually lost the ₫200M? **The person who owned the land on the day the tax was announced.** Their asset is now worth ₫800M instead of ₫1.0bn — a one-time ₫200M hit, taken the instant the policy lands. The *future* buyer at ₫800M is unharmed: they pay a lower price and then the annual tax, and the two exactly offset. This is why introducing a holding tax is so politically violent — it is, in effect, a one-time wealth tax on *current* landowners, even though it looks like a gentle annual charge.

*A holding tax feels like a small yearly fee but lands like a one-time hit to today's owners — which is precisely why today's owners fight it so hard.*

### The flip side: this is also why a holding tax is fair to future buyers

There is a hopeful corollary. Because the tax is capitalised, the *next generation* of buyers pays a **lower price** for land. A young Vietnamese family buying after a credible, permanent land tax would face cheaper land (the tax is baked into a lower price) and then pay the modest annual tax — which, unlike a price they must mortgage at 12–14% interest, is just a steady operating cost. In a country with [some of the worst housing affordability on Earth](/blog/trading/real-estate/anatomy-of-a-property-bubble-and-crash) — HCMC at roughly 32 years of median income per home — a tax that *lowers land prices* is not obviously the enemy of first-time buyers. It is the enemy of *current* owners and *speculators*, which is a very different political coalition.

## The property tax: land plus building, and why it's second-best

Almost no country runs a pure land-value tax. What they actually run — the US, the UK (council tax, loosely), most of Europe — is an ordinary **property tax** on *land plus building*. Why settle for second-best, and how much worse is it?

![A pipeline showing the millage calculation flowing from market value through an assessment ratio to assessed value, then multiplied by the millage rate and effective rate to produce an annual tax bill](/imgs/blogs/property-and-land-holding-taxes-5.png)

The figure above shows how a real property-tax bill is computed — the **millage calc** — which we'll work through, then explain why taxing the building too is a step down from the pure ideal.

### Reading the millage pipeline

Follow the figure left to right. The assessor estimates the **market value** (\$300,000). An **assessment ratio** (often near 100% in the US) converts it to the **assessed value** — the taxable base (\$300,000). The locality sets a **millage rate** — recall, mills are thousandths, set separately by county, city, and school district and stacked together. Folding millage and ratio into one honest number gives the **effective rate** (US average ~1.1%). Multiply: \$300,000 × 1.1% = **\$3,300** — the **annual tax bill** that funds the local government. Every American homeowner's tax bill is exactly this pipeline.

#### Worked example: Dana's US property tax vs Minh's Vietnamese near-zero

Dana owns a \$300,000 home. Effective rate 1.1%. Annual property tax = \$300,000 × 0.011 = **\$3,300 per year**. Over ten years, **\$33,000** — more than 10% of the purchase price, paid to the county for schools, fire, police, and roads. If her home appreciates to \$400,000, the bill rises with it to ~\$4,400.

Minh owns his ₫16 billion (≈ \$618,000) central HCMC plot. The non-agricultural land-use tax is ~0.03% of a *state* price that is itself well below market. Even taking a generous ~0.05% of a low official value, his annual holding tax lands in the low *hundreds of thousands of đồng* — call it ₫500,000–₫2,000,000 (≈ \$20–\$80) a year on a \$618,000 asset. Over ten years, perhaps ₫15 million (≈ \$580) — versus Dana's \$33,000 on a *cheaper* property.

*The same act of owning land costs Dana a new-car's-worth of tax per decade and costs Minh a few nice dinners — and that single gap shapes how each country's market behaves.*

### Why taxing the building is second-best

Return to the deadweight-loss logic. The *land* portion of a property tax is the good, harmless part — land supply is vertical, so taxing it distorts nothing. But the *building* portion is a tax on a **reproducible** thing whose supply is **not** fixed. People decide whether to build, how big, how nice, whether to renovate. Tax the building and you discourage exactly those choices at the margin: a higher tax on structures means slightly fewer and slightly smaller buildings get built and improved. That is a real (if modest) deadweight loss — the property tax has the harmless land component *and* a mildly harmful building component bolted on.

The cover figure captured this: under a property tax, improving your home raises your tax; under a pure land tax, it never does. A land-value tax says "do whatever you want with the site — build a tower, build nothing — your tax is the same", which removes any tax penalty on development. A property tax instead nudges, slightly, toward *under-improving* land.

### Why governments use it anyway

So why does essentially every government tax the building too, despite the theory? Three practical reasons:

- **Assessment is easier for the combined value.** Markets transact whole properties (land + building together), so there is abundant sales data on the *combined* value. Splitting out the pure land value requires extra modelling and is more contestable. Taxing the observable combined value is administratively simpler.
- **The building share is a big tax base.** Excluding buildings shrinks the taxable base substantially, so to raise the same revenue a pure LVT needs much higher *rates* on land — which looks alarming and invites more pushback, even if the economics are sound.
- **Historical inertia.** Property tax systems were built centuries ago around visible, countable things (hearths, windows, rooms, then buildings). Switching a running system to pure-land assessment is a large administrative reform with concentrated losers (land-rich, building-poor owners), so it rarely happens. A few places — parts of Pennsylvania, Estonia, Denmark, Taiwan — lean toward land-heavy taxation, but they are the exceptions that prove the rule.

The property tax, then, is the pragmatic compromise: it keeps the harmless land-tax core, accepts a small building-tax distortion for administrative ease, and funds local government reliably. Second-best — but the second-best that actually exists at scale.

## Vietnam's near-absent holding tax — and what it permits

Now we can be precise about Vietnam, and about what the *absence* of a real holding tax allows. This is the crux of the post's Vietnamese angle.

### What Vietnam actually charges

Vietnam taxes property *transactions* meaningfully — 2% personal income tax on a sale, 0.5% registration fee — but it has **no broad annual property tax** of the kind Dana pays. The only recurrent holding charge is the **non-agricultural land-use tax**, with statutory rates of roughly **0.03% to 0.15%**, and applied to the *state* land-price table, which has historically run well below market value. The combination — a tiny rate on a low base — makes the effective holding cost on Vietnamese urban land **close to zero**, on the order of 0.05% of true market value, more than twenty times below the US.

![A bar chart comparing the annual holding tax as a share of property value, about 0.05 percent in Vietnam against about 1.1 percent in the United States](/imgs/blogs/property-and-land-holding-taxes-3.png)

The figure above is the gap in one picture: Vietnam at ~0.05%, the US at ~1.1% — roughly a **22× difference** in the annual cost of holding land. That gap is not a footnote. It changes how the entire market behaves.

### What near-zero holding cost permits: land-banking and speculation

When holding land costs almost nothing per year, **sitting on idle land becomes a rational, even optimal, strategy**. Consider the incentive each owner faces:

- In the **US**, holding an empty lot costs ~1.1% of its value *every year* in property tax, plus the opportunity cost of the capital. That bleed pressures owners to *use* the land — build, rent, or sell to someone who will — because idle land is a cash drain. The holding tax is a clock ticking against doing nothing.
- In **Vietnam**, holding an empty lot costs ~0.05% a year — essentially nothing. With land appreciating 10–30%+ in good years, the math is overwhelming: **buy land, do nothing, and let it ride.** There is no annual penalty for leaving it idle, so why incur the cost, risk, and hassle of developing it? This is **land-banking** — accumulating and holding land for future appreciation rather than current use — and it is endemic in Vietnam precisely because the carrying cost is so low.

We trace the full behavioural chain — how low holding costs feed speculation, hoarding, and the boom-bust cycle — in the companion piece on [how tax shapes behaviour: speculation and hoarding](/blog/trading/real-estate/how-tax-shapes-behavior-speculation-and-hoarding). The headline: a near-zero holding tax doesn't just *fail to discourage* speculation; it actively *subsidises* it relative to a world where idle land is taxed. Land that could host homes, in a country with a brutal housing shortage, instead sits behind fences as a tax-free store of value.

#### Worked example: the land-banker's math, with and without a holding tax

Minh buys a ₫16bn plot expecting **12% annual** land appreciation. He plans to hold it idle for 5 years and flip it.

**In Vietnam (holding tax ~0.05%):** annual carrying tax ≈ ₫16bn × 0.0005 = **₫8 million**. Over 5 years, ~₫40M — trivial against a plot appreciating ₫1.9bn *in year one alone*. The land-bank is wildly profitable; doing nothing is the smart move. Net of the tiny tax, Minh's 12% gross return is essentially still 12%.

**Now imagine a 1% annual holding tax:** carrying cost ≈ ₫160 million per year, ₫800M+ over 5 years. Now idle holding bleeds real money every year. Minh's *net* return drops from ~12% to ~11%, and — more importantly — the calculus shifts toward *putting the land to use* (building, renting) so it earns income to cover the tax, rather than sitting empty. **A 1% holding tax wouldn't end appreciation, but it would punish idleness and reward use** — exactly the behavioural change a housing-short city wants.

*Vietnam's near-zero holding tax doesn't just permit land-banking; it makes doing nothing with prime urban land the financially smartest choice — which is the opposite of what the housing market needs.*

### The reform that's been floated — and stalled

Vietnam's Ministry of Finance is not unaware of this. A **property / second-home holding tax** has been floated and debated repeatedly, prominently in **2023–2026**, often framed as a tax on owners of multiple properties or on high-value/idle land, explicitly to curb speculation. As of mid-2026 it has **not been enacted**. The **Land Law 2024** (effective August 2024) did the necessary *plumbing* — replacing the old five-year framework price with **annual land-price tables** that track the market far more closely — which is precisely the assessment infrastructure a real holding tax would need. The base is being modernised; the tax itself remains politically stuck. Which brings us to the central puzzle.

## The politics: why a tax economists love is so hard to pass

Here is the paradox at the heart of this post. Economists across every ideological camp endorse land/property holding taxes as unusually efficient and fair. And yet they are among the *hardest* taxes to introduce or raise anywhere on Earth. Why?

### Reason 1: capitalisation makes it a one-time wealth grab from current owners

We saw the mechanism: a new holding tax **capitalises into a lower asset price immediately**, so the entire future tax burden is dumped, as a one-time loss, onto *whoever owns land on announcement day*. Those owners are the most politically powerful constituency in almost any country — they vote, they're older and wealthier, and they are highly motivated because the loss is concentrated and visible. Future buyers, who would *benefit* from cheaper land, don't yet exist as an organised group. So the politics are brutally asymmetric: a small, rich, organised group of current owners faces a clear loss, while a large, diffuse, unorganised group of future buyers would gain. Concentrated losses beat diffuse gains in politics almost every time.

### Reason 2: visibility

A holding tax is one of the most *visible* taxes there is. It arrives as a single, large, annual bill with the government's name on it — Dana's \$3,300, written as one number. Compare an income tax (withheld in small slices from every paycheck, barely noticed) or a VAT (buried in prices). Visible taxes are hated out of proportion to their size, and few are more visible than an annual property-tax bill. Politicians know this; raising the property tax is a famous third rail in American local politics, where it routinely triggers ballot revolts (California's Proposition 13 in 1978 capped property taxes after exactly this kind of backlash).

### Reason 3: the asset-rich, cash-poor problem

The genuinely sympathetic objection. A retiree who bought a modest home decades ago may now sit on land worth a fortune — but with a fixed pension and little cash. A holding tax based on the land's *market* value can exceed what they can actually pay from income, threatening to force them out of a home they own outright. This is real, it is emotionally powerful, and it is the objection that sinks the most reform proposals. (Good designs address it — deferral schemes that let the tax accrue against the estate, homestead exemptions, circuit-breakers tied to income — but these add complexity and political argument.)

### Reason 4: who assesses, and can you trust them

An ad valorem tax requires the government to put a *number* on everyone's property every year, and that number determines their bill. If assessments are inaccurate, inconsistent, or corruptible, the tax feels arbitrary and unfair — and people will fight a tax they believe is unfairly assessed far harder than one they accept as correct. In a country still building assessment capacity and market-price data, this is a serious live obstacle; it is exactly what the Land Law 2024's annual price tables are meant to fix, and exactly why the reform is sequenced this way.

### Why it's harder still in Vietnam

Layer on Vietnamese specifics. A huge share of household wealth is held in land and housing — far more than in most economies — so a holding tax touches almost everyone's largest asset directly and visibly. Land prices have risen so fast that a market-value tax could mean large bills relative to incomes. And the land-banking and speculative-holding that a holding tax would target is itself a significant store of wealth for politically connected owners. Every one of the four reasons above is amplified. None of which makes the tax wrong — only hard. The economics say "do it"; the politics say "good luck".

## Common misconceptions

Holding taxes attract more confident-but-wrong beliefs than almost any topic in real estate. Here are the big ones.

### "A land tax kills development"

The exact opposite is true, and it's the most important misconception to correct. A *property* tax (on the building) mildly discourages building, because improving your property raises your tax. But a **land-value tax** does the reverse: it taxes the land *whether or not you build*, so leaving land idle costs you just as much as developing it — which **pressures owners to put land to productive use** to generate income to cover the tax. Far from killing development, an LVT is a development *spur*: it punishes land-banking and rewards building. The confusion comes from conflating the two taxes. Tax the building: less building. Tax the land regardless of the building: more building.

### "Renters don't pay property tax — only owners do"

Half-wrong, and the incidence figure (coming next) makes the distinction precise. The *land* portion of a property tax falls on the landowner and **cannot** be passed to tenants, because land supply is fixed — a landlord who tries to raise rent to cover a land tax is already charging whatever the market bears (the rent is set by demand against fixed supply, not by the owner's costs). But the *building* portion can, over time, be partly passed forward: if taxing structures means fewer get built, tighter housing supply lets landlords raise rents, and tenants bear some of it. So renters bear *part* of the building-tax incidence but *none* of the pure land-tax incidence. "Renters don't pay" is too strong; "renters can't be made to pay the land part" is correct.

### "Vietnam will never tax property holding"

Plausible-sounding, probably wrong over a long horizon. The Land Law 2024's shift to annual market-tracking land-price tables is precisely the assessment infrastructure a holding tax requires — you don't build that machinery by accident. The Ministry of Finance has floated holding/second-home taxes repeatedly. The fiscal pressure (Vietnam needs sustainable local-government revenue as it urbanises) and the policy goal (curbing the speculation that prices young families out) both point one direction. The barrier is political timing and design, not principle. "Never" is a strong word for a reform whose plumbing is already being laid.

### "A holding tax just gets passed on to buyers, so it's pointless"

This misreads capitalisation. A holding tax *does* get reflected in price — but as a *lower* price, not a higher one. The tax capitalises *downward*: buyers pay less for land because they'll owe the annual tax. So the tax isn't "passed on" to future buyers as a higher cost; it's absorbed as a one-time loss by *current* owners, after which future buyers face cheaper land plus a manageable annual charge. The tax does real work — it transfers wealth from current landowners to the public and lowers land prices — it just does that work through a mechanism most people get backwards.

### "The property tax is a tax on your home, so it's anti-homeowner"

It's more anti-*speculator* than anti-homeowner. Because the tax falls hardest on holding *idle and appreciating* land, and because it *lowers* land prices (capitalisation), a holding tax tends to help first-time buyers (cheaper entry) at the expense of those holding land purely for appreciation. An owner-occupier living in their home faces an annual cost, yes — but in exchange for a market where homes are cheaper to *buy* and idle land-banking is discouraged. Whether any individual homeowner comes out ahead depends on their situation, but framing the tax as simply "anti-homeowner" misses that it reshuffles wealth from holders toward future buyers.

## How it shows up in real markets

Enough theory. Here is the holding tax operating in the wild — three concrete, datable cases.

### US local government runs on the property tax

The clearest real-world demonstration of the property tax's power is that it is the **backbone of American local government**. Roughly **70%+** of US local tax revenue comes from property taxes; they fund the lion's share of K-12 public schools, plus local police, fire, libraries, and roads. The link is direct and visible: a town's school quality and its property-tax base are tightly coupled, which is why American home-buyers obsessively check both the tax rate *and* the school district. The effective rate ranges from ~0.3% (Hawaii, Alabama) to ~2.2% (New Jersey, Illinois), with a ~1.1% national average (Tax Foundation / Census, 2024–25). The mechanism from this post — ad valorem, millage-based, reassessed periodically — is the everyday machinery of thousands of US counties. It works, it raises real money with little deadweight loss on the land portion, and it is *also* perennially the most contested tax in American politics. Both things are true.

### Vietnam's land-banking and the empty-lot economy

Walk through the fringes of HCMC or Hanoi, or the satellite provinces that saw the 2018 *đất nền* (land-plot) fever, and you'll see the holding tax's *absence* made physical: subdivided plots held idle for years, fenced and empty, waiting purely for appreciation. With holding costs near zero and land appreciating double digits in good years, this is the rational response to the incentives — exactly the land-banking the model predicts. The 2018 satellite-province plot mania and the broader speculative dynamics that fed into the **2022–23 bond crunch** (Tân Hoàng Minh in April 2022, Vạn Thịnh Phát/SCB in October 2022 freezing the corporate-bond market) all sit downstream of a system where holding land is cheap and flipping it is lightly taxed. A meaningful annual holding tax wouldn't have prevented the cycle — but it would have leaned against the *hold-idle-and-wait* behaviour that amplifies it. The full cycle mechanics are in [Vietnam's property cycles 2007, 2013, 2022](/blog/trading/real-estate/vietnam-property-cycles-2007-2013-2022); the tax angle is one important input.

### A metro line's land windfall — captured or gifted

The land-value-capture story plays out every time a city builds transit. Internationally, **Hong Kong's MTR** is the canonical case: the rail operator captures land value directly by developing the property *above and around* its stations ("Rail plus Property"), funding the railway from the land gains it creates rather than from fares alone — a private-sector version of value capture that makes the MTR one of the few profitable metros on Earth. In **Vietnam**, HCMC's Metro Line 1 lifted land values along its corridor for years before it opened, and — with no real holding tax and slow-moving official prices — almost all of that windfall accrued *privately* to corridor landowners rather than helping fund the line. The same metro, the same land-value lift, two opposite outcomes: Hong Kong designed to capture the windfall, Vietnam designed (by default) to gift it. The tool that determines which outcome you get is, precisely, the holding tax and the value-capture mechanisms built around it.

### Who really bears the tax — the incidence picture

![A matrix showing that under fixed land supply the landowner bears the full land-value tax, tenants pay nothing on the land portion but may pay part of a building tax, developers face no land tax but a building tax discourages building, and the public recaptures the land windfall](/imgs/blogs/property-and-land-holding-taxes-7.png)

The figure above pulls incidence together, row by row. Under a **land-value tax** (left column): the **landowner** bears the full burden as a lower land price; the **tenant** pays nothing extra (rent is set by demand against fixed supply, not by the owner's tax); the **developer** is untaxed on the building and free to build; the **public** recaptures the unearned land windfall. Under an ordinary **property tax** (right column): the landowner still bears the land part; the tenant may pay *part* of the building-tax burden over time (if taxing structures tightens supply and lifts rents); the developer faces a building tax that mildly discourages construction; the public gets revenue but accepts a little deadweight loss from the building portion. The single organising principle: **fixed land supply means the land tax sticks to the landowner and can't be shifted** — which is exactly why it's efficient and exactly why owners resist it.

#### Worked example: the renter-vs-owner incidence

A landlord owns a building on land worth ₫4 billion and rents the apartment for **₫20 million/month**. A new **1% land tax** lands: ₫40 million/year. Can the landlord raise the rent to cover it?

For the **land portion** — no. The rent of ₫20M is already whatever the local rental market will bear; it was set by what tenants will pay against the fixed supply of comparable apartments, *not* by the landlord's costs. Adding a land tax doesn't make tenants willing to pay more, and it doesn't reduce the supply of land. So the landlord eats the ₫40M; their net rental income falls, and the *land's* value falls with it (capitalisation). The **tenant pays nothing** of the land tax.

Now suppose instead it's a **building tax** that, applied broadly, makes developers build *fewer* new apartments. Over years, tighter supply lets landlords across the city raise rents — say to ₫21M. *Now* the tenant bears part of it, indirectly, through a tighter market. The land tax sticks to the owner; the building tax can leak partly onto tenants through supply.

*The fixed supply of land is what nails the land tax to the landowner — tenants can't be charged for something whose quantity never changes.*

## When this matters to you / further reading

The holding tax is the quiet variable that explains an astonishing amount of how real-estate markets behave — and it touches your life more directly than almost any other tax mechanism in this series.

If you are **buying in the US** (or any high-property-tax market), the annual holding tax is a major, permanent line item — Dana's \$3,300/year on a \$300,000 home is real money that recurs forever and rises with value. Budget for it as you would a mortgage payment, check the effective rate *and* the school district it funds, and remember it is already capitalised into the price you pay. If you are **buying in Vietnam**, the near-absence of a holding tax is why land-banking is rational, why idle plots dot the urban fringe, and why a future holding tax — increasingly likely as the Land Law 2024's assessment plumbing matures — would capitalise into *lower* land prices, hurting current owners and helping future buyers. If you ever hear a debate about a "second-home tax" or "idle-land tax", you now know exactly what's at stake and who wins and loses.

And if you take away one idea, take this: **land is the one thing you can tax without making less of it exist.** That single fact — the vertical supply curve — is why a tax economists have praised for two centuries is also a tax politicians dread, because it lands as a one-time hit on the most powerful constituency there is. The economics are settled; the politics never will be.
