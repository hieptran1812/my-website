> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — "Renting is throwing money away" is the most expensive cliché in personal finance. Rent-vs-buy is a comparison of *two* cash-flow streams, and the honest version counts the costs buyers conveniently forget.
>
> - Renting has one cost: the rent. Owning has *many* — mortgage interest (also "thrown away"), the opportunity cost of your down payment, maintenance, transaction taxes, and illiquidity. Comparing one cost to one cost is the mistake that sells houses.
> - Two numbers settle most of the argument: the **price-to-rent ratio** (price ÷ annual rent) and the **user cost of ownership** (the true annual cost of owning) versus the rent.
> - A rule of thumb: below a price-to-rent of **15**, buying usually wins; above **25**, renting plus investing the difference usually wins; 15–25 is borderline and depends on how long you stay.
> - The one number to remember: a ₫7.0 billion (≈ \$270,000) HCMC flat that rents for ₫15 million (≈ \$580) a month has a price-to-rent ratio of about **38.9** — a ~2.6% gross rental yield. In that math, owning is often the *worse* financial deal even though it feels safer.

Picture two people on the same day in 2025, in the same neighbourhood of Ho Chi Minh City, making opposite decisions about the same kind of apartment. The first, a marketing manager we'll call **Minh**, signs a contract to buy a ₫7.0 billion (≈ \$270,000) flat. His parents tell him, as parents do, that paying rent is "money down the drain" and that owning is the only way to build real wealth. The second, his colleague **Lan**, keeps renting the identical apartment one floor up for ₫15 million (≈ \$580) a month, and quietly moves the money she *didn't* sink into a down payment into a low-cost index fund. Minh's family thinks Lan is being financially reckless. The numbers, it turns out, think otherwise.

This is the single most consequential financial decision most people ever make, and it is taught worse than almost anything else in money. The folk wisdom — "rent is throwing money away, buy as soon as you can" — is delivered with total confidence and almost no arithmetic. It treats a home as if it were free to own and pure waste to rent. The truth is that *both* renting and owning cost money every month; they are simply two different **cash-flow streams**, and the honest question is never "rent bad, buy good" but "which stream is cheaper, for how long, and what do I give up either way?"

That reframing — from a moral question ("am I wasting money?") to a comparison of two cash flows — is the whole game. Once you see both columns honestly, a surprising thing happens: in expensive cities, where prices have run far ahead of rents, the math frequently favours the renter. Not always. Not everywhere. But far more often than the cliché admits. By the end of this article you will be able to do the comparison yourself, on the back of an envelope, for any home in any city.

![A line chart comparing the cumulative cost of renting versus buying a home over fifteen years, with the break-even crossover year highlighted](/imgs/blogs/rent-vs-buy-the-real-math-1.png)

The chart above is the mental model for the whole post. The green line is the cumulative cost of renting; the blue line is the cumulative cost of owning (the upfront transfer tax, then the net yearly cost of carrying the home). For the first several years the renter is *ahead* — owning costs more — because the buyer paid a big tax on day one and the early years of a mortgage are almost all interest. Only after the two lines cross, somewhere around year nine or ten in a pricey city, does owning pull ahead. Everything in this article is about understanding why that crossover happens where it does, and what it means for the choice in front of you. We'll keep Minh (in HCMC, in ₫) and a US renter-investor named **Dana** (in \$) as our running examples the whole way, so both a Vietnamese and an international reader stays oriented.

A quick honesty note before we start: this is educational, not individualized advice. The goal is to hand you the mechanism and the math so *you* can run your own numbers — not to tell you to rent or to buy.

## Foundations: the two cash-flow streams

Let's build the comparison from absolutely nothing. Forget everything you've been told about property being "an investment" or "throwing money away." A home, viewed purely financially, is a thing you need to occupy that costs money to occupy. You can pay for that occupancy two ways: by renting, or by owning. Each way is a *stream of cash flows* — money leaving your pocket (and, for the owner, some money quietly building up as wealth) over time.

### The rent stream

Renting is the simpler of the two, so we start there. **Rent** is the price you pay a landlord for the right to live in their property for a period — usually monthly. That's it. When you rent, you have exactly *one* recurring housing cost: the rent cheque. You don't pay for the roof when it leaks, the boiler when it dies, the property tax, or the agent's fee when the place is sold. Those are the landlord's problem. Your housing cost is fully visible and fully contained: it is the number on the lease.

People love to say rent "buys you nothing." That's not quite right either. Rent buys you *shelter* for the month — exactly the thing the owner also needs and also pays for, just through a different mechanism. The renter's mistake, if there is one, is not that they get nothing; it's that they build no **equity** (an ownership stake, the part of the home's value that is actually yours rather than the bank's). We'll see in a moment that the owner's equity isn't free either.

There's one more property of rent worth naming because it cuts both ways: rent is *flexible and capped*. It's flexible because you can leave at the end of a lease with no transaction cost and no months-long sale — a property the owner does not have. And it's capped in the sense that your maximum loss from renting is exactly the rent you pay; you cannot be surprised by a ₫200 million repair bill or a 30% fall in your home's value, because you don't own the home or the risks attached to it. The flip side — the genuine downside of renting — is that rent can *rise* at renewal, and in markets with weak tenant protections (Vietnam among them) the landlord can decline to renew at all. So renting trades the owner's price-and-tenure certainty for liquidity and a hard cap on the downside. Keep that trade in mind; it's the spine of the non-financial section later.

### The buy stream

Now the owner. When Minh buys his ₫7.0 billion flat, here is what actually leaves (and builds in) his accounts:

- **The down payment** — the cash he puts in upfront. At a typical 20% in Vietnam, that's ₫1.4 billion (≈ \$54,000). This is *not* a cost; it becomes his equity. But — and this is the hinge of the whole article — that ₫1.4 billion is now locked in a wall and can no longer do anything else. Holding that thought.
- **The mortgage payment**, every month, split into two parts. **Interest** is the rent he pays *the bank* for borrowing their money — it is gone, exactly as gone as rent. **Principal** is repayment of the loan itself; that part builds his equity. Early in a mortgage almost the entire payment is interest; only later does principal dominate. (We have a whole sibling piece on this — see [leverage and the mortgage](/blog/trading/real-estate/leverage-and-the-mortgage-how-debt-amplifies-property).)
- **Maintenance** — the roof, the boiler, the building management fee, the repaint. As an owner, Minh *is* the landlord now, and the landlord's costs are his. A common reserve is around 1% of the property's value per year.
- **Transaction taxes and fees** — to buy in Vietnam, 2% personal income tax on the transfer plus a 0.5% registration fee; to sell later, agent and tax costs again. These are large, one-off, and irrecoverable.
- **Property taxes** — small in Vietnam today (a few hundredths of a percent of the state land price; there is no broad annual property tax yet), but large in many countries — over 1% of value per year in much of the US.

And on the other side of the ledger, two things work *for* the owner:

- **Principal repayment** builds equity, as we said — forced saving by another name.
- **Appreciation** — if the property's market value rises, the owner captures that gain on the *whole* property, not just on his down payment. This is leverage, and it cuts both ways.

So the renter's stream is one line. The owner's stream is six or seven lines, some out, some in. The honest comparison adds up *all* of them — and the most common, most expensive error in personal finance is comparing the renter's full cost (the rent) to only *part* of the owner's cost (usually just the principal, or nothing at all). When your parents compare rent to a mortgage payment and conclude renting is waste, ask them where the interest, the maintenance, the tax, and the locked-up down payment went in their math. Usually: nowhere.

### What "throwing money away" really means

Here is the sentence that should be tattooed on the inside of every first-time buyer's eyelids: **mortgage interest is thrown-away money too.** When Minh sends the bank ₫43.2 million a month in his first year, the large majority of it — roughly ₫38 million — is interest. That interest builds him no equity, buys him no asset, and is exactly as "gone" as a rent cheque. It is rent he pays to a bank instead of to a landlord. The only part of his payment that "isn't thrown away" is the small principal sliver in the early years.

So the real comparison is not "rent (thrown away) vs mortgage (builds wealth)." It is "rent (thrown away) vs interest + maintenance + tax + opportunity cost (also thrown away), minus the bit of principal and appreciation that genuinely builds wealth." When you frame it that way, the question stops being rhetorical and becomes arithmetic. The rest of this article is that arithmetic.

#### Worked example: Minh's two streams, side by side

Let's lay it out. Minh is choosing between renting a flat for ₫15 million/month (≈ \$580) and buying the identical flat for ₫7.0 billion (≈ \$270,000).

- **If he rents:** his housing cost is ₫15 million × 12 = **₫180 million/year** (≈ \$6,950). One line. Done.
- **If he buys:** ₫1.4 billion down (≈ \$54,000, locked up), a ₫5.6 billion loan (≈ \$216,000), monthly payment ₫43.2 million of which ~₫38 million is interest in year one. Annualised, that's roughly ₫456 million in interest, plus ~₫70 million maintenance (1% of ₫7bn), plus ₫175 million in one-time transfer tax (2.5% of ₫7bn) on day one, plus the lost return on his ₫1.4 billion down payment.

Comparing only "₫180M rent" to "₫43.2M × 12 = ₫518M mortgage payment" makes buying look like it costs *more* — which is also wrong, because ₫5 million/month of that payment is principal he's saving. Both naïve comparisons mislead. You have to count every line on both sides.

*The renter has one cost; the owner has six. Honesty in this decision means counting all six.*

## The price-to-rent ratio: the single most useful number

If you remember one tool from this entire article, make it this one. The **price-to-rent ratio** is the purchase price of a home divided by one year of rent for an equivalent home:

$$
\text{Price-to-rent} = \frac{\text{Purchase price}}{\text{Annual rent}}
$$

It tells you, in a single number, how expensive it is to *own* a home relative to *renting* the same home. A low ratio means rent is high relative to price — owning is cheap, renting is expensive, buying tends to win. A high ratio means rent is low relative to price — owning is expensive relative to renting, and renting (plus investing what you save) tends to win.

Its inverse is the **gross rental yield** — the annual rent as a percentage of the price, which is what a landlord earns before any costs:

$$
\text{Gross rental yield} = \frac{\text{Annual rent}}{\text{Price}} = \frac{1}{\text{Price-to-rent}}
$$

A price-to-rent of 20 is a 5% gross yield; a ratio of 40 is a 2.5% yield. (Gross because it ignores the landlord's costs — maintenance, vacancy, tax. The *net* yield is lower. If you want the income-investor's version of this, see [cap rate, NOI and the income approach](/blog/trading/real-estate/cap-rate-noi-and-the-income-approach).)

### The 15 / 25 rule of thumb

Practitioners carry a rough heuristic, and it's a good one to start from:

![A guide matrix showing the three price-to-rent bands below fifteen, fifteen to twenty-five, and above twenty-five, with HCMC placed around thirty-eight](/imgs/blogs/rent-vs-buy-the-real-math-3.png)

- **Below 15** (gross yield above ~6.7%): rent is rich relative to price, so the rent you'd save by owning easily covers the costs of owning. **Buying usually wins**, even over fairly short horizons.
- **15 to 25** (gross yield ~4–6.7%): borderline. The answer depends heavily on how long you stay, your mortgage rate, and what else you'd do with the money. Break-even runs roughly 7–10 years.
- **Above 25** (gross yield below ~4%): rent is cheap relative to price. The cost of owning dwarfs the rent you'd save, so **renting plus investing the difference usually wins**, and the break-even horizon can stretch past 15 years.

The matrix above maps the bands to what they signal — the gross yield, the verdict, the owner's headwind, and a real market that lives in each band. The crucial thing to internalise: a high ratio doesn't mean the house is a *bad asset*; it means it's an *expensive way to obtain shelter* compared to renting the same shelter. Those are different statements.

### Where HCMC and Hanoi sit

Now the uncomfortable part for the Vietnamese reader. Ho Chi Minh City's primary apartments traded around 111 million VND/m² in 2025 (≈ \$4,222/m²), while equivalent rents have lagged far behind price growth. The result is a price-to-rent ratio of roughly **38** — among the highest in the world — implying a gross rental yield of only about 2.5–2.6%. Hanoi is close behind at roughly **33**. By the 15/25 rule, both sit deep in the "rent zone." This isn't a knock on Vietnamese real estate as a long-term store of value; it's a statement that, *as a way to pay for the shelter you need this decade*, owning in HCMC is mathematically expensive relative to renting it.

#### Worked example: Minh's price-to-rent and gross yield

Minh's flat costs ₫7.0 billion (≈ \$270,000) and the equivalent rent is ₫15 million/month, or ₫180 million/year (≈ \$6,950).

$$
\text{Price-to-rent} = \frac{7{,}000\text{M}}{15\text{M} \times 12} = \frac{7{,}000}{180} \approx 38.9
$$

$$
\text{Gross yield} = \frac{1}{38.9} \approx 2.57\%
$$

So Minh would pay nearly **39 years of rent** to own this flat outright, and a landlord renting it out earns a gross 2.57% before any costs — less than a Vietnamese savings account, before he's spent a đồng on maintenance, vacancy, or tax. Compare Dana in a US Sun Belt city, where a \$300,000 house rents for \$1,700/month (\$20,400/year): her ratio is 300,000 ÷ 20,400 ≈ **14.7**, a ~6.8% gross yield. Same decision, wildly different math. Dana sits in the buy zone; Minh sits deep in the rent zone.

*The price-to-rent ratio is a thirty-second sanity check that quietly settles most rent-vs-buy arguments before the emotions arrive.*

## The user cost of ownership: the full formula, line by line

The price-to-rent ratio is the screening tool. The **user cost of ownership** is the precise version — the actual amount, in money per year, that it costs you to *own and occupy* a home, which is the number you compare head-to-head against a year of rent. Economists call the rent you'd otherwise pay (or the rent you implicitly "pay yourself" by living in your own home) the **imputed rent**; the user cost is what owning costs you to enjoy that imputed rent.

Here is the formula, built up term by term. The annual user cost of ownership is:

$$
\text{User cost} = \underbrace{i \cdot D}_{\text{interest}} + \underbrace{r \cdot E}_{\text{opportunity cost}} + \underbrace{m \cdot P}_{\text{maintenance}} + \underbrace{t \cdot P}_{\text{property tax}} + \underbrace{\frac{F}{H}}_{\text{transaction friction}} - \underbrace{g \cdot P}_{\text{appreciation}}
$$

where $P$ is the property price, $D$ is the loan balance, $E$ is your equity (the down payment plus principal paid), $i$ is the mortgage interest rate, $r$ is the return you could earn on your equity elsewhere, $m$ is the maintenance rate, $t$ is the property-tax rate, $F$ is the total round-trip transaction cost, $H$ is your holding period in years, and $g$ is the expected appreciation rate. Let's walk each line.

![A pipeline decomposition of the annual user cost of ownership showing interest plus opportunity cost plus maintenance plus tax plus amortized transaction friction minus expected appreciation](/imgs/blogs/rent-vs-buy-the-real-math-2.png)

The figure above shows the whole chain for Minh: interest, plus opportunity cost, plus maintenance and tax, plus the amortised transaction friction, *minus* expected appreciation, equals the user cost — the one number to set against a year of rent.

- **Interest, $i \cdot D$.** The rent you pay the bank on the money you borrowed. At an ~11% blended Vietnamese rate on Minh's ₫5.6 billion loan, that's about ₫616 million in the first year (it falls as the loan amortises). This is pure cost; it builds nothing.
- **Opportunity cost, $r \cdot E$.** The return your equity *could* have earned elsewhere. Minh's ₫1.4 billion down payment, invested at a conservative 8%, would throw off ₫112 million a year. By locking it in the flat, he gives that up. This is the single most-forgotten cost in the entire decision, and it's enormous.
- **Maintenance, $m \cdot P$.** Roughly 1% of value per year — ₫70 million on a ₫7 billion flat — for repairs, building fees, and the slow decay of any physical thing.
- **Property tax, $t \cdot P$.** Tiny in Vietnam (call it negligible today), but in many US states this alone is 1–2% of value per year — \$3,000–\$6,000 on a \$300,000 home. Don't ignore it where it's large.
- **Transaction friction, $F / H$.** Buying costs 2.5% in Vietnam (2% transfer + 0.5% registration); selling costs roughly another 2% in fees. That ~4.5% round-trip on ₫7 billion is about ₫315 million, and you must spread it over however many years you stay. Stay 3 years and it's ₫105 million/year; stay 12 years and it's ₫26 million/year. *Holding period is a lever on cost.*
- **Minus appreciation, $g \cdot P$.** If the flat rises ~4% a year, that's ₫280 million of gain on the ₫7 billion — and crucially, you capture it on the whole property, not just your down payment. This is the term that makes owning worth it. The entire bull case for buying lives in this one line, which is why people who are very bullish on appreciation are, knowingly or not, betting the whole decision on $g$.

Net it out and you get the true annual cost of owning. Set that against the ₫180 million of rent. Whichever is smaller is the cheaper way to obtain the same shelter this year.

A subtle but important point about the appreciation term: it is the only line in the entire formula that is a *guess about the future* rather than a contractual fact. Interest is set by your loan; maintenance is roughly 1% of value; the transfer tax is a known 2.5%; the opportunity cost is the market return you can observe today. But $g$ — expected appreciation — is whatever you assume, and the whole verdict can flip on it. Assume HCMC grows 8% a year forever and owning looks fine; assume it grows 2% (or falls, as it did in 2011–13 and 2022–23) and owning is a disaster. This is why a careful analyst runs the user-cost formula across a *range* of appreciation assumptions rather than a single hopeful number — and why anyone who tells you buying is a no-brainer is, whether they realise it or not, smuggling in an aggressive forecast for $g$. The honest version of the analysis treats $g$ as the uncertain variable it is and asks: "what does appreciation have to be for owning to beat renting?" For Minh, that break-even appreciation is high — north of 5% a year, every year — which is a bet, not a certainty.

There's also a relationship hiding inside the formula that's worth surfacing. The user cost is essentially **the price-to-rent ratio in disguise.** A high ratio means the price $P$ is large relative to the rent, which inflates every cost term that scales with $P$ — interest, opportunity cost, maintenance, tax, friction — while the thing you're comparing against (the rent) stays small. That's the mechanical reason a high price-to-rent ratio pushes the user cost above the rent: all the costs scale with the big number (price), and only the appreciation term gives you anything back, and only if prices actually rise. The two tools — the ratio and the user cost — are two views of the same underlying fact.

#### Worked example: Minh's user cost of ownership, line by line

Let's compute Minh's first-year user cost in million VND:

| Term | Calculation | Amount |
|---|---|---|
| Interest | ~11% × ₫5,600M loan | **+₫616M** |
| Opportunity cost | 8% × ₫1,400M equity | **+₫112M** |
| Maintenance | 1% × ₫7,000M | **+₫70M** |
| Property tax | negligible in VN | +₫0M |
| Transaction friction | ₫315M ÷ 10-yr stay | **+₫32M** |
| *minus* appreciation | 4% × ₫7,000M | **−₫280M** |
| **= User cost of owning** | | **≈ ₫550M/year** |

Against ₫180 million of rent, Minh's user cost of owning is roughly **₫550 million in year one** — three times the rent. Even if you believe appreciation will run hot at 6% (₫420M), the user cost is still ~₫410M, more than double the rent. The owning case only gets close if you assume appreciation far above the rent-saving the buyer gives up — which is exactly the speculative bet the cliché never admits it's making.

*The user cost of ownership turns "owning vs renting" from a feeling into a single comparable number — and in HCMC that number is brutal.*

#### Worked example: Dana's user cost in a US Sun Belt city

Now run the identical formula for Dana, who's deciding between renting a \$300,000-equivalent house for \$1,700/month (\$20,400/year) and buying it. She puts 20% down (\$60,000), borrows \$240,000 at a 6.5% 30-year fixed rate (the Jun-2026 level), and her market charges ~1.2% annual property tax — far more than Vietnam.

| Term | Calculation | Amount |
|---|---|---|
| Interest | ~6.5% × \$240,000 loan | **+\$15,600** |
| Opportunity cost | 6% × \$60,000 equity | **+\$3,600** |
| Maintenance | 1% × \$300,000 | **+\$3,000** |
| Property tax | 1.2% × \$300,000 | **+\$3,600** |
| Transaction friction | ~\$24,000 round-trip ÷ 8-yr stay | **+\$3,000** |
| *minus* appreciation | 3.5% × \$300,000 | **−\$10,500** |
| **= User cost of owning** | | **≈ \$18,300/year** |

Dana's user cost of owning (~\$18,300) lands *below* her \$20,400 of rent — owning is the cheaper way to obtain her shelter, exactly as her 14.7 price-to-rent ratio predicted. Notice how different the picture is from Minh's even though the formula is identical: Dana's lower price relative to rent shrinks every cost term, and her market's higher rent-saving covers the costs. The US property tax (\$3,600) is a real drag that Minh barely faces, but it isn't enough to overturn the verdict. Same math, opposite answer — and the price-to-rent ratio told you which way it would go before you computed a single line.

*The user-cost formula is universal; it's the local price-to-rent ratio that decides whether it lands above or below the rent — which is why the right answer in Dallas is the wrong answer in HCMC.*

## The costs buyers forget

The user-cost formula already contains them, but they're worth pulling into the light one by one, because each is a place where buyers systematically deceive themselves. The cliché survives only because these five costs are *invisible* — none of them arrives as a monthly rent cheque, so the brain files them under "free."

![A graph showing the five hidden costs of buying a home: mortgage interest, the transfer tax, maintenance, the opportunity cost of the down payment, and illiquidity, all feeding into the true cost of owning](/imgs/blogs/rent-vs-buy-the-real-math-6.png)

The figure traces it: the cliché in the middle, the five forgotten costs radiating out — interest, transfer tax, maintenance, opportunity cost, illiquidity — and the truth they add up to. Let's take them in order.

### 1. Mortgage interest (also "thrown away")

We've said it, but it bears repeating because it's the master forgotten cost. In Minh's first year, ~₫456 million of his payments is interest paid to the bank for the use of their money — gone, building nothing. People who would never "waste" ₫15 million a month on rent cheerfully waste ₫38 million a month on interest, because interest doesn't feel like rent. It is rent. The bank is your landlord for the borrowed 80% of the house.

The amortisation structure makes this worse than people expect. On a 20-year loan, roughly the first decade of payments is interest-dominated. You don't start meaningfully "owning" the house through your payments until you're well into the loan. (This is why the early years of the cumulative-cost chart show owning so far behind: the buyer is paying a lot and building little.)

### 2. The opportunity cost of the down payment

This is the cost that, once you see it, you can never un-see. Minh's ₫1.4 billion (≈ \$54,000) down payment isn't spent — it becomes equity — but it is *locked*. It can no longer sit in an index fund earning 8%, in a bond earning 5%, or even in a savings account earning 5–6%. The return you forgo is a real cost of owning, every single year, and it doesn't show up on any bank statement, which is precisely why people ignore it.

At 8%, Minh's locked ₫1.4 billion costs him ₫112 million a year in forgone return. Over the first decade, ignoring compounding, that's well over a billion đồng of return quietly surrendered — money that, in the renter's hands, compounds. Economists treat the equity tied up in your home exactly like cash you've chosen not to invest, because that's what it is.

### 3. Maintenance

When you rent, the boiler is the landlord's problem. When you own, *you are* the landlord, and the building doesn't maintain itself. A reasonable reserve is ~1% of value per year — and over decades, things like roofs (every 20–25 years), HVAC systems, and structural work mean the long-run average is rarely lower. On Minh's flat, ₫70 million a year. This is a cost the renter simply does not bear; it's bundled invisibly into their rent, but the *risk* of a ₫200 million bad year sits with the owner, not the tenant.

### 4. Transaction taxes and fees

Property is the highest-friction major asset there is. In Vietnam, buying costs 2% personal income tax on the transfer plus a 0.5% registration fee (lệ phí trước bạ) — 2.5% on the way in. Selling adds agent commission and tax again, roughly another 2%. That ~4.5% round-trip on a ₫7 billion flat is about **₫315 million** (≈ \$12,000) of pure friction, and it's the single biggest reason short stays punish buyers: if you sell after three years, you've paid ₫315 million in friction for three years of occupancy, which is ₫105 million a year *on top* of everything else. In the US the round-trip can hit 8–10% once you include 5–6% agent commissions. Stocks cost basically nothing to trade; houses cost a fortune.

### 5. Illiquidity

You can sell a stock in seconds at a known price. A house takes months to sell, at a price you discover only when an offer arrives, and exactly when you most need to sell — a job loss, a recession — is exactly when buyers vanish and prices sag. This **illiquidity** is a genuine cost: it limits your flexibility, it can force a fire-sale, and it means your largest asset is the one you can least rely on in an emergency. It doesn't have a clean number, but it's real, and renters pay zero of it — a renter's "exit cost" is giving notice.

#### Worked example: the friction on a short stay

Suppose Minh buys the ₫7 billion flat and is relocated for work after just 3 years. His round-trip transaction cost is ₫175 million to buy (2.5%) plus roughly ₫140 million to sell (~2%), call it ₫315 million (≈ \$12,200). Spread over 3 years, that's **₫105 million/year** of friction alone — more than half of what he'd have paid in rent (₫180M), before counting a single đồng of interest, maintenance, or opportunity cost. In three years, with the loan barely amortised and ~4% appreciation giving maybe ₫840 million of gross gain on paper, the friction plus interest plus opportunity cost very likely swamp the appreciation. He'd have been far better off renting.

*Transaction friction is a fixed cost you amortise over your stay — which is why the length of your stay, more than almost anything, decides whether buying makes sense.*

## The break-even horizon

Everything so far converges on one variable: **how long you stay.** The **break-even horizon** is the number of years you must own before the cumulative cost of buying drops below the cumulative cost of renting — the crossover point on our very first chart. Before that year, renting was cheaper and you "lost" by buying. After it, owning pulls ahead and keeps pulling ahead, because the big upfront friction is now spread thin, the loan is amortising fast, and appreciation is compounding on the whole property.

![A before-and-after comparison showing that staying three years makes renting win while staying twelve years makes buying win](/imgs/blogs/rent-vs-buy-the-real-math-5.png)

The figure makes the mechanism vivid. Stay 3 years (left): you pay ₫175 million in buy tax plus ~2% to sell, the loan has barely amortised (year-1 payments are mostly interest), and three years of appreciation can't cover ~₫315 million of round-trip cost. Verdict: renting wins — you never reached break-even, so the friction is pure loss. Stay 12 years (right): the ₫175 million tax is now ~₫15 million/year and barely stings, the loan has amortised hard so a big chunk of your wealth is principal, and twelve years of ~4% appreciation compounds into a large gain on the whole ₫7 billion. Verdict: buying wins — you cleared break-even around year 9 and kept compounding.

The key insight is that the break-even horizon is *set by the price-to-rent ratio*. In a low-ratio city (Dana's Sun Belt, ratio ~15), the rent you save is large relative to owning costs, so the upfront friction is recovered quickly — break-even can be 3–5 years. In a high-ratio city (Minh's HCMC, ratio ~38), the rent you save is small relative to owning costs, so it takes a very long time to recover the friction — break-even stretches to 9–12 years or beyond. The cliché ("always buy") implicitly assumes a short break-even for everyone, everywhere. It's only true where rent is high relative to price.

Three other dials move the break-even point, and it's worth knowing which way each pushes. A **higher mortgage rate** lengthens it (more of your money goes to interest, less to equity). **Higher expected appreciation** shortens it (the appreciation term turns positive faster and bigger). And **higher transaction costs** lengthen it (more friction to recover) — which is exactly why high-stamp-duty markets like the UK and high-commission markets like the US push break-even out toward 7–10 years even at moderate price-to-rent ratios. When someone quotes you a break-even number, ask what mortgage rate, appreciation, and transaction cost they assumed; change any one and the number can swing by years. There is no universal break-even — only the break-even for *your* market, *your* loan, and *your* assumptions.

It also matters that the break-even is not a cliff you fall off but a slow crossover. Owning a year or two short of break-even doesn't ruin you — it just means renting would have been modestly cheaper. The catastrophic case is the *short, forced* sale: buying, then being relocated or laid off in year two or three, paying the full round-trip friction for almost no occupancy and no amortisation. That's the scenario the cliché never warns about, and it's the most common way real buyers lose money on a home that "always goes up."

This is why the standard advice "don't buy unless you'll stay at least 5 years" is *directionally* right but incomplete. Five years is roughly the break-even in a balanced market. In a hot, high-ratio market it should be more like 8–10 years. In a cheap, low-ratio market it might be 3. The threshold isn't a constant; it's a function of your local price-to-rent ratio and your mortgage rate.

#### Worked example: Minh's break-even given 2.5% transfer tax

Let's estimate Minh's break-even crudely. His net annual cost of owning starts around ₫205 million (just above the ₫180M rent, in year one, because the carrying cost barely beats rent at a 2.6% yield) and declines slowly as the loan amortises and appreciation kicks in. But he started ₫175 million behind on day one from the 2.5% transfer tax. With owning costing roughly the same as renting in the early years, that ₫175 million head start for the renter takes nearly a decade of slowly-accumulating owner advantage to erase. The crossover lands around **year 9–10** — exactly where our opening chart marks it. If Minh is confident he'll stay 15+ years, owning eventually wins on cost. If there's a real chance he moves within 5–7 years, renting is the better financial bet by a wide margin.

*The break-even horizon converts "should I buy?" into "how long will I stay?" — and in a high-price-to-rent city, the honest answer to the second question is the answer to the first.*

## Rent and invest the difference: the honest counterfactual

Here's where the analysis stops being defensive ("renting isn't as wasteful as you think") and goes on offence. The phrase **"rent and invest the difference"** names the renter's true strategy — and it's the counterfactual the cliché conveniently omits. The buyer doesn't just "have a house"; the renter doesn't just "have nothing." The renter has the **down payment that was never spent**, plus, in a high-ratio city, a **monthly cash-flow gap** (because owning often costs more per month than renting), both of which can be invested and compounded.

This is the comparison that's actually fair: not "buyer's equity vs renter's zero," but "buyer's equity vs renter's investment portfolio." When you run it honestly, the renter is not the financial fool of the story. In a high-price-to-rent market, the renter frequently *wins*.

![A side-by-side timeline of two ten-year paths, one where the buyer builds equity and one where the renter invests the difference, finishing close together](/imgs/blogs/rent-vs-buy-the-real-math-7.png)

The figure tracks both paths over ten years. At year 0, the buyer sinks ₫1.4 billion down plus ₫175 million in tax; the renter invests the same ₫1.4 billion. The buyer's wealth grows through amortisation (slowly at first — early payments are mostly interest) plus appreciation on the full ₫7 billion. The renter's wealth grows by compounding the ₫1.4 billion plus reinvesting the monthly gap at 8%. By year 7 they're neck-and-neck (~₫2.6–2.7 billion each); by year 10 the buyer's equity (~₫4.0 billion) just edges ahead of the renter's fund (~₫3.6 billion) — *close*, after a decade, in a market where the buyer also took on leverage risk, illiquidity, and a teaser-rate reset. In a balanced or low-ratio market, the gap would favour the buyer more clearly; in a city as expensive as HCMC, it's a coin flip — and the renter got there with far more flexibility and far less risk.

### Why the renter's path is underrated

Two reasons people underweight the rent-and-invest path. First, **forced saving is real but not magic.** People say "a mortgage forces you to save, and renters just spend the difference." That's a behavioural point, not a financial one — and it's true for many people. But a disciplined renter who *actually* invests the difference (via an automatic monthly transfer to an index fund — the renter's version of "forced saving") gets the same forced-saving benefit without the leverage, the friction, and the illiquidity. The cliché quietly assumes the renter is undisciplined and the buyer is forced into virtue. Make the renter disciplined and the math changes.

Second, **the down payment compounds, and compounding is the strongest force in the comparison.** ₫1.4 billion at 8% doubles in about 9 years and is worth ~₫3.0 billion in 10. That growth happens *whether or not house prices rise*, with full liquidity, and without a single transaction fee. The buyer's appreciation, by contrast, is leveraged (good in a boom, brutal in a bust — see the school teacher in our [leverage piece](/blog/trading/real-estate/leverage-and-the-mortgage-how-debt-amplifies-property)) and only realised after a 4–5% selling cost.

#### Worked example: Minh buys vs Lan rents and invests, 10 years

Let's run both paths for a decade. Both start with ₫1.4 billion (≈ \$54,000).

**Lan rents and invests.** She invests the ₫1.4 billion she didn't put down, at 8%/year. After 10 years: 1,400 × (1.08)^10 ≈ 1,400 × 2.159 ≈ **₫3,020 million**. In a high-ratio market like HCMC the monthly cash gap between owning and renting is small (owning barely costs more per month than rent at this yield), so we'll be conservative and not add much from the gap — call her total ~**₫3.0–3.6 billion** depending on how the gap is invested.

**Minh buys.** His equity after 10 years is his down payment plus the principal he's repaid plus appreciation, minus what he'd lose to a future sale. Principal repaid on a ₫5.6 billion 20-year loan over 10 years is roughly ₫2.0–2.2 billion, so his equity-from-payments is about ₫1.4bn + ₫2.1bn ≈ ₫3.5 billion. Appreciation at 4%/year lifts the ₫7 billion flat to ~₫10.4 billion, a ₫3.4 billion gross gain — but he still owes ~₫3.5 billion, and a sale costs ~₫450 million in fees, so his net realisable equity lands around **₫4.0 billion**. Close to Lan, slightly ahead — but he carried leverage risk, paid ₫456M/year of interest, and can't touch the money without a multi-month sale.

So after ten years, in HCMC, the buyer and the disciplined renter-investor finish within a few hundred million đồng of each other — and that's *assuming* a steady 4% appreciation. If HCMC has a flat or down decade (entirely possible after the 2022–23 freeze), Lan wins decisively. The cliché promised the buyer a landslide; the math delivered a coin flip.

*"Rent and invest the difference" is not a consolation prize for people who can't afford to buy — in a high-price-to-rent city it's frequently the winning strategy.*

## The non-financial side

If the math so often favours renting in expensive cities, why do so many people buy anyway — and why might they be *right* to? Because a home is not only a financial asset; it's where you live, and several of its benefits don't show up in any spreadsheet. An honest analysis names them rather than pretending the decision is purely numerical.

- **Security and stability.** An owner can't be evicted because the landlord wants to sell or move in a relative. For families with school-age kids, the certainty of staying put has real value that no cash-flow model captures. In markets with weak tenant protections — much of Vietnam included — this is not a small thing.
- **Control.** You can renovate, repaint, drill holes, keep pets, and make the place truly yours. Renters live within someone else's rules. For some people this autonomy is worth a meaningful premium.
- **Forced saving.** As discussed, a mortgage extracts savings from people who would otherwise spend. If you *know* you're undisciplined, the mortgage's coercion is a genuine benefit — you'll end up with more wealth than the spreadsheet-rational version of you who "meant to" invest the difference.
- **Inflation hedge and rent certainty.** A fixed mortgage payment is frozen in nominal terms while rents tend to rise with inflation over decades. After 15 years, the owner's housing cost can be far below the renter's. (Though in Vietnam, where teaser rates reset to 12–14%, the "fixed payment" story is weaker — your payment can *jump*, not hold.)

And on the other side, the non-financial case *for* renting:

- **Flexibility.** You can move for a job, a relationship, or a change of heart with a month's notice and no transaction cost. In a career where your best opportunity might be in another city, this optionality is worth a great deal — and it's exactly what a ₫315 million sale cost destroys.
- **No concentration risk.** Buying puts a huge, leveraged, illiquid bet on one asset in one city. Renting and investing in a diversified portfolio spreads your wealth across hundreds of companies and assets. For most households, the home is 60–80% of net worth — a dangerously undiversified position. (On how property fits a broader portfolio, see [real estate, REITs, income, leverage and rates](/blog/trading/cross-asset/real-estate-reits-income-leverage-and-rates).)
- **Freedom from maintenance and surprise costs.** No 2 a.m. burst pipe, no ₫200 million special assessment from the building, no roof. You trade upside for a quiet life.

The point is not that the non-financial factors override the math — it's that they're real, and a mature decision weighs them *alongside* the honest math rather than using them as an excuse to skip the math entirely. "I want the security of owning and I've checked that it costs me ~₫370 million a year more than renting, and I'm willing to pay that" is a wise decision. "Rent is throwing money away" is not a decision at all; it's a slogan.

## Common misconceptions

### "Renting is throwing money away"

The headline myth, and now you can dismantle it in one sentence: *mortgage interest is thrown away too, and so are maintenance, transaction tax, and the opportunity cost of your down payment.* Rent buys you a month of shelter — exactly what the owner's interest, maintenance, and tax also buy. The only "non-wasted" part of a mortgage payment is the principal, which in the early years is a sliver. Comparing the renter's *full* cost to the owner's *non-existent* cost is the trick that makes the slogan sound true.

### "You're always building equity when you buy"

Two ways this fails. First, early mortgage payments are almost entirely interest — you build very little equity in the first several years, no matter what the slogan says. Second, equity built through appreciation can *vanish*: the US lost ~27% of national home value from 2006 to 2012, and plenty of "wealth-building" owners ended up with negative equity, owing more than the house was worth. Equity is not a guaranteed escalator; it's a leveraged, illiquid bet that can go to zero. Renters, by contrast, cannot lose money they never put in.

### "Buying is always safer than renting"

It *feels* safer, but financially it's often riskier. Buying concentrates 60–80% of your net worth in one leveraged, illiquid asset in one city, with a loan whose rate can reset upward (in Vietnam, from ~8% to 12–14%). A job loss can force a fire-sale at the worst possible time. Renting keeps your wealth liquid and diversified, and your worst case is "I have to find a new apartment." Emotional safety and financial safety are not the same thing, and in expensive, leveraged markets they point in opposite directions.

### "Rent only goes up, so buying locks in your cost"

Half-true and worth a caveat. A *fixed-rate* mortgage does lock your principal-and-interest payment while rents drift up — a real advantage over a long horizon, mostly in the US. But your *other* owner costs (maintenance, tax, insurance) rise with inflation just like rent, and in Vietnam the mortgage rate itself resets after the teaser period, so the "locked-in cost" story largely doesn't hold. Don't assume the fixed-payment benefit applies to your market without checking how your loan actually prices.

### "If I rent, I have nothing to show for it after ten years"

You have a *portfolio* to show for it — if you actually invest the difference. The honest counterfactual to the buyer's equity is the renter's invested down payment plus monthly gap, compounding tax-efficiently and liquidly. In a high-price-to-rent city the renter's portfolio frequently matches or beats the buyer's net equity after a decade, as our worked example showed. "Nothing to show for it" describes an *undisciplined* renter, not renting itself.

### "Property always goes up, so the math doesn't matter"

It does not always go up. Japan's residential land fell for two decades after 1990 and still hasn't recovered its peak. US prices fell 27% nationally in the 2008 crisis. HCMC and Hanoi themselves froze in 2011–13 and again during the 2022–23 bond crunch. Appreciation is the term that makes owning worth it — and it's the one term you cannot rely on. A strategy that only works if prices keep rising fast is a speculation, not a housing decision.

## How it shows up in real markets

![A bar chart of price-to-rent ratios across major cities, with HCMC around thirty-eight, Hanoi around thirty-three, Hong Kong forty-five, London twenty-eight, NYC twenty-five, and US Sun Belt fifteen](/imgs/blogs/rent-vs-buy-the-real-math-4.png)

The bar chart above places real cities on the price-to-rent spectrum, and it tells the whole global story at a glance. The same rent-vs-buy decision has wildly different answers depending on where you stand.

**HCMC and Hanoi — the extreme rent-zone.** With ratios around 38 and 33 and gross yields near 2.5–3%, Vietnam's two big cities sit among the most owning-unfriendly in the world. Primary apartment prices ran +33% and +40% year-on-year in 2025 while rents lagged, pushing the ratio even higher. This is *exactly* the regime where renting and investing the difference tends to win, and where the cultural pressure to buy ("you must own a home before you marry") collides hardest with the arithmetic. A HCMC household that rents a ₫15 million flat instead of buying a ₫7 billion one, and invests the gap, is making a defensible — arguably superior — financial choice, whatever the relatives say. The risk to that choice is policy and supply-driven price surges; the comfort is liquidity and diversification.

**Hong Kong — the global ceiling.** At a price-to-rent ratio around 45 (gross yield ~2.2%), Hong Kong is the world's clearest "rent zone." For decades, owners were rescued by relentless appreciation in a supply-starved market — until 2021–24, when prices fell ~25% from the peak and the renters who'd been mocked for years looked very smart. Hong Kong is the cautionary tale: a sky-high ratio can persist for a long time on the strength of appreciation, right up until it doesn't.

**London — borderline, leaning rent.** A ratio around 28 puts London just into the rent zone. UK transaction taxes (stamp duty can be several percent) add heavy friction, lengthening the break-even. For internationally mobile professionals who may move within a few years, London's math strongly favours renting; for a settled family staying 15 years, owning can still work.

**New York City — the borderline case.** At a ratio around 25, NYC sits right on the buy/rent boundary, which is why the rent-vs-buy debate is endless there. High property taxes and 6% selling commissions push break-even out toward 7–10 years. The honest answer in NYC genuinely is "it depends" — on the specific apartment, your horizon, and the mortgage rate, which swung from 2.65% in 2021 to nearly 8% in 2023 and reshaped the whole calculation.

**US Sun Belt — the buy zone.** Cities like Dallas, Atlanta, and Phoenix carry ratios near 15 (gross yields ~6–7%), where the rent you save genuinely covers the cost of owning and break-even can be just 3–5 years. This is where the "just buy" advice is actually *correct* — the math agrees with the cliché. Dana's decision to buy her \$300,000 Sun Belt house at a 14.7 ratio is sound; the same Dana renting an identical-quality home in HCMC would, on the math, rent.

**The 2008 US crash — when "always buy" detonated.** From the Jan-2000 = 100 base, the US national index peaked at 184.6 in July 2006 and fell to ~134 by February 2012 — a 27% national drop, far worse locally. Millions of buyers who'd been told renting was for fools ended up underwater, foreclosed, or stuck. The renters who'd "wasted" money kept their savings, their mobility, and their credit. It was the most expensive object lesson in the difference between *feeling* safe and *being* safe that the modern era has produced.

**Vietnam's 2022–23 freeze — liquidity matters.** When the Tân Hoàng Minh and Vạn Thịnh Phát arrests froze Vietnam's corporate bond market in 2022, property transactions seized up. Owners who needed to sell discovered there were no buyers at any reasonable price — the illiquidity cost made real. Renters, meanwhile, simply kept paying rent and kept their savings liquid through the storm. The episode is a live demonstration that the "safety" of owning is conditional on being able to sell, which is exactly when you can't.

**Japan after 1990 — when appreciation runs in reverse for a generation.** The single most important counter-example to "property always goes up" is Japan. After the late-1980s bubble peaked in 1990, residential land prices fell for roughly two decades and, in much of the country, never recovered their peak — some metropolitan land is still worth a fraction of its 1990 value more than thirty years later. An owner who bought at the top and held "for the long run" did not get bailed out by time; the appreciation term that justifies owning was *negative* for a generation. Meanwhile, a Japanese renter who invested the difference in global equities did fine. Japan is the proof that $g$ is genuinely uncertain over horizons that matter for a housing decision, and that "just hold long enough" is not a guarantee — it's a hope that happens to have worked in most US decades and emphatically did not work in Japan's.

**The price-to-income trap in Vietnam.** Price-to-rent measures owning vs renting; a sister ratio, **price-to-income** (years of median income to buy a home), measures pure affordability. By that yardstick HCMC sits around 32 years and Hanoi around 31 — among the highest on Earth, well above New York's ~10 or London's ~12. These two ratios usually move together because both rise when prices outrun the local economy. For most HCMC households the price-to-income number is so high that buying isn't merely the worse *financial* deal — it's barely reachable at all without extreme leverage or family wealth. That's the deeper reason the rent-and-invest path deserves a fair hearing in Vietnam: for many people it's not a second-best compromise, it's the only path to building wealth that doesn't require betting everything on one leveraged, illiquid apartment.

**The 2021–23 US rate whipsaw — the same house, two different verdicts.** Mortgage rates are the hidden third variable that can flip the answer overnight. The US 30-year fixed hit a record-low 2.65% in January 2021 and then nearly tripled to ~7.79% by October 2023. At 2.65%, the interest term in the user-cost formula is tiny and owning wins almost everywhere; at 7.79%, the same house at the same price has a far higher user cost and renting wins in many more places — without the house changing at all. This is why a rent-vs-buy verdict has a *shelf life*: it depends on today's mortgage rate, and when rates move, the right answer moves with them. (For how rates ripple through every asset including housing, see [the liquidity cycle and asset prices](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-liquidity-cycle-and-asset-prices).)

## When this matters / Further reading

This decision touches almost everyone, almost always at the moment of least information — you're getting married, having a kid, starting a job in a new city, and a relative is telling you that renting is for fools. The single most valuable thing you can do is replace the slogan with two numbers. First, compute the **price-to-rent ratio** for the specific home: price divided by a year of equivalent rent. Below 15, buying probably makes sense; above 25, renting and investing the difference probably wins; in between, it turns on how long you'll stay. Second, estimate the **user cost of ownership** — interest plus opportunity cost plus maintenance plus tax plus amortised transaction friction, minus expected appreciation — and compare it honestly to a year of rent. If you're in HCMC or Hanoi, brace yourself: the math will likely tell you that owning is the more expensive way to obtain the same shelter, and that the renter who invests the difference is not the fool of the story.

None of this means "never buy." It means buy *with your eyes open* — knowing your break-even horizon, knowing what you give up, and choosing to pay any premium for the genuine non-financial benefits (security, control, forced saving) deliberately rather than because a slogan told you renting was waste. The worst reason to make the biggest financial decision of your life is a five-word cliché. The best reason is two numbers and a clear head about what you actually want.
