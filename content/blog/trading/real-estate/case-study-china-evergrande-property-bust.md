> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — China's property bust is not one company failing; it is an entire growth model breaking at national scale. Developers built a presale-plus-debt empire on the belief that prices only rise; local governments funded themselves by selling land to those developers; the whole edifice ran on leverage. When Beijing's **three red lines** (Aug 2020) finally capped developer debt, the presale-funded machine seized.
>
> - **Two addictions, one machine.** Developers were addicted to cheap money (bonds, bank loans, and the interest-free **presale float** — deposits from buyers for homes that did not yet exist). Local governments were addicted to **land finance** — selling land-use rights to those same developers funded a large share of their budgets. Each fed the other.
> - **The three red lines were the trigger, not the cause.** The cause was years of leverage on the belief prices only rise. The caps just removed the new debt that kept the old debt rolling. Evergrande — with liabilities around **US\$300 billion** — defaulted in December 2021, and millions of pre-sold, half-built homes stalled.
> - **It is a slow-motion bust, not a 2008-style crash.** No US-style securitization spread the losses worldwide; the state controls the banks and the timetable. The price is paid as years of deflation and a fiscal hole at the local level, not a single panic.
> - **The one case to remember:** this is the live, national-scale version of the [developer business model](/blog/trading/real-estate/the-developer-business-model) and the [credit cycle](/blog/trading/real-estate/the-credit-cycle-and-property-prices) — and the closest large-economy analogue to Vietnam's own 2022 freeze.

In the summer of 2021, a young couple in the Chinese city of Wuhan signed for an apartment in a tower that existed only as a render and a pit in the ground. Call them Wei and Lan. They paid a deposit, then took out a mortgage to pay the developer in installments tied to "construction progress." The developer's name was on glossy billboards across the country: Evergrande. By winter, the cranes on their site had gone still. Wei and Lan were now doing something strange and frightening — paying a mortgage every month, with interest, on a home that was not being built, sold to them by a company that owed more money than the entire economy of a mid-sized country. Their savings, their parents' savings, and a 30-year debt were all tied to a concrete shell that might never have a roof.

Multiply Wei and Lan by several million households, spread across hundreds of cities, and you have the shape of the largest property bust in history. It is easy to read the headlines — "Evergrande defaults on US\$300 billion," "China's Lehman moment," "ghost cities" — and come away thinking a single reckless company blew up. That is not what happened. Evergrande was the most extreme expression of a *model* that an entire nation ran for two decades: developers building with borrowed money and buyers' deposits, local governments funding themselves by selling land to those developers, and everyone betting that property prices in a fast-growing country could only rise. Evergrande was simply the first big piece to fall when Beijing finally pulled the leverage out from under the machine.

This post takes that machine apart, piece by piece, and shows exactly how it broke. We will build every concept from the ground up: how China grew through property, how local governments became dependent on land sales, how the developer presale model works (and why it is so fragile), what the "three red lines" actually did, why Evergrande's roughly US\$300 billion of liabilities were so dangerous, why stranded buyers started refusing to pay their mortgages, and why this is a slow-motion deflation rather than a sudden 2008-style crash. The diagram above is the mental model for the whole story: a loop in which local governments sell land, developers buy it with debt and presell off-plan, build, deliver, and recycle the cash into the next, bigger land purchase — a loop that ran beautifully for twenty years and then jammed all at once.

![A six step loop showing local government selling land, a developer buying it with debt, preselling homes off plan, building, delivering, and recycling the cash back into the next land purchase, the loop that seized when credit was cut](/imgs/blogs/case-study-china-evergrande-property-bust-1.png)

A note before we begin: this is educational, not investment advice, and not a political commentary. The goal is to make the *mechanism* legible — how the machine made money, when and why it broke, and who pays — not to tell you what to buy, sell, or believe about China. Numbers like Evergrande's "US\$300 billion" and the composition of its debt are widely reported figures rather than audited certainties; where a number is a round illustration of a magnitude rather than a precise audited fact, I flag it as illustrative.

## Foundations: the China property machine, built from zero

To understand why China's bust matters and how it differs from the busts you may already know, we have to build the machine from its parts. There are three players, and we will follow one of each through the whole post. Meet **Evergrande-style Developer** — a large, aggressive property company we will call **the Developer** — which buys land, borrows heavily, and presells homes. Meet **Wei and Lan**, our off-plan homebuyers, who hand over savings and take on a mortgage for an apartment that does not exist yet. And meet **the Local Government** — a city or county administration that sells land to the Developer and uses the proceeds to fund its budget. These three are locked together in a single loop, and you cannot understand any one of them without the other two.

### Growth via property: why building things became the engine

Start with the strangest fact about modern China: for roughly two decades, building and selling real estate was not just *a* part of the economy — it was, directly and indirectly, something like a quarter to nearly a third of all economic activity. Construction, the steel and cement and glass that feed it, the appliances and furniture that fill finished homes, the land sales that fund local government, the bank lending against property, the wealth effect that makes homeowners feel rich enough to spend — bundle all of that together and property became the single largest driver of Chinese growth.

Why did this happen? Because property is uniquely good at *absorbing investment*. When a country wants to grow fast, it needs somewhere to put enormous amounts of capital, labour, and steel, and somewhere for ordinary households to store their savings. Building cities does all of it at once. It employs migrant workers by the tens of millions, consumes the output of state-owned steel and cement giants, gives local governments revenue, gives banks collateral to lend against, and gives families an asset they trust more than the stock market. For two decades, China poured its savings and its labour into concrete, and the concrete grew the economy, and the growing economy made the concrete worth more — a self-reinforcing loop that looked, for a very long time, like a perpetual-motion machine.

The trouble with using property as the growth engine is that it only works while you are *adding* to the stock at an accelerating pace. A machine that runs on building more, selling more, and lending more against property has no stable resting state. The day construction stops accelerating, every part of the machine that depended on the acceleration — the steelmakers, the local budgets, the banks, the developers, the wealth effect — feels it at once. China spent two decades building the engine. This post is about the day the acceleration stopped.

### Local-government land finance: the fiscal addiction

Here is the piece foreigners most often miss, and it is essential. In China, urban land is owned by the state. Households and developers do not buy land outright; they buy **land-use rights** — a long lease, typically 70 years for residential use. And the entity that sells those rights is the **Local Government**.

This created one of the most consequential fiscal arrangements in modern economics. Chinese local governments were handed large spending responsibilities — building roads, schools, water, transit, and servicing their own debts — but a relatively small slice of the country's tax revenue. They needed another funding source, and they found it under their feet: **land finance**. A local government would assemble a parcel, clear it, and auction the land-use rights to developers at the highest price the market would bear. In boom years, those land-sale proceeds funded a very large share — often cited at roughly a third or more — of a local government's total budget.

Think about what that means. The Local Government is not a neutral referee in the property market; it is a *participant* with a direct financial stake in high land prices. The more developers paid for land, the more the city could spend. So local governments had every incentive to keep the boom going: to keep selling land, to keep prices rising, to keep developers bidding. And to bid, developers needed to keep borrowing — which links the second addiction directly to the first.

There is a further wrinkle that matters for the bust. Many local governments did not just *spend* the land money; they *borrowed against future land sales* through off-balance-sheet entities called **local-government financing vehicles (LGFVs)** — companies the local government controls, which borrow from banks and bond markets and invest in infrastructure, with future land revenue as the implicit backstop. So when land sales fell, two things broke at once: this year's budget lost a third of its revenue, *and* the debt taken out against next year's land sales suddenly looked unbacked. We will return to this fiscal hole; for now, hold the key idea: **the Local Government was financially hooked on selling land to the Developer.**

### The developer presale model: building with the buyer's money

Now the Developer. A Chinese property developer is not primarily a builder — the pouring of concrete is subcontracted to construction firms. The developer is a *financial machine* that does four things: it buys land-use rights with borrowed money, it presells the unbuilt homes to raise cash, it builds, and it recycles the proceeds into the next, larger land purchase. (If this sounds familiar, it is the universal pattern dissected in [the developer business model](/blog/trading/real-estate/the-developer-business-model) — China just ran it bigger and faster than anyone.)

The clever, dangerous heart of it is the **presale**. The Developer sells apartments *before they are built* — off the renderings, off a hole in the ground. Buyers like Wei and Lan sign a contract, often take out a mortgage immediately, and pay the Developer in stages. By handover, a buyer may have paid most or all of the price for a home that, until that moment, exists only on paper. In China, off-plan presales were the dominant way new homes were sold — the large majority of the market.

Why does the Developer love presales? Because the buyers' deposits are the cheapest financing on Earth. Economists call money you collect and use before you have to deliver the thing it paid for a **float** — the insurer's premiums-before-claims, the gift-card balance a shop holds before you spend it. The presale float is an *interest-free loan from the customer*: Wei and Lan's mortgage money sits in the Developer's hands for two or three years, funding construction, and the Developer pays no interest on it. Stack that float on top of bank loans and bonds, and the Developer can control projects worth many times its own equity. In a boom, that leverage turns a modest building margin into a spectacular return on the Developer's own money — and the recycle (deliver, book the profit, buy more land) compounds it.

But notice the risk transfer hidden in the float. When Wei and Lan prepay, they take on **construction risk** (the building might not get finished), **delivery risk** (it might be late or worse than promised), and **counterparty risk** (the Developer might go bankrupt with their money). And critically, in China the presale money was often *not* ring-fenced for the specific building it paid for. It flowed into the Developer's general pot and was used to buy more land, service other debt, or finish *other* projects. The presale float is the Developer's cheapest capital precisely because it offloads the project's risk onto people like Wei and Lan — who are, in effect, unsecured lenders to a leveraged company, except they also signed a 30-year mortgage to fund the loan.

### The belief that powered all of it: prices only rise

Every piece of this machine — the Developer's leverage, the Local Government's land finance, the buyer's willingness to prepay and take a mortgage on a hole in the ground, the bank's willingness to lend — rested on a single shared belief: **Chinese property prices only go up.** For two decades, that belief was self-fulfilling. Rising prices made leverage safe (the collateral kept appreciating), made presales safe (the finished home would be worth more than you paid), made land finance safe (next year's land would sell for more), and made the mortgage safe (you were buying an appreciating asset).

A belief that everyone acts on becomes true for as long as everyone keeps acting on it. The danger is that it is also *reflexive*: the moment enough participants doubt it, the actions reverse, and the doubt becomes self-fulfilling too. Falling prices make leverage dangerous, presales dangerous, land finance dangerous, and the mortgage a trap. This is the deep reason property busts are so violent: the same belief that drove the boom, running in reverse, drives the bust. China's machine was a monument to "prices only rise." The three red lines were the moment the state itself decided to stop feeding that belief — and discovered how much of the economy was standing on it.

### Moral hazard and the implicit guarantee

One last foundation. For years, investors lent to giant developers and to LGFVs at low interest rates partly because they assumed the state would never let a systemically important borrower fail — that there was an **implicit guarantee** behind the debt. When lenders believe a borrower is "too big to fail" and will be bailed out, they underprice the risk and lend too cheaply, which lets the borrower take on far more debt than is safe. Economists call this **moral hazard**: the protection against failure encourages the very recklessness that makes failure likely.

The implicit guarantee is why Evergrande could borrow so enormously for so long. And the deliberate puncturing of that guarantee — Beijing signalling that it would *not* simply bail out reckless developers — is a large part of what the three red lines were really about. The state wanted to deflate the bubble and break the moral hazard *without* triggering a 2008-style panic. Whether that is possible is the central question of the whole episode. With the players and beliefs in place, let us watch the machine run — starting with how the Developer grew so big.

## How developers grew on presales and debt

The Developer's growth was not powered by patiently retained profits. It was powered by *velocity* and *leverage* — the speed at which it recycled capital, multiplied by the borrowed money and free float it stacked on top of thin equity. To feel why Chinese developers ballooned to a scale the world had never seen, you have to put numbers on the recycle.

Walk the loop once. The Developer takes a slice of equity, borrows several times that amount in bank loans and bonds, and buys land-use rights from the Local Government. It immediately starts preselling the unbuilt homes, collecting buyers' deposits — the interest-free float — which fund most of the construction. The construction firm builds; the Developer delivers; the buyers pay the balance; the Developer books a profit and gets its equity back. Then it takes the original equity, plus the profit, plus the larger borrowing capacity its bigger balance sheet now supports, and buys an even larger parcel of land. Each turn of the loop is faster and bigger than the last.

Two engines compound here. The first is the development *margin* — the profit on each project, which adds to equity. The second, and far more powerful, is *velocity* — how fast the capital recycles. A developer that turns its capital every two years, earning a respectable margin each turn, and stacking the presale float and bank leverage on top, is not earning that margin; it is earning something far higher on its own money, again and again. This is how a regional builder became a national colossus in a single decade. It is also why the whole thing depended, utterly, on the loop *continuing* — on sales never stopping, on land prices never falling, on the next loan always being available to roll the last one.

#### Worked example: the leverage inside one presold project

The Developer launches a project to build 1,000 apartments, each selling for ¥1,000,000 — roughly \$140,000 apiece at about 7.2 yuan to the dollar. The total the project will sell for, its **gross development value**, is therefore ¥1,000,000,000 (¥1 billion, or about \$140 million).

The costs: the land-use rights cost ¥250 million (25% of the sale value), construction ¥500 million (50%), and financing plus marketing ¥150 million (15%). Total cost ¥900 million; the Developer's margin is ¥100 million, or 10% of sales.

Now watch the leverage on the Developer's *own* money. It funds this project with just ¥100 million of equity. The land was bought with a bank loan; construction was funded mostly by **presale deposits** — by handover, buyers had prepaid roughly 80% of the price, about ¥800 million of interest-free customer money, which covered the entire construction cost and more. The Developer paid almost no interest, because the float is not a loan. So a ¥100 million profit on ¥100 million of equity is a **100% return** for the project — and recycled into the next, bigger project every two years, that compounds into the kind of growth that builds a national empire.

*The presale float and bank leverage let a developer earn a 100% return on a 10% margin — which is exactly why Chinese developers chased land and presales as fast as the credit pipe would let them, and exactly why a 10% fall in prices could wipe the whole thing out.*

Feel the fragility hiding in that example. The margin was ¥100 million on ¥1 billion of sales — a 10% cushion on a stack of mostly fixed costs. If apartment prices fall 10%, the ¥1 billion in sales becomes ¥900 million, but the ¥900 million of costs is already locked in. The margin vanishes entirely. A 20% fall and the project is deeply underwater, with the Developer's ¥100 million of equity gone and bondholders and presale buyers staring at a loss. The same thin-margin, high-leverage structure that produced the miraculous boom returns guarantees a wipeout on a moderate price fall. That is not a flaw the Developer could fix; it is the model.

## Local-government land finance: the fiscal addiction

We met land finance in the foundations; now let us see it as the second engine of the machine, locked to the first. The Local Government and the Developer are not separate stories — they are two gears in the same gearbox. The Developer borrows to buy land; the Local Government sells the land and spends the proceeds; the spending fuels growth; the growth supports more borrowing and more building. Each gear turns the other.

![A five step flow showing local government selling land use rights to developers, the revenue funding the local budget, paying for roads schools and debt, then developers stopping their land purchases in the bust and the land revenue collapsing into a fiscal hole](/imgs/blogs/case-study-china-evergrande-property-bust-5.png)

The figure traces the dependency. Land-use rights are sold to developers; the revenue funds the local budget; the budget pays for roads, schools, and — crucially — services the debt that LGFVs took on against *future* land sales. As long as developers keep bidding for land at rising prices, the whole structure is solvent and self-reinforcing. The danger is entirely on the right side of the figure: when developers stop buying land — because they are deleveraging, or defaulting, or because nervous buyers have stopped buying apartments so developers no longer need new land — the land revenue collapses. And it collapses into a *hole*, because the budget had come to depend on it and the LGFV debt was issued against it.

This is the great second-order damage of China's bust, and it is why the pain spread far beyond a few developers. The Developer's failure was not contained within the property sector. It cut the Local Government's single largest discretionary revenue source, simultaneously made the LGFV debt taken against that revenue look shaky, and forced cities to cut spending or scramble for alternative funding at the very moment the local economy needed support. The land-finance gear, which had powered two decades of local investment, ran in reverse.

#### Worked example: the land-sale revenue hole

A mid-sized Chinese city budgets ¥120 billion of spending for the year — roads, schools, salaries, transit, and the interest on its LGFV debt. In the boom, it funded this from two main sources: ordinary tax and transfers of ¥80 billion, and **land sales of ¥40 billion** — fully a third of the budget — raised by auctioning land-use rights to developers.

Then the bust arrives. Developers, deleveraging under the three red lines and frightened by collapsing apartment sales, stop bidding for new land. The city's land auctions fail or clear at half the price. Land revenue falls from ¥40 billion to, say, ¥15 billion — a **¥25 billion hole**, more than a fifth of the entire budget, blown open in a single year.

Now it gets worse. The city's LGFV had borrowed ¥60 billion from banks and bondholders to build infrastructure, on the implicit assumption that future land sales would service it. With land revenue down by ¥25 billion a year, that debt is suddenly hard to service. The city must choose between slashing spending (cutting the very investment that supported the local economy), squeezing other revenue, or rolling the LGFV debt and praying land sales recover.

*A government that funds a third of its budget by selling land to developers has tied its solvency to the property boom — so when developers stop buying, the fiscal hole is as dangerous as the developers' own debt, and far harder to see.*

This is the part of China's bust that has no clean analogue in the US or Vietnam, and it is why the workout is so slow and so political. You cannot simply let the property market clear at a market price, because the price of land *is* local-government revenue. Letting land prices fall to where they would clear means blowing fiscal holes in hundreds of city budgets at once. The state is not only managing a property bust; it is managing the funding model of its own local governments. That entanglement is a major reason Beijing chose a slow, managed deflation rather than a fast crash — a choice we will return to.

## The three red lines: capping the leverage

By 2020, Beijing could see the machine for what it had become: a tower of developer debt, local-government land dependence, and household savings, all balanced on the belief that prices only rise. Policymakers had been warning for years that "houses are for living in, not for speculation." In August 2020, they finally acted on the developers directly, with a policy that became known as the **three red lines**.

The three red lines were three caps on a developer's balance sheet — three financial-health tests that a developer had to pass to be allowed to take on *more* debt. Stated simply:

1. **Liabilities to assets** (excluding advance receipts) must be **below 70%.** This caps how much of the developer's balance sheet is funded by debt rather than equity.
2. **Net debt to equity** must be **below 100%.** This caps how much net borrowing the developer carries relative to its own capital.
3. **Cash to short-term debt** must be **at least 1.0** — the developer must hold enough cash to cover the debt coming due within a year.

The mechanism was elegantly brutal. For each red line a developer breached, its *permitted growth in debt* was throttled. Breach none, and you could grow your interest-bearing debt by up to 15% a year. Breach one, 10%. Breach two, 5%. Breach all three — as the most reckless developers, including Evergrande, did — and you were **not allowed to increase your debt at all**.

![A before and after comparison of a developer's leverage, on the left unconstrained borrowing to buy land and roll debt, on the right the three red lines capping debt to assets, net debt to equity, and short term cash coverage, so missing a line means no new debt and funding seizes](/imgs/blogs/case-study-china-evergrande-property-bust-3.png)

The figure puts the two regimes side by side. On the left, the world before August 2020: a developer borrows freely to buy more land, carries liabilities far above 70% of assets, and — most importantly — **rolls its old debt with new debt**. This rolling is the key. Highly leveraged developers do not repay their bonds and loans; they *refinance* them. When a ¥10 billion bond matures, they issue a new ¥10 billion bond to pay it off. The debt never really shrinks; it just gets rolled forward, year after year, as long as the credit market stays open.

On the right, the world after the red lines: a developer that breaches all three is forbidden from increasing its debt. And here is the trap. If you cannot *increase* your debt, you cannot roll a maturing bond with a *larger* new one — and for a company whose entire model depended on continuous refinancing, even being unable to grow the debt pile means the refinancing tap is choked. The maturing debt comes due, and the new money to pay it is not there. The developer must find the cash from operations — from selling apartments. But the very announcement that the giants are in trouble makes buyers nervous, so apartment sales *also* fall, just when the developer needs them most. Both pipes — new debt and presales — narrow at the same time. The funding seizes.

The three red lines did not *cause* the bust in the way a spark causes a fire. The fuel — two decades of leverage on the belief that prices only rise — was already piled high. The red lines were the moment the state stopped throwing on more fuel and, in fact, started removing it. For a machine that only worked while the debt and the presales kept flowing, removing the new debt was enough to make the whole thing stall. The most leveraged developer in the country was the first to feel it.

#### Worked example: a developer forced to deleverage

The Developer enters 2020 with ¥2,000 billion of assets and ¥1,600 billion of total liabilities — an 80% liabilities-to-assets ratio, well above the 70% red line. Its net debt is roughly equal to its equity twice over, breaching the 100% red line. And it holds only ¥100 billion of cash against ¥300 billion of debt maturing within the year — a cash-to-short-term-debt ratio of just 0.33, far below the required 1.0. It breaches **all three** red lines.

Under the policy, the Developer may not increase its interest-bearing debt at all. So when ¥300 billion of debt matures over the next year, it cannot simply issue ¥300 billion of new bonds to roll it — that would grow the debt, which is forbidden, and in any case the bond market, now spooked, will not buy. The Developer must repay the ¥300 billion from cash and sales. It has ¥100 billion of cash. It needs ¥200 billion more, fast, from selling apartments — but apartment buyers, reading the headlines, are pulling back, so sales are falling, not rising.

To raise cash, the Developer slashes prices on finished units (angering earlier buyers and accelerating the price decline), sells half-finished projects and land at fire-sale prices, and stops buying new land entirely (blowing the Local Government's fiscal hole). None of it is enough to cover ¥200 billion in a market that has turned against it. The refinancing it relied on for a decade is gone, and there is no time to deleverage gracefully.

*The three red lines did not bankrupt the developer by taking its money; they bankrupted it by taking away the new debt it needed to roll the old debt — and a company that survives only by refinancing dies the moment refinancing stops.*

## Evergrande: the US\$300bn default

Now we can tell the Evergrande story properly, because it is just this machine running to its extreme and then breaking. Evergrande had grown into one of the largest property developers in the world by running the recycle harder than anyone: borrowing more, preselling more, buying more land, diversifying into electric vehicles, theme parks, bottled water, and even a football club — all stacked on a mountain of debt. It breached all three red lines. When the refinancing tap closed, it was the most exposed company in the most exposed sector of the most property-dependent major economy on Earth.

The headline number was staggering: total liabilities of roughly **US\$300 billion** (around ¥2 trillion+). But that headline hides the most important fact about the default — *who* Evergrande owed the money to. A US\$300 billion default sounds like a banking problem. It was something more human and more dangerous than that.

![A nested stack showing Evergrande's roughly three hundred billion dollar liability pile broken into buyer presale deposits, supplier and contractor IOUs, bonds, and bank loans, all backed by stalled assets of half built towers and a frozen land bank](/imgs/blogs/case-study-china-evergrande-property-bust-2.png)

The figure breaks the pile into its layers (the splits are illustrative of the widely reported composition, not audited exact figures). Only a *minority* of Evergrande's roughly US\$300 billion was the bonds and bank loans that financial markets obsess over. The two largest layers were something else entirely. The first was **buyer presale deposits** — money from over a million households who had prepaid for apartments that were not yet built. The second was **supplier and contractor IOUs** — unpaid bills owed to the construction firms, materials suppliers, and the migrant workers who built the towers. Beneath it all, the supposed backing was **stalled assets**: half-finished buildings worth a fraction of their cost while frozen, and a vast land bank that was falling in value and could not be sold.

This composition is why the Evergrande default was a social and political event, not just a financial one. When a normal company defaults on bonds, sophisticated investors take a loss they understood was possible. When Evergrande defaulted, the people holding the largest claims were *ordinary families who had prepaid for homes* and *small suppliers and workers who were owed wages*. The default did not hit Wall Street; it hit Wei and Lan, and the man who poured their concrete. That is what made it explosive — and what made the state's response so cautious.

#### Worked example: where the US\$300bn actually goes

Take Evergrande's roughly US\$300 billion of liabilities and split it into the rough buckets that have been widely reported (illustrative magnitudes, not audited figures):

- **Buyer presale deposits — about US\$80 billion.** Money from well over a million homebuyers who prepaid for unbuilt apartments. This is not a loan from a bank; it is the life savings of families, owed as *finished homes*, not cash.
- **Supplier and contractor IOUs — about US\$80 billion.** Unpaid bills to construction companies, cement and steel suppliers, and the workers on the sites. Many of these are small firms that themselves fail when Evergrande does not pay, spreading the damage down the chain.
- **Bonds — about US\$50 billion.** Domestic and offshore bonds held by funds and institutional investors. The offshore dollar bonds are the ones global headlines tracked, but they are a *minority* of the total.
- **Bank loans — about US\$40 billion.** Loans from Chinese banks, secured against projects and land.
- **Other liabilities — the remainder**, including various payables and obligations across a sprawling group.

Add the visible chunks: \$80bn + \$80bn + \$50bn + \$40bn = \$250 billion, with the rest spread across the group — landing in the neighbourhood of the US\$300 billion headline. The single most important line is the first one: the largest single category of what Evergrande owed was *homes it had been paid for and had not built.*

*Evergrande's US\$300 billion was not mostly a debt to bankers; the biggest slice was a debt to families, owed in the form of finished apartments — which is why its collapse stranded millions of homebuyers and could not be resolved by a simple financial bailout.*

Evergrande was formally labelled in default by international rating agencies in **December 2021**, after it failed to make payments on its offshore dollar bonds. Years of restructuring talks followed, and in **early 2024** a Hong Kong court issued a liquidation order against the group. But the liquidation of a company whose main assets are half-built towers full of families' deposits is nothing like the liquidation of a normal firm — you cannot simply sell the assets and pay the creditors, because the "assets" are homes people have already paid for and are waiting to live in. The default was the beginning of a years-long, household-by-household workout, not a clean ending.

## Stranded buyers and the mortgage-boycott risk

The most distinctly Chinese — and most socially dangerous — feature of this bust is what happened to people like Wei and Lan. To see it, follow the chain from the default to the family.

![A flow showing that when a developer defaults its cash runs out and construction halts, leaving pre sold homes unfinished, which pushes stranded buyers toward stopping their mortgage payments in a boycott](/imgs/blogs/case-study-china-evergrande-property-bust-4.png)

The figure traces the mechanism. When the Developer defaults, two things happen at once: its cash runs out (the presale deposits were spent on land and other debts, not ring-fenced for the building), and construction halts (the suppliers and workers stop, because they are not being paid). Both lead to the same place: the pre-sold homes are left unfinished. And then comes the response that turned a property bust into a social crisis: stranded buyers, paying mortgages every month on apartments that are not being built, began to **stop paying**.

This is the **mortgage boycott**, and you have to understand the buyer's position to see why it happened. Recall that in China, the buyer usually takes out the mortgage and starts paying it *before* the home is delivered — during construction. So a buyer like Wei and Lan can be three years into mortgage payments, with interest, on a concrete shell that has stood untouched for two years, sold by a company that has defaulted and may never finish it. From the buyer's chair, the deal is grotesque: you are sending money every month to a bank, which already paid the defaulted developer, for a home that does not exist and may never exist. In the summer of 2022, buyers across dozens of cities and hundreds of stalled projects began publicly refusing to pay their mortgages until construction resumed.

Why is a mortgage boycott so dangerous? Because it threatens to jump the crisis from the *developers* to the *banks*. As long as households keep paying their mortgages, the banks are insulated — the bad debt sits with the developers, not the lenders. But a wave of mortgage non-payment turns performing home loans into bad loans on the banks' books, threatening the one part of the system the state most needs to protect. The boycott was, in effect, the stranded buyers discovering the one lever that forced the authorities to act — and the authorities responded by pressing developers and local governments to **finish the buildings**, prioritising delivery of pre-sold homes ("ensuring delivery of homes" became an official watchword) precisely to keep families paying their mortgages and keep the contagion away from the banks.

#### Worked example: a deposit stranded on an unfinished tower

Wei and Lan buy a ¥2,000,000 apartment (about \$280,000) off-plan from the Developer in 2021. They pay a 30% deposit of ¥600,000 from their and their parents' savings, and take a mortgage of ¥1,400,000 from a bank to pay the rest, which the bank disburses to the Developer in stages tied to construction milestones. Their mortgage payment is about ¥7,500 a month.

By late 2021, the Developer has defaulted. Construction on their tower — which had reached perhaps the 12th floor — stops. Wei and Lan have paid their ¥600,000 deposit and roughly a year of mortgage payments, about ¥90,000, for a total of ¥690,000 (around \$96,000) sunk into an apartment that is a bare concrete frame. And the bank, which already disbursed most of the ¥1,400,000 to the Developer, still expects ¥7,500 every month for the next 29 years.

Do the grim arithmetic from their side. If they keep paying, they send ¥7,500 a month for decades for a home that may never be finished. If they stop, the bank can pursue them and wreck their credit. If they walk away, they lose the entire ¥690,000 they have already put in. There is no good option — which is exactly why, faced with that trap across millions of households, the boycott erupted and the state scrambled to get the towers finished.

*When a buyer pays the mortgage during construction and the developer fails, the buyer is trapped paying for a home that does not exist — and a few million such traps, all sprung at once, is how a developer's bankruptcy becomes a threat to the banking system.*

## Why it's a slow-motion bust, not a 2008-style crash

When Evergrande defaulted, Western commentators reached instinctively for the comparison they knew: "China's Lehman moment," a 2008-style financial crash about to ripple through the world. Years later, that crash has not come — not because the situation is mild, but because China's bust is *shaped differently*. It is a slow, grinding, managed deflation rather than a sudden financial panic. Understanding why requires comparing the plumbing of the two crises.

![A timeline showing the three red lines capping developer debt in August 2020, Evergrande defaulting in December 2021, new home sales collapsing and mortgage boycotts spreading in 2022, and a managed workout of restructuring and home delivery from 2023 onward](/imgs/blogs/case-study-china-evergrande-property-bust-6.png)

The timeline shows the *pace*. The trigger (red lines) came in 2020; the marquee default a full year later; the broad sales collapse and mortgage boycotts a year after that; and the managed workout has stretched across the years since. There was no single day when the system seized and markets froze worldwide. Instead, each stage arrived roughly a year apart, the pain spreading slowly through developers, then buyers, then local governments, then the broader economy — a deflation playing out over years.

Three structural differences explain the slow motion. The first is **securitization, or rather its absence.** The 2008 US crisis was so explosive because American mortgages were bundled into securities (mortgage-backed securities, or MBS) and sold to investors all over the world, with layers of derivatives stacked on top. When US house prices fell, the losses detonated inside the global financial system, and nobody knew who held the losing end, so credit froze everywhere at once. China did not securitize its mortgages this way. The bad property debt sits mostly on the balance sheets of *Chinese* banks and *Chinese* developers — concentrated and domestic, not sliced up and spread worldwide. There is no global web of derivatives to transmit a sudden panic.

The second is **state control of the banks and the timetable.** China's major banks are state-controlled, the largest developers operate under intense state influence, and the regulators can simply *order* a slow workout: forbearance on debts, instructions to keep lending to finish projects, restructuring on the state's schedule rather than the market's. In a market system, a default forces immediate price discovery — assets are dumped, losses are crystallised, and the crash happens fast. In China's system, the state can refuse to let the market clear, stretching the losses over many years to avoid a panic. The cost is that the deflation drags on; the benefit is that there is no single catastrophic moment.

The third is the **fiscal and political stake**. Because local-government revenue is tied to land prices, the state cannot let property fully crash without blowing up its own local finances. So it manages the decline — supporting prices enough to prevent collapse, but not enough to reflate the bubble. The result is years of stagnation: prices grind lower, sales stay weak, developers slowly restructure, and confidence is rebuilt apartment by apartment. It is the property equivalent of letting the air out of a balloon slowly rather than popping it.

#### Worked example: the same loss, fast crash versus slow deflation

Imagine the property sector must ultimately absorb a loss of, say, ¥10 trillion in overvalued assets — bad developer debt, overpriced land, unfinishable projects. The question is not *whether* the loss is taken but *how fast*, and the difference is everything.

**The 2008-style fast crash.** The loss is recognised almost at once. Assets are marked down, banks that securitized and sold the debt discover the losses are everywhere, credit freezes because nobody trusts anybody's balance sheet, and the financial system seizes in a matter of weeks. The ¥10 trillion is absorbed in a sharp, terrifying, globally transmitted shock — followed, often, by a violent but relatively quick recovery once the bad debt is cleared.

**The China-style slow deflation.** The same ¥10 trillion loss is recognised over, say, a decade. The state orders banks to extend and pretend, keeps the largest developers on life support, finishes the pre-sold homes to keep families paying, and lets land prices and developer balance sheets deflate slowly. There is no single panic and no global contagion. But the cost is a *long* hangover — years of weak growth in the once-dominant property sector, a generation of households poorer than they expected to be, and an economy that must find a new engine because the old one is being wound down rather than rebooted. Japan after 1990 is the cautionary template: a slow-motion property deflation that stretched into a "lost decade" and beyond.

*The total loss is roughly the same either way; the choice is between a fast, violent, globally contagious crash and a slow, managed, domestically contained deflation — and China, with state-controlled banks and land-dependent local finances, chose the slow grind.*

## Common misconceptions

China's property bust is one of the most misunderstood economic stories of the decade, partly because outsiders reach for the wrong analogies. Here are the beliefs that most often lead people astray.

**"China will just bail it out — the state has unlimited money."** This misreads what the state is trying to do. Beijing deliberately *triggered* the deflation with the three red lines precisely to break the moral hazard — the belief that reckless developers would always be rescued. A full bailout of a company like Evergrande would reward exactly the recklessness the policy was designed to punish, and would re-inflate the bubble the state wants to deflate. So the response has been selective, not unlimited: protect the *homebuyers* (finish the pre-sold homes), protect the *banks* (keep mortgages performing), but let the *developers' equity and bondholders take losses*. The state has the capacity to prevent a 2008-style crash; it has chosen *not* to use that capacity to save the developers, because saving them would defeat the purpose. "They'll just bail it out" misunderstands that the bust was, in part, intentional.

**"It's a Lehman moment — a 2008-style crash is coming."** This was the dominant Western take in 2021, and it has not played out, because the plumbing is different. The losses are not securitized and spread across the global financial system; they sit on domestic balance sheets the state controls. There is no worldwide web of derivatives to transmit a sudden panic, and the state can order a slow workout instead of allowing a fast crash. The danger is real, but its shape is a *slow deflation*, not a sudden seizure. Calling it a "Lehman moment" predicts the wrong kind of crisis — and explains why people who waited for the dramatic 2008-style crash kept being surprised when it did not arrive.

**"It can't affect anyone else — it's China's internal problem."** The contagion is not a 2008-style financial one, but the bust matters globally through a different channel: the *real economy*. China was the world's largest consumer of steel, cement, iron ore, copper, and construction machinery, and a huge share of that demand came from building apartments. When construction collapses, so does demand for those commodities — which hits the countries and companies that export them (Australia's iron ore, Brazil's, the global commodity complex). A weaker Chinese consumer, poorer because their main asset stopped appreciating, buys fewer imports. So the spillover is real, but it travels through *trade and commodities*, not through a financial panic. "It can't affect anyone" mistakes the absence of financial contagion for the absence of any contagion.

**"Evergrande was one bad, fraudulent company."** Evergrande was the most extreme case, and there were genuine governance failures. But the danger is precisely that it was *not* unique — it was the leading edge of a model that hundreds of developers ran. Other giants followed it into distress. Treating Evergrande as a single rotten apple misses the point: the problem was the *orchard* — the whole presale-plus-leverage-plus-land-finance system that an entire industry was built on. The default that mattered was not one company's; it was the model's.

**"The empty apartments mean China overbuilt and the homes are worthless."** The "ghost cities" framing is half right and badly misleading. China did build ahead of demand in some places, and there is genuine oversupply in lower-tier cities. But much of the unfinished-homes problem is the opposite of oversupply: these are homes that buyers *want*, have *paid for*, and are *waiting to live in* — stalled not because nobody wants them but because the developer ran out of money mid-build. The crisis is less "too many homes nobody wants" and more "homes people paid for that did not get finished." Conflating the two leads to the wrong policy conclusion: the fix that matters most is finishing the pre-sold towers, not bulldozing empty ones.

## How it shows up in real markets

The clearest way to see what is universal and what is specifically Chinese about this bust is to lay it next to the other great property busts of recent history. The mechanism rhymes; the plumbing differs.

![A grid comparing China 2021, Vietnam 2022, and US 2008 across developer leverage, off plan presales, securitized mortgages, and state control, showing developer leverage and presales are shared while securitization and state resolution differ](/imgs/blogs/case-study-china-evergrande-property-bust-7.png)

The grid lays the three episodes side by side across four dimensions. Read across each row and the pattern jumps out: developer leverage is present in all three; off-plan presales are central to China and Vietnam but largely absent in the US; securitized mortgages are the US's defining feature and absent in the other two; and state control of the resolution is high in China and Vietnam but low in the US, where the market crashed and the government bailed out banks rather than managing a slow workout. The same developer-model spine runs through all three, but the way the loss is transmitted and resolved is completely different.

**China, 2021 onward — the developer model at maximum scale.** Everything in this post. Developers ran the presale-plus-leverage recycle at a scale no economy had attempted, local governments funded themselves by selling land into it, and households prepaid for unbuilt homes. The three red lines (August 2020) cut the leverage; Evergrande defaulted (December 2021) on roughly US\$300 billion; sales collapsed and mortgage boycotts spread (2022); and a slow, state-managed workout has dragged on since. It is the developer-model fragility from [the developer business model](/blog/trading/real-estate/the-developer-business-model), playing out across an entire continental economy, with the unique twist of land-financed local government as the second engine.

**Vietnam, 2022–23 — the same machine, smaller, and the closest analogue.** Vietnam ran a strikingly similar model: developers building land banks funded heavily by corporate bonds, with construction financed by off-plan presales, all riding a belief in ever-rising prices. The trigger differed in form but not in kind: instead of leverage caps, it was a pair of high-profile arrests — Tân Hoàng Minh in April 2022 and Vạn Thịnh Phát, entangled with Saigon Commercial Bank, in October 2022 — that froze the corporate-bond market. With the bond channel shut, developers could not roll their debt; simultaneously, spooked buyers stopped preselling deposits. Both pipes shut at once, exactly as in China, and the property machine seized nationwide. The State Bank of Vietnam cut policy rates four times in 2023, and Decree 08 granted bond-restructuring relief — the same instinct as Beijing's, to manage the seizure rather than let it crash. The parallel is close enough that China is the best preview of where Vietnam's slow thaw may lead. The full Vietnamese arc is in [Vietnam's property cycles, 2007, 2013, 2022](/blog/trading/real-estate/vietnam-property-cycles-2007-2013-2022).

**The United States, 2005–2008 — same leverage, different transmission.** America's homebuilders ran a leveraged merchant-developer model too, accumulating land banks and building into a seemingly unstoppable boom. The US Case-Shiller national home-price index peaked at 184.6 in July 2006 and fell about 27% to a trough of 134 by February 2012. But the *transmission* was utterly different from China's. US homes were generally sold finished, not presold off-plan, so there was no stranded-buyer crisis. The lethal feature was **securitization**: mortgages bundled into MBS and CDOs and sold worldwide, with derivatives stacked on top, so when prices fell the losses detonated across the *global* financial system and credit froze everywhere at once. The US bust was a fast, globally contagious financial panic resolved by bank bailouts; China's is a slow, domestically contained deflation managed by the state. Same developer leverage; opposite plumbing.

**Japan, after 1990 — the cautionary template for the slow grind.** When Japan's epic 1980s land-and-stock bubble burst in 1990, the developers and banks that had lent against ever-rising land were left holding assets worth a fraction of what they paid. Rather than force a fast clearing, Japan's banks practised forbearance — "extend and pretend" — keeping zombie borrowers alive for years and stretching the losses across more than a decade. The result was the "lost decade" (which became two): years of stagnation, deflation, and weak growth as the property overhang was slowly worked off. Japan is the closest historical match for the *shape* China has chosen — a slow, managed deflation that avoids a sudden crash at the cost of a very long hangover. The fear that haunts China-watchers is precisely that the slow workout becomes a Japanese-style lost decade.

**The contained workout, so far.** The most striking thing about China's bust, years in, is what has *not* happened: no global financial panic, no run on the major Chinese banks, no 2008-style seizure of credit worldwide. The state's strategy of protecting homebuyers and banks while letting developers fail has, so far, kept the crisis from jumping the firewall into the financial system or the world economy in the way 2008 did. What it has not avoided is the slow cost: years of weak property activity, a serious drag on Chinese growth, a generation of households whose main asset stopped delivering the gains they were promised, and the unresolved fiscal hole at the local-government level. The bill is being paid in slow growth, not in a sudden crash — which is exactly the trade the state chose.

## When this matters / Further reading

This matters, first, because it is the largest live experiment in deflating a property bubble without crashing the financial system — and the world's second-largest economy is the laboratory. Whether China pulls off a managed deflation or slides into a Japanese-style lost decade will shape global growth, commodity prices, and the prospects of every country and company that sells into Chinese demand for the next decade. You do not have to own a Chinese apartment to be affected by how this resolves; if you own iron ore miners, sell into the Chinese consumer, or simply care about global growth, the slow grind in Chinese property is one of the most important macro stories of your investing life.

It matters, second, because it is the clearest possible illustration of the mechanisms this series keeps returning to. The developer presale model, the credit cycle, the role of leverage, the second-order damage through public finances, the reflexive belief that "prices only rise" — China ran all of them at maximum scale, and the result is a textbook you can read in real time. If you understood why Evergrande's largest creditors were homebuyers rather than bankers, why the mortgage boycott threatened the banks, and why the three red lines could trigger a national seizure without a single market crash, you understand the deep physics of how property bubbles form and break everywhere.

And it matters, third and most concretely, if you are a buyer in a presale market like Vietnam's. China's stranded buyers are the cautionary tale that should make you ask, before you prepay for an unbuilt home: Is my deposit ring-fenced, or does it flow into the developer's general pot? How leveraged is this developer, and how much of its funding comes from bonds that must be rolled? Am I taking out a mortgage *before* delivery, exposing myself to years of payments on a home that might stall? Those are the questions that separate Wei and Lan's nightmare from a finished apartment.
