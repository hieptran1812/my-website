> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Developers don't build with their own money. They build with a thin slice of equity stacked on **presales** (buyers' deposits) and **debt** (bank loans plus corporate bonds), and that leverage is both the engine of the boom and the fault line of the bust.
>
> - The **developer capital stack** is mostly other people's money: a typical project might be 15% the developer's equity, 40% buyer presale deposits, and 45% bank loan plus bonds. The equity sits at the bottom as the first loss.
> - The **presale model** lets a developer build with the customers' cash — but it shifts construction and delivery risk onto buyers who pay, often 30–70% of the price, for a home that does not exist yet and frequently has no real escrow protecting it.
> - **Leverage cuts both ways.** On a project funded 15% equity, a +10% move in project value is roughly a +67% move in the developer's equity — and a −10% move is roughly −67%. The same math that mints fortunes in a boom detonates them in a credit squeeze.
> - **The one case to remember:** Vietnam's developers leaned heavily on the corporate-**bond** channel. When the Tân Hoàng Minh (Apr-2022) and Vạn Thịnh Phát/SCB (Oct-2022) cases froze that market, bonds couldn't roll, funding gaps opened, and projects stalled nationwide. The SBV cut policy rates four times in 2023 and Decree 08 granted bond-restructuring relief — the textbook case of how developer financing breaks.

In April 2022, the chairman of one of Vietnam's flashiest developers, Tân Hoàng Minh, was arrested on bond-fraud charges. Six months later, in October 2022, the chair of the sprawling Vạn Thịnh Phát empire was arrested in a case entangled with Saigon Commercial Bank (SCB), triggering a bank run. To a homebuyer in Hanoi or Ho Chi Minh City, these looked like distant white-collar scandals — billionaire problems. They were not. Within months, cranes across the country went still. Half-built towers stood quiet. Buyers who had handed over deposits — sometimes hundreds of millions of đồng — for apartments that existed only as renderings discovered that the money to finish their building had simply vanished. The financing had frozen.

This is the part of real estate that nobody explains to the person buying the home. We obsess over location, over price per square metre, over interest rates on our own mortgage. But behind every shiny new development is a financial machine that decides whether the building ever gets finished — and that machine runs on a kind of leverage most buyers never see and never agree to. A developer is not a builder who happens to need a loan. A developer is, first and foremost, a financial engineer: someone who assembles a stack of other people's money, points it at a piece of land, and hopes the building gets done before the money runs out.

The diagram above is the mental model for this whole post: the **developer capital stack**. A finished project is funded by layers — a thin slice of the developer's own equity at the bottom, then buyers' presale deposits, then a bank construction loan, then corporate bonds — and the riskiest layer, the equity, sits underneath everything as the first to be wiped out if anything goes wrong. Once you can see that stack, you can see exactly why developers love it (it magnifies their returns enormously) and exactly why it breaks (any one layer drying up can bring down the whole tower). By the end, you'll understand the presale model that built modern Vietnam, the bond channel that froze it, and the universal mechanics that connect a stalled đất nền project in Đồng Nai to Evergrande in China and to a half-built condo in Miami.

![The developer capital stack showing a thin layer of developer equity at the bottom supporting presale deposits, a bank construction loan, and corporate bonds, with equity marked as the first loss](/imgs/blogs/credit-and-developer-financing-1.png)

A note before we start: this is educational, not investment advice. The goal is to make the machinery legible — how it works, when it breaks, and what it costs — not to tell you to buy or avoid anything.

## Foundations: The developer business model and the capital stack

Let's build the whole thing from zero, with a running character. Meet **Nam Land**, a mid-sized Vietnamese developer planning a 500-unit apartment project on the edge of Ho Chi Minh City. And meet **Minh**, a 32-year-old office worker who wants to buy one of those apartments off the renderings, before a single floor is poured. Their two stories — the developer assembling money and the buyer handing money over — are the two halves of developer financing.

### What a developer actually does

The first thing to unlearn is the idea that a developer is a construction company. The actual building — the pouring of concrete, the laying of bricks — is almost always subcontracted to a separate **general contractor**. What the developer does is *organize the project as a financial undertaking*. The developer:

1. Acquires the **land** (or the rights to use it — in Vietnam, land is state-owned and what you buy is a long-term land-use right).
2. Gets the project **permitted and approved** — zoning, planning permission, building permits, the legal right to sell units.
3. **Raises the money** to pay for all of it — this is the part this post is about.
4. Hires the contractor, manages the build, and **delivers** finished units to buyers.
5. **Recognizes the profit** — the difference between what units sell for and what the whole thing cost.

Step 3 is the heart of it. A 500-unit project might cost ₫2 trillion (≈ \$77 million at ₫25,900 to the dollar) to build and bring to market. Almost no developer has ₫2 trillion of cash lying around. So the question that determines everything is: *where does the money come from?*

### The three sources of money: equity, presales, debt

There are exactly three buckets a developer can draw from, and understanding the difference between them is 80% of understanding the whole industry.

**Equity** is the developer's own money — cash the owners put in, or profits retained from past projects. It is the most expensive money in one sense (the owners want a high return for risking it) and the cheapest in another (nobody can demand it back; there's no interest, no maturity date, no margin call). Equity is *patient* money that absorbs the first loss.

**Presales** are deposits collected from buyers like Minh *before* the building is finished — sometimes before it's even started. The buyer agrees to purchase a unit and pays in installments tied to construction milestones. Crucially, this is not a loan in the legal sense; it's a sales contract. But functionally, presale cash *is* financing: the developer gets to use Minh's money for two or three years to build the very apartment Minh is buying. It is, in effect, an interest-free loan from the customer. We'll spend a lot of time on this, because it's both the cleverest and the most dangerous part of the model.

**Debt** is borrowed money that must be repaid with interest. It comes in two main flavors that behave very differently:

- A **bank construction loan** — money lent by a bank, usually drawn down in tranches as construction progresses, secured against the land and the project itself. The bank can stop lending if things go wrong.
- **Corporate bonds** — IOUs the developer sells to investors (other firms, funds, sometimes ordinary savers). The developer gets cash now and promises to pay it back, with interest, on a fixed maturity date. Bonds are the channel that froze Vietnam in 2022, so they get their own section.

> One term, defined once: a *bond* is a tradable loan. Instead of borrowing from one bank, the developer borrows from many investors at once by selling them standardized IOUs called bonds. Each bond promises to pay back a fixed amount (the *face value* or *principal*) on a fixed future date (the *maturity*), plus periodic interest (the *coupon*) along the way.

### The capital stack: stacking the money by who eats the loss first

Now stack those sources up — but stack them in a specific order, by **who absorbs a loss first**. This ordering is the single most important concept in the post, so here it is plainly:

When a project loses money, the losses are eaten from the *bottom of the stack up*. The developer's **equity** is at the bottom — it's the first money to be destroyed. Only after the equity is entirely gone do the next layers start to feel pain. Buyers' presale deposits and lenders sit above the equity, protected (in theory) by that equity cushion. The **bank**, holding collateral and a senior legal claim, usually sits at the very top — last to lose, first to get paid back from any wreckage.

#### Worked example: Nam Land's capital stack for one tower

Nam Land's tower will cost **₫2,000 billion** (≈ \$77 million) to deliver. Here is how the company funds it:

| Layer | Amount | Share | What it is |
|---|---|---|---|
| Developer equity | ₫300 billion | 15% | Nam Land's own cash — the first loss |
| Presale deposits | ₫800 billion | 40% | Money from buyers like Minh, paid as floors go up |
| Bank construction loan | ₫400 billion | 20% | Drawn in tranches, secured on the land + project |
| Corporate bonds | ₫500 billion | 25% | Sold to investors, must be repaid at maturity |
| **Total funding** | **₫2,000 billion** | **100%** | The full cost of the project |

Read that table again and notice the headline fact: of ₫2,000 billion, only **₫300 billion** — 15% — is Nam Land's own money. The other 85% belongs to buyers and lenders. Nam Land is controlling a ₫2,000-billion project while risking ₫300 billion of its own capital. That ratio — controlling a big asset with a small slice of your own money — *is* leverage, and it is the entire reason the developer business is so profitable in a boom and so fragile in a bust.

*The intuition: a developer doesn't build with its own money; it builds with a thin equity cushion holding up a tall stack of buyers' deposits and borrowed cash.*

### Two kinds of property credit you must not confuse

One more foundational distinction, because beginners (and a lot of journalists) blur it constantly. There are two completely different credit channels in real estate:

- **Household mortgage credit** — the loan *you*, the buyer, take to purchase a home. This is the subject of our companion post on [leverage and the mortgage](/blog/trading/real-estate/leverage-and-the-mortgage-how-debt-amplifies-property). It sits on the *demand* side: it determines how much buyers can pay.
- **Developer credit** — the financing the developer uses to *build* the supply in the first place. That's this post. It sits on the *supply* side: it determines whether buildings get built at all.

The two are linked — if mortgage credit dries up, buyers vanish, presales stall, and developer financing collapses from the demand end — but they are distinct machines with distinct risks. When people say "the property market is driven by credit," they're usually mixing both together. We'll keep them separate, because they break in different ways. (For how the broader [credit cycle](/blog/trading/real-estate/the-credit-cycle-and-property-prices) drives both at once, see the companion post.)

## Presales: building with the buyers' money — and the risk shift

The presale model — selling homes before they're built, also called **off-plan** sales — is the beating heart of how Vietnam, China, and much of Asia builds housing. It's worth understanding deeply, because it's where the cleverness and the danger both live.

![A six-stage pipeline showing the presale model flowing from buying land to getting permits to preselling off-plan to building to delivering to recognizing profit, with delivery marked as the breaking point](/imgs/blogs/credit-and-developer-financing-2.png)

### How off-plan sales actually work

Here's the sequence, following Nam Land and Minh:

1. Nam Land acquires the land and gets permits. So far it has spent its equity and some bond money — pure cash burn, no revenue yet.
2. Nam Land opens a **sales gallery** with a model unit and glossy renderings, and starts selling units that exist only on paper. This is the *launch*.
3. Minh likes a 70 m² unit priced at **₫3.5 billion** (≈ \$135,000). He signs a sale-and-purchase agreement and pays a **deposit** — say 20% up front, ₫700 million (≈ \$27,000).
4. As construction hits milestones — foundation done, 10th floor poured, topping out, handover — Minh pays more, in **installments** tied to those milestones. By handover he might have paid 70% of the price; the final 30% comes when he gets his keys (often funded by his own mortgage).
5. Nam Land uses Minh's deposits, plus the deposits of hundreds of other buyers, plus the bank loan, to actually pay the contractor and build the tower.
6. On **handover**, Nam Land delivers the finished unit and *recognizes the profit* — books the revenue, repays the construction loan, and ideally repays the bonds.

The genius of this is in step 5. Nam Land is building the apartment with Minh's own money. The ₫800 billion of presale deposits in our capital-stack table is *interest-free financing from the customers*. No bank charges 0% interest. No bondholder lends for free. But buyers do — because they're not thinking of it as a loan. They're thinking of it as buying a home. The developer has turned its customers into its cheapest lenders.

#### Worked example: how presales shrink the money Nam Land has to raise

Imagine the presale model didn't exist and Nam Land had to build the tower first, then sell finished units. It would need to fund the *entire* ₫2,000 billion from equity and debt before earning a single đồng. At, say, 12% interest on ₫1,700 billion of debt over a three-year build, that's roughly **₫612 billion** of interest cost (₫1,700bn × 12% × 3 years) — a brutal drag.

Now switch the presale model on. By collecting ₫800 billion of *interest-free* presale deposits during construction, Nam Land borrows ₫800 billion *less*. At 12% over three years, the interest it avoids is about ₫800bn × 12% × 3 = **₫288 billion** (≈ \$11 million). That ₫288 billion is pure margin the presale model hands to the developer.

*The intuition: presales aren't just early sales — they're a giant interest-free loan from buyers that can be worth tens of millions of dollars to the developer over a single project.*

### The risk shift: who's holding the bag

Here's the part that's easy to miss when you're the excited buyer signing the contract. Every đồng of interest-free financing the developer gains is a risk the *buyer* takes on. The presale model doesn't make construction risk disappear — it *moves* it, from the developer onto the buyer.

Think about what Minh has actually done. He has paid real money — ₫700 million and climbing — for an apartment that does not exist. He is now exposed to:

- **Delivery risk**: the building might not get finished, or might be finished years late. Minh has paid; Minh has no home.
- **Quality risk**: the finished unit might not match the renderings — smaller, lower-spec, different layout.
- **Developer-default risk**: if Nam Land runs out of money and collapses, Minh is an unsecured creditor scrambling for whatever is left, behind the banks.

In a market with strong consumer protection, the deposits would sit in an **escrow account** — money held by a neutral third party and released to the developer only as construction milestones are independently verified. If the developer fails, the un-released escrow money goes back to the buyer. This is roughly how it works in much of the United States and in well-regulated markets.

> *Escrow*, defined: a neutral third party (often a bank or a lawyer) holds the buyer's money and releases it to the seller only when agreed conditions are met. It protects the buyer's cash from being spent before the buyer gets what they paid for.

In Vietnam — and in China, and in many emerging markets — escrow is often weak or absent in practice. There is a legal framework (Vietnamese law requires a bank guarantee for off-plan sales, for instance), but enforcement is patchy, guarantees are sometimes not actually obtained, and developers routinely use presale cash from one project to plug holes in another. The result: Minh's deposit is frequently *not* ring-fenced. It goes straight into the developer's cash pool and gets spent. If the project stalls, the money is gone, tied up in a half-built tower Minh can neither live in nor easily get a refund from.

#### Worked example: Minh's deposit at risk when the project stalls

Minh has paid installments totaling **₫1.96 billion** (≈ \$76,000) — 56% of the ₫3.5 billion price — by the time the building is topped out but unfinished. Then Nam Land hits a funding gap (we'll see exactly how in a moment) and construction halts. The tower sits at 70% complete for two years.

What is Minh's position? He has ₫1.96 billion sunk into an apartment he can't occupy. He can't easily sell his contract — nobody wants to buy into a stalled project. If he wants a refund, he must get in line behind the banks, who have a *secured* claim on the land and the building; Minh's claim is unsecured and ranks below them. In a worst case, the project is restructured, a new developer takes over years later, and Minh either waits half a decade for his home or accepts a partial recovery. The ₫1.96 billion he could have used as a down payment on a *finished* apartment elsewhere is frozen.

*The intuition: a presale deposit feels like buying a home, but legally and financially it's an unsecured, often-unprotected loan to a leveraged company — and if that company stalls, your money stalls with it.*

This is the central trade of the presale model: the buyer finances the developer interest-free, and in exchange takes on the risk that the building never gets built. In a boom, this is invisible — everything gets delivered, everyone's happy. In a squeeze, it's the whole ballgame.

## Bank construction loans: the senior, secured layer

The second pillar of developer financing is the **bank construction loan**. It behaves very differently from a presale deposit or a bond, and the difference is what makes it the safest layer in the stack — for the bank.

### How a construction loan works

A construction loan is not handed over as a lump sum. The bank commits to lend up to a maximum — say ₫400 billion for Nam Land's tower — but **disburses it in tranches** (stages) as construction progresses and as the bank's surveyor verifies the work. Foundation complete → release a tranche. Tenth floor poured → release another. This staging is the bank's protection: if the project stalls, the bank simply *stops disbursing*. It never has more money out than the value of the work actually completed plus its collateral.

And the loan is **secured** — collateralized — against the land-use rights and the project itself, sometimes plus a corporate guarantee from the parent developer. *Secured* means: if Nam Land defaults, the bank can seize and sell the collateral to recover its money. This is what makes the bank the senior, top-of-stack layer: it has both a legal priority claim and physical collateral.

> *Secured* vs *unsecured*, defined: a *secured* loan is backed by specific collateral the lender can seize on default; an *unsecured* loan has no collateral and the lender just gets in line with everyone else if the borrower fails. Secured lenders are paid first; unsecured creditors — including, usually, presale buyers — are paid last.

### Why the bank is the safest and the buyer is the least safe

Put the construction loan next to Minh's deposit and the asymmetry is stark:

- The **bank** lends in stages it controls, holds collateral worth more than its loan, and ranks first in any default. It can pause draws at the first sign of trouble. Its downside is capped and protected.
- **Minh** pays in stages *the developer controls* (tied to milestones the developer reports), holds no collateral, and ranks last. He can't pause his payments without breaching his contract, and he has no way to seize anything if it goes wrong.

This is why "the bank lent against this project, so it must be safe" is one of the most dangerous things a buyer can believe. The bank being comfortable tells you the *bank* is protected. It tells you almost nothing about whether *you* are. The bank's safety comes precisely from sitting above you in the stack — its protection is, in part, your exposure.

The cleanest way to see this is to lay all three players side by side and ask the same three questions of each: how much money is at stake, who bears the delivery risk, and who is actually protected. The matrix below does exactly that.

![A comparison matrix showing the off-plan buyer the developer and the bank across money at stake delivery risk and protection, with the buyer most exposed and least protected and the bank most protected](/imgs/blogs/credit-and-developer-financing-6.png)

The pattern jumps out: read top to bottom and the protection improves as the money-at-risk shrinks. The **buyer** has the most money exposed relative to their means — often a deposit of 30–70% of the full price, all of it before any home exists — and the *least* protection, ranking behind every secured lender with no escrow to fall back on. The **developer** has only thin equity at stake (the first loss, but small relative to the whole project) and the option, when things sour, to stall and renegotiate rather than deliver — it can lose at most its equity and walk away from the rest. The **bank** has the strongest position of all: it lends in tranches it can pause, holds collateral worth more than its loan, and ranks first in any default. The risk is *inversely* distributed to the sophistication and protection of the player: the least-protected party, the ordinary buyer, bears the most. That inversion is the moral core of why presale models are dangerous in markets without enforced escrow, and it's worth holding in your head for the rest of the post — every break we examine is, at bottom, this matrix turning real.

#### Worked example: the bank pauses, and Minh keeps paying

Nam Land's tower reaches the 15th floor. The bank's surveyor flags that costs are overrunning and presales have slowed. The bank exercises its right and **pauses the next ₫80 billion tranche** until Nam Land demonstrates it can finish. From the bank's seat, this is prudent risk management — it has ₫320 billion out against a building and land worth more than that, and it simply waits.

But the pause is exactly what *creates* the funding gap. Nam Land was counting on that ₫80 billion to pay the contractor. Without it, work slows, milestones slip, and the buyers' next installments — which are tied to milestones — also slow, choking the developer's cash further. The bank is fine. Minh, who has already paid ₫1.96 billion, watches the crane stop moving. The senior lender protected itself by stepping back; the junior buyer absorbs the consequence.

*The intuition: the bank's right to stop lending is its safety valve — and pulling that valve is often the trigger that strands everyone below it in the stack.*

## The corporate-bond channel: the layer that froze Vietnam

Now we arrive at the layer that detonated. For Vietnamese developers in the 2018–2021 boom, the corporate-**bond** market became the favorite way to raise money — and its sudden freeze in 2022 is the textbook case of how developer financing breaks.

### Why developers love bonds

Recall that a bond is a tradable loan: the developer sells IOUs to investors, gets cash now, and promises to repay the principal at a fixed maturity with interest along the way. Compared to a bank loan, bonds have features developers love:

- **Fewer strings.** A bank loan comes with covenants, staged disbursement, and a surveyor watching the site. A bond often comes with less oversight — the developer gets the cash up front and spends it more freely.
- **No collateral haircut.** Many developer bonds in Vietnam were effectively unsecured or thinly secured, so the developer didn't have to pledge as much.
- **Scale and speed.** In a hot market, a developer can sell a large bond issue quickly to yield-hungry investors, including ordinary retail savers chasing 10–12% returns far above bank deposit rates.

In the 2018–2021 boom, Vietnamese real-estate firms issued bonds at a furious pace. Money was cheap, property prices were rising, and investors were happy to lend to developers at fat coupons. The bond channel became, for many firms, the *primary* source of funding — bigger than bank loans, bigger than equity.

![A bar chart showing Vietnam real-estate bond issuance rising from 2019 to a 2021 peak then collapsing sharply in 2022 with a shaded freeze window and an annotation marking the Tan Hoang Minh and Van Thinh Phat cases](/imgs/blogs/credit-and-developer-financing-3.png)

The chart above shows the shape of it — illustrative, indexed to a 2021 peak rather than exact trillions, but the story is real: heavy issuance through 2019–2021, a peak around 2021, and then a near-total collapse in 2022 once the market froze. (The exact đồng figures vary by source and by how you count; the *shape* — boom then cliff — is what matters.)

### The fatal flaw: rollover risk

Here is the structural weakness baked into the bond channel, and it's worth slowing down for because it's the mechanism of the whole crisis.

A bond has a fixed **maturity** — say three years. When it matures, the developer must repay the principal in full. But a developer doesn't have that cash sitting in a vault; it's tied up in land and half-built towers. So what does the developer plan to do when the bond comes due?

**Roll it over.** *Rolling over* (or *refinancing*) means: when the old bond matures, you sell a *new* bond to raise the cash to pay off the old one. As long as you can keep selling new bonds, you never actually have to come up with the principal — you just keep refinancing, perpetually. The whole edifice depends on one assumption: *that the bond market stays open*.

> *Rollover / refinancing risk*, defined: the danger that when a debt matures, you can't borrow new money to repay it. If the market that lets you refinance shuts, a maturity you were never planning to actually pay in cash becomes a bill you can't pay — overnight.

This is the developer financing equivalent of a game of musical chairs. The music is the open bond market. As long as it plays, everyone keeps moving — old bonds replaced by new ones, principal never really repaid, just rolled. The moment the music stops — the moment new buyers refuse to show up — whoever has a bond maturing *right then* is caught without a chair. They owe principal they don't have and can't borrow.

#### Worked example: Nam Land's bond rollover fails

Nam Land has a **₫500 billion** bond (≈ \$19 million) maturing in December. Its plan, all along, was never to repay it in cash — it was to issue a fresh ₫500 billion bond in November and use the proceeds to pay off the December maturity. This worked for the last two cycles. The company's actual cash on hand is maybe ₫60 billion; the rest is sunk in land and a 70%-complete tower.

Then the bond market freezes. After the high-profile arrests, investors won't touch developer bonds at any price. Nam Land goes to market in November to roll the ₫500 billion — and there are *no buyers*. Not at 10%, not at 15%, not at 20%. The refinancing fails completely.

Now do the arithmetic of the trap. Nam Land owes ₫500 billion in December and has ₫60 billion. The gap is **₫440 billion** (≈ \$17 million) it cannot fill. It can't sell the half-built tower (who buys a stalled project?). It can't draw more bank loan (the bank paused). It can't collect more presales fast enough (buyers are spooked and milestones have slipped). The ₫440 billion gap is a wall. Nam Land defaults on the bond, and the cascade begins.

*The intuition: a maturity you always intended to refinance rather than repay is a hidden time bomb — it's only manageable as long as the market that lets you roll it stays open, and that market can slam shut without warning.*

This is precisely what happened across Vietnam in 2022. Hundreds of developers had built their funding on the assumption that the bond market would always be there to roll their maturities. When two fraud cases poisoned investor confidence and the market froze, the rollover machine seized for the entire sector at once.

## How leverage magnifies developer returns — and risk

Step back from the plumbing and look at the math of *why* developers take on all this debt and presale risk in the first place. The answer is **leverage**, and it's the same force we met in the [household mortgage post](/blog/trading/real-estate/leverage-and-the-mortgage-how-debt-amplifies-property), now operating on the developer's side of the table.

![A before and after comparison showing that on a project funded fifteen percent equity, a ten percent rise in project value lifts equity by sixty-seven percent while a ten percent fall destroys it by sixty-seven percent](/imgs/blogs/credit-and-developer-financing-4.png)

### The leverage math, from the developer's chair

Leverage is the use of borrowed money (and, here, presale deposits) to control an asset much larger than your own equity. Its defining property: it **magnifies the percentage return on your equity**, up and down, by the ratio of the whole asset to your slice of it.

Nam Land controls a ₫2,000 billion project with ₫300 billion of equity — a leverage ratio of about 6.7 to 1. That ratio is the magnification factor. A move in the *project's* value gets multiplied by roughly 6.7 when it lands on the *equity*. Let's watch it work in both directions.

#### Worked example: Nam Land's return on equity at +10% vs −10%

Start with the stack: project value **₫2,000 billion**, of which ₫300 billion is equity and ₫1,700 billion is the fixed claims of buyers and lenders (presales + loan + bonds). The key fact about those fixed claims: *they don't change when the project's value changes*. Buyers are owed their apartments; lenders are owed their principal. Whatever happens to the project value flows entirely to the equity.

**The boom (+10%).** Property prices rise; the finished project is now worth ₫2,200 billion — up ₫200 billion. The fixed claims are still ₫1,700 billion. So the equity is now ₫2,200 − ₫1,700 = **₫500 billion**, up from ₫300 billion. That's a gain of ₫200 billion on ₫300 billion of equity, or **+67%**. A 10% rise in the project became a 67% gain on the developer's money.

**The squeeze (−10%).** Prices fall; the project is now worth ₫1,800 billion — down ₫200 billion. Fixed claims are still ₫1,700 billion (debt doesn't shrink because your project got cheaper). So the equity is now ₫1,800 − ₫1,700 = **₫100 billion**, down from ₫300 billion. That's a loss of ₫200 billion on ₫300 billion, or **−67%**. The same 10% move, the same 6.7× magnification, now pointed the wrong way.

| Project value moves | Project value | Fixed claims (debt + presales) | Developer equity | Return on equity |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| +10% (boom) | ₫2,200 bn | ₫1,700 bn | ₫500 bn | **+67%** |
| Base | ₫2,000 bn | ₫1,700 bn | ₫300 bn | — |
| −10% (squeeze) | ₫1,800 bn | ₫1,700 bn | ₫100 bn | **−67%** |

*The intuition: leverage doesn't change the project's return — it changes whose return it is. A modest swing in the asset becomes a violent swing in the thin equity holding it up, and the more debt you stack, the more violent both directions get.*

### Why a rational developer levers up anyway

If leverage is so dangerous, why does every developer use it? Because the payoff is *asymmetric* in the developer's favor, thanks to **limited liability**. When the project rises, the developer keeps the magnified gain — the whole +67% is theirs. When the project falls far enough to wipe out the equity, the developer loses its ₫300 billion and not a đồng more; it can let the project default and walk away, leaving the losses beyond the equity to the lenders and buyers. The developer's downside is capped at its small equity stake; its upside is the full magnified gain. That shape — limited loss, unlimited gain — is, in the language of finance, very much like owning a *call option* on the project, and it makes piling on leverage perfectly rational from the developer's narrow point of view. The catch is who's on the other side of that option: the buyers and bondholders, who get a capped upside (they're only ever owed a fixed amount) but a real downside if the equity cushion fails. The presale-and-bond model quietly hands the developer the option-like payoff and hands the buyers the risk — which is exactly why developers lever up to the hilt in a boom, and exactly why the buyers are the ones left exposed when the boom ends.

### Where the magnification turns into wipeout

Now push the squeeze a little further. Suppose the project value falls not 10% but **15%**, to ₫1,700 billion. The fixed claims are ₫1,700 billion. Equity = ₫1,700 − ₫1,700 = **zero**. The developer's entire ₫300 billion is gone — wiped out — by a 15% fall in the asset.

And below zero is worse than zero. If the project falls to ₫1,600 billion but the fixed claims are still ₫1,700 billion, the project is now worth *less than what's owed on it*. The equity isn't just gone; the project is **underwater** (or *insolvent*) by ₫100 billion. Somebody above the equity in the stack — a bondholder, or a presale buyer — is now going to take a loss too, because there isn't enough value left to pay everyone back. This is the precise moment the protective cushion fails and the pain climbs up the stack from the equity into the layers that were supposed to be safe.

This is the whole drama of developer finance in two sentences. In a boom, 6.7× leverage turns a good market into spectacular equity returns, and developers race to build more, borrow more, presell more — leverage *up*. In a bust, that same 6.7× leverage means a modest price fall vaporizes the equity and starts eating the lenders and buyers — and everyone who lent against "safe" property discovers how thin the cushion really was.

## What breaks the model: the funding gap and the rollover failure

We've now seen all the pieces. Let's assemble the failure itself — the precise sequence by which a leveraged developer goes from "fine" to "frozen." It almost always runs through a **funding gap**: a moment when the money the developer owes *right now* exceeds the money it can raise *right now*.

![A four-node flow showing that when a bond cannot roll over it opens a funding gap which forces construction to halt and strands the presale buyers who already paid](/imgs/blogs/credit-and-developer-financing-5.png)

### The anatomy of a funding gap

A developer's cash position is a constant balancing act. Money flows *out* — to contractors, to land, to repay maturing debt. Money flows *in* — from presales, from new bond issues, from bank draws. As long as inflows keep pace with outflows, the project is alive. A funding gap is what happens when an inflow the developer was counting on disappears while the outflows keep coming.

The single most dangerous outflow is a **maturing bond**, because it's large, fixed, and non-negotiable. The single most fragile inflow is the **bond rollover**, because it depends entirely on a market staying open. Put those together and you have the classic break:

1. **A bond can't roll.** The market freezes; no new buyers; the refinancing fails. (Nam Land's ₫500 billion, with no takers.)
2. **A funding gap opens.** The developer owes principal it doesn't have and can't borrow. (₫440 billion it can't fill.)
3. **Construction halts.** With no cash, the developer can't pay the contractor. The contractor walks. The crane stops. (And because the bank's draws and the buyers' milestone payments are *tied* to construction, both of those inflows dry up too — the gap feeds itself.)
4. **Presale buyers are stranded.** Minh and hundreds like him have paid for homes that now won't be delivered, with no easy refund and no seniority in the wreckage.

That's the model breaking. And notice the cruelty of the ordering: the people who took the least sophisticated risk — ordinary buyers who thought they were just buying an apartment — are the ones left holding the emptiest bag, because they sit at the bottom of the protection hierarchy even though they're at the top of the moral one.

### Why one developer's break becomes the whole market's break

A single developer failing is a contained problem. The reason 2022 became a *crisis* is **contagion** — the way one failure poisons financing for all the others.

When a high-profile developer is arrested for bond fraud, investors don't just avoid that one firm's bonds. They avoid *all* developer bonds, because they can no longer tell the safe issuers from the fraudulent ones. The whole asset class becomes radioactive. So the rollover market freezes not for one developer but for *every* developer at once — including healthy ones that would have rolled their bonds fine in a calm market. Perfectly solvent firms get caught in funding gaps purely because the channel they depended on shut for reasons that had nothing to do with them. This is how a problem at two companies becomes a nationwide freeze: the financing model is *shared*, so when confidence in it breaks, it breaks for everyone.

## Vietnam 2022–2023: the bond freeze as the case study

Now we can tell the real story end to end, because every mechanism in it is one we've already built. This is the textbook case of developer financing breaking, and it happened on Vietnamese soil within the last few years.

![A six-event timeline showing Vietnam from the Tan Hoang Minh arrest in April 2022 through the Van Thinh Phat and SCB case in October 2022 to a frozen bond market and stalled projects then SBV rate cuts and Decree 08 relief and a slow thaw from 2024](/imgs/blogs/credit-and-developer-financing-7.png)

### The setup: a sector built on bonds

By 2021, Vietnamese real-estate developers had spent three years funding themselves heavily through corporate bonds — often sold to retail investors chasing double-digit yields, often thinly secured, often with the explicit plan of rolling each maturity into a fresh issue. The household-mortgage boom on the demand side and the bond boom on the supply side fed each other: rising prices justified more building, more building needed more bonds, more bonds were easy to sell because prices were rising. This is the [credit cycle](/blog/trading/real-estate/the-credit-cycle-and-property-prices) running hot on both sides at once — the same self-reinforcing loop that powered, and then broke, the broader [Vietnam property cycles of 2007, 2013, and 2022](/blog/trading/real-estate/vietnam-property-cycles-2007-2013-2022).

### The trigger: two arrests

**April 2022 — Tân Hoàng Minh.** The chairman of the Tân Hoàng Minh group was arrested on charges related to fraudulent bond issuance — bonds sold to the public on misleading terms. Regulators cancelled bond issues. Investor confidence in developer bonds cracked.

**October 2022 — Vạn Thịnh Phát and SCB.** The far larger blow. The chair of the Vạn Thịnh Phát conglomerate was arrested in a case entangled with Saigon Commercial Bank (SCB), one of the country's larger banks. The scandal — eventually one of the biggest financial fraud cases in Vietnamese history — triggered a run on SCB and shattered confidence in the entire developer-bond complex. Investors fled.

### The freeze and the funding gaps

With confidence gone, the corporate-bond market **froze**. New issuance collapsed (the cliff you saw in the chart). Developers who had planned to roll their 2022 and 2023 maturities found no buyers at any yield — the musical chairs stopped for everyone simultaneously. Funding gaps opened across the sector. Construction sites went quiet. Projects nationwide — including many that were fundamentally viable — stalled because their financing model, not their underlying economics, had broken. Presale buyers across the country found themselves exactly where Minh ended up in our example: money paid, home undelivered, refund impossible.

### The policy response: rate cuts and Decree 08

The authorities responded on two fronts, both of which map directly onto the mechanisms in this post.

**The State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) cut policy rates four times in 2023.** Lower policy rates lower the cost of credit across the system. For developers, this matters in two ways: it cuts the interest cost on the bank-loan layer of the stack, and it makes the eventual return of the bond market more likely by making yields elsewhere less competitive. It also helps the *demand* side — cheaper mortgages bring buyers back, which revives presales.

#### Worked example: what an SBV rate cut does to Nam Land's funding cost

Suppose Nam Land has ₫400 billion of bank construction loan outstanding at a floating rate that resets with policy. Before the cuts, it pays roughly **13%** — that's ₫52 billion a year in interest. After the SBV's 2023 cuts feed through, the rate resets to roughly **10%** — that's ₫40 billion a year. The cut saves Nam Land **₫12 billion** (≈ \$460,000) annually on that one loan. Across a sector carrying hundreds of trillions of đồng in developer debt, the aggregate relief is enormous — it's the difference between a manageable carry and a death spiral for marginal firms.

*The intuition: a central-bank rate cut doesn't just help homebuyers — it directly lowers the interest bleeding from every leveraged developer's stack, buying stalled projects time to find a way through.*

**Decree 08 (March 2023) granted bond-restructuring relief.** This was the targeted fix for the rollover problem itself. Decree 08 made it easier for issuers to *restructure* their bonds — to extend maturities by up to two years, and even (with bondholder agreement) to repay bonds with assets such as property instead of cash. In plain terms: it let developers who couldn't repay a maturing bond *legally push the maturity out* rather than default. It didn't make the debt disappear — it bought time, on the bet that with rates falling and the market slowly reopening, developers could grow back into their obligations.

### The slow thaw

From 2023 into 2024 and beyond, the market began a **slow, uneven thaw**. Some healthier developers regained access to bond and bank financing as confidence partly returned. The strongest firms refinanced; some stalled projects resumed. But it was not a clean recovery — many weaker developers remained frozen, their bonds restructured-but-not-resolved, their projects still stuck, their buyers still waiting. The episode left a lasting mark on how Vietnamese regulators, developers, and buyers think about the bond channel and the presale model. It was the clearest possible demonstration of the thesis of this post: developer financing is leverage, leverage is the engine *and* the fault line, and when the financing freezes, it's the buyers at the bottom of the stack who wait the longest.

## Common misconceptions

The presale-and-leverage model produces a specific set of comforting beliefs that the 2022 freeze demolished. Here are the ones worth unlearning.

**"My presale deposit is safe — it's a sale, not a loan."** This is the most dangerous misconception, and it's the one Minh held. A presale deposit *feels* like buying a home, but financially it's an unsecured advance to a leveraged company. Unless your money is in genuine, enforced escrow (rare in Vietnam in practice), it goes into the developer's general cash pool and gets spent — often on a *different* project. If the developer hits a funding gap, your deposit is frozen with everyone else's, and you rank *behind* the banks in any recovery. A sale contract is not the protection it feels like.

**"A big, famous developer can't fail."** Size is not safety; in fact, leverage often *grows* with size, because the biggest developers run the biggest stacks of bonds and presales. Vạn Thịnh Phát was enormous. Evergrande, in China, was the largest developer in the world. Their scale didn't protect their buyers — it meant that when they broke, they broke *more* projects and *more* buyers at once. A famous brand on the sales gallery tells you nothing about the firm's leverage or its bond maturity wall.

**"The bank lent against this project, so it must be sound."** The bank's comfort protects the *bank*, not you. The bank lends in controlled tranches, holds collateral, and ranks first in default — its safety comes precisely from sitting *above* you in the stack. The bank can be perfectly protected while you, the buyer, lose everything, because the bank's protection is partly built on your subordination. "A bank is involved" should never be read as "I am protected."

**"Presales are free money for the developer with no downside."** From the developer's side, presales feel like a gift — interest-free financing from customers. But they come with a hidden liability: a *delivery obligation* to hundreds of buyers, enforceable and reputationally explosive if broken. A developer that over-presells and under-delivers doesn't just lose money; it creates a political crisis (angry buyers protesting outside half-built towers is a recurring image in both Vietnam and China). Presale cash is borrowed against a promise that is far harder to walk away from than a bond.

**"If the project stalls, I'll just get a refund."** Refunds assume there's money to refund from and a legal mechanism that puts you ahead of the line. Usually neither holds. The money is sunk in a half-built tower; the legal mechanism puts secured lenders first; and the practical reality is years of waiting for a restructuring. "I'll get my money back" is a hope, not a contract term you can actually enforce quickly.

**"Developer debt and my mortgage are basically the same thing."** They're different machines on different sides of the market. Your mortgage is *demand-side* household credit — it sets how much you can pay. Developer debt is *supply-side* credit — it sets whether the building gets built. They interact (a mortgage-credit squeeze kills presales, which breaks developer financing), but they fail through different channels and are regulated by different rules. Confusing them obscures where the actual risk in a given downturn is coming from.

## How it shows up in real markets

The mechanics in this post aren't a Vietnam quirk — they're a global pattern with local variations. Here's how the same machine plays out across markets, with real cases.

### Vietnam: stalled projects and the long tail

The most visible legacy of the 2022 freeze is the inventory of **stalled projects** scattered across Vietnam — towers frozen at 30%, 50%, 70% complete, surrounded by hoarding and silence, with thousands of presale buyers in limbo. Many are not economically dead; they're *financially* dead, killed by a funding gap rather than by a lack of demand for the finished homes. Untangling them is slow: it requires a new source of financing, often a stronger developer buying in, or a bank converting debt, or the original buyers agreeing to a restructuring. The State Bank's rate cuts and Decree 08's maturity extensions were designed to buy exactly this untangling time. Years later, the stalled-project overhang is still working its way through the system — the clearest evidence that when developer financing breaks, the damage is measured in years, not months. (The supply-side mechanics of how slowly Vietnam delivers housing in the first place are covered in [supply, zoning, land use, and construction costs](/blog/trading/real-estate/supply-zoning-land-use-and-construction-costs); a funding freeze stacks on top of an already slow pipeline.)

### China and Evergrande: the same model, an order of magnitude larger

China ran the presale-and-debt model harder than anyone, and **Evergrande** is the preview Vietnam should study. At its peak Evergrande was the world's most indebted developer, with liabilities exceeding \$300 billion. It had presold *over a million* apartments that weren't yet built — a million households' deposits funding a giant leveraged machine. When Beijing tightened developer borrowing (the "three red lines" policy in 2020), Evergrande's ability to roll its debt evaporated. The funding gap that followed was the same one Nam Land hit, scaled to a continent: construction halted on projects across China, and over a million presale buyers were stranded exactly as our model predicts. The Chinese episode is the same diagram as the Vietnamese one — thin equity, vast presales, debt that couldn't roll, buyers at the bottom of the stack — just larger, and it's still being worked out. It's the cautionary mirror for every presale-heavy market.

### The United States: presales with real escrow

Contrast with the United States, where the presale model exists but the *risk shift* is far more contained. American condo developers do presell off-plan, and they do use construction loans and bonds. But buyer deposits typically sit in **genuine escrow**, legally protected, often capped at a modest percentage (commonly around 10–20%) and refundable if the developer fails to deliver. The buyer's exposure is a fraction of what a Vietnamese off-plan buyer takes on. When US developers fail — and they do, especially when [interest rates](/blog/trading/real-estate/leverage-and-the-mortgage-how-debt-amplifies-property) spike and construction loans get expensive — the lenders and the developer's equity absorb most of the damage, not the buyers. Same capital stack, same leverage math, but a regulatory floor under the buyer that emerging markets often lack. The lesson isn't that presales are inherently bad; it's that *escrow is the difference between a presale and a hostage situation*.

### The 2008 US developer wipeout

The American homebuilders of the mid-2000s are a clean example of the leverage math turning into a wipeout. Builders like the publicly traded national homebuilders carried heavy debt against land banks bought at peak prices. When US home prices fell roughly 27% peak-to-trough (2006–2012), the thin equity cushions on those land banks were obliterated — exactly the "−15% wipes out the equity" math from our worked example, except the fall was nearly twice that. Several builders went bankrupt or were forced into deeply dilutive rescues. The buyers were mostly protected (US escrow again), but the *equity* holders learned the downside of 6.7× leverage in the most expensive way possible.

### Why the pattern keeps repeating

Across all these markets, the same root cause recurs: developer financing is leverage layered on a long, illiquid project, funded substantially by money the developer must either repay (debt) or deliver against (presales). That structure produces enormous returns when prices rise and credit flows, which attracts more leverage, which makes the eventual reversal more violent. The boom *builds* the fragility. The specific trigger varies — a fraud case in Vietnam, a policy tightening in China, a price crash in the US — but the break is always the same shape: an inflow the developer counted on disappears, a funding gap opens, and the thin equity at the bottom of the stack can't absorb the blow. The model doesn't break because developers are reckless (though some are); it breaks because *leverage that magnifies returns must, by identical arithmetic, magnify losses* — and no amount of boom-time confidence repeals that symmetry.

## When this matters to you / further reading

If you are a **buyer** considering an off-plan purchase, this post is the part of the transaction the sales gallery will never explain. Before you hand over a deposit, the questions that actually protect you are financial, not aesthetic: *Is my money in genuine, enforced escrow or a bank guarantee — and can I see the documentation? How leveraged is this developer, and when do its bonds mature? Has it delivered its last few projects on time?* You are not just buying an apartment; you are lending a leveraged company a large, unsecured, hard-to-recover sum, and ranking behind its banks while you do. Knowing where you sit in the capital stack is the single most useful thing you can know before you sign.

If you are an **observer of the market** — an investor, a saver, anyone trying to read where a property cycle is headed — the developer-financing channel is one of the best leading indicators there is. When developers are issuing bonds freely, preselling aggressively, and leveraging up, the cycle is hot and the fragility is building. When the bond market tightens, when rollovers start to fail, when presales slow — that's the credit squeeze arriving on the supply side, and it tends to lead the visible price decline by months. Watching the financing is watching the engine, not the exhaust.
