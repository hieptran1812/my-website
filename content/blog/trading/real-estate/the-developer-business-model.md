> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A property developer is not a builder; it is a financial machine that buys a **land bank** cheaply, gets it **entitled** (rezoned and approved — where most of the value is actually created), **presells** units off-plan to fund construction with buyers' deposits plus leverage, **builds**, **delivers**, and then **recycles** the capital into the next, bigger land bank.
>
> - **Most of the value is created at entitlement, not construction.** Changing what land is *legally allowed to be* — turning an agricultural plot into approved residential building plots — can triple its value with a stamp, before a single brick is laid.
> - **Presales are the secret engine.** Buyers pay deposits for a home that does not exist yet, often 70% of the price before handover. That cash is an **interest-free float** that lets the developer build with the customer's money — cheaper than any loan.
> - **The model is pro-cyclical by design.** Land appreciation, the entitlement uplift, leverage, the free presale float, and the build margin all compound in a boom — and every one of them reverses in a bust. That is why even giant developers fail.
> - **The one case to remember:** Vietnam's developers leaned on presales and the corporate-**bond** channel. When the Tân Hoàng Minh (Apr-2022) and Vạn Thịnh Phát/SCB (Oct-2022) cases froze the bond market, presales stalled, debt could not roll, and the machine seized nationwide. SBV cut policy rates four times in 2023 and Decree 08 granted bond-restructuring relief.

In the spring of 2022, a 32-year-old office worker in Ho Chi Minh City — let's call him Minh — signed a contract for an apartment he had never seen, because it did not yet exist. The tower was a rendering on a glossy brochure: a sun-dappled balcony, a rooftop pool, a price of ₫3 billion (about \$116,000 at ₫25,900 to the dollar). Minh paid a deposit, then more in installments tied to "construction milestones." By autumn, the cranes on his site had gone still. The developer's name was tangled up in the bond scandals that froze Vietnam's property market that year. Minh's money — real money, his and his parents' savings — had been handed to a company that used it to build the very apartment he was buying, and that company had run out of cash before the building was done.

Most people experience real estate from the inside of a single transaction: their mortgage, their square metres, their location. But behind every project is a developer running a specific, fragile machine — and Minh had just discovered, the hard way, how that machine works and how it breaks. The developer is not the company that pours the concrete. It is the company that *organizes the money*: it buys land before anyone wants it, it gets the land legally transformed into something far more valuable, it persuades buyers like Minh to fund the construction in advance, and then it recycles whatever profit is left into a bigger pile of land to do it all again. When the music plays, this machine mints money. When it stops, it detonates.

This post is about that machine: who builds your city, why developers are obsessed with land, and why — counterintuitively — even the biggest developers in the world routinely go bankrupt. By the end, you will understand the land bank, entitlement and the value it creates, the presale float, the capital recycle, the math of development margin, and exactly why the model is pro-cyclical. The diagram above is the mental model for the whole post: the developer's six-step lifecycle, a loop that runs land → entitle → presell → build → deliver, and then recycles the cash back into the next land bank.

![The developer lifecycle as a six-step loop running from buying a land bank through entitlement, presales, building, and delivery, then recycling the freed capital back into the next land bank](/imgs/blogs/the-developer-business-model-1.png)

A note before we start: this is educational, not investment advice. The goal is to make the machinery legible — how developers make money, when they break, and what it costs — not to tell you to buy, sell, or invest in anything.

## Foundations: the developer machine, built from zero

Let's build the whole thing from the ground up, with two running characters. Meet **Nam Land**, a mid-sized Vietnamese developer. And meet **Minh**, the off-plan buyer from the opening — a 32-year-old who wants an apartment in one of Nam Land's projects, bought from the renderings before a single floor is poured. Their two stories — the developer assembling and deploying capital, and the buyer handing money over for a home that does not exist yet — are the two halves of the developer business model.

### What a property developer actually is

The first thing to unlearn is that a developer is a construction company. The pouring of concrete, the laying of bricks, the wiring and plumbing — that is almost always subcontracted to a separate firm called the **general contractor**, a company that builds to order for a fee. The developer hires that contractor the way you might hire a builder to renovate your kitchen. The developer's job is something else entirely.

A **property developer** is a firm that *organizes a project as a financial undertaking*. It identifies a piece of land, acquires the right to build on it, navigates the legal process to make building legal, raises the money to pay for everything, manages the contractor through construction, sells the finished units, and pockets the difference between what the units sell for and what the whole thing cost. The developer is, first and foremost, a *financial engineer and a risk-taker*, not a builder. It is the entity that says, "I will put a tower here, I will find the money, and I will bear the risk that it doesn't work."

That distinction matters because it tells you where developers make their money. They do not earn by building well — the contractor builds, and a good contractor earns a modest fee. Developers earn by being right about *land*, about *timing*, and about *demand*, and by using leverage and other people's money to magnify the bet. The whole skill is financial and entitlement-driven, not constructional.

### The land bank: the strategic asset

A **land bank** is the inventory of land a developer owns or controls but has not yet built on. It is, quite literally, a bank of land — a stockpile of building sites held for future projects. To a developer, the land bank is not a cost to be minimized; it is *the* strategic asset, because land is the one input you cannot manufacture. You can always hire more contractors and pour more concrete, but you cannot make more land in a good location. Whoever controls the land controls the pipeline of future projects.

This is why developers behave in a way that looks irrational to outsiders: they tie up enormous amounts of capital in raw, empty land that produces no income, sometimes for years, paying taxes and interest on it the whole time. They do it because the land is the seed corn. A developer with a deep land bank, bought cheaply before a district became fashionable, has a pipeline of future profit baked in. A developer with no land bank has nothing to build and must buy land at today's inflated prices, with today's thin margins. The race to accumulate land cheaply, ahead of the crowd, is the defining strategic contest of the industry.

### Entitlement: where the value is really created

Here is the single most important — and least understood — idea in the whole business. **Entitlement** is the legal process of getting permission to develop land: rezoning it from one use to another (agricultural to residential, low-density to high-rise), obtaining planning approval, securing the permits that make it legal to build and to sell. Entitlement does not change the physical land at all. The dirt is the same dirt before and after. What changes is what the land is *legally allowed to be* — and that is where the value is created.

Think about why. An agricultural plot at the edge of a growing city is worth a little, because you can only farm it. The same plot, rezoned for an apartment tower with planning permission in hand, is worth a great deal, because now you can put hundreds of households on it. The uplift in value from agricultural to residential is enormous — often a multiple, not a percentage — and it is captured at the moment the rezoning is approved, before any construction begins. The developer who can get land entitled has performed a kind of legal alchemy: turning cheap dirt into expensive building sites with a stamp.

This is why entitlement, not construction, is where the real money in development is made. Construction is a known cost — you can get three contractors to quote it, and the price is roughly the price. Entitlement is uncertain, political, slow, and risky — and precisely because it is hard, it is where the value lives. A developer's deepest skill is often the ability to identify land that *can* be rezoned, acquire it cheaply while it is still "just farmland," and then push the rezoning through. We will put numbers on this shortly.

### Off-plan presales and the float

**Off-plan presales** are sales of units that do not exist yet — sold from renderings and floor plans before, or during, construction. Minh's purchase was an off-plan presale. The buyer signs a contract to purchase a specific unit and pays the price in installments, usually tied to construction milestones: a deposit on signing, more when the foundations are poured, more at the topping-out, the balance at handover. By the time the building is finished, the buyer may have paid 70% or more of the price.

Crucially, this is not legally a loan. It is a sales contract. But *functionally*, the presale cash is financing — and it is the cheapest financing in the world, because the developer pays no interest on it. Minh's money sits in the developer's hands for two or three years, funding the construction of the very apartment Minh is buying. Economists call money that you hold and use before you have to deliver the thing it paid for a **float**. An insurance company holds a float (premiums collected before claims are paid); a developer running presales holds a float (deposits collected before homes are delivered). The presale float is, in effect, an interest-free loan from the customer — and it is one of the great engines of developer profitability.

### The capital recycle

Put the pieces in motion and you get the **developer capital recycle**: the loop by which a developer turns one project's profit into the next project's land. It runs land → entitle → presell → build → deliver → recycle, and then back to land. Buy a land bank cheaply. Get it entitled, multiplying its value. Presell the units to raise a float that funds construction. Build using the deposits plus a bank loan. Deliver the finished units and collect the final payments. Take the profit plus the freed-up capital and buy a bigger, better land bank — and run the loop again.

This recycle is why developers grow so fast in a boom. Each turn of the loop frees capital and adds profit, and that capital is recycled into a larger land bank, which supports a larger project, which throws off more profit. The compounding can be ferocious. A developer that turns its capital over every two or three years, earning a 20% margin each time and adding leverage, can grow its land bank and its balance sheet at a rate that looks miraculous — until the loop jams.

### Gross development value and development margin

Two terms you need before the math. **Gross development value (GDV)** is the total value of everything the project will sell for — add up the sale price of every unit, and that is the GDV. It is the top line of the whole undertaking. **Development margin** is the profit left after every cost is subtracted from the GDV: land, construction, financing, marketing, and overhead. Margin is the *residual* — the slice that remains for the developer once everyone else has been paid.

The residual nature of margin is the key to understanding developer risk. Because margin is what's left over after fixed costs, it is extremely sensitive to small moves in the GDV or in costs. If the sale price falls 10% but the land and construction costs are already locked in, the margin can be wiped out entirely. A developer is, in financial terms, a leveraged bet on the spread between the price units will sell for and the cost to build them — and that spread is thin and volatile. We will see exactly how thin shortly.

### A developer is not a landlord

One more distinction that clarifies everything. A **landlord** owns property and earns *rent* — a stream of income from holding the asset over time. A **developer** earns a *one-time profit* from creating and selling the asset, and then walks away. The landlord's business is patient and income-driven; the developer's business is episodic and capital-gain-driven. A landlord can ride out a downturn by collecting rent and waiting. A developer cannot wait — it has costs to pay now, debts to service now, and a land bank that produces no income while it sits. This is the deep reason the developer model is fragile and the landlord model is not: the developer has no income to fall back on when sales stop. It lives entirely on the next sale.

Many large property companies are both — they develop *and* they keep some buildings to rent — and the rental arm is precisely what stabilizes them when the development arm seizes. But the pure developer, the merchant who builds to sell, is the most pro-cyclical actor in all of real estate. To see why, we need to put numbers on each piece. That starts with land.

## The land bank: the strategic asset

A developer without land is a chef without ingredients. Everything starts with the land bank, and the whole game is to acquire it *cheaply* and *early* — before the district becomes fashionable, before the road is built, before the crowd arrives and bids the price up. The developer's edge is foresight about where the city will grow, combined with the patience and capital to buy ahead of that growth and sit on it.

Why is buying early so powerful? Because land in the path of urbanization appreciates not linearly but explosively. A plot 20 kilometres from the centre of a growing Asian city, today surrounded by rice paddies, may be nearly worthless as building land — there is no infrastructure, no demand, no permission. But when the ring road arrives, when the metro line is announced, when the city's growth finally reaches it, that same plot can become prime residential land worth many multiples of what it cost. The developer who bought it for a song while it was "just farmland" captures that entire uplift. The developer who waits until the metro is announced pays the appreciated price and captures almost nothing.

This is also why the land bank ties up so much capital and looks, on a balance sheet, like a vast dead weight. Raw land produces no rent. It generates no cash. It costs money to hold — property taxes (small in Vietnam, large elsewhere), the interest on any debt used to buy it, and the opportunity cost of the capital locked up. A developer carrying a large land bank through a slow market is bleeding carrying costs the whole time. In a boom, those carrying costs are trivial next to the appreciation; in a bust, they become a noose. We will return to that noose — the carrying cost of a frozen land bank — because it is one of the most common ways developers die.

#### Worked example: the carrying cost of a land bank when sales freeze

Nam Land holds a land bank it values at ₫2,000 billion (about \$77 million). It funded that land bank with ₫1,200 billion of debt at 12% annual interest and ₫800 billion of its own equity. In a normal year, the land appreciates 15%, so the bank gains ₫300 billion in value while the interest costs ₫144 billion — a comfortable net gain of ₫156 billion, and the carrying cost is invisible.

Now sales freeze. Nobody is buying, so Nam Land cannot sell down the land bank to repay debt. The land stops appreciating — in fact, in a real bust it might *fall* 20%, a ₫400 billion paper loss. Meanwhile the interest clock never stops: ₫1,200 billion × 12% = ₫144 billion of interest must be paid this year, in cash, on land that earns nothing. Next year, another ₫144 billion. The carrying cost that was trivial in the boom is now ₫144 billion a year of pure cash bleed on a depreciating asset, with no sales to cover it.

*A land bank is a coiled spring in a boom and an anchor chain in a bust: the very same asset that compounds your wealth when sales are rising bleeds you to death when they stop.*

The land bank, then, is the asset that defines the developer and also the asset that can kill it. The next question is how a developer makes the land bank worth multiples of what it paid — and the answer is almost never "by building." It is "by getting it entitled."

## Entitlement: where the value is really created

If you remember one thing from this post, make it this: **in development, most of the value is created at entitlement, not at construction.** The construction is a commodity service you can buy from any competent contractor at a competitive price. The entitlement — the legal transformation of cheap land into approved building sites — is the rare, hard, valuable thing, and it is captured before a brick is laid.

Recall what entitlement does. It changes the *legal* status of the land: from agricultural to residential, from low-density to high-rise, from "you may not build here" to "here is your permit for 400 apartments." None of that touches the dirt. But it transforms the economics, because the value of land is set almost entirely by what you are *allowed* to put on it. A hectare you can farm is worth what farming it earns. A hectare you can cover with a 30-storey tower of homes is worth what 400 households will pay to live there. The gap between those two numbers is the entitlement uplift, and it is the developer's deepest source of profit.

![A before and after comparison showing a one hectare agricultural plot worth about 30 billion dong on the left becoming rezoned residential land worth about 90 billion dong on the right, a threefold uplift created purely by the change in legal status](/imgs/blogs/the-developer-business-model-2.png)

The figure above puts the comparison side by side. On the left, the raw plot: agricultural, unbuildable, cheap. On the right, the same plot after rezoning: residential, permitted, worth a multiple. Note that nothing physical has changed between the two panels — no construction has occurred. The entire uplift is legal and economic, captured at the moment the rezoning is approved. This is why developers spend years and significant money cultivating the relationships, doing the studies, and navigating the bureaucracy required to get land entitled. The reward for clearing that gauntlet is the uplift.

Entitlement risk is also where many developers' profits actually come from, and where the ones who fail often fail. Buying land *on the bet* that it will be rezoned is a speculation. If the rezoning comes through, you triple your money. If it does not — if the planning authority says no, or the master plan changes, or the political winds shift — you are stuck holding expensive land you cannot build on, financed with debt you must still service. The developers who consistently win are the ones who read the entitlement game well: who buy land that is genuinely rezonable, who understand the planning process, and who do not overpay for land whose rezoning is a long shot.

#### Worked example: the entitlement uplift

Nam Land buys one hectare of agricultural land at the edge of a growing district for ₫30 billion (about \$1.16 million). As farmland, that is roughly what it is worth — there is no building permission, so no developer premium is in the price. Nam Land's thesis is that the district is in the path of the city's growth and that the land can be rezoned for residential use.

Two years and a great deal of paperwork later, the rezoning comes through. The hectare is now approved for an apartment development. Comparable entitled residential land in the area trades at ₫90 billion per hectare (about \$3.5 million). Nam Land has not built anything — not one wall — yet the land on its books is now worth ₫90 billion. The entitlement turned ₫30 billion into ₫90 billion: a **3× uplift**, a ₫60 billion gain (about \$2.3 million), created entirely by changing the land's legal status.

Contrast that with the construction to come. Building the tower will cost Nam Land, say, ₫1,200 billion, and the contractor doing the work will earn a normal, competitive margin on its fee — a few percent. The construction is a large *cost* but a small *profit*; the entitlement is a modest cost but an enormous profit.

*The contractor earns by building; the developer earns by changing what the land is allowed to become — and the second is where the real money lives.*

So the developer has cheap land made valuable by entitlement. Before we fund the actual building, it is worth pausing on the arithmetic that determines whether the whole project is even worth doing — the breakdown of the sale price into its component costs and the thin slice of margin that remains.

## What is inside the price: the GDV stack and the development margin

Everything a developer does ultimately has to fit inside one number: the price a buyer will pay. To see whether a project makes money, you decompose that price into its parts. Recall the two terms from the foundations. The **gross development value (GDV)** is the total the project sells for — the sum of every unit's sale price. The **development margin** is what is left after every cost is subtracted from the GDV. The discipline of development is, at bottom, making sure the costs plus a margin fit inside the GDV — and the margin is always the *last* thing to be filled, the residual after everyone else is paid.

![A vertical stack showing the gross development value of a unit broken into land cost, construction cost, financing and marketing, and the developer margin, with the margin as the thin residual slice at the bottom](/imgs/blogs/the-developer-business-model-3.png)

The figure breaks a single unit's price into its layers. At the top is the GDV — the sale price the buyer pays. Beneath it are the costs that consume that price: the **land cost** (the entitled land, allocated per unit), the **construction cost** (paid to the contractor), and **financing plus marketing** (loan interest, bond coupons, and the cost of actually selling the units). What remains at the very bottom, after all of those are subtracted, is the **developer margin** — and the figure deliberately draws it as the thin residual slice it really is. The visual makes the central risk obvious: because the margin is the leftover, anything that shrinks the GDV or swells the costs eats the margin first, and the margin can vanish entirely while the costs are barely touched.

This residual structure is why development is so much riskier than it looks from the outside. A buyer sees a confident developer selling a tower for a handsome price and assumes the developer is pocketing most of it. In reality, the developer is pocketing the thin slice that survives after land, construction, financing, and marketing have each taken their cut — and that thin slice is exposed to the full volatility of the sale price on top of a stack of mostly fixed costs. It is a leveraged bet on a spread, and the spread is narrow.

#### Worked example: the GDV breakdown of a unit

Nam Land sells a typical apartment for ₫3.0 billion (about \$116,000). That sale price is the GDV of the unit. Now subtract the costs the price has to cover:

- **Land:** the entitled land allocated to this unit costs about ₫0.75 billion — 25% of the price. (This is where the entitlement uplift was captured; the land was cheap before rezoning and is dear after.)
- **Construction:** building the unit costs about ₫1.20 billion, paid to the contractor — 40% of the price, the single largest slice.
- **Financing and marketing:** loan interest, bond coupons, sales commissions, and advertising come to about ₫0.45 billion — 15% of the price.

Add those up: ₫0.75 + ₫1.20 + ₫0.45 = ₫2.40 billion of cost. Subtract from the ₫3.0 billion GDV and the **developer margin is ₫0.60 billion** — 20% of the sale price. That is the slice Nam Land actually keeps.

Now feel the leverage of that residual. Suppose the sale price falls just 10%, from ₫3.0 billion to ₫2.7 billion, but the costs are already locked in at ₫2.40 billion. The margin drops from ₫0.60 billion to ₫0.30 billion — a 10% fall in price cut the developer's profit in *half*. A 20% fall in price would erase the margin entirely. The buyer who thought the developer had a fat cushion was wrong: the cushion is thin, and it is the first thing to go.

*The developer keeps only the residual after land, construction, financing, and marketing are paid — so a small fall in the sale price produces a large fall in profit, which is the hidden leverage inside every development.*

With the arithmetic of the margin in hand, return to the central problem: the developer still has to find the cash to actually build, and the build will take years. Here is where the model reveals its most ingenious trick — it builds with the buyers' money.

## Presales: building with buyers' money (the float)

A developer that has entitled its land faces an expensive problem: it needs hundreds of billions of đồng to actually build the tower, and the building will take two or three years, during which it earns nothing. The naive solution is to borrow the whole construction cost from a bank and pay it back when the units sell. That works, but it is expensive — every đồng borrowed accrues interest for the years it takes to build, and that interest eats directly into the thin development margin.

The clever solution is **presales**. Instead of borrowing the construction cost, the developer *sells the units before they exist* and collects the buyers' deposits to fund the build. Minh and hundreds of buyers like him pay in installments as the tower rises. The developer uses their cash to pay the contractor. By the time the building is finished, the buyers have collectively funded most of the construction — and the developer has paid no interest on that money, because it is not a loan. It is the **presale float**: the customers' money, held and used interest-free for the duration of the build.

![A before and after comparison showing that financing the full build with debt costs about 290 million dong of interest per unit, while funding it with buyers' presale deposits charges no interest and keeps that money in the developer's margin](/imgs/blogs/the-developer-business-model-4.png)

The figure makes the contrast concrete. On the left, the developer borrows the full construction cost and pays years of interest on it — money that flows straight out of the margin. On the right, the developer collects presale deposits as the tower rises and pays no interest on them, keeping that money in its pocket. The interest *saved* is, in effect, additional profit. For a developer running many projects, the cumulative value of the presale float is enormous — it is one of the main reasons developers can earn high returns on thin equity.

But the float is not free in the deeper sense. It comes with a transfer of risk that the buyer rarely understands. When Minh pays for an apartment that does not exist, he is taking on **construction risk** (the building might not get finished), **delivery risk** (it might be finished late, or smaller, or worse than promised), and **counterparty risk** (the developer might go bankrupt with his money). In many markets, including Vietnam historically, there is no real escrow protecting the buyer's deposits — the money goes straight into the developer's hands and can be, and often is, used for *other* projects or to service *other* debts. Minh discovered exactly this in 2022: the money he paid to build his tower had effectively been spent elsewhere, and when the developer's funding froze, there was nothing left to finish his building. The presale float is the developer's cheapest capital precisely because it offloads the project's risk onto the buyers.

#### Worked example: the presale float covering construction

Nam Land's project has 500 units. Construction costs ₫1,200 billion total, or ₫2.4 billion per unit. The build takes two years. Borrowing rates are 12%.

**Scenario A — build it all with debt.** Nam Land borrows the full ₫1,200 billion construction cost and pays 12% interest for the roughly two years of building. Even accounting for the fact that the loan is drawn down gradually (so the average balance is lower than the peak), the interest bill comes to roughly ₫145 billion over the build — about ₫290 million per unit, money that flows out the door and reduces the margin.

**Scenario B — build it with the presale float.** Nam Land presells aggressively. Buyers pay deposits in stages tied to construction milestones, and by handover they have collectively paid about 70% of the price. Those staged deposits arrive fast enough to fund most of the construction as it happens. Nam Land borrows only a thin bridge to cover timing gaps. The interest bill collapses — say, to ₫25 billion instead of ₫145 billion. The roughly ₫120 billion of interest *saved* (about ₫240 million per unit) stays in the margin.

On a project whose total developer margin might be ₫600 billion, saving ₫120 billion of interest is a 20% boost to profit — created not by building better or selling higher, but purely by replacing expensive debt with the customers' interest-free float.

*Presales let a developer build with its customers' money instead of the bank's, and the interest it never pays is pure margin — which is exactly why developers race to start selling before they start digging.*

The float funds the build. The build produces finished units. The units are delivered, the final payments collected, the profit booked. And then comes the move that makes developers grow like weeds: they recycle.

## The capital recycle: land → build → deliver → next land

A developer's growth is not driven by retained profit alone — it is driven by the *velocity* of capital. The faster a developer can turn one project's capital into the next project's land, the faster it compounds. This is the **capital recycle**, and it is the secret to how a mid-sized firm becomes a giant in a single boom.

Walk the loop. Nam Land starts with, say, ₫800 billion of equity. It uses that — leveraged with debt and stretched with the presale float — to acquire and entitle a land bank, build a project, and deliver it. The project throws off a profit and, critically, *returns the original capital*: once the units are delivered and the final payments collected, the equity Nam Land tied up in the project is freed. Now Nam Land has its original ₫800 billion *plus* the project's profit *plus* whatever capacity its larger, more proven balance sheet can borrow. It deploys all of that into a bigger land bank — and runs the loop again, at a larger scale.

Two things compound here, and it is worth separating them. The first is the *profit* — the development margin booked on each project, which adds to equity. The second, and more powerful, is the *velocity* — the speed at which capital is recycled. A developer that recycles its capital every two years, earning 20% each turn, is not earning 20%; it is earning something far higher on an annualized, compounded basis, because the same capital does the trick again and again. Add the presale float (which lets the developer control projects much larger than its equity) and leverage (which magnifies the equity return), and the compounding becomes explosive. This is how, in a Vietnamese or Chinese boom, a developer's land bank and balance sheet can multiply many times over in just a few years.

But notice what the recycle *requires*: it requires sales. Every turn of the loop depends on selling the units — to free the capital, to book the profit, to fund the next land bank. The recycle is a machine that runs on the fuel of continuous sales. As long as buyers keep buying and the float keeps flowing, the machine accelerates. The moment sales stop, the machine does not slow down gracefully — it seizes, because the capital that was supposed to be freed for the next project is now trapped in unsold units, and the land bank that was supposed to be funded by recycled profit must instead be carried with cash the developer no longer has.

#### Worked example: developer ROE in a boom

Nam Land does one project. The GDV — the total it sells the units for — is ₫1,500 billion (about \$58 million). The total cost (land, construction, financing, marketing, overhead) is ₫1,200 billion. The development margin is ₫300 billion — a 20% margin on GDV.

Now look at the *return on equity*, which is what the developer's owners actually care about. Nam Land funded the project with only ₫300 billion of its own equity; the rest came from the presale float and debt. So the ₫300 billion profit was earned on ₫300 billion of equity — a **100% return on equity** for the project. And because the project took two years, and the capital was then recycled into the next one, the compounding is even more dramatic.

This is the boom math that makes developers look like geniuses: a 20% margin on the project becomes a 100% return on equity, because the equity was a thin slice and the rest was other people's money. Leverage and the float turned a respectable margin into a spectacular equity return.

*In a boom, thin equity plus the presale float plus leverage turns a 20% project margin into a 100% return on the developer's own money — which is why developers chase growth so relentlessly.*

That is the machine working perfectly. Now we have to look at what happens when one input fails — because the very features that make the boom returns so spectacular are the features that make the bust so total.

## Why the model is fragile: presales stop, debt can't roll

Everything good about the developer model — the thin equity, the leverage, the interest-free float, the rapid recycle — depends on one condition: the cash keeps coming in. The machine has two pipes feeding it, presales and debt, and it runs continuously only as long as at least one of those pipes flows. The fragility comes from the fact that, in a downturn, *both pipes tend to shut at the same time*.

![A diagram showing two failure inputs, presales stalling and debt being unable to roll, both feeding into a funding gap that forces a fire sale of the land bank or outright failure](/imgs/blogs/the-developer-business-model-5.png)

The figure shows the failure mechanism. Two danger inputs feed a single funding gap. The first input: **presales stall.** In a downturn, buyers stop buying off-plan — they are afraid of falling prices, afraid the building won't get finished, unable to get mortgages because rates have risen. The float that funded construction dries up. The second input: **debt cannot roll.** Developers do not repay their debt so much as *refinance* it — when a bond matures or a loan comes due, they take out a new one to pay off the old one. This is called *rolling* the debt. In a credit squeeze, the new loan is not available: banks pull back, the bond market freezes, lenders demand repayment they know the developer cannot make. The developer is left with debt maturing and no way to refinance it.

When both pipes shut at once — and they shut together because the same downturn that scares buyers also scares lenders — the result is a **funding gap**: no cash coming in to carry the land bank or finish the half-built towers. And here the developer's lack of a fallback becomes fatal. A landlord could collect rent and wait. A developer has no rent. Its land bank produces no income; its half-built towers produce no income; its only source of cash was the sales that have now stopped. So it faces an ugly choice: a **fire sale** of the land bank at whatever price it can get (often a steep loss, because everyone else is selling too), or outright **failure** — default, restructuring, bankruptcy, the equity wiped out.

This is why the model is pro-cyclical to the point of self-destruction. In a boom, every force compounds in the developer's favor. In a bust, every force reverses *simultaneously*, and there is no income stream to cushion the fall. The leverage that magnified the gains now magnifies the losses. The float that funded the build is gone. The land bank that was a coiled spring is now an anchor chain. And because developers are leveraged on thin equity, it does not take a catastrophic fall in prices to wipe them out — a moderate downturn, combined with a frozen credit market, is enough.

![A two column matrix showing five forces — land appreciation, entitlement uplift, leverage, presale float, and build margin — each compounding in a boom and reversing in a bust](/imgs/blogs/the-developer-business-model-6.png)

The matrix above is the heart of the fragility argument. Five forces drive developer returns: land appreciation, the entitlement uplift, leverage, the presale float, and the build margin. The left column shows each one in a boom, where it compounds in the developer's favor. The right column shows the same force in a bust, where it reverses. The land bank that rose 20% a year now falls and must be carried at a loss. The rezoning that multiplied value 3× freezes — approvals stop, value trapped. The leverage that magnified the gain now magnifies the loss by exactly the same factor. The presale float that funded the build vanishes when buyers stop buying. The 20% margin on sold units turns negative on unsold ones. Every single driver flips. That simultaneous reversal — five tailwinds becoming five headwinds at once — is why a developer can go from record profit to insolvency in a matter of quarters.

#### Worked example: the wipeout in a bust

Recall Nam Land's boom project: GDV ₫1,500 billion, costs ₫1,200 billion, margin ₫300 billion, funded with ₫300 billion of equity — a 100% return on equity. Now run the same project into a bust.

Prices fall 15%, so the GDV drops from ₫1,500 billion to ₫1,275 billion. But the costs are already locked in — the land was bought, the construction contracted, the debt drawn — at ₫1,200 billion. The margin collapses from ₫300 billion to ₫75 billion. A 15% fall in price produced a 75% fall in profit — that is the leverage of a thin margin.

Now make it worse, which is what a real bust does. Presales stall, so Nam Land cannot even sell the units at the lower price — they sit unsold. Meanwhile the debt used to fund the project matures and cannot be rolled. To raise cash, Nam Land must fire-sale unsold units and land at a further discount, say another 15% off. Now the GDV realized is closer to ₫1,080 billion against ₫1,200 billion of cost — a ₫120 billion *loss*. Against ₫300 billion of equity, that is a real hit; combined with the carrying cost of the rest of the frozen land bank and the unrollable debt elsewhere on the balance sheet, the ₫300 billion of equity can be wiped out entirely.

*The same leverage and thin margin that turned a 20% project into a 100% equity gain in the boom turn a 15% price fall into a total loss in the bust — the model is symmetric, and the downside is a wipeout.*

This is not a Vietnam-specific story or a China-specific story. It is the universal physics of the merchant-developer model. But it played out in Vietnam with particular force and particular features in 2022, and that is the clearest case study we have.

## Vietnam's developers: the land-bank race, the bond reliance, 2022 stress

Vietnam's property boom of the late 2010s and early 2020s was a textbook run of the developer machine — and its 2022 freeze was a textbook seizure. Understanding it tells you everything about how the model works and how it breaks, in a market that scaled it up fast.

The boom had all the ingredients. A young, urbanizing population created relentless housing demand. Prices rose fast — Hanoi primary apartments went from around ₫72 million per square metre in 2024 to roughly ₫100 million in 2025 (about +40% in a year), and Ho Chi Minh City from around ₫91 million to ₫111 million per square metre (about \$4,222 per square metre), pushing the price-to-income ratio in HCMC to around 32 years of income, one of the highest in the world. In that environment, developers raced to accumulate land banks across the satellite provinces, fueled by the đất nền (land-plot) fever that swept HCMC's surrounding districts in 2018. Whoever controlled the land controlled the future, and the race to buy it — early, leveraged, on the bet of future rezoning — defined the era.

![A timeline of a Vietnamese developer from 2018 to 2023 moving through land bank build up, bond funded expansion, the 2022 freeze of the bond market, and restructuring in 2023](/imgs/blogs/the-developer-business-model-7.png)

The timeline above traces the arc, illustrative of the sector as a whole. Through 2018–2019, developers built land banks aggressively. Through 2020–2021, they funded ever-larger expansion by issuing **corporate bonds** — IOUs sold to investors, funds, and ordinary savers — which became the dominant funding channel as bank lending to property was constrained. This bond reliance is the crucial Vietnamese feature: developers were not just leveraged on bank loans and presales; they were heavily dependent on a corporate-bond market that could, and did, freeze.

It froze in 2022. In April, the chairman of Tân Hoàng Minh, a flashy developer, was arrested on bond-fraud charges. In October, the chair of the sprawling Vạn Thịnh Phát empire was arrested in a case entangled with Saigon Commercial Bank (SCB), triggering a bank run. The corporate-bond market — the channel developers relied on to roll their debt — seized. Suddenly, bonds could not be refinanced; new issuance collapsed; investors fled. At the same time, presales stalled, because nervous buyers stopped putting deposits into projects that might never be finished. Both pipes shut at once, exactly as the fragility model predicts. The funding gap opened nationwide. Cranes went still. Half-built towers — including, somewhere, the one Minh had paid for — stood quiet. Big developers came under acute stress; the most-leveraged among them faced default and restructuring.

The policy response is itself instructive. The State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) cut policy rates four times in 2023, trying to thaw credit and restart sales. The government issued **Decree 08**, which gave developers relief on bond restructuring — letting them extend maturities and renegotiate with bondholders rather than default outright. Together these measures were an attempt to restart the seized machine: lower the cost of money, buy time on the debt, and coax buyers back. Recovery has been slow and uneven, which is the normal shape of a property thaw — the developer model takes years to recover because the land banks must be worked down, the debt restructured, and buyer confidence rebuilt before the recycle can spin up again.

As for the giants — names like Novaland (NVL) and Vinhomes (VHM) are the ones an international reader will have heard of, illustrative of the spectrum from acute stress to relative resilience. (These are flagged as illustrative examples of the sector's range, not a verdict on any specific company's current state.) The general lesson holds regardless of the individual name: the most-leveraged, most-bond-dependent, most-presale-reliant developers were the ones the freeze hit hardest, and the ones with rental income or stronger balance sheets weathered it better. That is the model's fragility expressed across a whole industry at once.

## Common misconceptions

The developer business model is widely misunderstood, even by people who buy from developers. Here are the beliefs that most often lead buyers and observers astray.

**"Developers earn by building well."** They do not. The contractor builds, and a good contractor earns a modest, competitive fee for it. The developer earns by being right about *land*, *entitlement*, and *timing* — by buying land cheaply, getting it rezoned, and selling into demand. Construction is a commodity cost, not a profit center. The developer that built a beautiful tower but overpaid for the land, or got the timing wrong, loses money; the developer that built an ordinary tower on cheap, well-entitled land at the right moment makes a fortune. The skill is financial and entitlement-driven, not constructional.

**"A big developer can't fail."** Size is not safety; in this model, size is often *concentrated fragility*. A bigger developer has a bigger land bank, more debt, more presale obligations, and more projects mid-build — which means more carrying cost when sales freeze, more debt to roll, and more buyers waiting on undelivered homes. The biggest developers in China (Evergrande), in Vietnam (the 2022 stress cases), and in the US (the homebuilders of 2008) have all failed or come to the brink. The merchant-developer model has no income cushion, and leverage scales the downside as faithfully as the upside. Giants fail precisely *because* they are giants — they ran the recycle hardest and were most exposed when it jammed.

**"The land bank is just inventory."** A retailer's inventory is a cost to be minimized and turned over fast. A developer's land bank is a *strategic asset and a speculation* — held deliberately for years, bet on appreciation and rezoning, and central to the firm's value. But it is also the asset that turns most violently against the developer in a bust: it produces no income, costs money to carry, and falls in value when everyone is forced to sell at once. Calling it "just inventory" misses both its strategic upside and its lethal downside. It is the single most important and most dangerous thing on a developer's balance sheet.

**"Presales protect the buyer because the money funds my building."** In most unregulated markets, the buyer's deposit is *not* ring-fenced for their building. Without genuine escrow, the developer can — and often does — use Minh's money for other projects, to service other debt, or to buy more land. The presale model transfers construction, delivery, and counterparty risk onto the buyer while giving the developer interest-free capital. The buyer is, in effect, an unsecured lender to a leveraged company, with no collateral and often no protection if it fails. Markets with strong escrow rules (Singapore's, for instance) protect buyers far better than markets without them.

**"A developer is the same kind of company as a landlord."** They are opposites in the most important way: the landlord earns *patient income* and can wait out a downturn; the developer earns *episodic capital gains* and cannot wait, because it has no income and ongoing costs. Confusing the two leads people to expect developers to be as stable as landlords. They are not. The developer is the most cyclical, most leveraged, least cushioned actor in real estate.

## How it shows up in real markets

The developer model is universal, and its failures rhyme across countries and decades. Here are the clearest cases — real episodes where the machine in this post ran, and then broke.

**Vietnam's developer stress, 2022–23.** The cleanest contemporary example of the fragility model. Vietnamese developers had built large land banks funded heavily by corporate bonds, with construction financed by presales. When the Tân Hoàng Minh (April 2022) and Vạn Thịnh Phát/SCB (October 2022) cases froze the corporate-bond market, the debt pipe shut: bonds could not roll. Simultaneously, spooked buyers stopped presales, shutting the float pipe. Both inputs to the funding gap fired at once, projects stalled nationwide, and the most-leveraged developers faced default. The SBV's four rate cuts in 2023 and Decree 08's bond-restructuring relief were the policy attempt to restart the machine. It is the developer fragility diagram playing out across an entire national industry in real time.

**Evergrande and the China land-bank model, 2021 onward.** China ran the developer recycle at a scale the world had never seen, and Evergrande was its most extreme expression. Evergrande accumulated one of the largest land banks on Earth, funded with enormous debt and a vast presale book — millions of homes sold off-plan to ordinary Chinese families. When Beijing's "three red lines" policy (2020) clamped down on developer leverage, Evergrande could no longer roll its debt, and the presale float could not cover its obligations. The funding gap was measured in hundreds of billions of dollars. Construction halted on projects across the country, leaving millions of buyers who had prepaid for homes that were not being built — the presale risk transfer, realized on a national scale. Evergrande's default (2021) and eventual liquidation order (2024) is the canonical case of the land-bank-plus-presale-plus-leverage model failing when the credit pipe shuts. Same physics as Vietnam, an order of magnitude larger.

**US merchant homebuilders, 2005–2008.** America's publicly traded homebuilders — the merchant developers who build to sell — ran the model in suburban form. Through the mid-2000s boom, they accumulated huge land banks and land options, building and selling houses into seemingly unstoppable demand, funded with leverage. The US Case-Shiller national home-price index peaked at 184.6 in July 2006. When prices turned and then collapsed — falling about 27% to a trough of 134 by February 2012 — the builders were caught with land bought at peak prices and houses they could not sell. The margins, thin and leveraged, went negative; the land banks were written down by billions; several builders teetered, and the sector took years to recover as the index slowly climbed back (to 308 by mid-2022 and around 330 by early 2026). The lesson was identical to Vietnam's and China's: a merchant developer with a leveraged land bank and no income cushion is devastated when prices fall and credit tightens at the same time.

**Japan's developers after 1990.** When Japan's epic 1980s land bubble burst in 1990, the developers who had accumulated land at the peak — on the belief, common at the time, that Japanese land could only rise — were left holding assets worth a fraction of what they paid, financed with debt that could not be repaid. Many became "zombie" firms, kept barely alive by banks unwilling to recognize the losses, sitting on dead land banks for a *decade or more*. Japan's experience is the cautionary tale of how slowly the developer model recovers when the land bank turns: not quarters, but a generation, because the entire mechanism — buy land, entitle, presell, recycle — depends on rising prices and confident buyers, and when both vanish for years, the machine simply does not run.

**Singapore's escrow-protected presale model.** Not every market lets the presale risk fall entirely on the buyer. Singapore's regulated progress-payment scheme ties buyer payments to certified construction stages and routes them through protected accounts, with strong developer licensing and project-account rules. The result is a market that still uses presales to fund construction but protects buyers far better than the unregulated versions — a useful counterexample showing that the *float* (the developer's cheap capital) and the *risk transfer* (the buyer's exposure) can be partly decoupled by regulation. Where escrow is real, the buyer is much less likely to end up like Minh, paying for a building that never gets finished.

These cases share one spine. In every boom, the developer machine — land bank, entitlement uplift, presale float, leverage, recycle — compounds spectacularly and makes developers look invincible. In every bust, the same five forces reverse at once, the credit and presale pipes shut together, the funding gap opens, and the developer, with no income to fall back on, fire-sells or fails. The names and the decades change. The physics does not.

## When this matters / Further reading

This matters the moment you buy off-plan, like Minh — because when you do, you are not just buying an apartment; you are becoming an unsecured lender to a leveraged company, funding its construction with your savings and bearing the risk that it fails before it delivers. Understanding the developer model tells you what questions to ask: Is my deposit in real escrow, or does it go straight to the developer? How leveraged is this developer, and how much of its funding comes from bonds that must be rolled? Is it sitting on a vast land bank that becomes a noose when sales freeze? Does it have rental income to cushion a downturn, or is it a pure merchant developer with no fallback? Those questions are the difference between a finished home and a frozen one.

It also matters for understanding your city. The skyline you see is the output of this machine — developers who bought land cheaply, got it entitled, presold it, and built. The empty lots held for years, the sudden bursts of construction, the half-finished towers in a downturn — all of it is the developer recycle running, accelerating, and seizing. The model explains who builds your city, why developers chase land so relentlessly, why building booms and busts are so violent, and why even the giants fail. It is the financial machine beneath the concrete.
