> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Commercial real estate is pure income property. You value an office or a shopping centre the way you value a bond: take its net operating income, divide by a cap rate, and you have a price. The bond's coupon is the **lease**, so lease structure is everything — term, escalations, who pays the costs, and how good the tenant's credit is.
>
> - A commercial property is a **bond made of leases**. Its value is `NOI ÷ cap rate`, and that NOI is just the sum of the rent its leases throw off, after costs and vacancy.
> - **The lease is the coupon.** A 10-year lease to a strong tenant with built-in escalations is a high-grade bond; a 2-year lease to a shaky shop is junk. Read the lease, not the address.
> - CRE is **more cyclical and more rate-sensitive than housing** because it has no owner-occupier floor — nobody buys an empty office to live in, so when tenants leave, value can fall with nothing to catch it.
> - Two shocks reshaped the asset class: **work-from-home** gutted office demand and repriced towers (US office values fell roughly a third to a half in the worst markets), and **e-commerce** hollowed weak retail while *lifting* prime and experiential retail. The one number to remember: lift an office cap rate from 6.5% to 9.5% while NOI drops, and value can halve — the building never moved.

In late 2023 a 41-storey office tower in San Francisco's financial district sold for about \$60 million. The same building had traded for around \$300 million in 2019. Nothing about the steel and glass had changed. The elevators worked, the views were the same, the address was identical. What had changed was invisible: the leases inside it. Tenants had shrunk their footprints, walked at renewal, or stopped paying altogether, and the buildings on either side were doing the same thing. A reader who thinks of property as "bricks that go up over time" cannot explain how a tower loses 80% of its value while standing perfectly still. A reader who thinks of property as *a bond made of leases* can explain it in one sentence: the coupons collapsed, the market demanded a far higher yield, and price is just coupon divided by yield.

That is the entire thesis of this post. Commercial real estate — offices and retail, the two segments most people picture when they hear the phrase — is not "bigger houses." It is income property, and income property obeys a different and far harsher logic than the home you live in. A house has a floor under its price: even if no investor wants it, a family wants to live in it, and that owner-occupier demand catches the fall. An office tower has no such floor. If the tenants leave, no family moves in. The price can keep falling until someone is willing to gut the building or knock it down. That missing floor is why commercial real estate booms harder, busts deeper, and reacts to interest rates faster than the residential market — and why it rewards anyone who learns to read it properly.

The diagram below is the mental model for the whole post: the four kinds of commercial property we'll work through, and how they differ not in their address but in their *tenant, their lease length, their yield, and their cyclicality*. By the end you'll be able to look at any office or shopping centre and price it the way a professional does — from its leases up.

We'll build everything from zero, ground each idea in a worked number, and meet two recurring characters: **Dana**, a US investor who owns an office building and a strip mall in a mid-sized American city, and a **HCMC** landlord weighing a grade-A office floor against a mall unit. A quick note before we start: this is educational, not financial advice. We're explaining how commercial property is priced and where the model bites — not telling you to buy or sell anything.

## Foundations: commercial real estate is a bond made of leases

Let's define the terms from scratch, because the whole edifice is only as honest as these definitions. If you've read the [cap rate and income-approach piece](/blog/trading/real-estate/cap-rate-noi-and-the-income-approach) in this series, the valuation machinery will be familiar — we'll recap it quickly and then spend the post on what makes *commercial* property its own animal: the lease.

### What "commercial real estate" actually means

**Commercial real estate (CRE)** is property held to produce income from tenants who use it for business, rather than property someone lives in. The big buckets are **office** (where firms put their workers), **retail** (where shops and restaurants sell to the public), **industrial / logistics** (warehouses, factories, distribution centres — covered in its own [industrial and logistics piece](/blog/trading/real-estate/industrial-and-logistics-property)), **hospitality** (hotels), and **multifamily** (apartment buildings, which sit on the boundary — they house people but are run as income property). This post focuses on the two segments most people mean by "commercial": **office and retail**.

Within each segment there are sub-types that matter a lot:

- **Office** splits by quality grade. **Grade A** (or "prime") is the newest, best-located, best-specified stock — the gleaming towers with good lifts, modern air handling, and a prestige address. **Grade B** is older, serviceable, a notch down. **Grade C** is tired, peripheral, often a teardown candidate. The grade is not cosmetic; in a downturn the gap between grade A and grade C can be the gap between "still rents" and "worthless."
- **Retail** splits by format. **High-street retail** is the individual shop or restaurant unit on a street, usually a single tenant. **Neighbourhood / convenience centres** are small clusters anchored by a supermarket. **Malls / shopping centres** are large enclosed centres with one or more **anchor** tenants (a department store, a hypermarket, a cinema) that pull footfall, plus dozens of smaller **line shops** (also called "inline" units) that feed off that footfall. **Big-box / power centres** are clusters of large-format stores. **Experiential retail** — dining, entertainment, services you can't get online — is the format that has fared best against e-commerce.
- **Mixed-use** combines several of the above in one development: retail at street level, offices above, apartments higher still. It matters because it *diversifies the cash flows*, which we'll see dampens cyclicality.

### The lease is the cash flow

Here is the single most important idea, and the one that separates CRE from the house you live in. **A commercial property does not produce value by existing; it produces value by being leased.** The cash that flows in is rent, and rent flows in only because tenants have signed **leases** — legal contracts to pay a stated rent for a stated period.

So the entire economic substance of an office tower or a shopping centre is its **stack of leases**. The building is the wrapper; the leases are the cash flow. This is why professionals will tell you the most important document in a commercial sale is not the survey or the title — it's the **rent roll**, the schedule listing every tenant, what they pay, when their lease expires, and how creditworthy they are. The rent roll *is* the bond's coupon schedule.

That framing — a building as a bond whose coupons are leases — is what makes everything else in this post fall into place. A bond pays you fixed coupons for a fixed term and then returns (or doesn't return) your principal. A commercial lease pays you a rent coupon for a fixed term and then ends in a **roll** — the moment the tenant either renews, re-prices, or leaves. We'll spend the next section on exactly that structure.

### NOI and the cap rate, recapped

To value the bond we need two numbers. The first is **net operating income (NOI)** — the annual rent the property collects *after* vacancy and operating expenses, but *before* the mortgage, capital expenditures, income tax, and depreciation. NOI is the building's coupon: the cash the bricks throw off, independent of how they were financed.

The second is the **cap rate** (capitalization rate) — the unlevered yield on the building, defined as `cap rate = NOI ÷ price`. Rearranged, it gives the master valuation equation:

$$ \text{Value} = \frac{\text{NOI}}{\text{cap rate}} $$

A *basis point* — a term you'll see throughout — is one hundredth of a percent (0.01%), so 180 basis points is 1.8%. The cap rate is roughly the **risk-free rate** (the yield on a 10-year government bond) **plus a risk-and-growth premium**. When the government bond yield rises, cap rates tend to follow, and at a fixed NOI, a higher cap rate means a lower value. This is the same seesaw that links a bond's price to its yield, explored in the [price-and-yield piece](/blog/trading/fixed-income/price-and-yield-the-seesaw-at-the-heart-of-bonds): price moves inverse to yield.

#### Worked example: valuing Dana's office on NOI and cap rate

Dana owns a mid-sized suburban office building. Its leases produce **\$10 million** a year in rent. After a 5% vacancy allowance (−\$0.5M) and operating expenses of \$2 million (management, insurance, property tax, repairs, common-area utilities), her NOI is:

$$ \text{NOI} = \$10\text{M} - \$0.5\text{M} - \$2\text{M} = \$7.5 \text{ million per year} $$

Suppose office buildings of this quality in her market trade at a **7.5% cap rate**. Then:

$$ \text{Value} = \frac{\$7.5\text{M}}{0.075} = \$100 \text{ million} $$

Dana's building is worth roughly \$100 million. Notice we never mentioned the mortgage, the square footage, or the address — only the coupon (NOI) and the yield (cap rate). That is the whole valuation, on two lines.

*The intuition: a commercial building is priced exactly like a bond — you take what it pays and divide by what the market demands to be paid, and the address only matters insofar as it changes those two numbers.*

### Occupancy, vacancy, and the absence of an owner-occupier floor

**Occupancy** is the share of leasable space currently rented; **vacancy** is its complement (95% occupied means 5% vacant). For income property, vacancy is not a minor nuisance — it is the throttle on NOI. Empty space pays no rent but still costs money to hold (property tax, insurance, security, the utilities to keep it from rotting), so vacancy hits NOI more than proportionally.

And here is the structural fact that makes CRE its own beast: **there is no owner-occupier floor.** When a residential market falls, prices eventually find a floor because the property has a use to a human being — a family will buy a cheap house to live in. That demand catches the fall. An empty office floor has *no* such use to a buyer. Nobody buys a vacant 20th-floor office suite to live in it. The only buyer is another investor, and that investor will only pay `NOI ÷ cap rate` — so if NOI has collapsed, the price collapses with it, and there is nothing underneath. We'll dedicate a full section to this "value cliff" later, because it is the deepest reason CRE is more dangerous and more cyclical than the home you own.

## The lease: term, escalations, net versus gross, and tenant credit

If the lease is the coupon, then the four dials on the lease — **term, escalation, cost structure, and tenant credit** — are what determine whether the coupon is high-grade or junk. Let's turn each dial.

![A timeline showing a ten-year commercial lease paying a rent coupon that escalates three percent each year from one hundred to about one hundred thirty dollars per square foot, then a year-ten roll where the tenant renews re-prices or vacates, followed by a vacancy gap with zero rent](/imgs/blogs/commercial-real-estate-office-and-retail-2.png)

The figure above draws the lease as exactly what it is: a bond. A rent coupon is paid each year, the coupon **steps up** by the escalation, and at the end comes the **roll** — the lease equivalent of principal repayment, except the "principal" isn't cash; it's the *re-leasing risk*. If the tenant renews at the same or higher rent, the bond effectively rolls into a new bond at par or better. If the tenant leaves, you get a **vacancy gap**: months of zero rent plus the cost of fitting out the space for the next tenant. That gap is where CRE bonds default.

### Lease term: how long is the coupon locked?

The **lease term** is how many years the tenant is contractually bound to pay. Longer is generally safer for the landlord because it locks in the coupon — but only if the tenant is good for it. A 15-year lease to a national bank is a long-dated, high-grade bond. A 15-year lease to a struggling regional retailer is a long-dated bond from a shaky issuer, and the length is almost a liability because you're locked to a tenant who might default.

Term varies a lot by segment, which is exactly what the cover matrix showed. **Office** leases typically run 5–10 years, often with **break clauses** (the tenant's right to exit early at a defined point). **High-street retail** runs shorter, 3–7 years, because shops churn. **Mall anchors** sign very long — 10–20 years — because the centre is *designed* around them; **line shops** sign 3–5 years. The **weighted average lease term** (WALT, sometimes WAULT — weighted average unexpired lease term) across a whole building is one of the first numbers a buyer asks for: it tells you how soon the coupons start coming up for renewal, which is when risk concentrates.

A subtle point about term that catches beginners: a long WALT is *most* valuable when rates and risk are rising and *least* valuable when the market is booming. In a downturn, a building with a 12-year average lease term is a fortress — its coupons are locked while everyone else's are rolling into lower rents. But in a roaring market, that same long term means you're stuck collecting yesterday's rent while market rents climb past you; you can't reset until the lease expires. This is the same trade-off a bondholder faces: a long-dated bond protects you when yields fall but traps you at a low coupon when yields rise. Lease term, like bond duration, cuts both ways — which is why professionals don't just want a *long* WALT, they want it matched to where they think the market is going. The phrase to internalize is that a lease's term is its *duration*, and duration is a double-edged sword.

One more wrinkle that doesn't exist in residential: the **roll is staggered, not synchronized.** A 200-tenant office tower doesn't have all its leases expire at once; they're spread across years. This is a feature, because it means the building never faces a single cliff-edge renewal — but it's also why a downturn grinds rather than snaps. As each lease comes up in a weak market, it either rolls down or goes vacant, so the building's NOI erodes year after year as the leases roll, long after the headlines have moved on. The office distress of the 2020s played out exactly this way: the pain didn't hit in 2020, it *kept hitting* through 2024 and 2025 as long pre-pandemic leases expired into a permanently smaller demand pool.

### Escalations: a coupon that grows

A flat rent for ten years would lose value to inflation, so commercial leases almost always include **rent escalations** — scheduled increases written into the contract. The common forms are:

- **Fixed-percent bumps**: rent rises a set percentage each year (e.g. 3% per annum). Predictable for both sides.
- **Indexed bumps**: rent rises with an inflation index like the CPI, often with a floor and a cap.
- **Stepped bumps**: rent jumps at defined milestones (e.g. every five years) rather than annually.
- **Turnover / percentage rent** (common in retail): the tenant pays a base rent *plus* a percentage of their sales above a threshold, so the landlord shares in a successful shop's upside.

Escalations are what turn a CRE lease from a flat bond into a **growing-coupon bond**, and they're a big part of why real estate has historically held up against inflation.

#### Worked example: the cash flows of a 10-year NNN lease with 3% escalations

Dana leases a 50,000-square-foot floor of her office to a tenant on a **10-year triple-net lease at \$100 per square foot**, escalating **3% per year**. Let's trace the rent coupon (we'll define "triple-net" in a moment; for now it just means the tenant also pays the running costs).

- Year 1 base rent: 50,000 sf × \$100 = **\$5,000,000**.
- Year 2: ×1.03 → \$103/sf → **\$5,150,000**.
- Year 3: ×1.03 → \$106.09/sf → **\$5,304,500**.
- Year 5: \$100 × 1.03⁴ ≈ \$112.55/sf → **\$5,627,500**.
- Year 10: \$100 × 1.03⁹ ≈ \$130.48/sf → **\$6,523,900**.

Over the full ten years the tenant pays roughly **\$57.3 million** in total rent, and the coupon has grown 30% from start to finish without Dana lifting a finger. That is a growing-coupon bond. The whole stream is exactly the green steps in the timeline figure above — and the risk sits entirely at year 10, the roll.

*The intuition: an escalating lease is a bond whose coupon ratchets up on a schedule, which is why a long lease to a good tenant is one of the most reliable income streams in all of finance — right up until the roll.*

### Net versus gross: who pays the running costs?

This is the dial beginners most often miss, and it changes NOI dramatically. The question is simple: out of the rent, **who pays the property's running costs** — the property tax, the building insurance, the maintenance, the common-area utilities?

- In a **gross lease**, the tenant pays one all-in rent and the *landlord* pays the running costs out of that rent. The tenant's life is simple; the landlord absorbs every cost increase. Most residential leases and many older office leases are gross.
- In a **net lease**, the tenant pays a (lower) base rent *plus* some of the running costs directly. The gradations are named by how many cost buckets the tenant takes on. The purest form is the **triple-net (NNN) lease**, where the tenant pays the rent *and* the property taxes *and* the insurance *and* the maintenance. Under a true NNN lease, the landlord's rent is almost pure NOI — the tenant has taken on essentially all the operating costs.

Why does this matter so much? Because **NOI is rent minus operating expenses**, and net-versus-gross decides who eats those expenses. The same building can throw off a very different NOI depending on its lease structure.

![A four-by-four matrix of lease structures showing net or triple-net leases gross leases long-term leases and strong-credit tenants against what each means who bears the cost who bears the risk and the effect on the landlord, with NNN and strong tenants producing clean stable NOI](/imgs/blogs/commercial-real-estate-office-and-retail-5.png)

The matrix above lays out the four lease dials against who ends up bearing the cost and the risk. Notice the pattern: an **NNN lease to a strong-credit tenant on a long term** is, from the landlord's seat, almost a pure bond — costs passed through, coupon locked, default risk tiny. A **short gross lease to a weak tenant** is the opposite: the landlord eats the costs, faces the roll soon, and might not get paid at all.

#### Worked example: how net-versus-gross changes Dana's NOI

Suppose Dana's office floor produces **\$5,000,000** of rent (year 1, from the example above), and the property's running costs — property tax, insurance, maintenance, common-area utilities — total **\$1,200,000** a year for that floor.

**Under a gross lease**, Dana collects \$5,000,000 and pays the \$1,200,000 of costs herself:

$$ \text{NOI}_{\text{gross}} = \$5{,}000{,}000 - \$1{,}200{,}000 = \$3{,}800{,}000 $$

**Under a triple-net (NNN) lease**, the tenant pays the \$1,200,000 of costs directly. The base rent the tenant pays is lower — say \$3,900,000, because the tenant is now also covering \$1,200,000 of costs (a total occupancy cost of \$5.1M, roughly comparable) — but *all of that base rent is NOI* because Dana pays no operating costs:

$$ \text{NOI}_{\text{NNN}} = \$3{,}900{,}000 - \$0 = \$3{,}900{,}000 $$

Two things changed. First, Dana's NOI is slightly higher and — far more importantly — **predictable**: if the property tax jumps or the roof needs work, the *tenant* eats it, not Dana. Under the gross lease, a \$300,000 spike in property tax would cut Dana's NOI straight to \$3,500,000. Under NNN, her NOI doesn't move. That stability is why NNN-leased property to strong tenants trades at *lower* cap rates (higher prices) — the income is bond-like.

*The intuition: a triple-net lease turns a landlord into a bondholder — they clip a coupon and the tenant runs the building, which is why the cleanest, most predictable CRE income comes from NNN leases to strong tenants.*

### Tenant credit: is the coupon actually going to be paid?

A bond's coupon is only as good as the issuer's ability to pay it, and a lease's rent is only as good as the **tenant's credit**. A lease to the national government, a money-centre bank, or a blue-chip multinational is a coupon you can almost bank on. A lease to a single-location restaurant or a fashion startup is a coupon that might stop next quarter.

Professionals literally grade tenants like bond issuers. A building fully leased to **investment-grade** tenants (the credit-rating term for low-default-risk borrowers) on long NNN leases trades at a low cap rate because its income is nearly as safe as a corporate bond. A building leased to a jumble of small, un-rated local tenants trades at a much higher cap rate to compensate for the default risk. **Covenant strength** — the legal and financial quality of the tenant's promise — is, alongside lease length, the thing that most separates a 6% building from a 9% building of identical bricks.

There's an entire investment style built on this insight: **net-lease investing**, where the investor deliberately buys single buildings on very long (10–25 year) triple-net leases to investment-grade tenants — a pharmacy chain, a fast-food brand, a logistics operator — and treats the property as a corporate bond with a building attached. The appeal is precisely that it strips out almost everything that makes property hard: no management headaches (the tenant runs the building), no cost surprises (NNN passes them through), and a coupon as reliable as the tenant's credit rating. The price you pay is a lower yield and the concentration risk of betting the whole building on one tenant — if that single tenant fails, you don't have a half-empty building, you have a *fully* empty one. It's the clearest proof that CRE income is a credit instrument: a net-lease investor is, functionally, a corporate-bond investor who happens to hold the collateral directly.

The reverse case is just as instructive. A **multi-tenant** building diversifies the credit risk — if one of twenty tenants fails, you lose 5% of your income, not 100% — but it concentrates the *management* burden and the cost exposure, and it usually means shorter leases and more frequent rolls. There is no free lunch: you trade single-tenant credit concentration for multi-tenant operational intensity. Which one is "better" depends entirely on what you're being paid for the risk, which is exactly what the cap rate is supposed to tell you.

## Office: how it's valued, and the work-from-home shock

Office is the segment where the "bond made of leases" model was tested to destruction in the 2020s, so it's the best place to see the machinery under stress.

### How an office building is valued

An office building's value is `NOI ÷ cap rate`, and its NOI is driven by three things: the **rent per square foot** it can charge, its **occupancy**, and its **operating costs**. Rent per square foot is set by the local office market and the building's grade; occupancy is set by tenant demand; costs are set by the building and the lease structure (net vs gross). A grade-A tower in a tight market with long leases to strong tenants is a low-cap-rate, high-value bond. A grade-B building in an oversupplied market with short leases to weak tenants is a high-cap-rate, low-value bond — even if it sits across the street.

Office has always been the **most cyclical** of the mainstream CRE types, for a clean reason: office demand is a direct function of white-collar **employment and business confidence**. When the economy expands, firms hire and lease more space; when it contracts, they shed staff and hand back floors. Office leases are long, so the pain shows up with a lag — but it always shows up. That cyclicality is why office historically carried a higher cap rate than, say, a supermarket-anchored centre with a 20-year lease.

### The work-from-home shock

Then came 2020. The pandemic forced a global experiment in **remote and hybrid work**, and unlike most shocks, this one *did not fully reverse*. By the mid-2020s a large share of office workers in the US and Europe were in the office only part of the week. The structural consequence: firms need **less space**. A company that hybrid-works at 60% attendance can shed 30–40% of its floor space at the next renewal and lose nothing. Multiply that across an entire market and office *demand* — the thing that fills the building — fell hard and stayed down.

Crucially, because office leases are long, the damage rolled in slowly as leases expired and tenants downsized. US office vacancy climbed from roughly 12% pre-2020 to **around 20% by the mid-2020s** — and in the worst markets (San Francisco, parts of downtown Chicago) well above that. Higher vacancy means lower NOI. And buyers, seeing a structurally smaller demand pool and fearing *more* emptiness to come, demanded a much higher cap rate to take on the risk. Lower NOI divided by a higher cap rate is the value cliff.

![A before-and-after diagram of an office tower showing pre-2020 ninety-five percent occupancy and a six-and-a-half percent cap rate giving a value near one hundred forty-six million dollars, versus post-2020 seventy-five percent occupancy and a nine-and-a-half percent cap rate giving a value near sixty-eight million dollars, a value cliff of about fifty-three percent](/imgs/blogs/commercial-real-estate-office-and-retail-4.png)

#### Worked example: the work-from-home hit to an office tower

Take a grade-B office tower. Before 2020 it ran at **95% occupancy** producing **\$9.5 million** of NOI, and office was seen as a core, low-risk asset, so it traded at a **6.5% cap rate**:

$$ \text{Value}_{\text{2019}} = \frac{\$9.5\text{M}}{0.065} \approx \$146 \text{ million} $$

By the mid-2020s, hybrid work and downsizing pushed occupancy down to **75%**, cutting NOI to **\$6.5 million** (a 20-point drop in occupancy, plus the fixed costs of holding empty floors, hits NOI more than proportionally). And buyers, now wary of office, demanded a **9.5% cap rate**:

$$ \text{Value}_{\text{2025}} = \frac{\$6.5\text{M}}{0.095} \approx \$68 \text{ million} $$

The building lost **about 53% of its value** — \$146M to \$68M — while the steel and glass never moved. Note the *double hit*, which is the key lesson: NOI fell **and** the cap rate rose, and the two effects compound. That compounding is why office repricing was so violent. In the very worst cases — older buildings in downtowns that emptied out, like the San Francisco tower from the intro — the loss ran to 70–80%, because NOI didn't just fall, it approached zero, and an income asset with no income is worth only its land minus the cost of demolition.

*The intuition: the work-from-home shock hit office twice at once — it shrank the coupon and raised the yield the market demanded — and because value is coupon over yield, two adverse moves at once produce a cliff, not a dip.*

This is also where the **grade gap** turned brutal. The shrunken pool of demand that remained flew to **quality** — tenants who kept offices wanted the best, newest, greenest buildings to lure workers back. Grade-A towers in prime locations held occupancy and rent far better than grade-B and grade-C stock. The result was a **bifurcation**: prime office held its value reasonably well while commodity office cratered. "Office is dead" was always too crude — *some* office got stronger. The right statement is that the work-from-home shock **widened the gap between the best and the rest** more than any event in the segment's history.

The cruelest part of the office cliff is a structural feature of income property called **operating leverage**. A building's costs are largely *fixed* — property tax, insurance, basic upkeep, the mortgage — and they don't fall much when tenants leave. So when revenue drops, NOI drops *faster*, because the fixed costs eat a bigger share of a smaller top line. A 20% fall in rent collected can easily produce a 30–40% fall in NOI once you account for the fixed costs of holding the empty space. This is why the worked example showed occupancy falling from 95% to 75% — a 21% relative drop in occupied space — producing a 32% fall in NOI (from \$9.5M to \$6.5M): operating leverage amplified the demand shock before the cap rate ever entered the picture. The same mechanism that makes income property a great inflation hedge on the way up (revenue rises against fixed costs, so NOI rises faster) makes it a value cliff on the way down.

There is also a grim **conversion-cost** floor that is *negative* in practice. When an office gets cheap enough, you'd think someone converts it to apartments, putting a floor under the price. Often they can't economically: office floor-plates are deep (windows far from the core), plumbing and wiring are laid out for offices not flats, and the conversion cost can exceed the value of the resulting apartments. So a dead office isn't worth "land value" — it's worth *land value minus the cost of demolishing the tower that's standing on it*, which can make the whole asset worth less than the bare site. That is the true bottom of the office cliff, and it explains the genuinely shocking sale prices in the worst US downtowns.

## Retail: high street versus malls, and the e-commerce hollowing

Retail tells a parallel story to office — a structural demand shock (e-commerce instead of remote work) that **hollowed the weak and lifted the strong** — but the mechanics differ enough to be worth their own section.

### How retail is valued, and what's special about it

Retail value is still `NOI ÷ cap rate`, but retail rent has a feature the others mostly lack: it is often tied to **sales**. Many retail leases include **turnover rent** (percentage rent): a base rent plus a percentage of the tenant's sales above a threshold. This aligns landlord and tenant — the landlord wins when the shop does well — but it also means retail NOI is partly exposed to **consumer spending**, not just to occupancy. A mall's income falls if its shops sell less, even before anyone vacates.

Retail's other defining feature is the **anchor-and-line structure** of larger centres. A mall is built around one or more **anchor tenants** — a department store, a hypermarket, a cinema — that draw footfall. The dozens of smaller **line shops** pay high rents per square foot precisely because the anchor brings them customers. This creates a dangerous dependency: if the anchor leaves, footfall collapses, the line shops' sales fall, their turnover rent dries up, and many of them leave too. The anchor is the **keystone**; pull it out and the arch can come down. We'll work a number on exactly this collapse shortly.

### The e-commerce hollowing — and the bifurcation

The structural shock to retail was **e-commerce**. As shopping moved online, the demand for certain kinds of physical retail space fell. But — and this is the crucial nuance that "retail is dead" misses entirely — e-commerce did not hit all retail equally. It **hollowed the middle while leaving the ends stronger**:

- **Commodity retail** — the stuff you can buy more cheaply and conveniently online (books, electronics, generic apparel, the mid-tier department store) — got hammered. The American "dead mall," a once-thriving regional centre now mostly vacant after its anchor department stores went bankrupt, is the monument to this.
- **Prime retail** — flagship stores on the best high streets, in the best cities, where global brands want a showcase presence — held up or strengthened. Scarcity of truly prime locations protects their rents.
- **Experiential and convenience retail** — restaurants, entertainment, gyms, healthcare, services, grocery — *grew*, because they sell exactly what you cannot get from a delivery box. A supermarket-anchored neighbourhood centre, selling necessities to a local catchment, has been one of the most resilient CRE assets of the entire e-commerce era.

So retail **bifurcated** even harder than office. A grocery-anchored centre or a prime high-street flagship can be a strong, low-cap-rate asset, while a commodity regional mall is a high-cap-rate, often-distressed one. As with office, the right mental model is not "retail is dead" but "the gap between the best and the worst retail blew wide open."

It's worth being precise about *why* certain retail formats resisted e-commerce, because the logic generalizes. Three properties make a retail asset defensible against online competition. First, **necessity and immediacy** — a grocery store or pharmacy sells things people need now and won't wait two days for, so footfall is sticky and recurring. Second, **experience** — a restaurant, cinema, gym, or entertainment venue sells an *outing*, something a delivery box cannot replicate; this is why "experiential" retail has been the growth story of the format. Third, **showcase value** — a flagship store on a top high street works partly as advertising and brand-building for an omnichannel retailer, so its value isn't only the sales rung up in the store. Retail that has *none* of these properties — a mid-tier apparel store selling commodity goods in a weak location — is exactly what e-commerce eats, because its only offer was "the product is here," and now the product is also on your phone, cheaper.

This is also why **co-tenancy clauses** and the **anchor's** health matter so much more in retail than the headline rent suggests. A mall is a curated ecosystem, and its value depends on the *mix* staying intact. Lose the cinema and the food court empties at night; lose the grocery anchor and the daily-footfall engine stops. Sophisticated retail investors therefore underwrite not just each tenant's rent and credit, but the **interdependencies**: which tenants drive footfall for which others, which leases have co-tenancy outs, and how fragile the whole arch is to losing its keystone. That ecosystem analysis has no equivalent in office, where tenants are largely independent of one another, and it's a big part of why retail valuation is harder than the simple `NOI ÷ cap rate` formula makes it look.

#### Worked example: a mall's value cliff when its anchor leaves

A regional mall produces **\$20 million** of NOI and trades at a **7% cap rate**, so it's worth:

$$ \text{Value}_{\text{before}} = \frac{\$20\text{M}}{0.07} \approx \$286 \text{ million} $$

Its largest anchor — a department store paying modest rent itself but generating the footfall for everyone — goes bankrupt and vacates. Two things happen, and both are vicious:

1. **The direct loss.** The anchor's own rent and the empty space it leaves cut NOI by, say, \$3M directly.
2. **The contagion.** Footfall drops, line shops' sales fall, their turnover rent shrinks, several invoke **co-tenancy clauses** (lease terms that let a line tenant cut its rent or leave if an anchor goes dark), and some vacate. This second-order hit might cut NOI by another \$5M.

So NOI falls from \$20M to roughly **\$12M**. And buyers, now seeing a distressed, half-anchored mall with no obvious fix, demand a far higher cap rate — say **12%**:

$$ \text{Value}_{\text{after}} = \frac{\$12\text{M}}{0.12} = \$100 \text{ million} $$

The mall has lost **roughly two-thirds of its value** — \$286M to \$100M — from one anchor leaving, because the loss compounded through the whole centre and the cap rate ballooned on top. This is the retail version of the office value cliff, and it's why mall investors obsess over anchor health and co-tenancy clauses.

*The intuition: a mall is not a collection of independent shops but a single ecosystem held up by its anchor, so losing the anchor isn't losing one tenant — it can be losing the whole building's income at once.*

## Cap rates by CRE type, and what moves them

We've now seen office and retail repriced by demand shocks. Let's step back and look at the **cap rate landscape across CRE types**, because it tells you at a glance how the market grades the risk of each segment.

![A bar chart of US commercial property cap rates in 2026 showing industrial and logistics at five and a half percent retail at six point eight percent and office at eight point five percent, all measured against a ten-year Treasury floor of four point three percent, with each bar labelled by its risk-premium spread over the Treasury](/imgs/blogs/commercial-real-estate-office-and-retail-3.png)

The chart above shows representative US cap rates by type as of 2026, each measured against the **10-year Treasury** — the risk-free floor. Remember the core relationship: **cap rate ≈ 10-year government bond yield + risk-and-growth premium**. With the 10-year around 4.3%, the spread (the premium) is what the market charges for each segment's risk:

- **Industrial / logistics** trades tightest, around **5.5%** (a ~120-bps premium). Warehouses leased to e-commerce and logistics firms on long terms are seen as the lowest-risk, highest-growth segment — the structural winner of the same e-commerce wave that hurt retail. (It gets its own deep dive in the [industrial and logistics piece](/blog/trading/real-estate/industrial-and-logistics-property).)
- **Retail** sits in the middle, around **6.8%** on average (a ~250-bps premium) — but that average hides the bifurcation: grocery-anchored and prime retail trade far tighter, dead malls far wider.
- **Office** trades widest, around **8.5%** on average (a ~420-bps premium), reflecting the work-from-home demand shock — and again the average masks a chasm between prime and commodity stock.

The ranking is not arbitrary: it is the market pricing **structural demand**. Logistics is winning demand (tighter cap), office is losing it (wider cap), retail is split. Two forces move these cap rates over time:

1. **The risk-free rate.** When the 10-year Treasury rose from ~0.9% in 2020 to ~4.5% by 2026, the floor under every cap rate rose with it, pushing all CRE cap rates up and all CRE values down — the same mechanism explored in the [interest-rates-and-mortgages piece](/blog/trading/real-estate/interest-rates-and-mortgages-the-master-variable). This is why 2022's rate hikes repriced property worldwide, regardless of segment.
2. **The risk-and-growth premium.** This is segment-specific and moves with the demand story: it widened for office (more risk, less growth) and tightened for logistics (less risk, more growth) over the 2020s.

When **both** rise at once — as they did for office in the early 2020s — you get the value cliff: the floor went up *and* the premium went up *and* NOI fell. Three adverse moves, multiplied, produced the worst CRE repricing in a generation.

## Vacancy and the value cliff: why CRE has no floor

We've now seen the cliff three times — in the office worked example, the mall worked example, and the cap-rate discussion. Let's name the mechanism precisely, because it is the deepest single difference between commercial property and the house you live in.

![A flow diagram showing rising vacancy cutting NOI, which divided by a fixed cap rate drops value sharply, while a separate node noting there is no owner-occupier floor means nothing catches the falling price](/imgs/blogs/commercial-real-estate-office-and-retail-6.png)

The diagram traces the chain. A rise in **vacancy** cuts the rent collected, which cuts **NOI**. At a fixed cap rate, lower NOI means proportionally lower **value** — that's just `NOI ÷ cap rate`. So far this is the same maths that prices any income asset. The killer is the fourth node: **there is no owner-occupier floor.** When a house gets cheap enough, a family buys it to live in, and that demand catches the price. When an office or a half-empty mall gets cheap, *no* end-user wants it — only an investor, and an investor will only pay `NOI ÷ cap rate`. If NOI is near zero, the price is near zero (less than zero, really, once you subtract demolition or conversion costs). The fall has nothing to catch it.

This is why CRE is **more cyclical and more rate-sensitive than housing**:

- **More cyclical**, because its demand (firms leasing offices, shoppers filling malls) swings with the economy, and it has no owner-occupier demand to put a floor under the downside. Housing falls and finds a floor; CRE can fall and keep falling.
- **More rate-sensitive**, because CRE is valued *explicitly* on a cap rate that tracks bond yields, with no emotional owner-occupier buyer to muddy the maths. When rates rise, residential buyers grumble but many still buy because they need somewhere to live; CRE buyers simply re-solve `NOI ÷ cap rate` at the higher cap rate and bid less. The repricing is cleaner and faster.

#### Worked example: the value cliff from a 10-point vacancy rise

Dana's office building, worth \$100 million at \$7.5M NOI and a 7.5% cap rate, loses tenants. Occupancy falls 10 points, cutting NOI to **\$6.0 million** (the empty space still costs money to hold, so a 10-point occupancy drop cuts NOI by more than 10%). If the cap rate held at 7.5%:

$$ \text{Value} = \frac{\$6.0\text{M}}{0.075} = \$80 \text{ million} $$

— a 20% loss from a 10-point vacancy rise, *with the cap rate unchanged*. But of course the cap rate rarely holds when a building is visibly emptying; buyers demand more yield for the rising risk. At a **9%** cap rate:

$$ \text{Value} = \frac{\$6.0\text{M}}{0.09} \approx \$67 \text{ million} $$

— a 33% loss. A residential property with 10% of its rooms empty does not lose a third of its value, because someone will buy it to live in. Dana's office can, because nobody will.

*The intuition: in income property, vacancy doesn't just cost you the missing rent — it can trigger a re-pricing that costs you a multiple of the missing rent, because there's no end-user floor to stop the slide.*

## Vietnam's CRE picture: offices, malls, and the grade-A premium

Everything above is the universal model. Now let's see how it plays out in Vietnam, where the commercial property market is younger, thinner, and shaped by a fast-rising middle class and heavy foreign direct investment (FDI).

![A grid of Vietnam's commercial property showing grade-A office at fifty-five to sixty-five dollars per square metre per month earning a scarcity premium, malls and prime retail lifted by a consumption boom, a heavy supply pipeline pressuring grade-B rents, and FDI and consumption as the demand drivers that fill prime space first](/imgs/blogs/commercial-real-estate-office-and-retail-7.png)

### Offices and the grade-A premium

In Ho Chi Minh City and Hanoi, **grade-A office** is genuinely scarce. The stock of truly prime, international-standard towers in the central business district is limited, and the tenants who want it — multinational banks, tech firms, professional-services firms expanding into Vietnam — are growing. That scarcity supports a **grade-A premium**: prime rents (illustratively in the ~\$55–65 per square metre per month range in central HCMC) hold up well, and vacancy in the best buildings stays low even when the broader market softens.

The risk sits in the **supply pipeline**. Large new developments — in HCMC's Thủ Thiêm peninsula across the river from the old centre, and in western Hanoi — are adding significant new grade-A and grade-B space. When fresh supply arrives faster than demand, it pressures rents, especially in grade-B buildings that have to compete with shiny new towers offering incentives. This is the same demand-versus-supply tension the model predicts everywhere; Vietnam's version is that demand is rising fast (a tailwind office in the West lacks) but new supply is rising fast too. Note also that Vietnam did **not** experience the Western work-from-home shock to nearly the same degree — office attendance recovered far more fully — so the office demand story here is one of growth-versus-supply, not structural decline.

### Malls, retail, and the consumption boom

Vietnamese **retail** is riding a powerful tailwind that mature markets envy: a young, urbanizing population with **rising incomes** and a fast-growing appetite for modern retail. Domestic mall operators like **Vincom Retail** and international entrants like **AEON** and **Central Retail** have built large, successful shopping centres, and prime mall space commands strong rents (illustratively ~\$80–150 per square metre per month for prime line-shop space). Footfall in good centres is high precisely because the modern mall — air-conditioned, with dining and entertainment — is itself an *experience* in a hot, dense city, exactly the experiential format that holds up best against e-commerce.

Vietnam *also* has booming e-commerce (Shopee, Lazada, TikTok Shop), so the same hollowing pressure exists — but it lands on a market where modern physical retail is still *under*-penetrated and growing, so the net effect is far more benign than in the over-stored United States. The bifurcation logic still applies: prime, experiential, and grocery-anchored retail thrives; weak, commodity retail in poor locations struggles. But the rising tide of consumption lifts the average in a way it does not in a saturated market.

### Putting the model on Vietnam

#### Worked example: pricing a HCMC grade-A office floor

Suppose our HCMC landlord owns a floor in a grade-A tower leased to a multinational at **\$60 per square metre per month** for 2,000 m², on a 5-year lease. Annual rent is:

- \$60/m²/month × 2,000 m² × 12 = **\$1,440,000 per year** (about ₫37.3 billion at USD/VND ≈ 25,900).
- After ~10% for vacancy/incentives and operating costs the landlord bears, NOI is roughly **\$1,150,000** (≈ ₫29.8 billion).

Vietnamese grade-A office cap rates are lower (richer) than the stressed US office figure because the demand story is growth, not decline — say **7%**:

$$ \text{Value} = \frac{\$1{,}150{,}000}{0.07} \approx \$16.4 \text{ million} \;(\approx ₫425 \text{ billion}) $$

If the wave of new Thủ Thiêm supply pushed cap rates out to 8%, that same floor would be worth `\$1.15M ÷ 0.08 ≈ \$14.4M` (≈ ₫373 billion) — a 12% haircut from a 1-point cap-rate move, with the rent unchanged. The bond maths is identical to Dana's; only the inputs are Vietnamese.

*The intuition: Vietnam's commercial property obeys the exact same lease-and-cap-rate model as the West — what differs is that its demand story is youthful growth rather than structural decline, so its cap rates are richer and its risk is oversupply, not emptiness.*

## Common misconceptions

**"Commercial real estate is just bigger houses."** This is the error the whole post exists to correct. A house is valued on what comparable houses sold for and is backstopped by owner-occupier demand. A commercial building is valued on `NOI ÷ cap rate` — a bond calculation — and has *no* owner-occupier floor. The two assets respond to completely different forces: a house to local wages and mortgage rates, a CRE asset to its leases, its tenants' credit, and bond yields. Treating an office like a big house is how investors get blindsided by a value cliff.

**"A long lease is always safe."** A long lease locks in the coupon, which is great *if the tenant is good for it*. A 15-year lease to a tenant who goes bankrupt in year three is worse than useless — you're locked to a non-payer and the space is dark. Worse, a long lease at a *below-market* rent in a rising market means you're under-earning for a decade with no way to reset. Lease length is only as valuable as the tenant's credit behind it and the rent level it locks. Read the covenant, not just the calendar.

**"Retail is dead."** Retail *bifurcated*; it did not die. E-commerce hollowed commodity retail (the dead American mall) while prime flagships, grocery-anchored centres, and experiential retail held up or grew. Lumping a thriving supermarket-anchored neighbourhood centre together with a failing regional mall under "retail is dead" is exactly the kind of address-level thinking that misses the lease-level reality. Read the catchment, the anchor, and the format — not the word "retail."

**"Office will recover to where it was."** Some of it will; much of it won't. The work-from-home shift was partly structural, not purely cyclical, so a meaningful slice of office demand is *permanently* lower. Prime, well-located, modern buildings are recovering as the flight to quality concentrates the remaining demand. Commodity grade-B and grade-C towers in emptied-out downtowns may never recover as offices and will be converted to housing or demolished. "Office recovers" and "office is dead" are both too crude; the truth is a widening split.

**"A high cap rate means a great deal."** A high cap rate means a *high yield*, which the market is offering for a reason — usually risk. A 12% cap rate on a mall often means the market expects the anchor to leave, the tenants to churn, and the NOI to fall. The yield is high because the coupon may not last. Sometimes a high cap rate is genuine value (a mispriced building); far more often it is the market correctly pricing in trouble. The cap rate is a *question*, not an answer: "why is the market demanding this much yield here?"

**"Vacancy is a temporary cash-flow problem."** In residential, a vacant month is a cash-flow dent. In commercial, vacancy is a *valuation* event — because value is `NOI ÷ cap rate`, falling occupancy cuts NOI, and at the same time signals risk that pushes the cap rate up, so the two effects compound and there's no owner-occupier floor to stop the slide. Vacancy in CRE is not a temporary inconvenience; it is the mechanism of the value cliff.

## How it shows up in real markets

**US office distress, 2020–2026.** The clearest real-world demonstration of every mechanism in this post. Hybrid work cut office demand structurally; US office vacancy climbed from ~12% to ~20% (far higher in San Francisco and parts of Chicago); cap rates blew out as buyers fled the segment; and NOI fell as leases rolled and tenants downsized. Towers in the worst downtowns traded at 50–80% discounts to pre-2020 values — the San Francisco financial-district building from this post's intro is a real archetype of the pattern. The flight to quality meant prime grade-A buildings held up far better than commodity stock, producing the bifurcation. The lesson: when a demand shock cuts NOI *and* raises the cap rate, value doesn't dip, it cliffs.

**The American "dead mall."** Over the 2010s and 2020s, hundreds of US regional malls fell into distress as anchor department stores (Sears, JCPenney, and others) went bankrupt. The mechanism was exactly the worked example: the anchor leaves, footfall collapses, co-tenancy clauses let line shops cut rent or exit, NOI craters, and the cap rate balloons because nobody wants a half-dead mall. Many were sold for a fraction of their peak value and converted — to logistics, housing, or medical use — or demolished. Meanwhile **grocery-anchored** and **prime** centres in the same country thrived. One country, one asset class, two opposite fates, separated entirely by anchor health, catchment, and format — the bifurcation made concrete.

**Grade-A office in Vietnam.** While Western office cratered, prime office in HCMC and Hanoi held up, because Vietnam's office story was growth (FDI, a services boom, multinationals expanding) against limited prime supply, not structural demand decline. Grade-A vacancy stayed low and rents firm even as new supply in Thủ Thiêm and western Hanoi added pressure at the grade-B end. It's a clean illustration that "office" is not one thing globally: the same word describes a distressed asset in San Francisco and a scarce, sought-after one in HCMC, because the underlying lease-and-demand story is opposite.

**The 2022 global cap-rate reset.** When central banks raised rates in 2022, the 10-year government bond yields that anchor cap rates rose sharply (the US 10-year went from under 1% in 2020 toward 4–4.5%). Because `cap rate ≈ bond yield + premium`, the floor under *every* CRE cap rate rose, and at unchanged NOI, every CRE value fell. This hit all segments — even resilient logistics saw cap rates rise from their 2021 lows — and it stacked on top of office's own demand problem to produce that segment's worst year. It is the cleanest demonstration that CRE is a *rate* asset: its price is a bond calculation, and when the risk-free leg of that calculation moves, the whole asset class reprices, explored further in the [global-liquidity piece](/blog/trading/macro-trading/global-liquidity-the-worlds-money-tide).

**Vietnam's modern-retail build-out.** Vincom Retail, AEON, and Central Retail expanding across HCMC, Hanoi, and provincial cities is the upside of the bifurcation playing out in real time. They built large, experiential, well-anchored centres into a young, urbanizing, rising-income market, and footfall and rents have rewarded them. The same e-commerce wave that hollowed weak US retail coexists here with thriving modern malls, because Vietnam's physical retail was under-built to begin with and the experiential format is exactly what e-commerce can't replicate. The model predicts it precisely: prime, anchored, experiential retail wins; commodity retail in poor locations loses — and a fast-growing market lifts the prime end hardest.

## When this matters / further reading

This matters the moment you stop thinking of property as bricks and start thinking of it as **contracts that pay you rent**. Whether you're an investor sizing up an office floor, a business signing a lease (you're now an issuer of someone's bond coupon — negotiate accordingly), a saver wondering why a REIT holding offices fell 40% while the buildings stood still, or simply someone trying to understand why a tower can lose most of its value overnight, the lens is the same: find the leases, grade the tenants, work out the NOI, divide by a cap rate that reflects the risk, and remember there is no owner-occupier floor to catch the fall.

The single discipline that separates people who understand commercial property from people who get hurt by it is this: **read the lease and the cap rate, not the address.** A glamorous tower with short leases to weak tenants is a worse bond than a dull warehouse on a 15-year lease to a blue-chip logistics firm. The address is seductive; the rent roll is the truth.
