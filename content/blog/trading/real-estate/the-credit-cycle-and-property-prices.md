> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Almost nobody buys property with cash, so the availability and price of *credit* drives property prices far more than incomes or population do.
>
> - Loose lending — low rates, high loan-to-value, easy qualification — lifts what a buyer can *pay*, which lifts prices, which raises collateral values, which justifies still more lending. That self-reinforcing loop is the "financial accelerator," and it is the real engine of property booms.
> - When credit tightens or freezes, the same loop runs in reverse: prices fall even though the buildings, the rents, and the population are unchanged. The asset didn't move — the *debt* stacked on it did.
> - Hyman Minsky's ladder explains the timing: stable credit quietly breeds unstable credit, sliding from borrowers who can repay (hedge) to ones who only cover interest (speculative) to ones who need ever-rising prices (Ponzi) — then a shock triggers the "Minsky moment" of forced selling.
> - The one number to remember: hold a buyer's income and savings fixed, and moving from a loose regime (8% rate, 90% LTV) to a tight one (13% rate, 70% LTV) cuts the price they can pay from about **₫6.2 billion to about ₫2.1 billion** — a two-thirds collapse with no change in the buyer at all. Vietnam's 2022 to 2023 bond freeze is this in real life; the US 2008 crisis is the same machine at continental scale.

In April 2022, a half-built tower in a Ho Chi Minh City suburb stopped growing. The cranes went still, the site office emptied, and the buyers who had paid deposits — sometimes 70% of the price for a flat that did not yet exist — started phoning a developer who no longer answered. Nothing physical had changed. The concrete that had been poured was still there; the land under it was still worth what land in that district was worth. What had changed, four hundred kilometres away in Hanoi, was that the police had arrested the chairman of a property group called Tân Hoàng Minh and cancelled its bonds. Within months, the entire Vietnamese corporate-bond market — the pipe through which developers funded construction — froze solid. The tower stalled not because anyone stopped wanting apartments, but because the *credit* that turns a desire for apartments into actual apartments had vanished.

Fifteen years earlier and on the other side of the planet, a school district outside Phoenix, Arizona watched a third of its neighbourhood values evaporate between 2007 and 2009. The houses were the same houses. The families wanted to stay. But the mortgage machine that had been handing out loans to almost anyone, at almost any income, suddenly stopped — and the moment buyers could no longer borrow what the last buyer had borrowed, prices had nothing to stand on and fell.

These two events, on two continents, fifteen years apart, are the *same* event. That is the claim of this post, and it is a claim most people resist, because it sounds like it shouldn't be true. We are taught that property prices reflect *value* — the desirability of a location, the quality of a building, the number of people who want to live there. Those things matter. But they are slow and stable; they do not lurch 30% in eighteen months. What lurches is **credit**: how much banks and bond markets are willing to lend, at what price, against a piece of property. Because almost nobody pays cash, the price a buyer can offer is set by the loan they can get — and when the loan moves, the price moves with it.

The diagram above is the mental model for the whole post: a self-reinforcing loop in which easy credit lifts what buyers can pay, which lifts prices, which makes the collateral worth more, which lets banks lend still more — round after round, until something breaks and the loop spins the other way. This article builds that loop from the ground up. We will define credit and the credit cycle from zero, show exactly how a loan sets the price a buyer can pay, watch the collateral feedback loop amplify a small change into a big one, climb Minsky's ladder from safe finance to fragile finance, and then run the whole machine in reverse to see how a crunch destroys prices without touching a single building. We keep two people as running examples the whole way: **Minh**, a marketing manager buying an apartment in Ho Chi Minh City, and **Dana**, a US investor — in ₫ and \$ side by side, so a Vietnamese and an international reader both stay oriented.

## Foundations: property is bought with debt, so credit sets the price

Before any of the mechanics make sense, we have to define the words. Skim this if you already know them; do not skip it if you don't, because every later section leans on these exact terms.

**Credit** is just borrowed money — the trust (the Latin *credere*, "to believe") that a lender extends when they hand you cash today against your promise to repay tomorrow, plus interest. A mortgage is credit. A developer's bond is credit. A bank's construction loan is credit. When we say "credit is loose," we mean lenders are eager to extend that trust: rates are low, they will lend a large fraction of the price, and they will accept borrowers with thin income proof. "Credit is tight" means the opposite: high rates, small loans, strict checks.

The **interest rate** is the price of credit — the yearly percentage a lender charges for the use of their money. A low rate makes borrowing cheap; a high rate makes it dear. This is the single most important price in the whole economy, because it sets the cost of every loan, and almost every property is bought with a loan.

**Loan-to-value (LTV)** is the loan divided by the property's price, as a percentage. If Minh borrows ₫5.6 billion against a ₫7.0 billion flat, his LTV is 5.6 / 7.0 = **80%**. A high LTV (90%) means the buyer borrows almost the whole price and puts in almost nothing; a low LTV (50%) means they fund half themselves. LTV is a *lending* decision — the bank chooses how much of the price it will finance — and as we'll see, raising or lowering that single percentage moves prices more than almost anything a buyer does.

**Debt-to-income (DTI)** is the share of a borrower's income that goes to debt payments. If Minh earns ₫50 million a month and his loan payment is ₫43 million, his DTI on this loan is 86% — dangerously high; a prudent bank wants total debt payments under roughly 40–50% of income. **Debt service coverage ratio (DSCR)** is the income-property cousin: a rental's annual income divided by its annual loan payment. A DSCR of 1.3 means the rent covers the loan with 30% to spare; below 1.0 means the rent does *not* cover the loan and the owner tops up the difference every month. "Debt service" just means the loan payment. DTI governs whether a *person* qualifies; DSCR governs whether a *property* pays for itself.

**Underwriting standards** are the rules a lender uses to decide who gets a loan and how big: the maximum LTV, the maximum DTI, the income documentation required, the credit history demanded. Loose underwriting (90% LTV, 50% DTI allowed, a payslip is enough) lets far more people borrow far more money. Strict underwriting (70% LTV, 40% DTI, full tax records, a stress test at a higher rate) shrinks the pool of buyers and the size of their loans. Underwriting is the dial that, more than the headline interest rate, actually opens and closes the credit tap.

**Credit growth** is the rate at which the total stock of loans in an economy is expanding — typically measured as the year-on-year percentage change in bank lending, or in lending to a specific sector like real estate. When property-related credit grows 20% a year, a flood of new borrowing power is hitting the housing market; when it grows 2%, the flood has slowed to a trickle. Credit growth is the macro version of "loose versus tight," and it is one of the best leading indicators of where property prices are heading.

The **credit cycle** is the recurring swing between loose and tight: a period of expanding lending, easing standards, and rising leverage, followed by a period of contracting lending, tightening standards, and forced de-leveraging. It is not a calendar cycle that ticks on a fixed clock; it is a *behavioural* cycle driven by memory and incentive. After a bust, lenders are scarred and cautious; as good years accumulate, caution fades, competition pushes standards down, and the seeds of the next bust are sown. The credit cycle is the engine underneath the property cycle — and the property cycle, in turn, is the subject of its own post, [the anatomy of a real-estate cycle and its four phases](/blog/trading/real-estate/anatomy-of-a-real-estate-cycle-four-phases).

A **credit crunch** is the violent, fast end of the loose phase: lending doesn't just slow, it *seizes*. New loans stop being made, existing borrowers can't roll over (renew) maturing debt, and the price and availability of credit gap from "easy" to "impossible" almost overnight. A crunch is what turns a gentle cooling into a crash, because it removes the buyer's ability to pay *all at once* rather than gradually. Vietnam's 2022 bond freeze was a crunch; the 2008 interbank freeze was a crunch.

Finally, two flavours of property credit that behave very differently. **Mortgage credit** is the loan a *household* takes to buy a finished home — Minh's ₫5.6 billion against his flat. **Developer credit** is the financing a *builder* uses to construct property in the first place: bank construction loans, corporate bonds sold to investors, and — crucially in Asia — **presales**, where buyers pay deposits and instalments on apartments that don't exist yet, funding the construction with their own money. The two are linked: mortgage credit lets households buy; developer credit lets the buildings exist to be bought. A crunch can hit either pipe, and we'll see that Vietnam's 2022 crisis hit the developer pipe while America's 2008 crisis started in the mortgage pipe. Both ended the same way, because both are credit.

That's the vocabulary. The thesis of everything that follows fits in one sentence: **because property is bought with debt, the price a buyer can offer is set by the loan they can get — so when credit moves, prices move.**

## How credit sets the price a buyer can pay: the payment-affordability link

Here is the chain of reasoning that most people skip, and it is the foundation of the entire post. Start with a simple fact about how ordinary buyers actually think.

A normal household does not ask, "What is this apartment worth?" They ask, "What can I afford each month?" They have a salary, a sense of how much of it they can devote to housing, and they go to a bank to find out how big a loan that monthly budget supports. The bank runs the arithmetic backwards: given your monthly payment, the interest rate, and the loan term, here is the principal we'll lend you. That principal, plus your down payment, is the maximum price you can bid. **The buyer doesn't choose a price; the buyer chooses a payment, and credit translates that payment into a price.**

This means the price a buyer can pay is *mechanically* a function of three credit variables: the interest rate, the loan term, and the LTV (which, with their savings, sets the down payment). Change any one of them and the affordable price changes — even though the buyer's income hasn't moved a cent. Let's prove it with numbers.

The formula a bank uses to turn a monthly payment into a loan is the present value of an annuity. You don't need to memorise it, but seeing it once makes the mechanism concrete:

$$L = M \times \frac{1 - (1 + r)^{-n}}{r}$$

Here $L$ is the loan principal, $M$ is the monthly payment, $r$ is the monthly interest rate (the annual rate divided by 12), and $n$ is the number of monthly payments (years × 12). Every symbol is something we defined: $M$ is what the buyer can pay each month, $r$ is the price of credit, and $n$ is the term. The punchline is that $L$ falls when $r$ rises — a higher interest rate shrinks the loan a given payment supports. That is the whole payment-affordability link in one equation.

![A line chart of the US 30-year fixed mortgage rate from 2020 to 2026 on the left axis and the maximum loan a fixed monthly payment of fifteen hundred dollars supports on the right axis, showing that as the rate rose from its record low the affordable loan collapsed](/imgs/blogs/the-credit-cycle-and-property-prices-2.png)

Figure 2 shows this happening for real in the United States. The blue line is the 30-year fixed mortgage rate from Freddie Mac's weekly survey: a record low of **2.65% in January 2021**, then a spike to **7.08% by October 2022** and **7.79% by October 2023** as the Federal Reserve fought inflation, settling around 6.5% by mid-2026 (as of 2026-06-16). The amber line is the affordability proxy: how big a 30-year loan a fixed payment of \$1,500 a month can support at each rate. At the 2.65% trough, \$1,500 a month bought a **\$372,000 loan**. At the 7.79% peak, the *same* \$1,500 a month bought only a **\$209,000 loan** — a 44% collapse in borrowing power from a buyer whose income never changed. The two lines are near-mirror images, because they are two views of the same fact: the price of credit and the quantity of house a payment buys move in opposite directions.

This is why "rising rates cool the housing market" is not a vague slogan but an arithmetic certainty. When rates double, the loan a given payment supports falls by roughly a third to a half, so the price the marginal buyer can bid falls by a similar amount — and the marginal buyer sets the market price.

#### Worked example: a rate drop from 11% to 8% raises the price Minh can pay

Minh, our Ho Chi Minh City buyer, has decided he can devote **₫43.2 million a month** to a mortgage payment, on a 25-year term. (We'll keep this his fixed budget throughout, because his salary is what it is.) The question is what price that buys him, and the answer depends entirely on the rate.

Suppose Vietnamese home-loan rates are at **11%**. Running the annuity formula, ₫43.2 million a month over 25 years at 11% supports a loan of about **₫4.41 billion (≈ \$170,000)**. Add his savings of ₫0.62 billion as a down payment, and Minh can bid about **₫5.03 billion (≈ \$194,000)** for a flat.

Now the central bank eases and rates fall to **8%**. Minh's salary hasn't changed; his ₫43.2 million budget hasn't changed. But at 8%, that same payment over 25 years supports a loan of about **₫5.60 billion (≈ \$216,000)** — nearly ₫1.2 billion more. With the same ₫0.62 billion down, Minh can now bid about **₫6.22 billion (≈ \$240,000)**.

A three-percentage-point fall in the rate raised the price Minh can pay by about **₫1.2 billion, or 24%** — with no change in Minh whatsoever. And here's the kicker: every other buyer in his market got the same boost at the same moment. So they don't bid against each other for the *old* ceiling of ₫5.03 billion; they all bid up toward the *new* ceiling of ₫6.22 billion. The cheaper credit doesn't make Minh richer — it just lets everyone pay more, which pushes the price up to absorb the extra borrowing power.

*Cheaper credit rarely makes housing more affordable; it mostly raises the price, because every buyer's borrowing power rises at once and the price climbs to meet it.*

The LTV dial does the same job through a different door. Instead of changing how much a payment buys, it changes how much down payment a buyer needs — which, for a buyer constrained by savings rather than monthly income, sets a hard ceiling on price.

#### Worked example: raising LTV from 70% to 90% triples the price Minh can reach

Now hold the *rate* aside and look only at the LTV. Minh has ₫0.62 billion in savings — that's the cash he can put down, full stop. The question is what price that down payment unlocks at different LTV limits.

At a strict **70% LTV**, the bank finances at most 70% of the price, so Minh's down payment must cover the other 30%. His ₫0.62 billion is 30% of a price of ₫0.62bn / 0.30 = **₫2.07 billion (≈ \$80,000)**. That's the ceiling — his savings simply can't stretch the down payment on anything pricier, no matter what his monthly income could service.

Loosen to **90% LTV**, and the down payment shrinks to 10% of the price. His ₫0.62 billion is now 10% of ₫0.62bn / 0.10 = **₫6.20 billion (≈ \$240,000)**. The same savings, applied at a higher LTV, unlock a flat three times more expensive.

Notice this dial is *completely independent of the buyer's income or the interest rate* — it operates purely on the savings constraint. In a market where most buyers are down-payment-constrained (young households, first-time buyers, exactly the marginal buyers who set prices on the way up), raising the LTV limit from 70% to 90% can roughly triple the price they can reach. This is why regulators who want to cool a hot market reach for LTV caps first: lowering the maximum LTV is the fastest, bluntest way to take borrowing power out of buyers' hands.

*The LTV limit decides how far a buyer's savings stretch into a price; loosen it and a fixed down payment buys a far bigger home, which is why LTV caps are a regulator's first cooling tool.*

Put the two dials together — rate and LTV — and you have the whole "what can a buyer pay" engine. Both are credit decisions made by lenders, not by buyers, and both move the affordable price by tens of percent without the buyer's income shifting at all. That is the payment-affordability link, and it is the first half of why credit, not income, sets property prices.

## The collateral feedback loop: the financial accelerator

The payment-affordability link explains a *one-time* move: rates fall, prices rise, end of story. But property booms aren't one-time moves — they feed on themselves, accelerating as they go. The mechanism that turns a nudge into a stampede is the **collateral channel**, which economists also call the **financial accelerator**. It is the loop drawn in Figure 1, and it is worth walking around it slowly.

Start with the word **collateral**: the asset a lender can seize if the borrower defaults. For a mortgage, the collateral *is* the property. The bank lends against the house and, if Minh stops paying, takes the house and sells it to recover the money. This means the bank's safety depends on what the house is worth: a loan of ₫5 billion against a ₫6 billion house is well-covered (the bank can lose nothing as long as the house sells for over ₫5 billion), while the same loan against a ₫5 billion house is right at the edge.

Now watch the loop turn. Easy credit (low rates, high LTV) lets buyers pay more — the payment-affordability link we just built. Prices rise. But the houses that rose in price are *collateral* for the loans already on banks' books. As prices rise, every existing loan becomes better-covered: a loan that was 80% of a ₫5 billion house is suddenly only 67% of a ₫6 billion house. The banks' risk fell — not because the borrowers got safer, but because the collateral got more valuable. Lower risk means banks are willing to lend *more*: more loans, bigger loans, against the now-more-valuable property. That fresh lending lifts buyers' borrowing power again, which lifts prices again, which lifts collateral values again. **Each loop bids prices higher than the last, and each price rise justifies the next loop of lending.** Rising prices and rising credit are not two separate things that happen to coincide in a boom; they are the same self-reinforcing process viewed from two angles.

This is the financial accelerator: a small initial loosening of credit gets *amplified* by the collateral channel into a large move in prices and lending, because the collateral that backs the lending is itself inflated by the lending. It's the property market's version of a microphone too close to a speaker — a faint sound gets fed back, re-amplified, fed back again, and within seconds you have a deafening screech from a whisper. The whisper here is a rate cut or an LTV loosening; the screech is a 40% price boom.

#### Worked example: the collateral loop over two rounds

Let's quantify it. Suppose a standard flat in Minh's district trades at **₫5.0 billion**, and the local banks lend at a 90% LTV. Today the central bank eases — rates fall from 11% to 8% — and, as our earlier worked example showed, the marginal buyer's borrowing power jumps. Buyers bid the flat up 10%, to **₫5.50 billion**.

**Round one:** the flat is now worth ₫5.50 billion. A buyer financing it at 90% LTV gets a loan of 0.9 × 5.50 = **₫4.95 billion** — up from the ₫4.50 billion (90% of ₫5.0bn) a buyer could borrow against it last month. The collateral rose, so the loan the bank will write against it rose too. More credit is now chasing the same flat.

**Round two:** that fatter loan capacity feeds straight back into bidding. The next buyer, able to borrow ₫4.95 billion plus put down their own savings, can outbid the last, pushing the price up another 10% to **₫6.05 billion**. Against *that* collateral, the bank now writes a loan of 0.9 × 6.05 = **₫5.45 billion**.

In two rounds, with no change in rents, no change in the building, and no change in the number of people who want flats, the price has gone from ₫5.0 billion to **₫6.05 billion (+21%)**, and the lending against it from ₫4.50 billion to ₫5.45 billion. The price rise *caused* the extra lending and the extra lending *caused* the price rise. Try to point at which came first and you can't — that's the signature of a feedback loop.

*In the collateral loop, rising prices and rising credit cause each other, so a small loosening compounds into a large boom that looks driven by demand but is really driven by leverage.*

The accelerator has a dark symmetry that we'll exploit later: it runs just as hard in reverse. When prices *fall*, collateral shrinks, loans become worse-covered, banks pull back lending, the marginal buyer can pay less, prices fall further, collateral shrinks again. The same loop that levitated prices on the way up drags them down on the way back. But before we run it backwards, we need to understand *why* the loop always eventually breaks — and for that, we climb Minsky's ladder.

## Minsky: how stable credit breeds unstable credit

The economist Hyman Minsky spent his career on a single unfashionable idea: that **financial stability is destabilising**. Calm, profitable times don't make a system safer — they make it more fragile, because they erode the caution that kept it safe. His "financial instability hypothesis" is the best explanation we have for *why* the credit cycle turns, and it maps perfectly onto property. The mechanism is a slow slide down three rungs of borrowing, drawn in Figure 4.

![A timeline showing Minsky's three stages of financing degrading as a boom ages, from hedge finance where income covers principal and interest, to speculative finance where income covers only interest, to Ponzi finance where the borrower needs rising prices, ending in the Minsky moment of forced selling](/imgs/blogs/the-credit-cycle-and-property-prices-4.png)

**Hedge finance** is the safe rung. A hedge borrower's income comfortably covers *both* the interest and the principal on their debt — the loan repays itself out of cash flow. A landlord whose rent covers the full mortgage payment with room to spare is a hedge borrower; Minh, if his salary easily covers his amortising loan, is a hedge borrower. Hedge finance is robust: a hedge borrower survives a rate rise or a soft patch because they were never relying on anything but their own income. Early in a cycle, scarred by the last bust, most borrowing is hedge finance.

**Speculative finance** is the middle rung. A speculative borrower's income covers the *interest* but not the *principal* — they can service the debt but not actually retire it, so they must **roll it over**: when the loan matures, they take out a new loan to repay the old one. This is fine as long as lenders keep rolling, but it embeds a fragile assumption — that credit will still be available, at a tolerable rate, every time the debt comes due. An interest-only mortgage, or a developer who repays maturing bonds by issuing new bonds, is speculative finance. As a boom matures and the bust fades from memory, more and more borrowing migrates from hedge to speculative, because speculative finance lets you control more property with the same income.

**Ponzi finance** is the top rung, and the name (after the fraudster Charles Ponzi) is Minsky's, not mine. A Ponzi borrower's income covers *neither* principal nor interest — the only way they can stay afloat is by selling the asset at a higher price, or by borrowing still more against its rising value. A speculator who buys a pre-sale flat with no rental income, paying the interest out of fresh borrowing and counting on flipping it before completion for a profit, is pure Ponzi finance. Ponzi finance *only works if prices keep rising*. The moment prices stop rising, the Ponzi borrower can neither sell at a profit nor borrow more, and they are insolvent.

Here is Minsky's terrible insight: the system *naturally* climbs from hedge toward Ponzi over the course of a boom, because at every step the more-leveraged borrower out-bids the more-cautious one. The hedge buyer who insists rent must cover the mortgage gets outbid by the speculative buyer who only needs to cover interest, who gets outbid by the Ponzi buyer who needs only that prices rise. Prudence is competed away. By the top of the cycle, a large share of the market is financed by people who are solvent *only* if prices keep climbing — which means the market is now a structure that requires perpetual price rises just to avoid collapse.

That structure has a name for its end: the **Minsky moment**. It arrives when something — a rate rise, a regulatory crackdown, a scandal, a credit crunch — stops prices from rising. The Ponzi borrowers, unable to sell at a profit or refinance, are forced to sell at *any* price to raise cash. Forced selling pushes prices down. Falling prices catch the speculative borrowers, whose lenders now refuse to roll their maturing loans against shrinking collateral; they too must sell. The collateral loop, which had been running forward for years, slams into reverse, and the boom unwinds far faster than it built. The Minsky moment is not a black-swan accident that interrupts an otherwise sound market; it is the *destination* the market was walking toward the whole time, one rung at a time.

#### Worked example: a developer's bond rollover fails in 2022

Minsky's ladder is abstract until you watch a real borrower fall off it. Consider a Vietnamese developer — call it a typical mid-sized builder of the 2020–2021 boom. It has built its business on **speculative finance**: it funds construction by issuing corporate bonds, and it repays maturing bonds not out of project profits (the projects aren't finished) but by issuing *new* bonds to repay the old. As long as the bond market is open, this rolls forward indefinitely.

Say the developer has **₫1,000 billion (₫1 trillion, ≈ \$39 million)** of bonds maturing in the second half of 2022. Its plan, as every year, is to issue ₫1,000 billion of new bonds and use the proceeds to repay the old ones — a clean rollover. It holds maybe ₫200 billion of actual cash. As long as it can roll, the ₫800 billion gap doesn't matter.

Then, in April and October 2022, two high-profile arrests freeze the corporate-bond market (we'll tell that story in detail below). New bond issuance falls to essentially zero — nobody will buy a property developer's bond at any price. The rollover doesn't happen. The developer must now repay ₫1,000 billion in cash it does not have. With ₫200 billion on hand, it faces an ₫800 billion shortfall and three bad options: fire-sell finished inventory at a discount, default on the bonds, or beg bondholders to accept a restructuring. This is the Minsky moment for a single firm: the speculative-finance assumption — *credit will be there when I need to roll* — was the one assumption that failed, and everything was built on it.

*A speculative or Ponzi borrower is solvent only as long as credit keeps flowing; the instant the rollover fails, an entity that looked healthy for years is suddenly facing a cliff it cannot cross.*

## When credit tightens: the loop in reverse

Now we run the whole machine backwards, because the reverse is where property *crashes* come from — and the reverse is more violent than the boom, for a reason worth understanding.

When credit tightens — rates rise, LTV limits fall, underwriting hardens, or a crunch freezes lending entirely — the payment-affordability link runs in reverse first. The marginal buyer can suddenly borrow less, so the price they can bid falls. Figure 5 captures this with the starkest possible version: the *same* buyer, the *same* income, the *same* savings, under two credit regimes.

![A before and after comparison showing the same buyer with the same income and savings, who at an 8 percent rate and 90 percent loan-to-value can pay about 6.2 billion VND, but at a 13 percent rate and 70 percent loan-to-value can pay only about 2.1 billion VND, demonstrating that the price a buyer can pay follows credit rather than income](/imgs/blogs/the-credit-cycle-and-property-prices-5.png)

On the left, the **loose regime**: an 8% rate and a 90% LTV limit. Minh's ₫43.2 million monthly budget supports a ₫5.6 billion loan, and with his ₫0.62 billion savings as a 10% down payment, he can pay about **₫6.2 billion**. On the right, the **tight regime**: a 13% rate and a 70% LTV limit. The higher rate alone shrinks the loan his payment supports to about ₫3.8 billion — but the 70% LTV cap bites harder still, because now his ₫0.62 billion savings can only be 30% of the price, capping him at **₫2.1 billion**. Same Minh, same salary, same savings, and his buying power has collapsed by two-thirds. He didn't get poorer. The credit got tighter, and the price he can pay followed it straight down.

When *every* buyer's purchasing power collapses like this at once, the market price has to fall to find buyers — because the bids that used to clear at ₫6 billion now top out at ₫2–3 billion. This is the reverse payment-affordability link, and on its own it would already cool a market hard. But the collateral loop makes it worse.

As prices fall, collateral shrinks. Loans that were 80% of a ₫5 billion house are now 100% or more of a ₫4 billion house — the borrower is **underwater**, owing more than the property is worth. Banks staring at thinning collateral do two things, both contractionary: they refuse to roll over maturing loans against the shrunken collateral (catching the speculative borrowers), and they tighten new lending further to protect themselves, which lowers what the *next* buyer can pay, which pushes prices down again. The accelerator that levitated prices is now dragging them down, round after round, exactly as in the boom but with the sign flipped.

Why is the reverse faster and more brutal than the boom? Three reasons. First, **fear is faster than greed**: lenders who took years to relax their standards re-tighten them in weeks once losses appear. Second, **forced selling has no patience**: a Ponzi borrower who must raise cash sells at whatever price clears today, whereas a buyer on the way up can wait for the right deal. Third, **a crunch removes the buyer entirely** — in a normal slowdown buyers still exist at lower prices, but in a credit freeze the financing to buy *at any price* disappears, so the bid vanishes rather than merely dropping. That's why booms take years and busts take months.

#### Worked example: prices fall though the rent never changed

Here is the cleanest possible proof that credit, not value, is moving the price — an income property whose income is constant while its price halves.

Dana, our US investor, owns a small rental that earns **\$300,000 a year in net rent** (after expenses). In the boom, with cheap, abundant credit, buyers of such properties accepted a low yield — a **5% capitalisation rate**, meaning they'd pay 20 times the annual income. (The cap rate is just annual income divided by price; a 5% cap rate means price = income / 0.05.) So Dana's building is worth \$300,000 / 0.05 = **\$6.0 million**.

Now credit tightens. Lenders demand higher yields to compensate for risk and higher funding costs, and buyers — who must finance the purchase with dearer debt — can only pay a price that pencils out at a **7.5% cap rate**. The rent has not changed by a single dollar; the tenants are the same, the leases are the same, the building is the same. But at a 7.5% cap rate, the building is worth \$300,000 / 0.075 = **\$4.0 million**. A third of the value — \$2 million — vanished, and not one tenant moved out.

What changed was the price of credit, transmitted through the cap rate. When debt is cheap and plentiful, buyers accept low yields and pay high prices; when debt is dear and scarce, they demand high yields and pay low prices. The income is just along for the ride. (The deep mechanics of how cap rates move with credit and interest rates live in the companion post on [cap rates, net operating income, and the income approach](/blog/trading/real-estate/cap-rate-noi-and-the-income-approach), and the rate-and-liquidity backdrop is in [the liquidity cycle and asset prices](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-liquidity-cycle-and-asset-prices).)

*A property's price can halve while its rent is unchanged, because the price is set by the credit available to buy it, not by the income it throws off.*

Figure 3 puts the loosening and tightening side by side on the four dials that actually move a market, so the symmetry is impossible to miss.

![A two-column matrix comparing credit loosening and credit tightening across four rows, showing that loosening raises the loan-to-value limit, eases qualification, lifts prices and surges transaction volume, while tightening reverses every one of those](/imgs/blogs/the-credit-cycle-and-property-prices-3.png)

Read down the **loosening** column and the boom assembles itself: the LTV limit rises toward 90%, qualification eases (low DTI bars, teaser rates, thin proof), prices rise as bigger loans fund bigger bids, and transaction volume surges as easy finance turns lookers into buyers. Read down the **tightening** column and the bust assembles itself from the exact same four rows reversed: the LTV limit drops to 70%, qualification turns strict, prices fall as smaller loans fund smaller bids, and volume *freezes* — because in a tight market buyers can't fund deals and sellers won't cut prices, so transactions simply stop happening. That frozen-volume cell is the tell-tale sign of a credit crunch in property: prices on paper haven't fallen much yet, but *nothing is selling*, because the credit to transact has evaporated. Volume freezes before prices break.

## Developer credit versus household mortgage credit

So far we've mostly followed the household side — the buyer's mortgage. But the buildings the household buys had to be *built*, and construction is funded by an entirely separate credit pipe with its own cycle. Understanding the difference is essential, because it's the reason Vietnam's crisis looked different on the surface from America's while being identical underneath.

**Household mortgage credit** is the loan a family takes to buy a finished home. It's relatively standardised, secured by the property, repaid over decades, and regulated tightly because it touches ordinary citizens. When mortgage credit loosens, *demand* for existing homes rises (buyers can pay more); when it tightens, demand falls. America's 2008 crisis began here — in the mortgage pipe — with too-loose underwriting of household loans.

**Developer credit** is the financing that lets buildings exist in the first place, and it comes in three forms that matter for our story.

The first is the **bank construction loan**: a bank lends a developer the money to build, drawn down in stages as construction progresses, repaid when the finished units sell. This is the oldest and most conservative form, and banks ration it carefully because a half-built tower is poor collateral.

The second is the **corporate bond**: instead of borrowing from a bank, the developer sells bonds — IOUs paying a fixed coupon — directly to investors, often retail investors hunting for yields above bank deposits. Bonds let a developer raise large sums fast and roll them over as they mature. But bond funding is *fragile* in exactly Minsky's sense: it depends on the bond market staying open every time the developer needs to roll. When confidence cracks, the bond market can slam shut overnight, and a developer relying on rollovers is instantly in trouble — precisely the trap we walked through above. Vietnam's developers leaned heavily on bonds in 2020–2021, which is why the 2022 freeze hit them so hard.

The third, and most distinctive in Asia, is the **presale** (in Vietnam, selling *nhà ở hình thành trong tương lai* — "housing formed in the future"). Buyers commit to apartments before they're built, paying a deposit and then instalments tied to construction milestones — often reaching 70% of the price before the building is finished. In effect, **the buyers finance the construction**, lending the developer their money interest-free in exchange for a flat that doesn't yet exist. Presales are wonderful for developers in good times — free, fast funding — but they convert ordinary homebuyers into *unsecured creditors* of the developer. If the developer fails, the buyer has paid most of the price for an apartment that will never be finished, with no building to seize. When the 2022 freeze stalled construction, it was presale buyers — not banks — who were left most exposed, sitting on deposits for towers that had stopped rising.

The crucial link is this: **developer credit and mortgage credit feed each other.** Developer credit builds the supply; mortgage credit funds the demand. A boom needs both pipes open. And a crunch in *either* pipe can crash the market — freeze developer credit and the buildings stop being built and finished (Vietnam 2022); freeze mortgage credit and the buyers vanish even though buildings exist (US 2008). Either way, the common cause is the same: credit, the thing that turns desire into transactions, stopped flowing.

## Vietnam 2022 to 2023: the bond freeze as a credit-crunch case

Now we can tell Vietnam's story properly, because we have every concept it needs. It is, as promised, the textbook case — a credit crunch that crashed property prices and stalled construction without any change in the underlying demand for homes. Figure 6 traces the chain.

![A flow diagram of Vietnam's 2022 to 2023 property crunch, showing bond-funded developers hit by the Tan Hoang Minh arrest in April 2022 and the Van Thinh Phat and SCB arrests in October 2022, which froze the bond market and stalled projects, until the State Bank cut rates four times and Decree 08 allowed bond restructuring to thaw the funding chain](/imgs/blogs/the-credit-cycle-and-property-prices-6.png)

Through 2020 and 2021, Vietnamese property developers had funded a building boom largely with **corporate bonds** — speculative finance in Minsky's exact sense, rolling maturing bonds with new issuance and counting on the market staying open. Retail investors, chasing yields well above the ~6% on bank deposits, bought the bonds eagerly. The whole structure rested on one assumption: that the bond market would be there to roll the next maturity.

In **April 2022**, that assumption took its first hit. The authorities arrested the chairman of **Tân Hoàng Minh** and cancelled around ₫10 trillion of the group's bond issues over disclosure violations. The message to retail bond buyers was unmistakable — these instruments could be unsafe — and demand for property-developer bonds began to dry up.

In **October 2022**, the second and far larger blow landed: the arrest of the chair of **Vạn Thịnh Phát**, an enormous property group, alongside revelations of fraud at the linked **Saigon Commercial Bank (SCB)**. The scandal triggered a partial bank run on SCB and shattered what confidence remained in the corporate-bond market. New bond issuance collapsed toward zero. Developers who, like our worked-example firm, had planned to repay maturing bonds by issuing new ones suddenly *could not roll*. The rollover market — the lifeblood of speculative finance — had frozen.

This was a **credit crunch** in its purest form, hitting the *developer* pipe. The consequences cascaded exactly as our reverse-loop model predicts. Developers facing maturing bonds they couldn't roll halted construction to conserve cash — and across the country, projects **stalled**, leaving presale buyers stranded on deposits for towers that stopped rising. Transaction volume in the property market **froze** before headline prices had fully adjusted — the tell-tale crunch signature from Figure 3, where deals simply stop because the credit to transact is gone. Banks, wary of property exposure, tightened lending, which lowered what remaining buyers could pay, which pressured prices down further. The collateral loop ran in reverse, and the secondary market for completed apartments cooled sharply even as the primary (new-build) market locked up.

The authorities responded with the standard crunch playbook — restore the flow of credit. The **State Bank of Vietnam (SBV)** cut its policy rates **four times through 2023**, lowering the price of credit to coax lending back. And in **March 2023, Decree 08** gave issuers legal room to **restructure and extend bond maturities** — letting a developer negotiate to push a maturing bond out by, say, two years, or to repay bondholders in assets rather than cash, instead of defaulting outright. Decree 08 didn't make the debt disappear; it bought time, converting an acute crunch into a slow workout. Together, lower rates and maturity relief slowly **thawed** the funding chain, and through 2023–2024 the market stabilised, though many stalled projects took far longer to revive than the bonds did.

The lesson is the post's whole thesis in one episode: nothing about Vietnam's *demand* for housing changed in 2022. The population was still urbanising, incomes were still rising, and a 70-square-metre Hanoi flat that people wanted in 2021 was a flat people still wanted in 2023. What changed was **credit** — specifically, the developer-bond pipe froze on a confidence shock — and the property market crashed and stalled anyway. Credit was the engine; when it cut out, the whole machine coasted to a halt regardless of how much demand was in the tank.

## Common misconceptions

Five beliefs about property and credit that feel obviously true and are wrong in ways that cost people money.

**"Low rates always help buyers."** This is the most seductive error. Low rates raise *borrowing power*, but in a market where supply can't respond quickly (and property supply never can — buildings take years to build), that extra borrowing power gets bid straight into prices. We saw it in the worked example: a fall from 11% to 8% raised the price Minh could pay by 24%, but every other buyer got the same boost, so the flat just got 24% more expensive. The buyer ends up borrowing more to buy the same flat. Cheap credit doesn't make housing affordable — it makes housing *expensive but financeable*, which is a different and more dangerous thing, because it loads the buyer with more debt at the top of the cycle.

**"I can always refinance."** This belief is the engine of speculative and Ponzi finance, and it's wrong precisely when it matters most. Refinancing — replacing your loan with a new one, often to lower the rate or extend the term — depends on two things being true *at the moment you need it*: that lenders are still lending, and that your collateral is still worth enough to support the new loan. Both fail together in a crunch. When prices fall and credit freezes, the borrower who was counting on refinancing finds that no one will refinance an underwater property in a frozen market. The Las Vegas teacher couldn't refinance because she was underwater; the Vietnamese developer couldn't roll its bonds because the market was shut. "I'll just refinance" is a plan that works in every state of the world except the one where you'll actually need it.

**"Prices reflect value, not credit."** The intuition is that an apartment is "worth" some intrinsic amount set by its location and quality, and credit just helps you pay that amount. But we've now shown the opposite with arithmetic: the same building, with the same rent and the same tenants, was worth \$6 million at a 5% cap rate and \$4 million at a 7.5% cap rate — a 33% swing driven entirely by the cost of credit. Value and credit aren't separable; in a market where everyone buys with debt, the price *is* a function of the credit available, and "intrinsic value" is the slow-moving anchor that credit swings around, sometimes by 30% or more.

**"Population and income growth drive prices."** Demographics and incomes matter over decades — they set the long, slow trend. But they are far too stable to explain what property prices actually do, which is lurch 30–40% in a couple of years and then give it back. Vietnam's population didn't fall in 2022 and America's didn't fall in 2008, yet both markets crashed. Incomes move a few percent a year; credit conditions move tens of percent a year. When you see a fast move in prices, look at credit, not the census. Demographics are the tide; credit is the wave.

**"A property crash means the buildings lost value."** A crash feels like destruction — wealth evaporating, families ruined — and it's natural to think something real was lost. But the buildings are physically unchanged: same bricks, same location, same shelter. What's destroyed in a credit-driven crash is the *leverage* and the *price*, not the asset. The wealth that vanishes was, in a sense, never solidly there — it was the inflated price that easy credit had bid up, marked back down when the credit left. This is cold comfort to someone underwater on a mortgage, but it's analytically crucial: it means the crash can reverse just as fast as it came when credit returns, which is exactly what happens at the bottom of every cycle.

## How it shows up in real markets

The same machine, at three different scales, in three different decades.

**The United States, 2004–2008: securitization at continental scale.** America's housing boom ran the collateral loop on the mortgage pipe, supercharged by a financial innovation called **securitization** — bundling thousands of mortgages into bonds and selling them to investors worldwide. Securitization meant lenders no longer held the loans they made, so they stopped caring whether the loans were good; underwriting standards collapsed into "NINJA" loans (no income, no job, no assets) and 100%-plus LTVs. Easy credit bid prices up — the Case-Shiller National index ran from 100 in 2000 to a peak of **184.6 in mid-2006** — and rising prices made the mortgage bonds look safe, which fed more capital into more lending, the collateral loop at the scale of the global financial system. When prices finally stalled in 2006–2007, the Ponzi borrowers couldn't refinance, forced selling began, the bonds revealed their rot, and credit froze worldwide. Prices fell about **27% peak-to-trough**, bottoming near 134 in early 2012. The houses never moved; the credit did.

**Vietnam, 2022–2023: the bond freeze.** Covered in detail above, this is the developer-pipe version: a corporate-bond market built on speculative rollover finance, frozen by two confidence shocks (Tân Hoàng Minh in April 2022, Vạn Thịnh Phát/SCB in October 2022), crashing construction and transaction volume without any change in housing demand, then slowly thawed by four SBV rate cuts and Decree 08's maturity relief. It's the cleanest modern example precisely because the trigger was so clearly a *credit* event — arrests and a frozen bond market — rather than a demand collapse.

**China, 2020–2024: developer leverage at its absolute limit.** China ran the developer-credit loop harder than any country in history. Giant developers like Evergrande funded enormous building programs with a triple stack of leverage — bank loans, bonds, and presales — while local governments funded themselves by selling land to those same developers, so rising land prices and rising developer debt fed each other in a national-scale collateral loop. In 2020, Beijing imposed the "three red lines" — hard limits on developer leverage — deliberately tightening credit to deflate the bubble. The result was textbook: Evergrande, the most leveraged of all, couldn't roll its debt, defaulted in 2021 on over \$300 billion of liabilities, construction stalled on millions of presold apartments, and a years-long property downturn followed. China shows the loop at the scale where it merges with public finance, and where deliberately tightening credit — the regulator pulling the dial the other way — was enough to start the reverse.

**Japan, 1986–1991: the original.** The grandfather of all credit-driven property busts. In the late 1980s, loose Japanese monetary policy and a banking system willing to lend against ever-rising land values inflated a property bubble so extreme that the land under the Imperial Palace in Tokyo was, on paper, worth more than the entire state of California. Banks lent freely against land collateral, rising land prices made the loans look safe, and the collateral loop spun for years. When the Bank of Japan finally tightened in 1989–1990, the loop reversed, land prices fell by more than half over the following decade, and the banks — stuffed with loans against collapsed collateral — spent the "lost decades" working off bad debt. Japan is the cautionary tale of how long the reverse loop can grind when leverage was extreme at the top.

Across all four, the pattern is identical and the post's thesis holds: loose credit built the boom through the collateral loop, the system climbed Minsky's ladder from hedge to Ponzi finance, a trigger stopped prices rising, and the loop ran in reverse into a crash — with the buildings, the locations, and the underlying need for shelter unchanged throughout. The anatomy of how those phases feel from the inside is the subject of the companion post on [the anatomy of a property bubble and crash](/blog/trading/real-estate/anatomy-of-a-property-bubble-and-crash).

## When this matters to you / further reading

This isn't an abstraction reserved for crises — it touches anyone who buys, sells, or borrows against property, which is most people's largest financial decision.

If you're a **buyer**, the credit cycle decides whether you're buying near a top (credit loose, prices bid up, everyone able to pay more) or near a bottom (credit tight, prices depressed, few able to transact). The counter-intuitive truth that falls out of this whole post is that the *best* time to buy is often when credit is tight and rates are high — prices are low because few can finance — and the *worst* time is when credit is loosest and buying feels easiest, because that's when prices are most inflated by leverage. The feeling of affordability (a low monthly payment) and actual value (a low price) often point in opposite directions, because cheap credit lowers the payment while raising the price.

If you're a **borrower**, the lesson is to never build a plan on the assumption that credit will be there when you need it — that's the speculative/Ponzi trap. A loan you can only service by rolling it over, or only repay by selling at a higher price, is a bet that the credit window stays open, and credit windows slam shut exactly when you most need them open. Hedge finance — income that covers your debt without relying on refinancing or rising prices — is boring, and it's the only structure that survives a Minsky moment. The mechanics of how that leverage amplifies your gains and losses are worked through in detail in [leverage and the mortgage: how debt amplifies property](/blog/trading/real-estate/leverage-and-the-mortgage-how-debt-amplifies-property).

And if you simply want to *understand* property — why it booms and busts the way it does, why incomes and population can't explain the swings, why the same building can be worth a third more or less depending on nothing but the lending environment — then the credit cycle is the master key. Watch credit growth, LTV limits, and underwriting standards, and you'll see property's turning points coming before they show up in prices. The buildings are stable; the credit is not; and it's the credit that moves the price.

To go deeper, the natural next steps are the rate-and-liquidity backdrop that drives the whole credit cycle in [the liquidity cycle and asset prices](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-liquidity-cycle-and-asset-prices); the phase-by-phase shape of the property cycle this credit engine produces in [the anatomy of a real-estate cycle and its four phases](/blog/trading/real-estate/anatomy-of-a-real-estate-cycle-four-phases); and the inside view of a mania and its collapse in [the anatomy of a property bubble and crash](/blog/trading/real-estate/anatomy-of-a-property-bubble-and-crash).
