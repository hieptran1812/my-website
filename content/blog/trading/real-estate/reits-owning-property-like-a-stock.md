> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A REIT (real estate investment trust) lets you own a slice of a professionally-managed portfolio of income property as a *liquid, divisible share* — and in exchange for a tax break it must pay out most of its income (typically about 90%) to shareholders as dividends.
>
> - A REIT swaps the headaches of direct ownership — illiquidity, lumpiness, hands-on management, the huge ticket — for the **volatility of a publicly-traded stock** and the loss of control over your own leverage.
> - Because the trust distributes about **90%** of its income, it pays almost no corporate tax; the income is taxed once, in your hands, as a dividend. That payout rule is *why* REITs are famous for high, steady dividends.
> - REITs trade more like **stocks in the short run** but track **property fundamentals in the long run**. In 2022, listed REIT prices fell roughly a quarter on rising rates while the actual buildings barely moved — a reminder that the screen price and the brick value are two different things.
> - The US REIT market is deep and mature (200+ listed REITs, well over \$1 trillion). Vietnam's is **nascent** — essentially one listed REIT and a few property funds — so the REIT-vs-direct decision plays out very differently in Hanoi than in New York.

A friend of mine — call him **Minh** — spent most of 2025 hunting for an apartment in Ho Chi Minh City. He had saved hard, and he had, on paper, enough for a deposit on a modest 70 m² flat in a new building on the eastern edge of the city: about ₫7.0 billion (≈ \$270,000) all-in at 2025 prices. But every time he ran the numbers, the same three problems stared back. The ticket was enormous — almost everything he had, plus a 20-year mortgage at a teaser rate that would reset to 13% or 14%. It was completely illiquid — if his job moved him to Da Nang next year, selling would take months and cost a chunk in fees and taxes. And it was a second job he didn't want: tenants, repairs, the vacant months, the late-night phone calls.

Then a cousin who works in Singapore asked him a question that reframed the whole thing: *"Why do you want to own one whole building? In Singapore I own a sliver of about forty malls and office towers, I can sell it before lunch, and it pays me a dividend every quarter. It's called a REIT."* Minh had never heard the word. He is far from alone — most people, even people who own property, have never been taught that there is a way to own income real estate that behaves like a stock instead of like a building.

That instrument is the **REIT** — the real estate investment trust — and it is one of the most important financial inventions of the last seventy years, because it solved the four problems that had kept ordinary people out of professional income property forever: the ticket was too big, the asset was too illiquid, it needed active management, and you couldn't own a *fraction* of a tower. A REIT cracks all four at once. The price you pay for that is real and specific, and we will be honest about it throughout: you give up control of your own leverage, and you take on the daily mood swings of the stock market.

The diagram below is the mental model for the whole post — the two ways to own the very same income property, and what each one costs you.

![Side by side comparison of owning a building directly versus owning a REIT share, showing the direct path as one whole flat with a huge ticket that is illiquid and that you manage with your own leverage, and the REIT path as a divisible liquid managed share that carries stock market volatility](/imgs/blogs/reits-owning-property-like-a-stock-1.png)

We will build everything from zero. By the end you will know exactly what a REIT is, how the trust structure and the 90% rule work, why REITs are famous for dividends, the difference between equity and mortgage REITs, why **FFO** rather than net income is the right way to measure a property company's earnings, how to think about **NAV** and the premium or discount a REIT trades at, and — the decision that actually matters — when a REIT beats owning the building yourself, and when it doesn't. We'll keep Minh as our Vietnamese running example and add **Dana**, a US REIT investor, so the same ideas show up in both a mature market and a nascent one.

A quick note before we start: this is educational, not financial advice. We are explaining how REITs work and where the model bites — not telling you to buy or sell anything.

## Foundations: what a REIT is and the 90% rule

Let's define the thing precisely, because the whole post hangs on getting the structure right.

### A REIT is a company that owns income property, packaged as a tradable share

A **REIT** — real estate investment trust — is a company (or trust) whose business is owning, and usually operating, income-producing real estate: apartment blocks, shopping malls, office towers, warehouses, hotels, data centers, cell towers, hospitals. Instead of you buying one whole building, the REIT owns *many* buildings, and it divides ownership of that whole portfolio into shares. You buy shares on a stock exchange exactly the way you'd buy shares of a bank or a carmaker. Each share is a divisible, liquid claim on a slice of the rent that the portfolio throws off.

That one sentence already dissolves the four problems Minh faced. The ticket is whatever one share costs — you can put in ₫1 million or ₫1 billion. It's liquid — you sell on the exchange in seconds. It's managed — a professional team runs the buildings, signs the leases, handles the repairs. And it's divisible — you own a *fraction* of forty towers rather than the whole of one flat.

### The trust structure, and why it exists

The word **trust** is doing real work here. A REIT is a special legal structure that exists in the tax code of many countries (the US created the modern REIT in 1960; Singapore, Japan, the UK, Australia and dozens of others followed). The deal a country offers is this: *if* a company restricts itself to owning real estate, holds most of its assets as property, earns most of its income as rent, and — the crucial condition — **distributes most of its taxable income to shareholders**, *then* the country lets that company off corporate tax on the income it distributes.

Why would a government do that? To remove the **double taxation** that normally hits company profits. Ordinarily a company earns a profit, pays corporate tax on it, and then when it pays the after-tax profit out as a dividend, the shareholder pays tax *again*. Profit gets taxed twice on its way to you. The REIT structure removes the first layer: the trust pays the income straight through to shareholders without paying corporate tax on it first, and only the shareholder is taxed, once. In effect the REIT is a **pass-through** — a pipe that carries rent from the buildings to the investors with the tax collected only at the far end.

### The ~90% income-distribution rule

The price of that tax break is a hard rule: a REIT must **distribute most of its taxable income to shareholders every year** — in the US the threshold is **90% of taxable income**, and most REITs pay out essentially all of it to be safe. (Other countries set a similar bar; Singapore requires 90% to keep its tax exemption, for example.)

This single rule is the genetic code of a REIT, and it explains almost everything REITs are famous for. Because the trust *must* hand out roughly 90% of its income, a REIT is a **dividend machine** — it pays out far more of its earnings than a typical company. It cannot hoard profits to build an empire; it has to give the rent back to you. The flip side is that a REIT can't fund much growth from retained earnings — it has paid almost all of them out — so to buy more buildings it usually has to raise fresh money by issuing new shares or borrowing. Hold that thought; it matters later.

### Equity REITs versus mortgage REITs

There are two fundamentally different animals wearing the REIT label, and confusing them is a classic beginner error.

An **equity REIT** *owns the buildings*. Its income is **rent**. When you hear "REIT" with no qualifier, this is almost always what's meant, and it's what this whole post is about. An equity REIT is property ownership in a share.

A **mortgage REIT** (often "mREIT") owns *loans against buildings*, not the buildings themselves. Its income is **interest** on mortgages. It typically borrows short-term cheaply and lends long-term at a higher rate, pocketing the spread, and it's heavily leveraged. A mortgage REIT is really a leveraged bond fund in a property costume — its risks are interest-rate and credit risks, not landlord risks. Mortgage REITs are a small slice of the market and behave very differently (they got hammered in 2008 when the mortgage market seized). For the rest of this post, "REIT" means *equity* REIT unless we say otherwise.

The distinction matters more than it sounds, because the two react to the same shock in opposite ways through different channels. When interest rates rise, an *equity* REIT's buildings get repriced (higher cap rates lower the building values) and its dividend competes with fatter bond yields — both push its price down, but its underlying rent keeps flowing. A *mortgage* REIT, by contrast, can be hit directly in its cash earnings: if it funded long-term fixed-rate mortgages with short-term borrowing, a spike in short-term rates can compress or even invert its lending spread, gutting the income that funds the dividend. Mortgage REITs have historically carried eye-watering headline yields — 10%, 12%, sometimes higher — precisely *because* that income is fragile and the market demands a fat yield to bear the risk. A beginner who screens for "high-yield REITs" and buys the biggest numbers is very often buying mortgage REITs without realizing they've left property-ownership behind entirely and stepped into a leveraged interest-rate bet. Know which animal you own.

### FFO, dividend yield, NAV — the three numbers you'll need

Three pieces of vocabulary will recur, so let's plant them now and develop each later:

- **FFO (funds from operations)** is the property industry's preferred earnings measure. It starts from net income and *adds back depreciation*, because the accounting depreciation of a building is a paper charge that doesn't reflect a real cash cost — well-maintained buildings often *rise* in value, not fall. FFO is closer to the real cash a property company generates than net income is. We'll work this out in detail.
- **Dividend yield** is the annual dividend per share divided by the share price — the cash return you get just from the payout, as a percentage. Because of the 90% rule, REIT yields tend to be high and steady relative to ordinary stocks.
- **NAV (net asset value)** is what the REIT's buildings are worth (appraised value) minus its debt, divided by the number of shares — roughly, the "break-up" value per share if you sold all the buildings and paid off the loans. The share price can trade *above* NAV (a premium) or *below* it (a discount), and that gap is itself a signal.

With those four foundations — the trust, the 90% rule, equity vs mortgage, and the FFO/yield/NAV trio — we can now see how the machine actually runs.

## How a REIT works: from buildings to a dividend cheque

Let's trace the full path that turns a portfolio of buildings into the dividend that lands in Dana's brokerage account.

![Pipeline showing rental properties earning net rental income flowing into the trust, which under the ninety percent rule must distribute most of its income, then lists divisible shares on the exchange that pay the investor a dividend and carry a daily price](/imgs/blogs/reits-owning-property-like-a-stock-2.png)

Read it left to right. At the start are the **rental properties** — say, forty shopping centers. Tenants pay rent every month. After the REIT pays the costs of running those centers (management, maintenance, property taxes, insurance, the lease-up costs), what's left is the **net rental income** — the cash the portfolio actually earns. This is the same "net operating income" idea we built up in [the cap-rate post](/blog/trading/real-estate/cap-rate-noi-and-the-income-approach): rent minus the costs of being a landlord, before financing.

That cash flows up into **the trust** — the single legal entity that owns all forty centers. The trust then hits the **90% rule**: it must pay out about 90% of its taxable income to its owners. The owners are the holders of the **listed shares** — millions of divisible slices trading on the exchange. So the rent, having been pooled and net of costs, is split across all the shares and paid out. For you, the shareholder, that arrives as two things: a **dividend** (your slice of the rent, usually paid quarterly) and a **price** (the share's market value, which moves every second the exchange is open).

Notice the fork at the end. The dividend is the *property* part — it comes from real rent and is relatively stable. The price is the *stock* part — it's whatever buyers and sellers decide the shares are worth today, and it can swing far more than the rent does. That fork is the entire personality of a REIT, and we'll return to it in the section on why REITs trade like stocks short-term but property long-term.

#### Worked example: a REIT's dividend yield

Dana is looking at a US apartment REIT. It trades at **\$40 per share** and pays an annual dividend of **\$2.40 per share** (\$0.60 each quarter). The dividend yield is simply the annual dividend divided by the price:

`Dividend yield = \$2.40 ÷ \$40 = 0.06 = 6.0%`

So just from the dividend, Dana earns **6%** a year on her money, paid in cash, before any change in the share price. Compare that to the roughly 1.3% dividend yield of the broad US stock market at the same time, and you see why income investors love REITs: the 90% payout rule forces a fat, regular dividend. If the price later falls to \$30 but the dividend holds at \$2.40, the yield *rises* to \$2.40 ÷ \$30 = **8%** — a new buyer gets more income per dollar, which is exactly why falling prices can make a REIT *more* attractive to an income buyer even as it punishes the existing holder.

*A REIT's dividend yield is the rent the buildings throw off, repriced every day by the share price — the same NOI, divided by whatever the market will pay for it.*

## The REIT capital structure: what actually sits behind one share

It's worth getting concrete about what you own when you buy a share, because a REIT is not just "buildings." It's buildings *funded by a mix of debt and equity*, and your share is one specific layer of that funding.

![Vertical stack showing the buildings as the asset at the top, funded below by senior mortgage debt that is paid first and shareholder equity that is the residual, with your one share being a divisible slice of that equity layer](/imgs/blogs/reits-owning-property-like-a-stock-3.png)

At the top are **the buildings** — the assets, worth, say, ₫10 trillion (or \$10 billion; the structure is identical in any currency). Those buildings were paid for with two kinds of money. Some of it is **mortgage debt** — banks lent the REIT money against the buildings, and that debt is *senior*: it gets paid first, out of the rent, before any owner sees a cent. The rest is **shareholder equity** — all the shares, added together, represent the owners' stake.

Your one **share** is a divisible slice of that equity layer. And equity is, by definition, the *residual*: you own what's left after the rent has paid the operating costs and the lenders' interest. This is the same capital-stack idea we met for developers in [the credit-and-financing post](/blog/trading/real-estate/credit-and-developer-financing) — debt on the bottom (senior, safe, paid first), equity on top (junior, risky, paid last). It's why a REIT with a lot of debt is more volatile: the lenders take their fixed cut first, so a small wobble in rent swings the leftover equity much harder. Leverage cuts both ways here exactly as it does on a personal mortgage — which we dug into in [the leverage post](/blog/trading/real-estate/leverage-and-the-mortgage-how-debt-amplifies-property).

This also answers a question beginners often ask: *if a REIT pays out 90% of its income, how does it ever grow?* It can't grow much from retained cash — it paid that out. So it grows by adding to *this stack*: issuing new shares (more equity) or taking on more mortgages (more debt) to buy more buildings. A well-run REIT issues shares only when they trade at a healthy price and borrows only at sensible rates; a badly-run one dilutes shareholders or over-borrows to chase growth. When you judge a REIT, you're judging how skillfully its managers handle this stack.

There's a second, subtler reason the leverage in this stack deserves your attention: it's the leverage *you can no longer choose*. When Minh buys a flat directly he picks his own mortgage — how much, how long, at what rate — and that personal leverage is one of the great powers of direct ownership. When he buys a REIT, the leverage is already baked in: the REIT's managers decided to fund the buildings 40% with debt, and Minh inherits that choice whether he likes it or not. A conservative REIT might run 30% loan-to-value and sleep soundly through a downturn; an aggressive one might run 60% and amplify both its gains and its risk of distress when refinancing comes due in a high-rate year. So "how much leverage does this REIT carry?" is one of the first questions a careful buyer asks — it's a decision that's been made for you, and it shapes how violently your share will move. This is the precise sense in which a REIT *removes your control over leverage*, the trade-off the cover figure flagged.

A related judgment is *who runs the trust and how they're paid*. Some REITs are **internally managed** — the management team works directly for the shareholders, and their interests are reasonably aligned. Others are **externally managed** — a separate company runs the REIT for a fee, often a fee based on the *size* of the assets, which can tempt the manager to grow the portfolio (issuing shares, buying buildings) even when growth doesn't help shareholders, because a bigger portfolio means a bigger fee. It's not automatically bad, but external management with size-based fees is a conflict worth understanding before you buy. The buildings can be excellent and the structure can still leak value to the manager.

## FFO: the right earnings measure for a REIT

Here is where REITs trip up investors who treat them like ordinary stocks. If you pull up a REIT's net income and divide the price by it to get a P/E ratio, the number you get is **misleading** — often wildly so. The reason is depreciation, and fixing it is the single most useful piece of REIT-specific knowledge you can have.

### Why net income lies about a property company

Accounting requires a company to **depreciate** its buildings — to record a chunk of the building's cost as an expense every year, on the theory that the building wears out. For a factory machine that genuinely wears out, this is sensible. For a *building*, it's often nonsense: a well-located, well-maintained office tower or apartment block tends to *hold or grow* its value over decades, not crumble to zero on the accountant's schedule. Land underneath it certainly doesn't depreciate — as we saw in [the land-vs-buildings post](/blog/trading/real-estate/land-vs-buildings-two-assets-in-every-property), land is the part that usually *appreciates*.

So depreciation is a **paper charge**: it reduces reported net income, but no cash actually left the building that year because of it. A REIT can have a thin or even negative net income while generating a thick stream of real cash. If you valued it on net income, you'd think it earned almost nothing. That's the trap.

### FFO = net income + depreciation (roughly)

The industry's fix is **funds from operations (FFO)**. The clean definition: start from net income, **add back the depreciation and amortization** of real-estate assets, and subtract any one-off gains from selling buildings (because those aren't recurring rental income). What you're left with is much closer to the recurring cash the property operation actually throws off.

`FFO ≈ Net income + Real-estate depreciation − Gains on property sales`

A close cousin, **AFFO (adjusted FFO)**, goes one step further and subtracts the *recurring* capital you must spend to keep the buildings competitive (new roofs, lease commissions, tenant fit-outs) — the maintenance capex that FFO ignores. AFFO is the most honest "how much can this REIT really afford to pay me" number, and the one a careful investor checks the dividend against. The rule of thumb: if the dividend is comfortably below AFFO, it's well-covered; if the dividend exceeds AFFO, the REIT is paying you partly with money it doesn't sustainably have.

#### Worked example: FFO versus net income

Dana's apartment REIT reports for the year:

- Rental income net of operating costs (the property-level cash): **\$120 million**
- Interest on its mortgages: **−\$30 million**
- Building depreciation (a paper charge): **−\$70 million**

Its **net income** is \$120M − \$30M − \$70M = **\$20 million**. With 100 million shares, that's \$0.20 of net earnings per share. On a \$40 share price, the "P/E" looks like 40 ÷ 0.20 = a terrifying **200×** — it looks absurdly expensive.

Now compute **FFO**: add the \$70M of depreciation back, because no cash left for it: \$20M + \$70M = **\$90 million**, or \$0.90 per share. The price-to-FFO multiple is 40 ÷ 0.90 ≈ **44×** — still not cheap, but a completely different, and *correct*, picture. And notice the dividend: the REIT pays \$2.40 per share, or \$240M — wait, that exceeds FFO. Let me re-scale: at \$0.90 FFO per share the REIT can afford a dividend around \$0.80–\$0.85 (it must pay out ~90% of *taxable* income, which after depreciation is small, but it pays from cash/FFO). The point stands: **judge the dividend against FFO/AFFO, never against net income.** Net income said this REIT earned \$0.20 a share; the cash reality was \$0.90.

*Depreciation makes a healthy REIT look unprofitable on paper; add it back and you see the real rent-driven cash — which is exactly what the dividend is paid from.*

## REIT versus direct ownership: the core trade-offs

Now the decision that actually matters — the one Minh is wrestling with. Owning a REIT and owning a building directly give you the *same underlying thing*: exposure to income property. But they hand you a completely different bundle of frictions. Lay them side by side.

![Matrix comparing a REIT share against a directly owned building across liquidity, ticket size, management, leverage control, daily volatility and tax, showing the REIT winning on liquidity ticket and management while the direct building wins on leverage control and quoted volatility](/imgs/blogs/reits-owning-property-like-a-stock-4.png)

Walk across the row for the **REIT share**. Liquidity is high — you sell in seconds. The ticket is tiny — you can start with ₫1 million. Management is done for you — the trust runs everything, you're fully passive. Daily volatility is high — the price is on a screen, swinging every day. The REIT picks its own leverage — you don't control it. And tax: the trust pays little, you're taxed on the dividend.

Now the **direct building** row, and notice it's almost the mirror image. Liquidity is low — selling takes weeks or months and costs real money. The ticket is huge — ₫7 billion all at once. Management is your job — tenants, repairs, vacancy, your time. Daily volatility *looks* low — there's no daily price tag, so it *feels* calm (we'll see this is partly an illusion). You control the leverage — you choose your own mortgage. And you pay the property and rental taxes directly.

The pattern is clear: **a REIT wins decisively on liquidity, ticket size, and management** — the three things that lock ordinary people out of professional property. **Direct ownership wins on two things that matter to some people a lot**: you control your own leverage (you can put a big, cheap, long mortgage on a building you control, which a REIT can't do for you), and you escape the daily price ticker (which we'll argue is more about *feelings* than risk). Everything else — the actual property risk, the rent, the long-run value — is broadly similar, because under both wrappers you own buildings.

#### Worked example: a REIT versus buying a ₫7bn flat

Let's make Minh's choice concrete. He has ₫1.4 billion saved (≈ \$54,000) and is weighing two paths.

**Path A — buy the flat directly.** The flat costs ₫7.0 billion. He puts down his ₫1.4 billion (a 20% deposit) and borrows ₫5.6 billion on a mortgage. He now owns one whole flat, highly leveraged (5:1), illiquid, and he's the landlord. If the flat appreciates 10%, his ₫1.4bn equity roughly *doubles* to ₫2.1bn before costs (leverage amplifies the gain — and the loss). If he needs the money next year, he must sell the whole thing, pay the 2% transfer tax and agent fees, and wait months for a buyer.

**Path B — buy a property REIT.** Minh puts the same ₫1.4 billion into shares of a listed property REIT (or, since Vietnam's REIT market is tiny, a regional one). He now owns a *divisible slice* of dozens of professionally-run buildings. He can sell ₫200 million of it next Tuesday and keep the rest. He collects a quarterly dividend — say a 5% yield, ₫70 million a year in cash. He does zero management. But the price is on a screen, and in a bad year it might fall 25% even if the buildings are fine, and he can't put his own cheap 20-year mortgage on it.

The deciding questions aren't about returns — both own property. They're: *Does Minh need the money to stay liquid and divisible? Does he want to be a landlord? Does he want to control his own leverage?* If he values liquidity and a passive life, Path B. If he wants maximum leverage on an asset he controls and can stomach being a landlord, Path A. There's no universally right answer — but now he can actually *see* the trade.

*A REIT lets Minh own property with the ₫1.4 billion he has today, sell a piece of it next week, and never fix a tenant's plumbing — at the cost of a flashing price and someone else's hand on the leverage.*

## Why REITs trade like stocks short-term, but track property long-term

This is the most misunderstood thing about REITs, and getting it right is what separates a frustrated REIT investor from a calm one. The claim is precise: **in the short run a REIT's price moves with the stock market and its own supply-and-demand on the exchange; in the long run it's pulled back to the value of the buildings it owns.** The price and the property can diverge a lot, and for a while — but the rope between them is real.

![Illustrative line chart showing a smooth rising underlying property value line and a volatile listed REIT price line that swings around it, dropping sharply in a rate driven selloff well below the property value before reconverging over the long run](/imgs/blogs/reits-owning-property-like-a-stock-5.png)

The amber line is the **underlying property value (NAV)** — what the buildings would appraise for, minus debt, per share. It's smooth, because buildings are appraised only occasionally and their value changes slowly. The blue line is the **listed REIT price** — what the shares actually trade for, every second. It wanders all over the place around the property value: sometimes above (a *premium* to NAV), sometimes below (a *discount*). And in the middle, around year six, the price *cracks* — it plunges far below the property value in a rate-driven selloff — even though the amber property line barely dips. Then, over time, the rope pulls the blue line back toward the amber one.

### Why short-term: it's traded by stock investors on a stock exchange

A REIT share lives in a brokerage account next to your tech stocks and your bank shares. It's bought and sold by the same people, using the same risk appetite, on the same days. When the whole stock market panics, REITs get sold along with everything else — not because anyone reappraised the buildings that afternoon, but because *that's how listed things behave*. REITs are also acutely **interest-rate sensitive**, for two compounding reasons: their fat dividend competes with bond yields (when bonds yield more, a REIT's fixed dividend is worth less, so its price falls — the same seesaw we cover in [the rates-and-mortgages post](/blog/trading/real-estate/interest-rates-and-mortgages-the-master-variable)), and higher rates raise the cap rate used to value the buildings, which lowers property values too. So when rates jump, REIT *prices* fall fast — faster than the buildings actually reprice.

### Why long-term: the dividend is real rent, and value is buildings

But a REIT isn't a meme — it owns *cash-producing buildings*. Over years, two anchors drag the price back toward the property value. First, the **dividend is real**: it's paid from actual rent, and rent keeps coming whether the share price is up or down. An income that doesn't quit eventually re-rates a too-cheap stock — buyers show up for an 8% yield. Second, **the buildings are worth what they're worth**: if the share price falls far below NAV, the REIT becomes a takeover target (a buyer can purchase the whole company on the cheap and own the buildings below their value), or management can buy back shares at the discount. Those forces close the gap. Short-term, the screen rules. Long-term, the bricks rule.

### The premium or discount to NAV — and why the gap is a signal

The rope between price and property value has a name and a number, and it's worth knowing because it's one of the few genuinely useful gauges of whether a REIT is cheap or dear. Recall the definition of **NAV (net asset value)** from the foundations: take what all the REIT's buildings would appraise for, subtract its debt, and divide by the number of shares. That gives you the **NAV per share** — the per-share break-up value, roughly what each share would be worth if you sold every building at appraisal and paid off the loans.

Now compare that to the **market price** the shares actually trade at. They are almost never equal. When the price is *above* NAV, the REIT trades at a **premium to NAV** — the market is paying more than the buildings are appraised at, usually because it expects the REIT to grow its rents and portfolio, or because it trusts the management to create value. When the price is *below* NAV, the REIT trades at a **discount to NAV** — the market is valuing the wrapper below the bricks inside it, usually out of pessimism about the sector, fear of rate hikes, or distrust of the managers.

This premium/discount is a real, tradable signal, with two important caveats. A persistent **deep discount** can mean the market is right — that the appraisals are stale and the buildings are actually worth less than the books say (this is exactly what happened to many office REITs after 2020, which traded at 40–50% discounts to a NAV that was itself melting as appraisers caught up to the empty-office reality). But a discount on a REIT whose buildings are genuinely fine is the rope stretched taut — the situation that pulls in takeover bidders and buybacks. A persistent **premium**, conversely, can mean the market rightly prizes a great operator, or it can mean the shares are simply expensive and due for a fall. The discipline is to ask *why* the gap exists, not to treat "trades at a discount" as an automatic buy.

#### Worked example: the discount to NAV

Dana is looking at an office REIT after the 2022–23 rate shock. Its books say the buildings appraise at **\$8 billion**; it owes **\$3 billion** of mortgage debt; it has **100 million** shares outstanding. Its NAV per share is therefore (\$8B − \$3B) ÷ 100M = \$5,000,000,000 ÷ 100,000,000 = **\$50 per share**.

But the shares trade at only **\$35**. That's a discount to NAV of (\$50 − \$35) ÷ \$50 = **30%**. The market is valuing the equity 30% below the appraised break-up value. Dana's job is to figure out *which side is wrong*. If the appraisals are stale — if the offices are really half-empty and the true value is \$6 billion, making real NAV (\$6B − \$3B) ÷ 100M = **\$30** — then the shares at \$35 are actually trading at a *premium* to reality, and the "discount" is a mirage. But if the buildings are genuinely worth \$8 billion and full, then \$35 is buildings on sale, and the rope will eventually pull the price up toward \$50. The discount itself doesn't answer the question; it just tells her where to point the flashlight.

*A discount to NAV is the market betting the buildings are worth less than the books say — sometimes it's right, sometimes it's a gift, and your whole job is to tell which.*

### Where a REIT's total return actually comes from

It helps to decompose what you're really earning, because a REIT's return arrives in two distinct streams, and confusing them leads to bad expectations. Your **total return** from a REIT is the **dividend yield plus the price change**. The dividend is the *property* stream — funded by rent, relatively stable, and thanks to the 90% rule it's large, often making up the majority of a REIT's long-run total return. The price change is the *market* stream — driven by rent growth, rate moves, and sentiment, and far more volatile.

Over long horizons, US listed REITs have delivered total returns broadly comparable to the stock market — roughly high-single-digit to low-double-digit annual returns — but with a *different shape*: a bigger slice coming from the steady dividend and a smaller slice from price appreciation, versus a stock-market index where most of the return comes from price gains. That's why REITs appeal to income investors and retirees: you can live off the dividend stream without selling shares. It also explains their place in a diversified portfolio, which we explore in [the cross-asset REIT post](/blog/trading/cross-asset/real-estate-reits-income-leverage-and-rates) — REITs add a property-linked, income-heavy return stream that behaves like neither pure stocks nor pure bonds, even though short-term it's correlated with both.

The practical upshot for setting expectations: **don't buy a REIT for a tech-stock price ride.** Buy it for the dividend, treat the price as the volatile part that will test your nerves, and judge success over years by the income you collected plus the modest re-rating, not by the screen on any given Tuesday.

#### Worked example: the 2022 rate-driven REIT selloff

In 2022, the US 30-year mortgage rate rocketed from a record-low **2.65%** (Jan-2021) to over **7%** (Oct-2022), and the 10-year Treasury yield went from under 1% to over 4%. REIT *prices* were crushed: the broad US listed-REIT index fell roughly **25%** over the year — a brutal, stock-like drawdown.

Now ask the key question: did the actual buildings lose a quarter of their value in 2022? No. Occupancy held, rents in most sectors *rose* with inflation, and private-market appraised values barely moved that year — the US house-price index was still near its all-time high. What collapsed was the *price the stock market was willing to pay for the listed wrapper*, driven almost entirely by the rate shock and the competition from suddenly-attractive bond yields. An investor who panicked and sold locked in a 25% loss on buildings that were fundamentally fine. An income investor who held kept collecting the same rent-funded dividend — now at a fatter yield — and watched the prices grind back over the following years as the rope re-tightened.

*In 2022 the REIT price said the buildings had crashed; the buildings said otherwise — and over time the buildings won the argument.*

## Vietnam's nascent REIT market versus the mature US market

Everything so far is universal mechanism. But *where* you are changes the picture enormously, because the REIT market in the US is one of the deepest in the world while Vietnam's barely exists.

![Comparison grid of the United States and Vietnam REIT markets across market size, number of funds, liquidity and what is missing, showing the US as deep and diversified with hundreds of liquid listed REITs and Vietnam as tiny with essentially one listed fund and thin trading](/imgs/blogs/reits-owning-property-like-a-stock-7.png)

### The US: deep, mature, diversified

The US created the modern REIT in 1960 and has had six decades to build the market out. Today there are **200+ listed REITs** worth well over **\$1 trillion** combined, covering every property type imaginable: apartments, malls, offices, warehouses (industrial/logistics REITs boomed with e-commerce), data centers (riding the cloud and AI build-out), cell towers, self-storage, hospitals, timberland, even billboards. There are REIT index funds, armies of analysts, decades of total-return history, and a deep, liquid market where you can buy or sell a diversified slice of US income property in seconds for a few dollars. For an American like Dana, the REIT-vs-direct choice is a real, well-furnished menu.

### Vietnam: nascent, thin, missing the plumbing

Vietnam is at the very beginning. The legal framework for real-estate investment funds has existed since the early 2010s, but the listed market is tiny: in practice there is essentially **one listed REIT** — the Techcom Vietnam REIT (**TCREIT**, ticker **FUCVREIT**) — plus a small number of property-focused funds, and total assets are a *rounding error* next to the US market: far under \$1 billion. Trading is thin, spreads are wide, and the choice of property types is minimal.

Why so undeveloped? A few reasons compound. Vietnamese households have a deep, cultural preference for owning **physical land and apartments directly** — land is seen as the ultimate store of value (a theme we explored in [the asset-class post](/blog/trading/real-estate/what-is-real-estate-as-an-asset-class)), and a paper share of a fund doesn't carry the same psychological weight or status. The regulatory and tax plumbing that makes REITs attractive elsewhere — the clean pass-through treatment, the mandatory high payout, the investor protections — is still maturing. And the developers who *could* seed big REITs have mostly preferred the presale-and-bond financing model we covered in [the developer-financing post](/blog/trading/real-estate/credit-and-developer-financing), which lets them keep control and recycle capital fast, rather than spinning stabilized buildings into a REIT. So the instrument that would let Minh own income property like a stock barely exists in his home market yet — which is exactly why his Singaporean cousin, in a mature REIT market, takes it for granted.

The likely path is the one Singapore and Japan walked: as Vietnam's capital markets deepen, its middle class grows, and the rules tighten up, a real listed-REIT market should emerge over the coming decade. For now, a Vietnamese investor who wants REIT-style exposure mostly has to reach for **regional REITs** (Singapore's market is a popular gateway to pan-Asian property) rather than a deep domestic menu.

## Common misconceptions

REITs attract a specific set of myths, and each one costs investors money. Let's name and correct the big five.

### Myth 1: "REITs are just property with no volatility"

This is exactly backwards in the short run, and it's the most expensive misconception of all. People hear "real estate" and picture the calm, never-marked value of their own home — and assume a REIT behaves the same way. It does *not*, day to day. A REIT trades on a stock exchange and swings like a stock; in a bad year it can fall 25–40% in price even when its buildings are fine. The *volatility you can see* is higher than direct property, not lower. What's actually true is subtler: the **underlying property value** is calm, but the **listed price** is not, and the two only reconverge over the long run. If you buy a REIT expecting the serene price path of your own flat, the first market scare will shake you out at the bottom.

### Myth 2: "The REIT dividend is guaranteed"

A REIT's fat, regular dividend is *required by the structure* — but "required to pay out 90% of income" is not the same as "the income, and therefore the dividend, can never fall." If the buildings lose tenants, if rents drop in a recession, if a key sector (malls, offices) structurally declines, then income falls and **the dividend gets cut**. Plenty of REITs slashed dividends in 2008 and in the 2020 pandemic when tenants stopped paying. The 90% rule fixes the *payout ratio*, not the *amount*. Always check the dividend against **AFFO**: a dividend comfortably covered by recurring cash is durable; one that exceeds AFFO is being paid partly out of borrowed money or asset sales, and is a cut waiting to happen.

### Myth 3: "REITs aren't real real estate"

Some direct-property investors sneer that a REIT is "just a stock," not real real estate. But a REIT owns the same bricks, signs the same leases, collects the same rent, and rises and falls with the same property cycle as a building you'd buy yourself — over the long run its total return tracks the property market closely. What a REIT *adds* is liquidity, divisibility, professional management, and diversification across many buildings; what it *removes* is your personal control and adds a daily price. It is genuinely real estate — just wrapped so it can trade. Calling it "not real" is like calling a deposit "not real money" because it's a number in an app rather than cash in a drawer.

### Myth 4: "A high dividend yield means it's a great deal"

A REIT yielding 12% looks like a gift next to one yielding 4%. But yield is dividend ÷ price, and a sky-high yield usually means the *price has collapsed* because the market expects the dividend to be cut — a value trap, not a bargain. The 12% is the market screaming that it doesn't believe the dividend will survive. A sustainable, growing 5% from a well-run REIT with AFFO coverage beats an unsustainable 12% that gets halved next quarter (turning your "12% yield" into a 6% yield *and* a 50% capital loss). Yield is a starting question, never an answer.

### Myth 5: "REITs and direct property are interchangeable, so just pick the cheaper fees"

They give the same *exposure* but a different *experience and toolkit*. Direct ownership lets you apply your own large, cheap, long-term **leverage** — a structural advantage a REIT can't replicate for you, since the REIT sets its own debt and you can't put a 20-year mortgage on a share. Direct ownership also has no daily price to rattle your nerves. A REIT, conversely, gives instant liquidity and lets you own forty buildings instead of one. Choosing between them isn't about fees; it's about which *frictions and powers* fit your life — liquidity and passivity, or leverage and control.

## How it shows up in real markets

Let's ground all of this in concrete, real episodes — the kind of cases that show the mechanism actually biting.

### US REIT sectors: the same wrapper, wildly different fortunes

The US market makes vivid that "REIT" is a *wrapper*, and what's inside matters enormously. Over the 2010s and 2020s, **industrial/logistics REITs** (the warehouses behind e-commerce) and **data-center REITs** (the buildings behind the cloud and the AI boom) were among the best-performing investments of any kind, riding powerful secular demand. At the very same time, **mall REITs** and many **office REITs** struggled or collapsed — malls hollowed out by online shopping, offices gutted by the post-2020 shift to remote work, with some big-city office values falling 40% or more as leases expired into a half-empty market. Two investors who both "bought REITs" in 2015 could have ended the decade with a triple or a near-wipeout depending purely on *which buildings* their REIT held. The lesson: a REIT is property, and *the property still has to be good property*. The wrapper doesn't save bad buildings.

This sector spread is also why "buy a REIT index fund" is a genuinely different proposition from "buy one REIT." An index fund spreads you across all the sectors — warehouses, apartments, towers, malls, offices, storage, data centers — so the winners and losers partly cancel and you earn something close to the property market's overall return with the listed market's volatility. Picking a single REIT is an active bet on a sector *and* a management team *and* a capital structure, any of which can go wrong independently. The same secular forces that crushed malls (e-commerce) are the ones that lifted warehouses (e-commerce needs somewhere to ship from), so a thoughtful REIT investor reads the *demand story for the buildings* first and the financial wrapper second. This is exactly the kind of sector-level reasoning we develop in [the commercial-real-estate post](/blog/trading/real-estate/commercial-real-estate-office-and-retail): a REIT is only ever as good as the leases and locations underneath it, and a beautiful balance sheet can't rescue a building nobody wants to rent.

### The 2022 rate-driven REIT selloff

We worked the numbers above, so here's the takeaway as a market case: 2022 was a clean natural experiment separating **price** from **property**. Rates spiked, listed REIT prices fell ~25%, and yet appraised building values and rents barely moved — many rents *rose* with inflation. The listed and private markets, which own the *same kind of buildings*, diverged sharply, with the listed REIT market acting as a fast, jumpy, forward-looking proxy that overshot. By 2023–24, as rate expectations stabilized, much of the price gap closed. For an investor, 2022 was a stress test of whether you actually understood what you owned: those who knew REITs track buildings long-term held and recovered; those who thought they'd bought a calm bond panicked at the bottom.

### Vietnam's tiny market and the 2022 property freeze

Vietnam's REIT market is so small that it played almost no role in the country's big 2022 property event — and that absence is itself the lesson. When Vietnam's **corporate-bond market froze** in late 2022 (after the Tân Hoàng Minh and Vạn Thịnh Phát/SCB arrests), the developers who had financed themselves with short-term bonds were suddenly unable to refinance, and the property sector seized up — a crisis we trace in [the Vietnam property-cycles post](/blog/trading/real-estate/vietnam-property-cycles-2007-2013-2022). In a country with a deep REIT market, a lot of stabilized income property would have sat *inside* transparent, listed, professionally-managed trusts with permanent equity, rather than inside developers leaning on fragile bond financing. The freeze was, in part, a story about *who owned the buildings and how they were funded*. Vietnam's nascent REIT market is both a symptom of an immature capital market and, potentially, part of the cure — a way to move income property out of fragile developer balance sheets and into stable, liquid, widely-owned trusts. For now, a Vietnamese investor wanting that exposure looks abroad.

#### Worked example: the 90% distribution math and the tax break

Let's make the pass-through tax break, the engine of the whole structure, completely concrete with round numbers.

![Pipeline showing taxable income of one hundred that must be distributed at ninety percent, letting the trust deduct the payout so it owes almost no corporate tax, with the investor receiving ninety as a dividend and paying tax once on that dividend at their own rate](/imgs/blogs/reits-owning-property-like-a-stock-6.png)

A REIT earns **\$100** of taxable income from rent (after operating costs and interest). The diagram traces what happens next.

**Step 1 — the 90% rule.** To keep its REIT status, the trust must distribute at least 90% of that taxable income. Say it pays out the full **\$90** (most REITs pay essentially all of it) as a dividend to shareholders.

**Step 2 — the deduction.** Here's the magic: a REIT gets to *deduct* the dividends it pays from its taxable income. It earned \$100 and paid out \$90, so its taxable income at the trust level is only \$100 − \$90 = **\$10**. If it had paid out the full \$100, its taxable income would be **\$0**.

**Step 3 — almost no corporate tax.** Apply a corporate tax rate of, say, 21% to that tiny \$10 residual, and the trust owes about **\$2.10** — essentially nothing. A normal company earning \$100 would have paid 21% = **\$21** of corporate tax *first*, leaving only \$79 to distribute, and the shareholder would *then* be taxed on that \$79 again.

**Step 4 — the investor pays, once.** The shareholder receives the \$90 dividend and pays tax on it at their own income-tax rate. Say that's 25%: the investor owes \$22.50, keeping **\$67.50**.

Compare the two worlds. Through a normal company, \$100 of profit becomes \$21 corporate tax + (25% on the remaining \$79 ≈ \$19.75) investor tax = **~\$40.75 of total tax**, leaving the investor about \$59. Through a REIT, the \$100 is taxed essentially **once** at the investor level — about \$22.50 of investor tax (plus the trivial \$2.10 at the trust) — leaving the investor about \$75. The pass-through structure saves roughly the entire first layer of corporate tax, which is why REITs can pay such generous dividends: more of the rent survives the trip from the building to your pocket.

*The 90% rule isn't a burden — it's the toll a REIT pays to skip corporate tax, so the rent reaches you taxed once instead of twice.*

## When this matters / Further reading

A REIT is the answer to a very old problem: income real estate is the largest asset class on Earth — about **\$393 trillion** of property globally, dwarfing all stocks and bonds — and yet for most of history it was almost impossible for an ordinary person to own a professionally-managed, diversified slice of it. The ticket was too big, the asset too illiquid, the management too demanding, and you couldn't buy a fraction of a tower. The REIT cracks all four at once by wrapping a portfolio of buildings in a liquid, divisible share and forcing it — via the 90% rule — to pour most of its rent straight back to you as a dividend, lightly taxed.

What you give up is just as concrete as what you gain. You hand the leverage decision to the REIT's managers, and you accept a daily price that swings like a stock even when the buildings are calm. The deepest truth to carry away is the one the 2022 selloff taught: **a REIT trades like a stock in the short run but tracks property in the long run** — the screen price and the brick value are two different numbers tied by a long, elastic rope. If you understand that, the volatility stops being terrifying and starts being an opportunity, because a too-cheap REIT is buildings on sale.

This matters most when you're deciding *how* to own property rather than *whether* to. If you value liquidity, want to start small, want diversification across many buildings, and want a passive, professionally-managed income — a REIT is built for you, provided you can stomach the price swings and check the dividend against AFFO. If you want maximum personal leverage on a single asset you control, and you'd rather never see a daily price, direct ownership still wins. For a Vietnamese investor, the wrinkle is that the domestic REIT market barely exists yet, so the practical choice today is direct property at home or REITs abroad — a gap that should narrow as Vietnam's capital markets mature.
