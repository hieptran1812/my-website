> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — "Residential property" sounds like one thing, but in Vietnam it is three very different bets, and the difference is almost entirely *how much land versus structure you are buying*.
>
> - An **apartment** (chung cư) is mostly a *depreciating building* sitting on a tiny, shared, finite-life slice of land. It is the liquid urban default — but the structure wears out, and the land-use right is split across hundreds of units.
> - A **landed house** (nhà phố / biệt thự) is *mostly land* with a building on top. It is prestige, durable value, and a long hold — but it is expensive and slower to sell.
> - **Đất nền** (raw land plots) is *almost pure land*, with no building and no income. It is the highest-octane, most speculative bet and the epicentre of Vietnam's boom-bust fevers — wildly illiquid when the music stops.
> - The one fact to remember: **land share runs ~100% for đất nền, ~70% for a landed house, ~50% for a central apartment, ~30% for a suburban flat** — and that single number drives the income, the liquidity, and the risk of each.

Minh has saved ₫3.5 billion (≈ \$135,000) and he wants to put it into property in Ho Chi Minh City. His parents tell him to buy land — "đất is the only thing they aren't making more of." His colleague just bought a two-bedroom apartment in a glossy new tower in Thủ Đức and swears it is the only sensible choice for a young professional. His uncle, who is wealthier, owns a narrow four-storey nhà phố on a side street in District 3 and treats it like a family heirloom. Three relatives, three completely different ideas of what "buying property" even means — and all three are technically right, because they are describing three different *bets*.

This is the thing almost nobody tells a first-time buyer: residential property is not one asset. It is at least three, and they behave so differently that lumping them together is like saying "I invest in securities" without distinguishing a government bond from a penny stock. The whole post is built on one diagram — the mental model below — that lays the three side by side along the dimensions that actually matter: how much land versus structure you are buying, whether it produces income, how fast it sells, how risky it is, and who typically buys it.

![Matrix comparing apartment, landed house, and dat nen across land-structure mix, income, liquidity, risk, and typical buyer](/imgs/blogs/residential-property-apartments-houses-and-dat-nen-1.png)

The diagram above is the mental model for the entire post. Read across each row and you get the personality of one bet. The apartment row is a mix of green and amber: liquid-ish and income-producing, but the building wears out. The landed house row trades some income and liquidity for durable, prestigious land. The đất nền row is a wall of red on every dimension except the one that lures people in — the chance of the highest return in a boom. By the end of this piece you will be able to look at any residential listing and immediately place it on this grid, which is most of what separates a thoughtful buyer from someone chasing a story.

Before we go deep, one honest caveat: this is educational, not financial advice. I am going to explain *mechanisms* — what each bet is made of and how it behaves — so you can reason for yourself. I am not going to tell Minh which one to buy. By the end you will see why that question has no universal answer, and why "it depends on your goal and the cycle phase" is the genuinely correct response rather than a dodge.

## Foundations: the residential segment and its three forms

Let's build everything from zero. **Real estate** is land plus anything permanently attached to it — buildings, infrastructure, fixtures. The **residential** segment is the slice meant for people to live in, as opposed to *commercial* (offices, malls, hotels), *industrial* (factories, warehouses), or *agricultural* (farmland). Residential is by far the biggest segment of the biggest asset class on Earth: of roughly \$393 trillion of global real estate at end-2024, about \$301 trillion — over three-quarters — is residential. It is also the most personal: for most households it is the single largest financial decision they will ever make, far bigger than any stock or bond they own.

Within residential, the three forms in this post are:

- **Apartment / condominium (chung cư).** A self-contained dwelling that is *one unit inside a larger building*. You own your unit and a *share* of the common areas — the lobby, the lifts, the structure, and crucially the land underneath. In Vietnamese law you hold a "pink book" (sổ hồng) for the apartment, but the land-use right beneath the tower is *shared across every unit* and is typically tied to a finite term. Think of it as: you own the air your flat occupies and a sliver of everything below it.
- **Landed house (nhà phố, biệt thự).** A standalone building — a townhouse, a villa, a shophouse — that sits on *its own plot of land*, which is yours alone. Nhà phố is the narrow, deep "tube house" that defines Vietnamese streets; biệt thự is a detached villa. You hold a land-use right (and an ownership right to the building on it) for *the whole plot*. The land is not shared with anyone.
- **Đất nền (raw land plot).** A serviced or semi-serviced *plot of land with no building on it* — typically in a new subdivision, often on a city's fringe or in a satellite province. You are buying the land-use right and nothing else. There is no flat to rent, no house to live in. It is the purest form of "buying land."

Here are the terms you need, defined once and reused throughout:

- **Land-use right (LUR).** Vietnam does not have private freehold land — the state owns all land, and what you actually buy is the *right to use* a plot, recorded in the land registry. For residential land allocated to individuals this right is generally **long-term and renewable** (stable, treated almost like ownership). For apartment buildings, the project's land is typically a *fixed-term lease* to the developer (often 50 years), which is the source of the "finite life" question we explore later. The practical point: when you "own land" in Vietnam you own a *right*, and the terms of that right matter enormously.
- **The land-vs-structure split.** Every property is two assets glued together: the **land** (scarce, immovable, tends to *appreciate* because good locations are finite) and the **structure** (reproducible, wears out, *depreciates* like a car). We covered this in depth in [Land vs Buildings](/blog/trading/real-estate/land-vs-buildings-two-assets-in-every-property); here it is the single dial that distinguishes our three bets. More land share = more durable, appreciating value. More structure share = more of your money is in something that decays.
- **Useful life.** A building does not last forever. Concrete, steel, wiring, plumbing, and lifts age; a typical residential structure has an *economic* useful life of roughly 50–70 years before it is worth more to demolish than to repair. The land underneath does not age — but a *building* runs down a clock.
- **Liquidity.** How quickly and cheaply you can turn the asset into cash *near its fair value*. A liquid asset sells in days at a price close to the last trade; an illiquid one can take months or years and force a big discount. Liquidity is not a footnote in property — for our three bets it is one of the main things that separates them.
- **Income.** The cash an asset throws off while you hold it. For an apartment or house, that is *rent*. For đất nền, it is *zero* — bare land produces nothing, and you may even pay a small holding cost. An asset with income pays you to wait; an asset without income makes waiting expensive.
- **The speculative premium.** The part of a price that is paid *not* for the asset's current use or income, but purely on the expectation that someone will pay more later. Đất nền trades almost entirely on this premium, which is exactly why it can double in a fever and freeze in a bust.
- **Owner-occupier vs investor demand.** An *owner-occupier* buys a home to live in; they value shelter, schools, commute, and a place to raise a family. An *investor* buys to earn a return — rent, appreciation, or both. The two have very different price sensitivities, and which one dominates a segment tells you a lot about how it behaves. Apartments are bought by both; landed houses skew toward wealthier long-hold owners; đất nền is overwhelmingly an investor/speculator market.

With the vocabulary in place, the rest of the post is just walking the three bets one at a time, then comparing them on risk, return, and liquidity.

#### Worked example: splitting an apartment and a landed house into land + structure

Let's make the central idea concrete with Minh's choices. Suppose Minh is comparing two HCMC options at the same ₫3.5 billion (≈ \$135,000) price tag (we will assume he can stretch or borrow the gap where needed; the *mix* is the point).

A **suburban apartment** in Thủ Đức: 60 m² at the 2025 primary price of roughly ₫58 million/m² for that fringe submarket. The building is new, the land beneath is shared across ~400 units. Of his ₫3.5 billion, maybe **30% is the land-use share (₫1.05 billion)** and **70% is the structure and finish (₫2.45 billion)**. He is mostly buying a building.

A **landed nhà phố** further out, an older tube house on a small plot: of the same ₫3.5 billion, the dirt is the prize, so maybe **70% is land (₫2.45 billion)** and **30% is the ageing structure (₫1.05 billion)**.

Same price, *inverted* composition. In the apartment, ₫2.45 billion of his money sits in something that will wear out over decades. In the house, ₫2.45 billion sits in land that does not age.

*The price tags can match exactly while the assets underneath are near-opposites — and the composition, not the headline number, is what determines how each will behave for the next thirty years.*

## Apartments: the depreciating building on a shared, finite land right

Start with the form most young urban buyers actually purchase. An **apartment** is the urban default for a reason: it is (relatively) affordable per unit, it is in the city where the jobs are, it is the most liquid residential type, and a brand-new tower with a gym and a pool is genuinely nice to live in. In HCMC, the average *primary* (developer-sold, brand-new) apartment ran about **₫111 million/m² in 2025** (≈ \$4,222/m² at ₫25,900 to the dollar), up roughly 33% from ₫91 million in 2024. Hanoi was close behind at about **₫100 million/m²** in 2025, having jumped ~40% in a year. These are not small numbers — a modest 70 m² Hanoi flat is around ₫7.0 billion (≈ \$270,000).

But here is the part the glossy brochure does not emphasise: **most of what you buy in an apartment is a building, and buildings wear out.** The figure below puts the apartment and the landed house side by side so the composition is unmissable.

![Before and after comparison showing an apartment is mostly depreciating structure while a landed house is mostly durable land](/imgs/blogs/residential-property-apartments-houses-and-dat-nen-2.png)

On the left, the apartment: roughly **60% of the price is structure**, a building that wears out over something like 50 years, and the land underneath is a *shared* land-use right split across all the units in the tower. On the right, the landed house: roughly **70% of the price is land**, the land does not wear out, and the whole plot's land-use right is yours alone. Same diagram, opposite stories.

Two features of the apartment deserve unpacking, because they are widely misunderstood.

### The shared, finite land-use right

When you buy a flat, you do *not* get a private plot. The land under the tower belongs to a single project, and your share of it is your unit's slice of the building's total floor area. If there are 400 units, you own roughly 1/400th of the land right (weighted by unit size). That has two consequences. First, your exposure to *land appreciation* — the durable, can't-make-more-of-it asset — is diluted. Second, the project's land is typically a **fixed-term right**, commonly **50 years** for the development. As that term runs down, a real question appears: what happens at the end?

Vietnamese law has wrestled with this. The intent under the framework is that residential apartment ownership is meant to be durable and that, at the end of a building's safe useful life, the state and owners arrange for reconstruction or compensation rather than a simple expiry to zero — but the *mechanics* (who pays to rebuild, how owners are compensated, what the land right looks like afterward) are exactly the uncertainty. The honest summary: an apartment's land right is **shared, term-bound, and legally less clean than a landed plot's**, and that is a genuine difference, not a technicality.

### The building runs down a clock

The structure is the bigger, more certain issue. A new building is worth a lot; a 40-year-old building with tired plumbing, an ageing lift, and a dated façade is worth much less *as a structure*. The timeline figure below traces this.

![Timeline of an apartment building's value over its finite useful life from new build to land-use term decision](/imgs/blogs/residential-property-apartments-houses-and-dat-nen-3.png)

At **Year 0**, the structure is ~60% of the price — shiny and new. By **Year 30**, mid-life, the structure has worn and the land share has quietly risen as a fraction of what's left. By **Year 50**, useful life ends: the structure value is near zero, and around the same horizon the **land-use term comes due** — renew or revert? The outcome is a rebuild or compensation question. The whole asset is, in part, a *wasting* asset: the building portion is being consumed by time even as you live in it.

This is the deep reason the cliché "apartments always appreciate" is dangerous. The *land share* of an apartment can appreciate, and in a hot market like HCMC in 2024–25 the whole package rose sharply. But a meaningful chunk of the asset — the structure — is decaying every year. In a flat market, the structure's depreciation can quietly eat the land's appreciation, and an old building in a so-so location can absolutely be worth less in real terms decades on. Apartments are liquid, convenient, and often the right choice — but "guaranteed to go up" they are not.

#### Worked example: an apartment's structure value at year 40 vs the land right

Take a concrete flat. Minh buys a central HCMC apartment today for **₫6 billion (≈ \$232,000)**. Using the central-apartment split, call it **50% land share (₫3 billion)** and **50% structure (₫3 billion)**.

Now run the clock forward 40 years, holding today's prices flat just to isolate the *depreciation* effect (no market boom, no inflation — we're stripping it to the mechanism). The building has a ~50-year useful life, so at year 40 it is 80% of the way through its life. A reasonable straight-line write-down leaves the structure worth roughly **20% of its original ₫3 billion = ₫600 million**. The land *right*, meanwhile, has not worn out — call it still worth its ₫3 billion in real terms (in reality good HCMC land would have risen, but we are holding flat to be conservative).

So the asset that started at ₫6 billion is now worth about **₫3 billion land + ₫0.6 billion structure = ₫3.6 billion** — a **40% real decline** purely from the building ageing, before any market move. The land right held the value; the structure bled it away.

*An apartment is a land bet wrapped around a slowly dying building — and the longer you hold an old one, the more the land share has to carry the whole price.*

### The hidden costs and the presale gamble

Two more features of apartment ownership rarely make it into the buyer's mental model, and both cut against the "set and forget" story.

The first is **ongoing cost**. An apartment comes with a *management fee* — a monthly service charge (phí quản lý) for the lift, security, cleaning, lobby, and shared maintenance, typically a few thousand đồng per square metre per month. A well-run building also keeps a *maintenance reserve fund* (quỹ bảo trì), collected up front (commonly 2% of the purchase price in Vietnam) and topped up over time, to pay for big repairs — a new lift, a repainted façade, a re-waterproofed roof. These costs are real and they recur for as long as you own. A landed house has no service charge and no shared fund; you maintain your own building on your own schedule. So the apartment's headline rent yield is a *gross* number — net of management fees and your share of major repairs, the yield the owner actually pockets is lower, and a poorly governed building with a depleted reserve fund can hit owners with sudden, large special assessments when the lift finally dies.

The second is the **presale gamble**. Most new Vietnamese apartments are sold *off-plan* — you buy from the developer before the building exists, paying in instalments tied to construction milestones, and you take delivery (handover) one to three years later. This is cheaper than buying a finished unit and it is how developers fund construction (we cover the mechanics in [credit and developer financing](/blog/trading/real-estate/credit-and-developer-financing)). But it adds a layer of risk a finished apartment doesn't have: the developer can be late, can deliver something different from the brochure, can run into the kind of bond-market freeze that hit Vietnam in 2022 and *stall the project mid-build*, or can fail to deliver the pink-book title for years. Off-plan buyers in stalled 2022–23 projects learned this the hard way — they had paid most of the price for an apartment that was a hole in the ground with no clear delivery date. The presale discount is the *compensation* for taking developer and completion risk; it is not free money.

#### Worked example: gross vs net rent yield on an apartment

Minh's central HCMC apartment cost **₫6 billion** and rents for **₫25 million/month = ₫300 million/year**, a *gross* yield of **5.0%** (₫300m ÷ ₫6bn). Now net it down to what he actually keeps:

- **Management fee** he can't pass fully to the tenant, plus his amortised share of the maintenance fund: call it ₫1.5 million/month = **₫18 million/year**.
- **Vacancy** — the flat is empty ~1 month between tenants on average: **−₫25 million/year**.
- **Repairs and furnishing wear** inside the unit: **−₫20 million/year**.
- **Income tax and management hassle** on the rent (a presumptive ~5% on gross rental in Vietnam for individual landlords above the threshold): **−₫15 million/year**.

Net rent ≈ ₫300m − 18 − 25 − 20 − 15 = **₫222 million/year**, a *net* yield of about **3.7%** — a full 1.3 percentage points below the headline 5.0%. And that is before the structure's slow depreciation we computed earlier.

*The yield a brochure quotes is almost always gross; the number that pays your bills is net, and on an apartment the gap between them is the management fee, the vacancy, the tax, and the slow cost of a building that needs upkeep.*

## Landed houses: the land-heavy prestige bet

Now flip the composition. A **landed house** — a nhà phố tube house, a biệt thự villa, a shophouse — is the land-heavy form. The building matters (and a nice one is worth real money), but the *prize is the plot*. In central HCMC, the land can be 70–80% or more of the value; the house is almost incidental. This is why a tiny, crumbling old house on a good District 1 street can sell for a fortune: nobody is paying for the house, they are paying for the dirt, and the house is a teardown waiting to happen.

What you get for the higher price:

- **Durable, undiluted land value.** The whole plot's land-use right is yours alone, generally long-term and renewable. You are not sharing it with 399 strangers, and it is not running down a 50-year clock the way a tower's project land is. Land does not wear out; you own a lot of it.
- **Prestige and control.** A landed house is a status asset in Vietnamese culture — a family home, a multi-generational hold, often passed down rather than traded. You can rebuild, extend, run a shop on the ground floor, or rent rooms. You control the whole structure.
- **Lower *relative* exposure to depreciation.** Because the structure is a smaller share of the price, the wasting-asset problem is milder. When the building tires, you knock it down and rebuild on land you already own — the expensive part stays.

What you give up:

- **Price.** Landed houses are big-ticket. A central tube house easily runs into the tens of billions of đồng; few young buyers can touch one. This is a wealthy-family asset.
- **Liquidity.** Because the tickets are large and the buyer pool is small, landed houses sell *slower* — months, not weeks — and the price is more negotiated. You cannot quietly list it like a standard 2-bed flat and expect three offers in a fortnight.
- **Income.** You *can* rent a house, but the rent yield (annual rent ÷ price) is usually *low* — often lower than a comparable apartment — precisely because so much of the price is land, and land doesn't generate rent, the building does. You are paying up for durable land, not for cash flow.

Landed houses sit in the middle of every spectrum: more land and more durability than an apartment, but less liquidity and less income; far more income and liquidity than đất nền, but also far less explosive upside. They are the patient, prestige, long-hold bet — the asset Minh's uncle treats as a family heirloom rather than a trade.

One subtle advantage of the landed house deserves a name: **optionality**. Because you own the whole plot and a long-term land-use right, you control what sits on it. You can knock down a tired structure and rebuild a bigger one (subject to zoning); convert the ground floor to a shop and rent it; add floors; or, if the area gentrifies, hold the dirt and let "highest and best use" rise around you. An apartment owner has almost none of this — you cannot change your unit's footprint, add a floor, or alter the building. The land-heavy bet quietly buys you *flexibility*, and flexibility has value precisely in a long hold, when neighbourhoods and rules change. That is part of what the wealthy family is paying for: not just durable land, but the right to decide what to do with it for decades.

The shophouse (nhà phố thương mại) is the purest expression of this — a landed unit designed so the ground floor is a storefront and the upper floors are home or rental. It blends a landed plot's durable land with an apartment's income, which is why it commands a premium in busy commercial streets. It is the one residential form that meaningfully bridges the income gap, at the cost of a high ticket and the same medium liquidity as any landed asset.

#### Worked example: the same budget, three very different positions

Give Minh a fixed **₫4 billion (≈ \$154,000)** budget and watch what each bet buys.

- **Apartment:** ₫4 billion buys a solid ~50–60 m² flat in a decent HCMC district at ~₫70–80 million/m². He gets a *liquid* asset he can live in or rent for, say, ₫15 million/month — a gross rent yield of about **4.5%/year** (₫180 million ÷ ₫4 billion). Income now, sells in weeks later.
- **Landed house:** ₫4 billion does *not* buy a central tube house — it buys a small, older landed plot well out on the fringe, or a share toward one. He gets durable, undiluted land, but a *low* rent yield (~2–3%) and a slow sale if he ever needs out. Wealth storage, not cash flow.
- **Đất nền:** ₫4 billion buys a generous raw plot in a satellite province. **Zero income**, the highest potential appreciation in a boom — and, as we'll see, the worst freeze risk in a bust.

Same ₫4 billion, three positions that could not be more different in income, liquidity, and risk. That is the whole thesis in one budget line.

*Choosing a residential bet is really choosing where on the land-structure dial — and the liquidity ladder — you want to stand; the price tag barely tells you that.*

## Đất nền: the pure, speculative land bet

Now the high-octane one. **Đất nền** — literally "foundation land," a raw plot in a subdivision — is residential property stripped down to *just the land*. No building, no flat, no rent, no shelter. You are buying the land-use right and a story about what it will be worth later. It is the purest expression of "they aren't making more land," and it is the epicentre of Vietnam's most violent property fevers.

Why is đất nền so seductive? Because it is **almost all land** — the most durable, appreciating component — with none of the depreciating structure diluting it. In a rising market, a plot has nothing wearing out to drag it down; it is pure exposure to the land price. And the entry ticket on the fringe is *lower* than a city apartment, so ordinary people can play. When a new road, bridge, industrial park, or — the classic — a *rumoured airport* is announced near a subdivision, prices can move with terrifying speed.

But strip out the building and you also strip out everything that makes property *grounded*:

- **Zero income.** A flat pays rent; a plot pays nothing. While you hold đất nền you earn no cash flow at all, and you may carry small costs. Every month you wait is a month of pure opportunity cost.
- **No use value.** Nobody can live in a bare plot. There is no owner-occupier demand to put a floor under the price — it is **investors and speculators all the way down**. Owner-occupiers buy homes for reasons that don't vanish in a downturn (they still need somewhere to live); a market made entirely of speculators has no such anchor.
- **Wild illiquidity.** When the story stops, the buyers vanish. There is no rent to make holding tolerable and no resident who *needs* the asset, so the bid simply disappears. Đất nền can **freeze for years**.

The before-after figure captures the full arc of a fever.

![Before and after comparison of dat nen during the 2018 fever of rising prices and the 2022 freeze of no buyers](/imgs/blogs/residential-property-apartments-houses-and-dat-nen-4.png)

On the left, the **2018 fever**: a rumoured road or airport nearby, prices doubling on FOMO buying, and flippers assuming the next buyer will pay more — the textbook extrapolation that drives every speculative mania. On the right, the **2022 freeze**: credit tightens and the corporate-bond market seizes, buyers evaporate so the bid-ask gap blows out, and the plot sits frozen earning *zero rent* while the owner waits. The mechanism that made it soar — that every buyer was betting on the *next* buyer — is exactly the mechanism that makes it crash, because when the next buyer doesn't show up, there is nothing underneath.

### Why đất nền fevers form — and why the title can be a trap

It is worth understanding the *anatomy* of a đất nền fever, because the same script runs every time. It starts with a **catalyst** — a real or rumoured piece of infrastructure (a ring road, a bridge, a metro line, an industrial park, the perennial "new airport"). Early buyers, sometimes with genuine information, buy cheaply. Prices tick up. That tick attracts *momentum* buyers who see the rise and extrapolate it. As prices accelerate, the story spreads — brokers run buses of buyers out to the fringe, social media fills with screenshots of gains — and **FOMO** (fear of missing out) pulls in the late crowd, who are buying *purely because prices are rising*, not because they have any view on the underlying land. At the top, the marginal buyer is a leveraged flipper who plans to sell to the next leveraged flipper in weeks. There is no anchor — no rent, no resident, no use — only the chain of expectations. When credit tightens or the catalyst fails to materialise, the chain breaks, the late buyers are trapped, and because nobody actually *needs* the land, the bid simply vanishes.

There is also a quieter, nastier risk specific to raw land: **title and legality**. A finished apartment in a reputable tower has a clear, recorded pink book. A fringe đất nền plot in a hastily carved subdivision may have *murky title* — land that was not properly converted from agricultural to residential use, plots sold on a handshake or a "principle agreement" rather than a registered land-use-right certificate, or subdivisions that never received planning approval. In Vietnam's fevers, "phân lô bán nền" (carving farmland into plots and selling them) has repeatedly run ahead of the legal process, leaving buyers holding plots they cannot legally build on or cleanly resell. The land you thought you bought may not be the land the registry recognises — a risk that is essentially zero for a titled apartment and very real for cheap fringe land bought in a hurry.

This is why land share matters so much, and the data figure makes the gradient explicit.

![Stacked bar chart of land share by residential type with dat nen near 100 percent down to suburban apartment near 30 percent](/imgs/blogs/residential-property-apartments-houses-and-dat-nen-5.png)

Reading the bars: **đất nền is ~100% land** (no structure at all), a **landed house ~70% land**, a **central apartment ~50%**, and a **suburban apartment ~30%**. As you move left to right, more of your money sits in the depreciating structure and less in durable land. Đất nền is the far-left extreme: maximum land share, maximum exposure to the land price's swings, and — because there is literally nothing else in the asset — maximum dependence on the speculative story. More land means more *durable* value, but it also means more of the *volatile*, income-free, story-driven part of property. Đất nền has all of the land's upside and none of property's stabilisers.

#### Worked example: a đất nền plot bought in the 2018 fever, frozen by 2022

Walk through the carry. In 2018, at the height of the satellite-province fever, an investor — call her Lan — buys a fringe đất nền plot for **₫2 billion (≈ \$77,000)** on a rumour that a new expressway and an "airport" will lift the area. By late 2018, comparable plots are quoted at **₫3 billion** — a 50% paper gain. Lan feels brilliant.

Then 2022 arrives. The Tân Hoàng Minh and Vạn Thịnh Phát/SCB shocks freeze the corporate-bond market, credit tightens hard, and speculative land is the first thing to seize. The quoted price doesn't gently drift down — *there are simply no buyers*. To actually sell, Lan would have to accept maybe **₫1.4 billion**, if she could find anyone. So:

- **Mark-to-market loss:** from the ₫3 billion paper peak to a ₫1.4 billion realistic bid, that is a **~₫1.6 billion (53%) drawdown** from the high — and a **₫600 million (30%) loss on her actual ₫2 billion cost** if she sells.
- **Carried cost:** across roughly four years she earned **₫0 in rent** — a plot produces nothing. Had she put ₫2 billion in a 6% deposit instead, she'd have earned ~₫530 million in interest. So the *true* cost includes ~₫530 million of forgone income on top of the price loss.
- **The freeze:** the cruelest part is she may not be *able* to sell at ₫1.4 billion either. The market is frozen; the bid-ask gap is enormous; the plot just sits.

*A đất nền plot in a bust gives you the full menu of pain at once — a brutal price loss, zero income to cushion the wait, and the very real chance you simply cannot get out at any sensible price.*

## Risk, return, and liquidity: the three bets compared

Now put the three side by side on the dimensions an investor actually cares about. The matrix below is the payoff of the whole post.

![Matrix of risk and return profile across apartment, landed house, and dat nen showing expected return, volatility, income, and illiquidity](/imgs/blogs/residential-property-apartments-houses-and-dat-nen-7.png)

Read it row by row:

- **Apartment:** *moderate, steady* expected return; *lower* volatility; **rent income ~3–5%/year**; **low illiquidity** (sells in weeks). The all-rounder. It pays you to wait, it doesn't swing wildly, and you can get out fast.
- **Landed house:** *moderate-to-high, land-led* return; *medium* volatility; *low* rent yield; *medium* illiquidity (big ticket, smaller buyer pool). More durable upside than a flat, but you sacrifice income and quick exit.
- **Đất nền:** *highest return in a boom*; *extreme, boom-bust* volatility; **no income, and it carries cost**; **high illiquidity** (can freeze for years). The lottery ticket — biggest payoff if the story works, total loss of liquidity if it doesn't.

The crucial pattern is that **risk and return climb together, and they drag illiquidity and lost income along with them.** This is not a coincidence — it is the iron law of investing showing up in property. There is no version of đất nền that gives you the apartment's liquidity and income *plus* the highest boom return. The market prices the higher expected return precisely *because* you are accepting more volatility, zero cash flow, and the risk of being frozen. Anyone who tells you a bet has the upside of one column and the safety of another is selling you something.

A compact way to see the same tradeoff:

| | Apartment (chung cư) | Landed house (nhà phố) | Đất nền (raw land) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Land share | ~30–50% | ~70% | ~100% |
| What you mostly buy | a depreciating building | durable land + a house | pure land + a story |
| Income (rent yield) | ~3–5%/year | low (~2–3%) | zero (carries cost) |
| Liquidity | high — sells in weeks | medium — sells in months | low — can freeze for years |
| Volatility | lower | medium | extreme (boom-bust) |
| Land-use right | shared, finite-term | whole plot, long-term | whole plot, long-term |
| Wasting-asset problem | significant (building) | mild (small structure) | none (no building) |
| Typical buyer | owner-occupier, first home | wealthy family, long hold | speculator, flipper |
| Best for | live in it / steady income | long-hold wealth | pure appreciation bet |

#### Worked example: risk and return of the three for the same ₫ budget

Give each bet the same **₫3 billion (≈ \$116,000)** and sketch a plausible five-year outcome distribution — illustrative, to show the *shape* of each bet, not a forecast.

- **Apartment:** Over five years, maybe ₫3 billion grows to ₫3.6–4.2 billion in a normal-to-good market (the structure depreciates a little, the land share and the market carry it up), *and* he collects ~₫150 million/year in rent = ₫750 million. Total in a good case: ~ ₫4.5–4.9 billion including rent. In a bad case it dips to ~ ₫2.7 billion but rent keeps flowing and he can sell. *Narrow band, income cushion.*
- **Landed house:** ₫3 billion (a fringe plot) might run ₫2.7 billion (bad) to ₫4.5 billion (good), with minimal rent. Wider band than the apartment, little income, slower exit. *Land-led, patient.*
- **Đất nền:** ₫3 billion might be ₫6 billion in a fever (a double) or ₫1.5 billion *and frozen* in a bust — and **zero rent** the entire time. *The widest band by far, and the downside includes "can't sell at all."*

Same ₫3 billion, three radically different risk shapes. The expected returns might even look similar on a spreadsheet — but the *distribution* around them, and especially what happens in the bad case, is what you are really choosing between.

*Two bets can have the same average outcome and still be utterly different investments — the question is always how wide the band is and how ugly the left tail gets.*

## The liquidity ladder: apartment > house > đất nền

Of all the differences, liquidity is the one beginners most underestimate, so it earns its own section. **Liquidity** is how fast you can convert the asset to cash near its fair value. In a calm market the difference is annoying; in a crisis it is the whole ballgame, because the bet you can't sell is the bet that ruins you. The three residential types form a clean ladder, shown below alongside the goal each rung actually serves.

![Grid showing the liquidity ladder from apartment to landed house to dat nen and the investing goal each one serves](/imgs/blogs/residential-property-apartments-houses-and-dat-nen-6.png)

Three rungs:

- **Most liquid — apartments sell in weeks.** Standardised units, large buyer pool (both owner-occupiers and investors), clear price references from comparable sales in the same tower. If Minh needs cash, a decent 2-bed flat in a popular district lists and sells relatively quickly. The goal it serves: *live in it now, or rent it for income.*
- **Slower — landed houses sell in months.** Each plot is unique, the tickets are large, and the buyer pool is smaller and wealthier. Pricing is negotiated, not referenced. The goal: *long-hold wealth, durable land.* You are not buying it to flip; you are buying it to keep.
- **Least liquid — đất nền can freeze for years.** When the speculative story is alive, plots trade hand-to-hand fast; when it dies, the bid vanishes entirely because there is no income and no resident demand to anchor a price. The goal it honestly serves: *a pure appreciation bet — speculation.* If you cannot afford to have the money locked up for years, you cannot afford đất nền.

The deep reason for the ladder is *who the buyers are*. Apartments have the broadest buyer base — anyone who needs a home in the city. Houses narrow it to the wealthy. Đất nền narrows it to speculators who themselves only want in when prices are rising — so the buyer pool *expands in booms and collapses in busts*, which is the worst possible property for liquidity. The asset's liquidity is only as deep as its least-committed buyer, and for đất nền that buyer is a flipper who disappears the moment the trend turns.

#### Worked example: the liquidity cost of selling đất nền vs an apartment

Liquidity isn't free — illiquidity is a *cost*, and you can put a number on it. Imagine both Minh (apartment) and Lan (đất nền) suddenly need to raise **₫2 billion** in 60 days — a family emergency.

- **Minh's apartment**, fair value ₫2 billion. To sell in 60 days he might shave ~3–5% off the asking price to attract a quick buyer: he nets about **₫1.9 billion**, a ~₫100 million haircut. Painful but survivable, and *done in two months*.
- **Lan's đất nền**, fair value "₫2 billion" on paper. But the market is soft and there is *no one bidding* at ₫2 billion. To actually sell inside 60 days she may have to drop to **₫1.4 billion or worse** — a ~30% liquidity haircut — and even then she might not find a buyer at all and miss the deadline entirely.

The *liquidity cost* — the discount you eat to convert fast — is maybe ₫100 million on the apartment and ₫600 million (or "no sale") on the đất nền. Same nominal value, wildly different cost to escape.

*The price you see on an illiquid asset is the price in a calm market with a willing buyer; the price you actually get when you need cash in a hurry can be a third lower — and that gap is the true cost of illiquidity.*

## Which to buy when: by goal and cycle phase

There is no universal "best" bet — there is only the right bet *for a given goal at a given point in the cycle*. Match the rung to the purpose:

**By goal:**

- **"I need somewhere to live and want a return that pays me to wait."** → **Apartment.** Income now, liquidity later, the building's depreciation is the price of admission. The owner-occupier default.
- **"I have real wealth and want to store it in durable land for the next generation."** → **Landed house.** Prestige, undiluted land, slow but real. You are buying patience, not cash flow.
- **"I have money I can afford to lock up for years and I want maximum exposure to a land-price boom — and I accept I might be frozen."** → **Đất nền.** A speculative position, sized small, with eyes open.

#### Worked example: the same plot's structure-vs-land path over 40 years, three ways

Run the same 40-year clock past all three bets, each starting at **₫6 billion**, holding the underlying *land price* flat to isolate depreciation (real markets would lift the land; we strip that out to see the mechanism).

- **Apartment** (50% land / 50% structure): land right holds ≈ ₫3 billion; structure at 80% of life is worth ≈ 20% of ₫3 billion = ₫600 million. **End ≈ ₫3.6 billion** — a 40% real erosion from the building ageing.
- **Landed house** (70% land / 30% structure): land holds ≈ ₫4.2 billion; the smaller structure depreciates the same proportion — 20% of ₫1.8 billion = ₫360 million. **End ≈ ₫4.56 billion** — only a 24% erosion, because less of the asset was a building. And the owner can *rebuild* on the land he already owns, recovering structure value at construction cost.
- **Đất nền** (100% land / 0% structure): there is no building to depreciate, so the land right holds ≈ ₫6 billion in real terms. **End ≈ ₫6 billion** — zero depreciation. *But* it earned ₫0 rent for 40 years, and its price swings far more violently than the others around that flat baseline.

So on pure depreciation, đất nền "wins" (nothing wears out) and the apartment "loses" (most wears out). But that ranking inverts once you add income and volatility: the apartment paid rent the whole time and barely swung; the đất nền paid nothing and could have been frozen for years in any of those four decades.

*Stripped to depreciation alone, more land is strictly better — but property is never just depreciation; the income the building throws off and the calm of a steady price are exactly what the land-only bet gives up.*

**By cycle phase** — and here the [Vietnam property cycles](/blog/trading/real-estate/vietnam-property-cycles-2007-2013-2022) piece is the essential companion:

- **Early recovery (after a bust):** the most speculative, illiquid assets — đất nền — are the most beaten down and the cheapest, but also where the bodies are buried. Apartments in good locations recover first because real demand (people needing homes) returns first.
- **Expansion / mania:** đất nền screams highest, drawing in the most FOMO; this is exactly when it is *most* dangerous, because the speculative premium is largest and the next-buyer assumption is most stretched. Liquidity is plentiful — right up until it isn't.
- **The freeze (bust):** liquidity evaporates from the top of the risk ladder down. Đất nền seizes first and worst (2022 is the textbook case), landed houses go quiet, and even apartments slow — but the apartment, with its rent and its broad buyer base, is the one you can still actually sell.

The honest meta-lesson: the bet that looks best in a boom (đất nền, screaming up) is usually the one that hurts most in the bust, and the "boring" apartment that pays rent and sells in weeks is the one that lets you survive to buy when others are forced sellers. Liquidity and income are unglamorous in a mania and priceless in a crisis.

## Common misconceptions

**"Đất nền is safe because it's land — and they aren't making more of it."** This is the single most expensive belief in Vietnamese property. Land being finite makes it a good *long-run* store of value *in the right location with real demand*. It does **not** make a specific fringe plot, bought on a rumour, at a fevered price, safe. Đất nền has zero income to cushion you, no owner-occupier demand to floor the price, and can freeze for years — the exact opposite of "safe." Lan's 2018→2022 plot lost ~30% *and* she couldn't sell it. "It's land" is not a risk argument; it's a slogan.

**"Apartments always appreciate."** A central, well-located apartment in a growing city often does rise, and HCMC's 2024–25 surge was real. But an apartment is *part wasting asset*: the building depreciates over its ~50-year life, the land right is shared and finite-term, and in a flat or falling market the structure's decay can quietly outrun any land appreciation. An old building in a so-so location can absolutely be worth less in real terms decades on. "Always" is doing far too much work.

**"A landed house is just a more expensive apartment."** They are different *assets*, not different price points. The apartment is ~30–50% land (shared, finite-term) and mostly a depreciating building; the house is ~70% land (whole plot, long-term) and mostly durable dirt. They depreciate differently, are taxed and titled differently, attract different buyers, and trade with very different liquidity. Treating "house = pricier flat" leads you to misjudge everything that matters.

**"Higher potential return means it's a better investment."** Đất nền can post the highest return in a boom — and that is *precisely why* it is the riskiest, not a reason to prefer it. Higher expected return is the market's *compensation* for accepting extreme volatility, zero income, and the risk of being frozen. Return without context is meaningless; you have to look at the whole distribution, especially the left tail (the bad case), which for đất nền includes "can't sell at any price."

**"Rent yield is the same across residential types, so I'll just buy the cheapest land."** Rent yields are *not* the same. An apartment yields ~3–5% because the building generates rent; a land-heavy house yields less (~2–3%) because so much of the price is non-income-producing land; đất nền yields **zero** because there is no building to rent at all. The more land-heavy the bet, the less income — and bare land's "return" is *entirely* price appreciation, which is the volatile, story-driven part.

**"If the paper price is ₫3 billion, I can sell for ₫3 billion."** A quoted or "comparable" price is what an asset *might* fetch from a willing buyer in a normal market. The price you *actually realise* when you need to sell fast — or when the market is frozen — can be a third lower, or unobtainable. This liquidity gap is small for apartments and brutal for đất nền. Mark-to-market and mark-to-cash are very different numbers.

**"An off-plan apartment is the same as a finished one, just cheaper."** Off-plan (presale) units are cheaper for a *reason*: you are taking on developer risk, completion risk, and title-delay risk that a finished, titled unit does not carry. The discount is compensation for the chance the project is late, different from the brochure, or — as in Vietnam's 2022–23 freeze — stalled mid-build with your instalments already paid. A finished apartment with a pink book in hand is a genuinely different, lower-risk asset than a promise on a hole in the ground.

## How it shows up in real markets

**The HCMC apartment surge (2024–2025).** Primary apartment prices in HCMC ran roughly **₫91 million/m² in 2024 → ₫111 million/m² in 2025** (≈ \$4,222/m²), a ~33% jump in a year; Hanoi went ~₫72 million → ~₫100 million, up ~40%. This is the apartment story in action: in a hot market the land-share and demand carried the *whole* package up sharply, depreciation notwithstanding, while the broad buyer base kept these units liquid. It also pushed HCMC's **price-to-income ratio to around 32** (VnExpress cited "34 years' income," fifth-highest in the world) — meaning the median household would need ~32 years of *all* its income to buy a median home. That is the affordability cost of the surge: see [income, affordability and the price-to-income ratio](/blog/trading/real-estate/income-affordability-and-the-price-to-income-ratio). Apartments are liquid and convenient; they are also, increasingly, priced out of reach for the very owner-occupiers who anchor their demand.

**The đất nền fever and freeze (2018 → 2022).** In 2018, satellite provinces around HCMC — Long An, Đồng Nai, Bình Dương, and others — saw a classic đất nền fever, with plot prices in some areas doubling on rumours of roads, industrial parks, and airports. It was textbook extrapolation: each buyer paying up because they assumed the next buyer would pay more. Then came 2022. The **Tân Hoàng Minh** bond default and chairman's arrest (April 2022) and the **Vạn Thịnh Phát / SCB** scandal (October 2022) froze the corporate-bond market, credit slammed shut, and speculative land — the asset with no income, no use value, and the flimsiest buyer base — seized first and hardest. Plots that had quoted prices simply could not be sold; the bid-ask gap blew out; owners sat frozen earning nothing. The SBV cut policy rates four times in 2023 and Decree 08 gave bond restructuring relief, but the liquidity in fringe land did not come back quickly. Đất nền delivered the full boom-bust arc the figures describe.

**Landed scarcity in the urban core.** While apartments multiply vertically and đất nền sprawls on the fringe, *landed houses in established central districts* are genuinely scarce — you cannot make more District 1 street frontage. That scarcity, plus the cultural premium on owning a family home, keeps central nhà phố and biệt thự expensive and resilient even when speculative segments crater. The flip side is illiquidity: these are big-ticket, unique assets with a thin, wealthy buyer pool, so they trade slowly and the realised price is heavily negotiated. In a downturn they go *quiet* — fewer transactions, wide spreads — rather than visibly crashing, which can mask how much the mark-to-cash value has actually fallen.

**The global pattern: liquidity ladders repeat everywhere.** This is not a Vietnam-only quirk. In the United States, standardised suburban tract homes and condos are the most liquid residential assets, while raw land on the exurban fringe is notoriously illiquid and the first thing to freeze in a downturn — the 2008 bust wiped out countless speculative land and "lot" investors precisely because bare land has no income and no use-demand floor, exactly like đất nền. In every market, the residential asset with the broadest, most need-driven buyer base (the ordinary urban home) is the most liquid, and the asset bought purely on a future-price story is the most prone to freezing. The names change; the ladder doesn't.

## When this matters / Further reading

This matters the moment you — or someone you love — is about to make the biggest financial decision of their life. When Minh's parents say "buy land," his colleague says "buy the apartment," and his uncle quietly keeps the family nhà phố, they are not disagreeing about a price — they are describing three different bets with three different risk shapes, and the right answer depends entirely on *Minh's* goal, *Minh's* tolerance for being frozen, and *where the cycle is*. The land-vs-structure split tells you what you are really buying; the liquidity ladder tells you how easily you can get out; and the cycle tells you when each bet is cheap and when it is a trap. Hold those three lenses together and a chaotic, emotional market becomes legible.

A practical checklist before any residential purchase: What is the **land share** of this asset (and so how much is durable vs wasting)? What **income** does it throw off while I hold it (and what does waiting *cost* if it throws off none)? How fast and at what **discount** could I sell it if I had to in 60 days? And **where in the cycle** am I buying — early recovery, mania, or freeze? Answer those four and you have done more diligence than most buyers ever do.

The most important thing to carry away is that "residential property" is not one decision but three, and the difference between them is mostly the answer to a single question: *how much land, and how much building, am I actually buying?*
