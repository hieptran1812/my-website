> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Real estate is its own asset class — not a stock, not a bond — because it bundles a cocktail no other asset has: a cash-flowing productive asset, cheap leverage, a tax shelter, locality, and lumpy illiquidity, all in one.
>
> - It is the **largest store of wealth on Earth: about \$393 trillion**, roughly **4× the entire world's annual GDP**, and bigger than all the world's stocks and bonds *combined*.
> - Your total return on a property is the sum of **four separate engines**: income (rent), appreciation (price), leverage (the bank's money multiplying your equity), and tax (deductions and low transfer taxes). Decompose them and property stops being mysterious.
> - What makes it *different* from a stock or a bond is the combination: you can't sell a flat with one click, you routinely buy it with 5× borrowed money, it's glued to one location, and it comes in one lumpy ₫7-billion ticket you have to manage yourself.
> - The number to remember: **\$393 trillion**. The single biggest financial decision most people ever make is in this asset class — usually their own home.

In the spring of 2025, a software engineer in Ho Chi Minh City — let's call him **Minh** — sat down with a spreadsheet and a problem that millions of his neighbors share. He had saved roughly ₫1.4 billion (about \$54,000). A modest 70-square-meter apartment in a decent Hanoi or HCMC district now cost around ₫7 billion (≈ \$270,000). On paper that gap looked hopeless: the flat cost five times everything he had. And yet, walk through any new development in either city and you'll find it full of buyers exactly like Minh. How?

The answer is the entire subject of this post. Minh is not buying ₫7 billion of apartment with ₫1.4 billion of cash. He is putting down 20% and borrowing the rest from a bank — and in doing so he is reaching for a machine that no other ordinary investment offers him. That same ₫1.4 billion, if he put it in a savings account, earns him a few percent a year and nothing else. Put into the flat, it does four jobs at once: it collects rent, it rides the rising price of HCMC property, it controls an asset five times its own size, and it sits inside one of the lightest tax regimes in the world. Half a world away, a US investor we'll call **Dana** is running the same machine on a rental house in Texas, with different numbers and a heavier tax code, but the same four engines.

The diagram below is the mental model for the whole series: real estate is the largest asset class on the planet, and it is large precisely because it is the one asset almost everyone ends up owning. Before we can talk about cap rates, mortgages, or property cycles, we have to answer the most basic question — what *is* this thing, and why does it behave like nothing else you can buy?

## Foundations: what an asset class is, and the four engines of property

Let's build every piece from the ground up. By the end of this section you'll have the vocabulary to read the rest of the series without ever being lost.

### What is an "asset class"?

An *asset* is anything you own that has value and that you expect to either pay you cash or rise in price (ideally both). A *basis point*, a *coupon*, a *yield* — we'll define each as it comes up; for now, an asset is just "a thing of value you hold."

An **asset class** is a *family* of assets that share a common economic nature and tend to behave similarly. The four classic asset classes are:

- **Equities (stocks)** — you own a slice of a company. You're an *owner*. You profit if the company grows.
- **Fixed income (bonds)** — you lend money to a government or company. You're a *lender*. You profit from the interest they pay you back.
- **Cash** — money and near-money (bank deposits, money-market funds). Safe, liquid, low return.
- **Real assets** — physical things: real estate, commodities (oil, gold), infrastructure.

What makes something a *distinct* asset class isn't the look of the thing — it's that it answers four questions in its own way: *What do you actually own? What cash flow does it pay? What drives its price? What job does it do in a portfolio?* A stock and a bond are different classes because the first is ownership and the second is a loan; they pay different cash flows and respond to different forces. By that test, real estate is unmistakably its own class — and most of this post is an extended argument for *why*. (For the full map of every asset class side by side, see [the map of asset classes](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-map-of-asset-classes-what-you-can-own).)

### Real asset vs financial asset

This distinction matters more in real estate than almost anywhere. A **financial asset** is a *claim* — a piece of paper (or a database entry) that represents a promise. A bond is a promise to repay; a stock is a claim on a company's future profits. Its value lives in a contract.

A **real asset** is a *physical thing* with use-value in the world: a building you can live in, a field you can farm, a barrel of oil you can burn. Real estate is the archetypal real asset. This is why it behaves so differently from a stock: a share certificate can become worthless if the company fails, but the bricks, the roof, and — above all — the *land* beneath a building do not evaporate. They have a floor of intrinsic worth because people will always need somewhere to live, work, and store things.

### The land + structure split (a preview)

Here is a distinction we'll spend a whole future post on, but you need the seed of it now. A piece of real estate is really *two* assets fused together:

1. **The land** — the location. It is finite, it cannot be manufactured, and almost all of property's long-run appreciation comes from here. A plot in central HCMC and an identical plot 50 km out differ in price by an order of magnitude, and the difference is *entirely* land — that is, location.
2. **The structure** — the building sitting on the land. Unlike land, a building *depreciates*: it ages, wears out, and needs maintenance and eventual replacement.

When property prices "go up," it is overwhelmingly the *land* that appreciated while the building quietly got older. This split (we'll dig into it in [the next post](/blog/trading/real-estate/cap-rate-noi-and-the-income-approach) and beyond) is why a brand-new condo can lose value while the dirt under an old house in a good district keeps gaining.

### Rent and yield

If you own a property and let someone else use it, they pay you **rent** — a recurring cash payment, usually monthly. Rent is real estate's version of a stock's dividend or a bond's coupon: it's the *income* the asset throws off just for owning it.

To compare that income across properties of different prices, we convert it into a percentage called the **rental yield** (or *gross yield*): the annual rent divided by the price of the property.

$$\text{Gross rental yield} = \frac{\text{Annual rent}}{\text{Property price}}$$

If a ₫7-billion flat rents for ₫15 million a month, that's ₫180 million a year, so the gross yield is ₫180M ÷ ₫7,000M ≈ **2.6%**. That number — how much income the property pays relative to its price — is the single most important figure in real estate finance, and it has a cousin called the *cap rate* that we'll meet in the very next post. "Gross" means *before* costs; subtract maintenance, management, vacancy, and tax and you get the *net* yield, which is always lower. Hold that thought.

### Capital appreciation

The *second* way a property pays is **capital appreciation** — the rise in the property's market price over time. Buy at ₫7 billion, sell at ₫8 billion three years later, and you've earned a ₫1 billion *capital gain* without the tenant ever being involved. Appreciation is the engine most buyers fixate on (sometimes dangerously so), and in fast-growing markets like Vietnam it has historically dwarfed the rental income.

### Leverage and LTV

Here's the engine that makes real estate unique among assets ordinary people can buy. **Leverage** means using borrowed money to control an asset larger than your own cash. You buy a ₫7-billion flat with ₫1.4 billion of your own money (your *equity*, or *down payment*) and a ₫5.6-billion *mortgage* (a loan secured against the property).

The ratio of the loan to the property's value is the **loan-to-value, or LTV**: here ₫5.6bn ÷ ₫7bn = **80%**. The flip side — your equity as a fraction of value — is 20%. Because you control ₫7 billion of asset with ₫1.4 billion of cash, your *leverage ratio* is 7 ÷ 1.4 = **5×**.

Leverage is a multiplier, and multipliers work in both directions. We'll see exactly how in the worked examples — but the headline is that a 5% move in the *property's* price becomes a 25% move in *your money*. That asymmetry of effort (a little cash controlling a lot of asset) is why property, and not stocks, is how most middle-class families build wealth. Banks will happily lend 80% against a flat; almost no broker will lend you 80% to buy shares. (We devote the whole post [leverage and the mortgage](/blog/trading/real-estate/leverage-and-the-mortgage-how-debt-amplifies-property) to this engine.)

### Illiquidity and transaction costs

**Liquidity** is how fast and cheaply you can turn an asset into cash without moving its price. A stock is *liquid*: you can sell ₫100 million of shares in seconds with one click for a few thousand dong in fees. Real estate is the opposite — it is **illiquid**. Selling a flat takes weeks to months: you list it, wait for a buyer, negotiate, sign contracts, and transfer title at a notary. And it's expensive: in Vietnam you'll pay a **2% personal income tax** on the sale price plus a **0.5% registration fee**, before any agent commission. **Transaction costs** — the friction of buying and selling — are tiny for stocks and large for property. This single fact shapes how the whole asset behaves: you can't trade out of it in a panic, which is both its curse (you're stuck in a falling market) and, oddly, its protection (you can't sell at the bottom on emotion).

### The four return drivers

Pull it all together and you reach the central idea of the entire series. Your **total return** on a property is not one number from one source — it is the *sum of four separate engines*:

$$\text{Total return} = \underbrace{\text{Income}}_{\text{rent}} + \underbrace{\text{Appreciation}}_{\text{price rise}} + \underbrace{\text{Leverage}}_{\text{amplifier}} + \underbrace{\text{Tax}}_{\text{shelter}}$$

- **Income** — the rent, expressed as a yield (~3–4% gross in Vietnamese cities, before costs).
- **Appreciation** — the rise in the property's price (~5–8% a year in recent VN history).
- **Leverage** — borrowed money that *multiplies* whatever the first two produce, applied to your small slice of equity.
- **Tax** — the deductions, depreciation, and (in Vietnam) very light transfer taxes that mean you keep more of the gain than you would on most other assets.

The figure below lays out the four engines. Every later section of this post — and most of the series — is just one of these four engines examined in detail.

A quick honesty note before we go on: this is an educational deep dive, not financial advice. Real estate can lose money — leverage that multiplies gains multiplies losses just as faithfully — and we'll name the downside at every step. The goal here is to make the *mechanism* legible so you can think clearly, not to tell anyone to buy or sell.

## Why property is the biggest asset class: the \$393 trillion fact

Start with the number that should reframe how you see the entire financial world. As of end-2024, the total value of the world's real estate was about **\$393.3 trillion** (Savills' running estimate, the most widely cited figure for the asset class). Let's put that in context, because the comparisons are genuinely startling:

- All the world's **debt securities** — every government bond, every corporate bond, the entire fixed-income universe — total about **\$144 trillion** (SIFMA/BIS).
- All the world's **publicly listed stocks** — the combined market value of every company on every exchange — total about **\$126 trillion** (SIFMA).
- All the **gold ever mined** in human history, every coin, bar, ring, and central-bank reserve, is worth about **\$20 trillion** — roughly **5%** of the value of real estate.

Add stocks and bonds together and you get about \$270 trillion. Real estate, at \$393 trillion, is *larger than both combined*. It is, by a wide margin, the largest store of wealth on Earth — and the cover chart of this post (Figure 1) shows the gap at a glance. To anchor the scale one more way: global GDP — everything the entire world produces in a year — is around \$106 trillion. So **real estate is worth roughly four times the planet's annual output**. Four years of all human economic activity, frozen into bricks and land.

Of that \$393 trillion, the lion's share — around **\$301 trillion** — is *residential* property: the homes people live in. Commercial real estate (offices, shops, hotels) is about **\$58 trillion**, and agricultural land another ~\$34 trillion. The dominance of housing is the tell: real estate is the biggest asset class because it is the one almost every household on Earth participates in, whether they think of themselves as "investors" or not.

Why is it *so* much bigger than stocks and bonds? Three reasons stack up. First, **everyone needs shelter** — there is no household that opts out of the housing market the way most households opt out of owning stocks. Second, **property is global and old**: every city that has ever existed left behind buildings and titled land, while public equity markets are a relatively recent and geographically concentrated invention (a huge share of global stock-market value sits in just a handful of countries). Third, and most importantly for this series, **real estate is where ordinary wealth lives**. For a typical middle-class family in Vietnam, the US, or almost anywhere, their home is the largest single thing they own — often worth more than their pension, their savings, and their car combined. Multiply that across billions of households and you get a pool of wealth that no corporate stock market can rival.

This also explains a feature of the number that surprises people: most of that \$393 trillion is **not for sale and not financialized**. Unlike the stock market, where the headline value is by definition the price of shares actively changing hands, the vast majority of real estate is owner-occupied homes that will never trade in a given year. The asset class is enormous *and* mostly illiquid — a combination that, as we'll see, is the source of both its stability and its periodic crises.

#### Worked example: what \$393 trillion versus \$270 trillion actually means

Imagine you could buy *every share of every public company in the world* — the entire \$126 trillion of global equities. Then imagine you also bought *every tradable bond on the planet* — another \$144 trillion. You'd have spent \$270 trillion and owned the entire corporate and sovereign-debt machinery of human civilization. You would *still* not have enough to buy the world's real estate, which would cost you \$393 trillion. You'd be \$123 trillion short — a shortfall larger than the entire global stock market by itself.

*The intuition: property isn't a sideshow to the "real" financial markets of stocks and bonds — it is, by value, the main event, and the one most people actually own a piece of.*

This is the central paradox the series exists to fix: real estate is the largest asset class and most people's single biggest financial commitment, yet it is taught worst. People who would never buy a stock without checking its P/E ratio will buy a ₫7-billion flat — five times their net worth, with five-to-one leverage — on a Saturday afternoon based on a brochure and a feeling. The rest of this post gives you the framework that brochure leaves out.

## The four ways property pays you

Now we open each of the four engines in turn, with numbers. Throughout, we'll follow **Minh** and his ₫7-billion HCMC flat, and check the universal mechanism against **Dana** and her US rental.

### Engine 1 — Income (rent)

The most boring engine is also the most reliable: someone pays you to use your property. Minh's flat rents for **₫15 million a month**, or ₫180 million a year. Against the ₫7-billion price, that's a **gross yield of ≈ 2.6%** — which, frankly, is low. Vietnamese big-city apartments are notorious for thin rental yields (often 2–4%) precisely because prices have run so far ahead of rents. In other words, Vietnamese buyers are mostly paying for the *next* engine — appreciation — and accepting weak income.

Income is the engine that survives a downturn. When prices stop rising or fall, appreciation goes to zero or negative — but the tenant still pays the rent (most of the time). That's why income-focused investors, and the whole discipline of *cap rates* we cover next, treat rent as the bedrock. A property that pays you 5% net while you wait can survive years of flat prices; a property bought purely on the bet that the price will rise has no cushion at all if the bet is wrong.

There's a subtlety hiding in the word "gross." The 2.6% we computed is *gross* yield — rent over price, before a single cost. The number a landlord actually keeps is the **net yield**, after the running costs that the headline always omits: management, maintenance, insurance, vacancy, and tax. These costs are not small. Let's make them concrete.

#### Worked example: from Minh's gross yield to what he actually keeps

Minh's flat collects ₫180M of rent a year — a 2.6% gross yield on ₫7 billion. Now subtract what running it really costs over a typical year:

- **Vacancy:** assume the flat sits empty ~1 month between tenants (≈ 8% of the year). That's roughly −₫15M of lost rent.
- **Management / agent:** finding and handling tenants, say 8% of collected rent ≈ −₫13M (or his own time, if he self-manages).
- **Maintenance + building fees + insurance:** apartment service charges, repairs, the occasional new appliance — call it −₫25M.

Net rent ≈ ₫180M − ₫15M − ₫13M − ₫25M = **₫127M**. So the **net yield** = 127 ÷ 7,000 ≈ **1.8%** — meaningfully below the 2.6% gross headline. And we haven't even subtracted mortgage interest yet (because that's a *financing* cost, not a property cost — we handle it under leverage).

*The intuition: the rental yield that sells the deal is always the gross one; the yield that pays your bills is the net one, and the gap between them — vacancy, management, upkeep — is exactly the management burden that makes direct property a small business, not a passive holding.*

### The hidden fifth driver: principal paydown

There's one more way leverage quietly pays you that the four-engine model folds into "leverage" but is worth naming on its own: **amortization**, the gradual repayment of the loan principal. *Amortization* simply means each monthly mortgage payment is part interest (the bank's fee for the loan) and part principal (paying down what you borrowed). Over the years, the principal portion steadily shrinks the loan and grows your equity — and crucially, *the tenant's rent is what makes those payments*. You are slowly buying the flat with someone else's money. By the time a 20-year mortgage is paid off, the loan is gone, your equity is the full value of the (appreciated) flat, and a tenant funded most of the journey. We treat amortization in full in the [leverage and mortgage post](/blog/trading/real-estate/leverage-and-the-mortgage-how-debt-amplifies-property); for now, just know that "leverage" pays you twice — once by amplifying price moves, and once by letting rent retire your debt.

#### Worked example: decomposing Minh's total return

Let's put real numbers on all four engines at once, then return to the others. Minh buys the ₫7-billion flat. In one year:

- **Income:** ₫15M/month × 12 = ₫180M of rent. Gross yield = 180 ÷ 7,000 = **2.6%**.
- **Appreciation:** Suppose the flat rises **8%** (in line with recent VN history), so +₫560M.
- Total *property-level* return = 2.6% + 8% = **10.6%** on the ₫7-billion asset, or ₫740M before costs.

But Minh didn't put in ₫7 billion — he put in ₫1.4 billion. The ₫560M of appreciation lands on his *equity*, not on the full price. We'll do that leverage math precisely in Engine 3. For now, the decomposition is the lesson: of Minh's ~10.6% property-level return, only a quarter came from rent; three-quarters came from price appreciation. *Knowing which engine is doing the work tells you exactly how fragile or durable a deal is.*

*The intuition: a property's headline "return" hides which engine produced it — and a return that's 75% appreciation is a very different (riskier) animal than one that's 75% rent.*

### Engine 2 — Appreciation

Appreciation is the rise in the property's price, and it is where land does its quiet work. We'll see the data shortly (Figure 6), but Vietnamese city apartments roughly *tripled* between 2019 and 2025. Over that window, appreciation utterly dominated rental income as a source of return — a ₫7-billion flat that paid ₫180M of rent a year (2.6%) might have gained ₫560M+ in price in a single good year (8%+).

The danger of appreciation is that it feels like income but isn't. Rent is cash in your bank account every month. Appreciation is a *paper* gain — a number on a valuation — until you sell or refinance and turn it into cash. Markets where buyers treat unrealized appreciation as if it were guaranteed income are exactly the markets that produce bubbles, a pattern we'll trace through Vietnam's own 2007–08 and 2018 episodes and the US in 2006 in the [property cycle posts](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-liquidity-cycle-and-asset-prices).

Appreciation also hides a structural truth from the land/structure split. When a flat "appreciates 8%," it's almost never the *building* that gained — concrete and pipes don't get more valuable as they age; they get less. What appreciated is the **land share** of the value. Picture Minh's ₫7-billion flat as, say, ₫4.2 billion of land value and ₫2.8 billion of structure. Over a year, the structure quietly depreciates (it's a year older, a year closer to needing a new roof and new fittings), while the land — the location, the proximity to a new metro line or a growing district — appreciates enough to more than offset it. The net is "+8%," but underneath, two opposite forces were at work. This is why a 30-year-old house in a prime HCMC district can be worth a fortune (the land won; the house is almost a teardown) while a glossy new condo in a far suburb can stagnate (the building is depreciating and the location isn't pulling its weight). We unpack this fully in a later post; the seed to plant now is that *appreciation is a story about land, and depreciation is a story about structure, and every property's price is the tug-of-war between them.*

### Engine 3 — Leverage

Here is the engine that makes real estate the wealth-builder it is. Leverage doesn't create return out of nothing — it *amplifies* the return the first two engines already produce, applying it to your small slice of equity rather than the whole price.

![Pipeline diagram showing how a property makes money over a hold from buying with a deposit and mortgage, to collecting rent, to the value rising, to leverage amplifying the equity gain, to tax sheltering the gain, to selling or refinancing](/imgs/blogs/what-is-real-estate-as-an-asset-class-4.png)

The pipeline above is the whole game in one picture: buy with a deposit plus mortgage, collect rent, let the value rise, watch leverage land that rise on your equity, shelter the gain from tax, then sell or refinance and repeat. Let's do the arithmetic that makes the third box real.

#### Worked example: the leverage multiplier (and its symmetric danger)

Minh controls a ₫7-billion flat with ₫1.4 billion of equity (20% down, 80% LTV, 5× leverage).

**The upside.** Suppose the price rises just **5%** in a year — a modest move. That's +₫350M on the ₫7-billion flat. But Minh only invested ₫1.4 billion, and the entire ₫350M gain accrues to *him* (the bank's ₫5.6 billion loan doesn't share in the upside; it just wants its interest and principal back). So:

$$\text{Return on equity} = \frac{₫350\text{M gain}}{₫1{,}400\text{M equity}} = 25\%$$

A **5% move in the property became a 25% move on his money** — a 5× multiplication, exactly the leverage ratio.

**The downside, which is symmetric.** Now suppose the price *falls* 5%. That's −₫350M, and again it lands entirely on Minh's equity. His ₫1.4 billion becomes ₫1.05 billion — a **−25%** loss on his money. If the price fell 20%, his entire ₫1.4-billion equity would be wiped out, and he'd owe a mortgage on a flat worth less than the loan (*negative equity*, the exact trap that detonated the US in 2008).

The figure below shows both sides of that coin side by side.

![Before and after comparison showing a five percent property price fall turning into a twenty five percent loss on equity on the left and a five percent price rise turning into a twenty five percent gain on equity on the right](/imgs/blogs/what-is-real-estate-as-an-asset-class-5.png)

*The intuition: leverage is a magnifying glass held over your equity — it makes a small price move loom large in both directions, which is why it builds fortunes in rising markets and destroys them in falling ones.*

#### Worked example: stacking the engines — Minh's full year on equity

Now combine everything on Minh's actual capital. He put in ₫1.4 billion. Run one full year at the numbers we've used:

- **Appreciation:** flat rises 8% → +₫560M, all of which lands on his ₫1.4-billion equity = **+40%** on his money from price alone.
- **Net rent:** ₫127M of net rent (from the earlier example). But he also pays mortgage interest on the ₫5.6-billion loan. At a preferential ~8% teaser rate, year-one interest ≈ ₫448M. So rent *minus* interest ≈ ₫127M − ₫448M = **−₫321M** of cash flow — the flat is *negative-carry* in year one (the rent doesn't cover the mortgage). On his equity that's about −23%.
- **Net so far:** +40% (appreciation) − 23% (negative carry) ≈ **+17%** on equity, before principal paydown and before tax.

Two lessons fall out. First, leverage made an 8% asset move into a 40% equity swing — enormous. Second, the *income* engine went *negative* once the mortgage is counted: Minh is betting almost entirely on appreciation to bail out a flat that bleeds cash every month. That's the typical Vietnamese big-city profile, and it's exactly why a stall in prices (2011–13, 2022–23) is so dangerous to leveraged buyers — when appreciation stops, all that's left is the negative carry.

*The intuition: leverage doesn't just multiply your gains, it also multiplies your monthly bleed — a negative-carry, high-appreciation flat is a coiled spring that pays spectacularly if prices rise and punishes you every month if they don't.*

Leverage is also *cheap* and *long* in a way no stock margin loan ever is. A mortgage runs 15–25 years at single-digit (or low-teens) rates, and the *tenant's rent* often covers most of the interest. That combination — long, cheap, partly self-funding leverage on an appreciating real asset — simply does not exist anywhere else in an ordinary investor's toolkit. (The catch, which we cover in detail later, is the Vietnamese *teaser-then-reset* loan: a tempting ~8% rate for the first 12–24 months that then floats up to 12–14%. The reset is where over-leveraged buyers get hurt.)

### Engine 4 — Tax

The fourth engine is the quietest and, over a lifetime, one of the most powerful: the tax code treats real estate gently. The specifics differ wildly by country, which is exactly why we run both Minh and Dana.

In **Vietnam**, the tax treatment of property is famously light:

- Sell a property and you pay a flat **2% personal income tax** on the *declared sale price* — not on the gain, on the whole price, but at a low rate. Sell Minh's ₫7-billion flat and the PIT is ₫140M.
- A **0.5% registration fee** ("lệ phí trước bạ") on transfer.
- The annual non-agricultural land-use tax is tiny — roughly **0.03–0.15%** of the state-assessed price. Crucially, **there is no broad annual property tax yet** in Vietnam (a second-home/holding tax has been *debated* by the Ministry of Finance from 2023 onward but, as of mid-2026, not enacted). The **Land Law 2024**, effective August 2024, did change things by replacing the old 5-year framework price with *annual* land-price tables, which feeds these tax bases — but the headline remains: holding property in Vietnam costs almost nothing in tax per year.

In the **US**, Dana's world is heavier and more intricate: annual *property taxes* of roughly 1–2% of value every year, *capital gains tax* on appreciation when she sells — but also generous *depreciation* deductions that shelter her rental income, and the ability to defer gains entirely via a "1031 exchange" if she rolls the money into another property. The US code taxes property more, but it also hands landlords more tools to shelter income.

#### Worked example: Dana's depreciation shelter

Dana, our US investor, buys a \$300,000 rental house (say \$60,000 of that is land, \$240,000 the structure). US tax law lets her *depreciate* the structure over 27.5 years — a paper deduction reflecting the building's wear — of \$240,000 ÷ 27.5 ≈ **\$8,700 a year**. Suppose the house nets her \$12,000 of rental income after expenses. On paper, she subtracts the \$8,700 depreciation, so she's taxed on only \$12,000 − \$8,700 = **\$3,300** — even though \$12,000 of real cash landed in her account. The depreciation deduction *shelters* most of her income from tax, despite the building (and the land under it) likely *rising* in value. This is the engine that makes US rental real estate so tax-efficient: you deduct a "loss" that isn't economically happening. (The catch — *depreciation recapture* when she sells — is a story for another day.)

*The intuition: the tax engine can be worth as much as a point or two of yield a year, and it works in opposite ways across countries — Vietnam keeps the holding tax near zero, while the US lets you deduct a paper loss against very real income.*

#### Worked example: the illiquidity-and-friction cost of selling

The tax engine has a flip side — friction. Suppose Minh needs cash urgently and must sell his ₫7-billion flat. Watch what the round trip costs:

- **Time:** Realistically *2–4 months* to find a buyer, negotiate, and complete title transfer. He cannot get his money on Tuesday.
- **2% PIT** on the ₫7-billion price = **₫140M**.
- **0.5% registration / transfer-related fees** ≈ **₫35M**.
- **Agent commission** (typically ~1–2%) ≈ **₫70–140M**.

So before any price negotiation, selling costs him roughly **₫245–315M — about 3.5–4.5% of the flat's value** — and months of his time. Compare that to selling ₫7 billion of a stock index fund: a few minutes and a fee measured in tens of thousands of dong.

*The intuition: the same illiquidity that protects you from panic-selling at the bottom also means you can never treat property as an emergency fund — getting out is slow and expensive, and that cost is part of the asset's true return.*

## What makes real estate *different* from stocks and bonds

We've now met the four engines. But several individual assets share *one* of them — a high-dividend stock pays income, a growth stock appreciates, a margined position uses leverage. What makes real estate its own class is that it bundles all four *plus* a set of structural quirks no financial asset has. Let's name them precisely, because each one is a recurring character in the rest of the series.

![Comparison matrix of real estate versus stock versus bond versus cash across liquidity, leverage, income, volatility, minimum ticket size, and management showing real estate as the only illiquid, highly leveraged, hands-on, lumpy asset](/imgs/blogs/what-is-real-estate-as-an-asset-class-3.png)

The matrix above lines up all four asset classes across six attributes. Read down the real-estate column and you see the profile of an outlier: the *only* asset that is simultaneously illiquid, heavily leveraged, income-paying, lumpy in ticket size, and hands-on to manage. Now the individual quirks.

### Illiquidity

We've defined it: you can't sell quickly or cheaply. But the consequences run deep. Illiquidity means real estate prices are *sticky* and *opaque* — there's no live ticker, every property is a one-off negotiation, and sellers in a downturn tend to simply *not sell* (pulling listings) rather than cut prices, so reported prices fall slowly and lag reality by months. It also means the asset can't be marked-to-market the way a stock is, which is part of why property *feels* less volatile than it actually is.

### Leverage as the default, not the exception

With stocks, borrowing to invest (buying on *margin*) is something most people never do, and brokers cap it tightly. With real estate, borrowing is the *norm* — buying a home for cash is the rarity. The entire asset class is built on a credit relationship between buyers and banks, which is exactly why real estate is so sensitive to interest rates and credit availability. When mortgages get cheap and plentiful, prices rise; when credit freezes (Vietnam's 2022–23 corporate-bond crunch, the US in 2008), the whole asset class seizes up. Real estate is, in a real sense, a *leveraged bet on the availability of credit* — link out to [the liquidity cycle and asset prices](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-liquidity-cycle-and-asset-prices) for why this dominates property's behavior.

### Locality

A share of a global company is the same whether you buy it in Hanoi or New York. A property is welded to *one specific patch of Earth*, and its value is dominated by that location. This is the famous real-estate cliché — "location, location, location" — and it's literally true: the land component (recall the land/structure split) is pure location. Locality means real estate is not one market but *thousands* of local markets, each driven by its own jobs, schools, infrastructure, and zoning. HCMC and a rural province can boom and bust on completely different clocks. You cannot diversify a single property the way you diversify a stock portfolio — each one is a concentrated bet on one place.

### Lumpiness

You can buy one \$50 share of a company. You cannot buy 1% of a specific apartment (REITs aside — more below). Real estate comes in large, indivisible **lumps**: a ₫7-billion flat is a single ₫7-billion decision. This *lumpiness* is why property is so concentrated in most households' balance sheets — a family doesn't own "a bit of real estate," it owns *the house*, often worth more than everything else they own combined. It's also why leverage is necessary: almost no one can buy the lump outright.

### Management

A stock or bond is *passive* — you buy it and a company or government does the work; you just collect dividends or coupons. Real estate is *operational*: tenants must be found and screened, rent collected, leaks fixed, taxes filed, vacancies filled. Direct property ownership is a small business, not a passive holding. That management burden is a real cost (in time, money, or a property manager's fee, typically 5–10% of rent) and a real risk (a bad tenant or a long vacancy can erase a year's yield). It's the hidden line item that "the price went up 8%" never mentions.

### Low correlation with stocks and bonds

Finally, the quirk that makes real estate valuable in a portfolio even beyond its own returns: it tends to move *somewhat independently* of stocks and bonds. *Correlation* measures whether two assets move together (correlation of +1) or independently (0) or oppositely (−1). Real estate's correlation to equities is positive but well below 1 — partly real (it's driven by local rents and credit, not the stock market's mood) and partly an *illusion* of its illiquidity (since it isn't marked-to-market daily, its measured volatility and correlation look artificially smooth). Either way, adding property to a stock-and-bond portfolio has historically improved the risk-adjusted return — the reason it earns a permanent slot in serious allocations. For the portfolio-fit angle and how listed property (REITs) actually correlates once it *is* marked-to-market daily, see [real estate, REITs, income, leverage, and rates](/blog/trading/cross-asset/real-estate-reits-income-leverage-and-rates).

## The taxonomy of real estate: five families

"Real estate" is not one thing. Before you can analyze any deal, you have to know which *family* of property you're looking at, because the cash flow, the risk, and even the buyer are completely different across them. The tree below maps the five families.

![Tree taxonomy of real estate branching into residential, commercial, industrial, land, and REITs, with residential splitting into apartments, landed houses, and land plots, and REITs shown as the paper way to own property](/imgs/blogs/what-is-real-estate-as-an-asset-class-7.png)

**Residential** — where people live. The biggest family by far (~\$301 trillion of the global \$393 trillion). In Vietnam it splits three ways: *apartments* (căn hộ), *landed houses* (nhà phố — townhouses and villas, prized because you own the land outright), and *land plots* (đất nền — buying the raw plot, the most speculative and the engine of Vietnam's periodic land "fevers"). Residential is driven by household incomes, demographics, and mortgage availability.

**Commercial** — property that businesses rent: office towers and retail (shops, malls). Here the cash flow is a *lease* to a business tenant, often for years, and the value is driven by that business's health and the *cap rate* (the yield investors demand). When we say "CRE" later, this is what we mean.

**Industrial / logistics** — warehouses, factories, distribution centers. Long the dull cousin, this family has boomed globally with e-commerce and has been a particular star in Vietnam as manufacturing shifts in from China. Long leases, sticky tenants, lower glamour, steady yield.

**Land** — raw, undeveloped land and development sites. Pure location with no building to depreciate. The highest-risk, highest-potential family: no rent while you hold it (so all return must come from appreciation), but the biggest gains when a rural plot becomes a suburb.

**REITs (Real Estate Investment Trusts)** — *property as paper*. A REIT is a company that owns income-producing real estate and trades on a stock exchange like any share. Buy a REIT and you own a tiny, liquid slice of a portfolio of buildings — gaining real-estate income and appreciation *without* the lumpiness, illiquidity, or management. It's the bridge between the real-asset world and the financial-asset world, and it's how a small investor can own commercial property they could never buy directly.

#### Worked example: REIT vs direct ownership — the liquidity trade-off

Minh has ₫1.4 billion. He has two ways to get real-estate exposure:

- **Direct:** Put the whole ₫1.4 billion down on the ₫7-billion flat (5× leverage). He gets the full four-engine machine — rent, appreciation, leverage, tax shelter — but he's *concentrated* in one flat in one district, he's *illiquid* (months and ~4% friction to sell), and he's now a part-time landlord chasing a tenant and fixing the air-con.
- **REIT:** Put ₫1.4 billion into a listed property fund. He owns a *diversified* slice of many buildings, he can sell *any morning* with one click for a tiny fee, and he never fixes an air-con. But he gets *far less leverage* (a REIT carries its own modest debt; he isn't borrowing 5×), and his return is mostly the ~4–6% dividend yield plus the share price — no 25%-on-a-5%-move multiplier.

Same underlying asset class; almost opposite *experience*. The direct route trades liquidity and convenience for leverage and control; the REIT trades leverage and control for liquidity and ease.

*The intuition: REITs and direct property are two doors into the same building — one is liquid, diversified, and passive; the other is leveraged, concentrated, and hands-on — and which door you want depends entirely on whether you're after the leverage multiplier or the one-click exit.*

## Common misconceptions

Real estate is drowning in folk wisdom, much of it half-true and dangerous. Here are the big ones, named and corrected.

**"Property never goes down."** It does — often, and sometimes catastrophically. The US national house-price index fell about **27% peak-to-trough** from July 2006 to February 2012; Japan's property prices peaked in 1990 and were *still* below that peak two decades later; Vietnam itself had a hard freeze in 2011–13 when inflation hit ~18% and prices in many segments fell sharply. The belief that property "always goes up" comes from looking only at long, leveraged holding periods in growing markets — and survivorship bias hides everyone who was forced to sell in the trough. Add leverage to "property fell 27%" and you understand 2008's wave of wiped-out equity.

**"Rent is throwing money away."** This is the homeowner's favorite line, and it's incomplete. Renting *is* paying for shelter with nothing left over — but so is the *interest* portion of a mortgage, the property tax, the maintenance, and the transaction costs of buying and selling. Owning builds equity only through the *principal* you repay and the appreciation you capture, minus all those frictions. In a flat or falling market with high transaction costs, renting and investing the difference can genuinely beat owning. The honest framing isn't "rent = waste, buy = wealth"; it's "compare the full cost of owning, including the dead-money parts, against rent."

**"Land always wins."** Land *often* wins over long horizons because of the location story — but "always" is how Vietnamese đất nền investors got hurt in the 2018 and 2022 fevers, buying speculative plots in satellite provinces at prices that assumed infrastructure and development that never arrived (or arrived a decade late). Raw land pays *no rent*, so you bleed holding costs and opportunity cost the entire time you wait. Land is the highest-variance family precisely because all of its return is appreciation and none is income.

**"Real estate is a passive investment."** Only REITs are. Direct property is an operating business — tenants, repairs, vacancies, taxes, the 2 a.m. plumbing call. Investors who model property as "buy it and the rent rolls in" routinely forget the 5–10% management cost, the months of vacancy between tenants, and the capital expenditure when the building ages.

**"My home is my best investment."** Maybe — but your home is also a *consumption* good (you live in it) and an *undiversified, illiquid, leveraged* bet on one location, usually larger than your entire net worth. Treating the roof over your head as a pure investment leads to bad decisions (over-improving, refusing to move for a better job, ignoring that you can't eat home equity). A home is part shelter, part forced savings, part leveraged real-estate bet — and conflating the three is how people fool themselves.

**"Higher price means a better investment."** Price and *yield* move opposite ways. The HCMC flats that have appreciated most are exactly the ones with the *lowest* rental yields (~2.6%), because price ran ahead of rent. A cheaper property in a less glamorous area paying 6% net can be a far better *income* investment than a prestige flat paying 2.6% — they're just betting on different engines.

## How it shows up in real markets

Theory is clean; markets are not. Here are concrete, real, citable episodes — Vietnamese and global — where the mechanisms of this post played out.

### Vietnam's price climb, 2019–2025

The single clearest illustration of the appreciation engine is recent Vietnamese history. Average *primary* (new-build) apartment prices, in million VND per square meter, roughly tripled in six years.

![Line chart of Vietnam primary apartment prices from 2019 to 2025 in million VND per square meter showing Hanoi rising from 33 to 100 and HCMC rising from 45 to 111 with a steep surge in 2024 and 2025](/imgs/blogs/what-is-real-estate-as-an-asset-class-6.png)

The numbers (CBRE / Ministry of Construction via VnExpress): **Hanoi** went from ~33M VND/m² in 2019 to ~**100M in 2025** — and most of that came late, with roughly **+35%** in 2024 and another **~+40%** in 2025. **HCMC** climbed from ~45M to ~**111M** (≈ \$4,222/m² at an exchange rate around 25,900 VND to the dollar). On a 70-square-meter flat, the 2025 HCMC price implies roughly ₫7.0 billion (≈ \$270,000) — exactly Minh's flat. This is the appreciation engine running hot, and it's why Vietnamese buyers tolerate 2.6% rental yields: they were paid by price, not by rent. It's also a flashing caution light — when appreciation this steep is the *only* engine carrying the return, the deal has no income cushion if prices stall.

### The affordability wall

The flip side of that price climb is brutal affordability. *Price-to-income* — how many years of the median household's entire income it takes to buy a home — is a standard global yardstick (Numbeo, mid-2026). HCMC sits around **32 years** (VnExpress reported "34 years' income," 5th-highest in the world), Hanoi around **31**. For comparison: Singapore ~17, Tokyo ~15, London ~12, New York ~10, with a global median near 15. Vietnamese big cities are, on this measure, among the *least* affordable on Earth relative to local incomes — pricier in income-years than Tokyo, London, or New York. That gap is precisely why leverage and long mortgages are not optional in Vietnam; without them the lump is simply unreachable for a normal household.

### The US 2008 crash: leverage in reverse

The clearest global lesson in the *downside* of the leverage engine is the US housing crash. The S&P/Case-Shiller national index peaked at 184.6 in July 2006 and fell to 134 by February 2012 — about **−27%**. A 27% fall sounds survivable. But American buyers had often put down 5% or less (and during the bubble, sometimes 0%), meaning leverage of 20× or more. At 20× leverage, a 27% price fall doesn't dent your equity — it obliterates it many times over, leaving millions in *negative equity* (owing more than the home was worth). Forced sales then fed more price declines in a spiral. The mechanism was nothing exotic: it was Engine 3, leverage, running symmetrically in reverse on a market that had convinced itself of Misconception #1. (The index, for the record, recovered to 308 by mid-2022 and ~330 by early 2026 — but only for those who weren't forced to sell at the bottom.)

### Vietnam's 2022–23 credit freeze

Real estate is a leveraged bet on credit, and Vietnam proved it in 2022. The trigger wasn't mortgages but *developer funding*: Vietnamese property developers had financed themselves heavily through corporate bonds. When the Tân Hoàng Minh chairman was arrested in April 2022 and the Vạn Thịnh Phát / SCB case broke in October 2022, the corporate-bond market *froze* — developers couldn't roll over their debt, projects stalled mid-construction, and the whole sector seized. The State Bank of Vietnam cut policy rates four times in 2023 and Decree 08 gave bond-restructuring relief to thaw it. The lesson maps straight onto this post: when the *credit* an asset class runs on disappears, the asset class stops functioning — no matter how much physical demand for housing still exists underneath.

### Japan's lost decades

For the longest-horizon counterexample to "property always wins," look to Japan. Tokyo land prices in the late 1980s reached such extremes that, by legend, the land under the Imperial Palace was "worth more than all of California." Prices peaked in 1990 and then fell for the better part of *two decades*, with many areas still below their 1990 peak even today. An entire generation of Japanese buyers learned that real estate, bought at a bubble peak with leverage, can take 20+ years to break even — or never. It's the ultimate cure for the belief that this asset class has a one-way ratchet.

### The global \$393 trillion picture, revisited

Step back to the scale we opened with. The reason all of these episodes matter — Vietnam's tripling, the US crash, Japan's lost decades — is that they happen inside the *largest* pool of wealth humans have ever assembled. When the \$393-trillion asset class wobbles, it dwarfs anything the \$126-trillion stock market or \$144-trillion bond market can do. The 2008 crisis became a *global financial crisis*, and not merely a housing recession, precisely because real estate is so large, so leveraged, and so woven into the banking system through mortgages. The size isn't a trivia fact — it's *why* property cycles are the master cycle that drags the rest of the economy with them, the thread we'll follow through the whole series.

## When this matters to you / Further reading

For almost everyone reading this, real estate isn't an abstract asset class — it's the biggest financial decision you will ever make, usually in the form of your own home. Understanding that a property's return is the *sum of four engines* changes how you make that decision: you stop asking the single naive question ("will the price go up?") and start asking the four real ones. *What income does it actually pay, net of costs? How much of my hoped-for return is risky appreciation versus durable rent? How much leverage am I taking, and can I survive a 20% price fall and a teaser-rate reset to 13%? And what will the tax and transaction friction really cost me on the way in and out?* A buyer who can answer those four is no longer reading a brochure — they're underwriting a deal.

The other practical takeaway is humility about the engine mix. In Vietnam today, the return on a city apartment is overwhelmingly an *appreciation* bet financed with *leverage*, with a thin *income* cushion and a light *tax* drag. That's a perfectly rational machine in a market with Vietnam's growth — but it's a machine with no airbag if appreciation stalls, exactly as it stalled in 2011–13 and 2022–23. Knowing *which* engine is carrying your return tells you precisely where you're exposed.
