> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Property earns a place beside stocks and bonds because it does three jobs at once — it pays *income*, it *hedges inflation* as a real asset, and it *diversifies* because its returns don't move in perfect lockstep with equities. But you pay for those jobs with an **illiquidity premium**: you get a little extra return precisely because you *can't* sell the asset quickly, and it arrives lumpy and leveraged.
>
> - The three allocation questions are simple to ask and hard to answer: **how much** property, **direct or REIT**, and **are you actually being paid** for the illiquidity you're taking on?
> - Real estate's correlation with stocks is **partial in normal times** (roughly 0.3–0.6 for listed property) but tends toward **1 in a crisis**, exactly when you wanted the diversification — so don't overpay for a hedge that vanishes when you need it.
> - The honest starting point for most Vietnamese households is brutal: a single leveraged primary home is often **~90% of net worth**. That isn't a diversified real-estate allocation — it's an undiversified, illiquid, debt-amplified single bet that happens to also be where you sleep.
> - The one number to remember: real estate is the **largest asset class on Earth — about \$393 trillion** — yet most people own it in the worst possible way, as one concentrated, leveraged building rather than a sized sleeve.

Let me tell you about two people who both "own real estate," and who could not be in more different financial positions.

The first is **Minh**, a thirty-four-year-old engineer in Ho Chi Minh City. In 2025 he bought a 70 m² apartment on the eastern edge of the city for about ₫7.0 billion (≈ \$270,000), putting down ₫1.5 billion of savings and borrowing the other ₫5.5 billion on a 20-year mortgage at a teaser rate that will reset to 13–14%. He is proud of it, and he should be — it's a real achievement. But if you draw up his personal balance sheet, something jumps out: that one apartment, net of the mortgage, is about **90% of everything he owns**. His emergency cash, a little gold his mother gave him, a tiny brokerage account — that's the other 10%. Minh thinks of himself as a homeowner. Financially, he is a man who has bet almost his entire net worth on one leveraged building in one neighborhood of one city.

The second is **Dana**, a US investor in her forties. She owns a home too, but it's a smaller share of her wealth — maybe 40%. The rest is spread across a diversified portfolio: a big slug of stocks, a meaningful allocation to bonds, a cash buffer, and — relevant to us — a **real-estate sleeve** that she holds mostly through REITs (real estate investment trusts, which we'll define from scratch). Dana also "owns real estate." But for her, property is one *sized* ingredient in a recipe, not the entire meal.

This post is about the difference between those two people. We're stepping back from any single building — from cap rates and mortgages and leases — to ask the portfolio question: *how should property sit alongside stocks and bonds at all?* What jobs does it do that the others can't? What does it cost you that they don't? And how much should you actually hold? The diagram below is the mental model for the whole piece — the four asset classes and the handful of attributes on which they genuinely differ.

We'll build everything from zero — what a portfolio even is, what correlation means, why an "illiquidity premium" exists — and ground every idea in numbers, with Minh and Dana running through the whole thing. A quick note before we start: this is educational, not financial advice. We're explaining how the pieces fit together and where the model bites, not telling you to buy or sell anything.

## Foundations: why hold property in a portfolio at all

Before we can decide how much real estate to hold, we need a shared vocabulary. If you already know what correlation and the illiquidity premium are, skim this — but don't skip it, because the rest of the post leans on these definitions precisely.

### What a portfolio is, and why we hold more than one thing

A **portfolio** is just the collection of everything you own that's meant to build or store wealth: your stocks, your bonds, your cash, your gold, your property. The reason we hold *more than one thing* is the oldest idea in investing, and it's worth stating plainly: **different assets do well at different times**, so a mix is steadier than any single bet. When stocks crash, bonds often rise. When inflation eats cash, real assets often hold. By holding several things that don't all sink together, you get a smoother ride for the same average return — or a higher average return for the same bumpiness. That's the entire game.

The thing we're optimizing isn't just return; it's **return per unit of risk**, where "risk" in finance usually means *volatility* — how much your wealth bounces around from year to year. An asset that returns 8% with wild swings can be *worse* in a portfolio than one returning 6% smoothly, because the smooth one lets you sleep, stay invested, and not panic-sell at the bottom. Real estate's pitch to a portfolio is exactly this: it offers a return profile that doesn't perfectly overlap with stocks or bonds, so adding a slice can improve the whole mix.

### Real assets vs financial assets (a recap)

Assets come in two broad families. A **financial asset** is a *claim* — a piece of paper (or a database entry) that entitles you to someone else's future cash. A bond is a claim on a borrower's repayments; a stock is a claim on a company's future profits; cash is a claim on the central bank. A **real asset** is a physical thing with intrinsic use: land, a building, a barrel of oil, an ounce of gold. The distinction matters enormously for one reason we'll lean on hard: when the *value of money itself* falls — that's inflation — financial claims denominated in money tend to lose real value, while real assets, which are priced in whatever money is worth at the time, tend to hold. Property is the largest real asset most people will ever touch.

### Income vs appreciation: total return = income + capital gain

Every investment can pay you in two ways, and keeping them separate is essential to thinking clearly about property.

The first is **income** — the cash the asset throws off while you hold it. For a building, that's **rent** (net of costs). For a stock, it's the **dividend**. For a bond, it's the **coupon**. The second is **appreciation** (or capital gain) — the change in the asset's *price* between when you buy and when you sell. Put them together and you get **total return**:

$$\text{Total return} = \underbrace{\text{Income}}_{\text{rent / dividend / coupon}} + \underbrace{\text{Appreciation}}_{\text{price change}}$$

This decomposition is the cleanest way to compare property with stocks. A building might give you a 4% rental yield plus 5% appreciation for a 9% total return; a stock might give you a 2% dividend plus 7% appreciation for the same 9%. Same total, very different *shape* — and the shape is what matters in a portfolio, because income is steadier and more predictable than price.

### Correlation and diversification: the math of "don't move together"

Here's the single most important concept in the whole post, so let's build it carefully with no jargon assumed.

**Correlation** is a number between −1 and +1 that measures how two assets move *relative to each other*. If two assets have a correlation of **+1**, they move in perfect lockstep — when one goes up 10%, the other goes up 10% too. A correlation of **−1** means they move in perfect opposition — one up 10%, the other down 10%. A correlation of **0** means they're unrelated — knowing what one did tells you nothing about the other.

Diversification — the free lunch of investing — works *because* correlations are below 1. When you combine two assets that don't move in perfect lockstep, the bumps partly cancel out. The portfolio's volatility is *lower* than the average of the two assets' volatilities, because when one zigs, the other sometimes zags. The lower the correlation, the bigger the benefit. Two assets at +1 give you no diversification at all (they're effectively the same asset). Two assets at 0 give you a real reduction in bumpiness. Two assets at −1 could, in theory, be combined into something almost riskless.

Real estate's whole claim to a portfolio seat rests on this: its correlation with stocks is **meaningfully below 1**. It's not 0 — property and equities both depend on the economy, jobs, and interest rates, so they lean the same way. But it's far enough below 1 that mixing them smooths the ride. We'll spend a whole section on exactly how far below 1, and on the cruel fact that the correlation rises toward 1 in a crisis.

### The inflation hedge

An **inflation hedge** is an asset whose value tends to rise *with* the general price level, so it protects your purchasing power when money is losing value. We touched on this under real-vs-financial assets; the mechanism for property is concrete. Rents tend to rise with inflation (landlords re-price leases; in many markets they're explicitly indexed). Building costs rise with inflation (replacing a structure costs more, which supports the value of existing ones). And land, being finite, tends to hold real value over long stretches. So property's income *and* its capital value both tend to track the price level, which is exactly what an inflation hedge is supposed to do. Cash and conventional bonds, by contrast, are fixed in nominal money — inflation is their natural enemy.

### Liquidity, lumpiness, and the illiquidity premium

**Liquidity** is how quickly and cheaply you can turn an asset into cash at a fair price. A listed stock is extremely liquid — you can sell it in seconds for pennies of cost. A building is the opposite: selling takes *months*, costs a chunk in agent fees and transfer taxes, and the price you get depends on whether a buyer happens to be around. Direct property is one of the **least liquid** assets ordinary people own.

Closely related is **lumpiness** (or **indivisibility**): property comes in big, indivisible units. You can buy one share of a company for a few dollars, or sell exactly \$1,000 worth. You cannot buy 3% of an apartment, and you certainly can't sell the kitchen to raise cash. A building is an all-or-nothing chunk. This matters more than it sounds, and it has its own section later.

Now the punchline, the **illiquidity premium**: because illiquid assets are harder to sell, investors demand a *higher expected return* to hold them. If two assets offered the same return but one could be sold instantly and the other took a year, everyone would prefer the liquid one — so its price would be bid up and its return down, until the illiquid one offered *enough extra return* to compensate. That extra return is the illiquidity premium. It is real, it is one of the genuine reasons to hold direct property — and, as we'll see, it is also the single most over-claimed and misunderstood number in real-estate investing.

### Direct property vs REITs (a recap)

There are two fundamentally different ways to own income real estate, and the choice is central to the allocation question. **Direct property** means you own the actual building — you collect the rent, set the leverage, handle the management, and bear the illiquidity. A **REIT** (real estate investment trust) is a company that owns a portfolio of buildings and divides ownership into *shares* you can buy on a stock exchange; it's liquid and divisible, professionally managed, and (in exchange for a tax break) it must pay out most of its income as dividends. We covered REITs in depth in [REITs: owning real estate like a stock](/blog/trading/real-estate/reits-owning-property-like-a-stock); here we only need the trade-off they represent inside a portfolio, which gets its own section.

### Why a primary home is a strange hybrid

One last foundation, and it's the one most people get wrong. **A primary home is both a consumption good and an investment** — and that dual nature makes it a uniquely awkward "asset." When you live in a home you own, you are simultaneously (a) consuming housing services (you'd otherwise pay rent for them) and (b) holding a large, leveraged, undiversified, illiquid real-estate position. The consumption part is fine and even wise. The investment part is the one that quietly turns into the over-concentration problem at the heart of this post. We'll return to Minh's 90% repeatedly, because it's the most important number in most readers' financial lives.

With the vocabulary in place, let's look at what property actually *does* for a portfolio.

## The three jobs: income, inflation hedge, diversification

Property earns its seat at the table by doing three jobs. None of them is unique to real estate, but the *combination* — steady income, inflation protection, and imperfect correlation with stocks, all in one asset — is hard to replicate. Let's take them one at a time, with numbers.

### Job one: income

The first and most reliable job property does is pay you **rent**. Unlike a stock's dividend, which a company can cut at will, rent is contractual — a tenant has signed a lease — and it tends to be sticky and predictable. A stabilized, well-located income property in many markets throws off a **net rental yield** (rent after operating costs, as a percent of value) of roughly 3–6%, depending on the market and property type. That cash arrives whether or not the building's *price* is up or down that year, which is precisely what makes income valuable in a portfolio: it's the part of your return you can count on while prices do whatever they do.

#### Worked example: property's total-return decomposition vs equities

Let's make the income-plus-appreciation split concrete and put property next to a stock.

Dana buys a small US rental property for **\$300,000**. In year one it produces **\$15,000** of rent after operating costs (taxes, insurance, maintenance, vacancy) — a **5% net rental yield** (\$15,000 ÷ \$300,000). Over the year the property's value also rises to **\$312,000**, a **4% appreciation**. Her total return is:

$$\underbrace{\$15{,}000}_{\text{income, } 5\%} + \underbrace{\$12{,}000}_{\text{appreciation, } 4\%} = \$27{,}000 = 9\% \text{ total return}$$

Now compare a stock index fund. Dana puts the same **\$300,000** into equities. The index pays a **2% dividend** (\$6,000) and rises **7%** in price (\$21,000), for the same **\$27,000 = 9% total return**. Identical total — but look at the *shape*. The property delivered **\$15,000 of its \$27,000 (56%) as cash income**; the stock delivered only **\$6,000 (22%)** as cash, with most of its return riding on the price move. If markets fall the next year, the property's price might drop but the **rent keeps coming**; the stock's dividend is smaller and its return was mostly the volatile price piece.

*Same total return, but property pays you more of it in cash you can count on — and that steadiness is worth real money in a portfolio that has to survive bad years.*

### Job two: the inflation hedge

The second job is protecting your purchasing power. Because property is a **real asset** — rents re-price upward, replacement costs rise, land is finite — its value tends to track the price level over long horizons. This is the job cash and conventional bonds simply cannot do: they're fixed in nominal money, so inflation erodes them by construction.

![A before and after comparison showing cash held through a decade of inflation losing about forty percent of its purchasing power while property, a real asset, roughly keeps its real value as rents and prices rise](/imgs/blogs/real-estate-in-a-portfolio-3.png)

The figure above is the inflation-hedge picture, and the worked example makes it numerical.

#### Worked example: the inflation hedge — real value held vs cash eroded

Suppose inflation runs at **5% per year for ten years** — not extreme; Vietnam saw CPI near 18% in 2011, and even calm economies have decade-long stretches well above 2%. Compounding 5% over ten years multiplies the price level by $1.05^{10} \approx 1.63$. So **a basket of goods that costs ₫100 today costs ₫163 in ten years.**

Minh's friend keeps ₫1 billion (≈ \$38,600) in cash under the proverbial mattress for that decade. The nominal number is unchanged: still **₫1 billion**. But in real terms it now buys only ₫1bn ÷ 1.63 ≈ **₫613 million of the old basket** — he has quietly lost **~39% of his purchasing power** without a single dramatic event. The money didn't fall; the world got more expensive around it.

Now suppose instead he held **property** worth ₫1 billion. If rents and the building's value roughly track inflation — rising about 5% a year — the property is worth about **₫1.63 billion** in ten years, and it still buys roughly **₫1 billion of the old basket**: real value held. (And that's before counting the rent it paid along the way.) The cash investor lost two-fifths of his purchasing power; the property investor kept his.

*Inflation is a silent tax on money, and real assets like property are how you opt out of paying it — which is a big part of why property feels "safe" to households living through inflation.*

A caveat worth stating, because honesty is the brand: property is a *good* long-run inflation hedge, not a perfect or instantaneous one. In the short run, if a central bank fights inflation by hiking interest rates hard, property prices can *fall* even as prices rise, because higher mortgage rates crush demand and higher discount rates lower valuations. The US in 2022 is the clean example — inflation was high *and* home-price growth stalled as the 30-year mortgage rate rocketed from 2.65% (Jan 2021) to over 7% (Oct 2022). The inflation hedge shows up over years and decades, not quarters. Rates are the master variable; we cover that link in [interest rates and mortgages](/blog/trading/real-estate/interest-rates-and-mortgages-the-master-variable).

### Job three: diversification

The third job — the one that's really a *portfolio* job rather than a property job — is **diversification**. Because real-estate returns don't move in perfect lockstep with stocks, adding a real-estate sleeve to a stock-and-bond portfolio reduces the whole thing's bumpiness without necessarily reducing its return. This is the entire reason a sophisticated allocator like a pension fund or an endowment holds 10–20% in real estate: not because property always beats stocks (it doesn't), but because it *zigs when stocks zag* often enough to smooth the ride.

But "often enough" is doing heavy lifting in that sentence, and it's so important — and so widely misunderstood — that it gets its own section.

## Correlation with stocks and bonds: the diversification reality

Here's the diversification job examined honestly. The pitch is real, but it comes with a cruel asterisk, and a portfolio built on the pitch without the asterisk will fail at the worst possible moment.

![An illustrative chart of two indexed return paths over ten years, with property rising smoothly and equities rising higher but with bigger swings, and a shaded crisis year in which both fall together, showing that their correlation rises toward one exactly when diversification is needed most](/imgs/blogs/real-estate-in-a-portfolio-2.png)

The figure above is the whole story in one picture: most of the time, property (the smoother blue line) and equities (the choppier amber line) wander somewhat independently — property holds while stocks wobble, giving you the diversification benefit. But in the shaded crisis year, **both fall together**. The correlation, which was a comfortable 0.4 in calm times, snapped toward 1 exactly when you were counting on it to be low.

### What the correlation actually is

Empirically, **listed real estate (REITs) has a long-run correlation with broad equities of roughly 0.5–0.7** — meaningfully below 1, so it diversifies, but high enough that REITs are clearly a "risk asset" that leans with the stock market. **Direct (private) real estate appears to have a *much lower* correlation — often quoted at 0.1–0.3** — but this number is partly an illusion, for a reason that's crucial: private property is **appraised, not traded**. Its "price" comes from periodic valuations that lag and smooth reality, so the measured volatility and correlation look artificially low. The buildings are bouncing around as much as anything; you just can't see it on a screen, so the statistics flatter direct property. This is called **appraisal smoothing**, and it's why "direct property has almost no correlation with stocks" is half true and half a measurement artifact.

Real estate's correlation with **bonds** is different again. Property values are highly sensitive to interest rates (higher rates → higher cap rates → lower values), so property and bonds share a common rate driver — which can make them move *together* when rates are the story (both fall when rates spike, as in 2022) and *apart* when growth is the story. The cross-asset version of this — how property, REITs, stocks and bonds interrelate through income, leverage and rates — is the subject of [real estate, REITs, income, leverage, and rates](/blog/trading/cross-asset/real-estate-reits-income-leverage-and-rates).

#### Worked example: the diversification benefit of two assets that don't move together

Let's prove that diversification is real with the simplest possible numbers, because the result is genuinely surprising the first time you see it.

Dana holds two assets, each with an expected return of **8%** and the same volatility — say each one, on its own, swings by about **±15%** in a typical year. Asset A is a stock fund; asset B is a real-estate sleeve. She puts **50% in each**.

If the two were perfectly correlated (+1), the blend would have the same ±15% volatility as either one alone — no benefit. But suppose their correlation is **0.4** (a reasonable figure for REITs vs stocks). The volatility of a 50/50 blend isn't the average of the two; it's given by

$$\sigma_{\text{blend}} = \sqrt{0.5^2 \sigma_A^2 + 0.5^2 \sigma_B^2 + 2(0.5)(0.5)\rho\,\sigma_A\sigma_B}$$

where $\sigma_A = \sigma_B = 15\%$ and $\rho = 0.4$ is the correlation. Plugging in: $\sqrt{0.25(225) + 0.25(225) + 2(0.25)(0.4)(225)} = \sqrt{56.25 + 56.25 + 45} = \sqrt{157.5} \approx \textbf{12.5\%}$.

So Dana's blended portfolio still earns its **8% expected return**, but its volatility drops from 15% to about **12.5%** — a **one-sixth reduction in bumpiness for free**, purely because the two assets don't move in perfect lockstep. If the correlation were 0, the blend volatility would fall further, to $\sqrt{0.25(225)+0.25(225)} \approx 10.6\%$. The lower the correlation, the bigger the free lunch.

*Diversification is the only free lunch in finance, and it's free precisely because the assets don't all move together — which is exactly why a crisis that makes them move together is so dangerous.*

### When correlation goes to 1

The asterisk: **correlations rise in a crisis.** In a genuine panic — 2008, March 2020 — investors sell *everything* liquid to raise cash, and forced sellers don't care about asset class. Stocks fall, REITs fall, credit falls, even gold sometimes falls in the first dash for cash. The diversification you carefully built evaporates exactly when you needed it, because the thing driving prices is no longer the differing fundamentals of stocks vs property — it's a common, system-wide scramble for liquidity. REITs fell roughly as hard as stocks in 2008 and in March 2020. This is the single most important thing to understand about diversification: **it works in normal times and fails in crises**, which is the opposite of what you'd want from insurance.

The practical lesson isn't "diversification is useless" — it dramatically smooths the ordinary ups and downs that make up most of investing life. The lesson is *don't pay a premium for a crisis hedge that property doesn't actually provide*, and don't be shocked when your real-estate sleeve falls alongside your stocks in the next panic. Property diversifies the weather, not the earthquakes.

## The illiquidity premium: what you're paid to lock up capital

Now to the most distinctive — and most abused — concept in real-estate investing. Why would a rational investor accept an asset they *can't sell quickly*? Because they get paid to.

### Why the premium exists

Liquidity has value. The ability to turn an asset into cash *right now*, at a fair price, is worth something — it lets you handle emergencies, seize opportunities, and rebalance. An asset that takes you away that ability has to compensate you, or no one would hold it instead of the liquid alternative. So in equilibrium, an illiquid asset must offer a **higher expected return** than an otherwise-identical liquid one. That extra return is the **illiquidity premium** — the rent the market pays you for locking up your capital.

For real estate, this is one of the genuine, defensible reasons to own buildings directly rather than only through liquid REITs. You are being paid, in expected return, for accepting that you can't sell next Tuesday. Endowments and pension funds — which have very long horizons and don't need to sell — deliberately harvest this premium by tilting toward illiquid assets (private real estate, private equity, infrastructure) that a retail investor needing liquidity can't comfortably hold. It's one of the few edges a patient, long-horizon investor genuinely has.

#### Worked example: the illiquidity premium — extra yield for locking up capital

Let's size the premium with round numbers so it stops being abstract.

Dana is choosing between two ways to get real-estate exposure. **Option A** is a liquid REIT that she expects to return **7% per year** total. **Option B** is a direct private property — same underlying buildings, same kind of cash flow — but she'll have to commit her capital for years and accept that selling would take months and cost ~8% in transaction friction. For taking that on, the private deal is priced to offer an expected **9% per year** — **2 percentage points more**.

That **2% per year is the illiquidity premium** in this example. On a \$300,000 investment, it's an extra **\$6,000 a year** — Dana's paycheck for giving up liquidity. Over a 10-year horizon, compounding that 2% gap is large: \$300,000 at 7% grows to about **\$590,000**, while at 9% it grows to about **\$710,000** — a **\$120,000 difference** purely from harvesting the premium (before any of the offsetting risks).

But — and this is the honest part — the premium is **not free money**. Dana only truly *captures* it if she never has to sell during a bad stretch. If a personal emergency forces her to liquidate the private property in year three of a downturn, she'll eat that 8% transaction cost *and* sell into a weak market, easily wiping out years of premium. The premium compensates you for a real risk: the risk that you'll need liquidity exactly when it's most expensive to get. You earn it only if you can genuinely afford to lock the money up.

*The illiquidity premium is real, but it's a wage for patience you must actually have — if you might need the cash, you're not being overpaid, you're being set up to sell at the worst time.*

### How big is it really, and is it even there?

Honesty demands a hard question: **how much of the apparent illiquidity premium is real, and how much is appraisal smoothing in disguise?** Recall that private property is valued by appraisal, not by trade. Appraisals lag and smooth, so private real estate *looks* less volatile and less correlated than it truly is. Part of the "premium" private real estate appears to earn is really compensation for **risk you can't see on the statement** rather than a clean payment for illiquidity. When private real estate is finally marked to a real transaction — a forced sale, a fund liquidation — the gap between the smoothed appraisal and the true clearing price can be brutal (this is a recurring story in distressed commercial property cycles).

So the sober view is: the illiquidity premium is **probably real but modest** — historically argued to be in the low single digits of percent per year for genuinely illiquid private assets — and a chunk of what naive comparisons attribute to it is actually hidden volatility that the appraisal process papered over. Believe in the premium, but discount the headline number, and never assume you're being handsomely paid just because the screen looks calm.

## Direct vs REIT in a portfolio: the liquidity/return trade

Given the three jobs and the illiquidity premium, how should you actually *hold* real estate — as buildings you own, or as REIT shares? This is one of the two big allocation decisions, and it's a genuine trade-off with no universal right answer.

![A comparison matrix of direct property versus a listed REIT across liquidity, divisibility, volatility, the illiquidity premium and control, showing that a REIT gives liquidity and divisibility but surrenders the premium and control while direct property does the reverse](/imgs/blogs/real-estate-in-a-portfolio-4.png)

The figure lays out the trade in five dimensions. Let's walk them.

**Liquidity.** A REIT sells in seconds on an exchange; direct property takes months and meaningful cost. If you might need the money, REITs win decisively.

**Divisibility.** A REIT lets you invest ₫1 million or ₫1 billion and own a *fraction* of hundreds of buildings; direct property is one indivisible chunk. For most people building a position gradually, divisibility is a huge practical advantage — it's how you actually achieve diversification *within* real estate.

**Volatility (as you experience it).** Here the trade reverses. REITs are *marked to market every second*, so you see — and feel — stock-like swings; in 2022, listed REIT prices fell roughly a quarter on rising rates while the actual buildings barely moved. Direct property's appraised value is smooth, so it *feels* calmer even though the underlying economics are similar. Some investors value that smoothness genuinely (it stops them panic-selling); others recognize it as an illusion. Either way, it's a real difference in lived experience.

**The illiquidity premium.** Direct property can capture it; a liquid REIT, by being liquid, largely gives it up. If harvesting the premium is your goal, you need the illiquid form — but only if you can afford the lock-up.

**Control.** Direct ownership lets *you* set the leverage, pick the property, and time the sale. A REIT hands all of that to professional managers, who choose the leverage and the buildings for you. More control means more responsibility and more concentration risk; less control means you're trusting a management team but getting instant diversification.

The honest synthesis: for **most individual investors**, a REIT is the better default for the real-estate *sleeve* — liquid, divisible, diversified, professionally managed — and you accept stock-like volatility and the loss of the illiquidity premium as the price. **Direct property makes sense** when you want maximum personal control and leverage on a specific asset, you can genuinely afford the illiquidity (long horizon, no chance of a forced sale), and you want to harvest the premium. Many sophisticated allocators hold *both* — REITs for liquidity and rebalancing, direct property for the premium and control. And in **Vietnam specifically**, the REIT option barely exists yet (essentially one listed REIT and a few property funds), so the practical choice for a Vietnamese investor today is direct property at home or REITs abroad — a real constraint, not a preference.

## How much to hold: sizing, and the over-concentrated household

Now the first and biggest allocation question: **how much** real estate? The answer depends on who's asking, and for most readers the honest answer starts by pointing out that you almost certainly already hold *too much* — just not in the form you think.

### The sophisticated allocator's answer

A large, diversified institution — a pension fund, an endowment, a sovereign fund — typically holds **10–20% in real estate** as part of a broader "real assets" bucket. The logic is exactly the three jobs: income, inflation hedge, and diversification, sized so the illiquid sleeve is a *slice* and not the whole pie. The classic starting point most people have heard of is the **60/40 portfolio** — 60% stocks, 40% bonds — and the modern evolution is to carve out a real-assets sleeve, giving something like **50% stocks, 25% bonds, 15% real estate, 10% cash**.

![A nested stack showing a diversified portfolio with equities as the largest sleeve, then bonds, then a real-estate sleeve of about fifteen percent holding REITs and direct property, then a cash buffer, illustrating that property is one sized ingredient rather than the whole portfolio](/imgs/blogs/real-estate-in-a-portfolio-5.png)

The figure shows where the property sleeve sits: a meaningful slice, sized so that the part you *can't* easily rebalance stays bounded. The exact numbers aren't sacred — a younger investor might hold more equities, a retiree more bonds — but the *principle* is: real estate is a sleeve, not the structure.

### The household's actual answer: you're already 90% in

Here's where theory meets most readers' reality with a thud. The tidy "15% in real estate" assumes you're starting from cash and choosing. **Most households aren't.** They already own a primary home, usually leveraged with a mortgage, and that single asset typically dwarfs everything else they own. For a Vietnamese household that has stretched to buy in HCMC or Hanoi — where price-to-income ratios run around **30+ years of income**, among the highest in the world — the home isn't 15% of net worth. It's often **80–95%**.

![A before and after comparison showing an over-concentrated household with a leveraged primary home at about ninety percent of net worth on the left, versus a balanced household on the right with the home at about forty percent and the rest in liquid diversified stocks, bonds and cash](/imgs/blogs/real-estate-in-a-portfolio-6.png)

The figure contrasts Minh's situation with a balanced one, and the worked example puts numbers on it.

#### Worked example: a VN household 90% concentrated in one home

Let's draw Minh's real balance sheet. He bought the ₫7.0 billion (≈ \$270,000) apartment with **₫1.5 billion of his own money** and a **₫5.5 billion mortgage**. His other savings — emergency cash, a little gold, a small brokerage account — total about **₫600 million**.

Right after purchase, his **net worth** is: home value ₫7.0bn − mortgage ₫5.5bn = **₫1.5 billion of home equity**, plus ₫600 million of other assets = **₫2.1 billion total**. So his home equity is ₫1.5bn ÷ ₫2.1bn ≈ **71% of net worth** — already high. But that understates the *exposure*, because the home is **leveraged**. Minh controls a ₫7.0 billion asset on ₫1.5 billion of equity — leverage of about **4.7×**. A mere **10% fall** in the apartment's price (₫700 million) wipes out **47% of his net worth**; a **21% fall** would wipe out *all* his home equity and leave him owing more than the flat is worth. Meanwhile, if you measure concentration by *gross asset exposure* rather than net equity, the ₫7.0bn home is **92%** of his ₫7.6bn in total assets. By any honest measure, Minh is **~90% concentrated in one leveraged building**.

Now compare a **balanced** version of Minh. Same ₫2.1 billion net worth, but split: a home worth ₫4.0 billion against a ₫2.5 billion mortgage (₫1.5bn equity, ~40% of net worth), and ₫600 million spread across liquid stocks, bonds and cash. Same shelter, far less single-asset risk, and — crucially — assets he can actually *rebalance* and *sell* if life changes.

*For most Vietnamese households the real-estate allocation question isn't "should I add property" — it's "I am already dangerously over-allocated to one leveraged building, so the honest first move is not to add more."*

The deepest point of this whole post lives in that example. When a financial advisor abroad says "you should hold 15% in real estate," they're talking to someone whose home is a modest slice of a diversified sheet. Said to Minh, the same advice is **exactly backwards** — he doesn't need *more* property; he needs *everything else*. Recognizing that your primary home is already a massive, leveraged, undiversified real-estate position is the single most valuable idea here, and it's covered from the shelter-vs-asset angle in [housing as a financial asset vs shelter](/blog/trading/real-estate/housing-as-financial-asset-vs-shelter) and from the debt-amplification angle in [leverage and the mortgage](/blog/trading/real-estate/leverage-and-the-mortgage-how-debt-amplifies-property).

### So how much *should* you add?

Given all this, a sane sizing framework for an individual is: **count your home as your real-estate allocation first.** If your primary home is already 60–90% of net worth (the common case), your real-estate allocation is *maxed out* — adding REITs or a second property only deepens the concentration. Your job for years is to build *everything else* — stocks, bonds, cash — until the home shrinks to a reasonable share of a growing pie. Only once your home is a *minority* of net worth does the textbook "add a 10–20% real-estate sleeve" question even apply, and then a liquid REIT is usually the cleaner way to do it than a second building.

## Rebalancing an illiquid asset: you can't trim a wing of a house

Suppose you've built a balanced portfolio. Over time, the pieces drift: stocks soar and become 70% of the pie when you wanted 50%; property lags and shrinks. **Rebalancing** is selling what's grown and buying what's lagged to restore your target mix — and it's one of the quiet engines of long-run returns, because it systematically makes you sell high and buy low. But real estate's lumpiness and illiquidity make it the hardest sleeve to rebalance, and that constraint should shape how you hold it.

### Why direct property breaks rebalancing

The problem is indivisibility. If your stocks have grown and you want to trim 5% back into property, you can sell exactly 5% of your stock fund in one click. But you **cannot buy 5% of a building** — the smallest unit is a whole property worth hundreds of thousands of dollars. And you certainly can't *sell* 5% of a building you own; you can't sell the kitchen, or one wing, or the third floor. Direct property is **all-or-nothing**, so it simply doesn't participate in normal rebalancing. When your property sleeve drifts out of target, your only tools are blunt: buy a whole additional property (a giant, lumpy, leveraged commitment) or sell the whole thing (months, taxes, a market-timing bet).

#### Worked example: why you can't rebalance a lumpy, illiquid sleeve

Dana targets **15% real estate**. She holds it as a single **\$300,000** rental property inside a **\$2,000,000** portfolio — exactly 15%. Then stocks have a great two years and her portfolio grows to **\$2,600,000**, while the property is flat at \$300,000. Now property is only **\$300,000 ÷ \$2,600,000 ≈ 11.5%** of the pie — below target. To rebalance *up* to 15%, she'd need about **\$390,000** in property, i.e. **\$90,000 more**.

But she can't buy \$90,000 of *that building* — there's no such thing. Her real options are ugly: buy a **whole second property** (a ~\$300,000+ lumpy, probably-leveraged commitment that overshoots wildly), or do nothing and let the allocation drift. If instead she'd held the sleeve as a **REIT**, restoring the target would be trivial: buy \$90,000 of REIT shares in one click, done. The lumpiness of direct property didn't just make it illiquid to *exit* — it made it impossible to *fine-tune*.

*An illiquid, indivisible sleeve quietly opts out of rebalancing, which means it slowly takes over or fades from your portfolio on its own schedule, not yours — a hidden cost of holding real estate as whole buildings.*

### How to rebalance around an illiquid sleeve

Practitioners who hold illiquid assets solve this in three ways. **First, hold the rebalanceable part in liquid form** — use REITs for the slice you intend to adjust, and keep any direct property as a deliberate, set-and-forget long-term holding you don't expect to trim. **Second, rebalance with the *liquid* sleeves** — when property drifts low, you can't easily add to it, but you *can* let new savings and dividends flow toward it, and you can rebalance your stocks-vs-bonds split freely around the fixed property block. **Third, use cash flows rather than sales** — direct the rent, dividends, and fresh savings to whichever sleeve is below target, so you rebalance by *where new money goes* instead of by selling the illiquid asset. The cash sleeve in our sample allocation isn't idle; it's the **dry powder** that makes rebalancing possible when one of your assets can't be trimmed.

## Common misconceptions

Real estate accumulates more portfolio folklore than almost any asset, partly because everyone has a home and an opinion. Here are the beliefs that cause the most damage, each named and corrected.

**"Real estate is uncorrelated with stocks, so it's a great diversifier in a crash."** Half true, and the dangerous half is the part people remember. In *normal* times, property's correlation with stocks is genuinely below 1, so it does smooth ordinary volatility. But in a *crisis* — the one time you'd want a hedge — correlations rise toward 1: REITs fell roughly as hard as stocks in 2008 and March 2020, and private property eventually marked down too. The low correlation you see in calm data is also partly an appraisal-smoothing artifact for private property. Property diversifies the weather, not the earthquake.

**"More real estate is always more diversification."** No — *diversification is about how many uncorrelated bets you hold, not how much of one asset you pile on.* Adding a second apartment in the same city to a portfolio that's already a leveraged home isn't diversifying; it's *concentrating* — doubling down on the same local economy, the same interest-rate sensitivity, the same illiquidity. True diversification within real estate means different property types (residential vs commercial vs logistics), different geographies, and different vehicles (direct vs REIT) — which for most individuals is far easier to achieve through REITs than through buying more buildings.

**"The illiquidity premium is free money — just lock it up and collect."** It's not free; it's a *wage for patience you must actually be able to afford.* You only capture the premium if you never have to sell during a bad stretch. If an emergency forces you to liquidate an illiquid asset into a weak market, you'll pay heavy transaction costs and sell low, easily erasing years of premium. And part of the apparent premium is just hidden volatility that appraisal smoothing concealed — risk you bore without seeing, not a reward for cleverness. Believe in the premium; respect what it's compensating you for.

**"My home is my best investment."** Your home is primarily a *consumption good* — shelter you'd otherwise rent — wearing an investment costume. Treated as an investment, an owner-occupied home is a strange one: undiversified (one building), illiquid (you live in it; you can't sell a room), leveraged (the mortgage amplifies every move), and carrying real costs (maintenance, taxes, transaction friction) that the "it went up 50%!" story conveniently ignores. It may turn out fine, but calling it your *best* investment usually means you've never honestly measured the alternative of renting and investing the difference — see [rent vs buy: the real math](/blog/trading/real-estate/rent-vs-buy-the-real-math).

**"Property only ever goes up, so concentration is safe."** Property *can* and *does* fall — US national home prices dropped about **27% peak-to-trough** from 2006 to 2012; Japan's land values fell for *two decades* after 1990; Vietnam's market froze hard in 2011–13 and again in 2022–23. Leverage turns those drops into wipeouts: Minh's 4.7× leverage means a 21% price fall erases his entire equity. "It always goes up" is the belief that makes over-concentration feel safe right up until the cycle turns. We trace these in [the anatomy of a property bubble and crash](/blog/trading/real-estate/anatomy-of-a-property-bubble-and-crash).

**"REITs aren't 'real' real estate because they're just stocks."** REITs trade *like* stocks in the short run — they're volatile, marked-to-market, and move with the market's mood — but in the long run they track the buildings they own and the rent those buildings produce. A REIT *is* real estate; it's just real estate wrapped in a liquid, divisible share. Dismissing REITs because of their short-run volatility throws away the one form of property most individuals can actually hold in a diversified, rebalanceable way.

## How it shows up in real markets

Let's ground all of this in concrete, real episodes and structures — how the portfolio role of real estate plays out in actual markets, in Vietnam and globally.

### The 60/40 portfolio and the rise of "real assets"

The **60/40 portfolio** (60% stocks, 40% bonds) was the default institutional and retail allocation for decades. Its weakness is inflation: in a 1970s-style or 2022-style inflation shock, *both* stocks and conventional bonds can fall together, because bonds are fixed in nominal money and stocks get repriced by higher rates. That shared vulnerability is why large allocators evolved toward holding a **real-assets sleeve** — real estate, infrastructure, commodities, inflation-linked bonds — specifically to do the job 60/40 can't: protect purchasing power when money is losing value. The 2022 experience, when a standard 60/40 had one of its worst years in modern history as stocks *and* bonds fell together, was a live demonstration of why the real-estate and real-asset sleeve exists. Property's role in the modern portfolio is essentially "the inflation insurance that also pays income."

### Vietnam's households: a nation over-allocated to one asset

Vietnam is, at the household level, one of the most real-estate-concentrated societies on Earth — and it's instructive precisely because it's an extreme case of the over-concentration problem. With HCMC and Hanoi price-to-income ratios around **30+ years** (HCMC was cited at 32–34 years, fifth-highest globally), buying a home consumes a household's entire savings capacity and then some, leaving the home as **80–95% of net worth** for a generation of buyers. Cultural preferences reinforce it: land and property are seen as *the* store of value, partly because for decades the alternatives (a deep stock market, broad bond access, REITs) barely existed. The result is a country where the median household's financial fate is tied almost entirely to one leveraged building — and where a property downturn (2011–13, 2022–23) hits household balance sheets with full, undiversified, leveraged force. The portfolio lesson Vietnam teaches is the one Minh embodies: *the problem isn't too little real estate, it's a financial life that is nothing but real estate.*

### 2022: REITs as the canary, private property as the laggard

The 2022 rate shock is the cleanest recent demonstration of the direct-vs-REIT volatility difference. As the US 30-year mortgage rate rocketed past 7% and bond yields jumped, **listed REIT prices fell about 25%** within the year — instantly, visibly, because they're marked to market every second. Meanwhile **private (appraised) real estate barely moved on paper** for many quarters, because its valuations lag and smooth. Same rate shock, same underlying buildings, wildly different reported experience — REITs were the canary that priced the bad news immediately, while private property's appraisals caught up only slowly (and in some commercial sectors, painfully, over the following years). For a portfolio, the lesson is twofold: REITs give you honest, immediate (if uncomfortable) marks, while private property's calm is partly an accounting illusion you'll eventually pay for if forced to sell.

### Endowments harvesting the illiquidity premium

The big US university endowments (the "Yale model") built their reputation partly by deliberately tilting toward **illiquid assets** — private real estate, private equity, venture, infrastructure — to harvest the illiquidity premium that their permanent, never-needs-to-sell capital lets them safely capture. It worked well for decades because the endowments genuinely had the one thing the premium requires: the ability to *never be a forced seller*. But the model's vulnerability showed in 2008–09, when even some endowments faced liquidity strains (commitments to private funds came due while liquid assets had crashed), forcing a few to sell illiquid stakes at deep discounts in secondary markets. The episode is the clearest real-world proof of this post's warning: the illiquidity premium is only yours if you can *truly* afford to lock the money up — and even sophisticated institutions can misjudge how much liquidity they'll need.

### REITs as the liquid proxy in shallow markets

In markets where direct property is hard for individuals to access well — including, increasingly, Vietnam as its capital markets develop — **REITs (or property funds) act as the liquid proxy** for the asset class, letting ordinary investors hold a diversified, rebalanceable real-estate sleeve without buying whole buildings. The deep US and Singapore REIT markets show what this looks like at maturity: an investor can hold a sliver of hundreds of professionally-managed properties across sectors and cities, collect the dividends, and rebalance in one click. Vietnam's near-absence of this option is exactly why its households defaulted to whole-building concentration — and why the slow development of a real Vietnamese REIT market would be one of the most useful things that could happen for ordinary household balance sheets, giving people a way to own income property as a *sleeve* instead of a *bet-the-house* commitment.

## When this matters / Further reading

Step all the way back, and the portfolio role of real estate comes down to a single honest framing, captured in the checklist below.

![A grid checklist of five allocation questions: how much real estate to hold, direct property or REIT, whether you are paid for illiquidity, your rebalancing plan, and the home-as-concentration trap that most households are already caught in](/imgs/blogs/real-estate-in-a-portfolio-7.png)

The checklist is the post in five questions. **How much?** — size the sleeve so the illiquid part stays a slice, and remember that for most households the answer is "you're already maxed out via your home." **Direct or REIT?** — REITs for liquidity, divisibility, and rebalanceability; direct property for control and the illiquidity premium, but only if you can truly afford the lock-up. **Are you paid for illiquidity?** — believe in the premium but discount the headline, and never mistake appraisal smoothing for a free lunch. **What's your rebalancing plan?** — hold the adjustable part liquid, and rebalance with cash flows around the block you can't trim. And the trap that catches almost everyone: **the home as concentration** — a leveraged primary home is often ~90% of net worth, so the honest first move is usually not to add property but to build everything else.

This matters to you the moment you stop thinking about real estate as "a building I might buy" and start thinking about it as "a position in my total portfolio." If you're a young household stretching to buy a first home, the most valuable thing you can take from this is that the home you're about to buy *is* your real-estate allocation — a big, leveraged, undiversified one — so your financial job for the next decade is to build the liquid, diversified rest of the sheet around it, not to add more property on top. If you've already accumulated some wealth and you're deciding how to hold a real-estate sleeve, the question is direct-vs-REIT and how much, with the lumpiness and illiquidity of direct property weighed honestly against the premium it pays. And if you're a Vietnamese reader, the structural reality — a shallow REIT market, sky-high price-to-income, a culture that treats land as the only real store of value — makes the over-concentration trap unusually hard to escape, which is all the more reason to see it clearly.

The single number to carry away is the one we started with: real estate is the **largest asset class on Earth, about \$393 trillion** — and yet most people own it in the worst possible way, as one concentrated, leveraged, illiquid building rather than a sized, diversified, rebalanceable sleeve. Owning property *well* in a portfolio isn't about owning more of it. It's about owning the *right amount*, in the *right form*, for the right reasons — income, an inflation hedge, and diversification — while being clear-eyed that the illiquidity premium is a wage for patience you must genuinely be able to afford.
