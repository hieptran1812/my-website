> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A property bubble is a specific machine, not just "prices are high." It needs three ingredients *together*, and it breaks for one predictable reason.
>
> - The three ingredients are **cheap, abundant leverage** (low rates, high loan-to-value, easy loans), **extrapolated expectations** (people buy because prices already rose, not because of the rent), and a **supply lag** (it takes years to build, so the coming glut is invisible until it is too late). One or two of these is a strong market; all three together is a bubble.
> - The boom is a self-reinforcing spiral: rising prices create equity, equity supports more borrowing, more borrowing bids prices higher. The crash is the *same machine in reverse* — falling prices destroy equity, negative equity forces selling, and each distressed sale becomes the comparable that drags the next one down. That tipping point is the **Minsky moment**.
> - Because the selling is forced rather than chosen, prices do not stop at fair value — they **overshoot below** the rent the property can earn, then recover slowly as cheap, rent-backed prices draw real buyers back in.
> - The one tell that works across every market and every era: **price detaches from rent**. When a home costs 40, 50, 60 times its annual rent and the only reason to buy is that someone will pay more tomorrow, you are looking at the late innings — whether it is the US in 2006, Japan in 1990, or Vietnam in 2022.

In 2021, an apartment in a glossy new tower in Thủ Đức, on the eastern edge of Ho Chi Minh City, changed hands three times in eighteen months without anyone ever living in it. The first buyer paid a deposit on a floor plan. The second bought the contract from the first at a markup, sight unseen. The third bought it from the second, again at a markup, again sight unseen — and was already lining up a fourth buyer when, in late 2022, the phone stopped ringing. There was no fourth buyer. The corporate-bond market that funded the developer had frozen, the banks had pulled back, and the only people left in the room were holding contracts on a building that, suddenly, no one wanted to pay more for than the last person did. Within a year, secondary prices on some of those projects were down 30 to 40 percent, and the third buyer — the one holding the contract when the music stopped — was deeply under water.

Five thousand kilometres of ocean and fifteen years away, a stucco house in a subdivision outside Phoenix, Arizona told the identical story. Bought in 2005 with almost nothing down, refinanced in 2006 to pull out the "equity" that rising prices had conjured, it was worth less than its mortgage by 2008 and sitting empty, keys mailed back to the bank, by 2009. The family had done nothing wrong except believe what the entire market believed: that property prices go up, that you should buy as much as the bank will lend, and that you can always sell to the next person for more.

These two stories are not coincidences and they are not about Vietnamese or American character. They are the *same machine*, running in two places, decades apart, on the same physics. That is the claim of this post. Most people think a bubble is simply a market where "prices got too high," and that calling one is a matter of taste or vibes. It is not. A property bubble has a definite anatomy — three ingredients that must be present together, a specific way it builds, a specific reason it tops, and a specific way it falls *through* fair value rather than gently to it. Once you can name the parts, you can see the machine working in real time, and you can tell the difference between an expensive market (which can stay expensive) and a bubble (which cannot).

The diagram above is the mental model for the whole post: the bubble machine as three ingredients — cheap leverage, extrapolated expectations, a supply lag — plus the spark that turns the boom into a crash. This article builds that machine from the ground up. We will define every term from zero, assemble the three ingredients one at a time, watch the boom spiral upward, find the exact moment it tops, run the whole thing in reverse to see the crash, follow prices *below* fair value to the bottom, clear up the myths people believe about bubbles, and then walk through three real episodes — the US in 2006 to 2012, Japan in 1990, and Vietnam in 2022 — where the machine ran in full view. We keep two people as running examples: **Minh**, buying an apartment in Ho Chi Minh City, and **Dana**, a US investor — in ₫ and \$ side by side, so a Vietnamese and an international reader both stay oriented.

## Foundations: price versus the value rent can justify

Before any of the mechanics make sense, we have to define the words. Skim this if you already know them; do not skip it if you don't, because every later section leans on these exact terms.

**Price** is simply what the last buyer paid — the number on the most recent transaction. It is a fact about a single deal, set by whoever was willing to bid the most at one moment. Price is loud, public, and emotional. **Fundamental value**, by contrast, is what the property is *worth* based on the cash it can actually produce — the rent. A piece of real estate is, financially, a machine for producing rent; its honest value is the present value of all the rent it will ever earn, the same way a bond's value is the present value of its coupons. When we say a property's **rent-justified value**, we mean: if you ignore the hope of selling it to someone else for more, and you value it purely as a stream of rent, what is it worth? That number is slow, boring, and tethered to the real economy — incomes, jobs, how much people can pay to live somewhere.

The single most useful number in this whole post is the bridge between price and rent: the **price-to-rent ratio**, which is the price divided by the annual rent. If an apartment costs ₫7.0 billion (≈ \$270,000) and rents for ₫280 million a year (≈ \$10,800), its price-to-rent ratio is 7,000 / 280 = **25**. You can read that as "it takes 25 years of rent to pay back the price," and it is the inverse of the **rental yield** — the annual rent as a percentage of price, here 1/25 = **4%**. (Property people more often quote the **cap rate**, the net rental yield after costs; we have a whole post on [cap rates, NOI and the income approach](/blog/trading/real-estate/cap-rate-noi-and-the-income-approach) if you want the precise version. For bubble-spotting, the rough gross price-to-rent ratio is enough.) The key fact: rents are sticky and grow slowly, roughly with incomes. So if price-to-rent climbs from 25 to 50, it is almost never because rents collapsed — it is because price ran away from the rent. **A stretched price-to-rent ratio is the fingerprint of a price detaching from its fundamental value.**

A **bubble** is exactly that detachment, taken to an extreme and held aloft by belief rather than rent. Formally: a bubble is when the price of an asset rises far above the value its cash flows can justify, *sustained by the expectation that prices will keep rising*, rather than by any improvement in the cash flows themselves. The phrase to hold onto is "held up by expectations." In a normal market, price is anchored to rent. In a bubble, price is anchored to the *next price* — to the belief that a buyer will appear tomorrow willing to pay more.

That belief has a name: **extrapolative expectations**. To extrapolate is to assume the recent trend continues — to look at "prices rose 30% last year" and conclude "so they'll rise about 30% this year too." Humans do this instinctively, and in a boom it becomes the dominant way people forecast. The crucial thing is that extrapolation is *backward-looking dressed up as a forecast*: it uses the past price change as the reason to buy, which is circular, because the past price change was itself driven by people extrapolating the price change before it.

The honest version of buying-for-the-next-buyer is the **greater-fool theory**: you knowingly pay more than something is worth, because you expect to find a "greater fool" who will pay even more. In a greater-fool market, nobody needs to think the price is justified — they only need to think someone *more foolish* is coming. This works beautifully right up until it doesn't, because the supply of greater fools is finite. The last fool, the one who buys at the top, is the person left holding the asset when no greater fool appears. The greater-fool dynamic is what turns a market from "expensive but anchored" into "a game of musical chairs."

To understand the violence of the crash, you need three more terms. **Leverage** is borrowed money used to buy an asset — the mortgage, the developer's bond, the margin loan. Leverage **amplifies**: it multiplies both your gains and your losses relative to the cash you put in, because you control a big asset with a small slice of your own money. (We build this from scratch in [leverage and the mortgage](/blog/trading/real-estate/leverage-and-the-mortgage-how-debt-amplifies-property); here we just need the amplification.) **Negative equity** is when the loan against a property is bigger than the property is worth — you owe more than you could sell for. **Equity** is the value of the asset minus the debt against it; negative equity means that number has gone below zero. And **forced selling** is selling not because you want to but because you *have* to — because the bank is foreclosing, a margin call has hit, a loan can't be rolled over, or you simply can't make the payments. Forced selling is the opposite of patient selling: a forced seller takes whatever price is offered today, because waiting is not an option.

Put forced selling, negative equity and leverage together at the moment a boom breaks, and you get the **Minsky moment** — named for the economist Hyman Minsky, who argued that stability itself breeds instability. A Minsky moment is the tipping point at which asset prices, propped up by debt, start to fall enough that over-leveraged owners are forced to sell, and that selling drives prices down further, forcing still more selling. It is the precise instant the spiral reverses. (We climb Minsky's full ladder — from safe borrowers to fragile ones — in [the credit cycle and property prices](/blog/trading/real-estate/the-credit-cycle-and-property-prices); here we focus on the moment it snaps.)

A **distressed sale** is a single forced sale at a knock-down price — a foreclosure auction, a panicked owner dumping a flat below market, a developer fire-selling unsold units to raise cash. Distressed sales matter out of all proportion to their number because of **comparables** (or "comps"): the way every property is valued by reference to recent nearby sales. One distressed sale at 30% below the last price becomes the new comp, which lowers the appraised value of every similar property nearby — which can tip *those* owners into negative equity, forcing *their* sales. Distressed sales are contagious through the comp mechanism.

Finally, two words people use loosely that mean different things. A **correction** is a moderate fall — conventionally around 10 to 20% — that brings an over-extended price back toward its fundamental value and then stabilises. A **crash** is a violent, self-reinforcing collapse — 30%, 40%, 50% or more — driven by forced selling, that overshoots *below* fundamental value before recovering. A correction is the market letting air out; a crash is the machine running in reverse. The difference between them is leverage: a market with little debt corrects; a market saturated with debt crashes. That single distinction is most of what this post is about.

That's the vocabulary. The thesis of everything that follows fits in one sentence: **a bubble is a price detached from rent and held up by leverage and extrapolated hope, and it crashes — not corrects — because leverage forces selling once the marginal buyer is gone.**

## Ingredient 1: cheap, abundant leverage

No leverage, no bubble. This is the first and most important ingredient, and it is worth being precise about *why*. The reason is that leverage does two things at once: on the way up it lets buyers bid prices far higher than their own cash could support, and on the way down it forces them to sell. Both directions matter, and both come from the same arithmetic.

Start with the up direction. Almost nobody buys property with cash. A buyer brings a down payment and borrows the rest, so the price they can offer is set not by what they have but by what they can *borrow*. The **loan-to-value ratio (LTV)** — the loan as a percentage of the price — is the dial that sets this. At 50% LTV, a buyer must fund half the price themselves; at 90% LTV, only a tenth. As lending loosens and LTVs rise, the same buyer, with the same savings, can suddenly bid far more. Multiply that across every buyer in a market and prices lift bodily, with no change in incomes, rents, or population — purely because the bank decided to lend a larger fraction of a larger number.

Now the amplification, which is the heart of leverage. When you put down 20% and borrow 80%, you control five times your cash in property. If the price rises 10%, your *equity* — your slice — rises far more than 10%, because the gain accrues entirely to you while the loan stays fixed. The same lever works in reverse, and that is the dangerous part.

#### Worked example: how 80% leverage turns a 30% price fall into a wipe-out

Dana buys a US rental for \$500,000 with 20% down — \$100,000 of her own cash — and an \$400,000 mortgage (80% LTV). Her equity is the value minus the debt: \$500,000 − \$400,000 = **\$100,000**, exactly her down payment.

Now suppose prices fall 30%, a routine crash-sized move. The house is worth \$500,000 × 0.70 = **\$350,000**. The mortgage is still \$400,000 — debt does not fall when prices do. Her equity is now \$350,000 − \$400,000 = **−\$50,000**. She is \$50,000 *under water*: she owes more than the house is worth. A 30% fall in the asset produced a 150% loss on her cash (she's lost her entire \$100,000 *and* owes another \$50,000). That is leverage running in reverse: the 5× lever that multiplied her gains on the way up multiplies her losses on the way down.

Here is the part that turns a personal loss into a market crash. Dana now has every incentive — and, depending on the loan, sometimes the obligation — to walk away or sell. If she sells, she sells into a falling market at whatever price clears, because she *must*. Her sale becomes the new comp. And every other owner on her street who bought at 80% LTV is in the same trap.

*The lesson: leverage doesn't just make losses bigger, it converts them into forced selling — and forced selling is what makes a crash a crash.*

This is why the cost and availability of credit is the master variable. Cheap leverage means low interest rates (so the monthly payment on a big loan is bearable), high LTVs (so small down payments control big assets), and loose underwriting (so more people qualify for more). When all three are present, the *amount* of money that can be bid for property expands enormously — and crucially, it expands the pool of buyers who are one bad year away from negative equity. A market bought with 50% down payments can fall 40% and almost nobody is under water; a market bought with 5% down goes under water on a 5% dip. **The more leverage in a market, the smaller the price fall needed to trigger forced selling — and the more violent the crash when it comes.**

This is also why bubbles cluster around periods of cheap money. The US bubble of the 2000s rode mortgage rates that fell and lending standards that all but vanished — no-documentation "liar loans," 100% financing, teaser rates that reset later. Vietnam's 2021 to 2022 froth rode bank credit growth running hot and developers funding themselves with a flood of corporate bonds, plus the local twist of *presales*, where buyers fund construction with deposits and instalments on apartments that don't yet exist — leverage on the buyer side and the developer side at once. The leverage is never identical in form across countries, but it is always there, because **without borrowed money, prices simply cannot detach far from what buyers can actually pay out of income.**

## Ingredient 2: extrapolated expectations

Leverage provides the fuel, but something has to light it. The second ingredient is psychological: a shift in *why* people buy, from the rent the property earns to the price they expect to sell it for. This is the moment a market stops being an investment in cash flow and becomes a bet on the next buyer.

Recall the anchor. In a healthy market, price is tethered to rent: you buy a flat because it produces ₫280 million a year and a price of ₫7.0 billion gives you a sensible 4% yield. Your return comes from the rent. The price can drift around, but the rent keeps pulling it back, because if price runs too far ahead of rent, the yield gets so low that buying makes no sense versus simply renting, and demand cools. Rent is the anchor that keeps a market honest.

Extrapolative expectations cut the anchor. Once prices have risen sharply for a few years, people stop asking "what yield does this give me?" and start asking "how much will this be worth next year?" — and they answer that question by extrapolating the recent trend. "Prices rose 30% last year, so they'll rise again; even at a 2% yield I'll make 30% on the price, so the rent barely matters." The rent, which used to be the whole point, becomes a rounding error in the buyer's mind. **The instant the expected price gain dwarfs the rental yield, the rent stops disciplining the price, and the only thing holding the price up is the expectation of more price.**

This is where the **greater-fool theory** takes over. When you buy purely because you expect to resell higher, you don't need to believe the price is justified — you only need to believe a buyer is coming who will pay more. The market becomes a relay race of greater fools, each one buying not the rent but the *option to sell to the next person*. And because everyone is doing the same arithmetic — recent gains, extrapolated forward — the expectation is self-fulfilling for a while: people buy because prices rose, which makes prices rise, which confirms the extrapolation, which brings in more buyers. The belief manufactures the reality that justifies the belief. This is the engine of every mania, and it explains the otherwise baffling fact that the *flimsiest* fundamentals often appear at the *highest* prices: by the top, almost no one is buying for the rent.

#### Worked example: the greater-fool math, when rent has stopped mattering

Minh is offered a Thủ Đức apartment at ₫5.0 billion (≈ \$193,000). It rents for ₫150 million a year (≈ \$5,800) — a gross yield of 150 / 5,000 = **3.0%**, low, but never mind. Minh isn't buying the rent. Prices in the area rose 35% last year, and he extrapolates: he expects to sell in twelve months for ₫6.5 billion, a 30% gain.

His mental math: pay ₫5.0 billion now, collect ₫150 million of rent, sell for ₫6.5 billion. The rent (₫150 million) is dwarfed by the expected price gain (₫1.5 billion). The rent is 10% of his expected return; the *resale* is 90% of it. So Minh — quite rationally, given his belief — barely glances at the yield. He is not buying an income-producing asset; he is buying a ticket that he expects a greater fool to buy from him for ₫6.5 billion.

Now ask the only question that matters: *why* would that greater fool pay ₫6.5 billion for an apartment yielding, at that price, just 150 / 6,500 = **2.3%**? The honest answer is: only if *they*, in turn, expect to extrapolate the gain and sell to a still-greater fool at ₫8.5 billion. The chain has no anchor in rent at all. It is fools all the way up — and it holds only as long as the next fool keeps appearing.

*The lesson: in a greater-fool market the price is justified by the next price, not by the rent — which means it is justified by nothing at all, and ends the instant the next fool fails to show.*

Extrapolation also rewires emotion, and the emotions are part of the machine. Early gains breed confidence; confidence breeds *more* buying; visible riches breed envy and the fear of missing out, which drags in people who swore they'd never speculate. Skeptics are not just ignored but *punished* — every month they stay out, prices rise and they look more foolish, so the social and financial pressure to capitulate is enormous. This is why bubbles feel, from inside, less like greed than like *prudence*: not buying seems like the reckless choice, because everyone who bought is richer. The extrapolation becomes a culture, complete with folk wisdom ("they're not making more land," "property only ever goes up," "buy now or be priced out forever"). Those slogans are not analysis; they are extrapolation wearing the costume of timeless truth.

The tell, always, is the gap between price and rent. You cannot see "expectations" directly, but you can see the price-to-rent ratio, and when it stretches to twice its own history while rents barely move, you are watching extrapolation in action. The rent is telling you what the asset is worth as a machine for housing people; the price is telling you what the crowd expects the next buyer to pay. When those two numbers diverge sharply, the second ingredient is present.

![Two diverging lines on a chart, the market price ballooning far above the rent-justified fundamental value, then crashing through it and overshooting below before recovering](/imgs/blogs/anatomy-of-a-property-bubble-and-crash-2.png)

The chart above is the shape every bubble traces, drawn schematically: the dashed line is the rent-justified fundamental value, climbing gently as rents grow; the solid line is the market price, which tracks it, then detaches into a bubble, peaks at roughly twice fundamental value, crashes back *through* the fundamental, overshoots below it, and finally recovers toward it. Hold this picture — the rest of the post is just an explanation of each segment of that solid line.

#### Worked example: price-to-rent stretched to 2× fundamental, and the snap-back

Take a market where the sane, rent-justified price-to-rent ratio is **25** (a 4% gross yield). Rents in this market are ₫200 million a year (≈ \$7,700) on a typical flat, so the fundamental value is 25 × ₫200 million = **₫5.0 billion**.

Now a bubble inflates and the *price* climbs to a price-to-rent ratio of **50** — twice fundamental — while rents, sticky as ever, barely budge, still around ₫220 million. The market price is now 50 × ₫220 million = **₫11.0 billion** (≈ \$425,000). On paper everyone who owns is rich. But the yield has collapsed to 220 / 11,000 = **2.0%** — less than a bank deposit, with none of the liquidity. The only reason to own at this price is the expectation of a higher price.

When the expectation breaks, price-to-rent doesn't drift gently back to 25 — it overshoots *below* it, to perhaps **18** (we'll see why in the crash section), as forced sellers dump into a market with no buyers. At a ratio of 18 on ₫220 million of rent, the price is 18 × 220 = **₫3.96 billion**. So the round trip is ₫5.0 billion (fair) → ₫11.0 billion (bubble) → ₫3.96 billion (overshoot) — a fall of 64% from the top, and the asset ends up *below* where it started, despite rents having risen slightly the whole time.

*The lesson: the further price-to-rent stretches above its own history, the further it tends to snap back below it — the rubber band's recoil is proportional to its stretch.*

## Ingredient 3: the supply lag

The third ingredient is the one people forget, and it is what makes property bubbles especially treacherous compared to, say, stock bubbles. It is the **supply lag**: the long delay between deciding to build and actually delivering finished space.

Here is why it matters. In a normal, well-functioning market, rising prices are supposed to be self-correcting on the supply side. Prices go up → building becomes profitable → developers build more → the extra supply meets the demand → prices stabilise. The price signal calls forth the supply that satisfies it. But in real estate, this feedback loop is *slow*, because you cannot conjure a finished apartment tower in response to a price spike. You have to buy land, get permits, arrange financing, pour foundations, and build — a process that takes anywhere from two to six years. During all that time, prices keep rising (because the new supply hasn't arrived), which makes building look ever more profitable, which sets off *more* projects — and now an enormous wave of supply is in the pipeline, all triggered by the high prices, all due to arrive at roughly the same time, years later. (Why some cities have worse supply lags than others — geography, zoning, approval speed — is the subject of [supply elasticity and why some cities boom and bust](/blog/trading/real-estate/supply-elasticity-why-some-cities-boom-and-bust).)

The cruel timing is the point. The supply that the boom calls forth arrives *after* the peak. The cranes are most numerous, the construction loudest, the new launches most frequent — at the very moment demand is about to roll over. So just as the marginal buyer is running out (next section), a record wave of new apartments completes and hits the market looking for buyers who are no longer there. The supply lag hides the oversupply during the boom and then dumps it precisely when the market can least absorb it. It converts what might have been a gentle plateau into a glut.

#### Worked example: the glut that was invisible until it arrived

Suppose a city has demand for about 20,000 new apartments a year. Prices rise 30% over two years, and developers, extrapolating, all decide to build. Each project takes three years from start to finish. In years 1, 2 and 3 of the boom, developers *start* 40,000 units a year — double the real demand — but almost none have *completed* yet, so the visible market still looks tight: few finished units for sale, prices still rising, everyone reassured.

Then year 4 arrives. The projects started in year 1 complete — 40,000 units. The projects from year 2 are completing too. Suddenly the market is absorbing 40,000 finished units a year against demand for 20,000, *and* demand itself is softening because prices got too high. The overhang is enormous: tens of thousands of empty new apartments, all needing a buyer at once, many owned by developers who borrowed to build them and now must sell to repay the loans.

The whole time the glut was being created — years 1 through 3 — it was *invisible*, because finished supply lagged the building decision by three years. By the time it became visible, it was too late to stop: the concrete was poured, the loans were drawn, the units were coming whether anyone wanted them or not.

*The lesson: in real estate the oversupply is decided years before it appears, so the market looks tightest right before the flood — the supply lag is a time bomb set during the boom and detonated after the top.*

The supply lag is why property cycles are *long* — measured in years and decades, not the weeks of a stock panic — and why they overshoot so badly in both directions. (The deep version of this, the multi-decade rhythm, is the [18-year property cycle](/blog/trading/real-estate/why-real-estate-cycles-are-long-the-18-year-cycle).) It also explains a paradox that confuses people: bubbles often top out amid a *construction boom*, not a construction bust. The forest of cranes that looks like proof of a healthy, growing market is in fact the supply that will break it. When you see record construction *and* stretched prices *and* heavy leverage together, all three ingredients are present, and the supply lag is quietly guaranteeing that the correction, when it comes, will be amplified by a glut.

## How the boom builds: the spiral up

Now we can assemble the three ingredients into the machine and watch it run. The boom is not a straight line that someone draws; it is a *feedback loop*, and feedback loops have a particular property — they accelerate, because each turn of the loop makes the next turn bigger.

Trace one full turn. Prices rise a little, for some ordinary reason — rates fell, the city grew, a new metro line opened. Because almost everyone owns with leverage, that price rise creates **equity**: every owner's slice of their property just got bigger, even though they did nothing. Bigger equity does two things. First, it lets owners *borrow more* — they can remortgage to pull cash out, or use the paper gains as the down payment on a second property. Second, it makes them *feel* richer, which loosens their spending and their willingness to bid. So the equity created by the price rise funds more buying — both by existing owners trading up or buying second homes, and by banks, who see rising collateral values and happily lend against them. More buying meets the same slow-to-respond supply, and prices rise *again*. The new, higher prices reset the comps, create still more equity, justify still more lending — and the loop turns again, faster.

![A before and after diagram contrasting the leverage spiral running upward in the boom with the same spiral running downward in the bust](/imgs/blogs/anatomy-of-a-property-bubble-and-crash-3.png)

The left side of the figure above is this upward spiral: rising prices create equity, equity supports borrowing, borrowing funds buying, buying lifts prices — round and round. (The right side is the same loop in reverse, which we get to shortly.) Notice that *nothing real needs to be improving* for this loop to run. Rents can be flat, incomes can be flat, the population can be flat — and prices can still spiral upward for years, purely on the self-reinforcing dynamic of leverage and rising collateral values. That is the financial accelerator, and it is why booms run so far past what fundamentals justify.

As the loop turns, the three ingredients reinforce each other. Rising prices *strengthen* extrapolation (the trend is now longer and more convincing). Extrapolation *increases* leverage demand (people borrow more to buy more, sure they'll sell higher). More leverage *lifts* prices further. And the high prices set off the construction wave that the supply lag will deliver later. Meanwhile the *character* of the buyers changes. Early in the boom, buyers are owner-occupiers and rent-focused investors — people anchored to fundamentals. As prices climb, those buyers get priced out or refuse to pay, and they are replaced by *speculators*: flippers, second-home buyers, contract-traders, who are anchored only to the next price. The rising share of these buyers is itself a warning sign, because they are the most leveraged and the quickest to sell — they have no rent to fall back on, only the resale.

This is also where Minsky's insight bites: **stability breeds instability.** The longer the boom runs without a setback, the more reasonable it seems to take on more debt, accept a thinner margin of safety, buy with less down. Lenders, competing for business in a market where no loan has gone bad in years, loosen their standards — higher LTVs, lower documentation, interest-only loans. Each loosening adds fuel and removes a brake. The market becomes progressively more fragile *because* it has been calm, until it is a structure that requires prices to keep rising merely to avoid collapse — the Ponzi stage, where borrowers can only repay by selling or refinancing into ever-higher prices. By the late boom, a huge share of the market is in this condition, and the whole edifice depends on one thing: that the next buyer keeps showing up.

## The top: when the marginal buyer runs out

Bubbles do not pop because someone rings a bell or because prices reach a "correct" level — there is no correct level when price is anchored to the next price. They top for a much more mechanical reason: the **marginal buyer runs out.**

The marginal buyer is the person whose bid sets the price — the last, highest bidder at any moment. The whole edifice of the boom rests on a steady supply of these marginal buyers, each willing to pay a bit more than the last. The price only keeps rising as long as the *next* marginal buyer can be found and can pay. So the question that determines the top is simply: what could make the supply of marginal buyers dry up?

There are two ways, and they usually happen together. The first is **exhaustion**: the market literally runs out of people who can or will pay the next, higher price. Affordability is a hard ceiling. Incomes grow slowly; at some point the price is so far above what ordinary buyers earn that the only remaining buyers are pure speculators — and even they need a *greater* fool to sell to. When price-to-income reaches extremes (recall that Ho Chi Minh City sits near the top of global rankings, with prices around 32 times median income, and Hanoi near 31), the pool of buyers who can fund the next bid out of income has shrunk to almost nothing. The market is then standing on a tiny base of speculators selling to each other, and that base is fragile. The moment the flippers can't find the next flipper, the bid vanishes.

The second way is **a tightening of credit**, and it is usually the trigger that makes exhaustion bite. Remember that the marginal buyer bids with borrowed money, so their bid is only as big as their loan. If interest rates rise, or LTV limits are cut, or a lending channel freezes, the marginal buyer's *ability to pay* drops overnight — not because they changed their mind, but because the loan they could have gotten last month is no longer available. This is the spark in the cover figure. It can come from a central bank raising rates to cool inflation (the US in 2006, Vietnam's funding squeeze in 2022), from a regulator tightening mortgage rules, or from a credit market seizing up on its own (Vietnam's corporate-bond freeze after the Tân Hoàng Minh and Vạn Thịnh Phát arrests in 2022). However it arrives, tighter credit shrinks every marginal bid at once, and the price can no longer make a new high.

The top, then, is the moment these two forces meet: a market standing on exhausted, purely-speculative demand gets hit by a credit tightening that knocks out the buyers' borrowing power. The last marginal buyer pays the peak price, and then no greater fool appears. Crucially, **the top is silent.** There is no crash on the day of the peak — transactions just slow, listings sit longer, the easy resales stop happening. The flippers who counted on a quick sale find the phone has gone quiet. For a while, sellers refuse to cut prices (nobody wants to admit the gain is gone), so the *headline* price barely moves even as the market freezes underneath. This is the deceptive plateau at the very peak: prices look stable, but the engine has stalled, and the supply lag is about to deliver a glut into a market that has just lost its last buyer.

This is also why "the top" is invisible in real time and only obvious in hindsight. At the peak, the data still looks bullish — prices at all-time highs, construction booming, credit still flowing from loans made months ago. The thing that has changed — the disappearance of the marginal buyer — doesn't show up in the price for months, because it takes time for the absence of buyers to force the first sellers to cut. By the time the price visibly turns, the top is long past. We'll return to why you can't reliably *time* this in the misconceptions section; for now the key point is mechanical: **a bubble tops when the marginal buyer can no longer pay the next price, which happens when credit tightens against an exhausted, speculative buyer base.**

## The crash: leverage in reverse and forced selling

Now the machine runs backward, and this is where a bubble differs from a merely expensive market. An expensive market with little leverage, having lost its marginal buyer, simply stops rising and drifts sideways or down — a *correction*. A leveraged market does something far more violent, because the same leverage that lifted prices on the way up now *forces selling* on the way down. That is the difference between a correction and a crash, and it is entirely about debt.

Watch the spiral reverse, turn by turn. The marginal buyer is gone, so the first prices have to come down to find any buyer at all — call it a 10% dip. For most assets, a 10% dip is a nuisance. But recall Dana's arithmetic: a buyer who put 20% down is wiped out by a 20% fall, and *under water* — in **negative equity** — beyond that. As prices slip, the most leveraged owners cross into negative equity first. An owner in negative equity has a brutal choice: keep paying a mortgage larger than the house is worth, or default. Many default, especially where they can walk away without further liability. The bank forecloses and must sell the collateral — a **distressed sale**, at whatever price clears quickly, because the bank wants the loan repaid, not a real-estate portfolio.

That distressed sale is the contagion vector. It becomes the new **comp**: the most recent nearby transaction, now 30% below the last "normal" sale. Every appraiser, every bank, every buyer now values similar properties off that lower comp. So the *paper* value of every neighbouring property drops to match — which tips the *next* tier of owners into negative equity, forcing *their* sales, which set still lower comps, which tip still more owners under water. The loop feeds itself, and it feeds *faster* the further it runs, because each round creates more forced sellers than the last.

![A six-step pipeline showing the forced-selling cascade, where a price fall causes negative equity, which forces a distressed sale, which lowers comparables, which pushes more owners under water, repeating](/imgs/blogs/anatomy-of-a-property-bubble-and-crash-4.png)

The figure above is this cascade as a loop: price falls → negative equity → forced/distressed sale → lower comps → more owners under water → back to negative equity, faster. The instant this loop becomes self-sustaining — when falling prices are forcing enough selling to drive prices down further on their own, without any new bad news — is the **Minsky moment.** Before it, the market is falling because the marginal buyer left; after it, the market is falling because *the falling is forcing more falling*. It has become a self-reinforcing collapse, and that is what the word "crash" actually means.

#### Worked example: one forced sale cascading through a street

Picture a street of ten identical apartments, each "worth" ₫10 billion (≈ \$386,000) at the peak, each bought with 80% loans — so each owner owes ₫8 billion and has ₫2 billion of equity.

Prices dip 10%. Each flat is now worth ₫9 billion; equity has dropped to ₫1 billion. Uncomfortable, but everyone is still solvent. Then owner A — a flipper who borrowed to the hilt and counted on selling — can't make payments and is foreclosed. The bank sells flat A fast, for ₫7 billion, a fire-sale price. Flat A is now the comp.

Every other flat is re-valued off ₫7 billion. Now each is worth ₫7 billion against an ₫8 billion loan: every remaining owner is **₫1 billion under water.** Three of them, also over-leveraged, default. Three more forced sales hit the market at once, and with so many sellers and no buyers, they clear at ₫6 billion. The comp drops again. Now the survivors are ₫2 billion under water, and the next tier cracks.

In a few rounds, a 10% dip has become a 40% crash — ₫10 billion to ₫6 billion — not because anything fundamental changed about the apartments (same flats, same street, same city), but because leverage converted a price fall into a wave of forced sales, and each forced sale dragged the comp down for everyone.

*The lesson: in a leveraged market a small price fall doesn't stay small — negative equity turns it into forced selling, and forced selling turns one fire sale into a cascade that no single owner can stop.*

Two things make the crash worse, and both are about liquidity vanishing exactly when it's needed. First, **credit freezes on the way down** just as it flooded in on the way up. Banks, watching their collateral evaporate and their loans go bad, slam the lending window shut — higher rates, lower LTVs, stricter rules, or simply "no." So at the precise moment the market needs new buyers to absorb the forced sales, the buyers can't get loans. The pool of buyers, fed by leverage on the way up, is starved by the lack of it on the way down. Second, **everyone tries to sell at once.** Speculators with no rent to live on, developers who must repay construction loans, owners fleeing negative equity — they all want out simultaneously, while the buyers have vanished. Supply spikes as demand collapses. Price is set at the margin, and when the margin is a desperate forced seller meeting a thin, credit-starved trickle of buyers, the clearing price can be brutally low.

This is also where the supply lag delivers its payload. The construction wave that the boom set off — invisible for years — now completes, dumping tens of thousands of new units into a market that is already drowning in forced sales. The developers who built them, themselves leveraged, must sell to repay their loans, adding yet more forced supply. The glut and the deleveraging arrive together, and they compound. This is exactly what froze Vietnam's market in 2022 to 2023: developers who had pre-sold and borrowed against half-built towers suddenly couldn't roll their bonds, buyers couldn't get mortgages, and projects stalled with unsold inventory — the supply lag and the credit freeze hitting at once.

## The overshoot below fundamental, and the recovery

Here is the most counterintuitive part of the whole machine, and the part that separates a crash from a correction: **the price does not stop falling when it reaches fair value. It falls *through* it, and overshoots below.**

Why would a price drop below what the rent can justify — below the level where, on pure economics, the property is a screaming bargain? Because the selling at the bottom is *forced*, not chosen, and a forced seller does not care about fundamental value. A bank foreclosing on a flat is not asking "what is this worth as a stream of rent?" — it is asking "what can I get for this today so I can close the loan?" A developer dumping unsold units to avoid bankruptcy is not running a discounted-cash-flow model; it is raising cash to survive. When the marginal seller is desperate and the marginal buyer is scarce and credit-starved, the clearing price is set by desperation on one side and fear on the other, and it has nothing to do with the rent the property earns. The anchor that normally pulls price back to fundamental — value-conscious buyers stepping in when yields get attractive — is missing, because those buyers either can't get loans, are frozen by fear, or are themselves wiped out.

So the price *overshoots*. It blows past the rent-justified value and keeps falling, until it reaches a level so cheap that the few buyers with cash, steady nerves, and no leverage finally cannot resist. At the bottom, yields are extraordinarily high — the rent, which barely moved through the whole cycle, is now a large percentage of a tiny price. That is what eventually stops the fall: at some price, the rent alone is such a good return that cash buyers step in regardless of whether prices will rise, because the income justifies the purchase by itself. **The fundamental value, ignored on the way up and overshot on the way down, is what finally catches the falling price — from below.**

#### Worked example: how far prices overshoot below fundamental at the bottom

Return to our market where the sane price-to-rent ratio is 25 (a 4% yield) and rents are ₫220 million a year. Fundamental value is 25 × 220 = **₫5.5 billion** (≈ \$212,000). The bubble took the price to a ratio of 50 — ₫11.0 billion — as we computed earlier.

Now the crash. Forced selling doesn't stop at the fundamental ratio of 25 (₫5.5 billion). It overshoots, because no value-buyer can get a loan and every seller is desperate. The price-to-rent ratio falls to perhaps **16** — a 6.25% gross yield. At that ratio, the price is 16 × 220 = **₫3.52 billion** (≈ \$136,000). That is **36% below** the fair value of ₫5.5 billion, and **68% below** the bubble peak of ₫11.0 billion.

And here is what stops it: at ₫3.52 billion, the ₫220 million of rent is a 6.25% yield, in a country where deposit rates might be 6%. A cash buyer can now earn a stock-like income return from a flat *and* own a hard asset, with all the bubble froth wrung out. That return, finally, is good enough that buyers step in without needing prices to rise — and the overshoot bottoms. The recovery then begins not with euphoria but with cold-eyed value buyers, slowly, as cheap rent-backed prices draw real money back in. Years later, price-to-rent drifts back toward 25 and the cycle is reset.

*The lesson: the crash overshoots below fair value because forced sellers ignore fundamentals — and it bottoms only when the rent alone makes the price irresistible to a buyer who needs no greater fool.*

This overshoot is why the recovery is slow and why it feels nothing like the boom. The boom was driven by extrapolation and leverage — fast, emotional, self-reinforcing upward. The recovery is driven by value and rebuilt confidence — slow, skeptical, grinding. The same psychology that overshot on the way up (everyone euphoric) overshoots on the way down (everyone disgusted with property, swearing never again), and it takes years for that mood to thaw. Negative equity has to be worked off, bad loans written down, gluts absorbed, and trust in borrowing rebuilt before leverage and optimism can begin a new cycle. The full arc — boom, peak, crash, overshoot, recovery — is the picture below, with the emotion and leverage at each stage, because the emotional cycle and the leverage cycle are really the same cycle seen from two angles.

![A timeline showing the full arc of a bubble through five stages, boom, peak, crash, overshoot below fundamental and recovery, with the emotion and leverage at each stage](/imgs/blogs/anatomy-of-a-property-bubble-and-crash-7.png)

The figure above is the whole machine in one line: optimism turning to greed and euphoria as leverage climbs through the boom and peak; fear turning to panic and capitulation as leverage runs in reverse through the crash and overshoot; hope returning slowly as value buyers rebuild the market in the recovery. Every bubble, in every country, in every era, traces some version of this arc — because it is not about the country or the era, it is about leverage, extrapolation, and the supply lag, which are features of human beings and concrete, not of any particular market.

## Common misconceptions

A bubble's machinery is counterintuitive enough that several confident, widely-held beliefs about it are simply wrong. Each of these costs people real money, so each is worth correcting precisely.

**"High prices mean a bubble."** No. High prices can be perfectly justified by fundamentals — a city with strong income growth, severe land constraints, and rising rents *should* have high and rising prices, and they need not crash. The fingerprint of a bubble is not the price level but the *detachment* of price from rent: a price-to-rent ratio stretched far above its own history, *and* held up by leverage and extrapolation rather than income. Singapore and Tokyo are expensive but not obviously bubbly, because their prices, while high, are anchored to high incomes and rents and are not floating on a wall of speculative leverage. Conversely, a market can be in a dangerous bubble at prices that seem modest, if those prices are 50× rent and bought with 95% loans. **Look at price *relative to rent and income*, and at how much leverage holds it up — not the headline number.**

**"You can time the top."** You almost certainly can't, and believing you can is how people get hurt. The top is silent and only visible in hindsight; at the peak, every indicator still looks bullish (record prices, booming construction, flowing credit). The thing that changes at the top — the disappearance of the marginal buyer — doesn't show in the data for months. Worse, bubbles routinely run *far* longer and higher than any reasonable person expects, because extrapolation is self-fulfilling: the more overvalued it gets, the more the recent trend "proves" it will keep going. The economist's adage applies — "the market can stay irrational longer than you can stay solvent." The practical implication is not "buy the dip" or "short the top" but humility: if you can identify the *ingredients* (stretched price-to-rent, loose credit, speculative buyers, surging supply), you can recognise you're in dangerous territory and *reduce your own leverage and exposure* — which you control — rather than trying to call the exact day, which you don't.

**"Bricks can't crash — property always goes up."** This is extrapolation masquerading as a law of nature, and it is false. Property has crashed, hard, in living memory: US national prices fell 27% from 2006 to 2012, and far more in the worst metros; Japanese urban land prices fell roughly 70% from the 1990 peak and *still* haven't recovered three decades later; Spain, Ireland, and Vietnam have all seen 30 to 50% drawdowns in segments. The slogan feels true because property is *local and infrequent* — most people only watch their own market, and crashes are rare enough that a generation can grow up without seeing one, which makes the next one more likely (no memory, more leverage). "Land always wins" is a claim about a multi-decade average in a growing economy; it is not a promise about the price between when you buy with 90% leverage and when you're forced to sell.

**"Rents will catch up to justify the price."** Almost never, on the timescale that matters. Rents are tethered to incomes, which grow slowly — a few percent a year. For rents to "catch up" to a price that doubled relative to rent, incomes would have to roughly double, which takes a decade or more even in a fast-growing economy. Meanwhile the bubble price needs the next buyer *now*. The fantasy that rents will rise to meet the price gets the causality backwards: in reality, the price falls to meet the rent, because the rent is the anchor and the price is the thing that wandered off. If you ever hear "the yield is low now but rents will catch up," translate it as "I am paying for housing I cannot justify and hoping the economy bails me out."

**"It's different this time — new demand makes the old rules obsolete."** Every bubble has its story for why fundamentals no longer apply: a new technology, a structural shortage, a wave of foreign buyers, a demographic boom, a government that won't let prices fall. Some of these stories even contain truth — there often *is* real demand underneath. But "it's different this time" is the phrase that turns a justified boom into a bubble, because it is used to wave away the *ingredients* — to argue that this time the leverage is safe, the price-to-rent doesn't matter, the supply won't arrive. The mechanism doesn't care about the story. Cheap leverage plus extrapolation plus a supply lag produces the same arc whether the cover story is the internet, China's urbanisation, or Vietnam's young population. The new demand can be real *and* the price can still be a bubble built on top of it.

**"A crash hurts only speculators, so a real homeowner is safe."** Partly true, importantly false. If you bought a home to live in, with a sustainable mortgage you can keep paying, a crash that you ride through costs you nothing real — you still have the home, the payment is the same, and the paper loss reverses over the cycle. *But* leverage doesn't care about your intentions. A homeowner in deep negative equity who loses their job, or whose teaser rate resets to a payment they can't afford, becomes a forced seller exactly like a speculator — and is then crushed by the same cascade. The protection isn't being an "owner-occupier"; it's having a payment you can sustain through a downturn and not *needing* to sell at the bottom. The homeowner who is safe is the one who isn't forced to transact during the crash. The one who bought at the peak with 95% leverage and a resetting rate is not safe at all, however much they wanted a home rather than a flip.

## How it shows up in real markets

The machine is universal, but it is most convincing when you watch it run in real episodes. Here are three, on three continents and across three decades, each illustrating the same anatomy with local clothing.

### The United States, 2006 to 2012: the textbook crash

The US housing bubble of the 2000s is the most documented bubble in history, and it is the machine in its purest form. **Leverage** was everywhere and reckless: subprime mortgages to borrowers who couldn't document income, 100% financing with no down payment, teaser-rate loans that reset to unaffordable payments later, and a securitization pipeline that let banks lend without keeping the risk, which destroyed any incentive to lend carefully. **Extrapolation** was the national religion: "house prices have never fallen nationally" was repeated as fact (it hadn't, in the postwar data — which is exactly why everyone believed it couldn't), and buyers, lenders, and rating agencies all built their models on the assumption that prices would keep rising. **The supply lag** delivered a glut: builders, extrapolating the boom, started a record number of homes, which completed into the teeth of the downturn, especially in the "sand states" — Arizona, Nevada, Florida, California.

![A line chart of the US Case-Shiller National Home Price Index from 2000 to 2022, peaking at 184 in 2006, falling 27% to a trough of 134 in 2012, then recovering to 308 by 2022](/imgs/blogs/anatomy-of-a-property-bubble-and-crash-6.png)

The chart above is the result, in the S&P/Cotality Case-Shiller National Home Price Index (set to 100 in January 2000). Prices peaked at **184.6 in July 2006**, then fell to a trough of **134 in February 2012** — a **27% national decline**, the first sustained nationwide fall in the postwar era, and far worse in the bubble epicentres, where many metros fell 50% or more. The crash followed the script exactly: the Federal Reserve had been raising rates from 2004, which choked off the marginal subprime buyer; the first defaults set off forced sales; foreclosures became comps that dragged whole neighbourhoods into negative equity; the cascade became self-reinforcing — the Minsky moment of 2008 — and the freezing of credit (the interbank market seized, banks failed) starved the market of new buyers exactly when it needed them. Prices overshot below fundamental in the worst-hit areas, where you could buy a house for less than the cost of building it, and the recovery — visible in the chart's long climb back to 308 by 2022 — took the better part of a decade. Every part of the anatomy is there.

### Japan, 1990: the bubble that never fully recovered

If the US shows the crash, Japan shows the overshoot and the failure to recover — a warning that "property always recovers" is not guaranteed. In the late 1980s, Japan ran the most extreme real-estate bubble of the modern era. Cheap leverage flooded in as the Bank of Japan held rates low and banks lent aggressively against land, which they treated as collateral that could only rise. Extrapolation reached almost mythical heights: the grounds of the Imperial Palace in Tokyo were said, at the peak, to be worth more than all the real estate in California, and the slogan "land never falls" (the *fudōsan shinwa*, the "land myth") was treated as an iron law. The price-to-rent and price-to-income ratios reached levels that made no fundamental sense, sustained entirely by the belief that land could only appreciate.

When the Bank of Japan finally raised rates sharply in 1989 to 1990 to deflate the bubble, the marginal buyer vanished and the machine reversed. Urban land prices fell roughly **70% from the 1990 peak**, and — this is the haunting part — they did not bounce back. Three decades later, many Japanese land prices remain below their 1990 highs. Why no recovery? Because the deleveraging was so deep and the banking system so wounded that credit stayed scarce for a generation (the "lost decades"), demographics turned against demand (a shrinking, aging population), and the psychological scar killed the extrapolation that fuels recoveries. Japan is the reminder that the overshoot below fundamental can persist for a very long time, and that "it always recovers" is true only on average, only in a growing economy, and not on any timescale you can count on.

### Vietnam, 2022 to 2023: a credit-freeze crash with local mechanics

Vietnam's most recent episode shows the same machine with a distinctly Vietnamese transmission. The boom of roughly 2017 to 2021 had all three ingredients. **Leverage** came in two local forms: rapid bank-credit growth into property, and a flood of developer **corporate bonds** sold to retail investors, plus the **presale** system in which buyers fund construction with deposits and instalments on apartments that don't yet exist — leverage layered on leverage. **Extrapolation** was intense in the *đất nền* (land-plot) markets of the HCMC and Hanoi satellite provinces, where plots changed hands repeatedly among speculators at rising prices, and in glossy apartment projects flipped on contract before completion — pure greater-fool dynamics, with prices anchored to the next buyer, not the rent. **The supply lag** was building a pipeline of towers that would complete into a softening market. By 2021, HCMC price-to-income had reached around 32 years and prices had detached badly from what locals could pay out of income.

The spark was a credit tightening with a uniquely Vietnamese trigger. In April 2022, the chairman of the Tân Hoàng Minh property group was arrested and its bond issues cancelled; in October 2022, the Vạn Thịnh Phát and SCB bank scandal broke. These shocks **froze the corporate-bond market** almost overnight — the pipe through which developers funded construction. At the same time, banks pulled back on property lending and mortgage rates, after teaser periods, reset toward 12 to 14%. The marginal buyer's borrowing power collapsed; developers couldn't roll their maturing bonds; presold projects stalled with cranes idle; and secondary apartment and *đất nền* prices in the frothiest segments fell an estimated 30 to 40%. It was a textbook crash, transmitted through the developer-funding pipe rather than the household-mortgage pipe — but the same anatomy, the same forced selling, the same freeze. The recovery, aided by the State Bank of Vietnam cutting policy rates four times in 2023 and by Decree 08 letting issuers restructure bonds, has been the slow, value-led grind the machine predicts — not a return to the euphoric flipping of 2021.

The three episodes differ in their leverage plumbing — subprime mortgages and securitization in the US, bank land-lending in Japan, corporate bonds and presales in Vietnam — but the anatomy is identical. Cheap leverage and extrapolated hope inflated a price detached from rent; a credit tightening removed the marginal buyer; forced selling cascaded through negative equity and falling comps; and the price overshot below fundamental before a slow, value-led recovery. The plumbing is local; the machine is universal.

## When this matters / Further reading

This matters the moment you are about to make the largest financial decision of your life with the largest amount of borrowed money you will ever touch — which is exactly what buying property is for most people. You do not need to predict the top to protect yourself; you need to recognise the *ingredients*. Before you buy, check the four dials: Is the price-to-rent ratio stretched far above its own history (is the yield absurdly low)? Is credit unusually loose — high LTVs, teaser rates, easy qualification? Are the buyers around you flippers and speculators rather than people who want to live there? Are cranes everywhere, with a wave of supply about to complete? When all four read extreme together, you are in late-bubble territory, and the single most important thing you control is your *own* leverage and your *own* ability to ride out a downturn without being forced to sell. The homeowner who survives a crash is not the one who timed it; it's the one whose payment was sustainable and who never *had* to transact at the bottom.
