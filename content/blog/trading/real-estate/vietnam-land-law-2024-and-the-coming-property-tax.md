> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — For decades, Vietnam was one of the cheapest places on Earth to *own* property. You paid a 2% tax when you sold, a 0.5% registration fee when you titled, and almost nothing — a tiny land-use tax of roughly 0.03–0.15% — for the privilege of *holding* a flat or a plot of land for years while it doubled in value. That era is quietly ending. Two forces are converging.
>
> - **This is law:** the **Land Law 2024** (passed January 2024, effective **1 August 2024**) scrapped the old five-year "framework" land-price table — a single official number frozen far below the market — and replaced it with **annual land-price tables** that each province reprices toward real transaction values every year. The headline tax *rates* did not change. The *base* they apply to just got much bigger.
> - **This is still only proposed:** the **Ministry of Finance (MoF)** has repeatedly floated a **second-home / property holding tax** — an annual tax on people who own more than one home, or who sit on idle land — debated on and off from **2023 through 2026**. It is **not enacted**. Anyone who tells you "Vietnam now has a property tax" is wrong as of mid-2026.
> - Because one official table feeds *every* land tax and fee — transfer tax, land-use fees, compensation, registration — raising the table raises all of them at once, without a single rate ever changing. A higher base is a tax increase in disguise.
> - The one number to remember: Vietnam's effective annual holding-tax burden today is **~0.1% of value** — versus roughly **1.1%** in the US and **~0.6%** for an owner-occupier in Singapore. That gap is the room the reform has to move into, and the direction of travel is up.

In the summer of 2024, a homeowner in Ho Chi Minh City — call him **Minh** — went to the district office to transfer a 70-square-metre apartment he had bought years earlier. Minh expected the same routine every Vietnamese property owner had learned to expect: declare a price on the contract, pay 2% of it as personal income tax, pay a small registration fee, and walk out. For as long as anyone could remember, the trick everyone knew was to declare a *low* price — because the government's own official price table, the one the tax office checked against, was frozen so far below the real market that you could under-declare aggressively and still be "above the table." The state quietly tolerated a system where the taxable value of a ₫7 billion (≈ \$270,000) apartment might be ₫2 billion on paper.

By the time Minh sells his *next* apartment, that game is over. Not because the rate changed — it is still 2% — but because the *number the 2% is multiplied by* has been redefined. The Land Law 2024 pulled the floor up under the entire tax system. And looming behind it is a second, larger change still being argued over in Hanoi: a tax not on selling, but on *holding* — the thing Vietnam has never really taxed.

This post is about that shift, and it is careful about one distinction above all others: **what is law versus what is merely proposed.** The annual land-price table is law and is already biting. The property holding tax is a proposal, repeatedly floated and repeatedly shelved. Confusing the two is the single most common mistake in every conversation about Vietnamese property tax, so we will flag it relentlessly.

![Before and after comparison showing the old frozen five-year framework land price far below market with tiny tax base, versus the new annual land-price table that tracks the market and lifts the whole tax base](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-land-law-2024-and-the-coming-property-tax-1.png)

The diagram above is the mental model for the whole post. On the left is the world Vietnam is leaving: a framework price set once every five years and then left frozen, sitting at maybe 30% of what properties actually sold for, so every tax tied to it was tiny. On the right is the world it is entering: an annual table that each province reprices toward the market, climbing toward 70–80% of real sale prices in hot districts — and dragging transfer taxes, land-use fees, and compensation payouts up with it. Same rates. Bigger base. A tax increase nobody had to vote for as a "tax increase."

## Foundations: the land-price table runs everything

Before we can talk about reform, we have to understand the one object that makes Vietnamese property tax tick — because almost nothing about it is intuitive if you come from a Western property-tax system. We will build every term from zero.

### The land-price table (bảng giá đất)

In Vietnam, every province publishes an official **land-price table** — in Vietnamese, *bảng giá đất*. It is a giant schedule: for each street, each zone, each category of land, it lists an official price per square metre that the *state* considers that land to be worth. It is not the market price. It is the *administrative* price — the number the government uses for its own calculations.

Think of it as the state's official answer to the question "how much is a square metre of this land worth?" — an answer that, historically, everyone knew was wrong, and wrong in a specific direction: too low.

Why does this single table matter so much? Because Vietnam plugs it into *almost every* land-related tax and fee. It is not one input among many. It is *the* input. When you sell, the transfer tax is computed on the higher of your contract price or the table price. When you convert farmland to residential land, the **land-use fee** you owe is scaled off the table. When the state takes your land for a road, the **compensation** it pays you is benchmarked to the table. The 0.5% **registration fee** is floored at the table price. And — crucially for the second half of this post — any *future* property holding tax would almost certainly read the value to be taxed off this same table.

So the land-price table is a single dial. Turn it, and every one of those bills moves at once.

### Land-use fees (tiền sử dụng đất)

This term confuses every foreigner, so let us be careful. In Vietnam, individuals do not technically own land outright — the state owns all land, and you hold a **land-use right** (a long-term, transferable, inheritable right that functions very much like ownership in practice). When you want to *upgrade* your relationship to a piece of land — convert agricultural land to residential land, or obtain a residential title for the first time — you pay a one-time **land-use fee**, *tiền sử dụng đất*, to the state. It is, roughly, the price of converting your weaker right into a stronger one.

That fee is computed from the land-price table. A higher table means a higher land-use fee. This is where the reform has bitten ordinary households hardest and fastest in 2024–25 — more on that later.

### Compensation and site clearance

When the state needs your land for a public project — a highway, a metro line, an industrial park — it can compulsorily acquire it (this is "site clearance," *giải phóng mặt bằng*). It must pay you **compensation**, and that compensation is benchmarked to — you guessed it — the land-price table. For decades, the table's frozenness cut both ways: it made taxes cheap, *but it also made compensation insultingly low*. Families displaced for a road were paid the frozen 2015 table price for land worth ten times that on the open market. This was one of the largest sources of land-related grievance and protest in Vietnam, and it is one of the genuine reasons the law changed.

### What Vietnam taxes today

Here is the full, honest picture of Vietnamese property taxation as of mid-2026, which is remarkably light by global standards:

| Event | Tax / fee | Rate | Base |
|---|---|---|---|
| Selling a property | Personal income tax (PIT) | **2%** | Higher of contract or table price |
| Titling / transferring | Registration fee (lệ phí trước bạ) | **0.5%** | Floored at table price |
| Simply *owning* land | Non-agricultural land-use tax | **~0.03–0.15%** | Of the (low) state price |
| Owning a *home* annually | **Nothing.** No broad annual property tax exists. | — | — |

Read that last row again. **Vietnam has no broad annual property tax.** You can own a ₫10 billion apartment and pay essentially zero each year to hold it. That single fact — the near-absence of a holding tax — is the protagonist of this entire post. It is what made Vietnamese property such a perfect store of speculative value, and it is precisely what reformers are now circling.

### Why Vietnam historically had almost no holding tax

Three reasons, briefly. First, **administrative**: building a fair, market-based annual property tax requires an accurate, current valuation of every property — and Vietnam, until very recently, deliberately did *not* keep one (the frozen framework price was the opposite of accurate). You cannot tax 1% of value if you refuse to know the value. Second, **political**: a holding tax is the most *visible*, most *resented* tax there is — it arrives as a bill every year whether or not you sold anything, whether or not you have the cash. Transaction taxes hide inside a sale you chose to make; holding taxes do not hide at all. Third, **developmental**: for a fast-growing, asset-accumulating society, taxing the accumulation of property was politically toxic. Owning land was how ordinary Vietnamese families built wealth, and taxing that felt like taxing aspiration itself.

For a deeper treatment of *why* economists love land and holding taxes — and why they are nonetheless rare — see the companion post on [property and land holding taxes](/blog/trading/real-estate/property-and-land-holding-taxes). Here, our job is the Vietnam-specific story: the table that runs everything, the law that just changed it, and the holding tax that has not arrived yet.

### A note on "two prices" — the land-price *table* vs the land-price *list*

One more piece of vocabulary, because it trips up even careful readers. The Land Law 2024 actually works with *two* official price concepts, and conflating them causes confusion. The **land-price table** (*bảng giá đất*) is the broad, published schedule — the per-street, per-zone list we have been describing, now repriced annually. But for *specific, large, or one-off* cases — a big plot the state is acquiring, a major land allocation to a developer — the law also allows a **specific land price** (*giá đất cụ thể*) determined by a formal valuation for that exact parcel, typically aimed even closer to true market value than the general table. For an ordinary household selling a flat or converting garden land, the *table* is what bites. For a developer being allocated a 20-hectare site, the *specific price* is what bites. Both moved toward market under the 2024 reform; both feed the same downstream bills. When this post says "the table," read it as shorthand for "the official price the state uses against you," whichever of the two applies to your transaction. The reform's core idea — *make the official price honest* — applies to both.

### Land vs structure: what the table actually values

A subtle but important point: the Vietnamese system is built around the *land*, not the building on it. The land-price table values the *land-use right* — the dirt and your right to use it — far more than the bricks. This is why "land-price table" is the right name: in Vietnam, land is the asset that holds and gains value, while the structure depreciates like any built thing. A 30-year-old apartment in a prime district can be worth a fortune almost entirely because of the *land share* it sits on, even as the concrete crumbles. That land-centric design is, incidentally, exactly what makes a *land*-value tax (rather than a building tax) the natural fit for a future Vietnamese holding tax — you tax the scarce, appreciating, immovable thing, not the depreciating structure. The companion post on [holding taxes](/blog/trading/real-estate/property-and-land-holding-taxes) explains why economists consider taxing land, specifically, close to a perfect tax.

## One table, every tax: how the base feeds the whole system

Before we look at the change, look at the *structure* the change acts on — because the structure is what makes a single reform so powerful.

![Pipeline diagram showing the land-price table feeding into transfer tax, land-use fee, compensation, registration fee, and a future holding tax all at once](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-land-law-2024-and-the-coming-property-tax-2.png)

The land-price table sits at the top, and from it flow five separate obligations:

1. **Transfer tax (2% PIT)** — computed on the higher of contract or table price.
2. **Land-use fee (tiền sử dụng đất)** — scales directly with the table.
3. **Compensation** — what the state pays you when it clears your land, benchmarked to the table.
4. **Registration fee (0.5%)** — also floored at the table price.
5. **Future holding tax** — *if* a property tax ever passes, it would tax value read off this same table.

This is the architectural fact that makes the Land Law 2024 so consequential. In a system where five different bills all reference one number, you do not need five separate reforms to raise five taxes. You need *one* reform — to the table — and all five move together. The legislature never has to cast a single politically painful vote labelled "we are raising your transfer tax by 160%." It just makes the table more accurate, and accuracy does the rest.

Keep this pipeline in mind. The rest of the post is, essentially, the story of someone reaching up and turning that top dial — and everything below it responding.

There is a second, more strategic reason this single-dial design matters. Because all five bills key off one number, the *accuracy* of that number is itself a policy lever. A government that wants to discourage land speculation, fund local services, and pay fair compensation does not need to win five separate, politically bloody votes to raise five separate taxes. It needs to win *one* argument: "our official prices should be accurate." That argument is almost impossible to oppose in public — who stands up to demand that the state keep *lying* about what land is worth? Yet winning it raises every keyed tax at once. The Land Law 2024 is, in this sense, a masterclass in *quiet* tax reform: it dressed a multi-tax increase as a transparency and fairness measure, which is exactly what it also genuinely was. Both things are true, and that is why it passed.

## The old framework price, and why it was broken

To feel the change, you have to feel how broken the old system was.

Under the law that governed Vietnam before August 2024, the land-price table worked on a **five-year framework**. The central government set a "price framework" (*khung giá đất*) — floors and ceilings — and then each province built its own table inside that framework. Crucially, the framework was set **once every five years** and then left *frozen* for the whole period. In a market that doubled in a few years, a price frozen for five years was obsolete almost the moment the ink dried.

The result was a table that sat *far* below the real market — commonly cited at around 30% of actual sale prices, and in the hottest central districts, far less. A plot on a prime HCMC street might sell for ₫300 million per square metre while the table said ₫90 million. The gap was not a rounding error. It was the whole game.

This brokenness produced four pathologies, each of which the reform was designed to attack:

**1. Taxes were tiny.** Because the base was 30% of reality, a 2% transfer tax effectively became a ~0.6% transfer tax on the true value. The state collected far less than the headline rate suggested.

**2. Under-declaration was rampant.** When the official floor is already absurdly low, you can declare a price on your sales contract that is also absurdly low — anywhere above the table — and the tax office, checking only against the table, has no basis to object. Two parties would sign a real ₫7 billion deal and a paper ₫2 billion contract. Everyone knew. It was structural.

**3. Compensation was unfair.** The same frozen-low table that made taxes cheap made *compensation* cruel. Families cleared for public projects were paid the frozen price for land worth multiples more, fuelling years of land disputes — Vietnam's single largest category of citizen complaint.

**4. Land was nearly free to hold.** With a tiny non-agricultural land-use tax computed on a tiny base, the annual cost of sitting on land or empty flats rounded to zero. This is the deep link to speculation: when holding costs nothing, the rational move is to buy and *wait*, never to sell or develop. We will return to this.

#### Worked example: the old framework, the under-declared sale

Minh sells an apartment whose true market value is **₫7 billion (≈ \$270,000)**. Under the old framework, the relevant land-price table values the underlying land at a level so low that the *total* table-based valuation for his unit comes to about **₫2 billion**. Because Minh need only declare a price *at or above* the table, he and the buyer agree to write **₫2 billion** on the contract — perfectly legal under the rules as enforced, because it matches the table floor.

His 2% transfer PIT: `2% × ₫2 billion = ₫40 million (≈ \$1,540)`.

On a ₫7 billion transaction, Minh paid the state ₫40 million. That is an *effective* tax of about **0.57%** of the real value — not the 2% the law nominally charges. The 2% headline was a fiction; the frozen table was the reality.

*The old system's headline rates were honest; its base was a polite lie, and the lie is where all the money leaked out.*

It is worth pausing on *why* a sophisticated state would tolerate such an obviously broken system for so long. It was not incompetence — it was a kind of equilibrium that suited many parties at once. Buyers and sellers liked the low taxes. Local officials liked the discretion that came with a system where "real" value was a negotiation. And the central government tolerated the lost revenue because the alternative — a fair, market-tracking valuation of every property — required data infrastructure that simply did not exist, plus the political will to confront the howl of protest that any honest revaluation would trigger. The frozen table was the path of least resistance. What changed by 2024 was that the *costs* of the old equilibrium — unfair compensation fuelling land disputes, a transfer-tax base leaking most of its potential, a speculative property market the state increasingly wanted to cool — finally outweighed the comfort of leaving it alone. Reform is rarely about discovering a better idea; the better idea (an accurate table) was always obvious. It is about the moment the old arrangement's costs become unbearable. For Vietnam, the 2022–23 property and bond crisis — covered in the [property cycles](/blog/trading/real-estate/vietnam-property-cycles-2007-2013-2022) post — was part of what tipped that balance.

## Land Law 2024: the switch to annual tables

On 18 January 2024, Vietnam's National Assembly passed a new **Land Law** (Luật Đất đai 2024). It took effect on **1 August 2024** (brought forward several months from the original January 2025 date — itself a signal of how urgent the government considered it). For our purposes, the single most important thing it did was this:

**It abolished the five-year national price framework and the frozen table, and replaced them with annual land-price tables, repriced every year by each province toward actual market levels.**

That is the whole pivot. Read slowly:

- **Old:** one framework, set every **5 years**, frozen, ~30% of market.
- **New:** a table per province, repriced **every year**, tracking market, climbing toward **70–80%** of market in active areas.

The law instructs provinces to build these tables from real transaction data and market surveys, and to update them annually (with a first set of new tables effective from the start of 2026, after a transition). It also created a national land-price database to support the valuations — the missing infrastructure that had made an honest table impossible before.

![Timeline of the reform arc from the frozen pre-2024 framework price era, through the Land Law 2024 passing in January and taking effect in August, the annual tables rolling out through 2026, the holding tax repeatedly floated but not enacted, and likely next steps after 2026](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-land-law-2024-and-the-coming-property-tax-3.png)

The timeline above lays out the arc, and it is worth narrating because each step has a different legal status:

- **Pre-2024 (framework era):** the frozen five-year table, far below market, tiny taxes. *Was law; now replaced.*
- **January 2024 (Land Law passed):** the National Assembly votes the new regime into law. *Law.*
- **1 August 2024 (effective):** the five-year framework is scrapped; annual tables become the rule. *Law, in force.*
- **2024–2026 (tables roll out):** provinces reprice toward market; HCMC and Hanoi jump first and hardest. *Happening now.*
- **2023–2026 (holding tax floated):** the Ministry of Finance repeatedly proposes a second-home / property tax. **Debated, NOT law.**
- **After 2026 (likely next):** a draft property-tax law *could* reach the Assembly later this decade. *Speculative.*

Notice how the first three are settled law, the fourth is live administration, and only the last two concern the holding tax — and both are explicitly *not law*. This is the spine of the careful distinction this post keeps insisting on.

When HCMC published draft adjusted tables in late 2024, some street prices rose by **multiples** — in a few central locations the increase was reported at five to thirty times the old figure, precisely because the old figure had been so absurdly frozen. The tables were not "raising" prices so much as *finally admitting* what prices already were. But admission has consequences, because of the pipeline we drew: every bill keyed to the table moved with it.

### Why "annual" is the whole point

It is easy to skim past the word *annual* and miss that it is the entire reform. A price set once and then updated *every year* behaves completely differently from one frozen for five. Consider the dynamics. Under the five-year freeze, the table was accurate (if ever) only for an instant after it was set, then drifted further below the galloping market with each passing month — so the *gap* between table and reality was largest right before the next reset, which is exactly when the most value leaked. An *annual* table never drifts more than a year from reality. In a market rising 30–40% a year, as Hanoi and HCMC primary apartments did in 2024–25, that is the difference between a table that lags the market by ~5% and one that lags by ~150%. The reform did not just make the table higher once; it changed the table from a *snapshot that rots* into a *living number that keeps pace.* That is why the effect compounds: every year the market rises, the table rises with it, and the tax base never again falls catastrophically behind. The frozen table was a leak that widened over time; the annual table seals it permanently.

There is a transitional wrinkle worth flagging honestly. Building these annual tables is genuinely hard — provinces need real transaction data, valuation staff, and a process to handle the objections that pour in whenever a street's official price jumps tenfold. So the rollout has been *staggered*: the law took effect August 2024, but the first fully market-tracking annual tables for many provinces land for 2026, with transitional tables and phased adjustments in between. The direction is settled; the speed varies by province. A reader transacting in 2025 may have hit a transitional table; one transacting in 2026 or later faces the full annual regime. Always check *your* province's *current* table — that is the only number that governs your bill.

## How a higher base raises transfer tax, land-use fees, and compensation

Here is the heart of the matter, and the reason a reform that touched *no tax rate* is nonetheless a major tax change. Let us walk each of the four downstream effects.

### Transfer tax

The 2% PIT on a sale is computed on the *higher of contract price or table price*. When the table sat at 30% of market, sellers under-declared to the table and paid 2% of a small number. When the table climbs to 70–80% of market, the *floor itself* is now close to the real price — so under-declaration buys you almost nothing, and the 2% lands on something close to the full value.

![Two bar charts: the left shows the official taxable base jumping from 1.8 billion to 4.8 billion VND against a 6.0 billion market value when the table moves from 30 to 80 percent of market, and the right shows the 2 percent transfer tax rising from 36 to 96 million VND, about 2.7 times the tax at the same rate](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-land-law-2024-and-the-coming-property-tax-4.png)

The chart makes the mechanism unmistakable. Take a plot with a true market value of **₫6.0 billion**. Under the old framework, the table valued it at about **30% → ₫1.8 billion**; the new annual table values it at about **80% → ₫4.8 billion**. The rate never changes — it is 2% in both worlds — but:

- Old tax: `2% × ₫1.8bn = ₫36 million`.
- New tax: `2% × ₫4.8bn = ₫96 million`.

That is **2.7× the tax** from the *same rate*, achieved purely by making the base honest. No politician ever stood up and said "we are tripling the property transfer tax." They said "we are making the price table more accurate." Economically, those are the same sentence.

#### Worked example: Minh's next sale, old base vs new base

Recall Minh's ₫7 billion (≈ \$270,000) apartment. Under the **old framework** he declared ₫2 billion and paid `2% × ₫2bn = ₫40 million (≈ \$1,540)`.

Now he sells a similar ₫7 billion apartment under the **new annual table**. The table for his district has been repriced to roughly 85% of market, so the table-based valuation of his unit is about **₫6 billion** — and because the contract must be at least the table price, under-declaring to ₫2 billion is no longer possible. He declares **₫6 billion** and pays:

`2% × ₫6 billion = ₫120 million (≈ \$4,630)`.

Minh's transfer-tax bill on an economically identical apartment went from **₫40 million to ₫120 million** — a **3×** increase — with the rate frozen at 2% the entire time. The whole change lived in the base.

*When the floor under a tax rises, you do not need a new rate to collect far more — you just need to stop pretending the property is worth less than it is.*

### Land-use fees

This is where ordinary households — not speculators — got the rudest shock of 2024–25, and it is the most-reported real-world effect so far. Recall that converting agricultural land to residential land, or obtaining a residential title, triggers a one-time **land-use fee** scaled off the table. When the table multiplied, so did the fee.

#### Worked example: a land-use fee under the new table

A family outside Hanoi has long held a strip of garden land (agricultural-category) next to their house and wants to convert **100 m²** of it to residential so a child can build. The conversion land-use fee is, simplifying, charged on the *difference* between the residential and agricultural table prices per square metre.

- **Old table:** residential ₫10 million/m², agricultural ₫1 million/m² → fee ≈ `100 × (10 − 1) million = ₫900 million (≈ \$35,000)`.
- **New table** (repriced toward market): residential ₫40 million/m², agricultural ₫2 million/m² → fee ≈ `100 × (40 − 2) million = ₫3.8 billion (≈ \$147,000)`.

The same conversion that would have cost **₫900 million** under the old table now costs roughly **₫3.8 billion** — over **4×** — because the residential table price quadrupled. Across Vietnam in late 2024 and 2025, families reported land-use-fee assessments jumping from the hundreds of millions into the billions, sometimes turning a routine conversion into something simply unaffordable. This was not the speculator the reform aimed at; it was an ordinary household caught by the same dial.

*A "more accurate table" is a blessing for the treasury and, for the household converting land, a bill that can multiply overnight without any rate ever changing.*

### Compensation

The under-appreciated *upside* of the reform: because compensation is also benchmarked to the table, a higher table means *fairer* payouts when the state clears your land. The same dial that raised your transfer tax raised the cheque you get when a highway takes your plot. This is genuinely part of the reform's political justification — and one reason it could pass despite raising several taxes. The frozen table had been a quiet wealth transfer *from* displaced families *to* the projects that displaced them; the annual table narrows that gap.

### Registration fee

The 0.5% registration fee, also floored at the table, rises in lockstep with everything else. It is the smallest of the four, but it compounds the others: on Minh's ₫6 billion declared transfer, the registration fee alone is `0.5% × ₫6bn = ₫30 million`, where on the old ₫2 billion base it was `0.5% × ₫2bn = ₫10 million`.

## The proposed property / second-home tax (this is NOT law)

Now we cross the line from *law* to *proposal*, and the change of register matters. Everything above is enacted and operating. Everything in this section is *debated and unenacted as of mid-2026.* We will say so several times, on purpose.

For years, the Ministry of Finance has floated the idea of a genuine **holding tax** — an annual tax on people who own property they do not live in, or on idle land. The motivations are the textbook ones: cool speculation, discourage land-banking and empty "investment" flats, capture some of the windfall that public infrastructure hands to nearby landowners, and give cash-strapped local governments a stable annual revenue source instead of the boom-and-bust of one-off land-sale revenue. The proposal has appeared in various forms — in draft strategies, in National Assembly discussion, in MoF consultations — across **2023, 2024, 2025, and into 2026**. It has been **studied, debated, and shelved** each time. There is, as of this writing, **no property holding tax law in Vietnam.**

When people in HCMC say "they're bringing in a property tax," they are conflating two different things: the *land-price table reform that already happened* (law) with the *holding tax that keeps being proposed* (not law). The table reform is why your transfer tax went up. The holding tax — the annual bill for simply owning — does not yet exist.

What might the proposed tax look like? The designs the MoF has floated share a common shape, and it is worth understanding because the *shape* tells you the *intent*.

![Matrix showing a possible property tax design that is proposed not law, with rows for first home, second home, and third or idle home, and columns for taxing by number of homes, by total area, and by vacancy, where the first home is exempt and rates rise progressively toward a penalty rate on idle speculative land](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-land-law-2024-and-the-coming-property-tax-5.png)

The matrix above — and again, this is a **proposed design, not law** — shows the three levers the floated proposals tend to combine:

- **By number of homes.** Your *first* home (your only residence) is **exempt** — a generous threshold protects ordinary owners. A *second* home pays a low rate, perhaps **~0.3–0.5%** a year on the value read off the table. A *third or later* home pays a **higher, progressive rate, ~1% or more**.
- **By total area.** Even your home might be exempt only *up to a cap* — say ~150 m² — with the area *above* the cap taxed. A small flat pays nothing; a sprawling villa pays on its excess.
- **By vacancy.** A *lived-in* unit pays no vacancy charge; a unit left **empty 6–12 months** could face a **vacancy surcharge** designed to push it onto the rental or sale market; *idle land left purely to speculate* would be taxed **hardest of all**.

The design philosophy is clear and is shared with most well-built property taxes worldwide: **protect the family's only home, and aim the tax squarely at hoarding and speculation.** The first home stays untaxed; the third empty flat bleeds cash every year.

Why has this proposal — sensible-sounding, common abroad, revenue-rich — been floated and shelved so many times rather than simply passed? The objections are real and they are the same ones we met in Foundations, now sharpened by the specifics:

- **Valuation accuracy (now partly solved).** You cannot fairly levy 0.5% of *value* per year if you do not know the value. This was the killer objection for decades — and it is precisely the one the Land Law 2024 just neutralised by building annual, market-tracking tables. The holding tax's biggest technical obstacle has, quietly, been removed.
- **The asset-rich, cash-poor problem.** A retiree who bought a now-valuable home decades ago, on a modest pension, could owe a real annual bill on a property they have no intention of selling and no cash to pay the tax on. Every serious design needs deferral or exemption mechanisms for exactly this case, and getting them right is genuinely hard.
- **Double-taxation grievance.** Owners argue they already paid tax (registration, transfer) when they *acquired* the property, and a holding tax taxes them again, annually, for the same asset. Economists answer that a holding tax is a tax on the *flow of services* the property provides each year, not a re-tax of the purchase — but the grievance is politically potent regardless of the theory.
- **Market timing.** Introducing a holding tax into a *falling* market risks accelerating the fall (more forced selling); introducing it into a *rising* market invites the charge of killing the boom that built household wealth. There is rarely a "good" moment, which is part of why the proposal keeps getting deferred to "later."

None of these is fatal — every country with a property tax solved them somehow — but together they explain the *repeated deferral.* The idea is not dead; it is waiting for the politics to line up behind the now-built machinery.

#### Worked example: a proposed second-home tax on a three-flat speculator

Meet our second recurring character: a speculator — call her **Lan** — who owns three HCMC flats, each worth about **₫5 billion (≈ \$193,000)**. Two sit empty; she is waiting for prices to rise.

**Today (law):** her annual holding cost is essentially the non-agricultural land-use tax, on the order of **0.05% of a low state price**. Across three flats it might total a few million dong a year — call it **₫5–10 million** all in. Rounding error. She holds indefinitely at near-zero carry.

**Under a *proposed* second-home tax** (illustrative, NOT law), using the matrix above and values read off the *new* annual table:

- Flat 1 (her home): **exempt**.
- Flat 2 (extra): `~0.5% × ₫5bn = ₫25 million/year (≈ \$965)`.
- Flat 3 (extra *and* idle): a higher progressive rate plus a vacancy surcharge, say `~1% × ₫5bn = ₫50 million/year (≈ \$1,930)`.

Her annual carrying cost jumps from a few million dong to roughly **₫75 million a year (≈ \$2,900)** — and that is *every year, whether or not prices rise.* If the market is flat for three years, she has paid ₫225 million simply to wait. That is the whole point of a holding tax: it puts a *clock* on speculation.

*A transaction tax punishes selling; a holding tax punishes waiting — and waiting is exactly what a speculator does.*

## How a holding tax would change speculation and land-banking

To see *why* reformers want a holding tax so badly, you have to understand the behaviour the current near-zero holding cost creates — and then watch what a holding tax does to it.

![Before and after comparison of a speculator: today three empty flats cost almost nothing to hold so the incentive is to hoard and wait, versus after a second-home tax where each extra flat carries an annual bill of tens of millions of dong, creating an incentive to rent or sell](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-land-law-2024-and-the-coming-property-tax-6.png)

On the left is today's incentive structure. Flat 1 is the home — no annual tax. Flats 2 and 3 sit empty, each costing only the tiny ~0.03–0.15% land-use tax to carry. The holding cost rounds to zero, so the rational answer to "why ever sell?" is "never — just hold and wait." This is the deep economic reason Vietnamese cities are dotted with dark, empty "investment" apartments and why *đất nền* (raw land plots) get bought and sat on for years. When carry is free, the optimal strategy is to *not transact* — to hoard scarce urban land out of productive use and wait for someone else's infrastructure to lift its price.

On the right is the world *after* a holding tax (proposed). Flat 1 stays exempt. But Flat 2 now costs ~0.5% a year — ~₫25 million on a ₫5 billion flat — and Flat 3, idle, costs a higher rate plus a vacancy charge, perhaps ₫50 million or more. Suddenly an empty flat is not a free option on future appreciation; it is a *bleeding* asset. The incentive flips: **rent it or sell it.** Empty units now cost money every year, so they get pushed onto the market — which is exactly the supply the cities need.

This is the textbook case for a property/holding tax, and it is the same logic everywhere from Singapore's punitive rates on non-owner-occupied homes to vacancy taxes in Vancouver and Paris. A holding tax converts the *option value of waiting* into a *recurring cost of waiting*, and in doing so it nudges land and flats out of speculative storage and into use. For the universal version of this argument — tax incidence, capitalisation into prices, who actually pays — see [how tax shapes behaviour: speculation and hoarding](/blog/trading/real-estate/how-tax-shapes-behavior-speculation-and-hoarding).

#### Worked example: the cooling effect on a đất nền plot's holding cost

A speculator buys a **₫4 billion (≈ \$154,000)** đất nền plot in a satellite province on a rumour of a new highway, intending to hold and flip.

**Today:** his annual carry is the tiny land-use tax — call it **0.1% × a low state base ≈ ₫1–2 million a year.** If he holds for three years, his total holding cost is perhaps **₫5 million** — utterly negligible against a hoped-for ₫2 billion gain. The math screams "hold."

**Under a proposed idle-land tax** (illustrative, NOT law) at, say, **1% a year** on a table value of ₫3 billion (75% of market): `1% × ₫3bn = ₫30 million/year`. Over three years that is **₫90 million** — eighteen times the old cost. Now the calculus tightens: if the highway rumour fizzles and the plot does not appreciate, he is steadily losing real money. The plot is no longer a free lottery ticket; it has a *carrying cost* that compounds against him every year he refuses to build or sell.

*Idle land is only worth hoarding when hoarding is free; put a yearly price on the waiting and the speculative plot starts to look like the liability it economically is.*

The reason this matters for *prices*, not just behaviour, is **capitalisation**: a permanent annual holding tax gets baked into a *lower purchase price*. If buyers know they will owe 1% a year forever, they pay less today — roughly the present value of that future tax stream less — and the one-time hit lands on whoever owns the property on announcement day. That is why every serious holding-tax proposal triggers fierce lobbying from current owners: they, not future buyers, eat the capitalised loss.

## Where Vietnam sits, and where the reform can go

How light *is* Vietnam's holding-tax burden, really? Light enough that the room to move is enormous.

![Bar chart comparing the annual holding-tax burden as an effective percent of property value, showing Vietnam near 0.1 percent today, China pilot cities around 0.8 percent, Singapore owner-occupiers around 0.6 percent, and the US state and local average around 1.1 percent, illustrating that Vietnam sits near zero with lots of room to move](/imgs/blogs/vietnam-land-law-2024-and-the-coming-property-tax-7.png)

The chart (illustrative effective rates, as-of mid-2026) tells the strategic story:

- **Vietnam (today): ~0.1%** of value per year — effectively nothing.
- **China (pilot cities): ~0.8%** — Shanghai and Chongqing have run property-tax pilots on new and second homes since 2011, in the rough 0.4–1.2% range.
- **Singapore (owner-occupier): ~0.6%** — a steeply *progressive* tax that is gentle on modest owner-occupied homes and brutal on high-value and non-owner-occupied property.
- **USA (state/local average): ~1.1%** — the workhorse that funds American towns and schools, levied annually on market value.

Vietnam sits at the far left, near the floor. Even a modest holding tax — half a percent on second homes — would move it only a fraction of the way toward its peers while raising meaningful revenue and biting on speculation. This is the "room to move" the chart annotates, and it is why the direction of travel is so clearly *upward* even if the timing is uncertain.

One honest caveat on these figures: they are *representative effective rates*, not single official statistics, and they vary enormously by city, value, and tier. The comparison is meant to show *orders of magnitude* — Vietnam is ~10× lighter than the US on holding — not to be read to the second decimal place.

## The reform arc, 2024 → 2026, and the likely next step

Let us assemble the timeline into a single narrative, because the *sequencing* is the strategy.

**Step 1 (done, law): fix the base.** Land Law 2024 replaced the frozen framework with annual tables. This was the *prerequisite* for everything else. You cannot levy a fair 1%-of-value holding tax until you actually *know* the value of every property — and the frozen table guaranteed you did not. By forcing provinces to build and annually update market-tracking tables (backed by a new national land database), Vietnam built the *valuation infrastructure* a holding tax would require. The table reform is not just a transfer-tax increase; it is the **scaffolding** for a future holding tax.

**Step 2 (happening, law in force): let the tables climb.** Through 2024–2026, provinces reprice toward market, with the first fully new annual tables landing for 2026. Transfer taxes, land-use fees, registration fees, and compensation all rise. Households feel the land-use-fee shock; sellers feel the transfer-tax shock; displaced families get fairer compensation.

**Step 3 (proposed, NOT law): the holding tax.** With an accurate, annual valuation finally in place, the long-floated second-home / idle-land tax becomes *administratively feasible* for the first time. The MoF keeps proposing it; it keeps being debated and deferred. A draft property-tax law *could* reach the National Assembly later this decade — but as of mid-2026, it has not, and predicting Vietnamese legislative timing is a fool's errand.

So the arc is: **fix the valuation (done) → raise the transaction taxes through the table (happening) → add a holding tax (proposed, unenacted).** Whether step 3 ever fully arrives is genuinely uncertain — it has stalled repeatedly on exactly the political and administrative grounds we listed in Foundations. But step 1 has already quietly removed the biggest *technical* obstacle. That is why thoughtful observers treat the holding tax not as a question of *if* the capability exists, but of *when* the politics allow it.

#### Worked example: a rough national revenue estimate

How much money is on the table? Let us do a deliberately rough, illustrative back-of-envelope — flagged as such, not a forecast.

Suppose Vietnam levied a **0.3% annual holding tax** on the value of *second and subsequent homes and idle land* only (first homes exempt). The total value of Vietnamese residential real estate runs into the *hundreds of billions of dollars*; suppose the slice that is second-plus homes and idle speculative land is, conservatively, **\$100 billion** in market value.

`0.3% × \$100 billion = \$300 million per year`.

At a higher 0.5% rate on a larger \$200 billion taxable slice: `0.5% × \$200 billion = \$1 billion per year`. These are crude figures — the real taxable base, exemptions, and compliance would all move them — but they show the order of magnitude: a well-designed holding tax on second-plus property could plausibly raise *hundreds of millions to low billions of US dollars a year*, a material new revenue stream for local governments currently dependent on volatile one-off land sales.

*Even a gentle holding tax, aimed only at the homes people don't live in, taps a base so large that the revenue is serious — which is exactly why the idea will not go away even as it keeps being deferred.*

## Common misconceptions

Property tax in Vietnam is a swamp of half-truths. Here are the ones to kill.

**1. "Vietnam now has a property tax." — No. This is the single biggest error.** As of mid-2026, Vietnam has **no broad annual property holding tax.** What changed is the *land-price table* (Land Law 2024), which raised the *base* for the taxes that already existed — transfer, land-use fee, registration, compensation. The annual *holding* tax on simply owning a home is **proposed and repeatedly debated, but not enacted.** If someone tells you they're now "paying a property tax," they almost certainly mean a higher transfer tax or land-use fee driven by the new table — not an annual ownership tax, which does not exist.

**2. "The rates went up, so this was a tax hike." — The rates did *not* change; the *base* did.** Transfer PIT is still 2%, registration is still 0.5%. The reform made the price *table* those rates apply to much larger. A bigger base at the same rate is a tax increase in economic substance but not in the law's letter — which is precisely what made it politically passable.

**3. "The new tables just made property more expensive." — The tables didn't set market prices; they admitted them.** The annual table tracks *toward* what properties already sell for. The old table's frozen-low number was the fiction; the new number is closer to reality. Property is not more expensive *because of* the table — the table is finally catching up to prices the market set on its own.

**4. "A holding tax would crush ordinary homeowners." — Every floated design exempts the first home.** The proposals aim at *second-plus* homes, idle land, and vacancy — at hoarding, not at families living in their one apartment. A well-built holding tax is *progressive*: gentle-to-zero on your only home, steep on the speculator's third empty flat. The political risk is real, but the design intent is explicitly to spare the ordinary owner-occupier.

**5. "Under-declaring the sale price still works." — It works far less well now.** The whole point of under-declaration was that the table floor was so low you could write a fictitiously small contract above it. With the table now near 70–80% of market, the floor itself is close to the real price, so under-declaring buys you little. The reform quietly closed the loophole by raising the floor under it.

**6. "Compensation is just another way the state takes from you." — The same reform *raised* compensation.** Because payouts for cleared land are benchmarked to the table, the higher annual table means *fairer*, larger compensation for families displaced by public projects — a genuine improvement and one of the reform's real justifications, not just a revenue grab.

## How it shows up in real markets

Abstractions become concrete fast in property. Here is where the table reform and the proposed holding tax are already visible — or soon will be.

**Land-use fees jumping for ordinary households (2024–25).** The most-reported real-world effect so far has nothing to do with speculators. As provinces published new tables, families converting agricultural land to residential — a routine step when a child wants to build on family land — found their land-use-fee assessments multiplying. Bills that would have been hundreds of millions of dong under the old table arrived in the *billions* under the new one. In several reported cases, households that had planned conversions for years found them suddenly unaffordable, and local authorities faced pressure to phase in or cap the increases. This is the table reform's sharpest immediate human edge: it bites the asset-rich-but-cash-poor household, not the cash-rich speculator, first.

**đất nền speculators and the carry question.** *Đất nền* — raw land plots in satellite provinces — have driven Vietnam's most violent speculative fevers (the 2018 HCMC-periphery frenzy is the canonical case). The entire trade rests on near-zero carry: buy a plot on a highway rumour, sit on it for free, flip it to the next believer. A holding tax on idle land — repeatedly floated, never yet passed — would directly attack this trade by putting an annual cost on the waiting. Even before any holding tax, the *higher transfer tax* from the new table raises the cost of *flipping*, taking some froth out of the rapid buy-sell churn that defines a đất nền mania. For the full anatomy of these cycles, see [Vietnam's property cycles: 2007, 2011–13, 2018, and the 2022–23 freeze](/blog/trading/real-estate/vietnam-property-cycles-2007-2013-2022).

**Developers' cost base.** Developers acquire land and pay land-use fees to convert and title it. When the table climbs, their *land cost* climbs — the land-use fee on a large project can rise into the hundreds of billions of dong. That cost flows straight into the price of the finished apartments, which is one (often overlooked) channel by which the table reform feeds *higher primary-market prices*: the developer's higher land-conversion bill is passed to buyers. With Hanoi primary apartment prices averaging ~₫100 million/m² and HCMC ~₫111 million/m² (≈ \$4,222/m²) in 2025, even small percentage additions to the land cost base are large absolute sums.

**The transfer-tax base closing the under-declaration gap.** For years, Vietnam's property transfer-tax revenue badly undershot what the headline 2% rate implied, because everyone declared to the frozen floor. As tables rise toward market, the *effective* transfer-tax take rises toward the *nominal* 2% — a revenue gain the state collects without ever touching the rate. This is the cleanest example of the "raise the base, not the rate" strategy in action, and it is already showing up in transfer-tax collections.

**The fairness story on compensation.** In provinces where land has been cleared for major infrastructure — ring roads, the North-South expressway, industrial zones — the higher annual table means displaced families are paid closer to market value than the frozen table ever allowed. This is the reform's most sympathetic face and a real part of why it could pass: it is not *only* a tax increase; it is also a long-overdue correction to one of Vietnam's deepest land-related grievances.

**The global mirror.** Vietnam is walking a path others have walked. China has run Shanghai/Chongqing property-tax *pilots* since 2011 without ever generalising them nationally — a reminder that even a determined state can stall a holding tax for over a decade on political grounds. Singapore funds itself partly through a steeply progressive property tax that spares modest owner-occupiers and hammers investors and luxury homes — close to the design Vietnam's MoF keeps sketching. The US runs the workhorse ~1.1%-of-value annual tax that funds local schools. Vietnam, at ~0.1%, is the global outlier on the *low* side, with the most room to move — and a newly built valuation system that, for the first time, makes moving administratively possible.

## When this matters / Further reading

This matters to you the moment you *transact*, not just the moment a holding tax (maybe) arrives. If you are buying or selling Vietnamese property, the new annual table is *already* lifting your transfer tax, your registration fee, and — if you are converting or titling land — your land-use fee, even though no rate changed and no "property tax" exists. Budget for a base that tracks the market, not the frozen fiction your parents transacted against. If you are a longer-horizon holder or a speculator, watch the holding-tax debate carefully but do not panic-trade on it: it has been proposed and shelved repeatedly, it is *not* law, and every serious design exempts the first home. And if you are displaced by a public project, the same reform that raised your transfer tax should now pay you fairer compensation.

The two-sentence summary to carry away: **the Land Law 2024's annual tables are law and are quietly raising every tax keyed to the land-price table, by lifting the base while leaving the rates alone — and the property holding tax is a serious, repeatedly-floated proposal that is not, as of mid-2026, enacted.** Vietnam has not yet ended its cheap-to-hold era. It has built the machinery that could.
