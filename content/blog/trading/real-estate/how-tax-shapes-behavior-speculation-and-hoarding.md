> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — A property tax is never *just* a way to raise money. It is an incentive, and every owner in the market quietly reorganises their behaviour around it. The *kind* of tax a country leans on decides whether land gets hoarded, left empty, traded freely, or put to work.
>
> - **Near-zero holding taxes** (Vietnam's effective ~0.03–0.15% of a low state price) make it almost free to sit on idle land, so investors *land-bank* — they buy plots and empty flats and just wait. This is the engine behind speculation and "ghost" units.
> - **High transaction taxes** (Vietnam's flat 2% on every sale, Singapore's stamp duties) are a toll on moving. Owners trade less to dodge the bite, so the market gets *thin* — few sellers, low volume, stale prices.
> - **High capital-gains taxes** create the **lock-in effect**: because selling triggers a tax on the gain, owners with big paper profits simply refuse to sell. Listings vanish, owners get stranded in homes that no longer fit, and the whole market stiffens.
> - The economists' prescription is the *reverse* of what most places do: **tax the holding of land heavily** (land can't flee and the tax doesn't shrink the supply, so there's no waste) and **tax transactions lightly** (so property changes hands freely). Vietnam runs almost exactly the wrong mix.
> - The one number to remember: at a **1% annual holding tax**, sitting on a ₫16 billion (≈ \$618,000) idle plot costs **₫160 million a year** — versus a few hundred thousand đồng today. That single change turns free hoarding into an expensive clock.

In 2025, our recurring HCMC buyer **Minh** paid ₫16 billion (≈ \$618,000) for a narrow but central plot in Ho Chi Minh City. He had no plan to build on it. He just wanted to *own* it — to watch the number climb behind a sheet-metal fence. And here is the thing that makes his strategy so rational: from the day he signs to the day he eventually sells, the Vietnamese state asks Minh for almost nothing. The annual land-use tax on that prime plot runs to a few hundred thousand đồng — the price of a couple of lunches. So Minh sits. So do tens of thousands of investors like him.

Now meet **Dana**, a US investor who buys a \$300,000 house in a typical American suburb. Every single year her county sends a bill for about **\$3,300** — roughly 1.1% of the home's value — whether she lives in it, rents it, or leaves it empty. If Dana tried Minh's strategy, that bill would bleed her every year she failed to put the property to use. She *can't* afford to just sit. The tax won't let her.

Same human impulse — own land, watch it appreciate. Wildly different behaviour, and the difference is almost entirely about *which taxes apply and how heavy they are*. That is the whole subject of this post. A tax is not a neutral pipe that moves money from your pocket to the treasury. A tax is a price the government attaches to an action, and people respond to prices. Put a price on *selling* and people sell less. Put a price on *holding idle land* and people stop hoarding it. Put a big price on *realising a profit* and people refuse to take the profit at all. The property market you see — speculative or productive, liquid or frozen, full of ghost towers or full of homes — is to a large degree the market the tax code has *designed*.

The figure above is the mental model for the entire post. Down the left are the four property taxes that matter. Across the top: what each one actually taxes, the behaviour it pushes owners toward, and the effect that has on the whole market. A **transfer tax** on the sale makes owners trade less, thinning liquidity. A **holding tax** on the yearly act of owning pushes idle land into use. A **capital-gains tax** on the profit freezes owners who don't want to trigger the bill — the lock-in effect. A **vacancy tax** on emptiness pushes empty units back into circulation. We are going to build each of these from zero, ground every one in numbers with Minh and Dana, and then arrive at the punchline: the tax mix economists recommend is almost the exact opposite of the one Vietnam — and many other places — actually runs.

## Foundations: a tax is an incentive, not just a bill

Before we touch real estate, let's get one idea airtight, because everything else hangs off it: **a tax changes behaviour.** This sounds obvious, but its consequences are deep and constantly underrated.

### Tax as a price on an action

When the government taxes something, it is putting a price on doing that thing. Tax cigarettes, and some people smoke less. Tax sugary drinks, and some people switch to water. Tax labour income heavily, and at the margin some people work fewer hours or move their income offshore. The tax raises money, yes — but it *also* discourages whatever it lands on. Economists call the discouragement the **behavioural response** to a tax, and it is the single most important concept in this entire post.

The size of that response depends on how easy it is to *avoid* the taxed thing. If you can dodge the tax by changing your behaviour cheaply, you will, and the tax raises little money while distorting a lot. If you *can't* dodge it — if the thing being taxed can't move, hide, or shrink — then the tax raises money *without* changing much, which is the holy grail of tax design. Hold that distinction. It is the key that unlocks why land is special.

### Deadweight loss: the waste a tax can create

Here is the part most people never learn. When a tax discourages a useful activity, society loses the value of the trades that no longer happen. Suppose a builder would have happily put up an apartment block that buyers wanted — but a tax on construction makes the project just barely unprofitable, so it never gets built. Nobody pays that tax (the building doesn't exist), the treasury collects nothing from it, and yet the buyers don't get their apartments and the builder doesn't get the work. That lost value — trades that *would* have benefited everyone but didn't happen because of the tax — is called **deadweight loss**. It is pure waste: a cost to society that nobody collects as revenue.

A *good* tax minimises deadweight loss. It raises money while distorting behaviour as little as possible. And the cleanest way to do that is to tax something whose supply *can't shrink in response* — because then the tax can't scare any supply away, and there's nothing to lose.

Hold these two yardsticks side by side, because we'll grade every property tax on them: **how much revenue does it raise?** and **how much waste (deadweight loss) does it create per dollar raised?** A tax can score well on both, badly on both, or — and this is the interesting case — *well on revenue but terribly on waste*. A transaction tax is the classic well-on-revenue, terrible-on-waste tax: it collects cleanly from every sale, but each dollar it raises comes bundled with a pile of cancelled trades. A land-holding tax is the rare win-win: it raises real revenue *and* creates essentially no waste. Most of this post is an exercise in scoring the four property taxes against these two yardsticks, and the scores are what produce the surprising prescription at the end.

### Taxing a stock versus taxing a flow

There are two fundamentally different things a tax can land on, and the distinction runs through the rest of this post.

- A **flow** is an *event* — something that happens at a point in time. A sale is a flow. A profit being realised is a flow. Income earned this year is a flow. You tax a flow *when the event occurs*: a tax on selling only bites when you sell.
- A **stock** is a *thing you hold* — a standing quantity that exists whether or not anything happens. The land you own is a stock. The flat sitting in your name is a stock. You tax a stock *continuously, just for holding it*: an annual holding tax bills you every year regardless of whether you do anything.

This is not a pedantic distinction. It is the difference between a tax you can avoid by *not acting* (a flow tax — just don't sell, and you never pay) and a tax you can only avoid by *getting rid of the thing* (a stock tax — the meter runs until you sell or develop). A flow tax on transactions *rewards inaction*: the way to dodge it is to sit still. A stock tax on holding *punishes inaction*: the way to dodge it is to do something useful with the asset. Same money raised, opposite behaviour. We will see this flip drive everything.

### The four property taxes, defined

With that scaffolding, here are the four taxes the post turns on, each defined from zero:

- **Transfer tax (a transaction tax):** a charge levied *once, when a property changes hands*. In Vietnam it's a flat **2% personal-income tax on the declared sale price**, plus a **0.5% registration fee** ("lệ phí trước bạ"). It taxes a *flow* — the sale event. Avoid it by not selling.
- **Holding tax (a property or land tax):** a charge levied *every year, just for owning*. The US property tax (~1.1% of market value annually) is the classic example. Vietnam's non-agricultural land-use tax is the near-absent version: **~0.03–0.15% of a deliberately low state price**, effectively zero. It taxes a *stock* — the land you hold. You can't avoid it by sitting still; the meter runs.
- **Capital-gains tax:** a charge on the *profit* when you sell — the difference between what you bought for and what you sold for. It taxes a *flow* (the realised gain), and critically, the gain is only *realised*, and the tax only *triggered*, **when you actually sell**. Hold forever and you never pay. (Vietnam's 2% transfer tax is levied on the gross price, not the gain, so it behaves like a transaction tax; the US, by contrast, has an explicit capital-gains tax of up to ~20% federal on the profit. We'll treat the "gain tax" as the general mechanism.)
- **Vacancy tax:** a charge on *units left empty*. Vancouver's Empty Homes Tax (started at 1% of assessed value, later raised to 3%) is the canonical example. Vietnam has *none* — leaving a flat empty costs nothing. It taxes a *stock*, specifically the *idle* stock, and pushes owners to fill or sell.

Now we can run the experiments. We'll take each tax in turn, hold the others fixed, and ask: *what does this tax, on its own, make owners do?*

## Low holding tax → land-banking and idle land

Start with the holding tax, because it is the one Vietnam essentially doesn't have, and its absence explains more about the local market than any other single fact.

Recall the structure: a holding tax is a *stock* tax. It bills you every year just for owning, and you can only avoid it by getting rid of the asset or putting it to use. Now ask what happens when that tax is **near zero**.

### The holding cost of land

Every asset has a **holding cost** — what it costs you, per year, to keep owning it. For a car it's depreciation, insurance, and maintenance. For land, the natural holding costs are tiny: dirt doesn't rust, doesn't need feeding, doesn't depreciate. The main holding cost of land is whatever the *government* puts on it. If the annual holding tax is near zero, then the holding cost of land is near zero. And an asset that costs nothing to hold, in a market where prices generally rise, is an almost irresistible thing to *hoard*.

This is **land-banking**: buying land (or finished units) not to use, but to hold and wait for appreciation. The bank in the name is the land itself — a store of value you sit on. When holding is free and appreciation is likely, land-banking is not greed; it's the *rational* response to the incentives in front of you. Minh isn't being foolish. He's being perfectly logical given the price the system has set for what he's doing — which is roughly nothing.

![A before-and-after diagram contrasting a near-zero holding tax where idle land costs almost nothing against a one percent annual tax that meters the land and forces it toward use](/imgs/blogs/how-tax-shapes-behavior-speculation-and-hoarding-2.png)

The figure above runs the experiment on a single plot. On the left, under Vietnam's near-zero holding tax, Minh's ₫16 billion idle central plot costs roughly **0.05% of a low state price** to hold — a few hundred thousand đồng a year. His incentive: sitting is nearly free, so land-bank and wait. On the right, under a **1% annual holding tax**, the *same* plot now costs **1% of ₫16 billion = ₫160 million every year** just to keep idle. His incentive flips entirely: idle land now bleeds cash, so build it, rent it, or sell it to someone who will. Nothing about the land changed. The *clock* changed.

#### Worked example: the cost of holding Minh's idle plot

Minh owns a ₫16 billion (≈ \$618,000) central HCMC plot, empty, behind a fence.

**Under Vietnam today (~0% holding tax).** The non-agricultural land-use tax is levied on a *state-set* land price that is deliberately far below market — and at a rate around 0.03–0.15%. Say the state price is a fraction of market and the effective rate lands near **0.05% of ₫16 billion ≈ ₫8 million a year** as a generous upper estimate; in practice it is often a few hundred thousand đồng. Call it, charitably, ₫8 million. Against a plot worth ₫16 billion, that is **0.05% per year**. If the land appreciates even 10% in a year — ₫1.6 billion — the holding tax eats **half of one percent** of the gain. The carry is, for all practical purposes, free.

**Under a 1% annual holding tax.** Now the bill is **1% × ₫16 billion = ₫160 million per year** (≈ \$6,180). Hold it idle for five years and you've paid **₫800 million** — and that's before counting the opportunity cost of the ₫16 billion sitting in dirt. Suddenly doing *nothing* has a large, visible, recurring price.

Watch the behavioural flip. At ₫8 million a year, the rational move is to hold and wait — the cost of patience is trivial. At ₫160 million a year, every month of idleness is a month of bleeding, and the rational move is to *do something*: build a townhouse you can rent, sell the plot to a developer who will build now, or at minimum lease it. The tax didn't confiscate the land. It just made *idleness* expensive, which is exactly the point.

*A near-zero holding tax makes hoarding free; a one percent holding tax puts a meter on the land and forces the owner to choose between using it and paying.*

### Why this drives speculation specifically

There is a subtle, important consequence. When holding land is free, you can profit from real estate *without producing anything*. You don't have to build, rent, manage tenants, or add a single square metre of housing. You just buy, wait, and sell to the next person for more. That is **speculation** in its purest form: betting on the price, not on the use.

A holding tax attacks this directly. It makes the pure wait-and-flip strategy *carry a cost* the productive strategy doesn't have to bear in the same way — because a developer who builds and rents at least generates income to cover the tax, while the pure speculator generates nothing and just bleeds. The holding tax tilts the field away from passive hoarding and toward active use. Remove the holding tax, and you've tilted it the other way: you've made the market *safe for speculators* and slightly hostile to builders, which is one reason land prices can race ahead of any plausible use-value when holding is cheap. (This is also why supply matters so much — when speculation is cheap and [land is hard to bring into use, prices can detach from incomes for years](/blog/trading/real-estate/supply-elasticity-why-some-cities-boom-and-bust).)

### The compounding maths of cheap carry

There's a quieter reason near-zero holding costs are so combustible, and it's worth seeing the arithmetic. A speculator's *return on holding land* is roughly the appreciation rate minus the carry cost. When carry is essentially zero, the speculator keeps *all* of the appreciation. When carry is 1% of value a year, the speculator keeps appreciation minus 1%. That sounds small, but it changes the *whole population of people for whom land-banking is worth it.*

Consider a plot expected to appreciate **8% a year**. With zero carry, the holder nets ~8% — a great return for doing literally nothing, so a huge crowd of investors piles in. With a 1% holding tax, the holder nets ~7% — still fine, but now they're also handing 1% a year to the city, and the *marginal* speculators (the ones who only bought because the carry was free) drop out. With a 2% holding tax, the net falls to ~6%, and a passive flat with no income looks distinctly worse than an asset that actually pays you. Crucially, the holding tax hurts the *pure* speculator far more than the *productive* owner, because the productive owner is collecting rent to offset the tax while the speculator collects nothing. The tax doesn't ban speculation; it just stops *subsidising* it relative to use — and at the margin, that's enough to redirect a meaningful slice of capital from sitting-on-dirt to building-on-it.

This is why the *level* of the holding tax is not a detail. It sets the entry bar for the speculative crowd. Drop it to zero and you invite everyone with spare capital to park it in land; raise it to 1% and you quietly evict the speculators whose only edge was free carry, leaving the field to people who'll actually do something with the site.

## Vacancy and ghost units: hoarding, but for finished buildings

Land-banking has a tower-shaped cousin. The same near-zero holding cost that makes investors sit on empty *plots* makes them sit on empty *apartments*. The result is the eerie phenomenon of the **ghost unit**: a finished, sold, fully-owned flat that nobody ever lives in — bought purely as a store of value, lights off, year after year.

### Why empty flats happen

Think about what an empty flat *is* to an investor in a low-holding-tax country. It's a chunk of value that, like gold or a savings deposit, tends to hold or grow its worth — except, unlike a savings deposit, *nobody is charging you to keep it*. You don't have to find a tenant, deal with wear and tear, or accept a rental yield that in many hot markets is a paltry 2–4% a year. You can simply hold the *capital appreciation* with zero hassle and zero annual cost. The flat becomes a vault.

Multiply that decision across a whole development and you get a building — sometimes a whole district — that is sold out but dark at night. This is not hypothetical. It is a documented pattern across hot Asian property markets, and it sits on exactly the same incentive as Minh's empty plot: **when holding costs nothing, emptiness is free.**

#### Worked example: the near-zero carry on a vacant HCMC flat

Suppose an investor buys a ₫7 billion (≈ \$270,000) 70 m² flat in Hanoi at 2025 prices and leaves it empty.

**Holding cost in Vietnam.** Property tax on it is effectively zero. Maintenance fees and a sinking-fund contribution to the building might run ₫1–2 million a month, call it **₫18 million a year** — about **0.26%** of the flat's value. That's the *entire* annual cost of keeping ₫7 billion of value sitting idle as a vault.

**What renting it would require.** To rent it, the investor takes on tenant search, agent fees, furnishing, repairs, void periods, and the management hassle — all to earn a gross yield that in central Hanoi is often **~3–4%**, much of which evaporates into those costs. The *net* hassle-adjusted return on renting may be barely above the appreciation they'd capture by doing nothing.

**The decision.** With holding free, the rational investor often just leaves it empty: capture the price appreciation, skip the hassle, pay the trivial ₫18 million carry. Now imagine a **1% holding tax** (or a vacancy tax) lands: that's another **₫70 million a year** on top. Suddenly the maths inverts — ₫70 million is a lot to pay for the privilege of an *empty* flat, and renting it (which at least throws off ₫200–280 million gross) becomes the obvious move. The unit re-enters the housing supply.

*An empty flat in a no-holding-tax world is a free vault; put any meaningful annual cost on it and the vault has to start earning its keep, which usually means a tenant.*

### The vacancy tax as a targeted fix

A **vacancy tax** is the surgical version of a holding tax: instead of taxing *all* property every year, it taxes only the *empty* units, leaving occupied homes alone. The logic is to push idle housing back into use without burdening people who actually live in or rent out their homes. Vancouver pioneered the modern version in 2017 with its Empty Homes Tax (1% of assessed value, later raised to 3%); the policy is credited with returning thousands of units to the rental market. We'll return to the real-world record later, but the mechanism is the one we just computed: put a price on emptiness, and emptiness gets scarcer.

The vacancy tax's weakness is enforcement, not theory. Because it taxes only *empty* units, the tax authority has to actually *know* which units are empty — and owners have every incentive to claim occupancy they don't have, leaving a token bed and a flickering light. A broad holding tax sidesteps this entirely: it bills *every* unit regardless of occupancy, so there's nothing to verify and nothing to game — the empty flat and the lived-in one get the same bill, and the empty one simply feels it more relative to the (zero) income it produces. That's the trade-off between the two tools: a vacancy tax is politically gentler (it only hits the "deserving" empty unit) but administratively fiddly; a holding tax is politically harder (it hits everyone) but clean to collect and impossible to dodge by faking a tenant.

## High transaction tax → low liquidity

Now flip to a flow tax. A **transaction tax** (transfer tax, stamp duty, the 2% Vietnamese PIT on a sale) is charged *each time property changes hands*. Unlike the holding tax, you can avoid it completely by one simple move: **don't sell.** And that's exactly the problem.

### Friction and the deep-versus-thin market

Every transaction tax is a form of **friction** — sand in the gears of trading. Friction reduces how often people are willing to transact, because each trade now has to clear a higher bar to be worthwhile. To see why that matters, you need one concept: **liquidity**, which in a market means *how easily you can buy or sell without moving the price much*. A **deep** (liquid) market has many buyers and sellers, lots of transactions, and prices that update quickly and reliably. A **thin** (illiquid) market has few participants, sparse transactions, and prices that are stale, laggy, and hard to trust because so little actually trades.

Transaction taxes push markets from deep toward thin. The more it costs to trade, the fewer trades happen, the thinner the market gets. And a thin property market is a genuinely worse market: prices are unreliable (when did a comparable flat *last* sell?), people who need to move can't easily, and the matching of homes to the people who'd value them most gets gummed up.

![A before-and-after diagram contrasting a low transaction tax where owners trade freely against a high transaction tax that acts as a toll, cutting the number of deals and thinning the market](/imgs/blogs/how-tax-shapes-behavior-speculation-and-hoarding-3.png)

The figure above runs the experiment. On the left, with a low transaction tax, selling to upgrade carries a tiny tax bill, so owners trade often — they relocate, resize, and adjust freely as life changes. The result is a deep market: many buyers and sellers, prices discovered fast. On the right, a high transaction tax turns every sale into a *toll*. Selling to upgrade now costs 2% of the price each time, so owners stay put to dodge the repeated bite. The result is a thin market: few sellers, low volume, stale and laggy prices.

#### Worked example: how a 2% transaction tax cuts trading

Minh's friend **Lan** owns a ₫5 billion (≈ \$193,000) HCMC apartment and is thinking about upgrading to a bigger place.

**With no transaction tax.** Selling costs her almost nothing in tax. If a slightly better-fitting home comes along — a bit more space, a better location for her commute — she can sell and rebuy with only the usual agent fees. Over a decade, as her life changes (kids, a new job across town, ageing parents), she might trade *three or four times*, each time landing in a home that fits a little better. The market stays liquid because people like Lan move freely.

**With Vietnam's 2% transfer tax (plus 0.5% registration fee = 2.5% all-in on the buy/sell round trip).** Now each sale costs Lan **2% of ₫5 billion = ₫100 million** in PIT, and rebuying costs another **0.5% registration ≈ ₫25 million** on the new place — call it **₫125 million per move**, every time, before agent fees. Suddenly a "small upgrade" that improves her life by, say, ₫80 million of value isn't worth it — the tax alone eats more than the benefit. So Lan stays put. The marginal moves — the ones that would have nudged her into a better-fitting home — simply don't happen. Over a decade she trades *once* instead of three times.

**The aggregate effect.** Multiply Lan across a city. Every owner sets a higher bar for moving; the small, life-fit trades vanish; volume collapses. Where the market would have had, say, 100,000 transactions a year, it now has 60,000. Prices update less often, mismatches persist, and "what's it worth?" becomes genuinely hard to answer because so little trades.

*A transaction tax doesn't just take 2% from each sale — it cancels the marginal sales entirely, and those vanished trades are the market's liquidity.*

### Why this is a worse tax than it looks

Here's the trap with transaction taxes: they *feel* fair (you only pay when you actually transact, when you "have the money") and they're easy to collect (the sale is a clean, documented event). But measured by *deadweight loss*, they are among the worst property taxes you can choose. By taxing the *act of trading*, they directly suppress the thing a healthy market needs most — trades. They lock people into ill-fitting homes, freeze labour mobility (you can't easily move for a better job if moving house costs a fortune in tax), and starve the market of the transactions that make prices meaningful. The waste isn't the 2% collected; it's all the beneficial moves that never happen *because* of the 2%.

There's a further nasty property: a transaction tax punishes *frequent* trading geometrically. If you'd ideally move three times over a decade — chasing a job, then a growing family, then a downsize — a 2.5% all-in toll applies to *each* round trip, so you pay it three times, ~7.5% of value cumulatively, just in tax. The owner who happens to land in a "good enough" home and never moves pays it once or never; the owner whose life keeps changing pays it again and again. So the tax falls hardest on exactly the people who most *need* the market to be fluid — the mobile, the growing family, the worker following opportunity — and lightest on the static. It's a tax on adapting your housing to your life, and the more your life changes, the more it costs you.

A second-order effect lurks here too. When a transaction tax suppresses volume, it doesn't just hurt the people who'd have traded — it degrades *price discovery* for everyone, including people who never sell. Property valuation leans heavily on *comparables*: what did similar nearby homes recently sell for? In a thin market, the comparables are few and stale, so every appraisal, every mortgage decision, every "what's my flat worth?" rests on shakier ground. Banks lend more cautiously against assets whose prices they can't pin down, which tightens credit, which thins the market further — a small doom loop seeded by the transaction tax. Liquidity, it turns out, is not a luxury; it's the thing that makes prices *mean* anything.

## High capital-gains tax → the lock-in effect

The third tax produces the most insidious behaviour of all, and it has a name: **lock-in**.

A capital-gains tax is a flow tax on the *profit* you realise when you sell — and the crucial word is **realise**. Your gain is a paper number until you sell; the moment you sell, you *realise* it, and the tax fires. Hold forever and the gain stays unrealised and untaxed. This timing creates a powerful, perverse incentive: **selling is the thing that triggers the tax, so the way to avoid the tax is to never sell.**

### What lock-in is

The **lock-in effect** is the tendency of owners sitting on large unrealised gains to refuse to sell *specifically because selling would crystallise a big tax bill*. The bigger the embedded gain and the higher the rate, the tighter the lock. An owner who'd happily move — the house is too big now, the kids have left, a job beckons in another city — looks at the tax that selling would trigger and decides to stay. They are "locked in" by the tax, frozen in a home that no longer fits.

This is different from the transaction-tax friction we just saw, though they rhyme. A transaction tax is a *flat toll* on every sale regardless of profit. A capital-gains tax scales with the *gain*, so it hits hardest exactly the owners who've held longest and appreciated most — the very people most likely to be mismatched with their home. The longer you've held, the bigger your embedded gain, the bigger the tax to sell, and the more stuck you are. Lock-in tightens with time.

![A flow diagram showing a high capital-gains tax leading owners to refuse to sell, which removes listings and traps mismatched owners, leaving the market tighter and less efficient](/imgs/blogs/how-tax-shapes-behavior-speculation-and-hoarding-4.png)

The figure above traces the mechanism. A high gains tax — selling a ₫5 billion gain triggers a big bill — leads the owner not to sell; they sit tight. That single decision branches two ways. First, **fewer listings**: less inventory comes to market, so buyers have less to choose from. Second, **mismatched owners**: people stay trapped in homes that no longer fit them, instead of trading into something better. Both branches feed the same result — a **tighter, less efficient market** with fewer trades and staler prices. Lock-in doesn't just reduce supply; it also strands the owners themselves.

#### Worked example: the owner who won't sell

**Dana**, our US investor, bought a property years ago for \$200,000. It's now worth \$700,000 — a **\$500,000 unrealised gain**. She's outgrown it and would love to move.

**The tax to sell.** Suppose her all-in capital-gains rate (federal plus state, above any exemption) is **20%** on the gain. Selling realises \$500,000 of gain and triggers **0.20 × \$500,000 = \$100,000** in tax. To move, Dana has to hand over \$100,000 she'd otherwise keep — money that would have gone toward the down payment on the next place.

**The decision.** That \$100,000 is a wall. The next home she wants might genuinely fit her life \$50,000 better — but it's not worth paying \$100,000 in tax to capture \$50,000 of fit. So Dana stays. She's locked in: a willing-but-for-the-tax seller, frozen by the bill that selling would trigger.

**Now the Vietnamese version.** Minh's neighbour bought a plot for ₫3 billion that's now worth ₫8 billion — a **₫5 billion gain (≈ \$193,000)**. Vietnam doesn't tax the *gain* explicitly, but the 2% transfer tax falls on the gross **₫8 billion sale price = ₫160 million**, regardless of the embedded profit. It's a smaller, flatter bite than Dana's gains tax, which is one reason lock-in is *weaker* in Vietnam than in places with steep explicit gains taxes — but the same logic applies wherever the tax on selling is large enough to deter the marginal move.

*A capital-gains tax is the only tax you can dodge forever simply by refusing to act — and that's precisely why it freezes the people holding the biggest gains in place.*

### Why lock-in is so corrosive

Lock-in is corrosive because it attacks *allocation* — the matching of homes to the people who'd use them best. The empty-nester who'd downsize, freeing a family home; the worker who'd relocate for a better job; the owner who'd cash out and let a young family move in — lock-in freezes all of them. The homes that *should* be changing hands as people's lives change stay stuck with owners they no longer suit, while the people who'd value those homes can't get at them. The market gets less efficient not because anyone is acting irrationally — everyone is responding sensibly to the tax — but because the tax makes the *socially* useful trade *privately* unprofitable. (For the deeper logic of why taxing the realised gain on a sale behaves so differently from taxing the asset itself, see [transfer taxes and capital gains on property](/blog/trading/real-estate/transfer-taxes-and-capital-gains-on-property).)

There's a darkly elegant way lock-in resolves itself that's worth naming, because it shows how powerful the incentive is. In systems that *step up* an asset's cost basis at death — wiping out the embedded gain for the heirs — the cheapest way for an owner to "escape" a capital-gains tax is simply to **never sell while alive**. Hold the appreciated property until you die, pass it to your children at the stepped-up value, and the gain is never taxed at all. The tax that was meant to capture profit instead motivates owners to clutch assets to the grave, which is lock-in in its most extreme form: a generation of homes and rentals held not because anyone wants to live in or use them optimally, but because *dying still holding them* is the tax-optimal move. A tax designed to raise revenue on gains ends up suppressing both the trades *and* much of the revenue. It's a vivid reminder that taxes on *realisation* — on the act of selling — share the transaction tax's deepest flaw: the way to dodge them is to freeze.

## The data: how a holding tax meters idle land

Let's pin one of these mechanisms to a clean number, because it's the hinge of the whole argument. We've claimed that the *level* of the holding tax is what decides whether idle land is free to hoard or expensive to sit on. Here's that claim as a chart.

![A bar chart showing the annual cost to hold a sixteen billion VND idle plot rising from near zero at a zero percent tax to 160 million VND at one percent and 320 million at two percent](/imgs/blogs/how-tax-shapes-behavior-speculation-and-hoarding-5.png)

The figure plots the **annual cost to hold Minh's ₫16 billion idle plot** at four different holding-tax rates:

- **0% (Vietnam today):** ≈ **₫0** — hoarding is essentially free. This is the bar that explains land-banking.
- **0.5%:** **₫80 million a year** — already a meaningful annual cost.
- **1%:** **₫160 million a year** — the number from our worked example, the "expensive clock."
- **2%:** **₫320 million a year** — a cost so large that holding idle land becomes plainly irrational.

The shape of this chart *is* the policy lever. Vietnam sits at the leftmost bar — the green "near-zero, hoarding is almost free" column. Move the country two bars to the right, to a 1% tax, and every idle plot in the country suddenly carries a ₫160-million-per-₫16-billion annual bill that the owner has to either earn back (by developing or renting) or escape (by selling to someone who will). The behaviour change is not subtle.

#### Worked example: revenue *and* behaviour from a 1% land tax

Here's the part politicians underrate: a holding tax does *two* useful things at once, and they pull in the same direction.

Imagine a city with **₫1,000 trillion** of land value sitting in idle and underused plots — land-banked sites, empty lots behind fences, parcels held purely for appreciation.

**The revenue.** A **1% annual holding tax** on that ₫1,000 trillion raises **₫10 trillion a year** — real money the city can spend on the metro lines and roads that, not coincidentally, *make the surrounding land more valuable.* The tax partly recaptures the windfall public investment hands to landowners.

**The behaviour.** But the tax also changes what owners *do* with that land. Faced with ₫10 trillion of new annual carrying cost spread across them, owners can't all just absorb it forever. Many will develop (build the housing the city needs), rent (put the empty flats into use), or sell to someone who will. So the same tax that raised ₫10 trillion *also* pushes idle land toward productive use — which over time means more housing supply, which eases the very price pressure that made the land so valuable to hoard.

**The kicker.** Because land's supply is fixed, the tax causes **no deadweight loss** — it doesn't make any less land exist; you can't scare land away with a tax. It raises ₫10 trillion *and* improves how land gets used *and* wastes nothing. That combination is why economists from Adam Smith to Milton Friedman called the land tax the "least bad tax." (We build that case fully in [property and land holding taxes](/blog/trading/real-estate/property-and-land-holding-taxes).)

*A holding tax is rare among taxes: it raises revenue, improves how the asset is used, and creates no waste — all from the single fact that land can't run away.*

## The ideal tax mix: tax holding, free the transaction

Put the four experiments side by side and a clear design principle falls out — and it is the *opposite* of what most property-tax regimes actually do.

The behaviours we've traced sort cleanly into "good for the market" and "bad for the market":

| Tax | What it taxes | Behaviour it drives | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Holding tax** | the *stock* (owning, every year) | put idle land to use | **good** — pushes land into use, no deadweight loss |
| **Transaction tax** | the *flow* (each sale) | trade less, hold longer | **bad** — kills liquidity, strands movers |
| **Capital-gains tax** | the *flow* (realised profit) | refuse to sell (lock-in) | **bad** — freezes owners, shrinks inventory |
| **Vacancy tax** | the *idle stock* | fill or sell empty units | **good** — returns ghost units to use |

The pattern is stark. The two taxes that fall on a **stock** — holding land, holding empty units — push owners toward *using* the asset, and (because the asset can't flee) waste nothing. The two taxes that fall on a **flow** — selling, realising a gain — push owners toward *not transacting*, gumming up the market. So the prescription writes itself: **tax the holding heavily; tax the transaction lightly.**

![A two-by-three matrix showing the ideal mix taxes holding heavily because land cannot flee and it forces land into use, while taxing transactions lightly so property trades freely](/imgs/blogs/how-tax-shapes-behavior-speculation-and-hoarding-6.png)

The figure above lays out the economists' prescription. **Holding tax: HEAVY** — around 1% of land value every year — because *land cannot flee* (its fixed supply means no deadweight loss) and the behaviour it produces is exactly what you want: idle land gets costly, so land is put to use. **Transaction tax: LIGHT** — a minimal toll on buying and selling — because trading is *healthy*; friction freezes both the market and the people in it. The behaviour you want from a light transaction tax is free trading: homes find the owner who values them most, and liquidity stays deep.

This is genuinely counterintuitive, so let's say it plainly. Most people's instinct is the reverse. A transaction tax *feels* fair — you only pay when you sell, when you "have the cash in hand." An annual holding tax *feels* harsh — you pay every year whether or not you've made any money, and it can force people to sell a beloved home they can't afford the tax on. So politics drifts naturally toward *heavy* transaction taxes and *light or absent* holding taxes — which is precisely the mix that maximises deadweight loss, freezes liquidity, and rewards hoarding. The intuitive policy is the wrong policy. The right policy — tax the thing that can't run, free the thing the market needs — is the one that's hardest to sell at the ballot box.

### Why "land can't flee" carries the whole argument

The entire case rests on one fact, so it's worth isolating it. When you tax labour heavily, people can work less, retire early, or move to a lower-tax country — the tax base *responds and shrinks*, and you get deadweight loss. When you tax corporate profit heavily, firms can relocate, restructure, or invest less — again, the base flees. But **land cannot move.** A central HCMC plot will be a central HCMC plot no matter how you tax it. Its supply is perfectly fixed. So a tax on it can't scare any of it away — there is nothing to lose, no deadweight loss, no waste. You can tax it heavily and the only thing that changes is *who captures its value* (the owner versus the public) and *whether the owner bothers to use it*. That is why the holding side of the mix can be heavy without harm, while the transaction side must stay light to keep the market alive.

## Common misconceptions

**"Taxes just raise money; they don't really change behaviour."** This is the foundational error the whole post refutes. A tax is a price on an action, and people respond to prices. The clearest proof is in the contrast between Minh (sits on idle land because holding is free) and Dana (can't, because her county bills her 1.1% a year). Same impulse, opposite behaviour — and the *only* difference is the tax. Every property market's character is partly a behavioural fingerprint of its tax code.

**"A transaction tax is the fairest property tax — you only pay when you have the money."** It *feels* fair, but by deadweight loss it's among the worst. It taxes the *act of trading*, which is the one thing a healthy market needs, so it suppresses the beneficial moves — the upgrade, the relocation for a job, the downsizing empty-nester — that would have made everyone better off. The 2% collected is visible; the dozens of cancelled trades behind it are invisible, and they're the real cost.

**"An annual holding tax is harsh and unfair — taxing people who haven't sold or made any cash."** This intuition is exactly why holding taxes are politically hard and yet exactly why economists love them. The "harshness" *is* the mechanism: by costing money every year, the tax forces idle land into use and recaptures the public-investment windfall that landowners would otherwise pocket for free. And because land can't flee, the tax creates no waste. It feels harsh precisely because it works.

**"High capital-gains taxes are good because they soak speculators."** They sound like they punish flippers, but their main effect is **lock-in** — freezing the *long-term* holders sitting on the biggest gains, the very people whose homes most need to change hands as their lives change. A steep gains tax doesn't mostly catch the fast speculator (who can structure around it); it catches the empty-nester who'd downsize and the worker who'd relocate, stranding them in ill-fitting homes. It gums up allocation more than it deters speculation.

**"Empty apartments mean there's plenty of supply — the housing shortage must be fake."** Ghost units are *technically* built supply, but they're *functionally* withdrawn from the market — held as vaults, not homes. When holding is free, an empty flat is a perfectly rational store of value, so a city can have both a housing shortage *and* dark towers at the same time. The fix isn't to disbelieve the shortage; it's to put a cost on emptiness (a vacancy or holding tax) so the vaults have to start housing people.

**"If we tax property more, prices will rise and buyers will pay for it."** For a *transaction* or *building* tax there's something to this — it can get passed along. But a pure *land-value holding tax* mostly **capitalises into a lower purchase price**: announce a permanent annual tax on land, and buyers pay *less* up front because they're now signing up for that yearly stream. The one-time hit lands on whoever owns the land on announcement day, not on future buyers — which is another reason it's such an efficient tax, and another reason sitting owners fight it.

## How it shows up in real markets

**Vietnam's idle land and ghost units.** Vietnam runs almost exactly the mix this post warns against: a near-zero holding tax, a 2% transaction tax on every sale, and no vacancy tax at all. The predictable result is everywhere — đất nền (raw-land) speculation in HCMC's satellite provinces (the 2018 fever is a textbook episode), land-banked plots held purely for appreciation, and finished-but-dark apartment blocks held as stores of value. With holding free, hoarding bare land and empty flats is the *rational* strategy, which is why so much capital pours into speculation on land rather than into building on it. The Ministry of Finance has floated a second-home / holding tax repeatedly since 2023, and [the **Land Law 2024**](/blog/trading/real-estate/vietnam-land-law-2024-and-the-coming-property-tax) (effective August 2024) replaced the old five-year framework price with **annual land-price tables** — the administrative machinery a real holding tax would need. Whether the tax itself ever arrives is a political question, not a technical one.

![A three-by-three grid showing Vietnam's near-zero holding tax, 2% transaction tax, and absent vacancy tax driving land-banking, low liquidity, and ghost units](/imgs/blogs/how-tax-shapes-behavior-speculation-and-hoarding-7.png)

The figure above assembles the Vietnamese case. Across the top, the three tax settings: holding tax ~0.05% effective (near zero), transaction tax 2% PIT plus 0.5% fee on every sale, and *no* vacancy tax (empty is free). The middle row shows the behaviour each setting produces: **land-banking** (idle plots cost nothing to hold), **low liquidity** (the sale toll discourages trading), and **ghost units** (finished flats sit empty as a store of value). The bottom row shows the results: speculation on bare land instead of building, prices that lag in a thin market, and housing locked out of use while real demand goes unmet. It is not an accident or a cultural quirk — it is the precise, predictable output of the incentive mix.

**The US and Proposition 13 lock-in.** California's Proposition 13 (1978) caps a property's *assessed* value, letting it rise only ~2% a year regardless of market value, with reassessment to full market value only upon *sale*. The effect is a brutal lock-in: a household that bought in 1985 may pay property tax on a 1980s assessed value, while an identical neighbour who just bought pays on today's price — sometimes a 5–10× difference in annual tax. Selling means losing that grandfathered low assessment, so longtime owners stay put for *decades*, even in homes far too big for them. Studies have found Prop 13 measurably reduces residential mobility and ties up family-sized homes with empty-nesters — a live, well-documented demonstration that how you tax the *holding* and the *sale* reshapes who lives where.

**The US capital-gains exemption and its absence.** The US softens lock-in for *primary residences* with a capital-gains exclusion (up to \$250,000 of gain for a single filer, \$500,000 for a couple). Where that exclusion runs out — for owners with very large embedded gains, or for *investment* properties with no exclusion at all — the lock-in we modelled with Dana bites hard: investors hold appreciated rentals indefinitely rather than realise a 20%+ gain, and some hold until death to step up the cost basis and escape the tax entirely. The behaviour is exactly the model's prediction: the bigger the embedded gain, the tighter the freeze.

**Vancouver's Empty Homes Tax.** Faced with visible vacancy amid a housing crisis, Vancouver introduced an Empty Homes Tax in 2017 at 1% of assessed value on homes left empty most of the year, later raising it to 3%. The city has reported thousands of formerly-empty units returning to the rental market and tens of millions of dollars raised (earmarked for housing). It is the cleanest real-world test of the vacancy-tax mechanism in this post: put a price on emptiness, and a chunk of the ghost stock chooses occupancy over the bill. (Other cities — parts of the UK with council-tax premiums on empty homes, and various second-home levies — show the same directional effect.)

**Singapore's stamp-duty cooling tools.** Singapore wields transaction taxes deliberately as a *cooling* lever — Additional Buyer's Stamp Duty (ABSD) of up to 60% for foreign buyers and steep rates for second and third properties, plus a Seller's Stamp Duty that taxes quick flips. It works as designed: it suppresses speculative volume and short-term trading. But it's the flip side of our liquidity argument — Singapore is *intentionally* accepting thinner, less liquid trading in exchange for cooler prices. It's a reminder that a transaction tax's market-freezing effect isn't always an accident; sometimes a government wants the brakes on, and reaches for exactly the tool that thins the market.

## When this matters / Further reading

This matters the moment you stop seeing a property tax as a *bill* and start seeing it as an *instruction*. When you read that a country has near-zero holding taxes, you can now predict land-banking and ghost towers before anyone tells you they exist. When you see a 2% transfer tax or a steep stamp duty, you can predict thin liquidity and owners stuck in ill-fitting homes. When you see a high capital-gains tax with no exemption, you can predict lock-in — empty-nesters frozen in family homes, investors holding appreciated rentals until death. The tax code is a blueprint for behaviour, and once you can read it, a lot of a property market that looked mysterious — *why is so much land empty? why won't anyone sell? why are there dark towers in a housing shortage?* — resolves into incentives doing exactly what incentives do.

It matters practically, too. If you're a buyer in Vietnam, the absence of a holding tax is *why* you're competing against speculators who can hold land for free — and the floated second-home tax is a real risk to price your own decisions around. If you're an owner anywhere with a capital-gains tax, the lock-in maths is *your* maths: the longer you hold, the more a sale will cost you, and that should shape when (and whether) you ever move. And if you're trying to understand why one city's housing is dysfunctional and another's isn't, the tax mix — heavy-on-holding versus heavy-on-transactions — is one of the first things to check, because it's one of the strongest forces in the whole market and one of the least visible.

The deepest takeaway is the one economists have repeated for two centuries and politicians keep ignoring: the *intuitive* tax policy (tax the sale, spare the holding) is almost exactly backwards. The efficient policy taxes the thing that can't run — land you hold — and frees the thing the market needs — trading. Vietnam, like much of the world, runs the intuitive mix and lives with its consequences. Whether that changes is one of the most consequential open questions in the country's property market.

To go deeper, start with [property and land holding taxes](/blog/trading/real-estate/property-and-land-holding-taxes) for the full case for why a land-value tax is, to economists, almost a perfect tax — the "least bad tax" from Smith to Friedman. Then read [transfer taxes and capital gains on property](/blog/trading/real-estate/transfer-taxes-and-capital-gains-on-property) for the mechanics of the transaction and gains taxes that drive lock-in and thin liquidity. And for why the *supply* side amplifies all of this — why cheap speculation plus inelastic supply lets prices detach from incomes for years — see [supply elasticity and why some cities boom and bust](/blog/trading/real-estate/supply-elasticity-why-some-cities-boom-and-bust). Throughout, remember the one number: at a 1% holding tax, sitting on a ₫16 billion idle plot costs ₫160 million a year — and that single change is the difference between a market built for hoarding and a market built for use.
