> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Japan's 1990 land bubble is the cautionary extreme of "land always wins": a credit-fuelled mania pushed urban land so high that, by one famous calculation, the ground under Tokyo's Imperial Palace was worth more than all of California — and then it broke, falling roughly 70-80% over two decades without recovering.
>
> - The setup was cheap money. After the 1985 Plaza Accord pushed the yen up and hurt exporters, the Bank of Japan cut its policy rate to 2.5%, and a wall of cheap credit poured into land and stocks from 1986 to 1990.
> - The mechanism was a **credit-land feedback loop**: cheap loans funded land bids, the rising land became collateral for still-bigger loans, which funded still-higher bids — until prices detached from the rent the land could ever earn.
> - The bust was not a normal dip. Urban land fell ~70-80% from its 1990 peak and **stayed down for twenty years**, dragging banks and firms into a *balance-sheet recession*: everyone repaying debt at once, nobody investing, prices drifting into mild deflation.
> - The one number to remember: roughly **−75%** in urban land, sustained for **two decades**. Land detached from ground rent can crash as hard as anything, and the hangover can last a generation.

In 1989, a widely repeated calculation made the rounds in newspapers and trading desks around the world: the patch of land under Tokyo's Imperial Palace — about 1.3 square miles of ornamental gardens and moats in the center of the city — was, on paper, worth more than the entire state of California. Not a building in California. *All* of California: every house in Los Angeles, every office tower in San Francisco, every acre of farmland in the Central Valley, every inch of beachfront in Malibu. A second version of the factoid held that all the land in Japan, a country roughly the size of Montana, was worth about four times all the land in the United States — a country some twenty-five times larger.

These were not the ravings of a fringe newsletter. They were back-of-the-envelope sums on official land prices, and the official land prices were real. A Japanese owner who had bought a modest plot in central Tokyo in 1980 had, by 1989, watched its assessed value rise four or five times over. Banks lent against those values eagerly. Companies that made cars and cameras found that the land under their factories had become worth more than the factories themselves, and some of them quietly started behaving less like manufacturers and more like property speculators. The whole country seemed to have discovered a law of nature: land in Japan only goes up.

Then, in 1990, it stopped going up. And it did not start again — not the next year, not the next decade, not for a generation. Urban land prices fell, by the standard measures, something like 70-80% from their 1990 peak over the following twenty years, and the patch under the Imperial Palace turned out to be worth a great deal less than California after all. The crash dragged Japan's banks into insolvency they hid for years, turned thriving companies into "zombies" that existed only to repay debt, and locked the world's second-largest economy into two decades of stagnation that came to be called the **Lost Decades**.

This post is the autopsy. The figure below is the mental model — the whole arc on one timeline, from the cheap money of 1985 to the long flat hangover of the 2000s — and the rest of the post walks each phase, builds every term from zero, and pulls out the lessons that cut against every "property only goes up" instinct.

This is a case study in a longer series on how property markets actually work. If you want the universal machinery behind what you are about to read — how land and buildings are two different assets, how a property bubble inflates and pops, how credit drives price — those live in companion posts on [land versus buildings](/blog/trading/real-estate/land-vs-buildings-two-assets-in-every-property), [the anatomy of a property bubble and crash](/blog/trading/real-estate/anatomy-of-a-property-bubble-and-crash), and [the credit cycle and property prices](/blog/trading/real-estate/the-credit-cycle-and-property-prices). Japan is where all three mechanisms ran to their logical, terrifying extreme.

A note before we start: everything here is educational, a study of history and mechanism — not advice to buy, sell, or avoid anything. The numbers are the ones commonly cited from the episode; where a figure is a reconstruction or a round approximation rather than an exact official series, I flag it.

## Foundations: the words you need before the story

Before we can tell the story honestly, we need a small vocabulary. Each of these terms recurs throughout the post, so we define them once, from scratch, here.

### Land versus ground rent (the recap that explains everything)

A piece of real estate is really two assets bolted together: the **land** and the **building** (or "structure" or "improvement") that sits on it. They behave completely differently. A building wears out — it depreciates, needs a new roof, eventually gets knocked down. Land does not wear out; it just sits there. So when people say "property always goes up," they almost always mean the *land* under it, because the building is slowly falling apart.

What is land actually worth? In the end, a plot of land is worth the stream of **ground rent** it can earn — the income you could collect from whoever uses that location. A shop on a busy corner can pay more rent than a shop on a quiet lane, so the corner's land is worth more. *Ground rent* is just the location's earning power, stripped of the building. A plot's honest value is the present value of all the future ground rent it can produce — the same way a bond is worth the present value of its future coupons. (If "present value" is new: a dollar next year is worth a little less than a dollar today, because you could have earned interest on today's dollar; *present value* is what a future stream of cash is worth in today's money once you discount it back.)

Hold onto this, because it is the hinge of the entire Japan story: **price can drift away from rent.** If a plot earns ¥1 million a year in ground rent and sells for ¥20 million, its rental yield is 5% — sane. If the same plot, earning the same ¥1 million, sells for ¥100 million, its yield is 1% — and now you are not buying an income, you are buying the *hope that someone will pay you even more later.* That gap, between the price and the rent that justifies it, is the bubble. Everything that follows is the story of that gap opening to an absurd width and then slamming shut.

### The Japanese asset bubble

The "Japanese asset bubble" is the name historians give to the simultaneous, self-feeding run-up in two markets — **land** and **stocks** — in Japan from roughly 1986 to 1990. The Nikkei 225 (Japan's main stock index, like the Dow or the VN-Index) tripled in those years and peaked at **38,915 on the last trading day of December 1989**. Urban land peaked a few months later, in 1990. Both then collapsed. We will spend most of our time on land, because land is what this series is about and because land is what did the lasting damage — but the two bubbles were the same phenomenon wearing two costumes.

### The Plaza Accord and the easy money it triggered

In September 1985, the finance ministers of five big economies — the US, Japan, West Germany, France, and the UK — met at the Plaza Hotel in New York and agreed to push the US dollar *down* against the other currencies, chiefly the Japanese yen and German mark. The US was running a huge trade deficit and wanted its exports to be cheaper abroad; a weaker dollar does that. This agreement is the **Plaza Accord**.

It worked, dramatically. The yen soared — from around 240 yen to the dollar in 1985 to about 120 by 1988, meaning the yen roughly doubled in value. For a Japanese exporter, that is a catastrophe: a car that cost the American buyer \$10,000 at 240 yen now effectively had to be priced far higher to bring home the same yen, so Japanese exports suddenly looked expensive and sales stalled. This was called **endaka** — "high yen recession" fear.

The Bank of Japan (the BoJ — Japan's central bank, the equivalent of the US Federal Reserve or the State Bank of Vietnam) responded the way central banks do when the economy looks weak: it cut interest rates to make borrowing cheap and stimulate spending. It cut its key policy rate in steps down to **2.5% by early 1987**, a record low at the time, and held it there for over two years. That is the *easy money*. (For how a rate cut feeds straight into property and credit, the macro companion is [how monetary policy moves real estate and credit](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-monetary-policy-moves-real-estate-and-credit).)

Cheap money plus a strong currency that made imports cheap and inflation low is a peculiarly dangerous mix: there was no consumer-price inflation to scare the central bank into raising rates, so it kept money cheap far longer than it should have — and all that cheap money, instead of chasing goods, went chasing *assets*. Land and stocks.

### Credit, collateral, and the feedback loop

Here is the engine. When a bank lends you money to buy a plot of land, it takes the land as **collateral** — security it can seize and sell if you don't repay. The amount the bank will lend depends on what the collateral is worth. If your land is worth ¥100 million, a bank might lend you ¥70 million against it.

Now watch what happens when land prices rise. Your ¥100 million plot is suddenly "worth" ¥150 million on the bank's books. The same plot now supports a ¥105 million loan instead of ¥70 million. You can borrow the extra ¥35 million and use it to buy *more* land — which pushes land prices up further — which raises everyone's collateral again — which unlocks more loans. This is the **credit-land feedback loop**, and it is the single most important mechanism in this entire episode. Rising land creates lending capacity, and lending capacity creates rising land. We will diagram it in full below.

### The keiretsu and bank cross-holdings

Japanese corporate life was organized into **keiretsu** — clusters of companies grouped around a "main bank," tied together by **cross-shareholdings** (each company owning chunks of the others, and the bank owning chunks of all of them). In a cross-holding, the bank's health and the companies' health are wired together: if the companies' shares and land rise, the bank's balance sheet looks great; if they crash, the bank crashes with them. This structure poured fuel on the boom — the main bank lent generously to its keiretsu firms against rising land and shares — and then transmitted the bust straight into the banking system when those same assets collapsed.

### Balance-sheet recession, zombie banks, and deflation

Three terms describe the *aftermath*, and they are the heart of why this bust was so long.

A **balance-sheet recession** (a term coined by economist Richard Koo to describe exactly this episode) is a downturn caused not by high interest rates or a credit cutoff, but by a mass scramble to *repair balance sheets*. When the value of what you own (your land, your shares) collapses below the value of what you owe (your debt), you are *underwater* — technically insolvent. The rational response is to stop spending and investing and instead throw every spare yen at paying down debt until you are solvent again. That is sensible for one firm. But when *every* firm and household does it at once, total spending collapses, and the economy stalls — even though interest rates are near zero, because nobody wants to borrow at any price; they want to *de*-borrow.

A **zombie bank** (or zombie firm) is one that is effectively insolvent — its bad loans exceed its capital — but is kept alive by accounting forbearance, evergreening of loans (lending a struggling borrower just enough to make the interest payment on its existing loan), and regulators who look the other way. A zombie doesn't lend to healthy new businesses; it exists to hide its losses and roll over its bad debt. A banking system full of zombies cannot fund a recovery.

**Deflation** is when the general price level *falls* — your money buys more next year than this year. It sounds nice and is poison for a debtor economy: if prices and wages fall but your debt is fixed in yen, the *real* burden of your debt rises every year. Deflation also makes people delay purchases ("it'll be cheaper next year"), which depresses demand further, which pushes prices down more — a self-reinforcing trap. Japan slid into mild but persistent deflation in the late 1990s and could not climb out of it for years.

With this vocabulary in hand, we can tell the story properly.

## The mania: land prices detach from any rent

From 1986 to 1990, Japanese urban land did something land is not supposed to do: it left its rent behind entirely.

Recall the anchor from Foundations — a plot's honest value is the present value of the ground rent it can earn. Through the boom, ground rents in Tokyo *did* rise, because the economy was genuinely strong and demand for office space was real. But rents rose by tens of percent. Land *prices* rose by hundreds. The two series, which should travel roughly together, ripped apart.

By the 1990 peak, prime commercial land in Tokyo's best districts was trading at rental yields of roughly **1-2%** — meaning the rent it could earn was 1-2% of its price. Put the other way: prices were 50 to 100 times annual rent. To make that concrete, imagine a plot earning ¥10 million a year in ground rent. At a sane 5% yield, that plot is worth ¥200 million. At a 1.5% yield, the *same plot, earning the same rent*, "costs" about ¥667 million. Nothing about the location's earning power had tripled; only the price had.

#### Worked example: price-to-rent at the peak

Let us put numbers on the detachment. A Tokyo commercial plot earns **¥10 million** a year in ground rent (≈ \$67,000 at ~150 yen/dollar). What should it be worth?

- At a healthy **5% rental yield**, value = rent ÷ yield = ¥10M ÷ 0.05 = **¥200 million** (≈ \$1.33 million).
- At the bubble's **1.5% yield**, value = ¥10M ÷ 0.015 = **¥667 million** (≈ \$4.4 million).

So the bubble price was about **3.3 times** the rent-justified price, on a plot whose rent had not changed at all. And here is the killer detail: the buyer at ¥667 million is earning 1.5% on the land while *financing* it with debt — and even at the BoJ's rock-bottom rates, mortgage and corporate borrowing cost more than 1.5%. The owner was paying more in interest than the land earned in rent. The only way that math works is if the land keeps rising fast enough to cover the gap. The moment it stops rising, the owner is bleeding cash on a falling asset.

*When the price you pay is 50-100x the rent and you borrow to pay it, you are not an investor collecting income — you are a speculator betting the next buyer pays more, and the rent is just a rounding error.*

The detachment was visible in behavior, not just spreadsheets. Companies set up *zaitech* ("financial engineering") desks — manufacturers borrowing cheaply to punt on land and shares, sometimes earning more from speculation than from making things. Banks competed to lend against real estate because real estate prices "always rose," so the loans looked riskless. Ordinary households watched homeownership in central Tokyo float permanently out of reach and bought farther and farther out, or bought tiny apartments at price-to-income ratios that would make a 2025 Hanoi buyer wince. (Today's [HCMC price-to-income near 32 years](/blog/trading/real-estate/income-affordability-and-the-price-to-income-ratio) is steep; bubble-Tokyo went beyond even that in the prime wards.) The detachment of price from rent was not a statistical curiosity — it was the lived reality of a country that had collectively decided rent didn't matter.

### The two clocks: why rent and price ran apart

It helps to picture the gap as two clocks ticking at different speeds. The first clock is **rent**, and it ticks with the real economy: as offices fill, shops get busier, and incomes rise, the rent a location can command rises too — but slowly, because rent is tied to what tenants can actually afford to pay out of real earnings. Through the boom, Tokyo rents did rise, perhaps 30-50% over the four years; that was the rent clock ticking at a healthy real-economy pace.

The second clock is **price**, and during a credit mania it ticks with the *availability of credit*, not with rent. When banks are flooding the market with cheap loans against land, the price can rise 20% a year regardless of what rent is doing, because each new wave of borrowed money has to land somewhere and a fixed quantity of prime dirt is where it lands. The price clock spun three or four times faster than the rent clock.

The danger is that the two clocks *look* synchronized for a while, because both are moving up. A casual observer in 1988 saw "rents up, prices up, economy strong — all consistent." Only when you put the two on the same axis and notice that price has tripled while rent has risen by half do you see that they have come unbolted. The yield — rent divided by price — is the gauge that makes the divergence impossible to miss: a yield falling from 4% toward 1.5% is the mathematical fingerprint of price racing ahead of rent. That single number is the most useful early-warning instrument in all of real estate, and almost nobody watched it in 1989.

#### Worked example: the two clocks, on one plot

Take a single Tokyo plot through the boom. In **1986** it earns **¥6 million** a year in ground rent and trades at a **4% yield**, so it is worth ¥6M ÷ 0.04 = **¥150 million.**

By **1990**, the real economy has lifted its rent by a healthy 50%, to **¥9 million** a year. If the yield had *held* at 4%, the plot would be worth ¥9M ÷ 0.04 = **¥225 million** — a 50% rise, exactly tracking the rent. That would have been a boom, but a sane one.

Instead, the yield collapsed to **1.5%**, so the plot traded at ¥9M ÷ 0.015 = **¥600 million** — a **4x** rise. Of that increase, only the move from ¥150M to ¥225M (the first 50%) was *earned* by rising rent. The rest — the leap from ¥225M to ¥600M — was pure yield compression: price detaching from rent, funded by credit. When the credit stopped, that ¥375 million of "extra" had nothing real holding it up.

*Decompose any boom into the part justified by rising rent and the part that is just a falling yield; the second part is the bubble, and it is the part that evaporates.*

## The famous valuations: "Japan's land is worth more than all of America"

The detachment produced the factoids that made the bubble famous, and those factoids are worth taking seriously — not as trivia, but as a lesson in how to *smell* a bubble using nothing but orders of magnitude.

![Before and after comparison showing the peak factoid that Imperial Palace land was worth more than all California, then the crash exposing it as a measure of mania not of worth](/imgs/blogs/case-study-japan-1990-land-bubble-4.png)

The two claims, both widely cited at the time, were:

1. The land under Tokyo's **Imperial Palace** — roughly 1.3 square miles — was worth more than all the land in the state of **California**.
2. All the land in **Japan** was worth roughly **four times** all the land in the **United States**.

You do not need precise data to know something is wrong with these. You just need to ask: *does the rent justify it?*

#### Worked example: the Imperial-Palace-versus-California sanity check

The point of this example is not a precise valuation — nobody was buying the Imperial Palace — but a sense of scale, because *orders of magnitude* are how you catch a bubble.

The continental United States covers roughly **3 million square miles**; Japan covers about **146,000 square miles** — call it **1/20th** the area. Yet at the peak, Japanese land was claimed to be worth several times US land. So Japanese land was being valued at something like **80 to 100 times** the per-square-mile value of American land.

Now ask the rent question. Was the average acre of Japanese soil generating 80-100 times the rent of the average American acre? Of course not. Japan's economy in 1989 was large — second in the world — but it was not 80 times more productive per acre than the United States; it was, if anything, comparable in output per worker and *smaller* in total. The land "value" was not measuring earning power. It was measuring how much credit the feedback loop had pumped into a fixed quantity of dirt.

*When a country one-twentieth the size of another is said to have land worth several times as much, and rents are nowhere near that ratio, you are not looking at value — you are looking at the high-water mark of a credit tide.*

The California comparison is the same arithmetic in miniature. The Imperial Palace grounds couldn't be sold and earned essentially no commercial rent at all — they are a palace. Valuing them above an entire productive US state was a *reductio ad absurdum* of the price-equals-whatever-the-neighbor-sold-for logic. The market was extrapolating recent transaction prices onto land that could never earn an income to match. That is the definition of price detached from rent, scaled up to the size of a country.

The tragedy is that these factoids circulated as *boasts*, not warnings. "Look how valuable our land is" is exactly the wrong reading. The right reading was: "Look how far our prices have run from anything that could pay for them."

## The credit-land loop that inflated it

We have named the engine; now let's run it step by step, because understanding the loop is the difference between thinking "Japanese investors were irrational" and seeing that the structure made the mania nearly automatic.

![Pipeline of the credit-land feedback loop where easy money funds land bids, rising land becomes collateral for more loans which fund higher bids until price detaches from rent](/imgs/blogs/case-study-japan-1990-land-bubble-3.png)

Trace it one turn at a time:

1. **Easy money.** The BoJ holds its rate at 2.5%. Banks are flush with cheap funding and, in a deregulating financial system, hungry to lend. Their biggest, safest-looking collateral is land.
2. **Land bids.** Firms and wealthy individuals borrow to buy land (and shares). Prices rise quickly because a lot of new credit is chasing a fixed amount of prime urban dirt.
3. **Collateral up.** Every existing landowner's plot is now "worth more." On the banks' books, the loan-to-value ratio on old loans has fallen — the same plot now backs a bigger loan.
4. **More lending.** Banks extend new credit against the higher collateral values. A landowner can remortgage the appreciated plot and pull cash out.
5. **Higher bids.** That fresh credit chases the next parcel, pushing prices up again.
6. **Back to step 2** — except now from a higher base. The loop has gone around once and is ready to go again.

Each loop pushes the price farther from the rent, because rent grows with the real economy (slowly) while the price grows with credit (fast). After enough turns you arrive at the 1.5% yields of the peak: a price that only makes sense if the loop keeps spinning forever.

This is why I diagram it as a *loop*, not a line. A line ("cheap money causes high prices") makes it sound like a one-time push. The reality is a flywheel that accelerates itself: the output (higher land prices) is also an input (more collateral, more credit). Flywheels are wonderful on the way up and brutal on the way down, because the same self-reinforcement runs in reverse.

#### Worked example: how collateral manufactures its own loan

Watch one owner ride the loop. **Tanaka-san** buys a Tokyo plot in 1986 for **¥100 million**, putting **¥30 million** of his own money down and borrowing **¥70 million** (a 70% loan-to-value loan — *loan-to-value*, or LTV, is the loan divided by the asset's value).

- By 1988 the plot is "worth" **¥180 million.** Tanaka's loan is still ¥70 million, so his LTV has fallen to 70 ÷ 180 ≈ **39%.** The bank, seeing all that equity, happily lets him remortgage back up to 70% LTV: a new loan of 0.70 × ¥180M = **¥126 million.**
- He pulls out the difference: ¥126M − ¥70M = **¥56 million** in fresh cash, without selling a thing.
- He uses the ¥56 million as a down payment on *another* plot, borrowing the rest — and his bid helps push land prices up again, which raises everyone's collateral, including his.

Tanaka has turned ¥30 million of real money into two leveraged plots and a paper fortune, purely on the back of rising collateral. Notice he never earned much rent; the "return" was the appreciation, and the appreciation was funded by the credit that the appreciation itself unlocked.

*Rising collateral doesn't just allow more borrowing — it manufactures it, and the new borrowing pushes the collateral higher still; that is the loop, and it works in exactly the same way in reverse.*

## The bust: land falls 70-80% and stays down

By 1989 even the Bank of Japan could see the problem. Asset prices were grotesque, banks were dangerously exposed to land, and the social cost — young families priced out of cities forever — was becoming a political issue. So the BoJ slammed the brakes: it raised its policy rate in steps from 2.5% in 1989 to **6% by August 1990**. The government separately told banks to cap their property lending growth (the "total volume control" on real-estate loans, introduced in 1990). The cheap credit that fed the loop was cut off.

The flywheel ran in reverse, and reverse was vicious.

![Reconstructed chart of the Japanese urban land price index rising about fourfold into a 1990 peak then sliding 70 to 80 percent over two decades and staying flat](/imgs/blogs/case-study-japan-1990-land-bubble-2.png)

The chart above is an *illustrative reconstruction* — the shape and magnitudes follow the Japan Real Estate Institute's urban land price index for the six largest cities, but the exact half-year values are stylized, not an official series. Read it for the *shape*: a roughly fourfold climb into 1990, then a long grinding slide of something like **70-80%** that bottomed in the mid-2000s and went essentially flat, ending up near where the boom began two decades earlier.

Why did it fall so far and stay down? Run the loop backwards:

- The Nikkei peaked at 38,915 at the end of 1989 and started falling in January 1990. Land, slower-moving, peaked in 1990 and turned in 1991.
- As land prices fell, **collateral fell.** Loans that were 70% LTV at ¥180 million became 100%+ LTV as the plot sank toward ¥90 million — the owner was now underwater.
- Banks, sitting on collapsing collateral, stopped extending new property loans and began demanding repayment. Less credit chasing land meant lower prices, which meant lower collateral, which meant still-less credit. The same flywheel, spinning down.
- Forced sellers — owners who *had* to sell to repay margin calls and maturing loans — dumped land into a market with no buyers, and each distressed sale reset the "market price" lower for everyone.

#### Worked example: a −75% land fall on a leveraged owner

Return to **Tanaka-san**, now a cautionary tale. Take his 1986 plot bought for **¥100 million** with **¥30 million** down and a **¥70 million** loan.

Say urban land falls **75%** from peak (within the cited 70-80% band). His plot, which peaked around ¥180 million, ultimately sinks to roughly **¥45 million** — below even his original purchase price.

- His debt is still about **¥70 million** (he has paid down some principal, but let's keep it round).
- His plot is worth **¥45 million.**
- His equity is ¥45M − ¥70M = **−¥25 million.** He is **underwater by ¥25 million.** The plot he owns is worth less than the loan against it.

His real ¥30 million of down payment is gone — wiped out several times over. To get back to even, the land has to roughly *quadruple* from its trough (¥45M → ¥180M is +300%). At the post-bust trajectory — flat for two decades — that recovery simply never came in his investing lifetime. He cannot sell (the sale wouldn't cover the loan, so he'd owe the bank cash he doesn't have), so he holds a dead asset and services a loan larger than the asset, year after year, for decades.

*A 75% fall on a leveraged asset does not mean a 75% loss — it means your equity is annihilated and you are left owing more than you own, possibly for the rest of your working life.*

This is the part that breaks the "land always recovers" intuition. In a normal cyclical correction, prices fall 10-30% and recover within a few years; a patient, unleveraged owner waits it out. Japan was not that. The fall was an order of magnitude bigger, and it did not mean-revert. Twenty years is longer than most mortgages, longer than most careers in a job. "Just wait for it to come back" was, for Japanese land bought near the peak, advice that outlasted the person who took it.

There is also a mechanical reason the fall fed on itself, beyond the credit loop running in reverse: **forced selling**. A leveraged owner can hold a falling asset only as long as the bank lets him. When a loan matures and the bank refuses to roll it — because the collateral is now worth less than the loan — the owner must sell *now*, at whatever the market will bear, to raise cash. These distressed sales happen at fire-sale prices, and crucially each one becomes a *comparable* that resets the appraised value of every similar plot downward. The next bank looks at the fresh low comp, marks down its own collateral, and calls its own loan. Forced selling is contagious in exactly the way panic is: one owner's liquidation lowers the price that determines the next owner's solvency.

#### Worked example: why a forced seller accepts a fire-sale price

Picture **Watanabe-san**, who owes **¥80 million** on a plot whose market value has slid from ¥150 million to about **¥70 million.** His loan comes due and the bank will not renew it. He must repay ¥80 million.

- He has no spare cash, so he must sell the plot. But buyers, knowing he is forced, lowball him: the best bid is **¥60 million**, not ¥70 million.
- He sells at ¥60 million, hands it to the bank, and *still owes the bank ¥20 million* in unsecured debt he must somehow pay off for years.
- Meanwhile, his ¥60 million sale is now the freshest comparable in the district. The bank holding a loan on the identical plot next door re-appraises it from ¥70 million down to ¥60 million — and discovers *that* borrower is now underwater too. It calls that loan next.

Watanabe didn't sell at ¥60 million because he thought it was a fair price. He sold because the alternative was default, and the bank's calendar, not the market's recovery, set his deadline. *In a leveraged bust, the price isn't set by what the asset is worth to a patient holder — it's set by the worst forced seller whose loan happens to come due that week, and each fire sale drags the next owner underwater.*

## The balance-sheet recession and zombie banks

A 70-80% land crash would have been painful anywhere. In Japan it became a *generational* event because of what it did to balance sheets — the firms', the banks', and through them the whole economy's.

![Graph showing a land crash leaving firms and banks underwater, both repaying debt instead of investing or lending, demand falling, and deflation setting in](/imgs/blogs/case-study-japan-1990-land-bubble-5.png)

Walk the diagram. The land crash leaves two groups underwater at once:

- **Firms** that had borrowed against land (and held cross-shareholdings that also cratered) found their assets worth far less than their debts. A profitable manufacturer could be technically insolvent purely because of its property and equity portfolio.
- **Banks** that had lent against that land held loans backed by collateral now worth a fraction of the loan. A huge slice of the banking system's loan book was, in reality, non-performing.

Both groups did the individually rational thing: they prioritized **repaying debt over investing or lending.** Firms used profits to pay down loans and shore up their balance sheets rather than build factories or hire. Banks, desperate to rebuild capital and hide bad loans, stopped lending to new ventures.

This is Richard Koo's **balance-sheet recession** in action, and it has a cruel signature: it doesn't respond to cheap money. Ordinarily, when an economy slows, the central bank cuts rates and businesses borrow more. But in a balance-sheet recession, businesses don't *want* to borrow at any rate — they want to *repay*. The BoJ eventually cut rates to essentially **zero** and the economy still didn't take off, because the problem wasn't the price of new debt; it was the overhang of old debt. (This is also why the macro companion on [how credit creates money](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-credit-creates-money-lending-channel-cycles) matters here: when everyone deleverages at once, the money-creation machine runs in reverse and demand drains out of the system.)

#### Worked example: the firm that repays instead of invests

Meet **Sakura Manufacturing**, a profitable Japanese firm. In 1995 it earns **¥10 billion** in profit. In a healthy economy it would reinvest most of that — new equipment, new hires, expansion — because growth pays off.

But Sakura's balance sheet is wrecked: during the boom it borrowed **¥80 billion** against land and shares now worth only **¥30 billion.** It is **¥50 billion** underwater. Its rational priority is survival: it takes its ¥10 billion profit and puts **all of it toward paying down debt.** No new factory. No new hires. No new orders to its suppliers.

Now multiply Sakura by every firm in Japan. Each is profitable, each is solvent on a cash-flow basis, and each is funneling every spare yen into debt repayment instead of spending. The ¥10 billion that *would* have become someone's wages, someone's equipment order, someone's revenue, instead vanishes into loan paydowns. Aggregate demand collapses — not because firms are failing, but because they are all *succeeding* at deleveraging simultaneously. That is the paradox at the center of the Lost Decades.

*A balance-sheet recession is what happens when an entire economy does the right thing for each firm — pay down debt — and the sum of all those right decisions is a decade of stagnation.*

The banking side curdled into the **zombie bank** problem. Rather than recognize their enormous losses, accept failure, and recapitalize — which would have meant admitting the bad loans and wiping out shareholders — Japan's banks and regulators chose forbearance. Banks "evergreened" loans to insolvent borrowers, lending a dying firm just enough to make this quarter's interest payment so the loan could be reported as performing. This kept zombie firms alive and kept zombie banks from collapsing, but it also kept capital trapped in the walking dead instead of flowing to healthy new businesses. The financial system that should have funded a recovery was instead a hospice. It took until the late 1990s and a banking crisis — including the failure of major institutions in 1997-98 — before Japan seriously forced loss recognition and recapitalization. The delay cost years.

## Why the hangover lasted a generation

Put the pieces together and the duration stops being mysterious. Most asset crashes hurt for a few years; Japan's hurt for twenty. Five reinforcing reasons:

**1. The fall was enormous and on leverage.** A 70-80% decline in the dominant collateral asset, held on borrowed money, doesn't just dent net worth — it vaporizes equity across the whole economy and leaves a vast pool of underwater borrowers. The hole was simply far deeper than a normal correction's.

**2. Deleveraging is slow by nature.** Repairing a balance sheet means earning profits and applying them to debt, year after year. If firms collectively owe trillions of yen more than their assets are worth, and they can only pay it down out of annual profits, the repair takes a decade or more — by arithmetic, not by mismanagement.

**3. Bad debt was hidden instead of cleaned.** Forbearance and zombie lending postponed the reckoning. A faster, more brutal approach — force the losses, fail the insolvent banks, recapitalize the survivors — is painful in year one but clears the ground for growth. Japan chose slow, which spread the pain over many years and left zombies clogging the system the whole time. *This is the single most actionable lesson for any country watching a property bust: clean the bad debt fast.*

**4. Deflation made debt heavier over time.** As Japan slid into mild deflation, prices and wages stopped rising. But the ¥80 billion Sakura owed stayed ¥80 billion. With nominal incomes flat or falling, the *real* weight of every fixed debt crept upward — the opposite of the gentle inflation that normally erodes old debts and helps borrowers heal. Deflation turned the deleveraging treadmill into an uphill one.

**5. Demographics removed the bailout.** Property markets can sometimes grow out of a bust if a wave of new households arrives to buy. Japan's population was aging and its workforce began to shrink in the mid-1990s. Fewer new buyers meant weaker underlying demand for housing and land, so there was no demographic tide to refloat prices. (The demand-side mechanics live in the companion on [demographics and urbanization](/blog/trading/real-estate/demographics-and-urbanization-the-demand-wave); Japan is the case where that wave went *out*.)

#### Worked example: how long to recover at the post-bust trajectory

Suppose you bought land at the 1990 peak and it fell **75%** to a trough. For it to return to your purchase price, it must rise from 25 (indexed, peak = 100) back to 100 — a gain of **+300%**.

How long does +300% take? It depends entirely on the growth rate:

- At a brisk **+7% a year**, the rule of 72 says price doubles about every 10 years (72 ÷ 7 ≈ 10). Two doublings (×4) is +300%, so ≈ **20 years** *if it grows steadily at 7%.*
- But post-bust Japanese land didn't grow at 7%; it was roughly **flat** for two decades. At ~0% growth, the time to recover +300% is **never** — within any normal investing horizon.

So even under an optimistic 7% assumption you're underwater for two decades; under the trajectory that actually occurred (flat), you simply never get back to your purchase price. *The math of recovering from a deep, leveraged crash is unforgiving: the bigger the fall, the more spectacular the growth you need just to break even — and a stagnant economy supplies none of it.*

## Common misconceptions

This episode exists in popular memory mostly as a set of confident half-truths. Here are the ones worth correcting.

### "Land always recovers — you just have to wait"

This is the belief Japan demolished. Over a *long enough* horizon and in a *growing* economy, real land in great locations does tend to rise, because rents rise with the economy. But "long enough" can be longer than your investing life, and the qualifier "growing economy" is load-bearing. Japanese urban land bought at the 1990 peak had not recovered its nominal value twenty years later, and in many areas still hasn't. "Always recovers" quietly assumes a recovery arrives *within a useful time frame* — Japan is the counterexample that proves it need not. If you bought with leverage near a peak, "just wait" can mean servicing a loan bigger than your asset for the rest of your career.

### "It can't happen in a strong, growing economy"

Japan in the late 1980s was the envy of the world — high savings, world-beating exporters, low unemployment, advanced technology. Pundits wrote books about how Japan would overtake America. *That confidence was part of the fuel.* A strong economy doesn't prevent a bubble; it provides the optimism and the rising rents that let the bubble's narrative ("of course prices keep rising, look how great we are") sound reasonable. The strength was real. The *prices* still detached from it. A growing economy can absolutely host a credit-fuelled asset bubble — and the strength can make the eventual bust more shocking, because nobody believed it could happen here.

### "It was just a stock bubble, the land thing is overstated"

The stock crash got the headlines because the Nikkei is a daily number, but the **land** crash did the deeper, longer damage. Stocks are owned mostly by investors who can absorb a loss and move on. Land was the **collateral** under the entire banking system and corporate sector — the asset against which the country's debt was secured. When land fell, every loan it backed went bad, which is why the bust metastasized into a balance-sheet recession and a banking crisis. The stock bubble was the spark; the land bubble was the load-bearing wall that collapsed.

### "The Bank of Japan caused it all by raising rates"

The rate hikes of 1989-90 *triggered* the bust, in the sense that they pricked the bubble — but a bubble is by definition something that must eventually pop, with or without a trigger. The deeper cause was the years of cheap money and a credit-land loop that let prices detach from rent in the first place. Blaming the pin misses the balloon. The honest critique of the BoJ is the *opposite* of "it tightened too fast": it kept money too cheap for too long during 1987-89 (lulled by low consumer inflation) and let the bubble inflate to a size where *any* eventual normalization of rates would be catastrophic.

### "Negative-yield buying is irrational, so the people involved were fools"

Buying land at a 1.5% yield while borrowing at more than 1.5% looks insane in isolation. But inside the loop it was *locally* rational: as long as land rose 20% a year, a buyer earning 1.5% rent and paying 5% interest still made a fortune on appreciation. Every individual was reacting sensibly to the incentives the credit-land loop created — banks rewarded for lending against rising collateral, firms rewarded for speculating, households terrified of being priced out forever if they waited. *Bubbles are not built by stupid people; they are built by smart people responding rationally to a structure that rewards the bid until, suddenly, it doesn't.*

## How it shows up in real markets — and what Vietnam should learn

Japan is not a museum piece. Its mechanism — credit detaching land price from rent, then a leveraged crash and a balance-sheet recession — is a template that keeps reappearing. Here is where the echoes show up and what a Vietnamese reader, in particular, should take from it.

![Matrix comparing bubble-era Japan 1990 against a healthy market across price-to-rent, credit growth, leverage, and the aftermath](/imgs/blogs/case-study-japan-1990-land-bubble-6.png)

The matrix above is the diagnostic. On every row — price-to-rent, credit growth, leverage, the aftermath — bubble-Japan sat at the dangerous extreme, while a healthy market stays tethered to rent and to income. You can run this same four-row checklist on any market today.

**The United States, 2006-2012.** Different cast, same play in miniature. Easy money and a credit-land loop (here through securitized mortgages) pushed US home prices to a 2006 peak; the national index fell about **27%** to its 2012 trough — far milder than Japan, partly *because* the US forced loss recognition relatively fast (banks were recapitalized in 2008-09 rather than left as zombies for a decade). The lesson Japan taught and America half-learned: clean the bad debt quickly and the recession is years, not decades.

**China and Evergrande, 2021 onward.** The closest living echo. China ran a multi-decade property boom on a credit-land loop — local governments selling land to developers, developers pre-selling apartments and borrowing against land banks, households piling savings into property as the default store of wealth. Price-to-rent ratios in big Chinese cities reached Japan-like extremes (yields well under 2%). The 2021 crackdown on developer leverage ("three red lines") cracked the loop; Evergrande, with over \$300 billion in liabilities, defaulted, and a string of developers followed. China now faces the central Japan question in real time: will it force the bad debt into the open quickly, or let zombie developers and underwater local governments fester into a balance-sheet recession of its own? The answer will decide whether China's hangover lasts years or, like Japan's, a generation.

**Vietnam, 2022-2023.** Vietnam's property market is far younger and smaller than bubble Japan's, but the *grammar* rhymes. The 2022-23 corporate-bond crunch — frozen after the **Tân Hoàng Minh** (April 2022) and **Vạn Thịnh Phát / SCB** (October 2022) cases — was a credit-land loop seizing up: developers had funded land banks and projects with short-term bonds, and when that funding vanished, projects froze and prices in the speculative *đất nền* (land-plot) segments fell sharply in some satellite provinces. Vietnam avoided a Japan-scale disaster for concrete reasons: the State Bank cut policy rates four times in 2023, Decree 08 gave issuers room to restructure bonds, and crucially Vietnam's *demographics and urbanization are still a rising tide*, not a falling one — the demand wave Japan lost is exactly the wave Vietnam still has. (The full local story is in the companion on [Vietnam's property cycles of 2007, 2013, and 2022](/blog/trading/real-estate/vietnam-property-cycles-2007-2013-2022).)

But the warnings transfer directly. Vietnamese urban price-to-income ratios are among the highest in the world (HCMC near 32 years of income), rental yields in prime segments have compressed toward the low single digits, and a great deal of property is held *for appreciation*, with the rent treated as an afterthought — the exact detachment that defined Tokyo in 1989. The protections Vietnam relied on (cheap policy and a young population) are not permanent: rates can rise, and demographics, while favorable now, will eventually mature. The discipline Japan lacked — keep price anchored to the rent a property can actually earn, and force bad debt into the open early rather than ever-greening it — is the discipline that determines whether a *đất nền* correction stays a correction or becomes a lost decade.

**Singapore, the deliberate counterexample.** It is worth naming a market that did *not* turn into Japan, to see what discipline looks like. Singapore is a tiny island with genuinely scarce land and strong demand — exactly the conditions in which a "land always wins" mania could run riot. Instead, the government treated property prices as a policy variable to be actively managed, deploying repeated rounds of "cooling measures": stamp duties that rise the more property you own and the faster you flip it (the Additional Buyer's Stamp Duty and Seller's Stamp Duty), hard caps on how much of your income can go to mortgage payments (the Total Debt Servicing Ratio), and a large stock of public housing that anchors the bottom of the market. Singapore has had property booms and corrections, but it has deliberately leaned against the credit-land loop — taxing the speculative bid and capping the leverage — precisely so that price cannot detach from income and rent the way Tokyo's did. The contrast is the lesson: Japan let the loop run and paid for twenty years; Singapore taxes and throttles the loop on purpose. (The mechanics of using tax to bend behavior are in the companion on [how tax shapes behavior](/blog/trading/real-estate/how-tax-shapes-behavior-speculation-and-hoarding).)

**The recurring 1990-style land peak.** Japan's 1990 peak was not a lonely freak. Students of the long property cycle point to a striking rhythm of major land-price peaks roughly every eighteen years — notably **1973, 1990, and 2008** in the US and several other economies — each preceded by a credit-fuelled run-up in land and each followed by a banking-strained bust. Japan's 1990 peak sits squarely on that clock. The recurrence matters because it argues the mechanism is structural, not a one-off cultural quirk of 1980s Japan: when credit lets the land bid detach from rent, the unwind follows, and it tends to cluster in time across countries because credit conditions are global. (The full pattern is in the companion on [why real estate cycles are long — the 18-year cycle](/blog/trading/real-estate/why-real-estate-cycles-are-long-the-18-year-cycle).) The uncomfortable implication is that the *next* such window is not far off, and the four-row checklist above — price-to-rent, credit growth, leverage, what-happens-after — is exactly how you'd watch for it.

**The "land always wins" myth, everywhere.** The single most dangerous sentence in real estate is "they're not making any more land." It is true and it is irrelevant. Land's *price* is not set by its scarcity; it is set by the credit available to bid for it and, ultimately, by the rent it can earn. When credit is cheap and abundant, the bid runs far above the rent; when credit tightens, the bid collapses back toward — and in a leveraged unwind, *below* — what the rent justifies. Japan is the proof, written across an entire generation, that "land always wins" is a slogan, not a law. Land detached from rent can crash as hard as a stock, and unlike a stock it usually crashes on borrowed money, which is why the damage spreads to the banks and lingers for decades.

The grid above is the post in four boxes. Land can crash, so anchor price to rent. The hangover is long, so clean bad debt fast. Those are the takeaways worth a generation's pain to learn secondhand.

## When this matters / Further reading

This matters the moment you catch yourself — or your government, or your banking system — saying "property only goes up." Japan didn't say it as a joke; the smartest people in the world's second-largest economy believed it, lent against it, and built a financial system on it. The belief survived for years because it kept being true, right up until it wasn't, and then the unwinding took twenty years and a generation's wealth.

The practical takeaways are concrete:

- **Watch the gap between price and rent.** A rental yield collapsing toward 1-2% while borrowing costs more is not a sign of a hot market; it is a sign that price has left rent behind, and price detached from rent is the definition of a bubble. This is the same yield-versus-price relationship that drives bonds — the [cap-rate intuition](/blog/trading/real-estate/cap-rate-noi-and-the-income-approach) is exactly this.
- **Respect leverage on the way down.** A 75% fall on an unleveraged asset is a 75% loss; on a leveraged one it can be a total wipeout plus a debt you still owe. The deeper the fall and the higher the leverage, the more spectacular the growth you'd need just to break even — and stagnant economies supply none of it.
- **In a bust, speed of cleanup is destiny.** The difference between America's roughly four-year housing recession and Japan's two lost decades was, in large part, how fast each forced its banks to recognize losses and recapitalize. Zombies make the hangover long.

If you want the universal machinery behind this case study, read [the anatomy of a property bubble and crash](/blog/trading/real-estate/anatomy-of-a-property-bubble-and-crash) for the bubble mechanism in general, [the credit cycle and property prices](/blog/trading/real-estate/the-credit-cycle-and-property-prices) for how credit drives the whole thing, and [land versus buildings](/blog/trading/real-estate/land-vs-buildings-two-assets-in-every-property) for why it is always the *land* that booms and busts hardest. For the monetary side — why cheap money pours into property and what happens when an economy deleverages all at once — see [how monetary policy moves real estate and credit](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-monetary-policy-moves-real-estate-and-credit) and [how credit creates money](/blog/trading/macro-trading/how-credit-creates-money-lending-channel-cycles). And for the closest living echo of Japan's mistakes — and Vietnam's near-miss — read [Vietnam's property cycles of 2007, 2013, and 2022](/blog/trading/real-estate/vietnam-property-cycles-2007-2013-2022).

Japan's lesson is not "real estate is bad" — it is one of the great wealth-building assets when its price stays tethered to the rent it can earn. The lesson is that the tether can snap, that credit is what snaps it, and that when it snaps on a whole country's balance sheet, the rope takes a generation to mend.
