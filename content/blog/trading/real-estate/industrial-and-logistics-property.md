> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Industrial and logistics property — factories, warehouses, and the industrial parks that house them — is the "boring" property segment that quietly became one of the best performers, because it rides two structural waves at once: the manufacturing-FDI shift out of China, and e-commerce, which runs on warehouses.
>
> - **Two tailwinds, one segment.** China+1 supply-chain relocation creates demand for factory land; e-commerce creates demand for warehouses. Industrial property sits in the path of both.
> - **The lease is the moat.** A logistics shed leased on a 10–20 year *triple-net* contract to a creditworthy multinational is one of the most boring, dependable cash flows in real estate — and boring cash flows get priced richly.
> - **Vietnam's brightest property story.** The industrial-park model turns cheap farmland into serviced plots leased to global manufacturers; FDI is rising and the parks are filling. It is the one VN property segment underwritten by foreign corporate balance sheets, not domestic speculation.
> - **The one number to remember:** industrial trades at the *tightest cap-rate spread* of any major segment — roughly **0.33% (33 bps) over the 10-year** in the richest markets — because demand and tenant credit are strongest. The catch: it is lumpy, infrastructure-dependent, and tied to the global trade cycle.

In 2008, if you had told a property investor that the most exciting corner of real estate over the next two decades would be *warehouses* — grey, windowless boxes next to a motorway — they would have laughed. Warehouses were where you put inventory because you could not sell it yet. Glamour lived in trophy office towers and gleaming shopping malls. Sheds were an afterthought.

Fast-forward to today and the joke has flipped. Office towers are wrestling with half-empty floors as hybrid work hollows out demand. Shopping malls are fighting a slow war against the very thing the warehouses enable: online shopping. And the boring grey box by the motorway? It is leased for fifteen years to a logistics giant, the rent steps up every year, and investors are paying a *premium* — a higher price for each dollar of rent — to own it. The afterthought won.

This post is about why. We will build the industrial and logistics segment from the ground up: what it actually is, the industrial-park model that turns farmland into rent, the two structural waves (manufacturing-FDI and e-commerce) that drive it, why its cap rates compressed below every other segment, and how all of this lands in Vietnam — which has become, on the strength of this exact story, the brightest property market in Southeast Asia. We will keep one investor, **Minh**, in front of us the whole way: a would-be buyer trying to decide whether the boring box is actually the smart box.

![A comparison matrix showing industrial and logistics property scoring best on demand drivers and lease quality with the tightest cap rate, versus office and retail which carry higher risk](/imgs/blogs/industrial-and-logistics-property-1.png)

The matrix above is the mental model for the whole post. Read it row by row: industrial property is the only major segment whose demand is pushed by *two* structural tailwinds at once (the FDI shift and e-commerce), whose leases are the longest and to the strongest tenants, and whose cap rate is therefore the tightest. Office and retail each carry a structural headwind — work-from-home, online shopping — that industrial does not. The one thing industrial *does* carry is a different risk: it is hostage to the global trade cycle. By the end, every cell in that grid will be something you can explain to a friend.

## Foundations: what industrial and logistics property actually is

Before anything else, let's nail down the vocabulary. Real estate is usually sorted into a few big buckets: **residential** (places people live), **commercial** (offices and shops — covered in the sibling post on [office and retail property](/blog/trading/real-estate/commercial-real-estate-office-and-retail)), and **industrial**. Industrial is the catch-all for property used to *make, store, and move things* rather than to live or shop in. It splits into a few sub-types worth knowing by name.

**Manufacturing / factory space.** Buildings where goods are produced — an electronics assembly plant, a textile mill, a furniture factory. These are often purpose-built for one tenant on land the tenant leases for decades, and they tend to cluster inside industrial parks (more on those in a moment).

**Warehouses and distribution centres.** Buildings whose entire job is to hold inventory and ship it onward. A *warehouse* is the generic term; a *distribution centre* (DC) is a warehouse optimised for fast throughput — goods come in, get sorted, and leave quickly, rather than sitting for months. A *fulfilment centre* is the e-commerce version: a giant DC where individual online orders are picked, packed, and shipped to your door. The defining feature of all three is that they are *logistics* real estate — their value comes from how well they connect to roads, ports, and customers.

**Logistics property** is the umbrella term for that second group plus everything that moves freight: cross-docks, cold storage, last-mile depots. When people say "industrial and logistics" (often shortened to "I&L" in the trade), they mean both the make-it buildings and the move-it buildings, because the same investors, the same parks, and the same demand drivers tend to cover both.

A few more terms we'll use constantly, defined here so nothing trips you later:

- A **cap rate** (capitalization rate) is the annual net rental income a property produces divided by its price. A property earning ₫1 billion of net rent that sells for ₫20 billion has a 5% cap rate. It is the property world's version of a yield. Crucially, a *lower* cap rate means a *higher* price per dollar of rent — investors are paying up. We build this from scratch in the [cap rate and the income approach](/blog/trading/real-estate/cap-rate-noi-and-the-income-approach) post; here you only need the one-line intuition: **cap rate down = price up = the market loves this asset.**
- **NOI** (net operating income) is the rent left after the property's own running costs (maintenance, management, property tax, insurance). Cap rate = NOI ÷ price. NOI is the numerator that matters.
- A **triple-net lease** (written "NNN") is a lease where the *tenant*, not the landlord, pays the three big operating costs — property tax, building insurance, and maintenance. The landlord just collects rent and keeps almost all of it. We'll see why this is the magic ingredient of industrial property.
- **FDI** is foreign direct investment — money a foreign company spends to build or buy physical operations in another country (a factory, not just shares). It is the engine behind the whole industrial-park story, and gets its own treatment in the sibling post on [foreign capital and FDI in property](/blog/trading/real-estate/foreign-capital-and-fdi-in-property).
- **China+1** is the corporate strategy of keeping production in China but adding ("+1") a second manufacturing base elsewhere to reduce the risk of betting on a single country. Vietnam is one of the biggest winners of China+1.

With that vocabulary in hand, here is the thesis stated plainly. Industrial property is "boring" in the best possible sense: it does not depend on shoppers being in the mood, or office workers coming back downtown, or a hot residential market. It depends on two slow, structural forces — companies relocating factories, and commerce moving online — both of which have years of runway left. Boring, structural demand, leased long to tenants who pay their bills, is exactly the recipe that produces the calmest, most richly-priced cash flows in real estate.

### What actually makes one industrial building worth more than another

It is tempting, looking at a row of grey sheds, to assume they are interchangeable. They are not, and understanding *why* is the difference between buying a building that leases instantly and one that sits empty. A handful of physical and locational features drive most of the value, and they are worth itemising because they recur in every worked example below.

- **Location relative to ports, highways, and customers.** This is the single biggest driver. A logistics building's whole job is to move freight cheaply, so every kilometre between it and a deep-water port or a motorway interchange shows up as higher transport cost for the tenant — which means lower rent the landlord can charge. For e-commerce fulfilment, the relevant distance is to *people*: a last-mile depot must sit near a dense population so parcels reach doorsteps fast. The same shed is worth far more 10 km from a port than 60 km from it.
- **Clear height and floor load.** Modern warehouses stack goods high and run heavy racking and forklifts, so *clear ceiling height* (often 10–12 metres in a modern shed versus 6 in an old one) and *floor load capacity* directly determine how much inventory the building can hold per square metre — and therefore how much a tenant will pay.
- **Loading docks and truck yards.** A distribution centre that turns over thousands of parcels a day needs many loading docks and a deep yard for trucks to manoeuvre. Too few docks and the building is a bottleneck no logistics tenant wants.
- **Power, and increasingly cold storage.** Manufacturing needs reliable, heavy power; cold-chain logistics (food, pharma) needs refrigeration, which is expensive to build and commands premium rent. A building wired and chilled for cold storage serves a scarcer, higher-paying tenant pool.
- **Legal status and land-use term.** In Vietnam especially, the *legal* readiness of the land — properly zoned for industry, permitted, with a clean multi-decade land-use right — is as load-bearing as the concrete. A physically perfect plot with a murky title is not a usable industrial asset.

Hold these five features in mind. When we say later that "a generic, well-located shed re-leases far more easily than a bespoke factory," this list is what "generic and well-located" means: high clear height, plenty of docks, near a port and customers, properly permitted. Those are the boxes that won.

## The industrial-park model: farmland in, rent out

The single most important structure to understand in this segment — especially in Vietnam — is the **industrial park** (often "IP", or in Vietnam a *khu công nghiệp*). An industrial park is a large tract of land that a developer assembles, services, and then sub-leases to manufacturers as ready-to-build plots. It is, in effect, a factory landlord at the scale of a small town.

The model is beautifully simple, and the figure below walks the whole pipeline. A developer buys raw farmland in bulk, cheaply, because as farmland it is worth very little. They then spend money *servicing* it: clearing it, levelling it, laying roads, running electricity, water, sewerage, and telecoms to the plot edges, and getting the legal land-use status converted from agricultural to industrial. That servicing is the value-creation step — it turns a muddy field into a plot where a multinational can pour a foundation next month. The developer then leases those serviced plots to factory tenants, typically on very long land-use terms (30–50 years in Vietnam), and often layers on extra revenue: ready-built factory shells for rent, logistics and warehouse space, and worker housing and services that feed the park.

![A pipeline diagram showing the industrial-park model converting cheap raw farmland through clearance and servicing into serviced plots leased to multinational factory tenants, with logistics warehousing and worker housing capturing additional value](/imgs/blogs/industrial-and-logistics-property-2.png)

The genius of the model is the *spread between raw and serviced land*. Raw farmland might cost the developer a few dollars per square metre. Once serviced and de-risked, that same square metre leases to a factory at many times the price. The developer is not really a builder; they are a manufacturer of *certainty* — taking on the messy, slow work of land assembly, infrastructure, and permits so that a global company can sign a clean lease and start building.

Let's put real numbers on it.

#### Worked example: an industrial-park developer's economics

Minh's friend runs a small piece of an industrial-park developer near Hải Phòng, and Minh wants to understand whether the business actually makes money or just sounds impressive. Take a single hectare — 10,000 m².

The developer buys raw farmland at, say, **\$10/m²** in bulk: that is **\$100,000** for the hectare. Then it spends on servicing — clearing, roads, power, water, drainage, and the legal conversion — at roughly **\$45/m²**, another **\$450,000**. So the all-in cost to create one serviced hectare is about **\$550,000**, or **\$55/m²**.

Now the developer leases that serviced land to factory tenants. In a desirable northern park, serviced industrial land leases at roughly **\$140/m²** — but here is the Vietnamese twist: it is usually leased for the *whole remaining land-use term* (say 45 years) and paid largely up front, so think of \$140/m² as the price the tenant pays to control the plot for decades. On the full hectare that is **\$1.4 million** of lease revenue against **\$0.55 million** of cost.

The gross margin on the land itself is therefore about **\$850,000 per hectare**, before the developer's own overheads, financing, and the cost of the unsold inventory it carries for years. A park might cover 200–500 hectares, so the arithmetic scales into serious money — but only if the plots actually lease. The whole model rests on *occupancy*: an empty serviced hectare earns nothing and still cost \$550,000 to create.

*The industrial-park developer's real product is not land — it is a permitted, serviced, ready-to-build plot, and the margin is the reward for absorbing the years of risk between farmland and a signed factory lease.*

This is also why the model is **lumpy**, a word we'll return to. The developer spends a fortune up front servicing a phase of the park, then waits — sometimes years — to lease it out plot by plot. Revenue arrives in big, irregular chunks tied to a handful of large tenant signings, not as a smooth monthly stream. A developer can look brilliant in a year when three multinationals sign, and dead in a year when none do, even though the underlying business is the same. Lumpiness is the price of operating at the scale of a town.

#### Worked example: the lumpiness, year by year

To feel the lumpiness, let's run Minh's friend's park as a tiny cash-flow schedule over five years. The developer services a 50-hectare phase at the **\$55/m²** cost we computed — that is **\$27.5 million** spent up front in year 0 (50 ha × 10,000 m² × \$55). The land leases at **\$140/m²**, or **\$1.4 million per fully-leased hectare**. Now watch how the revenue *actually* arrives:

- **Year 0:** spend **−\$27.5M** servicing the whole phase. Lease nothing yet. Net: deeply negative.
- **Year 1:** one big anchor tenant takes 8 hectares. Revenue **+\$11.2M**. Net positive — but it was one signing.
- **Year 2:** the global trade cycle softens; *no* major tenant signs. Revenue **\$0** from new leases. The developer still pays to carry 42 unleased, serviced hectares.
- **Year 3:** China+1 momentum returns; two tenants take 14 hectares between them. Revenue **+\$19.6M**.
- **Year 4:** the phase finishes leasing — the last 28 hectares go over the year. Revenue **+\$39.2M**.

Add it up: \$27.5M out, then \$70M in across four wildly uneven years. The business is excellent in aggregate — it roughly doubled the money on the land — but a quarterly analyst looking only at year 2 would have called it a failure. *The industrial-park developer earns a fat total margin, but only a patient holder collects it, because the revenue arrives in a handful of large, unpredictable lumps rather than a smooth stream — and a single soft year in the trade cycle can zero out new leasing entirely.*

This lumpiness is also why listed industrial-park developers trade with more volatility than their steady underlying economics would suggest, and why their reported earnings swing year to year. It is a feature of the model, not a flaw in any one company.

## The manufacturing-FDI tailwind: China+1

Now to the demand side — the reason those serviced plots lease at all. The first of the two structural waves is the **relocation of manufacturing**, and its current driver is **China+1**.

For three decades, China was *the* factory of the world. If you made physical goods at scale, you made them in China, because that is where the supply chains, the skilled labour, the ports, and the cost advantage all converged. But over the 2010s and especially the 2020s, three things changed at once. Chinese wages rose, eroding the pure cost edge. Trade tensions and tariffs made betting your entire supply chain on one country look dangerous. And the pandemic taught every multinational a brutal lesson about *concentration risk* — when one country's factories shut, your whole product line stops.

The corporate response was not to *leave* China — China is still indispensable — but to *diversify*. Keep China, and add a second base: China+1. The figure below shows the mechanism. A company that once ran all its production in one mega-hub now keeps that hub and stands up a second plant elsewhere to spread the risk. And when a multinational decides "we will add a plant in Vietnam," that abstract boardroom decision becomes a very concrete piece of demand: a lease on a serviced industrial plot.

![A before-and-after diagram showing manufacturing concentrated in China on the left, then diversified to keep China and add a Vietnam plant on the right, with the relocation becoming demand for a serviced industrial plot](/imgs/blogs/industrial-and-logistics-property-4.png)

Vietnam is one of the largest beneficiaries of China+1 for reasons that are not accidental. It shares a land border with southern China, so supply chains barely have to stretch. Its labour is younger and cheaper than China's. It has signed a thick web of free-trade agreements (the CPTPP, the EU–Vietnam FTA, RCEP) that let goods made in Vietnam reach rich-country markets with low tariffs. And the government has actively courted manufacturers with tax holidays and ready-built industrial parks. The result is that the supply-chain relocation does not just *pass through* Vietnam — it *lands* there, as factories, in parks, on leased land.

This is the link the whole segment turns on: **manufacturing FDI → industrial-land demand**. A dollar of foreign investment in a Vietnamese factory is, almost mechanically, a dollar of demand for industrial real estate. It needs land to sit on, a building to operate in, warehouses to feed it, and roads to ship from. The property is not incidental to the factory; it is the factory's foundation.

It also matters *which kind* of FDI arrives, because Vietnam has been slowly climbing the value chain — and the climb changes the real estate. Early China+1 relocation into Vietnam was dominated by *labour-intensive, low-value* manufacturing: garments, footwear, simple assembly. Those tenants want cheap, basic factory space and large workforces, which is why the southern belt around Bình Dương and Đồng Nai filled first. As Vietnam built capability, *higher-value* electronics and components moved in — the Samsung supply chain in the north is the headline — and these tenants want better-specced buildings, more reliable power, and proximity to ports for high-value, time-sensitive exports. Each rung up the value chain raises the rent a plot can command and deepens the tenant's commitment (a chip-assembly line is far more expensive to relocate than a sewing floor), which makes the lease longer and the income safer. The FDI tailwind is not static; it is *upgrading*, and upgrading tenants pay more and stay longer.

There is a risk hidden in that progression, and honesty demands naming it. Labour-intensive manufacturing is *footloose* — it chases the lowest wage, so as Vietnamese wages rise, the garment and footwear tenants can in principle move on to the next cheaper country (Bangladesh, Cambodia, parts of Africa). The bet on Vietnam's industrial future is therefore really a bet that it keeps *climbing* — replacing departing low-value tenants with stickier high-value ones faster than wages erode the low end. So far the climb has held, but it is the central question hanging over the whole story.

#### Worked example: a 15-year NNN lease to a multinational

Let's make the demand side concrete from the *landlord's* point of view, because this is where the segment's quality really shows. Suppose a global electronics contract manufacturer — call it the tenant — signs a lease on a ready-built 20,000 m² factory inside Minh's friend's park.

The base rent is **\$5.00/m² per month**, which on 20,000 m² is **\$100,000 per month**, or **\$1.2 million per year**. The lease is **triple-net (NNN)**: the tenant pays the property tax, the building insurance, and the maintenance directly. So out of that \$1.2 million, the landlord's own operating costs are close to *zero* — the NOI is almost the entire rent, say **\$1.15 million** after a thin sliver of landlord-side admin.

The term is **15 years**, with a contractual **escalation** — the rent steps up about **3% a year**. So in year 1 the landlord nets ~\$1.15 million; by year 10, the same tenant in the same building is paying roughly **\$1.5 million** a year, because the rent only ratchets one direction. And the tenant is a multinational with a real balance sheet and a real reason to stay — they have sunk millions into fitting out the plant, training workers, and qualifying it into their global supply chain. They are not going to walk over a rent dispute.

So what has the landlord actually bought? Fifteen years of rising, near-pure rent, paid by a tenant who is expensive to replace and unlikely to default. *That* is the cash flow.

*A triple-net industrial lease hands the landlord the rent and hands the tenant the costs, the risk, and the reason to renew — which is why this is one of the most boring and dependable income streams in all of real estate.*

The figure below traces that cash flow across the full term so you can see its shape: it goes up, never down, and the operating headaches sit on the tenant's side of the ledger the whole way.

![A timeline diagram of a 15-year triple-net industrial lease showing base rent of one million dollars per year escalating about 3 percent annually to roughly 1.34 million by year ten, with the tenant paying tax insurance and upkeep, ending at a renewal or re-lease decision in year 15](/imgs/blogs/industrial-and-logistics-property-5.png)

(Note the slight difference in the round numbers between the worked example and the figure: the figure uses a clean \$1.0M base to show the *shape* of the escalation; the worked example uses the more realistic \$1.2M from a specific \$5/m² rent. Both make the same point — net rent that only ratchets up.)

## The e-commerce tailwind: warehousing demand

The second structural wave is **e-commerce**, and it drives the *logistics* half of the segment — the warehouses and distribution centres rather than the factories.

The intuition is almost too simple, which is why it gets overlooked. When you buy a television in a physical shop, that television sat on a shelf in a store, in a city centre, near you. The store *was* the storage. But when you buy that same television online, the journey is completely different. It sits in a giant warehouse, gets picked off a shelf by a worker or a robot when you click "buy", gets packed, and gets driven to your door. Online retail does not eliminate the need to store and move goods — it *relocates* it, from the shop floor you see to the warehouse you never see.

And here is the part that matters for property: e-commerce needs *more* warehouse space per dollar of sales than physical retail does. A shop holds a curated, limited selection; an online retailer offering "everything" must physically hold a vast catalogue somewhere. It needs *fulfilment centres* near population centres for fast delivery, *sortation hubs* to route parcels, and *last-mile depots* on the edge of every city. A widely-cited rule of thumb in the industry is that e-commerce demands roughly **three times** the logistics space of equivalent in-store sales. So every percentage point of retail that shifts online is a percentage point of new warehouse demand — and a percentage point of pressure on the shopping malls it leaves behind.

This is the quiet symmetry that makes the segment so interesting. The same force — online shopping — is a *headwind* for retail property and a *tailwind* for industrial property. The shopping mall and the fulfilment centre are two ends of the same shift. One is losing the tenants the other is gaining. When you own the warehouse, you are on the right side of a trend that is hollowing out the mall.

There is a second-order effect worth spelling out, because it explains why warehouse demand has been even hungrier than the headline retail shift would suggest. E-commerce does not just need *more* space; it needs a *different shape* of space, in *more locations*. A traditional retailer might run a handful of huge regional distribution centres far from the city, where land is cheap, and accept slow delivery. An online retailer competing on next-day or same-day delivery cannot. It needs a *tiered* network: large fulfilment centres on the outskirts, smaller sortation hubs closer in, and tiny last-mile depots right inside the city. Each tier is its own piece of real estate, and the inner tiers sit on expensive urban-edge land. So the e-commerce demand wave is not one big warehouse — it is a whole pyramid of buildings, and the most valuable ones are squeezed onto the scarcest, best-located land. That scarcity is part of why prime logistics rents and values rose so hard.

Two more drivers stacked on top during the early 2020s, and they are worth naming because they were not purely structural. First, the pandemic *pulled forward* years of e-commerce adoption into a few months, so demand for fulfilment space spiked. Second, the supply-chain chaos of 2021–22 taught retailers a lesson about *inventory*: running lean "just-in-time" stock left them with empty shelves when shipping seized up, so many shifted toward "just-in-case" — holding more inventory as a buffer. More inventory held means more warehouse space rented. Some of that buffer demand has since normalised, which is part of why the white-hot warehouse leasing of 2021 cooled in 2023 — a reminder that even a structural tailwind has a cyclical wobble layered on top.

#### Worked example: a warehouse's value from e-commerce demand

Minh is now looking at an actual building: a 30,000 m² distribution centre on the edge of Hồ Chí Minh City, leased to a logistics company that fulfils online orders for several retailers.

The rent is **\$4.50/m² per month** — logistics sheds rent a bit below shiny factories — which is **\$135,000/month**, or **\$1.62 million per year**. After a small slice of landlord costs (most are passed to the tenant under the net lease), the **NOI is about \$1.55 million**.

Now, how much is the building worth? That depends entirely on the *cap rate* the market applies. Recall: value = NOI ÷ cap rate.

- A few years ago, before the e-commerce demand was fully appreciated, the market might have valued this kind of shed at a **7.5% cap rate**: \$1.55M ÷ 0.075 ≈ **\$20.7 million**.
- As investors woke up to the structural demand and the quality of the tenant, the cap rate *compressed* to, say, **6.0%**: \$1.55M ÷ 0.060 ≈ **\$25.8 million**.

Notice what happened. The rent did not change. The building did not change. The *tenant* did not change. The only thing that changed is the market's willingness to pay for that cash flow — the cap rate fell from 7.5% to 6.0% — and the building's value jumped by roughly **\$5 million, about 25%**. That is the e-commerce tailwind expressed as a number: not (only) higher rents, but a market re-rating the *certainty* of warehouse income.

*A warehouse's value is mostly a bet on how durable its demand is — and when e-commerce made that demand look structural rather than cyclical, the market paid up by compressing the cap rate, lifting the value with no change in rent at all.*

#### Worked example: ready-built shed versus build-to-suit

Minh now faces a choice that every industrial investor meets: own a *ready-built* speculative shed and find a tenant, or sign a tenant first and *build to suit* their exact needs. The trade-off is risk versus rent.

Take the same 30,000 m² of logistics space. The **speculative (ready-built)** route: Minh builds a generic, well-specced shed for, say, **\$300/m²**, or **\$9 million**, *before* he has a tenant. If he leases it quickly at \$4.50/m²/month (\$1.62M/year, ~\$1.55M NOI), the yield-on-cost is \$1.55M ÷ \$9M ≈ **17%** — handsome, because he took the leasing risk. But if the shed sits empty for a year, he carries \$9 million of dead capital plus costs, and the gamble sours.

The **build-to-suit** route: a tenant commits to a 15-year NNN lease *first*, and Minh builds the shed to their specification. The tenant might pay a bit *less* rent — say \$4.20/m²/month — because they got a customised building and Minh took no leasing risk. The yield-on-cost is lower, but the income is *certain from day one*: no vacancy gamble. The generic shed earns a higher headline return *if* it leases; the build-to-suit earns a lower, *guaranteed* return.

The choice is really a choice about *re-lettability*. A generic, well-located shed (high clear height, many docks, near a port) can be leased speculatively because many tenants could want it. A bespoke factory built around one tenant's production line cannot — if that tenant leaves, the building may suit no one else, which is exactly the concentration risk we'll hit in the trade-cycle example. *The more a building is tailored to one tenant, the safer its first lease and the more dangerous its second — which is why generic, well-located sheds are the segment's most liquid, most re-rateable asset.*

## Why cap rates compressed: demand plus tenant credit

We have now met the two demand waves; let's tie them to the segment's defining financial fact — its **cap-rate compression**. This is the part that turns "boring" into "won."

Recall the one-line intuition: a *lower* cap rate means investors are paying a *higher* price for each dollar of rent. Cap rates compress (fall) when a cash flow comes to be seen as safer, more durable, or more in-demand. So if industrial cap rates have fallen below office and retail, the market is telling us it views industrial income as the *highest-quality* of the three. Why would it?

Two reasons, and they compound.

**First, demand.** Both structural waves — FDI relocation and e-commerce — point at industrial property and only at industrial property. Office faces the *opposite* of a tailwind (hybrid work permanently dented demand for desks). Retail faces e-commerce as a headwind. Industrial gets both winds at its back. When demand for a building type is structurally rising and supply is constrained (you cannot conjure a serviced industrial plot near a port overnight), buyers compete for the assets and bid prices up — which *is* cap-rate compression.

**Second, tenant credit.** This is the under-appreciated half. The *quality of the tenant* feeds directly into the cap rate, because the cap rate is partly a measure of how risky the rent is. A shed leased on a 15-year NNN contract to a multinational with a global balance sheet is a far safer rent stream than a five-year office lease to a startup or a one-year retail lease to a local shop. The market discounts safe cash flows at a lower rate — exactly as it does with bonds, where a government bond yields less than a junk bond because the income is safer. Industrial's long leases to creditworthy corporates make its rent *bond-like*, and bond-like income gets a bond-like (low) yield.

This is the bridge to fixed income, and it is worth making explicit because it is the deepest reason the segment is priced the way it is. A cap rate behaves like a bond yield: **cap rate ≈ risk-free rate + risk premium**. The risk-free rate is roughly the 10-year government bond yield; the risk premium is the extra return investors demand for taking on a property's specific risks (vacancy, illiquidity, tenant default). Anything that *shrinks* the risk premium — stronger demand, safer tenants, longer leases — compresses the cap rate toward the risk-free rate. (If the seesaw between price and yield feels slippery, the [price-and-yield seesaw](/blog/trading/fixed-income/price-and-yield-the-seesaw-at-the-heart-of-bonds) post in the fixed-income series is the clean derivation.)

The chart below puts the segments side by side. Each bar is built as the risk-free 10-year base plus the segment's own risk spread stacked on top, so you can read the *spread* directly. Industrial sits closest to the 10-year line — the tightest spread, because its demand and tenant credit are strongest. Office sits furthest out — the widest spread, because remote-work vacancy makes its income riskiest.

![A stacked bar chart comparing cap rates by property segment showing each as the ten-year risk-free base plus a risk spread, with industrial logistics carrying the tightest spread of about 0.30 over the ten-year and office the widest](/imgs/blogs/industrial-and-logistics-property-3.png)

#### Worked example: industrial vs office cap rate and what the spread means

Let's read that chart through a worked example, because the spread is the whole game.

Take the 10-year government bond at **4.4%** as the risk-free floor. Now price two buildings, each producing **\$1 million of NOI**.

- An **office tower** trades at a **7.4% cap rate**. Its spread over the 10-year is 7.4% − 4.4% = **3.0%** — a fat risk premium, because the market is nervous about vacancy and work-from-home. Value = \$1M ÷ 0.074 ≈ **\$13.5 million**.
- An **industrial shed** trades at a **4.7% cap rate**. Its spread is 4.7% − 4.4% = just **0.3%** — a razor-thin risk premium, because the market trusts the demand and the tenant. Value = \$1M ÷ 0.047 ≈ **\$21.3 million**.

Same \$1 million of rent. The industrial building is worth **almost 60% more** than the office building — \$21.3M versus \$13.5M — *purely because the market judges its income safer and more durable*. The spread is not a footnote; it is the difference between a \$13 million building and a \$21 million building.

*The cap-rate spread is the market's risk verdict written as a number — and industrial property has earned the lowest spread of any segment because two structural waves and a roster of creditworthy tenants make its rent the closest thing in real estate to a bond.*

A word of honesty, since this is finance and not cheerleading: a tight spread is a *double-edged sword*. When you buy at a 4.7% cap rate, you have paid up; there is little spread cushion left, so if the 10-year yield rises or the demand story wobbles, the cap rate can re-widen and the value can fall fast — the same arithmetic in reverse. Richly-priced assets have the most to lose when the story changes. Industrial property has won, but it is not free of gravity.

To see how dangerous that gravity can be, walk the arithmetic forward through a rate shock. Our industrial shed produces **\$1 million** of NOI and was worth **\$21.3 million** at a 4.7% cap rate. Now suppose the 10-year yield climbs from 4.4% to 5.4% — a one-point move, of the kind markets saw in 2022 — and the cap rate re-widens with it, from 4.7% to 5.7%. The rent has not changed. But the value is now \$1M ÷ 0.057 ≈ **\$17.5 million**. A single percentage point on the cap rate erased roughly **\$3.8 million, about 18% of the value**, with no change in the building or its tenant at all. This is the *duration*-like sensitivity that richly-priced (low-cap-rate) assets carry: the lower the cap rate, the more a given move in rates whipsaws the price. The boring box's stability is in its *cash flow*; its *price* still answers to the bond market. (This is the same mechanism that makes long-dated bonds more rate-sensitive than short ones — the [price-and-yield seesaw](/blog/trading/fixed-income/price-and-yield-the-seesaw-at-the-heart-of-bonds) post derives it in full.)

So the cap-rate story has two layers that move on different clocks. The *structural* layer — strong demand, safe tenants, long leases — pushes cap rates down and stays down for years. The *cyclical and rate* layer — the level of the 10-year yield, the temperature of the trade cycle — pushes cap rates up and down around that structural floor, sometimes violently. Industrial property won on the structural layer. It still gets knocked around by the cyclical one, and 2022–23 was a reminder that the two are not the same.

## Vietnam's industrial story: the bright spot

Now we bring the whole model home to Vietnam, where industrial property is — without much competition — the *brightest* corner of the entire real-estate market. While the residential market wrestled with affordability and the [2022–23 bond freeze](/blog/trading/real-estate/foreign-capital-and-fdi-in-property), and developers of [apartments and đất nền](/blog/trading/real-estate/commercial-real-estate-office-and-retail) navigated frozen credit, the industrial-park operators kept signing multinational tenants. The reason is the single most important structural fact about this segment in Vietnam: **its demand is underwritten by foreign corporate balance sheets, not domestic speculation.**

That distinction is everything. Most of Vietnam's property booms and busts — the 2007–08 mania, the 2011–13 freeze, the 2018 đất nền fever, the 2022 bond crunch — were driven by *domestic* money chasing *domestic* assets with leverage, which is inherently boom-bust. Industrial demand is different in kind. When Samsung, or a Samsung supplier, or a footwear contract manufacturer for a Western brand decides to put a plant in Vietnam, that decision is made on a global supply-chain logic and funded by a foreign parent. It does not evaporate when Vietnamese credit tightens. It is the steadiest, least-leveraged, least-speculative source of property demand in the country.

The chart below shows the proxy for that demand. Manufacturing-heavy FDI disbursed into Vietnam has trended up to roughly **\$25 billion a year** by the mid-2020s, and the occupancy of operating industrial parks has climbed toward **~90%** in the major clusters — parks are filling faster than new serviced land comes online. Both series are illustrative and rounded (flagged in the chart), but the *direction* is well-documented: rising FDI, filling parks, demand outrunning supply.

![A combination chart showing Vietnam FDI disbursed rising toward 25 billion dollars a year as bars and industrial-park occupancy climbing toward 90 percent as a line, with a shaded band marking the post-2020 China-plus-one acceleration, labelled illustrative](/imgs/blogs/industrial-and-logistics-property-6.png)

Vietnam's industrial geography splits into two belts, and the difference matters for anyone underwriting a plot. The figure below maps them.

![A grid mapping Vietnam's two industrial belts: the northern electronics belt around Bac Ninh and Hai Phong driven by the Samsung supply chain and China-plus-one, and the southern diversified belt around Binh Duong and Dong Nai running textiles footwear and furniture, both dependent on ports and roads and both exposed to the global trade cycle](/imgs/blogs/industrial-and-logistics-property-7.png)

The **northern belt** — Bắc Ninh, Bắc Giang, Hải Phòng, Hải Dương — is the **electronics** cluster. It grew up around the Samsung supply chain and is the prime landing zone for China+1 electronics relocation, partly because it is close to the southern-China border and the Hải Phòng deep-water port. The **southern belt** — Bình Dương, Đồng Nai, Long An, around Hồ Chí Minh City — is older and more **diversified**: textiles, footwear, furniture, food processing, auto parts. It leans on the Cái Mép deep-water port and the expanding ring roads and expressways around the city.

What both belts share is total dependence on **infrastructure** — and total exposure to the **global trade cycle**, which is the segment's defining risk. A factory inside a brilliant park is worthless if the port is clogged, the road to it is gridlocked, or the power supply is unreliable (Vietnam suffered real industrial power shortages in the north in 2023). Infrastructure is not a nice-to-have for industrial property; it is the precondition. And because the tenants make goods for export, the whole edifice rises and falls with global demand — when world trade slows, factory orders fall, expansions pause, and the lumpy demand for new plots can dry up for a year or two.

### How an ordinary investor actually touches this segment

A natural objection: this is all well and good, but Minh cannot buy a 200-hectare industrial park or a 30,000 m² shed. Almost no individual can. So how does a normal investor get exposure to a story they cannot buy directly? Three routes, in rough order of accessibility.

**Listed industrial-park developers.** In Vietnam, several industrial-park operators are publicly listed on the stock exchange, so Minh can buy shares and own a sliver of the farmland-to-rent machine we diagrammed. The upside is direct exposure to the development margin; the downside is the *lumpiness* — those shares swing with each big tenant signing and each turn of the trade cycle, so they are far more volatile than the underlying serviced land. You are buying the developer's whole business (and its leverage, and its other projects), not a single clean lease.

**REITs and listed logistics landlords.** Globally, the purest way to own *finished, leased* industrial property is through a real estate investment trust (REIT) — a listed company that owns income-producing buildings and passes most of the rent through to shareholders as dividends. The largest logistics REITs own thousands of warehouses leased on exactly the long NNN terms we worked through, so a share is a diversified claim on that bond-like rent. Vietnam's REIT market is still nascent, so this route is more available offshore for now, but it is the cleanest expression of the "own the boring box's cash flow" thesis. The [REITs, income, leverage and rates](/blog/trading/cross-asset/real-estate-reits-income-leverage-and-rates) post in the cross-asset series unpacks exactly how that wrapper works and how rate-sensitive it is.

**Suppliers and the ecosystem.** A more indirect route is to own the *beneficiaries* of the industrial build-out rather than the land itself — construction firms, building-materials and steel makers, port operators, and power utilities all earn from factories being built and run. This is exposure to the *demand wave* without owning the real estate, and it carries its own, different risks.

The point is not to recommend any of these — it is educational, not advice — but to make clear that "industrial property won" is an *investable* observation, not just an interesting one, and that the wrapper you choose (developer equity, REIT, supplier) changes the risk profile dramatically even when the underlying story is identical.

#### Worked example: the trade-cycle risk — a tenant pulling out

Let's make the risk concrete, because every segment that can make money can lose it. Minh is tempted by an industrial shed leased to an export-oriented manufacturer at a 6% cap rate. On paper it looks like the dependable cash flow we have been praising. So what could go wrong?

Suppose a global trade slump hits — a recession in the export markets, a tariff shock, a demand collapse. The tenant, whose factory makes goods for export, sees its orders fall by 40%. It does not need all its capacity. When its lease comes up for renewal, instead of renewing at the escalated rent, it **does not renew at all** — it consolidates production into its other plants and walks away.

Now Minh owns an empty shed. The math turns ugly fast:

- **NOI goes to roughly zero** while the building sits vacant — and industrial buildings can take many months, sometimes more than a year, to re-lease, because the pool of tenants who need a 30,000 m² shed in that exact location is small.
- Meanwhile Minh still pays the building's carrying costs — property tax, insurance, security, basic upkeep — which under a vacancy he can no longer pass to a tenant. Call it **\$120,000 a year** of bleed on an empty building.
- And when Minh finally does re-lease, it may be at a *lower* rent in a weak market, which re-rates the building's value downward at the same cap rate.

The "boring, dependable" cash flow was dependable right up until the global trade cycle turned. The single-tenant, long-lease structure that makes industrial income so clean in good times concentrates the risk in bad times: one tenant leaving can take the entire building's income to zero overnight.

*Industrial property's stability is conditional, not absolute — it holds as long as global trade flows and the tenant stays, and the same single-tenant structure that makes the rent so clean is what makes a vacancy so brutal.*

This is why sophisticated industrial investors care intensely about *diversification across tenants and locations*, *lease length and break clauses*, and the *re-lettability* of a building (a generic, well-located shed re-leases far more easily than a bespoke factory built for one tenant's process). The segment is not recession-proof. It is recession-*resistant*, and only when you respect the trade cycle.

## Common misconceptions

A few beliefs about this segment are widespread and wrong. Each one, named and corrected.

**"Industrial property is low-return and boring."** This is the misconception the entire post is built to puncture. "Boring" describes the *experience* of owning it — predictable, undramatic, no shoppers to court — not the *return*. Over the last decade, industrial and logistics has been one of the best-performing property segments globally precisely *because* it is boring: structural demand plus long, safe leases produced both rising rents and cap-rate compression, and the two together drove total returns that beat the "exciting" segments. Boring cash flows are what serious money pays up for. The boredom is the feature.

**"Industrial property is recession-proof."** It is not. It is *less* cyclical than, say, hotels, and its long leases delay the pain — but its demand is tied to the global trade cycle, and a deep trade slump genuinely hurts it (orders fall, expansions pause, some tenants downsize or leave). The single-tenant structure also concentrates risk: one big tenant walking can take a building's income to zero. "Recession-resistant, with delayed and concentrated risk" is the honest description; "recession-proof" is a fantasy that gets investors hurt at the top of the cycle.

**"Any land near a road works for industrial."** Location and infrastructure are far more demanding than that. Industrial property lives or dies on its connection to *deep-water ports*, *highways*, *reliable power*, and a *labour pool* — and on the legal status of the land (properly zoned and permitted for industrial use, with a clean long land-use term). A plot near a small road but far from a port, or in a region with power shortages, or without industrial zoning, is not "industrial land" in any useful sense. The serviced, permitted, well-connected plot is the asset; raw land near a road is just a field.

**"Warehouses are commodity boxes — they're all the same."** Modern logistics property is surprisingly specialised. Clear ceiling height, floor load capacity, the number of loading docks, the size of the truck yard, cold-storage capability, and above all *location relative to customers* (last-mile fulfilment must be near population centres) all drive value. A poorly-specced or badly-located shed can sit empty while a well-specced, well-located one nearby leases instantly. The grey box is not generic; it is engineered for a logistics job, and the engineering matters.

**"Vietnam's industrial boom is just cheap labour, and it'll move on the moment labour gets expensive."** Cheap labour got Vietnam in the door, but it is not the only moat. The web of free-trade agreements, the proximity to the Chinese supply chain, the government's tax incentives and ready-built parks, and a decade of accumulated supply-chain investment (which is *sticky* — relocating a qualified plant is expensive and slow) all anchor the manufacturing base beyond a wage number. Labour costs will rise; the question is whether the rest of the package keeps Vietnam competitive as it climbs the value chain from footwear toward electronics. So far, it has.

## How it shows up in real markets

Enough theory — here is the model running in the wild, in four concrete scenes.

**Vietnam's industrial parks and the Samsung gravity well.** The clearest real-world case is the northern electronics belt. When Samsung made Vietnam a core node of its global smartphone and electronics production, it did not just build its own plants — it pulled an entire ecosystem of suppliers, who each needed their own factory land. Industrial-park operators around Bắc Ninh and Bắc Giang became, in effect, landlords to a global supply chain, with occupancy in the major northern parks reported in the high-80s to ~90% and serviced-land rents that climbed steadily through the 2020s. The demand was not speculative Vietnamese money; it was foreign corporate capacity, which is why it kept coming even as the domestic residential and bond markets froze in 2022–23. The industrial-park developers were the property winners of exactly the years that broke the residential developers.

**The global warehouse boom and the great re-rating.** Globally, the defining industrial story of the last decade was the re-rating of logistics property. As e-commerce penetration climbed and the pandemic accelerated it, investors realised warehouse demand was structural, and capital flooded the segment. Prime logistics cap rates compressed sharply — in some markets falling below 4% — meaning investors were paying *more than 25 times* annual rent for sheds that a decade earlier had been an afterthought. The largest logistics landlords became some of the most valuable property companies on Earth. The lesson the market learned, expensively and in public, was that the boring box's cash flow was worth a premium — exactly the cap-rate-compression mechanism we worked through above.

**The trade-cycle reminder.** The segment got a sharp reminder that it is not invincible when global trade wobbled. Through 2022–23, as central banks hiked rates hard and goods demand cooled after the pandemic spending surge, industrial leasing *slowed* — not collapsed, but slowed — and the cap-rate compression of the boom years partly *reversed* as the 10-year yield rose and pushed cap rates back up. Investors who had bought at sub-4% caps watched values soften. The structural demand story stayed intact, but the cyclical and rate-driven layer on top of it reasserted itself, exactly as the "tight spread is a double-edged sword" warning predicts. Industrial won the decade; it did not win every quarter.

**Vietnam's infrastructure bottleneck made visible.** The dependence on infrastructure stopped being theoretical in mid-2023, when northern Vietnam suffered power shortages serious enough to force some factories to curtail production. For an industrial-park operator and its tenants, that is an existential risk: the entire value proposition is "a place where a global manufacturer can reliably operate," and unreliable power undercuts it directly. The episode reframed Vietnam's industrial future as a race between *demand* (which keeps rising on China+1) and *infrastructure* (power, ports, roads, which must keep pace). The plots are only as valuable as the grid and the highways that serve them — which is why so much of Vietnam's public investment is now pointed at exactly those bottlenecks.

## When this matters / Further reading

Industrial and logistics property matters to you the moment you stop thinking of real estate as "homes and shops" and start seeing it as the physical layer of the economy — the layer where goods get made, stored, and moved. That layer is being reshaped by two of the biggest structural forces of our era: supply chains diversifying out of China, and commerce moving online. The boring grey box by the motorway is where both of those forces become real estate.

For a would-be investor like Minh, the practical takeaways are concrete. The segment's strength is the *lease* — long, net, to creditworthy tenants — and its weakness is the *cycle* and the *concentration* (one tenant, one building, one global trade environment). A tight cap rate is the market's compliment and its warning at once: you are buying quality, and you are paying for it, so you have little cushion if the story or the rate environment turns. And in Vietnam specifically, this is the rare property segment underwritten by foreign corporate balance sheets rather than domestic leverage — which is exactly why it has been the bright spot, and exactly why its fate is tied less to Vietnamese credit and more to global trade and the country's own infrastructure build-out.

This is educational, not investment advice — it explains the mechanism so you can read the segment, not a recommendation to buy any building or any park.
