> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Rent is income, so every system taxes it, but *how* it is taxed differs so much that two landlords earning the identical rent can owe wildly different tax.
>
> - **Vietnam uses a flat presumptive tax**: once your rent passes about ₫100 million a year, you owe roughly **10% of the gross rent** (5% VAT + 5% personal income tax) — with **no deductions at all**. Simple, blunt, and it doesn't care whether the property made money.
> - **The US taxes net income**: rent *minus* operating costs, *minus* mortgage interest, *minus* **depreciation** — a non-cash "paper" expense that often turns a cash-profitable rental into a **taxable loss**. The depreciation shield is the landlord's single biggest tax advantage.
> - The US claws part of it back as **depreciation recapture** when you sell (taxed up to 25%), so the shelter is a *deferral*, not a gift. Rental losses are "passive" and limited in how much they can offset other income.
> - The one fact to remember: on the same rent, the Vietnamese landlord pays **~10% of gross** every single year, while the American landlord can pay **near 0% in the early years** — because of a deduction for wear-and-tear on a building that is usually going *up* in value.

A friend of mine — call him Minh — owns a small apartment in Ho Chi Minh City that he rents out for ₫15 million a month. Across the Pacific, an American investor we'll call Dana owns a small condo she rents for the equivalent of ₫15 million a month too. Same rent. Same idea: buy a flat, let a tenant pay it off, keep the income. You would expect them to owe roughly the same tax on that rent. They don't — not even close. In a typical early year, Minh hands the Vietnamese tax authority about ₫18 million, while Dana hands the IRS *nothing at all*, and may even use her rental to lower the tax on her salary.

How can two people earning the same rent owe such different tax? The answer is one of the most consequential, least understood facts in all of real estate: **countries don't tax "rent" — they tax a *number they construct from* rent, and the rules for constructing that number are completely different from place to place.** Vietnam taxes a slice of the *gross* rent the moment it lands in your account. The US taxes the *net* — what's left after you subtract every cost of owning the property, including a large deduction for the building "wearing out" that doesn't cost you any cash. That single design choice — gross versus net — is why American real estate is famously tax-favored and why the same rental can be a tax loser on paper while quietly making money in the bank.

The figure below is the whole post in one picture: the same rent, two completely different taxable bases.

![Two columns comparing how Vietnam and the United States tax the same rent, Vietnam applying a flat ten percent to the entire gross rent while the United States taxes only the net income left after costs and depreciation which is often nothing](/imgs/blogs/taxing-rental-income-1.png)

We're going to build this from zero. By the end you'll understand exactly why the bases differ, how depreciation can shelter a cash-positive rental down to a taxable loss, why the US government later claws some of that shelter back, and what the same rent really costs after tax in each system. We'll keep Minh (in đồng, ₫) and Dana (in dollars, \$) as our two running characters so the examples compound. One note before we start: this is educational, not financial or tax advice — we're explaining mechanisms and tradeoffs, not telling you to buy, sell, or structure anything.

## Foundations: gross versus net taxation, and the vocabulary of rental tax

Almost every confusion about rental tax dissolves once you understand one distinction: **what is the government actually taxing — the whole rent, or only the profit left after expenses?** Let's define every term from scratch, because the entire post rests on these.

### Rental income

**Rental income** is simply the money a tenant pays you for the use of your property — rent, plus any extras the tenant covers (a parking fee, a furniture charge). It's income in the plainest sense: cash flowing *in* to you because you own an asset and let someone else use it. Every tax system on Earth treats rent as taxable income. The disagreement is never *whether* to tax it — it's *how much of it* counts as the thing you pay tax on.

### Gross versus net taxation — the fork in the road

This is the single most important idea in the post, so let's be precise.

- **Gross taxation** means the government taxes the *whole rent you collect*, before you subtract any costs. If you collect ₫180 million in rent and the rate is 10%, you owe ₫18 million — full stop. It doesn't matter what the property cost you to run. Gross taxation is simple to administer (the government just needs to know the rent) but it can be brutal: you can owe tax even in a year the property *lost* money.
- **Net taxation** means the government taxes only the *profit* — the rent *left over after* you subtract the legitimate costs of producing it. If you collect \$24,000 in rent but spent \$10,000 running the place and \$8,000 on mortgage interest, your net is \$6,000, and you only pay tax on that \$6,000. Net taxation is fairer to the landlord (you're taxed on what you actually made) but it's far more complex, because now the government has to define, verify, and police every "cost" you're allowed to subtract.

Vietnam sits firmly on the **gross** side for rental income. The US sits firmly on the **net** side. Almost everything else in this post is a consequence of that one fork.

### Presumptive (flat) tax

A **presumptive tax** is a tax that *presumes* — assumes — a simple, fixed relationship between some easily observed number (here, gross rent) and the tax due, instead of computing your real profit. Rather than auditing your actual expenses, the government just says: "We presume your tax is X% of your gross receipts, and that's that." It's called presumptive because it doesn't try to measure your true net income; it *presumes* a rate and applies it to the top line. Vietnam's rental tax is presumptive: a flat percentage of gross rent, no questions asked about what the property cost you.

Presumptive regimes are common in developing economies and for small businesses everywhere, precisely because they're cheap to run and hard to game on the expense side. The trade-off is fairness: a high-cost landlord and a low-cost landlord on the same rent pay exactly the same tax, even though one made far more profit than the other.

### Deductible operating expenses

In a *net* system, the costs you're allowed to subtract from rent are called **deductible expenses**. For a rental, **operating expenses** ("opex") are the recurring costs of running the property *as* a property: property tax, building insurance, repairs and maintenance, property-management fees, the cost of finding new tenants, common-area utilities, landscaping. These are the costs you'd pay whether you owned the place with cash or with a loan — the cost of *being a landlord*. In a net system they come straight off the rent before tax. In a gross system (Vietnam) they're irrelevant — you get no credit for any of them.

### The mortgage-interest deduction

If you borrowed to buy the property, you pay the lender **interest** on the loan. In a net system, that interest is a deductible cost of producing the rental income — so it comes off too. (The *principal* you repay — the part that pays down the loan balance — is **not** deductible, because it's not an expense; it's you converting cash into ownership equity. Only the interest leg is deductible.) This matters enormously: a leveraged landlord in the US gets to subtract their mortgage interest from rent before tax, which in the early years of a loan — when almost every payment is interest — can be a very large deduction. In Vietnam's gross system, interest is irrelevant; you're taxed on rent regardless of how much you owe the bank.

### Depreciation — the non-cash "paper" expense

Here's the deduction that makes US real estate special, and the one most people have never heard of. **Depreciation** is a deduction for the *wearing out* of the building over time. The tax code assumes that a physical structure — the bricks, the roof, the plumbing, the wiring — gradually deteriorates and will eventually need to be replaced, so it lets you deduct a slice of the building's cost every year as if you were setting aside money to replace it.

The crucial, almost magical, feature of depreciation is that **it is non-cash**. You don't actually spend any money to "depreciate" the building this year — no money leaves your bank account. It's a *paper* expense: an accounting deduction the tax code grants you for the theoretical decline in the structure's value. And yet it comes off your rental income exactly like a real cost. That's why people call it a **paper loss** — depreciation can drag your *taxable* income below zero while your *actual cash* income is comfortably positive.

There's a delicious irony here: depreciation assumes the building is losing value, but in most markets the property as a whole is *gaining* value over time (mostly because the land under it appreciates). The tax code lets you deduct as if the structure is crumbling while the asset is actually rising. That gap — deduct for decline, enjoy actual appreciation — is the heart of the real-estate tax advantage.

### The depreciation schedule — US residential is 27.5-year straight line

How much can you deduct each year? The US tax code sets a fixed **depreciation schedule**: residential rental buildings are depreciated **straight-line over 27.5 years**. "Straight-line" means you take the same equal slice every year. "Over 27.5 years" means you spread the building's cost across 27.5 equal annual deductions. So each year you deduct about `1 ÷ 27.5 ≈ 3.636%` of the building's value.

One critical detail: **only the building depreciates, not the land.** Land doesn't wear out, so the tax code won't let you depreciate it. If you buy a property for \$300,000 and the land is worth \$50,000, only the \$250,000 building is depreciable, giving `250,000 ÷ 27.5 ≈ \$9,090` of deduction per year. (Commercial buildings use 39 years; we'll focus on residential's 27.5-year schedule throughout, since that's where most individual landlords live.)

### Depreciation recapture — the clawback at sale

Depreciation isn't free money — it's *deferred* tax. When you eventually sell, the IRS notices that you spent years deducting the building's "decline" while the building was, in fact, holding or gaining value. So it claws back the benefit through **depreciation recapture**: the portion of your sale gain equal to all the depreciation you ever took is taxed at a special rate — up to **25%** for residential rental property — rather than at the lower long-term capital-gains rate. In plain terms, the government lets you defer tax now in exchange for paying it back (at a capped rate) when you sell. The shield is a loan from the tax authority, not a gift — but a very cheap, very long-duration loan.

### Passive income and passive losses

US tax law puts rental income into a special bucket called **passive activity income**. "Passive" means you're not materially, actively working the business day to day — you own the asset and collect the rent. The catch: **passive losses** (like the paper loss depreciation creates) can generally only offset *other passive income*, not your salary or your stock gains. There's a major carve-out — a landlord who "actively participates" and earns under certain income limits can deduct up to \$25,000 of rental losses against ordinary income each year — but above those limits, the losses are *suspended* and carried forward to use against future rental profit or the eventual sale. This is why "my rental shows a tax loss" doesn't automatically mean "I get a refund on my paycheck." Sometimes you do; often the loss just banks for later.

### The tax shield

A **tax shield** is any deduction that *shields* income from tax — it reduces your taxable income, and therefore your tax bill, by the deduction amount times your tax rate. Mortgage interest is a tax shield; operating expenses are a tax shield; but **depreciation is the big one**, because it's a deduction you get *without spending cash*. A \$9,000 depreciation deduction for someone in a 24% bracket is worth `9,000 × 24% = \$2,160` of tax saved — real money kept, in exchange for a paper entry. That's the "depreciation shield," and it's the engine of real-estate tax efficiency.

### The effective tax rate on rent

Finally, the number that lets us compare systems fairly: the **effective tax rate on rent** is simply the tax you actually pay divided by the gross rent you collected. If Minh collects ₫180M and pays ₫18M, his effective rate on rent is `18 ÷ 180 = 10%`. If Dana collects \$24,000 and pays \$0 because depreciation wiped out her taxable income, her effective rate on rent is `0 ÷ 24,000 = 0%`. Same rent, radically different effective tax. This single ratio is how we'll keep the two systems honest against each other.

With the vocabulary in place, let's see each system actually work.

## Vietnam's flat presumptive tax: simple, blunt, no deductions

Vietnam taxes rental income the way it taxes most small-scale business income: presumptively, on the **gross**. There's no profit-and-loss statement, no list of deductible expenses, no depreciation. The rules fit on an index card.

The headline mechanics, as of 2026:

- If your **total annual rental revenue is at or below roughly ₫100 million** (about \$3,860 at USD/VND ≈ 25,900), you owe **no** VAT and no personal income tax on it. This threshold is meant to exempt very small, casual landlords — someone renting a spare room.
- If your annual rental revenue **exceeds that threshold**, you owe a flat combined rate of about **10% on the gross rent**: **5% value-added tax (VAT)** plus **5% personal income tax (PIT)**. Both are applied to the *whole* rent — not the profit.
- There are **no deductions**. Not for repairs, not for management fees, not for the property tax, not for mortgage interest, and certainly not for depreciation. The base is the rent, period.

Notice the threshold is a cliff in spirit but applies to the whole amount once crossed — if you're over ₫100M for the year, the presumptive rate applies to your rental revenue. The design philosophy is pure administrative convenience: the tax office needs only one number from you (the rent), can't be argued with about phantom expenses, and collects a predictable slice. The figure below shows the three-step calculation.

![A three step flow showing Vietnamese rental tax taking gross rent, checking it clears the hundred million dong threshold, then applying a flat ten percent split as five percent VAT plus five percent personal income tax with no costs deducted](/imgs/blogs/taxing-rental-income-4.png)

The defining feature — and the one that makes this so different from the US — is that **the tax doesn't care whether you made money.** A landlord whose building is fully paid off and cheap to run pays exactly the same 10% as a landlord who's drowning in mortgage interest and repair bills on the identical rent. The first is wildly profitable; the second might be losing cash; Vietnam taxes them identically. That's the price of simplicity.

#### Worked example: Minh's flat tax on ₫180M/yr rent

Minh rents his Ho Chi Minh City flat for ₫15 million a month, so his gross annual rent is `₫15M × 12 = ₫180 million` (about \$6,950). That's comfortably above the ₫100M threshold, so the presumptive regime applies in full.

His tax:

- VAT: `₫180M × 5% = ₫9 million`
- PIT: `₫180M × 5% = ₫9 million`
- **Total: ₫18 million per year** (about \$695).

Now here's the part that stings. Suppose Minh borrowed to buy the flat and pays ₫120 million a year in mortgage interest, plus ₫30 million in management, repairs, and fees. On a *net* basis he might be barely breaking even or even losing cash. Vietnam doesn't care: he still owes the full ₫18 million, computed on the ₫180M of rent, as if his costs were zero. His effective tax rate on rent is `18 ÷ 180 = 10%`, and there is no legal lever to lower it — no expense to deduct, no depreciation to claim.

*The Vietnamese landlord's tax is dead simple and impossible to game, but it's charged on rent he collected, not profit he kept — so a high-cost, highly leveraged landlord is taxed exactly like a low-cost one on the same rent.*

#### Worked example: the threshold and a smaller landlord

The ₫100 million threshold is worth working through, because it's where the regime turns on. Imagine Minh's neighbor, Lan, who rents a single room for ₫7 million a month.

- Lan's gross annual rent: `₫7M × 12 = ₫84 million` (about \$3,245).
- That's *below* the ~₫100M threshold, so Lan owes **₫0** — no VAT, no PIT on the rental.

Now suppose Lan raises the rent to ₫9 million a month to cover rising costs:

- New gross annual rent: `₫9M × 12 = ₫108 million` (about \$4,170).
- That clears the threshold, so the presumptive regime applies to the rental revenue: `₫108M × 10% = ₫10.8 million` of tax.

Notice the discontinuity: a landlord just *under* the threshold pays nothing, while one just over it pays 10% on a base that now includes the rent that was previously exempt. The threshold protects genuinely small landlords (a spare room, a single modest unit) and pulls in anyone running rental as a real income stream. It also creates a mild incentive, at the margin, to keep a single tenancy just under ₫100M — though splitting one property's rent to dodge the threshold is exactly the kind of thing the tax authority watches for.

*Vietnam's threshold means the very smallest landlords pay nothing at all, but the moment rent crosses about ₫100M a year, the flat 10% lands on the whole stream — there's no gentle phase-in.*

It's worth flagging the real-world wrinkle: because the regime is presumptive and enforcement on small residential landlords has historically been light, a large share of Vietnamese rental income simply goes **unreported**. Many tenants pay cash; many leases are informal. The official 10% is the rule; the *realized* collection rate on small residential rentals is lower than the statute implies. That's a feature of the informality, not the law — and it's something the tax authority has been tightening, particularly for landlords who advertise online or rent to companies that need an invoice. We'll come back to under-reporting at the end, because it changes the *practical* tax picture more than any rule on paper.

One more piece of orientation, since rental tax never lives alone: in Vietnam, the heavy taxes on property fall on the *transaction*, not the *holding* — a 2% personal income tax plus a 0.5% registration fee when the property changes hands, and almost no annual property tax. The rental tax we're discussing is the "earn" leg of the broader system. If you want the whole four-moment map — acquire, hold, earn, sell — see the companion post on [how real estate is taxed, the full map](/blog/trading/real-estate/how-real-estate-is-taxed-the-full-map), and for the transaction taxes specifically, [transfer taxes and capital gains on property](/blog/trading/real-estate/transfer-taxes-and-capital-gains-on-property).

## The US net model: rent minus expenses minus interest minus depreciation

Now cross the Pacific to Dana. The US taxes rental income on a fundamentally different principle: it taxes your **net rental income** — your real economic profit from the property — and it gives you a generous, specific list of things to subtract before you get there.

The calculation is a little profit-and-loss statement (a "Schedule E," in US tax-form terms) that runs like this:

```
Gross rent collected
  minus  operating expenses (tax, insurance, repairs, management, etc.)
  minus  mortgage interest (the interest leg only, not principal)
  minus  depreciation (the non-cash paper expense)
  =      taxable rental income (often zero or negative)
```

The figure below walks Dana's numbers down that ladder.

![A pipeline showing the US rental profit and loss starting with twenty four thousand dollars of gross rent, subtracting ten thousand of operating expenses, eight thousand of mortgage interest, and nine thousand of non-cash depreciation, landing at a taxable income of negative three thousand dollars and zero tax owed](/imgs/blogs/taxing-rental-income-2.png)

Let's read it line by line, because each subtraction is doing real work.

- **Gross rent — \$24,000.** Dana rents her condo for \$2,000 a month. That's the top line — the same place Vietnam *stops*.
- **Minus operating expenses — \$10,000.** Property tax, insurance, repairs, management fees, condo association dues, the cost of re-leasing. These are the real cash costs of being a landlord, and every one of them is deductible.
- **Minus mortgage interest — \$8,000.** Dana borrowed to buy. In a typical early year her loan payments are mostly interest, and that interest is deductible. (The principal she repays — say another \$4,000 — is *not* deductible; it's not an expense, it's her buying equity. Hold that \$4,000 in mind; it matters for cash flow.)
- **Minus depreciation — \$9,000.** Her building (not the land) is worth about \$247,500, depreciated straight-line over 27.5 years: `247,500 ÷ 27.5 = \$9,000` per year. No cash leaves her account; it's the paper expense.
- **= Taxable rental income — minus \$3,000.** Add it up: `24,000 − 10,000 − 8,000 − 9,000 = −\$3,000`. On paper, Dana's rental *lost* \$3,000 this year. Her tax on the rental: zero — and that \$3,000 paper loss may even shelter \$3,000 of her other income, if she qualifies under the passive-loss rules.

Pause on how different this is from Minh. Same conceptual rent. Minh's taxable base is ₫180M — *the whole thing*. Dana's taxable base is *negative*. The US didn't tax less by setting a lower rate; it taxed less by **defining a completely different base** — and the depreciation deduction is what dragged that base below zero.

This is the structural reason US real estate is so tax-favored: the tax base for a leveraged, depreciating rental is small or negative for years, so the income tax on the rent is small or zero for years. It's not a loophole in the sense of a trick; it's the deliberate architecture of taxing net rather than gross, combined with a depreciation schedule that's faster than real-world wear.

#### Worked example: Dana's net rental — a paper loss on a cash-positive property

Let's do Dana's full picture, separating the *tax* story from the *cash* story, because the gap between them is the entire point.

Her tax P&L (what the IRS sees):

```
  Gross rent          +24,000
  Operating expenses  −10,000
  Mortgage interest    −8,000
  Depreciation         −9,000
  ----------------------------
  Taxable income       −3,000   ->  tax owed: 0
```

Her cash flow (what her bank account sees):

```
  Gross rent          +24,000
  Operating expenses  −10,000
  Mortgage interest    −8,000
  Principal repaid     −4,000   (cash out, but NOT deductible)
  ----------------------------
  Cash profit          +2,000   ->  money actually kept
```

Look at the two bottom lines. On *paper*, Dana lost \$3,000. In *cash*, Dana made \$2,000 — and paid no tax on it. The difference between the two is the two items that don't line up: depreciation (a \$9,000 deduction with no cash outflow) and principal repayment (a \$4,000 cash outflow that isn't a deduction). Depreciation is the bigger of the two, and it's what flips a profitable rental into a tax loss.

*Dana put \$2,000 in her pocket and reported a \$3,000 loss to the IRS on the same property in the same year — the depreciation deduction is the entire reason those two numbers point in opposite directions.*

## Depreciation: the paper expense that shelters cash income

Depreciation deserves its own section, because it's the mechanism that does the heavy lifting and the one beginners most often misunderstand. Let's build the intuition carefully.

### Why the tax code lets you deduct "wearing out"

The logic is borrowed from ordinary business accounting. If a bakery buys a \$110,000 oven that lasts 10 years, it doesn't make sense to deduct the whole \$110,000 in year one (it didn't *use up* the oven in year one) or to deduct nothing (the oven is genuinely being consumed). So accounting spreads the cost: deduct \$11,000 a year for 10 years, matching the expense to the years the oven helps earn revenue. That's depreciation — a way to spread a long-lived asset's cost across its useful life.

Real estate borrows the same idea. A building is a long-lived asset that (in theory) wears out, so the tax code lets you spread its cost across an assumed useful life — 27.5 years for residential. Each year you deduct `building value ÷ 27.5` as if you'd spent that much maintaining the structure's value.

### The "paper" part — no cash changes hands

The bakery's oven deduction also involves no fresh cash each year (the cash went out the door when they bought the oven). But real estate's version is more powerful for one reason: **the building usually isn't actually losing value.** The oven really does wear out and hit scrap value. A house in a desirable city, by contrast, tends to *appreciate* — and even when the structure ages, the land under it is doing the appreciating. So the landlord gets to take a deduction for "decline" on an asset that's quietly rising. That's the gap that makes depreciation a genuine tax advantage rather than a mere timing nicety. You can read more on why land and structure behave so differently in [land vs buildings, two assets in every property](/blog/trading/real-estate/land-vs-buildings-two-assets-in-every-property).

### Only the building depreciates — land doesn't

This is the rule beginners trip on. **Land never depreciates** — it doesn't wear out — so you must split the purchase price into land and building and depreciate only the building.

#### Worked example: computing Dana's annual depreciation

Dana paid \$300,000 for her condo. The county tax assessment (a common way to split) attributes \$50,000 to the land and \$250,000 to the structure, so her **depreciable basis** is \$250,000.

Her annual depreciation:

```
  Depreciable basis           250,000
  Useful life (residential)   27.5 years
  Annual depreciation = 250,000 / 27.5 = 9,090.91
```

We'll round to **\$9,000** to keep the arithmetic clean across the post. Every year, for 27.5 years, Dana gets to subtract about \$9,000 from her rental income — `9,000 ÷ 250,000 ≈ 3.64%` of the building's cost, year after year, whether or not she spends a dime maintaining it.

*Dana spends nothing to claim \$9,000 of deductions a year, because depreciation isn't a bill — it's the tax code pretending her building wears out a 27.5th at a time, even as the property's market value climbs.*

Why 27.5 years specifically? It's a legislative choice, not a measurement — Congress picked it (commercial property got 39) as a compromise between letting landlords recover their cost quickly and not draining the Treasury too fast. The shorter the assumed life, the bigger each annual deduction and the more valuable the shield. There's nothing physically magical about 27.5 years; plenty of residential buildings stand for a century or more. That's precisely the point: the schedule is *faster* than real-world deterioration for a well-kept building, which is what makes the deduction a genuine subsidy rather than a neutral matching of cost to wear. The slower the building actually decays relative to its 27.5-year tax life, the larger the gap between the paper expense and reality — and that gap is the landlord's gain. It also means the shield has a hard end date: after 27.5 years the building is fully depreciated, the deduction stops, and the rental's taxable income jumps. Many investors sell or refinance before that cliff, or buy a new property to start a fresh depreciation clock — one more reason real-estate portfolios churn.

The size of that deduction relative to the rent is what determines how much it shelters. \$9,000 of depreciation against \$24,000 of rent is enormous — it's 37.5% of the entire top line, gone before any real cost is even counted. That's why depreciation, not the operating expenses, is the line that usually pushes a rental into a taxable loss.

### The depreciation shield in action

Let's make the shield concrete with the chart, which puts Dana's two bottom lines side by side.

![A bar chart comparing the same US rental, a positive two thousand dollar cash profit on the left in green and a negative three thousand dollar taxable income on the right in red, with a note that the nine thousand dollar depreciation deduction is what flips one into the other](/imgs/blogs/taxing-rental-income-3.png)

The green bar is the cash Dana actually kept: \$2,000. The red bar is what she reported to the IRS: a \$3,000 loss. The entire span between them — \$5,000 — is created by the two non-aligned items (\$9,000 depreciation deduction minus \$4,000 non-deductible principal). Depreciation is the dominant force: strip it out and her taxable income would be `−3,000 + 9,000 = +\$6,000`, a healthy profit she'd owe tax on. With it, she owes nothing and may shelter other income besides.

#### Worked example: the value of the shield in dollars of tax saved

How much is the depreciation deduction actually worth to Dana? A deduction's value is `deduction × your marginal tax rate`. Suppose Dana is in the 24% federal bracket.

- The \$9,000 depreciation deduction shields \$9,000 of income from tax.
- At 24%, that's `9,000 × 24% = \$2,160` of tax she doesn't pay.

Put differently: without depreciation, Dana's rental would show \$6,000 of taxable profit and she'd owe `6,000 × 24% = \$1,440` in tax on it. With depreciation, she shows a \$3,000 loss and owes \$0 — and if she qualifies to use the passive loss, she saves another `3,000 × 24% = \$720` on her other income. The swing from "+\$1,440 owed" to "−\$720 saved" is \$2,160 — exactly the depreciation deduction times her rate.

*The depreciation shield isn't abstract — for a landlord in the 24% bracket it's worth real cash equal to the deduction times her tax rate, every year, for spending nothing.*

### A subtlety: depreciation is not optional

One thing that surprises people: in the US you don't get to *skip* depreciation to keep your basis high. The tax code computes your eventual gain as if you took "allowed or allowable" depreciation, meaning you'll face recapture (next section) on the depreciation you *should* have taken even if you forgot to claim it. So there's no advantage to ignoring it — claim it, enjoy the shield, and plan for the recapture. Which brings us to the catch.

## Depreciation recapture: the clawback at sale

Depreciation feels like a free lunch — deduct for decline, enjoy appreciation, pay no tax. But the US tax code isn't quite that generous. It lets you defer the tax, then collects it when you sell, through **depreciation recapture**.

Here's the mechanism. Every year you depreciate, your **tax basis** in the property falls by the deduction. Dana's building started at a \$250,000 basis; after one year of \$9,000 depreciation her basis is \$241,000; after ten years it's `250,000 − 90,000 = \$160,000`. Your basis is what you subtract from the sale price to compute your gain. So all that depreciation you deducted along the way *increases your taxable gain* at sale by the same amount — the deductions don't vanish, they're stored up and surface as extra gain when you sell.

The recapture rule says: the portion of your gain that's attributable to depreciation you took is taxed at a special rate — up to **25%** for residential rental real estate (technically "unrecaptured Section 1250 gain") — instead of the lower long-term capital-gains rate (0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income). The rest of your gain — the part from genuine price appreciation — gets the normal capital-gains treatment.

The figure below shows the full arc: deduct year after year, then settle up at sale.

![A timeline showing a US landlord buying a building with a basis of two hundred forty seven thousand dollars, deducting nine thousand dollars of depreciation each year for ten years to bank ninety thousand dollars of deductions, then paying depreciation recapture taxed up to twenty five percent at sale, leaving a net result of tax deferred for a decade and paid back at a capped rate](/imgs/blogs/taxing-rental-income-5.png)

The single most important reframing: **depreciation is a deferral, not a forgiveness.** You're not escaping the tax; you're postponing it, often for many years, and paying it back at a capped rate (25%) that's frequently *lower* than the ordinary rate at which you deducted it. That spread — deduct at, say, 24% or 32% now, repay at a capped 25% later, with years of use of the money in between — is where the real, durable benefit lives. The time value of money does the rest: a tax you pay in 10 years is worth far less than a tax you pay today.

#### Worked example: depreciation recapture when Dana sells

Suppose Dana holds the condo for 10 years, taking \$9,000 of depreciation each year, then sells.

- Total depreciation taken: `9,000 × 10 = \$90,000`.
- Her basis has fallen from \$250,000 (building) to `250,000 − 90,000 = \$160,000`. (We'll set the \$50,000 land aside; it doesn't depreciate.)
- At sale, \$90,000 of her gain is **recaptured depreciation**, taxed at up to 25%: `90,000 × 25% = \$22,500`.

So Dana's decade of "free" shelter eventually costs her up to \$22,500 of recapture tax at sale. But weigh that against what she saved: roughly \$2,160 a year in tax for ten years is about \$21,600, *plus* she had the use of that money for years, *plus* the recapture rate (25%) may be at or below the ordinary rate she deducted at. The arithmetic isn't a wash in her favor by a giant margin in this stylized case — but the *timing* is. Deferring \$22,500 of tax for ten years, at even a modest discount rate, is worth thousands in present-value terms. And savvy owners often avoid recapture entirely by using a "1031 exchange" to roll the gain into a new property — but that's a topic for the transfer-and-gains post.

*Recapture is the bill that comes due for the depreciation shield — but you pay it late, at a capped rate, and only if you sell, which is exactly why deferral, not deduction, is the real prize.*

To make the deferral value concrete: \$22,500 of tax paid ten years from now is not worth \$22,500 today. At a 5% discount rate, its present value is `22,500 ÷ (1.05)^10 ≈ \$13,800` — so the *real* cost of the recapture, in today's money, is well under what Dana saved along the way. And if she never sells (passing the property to heirs, whose basis "steps up" at death and erases the recapture, or rolling it into a new property via a 1031 exchange), the recapture may never be paid at all. That's the spectrum the US landlord plays on: at worst, a deferral that costs a discounted fraction of the nominal tax; at best, a deduction that's never clawed back. Vietnam's flat 10% offers no such spectrum — it's paid in full, in cash, every single year.

## Effective tax on the same rent: Vietnam versus the US

Now we can answer the question the whole post is built around: on the *same rent*, what's the effective tax rate in each system? Recall the effective rate on rent is `tax paid ÷ gross rent`.

For Minh in Vietnam, it's mechanical and constant: 10% of gross, every year, forever. `₫18M ÷ ₫180M = 10.0%`. There's no path to lower it within the law.

For Dana in the US, it's near **zero in the early years** because depreciation (plus interest and operating costs) shelters the rent down to a loss. Over the *full life* of the investment, once depreciation runs out (after 27.5 years) and especially once recapture is paid at sale, the *lifetime* effective rate on the rent rises into the mid-single digits — but for the years that matter most to a typical hold, it's far below Vietnam's flat 10%. The chart below makes the contrast vivid.

![A bar chart of the effective tax as a percentage of gross rent, Vietnam at about ten percent in red, the US early years at about zero percent in green thanks to the depreciation shield, and the US lifetime blend at about six percent in amber after recapture is paid](/imgs/blogs/taxing-rental-income-6.png)

The three bars tell the story:

- **Vietnam, ~10%.** Flat on gross, every year. Predictable and unavoidable.
- **US early years, ~0%.** Depreciation shelters the net to zero or below, so the income tax on the rent is roughly nothing while the shield is running.
- **US lifetime blend, ~6%.** Once you average in the post-shield years and the recapture paid at sale, the *lifetime* effective rate on the rent settles into the mid-single digits — still below Vietnam's flat 10%, and paid much later.

#### Worked example: effective tax rate on the same rent, side by side

Let's normalize both to the same conceptual rent — call it \$24,000-equivalent of gross rent — and compute the early-year effective rate on rent.

Vietnam (Minh, in đồng, but expressed as a rate):

```
  Tax  = 10% of gross
  Rate = 10.0% of rent, every year
```

US (Dana, early year):

```
  Taxable income = 24,000 − 10,000 − 8,000 − 9,000 = −3,000
  Tax on rent    = 0
  Rate           = 0 / 24,000 = 0.0% of rent
```

The gap is enormous: 10% versus 0% on identical rent, in the same year. Even when we fold in the eventual recapture and the post-depreciation years, the US lifetime rate (~6%) stays below Vietnam's (10%), and it's deferred. The US landlord isn't paying a lower *rate on profit* — federal rates aren't especially low — she's paying tax on a *smaller, later base*, and that's a more powerful advantage than any rate cut.

*Two landlords, the same rent: one pays a clean 10% of it every year; the other pays roughly nothing for years and a modest blended rate over the full life — because the systems tax different things, not at different rates.*

### After-tax rental yield: the bottom line for an investor

Tax isn't an abstraction — it eats directly into the return you actually keep. The cleanest way to see that is the **after-tax rental yield**: the rent you keep after tax, divided by the property's price. We covered pre-tax yield (the cap rate) in [cap rate, NOI, and the income approach](/blog/trading/real-estate/cap-rate-noi-and-the-income-approach); here we put tax on top of it.

![Two columns comparing after-tax rental yield on identical pre-tax economics, Vietnam where the gross-rent tax shaves a four percent pre-tax yield to about three point six percent, versus the United States where the depreciation shield leaves the early-year after-tax yield near the full four percent](/imgs/blogs/taxing-rental-income-7.png)

Take two rentals that look identical *before* tax — same price, same rent, same 4% pre-tax yield. Watch what tax does to each.

#### Worked example: after-tax yield, Vietnam versus US

Start both at a 4.0% pre-tax rental yield (rent ÷ price).

Vietnam:

```
  Pre-tax yield        4.00%
  Tax = 10% of rent -> 10% of the 4.0% yield = 0.40% of price
  After-tax yield      4.00% − 0.40% = 3.60%
```

US (early years, depreciation shield active):

```
  Pre-tax yield        4.00%
  Tax on rent          ~0 (depreciation shelters it)
  After-tax yield      ~4.00%
```

On identical pre-tax economics, the Vietnamese landlord keeps about **3.6%** while the US landlord keeps close to the full **4.0%** — and the US figure ignores the *extra* benefit that the paper loss might be sheltering other income too. Tax alone hands the US landlord roughly 0.4 percentage points of extra yield a year, every year the shield runs. Compounded over a long hold, that's a meaningful edge.

*Two properties with identical pre-tax yields don't deliver identical after-tax yields — the Vietnamese gross-rent tax quietly clips the yield every year, while the US depreciation shield leaves it nearly intact until the property is sold.*

A fair caveat: this isolates *rental* tax. Vietnam's overall property tax burden is famously light on the *holding* side (almost no annual property tax) and heavier on the *transaction* side (2% of the whole sale price). The US is the reverse — heavy annual property tax (~1.1% of value a year), light transfer taxes, and the rental shield we've described. So the *rental* picture flatters the US, but the *total* lifetime tax picture is more balanced than the rental line alone suggests. Always compare the whole system, not one leg of it.

## Common misconceptions

Rental tax is a swamp of half-truths. Here are the ones that trip people up most.

### "Rental income is taxed the same everywhere"

This is the big one, and it's flatly wrong. We've just seen two landlords on the same rent owe 10% versus 0% in the same year. The *rate* might look similar on paper — Vietnam's 5% PIT, a US bracket of 22–24% — but the **base** differs so much that the rate comparison is meaningless. Vietnam taxes the *whole rent*; the US taxes a *net figure that's often negative*. Whenever someone compares "rental tax rates" across countries without specifying the base, they're comparing nothing. The base is the whole game.

### "Depreciation is free money"

Tempting, but no — depreciation is a *deferral*, not a gift. Every dollar you deduct lowers your basis, and that lower basis surfaces as extra gain (taxed via recapture, up to 25%) when you sell. What makes it genuinely valuable isn't that it's free; it's that you pay the tax *later*, at a *capped rate*, with years of use of the money in between, and often on an asset that appreciated while you deducted for its "decline." That's a real, durable advantage — but call it what it is: a cheap, long-duration loan from the tax authority, not free money. People who treat the shield as permanent get an unpleasant surprise at closing.

### "A tax loss means a bad investment"

This one inverts reality in real estate. Dana's rental shows a \$3,000 *tax* loss while putting \$2,000 of *cash* in her pocket — it's a good investment that *looks* bad on the tax return precisely because of the depreciation deduction. In real estate, a paper loss is frequently the signature of a healthy, tax-efficient rental, not a failing one. The number that tells you whether the investment is good is the **cash flow** (and the equity you're building and the appreciation you're capturing), not the taxable-income line. Confusing the two leads people to either panic about a profitable property or brag about a money-losing one.

### "If I don't report it, there's no tax"

In Vietnam, where enforcement on small residential landlords has been historically loose and much rent is paid in cash, under-reporting is widespread — but "widespread" isn't "legal" or "safe." The statutory rule is 10% of gross above the threshold, and the tax authority has been steadily closing the net: matching online rental listings, requiring invoices for corporate tenants, and cross-checking property registrations. The gap between the rule and the realized collection is shrinking. In the US, rental income is heavily documented (1099s, mortgage records, the very depreciation you claim creates a paper trail), so non-reporting is both detectable and penalized. Informality lowers the *effective* tax today; it doesn't make the tax disappear, and it carries real risk.

### "The mortgage payment is deductible"

Only the *interest* is. The principal portion of your mortgage payment — the part that pays down the loan balance — is not a deductible expense, because it's not an expense at all; it's you converting cash into equity. This trips up new US landlords constantly: they see a \$1,000 monthly payment and assume the whole \$12,000 a year is deductible, when only the interest slice (large early, shrinking over time) actually reduces taxable income. The principal is a cash outflow that builds your wealth, not a cost that lowers your tax.

### "Vietnam's 10% is high, so Vietnam taxes landlords harshly"

Not in the round. Vietnam's rental tax looks heavy *because it's on gross*, but Vietnamese landlords pay almost **no annual property tax** and benefit from the country's light holding-tax regime. A US landlord pays ~1.1% of the property's *value* in property tax every year — which, on an expensive property, can dwarf the income-tax savings from the depreciation shield. You can't judge a system from one leg. Vietnam front-loads tax onto transactions and the gross rent; the US back-loads it onto annual property tax and recapture. Different shapes, comparable totals.

## How it shows up in real markets

The abstractions above are visible everywhere once you know to look. Here are concrete, real-world ways the gross-versus-net divide plays out.

### US "tax-sheltered" landlords and the depreciation industry

In the United States, the depreciation shield is so central that an entire industry exists to maximize it. **Cost-segregation studies** are engineering analyses that carve a building into components — carpets, appliances, fixtures, land improvements — that depreciate faster than the 27.5-year structure (some over 5, 7, or 15 years), front-loading the deductions into the early years when they're most valuable. A landlord might pay an engineering firm a few thousand dollars to "find" tens of thousands of dollars of accelerated depreciation. This only makes economic sense in a *net* tax system where depreciation is a deductible cost — it would be pointless in Vietnam, where no deduction exists to accelerate. The very existence of the cost-segregation business is a monument to how valuable the net-income, depreciation-based model is to US property investors. It's also why you'll hear American real-estate investors talk endlessly about "paper losses" and "tax-free cash flow" — they're describing the shield in action.

### Real-estate professionals and the passive-loss carve-out

The passive-loss rules normally cap how much rental loss you can use against your salary — but there's a powerful exception. A taxpayer who qualifies as a **real-estate professional** (broadly, someone who spends more than half their working time and 750+ hours a year materially in real-estate activities) can treat rental losses as *non-passive*, meaning they can offset *unlimited* amounts of other income — including a spouse's high salary. This is why you sometimes read about high earners whose spouse "manages the rentals full time": the structure lets the household's substantial depreciation losses wipe out tax on ordinary income. It's entirely legal, heavily scrutinized, and a vivid example of how the *net* model — once you stack depreciation on top of it — becomes a serious wealth-building and tax-management tool, in a way Vietnam's flat gross tax simply has no equivalent for.

### Vietnam's flat regime and the small-landlord reality

In Vietnam, the presumptive 10% does exactly what it was designed to do: keep things simple for a market full of small, individual landlords and a tax authority that can't audit millions of informal leases. A retiree renting out a spare apartment doesn't keep books, doesn't compute depreciation, and doesn't argue about deductible repairs — they (in principle) pay 10% of the rent and move on. The flip side is the informality we keep flagging: a large share of residential rent is paid in cash and never reaches a tax form. Estimates of the *reported* fraction of residential rental income are well below the rent actually paid. The state is tightening — Decree-level rules now push platforms and corporate tenants toward documentation, and the 2024 tax-administration push targets online landlords — but the *practical* effective tax on a small cash-renting landlord today is often below the statutory 10%. That gap is a real feature of the market, not a quirk.

### The leverage interaction: why US landlords love debt at tax time

Mortgage interest is deductible in the US, depreciation is deductible, and together they make *leverage* doubly attractive at tax time. A heavily mortgaged US rental generates a large interest deduction (on top of depreciation), pushing the taxable income deep into loss territory in the early years — while the tenant's rent quietly pays down the principal, building the landlord's equity tax-free until sale. In Vietnam, leverage gives you *no* tax benefit on the rental: you're taxed on gross rent whether you borrowed or not, so a leveraged Vietnamese landlord carries all the cash strain of the mortgage with none of the tax relief. This is one reason US residential investing is so debt-driven and tax-optimized, while Vietnamese residential investing leans more on capital appreciation and the light holding-tax regime than on rental-income tax engineering. For how leverage amplifies the underlying property economics — before tax — see [leverage and the mortgage, how debt amplifies property](/blog/trading/real-estate/leverage-and-the-mortgage-how-debt-amplifies-property).

### The rent-versus-buy distortion

Because the tax treatment of rental *income* is so different across systems, the math of whether to rent or buy your own home shifts too — landlords in tax-favored systems can charge *lower* rents and still profit (the shield subsidizes them), which changes the buy-versus-rent breakeven for tenants. In the US, the depreciation shield effectively lets landlords accept lower gross yields because their after-tax return is propped up by the tax benefit; in Vietnam, landlords need the gross rent to clear both their costs *and* the 10% tax, with no shield to lean on. The interplay between how rent is taxed and what rents the market can sustain feeds directly into the personal decision we work through in [rent vs buy, the real math](/blog/trading/real-estate/rent-vs-buy-the-real-math).

### A note on the rest of the world

Vietnam and the US bracket a wide spectrum. Many countries sit in between: the UK taxes net rental profit but has *restricted* mortgage-interest relief for individual landlords (a deliberate move to cool buy-to-let), so the interest shield is weaker than in the US. Singapore taxes net rental income and allows a simplified flat 15%-of-gross deemed-expense deduction in lieu of itemizing — a hybrid of the two models. Australia famously allows "negative gearing," letting rental losses (interest plus depreciation) offset *all* other income with few limits, which supercharged its buy-to-let market. The point isn't to memorize each regime — it's to internalize the *axis*: every country is somewhere on the line between "tax the gross rent simply" (Vietnam) and "tax the net after generous deductions including depreciation" (the US), and where it sits determines how tax-favored landlording is.

## When this matters / Further reading

This matters the moment you own — or are thinking about owning — a property that someone else pays you to use. If you're a Vietnamese landlord, the practical takeaways are concrete: your tax is roughly 10% of gross rent above the ₫100M threshold, there are no deductions to chase, and your real lever is the *transaction* taxes and the holding regime, not rental-tax engineering. If you're a US (or US-style) landlord, the depreciation shield is your single biggest tax tool — understand that it's a deferral that comes back as recapture, that a "tax loss" can be the mark of a *good* investment, and that cash flow, not taxable income, tells you whether the property is working.

The deeper lesson is the one we opened with: **never compare rental-tax "rates" across systems without comparing the base.** A 5% PIT on gross rent and a 24% bracket on net-after-depreciation income are not remotely the same tax, even though the first number is smaller. The base — gross versus net — is the whole story, and depreciation is the line that most dramatically separates the two.
