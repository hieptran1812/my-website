> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Buying property well is not about predicting the top; it is about running the same disciplined process every time, so that you are never the one who gets wrecked. Run six steps before you commit: **(1) read the phase** of the cycle, **(2) check the macro** — rates, affordability, credit, **(3) underwrite the specific deal** so it survives a stress test, **(4) size the risk** so you can survive a drawdown and are never a forced seller, **(5) fit it in your portfolio** so you are not dangerously concentrated, and **(6) act by phase** — accumulate in recovery, be selective in expansion, defend at the top, pounce in the bust.
>
> - A framework beats a forecast because nobody can time the exact bottom, but everybody *can* control their leverage, their margin of safety, and their liquidity buffer. Those three are the difference between a painful drawdown and a terminal one.
> - The single rule that does the most work: **never be a forced seller.** A buffer of 6–12 months of payments in cash lets you wait out a freeze instead of dumping at the worst possible bid — which is exactly what happened to over-levered buyers in Vietnam's 2022–23 freeze.
> - The phase changes the rational move. Most people do the opposite of the framework: they buy with maximum leverage at the top because prices are rising, and they sit frozen in cash at the bottom because prices are falling.
> - The number to remember: an ordinary turn of the cycle is a **20–30% drawdown** (US housing fell ~27% from its 2006 peak; HCMC's secondary market fell 20–30% in 2022–23). Size every position so that a 30% fall is survivable, not fatal.

In late 2021, two people in Ho Chi Minh City were looking at the same kind of apartment in the same district. Both could see prices had roughly doubled in five years. Both felt the same pull — the fear that if they did not buy *now*, they would be priced out forever. The first borrowed 85% of the price at an 8% teaser rate, stretched the budget to the limit, and signed. The second ran a checklist, concluded the market was in the most dangerous phase of its cycle, and waited with cash.

Eighteen months later their lives had diverged completely. In April and October of 2022, two high-profile arrests froze Vietnam's corporate-bond market — the funding source that had been fueling the whole property boom. Developers could not refinance; the secondary market seized up; prices in many segments quietly fell 20–30%. The first buyer's teaser rate reset toward 13.9%, the payment jumped, and when the bank wanted its money there was no bid to sell into. He became a *forced seller* at the worst possible moment. The second buyer, sitting on cash with no payment due, bought a better unit from a distressed seller a year later — cheaper, with half the leverage and a year of payments in the bank.

Nothing about the apartments changed. What separated these two outcomes was not luck, a hot tip, or a forecast. It was a *process* — a disciplined way of deciding that the second buyer ran and the first one skipped. This post is that process, written out in full. It is the capstone of the whole series: everything we built — the [cycle](/blog/trading/real-estate/anatomy-of-a-real-estate-cycle-four-phases), [rates](/blog/trading/real-estate/interest-rates-and-mortgages-the-master-variable), [affordability](/blog/trading/real-estate/income-affordability-and-the-price-to-income-ratio), [credit](/blog/trading/real-estate/the-credit-cycle-and-property-prices), [leverage](/blog/trading/real-estate/leverage-and-the-mortgage-how-debt-amplifies-property), [tax](/blog/trading/real-estate/how-real-estate-is-taxed-the-full-map) — folded into one checklist you can actually run before you sign.

By the end you should be able to take any property decision — your own home, a rental, a plot of land — and walk it through these six steps from public information, ending with a clear *buy*, *wait*, or *pass*. ## Foundations: why a framework beats a forecast

Before we walk the six steps, we need to be honest about what a framework *is* and what it is *not*. This is the most important idea in the post, so we build it from zero.

### What a forecast tries to do, and why it fails

A **forecast** is a prediction of where prices will be at a specific future time: "HCMC apartments will be up 15% by the end of next year." Forecasts feel like the thing you want, because if you knew the future you could buy at the bottom and sell at the top. The trouble is that nobody reliably knows the future of an asset as complex as property. Prices depend on interest rates (which depend on inflation and central-bank decisions), on credit availability (which depends on bank balance sheets and regulation), on demand (which depends on jobs, migration, and confidence), and on supply (which arrives with a multi-year lag). Each of those is hard to predict on its own; their *combination*, with feedback loops, is effectively impossible to call to the month.

There is a deeper problem. Even a forecaster who is right about *direction* is usually wrong about *timing*. "Prices are too high and will fall" can be correct and still ruin you, because the market can stay irrational — and keep rising — for years before it turns. The famous warning is that the market can stay overvalued longer than you can stay solvent. A forecast gives you a number and a date; reality gives you neither on schedule.

### What a framework does instead

A **framework** is not a prediction. It is a *process* — a fixed sequence of checks you run on every decision, regardless of what you think the market will do. The framework does not tell you where prices are going. It tells you something far more useful: *given where things are right now, and given your own constraints, is this a deal you should do, and at what size?*

The shift is from **prediction** to **preparation**. A forecaster asks "will prices go up?" A framework asks "if prices fall 30% after I buy, do I survive — and am I getting enough margin that being early or wrong still leaves me whole?" The first question you cannot answer honestly. The second you can answer completely, today, from your own numbers.

This is why disciplined investors across every asset class — not just property — talk about *process over outcome*. A good process can have a bad outcome (you bought carefully and the market still fell), and a bad process can have a good outcome (you gambled and got lucky). Over many decisions and many years, the process is what compounds. Luck does not.

### The four things a framework controls that a forecast ignores

A forecast stakes everything on one thing you cannot control: the future price. A framework focuses on four things you *can* control completely:

1. **Your margin of safety.** Buy below your own conservative estimate of value, not at it. If you are wrong about something — and you will be — the discount absorbs the error.
2. **Your leverage.** How much you borrow is entirely your choice. Leverage is the single biggest determinant of whether a normal drawdown is survivable or fatal.
3. **Your liquidity.** How much cash you hold in reserve decides whether a market freeze makes you a forced seller or a patient buyer.
4. **Your concentration.** How much of your total wealth rides on one illiquid asset is your decision, made before you buy.

A forecaster who calls the top perfectly but uses 90% leverage with no cash buffer can still be wiped out by a small surprise. A framework user who has no idea where the top is, but caps leverage at 50% and holds a year of payments in cash, survives almost anything the cycle throws at them. *The framework wins not by being smarter about the future, but by being disciplined about the present.*

### The running example: Minh decides whether to buy

Throughout this post we follow **Minh**, a salaried professional in HCMC, mid-thirties, who has saved ₫2.5 billion (≈ \$96,000) and is deciding whether to buy an apartment now. He is not a speculator; he wants a home, and he would like it to also be a sound financial decision. We will run his decision through all six steps and end with a concrete answer. We will also bring back **Dana**, a US investor from earlier posts, to keep the universal model honest against a different market. By the end, the abstract framework will be a worked decision with real numbers.

### The framework as one page of numbers you write down

There is a reason the framework is a checklist and not a feeling. The act of *writing the numbers down* is itself most of the discipline. A decision that lives only in your head can be quietly bent by hope — you round the rent up, you assume the teaser rate lasts forever, you forget the down payment is your entire savings. A decision written on one page cannot hide from you. So before any property purchase, the framework asks you to fill in, on paper, the following:

- **Phase:** one word — recovery, expansion, hypersupply, or recession — plus the two or three indicators that justify it.
- **Macro:** the rate you would actually pay *after* any teaser reset; the price-to-income ratio of the market versus its history; one word on credit — easing or tightening.
- **The deal:** your own conservative value estimate, and the gap between it and the asking price (the margin of safety, in money and in percent).
- **The risk:** your loan-to-value, the price fall you survive while staying in positive equity, and the months of payments you hold in cash.
- **The fit:** the share of your net worth this asset will be after you buy, and whether it moves with your income.
- **The action:** buy, wait, or pass — and the price and leverage at which the answer flips.

Six lines. If you cannot fill all six in honestly, you are not ready to sign. The rest of this post is simply how to fill in each line well — and the running example shows what they look like filled in for a real buyer.

With the philosophy in place — process over prediction — we can walk the six steps.

## Step 1: Read the cycle phase (the dashboard)

The first question is not "is this a good deal?" It is "*where are we in the cycle?*" — because the same deal is good in one phase and a trap in another. Everything else in the framework is calibrated by the answer.

### A two-minute recap of the four phases

We covered this in depth in [the anatomy of a real-estate cycle](/blog/trading/real-estate/anatomy-of-a-real-estate-cycle-four-phases), so here is the compressed version. Property markets move through a repeating four-phase loop, driven by the long lag between a price signal and the new supply it triggers:

- **Recovery.** Occupancy is below its long-run normal but *rising*. Prices are still soft, few people are building, sentiment is gloomy. This is when quality assets are cheapest and competition is thinnest.
- **Expansion.** Occupancy has climbed above normal and is *still rising*. Rents grow, building accelerates, sentiment turns optimistic. Prices are fair-to-rich; the easy money has been made.
- **Hypersupply.** Occupancy is above normal but now *falling*, because the building started in the boom is finally delivering. Cranes are everywhere, sentiment is euphoric, and prices peak. This is the most dangerous phase to buy.
- **Recession.** Occupancy is below normal and *still falling*. Building stops, distress appears, sentiment is fearful. This is when the patient buyer with cash does best, even though it feels worst.

The trap is that the *feeling* of each phase is the opposite of the *opportunity*. Hypersupply feels like the safest time to buy (everyone's making money, prices only go up) and is the most dangerous. Recession feels like the most dangerous time to buy (everyone's losing money, prices only go down) and is the safest. The framework exists partly to override that emotional inversion.

### Building the dashboard

You read the phase from a handful of public indicators — the subject of [leading indicators: reading the cycle in real time](/blog/trading/real-estate/leading-indicators-reading-the-cycle-in-real-time). You do not need all of them; you need enough to triangulate. The most useful, in rough order:

- **Vacancy / absorption trend.** Is empty space filling (occupancy rising) or emptying (falling)? This is the single most diagnostic reading, because the phase is *defined* by where occupancy sits versus normal and which way it is moving.
- **Rent growth.** Accelerating rents signal expansion; flat or falling rents with "incentives" signal a turn.
- **Construction starts and the development pipeline.** A flood of starts and cranes means supply is coming — the seeds of hypersupply. A pipeline that has dried up means future supply is scarce, which sets up the next recovery.
- **Credit conditions.** Is lending loose and cheap, or tightening? Property booms run on credit; a credit crunch (Vietnam 2022) is what turns hypersupply into recession.
- **Sentiment and transaction volume.** Euphoria with rising volume is late-cycle; fear with collapsed volume (nobody transacting) is the bottom forming.

#### Worked example: scoring HCMC's current cycle phase

Suppose Minh assembles his dashboard for HCMC as of his decision. He sees: primary apartment prices up sharply over recent years (Hanoi from ~₫72M/m² to ~₫100M/m², HCMC from ~₫91M/m² to ~₫111M/m² — ≈ \$4,222/m²); a development pipeline that thinned dramatically during the 2022–23 bond freeze and is only now restarting; secondary transaction volume that collapsed in the freeze and is recovering slowly; and credit that tightened brutally in 2022 but where the central bank has since cut policy rates four times. He scores it like this:

- Occupancy / demand: recovering off a low base — *rising* → not recession.
- New supply pipeline: thin after the freeze killed many projects → supply is *scarce*, not flooding.
- Credit: was a crunch, now easing → moving from tight to loose.
- Sentiment: cautious, not euphoric; volume recovering, not exploding.

His read: HCMC is in the **early-to-mid recovery** phase of its own cycle — demand rising off a low base, supply scarce after the freeze, credit easing, sentiment still cautious. That is a constructive phase to *accumulate quality*, not a top to avoid. He notes the caveat that the broader 18-year land cycle's projected global peak window is 2026–2028, so he should stay alert for the next hypersupply build-up — but the *current* local read is recovery. *Reading the phase first turns a vague "is now a good time?" into a specific, defensible answer that calibrates every later step.*

The dashboard does not tell Minh prices will rise. It tells him the *odds are tilted* the way an accumulator wants — cheap relative to the last peak, scarce new supply, easing credit. That tilt is what Step 1 buys him.

## Step 2: Check the macro — rates, affordability, credit

Step 1 placed us on the cycle. Step 2 checks the three macro forces that decide how much *fuel* the market has and how survivable a purchase is: interest rates, affordability, and credit. These are market-wide; they apply to every deal, not just the one in front of you.

### Rates: the master variable

We called interest rates [the master variable](/blog/trading/real-estate/interest-rates-and-mortgages-the-master-variable) for a reason. A property is worth the income it throws off (for a rental) or the housing it provides (for a home), and the price you can pay is set by what you can *finance*. When mortgage rates fall, the same monthly payment supports a bigger loan, so prices can rise even if nothing else changes. When rates rise, the reverse: the same income now "buys" a smaller loan, and prices come under pressure. This is the same seesaw that governs bonds — [price and yield move opposite](/blog/trading/fixed-income/price-and-yield-the-seesaw-at-the-heart-of-bonds) — and it is why the rate environment is the second thing the framework checks.

For the framework, the rate question is not "where will rates go?" (a forecast) but two preparation questions:

1. **Is the loan I would take survivable if rates rise?** In Vietnam especially, the teaser-then-reset structure is a trap: a preferential ~8% rate for 12–24 months, then a floating reset to **12–14%** (one SOE post-preferential ceiling was cited at 13.9%). You must underwrite the *reset* rate, not the teaser.
2. **Are rates high or low relative to history?** Buying when rates are at a record low (US 30-year hit **2.65%** in January 2021) means you are buying near peak affordability *from financing* — which often coincides with peak prices. Buying when rates have spiked (US 30-year hit **7.79%** in October 2023) often coincides with depressed prices and is, counter-intuitively, the better entry, because you can refinance lower later but you locked in a cheaper price.

#### Worked example: the teaser-rate trap

Minh is offered a ₫5 billion (≈ \$193,000) loan at a teaser rate of 8% for 24 months, resetting afterward to a floating rate he is told is "around 13%." He models both. At 8% over a 20-year term, the monthly payment is roughly ₫41.8 million. At 13%, the payment on the remaining balance jumps to roughly ₫58 million — a **39% increase** in his largest monthly outflow, arriving on a fixed date he knows in advance. His take-home income is ₫70 million a month. At the teaser rate the payment is 60% of income — already stretched; at the reset rate it is **83% of income**, which is not survivable alongside food, utilities, and a life. The deal only works if he either borrows less or has a plan to refinance or prepay before the reset. *Underwriting the reset rate instead of the teaser is the difference between a budget that holds and one that breaks on a date you could have seen coming.*

### Affordability: the ceiling on how far prices can run

Affordability — captured by the **price-to-income ratio**, the number of years of median income it takes to buy a median home — is the brake on a market. We unpacked it in [income, affordability, and the price-to-income ratio](/blog/trading/real-estate/income-affordability-and-the-price-to-income-ratio). When the ratio gets extreme, prices are running on credit and speculation rather than on incomes, which is fragile. HCMC sits around **32** years of income (one VnExpress framing put it at "34 years," fifth-highest globally); Hanoi around **31**. For comparison: Singapore 17, Tokyo 15, London 12, New York 10, global median ~15. A ratio in the low thirties is a flashing amber light: it does not mean prices fall tomorrow, but it means the cushion is thin and the market is vulnerable to any shock to credit or confidence.

For the framework, affordability is a *context* gauge. A high ratio says: be more conservative on leverage, demand a bigger margin of safety, and weight the downside more heavily — because if the market does turn, an unaffordable market has further to fall and fewer marginal buyers to catch it.

### Credit: the fuel line

Property booms run on credit, and busts are usually *credit events*. We traced this in [the credit cycle and property prices](/blog/trading/real-estate/the-credit-cycle-and-property-prices) and [credit and developer financing](/blog/trading/real-estate/credit-and-developer-financing). The framework's credit check is simple: *is lending getting easier or harder?* Easing credit (more lenders, looser terms, lower rates) is fuel for prices and supports an accumulate stance. Tightening credit — and especially a sudden freeze like Vietnam's 2022 corporate-bond crunch, when the funding for developers vanished almost overnight — is the trigger that turns the cycle down. If credit is freezing, the only safe stance is defensive: hold cash, avoid leverage, and wait for the distress that a credit freeze inevitably produces.

For broader rates-and-liquidity context that sits underneath all three of these gauges, the [liquidity cycle and asset prices](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-liquidity-cycle-and-asset-prices) is the macro backdrop the whole framework runs against.

The cycle phase and the macro together tell you *whether the environment is friendly*. The next three steps move from the market to the specific deal and to you.

## Step 3: Underwrite the deal (does it pencil with a margin of safety?)

Steps 1 and 2 were about the *market*. Step 3 is about the *specific property*. A friendly cycle and benign macro do not make a bad deal good; you still have to verify that *this* property, at *this* price, makes financial sense — and that it makes sense even if several things go wrong. This is **underwriting**, the subject of [underwriting a property deal](/blog/trading/real-estate/underwriting-a-property-deal), recapped here as a step in the larger process.

![The pro-forma walks gross rent down through vacancy, expenses, and debt service to the net cash flow on which all the return metrics are built](/imgs/blogs/a-framework-for-buying-through-the-cycle-2.png)

### What underwriting means

To **underwrite** a deal is to build a *pro-forma* — a line-by-line projection of the money in and the money out — and then ask whether the resulting cash flow and value justify the price. For an income property the spine is:

1. **Gross potential rent** — what the property would earn fully rented at market rates.
2. **minus vacancy** — no property is rented 100% of the time; subtract a realistic allowance (often 5%).
3. **minus operating expenses** — taxes, management, insurance, repairs, the things that do not go away.
4. **= net operating income (NOI)** — the property's pre-debt earnings, the number everything else is built on.
5. **minus debt service** — your mortgage payment (principal + interest).
6. **= cash flow** — what actually lands in your pocket, from which you compute the return metrics: cap rate (NOI ÷ price), cash-on-cash (year-1 cash flow ÷ cash invested), and IRR (the whole hold including the sale). We defined these in [cap rate, NOI, and the income approach](/blog/trading/real-estate/cap-rate-noi-and-the-income-approach).

For a home rather than a rental, the same discipline applies in a different currency: instead of rent, you weigh the cost of owning (mortgage, maintenance, taxes, the opportunity cost of your down payment) against the cost of renting the same place — the [rent-vs-buy math](/blog/trading/real-estate/rent-vs-buy-the-real-math). The point is identical: write the numbers down and make them survive. In a high-price-to-income market like HCMC, this comparison is often uncomfortable: the rental yield on a ₫3.5 billion apartment might be only 3–4%, while the *rent* on the identical unit costs far less per year than the full cost of owning it (mortgage interest plus maintenance plus the foregone return on a ₫2 billion down payment). That gap does not mean "never buy" — a home delivers security, control, and a hedge against future rent increases that a spreadsheet undervalues — but it does mean the buyer should *know* they are paying a premium for those non-financial benefits and for expected appreciation, rather than pretending the purchase is a slam-dunk on yield alone. The framework's job here is to make that premium explicit, not to hide it.

### The two non-negotiables: stress and margin

A pro-forma built on optimistic assumptions is a story, not a number. Two disciplines turn it into a real underwrite:

**Stress the assumptions.** Re-run the pro-forma with the rosy inputs replaced by pessimistic ones: rent flat instead of rising, vacancy at 10% instead of 5%, the teaser rate reset to 13% instead of 8%, the exit value (resale price) at the going-in cap rate or worse rather than a richer one. The exit assumption deserves special suspicion: assuming you sell at a lower cap rate than you bought (i.e., a higher price multiple) is the most common way to manufacture a fake IRR. A deal that still works under stress is a deal; one that only works under sunshine is a hope.

**Demand a margin of safety.** Compute your own conservative estimate of the property's value, then refuse to pay it. Buy *below* fair value, so that if you are wrong about something — and underwriting is full of estimates — the discount absorbs the error. A margin of safety is the financial equivalent of leaving room between your car and the one ahead: it costs you nothing when nothing goes wrong, and it saves you when something does.

#### Worked example: the margin-of-safety go/no-go

Minh finds a ₫4 billion (≈ \$154,000) apartment he could rent for ₫16 million a month (₫192M/yr gross). He underwrites it conservatively. Apply 8% vacancy (₫15M) and ₫45M of annual expenses → NOI ≈ ₫132M. That is a cap rate of ₫132M ÷ ₫4,000M = **3.3%** — thin, but typical for high-price-to-income Vietnamese residential, where buyers price in expected appreciation, not just yield. Now he checks the *margin*. He estimates that for the cash flow to make sense at a more normal 4.0% yield, the property would be worth ₫132M ÷ 0.040 = **₫3.3 billion**. The asking price of ₫4.0 billion is *above* his conservative value — there is no margin of safety; he would be paying full price (or more) for the income, betting entirely on appreciation. The framework's verdict on this specific deal is **no-go at ₫4.0bn**: either negotiate toward ₫3.3–3.5bn, or pass and keep looking. *Margin of safety converts "I like this apartment" into a price discipline that protects you from your own enthusiasm.*

Notice what Step 3 did *not* do: it did not tell Minh prices would rise or fall. It told him that *at this price*, this specific deal has no cushion. That is a fact about the deal, knowable today, independent of any forecast.

## Step 4: Size the risk (survive a drawdown, never be forced to sell)

Suppose the deal does pencil with a margin of safety. The next question is the one that separates investors who last from investors who blow up: *how big a position, and with how much leverage?* This is **risk sizing**, the subject of [risk management in real estate](/blog/trading/real-estate/risk-management-in-real-estate), and it is where most of the real damage in property is done — not in choosing the wrong building, but in choosing the wrong leverage.

### Leverage: the amplifier that works both ways

We dissected this in [leverage and the mortgage](/blog/trading/real-estate/leverage-and-the-mortgage-how-debt-amplifies-property). The key fact: leverage multiplies your return *and* your loss, symmetrically, around the loan rate. Borrow 70% to buy a property and a 7% gain on the asset can become a much larger gain on your *equity* — but a 20% fall in the asset can wipe out a multiple of your equity, because the loan does not shrink when the value does. Loan-to-value (**LTV**) is the dial: at 50% LTV a 30% price fall leaves you with positive equity; at 90% LTV a 20% fall puts you *underwater* — you owe more than the property is worth.

The framework's leverage rule is not "use less leverage" as a slogan. It is precise: **choose the LTV at which a normal drawdown — call it 30% — leaves you still able to service the loan and still in positive equity.** That makes a drawdown painful but survivable, never terminal.

### The cardinal rule: never be a forced seller

Here is the single most important rule in the entire framework, and it deserves its own line:

> **Never put yourself in a position where you are *forced* to sell at the bottom.**

A *forced seller* is someone who must sell *now*, at whatever price is on offer, because they cannot make a payment, the bank is calling the loan, or they need the cash. Forced sellers are how ordinary drawdowns become personal catastrophes. The market falling 30% does not hurt you if you can simply *wait* — prices recover over the cycle, and a buyer who holds through the trough is fine. It is the *forced* sale into the trough that crystallizes a paper loss into a real, permanent one, and often leaves you still owing the bank.

What forces a sale? Two things, both controllable in advance: **a payment you cannot make** (too much leverage, or a teaser reset you did not plan for) and **no cash buffer** to ride out a freeze. So the framework demands a **liquidity buffer**: 6–12 months of mortgage payments (and ideally the property's running costs) held in cash, untouchable, before you buy. That buffer is what lets you sit still while panicked, over-levered owners sell into your eventual bid.

This is the same logic that governs any leveraged position across markets — the [cross-asset view of real estate, REITs, income, leverage, and rates](/blog/trading/cross-asset/real-estate-reits-income-leverage-and-rates) shows the identical dynamic playing out in listed property, where leverage and a funding squeeze turn a price dip into forced deleveraging.

#### Worked example: sizing the position for a 30% drawdown

Minh is tempted to buy the ₫3.5 billion (≈ \$135,000) apartment he negotiated down to, using his full ₫2.5 billion of savings as down payment and borrowing ₫1.0 billion — an LTV of about 29%, which sounds safe. But that would leave him with **zero cash buffer**: every dong of savings is in the walls. The framework reframes it. He instead puts ₫2.0 billion down (57%) and borrows ₫1.5 billion (43% LTV), keeping **₫500 million in cash** — roughly 12 months of the ₫41.8M payment plus running costs. Now he stress-tests a 30% fall: the apartment drops to ₫2.45 billion, still comfortably above his ₫1.5 billion loan (positive equity of ₫950M), and his ₫500M buffer covers a year of payments even if his income is interrupted. He is *never* a forced seller in this configuration. The "safe-sounding" all-savings-in version, by contrast, would have left him solvent on paper but with no cash to survive a shock — the most common way people get forced out. *Sizing for the drawdown — leverage you can service plus a year of payments in cash — is what converts a 30% fall from a catastrophe into an inconvenience.*

#### Worked example: Dana sizes the same rule in dollars

The rule is universal, so watch Dana, a US investor, run it on a \$500,000 rental. If she pays all cash, a 30% fall costs her \$150,000 of equity but cannot *force* a sale — there is no loan to call, so she simply waits. If instead she borrows 80% (\$400,000 loan, \$100,000 down), that same 30% fall takes the property to \$350,000, *below* her \$400,000 loan: she is \$50,000 underwater, and if a tenant leaves while the mortgage runs, she can be forced to sell into the trough and still owe the bank. At 50% leverage (\$250,000 loan), the 30% fall leaves the property at \$350,000 against a \$250,000 loan — \$100,000 of equity remains, and with even six months of payments in reserve she rides it out. The framework's number for Dana is therefore a leverage cap somewhere near 50–60%, not the 80% the bank will happily offer. *The same drawdown is a paper dip at 50% leverage and a personal bankruptcy at 80% — and the only variable that changed was a number she chose herself.*

![Two buyers entering at the 2021 to 2022 top, contrasting a FOMO buyer with no framework who is forced to sell against a disciplined buyer who keeps liquidity and buys in the recession](/imgs/blogs/a-framework-for-buying-through-the-cycle-5.png)

The figure above is the same lesson told as two life paths through the 2022–23 freeze — the FOMO buyer wiped out by a reset and a forced sale, the disciplined buyer waiting in cash and buying the distress. We return to the numbers behind it in the real-markets section.

## Step 5: Fit it in your portfolio (the concentration check)

Steps 3 and 4 made sure the deal is sound and survivable *on its own*. Step 5 zooms out to your whole financial life and asks a different question: *given everything else you own, should you put this much into one illiquid asset?* This is portfolio fit, the subject of [real estate in a portfolio](/blog/trading/real-estate/real-estate-in-a-portfolio), and it is the step individual buyers most often skip — usually because for most people, a home *is* the entire portfolio.

### Why concentration is the hidden risk

Property has two features that make concentration dangerous. First, it is **illiquid**: you cannot sell a fraction of an apartment, and selling the whole thing takes months even in good times and can be impossible in a freeze. Second, the minimum position is **enormous relative to a typical net worth**: a single apartment can be several times a household's annual income and the bulk of its wealth. Put those together and a property buyer is making a bet that is both huge and impossible to trim. A stock investor who is too concentrated can sell 10% of a position in seconds; a homeowner cannot.

The diversification logic is universal — the [diversification free lunch](/blog/trading/cross-asset/correlation-and-the-diversification-free-lunch) is that spreading wealth across assets that do not move together reduces risk without necessarily reducing return. Property's lumpiness makes that free lunch hard to eat, which is *more* reason to be deliberate about how much of your wealth one property represents.

### The framework's concentration check

The check is a single ratio, computed *after* the purchase: **what share of your total net worth is this one property?** There is no universal cap, but the framework's discipline is to *know the number and decide consciously*, not to back into 100% by default. A useful rule of thumb for an investor (as opposed to a primary home) is to keep any single illiquid asset from dominating the portfolio — and for a primary home, to at least ensure you hold meaningful liquid assets *outside* the house, so the house is not literally everything.

The other half of fit is **correlation with your income.** Your job and your local property market are often driven by the same local economy. If HCMC's economy turns down, Minh could lose income *and* see his apartment fall *at the same time* — a doubly bad outcome the framework flags. Holding some assets uncorrelated with both (cash, broad equities, gold) is the hedge.

#### Worked example: the concentration check for Minh

Minh's total net worth before buying is his ₫2.5 billion in savings plus, say, ₫0.5 billion in a retirement fund and equities — ₫3.0 billion (≈ \$116,000) total. If he buys the ₫3.5 billion apartment with ₫2.0 billion down and a ₫1.5 billion loan, his *equity* in the property is ₫2.0 billion, and his net worth becomes ₫2.0bn (home equity) + ₫0.5bn (other) = ₫2.5 billion. The apartment equity is **80% of his net worth**, and it is completely illiquid and highly correlated with his HCMC income. That is concentrated — acceptable for a primary home he intends to live in for a decade, far too concentrated if this were a pure investment. The framework's verdict: *as a home, proceed but keep the ₫500M buffer sacrosanct as the only liquid asset standing between him and a forced sale; as an investment, this would fail the concentration check and he should buy something smaller or wait to build more outside-the-house wealth first.* *The concentration check does not always say no, but it forces you to see the bet for what it is — and to protect the small liquid cushion that keeps the whole thing from being all-or-nothing.*

With the deal sound, survivable, and consciously sized within his life, the last step is to act — and the *right* action depends on the phase we read in Step 1.

## Step 6: Act by phase (the phase-by-phase playbook)

The first five steps produce a decision about *this* deal. Step 6 sets the *posture* — the stance you take across all your property decisions given the phase of the cycle. The same underwriting discipline applies in every phase, but the aggressiveness, the leverage, and the conviction shift.

![A matrix showing the recommended action, leverage, conviction, and what to avoid in each of the four cycle phases of recovery, expansion, hypersupply, and recession](/imgs/blogs/a-framework-for-buying-through-the-cycle-3.png)

The matrix above is the playbook. Read down a column to see how one variable (say, leverage) changes across phases; read across a row to see the full stance for one phase.

- **Recovery — accumulate.** Prices are below trend, competition is thin, credit is easing, supply is scarce. This is when you *buy quality at a discount*, with moderate leverage (rates are often still low) and high conviction (you are early, but the odds are tilted your way). What to avoid: waiting for the "all-clear," which only arrives after prices have already run.
- **Expansion — be selective.** Prices are fair-to-rich, building is accelerating, sentiment is optimistic. Keep buying *only* deals that still pencil with a margin of safety, keep a leverage buffer (do not max out), and accept medium conviction (you are paying closer to fair value). What to avoid: paying up *just because prices rose* — the late-cycle FOMO that the next phase punishes.
- **Hypersupply — defend.** Occupancy is falling, the pipeline is flooding, sentiment is euphoric, prices peak. The stance flips from offense to defense: *sell your weakest assets, trim, de-lever, raise your cash buffer.* Cut leverage and conviction to the bone. What to avoid above all: **buying your biggest position at the top** — the single most expensive mistake in property.
- **Recession — pounce.** Occupancy is below normal and still falling, distress is everywhere, sentiment is fearful. This is when the buyer with cash and low leverage does best, buying *from* forced sellers at prices that build in a large margin of safety. Conviction rises *because* of the distress. What to avoid: the fantasy of catching the *exact* bottom — which usually means having no cash deployed when the real bargains appear, because you were waiting for one more down-tick.

The deep irony, drawn in the matrix, is that the natural human posture is the *inverse* of this playbook: people feel most confident and lever up most at the top (hypersupply), and feel most fearful and sit frozen at the bottom (recession). The framework's whole job in Step 6 is to override that instinct — to make you defensive when you feel invincible and opportunistic when you feel terrified.

#### Worked example: the buy/wait decision for Minh today

Now we run the whole framework on Minh's actual decision. **Step 1 (phase):** he scored HCMC in early-to-mid *recovery* — supply scarce after the freeze, credit easing, sentiment cautious. Posture: *accumulate*. **Step 2 (macro):** rates have come down from the freeze highs and the central bank has cut four times, but he must underwrite the loan at the ~13% reset, not the 8% teaser; affordability at ~32× income is a flashing amber, so he stays conservative on leverage. **Step 3 (underwrite):** the ₫4.0 billion asking failed his margin-of-safety test; he negotiated to ₫3.5 billion, at which the deal has a thin but real cushion for a home he will hold a decade. **Step 4 (size):** he puts ₫2.0 billion down (43% LTV) and keeps ₫500 million — about 12 months of payments — in cash, so a 30% fall leaves positive equity and he is never a forced seller. **Step 5 (fit):** as a *primary home* held long-term, the 80%-of-net-worth concentration is acceptable provided the cash buffer stays untouched; as an investment it would fail. **Step 6 (act):** the phase says accumulate, the deal pencils with a buffer, and the risk is sized to survive — so the verdict is **buy, at ₫3.5 billion, at 43% LTV, with the ₫500M buffer ring-fenced.** Had any one gate failed — a top-of-cycle phase, a teaser he could not survive, no margin of safety, or no cash buffer — the answer would have been *wait* or *pass*. *Running the deal through all six steps turns an emotional, once-in-a-decade decision into a checklist with a defensible answer at the end.*

![A matrix of five discipline rules, each paired with why it protects the buyer and the specific failure it prevents](/imgs/blogs/a-framework-for-buying-through-the-cycle-4.png)

The figure above distills the framework into the five rules that do the most work. If you forget everything else, these five are the spine: don't buy your biggest position at the top, never be a forced seller, demand a margin of safety, respect affordability, and size for a drawdown. Each one blocks a specific, common way people get hurt.

## Putting it together: the buy / wait / pass decision

The six steps converge on one of three actions, and the logic is a simple gate.

![A decision graph showing three gates of pencils with margin, survives a drawdown, and fits the portfolio, leading to buy if all pass, wait if fixable, or pass if a gate fails](/imgs/blogs/a-framework-for-buying-through-the-cycle-7.png)

A deal earns a **buy** only when it clears all three gates at once: it *pencils with a margin of safety* (Step 3), you can *survive a 30% drawdown without being forced to sell* (Step 4), and it *fits your portfolio* without dangerous concentration (Step 5) — in a *phase* that supports the action (Steps 1, 2, 6). If a gate fails but the failure is *fixable* — the price is too high but negotiable, the timing is wrong but you can wait for a better phase — the answer is **wait**: keep your cash and revisit at the next turn. If a gate fails on the *deal itself* — the property is fundamentally overpriced, you cannot survive its leverage, or it would wreck your concentration — the answer is **pass**, and you walk away knowing the next opportunity always comes around. The cycle guarantees it.

The discipline here is that *any one red gate fails the whole deal.* A great property at a great price that you can only afford with terminal leverage is still a pass. A perfectly-sized purchase in a market at the euphoric top is still a wait. You do not get to average the gates; you must clear all of them. This is what makes the framework a *brake* and not just a *guide* — it gives you explicit permission, in advance, to say no.

### The whole series in one dashboard

Step back and the framework is really a way of reading the series itself. Every post we wrote is one dial on a single dashboard.

![A grid dashboard of seven levers that move property, cycle phase, interest rates, credit, supply elasticity, demographics, tax, and affordability, to be read together as a system](/imgs/blogs/a-framework-for-buying-through-the-cycle-6.png)

The dashboard above is the synthesis. Seven levers move property: the **cycle phase** (the lag between price and supply), **interest rates** (the master variable), **credit** (the fuel that freezes), **supply elasticity** (why some cities boom and bust harder than others — the subject of [supply elasticity](/blog/trading/real-estate/supply-elasticity-why-some-cities-boom-and-bust)), **demographics** (the slow, powerful [demand wave](/blog/trading/real-estate/demographics-and-urbanization-the-demand-wave)), **tax** (which [shapes behavior](/blog/trading/real-estate/how-tax-shapes-behavior-speculation-and-hoarding), speculation, and supply), and **affordability** (the ceiling on how far prices run on income). The framework's job is to read these dials *together*, as a system, rather than reacting to any single headline. No one dial decides; their combination sets the phase and the price.

This is also where the [land-versus-structure lens](/blog/trading/real-estate/land-vs-buildings-two-assets-in-every-property) sharpens the read: in a boom, it is almost always the *land* component appreciating (structures depreciate), so a market running hot on land speculation — Vietnam's *đất nền* fevers, for instance — is more cyclical and more dangerous than one where you are paying mostly for a building's use. And the [tax lens](/blog/trading/real-estate/how-real-estate-is-taxed-the-full-map) reminds you that the *after-tax* return is the real return: a 2% transfer tax and 0.5% registration fee on each trade penalize flipping, while the absence (so far) of a broad annual holding tax in Vietnam removes a carrying cost that, elsewhere, forces hoarders to sell. [Land Law 2024](/blog/trading/real-estate/vietnam-land-law-2024-and-the-coming-property-tax) and the floated second-home tax could change that math, which is why the framework re-runs every cycle rather than being set once.

## Common misconceptions

### "A framework lets you time the exact bottom"

It does not, and that is the point. No framework, indicator, or expert reliably calls the exact bottom — the bottom is only visible in hindsight, after prices have already turned up. What the framework gives you is a *range* and a *posture*: it tells you when the odds favor accumulating (recovery, distress, large margins of safety) versus when they favor defense (euphoria, thin margins, flooding supply). Acting on a favorable *posture* with a margin of safety and a cash buffer beats waiting for a perfect *moment* that you will never recognize in time. The buyers who do best in a recession are not the ones who nailed the exact low; they are the ones who had cash and conviction *across* the bottoming region and kept buying as bargains appeared.

### "A framework removes risk"

It does not remove risk — it *manages* it. Property can fall after you buy no matter how carefully you underwrote; a careful buyer in 2021 could still have caught the 2022 freeze. What the framework does is ensure that when the downside hits, it is *survivable*: your margin of safety cushions the price, your leverage keeps you in positive equity, and your liquidity keeps you from being a forced seller. The goal was never zero risk — that would mean never buying. The goal is to *never take a risk that can wreck you*, while still taking the risks that, over a cycle, are well paid. Confusing "managed risk" with "no risk" is how people abandon the framework the first time it does not prevent a paper loss.

### "If it cash-flows, nothing else matters"

Positive cash flow is necessary but not sufficient. A property that throws off cash today can still be a bad decision if you bought it at the top with maximum leverage, because a price fall can wipe out your equity even while the rent keeps coming — and if the *teaser rate resets* or a tenant leaves, the cash flow you were counting on can vanish exactly when you need it. Cash flow tells you the deal feeds itself in *year one* under *current* conditions; it tells you nothing about the exit, the sensitivity to a rate reset, or whether you can survive a freeze. The framework checks cash flow as *one* gate (Step 3), not the only one. Many over-levered buyers in 2022 had positive cash flow right up until the rate reset turned it negative.

### "Buying a home is different — the framework is for investors"

The framework applies *more* forcefully to a home, not less, because a home is usually a household's single largest and most concentrated financial commitment. The cash-flow line becomes a rent-vs-buy comparison rather than a rental yield, but every other step is identical: read the phase (are you buying at the euphoric top?), check the macro (can you survive the rate reset?), demand a margin of safety (negotiate below asking), size the leverage so a drawdown does not bury you, and keep a cash buffer so a job loss does not force a sale. Treating "it's just our home" as a reason to skip the discipline is precisely how families end up forced sellers in a downturn.

### "Property always goes up over the long run, so timing doesn't matter"

Over a *long enough* horizon and in a *growing* economy, property has historically trended up — but "the long run" can be brutally long, and you may not have it. US housing took roughly **six years** to recover its 2006 peak (and longer in real, inflation-adjusted terms); Japanese land prices peaked in 1990 and *still* have not recovered three decades later. If you bought at the top with high leverage and got forced out in year three, "it recovers eventually" is cold comfort — you crystallized the loss and missed the recovery. Timing the *exact* top is impossible, but *avoiding the obvious euphoric top with maximum leverage* is entirely possible, and that is most of what the framework asks.

## How it shows up in real markets

### The disciplined buyer vs the FOMO buyer through 2022–23

This is the case the whole post has been building toward, now with numbers. Consider two HCMC buyers at the 2021 top. The **FOMO buyer** purchases at ₫8.5 billion (≈ \$328,000) with 85% LTV (₫7.2bn loan), an 8% teaser rate, and essentially no cash buffer — every spare dong went into the down payment. The **disciplined buyer** runs the framework, reads the market as late-cycle/hypersupply, and *waits* in cash. Through 2022–23, the bond freeze hits: the FOMO buyer's teaser resets toward 13.9%, his payment jumps ~39%, and when the bank tightens there is no secondary bid. He becomes a forced seller at roughly ₫6.0 billion — a **~30% loss** that wipes out his equity and may leave him still owing on the loan. The disciplined buyer, holding cash through the freeze, buys a comparable unit in the 2023 recession at ₫6.0 billion with 50% LTV and a 12-month payment buffer, then rides the recovery up. Same market, same starting point; the framework decided who survived. *The freeze did not choose its victims at random — it chose the over-levered, buffer-less buyers who had skipped Step 4.*

### What the framework would have said at each cycle top

**US 2006.** By 2005–06, US housing was screaming hypersupply: the Case-Shiller national index hit **184.6** (July 2006) on a wave of subprime credit, affordability was stretched, and building was at record highs. The framework's reading — euphoric sentiment, flooding supply, dangerously loose credit — would have said *defend, de-lever, do not buy your biggest position here.* The index then fell to **134** by February 2012, a **−27%** national drop (far worse in the bubble cities). Buyers who used the framework's leverage and liquidity rules survived; those who bought at the top with no-money-down loans were the forced sellers who made the crisis a [catastrophe](/blog/trading/real-estate/case-study-us-2008-subprime-crisis).

**Japan 1990.** Japanese land prices in the late 1980s reached valuations so extreme that the grounds of the Imperial Palace were said to be worth more than all of California. Affordability had detached completely from incomes; credit was wildly loose. The framework — affordability off the charts, euphoria total — screamed *defend*. The bubble burst in 1990 and land prices fell for *decades*; a buyer at the top with leverage was not just drawn down but, in many cases, permanently impaired. This is the cautionary extreme of "property always goes up." See [Japan's 1990 land bubble](/blog/trading/real-estate/case-study-japan-1990-land-bubble).

**China / Evergrande, 2021.** China's property developers, Evergrande chief among them, had built an entire model on ever-rising land prices and ever-available credit — the very fuel line the framework flags. When credit tightened (the "three red lines" policy), the model broke, Evergrande defaulted, and a wave of distress followed. A developer or buyer running the framework would have seen the extreme leverage and credit dependence and stayed defensive. See [China Evergrande](/blog/trading/real-estate/case-study-china-evergrande-property-bust).

**Vietnam 2022.** The local case we have returned to throughout. After a 2018 *đất nền* fever and a 2021 run-up, the market was late-cycle when the Tân Hoàng Minh (April 2022) and Vạn Thịnh Phát/SCB (October 2022) arrests froze the corporate-bond market that funded developers. Prices in many segments fell 20–30%, projects stalled, and over-levered buyers were forced out. The framework's credit gauge — a freezing funding line — was the clearest possible *defend* signal. See [Vietnam's 2022 bond and property freeze](/blog/trading/real-estate/case-study-vietnam-2022-bond-and-property-freeze) and the broader [Vietnam property cycles](/blog/trading/real-estate/vietnam-property-cycles-2007-2013-2022).

### Singapore: a market where the framework is partly run *for* you

Singapore is instructive because its government actively leans against the cycle with [cooling measures](/blog/trading/real-estate/case-study-singapore-cooling-measures) — stamp duties that rise with how many properties you own and how foreign you are, loan-to-value caps that tighten in booms. In effect, the state forces a version of Step 4 (leverage limits) and Step 2 (affordability discipline) on every buyer. The lesson for an investor in a *less* managed market like Vietnam is that you must impose this discipline on *yourself*, because no one else will. The cooling measures exist precisely because, left alone, buyers reliably over-lever at the top.

### The patient buyer in every recession

The recurring winner across all these episodes is the same character: the buyer who kept cash and low leverage through the boom, stayed defensive at the top, and then *bought from forced sellers* in the bust. After 2008, the investors who bought distressed US homes in 2010–2012 — near the ₫134 / index-134 trough — rode the recovery to ~322 by 2024. In Vietnam, the buyers with cash in 2023 picked up units the 2021 FOMO crowd was dumping. The framework does not make you that buyer by predicting the bottom; it makes you that buyer by ensuring you have *cash and conviction* when everyone else has neither. *Being the buyer of last resort is not a personality trait — it is the mechanical result of following Steps 4 and 6.*

## When this matters / Further reading

This framework matters most at the single moment it is hardest to think clearly: when you are about to make the largest financial commitment of your life, under emotional pressure — the fear of being priced out, the excitement of a rising market, the urgency of a "now or never" deal. That is exactly when a pre-committed checklist earns its keep, because it lets your calm, prepared self overrule your anxious, present self. Print the six steps. Run them every time. The discipline is not glamorous and it will not let you brag about calling the top — but it is the difference between riding the cycle and being crushed by it.

If you internalize one thing from the whole series, make it this: **you cannot control the cycle, but you can control your leverage, your margin of safety, and your liquidity — and those three decide whether a normal downturn is a setback or a ruin.** Read the phase, check the macro, underwrite the deal, size the risk, fit your portfolio, and act by phase. Then loop, and run it again at the next turn.
