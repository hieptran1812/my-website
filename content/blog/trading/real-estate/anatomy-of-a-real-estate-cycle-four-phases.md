> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Property markets move through the same four-phase loop over and over — **Recovery → Expansion → Hypersupply → Recession** — and back to Recovery. The engine is the long *lag* between a demand signal and the new supply it triggers: by the time builders finish responding to high prices, demand has often already turned, so the market overshoots in both directions.
>
> - The four phases are defined by two things you can actually measure: where **occupancy** sits relative to its long-run normal, and whether it is rising or falling. That single reading tells you what rents, building, and prices are about to do.
> - Cycles exist because **demand turns on a dime but supply takes 2–4 years to arrive.** A building started in the boom gets delivered into the bust. This *construction lag* is why property overshoots where a frictionless market never would.
> - Knowing the phase changes the rational move: **buy/build in Recovery, hold and lease in Expansion, sell and de-lever in Hypersupply, wait with cash in Recession.** Most people do the exact opposite.
> - The number to remember: from its 2006 peak to its 2012 trough, the entire US housing market fell about **−27%** — and that was one ordinary turn of a cycle that has repeated for centuries.

In early 2007, a buyer in suburban Phoenix could watch the price of an identical house down the street rise almost monthly. Builders were pouring foundations on every spare acre. Everyone "knew" prices only went up, so the rational thing seemed obvious: buy now, before it costs more. Five years later that same house was worth roughly a third less, the builder was bankrupt, and the streets were dotted with foreclosure signs. Nothing about the house had changed. What changed was the *phase of the cycle* — and the buyer had bought at exactly the wrong point in it.

Now move the camera to Ho Chi Minh City. In 2018, land plots — *đất nền* — in the satellite provinces around HCMC were doubling on little more than rumor of a new ring road. By late 2022, after two arrests froze the corporate-bond market that had been funding the whole boom, those same developers could not sell a unit, projects sat half-built, and prices in the secondary market quietly fell 20–30%. Again: same land, same city, opposite outcome. Same cycle, different phase.

These are not two stories. They are one story told twice, because **property markets everywhere run the same four-phase loop.** It has repeated through Roman land speculation, the Florida boom of the 1920s, Japan in 1990, the US in 2008, China's Evergrande, and Vietnam's 2022 bond freeze. The cast changes; the choreography does not. The figure below is the whole dance in one picture — four phases, each with its own unmistakable signature in vacancy, rents, and building activity — and the rest of this post is a slow walk around that loop, building every term from zero and grounding every claim in a worked number.

![A four-phase loop showing recovery, expansion, hypersupply, and recession, each labeled with its signature in vacancy, rent growth, and building activity](/imgs/blogs/anatomy-of-a-real-estate-cycle-four-phases-1.png)

This is the mental model. By the end, you should be able to look at any property market — your own city, a market in the news, the office tower down the road — and place it on this wheel from a handful of public numbers. That single skill is worth more than any forecast, because it tells you not *where prices will be* but *what the market is about to do next*, which is the only thing an investor or a homebuyer can actually act on.

> This post is educational, not financial advice. It explains how the cycle works and what it has historically rewarded; it does not tell you to buy or sell anything.

## Foundations: why real-estate cycles exist at all

Before we can name the four phases, we have to answer a deeper question: *why is there a cycle in the first place?* Most things you buy don't have a multi-year boom-bust rhythm baked into them. Bread doesn't. Haircuts don't. Why does property? The answer is a single, stubborn fact — **supply takes years to respond to demand** — and to see why that creates a cycle, we need a handful of plain-English building blocks first. Take your time here; everything later is just these terms in motion.

### The vocabulary of a property market

**Demand** is simply *how much space people want and can pay for* right now. For housing, it comes from population growth, migration into a city, rising incomes, household formation (young people moving out of their parents' home), and — crucially — *credit*, because almost nobody buys property with cash. When mortgage rates fall or banks lend more freely, demand jumps even if the population hasn't changed, because the same income now "buys" a bigger loan. Demand can shift in *weeks*: a rate cut, a job boom, a wave of migration.

**Supply** is *how much space exists to rent or buy*. Here is the asymmetry that drives everything: supply cannot shift in weeks. To add a new apartment building you must find land, buy it, get it zoned, draw plans, secure permits, arrange financing, then physically build — pouring concrete, raising steel, fitting out. In most markets that is **two to four years** from decision to a tenant moving in (longer for a master-planned township; the đất nền land cycle is faster but still measured in years to titling and infrastructure). This delay has a name: the **construction lag**, and it is the single most important concept in this entire post. Supply responds to demand with a multi-year delay.

**Vacancy rate** is the share of available space that sits empty — unrented apartments, unsold units, vacant offices. If a city has 100,000 apartments and 5,000 are empty, the vacancy rate is 5%. Vacancy is the market's *thermometer*. Low vacancy means landlords have the power (every unit rents instantly, so they can raise rents); high vacancy means tenants have the power (landlords compete with empty-unit incentives to fill space). **Occupancy** is just the flip side: 100% minus vacancy. 5% vacancy = 95% occupancy.

**Equilibrium occupancy** (or *equilibrium vacancy*) is the long-run *normal* level for a given market — the level at which supply and demand are roughly balanced and rents rise gently with inflation. It is never zero vacancy; a healthy market always has some empty units because people are moving, leases are turning over, and new buildings need time to fill. For US apartments, equilibrium vacancy is often cited around **5%**; for offices it might be 10%; in fast-growing HCMC, primary units sell quickly but absorb over quarters. The exact number is market-specific. What matters is the *concept*: there is a normal level, and the whole cycle is the market swinging **above and below that line**.

**Absorption** is the *rate at which empty space gets filled* — how many units the market rents or sells per month or quarter. If a city absorbs 2,000 new apartments a quarter and a developer delivers 8,000 at once, it will take roughly four quarters (a year) to absorb that wave, *assuming demand holds*. Absorption is how you translate "there's a glut" into a number of months.

**Rent growth** is the percentage change in rents over a year. It is the cleanest signal of which side has the power. Rents rising 8% a year says landlords are winning (low vacancy); rents *falling*, or landlords offering "two months free," says tenants are winning (high vacancy). Sale **prices** for income property ultimately follow rents — a property is worth the income it throws off, a relationship we unpack in [cap rate, NOI, and the income approach](/blog/trading/real-estate/cap-rate-noi-and-the-income-approach) — so rent growth leads price growth.

**New supply / completions** is the volume of new space *delivered* in a period — buildings finished and handed over. Because of the construction lag, completions reflect decisions made *years earlier*. The cranes you see today were financed when the market looked hot; they will deliver units into a market that may have completely changed.

### The engine: a signal, a lag, and an overshoot

Now assemble these into a machine. Picture a market sitting peacefully at equilibrium: occupancy at its normal level, rents rising with inflation, no boom, no bust. Then a shock lifts demand — a wave of new jobs, a migration surge, a sharp drop in mortgage rates. What happens?

First, **vacancy falls.** More people want space; the existing stock fills up. Supply can't respond yet — the lag — so the only release valve is price. **Rents and prices rise.** Now landlords are making more, so developers look at those high prices and think: *building is hugely profitable right now.* They start projects. But those projects take 2–4 years. Meanwhile prices keep rising (still no new supply), which makes building look *even more* profitable, so *more* developers start *more* projects. Everyone is reacting to the same hot signal at the same time.

Then, years later, all of those projects finish — roughly together, because they all started when the signal was hot. A flood of new supply hits the market at once. And here is the cruel twist: **by the time that supply arrives, demand has often already turned.** Rates rose again, the job boom cooled, the cycle aged. So the market now has *too much* supply meeting *less* demand. Vacancy spikes, rents fall, prices drop. The market **overshoots** past equilibrium into a glut — exactly the mirror image of the boom.

That is the cycle in one paragraph: **demand turns fast, supply turns slow, so the market always overshoots the level a frictionless market would find.** The figure below traces the causal chain, with the lag sitting right in the middle where it does its damage.

![A flow diagram showing demand rising, then prices rising, then builders reacting, then a two-to-four-year construction lag, then supply arriving after demand has already turned, producing a glut](/imgs/blogs/anatomy-of-a-real-estate-cycle-four-phases-2.png)

If supply could appear instantly, this would not happen. The moment prices rose, builders would add exactly enough space to meet demand, prices would settle, and there would be no boom and no bust — just a smooth adjustment. It is *precisely* the construction lag that turns a demand wobble into a multi-year cycle. The longer the lag (offices and large townships have the longest), the bigger the overshoot. This is why this series argues, in [why real-estate cycles are so long](/blog/trading/real-estate/why-real-estate-cycles-are-long-the-18-year-cycle), that the property cycle is one of the most reliable rhythms in all of finance.

There are three further forces that make property cycles wider than almost any other market's, and they're worth naming because they amplify the basic lag:

- **Property is illiquid and slow to price.** A stock reprices every second; a building reprices only when one nearby actually sells, which might be once a year. So information travels slowly, and prices *lag reality* — they keep drifting up after demand has peaked (because the last few euphoric sales set the comparables) and keep drifting down after the bottom (because the last few forced sales set them). This stickiness stretches each phase.
- **Property is almost always bought with leverage.** Most buyers put down 20–30% and borrow the rest, so a 20% price move is a 100% move on their equity. Leverage turns a mild downturn into forced selling (owners who can't service debt must sell, which pushes prices down further, which forces more selling) and a mild upturn into a frenzy (rising equity lets owners borrow against it to buy more). Leverage is an *accelerant* on both sides of the cycle — the full mechanics are in [how debt amplifies property](/blog/trading/real-estate/leverage-and-the-mortgage-how-debt-amplifies-property).
- **Everyone watches the same recent prices.** Property is the most "what did the neighbor's place sell for?" market on Earth. Buyers extrapolate the recent trend, so rising prices *create* the expectation of more rising prices (and the reverse). This reflexivity means the crowd buys *most* aggressively at the top and sells *most* fearfully at the bottom — exactly out of step with the phase.

Stack these on the construction lag and you get the property cycle's signature: long, wide swings — measured in years, not months — that overshoot equilibrium by a lot, in both directions.

### Residential versus commercial: same loop, different speed

The four phases apply to *every* kind of property, but they don't run at the same speed, because the lag and the lease structure differ. It helps to hold two clocks in your head.

**Residential** (homes, apartments) has a shorter lag and no long leases — tenants turn over yearly, owners can move quickly — so the residential cycle is faster and more demand-driven (rates and incomes move it). It's also the one most distorted by emotion and credit, because it's where ordinary households participate.

**Commercial** (offices, retail, industrial, hotels) has a *longer* lag (bigger projects) and *long leases* (5–10 years), which makes its cycle slower and stickier. A commercial Recession can grind on for years because existing leases keep tenants in place even as the market sours, and new supply takes longer to both arrive and clear. The post-COVID office Recession, which we'll meet later, is the extreme case — a structural demand shock meeting the longest lag and longest leases in the business, producing the slowest-clearing glut of all.

The takeaway: when you read a phase, read it for the *specific* asset class. HCMC apartments and HCMC offices can sit in completely different phases at the same time.

#### Worked example: a developer who builds into the lag

Let's make the lag concrete. Meet **Minh**, a HCMC developer (not our usual apartment-buyer Minh — same name, different hat, to keep the lag vivid). In early 2019, the HCMC market is hot: primary apartments are selling within weeks of launch, prices are rising ~10% a year, and a competitor just sold out a tower in a month. Minh looks at the math: build cost ₫30 million/m² (≈ \$1,150/m²), current selling price ₫55 million/m² (≈ \$2,120/m²). That's a gross margin of ₫25 million/m² — about 45%. *Obviously* he should build.

He buys land in 2019, spends 2020 on permits and design, breaks ground in 2021, and tops out in late 2022 — a **3.5-year** journey from decision to delivery. Now it is the start of 2023. In the meantime: COVID froze demand for two of those years, then the 2022 bond-market freeze cut off the credit that buyers used to finance purchases. The market Minh built for has vanished. He is now trying to sell into a market where *secondary* prices have fallen ~20% and buyers can't get loans. His ₫55 million/m² selling assumption is now ₫44 million/m². His 45% margin is closer to 5% — if he can sell at all. Several of his competitors, who ran the same math at the same time, are also delivering *their* towers into the same frozen market. The supply they all started in the boom is landing in the bust, together.

Minh did nothing irrational at any single step. The arithmetic in 2019 was sound. The trap was the *lag*: he was forced to bet, in 2019, on what the market would look like in 2023 — and the cycle had turned underneath him.

*The construction lag means a developer is always building for a market that no longer exists by the time the building is done; the boom signal that triggers building is precisely what guarantees the glut.*

### From the engine to four named phases

The overshoot mechanism gives us the cycle. The four *phases* are simply the four quarters of that swing, defined by two questions:

1. **Is occupancy above or below its long-run equilibrium?** (Above = landlords have power; below = tenants do.)
2. **Is occupancy rising or falling?** (The direction tells you what's coming next.)

Two yes/no questions make four combinations, and those are the four phases. This framing is the heart of **Glenn Mueller's market-cycle model**, the cleanest formalization of the property cycle, which divides the swing into Recovery, Expansion, Hypersupply, and Recession by exactly these two axes. Let's walk each one.

| Occupancy vs equilibrium | Direction | Phase |
|---|---|---|
| Below normal | Rising | **Recovery** |
| Above normal | Rising | **Expansion** |
| Above normal | Falling | **Hypersupply** |
| Below normal | Falling | **Recession** |

Notice the order isn't random — it's a clockwise rotation. You can only get from "below and rising" to "above and rising" by passing through the equilibrium line going up (that's the Recovery→Expansion handoff). You can only get from "above and falling" to "below and falling" by crossing equilibrium going down (Hypersupply→Recession). The phases *must* occur in this sequence. A market can't skip from Recovery straight to Hypersupply any more than a clock can jump from 3 to 9. That's what makes the model useful: if you know which phase you're in, you know which one comes next.

## Phase 1 — Recovery: cheap, scary, and ignored

Recovery begins at the bottom — the moment of maximum pessimism. The previous bust has run its course. Vacancy is **high** (occupancy below equilibrium), so on paper the market still looks terrible. The newspapers are full of foreclosures and stalled projects. Nobody wants to touch property. But underneath, something has quietly changed: **vacancy has stopped rising and started to fall.** Demand, however weak, is now growing faster than supply — because in the recession, builders stopped building entirely, so no new units are arriving while the population keeps slowly growing and absorbing the glut.

The signatures of Recovery:

- **Vacancy: high, but falling.** Still above normal, so still a "tenant's market" on the surface — but the direction has flipped. This is the single most important and most missed signal in the whole cycle.
- **Rent growth: roughly zero, just stops falling.** Landlords can't raise rents yet (too much empty space), but the bleeding stops. Concessions ("two months free") start to disappear.
- **New construction: almost none.** The bust scared off and bankrupted the builders; banks won't finance new projects against falling prices. This is *why* Recovery happens — supply is frozen while demand thaws.
- **Prices: at or near the bottom, flat to slightly up.** Cheap relative to replacement cost (you could not build the building for what it sells for). Distressed and forced sales still set the tone.

The psychology of Recovery is *fear*. The market is cheap precisely because the last crash is fresh and everyone assumes it could keep falling. This is the phase where the best risk-adjusted opportunities sit — and where almost nobody acts, because acting means buying the thing everyone just watched collapse.

#### Worked example: buying in Recovery vs at the peak

Let's put numbers on why the phase matters more than the property. Consider our recurring US investor, **Dana**, looking at the same apartment building at two different points in the cycle. The building throws off \$100,000 a year in net operating income (rent minus operating costs — its "coupon").

**Scenario A — Dana buys at the peak of Expansion (cycle top).** Optimism is high, so buyers accept a low yield. The market cap rate (the yield buyers demand; price = income ÷ cap rate) is **5%**. Dana pays \$100,000 ÷ 0.05 = **\$2,000,000**. Then the cycle turns. In the bust, rents fall and risk-appetite collapses, so the cap rate rises to 7% *and* income drops to \$90,000. The building is now worth \$90,000 ÷ 0.07 ≈ **\$1,286,000.** Dana is down roughly **−36%** on the asset, before leverage — and if she borrowed 70%, her equity is wiped out.

**Scenario B — Dana buys the same building in Recovery (near the bottom).** Fear is high, so buyers demand a high yield: cap rate **8%**, and income is depressed at \$90,000. She pays \$90,000 ÷ 0.08 = **\$1,125,000.** Then the cycle recovers: income climbs back to \$100,000 and the cap rate compresses to 6% as confidence returns. The building is now worth \$100,000 ÷ 0.06 ≈ **\$1,667,000** — a gain of about **+48%**, again before leverage, which would *amplify* it.

Same building. The only difference is *which phase Dana bought in*. The peak buyer lost a third; the trough buyer made nearly half. The asset never changed — the phase did everything. (For exactly how that leverage amplification works, see [how debt amplifies property](/blog/trading/real-estate/leverage-and-the-mortgage-how-debt-amplifies-property).)

*In real estate, when you buy matters more than what you buy; the phase sets the entry yield, and the entry yield sets your return.*

## Phase 2 — Expansion: the good years (and the trap)

Recovery hands off to Expansion at the moment occupancy crosses *above* its equilibrium line, going up. Now the market is genuinely tight: vacancy is **below** normal and still falling. There aren't enough units. This is the landlord's golden age.

The signatures of Expansion:

- **Vacancy: below normal and falling.** Every unit rents instantly. Landlords have the whip hand.
- **Rent growth: fast and accelerating.** With no slack, landlords push rents hard — high single digits or double digits. This is where the eye-popping "rents up 15% this year" headlines come from.
- **New construction: ramping up hard.** This is the key. The high rents and prices finally pull builders off the sidelines. Cranes appear across the skyline. Banks, seeing rising values, lend eagerly. *But every project started now will deliver into the future — into the lag.*
- **Prices: rising strongly, with momentum.** Buyers compete; bidding wars appear; "buy now before it costs more" becomes the city's mantra.

Expansion is the phase everyone *loves* and the phase that *creates the next bust*. Because the construction that ramps up here doesn't arrive for years, the early and middle of Expansion can feel like a permanent boom — demand keeps outrunning supply, so prices keep climbing. The danger lives in the *late* Expansion, when so much building has been started that the future glut is already locked in. The buildings aren't finished yet, so the market still looks fine. But the seeds of Hypersupply are already in the ground.

This is why "buy at the top of Expansion" is one of the most expensive mistakes in property (we'll return to it under misconceptions). The market feels safest exactly when it is most dangerous, because the danger — all that started construction — is invisible until it delivers.

#### Worked example: vacancy → rent math across the Expansion

Let's see how tight vacancy translates into the rent growth that defines Expansion, with real arithmetic. **Minh** (back in his apartment-buyer hat now) owns a small block of 20 rental units in HCMC. We'll track one full swing.

**At equilibrium (start):** vacancy is the healthy 5% — so 1 unit empty, 19 rented. Average rent is ₫12 million/month (≈ \$465). His annual rental income is 19 units × ₫12m × 12 months = **₫2,736 million** (≈ \$106,000). Landlords have no special power; he raises rent ~4% a year with inflation.

**Into Expansion:** a job boom pulls workers into the district. Demand surges; with no new buildings yet (the lag), vacancy drops to **2%** — effectively zero empty units; there's a waiting list. Now Minh has pricing power. As leases roll over, he raises rents **12%** to ₫13.44 million. With ~20 units full, his income is 20 × ₫13.44m × 12 = **₫3,226 million** (≈ \$125,000) — up about 18% in a year, from filling the vacant unit *and* the rent hike.

That ₫490 million jump in annual income is the Expansion phase paying out. And here's the second-order effect: at a 6% cap rate, that extra ₫490m of income adds ₫490m ÷ 0.06 ≈ **₫8.2 billion** (≈ \$316,000) to the *value* of his block. The tight vacancy didn't just raise his rent; it re-priced the whole asset upward. This is also exactly the signal that makes Minh's developer-self want to build — and starts the clock on the next glut.

*Low vacancy is the engine of Expansion: it hands landlords pricing power, the rent growth re-prices the asset upward, and that upward re-pricing is precisely what lures the supply that will eventually end the boom.*

## Phase 3 — Hypersupply: the glut arrives

Hypersupply (often just called "oversupply") is the phase most people don't have a name for, and it's the most important one to recognize, because it's where the smart money sells and the crowd keeps buying. The handoff from Expansion happens at the *peak* of occupancy — the exact top of the cycle. After that, occupancy is still **above** normal (so the market still *looks* healthy) but it has started **falling.** That's the tell.

What turns the corner? **All that construction started in Expansion now delivers.** Remember the lag: the cranes you saw two or three years ago are now finished buildings handing over units. Supply finally catches up to — and then exceeds — demand. The flood arrives all at once because everyone started building off the same hot signal.

The signatures of Hypersupply:

- **Vacancy: still below normal, but now rising.** The waiting lists shorten. The "for rent" signs reappear. Occupancy is past its peak.
- **Rent growth: slowing and stalling.** Landlords lose pricing power as new buildings compete for tenants. Rent growth drops from double digits to low single digits to flat.
- **New construction: at its absolute peak in *deliveries*, but new *starts* collapse.** This is a subtle, vital distinction. Completions peak here (everything from the boom finishes), but smart developers *stop starting* new projects — the savvy ones can already see the glut forming. Watching new permits and starts roll over while completions are still high is one of the cleanest "you are in Hypersupply" signals.
- **Prices: plateauing, topping out.** Momentum stalls. The "prices only go up" crowd is still buying, but the marginal buyer is getting scarce.

Hypersupply *feels* like Expansion to most people — occupancy is still high, prices are still elevated, the news is still positive. But the *direction* has reversed, and direction is destiny. This is the phase to sell, lighten leverage, and absolutely not start new projects. The figure below shows the difference between a market sitting at equilibrium and the same market once the lagged supply lands all at once.

![A before-and-after comparison showing a balanced market at equilibrium occupancy with gentle rent growth, versus an oversupplied market where occupancy has crashed below normal and rents are falling](/imgs/blogs/anatomy-of-a-real-estate-cycle-four-phases-5.png)

#### Worked example: absorption math — how many months to clear the glut

The cleanest way to quantify Hypersupply is **absorption**: how long the new supply will take to clear. Let's do it for a stylized HCMC submarket.

Suppose the submarket normally **absorbs** (sells/leases) **2,000 units per year** when demand is healthy — call it ~500 a quarter. During the boom, developers started enough projects that **12,000 new units** are now delivering over the next 18 months. Question: how long to clear that wave?

At healthy absorption of 2,000/year, 12,000 units take 12,000 ÷ 2,000 = **6 years** to clear. Six years of overhang. But it's worse, because Hypersupply *also* coincides with demand cooling. Say demand drops and absorption falls to **1,200/year.** Now the math is 12,000 ÷ 1,200 = **10 years** of inventory. Ten years is not a dip; it's a structural glut that will crush rents and prices until the overhang clears. (This is roughly the dynamic China's tier-2 and tier-3 cities faced post-Evergrande: years of inventory and no buyers.)

Flip it to see the policy lever: if the state stimulates demand — rate cuts, easier mortgages — and absorption recovers to 2,500/year, the 12,000 units clear in under 5 years, and prices stabilize sooner. This is precisely why Vietnam's SBV rate cuts in 2023 mattered: they were an attempt to lift absorption and shorten the overhang. Absorption is the dial that decides whether Hypersupply is a brief pause or a lost decade.

*Hypersupply is just arithmetic: divide the wave of new supply by the rate the market can absorb it, and you get the number of years the glut will weigh on prices — and the demand side, not the supply side, is what makes that number swing from painful to catastrophic.*

## Phase 4 — Recession: the bust

Recession begins when occupancy crosses *below* its equilibrium line, falling. Now the market is unambiguously bad and *looks* it: too much empty space, and getting worse. The glut from Hypersupply has fully arrived and demand has weakened further. This is the foreclosure-sign phase, the stalled-project phase, the frozen-credit phase.

The signatures of Recession:

- **Vacancy: high and rising.** Occupancy below normal and still falling. Landlords are desperate to fill space.
- **Rent growth: negative.** Rents fall outright; landlords offer aggressive incentives — free months, fit-out allowances, dropped deposits — which are really hidden rent cuts.
- **New construction: stops cold.** No one builds into a glut, and banks won't finance it anyway. *This freeze is what eventually creates the next Recovery* — supply stops while the population keeps slowly growing, so the overhang slowly clears.
- **Prices: falling, set by forced and distressed sales.** Owners who over-borrowed in the boom can't service their debt as rents fall; some are forced to sell at any price, which drags the whole market's comparables down. This is where the −27% national US drops and the 20–30% HCMC secondary drops happen.

The psychology is *fear and capitulation* — the mirror of Expansion's greed. And here is the cruel symmetry of the cycle: the Recession that everyone hates is *building the next Recovery*. Because construction stops cold, supply freezes; meanwhile demand, however slowly, keeps growing and absorbing the glut. At some point — the exact bottom — vacancy stops rising and starts to fall, and you are back in Recovery, and the whole loop begins again with a new cast of characters who have forgotten the last one.

#### Worked example: the over-leveraged owner in the Recession

Recession is where leverage turns from friend to executioner. Let's watch it happen to **Dana** with a single building she bought late, near the cycle top.

She bought a \$2,000,000 building at the peak with **75% leverage**: \$500,000 of her own equity, a \$1,500,000 mortgage. The building's net operating income was \$100,000, and her annual mortgage payment is \$95,000 — so she had \$5,000 a year of cushion. Tight, but it "worked" at the top.

Now the Recession hits. Two things move against her at once. First, **income falls**: with rising vacancy and falling rents, her NOI drops from \$100,000 to \$78,000. Her mortgage payment is still \$95,000 — so she's now **bleeding \$17,000 a year** out of pocket just to hold the building. Second, **value falls**: at a recession cap rate of 7.5%, the building is worth \$78,000 ÷ 0.075 = **\$1,040,000** — below her \$1,500,000 loan. She is *underwater*: she owes more than the building is worth.

She has three bad options: feed it \$17,000/year and wait for Recovery (if she has the cash and nerve), try to sell at a \$1m loss that wipes out her \$500k equity and then some, or default and hand the keys to the bank. Multiply Dana across a whole market and you get the foreclosure wave that *is* the Recession — and the distressed inventory that the Recovery buyers will eventually pick up cheap.

*Leverage doesn't change the cycle; it changes who survives it. The Recession transfers assets from owners who borrowed against the boom to buyers holding cash at the bottom — which is the entire point of knowing the phase.*

## How to read which phase you're in

Forecasting *prices* is nearly impossible. Reading the *phase* is surprisingly tractable, because the four phases each print a distinct, public signature. You don't need a model — you need three or four numbers and the discipline to read their *direction*, not just their level. Here's the practical checklist.

**1. Vacancy / occupancy — level AND direction.** This is the master signal. Get the vacancy rate for your market (published by CBRE, Savills, JLL for commercial; harder for residential, but absorption and unsold-inventory data substitute). Then ask the two questions: Is it above or below the long-run normal? Is it rising or falling? Those two answers *are* the phase. The most common error is reading only the level — "vacancy is low, so we're fine" — and missing that it just turned up (that's Hypersupply, the sell signal, not Expansion).

**2. Rent growth and concessions.** Accelerating rents with no incentives = Expansion. Stalling rents and the *reappearance* of "free months" = Hypersupply. Falling rents and aggressive incentives = Recession. Flat rents and *disappearing* incentives = Recovery. Concessions are a leading indicator — landlords cut them quietly before they cut headline rents.

**3. Construction: starts vs completions.** Pull permit and housing-start data (public in most countries). The tell is the *gap* between starts and completions. Starts collapsing while completions are still high = you're in Hypersupply, with the glut about to land. Starts and completions both near zero = Recession or early Recovery. Starts ramping = Expansion.

**4. Credit conditions.** Are banks eager or frightened to lend on property? Easy credit fuels Expansion; a credit freeze (like Vietnam's 2022 bond crunch) can *force* Hypersupply into Recession overnight by removing the buyers' financing. The credit cycle and the property cycle are joined at the hip — so tightly that we give it its own treatment in [the credit cycle and property prices](/blog/trading/real-estate/the-credit-cycle-and-property-prices). When credit turns, the property cycle often turns with it, faster than supply alone would dictate.

**5. Sentiment and the news.** Useful as a *contrarian* gauge. Euphoria ("prices only go up," everyone's a developer, taxi-driver property tips) clusters at the top of Expansion / start of Hypersupply. Despair ("property is dead," foreclosures everywhere) clusters at the Recession bottom / start of Recovery. When the crowd is *most* sure, the cycle is usually *most* turned against them.

The investor playbook grid below maps each phase to its rational move — and the rotation of that move around the wheel.

![A two-by-two grid mapping each of the four phases to the rational investor move: buy or build in recovery, hold or sell early in expansion, sell or lighten in hypersupply, and wait or hunt in recession](/imgs/blogs/anatomy-of-a-real-estate-cycle-four-phases-6.png)

#### Worked example: reading HCMC's current phase from the numbers

Let's actually run the checklist on a real market, using the cited 2024–2025 figures (as-of mid-2026; treat as illustrative, not a forecast). HCMC primary apartment prices rose from ~91 million VND/m² in 2024 to ~111 million VND/m² in 2025 (≈ \$4,222/m²) — strong, ~+22% price growth. Hanoi went even harder, ~+40%. So **prices: rising fast.** That alone looks like Expansion.

But layer in the rest. Construction *starts* through 2022–2023 were strangled by the bond freeze, so the pipeline of *completions* is thin — new launches have been scarce, which is why the limited supply that does launch sells out and prices spike. **Construction: starts were frozen, completions thin.** Credit: SBV cut rates four times in 2023 and lending has eased, so **credit: thawing.** Absorption of new launches is brisk because there's so little of it. Put together — prices rising, supply still constrained from the freeze, credit thawing, sentiment recovering from the 2022–23 trough — the signature reads like **Recovery handing into early Expansion**: the market climbed out of the 2022–23 Recession, and the *scarcity* of supply (a hangover from the freeze) is doing a lot of the price work.

The phase-aware reading, though, carries a warning the price chart hides: those rising prices are now luring developers back. Land is being assembled, projects relaunched. Run the lag forward — projects starting in 2025–2026 deliver in 2028–2030 — and you can see the *next* Hypersupply being seeded right now, even as today's market feels tight. That is the value of reading the phase: it tells you not just where you are, but what your own position is busy creating.

*Rising prices alone can't tell you the phase — a Recovery rebound off a supply freeze and a late-Expansion blow-off both show green candles; only the vacancy direction, the starts-versus-completions gap, and the credit backdrop separate them.*

## What an investor does in each phase

The whole point of placing yourself on the wheel is that each phase has a *different* rational action. The crowd, driven by recent price action, tends to do the opposite — buying hardest at the top and selling hardest at the bottom. Here's the phase-by-phase playbook, with the *why* attached.

**Recovery → Buy and build.** This is the best risk-adjusted entry: assets are cheap (high entry yields, like Dana's 8% cap rate), there's little competition (everyone's scared), and you're buying *before* the rent growth of Expansion re-prices everything upward. For developers, it's the ideal time to *start* projects, because the 2–4 year lag means you'll deliver into the heart of the next Expansion, when demand is strong and supply is still thin. The catch: it requires buying the asset everyone just watched collapse, with capital you protected through the bust. Cheap is the reward for being early and brave.

**Expansion → Hold, lease, and sell early.** Ride the rent growth on what you already own; finish and lease up the projects you started in Recovery. But be *increasingly* careful about new buys as Expansion ages — late-Expansion entry prices bake in the boom and leave no margin for the turn. The discipline here is to *resist the momentum*: every signal says "buy more," but you're paying peak prices for assets whose future glut is already being built. Smart capital starts trimming and de-risking in late Expansion, not waiting for the obvious top.

**Hypersupply → Sell and de-lever.** This is the contrarian's sell signal, and the hardest to act on because the market still *looks* great (occupancy high, prices elevated, news positive). But occupancy has turned down, the glut is landing, and rent growth is stalling. The move: sell into strength, trim leverage, build cash, and absolutely do *not* start new projects (anything you start now delivers into the bottom). You're selling to the "prices only go up" crowd who can't see the direction change.

**Recession → Wait, stay liquid, and hunt.** Hold cash and stay patient — there's no rush, because Recession can last years and the bottom is a process, not a day. But this is also when you build your buy list and stalk distressed assets: the forced sellers (over-leveraged owners like late-cycle Dana, stranded developers like 2019-Minh) are handing over real estate at fire-sale prices. The cash you preserved in Hypersupply is the dry powder that becomes Recovery's bargains. The cycle is, at heart, a machine for transferring assets from the over-leveraged to the patient.

Notice the rotation: **Buy → Hold → Sell → Wait → Buy.** It's a clock. And it's almost exactly out of phase with what *feels* right, because feelings track recent prices, and recent prices are highest right before they fall.

#### Worked example: the same investor through a full loop

Let's compound everything into one number by walking **Dana** through a complete cycle with the discipline the playbook prescribes, and contrasting her with a crowd-following twin, **Drew**, who does what feels right.

Both start with \$500,000 of cash at the Recession bottom.

**Recovery:** Dana buys a building yielding 8% — \$80,000 income on a \$1,000,000 price, financed with her \$500k plus a \$500k loan. She's terrified but acts. **Drew** sits in cash; property "is dead," and he's waiting for "confirmation" that it's safe.

**Expansion:** the cycle turns. Dana's rents climb; her income rises from \$80,000 to \$110,000, and as confidence returns the cap rate compresses from 8% to 5.5%. Her building is now worth \$110,000 ÷ 0.055 = **\$2,000,000.** Her equity (value minus the \$500k loan) has gone from \$500k to \$1.5m — she's *tripled* her money. **Drew**, now seeing two years of rising prices, finally gets "confirmation" and buys near the top: he pays \$2,000,000 for a similar building at the 5.5% cap rate, with his \$500k down and a \$1.5m loan.

**Hypersupply → Recession:** the glut lands. Income falls, cap rates rise to 7.5%. Both buildings drop to roughly \$95,000 ÷ 0.075 ≈ **\$1,267,000.** Dana sold most of her position in Hypersupply (the playbook's sell signal), banking her gains into cash, so the drop barely touches her. **Drew**, who bought at \$2m with a \$1.5m loan, now owns a \$1.27m building — **underwater by ~\$230k**, bleeding cash on the mortgage, facing the exact choice late-cycle Dana faced earlier. When the next Recovery comes, Dana is the one with cash, buying Drew's building out of distress.

Same cycle, same starting capital, opposite outcomes — driven entirely by *which phase each one acted in*. Dana's edge wasn't a better building or a forecast; it was reading the phase and acting against the crowd's feelings.

*Over a full loop, the investor who buys fear and sells euphoria can triple capital while the one who buys "confirmation" and holds through the bust goes underwater — and they trade the very same buildings.*

## Common misconceptions

The cycle is simple to state and brutally hard to act on, because every phase generates a *story* that justifies doing the wrong thing. Here are the most expensive of those stories, named and corrected.

**"This time is different."** The eternal top-of-cycle refrain. There's always a fresh narrative for why the old rules don't apply — a new technology, a structural shortage, a permanent demand shift, foreign buyers, "they're not making more land." Sometimes there *is* a real structural change. But the construction lag is a law of physics, not a narrative, and it hasn't been repealed. Whenever you hear "this time is different" used to justify paying peak prices, you are almost certainly listening to late Expansion or Hypersupply talking. The phrase is, historically, one of the most reliable *sell* signals in finance.

**"Property prices only ever go up."** They don't. US national home prices fell **−27%** from 2006 to 2012. Japanese urban land fell ~70% from its 1990 peak and still hasn't recovered three decades later. HCMC secondary prices fell 20–30% in 2022–23. Irish, Spanish, and US prices all crashed in 2008. The "only goes up" belief comes from looking at long enough horizons (over 30 years, well-located property in a growing economy usually does rise) and forgetting that you can spend a *decade* underwater if you buy at the wrong phase — and that leverage can wipe you out long before the eventual recovery arrives. Prices go up *over the cycle's average*; within a cycle, they absolutely fall.

**"Buy at the top of Expansion — it's still going up."** This is the most dangerous misconception because it's *partly true*: in late Expansion, prices genuinely are still rising, so buying "works" right up until it doesn't. The problem is that late Expansion is exactly when the future glut is already under construction, invisible. You're buying at the highest entry price, the lowest entry yield, with the smallest margin of safety, right before the supply you can't yet see arrives. As Dana's worked example showed, the peak buyer lost a third while the trough buyer made nearly half — same building. "It's still going up" is true and irrelevant; what matters is the *direction it's about to go*.

**"Low vacancy means it's safe to buy."** Level without direction is the classic trap. Low *and falling* vacancy is Expansion — fine. Low *and rising* vacancy is Hypersupply — the sell signal. The number looks identical at a glance ("vacancy is only 4%!"); the *direction* is the difference between the second-best and the worst time to buy. Always read the derivative, not just the level.

**"High prices mean there's a shortage, so I should build."** This is the developer's version of the trap, and it's the *engine* of the whole cycle. High prices in Expansion genuinely do signal that demand exceeds current supply. But if you act on that signal — and so does every other developer, simultaneously — the collective response overshoots, because of the lag. By the time all your buildings deliver, the shortage is a glut. The signal is real; the *crowded, lagged response* to it is what creates the bust. Building because prices are high is rational individually and catastrophic collectively.

**"The cycle is unpredictable, so phase-reading is pointless."** Forecasting the *timing* of turns is genuinely hard — nobody rings a bell at the top. But that's not the same as the cycle being random. The *sequence* of phases is fixed (Recovery → Expansion → Hypersupply → Recession, always in that order), and the *signature* of each phase is readable from public data. You can't say *when* Expansion will end, but you can usually tell *that* you're in late Expansion versus early Recovery — and that's enough to change what you do. Confusing "I can't time the exact turn" with "I can't read the phase" is how people justify buying at the top.

**"Rising prices mean Expansion."** Price *level* is the worst phase-indicator there is, because prices rise in two completely different phases — early-to-mid Expansion (healthy, demand-driven) and a Recovery rebound off a supply squeeze (prices spike because *nothing* is being built, not because demand is booming). The HCMC worked example above showed both happening at once. Worse, prices keep rising *into* Hypersupply, because the "prices only go up" crowd is still buying even after occupancy has turned down. Prices are a *lagging*, sentiment-driven signal. Vacancy direction, the starts-versus-completions gap, and credit are *leading* ones. When you find yourself reasoning purely from the price chart, you've lost the plot — read the underlying market, not its echo.

**"Government can prevent the cycle."** Policy can *soften* a cycle — rate cuts that lift absorption (Vietnam 2023), demand subsidies, supply incentives — and it can *trigger* a turn (the 2022 bond-market crackdown that snapped Vietnam into Recession). But no government has repealed the construction lag, and most interventions arrive *late* (by the time the data confirms a bust, the policy is months behind) and often *amplify* the next swing (cheap money to fight one bust seeds the next boom). Treat policy as a force that bends the cycle's speed and depth, not one that abolishes its shape.

## How it shows up in real markets

The four-phase model isn't a textbook abstraction; it's the skeleton under every property boom and bust in history. Here are three full cycles — one global benchmark, one Vietnamese, one sector-specific — with the phases labeled.

### The US, 2000–2012: a complete cycle in one chart

The cleanest illustration of a full cycle is the US national housing market, captured by the S&P/Case-Shiller National Home Price Index (set to 100 in January 2000). The chart below traces the entire boom and bust, with the phases shaded.

![A line chart of the Case-Shiller US national home price index from 2000 to 2026, with phase bands shaded, showing the 2006 peak at 184.6, the 2012 trough at 134, and the recovery to 330 by early 2026](/imgs/blogs/anatomy-of-a-real-estate-cycle-four-phases-3.png)

Read it as a cycle. **2000–2004 was Recovery into Expansion**: prices climbed from 100 toward 130 as the post-dot-com rate cuts and loosening mortgage credit (subprime, no-doc loans) pulled in a flood of demand. **2004–2006 was late Expansion into Hypersupply**: prices melted up to the **184.6 peak in July 2006** while builders, seeing those prices, poured foundations across the Sun Belt — Phoenix, Las Vegas, Florida, Inland California. The construction lag was doing its work; all that building was started into the boom. **2006–2012 was the Recession**: the lagged supply delivered into a market where demand had cratered (rates rose, then the financial system seized), and prices fell to a **trough of 134 in February 2012 — about −27% peak to trough.** Foreclosures, underwater owners (millions of real Danas), and bankrupt builders defined those years. Then **2012 onward was a new Recovery and Expansion**, prices grinding back up to ~308 by mid-2022 and ~330 by early 2026. The whole arc — boom, bust, recovery — is one complete turn of the wheel, and it took roughly a full property cycle to play out. (This was the down-leg of the long ~18-year land cycle that peaked in 2006–2008; the [18-year cycle post](/blog/trading/real-estate/why-real-estate-cycles-are-long-the-18-year-cycle) sets the four phases inside that longer rhythm.)

### Vietnam, 2018–2023: the bond-freeze cycle

Vietnam ran the same four phases on a tighter, more dramatic timeline, with a credit shock accelerating the turn. The timeline below traces it.

![A timeline of Vietnam's property cycle from 2018 to 2023, showing the 2018 land fever, the 2019 to 2021 supply build-up and COVID, the 2022 bond freeze tipping hypersupply into recession, and the late-2023 rate cuts attempting recovery](/imgs/blogs/anatomy-of-a-real-estate-cycle-four-phases-7.png)

**2018 was late Expansion bordering on mania**: the đất nền (land-plot) fever in HCMC's satellite provinces saw prices double on rumors of infrastructure, with speculators flipping plots they'd never visited. Developers, funded by an explosion of corporate-bond issuance, started projects everywhere. **2019–2021 was Hypersupply building**: projects piled up, the bond-funded supply pipeline swelled, and then COVID froze demand for two years even as the supply kept coming — classic oversupply, occupancy turning down. **2022 was the snap into Recession**: the arrests of Tân Hoàng Minh executives (April) and the Vạn Thịnh Phát / SCB case (October) froze the corporate-bond market overnight, cutting off the credit that funded both developers and buyers. This is the credit-cycle mechanism in raw form — the financing vanished, so the buyers vanished, so a Hypersupply that might have deflated slowly *collapsed* instead. Projects stranded half-built, secondary prices fell 20–30%, liquidity evaporated. **Late 2023 was the Recovery attempt**: the SBV cut policy rates four times and Decree 08 gave bond issuers breathing room to restructure, lifting absorption and arresting the slide. Same four phases as the US — but where the US bust took a slow financial unwind, Vietnam's was triggered by a sudden credit freeze that compressed Hypersupply and Recession into a single brutal year.

### Offices after COVID: a Hypersupply you can still see

A sector-specific cycle worth watching live is US (and global) office property after 2020. Offices were arguably in late Expansion / early Hypersupply when COVID hit — plenty of new towers had been started in the 2010s boom and were still delivering. Then remote and hybrid work delivered a *demand* shock on top of the supply: companies needed far less space. Office vacancy in major US markets climbed from ~12% pre-COVID toward 18–20% in some cities by 2024 — occupancy well below equilibrium and, in the worst markets, *still falling.* That's a deep Recession for the asset class. The mechanism is pure cycle: supply (towers from the last boom) met a demand collapse (hybrid work), occupancy crashed below equilibrium, rents fell, landlords offered huge concessions, and some owners handed keys back to lenders (the office Danas). What makes offices instructive is the *length*: because office construction has the longest lag and leases are long (5–10 years), the office Recession is unusually slow to clear — there's no quick Recovery while there's still a structural demand deficit. It's a reminder that the four phases are universal, but the *speed* of the loop depends on the asset's lag and lease structure.

The office case also teaches a subtler lesson about *which* signal leads. Because office leases run 5–10 years, the *headline* occupancy held up for a while after 2020 — tenants were still contractually in space they'd stopped using. The real signal was hiding in *sublease availability* and *lease expirations*: companies dumping space onto the sublet market, and a wall of expiries where tenants would clearly shrink or leave. An investor reading only headline occupancy in 2021 would have thought offices were merely in Hypersupply; one reading the sublease and expiry data could see the Recession already locked in. The general principle: in every asset class, find the *leading* version of the occupancy signal — the one that turns before the headline number does. For apartments it's concessions; for offices it's sublease space and expiries; for for-sale homes it's unsold-inventory months and the gap between list and sale price.

There's also a Vietnamese echo worth flagging. HCMC and Hanoi have their own office and retail cycles running *underneath* the residential one, on different clocks. New Grade-A office towers delivered into HCMC through 2023–2024 just as some tenants consolidated, pushing certain submarkets toward Hypersupply even while *residential* was climbing out of its 2022–23 Recession into recovery. Two asset classes, one city, two different phases at the same moment — exactly the warning from the Foundations section to always read the phase for the *specific* asset, never "the market" as a single thing.

### The pattern behind the patterns

Three markets, three eras, three triggers — subprime credit, a bond freeze, a remote-work demand shock — and the *same four phases* in the *same order* every time. The trigger that ends Expansion changes (it's always "this time's" unique story), but the underlying machine doesn't: supply responds to a demand signal with a multi-year lag, the response overshoots, and the market swings above and below equilibrium in a fixed rotation. Japan 1990, the US 2008, China's Evergrande, Vietnam 2022, post-COVID offices — different casts, identical choreography. Once you've seen the skeleton, you can't unsee it, and you'll spot it forming in whatever market is currently telling you "this time is different."

## When this matters to you / further reading

This cycle touches almost everyone's biggest financial decision. If you're **buying a home**, the phase decides whether you're paying a peak price with a thin margin of safety or a Recovery price with room to grow — and whether you might spend years underwater if you buy at the top with a big mortgage. If you're an **investor or developer**, the phase *is* the strategy: the same building bought in Recovery versus at the peak was the difference between +48% and −36% in Dana's example, before leverage amplified both. If you simply want to **understand the news**, the four phases turn "is property in a bubble?" from an unanswerable forecast into a readable question: where is occupancy relative to normal, and which way is it moving?

You won't time the exact turns — nobody does. But you can almost always tell *which phase you're in* from a handful of public numbers: vacancy level and direction, rent growth and concessions, the gap between construction starts and completions, and the state of credit. That single skill — reading the phase rather than forecasting the price — is the most durable edge in real estate, because the loop has repeated for centuries and will keep repeating as long as supply takes years to answer a demand that turns in weeks.
