> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — In the 2010s and early 2020s, housing became unaffordable in dozens of rich-world cities at the *same time* — Sydney, Toronto, London, Auckland, and Hong Kong, right alongside Ho Chi Minh City and Hanoi. That synchronization is the tell: it is too coordinated to be a local accident. It was a shared recipe.
>
> - **One recipe, many kitchens.** A decade of near-zero interest rates after 2008 inflated every asset; supply was throttled by zoning and slow approvals; housing was financialized into the world's favorite leverage-able store of value; and the 2020–21 rate lows were the final accelerant. The same four ingredients showed up in city after city.
> - **The numbers are extreme and global.** Price-to-income — the number of years of a typical income it takes to buy a home — hit roughly **32 in HCMC**, **31 in Hanoi**, **44 in Hong Kong**, and **35 in Beijing**, against a global median near **15** and a healthy market of just **3–5**.
> - **It is a *price* problem layered on a *supply* problem.** Cheap money set the fuse; constrained supply meant the extra demand landed on price instead of on new homes. Cities that built — Tokyo, Houston — stayed near 10–15. Cities that didn't — Sydney, HCMC — ran to 30+.
> - **The one lesson to remember:** of everything tried, only adding supply has durably moved affordability. This case ties together [interest rates](/blog/trading/real-estate/interest-rates-and-mortgages-the-master-variable), [supply elasticity](/blog/trading/real-estate/supply-elasticity-why-some-cities-boom-and-bust), the [asset-vs-shelter tension](/blog/trading/real-estate/housing-as-financial-asset-vs-shelter), and the [price-to-income gauge](/blog/trading/real-estate/income-affordability-and-the-price-to-income-ratio) into a single picture.

Picture a 30-year-old in 2023 trying to buy a first home. In Sydney, she runs the numbers and finds that a median apartment costs around eleven times what a household like hers earns in a year. She has a friend in Toronto in exactly the same bind, another in London, one in Auckland, and a cousin in Hong Kong staring at a ratio closer to *forty-four*. Across the world in Ho Chi Minh City, a young engineer named Minh — who we'll follow through this piece — does the same arithmetic on a modest two-bedroom flat and gets a number around thirty-two years of his salary. None of these people know each other. They live on four continents, under different governments, different banks, different cultures. And yet they all hit the same wall in the same decade.

That is the strange and important fact at the heart of this case study. Housing did not become unaffordable in one unlucky city because of one local mistake. It became unaffordable in *dozens* of the world's richest, most desirable cities at roughly the same time. When the same symptom appears everywhere at once, you should stop looking for a local cause and start looking for a shared one — the way a doctor seeing the same fever in patients across a continent stops blaming each patient's diet and starts looking for the virus they all caught.

The chart above is the picture of the crisis: each bar is the number of years of a typical household's income it takes to buy a home in that city. The figure we'll keep coming back to is the contrast between the bars and the two reference marks — the faint green band at 3–5 years (what a healthy market looks like) and the dashed line at 15 (the rough global median). ## Foundations: what the affordability crisis is, and the gauges that measure it

Before we can explain *why* housing broke, we need to agree on what "broke" means — precisely, with numbers. Three plain definitions do almost all the work.

### The affordability crisis, defined

"Affordability" is not a vibe; it is a ratio between the cost of housing and the income of the people who need it. A market is *affordable* when an ordinary household, working an ordinary job, can buy or rent an ordinary home without devoting an extreme share of its income or waiting a lifetime to save the down payment. The **affordability crisis** is simply the condition where that stops being true across a whole city or country at once — where the median home drifts so far above the median income that ordinary buyers are priced out, not at the margins but as the rule.

The crisis has two faces. One is the *buyer's* face: the home costs too many years of income to ever save up for. The other is the *renter's* face: even renting eats an extreme share of the monthly paycheck. They share a root — homes cost too much relative to incomes — and they're measured by two closely related gauges that we recap here because they are the spine of the whole analysis.

### Price-to-income: years of income to buy

The **price-to-income ratio** is the single most useful number in the whole subject. It is the median home price divided by the median annual household income:

$$\text{price-to-income} = \frac{\text{median home price}}{\text{median annual household income}}$$

Read it as *years of income to buy a home outright*. If a home costs \$400,000 and the typical household earns \$80,000 a year, the ratio is 5 — five years of *every dollar* the household earns, spent on nothing else, to buy the home. (Of course nobody spends every dollar on housing; that's why the gauge is a *relative* yardstick, not a literal savings plan. A ratio of 5 means a household saving a healthy 20% of income would need 25 years to buy with cash — long, but conceivable. A ratio of 30 means 150 years. That's the difference between "hard" and "impossible.")

A *healthy* market sits around **3–5**. That is roughly where post-war America, much of continental Europe, and many mid-size cities still are. The rough **global median is about 15** — already stretched. And the cities in our chart run from New York at **10** and London at **12**, up through Tokyo **15**, Singapore **17**, Seoul **26**, Mumbai **28**, Hanoi **31**, HCMC **32**, Beijing **35**, to Hong Kong at a barely-believable **44**. (These are Numbeo's mid-2026 city ratios; Vietnam's are corroborated by VnExpress, which has cited HCMC at around 32–34 years of income, fifth-highest in the world. As with all live figures, treat them as as-of 2026 and directional rather than to-the-decimal.) We covered this gauge in depth in [income, affordability, and the price-to-income ratio](/blog/trading/real-estate/income-affordability-and-the-price-to-income-ratio); here it's the thermometer.

#### Worked example: years-to-buy across three cities

Let's make the gauge concrete with three of our characters. Take a household earning the local median in each city, and a median home.

- **Tokyo, ratio 15.** Suppose the median household earns the equivalent of \$60,000 a year and the median home is \$900,000. The ratio is \$900,000 / \$60,000 = **15**. A household saving 20% of income (\$12,000/yr) toward the home would need 75 years of pure saving to buy with cash — but with a normal mortgage, a stretched-but-real purchase is possible.
- **HCMC, ratio 32.** Minh and his partner together earn about ₫360 million a year (≈ \$13,900). A modest 70 m² flat at 2025 prices runs about ₫7.0 billion (≈ \$270,000). The ratio is \$270,000 / \$13,900 ≈ **19–20** for a *dual-income* couple, and closer to **32** for a *single* median earner — which is exactly why the headline city ratio (single median earner vs median home) reads 32. Either way, saving 20% of income, Minh would need a *human lifetime* of pure saving to buy with cash.
- **Hong Kong, ratio 44.** A median household earning \$50,000 against a median home of \$2.2 million yields \$2.2m / \$50,000 = **44**. Even with two incomes and aggressive saving, the down payment alone — say 20%, or \$440,000 — is nearly nine years of the entire household income.

*The intuition: price-to-income turns an abstract "expensive" into a hard count of years, and once that count crosses ~25, ordinary buying stops being merely hard and becomes structurally impossible without a windfall, a co-signer, or inherited wealth.*

### Payment-to-income: the monthly squeeze

Price-to-income measures the *purchase*. The other gauge, **payment-to-income**, measures the *monthly burden*: the mortgage payment (or rent) as a share of monthly income. A common rule of thumb is that housing should take no more than ~30% of gross income; "housing-cost-burdened" households spend over 30%, and "severely burdened" over 50%. This is the gauge that makes the role of interest rates vivid, because the *payment* on a fixed loan depends heavily on the rate. We'll lean on it when we get to the low-rate era. (For the full mechanics of how a mortgage payment is built from rate, term, and principal, see [interest rates and mortgages, the master variable](/blog/trading/real-estate/interest-rates-and-mortgages-the-master-variable).)

### Three more terms we'll use constantly

Three concepts are load-bearing for the rest of the piece, so let's define them now, from zero.

The **global low-rate era** is the roughly twelve-year stretch from the 2008 financial crisis to 2021 when the major central banks — the US Federal Reserve, the European Central Bank, the Bank of England, the Bank of Japan — held their **policy rates** (the interest rate they set, which anchors all other rates in the economy) at or near zero. They did it to fight the deflationary aftermath of 2008 and again, harder, during the 2020 pandemic. A *low policy rate* makes borrowing cheap throughout the economy, including mortgages, and it pushes the return on safe assets like government bonds toward nothing.

**Asset-price inflation** is what happens next. When safe assets pay almost nothing, the *price* of any asset that produces an income stream — a bond, a dividend stock, a rental property — gets bid up, because investors will pay more and more for the same income when the alternative (cash, safe bonds) pays zero. This is not consumer-price inflation (the price of groceries); it is *asset*-price inflation (the price of things you own to store wealth). Housing is the largest asset class on Earth — around \$393 trillion globally, roughly four times world GDP — so when asset prices inflate, housing inflates with them, in a big way.

The **financialization of housing** is the gradual shift in how the world treats a home: from primarily a *place to live* (shelter, a consumption good) to primarily a *thing to own for its value* (a financial asset, an investment, a store of wealth you can leverage). When a home is treated as an asset, it competes for global capital with stocks and bonds — and global capital is vast, mobile, and yield-hungry. This is the tension we explored in [housing as a financial asset versus shelter](/blog/trading/real-estate/housing-as-financial-asset-vs-shelter); here it's one of the four ingredients.

With those four pieces — the crisis, the two gauges, the low-rate era, asset inflation, and financialization — we have the vocabulary to explain the synchronized break.

## The synchronized break: why so many cities at once

Here is the fact that should bother you. If housing had become unaffordable in *one* city, you could tell a local story: a tech boom in San Francisco, a flood of mainland buyers in Hong Kong, a planning scandal in Auckland. Local stories are real and they matter. But they cannot explain why the *same* thing happened in *dozens* of unrelated cities inside the *same* decade.

![Matrix of four forces low rates, constrained supply, financialization, and the 2020 to 2021 lows, each row showing what it did, how it pushed prices up, and why it was global](/imgs/blogs/case-study-global-housing-affordability-crisis-2.png)

The matrix above lays out the recipe. Read down the rows: ultra-low rates, constrained supply, financialization, and the 2020–21 lows. Read across the columns: what each force did, how each pushed prices *up*, and — the crucial column — why each was *global* rather than local. Notice that every ingredient in the right-hand column has a reason to show up everywhere at once. The central banks of the rich world cut rates *together*, in coordinated response to the same two global shocks (2008 and 2020). The zoning that throttles supply is a near-universal feature of desirable cities — the political economy of "not in my backyard" is the same in Sydney and San Jose. Financial capital flows across borders looking for yield, so a wall of money hunting for hard assets bids up homes in *every* attractive city at once. And the 2020–21 stimulus was the most synchronized policy event in modern history.

That is the synchronization mechanism: **the inputs were global, so the output was global.** When the fuel (cheap money), the constraint (supply), and the demand (financial capital) all move together across borders, the result — unaffordable housing — appears together too.

There's a useful analogy to a different kind of synchronized event. In a forest, a single tree catching fire is a local accident — a careless camper, a lightning strike. But when whole forests across a continent ignite in the same summer, the cause isn't a continent of careless campers; it's a shared condition — a drought, a heat dome — that made *everything* flammable at once. The 2010s were a drought of cheap money on a tinder-dry landscape of constrained supply. The fires that followed weren't coincidences.

We'll now walk the three structural causes one at a time — low rates, constrained supply, financialization — and then the 2020–21 accelerant that lit the whole thing.

## Cause 1: the global low-rate era and asset inflation

The first and most powerful ingredient is the one that's easiest to underrate because it operates so quietly: a decade of near-zero interest rates.

![Line chart of a rich-world policy-rate proxy near zero through the 2010s, cut to near zero again in 2020 to 2021, then snapping up sharply in 2022 to 2023, with the ultra-low-rate era shaded](/imgs/blogs/case-study-global-housing-affordability-crisis-5.png)

The chart above is the backdrop to everything else. It's an *illustrative* proxy — a stylized blend of the major rich-world policy rates, with levels rounded — but the shape is faithful and it's the shape that matters: after 2008, the rich world's safe interest rate fell to the floor and *stayed* there for over a decade, dipped to the floor again in 2020–21 (when the US 30-year mortgage hit a record **2.65%** in January 2021), and only snapped back up in 2022–23. For twelve years, money was nearly free.

### Why cheap money lifts the price a payment supports

The mechanism that turns cheap money into expensive homes runs through the *payment*. Most people don't buy a home with the price in mind; they buy with the *monthly payment* in mind — "what can I afford each month?" The bank then works backward from that payment to a loan size. And here is the trap: when the interest rate falls, the *same monthly payment supports a much larger loan*, which means buyers can bid more for the same house, which means the price rises to absorb the extra borrowing capacity. Lower rates don't make housing cheaper; they make it *more* expensive, by handing every buyer a bigger checkbook at once.

#### Worked example: how a rate drop from 5% to 2% lifts the price a payment supports

Let's make this precise, because it's the engine of the whole boom. Suppose a buyer — call her Dana, a US first-time buyer — can comfortably afford a mortgage payment of **\$2,000 a month** on a standard 30-year fixed loan. How big a loan does that payment buy?

The mortgage formula relates the monthly payment $P$, the loan principal $L$, the monthly rate $r$ (annual rate ÷ 12), and the number of payments $n$ (here 360):

$$P = L \cdot \frac{r(1+r)^n}{(1+r)^n - 1}$$

We solve for $L$ given $P = \$2{,}000$ and $n = 360$.

- **At 5% (a normal rate):** monthly rate $r = 0.05/12 ≈ 0.004167$. Plugging in, a \$2,000 payment supports a loan of about **\$372,000**.
- **At 2.65% (the 2021 record low):** monthly rate $r = 0.0265/12 ≈ 0.002208$. The *same* \$2,000 payment now supports a loan of about **\$494,000**.

That's a **33% jump in borrowing power** from the rate move alone — no change in Dana's income, no change in her job, no change in the house. If every buyer in the market gets the same 33% bigger checkbook at the same time, and the *number of houses hasn't changed*, the houses don't get cheaper. The bidding gets fiercer and the prices rise — by something close to that 33%, in a tight market — to soak up the new borrowing power. Dana feels like she can "afford more house," but what's actually happened is that the price tag moved to meet her bigger checkbook.

*The intuition: ultra-low rates don't make homes affordable; they inflate the price a fixed payment can chase, so in a supply-constrained market the entire windfall is captured by the seller as a higher price.*

The 33% in Dana's example is, if anything, conservative — it understates how violent the effect gets at the extremes. The relationship between the rate and the loan a payment supports is *non-linear*: each step *down* toward zero buys disproportionately more borrowing power than the step before, because at very low rates almost none of the payment is being eaten by interest, so almost all of it goes to principal. That's why the move from the 2010s' "low" rates to the 2020–21 *record* lows did so much damage so fast. It's worth seeing the most dramatic version of the same calculation, because it is the single most important number in the whole crisis.

#### Worked example: how a fall from 5% to 2% raises the price a fixed payment supports across every city at once

Forget the loan formula's machinery for a moment and look at the headline. A buyer who can pay a fixed amount per month — say **\$2,000** — and takes a 30-year loan supports roughly:

- **\$372,000** at a 5% rate,
- **\$494,000** at 2.65% (the 2021 US record),
- and about **\$542,000** at a flat 2%.

From 5% to 2%, the *same* untouched monthly payment chases roughly **45–50% more house** — no raise, no promotion, no extra savings. Now hold the crucial fact in your head: the central banks of the rich world cut roughly *together*, so this 45–50% bigger checkbook was handed to buyers in Sydney, Toronto, London, Auckland, *and* HCMC in the same window. If a city could not add 45–50% more homes to meet 45–50% more borrowing power — and almost none could — the extra money had nowhere to go but into the price. The math is identical in every currency: a buyer in Hanoi with a fixed ₫30 million monthly budget supported a roughly 45–50% larger loan after the cut, just as a buyer in London with a fixed £1,500 budget did. *That is the synchronization in one calculation:* one global rate move, the same proportional jump in borrowing power, the same fixed housing stock, the same outcome everywhere.

*The intuition: a fall from 5% to 2% raises the price a fixed payment supports by roughly half — and because the world's central banks cut in concert, that same half-again bigger checkbook arrived in a dozen supply-locked cities at once, which is exactly why a dozen cities broke together.*

### The search for yield: why the world's savings hunted for housing

There is a second, quieter channel that ran *alongside* the cheap-mortgage channel, and for understanding the *global* scale of the boom it may matter even more. Call it the **search for yield**. *Yield* is just the income an asset throws off each year as a percentage of its price — the 4% a bond pays, the 3% rent a flat earns net of costs. When central banks pin the safe rate near zero, the yield on the safest assets — government bonds, bank deposits — collapses toward nothing. But the world's pools of savings still have obligations to meet: a pension fund has to pay retirees, an insurer has to cover claims, a sovereign-wealth fund has a target return written into its mandate. When the safe 4% becomes a safe 0.5%, all of that money is forced *out* along the risk spectrum, hunting for anything that still pays a respectable income. This is not greed; it is arithmetic. A pension promising 7% a year cannot meet that promise holding 0.5% bonds, so it *must* reach for higher-yielding assets or break its promise.

Housing — and especially rental housing — sat near the top of the list of places that money fled to. A residential block yielding a net 4% looked spectacular next to a government bond yielding 0.9%, and unlike the bond, the building's rent tended to *rise* with inflation while its value compounded. So a wall of institutional capital that had never before thought of itself as being in the landlord business went looking for housing to buy. The numbers are staggering precisely because housing is the largest asset class on Earth: when even a small slice of the world's \$144 trillion in bonds and \$126 trillion in equities decides that near-zero yields are intolerable and rotates toward the \$393 trillion housing market, the inflows dwarf anything local first-time buyers can muster. The search for yield is why the demand for housing-as-asset was not a trickle of speculators but an ocean of mainstream, conservative capital — and why it showed up in *every* city a global fund could buy into, all at once.

![Pipeline showing how near-zero rates inflate housing in six steps from central banks cutting the safe rate to cheap mortgages and yield-hunting capital both bidding up the same homes worldwide](/imgs/blogs/case-study-global-housing-affordability-crisis-3.png)

The pipeline above is the full causal chain, and it has *two* parallel currents that converge on the same place. The first current is the one we just walked: cheap mortgages hand buyers bigger checkbooks. The second is subtler and just as powerful — **yield-hunting capital**. When safe bonds pay almost nothing (step 3), every investor with money to park — pension funds, insurers, family offices, individual landlords — goes hunting for something that still produces an income. Housing is close to ideal: it produces rent, it tends to hold value, and crucially you can *borrow against it cheaply* to amplify the return. So a wall of investor capital floods into housing (step 4) at the very same time buyers are armed with bigger mortgages, and both bid up the same homes (step 5). The loop runs as long as rates stay low (step 6). This is asset-price inflation in action, and because *every* rich-world central bank cut at once, it ran in *every* desirable city at once.

This is the deepest reason the break was synchronized. Interest-rate policy is the master variable of property prices everywhere — and in the 2010s, the master variable was pinned to the floor across the entire rich world simultaneously. For the full mechanics of how rates set the floor under all property prices, see [interest rates and mortgages, the master variable](/blog/trading/real-estate/interest-rates-and-mortgages-the-master-variable); and for the broader money-and-liquidity picture, [the liquidity cycle and asset prices](/blog/trading/cross-asset/the-liquidity-cycle-and-asset-prices).

## Cause 2: constrained supply — zoning, approvals, and the wall

Cheap money set the fuse. But cheap money alone doesn't have to produce a crisis. If a city could *build* in response to all that new demand and borrowing power, the extra demand would be absorbed by new homes rather than by higher prices. The reason it wasn't — the reason the windfall landed on price instead of on quantity — is the second ingredient: **constrained supply**.

### What "constrained supply" means, from zero

Housing supply is *elastic* when a city can quickly add a lot of new homes when prices rise, and *inelastic* (constrained) when it can't. The constraint comes from a stack of frictions: **zoning** (legal rules dictating what can be built where — single-family-only neighborhoods, height limits, density caps), **slow approvals** (permits that take years and invite endless review and litigation), **physical geography** (a city hemmed in by water or mountains, like Hong Kong or San Francisco), and **construction costs** (labor, materials, and the cost of land itself). When demand surges in a constrained city, the supply curve barely moves — so the entire surge converts into price. We built this idea up in detail in [supply elasticity: why some cities boom and bust](/blog/trading/real-estate/supply-elasticity-why-some-cities-boom-and-bust) and [supply, zoning, land use, and construction costs](/blog/trading/real-estate/supply-zoning-land-use-and-construction-costs); here is why it's the silent partner of the rate boom.

The deep point is that **constrained supply converts a demand shock into a price shock instead of a quantity shock.** Without the supply constraint, cheap money would have produced a *building boom*: more homes, roughly stable prices, no affordability crisis. With the constraint, cheap money produced a *price boom*: the same number of homes, far higher prices, an affordability crisis. The two causes are not independent — the supply constraint is what *channels* the rate shock into the affordability gauge.

### Why constraints differ city to city — and why they all bind

It is worth being precise about *why* supply is constrained, because the reasons differ from city to city even though the *outcome* — too few homes — is the same. In **Hong Kong** and **San Francisco**, the binding constraint is partly physical: mountains, water, and steep terrain leave little developable land, and in Hong Kong the government compounds it by owning the land and releasing it slowly. In **London**, the constraint is a deliberate ring of protected countryside — the *green belt*, a band of land around the city where building is largely forbidden — plus a discretionary planning system where each project can be argued over for years. In **Sydney** and **Auckland**, the constraint is mostly *zoning*: vast areas reserved by law for detached single-family houses, so that even where land exists, you may not legally build the apartments that would house more people on it. In **HCMC** and **Hanoi**, the constraint is administrative — slow and unpredictable land-clearance, project approvals, and the legal-title frictions that can stall a development for years.

These look like different problems, and politically they are. But they bind through the *same* economic channel: each one caps how fast the quantity of housing can grow, so each one forces a demand surge onto price. And there is a common thread beneath the variety. In almost every case the constraint is *not* a law of nature but a **political choice** — a choice that, crucially, the people who already own homes have every incentive to defend, because a constrained supply is precisely what keeps their own home's value rising. A mountain is a hard constraint; a single-family zoning rule is a *soft* one that could be changed with a council vote — and the reason it so rarely is changed is the subject of the financialization section below. The constraints differ in their mechanics but converge in their politics: they are durable because a majority benefits from them, which is exactly why the supply problem proved so much stickier than the rate problem once rates eventually rose.

![Before-and-after comparison of a supply-constrained city absorbing a demand wave entirely in higher prices versus an elastic city absorbing the same demand in new homes and staying affordable](/imgs/blogs/case-study-global-housing-affordability-crisis-4.png)

The figure above makes the channeling visible. The *same* demand wave — jobs, migration, cheap credit — arrives in two cities. On the left, a supply-constrained city (Sydney, HCMC): zoning and slow approvals cap construction, few new homes get built, and the price absorbs the whole shock, running price-to-income to 30+. On the right, an elastic city (Houston, Tokyo): permissive zoning and fast permits let builders add many units, supply rises to meet the demand, and price-to-income stays near 10–15. *Same input, opposite output* — and the only difference is whether the city could build.

#### Worked example: same demand, a constrained city vs an elastic one

Let's put numbers on it. Two cities each receive a demand shock — 10,000 new households want to move in over five years, each able (thanks to cheap rates) to bid 20% more than before.

- **Constrained city (call it "Locktown").** Zoning and approvals let it build only 2,000 new homes over the five years. So 10,000 new households chase 2,000 new homes plus whatever existing owners sell. Demand vastly outstrips new supply; the 20% bigger checkbooks collide with near-fixed inventory. Prices rise sharply — say **+45%** over five years — and price-to-income climbs from ~18 to ~26. Almost the entire demand shock shows up as price.
- **Elastic city (call it "Buildville").** Permissive zoning and fast permits let it build 9,000 new homes over the same five years. Now 10,000 new households chase 9,000 new homes; supply nearly keeps pace. Prices barely move — say **+8%** — and price-to-income inches from ~12 to ~13. Almost the entire demand shock shows up as new *homes*, not new *prices*.

Same demand, same cheap money, wildly different affordability — because one city converted the shock into quantity and the other into price.

*The intuition: supply elasticity is the valve that decides whether a demand shock becomes new homes or new prices — and a city that can't build is a city that turns every boom into an affordability crisis.*

This is also the cleanest answer to a question we'll return to in the misconceptions: is the crisis a "price problem" or a "supply problem"? It is *both*, in sequence. Cheap money is the price problem (it inflated the bids). Constrained supply is the supply problem (it forced those bids onto a fixed stock). Neither alone is sufficient; together they're the crisis.

## Cause 3: financialization — housing as the world's store of value

The third ingredient explains why housing — as opposed to, say, stocks — became the *preferred* destination for all that cheap money, and why the demand was so relentless. It is the **financialization of housing**: the global shift toward treating a home primarily as a leverage-able store of value rather than as shelter.

### Why housing is the world's favorite asset

Step back and ask what an investor in the 2010s wanted. They wanted something that (1) holds its value against inflation, (2) produces an income stream, (3) can be bought with cheap borrowed money to amplify the return, and (4) feels safe — a *hard* asset you can see and touch, not a number on a screen. Housing checks every box. It is the largest asset class on Earth — around **\$393 trillion** globally, with residential alone near \$301 trillion, dwarfing global equities (~\$126 trillion) and even all the gold ever mined (~\$20 trillion, about 5% of real estate). When the world's investors went hunting for a hard, leverage-able store of value, the biggest, most familiar, most easily-borrowed-against asset class was always going to absorb a huge share of the flow.

And the leverage point is what makes housing *special* among assets. You can typically borrow 70–90% of a home's value at a mortgage rate, which means a small amount of equity controls a large asset — so when prices rise, the return on your *equity* is enormous. In a world of near-zero borrowing costs, that leverage was nearly free. We unpack this amplification in [leverage and the mortgage: how debt amplifies property](/blog/trading/real-estate/leverage-and-the-mortgage-how-debt-amplifies-property). Financialization is, in large part, the story of the whole world discovering at once that cheap leverage on a hard asset is the best deal available.

### How financialization shows up

Financialization isn't one thing; it's a cluster of behaviors that all treat the home as an asset first:

- **Buy-to-let investors** — individuals owning a second, third, or tenth home purely to rent and to ride the price.
- **Institutional landlords** — funds buying thousands of single-family homes as a yield asset (a major US phenomenon in the 2010s).
- **Cross-border capital** — wealth from one country parked in the property of another (mainland capital in Hong Kong and Vancouver; global money in London and Sydney).
- **The home-as-retirement-plan** — ordinary owners treating their primary home as their main savings vehicle, which makes them politically *opposed* to anything (like new supply) that might lower its price.

That last point is the quiet engine. Once a majority of voters own homes and treat them as their nest egg, the political system acquires a permanent constituency *for* high and rising prices and *against* new construction. Financialization and the supply constraint reinforce each other: homeowners who see their house as an asset vote to block the supply that would make housing affordable. This is why the supply constraint is so durable — it is not a bureaucratic accident, it is a political equilibrium.

### How financialization eats supply, not just bids on it

There's a subtler harm in financialization that the simple "investors bid against families" story misses: the asset-treatment of housing doesn't only add *demand*, it actively *subtracts from the usable supply* of homes for people who need to live in them. Three mechanisms do this, and they grew up together in the 2010s.

The first is **build-to-rent**: institutional funds that buy — or commission the building of — entire blocks of housing to hold and rent rather than sell to owner-occupiers. In the US, funds bought up tens of thousands of single-family houses after 2008, often outbidding first-time buyers with all-cash offers and no financing contingency, then renting the same homes back to the families who couldn't buy them. The home still shelters someone, but it has been permanently removed from the *ownership* market — converted from a thing a family could buy into a yield asset a fund collects rent on.

The second is **second and holiday homes**: as housing became a store of value, the wealthy bought additional homes they barely occupy — a pied-à-terre in London, a beach flat in Đà Nẵng, a ski chalet in the Alps. Every such unit is a roof that exists but houses no permanent resident; in some desirable towns, a quarter or more of the housing stock sits dark most of the year. The supply is built but effectively withdrawn.

The third, and fastest-growing, is **short-term-rental conversion**: the rise of platforms like Airbnb turned a long-term rental that housed a local family into a nightly rental for tourists, which in hot tourist cities often earns the owner far more. A flat that once sheltered a teacher on a 12-month lease becomes a rotating hotel room — counted in the housing stock, but no longer available to anyone who actually lives in the city. Across Barcelona, Lisbon, and the centers of dozens of tourist cities, studies have tied concentrated short-term-rental growth to measurably higher long-term rents, because every flat converted is a flat subtracted from the pool locals can rent. Financialization, in short, doesn't just raise the bids on the homes that remain available — it shrinks how many remain available at all.

#### Worked example: a build-to-rent fund buys a whole block

Make the supply-eating concrete. Imagine a suburb where a developer completes a new block of **100 townhouses**, each expected to sell to a first-time-buyer family at about **\$400,000**. In a normal market, 100 families become homeowners and the local price-to-income gauge gets 100 units of relief.

Now a build-to-rent fund steps in *before* the block hits the open market. The fund's logic is the search-for-yield logic from earlier: each \$400,000 home rents for, say, \$24,000 a year, a **6% gross yield** — and with cheap leverage the return on the fund's equity is far higher. The fund offers the developer **\$420,000 per home, all cash, the entire block in one transaction, closing in 30 days** — no mortgage approvals, no financing fall-throughs, no chain. The developer takes the certain, premium, single-buyer deal in a heartbeat.

The result: **zero** of those 100 homes reach the 100 families. The block that was supposed to add 100 units of ownership supply added *none* — it was converted wholesale into a rental asset before a single family could bid. The families are now renting those very homes from the fund, paying \$24,000 a year, with no path to ownership and no equity building. And note what the fund's \$420,000 all-cash bid did to the *price*: it reset the local benchmark 5% higher than the family-buyer price, so the *next* block, too, will clear above what families can pay. One transaction removed 100 homes from the for-sale market and nudged the whole neighborhood's price up at the same time.

*The intuition: financialized capital doesn't just outbid families home by home — at scale it can buy the supply wholesale before families ever get to bid, converting would-be ownership into permanent tenancy and ratcheting the local price up in the same stroke.*

#### Worked example: the leverage that made housing irresistible

Let's see why cheap leverage on a hard asset was the deal of the decade. An investor, Dana again, buys a \$500,000 rental property in 2015 with 20% down — \$100,000 of her own equity and a \$400,000 mortgage at, say, 4%.

- Over five years to 2020, the home appreciates a modest **30%**, to \$650,000.
- The mortgage balance has barely fallen (early payments are mostly interest), so call it ~\$370,000.
- Dana's equity is now \$650,000 − \$370,000 = **\$280,000**.

She put in \$100,000 and now has \$280,000 — a **180% return on her equity** from a mere 30% rise in the home's price. That's leverage: the price rose 30%, but her *equity* nearly tripled, because she controlled a \$500,000 asset with \$100,000. Meanwhile her borrowing cost was a cheap 4% (and falling). Now multiply Dana by millions of investors across the rich world, all making the same trade at the same time, and you have a wall of leveraged demand pounding on a fixed supply.

*The intuition: financialization plus cheap leverage turned the home into the highest-return, lowest-stress asset most people could access — which is exactly why so much of the world's capital piled into it at once, crowding out the families who just needed somewhere to live.*

This is the asset-versus-shelter tension at the center of the whole crisis: a home is simultaneously a place to live and the world's favorite investment, and when the *investment* demand swamps the *shelter* supply, the people who only wanted shelter lose. See [housing as a financial asset versus shelter](/blog/trading/real-estate/housing-as-financial-asset-vs-shelter) for the full treatment, and [real estate, REITs, income, leverage, and rates](/blog/trading/cross-asset/real-estate-reits-income-leverage-and-rates) for how property fits a global portfolio.

## The 2020–21 accelerant: the final shove

By 2019, the three structural causes had already pushed many cities deep into unaffordability. Then came the accelerant. When the pandemic hit in 2020, central banks did the only thing they knew: they cut rates back to zero, *fast*, and flooded the system with liquidity. The US 30-year mortgage fell to its **all-time record low of 2.65% in January 2021**. Governments sent cash directly to households. And lockdowns suddenly made *space* — a home office, a yard, a spare room — feel essential.

The result was a buying frenzy. With the cheapest mortgages in history, a pile of savings (people couldn't travel or eat out), a sudden craving for more space, and a remote-work revolution that untethered buyers from city-center jobs, demand exploded — and it slammed into the same constrained supply as before, now made *worse* by pandemic-disrupted construction. US national home prices, which had recovered from the 2008 crash to about 184 (on the Case-Shiller index where 2000 = 100) at the prior peak, blew past it to **308 by mid-2022**. In Vietnam, Hanoi's average primary apartment price jumped roughly +35% in 2024 and another +40% in 2025; HCMC ran +33% then to about ₫111 million per square meter (≈ \$4,222/m²). The 2020–21 lows were the final shove that turned a chronic problem into an acute crisis — and once again, because the stimulus was globally synchronized, the frenzy was global.

There's a cruel twist in the chart's right edge. When inflation forced central banks to *raise* rates hard in 2022–23 (the US 30-year mortgage shot to 7.79% by late 2023), affordability didn't improve — it got *worse*. Prices had already locked in at boom levels, and now the *payment* on those high prices ballooned with the higher rate. Existing owners with cheap fixed mortgages refused to sell and give up their 2.65% loan, which froze supply further. So buyers faced both high prices *and* high payments at once. The accelerant didn't reverse when rates rose; it just changed which gauge hurt.

#### Worked example: the 2021 buyer who got squeezed from both sides

Trace one buyer, Minh, through the whiplash. In early 2021, Minh is looking at a flat priced at ₫5.0 billion (≈ \$193,000). He hesitates. By 2023, the cheap-money frenzy has pushed the same flat to ₫7.0 billion (≈ \$270,000) — up 40%. Worse, his mortgage rate has reset: Vietnamese home loans typically start with a teaser around 8% for a year or two, then float up to **12–14%**. Suppose Minh's effective rate went from a teaser 8% to a floating 13%.

- **In 2021:** a ₫5.0 billion flat, 70% financed (₫3.5 billion loan) at an 8% teaser, 20-year term → a monthly payment around ₫29 million.
- **By 2023:** a ₫7.0 billion flat, same 70% financing (₫4.9 billion loan) at a reset 13%, 20-year term → a monthly payment around ₫57 million.

His monthly payment nearly *doubled* — driven both by the 40% higher price *and* the reset to a far higher rate. The frenzy raised the price; the rate snap-back raised the payment. Minh got squeezed from both sides. (This teaser-then-reset trap is exactly the one we flagged in the rates post — the low headline rate is a hook, not the real cost.)

*The intuition: the 2020–21 lows were an accelerant, not a cure — they jammed prices to boom levels, and when rates snapped back the high price stayed while the payment exploded, leaving the would-be buyer worse off on both gauges at once.*

## What's been tried: the policy menu

So a dozen cities broke at once, for shared structural reasons. What have governments actually *done* about it? The policy responses fall into four families, and — this is the important part — they have very different track records. Most aim at the symptom (high prices) rather than the root (too few homes for too much money), and it shows.

![Matrix of four policy families demand cooling, supply expansion, buyer subsidies, and rent control, showing what each does, what it actually did, and a verdict on each](/imgs/blogs/case-study-global-housing-affordability-crisis-6.png)

The matrix above is the policy menu with an honest scorecard. Let's walk the four families.

### Demand cooling

**Demand cooling** tries to suppress the *demand* side directly — usually by taxing the buyers a policymaker most wants to discourage. The archetype is Singapore's **Additional Buyer's Stamp Duty (ABSD)**: a tax that's zero on a citizen's first home but rises steeply on second and third homes and on foreign buyers (up to 60% for foreigners). Hong Kong, Vancouver, Australia, and New Zealand all deployed versions — extra stamp duties, foreign-buyer taxes, vacancy taxes. We covered the archetype in [the Singapore cooling-measures case study](/blog/trading/real-estate/case-study-singapore-cooling-measures).

What did it do? Demand cooling reliably *takes the froth off* — it cools speculation, slows foreign inflows, and buys time. Singapore used it to keep its market from running away the way Hong Kong's did. But here is the limit: **demand cooling does not build a single new home.** It rearranges *who* buys (toward owner-occupiers, away from speculators) and *when* (slowing the boom), but the underlying shortage is untouched. It's a tourniquet, not a cure — valuable for stopping the bleeding, useless for healing the wound.

### Supply expansion

**Supply expansion** attacks the root: it tries to let cities *build more homes*. The tools are **upzoning** (legally allowing denser construction — apartments where only houses were permitted, taller buildings, smaller lots), faster permitting, cutting approval times and litigation, and direct public construction. This is the family with by far the best track record, which we'll detail in the next section. It is also, not coincidentally, the *hardest* politically, because it directly threatens the home values of the existing-owner majority.

### Subsidies

**Subsidies** try to help buyers afford the existing prices: first-home-buyer grants, down-payment assistance, cheap government-backed loans, and (a better-targeted cousin) **social housing** — homes built or subsidized by the state and let below market. The grants and assistance are politically popular because they *feel* like help. But on the demand-without-supply logic we've built, they have a notorious failure mode, which we'll show in a worked example below: in a supply-constrained market, a buyer subsidy mostly **raises the price by the amount of the subsidy**, capturing the benefit for sellers, not buyers. Social housing is the exception — because it *adds units*, it's really a supply policy wearing a subsidy's clothes.

### Rent control

**Rent control** caps what landlords can charge, to protect existing tenants from runaway rents. It does help *sitting* tenants in the short run — that's real. But the economics are unforgiving over time: caps reduce the return on building or maintaining rental housing, so they tend to *shrink* the future supply of rentals, worsening scarcity for everyone not lucky enough to already hold a controlled unit. It shifts the pain (from current tenants to future ones) rather than removing it. Most economists treat broad, hard rent control as a cautionary tale — though milder forms (capping *increases* rather than levels, with exemptions for new construction) are less damaging.

## What actually worked — and what backfired

Now the payoff of the whole case study. With four families of policy and decades of experiments across dozens of cities, what does the record actually say worked?

![Grid sorting policy levers into what worked and what backfired across supply and demand, ending with the lesson for Vietnam to speed approvals, zone for density, and build social housing](/imgs/blogs/case-study-global-housing-affordability-crisis-7.png)

The grid above is the verdict, sorted into what durably *worked* versus what *backfired*, lever by lever, ending on the lesson for Vietnam.

### What worked: adding supply

The clearest success stories are the cities that *built*. **Tokyo** is the canonical case: Japan's relatively permissive, nationally-set zoning let Tokyo add housing prolifically even as its population and economy stayed large, and the result is that one of the world's biggest, richest, most desirable cities has a price-to-income ratio of about **15** — high by healthy standards, but a fraction of Hong Kong's 44 or Sydney's stratosphere. **Houston** and much of the US Sun Belt tell the same story from the other side of the world: minimal zoning, abundant land, and fast permitting kept ratios near 5–8 even through population booms.

The two cleanest natural experiments, though, are the upzonings, because they let us compare the *same* city before and after a rule change. **Auckland** rezoned about three-quarters of its residential land for greater density in its 2016 *Unitary Plan* — and the studies that followed found that the upzoned areas roughly *tripled* their rate of new dwelling construction over the next several years, with researchers crediting the plan for tens of thousands of additional homes and measurably slower rent growth than the counterfactual. **Minneapolis** went further in 2018, becoming the first major US city to abolish single-family-only zoning citywide while also legalizing fourplexes and cutting parking mandates; in the years after, the city permitted homes far faster than the rest of its state and its rents rose only a few percent while comparable metros saw double-digit increases. Both cases carry the same lesson: where the rules let builders add homes, they did — and affordability stopped deteriorating in exactly those places. Adding supply is slow, unglamorous, and politically painful, but across the global record it is the *only* lever that has durably moved affordability.

### What worked partially: well-targeted demand cooling

Singapore's ABSD deserves credit for what it actually achieved: keeping a small, land-scarce, wealthy, capital-magnet city from spiraling the way Hong Kong did. But notice *why* it worked where it did — Singapore paired demand cooling with one of the world's most aggressive *supply* programs (the Housing and Development Board houses roughly 80% of citizens in subsidized flats). The cooling measures bought time; the public housing supplied the homes. Demand cooling worked because it wasn't *alone*.

The cleaner test cases for demand cooling *by itself* are the foreign-buyer and vacancy taxes, and their record is honestly mixed. **Vancouver** imposed a 15% foreign-buyer tax in 2016 (later 20%) plus an *empty-homes tax* on dwellings left vacant; the foreign-buyer tax produced a sharp but brief dip in foreign purchases and prices before the market resumed climbing on domestic demand, while the empty-homes tax did the more useful work — it nudged thousands of vacant units back onto the rental market and shaved the city's vacancy stock. **New South Wales** (Sydney) layered on foreign-buyer surcharges of its own; like Vancouver's, they trimmed the visible foreign bid at the top end but left the structural shortage — and therefore the broad price level — essentially intact. The pattern is consistent: these taxes can *redistribute* who owns and can pull *idle* units back into use, but because none of them builds a home, none durably fixed affordability where supply stayed locked.

### What backfired: subsidies without supply

The most instructive failure is the buyer subsidy in a supply-constrained market. Time and again — first-home-buyer grants in Australia, help-to-buy schemes in the UK — the subsidy designed to make homes affordable ended up *raising prices*, because in a market that can't add homes, handing buyers more money just lets them bid more for the same fixed stock. The UK's *Help to Buy* scheme (2013–2023) is the textbook case: independent evaluations, including by the National Audit Office and academic studies, found that in supply-constrained areas a large share of the subsidy was simply *capitalized into the price* — builders and sellers raised asking prices toward the extra money buyers now held, so the policy inflated new-build prices and padded developer margins while doing little for affordability. Australia's recurring *First Home Owner Grants* show the same fingerprint: each fresh grant tended to be followed by a step-up in starter-home prices roughly tracking the grant, leaving buyers no better off net of the higher price.

#### Worked example: the first-buyer subsidy that just raised the price (backfire)

Make it concrete. A government wants to help first-time buyers, so it offers a **\$50,000 grant** to anyone buying their first home. There's a fixed stock of starter homes and many more buyers than homes. Before the grant, a typical starter home cleared at **\$400,000** — the price at which the most-motivated buyer was just able and willing to pay.

Now every first-time buyer has \$50,000 *more* to spend. They're all bidding on the same fixed set of homes. The auction simply re-clears higher: the most-motivated buyer can now afford \$450,000, so that's roughly what the home sells for. The grant didn't lower the cost of housing; it raised the price by ~\$50,000 and handed the money straight to the *seller*. The buyer is no better off — they have a \$50,000 grant *and* a \$50,000-bigger price tag. Worse, the *next* cohort of buyers without grants now faces a \$450,000 market. In a supply-constrained city, the subsidy is a transfer from taxpayers to existing homeowners, dressed up as help for buyers.

*The intuition: any policy that adds demand to a market that can't add supply gets capitalized into price — so a subsidy without supply doesn't make housing cheaper, it makes it more expensive and pays the difference to whoever already owns.*

### What backfired: hard rent control and endless review

Two other backfires round out the picture. **Broad, hard rent control** (as opposed to mild caps on increases) has repeatedly shrunk rental supply — landlords convert units to condos, defer maintenance, or simply build less — leaving future renters worse off even as current tenants are protected. The most vivid recent demonstration is **Berlin's 2020 rent freeze** (the *Mietendeckel*), which capped and even rolled back rents on most of the city's apartments. For the year and a half it was in force, the supply of rental flats offered on the open market *collapsed* — listings of controlled units fell by roughly half as owners pulled them or sold them off, while rents on the *uncontrolled* new-build segment shot up as demand piled into the only flats still freely available. Germany's constitutional court struck the freeze down in 2021 on jurisdictional grounds, and tenants who had enjoyed the lower rents suddenly owed back-payments — a clean illustration that a price cap which ignores supply doesn't create homes, it just reshuffles scarcity and often deepens it. And the most pervasive backfire of all is **the supply constraint itself as policy**: the endless environmental reviews, design-review boards, neighborhood vetoes, and litigation that, in the name of process, prevent the one thing that durably helps. A city that takes five years and three lawsuits to approve an apartment building has, in effect, chosen high prices — it just doesn't say so out loud.

#### Worked example: the policy scorecard — which lever moved affordability

Let's score the four levers against the one outcome that matters — did price-to-income actually fall (or stop rising)?

| Lever | Mechanism | Effect on price-to-income |
|---|---|---|
| **Supply (upzoning, fast permits)** | adds homes | **Falls / stops rising** where built (Tokyo ~15, Houston ~6) |
| **Demand cooling (ABSD)** | cools speculation | **Holds the line** temporarily; needs supply to last (Singapore) |
| **Buyer subsidy (grants)** | adds demand | **Rises** by ~the subsidy; backfires (Australia, UK) |
| **Hard rent control** | caps rents | **Worse over time** as supply shrinks; protects only incumbents |

Read the right-hand column top to bottom and the lesson is unmistakable: only the lever that *adds homes* reliably moves affordability the right way. Everything else either holds the line temporarily (demand cooling) or actively backfires (subsidies, hard rent control).

*The intuition: the scorecard collapses a decade of global experiments into one rule — build, and affordability improves; do anything else without building, and you at best buy time and at worst make it worse.*

## Common misconceptions

The affordability debate is full of half-truths, each true enough to be seductive and wrong enough to misdirect policy. Four are worth correcting head-on.

**"It's purely a supply problem."** This is the most popular take among economists, and it's *mostly* right — but "purely" overstates it. If supply were the *only* problem, affordability wouldn't have worsened in *sync* across cities with very different supply constraints. The demand side — cheap money and financialized capital — is what made *every* city's supply constraint bind at once. Supply is the lever that *fixes* it (because demand from cheap money is hard to switch off without crashing the economy), but the *trigger* was the global rate collapse hitting constrained supply everywhere simultaneously. Call it a supply problem *and* a price problem: the price problem lit the fuse, the supply problem was the powder.

**"It's purely a rate problem."** The mirror error. Yes, ultra-low rates inflated the bids — but rates fell just as far in Tokyo and Houston as in Sydney, and *those* cities stayed affordable. The rate cut was the same; the outcome diverged on supply. If it were purely rates, every city would have broken equally, and they didn't. Rates are why the bids got bigger; supply is why the bigger bids landed on price instead of on new homes.

**"Foreigners caused it."** Foreign buyers are real, visible, and politically convenient to blame — and in a few specific markets (mainland capital in Hong Kong and Vancouver pre-tax) they genuinely added meaningful demand at the top. But they cannot be the main cause, for the same synchronization reason: the crisis hit cities with *trivial* foreign-buyer shares (much of suburban America, plenty of mid-size Australian cities) just as hard. Foreign demand is a *local amplifier* on top of the global causes, not the engine. Blaming foreigners is appealing because it externalizes the problem; the data say the call is coming from inside the house — domestic cheap credit, domestic investors, and domestic zoning.

**"The market will fix it on its own."** The hope is that high prices will eventually summon enough new building to bring prices back down — the normal way a free market clears a shortage. The problem is that the supply constraint is *not* a market failure that markets can self-correct; it's a *political* constraint imposed *on* the market. Zoning, approval delays, and homeowner vetoes are exactly the mechanisms that *prevent* the market from responding to high prices by building. The market can't fix what the political system has forbidden it to do. Affordability improves only when the rules change to *let* the market build — which is a political act, not a market one.

## How it shows up: the cross-city comparison, and what Vietnam can learn

Let's land the abstraction in real cities, then bring it home.

**Hong Kong (ratio ~44).** The rich-world extreme, and a clean demonstration of the recipe. Geography (mountains and water) and policy (a government that controls and slowly releases land) make supply almost perfectly inelastic. Add a flood of mainland capital treating Hong Kong property as a store of value, add the low-rate era, and you get the world's least affordable major city — 44 years of income for a home. Hong Kong is what happens when *every* ingredient maxes out at once.

**Sydney and Auckland (ratios ~11–13 and rising).** The Anglo template: desirable cities, restrictive zoning, a national obsession with housing as the household's main investment, and a wall of cheap credit. Both deployed foreign-buyer taxes and demand cooling; both saw the relief fade because they didn't build enough. Auckland's 2016 upzoning is the hopeful footnote — early evidence that where it allowed density, building picked up and rent growth slowed.

**London (ratio ~12).** A global-capital magnet with green-belt land restrictions ringing the city and a planning system famous for delay. The same recipe: financialized housing, constrained supply, cheap money. Help-to-buy subsidies are the textbook backfire — widely judged to have pushed prices up rather than helping buyers.

**Tokyo (ratio ~15) and Houston (~6).** The control group. Same global cheap money, same desirability, but *permissive supply* — and ratios that, while not cheap, never spiraled. They are the living proof that the crisis was avoidable where cities chose to build.

**HCMC (~32) and Hanoi (~31).** Vietnam's cities now sit *above* the global median and in the company of Beijing and Mumbai. The recipe is the same with local seasoning: a fast-growing, urbanizing population driving real demand (see [demographics and urbanization: the demand wave](/blog/trading/real-estate/demographics-and-urbanization-the-demand-wave)); a banking and developer system that funneled cheap credit into property through the 2010s and into the 2020–21 lows; land and approval frictions that throttle supply; and a cultural treatment of property as *the* store of value (land especially — đất nền — is the national savings vehicle). Vietnam even ran its own boom-and-freeze cycle (the 2022–23 bond crunch we cover in [Vietnam's property cycles](/blog/trading/real-estate/vietnam-property-cycles-2007-2013-2022)).

So what can Vietnam learn from the global scorecard? The lesson is the grid's bottom row, and it's blunt. First, **the durable fix is supply** — speed up approvals, zone for density in and around the major cities, and build social/affordable housing at scale. Vietnam's land-and-approval frictions are exactly the kind of constraint that turns a demand boom into a price boom; the Land Law 2024's move to annual land-price tables is a step, but the binding constraint is the *pace and predictability of building*. Second, **avoid the subsidy trap**: with HCMC already at a 32-year ratio, a first-buyer cash subsidy applied to that fixed stock would mostly feed the boom, as it did in Australia and the UK — unless it's paired with real new supply. Third, **demand cooling can buy time but won't cure**: a holding tax on speculative second homes (long debated by the Ministry of Finance) could cool the froth and discourage hoarding — useful, but only as a complement to building, never a substitute. The single most important thing Vietnam can do is the single hardest thing politically everywhere: let the cities build, faster and denser, before the asset-owning majority hardens into the same veto bloc that locked Sydney and London into permanent unaffordability.

## When this matters / Further reading

This is the synthesis case, so its lesson is the whole series compressed into one sentence: **housing broke in so many cities at once because the world cut rates to zero together, treated homes as the planet's favorite leverage-able asset, and refused to let its best cities build — and only the cities that built escaped.** The synchronization is the proof that the causes were shared and structural, not local and accidental.

It matters to you in the most direct way imaginable: whether you are Minh in HCMC, Dana in the US, or the 30-year-old in Sydney, the price-to-income ratio of your city is mostly *not* about you — your savings rate, your job, your timing. It's about a global recipe that was cooked before you ever started looking. Knowing that won't lower your rent, but it will tell you which political fights actually matter (supply, every time), which "solutions" are traps (subsidies and grants without building), and how to read your own city's number: a ratio near 5 is a healthy market, near 15 is stretched, and past 25 is a structural crisis that no amount of personal hustle will fix. The number on the chart is a measure of the rules, not of you.
