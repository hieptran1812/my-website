> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Property cycles are unusually long — averaging roughly **18 years peak-to-peak** — because two slow, self-reinforcing forces drive them: the **fixed supply of land** (which lets a speculative boom capitalize straight into land prices) and the **credit cycle** that funds the bidding. It is not mysticism — it is land plus credit plus the years-long lag to build.
>
> - The rhythm is old and eerily regular. Homer Hoyt mapped ~18-year Chicago land cycles back to the 1830s; Fred Harrison and Phillip Anderson tracked it across US and UK markets; Fred Foldvary used it to call the 2008 bust *in advance, in 1997*.
> - Major modern peaks cluster around **1973, 1990, and 2008**, with the next window projected for **2026–2028**. A shallow **mid-cycle dip** roughly seven years in (1980, 1999, 2017) splits each cycle in two and fools people into selling at the wrong time.
> - The shape is lopsided: about **14 years up** (in two legs, with the mid-cycle pause between them) and only about **4 years down**. The climb is long and the bust is fast.
> - The one thing to remember: this is a **weak prior, not a clock**. Cycles run "about" 18 years — sometimes 16, sometimes 21 — and the *signals* (loose credit, land speculation, record construction starts) confirm or deny the calendar. Treat it as a tilt, never a timer.

In March 2007, a friend of a friend in Ho Chi Minh City — call him **Minh's uncle** — sold a small plot of land on the city's eastern fringe for what felt like a life-changing sum. He had bought it five years earlier for almost nothing; now satellite-district *đất nền* (raw residential land plots) were the talk of every coffee shop, the VN-Index had just touched 1,170, and everyone he knew was either buying land or regretting that they hadn't. He took his profit, congratulated himself on his timing, and watched in disbelief over the next four years as the market froze, inflation hit 18%, and half-finished towers stood empty across the country. He had, almost by accident, sold near a top. He spent the next decade convinced he was a genius. He was mostly just standing in the right place when an 18-year-old tide went out.

Here is the strange thing. If you had handed Minh's uncle a chart of property peaks across the United States and Britain over the previous two centuries, he would have seen that his "lucky" 2007 sale landed almost exactly where a small group of unfashionable economists had been pointing for years. Not because they knew anything about Vietnam — they had never heard of his plot — but because property, almost everywhere it is bought and sold with borrowed money, marches to a beat that is far slower and far more regular than the stock market, the business cycle, or the news. That beat is roughly **18 years from one peak to the next**, and it has been ticking, with remarkable stubbornness, since at least the 1830s.

This post is about *why*. Not "buy here, sell there" — that is not what this is, and anyone who sells you a property clock as a money machine is selling you a story. This is about the **mechanism**: the two slow engines, land and credit, that stretch property's rhythm out to nearly two decades when stocks turn over every four or five years. Once you see the machine, the regularity stops being spooky and starts being almost obvious — and so do the ways it tricks people.

The figure above is the mental model for the whole post. Those red stems are the big property peaks — 1973, 1990, 2008 — and the gaps between them are all roughly 18 years. The little amber stems below the line are the mid-cycle dips that split each long cycle in half. The shaded band on the right is the window the 18-year school projects for the next peak: roughly **2026–2028**. We are going to take this timeline apart, build it back up from first principles, and end with the honest caveat it deserves: a pattern is not a law, and 18 years is a tilt, not a timer.

This is educational, not financial advice. The numbers our recurring characters — **Minh**, a Ho Chi Minh City buyer, and **Dana**, a US investor — work through are illustrative and rounded so you can do them in your head. The mechanism is real; the precise dates are approximate by nature.

## Foundations: land plus credit equals a long cycle

Before we can explain an 18-year rhythm, we need to define every moving part from zero. A practitioner can skim this section; a beginner cannot proceed without it. Take it slowly — almost everything later falls out of these definitions.

### What a "cycle" even is

A **cycle** is just a pattern that repeats: a rise, a peak, a fall, a trough, and then a rise again, over and over. The word does *not* mean the pattern is perfectly regular or that you can set your watch by it. Day and night is a near-perfect cycle; the business cycle (boom and recession in the broad economy) is a loose, messy one that runs roughly 4–7 years but varies wildly. When we say property runs an "18-year cycle," we mean something in between: more regular than the business cycle, far less regular than the seasons. The interval is *about* 18 years, and the spread around that average is several years in either direction.

A useful piece of vocabulary: the **peak** (or *top*) is the moment prices stop rising and start falling; the **trough** (or *bottom*) is the moment they stop falling and start rising. "Peak-to-peak" means measuring from one top to the next — that is the ~18-year figure. You could equally measure trough-to-trough and get the same average.

### Land versus the building on it

This is the single most important distinction in real estate, and the whole cycle hangs on it. Every property is really *two assets fused into one price*:

- **The land** — the patch of Earth at a specific location, plus the legal right to use it. Land is **immovable** (you cannot carry a District 1 plot out to a cheaper suburb) and, for any given spot, **fixed in supply** (they are not making another corner of central Hanoi). Land tends to *appreciate* over time, because good locations are scarce and demand for them grows with the city.
- **The building** (the *structure* or *improvements*) — everything humans added on top: foundation, walls, roof, plumbing. A building is **reproducible** (you can always build another one) and it *depreciates* — it wears out like a car and falls toward zero over its useful life.

We dig into this split in depth in [land vs buildings](/blog/trading/real-estate/land-vs-buildings-two-assets-in-every-property); here you only need the headline. When property prices "go up," it is almost always the **land** going up. The building is a wasting asset; the land is where the action is. Hold that thought — it is the slow engine of the entire cycle.

### Capitalization: how a flow of value turns into a price

Here is a word that sounds technical but is simple once you see it. To **capitalize** something means to turn a *stream of future benefits* into a single *price today*.

Suppose a piece of land lets you earn ₫100 million a year in rent, forever, and the going return investors demand on land is 5% a year. What is the land worth? You divide the annual rent by the required return: ₫100 million ÷ 0.05 = **₫2.0 billion** (≈ \$77,000). That ₫2.0 billion is the *capitalized value* of the rent stream. The price of land is just its future usefulness, squeezed into a number you can pay today.

Now watch what happens when the location gets better — a new metro stop, a rezoning, a booming neighbourhood — and the land can suddenly earn ₫150 million a year. At the same 5% return, it is now worth ₫150 million ÷ 0.05 = **₫3.0 billion** (≈ \$116,000). The land got ₫1.0 billion more valuable *the instant the future rent rose*, even though not one shovel of dirt moved. This is **land-value capitalization**: improvements in a location flow straight into the land price. Remember the phrase — it is the heart of why land, not buildings, drives the cycle.

### Speculation versus use

A buyer who wants a property to *live in or rent out* is buying **for use** — they care about the rent or the roof over their head. A buyer who buys mainly because they expect the *price to rise* and plans to flip it is **speculating** — they care about the next buyer, not the building.

Speculation is not a dirty word; it is a normal feature of every asset market and it can even be useful (speculators provide liquidity). But speculation has a special, dangerous interaction with *land*, precisely because land is fixed in supply. When people speculate on stocks, companies can issue more shares; when they speculate on a commodity, producers can pump more of it. When people speculate on **land**, no one can make more of it. So the speculative demand has nowhere to go *except into the price*. Speculation on a fixed-supply asset capitalizes directly and fully into that asset's price — and that is the accelerant that turns a gentle land-cycle into a mania.

### Ricardo's law of rent and Henry George

Two names you should know, because the modern 18-year-cycle school is built on their ideas.

**David Ricardo** (an English economist, writing around 1817) gave us the **law of rent**. In plain terms: the rent a piece of land can command is set by how much *more* productive it is than the worst land still in use. If the best farmland grows 100 bushels and the marginal (worst) farmland in use grows 60, the best land can command a rent equal to that 40-bushel *difference*. As a society grows richer and more crowded and pushes worse and worse land into use, the gap — and therefore the rent on the good land — widens. **Economic progress flows into land rent.** When a city booms, the gains don't stay with the workers or the businesses; they get bid into the rent of the well-located land, because that land is the scarce thing everyone competes for.

**Henry George** (an American writer, *Progress and Poverty*, 1879) took Ricardo's insight and made it the centre of an entire worldview. George noticed the paradox of his age: as the economy advanced, land values soared, yet poverty persisted. His explanation was that the fruits of progress were being captured by *landowners* — people who happened to own the well-located dirt — rather than by the people who actually produced the value. His famous policy proposal was a single **land-value tax** to recapture that "unearned" land rent for the public. We don't need the policy debate here. What we need is George's core economic claim, which the cycle theorists adopted wholesale: **the gains from economic growth get capitalized into land, and the resulting land speculation is what periodically wrecks the economy.** The school that grew from George is often called *Georgist*, and the 18-year cycle is, at its root, a Georgist idea.

### The construction lag

One more piece, and it is the reason supply can never quickly fix a property boom. When prices and rents rise, developers *want* to build more — but a building takes **years** to deliver. You have to assemble the land, get permits and rezonings, arrange financing, design it, then physically construct it. From "let's build" to "tenants move in" can easily be 3–5 years for a large project, longer in places with slow permitting.

So when demand surges, supply does *not* respond for years. Prices and rents keep climbing in the meantime, which makes everyone *more* confident and pulls in *more* projects — all of which are also years from completion. Then, near the top, the whole backlog of delayed projects finally delivers *at once*, into a market that has already turned. The slow supply response is exactly what makes a property cycle long and prone to overshoot in both directions. (We trace the four phases this produces in [the anatomy of a real-estate cycle](/blog/trading/real-estate/anatomy-of-a-real-estate-cycle-four-phases).)

### Why the stock and business cycle are shorter

Put these together and you can already see why property's clock runs so much slower than the stock market's. Stocks reprice in *seconds*; their fundamental, corporate earnings, turn over with the business cycle every 4–7 years; and companies can raise capital and expand quickly. Property has none of that speed: transactions take months, the supply response takes *years*, and the central scarce asset (land) cannot be expanded at all. The business cycle is the fast wheel; the **land cycle is the slow wheel underneath it**, and roughly two business cycles fit inside one land cycle. That ratio — two short cycles inside one long one — is the source of the mid-cycle dip we will meet later.

With these foundations — capitalization, speculation, Ricardo and George, the construction lag — we can now state the thesis precisely. **Land is the slow engine because speculation capitalizes straight into its fixed supply; credit is the accelerant because it funds the bidding; and the construction lag stretches the whole thing out and guarantees the overshoot.** Land plus credit plus lag equals a long, lopsided, roughly 18-year cycle. Everything else in this post is detail on that one sentence.

## The historical record: Hoyt, Harrison, Anderson, Foldvary

Before we explain the mechanism in depth, let's establish that the pattern is *real* and not a chart drawn after the fact. The 18-year cycle has a lineage, and it includes at least one genuinely impressive forecast made in advance.

### Homer Hoyt and a century of Chicago land

The foundational work is **Homer Hoyt's** 1933 book *One Hundred Years of Land Values in Chicago*. Hoyt, an economist, did something tedious and brilliant: he reconstructed Chicago land prices, building activity, and rents year by year all the way back to the 1830s. What he found was a recurring rhythm in land values of roughly **18 years** between major peaks — booms in the late 1830s, mid-1850s, early 1870s, around 1890, and around the 1910s — each followed by a collapse, and each driven by the same sequence of land speculation, overbuilding, and a credit bust. Hoyt was not a mystic; he was a careful empiricist who noticed that Chicago's land kept doing the same thing on a roughly two-decade beat. His book is where the "18-year land cycle" gets its number.

What makes Hoyt's work convincing rather than coincidental is that he documented the *same internal sequence* in each cycle, not just the spacing of the peaks. In every Chicago boom he traced, the order of events was identical: first rents and land values rose as the city grew; then subdividers and speculators carved up the fringe into building lots far faster than the population could fill them; then a wave of construction and mortgage lending followed the land speculation; then the boom outran the real demand, the easy credit reversed, and the whole edifice — land prices, building, and the banks that financed both — collapsed together. He even noticed that the *land* speculation consistently led the *building* boom, and that both led the credit bust, so the cycle had a recognizable choreography. A pattern that merely repeats in *timing* could be luck; a pattern that repeats in its *internal mechanism* over a century is telling you something about how the system actually works. That mechanism — land speculation, then overbuilding, then a credit reversal — is precisely the land-and-credit engine we are building in this post.

### Fred Harrison and the British record

Decades later, the British economist **Fred Harrison** revived and extended Hoyt's work, applying the Georgist lens explicitly. Harrison tracked UK and US property and argued that the same ~18-year land cycle was still running. Crucially, Harrison made *forecasts*. In the 1990s he warned that the cycle pointed to a property peak around **2007–2008** followed by a serious recession — a call he published well before the event, in books like *Boom Bust* (2005). When the global financial crisis hit in 2008, having been preceded by exactly the housing-and-credit blowoff his model described, Harrison's stock among cycle-watchers rose sharply.

### Phillip Anderson and the "secret life of real estate"

The Australian analyst **Phillip Anderson** synthesized the tradition in *The Secret Life of Real Estate and Banking* (2008), laying out US property peaks across the 19th and 20th centuries and arguing for the persistence of the ~18-year rhythm, with the mid-cycle dip as a regular feature. Anderson's contribution was partly to make the cycle *operational* — to describe the phases and the mid-cycle pause clearly enough that a reader could try to locate the present moment within them. He is one reason the cycle has a following among investors today rather than only among economic historians.

### Fred Foldvary's advance call on 2008

The most-cited single prediction belongs to the American Georgist economist **Fred Foldvary**. In a 1997 publication, *The Depression of 2008*, Foldvary used the 18-year land cycle (anchored on the prior ~1990 peak plus the typical 18-year span) to forecast a major economic downturn around **2008**, driven by a real-estate and credit bust. He published this *eleven years in advance*. When the crisis arrived essentially on schedule, it became the cycle school's headline evidence. One has to be careful here — a single correct call is not proof of a law, and Foldvary, Harrison, and Anderson all made other calls that were fuzzier. But it is genuinely hard to dismiss a two-century pattern plus a decade-ahead forecast as pure coincidence.

### The modern peaks: 1973, 1990, 2008, and 2026?

Pulling the strands together, the school points to a cluster of major US and global land-price peaks at roughly even spacing:

| Peak | Roughly | Years since prior peak | What it looked like |
|---|---|---|---|
| ~1973 | early 1970s | — | US property + early-1970s boom, then the 1973–75 recession and a sharp property downturn |
| ~1990 | around 1989–90 | ~17 | The late-1980s real-estate boom, the savings-and-loan crisis, the early-1990s bust |
| ~2008 | 2006–08 | ~18 | The US housing bubble, subprime, and the global financial crisis |
| projected ~2026 | 2026–2028 | ~18–20 | The window the cycle school is watching now |

And splitting each of those long cycles, a **mid-cycle dip** roughly seven years after the prior bottom: around **1980**, **1999**, and **2017**. None of those was a full property bust — each was a real but recoverable wobble that scared people into thinking the top had arrived years early.

That table is the spine of this post. The rest is mechanism: *why* those peaks land ~18 years apart, *why* the mid-cycle dip exists, and *why* you should still treat the whole thing as a prior rather than a prophecy.

## Why land is the slow engine

Now the core of the argument. Property cycles are long primarily because of **land** — its fixed supply and the way speculation capitalizes into it. Let's build this carefully.

### Fixed supply turns demand into price, not quantity

In an ordinary market, when demand rises, two things happen: the price goes up *and* the quantity supplied goes up, and the two share the adjustment. Demand for smartphones rises, prices tick up, factories make more phones, and the price settles back down as supply catches up.

Land cannot do the second half. The supply of well-located land is essentially **fixed** — you can build *taller* (more floor area on the same plot, which we'll get to), but you cannot create another central plot. So when demand for a location rises, the entire adjustment has to come through **price**. There is no "make more land" valve to release the pressure. This is why land prices can run so much further and so much faster than the prices of reproducible goods: nothing supplies its way back to equilibrium.

### Speculation capitalizes directly into land

Combine fixed supply with speculation and you get the cycle's engine. Recall that to speculate is to buy because you expect the price to rise. When speculators pile into a fixed-supply asset, their demand cannot be absorbed by new supply, so it goes *entirely into the price* — and because land's price is just its capitalized future rent, a wave of speculation effectively says "the future rent of this location is going to be enormous," and prices it in immediately.

This creates a feedback loop with land at the centre, and it is the single most important diagram in this post.

![A feedback loop showing rising land value creating more collateral and confidence, both pulling in more credit, which funds more land bidding, which lifts land value again, until credit tightens and the loop breaks](/imgs/blogs/why-real-estate-cycles-are-long-the-18-year-cycle-2.png)

Read the loop clockwise. Rising land value makes the land worth more as **collateral** (the asset a lender can seize if you default) *and* makes everyone more **confident** that prices only go up. Both pull in more **credit** — banks happily lend against an appreciating asset. That credit funds more **land bidding**, buyers and developers outbidding each other for the scarce plots. The bidding lifts **land value again**, and the whole loop goes round, faster and faster, for years. It runs until something breaks the credit leg — rates rise, a lender fails, a regulator tightens — and then the loop runs *in reverse*: falling land value destroys collateral, which kills credit, which kills bidding, which crushes land value. The bust is the same machine running backward, which is why it is so violent.

#### Worked example: a rezoning capitalizes into land price

Let's make capitalization concrete with the Georgist mechanism and real numbers. Minh is looking at an old single-storey house on a 100 m² plot in a Ho Chi Minh City district. Today the plot is zoned for low-rise only. The land, given what you're allowed to build, is worth **₫4.0 billion** (≈ \$154,000); the tired old structure on it is worth maybe **₫1.0 billion** to rebuild. Total asking price: **₫5.0 billion** (≈ \$193,000).

Now the city rezones the street for mid-rise apartments. Nothing physical changes — the same house sits on the same dirt — but the plot can now legally hold *four times* the floor area. The future rent the land can earn has roughly doubled, so its capitalized value roughly doubles too, to **₫9.0 billion** (≈ \$347,000). The structure is still worth ₫1.0 billion (it's the same old house; in fact you'll probably knock it down). The total is now **₫10.0 billion** (≈ \$385,000).

The plot just gained **₫5.0 billion** — the entire structure could burn down and the owner would still be ₫4.0 billion richer than yesterday. Not one brick moved. The rezoning capitalized straight into the **land**.

![A before and after comparison showing a plot worth four billion VND in land plus a one billion VND structure, then after rezoning the land jumps to nine billion VND while the same structure is unchanged](/imgs/blogs/why-real-estate-cycles-are-long-the-18-year-cycle-5.png)

The figure makes the split vivid: the structure column is *identical* on both sides; only the land moves, and it moves by the full value of the new permission. *This is the whole reason the cycle is a land cycle and not a building cycle — value lands on the dirt, and the dirt is the thing in fixed supply that speculation can chase without limit.*

### Why land is slow as well as powerful

If land is such a powerful engine, why is the cycle *slow* — 18 years rather than 18 months? Two reasons, both already in our foundations.

First, the **construction lag**. The only way the market can respond to high land prices is to build more floor area on the scarce land — taller buildings, denser development. But that takes years, so the supply response that might cool prices arrives long after the boom started, often only at the very top. There is no fast feedback to stop the rise early.

Second, **land doesn't change hands often**. Most owners sit on their land for years or decades; the float that actually trades is small. A small, slow-turning market with a years-long supply response simply cannot complete a full boom-bust in a couple of years. It needs the better part of two decades to build the speculation up and then wash it out.

There's a third, subtler reason land is slow: **transactions themselves are slow and expensive**. Buying a property is not like buying a stock. It takes weeks or months of searching, negotiating, surveying, arranging a mortgage, and clearing legal title; the transaction costs (in Vietnam, a 2% personal-income-tax on the transfer plus a 0.5% registration fee, plus agent commissions and notary fees) run to several percent of the price each way. High friction means people transact rarely and hold for a long time, which damps the speed at which the market can reprice. A stock can round-trip its entire owner base in a year; a neighbourhood might turn over a few percent of its homes annually. The slower the turnover, the longer it takes for a new mood — euphoria or fear — to fully express itself in prices. The very illiquidity of property, often cited as its weakness, is part of what gives the cycle its long wavelength.

There is also a behavioural slow-down worth naming. Property owners are famously reluctant to sell at a loss — a tendency psychologists call *loss aversion* and the *disposition effect* (the urge to hold losers and sell winners). When prices start falling, instead of cutting prices to clear, sellers simply *withdraw*: they take the listing down and wait, hoping the market comes back. Transaction *volume* collapses long before *prices* do. This is why the down-leg, although faster than the up-leg, is still measured in years rather than months — the market grinds lower through a stand-off between stubborn sellers and absent buyers, resolved only slowly as forced sellers (those who *must* sell because of a job loss, a margin call, or a maturing loan they can't refinance) drag the price level down behind them.

This is also the answer to *why land falls at all*, despite the folk belief that "land never goes down." Land's price is capitalized future rent *plus* a speculative premium. In the boom, the speculative premium swells enormously — people pay not for today's rent but for the rent they imagine in a euphoric future. When credit tightens and confidence cracks, that speculative premium collapses, and land can fall hard — US land values fell with the ~27% national house-price drop into 2012, Japanese land lost roughly two-thirds of its value after 1990, and Vietnamese fringe land plots routinely halve in a freeze. Land does fall. It falls when the speculation that inflated it runs out of credit.

## The credit accelerant

Land is the engine; **credit** is the fuel injector. Without borrowed money, the land cycle would still exist but would be far milder. Credit is what turns a slow appreciation into a mania and a correction into a crash. We treat credit and property at length in [the credit cycle and property prices](/blog/trading/real-estate/the-credit-cycle-and-property-prices); here is the part that matters for the *length* of the cycle.

### Property is bought with leverage, and land is the collateral

Almost nobody buys property with cash. A buyer puts down a fraction — say 20–30% — and **borrows the rest** against the property itself. The technical terms: your down payment is **equity**; the loan is **debt**; the ratio of loan to property value is the **loan-to-value (LTV)**; and the property is the **collateral** the bank can seize if you default. We unpack the mechanics in [leverage and the mortgage](/blog/trading/real-estate/leverage-and-the-mortgage-how-debt-amplifies-property).

The critical fact for the cycle is that the collateral *is mostly land*, and land's value rises in the boom. So as land appreciates, the same plot supports a bigger and bigger loan. A property worth ₫5 billion supports (at 70% LTV) a ₫3.5 billion loan; when it rises to ₫10 billion, it suddenly supports a ₫7 billion loan. The rising collateral *creates its own credit*, and that credit chases more land — which is exactly the top of the feedback loop we drew. Credit and land amplify each other on the way up, and they amplify each other on the way down. This two-sided amplification is why the cycle overshoots so far in both directions, and why the down-leg, when it comes, is so fast and brutal.

### Credit makes the cycle longer *and* sharper

You might think more credit would make the cycle *faster* — more fuel, quicker fire. In fact credit tends to make the up-leg *longer* before it makes the down-leg sharper. Here's why. As long as land keeps rising, the collateral keeps growing, the loans keep being serviceable (you can always refinance or sell at a profit), and the banks keep lending. The boom can sustain itself for years on this self-financing logic, far longer than the underlying rent growth would justify, because the loans look safe *as long as prices rise*. The credit machine extends the party. But the same logic means the bust is delayed until something forces credit to stop — and when it stops, *everything* that depended on rising collateral fails at once. Long fuse, big bang.

#### Worked example: buying at the 1990 peak versus the 1995 trough

Let's use Dana, our US investor, to feel the leverage. In 1990, near a US property peak, Dana buys a building for **\$100,000**, putting **\$20,000** down (20% equity) and borrowing **\$80,000**. Then the early-1990s bust hits and the property falls 25% to **\$75,000**.

Dana's equity is the property value minus the loan: \$75,000 − \$80,000 = **−\$5,000**. The price fell 25%, but Dana's *equity* fell by 125% — wiped out and then some. That is leverage in reverse: a modest price drop annihilates a leveraged owner, which is why forced sales cascade in a property bust. Dana is *underwater* (owes more than the property is worth).

Now run the trough. A different buyer — call her Dana's cousin — buys the same building in 1995 at the bottom for **\$75,000**, also 20% down (\$15,000 equity, \$60,000 loan). By the time the next cycle matures and the building is worth **\$130,000**, the cousin's equity is \$130,000 − \$60,000 = **\$70,000** on a \$15,000 stake — nearly a **5×** return on the cash invested, while the property itself rose only ~73%. *Buying near the trough and selling near the peak isn't just better timing — leverage multiplies the gap between the two outcomes into the difference between ruin and a fortune.* And the cycle is what defines where the trough and peak are.

## The anatomy of one cycle: ~14 years up, ~4 years down

Now we can describe the *shape* of a single ~18-year cycle. It is not a symmetric sine wave. It is **lopsided**: a long climb of about 14 years (in two distinct legs) and a short, sharp fall of about 4 years.

![A timeline of one eighteen-year cycle showing recovery, expansion as the first leg up, a mid-cycle dip, a second longer leg up into mania, then a four-year recession](/imgs/blogs/why-real-estate-cycles-are-long-the-18-year-cycle-4.png)

Walk the figure left to right:

- **Recovery (years 0–4).** Prices come off the bottom. Credit thaws, the fear from the last bust slowly fades, and almost no one believes the recovery is real — the previous crash is too fresh. Bargains are everywhere but they are *scary* bargains. This is when the boldest contrarians buy.
- **Expansion — the first leg up (years 4–7).** Prices and rents climb convincingly. Construction cranes reappear, credit gets easy, optimism returns. The recovery is now undeniable and the crowd piles back in.
- **The mid-cycle dip (around year 7).** A real but *shallow* pause — a slowdown, sometimes a mild recession, that makes many people conclude the top has arrived. They sell. They are wrong; the cycle has years left.
- **The second wind / mania — the second leg up (years 8–14).** The longer, stronger, crazier leg. Land speculation takes over from genuine use-demand; credit reaches its loosest; "everyone is a developer." This is where the most spectacular gains *and* the seeds of the bust both live.
- **Recession (years 14–18).** Credit snaps, bids vanish, the backlog of delayed construction floods the market, and forced sales cascade. The fall is fast — about four years — and it sets up the next recovery.

The asymmetry is not an accident. The up-leg is slow because it is *built* — assembled brick by brick over a decade and a half of capitalization and credit growth. The down-leg is fast because it is a *collapse* — the credit and confidence that held the structure up vanish almost at once. Things take years to build and moments to fall.

#### Worked example: the ~14+4 structure applied to dates

Let's anchor the abstract shape to the real timeline. Take the **2008** peak as our reference and count backward and forward.

Counting **back** ~14 years from a top around 2006–2008 lands the *trough* near **1992–1994** — which is roughly when US property bottomed after the early-1990s bust. Inside that up-leg, the mid-cycle dip should fall ~7 years after the trough, around **1999–2001** — and indeed there was a slowdown around the 2001 recession that, in hindsight, was the mid-cycle pause, not the top.

Counting **forward** ~4 years of decline from the 2006 peak lands the *bottom* near **2010–2012** — and US house prices troughed in **February 2012**, about 27% below the 2006 peak. Then count ~14 up again from 2012: the first leg into the late 2010s, a mid-cycle dip around **2017**, a second leg up through the 2020s, and a projected top around **2026–2028**.

The dates don't fall on exact anniversaries — the gaps run 16 to 20 years, not a clean 18 — but the *structure* holds: a long two-legged climb, a dip in the middle, a fast fall, repeat. *The 18-year number is the average of a lopsided shape, not the length of a metronome tick.*

## The mid-cycle dip — why 1999 and 2017 fool people

The mid-cycle dip deserves its own section, because it is the single feature of the cycle that costs people the most money — not by crashing, but by *faking a crash*.

### Two business cycles inside one land cycle

Recall the foundations: the fast business cycle runs ~4–7 years, the slow land cycle ~18. Roughly **two business cycles fit inside one land cycle**. The land cycle climbs through the *first* business-cycle expansion, hits a normal business-cycle recession around the middle — and that recession is the **mid-cycle dip**. Then the land cycle resumes climbing through the *second* business-cycle expansion, all the way to the real land top. The dip is simply the business cycle's ordinary down-phase showing up partway through the land cycle's long up-phase. It is real — unemployment rises, prices wobble, some weak speculators get shaken out — but it is *not* the land top, because the land cycle's own forces (land speculation, peak credit) haven't run their course yet.

### The dips in the record

- **1980** — a sharp recession in the early 1980s, mid-way through the cycle running into the ~1990 peak.
- **1999–2001** — the slowdown around the dot-com bust and the 2001 recession, mid-way through the cycle running into the ~2008 peak. Property barely paused and then ran for seven more years.
- **2017** — a soft patch in housing mid-way through the cycle running (the school argues) into the projected ~2026 peak.

In each case, people who mistook the mid-cycle dip for the top sold years early and watched the biggest leg of the cycle happen without them.

![A matrix showing what leads at each stage of the cycle, with recovery sectors and thawing credit early, a deceptive pause in the middle, and land speculation and mania late before the bust](/imgs/blogs/why-real-estate-cycles-are-long-the-18-year-cycle-6.png)

The matrix above maps what leads at each stage. Early (years 0–6), recovery sectors lead, credit thaws, land is still cheap, and you watch for rising rents and the first cranes. In the middle (years 7–8), there's a scary pause, a brief credit tightening, and the key question is whether credit is *dead* or just *resting* — usually just resting. Late (years 9–18), land and developers lead with the biggest gains *and* the biggest risk, credit is at its loosest, land speculation becomes outright mania, and you watch for tightening credit and record construction starts as the warning of the real top. The middle row is the trap: it looks like the bottom row (a top) but behaves like the top row (more to come).

#### Worked example: the mid-cycle dip catching a seller out

Dana, scarred by the 2008 bust, buys a building in the 2012 trough for **\$200,000**. By 2017 it's worth **\$280,000** — a tidy \$80,000 gain. Then 2017's housing soft patch hits, headlines warn of "another 2008," and Dana, determined not to get caught again, sells at **\$280,000** and sits in cash, convinced the top is in.

But 2017 was the mid-cycle dip, not the top. The building keeps climbing through the second leg and is worth roughly **\$370,000** by the mid-2020s. Dana left **\$90,000** on the table — *more than the entire gain Dana captured* — by mistaking the dip for the peak. The cycle's cruelest trick is not the crash; it's the pause that *looks* like the crash and shakes you out right before the best leg. *Knowing the dip exists is worth as much as knowing the peak exists — maybe more, because the dip is the one that fools the careful.*

## Where are we now: the projected 2026 peak window

So where does the 18-year clock point today, in mid-2026? Honestly, this is the part to hold most loosely — but it's also the part everyone wants, so let's reason it through carefully.

![A graph anchoring on the 2008 bust, projecting a 2026 to 2028 peak window, then branching to the credit, land speculation, and construction signals that either confirm or deny it](/imgs/blogs/why-real-estate-cycles-are-long-the-18-year-cycle-7.png)

The logic in the figure is simple. Anchor on the **2008** bust. Count the cycle forward — recovery and first leg through the 2010s, the **2017** mid-cycle dip, a second leg through the 2020s — and ~18 years from the 2008-era top points to a projected peak **window around 2026–2028**. (You can also count ~14 years up from the 2012 trough, which lands in the same neighbourhood.) That's the calendar prior.

But the calendar is the *weakest* part of the argument. What actually confirms or denies a top is the **signals**, and the figure branches to the three that matter:

- **Credit.** Are loans loosening — LTVs climbing, lending standards relaxing, rates easing into the boom? Late-cycle credit is loose, abundant, and reaching for marginal borrowers.
- **Land speculation.** Are people flipping *bare land*, not buildings? When the action moves from "buy a home to live in" to "buy a plot to flip to the next speculator," you're late in the cycle. This is the most reliable single tell.
- **Construction starts.** Are housing starts and permits hitting cycle records? The peak comes when the years-delayed supply backlog is finally all under construction, about to flood the market.

If all three light up — loose credit, land mania, record starts — the signals *confirm* a late-cycle top. If they don't — credit tight, no land flipping frenzy, restrained building — the signals *deny* it, and there may be more runway. As of 2026, the US picture is mixed: house prices are at record highs (Case-Shiller national ~330, well above the 2006 peak), but mortgage rates are still elevated (~6.5%) rather than easy, which has *restrained* the credit-fuelled blowoff that usually marks a true top. That tension — record prices but not-yet-easy credit — is exactly why the cycle school says "window," not "date."

![A line chart of the US Case-Shiller national home price index showing the 2006 peak at 184, the 2012 trough at 134 down 27 percent, and the 2022 peak at 308, climbing to about 330 by 2026](/imgs/blogs/why-real-estate-cycles-are-long-the-18-year-cycle-3.png)

The chart above is the US price record that anchors the dating. Rebased to 100 in January 2000, the national index peaked at **184** in 2006, fell ~27% to a **134** trough in February 2012 (the four-year bust), then climbed for a decade — past the old peak, accelerating through the pandemic — to **308** by mid-2022 and on toward **330** by early 2026. The 2012 bottom is the trough you count the current cycle from; the long, steep climb since is the up-leg the 18-year model expects to terminate somewhere in the 2026–2028 window. Notice the shape echoes our anatomy: a fast fall (2006→2012), then a long climb with a wobble around 2018–2019 (the mid-cycle area) before the final surge.

#### Worked example: the cost of buying near a projected peak versus waiting

Suppose Minh is deciding whether to buy an investment apartment in 2026, with the cycle school flashing "late-cycle." The flat costs **₫7.0 billion** (≈ \$270,000), and he'd put 30% down (**₫2.1 billion** equity) and borrow **₫4.9 billion**.

*Scenario A — he buys at the top.* If 2026 is near the peak and the next bust takes prices down ~25% (a normal property correction), the flat falls to **₫5.25 billion**. Minh's equity = ₫5.25b − ₫4.9b = **₫0.35 billion** — he's lost ₫1.75 billion of his ₫2.1 billion stake, an **83% loss on his cash**, and he's nearly underwater. He'd have to wait out the full ~4-year down-leg and several recovery years just to get back to even.

*Scenario B — he waits for the trough.* If he holds his ₫2.1 billion and buys the *same* flat after the bust at ₫5.25 billion (30% down = ₫1.575 billion), then rides the next recovery back to ₫7.0 billion and beyond, his equity roughly doubles off a smaller stake while he was never underwater.

The difference between the two isn't a forecast genius — it's just *not buying with maximum leverage right as a long, lopsided cycle is most likely to roll over.* *You don't need to time the peak to the month; you need to not bet the house, leveraged, in the one part of the cycle the model says is most dangerous.* That's the practical, humble use of the 18-year idea: a tilt away from leverage late in the cycle, not a promise about a date.

## Common misconceptions

The 18-year cycle attracts both true believers and outright dismissers, and both miss the point. Here are the beliefs to correct.

### "It's an exact clock — peaks come every 18 years on the dot"

No. The interval is an *average* of a noisy series — the real gaps run roughly 16 to 21 years. Hoyt's Chicago cycles weren't identical lengths; the modern peaks (~1973, ~1990, ~2008) are ~17 and ~18 years apart, not a clean 18 each. Treating "18 years" as a precise countdown is the fastest way to discredit a genuinely useful idea. The cycle is a *tendency* produced by land and credit and the construction lag — and tendencies have variance. Anyone quoting you a peak month is overselling.

### "The cycle is destiny — nothing can stop it"

Also no. The cycle is a product of *specific conditions*: leveraged land speculation, a credit system that lends against rising collateral, and a slow supply response. Change those conditions and you change the cycle. Strong macroprudential rules (caps on LTV, limits on speculative lending), a serious land-value tax (George's prescription, which would drain the speculative premium), or a genuinely elastic housing supply would all dampen or reshape the rhythm. Policy and shocks can stretch a cycle, compress it, or — in extreme cases like Japan — *break* it. The cycle is a strong prior, not a law of physics.

### "Land never falls"

This is the most expensive myth of all, and 2008 alone should have killed it. Land's price is capitalized rent *plus a speculative premium*, and the premium can collapse. US land values fell sharply into 2012; Japanese land lost roughly two-thirds of its peak value through the 1990s and 2000s; Vietnamese fringe *đất nền* routinely halves in a freeze (2011–13, 2022–23). Land falls reliably *at the top of the cycle*, when the speculation that inflated it loses its credit. "Land never falls" is true only over very long horizons and only for genuinely prime locations — and even then, not in nominal terms through a real bust. The belief that land can't fall is itself a *symptom* of the late-cycle mania.

### "The stock market and property move together, so why a separate cycle?"

They're related but run on different clocks. The stock and business cycle turn every 4–7 years; the land cycle turns every ~18. They interact — a property bust *causes* a stock and economic bust (2008), and roughly two business cycles fit inside one land cycle — but they are not the same wave. Confusing them is exactly what produces the mid-cycle-dip error: a normal business-cycle recession partway through the land cycle gets mistaken for the land top. If you only watch the business cycle, you'll call the property top twice as often as you should. Compare with [the business cycle's four phases for traders](/blog/trading/macro-trading/the-business-cycle-four-phases-for-traders) to see the faster wheel running inside the slower one.

### "If the cycle is so well-known, it must already be priced in and therefore gone"

This is the sophisticated objection, and it's partly right and partly wrong. If everyone truly traded on the cycle, it would self-destruct. But two things keep it alive. First, the cycle is driven by *credit and collateral mechanics* that operate regardless of belief — banks lend more against rising land whether or not anyone has read Hoyt. Second, human memory is short and greed is durable; each generation rediscovers "this time is different" near the top. A pattern rooted in institutional incentives (banks lend against collateral) and human nature (we extrapolate booms) is remarkably hard to arbitrage away. That said — it's weaker and noisier than the believers claim, which is why we keep calling it a *prior*.

## How it shows up in real markets

The mechanism is universal; the local flavour varies enormously. Here are three contrasting cases — the textbook US cycle, Vietnam's compressed version, and Japan's *broken* cycle — that together show what the model gets right and where it bends.

### The United States: the clean 1990 → 2008 → 2026 sequence

The US is the cleanest modern illustration because its data is deep and its property market is heavily credit-driven. The ~1990 peak (late-1980s commercial-property and S&L boom, then the early-1990s bust) gave way to a long recovery; the mid-cycle dip around the 2001 recession scared people but property ran on; the cycle blew off in the 2004–2006 housing-and-subprime mania, peaking in 2006 with the Case-Shiller national index at 184. The bust ran almost exactly four years to the February-2012 trough at 134 (−27%), precisely as Foldvary and Harrison had pointed to a decade earlier. Then the textbook repeated: recovery through the 2010s, a 2017–2019 mid-cycle soft patch, a furious second leg (turbocharged by pandemic-era cheap money) to a 308 peak in 2022 and ~330 by 2026 — putting the projected next top in the 2026–2028 window. The American cycle is the one the whole theory is calibrated to, and it has, so far, kept time reasonably well.

### Vietnam: the compressed, policy-driven cycle

Vietnam shows the same *forces* — land, credit, speculation, construction lag — but on a faster, jumpier clock, because the economy is young, urbanizing rapidly, and far more sensitive to policy and credit swings. Vietnam doesn't run a clean 18-year wave; it runs a series of sharper, shorter booms and freezes driven by the credit tap:

- **2007–2008 post-WTO mania.** Joining the WTO in 2007 unleashed capital and optimism; the VN-Index hit ~1,170 in March 2007 and *đất nền* speculation ran wild — Minh's uncle's moment.
- **2011–2013 freeze.** Inflation spiked to ~18% in 2011, credit slammed shut, projects stalled nationwide, and the government set up the VAMC (a bad-debt agency) in 2013 to clean up the wreckage. Fringe land plots halved.
- **2018 *đất nền* fever** in HCMC's satellite provinces — a credit-fuelled land-flipping frenzy, the same "speculate on bare land" signal that marks a late stage.
- **2022–2023 bond crunch.** The arrests at Tân Hoàng Minh (April 2022) and Vạn Thịnh Phát / SCB (October 2022) froze the corporate-bond market that developers depended on. Land and apartment markets seized; the SBV cut policy rates four times in 2023 and Decree 08 gave bond-restructuring relief to thaw it.

Notice the pattern: each Vietnamese boom ends not with a slow 18-year roll-over but with a *credit event* — an inflation spike, a bond freeze, an arrest. In a young, credit-sensitive market, the credit accelerant dominates and *compresses* the cycle into sharper, shorter swings. The land-and-credit engine is the same; the gearing is faster. For a Vietnamese reader, the practical lesson isn't "watch for 2026" — it's "watch the credit tap and the *đất nền* flipping, because those are what turn your local cycle."

### Japan: the cycle that broke

Japan is the essential counter-case, because it proves the cycle is a *prior, not a law*. Through the 1980s, Japan ran the most extreme land-and-credit boom in modern history — at the 1990 peak, the land under the Imperial Palace was claimed to be worth more than all of California, and Japanese land in aggregate was theoretically worth more than all the land in the United States. Then it burst. But instead of a four-year bust and an 18-year recovery, Japan got *decades* of grinding decline — land prices fell for roughly 15 *consecutive years*, losing around two-thirds of their value, and never returned to the 1990 peak even a generation later. The normal cycle's recovery leg simply didn't fire.

Why? Because the conditions that drive the cycle were broken. The banking system was so damaged it couldn't extend new credit (the collateral channel seized); demographics turned (a shrinking, aging population means *falling* demand for land, removing the long-run tailwind); and policy responses were slow and half-hearted. With the credit accelerant dead and the demographic engine in reverse, the self-reinforcing loop couldn't restart. Japan is what happens when you knock out the credit leg *and* the demand leg at once: the 18-year rhythm doesn't just stretch, it *breaks*. That's the humility the model demands — change the underlying conditions enough, and the pattern dissolves.

### What the three cases teach together

The US shows the model working as advertised. Vietnam shows the same forces *compressed* by a young, credit-driven economy into sharper swings. Japan shows the model *broken* when credit and demographics both fail. The common thread is the **mechanism** — land plus credit plus the construction lag — not the magic number 18. Where those forces operate normally, you get something like an 18-year cycle. Where they're amplified, you get faster cycles. Where they're disabled, you get no cycle at all, just a long decline. The lesson is to watch the *forces*, and treat the number as their typical, not guaranteed, output.

## When this matters / Further reading

The 18-year land cycle matters to you in exactly one practical way, and it is not "buy in year 4, sell in year 14." It is this: **late in a long property cycle, when credit is loose and people are flipping bare land, leverage is at its most dangerous — and that is precisely when it feels safest.** The cycle's deepest lesson is about *position sizing and timing relative to credit*, not about a calendar. Knowing the cycle exists is what stops you from betting the house, fully leveraged, into the one part of the rhythm most likely to roll over — and what stops you from selling at the mid-cycle dip and missing the best leg.

Hold the whole idea at the right altitude. It is a **weak prior**: a tilt that says "be more cautious about leverage late in the cycle, more willing to buy after a bust, and deeply suspicious of the mid-cycle dip that masquerades as a top." It is not a clock, not destiny, and not a substitute for understanding the property in front of you — its location, its rent, its land share, its financing. The number 18 is the *output* of land plus credit plus the construction lag; when those forces change, so does the number, as Japan proves.
