> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — You cannot time the exact top of a property market, but you *can* read the phase, because a dashboard of leading indicators turns weeks to months before price itself confirms the cycle.
>
> - Price is a *lagging* indicator. By the time the headline price index falls, the market actually turned six to eighteen months earlier — you just could not see it in the one number everyone watches.
> - The early signals live in *volume* and *inventory*, not price: mortgage applications and sales volume soften first, then inventory builds (months of supply rises), then homes sit longer (days on market lengthens), then valuation (price-to-rent) peaks — and only *then* does price fall.
> - The key insight is the **order**. Indicators turn in a reliable sequence, so you watch the relay race, not the finish line. A market where volume is down 20% while price is still up 5% is not strong — it is rolling over, and the price just has not caught up yet.
> - Build a scorecard, not a single number. The one fact to remember: when months of supply crosses from under four (a seller's market) toward nine or more (a glutted buyer's market), prices follow — and that gauge moves long before the price does.

In the spring of 2006, the headlines about American housing were still triumphant. The S&P/Case-Shiller national home-price index would not print its all-time peak until July of that year, and on the surface everything looked like a market at the top of its game: prices up double digits year on year, builders breaking ground, buyers stretching to get in before they were "priced out forever." If you watched only the price, you would have concluded the boom had years left to run.

But underneath, the gauges had already turned. Mortgage-application volume — the count of people actually applying for loans to buy a home — had rolled over in 2005. New-home sales had peaked. The number of unsold homes sitting on the market was climbing fast, and the time it took to sell a house was stretching from a few frantic weeks to a few patient months. None of these showed up in the price yet. The price was the *last* thing to move. A patient observer with a dashboard saw the rollover roughly a year before the crash made the front page; a buyer watching the price alone got the news at the worst possible time.

Now fast-forward to Ho Chi Minh City. **Minh**, a marketing manager we will follow throughout this post, is trying to decide whether to buy an apartment in 2026. The price chart for HCMC primary apartments looks relentless — average primary prices ran from about 91 million VND per square metre in 2024 to roughly 111 million in 2025 (about \$4,222 per square metre), a 33% jump in a single year. If Minh watches only that line, he sees an unstoppable freight train and feels the same pressure the Phoenix buyer felt in 2006: get in now or be left behind. But Minh has read this post. So instead of watching the price, he watches the *other* gauges — how fast new launches are selling, how many months of unsold inventory are stacking up, how stretched the price-to-income ratio has become, whether bank credit to property is accelerating or cooling. Those gauges tell him something the price cannot: which *phase* the market is in, and roughly how much room is left.

The diagram above is the mental model for the whole post: a dashboard of six leading gauges — months of supply, days on market, price-to-rent, mortgage applications, construction starts and permits, and credit growth — each with a clear bullish and bearish direction, read *together*. No single one of them times the top. But read as a panel, they tell you whether a market is heating up, peaking, or rolling over, and they do it weeks to months before the price confirms it. This article builds that dashboard from zero. We define every gauge from scratch, show exactly why price lags, walk through the *order* in which the indicators turn near a top, build a real-time scorecard you can actually run, correct the misconceptions that trap most buyers, and then test the whole framework against the US 2005–06 rollover and Vietnam's signals into 2022. We keep **Minh** in HCMC and **Dana**, a US investor, as running examples — in ₫ and \$ side by side — so a Vietnamese and an international reader both stay oriented.

## Foundations: leading, coincident, and lagging indicators, and why price lags

Before any of the gauges make sense, we need a single organizing idea borrowed from economics: indicators come in three flavours, sorted by *when they move relative to the thing you actually care about*.

A **leading indicator** moves *before* the outcome. It is an early warning — a gauge that turns up or down ahead of the variable you are trying to predict. In housing, the variable we care about is the price level, and the leading indicators are the things that move first: mortgage applications, sales volume, new listings, days on market, building permits. They are "leading" because they sit *upstream* of price in the chain of cause and effect — they are about demand and supply *flow*, and flow changes before the *price* it eventually sets.

A **coincident indicator** moves *at the same time* as the outcome. It confirms what is happening right now but gives you no head start. Sales volume in the current month, the current month's absorption — these tell you the state of the market today, not where it is going.

A **lagging indicator** moves *after* the outcome — it confirms a turn that already happened. In housing, the single most-watched number, the **price index**, is a lagging indicator. This is the claim most people find hardest to accept, so let us be precise about *why* price lags. There are three concrete reasons.

First, **price is the result of a negotiation, and negotiations are sticky.** When demand softens, sellers do not immediately cut their asking prices. They are anchored to what their neighbour got six months ago, to what they "need" to clear their mortgage, to the price they have in their head. So when buyers thin out, the first thing that happens is not a price cut — it is that homes stop selling. Volume falls and inventory builds while prices stay frozen, sometimes for many months. Price is the *last* variable to give way because human beings resist marking down their largest asset.

Second, **price indices are built from closed transactions, which are old news.** A reported "May" home-price index is not measuring decisions made in May. It is measuring sales that *closed* in May — deals that were negotiated in March, that began as showings in February, that began as a mortgage application in January. The very construction of a price index bakes in a delay of two to four months, sometimes more, before a behavioural change shows up in the headline number. The Case-Shiller index, the US gold standard, uses a three-month moving average and is published with roughly a two-month lag, so the number you read in July reflects a market centred on roughly April.

Third, **mix and survivorship distort the price during a turn.** When a market starts to cool, the cheaper, harder-to-sell homes are the first to sit unsold, so the homes that *do* close skew toward the better, more desirable properties. That "mix shift" can actually push the *average* price up for a while even as the underlying market weakens — the average sale price rises not because homes got more valuable but because only the good ones are selling. Price can lie to you exactly when you most need the truth.

Put those three together and you get the core thesis of this entire post: **the price is the last domino to fall.** If you want to read the cycle in real time, you have to watch the dominoes *upstream* of price — the volume, the inventory, the days on market, the valuation ratios — because those move first, and they move while the price is still pointing the wrong way.

A few more terms we will lean on throughout. **Sales volume** is simply the *count* of transactions in a period — how many homes actually sold this month, regardless of price. It is one of the purest leading signals, because buyers vote with their feet before sellers move their prices. **Inventory** (or "active listings") is the count of homes currently for sale. **A seller's market** is one where buyers outnumber the available homes, so sellers have pricing power and prices rise; **a buyer's market** is the reverse, where homes outnumber willing buyers, so buyers have pricing power and prices fall. The whole dashboard is, in a sense, just a set of instruments for measuring *which side of that balance the market is on right now* — and, crucially, which way it is *tipping*.

One honest caveat before we go deeper, because finance posts earn trust by naming limits. None of this is a crystal ball. Leading indicators tell you the *phase* and the *direction*, not the date and not the magnitude. They will tell you a market is rolling over; they will not tell you it peaks in March rather than September, or falls 8% rather than 30%. The goal is not to pinpoint the top — that is a fool's errand we will dismantle in the misconceptions section — but to *know which phase you are standing in*, which is a far more achievable and far more useful thing. With that framing set, let us build the gauges, one family at a time.

## The inventory family: months of supply, days on market, and absorption

The first and most important family of leading indicators measures one thing: **how fast the market is clearing its inventory.** When homes sell faster than new ones are listed, inventory shrinks, sellers gain power, and prices rise. When homes sell slower than new ones are listed, inventory builds, buyers gain power, and prices fall. Three gauges measure this from slightly different angles.

### Months of supply (the inventory clock)

**Months of supply** is the single most useful number on the whole dashboard, and it is beautifully simple. It asks: *at the current pace of sales, how many months would it take to sell every home currently for sale, if no new homes were listed?* The formula is just:

$$\text{Months of supply} = \frac{\text{Active listings (homes for sale)}}{\text{Homes sold per month}}$$

If a city has 12,000 homes listed for sale and is selling 4,000 a month, it has 12,000 / 4,000 = **3 months of supply**. The convention, refined over decades of US data, reads roughly like this: **under about 4 months is a seller's market** (tight, prices rising), **4 to 6 months is balanced**, and **over about 6 months is a buyer's market** (loose, prices flat-to-falling), with anything **over 9 or 10 months signalling a genuine glut** where prices fall outright. The exact thresholds vary by country and city, but the *direction* is universal: rising months of supply is bearish, falling is bullish.

Why is this a *leading* indicator? Because both of its ingredients move before price does. When demand softens, the denominator (homes sold per month) falls first — buyers step back. At the same time, the numerator (active listings) rises, because homes that would have sold are now sitting. So the *ratio* jumps for two reasons at once, and it jumps while sellers are still holding their asking prices firm. Months of supply is the canary; the price is the miner who notices the gas much later.

#### Worked example: computing months of supply in Minh's district

Minh is watching one district in HCMC. He pulls the numbers from a property portal and the local brokerage reports. At the start of the year, the district has **3,000 apartments listed** for sale and is closing about **1,000 sales per month**. Months of supply = 3,000 / 1,000 = **3.0 months** — a tight seller's market, consistent with the 33% price jump he sees in the data.

Six months later, Minh runs the same calculation. Sales have cooled: only **600 apartments are closing per month** now, because buyers got nervous and credit got more expensive. Meanwhile listings have climbed to **4,200**, as more sellers try to cash out near the top. Now months of supply = 4,200 / 600 = **7.0 months**. The price index has barely moved — it is still printing year-on-year gains — but the inventory clock has more than doubled, from 3.0 to 7.0 months. Minh does not need the price to fall to know the balance has tipped: the *clearing speed* has collapsed.

*The price was still smiling, but the inventory clock had already crossed from seller's market into buyer's market — and that crossing, not the price, is the signal.*

### Days on market (how long a home sits)

**Days on market (DOM)** is the median number of days a home is listed before it goes under contract. It measures the same underlying force as months of supply — clearing speed — but from the individual home's point of view rather than the whole market's. In a hot market, a desirable home gets multiple offers in a weekend and DOM is a handful of days. As the market cools, the same home sits a week, then a month, then a season, and the median DOM climbs from say 15 days to 60 to 120.

DOM is a wonderfully *human* indicator because it captures the psychology of the turn. In a frenzy, buyers fear missing out and bid instantly; as confidence drains, they wait, lowball, and shop around. The lengthening of DOM is the visible footprint of that drained confidence — and it shows up well before sellers capitulate on price. A close cousin, the **sale-to-list ratio** (the price a home actually sold for divided by its asking price), tells the same story from the price side: above 100% means buyers are bidding *over* ask (red-hot); falling below 100% and dropping means sellers are accepting discounts (cooling). When you see DOM lengthening *and* the sale-to-list ratio slipping below 1.0 *and* "price reduced" tags multiplying on listings, you are watching a market roll over in slow motion, months before the index reflects it.

### Absorption rate (the percentage cleared)

The **absorption rate** is months of supply turned inside out — it expresses clearing speed as a *percentage of inventory sold per month* rather than as a count of months. The formula:

$$\text{Absorption rate} = \frac{\text{Homes sold per month}}{\text{Active listings}} \times 100\%$$

It is literally the reciprocal of months of supply (times 100). If a market sells 1,000 of its 4,000 listings in a month, the absorption rate is 1,000 / 4,000 = **25% per month**, which is the same as 4 months of supply. A high absorption rate (say 30%+ per month) means inventory is flying off the shelves — a hot market. A low absorption rate (say under 10%, i.e. over 10 months of supply) means inventory is barely moving — a cold one. In Vietnam's primary (new-build) market, analysts and developers usually quote *absorption* rather than months of supply, because they care about how quickly a *specific launch* sells out: "the project achieved 70% absorption at launch" means 70% of the units sold in the launch window, a sign of strong demand.

#### Worked example: absorption rate on a new HCMC launch

A developer launches **500 apartments** in a new HCMC tower. In the first month, **350 units** sell. The launch-month absorption rate is 350 / 500 = **70%** — strong, the kind of number you see late in a boom when buyers are confident and credit is easy. The developer crows about it in the press, and the price line keeps climbing.

A year later, the same developer launches a second tower, also **500 apartments**, at a price about 20% higher (because, after all, the market "only goes up"). This time only **120 units** sell in the first month. Absorption is 120 / 500 = **24%**. Nothing about the price has fallen — in fact the asking price is *higher* than last year. But the *demand* behind that price has thinned dramatically: absorption collapsed from 70% to 24%. The developer will quietly extend the launch, add incentives (a free year of management fees, a furniture package, a discount for early payment), and avoid cutting the headline price for as long as possible — which is exactly why the price index will be the last thing to show what already happened in the absorption number.

*Absorption is the demand showing its hand: when the same launch sells a third as fast at a higher price, the market has rolled over even though the price has not.*

![A before-and-after comparison of months of supply, contrasting a tight seller's market under three months with rising prices against a glutted buyer's market over nine months with falling prices](/imgs/blogs/leading-indicators-reading-the-cycle-in-real-time-3.png)

The figure above puts the two regimes side by side. On the left, the seller's market: months of supply at or below three, homes selling in one to three weeks, bidding wars and no price cuts, prices rising. On the right, the buyer's market: months of supply at nine or more, homes sitting three to six months, price cuts everywhere, prices falling. The crucial thing to internalize is that a market *travels from left to right through the middle* — and you can watch it cross the balanced zone (four to six months) in real time, long before the price on the right-hand panel actually turns down. The inventory family is your early-warning radar.

## The valuation family: price-to-rent and price-to-income

The inventory family tells you about *clearing speed* — the short-term balance of buyers and sellers. The valuation family tells you something different and slower: *how stretched the price is relative to the fundamentals that should anchor it.* These ratios will not flip from week to week. Their job is to tell you whether the market is cheap, fair, or dangerously expensive — which determines how much *fuel* a downturn has if one arrives.

### Price-to-rent (the buy-versus-rent ratio)

**Price-to-rent** is the price of a home divided by the *annual rent* that same home would fetch. It is the property world's version of a price-to-earnings (P/E) ratio for stocks: it tells you how many years of rent you are paying for the asset.

$$\text{Price-to-rent} = \frac{\text{Home price}}{\text{Annual rent}}$$

If an apartment costs \$300,000 and rents for \$1,500 a month, its annual rent is \$18,000, and its price-to-rent ratio is 300,000 / 18,000 = **16.7**. A low ratio (say 12–15) means buying is cheap relative to renting — the asset is reasonably priced against the income it produces. A high ratio (say 25–30+) means buying is expensive relative to renting — you are paying a lot of price for a little rent, which is the textbook signature of a speculative market where people are buying for *price appreciation*, not for the *yield*.

Why does price-to-rent lead? Because **rent is sticky and fundamental, while price is fluid and speculative.** Rent is set by what tenants can actually pay out of their incomes month to month — it grows roughly with wages and inflation, slowly and steadily. Price, by contrast, can detach from rent during a boom, because buyers chase capital gains and credit lets them bid prices up far faster than rents can follow. So in a boom, price-to-rent *expands* — price races ahead of rent. And here is the leading-indicator magic: that expansion has a ceiling, because at some point the gap between what it costs to own and what it costs to rent gets so wide that renting becomes obviously cheaper, demand to *buy* dries up, and the ratio rolls over. Price-to-rent often peaks *before* the price index, because the ratio captures the *exhaustion of the buy-side demand* that the price has not yet reflected. (We unpack the full buy-versus-rent calculation in [the rent-versus-buy real math post](/blog/trading/real-estate/rent-vs-buy-the-real-math); here we use price-to-rent purely as a *cycle gauge*.)

![A line chart showing the price-to-rent ratio rising through a boom to a peak around twenty-five and rolling over about a year before the home-price index peaks, with the divergence window highlighted](/imgs/blogs/leading-indicators-reading-the-cycle-in-real-time-4.png)

The chart above traces a stylized full cycle (illustrative, modelled on the US 2000s). The blue line is the price-to-rent ratio; the dashed grey line is the home-price index. Notice the green band — the long-run "normal" range of roughly 15 to 18 — and notice how far the ratio overshot it during the boom, peaking around 25 in 2005. Then notice the timing: the price-to-rent ratio *peaked and began falling in 2005, while the price index kept climbing into 2006.* That gap — the amber "divergence" window — is the leading signal. When valuation rolls over while price is still rising, the buy-side demand has been exhausted and the price is running on momentum and fumes. It will follow the valuation down; it just has not yet.

#### Worked example: reading a stretched price-to-rent against its own history

Dana, our US investor, is looking at a metro where homes are selling for **\$400,000** and renting for **\$1,600 a month**, i.e. **\$19,200 a year**. Price-to-rent = 400,000 / 19,200 = **20.8**. Is that high? The number alone is meaningless — *you must compare it to the market's own history.* Dana pulls fifteen years of data and finds this metro's price-to-rent has averaged about **16**, ranged from a low of **13** (after the last bust) to a previous peak of **22** (at the last top). So 20.8 is in the top decile of its own history — within shouting distance of the previous bubble peak. That is the signal: not "20.8 is a bad number" in the abstract, but "20.8 is 30% above this market's own long-run norm and approaching its prior peak."

To sanity-check, Dana computes the *gross rental yield*, which is just price-to-rent flipped over: annual rent / price = 19,200 / 400,000 = **4.8%**. A 4.8% gross yield (before any costs) is thin — once you subtract property tax, insurance, maintenance, and vacancy, the *net* yield might be 2.5–3%, barely above a risk-free bond. When the yield gets that thin, buyers are clearly paying for *appreciation*, not income — and a market that depends on appreciation to justify its prices is, by definition, late in the cycle. (We build the income side of this — net operating income and cap rates — in [the cap-rate and income-approach post](/blog/trading/real-estate/cap-rate-noi-and-the-income-approach); the cap rate is essentially the *net* version of this same yield.)

*A price-to-rent ratio means nothing as a bare number; it screams only when you set it against the market's own fifteen-year history and find it near the top of its range.*

### Price-to-income (the affordability ceiling)

**Price-to-income** is the price of a typical home divided by typical *annual household income*. It tells you how many years of a household's entire pre-tax income it would take to buy a home outright — a raw measure of *affordability*, and ultimately of how much further prices can stretch before they run out of buyers who can possibly pay.

$$\text{Price-to-income} = \frac{\text{Home price}}{\text{Annual household income}}$$

This is where Vietnam screams. Using the affordability data as of mid-2026, HCMC's price-to-income ratio sits around **32** — meaning it takes roughly thirty-two years of an entire median household's income to buy a standard apartment. (VnExpress, citing Numbeo, put it at "34 years' income," the fifth-highest in the world.) Hanoi is around **31**. For comparison: Hong Kong leads the world at about **44**, Beijing **35**, then HCMC and Hanoi, then Mumbai **28**, Seoul **26**, Singapore **17**, Tokyo **15**, London **12**, New York **10** — against a *global median of roughly 15*. By this gauge, Vietnamese cities are among the least affordable on Earth relative to local incomes — more stretched than Tokyo, London, or New York, in cities where incomes are a fraction of those places'.

Price-to-income is a *slow* leading indicator — it does not predict next quarter — but it is a powerful one for a different reason: it sets a *ceiling* on how far a price-led boom can run before it has to either (a) wait years for incomes to catch up, (b) keep going only by piling on ever more leverage and longer loan terms, or (c) break. A market at 10× income (New York) has room to rise; a market at 32× (HCMC) is testing the outer limit of what local buyers can finance, which means its boom increasingly depends on credit, speculation, and foreign or investor demand rather than ordinary owner-occupiers. When affordability is *this* exhausted, the market is fragile to anything that removes the marginal buyer — a rate hike, a credit tightening, a confidence shock.

#### Worked example: how stretched is HCMC's price-to-income, concretely?

Let us make the abstraction concrete with Minh. A modest **70 m² Hanoi-style flat** runs about **₫7.0 billion** at 2025 prices (about **\$270,000**). Suppose Minh's household earns a comfortable-by-local-standards **₫50 million a month**, which is **₫600 million a year** (about **\$23,000**). His personal price-to-income is 7,000 / 600 = **11.7 years of his entire pre-tax income** — and Minh is *well above* the median earner. For a *median* HCMC household earning far less, the ratio balloons to the ~32 the city-wide statistic reports. Now ask the practical question: if Minh devotes a aggressive 40% of income to housing, that is **₫240 million a year**, or **₫20 million a month**, toward a flat that costs ₫7.0 billion. Even ignoring interest, principal alone would take 7,000 / 240 ≈ **29 years** to repay — and with interest at a post-teaser **12–14%**, the math becomes brutal.

The point is not that no one can buy — clearly people do, through family help, investor demand, and long mortgages. The point is what the *ceiling* tells you about the cycle: at 32× income, the pool of buyers who can pay *higher* prices out of *income* is nearly empty, so further gains must come from credit and speculation. That makes the market a *late-cycle* market by the valuation gauge — stretched, dependent on financing, and fragile to a credit or rate shock. (This is exactly the mechanism in [the credit-cycle and property-prices post](/blog/trading/real-estate/anatomy-of-a-real-estate-cycle-four-phases) — when affordability is exhausted, credit becomes the only fuel left, and credit is the most fickle fuel of all.)

*Price-to-income does not call the top, but it tells you how little headroom is left: a market at 32× income has run out of buyers who can pay more from their paychecks, so everything from here depends on credit.*

## The demand family: mortgage applications, traffic, and auction clearance

If the inventory family is the radar and the valuation family is the fuel gauge, the demand family is the *leading edge of the leading edge* — the indicators that turn *first of all*, because they measure intent before it becomes a transaction. A sale is the *end* of a process that begins weeks earlier with someone deciding to look, getting pre-approved, and applying for a loan. Watch the *start* of that process and you get the earliest warning of all.

### Mortgage applications (intent to buy, measured directly)

A **mortgage application** is exactly what it sounds like: a household formally applying to a bank for a home loan. In the US, the Mortgage Bankers Association publishes a weekly index of *purchase* mortgage applications (separated from refinancing applications, which are about rates, not buying). This is one of the cleanest leading indicators that exists, because applying for a purchase mortgage is an unambiguous statement of intent to buy *within the next month or two* — it sits roughly 30–60 days *upstream* of a closed sale, which itself sits two to four months upstream of the price index. So the mortgage-application index leads the price index by something like *six months or more*. When purchase applications roll over, you are watching tomorrow's buyers walk away today.

In 2005, US purchase-mortgage applications peaked and began falling — a full year before the price index topped in mid-2006. The intent to buy evaporated first; the closings followed; the inventory built; and only at the very end did the price give way. Anyone watching the application index had the rollover in hand twelve months early.

In Vietnam, there is no clean weekly mortgage-application index, but you can read the same force through proxies: **bank credit growth to the real-estate sector** (how fast property lending is expanding — accelerating is bullish demand, decelerating is bearish), **disbursement of presale instalments** (whether buyers are keeping up their staged payments on under-construction units), and the **teaser-rate dynamics** that drive Vietnamese demand. Recall that Vietnamese home loans often start with a preferential teaser around **8%** for 12–24 months, then *reset* to a floating **12–14%**. When teasers are cheap and plentiful, demand surges; when banks pull the teasers or the resets bite, the marginal buyer disappears. Watching whether banks are *offering* aggressive teasers is, in Vietnam, the closest equivalent to watching the US application index.

### Traffic and search (the very earliest funnel)

Even *earlier* than a mortgage application is plain *interest*: the foot traffic at open houses and sales galleries, the volume of online property searches, the number of inquiries a listing gets. These are noisy and hard to measure precisely, but at the market level they are real leading signals. When sales-gallery traffic in HCMC thins out — when the launch event that drew a thousand people last year draws three hundred this year — that is demand softening at the *very* top of the funnel, weeks before it shows up as slower absorption and months before it shows up in price. Developers watch this obsessively (it is why they track gallery footfall and reservation deposits) precisely because it is the earliest read on the cycle they have.

### Auction clearance rate (the real-time auctioneer's thermometer)

In auction-driven markets — Australia is the classic example, and parts of the UK and Hong Kong — the **auction clearance rate** is one of the best real-time leading indicators in all of real estate. It is the percentage of homes offered at auction in a given week that actually *sell* at auction (rather than being passed in / withdrawn for lack of a high-enough bid):

$$\text{Auction clearance rate} = \frac{\text{Homes sold at auction}}{\text{Homes offered at auction}} \times 100\%$$

A clearance rate above about **70%** signals a hot, competitive market with prices rising; a rate dropping toward **50% or below** signals a cooling market where sellers are not getting their reserve prices and buyers are stepping back. Because auctions happen *weekly* and report *immediately*, the clearance rate is almost a real-time pulse — it leads the official price indices (which are monthly and lagged) by weeks to months. Sydney's clearance rate famously rolls over months before the formal price index confirms a downturn. Vietnam's market is not auction-driven in this way, so the clearance rate is less directly applicable, but the *principle* — measure the percentage of offered stock that finds a buyer at an acceptable price, in real time — translates to absorption on new launches.

#### Worked example: the lead-lag in numbers (volume down 20%, price still up)

This is the most important worked example in the post, because it captures the entire thesis in a single snapshot. Dana is tracking a US metro. Here is the data as of this month, each compared to a year ago:

- **Purchase mortgage applications: down 20% year on year.**
- **Sales volume (closed transactions): down 12% year on year.**
- **Active inventory: up 35% year on year.**
- **Months of supply: up from 3.2 to 5.8.**
- **Median days on market: up from 18 days to 52.**
- **Price index: still up +5% year on year.**

A naive observer reads the last line and concludes: "Prices are up 5% — the market is strong." That is exactly backwards. *Five of the six gauges have rolled over hard.* Applications, the earliest signal, are down 20%; volume is down; inventory is up a third; the inventory clock has nearly doubled; homes are sitting three times as long. The only gauge still positive is the *one that lags* — the price — and it is positive precisely because it is the *last* to move. This market is not strong; it is rolling over in plain sight, and the +5% price is a *trailing echo* of a boom that has already ended in the demand and inventory data. A buyer who waits for the price to confirm the turn will buy near the top; a buyer reading the dashboard sells or waits.

*When volume is down 20% but price is still up 5%, the market is not strong — the price is simply the last gauge to get the memo, and it always is.*

## The supply family: starts, permits, and completions

So far every gauge has been about *demand* — how fast homes are clearing, how stretched valuations are, how many buyers are showing up. The last family is about *supply*: how much *new* property is being created, which determines whether tomorrow's market is starved or glutted. Supply indicators are leading in a peculiar, important way — they lead the *glut*, because of the long lag between deciding to build and actually delivering.

There are three distinct stages in the supply pipeline, and the order matters:

A **building permit** is government authorization to construct — the *earliest* formal supply signal. A developer pulls a permit *before* breaking ground, so permits lead actual building by months. A surge in permits is a leading indicator of a *future* surge in supply. A **housing start** is the moment construction physically *begins* — the foundation is poured. Starts lag permits by a few months and lead completions by the full construction period. A **completion** is when the home is *finished* and ready to occupy or sell. Completions are the supply that actually hits the market — and they *lag* the original decision to build by the entire construction lag, which for apartment towers is typically **two to four years**.

That long lag is the heart of why property cycles overshoot, and we devote a whole post to it — [the anatomy of a real-estate cycle and its four phases](/blog/trading/real-estate/anatomy-of-a-real-estate-cycle-four-phases) — but the leading-indicator lesson here is sharp: **a surge in permits and starts during a boom is bearish for the *future*, not bullish for the present.** When you see permits and starts spiking — cranes everywhere, every developer breaking ground at once — you are watching the *seeds of the next glut* being planted. Those projects will all complete two to four years later, often *after* demand has already turned, dumping a wall of supply onto a cooling market. The construction crane is a beautiful, terrible leading indicator: a forest of cranes at the top of a boom is the surest sign that hypersupply is coming.

This is exactly the pattern that destroyed so many markets. In the US, housing starts ran far above the long-run replacement rate from 2003 to 2006 — the country was building far more homes than household formation justified — and that overhang of supply, completing into a market where demand had already cracked, deepened the 2008 bust. In Vietnam, the developer building boom of 2019–2021, funded by the corporate-bond market, planted exactly this kind of overhang: a wall of projects that were still completing (or stranded half-built) when the 2022 bond freeze cut off the credit that funded them.

The practical rule: track permits and starts as a *forward supply pipeline.* If starts are running well above the rate of new household formation (the number of new households being created each year, which is the *true* underlying demand for housing units), supply is being overbuilt and a future glut is loading. If starts are *below* household formation — as they were in the US for years after 2008, creating a genuine housing shortage — supply is tight and prices have a structural floor. Supply tells you about the *medium-term* balance the way valuation tells you about the *stretch*: neither times next month, but both tell you how much trouble is loading in the pipe.

#### Worked example: reading the supply pipeline against household formation

Dana wants to know if her metro is overbuilding. The metro forms about **20,000 new households a year** — that is the underlying annual demand for net new housing units (from population growth, immigration, young people moving out, divorces creating two homes from one). She checks the permit data: developers pulled permits for **34,000 units** last year, and **31,000 starts** broke ground. So the metro is *starting* roughly 31,000 units a year against demand for 20,000 — building at **155% of underlying demand.** That 11,000-unit-a-year surplus will complete in two to three years, into a market that needs far fewer. Unless demand accelerates dramatically (it rarely does at the top of a cycle), Dana is watching a glut load into the pipe. The price is still rising today; the supply gauge is telling her that the *future* price has a problem.

Contrast a different metro that forms 20,000 households a year but is only *starting* 12,000 units — building at **60% of demand.** That market is *under*-supplied; even if demand softens, the structural shortage puts a floor under prices. Same price chart today, opposite future, and only the supply gauge tells them apart.

*A forest of cranes at the top of a boom is not strength — it is the next glut being poured, and the supply pipeline lets you see it two to four years before it lands.*

## The order indicators turn: the sequence is the signal

We have now built every gauge. Here is the single most valuable idea in the entire post, the one that turns a pile of indicators into a *system*: **the indicators turn in a reliable order.** You do not watch them as a static snapshot; you watch them as a *relay race*, and the position of the baton tells you exactly where in the cycle you stand. The timeline figure above this section lays out the sequence; let us walk it.

**Step one — demand softens (month zero).** The very first thing to move is *intent*. Mortgage purchase applications roll over. Sales-gallery traffic thins. Online search interest fades. Then *sales volume* — the count of closed deals — starts to fall, because fewer applications today means fewer closings next month. Crucially, at this stage *price is still rising.* The headline number looks great; only the demand gauges have turned. This is the moment of maximum disagreement between the price (still bullish) and the leading indicators (already bearish), and it is the moment a dashboard-reader gains their entire edge.

**Step two — inventory builds (one to three months later).** With fewer buyers, homes that would have sold now sit. Active listings climb (more sellers trying to cash out near the perceived top, fewer buyers absorbing them). Months of supply rises — both because the numerator (listings) grows and the denominator (sales) shrinks. The absorption rate falls. Price is *still* firm here, sometimes still ticking up on mix effects. The inventory clock has started ringing.

**Step three — the market slows visibly (three to six months later).** Days on market lengthen — the homes that are sitting are now *visibly* sitting. "Price reduced" tags spread across listings. The sale-to-list ratio slips below 100% as the first sellers accept discounts. Bidding wars vanish. By now even casual observers sense the market has "cooled," but the *index* — built from closed, lagged transactions — is only flat, not yet down.

**Step four — valuation peaks (six to nine months later).** Price-to-rent tops out and begins to roll over, because the buy-side demand that stretched it is now exhausted and rents (sticky, fundamental) start to catch up to a price that has stopped rising. Affordability is maxed out. The price index is now plateauing — flat year on year, the momentum gone.

**Step five — price finally falls (nine to eighteen months later).** Only now, at the very end of the relay, does the lagging price index turn down. The headlines announce a "housing downturn" or a "crash." Sellers who held out finally capitulate. But for anyone who read the dashboard, this is *old news* — the turn happened six to eighteen months ago in the demand and inventory data, and the price is simply the last domino, falling exactly as the sequence predicted.

The power of knowing the *order* is that you can locate yourself in the cycle from a *single cross-section* of the dashboard. If applications are down but inventory is still tight and DOM is still short, you are at step one — the very start of the rollover, with the most warning. If inventory is built, DOM is long, valuation has peaked, but price is only flat, you are at step four — the turn is imminent and nearly everyone still thinks the market is fine. You are reading the relay's baton position, not waiting for the runner to cross the line.

One honest caveat, because the sequence is a *typical* order, not a law of physics. A sudden, violent shock — a credit freeze, a rate spike, a pandemic — can *compress* the sequence, collapsing what normally takes a year into a few months, or even invert pieces of it. Vietnam's 2022 bond freeze did exactly this: the credit shock hit so hard and so fast that volume, liquidity, and developer solvency all cratered almost at once, rather than rolling over in the slow, orderly sequence a demand-led turn produces. The order is the *base case* — the way a normal, demand-driven cycle tops out. A balance-sheet or credit shock can override it. Knowing which kind of turn you are watching is itself part of reading the dashboard.

## Building a real-time scorecard

A dashboard with seven gauges is useless if you cannot *aggregate* it into a judgment. The discipline is a **scorecard**: for each indicator, you record its current reading and classify it into a phase — *heating*, *peaking*, *cooling*, or *bottoming* — and then you read the *pattern* across all the gauges to place the market. The matrix figure below is the template.

![A scorecard matrix with each leading indicator as a row and the four cycle phases heating, peaking, cooling, and bottoming as columns, showing the characteristic reading of each gauge in each phase](/imgs/blogs/leading-indicators-reading-the-cycle-in-real-time-6.png)

Read the matrix one row at a time. In the **heating** phase, months of supply is low and falling, days on market is short and shrinking, price-to-rent is below its historical norm, mortgage applications are rising strongly, starts are picking up off the lows, and credit growth is healthy and accelerating. Everything is green — this is early-to-mid expansion, the best time to be a buyer. In the **peaking** phase, the early gauges flip first: months of supply starts rising off the lows, days on market begins lengthening, price-to-rent gets stretched and tops out, *mortgage applications roll over first of all*, starts surge into a building boom, and credit growth spikes into overheating. The demand gauges are red while price is still high — the classic top. In the **cooling** phase, months of supply is high and climbing, days on market is long with price cuts spreading, price-to-rent is falling from the peak, applications are down year on year, starts are rolling over, and credit growth is slowing hard — this is the downturn, now visible to everyone. In the **bottoming** phase, the gauges stabilize: months of supply is very high but peaking, days on market is long but no longer rising, price-to-rent gets cheap relative to rent again, applications make their first uptick, starts fall to near zero (no one builds at the bottom), and credit is flat as banks stay cautious — this is the base, where the next cycle's buyers find their entries.

The art is that *real markets are rarely cleanly in one column.* You will see a mix — some gauges peaking, some still heating — and the *mix itself* tells you which step of the sequence you are on. The trick is to weight the *leading* gauges (applications, volume, absorption) more heavily than the lagging ones (price), because the whole point is to read the future, not confirm the past.

#### Worked example: scoring a market with the scorecard

Dana runs her metro through the scorecard this quarter. Here are her readings, gauge by gauge:

- **Months of supply: 5.8 and rising** (was 3.2 a year ago) → *peaking / early cooling.*
- **Days on market: 52 days, up from 18** → *peaking / cooling.*
- **Price-to-rent: 21, against a 16 historical norm and a 22 prior peak** → *peaking* (stretched, near prior top).
- **Mortgage applications: down 20% year on year** → *cooling* (the leading gauge, already rolled over).
- **Starts: up 18% year on year, building at 150% of household formation** → *peaking* (overbuilding into the top).
- **Credit growth: still positive but decelerating sharply** → *peaking.*
- **Price index: still +5% year on year** → *the lone lagging gauge, still printing the old boom.*

Five of seven gauges say *peaking-to-cooling*; the most *leading* one (applications) is firmly in *cooling*; only the most *lagging* one (price) still says *heating*. Dana's verdict is unambiguous: **this market is at the top, in the early stage of rolling over** — somewhere around step three or four of the sequence. The price will be the last to confirm it, and it has not yet. If Dana were a seller, this is the signal to act before the price catches the demand data down. If she were a buyer, this is the signal to wait. Either way, the scorecard gave her a clear read while the price chart was still lying to everyone else.

*A single gauge can mislead; the scorecard cannot, because it forces you to weigh the leading signals that have already turned against the lagging price that has not.*

## Common misconceptions

This is where a finance post earns its trust — by naming the wrong ideas most people hold and correcting them with the *why*.

**"The price is the signal."** This is the master misconception, the one the whole post exists to demolish. The price is the *last* signal, not the first — it is a lagging indicator built from old, closed, sticky, mix-distorted transactions. Watching the price to read the cycle is like watching the taillights of the car ahead to know when *you* should brake: by the time you see them, it is late. The signal lives upstream of price, in volume, intent, and inventory. If you take one idea from this post, take this one: *stop watching the price to time the market, and start watching the gauges that move before it.*

**"One indicator is enough."** No single gauge is reliable on its own — every one of them has false positives. Months of supply can spike for a seasonal reason (winter listings sit longer everywhere). Mortgage applications can wobble on a rate move that reverses. Price-to-rent can look high in a structurally expensive city for good reasons. The signal is in the *confluence* — when *several* leading gauges turn the *same way* at the *same time*, that is real. A scorecard exists precisely so that one noisy gauge cannot fool you; you require agreement across the panel.

**"This metric predicts the exact top."** No metric does, and anyone who claims theirs does is selling something. Leading indicators tell you the *phase* and the *direction* — "this market is rolling over" — not the *date* and not the *magnitude*. The application index turned in 2005; the price peaked in mid-2006 and bottomed in 2012. That is a *fourteen-month* lead to the top and a *seven-year* span to the bottom — useful for knowing *which phase you are in*, useless for pinpointing a day. Demand precision you do not have, and you will lose money trying to get the last 5% by waiting for confirmation. The achievable goal is knowing the phase, not the peak.

**"Rising prices mean a strong market."** As the lead-lag worked example showed, rising prices late in a cycle are perfectly consistent with a market that has already rolled over in volume, intent, and inventory. A +5% price with applications down 20% is a *weak* market wearing a strong price. The strength or weakness is in the *flow* (who is showing up to buy), not the *level* (what the last few closed deals printed).

**"A glut takes years to build, so I have time."** True that supply takes two to four years to complete — but the *demand* side of the turn happens in *months*. You do not get years of warning from the demand gauges; you get months. The supply pipeline (permits, starts) gives you a long lead on the *glut*, but the demand pipeline (applications, volume, absorption) gives you only a short lead on the *turn*. Watch both clocks; they run at different speeds.

**"Vietnam is different — these indicators don't apply here."** The *labels* differ (Vietnam quotes absorption rather than months of supply, credit growth rather than a weekly application index, presale data rather than auction clearance), but the *mechanism* is universal. Demand-flow leads price everywhere, because sticky human sellers and lagged price indices are human universals, not American ones. The 2022 freeze proved it: absorption and developer liquidity collapsed *before* the official price data caught up, exactly as the framework predicts. Adapt the gauges to local data; the logic is the same.

## How it shows up in real markets

Theory is cheap. Here are two concrete cases where the dashboard would have read the cycle in real time, with real dates and numbers.

### The US 2005–06 rollover

The cleanest case in modern history. Watch the gauges turn in textbook order. **Mortgage purchase applications** (MBA index) peaked and began declining in **2005**. **New-home sales** peaked in mid-2005. **Existing-home sales volume** peaked in 2005 and fell through 2006. **Inventory of unsold homes** climbed sharply through 2005–06, and **months of supply** rose from roughly 4 months at the peak of the frenzy toward 7+ months by 2006. **Price-to-rent** ratios, which had stretched to extreme levels nationally (and far more in bubble metros like Phoenix, Miami, and Las Vegas), peaked around 2005. And the **price** — the lagging gauge, the Case-Shiller national index — did not print its all-time high until **July 2006** (184.6 on the Jan-2000=100 scale), then fell roughly **27%** to its trough of about **134 in February 2012**.

![A line chart of the US 2000s cycle with the leading gauge of mortgage applications and housing starts peaking in early 2005 plotted against the lagging home-price index peaking in mid-2006, with the roughly twelve-to-eighteen-month lead between the two turns marked](/imgs/blogs/leading-indicators-reading-the-cycle-in-real-time-5.png)

Lay those dates side by side and the lesson is undeniable: the demand and inventory gauges rolled over in 2005, a *full year* before the price peaked in mid-2006, and *six-plus years* before the bottom. The chart above stacks the leading gauge against the lagging price index for exactly this episode — the amber leading line turns down in early 2005 while the blue price line keeps climbing into 2006, the twelve-to-eighteen-month lead drawn between the two peaks. A buyer in early 2006 watching only the still-rising price felt FOMO and bought near the top. A buyer watching the dashboard saw applications down, inventory up, and months of supply climbing — and *waited*, eventually buying near the 2012 bottom at prices 27% lower. The gauges were not subtle; they were screaming for a year. The only people who missed it were the ones watching the one number that always moves last.

### Vietnam's signals into 2022

Vietnam's most recent cycle did not roll over in the slow, orderly American sequence — it was snapped by a *credit shock*, which compresses the timeline, but the leading-versus-lagging logic still holds. Through **2019–2021**, the warning signs accumulated: developers were building furiously, funded by a booming **corporate-bond market**, planting a supply overhang; primary prices were stretching affordability to among the world's worst (price-to-income climbing toward the 30s); and absorption on new launches, while still strong in the frenzy, increasingly depended on *investors and speculators* rather than owner-occupiers — a late-cycle tell. The valuation and supply gauges were flashing red well before the price did.

Then came the shock. In **April 2022**, the chairman of **Tân Hoàng Minh** was arrested and its bonds cancelled; in **October 2022**, the **Vạn Thịnh Phát / SCB** arrests froze the entire corporate-bond market — the pipe through which developers funded construction. Credit to property *seized*. The leading gauges collapsed almost at once: presale absorption cratered, developer liquidity vanished, half-built projects stranded, and *transaction volume* dried up long before the official "average price" statistics admitted much weakness — because, just as the framework predicts, sellers and developers held their asking prices as long as humanly possible while the *flow* underneath them disappeared. **SBV cut policy rates four times in 2023** and **Decree 08** gave bond-restructuring relief, beginning a halting recovery, but the lesson stands: the leading indicators — credit growth, absorption, developer liquidity, the bond market itself — told the story months before the lagging price data did, exactly as they always do. (The full mechanism of how credit drives these turns is the subject of the credit-cycle and four-phase-anatomy posts linked throughout.)

What does Vietnam's dashboard read *now*, in mid-2026? The figure below is an illustrative scoring.

![A dashboard grid showing an illustrative mid-2026 reading of Vietnam's leading indicators, with price-to-income around thirty-two, primary inventory, credit growth, presale absorption, and the central bank rate stance each scored as a cell](/imgs/blogs/leading-indicators-reading-the-cycle-in-real-time-7.png)

The reading is mixed and late-cycle. **Price-to-income at ~32** is screaming bearish — HCMC is near the fifth-least-affordable city on Earth, with affordability exhausted. **Primary inventory** is skewed: high-end and luxury units are piling up (a glut at the top of the market) even as genuine mid-market supply stays tight, a divergence worth watching. **Credit growth** is recovering from the 2022 freeze but has not yet spiked into a fresh overheating — neutral. **Presale absorption** has softened from the 2021 frenzy as buyers turn cautious — soft. And the **SBV rate stance** is supportive (policy held low, teasers around 8%), but trap-laden, because those teasers reset to 12–14%. The dashboard verdict is *late-cycle and stretched on valuation, but not yet a clean top*, because credit and rates are still supportive rather than tightening. The single gauge to watch next, per the sequence, is *volume* — presale absorption and transaction counts — because that is the gauge that always turns first. (This reading is illustrative and educational, not a forecast or investment advice; the as-of date is mid-2026, and property data goes stale fast.)

## When this matters to you / further reading

This dashboard matters the moment you have real money riding on a property decision — which, for most people, is the largest financial decision of their lives. If you are **buying a home to live in**, the gauges will not tell you the exact bottom, but they will keep you from buying at a screaming top: when months of supply is climbing, days on market is stretching, applications are falling, and price-to-income is near a record, you are buying late, and knowing that lets you negotiate harder, wait, or at least go in with open eyes. If you are an **investor**, the dashboard is your entry-and-exit discipline — it tells you which phase you are buying or selling into, which is most of the battle. And if you are simply a **citizen trying to understand the headlines**, the dashboard inoculates you against the price-obsessed coverage that always arrives a year late: when the news breathlessly reports a "housing crash," you will already know the demand gauges called it twelve months earlier, and when the news cheers a "red-hot market," you will check whether the *flow* underneath the price is actually strong or whether the price is just the last domino still standing.

The single habit to build is this: **stop watching the price, and start watching the gauges that move before it.** Pick three or four you can actually track in your market — months of supply (or absorption), days on market, mortgage applications (or credit growth), and price-to-rent against its own history — and read them as a panel, every month, weighting the leading ones over the lagging price. You will not call the exact top. No one does. But you will know which *phase* you are standing in, which is the difference between a buyer who gets the news on time and one who reads it on the front page, a year too late.
