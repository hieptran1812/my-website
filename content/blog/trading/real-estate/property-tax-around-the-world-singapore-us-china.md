> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — How a country taxes property is not a technical footnote; it is a *menu of choices*, and three big economies have already run the experiments Vietnam is now debating. Singapore wields an annual property tax **plus** some of the heaviest transaction taxes on Earth — an Additional Buyer's Stamp Duty (ABSD) that reaches **~60% of price** for foreigners, a Seller's Stamp Duty (SSD) that punishes quick flips, and a debt-service cap (TDSR) — as a precision tool that genuinely cools the market. The US runs a decentralised annual property tax averaging **~1.1% of value every year** that funds local schools and produces the "house-rich, cash-poor" squeeze and California's Prop-13 lock-in. China — which, like Vietnam, historically had almost **no holding tax** and funded local government by *selling land* — ran property-tax pilots in Shanghai and Chongqing since 2011 and has debated a national property tax for over a decade as its land-finance model breaks.
>
> - **Two completely different taxes get confused.** An *annual holding tax* is a bill you pay every year for *owning*. A *transaction / stamp duty* is a one-time bill you pay for *buying or selling*. They behave nothing alike: holding taxes punish *sitting* on property, stamp duties punish *moving* it.
> - **Singapore taxes the deal hardest; the US taxes the holding hardest; China taxes almost nothing and lives off land sales; Vietnam looks most like old China.** Each design has a documented effect you can already see playing out somewhere.
> - **Foreign-buyer taxes are a real lever, and Singapore's 60% ABSD is the most aggressive on the planet** — versus Vietnam's roughly **0%** surcharge (it uses a 30%-per-block ownership cap instead).
> - The one number to remember: a US homeowner pays **~1.1% of value, every single year**; a Vietnamese owner pays **~0.05%** — a holding tax close to zero. Every behaviour difference between the two markets flows downstream of that gap.

In early 2026, three people on three continents bought almost the same thing — a roughly two-million-dollar home — and walked away owing the tax authorities three wildly different amounts. In **Singapore**, a foreign buyer signing for a S\$2 million condominium discovered that, on top of the price, the government wanted another **S\$1.2 million** in Additional Buyer's Stamp Duty — a 60% surcharge, larger than the deposit on most mortgages, payable on the day of purchase. In a **US** suburb, our recurring investor **Dana** paid \$400,000 for a house and owed nothing extra at closing beyond modest fees — but every year for the rest of her ownership, like clockwork, her county would mail a bill for about **\$4,400**. And in **Vietnam**, our recurring HCMC buyer **Minh** bought a ₫3.5 billion (≈ \$135,000) apartment, paid a **2% transfer tax** at the deal, and would then owe the state essentially *nothing* to hold it — a few hundred thousand đồng a year, the price of a couple of lunches.

Same asset class. Same human impulse to own property. Three governments that each decided to load the tax onto a completely *different moment* of owning. Singapore taxes the *purchase* until it bleeds; the US taxes the *holding* relentlessly, year after year; Vietnam taxes only the *transfer* and lets you sit on the asset almost free; and China, fascinatingly, used to look exactly like Vietnam — and is now, painfully, trying to change.

This is not an accident of paperwork. *How you tax property determines how property behaves.* Tax the holding and idle land becomes expensive, so owners build, rent, or sell — but homeowners get squeezed by an annual bill. Tax the transaction and people stop transacting — markets freeze, families stay in homes that no longer fit them. Tax foreign buyers and you protect locals from a flood of global capital — but you can deter real investment and dent the construction pipeline. Tax almost nothing and let local governments live off selling land — and you build a machine that runs beautifully until the land stops selling, which is exactly what happened to China.

## Foundations: the global menu of property taxes

Before we can compare countries, we need to be ruthless about a distinction that almost everyone blurs. There are *two completely different taxes* that both get loosely called "property tax", and confusing them is the single biggest mistake people make in this whole subject.

### The two taxes: holding versus transaction

An **annual holding tax** (also called a property tax, a recurrent property tax, or a land-use tax) is a bill you pay *every year* simply for *owning* a property. It does not matter whether you bought today or thirty years ago, whether you live in it, rent it, or leave it empty — every year, the bill arrives. It is a tax on the *stock* of property you hold. The US property tax is the textbook example: own a \$400,000 house in a typical American county and you owe roughly \$4,400 *this year, and next year, and every year after.*

A **transaction tax** (also called a stamp duty, transfer tax, deed tax, or registration fee) is a *one-time* bill you pay when the property *changes hands* — when you buy it, or sometimes when you sell it. It is a tax on the *flow* of property through the market — on the *event* of a deal, not the *fact* of ownership. Vietnam's 2% transfer tax is the textbook example: pay it once when you buy, and then you can hold the property for fifty years and never see that tax again.

Hold onto the difference, because the two taxes pull in opposite directions:

| | Annual holding tax | Transaction / stamp duty |
|---|---|---|
| **What triggers it** | Owning, every year | A purchase or sale (the deal) |
| **Frequency** | Recurring, forever | One-time, per transaction |
| **What it discourages** | *Sitting* on idle property | *Moving* property (buying, selling, flipping) |
| **Who feels it** | Long-term holders, the asset-rich | Frequent buyers, flippers, movers |
| **Side effect** | "House-rich, cash-poor"; forces property to be used | Markets freeze; people stay put ("lock-in") |
| **Classic example** | US ~1.1%/yr | Vietnam 2%; Singapore ABSD up to 60% |

If a government wants to **discourage land banking and idle property**, it reaches for a holding tax — make owning costly every year and you force the owner to build, rent, or sell. If a government wants to **discourage speculation and flipping**, it reaches for a punishing transaction tax — make every deal expensive and you cool the churn. Singapore, as we will see, does *both* at once, with surgical precision. The US leans almost entirely on the holding side. Vietnam leans almost entirely on the (light) transaction side. That is the entire story in miniature.

### The supporting cast of terms

A few more definitions, built from zero, that we will use throughout:

- **Stamp duty.** A historical name for a transaction tax — once you literally bought a physical stamp to make a deal legal. Today it is just a percentage tax on the purchase price. Singapore's **Buyer's Stamp Duty (BSD)** is the base version everyone pays; its **Additional Buyer's Stamp Duty (ABSD)** is an *extra* surcharge layered on top depending on *who you are* and *how many homes you already own*.
- **Assessment / assessed value.** For an annual holding tax you need a *value to tax*. The **assessed value** is the number the tax authority assigns to your property — ideally close to what it would actually sell for (its *market value*), though many systems deliberately assess below market. The official who sets it is the *assessor*.
- **Mill rate (or millage).** The US unit for a property-tax rate. One *mill* is one-thousandth — 0.1%. A property taxed at "11 mills" pays 11 ÷ 1,000 = **1.1%** of its assessed value per year. We will translate every mill rate into a plain percentage so nobody gets lost.
- **Progressive versus flat.** A *flat* tax charges the same rate to everyone (2% of any price). A *progressive* tax charges a *higher rate as the amount rises* — your second home is taxed harder than your first, your third harder than your second. Singapore's ABSD is steeply progressive in *who you are* and *how many homes you own*; the US property tax is essentially flat (same rate per dollar of value).
- **Foreign-buyer tax.** A surcharge — usually a transaction tax — applied specifically to *non-resident foreign* buyers, to slow foreign capital from bidding up local housing. Singapore's 60% ABSD for foreigners is the most aggressive on Earth; the US has essentially none federally; Vietnam uses an *ownership cap* (foreigners may own at most 30% of a block, on 50-year leasehold) instead of a tax.
- **Land finance (土地财政).** China's model, which we will dissect: local governments raised the bulk of their money not by *taxing* property but by *selling* long leases on state-owned land to developers. It is the opposite of an annual holding tax — revenue comes from *moving* land, not from *holding* it.

### Why the design choice is everything

Two governments can raise the *same amount of revenue* from property and produce *opposite markets*, depending purely on which lever they pull. Tax \$10 billion through an annual holding tax and you discipline idle land, fund local services steadily, and squeeze the asset-rich. Raise the same \$10 billion through transaction taxes and you instead freeze turnover, reward sitting, and leave idle land untouched. The *amount* of tax is almost beside the point; the *shape* of it — holding versus transaction, flat versus progressive, resident versus foreigner — is what determines how property behaves.

This is why "comparative property taxation" is worth studying as its own subject rather than a list of rates. The interesting question is never "is the tax high or low?" but "*which moment of ownership does it tax, and whom does it spare?*" Singapore taxes the foreigner and the flipper and the second-home buyer, and spares the first-home citizen. The US taxes everyone's holding flatly and spares no one (until Prop 13 carved out long-time owners). Vietnam taxes the deal lightly and spares the holder almost entirely. Each answer to "which moment, and whom" produces a recognisably different market — and that is the lens we will carry through all three case studies.

With the vocabulary in place, we can now walk the three case studies — Singapore, the US, China — and then bring every lesson home to Vietnam.

## Singapore: stamp duties as a cooling weapon

Singapore is the world's clearest demonstration that you can use *taxes* to *steer* a property market with something close to a thermostat. It is a tiny, land-scarce island where homeownership is near-sacred public policy — over 80% of citizens live in subsidised public (HDB) flats — and where a flood of global capital could, if unchecked, price locals out of their own city. So Singapore built a *cooling stack*: not one tax but a layered system of blunt instruments, each one calibrated to who you are and how you behave.

![A grid showing Singapore's cooling stack with the buyer stamp duty, the additional buyer stamp duty by buyer type and number of homes, and the seller stamp duty plus loan curbs, applied across citizens, permanent residents, and foreigners](/imgs/blogs/property-tax-around-the-world-singapore-us-china-2.png)

The figure above lays out the stack. Read it top to bottom as *layers of friction*, and left to right as *who pays how much*. There are three layers — the base **Buyer's Stamp Duty**, the **Additional Buyer's Stamp Duty**, and a pair of curbs (the **Seller's Stamp Duty** plus the **TDSR** loan cap) — and three kinds of buyer: a citizen, a permanent resident, and a foreigner. The citizen buying a first home slips through almost untaxed. The foreigner hits *all three* layers at full force. That gradient is the whole design.

### Layer 1 — Buyer's Stamp Duty (everyone pays)

The base layer is the **Buyer's Stamp Duty (BSD)**: a progressive transaction tax everyone pays on any property purchase, running from roughly **1% to 6%** of price as the price rises. It is modest and universal — the foundation the spicier layers sit on top of. On a S\$2 million home the BSD alone is on the order of S\$70,000-80,000. Notable, but not the weapon.

### Layer 2 — Additional Buyer's Stamp Duty (the precision tool)

The **Additional Buyer's Stamp Duty (ABSD)** is where Singapore gets serious. It is an *extra* transaction surcharge that scales steeply with *who you are* and *how many residential properties you already own*. As of the April-2023 tightening, the headline tiers run roughly:

- **Citizen, first home: 0% ABSD.** The system deliberately spares the ordinary citizen buying a place to live. This is the political genius of it — the cooling weapon barely touches the median voter.
- **Citizen, second home: ~20%. Third and beyond: ~30%.** Buy a second property as a Singaporean and you owe a fifth of the price on top, as a one-time tax. That is a brutal disincentive to accumulate a portfolio of homes.
- **Permanent resident, first home: ~5%; second: ~30%.** Residents pay a little even on their first.
- **Foreigner, any home: ~60%.** This is the headline number, raised from 30% to 60% in 2023. A non-resident foreign buyer pays a surcharge of roughly *sixty percent of the price* on *any* home, first or fifth.

The logic of ABSD is precision. It does not blanket-cool the market for everyone — it pinpoints the buyers a government most wants to slow (foreign capital, domestic speculators stacking multiple homes) and leaves the owner-occupier citizen almost untouched. It is *progressive in behaviour and identity*, not just in price.

#### Worked example: the S\$2M condo and the 60% ABSD

A foreign buyer — call her our running *foreign buyer* — wants a S\$2 million condominium in Singapore as a second home and an investment.

- **Base Buyer's Stamp Duty:** roughly 4-5% on a price this size, say about **S\$75,000**.
- **Additional Buyer's Stamp Duty (foreigner):** 60% × S\$2,000,000 = **S\$1,200,000**.
- **Total stamp duty at the deal:** about **S\$1,275,000** — on top of the S\$2,000,000 price.

So to *acquire* a S\$2M home, the foreign buyer must hand the government roughly **S\$1.2 million in ABSD alone** — more than 60% of the purchase price, payable on day one, before she has earned a single dollar of rent or appreciation. Her all-in cost is over **S\$3.27 million** for a S\$2 million asset.

*A 60% acquisition tax does not "discourage" foreign buying so much as it amputates it — which is exactly the point.*

### Layer 3 — the curbs: Seller's Stamp Duty and TDSR

Two more tools finish the stack, aimed not at *who buys* but at *how they behave after*:

The **Seller's Stamp Duty (SSD)** punishes *quick flips*. Sell a residential property within a holding period (roughly **three years**), and you owe a surcharge on the *sale* — historically up to **12% if you sell within the first year**, tapering to zero as you cross the three-year mark. The SSD directly attacks speculation: it makes the buy-it-cheap-flip-it-fast trade prohibitively expensive, so capital that comes in must *stay* in.

The **Total Debt Servicing Ratio (TDSR)** is not a tax at all but a *lending rule*, and it belongs in the cooling stack because it caps the *fuel*. Under TDSR a borrower's total monthly debt payments — mortgage plus car loan plus everything else — cannot exceed **55% of their gross monthly income**. By capping how much anyone can borrow against income, TDSR stops the leverage spiral that inflates bubbles. (If you want the deeper mechanics of how borrowed money amplifies property both up and down, see [leverage and the mortgage](/blog/trading/real-estate/leverage-and-the-mortgage-how-debt-amplifies-property).)

#### Worked example: an SSD-killed flip

Suppose a speculator buys a S\$1.5 million condo intending to flip it in nine months for S\$1.65 million — a tidy S\$150,000 gross gain on paper.

- **Seller's Stamp Duty (sold within ~1 year): ~12%** of the S\$1,650,000 sale = about **S\$198,000**.
- The S\$150,000 paper gain is *more than wiped out* by the S\$198,000 SSD — the flip turns a roughly **S\$48,000 loss** before agent fees.

*The SSD doesn't tax the flipper's profit; it taxes the act of flipping itself, hard enough that the fast trade simply stops being worth attempting.*

### The annual property tax underneath it all

It is easy to fixate on the dramatic stamp duties and forget that Singapore *also* runs a genuine **annual property tax** — a recurring holding tax most countries' headlines skip. Singapore taxes the **Annual Value (AV)** of a home, which is roughly the estimated *yearly rent* the property could fetch — typically on the order of 3-4% of the home's price. It then applies a **progressive rate** to that Annual Value, and — this is the elegant part — the rate depends on whether you *live in the home or not*:

- **Owner-occupiers** pay a gentle progressive schedule, starting near 0% on the first slice of Annual Value and rising into the low double digits only for very high-value homes. For a typical owner-occupied flat, the effective annual tax works out to roughly **0.3% of the home's price** per year.
- **Non-owner-occupied (investor) homes** pay a steeper schedule — historically up to ~32-36% of Annual Value at the top — which, because Annual Value is roughly 3-4% of price, lands at an effective **~1.0%+ of price** per year, comparable to a US property tax.

So Singapore is the only one of our four countries that taxes *both* sides hard: a punishing transaction stack *and* a recurring holding tax that itself discriminates between the family living in its home and the investor sitting on a second one. The owner-occupier is spared on both fronts; the speculator and the foreigner are hit on both. That doubled-up precision is what gives Singapore its thermostat-like control — there is no escape hatch where holding is free, the way there is in Vietnam.

### Does it actually work?

Yes, demonstrably — which is what makes Singapore the key case. Every time Singapore's property market has run hot, the government has *tightened* the cooling stack (raised ABSD, lengthened SSD, lowered TDSR), and prices have measurably cooled within quarters. The 2023 jump in foreign ABSD from 30% to 60% visibly cut foreign transaction volumes. This is the rare instance where a tax tool *bends the market on command*. The cost is real — Singapore is still one of the most expensive housing markets on Earth (a price-to-income ratio around 17 years), and critics argue the cooling stack mostly protects existing owners — but as a precision instrument, it works. The lesson Vietnam should file away: *a targeted, behaviour-based transaction tax can genuinely cool speculation without crushing the ordinary owner-occupier* — if, and only if, it is designed to spare the first-home citizen.

## The US: the local property tax that funds schools

Cross the Pacific and the entire philosophy flips. The United States barely taxes property *transactions* — transfer taxes are light and vary by state, from essentially 0% to maybe 4%, and there is no federal foreign-buyer tax to speak of. Instead, America leans almost its entire property-tax weight on the *holding* side: a recurring **annual property tax** that is the single most important source of *local* government money in the country. It is not set in Washington; it is set, collected, and spent by your county, your city, and above all your **school district**.

![A five step pipeline showing how a US property tax bill is built, flowing from market value to assessed value to the mill rate to the annual bill and into funding local schools, then reassessed the next year](/imgs/blogs/property-tax-around-the-world-singapore-us-china-3.png)

The figure above traces the pipeline every American property-tax bill flows through. Start with **market value** — what the home would sell for. The local *assessor* converts it to an **assessed value**, often near 100% of market. The county, city, and school district each stack on a **mill rate** — say 11 mills total, which is 1.1%. Multiply assessed value by the rate to get the **annual bill**. The cash flows out to schools, fire, and roads — *roughly 60% of a typical US property-tax bill funds the local public schools.* And next year the assessor reassesses: as the home's value rises, so does the bill — *unless a cap intervenes*, which is the Prop-13 twist we come to in a moment.

### How the bill is built, in numbers

The American property tax is conceptually simple: **annual bill = assessed value × tax rate.** The national average effective rate is about **1.1% of market value per year**, but the range across states is enormous — from roughly **0.3%** in low-tax states like Hawaii and Alabama to **2.0-2.2%** in high-tax states like New Jersey and Illinois. (Source: Tax Foundation / US Census ACS, 2024-25.) Two identical houses, one in New Jersey and one in Alabama, can carry property-tax bills that differ by *seven times*.

#### Worked example: Dana's \$400,000 house and the school district

Dana buys a \$400,000 house in a typical American suburb taxed at 11 mills (1.1%).

- **Annual property-tax bill:** \$400,000 × 1.1% = **\$4,400 per year.**
- **Where it goes:** about 60% — roughly **\$2,640** — funds the local public *schools*; the rest covers the fire department, police, roads, libraries, and county services.
- **Over ten years:** \$4,400 × 10 = **\$44,000** — about 11% of what she paid for the house — handed to local government, *whether or not the house appreciates, whether she lives in it or leaves it empty.*

Now contrast Dana with Minh holding a similar-value apartment in HCMC, who pays an annual holding tax of roughly **0.05%** — on the order of a few hundred thousand đồng, call it under \$50 a year. *Dana pays in a single year what Minh pays in roughly a century.*

*The US property tax ties the cost of owning to the value of what you own, every year — which is why an empty, appreciating American house still bleeds cash, and an empty, appreciating Vietnamese one does not.*

### Why assessment is the hidden battleground

The whole American system rests on one fragile step: the **assessment** — the value the assessor assigns. Get it wrong and the entire bill is wrong. Two failure modes recur. *Over-assessment* means you are taxed on a value higher than your home would actually sell for, which is why American homeowners have the right to *appeal* their assessment, and millions do every year. *Under-assessment*, the more insidious one, means the tax base quietly erodes: if assessors systematically value homes below market — out of inertia, political pressure, or law — the local government either collects too little or must crank the rate up to compensate.

This matters enormously for Vietnam, because Vietnam's official "state prices" have historically been set *far below* true market value — sometimes a half or a third — which is exactly why its land-use tax of "0.03-0.15%" translates to an effective ~0.05% of *real* value. You cannot run an honest annual holding tax on a dishonest valuation. The American lesson is that a property tax is only as good as its assessment machinery: you need regular, transparent, near-market valuation of every parcel, the right to appeal, and the political will to let assessed values track reality. The **Land Law 2024**'s shift to *annual land-price tables* is precisely Vietnam beginning to build that machinery — the unglamorous prerequisite without which no holding tax can function.

### The "house-rich, cash-poor" tension

A recurring tax on *value* has a hard edge: it does not care about your *income*. Picture a retiree who bought a modest house decades ago for \$60,000 in a neighbourhood that has since boomed; the house is now worth \$900,000 but she lives on a fixed pension. Her annual property-tax bill — 1.1% of \$900,000 — is nearly **\$10,000 a year**, money she does not have, on an asset she cannot eat. She is *house-rich and cash-poor*: wealthy on paper, squeezed in cash. This is the central political wound of any annual holding tax, and it is exactly the tension Vietnam would import if it adopted one. The economist's answer is that the tax forces property to be put to productive use or sold to someone who will — but the homeowner's experience is a relentless bill on a home she just wants to live in.

### Prop 13: the cap that froze California

In 1978, that house-rich-cash-poor anger boiled over in California, where rapidly rising home values were driving rapidly rising tax bills, and voters passed **Proposition 13**. Prop 13 did two things: it capped the property-tax rate at roughly 1% of *purchase price*, and — the consequential part — it limited the *growth* of assessed value to about **2% per year** for as long as you own the home. Your assessment is effectively *frozen near what you paid*, and only resets to market value when the property *sells*.

The result is a textbook example of how a well-meaning tax cap creates **lock-in**. Consider two identical houses next door to each other. One was bought in 1985 for \$150,000; under Prop 13 its assessed value has crept up only ~2% a year and now sits around \$300,000, so the owner pays about \$3,000 a year. The other just sold for \$1.2 million, so the new owner is assessed at \$1.2 million and pays \$12,000 a year — *four times the tax for the identical house next door.* The long-time owner faces a powerful reason *never to sell or move*: doing so would forfeit a tax assessment frozen at 1985 levels and reset her to today's market. So she stays put — in a house too big, in a job-rich area a younger family would kill for — and the housing market seizes up. Prop 13 is the most famous demonstration anywhere that *how you tax the holding* can freeze a market just as effectively as a transaction tax can.

The American lesson for Vietnam is twofold. The upside: an annual holding tax makes *idle land expensive*, funds local services directly from local property wealth, and discourages the buy-and-sit speculation that plagues Vietnamese land markets. The risk: it squeezes the house-rich-cash-poor, it requires *honest, regular valuation* of every property (which Vietnam has historically lacked — its official "state prices" run far below market), and if you bolt on a Prop-13-style cap to soften the politics, you can manufacture lock-in. (For the full theory of why a holding tax is, to economists, almost a perfect tax — and why it capitalises into lower prices — see [property and land holding taxes](/blog/trading/real-estate/property-and-land-holding-taxes).)

## China: land finance and the stalling property-tax pilots

China is the most important case for Vietnam, because China *started where Vietnam is now* — and is living through the painful consequences of having stayed there too long. For decades, China had essentially **no annual property tax**. Like Vietnam, all land is ultimately state-owned; what changes hands is a long lease (70 years for residential). And like Vietnam, Chinese local governments funded themselves not by taxing the *holding* of property but by *selling* those land leases to developers. This is the famous **land-finance** model (土地财政).

### How land finance works — and why it is a trap

The mechanism is seductive. A city government owns the land. It rezones farmland or village land for development, then *auctions* long leases on it to property developers, who pay enormous up-front sums. Those land-sale proceeds — at the peak, this was the *largest single source* of many Chinese cities' revenue, frequently 30-40% of local government income — paid for roads, metros, and the apparatus of the local state. The more the city developed and the more land it sold, the more revenue it raised, which funded more development, which raised land values further. A self-reinforcing engine.

The problem is structural and it is the *exact* problem Vietnam should study. A government funded by *selling* land has a powerful built-in incentive to keep land *expensive* and to keep *selling more of it* — the opposite of what is good for housing affordability. It is also a *flow* dependent on a *transaction* continuing to happen. The day the music stops — the day developers stop buying land because they cannot sell the apartments they already built — the revenue *vanishes*, because there is no *recurring* holding tax underneath to catch the city when land sales dry up. Land finance is a machine with no backstop.

That is precisely what broke. As China's property developers (Evergrande the most famous) defaulted from 2021 onward, demand for new land collapsed, land-sale revenue cratered, and local governments — utterly dependent on that flow — found their finances cracking with nothing to replace them. A holding tax, had one existed, would have kept money flowing in *regardless* of whether new deals happened. China had no such backstop. This is the cautionary tale at the heart of the whole post.

To feel why a holding tax is structurally *safer* than land finance, compare the two revenue engines directly. Land finance is a **flow tax on a one-time event**: a city earns money only when it sells *new* land, so its budget rises and falls with the development cycle and runs out of fuel entirely if there is no more land to sell or no developer willing to buy. An annual holding tax is a **stock tax on an existing base**: the city earns money every year from *every* property already standing, whether or not anyone buys or sells anything. The stock of property is vast and stable; the flow of new deals is small and volatile. A government that funds itself from the stock has a steady, recession-resistant base; a government that funds itself from the flow has a boom-and-bust budget that mirrors — and amplifies — the property cycle. China built the volatile one and skipped the stable one, and the 2022-2024 fiscal crunch in its local governments is the bill for that choice arriving.

### A decade of trying — and stalling

The figure above tracks China's long, stalled attempt to fix this by adding an annual property tax. Read it left to right as a *saga of delay*:

- **2011 — the pilots.** Shanghai and Chongqing launched narrow, low-rate property-tax pilots — the first recurrent property taxes in modern China. They were deliberately small: Shanghai taxed mainly *second homes and new luxury purchases* at roughly 0.4-0.6% of value; Chongqing taxed high-end villas and luxury flats at up to ~1.2%. Crucially, they covered only a *sliver* of homes, so their nationwide effect was minimal.
- **2013-2020 — debated and shelved.** A national property tax was discussed at nearly every major Party plenum. A draft law was floated. And each time, it was *shelved* — too politically explosive, too threatening to the land-finance revenue local governments still depended on, too likely to trigger price falls the leadership feared.
- **2021-2022 — the trial that got paused.** In 2021 the leadership formally approved a *new, wider* property-tax trial — and then, in 2022, *paused* it as the property market weakened sharply. The reform that would help in the long run was politically impossible in a downturn.
- **2022-2024 — the land model breaks.** Evergrande's collapse, plunging land sales, and cracking local-government finances exposed the absence of a backstop in full.
- **2025 onward — still no national tax, but rising pressure.** As of 2026 China *still* has no national property tax, but the pressure to introduce a holding tax to replace lost land revenue is now structural, not theoretical.

#### Worked example: a Shanghai pilot tax on a flat

Under the Shanghai pilot, suppose a family buys a second apartment for ¥6 million (≈ \$830,000) that falls into the taxable band, assessed at a tax rate of about 0.6% of value.

- **Annual pilot tax:** ¥6,000,000 × 0.6% = **¥36,000 per year** (≈ \$5,000/year).
- But the pilot exempted *most* homes — first homes, and value below a per-person floor — so a typical family's *primary* residence often owed **nothing**. The pilot's nationwide effective rate, averaged over *all* homes, rounded to roughly **zero**.

*China's pilots prove the design works in principle — but a tax that only touches a sliver of luxury second homes raises little revenue and cools little speculation; the political courage to broaden it is the part China never found.*

The China lesson for Vietnam could not be sharper. **China is Vietnam's near-future if nothing changes.** A land-finance model that funds local government by selling land works wonderfully — until land stops selling, at which point you discover you built no recurring backstop. The window to introduce a holding tax is *during the boom*, when the politics are easiest and the market can absorb it — and China's tragedy is that it kept waiting for a "better time" that the downturn then took off the table. (Vietnam's own cycle of land-sale-funded local government and the 2022-23 freeze is mapped in [Vietnam property cycles 2007-2013-2022](/blog/trading/real-estate/vietnam-property-cycles-2007-2013-2022).)

## Foreign-buyer taxes compared

One lever deserves its own section because it is the one Vietnam debates most loudly: the **foreign-buyer tax**. When global capital pours into a small or hot housing market, it can bid prices beyond what locals earning local wages can pay. Governments respond either with a *tax* (a surcharge on foreign buyers) or with a *cap* (a limit on how much foreigners may own). The range of choices, and their aggressiveness, is wide.

![A bar chart comparing foreign-buyer surcharge rates across Singapore at sixty percent, Hong Kong's historic thirty percent peak, Vancouver at twenty percent, Australia's state surcharges, the United States at near zero, and Vietnam at near zero](/imgs/blogs/property-tax-around-the-world-singapore-us-china-4.png)

The figure above ranks the headline foreign-buyer surcharge — the extra tax, as a percent of price, a non-resident foreign buyer pays on top of a normal purchase — across six markets:

- **Singapore — ~60% (ABSD for foreigners).** The most aggressive on Earth, raised to 60% in April 2023. A near-prohibitive wall.
- **Hong Kong — ~30% (historic peak).** Combined buyer's stamp duties for non-permanent-resident buyers reached roughly 30% before Hong Kong *removed* them in February 2024 to revive a flagging market — itself a lesson that these taxes are reversible policy dials, not permanent fixtures.
- **Vancouver / British Columbia — ~20%.** A 20% foreign-buyer tax, introduced to cool a market widely seen as distorted by overseas capital.
- **Australia (state surcharges) — ~7-8%.** Victoria and New South Wales levy land-transfer surcharges on foreign purchasers in this range.
- **United States — ~0%.** No federal foreign-buyer tax; foreigners buy largely on the same terms as locals (a few state-level rules aside). America's openness is itself a policy choice.
- **Vietnam — ~0% surcharge.** Vietnam levies *no* foreign-buyer tax. Instead it uses a *cap*: foreigners may own at most **30% of the units in a residential block**, on a **50-year leasehold**. It controls *quantity*, not *price*.

#### Worked example: the same home, six tax regimes

Take one identical \$1,000,000 home and ask what a *foreign* buyer's surcharge would be in each market:

| Market | Foreign-buyer surcharge | Surcharge on \$1,000,000 |
|---|---|---|
| Singapore | ~60% | **\$600,000** |
| Hong Kong (historic peak) | ~30% | \$300,000 |
| Vancouver (BC) | ~20% | \$200,000 |
| Australia (state) | ~7-8% | ~\$75,000 |
| United States | ~0% | **\$0** |
| Vietnam | ~0% (uses a 30% cap instead) | **\$0** |

*The same foreign buyer pays \$600,000 to buy in Singapore and \$0 to buy in Vietnam or the US — proof that a foreign-buyer tax is a deliberate, dial-able choice, not a force of nature.*

The trade-off is genuine and runs in both directions. A heavy foreign-buyer tax *protects local buyers* from being outbid by global capital — that is its purpose and Singapore shows it works. But it can also *deter real foreign direct investment*, dent the new-build pipeline that foreign-funded projects support, and — as Hong Kong's 2024 *removal* showed — become a liability when you suddenly *want* foreign money back. Vietnam's cap-not-tax approach is a middle path: it limits foreign ownership without slamming the door, and without forfeiting the transaction tax revenue and FDI a more open stance brings.

## What works versus what backfires

Step back from the three cases and the pattern is legible. Each tool has a documented effect — a thing it reliably *fixes* and a thing it reliably *breaks*. Here is the honest scorecard.

**What demonstrably works:**

- **Targeted, behaviour-based transaction taxes cool speculation (Singapore).** ABSD and SSD provably bent the Singapore market when tightened, *because* they spared the first-home owner-occupier and pinpointed foreigners and multi-home buyers. Precision is what makes them politically survivable and economically effective.
- **An annual holding tax funds local government and discourages idle land (US).** A recurring tax on value gives local government a stable revenue base that does *not* depend on deals happening, and makes sitting on empty, appreciating property genuinely expensive — exactly the discipline land-banking markets lack.
- **Foreign-buyer rules protect locals from capital floods (Singapore, Vancouver, Vietnam's cap).** Whether a tax or a cap, controlling foreign demand demonstrably keeps a hot market more accessible to local-wage buyers.

**What backfires:**

- **A pure transaction tax with no holding tax freezes markets and rewards sitting (Vietnam, old China).** If holding is free and only *moving* is taxed, the rational move is to *never move* — buy land and sit, which is precisely the speculative land-banking Vietnam wants to stop. The tax is aimed at the wrong moment.
- **Land finance with no backstop collapses when deals stop (China).** Funding local government by selling land is a flow that vanishes in a downturn, with no recurring holding tax to catch the fall. China is the live demonstration.
- **A holding tax with a value cap creates lock-in (US / Prop 13).** Soften an annual tax with a Prop-13-style assessment freeze and you manufacture a powerful reason never to sell, seizing up the very market mobility you need.
- **A foreign-buyer tax set too high can choke real investment (the Hong Kong reversal).** Push the surcharge to a wall and you may deter the genuine FDI and construction the market needs — which is why Hong Kong reversed course.

The meta-lesson: *there is no free tool.* Every property tax that fixes one behaviour distorts another. The art — the thing Singapore does best and China conspicuously failed to do — is choosing *which* distortion you can live with, and designing the tax to spare the people you cannot afford to anger.

## Lessons for Vietnam

Now bring it all home. Vietnam today looks most like *old China*: a light 2% transfer tax at the deal, a holding tax near zero (the non-agricultural land-use tax runs roughly **0.03-0.15% of a deliberately low state price**, an effective ~0.05% of true market value), no foreign-buyer tax (a 30%-per-block ownership cap instead), and local governments long reliant on *selling* land-use rights. The **Land Law 2024** (effective August 2024) quietly began to change the foundations by replacing the old five-year framework price with *annual land-price tables* — which, for the first time, gives Vietnam a more honest value base on which a real holding tax *could* one day sit. A second-home / property holding tax has been floated by the Ministry of Finance and debated repeatedly from 2023 to 2026, but not enacted.

![A grid mapping three property tax tools Vietnam could borrow from abroad against their upside and their documented failure mode, covering an annual land tax, a progressive second home tax, and foreign buyer rules](/imgs/blogs/property-tax-around-the-world-singapore-us-china-7.png)

The figure above is the decision grid. Each row is a tool Vietnam could borrow from one of our three cases, paired with the upside it could deliver and the risk it carries — and crucially, *every one of those risks is already visible somewhere on Earth*, which is the entire point of comparative taxation. Read it as Vietnam's menu:

- **An annual land tax, like the US ~1.1% on true value.** *Upside:* it makes idle land costly (directly attacking the land-banking and đất nền speculation that drives Vietnamese cycles) and funds local schools and metros from local property wealth. *Risk:* the house-rich-cash-poor squeeze on ordinary owners, and the hard prerequisite of *honest valuation* — Vietnam's state prices have historically run far below market, and the Land Law 2024 price tables are the first step toward fixing that.
- **A progressive second-home tax, like Singapore's ABSD.** *Upside:* it cools speculation while sparing the single owner-occupier — politically the most survivable design, exactly as Singapore showed. *Risk:* owners may hold property through shell companies to dodge it, and if set too punitively it creates lock-in.
- **Foreign-buyer rules, like Singapore's ABSD or Vietnam's existing 30% cap.** *Upside:* protects local buyers from global capital inflows. *Risk:* deters genuine foreign direct investment and can dent the new-build pipeline — the Hong Kong reversal in miniature.

#### Worked example: what a Singapore-style SSD would do to VN đất nền flipping

Vietnam's most notorious speculation is *đất nền* — raw, sub-divided land plots in satellite provinces, bought and flipped rapidly in fevers like the 2018 HCMC-periphery boom. Suppose a speculator buys a plot for ₫2 billion (≈ \$77,000) and flips it eight months later for ₫2.6 billion (≈ \$100,000) — a ₫600 million paper gain.

- **Under today's Vietnam:** 2% transfer tax on the ₫2.6bn sale = **₫52 million**; the speculator keeps roughly **₫548 million** of the gain. The flip is wildly profitable, so the fever spreads.
- **Under a Singapore-style SSD (≈12% for a sale within ~1 year):** 12% × ₫2.6bn = **₫312 million** in seller's stamp duty. The ₫600 million gain shrinks to about **₫236 million** after the SSD — *less than half* — and on a thinner gain the flip might not clear costs at all.

*A holding-period-based seller's tax does to đất nền exactly what it does to Singapore condos: it makes the fast flip uneconomic, so the speculative churn that inflates Vietnamese land fevers loses its fuel — without taxing the family that buys one plot and builds a home on it.*

The honest synthesis: Vietnam is not choosing *whether* its property-tax system has effects — it already does, and they are *visible*. A near-zero holding tax plus a light transaction tax is itself a *choice*, and its consequences (land banking, speculation, land-finance-dependent localities, a 2022-23 freeze) are the predictable downstream of that design. The world has already run the experiments. Singapore shows what disciplined transaction and foreign-buyer taxes can do. The US shows what a recurring holding tax funds — and what it costs the house-rich-cash-poor. China shows what happens when you wait too long. Vietnam's choices are not a leap into the dark; they are a menu with a track record.

## Common misconceptions

**"China already has a property tax."** This is the single most common error, and it is wrong. China has run *narrow pilots* in Shanghai and Chongqing since 2011 — covering a sliver of luxury and second homes — but it has **no national property tax**, and a nationwide rollout has been debated and *repeatedly shelved* for over a decade. Nationwide, the effective holding tax rounds to roughly *zero*. China is the country *trying and failing* to introduce one, not the country that has it. That distinction is the whole reason China is the cautionary tale for Vietnam.

**"Property tax and stamp duty are basically the same thing."** They are opposites in behaviour. A *property tax* is annual and falls on *holding*; a *stamp duty* is one-time and falls on the *transaction*. A holding tax punishes *sitting* on property; a stamp duty punishes *moving* it. A country can have a heavy stamp duty and zero holding tax (Vietnam) or a heavy holding tax and light stamp duty (the US). Confusing the two makes the rest of the subject incomprehensible.

**"The US is a low-tax country, so its property tax must be light."** On *property holding*, the US is one of the *higher*-tax developed economies — an average ~1.1% of value *every year*, reaching 2%+ in New Jersey and Illinois. Over a decade that is more than 10% of a home's value handed to local government. America keeps income and consumption taxes comparatively modest in places, but its *local* property tax is heavy and is the backbone of school funding. "Low-tax America" and "high property tax" coexist comfortably.

**"A foreign-buyer tax is a permanent, principled policy."** It is a *dial*, set by the politics of the moment, and it reverses. Hong Kong's combined foreign-buyer stamp duties hit ~30% — and were *removed* in February 2024 when the market needed reviving. Singapore *doubled* its ABSD to 60% in 2023 when it needed cooling. These are thermostats, not constitutions; they go up and down with the cycle.

**"Singapore's high property taxes mean ordinary Singaporeans are crushed by them."** The opposite, by design. The cooling stack is engineered to *spare* the citizen buying a first home — 0% ABSD, no SSD if you don't flip — and to fall hardest on foreigners and multi-home buyers. The median Singaporean owner-occupier barely feels ABSD at all. That precision is *why* the system is politically durable; a blanket tax on all buyers would have been repealed.

**"If Vietnam introduces a property tax, prices will simply rise by the amount of the tax."** Economically, the reverse is more likely for the holding tax: a permanent annual tax *capitalises* into a *lower* purchase price, because a rational buyer pays less today for an asset that now carries a yearly cost. The seller who owns the property on announcement day tends to eat the one-time value hit. (The capitalisation mechanism is worked through in detail in [property and land holding taxes](/blog/trading/real-estate/property-and-land-holding-taxes).)

## How it shows up in real markets

![A bar chart comparing the annual holding tax as a share of property value across the United States at about one point one percent, a Singapore investor home near one percent, a Singapore owner occupier near zero point three percent, China pilot homes near zero point six percent, China nationwide at near zero, and Vietnam at about zero point zero five percent](/imgs/blogs/property-tax-around-the-world-singapore-us-china-6.png)

The chart above is the single clearest summary of the whole post: *what it costs to hold*, country by country, as an effective annual rate on market value. The US homeowner pays ~1.1% *every year*. A Singapore investor home runs ~1.0%, an owner-occupied Singapore home only ~0.3% (the progressive sparing of the resident again). China's pilot homes pay ~0.6% — but *nationwide* China rounds to ~0%, because the pilots touch so few homes. And Vietnam sits at ~0.05% — a holding tax that is, in practice, zero. Every behavioural difference in the case studies below flows downstream of these bars.

**Singapore, 2023 — the 60% ABSD shock.** When Singapore doubled its foreign-buyer ABSD from 30% to 60% in April 2023, foreign purchase volumes fell sharply within quarters — a clean, near-real-time demonstration that a transaction tax can throttle a specific buyer class on command. Domestic owner-occupiers, untouched by the change, kept transacting. This is the mechanism from the Singapore section in live action: precision cooling that bends the foreign-demand curve without freezing the whole market.

**California, post-1978 — Prop 13 and the frozen suburb.** Four decades after Proposition 13 capped assessment growth at ~2% a year, the lock-in it created is everywhere in California: long-time owners paying a fraction of their neighbours' property tax on identical homes, and refusing to sell or move because doing so would reset their assessment to market value. The result is suppressed housing turnover in exactly the high-demand, job-rich areas where mobility matters most — the US section's warning, visible on the ground for a generation.

**China, 2021-2024 — Evergrande and the land-finance crack.** Evergrande's 2021 default triggered a chain reaction: developers stopped buying land, land-sale revenue (30-40% of many cities' income) cratered, and local-government finances cracked with no recurring holding tax to cushion the fall. This is the land-finance trap from the China section playing out in full — a flow-based revenue model meeting the moment the flow stops. It is the most important real-market lesson in the post for Vietnam, whose local-government finance has rested on the same land-sale foundation.

**Hong Kong, February 2024 — the foreign-buyer tax reversal.** Hong Kong scrapped its ~30% combined foreign-buyer stamp duties when a sliding market made deterring capital the *wrong* policy. The episode proves the foreign-buyer section's point: these surcharges are reversible cyclical dials, raised to cool and cut to revive, not permanent features of a tax code.

**Vietnam, 2018 and 2022-2023 — đất nền fevers and the freeze.** With holding nearly free and only the deal lightly taxed, Vietnamese land has been a near-perfect speculative vehicle: the 2018 đất nền fever in HCMC's satellite provinces saw raw plots flipped repeatedly for rapid gains, and the 2022-2023 corporate-bond freeze (Tân Hoàng Minh, Vạn Thịnh Phát/SCB) exposed how much of the market ran on leverage and land-banking. A Singapore-style SSD or a US-style holding tax would have changed the arithmetic of both — which is exactly why the Ministry of Finance keeps debating one. (For how tax design steers speculation and hoarding in general, see [how tax shapes behavior](/blog/trading/real-estate/how-tax-shapes-behavior-speculation-and-hoarding); for the full map of every way real estate gets taxed, see [how real estate is taxed, the full map](/blog/trading/real-estate/how-real-estate-is-taxed-the-full-map).)

## When this matters / Further reading

This matters the moment you stop seeing property tax as paperwork and start seeing it as a *steering wheel*. If you are a buyer, the tax regime decides whether sitting on an empty appreciating home is free (Vietnam) or bleeds you 1.1% a year (the US), and whether a foreign purchase costs you nothing extra or 60% on top (Singapore). If you are watching Vietnam's market, the property-tax debate is not a side issue — it is the central question of whether the country keeps the land-banking, land-finance, speculation-prone system it shares with *old China*, or borrows the disciplined tools Singapore and the US have already stress-tested. And if you are simply trying to understand why housing behaves so differently across borders, the answer is very often hiding in *which moment of ownership* each government chose to tax.

The honest takeaway is that Vietnam is not facing an unknown. Singapore has shown what precision transaction and foreign-buyer taxes can cool. The US has shown what a recurring holding tax funds — and what it costs the house-rich and cash-poor. China has shown, in real time and at enormous cost, what happens when a land-finance economy waits too long to build a holding-tax backstop. The experiments are run; the track records exist. Whatever Vietnam chooses next, it is choosing from a menu whose dishes have already been tasted.
