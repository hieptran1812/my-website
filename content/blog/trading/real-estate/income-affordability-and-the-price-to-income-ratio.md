> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Over the long run, house prices cannot outrun incomes. Affordability is the gravity under prices, and two gauges measure it: the **price-to-income ratio** (years of income to buy a home) and the **payment-to-income ratio** (the share of income the mortgage eats).
>
> - The price-to-income ratio is the headline gauge: a city where the median home costs four years of median income is affordable; one where it costs thirty is not. Hanoi and HCMC sit at roughly **31–32 years**, among the very highest on Earth — far above the ~15 global median and the ~3–5 of a genuinely affordable market.
> - The payment-to-income ratio is where **interest rates** enter. The same price flips from "affordable" to "stretched" when the rate rises — an 8% teaser eats ~38% of income; a reset to 13% eats ~53% of the *same* income on the *same* loan.
> - When affordability snaps, only three things can give: **prices stall or fall**, **incomes catch up**, or **credit stretches** to paper over the gap. The third is the seductive, dangerous one — it buys affordability today and builds fragility for tomorrow.
> - The one number to remember: a ₫7.0 billion (≈ \$270,000) HCMC flat against a ~₫220 million (≈ \$8,500) median annual household income is a price-to-income ratio of about **32** — which is another way of saying the median household, saving every spare đồng, simply cannot buy the median home at all.

In the autumn of 2025, a software engineer in Ho Chi Minh City we'll call **Minh** sat down with a spreadsheet and a strong coffee and tried to answer the only financial question that really mattered to him: could he buy an apartment? Not a fancy one — a modest two-bedroom flat of about 70 square metres in a decent district, the kind of place where a young family could actually live. The asking price was ₫7.0 billion (≈ \$270,000). Minh earned well by local standards, his partner earned too, and between them they pulled in something like ₫1.5 billion a year (≈ \$58,000) — comfortably in the top slice of the city's earners. And the spreadsheet, when he was honest with it, said *no*. Not "tight". Not "in a few years". A flat clear-eyed *no*.

This is the strange, defining fact of housing in cities like HCMC, Hanoi, Hong Kong, and Mumbai: the price of a home has come unmoored from the income of the people who are supposed to live in it. A house, in the end, has to be paid for out of wages — there is no other source. So there ought to be a kind of gravity that ties prices to incomes, the way gravity ties a thrown ball to the ground. And there is. But over the last two decades, in a handful of cities, prices have been thrown so high and held aloft so long that the gravity seems to have switched off. It hasn't. It never does. This article is about that gravity — what it is, how to measure it, why it can be defied for years but never forever, and what happens when it finally reasserts itself.

![A horizontal bar chart of the price-to-income ratio across ten major world cities, with Hanoi and HCMC highlighted in amber near the top, a dashed line marking the global median of about fifteen years, and a shaded green band marking the affordable range of three to five years](/imgs/blogs/income-affordability-and-the-price-to-income-ratio-1.png)

The chart above is the mental model for the whole post, and it is worth sitting with for a moment. Each bar is one city, and the bar's length is the **price-to-income ratio** — the number of years of a typical household's *entire* income it would take to buy a typical home, if they spent nothing on food, transport, or anything else. The green band on the left, three to five years, is roughly where an affordable market sits and where housing economists have historically drawn the line for "healthy". The dashed line at fifteen is the rough global median across major cities. And there, near the very top of the chart in amber, sit Hanoi and HCMC at thirty-one and thirty-two — alongside Hong Kong, Beijing, and Mumbai, in the small club of the world's least affordable places. A Vietnamese reader will not be surprised by where the bars land; the surprise, if there is one, is just how visible and how measurable the thing everyone *feels* turns out to be. By the end of this article you will be able to compute this number for any city, read what it means, and understand the forces that will eventually bring those amber bars back down — and how much it might hurt when they do.

A quick honesty note before we begin: this is educational, not individualized advice. The aim is to hand you the mechanism and the arithmetic so you can run your own numbers — not to tell you to buy, sell, rent, or wait.

## Foundations: the two affordability gauges

Let's build the whole idea from nothing. Forget house prices for a second and think about what "affordable" even means.

**Affordability** is not about whether something is cheap or expensive in absolute terms. A ₫7 billion flat is expensive; a ₫700 million plot in a far province is cheap. But "expensive" and "unaffordable" are different words pointing at different ideas, and conflating them is the single most common mistake in this entire subject. *Expensive* is a fact about the price tag. *Affordable* is a fact about the relationship between the price tag and your ability to pay. A sports car is expensive for almost everyone; it is unaffordable only for the people whose income can't carry it. Affordability is always a **ratio**, never a level — it is price measured in units of income, never price measured in đồng or dollars.

That single reframing — from *level* to *ratio* — is the key that unlocks everything that follows. The reason a headline like "HCMC apartments now average ₫111 million per square metre" tells you almost nothing on its own is that the number has no denominator. Per square metre compared to *what*? Expensive relative to *whose* wages? A price is just a number until you divide it by an income; only then does it become a statement about whether ordinary people can actually live there.

There are two ways to perform that division, and they measure two genuinely different things. Both matter, and a serious analyst watches both. The first asks: *how many years of income does the whole house cost?* The second asks: *how big a bite does the monthly payment take out of the monthly paycheque?* These are our two gauges — the price-to-income ratio and the payment-to-income ratio — and the rest of this section defines them and the supporting vocabulary from zero.

### Median versus mean (and why we always use the median)

Before we divide anything, we have to be careful about *which* income we use. When economists talk about a "typical" income or a "typical" home price, they almost always mean the **median**, not the **mean** (the average).

The **median** is the middle value: line every household up from poorest to richest, and the median household is the one standing exactly in the middle, with as many households below it as above. The **mean** is the arithmetic average: add everyone's income up and divide by the number of households. The two can be wildly different, and the gap between them is itself informative. Imagine ten households: nine earn ₫200 million a year and one earns ₫20 billion. The *median* income is ₫200 million (the middle household), but the *mean* is about ₫2.18 billion — more than ten times higher, dragged up by the single outlier. The mean describes a fictional household that doesn't exist; the median describes a household you'd actually meet.

For affordability we always use medians, on both sides of the ratio — the median home price over the median household income — because a handful of billionaires and a handful of palaces would otherwise distort the average beyond usefulness. When you read "price-to-income ratio of 32 in HCMC", read it as *the median home costs 32 years of the median household's income*. That precision matters, because it is exactly the median household — the ordinary family in the middle — whose ability to buy tells you whether a city's housing market is functioning or broken.

### Real versus nominal income

One more piece of vocabulary, because incomes are quoted in money and money loses value over time. **Nominal income** is the number on your payslip — ₫220 million a year, ₫18 million a month. **Real income** is that number adjusted for inflation, expressed in constant purchasing power. If your nominal wage rises 8% in a year but prices in the shops also rise 8%, your *real* income hasn't moved at all — you can buy exactly the same basket of goods. Only real income growth makes you genuinely better off.

This distinction is load-bearing for housing because the optimistic story people tell themselves — "don't worry, incomes will catch up to prices eventually" — is only true if *real* incomes rise faster than *real* house prices over a long stretch. Nominal wage growth that just keeps pace with inflation does nothing to close an affordability gap, because house prices tend to inflate too. So whenever someone waves away unaffordability with "wages always rise", the honest question is: rise in real terms, by how much, and faster than what? We'll return to this when we examine whether incomes really can catch up.

### The price-to-income ratio, defined

The **price-to-income ratio** (often abbreviated PIR, or just "price-to-income") is the median home price divided by the median annual household income:

$$\text{Price-to-income ratio} = \frac{\text{median home price}}{\text{median annual household income}}$$

The result is a pure number with units of *years* — the number of years a median household would need to work, spending nothing on anything else, to buy a median home outright with cash. A ratio of 5 means five years of total income buys a home; a ratio of 32 means thirty-two years. It is the cleanest single gauge of whether housing has come unmoored from wages, and it is the number plotted in Figure 1.

### The payment-to-income ratio, defined

The **payment-to-income ratio** (PTI, sometimes called the debt-service ratio or the housing-cost burden) asks a different and more immediate question: of the income that lands in your account each month, what fraction does the housing payment consume?

$$\text{Payment-to-income ratio} = \frac{\text{monthly housing payment}}{\text{monthly household income}}$$

Here the "housing payment" is the actual monthly mortgage payment — principal plus interest — for someone who buys with a loan, or the rent for someone who rents. The result is a percentage. A long-standing rule of thumb, dating back to mid-20th-century mortgage underwriting, says housing should eat no more than about **30%** of gross income; many lenders draw a harder line around **40%** including all debts, beyond which a household is officially "cost-burdened" and one shock away from trouble.

The crucial thing — and the reason we need *both* gauges — is that the payment-to-income ratio depends on something the price-to-income ratio is blind to: the **interest rate**. The price-to-income ratio compares a price to an income and never mentions financing. But almost nobody buys a home with cash; they buy it with a mortgage, and the monthly payment on that mortgage depends enormously on the interest rate. The same price can be comfortably affordable on a monthly basis at a 3% rate and crushingly unaffordable at 13%, even though the *price-to-income* ratio hasn't budged. That gap between the two gauges is where most of the drama in housing lives.

### Why ratios beat headline prices

We can now state the foundational principle plainly: **ratios beat headline prices, every time.** A headline price ("the average flat now costs ₫7 billion") is a number floating in space. A ratio ("the average flat costs 32 years of median income") is a *measurement* — it has a denominator, so it can be compared across cities, across decades, and against a sane benchmark. When a politician or a developer's marketing brochure quotes you a level, your first instinct should be to ask "compared to whose income?" and convert it into a ratio in your head. Only the ratio tells you whether you're looking at an expensive-but-functional market or an unaffordable-and-fragile one.

#### Worked example: the headline price says nothing

Suppose two cities both report an "average home price of \$300,000". City A has a median household income of \$100,000; City B's is \$30,000. In City A, the price-to-income ratio is \$300,000 ÷ \$100,000 = **3** — three years of income, deeply affordable. In City B, it's \$300,000 ÷ \$30,000 = **10** — ten years, badly stretched. *Same headline price, completely different worlds. The price tag told you nothing; the ratio told you everything.*

## The price-to-income ratio: how to read it

Now that we can compute the ratio, let's learn to read it — because a number on its own is just a number until you know what counts as healthy, what counts as worrying, and what counts as alarming.

The historical benchmarks are reasonably well established. A price-to-income ratio of **3 or below** has traditionally been called "affordable"; **3 to 4** is "moderately unaffordable"; **4 to 5** is "seriously unaffordable"; and anything **above 5** is "severely unaffordable" by the long-running international yardsticks (such as the Demographia survey, which has used these bands for two decades). By that scale, a ratio of 5 is already a red flag. So what do we make of a ratio of 32?

The honest answer is that the cities at the top of Figure 1 — Hong Kong at 44, Beijing at 35, HCMC at ~32, Hanoi at ~31, Mumbai at 28, Seoul at 26 — are not merely "severely unaffordable". They are off the end of the scale the framework was built to measure. The framework imagined that the worst markets might reach 8 or 9. A ratio in the thirties describes a market where the median household will *never* buy the median home out of wages within a working lifetime, full stop, and where the only buyers are top-decile earners, the recipients of family wealth, and investors. We'll dig into how a market reaches that state, and how it sustains it, in a moment.

It is worth being concrete about what the rule-of-thumb bands feel like from the buyer's side. At a ratio of 3, a couple can save a deposit in a few years and carry the loan on one-and-a-bit incomes — homeownership is a normal milestone of adult life. At a ratio of 8, it takes a decade of disciplined saving just for the deposit, and the loan stretches the budget hard. At a ratio of 32, the arithmetic simply breaks: the deposit alone is more than a decade of total savings, the loan is several times what any prudent lender would extend against the household's income, and the only way the transaction happens at all is if money comes from somewhere other than the buyer's own wages.

#### Worked example: Minh's price-to-income ratio

Let's put real numbers on Minh's flat. The asking price is ₫7.0 billion (≈ \$270,000). The *median* HCMC household income is roughly ₫220 million a year (≈ \$8,500) — note this is the median household, not Minh's own well-above-median income, because the ratio is built on medians. So:

$$\text{Price-to-income} = \frac{₫7{,}000\text{M}}{₫220\text{M/yr}} \approx 31.8 \text{ years}$$

About **32 years**. That is the number on the HCMC bar in Figure 1, and it is a startling thing to say out loud: *the median HCMC household, if it somehow spent zero đồng on food, clothing, transport, school, and everything else, and banked every single đồng it earned, would need thirty-two years to buy this flat with cash.* Of course no household can save 100% of its income; even an extraordinarily frugal family saving a fifth would need 160 years. *The ratio's real message is not "it takes 32 years to save up" — it is "this home is not priced for the median household at all; it is priced for someone else entirely."*

That last point is the one most people miss, so let's make it explicit. A price-to-income ratio of 32 doesn't mean the typical buyer takes 32 years to buy. It means the typical *household* is not the buyer. The actual buyers are the people whose incomes are several times the median — like Minh, in the top slice of earners — or households pooling two or three generations of savings, or investors buying with capital rather than wages. The ratio's height is the most precise possible statement of how far the market has drifted from the people it nominally serves.

To feel just how far that drift has gone, it helps to put the Vietnamese cities side by side with the two benchmarks that bracket a healthy market: the affordable target of about four years, and the global median of about fifteen.

![A bar chart comparing the years of income needed to buy a home, with the affordable benchmark at about four years and the global median at about fifteen shown as hatched reference bars, and Hanoi at thirty-one and HCMC at thirty-two shown as solid amber bars roughly eight times the affordable benchmark](/imgs/blogs/income-affordability-and-the-price-to-income-ratio-7.png)

Figure 7 makes the scale visceral. The two hatched bars on the left are the reference points: the affordable benchmark of about four years (the classic "a home should cost three to five times income" rule that underwrote a generation of functioning housing markets) and the global median of about fifteen years across major cities. Then the two solid amber bars: Hanoi at about thirty-one and HCMC at about thirty-two. The Vietnamese cities are roughly **eight times** the affordable benchmark and more than **twice** the global median. That multiple — eight times — is the number to carry away. It is not that HCMC is a little less affordable than a healthy city; it is that a home there costs eight times what an affordable market would charge in years of income. A household that could buy a home in four years in an affordable city would need thirty-two in HCMC. The bars are a different order of magnitude, and no amount of belt-tightening closes a gap of that size — only one of the three resolutions we'll meet later can.

It is worth pausing on *why* the affordable benchmark is around three to five years rather than, say, ten. The logic is mechanical. If a home costs about three to five years of household income, then a household saving a sensible 15–20% of income can accumulate a deposit in a handful of years, and a mortgage of three-to-five-times income can be serviced at normal interest rates for well under a third of the monthly paycheque. The whole apparatus of homeownership — save a deposit, carry a loan, own outright by retirement — works arithmetically at a ratio of four. At a ratio of thirty-two, every step of that apparatus jams: the deposit is unsaveable, the loan is several times what any prudent lender would extend, and the payment swallows the budget. The benchmark isn't an arbitrary nicety; it is roughly the highest ratio at which the normal machinery of buying a home still functions.

## The payment-to-income ratio: where rates enter

The price-to-income ratio is a stock measure — it compares a *stock* of value (the whole house) to a *flow* of income. But almost nobody pays for a house all at once. They borrow most of the price and repay it monthly over twenty or thirty years, which turns the giant stock into a manageable monthly flow. That transformation is the entire economic function of a mortgage, and it is why the payment-to-income ratio often matters more for whether a deal actually closes than the price-to-income ratio does.

Here is the mechanism. When you take a mortgage of principal $P$ at a monthly interest rate $r$ (the annual rate divided by twelve) over $n$ monthly payments, the fixed monthly payment $M$ that exactly pays the loan off is:

$$M = P \cdot \frac{r}{1 - (1+r)^{-n}}$$

You don't need to memorize the formula — every mortgage calculator computes it — but you do need to internalize one fact buried inside it: **the monthly payment is exquisitely sensitive to the interest rate.** Double the rate and the payment doesn't double, but it rises a lot, because the entire cost of borrowing is compounding against you every month for decades. This is the door through which interest rates walk into the affordability story.

Let's see it. We'll use Minh's flat: a ₫7.0 billion price, a 20% deposit of ₫1.4 billion (≈ \$54,000), and therefore a loan of **₫5.6 billion** (≈ \$216,000) over a 20-year term. And because the people who actually buy these flats earn far above the city median, we'll use a buyer household income of about ₫123 million a month (≈ \$4,750) — a genuine top-decile HCMC income, the kind of income it takes to even be in the room for a ₫7 billion flat.

![A two-panel before-and-after comparison showing the same five-point-six billion dong loan at an eight percent rate eating thirty-eight percent of income on the left and at a thirteen percent rate eating fifty-three percent of the same income on the right](/imgs/blogs/income-affordability-and-the-price-to-income-ratio-3.png)

Figure 3 shows what happens. On the left, at a **8% teaser rate** — the kind of preferential rate Vietnamese banks dangle for the first year or two — the monthly payment on the ₫5.6 billion loan is about ₫46.8 million. Against a ₫123 million monthly income, that's a payment-to-income ratio of about **38%** — right at the edge of the 40% comfort line, tight but liveable. The buyer signs, feeling stretched but okay. Then, on the right, the teaser expires and the rate **resets to 13%** — the floating rate Vietnamese mortgages snap to after the promotional period. The payment on the *identical* loan jumps to about ₫65.6 million a month, and the payment-to-income ratio leaps to about **53%**. The price didn't change. The loan didn't change. The income didn't change. Only the rate moved, and a household that was at the edge of comfort is now spending more than half of every paycheque on the mortgage — the definition of distress.

#### Worked example: payment-to-income at 8% versus 13%

Let's show the arithmetic in full, because the size of the swing is the whole point. Loan ₫5.6 billion, 20-year (240-month) term.

- **At 8% (monthly rate 0.6667%):** $M = ₫5{,}600\text{M} \times \frac{0.006667}{1 - 1.006667^{-240}} \approx ₫46.8$ million per month. Against ₫123M income: **38%**.
- **At 13% (monthly rate 1.0833%):** $M = ₫5{,}600\text{M} \times \frac{0.010833}{1 - 1.010833^{-240}} \approx ₫65.6$ million per month. Against ₫123M income: **53%**.

The payment rose by ₫18.8 million a month — nearly ₫226 million a year, about \$8,700 — purely from the rate reset. *That is why the interest rate is not a footnote to affordability; it is half the story. A price you can carry at one rate can bury you at another, and the most dangerous mortgages are the ones that show you the friendly rate first and the real one later.* We have a whole sibling piece on this dynamic — see [interest rates and mortgages, the master variable](/blog/trading/real-estate/interest-rates-and-mortgages-the-master-variable).

This is also why two cities with the *same* price-to-income ratio can feel completely different to live in. A city at a ratio of 10 where mortgages cost 3% is far more *livable* — far lower payment-to-income — than a city at a ratio of 10 where mortgages cost 13%. The stock looks identical; the flow does not. Affordability, properly understood, is never a single number.

There's a useful piece of vocabulary that flows directly out of the payment-to-income ratio: a household is called **cost-burdened** when housing eats more than 30% of its gross income, and **severely cost-burdened** when it eats more than 50%. These aren't arbitrary lines; they come from decades of housing research showing that once housing crosses roughly a third of income, families systematically cut back on food, healthcare, and savings to make the payment, and once it crosses half, they are one missed paycheque from missing the mortgage. By that standard, the buyer in Figure 3 starts the relationship merely stretched (38%, approaching cost-burdened) and ends it severely cost-burdened (53%) the moment the teaser expires — without buying a more expensive home, without losing any income, purely because a number on a loan document changed. The payment-to-income ratio is the gauge that tells you which side of those lines a household sits on, and it is the gauge that moves when rates move.

#### Worked example: the same payment-to-income at three different rates

To see how completely the interest rate, not the price, governs the monthly burden, hold the *payment-to-income ratio* fixed at a comfortable 35% on a ₫123 million monthly income — a payment of about ₫43 million a month — and ask how big a loan that payment can carry at different rates, over a 20-year term:

- **At 5%:** ₫43M/month services a loan of about **₫6.5 billion**.
- **At 8%:** the same ₫43M/month services only about **₫5.1 billion**.
- **At 13%:** the same ₫43M/month services only about **₫3.7 billion**.

The household's budget didn't change — it can always comfortably pay ₫43 million a month. But the *home* that budget can buy shrank from ₫6.5 billion to ₫3.7 billion, a 43% collapse in purchasing power, purely because the rate tripled. *This is the deepest reason rising rates are poison for an expensive market: they don't just raise payments, they shrink the size of home every income can reach, and when every buyer can suddenly afford 43% less house, prices have nowhere to go but down. The rate doesn't appear in the price-to-income ratio, but it sets the price the market can sustain.*

## The affordability triangle: price, income, and rates move together

We now have the three levers that jointly determine whether a household can buy: the **price** of the home, the household's **income**, and the prevailing **interest rate**. Affordability is the output of all three together, and the cleanest way to hold them in your head is as a triangle — change any corner and the whole shape of affordability shifts.

![A three-by-three matrix showing how price, income, and the interest rate each affect affordability when they move up or down, with a higher price and a higher rate hurting affordability and a higher income and a lower rate helping it](/imgs/blogs/income-affordability-and-the-price-to-income-ratio-2.png)

Figure 2 lays out the logic. Read each row as one lever:

- **Price** sits in the numerator of the price-to-income ratio. A higher price means more years to buy and a bigger loan, so a bigger monthly payment — affordability worsens. A lower price helps. Straightforward.
- **Income** sits in the denominator. A higher income, for the same price, means fewer years to buy and a smaller share of the paycheque consumed — affordability improves. Income is the *long-run gravity* of the whole system: in a healthy market, rising real incomes are what keep prices anchored.
- **The interest rate** is the hidden third lever, and it acts only on the *payment*, not on the price. A lower rate shrinks the monthly payment on any given price — which is exactly how a falling-rate environment can make a sky-high price feel temporarily affordable. A higher rate does the reverse, inflating the payment without touching the price.

The reason this triangle matters so much is that the three corners *interact*, often perversely. The most important interaction in modern housing is this: **falling interest rates can mask a rising price-to-income ratio.** When central banks cut rates toward zero, as much of the world did through the 2010s and again in 2020–21, the monthly payment on a given price falls — so buyers can "afford" more, so they bid prices up, so the price-to-income ratio climbs even as the *payment*-to-income ratio stays flat or even falls. For years, it can look like affordability is fine (payments are manageable) while the underlying price-to-income ratio quietly inflates to a dangerous level. Then rates rise — and the masking effect reverses violently. The payment-to-income ratio spikes overnight, exposing the price-to-income ratio that was always there underneath. This is precisely the trap we'll see in the US 2006 story and in Vietnam's own teaser-rate mortgages.

#### Worked example: a rate cut that hides a price surge

Suppose a flat costs \$200,000 and a household earns \$50,000 a year, for a price-to-income ratio of **4** — affordable. The mortgage rate is 6%, and on a \$160,000 loan (20% down) over 30 years the payment is about \$959/month, or **23%** of the \$50,000 income — comfortable. Now the central bank cuts the rate to 3%. The payment on the same loan falls to about \$675/month, just **16%** of income. Buyers, feeling flush, bid the *price* up to \$280,000. The new loan is \$224,000, the new payment at 3% is about \$944/month — back to **23%** of income, exactly where it started. *The payment-to-income ratio is unchanged, so it feels like nothing happened — but the price-to-income ratio has jumped from 4 to 5.6. The rate cut didn't make housing more affordable; it converted lower rates straight into higher prices, and left the household more exposed the moment rates rise back.*

## Why HCMC and Hanoi are so unaffordable

So how does a city arrive at a price-to-income ratio of 32 — six times the "severely unaffordable" threshold — and stay there? Vietnam's two big cities did not get there by accident, and understanding the mechanism is the most useful thing in this article for a local reader, because the same forces are still operating.

![A line chart illustrating HCMC's price-to-income ratio rising from about twenty years a decade ago to about thirty-two today, with the affordable band of three to five years shaded green near the bottom, the global median of about fifteen marked with a dashed line, and the widening gap above the median shaded red](/imgs/blogs/income-affordability-and-the-price-to-income-ratio-4.png)

Figure 4 shows the trajectory — and the shape of the line is the whole story. A decade ago, HCMC's price-to-income ratio was already high by international standards, somewhere around twenty years, but it sat well below where it is now. Year after year, prices ran faster than incomes, and the ratio climbed steadily up through the twenties and into the thirties, opening an ever-wider gap above the global median of fifteen (the red-shaded region). The figure is flagged illustrative — the precise yearly path is a stylized rising curve anchored on the current ~32 level, not an official historical series — but the direction and the order of magnitude are exactly right, and they capture the mechanism plainly: this is what it looks like when the numerator sprints and the denominator walks. Let's take the two halves of that race in turn.

Start with the **numerator running fast**. HCMC and Hanoi have seen primary apartment prices rise at a blistering pace — Hanoi's average primary price went from around ₫72 million per square metre in 2024 to around ₫100 million in 2025 (a roughly 40% jump in a single year), and HCMC from around ₫91 million to around ₫111 million per square metre over the same span. Prices like that, compounding year after year, are the numerator of the price-to-income ratio sprinting away.

Now the **denominator crawling behind**. Vietnamese incomes have grown impressively by global standards — real GDP per capita has multiplied many times over the past two decades — but they have grown from a low base and nowhere near fast enough to keep up with property. A median HCMC household income of ~₫220 million a year is a genuine achievement relative to a generation ago, but set against a ₫7 billion flat it produces a ratio of 32. When the numerator runs at 30–40% a year and the denominator at single digits, the ratio can only go one way.

Then layer on the **structural forces** that keep the gap wide:

- **Land is scarce and supply is slow.** Both cities are hemmed in, and the regulatory and infrastructure machinery for delivering new housing — especially affordable housing — is slow and bottlenecked. When demand surges and supply can't respond, price absorbs the entire imbalance. (We dig into this in the sibling piece on [supply elasticity](/blog/trading/real-estate/supply-elasticity-why-some-cities-boom-and-bust).)
- **Property is the default store of wealth.** In a country with limited deep financial markets and a cultural and historical preference for tangible assets, real estate — and land in particular — has been *the* place Vietnamese households park savings. That converts a huge pool of capital into property demand that has nothing to do with needing somewhere to live, and it bids prices far above what local wages alone would support.
- **Speculation and investor demand.** A large share of urban apartment purchases are investment buys, not owner-occupier buys — second, third, and fourth units bought to hold and flip. Investor demand is income-blind: it cares about expected appreciation, not about whether a local family could afford the place. That decouples price from local income by construction.
- **A demographic and urbanization wave.** A young, urbanizing population pours into the two big cities faster than housing can be built for them, sustaining demand at the bottom even as prices climb at the top. (See [demographics and urbanization, the demand wave](/blog/trading/real-estate/demographics-and-urbanization-the-demand-wave).)

The result is a market where price has been driven by capital flows, speculation, scarcity, and store-of-wealth demand — forces that have very little to do with local wages — while affordability is, by definition, all about local wages. The two have drifted apart, and the ratio of 32 is the precise measure of how far. VnExpress, citing international affordability surveys, has put HCMC's ratio as high as 34 years of income, the fifth-highest of any major city on Earth. Whether the true figure is 30 or 32 or 34, the message is identical: these are among the least affordable cities in the world, and they got that way because everything that drives the numerator was given free rein while the denominator was left to crawl.

#### Worked example: how many years to save the deposit alone

People imagine the hard part of an unaffordable market is the mortgage. Often the deposit is harder. Minh's flat needs a 20% deposit of ₫1.4 billion (≈ \$54,000). Suppose a disciplined median-ish household saves 20% of a ₫220 million income — that's ₫44 million a year. Saving the deposit alone takes ₫1,400M ÷ ₫44M ≈ **32 years** — and that's before the mortgage even begins, and it assumes the price doesn't rise while you save (it does). *In an unaffordable market the deposit is not a hurdle on the way to the home; it is a wall. This is why, in practice, almost every young buyer in these cities is funded by family — the deposit comes from parents and grandparents pooling a lifetime of savings, because a single income simply cannot scale that wall in a working life.* That family-pooling is itself a form of credit stretching, which is exactly where we go next.

## What happens when affordability breaks: the three resolutions

Here is the iron law of the whole subject, and the reason affordability is gravity rather than a mere statistic: **a price-to-income ratio cannot rise forever.** A ratio is a fraction, price over income, and a fraction in the thirties is unstable. Something has to give. And there are only three things that *can* give — only three ways the gap between prices and incomes can ever close. Everything that happens in an unaffordable market, every boom and every bust, is one of these three resolutions or a combination of them.

![A four-row grid laying out the three ways an affordability gap can close, with prices stalling or falling as the painful resolution, incomes catching up as the healthy one, and credit stretching as the dangerous one, each shown alongside its mechanism and its risk](/imgs/blogs/income-affordability-and-the-price-to-income-ratio-6.png)

Figure 6 lays them out. Let's take each in turn.

**Resolution one: prices stall or fall.** The numerator of the ratio comes down (or stops rising while incomes catch up). This is the most direct way to fix unaffordability, and the most painful, because falling prices in a leveraged market are how crashes happen. If prices fall 20%, a ratio of 32 drops to about 26 in one move. The mechanism is clean; the consequences are brutal. Falling prices push leveraged owners into **negative equity** (owing more than the home is worth), which triggers defaults, which force more sales, which push prices down further — the doom loop of a property crash. So while a price correction is the most honest cure for unaffordability, it is the one nobody in the market wants, because it lands hardest on the most recent, most leveraged buyers. (We anatomize this loop in [the anatomy of a property bubble and crash](/blog/trading/real-estate/anatomy-of-a-property-bubble-and-crash).)

**Resolution two: incomes catch up.** The denominator rises to meet the price. This is the *healthy* resolution — the one where nobody loses money — and it is how genuinely fast-growing economies can grow into prices that looked unaffordable at the time. If real incomes rise faster than prices for long enough, the ratio drifts down without a single price falling. The catch is that it is *slow*, and it is rare for incomes to durably outpace prices, because the same prosperity that lifts incomes also lifts the demand for, and the price of, scarce urban land. Incomes catching up is the resolution everyone hopes for and few markets actually get, at least not fast enough to do all the work alone.

**Resolution three: credit stretches.** This is the seductive, dangerous one, and it deserves its own section, which follows. Instead of the price falling or the income rising, the *financing* changes so that the same price fits a smaller monthly payment — longer loan terms, lower rates, higher loan-to-value ratios, family pooling. Credit stretching doesn't fix the affordability gap; it *hides* it, by letting unchanged prices be carried on unchanged incomes through financial engineering. It buys real affordability today and builds real fragility for tomorrow. When a market resolves unaffordability mainly through credit stretching, it is not healing — it is loading a spring.

#### Worked example: a 20% price fall versus 5%-a-year income growth

Let's race resolution one against resolution two on Minh's flat (ratio ≈ 32 at a ₫7 billion price and ₫220 million income).

- **Resolution one — a 20% price fall:** price drops to ₫5.6 billion, income unchanged. New ratio = ₫5,600M ÷ ₫220M ≈ **25.5**. One sharp, painful move from 32 to 25.5.
- **Resolution two — incomes grow 7% a year, price flat:** after ten years, income compounds to ₫220M × 1.07^10 ≈ ₫433M. New ratio = ₫7,000M ÷ ₫433M ≈ **16.2**. A whole decade of strong income growth, with prices frozen, gets you from 32 down to 16 — better than the crash, but it took ten years and required prices to do something they rarely do, which is nothing.

*Notice how much more the slow resolution accomplishes if you can get it — a decade of 7% real income growth nearly halves the ratio, far more than a 20% crash — but notice too how much it asks: ten patient years and frozen prices. That gap between what's healthiest and what's likeliest is the whole tragedy of unaffordable markets, and it is why so many of them reach instead for the third resolution.*

## How credit stretches to paper over unaffordability

Because the painful resolution (prices fall) is the one everyone resists and the healthy one (incomes catch up) is too slow, markets reach instinctively for the third: stretch the credit. It is worth understanding this mechanism in detail, because it is simultaneously the most powerful affordability tool ever invented and the seed of nearly every property crash in history.

![A flow diagram showing prices outrunning incomes leading to credit stretching through longer terms, higher loan-to-value, and family pooling, which produces temporary affordability and lasting fragility at the same time](/imgs/blogs/income-affordability-and-the-price-to-income-ratio-5.png)

Figure 5 traces the logic. It starts with the gap: prices have outrun incomes, the flat costs 32 years of income, and on paper nobody local can buy it. Then credit stretches — and notice in the figure that the single act of stretching produces *two* outcomes at once. It produces **temporary affordability**, because the monthly payment now fits today's income. And it produces **fragility**, because the household now has no buffer for any shock. These are not two separate things; they are the same thing seen from two angles. The stretch that makes the home affordable is exactly the stretch that makes the household fragile.

What does "stretching credit" actually mean in practice? Several levers, often used together:

- **Longer loan terms.** Spreading the same loan over 30 years instead of 20, or even introducing 40-year mortgages, shrinks the monthly payment. It does nothing to the price or the total interest paid (which rises); it just slices the same debt into thinner monthly pieces.
- **Lower interest rates.** When the central bank cuts, the payment on any given price falls — the masking effect from the affordability-triangle section. Cheap credit makes high prices feel cheap to carry.
- **Higher loan-to-value ratios.** Lending 90% or 95% of the price instead of 70–80% means the buyer needs a smaller deposit, scaling the deposit wall. It also means more leverage, so a small price fall wipes out the buyer's equity faster.
- **Teaser rates that reset.** The Vietnamese specialty: a low promotional rate (say 8%) for the first year or two, then a reset to the real floating rate (12–14%). The teaser makes the deal look affordable at signing; the reset reveals the truth a year later — exactly the swing in Figure 3.
- **Family pooling.** Three generations contribute to one deposit. Economically this is the household borrowing against its extended family's lifetime savings — a real and common form of stretch in Vietnam, and the main reason any young buyer transacts in a ratio-32 market at all.

Each of these makes an unaffordable home affordable *this month*. None of them makes it affordable in any deeper sense, because none of them changes the underlying fact that the price is 32 years of income. They redistribute the strain across time (longer terms), across the rate cycle (lower rates and teasers), or across the family (pooling) — but the strain is still there, and it is larger than before, because the household is now more leveraged and less buffered than it was. That is why credit stretching is the dangerous resolution. It feels like a solution and behaves like a fuse.

#### Worked example: how a longer loan tenor masks unaffordability

Take Minh's ₫5.6 billion loan at the real 13% rate and watch what stretching the term does to the monthly payment:

- **10-year term:** about ₫83.6 million per month.
- **20-year term:** about ₫65.6 million per month.
- **30-year term:** about ₫61.9 million per month.

Stretching from 10 to 30 years cuts the monthly payment from ₫83.6M to ₫61.9M — a 26% reduction — and makes a payment a household couldn't carry at 10 years suddenly fit the budget at 30. But the household now pays the bank for thirty years instead of ten, the total interest balloons, and equity builds at a glacial pace in the early years, so the buyer is exposed to negative equity for far longer if prices wobble. *The longer tenor didn't make the home cheaper; it made the monthly payment smaller and the household's situation more fragile. That trade — a smaller payment now for a longer, riskier debt later — is the entire mechanism by which unaffordable markets keep transacting, and the entire reason they're fragile.* This is leverage doing what leverage does, a theme we develop in [leverage and the mortgage](/blog/trading/real-estate/leverage-and-the-mortgage-how-debt-amplifies-property).

## Common misconceptions

Affordability is a subject riddled with comfortable half-truths. Here are the ones worth correcting.

**"High prices mean a bubble."** Not necessarily. A high price-to-income ratio is a *risk factor*, not proof of a bubble. Some cities sustain elevated ratios for decades because of genuine, durable scarcity — land that truly cannot be expanded, like Hong Kong's, or persistent capital inflows. A bubble is specifically a price level that *cannot be sustained by fundamentals and is held up only by the expectation of further price rises* — it is about the *cause* of the price, not just its height. A ratio of 32 driven by real, persistent scarcity is dangerous-but-stable; the same ratio driven by leveraged speculation and teaser-rate credit is a bubble waiting to pop. The ratio tells you something is stretched; it doesn't, by itself, tell you whether it's a bubble. (For what actually makes a bubble, see [the anatomy of a property bubble and crash](/blog/trading/real-estate/anatomy-of-a-property-bubble-and-crash).)

**"Affordability is about price alone."** This is the single most common error, and the whole affordability-triangle section was built to bury it. Affordability is price *relative to income*, carried at a *given interest rate*. A market can become dramatically less affordable with no change in price at all — just a rate rise. It can become more affordable with no price fall — just rising incomes or falling rates. Watching prices alone, you will miss half the changes in affordability and most of the dangerous ones.

**"Incomes will always catch up."** The hope that wages will rise to meet prices is the favourite reassurance of every overheated market, and it is sometimes true and often not. It requires *real* income growth (not just nominal) to outpace *real* price growth, durably, for years — and the same prosperity that lifts incomes usually lifts land prices too, so the gap doesn't close as fast as the optimists assume. Japan is the cautionary tale: incomes did *not* catch up to 1989 prices; instead prices fell for two decades until they met incomes. Betting on resolution two is betting on the slowest and least reliable of the three.

**"A teaser rate I can afford means I can afford the house."** No — it means you can afford the teaser. Vietnamese mortgages routinely offer a low promotional rate for the first 12–24 months, then reset to a floating rate of 12–14%. The honest affordability test is always the *reset* rate, not the teaser. If the payment at 13% would break your budget, you cannot afford the house; you can only afford the first year of it. Figure 3 is the whole warning in one picture.

**"Renting is throwing money away, so any purchase is better."** A separate but related myth that pushes people to buy into unaffordable markets at any price. In a market with a price-to-income ratio of 32 and a correspondingly low rental yield, the math frequently favours renting and investing the difference — the rent is far cheaper than the true cost of carrying the home. Affordability and the rent-versus-buy decision are tightly linked; an unaffordable market is often precisely the kind where renting wins. (We run that math in full in [rent vs buy, the real math](/blog/trading/real-estate/rent-vs-buy-the-real-math).)

## How it shows up in real markets

Enough theory. Here is the affordability gravity at work in real cities, with real numbers and real dates.

**HCMC and Hanoi today — the extremes.** As of mid-2026, HCMC sits at a price-to-income ratio of roughly 32 (VnExpress, citing international surveys, has quoted figures as high as 34, calling it the fifth-least-affordable major city on Earth), and Hanoi at roughly 31. These are not numbers from a crisis decade ago — they are *current*, and they coexist with prices still rising 30–40% a year in the primary market. The two cities are a live demonstration of resolution three: the gap between ratio-32 prices and median incomes is being bridged almost entirely by credit stretching and family pooling, with teaser-rate mortgages that reset to 12–14% as the dominant financing. The fragility that builds in such a market is exactly what surfaced in Vietnam's own 2022–23 episode, when the corporate-bond freeze and rate spike froze transactions and stranded leveraged developers and buyers — a reminder that a market held up by stretched credit is a market exposed to anything that tightens credit. (See [Vietnam property cycles, 2007, 2013, 2022](/blog/trading/real-estate/vietnam-property-cycles-2007-2013-2022).)

**The United States, 2006 versus now.** The American housing bubble of the mid-2000s is the textbook case of affordability masked by credit stretching, then violently unmasked. Through 2003–2006, rock-bottom teaser rates, exotic mortgages (interest-only, negative-amortization, "no-doc" loans), and ever-higher loan-to-value ratios let prices race far ahead of incomes while monthly payments stayed deceptively manageable. The national house-price index (S&P/Cotality Case-Shiller) peaked at about **184.6 in July 2006**. Then the teasers reset, the credit stretch snapped back, and resolution one took over: prices fell to a trough of about **134 by February 2012**, a peak-to-trough decline of roughly **27%**. Affordability was restored the painful way — through a crash that wiped out trillions in household wealth — precisely because it had been faked the dangerous way for years beforehand. (US prices have since more than recovered, reaching ~330 by early 2026, but on the back of *much* higher incomes and, until 2022, much lower rates.)

**Japan's long normalization.** Tokyo in the late 1980s was the most extreme affordability blowout in modern history — at the 1989–1990 peak, the land under the Imperial Palace was famously said to be worth more than all the real estate in California, and price-to-income ratios in central Tokyo reached the high teens and beyond. Crucially, incomes did *not* catch up. Instead, resolution one ground on for two full decades: Japanese land prices fell, year after year, through the 1990s and 2000s, until prices finally met incomes again. Today Tokyo's price-to-income ratio is around **15** — close to the global median, and *more affordable* than HCMC or Hanoi — not because Tokyo got cheaper in absolute terms overnight, but because a generation of falling prices and slowly rising incomes brought the ratio back down. Japan is the proof that the gravity is real: a ratio in the high teens or twenties is not a permanent plateau, it is a stretched spring, and the spring can take twenty years to unwind.

**Hong Kong — the durable extreme.** At a ratio around **44**, Hong Kong is the least affordable major market on the planet, and it has stayed extreme for decades — which seems to contradict the "ratios can't rise forever" law. It doesn't, quite. Hong Kong's extreme is held up by genuinely fixed land supply (a small territory with deliberately constrained land release) and persistent mainland capital inflows — durable structural forces, not just leveraged speculation. That makes it dangerous-but-stable rather than a classic bubble: it can stay unaffordable far longer than a credit-driven market, but it is also acutely sensitive to anything that changes the capital flows or the land policy. Hong Kong shows that the *level* of the ratio tells you how stretched a market is, but the *cause* of the ratio tells you how it will end.

**Singapore and the policy lever.** Singapore sits at a ratio around **17** — high, but far below Hong Kong or the Vietnamese cities — and that gap is largely deliberate policy. Through a dominant public-housing program (the HDB flats most citizens actually live in), aggressive cooling measures (stamp duties on additional and foreign purchases), and managed land release, Singapore has kept owner-occupier affordability in check even as its private-market and investor-grade properties command rich prices. It is the clearest real-world demonstration that a city's affordability is not fate — it is the product of supply policy, credit policy, and tax policy, all of which a government can choose to deploy or not.

## When this matters / further reading

The price-to-income ratio is the single most useful number you can carry in your head about any housing market, because it cuts through every headline, every marketing brochure, and every confident assertion that "property only goes up". The next time you read that a city's average home now costs some impressive sum, do the division: against the median income, how many years is that? If the answer is three to five, the market is anchored to the people who live in it. If the answer is in the twenties or thirties, the market has come unmoored — held aloft by capital, scarcity, speculation, and stretched credit — and you are looking at one of the three resolutions waiting to happen.

For you personally, the two gauges translate into two questions you should always ask before the biggest purchase of your life. The price-to-income question: *is this home priced for someone like me, or for a buyer several income brackets up?* And the payment-to-income question, computed at the *reset* rate, not the teaser: *what share of my real monthly income will this payment eat when the friendly rate expires — and could I still carry it if rates rose another two or three points, or if one income disappeared?* If the honest answers are "for someone else" and "more than half", the gravity is telling you something, and the gravity always wins in the end.
