> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Demographics are the slow, almost unstoppable tide beneath housing demand. What moves that tide is not the raw population count but **household formation** — the number of new *households* that each need a roof — and household formation is driven by age structure, the timing of marriage and divorce, the steady **shrinking of household size**, and **migration**. Urbanization then concentrates all of that demand into a handful of cities. Vietnam sits in the powerful middle of its urbanization wave — young, fast-urbanizing, with household size still falling — which is a structural tailwind under urban housing for decades. Much of the developed world (Japan, parts of Europe, increasingly China) faces the exact reverse: aging, shrinking, and a demand *drag*.
>
> - **Housing demand tracks households, not headcount.** A city whose population is flat but whose household size falls from 5.0 to 2.5 needs *twice* as many homes. The number to remember: homes needed ≈ population ÷ household size.
> - **Five forces drive household formation** — young cohorts reaching home-forming age, later marriage, more divorce, shrinking household size, and net migration. In Vietnam all five currently point toward *more* households; in Japan they point the other way.
> - **Urbanization is an S-curve.** Vietnam is at ~40% urban and rising ~1 point a year toward the developed-world plateau of 70–80% — the steepest, fastest-demand stretch of the whole curve. Japan is at ~92%, plateaued, and its rural towns are emptying into vacant *akiya*.
> - **Demographics are powerful but slow, not destiny on any timeframe.** A young population is a tailwind over decades; over the next 18 months, credit and policy still set the price.

In 2009, a 24-year-old we'll call **Minh** finished an engineering degree in a small town in the Mekong Delta and did what millions of young Vietnamese were doing that decade: he got on a bus to Ho Chi Minh City. He moved into a rented room in a shared house in District 12, found a job, and for several years was statistically invisible to the housing market — he wasn't a buyer, he wasn't even his own household, just one more person crammed into someone else's roof. Then, around 2016, three things happened almost together. He got a real salary. He got married. And he and his wife rented their own small apartment, becoming, for the first time, a *household* — a single economic unit that needs exactly one home. A few years later they were shopping for a flat to buy. Minh, the person, had been in HCMC for seven years before Minh, the *unit of housing demand*, fully arrived.

Multiply Minh by tens of millions, stretch it across thirty years, and you have the single most powerful force in all of real estate — more powerful, over long horizons, than interest rates, credit cycles, or government policy. It is the slow, almost geological pressure of **demographics**: the changing number, age, and living arrangements of people, and the way cities pull them in. It is slow enough that it disappears from view in any given year, which is exactly why it is so underrated. Nobody trades on it. It doesn't make headlines. And yet it is the tide under everything else's waves.

The trap most people fall into is thinking demographics means *population*. "More people, more demand for housing, higher prices" — it sounds obviously true, and it is mostly wrong, or at least badly incomplete. Housing isn't demanded by *people*; it's demanded by *households*. And the number of households can rise fast even when the population is flat, or fall even when the population is still growing. The figure below is the mental model for the entire post: hold the population perfectly constant, shrink the average household, and watch the number of homes a city needs climb anyway.

![Two panels showing the same one thousand people needing two hundred homes when household size is five but four hundred homes when household size falls to two and a half, doubling housing demand with zero population growth](/imgs/blogs/demographics-and-urbanization-the-demand-wave-1.png)

We'll keep two characters honest throughout. **Minh** is our HCMC household-in-formation — young, urbanizing, the demographic tailwind made personal. And we'll compare him repeatedly to **Japan**, the clearest case on Earth of the same forces running in reverse: an old, shrinking, fully-urbanized country whose villages are filling with empty houses. Vietnam and Japan are the two poles of this story, and watching them side by side will make the abstraction concrete. (This is educational, not investment advice — we're explaining a mechanism, not telling anyone to buy or sell.)

## Foundations: population, households, and what actually demands a home

Before we can talk about waves and tides we need a small vocabulary, all built from zero. Five terms do almost all the work: **population**, **household**, **household formation**, **household size**, and the **headship rate**. Get these straight and most of the confusion around "demographics and housing" simply evaporates.

### Population vs households: the distinction that changes everything

The **population** of a place is just the headcount — the total number of people living there. Vietnam's population is roughly 100 million; HCMC's is somewhere around 9–10 million depending on how you draw the lines. Easy.

A **household** is something different and more important for housing: it is a group of people who live together in one dwelling and share its economics — typically a family, but also a single person living alone, a couple, or a few unrelated flatmates pooling rent. The defining feature is that **one household occupies one home**. A household is the actual *unit of housing demand*. People don't each need a house; *households* do. Minh, his wife, and (later) their child are three people but one household, needing one apartment.

This is the hinge of the whole post, so let's say it as plainly as possible: **the housing market is driven by the number of households, not the number of people.** A country could add ten million people and need very few new homes if those people are absorbed into existing households (kids born into families that already have a home, young adults staying with parents). And a country could add *zero* people and still need millions of new homes if its households keep splitting into smaller ones. Population is the raw material; households are what actually pulls a home off the market.

### Household formation: the real demand variable

**Household formation** is the flow that matters: the *net new households* created in a period. Every time someone moves out of their parents' home into their own rented room, a household forms. Every time a couple who lived with in-laws gets their own flat, a household forms. Every time a marriage dissolves and one home becomes two, a household forms. And households *dissolve* too — when an elderly person dies and their home empties, when adult children move back in with parents during a recession, when two people marry and merge two homes into one.

Net household formation = households formed − households dissolved. *That* number, not population growth, is the true driver of how many homes a market needs each year. When you read that a country "needs to build 1.5 million homes a year," that figure comes from a household-formation forecast, not a population one.

### Household size: people per home, and why it only ever seems to fall

**Household size** is simply the average number of people per household — population divided by number of households. Vietnam's has been falling for decades, from well over 4 people per household toward roughly 3.5 today and heading lower; rich countries are down around 2.2–2.5; Japan is near 2.2 and still declining, with single-person households now the most common type.

Why does household size fall almost everywhere as a country develops? Several reasons stack up. Birth rates fall, so there are fewer children per home. Multi-generational living gives way to nuclear families as incomes rise and young people can afford their own place. People marry later, spending years living alone first. Divorce rises. People live longer, leaving more elderly-only and single-person households. Every one of these forces pushes the *same* population into *more, smaller* households — and every new household needs its own roof. This is why a falling household size is, mechanically, a *rise* in housing demand even with a flat population. It's the headship effect, and it's the engine in Figure 1.

### The headship rate: the formal version of the same idea

If you want the demographer's precise tool, it's the **headship rate**: the share of people in a given age group who are the "head" of their own household — i.e., who have their own home rather than living in someone else's. A 22-year-old living with parents is not a household head; the same person at 30, renting their own flat, is. Multiply each age group's population by its headship rate, sum across all ages, and you get the total number of households — and therefore the total number of homes a country needs.

This formalizes why *age structure* matters so much. Headship rates are low for the young (teenagers almost never head a household), rise sharply through the late 20s and 30s as people leave home, marry, and set up on their own, stay high through middle age, and then complicate in old age. So the number of households a country generates depends heavily on **how many people sit in the high-headship, household-forming ages of roughly 25–40**. A country with a bulge of people in that band is a household-formation machine. A country whose bulge has aged past it is running down.

### How analysts actually forecast housing demand

It's worth seeing how professionals turn these definitions into a number, because it shows which inputs really matter. A housing-demand forecast is, at its core, a forecast of the *change in the number of households*, and it's built in three steps. First, take the projected population by age band (demographers can do this decades out with real confidence, because most of the relevant people are already born). Second, multiply each age band by its projected headship rate to get the number of households that band will generate. Third, take the difference between this year's household total and next year's — that's net household formation, the new homes the market needs — and add a replacement allowance for homes lost to demolition, fire, or obsolescence.

Notice what's *in* and what's *out*. Population growth is in, but only as one input filtered through headship rates. Household-size shrinkage is in, captured by *rising* headship rates as more young people set up on their own. Migration is in, because it moves people between regions with different headship profiles. What's strikingly *not* in this demand calculation is price, interest rates, or the economy — those affect whether the demand gets *satisfied* and at what price, but the underlying *need* for households is a demographic fact that exists whether or not anyone can afford to meet it. That's why demographics is the demand *floor*: it tells you how many homes a society fundamentally needs, before economics decides how many get built and how much they cost.

#### Worked example: how a falling household size builds demand from nothing

Let's put real arithmetic on the headship effect, because it is the most counterintuitive idea in the post. Take a city of exactly **1,000,000 people** whose population is *not growing at all* — flat as a table. At the start, the average household size is **4.0**. So the number of households is:

`1,000,000 ÷ 4.0 = 250,000 households` → 250,000 homes needed.

Now let one decade pass. The population is *still* 1,000,000 — not one extra person. But over that decade, as incomes rose and young people set up on their own, the average household size fell to **3.5**. Now:

`1,000,000 ÷ 3.5 ≈ 285,700 households` → about 285,700 homes needed.

The city needs **35,700 more homes** — a **+14% jump in housing demand** — with **zero population growth**. The homes are demanded not by new people but by the *unbundling* of existing people into smaller units. Push the household size to 2.5 (where rich-world cities live) and the same million people need 400,000 homes — a 60% increase over the starting point, all from shrinkage. *Population growth grabs the headlines, but in a developing country the quiet fall in household size can demand just as many new homes as the babies do.*

## Shrinking household size: more units per person, decade after decade

That worked example is worth lingering on, because it overturns the most common instinct in the entire subject. Walk up to anyone and ask "what drives housing demand?" and they'll say "population growth." But in a country like Vietnam, a large share of the demand for the next two decades will come not from *more Vietnamese* but from the *same* Vietnamese spreading out into smaller households.

### Why the shrink is so reliable

Falling household size is one of the most dependable trends in all of demography. It moves in one direction across almost every country that develops, and it rarely reverses for long. The drivers compound:

- **Fewer children.** As Vietnam's total fertility rate fell from over 6 children per woman in the 1960s to around replacement (~2.0) and now slightly below in the cities, the average family simply has fewer kids under one roof.
- **The death of multi-generational living.** Historically, a Vietnamese household often meant grandparents, parents, and children — sometimes married siblings too — all in one dwelling. As young couples can afford their own place, they leave, splitting one big household into two or three smaller ones.
- **Later marriage and more years living solo.** A generation ago, many Vietnamese married in their early 20s and went more or less straight from their parents' home to a marital one. Now urban professionals routinely marry in their late 20s or 30s, and those extra single years are increasingly spent in their *own* rented place — a brand-new household that didn't exist before.
- **More divorce and longer lives.** Both create single-person households where there used to be one shared one.

### Why this is a slow but enormous force

Here is the thing about household size: it changes slowly — maybe a tenth of a person per year — but it acts on the *entire* population at once, which makes the cumulative effect huge. A country doesn't shrink its household size by 0.5 in a dramatic year; it does it over a decade, almost invisibly. But applied to a hundred million people, a half-person fall is the demand for millions of additional homes. It's the textbook slow tide: undramatic per day, decisive per decade.

#### Worked example: Minh's family unbundles across one generation

Trace one real family through it. In 1995, Minh's grandparents, his parents, Minh, and his two siblings all lived in one house in the Delta — **seven people, one household, one home.** Household size 7.0.

By 2025, that same bloodline occupies several homes. The grandparents have passed. Minh's parents are a two-person household in the old house. Minh and his wife and child are a three-person household in an HCMC apartment (≈ ₫7.0 billion at 2025 prices, or about \$270,000). His brother and brother's wife are a two-person household in Bình Dương. His sister, unmarried and working in HCMC, rents her own studio — a one-person household.

So the *same descendants* now occupy **four homes instead of one**. The population descended from that house grew only modestly, but the number of *households* it generates quadrupled — and three of those four homes are in cities, not the village. *One family, one generation, four roofs where there used to be one: that is household formation and urbanization working together, and it is the demand wave in miniature.*

### Marriage and divorce: the hidden levers on household count

Two demographic behaviors deserve their own spotlight because they move household formation directly and in opposite, sometimes surprising, directions: the *timing* of marriage and the *rate* of divorce.

Marriage is, mechanically, a household *merger*: two people who might have headed two separate households (a renter and a renter) combine into one (a couple in one home). So you might think marriage *reduces* housing demand — and at the moment of the wedding, it does. But the demographic story is about *timing*. When a society shifts from marrying at 22 to marrying at 30, it inserts roughly eight extra adult years during which young people increasingly live *on their own* rather than with parents — and each of those solo years is a household that wouldn't have existed under the early-marriage pattern. The *delay* of marriage is one of the most powerful quiet drivers of rising housing demand in urbanizing societies, because it converts what used to be "child in parents' home → spouse in marital home" into "child → solo renter → spouse in marital home," sneaking an extra household into the middle. Vietnam is living through exactly this shift: urban professionals now routinely marry in their late 20s or 30s, and the years before are increasingly spent in their own rented place.

Divorce is the purer case: it is a household *split*. One home becomes two, and the housing market must supply a second dwelling that didn't exist the day before. Rising divorce rates — a near-universal feature of development and urbanization — are a steady, if uncomfortable, source of net household formation. A society where divorce goes from rare to common is, all else equal, a society that needs more homes per thousand people. None of this is a moral claim; it's just the arithmetic of how many separate roofs a population's living arrangements require. The demographer doesn't judge the wedding or the divorce; they just count the households each produces.

### The reverse is also true — and that's the warning

Everything above runs in reverse in an aging society, which is why we keep Japan in frame. When a population stops having children and starts dying off faster than it forms new households, household *dissolution* eventually outpaces formation. Homes empty out. We'll see exactly how that looks later — Japan's roughly nine million vacant *akiya* are household size shrinking past the point where there are enough people left to fill the homes. For now, hold the symmetry: a falling household size raises demand *while the population is still young and spreading out*, but once the population is old, the same arithmetic starts dissolving households and *releasing* homes back onto the market. The very same lever — fewer people per home — adds demand on the way up and destroys it on the way down. Which side you're on depends entirely on whether your population is still young enough to keep forming new households faster than the old ones dissolve.

## Age structure and the demographic dividend

If household formation is the demand variable, then **age structure** — how a population is distributed across age bands — is the master setting that determines it. And the cleanest way to see age structure is the **population pyramid**: a chart that stacks age bands from youngest at the bottom to oldest at the top, with bar length showing how many people are in each band.

### Reading a pyramid in five seconds

A *true pyramid* — wide at the base, tapering to a point — describes a young, fast-growing population: lots of children, fewer elderly, each generation bigger than the last. A *rectangle* or *barrel* — roughly equal bars all the way up — describes a stable, mature population. And an *inverted* or *top-heavy* shape — narrow base, bulging middle-and-top — describes an old, shrinking population with more grandparents than grandchildren. Figure 3 puts the two extremes side by side: Vietnam's broad-based pyramid against Japan's top-heavy one.

![Two demographic pyramids side by side, Vietnam with a wide young base and a bulge in the twenty to thirty nine home-forming bands, versus Japan with a narrow base and a top-heavy shape concentrated in older age bands](/imgs/blogs/demographics-and-urbanization-the-demand-wave-3.png)

The crucial band to watch — shaded in the figure — is roughly **20 to 39**: the home-forming years, where headship rates climb steepest and people set up their own households for the first time. Vietnam has a fat bulge sitting right in or just entering that band. Japan's equivalent band is thin and getting thinner. That single contrast — fat home-forming cohort vs thin one — predicts more about the two countries' housing demand over the next twenty years than any forecast of interest rates.

### The demographic dividend

When a country has a large working-age population relative to its dependents (children and the elderly), economists call it a **demographic dividend**: a window, usually lasting a few decades, when the ratio of workers to dependents is unusually favorable. Lots of workers, few mouths to feed per worker — so savings rise, investment rises, incomes rise, and the economy grows fast almost mechanically. Much of the East Asian "miracle" (Japan in the 1960s–80s, South Korea, China after 1990) rode a demographic dividend.

For housing, the dividend is a double tailwind. First, the big working-age cohort is *exactly* the cohort that forms households and buys homes — demand. Second, the rising incomes the dividend produces turn that latent demand into *effective* demand: people who not only need a home but can now afford to buy or rent one. Vietnam is squarely inside its demographic dividend window right now, with a median age around **33** and a large cohort moving through its prime household-forming and earning years.

There's a third, subtler dividend effect that property analysts prize: a young, growing, working-heavy population also produces high *savings*, and in a country with thin pension systems and limited trust in financial markets, those savings flow heavily into real estate. In Vietnam, property is not just shelter — it is the default savings vehicle, the way families store and pass down wealth, precisely because banks pay little, the stock market is young and volatile, and there's no broad property tax to penalize hoarding. So the demographic dividend doesn't only create housing *need* and the *income* to fund it; it creates a flood of savings looking for a home, and a culture that sends those savings into land and apartments. That's a fourth force on top of the three in Figure 7, and it's a big part of why Vietnamese property demand is so intense relative to incomes. The dividend feeds the bid from three directions at once: more households, higher incomes, and more savings hunting for a store of value.

### The dividend is a window, not a gift

The catch — and it is the whole reason demographics is a *cycle*, not a one-way street — is that a dividend closes. The big young cohort that drives demand today *becomes* the elderly dependent bulge of tomorrow. If birth rates fell to produce that favorable ratio (and they always do as countries develop), then a generation later there's a small cohort of workers supporting a large cohort of retirees: the dividend flips into a *demographic drag*. China crossed this line around the mid-2010s; Japan crossed it decades ago. Vietnam's window is open now but won't stay open forever — its own fertility rate has fallen near replacement, which means the dividend has perhaps two more decades to run before the math turns. Demographics gives, and then — slowly, predictably — it takes back.

#### Worked example: a youth bulge entering the buying years

Put numbers on the dividend's housing punch. Suppose Vietnam has a cohort of roughly **1.5 million people per single year of age** in their early 20s (illustrative, but the right order of magnitude for a ~100-million country with this age structure). Each year, as the front edge of that cohort ages from 24 to 25 to 26, a slice of them crosses from "living with parents or flatmates" into "heads their own household." Say the headship rate for that band rises by about **5 percentage points per year of age** through the late 20s.

Then each passing year, roughly `1,500,000 × 5% = 75,000` *new household heads* are minted from that one birth-year cohort alone — 75,000 brand-new units of housing demand, every year, just from people aging one year forward through the home-forming band. Stack several adjacent birth-years all moving through that band at once and you get hundreds of thousands of new urban households a year, *before counting any population growth or household-size shrinkage on top.* *A youth bulge isn't just a statistic; it's a conveyor belt feeding the housing market a fresh wave of first-time households every single year it passes through the home-forming ages.*

## Urbanization: the S-curve and where Vietnam sits

So far we've talked about *how many* households form. Urbanization is about *where* they form — and it is the force that concentrates a diffuse national demand into a handful of cities, which is why it matters so much more for property prices than for the country as a whole.

### What urbanization is, precisely

The **urbanization rate** is simply the share of a country's population that lives in urban areas (cities and towns) rather than rural ones. It rises when people move from countryside to city faster than the rural population grows on its own — which, during development, they almost always do. Vietnam's urbanization rate is around **38–40%** today and climbing roughly **1 percentage point per year**. Rich countries sit at **70–85%**: the US around 83%, Japan around 92%, most of Western Europe 75–85%.

That gap — 40% versus 75-plus — is the headline. It means Vietnam has, very roughly, *another 30-plus percentage points* of its population still to move into cities over the coming decades. On a base of 100 million people, each percentage point is a million people relocating to urban housing demand. The runway is enormous, and it's the single biggest reason urban Vietnamese property has a structural demand story that, say, rural French property simply does not.

### The S-curve: why urbanization isn't a straight line

Urbanization doesn't proceed at a constant pace. It follows an **S-curve** (a logistic curve): slow at first when a country is overwhelmingly rural, then *fast and roughly linear* through the middle as industrialization pulls people to cities en masse, then slowing again as it approaches a developed-world plateau because there are simply fewer rural people left to move. Figure 2 traces that curve and marks where Vietnam sits.

![An S-shaped urbanization curve rising from about twenty percent in nineteen eighty through forty percent today toward a seventy to eighty percent developed-world plateau, with Vietnam marked at forty percent in the steep middle stretch of fastest demand growth](/imgs/blogs/demographics-and-urbanization-the-demand-wave-2.png)

The position on the curve is everything. A country at 15% urban (the bottom of the S) is urbanizing, but slowly and off a small base. A country at 85% (the top) has almost finished — the curve is flattening, the rural reservoir is nearly drained, and urban demand growth from migration is petering out. The **steep middle of the S** — roughly 30% to 60% urban — is where urbanization adds people to cities *fastest*, and that is precisely where Vietnam sits. It is, by this logic, in the most demand-intensive stretch of its entire urban history. Not at the start (too small a base), not near the end (curve flattening), but in the powerful middle, where the slope is steepest.

### Why the middle of the S-curve is the property sweet spot

Combine the S-curve with everything before it and you see why this period is special for Vietnamese urban housing. In the steep middle, three things happen *simultaneously*: the urban population is growing fast (migration), the people arriving are disproportionately young and in the home-forming ages (age structure), and rising incomes from the demographic dividend turn their need into purchasing power (effective demand). It's not one tailwind; it's three reinforcing each other. A country gets this alignment once, for a few decades, on its way up the curve — and then never again in quite the same way.

#### Worked example: how many households urbanization adds to a city each year

Let's size the urbanization flow into one city. Suppose HCMC's greater metro has a population of about **10 million**, growing roughly **2.5% per year** once you include net migration from the countryside — call it **250,000 additional people per year** flowing in (illustrative, but the right scale for a fast-growing Southeast Asian metro).

Those arrivals are disproportionately young adults, so they form households at a *higher* rate than the existing population. At an urban household size of about **3.5 people**, those 250,000 new residents translate into roughly:

`250,000 ÷ 3.5 ≈ 71,000 new urban households per year` — 71,000 homes the city needs to supply annually, *from migration alone*, before adding the household-size shrinkage among existing residents.

Now layer the shrinkage on top. If the city's existing ~2.86 million households (10M ÷ 3.5) see their average size tick down by even 0.02 people in a year, that releases demand for tens of thousands more homes. Add it up and a fast-urbanizing metro of 10 million can easily need **80,000–100,000 net new homes a year**. *Urbanization doesn't trickle demand into a city; it pumps it in by the tens of thousands of households annually, which is why fast-urbanizing cities feel a permanent housing squeeze.*

#### Worked example: how rising income and urbanization compound

The demand wave's real force comes from its tailwinds *multiplying*, not just adding. Watch two of them — urbanization and rising income — compound on one cohort. Start with 100,000 rural young adults who, in their village, have essentially zero *effective* housing demand: they live with family and earn ₫6 million a month (≈ \$230), nowhere near enough to buy or even rent independently. Their *need* for a home is real, but their *purchasing power* is roughly nil, so the market never sees them.

Now urbanize them: they move to the city, where the same people earn ₫18 million a month (≈ \$695). Two things just happened *at once*. Urbanization moved them into the high-headship environment where they form their own households (a quantity effect — more households). And the tripling of income turned their latent need into *effective* demand — they can now afford to rent, and within a few years, with a mortgage, to buy (a value effect — each household can pay more). The first force adds bodies to the city's household count; the second force raises how much each of those households can spend on housing. Demand in money terms is roughly *quantity × price-per-household*, and urbanization plus income growth pushes *both* terms up together.

So 100,000 rural non-buyers don't become 100,000 modest urban renters — they become tens of thousands of new urban households *each able to outbid the rural version of themselves several times over*. That compounding is why apartment prices in fast-urbanizing, fast-growing cities can run up so violently: it isn't just more people wanting homes, it's more people who can each afford to pay much more, arriving at the same time. *Urbanization and income growth don't add to demand side by side; they multiply, turning invisible rural need into a visible, well-funded urban bid.*

## Migration concentrates demand into a few cities

Urbanization is national accounting; **migration** is the mechanism that actually does it, and it's worth treating on its own because of *where* it sends the demand. The national household-formation story would be a gentle, diffuse thing if people stayed put. Migration takes that diffuse demand and slams it into a handful of cities — which is why those specific cities have the price pressure, the affordability crises, and the development booms, while the country as a whole looks unremarkable.

### Net migration, defined

**Net migration** for a place is arrivals minus departures over a period. Positive net migration means a place is gaining people from elsewhere; negative means it's losing them. For housing, internal (within-country) net migration is often more important than international: it's the rural-to-urban flow, and within the urban world, the flow toward the *biggest, richest* cities. People don't move to cities at random — they move where the jobs, wages, and opportunities are densest, which means a small number of magnet cities absorb a hugely disproportionate share of a country's entire household formation.

In Vietnam, that means HCMC, Hanoi, and their industrial satellites (Bình Dương, Đồng Nai, Bắc Ninh) capture the lion's share of internal migration. Minh's bus from the Delta went to HCMC, not to a provincial town, and that's the pattern: the wage gap pulls migrants toward the largest metros, and that's where the household-formation demand piles up. ### Why concentration matters more than the national average

This concentration is why national demographic averages mislead so badly about property. A country can have modest national population growth and still have *furious* housing pressure in two or three cities, because nearly all of the net household formation is landing there. The national number is the average of booming magnet cities and emptying rural provinces; the *property market* lives entirely in the former. When you read a national statistic — "Vietnam's population grows ~0.9% a year" — and try to reason about HCMC apartment prices from it, you're using the wrong number by an order of magnitude. The relevant number is HCMC's net in-migration plus its household-size shrinkage, which is several times the national growth rate.

### The drivers behind the magnet

Figure 4 lays out the full set of forces behind household formation and which direction each pushes — and migration is the force that makes the whole thing geographic.

![A five-by-three matrix listing the drivers of household formation, age cohort entering adulthood, marriage timing, divorce, household size, and migration, with each row showing what pushes households up and what pushes them down](/imgs/blogs/demographics-and-urbanization-the-demand-wave-4.png)

Read down the matrix and notice that in Vietnam *right now*, every single row's "up" condition is the one in force: a big youth cohort is reaching adulthood, marriage is happening later, divorce is slowly rising, household size is falling, and net migration into the big cities is strongly positive. Five forces, all pushing the same way, all toward more urban households. That alignment is rare and temporary, and it's the precise reason Vietnam's urban housing demand has the runway it does. In an aging country, you'd read the *same matrix* with the "down" column lit up on most rows — and that's our cue to turn to Japan.

#### Worked example: the wage gap that makes Minh move

Migration runs on arithmetic as cold as any spreadsheet. Suppose Minh, fresh out of university, could earn about **₫6 million a month** (≈ \$230) doing technical work in his Delta hometown, where housing is cheap and life is slow. In HCMC, the same skills command perhaps **₫18 million a month** (≈ \$695) — three times as much — even though rent for his shared room costs **₫3 million** (≈ \$115) versus near-zero living with parents back home.

Run the net: at home he nets ₫6M minus minimal costs, call it ₫5M of disposable income. In HCMC he nets ₫18M minus ₫3M rent minus higher living costs, call it ₫11M. He more than *doubles* his disposable income by moving, and gains a career ladder that barely exists in the Delta. For a 24-year-old, that decision makes itself. Forty million young Vietnamese run a version of that same calculation, and the aggregate of those decisions is the migration flow — which is the same thing as a few cities absorbing the nation's household formation. *Migration looks like culture or ambition from the outside, but underneath it's a wage gap doing math, and the math points at the big cities.*

## The reversal: aging and shrinking demand

Everything so far has been the tide coming *in*. To really understand why Vietnam's situation is a tailwind, you have to see the same forces running the other way — the tide going *out*. The clearest case on Earth is Japan, and it is not a curiosity; it is a preview of where every developing country, Vietnam included, eventually arrives.

### How the engine runs in reverse

Japan's population peaked around 2010 at roughly 128 million and has been *shrinking* ever since, now under 125 million and falling faster each year. Its median age is about **49** — among the oldest in the world — against Vietnam's ~33. Its fertility rate has been far below replacement for decades. And it is ~92% urbanized: there is essentially no rural reservoir left to move to cities. Every demographic dial that points "up" for Vietnam points "down" for Japan. Figure 6 sets the two countries' force-stacks against each other directly.

![A two-panel comparison of Vietnam's demographic tailwind with a young median age, rising urbanization, falling household size, and net city migration, against Japan's demographic drag with an old median age, a plateaued urbanization rate, a shrinking population, and emptying rural towns](/imgs/blogs/demographics-and-urbanization-the-demand-wave-6.png)

Watch what happens to household formation in this regime. The home-forming cohort (20–39) is small and shrinking, so few new households form. Meanwhile the elderly cohort is enormous, and as those people pass away, their households *dissolve* — and there's no younger generation large enough to re-occupy the homes. Household dissolution starts to outpace household formation. For the first time, the country needs *fewer* homes than it has. The slow tide that built demand for a century goes into reverse.

### Akiya: what a demand drag looks like on the ground

Japan's most visible symptom is the **akiya** — abandoned or vacant houses. Estimates put Japan's vacant homes at roughly **9 million**, around 14% of the entire housing stock, concentrated in rural and small-town areas the young have left and the old have died out of. Some municipalities give akiya away for free, or nearly so, just to get them occupied and back on the tax rolls — and still struggle to find takers, because the demand simply isn't there. This is what the headship effect looks like in reverse: not too many people chasing too few homes, but too few people to fill the homes that exist.

Crucially, this isn't uniform. Tokyo, the magnet metro, still draws Japan's dwindling young people through internal migration and remains expensive and tight even as the country shrinks around it — the same concentration effect, just operating on a shrinking national pool. The akiya are overwhelmingly rural and provincial. Aging plus migration doesn't make *all* property fall; it hollows out the periphery and props up the one or two magnet cities. That nuance matters: even in a shrinking country, the concentration force keeps a handful of cities alive.

### China: the reversal arriving in fast-forward

Japan took the better part of a century to age. China is doing it in a few decades, thanks to the one-child policy compressing the whole transition. China's working-age population peaked around 2014, its total population began shrinking around 2022, and its household formation is now slowing sharply — which, combined with massive overbuilding, is a core reason for the Evergrande-era property bust. China is a warning that the reversal can arrive *faster* than the demand wave did, and while a country still feels young, because age structure has so much momentum baked in.

#### Worked example: a shrinking country's housing math

Run the headship arithmetic backward. Take a rural Japanese prefecture with **500,000 people** and a household size of **2.4**, so:

`500,000 ÷ 2.4 ≈ 208,000 households` → 208,000 homes occupied.

Over fifteen years, the population shrinks 15% to **425,000** as the elderly die and the young leave for Tokyo. But household size *also* keeps falling — to about **2.2** — as the remaining population skews toward elderly singles. So:

`425,000 ÷ 2.2 ≈ 193,000 households` → 193,000 homes needed.

The prefecture has lost demand for about **15,000 homes** over fifteen years. Notice the cruel twist: the falling household size, which *added* demand when the population was young and growing, now *cushions* the fall but can't reverse it — there just aren't enough people left to head 208,000 households anymore. The surplus 15,000 homes become akiya. *In a young country, shrinking households and a growing population both add demand; in an old one, the population fall wins, and the homes empty out faster than smaller households can fill them.*

## Common misconceptions

This subject is a minefield of plausible-sounding errors. Five of them do most of the damage.

**"Population growth equals housing demand."** This is the master error, and the whole post has been an argument against it. Housing is demanded by *households*, not people. A country with flat population but falling household size can need millions of new homes (the Figure 1 effect); a country with growing population but a household-formation slowdown can need fewer than its headcount suggests. Always translate population into *households* before reasoning about housing. Population is the raw material; household formation is the demand.

**"More people always means higher prices."** Even where population *is* growing, prices depend on the race between demand (household formation) and *supply* (how fast the city can build). A fast-growing city that builds freely — think the elastic Sun Belt, or HCMC's satellite provinces with room to sprawl — can absorb huge demand with only moderate price gains, because supply keeps pace. A growing city that *can't* build (central Hanoi, San Francisco) turns the same demand into pure price. Demographics sets demand; it does not set price on its own. (We unpack this fully in [supply elasticity](/blog/trading/real-estate/supply-elasticity-why-some-cities-boom-and-bust) — demand and supply are two blades of the same scissor.)

**"Demographics are destiny on any timeframe."** Demographics are close to destiny over *decades* — the age structure of 2045 is mostly already born, and you can forecast it with eerie confidence. But over the next *year or two*, demographics barely move and explain almost nothing about prices. In the short run, interest rates, credit availability, and policy dominate utterly: Vietnam's 2022–23 freeze had nothing to do with demographics (the demand wave kept rolling) and everything to do with a bond-market seizure and a credit crunch. Use demographics for the decade, not the quarter. Confusing the timeframes is how people lose money being "right" about the long run at the wrong moment.

**"A young population guarantees rising prices."** A young population guarantees rising *demand*, which is not the same as rising *prices* or rising *affordability*. If incomes don't keep up with prices, a young, demanding population can be priced out — exactly what's happening in Vietnam, where the price-to-income ratio in HCMC is among the highest in the world (over 30 years of income for a typical flat) precisely *because* demand is so strong relative to what young buyers can afford. Strong demographics can produce an *affordability crisis* rather than a wealth boom for the young. (See [income, affordability, and the price-to-income ratio](/blog/trading/real-estate/income-affordability-and-the-price-to-income-ratio).)

**"Urbanization will keep going forever."** Urbanization is an S-curve, not a ramp. It slows hard as a country approaches the developed-world plateau because the rural reservoir runs dry — you can't urbanize people who already live in cities. Vietnam at 40% has a long runway; Japan at 92% has none. Extrapolating today's ~1-point-a-year pace out indefinitely would have Vietnam at 90% urban implausibly fast. The wave is powerful *because* of where Vietnam sits on the curve, and it will flatten as the country climbs — which is also when the demographic dividend closes. The tailwinds arrive together and they leave together.

## How it shows up in real markets

Abstractions earn their keep when they explain real episodes. Here are the demand wave and its reversal at work in actual markets.

### Vietnam's urban demand runway

The defining feature of Vietnamese urban property is a demand runway that most developed markets lost decades ago. Put the pieces together: a population of ~100 million with a median age of ~33; urbanization at ~40% and rising ~1 point a year toward a 70–80% plateau; household size still falling from ~4 toward ~3.5; and incomes rising fast off the demographic dividend. Each force is a tailwind, and Figure 7 shows them stacked around the structural demand they jointly produce.

![A grid figure showing Vietnam's four structural demand forces, a young median age around thirty three, urbanization near forty percent and rising, household size falling toward three point five, and rising incomes, arranged around a central cell labeled structural urban housing demand rising for decades](/imgs/blogs/demographics-and-urbanization-the-demand-wave-7.png)

This is why HCMC and Hanoi apartment prices have climbed so relentlessly even through credit shocks — primary apartment prices in both cities rose on the order of 30–40% year-on-year in 2024–25, pushing average HCMC primary prices past **₫110 million/m²** (≈ \$4,200/m²). The credit cycle pauses the wave (as in 2022–23) but doesn't end it; underneath, tens of thousands of new urban households form every year and need somewhere to live. The demand wave is the reason the dips have been *dips* rather than the multi-decade stagnation an aging market would deliver. It's also, more soberingly, why affordability is so stretched: the demand is structural and the supply is constrained, so the price-to-income ratio sits near the top of the global league table. Strong demographics gave HCMC a permanent bid *and* a permanent affordability problem — the same coin, both sides.

### Japan's emptying houses

Japan is the demand wave forty years after it crested. The country built furiously through its post-war dividend, its cities filled, prices ran to an epic 1980s bubble — and then the population aged, the dividend closed, and the tide turned. Today Japan's roughly 9 million vacant *akiya*, concentrated in the rural periphery, are the physical residue of household dissolution outpacing formation. National house prices spent two decades going essentially nowhere or down after the 1990 peak. And yet *Tokyo* — the magnet that still pulls Japan's shrinking young population — has firmed and even risen in recent years, because concentration keeps working even in a shrinking country. Japan teaches two lessons at once: demographics can turn a market into a multi-decade demand drag, and even then, the one or two magnet cities can buck the national trend. It is, plausibly, a preview of Vietnam's own situation sometime around the 2050s–2060s, when its dividend closes and its own pyramid goes top-heavy.

### US household-formation swings

The United States shows that household formation is not just a slow trend but a *volatile* one in the short run — and that the swings move the market. In the 2008–2012 bust and its aftermath, US household formation collapsed: young adults, unable to find jobs or credit, "doubled up" — moved back in with parents or crammed in with roommates — so *household formation fell even though the population kept growing*. That suppressed housing demand for years and deepened the glut. Then, as the economy healed and millennials finally left home, household formation surged back, helping drive the 2013–2022 housing boom that took the Case-Shiller index from ~134 to over 300. Same population growth throughout; wildly different *household* formation, and the housing market followed the households, not the headcount. It's the cleanest proof that the household — not the person — is the unit of demand, and that it can lurch in the short run even as the demographic tide moves slowly underneath.

### China's compressed reversal

China is the demand wave and its reversal compressed into a single generation, and a warning about momentum. For thirty years China rode the most dramatic urbanization in history — hundreds of millions moving to cities — and built more housing, faster, than any country ever has. But the one-child policy meant the demographic dividend was always going to close abruptly. China's working-age population peaked in 2014; its total population began shrinking in 2022; household formation is decelerating fast. Combined with enormous overbuilding in the boom, that demographic turn helped trigger the Evergrande-era property bust, with prices falling and a vast inventory of unsold (and sometimes unfinished) apartments. China shows that a market can *feel* young and unstoppable right up until the age structure turns — and that when it turns, the adjustment can be brutal precisely because so much supply was built assuming the wave would never crest.

### Europe's two-speed demographic map

Europe is a useful middle case between Vietnam's tailwind and Japan's drag, because the continent splits in two. Much of Southern and Eastern Europe — Italy, Spain, Portugal, the Balkans, the Baltics — has fertility far below replacement and, in many countries, *shrinking* populations made worse by young people emigrating for work. The result is the early stages of the Japan pattern: depopulating villages, surplus housing in the interior, and demand clinging to a few capital cities. Italy now has its own version of the akiya giveaway, selling crumbling village homes for a symbolic one euro to anyone who'll renovate them — the same household-dissolution-outpacing-formation arithmetic that fills Japan's countryside with empty houses. Meanwhile a handful of magnet cities — London, Paris, Munich, Amsterdam — stay expensive and supply-starved because internal *and* international migration concentrates the continent's household formation into them even as the periphery hollows out. Europe is the demographic map in miniature: a few magnet cities riding concentration, a vast periphery sliding into the demand drag, and immigration as the swing factor that determines whether a country's total household count still grows at all. It's a preview of how unevenly the reversal arrives — not as a uniform national decline, but as a widening gap between a few thriving cities and an emptying rest.

### The satellite-province pattern

Within Vietnam, the demand wave plus *supply elasticity* explains the geography of price moves. The migration flow concentrates in HCMC and Hanoi, but the *most elastic* supply sits in the satellite provinces — Bình Dương, Đồng Nai, Long An — where there's room to sprawl. So a share of the demand wave gets absorbed there at lower prices, which is exactly why those provinces saw the đất nền (land-plot) fevers of 2018 and beyond: demand from the metro spilling into the buildable fringe. The central cores, where supply is locked, take the demand as pure price. Demographics provides the demand; where it lands as price versus quantity depends on which districts can actually build. This is the demand wave meeting the supply curve, and it's why the same wave produces a price spike downtown and a building boom on the fringe.

## When this matters / further reading

Demographics is the layer of real estate you should think about *least often and most seriously*. Least often, because it barely moves year to year and is useless for timing anything inside a few years — if you're trying to read the next eighteen months, look at credit and rates, not the population pyramid. Most seriously, because over the horizon that actually matters for a home you'll own for decades, or a city you're betting on, the demographic tide is the most powerful and most predictable force there is. The cohort that will form households in 2045 is already born; you can see the wave coming with more confidence than almost anything else in finance.

The practical takeaways are simple. When you assess any property market, don't ask "is the population growing?" — ask "is the *number of households* growing, and where are they forming?" Translate population into households via household size, find where the country sits on the urbanization S-curve, and check whether the home-forming cohort (20–39) is fat or thin. For Vietnam today, all three answers are favorable, which is a genuine multi-decade tailwind — *and* the source of its punishing affordability problem, because strong demand plus constrained supply is, by definition, expensive. For Japan, all three are unfavorable, which is why the periphery empties even as Tokyo holds. And remember the symmetry that hangs over the whole story: the tailwinds arrive together and leave together. Vietnam's dividend, its urbanization runway, and its falling household size are all features of the *same* moment on the development curve — and that moment, like Japan's before it, will eventually pass.
