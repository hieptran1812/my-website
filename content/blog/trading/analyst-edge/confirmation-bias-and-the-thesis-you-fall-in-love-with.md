> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Confirmation bias is the act of seeking, weighting, and remembering evidence that supports a view you already hold while discounting everything that contradicts it. Once a thesis becomes your identity, you defend it past the point where the data says you are wrong — and that is where the money goes. You do not beat it by trying to be objective; you beat it with a routine: pre-commit your invalidation in writing, schedule a search for disconfirming evidence, assign someone to break the trade, and keep your ego out of the position.
>
> - Confirmation bias corrupts two things at once: your **information diet** (you collect the data that agrees with you) and your **Bayesian update** (you under-weight the data that doesn't), so a wrong thesis gets *more* confident as more evidence arrives.
> - It is strongest exactly where it hurts most — on your **high-conviction, public, and large** positions. The more you have staked, said out loud, and believed, the harder your brain works to protect the belief.
> - "Doing more research" makes it *worse*, not better, when the search itself is biased. You will find ten more confirming data points and feel more sure of a thesis that is dying.
> - The one rule to remember: **write down, before you enter, the specific observation that would prove you wrong — and when that observation arrives, act on it without negotiation.** A thesis you will not let yourself disprove is not analysis; it is a position you are emotionally long.

## The trader who rode it into the ground

It is February and a trader — call him Marcus — has found the trade of the year. A mid-cap software name, growing 40% a year, that the market is treating like it grows 15%. He has done the work: he has read every transcript, built the cohort model, talked to two customers. He is right, and he knows he is right, and over the next few weeks he tells everyone. He posts the thesis. He argues it in the group chat. He tells his PM it is his highest-conviction idea of the cycle. He puts on a position around \$60 a share, sized big because he is so sure, and he sits back to wait for the market to come to its senses.

It does not come to its senses. The stock drifts to \$54 on no news, and Marcus adds — the thesis is even better now, the entry is even cheaper. A competitor announces a price cut, and Marcus reads it as desperation, a sign the incumbent is scared of his company, confirming his view. The stock falls to \$48. A sell-side analyst he respects downgrades the name on slowing net-revenue retention — the exact metric that is the spine of Marcus's bull case — and Marcus spends an afternoon writing a point-by-point rebuttal of why the analyst is wrong. The stock hits \$42. Every red candle becomes "an opportunity to add at a better price." Every bear becomes "someone who doesn't get the story." By the time the company actually guides down retention on the next print, Marcus is down 35% on a position that has become, somewhere along the way, less a trade than a part of who he is. He cannot sell it, because selling it would mean he was wrong, and he is not wrong. He is just early.

Here is what makes Marcus's story so dangerous: at no point did he behave irrationally *by his own lights*. Every decision he made was a locally sensible response to the evidence as he perceived it. The problem was upstream of the decisions — it was in the perception itself. From the moment he committed to the thesis, his brain quietly reorganized its entire relationship with incoming information around defending the conclusion he had already reached. He did not lie to himself. He did something subtler and far more dangerous: he reasoned, carefully and in good faith, toward the answer he already wanted. This post is about the bias that did that to him, and the specific habits that would have saved him \$9,000 he never needed to lose.

It is the analyst's deadliest enemy precisely because it operates *after* you have done everything right, at the moment you need your judgment most.

## Foundations: confirmation bias, motivated reasoning, and the thesis-as-identity trap

Three terms have to be precise before we can do anything useful with them, because each is routinely used loosely in a way that hides exactly the mechanism you need to defeat.

### Confirmation bias

**Confirmation bias** is the well-documented tendency to seek out, notice, weight, and remember evidence that supports a belief you already hold, while overlooking, discounting, or forgetting evidence that contradicts it. It is not a single act of self-deception. It is a *systematic tilt* in how you handle information, applied across every stage of the process — what you go looking for, how you read what you find, and what you remember later. The British psychologist Peter Wason coined the term in the 1960s after a series of experiments in which people, asked to discover a rule, reliably tested only the examples they expected to *confirm* the rule and almost never tested the examples that could *falsify* it. Given the chance to be proven right or proven wrong, people overwhelmingly chose to be proven right — even when proving themselves wrong was the faster route to the truth.

The crucial property is that confirmation bias operates *below conscious awareness*. You do not experience yourself as ignoring contrary evidence. You experience yourself as evaluating it and — wouldn't you know it — finding it unpersuasive. That is what makes it so much more dangerous than ordinary stubbornness. A stubborn person knows they are digging in. A person in the grip of confirmation bias feels like the most reasonable person in the room, calmly weighing the evidence, which all happens to point the way they already believed. You cannot will yourself out of it by resolving to be more objective, any more than you can tickle yourself. The bias is in the machinery doing the resolving.

### Motivated reasoning

**Motivated reasoning** is the engine underneath confirmation bias. Confirmation bias describes the *pattern* — the tilt toward supportive evidence; motivated reasoning describes the *cause* — the fact that you have a stake in the conclusion. When you *want* a belief to be true, you apply two different standards of proof. For evidence that supports what you want, you ask "can I believe this?" and the answer is almost always yes — one supportive data point is enough. For evidence that threatens what you want, you ask "must I believe this?" and the answer is almost always no — you can always find a reason to set it aside. The psychologist Tom Gilovich captured it in one line: for things we want to be true, we ask whether the evidence *permits* belief; for things we don't want to be true, we ask whether it *compels* it. Same evidence, two thresholds, and the gap between them is the size of your stake in the answer.

This is why the bias is not uniform. It scales directly with how much you have riding on the conclusion. A view you hold lightly, that costs you nothing to abandon, you can update cleanly. A view you have staked money on, said out loud, and built part of your professional self-image around — that view you will defend with the full, unconscious resourcefulness of a very smart brain that does not want to lose face. The reasoning gets *better*, more sophisticated, more lawyerly, exactly as the stakes rise. That is the trap.

### The thesis-as-identity trap

The most dangerous transformation in trading is the quiet moment a *thesis* becomes an *identity*. A thesis is a falsifiable claim about the world: "this company's revenue retention is structurally higher than the market believes, and the next two prints will show it." It is the kind of thing that can be true or false, and you can update your estimate of which as evidence arrives. An identity is not falsifiable. "I am the guy who is long this name and right about it" is not a claim about the world; it is a claim about *you*. And you cannot update a claim about you the way you update a claim about a company, because abandoning it does not feel like changing your mind — it feels like losing.

The transition happens gradually and almost always involves three accelerants: **conviction**, because the surer you were, the more the reversal costs your self-image; **publicity**, because once you have told people, there is now an audience to be wrong in front of; and **size**, because a large position is a large, visible, daily reminder of the stake. When all three are high — high conviction, publicly committed, large size — the thesis fuses with the ego completely, and at that point new information stops being data and starts being a threat. A bear is no longer a counterparty with a different read; he is an attacker. A red candle is no longer a price move; it is a personal affront. This is the fusion that destroyed Marcus, and it is the fusion the rest of this post is engineered to prevent.

The reason the three accelerants are so dangerous together is that they reinforce each other. High conviction makes you want to size big; a big position is naturally something you talk about, so you go public; and having gone public, you defend the conviction more fiercely to avoid the public reversal — which makes you reluctant to trim, so the size stays big. The three feed a loop, and a trader who is unaware of the loop experiences each step as an independent, sensible decision rather than as the tightening of a trap. The defense, as we will see, is to deliberately *break* the correlations: cap size so no position is large enough to fuse with, keep commitment private precisely on the positions you are most certain of, and treat the felt conviction itself as a warning rather than a green light.

### How it corrupts the information diet and the Bayesian update

To see why confirmation bias is specifically lethal to *analysts*, look at what good analysis actually is: collect evidence, weight it honestly, and update your probability estimate as it arrives. That is a Bayesian process — you start with a prior belief and revise it in proportion to how surprising the new evidence is given your hypothesis versus the alternatives. Confirmation bias attacks this process at *both* ends simultaneously, which is what makes it uniquely destructive rather than merely annoying.

![Held view leads to selective search and biased interpretation feeding a reinforced view](/imgs/blogs/confirmation-bias-and-the-thesis-you-fall-in-love-with-1.png)

First, it corrupts the **information diet** — the evidence you actually collect. A clean Bayesian update requires a representative sample of evidence. But the biased searcher does not sample randomly; he goes looking for confirmation. He re-reads his own bull notes, scrolls the accounts that agree with him, and skims past the disclosure that worries him. So the evidence that reaches the update is already filtered, pre-selected to support the prior. Second, it corrupts the **update itself** — the weighting. Even when disconfirming evidence does arrive, motivated reasoning down-weights it ("the analyst is wrong about retention") and up-weights the confirming evidence ("the competitor's price cut proves they're scared"). The net effect is perverse: a thesis that is *actually getting more wrong* — where the disconfirming evidence is piling up — produces a feeling of *increasing confidence*, because both the data you collect and the way you weigh it have been bent to protect the conclusion. The posterior moves the wrong way. That is not a small error of degree; it is the update running in reverse.

With those four ideas in hand — the bias, its motivated-reasoning engine, the identity trap, and the two-front corruption of the update — we can build the defense.

## How confirmation bias actually operates

The bias is not one move; it is three distinct mechanisms operating at three different stages of handling information. You have to know all three by name, because the countermeasures are different for each, and a defense that addresses only one leaves the other two free to work.

### Mechanism 1: selective search

The first mechanism operates *before* you have evaluated anything — in the choice of what to look at. A person holding a view does not survey the evidence landscape neutrally; he navigates straight to the parts of it that agree with him. He follows the analysts who share his thesis, reads the bullish note twice and the bearish note never, and runs the screen that confirms the pattern rather than the one that might break it. The classic laboratory demonstration is Wason's: given a rule to discover, people generate tests they expect to pass, not tests that could fail. In markets this looks completely reasonable from the inside — "I'm doing my research" — which is exactly why it is so insidious. You feel diligent. You are accumulating evidence. But the evidence is a rigged sample, and a rigged sample run through even a perfectly honest brain produces a confident wrong answer.

Selective search is the mechanism most people don't believe applies to them, because the act of searching *feels* like open-minded investigation. The tell is in what you skip. If you can recall the last five pieces of evidence that supported your thesis but cannot recall the last time you went looking, specifically, for the strongest argument *against* it, your search is selective. The diligence is real; the sample is broken.

Modern information environments make this far worse than it was in Wason's lab. The feeds, communities, and follow-lists you have curated are optimized to show you more of what you already engage with, so the moment you become a bull on a name, the algorithmic and social plumbing around you begins delivering a steadily more bullish information stream — not because anyone intends to mislead you, but because your own attention has told the system what you want to see. The result is that selective search now happens *to* you even when you are not actively doing it: the disconfirming evidence is not so much discounted as never surfaced. Defeating it requires going *out of your way* to the sources that disagree, because they will not come to you on their own.

### Mechanism 2: biased interpretation

The second mechanism operates *during* evaluation, on evidence that is genuinely ambiguous — and most market evidence is ambiguous. The same data point gets read as supportive if it can possibly be read that way. The competitor cuts prices: a bear reads it as competitive pressure on your company's pricing power; Marcus read it as the competitor being scared, which *confirms* his bull case. Both readings are available in the data; motivated reasoning picks the one that protects the thesis. A famous 1979 study by Lord, Ross, and Lepper showed this cleanly: people on opposite sides of the death-penalty debate were shown the *same* mixed body of research, and both sides came away *more* convinced of their original position. Identical evidence, opposite updates, because each side credited the studies that agreed with them and found methodological reasons to dismiss the ones that didn't.

Biased interpretation is more sophisticated than selective search and harder to catch, because it produces genuine-feeling reasoning. You are not ignoring the disconfirming data; you are *explaining* it. And your explanation is often clever — that is the problem. The smarter the analyst, the better the explanation, the more thoroughly the threatening evidence gets neutralized. Intelligence is not a defense against confirmation bias; deployed in service of a conclusion you are committed to, it is an accelerant.

### Mechanism 3: selective memory

The third mechanism operates *after the fact*, in what you retain. Even if your search were unbiased and your interpretation fair, your memory would still betray you, because confirming evidence is encoded more deeply and recalled more readily than disconfirming evidence. Over the life of a trade, you will vividly remember the customer call that supported the thesis and have only a vague, easily-dismissed recollection of the channel data that worried you. When you later "review the evidence," you are reviewing a memory that has already been curated in your favor. This is also why traders systematically misremember their own track records: the wins are crisp and the losses are foggy, which inflates felt skill and feeds overconfidence into the *next* thesis.

The three mechanisms compound. Selective search narrows the input; biased interpretation tilts the reading; selective memory curates the record. Run them in sequence over a few weeks and you get the Marcus effect: a man who is more sure of a thesis that is more wrong, sitting on a record (in his head) of having been right all along.

## The cost: holding past invalidation and the update that stops

Cognitive biases that cost nothing are curiosities. Confirmation bias is not a curiosity, because in markets it has a price, and the price is concentrated in two specific failures.

### You hold past invalidation

The first and largest cost is that confirmation bias makes you hold a position past the point where your own pre-trade analysis said you should be out. You wrote, calmly, before you entered: "if retention prints below 110%, the thesis is wrong and I exit." Then retention prints at 108%, and the bias goes to work. It reframes the print as a one-quarter blip, finds the line in the transcript about a "tough comp," and produces a perfectly reasonable-sounding case for holding. The invalidation level you set in cold blood gets renegotiated in hot blood, every single time, and the renegotiation always concludes that *this* breach doesn't count.

This is the mechanism behind the asymmetry that makes losses so much more damaging than gains. A position you exit at your planned invalidation loses a small, pre-decided amount. A position you defend past invalidation loses an unbounded amount, because there is no longer any level at which you have agreed to stop. The worked example below makes this concrete with dollars, but the shape of it is the entire game: small planned losses are survivable, and confirmation bias is the thing that turns small planned losses into large unplanned ones.

#### Worked example: a thesis defended past invalidation turns a \$2,000 loss into \$11,000

Marcus runs a \$100,000 account. His software-name thesis is a high-conviction idea, so he sizes it at \$20,000 — 20% of the account, large for him. Before entering at \$60, he does everything right: he writes the invalidation. "If the stock breaks \$57 *and* the next retention print is below 110%, the variant perception is gone and I exit." At \$57 with a clean break, his planned loss is the \$3 per share times his share count — on \$20,000 at \$60, that is roughly 333 shares, so a stop at \$57 is a planned loss of about **\$1,000**. He tells himself \$2,000 is the absolute most he would lose if it gapped through.

Then the stock breaks \$57. The bias activates: "it's a clean technical level, not a thesis breach — retention hasn't printed yet." He holds. At \$54 he adds 200 shares, "improving the entry," now long 533 shares at an average of about \$57.75. At \$48 the respected analyst downgrades on retention; he writes the rebuttal and holds. At \$42 the company guides retention down — the explicit invalidation, now undeniable — and he is paralyzed, because exiting means booking the loss *and* admitting the analyst was right *and* eating the public reversal. He finally capitulates at \$37, selling 533 shares bought at an average of \$57.75. The loss: about \$20.75 per share times 533 shares = roughly **\$11,000**, or 11% of his entire account.

The thesis being wrong cost him the planned \$1,000–\$2,000. *Confirmation bias* cost him the other \$9,000. The trade he could have exited for a rounding error on his year became the trade that defined it — not because his analysis was bad, but because the bias would not let him act on his own invalidation rule when the moment came.

### The update stops

The second cost is quieter and, over a career, larger: confirmation bias makes you stop updating. The whole value of an analyst is the ability to take in new information and revise. A trader whose posterior no longer moves in response to evidence has stopped being an analyst and become a billboard — he is displaying a fixed opinion, not processing a changing world. And because the bias hides this from him, he experiences his rigidity as *conviction*, which the culture of trading celebrates. "Strong hands." "High conviction." "Sticking to the thesis." Some of those phrases describe genuine discipline — refusing to be shaken out by noise. But the same phrases also describe a man who has simply stopped listening, and from the inside the two are nearly indistinguishable. (Knowing which one you are in is hard enough that it gets [its own post](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/thesis-broken-or-just-noise-the-hardest-call-you-make).)

The career cost compounds because the trader who stops updating does not just lose on one trade. He carries the un-updated belief forward, applies it to the next setup, and — because selective memory has filed away the loss as bad luck rather than bad process — he never extracts the lesson that would have improved the next decision. The bias does not just take the \$9,000; it takes the learning that the \$9,000 was supposed to buy.

## Countermeasures: the routine that beats the bias

The single most important thing to understand about defeating confirmation bias is *what does not work*: trying to be objective. Resolving to weigh the evidence fairly is useless, because the bias operates below the level at which your resolve has any purchase. By the time the evidence reaches your conscious, well-intentioned evaluation, it has already been filtered by selective search and is about to be tilted by biased interpretation, and your honest intent to be fair will be applied to a sample that is already rigged. You cannot out-discipline a bias you cannot feel.

What works is a *routine* — a set of external, pre-committed procedures that don't depend on your in-the-moment objectivity, because you set them up when you had nothing at stake. The routine substitutes process for willpower. Here are the six components.

![Five-stage process from thesis stated to assign red-team to steelman to find the kill to resize](/imgs/blogs/confirmation-bias-and-the-thesis-you-fall-in-love-with-4.png)

### Countermeasure 1: actively seek disconfirming evidence

The first and most powerful move is to flip the question your search is built around. The default question — the one selective search answers — is "what evidence supports my thesis?" Replace it, deliberately and on a schedule, with "what is the single strongest piece of evidence that I am wrong, and where would I find it?" This is not a vibe; it is a different *task*, and it sends you to different places. Instead of re-reading your bull notes, you go find the best-argued bear case in existence and read it as if it were written by someone smarter than you. Instead of counting the data points that agree, you go hunting specifically for the data point that disagrees.

The reason this works is that it does not require you to be unbiased. It redirects the bias. Your motivated reasoning is still operating at full strength — but now it is pointed at the task of *finding holes*, which it can do brilliantly when that is the assigned goal. You are using the same engine that defends the thesis, turned around to attack it. The schedule matters: if you leave the disconfirming search to "whenever I feel like it," the bias will ensure you never feel like it. Put it on the calendar. Every week the position is live, you owe it one deliberate hour of looking for the reason you are wrong.

![Seek-confirming default on the left versus seek-disconfirming trained on the right](/imgs/blogs/confirmation-bias-and-the-thesis-you-fall-in-love-with-2.png)

#### Worked example: a disconfirming search cuts size from \$20,000 to \$8,000

Take Marcus before he enters, but this time he runs the search first. His instinct is the \$20,000 high-conviction size. Before sizing, he spends one hour on a single question: "what is the strongest reason this thesis is already wrong?" The honest search surfaces three things his confirming research had skated past. First, the retention number that anchors his bull case is reported on a *trailing* basis, so by the time it prints low it has already been low for a quarter — the data confirms the thesis with a lag that makes the invalidation slow and expensive. Second, the two customers he talked to were both in his network, both early adopters, and badly unrepresentative of the broad base. Third, the short interest had quietly doubled, meaning sophisticated money was already positioned against him and had presumably seen what he was now seeing.

None of this kills the thesis. But it changes the honest probability he assigns to it from a felt 80% to something closer to 55%, and it reveals that his invalidation signal is laggy and therefore costly to act on. He resizes. Instead of \$20,000, he puts on **\$8,000** — a position whose worst case, at his \$57 invalidation, costs about \$400, a number he can absorb without his ego getting involved. He is still expressing the view. He is just expressing it at a size that survives being wrong. When the stock later craters to \$37, the disconfirming search has already saved him: his loss on the smaller position, exited at invalidation, is a survivable few hundred dollars instead of \$11,000. The hour of looking for reasons he was wrong was the highest-return hour of the trade.

### Countermeasure 2: steelman and red-team

The second move is to build the strongest possible version of the opposing case — a **steelman**, the opposite of a strawman. A strawman is the weakest version of the bear case, built so you can knock it down and feel right. A steelman is the *strongest* version, stronger than any actual bear has bothered to articulate, constructed by you on purpose. The test is emotional: a real steelman should make you genuinely uncomfortable. If your "bear case" is a list of objections you can answer in a sentence each, you have built a strawman and are about to mistake the demolition of it for confidence.

To **red-team** is to do the same thing as a structured exercise, ideally with a person whose explicit job is to break the thesis. The point of designating a person — not just "considering the other side" — is that it externalizes the disconfirmation. As long as finding holes is *your* job, motivated reasoning will sabotage it; you have a stake in there being no holes. But if breaking the trade is *someone else's* mandate, that person has no stake in your being right and every incentive to find the flaw. Even when you trade alone, you can red-team by literally assigning the role to yourself for a fixed window — "for the next thirty minutes, my only job is to prosecute this trade" — which is weaker than a real second person but far better than nothing.

#### Worked example: a red-team catches the bias before entry and saves a \$2,500 loss

A portfolio manager is about to put on a \$25,000 macro trade: long the dollar into a Fed meeting, conviction high, because the inflation data has been hot and she is sure the Fed will sound hawkish. Before she sizes it, she walks it to a colleague whose only instruction is "tell me why this is wrong." The colleague does his job. He points out that the hawkish outcome is the *consensus* — the dollar has already rallied 3% into the meeting, so the hawkish surprise she is betting on is largely priced; what is *not* priced, and what would move the dollar hard, is any hint of the Fed flagging downside risks to growth. He points to [the positioning data](/blog/trading/event-trading/positioning-and-the-pain-trade): speculative dollar longs are at a six-month extreme, which means the pain trade — the move that hurts the most people — is *down*.

The PM had done real work, but her work had been confirming work: she had assembled the case for why the Fed would be hawkish and never once asked what was already in the price or who was already positioned the way she was about to be. The red-team did not change her directional view, but it convinced her the risk/reward was now badly skewed — most of the upside priced, a fat tail of downside from crowded positioning. She passes on the trade. The meeting comes; the Fed sounds hawkish *and the dollar falls 1.2%* anyway, exactly the "buy the rumor, sell the fact" move the positioning had set up. On \$25,000, the 1.2% adverse move plus the slippage of fighting it would have cost her roughly **\$2,500**. The thirty minutes with a colleague mandated to break the trade saved every dollar of it.

### Countermeasure 3: pre-committed invalidation

The third countermeasure is the spine of the whole series and gets [its own full post](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/what-would-change-my-mind-defining-invalidation-upfront), but it earns a place here because it is the specific antidote to "holding past invalidation." You write down, *before you enter*, the specific, observable event that would prove you wrong — a price level, a data print, a date by which the catalyst must have happened — and you write it when you have nothing at stake, because that is the only moment you can think clearly about it. The pre-commitment matters because the version of you that sets the invalidation level is sober and the version of you that faces the breach is not. You are leaving instructions for your future, biased self, who will arrive at the moment of decision already in the grip of motivated reasoning and unable to set a fair level for himself.

The discipline is in honoring it without renegotiation. The bias's favorite move is to argue that *this particular breach* is the exception — a technical overshoot, a one-quarter blip, a misread by the market. Sometimes it genuinely is, and distinguishing a real break from noise is the hardest call you make. But the default must be that the level holds, because if you allow yourself to renegotiate every breach, you have no invalidation level at all — you have a suggestion, and the bias eats suggestions for breakfast.

#### Worked example: pre-committed invalidation caps a public trade at \$2,000 instead of \$7,500

A trader puts on a \$15,000 position in a retailer ahead of earnings, convinced the consensus is too low. He has been vocal about it — posted the thesis, argued it publicly. Knowing he has made it public, and knowing what that does to his ego, he pre-commits hard: "I exit at \$2,000 of loss, full stop, no matter what I've said about it." He sets the stop and, critically, tells the *same* public audience the invalidation level, so that honoring it becomes the thing he's accountable for, not the direction.

Earnings come; the company beats on revenue but guides margins down, and the stock falls. The bias screams the familiar lines: the margin guide is conservative, the market is overreacting, selling now in front of everyone he told would be humiliating. But the public pre-commitment has flipped the social pressure. Backing out at \$2,000 is now the disciplined thing he *said* he would do, not a cowardly reversal. He exits at his level, down \$2,000. The stock keeps falling and is down a further 5% a week later — had he defended the position the way the un-pre-committed version of him would have, the loss on \$15,000 would have run to roughly **\$7,500**. The pre-commitment did not make him right. It made being wrong cost \$2,000 instead of \$7,500, and it did so by converting his public commitment from an ego trap into an accountability device.

### Countermeasure 4: depersonalize the trade

The fourth countermeasure attacks the root: the fusion of ego and position. A position is a probabilistic bet with a defined edge and a defined risk. It is not a referendum on your intelligence, and a loss on it is not a verdict on you as an analyst — it is one realization of a distribution you knew was there when you put the trade on. The mental move is to install and maintain a *gap* between yourself and the position, so that when the position takes a hit, the hit lands on the position and not on your sense of self.

This sounds soft, but it has hard, behavioral consequences. When ego and position are fused, every red candle is an attack, so you defend; every bear is an enemy, so you fight; cutting the trade is a confession, so you can't. When they are separated, a red candle is just new data to fold into the update, a bear is a free stress test of your thesis, and cutting the trade is simply good process — the unemotional execution of a rule. The practical techniques are concrete: size positions small enough that no single one *can* become your identity (an 8% position is hard to fall in love with; a 30% position is hard not to); commit privately rather than publicly so there's no audience to be wrong in front of; and talk about your trades in the language of probabilities and process ("this was a 55/45 bet that didn't work") rather than the language of being right ("they were wrong about this name").

The language point is not cosmetic. How you describe a trade to yourself shapes what a loss means. If your internal narrative is "I called it" then a loss is a refutation of *you*, and your ego will fight it. If your internal narrative is "I put on a bet with positive expected value at these odds" then a loss is one draw from a distribution you accepted in advance, and there is nothing personal to defend. A useful discipline is to keep the language of every journal entry strictly probabilistic — never "this stock is going up," always "I estimate a 60% chance the thesis pays, with this payoff and this invalidation." The trader who consistently writes in odds finds, over time, that he simply cannot fall in love with a probability the way he can fall in love with a prediction. The framing starves the ego of the certainty it feeds on.

### Countermeasure 5: track what would change your mind

The fifth countermeasure is a small habit with outsized power: for every live thesis, maintain an explicit, written list of the observations that *would* change your mind, and revisit it. This is subtly different from the invalidation level. The invalidation level is the one hard line that ends the trade; the change-my-mind list is the broader set of signals that should *move your probability*, in either direction, before you ever reach the hard line. Writing it down does two things. It makes selective memory impossible — you cannot quietly forget the worrying signal when it is on a list you check. And it makes biased interpretation visible — when a piece of evidence arrives that matches an item on your "would change my mind" list, you have already pre-committed to treating it as meaningful, so you cannot retroactively reinterpret it as noise.

The deeper habit this rests on is the **decision journal** — recording, at the moment of decision, your thesis, your probability, your invalidation, and your reasons, so that later you can audit what you actually believed against what actually happened, instead of auditing a memory the bias has already curated. The journal is the single most effective long-run defense against selective memory, which is why it gets [its own post in this wave](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/the-decision-journal-the-habit-that-compounds). It is also where **base rates** live: if your journal records that "high-conviction catalyst trades I felt this sure about" actually paid 55% of the time, that number is a permanent, bias-proof anchor that no amount of in-the-moment confidence can argue away.

#### Worked example: a Bayesian update done honestly versus done with the bias

Two traders hold the same long thesis at the same felt 75% confidence. Disconfirming evidence arrives: a key customer publicly switches to a competitor — exactly the kind of event that, on a clean read, should lower the probability the thesis is right. The honest trader treats it as a Bayesian update. He had estimated maybe a 20% chance of a major customer defecting if the thesis were right (the product is sticky) versus a 60% chance if the thesis were wrong (the product is not as differentiated as he thinks). The defection is therefore three times more likely in the "I'm wrong" world, so his posterior drops sharply — from 75% to roughly 50%. On a \$10,000 position that now reads as a coin flip, he cuts to \$4,000, locking the loss at a level his updated odds justify.

The biased trader runs the same evidence through motivated reasoning. The defection becomes "an idiosyncratic decision by one customer's new CFO, not a verdict on the product." He assigns it almost no weight — in effect treating the defection as *equally* likely whether the thesis is right or wrong, which makes the evidence uninformative and leaves his 75% untouched. He holds the full \$10,000. The difference between the two traders is not intelligence or information — they have identical information. It is that one let the evidence move his posterior and the other built a story that froze it. When the thesis ultimately breaks, the honest trader loses \$2,400 on his trimmed position and the biased trader loses \$6,000 on his full one. The entire \$3,600 gap is the cost of an update that the bias refused to make.

### Countermeasure 6: the danger of public commitment

The sixth point is less a countermeasure than a warning that shapes the other five: **public commitment is the single biggest accelerant of the thesis-as-identity trap, and you should treat it as a cost.** The moment you tell people your thesis — post it, argue it, stake your reputation on it — you have attached your ego to the position in a way that is extraordinarily hard to detach. Now being wrong is not just losing money; it is being wrong *in front of an audience*, and the unconscious drive to avoid that humiliation will distort every subsequent decision. There is a large body of social-psychology research on this: people who state a position publicly become measurably more resistant to changing it than people who hold the identical position privately, because the public statement creates a consistency pressure that operates independently of the evidence.

This does not mean never share a view — sharing and debating views is how analysts sharpen them. It means *be aware of the price*. Frame public views as provisional ("here's my current read, here's what would change it") rather than as flags planted on a hill. And when you genuinely need maximum flexibility to update — on your largest, highest-conviction positions, exactly where the identity trap is most dangerous — keep your commitment private, or commit publicly to your *process and invalidation* rather than to your *conclusion*. "I'm long X with a stop at \$57 and I'll be out if retention breaks 110%" is a public statement you can honor by exiting. "X is going to \$100 and the doubters will see" is a public statement you can only honor by being right.

## Common misconceptions

Four beliefs about confirmation bias are so common, and so wrong, that they deserve direct correction — because each one is the bias defending itself.

### "I'm objective — this applies to other people"

This is the master misconception, and it has a name: the **bias blind spot**, the well-documented finding that people rate themselves as less susceptible to cognitive bias than the average person, *including the bias of thinking they're less biased*. The reason is structural: you detect bias in others by observing their behavior, but you assess your own bias by introspecting on your intentions — and your intentions are pure, because the bias operates below the level you can introspect. You feel objective because the filtering happened before the feeling. The correct stance is not "I am unbiased"; it is "I am as biased as everyone else, the bias is invisible to me from the inside, and therefore I need external procedures that don't rely on my feeling objective." A trader who believes he is immune is the most exposed trader in the room, because he has declined the only defense that works.

### "Conviction means ignoring the noise and holding"

This one is dangerous because it is *half true*. Genuine discipline does mean holding through noise — not getting shaken out of a good thesis by a random 3% drawdown is a real skill, and traders who lack it bleed out through a thousand premature exits. But the bias hijacks this true principle and uses it as cover. "Holding through the noise" becomes the justification for holding through *signal* — through the actual invalidating evidence — because the bias reclassifies the signal as noise. The distinction that matters is not "do I hold or fold" but "is the new evidence noise or signal," and confirmation bias systematically miscalls signal as noise whenever the signal threatens the thesis. Conviction is supposed to be earned by surviving disconfirmation; the bias counterfeits it by suppressing disconfirmation. (Telling the two apart is hard enough to deserve [its own treatment](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/thesis-broken-or-just-noise-the-hardest-call-you-make).)

### "Doing more research will fix it"

Intuitively, if you're worried about being wrong, the answer is more analysis — more reading, more modeling, more data. But when the *search* is biased, more research makes the problem worse, not better. Every additional hour spent looking for evidence, while motivated reasoning steers you toward the confirming kind, just piles up more confirmation and produces more confidence in a thesis that may be dying. Marcus did enormous amounts of research, and all of it deepened his conviction precisely because all of it was confirmation-shaped. The fix is not *more* research; it is *differently-aimed* research — the deliberate, scheduled hunt for disconfirming evidence specifically. An hour spent trying to break the thesis is worth ten hours spent reinforcing it. Volume is not the lever; direction is.

### "I'll cut it if it's really wrong"

The most reassuring and most false belief. The trader tells himself he is being patient, not stubborn — that of course he would exit if the thesis were *really* broken; he's just waiting for confirmation. The flaw is that confirmation bias is *precisely the mechanism that hides "really wrong" from you.* The point at which a thesis is really wrong is the point at which the disconfirming evidence is overwhelming — and that is exactly the evidence the bias has been most aggressively discounting all along. By the time the breakage is undeniable even to a motivated reasoner, you are at \$37, not \$57; the bias has eaten the entire distance between your invalidation level and your eventual capitulation. "I'll cut it when it's really wrong" outsources the exit decision to the one judgment the bias has most thoroughly compromised. The whole purpose of *pre-committed* invalidation is that it makes the cut decision *before* the bias gets a vote.

## How it plays out in real markets

Confirmation bias is not a personal failing of undisciplined traders; it shows up in the most sophisticated and well-resourced corners of the market, which is the clearest evidence that intelligence and effort are not defenses. Four well-known episodes show the shape.

### A thesis defended past invalidation — the 2021–2022 "transitory" debate

Through 2021, as inflation began climbing, a large and intelligent cohort — including parts of the Federal Reserve — held the "transitory" thesis: the spike was driven by temporary supply-chain dislocations and base effects, and would fade without aggressive tightening. As CPI prints kept coming in hot through late 2021 — well past the point at which a clean read would have downgraded the transitory thesis — the framing did not so much break as *adapt*: each high print was attributed to a specific transitory factor (used cars, then energy, then shelter lag), which is biased interpretation in textbook form, reinterpreting each piece of disconfirming evidence as a special case rather than letting the accumulating evidence move the posterior. Traders who anchored to "transitory" and stayed long duration and long rate-sensitive growth defended the thesis past invalidation and took severe losses when the Fed finally pivoted hard in 2022. The traders who did well were the ones who treated each hot print as a Bayesian update and let their posterior on "transitory" fall as the evidence demanded.

### A disconfirming search that changed sizing — the perennial crowded long

Every cycle has a consensus long that "everyone knows" is going higher — a megacap tech name, a commodity, a theme. The traders who get hurt are the ones whose research is entirely confirmation-shaped: they read the bull notes, follow the bull accounts, and never once check who is already positioned. The traders who size it right run the disconfirming search first and discover, via [positioning data](/blog/trading/event-trading/positioning-and-the-pain-trade), that the trade is crowded — that the marginal buyer is already in, which means the asymmetric risk is a violent unwind. The disconfirming search does not change their directional view; it changes their size, cutting it precisely because the very crowdedness that feels like confirmation ("everyone agrees with me") is in fact the strongest disconfirming signal about the risk/reward.

### The public-commitment trap — the activist who couldn't fold

Public commitment turns thesis into identity with brutal efficiency, and nowhere is it more visible than in activist and high-profile single-stock campaigns. When an investor stakes a large, very public position on a name and goes on television to argue it, the cost of being wrong is no longer just financial — it is reputational and personal, in front of the entire market. The result, repeatedly, is positions defended long past the point a private investor would have folded, because the public commitment created a consistency pressure that operated independently of the deteriorating fundamentals. The lesson is not that conviction is bad; it is that *publicity* loads the ego onto the position and makes the eventual, necessary update vastly harder. The most flexible money is often the quietest.

### A red-team that caught the bias — the structured investment process

The best institutional shops do not rely on individual analysts being unbiased; they build red-teaming into the process. A thesis brought to an investment committee is not just presented; it is *attacked*, with one or more members explicitly tasked to argue the bear case as hard as they can. This works for the structural reason discussed above: it externalizes disconfirmation onto people with no stake in the conclusion. The analyst who built the thesis cannot fairly red-team his own work — his ego is on it — but a colleague whose job is to break it has every incentive to find the flaw. The shops that institutionalize this catch biased theses *before* capital is committed, which is the only cheap place to catch them.

## The playbook

Here is the debiasing routine, reduced to a card you run on every thesis — once before you enter, and again on every scheduled review while the position is live. It takes the place of the willpower you don't have. The whole design principle is that none of these steps require you to feel objective in the moment, because you set them up when you had nothing at stake.

![A two-column checklist card of six debiasing routines run before entry and on every review](/imgs/blogs/confirmation-bias-and-the-thesis-you-fall-in-love-with-7.png)

**Before you enter:**

1. **Pre-commit the invalidation.** Write the specific, observable event — a price, a data print, a date — that proves you wrong, *before* you have a position and your judgment is clear. This is the hard line that ends the trade. If you cannot name what would change your mind, stop: you do not have a thesis, you have an attachment.
2. **Run the disconfirming search.** Spend a deliberate hour answering one question: "what is the single strongest reason this thesis is already wrong, and where would I find the evidence?" Go read the best bear case in existence. Direction of search, not volume of search, is the lever.
3. **Steelman the other side.** Build the strongest possible version of the opposing case — stronger than any real opponent's — and check that it makes you genuinely uncomfortable. If it doesn't, you built a strawman. Let the surviving doubts cut your size.
4. **Assign a red-team.** Hand one person (or, alone, yourself for a fixed window) the explicit mandate to break the trade. Disconfirmation done by someone with no stake in your being right is the only disconfirmation the bias can't sabotage.
5. **Depersonalize and size to it.** The position is a probabilistic bet, not your identity. Size it small enough that it *cannot* become your identity, commit privately rather than loudly, and translate the honestly-disconfirmed probability into a dollar size whose worst case you can absorb without your ego getting involved.

**On every review while it's live:**

6. **Re-run the search and read the journal.** Repeat the disconfirming search on schedule — the bias regenerates. Read your own decision-journal entry: are you *updating the odds* as evidence arrives, or are you *rationalizing the hold*? Then ask the two questions that cut through everything: *Has my invalidation level been hit?* (if yes: out, no negotiation) and *Would I open this trade today, fresh, knowing what I now know?* (if no: why am I still in it?).

A closing note on *when* the routine matters most, because it is exactly backwards from instinct. The natural impulse is to run the debiasing hardest on the trades you're unsure about and to skip it on the ones you're certain of — after all, if you're sure, what is there to check? This is precisely wrong. Certainty is not evidence the thesis is safe; it is evidence that confirmation bias has already done its most thorough work, scrubbing the disconfirming data from your awareness until nothing remains to make you doubt. The trades you are proudest of, the ones that feel like layups, the ones where you catch yourself thinking "this is the easiest money I'll make all year" — those are the trades where the hidden flaw is largest, because your enthusiasm has buried it deepest. Marcus did not lose \$11,000 on a trade he was nervous about. He lost it on the one he was *certain* of. Treat your own certainty as the alarm bell that triggers the routine, not as the excuse to skip it.

The throughline of this series holds here as everywhere: a view that cannot be falsified, sized, and updated is a horoscope, not analysis. Confirmation bias is the specific force that strips a view of all three properties at once — it hides the evidence that would falsify it, inflates the conviction that drives you to oversize it, and freezes the update that would let you revise it. The defense is never to be smarter or more objective; it is to install procedures that act *for* you at the moment your judgment is most compromised. Write down what would prove you wrong before you have a position. Schedule the search for the reasons you're wrong. Assign someone to break the trade. Keep your ego off the position and out of the public square. And when the certainty rises, recognize it for what it usually is — not the absence of risk, but the bias at the height of its powers — and run the routine hardest exactly there.
