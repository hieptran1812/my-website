> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Markets are probabilistic, so the unit of analysis is not a prediction ("oil goes to \$85") but a *distribution* ("\$85 is 60% likely, and here is the other 40% and what it costs me"). The amateur wants certainty; the professional holds the whole range of outcomes and the odds on each, then sizes for the part that loses.
>
> - A prediction is one outcome stated as fact; a probabilistic view is a set of outcomes each with a probability attached. Only the second can be sized, graded, and fed to expected value.
> - "Being right" is not the goal — having *good odds* is. You can be right on a coin-flip call and broke; you can be wrong on a well-priced bet and rich. Process, not outcome, is what you control.
> - Calibrated subjective probabilities beat false certainty. The skill is to assign honest odds, use language that is neither falsely precise ("87.3% sure") nor uselessly vague ("probably"), and to hold the tails, not just the most-likely outcome.
> - The one rule to remember: **state the odds, write down the other side, and size the position to the part of the distribution that loses.**

## Two traders, the same call, two endings

It is early 2022. Inflation is running hot, the Fed has signalled it is done with easy money, and two traders on the same desk look at the same screen and reach the same conclusion: long-duration tech is going to get hit as rates rise. Both are *right* about the world — over the next ten months, the Nasdaq 100 falls roughly a third and the most speculative growth names fall far more. The macro call was correct. And yet one of these two traders is, by autumn, effectively out of a job, while the other is barely scratched and still trading. They made the *same correct call*. What happened?

The first trader was certain. He had done the work, the logic was airtight, and he *knew* tech was going down. So he expressed that certainty the way certainty demands to be expressed: he shorted aggressively, sized as if there were no other side, and added on every bounce because every bounce was just the market being slow to agree with him. When tech rallied 20% off the June lows into August — a vicious bear-market rally that retraced nothing of the larger move but everything of *his* margin — he was so leveraged to a single outcome that the bounce blew through his stops and his capital before the larger thesis ever got to be right. He was right about the destination and wiped out by the path.

The second trader believed the same thing but held it differently. Her note didn't say "tech goes down." It said: *"I think the de-rating continues — call it 60% likely over the next two quarters. But there's a real 40% where a soft inflation print or a Fed pivot rumour triggers a face-ripping rally that retraces 20–30% before the trend resumes. I'm going to be wrong-footed at some point even if my call is right."* Because she had written down that 40% explicitly, she sized for it. Her short was a fraction of his. She used options to cap the cost of being squeezed. When the August rally came, it stung — but it was a budgeted sting, not an extinction event. She rode the budgeted sting and was still in the seat when the trend resumed, and she compounded the rest of the year while he watched from the sidelines. The difference between them was not the quality of the call. It was that only one of them sized to the 40% she openly carried.

![A prediction states one outcome as fact while a probability view holds the whole distribution and sizes for the other side](/imgs/blogs/thinking-in-probabilities-not-predictions-1.png)

That is the entire subject of this post. The first trader was *predicting* — collapsing a fuzzy, uncertain future into a single confident point and betting as if the point were a fact. The second was *thinking in probabilities* — holding the whole distribution of what could happen, attaching honest odds to each branch, and treating the part she'd be wrong about as a real, budgeted cost rather than an embarrassment to be denied. This is the deepest mental shift in the whole craft of forming a view, and almost everyone resists it, because predictions feel like competence and distributions feel like hedging. They are exactly backwards. We will build the idea from zero: what probability actually means when you can't run an experiment a thousand times, why the distribution beats the point estimate, how to assign and *speak* odds without lying to yourself, and the concrete habit that turns "I think X" into "X is 60% likely, here is the 40%, and here is what it costs."

## Foundations: probability, prediction, and the distribution

Before any of the technique, we need to be precise about four words that get thrown around interchangeably and shouldn't be: prediction, probability, distribution, and calibration. These are process words, not market mechanisms, so we build each from the ground up.

A **prediction** is a statement that a specific outcome *will* happen: "oil goes to \$85," "the Fed cuts in September," "this stock doubles." It collapses an uncertain future into one point and asserts it as fact. A prediction has a binary fate — it comes true or it doesn't — and because it admits no other possibility, it carries no information about what happens if it's wrong, how likely "wrong" was, or what wrong costs. It is the future treated as if it were already written and you happened to read the page.

A **probability** is a number between 0 and 1 (or 0% and 100%) that expresses *how likely* you think an outcome is, given what you know now. "I'm 60% confident oil reaches \$85 in three months" is a probability. The crucial move is that it does not assert the outcome; it assigns a *weight* to it, which means it simultaneously, by construction, admits the complementary 40% where oil does *not* reach \$85. A probability is a prediction that has stopped pretending and told you its actual confidence. You cannot have a probability of one thing without implicitly holding probabilities for everything else that could happen instead — and that is the whole point.

A **distribution** is the full set of outcomes you think are possible, each with a probability attached, summing to 100%. Instead of "oil to \$85," the distribution might be: 15% chance it rips above \$90, 35% it reaches the \$83–87 zone, 20% it drifts sideways around \$72, 20% it slips to \$67, 10% it craters below \$62. The distribution is the honest object. The prediction "oil to \$85" is just one slice of it — and not even the biggest slice — wearing a suit and pretending to be the whole thing. Holding the distribution rather than the point is the single behaviour that separates the two traders in the opening.

### Where do these numbers come from? Frequentist vs subjective probability

A fair objection arrives immediately: "Where do I get '60%'? I can't replay 2022 a thousand times. Isn't that number just made up?" This is the question that makes people abandon probabilistic thinking, so we handle it head-on, lightly and without heavy math.

There are two ways to ground a probability. The **frequentist** way defines the probability of an event as the long-run fraction of times it happens if you repeat the experiment many times. The probability a fair coin lands heads is 50% because over a million flips, almost exactly half come up heads. This is rigorous and clean — and almost useless for markets, because you never get to flip the 2022 coin a million times. There is exactly one 2022. There is exactly one *this* earnings report, one *this* Fed meeting. The frequentist machinery has nothing to chew on.

The **subjective** (or **Bayesian**) way defines a probability as a *degree of belief* — your honest, quantified confidence given everything you currently know, which you update as new evidence arrives. When you say "60% oil reaches \$85," you are not claiming you ran the experiment 100 times; you are saying "if I had to bet, I'd lay 60-40 odds, and I'd take either side at worse than those odds." That is a perfectly meaningful, *operational* statement: it tells you exactly what bets you should and shouldn't accept. The number isn't pulled from thin air — it's anchored on base rates (how often have similar setups resolved this way?), adjusted for the specifics of this case, and *checked over time* against what actually happens, which is where calibration comes in. Subjective probability is not "making things up." It is the disciplined quantification of a belief you already hold implicitly the moment you say "I'm bullish."

The deep point is this: **you are already assigning probabilities — you're just doing it badly and silently.** When you say "I'm pretty sure tech goes down," your brain has already done a fuzzy, unexamined probability calculation. Writing it as "65%" doesn't add false precision; it *exposes* a number you were already acting on, so you can interrogate it, size to it, and grade it later. The choice is not "probability vs no probability." It is "explicit, checkable probability vs hidden, uncheckable hunch."

### Calibration: the only way to know if your numbers are any good

If subjective probabilities are degrees of belief, what stops them from being self-flattering nonsense? **Calibration.** A forecaster is *calibrated* when their stated probabilities match reality over many forecasts: of all the things you called "70% likely," about 70% should actually happen; of all your "90%" calls, about 90% should come true. Calibration is the bridge that turns a subjective number into an accountable one. You cannot check a single forecast — one "60%" call that fails proves nothing, since 40%-events happen 40% of the time. But across fifty forecasts, your calibration is brutally measurable, and that measurability is what makes subjective probability a discipline rather than a vibe.

This reframes the entire game. The amateur asks "was I right?" about each call. The professional asks "are my probabilities calibrated?" across all calls. A weather forecaster who says "70% chance of rain" and is rained on 70% of the time is *excellent* even though it didn't rain 30% of the time they warned of it. Being "wrong" on a 70% call is not a failure; being *miscalibrated* — saying 90% when you're only right 65% of the time — is the failure. We give calibration its own deep treatment later and a full sibling post, but plant the idea now: probabilistic thinking is only worth anything if you keep score on the probabilities, not just the outcomes.

### The discomfort, named

There is a reason people resist all of this, and it is emotional, not intellectual. Stating "60%" *feels* weak. It feels like you don't know, like you're covering yourself, like the confident pundit who says "tech is going down, full stop" sounds smarter than you do. Admitting the 40% feels like admitting you might be wrong — which you might, because you might. The whole culture of market commentary rewards the confident point prediction and punishes the honest distribution, because certainty sells and nuance doesn't. Internalising probabilistic thinking means accepting that *the honest answer will always sound less impressive than the dishonest one*, and choosing the honest one anyway because it's the one that survives contact with reality. That discomfort never fully goes away. The pros just stop letting it drive the bus.

It helps to name what the discomfort is actually protecting. Certainty is *cognitively cheap* — a single confident answer ends the thinking, closes the loop, and feels like competence, while a distribution keeps the question open and the loop running, which the brain experiences as unfinished and uncomfortable. There's also a social cost: in a meeting, the person who says "it's going up" sounds like they have an answer, and the person who says "60% up, 30% chance of a real drop, here's the tail" sounds like they're equivocating. The incentive gradient pushes hard toward false certainty, and resisting it is an act of discipline against both your own brain's craving for closure and the room's preference for confident answers. The reward for resisting is not that you sound smarter — you won't, in the moment — but that you're still solvent a year later, which is the only scoreboard that ultimately matters. Every professional has had to make peace with sounding less certain than the loudest person in the room and being right about the distribution anyway.

## Why the distribution beats the point estimate

Now the central technical claim of the post, the one everything else rests on: **you should hold and act on the whole distribution, not the single most-likely outcome (the mode).** This sounds abstract until you put dollars on it, so we will.

Most people, asked for their view, give you the mode — the single outcome they think most likely — and then act as if that outcome is the only one. "I think oil goes to \$85" usually means "\$85 is the outcome I'd bet on if forced to name one." But the mode can be the most likely outcome and *still be a minority of the probability mass.* If \$85 is 35% likely and the other 65% is spread across worse outcomes, then betting on \$85 as if it's a fact means betting on something that probably *won't* happen. The mode is a terrible summary of a view precisely because it discards the 65% that determines whether the bet is any good.

A weather analogy is exact and worth holding onto. A forecaster who reports only the mode would say "it will not rain tomorrow" whenever a dry day is the single most likely outcome — even when there's a 40% chance of rain, because 60% dry beats any single wet scenario. You would never plan a wedding on that forecast; you'd want the *whole* distribution: 60% dry, 30% light rain, 10% downpour, so you can decide whether to rent the tent. Markets are identical. The mode tells you the single most likely outcome; only the distribution tells you whether to rent the tent — that is, whether to hedge, to size down, or to walk away because the tail is too fat to insure against affordably. An analyst who reports only the mode is the forecaster telling you it won't rain while a 40% storm builds, and the difference between those two reports is, quite literally, whether you brought an umbrella.

Here is the same "bullish" call from the opening, held properly — as a distribution over five outcomes, with a dollar position attached so the stakes are real.

#### Worked example: converting a "bullish" call into a distribution and its dollar EV

You're bullish a stock and you put \$20,000 of risk capital into a three-month position. The amateur version of your view is "I think it goes up." The professional version assigns probabilities to a range of P&L outcomes:

- **Big up, +\$6,000:** 15% probability
- **Up, +\$3,000:** 35% probability (this is your *mode* — the single most likely outcome)
- **Flat, \$0:** 20% probability
- **Down, −\$3,000:** 20% probability
- **Big down, −\$7,000:** 10% probability

The probabilities sum to 100%. Notice three things immediately. First, your most likely single outcome is a \$3,000 gain — so "I think it goes up" isn't crazy. Second, the total probability of *losing money* is 20% + 10% = 30%, and the probability of making nothing-or-worse (flat or down) is fully 50%. Your "bullish" call is, on its own numbers, a coin flip on whether you make a cent. Third — and this is the punchline — the **expected value** of the position, the probability-weighted average of all outcomes, is:

$$\text{EV} = 0.15(6000) + 0.35(3000) + 0.20(0) + 0.20(-3000) + 0.10(-7000)$$
$$= 900 + 1050 + 0 - 600 - 700 = +\$650$$

The position has a positive expected value of \$650 on \$20,000 of risk — a real but modest edge, about 3.25% of capital at risk, *not* the slam-dunk that "I think it goes up" implied. The mode (+\$3,000) wildly overstates the attractiveness of the trade; the distribution tells you the truth. **The mode is what you hope happens; the distribution is what you're actually being paid, and it folds in every way you can be wrong.**

![One bullish call held as a probability distribution over five P and L outcomes with the mode and expected value marked](/imgs/blogs/thinking-in-probabilities-not-predictions-2.png)

Look at the figure and sit with what it shows. The tallest bar — the mode — is a gain. If you only held the mode, you'd think this trade was a clear winner. But the eye is drawn to the wrong place. The *story of the trade* is in the spread: a fat left tail (the −\$7,000 outcome at 10%) that the confident "I think it goes up" never mentions, and a 30% chance of an outright loss. The position is still worth doing — \$650 of positive EV is real edge — but you now know to size it modestly and to expect to lose on it almost a third of the time without that meaning your process was broken. The amateur who held only the mode would have been blindsided by the −\$7,000 outcome and concluded he was "wrong." The pro who held the distribution sees the −\$7,000 land, shrugs, and notes that a 10% event happened, which is exactly what 10% events do.

### The tails are where you live or die

The most dangerous part of any distribution is not the middle — it's the tails, the low-probability extreme outcomes that the mode-thinker ignores entirely. The −\$7,000 outcome above is "only" 10% likely, but it is more than twice the size of the +\$3,000 mode. Tails are where the asymmetry hides. A view with a pleasant mode and a brutal left tail can have *negative* EV even though "the most likely thing is a gain" — and a view with a modest mode and a fat *right* tail can be a screaming buy even though it'll lose more often than it wins.

This is why **tail-blindness** — looking only at the central outcome and treating the extremes as "that won't happen" — is one of the costliest errors in the book. The 2022 trader who was wiped out was tail-blind: he held the mode ("tech down") and ignored the 40% tail (a violent bear-market rally) that, while less likely than his call, was more than large enough to end him before his call could pay off. The market does not care that an outcome was unlikely. A 10% event happens one time in ten, and if that one time is large enough relative to your position, "unlikely" becomes "terminal." Holding the whole distribution — and especially respecting the tails — is not pessimism. It is the difference between a budgeted sting and an extinction event. (The deeper mechanics of why financial tails are fatter than a normal distribution implies — and why standard risk models systematically under-price them — are the subject of [fat tails and the normal distribution trap](/blog/trading/risk-management/fat-tails-and-the-normal-distribution-trap).)

### The market is already quoting you a distribution

Here is the part of the spine that makes probabilistic thinking concrete rather than philosophical: *the market price is itself a probability distribution*, and once you see that, your job snaps into focus. A stock price is not the market's prediction of where the stock will be; it is the present value of the market's whole distribution of future outcomes, weighted by the capital behind each. The options market makes this literal — the prices of calls and puts across strikes encode an *implied distribution* of where the underlying might end up, including the market's own estimate of the tails. When you trade, you are not betting against "the market's prediction." You are betting that *your* distribution differs from the *market's* distribution in a way you can get paid for. The whole edge is the gap between two distributions, not the gap between your prediction and a single market number.

This reframes the analyst's job one more useful notch. The amateur asks "where is the stock going?" — a prediction question with a single-point answer. The professional asks two distribution questions: "what's my probability distribution over outcomes?" and "what distribution is the market pricing?" The trade exists only where those two distributions disagree. If you think a binary event is 60% likely and the options market is pricing it at 60%, you have no trade no matter how confident you feel — you *agree* with the consensus distribution, and agreeing pays nothing. If you think it's 60% and the market is pricing 35%, *that 25-point gap is the entire trade*, and the size of the gap, not the level of your confidence, is what determines the edge. This is why two analysts with the identical view ("this is 60% likely") can have completely different trades: one is 60% against a market pricing 58%, with no edge, and the other is 60% against a market pricing 35%, with enormous edge. The mechanism by which the market's consensus distribution forms and embeds itself in the price is the subject of [what's priced in: the question behind every trade](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/whats-priced-in-the-question-behind-every-trade); the point to carry forward here is that *both sides of the trade are distributions*, and probabilistic thinking is what lets you see and exploit the gap between them.

There's a second consequence that beginners miss. Because the market is quoting a distribution, the *tails are priced too* — and they're often priced expensively, because everyone wants insurance against the same crash. A trader who is right that a tail event is more likely than the consensus thinks can still *lose money buying protection against it*, if the protection was already priced for an even fatter tail. Probabilistic thinking cuts both ways: it stops you from being blind to tails, and it stops you from overpaying for tails the market has already priced in. The discipline is always the same — your distribution versus the market's, and the edge living only in the difference.

## Assigning subjective probabilities without lying to yourself

So you're convinced you should hold a distribution. How do you actually *generate* the numbers? "60%" can't be a feeling you blurt out; it has to be a number you can defend. There are three disciplines that turn a gut sense into a probability you'd stake money on.

**Start from the base rate.** Before you look at anything specific about this situation, ask: how often does this *kind* of thing happen in general? If a richly-valued momentum stock with a great story is being pitched to triple in twelve months, the relevant base rate is "how often do richly-valued momentum names actually triple in a year?" — and the honest answer is *rarely*, maybe high single digits. The base rate is the gravitational centre your estimate should orbit. You then *adjust* for the specifics of this case, but you start from the base rate, because the base rate already encodes a thousand situations that looked just as compelling and mostly didn't pan out. We'll see the dollar cost of skipping this step shortly.

**Decompose the bet into a sequence of conditions.** A single big probability is hard to estimate, but a chain of smaller ones is tractable. "Will this biotech stock double on its drug?" is hard. But "What's the probability the trial reads out on time (80%), times the probability it hits its primary endpoint given it reads out (50%), times the probability the FDA approves given it hits (70%), times the probability the stock doubles given approval (60%)?" is four estimable numbers — and their product, 0.8 × 0.5 × 0.7 × 0.6 ≈ 17%, is far more defensible (and far lower) than the 50%+ your gut would have produced staring at the whole thing at once. Decomposition is the single best antidote to overconfidence, because each link in the chain shaves the total down toward reality.

**Calibrate against bets you'd actually take.** A clean test for any probability: would you take the bet at those odds? If you say "70% this happens," you're claiming you'd happily take a bet that pays \$1 if it happens and loses you \$2.33 if it doesn't (because 70/30 odds are 2.33-to-1). If that bet makes you flinch, your 70% is a lie — your real number is lower, and you should write the lower one down. Reframing a probability as a bet you'd actually place strips out the wishful thinking, because money concentrates the mind in a way that opinions don't.

#### Worked example: decomposing a bet to find the honest probability

A biotech name at \$20 is being pitched as a double on its lead drug. Your gut, staring at the whole story at once, says "I'd put it at 55% it doubles in the next year," and you're tempted to put \$10,000 behind that. Stop and decompose the path into the conditions that actually have to hold, estimating each one and asking yourself the bet-test at every link:

- The trial **reads out on time** (vs. delay): about **80%** likely.
- *Given* it reads out, it **hits its primary endpoint**: about **50%** — these are coin flips even for good drugs.
- *Given* it hits, the **FDA approves**: about **70%**.
- *Given* approval, the stock actually **doubles** (much is already priced into a \$20 hopeful): about **60%**.

Multiply the chain: 0.80 × 0.50 × 0.70 × 0.60 ≈ **0.17**, or 17%. Your defensible probability of a double is 17%, not the 55% your gut produced — because your gut quietly assumed every link was near-certain and never made itself multiply four real risks together. Now price the bet: a 17% shot at +\$10,000 against an 83% chance of, say, −\$6,000 (a failed-trial biotech doesn't go to zero but routinely halves) has an EV of 0.17(10,000) + 0.83(−6,000) = 1,700 − 4,980 = **−\$3,280**. The trade your gut loved is sharply negative-EV once the probability is honest. **Decomposition is the cheapest overconfidence-killer there is: each "given that" you're forced to write down shaves the fantasy probability back toward what the math will actually pay.**

### The language of confidence: between false precision and useless vagueness

Once you have a number, you have to *say* it — to yourself in a journal, to a PM, to a risk committee. And there is a narrow band of useful language flanked by two failure modes on either side.

On one side is **false precision**: "I'm 87.3% confident." No one's read of a market is accurate to a tenth of a percent. A decimal point on a subjective probability is not extra rigour; it's a *lie about your own resolution*, implying a precision your process can't possibly support. It also anchors you and your listener to a fake exactness that discourages updating. If your honest internal resolution is "somewhere in the high 80s," say "about 85%" or "roughly six-to-one," not "87.3%."

On the other side is **useless vagueness**: "probably," "likely," "I feel good about this." These words feel safe because they commit to nothing, but that's exactly why they're worthless — "likely" can mean 55% to one person and 95% to another, so it can't be sized, can't be graded, and can't be checked for calibration. Famously, intelligence analysts studying the phrase "a serious possibility" found colleagues who used it meant anything from 20% to 80%. A word that spans that range transmits no information. If you can't be pinned to a number, you can't be wrong — which is precisely the horoscope move we're trying to escape.

The useful middle is **coarse but committed**: round numbers and simple ratios you can actually distinguish and defend. "About 60%." "Two-to-one in favour." "I'd call it 70-30." "Maybe one-in-five." These are precise enough to size and grade, honest enough not to lie about resolution, and committed enough to be checked against reality later. The skill is to live in this band — never sharper than your process supports, never vaguer than a bet requires.

![The confidence language ladder from useless vagueness through the calibrated band to false precision](/imgs/blogs/thinking-in-probabilities-not-predictions-3.png)

The ladder in the figure is the mental model for how you should speak about every view. The bottom rung — "probably," "I feel good" — gives you no number, so it gives you no size and no grade; it's where most market chatter lives and why most market chatter is useless. The top rung — "87.3% sure" — claims a resolution you don't have and quietly discourages you from updating. The wide middle band is the only place a tradeable view can live: coarse buckets like "~75% it holds, and if it breaks I'm wrong" that you can put a position behind and mark against reality on a deadline. Aim for the middle, every time. The instant you hear yourself reach for "probably," push down to a number; the instant you write a decimal, round up to a bucket.

## "Being right" vs "having good odds"

Here is the idea that, once it clicks, changes how you think about every trade forever — and the one that most people never fully accept because it offends the ego. **The goal is not to be right. The goal is to have good odds and to size them well.** These come apart constantly, and conflating them is the root of more blown-up accounts than any single technical error.

Consider four cases. You can be *right with bad odds*: you bet everything on a 55-45 coin flip, it lands your way, you make money, and you congratulate yourself on a great call — when in fact you got lucky on a barely-edged bet that you'd lose nearly half the time and that, sized that aggressively, will eventually ruin you. You can be *wrong with good odds*: you take an 80-20 bet, the 20% happens, you lose — and you did *everything right*, because 20%-events happen one time in five and your job was never to avoid the 20%, only to get paid for the 80%. You can be *right with good odds* (great, but partly luck that it resolved this time) and *wrong with bad odds* (you deserved it). The outcome of any single bet tells you almost nothing about the quality of the decision, because a single draw can't distinguish a good process from a lucky one or a bad process from an unlucky one.

What you *control* is the odds you take and the size you put on. What you *don't control* is which outcome the dice produce on any given roll. This is the heart of **process over outcome**: judge your decisions by whether you had a genuine edge and sized it sanely, not by whether the most recent one paid off. A trader who only ever asks "did this trade make money?" is optimising the one thing they can't control and ignoring the only things they can. (This is the same trap that makes win rate such a deceptive number — a high win rate can hide a negative expectancy, as [expectancy: why win rate lies](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/expectancy-why-win-rate-lies) lays out in detail.)

The practical consequence: when a trade loses, the *first* question is never "was I wrong?" It's "was the process sound — did I have an edge, were the odds in my favour, was the size sane?" If yes, a loss is just a 20%-event landing, and you do exactly the same thing next time. If no, the loss is a gift that exposed a broken process, and the fact that you might have *made money* on it is the dangerous part, because a bad process that gets lucky teaches you to repeat it until it kills you.

The most insidious version of this is the **lucky idiot** — the trader whose process is negative-EV but who happens to draw a good run, gets promoted or funded on the strength of it, sizes up, and then reverts to the true mean of their process at the worst possible scale. Put numbers on it: a trader makes ten bets, each a 40/60 *against* him (he wins \$10,000 with 40% probability, loses \$10,000 with 60%). His EV per bet is 0.40(10,000) + 0.60(−10,000) = −\$2,000 — a genuinely bad process, bleeding \$2,000 of expected value every time he clicks. But variance is wide over ten bets: there's a real chance he wins five or six of them and finishes the stretch *up* \$10,000 to \$20,000, purely on luck. If he reads that profit as proof of skill and triples his size, the negative EV — now −\$6,000 a bet — grinds him to zero over the next fifty trades. The profit was the trap. **A positive P&L on a negative-EV process is the most expensive thing that can happen to you, because it buys you the confidence to do more of the thing that's killing you.** The only defence is to grade the process, not the outcome — to know your edge cold, in probabilities, *before* the dice are thrown, so a winning streak can't be mistaken for a signal.

This is also the deepest reason to write probabilities down before the resolution. Once you know the outcome, hindsight rewrites your memory of how confident you "really" were — you'll swear you "always knew" the thing that happened was likely, and "always had doubts" about the thing that didn't. A probability committed to paper *before* the event is the only honest record of your process, immune to the self-flattering edit that hindsight performs automatically. The number on the page is your process; the outcome is the dice. Keep them separate or you'll never learn anything from either.

## Probability as the input to expected value and sizing

Everything so far has been about generating honest odds. But odds are not the *output* of analysis — they are the *input* to the two things that actually determine your P&L: expected value and position size. A probability sitting in a journal does nothing. A probability fed into EV and then into size is the whole machine.

![From probability through expected value and the edge check to position size](/imgs/blogs/thinking-in-probabilities-not-predictions-6.png)

The pipeline in the figure is the complete logic of a quantitative view. Your view is a *distribution* — bull, base, bear, each with a probability and a payoff. Those feed **expected value**: the probability-weighted average payoff, the single number that says whether the bet is worth taking at all. EV in turn feeds the **edge check** — is the EV positive *after* accounting for what's already priced in, fees, and the cost of carry? And the edge, combined with the odds, feeds **position size**: how much you put on. Crucially, the arrows only run one direction. You cannot skip to "how much do I bet?" without first having the probabilities, because size is a *function* of edge and odds. The amateur starts at the end ("I'll buy \$50,000 because I'm confident") and works backward to justify it. The pro starts at the beginning, with the distribution, and lets size fall out of the math.

We treat EV in full in [expected value: the only math a view really needs](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/expected-value-the-only-math-a-view-really-needs), so here we focus on the connection that the present post owns: *how the shape of your probability changes the size you should put on.* Two views with the same direction but different confidence should be sized completely differently, and getting this wrong is how people turn good calls into bad outcomes.

#### Worked example: sizing a 60/40 view vs an 80/20 view

You have two separate trade ideas, each a binary bet that either pays +\$10,000 or loses −\$10,000, and on each you're considering risking from a \$200,000 book. The only difference is your confidence.

**Idea A — a 60/40 view.** 60% it works (+\$10,000), 40% it doesn't (−\$10,000).
$$\text{EV}_A = 0.60(10{,}000) + 0.40(-10{,}000) = 6{,}000 - 4{,}000 = +\$2{,}000$$

**Idea B — an 80/20 view.** 80% it works (+\$10,000), 20% it doesn't (−\$10,000).
$$\text{EV}_B = 0.80(10{,}000) + 0.20(-10{,}000) = 8{,}000 - 2{,}000 = +\$6{,}000$$

Both are positive-EV; both are worth doing. But idea B has *three times* the expected value of idea A on the same payoff structure, and its loss probability is half. A sizing rule that scales with edge — the spirit of the Kelly criterion, which says bet a fraction of capital proportional to your edge — would put far more behind B than A. Using the simplified Kelly fraction for an even-money bet (`f = 2p − 1`, the edge), idea A says risk 2 × 0.60 − 1 = **20%** of the Kelly-optimal unit, while idea B says risk 2 × 0.80 − 1 = **60%** — three times the position. If you instead size A and B *the same* because "I like both ideas," you are massively over-betting the 60/40 relative to its edge and under-betting the 80/20 relative to its edge, leaving expected growth on the table on B while taking too much ruin risk on A. **The probability isn't just whether you take the bet — it's how big the bet is, and a 60/40 and an 80/20 are different-sized trades even when they point the same way.** (The full machinery of scaling size to edge lives in [position sizing and the Kelly criterion](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/position-sizing-and-kelly-criterion); the point here is only that the probability is the input that drives it.)

This is also why holding the distribution rather than the mode matters for survival, not just accuracy. The trader who holds only "I'm bullish" has no probability, so he has no principled size — he sizes to conviction, to mood, to how good the story sounds, and conviction is exactly the thing that's highest right before a fat tail eats him. The trader who holds "60% up, 30% chance of a real loss" has a number, and the number caps the size at something he can survive the 30% on.

## Common misconceptions

A few beliefs about probabilistic thinking are not just wrong but *actively prevent* people from adopting it. Each one deserves a direct correction.

**"Good analysts make confident predictions."** This is the most damaging myth, because it's reinforced by every confident pundit on television. In reality, the most confident-sounding analysts are usually the *worst* calibrated — confidence is cheap and inversely correlated with honesty about uncertainty. The best analysts sound *less* certain in public because they're carrying the whole distribution in their heads, and the distribution is genuinely uncertain. A confident point prediction is not a sign of skill; it's a sign that someone has either fooled themselves into ignoring the other outcomes or is performing confidence for an audience. The skill is in the *odds*, not the swagger. The world rewards the swagger; the market rewards the odds.

**"Saying '60% confident' is wishy-washy — just tell me what you think."** This treats a probability as evasion when it is the opposite: it is the *most* committed thing you can say, because it can be checked. "I think it goes up" can never be graded — up by how much, by when, and was a 1% drift a win? "60% it's above \$85 in three months" can be marked exactly and rolled into your calibration record. The vague version protects the speaker; the probabilistic version exposes them. "60%" isn't hedging — it's the only statement in the room with skin in the game, because it's the only one that can be proven wrong.

**"The most likely outcome is what will happen."** This is the mode fallacy, and it's why the opening trader blew up. The most likely single outcome can be — and in dispersed distributions usually *is* — a minority of the probability mass. If the mode is 35% likely, then 65% of the time *something else* happens. Treating the mode as "the answer" means being surprised 65% of the time and sizing as if you won't be. The most likely outcome is worth naming; it is never worth betting on as if it were certain.

**"Probabilities are just made up — they're not real data."** This confuses *frequentist* probability (long-run frequencies) with *subjective* probability (degrees of belief), and concludes that since markets aren't repeatable experiments, the numbers are fiction. But the alternative to a calibrated subjective probability is not "no probability" — it's a hidden, unexamined, *uncalibrated* hunch that you're acting on anyway. You're already assigning odds the moment you say "I'm bullish"; the only question is whether you write them down where they can be checked and improved, or leave them implicit where they can't. A calibrated subjective probability — one you've tested against fifty past forecasts and found roughly accurate — is enormously more reliable than the false certainty of a confident prediction. "Made up but checked" beats "felt but hidden" every time.

**"Thinking in distributions makes you indecisive — you'll never pull the trigger."** This is the fear that holding the whole range of outcomes leaves you paralysed, forever seeing both sides and never acting. The reality is the exact opposite: probabilistic thinking is what *makes* decisive action possible, because it tells you precisely when and how big to act. A point-predictor is actually the indecisive one — when his single prediction starts to fail, he has no framework for what to do, so he freezes, hopes, or doubles down on the broken call. The distribution-thinker has already written down the other side, so when the 40% starts happening she's not surprised, not paralysed, and not in denial — she's executing a plan she made in advance. Decisiveness isn't the absence of doubt; it's having priced the doubt ahead of time so it can't ambush you. The probabilistic trader pulls the trigger *faster*, because the analysis that usually causes hesitation — "but what if I'm wrong?" — was completed before the position went on.

## How it plays out in real markets

Theory is cheap. Here is what each failure mode actually costs, in dollars, in three episodes you'll recognise.

#### Worked example: overconfidence — 90% calls that hit 65% — over 20 trades

A trader keeps a journal and, reviewing a year, discovers something uncomfortable: across the calls he marked "90% confident," he was actually right only **65%** of the time. He's not stupid or unlucky — he's *overconfident*, a near-universal human bias where stated confidence systematically exceeds realised accuracy, especially at the high end. The problem is that he *sized* to 90%. On each such call he risked \$20,000, expecting to lose roughly one time in ten, so the occasional loss felt acceptable. But at a true 65% hit rate, over 20 such trades, he wins about 13 and loses about 7 — not the 18-and-2 he priced in.

Suppose each winner makes \$8,000 and each loser drops \$20,000 (he sized big because he was "90% sure," so his bet was asymmetric against him — small wins, big position, fat loss when wrong). At his *assumed* 90%: 18 wins × \$8,000 − 2 losses × \$20,000 = \$144,000 − \$40,000 = **+\$104,000**. At his *actual* 65%: 13 × \$8,000 − 7 × \$20,000 = \$104,000 − \$140,000 = **−\$36,000**. The 25-percentage-point gap between stated and realised confidence turned a fantasy +\$104,000 into a real −\$36,000 — a \$140,000 swing — entirely because he sized to a confidence he didn't have. **Overconfidence isn't a personality quirk; it's a sizing error, and the bill comes due in the gap between what you said and what was true.**

![Calibration curve showing stated confidence versus realized hit rate with the ninety to sixty-five gap marked](/imgs/blogs/thinking-in-probabilities-not-predictions-4.png)

The figure shows what his calibration record looks like once he plots it: the dashed diagonal is perfect calibration (when you say 70%, you're right 70%), and his measured curve sags below it across the whole high-confidence range, with the "90% stated, 65% realised" point — a 25-point gap — sitting deep in the overconfidence zone. The fix is not to try harder to be right; it's to *recalibrate the numbers*. Once he knows his "90%" really means 65%, he can either shrink the size to match the true odds or do the work to genuinely earn the 90%. Either way, the journal — the keeping of score — is what surfaced a bias he'd never have seen from the inside. This is the entire argument for [calibration: keeping score on your own forecasts](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/calibration-keeping-score-on-your-own-forecasts), which makes the practice concrete.

#### Worked example: base-rate neglect on a hyped name

It's the height of a thematic mania — pick your era; the structure repeats. A charismatic story stock is being pitched everywhere: revolutionary product, visionary founder, "this could be a ten-bagger." You read the deck, you watch the founder's interview, and your gut says "yeah, this triples in a year — I'd put it at 70%." You're about to size a \$15,000 position to that 70%.

Stop and ask the base-rate question: *historically, how often do richly-valued, heavily-hyped momentum names actually triple within twelve months?* Pull the reference class — high-multiple growth names with similar hype — and the honest base rate is around **8%**. Not zero, but nowhere near 70%. The story is vivid and specific and the base rate is dull and statistical, and your brain weights the vivid story far above the dull statistic — that's **base-rate neglect**, and it's the engine of every bubble. Now blend properly: *anchor* on the 8% base rate and *nudge* for whatever is genuinely better-than-average about this specific name, landing maybe at **18%** — higher than the base rate because the case has real merits, but a universe away from your gut's 70%.

![Base-rate neglect bar chart comparing gut estimate, historical base rate, and a calibrated blended estimate](/imgs/blogs/thinking-in-probabilities-not-predictions-5.png)

The figure makes the gap unmissable: a 62-percentage-point chasm between the story (70%) and the calibrated estimate (18%), with the 8% base rate as the gravity your number should have started from. The dollar consequence: at your gut's 70%, a \$15,000 bet on a triple looks like a no-brainer with a huge EV. At the calibrated 18%, with a realistic downside (these names routinely fall 60–80% when the story breaks), the EV is likely *negative* — you'd be paying for a lottery ticket priced as if it were a sure thing. **Base-rate neglect is expensive precisely because it feels like insight: the story is doing the work the statistics should be doing, and the market charges full price for the difference.** (The mechanism by which a hyped name's optimism gets fully embedded in the price — so that even being right on the story can lose money — is [what's priced in: the question behind every trade](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/whats-priced-in-the-question-behind-every-trade).)

### Back to 2022: the budgeted sting vs the extinction event

Return to the two traders from the opening, now with the framework to see exactly what happened. Both held the same *mode*: tech down. The first held *only* the mode and sized to it as certainty, which is overconfidence and tail-blindness fused into one position. The 40% tail — the bear-market rally that wasn't his base case — was, by his own implicit numbers, a four-in-ten event, and four-in-ten events are not rare; they're *routine*. He treated a routine event as impossible and sized accordingly, so when it arrived it didn't dent him, it ended him. The second trader held the distribution: 60% down, 40% a vicious counter-rally first. Because the 40% was written down, it was budgeted, and because it was budgeted, it was sized to be survivable. When the August squeeze came, it cost her exactly what she'd reserved for it and no more. She was wrong-footed for six weeks and right for the year. The macro call — the thing pundits would have graded them on — was identical. The probabilistic discipline was the entire difference between the two P&Ls.

## The playbook: the probability habit

Probabilistic thinking is not a talent; it's a *habit* you install, the same four moves run on every view until they're automatic. Here is the concrete process.

**1. State the odds — convert every prediction into a probability.** The moment you catch yourself making a flat prediction ("X will happen"), stop and put a number on it. Not "I'm bullish" but "60% it's above the target in three months." Use the calibrated middle band: round numbers and simple ratios, never a decimal of false precision, never a vague "probably." If you can't put a number on it, you don't have a view — you have a feeling, and feelings can't be sized or graded.

**2. Write down the other side.** For every view, explicitly write the complement: if you're 60% bull, write down the 40% — *what specifically happens in that 40%, how bad it is, and what would trigger it.* This single act defeats both overconfidence (you can't claim 95% once you've written a credible 40% other-side) and tail-blindness (the fat left tail is now on paper instead of in your blind spot). The other side is not a hedge against your conviction; it is *part of your view*, and a view without an explicit other side is half a view.

**3. Hold the whole distribution, not the mode.** Sketch the range of outcomes — base, bull, bear, and the tails — each with a probability that sums to 100%. Look at the spread, not just the peak. Where is the fat tail? What's the total probability of an outright loss? Is the mode a majority or a minority of the mass? You're not betting on the most likely outcome; you're betting on the *whole shape*, and the shape — especially the tails — is what determines whether the bet is good. (Building this base/bull/bear range systematically is the subject of [base, bull and bear: building three scenarios](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/base-bull-and-bear-building-three-scenarios).)

**4. Feed it to EV and size.** Run the distribution through expected value — the probability-weighted payoff — to confirm the bet is positive after what's priced in, fees, and carry. Then let *size* fall out of the edge and the odds: an 80/20 view is a bigger position than a 60/40 view even when they point the same way. Never start from "how much do I want to bet?"; always start from the distribution and let the math hand you the size. Then size to survive the part you'll be wrong about — the 30% or 40% you wrote down in step two.

**5. Keep score and recalibrate.** Log every probability you state alongside what actually happened. Over fifty forecasts, your calibration becomes measurable: do your "70%" calls hit 70%? If your "90%" calls hit 65%, you're overconfident and you shrink your sizing or sharpen your work. This closes the loop — it's the only thing that turns subjective probabilities from self-flattering guesses into a genuine, improving skill. (The full scorekeeping method is [calibration: keeping score on your own forecasts](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/calibration-keeping-score-on-your-own-forecasts); the related discipline of grading *how sure you were* is [measuring conviction: how sure are you really?](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/measuring-conviction-how-sure-are-you-really).)

![The overconfidence trap diagram showing the same call sized two ways leading to wiped out or barely dented](/imgs/blogs/thinking-in-probabilities-not-predictions-7.png)

The trap in the figure is the whole post in one picture, and it's worth ending on. The same call — the one you feel "90% sure" about — splits into two fates. Size it to your *felt* 90% conviction and the 35% miss rate that reality actually delivers wipes you out, because you bet as if the miss couldn't happen. Size it to your *calibrated* 65% and the very same miss barely dents you, because you budgeted for it from the start. The call was identical. The market was identical. The only variable was whether you sized to a confidence you wished you had or to one you'd actually earned. That is the entire edge of thinking in probabilities instead of predictions: not that you'll be right more often — you won't — but that you'll *survive being wrong*, which is the only way to still be in the seat when you're right.

The shift, finally stated plainly: stop asking "what will happen?" and start asking "what *could* happen, how likely is each, and what does the part I'm wrong about cost me?" The amateur wants the comfort of a single confident answer. The professional has made peace with the fact that the future is a distribution, that the honest answer always sounds less impressive than the confident lie, and that the edge was never in being right — it was in holding the whole distribution and sizing for the 40% you openly carried.
