> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Being *calibrated* means your stated confidence matches reality: of everything you called 70% likely, about 70% actually happens. Most people are overconfident — their 90% calls land closer to 60% — and that gap silently poisons every downstream calculation. Calibration is measurable (the calibration curve, the Brier score) and trainable, and it is the *input* to expected value and sizing: honest probabilities make the math work; inflated ones turn a winning book into a losing one.
>
> - Calibration is not "are you usually right?" — it is "do your stated probabilities match your realized hit rates, bucket by bucket?" A weather forecaster who is rained on 70% of the time they say 70% is perfectly calibrated even though it stayed dry 30% of those times.
> - You measure it mechanically: bucket your last N journaled calls by stated confidence, compute the hit rate inside each bucket, plot realized against stated, and read the gap. A single number — the Brier score — rolls calibration and resolution into one grade you can track over time.
> - Overconfidence is the default failure, and it is expensive in dollars, not just pride: a +EV-looking book built on 90% calls that really land 62% can have a *negative* true expected value. Garbage probabilities in, garbage sizing out.
> - The one rule to remember: **you cannot fix a gap you have not measured — bucket your last 50 calls, plot the curve, compute the Brier, then widen your ranges and anchor to base rates until your stated confidence stops lying to you.**

## The trader who was "right 90% of the time"

He will tell you, without prompting, that he is right "90% of the time." He says it in the morning meeting and he believes it completely — he has been trading for eleven years, he reads everything, his logic is usually airtight, and the calls he remembers are the ones that worked. When he puts on a position with that kind of certainty, he sizes it like certainty: a big slug of the book, no hedge, and he adds on any move against him because the move against him is just the market being slow. Ninety percent, after all, barely loses.

Then one quiet afternoon, bored between catalysts, he does something he has never actually done before. He goes back through his trade notes for the year — the ones where he wrote down a real conviction before the outcome was known — and he sorts them. He pulls out every call where he wrote something like "I'm 90% on this" or "near-certain" or "this is happening." There are forty-one of them. He counts how many actually played out the way he said. The answer is twenty-five. Twenty-five out of forty-one. His 90% calls, the ones he sized like coin-flips-that-can't-lose, came true **62% of the time.**

That gap — 90% stated, 62% realized — is not a rounding error or a bad month. It is a systematic, repeatable, *expensive* bias baked into the way his brain converts a feeling into a number. And because every position he sizes runs that inflated number through his expected-value math and his sizing rule, the bias is not sitting harmlessly in a journal. It is in every trade he has on right now. He has been sizing 62%-likely bets as if they were 90%-likely bets for eleven years, and the slow leak between the two has been quietly clipping his returns the entire time — not in any single dramatic blowup, but in a thousand positions that were a little too big for the edge they actually carried.

![The calibration curve showing perfect diagonal versus an overconfident curve that sags below it](/imgs/blogs/calibration-keeping-score-on-your-own-forecasts-1.png)

That is the entire subject of this post. *Calibration* is the discipline of keeping score on your own forecasts — of checking, across many calls, whether your stated probabilities match what actually happens. It is the foundation underneath everything else in this series: [thinking in probabilities, not predictions](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/thinking-in-probabilities-not-predictions) tells you to state odds; calibration is what tells you whether those odds are worth anything. A probabilistic view is only as good as the probabilities, and uncalibrated probabilities — however confidently stated — are noise wearing a number. We will build the idea from zero: what calibration actually means, why overconfidence is the near-universal default, how to *measure* your calibration with a curve and a single Brier number, and the concrete habits that close the gap between what you say and what comes true.

## Foundations: calibration, the curve, and the Brier score

Before any technique, we need to be precise about a cluster of words that get used loosely and mean very different things: calibration, accuracy, resolution, overconfidence, the calibration curve, and the Brier score. These are scoring concepts, not market mechanisms, so we build each from the ground up with a concrete example.

A **forecast**, for our purposes, is a statement of the form "X will happen, and I am P% confident." "The S&P holds 4,200 into month-end — I'm 75% on that." "This biotech gets FDA approval — 40%." "Earnings beat and the stock gaps up — 65%." The forecast has two parts that we must keep separate: the *claim* (what you say will happen) and the *stated probability* (how confident you are). A forecast resolves to one of two states when its date arrives: it **happened** (we score it 1) or it **did not** (we score it 0). Everything that follows is about comparing the probabilities you stated to the 1s and 0s that came back.

**Calibration** is the property that your stated probabilities match your realized hit rates *over many forecasts*. You are calibrated if, of all the things you called 70% likely, about 70% of them actually happen; of all your 90% calls, about 90% come true; of all your 30% calls, about 30% happen. Calibration is a statement about a *whole collection* of forecasts, not any single one. You literally cannot tell whether one "70%" call was well-calibrated — it either happened or it didn't, and both are perfectly consistent with a true 70% probability. Calibration only becomes visible when you have dozens of calls at similar confidence levels and can ask: did the right *fraction* of them come true? This is the single most important and most counterintuitive thing about the whole subject, so hold it: **calibration is invisible on any single call and brutally measurable across many.**

**Overconfidence** is the failure mode where your stated probabilities are systematically *higher* than your realized hit rates — your 90% calls land 62%, your 70% calls land 55%. Your curve sags below the line. **Underconfidence** is the opposite and rarer failure: your stated probabilities are systematically *lower* than reality — your 60% calls land 80% — so you leave edge on the table by sizing timidly. The overwhelming, documented default across nearly every domain studied — from doctors to engineers to weather forecasters before they were trained, and emphatically including traders — is overconfidence. The brain manufactures certainty cheaply and reluctantly admits doubt, and the result is that the number you blurt out is almost always too high.

### Calibration vs accuracy vs resolution

Three words get tangled here, and untangling them is worth the paragraph. **Accuracy** is the crude "was I right?" count — how often your most-likely call came true. It is a bad measure of a forecaster because it ignores the probabilities entirely: a forecaster who says "70% rain" every single day in a city that rains 70% of days is perfectly accurate in aggregate but has told you nothing useful on any given day.

**Calibration**, as we said, is whether your stated probabilities match reality. But calibration alone is not enough either — and this trips people up. A forecaster who says "70% chance of rain" *every single day* in a place that genuinely rains 70% of the time is **perfectly calibrated** and yet **useless**, because the forecast never changes and so never helps you decide whether to bring an umbrella *today*. What that forecaster lacks is **resolution** — the willingness and ability to push forecasts *away* from the base rate toward 0 or 1 when the situation actually warrants it. A great forecaster is *both* calibrated (the 90% calls happen 90%) *and* resolved (they actually make confident 90% calls and confident 10% calls, not a permanent mush of "70% maybe"). Calibration says your numbers are honest; resolution says your numbers are informative. You want both, and the Brier score, beautifully, measures both at once.

### The calibration curve

The cleanest way to *see* calibration is the calibration curve. Put your stated confidence on the horizontal axis (0% to 100%) and your realized hit rate on the vertical axis. For each level of confidence, plot a point: "of everything I called 70%, this fraction came true." A perfectly calibrated forecaster's points all sit on the 45-degree diagonal — stated equals realized everywhere. An overconfident forecaster's curve sags *below* the diagonal on the right side: at every confidence level above 50%, the realized hit rate is lower than what was stated. The size of the vertical gap between your curve and the diagonal *is* your miscalibration, made literal. Look back at the cover figure: the dashed line is perfect calibration, and the red curve that sags below it — 90% stated, 62% realized — is the trader from the opening, his overconfidence drawn as the shaded gap between what he says and what happens.

### The Brier score, intuitively

A curve is wonderful for diagnosis but you cannot track a curve as a single number over time. For that we use the **Brier score**, which is the standard one-number grade for probabilistic forecasts. The intuition first, then the formula. For a single forecast, the Brier penalty is the *squared distance* between the probability you stated and the outcome — where the outcome is 1 if the event happened and 0 if it did not.

So if you said 90% and the event happened (outcome = 1), your penalty is $(0.9 - 1)^2 = 0.01$ — tiny, you were confident and right. If you said 90% and the event did *not* happen (outcome = 0), your penalty is $(0.9 - 0)^2 = 0.81$ — enormous, you were confident and wrong. And critically, if you had only said 60% and been wrong, the penalty would have been $(0.6 - 0)^2 = 0.36$ — less than half as bad. **The squaring is the whole point: it punishes being confident-and-wrong far more harshly than being hedged-and-wrong.** That asymmetry is exactly what disciplines overconfidence — it makes inflating your numbers a losing strategy in the long run, which is precisely what you want a scoring rule to do.

![The Brier penalty as the squared distance between stated probability and the outcome](/imgs/blogs/calibration-keeping-score-on-your-own-forecasts-2.png)

The figure draws the two penalty curves. The green parabola is your penalty when the event *happens*: it is lowest when you said something near 100% and rises steeply as your stated probability drops — being unconfident about a thing that happened is penalized, but mildly. The red parabola is your penalty when the event does *not* happen: lowest when you said near 0%, and it climbs to a brutal 0.81 if you confidently said 90% and were wrong. The single marked point at (0.6, 0.36) versus (0.9, 0.81) is the heart of it: being wrong from 60% costs you 0.36, being wrong from 90% costs you 0.81 — more than double the penalty for the same wrong call, purely because you were more arrogant about it.

Formally, the Brier score is the *average* of those squared penalties over all $N$ of your forecasts:

$$\text{Brier} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}(p_i - o_i)^2$$

where $p_i$ is the probability you stated on forecast $i$ and $o_i$ is its outcome (1 or 0). The score ranges from **0 (perfect)** to **1 (worst possible)**. A useful reference point: if you have *no skill at all* and just say "50%" on every binary call, your Brier score is $0.25$ (because $(0.5 - 1)^2 = (0.5 - 0)^2 = 0.25$ every time). So **0.25 is the line between adding information and not** — a Brier above 0.25 means you would have been better off saying "coin flip" on everything, which is a genuinely humbling thing to discover about your forecasts.

The deep elegance of the Brier score is that it can be *decomposed* — algebraically split — into exactly the two pieces we care about: a **calibration** term (how far your stated probabilities are from your realized hit rates, which you want to be zero) and a **resolution** term (how much your forecasts vary from the base rate in a way that tracks reality, which you want to be large). A low Brier score requires being both calibrated *and* resolved. We will use the decomposition concretely later; the point to plant now is that one number you can compute in a spreadsheet captures the entire quality of your forecasting, and you can watch it fall as you improve.

### Why calibration is the foundation, not a footnote

Here is why this matters more than any other single skill in forming a view, and why it sits underneath the whole series. Every downstream calculation — [expected value](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/expected-value-the-only-math-a-view-really-needs), [position sizing](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/from-conviction-to-size-the-bet-sizing-bridge), the edge-versus-priced-in check — *takes your probabilities as inputs*. EV is the probability-weighted average of outcomes; you multiply payoffs by probabilities. Kelly sizing scales your bet by your edge, which is computed from your probability. If those input probabilities are inflated — if your "90%" is really 62% — then every one of those calculations is computing the right answer to the wrong question. The machinery downstream is flawless and the output is still garbage, because garbage went in.

This is the reason the spine of the whole series — *what is priced in versus what I believe* — collapses without calibration. Your "edge" is the gap between your probability and the market's. If your probability is systematically 28 points too high, then most of your perceived edge is not edge at all; it is your own overconfidence, and you are betting against a market that is, on average, better calibrated than you are. **Calibration is the thing that makes a subjective probability trustworthy enough to act on.** Without it, "I'm 90% sure" is just a feeling with a number stapled to it.

### Where overconfidence comes from

It helps to know *why* overconfidence is the near-universal default, because the causes are also the levers you pull to fix it. There are three reinforcing sources. The first is **memory's bias toward hits.** Your brain stores the calls that worked far more vividly than the ones that fizzled, and the misses get quietly reclassified — "I was wrong on timing, not direction" — so when you introspect on your track record, you sample a flattering, hit-heavy subset and conclude you are right 90% of the time. The journal exists precisely to override this: it remembers the misses your memory edits out.

The second source is **the planning-fallacy mechanism applied to forecasts.** When you reason about a specific case, you focus on the vivid, concrete details in front of you — the great management team, the clean chart, the airtight logic — and you systematically ignore the boring statistics of the reference class, the base rate that says most setups like this do not work. This "inside view" inflates confidence because the specifics always feel more compelling than the average. The cure is the base-rate anchor: deliberately step out to the "outside view" before you set the number.

The third source is **the social reward gradient.** Confidence is rewarded in meetings, on television, and on trading desks; the person who says "it's going up, full stop" sounds more competent than the one who says "60%, here's the other 40%." Over years, that gradient trains you to round your confidence up, because the up-rounded number is the one that gets respect. The cure is to score yourself against your journal rather than the room — the journal does not care how confident you sounded, only whether the right fraction came true. All three sources push the same direction, which is why overconfidence is so stubborn and why fixing it requires an external instrument (the journal and the curve) rather than more introspection. You cannot think your way out of a bias that corrupts the very thinking you would use to escape it.

## Measuring your calibration: the bucketing method

Enough foundations. How do you actually *compute* whether you are calibrated? The method is mechanical, and that is its virtue — there is no judgment, no self-flattery, just counting. The only prerequisite is that you have a body of *numbered, dated, resolved* predictions, which is precisely what [the decision journal](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/the-decision-journal-the-habit-that-compounds) exists to give you. If you have not been journaling stated probabilities, you have nothing to score, and your calibration is simply *unknown* — not good, not bad, unknown. That is the first and most important thing to internalize: you cannot have an opinion about your own calibration. You can only have data or not.

Assuming you have the data, here is the procedure, in five steps.

![Five-step pipeline from logging a call to a calibration score](/imgs/blogs/calibration-keeping-score-on-your-own-forecasts-5.png)

**Step 1 — Log the call with a stated probability.** Every forecast goes in the journal as a dated claim plus a number: "Aug 14 — SPX holds 4,200 into month-end — 75%." Not "likely," not "I feel good about it." A number, because a word cannot be bucketed or scored. This is the discipline that everything else depends on, and it is the one most people skip.

**Step 2 — Resolve it honestly, with no edits.** When the date arrives, mark the call 1 (happened) or 0 (did not), exactly as you stated it, with no retroactive reinterpretation. The single biggest source of corrupted calibration data is the temptation to decide, after the fact, that your call was "basically right" or that "the thesis was correct, the timing was just off." A resolved call is a 1 or a 0 against the claim *as written*. If you find yourself arguing with your own journal, the journal is right and you are rationalizing.

**Step 3 — Bucket by stated confidence.** Group all your resolved calls into confidence bands: the 50–59% calls, the 60–69% calls, the 70–79% calls, the 80–89% calls, and the 90–100% calls. (You can use finer or coarser buckets depending on how many calls you have; five buckets is a good default for fifty-ish calls.) Each bucket now contains a pile of 1s and 0s.

**Step 4 — Compute the realized hit rate per bucket.** For each bucket, divide the number of 1s by the total number of calls in the bucket. That fraction is your realized hit rate for that confidence level. Compare it to the bucket's midpoint (the 70–79% bucket's midpoint is ~75%). If your 70–79% calls came true 54% of the time, your realized hit rate (54%) is far below your stated confidence (75%), and you have just measured 21 points of overconfidence in that band.

**Step 5 — Plot the curve and compute the Brier.** Plot realized hit rate (vertical) against stated confidence (horizontal) for each bucket; that is your calibration curve. Then compute the single Brier number across all calls. The curve diagnoses *where* you are miscalibrated (which confidence bands sag); the Brier gives you *one number to track* as you improve. Now let us run the whole thing on real journal data.

#### Worked example: computing a calibration curve from 50 journal predictions

The trader from the opening finally bucketed his year. He had 50 resolved binary calls in his journal, each with a stated probability. Here is what the bucketing produced — the count of calls in each band and how many came true:

- **50–59% bucket (midpoint ~55%):** 8 calls, 4 came true → realized **50%**
- **60–69% bucket (midpoint ~65%):** 14 calls, 7 came true → realized **50%**
- **70–79% bucket (midpoint ~75%):** 13 calls, 7 came true → realized **54%**
- **80–89% bucket (midpoint ~85%):** 9 calls, 5 came true → realized **56%**
- **90–100% bucket (midpoint ~95%):** 6 calls, ~4 came true → realized **62%**

Read down the realized column: 50%, 50%, 54%, 56%, 62%. Now read down the stated column: 55%, 65%, 75%, 85%, 95%. The realized hit rate barely moves — it crawls from 50% to 62% — while the stated confidence marches from 55% all the way to 95%. **His confidence has resolution; his outcomes do not.** He *feels* much surer about his 95% calls than his 55% calls, but the world barely notices the difference: a call he is "95% sure" of comes true 62% of the time, while a call he is "55% sure" of comes true 50% of the time. The gap between what he says and what happens widens at exactly the confidence levels where he sizes biggest. That is the recipe for maximum damage. To put a number on it: on a \$100,000 book, his sizing rule puts roughly \$4,000 on a "55%" call and roughly \$20,000 on a "95%" call — so he is staking five times the capital on the calls where his curve is *most* wrong. The overconfidence is not evenly spread; it is concentrated precisely where the dollars are largest.

![Confidence buckets comparing what he said to the realized hit rate that actually happened](/imgs/blogs/calibration-keeping-score-on-your-own-forecasts-3.png)

The paired bars make it visceral. In every bucket the blue bar (what he said) towers over the gray/red bar (what happened), and the gap *grows* as you move right: −5 points in the 50s, −15 in the 60s, −21 in the 70s, −29 in the 80s, and a yawning −33 in the 90s. This is the textbook signature of overconfidence — not a constant offset, but a fan that opens wider the more certain he claims to be. The lesson is uncomfortable and precise: his confidence is nearly *uncorrelated* with his accuracy above 50%, which means the confidence number, as he generates it, is carrying almost no information. He would be better calibrated saying "60%" on everything than saying what he actually feels.

#### Worked example: the same calls, scored with a Brier number

The curve shows the *shape* of his problem; the Brier score gives it a single grade. To compute it we need each individual call's stated probability and outcome, but we can approximate using the bucket midpoints and hit rates. Take the 90–100% bucket: 6 calls stated at ~0.95, of which 4 came true (outcome 1) and 2 did not (outcome 0). The squared penalties are $(0.95-1)^2 = 0.0025$ for each of the 4 winners and $(0.95-0)^2 = 0.9025$ for each of the 2 losers. That bucket alone contributes $4(0.0025) + 2(0.9025) = 0.01 + 1.805 = 1.815$ to the running sum — and two confident-and-wrong calls are doing virtually all of the damage.

Carrying the same arithmetic across all five buckets and dividing by the 50 total calls, his Brier score works out to approximately **0.30.** Sit with that number. Recall that saying a flat "50%" on every call scores **0.25.** His Brier of 0.30 is *worse than no skill at all* — his confident, hard-won, eleven-years-of-experience forecasts would have scored better if he had simply written "coin flip" on every single one and gone home. And this is not an abstract grade: a Brier above 0.25 means his probability inputs are net-destructive, and on the \$100,000 book those destructive inputs were sizing every position — so the bad score and the bleeding account are the same fact measured two ways, one in points and one in dollars. The overconfidence is so severe that the resolution he does have (he does push toward 90%) is more than wiped out by the calibration penalty (those 90% calls land 62%). The squaring in the Brier formula is unforgiving precisely here: the handful of "95% sure" calls that went to zero detonate his score. **This is the moment calibration becomes real for most people** — discovering their confident forecasts grade out below a coin flip is the jolt that finally makes them take it seriously.

## Reading the curve: the four common ways to be miscalibrated

The shape of your calibration curve diagnoses *which* bias you have, and there are four common patterns. Each has a distinct tell in the journal and a specific fix.

![Grid of four common miscalibration patterns with tells and fixes](/imgs/blogs/calibration-keeping-score-on-your-own-forecasts-6.png)

**Overconfidence — the default.** Your curve sags below the diagonal everywhere above 50%: stated consistently exceeds realized, and the gap widens at the high end. This is by far the most common pattern, the one the opening trader has, and the one you should assume you have until your data proves otherwise. The tell is exactly what we saw: 90% calls landing 62%. The fix is to pull every stated probability toward 50% — to *widen your ranges* — and to anchor harder on base rates, both of which we cover below.

**The 100% / 0% trap.** This is overconfidence in its most lethal form: stating certainty you cannot possibly possess. Any time you write "100%" or "0%" on a forecast, you are claiming the event is *impossible to be wrong about* — and the Brier penalty for a 100%-call that fails is the maximum possible, $1.0$, the single most punishing outcome the score can hand out. There is no such thing as a 100% call about the future in markets. The fix is a hard rule: **cap your stated probabilities at 95% and floor them at 5%.** Reserve the certainty you do not have, and you protect yourself from the one mistake that does the most damage per occurrence.

**Round-number bias.** Your calls cluster suspiciously on 50%, 75%, and 90% — the "default" numbers the brain reaches for — with nothing in between. This is a subtler problem: it means you are not actually *distinguishing* a 72% call from a 78% call, you are just slapping "75%" on everything in the neighborhood. The tell is a histogram of your stated probabilities with tall spikes on round numbers and empty gaps between them. The fix is to *force a real number* — when you catch yourself writing "75%," ask "is it really 75, or is it 71, or 79?" and commit to the honest figure. The discipline of a non-round number forces a second of genuine thought.

**Underconfidence — the rarer mirror image.** Your curve floats *above* the diagonal: your 60% calls land 80%, your 70% calls land 90%. You are systematically *understating* your edge, which means you are sizing too small and leaving money on the table. This is rare — most people err the other way — but it is real, and it tends to afflict careful, self-critical analysts who have over-corrected for fear of looking arrogant. The fix is the opposite of the overconfidence fix: trust your edge, and when the data says your 60% calls are really 80% calls, size up a notch to match. Underconfidence is a more pleasant problem to have, but it is still leaving money on the table.

The reason this taxonomy matters is that *the fix depends on the diagnosis.* If you are overconfident and you read a generic "be more careful" prescription, you widen your ranges and improve. But if you are one of the rare underconfident analysts and you apply the same prescription, you make yourself *worse* — more timid, smaller, more edge left on the table. You cannot prescribe a fix until you have plotted your own curve. Measure first, always.

## Why miscalibration is expensive: the EV and sizing damage

Up to now this could read as an academic exercise in self-knowledge. It is not. Miscalibration costs real money, and the mechanism is precise: inflated probabilities feed directly into the expected-value and sizing calculations, and they can flip a winning book into a losing one without the trader ever noticing the input was wrong. Let us make the damage concrete with dollars.

![Before and after comparison of the overconfidence trap poisoning expected value and sizing](/imgs/blogs/calibration-keeping-score-on-your-own-forecasts-4.png)

The before-after figure traces the two paths. On the overconfident side: he says 90%, the journal says it lands 62%, but he computes EV at 90% — so the EV looks fat and positive, he sizes big to that fat edge, and because the true probability is 62% the real EV is *negative* and the book bleeds. On the calibrated side: he states the honest 62%, computes EV at 62% — modest but real and positive — sizes to the true edge, and the book compounds. Same calls, same world; the only difference is whether the probability that entered the math was honest. Now the numbers.

#### Worked example: overconfidence turning a +EV book negative on a \$100,000 account

You run a \$100,000 account. You have a strategy of binary event trades — each one risks \$5,000 to make \$5,000 if you are right (a symmetric, roughly even-money payoff after costs). You believe you are skilled: you put these trades on only when you are "90% sure," and at 90% the expected value of each trade is gorgeous:

$$\text{EV}_{\text{believed}} = 0.90(+\$5{,}000) + 0.10(-\$5{,}000) = \$4{,}500 - \$500 = +\$4{,}000$$

A \$4,000 expected profit on \$5,000 risked — an 80% edge per trade. With numbers like that you size aggressively, you put on a lot of them, and you expect to compound the \$100,000 account into the stratosphere. Over the year you place 40 of these trades. If your 90% were real, you would expect to win 36 and lose 4, for a profit of $36(\$5{,}000) - 4(\$5{,}000) = \$180{,}000 - \$20{,}000 = +\$160{,}000$. You are mentally spending it.

But your 90% calls land 62%. So the *true* expected value of each trade is:

$$\text{EV}_{\text{true}} = 0.62(+\$5{,}000) + 0.38(-\$5{,}000) = \$3{,}100 - \$1{,}900 = +\$1{,}200$$

Still positive — but a *third* of what you believed, and that is before the bias really bites. Because the payoff here is symmetric (\$5,000 to make \$5,000), the breakeven win rate is exactly 50%, and 62% clears it, so you scrape out a real edge. Now change one realistic detail: costs. Say spread, slippage, and fees clip \$600 off each trade. Your believed EV barely notices (\$4,000 − \$600 = \$3,400, still gorgeous). But your *true* EV becomes $\$1{,}200 - \$600 = +\$600$ per trade — and if the realized hit rate in the heat of the year is a hair worse than 62%, or the payoff a touch less than even after a bad fill or two, you cross into negative territory. At a true 55% hit rate with the \$600 cost, $\text{EV} = 0.55(5{,}000) + 0.45(-5{,}000) - 600 = \$2{,}750 - \$2{,}250 - \$600 = -\$100$ per trade. Across 40 trades that is a \$4,000 loss on a book you were *certain* was minting \$160,000. **The overconfidence did not just shrink the edge; it sat the trade right on the knife-edge of profitability while you believed you had an 80% edge — so you sized as if you could not lose, and you bled.**

The killer is that none of this shows up as a single dramatic error. Each individual trade looks fine; you win more than you lose. The damage is structural and invisible from inside any one position: you are systematically *oversizing* every trade relative to its true edge, so when the inevitable string of losses arrives, it is bigger than your true edge can support, and the account grinds down instead of up. The overconfidence is not in any one trade. It is in the input you feed all of them.

#### Worked example: widening a range to get calibrated, and the sizing payoff

Now the fix, in dollars. The same trader plots his curve, sees the 90%-lands-62% gap, and adopts a calibration correction: **for every call, he states the probability his journal says he actually hits, not the one he feels.** His feeling-of-90% becomes a stated 62%. His feeling-of-75% (which his journal says lands ~54%) becomes a stated 54%. He has widened his ranges — pulled every number toward 50% by the amount his data demands.

What does this do to sizing? Take his sizing rule as a simple edge-proportional one (a fractional-Kelly-style rule): risk a fraction of the account proportional to the edge, where edge for an even-money bet is $2p - 1$. Before the correction, on a "90%" call, his perceived edge was $2(0.90) - 1 = 0.80$, and his rule put on, say, a quarter-Kelly fraction of $0.25 \times 0.80 = 20\%$ of the \$100,000 account — a \$20,000 position. That is a *catastrophic* over-bet for a trade whose true edge is much smaller.

After the correction, the same call is stated at 62%, so the edge is $2(0.62) - 1 = 0.24$, and the quarter-Kelly fraction is $0.25 \times 0.24 = 6\%$ — a \$6,000 position. **He has cut his position to less than a third of its former size, not because the trade got worse, but because he is finally sizing to its true edge instead of an inflated one.** And here is the payoff: that \$6,000 position is one he can survive a losing streak on. A run of four losses costs him \$24,000 from \$6,000 positions — painful but survivable on \$100,000. The old \$20,000 positions would have cost him \$80,000 over the same four losses — an account-ending hole. Calibration did not make him more cautious for caution's sake; it made his size *correct*, which is the difference between a book that compounds and one that detonates. The full machinery of turning a calibrated probability into a survival-bounded size is the subject of [from conviction to size](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/from-conviction-to-size-the-bet-sizing-bridge); the point here is that the *size is only as honest as the probability that fed it.*

## Improving your calibration: it is a trainable skill

The best news in this entire field is that calibration *improves with deliberate practice* — it is a trainable skill, not a fixed trait. The weather-forecasting community is the proof of concept: professional meteorologists are, as a group, among the best-calibrated forecasters on earth, and they got that way not through superior intelligence but through a *tight feedback loop* — they make explicit probabilistic forecasts every single day and find out within hours whether it rained. That loop, repeated thousands of times, trains the brain to map its internal sense of confidence onto honest numbers. You can build the same loop. Here are the five habits that close the gap.

**Track everything, with numbers.** This is the non-negotiable foundation and the one most people skip. Every forecast goes in the journal with a stated probability and a resolution date. No number means no score, and no score means no improvement — you simply cannot train a feedback loop with no feedback. This is why calibration and [the decision journal](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/the-decision-journal-the-habit-that-compounds) are inseparable: the journal is the instrument that makes calibration measurable in the first place.

**Get feedback fast and re-plot often.** The shorter the loop between forecast and resolution, the faster you learn. Resolve calls on schedule, and re-plot your calibration curve at least monthly. Do not wait a year to discover you are overconfident; discover it in six weeks and start correcting. Short-horizon calls (does this level hold into Friday's close?) are calibration gold precisely because they resolve fast and give you many data points quickly — front-load your tracking with calls that resolve soon.

**Widen your ranges.** This is the direct fix for the universal default of overconfidence. If your curve shows 90%-lands-62%, then *state 62% where you feel 90%.* Mechanically pull every confidence number toward 50% by the amount your data demands. It will feel like you are being wishy-washy and unconfident — and that feeling is exactly the overconfidence bias resisting its own correction. The honest number always feels too low to the overconfident brain. Write it anyway.

**Anchor to base rates.** Before you set a probability from the specifics of the case, ask: how often does this *kind* of thing happen in general? If you are about to call a richly-valued momentum stock 80% likely to keep ripping, and the base rate for "richly-valued momentum names keep ripping over the next quarter" is more like 45%, your 80% needs to be dragged hard toward that 45% anchor and only nudged up by whatever genuinely distinguishes this case. Overconfidence is, mechanically, the failure to anchor on base rates — the brain looks at the vivid specifics in front of it and ignores the boring statistics of the reference class. Anchoring is the single most powerful debiasing move, because the base rate already encodes a thousand cases that looked just as compelling and mostly did not pan out.

**Run a pre-mortem before you log the number.** Before you commit a probability to the journal, force yourself to write down *three concrete ways the call is wrong.* "The print could surprise the other way." "Positioning could be more extreme than I think and trigger a squeeze." "I might be the last to know what's already priced." The act of naming the failure paths drags an inflated 90% down toward an honest 70% *before the number ever hits the journal* — it makes the other side of the bet vivid, which is the only thing that reliably deflates overconfidence in the moment. The [pre-mortem](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/stress-testing-your-thesis-with-a-pre-mortem) is usually framed as a thesis-stress-testing tool, but it is also the single best in-the-moment calibration corrector, because it attacks overconfidence at the instant the number is born.

#### Worked example: the Brier score before and after the fix

The trader runs the loop for two quarters and re-scores. The instructive thing is *what* the fix does to the Brier number, because it reveals that calibration and resolution move differently. Recall his "before" Brier was about **0.30** — worse than the no-skill 0.25 baseline, dragged down by the confident-and-wrong calls.

After the fix, he changes nothing about *which* calls he makes — same edges, same trades — he only widens his stated probabilities to match his realized rates and caps them at 95%/5%. His "90% sure" calls now go in the journal at 62%. Score the same 6 calls from the old 90–100% bucket, now restated at 0.62: the 4 winners each penalize $(0.62-1)^2 = 0.144$ and the 2 losers each penalize $(0.62-0)^2 = 0.384$, contributing $4(0.144) + 2(0.384) = 0.576 + 0.768 = 1.344$ — versus the old 1.815 from those same six calls. **Just by relabeling those six calls honestly, he shaved 0.47 off the running sum.** Across all 50 calls, his Brier falls from ~0.30 to roughly **0.235** — finally *below* the no-skill 0.25 line. He is now genuinely adding information.

The deep lesson hides in the arithmetic: he got better *without making a single better call.* The forecasts were always carrying real, if modest, information; the overconfident labels were throwing that information away under the squared penalty. Calibration recovered edge that was already there but was being destroyed by dishonest numbers. That is the cheapest improvement available in all of forecasting — you do not have to get smarter, you only have to stop lying about how smart you are. And note the limit: widening ranges fixes *calibration* but does nothing for *resolution* — to push the Brier still lower he would have to make genuinely more-informative calls (pull confidently toward 0 or 1 when warranted *and* be right), which is the harder, slower work of actually improving the underlying forecasting. Calibration is the free lunch you eat first; resolution is the meal you cook afterward.

![Calibration improvement checklist with measure-first and five habits](/imgs/blogs/calibration-keeping-score-on-your-own-forecasts-7.png)

The checklist card collects the loop. **Measure first** — bucket your last 50 calls, plot the curve, compute the Brier, because you cannot fix a gap you have not measured. Then run the five habits: track every call with a number, get feedback fast, widen your ranges, anchor to base rates, and run a pre-mortem before logging the probability. The gap between your stated curve and the 45-degree line is your edge leak, and these five habits are how you close it. Run the loop for a quarter and re-plot; the curve will visibly straighten toward the diagonal, and your Brier score will fall. That falling number is the most honest progress report in all of trading.

### Calibration is not only about binary calls

So far every example has been a binary forecast — it happened or it did not. But calibration applies just as sharply to *ranges*, and most market views are really range views ("oil ends the quarter between \$70 and \$80," "earnings come in around \$2.40 a share"). The range version asks: when you state a 90% confidence *interval* — a low-to-high band you are 90% sure the outcome falls inside — does the actual outcome land inside that band 90% of the time? The overconfident analyst's tell here is identical and equally measurable: their "90% intervals" capture the real outcome only 60% of the time, because their bands are *too narrow.* They draw a tight range around their best guess and the world keeps surprising them outside it.

The fix is the same widening move, applied to the band rather than the point: make your intervals wider until they actually capture the outcome the stated fraction of the time. A clean self-test, used in calibration training, is to set ten 90% intervals for ten quantities — and if more than one of them misses, your intervals are too tight and you are overconfident about ranges, not just about binaries. The discipline transfers directly to markets: the analyst who quotes a realistically wide range for next quarter's number, and whose ranges actually contain the outcome as often as advertised, is calibrated; the one who quotes a confident tight band that the outcome keeps escaping is overconfident, and will be repeatedly "shocked" by prints that were always inside the true distribution. Interval calibration also connects directly to tail awareness — narrow bands amputate exactly the [fat tails](/blog/trading/risk-management/fat-tails-and-the-normal-distribution-trap) that do the real damage, which is why an overconfident analyst is doubly exposed: their point estimates are too high *and* their ranges are too tight.

### Stating probabilities so you *can* be scored

One subtle but essential habit underlies all of this: you must state your forecasts in a form that *can* be scored, which means a falsifiable claim, a number, and a date. "I'm bullish tech" cannot be scored — bullish how much, by when, and what counts as right? "Tech (QQQ) is higher than today's close in 30 days — 65%" can be scored exactly: in 30 days it is a 1 or a 0, and the 65% goes in the bucket. The discipline of *scoreable* forecasting forces the kind of precision that [writing market commentary that actually says something](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/writing-market-commentary-that-actually-says-something) is built on. A view you cannot score is a view you cannot improve, because you can never find out if you were right. Make every consequential forecast scoreable, and your calibration becomes a measurable, improvable quantity instead of a comforting story you tell yourself.

## Common misconceptions

Several beliefs about confidence and forecasting are not just wrong but actively dangerous, because they feel like wisdom while corrupting the inputs to every decision.

**"I'm usually right, so my calibration is fine."** Untracked, your calibration is not fine *or* not fine — it is *unknown*, and unknown almost always means overconfident, because the brain's default is overconfidence and memory is a flatterer. You remember your hits vividly and your misses get quietly reclassified as "basically right" or "wrong on timing." The opening trader was *certain* he was right 90% of the time, right up until he counted and found 62%. The feeling of being usually right is not evidence; it is the bias itself talking. The only thing that settles it is the bucketed data. If you have not bucketed your calls, you do not have an opinion about your calibration worth having.

**"Confidence equals competence."** This is the most seductive error, because in social settings it is often *rewarded* — the confident person sounds smarter in the meeting. But confidence and competence are *separately measurable* and frequently *diverge*: the documented pattern is that the genuinely most skilled forecasters are often *less* confident than the mediocre ones, because they have a more accurate sense of how much they do not know. Loud certainty is, if anything, a mild negative signal about calibration. The competent forecaster's edge shows up in a low Brier score, not a high decibel level. Never confuse the feeling of certainty with the quality of a forecast; the Brier score does not care how sure you sounded.

**"Calibration is academic — it doesn't matter for actually making money."** This one is refuted by the dollar arithmetic above. Calibration is the *input* to expected value and sizing, the two calculations that most directly determine whether you make or lose money. A 28-point overconfidence bias does not stay in a journal; it oversizes every position and turns a +EV book negative, as the \$100,000-account example showed. Calibration is the least academic thing in the world — it is the difference between a size that compounds your account and a size that ends it. The math only works if the probabilities are honest, and calibration is the only way to know if they are.

**"Being 90% sure is good."** Being 90% sure is good *only if* things you are 90% sure of happen about 90% of the time. If your 90% calls land 62%, then "being 90% sure" is not a strength — it is a 28-point lie you are telling your sizing model, and the more 90%-sure you feel, the more damage you do. High stated confidence is a liability, not an asset, until your calibration curve earns it. The goal is never to feel more confident; it is to make your confidence *true*, even when true means lower.

**"One wrong 90% call means I was overconfident on it."** No — a single resolved call tells you nothing about calibration. A 90% call that fails is exactly what you should expect to see roughly one time in ten; if your 90% calls *never* failed, you would be *underconfident* and should have said 99%. Calibration lives in the aggregate, never the instance. Do not flagellate yourself over one confident call that went wrong, and do not congratulate yourself over one that went right. Bucket fifty of them and *then* judge — the single call is noise, the bucket is signal.

## How it plays out in real markets

Calibration is not a back-office spreadsheet exercise; it shows up in the texture of how good and bad forecasters behave around real events. Here are the patterns, grounded in episodes most market participants lived through.

**The 2022 inflation calls.** Through the first half of 2022, a great many strategists made confident, high-conviction calls that inflation was "transitory" and would roll over by mid-year, and an equally confident cohort called for an imminent recession that kept not arriving. Both camps stated their views with the language of near-certainty. The well-calibrated participants did something different: they held inflation as a *distribution* with honest, moderate probabilities — "60% it stays sticky through Q3, 40% it rolls" — and they sized to that distribution rather than to a confident point. When the June CPI printed a shocking 9.1%, the confident "transitory" camp was not just wrong, they were *confidently and largely* wrong, which under a Brier score is the maximum-penalty outcome — and crucially, having sized to their certainty, they had no room to adapt. The calibrated participants took a smaller hit because they had never claimed the certainty that would have justified the oversize. The episode is a clean illustration that the cost of overconfidence is paid precisely when the confident call fails, and the failures are correlated with exactly the calls people sized biggest.

**The serial recession-callers.** A recurring market figure is the strategist who has "predicted" a recession or a crash with high confidence for years running, finally gets one right, and is celebrated. Run that record through a calibration lens and it collapses: a forecaster who says "90% recession this year" every year for five years and is right once has a *catastrophic* calibration — five 90% calls, one hit, a realized rate of 20% against a stated 90%, a Brier score deep in the penalty box. Accuracy ("he called the crash!") flatters him; calibration exposes him. The lesson for your own forecasting is to score the *whole record*, including all the confident calls that quietly did not happen, not the cherry-picked hit. The market remembers the one right call; your journal must remember all the wrong ones, because that is where your calibration actually lives.

**The earnings-season overconfidence cycle.** Watch any analyst around a busy earnings season and you will see the overconfidence fan we plotted earlier, in real time. Going in, the confident calls cluster at "definitely beats" and "definitely gaps up" — high stated confidence. Coming out, the realized hit rate on those confident single-name calls is humbling, because earnings reactions are dominated by [what was already priced in](/blog/trading/event-trading/consensus-expectations-and-priced-in) and by positioning, both of which are exactly the things an overconfident analyst underweights in favor of their own vivid thesis. The well-calibrated analyst goes into earnings season with *moderate* probabilities, anchored on the base rate of how often confident single-name earnings calls actually work (lower than anyone wants to admit), and comes out with a Brier score that did not blow up. The discipline is the same one the whole series teaches: anchor on what is priced, hold the distribution, and never let the vividness of your own thesis inflate the number you stake money on.

The thread through all three is identical to the thread through this whole series: a view is only as good as the honesty of the probability attached to it, and calibration is the only way to measure that honesty. The confident pundit, the serial crash-caller, and the earnings-season optimist all share one flaw — they never counted. The professional counts.

## The playbook

Here is the repeatable process. Run it as a standing quarterly review of your own forecasting, and run the in-the-moment steps every time you log a consequential call.

**1. Pull your last N resolved calls.** Go to the [decision journal](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/the-decision-journal-the-habit-that-compounds) and extract every forecast that has both a stated probability and a known outcome. Fifty is a good minimum for stable buckets; more is better. If you have fewer than ~30, your buckets will be noisy — keep tracking and re-run when you have enough. If you have *none*, your calibration is unknown and step one is simply to start journaling numbers today.

**2. Resolve each call honestly as a 1 or a 0.** Score it against the claim *as written*, with zero retroactive reinterpretation. "Basically right" is not a category. If you are tempted to argue with your own journal, the journal wins.

**3. Bucket by stated confidence.** Group into 50–59, 60–69, 70–79, 80–89, 90–100 (use coarser buckets if you have fewer calls). Count the calls and the hits in each.

**4. Compute realized hit rate per bucket and plot the curve.** Hit rate = hits ÷ calls in the bucket. Plot realized (vertical) against the bucket midpoint (horizontal). Lay the 45-degree line over it. Where your curve sags below the line, you are overconfident; where it floats above, underconfident. The vertical gap is the size of the problem at each confidence level.

**5. Compute one Brier score across all calls.** Average $(p_i - o_i)^2$ over every call. Write the number down and date it. Remember the reference points: 0.25 is "no skill" (you'd do as well saying 50% on everything), below 0.25 is adding information, and the lower the better. This is the number you will track over time.

**6. Diagnose the pattern and apply the matching fix.** Overconfident → widen ranges and anchor harder on base rates. The 100%/0% trap → cap stated odds at 95%/5%. Round-number bias → force non-round numbers. Underconfident → trust your edge and size up a notch. The fix depends on the diagnosis; do not prescribe before you plot.

**7. Install the in-the-moment habits.** From now on, every consequential call gets: a stated number (never a word), a resolution date, a pre-mortem of three ways it is wrong *before* the number is logged, and a base-rate anchor. These four habits attack overconfidence at the instant the forecast is born.

**8. Re-plot quarterly and watch the Brier fall.** Re-run the whole loop every quarter. The curve should straighten toward the diagonal and the Brier should decline. A falling Brier is the most honest progress report you will ever get as a forecaster — it cannot be faked, flattered, or rationalized.

The deepest lesson is the one the opening trader learned the hard way. He spent eleven years certain he was right 90% of the time, sizing every position to that certainty, and the certainty was a 28-point lie that quietly clipped his returns on every trade he ever put on. The fix cost him nothing but an afternoon of counting and the discomfort of writing smaller numbers than he felt. Calibration does not make you feel more confident — it makes your confidence *true*, which is the only thing that survives a long run of trades. State the number, log the date, run the pre-mortem, bucket the calls, plot the curve, compute the Brier, and let the measurement — not the mood — decide what your probabilities are worth. Your 90% should happen 90% of the time. If it does not, you do not have a 90% call; you have a feeling, and feelings do not size positions. The forward step from here is to stop judging yourself by whether each call came true and start judging the *process* that produced it, which is the subject of [process versus outcome: judging decisions, not results](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/process-versus-outcome-judging-decisions-not-results).
