> [!important]
> **TL;DR** — Any market can be read through six lenses: macro, fundamental, technical, flows, positioning, sentiment. No single lens is sufficient, and the edge is not in owning the best lens — it is in the **synthesis**.
>
> - Each lens answers a *different question* off a *different data feed* and has a *different blind spot*. Macro reads the tide; fundamental reads the worth; technical reads the path; flows read who is buying; positioning reads who is already committed; sentiment reads the mood.
> - The lenses are **semi-independent evidence**. When they agree, agreement compounds into conviction. When they disagree, you have either caution or an opportunity — never a contradiction to ignore.
> - **Weight the lenses by regime and horizon**, not by personality. In a macro shock the tide drowns the worth; over a three-year horizon the worth drowns the path. "I'm a fundamental guy" is a confession, not a strategy.
> - **The one rule:** read all six, score each as a stance plus a weight, then synthesize into one sized call. The number of lenses pointing the same way is your conviction dial — and your size dial.

In the spring of a recent year, three analysts sat in the same morning meeting and pulled up the same stock. The first, a fundamental analyst, had built a discounted-cash-flow model the night before and was emphatic: the company traded at eleven times forward earnings against a sector at nineteen, generated more free cash than it knew what to do with, and had just guided revenue up. *It's cheap. Buy it.* The second analyst, a technician, had the daily chart on her screen and was equally emphatic the other way: the stock had broken its two-hundred-day moving average six weeks ago, was making a clean series of lower highs and lower lows, and every bounce was being sold. *It's in a downtrend. Don't touch it.* The third analyst said almost nothing for most of the meeting, then pulled up one chart: the flow data showed that the three largest index funds holding the name had been net sellers for eleven straight weeks, mechanically, because the stock was being shrunk in a major index after a reclassification. *Someone has to sell about four million shares a week regardless of price for two more months. That's your downtrend, and it has nothing to do with the company.*

Here is the uncomfortable part: **all three analysts were right.** The fundamental analyst was right that the business was cheap. The technician was right that the price action was ugly. The flows analyst was right about the mechanical selling. None of them was wrong about what their lens showed. But only the third analyst could make a call worth acting on, because she was the only one who could see that the cheapness was *real*, the downtrend was *temporary and mechanical*, and therefore the right trade was to wait for the forced selling to exhaust itself and then buy the fundamentally cheap stock into a base — exactly the move the other two would have gotten wrong by trusting one lens to the exclusion of the others.

This post is about being that third analyst on every name you look at. It is the core framework of the synthesis track: a repeatable way to read *any* market — a single stock, gold, the dollar, a credit spread — through six lenses, and to turn six partial readings into one accountable, sized call. The lenses are not a menu you pick from according to taste. They are six instruments you read in order, every time, and the skill that pays is the synthesis.

## Foundations: the six lenses, the question each answers, and its blind spot

Let us define the words precisely before we use them, because the entire framework hangs on keeping the six lenses distinct. A lens is not a strategy and not an indicator. **A lens is a way of looking at the market that answers one specific question off one specific kind of data, and is structurally blind to a different question.** The reason you need six of them is the same reason a doctor takes your temperature *and* your blood pressure *and* listens to your chest: each instrument reads one thing well and reads the others not at all, and a diagnosis is the synthesis.

Here are the six, each with the question it answers, the data that feeds it, and — most important — the thing it cannot see.

**Macro — the tide.** The question: *which way is the broad environment pushing every asset?* The data: interest rates and the rate path, growth and the data-surprise stream, inflation, liquidity conditions, the dollar, credit spreads. Macro is the tide that lifts or lowers every boat in the harbor regardless of how well-built any individual boat is. Its blind spot: it tells you nothing about the idiosyncrasy of a single name. A great company and a terrible one both fall when the tide goes out.

**Fundamental — the worth.** The question: *what is this thing actually worth, independent of today's price?* The data: earnings, free cash flow, margins, the balance sheet, the growth rate, the multiple versus peers and versus history. The fundamental lens estimates intrinsic value and compares it to the price. Its blind spot: *timing and the path.* A stock can be fifty percent undervalued and get more undervalued for two years before it converges. The fundamental lens knows the destination and is nearly silent on the route or the schedule.

**Technical — the path.** The question: *what is the price itself doing — trend, levels, momentum?* The data: the chart. Trend direction, support and resistance, moving averages, momentum, volume, breadth. The technical lens reads the market's own footprints. Its blind spot: *the why.* A chart can show you that a stock is breaking down without telling you whether the cause is a forced index seller (temporary) or a deteriorating business (permanent) — and those demand opposite trades.

**Flows — who is buying.** The question: *who is actually moving money into or out of this, right now, and why?* The data: ETF creation and redemption, fund-flow reports, dealer hedging flow, buybacks, index rebalancing, systematic strategy flows. Flows are the marginal buyer and seller — the supply and demand underneath the price. Its blind spot: flows are often *reported with a lag* and can reverse abruptly; a flow that was relentless can stop the day a rebalance completes.

**Positioning — who is already committed.** The question: *who is already in this trade, how heavily, and how fragile is that?* The data: the CFTC Commitments of Traders report, short interest and days-to-cover, fund-manager surveys, dealer gamma, the put/call ratio. Positioning is *capital already at risk on a belief* — it tells you where the crowd is leaning and therefore which surprise would force a stampede. Its blind spot: positioning gives you no *timing trigger*. A market can stay crowded for months. Crowding is a condition, not a catalyst. We cover this lens in depth in the companion post on [reading flows and positioning](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/reading-flows-and-positioning-the-tell-most-analysts-miss).

**Sentiment — the mood.** The question: *what is the emotional temperature — fear or greed?* The data: fear-and-greed indices, the AAII bull/bear survey, financial-media tone, the VIX as a fear gauge, option skew, the breathlessness of the headlines. Sentiment reads the crowd's *feelings*, which at extremes become contrarian signals. Its blind spot: sentiment is *noisy and has no natural level*. "Greedy" can get greedier for a long time, and there is no price you can point to and say "this is where the mood breaks."

![Matrix of six lenses showing the question each answers, its main data feed, and its blind spot](/imgs/blogs/the-six-lenses-a-framework-for-reading-any-market-2.png)

The matrix above is the whole foundation on one screen, and it is worth memorizing the *structure* of it rather than the entries: every lens has a question column and a blind-spot column, and **no two lenses share a blind spot.** That is not a coincidence; it is the entire reason the framework works. If two lenses were blind to the same thing, the second one would be redundant. The lenses are valuable precisely because each one sees what the others miss. Macro covers fundamental's blind spot to the environment; flows covers technical's blind spot to *why* the price is moving; positioning covers everyone's blind spot to fragility. Read together, the six lenses have no shared blind spot — which is the formal version of the claim that synthesis is where the edge lives.

### Top-down versus bottom-up: starting points, not religions

You will hear analysts identify as "top-down" or "bottom-up" as though these were rival faiths. They are not. They are simply *which lens you start from* and which direction you traverse the stack.

A **top-down** analyst starts at the tide. She reads the macro regime first — what are rates doing, where is liquidity, what is the dollar doing — then works down through flows and positioning to the broad technical picture, and only at the end asks whether a specific name fits the environment she has mapped. Top-down is the natural order when the macro lens dominates: in a Fed-driven market, the answer to "should I own this stock?" is mostly the answer to "should I own *any* stock?"

A **bottom-up** analyst starts at the worth. He builds the fundamental case for a single name first — is it cheap, is it growing, is the balance sheet sound — then reads the technical path, checks who is positioned in it, and only at the end overlays the macro question of whether the broad tide will let the thesis play out. Bottom-up is the natural order when the fundamental lens dominates: in a stock-picker's market, the dispersion between companies is larger than the dispersion driven by the macro tide.

![Top-down versus bottom-up traversal of the same six-lens stack from opposite starting ends](/imgs/blogs/the-six-lenses-a-framework-for-reading-any-market-4.png)

The crucial point the figure above makes: **both paths visit all six lenses and both arrive at one synthesized call.** They differ only in starting point and the order of traversal. A top-down analyst who refuses to ever look at a single company's fundamentals is not "top-down" — she is one-eyed. A bottom-up analyst who builds a beautiful DCF and never asks what the macro tide is doing is not "bottom-up" — he is about to get run over by a tide he never checked. The religions are a trap. The starting point is a tool: start from the lens most likely to dominate the call you are about to make, then make sure you still complete the circuit.

### The synthesis principle, stated precisely

Here is the principle that makes the whole framework more than a checklist. **The six lenses are semi-independent sources of evidence about the same question, and independent evidence compounds.** When two lenses that read different data through different blind spots both point the same way, the probability that they are *both* being fooled by the same fluke is much lower than the probability that either one alone is being fooled. Agreement is not just additive comfort — it is multiplicative evidence.

Make this concrete with the language of odds. Suppose, on a given name, each lens is right about sixty percent of the time on its own — a modest edge. If the lenses were perfectly correlated (all reading the same underlying signal), having all six agree would tell you nothing more than having one agree: they would always agree, and your edge would stay at sixty percent. But the lenses are *not* perfectly correlated; they read genuinely different data with genuinely different blind spots. So when all six of these semi-independent witnesses point the same way, the compound probability that the call is right climbs well above any single lens — that is the mechanism behind the conviction curve we will build in a moment. Conversely, when the witnesses disagree, the disagreement is information: it is telling you exactly which blind spot to investigate.

This is why "the lenses contradict each other" is a category error we will demolish in the misconceptions section. Lenses do not contradict; they *report*. The fundamental lens reporting "cheap" and the technical lens reporting "downtrend" are not in conflict any more than a thermometer reading "fever" and a stethoscope reading "clear lungs" are in conflict. They are two true readings that *together* point at a specific diagnosis — in the opening story, "cheap but under temporary mechanical selling pressure." The synthesis is the diagnosis.

There is one more property of the synthesis principle that separates it from naive "use more data" advice, and it is worth stating sharply: **the lenses must be semi-independent for agreement to mean anything.** If you add a seventh "lens" that is really just the technical lens dressed up — a second momentum indicator, say — its agreement with the technical lens tells you nothing, because the two are reading the same data. Adding correlated lenses is like asking the same witness the same question twice and counting it as two confirmations. The framework picks exactly six lenses because they read the *most* different things about the same market: macro reads the environment, fundamental reads the company's books, technical reads the price tape, flows read the order flow, positioning reads the futures and short data, sentiment reads surveys and tone. Those are six genuinely different data sources. Their *semi*-independence — not full independence, because they all ultimately describe one market — is precisely calibrated so that agreement is informative and disagreement is diagnostic. When you are tempted to add a lens, ask: does it read data the other six don't? If not, it is not a seventh lens; it is a louder version of one you already have.

## The six lenses in depth

Now we go through each lens: what it sees, where to get the data (almost all of it free), and the regime in which it dominates. As you read, hold the synthesis principle in mind — each lens is one witness, and we are building toward reading all six together.

### Macro — the tide

The macro lens answers *which way is the broad environment pushing everything?* In practice you are reading four sub-currents: the **rate path** (where the market thinks policy rates are going, read off fed funds futures and the OIS curve), **growth and surprises** (is incoming data beating or missing expectations — the surprise stream matters far more than the level, a point the [macro-correlations work](/blog/trading/macro-correlations/the-surprise-not-the-level-betas-to-data-surprises) makes precisely), **liquidity** (is the system flush or draining), and the **dollar and credit** (a rising dollar and widening credit spreads are the tide going out). Free data: the Treasury curve and economic releases from public sources, the CME's published rate-path probabilities, the dollar index, high-yield spread series.

The macro lens **dominates in a shock.** When the Fed pivots, when an inflation print lands far from consensus, when a credit event hits — on those days correlations across every asset rush toward one, dispersion between good and bad companies collapses, and the only lens that matters is the tide. In 2022 this was the entire year, and we will walk that episode in detail. The macro lens recedes in a calm, range-bound, stock-picker's market where the tide is flat and the dispersion is idiosyncratic.

The blind spot to keep front of mind: macro tells you nothing about *which* boat to own. It tells you whether to be in the water at all.

One refinement that separates a good macro read from a naive one: the macro lens reads the *surprise*, not the *level*. A rate at 5% is not bullish or bearish in itself — what moves markets is whether 5% is higher or lower than what was already priced in. If the market expected 5.25% and gets 5%, that is a dovish surprise even though rates are high; if it expected 4.75% and gets 5%, that is a hawkish surprise even though the level is identical. The macro lens, run well, is always reading the *gap between the outcome and the expectation* — which is why mapping consensus is the prerequisite to reading macro, and why the macro lens overlaps so heavily with the priced-in question that anchors this whole series. Read the level and you will trade every print backwards; read the surprise and you have the lens working as intended.

### Fundamental — the worth

The fundamental lens answers *what is this worth, independent of price?* You are estimating intrinsic value — through a discounted cash flow, a multiple comparison, a sum-of-the-parts, or a simple earnings-power judgment — and comparing it to the market price to get a margin of safety or an overvaluation. Free data: company filings, earnings releases and call transcripts, consensus estimate medians from public aggregators, peer multiples.

The fundamental lens **dominates over long horizons and in dispersion-rich markets.** Over a three-year holding period, the price converges toward value with high reliability; the worth is the gravity that wins eventually. In a market where companies are diverging — some compounding earnings, some bleeding — the fundamental lens is what separates them. It recedes over short horizons (where value is nearly irrelevant to a three-day move) and in a macro shock (where the tide overwhelms the worth temporarily).

Its blind spot — timing and path — is the single most expensive thing analysts forget. "Cheap" is a statement about the destination, not the schedule, and you can be early by years.

### Technical — the path

The technical lens answers *what is the price doing?* — trend, support and resistance, momentum, volume, breadth. It reads the market's own footprints with no opinion about the company. The trend tells you the prevailing direction; the levels tell you where supply and demand have clustered; momentum tells you whether the move is accelerating or fading. We treat the technical lens honestly here as *information about the path*, not magic — for the full honest treatment see [what technical analysis really is](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/what-technical-analysis-really-is) and the warning in [the indicator trap](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/the-indicator-trap) about drowning in redundant indicators. Free data: any charting package.

The technical lens **dominates over short horizons and at decision points** — entries, stops, where to add. For a three-day trade, the path *is* the trade; the worth is almost irrelevant. It also earns its keep at the *level* level: even a multi-year fundamental buyer uses the technical lens to avoid buying into a falling knife. It recedes as a *directional* signal over long horizons, where the path is dominated by the worth.

Its blind spot — the *why* — is why a technician alone got the opening story wrong. The chart showed a downtrend; it could not show that the downtrend was a temporary index seller rather than a deteriorating business.

### Flows — who is buying

The flows lens answers *who is actually moving money in or out, right now?* This is the marginal supply and demand under the price: ETF creations and redemptions, mutual-fund flows, corporate buybacks, index rebalancing, dealer hedging, and systematic strategy flows (volatility-targeting funds, CTAs, risk parity). Flows are the *mechanical* layer — buying and selling that happens regardless of anyone's opinion of value. Free and low-cost data: published fund-flow reports, ETF holdings and share-count changes, index reconstitution schedules, buyback announcements.

The flows lens **dominates when there is a large price-insensitive participant.** An index rebalance forces funds to buy or sell a fixed quantity regardless of price; a major buyback puts a steady bid under a stock; a wave of passive inflows lifts the biggest names mechanically. In the opening story, the flows lens was the *decisive* lens because it identified a large forced seller that the other two lenses misread as either a business problem (it wasn't) or a value opportunity to buy immediately (it wasn't yet). It recedes when no large mechanical participant is active and the price is set by discretionary opinion.

Its blind spot is the lag and the cliff: flow data is often reported days late, and a flow that looks relentless can stop the instant a rebalance completes — so flows tell you *who is buying* but require you to also know *for how long*.

A note on why flows and positioning are *two* lenses and not one, because the distinction trips up most analysts. Flows are a *rate* — money moving per unit time, the marginal buyer and seller acting right now. Positioning is a *stock* — the total accumulated bets already on the table. The relationship is the same as between the speed of a car and its position on the road: flows tell you how fast capital is moving and in which direction; positioning tells you where the crowd has already ended up. They can point in opposite directions, and when they do, the read is rich. Heavy *inflows* (fast money piling in) into an *already-extreme long position* (a stock that has accumulated to a crowded extreme) is the classic late-blow-off-top signature — the last buyers rushing in just as the crowd is maximally committed. Conversely, *outflows* into a *light* position means a trend that is being abandoned before it ever got crowded — often a base, not a top. Reading flows and positioning as one merged "supply-demand" lens throws away exactly this rate-versus-stock information, which is why the framework keeps them separate.

### Positioning — who is already committed

The positioning lens answers *who is already in this trade, and how fragile is that?* Where flows are the marginal money moving *now*, positioning is the *stock* of capital already committed to a belief. The data: the CFTC Commitments of Traders report (large-speculator net long/short in futures), short interest and days-to-cover, fund-manager surveys, the put/call ratio, dealer gamma. Positioning reveals the **pain trade**: when everyone is leaning the same way, the move that hurts the most people is the one that forces them all to unwind at once. The [event-trading work on positioning and the pain trade](/blog/trading/event-trading/positioning-and-the-pain-trade) is the deep treatment.

The positioning lens **dominates at extremes and into catalysts.** When positioning is at a multi-year record on one side, the marginal buyer (or seller) is exhausted and the only thing left to happen at the margin is the reversal. Into a known catalyst — an earnings report, an FOMC, a data print — positioning tells you which outcome would inflict maximum pain, and markets gravitate toward pain. It recedes when positioning is balanced; a balanced book is just a forecast with no fragility.

Its blind spot — no timing trigger — is critical. Crowding is a *condition* that loads the spring; it does not pull the trigger. You pair positioning with a catalyst (which often comes from the macro or fundamental lens) to time it.

### Sentiment — the mood

The sentiment lens answers *what is the emotional temperature?* Fear-and-greed gauges, the AAII bull/bear spread, the put/call ratio as a fear proxy, the VIX, financial-media tone. At extremes, sentiment is a contrarian signal: peak greed marks tops and peak fear marks bottoms, because the emotion is the residue of capital already committed (which links sentiment tightly to positioning). Free data: published fear-greed indices, the AAII survey, the VIX.

The sentiment lens **dominates at emotional extremes — panics and manias.** When fear is total, the selling is indiscriminate and the bargains are everywhere; when greed is total, the last buyer has bought. In the muddy middle, sentiment is the *weakest* of the six lenses — noisy, no natural level, easily faked by a single news cycle. That is why, in the run order, sentiment goes *last*: it is a tiebreaker and an extreme-detector, not a foundation. Letting the mood lead the read is how you get whipsawed by every headline.

Its blind spot — noise and no level — is why sentiment earns the smallest default weight. It confirms; it rarely decides.

## Weighting the lenses by regime and horizon

The single most common mistake after "I only use one lens" is "I weight all six lenses equally, always." The lenses do not have fixed weights. **The regime reweights the stack, and so does the horizon.** Reading all six is mandatory; weighting them by context is the skill.

### Weighting by regime

A regime is the prevailing character of the market — what is driving prices right now. The same six lenses get reordered by which force is in charge.

![Matrix of five market regimes and which lens leads, follows, or is demoted in each](/imgs/blogs/the-six-lenses-a-framework-for-reading-any-market-3.png)

The figure above is the regime playbook. In a **macro shock**, the tide leads, positioning matters (because the shock forces the crowded side to unwind), and fundamentals are temporarily demoted — a great company falls with everything else. In a **calm uptrend**, technical and fundamental lead (path and worth both work), flows confirm, and macro is demoted because the flat tide is not the story. In **earnings season**, the fundamental lens leads on a name, sentiment matters (the reaction depends on the mood going in), and macro is demoted. At a **crowded extreme**, positioning and sentiment lead — when everyone is on one side, the fragility *is* the trade — and the fundamental lens is demoted because value is not what breaks a crowded trade; the pain trade is. In a **panic or crash**, flows and positioning lead (forced selling and margin calls are mechanical), macro is the backdrop, and fundamentals are completely ignored in the moment.

The practical rule: **before you weight, name the regime.** One sentence — "we are in a macro-driven, rates-led, risk-off regime" or "this is a calm, dispersion-rich stock-picker's tape." Naming the regime tells you which lens to trust most and which to down-weight, before you have read a single chart.

Why name the regime *first*, before reading any lens? Because the order protects you from a subtle bias: if you read the lenses first and then decide the weights, you will unconsciously up-weight whichever lens happens to support the conclusion you are already leaning toward. An analyst who is bullish a stock will, after the fact, decide that "this is a fundamentals-led market" precisely because the fundamentals are bullish — which is circular reasoning that launders a hunch into a framework. Setting the regime and the weights *before* you look at any data forces honesty: you commit to "macro leads at 40% here" because of the observable regime, and then you have to live with whatever the macro lens actually says, even if it kills your trade. The regime call is a pre-commitment, and pre-commitment is what keeps the framework from degenerating into confirmation bias with extra steps.

There is also a fast way to name the regime when you are unsure: look at *correlations*. When cross-asset correlations are high — everything moving together, stocks and bonds and gold all reacting to the same headline — you are in a macro regime and macro leads. When correlations are low — names moving on their own news, dispersion wide — you are in a stock-picker's regime and the fundamental and technical lenses lead. Correlation regime is the single best free tell for which lens deserves the top weight.

### Weighting by horizon

Horizon reweights the stack as decisively as regime. The shorter the horizon, the more the *fast* lenses (technical, flows, positioning, sentiment) dominate; the longer the horizon, the more the *slow* lenses (fundamental, macro) dominate. This is not opinion — it is mechanical. Over three days, the worth of a company cannot change; only the path, the flow, the positioning, and the mood can move the price. Over three years, the path is noise around a trend set by the worth.

#### Worked example: same name, two horizons, two weightings

Take a single stock — call it the same NVDA-type name from our scorecard — and read it for two different horizons.

For a **3-day** horizon, your lens weights might be: technical 35%, flows 25%, positioning 20%, sentiment 12%, macro 5%, fundamental 3%. The fundamental lens is nearly zeroed because the worth cannot move in three days; the trade is the path and the flow. Suppose you size a \$20,000 short-term position and the lenses say: technical bullish (uptrend intact), flows bullish (passive bid), positioning *caution* (crowded long into an event), sentiment *caution* (euphoric). The weighted read is mildly bullish but the two caution flags carry 32% of the weight — so you take the trade smaller, say \$12,000 of the \$20,000, with a tight stop, because the fast lenses that dominate this horizon are split.

For a **3-year** horizon on the *exact same name*, the weights invert: fundamental 35%, macro 25%, flows 15%, positioning 12%, technical 8%, sentiment 5%. Now the fundamental lens leads. The same stock reads: fundamental strongly bullish (earnings compounding), macro neutral (tide flat), positioning caution (crowded), sentiment caution (euphoric). The weighted read is *strongly* bullish because the dominant lens (fundamental, 35%) is the most bullish, and the caution flags — which dominated the 3-day read — now carry only 17% combined. So on the same \$20,000 of intended capital, the 3-year version takes the *full* \$20,000 and accepts more drawdown, because the lens that owns this horizon says the worth wins.

The intuition: the same six readings produced a \$12,000 cautious trade over three days and a \$20,000 conviction position over three years, purely because the horizon reweighted which lenses got to vote.

This worked example also kills the idea that "the lenses disagree, so I can't act." They disagreed identically in both cases — the disagreement did not change. What changed was the *weight* on each disagreeing lens, and that weight is set by your horizon.

## How synthesis becomes conviction and size

The payoff of reading six semi-independent lenses is that *agreement is quantifiable*. The more lenses point the same way, the higher your justified conviction — and conviction is what you convert into size. This is the bridge from "reading the market" to "taking a position."

![Bar chart showing realized hit rate rising as more of six lenses point the same way](/imgs/blogs/the-six-lenses-a-framework-for-reading-any-market-6.png)

The chart above is illustrative — the exact percentages are made up to show the *shape* — but the shape is the real lesson and it follows directly from the independence math. When only one of six lenses points your way, you are at roughly a coin flip; you have no edge, and any position is paying the spread for nothing. As more semi-independent lenses align, the hit rate climbs, and crucially it climbs *faster than linearly* near the top, because each additional independent confirmation multiplies rather than adds. Six aligned lenses is a genuinely different animal from three.

#### Worked example: a full six-lens read producing a sized \$20,000 position

Here is the complete process on one name, end to end, producing a dollar position. The setup: a large-cap semiconductor stock, a 3-year horizon, and you have \$200,000 of risk capital with a rule that a full-conviction single-name position is 8% of the book, or \$16,000, and a maximum single name of 10% (\$20,000).

Run the six lenses and score each as a stance plus a weight:

1. **Macro:** rates stable, growth steady, tide flat. Stance: *neutral.* Weight: 20%.
2. **Fundamental:** earnings compounding at 25% a year, trades at a discount to its own history on growth-adjusted multiples. Stance: *bullish.* Weight: 30%.
3. **Technical:** clean uptrend above a rising 200-day, higher highs and higher lows. Stance: *bullish.* Weight: 15%.
4. **Flows:** steady passive inflows into the broad index lift the name mechanically every month. Stance: *bullish.* Weight: 15%.
5. **Positioning:** large-speculator longs near a two-year high; the name is a crowded favorite. Stance: *caution.* Weight: 12%.
6. **Sentiment:** retail euphoria rising, the name is a media darling. Stance: *caution.* Weight: 8%.

![Filled six-lens scorecard for a single ticker collapsing into one sized long position](/imgs/blogs/the-six-lenses-a-framework-for-reading-any-market-5.png)

Now synthesize. Four lenses are bullish (fundamental, technical, flows — and macro is at least not against you), carrying 60%+ of the weight. Two lenses are caution (positioning and sentiment), carrying 20% combined. The synthesized stance is **clearly bullish**, but the two caution flags are not noise — they are the crowding-and-mood pair, which is exactly the configuration that precedes sharp pullbacks even inside an uptrend. So the synthesis is "bullish thesis, fragile entry."

That synthesis maps directly to size. A clean six-for-six bullish read would justify the full 8% (\$16,000) or even a stretch toward the 10% cap (\$20,000). But the two caution flags carrying 20% of the weight knock the conviction down a tier. So you commit **4% — \$8,000 — not 8%**, and you split it: half now (\$4,000) and half on a pullback that lets the crowding flush out (the positioning caution is precisely a warning to expect that flush). The position is real, the thesis is bullish, and the size honestly reflects that two independent lenses are flashing caution.

The intuition: the scorecard did not just tell you *whether* to buy; it told you *how much*, and the \$8,000 figure is the arithmetic of four bullish lenses minus two cautious ones, weighted by regime and horizon.

This is the entire framework in one example. Read all six, score each as stance plus weight, count the agreements, and let the count set the size. For the formal conviction-to-size mapping — Kelly fractions, expectancy — see [position sizing and the Kelly criterion](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/position-sizing-and-kelly-criterion).

### The order to run the lenses

The order matters, and the principle is **slow context first, fast mood last.**

![Pipeline of the seven-step run order from macro through sentiment to synthesis](/imgs/blogs/the-six-lenses-a-framework-for-reading-any-market-7.png)

Run **macro first** to set the tide — it frames everything and you do not want to read a chart before you know whether you should be in the water at all. Then **fundamental** to anchor the worth. Then **flows** to find the buyer and **positioning** to find the crowded side. Then **technical** to read the path and find your levels. Then — *last* — **sentiment**, as a tiebreaker and extreme-detector. Finally, **synthesize**: where the lenses agree, that is your conviction and your size; where they disagree, that is your flag to investigate the blind spot in conflict.

The reason sentiment goes last is anchoring. If you read the breathless headlines first, the mood colors everything you see afterward — you will read the chart as bullish because you are already excited. Reading the slow, factual lenses first builds a sober base; the mood is then a final calibration, not the lens you anchor on. This run order is also the spine of the [daily and weekly reading routine](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/the-daily-and-weekly-process-a-repeatable-reading-routine).

## Common misconceptions

The six-lens framework is simple to state and hard to practice, mostly because four very natural-sounding beliefs sabotage it. Each one is wrong in a specific, correctable way.

### "I'm a fundamental guy" (or "I'm a technical guy")

This is the most common and most expensive self-description in the business, and it is a confession dressed as an identity. Saying "I'm a fundamental guy" means "I read one lens and I am structurally blind to five things." The fundamental guy is blind to the path (he buys cheap stocks that get cheaper for two years), to flows (he buys into a forced seller), to positioning (he buys the most crowded long in the market right before it unwinds), and to the mood (he buys at peak euphoria because the numbers still look fine). The technical guy is the mirror image — he sells a downtrend that is a temporary index rebalance, exactly the mistake in our opening story.

The correction is not "abandon your strength." Keep your best lens; just *finish the circuit.* A fundamental analyst who spends ninety percent of his energy on the worth and ten percent confirming the other five lenses are not screaming is using his edge without being blindsided. The one-lens analyst is not committed; he is exposed.

#### Worked example: the cost of a pure-technical view that missed a fundamental catalyst (\$24,000)

A purely technical trader is short a \$120,000 notional position in a stock — a clean downtrend, lower highs, below the 200-day, every bounce sold. The chart is unambiguous and he is right about the path. What the chart cannot show: the company is two weeks from an earnings report where a new product line — visible to any fundamental analyst reading the filings — is about to swing the company from a loss to a profit. The fundamental catalyst is invisible to the technical lens by construction; the chart only shows the *past* path, and the catalyst is in the *future*.

Earnings land. The stock gaps up 20% overnight on the surprise profit. Our technician, short \$120,000, eats a 20% adverse move: a **\$24,000 loss**, most of it in a single overnight gap he could not stop out of because the move happened while the market was closed. Had he run even a cursory fundamental lens — "is there a catalyst in the next two weeks that could break this trend?" — he would have either avoided the short into the print or sized it at a fraction. The \$24,000 is the literal price of a one-lens view: the cost of being blind to the one thing your single lens cannot see. The fix costs ten minutes of reading the earnings calendar and the filings.

### "More lenses just means more confusion"

The fear here is that six readings will paralyze you — six opinions, no decision. This gets the relationship exactly backward. **More lenses produce *less* confusion, because they resolve ambiguity rather than create it.** A single lens reading "cheap" leaves you genuinely confused about whether to buy now or wait. Add the flows lens showing a forced seller with two months to run, and the confusion *resolves* into a clear plan: wait, then buy. The second lens did not add noise; it answered the question the first lens couldn't.

Confusion comes from *unweighted* lenses, not from *too many* lenses. If you treat all six as equal votes with no regime or horizon weighting, then yes, a split read is paralyzing. The discipline of assigning each lens a weight by regime and horizon is exactly what converts six readings into one decision. The framework is not "collect six opinions and feel torn"; it is "collect six weighted readings and synthesize one call."

### "The lenses are redundant — they all read the same market"

They read the same market, but they read *different data about it* through *different blind spots*, and that is the opposite of redundant. The whole value of the framework is that the lenses are **semi-independent.** Recall the matrix: no two lenses share a blind spot. The fundamental lens and the flows lens can disagree completely — one says "worth \$100, trading at \$70" and the other says "a forced seller is dumping it" — precisely because they are reading different things. If the lenses were redundant, they could never disagree, and disagreement is half the information the framework gives you.

A useful test: if two of your lenses *never* disagree, one of them is not really an independent lens — you are probably reading the same data twice (this is the trap in over-stacking redundant technical indicators, the subject of [the indicator trap](/blog/trading/technical-analysis/the-indicator-trap)). Genuine independence means genuine occasional disagreement. The redundancy fear is actually a sign of *too few* independent lenses, not too many.

### "Technical and fundamental contradict, so one of them must be wrong"

This is the deepest error and the one the opening story is built to destroy. When the fundamental lens says "cheap" and the technical lens says "downtrend," people conclude that one reading is false and try to pick which. But **both can be — and usually are — true at the same time**, because they answer different questions. "Cheap" is a statement about the *worth*; "downtrend" is a statement about the *path*. A stock can absolutely be cheap *and* in a downtrend — that is, in fact, the textbook description of a value opportunity that has not yet turned, or of a value trap. The two readings are not in conflict; together they define the *situation* and demand a third question: *why* is a cheap stock falling?

The synthesis is the answer to that third question, and it comes from the other lenses. If the *why* is a temporary forced seller (flows) into a crowded short (positioning) at peak fear (sentiment), the cheap-but-falling stock is a *buy-the-flush* setup. If the *why* is a deteriorating business the consensus hasn't fully marked down yet (a fundamental signal the lagging multiple hides), the cheap-but-falling stock is a *value trap* to avoid. Same two "contradicting" lenses, opposite conclusions — and you can only tell which by reading the other four. The lenses never contradicted; they were waiting for synthesis. The full treatment of conflicting reads is in [reconciling conflicting signals](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/reconciling-conflicting-signals-when-the-lenses-disagree).

## How it plays out in real markets

Frameworks are easy to admire and hard to run. Here are three end-to-end reads that show the six lenses working under real conditions, including the case where one lens drowned all the others.

### A single stock through all six lenses

Take a real-shaped scenario: a high-quality consumer-staples stock in a steady market. You run the stack in order.

**Macro:** rates are stable, the consumer is holding up, the tide is flat-to-mildly-positive. Stance neutral-bullish, weight 15% (defensive staples are not macro-sensitive). **Fundamental:** the company trades at 18x forward earnings against a five-year average of 22x, grows earnings at a steady 7%, and pays a secure 3% dividend — modestly cheap, high quality. Stance bullish, weight 35% (this is a long-horizon, fundamental-led name). **Technical:** the stock is basing after a 15% decline, just reclaimed its 50-day, volume rising on up days. Stance turning-bullish, weight 15%. **Flows:** defensive funds are seeing inflows as investors rotate toward safety; the name benefits mechanically. Stance bullish, weight 15%. **Positioning:** nothing crowded — large specs are neutral, short interest is low. Stance neutral, weight 12%. **Sentiment:** boring, ignored, no euphoria and no panic — a good sign for a value entry. Stance mildly bullish (boredom is bullish for a cheap quality name), weight 8%.

Synthesis: five of six lenses are bullish or turning bullish, none is a caution flag, and the dominant fundamental lens leads. This is a **high-conviction six-for-six-ish read** — the kind that justifies a full-size position, because semi-independent lenses are all pointing the same way and the disagreement that would cap your size simply isn't there. Contrast this with the semiconductor scorecard earlier: same process, but there two caution flags capped the size. Here, clean agreement uncaps it.

### A macro asset — the dollar — through all six lenses

The framework reads a macro asset just as cleanly as a stock; you just lean the weights toward the macro lens. Read the U.S. dollar (the dollar index).

**Macro:** the dominant lens for a currency. Suppose U.S. rates are rising relative to the rest of the world, growth is diverging in America's favor, and risk is off — all three push the dollar up. Stance strongly bullish, weight 40%. **Fundamental:** for a currency the "worth" is purchasing-power-parity and the balance of payments; suppose the dollar is already expensive on PPP. Stance mildly bearish, weight 15%. **Technical:** the dollar index is in a clean uptrend above its 200-day. Stance bullish, weight 15%. **Flows:** safe-haven flows into Treasuries and dollars are accelerating. Stance bullish, weight 12%. **Positioning:** large speculators are already heavily net-long the dollar — a crowded trade. Stance caution, weight 12%. **Sentiment:** "king dollar" headlines everywhere — euphoric. Stance caution, weight 6%.

Synthesis: the macro lens (40%) is strongly bullish and three other lenses confirm, so the *direction* is up. But the fundamental lens (expensive) plus the positioning and sentiment caution flags (crowded and euphoric) together carry 33% of the weight and all say the same thing: *the up-move is mature and fragile.* The synthesized call is "stay long the trend but tighten stops and do not add — this is late-stage, crowded strength." That is a far more useful, far more *sized* call than any single lens could produce, and it shows the framework working identically on a currency and a stock.

### The 2022 setup where macro dominated everything

2022 is the cleanest example in recent memory of a single lens drowning all the others, and it is the case study every bottom-up analyst should keep taped to the wall.

Coming into 2022, plenty of analysts had beautiful bottom-up theses on high-quality growth stocks: great businesses, expanding markets, strong management. The fundamental lens, in isolation, said *own these compounders.* And the fundamental lens was *not wrong about the businesses* — many of those companies grew revenue and earnings right through the year exactly as the bulls forecast.

Then the macro lens took over completely. Inflation ran far above expectations, and the Fed embarked on the fastest hiking cycle in decades. When the discount rate that values every future cash flow rises violently, the longest-duration assets — high-multiple growth stocks whose value is mostly in distant cash flows — get repriced down hardest, *regardless of how well the business is doing.* The tide went out for every long-duration boat at once. A basket of high-quality growth names fell 50% to 70% even as the underlying businesses kept growing.

#### Worked example: a macro-dominated regime overriding a great bottom-up thesis (\$45,000)

Concretely: a bottom-up analyst commits \$100,000 across three high-quality growth stocks in January 2022, on an airtight fundamental thesis — all three were genuinely great businesses that did grow earnings through the year. By the October low, that \$100,000 basket is worth about \$55,000, a **\$45,000 drawdown**, despite the businesses performing as forecast. The fundamental lens was right about the worth and *completely overridden* by the macro lens in that regime.

Now run the six-lens framework as it should have been run in January 2022. **Macro:** inflation at multi-decade highs and a Fed openly signaling aggressive hikes — a screaming bearish tide for long-duration assets, and in a macro-shock regime macro leads with the highest weight. **Positioning:** growth-stock ownership was at record-crowded levels after a two-year mania. **Sentiment:** euphoric. Three of the six lenses — including the regime's *dominant* lens — were flashing red, and the regime was unambiguously a macro-shock regime in which fundamentals get demoted. A six-lens analyst would have *kept the bullish long-term thesis* but cut the position size dramatically or hedged the duration risk, turning a \$45,000 drawdown into a fraction of that. The fundamental thesis was a horoscope until the macro lens was allowed to override it; the \$45,000 is the tuition for letting one lens own the call in a regime that belonged to a different lens.

The lesson is not "macro always wins." It is "name the regime, then let the regime's dominant lens own the weight." In 2022 that lens was macro, and any framework that did not let it dominate produced a \$45,000 hole. In a calm 2017-style market the same framework would have let the fundamental lens lead and the bottom-up thesis would have worked beautifully. The framework is regime-aware; the one-lens analyst is not.

## The playbook

Here is the concrete, repeatable process — the six-lens scorecard you fill for any ticker, any asset, any time. This is the framework as a tool you run, not a theory you admire.

**Step 0 — Name the regime and the horizon.** Before any lens, write one sentence: *"We are in a [macro-shock / calm-uptrend / earnings-season / crowded-extreme / panic] regime, and my horizon is [3-day / 3-month / 3-year]."* This sets your weights before you read a single data point, so the data can't bias your weighting.

**Step 1 — Fill the scorecard, one row per lens, in run order.** For each of the six lenses, in the order macro → fundamental → flows → positioning → technical → sentiment, write down three things:

- **Stance:** bullish / neutral / bearish / caution (caution = "leaning your way but fragile").
- **Weight:** a percentage that reflects the regime and horizon from Step 0. The six weights sum to 100%.
- **Why:** one short phrase — the single fact driving the stance. If you can't name the fact, the stance is a guess; mark it neutral.

The scorecard is one table:

```
Lens          Stance     Weight   Why (one fact)
Macro         ______     ___%     ________________
Fundamental   ______     ___%     ________________
Flows         ______     ___%     ________________
Positioning   ______     ___%     ________________
Technical     ______     ___%     ________________
Sentiment     ______     ___%     ________________
                         100%
```

**Step 2 — Count the agreements.** How many lenses point the same direction, and do they carry the weight? Four-plus aligned lenses carrying 60%+ of the weight is a conviction read. A 3-3 split, or alignment that is all in the low-weight lenses, is a low-conviction read. The *count weighted by the regime* is your conviction dial.

**Step 3 — Read the disagreements as information, not noise.** Every caution or opposing stance is pointing at a specific blind spot. A cheap fundamental versus a downtrending technical is asking "why is it falling?" — go answer it with flows and positioning. Do not average disagreements away; investigate them. Often the disagreement *is* the opportunity (a temporary forced seller in a cheap name) or the *warning* (a crowded, euphoric name in a great uptrend).

**Step 4 — Synthesize into one sentence and one size.** Collapse the scorecard into a single sentence — "bullish thesis, fragile entry, buy half now and half on a flush" — and a single dollar size driven by the weighted agreement count. High agreement → full size (e.g., your 8% slot). Two caution flags → half size. A genuine 3-3 split with no resolution → stand aside; you do not have a call, you have a coin flip. The mechanics of collapsing six readings into one sentence are the subject of [from data to a read](/blog/trading/analyst-edge/from-data-to-a-read-collapsing-everything-into-one-sentence).

**Step 5 — Write the invalidation.** Note which lens, if it flipped, would kill the call. "If positioning gets *more* crowded into the catalyst, or if the macro tide turns risk-off, I'm out." A call you cannot invalidate is a horoscope. The invalidation is what makes the six-lens read an accountable, updatable view rather than a static opinion.

Run this scorecard on every name, every time. It takes fifteen minutes once you know where the free data lives, and it is the difference between being one of the first two analysts in the opening story — each correct and each about to lose money — and being the third, who synthesized all the lenses and made the only call worth making. The lenses are not the edge. The synthesis is.

One last discipline to bolt onto the scorecard: keep it. Save each filled scorecard with the date, the regime call, and the synthesized sentence, and revisit it when the trade resolves. Over fifty names you will learn which lens you systematically over-weight (almost everyone over-weights the lens they were trained on) and which regime calls you keep getting wrong. That feedback loop is what turns the framework from a static checklist into a calibrated instrument tuned to *your* blind spots — because the framework's job is to cover the market's blind spots, and the scorecard archive's job is to cover yours. Read all six lenses, weight them by regime and horizon, synthesize one sized call with a written invalidation, and then grade yourself honestly. That full loop — read, weight, synthesize, size, invalidate, review — is the analyst's edge made repeatable.
